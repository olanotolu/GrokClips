Hakainde Hichilema


Hakainde Hichilema (born June 4, 1962) is a Zambian businessman and politician who has served as the seventh president of Zambia since August 2021.[1][2]
Born into humble circumstances in Monze District, Hichilema attended the University of Zambia, graduating in 1986 with a Bachelor of Arts in Economics and Business Administration, followed by an MBA in Finance and Business Strategy from the University of Birmingham.[2] He built a career in consulting and finance, serving as CEO of Coopers & Lybrand Zambia and Grant Thornton Zambia, chairing boards such as Barclays Bank Zambia, and establishing himself as a prominent cattle rancher and entrepreneur with investments in tourism and agribusiness.[2][1]
Entering politics as leader of the United Party for National Development, Hichilema contested five prior presidential elections unsuccessfully before winning in 2021 with 59% of the vote amid high turnout, campaigning on economic revival, debt management, and anti-corruption measures.[3][2] His administration has prioritized fiscal reforms, achieving the restructuring of over 92% of Zambia's external debt by 2025, which alleviated repayment pressures following the 2020 default under the prior government, alongside reductions in inflation from over 20% in 2021 to single digits in subsequent years, and GDP growth averaging around 4% in 2024 with projections exceeding 6% for 2025.[4][5][6] Policies such as free primary and secondary education and increased constituency development funding have expanded access to services, though challenges persist including volatile inflation spikes to 16.7% by late 2024 and criticisms from opposition figures and human rights observers regarding judicial independence, arrests of critics, and restrictions on civil liberties.[2][7][5]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

Hakainde Hichilema was born on June 4, 1962, in Monze District, southern Zambia, into a Tonga family of modest means engaged in subsistence farming and livestock herding.[1][8] His early years were marked by rural economic constraints typical of the region, where households relied on small-scale agriculture and pastoral activities for survival.[9] As a child, Hichilema contributed to family duties by herding cattle, an experience that instilled familiarity with self-reliant rural labor amid limited resources and infrastructural challenges in post-independence Zambia.[10] Available biographical accounts indicate no notable family migrations within southern Zambia, with the household rooted in local agrarian patterns rather than urban pursuits.[1]

Academic Qualifications

Hichilema completed his secondary education at Kalomo Secondary School in Southern Province, Zambia, attending from 1976 to 1980.[11] Admitted to the University of Zambia on a government scholarship, he graduated in 1986 with a Bachelor of Arts degree in Economics and Business Administration.[10] [1] He later pursued postgraduate studies abroad, earning a Master of Business Administration in Finance and Business Strategy from the University of Birmingham in the United Kingdom.[12] [10]

Business and Professional Career

Corporate Roles

Hichilema commenced his corporate career in the consulting sector following his academic qualifications, ascending to executive leadership in international firms operating in Zambia. From 1994 to 1998, he served as Chief Executive Officer of Coopers & Lybrand Zambia, a predecessor entity to PricewaterhouseCoopers, where the firm delivered audit, tax, and advisory services amid the country's post-independence economic reforms.[12][1] During this period, Coopers & Lybrand participated in Zambia's privatization program initiated after the 1991 shift to multiparty democracy and market-oriented policies under President Frederick Chiluba, advising on the divestiture of state-owned enterprises to enhance operational efficiencies and reduce fiscal burdens.[13] The process transferred approximately 250 parastatals to private ownership by the early 2000s, yielding empirical gains such as productivity improvements in privatized mining and manufacturing firmsâ€”evidenced by post-privatization output increases of up to 20% in select casesâ€”though marred by instances of asset undervaluation and limited reinvestment.[13] Hichilema has stated that his involvement was through the firm's professional capacity, not personal decision-making on asset sales.[14] In 1998, following the global merger forming PricewaterhouseCoopers, Hichilema transitioned to lead Grant Thornton Zambia as CEO until 2006, expanding its footprint in financial advisory, risk management, and compliance services for Zambia's burgeoning private sector.[1][15] Under his tenure, the firm supported corporate governance enhancements and transaction advisory, aligning with Zambia's ongoing liberalization that attracted foreign investment and diversified economic activities beyond copper mining.[15]

Agricultural Ventures and Wealth Accumulation

Hichilema established his agricultural enterprises in the early phases of his business career following his education, beginning with a modest cattle herd of six animals that expanded significantly through disciplined management and reinvestment.[16] By the 2010s, he had developed operations across multiple ranches, primarily in southern Zambia, including sites in Namwala District such as Naminwe, Ngabo, and Ibamba farms.[17] These ventures encompassed commercial livestock rearing, with Hichilema emerging as Zambia's second-largest cattle rancher, managing nearly 100,000 head across four primary ranches.[18] [19] His operations focused on high-quality beef production, positioning him as one of the country's leading meat suppliers to local markets, which supported sustained revenue generation independent of government dependencies.[19] In addition to livestock, Hichilema diversified into crop farming, cultivating maize and other staples on his southern properties, demonstrating integrated agribusiness practices that enhanced operational resilience.[17] The scale of these activities, including periodic auctions of thousands of bullsâ€”such as over 3,000 in 2025â€”underscored the commercial viability and output capacity of his ranches.[20] This agricultural foundation yielded substantial self-made wealth, establishing Hichilema as a millionaire whose fortunes derived primarily from ranching successes rather than inherited or state-linked assets.[9] The enterprises contributed tangibly to Zambia's livestock sector by improving breed quality and supply chains, though their direct export volumes prior to his presidency remain limited in documented records, with emphasis instead on domestic market dominance.[19] Overall, these ventures exemplified scalable private-sector farming, amassing economic value estimated in the tens of millions of Zambian kwacha through herd growth and sales.[18]

Entry into Politics

Founding Involvement with UPND

Hakainde Hichilema, a successful businessman at the time, became involved with the United Party for National Development (UPND) in its early years through his association with founder Anderson Mazoka, whom he regarded as a mentor.[19] The UPND was established in December 1998 by Mazoka, a former executive at the Anglo American Corporation, as an alternative to the ruling Movement for Multi-Party Democracy, emphasizing economic reform and transparency in governance.[21] [22] Hichilema provided advisory input to Mazoka during the party's formative period in the early 2000s, drawing on his private-sector experience to shape its pro-business orientation. The party's ideological foundations under Mazoka centered on economic liberalism, advocating fiscal discipline, export promotion, and reduced government intervention to foster growth and improve the balance of payments.[23] This approach reflected Mazoka's background in corporate management and positioned UPND as a proponent of market-oriented policies amid Zambia's post-liberalization challenges. Anti-corruption measures were implicit in calls for accountable governance, though formalized later in party platforms.[24] Following Mazoka's death on May 24, 2006, Hichilema assumed the UPND presidency, stepping into an interim leadership role during a period of internal strife and organizational weakening that threatened the party's viability. [25] His election to the position helped stabilize the party amid succession disputes, preserving its opposition stance against the incumbent administration.[19]

Leadership Ascension

Hakainde Hichilema was elected president of the United Party for National Development (UPND) on July 15, 2006, following the death of the party's founder and leader, Anderson Mazoka, on May 24, 2006.[26][27] Mazoka, a prominent businessman and Hichilema's mentor, had established UPND in 1998 as a key opposition force against the ruling Movement for Multi-Party Democracy (MMD), but his passing created a leadership vacuum that threatened party cohesion.[28] Hichilema, a founding member and fellow Tonga tribesman from southern Zambia, leveraged his business acumen and close ties to Mazoka to secure the position at the party convention, positioning himself as a continuity candidate amid internal calls for a decisive figure to sustain opposition momentum.[18] Upon assuming leadership, Hichilema prioritized stabilizing UPND by consolidating its core support in southern Zambia while signaling readiness to contest national power against the MMD, whose long dominance under presidents like Levy Mwanawasa had entrenched one-party-like control despite multiparty reforms.[26] He immediately declared his intent to run for the presidency in the upcoming 2006 general election, framing UPND as a viable alternative focused on economic revitalization and anti-corruption, drawing on his private-sector experience to differentiate from MMD's governance record.[19] This ascension marked a transitional phase for UPND, which had previously secured strong regional parliamentary gains under Mazoka but struggled with national fragmentation post his death.[28] Hichilema's early tenure emphasized strategic outreach to mitigate UPND's perception as a regional, Tonga-centric party, initiating efforts to forge ties with non-southern ethnic groups and disaffected MMD elements to build a broader opposition front against the ruling party's entrenched power.[29] These moves aimed at diluting ethnic voting patterns that confined UPND's base primarily to southern provinces, though initial expansion remained limited as the party navigated internal stabilization and MMD's electoral advantages from 2006 to 2011.[30] By positioning UPND as a principled economic reformer, Hichilema sought to cultivate cross-regional alliances, setting the stage for sustained challenges to MMD hegemony without immediate large-scale coalitions.[31]

Pre-Presidency Political Activities

Electoral Campaigns (2006â€“2016)

Hakainde Hichilema entered national politics as the presidential candidate for the United Democratic Alliance (UDA), a coalition anchored by his United Party for National Development (UPND), in Zambia's 28 September 2006 general election. Incumbent President Levy Mwanawasa of the Movement for Multi-Party Democracy (MMD) won re-election with 1,177,846 votes (42.98%), while Hichilema secured 677,124 votes, approximately 25% of the total, finishing third behind Michael Sata of the Patriotic Front (PF) who received 762,118 votes (27.79%).[32][33] Hichilema's platform critiqued MMD's economic management, advocating for private sector-led growth and reduced state intervention, drawing on his business experience to promise job creation and agricultural diversification amid persistent rural poverty.[34] In the 20 September 2011 general election, Hichilema again represented UPND, contesting against MMD incumbent Rupiah Banda and PF's Michael Sata following Mwanawasa's death in 2008. Sata emerged victorious with 1,150,045 votes (43.0%), Banda received 987,195 (36.9%), and Hichilema garnered 521,110 votes, roughly 18% of the total, placing third.[35][36] Campaigning on economic liberalization and anti-corruption measures, Hichilema highlighted fiscal indiscipline under Banda's administration, including rising public debt and inefficiencies in copper-dependent revenues, while positioning UPND as an alternative to MMD's perceived cronyism and PF's populist appeals.[37] Hichilema's 2016 presidential bid against incumbent Edgar Lungu of PF occurred amid acute economic distress, including widespread power shortages from drought-induced hydropower deficitsâ€”up to 20 hours of daily load-sheddingâ€”and escalating public debt that reached 60% of GDP by mid-year.[38][39] In the 11 August election, Lungu was declared winner with 1,860,651 votes (50.35%), narrowly defeating Hichilema's 1,760,759 votes (47.63%), a margin of under 100,000 votes.[40][41] Hichilema's campaign centered on restoring private sector vitality through deregulation, prudent debt management to avert default risks, and combating state capture by PF-linked elites, arguing that Lungu's policies had fueled corruption and stifled investment.[42] Following results, Hichilema alleged electoral fraud, including ballot stuffing and irregularities in urban strongholds like Lusaka, prompting calls for a recount and a failed constitutional petition dismissed by the Supreme Court on procedural grounds.[43][44][45]

Alliances and Opposition Strategies

To counter the Patriotic Front's (PF) hold on power following Michael Sata's 2011 victory and Edgar Lungu's 2015 ascension, Hakainde Hichilema's United Party for National Development (UPND) pursued coalition-building with fragmented opposition elements, though comprehensive pacts proved elusive amid ethnic and regional divisions. In the lead-up to the 2016 general election, UPND secured endorsements from factions within the former ruling Movement for Multi-Party Democracy (MMD), including public support from 12 MMD parliamentarians who backed Hichilema as a viable alternative to PF incumbency. These alliances aimed to consolidate anti-PF votes but were undermined by MMD internal splits and competing overtures to the ruling party.[46] Opposition strategies emphasized youth mobilization, leveraging UPND's urban base in Lusaka and Southern Province to highlight economic stagnation, youth unemployment rates exceeding 20%, and PF governance failures.[47] Party youth cadres were actively deployed in campaign rallies and constituency-level organizing, though this contributed to heightened electoral tensions, with youth involvement in clashes between UPND and PF supporters documented in multiple constituencies.[48] Hichilema appealed to international election observers, including the European Union Election Observation Mission, which reported uneven playing fields, delays in voter registration, and isolated irregularities favoring the incumbent, bolstering UPND claims of systemic bias.[49] A core tactic involved advocacy for constitutional and electoral reforms to curb incumbency advantages, such as strengthening the Electoral Commission of Zambia's independence and enforcing existing term limits to prevent extensions of PF rule. UPND criticized the PF government's handling of the 2016 constitutional referendumâ€”intended to expand bill of rights protections but marred by 40% turnout and opposition boycottsâ€”as insufficient, pushing instead for comprehensive changes to mandate 50%+1 victory thresholds and running mates to reduce ethnic politicking.[50] Post-election, Hichilema filed a Supreme Court petition alleging rigging through inflated voter rolls and collusion, garnering international sympathy but ultimately dismissed on procedural grounds, highlighting legal avenues as a protracted strategy against perceived PF manipulation.[51]

Legal and Political Persecutions

2017 Arrest and Treason Charges

On April 8, 2017, during President Edgar Lungu's attendance at the Kuomboka traditional ceremony in Mongu, Western Province, Hakainde Hichilema's convoy of over 60 vehicles allegedly obstructed the presidential motorcade on Limulunga Road by failing to yield right of way, prompting police intervention to allow Lungu's escort to pass.[52][53] The incident occurred amid heightened post-election frictions following the August 2016 presidential vote, where Lungu secured victory with 50.35% of the vote against Hichilema's 47.63%, a result contested by the opposition through a failed constitutional petition and subsequent street protests that led to arrests and sporadic violence.[54][55] Hichilema was arrested at his residence in Lusaka on the night of April 10, 2017, by police who reportedly broke in to effect the detention, and formally charged the following day with treason under Zambia's Penal Code, alongside five associates including party vice-president Geoffrey Bwalya Mwamba.[52][56] The charges alleged that the motorcade obstruction constituted an act of treason by endangering the president's life and forming part of a broader plot to overthrow the government, with additional counts of disobeying lawful orders and using insulting language toward police.[57][58] Zambian authorities maintained the actions posed a direct threat to national security, while Hichilema and the United Party for National Development (UPND) denied intent to harm, describing the event as a routine traffic occurrence exaggerated for political suppression.[53][59] In initial court appearances before the Lusaka Magistrates' Court, Hichilema pleaded not guilty, but bail was denied on grounds that treason is a non-bailable offense punishable by at least 15 years imprisonment or death.[60][56] On April 26, 2017, the court rejected defense motions to dismiss the treason charges, upholding the state's case for trial and citing sufficient evidence of seditious intent linked to the incident.[60][61] International observers, including Amnesty International, criticized the charges as disproportionate and politically motivated, arguing they stemmed from routine protocol disputes rather than verifiable coup plotting.[57]

Subsequent Detentions and Releases

Hichilema remained in custody for 127 days following his April 2017 arrest, held at Zambia's Maximum Security Prison under conditions described by Amnesty International as degrading and inhumane, potentially amounting to torture or other ill-treatment.[62] These included inadequate access to medical care and legal representation, contributing to reports of health deterioration amid prolonged pretrial detention without bail.[57] Domestic protests erupted across Zambia, with opposition supporters decrying the charges as politically motivated, while international organizations condemned the detention as persecution through prosecution.[63] Amnesty International repeatedly called for the immediate release of Hichilema and his co-accused, labeling the treason charges trumped-up and emblematic of broader suppression of dissent.[64] The European Union expressed concerns over the erosion of democratic norms, though specific statements focused on the need for fair trials and respect for opposition rights amid Zambia's declining political freedoms.[65] These reactions underscored causal pressures from external scrutiny and internal unrest, which likely influenced the government's calculus on sustaining the case amid accusations of authoritarian consolidation under President Edgar Lungu. On August 16, 2017, the Director of Public Prosecutions entered a nolle prosequi, effectively dropping the treason charges against Hichilema and five associates before trial, citing procedural irregularities and evidential shortcomings that undermined prosecution viability.[66] [67] This release mechanism, bypassing full judicial adjudication, highlighted compromised independence in Zambia's legal system, where executive influence over prosecutorial decisions facilitated the withdrawal rather than a court quashing on merits.[65] Post-release, Hichilema encountered a pattern of shorter detentions and harassments, such as a two-hour hold at Chipata Airport in July to thwart political engagements, reflecting ongoing tactics to impede opposition activities under Lungu's tenure.[68] These incidents, while not matching the 2017 duration, evidenced systematic efforts to neutralize rivals through selective enforcement, eroding judicial checks and fostering an environment of political intimidation.[30]

2020 Police Actions

In 2020, Zambian police imposed restrictions on activities by Hakainde Hichilema and the United Party for National Development (UPND), citing the Public Order Act of 1955 and COVID-19 containment measures, which opposition figures argued suppressed mobilization ahead of the 2021 elections. Police routinely denied permits for UPND rallies and gatherings, particularly in Lusaka and peri-urban areas, while permitting similar events by the ruling Patriotic Front (PF), creating disparities in campaign access.[69][70] For instance, UPND was barred from holding district elections in Ndola on May 24, and Hichilema's radio appearances faced interference, including prevention of a broadcast in Mpika on May 13 and disruptions by PF activists in Isoka (May) and Chama (June).[69] These measures extended to enforcement during the pandemic, where police dispersed unauthorized assemblies under curfew and gathering limits, but selectively targeted opposition events, leading to claims of a chilling effect on political discourse. The first UPND rally approved in Lusaka since early 2020 occurred on November 14, after prolonged denials that limited voter outreach in key urban centers.[71] Human rights monitors documented over a dozen instances of police blocking opposition protests throughout the year, often without formal justification beyond notification requirements.[69] A notable escalation happened on December 23, when police summoned Hichilema to Lusaka headquarters, issuing a formal warning and caution for conspiracy to defraud under section 313 of the Penal Code, linked to an alleged irregular farm purchase.[72][73] During the ensuing dispersal of a supporter gathering outside his residence, officers fired live rounds and tear gas, killing two unarmed individualsâ€”identified as Vespers Shamoya and Emmanuel Bandaâ€”and injuring several others.[74] Hichilema described the shootings as targeted by snipers, while police claimed self-defense against stone-throwing crowds; an inquest was promised but yielded no convictions by year's end.[72] Such incidents fueled allegations of systemic voter suppression, with UPND reporting restricted access to over 20 constituencies and unequal media airtime, contributing to pre-election tensions documented in observer pre-poll assessments.[75] Independent analyses noted that these police interventions disproportionately affected opposition logistics, potentially disenfranchising supporters in opposition strongholds by limiting registration drives and visibility, though no comprehensive quantitative data on suppressed voters emerged from official audits.[69][70]

Presidency (2021â€“Present)

2021 Election Victory and Inauguration

The general election held on August 12, 2021, saw Hakainde Hichilema of the United Party for National Development (UPND) secure victory with 2,810,757 votes, equivalent to 59% of the valid ballots cast, against incumbent Edgar Lungu's 1,814,201 votes (38%) for the Patriotic Front (PF).[76][77] Voter turnout reached approximately 70%, the highest in Zambia's multiparty era, reflecting widespread participation amid economic discontent and pre-election tensions.[78] The Electoral Commission of Zambia (ECZ) declared the results on August 16, with international observers, including the Commonwealth and European Union missions, noting technical competence despite isolated reports of delays and bias allegations against state institutions favoring the PF.[79] Opposition campaigns, including Hichilema's, had raised concerns over PF-linked irregularities such as voter intimidation and media restrictions prior to polling day.[80] Lungu conceded defeat on August 16, facilitating a peaceful power transition and averting potential unrest seen in prior cycles.[81] Hichilema was sworn in as Zambia's seventh president on August 24, 2021, at Heroes Stadium in Lusaka, in a ceremony attended by regional leaders including Tanzania's Samia Suluhu Hassan and Malawi's Lazarus Chakwera.[82] In his inaugural address, Hichilema pledged a "new dawn" focused on economic recovery, democratic freedoms, and unity, emphasizing evidence-based governance over patronage.[83] Following the inauguration, Hichilema prioritized cabinet formation, appointing 24 ministers by early September 2021, with selections drawn from technocrats, private sector experts, and civil society figures rather than partisan loyalists, aiming for competence in addressing fiscal challenges.[84] This merit-oriented approach marked a departure from predecessors' cabinets, which observers noted often rewarded political alliances over expertise.[85]

Economic Policies and Debt Management

Upon assuming the presidency in August 2021, Hakainde Hichilema inherited a sovereign debt crisis, with Zambia in default since November 2020 and external debt totaling approximately $11 billion, including $3 billion in Eurobonds, amid a public debt stock exceeding 120% of GDP.[86][87] The prior administration's accumulation of unsustainable borrowing, compounded by fiscal mismanagement and external shocks like COVID-19, had depleted reserves and halted access to international capital markets.[88] To restore fiscal credibility, Hichilema's government negotiated an Extended Credit Facility (ECF) from the International Monetary Fund (IMF), approved on August 31, 2022, for SDR 978.2 million (about $1.3 billion) over 38 months, conditional on reforms to stabilize the macroeconomy and boost growth.[89] Key structural measures included phasing out fuel subsidies, which had become fiscally regressive and contributed to arrears, and liberalizing the foreign exchange regime by allowing the kwacha to float, ending central bank interventions that masked distortions.[90] These steps, while attracting foreign direct investment (FDI) inflows that rose 62.6% and surpassed $1 billion in recent years, initially triggered kwacha depreciation exceeding 50% and inflation peaking at 25.7% in late 2022.[91][92] Debt management progressed through restructurings under the G20 Common Framework: bilateral creditors agreed to reprofile $6.3 billion in March 2024, extending maturities and reducing interest burdens, while Eurobond holders finalized a $3 billion deal in June 2024, consolidating into longer-term instruments with contingent payments tied to economic performance.[93][94] By September 2025, approximately 92% of external debt had been restructured, enabling Zambia to service all 2025 obligations without default, though new commercial borrowing has drawn criticism for potentially offsetting sustainability gains.[4][95] Macroeconomic outcomes reflect partial stabilization: GDP growth averaged 5.2% from 2021 to 2024, rebounding from near-zero in 2021 to around 4% annually thereafter, driven by mining sector recovery and FDI in copper production.[96] Inflation eased from its post-reform peak to 12.6% by August 2025, supported by tighter monetary policy, though vulnerabilities persist, including drought-induced food price pressures.[97] Ongoing hurdles, such as stalled talks with Afreximbank over $50 million in obligations as of September 2025, risk delaying full exit from default status into 2026, underscoring uneven progress in comprehensive creditor coordination.[98][99]

Domestic Governance and Reforms

Upon assuming office in August 2021, Hichilema prioritized institutional reforms to restore rule of law and curb abuses prevalent under the prior Patriotic Front administration, including directives to dismantle patronage networks embedded in public institutions.[100] One early measure banned political party cadresâ€”informal enforcers often linked to violence and extortionâ€”from operating in markets, bus stations, and other public spaces, aiming to reduce thuggery and enhance civic order.[100] This addressed longstanding complaints of cadre intimidation, though enforcement has varied amid reports of residual incidents involving ruling United Party for National Development affiliates.[55] Hichilema's anti-corruption campaign targeted former officials, establishing an asset recovery team in December 2021 and launching the National Policy Against Corruption in May 2024, which emphasizes prosecution alongside forfeiture of illicit gains.[101] Legal amendments have expedited recoveries, with government reports citing returned stolen assets as evidence of progress, including streamlined processes for forfeiting properties linked to graft under the previous regime.[102] Prosecutions have proceeded against ex-ministers and officials for embezzlement, though critics note selective application and judicial bottlenecks persist, with the Anti-Corruption Commission facing internal challenges like the 2024 board dismissal amid misconduct allegations.[103] Media freedoms saw initial liberalization post-2021, with Reporters Without Borders documenting reduced harassment compared to the Lungu era's crackdowns, enabling broader opposition coverage and decriminalizing presidential defamation in 2022.[104][105] However, subsequent cyber laws and detentions of journalists at protests have tempered these gains, per Human Rights Watch assessments.[7] Social reforms included expanding free primary and secondary education in 2022, fulfilling a campaign pledge that boosted enrollment by removing fees for over 3 million pupils, with plans announced in October 2025 to enshrine it in law for permanence.[106][107] Healthcare allocation rose 13% in the 2026 budget to K26.2 billion (about $1.1 billion), funding 282 new facilities amid efforts to offset aid reductions through domestic prioritization.[108] Despite these, youth unemployment hovers around 25%, constraining reform impacts on job creation for the 15-24 cohort comprising a quarter of the labor force.[109]

Foreign Policy Orientation

Hakainde Hichilema's foreign policy has shifted toward pragmatic economic diplomacy, prioritizing private sector investment and debt resolution over ideological alignments.[110] Since assuming office in August 2021, his administration has focused on renegotiating Zambia's external debt burden, which stood at approximately $17 billion prior to his presidency, with a significant portion owed to Chinese creditors.[111] This approach seeks to diversify partnerships, reducing reliance on any single creditor nation.[112] In debt diplomacy, Hichilema secured a landmark agreement in June 2023 to restructure $6.3 billion in obligations with official creditors, including China, under the G20 Common Framework.[113] Bilateral talks with China continued, formalizing aspects of the deal during Hichilema's September 2023 visit to Beijing, where agreements on economic cooperation and investment were signed.[114] Concurrently, ties with Western partners strengthened; Hichilema pursued closer collaboration with the United States, emphasizing a policy reset in July 2025 amid evolving global dynamics, while advocating for coordinated creditor action to avoid standoffs that delay relief for developing nations.[115][116] Efforts extended to Japan, culminating in a February 2025 summit that advanced bilateral investment frameworks.[117] These moves reflect a strategy of balanced engagements to foster self-reliance, particularly as international aid landscapes shifted in 2025. Maintaining a non-aligned posture, Hichilema's policy underscores regional stability and multilateralism through active roles in the African Union (AU) and United Nations (UN).[118] Zambia under Hichilema has supported AU-led mediation and peacekeeping in conflicts threatening continental peace, including engagements on eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo tensions.[119] In August 2025, he participated in an extraordinary EAC-SADC virtual summit with regional leaders to address stability issues, reinforcing Zambia's commitment to dialogue over confrontation.[120] At the UN General Assembly in September 2025, Hichilema called for an "orderly, peaceful world" aligned with rules-based principles, arguing that conflicts divert resources from economic development and mutual prosperity.[121] This orientation prioritizes partnerships grounded in peace, stability, and shared respect, as reaffirmed in Zambia's international engagements throughout 2025.[122]

Crisis Responses

In response to the 2023â€“2024 cholera outbreak, which began in October 2023 and spread to nine provinces, resulting in over 10,887 cases and 432 deaths by January 2024, the Zambian government under President Hichilema intensified vaccination campaigns, improved water and sanitation infrastructure in affected districts, and enhanced case management protocols.[123] By April 2024, daily cases had declined to 38 from peaks exceeding hundreds, with the outbreak declared under control by mid-2024, yielding a case fatality rate of 1.3% amid 14,900 cumulative cases and 560 reported deaths nationwide.[124][125] These measures, including partnerships for oral cholera vaccine distribution, mitigated further escalation, though community deaths remained a challenge due to delayed reporting.[126] The 2024 drought, exacerbated by El NiÃ±o, prompted Hichilema to declare a national disaster on February 29, 2024, affecting agriculture across the country and leaving over 6.6 million people food insecure, with more than 1 million households impacted.[127][128] Emergency aid appeals sought nearly $1 billion in humanitarian support to address hunger for 9.8 million affected individuals, alongside initiatives like the Food Security and Drought Response Plan, which planned construction of 12 new dams, rehabilitation of 14, and maintenance of 385 to bolster irrigation and resilience.[129][130] Hydropower output fell sharply due to low reservoir levels, but alternatives such as solar and coal investments were accelerated to offset deficits, with no widespread famine mortality reported but elevated malnutrition risks noted.[131] Zambia's energy crisis, intensified by the drought's impact on hydropower dams like Kariba, led to blackouts lasting up to three days in some areas by mid-2024, disrupting mining and households with economic costs estimated in billions from lost productivity.[132][133] Hichilema's administration facilitated private sector investments in solar power and thermal alternatives, removing regulatory barriers as early as January 2023 to diversify from hydro dependency, which contrasted with prolonged state-led outages under prior regimes.[134] By late 2024, these efforts began yielding phased relief, including new solar projects, though full resolution remained ongoing amid fiscal strains from imported power.[135]

Performance Metrics and Public Reception

Zambia's GDP growth under President Hichilema reached an estimated 4% in 2024, driven by mining and services sectors, with projections for 5.8% in 2025 amid agricultural recovery and structural reforms.[136] [137] Inflation declined to 13.0% by August 2025 and further to 12.3% by October, reflecting tighter monetary policy and kwacha stabilization, though still elevated compared to pre-crisis norms.[138] [139] External debt restructuring agreements covered 94% of obligations by 2025, reducing annual service payments and freeing fiscal space for infrastructure, but the external public and publicly guaranteed debt stock rose to approximately US$21.4 billion by end-2024 due to accumulated arrears and new borrowing.[137] [4] [140] Public approval for Hichilema's administration started high following the 2021 electoral mandate for economic renewal and anti-corruption measures, but Afrobarometer surveys indicate a drop in satisfaction with democratic performance from 71% in 2022 to 54% in 2024, linked to ongoing economic hardships.[141] [142] Cost-of-living pressures, including high food prices and energy shortages, spurred protests in 2024 and calls for presidential addresses into 2025, highlighting unfulfilled campaign pledges on job creation amid persistent youth unemployment.[143] [144] [97] Opposition Patriotic Front (PF) leaders have criticized Hichilema's economic management as exacerbating inherited issues through policy missteps, claiming higher living costs and elite favoritism under UPND rule compared to prior stability.[145] UPND officials counter that macroeconomic stabilization, including debt relief and growth rebounds, demonstrates progress despite a severe legacy of default and fiscal imbalances from the PF era, attributing public discontent to global factors like droughts rather than governance failures.[146] [147] While 85% of Zambians continue to prefer democracy, the divergence in partisan assessments underscores debates over causal responsibility for persistent structural challenges.[141]

Controversies and Criticisms

Allegations of Authoritarianism

Since taking office in August 2021, President Hakainde Hichilema's administration has been accused of eroding democratic norms through targeted actions against political opponents, including arbitrary detentions and curbs on assembly rights. Human Rights Watch documented a marked increase in intolerance toward dissent and opposition in 2024, with authorities employing the Public Order Act to restrict political gatherings and detain critics.[7] Similar patterns persisted into 2025, as police routinely denied permits for opposition rallies on grounds of public security, a practice Freedom House described as systematic in Lusaka and other areas.[148] Prominent cases involve detentions of Patriotic Front (PF) figures under defamation provisions. In May 2024, PF Secretary General Raphael Nakacinda received an 18-month prison sentence for statements deemed defamatory toward Hichilema, despite the underlying law's repeal in 2022, raising alarms over retroactive enforcement and its chilling effect on expression.[149] The Lusaka High Court upheld this conviction on October 21, 2025, prompting Nakacinda's immediate incarceration and further outcry from rights monitors.[150] Amnesty International highlighted broader restrictions, noting arrests of opposition members and severe limits on peaceful assembly amid claims of selective prosecution.[151] Critics have also pointed to judicial maneuvers as evidence of power consolidation. In February 2023, Hichilema appointed 20 judges to key courts, including figures perceived as aligned with his United Party for National Development (UPND), echoing prior regime patterns of loyalty-based selections despite constitutional requirements for merit.[152] Tensions escalated in October 2024 when he dismissed three senior judgesâ€”previously suspended for alleged misconductâ€”who had ruled against him in Edgar Lungu's 2021 election challenge, actions the BBC reported as fueling perceptions of executive overreach into judicial independence.[153] Government officials counter that such measures enforce accountability and restore legal order undermined by the prior Patriotic Front administration's abuses, with detentions and bans framed as responses to verifiable threats rather than political vendettas.[154] However, empirical data from observers like Human Rights Watch indicate disproportionate enforcement against opposition actors, with fewer parallel actions against ruling party affiliates, suggesting potential selectivity over impartial rule of law.[7][155]

Economic Mismanagement Claims

Opposition figures and economic analysts have accused President Hakainde Hichilema's administration of exacerbating Zambia's debt burden through fiscal decisions that prioritize new borrowing over sustainable restructuring, pointing to the government's failure to fully offset inherited obligations with relief gains.[97] Upon taking office in 2021, Zambia faced a sovereign default from November 2020 under the prior Patriotic Front (PF) regime, with external debt reaching approximately $18.6 billion, up from $1.9 billion in 2011.[97] Critics argue that while 92% of external debt was restructured by September 2025, new loans and unresolved creditor disputes have undermined progress, leaving public debt projected at 90.7% of GDP by end-2025 despite International Monetary Fund (IMF)-backed reforms.[156] [157] A key flashpoint has been the ongoing impasse with the African Export-Import Bank (Afreximbank), which holds Zambian loans classified by Lusaka as commercial but contested for preferred-creditor treatment, delaying full restructuring into 2026 and prompting arbitration threats in September 2025.[98] [158] Opponents, including former PF officials, contend this reflects mismanagement in creditor negotiations, as Afreximbank's demands for superior terms mirror holdups seen in Ghana's process and risk inflating effective debt service costs amid Zambia's limited fiscal space.[159] Such delays, they claim, counteract the $6.3 billion bondholder deal of June 2023 and IMF validations, perpetuating vulnerability to external shocks like commodity price fluctuations.[160] Fuel subsidy removals under Hichilema's IMF-aligned policies have drawn sharp criticism for driving persistent inflation and eroding household purchasing power, despite campaign pledges to lower living costs.[161] Petrol prices nearly doubled from December 2021 to 2024, rising to around 21 Kwacha per litre by mid-term, while diesel followed suit, contributing to headline inflation that peaked above 20% early in the administration before easing to 9.8% by late 2022.[162] [161] Further hikes in September 2025â€”to 29.18 Kwacha per litre for petrolâ€”exacerbated transport sector strains and broader price pressures, with analysts attributing sustained inflation above single digits to these adjustments rather than solely global factors.[163] [164] These claims are tempered by evidence of the crisis's inherited severity, including an "empty treasury" and debt-to-GDP ratio exceeding 100% by 2020, which constrained immediate relief without external support.[88] [5] IMF approvals, such as the $1.3 billion extended credit facility in 2022 and subsequent disbursements totaling $184 million in July 2025, affirm macroeconomic stabilization efforts, with GDP growth projected at 6% for 2025 amid copper export gains and kwacha appreciation.[92] [165] However, public hardship metricsâ€”persistent cost-of-living complaints and opposition polling on electabilityâ€”highlight a disconnect between aggregate indicators and lived realities, where debt servicing absorbed over 30% of revenues through 2024.[166] [167]

Other Disputes

In 2018, prior to his presidency, Hichilema faced a land dispute in Namwala District where local villagers sought to reclaim over 3,500 hectares of communal land they claimed had been encroached upon by Hichilema and another businessman for farming purposes.[168] The conflict highlighted tensions between commercial agriculture and traditional land rights in rural Zambia, though resolution details remain limited in public records. Allegations of ethnic favoritism have been leveled against Hichilema's administration, particularly by opposition figures from the Patriotic Front, who claim disproportionate appointments of individuals from Hichilema's Tonga ethnic group to key government positions, including ministerial roles.[169] Critics argue this pattern undermines national unity and merit-based selection, with specific references to regional imbalances in cabinet composition as of early 2025.[170] Hichilema's supporters dismiss these as politically motivated smears from defeated rivals, emphasizing appointments based on competence rather than ethnicity. No independent empirical analysis has quantified such favoritism beyond anecdotal claims. Government transparency has drawn scrutiny following Transparency International Zambia's 2025 anti-corruption assessment, which scored the Hichilema administration 2.1 out of 5, citing persistent issues in procurement processes and public accountability despite promises of reform.[171] An IMF governance diagnostic from 2023 further identified vulnerabilities in oversight mechanisms, recommending enhanced audit implementation, though the government maintains that ongoing forensic audits of prior regimes demonstrate commitment to openness.[172] These findings contrast with official narratives of progress, fueling debates over the pace of institutional reforms.

Personal Life and Recognitions

Family and Private Interests

Hakainde Hichilema is married to Mutinta Hichilema, whom he wed in 1988.[173] The couple has three children: Chikonka, Miyanda, and Habwela.[174] Their daughter Miyanda married Meenda Milambo in May 2023.[175] Hichilema maintains an active involvement in ranching, owning four commercial ranches that make him Zambia's second-largest cattle producer and a major meat supplier.[19] He acquired the Mayoba Ranch in 1997, reflecting a 27-year commitment to livestock farming as of 2024.[176] This interest stems from his youth herding family cattle, which he has described as formative.[10] His private pursuits include reading and an appreciation for sports, which he has highlighted as avenues for personal development and national economic potential.[177][178] Hichilema identifies as Christian and participates in events like Zambia's National Day of Prayer, Fasting, Repentance, and Reconciliation.[179] Prior to entering politics, Hichilema engaged in philanthropy for over 15 years, focusing on education for underprivileged youth and community projects such as clinics through his business initiatives.[180]

Honours and Awards

In recognition of his contributions to business and leadership prior to entering politics, Hakainde Hichilema was noted for entrepreneurial success in Zambia's livestock and consultancy sectors, though formal international business awards from that period remain sparsely documented in verifiable records.[181] As President, Hichilema received an Honorary Doctor of the University (DUniv) from Heriot-Watt University on June 20, 2024, honoring his role in promoting economic development and education in Zambia.[181] On September 11, 2025, he was awarded the Energy Compact Award for advancing energy sector reforms, including initiatives to enhance power access and industrialisation through public-private partnerships.[182][183] Hichilema also received the VIP Global Water ChangeMaker Award at the United Nations Headquarters in New York, acknowledging efforts to address water resource management amid Zambia's drought challenges.[184] In October 2025, he was conferred the Lifetime Achievement Award at the Zambian Man of the Year Awards, the event's highest honour, citing his overall impact on national governance and economic recovery; critics have questioned the selection process given the organizing committee's ties to pro-government networks.[185][186]
