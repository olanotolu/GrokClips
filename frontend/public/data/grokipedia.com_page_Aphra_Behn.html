Aphra Behn


Aphra Behn (baptised 14 December 1640 – 16 April 1689) was an English Restoration-era dramatist, poet, translator, and prose writer, distinguished as the first woman in England to earn her livelihood solely through literary endeavors.[1][2]
Employed earlier as a spy for King Charles II under the code name Astrea, Behn gathered intelligence in Antwerp during the Second Anglo-Dutch War, leveraging her social connections to extract information from Dutch informants like the regicide William Scot.[2][3]
A committed Royalist, she produced over a dozen plays, including the popular comedy The Rover, alongside poetry, translations from French and Latin, and the influential novella Oroonoko, which recounted the enslavement and execution of an African prince based on her experiences in Surinam.[4][5]
Behn's works, often bold in exploring sexuality and power, faced posthumous criticism for moral looseness amid a male-dominated literary scene, yet her productivity and commercial success established her as a pivotal figure in English literature's transition to professional authorship.[6][7]

Early Life and Background

Origins and Family

Aphra Behn's birth and parentage are documented through conflicting parish records and early biographical accounts, with scholarly consensus leaning toward her baptism as Eaffry (variously spelled Eafry or Aphra) Johnson on 14 December 1640 in Harbledown, a parish immediately outside Canterbury in Kent, England.[8][9] Her parents were Bartholomew Johnson, a barber whose profession likely encompassed minor surgical duties typical of the era, and Elizabeth Denham, who worked as a wet nurse.[9][10] This baptism places her as the second daughter in a modest household, with an older sister and a brother also recorded in family registers.[10] An earlier narrative, propagated in 18th-century biographies like Charles Gildon's Life and Memoirs of Mrs. Behn (1696), asserts that she was born Aphara Amis and baptized on 10 July 1640 in Wye, approximately 20 miles southeast of Canterbury, with her father portrayed as a man of gentrified status appointed to a lieutenant-governorship in Surinam.[11] This account, however, lacks corroborating primary evidence and has been critiqued by historians as a probable conflation or invention, possibly derived from misread parish entries or embellished to elevate her origins amid posthumous scrutiny of her unconventional life.[12] The Harbledown record aligns more closely with verifiable genealogical traces, including family connections to Canterbury's artisan class, though no definitive proof excludes a London birth amid the era's mobility.[12][8] Behn's early family milieu unfolded against the backdrop of escalating English Civil War tensions, with Kent's divided allegiances—Parliamentarian strongholds in Canterbury juxtaposed against rural Royalist pockets—shaping local dynamics, though specific parental political stances remain undocumented in surviving records.[9] The Johnsons' artisan status positioned them amid Puritan-leaning urban influences, yet Behn's later staunch Royalism suggests possible latent family inclinations toward the Stuart cause, emergent in the pre-war 1640s as royal prerogatives clashed with parliamentary reforms.[10] Elizabeth Denham's role as a wet nurse may have afforded modest social ties, potentially exposing the household to varied patrons, but no evidence indicates elevation beyond working-class roots.[9]

Possible Surinam Expedition

Behn's Oroonoko (1688) includes detailed depictions of Surinam's geography, such as the Parham House estate and the habits of indigenous groups like the "Indians," alongside observations of enslaved Africans, which the narrator presents as derived from personal experience during the English colonial period from 1650 to 1667.[13] These elements imply a visit in the mid-1660s, likely 1663–1664, when Behn would have been in her early twenties, exposing her to the colony's plantation economy, native trade in "rarities," and interracial dynamics under Governor William Willoughby.[14] The work's preface and narrative voice reinforce this by claiming the author's familiarity with local figures and events, including the transport of enslaved people from West Africa, positioning Oroonoko as a "true history" informed by direct witness.[15] Potential familial ties bolster the case for travel; Behn's father, Bartholomew Johnson, received a royal commission in 1663 as lieutenant-general to the governor of Surinam but died during the voyage, with some accounts suggesting Behn traveled with relatives connected to the administration, possibly through alliances with Willoughby's household or figures like John Harman, a colonial associate.[16] Such links align with the era's patronage networks, where young women of middling gentry background occasionally joined colonial ventures for economic or social advancement, though no surviving letters or manifests name Behn explicitly among passengers.[17] Scholarly consensus remains divided, with early biographers accepting the trip based on Oroonoko's specificity, yet lacking corroborative primary evidence—such as shipping logs, parish records, or Willoughby correspondence—has fueled doubt since Ernest Bernbaum's 1912–1920 analysis, which posited Behn fabricated details from published accounts like those of John Huygen van Linschoten or Thomas Tryon rather than firsthand observation.[18] Recent examinations highlight inconsistencies, including anachronistic elements in the narrative and Behn's silence on the journey in her poetry or dedications, suggesting possible embellishment for literary authority in a male-dominated publishing sphere; while descriptive accuracy supports exposure to Surinam materials, causal inference from texts alone risks overattribution absent archival confirmation.[19] This evidentiary gap underscores broader challenges in Behn biography, where self-authored claims often substitute for verifiable records, potentially amplified by posthumous hagiography in 18th- and 19th-century editions.[16]

Marriage and Early Adulthood

Behn married Johan Behn, a merchant of German or Dutch origin, circa 1663, a union that facilitated her access to the court circles of the newly restored monarchy under Charles II.[20] The marriage ended abruptly with her widowhood, possibly by 1665 or 1666, though surviving records provide scant details on her husband or the cause of his death or disappearance.[21][22] In early adulthood, Behn resided in London during the Restoration's effervescent yet precarious social milieu, marked by the court's libertine ethos of pleasure-seeking and skepticism toward Puritan legacies following the Commonwealth's collapse in 1660. This environment, with its emphasis on monarchical splendor amid recent civil strife, aligned with and likely reinforced her preexisting royalist sympathies, rooted in familial ties to the crown's supporters. Widowhood brought financial strains common to unpropertied women of the era, where legal independence coexisted with economic vulnerability absent substantial inheritance or family provision.[23][24]

Espionage Activities

Recruitment by Charles II

In 1666, amid the Second Anglo-Dutch War, Aphra Behn was recruited into English intelligence service by agents of King Charles II's administration, marking her entry as one of the few documented female spies of the Restoration era.[2][3] Her royalist loyalties, forged in the aftermath of the English Civil War and the monarchy's restoration in 1660, motivated this service; as a supporter of the Stuart crown, Behn sought to aid the king against Dutch naval threats and exiled republican plotters.[25] Court connections, possibly facilitated by figures like Thomas Killigrew, the Master of the Revels, elevated her from obscurity to this role, leveraging her presumed familiarity with continental networks and languages acquired through prior travels or associations.[26] Assigned the codename "Astrea" (or Agent 160 in state papers), Behn received initial funding of £50 to support her operations abroad, with assurances of reimbursement for expenses and compensation for intelligence yielded—standard though often unreliable promises in the ad hoc espionage practices of Charles II's court, which relied on personal networks rather than formalized agencies.[3][2] Primary evidence for her commissioning survives in 19 letters preserved in The National Archives (State Papers Foreign, Flanders), where she corresponds with spymaster Henry Bennet, Lord Arlington, detailing her readiness and pleading for prompt payment to avoid personal ruin.[2] These documents confirm her direct ties to the royal intelligence apparatus, underscoring the empirical basis for her involvement despite the era's sparse records on non-elite actors. Restoration spying under Charles II typically involved courtiers and opportunists motivated by patriotism, financial incentive, or access to informants, with women occasionally employed for their perceived invisibility in male-dominated diplomatic circles.[2] Behn's recruitment exemplifies this, prioritizing her ideological alignment and relational leverage over formal training, as Dutch shipping raids on English trade necessitated rapid intelligence gathering from Antwerp, a hub of enemy activity.[25] While later payment disputes highlight the precariousness of such service—Behn incurred debts awaiting reimbursement—the initial commission reflects the crown's pragmatic use of loyal subjects in a conflict where naval supremacy hinged on covert insights into Dutch fleet movements and alliances.[3]

Antwerp Mission and Outcomes

In July 1666, during the Second Anglo-Dutch War, Aphra Behn was dispatched from England to Antwerp, a neutral port city facilitating cross-border intelligence exchanges, under the code name "Astrea." Her primary assignment, authorized by King Charles II's secretary of state Henry Bennet (Lord Arlington), was to contact William Scot, son of the executed regicide Thomas Scot and a former associate from Behn's possible time in Surinam, who had relocated to the Netherlands. Behn aimed to extract confessions or actionable intelligence from Scot regarding Dutch naval plans and regicide networks, offering him bribes, a royal pardon, and financial incentives to defect as a double agent. She employed rudimentary ciphers in her correspondence, including numeric codes for sensitive details, and navigated personal risks such as interception by Dutch authorities or betrayal by contacts.[2][3][27] Behn's operations yielded partial successes amid mounting frustrations. Upon arrival, she quickly established contact with Scot during a clandestine meeting, securing initial intelligence on Dutch fleet movements that reportedly informed English defenses against Admiral Michiel de Ruyter's raids, though the value of this data remains debated due to its limited scope and Scot's reluctance to provide deeper regicide confessions without guaranteed immunity. Scot dispatched several coded letters to Behn, which she forwarded to London via couriers, but he resisted full commitment, demanding £100 upfront and explicit royal assurances that proved politically untenable given his regicide lineage. By August 1666, the mission faltered as Scot grew evasive, possibly suspecting entrapment, and Behn's pleas for operational funds went unanswered, forcing her to self-finance bribes and lodgings amid Antwerp's rising tensions.[2][28][27] The mission's ultimate failure, coinciding with the war's Dutch victories and the Treaty of Breda in July 1667, left Behn in financial ruin. Stranded without reimbursement, she borrowed approximately £150 from merchant Edward Butler to cover debts and return to England, only to face arrest upon arrival for non-repayment. Imprisoned in London's debtors' gaol around 1667–1668, Behn petitioned the crown repeatedly for back pay, receiving partial funds after royal intervention but no full compensation, as evidenced by surviving State Papers documenting her "Memorialls for Mrs Affora." This ordeal, exacerbating her poverty, directly prompted her pivot to professional writing as a means of self-support, marking the end of her espionage career.[2][3][27]

Literary Career

Entry into Playwriting

Following her return from espionage duties in Antwerp, where she incurred significant debts due to unpaid royal reimbursements and the costs of her covert operations, Aphra Behn faced imminent financial ruin, including potential imprisonment for debt.[3][29] With no further spy assignments forthcoming and urgent need for income, Behn pivoted to professional writing, vowing in her own words to "starve before I stoop" to other means, marking a pragmatic shift to the theater as a viable livelihood in Restoration London.[6][3] Behn's theatrical debut came with The Forc'd Marriage, or the Jealous Bridegroom, a tragicomedy staged at the Duke's Theatre in September 1670, which ran for six nights and established her as one of the earliest women to have a play professionally produced on the English stage.[30][31] This initial success, yielding earnings from benefit shares typical of Restoration playwrights—such as proceeds from designated performance days—enabled her to forgo patronage dependency and sustain herself independently, a feat that positioned her as the first Englishwoman to earn a living solely through literary output.[32][33] Emboldened, Behn rapidly followed with The Amorous Prince, or the Curious Husband in early 1671, demonstrating her adept navigation of the male-dominated playhouse system, where she secured production slots amid competition from established dramatists like John Dryden.[34] Her prologues and epilogues, often laced with appeals to audiences' novelty interest in a female author, further capitalized on her gender as a commercial draw, allowing her to accrue theater profits without formal guild membership barred to women.[35][30]

Prolific Output and Financial Independence

Behn authored and saw staged at least eighteen plays between 1670 and 1687, primarily for London theaters such as the Duke's Company and the King's Company, in addition to publishing prose narratives like Oroonoko (1688) and volumes of poetry.[36][37] Her dramatic output intensified during the Exclusion Crisis (1679–1681), when she composed pro-Stuart works such as The Feigned Courtesans (1679) and The Rover, Part II (1681) that aligned with Tory efforts to counter Whig agitation against James, Duke of York's succession.[38] These pieces contributed to her reputation as a partisan voice, with performances drawing audiences amid political fervor.[39] In prefaces and dedications, Behn vigorously defended her intellectual property, protesting unauthorized adaptations and plagiarism by rivals, as in her epistle to The Dutch Lover (1673) where she condemned theatrical thefts common in the era's collaborative playhouses.[40] She dedicated works to patrons like James II's court figures to secure benefits while asserting her professional stake, marking an early claim to authorship amid a market where plays were commodities sold for performance rights and printed editions.[40] Financially, Behn transitioned to writing as her primary income after espionage debts left her imprisoned around 1668, with no royal reimbursement forthcoming from Charles II.[41] Earnings from playhouse shares—typically £30–50 per new production plus benefit nights—and printed sales enabled self-sufficiency, a rarity for women barred from most trades; she reportedly lived modestly but independently in London, subsidizing her output despite recurrent debts and declining health from ailments like arthritis in her final years (1680s).[32][42] This market-driven autonomy stemmed from Restoration theater's commercial boom, where her productivity—averaging multiple works annually—outpaced many male contemporaries, though vulnerabilities like unpaid royal promises persisted.[29]

Major Works

Dramatic Works

Behn's entry into professional playwriting occurred with The Forced Marriage, or the Jealous Bridegroom, a tragicomedy performed at Lincoln's Inn Fields in September 1670, marking her debut on the Restoration stage and establishing themes of romantic intrigue and marital conflict.[10] This was followed by The Amorous Prince, or The Curious Husband in 1671, another tragicomedy exploring courtly love and deception, and The Dutch Lover in 1673, a romance complicated by political conspiracy, though contemporary reception noted its convoluted plot.[10] These early works demonstrated Behn's adaptation of continental influences and her focus on libertine elements within aristocratic settings, often aligning with royalist sympathies through portrayals of honorable cavaliers. Her most enduring dramatic success came with The Rover, or The Banish'd Cavaliers, premiered at the Duke's Theatre on March 24, 1677, an adaptation of Thomas Killigrew's unpublished Thomaso, or The Wanderer (1654), which celebrated exiled royalists during the Interregnum through Carnival intrigue in Naples, blending comedy, disguise, and amorous pursuits to defend monarchical loyalty.[43] The play's commercial triumph—evidenced by multiple revivals and a sequel, The Rover Part II in 1681—secured Behn's financial viability as a dramatist, with its witty dialogue and pro-Cavalier ethos resonating amid post-Popish Plot tensions.[44] In the same year, Abdelazer, or The Moor's Revenge, a tragedy drawing from Lust's Dominion, introduced Moorish villainy and revenge motifs, performed during summer at the Duke's Theatre.[10] Behn's political engagement intensified in Sir Patient Fancy, staged January 1678 at the Duke's Theatre, her first explicitly partisan comedy satirizing Whig merchants and Dissenters through the hypochondriac title character, a republican-leaning alderman cuckolded in favor of royalist gallants, thereby glorifying Stuart absolutism against exclusionist threats.[38] [24] Subsequent plays like The Town Fop (1676, revised from Brome's The Novella) and The Feigned Courtesans (1679) innovated Restoration comedy by amplifying female agency in intrigue while critiquing puritanical restraint, with empirical popularity inferred from dedicatory prefaces boasting full houses and actor endorsements.[10] Later efforts, including the tragi-comedy The Young King (published 1683, likely earlier composition) and The Lucky Chance (1687), experimented with hybrid forms to reconcile tragic reversals and comic resolutions, supporting Tory causes through noble heroism amid factional strife.[45] Across her approximately 16 to 19 plays, produced primarily at the Duke's and King's Theatres until 1687, Behn innovated by infusing comedy of manners with royalist propaganda and proto-feminist wit, achieving variable but notable box-office reception—The Rover's sequels and revivals contrast with flops like The Dutch Lover's hisses—while prioritizing empirical fidelity to courtly causation over moral didacticism.[44] [10]

Prose Narratives

Aphra Behn's prose narratives, published mainly in the mid-to-late 1680s, represent early English experiments in the novella and novel forms, blending romance, adventure, and autobiographical elements with influences from French nouvelles and heroic tales.[46] Her works often feature exotic locales, noble protagonists, and intricate plots of love and betrayal, reflecting the era's fascination with continental fiction while incorporating purportedly eyewitness details from her travels.[47] Behn's earliest significant prose work, Love-Letters Between a Nobleman and His Sister, appeared in three parts from 1684 to 1687, drawing on the real-life scandal involving Lord Grey and his sister-in-law, presented in epistolary form to mimic intimate correspondence.[48] This narrative explores themes of illicit passion and political intrigue amid the Monmouth Rebellion, showcasing Behn's skill in adapting contemporary events into serialized fiction for commercial appeal.[47] In 1688, Behn produced a cluster of novellas, including The Fair Jilt; or, The History of Prince Tarquin and Miranda, the first to feature her name on the title page, which recounts the amorous deceptions of Miranda in a convent setting leading to adventures in Antwerp and eventual redemption through marriage.[49] Similarly, Agnes de Castro; or, The Force of Generous Love dramatizes a tale of jealous rivalry and tragic loyalty among Portuguese nobility, emphasizing heroic constancy amid betrayal.[47] These stories fuse moral instruction with sensational elements, prioritizing narrative momentum over didacticism. Oroonoko: or, The Royal Slave, also published in 1688, stands as Behn's most enduring prose contribution, a proto-novel subtitled a "true history" based on her claimed encounters in Surinam during the 1660s.[50] The titular prince, captured and enslaved after being deceived by a British trader, endures betrayal, forced labor, and mutilation before leading a failed rebellion and suicide alongside his wife Imoinda, portrayed with noble dignity contrasting the colonists' perfidy.[51] While highlighting the cruelties of plantation slavery—including whippings, branding, and family separations—the account integrates these into a romance of exotic valor and personal betrayal, without advocating institutional reform, consistent with Behn's royalist acceptance of colonial expansion.[52]

Poetry and Translations

Aphra Behn published several volumes of verse during the 1680s, reflecting the Restoration era's libertine sensibilities alongside political and amatory themes. Her Poems upon Several Occasions: With a Voyage to the Island of Love (1684) included original lyrics on love, betrayal, and female agency, such as "The Disappointment," which explored impotence and desire from a woman's perspective in a mock-pastoral mode.[6] These works circulated in manuscript and print miscellanies, evidencing Behn's integration into the burgeoning commercial poetry market, where authors increasingly relied on sales rather than exclusive patronage.[53] Lycidus; or, The Lover in Fashion (1688) comprised a sequence of pastoral eclogues and songs centered on unrequited passion and social intrigue, drawing from French models while infusing English courtly politics. Themes of erotic pursuit and libertine excess predominated, as in the titular character's futile wooing, mirroring the era's fascination with refined sensuality amid political instability. Behn also composed dedicatory and panegyric verses for royalist patrons, including odes to James II upon his accession in 1685 and tributes to figures like the Earl of Rochester, demonstrating her adaptability to neoclassical forms and Tory allegiance.[54] Such poems, often appended to collections, underscored her strategic use of verse for networking in a patronage-driven literary economy.[55] Behn's translations from French amplified her output, introducing continental libertine and scientific discourse to English readers. Her rendition of Paul Tallemant des Réaux's Le Voyage de l'Isle d'Amour (1663) as The Voyage to the Island of Love (1684), embedded in her poetry volume, rendered verse narratives of idealized romance and seduction, adapting the original's erotic escapism to suit Restoration tastes. Later, she translated Bernard le Bovier de Fontenelle's Entretiens sur la pluralité des mondes (1686) as A Discovery of New Worlds (1688), a dialogue on Cartesian cosmology positing infinite inhabited worlds, which she framed in her preface as accessible philosophy rather than esoteric speculation. This work bridged empirical astronomy with literary dialogue, reflecting Behn's interest in mechanistic explanations of nature amid the era's scientific ferment.[56] Additional efforts included versions of François de La Rochefoucauld's Maxims (1685), emphasizing cynical observations on human motive. These translations, produced rapidly from 1683 onward, highlight Behn's linguistic facility and role in disseminating French intellectual currents, though her adaptations occasionally prioritized rhetorical flair over literal fidelity.

Political Views and Personal Conduct

Royalist and Tory Allegiances

Aphra Behn's espionage for Charles II in Antwerp beginning in July 1666 exemplified her early and unwavering commitment to the Stuart monarchy, as she reported on Dutch political intentions amid the Second Anglo-Dutch War, enduring financial hardship without full reimbursement despite repeated petitions to the king.[42] This service reflected a personal stake in royal restoration, forged in the instability following the English Civil War, where republican experiments had disrupted social order and property rights, a trauma Behn invoked in her writings to justify monarchical absolutism over parliamentary encroachments.[57] During the Popish Plot hysteria of 1678–1681 and the ensuing Exclusion Crisis, Behn's dramatic output served as overt Tory advocacy, countering Whig agitation for excluding James, Duke of York, from succession by portraying dissenters and republicans as threats to stability. In The Roundheads, or, The Good Old Cause (performed 1681, published 1682), she satirized Puritan parliamentarians as hypocritical opportunists, deploying the Tory slogan "forty-one all over again" to equate Exclusionist plots with the Civil War's onset in 1641, thereby defending hereditary rule against elective republicanism.[58] Similarly, The City Heiress (performed 1682) contrasted impoverished royalist cavaliers with scheming Whig merchants who profited under Cromwell, reinforcing narratives of moral and economic continuity under Stuart governance.[57] These works prioritized causal continuity from past upheavals, arguing that unchecked factionalism inevitably led to anarchy, rather than abstract egalitarian ideals promoted by Whig polemicists. Behn's poetic dedications and panegyrics further entrenched her as a Stuart apologist, including odes celebrating Charles II's reign and James II's 1685 accession, such as her Pindaric Poem mourning Charles's death while prophesying James's legitimacy.[59] Even after James's 1688 deposition, she refused overtures from the Williamite regime, composing defiant verses like A Pindaric to the Reverend Doctor Burnet (1689) that lambasted Whig betrayal and upheld divine-right monarchy, a stance rooted in empirical recollection of interregnum confiscations rather than opportunistic adaptation.[60] This fidelity persisted amid shifting literary allegiances, underscoring her views as pragmatic responses to historical precedents of factional violence, not detached philosophical abstraction.[54]

Libertine Themes and Sexuality

Behn's dramatic works frequently incorporated libertine motifs, portraying characters who pursued unbridled sexual gratification as a form of rebellion against Puritan restraint, reflective of the hedonistic excesses prevalent in the Restoration court under Charles II.[61] In The Rover (performed 1677), set during Carnival in Naples, protagonists like the rake Willmore engage in serial seduction and infidelity, endorsing male libertine agency while exposing its risks to women, such as deception and abandonment, thereby critiquing the double standards of courtly libertinism.[62] This duality—celebrating erotic freedom yet highlighting its predatory underside—aligned with the era's philosophical currents, influenced by figures like the Earl of Rochester, whose verse Behn emulated to explore desire's material and rhetorical dimensions.[63] Her poetry extended this frankness into personal erotic address, often blurring gender boundaries in ways that evoked bisexual undertones within a libertine framework unconstrained by modern identity categories. In "The Disappointment" (published 1684), Behn depicted male impotence during an attempted ravishment, using explicit anatomical detail to subvert heroic expectations of conquest and underscore the body's unreliability in carnal pursuits.[64] Similarly, "To the Fair Clarinda, Who Made Love to Me, Imagin'd More than Woman" (1688) addressed a female figure with layered desire—combining platonic admiration, sisterly affection, and erotic tension—positioning Clarinda as both "fair" object and ambiguous partner, a trope echoing classical models but adapted to Restoration libertine experimentation with same-sex intrigue.[6] Such verses, dedicated to patrons immersed in courtly vice, presented sexuality as a sensual ethos rather than moral taboo, yet invited scrutiny for prioritizing libertine critique over conventional piety.[53] These portrayals drew sharp rebukes from moral conservatives, who viewed Behn's emphasis on carnality as corrosive to public virtue, associating her with the playhouse's role in disseminating indecency. Puritan holdovers and post-Restoration reformers condemned her output for fostering libertine excess, with critics decrying female dramatists like Behn for crafting narratives that normalized seduction and infidelity, thereby undermining marital norms.[65] By the 1690s, amid campaigns against stage obscenity led by figures such as Jeremy Collier, Behn's works were targeted for their "lascivious" content, exemplifying broader assaults on Restoration comedy's endorsement of sexual license as a threat to social order.[66] This backlash, rooted in empirical observations of theater's influence on audiences, contrasted with Behn's self-presentation as a professional innovator unbound by decorum, though it underscored causal links between her thematic boldness and enduring notoriety as "the loose Astraea."[53]

Death and Contemporaneous Reception

Final Years and Demise

In the latter part of the 1680s, Aphra Behn grappled with declining health and persistent financial hardship, including debts that necessitated reliance on aristocratic patrons for support, yet she maintained a rigorous pace of literary production to sustain herself.[67][68] Her ailments, compounded by poverty, did not halt her work; she composed her final play, The Younger Brother, or, The Amorous Jilt, a comedy that was staged at the Theatre Royal and published in 1696, seven years after her death, following completion and edits by associates such as Charles Gildon.[69][70] Behn succumbed on April 16, 1689, reportedly due to complications from inadequate medical care during her final illness.[71] She was interred in the East Cloister of Westminster Abbey, a rare honor for a female writer of her era, with her gravestone inscribed: "Here lies a Proof that Wit can never be / Defence enough against Mortality."[72][73] This epitaph, emphasizing the limits of intellectual prowess in the face of death, was funded through subscriptions from admirers, reflecting her lack of substantial estate or property at the time of her passing.[67]

Immediate Critical Responses

Behn's plays enjoyed theatrical success in the Restoration period, with works like The Rover (1677) drawing audiences for their comedic intrigue and libertine themes, indicative of appreciation among theatergoers and fellow dramatists aligned with courtly tastes.[6] Royalist supporters acclaimed her propagandist comedies and poetry for advancing Tory causes against Whig exclusionists, viewing her as a defender of monarchical legitimacy during the 1680s crises.[54] [4] Critics, however, assailed her for immorality, decrying the bawdiness in her dramas as emblematic of stage profaneness that corrupted public morals.[74] Satires portrayed her personally as a "monster of immorality," linking her gender to perceived indecency and lack of scholarly credentials.[74] [75] Whig-leaning detractors expressed disdain for her partisan content, contrasting sharply with Royalist endorsement and fueling broader attacks on her invasion of male literary domains.[76] Behn countered such vitriol in her prefaces and epilogues, asserting that dramatic merit derived from wit and observation rather than formal education or gender, and that her critics envied her success out of pedantry.[77] In the Epistle to the Reader for The Dutch Lover (1673), she argued women were unjustly barred from learning, implying her plays' flaws stemmed from systemic exclusion rather than innate inferiority.[78] By the preface to The Lucky Chance (1687), she defiantly prioritized perfected drama over idleness, dismissing "envious trifles" from detractors who undervalued women's contributions.[77] Posthumously in 1698, Jeremy Collier's A Short View of the Immorality and Profaneness of the English Stage indicted Restoration comedy, including Behn's, for profane language and immoral exemplars that undermined virtue, though he focused more on living playwrights; this reflected ongoing moralist scrutiny of her oeuvre's libertine elements.[79] [80]

Posthumous Legacy and Reappraisal

Period of Obscurity

After Aphra Behn's death in 1689, her literary standing diminished markedly during the 18th and 19th centuries amid shifting cultural and moral sensibilities that increasingly condemned the perceived indecency of Restoration-era writings. Critics and anthologists, influenced by rising standards of propriety, castigated her works for their frank depictions of sexuality and libertine themes, associating them with a licentious past deemed incompatible with emerging ideals of decorum.[81] This moral reevaluation, exacerbated by anti-Restoration backlash following Jeremy Collier's 1698 Short View of the Profaneness and Immorality of the English Stage, portrayed Behn's comedies as emblematic of theatrical excess, leading to their effective sidelining in favor of sentimental genres emphasizing virtue and emotional refinement.[82] Prominent moralists amplified this disdain, with Alexander Pope lampooning Behn as "Astrea" in The Dunciad (1728), deriding her poetic style and implying promiscuity through lines critiquing her self-indulgent stage presence.[78] Similarly, figures like Richard Steele and John Duncombe echoed condemnations of her as scandalous, reinforcing her image as a morally lax female author unfit for serious consideration.[81] Gender biases compounded the exclusion, as Behn faced disproportionate scrutiny for "unwomanly" boldness, barring her from literary canons that privileged male-authored classics and sidelined women writers perceived as transgressive.[4] Literary histories often reduced her to a byword for immorality, with subsequent writers repeating unsubstantiated charges of indecency without engaging her texts, perpetuating a cycle of neglect.[83] Reprints of her works were infrequent and partial, reflecting this eclipse; while individual plays like The Rover saw occasional editions and her poems appeared in some early 18th-century anthologies, comprehensive collections waned after initial posthumous volumes around 1702–1716, with no major scholarly edition until Montague Summers's in 1915.[81][84] In the 19th century, a brief revival via W. C. Hazlitt's and A. H. Bullen's partial reprints gave way to further obscurity, as Victorian sensibilities aligned against her unapologetic eroticism and political edge, ensuring her marginalization until modern reappraisals.[84]

Twentieth-Century Revival

Interest in Aphra Behn's literary output revived in the early twentieth century, sparked by Virginia Woolf's 1929 essay A Room of One's Own, in which Woolf described Behn as "the first English woman to have earned her living by her wits" and a foundational figure whose professional success defied patriarchal constraints on female authorship.[85] Woolf argued that Behn's example paved the way for subsequent women writers by demonstrating the viability of literary labor independent of marriage or patronage.[86] This early recognition gained momentum through Montague Summers' six-volume edition of The Works of Aphra Behn (1915–1916), which compiled and annotated her plays, poetry, and prose, aiming to rehabilitate her from eighteenth- and nineteenth-century obscurity amid moral critiques of her libertine themes.[87] Scholarly attention intensified in the 1970s and 1980s, driven by second-wave feminist criticism that positioned Behn as a proto-feminist pioneer—the first professional woman writer in English literature—and emphasized her challenges to gender norms in works like The Rover (1677). Biographies such as Angeline Goreau's Reconstructing Aphra (1980) reconstructed her life and oeuvre through archival evidence, while Janet Todd's multi-volume The Works of Aphra Behn (1992–1996) provided a scholarly baseline for textual analysis, incorporating previously overlooked materials.[88][87] Following the 1980s, publications on Behn proliferated, reflecting a broader academic turn toward early modern women writers, though this surge was qualitatively marked rather than quantified in bibliometric studies; for example, monographs and journal articles multiplied, addressing her dramatic innovations and narrative techniques. However, later scholarship tempered the initial feminist emphasis by re-examining Behn's staunch Tory and royalist politics, which aligned her with hierarchical absolutism rather than egalitarian ideals, rendering projections of modern feminism anachronistic given her endorsements of monarchical authority and critiques of Whig republicanism.[38][89] These analyses highlight tensions between her gender critiques and conservative worldview, where libertine elements served ideological rather than subversive ends, cautioning against ideologically driven reinterpretations that overlook her partisan commitments.[90]

Contemporary Scholarship and Debates

Scholars continue to debate the authenticity of biographical details surrounding Aphra Behn, particularly the reliability of early accounts that blend her personal experiences with fabricated elements, as evidenced by analyses of her self-presentation in works like Oroonoko (1688), where purported eyewitness claims have been scrutinized for embellishment to enhance narrative authority.[91] These disputes highlight how scant primary documentation—limited to letters from her Antwerp espionage mission in 1666—fuels speculation, with critics arguing that over-reliance on anecdotal traditions has distorted interpretations of her career, potentially undervaluing her literary innovations by prioritizing unverified personal lore. [92] Recent scholarship emphasizes the causal impact of Behn's espionage activities on her prose realism, positing that her 1666 intelligence-gathering in Antwerp honed observational acuity evident in the empirical detail of Oroonoko, where descriptions of Surinamese settings reflect firsthand colonial reconnaissance rather than mere invention.[17] A 2024 study traces this influence to her covert networks, arguing that the duplicity required in spying fostered a narrative style blending factual reportage with strategic deception, distinct from romantic fabulation in contemporaries like Delarivier Manley.[17] Similarly, examinations of her potential Surinamese spying role underscore how such experiences grounded her abolitionist undertones in Oroonoko, prioritizing causal chains of enslavement over abstract moralism.[93] Conservative critiques challenge anachronistic alignments of Behn with modern egalitarian feminism, asserting that her staunch royalism—evident in pro-Stuart propaganda plays like The Roundheads (1674)—prioritized hierarchical loyalty over gender equity, rendering her advocacy for women's economic independence compatible with Tory absolutism but incompatible with anti-monarchical progressivism.[57] These analyses contend that feminist readings overlook how Behn's defense of female agency often served to reinforce elite royalist virtues, not dismantle patriarchal structures, as her works critique Whig hypocrisy without endorsing universal suffrage or meritocracy.[94] On libertinism, scholars argue it functions in Behn's oeuvre as a depiction of aristocratic excess rather than proto-liberatory sexuality, with plays like The Rover (1677) satirizing male rakishness as a symptom of post-Restoration moral decay among the privileged, not a model for female emancipation, thereby critiquing elite vice through gendered power imbalances.[95] [96] This perspective counters prevailing academic tendencies to romanticize her libertine themes, emphasizing instead their rootedness in Restoration class dynamics and causal links to political instability.[97]

Controversies and Critiques

Biographical Uncertainties

The parentage and precise origins of Aphra Behn are disputed owing to the paucity of contemporaneous records, with early biographers offering conflicting accounts lacking primary corroboration. One tradition holds that she was baptized as Eaffrey Johnson on 14 December 1640 in Harbledown, Kent, daughter of Bartholomew Johnson, a barber-surgeon, and his wife Elizabeth, potentially connected to colonial administrator Francis Willoughby.[98] [6] An alternative narrative, drawn from anecdotal reports, identifies her as Aphra Amis, daughter of a Canterbury barber named John Amis and his wife Amy, though this rests on unverified oral traditions rather than documents.[99] These discrepancies highlight the reliance on posthumous speculation, as no definitive family correspondence or legal papers survive to resolve them.[98] Behn's purported journey to Surinam in the mid-1660s, referenced in her narrative Oroonoko (1688), similarly lacks robust evidentiary support beyond her own assertions. Claims of accompanying her father—variously described as appointed lieutenant-general or lost at sea en route—find no backing in colonial dispatches or shipping manifests, casting doubt on the expedition's occurrence for her personally.[100] While some independent colonial records mention Willoughby's administration, they do not link a Johnson or Amis family member to the voyage, suggesting the account may blend personal fabrication with observed colonial practices.[100] Historians emphasize that such self-reported travels, absent archival traces, warrant skepticism, particularly given Behn's later propensity for dramatic embellishment in prose.[101] Her intelligence activities for the English Crown around 1666–1667 in Antwerp, under the code name "Astrea," are substantiated by 19 preserved letters in state papers, confirming recruitment to extract information from regicide William Scot.[2] Yet, the operation proved ineffectual, yielding negligible results amid agent unreliability and logistical failures, culminating in Behn's unpaid debts and return without notable achievements.[2] Popular retellings have amplified this episode into a tale of proficient espionage, but primary documents reveal a brief, financially ruinous endeavor rather than sustained covert prowess.[2] These biographical gaps underscore the imperative for primary verification over anecdotal amplification, as later hagiographies often prioritize ideological alignment—such as elevating Behn as an empowered adventurer—over empirical restraint.[98]

Moral and Ideological Criticisms

Behn's dramatic works, such as The Rover (1677), featured explicit libertine themes of sexual promiscuity and hedonism, which provoked contemporary moral outrage for ostensibly glorifying vice at the expense of traditional virtue. Critics, including those aligned with Puritan sensibilities lingering post-Restoration, condemned her plays' portrayal of female characters engaging in casual liaisons without narrative punishment, viewing it as a corruption of public morals and a challenge to marital fidelity.[62][4] This reflected broader attacks on Restoration theatre's indecency, where Behn's endorsement of libertine philosophy—rewarding male rakishness while critiquing its inequalities for women—was seen as undermining ethical restraint rather than satirizing it effectively.[23] Ideologically, Behn's staunch Tory allegiance manifested in partisan satires like The Roundheads (1682), which subverted Whig rhetoric and equated dissenters with hypocrisy, prioritizing royalist loyalty over impartial analysis of political events. Such writings, including assaults on Whig merchants and religious radicals, were critiqued even among contemporaries for bias that distorted causal realities of factional strife, favoring propaganda over truth-seeking inquiry into governance failures.[38][102] Her anti-Whig stance, while consistent with her spy background under Charles II, rendered her output more polemical than objective, as evidenced by plays that amplified Tory narratives without empirical balance on events like the Exclusion Crisis.[103] In Oroonoko (1688), Behn's depiction of slavery emphasized the noble African prince's betrayal by Europeans but stopped short of abolitionist advocacy, portraying the institution as an observational tragedy rooted in betrayal rather than inherent evil; Oroonoko himself owned slaves per African customs, complicating claims of anti-slavery intent.[13][104] This approach, blending royalist honor codes with exoticized nobility, has drawn ideological critique for reinforcing hierarchical norms—including racial and servile—without challenging the economic or moral foundations of colonial trade, thus observing rather than condemning the vice of enslavement.[105] While her narrative craft humanized Oroonoko, it arguably privileged aesthetic tragedy over principled opposition to systemic exploitation.[106]
