New York City (disambiguation)

New York City most commonly refers to the most populous incorporated place in the United States, a consolidated city-county municipality in the U.S. state of New York comprising five boroughs—Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island—with a resident population of 8,478,072 as of July 1, 2024.[1][2] This densely populated urban area, exceeding 27,000 residents per square mile, functions as a global hub for finance, media, entertainment, fashion, and culture, anchored by Manhattan's skyscrapers and the New York Stock Exchange.[3] The term New York City may also refer to other entities, including the New York City Football Club (a Major League Soccer team), the New York City Ballet (a renowned dance company), or various media properties and fictional settings evoking the city's name, though these are distinct from the primary municipal designation.[2]

Ships

Naval Ships

USS New York City (SSN-696) was a Los Angeles-class nuclear-powered fast attack submarine and the only United States Navy vessel named specifically for New York City.[4] Laid down on 15 December 1973 by the Electric Boat Division of General Dynamics at Groton, Connecticut, the submarine was launched on 18 June 1977, sponsored by Mrs. James R. Schlesinger.[4] She was commissioned on 3 March 1979 under the command of Cmdr. James A. Ross, with a displacement of approximately 6,927 tons submerged, a length of 360 feet, and a crew of 12 officers and 98 enlisted personnel.[4] [5] During her service, New York City conducted multiple deployments, including operations in the Western Pacific and Northern Pacific, and underwent modernization at Pearl Harbor from December 1985 to February 1988.[4] The submarine participated in exercises such as MEKAR 95 with allied forces in 1995.[6] She was simultaneously inactivated, decommissioned, and stricken from the Naval Vessel Register on 30 April 1997, after which she entered the ship-submarine recycling program for disposal.[4] [5]

Commercial Ships

The SS City of New York was a transatlantic passenger liner launched on March 15, 1888, by J. & G. Thomson of Glasgow, Scotland, for the Inman Line (later transferred to the American Line).[7] At 10,499 gross register tons with twin screws and a service speed of 20 knots, she was the largest passenger steamer afloat upon entering service that August, primarily operating between Liverpool, Queenstown (now Cobh), and New York.[8] The vessel featured a clipper bow, three funnels, and three masts rigged for sail auxiliary power, accommodating up to 540 first-class, 200 second-class, and 1,000 third-class passengers; she held the Blue Riband for fastest eastbound Atlantic crossing in 1893 before losing it to her sister ship City of Paris.[8] During the Spanish–American War (1898) and World War I, she served as an armed auxiliary cruiser under U.S. Navy charters as USS Harvard and USS Plattsburg, respectively, before resuming commercial service and being scrapped in 1923 at Osaka, Japan.[7] An earlier City of New York (3,020 gross register tons), built in 1875 as a U.S. passenger-cargo steamer, operated coastal and Pacific routes before wrecking on October 26, 1893, off Point Bonita, California, with no loss of life reported.[9] The 1930 City of New York was an 8,272-ton motor passenger ship constructed by Sun Shipbuilding & Dry Dock Co. in Chester, Pennsylvania, for commercial service, later requisitioned during World War II.[10] In 1949, the Ellerman Lines freighter City of New York became the first post-World War II ocean-going vessel to bear the name, arriving in New York Harbor on November 25 as a cargo carrier.[11]

Music

Albums

"New York City" is the title of a 2003 album by the Peter Malick Group, featuring vocals by Norah Jones on tracks recorded in 2000 and remixed for release by Koch Records; it includes original compositions by Peter Malick alongside covers like "All Your Love" by Sam Maghett.[12][13] "New York City" is also the title of a 2023 album by the Brooklyn-based rock band The Men, released on February 3 by Fuzz Club Records; the ten-track LP draws on garage punk and noise rock influences, with songs such as "Hard Livin'" and "Eternal Recurrence" reflecting themes of urban endurance and raw energy.[14][15]

Songs

"New York City" is the title of multiple songs by various artists. John Lennon wrote and recorded "New York City", which appeared on the 1972 double album Some Time in New York City by John Lennon, Yoko Ono, and the Plastic Ono Band.[16] The track, credited to Lennon and Ono, draws from Lennon's experiences after relocating to New York, featuring raw, observational lyrics about urban encounters.[17] They Might Be Giants released a cover of "New York City" on their 1996 album Factory Showroom.[18] Originally performed by the Canadian punk band Cub on their 1994 debut Bettty, the song portrays a whimsical romance set against New York landmarks like the Statue of Liberty and Central Park.[19] Lenny Kravitz's "New York City", co-written with Craig Ross, was released on September 23, 2014, as part of his tenth studio album Strut.[20] The funk-rock track serves as a tribute to Kravitz's hometown, emphasizing its enduring spirit and personal significance.[21] Kylie Minogue issued "New York City" as a standalone digital single on May 3, 2019, following sessions for her 2018 album Golden.[22] The upbeat pop song, produced with collaborators including DJ Fresh, evokes themes of escape and excitement in the city.[23]

Visual Arts

Paintings

New York City I is an abstract oil-on-canvas painting created by Dutch artist Piet Mondrian in 1942, measuring 114 by 107 cm.[24] It consists of dynamic, intersecting lines in primary colors—yellow, red, and blue—against a white ground, marking a shift from Mondrian's traditional rectilinear grids by omitting black outlines and introducing rhythmic, boogie-woogie-inspired forms influenced by New York's jazz scene and urban energy.[25] The painting is held in the collection of the Musée National d'Art Moderne at the Centre Pompidou in Paris, where it was displayed upside down from 1980 until its orientation was corrected in 2022 based on archival evidence of Mondrian's studio setup.[26] This work belongs to a small series of similarly titled paintings Mondrian produced during his final years in New York City after fleeing Europe in 1940, including unfinished variants such as New York City 2 (1941, oil, pencil, and tape on canvas, 104.5 x 99 cm) at the Metropolitan Museum of Art and New York City 3 (1941, oil, pencil, chalk, and tape on canvas, 117 x 110 cm) at the Museo Thyssen-Bornemisza.[27][28] These pieces reflect Mondrian's late experimentation with looser, more fluid abstractions evoking the city's verticality and vitality, though only New York City I was fully realized before his death in 1944.[24]

Media

Video Games

New York City, also known as N.Y.C. or The Big Apple, is an action video game developed and published by Synapse Software Corporation for Atari 8-bit computers, with a release date of November 1983.[29] Programmed by Russ Segal, the game simulates navigating the chaotic streets of Manhattan as a tourist, using a top-down perspective on a grid-based representation of the city.[29] Players maneuver a vehicle through traffic, evade muggers on foot, dodge aggressive taxis and pedestrians, and avoid construction obstacles to reach landmarks and accumulate points.[30] Crashes or encounters with hazards result in damage, requiring repairs at service stations, while successful sightseeing boosts the score.[30] A port titled N.Y.C. was released for the Commodore 64 in 1984 by the same publisher, retaining core gameplay mechanics but adapted for the platform's hardware.[31] The game emphasizes survival and exploration in a stylized urban environment, reflecting 1980s concerns about city dangers like crime and disorder, though its simplistic graphics and controls drew mixed reception for repetitive challenges.[32] No official sequels or modern re-releases exist, positioning it as an obscure entry in early home computing action titles.[29]

Television

New York: A Documentary Film is an American documentary television mini-series directed by Ric Burns and produced by Steeplechase Films in association with WGBH and the New York Center for Visual History. First aired on PBS from November 1999 to November 2003, the series comprises eight episodes totaling approximately 17.5 hours, chronicling the development of New York City from its 17th-century Dutch settlement as New Amsterdam through its evolution into a global metropolis, including key events like the Revolutionary War, mass immigration waves in the 19th and early 20th centuries, the 1929 stock market crash, and post-9/11 recovery up to the early 2000s.[33] [34] The production draws on archival footage, photographs, maps, and interviews with historians and contemporaries to emphasize causal factors such as geography, immigration, economic booms and busts, and infrastructure projects like the Erie Canal and subways in shaping the city's growth and cultural dominance. Additional episodes were released in 2010 ("New York 2010: State of Mind") and 2015 ("The Feud"), extending coverage to contemporary challenges including fiscal crises and urban renewal.[34] The series received critical acclaim for its depth and visual storytelling, earning multiple awards including Peabody and Emmy nominations, though some critiques noted its focus on elite narratives over everyday immigrant experiences.[33]
