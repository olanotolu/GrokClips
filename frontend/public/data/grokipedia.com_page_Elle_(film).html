Elle (film)

Elle is a 2016 French-language psychological thriller film directed by Paul Verhoeven, starring Isabelle Huppert as Michèle Laronde, the chief executive of a video game company who is raped in her home by a masked intruder and embarks on a twisted pursuit to identify and confront her assailant.[1][2] Adapted by David Birke from the 2012 novel Oh... by Philippe Djian, the screenplay was translated into French for production, marking Verhoeven's return to provocative cinema following his Hollywood period.[3][4] The film premiered in competition at the 2016 Cannes Film Festival on May 21, where it received a standing ovation and Huppert shared the Best Actress award with two others.[5] It was released theatrically in France on May 25, 2016, and in the United States on November 11, 2016, by Sony Pictures Classics, earning critical acclaim for its genre-blending of drama, comedy, and thriller elements, with a 91% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 245 reviews.[5][6] Huppert's performance garnered the Golden Globe Award for Best Actress in a Motion Picture – Drama, as well as the film winning Best Foreign Language Film at the same ceremony, though it faced Oscar nomination snubs amid debates over its content.[7][8] Elle provoked significant controversy for its unconventional depiction of sexual violence, with some critics labeling it misogynistic or insensitive to rape survivors due to Michèle's ambiguous response blending arousal, revenge, and detachment rather than conventional victimhood.[9][10] Others defended it as a bold feminist exploration of female agency and repressed desires, aligning with Verhoeven's history of subverting audience expectations in films like Basic Instinct.[11] The film's box office was modest, grossing $2.3 million in the U.S. on a limited release, but it solidified Huppert's reputation for tackling complex, unflinching roles.[6]

Synopsis

Plot summary

Michèle Leblanc, CEO of a Paris-based video game company, is raped in her home by an unidentified man wearing a ski mask, with her cat witnessing the assault from atop a cabinet. Instead of summoning authorities, she methodically cleans the bloodstains from her living room, discards her ripped dress and underwear, takes a bath while noting residual blood in the water, feeds the cat, and orders Chinese takeout to continue her evening.[12][13] During a subsequent dinner with friends, including her business partner Anna and Anna's husband Charles (with whom Michèle conducts an extramarital affair), she matter-of-factly recounts the incident without evident distress or demands for sympathy. Michèle initiates a private investigation into the attacker's identity, scrutinizing male figures in her orbit—including Charles, her ex-husband Richard, her devoutly Christian neighbor Patrick (whose wife suffers from multiple sclerosis), and employees at her firm—after receiving anonymous emails hinting at the perpetrator's ongoing fixation. These communications escalate into further masked intrusions and encounters, during which Michèle blackmails and engages in scripted role-playing scenarios with the intruder, blending coercion and complicity.[12][13][14][15] Parallel to this pursuit, Michèle navigates familial entanglements: her aimless son Vincent shacks up with his domineering, pregnant girlfriend Josie amid threats of violence; her aging mother Irene prepares to wed her kept younger lover; and Michèle anticipates the parole of her father, a convicted 1970s serial killer responsible for murdering multiple neighborhood children. At work, delays on a dragon-themed game project strain relations with Anna, while an anonymously circulated video—depicting a graphic scene reminiscent of the rape—fuels suspicions toward a coworker, prompting Michèle to wield leverage through surveillance footage from her home.[12][13][15] The rapist is unmasked as Patrick during a tense escalation of their encounters. In the climax, Patrick assaults Michèle anew in her home, but Vincent arrives and bludgeons him to death with a fireplace poker, mistaking the struggle for an unprovoked attack. The resolution intertwines this violence with family legacy: upon his release, Michèle's father confesses remorse before committing suicide in her presence, fulfilling her late mother's wish for confrontation and echoing the intergenerational pattern of brutality originating from his crimes.[14][15][16]

Principal cast and roles

Production

Development

The film Elle is an adaptation of the 2012 novel Oh... by French author Philippe Djian, originally published in France and later translated into English as Elle.[19] Producer Saïd Ben Saïd acquired the adaptation rights and approached director Paul Verhoeven in the United States with the book, initiating the project.[20] Verhoeven, drawn to the novel's provocative exploration of a woman's unconventional response to sexual violence, initially envisioned an English-language version set in an American city such as Boston or Chicago.[20] Screenwriter David Birke penned the initial English screenplay, which Verhoeven refined to emphasize a tonal blend of psychological thriller, dark comedy, and family drama, using storyboards to visualize key sequences.[20] Efforts to produce the film in the U.S. stalled after approximately six months due to casting difficulties; Verhoeven approached actresses including Nicole Kidman, who ultimately declined, and considered Kristen Stewart, but no suitable lead emerged amid concerns over the story's unconventional themes and American cultural sensitivities toward depictions of sexual assault.[19][20] Ben Saïd's French production company SBS Productions then proposed relocating the project to France, aligning with the novel's origins and enabling greater authenticity in portraying the protagonist's mindset.[20] Verhoeven agreed, opting to direct in French despite needing to relearn the language, as he believed the narrative's ambiguities—particularly around power dynamics and victim agency—suited a European context better than a Hollywood framework.[19] The screenplay was subsequently translated and adjusted by Harold Manning, finalizing pre-production by late 2014 ahead of principal photography in 2015.[21]

Filming

Principal photography for Elle took place primarily in Paris and its suburbs over a ten-week period beginning in early 2015. The production utilized practical locations such as apartments in Saint-Germain-en-Laye and offices to enhance the film's intimate, domestic atmosphere. A sequence intended for Paris's main police station was abandoned after the Charlie Hebdo shooting on 7 January 2015, which heightened security concerns around public institutions.[22] Director Paul Verhoeven focused on capturing nuanced performances through extended long takes and a structured shot progression—alternating long shots, medium shots, and returning to long shots—to maintain narrative momentum and actor immersion without excessive cuts. Cinematographer Stéphane Fontaine employed two lightweight RED cameras for multi-angle coverage, allowing fluid two-camera setups that emphasized the claustrophobic tension in confined spaces like homes and workplaces. These choices supported Verhoeven's aim for raw realism, with the shoot completing without reported major delays or reshoots on a budget of approximately €8.7 million. Sensitive material involving violence and sexuality was filmed with professional restraint, prioritizing actor safety through preparation and limited crew presence where necessary.[23][24][25]

Post-production and music

The film's editing was handled by Job ter Burg, who crafted a rhythm that sustains the narrative's blend of suspense and dark comedy through precise cuts emphasizing character intimacy and tonal shifts, including subtle non-linear echoes of trauma.[26] Post-production refinements focused on minor visual effects and audio enhancements rather than extensive alterations, preserving the raw footage's psychological edge without large-scale interventions like those in Verhoeven's earlier sci-fi works.[27] Sound design incorporated heightened ambient elements, such as the amplified crash of breaking glass in the opening sequence, to intensify the intrusion of violence into domestic spaces and underscore underlying unease.[28] The original score, composed by Anne Dudley, employs sparse piano motifs and orchestral swells to heighten moments of internal conflict and ambiguity, with tracks like "Little Psycho" and "Primal Scream" evoking restrained psychological turmoil.[29] [30] Dudley began recording sessions in late 2015, integrating the music to complement diegetic elements such as seasonal holiday tunes that contrast sharply with the protagonists' escalating tensions.[29] The final cut, incorporating these audio layers, was completed in time for the film's world premiere at the Cannes Film Festival on May 21, 2016.[31]

Release

Premiere and distribution

Elle had its world premiere in competition at the 2016 Cannes Film Festival on May 21, 2016. The screening concluded with a seven-minute standing ovation.[32] The film was released theatrically in France on September 21, 2016, distributed by Pathé.[33] In the United States, Sony Pictures Classics handled distribution for a limited release starting November 11, 2016.[6] It rolled out across various European markets in the months following the Cannes debut, with local distributors managing regional theatrical runs. Marketing campaigns highlighted Isabelle Huppert's lead performance and Paul Verhoeven's signature provocative style, featuring trailers that teased the thriller elements without revealing key plot twists.[2] The film received an R rating from the MPAA for violence involving sexual assault, some grisly images, language, and brief drug use.[34] Following its theatrical run, Elle became available for streaming on platforms such as Netflix in select regions.[35]

Box office

Elle was produced with a budget of $9.1 million.[36] In the United States and Canada, the film earned $2.3 million from a limited theatrical release that began on November 11, 2016, with an opening weekend gross of $50,934 across two theaters.[36] Its domestic performance demonstrated strong arthouse appeal, achieving a multiplier of 18.5 times the opening weekend and expanding to a maximum of 143 theaters, though it underperformed relative to wider commercial releases.[36] Internationally, Elle grossed $14.2 million, led by France where it collected $4.2 million, contributing significantly to its profitability in Europe.[36] The film's overall worldwide theatrical earnings totaled $16.5 million, approximately 1.8 times the production budget, bolstered by awards recognition including Cannes acclaim and a Golden Globe win for Best Foreign Language Film, which extended its run in select markets.[36] This outcome reflected director Paul Verhoeven's pattern of niche success with provocative European arthouse films, contrasting with broader mainstream hits like Basic Instinct.[37]

Reception

Critical reviews

Elle received widespread critical acclaim upon release, particularly for Isabelle Huppert's performance and Paul Verhoeven's direction. On Rotten Tomatoes, the film holds a 91% approval rating based on 245 reviews, with an average score of 8/10, reflecting praise for its bold narrative and Huppert's portrayal of a complex, unyielding protagonist.[6] Metacritic assigns it a score of 89 out of 100 from 36 critics, indicating strong consensus on its artistic provocation and stylistic audacity.[38] Critics frequently highlighted Huppert's "fearless" and multifaceted performance as the film's anchor, enabling Verhoeven to blend genres including thriller, comedy, and melodrama without descending into exploitation. NPR described it as a "brutal, elegant pitch-black comedy," noting Verhoeven's sardonic approach to the rape aftermath that gleefully courts controversy while maintaining narrative tension.[21] RogerEbert.com awarded it four stars, calling it a "maniacal and confident hybrid" of rape-revenge, ensemble comedy, and stalker thriller, crediting the film's refusal to moralize.[12] However, reception divided over the film's tone, particularly its treatment of sexual violence and dark humor, with some viewing it as transgressive genius and others as discomforting or ethically fraught. The Guardian's Peter Bradshaw praised its "outrageous black comedy" and volatile energy, yet a separate commentary critiqued it as "sick" for elements evoking rape fantasy, arguing it undermined survivors by implying subconscious desire.[15] [9] French critics, attuned to the cultural specificity of Verhoeven's adaptation from Philippe Djian’s novel, largely embraced the provocative satire over Anglo-American concerns about prudishness, contributing to its domestic success and acclaim for subverting victim tropes.[3]

Awards and nominations

At the 2016 Cannes Film Festival held in May, Isabelle Huppert received the Best Actress award for her performance as Michèle in Elle. The film achieved significant recognition at the 42nd César Awards on February 24, 2017, winning six categories: Best Film, Best Director for Paul Verhoeven (his first César), Best Actress for Huppert, Best Editing, Best Sound, and Best Adapted Screenplay.[39][40] In the United States, Huppert won the Golden Globe Award for Best Actress in a Motion Picture – Drama on January 8, 2017, while Elle also secured the Golden Globe for Best Motion Picture – Foreign Language.[41][42] At the 89th Academy Awards, Huppert earned a nomination for Best Actress but did not win.[43] The film received a nomination for Best Film Not in the English Language at the BAFTA Awards.[44] Elle garnered over 70 awards and 97 nominations internationally, with stronger acclaim in European ceremonies compared to major American prizes beyond Huppert's individual honors.[45]

Themes and analysis

Psychological and character dynamics

Michèle Leblanc's psychological profile is profoundly shaped by her childhood exposure to paternal violence, as her father was a convicted serial killer whose crimes she witnessed and possibly aided at age 10, fostering a rejection of passive victimhood in favor of assertive agency.[46] This early trauma, rather than inducing helplessness, manifests in her adult life as a calculated detachment from emotional vulnerability, evident in her business acumen as CEO of a video game company specializing in violent narratives and her interpersonal manipulations.[47] Empirical psychological research on trauma resilience indicates that individuals with histories of familial violence may develop adaptive strategies emphasizing control and self-reliance, diverging from normative grief responses to reframe adversity as a site for personal mastery.[48] Following the initial home invasion and rape by a masked intruder, Michèle's trauma response eschews conventional reporting to authorities or therapeutic disclosure, instead prioritizing identification of the perpetrator through surveillance and indirect confrontation, reflecting a causal chain from her father's legacy of concealed brutality to her own preference for covert dominance over public victim narratives.[49] This evolution underscores masochistic undertones intertwined with empowerment, where initial fear transmutes into provocative engagements that invert power imbalances, as she documents encounters to leverage exposure, embodying a non-binary psychological negotiation of desire and retribution beyond simplistic binaries of abuser and victim.[50] Such atypical resilience aligns with studies showing varied posttraumatic growth trajectories post-sexual assault, where pre-existing traits like high agency can lead to proactive reclamation rather than withdrawal, though her approach risks self-retraumatization.[51] Her familial relationships further illuminate inherited dysfunction, as Michèle maintains strained bonds marked by emotional unavailability toward her son Vincent, whom she critiques harshly amid his own relational turmoil, and codependent tensions with her aging mother, whose romantic indiscretions mirror Michèle's own boundary-crossing behaviors.[12] These dynamics reveal intergenerational transmission of maladaptive coping, where Michèle's paternal inheritance of concealed aggression disrupts normative empathy, yet enables a resilient functionality absent in more victim-oriented frameworks.[46] Psychological literature on family systems post-trauma supports this, noting how early exposure to violence can perpetuate cycles of detachment and control within kin networks, contributing to individual fortitude amid relational discord.[48]

Gender and power structures

In "Elle", male-female power dynamics are portrayed through Michèle Leblanc's assertive navigation of professional and intimate relationships, emphasizing female agency over passive victimhood. As CEO of a video game company specializing in fantasy narratives of conquest, Michèle wields economic authority by firing underperformers and demanding script revisions that align with her vision of empowered female protagonists, extending this control to her personal life where she initiates and dictates terms in affairs with married men like her boss's spouse.[2][12] This depiction contrasts with conventional cinematic tropes by granting women multifaceted control, including over their sexual interactions, without reducing them to reactive figures in male-driven plots. The narrative subverts standard revenge archetypes by integrating Michèle's complicity and derived pleasure in encounters with her masked intruder, undermining assumptions of inherent, one-way male predatory causality in gender imbalances. Rather than a linear path to retribution, Michèle's responses—ranging from surveillance to seduction—position her as an active participant who reframes violation into a site of mutual entanglement, highlighting how power flows bidirectionally when women reject unilateral subjugation.[12][15] Verhoeven's construction avoids privileging trauma as defining female identity, instead illustrating agency through Michèle's deliberate choices that blur victim-perpetrator lines. Critiques of masculinity emerge via characters like neighbor Patrick, whose outward religiosity and family-man facade conceal adulterous impulses and escalating obsession, exposing frailties masked by performative piety. Patrick's crucifixion imagery during intimate scenes underscores ironic hypocrisy, as his moral posturing crumbles under carnal drives, contrasting Michèle's unapologetic pragmatism in exploiting such weaknesses for leverage.[52][53] Other men, including Michèle's ex-husband and son, further reveal patterns of emotional dependency and ethical inconsistency, with Verhoeven noting in interviews that the film deliberately accentuates female resilience to reveal systemic male vulnerabilities in modern social structures.[54] Director Paul Verhoeven framed these dynamics as reflective of "French" sexual candor, drawing from the source novel's European context to depict unvarnished human motivations over sanitized Hollywood equivalents, where female characters rarely exhibit comparable moral ambiguity or proactive subversion.[55] In contrast to American cinema's frequent binarization of gender roles—strong heroines versus irredeemable villains—"Elle" employs empirical realism from observed behaviors, portraying power as negotiated through cunning and desire rather than innate traits, a approach Verhoeven attributed to cultural differences in addressing sexuality without puritanical filters.[56][53]

Controversies

Depiction of rape and consent

The film opens with a masked intruder raping Michèle Leblanc (Isabelle Huppert) in her Paris home, depicted graphically over ten shots lasting one minute and thirty-nine seconds, accompanied by a soundtrack featuring a cat's meowing that underscores the domestic interruption.[57] The sequence emphasizes Michèle's immediate aftermath: she rises stoically, sweeps shattered glass from broken dishes, discards her torn clothing in the trash, feeds her cat, and resumes her routine without alerting police or displaying overt distress.[13] This initial assault sets a tone of restraint in visual execution, focusing on procedural aftermath rather than prolonged sensationalism. Subsequent encounters between Michèle and her assailant evolve into interactions blending coercion with mutual provocation, including a later scene where she verbally directs him during a masked intrusion that shifts into scripted dominance-submission dynamics resembling role-play.[58] These moments retain ambiguity regarding consent, as Michèle's pursuit of the perpetrator incorporates fantasy elements tied to her psychological response, mirroring the source novel's progression from violation to entangled agency without explicit resolution of victim-perpetrator boundaries.[59] Director Paul Verhoeven integrates dark humor through ironic narration and situational absurdities—such as Michèle's casual continuation of a dinner party post-assault—to highlight the persistence of violence in interpersonal cycles, while maintaining that the rape itself elicits no laughter and serves narrative exploration rather than comedy or endorsement.[55] Huppert approached the physical demands via intensive rehearsals and a professional intimacy coach, treating sequences as technically precise performances to convey Michèle's internal complexity without exploitative excess in blocking or camera work. Verhoeven's editing and lighting choices prioritize character psychology over graphic lingering, aligning with the film's adaptation of the novel's events to probe behavioral realism in trauma's aftermath.[23]

Feminist critiques and responses

Feminist critiques of Elle frequently argued that the film's depiction of the protagonist's evolving response to assault normalized masochism and undermined narratives of unambiguous victimhood. In a March 28, 2017, Guardian opinion piece, Bidisha condemned the film as a "rape fantasy" that portrayed women as complicit in their own abuse while framing abusive men as victims of female "demented caprices," dismissing its critical acclaim as overlooking inherent sickness in the premise.[9] Similarly, Richard Brody's November 15, 2016, New Yorker review labeled the film's sexual transgressions "phony," asserting it failed to penetrate the essence of character or dramatic realism despite Isabelle Huppert's performance.[60] These objections intensified in the post-#MeToo context of 2017 onward, with some viewing the narrative as insensitive to survivors by complicating consent and agency rather than adhering to trauma scripts that emphasize perpetual victim status. Responses from the film's creators emphasized the portrayal of multifaceted female autonomy over reductive ideological frameworks. Huppert described protagonist Michèle as a "post-feminist" character who constructs her own behavioral space, explicitly rejecting victimhood or conventional revenge arcs in favor of self-directed complexity.[61][62] She elaborated in a November 1, 2016, RogerEbert.com interview that this stance reflected a rejection of imposed uniformity in female representation, allowing for individuality beyond feminist orthodoxy.[63] Director Paul Verhoeven, in a November 23, 2016, Slate discussion, defended the work's basis in the source novel's unflinching psychology, arguing it interrogated power imbalances through a woman's active navigation of desire and threat, rather than passive suffering.[64] The 2016-2017 reception revealed ideological tensions, with broad critical praise (including from outlets like Variety, which termed it "sex-positive" despite provocations) clashing against targeted feminist rebukes, often from left-leaning publications prone to enforcing narrative conformity on gender dynamics.[10] Defenders, including in an November 8, 2016, IndieWire analysis, countered that demanding uniform female portrayals—eschewing agency for solidarity—itself diminishes women's capacity for divergent interior lives, positioning Elle as a challenge to such constraints rather than an endorsement of misogyny.[11] This divide underscored empirical patterns in media discourse, where empirical fidelity to source material and character causality yielded higher aggregate approval than adherence to post-assault orthodoxy.[65]

Legacy

Cultural and critical reevaluation

In the years following its 2016 release, particularly amid the #MeToo movement's emergence in late 2017, Elle underwent critical reassessment for its defiance of conventional trauma narratives, with scholars noting its deliberate complication of victim agency in rape-revenge cinema. Rather than framing protagonist Michèle as a straightforward survivor seeking unambiguous justice, the film explores her ambiguous psychological entanglement with her assailant, subverting genre expectations of clear victimhood and empowerment through retaliation. This approach, rooted in the source novel's unflinching portrayal of desire and control, has been analyzed as a challenge to post-trauma depictions that prioritize unalloyed indignation over internal conflict. Paul Verhoeven, anticipating backlash, positioned the film as an intentional provocation to mirror the raw, multifaceted realities of sexual violence, describing rape as a "daily threat" for women exceeding risks like traffic accidents or firearms. He emphasized that the narrative's black comedy and moral ambiguity aimed to provoke reflection on human perversity without prescribing simplistic resolutions, a stance that resonated in later discussions contrasting Elle's realism against culturally dominant scripts of perpetual victim status. Verhoeven's readiness for controversy, expressed prior to but contextualized by #MeToo sensitivities, highlighted the film's design to unsettle viewers and expose biases in how violation is narrativized.[59][66] Retrospectives from the late 2010s onward have lauded Elle's anti-victim complexity as prescient, praising its refusal to align with rising emphases on unnuanced survivor solidarity and instead favoring depictions of flawed autonomy that defy ideological streamlining. This reevaluation underscores the film's sustained intellectual draw, where Michèle's inscrutable agency—neither fully defiant nor submissive—offers a counterpoint to narratives privileging collective outrage over individual opacity, fostering ongoing discourse on psychological depth amid cultural shifts toward trauma's commodification.[18]

Influence on cinema

Elle advanced the rape-revenge subgenre by incorporating black comedy and psychological nuance, shifting emphasis from exploitative vengeance to satirical exploration of trauma and agency. Critics described it as pioneering a "rape-revenge black comedy," where protagonist Michèle's response to assault blends defiance, complicity, and humor rather than linear retribution.[3] This approach contrasted with earlier entries like I Spit on Your Grave (1978), favoring ambiguity over cathartic violence and influencing subsequent films to probe survivor psychology more deeply.[67][68] Paul Verhoeven's direction in Elle reinvigorated his career trajectory, demonstrating his signature provocation—seen in Basic Instinct (1992) and Showgirls (1995)—could thrive in arthouse contexts post-Hollywood. At age 77, the film's Cannes premiere and international distribution underscored his adaptability, encouraging European productions to merge thriller mechanics with social satire unburdened by U.S. censorship norms.[10] This late-career pivot validated Verhoeven's transnational style, paving the way for his subsequent work like Benedetta (2021), which echoed Elle's blend of eroticism and critique.[69] Isabelle Huppert's performance as Michèle established a template for ambiguous female protagonists in thrillers, portraying agency amid moral complexity without reductive victimhood. Her embodiment of a character navigating power imbalances with calculated detachment inspired casting trends toward actresses capable of such unvarnished portrayals, evolving representations beyond archetypal resilience.[11] This reinforced Huppert's oeuvre of challenging roles, signaling to directors the commercial viability of leads defying conventional empathy demands in genre films.[70]
