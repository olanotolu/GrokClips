Sarah Murdoch

Sarah Murdoch (née O'Hare) is a British-Australian former model, television presenter, and philanthropist whose career spans international fashion, media, and charitable endeavors focused on children's health, medical research, and cancer advocacy. Born in England and raised in Sydney, she rose to prominence as a top model in the 1990s, signing with agencies at age 17 and working with luxury brands including Chanel and Valentino.[1] Murdoch hosted Australia's Next Top Model from its inception in 2005 through 2010, during which she hosted cycles 5 through 7 of the international franchise as well. In the 2010 season finale, she inadvertently announced the incorrect winner live on air due to a production communication error, an incident that drew significant media attention but was confirmed not to be her fault. Married to media executive Lachlan Murdoch since 1999, she has shifted focus to philanthropy, serving as an ambassador for the Murdoch Children's Research Institute for over 25 years and co-endowing a $5 million research fellowship with her husband in 2020 to advance child health studies.[2][3] As patron of Australia's National Breast Cancer Foundation for 18 years, Murdoch helped expand funding and launched advocacy initiatives targeting zero breast cancer deaths by 2030. Her broader charitable commitments include support for education, the arts, and medical research, exemplified by a million-dollar donation from the Lachlan and Sarah Murdoch Foundation to establish Qtopia Sydney, Australia's first permanent queer museum.[4][5]

Early life

Upbringing and entry into modeling

Sarah Murdoch, born Sarah O'Hare on 31 May 1972 in Croydon, South London, England, relocated to Australia during her early childhood and was raised primarily in Sydney.[6][7] Described in contemporary profiles as a chronically shy child, she initially aspired to a career in ballet but was discouraged due to her height of approximately 5 feet 10 inches, which exceeded typical requirements for professional dancers.[8][9] At age 17, in 1989, Murdoch entered the modeling industry by approaching Vivien's Management, a prominent Sydney-based agency, where she was signed on the spot and completed her first photoshoot the following day.[10][1] This marked her professional debut, transitioning from personal aspirations to a career that quickly expanded internationally; shortly thereafter, she relocated to Paris to pursue runway work and higher-profile opportunities in Europe and the United States.[11][12]

Professional career

Modeling achievements

Sarah Murdoch, born Sarah O'Hare, commenced her modeling career at age 17 in 1989 after signing with the Sydney-based agency Vivien's Management.[13] She rapidly advanced to international work, initially focusing on runway modeling in Paris before expanding to New York, where she established a prominent presence in the fashion industry during the 1990s.[12] Notable achievements include multiple magazine covers, such as Vogue Australia in June 1994 and Spanish editions of Vogue in November 1993 and August 1994.[14] She featured in the Sports Illustrated Swimsuit Issue in 1999, with photography sessions conducted in the British Virgin Islands, and appeared in subsequent editions, contributing to two cover features overall.[15][16] Murdoch collaborated with high-end brands such as Chanel and Valentino, underscoring her status in luxury fashion campaigns.[1] Her career trajectory from local discovery to global runways and editorial work positioned her as a recognized figure in Australian and international modeling circles by the mid-1990s.[14]

Transition to television hosting

Following a successful international modeling career that spanned the 1990s, including high-profile work in Paris and New York for brands such as Chanel and Valentino, Sarah Murdoch reduced her commitments after marrying Lachlan Murdoch in 1999 and giving birth to their first child in 2000.[1][13] By 2006, she leveraged her public profile and on-camera poise to enter television presenting, accepting a hosting position on the Nine Network's breakfast program Today despite having no prior journalistic experience.[13][17] Murdoch was recruited to replace Jessica Rowe, who had left the show amid reported internal tensions, and she debuted in the role around October 2006.[13] The position involved co-hosting alongside Karl Stefanovic, covering news, interviews, and lifestyle segments from 5:30 a.m. to 9:00 a.m. weekdays.[18] She hosted for roughly four months, concluding her tenure on March 30, 2007, after which Tracy Grimshaw assumed the role permanently.[12] Murdoch later reflected on the stint as "one of the most challenging experiences" of her career, citing the steep learning curve of live broadcasting and early-morning demands while managing family responsibilities.[17] This initial foray established her as a viable television personality in Australia, transitioning her from print and runway visibility to on-air media presence.[18] Subsequent opportunities followed, including co-hosting lifestyle programs and producing content through her company Room 329 Productions, which signed a deal with Foxtel in 2008.[18] These steps built toward her prominent role on reality competition series, marking a deliberate shift toward media production and presenting as modeling waned.[7]

Australia's Next Top Model

Sarah Murdoch joined Australia's Next Top Model in 2008 through her production company, Room 329 Productions, signing a multi-year deal with Foxtel to serve as host and executive producer.[18] She debuted in this role for cycle 5, which premiered on Fox8 on April 28, 2009, and continued as host and lead judge for cycles 6 and 7.[19][20] Her tenure emphasized mentoring aspiring models on runway techniques, photoshoot challenges, and industry professionalism, drawing from her own experience as a former model. Cycle 6 premiered on July 20, 2010, featuring international travel and high-profile guest judges, while cycle 7, airing in 2011, incorporated social media elements and diverse contestant backgrounds to reflect evolving modeling standards.[20][1] A notable controversy occurred during the cycle 5 live finale on September 29, 2010, when Murdoch initially announced 19-year-old Kelsey Martinovich as the winner after her victory speech, only to correct herself moments later, revealing Amanda Ware as the actual winner; Murdoch later attributed the error to an autocue malfunction and production miscommunication.[21][22] The incident, viewed by over 1.5 million Australian households, sparked widespread media coverage and online debate about live television pressures, though Ware affirmed no lasting rift with Murdoch.[23] Murdoch departed the series after cycle 7, announcing on December 12, 2011, that she was stepping down as host and co-executive producer to pursue other projects, describing the role as "an incredible opportunity" but citing a desire for new challenges.[21] Her involvement elevated the show's production values and international appeal, contributing to its status as a top-rating Foxtel program during her seasons.[24]

Other television and media projects

Murdoch temporarily co-hosted the morning news and talk program The Today Show on the Nine Network from November 2006, filling in for Jessica Rowe over approximately four months.[25][12] In 2008, Murdoch established the production company Room 329 Productions, named after the hospital birthing suite where her children were born, and signed a deal with Foxtel to develop content including documentaries.[26][18] Through this company, she produced and narrated the four-part documentary series Pride of Australia for Foxtel's Biography Channel, focusing on inspirational Australians and airing in November 2009.[13] Murdoch hosted the reality dance competition Everybody Dance Now on Network Ten, which premiered on 12 August 2012 and featured amateur dancers mentored by professionals like Kelly Rowland and Jason Derülo.[27] The series faced low viewership, leading to its schedule being halved to Sunday nights only after the debut week and ultimate cancellation after eight episodes.[28][29]

Philanthropic activities

Health and breast cancer advocacy

Sarah Murdoch served as Patron of the National Breast Cancer Foundation (NBCF) of Australia for over 18 years, a role in which she substantially increased funding for breast cancer research initiatives.[4] For her contributions, she received the Inaugural Advocacy for Breast Cancer Research Award and the Honorary Award for Extraordinary Service from the NBCF.[4] In February 2014, Murdoch promoted collaboration among Australian cancer foundations to accelerate progress against the disease, including the launch of Register4, an online registry aimed at improving data collection for research.[30] She emphasized the involvement of major businesses in funding efforts, drawing inspiration from family philanthropic traditions.[31] On September 10, 2014, Murdoch launched the NBCF's first comprehensive report on breast cancer in women under 40, documenting that over 800 such cases occur annually in Australia and highlighting diagnostic delays due to low awareness.[32] She urged young women and physicians to recognize symptoms in individuals as young as their 20s or 30s, despite the rarity, and stressed that this demographic receives insufficient specialized support.[32] The NBCF under her patronage set a target of zero breast cancer deaths by 2030 through enhanced research.[33] Earlier, in 2003, Murdoch received Research Australia's Advocacy Award for her foundational support of NBCF programs.[34]

Children's research and education initiatives

Sarah Murdoch has maintained a longstanding commitment to the Murdoch Children's Research Institute (MCRI), Australia's largest independent child health research organization, with involvement spanning over 25 years.[35] She joined the MCRI board in 2014 and currently serves as co-chair of the board and global ambassador, while also founding and chairing its Global Advisory Board to advance international collaborations in pediatric research.[35] In November 2020, Murdoch and her husband Lachlan endowed a $5 million fellowship at MCRI to support long-term child health research initiatives, reflecting the family's multi-generational ties to the institute, which was originally established in 1988 with support from Rupert Murdoch's late wife, Dame Elisabeth.[3] In October 2024, Murdoch announced a collaborative research initiative between MCRI and the Gladstone Institutes in San Francisco, focusing on using human stem cells, genomics, and artificial intelligence to address congenital heart disease in children—a condition affecting approximately 1 in 100 newborns worldwide. This partnership aims to accelerate discoveries in regenerative medicine, building on MCRI's expertise in pediatric cardiology and stem cell therapies.[36] Murdoch's philanthropic efforts extend to children's education and resilience-building, including support through the Lachlan and Sarah Murdoch Foundation for the Resilient Kids Toolkit, launched in February 2023 in partnership with Royal Far West.[37] This initiative provides practical, evidence-based resources—such as age-appropriate worksheets, games, and coping strategies—to help Australian children aged 5–12 process emotional trauma from natural disasters like bushfires and floods, which have intensified in frequency due to climate variability.[37] The toolkit emphasizes psychological resilience training grounded in cognitive behavioral techniques, distributed to schools and families in disaster-prone regions to mitigate long-term mental health impacts.[38] Her broader work prioritizes empirical advancements in child welfare, integrating research with practical educational tools to foster measurable outcomes in health and development.[35]

Broader contributions to arts and community

Sarah Murdoch has been deeply involved with The Australian Ballet, serving as a board director for over a decade and as deputy chair.[4][35] In 2015, she founded the Ballet Ambassadors program to engage younger patrons and ballet enthusiasts, limiting membership to 100 per chapter with annual donations starting at $1,000 to support dancer health and wellbeing through tax-deductible contributions.[39] As the program's founder, global ambassador, and president of its global network, Murdoch has organized exclusive events including mainstage productions and intimate gatherings to foster community support for the organization.[39][4] She received honorary life membership from The Australian Ballet in recognition of her service.[4] In 2017, Murdoch and her husband Lachlan donated $1 million to The Australian Ballet to fund dancer training and development programs, ensuring opportunities for aspiring performers.[40][41] The Sarah Murdoch Foundation has further supported productions, including matching donations for a new staging of Swan Lake in 2022 to mark the company's 60th anniversary.[42] Additionally, she serves on the global council of the American Ballet Theatre.[4][35] Murdoch's arts engagement extends to theater; she served as a board director for New York's Public Theater, where she founded and chaired a young patron program to cultivate emerging supporters.[4][35] Through the Lachlan and Sarah Murdoch Foundation, she co-donated $1 million in 2023 to Qtopia Sydney, the city's inaugural queer museum dedicated to preserving and exhibiting LGBTQIA+ history and culture.[43] These efforts reflect her commitment to building arts communities by attracting new audiences and funding cultural preservation.[4]

Personal life

Marriage to Lachlan Murdoch

Sarah Murdoch, née O'Hare, met Lachlan Murdoch, the eldest son of media proprietor Rupert Murdoch, in Sydney during the late 1990s at a social event hosted by fashion designer Collette Dinnigan on a boat in Sydney Harbour.[44] Alternative accounts place their initial encounter at the Fringe Bar on Oxford Street in Sydney.[45] The couple began dating in 1998, following Lachlan's return from a trip to the United States.[9] They married on 27 March 1999 in a private ceremony at Cavan Station, the Murdoch family's expansive estate near Wee Jasper in New South Wales, Australia, approximately 15 miles from Canberra.[46][47] The event, attended by about 100 guests including Rupert Murdoch, proceeded despite heavy rain, with Rupert personally distributing umbrellas to participants; high-security measures included helicopter transport for arrivals, and the nuptials were held in a specially constructed chapel on the property.[47][48] Media coverage emphasized the union's exclusivity, with details not publicly confirmed until after the ceremony concluded.[46] The marriage has endured for over 25 years as of 2025, with the couple marking milestones such as their 20th anniversary in 2019 at a family gathering in Bondi, Australia.[49][50] No public separations or divorces have been reported, distinguishing it from other high-profile unions in the Murdoch family.[1]

Family and children

Sarah Murdoch and Lachlan Murdoch have three children. Their eldest child, son Kalan Alexander Murdoch, was born on November 9, 2004, in Sydney.[51] Their second son, Aidan Patrick Murdoch, was born on May 6, 2006, also in Sydney.[52] Their daughter, Aerin Elisabeth Murdoch, was born on April 12, 2010.[53] The family maintains a low public profile regarding the children, with limited details released about their upbringing or activities beyond occasional mentions in media coverage of family events.

Residences and lifestyle

Sarah Murdoch and her husband, Lachlan Murdoch, primarily reside in a palatial home in Bellevue Hill, Sydney, which they acquired for A$23 million.[54] The property underwent extensive renovations costing A$11.6 million between 2011 and 2014.[55] To safeguard their privacy and harbor views, the couple purchased adjacent properties in the area for A$2.63 million and A$4.4 million.[55][56] In 2021, they acquired a harborside boatshed in the nearby suburb of Point Piper for A$38.5 million, with plans for further development into additional living space.[57] Prior to these purchases, the family owned a waterfront residence in Point Piper, sold in 2005 for A$20.8 million, and a home in Bronte, sold in 2014 for A$10 million.[58][59] The Murdochs also own a 29-acre estate in Bel Air, Los Angeles, purchased in 2019 for US$150 million—the property famously used as the Clampett family mansion in the television series The Beverly Hillbillies.[60] However, the family relocated from California to Sydney in 2020, establishing Australia as their primary base.[61] Their lifestyle reflects the affluence of Sydney's eastern suburbs elite, with occasional public appearances at high-society events, such as a 2025 socialite birthday gathering where Sarah mingled with local influencers.[50] Despite their extensive property portfolio and Lachlan's role in global media, the couple prioritizes family privacy, avoiding ostentatious displays amid their estimated collective wealth exceeding billions.[62]

Involvement in Murdoch family affairs

Support for family media enterprises

Sarah Murdoch has contributed to Murdoch family media enterprises through her professional role as a television presenter on Foxtel, a pay-television platform majority-owned by News Corporation Australia.[63] She hosted Australia's Next Top Model from its fourth cycle in 2009 until 2010, a Foxtel-commissioned series produced in partnership with the network, which drew significant viewership and elevated the platform's entertainment profile.[64] This involvement predated but continued after her 1999 marriage to Lachlan Murdoch, executive chair of Fox Corporation and a key figure in the family's Australian media operations, aligning her career with family-controlled assets.[65] Her hosting tenure on Foxtel, which operates under News Corp's oversight, represented a direct professional endorsement of the enterprise's content strategy, focusing on reality television to compete in Australia's fragmented media market.[1] The show, renewed for multiple seasons under her presentation, generated revenue through advertising and subscriber retention for Foxtel, which reported annual revenues exceeding AUD 1.5 billion in the period. However, her association drew scrutiny following a live broadcast error in September 2010, where she announced the wrong winner, prompting Foxtel to end its production deal with the show's maker amid public backlash.[65] Despite this, no evidence indicates Sarah Murdoch held formal executive or strategic roles in News Corp or Fox entities, with her support manifesting primarily through on-air contributions rather than governance.[66]

Role in key decisions and succession events

Sarah Murdoch has served as a key personal advisor to her husband Lachlan Murdoch amid the protracted succession struggles within the Murdoch family empire, particularly influencing his alignment with the conservative editorial direction that bolstered his position as heir apparent. Her own political activities, including hosting the launch of Tony Abbott's successful 2013 federal election campaign as leader of Australia's Liberal Party, mirrored and reinforced Lachlan's shift toward a firmer conservative stance, which Rupert Murdoch cited as a reason to favor him over siblings like James, whose liberal views raised concerns about the future of outlets such as Fox News.[67][68] In pivotal career decisions, Sarah supported Lachlan's 2014 return to the family fold as non-executive co-chairman of 21st Century Fox after his 2005 exit amid disagreements with Rupert over strategy, a move that reacquainted him with operational leadership and positioned him for greater responsibility.[69][70] This reconnection proved instrumental during Rupert's 2023 announcement naming Lachlan sole chairman of News Corp and continuing CEO of Fox Corp, amid ongoing trust amendments favoring Lachlan's sole control.[44] The culmination of these events occurred on September 8, 2025, when the family resolved litigation over the irrevocable Murdoch Family Trust—established in 1999 to distribute voting shares equally among Prudence, Elisabeth, Lachlan, and James—through a $3.3 billion settlement. Lachlan personally financed the buyout of his siblings' stakes at $1.1 billion each, ensuring his exclusive voting power over Fox Corp and News Corp post-Rupert's death, with Sarah as his steadfast partner throughout the negotiations and family tensions.[71][68][66] This outcome preserved the empire's conservative influence, aligning with the ideological continuity Sarah helped sustain in Lachlan's decision-making.[63]

Public perception and controversies

Media gaffes and public incidents

In September 2010, Sarah Murdoch hosted the live finale of the sixth cycle of Australia's Next Top Model on Fox8, during which she announced Kelsey Martinovich as the winner of the public vote prize, prompting Martinovich to begin an acceptance speech.[64] Murdoch then interrupted, visibly distressed and stating on air, "I'm so sorry, there's been a massive mistake. I'm feeling sick about this. So sorry Kelsey, so sorry," before correcting that Amanda Ware was the actual winner.[72] The error stemmed from a human communications breakdown in which incorrect information was relayed to Murdoch, though Foxtel initially attributed the mistake to her before later clarifying it as a production fault not involving the host's assumption.[2] [73] The incident drew widespread media coverage as one of Australia's most notable live television blunders, with Murdoch appearing close to tears during the broadcast and later denying speculation that it was a publicity stunt.[74] [75] In response, Murdoch insisted on terminating the contract with ITV Studios Australia (formerly Granada Media Australia), the production company responsible for the series, leading Foxtel to award the next season to a different producer in November 2010.[76] The gaffe's aftermath included sealed-envelope protocols for the 2011 finale to prevent recurrence, and it contributed to perceptions of heightened scrutiny on Murdoch's on-air poise, though Ware later expressed no resentment toward the correction.[77]

Criticisms of influence on conservative media

Sources close to the Murdoch family reported that Sarah Murdoch, alongside Kathryn Murdoch (wife of James Murdoch), advocated for Fox News to terminate Bill O'Reilly's contract in April 2017 amid allegations of sexual harassment involving settlements exceeding $32 million paid by the network to multiple accusers.[78] O'Reilly, a prominent conservative commentator whose program The O'Reilly Factor averaged 4 million nightly viewers and anchored Fox's prime-time dominance, was ousted following advertiser boycotts and public scrutiny, marking a significant shift at the network.[79] The New York Daily News described Sarah Murdoch as leaning progressive politically, framing her involvement as part of a broader effort by the wives of Rupert Murdoch's sons to distance the outlet from figures embroiled in such controversies.[78] This episode drew implicit criticism from conservative observers who attributed O'Reilly's departure partly to internal pressures, including spousal influence, that prioritized sensitivity to harassment claims over retaining a key voice in right-leaning commentary.[78] However, the decision aligned with broader corporate responses to #MeToo-era accountability, as Fox faced risks of further financial and reputational damage from ongoing litigation and viewer exodus. Sarah Murdoch holds no formal executive role at Fox Corporation or News Corp, limiting documented instances of her direct media influence to familial advisory contexts.[80] Broader critiques of Murdoch family media often focus on Lachlan Murdoch's leadership rather than Sarah's peripheral involvement.[66]

Balanced views on political alignment

Sarah Murdoch's political alignment has been characterized as more progressive than the conservative bent of the Murdoch media empire, with reports indicating her influence in decisions diverging from traditional right-leaning stances. In 2017, alongside other family members' spouses, she was credited with advocating for the ouster of Fox News host Bill O'Reilly amid sexual harassment allegations, reflecting a left-leaning sensitivity to such issues within a network often aligned with conservative politics.[78] This perception aligns with broader observations that the wives of Rupert Murdoch's sons, including Sarah, lean left despite the family's media outlets' rightward tilt.[78] Countering this, her public actions remain tied to the family's conservative infrastructure through her marriage to Lachlan Murdoch, who is viewed as a steward of the empire's right-leaning legacy, including Fox News' role in U.S. conservative politics. No records of direct political donations from Sarah to partisan causes have surfaced, with her philanthropy emphasizing child welfare via the Murdoch Children's Research Institute rather than explicit ideological campaigns.[3] However, joint donations with Lachlan, such as the $1 million gift to Qtopia, Australia's inaugural queer museum in February 2023, have highlighted support for LGBTQ+ initiatives, prompting both praise for corporate backing of marginalized communities and internal tensions within progressive circles over the Murdoch brand's conservative associations.[81][82] Analysts note a nuanced family dynamic where Sarah's reported progressivism may moderate but not override the empire's conservative orientation, as evidenced by Lachlan's consolidation of control in September 2025 to preserve its political influence.[83] Lacking overt partisan endorsements from Sarah herself, interpretations often derive from these indirect involvements, underscoring a balance between personal inclinations and familial imperatives rather than rigid ideological commitment.[84]
