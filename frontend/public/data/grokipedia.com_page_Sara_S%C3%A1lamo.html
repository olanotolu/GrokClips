Sara Sálamo

Sara Ainhoa Concepción Sálamo (born 20 January 1992) is a Spanish actress born in Santa Cruz de Tenerife, Canary Islands.[1][2] Sálamo began her acting career in 2012 with appearances in the television series Águila Roja and Toledo.[3] She gained prominence through roles in Spanish television productions such as Arrayán, where she portrayed Esther, B&B, De boca en boca, and Brigada Costa del Sol, in which she played Yolanda.[4][5][6] In film, Sálamo received recognition for her performances in Perdida and Everybody Knows (2018), the latter directed by Asghar Farhadi.[1] She has also appeared in La Llamada (2017) and continued working in cinema and theater, accumulating credits under various directors over more than a decade.[1][7]

Early Life

Family and Upbringing

Sara Ainhoa Concepción Sálamo was born on January 20, 1992, in Santa Cruz de Tenerife, Canary Islands, Spain.[8] Her full surname reflects the Spanish naming convention, with Concepción as the paternal surname and Sálamo as the maternal surname.[9] Little public information exists regarding her parents' identities or professions beyond her father's involvement in filmmaking; at age 11, Sálamo dubbed a short film he directed, an early encounter with performance that sparked her interest in acting.[10] She has described her upbringing in a supportive household where her parents avoided invasiveness, allowing her considerable autonomy and freedom to explore her interests.[11] No verified details on siblings or extended family are widely documented in reputable sources. Sálamo was raised in Tenerife, where her family resided, fostering a deep connection to the island that persists in her personal reflections and creative work.[12] This environment, marked by familial encouragement rather than pressure, contributed to her early creative inclinations without formal constraints.[11]

Education and Early Influences

Sálamo's passion for acting originated in childhood, specifically at age 11 when she dubbed a short film directed by her father, David S. Alarcón, an experience that ignited her interest in performance and storytelling.[13] [14] This early involvement marked the beginning of her self-directed pursuit of acting, as she later described it as the "seed" for her career, motivating her to enroll in acting studies.[14] Upon turning 18 in 2010, Sálamo relocated from her native Tenerife to Madrid to advance her training and professional opportunities in the performing arts.[15] Between 2011 and 2012, she attended the Universidad Complutense de Madrid, though details of her specific program remain unspecified in available records.[16] Her formative years emphasized a rigorous approach to learning, with Sálamo recalling herself as a diligent student who valued education highly, often achieving high marks and deriving personal satisfaction from academic rigor.[17] These influences, rooted in familial creative exposure rather than formal institutional prestige, shaped her transition from amateur dubbing to professional debut roles by 2012.

Professional Career

Training and Debut Roles

Sálamo developed an early interest in acting after dubbing a short film directed by her father at age 11.[17] She participated in school theatre activities in Tenerife before relocating to Madrid at age 18 in 2010 to pursue professional training.[17] There, she enrolled in classes at La Barraca under actress Alicia Hermida, focusing on interpretive techniques, and explored various acting methodologies.[18][10] Her television debut occurred in 2012 with a minor role as the young Violante de Aragón in the series Toledo, cruce de destinos, secured through her first casting via agency Delphoss.[18] That same year, she appeared as Esther Machado in Arrayán for four months.[17] Subsequent early episodic roles included appearances in Águila Roja and Con el culo al aire.[18] Sálamo made her feature film debut in 2013 as Daniella, a Fine Arts student and love interest, in the teen drama Tres 60.[19] Her stage debut followed in 2015 with the role of a character in Joe Orton's comedy Lo que vio el mayordomo at Madrid's Teatro Infanta Isabel.[20]

Television Work

Sálamo's television career began with guest and recurring supporting roles in several Spanish series during the early 2010s. She portrayed Esther in the Canal Sur soap opera Arrayán, appearing in 69 episodes between March and October 2012.[21] Earlier, she featured in episodes of Toledo: Cruce de destinos on Antena 3, Aída on Telecinco as the Telecupón girl, Águila Roja as Beatriz, La que se avecina, and Con el culo al aire.[22] Her breakthrough in national television came with the role of Cayetana "Caye" Bornay in the Telecinco workplace comedy B&b, de boca en boca, where she appeared in 29 episodes across two seasons from February 2014 to December 2015.[21][23] Following this, she guest-starred as Doctora Aguilar in four episodes of Olmos y Robles on RTVE in 2015, and as Reme in three episodes of an unspecified series in 2016.[21] In the miniseries El caso. Crónica de sucesos (2016), Sálamo played Dolores Fosco, a key character in the Telecinco production based on real events from the Franco era.[21] She continued with guest appearances, including Clara Maqueda in one episode of BByC on Telecinco in 2018.[21] Sálamo took a prominent role as Yolanda "La Buhíta" in the Telecinco crime drama Brigada Costa del Sol, appearing in all 13 episodes of its single season in 2019.[21][23] In 2020, she featured in the Amazon Prime Video anthology Relatos con-fin-a-dos and a single-episode role as Sandra.[23][21]

Film Roles

Sálamo debuted in feature films in 2013 with the role of Daniela, a Fine Arts student assisting in an investigation, in the thriller Three-60, directed by Alejandro Ezkurdia.[24] In 2017, she portrayed Elisa in the drama Como la espuma.[22] Her visibility increased in 2018 through multiple projects, including Leonor in the mystery Las grietas de Jara and Claudia Marini, a central character in the disappearance thriller Perdida.[1] That year, she also played Rocío, the daughter of characters portrayed by Penélope Cruz and Javier Bardem, in Asghar Farhadi's family drama Everybody Knows. Subsequent roles encompassed Jenny in the action film El año de la furia (2020), appearances in El refugio (2021) and the comedy ¡A todo tren! Destino Asturias (2021), and Conchita in Pedro Almodóvar's 2023 short western Strange Way of Life, which starred Ethan Hawke and Pedro Pascal.[25] In 2024, Sálamo directed, wrote, and produced the short film La manzana, marking her debut behind the camera, though she did not act in it.

Other Professional Endeavors

Sálamo has expanded her career into directing, screenwriting, and film production. In addition to her acting roles, she founded Montauk Cinema, a production company based in Madrid and the Canary Islands, and Studios Montauk, through which she develops and produces independent projects.[16][1] As a director, Sálamo has helmed short films, including a 2023 project for Landmar Hotels that explores themes of memory, environmental concerns, and sea-inspired dreams, filmed on location at their Tenerife properties.[26] Her documentary-style feature En silencio, released in 2024, focuses on footballer Isco Alarcón's injury recovery and its emotional toll, with Sálamo serving as director, screenwriter, and producer; the film premiered at the Málaga Film Festival and emphasizes intimate, non-traditional sports storytelling.[27] In November 2024, Sálamo's directorial debut feature Hortelana received recognition at the Ventana México festival in Madrid, marking her transition to longer-form narrative work under her production banner. These endeavors reflect her multifaceted involvement in independent cinema, often blending personal themes with Canarian influences.[16]

Activism and Public Views

Animal Rights Advocacy

Sara Sálamo has publicly identified as an animal rights activist, emphasizing ethical treatment and reduced exploitation of animals in her lifestyle and public statements. She adopted an ovolactovegetarian diet approximately seven to eight years prior to 2020, abstaining from meat consumption while occasionally including eggs and dairy, motivated by a belief that unnecessary use of animals for food or appearance is avoidable.[28][29] In interviews, she has advocated for evolving societal norms to grant voice to animals incapable of self-advocacy, linking her activism to broader ethical progression.[30] Sálamo has collaborated with organizations such as Igualdad Animal on campaigns targeting specific animal welfare issues. In April 2022, she starred in and provided voiceover for a video campaign demanding a Spanish government ban on forced feeding of ducks and geese for foie gras production, highlighting the cruelty of the practice documented in Spanish farms.[31][32] She participated in a July 2020 interview series, Voces Animales, hosted by Igualdad Animal, discussing her commitment to animal equality and personal motivations for advocacy.[33] Her advocacy extends to promoting animal adoption and opposing their use in public events. Sálamo and her partner maintain a household with five adopted dogs, using social media to encourage adoption over purchase from breeders.[34] In January 2022, she criticized the inclusion of live animals in Three Kings Day parades, arguing against their forced participation in human festivities.[35] Similarly, in April 2024, she condemned the use of horses in Seville's Feria de Abril, asserting that animals do not consent to such exploitation.[36]

Feminist Stance and Gender Issues

Sara Sálamo has expressed strong support for feminism, describing it in January 2025 as "not an option, but a necessity" amid discussions on gender equality and societal pressures.[37] She has linked her advocacy to personal experiences of systemic violence, stating in December 2024 that "there is no woman who has not suffered systematic violence from childhood," attributing this to pervasive machismo that disadvantages both genders.[38] In November 2021, she received the "Culture Against Gender Violence" award from the Spanish government's delegation, where she emphasized that awareness of machista violence predates ideological commitments, drawing from early-life observations.[39] Sálamo has highlighted workplace inequalities in the entertainment industry, recounting in June 2020 that she encountered machismo as a young actress, often responding with tears due to inexperience rather than confrontation.[40] On International Women's Day in March 2021, she enumerated 12 specific disparities, including wage gaps, disproportionate mental load as a mother, workplace harassment, and public vejations tied to her partner's career, underscoring that equality cannot be "begged for" but must be demanded.[41][42] She has critiqued linguistic sexism, such as the derogatory use of "coñazo" in October 2018, arguing it perpetuates gender-based insults.[43] In response to public figures promoting traditional roles, Sálamo condemned Mexican footballer Javier Hernández's July 2025 comments on masculinity and women's responsibilities in July 2025, labeling them as endorsing "rancio" (stale) and systemic machismo that assigns women to caregiving while men lead, and called for FIFA intervention to uphold equality and respect.[44][45] Her criticism of Spanish football federation president Luis Rubiales's August 2023 non-consensual kiss of a player led to online threats, which she described in interviews as instilling real fear equivalent to street harassment, yet she maintained that feminism is not mere "marketing" but essential resistance.[46] In July 2021, she defended women's right to assertiveness, rejecting labels like "hysterical" for firm stances against inequality.[47] Sálamo's advocacy extends to practical initiatives, such as directing and producing a 2023 short film with an all-female technical team to challenge male dominance in film production, though this approach has been critiqued by some as prioritizing parity over merit.[48] Her public positions have provoked backlash on social media, including accusations of overreach, but she has persisted, noting in November 2024 that online aggression evokes tangible fear while affirming her commitment to speaking out selectively.[49][50]

Controversies

Opposition to Bullfighting

Sara Sálamo has been a vocal critic of bullfighting, framing it as institutionalized animal torture incompatible with modern ethical standards. In a 2021 interview with El País, she argued that bullfighting should be removed from Spanish cultural classification, stating, "We must hold a referendum in Spain on bullfighting and remove the bulls from culture because they are not," emphasizing that public subsidies and legal protections enable unnecessary suffering.[51] Her position aligns with broader animal welfare campaigns, including support for protests against the practice organized by groups like PACMA, where she highlighted the growing societal rejection of spectacles involving animal harm.[52] Public clashes have amplified her stance, particularly on social media. In May 2020, amid economic aid requests for bullfighting during the COVID-19 pandemic, Sálamo criticized torero Cayetano Rivera's petition for subsidies, prompting a Twitter exchange with him and fellow bullfighter Miguel Abellán, whom she accused of defending an outdated tradition at taxpayer expense.[53][54] Similar confrontations occurred in August 2020, when she denounced a bullfight at El Puerto de Santa María as "torture and murder," responding to critics by questioning the moral consistency of supporting animal rights selectively.[55][56] Her opposition drew significant backlash, including threats in August 2018 after she endorsed a petition to halt bullfighting instruction for minors, declaring she could not comprehend deriving pleasure from an animal's torture and death; she subsequently shared examples of the harassment received.[57][58] In August 2021, Sálamo labeled torero Enrique Ponce a "maltratador" (abuser) for his defense of killing bulls as essential to their existence, underscoring her view that such rationalizations perpetuate cruelty under cultural guise.[59][60] These incidents reflect her consistent advocacy, often intersecting with her animal rights activism, though they have fueled polarized debates in Spain where bullfighting retains regional support and economic significance.[61]

Comments on Traditional Gender Roles

In July 2025, Sálamo publicly criticized Mexican footballer Javier "Chicharito" Hernández for comments suggesting women were eradicating traditional masculinity by failing to fulfill roles such as caring for the home, cleaning, and supporting the family while men lead, describing his views as perpetuating "rancio, violento y sistémico" machismo (stale, violent, and systemic machismo).[45][44] She urged FIFA to penalize such statements, arguing they reinforced outdated gender hierarchies that harm societal progress.[62] Earlier, in February 2021, Sálamo expressed indignation over media framing of parental responsibilities that implied fathers merely "help" with childcare rather than share equal duties, rejecting the traditional assumption that child-rearing is primarily a maternal obligation.[63] She emphasized that both parents must actively parent, countering narratives that position women as default caregivers in family structures.[63] Sálamo has linked these critiques to broader feminist principles, stating in interviews that machismo disadvantages both genders by enforcing rigid roles, and affirming that feminism is "no es una opción, es una necesidad" (not an option, but a necessity) to dismantle them.[64] Her positions align with her self-identified feminist activism, though they have drawn backlash from those defending conventional family dynamics.[64]

Personal Life

Relationships and Marriage

Sara Sálamo began a relationship with Spanish footballer Francisco Alarcón Gutiérrez, known professionally as Isco, in 2017.[65] The couple confirmed their partnership publicly that year after Isco had been interacting with her social media posts for several months.[65] They have three children together: their first son, Theo Suárez Sálamo, born on July 11, 2019; their second son, Piero Suárez Sálamo, born on December 30, 2020; and a third child whose details have not been publicly specified.[66][67] On June 5, 2024, Sálamo and Isco announced their marriage via social media, revealing a private ceremony held days earlier after seven years together.[68][69] The event was intimate, with Isco attending on crutches due to a leg injury, and Sálamo wearing two dresses by designer Rosa Clará.[68][70]

Family and Children

Sara Sálamo and her partner Isco Alarcón welcomed their first son, Theo, on July 11, 2019. Their second son, Piero, was born on December 30, 2020.[71] [72] The family includes Alarcón's son Francisco from a prior relationship, forming a blended household of three children.[72] Sálamo has spoken publicly about the challenges of balancing her acting career with motherhood, noting her mother's assistance in childcare to manage professional commitments.[73]
