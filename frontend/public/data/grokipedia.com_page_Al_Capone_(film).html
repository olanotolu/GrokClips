Al Capone (film)

Al Capone is a 1959 American biographical crime film directed by Richard Wilson and starring Rod Steiger as the notorious Chicago gangster Alphonse Capone. The film traces Capone's ascent from a Brooklyn enforcer arriving in Chicago in 1919 to the pinnacle of the city's underworld during Prohibition, building an empire through bootlegging, gambling, and brutal enforcement, marked by events such as the 1929 St. Valentine's Day Massacre, before his 1931 conviction on tax evasion charges leading to imprisonment at Alcatraz.[1][2] Written by Malvin Wald and Henry F. Greenberg, the screenplay draws on historical accounts of Capone's operations under mentors like Johnny Torrio and his clashes with rivals, culminating in federal scrutiny that exploited fiscal vulnerabilities over direct Prohibition enforcement failures. Produced on a modest budget by Allied Artists Pictures and filmed primarily in Los Angeles with second-unit shots in Chicago, it premiered on February 25, 1959, in Miami and employed a gritty black-and-white cinematography style by Lucien Ballard to evoke documentary realism.[3][2] Rod Steiger's portrayal, involving physical transformation and intense Method acting, garnered acclaim for capturing Capone's charisma, volatility, and moral decay, though contemporary critics like Bosley Crowther described it as an "odious skunk" in a rehashed narrative, while later assessments highlight it as one of Steiger's defining early roles amid the film's pulp-infused biopic approach. Reception was mixed, with praise for promoting law and order from figures like Senator John L. McClellan, but critiques of its sensationalism and historical liberties in dramatizing causal chains of criminal success tied to policy gaps. The production avoided glamorizing the protagonist, emphasizing inevitable downfall from unchecked power and legal persistence, aligning with mid-century cinematic trends toward cautionary gangster tales post-Hays Code relaxations.[4][2][5]

Synopsis

Plot Summary

The film chronicles Al Capone's ascent in Chicago's underworld beginning in 1919, when the Brooklyn-born gangster arrives to serve as a bodyguard for racketeer Johnny Torrio, whose operations include bootlegging, gambling, and prostitution.[2] Following the onset of Prohibition in 1920, Capone expands the liquor trade, orchestrating the murder of Torrio's associate Big Jim Colosimo that year to consolidate control.[2] Capone's empire grows through violent territorial disputes, including the elimination of Irish gang leader Dion O'Bannion in 1924 and escalating conflicts with North Side boss Bugs Moran.[2] By the late 1920s, his operations generate over $20 million annually from illegal alcohol distribution amid ongoing gang wars.[2] A pivotal event is the St. Valentine's Day Massacre on February 14, 1929, where seven members of Moran's gang are machine-gunned in a garage, intensifying public and law enforcement scrutiny.[2] Internal betrayals and federal investigations culminate in Capone's 1931 conviction for income tax evasion, leading to his imprisonment at Alcatraz in October of that year.[2] The narrative frames his criminal dominance and downfall in a biographical style, highlighting the mechanics of his bootlegging syndicate and the role of violence in maintaining power.[1]

Production

Development and Pre-Production

The film Al Capone was conceived as a low-budget biographical crime drama by Allied Artists Pictures, a studio known for economical productions, with initial planning announced in November 1957 under the working title The Al Capone Story.[2] Producers John H. Burrows and Lindsley Parsons spearheaded early development, securing financing and distribution through Allied Artists, though Parsons was later replaced by Leonard J. Ackerman as the credited producer.[2] The screenplay, credited to Malvin Wald and Henry F. Greenberg, drew from verifiable historical events in Al Capone's life (1899â€“1947), including his ascent from Brooklyn enforcer to Chicago bootlegging kingpin amid Prohibition, without relying on copyrighted materials.[3] An earlier draft by Jack DeWitt was considered but unconfirmed in final credits; the Production Code Administration rejected the initial script in July 1958 for risking glorification of Capone's violence and success, prompting revisions that emphasized his eventual downfall via tax evasion conviction, with approval granted in September 1958.[2] Richard Wilson, a director with prior collaboration on Orson Welles's unfinished It's All True (1942), was selected to helm the project, adopting a semi-documentary style with narrated framing to underscore factual elements like the St. Valentine's Day Massacre, echoing 1930s gangster films such as Little Caesar (1931) while updating for 1950s public scrutiny of syndicates following the Kefauver Committee's 1950â€“1951 exposÃ©s on interstate crime.[6] The production operated on a constrained budget of approximately $550,000, prioritizing Capone's biography as a cautionary narrative of hubris-fueled empire-building, where federal alcohol prohibition from 1920â€“1933 inadvertently created lucrative illicit markets that empowered figures like Capone despite policy aims to reduce consumption and disorder.[7]

Casting and Performances

Rod Steiger was selected for the lead role of Al Capone due to his reputation as a Method actor capable of delivering intense, character-driven performances that captured the gangster's ruthless ambition and psychological depth.[3] His portrayal emphasized Capone's volatile temper and cunning charisma, employing dramatic gestures such as slinging a camel-hair coat over his shoulders to highlight the mobster's showy persona.[8] Fay Spain portrayed Maureen Flannery, a fictionalized mistress who provided an intimate perspective on Capone's personal life amid his criminal ascent.[1] James Gregory played Sgt. Schaeffer, the film's narrator and a determined lawman analogous to figures like Eliot Ness, offering a counterpoint to Capone's dominance through steadfast opposition.[1] Supporting actors contributed to the authentic depiction of Chicago's mob ecosystem, with Nehemiah Persoff as Johnny Torrio, Capone's mentor who introduced him to Prohibition-era bootlegging.[1] Persoff's restrained performance contrasted Steiger's explosive energy, underscoring the hierarchical dynamics within the underworld. Other ensemble members, including Martin Balsam as reporter Mac Keely, maintained a grounded tone that amplified the lead's intensity without overshadowing the narrative's focus on Capone's rise.[9]

Filming and Technical Details

The film was shot in black-and-white by cinematographer Lucien Ballard, who employed unobtrusive photography to capture gritty period details in a semi-documentary style.[10][11] Principal photography occurred primarily at Allied Artists Studios in Los Angeles, with backlots dressed to simulate 1920s Chicago settings amid the constraints of a low-budget production estimated at $550,000.[1] This necessitated a compressed schedule completed in 1958, prioritizing efficient staging of sparse sets that evoked Prohibition-era speakeasies and mob hideouts through practical construction rather than expansive recreations.[1][6] Editing by Walter Hannemann assembled the footage into a linear narrative tracing Capone's criminal ascent and downfall, incorporating voice-over narration to delineate key historical timelines and events with factual precision.[3] David Raksin composed the score, using tense orchestral motifs to heighten the dramatic tension of escalating gang conflicts and underscore the inexorable progression from minor infractions to systemic collapse.[3][12] While stock footage augmented establishing shots for era authenticity, violent sequences like the St. Valentine's Day Massacre relied on straightforward practical effects, including simulated gunfire and on-set choreography, to depict raw brutality within budgetary limits.[11]

Release

Premiere and Distribution

The film had its world premiere on February 25, 1959, in Miami, Florida.[3] It was released theatrically in the United States on March 25, 1959.[13] Allied Artists Pictures handled distribution in the United States and facilitated international releases, with the film opening in the United Kingdom on May 24, 1959, in France on June 12, 1959, and in other markets throughout 1959.[13][3] This rollout capitalized on ongoing public interest in Prohibition-era gangsters, amplified by contemporary media like the television series The Untouchables, which premiered in 1959 and drew from similar historical figures.[2] Marketing efforts included promotional stills, campaign press books, and lobby materials that emphasized the film's biographical roots in Al Capone's documented criminal career and Rod Steiger's portrayal of the gangster's ruthless ascent and downfall, positioning it as a stark, consequence-driven narrative rather than a glamorized crime tale.[14][15] The production received approval from the Motion Picture Production Code Administration in 1958â€“1959, navigating the era's standards on violence and crime depiction amid the Code's gradual relaxation, which allowed for more graphic elements than in earlier decades without triggering widespread local censorship challenges.[16]

Box Office Results

Al Capone was produced on a low budget by Allied Artists Pictures, a studio specializing in economical genre films, with contemporary accounts highlighting its spare production values despite competent execution.[6][1] Exact costs are undocumented in available records, but aligned with B-movie norms under $1 million, enabling quick recoupment potential through targeted distribution strategies. The film's commercial viability stemmed from Rod Steiger's intensifying profile as a dramatic lead, following acclaimed supporting turns in On the Waterfront (1954), which attracted niche audiences to this biographical gangster entry amid waning studio system reliance on star-driven draws. Released in early summer 1959, Al Capone generated sufficient domestic returns to profit Allied Artists, primarily via double features and drive-in circuits that amplified exposure for lower-tier releases without heavy marketing spends.[17] Its performance reflected causal constraints of the era's fragmented market, where competition from lavish epics like Ben-Hurâ€”which amassed over $36 million in rentalsâ€”diverted family and prestige viewers, limiting appeal to urban and genre enthusiasts.[18] International play added ancillary revenue, though precise figures are scarce, underscoring the film's role as a reliable earner for a specialty distributor rather than a blockbuster contender. Overall, the modest haul underscored B-pictures' resilience in profiting from efficient costs and opportunistic pairings amid 1959's $261 million domestic market total.[18]

Reception

Critical Reviews

Contemporary critics offered mixed assessments of Al Capone, frequently praising Rod Steiger's intense performance while critiquing the film's lack of originality in retelling the gangster's story. Bosley Crowther of The New York Times described the production as having a "strong documentary flavor" but faulted screenwriters Malvin Wald and Henry Greenberg for rehashing "the ancient history of the notorious Chicago gangster," rendering it redundant amid prior Capone depictions.[4] Nonetheless, Crowther highlighted Steiger's portrayal as an "odious skunk," emphasizing the actor's ability to embody the character's repugnant traits.[4] Aggregate critic scores reflect this ambivalence, with Rotten Tomatoes compiling a 56% approval rating from eight reviews, indicating middling reception equivalent to roughly 6/10 on numerical scales.[8] Reviews consistently lauded Steiger's Method-infused depiction of Capone's volatile persona, noting his dominance in scenes through boorish charm and brutality, though some observed the approach bordered on unsubtle excess.[8] Criticisms centered on the film's derivative narrative and modest production, with limited innovation in exploring Capone's ascent and downfall. Subsequent reevaluations, particularly in home video commentaries, have credited the film for its unflinching focus on Capone's savagery without romanticization, distinguishing it from more sympathetic gangster portrayals. DVD Savant reviewer Glenn Erickson commended Steiger's commanding presence and the consistent depiction of the mobster's scarred visage, underscoring the actor's scene-stealing vigor.[19] Time Out later assessed it as "one of the better gangster biopics," valuing its period authenticity and refusal to sanitize the protagonist's violence.[20] These perspectives highlight a pattern where Steiger's visceral criminal embodiment outweighed structural familiarity in bolstering the film's enduring, if niche, appeal among genre enthusiasts.

Audience and Contemporary Response

The 1959 release of Al Capone generated positive word-of-mouth among audiences, particularly drawn to Rod Steiger's forceful depiction of the titular gangster, which conveyed raw ambition and brutality without romanticization.[21] Viewers appreciated the film's unsparing focus on Capone's rise through violence and corruption, aligning with interest in authentic crime narratives amid the era's real-world scrutiny of syndicates.[1] Empirical data from user evaluations indicate solid reception, with an average IMDb rating of 6.7 out of 10 from 2,091 votes, positioning it as a reliable genre piece rather than a blockbuster.[22] This feedback underscores appreciation for the movie's chronicle of Capone's ascent and downfall, including depictions of gang conflicts and governmental responses.[21] Contemporary viewership reflected broader 1950s shifts toward condemning organized crime, fueled by the McClellan Committee's 1957â€“1959 hearings that exposed mob operations and invoked figures like Capone's former associates.[23] Audiences engaged with the film's portrayal of Prohibition's direct causation of illicit empires, viewing it as a cautionary extension of ongoing public reckonings with racketeering's societal costs.[1] While some expressed reservations over its intense violence, the overall response affirmed its role in satisfying demand for stark, consequence-driven mob tales.[21]

Legal Issues

Lawsuit from Capone's Family

In December 1959, Mafalda Capone Maritote, sister of the late gangster Alphonse "Al" Capone and executrix of his estate, filed a $10 million lawsuit against Allied Artists Pictures, the film's distributor, along with other involved parties, claiming invasion of privacy, unauthorized appropriation of Capone's name, likeness, and life story, and resulting emotional distress to surviving family members.[24][25] The complaint argued that the production failed to obtain family permission for depicting Capone's criminal activities and personal history, treating it as a proprietary right belonging to the estate rather than public domain material.[24] The suit paralleled a separate, contemporaneous action by Capone's widow Mae and son Albert against Desilu Productions over a television portrayal, reflecting broader family efforts to control narratives of Al Capone's legacy amid rising media interest in his era.[24] However, in 1962, a federal judge dismissed Maritote's claims against the filmmakers, determining that Capone's status as a notorious public figure during his lifetime negated posthumous privacy rights for his estate and that First Amendment protections shielded biographical depictions of historical criminals from such restrictions.[26][27] This ruling established a key precedent in U.S. entertainment law, affirming that the life stories of deceased public figuresâ€”particularly those involved in high-profile crimesâ€”remain fair subjects for unauthorized films without constituting actionable privacy invasions, thereby facilitating future biopics on figures like Capone without mandatory familial approval.[26][24] The decision underscored the limits of privacy claims in commercial speech, prioritizing expressive freedoms over estate interests in notoriety-derived narratives.[28]

Historical Fidelity

Accurate Depictions of Events

The film correctly portrays Al Capone's relocation to Chicago in 1919 at the invitation of his mentor Johnny Torrio, who had established a bootlegging network following the enactment of Prohibition under the Volstead Act on January 17, 1920, which banned the production and sale of alcohol and incentivized black-market operations by creating high-profit opportunities for organized crime syndicates to supply illicit liquor through speakeasies and smuggling.[29][30] Capone's role in scaling Torrio's operations, including violent enforcement against competitors, aligns with historical records of the Chicago Outfit's dominance in the city's underworld, where bootlegging generated millions in untaxed revenue and fueled territorial wars.[29] A key accurate sequence depicts the St. Valentine's Day Massacre on February 14, 1929, as a calculated retaliation by Capone's subordinates against the rival North Side Gang led by George "Bugs" Moran, resulting in the execution-style killing of seven men in a Lincoln Park garage, which exemplified the Outfit's use of impersonated police to execute rivals while minimizing direct traceability to Capone himself.[31] This event, planned from afar to avoid personal involvement, reflected the causal dynamics of Prohibition-era gang conflicts, where control over alcohol distribution routes necessitated preemptive violence against encroaching factions, contributing to Chicago's elevated homicide rates during the 1920s bootlegging wars that claimed hundreds of lives among gang members.[32] The portrayal of Capone's 1931 downfall via federal income tax evasion charges mirrors the U.S. Treasury Department's strategy under Prohibition Director Eliot Ness and IRS agents, who, unable to secure convictions for bootlegging or murders due to witness intimidation and corruption, pursued financial records to prove unreported income exceeding $1 million from 1924 to 1929, leading to his conviction on five counts on October 17, 1931, and an 11-year sentence.[33][29] This approach exploited the Outfit's reliance on cash-based, undocumented transactions, underscoring how legal vulnerabilities in tax compliance proved decisive against otherwise insulated mob figures whose operations thrived on Prohibition's prohibition of legitimate economic channels.[34]

Inaccuracies and Fictional Elements

The film introduces a fictional romantic subplot involving Capone's relationship with Maureen Flannigan, portrayed by Fay Spain as a prostitute who becomes his mistress and influences his decisions, which has no basis in historical accounts of Capone's personal life.[29] Capone was married to Mae Coughlin from December 30, 1918, until his death in 1947, and biographies emphasize his family-oriented domesticity in later years rather than tawdry extramarital affairs central to the plot.[29] This invention dramatizes Capone's character through personal vice, altering the causal narrative of his rise by attributing emotional motivations absent from records focused on business alliances and violence.[35] The depiction compresses and simplifies gang rivalries, portraying events like the 1919 Chicago Race Riot and early bootlegging wars as immediate precursors to Capone's dominance, whereas historically, Capone arrived in Chicago around 1920 as Johnny Torrio's subordinate, with turf battles escalating gradually into the mid-1920s.[29] The St. Valentine's Day Massacre on February 14, 1929, is shown as a direct culmination of Capone's lone orchestration against rivals like Bugs Moran, but investigations attributed it to his orders without evidence of his personal presence, and the film elides the multi-year buildup involving alliances beyond Italian factions.[29] Such timeline alterations prioritize dramatic pacing over the incremental consolidation of power through ethnic syndicates, including Irish and Jewish gangs, which facilitated Capone's operations.[36] Capone's ascent is framed as primarily driven by individual ambition and brutality, underplaying the structured hierarchy of the Chicago Outfit, where he operated as Torrio's lieutenant within an Italian-American network that coordinated bootlegging, gambling, and extortion across the Midwest.[37] The film minimizes inter-ethnic collaborations, such as those with Jewish mobsters like Jake Guzik, presenting Capone as a near-autonomous figure rather than a key player in a syndicate that Torrio imported from New York, which distributed profits upward and relied on collective enforcement.[38] This lone-wolf emphasis distorts causal realism by ignoring how organized alliances, not solo ruthlessness, scaled operations amid Prohibition's demand surge. While the voice-over narration acknowledges some graft, it glosses over systemic government corruption that enabled Capone's empire, including bribed police and judges who overlooked violations until federal intervention.[39] Chicago officials under Mayor William "Big Bill" Thompson tolerated mob activities, with Capone's payments to politicians shielding breweries and speakeasies from raids, a factor the film subordinates to Capone's inherent criminality.[32] The film omits deeper exploration of Prohibition's origins in the temperance movement, which through organizations like the Anti-Saloon League lobbied for the 18th Amendment ratified January 16, 1919, to curb alcohol as a social ill rooted in Protestant moral reform.[40] Instead, it portrays bootlegging as stemming from Capone's moral failings amid opportunity, framing crime as personal pathology rather than a response to legislative causation that created black markets profiting syndicates.[41] This selective lens counters romanticized gangster tropes but sidesteps how temperance-driven policy, not innate vice, generated the economic incentives for Capone's violence.[42]

Legacy and Impact

Influence on Gangster Films

The 1959 film Al Capone employed a semi-documentary format, utilizing voice-over narration and chronological recounting of historical events to depict the gangster's ascent and decline during Prohibition, echoing stylistic elements from earlier Warner Bros. productions like The Public Enemy (1931) while adapting them to post-Hays Code sensibilities.[6] This approach underscored the causal consequences of criminal ambitionâ€”violence, betrayal, and inevitable legal reckoningâ€”rather than romanticizing underworld success, thereby reinforcing a genre shift away from pre-Code glamour toward cautionary realism.[1] Rod Steiger's Method acting-infused performance as Capone portrayed the mobster as a volatile, psychologically tormented figure whose pursuit of dominance exacted a personal toll, establishing a template for anti-heroic gangster roles that delved into inner turmoil over mere bravado.[43] This characterization prefigured the introspective mob portrayals in 1970s New Hollywood films, where actors like Marlon Brando and Al Pacino in The Godfather (1972) similarly explored the corrosive effects of power through immersive, Stanislavski-derived techniques.[43] By framing Capone's empire as a byproduct of Prohibition's black-market incentivesâ€”federal alcohol bans that supplanted legitimate enterprise with illicit syndicatesâ€”the film advanced narratives critiquing policy-induced crime waves, portraying government enforcement failures as enabling rather than curbing organized vice.[44] Such depictions aligned with broader post-Prohibition discourse on regulatory overreach, influencing later gangster works that demythologized mob allure by emphasizing systemic enablers and retributive justice over heroic individualism.[1]

Availability and Modern Assessments

The film received a DVD release on March 23, 2009, through Warner Bros., marking its primary entry into home video distribution following earlier VHS formats in the preceding decades.[45][46] No 4K UHD or significant Blu-ray restoration has been produced or announced as of October 2025, limiting high-definition access to standard-definition transfers. Digital availability includes rental and purchase options on platforms such as Amazon Video, Apple TV, and Fandango at Home, with occasional free full-length viewings appearing on YouTube via unofficial uploads.[47][48] These formats have sustained its accessibility to niche audiences, including enthusiasts of pre-Code gangster cinema and biographical dramas, without widespread remastering efforts from rights holders like Allied Artists Pictures' successors. In a 2010 assessment by DVD Savant, the film was characterized as a "solid show" for its unpretentious depiction of Capone's criminal ascent, prioritizing raw historical events over stylistic flourishes or profound societal analysis, though critiqued for mechanical scripting and limited revelations about Prohibition-era crime dynamics.[19] Later reviews from the 2010s echoed this, praising Rod Steiger's visceral, unromanticized portrayal of Capone's brutality and ambition as a standout amid the film's low-budget constraints, while noting dated visual effects and sets that reflect its 1959 production values rather than contemporary polish.[45][5] Such evaluations position Al Capone as a straightforward biopic that demystifies its subject through factual recounting of events like the St. Valentine's Day Massacre and tax evasion prosecution, contrasting with more sensationalized later depictions, without delving into causal debates on socioeconomic factors in organized crime.[49] Its enduring appeal lies in this factual restraint, appealing to viewers seeking unvarnished accounts over mythologized narratives.
