Italy–Spain relations

Italy–Spain relations comprise the diplomatic, economic, cultural, and security ties between the Italian Republic and the Kingdom of Spain, two sovereign states with profound historical connections originating in ancient Mediterranean civilizations and reinforced by shared Roman imperial legacies.[1] These relations are characterized by mutual interests in regional stability, given their geographic proximity across the Mediterranean Sea, and by linguistic and religious affinities as Romance-language-speaking nations with predominantly Catholic populations.[1] In the contemporary era, Italy and Spain collaborate extensively within multilateral frameworks, with Italy as a founding member of NATO in 1949 and the European Economic Community in 1957, and Spain joining NATO in 1982 and the European Communities in 1986, enabling joint positions on Euro-Atlantic security, migration management, and EU integration.[2][3] Economically, bilateral trade remains robust, with Spain's exports to Italy totaling key sectors such as automobiles ($5.05 billion) and olive oil ($1.13 billion) in 2023, underpinned by over 2,000 Italian firms operating in Spain that generate 690,000 jobs and substantial turnover.[4][5] Efforts to deepen ties include negotiations for a treaty of enhanced cooperation announced in 2022, reflecting strategic alignment on Mediterranean issues and European economic resilience.[6] Historically, periods of union under Habsburg rule in the 16th–18th centuries and alignments during the interwar fascist era shaped interactions, though post-World War II democratization shifted focus to democratic consolidation and EU-oriented partnership, avoiding overreliance on ideologically charged narratives from potentially biased academic sources.[1]

Historical Background

Ancient and Shared Roman Heritage

The Roman civilization, originating in the central Italian peninsula around the 8th century BC, expanded its influence to the Iberian Peninsula—later termed Hispania—beginning in 218 BC during the Second Punic War against Carthage. Roman forces initially seized Carthaginian-held territories in southern and eastern Hispania following victories such as the Battle of Ilipa in 206 BC under Scipio Africanus, establishing initial footholds like the colony at Italica near modern Seville.[7][8] Prolonged resistance from Celtiberian, Lusitanian, and other indigenous groups delayed full control, with major campaigns including the Celtiberian Wars (181–133 BC) and the Cantabrian Wars (29–19 BC), culminating in Augustus's pacification of the northern regions by 19 BC.[7][8] This conquest integrated Hispania as key provinces (Hispania Tarraconensis, Baetica, and Lusitania), fostering administrative ties to Rome through governors, legions, and infrastructure like the Via Augusta road network linking Italian-style urban centers.[9] Romanization profoundly shaped Hispania, disseminating Latin language, legal systems, and cultural practices from the Italian core, with elite locals adopting Roman citizenship and customs to facilitate governance and trade.[9] By the 1st century AD, cities such as Tarraco (Tarragona) and Emerita Augusta (Mérida) mirrored Roman urban models from Italy, featuring amphitheaters, aqueducts, and forums that endured as architectural legacies.[9] Hispania produced influential figures integrated into the imperial center, including three emperors—Trajan (r. 98–117 AD), Hadrian (r. 117–138 AD), both born in Italica, and Theodosius I (r. 379–395 AD)—who ruled from Rome and extended Roman engineering and law across the empire, reinforcing bidirectional cultural flows.[9] These provincial leaders exemplified Hispania's elite assimilation, with Trajan's expansionist policies and Hadrian's wall-building echoing Roman traditions originating in Italy. This shared Roman foundation laid enduring commonalities between the Italian and Iberian regions, evident in the evolution of Vulgar Latin into modern Italian and Spanish—Romance languages retaining over 80% lexical similarity—and in parallel inheritances of Roman civil law, municipal governance, and Christianization via the Latin Church centered in Rome.[9] Post-empire fragmentation preserved these elements, distinguishing both areas from non-Romanized Europe and underpinning later cultural affinities despite medieval divergences.[9]

Medieval and Early Modern Interactions

The medieval interactions between the Italian peninsula and the Iberian kingdoms were predominantly shaped by the expansionist policies of the Crown of Aragon, which exerted direct political control over key southern Italian territories. The Sicilian Vespers revolt erupted on March 30, 1282, against the Angevin rulers imposed by Charles I of Anjou, prompting an invitation to Peter III of Aragon, whose wife Constance was a Hohenstaufen heiress with claims to the Sicilian throne. Peter III's subsequent invasion and military campaigns established Aragonese sovereignty over the island, initiating a dynasty that ruled Sicily until the late 15th century and integrating it into Aragon's Mediterranean empire.[10] [11] This dominion extended northward in the 15th century under Alfonso V of Aragon, dubbed the Magnanimous, who capitalized on dynastic disputes in the Kingdom of Naples. Amid conflicts involving Queen Joanna II and rival claimants, Alfonso launched campaigns from 1421 onward; after capturing Naples on June 2, 1442, following a siege and naval engagements, he unified Sicily and Naples as viceregal dependencies of the Crown of Aragon.[12] This conquest not only consolidated Aragonese influence in southern Italy but also shifted the royal court to Naples, where Alfonso fostered a vibrant cultural hub blending Iberian and Italian elements until his death in 1458.[13] Economic ties underpinned these political unions, with Mediterranean trade networks linking Italian city-states to Aragonese ports. Catalan merchants from Barcelona and Valencia competed with Genoese and Venetian traders in Levantine commerce, importing spices and exporting textiles, while the incorporation of Sicily boosted Aragonese grain shipments to Italy and wool from Castile to northern textile centers like Florence and Milan.[14] [15] Aragonese rule introduced Catalan administrative practices and fiscal systems to southern Italy, evidenced by bilingual documentation in courts at Palermo and Naples.[16] Cultural exchanges were evident in the Iberian imprint on southern Italian society, particularly through royal intermarriages and pilgrimage routes. Norman kings of Sicily, such as Roger II and Frederick II, wed Iberian princesses in the 12th and 13th centuries, facilitating the transmission of Mozarabic artistic motifs and Islamic-influenced courtly customs to the mainland.[17] Under Aragonese governance, southern Italian architecture and governance adopted Catalan elements, including fortified palaces and notarial traditions, though these were often adapted to local feudal structures rather than imposed wholesale.[18] In the early modern prelude before the Habsburg inheritance, these relations evolved amid rising tensions in the Italian Wars. Ferdinand II of Aragon, after unifying Castile and Aragon in 1479, intervened in peninsular affairs to protect Aragonese holdings, forming alliances like the 1495 League of Venice against French incursions, which preserved Spanish interests in Naples and Sicily without yet merging them into a broader imperial framework.[19] Trade persisted, with Italian financiers funding Aragonese naval expeditions, but growing Ottoman threats in the Mediterranean shifted focus toward defensive coalitions rather than expansion.[20]

Habsburg Dynasty and Spanish Dominion in Italy

The Habsburg dynasty's ascension to the Spanish throne with Charles I (also Holy Roman Emperor Charles V) in 1516 integrated Italian territories into the Spanish orbit through dynastic inheritance, strategic marriages, and military victories in the Italian Wars (1494–1559). Spain gained effective control over the Kingdom of Naples in 1504 following the expulsion of French forces, establishing a viceregal system under Spanish monarchs that endured until 1713.[21] The Duchy of Milan similarly fell under Habsburg Spanish rule in 1535 after the extinction of the Sforza line, with Charles V assuming direct authority and appointing governors to administer it as a frontier province.[22] Sicily and Sardinia, already linked to the Aragonese crown, remained integral to Spanish possessions, providing naval and logistical support against Ottoman incursions in the Mediterranean.[23] Administration of these Italian states relied on viceroys and governors dispatched from Madrid, who maintained local feudal structures while enforcing fiscal policies to fund Spain's broader imperial commitments, including wars in Europe and the Americas. This extractive approach imposed heavy taxes and trade restrictions, straining local economies and sparking periodic resistance, as evidenced by the 1647 Neapolitan revolt led by fisherman Tommaso Aniello (Masaniello), which erupted over a tax on fruit vendors and escalated into a broader challenge to Spanish viceregal authority amid the fiscal burdens of the Thirty Years' War.[24] [25] The revolt, though suppressed after nine months, highlighted underlying tensions between Spanish overlords and Italian subjects, with viceroys often relying on alliances with local nobility to preserve order. Militarily, these territories bolstered Spain's position in Italy, deterring French ambitions and facilitating Habsburg influence over the Papal States and other principalities. Spanish dominion facilitated cultural and artistic exchanges, with viceregal courts patronizing Baroque styles that blended Iberian and Italian traditions, influencing architecture and painting through imported artists and shared Catholic iconography.[26] However, the impact was uneven, marked by Spanish diplomatic presence in non-directly ruled states like Tuscany and Venice, where ambassadors shaped alliances amid fragile control.[27] This era of Spanish hegemony in Italy ended with the War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714), as the Bourbon claimant Philip V's ascension prompted Allied intervention; the Treaty of Utrecht in 1713 transferred Milan, Naples, Sardinia, and the Presidi of Tuscany to Austria, severing direct Spanish ties and redistributing Habsburg Italian assets.[28] Subsequent treaties at Rastatt and Baden in 1714 confirmed these shifts, initiating Austrian Habsburg rule over former Spanish Italian domains until mid-century Bourbon reconquests.[29]

Diplomatic and Political Evolution

Establishment of Modern Relations

The unification of Italy, culminating in the proclamation of the Kingdom of Italy on March 17, 1861, under King Victor Emmanuel II, prompted the establishment of formal diplomatic relations with Spain, which occurred in 1865.[30] This step followed Spain's implicit recognition of the new Italian state amid European powers' responses to the Risorgimento, as both nations shared Bourbon and Savoy dynastic ties with broader monarchical Europe.[31] The timing reflected Spain's Queen Isabella II's government seeking stability in Mediterranean alliances, while Italy aimed to legitimize its sovereignty through bilateral engagements.[32] Early modern relations emphasized mutual Catholic interests and trade resumption, unencumbered by the prior Habsburg-era dominions that had ended with the War of the Spanish Succession in 1713–1714.[31] Diplomatic exchanges included the appointment of envoys, with Spain maintaining a legation in Turin (Italy's initial capital) before relocating to Rome after 1871.[33] No major territorial disputes arose, as Spain focused on its domestic Carlist Wars (1833–1876) and Italian irredentism targeted Austrian holdings rather than Spanish territories.[34] Bilateral commerce grew modestly, centered on Mediterranean ports, with Italy exporting silk and machinery while importing Spanish wines and minerals, though volumes remained secondary to ties with France and Britain.[35] By the 1870s, relations solidified through shared opposition to revolutionary upheavals, as evidenced by Spain's 1868 Glorious Revolution paralleling Italy's internal unification challenges, yet without straining ties.[36] The 1880s saw informal naval cooperation in the western Mediterranean, reflecting converging interests in countering French expansionism post-1870 Franco-Prussian War.[37] These foundations laid the groundwork for 20th-century alignments, prioritizing anti-republican solidarity over colonial rivalries, such as Spain's Moroccan protectorate (1912) and Italy's Libyan conquest (1911–1912).[34]

World War I and Interwar Alignments

During World War I, Spain adhered to a policy of strict neutrality declared on August 7, 1914, which it maintained until the armistice on November 11, 1918, despite internal pressures from pro-Allied and pro-Central Powers factions and economic opportunities from wartime trade.[38] In contrast, Italy initially bound by the Triple Alliance, negotiated the secret Treaty of London on April 26, 1915, and entered the war against Austria-Hungary on May 23, 1915, committing over 5 million troops and suffering approximately 650,000 military deaths by 1918.[39] This fundamental divergence—Spain's non-belligerence versus Italy's alignment with the Entente—limited bilateral diplomatic initiatives, though Spain's neutrality facilitated indirect economic interactions, including exports of foodstuffs and raw materials to Italy amid Allied blockades.[40] In the interwar period, both nations grappled with postwar economic dislocations and rising authoritarianism, fostering a temporary convergence in political alignments. Italy's Fascist regime under Benito Mussolini, consolidated after the March on Rome in October 1922, inspired similar conservative military responses in Spain, where General Miguel Primo de Rivera, with King Alfonso XIII's endorsement, proclaimed a dictatorship on September 13, 1923, suspending the constitution and prioritizing stability over parliamentary governance.[41] Relations warmed between the two dictators; Primo de Rivera publicly praised Mussolini during his first foreign visit as head of state in 1923 and engaged in trilateral discussions involving Italian and Vatican representatives to align anti-socialist policies. [42] Diplomatic and commercial ties strengthened in the mid-1920s, with Italy and Spain signing a treaty in 1926 that enhanced mutual interests in the Mediterranean, including provisions for closer economic cooperation and potential strategic alignment, though its full terms remained confidential until registered with the League of Nations.[43] This accord addressed prior disruptions in trade dating to 1905, resuming exchanges in goods like Italian machinery for Spanish agricultural products, amid shared concerns over Bolshevik influences.[44] However, no formal military pact emerged, and relations yielded limited transformative outcomes beyond rhetorical solidarity between the regimes.[45] Primo de Rivera's dictatorship collapsed in January 1930 amid economic depression and opposition, leading to municipal elections in April 1931 that precipitated the Second Spanish Republic's proclamation on April 14, 1931.[46] The republican government's left-leaning reforms, including secularization and land redistribution, contrasted sharply with Italy's entrenched fascism, cooling bilateral warmth and introducing ideological frictions.[47] Nonetheless, cultural diplomacy persisted, with interwar agreements promoting intellectual exchanges as a tool for soft influence, exemplified by Italy's model of state-directed cultural pacts that Spain engaged selectively.[48] Italy's invasion of Ethiopia on October 3, 1935, further highlighted diverging paths, as the League of Nations imposed sanctions on Italy, while Spain under the radical-left coalition from 1936 adopted a more critical stance toward fascist expansionism.[45]

Spanish Civil War: Italian Support for Nationalists

Following the Nationalist uprising against the Spanish Republic on July 17, 1936, Italian dictator Benito Mussolini quickly authorized military aid to General Francisco Franco's forces, viewing the conflict as an opportunity to counter Soviet-backed communism and extend fascist influence in the Mediterranean.[49] Initial support included the dispatch of 12 Fiat CR.32 fighter aircraft on July 29, 1936, marking the start of Italy's intervention.[50] Mussolini's motivations encompassed ideological opposition to Bolshevism, strategic ambitions to control key sea routes, and a desire to demonstrate military prowess to Adolf Hitler, thereby strengthening the Rome-Berlin axis.[51] The bulk of Italian ground involvement came through the Corpo Truppe Volontarie (CTV), an expeditionary force officially framed as volunteers but directed by the Italian state, which peaked at approximately 50,000 to 75,000 troops organized into motorized infantry divisions.[52] Over the course of the war, Italy committed around 80,000 personnel in total, including 45,000 army troops, 6,000 from the air force, and 29,000 Blackshirt militiamen, supported by 758 aircraft from the Aviazione Legionaria, artillery, tanks, and vehicles.[49] These forces enabled early Nationalist successes, such as the occupation of Majorca in August 1936 and the capture of Málaga on February 6, 1937, where Italian units played a pivotal role in the rapid advance.[53] A major setback occurred during the Battle of Guadalajara from March 8 to 23, 1937, when roughly 35,000 CTV troops, alongside Spanish Nationalists, attempted to encircle Madrid but were halted and counterattacked by Republican International Brigades and Soviet-supplied T-26 tanks, resulting in over 2,000 Italian casualties and exposing deficiencies in Italian command, logistics, and equipment.[54] Despite this defeat, which Mussolini attributed to poor coordination rather than systemic weaknesses, Italian air superiority—providing bombing and reconnaissance—continued to bolster Franco's campaigns in subsequent offensives like Santander and the Ebro.[49] Italian aid, estimated to have included 3,800 artillery pieces and significant munitions shipments, proved decisive in sustaining the Nationalists' material edge against the Republic's arms procurement challenges under the Non-Intervention Agreement, ultimately contributing to Franco's victory on March 28, 1939.[49] This intervention not only forged a personal rapport between Mussolini and Franco but also tested fascist military doctrines, revealing vulnerabilities that foreshadowed later performances in World War II, while enhancing Italy's geopolitical leverage in post-war Iberian affairs.[51]

World War II: Divergent Paths and Neutrality

Italy entered World War II on the Axis side on June 10, 1940, declaring war on France and the United Kingdom, thereby committing to aggressive expansion alongside Nazi Germany.[55] In response, Spain under Francisco Franco, which had emerged victorious in the Spanish Civil War with significant Italian military aid including over 75,000 troops and substantial aircraft, adopted a policy of non-belligerency on June 13, 1940, signaling sympathy for the Axis without direct participation.[56] This stance reflected Spain's exhaustion from its 1936–1939 civil war, acute economic shortages, and Franco's pragmatic calculation that entry would risk invasion or collapse rather than gain, despite ideological alignment with fascist Italy.[57] The divergent paths strained but did not sever bilateral ties, as Mussolini repeatedly pressed Franco to join the war to secure Gibraltar and the Mediterranean flank, viewing Spanish neutrality as ingratitude for prior support.[58] A key effort occurred at the Bordighera meeting on February 11–14, 1941, where Franco conferred with Mussolini, Foreign Minister Galeazzo Ciano, and his brother-in-law Ramón Serrano Suñer, discussing potential entry in exchange for territories like French Morocco and Oran; however, Franco remained evasive, prioritizing aid over commitment.[59] Mussolini's frustration grew evident, as Italy bore the burdens of campaigns in Greece, North Africa, and the Balkans without Spanish assistance, while Franco leveraged Axis overtures for economic concessions like wheat imports to avert famine.[60] Limited cooperation persisted, including Spain's tolerance of Italian diplomatic initiatives and indirect support via the Blue Division of 47,000 volunteers sent to fight Soviets on the Eastern Front from July 1941, demonstrating solidarity without broader belligerency.[61] As Allied fortunes turned, particularly after the Italian armistice on September 8, 1943, Franco shifted to strict neutrality on October 1, 1943, withdrawing the Blue Division and curtailing Axis activities to appease the Western powers, further diverging from Italy's collapsing Axis commitment.[57] This policy preserved Franco's regime amid Italy's descent into civil war and occupation, maintaining formal diplomatic relations with Mussolini's Italian Social Republic until war's end.[62]

Cold War: Anti-Communist Alliance and NATO Integration

Following the end of World War II, both Italy and Spain adopted staunch anti-communist stances amid the emerging bipolar confrontation. Italy's Christian Democratic governments, led by Prime Minister Alcide De Gasperi, prioritized integration into Western institutions to counter domestic communist influence from the Italian Communist Party, which held significant parliamentary seats and organized labor support. On April 4, 1949, Italy became a founding member of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO), committing to collective defense against Soviet expansionism despite internal divisions, including opposition from leftist parties. Spain, under General Francisco Franco's regime—victorious against Republican forces including communist militias in the 1936–1939 Civil War—faced ostracism from the United Nations and early Western alliances due to its Axis sympathies during the war, yet maintained an explicitly anti-communist orientation, viewing the Soviet Union as an existential threat. Franco's government secured a bilateral military agreement with the United States via the Pact of Madrid on September 26, 1953, granting U.S. access to Spanish bases in exchange for economic aid, effectively aligning Spain with the Western bloc without formal NATO entry.[63] Bilateral ties between Italy and Spain, rooted in shared Mediterranean interests and opposition to communism, saw gradual strengthening despite Spain's pariah status. Diplomatic relations, re-established post-war, facilitated cultural and economic exchanges; a notable milestone was the Cultural Treaty of Rome signed on November 22, 1955, which promoted collaboration in education, arts, and scientific research, reflecting mutual interest in bolstering non-communist cultural identities. Trade volumes grew modestly in the 1950s and 1960s, with Italy exporting machinery and chemicals to Spain in return for agricultural products, though constrained by Spain's autarkic policies and international sanctions until the late 1950s. Military cooperation remained limited, overshadowed by U.S.-centric arrangements, but both nations coordinated informally on anti-communist intelligence sharing within broader Western networks, including operations against subversion. Italy's democratic framework distanced it from overt endorsement of Franco's authoritarianism, yet pragmatic alignment prevailed, as evidenced by Italian support for Spain's 1955 United Nations admission alongside other Western states, easing Madrid's isolation.[64] Spain's transition to democracy following Franco's death on November 20, 1975, under King Juan Carlos I and Prime Minister Adolfo Suárez, accelerated its integration into NATO, paralleling Italy's earlier path. Amid heightened Cold War tensions, including the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in 1979, Spain applied for NATO membership in 1977; Italy, as an established member, backed this move within alliance consultations, viewing it as reinforcement of the southern flank against potential Warsaw Pact incursions into the Mediterranean. Spain acceded as NATO's 16th member on May 30, 1982, after parliamentary ratification on October 29, 1981, despite domestic protests from socialist and communist groups. This integration fostered joint military exercises and standardized defense planning between Italian and Spanish forces, solidifying their roles in NATO's anti-communist deterrence strategy until the Soviet Union's dissolution in 1991. Bilateral agreements, such as the 1974 continental shelf delimitation treaty signed on February 19, further underpinned maritime security cooperation in the Western Mediterranean.[3][65]

Economic Relations

Trade Dynamics and Key Sectors

Bilateral trade between Italy and Spain, facilitated by their membership in the European Union and the single market, reached nearly €61.3 billion in 2023, reflecting steady growth driven by complementary industrial strengths and shared Mediterranean supply chains.[66] Spain held a trade surplus with Italy in that year, exporting $34.7 billion worth of goods while importing $30.1 billion from Italy.[67] By 2024, Italy's exports to Spain rose to $37.37 billion, indicating resilience amid global economic pressures, though quarterly data for early 2025 showed fluctuations, such as a 12.4% increase in Italian exports to €9.88 billion in Q2.[68] [67] Key export sectors from Spain to Italy are dominated by the automotive industry, with cars valued at $5.05 billion in 2023, alongside agricultural products like pure olive oil ($1.13 billion) and chemical intermediates such as industrial fatty acids, oils, and alcohols ($953 million).[67] These reflect Spain's strengths in vehicle manufacturing—bolstered by firms like SEAT and Volkswagen's Spanish operations—and its position as the world's leading olive oil producer, supplying over 40% of global output. Italy's principal exports to Spain center on energy products like refined petroleum ($1.92 billion in 2023), electronics including broadcasting equipment ($1.41 billion), and automotive components such as motor vehicle parts ($1.28 billion), highlighting Italy's advanced manufacturing base in machinery and petrochemical refining.[67] Emerging dynamics include robust growth in Italian pharmaceutical exports to Spain, surging 325% in Q2 2025 to €1.76 billion, driven by demand for high-value generics and biotech amid post-pandemic supply chain diversification.[67] Conversely, Spanish transport equipment exports to Italy declined 9.76% in the same period, amid broader EU automotive shifts toward electrification. Overall, trade remains balanced by intra-industry exchanges in machinery and chemicals, with both nations prioritizing resilience through EU-funded initiatives like the NextGenerationEU recovery plan, which has indirectly boosted cross-border investments in green technologies.[67]

Investment and Business Collaborations

Italian foreign direct investment (FDI) in Spain constitutes the second-largest outward destination for Italian capital, with a stock exceeding €43 billion as of 2022, supporting operations by over 2,000 Italian firms that collectively generate approximately 690,000 jobs and annual turnover surpassing €400 billion.[69][70] These investments span sectors including manufacturing, energy, and real estate, exemplified by the entry of Italian group Dils into the Spanish property market in 2025 to leverage digital innovation.[71] In 2024, Italian FDI inflows to Spain tripled from the prior year, reaching nearly €1 billion, while 71% of established Italian companies there planned further expansion into 2025, citing Spain's strategic market access and growth potential.[72][73] Conversely, Spanish FDI in Italy has accumulated gross flows of €28.7 billion since 1993, representing 2.7% of Spain's total outward investments, with a stock around €19 billion that sustains over 77,000 jobs as of 2023, particularly in telecommunications (31.4% of flows, €5.3 billion) and information technology services.[74][69][75] Flows grew 60% year-on-year in 2023 before moderating with a 15.4% decline in 2024, yet 70% of Spanish firms in Italy anticipated investment increases that year, viewing the market as strategically vital (95% of respondents).[74][76] Notable examples include Solarig's €300 million commitment through 2030 for biomethane plants in Italy.[77] Bilateral business ties are bolstered by institutional alliances, such as the 2023 partnership between Italy's Confindustria and Spain's CEOE to advocate for EU growth policies, including reduced regulatory burdens and enhanced Mediterranean infrastructure.[5] This framework facilitates joint ventures, with both nations leveraging shared Latin American gateways—Spain's €66.8 billion in regional inflows since 2010 complementing Italy's expansion strategies.[78] Over the past decade, Spain attracted €304 billion in total FDI versus Italy's €191 billion, underscoring Spain's edge in drawing cross-Mediterranean capital, though mutual investments reflect complementary strengths in renewables, logistics, and digital sectors.[79]

Security and Military Cooperation

NATO Framework and Joint Exercises

Italy and Spain, as NATO members, operate within the Alliance's collective defense framework established by Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty, emphasizing interoperability, joint operations, and shared burden in Euro-Atlantic security. Italy, a founding member that signed the Treaty on April 4, 1949, has prioritized NATO's southern flank, particularly Mediterranean stability, hosting key assets like the Joint Force Command Naples.[80][81] Spain acceded to NATO on May 30, 1982, and integrated into its military command structure in 1999, contributing significantly to missile defense via U.S. Aegis destroyers at Rota Naval Station and participating in rapid reaction forces.[3][3] This framework fosters bilateral military ties through standardized training, equipment compatibility, and coordinated deployments, enhancing deterrence against regional threats like instability in North Africa and maritime chokepoints. A prominent area of cooperation involves NATO Air Policing missions, where Italian and Spanish air forces frequently integrate operations and conduct joint training to maintain airspace sovereignty. In August 2023, Italy and Spain assumed joint leadership of the Baltic Air Policing mission, deploying Eurofighter Typhoons from Šiauliai Air Base in Lithuania to patrol skies over Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania, building on prior rotations including Spain's 2006 debut and handovers such as Italy to Spain in 2021.[82][83] On June 28, 2024, Spanish and Italian jets executed combined flying exercises in Polish airspace focused on air superiority tactics, simulating contested environments while supporting ongoing NATO Air Policing in the region since April 2024.[84] Similar transitions occurred in Romania, with Spain handing over enhanced Air Policing to Italy in March 2025, underscoring routine bilateral synchronization in forward deployments.[85] Broader joint exercises reinforce this framework, with both nations contributing to multinational NATO drills emphasizing maritime and rapid response capabilities in the Mediterranean. Spain hosted elements of Dynamic Mariner 25 in March-April 2025, involving NATO warships—including potential Italian participation—in anti-submarine and surface warfare scenarios across Spanish waters and the Atlantic, aimed at validating Allied Maritime Command procedures.[86] Italy's Open Sea 2025 exercise in Sardinia similarly integrated NATO allies for amphibious and naval rehearsals, promoting interoperability that Spain supports through its naval commitments.[87] These activities, alongside Spain's deployments in Steadfast Dart 25 (involving 3,200 personnel across NATO components), highlight sustained collaboration to counter hybrid threats and ensure force readiness.[88]

Mediterranean Security and Recent Operations

Italy and Spain, both NATO and EU members bordering the Mediterranean, share strategic interests in addressing maritime threats including illegal migration, arms trafficking to Libya, terrorism, and disruptions to freedom of navigation. Their cooperation in this domain primarily occurs through multilateral frameworks, with bilateral elements emerging in ad hoc responses to immediate crises. These efforts build on post-Cold War alignments, emphasizing joint patrols, intelligence sharing, and capacity-building to stabilize the region amid instability in North Africa.[89] A key venue for collaboration is the European Union's Operation EUNAVFOR MED Irini, established in March 2020 to monitor compliance with the United Nations arms embargo on Libya using aerial, satellite, and naval assets. Headquartered in Rome, the operation relies on Italian leadership for command rotations alongside Greece, while Spain has contributed frigates and personnel to support maritime interdictions, conducting thousands of boardings to inspect suspect vessels. By October 2025, Irini had performed over 20,000 boardings across Libyan ports, airports, and Mediterranean routes, with Italy and Spain's assets enhancing enforcement against illicit arms flows that exacerbate regional conflicts. This mission succeeded the earlier Operation Sophia (2015–2020), in which Spain deployed frigates and patrol aircraft alongside Italy's hosting role, reflecting sustained southern European commitment to curbing smuggling networks fueling Libya's civil war.[90][89][91][92] Within NATO, Italy and Spain participate in Standing NATO Maritime Group 2 (SNMG2) exercises and patrols across the Mediterranean, focusing on counter-terrorism, maritime domain awareness, and interoperability. Recent joint training involving Spanish training ship Juan Sebastián de Elcano and Italian frigate Martinengo has honed tactics for crisis response in areas from the Strait of Gibraltar to the Suez Canal. Spain's deployment of the Isaac Peral (S-81) submarine to Operation Sea Guardian in October 2025 underscores enhanced surveillance against hybrid threats, complemented by Italian naval integrations. These activities align with broader NATO efforts to deter aggression and secure sea lanes vital for both nations' economies.[93][94] A notable recent operation highlighting direct coordination occurred in September 2025, when drone attacks using explosive devices targeted the Global Sumud Flotilla carrying aid to Gaza. Italy dispatched frigates Fasan and Alpino to escort and protect Italian nationals aboard, while Spain sent the offshore patrol vessel Furor for similar safeguarding duties, enabling the flotilla's safe transit from Greek waters through the Mediterranean. This parallel deployment, involving real-time communication between the navies, demonstrated rapid bilateral responsiveness to protect civilian maritime activities amid escalating regional tensions.[95][96]

Cultural and Societal Ties

Linguistic and Artistic Exchanges

The Italian and Spanish languages, both Romance tongues derived from Vulgar Latin disseminated by the Roman Empire, exhibit lexical overlap exceeding 80% in core vocabulary, with mutual intelligibility often reaching 85-90% in spoken form due to shared phonetic and grammatical structures.[97] This common ancestry, traceable to the 3rd century BCE Roman conquests of the Iberian Peninsula and Italic regions, fostered bidirectional lexical borrowing; for instance, Italian terms related to Renaissance humanism and maritime trade permeated Spanish via Genoese merchants active in 15th-16th century ports like Seville and Barcelona.[98] Phonological affinities—such as vowel harmony and consonant lenition—eased the assimilation of Italian loanwords into Spanish, particularly in domains like cuisine (pasta from Italian pasta) and administration (balcone influencing Spanish balcón), reflecting centuries of Mediterranean commerce predating modern nation-states.[98] Literary exchanges intensified during the 16th-century Spanish Renaissance, when Italian Petrarchan sonnet forms and classical revivalism, imported via diplomats and exiled scholars, reshaped Castilian poetry; Garcilaso de la Vega (1501–1536), a key figure, adapted Italian meters and pastoral themes from Petrarch and Sannazaro, elevating Spanish verse beyond medieval ballads and contributing to the genre's formal sophistication by 1530s publications.[99] This "Italianizing" trend, peaking under Habsburg patronage, integrated Ovidian and Virgilian motifs into works like those of Boscán and Herrera, with over 200 Italian-derived neologisms entering Spanish literary lexicon by mid-century, as evidenced in royal libraries' inventories from Toledo and Naples.[100] Reciprocal influence appeared in Italian translations of Spanish picaresque novels, such as Alemán's Guzmán de Alfarache (1599), which circulated in Venetian presses by 1600, influencing Mannerist prose styles.[101] Artistically, exchanges flourished from 1516 to 1621, driven by Spanish Habsburg control over southern Italy (Naples, Sicily) and artist migrations; Italian painters like Titian supplied canvases to Philip II's court, introducing oil techniques and Venetian colorism that transformed Spanish religious panels, as seen in Berruguete's altarpieces incorporating Raphael-inspired compositions by the 1520s.[102] Human agents—sculptors like Guglielmo della Porta relocating to Madrid—and objects like engravings of Michelangelo's works catalyzed stylistic shifts, with Spanish artists training in Rome adopting chiaroscuro for tenebrism precursors in Zurbarán's 1620s monastic scenes.[103] Baroque synergies emerged post-1600, with Italian quadratura frescoes influencing Madrid's royal sites and Spanish naturalism informing Neapolitan still lifes, though Spain's autos-da-fé iconography retained distinct Counter-Reformation austerity over Italian theatricality.[104] These circuits, documented in 17th-century correspondence from academies like San Luca, underscore causal flows from Italian innovation to Spanish adaptation, tempered by local patronage demands.[105]

Tourism and Media Influences

In 2023, approximately 4.8 million Italians traveled to Spain as tourists, comprising 5.7% of Spain's total international arrivals and generating 4.3 billion euros in expenditures.[106] This figure reflects Spain's status as the top outbound destination for Italians, driven by affordable flights, coastal resorts, and cultural affinities such as shared Mediterranean lifestyles and cuisine.[107] Conversely, Spanish tourism to Italy has expanded rapidly, with arrivals rising 35.6% in the first nine months of 2023 relative to the equivalent period in 2022, followed by a 16% increase in 2024 alongside 6.9% more overnight stays and 33.3% higher spending.[108][109] These flows underscore reciprocal appeal, bolstered by Schengen Area mobility and seasonal peaks in summer months, though both nations grapple with overtourism strains in urban centers like Barcelona and Rome.[110] Media portrayals significantly enhance these exchanges by romanticizing each country's heritage, landscapes, and lifestyles, thereby motivating travel decisions. Italian neorealist films and postwar cinema have exerted lasting influence on Spanish filmmakers, fostering a tradition of co-productions that highlight shared themes of family, passion, and regional identity.[111] Examples include the 1999 Spanish-Italian-French television series Pepe Carvalho, adapted from novels and distributed across borders to promote detective genres rooted in urban grit.[112] In turn, contemporary Spanish series like those analyzed in film tourism studies have spurred international visits to filming locations, with Italian viewers—accustomed to analogous dramatic formats—contributing to heightened awareness of Andalusian or Catalan sites.[113] Football broadcasting further amplifies mutual cultural penetration, as Serie A matches draw Spanish audiences via platforms like DAZN, while La Liga games captivate Italians, reinforcing national stereotypes of flair and rivalry that indirectly boost tourism through fan pilgrimages to stadiums in Milan or Madrid.[114] Such media dynamics, however, occasionally fuel competitive narratives in coverage of economic or political divergences, though empirical viewership data indicates predominantly positive reinforcement of bilateral affinities over divisive content.[115]

Demographic and Migration Patterns

Historical Diaspora Movements

During the early modern period, Spanish Habsburg dominance over significant portions of Italy, including the Kingdoms of Naples and Sicily as well as the Duchy of Milan from the early 16th century until the War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714), facilitated substantial migration of Spaniards to Italian territories.[116] This influx primarily consisted of military personnel, administrative officials such as viceroys and governors, clergy, and their accompanying families and retainers, driven by the needs of governance, defense, and ecclesiastical administration under Spanish rule.[116] Principal destinations were southern Italy, particularly Naples and Sicily, where Spanish settlers influenced local elites, commerce, and court culture, though precise demographic scales remain under-quantified in surviving records due to incomplete censuses and transient postings.[116] The 1492 Alhambra Decree expelling Jews from Spain triggered a Sephardic diaspora, with a notable portion relocating to Italian city-states offering relative tolerance, such as Venice, Livorno, and Ancona.[117] Estimates suggest thousands of Sephardim settled in these ports by the early 16th century, bringing mercantile expertise, Ladino language, and cultural practices that enriched Italian Jewish communities and Mediterranean trade networks, though many faced periodic restrictions and later integrated variably.[117] This movement contrasted with broader Sephardic flows to the Ottoman Empire and North Africa, highlighting Italy's role as a proximate refuge amid expulsions.[117] The expulsion of Moriscos—nominal Muslim converts to Christianity—from Spain between 1609 and 1614, decreed by Philip III on April 9, 1609, displaced approximately 300,000 individuals, with a fraction redirected to Italy after initial routes to North Africa proved insufficient or hostile. Some 4,000–5,000 Moriscos, including skilled artisans, arrived in Sicily and northern Italian ports like Genoa, often via French intermediaries following the 1610 assassination of Henry IV, which disrupted overland paths.[118][119] These settlers faced scrutiny and limited integration, with provisional communities forming in Syracuse and Palermo, but most were transient, contributing minimally to long-term demographics amid local suspicions of crypto-Islam.[120] In contrast, pre-20th-century Italian migration to Spain was negligible, overshadowed by Italy's primary emigration targets in the Americas and northern Europe from the 1870s onward, with no comparable institutional or imperial drivers prompting mass settlement in Spain.[121] Early Roman-era foundations like Italica in Hispania Baetica (founded 206 BCE) represented colonial outposts rather than voluntary diaspora. These asymmetric flows underscored Spain's projective influence on Italy through governance and forced displacements, fostering enduring cultural exchanges like shared Iberian naming conventions in southern Italian lineages.[122]

Contemporary Migration and Labor Flows

In the European Union context of free movement, contemporary bilateral migration between Italy and Spain has been asymmetric, with a significant outflow of Italian citizens to Spain since the early 2010s, driven primarily by economic factors including higher youth unemployment in Italy and Spain's relatively stronger post-financial crisis recovery in sectors like tourism, services, and renewable energy.[123] In 2022, emigration of Italian nationals to OECD destinations reached 152,000, of which approximately 32%—or about 48,640 individuals—headed to Spain, reflecting a 14% year-over-year increase in overall Italian outflows.[123] This trend has contributed to a resident Italian community in Spain estimated at nearly 256,000 as of 2025, concentrated in urban centers like Barcelona and Madrid, where many engage in professional services, education, and hospitality.[124] Labor mobility under EU directives has facilitated these movements, with Italian migrants often filling skilled gaps in Spain's labor market amid demographic aging and regional demand for bilingual professionals; for instance, sectors such as information technology and agribusiness have seen notable Italian inflows, supported by Spain's lower effective tax burdens for certain expatriates and cultural affinities including shared Mediterranean lifestyles.[123] Conversely, migration from Spain to Italy remains modest, with historical data indicating around 19,000 Spanish residents in Italy as of 2016, and limited evidence of substantial growth thereafter, partly due to comparable economic challenges in both nations during the 2008-2014 period that directed Spanish outflows more toward northern Europe.[125] Net bilateral flows underscore Italy's higher emigration pressure, with over 48,000 annual registrations of Italians in Spain noted in recent years, exacerbating Italy's brain drain of young graduates while bolstering Spain's workforce integration; Eurostat data on EU intra-mobility highlights how such patterns align with broader southern European labor reallocations post-COVID, though both countries continue to rely heavily on non-EU inflows for low-skilled roles.[123][125] These dynamics have prompted bilateral discussions on talent retention, as evidenced by joint EU initiatives, but no specific Italy-Spain labor pacts have emerged to reverse the imbalance.[123]

Contemporary Developments

European Union Partnership

Italy and Spain maintain a robust partnership within the European Union, leveraging their positions as major southern member states to advance shared interests in economic policy, migration management, and Mediterranean security. Both nations emphasize the need for a strengthened EU framework, particularly in institutional reforms and defense integration, viewing enhanced Union capabilities as essential for addressing regional challenges.[126][127] This alignment stems from their geographic proximity and exposure to common pressures, such as irregular migration across Mediterranean routes, prompting coordinated advocacy for EU-wide solidarity mechanisms.[128] In economic spheres, Italy and Spain collaborate to promote EU growth and competitiveness, exemplified by the 2023 alliance between Italy's Confindustria and Spain's CEOE, which facilitated discussions among 130 companies during King Felipe VI's state visit to Italy, focusing on joint investments in energy and infrastructure to bolster the European economy.[5] They have historically supported flexible fiscal policies within the Eurozone and the EU's Multi-Annual Financial Framework, as outlined in the 2012 Spain-Italy Summit Joint Declaration, which endorsed agreements for the 2014-2020 period to enhance budgetary resilience.[129] Recent initiatives include a 2024 cybersecurity collaboration agreement between Spain's INCIBE and Italy's ACN to counter emerging threats, aligning with broader EU digital resilience goals.[130] On migration, Italy and Spain, as primary entry points for irregular arrivals, have jointly pushed for reforms emphasizing external partnerships and burden-sharing, as evidenced by their 2020 non-paper with Greece and Malta critiquing insufficient EU support and advocating for preventive measures. This was reiterated in the April 2023 bilateral meeting between Prime Ministers Giorgia Meloni and Pedro Sánchez in Rome, where they addressed the external dimension of the EU Migration Pact, seeking to externalize border controls while avoiding internal disagreements on implementation.[131][132] Their strategic roles are further highlighted in EU defense discussions, where both countries' military capacities and Mediterranean positioning are deemed critical for initiatives like the European Defence Fund.[133] Efforts to formalize this partnership include Italy's 2022 initiative, announced by Foreign Minister Luigi Di Maio, to negotiate a treaty of enhanced cooperation with Spain, aiming to deepen bilateral ties within the EU context despite occasional divergences on specifics like energy policy models.[6] High-level engagements, such as the February 2023 Meloni-Sánchez call on EU support for Ukraine and enterprise competitiveness, underscore ongoing alignment on geopolitical priorities.[134] This cooperation extends to research under programs like PRIMA, fostering innovative solutions for water scarcity and food security in the Mediterranean basin.[135]

Bilateral Agreements and Challenges Post-2000

In 2017, Italy and Spain agreed to resume annual bilateral summits to enhance coordination on shared interests, including European integration and Mediterranean security, following a meeting between Prime Ministers Mariano Rajoy and Paolo Gentiloni.[136] These summits have facilitated discussions on economic recovery and EU policies, though not all have resulted in formalized treaties. In April 2022, Italian Foreign Minister Luigi Di Maio announced efforts toward a treaty of enhanced cooperation to strengthen bilateral ties, building on a Spanish proposal for a friendship treaty earlier that year.[137] Sector-specific agreements have advanced collaboration post-2000. In March 2024, Spain and Italy signed a cybersecurity cooperation agreement to bolster capabilities against digital threats, valid for one year and renewable up to four years.[130] In October 2024, their space agencies, ASI and AEE, established a protocol for joint activities in space exploration and technology.[138] Economic ties have deepened, with over 2,000 Italian firms operating in Spain by 2023, generating approximately 690,000 jobs and annual turnover exceeding €40 billion, alongside reciprocal Spanish investments.[5] Challenges have arisen from asymmetric migration pressures and diverging domestic policies. Both nations, as primary entry points for irregular Mediterranean crossings, have pursued externalization strategies with third countries since the early 2000s, but Italy's bilateral pacts, such as with Libya, have contrasted with Spain's readmission agreements, leading to uneven EU burden-sharing debates.[139] Political divergences intensified post-2010 financial crisis, with Spain implementing structural reforms yielding faster recovery while Italy faced prolonged stagnation and frequent government changes, complicating aligned stances on EU fiscal rules.[140] Recent ideological contrasts—Spain's socialist-led government versus Italy's center-right administration—have surfaced in migration policy critiques, as seen in joint 2025 opposition to a Franco-British returns deal potentially increasing southern frontline loads.[141] Despite these, pragmatic cooperation persists, evidenced by 2023 meetings between Prime Ministers Pedro Sánchez and Giorgia Meloni focusing on EU solidarity.[132]
