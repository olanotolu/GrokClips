New York City Federation of Black Cowboys

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization established in 1994 to preserve and disseminate knowledge of the "Black West," emphasizing the historical contributions of African American cowboys through hands-on western horsemanship training and cultural education.[1] Headquartered at Cedar Lane Stables in Howard Beach, Queens, since its founding, the group draws on the legacy of black cowboys who comprised up to one-quarter of cattle drivers in the post-Civil War American West, figures such as Bill Pickett, Bass Reeves, Nat Love, and Stagecoach Mary, to counter urban disconnection among youth by imparting skills in horse care, riding, and ranching traditions.[2][3] The federation's core activities include youth mentorship programs that expose inner-city children to equine handling and the discipline of cowboy life, fostering self-reliance and historical awareness amid New York City's concrete landscape, alongside community outreach such as prison visitations, parades (including Macy's Thanksgiving Day), rodeos, lectures, and an annual Prayer Breakfast.[1][3] Partnerships with entities like the Fresh Air Fund, Gateway National Park, and local schools have enabled broader access to these initiatives, which prioritize practical horsemanship over recreational riding to instill values of responsibility and perseverance.[2] Notable members, such as Ellis "Mountain Man" Harris, who achieved championship status in steer wrestling in his 50s, exemplify the organization's emphasis on personal achievement through equestrian pursuits.[3] Despite operational hurdles, including a 2016 eviction from their stables that threatened continuity, the federation has sustained its mission into the 2020s, maintaining around 40 members and advocating for a permanent national headquarters featuring a museum and indoor arena to institutionalize black cowboy heritage.[4][3] This urban outpost of western culture underscores causal links between historical agrarian skills and modern community stabilization, prioritizing empirical transmission of cowboy lore over stylized narratives.[1]

Historical Background

Pre-Federation Roots in New York

In the decades following the American Civil War, historians estimate that 20-25% of cowboys engaged in the Western cattle industry were African American, comprising approximately 6,000 to 9,000 individuals out of a total workforce of around 35,000 during the peak trail-driving era from the 1860s to the 1880s; these workers, often formerly enslaved, contributed significantly to cattle herding, ranching, and frontier expansion based on employment records from Texas outfits and trail boss accounts.[5][6] While the majority migrated westward from Southern states to exploit opportunities in the expanding range cattle industry, equestrian skills rooted in antebellum plantation labor and post-emancipation ranch work persisted among Black communities.[7] The Great Migration (1916-1970), which saw over 6 million African Americans relocate from rural Southern agricultural regions to northern industrial cities including New York, facilitated the northward transmission of these traditions amid urbanization and economic shifts away from farming. In New York City, this demographic influx created pockets of interest in horsemanship among Black residents familiar with rural riding practices, though organized equestrian activities remained informal and marginal until mid-century influences. A pivotal local catalyst occurred in 1954, when a Black cowboy known as "Uncle Ben" performed during a rodeo tour at Madison Square Garden, exposing urban African American audiences to Western riding demonstrations and inspiring early enthusiasm for cowboy culture in the city.[4] This event spurred the emergence of informal riding groups among Black New Yorkers, who adapted equestrian pursuits to available urban stables and trails, often drawing on Southern heritage. These precursors evolved into structured entities like the Black Cowboy Association of New York, an early collective of African American riders that emphasized skill-sharing and cultural preservation prior to more formalized organizations.[8]

Founding and Initial Development

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys was established in 1994 by a coalition of horse riders, predominantly from the Black Cowboy Association of Brooklyn, including figures such as Willie â€œJackâ€ Dunne and James â€œJ.R.â€ Rogers.[8] This formation arose from a desire to disseminate knowledge of African American contributions to the American West, prioritizing structured horsemanship education and youth-oriented community service over the primarily social gatherings of the earlier association.[8][1] The group promptly initiated operations at Cedar Lane Stables in Howard Beach, Queens, through a lease from the New York City Department of Parks and Recreation, transforming the city-owned site into a hub for equestrian practice amid limited urban riding opportunities.[9][10][11] This arrangement, effective from 1994, supported the maintenance of approximately 25 horses and facilitated direct engagement in riding and stable management.[8] Securing 501(c)(3) nonprofit designation enabled formalized funding and operations for its core activities.[1] Early programs emphasized instructing inner-city youth in western-style horsemanship, horse husbandry, and the legacy of Black cowboys, linking these skills to historical narratives of resilience and independence in frontier life.[8][2] In the ensuing decade, milestones included regular participation in events like the African American Day Parade and biannual rodeos, which broadened public awareness and youth involvement.[8]

Organizational Mission and Structure

Core Objectives

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys seeks to promote greater understanding of African American culture and heritage by educating youth on the historical contributions of Black cowboys to the American West, where approximately 25% of cowhands were African American.[12] This includes highlighting figures such as Bill Pickett, who invented the rodeo technique of bulldogging in the late 19th century, and Bass Reeves, the first Black deputy U.S. marshal west of the Mississippi, who served with distinction until 1910.[12] The organization employs hands-on instruction in western horsemanship and horse care to transmit this knowledge, enabling direct engagement with the skills that underpinned these historical roles.[2] Central to its objectives is the cultivation of personal discipline, responsibility, and self-improvement among urban youth through mastery of equestrian practices, which demand consistent care, focus, and accountability in handling animals and equipment.[2] By prioritizing verifiable skills derived from frontier laborâ€”such as riding and livestock managementâ€”the Federation counters narratives of inevitable urban limitation with evidence-based pathways to resilience and capability, drawing on the causal link between practical horsemanship and behavioral outcomes observed in similar programs.[2] In pursuit of institutional permanence, the organization envisions establishing a national headquarters featuring a museum, indoor riding arena, library, and learning center to archive and disseminate Black Western history without embellishment, ensuring sustained access to primary artifacts and narratives for future generations.[2]

Governance and Membership

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys operates as a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt nonprofit organization, registered under the name The Federation of Black Cowboys Inc., focused on educational and charitable purposes.[1][13] Formed in 1994 by a group of horse riders previously affiliated with the Black Cowboy Association of New York City, the federation maintains an informal governance structure emphasizing collaborative decision-making among members rather than a rigid hierarchy.[8] Membership is open to applicants of any background, subject to review based on personal character, record of community service, and horsemanship skills that contribute to the group's objectives.[8] As of early 2025, active membership has declined to approximately 30 individuals, many of whom are African American riders continuing multi-generational family traditions in equestrian arts.[14] The organization prioritizes merit in riding proficiency over demographic quotas, fostering a volunteer-driven model where participants handle operations, training, and outreach without reliance on paid staff.[8][1] The federation collaborates with various civic and community groups to support its initiatives, integrating member expertise into broader public programs while relying on volunteer contributions for sustainability.[2] This structure underscores a commitment to self-reliant operations, distinct from government-subsidized models, with decisions guided by collective input from skilled riders dedicated to preserving equestrian heritage.[8]

Programs and Activities

Horsemanship and Training Initiatives

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys conducts youth-oriented horsemanship programs centered on Western riding techniques, including foundational skills in mounting, control, and navigation adapted to confined urban stable spaces. Instruction prioritizes hands-on horse care, such as grooming, feeding, and stable upkeep, which participants must master to demonstrate accountability before advancing to riding.[1] [15] Specific training elements encompass basic horse handling and roping drills, as shown in demonstrations by experienced members like Jose Lopez, who illustrate foundational maneuvers essential to cowboy proficiency.[16] These methods draw from traditional ranch practices, fostering precision and safety in equine interaction despite limited acreage compared to open ranges.[8] Initiatives target inner-city youth, particularly those from at-risk backgrounds, with the objective of cultivating discipline and responsibility via structured equine tasks that mirror ranch labor's demands for reliability and foresight. Anecdotal reports highlight behavioral improvements, including enhanced respect and honesty, alongside practical outcomes like sobriety maintenance for individuals such as Eric "Red" Jackson; select participants have transitioned to roles in horse training or mounted law enforcement units, underscoring causal pathways from consistent animal stewardship to self-regulation and vocational readiness.[8] Historical integration occurs through curriculum that references skills developed by Black pioneers, such as bulldogging techniques attributed to Bill Pickett and endurance riding exemplified by Nat Love, corroborated by 19th-century frontier records, thereby linking modern drills to empirically documented African American contributions in equestrian mastery.[1]

Community Outreach and Events

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys conducts semiannual rodeos, parades, lectures, and block parties to engage the broader public in preserving and disseminating African American cowboy heritage.[17][1] The organization partners with civic groups for prison visitations and work release programs aimed at rehabilitation through exposure to horsemanship and related skills.[1][18] Youth outreach initiatives emphasize historical education on Black contributions to the American cattle industry, highlighting verifiable facts such as the estimated 25% proportion of Black cowboys among those participating in trail drives from the 1860s to 1880s.[6][19] These efforts target urban youth, fostering community connections via public demonstrations and interactive sessions distinct from internal training.[1]

Facilities and Operations

Stables and Location

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys maintains its primary operations at Cedar Lane Stables in the Howard Beach section of Queens, situated adjacent to the low-lying neighborhood known as The Hole on the Queens-Brooklyn border.[1] [8] The organization has utilized this site since February 1994, leveraging approximately 25 acres of city-owned land that includes a renovated horse barn and a corral roughly the size of a football field for housing and training activities.[1] [8] This location embodies a stark urban-rural contrast, with stables bordered by industrial zones and proximity to major roadways like the Belt Parkway, yet enabling inner-city youth access to horsemanship amid New York City's high land costs and dense development.[20] The Hole's post-industrial character, marked by historical neglect and irregular zoning, facilitates such equestrian use but requires adaptations to environmental hazards, including recurrent flooding from the area's sub-grade elevationâ€”often 30 feet below surrounding streetsâ€”and inadequate drainage infrastructure.[21] [22] Horse maintenance at the stables supports daily training regimens, with the facility accommodating around 25 animals through standard care protocols emphasizing veterinary oversight, feeding, and exercise to uphold welfare standards in an urban setting prone to disruptions like stormwater incursions.[20] Equipment storage and grooming areas are integrated into the barn structure, allowing consistent preparation for riding programs despite the site's exposure to Queens' variable weather and zoning constraints that limit expansion on public land.[8]

Museum and Expansion Efforts

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys maintains an on-site museum at its Cedar Lane Stables headquarters in Howard Beach, Queens, featuring authentic cowboy artifacts and memorabilia that highlight African American contributions to Western horsemanship.[14] [4] Visitors, including students participating in riding lessons, can tour the museum to learn about this heritage, with exhibits drawing from historical items preserved by the organization since its founding in 1994.[14] [1] Expansion efforts focus on acquiring land in Howard Beach to establish a national headquarters, envisioned to include an enhanced museum, indoor riding arena, library, and learning center dedicated to youth education in Black Western history.[1] These plans, outlined as long-term goals by the nonprofit, aim to create dedicated preservation infrastructure amid ongoing urban development pressures that have enclosed the current stables with surrounding housing.[1] Funding relies primarily on private donations, supporting self-sustaining initiatives like youth horsemanship programs that emphasize skill-building and cultural pride over dependency on public grants.[1] Progress has been gradual, with the organization prioritizing partnerships for community-based youth development to sustain operations in a changing urban landscape.[1]

Cultural and Social Impact

Preservation of Black Cowboy Heritage

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys addresses historical omissions in the narrative of the American West by emphasizing the substantial role of African American cowboys, estimated by historians to have constituted 20-25% of those participating in cattle drives from the 1860s to the 1880s, drawing from census records and ranch documentation.[7] [6] This demographic share contrasts sharply with Hollywood's predominant portrayal of cowboys as white protagonists, a depiction that has marginalized Black contributions despite their prevalence in roping, branding, and trail herding following the Civil War.[6] The organization's efforts underscore these facts without disputing the white majority, focusing instead on verifiable records to rectify the underrepresentation rooted in selective cultural storytelling. Through educational programs, the Federation documents and disseminates suppressed personal histories, relying on primary accounts like Nat Love's 1907 autobiography Life and Adventures of Nat Love, which details the author's experiences as a skilled Black cowhand, including encounters with frontier figures and participation in rodeos.[23] This approach pushes back against minimized depictions of non-white ranchers by prioritizing undiluted eyewitness testimonies over secondary interpretations, promoting awareness of the "Black West" as integral to cattle industry operations.[1] The group integrates this heritage into urban New York City contexts via public demonstrations, trail rides, and horsemanship events in locations such as Queens stables and Battery Park City gatherings, enabling direct transmission of traditional skills and narratives to contemporary audiences.[8] [4] These initiatives maintain cultural continuity by adapting frontier practices to city environments, ensuring that the legacy of Black cowboysâ€”evident in their historical labor on drives and ranchesâ€”remains accessible and practiced amid modern settings.[3]

Achievements and Public Recognition

The New York City Federation of Black Cowboys has achieved sustained engagement through its youth programs, which emphasize horsemanship training and education on African American contributions to the American West, fostering personal development among inner-city participants since 1994.[8][4] Notable individual outcomes include participants like Hondo, an inner-city youth from Coney Island, who gained improved self-esteem and a sense of direction through the organization's mentorship.[3] Similarly, member Eric "Red" Jackson credits the group with supporting his sobriety for nearly 20 years since joining in 2000.[8] The organization has hosted semi-annual rodeos that draw over 5,000 attendees, contributing to broader public awareness of Black cowboy heritage.[8] Participation in prominent events, such as the African American Day Parade in Harlem and Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade, has further elevated its visibility.[8] Member Ellis "Mountain Man" Harris earned recognition as a champion steer wrestler in his 50s, exemplifying competitive successes within the group.[3] Media coverage has underscored these efforts, including a 2024 ABC7 New York feature on the federation's mission to preserve history and inspire youth under the leadership of its first female president, Kesha Morse.[4] In June 2025, New York City government highlighted the organization's decades-long role in teaching horsemanship to young New Yorkers.[24] Morse received public honors through 2022 community art installations recognizing her as a hero for advancing the group's legacy.[25] City Lore has documented and featured the federation in videos and profiles, affirming its cultural significance.[8]

Challenges and Critiques

Operational and Financial Hurdles

In 2016, the New York City Federation of Black Cowboys lost operational control of Cedar Lane Stables in Queens' Howard Beach neighborhood, a facility it had managed since 1994 under a Parks Department license, due to declining membershipâ€”down to 18 active ridersâ€”and mounting financial pressures that included maintenance costs of approximately $400 per horse monthly.[26][27] The New York City Parks Department awarded the contract to GallopNYC, a therapeutic riding nonprofit, following reports of horse health issues and operational lapses at the site, exacerbating the federation's relocation challenges amid competitive bidding for limited urban stable space.[28][10] These real estate constraints in New York City, where nonprofit equestrian operations face escalating land values and regulatory scrutiny, compelled the group to disperse its approximately 25 horses to private facilities, disrupting training continuity and increasing logistical overhead without guaranteed long-term leases.[29][3] As of February 2024, the organization continued seeking a larger permanent stable after being outbid on reacquiring Cedar Lane, highlighting persistent vulnerabilities in securing affordable urban infrastructure for horse boarding and youth programs.[4] Financially, the federation relies heavily on donations to cover horse care and operational expenses, with leadership noting reduced membership as a key barrier to revenue generation in an era of rising urban costs for feed, veterinary services, and storage.[4] This dependence underscores broader economic hurdles for small nonprofits in high-cost environments like Queens, where equestrian activities compete with commercial development and lack institutional subsidies, necessitating adaptive strategies such as fundraising drives without consistent external grants.[27][3]

Historical Representation Debates

The historical significance of African American cowboys, as preserved by organizations such as the New York City Federation of Black Cowboys, centers on their documented role during the late 19th-century cattle drive era, when historians estimate that approximately 6,000 to 9,000 out of roughly 35,000 working cowboysâ€”about one in fourâ€”were Black, many of them formerly enslaved individuals seeking economic independence on the frontier.[5] [6] This proportion, derived from trail crew logs, employer records, and census extrapolations from cattle-raising states like Texas (where African Americans comprised around 17% of the population in 1880), underscores real contributions including roping, branding, and trail herding, often under egalitarian conditions within crews despite broader societal discrimination.[30] Debates arise over the temporal scope and proportionality of this emphasis, as the peak open-range era ended by the 1890s with the advent of barbed wire fencing, refrigerated rail transport, and ranch consolidation, leading to a sharp decline in the demand for traditional cowboys and a corresponding drop in Black participation to marginal levels by the early 20th century, when white laborers dominated the increasingly mechanized industry.[6] Critics, including historians wary of identity-driven historiography, argue that framing Black cowboys as emblematic of the "Wild West" risks inflating their representativeness beyond this narrow window, potentially sidelining census evidence of low African American settlement rates in most western territories (typically under 5% outside southern border states) and the white majority in overall ranching occupations per agricultural reports.[5] A related contention involves the multicultural origins of cowboy techniques, with some scholars critiquing narratives that prioritize African American adaptations while understating the foundational influence of Mexican vaqueros, Indigenous Mesoamerican herders who, from the 16th century onward, pioneered lassoing (reata), saddle designs, and cattle management practices that Anglo-American cowboys later adopted during westward expansion.[31] These critiques, rooted in primary accounts from Spanish colonial ranchos and U.S. military observations, stress causal realism in crediting vaquero innovationsâ€”such as the charro bit and wide-brimmed sombreroâ€”as precursors, rather than retrofitting them into post-Civil War Black narratives without proportional acknowledgment of Hispanic and Native inputs.[31] Proponents of robust historical representation view such focus as essential corrective justice against media whitewashing that obscured Black agency, citing erased figures like trail boss Bose Ikard and bulldogger Bill Pickett as evidence of overlooked excellence.[6] Skeptics counter that empirical fidelity demands tempering enthusiasm with data-driven limits to avert compensatory myths, urging reliance on verifiable industry logs and demographics over anecdotal elevation, particularly given institutional tendencies in academia and media toward selective amplification of minority narratives at the expense of aggregate realities.[5]
