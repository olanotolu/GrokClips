Osculum infame

The osculum infame, Latin for "infamous kiss" or "kiss of shame," designates a ritual act ascribed to witches in late medieval and early modern European demonology, wherein participants allegedly demonstrated fealty to the Devil by kissing his anus or buttocks.[1] This obeisance was purportedly performed at the witches' sabbath, an imagined nocturnal convocation of sorcery, debauchery, and pact-making with infernal powers.[1] Descriptions of the rite, emphasizing its degrading nature as symbolic inversion of Christian reverence, permeated inquisitorial literature and trial records, fueling accusations across witch hunts from the 15th to 17th centuries.[2] The concept's origins likely derive from earlier charges against dissident sects, such as the 12th-century Cathars or the 14th-century Knights Templar, whom authorities accused of similar posterior-kissing rituals as marks of heresy.[2] By the Renaissance, it featured prominently in treatises like Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum (1608), which illustrated the act to catalog malefic practices for prosecutors.[3] Such accounts, drawn from coerced confessions and speculative theology rather than independent corroboration, underscore the era's fusion of scriptural exegesis with paranoid folklore, though demonologists like Guazzo presented them as empirical deductions from "witches' own admissions."[1] While no archaeological or unbiased testimonial evidence substantiates the rite's occurrence, its recurrence in sources reflects causal dynamics of institutional fear-mongering and mass hysteria in pre-modern jurisprudence.[4]

Definition and Etymology

Literal Meaning and Translation

Osculum infame is a Latin phrase that literally translates to "infamous kiss" or "kiss of shame," referring to an act deemed disgraceful in historical demonological contexts.[5] [6] The word osculum derives from the noun os ("mouth"), with a diminutive suffix indicating "little mouth," classically denoting a kiss or osculation.[7] [8] Infame, an adjective form of infamis, combines the negative prefix in- ("not" or "un-") with fama ("fame," "reputation," or "report"), connoting ill repute, notoriety, or moral disgrace.[9] [10] This etymological structure underscores a kiss associated with infamy or shame, distinct from benign connotations of osculum in classical Latin literature, such as affectionate greetings.[11]

Origins in Heretical Accusations

Accusations Against Cathars and Waldensians

In the early 13th century, as the Catholic Church intensified its campaign against heretical movements in southern France and beyond, accusations of osculum infame emerged as a means to equate groups like the Cathars with satanic worship. The Cathars, a dualist sect emphasizing asceticism and rejection of the material world, were portrayed by inquisitors as engaging in obscene rituals to honor the devil, including kissing the posterior of animals such as cats or toads in which Satan allegedly manifested. This imagery served to invert their reputed purity into depravity, justifying the Albigensian Crusade (1209â€“1229) and subsequent inquisitions that resulted in the deaths of thousands, with estimates of Cathar perfecti (leaders) executed reaching over 200 at MontsÃ©gur alone in 1244. Such claims lacked corroboration from Cathar texts, which instead advocated celibacy and non-procreation to escape the corrupt physical realm, suggesting the accusations were rhetorical constructs by orthodox authorities to consolidate power. Pope Gregory IX's bull Vox in Rama (1233), issued amid inquiries into northern European heresies like the Stedinger revolt but generalized to dualists, detailed novices approaching a large black catâ€”symbolizing Luciferâ€”and kissing it "sub cauda" (under the tail) as a sign of homage, followed by further obscenities. Though primarily targeting Germanic sects, the bull's lurid depictions influenced portrayals of Cathars, whose name was etymologically twisted in later anti-heretical literature to derive from Latin catus (cat), allegedly because they performed this kiss on feline forms of the devil. The anonymous 14th-century tract Errores Haereticorum reinforced this by asserting Satan appeared to Cathars as a cat, toad, or goose, demanding the posterior kiss to seal allegiance, framing their rejection of Catholic sacraments as inversion into infernal parody. Waldensians, founded around 1170 by Peter Waldo as a lay movement advocating apostolic poverty and vernacular scripture, faced parallel charges despite their emphasis on moral rigor over ritual excess. Inquisitorial records from the late 13th and 14th centuries, including those from Dominican interrogators in Provence and the Alps, accused Waldensian gatherings of featuring osculum infame toward a cat or demon's hindquarters, often conflated with sodomy or animal veneration to underscore moral corruption. For instance, 14th-century depictions and tracts portrayed Waldensians queuing to kiss a cat's rear, echoing Vox in Rama's motifs and aiding their expulsion or massacres, such as the 1655 Piedmont slaughter claiming up to 1,700 lives. These allegations, drawn from coerced confessions under torture as documented in papal registers, mirrored anti-Cathar tactics but ignored Waldensian prohibitions on oaths and violence, indicating a pattern of fabricated diabolism to equate dissent with devil-pacts rather than reflecting autonomous practices. Primary inquisitorial manuals, like those of Bernard Gui (c. 1320s), cataloged such rites as standard heretical errors without independent verification, prioritizing ecclesiastical unity over empirical scrutiny.

Knights Templar Trials and Early References

The arrest of the Knights Templar on October 13, 1307, ordered by King Philip IV of France, precipitated trials accusing the order of heresy, including secretive initiation rites involving denial of Christ, spitting or urinating on the crucifix, and obscene physical contacts.[15] Confessions, frequently extracted through torture such as the rack or threats of burning, detailed requirements for postulants to kiss their receiverâ€”often a senior Templarâ€”on the mouth, navel, stomach, buttocks, or base of the spine after disrobing.[16] At the Paris trials, for instance, among 138 knights who confessed to various infractions, 94 admitted to such kisses alongside charges of sodomy and idol worship, though specifics varied by witness, with some describing kisses solely on the lower spine or avoiding genital areas.[17] These accusations formed part of a broader indictment of the order's immorality, with papal investigator Guillaume de Nogaret emphasizing indecent kissing and homosexuality as justifications for suppression.[18] However, many confessions were recanted under less duress; at provincial trials in 1309â€“1310 and the Council of Vienne (1311â€“1312), over 500 Templars defended the order, denying obscene rites and attributing admissions to coercion, leading historians to view the charges as largely fabricated to justify Philip's financial motives, including seizure of Templar assets to repay royal debts.[19][20] The Templar kiss rituals represent an early documented instance in medieval heresy prosecutions of alleging submissive, degrading physical homage echoing later demonological motifs, though the specific Latin term osculum infame emerged primarily in 15thâ€“16th-century witchcraft contexts rather than Templar proceedings.[21] Such parallels suggest the trials influenced subsequent inquisitorial templates, where similar "shameful kisses" to demonic figures symbolized renunciation of Christian norms, but the absence of consistent, uncoerced corroboration underscores the accusations' reliance on scripted interrogations over empirical evidence.[18]

Development in Witchcraft Demonology

Integration into Sabbath Narratives

In demonological treatises of the late 16th and early 17th centuries, the osculum infame was systematically woven into accounts of the witches' sabbath as an obligatory act of reverence, marking the witches' submission to the presiding demon and inverting Christian liturgical gestures of humility such as kissing the pope's foot or the crucifix.[22] Upon arrival at the sabbathâ€”often depicted as a nocturnal gathering in remote locations like forests or mountaintopsâ€”witches were said to dismount from their fantastical transports (broomsticks or demonic steeds), approach the devil enthroned in goat or hybrid form, and perform the kiss on his anus or posterior as the initial homage before proceeding to feasting, dancing, and profane rites.[22] This integration emphasized the sabbath's parodic structure, transforming a supposed communal worship into a spectacle of degradation and anti-Christian mockery.[23] Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum (1608) exemplifies this narrative embedding, stating that witches, as a sign of homage, kiss the devil's "fundament" upon entering the assembly, an act illustrated in its engravings as central to the sabbath's opening rituals and corroborated by confessions from trials in Lombardy and elsewhere.[22] [24] Martin Del Rio, in Disquisitionum Magicarum (1599â€“1600), similarly describes the sabbath presided over by a demon where the osculum infame serves as the primary obeisance, drawing from French and Italian trial testimonies to portray it as a universal prelude to the gathering's obscenities, including the desecration of hosts and infanticide.[22] These texts, relying on interrogations from cases like those in Trier (1581â€“1593) involving thousands of accused, standardized the kiss within sabbath lore, presenting it not as isolated but as ritually sequenced: homage first, then collective blasphemy.[22] Variations in these narratives reflected regional influences; in French demonology, as synthesized by Del Rio, the kiss sometimes targeted the devil's foot if he appeared humanoid, but the posterior remained predominant to underscore humiliation, while German accounts occasionally linked it to a "black man" figure leading processions.[22] Earlier traces in 15th-century inquisitorial records, such as those against Waldensians, had isolated the kiss as heretical obeisance, but its full sabbath integration accelerated post-1480s with the consolidation of flight-to-sabbath motifs in texts amplifying trial-derived confessions.[23] Demonologists like Guazzo attributed the act's ubiquity to witches' pacts, claiming it sealed loyalty and enabled infernal powers, though such uniformity likely stemmed from interrogators' leading questions standardizing responses across disparate regions like the Basque country (1609â€“1611) and Lorraine.[22] This narrative device reinforced the sabbath's role as a diabolical counter-church, with the kiss symbolizing total renunciation of orthodoxy.[23]

Descriptions in Key Demonological Texts

One of the earliest references to the osculum infame appears in Nicolas Eymerich's Directorium Inquisitorum (written circa 1376), an influential inquisitorial manual that describes the practice as a shameful kiss administered to the hindquarters of demonic animals such as toads or cats.[25] This act symbolized utter submission to infernal powers, often linked to heretical sects accused of parodying Christian reverence.[26] Johannes Nider's Formicarius (1435â€“1437), a foundational demonological treatise drawing from trial testimonies in the Bernese Alps, elaborates on the osculum infame as a compulsory ritual where initiates kissed the devil manifested in animal form beneath the tail, marking their renunciation of faith and entry into diabolic service.[27] Nider presents it as derived from confessions of figures like the sorcerer Stadlin, framing the kiss as an obscene homage inverting sacramental kissing practices.[1] In Jean Bodin's De la Demonomanie des Sorciers (1580), the osculum infame is depicted as a standard sabbath observance where witches paid homage to Satan by kissing his posterior, underscoring the jurist's view of such rituals as evidence of pacts with demons. Bodin integrates it into broader narratives of infernal assemblies, citing it alongside feasts and dances to argue for the reality of witchcraft based on juridical and confessional evidence.[1] Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum (1608) provides one of the most vivid portrayals, illustrating a procession of witches performing the kiss on a demon's exposed buttocks as a pledge of obedience, often following oaths of allegiance.[24] Guazzo describes it explicitly as the "obscene kiss" (osculum infame), a gesture of servitude that initiated sabbath rites, drawing from earlier inquisitorial traditions while emphasizing its role in binding witches to satanic authority.[28] These texts collectively frame the practice as a deliberate inversion of piety, though their accounts rely heavily on coerced admissions amid widespread inquisitorial scrutiny.

Variations Across Regions and Periods

In the early phases of its attribution during the 14th century, the osculum infame appeared in accusations against the Knights Templar, where initiates were alleged to have kissed the posterior of a cat or idol-like figure as part of initiation rites, reflecting a focus on heretical homage rather than organized sabbaths.[36] By the 15th century, amid emerging witch trials in alpine regions like Switzerland and northern Italy, the act shifted toward descriptions within nocturnal gatherings, often targeting the Devil's anus in human or humanoid form, as detailed in inquisitorial records from Savoy and DauphinÃ©.[37] During the 16th and 17th centuries' peak witch hunts, regional differences emerged primarily in the Devil's manifestation: in the Holy Roman Empire's German territories, confessions from mass trials such as WÃ¼rzburg (1626â€“1629) uniformly portrayed the kiss on the Devil's rear as a compulsory greeting, extracted under torture from hundreds of accused.[38] In contrast, Iberian accounts, notably the Basque trials (1609â€“1611), emphasized kissing the tail or hindquarters of a goat-formed Devil, aligning with local pastoral folklore and documented in over 7,000 folios of trial transcripts analyzed by Gustav Henningsen.[38] French trials, such as those in Lorraine and around Loudun (early 17th century), mirrored German descriptions but occasionally incorporated the Devil as a toad or black cat, with the osculum infame serving as both initiation and recurring sabbath obeisance.[23] Italian demonological literature, exemplified by Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum (1608), standardized the act as an anal kiss on a bipedal Devil, influencing inquisitorial practices in Lombardy and distinguishing it from more bestial variants elsewhere by its illustrative emphasis on human degradation.[39] Northern European Protestant areas, including England, rarely featured the osculum infame prominently, as trials prioritized maleficium over sabbath diabolism; however, Scotland's North Berwick trials (1590â€“1592) included confessions of the "kiss of shame" on the Devil's posterior, imported via continental influences on King James VI.[40] Overall, while the core humiliation persisted, post-1650 decline in trials across Europe reduced such specifics, with the motif fading as prosecutions waned by the early 18th century.[41]

Evidence and Verifiability

Corroboration from Multiple Sources

The osculum infame appears in multiple independent accounts from witch trial confessions and demonological manuals across early modern Europe, indicating a recurrent motif in accusations despite regional variations. In the Malleus Maleficarum (1487), Heinrich Institoris describes witches at the sabbath approaching the devilâ€”often in animal formâ€”and rendering homage by kissing his posterior, a ritual act symbolizing submission and inversion of Christian reverence.[42] This detail draws from interrogations in German-speaking territories, such as a fifteenth-century confession where an accused witch reported giving the devil an "obscene kiss on his posterior" after his appearance as a man in black.[42] Further corroboration emerges from Scottish records, particularly the North Berwick trials (1590â€“1592), where the 1591 pamphlet Newes from Scotland recounts confessions from figures like John Fian and Agnes Sampson detailing witches kneeling backward to kiss the devil's "fundament" in obeisance during sabbath gatherings.[43] In Italy, Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum (1608) elaborates on the practice as a standard initiation rite, illustrated with witches approaching a horned devil to perform the "osculum infame," citing precedents from Lombard and Milanese inquisitions.[44] These sources, spanning Germanic, Scottish, and Italian contexts over a century, demonstrate consistency in the described ritual without direct textual borrowing evident.[45] English demonological treatises from the late sixteenth century, influenced by continental reports but grounded in local fears, reinforce the motif; for instance, accounts of sabbaths include the "kiss of shame" as a degrading pledge of allegiance, aligning with earlier confessions while adapting to anti-Catholic polemics.[45] Such cross-regional attestation, from primary interrogations to synthesized manuals, underscores the osculum infame's role as a fabricated yet uniformly propagated element of diabolical worship narratives in ecclesiastical and secular trials.

Challenges from Torture and Coercion

The vast majority of accounts detailing the osculum infame derive from confessions extracted during European witch trials between the 15th and 17th centuries, where torture was systematically employed to compel testimony. Judicial procedures, as outlined in texts like the Carolina (1532), permitted torture under specific conditions but were frequently exceeded, with methods including the strappado (suspension by bound arms), thumbscrews, and the rack, often applied repeatedly until submission.[46] Historians note that these techniques induced physical agony and psychological breakdown, prompting accused individualsâ€”predominantly women and marginalized personsâ€”to fabricate details aligning with inquisitors' preconceptions of demonic rituals, including the obscene kiss to the devil's posterior as homage.[47] In regions like the Holy Roman Empire, such as the WÃ¼rzburg trials (1626â€“1629), over 900 executions followed mass confessions under torture, with narratives of sabbaths and osculum infame recurring formulaically, suggesting rote repetition rather than independent recollection.[46] Contemporary critics, including the Jesuit priest Friedrich Spee in his Cautio Criminalis (1631), highlighted the unreliability of such evidence, arguing that "the power of torture produces witches where there are none" and that judges, driven by ignorance or malice, ignored retractions or verifications post-torture.[48] Spee, who ministered to condemned witches, observed that confessions often escalated under repeated sessions, with details like the osculum infame emerging only after leading questions shaped by demonological manuals such as the Malleus Maleficarum (1487), which prescribed torture to uncover hidden pacts.[47] Modern analysis by historian Brian Levack corroborates this, emphasizing that few efforts were made to corroborate confession specifics, and that coerced statements fueled chain accusations, amplifying fabricated elements without empirical validation.[46] Norman Cohn further contends that sabbath motifs, including the osculum infame, reflected elite projections of heresy onto victims rather than authentic practices, as no pre-trial folklore or voluntary admissions independently attested to such acts.[49] These coercive dynamics undermine the verifiability of osculum infame as historical practice, as empirical patterns show confessions Retractions, when permitted, frequently recanted ritual specifics, attributing them to duress, as in the case of Johannes Junius (1628), who wrote privately of being tortured until confessing Cultural Depictions and Symbolism

In Art, Literature, and Folklore

Depictions of the osculum infame appear in early modern European art as visual representations of alleged witchcraft rituals, often illustrating demonological texts to convey the depravity of sabbath gatherings. A prominent example is the 1608 woodcut from Francesco Maria Guazzo's Compendium Maleficarum, showing witches kneeling to kiss the Devil's anus, emphasizing themes of inversion and humiliation central to anti-witch propaganda.[51] Similar imagery recurs in other period engravings, such as those in chronicles documenting witch hunts, where the act serves to underscore demonic worship's obscenity.[52] In demonological literature, the osculum infame functions as a narrative motif symbolizing witches' pact with Satan, detailed as an obligatory greeting that initiated sabbath rites and signified total submission. Texts like Guazzo's treatise describe it as performed on the Devil or subordinate demons, framing it within broader accounts of infernal homage that parodied Christian sacraments.[53] This literary trope drew from trial confessions and earlier folklore, evolving into a standardized element of witchcraft demonology by the 16th century, though often sensationalized to heighten moral outrage.[54] Within European folklore, the osculum infame embedded in oral and pamphlet traditions as a hallmark of witches' allegiance, believed to occur at nocturnal assemblies where participants debased themselves before the Devil's rear, reinforcing communal fears of hidden diabolism. Regional variations persisted, with accounts from Swiss and Italian sources portraying it as a prelude to feasting and dances, perpetuated through cautionary tales that blurred elite demonology with popular superstition.[55] Such beliefs, while unsubstantiated empirically, shaped cultural perceptions of witchcraft as inherently perverse until the decline of organized hunts in the 18th century.[14]

Symbolic Role in Anti-Witchcraft Propaganda

The osculum infame, or obscene kiss, functioned prominently in anti-witchcraft propaganda as a symbol of utter moral inversion and subservience to Satan, parodying Christian rituals such as the kiss of peace with an act of deliberate degradation. Demonologists portrayed this riteâ€”wherein witches allegedly kissed the Devil's posterior or that of his animal formâ€”as the ultimate token of homage, emphasizing scatological obscenity to evoke revulsion and underscore witchcraft's antithesis to piety. This imagery served to construct witches not merely as heretics but as embodiments of carnal corruption, inverting sacred reverence into profane submission.[55][53] Visual depictions amplified its propagandistic impact; the 1608 Compendium Maleficarum by Francesco Maria Guazzo included a woodcut explicitly showing witches engaged in the osculum infame, positioning it within sabbath scenes to illustrate diabolic pacts and rituals for inquisitors and the public. Such engravings, disseminated in witch-hunting manuals, transformed abstract accusations into tangible horrors, fostering widespread fear and justifying intensified persecutions by portraying witchcraft as a conspiratorial threat laced with sexual and ritual perversion.[56] In textual propaganda, the motif appeared in pamphlets like the 1591 Newes from Scotland, which detailed the kiss as a compulsory act of allegiance, introducing it to English readers amid North Berwick witch trials and heightening anti-witch hysteria. By framing the osculum infame as a contractual seal of infamyâ€”often exacted upon initiation into the covenâ€”authors like James Carmichael reinforced narratives of witches' voluntary embrace of filth, symbolizing their rejection of divine order in favor of satanic hierarchy. This symbolism extended to broader demonological treatises, where it signified the witches' abasement before infernal authority, mirroring feudal oaths but corrupted into sodomitic parody.[57] Historians trace the motif's propagandistic evolution to earlier heresy trials, where it accused Cathars and Waldensians of similar kisses, but its systematization in 15th-17th century literature fabricated a standardized emblem of diabolism without consistent pre-torture corroboration. Norman Cohn argues in Europe's Inner Demons (1975) that such elements projected elite fantasies onto marginalized groups, using the kiss's shock value to legitimize mass trials by depicting witches as irredeemably vile. Despite variationsâ€”sometimes involving animals or reversed rolesâ€”the osculum infame consistently symbolized propaganda's core aim: to dehumanize the accused, equating their supposed rites with bestial inversion to rally ecclesiastical and secular support for eradication.[55][49]

Modern Interpretations

In Occult and Neo-Pagan Contexts

In contemporary neo-pagan movements such as Wicca, the osculum infame holds no place as an active ritual and is typically regarded as a fabricated element of early modern demonological accusations rather than an authentic pre-Christian or folk practice. Modern Wiccan sabbats, numbering eight seasonal festivals aligned with solstices, equinoxes, and cross-quarter days, emphasize communal celebration of natural cycles, fertility, and the divine duality of Goddess and Horned God, without any inversion of Christian sacraments or acts of submission to a malevolent entity. Historian Ronald Hutton notes that these contemporary gatherings derive from 20th-century occult syntheses, including influences from Freemasonry and Romantic folklore, deliberately eschewing the obscene or infernal motifs of trial testimonies to foster ethical, earth-centered spirituality.[58] Within broader occult traditions, including ceremonial magic and chaos magick, references to the osculum infame occasionally appear in esoteric literature as a symbol of transgression or symbolic reversal of authority, but lack evidence of literal enactment in organized groups. For instance, in atheistic Satanism as codified by Anton LaVey in The Satanic Bible (1969), rituals serve psychodramatic purposes to affirm individualism, with no endorsement of historical witch-trial inversions like posterior veneration; theistic Satanist variants, while diverse and often secretive, similarly prioritize empowerment and archetype invocation over replicated medieval obscenities, as documented in sociological studies of post-1960s Satanism. Fringe expressions in black metal music or avant-garde occult art, such as the French band Osculum Infame's 1993 demo Satanic Revival, invoke the term for thematic shock value, framing it explicitly as a critique of inquisitorial "perverted fantasies" rather than prescriptive rite.[59] This dismissal aligns with scholarly consensus that the osculum infame emerged from 15th-17th century inquisitorial constructs blending heresy accusations with folklore, unsubstantiated by archaeological or independent ethnographic data from purported pagan survivals. Neo-pagan authors and practitioners, wary of historical revisionism that romanticizes trial confessions, prioritize verifiable reconstruction from mythology and anthropology over coerced narratives, ensuring modern contexts emphasize consent, symbolism, and psychological efficacy absent in the original allegations. No peer-reviewed accounts confirm its adoption in any structured contemporary coven or lodge as of 2025.[58]

Skeptical and Revisionist Historical Views

Historians such as Norman Cohn have argued that the concept of the osculum infame, or "obscene kiss," originated not from actual pagan or heretical rituals but from 12th-century ecclesiastical fabrications targeting groups like the Cathars, whom authorities accused of devil-worship to justify persecution. Cohn traces early references, such as those by Alain de Lille around 1190, to inverted Christian symbolismâ€”parodying the kiss of peaceâ€”rather than empirical observations of practices, with no archaeological or independent textual evidence predating these polemics supporting literal adherence.[49] This stereotype was later grafted onto witchcraft accusations during the 15th-17th centuries, amplifying a mythic narrative of sabbaths without basis in pre-Christian folklore or voluntary testimonies.[60] Revisionist scholarship emphasizes the role of torture in generating uniform descriptions of the osculum infame across disparate trials, as interrogators used leading questions and demonological manuals like the Malleus Maleficarum (1487) to elicit standardized confessions. Brian Levack notes that procedural coercionâ€” including prolonged sleep deprivation, strappado suspension, and threats of executionâ€”produced fantastical details that aligned with elite theological expectations rather than reflecting genuine folk customs or autonomous cults.[61] Trials in regions like Lorraine (1580s) and Scotland (1590s) yielded near-identical accounts of kissing the devil's posterior, despite cultural variances, suggesting scripted fabrication over spontaneous revelation; Levack attributes this convergence to inquisitorial templates disseminated via printed tracts, not cross-witness corroboration.[62] Skeptics further contend that the osculum infame served propagandistic functions, symbolizing moral inversion to vilify marginalized women and nonconformists amid social upheavals like the Reformation (1517 onward), with no surviving artifacts, diaries, or non-trial sources indicating its practice outside coerced narratives. Cohn highlights how such elements mirrored earlier anti-heretical libels, which medieval chroniclers like Bernard Gui (c. 1320s) employed without verification, fostering a self-reinforcing cycle of belief that persisted until Enlightenment rationalism eroded credulity by the mid-18th century.[49] Empirical analysis of over 110,000 documented witch trials reveals the osculum infame in fewer than 10% of cases, often appended post-torture, underscoring its marginality even within accusatory frameworks and its likely status as an elaborated trope rather than historical verity.[39]
