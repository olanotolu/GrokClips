Sara Hughes

Sara Elizabeth Hughes (born February 14, 1995)[1] is an American beach volleyball player. With teammate Kelly Cheng she achieved a career-high world ranking of No. 2 in August 2023. Hughes has won six tournaments on the AVP Pro Tour and eight gold medals, one silver medal, and four bronze medals on the FIVB World Tour/Pro Beach Tour. Hughes began her beach volleyball training in Huntington Beach, California, at the age of eight. As a junior, she partnered with Cheng to win bronze medals at the 2013 U19 and 2014 U21 World Championships. Her partnership with Cheng continued through college, where the pair won 103 consecutive collegiate matches and led the USC Trojans to back-to-back NCAA Championships in 2016 and 2017. Soon after turning professional in mid-2017, Hughes and Cheng became the youngest team to win an AVP event when they won the season-ending Championship. Hughes split from Cheng in early 2018 and teamed up with Summer Ross. In their first year playing together, Hughes and Ross won their first World Tour title and entered the top ten of the world rankings. Hughes re-partnered with Cheng in late 2022, winning several Beach Pro Tour events including the Finals in Doha in January 2023.[2] Hughes and Cheng represented the United States at the 2024 Paris Olympics, reaching the quarterfinals.[3] Following the Olympics, Cheng teamed with Molly Shaw, while Hughes, after undergoing Achilles tendon surgery in January 2025 due to injury, announced a new partnership with Ally Batenhorst in July 2025.[4]

Early life and junior career

Hughes was born in Long Beach, California,[3][8] to Rory and Laura. She has an older brother, Connor, and an older sister, Lauren.[1] Her mother is a former volleyball player[9] and both her siblings played the sport in college, with Connor winning two NCAA Men's Volleyball Championships with the UC Irvine Anteaters.[1] Growing up in Costa Mesa, California, in a volleyball-playing family, Hughes regularly attended her siblings' practices and tournaments. During one such instance, a player's parent was impressed by eight-year-old Hughes' peppering and recommended her to local beach volleyball youth coach Bill Lovelace.[9] According to Hughes, she first came to love the sport when Lovelace praised her ball control as the best he had ever seen for an eight-year-old.[10] After a successful tryout,[9] she began training under Lovelace every summer in Huntington Beach until she was 15.[11] A standout junior beach volleyball player, Hughes won numerous tournaments on the Amateur Athletic Union and California Beach Volleyball Association circuits.[11] From 2004 to 2012, she was mostly partnered with Justine Wong-Orantes,[12][13] playing as a blocker.[11] With Wong-Orantes, Hughes placed ninth at the 2011 and 2012 U19 World Championships.[11] She also finished fourth at the 2012 U21 World Championships with Summer Ross.[14] The following year, Hughes began playing with Kelly Claes and transitioned into a full-time defender.[11] The duo won bronze medals at the 2013 U19 and 2014 U21 World Championships.[9] Hughes also played club indoor volleyball as the setter for Mizuno Long Beach, and was named most valuable player after her club won the 16-U Junior Olympics national championship in 2011.[15] She played indoor volleyball for Mater Dei High School as well and was the Orange County Player of the Year as a senior.[1]

College

Regarded as one of the top high school recruits for both beach and indoor,[11] Hughes committed to playing beach volleyball for the USC Trojans in her junior year of high school.[16] Beach volleyball had just become an NCAA Emerging Sport for Women at the time[17] and Hughes decided to forgo collegiate indoor volleyball as "sand was her real passion."[16] Hughes joined the Trojans in the 2013â€“14 season, partnering with Kirby Burnham as the top-flight pair throughout her freshman year. The duo won the AVCA Pairs Championship and recorded 42 wins and 4 losses by the end the season.[1] She was teamed with Claes as the Trojan's top-flight pair for the next three seasons. As sophomores, Hughes and Claes won the AVCA Pairs title and led the Trojans to their first AVCA National Championship,[9] completing the season with a 44â€“3 winâ€“loss record.[1] In their junior year, they won the inaugural Pac-12 Pairs Championship and were named the Pac-12 Pair of the Year.[18] Women's beach volleyball was also promoted to an NCAA Championship sport that year,[17] and Hughes and Claes helped the Trojans win the first-ever NCAA Beach Volleyball Championship, defeating the Florida State Seminoles' top pair in straight sets in the finals.[19] They ended the season with an undefeated 48â€“0 record and were selected to the NCAA All-Tournament Team.[1] Hughes and Claes capped off a dominant year by winning the 2016 World University Championships without dropping a set the entire tournament.[20] As seniors, the duo repeated as Pac-12 Pairs Champions and were once again named Pair of Year.[18] They led the Trojans to their second consecutive NCAA title, coming back from a first set loss in the finals to beat the top-flight duo from Pepperdine.[21] Hughes and Claes completed their senior year with a 55â€“1 winâ€“loss record,[22] amassing an overall record of 147 wins and 4 losses in their three seasons together.[23] Between their sophomore and senior years, Hughes and Claes had a win streak of 103 collegiate matches,[24][25] losing just seven sets during this run.[26] Their streak began in April 2015[26] and was broken two years later in a three-set loss to a team from the Saint Mary's Gaels.[27] Hughes was named an AVCA Division I Collegiate Beach All-American in all her four years of college.[28] She graduated with a Bachelor's degree in business administration in 2017,[29] and earned a Master's degree in Entrepreneurship and Innovation the following year.[30]

Amateur career

While still in high school and college, Hughes competed as an amateur on the domestic and international professional tours.[10][31] Her first professional tournament result was a 17th place at the 2011 Manhattan Beach Open.[31] In October 2012, she debuted in her first FIVB World Tour event at the $190K Bangsaen Thailand Open, where she and teammate Kaitlin Nielsen lost in the first round of the country quota qualifier.[31] Hughes partnered with Lane Carico to win her first international event the following year at the $8K NORCECA tournament in BoquerÃ³n, Cabo Rojo.[14] She made her Association of Volleyball Professionals (AVP) debut playing with Geena Urango at the $75K Milwaukee Open in 2014, but did not progress past the qualifying rounds.[31] After partnering with Kelly Claes, her results improved over the next two years, highlighted by three more NORCECA titles and two AVP semifinal appearances.[31] Their breakthrough came in June 2016, when Hughes and Claes narrowly lost to Olympians April Ross and Kerri Walsh Jennings with a score of 21â€“17, 18â€“21, 15â€“17[31] in the third round of the $75K AVP San Francisco Open.[10][24] Despite the loss, they eventually made it to the finals of the double-elimination tournament where they were defeated once again by A. Ross and Walsh Jennings.[31] Hughes and Claes were given a wild card entry into the main draw of the $400K Klagenfurt Major a month later, where they upset the top-seeded German team of Kira Walkenhorst and Laura Ludwig in the group stage, eventually finishing 17th;[31] Walkenhorst and Ludwig would go on to win gold at the 2016 Summer Olympics a few weeks later.[32][33]

Professional career

2017: Partnering with Claes

Hughes turned professional upon graduating from college in the summer of 2017,[23] and turned down a partnership with three-time Olympic gold medalist Walsh Jennings,[34][35] choosing instead to continue playing with her collegiate partner Claes.[33][36] In their first professional season, Hughes and Claes got their highest finish in international competition at the $115K Long Beach Presidents Cup exhibition event in July, beating Germany's Walkenhorst and Ludwig in the bronze-medal match.[37] Two weeks later, they were knocked out of the World Championships by eventual champions Walkenhorst and Ludwig for a ninth-place finish.[29] On the AVP, the 12th-seeded pair won their first title at the $112.5K Chicago Championships in September, beating Brooke Sweat and S. Ross in straight sets in the finals.[38][39] With this win, Hughes and Claes, aged 22 and 21 at the time, became the youngest team in history to win an AVP tournament.[38] On the World Tour, their best results were fifth-place finishes at the $150K Rio de Janeiro Open and the $300K PoreÄ Major.[31] Hughes and Claes ended the year ranked No. 16 in the world.[40]

2018â€“2019: Partnering with S. Ross

After ninth-place finishes in their first two World Tour tournaments of 2018, Hughes ended her partnership with Claes to team up with S. Ross.[41] According to Hughes, she made the switch because she "needed to grow a little more as a volleyball player."[42] Hughes and S. Ross entered the AVP season as the top seeds, winning two of the four events they competed in.[31] They won their first tournament together at the $100K AVP New York Open in June by defeating Nicole Branagh and Brandie Wilkerson in the final match in two sets.[43] The following month, they beat A. Ross and Alix Klineman in three sets to win another AVP title at the $79K Hermosa Beach Open.[44] The duo were runners-up to A. Ross and Klineman at the $125K Chicago Championships[45] and the $75K Hawaii Invitational in September.[46] Hughes and S. Ross also reached their first podium on the World Tour by taking the bronze medal at the $150K Espinho Open in July.[47] The pair then won their first World Tour title the next month at the $150K Moscow Open.[48] Seeded ninth in Moscow, they upset three of the top five seeds, beating the second-seeded Brazilian team of Ãgatha Bednarczuk and Eduarda Santos Lisboa in the gold-medal match.[49] After Moscow, Hughes and S. Ross were ranked No. 9 in the world, a career-best for Hughes.[50] They were awarded a wild card entry to the World Tour Finals in Hamburg at the end of the season, in which the eight top-ranked teams and two wild cards compete for the $400K prize pool.[51] As the tenth seeds in the Finals, they notched victories over the top-seeded German team of Chantal Laboureur and Julia Sude and the eight-seeded Dutch team of Sanne Keizer and Madelein Meppelink. However, losses to the fifth-seeded Heather Bansley and Brandie Wilkerson of Canada and the fourth-seeded Maria Antonelli and Carolina Solberg Salgado of Brazil meant they did not progress to the quarterfinals, finishing tied for seventh place.[52] Hughes and S. Ross concluded 2018 with a third-place finish at the $150K Yangzhou Open, defeating Canada's Sarah Pavan and Melissa Humana-Paredes in the bronze-medal match.[53] In 2019, Hughes continued with Ross for the early AVP season, earning third-place finishes at the Austin and Huntington Beach Opens. She then partnered with multiple players throughout the year, including Taryn Kloth and Betsy Black-McCoy, competing in six AVP events overall without additional wins. On the FIVB Tour, Hughes played with various partners, achieving top-10 finishes but no medals that year.[54]

2020â€“2021: Multiple partnerships

The COVID-19 pandemic limited the 2020 season. Hughes teamed with Brandie Wilkerson for AVP events, finishing third at the Atlanta Open. Internationally, she partnered with Lauren Fendrick to win gold at the FIVB 2-star Siem Reap Open in February 2020, her first FIVB title outside the Ross partnership.[55] In 2021, Hughes had several short-term partnerships due to the ongoing search for a stable teammate. On AVP, she again paired with Wilkerson, reaching semifinals in all three events. For FIVB, she won gold at the 1-star Rubavu Open with Emily Day in May. Later, with Terese Cannon, they took bronze at the Itapema Challenge in November and another bronze at the Doha 4-star. Hughes ended the year ranked in the top 20.[56][57]

2022: Partnering with Kolinske and Cheng

Hughes began 2022 partnering with Kelley Kolinske on the AVP Tour, where they played seven events and won the Waikiki Open. Internationally, the pair claimed gold at the Beach Pro Tour Itapema Challenge in May, defeating Daria Mushkova and Nadezhda Makroguzova of Russia in the final. They also reached semifinals in several other Pro Tour stops.[58] In October 2022, Hughes reunited with Kelly Cheng ahead of the Paris Olympics cycle. Their first event together was the Beach Pro Tour Torquay Challenge in November, where they won gold, beating China's Yuan Lvwen and Dong Jie in the final. They followed with another gold at the Torquay Elite 16 in December. The reunion quickly propelled them into the top rankings.[59][60]

2023â€“2024: Partnering with Cheng

Hughes and Cheng dominated the Beach Pro Tour in 2023, winning gold at the Tepic Challenge, silver at the Gstaad Elite 16, and the Doha Finals in January. They also claimed bronze at the Itapema Elite 16. Their success culminated in gold at the 2023 Beach Volleyball World Championships in Tlaxcala, Mexico, defeating Australia's Taliqua Clancy and Mariafe Artacho del Solar in the final for Hughes' first world title. The pair reached a career-high world ranking of No. 2 in August 2023. On AVP, they won the Manhattan Beach Open.[2][62] In 2024, Hughes and Cheng won gold at the Ostrava Elite 16 and bronze at the Doha Elite 16. They qualified for the Paris Olympics as the top-ranked U.S. team. At the Olympics, the duo advanced to the quarterfinals with wins over Czechia and France but were eliminated by Switzerland's Nina Brunner and Tanja HÃ¼berli in straight sets, finishing ninth overall.[63]

2025: Partnership with Batenhorst

Following the Olympics, Hughes and Cheng continued competing but parted ways in mid-2025. In July 2025, Hughes announced a new partnership with Ally Batenhorst, a former indoor volleyball standout transitioning to beach for the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics qualification. As of October 2025, the duo has begun training and competing in early qualifiers.[4]

Accolades

Hughes is the 2017 FIVB Top Rookie[64] and the 2018 AVP Best Defender.[65] She and Claes were named Sportswomen of the Year at the 2017 LA Sports Awards, organized by the Los Angeles Sports Council.[66] Additional accolades include: FIVB World Ranking No. 2 (August 2023, with Cheng); 2023 FIVB Beach Volleyball World Champion (with Cheng); AVP Most Valuable Player (2023); and participation in the 2024 Paris Olympics (9th place, with Cheng). She has accumulated seven FIVB gold medals across partnerships as of October 2025.[63]

Style of play

Hughes is a defender and right-handed right-side player.[5] Originally a blocker in her youth, she moved to the backcourt when she started playing with the taller Claes.[11] Known as a fierce competitor,[11][67][68] Hughes has been noted for her "speed and relentless pursuit of every ball."[24] Her USC head coach Anna Collier described her as "one of the fastest and smartest defenders," with the ability to anticipate her opponents' attacks.[23] According to three-time Olympian Holly McPeak, Hughes possesses the competitive drive, work ethic and athleticism necessary to compete at the professional level.[69] Of the 87 players who competed in a Major Series main draw on the 2018 World Tour, Hughes ranked 33rd for total points scored, averaging 5.61 points per set; 25th for total kills, averaging 5.21 kills per set; and 40th for number of aces, with around four percent of her serves being aces.[70]

Personal life

Hughes' childhood idol was Misty May-Treanor,[24][71] and she grew up with a poster of the three-time Olympic gold medalist in her bedroom.[67] May-Treanor, who often trained in Huntington Beach when Hughes was young, would occasionally let the latter help with her practice sessions.[10][11] May-Treanor was later the volunteer assistant coach for the USC Trojans during Hughes' freshman year, and the two have formed a close relationship according to Hughes.[71] A ball girl at AVP tournaments in her youth, Hughes also came to know two-time Olympic medalist April Ross.[71] A. Ross, a fellow Costa Mesa resident, would invite Hughes to her practices when Hughes was in high school.[71] Hughes was born to parents Rory and Laura Hughes in Long Beach, California.[3] Her mother Laura is a former volleyball player, and her family has deep roots in the sport. She has two older siblings: brother Connor, who played outside hitter at UC Irvine and helped win NCAA championships in 2012 and 2013, earning tournament MVP honors in 2013; and sister Lauren, who played volleyball at Fairfield University.[72][73] Hughes graduated from the University of Southern California with a bachelor's degree in business administration in May 2017 and earned a master's degree from USC's Marshall School of Business in 2018.[74] When Hughes and Claes were just starting to compete on the professional circuits, their biggest challenge was not being able to afford a coach. As their tournament results improved, the pair received more financial assistance from USA Volleyball and began working with Volleyball Hall of Fame inductee JosÃ© Loiola.[33] Hughes has continued to be coached by Loiola since 2016 through various professional partnerships.[7] As of 2025, she is sponsored by Nike (through 2028), Molten USA (as of March 2025), and Optimum Nutrition.[30][75][76][77]
