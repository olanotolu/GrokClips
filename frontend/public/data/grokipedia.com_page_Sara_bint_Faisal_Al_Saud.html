Sara bint Faisal Al Saud


Sara bint Faisal Al Saud (born 1935) is a member of the Saudi royal family and philanthropist renowned for advancing women's social and economic roles in Saudi Arabia.[1] As the daughter of King Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud and his wife Queen Iffat Al Thunayan, she has dedicated over six decades to non-profit initiatives focused on education, child welfare, and female empowerment.[2][1] She co-founded the Al Nahda Society in 1963, serving as its president and driving programs that support women's leadership, poverty alleviation, and community development, earning the organization UN ECOSOC accreditation in 2019.[1] Sara bint Faisal also chairs the board of trustees at Effat University, a private non-profit institution for women's higher education, and leads the Maharat Centre, which aids children with learning difficulties.[2][3] In 2013, King Abdullah appointed her to the Consultative Assembly (Majlis al-Shura), where she served until 2016 as one of the inaugural female members of this advisory body.[1][3] Married to the late Prince Mohammed bin Saud bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, former governor of Al Bahah Province, she has received awards including the King Abdulaziz Medal of First Degree for her contributions to social development.[2][1]

Early Life and Family Background

Birth and Parentage

Sara bint Faisal Al Saud was born in 1935 to Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud and Iffat bint Mohammad Al-Thunayan.[4] Her father, born on 14 April 1906, served as King of Saudi Arabia from 1964 until his assassination on 25 March 1975.[5] He married Iffat, his third wife, in 1932 after meeting her during a visit to Istanbul; she was born in 1916 in Ta'if to a family of Turkish descent and died on 17 February 2000.[6][7] As the eldest child of Iffat, Sara was the first of nine children born to the coupleâ€”five sons and four daughtersâ€”though she ranked as the sixth child overall of King Faisal, who had five children from prior marriages.[1][5] Iffat's background influenced the family; Sara learned Turkish from her mother, reflecting Iffat's non-Arabian heritage.[8]

Siblings and Upbringing

Sara bint Faisal Al Saud was born in 1935 as the eldest child of then-Prince Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud and Iffat bint Mohammad Al Thunayan, his wife of Turkish descent.[5] She was the first of nine children from this marriage, comprising four daughters and five sons.[9] Her full siblings included sisters Lulwah bint Faisal Al Saud, Latifah bint Faisal Al Saud, and Haifah bint Faisal Al Saud, as well as brothers Mohammed bin Faisal Al Saud (1937â€“2017), Saud bin Faisal Al Saud, Abdul Rahman bin Faisal Al Saud, Bandar bin Faisal Al Saud, and Turki bin Faisal Al Saud.[5][7] Raised in the royal palaces of Riyadh amid the privileges of the House of Saud, Sara and her siblings experienced an upbringing rooted in Islamic traditions and familial loyalty to the Al Saud dynasty.[5] Iffat Al Thunayan, whose family had been exiled to Istanbul following Saudi-Egyptian conflicts in the early 19th century, introduced elements of broader cultural exposure and advocated for education within the household, influencing her children's development.[5][10] This environment, combining royal discipline with Iffat's progressive outlook on learning, shaped Sara's early life prior to her father's ascension to the throne in 1964.[9]

Education

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud, as one of the daughters of King Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud and Queen Effat, received her education through private tutoring provided by European instructors.[5] This approach aligned with the limited formal educational opportunities available to Saudi royal women during her formative years in the mid-20th century, emphasizing personalized instruction over institutional attendance.[5] She pursued supplementary studies in Switzerland, reflecting the royal family's practice of exposing children to international academic environments.[5]

Philanthropic Contributions

Founding of Al-Nahda Foundation

Al-Nahda Philanthropic Society for Women (Alnahda) was established in 1382 AH (corresponding to 1962â€“1963 Gregorian) in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, as one of the kingdom's earliest nonprofit organizations dedicated to women's empowerment.[1] It was founded by Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud, the sixth daughter of King Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, alongside her sister Princess Latifa bint Faisal Al Saud, and two other women, Ms. Muzfer Adam and Ms. Samira Khasheqji.[1] The society was formally registered with the Ministry of Labor and Social Development on 1 Jumada al-Thani 1383 AH (23 July 1963), under registration number 2, marking its official recognition as a nonprofit entity limited initially to serving the Riyadh region.[1] The founding aimed to address the social and economic challenges faced by Saudi women through targeted rehabilitation programs, reflecting a pioneering effort in a conservative society where women's public initiatives were rare.[1] [11] Princess Sara, who assumed the role of president, drove the initiative to foster economic independence and social development via vocational training, skill-building projects, and community support, benefiting thousands of women over decades.[1] [12] This establishment predated many similar efforts and laid groundwork for broader philanthropic work in women's advancement within Saudi Arabia.[2]

Other Charitable Initiatives

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud received the "Knight of Arab Giving" designation in 2018 during the Pioneers of Arab Giving Award ceremony in Riyadh, honoring her longstanding contributions to social work and philanthropy in Saudi Arabia. This recognition underscores her broader role in spearheading non-profit efforts aimed at social development, distinct from her foundational work with Al-Nahda.[13] Specific additional foundations or programs under her direct patronage beyond Al-Nahda are not extensively detailed in public records, with her influence primarily channeled through established royal and societal frameworks for community welfare.

Public Service and Professional Roles

Membership in the Consultative Assembly

Sara bint Faisal Al Saud was appointed to the Consultative Assembly (Majlis al-Shura) of Saudi Arabia on 11 January 2013 by King Abdullah ibn Abdulaziz Al Saud, through a royal decree that expanded the council to 150 members and introduced 30 women for the first time in its history.[14] This appointment positioned her as one of two royal family princesses selected, alongside Moudi bint Khalid Al Saud, daughter of King Khalid, highlighting the inclusion of high-profile female royals in the advisory body tasked with reviewing laws, budgets, and international treaties.[15] [16] Her membership aligned with King Abdullah's reforms aimed at increasing women's participation in governance, though the assembly lacks legislative power and serves solely in an advisory capacity to the king.[3] The new female appointees, including Sara bint Faisal, possessed advanced qualificationsâ€”many holding PhDsâ€”and were sworn in during the council's subsequent sessions, contributing to discussions on national policy without voting rights on final decisions.[15] She served through the 2013â€“2016 term, after which subsequent royal appointments in 2017 did not renew her position, reflecting the council's four-year renewable terms selected by royal decree based on expertise and specialization.[1] During her tenure, the assembly addressed issues such as economic development and social welfare, areas intersecting with her prior philanthropic work in education and women's welfare, though specific committee assignments or sponsored recommendations attributable to her are not publicly detailed in official records.[16]

Leadership at Effat University

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud has chaired the Board of Trustees of Effat University since 1999.[17] Effat University, a private non-profit institution focused on higher education for women in Saudi Arabia, was founded that year by her mother, Queen Effat Al-Thunayan, shortly before the queen's death.[18] As board chair, Princess Sara has provided strategic oversight for the university's governance and development, working alongside President Dr. Haifa Reda Jamalallail.[19] Under her tenure, Effat evolved from a college to a chartered university in 2009, marking a decade of growth in infrastructure and accreditation.[20] The institution introduced specialized programs tailored to Saudi women's advancement, including the country's first women's degrees in architecture, electrical and computer engineering, and entrepreneurship, aligning with national goals for female workforce participation.[21] These initiatives supported Effat's mission to foster leadership and innovation among female students, with enrollment expanding to serve thousands annually by the 2020s.[22] The university earned the King Abdulaziz Award for Quality in 2017 and secured a position among the top 100 Arab universities, reflecting advancements in academic standards and research output during this period.[23] By its 20th anniversary in 2019, Effat had graduated more than 1,100 women, contributing to broader efforts in Saudi female empowerment amid reforms like Vision 2030.[23] Princess Sara's role emphasized sustaining her mother's legacy of pioneering girls' education, originally rooted in Queen Effat's establishment of Dar Al-Hanan, the kingdom's first girls' school in 1955.[24]

Personal Life

Marriage and Relationships

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud was married to Prince Mohammed bin Saud bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, a son of former King Saud bin Abdulaziz and former governor of Al Bahah Province as well as a member of the Allegiance Council.[2] [3] The marriage produced no children.[25] Prince Mohammed, who served in various governmental roles including as deputy governor of Riyadh, predeceased her.[2] Details of their relationship remain largely private, consistent with the discretion observed in Saudi royal family matters.[3]

Family and Descendants

Sara bint Faisal Al Saud was married to Prince Mohammed bin Saud Al Saud, a son of King Saud bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, who served as governor of Al-Bahah Province and sat on the Allegiance Council.[2] The couple had no children, leaving Sara without direct descendants.[26]

Honors and Recognition

Awards and Titles

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud received the King Abdulaziz Medal of the First Class in May 2013, awarded by King Abdullah for her contributions to international service, philanthropy, and public roles including leadership at Al-Nahda Foundation and membership in the Shoura Council.[1][27] In 2014, she was given Special Recognition at the Arab Woman Awards Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, acknowledging her pioneering work in women's empowerment and education.[28] As a daughter of King Faisal bin Abdulaziz Al Saud, she holds the hereditary title of Princess (Her Royal Highness) within the House of Saud, reflecting her position in the extended royal family.[2]

Public Acknowledgments

In 2013, during the 50th anniversary celebration of the Al-Nahda Philanthropic Society for Women, Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques King Abdullah bin Abdulaziz issued a letter of appreciation publicly acknowledging Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud's longstanding contributions to charitable work and advocacy for Saudi women's issues. The letter commended her half-century of service, emphasizing her role in fostering community development through the society she helped establish.[29][30] The event, held on April 14 in Riyadh and attended by over 500 members and dignitaries, featured the reading of the King's message, which served as an official endorsement of her leadership as president of Al-Nahda. This acknowledgment underscored her efforts in capacity-building programs for women, aligning with broader national initiatives for social progress.[29]

Legacy and Impact

Influence on Saudi Women's Roles

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud exerted influence on Saudi women's roles primarily through her leadership of the Al Nahda Philanthropic Society for Women, which she founded in 1962 as one of the kingdom's earliest charitable organizations dedicated to female advancement. The society focuses on economic and social rehabilitation programs, including skill-building for labor market entry, networking opportunities with prominent Saudi women, and initiatives to raise awareness of women's rights and encourage broader societal participation. These efforts have targeted poverty alleviation among low-income households and promoted women's involvement in cultural, economic, and social spheres, fostering greater self-sufficiency and challenging entrenched gender barriers through evidence-based advocacy.[12][1][31] In parallel, her role as chairwoman of Effat University's Board of Trustees has advanced women's higher education, with the private, non-profit institutionâ€”established in 1999 and dedicated exclusively to female studentsâ€”providing access to undergraduate and graduate programs in fields such as engineering, business, and architecture under her oversight. This has enabled thousands of Saudi women to attain advanced qualifications, correlating with increased professional integration and leadership positions, as the university emphasizes practical skills aligned with national development needs.[2] Collectively, these endeavors have modeled sustainable pathways for female empowerment within Saudi cultural and legal contexts, contributing to a gradual expansion of women's public roles by prioritizing education, economic agency, and social engagement over decades of institutional building.[1][2]

Broader Societal Contributions

Princess Sara bint Faisal Al Saud co-founded Al Nahda Philanthropic Society for Women in 1962 alongside her sister Princess Latifa bint Faisal and other collaborators, establishing it as one of the earliest charitable organizations in Saudi Arabia dedicated to social welfare.[2][32] As longstanding chairwoman, she has directed the society's efforts toward economically and socially empowering women through financial aid, vocational training, and employment facilitation, targeting widows, divorcees, orphans, and low-income families.[1][31] The organization, formally registered in 1963 and later accredited by the United Nations Economic and Social Council in 2019, has advocated for policy reforms enabling women's workforce participation, such as factory employment in the 1990s and municipal voting rights in subsequent years.[1][33] Her leadership extended Al Nahda's scope to poverty alleviation and sustainable development initiatives, including monitoring Saudi commitments to women's rights under international frameworks and fostering female leadership in community roles.[1] These programs have supported thousands of beneficiaries by providing resources for self-sufficiency, contributing to gradual expansions in women's societal participation amid Saudi Arabia's conservative cultural norms.[31] Additionally, Princess Sara has chaired the Maharat Centre, which addresses educational needs for children with learning difficulties, broadening her impact to family and youth welfare.[2] Her nearly six decades of non-profit stewardship earned recognition, including the King Abdul Aziz Medal of the First Degree in 2013 for advancing social development.[29]
