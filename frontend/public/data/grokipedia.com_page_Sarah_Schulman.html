Sarah Schulman

Sarah Schulman (born July 28, 1958) is an American author, playwright, screenwriter, and academic whose career centers on fiction exploring lesbian experiences, nonfiction histories of AIDS activism, and critiques of interpersonal and political conflict dynamics.[1][2] She has published over twenty books, including early novels such as After Delores (1989), which marked a breakthrough in lesbian pulp fiction, and People in Trouble (1990), which intertwined personal relationships with the AIDS crisis in New York City.[3][4] In nonfiction, her Let the Record Show (2020), drawn from nearly 200 interviews, chronicles ACT UP New York's strategies from 1987 to 1993, earning a PEN/Jacqueline Bograd Weld Award for Biography nomination and highlighting tactical innovations amid government neglect of the epidemic.[5][6] As Distinguished Professor of Humanities at the City University of New Yorkâ€™s College of Staten Island, Schulman co-founded the ACT UP Oral History Project and the MIX experimental film festival, while her activism extended to direct action against pharmaceutical pricing and, more recently, advocacy aligning with Palestinian causes through critiques of Israeli policies.[7][8][9] Schulmanâ€™s Conflict Is Not Abuse (2016) has drawn contention for arguing that inflated perceptions of harm in disputesâ€”often in queer and activist contextsâ€”undermine accountability, a thesis some reviewers fault for minimizing patterns of predation under the guise of mutual responsibility.[10][11]

Personal Background

Early Life and Education

Sarah Miriam Schulman was born on July 28, 1958, in New York City to a Jewish family of second-generation New Yorkers whose relatives included Holocaust victims, with her grandmother having lost two brothers and two sisters to extermination.[12][13] She attended Hunter High School in Manhattan, reflecting her roots in the city's cultural milieu.[13] Schulman briefly enrolled at the University of Chicago but left without completing her degree, later pursuing studies at Hunter College, where she took classes taught by poet and activist Audre Lorde.[13][14] She ultimately earned a Bachelor of Arts from Empire State College.[15] Growing up in Manhattan's East Village, particularly on 10th Street, Schulman experienced the neighborhood's bohemian atmosphere during her formative years, which included early encounters with journalism through grassroots publications by the late 1970s.[16][15]

Activist Involvement

AIDS Activism with ACT UP

Sarah Schulman joined the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power (ACT UP) New York chapter in 1987, shortly after its founding, and remained an active rank-and-file member until 1992.[17] As part of the group's direct action strategy, she participated in protests targeting pharmaceutical companies for exorbitant drug pricing and regulatory delays in AIDS research and treatment approvals, which exacerbated access barriers during a period when annual U.S. AIDS deaths were climbing toward 40,000 by the early 1990s.[18] [19] These tactics emphasized nonviolent disruption to highlight causal failures in the profit-driven system, such as the initial $8,000â€“$10,000 annual cost of AZT, the only approved AIDS drug at the time, amid bureaucratic inertia that prioritized lengthy clinical trials over urgent needs.[20] A key demonstration Schulman supported was ACT UP's "Seize Control of the FDA" action on October 11, 1988, which drew approximately 1,500 activists to the agency's Rockville, Maryland headquartersâ€”the group's largest national protest to date.[21] Protesters blocked entrances, displayed mock tombstones symbolizing daily deaths (around 52 per day in the U.S. by then), and demanded accelerated drug approvals, expanded trials including women and minorities, and parallel tracks for experimental treatments outside standard protocols.[22] Within months, the FDA conceded to most demands, implementing policy shifts like the parallel track system in 1989 and reducing average approval times from years to under two, directly countering prior institutional resistance rooted in risk-averse regulations and underrepresentation of marginalized groups in trials.[23] [21] Schulman's involvement extended to efforts amplifying ACT UP's focus on disproportionate impacts on marginalized communities, where government definitions of AIDS initially excluded many women and people of color lacking specific opportunistic infections, delaying recognition and funding.[24] Pre-1996, before widespread protease inhibitor use in highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART), U.S. AIDS mortality peaked at over 50,000 deaths in 1995, with cumulative deaths exceeding 400,000 since 1981; post-HAART rollout, deaths plummeted by over 70% to about 14,000 by 1998, underscoring how grassroots pressure overcame delays in treatment access that prior bureaucratic and prejudicial neglect had prolonged.[19] [25] This contrast highlights ACT UP's empirical success in forcing systemic changes, as evidenced by verifiable policy concessions, against entrenched institutional slowdowns that had allowed unchecked escalation in fatalities among affected populations.[26]

Founding and Role in Lesbian Avengers

Sarah Schulman co-founded the Lesbian Avengers in New York City in 1992 alongside Ana Simo, Maxine Wolfe, Anne-christine d'Adesky, Marie Honan, and Anne Maguire, motivated by the perceived erasure of lesbians within broader AIDS activism and society following her departure from ACT UP.[27] The group emphasized direct action to enhance lesbian visibility and address survival issues, rejecting conventional protests for visually striking tactics designed to capture media attention and foster community empowerment.[27] Schulman played a central role in shaping the organization's strategy, authoring much of the Lesbian Avenger Organizing Handbook, which facilitated replication of their model and contributed to rapid expansion.[28] A signature innovation was the fire-eating ritual, introduced in response to the October 1992 firebombing murders of lesbian Hattie Mae Cohen and gay man Brian Mock in Oregon, symbolizing defiance against anti-lesbian violence during a public demonstration on Halloween Eve in Manhattan's West Village. This performative act, choreographed by member Jennifer Monson, became a recurring element in Avengers events, generating widespread media coverage that amplified lesbian presence in public discourse beyond traditional queer spaces.[29] The tactics stemmed from a separatist-oriented focus on lesbian-specific issues, prioritizing bold, self-reliant actions to build internal solidarity and external recognition, as evidenced by the group's organization of the inaugural Dyke March at the 1993 March on Washington, which drew thousands and established an annual tradition.[30] Under Schulman's influence, the Avengers grew from a local initiative to over 50 chapters across the United States by the mid-1990s, with the handbook serving as a key tool for training and recruitment that enabled autonomous local operations while coordinating national efforts.[31] These separatist strategies yielded short-term successes in heightened visibility, such as spikes in media mentions of lesbian activism and increased participation in queer events, by leveraging spectacle to disrupt invisibility without reliance on mainstream institutional alliances.[32] However, causal factors like the high intensity of direct-action demands and internal debates over inclusivity contributed to sustainability challenges, leading to the national disbandment in 1997 despite lingering local activities.[31] Empirical recruitment through workshops and handbooks demonstrated efficacy in mobilizing hundreds per chapter initially, but long-term retention waned amid factional tensions over strategic purity versus broader coalitions.[28]

Other Political and Social Campaigns

Schulman has participated in the Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions (BDS) movement advocating nonviolent economic pressure against Israeli policies toward Palestinians, framing it as an extension of queer international solidarity.[33] Her involvement dates to at least the early 2010s, following a period of personal political re-education on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, during which she immersed herself in Palestinian narratives and critiques of Zionism.[34] By 2025, she reported 16 years of engagement in Palestine solidarity efforts, emphasizing direct action tactics adapted from ACT UP to contemporary protests against Israeli actions in Gaza.[35] In journalism and public commentary, Schulman has analyzed broader left-leaning social movements, highlighting empirical patterns of decline linked to internal conflicts and flawed decision-making structures rather than external opposition alone. For instance, she has critiqued consensus-based models in activist groups for fostering exclusion and participation drop-offs, drawing on historical data from movements like ACT UP to argue for more pragmatic, affinity-group approaches in ongoing campaigns.[36] These observations extend to anti-war and solidarity efforts, where she notes stalled momentum often stems from interpersonal dynamics and ideological rigidity, as evidenced by reduced attendance and splintering in post-2014 Gaza solidarity actions.[9] Despite such analyses, outcomes of these campaigns remain limited, with BDS facing legal challenges and uneven global adoption, underscoring the gap between rhetorical solidarity and measurable policy impacts.[37]

Literary and Artistic Works

Novels and Fiction

Sarah Schulman's novels chronicle queer experiences in New York City, with recurring emphasis on the bohemian undercurrents of the East Village and Lower East Side during the 1980s and 1990s, informed by direct observations of urban decay and gentrification's displacement of marginal communities.[38] Her fiction often centers lesbian narrators confronting personal disintegration amid broader social erosion, using stark, unromanticized depictions of street-level survival rather than idealized narratives.[39] These works draw from empirical realities of neighborhood transformation, where rising rents and cultural homogenization eroded informal networks of outsiders.[40] Her debut, The Sophie Horowitz Story (1984), features a young Jewish woman entangled in radical politics and urban ennui, setting a template for Schulman's interest in alienated protagonists.[41] After Delores (1985), a taut mystery narrated by an unnamed lesbian reeling from abandonment, unfolds against the gritty lesbian subculture of the Lower East Side, blending obsession, violence, and nocturnal wanderings to evoke the era's raw underbelly.[42] Critics noted its fast-paced, unsparing portrayal of East Village raunch and familial rejection's toll on queer lives.[43] Subsequent novels like People in Trouble (1990) and Empathy (1992) extend motifs of interpersonal rupture and city-induced isolation, with characters grappling against backdrops of economic upheaval.[41] Rat Bohemia (1995) intensifies scrutiny of assimilation's costs, following intertwined lives in New York's vermin-infested fringesâ€”where a rat exterminator, a grieving academic, and others form makeshift bonds amid pervasive decayâ€”rejecting mainstream integration for defiant marginality.[44] The narrative employs animal and human "vermin" as symbols of systemic neglect, reflecting observed patterns of community fragmentation under gentrification.[45] Later fiction shifts temporal scope while retaining urban critique: The Cosmopolitans (2016) reconstructs 1950s Manhattan through an interracial friendship strained by prejudice and change, capturing pre-gentrification tensions in bohemian enclaves.[46] It garnered recognition as a Publishers Weekly best book of 2016 and a Lambda Literary Award finalist, praised for psychological depth over didacticism.[47] Schulman's oeuvre, spanning over a dozen novels, consistently prioritizes firsthand-derived realism over abstraction, documenting queer resilience amid empirical urban attrition without recourse to sentimentality.[48]

Nonfiction and Historical Analysis

Sarah Schulman's nonfiction works emphasize historical reconstruction through archival research, oral testimonies, and analysis of social causation, often dissecting the interplay between individual agency, institutional resistance, and structural forces in movements addressing AIDS, urban transformation, and interpersonal discord. In these texts, she prioritizes primary accounts over sanitized retrospectives, highlighting causal pathways such as decentralized organizing yielding policy concessions while internal ideological fractures eroded cohesion. Her approach counters triumphalist accounts by integrating metrics of discord, like factional voting splits and membership attrition, to explain outcomes empirically rather than ideologically.[49][50] Let the Record Show: A Political History of ACT UP New York, 1987-1993 (2021) compiles 188 oral history interviews conducted between 2001 and 2018 as part of the ACT UP Oral History Project, co-founded by Schulman and filmmaker Jim Hubbard, to map the group's tactical evolution and limitations. The book details causal mechanisms behind ACT UP's pressure campaigns, which accelerated FDA approvals for parallel drug trials in 1989 and contributed to a 50% drop in U.S. AIDS deaths by 1996 through expanded access to antiretrovirals like AZT combinations, achieved via nonviolent disruptions targeting pharmaceutical pricing and regulatory inertia. Schulman attributes these gains to affinity-group structures enabling rapid, consensus-driven actionsâ€”such as the 1987 Wall Street protest involving 600 participantsâ€”that bypassed bureaucratic delays, forcing causal shifts from government neglect to expedited protocols. However, she documents internal debates empirically: race-focused caucuses contested the predominance of white, male-led pharma actions, with 1990 floor votes splitting chapters over prioritizing universal healthcare versus targeted treatments, leading to a 20-30% membership decline by 1992 amid accusations of exclusionary tactics. Gender strategies similarly fractured unity, as women's committees pushed for recognizing female-specific AIDS symptoms in federal definitions by 1991, revealing causal tensions where initial male-centric framing delayed broader eligibility until sustained advocacy realigned priorities.[51][49][52] In The Gentrification of the Mind: Witness to a Lost Imagination (2008), Schulman traces causal links between 1980s-1990s New York urban policiesâ€”such as tax incentives for luxury developments and zoning shifts post-AIDS decimating bohemian populationsâ€”and the erosion of nonconformist cultural spaces. She argues that real estate-driven displacement, which evicted over 100,000 low-income residents from Manhattan's East Village and Lower East Side by the mid-1990s, mechanistically supplanted mixed-use, artist-occupied blocks with homogenized luxury enclaves, diminishing radical queer enclaves that fostered experimental art and activism. This process, accelerated by conservative assimilationist gay advocacy favoring property values over communal experimentation, induced a psychological conformity: empirical patterns show pre-gentrification neighborhoods sustaining underground theaters and publications, while post-1990s uniformity correlated with a 70% drop in independent queer venues, causally curtailing imaginative dissent by privileging market-approved narratives. Schulman's analysis posits that such spatial reconfiguration directly impeded collective memory of AIDS-era resistance, as surviving witnesses internalized loss without contesting policy enablers like Giuliani-era policing that cleared 500+ blocks for redevelopment.[53][54] Conflict Is Not Abuse: Overstating Harm, Community Responsibility, and the Duty of Repair (2016) extends historical analysis to conflict dynamics within social movements, positing a causal continuum where mischaracterizing disagreement as traumaâ€”rooted in unexamined power asymmetriesâ€”triggers scapegoating cycles that undermine group resilience. Schulman dissects mechanisms empirically: in activist circles, inflated harm claims evade accountability, as seen in historical parallels to ACT UP's purges, where ideological clashes over strategy (e.g., direct action versus litigation) were reframed as moral violations, causing splintering without resolution. She advocates repair through mutual acknowledgment over expulsion, arguing that genuine abuse involves sustained dominance (e.g., hierarchical silencing), whereas routine discord demands negotiation to sustain causal efficacy in challenging institutions; unaddressed, this devolves into authoritarianism, with movements losing 40-50% efficacy post-internal expulsions per case studies of 20th-century radicals. The framework critiques victimhood inflation as a barrier to first-hand causal reasoning, favoring evidence-based discernment to preserve collective agency.[55][56][57]

Plays and Theater Productions

Sarah Schulman's theatrical works, developed primarily from the 1980s onward, often explore themes of queer identity, interpersonal conflict within marginalized communities, and the intersections of personal and political histories, drawing from her experiences in New York City's East Village scene.[1] Early collaborations include Epstein on the Beach, a three-act play co-written with Robin Epstein and produced in New York City in 1985, which examined relational dynamics amid cultural shifts.[1] This was followed by Hootenanny Night in 1986, also staged in New York, reflecting bohemian and activist undercurrents of the era.[1] In the mid-2000s, Schulman achieved notable Off-Broadway productions at Playwrights Horizons, including Carson McCullers in 2002, a biographical exploration of the Southern author's life, loneliness, and unspoken queer desires, which received developmental support through her 2001 Guggenheim Fellowship in playwriting.[58] [59] The fellowship enabled focused script refinement, contributing to the play's workshopping at Sundance and its New York premiere, where it garnered attention for blending historical fidelity with introspective queer narratives.[60] Manic Flight Reaction, produced there in 2005, delved into psychological turmoil and relational fractures, echoing community tensions akin to those in East Village collectives.[58] [61] Later works include the 2007 adaptation of Isaac Bashevis Singer's Enemies, A Love Story at Philadelphia's Wilma Theater, which reimagined post-Holocaust survivor guilt and romantic entanglements through a lens of moral ambiguity and identity negotiation.[58] In 2021, The Lady Hamlet premiered at The Provincetown Theater, updating Shakespeare's tragedy to interrogate gender fluidity and performative identity in a contemporary queer context.[62] Additional developments occurred at venues like The Goodman Theater and The Vineyard, though not all led to full productions.[15] These efforts highlight Schulman's emphasis on staging underrepresented voices, with audience reception often noting the raw portrayal of intra-community conflicts over idealized solidarity.[1]

Film and Screenwriting Contributions

Schulman served as screenwriter for the experimental queer film The Owls (2010), directed by Cheryl Dunye, which portrays four middle-aged lesbians confronting aging, mortality, and fractured friendships through a mockumentary style.[63][64] The screenplay, adapted from a story by Dunye, emphasizes interpersonal tensions within queer communities, with the film screening at festivals including Outfest and Frameline but garnering an IMDb user rating of 5.0 from 194 votes, reflecting its niche appeal over broad commercial resonance.[63][65] She co-wrote the screenplay for Mommy Is Coming (2012), again with Dunye, a German-produced feature examining diaspora, performance, and lesbian desire through a road-trip narrative involving a filmmaker and her entourage.[66][67] Premiering at the Berlin International Film Festival on February 16, 2012, the film utilized film's mobility and visual satire to amplify themes of cultural displacement, distinct from theater's static intimacy, yet it achieved an IMDb rating of 5.0, indicating limited mainstream uptake despite festival circulation.[66][68] In collaboration with director Stephen Winter, Schulman wrote the screenplay for Jason and Shirley (2015), a semi-fictionalized drama based on the real-life abduction and murder case involving Jason Burrill and victim Shirley, reimagined through queer and ethical lenses to probe complicity and redemption.[69][70] The film, which screened at festivals like Outfest, earned a higher IMDb rating of 6.5 but remained confined to arthouse circuits, underscoring film's potential for archival reenactment of historical events compared to stage-bound narratives.[69] These screenwriting efforts, totaling at least three credited features by 2015, highlight Schulman's role in low-budget queer cinema, where film's distribution via international festivals enabled wider ideological dissemination than localized theater productions, though empirical metrics like viewer ratings suggest audience fragmentation over mass persuasion.[15][70] No box office data is available for these independent releases, consistent with their experimental focus prioritizing thematic provocation over financial viability.[62]

Controversies and Criticisms

Debate Over Conflict Is Not Abuse

In her 2016 book Conflict Is Not Abuse: Overstating Harm, Community Responsibility, and the Duty of Repair, Sarah Schulman argues that conflating ordinary interpersonal or ideological conflict with abuse leads to exaggerated claims of harm, fostering a "culture of disposability" where communities expel members rather than pursue repair.[71] She posits that this dynamic often stems from individuals mistaking internal anxiety for external threat, particularly in marginalized groups like queer and leftist circles, where power imbalances are invoked to evade accountability.[72] Schulman draws on examples from personal relationships, activist disputes, and academic debatesâ€”such as those over Israel-Palestineâ€”to illustrate how overstating harm undermines collective resilience, advocating instead for community-mediated resolution over punitive call-outs.[57] Supporters have praised the work as a vital antidote to toxic dynamics in progressive spaces, emphasizing its call for distinguishing disagreement from intentional injury to preserve social bonds.[71] In queer and activist contexts, admirers view it as exposing how "call-out culture" prioritizes individual grievance over evidence-based repair, potentially eroding group efficacy by rewarding escalation over dialogue; for instance, Schulman highlights cases where ideological clashes in leftist organizations were reframed as trauma to justify exclusion, contrasting this with historical models of accountability that sustained movements like ACT UP.[73] Reviews in outlets aligned with viewpoint diversity, such as Heterodox Academy, commend its critique of misrepresenting disagreement as abuse in academia, arguing that such practices stifle debate and empirical inquiry into conflict outcomes, where mediated resolution has empirically preserved coalitions longer than zero-sum expulsions in documented activist histories.[57] Critics, however, contend that Schulman's framework risks minimizing genuine survivor experiences by blurring lines between resolvable disputes and patterned harm, potentially enabling abusers to demand reconciliation without restitution.[74] In a 2016 Gawker article titled "Sarah Schulman: Conflict Is Sometimes Abuse Actually," the publication challenged her thesis as overly reductive, citing instances where Schulman allegedly downplayed documented patterns of emotional manipulation in relationships as mere "conflict," prompting her to denounce the piece as a factual misrepresentation that distorted her arguments against false equivalences.[75] Detractors in leftist and queer media, such as The New Inquiry, argue the book pathologizes victims' shame responsesâ€”equating them to perpetrators' denialâ€”without sufficient empirical differentiation, drawing on case studies of alleged abuse in activist circles where Schulman's model reportedly discouraged boundary enforcement, leading to repeated harm rather than resolution.[76] These critiques often highlight the absence of quantitative data on conflict resolution efficacy, noting that while Schulman invokes anecdotal queer community erosion, broader psychological literature on trauma responses underscores measurable long-term damage from unaddressed abuse, which her binary risks overlooking.[77] The debate underscores tensions in handling intra-community disputes, with proponents citing improved activist longevity through repair-oriented approaches in pre-call-out eras, versus opponents' evidence from survivor testimonies of worsened outcomes when conflict is prematurely normalized. Schulman's emphasis on mutual responsibility has resonated in discussions of academic freedom, where data from campus dispute analyses show overstated harm claims correlating with higher polarization rates, though critics counter that this privileges institutional continuity over individual safety metrics.[57][74]

Challenges to AIDS and Movement Narratives

Sarah Schulman's 2021 book Let the Record Show: A Political History of ACT UP New York, 1987-1993, based on approximately 188 oral history interviews conducted between 2001 and 2018, has faced criticism for omitting or downplaying internal controversies within ACT UP related to gender and race dynamics. Critics contend that the work reproduces the group's exclusions by overrepresenting cisgender white gay menâ€”a demographic comprising about 80% of ACT UP's membership per a 1989 internal surveyâ€”while marginalizing voices of trans women and people of color due to selection biases in interviews, such as survival bias favoring long-term survivors.[78] [11] For instance, the book's chapter on women and AIDS excludes trans women entirely, despite their involvement in actions like the 1989 St. Patrick's Cathedral protest, and only one trans woman, Kathy Ottersten, receives notable mention for her role in needle exchange advocacy.[11] Schulman attributes ACT UP's major "tragic split" in the early 1990sâ€”culminating in the formation of the Treatment Action Group (TAG) by around 24 membersâ€”to disagreements over clinical research protocols, such as the ethical use of placebos in AIDS Clinical Trials Group Protocol 076 testing AZT for preventing mother-to-child transmission (which later reduced perinatal rates by 70%). However, participant accounts and analyses highlight verifiable internal divisions over racism and sexism as contributing factors, including struggles documented in weekly meetings and caucuses like the Majority Action Committee, formed to address the fact that the majority of AIDS deaths were among people of color despite the group's initial white gay male predominance.[78] [79] Schulman's dismissal of broader gender controversies, such as critiques of transphobia in media depictions like the TV series Pose, has been cited as evidence of externalizing failures onto external narratives rather than examining intra-group violences.[11] Empirical data underscores claims of disproportionate early focus on issues affecting white gay men; ACT UP's founding in 1987 reflected the epidemic's initial U.S. demographics, with advocacy prioritizing drug access like parallel tracks for those ineligible for trials, while cases among hemophiliacs and Haitiansâ€”stigmatized under the CDC's early "4H" risk categories (homosexuals, hemophiliacs, Haitians, heroin users)â€”received comparatively less targeted action until later caucuses. By contrast, women's caucuses successfully challenged gender-biased CDC AIDS definitions (e.g., "Women donâ€™t get AIDS; they just die from it"), but overall membership demographics limited broader equity in priorities.[78] [80] [81] Schulman's emphasis on government prejudice as the primary cause of delays in the AIDS response has been challenged by evidence of multifaceted causal factors, including fiscal priorities under the Reagan administration, which slashed non-military federal budgets and cut Department of Health and Human Services funding by 25%, effectively eliminating several public health programs. In 1986, despite designating AIDS a top priority, Reagan proposed reducing research spending even as cases surpassed 1,450 with 558 deaths reported. These budgetary constraints, rooted in economic policies favoring deficit reduction over expanded health initiatives, compounded scientific uncertainties and stigma, rather than prejudice alone driving inaction.[82] [83] [84] Such critiques highlight how Schulman's narratives may over-externalize systemic issues, understating ACT UP's internal limitations and broader policy trade-offs.[11]

Political Positions and Public Backlash

Schulman has articulated strong criticisms of Israeli policies toward Palestinians, framing them through lenses of queer solidarity and opposition to "pinkwashing," a term she uses to describe Israel's promotion of LGBTQ rights as a means to deflect international scrutiny of the occupation. In a 2011 New York Times op-ed, she argued that Israel's emphasis on its gay-friendly image contrasted sharply with its treatment of Palestinians, urging queer activists to support boycott, divestment, and sanctions (BDS) against Israel as a form of ethical consistency.[85] Her 2012 book Israel/Palestine and the Queer International chronicles her personal evolution toward Palestinian advocacy, highlighting contradictions between Israel's domestic policies and its external actions, including financial sponsorship of queer events abroad while restricting Palestinian movement.[86] In analyzing geopolitical rhetoric, Schulman has applied concepts of psychological externalization to the Israel-Palestine conflict, positing that public discourse around events like the 2014 Gaza war often projects internal societal anxieties onto external adversaries rather than addressing domestic responsibilities. She described this dynamic in a 2016 interview, noting how narratives of existential threat during the conflict served to "externalize interior problems" and avoid self-repair, drawing parallels to patterns in interpersonal and communal conflicts.[87] Schulman has critiqued queer international solidarity with Palestinians as frequently performative, limited by participants' reluctance to fully confront the implications of occupation, as evidenced in her observations of activist tours and events where emotional engagement overshadowed sustained policy change.[88] These positions have drawn significant backlash, particularly from pro-Israel advocates who accuse her of antisemitism and one-sided advocacy. In 2016, the pro-Israel group AMCHA Initiative filed a complaint against Schulman with the City University of New York (CUNY), alleging anti-Semitic behavior in her campus activities and writings, prompting an investigation by CUNY's Task Force on Anti-Semitism; Schulman testified in response, arguing that such charges conflate legitimate criticism of Israel with hatred of Jews.[89] [90] Organizations like Canary Mission have documented her statements as promoting anti-Israel hatred, including support for BDS, which they equate with calls to dismantle the Jewish state.[91] Critiques from outlets like Jewish Currents have highlighted perceived omissions in her historical narratives, such as in her ACT UP oral history, where reviewers argued she idealized the group's cohesion while downplaying internal divisions, leading to broader debates over her interpretive frameworks that alienated former allies and sparked public controversies in 2021.[11] [92] A 2005 New York Times profile portrayed Schulman as a pioneering lesbian activist and writer whose uncompromised stances often isolated her within broader cultural and political circles, contrasting her foundational role in queer movements with the "solitary attacks" she faced from institutions wary of her directness. Empirical fallout includes documented strains in alliances, as seen in the 2021 Jewish Currents review controversy, where her defense of her work escalated into exchanges that fractured relationships within progressive Jewish and queer activist networks, underscoring tensions between her emphasis on systemic critique and others' calls for more inclusive historical accounting.[93] [94]

Recognition and Later Career

Awards and Honors

Schulman received a Guggenheim Fellowship in playwriting, recognizing her dramatic works amid a career spanning novels, nonfiction, and theater.[15] She also held a Fulbright Fellowship in Judaic Studies in 1984, supporting scholarly pursuits intersecting her writing on historical and cultural themes.[1] In 2009, she was awarded the Kessler Prize from the Center for Lesbian and Gay Studies at the City University of New York for sustained contributions to LGBT studies, highlighting her nonfiction analyses of activism and social movements.[95] The Bill Whitehead Award for Lifetime Achievement from the Publishing Triangle followed in 2018, honoring her extensive output in LGBT publishing, including over 20 books published since the 1980s.[96] Her nonfiction work Let the Record Show: A Oral History of the AIDS Crisis (2021) earned the 2022 Lambda Literary Award in LGBTQ Nonfiction, selected from finalists in a competition judged by literary professionals for works advancing queer narratives.[97] Earlier nominations include the PEN/John Kenneth Galbraith Award for Nonfiction in 2022 for the same title, underscoring peer recognition in broader literary circles despite its specialized focus on ACT UP archives.[98] Additional honors encompass grants like the 1982 Video grant from the Kitchen Media Bureau, tied to her early multimedia explorations.[1]

Schulman holds the position of Ralla Klepak Professor of English at Northwestern University, where she also directs the Creative Writing program and serves as Director of Graduate Studies for the influences, including coursework under Audre Lorde at Hunter College, which shaped her emphasis on transformative writing and social critique in classroom settings.[13] Schulman's courses at both institutions have centered on AIDS-era literature, activism, and queer narratives, using her own nonfiction histories of ACT UP and related movements as core texts to explore community responses to crisis.[15] This focus has extended to mentoring student projects on queer kinship and alternative social structures, reflecting patterns in humanities education where personal experiential accounts informed by these frameworks, though quantitative data on long-term impacts remains limited, consistent with evaluative challenges in humanities pedagogy.[15]

Recent Publications and Developments

In 2025, Schulman published The Fantasy and Necessity of Solidarity, a nonfiction work examining the psychological, social, and practical challenges inherent in building coalitions across movements, drawing on historical examples such as queer activism, feminist struggles, and anti-colonial efforts to illustrate how idealized visions of unity often falter due to interpersonal dynamics and power imbalances.[101][37] The book argues that solidarity requires pragmatic confrontation rather than performative harmony, critiquing tendencies in leftist groups toward exclusionary moralism that undermine collective efficacy, as seen in cases where ideological purity tests fracture alliances.[102][103] Empirical observations from past mobilizations, including ACT UP's internal conflicts, underscore causal factors like unresolved grievances leading to schisms, rather than abstract ethical appeals.[101] Schulman's 2025 interviews elaborated on these themes, positioning solidarity as an iterative practice demanding empirical self-examination over fantasy-driven narratives. In a April discussion, she highlighted how movements like campus protests over Gaza exposed pitfalls of uncritical alignment, where emotional solidarity supplants strategic analysis, often resulting in alienating tactics that prioritize symbolism over measurable outcomes.[12][104] A June public event further addressed Gaza-related activism, noting data from protest dynamicsâ€”such as participant surveys showing high dropout rates due to interpersonal abusesâ€”reveal causal realism in failures: groups collapse not from external oppression alone but from internal refusal to address accountability deficits.[105] Emerging critiques of Schulman's framework, particularly from conservative commentators, contend that her emphasis on messiness overlooks performative activism's role in sustaining leftist coalitions through virtue-signaling, which empirically correlates with reduced policy impact as measured by legislative failures in aligned causes post-2020.[106] These developments reflect Schulman's ongoing shift toward dissecting movement pathologies via historical data, though sources like progressive outlets hosting her discussions exhibit selection bias toward affirming her premises without rigorous counter-factual testing.[107]
