2023 Kogi State gubernatorial election

The 2023 Kogi State gubernatorial election was held on 11 November 2023 to elect the governor of Nigeria's Kogi State for a four-year term, succeeding term-limited incumbent Yahaya Bello of the All Progressives Congress (APC).[1] Ahmed Usman Ododo, the APC candidate and Bello's anointed successor, won decisively with 446,237 votes, representing approximately 58.6% of valid votes cast, as declared by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC).[2][3] Ododo's main challengers were Murtala Yakubu Ajaka of the Social Democratic Party (SDP), who garnered 189,199 votes (24.8%), and Dino Melaye of the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), who received only 46,362 votes (6.1%), underscoring the opposition's fragmentation and inability to mount a credible threat in the APC stronghold.[2][4] The election occurred amid ethnic tensions between the dominant Igala and Ebira groups, with voting patterns reflecting regional strongholds, particularly APC dominance in Ebira areas.[5] Despite reports of localized violence, voter intimidation by masked thugs, and allegations of ballot stuffing raised by opposition partiesâ€”claims that INEC dismissed for lack of substantiationâ€”the poll was deemed largely free and fair by the electoral body, though Ajaka pursued legal challenges through the tribunal and up to the Supreme Court, which ultimately upheld Ododo's victory.[6][7] Melaye, citing judicial inefficacy, opted not to contest the results, highlighting broader skepticism toward Nigeria's post-election dispute resolution mechanisms.[8] The outcome reinforced APC's incumbency advantage in Kogi, where governance under Bello had prioritized infrastructure but faced criticism for debt accumulation and security lapses.[4]

Electoral Framework

Electoral System

The gubernatorial election in Kogi State follows the framework established by the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, 1999 (as amended), and the Electoral Act 2022, administered by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC).[9][10] INEC conducts voter registration, accreditation, voting, and result collation at polling units, local government areas (LGAs), and state levels, with Kogi State comprising 21 LGAs and approximately 1.8 million registered voters for the 2023 cycle.[11][12] Eligible voters, aged 18 and above with valid permanent voter's cards, undergo accreditation using the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS), which employs biometric fingerprint and facial recognition to verify identity and prevent multiple voting or impersonation.[13] Voting occurs via secret ballot at designated polling units, where voters select one candidate by marking the corresponding box or party symbol on paper ballots, typically with thumbprint or pen; no electronic voting machines are used for casting votes.[14] Polling officials manually sort, count, and record votes on result sheets (Form EC8A) at each unit, with party agents and observers present; results are then collated hierarchically to the state collation center.[15] The winner is determined by the candidate receiving the highest number of valid votes statewide, provided they also secure at least 25% of votes cast in no fewer than two-thirds (14) of Kogi's 21 LGAs, as mandated by Section 179(1) of the Constitution.[9] Failure to meet both thresholds triggers a supplementary election between the top two candidates, excluding votes from affected LGAs if necessary; a tie for highest votes leads to an inconclusive declaration and rerun.[9] Under the Electoral Act 2022, INEC must electronically transmit polling unit results to the INEC Result Viewing Portal (IReV) for public viewing, enhancing transparency, though manual collation remains authoritative.[10][16] In the November 11, 2023, election, votes were invalidated only for over-voting or ambiguity, with final declaration by the state returning officer after LGA collation.[17]

Political Background

Incumbent Administration and Term Limits

Yahaya Bello of the All Progressives Congress (APC) served as Governor of Kogi State from January 2016 to January 2024, having been elected in the 2015 gubernatorial election following the death of incumbent Abubakar Audu and subsequently re-elected in 2019 with 58.61% of the vote.[20] His administration emphasized infrastructure development, including road construction and urban renewal projects in Lokoja and other areas, alongside human capital initiatives such as youth empowerment programs and education reforms.[21] In the health sector, Bello's government established the Kogi State Health Insurance Agency to reduce out-of-pocket expenses and expanded primary healthcare facilities.[22] Security improvements were also prioritized, with claims of transforming Kogi into one of Nigeria's safer states through vigilant policing and community engagements, though these efforts faced criticism amid ongoing communal clashes and banditry incidents.[23] Bello's tenure was marked by fiscal challenges, including rising state debt and allegations of financial mismanagement, with the administration defending expenditures as necessary for development amid federal revenue shortfalls.[24] Controversies included disputes with labor unions over unpaid salaries and pensions, as well as federal probes into corruption post-tenure, though Bello maintained these were politically motivated.[21] His governance style, often described as centralized and decisive, fostered APC dominance in Kogi but drew accusations of suppressing opposition voices, influencing the 2023 election's focus on continuity under his endorsed successor.[20] Under Section 182(1)(a) of the 1999 Constitution of Nigeria (as amended), no person may be elected governor more than twice, limiting incumbents like Belloâ€”who completed his second four-year termâ€”to ineligibility for the 2023 race. This constitutional provision, unchanged as of the 2023 election cycle despite periodic reform proposals for single six-year terms, compelled Bello to support a successor rather than seek re-election, aligning with the two-term norm observed across Nigerian states since 1999.[25] The term limit reinforced APC's internal primaries to select a proxy, emphasizing legacy preservation over personal extension of power.[26]

State Political Dynamics

Kogi State, located in Nigeria's North Central region, features a diverse ethnic composition that profoundly shapes its political dynamics, with the Igala predominant in Kogi East Senatorial District (encompassing nine local government areas), the Ebira in Kogi Central (five local government areas), and the Okun (a Yoruba subgroup) in Kogi West (six local government areas).[27][5] These groups, alongside minorities such as the Bassa, Nupe, and Ogori-Magongo, form the state's estimated 4.5 million population, where ethnic identity often drives voter alignments and candidate selections.[28] Political competition frequently hinges on perceived equity in power distribution, fostering informal zoning practices that rotate the governorship among the three senatorial districts to mitigate inter-ethnic tensions and promote stability.[29] Historically, the People's Democratic Party (PDP) dominated Kogi's governance from state creation in 1991 through 2011, producing governors like Adamu Atta and Ibrahim Idris, often from Igala strongholds, reflecting early ethnic leverage in the east.[30] This era saw PDP securing consistent victories amid national party strength, but internal defections and alignment with federal shifts eroded its hold.[31] The All Progressives Congress (APC) disrupted this pattern in 2015, with Yahaya Bello, an Ebira from Kogi Central, defeating PDP incumbent Idris Wada, capitalizing on APC's national momentum post-2015 presidential win and Bello's appeal to youth and security-focused voters.[31] Bello's re-election in 2019 solidified APC dominance, attributed to incumbency advantages, infrastructure projects like road networks, and control over state security resources, though marred by violence allegations.[32] By 2023, amid Bello's term limits, state politics intensified around succession and zoning adherence, with stakeholders in Kogi West and East advocating rotation away from Central after eight years of Ebira leadership to uphold equity precedents.[33] Bello dismissed strict zoning, prioritizing merit and party unity, leading APC to nominate Ahmed Usman Ododo, another Ebira, which fueled intra-party rifts and opposition narratives of ethnic favoritism.[34] This decision amplified multi-ethnic contestation, as PDP fielded Dino Melaye (Okun from West) and SDP's Murtala Ajaka (Igala from East), positioning the race as a de facto ethnic triad despite candidates' cross-ethnic outreach on issues like economic diversification and insecurity.[5][29] APC's entrenched machinery, bolstered by Bello's influence and federal ties, contrasted with fragmented opposition, underscoring how incumbency and resource control often override zoning ideals in causal power retention.[35]

Primary Elections

All Progressives Congress Primaries

The All Progressives Congress (APC) conducted its gubernatorial primary election for Kogi State on April 14, 2023, to select its candidate for the November 11, 2023, general election.[36] The primary was held at the party's state secretariat in Lokoja, utilizing indirect voting by delegates.[37] Ahmed Usman Ododo, the former Auditor General for Local Governments in Kogi State and a close ally of incumbent Governor Yahaya Bello, emerged as the winner with 78,704 votes.[36] [38] Other contestants included Salami Momodu Ozigi, who received 1,506 votes, and additional aspirants such as former Senator Smart Adeyemi, though detailed vote tallies for all were not uniformly reported across sources.[37] Patrick Obahiagbon, a former House of Representatives member serving as the primary's returning officer, announced the results on April 15, 2023, declaring Ododo unopposed in the final count after initial screenings.[39] Ododo's victory was attributed by party officials to strong grassroots support and alignment with Bello's administration, which had endorsed him as a continuity candidate focused on ongoing infrastructure and security initiatives.[38] The primary faced allegations of irregularities from aggrieved aspirants, including claims of absent election materials, insufficient electoral officers, and manipulation favoring Ododo.[40] Smart Adeyemi described the process as fraudulent, asserting non-compliance with party guidelines and lack of transparency in delegate accreditation.[41] These complaints led to legal challenges, such as a suit by Adeyemi seeking to nullify the primary, which was dismissed by a Federal High Court in Abuja on July 12, 2023, for lack of merit.[42] Separate petitions by party members to disqualify Ododo and five others were filed but did not alter the candidacy outcome.[43] Despite disputes, the APC National Working Committee upheld Ododo's nomination, citing adherence to internal procedures.[38]

People's Democratic Party Primaries

The People's Democratic Party held its indirect gubernatorial primary for the 2023 Kogi State election on April 15, 2023, at Kafa's Hall event centre in Lokoja, where party delegates voted to select the candidate.[44] Multiple aspirants participated, including former Senator Dino Melaye from Kogi West senatorial district, former Deputy Governor Yomi Awoniyi, and businessman Idoko Ilonah, amid competition reflecting internal party divisions over zoning and delegate selection.[45][46] Dino Melaye emerged victorious, securing 313 votes from the delegates, far outpacing his rivals, with his closest challenger receiving significantly fewer.[44][47] The result was announced the following day, April 16, 2023, positioning Melaye as the PDP's standard bearer against the incumbent All Progressives Congress administration.[45] The primary faced immediate challenges, as aspirants including Awoniyi rejected the delegate list, alleging irregularities in its compilation and imposition by party leaders, which they claimed undermined a fair process.[46] Melaye defended the outcome, attributing it to grassroots support and dismissing critics as spoilers, though the disputes highlighted ongoing PDP internal fractures in Kogi State, where the party had struggled in prior elections.[48] No formal legal challenges overturned the result, and Melaye proceeded as the nominee.[44]

Social Democratic Party Primaries

The Social Democratic Party (SDP) nominated Murtala Yakubu Ajaka as its candidate for the 2023 Kogi State gubernatorial election through a consensus adoption process rather than a competitive primary. Ajaka, a businessman and politician from Kogi Central senatorial district who had previously aspired under the All Progressives Congress (APC) but was disqualified from that party's primary, defected to the SDP following his APC setback.[49] The adoption was formalized on April 29, 2023, positioning Ajaka as the party's unopposed flagbearer without reports of rival aspirants or intra-party contests.[49] This approach aligned with practices among smaller opposition parties in Nigeria, where consensus selections often bypass formal primaries to conserve resources and unify support ahead of off-cycle polls.[50] No significant controversies or legal challenges emerged from the SDP's selection process, contrasting with primaries in major parties like the APC and PDP. Ajaka's nomination was cleared by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) in its final list of candidates published in October 2023.[51]

Campaign Period

Major Issues and Platforms

The primary issues dominating the 2023 Kogi State gubernatorial election campaigns included persistent insecurity, economic challenges such as incomplete salary payments and unemployment, inadequate infrastructure, demands for zonal rotation of the governorship, and deficiencies in education and healthcare delivery.[52][53] Insecurity, particularly banditry, kidnappings, and communal clashes, was a recurrent concern, with stakeholders highlighting its potential to disrupt voting and exacerbate voter apathy; reports noted over 100 insecurity incidents in the lead-up to the November 11 election, including attacks in Kogi Central and West senatorial districts.[54] Economic grievances centered on the state government's practice of paying public sector salaries at reduced percentagesâ€”often 50-70%â€”due to revenue shortfalls, which candidates linked to mismanagement and over-reliance on federal allocations amid high poverty rates exceeding 60% in rural areas.[55] Infrastructure deficits, including dilapidated roads and unreliable power supply, were tied to the state's agrarian economy, where poor connectivity hindered agricultural exports from key areas like the Igala and Ebira farmlands.[52] Zonal rotation debates intensified ethnic tensions among the Igala (East), Ebira (Central), and Okun/Yoruba (West) groups, with West and East advocates arguing that the incumbent All Progressives Congress (APC) administration under Yahaya Belloâ€”rooted in Centralâ€”had monopolized power since 2016, violating informal equity norms despite no constitutional mandate.[56][57] Candidates' platforms largely reflected these issues, with the APC's Ahmed Usman Ododo emphasizing continuity of Bello's policies, including sustained investments in security architectureâ€”such as vigilante integrations and road rehabilitations that reportedly reduced banditry incidents by 40% in 2022â€”and expansion of free education and agricultural mechanization programs to boost youth employment.[58] Ododo, an Ebira from Kogi Central, positioned his agenda as building a "prosperous Kogi" through infrastructure legacies like the ongoing Ajaokuta-Kabba road project and fiscal prudence to address salary arrears, though critics noted his platform avoided explicit commitments to zonal rotation.[52] The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) candidate, Dino Melaye from Kogi West, campaigned on restoring workers' welfare by pledging to end percentage-based salary payments within his first 100 days, alongside anti-corruption drives to recover misappropriated funds for healthcare and education upgrades; his platform appealed to Okun voters by invoking power shift to the West and critiquing the APC's security record amid rising kidnappings.[55][59] Melaye promised economic diversification through mineral resource exploitation in Kogi's solid minerals belt, aiming to create 50,000 jobs via small-scale industries, though his proposals drew skepticism from analysts citing past implementation gaps in similar PDP state agendas.[60] Social Democratic Party (SDP) candidate Murtala Ajaka, also from Kogi Central, focused on private-sector-led growth, vowing to tackle unemployment via agro-industrial hubs and improved security through community policing enhancements, while addressing infrastructure by prioritizing federal road interventions like the Lokoja-Abuja highway upgrades.[52] His platform implicitly challenged APC dominance by highlighting governance transparency and youth inclusion, though it received less media elaboration compared to rivals, amid his post-primary fallout with Bello's faction.[61] Overall, campaigns featured calls for issue-based discourse over ethnic mobilization, yet violence and vote-buying overshadowed substantive policy debates in several local government areas.[62]

Strategies and Endorsements

The All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate, Usman Ododo, centered his campaign on continuity with the policies of incumbent Governor Yahaya Bello, emphasizing infrastructure development, security enhancements, and inclusive governance to address unemployment and healthcare gaps.[63] Ododo's strategy included grassroots community outreach with local leaders and data-driven voter analytics via social media to broaden appeal beyond his Ebira base in Kogi Central.[63] [64] He received strong endorsement from Bello, who positioned Ododo as his anointed successor following the APC primaries on April 14, 2023, leveraging the party's control of 22 out of 25 state assembly seats for mobilization.[65] [66] Ododo selected a running mate from Kogi East to counter ethnic balancing concerns and expand support in Igala-dominated areas.[64] The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) candidate, Dino Melaye, pursued a strategy of anti-incumbency agitation, highlighting unpaid salaries, pensions, and a deficient health sector under the APC administration to portray the state as neglected.[67] Melaye promised to rebuild infrastructure, including a "new Lokoja," within four years and focused on grassroots mobilization in Kogi West's Okun communities while assuring voters of protection against alleged APC intimidation.[68] [67] [64] Endorsements included the National Association of Okun Students, Kogi Central leaders, PDP Senator-elect Natasha Akpoti-Uduaghan, and Diasporans for PDP, aiming to consolidate PDP's historical 13-year governance legacy and appeal to marginalized groups via a female running mate.[69] [70] [71] [72] Social Democratic Party (SDP) candidate Murtala Ajaka, a former APC member who defected after losing the primaries, targeted Igala voters in Kogi Eastâ€”comprising about 50% of the state's populationâ€”through grassroots efforts and positioning himself as a consensus alternative to APC dominance.[64] His approach included selecting a running mate from Kogi Central to mitigate ethnic isolation, though the SDP lacked legislative seats for structural support.[64] Ajaka garnered endorsements from Kogi-East elders, traditional rulers in Ogori-Magongo, Kogi indigenes in Abuja, and Igala stakeholders who viewed him as a unified choice.[73] [74] [75] [76]

Election Conduct

Pre-Election Logistics and Security

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) published the final list of 18 gubernatorial candidates on June 9, 2023, following primaries concluded by June 6, with campaigns running from June 14 to November 9, 2023.[17] INEC registered 1,932,591 voters in Kogi State for the election, deploying 3,876 Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) machines across 3,508 polling units.[64][54] Permanent Voter Cards (PVCs) were distributed from September 11 to October 9, 2023, leaving 119,052 uncollected, while ad-hoc staff recruitment via the INECPRES portal occurred from August 31 to October 2, 2023.[17][64] Logistical challenges arose from flooding in nine local government areas, including Ibaji and Lokoja, prompting INEC to recommend Nigerian Navy boats for material transport to riverine zones.[64] INEC conducted a mock voter accreditation exercise on October 14, 2023, in nine selected polling units to test BVAS functionality and procedures.[64] Voter education efforts focused on BVAS usage and peaceful conduct, though civil society observers noted incomplete uploads of polling and collation agents by early October, with only 37.6% of polling agents and 21.9% of collation agents registered.[64] Security preparations addressed high risks from ethnic rivalries among Ebira, Okun, and Igala groups, historical electoral violence since 2003â€”including the 2019 killing of PDP leader Salome Abuhâ€”and recent incidents like campaign office arsons and convoy clashes.[54] Assessments identified 20 of 21 local government areas as violence-prone, with flashpoints in Dekina and Lokoja due to thuggery, banditry, and kidnappings exacerbating tensions.[64][54] The Nigeria Police Force deployed 40,000 officers under Deputy Inspector-General Habu Sani, while military presence was intensified to deter disruptions, following President Tinubu's directive against interference.[77][78] Agencies committed to robust pre-election patrols, prioritizing high-risk polling units amid concerns over incumbency leverage and economic desperation fueling vote-buying.[54][79]

Voting Day Operations

Polling stations across Kogi State opened for the gubernatorial election on November 11, 2023, with INEC officials arriving as early as 7:30 a.m. in 64% of observed units, though 40% of units started accreditation and voting after 8:00 a.m.[80][81] By 9:30 a.m., processes had begun in 53% of sampled polling units, utilizing the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) for fingerprint and facial recognition to verify voters simultaneously with ballot issuance.[80][82] BVAS devices were deployed in all observed polling units, recording zero pre-accredited voters in 94% of cases prior to opening, which helped minimize discrepancies in initial tallies.[80] Functionality issues arose sporadically, such as failures to display accreditation data in units like PU 047 in Adankolo Ward, Lokoja LGA, or malfunctions in Kabawa PU, where alternatives like Voter Identification Numbers were temporarily employed before quick resolutions.[81] Manual accreditation occurred rarely, as in PU 006, Ward 03, Ofu LGA.[80] Election materials, including ballot papers and result sheets, had been distributed to local government areas the prior day, supporting operational rollout despite minor delays in some remote wards like those in Ogori-Magongo LGA.[83] Voter participation remained low throughout the day, with sparse queues reported at most polling units, contributing to efficient but underwhelming accreditation volumes.[84] Closing proceeded at 4:30 p.m. where no queues persisted, followed by manual collation of results at ward and local government levels for onward transmission, with INEC emphasizing BVAS-uploaded data to the IReV portal for transparency.[82]

Incidents of Violence and Irregularities

The 2023 Kogi State gubernatorial election on November 11 was preceded by several incidents of political violence, including an alleged attack on Governor Yahaya Bello's convoy in June 2023, attributed by the governor to supporters of the Social Democratic Party (SDP) candidate Murtala Ajaka.[85] On July 9, 2023, the SDP campaign office in Lokoja was set ablaze by arsonists following Ajaka's visit, amid escalating tensions.[85] Further unrest occurred on August 28, 2023, in Anyigba, Dekina Local Government Area (LGA), during the commissioning of Ajaka's campaign office, where the structure was vandalized for the second time and one death was reported.[85] On election day, violence was limited but notable in Dekina LGA, where Umaru Iduh was fatally shot by military personnel while attempting to snatch a ballot box in Anyigba town.[86][87] The Nigeria Civil Society Situation Room documented 19 violence incidents across Kogi, Imo, and Bayelsa states by noon, with specific occurrences in Kogi's Anyigba area.[88] In Olamaboro LGA, authorities arrested the Senior Special Assistant to the Governor at Sabon Gari Roundabout in Ogugu Town, recovering N28 million in cash and firearms, suggesting potential orchestration of disruptions.[86] Election observers noted Kogi's overall security as relatively stable compared to Imo and Bayelsa, though non-compliance with pre-election peace accords contributed to isolated clashes.[86] Electoral irregularities included widespread reports of pre-filled result sheets before voting began, observed in five LGAs: Adavi, Ajaokuta, Ogori/Magongo, Okehi, and Okene.[88] A specific case occurred at Polling Unit 004 in Eno Ward, Ogori/Magongo LGA, where INEC pledged investigation but proceeded with collation from affected areas without resolution.[86][88] Over-voting was documented in multiple units, yet INEC did not consistently cancel results exceeding 2022 Electoral Act thresholds, alongside failures in Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) synchronization and real-time uploads to the INEC Result Viewing Portal (IReV).[88][87] Vote buying surfaced in Okene and Lokoja LGAs, the second- and third-most populous voter areas, while late arrivals of INEC officials delayed proceedings in 40% of observed polling units.[87] INEC later denied systemic irregularities in result collation for Kogi, Imo, and Bayelsa, attributing discrepancies to isolated technical issues.[89]

Election Results

Official Results

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) declared Ahmed Usman Ododo, the All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate, as the winner of the Kogi State gubernatorial election held on November 11, 2023, during a collation exercise in Lokoja on November 12, 2023.[2][3] Ododo secured 446,237 votes, surpassing the Constitutional threshold of a simple majority and obtaining at least 25% of votes in more than two-thirds of the state's 21 local government areas.[2][90] Murtala Yakubu Ajaka of the Social Democratic Party (SDP) came second with 259,052 votes, while Dino Melaye of the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) received 46,362 votes.[90] Other candidates, including Leke Abejide of the African Democratic Congress (ADC) with 21,819 votes, trailed significantly.[90] Of 1,932,474 registered voters, 794,500 were accredited, yielding approximately 773,470 valid votes excluding minor parties and rejected ballots.[90]

Criticisms of INEC and Security Forces

Opposition parties, particularly the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), alleged that the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) engaged in result falsification during the collation process, including repeated changes to figures and the use of pre-recorded result sheets before voting concluded.[95] Over-voting was reported in 17 of Kogi's 21 local government areas (LGAs), yet INEC proceeded with collation without enforcing Electoral Act 2022 provisions to nullify affected results.[95] [88] Civil society observers, including the Nigeria Civil Society Situation Room, documented pre-filled result sheets in five LGAsâ€”Adavi, Ajaokuta, Ogori/Magongo, Okehi, and Okeneâ€”without subsequent INEC investigation or cancellation.[88] Additionally, biometric voter accreditation system (BVAS) data was not synchronized in real-time with the INEC Results Viewing Portal (IReV), compromising transparency despite legal requirements.[88] Yiaga Africa highlighted anomalies such as the failure to conduct voting in certain polling units and urged probes into unprofessional conduct, though their sampled-unit analysis aligned with official outcomes.[97] [98] Security forces faced accusations of collusion with political actors, facilitating vote buying and failing to curb 19 reported violence incidents by midday on November 11, including clashes in Anyigba, Dekina LGA.[88] PDP claimed distribution of police and National Youth Service Corps (NYSC) uniforms to civilians by All Progressives Congress (APC) affiliates enabled thuggery, with one NYSC member reportedly receiving one million naira from APC agents.[95] Observers noted security agents' complicity undermined neutrality, though pre-election assessments had flagged risks like ethnic tensions and historical violence patterns.[88] [54] These claims were raised in post-election petitions dismissed by the Kogi State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal and Court of Appeal for lack of sufficient proof.[99]

Viewpoints from Stakeholders

The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) gubernatorial candidate, Dino Melaye, rejected the election results, asserting that "there was no election in Kogi" and accusing the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) of allocating votes rather than conducting a credible poll.[100] Melaye highlighted instances of pre-filled result sheets and irregularities, claiming these undermined the process.[101] The PDP as a party echoed this rejection, declaring the announced results invalid due to alleged over-voting, voter suppression, and failure to adhere to electoral laws, while vowing to pursue legal redress.[102][95] The Social Democratic Party (SDP) candidate, Murtala Yakubu Ajaka, accused INEC of manipulating results through discrepancies in voter accreditation and ballot figures, particularly citing over-voting in multiple local government areas.[103] Ajaka expressed determination to reclaim his mandate via judicial means, framing the contest as a fight against electoral theft despite initial reluctance to litigate.[104] In contrast, the All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate and declared winner, Ahmed Usman Ododo, celebrated the victory as a reflection of voter endorsement, dedicating it to the people of Kogi State and calling for unity to advance development.[105] APC stakeholders, including party affiliates, described the outcome as well-deserved and indicative of effective campaigning, while commending INEC's conduct.[106] INEC maintained that the election was free, fair, and credible, refuting claims of tampering with accredited voter figures and attributing isolated reports of pre-filled sheets to procedural lapses rather than systemic fraud.[107] The commission suspended voting in affected wards and emphasized compliance with result transmission protocols via the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS).[108] Civil society observers offered mixed assessments. Yiaga Africa validated INEC's official talliesâ€”APC at 57.04%, SDP at 33.11%, and PDP at 5.93%â€”as consistent with their parallel vote tabulation estimates from sampled polling units, noting alignment in turnout (around 40%) but flagging pre-filled result sheets in several local government areas, BVAS glitches in 6% of units, and minor incidents of intimidation as threats to integrity.[109] The Nigeria Civil Society Situation Room reported pervasive vote buying (with inducements up to N30,000 per voter), 19 violence incidents including fatalities, logistical delays in material distribution, and confirmed pre-filled sheets, concluding that these marred an otherwise improved process compared to prior off-cycle polls.[110]

Post-Election Litigation

Tribunal Proceedings

The Social Democratic Party (SDP) and its candidate, Murtala Ajaka, filed a petition at the Kogi State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal in Abuja shortly after the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) declared Usman Ododo of the All Progressives Congress (APC) the winner of the November 11, 2023, election, alleging electoral malpractices, irregularities, and non-compliance with the Electoral Act that purportedly affected the results.[111] The petition named Ododo, APC, and INEC as respondents and sought to nullify the declaration and declare Ajaka the winner.[111] Separately, the Action People's Party (APP) filed a petition but sought and obtained permission to withdraw it on February 20, 2024.[112] The Labour Party's candidate, Dino Melaye, announced on November 29, 2023, that he would not challenge the results at the tribunal.[113] Proceedings on the SDP petition included pre-hearing sessions to streamline issues, followed by substantive hearings where Ajaka's counsel presented witnesses and documentary evidence alleging over-voting, ballot stuffing, and other infractions.[114] Respondents, including INEC, countered with defenses emphasizing substantial compliance with electoral laws and the absence of evidence showing irregularities pervasive enough to alter the outcome; INEC closed its defense on April 16, 2024.[115] The tribunal adjourned for adoption of final written addresses on April 26, 2024, after which parties submitted their arguments.[116] On May 13, 2024, the three-member tribunal panel, chaired by Justice Ado Birnin-Kudu, concluded hearings and reserved judgment, later fixing May 27, 2024, for delivery.[114] [117] In its ruling on May 27, 2024, the tribunal dismissed the petition, holding that Ajaka failed to prove his allegations beyond reasonable doubt and that Ododo met the constitutional requirements for election; it thereby affirmed Ododo's victory as governor.[111] [118]

Appellate and Supreme Court Rulings

The Court of Appeal, Abuja Division, delivered its judgment on July 11, 2024, unanimously upholding the Kogi State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal's affirmation of Usman Ododo's victory in the November 11, 2023, election.[119] [120] A three-member panel ruled that Murtala Ajaka, the Social Democratic Party (SDP) candidate, and his party failed to prove allegations of over-voting and non-compliance with the Electoral Act, 2022, beyond reasonable doubt, as required under Nigerian electoral jurisprudence.[119] The court also dismissed claims of certificate forgery against Ododo, classifying them as pre-election matters cognizable only by the Federal High Court, thereby lacking jurisdiction in the post-election petition.[119] On August 23, 2024, the Supreme Court of Nigeria unanimously dismissed Ajaka's appeal (SC/CV/654/2024) against the concurrent findings of the tribunal and Court of Appeal, led by a five-member panel chaired by Justice Sadiq Umar.[121] [122] The apex court resolved three principal issues in favor of Ododo, holding that the appeal was incompetent and devoid of merit due to the petitioners' failure to substantiate claims of forgery (reiterated as a pre-election issue), over-voting, and non-compliance.[122] It further invalidated key prosecution evidence, such as testimony from witness PW-1, as incompetent on grounds of documentary hearsay and material inconsistencies, thereby affirming Ododo's election with 446,237 votes as valid.[121] [122] The Supreme Court also dismissed related appeals by the Action Alliance candidate (SC/CV/653/2024) as time-barred, having been filed beyond the 14-day statutory limit under the Constitution, and imposed a N5 million cost against them; the Peoples Redemption Party's challenge was similarly rejected for lacking merit.[121] [122] These rulings exhausted all appellate remedies, conclusively validating Ododo's tenure as Governor of Kogi State.[121][123]
