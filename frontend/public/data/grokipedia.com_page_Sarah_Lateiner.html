Sarah Lateiner

Sarah "Bogi" Lateiner is an American master automobile technician, entrepreneur, educator, and television host who specializes in automotive repair and has focused on initiatives to increase women's participation in the industry as both professionals and customers.[1][2] After earning a degree from Oberlin College in women's studies, pre-law, and politics, she transitioned to automotive training at Universal Technical Institute and the BMW STEP program, becoming a dealer-trained BMW technician with seven years of experience at dealerships before achieving ASE Master Technician certification.[1] In 2006, she founded and operated 180 Degrees Automotive, an award-winning boutique repair shop in Phoenix, Arizona, for over a decade, starting initially from her driveway.[1][2] Lateiner later established Girl Gang Garage to support women through automotive education, including nationwide car care classes and shop evaluations aimed at addressing gender gaps in the field.[2] She gained prominence as a host on MotorTrend's All Girls Garage, where she demonstrates repair techniques, and has appeared on Garage Squad, while also leading projects like all-female vehicle builds to inspire participation in skilled trades.[1][2]

Early life

Childhood and family

Sarah Lateiner was born on December 15, 1977, in Flushing, a neighborhood in the New York City borough of Queens.[3] She spent much of her childhood in Montclair, New Jersey, approximately 12 miles west of Manhattan.[4] Lateiner grew up in a middle-class household lacking any familial tradition or enthusiasm for automobiles, with no immediate relatives sharing her eventual passion for mechanics or vehicles.[5][6] This environment provided little automotive inspiration, as her family and peers showed minimal interest in cars during her formative years.[7]

Initial interest in automobiles

Sarah Lateiner, known professionally as Bogi Lateiner, developed her initial interest in automobiles during her teenage years, despite growing up in a family with no particular affinity for cars. At age 16, she purchased a dilapidated Volkswagen Beetle for $500 using money earned from babysitting, marking the beginning of her hands-on engagement with vehicle repair.[6] This acquisition stemmed from a personal obsession with VW Beetles, which she had long planned to own as her first car.[6] Frustrated by negative encounters at repair shops, where she faced criticism and unhelpful attitudes, Lateiner sought self-education through automotive magazines, repair manuals, and informal guidance.[3] [8] She enrolled in her high school auto shop classâ€”the second girl to do soâ€”despite discouragement from counselors, peers, and teachers who questioned her suitability for the field.[6] [8] There, with assistance from her shop teacher and classmates, she undertook a complete restoration of the Beetle, an experience that solidified her passion and demonstrated her capability in mechanical work.[1] [8] These early efforts not only empowered Lateiner personally but also highlighted broader barriers for women in automotive pursuits, as she noted the scarcity of female representation in repair literature beyond superficial imagery.[8] The successful rebuild boosted her confidence, motivating her to challenge stereotypes and pursue formal training in auto mechanics rather than initially planned paths like law school.[1] [6]

Education and training

High school experiences

Lateiner attended high school in Montclair, New Jersey, where she developed an early hands-on interest in automobiles.[9] She purchased a used Volkswagen Beetle for $500 and rebuilt it extensively as part of her auto shop class, gaining practical mechanical skills from the ground up.[9] During her high school years, Lateiner participated in a year-long study abroad exchange program in Hungary organized by Rotary International, during which she adopted the nickname "Bogi," derived from a local term of endearment.[1] This international experience, combined with frustrations from prior interactions at automotive repair shops, motivated her to enroll in the auto shop class and deepen her understanding of vehicle maintenance.[10] Despite these pursuits, her post-high school plans initially focused on higher education rather than trades, as she graduated intending to pursue law.[11]

Vocational training at UTI

Sarah Lateiner enrolled at Universal Technical Institute (UTI) in Avondale, Arizona, in the Automotive Technology program shortly after graduating from Oberlin College, forgoing planned attendance at Harvard Law School to pursue hands-on training in automotive repair.[11] She selected UTI for its structured, ground-up curriculum that combined theoretical instruction with practical application, which she viewed as ideal for someone lacking prior industry experience.[12] Lateiner completed the core automotive program in 2001, during which she was one of only seven female students in a predominantly male cohort.[12] The training emphasized foundational skills in vehicle diagnostics, repair techniques, and systems operation, supported by faculty and peers who provided encouragement amid the field's gender imbalances.[12] Following the core program, she advanced to Manufacturer-Specific Advanced Training (MSAT), completing the BMW STEP specialization in 2002, which focused on BMW-specific diagnostics, electrical systems, and performance tuning.[13] [12] This vocational pathway equipped her with certifications enabling dealership employment, though she later encountered hiring barriers as a woman in the trade.[1]

Professional career

Early employment and certifications

Following her completion of Universal Technical Institute's core automotive program and BMW FASTTrack training in 2001, Lateiner encountered difficulties securing initial employment in the field but ultimately began as an apprentice at a BMW service shop.[12] She advanced to roles at BMW dealerships, first in New York, where she worked as a technician, before relocating to Phoenix, Arizona, around 2004 to join a local BMW dealership.[12] In these positions, she functioned as a dealer technician for roughly seven years, specializing in BMW repairs and consistently being the only woman on staff.[1] During her early career, Lateiner pursued manufacturer-specific and industry-standard certifications to bolster her expertise. She completed BMW factory training through the STEP program, which provided advanced diagnostics and repair skills for BMW vehicles.[1] Additionally, she attained Automotive Service Excellence (ASE) Master Technician certification, validating proficiency across multiple automotive systems.[1] These credentials, earned amid her dealership tenure, positioned her as a BMW-certified mechanic capable of handling complex European vehicle servicing.[14]

Ownership of 180 Degrees Automotive

Sarah Lateiner founded 180 Degrees Automotive in Phoenix, Arizona, in October 2006, initially operating the business from her driveway as a one-woman auto repair shop focused on general vehicle maintenance and repairs.[1][15] Over the subsequent 13 years of ownership, she expanded the operation into a full-service facility, achieving ASE certification for herself and growing the business to employ additional technicians while maintaining 100% ownership.[14][16] The shop specialized in diagnostic services, brake repairs, and engine work, serving a diverse clientele in central Phoenix and earning recognition for quality service, including awards from industry groups such as the Car Care Council Women's Board, which named Lateiner Female Shop Owner of the Year in an earlier phase of the business's development.[16][4] Lateiner's management emphasized practical, hands-on training for staff, drawing from her vocational background to prioritize efficiency and customer education on vehicle issues, which contributed to the shop's reputation as a reliable independent repair center.[17][18] By 2019, after 12 years and six months of operation, Lateiner transitioned away from daily ownership of 180 Degrees Automotive to pursue broader educational and media initiatives, marking the end of her direct involvement in the shop's management.[15] During her tenure, the business demonstrated viability in a male-dominated industry, with Lateiner crediting its success to rigorous self-taught business acumen and persistence in overcoming initial logistical challenges like limited space and funding.[14][1]

Media and business expansion

Television appearances

Lateiner co-hosted the reality television series All Girls Garage on the Motor Trend network (previously Velocity, a Discovery Channel affiliate) starting with its premiere on March 3, 2012.[13] The program features female mechanics undertaking automotive repair projects to demonstrate technical skills and challenge stereotypes in the male-dominated trade.[19] Lateiner, appearing under her nickname "Bogi," contributed as a certified BMW technician and shop owner, participating in over 180 episodes across 12 seasons through at least 2022.[20][1] In 2021, Lateiner joined the cast of Garage Squad, another Motor Trend series focused on transforming stalled automotive projects for viewers, as a co-host alongside mechanics Bruno Massel and Joe Elma.[8] Her role leveraged her expertise in diagnostics and repairs, with the addition announced to bring diverse perspectives to the show's hands-on builds.[21] Episodes featuring Lateiner aired starting in late 2021, emphasizing practical problem-solving in real-world garage settings.[7]

Founding of Girl Gang Garage

Girl Gang Garage was founded in October 2016 by Sarah "Bogi" Lateiner in Phoenix, Arizona, in partnership with design technologist and strategist Shawnda Williams.[15][22] Lateiner, an ASE-certified master technician with prior experience owning 180 Degrees Automotive, established the initiative to create a community-focused space dedicated to hands-on automotive training and empowerment for women in male-dominated skilled trades.[23][2] The organization aimed to provide a judgment-free environment where women could explore vehicle repair, fabrication, and custom builds, addressing barriers such as lack of access to inclusive learning opportunities in the automotive sector.[23][24] Initially self-funded by Lateiner, the garage focused on collaborative projects using scrapyard vehicles to train participants in practical skills like welding, painting, and mechanical assembly, with an emphasis on all-female teams to foster confidence and skill-building.[23] The founding vision stemmed from Lateiner's observations of gender disparities in the industry, where women comprised less than 2% of automotive technicians, prompting her to prioritize low-cost classes and restoration opportunities to recruit and retain female talent.[25][26] By 2017, the group debuted its first major project, a restored 1957 Chevrolet pickup truck named "Montage" displayed at the SEMA show, involving approximately 90 women aged 8 to over 70 and marking an early success in community engagement and visibility.[23] This build exemplified the garage's model of transforming novices into capable mechanics through structured, supportive programs, while later efforts secured sponsorships to expand operations without compromising its core mission.[23][27]

Advocacy and influence

Efforts to recruit women into automotive trades

Lateiner founded Girl Gang Garage in 2016 as a primary vehicle for recruiting women into automotive trades, beginning with an all-female restoration of a 1957 Chevrolet truck dubbed the "Montage," in partnership with BASF to highlight female participation in vehicle repair and customization.[1][10] The initiative emphasizes hands-on training in welding, bodywork, painting, and mechanical repair, targeting women of all experience levels to build skills and confidence in a field where women comprise less than 3% of automotive mechanics and about 9.6% of repair and maintenance employees.[28][27] Through Girl Gang Garage, Lateiner organizes All-Female Builds, collaborative projects that serve as recruitment and training platforms, such as the 2021-2022 "Iron Maven" conversion of a 1961 Volvo PV544 with an electrified powertrain from a modern Volvo, partnered with Volvo Cars to draw attention to gender diversity in the industry.[29][30] These builds host dozens of participants per project, including the restoration of a van in 2025 involving over 50 women training in repair techniques for a nonprofit aiding homeless pregnant women, fostering direct entry into trade roles via practical experience.[31] The organization annually accommodates more than 200 women in project builds, workshops, and professional development sessions, including monthly networking events exclusive to women in trades, aimed at overcoming barriers like male-dominated shop environments and providing mentorship for career advancement.[32][33] Outcomes include skill acquisition leading to industry employment, as evidenced by participant transitions into mechanic positions, though aggregate data on long-term retention remains limited given the field's persistent underrepresentation of women.[23][25] Lateiner's recruitment extends to public outreach via television appearances and talks, where she advocates for basic maintenance education as an entry point to professional trades, partnering with entities like BASF for additional All-Female Builds to inspire career consideration among women.[34][3] These efforts prioritize empirical skill-building over symbolic gestures, aligning with industry calls for mentoring to incrementally increase female technicians from the current 1% at dealerships.[35]

Educational programs and workshops

Lateiner founded Girl Gang Garage in Phoenix, Arizona, in 2017 to provide educational opportunities for women in skilled trades, particularly automotive repair and restoration.[25] The organization conducts hands-on workshops and all-female build projects aimed at participants with varying experience levels, from novices to professionals, emphasizing practical skills such as automotive repair, welding, painting, and bodywork.[27] [36] A flagship initiative is the all-female vehicle restoration builds, beginning with the "Chevy Montage" 1956 Chevrolet pickup truck project launched in 2016, which has involved over 200 women annually in collaborative hands-on training sessions.[32] These programs offer free access to tools and mentorship, fostering skill development and industry networking through monthly professional events where participants share techniques and build confidence in trade environments.[32] In addition to build projects, Lateiner hosts nationwide workshops focused on basic car care and maintenance, such as oil changes, tire rotations, and diagnostic fundamentals, designed to empower women intimidated by male-dominated service settings.[37] [38] She has conducted monthly local sessions in Arizona since at least 2018, alongside traveling presentations and clinics that integrate soft skills like communication and teamwork with technical training.[37] [39] These efforts extend to specialized workshops on advanced trade skills, including welding and fabrication, often in partnership with industry events, aiming to address the underrepresentation of womenâ€”who comprise only about 9.6% of automotive repair employeesâ€”by providing accessible entry points into the field.[36] [27] Outcomes include increased participant confidence and career transitions, as evidenced by alumni pursuing certifications and employment in automotive roles.[24]

Reception and legacy

Achievements and industry recognition

Lateiner's automotive repair shop, 180 Degrees Automotive, founded in 2006, received the Spirit of Enterprise award from AZ Big Media in 2012 for its innovative approach to serving underserved customer demographics, including women and minorities.[40] The business expanded from a one-person driveway operation to a multi-bay facility staffed primarily by women, earning recognition as an award-winning boutique repair shop during Lateiner's over decade-long ownership.[2] In professional accolades, Lateiner was named Female Shop Owner of the Year by the Car Care Council Women's Industry Network for growing her business while promoting female participation in the automotive sector.[16] She holds Automotive Service Excellence (ASE) certification and completed Universal Technical Institute's competitive BMW technician program, establishing her technical expertise in a field where women comprise fewer than 2% of mechanics.[14][41] Lateiner's contributions to industry diversification were further acknowledged through her inclusion in Universal Technical Institute's Forty Under 40 list, honoring her as a trailblazing graduate who transitioned from pre-law studies to owning a successful repair shop.[41] Her work has been profiled in trade publications for challenging gender norms in skilled trades, including a 2023 Society of Manufacturing Engineers feature on expanding opportunities for women in manufacturing and automotive roles.[14] These recognitions underscore her role in practical workforce initiatives rather than abstract advocacy.

Criticisms regarding gender-focused initiatives

Sarah Lateiner's establishment of Girl Gang Garage in 2019, which provides women-only hands-on workshops, vehicle builds, and training to encourage female participation in automotive trades, has encountered limited direct criticism but aligns with broader debates over gender-segregated vocational programs. Where women comprise approximately 1-2% of automotive service technicians in the United States, proponents like Lateiner argue such initiatives address barriers like harassment and lack of confidence in male-dominated environments.[42] [43] However, the program's explicit exclusion of men from classes and collaborative projectsâ€”to foster an "inclusive" space for womenâ€”has raised questions about potential reverse discrimination and the reinforcement of gender divisions.[44] Critics of analogous single-sex training models contend that they rest on overstated or discredited claims of inherent gender differences in aptitude or learning styles, which lack robust empirical support. For example, analyses of single-sex education programs highlight that assumptions about boys' and girls' brains being "wired differently" for tasks like mechanical work are not substantiated by neuroscience, potentially shortchanging participants by limiting exposure to real-world mixed-gender dynamics.[45] The American Civil Liberties Union has similarly challenged such programs for relying on "bad science" that undermines equal educational opportunities, arguing they often fail to deliver measurable benefits in skill acquisition or career retention while risking legal scrutiny under anti-discrimination laws like Title IX, even if applied analogously to private initiatives.[46] Empirical evaluations of women-only vocational technology training from the late 20th century suggest short-term boosts in participation but question long-term efficacy, as such programs waned by the early 2000s amid doubts over scalability and integration into broader industry settings. Lateiner's model, while innovative in partnering with entities like Volvo for all-female builds, has not faced major lawsuits or widespread backlash, but its emphasis on gender-specific safe spaces invites scrutiny over whether it prioritizes comfort over merit-based, co-educational advancementâ€”a causal tension in trades where physical and technical demands are gender-neutral.[47] No peer-reviewed studies specifically assess Girl Gang Garage's outcomes, leaving its impact on reducing the field's gender gapâ€”currently stagnant at low single digitsâ€”open to first-principles evaluation: initiatives presuming systemic female disadvantage may overlook individual agency and market-driven recruitment.[48]
