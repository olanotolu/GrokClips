Sara Roy

Sara Roy is an American political economist and academic researcher specializing in the political economy of the Palestinian territories, particularly the Gaza Strip, and the social infrastructure of Islamist movements.[1]
Roy holds an EdD from Harvard University and serves as a senior research scholar at the university's Center for Middle Eastern Studies, where she has conducted extensive fieldwork in Gaza and the West Bank to analyze economic decline, de-development processes, and the role of groups like Hamas in providing social services.[1][2] Her key publications include The Gaza Strip: The Political Economy of De-development (Institute for Palestine Studies, 1995; expanded third edition, 2016), which empirically documents Gaza's economic stagnation amid Israeli restrictions, Palestinian governance failures, and external aid dependencies, and Hamas and Civil Society in Gaza: Engaging the Islamist Social Sector (Princeton University Press, 2011), based on direct observation of Hamas-affiliated institutions delivering education, health, and welfare amid conflict.[1][2] These works challenge prevailing narratives by emphasizing causal factors like blockade-induced isolation and internal mismanagement over ideological extremism alone, though they have drawn criticism for arguably understating Hamas's violent actions while focusing on its non-military functions, reflecting broader academic debates on interpreting empirical data from contested regions.[2][3] Roy's recent book, Unsilencing Gaza: Reflections on Resistance (Pluto Press, 2021), extends this analysis to critique U.S. and Israeli policies as perpetuating cycles of resistance and deprivation, and she was named a 2024-25 fellow at the New York Public Library's Cullman Center for Scholars and Writers for her contributions to understanding Middle Eastern political dynamics.[1][4]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Sara Roy was born and raised in West Hartford, Connecticut, where she attended Hall High School.[5] She is the daughter of Jewish parents who survived the Holocaust, an experience that profoundly shaped her family's worldview and transmitted to her a strong emphasis on ethical imperatives derived from historical trauma.[6][7][5] In her upbringing, Judaism was defined less as a formal religion and more as a framework of ethics and culture, with religious observance secondary to moral and communal values; God was acknowledged but not the focal point of household life.[8] This environment instilled in Roy a commitment to justice and human dignity, lessons her parents sought to impart through their post-Holocaust resilience and rejection of victimhood narratives that excused further injustice.[8]

Academic Training and Influences

Sara Roy completed her undergraduate studies at Harvard College before pursuing advanced degrees at Harvard University. In 1988, she earned an Ed.D. from the Harvard Graduate School of Education, with a specialization in international development.[1][9] Her doctoral training emphasized economic and developmental analysis in conflict zones, aligning with her early fieldwork in the Gaza Strip and West Bank, which commenced in 1985 and focused on local economic structures, social dynamics, U.S. aid policies, and political constraints on development. This hands-on research during her graduate years informed her approach to political economy, prioritizing empirical observation over abstract modeling.[1] Key academic influences include Herbert C. Kelman, a Harvard social psychologist renowned for interactive problem-solving in intractable conflicts, with whom Roy co-chaired seminars on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict for over two decades; some observers have described Kelman as her mentor in this domain.[10][11] Her analytical framework also draws from philosophical critiques of vulnerability and grievability in occupied territories, echoing themes in Judith Butler's work, though Roy grounds these in socioeconomic data rather than pure theory.[12] This blend of developmental economics and conflict resolution shaped her rejection of standard liberalization paradigms in favor of context-specific causal assessments of underdevelopment.

Professional Career

Initial Positions and Fieldwork

Sara Roy commenced her fieldwork in the Gaza Strip and West Bank in 1985, concentrating on the economic, social, and political development of Gaza alongside the effects of U.S. foreign assistance programs.[1] This early research laid the groundwork for her analysis of structural impediments to Palestinian economic viability under Israeli occupation.[13] During her doctoral studies at Harvard University, where she obtained an Ed.D., Roy contributed as a research assistant to the West Bank Data Base Project, undertaking fieldwork in Israel and the Gaza Strip to compile data on regional demographics, infrastructure, and socioeconomic conditions.[14] In 1986, she produced The Gaza Strip Survey as part of this project, documenting agricultural output, employment patterns, and trade dependencies in Gaza, which highlighted early signs of economic stagnation predating the Oslo Accords.[1] Roy's initial formal academic position emerged through her affiliation with Harvard's Center for Middle Eastern Studies, where she served as an associate, enabling continued on-site investigations into Gaza's political economy amid the Oslo peace process (1993â€“2000).[1] This period involved extensive immersion in local institutions, interviews with Palestinian economic actors, and observation of Islamist social services, informing her concept of "de-development"â€”a deliberate process of economic undercutting rather than mere underdevelopment. Her fieldwork during this phase, spanning multiple visits, emphasized empirical data collection on aid distribution, market distortions, and governance failures, often amid restrictions imposed by Israeli closures.[15]

Role at Harvard University

Sara Roy serves as an Associate of the Center for Middle Eastern Studies (CMES) at Harvard University, a position focused on research rather than full-time teaching or tenure-track faculty duties.[1] In this capacity, she specializes in the political economy of the Palestinian territories, particularly Gaza's de-developmentâ€”a concept she originatedâ€”along with Palestinian Islamism and the broader Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[1] Roy earned her EdD in international development from Harvard's Graduate School of Education, which underpins her scholarly approach emphasizing empirical fieldwork conducted in Gaza and the West Bank since 1985.[1] Her role at CMES includes co-chairing the Middle East Seminar and the Middle East Forum, series that host academic discussions, lectures, and events on regional topics, thereby contributing to Harvard's interdisciplinary engagement with Middle Eastern studies.[1] Through this affiliation, Roy has produced key works such as The Gaza Strip: The Political Economy of De-development (first published 1995, with editions in 2001 and 2016) and Hamas and Civil Society in Gaza (2011, paperback 2014, recipient of the 2012 British-Kuwait Friendship Society Book Prize), drawing on decades of on-the-ground data collection amid restrictions imposed by Israeli authorities.[1] These publications reflect her institutional base's support for sustained, independent analysis, though her non-tenured status has been noted in contexts of academic freedom debates.[4] Roy's Harvard association has also enabled external fellowships and public engagements, such as her selection as a 2024-25 Fellow at the New York Public Library's Dorothy and Lewis B. Cullman Center for Scholars and Writers, where she advanced work on Gaza.[4] This role underscores CMES's role in fostering specialized expertise outside mainstream departmental structures, prioritizing archival and field-based research over broader pedagogical responsibilities.[1]

Core Research Areas

Economic Analysis of Gaza

Sara Roy's economic analyses of Gaza emphasize the territory's transformation under Israeli occupation since 1967, characterizing it as a deliberate process of de-development, whereby economic growth is systematically stifled, resources depleted, and prior developmental gains reversed, distinct from mere underdevelopment or dependency.[16] [1] This framework, originated in her fieldwork beginning in 1985, posits that Gaza's economy was reoriented toward subservience to Israel, with labor migration and imports fostering integration without fostering indigenous productive capacity, particularly in agriculture and light industry, which stagnated due to land confiscations, water restrictions, and market controls.[1] [13] Roy documents how, by the late 1980s, over 40% of Gaza's workforce depended on jobs in Israel, while local manufacturing remained rudimentary and export-oriented farming declined, rendering the economy vulnerable to external shocks rather than self-sustaining.[16] In the post-Oslo period following the 1993 accords, Roy argues that Israel's closure policiesâ€”intensified after 1991 and formalized in responses to violence, such as the February 1996 restrictionsâ€”severed Gaza's economic lifelines to Israel and the West Bank, accelerating de-development into institutional isolation.[17] [13] These measures drastically reduced cross-border labor and goods movement; for instance, truck crossings at Erez fell from 700 daily to 7-8 by April 1996, causing daily economic losses estimated at $3 million in Gaza alone and contributing to unemployment spiking from 50% to 74% as tens of thousands lost access to Israeli jobs.[17] Agricultural exports, like citrus, plummetedâ€”March 1996 revenues dropped to $440,000 from $4 million in late 1995â€”while fishing yields suffered from naval enforcement zones, exacerbating poverty and food price inflation amid Palestinian Authority monopolies.[17] Roy contends this separation, rather than integration, entrenched dependency on aid and informal sectors, with the 2007 blockade further entrenching a dysfunctional market by limiting trade and investment.[18] Extending her analysis to recent decades, Roy describes Gaza's economy as having devolved from functional productivity to widespread impoverishment over 56 years, with unemployment reaching 46.4% on the eve of the 2023 conflictâ€”up from 18.9% in 2000â€”and 80% of residents reliant on international food assistance amid 65% food insecurity rates.[18] In the expanded 2016 edition of The Gaza Strip: The Political Economy of De-development, she concludes that these policies have pushed Gaza toward unviability, creating an "isolated, disposable enclave" incompatible with state-building or self-reliance, as military incursions and restrictions preclude capital accumulation or diversification.[13] Roy advocates short-term relief through eased labor accessâ€”potentially reinstating 150,000 workersâ€”but warns that without reversing de-developmental structures, Gaza's economic debility remains permanent.[17] [13]

Examination of Hamas's Social Institutions

Sara Roy's research on Hamas's social institutions focuses primarily on the Islamist social sector in Gaza, which she examines through the lens of its dawa (welfare and propagation) activities rather than its military operations. In her 2011 book Hamas and Civil Society in Gaza: Engaging the Islamist Social Sector, Roy analyzes how these institutionsâ€”encompassing health clinics, schools, orphanages, and charitable organizationsâ€”emerged from the Muslim Brotherhood tradition and expanded during the Oslo peace process era (1993â€“2000), filling voids left by inadequate Palestinian Authority services and economic constraints imposed by Israeli policies.[2][19] Drawing from extensive fieldwork, including interviews with institution staff, beneficiaries, and Hamas affiliates in Gaza and the West Bank, Roy estimates that Islamist entities comprised 10â€“40% of all social institutions in these territories by the late 1990s, providing essential services like medical care and education to underserved populations.[19][15] Roy contends that these social institutions promote a pragmatic, moderate form of Islamism, prioritizing community welfare, stability, and incremental reform over revolutionary violence or strict ideological enforcement.[2][20] She argues that dawa operations humanize Hamas for Gaza residents by addressing daily hardships, thereby building legitimacy through tangible benefits rather than coercion or militancy, and that this engagement tempers the group's more radical elements by embedding them in governance-like responsibilities.[15][20] For instance, Roy's case studies reveal that many institutions avoided overt political mobilization, focusing instead on professional service delivery infused with Islamic ethics, which she interprets as evidence of an adaptive civil society role that could support broader political accommodation.[15] This perspective challenges narratives framing Hamas's social work solely as a front for recruitment, emphasizing instead its role in fostering social cohesion amid economic de-development.[19] In the 2014 afterword to her book, Roy extends her analysis to developments following Hamas's 2006 electoral victory and 2007 Gaza takeover, noting how blockade conditions intensified reliance on these institutions for survival services, potentially reinforcing their moderating influence by compelling Hamas toward administrative pragmatism.[1] However, she acknowledges internal tensions, such as competition between social and military wings, while maintaining that the dawa sector's emphasis on order and welfare represents an underappreciated pathway for ideological evolution within the movement.[2] Roy's findings, grounded in direct observation rather than secondary reports, underscore the sector's scaleâ€”with hundreds of facilities by the early 2000sâ€”and its appeal to diverse socioeconomic groups, but she cautions that external pressures, like donor restrictions on funding, have strained operations without eradicating their community embeddedness.[19][15]

Positions on the Israeli-Palestinian Conflict

Critique of Israeli Policies

Sara Roy has argued that Israeli policies toward Gaza since the 1967 occupation constitute a deliberate process of "de-development," defined as the systematic reversal of economic and social development through control over land, water, and labor markets, reorienting Palestinian workers toward low-skilled employment in Israel rather than indigenous production.[16] This framework, introduced in her 1995 analysis, posits that such policies prevented Gaza from achieving self-sustaining growth, with agricultural and industrial sectors stifled by restrictions on resources and markets.[13] In her 2007 book Failing Peace: Gaza and the Palestinian-Israeli Conflict, Roy details how post-Oslo Accords (1993) policies exacerbated de-development by prohibiting Palestinian trade and labor mobility, transforming Gaza from a functional economy into one perpetually on the brink of collapse, as confirmed by Israeli and U.S. officials aiming to maintain economic pressure without total breakdown.[21] Drawing on over 2,000 interviews, she chronicles the occupation's fault lines, including closures starting in 1991 that canceled general exit permits, leading to unemployment rising from 18.9 percent in 2000 to 46.4 percent by 2022.[18] Roy critiques the 2007 blockadeâ€”imposed after Hamas's takeover and intensified by Israel, Egypt, the U.S., and EUâ€”as punitive rather than purely defensive, arguing it narrows Gaza's social and economic space, rendering normal life impossible and fostering dependency on aid for 80 percent of residents by 2023, with 65 percent facing food insecurity.[18] She contends this approach is counterproductive for Israeli security, as economic desperationâ€”evidenced by 42 percent overall unemployment and 60 percent among youth aged 15-29â€”fuels militancy and social breakdown, including rising suicide, divorce (40 percent rate), and informal economies like prostitution in a conservative society.[22] Rather than easing restrictions, Roy asserts Israeli strategies prioritize containment over viability, treating Gaza as a humanitarian issue divorced from political resolution, with policies like export bans preventing industrial zones that could generate jobs and undermine groups like Hamas.[22] In a 2017 analysis, she proposed alternatives such as opening borders for exports and establishing industrial zones, warning that sustained isolation perpetuates a consumption-based economy reliant on aid and tunnels, ultimately strengthening resistance rather than weakening it.[22]

Assessment of Palestinian Governance and Resistance

Sara Roy has critiqued the Palestinian Authority (PA) for systemic corruption and failure to deliver effective governance, attributing these weaknesses to the Oslo Accords' structure, which empowered Fatah-dominated institutions while fostering dependency and authoritarianism. In her analysis, the PA's moral and political bankruptcy, marked by cronyism and collaboration with Israel, alienated Palestinians and contributed to Hamas's electoral victory in January 2006, as voters rejected Fatah's arrogance and graft.[23][24] Roy assesses Hamas's governance in Gaza as initially rooted in its robust civil society networks, which provided social services, economic support, and community stability where secular institutions faltered, serving a diverse population beyond ideological loyalists and emphasizing moderate, nonviolent civic renewal over radicalism. Post-2007 blockade and intra-Palestinian rift, however, Hamas shifted toward survivalist authoritarianism, imposing heavy taxes (e.g., tripling cigarette prices to 25 NIS and doubling quarterly property taxes) and prioritizing self-preservation amid resource scarcity, while struggling to address escalating domestic disputes (up to 2,000 per month in areas like Shati camp). Tensions with the PA intensified as Ramallah withheld salaries for 45,000 Hamas civil servants, slashing payments by 30-70% to exert pressure, exacerbating Gaza's economic collapse with 42% overall unemployment (60% for youth aged 15-29) and aid dependency for 70% of its 1.9 million residents.[2][22] In Roy's view, Palestinian resistance in Gaza emerges as a rational response to "de-development"â€”a deliberate Israeli strategy of economic strangulation and isolation since the 1990s, intensified post-2005 disengagement and Hamas's riseâ€”transforming Gaza into a humanitarian crisis rather than a political entity with agency. She argues that abnormal conditions under blockade foster abnormal behaviors, including militancy as an economic livelihood rather than pure ideology, with groups like the al-Qassam Brigades expanding amid desperation (e.g., rising prostitution and youth radicalization). Roy posits that Hamas's embedded social institutions demonstrate potential for pragmatic governance and resistance sustainability, advocating engagement over isolation to harness their stabilizing role, though blockade-induced pressures have eroded ideological moderation.[25][22][26]

Controversies and Criticisms

Claims of Academic Censorship

In 2007, Sara Roy described the rejection of her book review of Matthew Levitt's Hamas: Politics, Charity and Terrorism in the Service of Jihad by Tufts University's Fletcher Forum of World Affairs as a "blatantâ€¦case of censorship."[27] The review, initially accepted but later declined after editorial exchanges citing its one-sidedness and lack of objectivity, criticized Levitt's portrayal of Hamas and recommended alternative sources for understanding the group.[27] Roy's review was subsequently published in the Middle East Policy Council Journal, undermining claims of outright suppression.[27] Critics, including analysts from the Middle East Forum, argued that Roy's equation of editorial rejection with censorship reflects a broader pattern among some Middle East scholars, where substantive critique of their work is reframed as silencing to evade accountability.[27] They contended that her review's dismissal stemmed from merit-based concerns rather than ideological suppression, noting Roy's history of portraying Hamas's social institutions positively despite the group's designated terrorist status by the U.S. government.[28] Roy has more broadly alleged a chilling effect on U.S. academics critical of Israeli policies, citing post-9/11 campaigns like Campus Watch, which monitors faculty for perceived anti-Israel bias and encourages student reporting.[29] In a 2004 London Review of Books piece, she highlighted efforts to equate such criticism with anti-Semitism or anti-Americanism, including legislative proposals like HR 3077 (passed by the House in October 2003) that could impose national security alignments on federally funded programs.[29] She recounted a Harvard-specific incident where a Jewish student, using a pseudonym, posted anti-Semitic content on a pro-Palestinian group's weblog to expose alleged biases, illustrating what she viewed as orchestrated intimidation.[29] In a 2005 London Review of Books article, Roy warned of student-led syllabus controls via groups like Students for Academic Freedom, which track "abuses" such as one-sided lectures, and referenced cases like Columbia University's response to the documentary Columbia Unbecoming, where faculty faced scrutiny for intimidating pro-Israel students.[30] While not detailing personal course rejections at Harvard, she framed these trends as eroding academic freedom, particularly in Middle East studies, amid pushes for "academic bills of rights" in states like Colorado and Georgia.[30] Opponents maintain that such monitoring promotes balance rather than censorship, countering what they see as dominant anti-Israel narratives in academia.[27]

Accusations of Bias Toward Hamas

Critics, including pro-Israel advocacy organizations and academic commentators, have accused Sara Roy of bias toward Hamas by arguing that her scholarship emphasizes the group's provision of social services in Gaza while downplaying its designation as a terrorist organization, its charter's calls for Israel's destruction, and its use of civilian institutions to support military activities.[14][31] Steven Plaut, an economist and frequent critic of anti-Israel academics, described Roy in a 2009 Think-Israel article as "arguably the leading apologist for Hamas in American academia," claiming she constructs a narrative of a moderate "New Hamas" focused on welfare rather than confronting its Islamist ideology and post-2005 attacks on Israel following the Gaza disengagement.[14] A focal point of these accusations is Roy's 2007 book review of Matthew Levitt's Hamas: Politics, Charity, and Terrorism in the Service of Jihad, which details Hamas's integration of charitable fronts with funding for attacks; the review, submitted to Tufts University's Fletcher Forum of World Affairs, was rejected unanimously by peer reviewers as "one-sided" for allegedly minimizing Hamas's terrorist dimensions.[31][32] The incident drew further scrutiny in a June 2009 Harvard Crimson opinion piece by Nina Weiner, which highlighted Roy's "well-documented" anti-Israel bias and her tendency to attribute Gaza's economic hardships solely to Israeli policies while overlooking Hamas's Iranian sponsorship, internal governance failures since seizing power in 2007, and diversion of aid toward militancy.[32] Roy's public statements have also fueled claims of sympathy; at a 2008 Fora.tv forum on "Beyond Occupation," she defended Hamas's social sector as a legitimate response to governance vacuums left by the Palestinian Authority, a segment titled "Defending Hamas" in archival footage.[33] Similarly, in a June 2017 London Review of Books article, Roy proposed that Israeli establishment of industrial zones and reopening borders would cause Hamas's Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades to "evaporate very quickly" by addressing economic despair, a view critiqued by CAMERA's Gilead Ini as ahistorical and overly optimistic, given documented Palestinian sabotage of prior projects like the Erez industrial park through attacks that killed workers and halted operations by 2004.[3] Critics from groups like CAMERA, which monitor media accuracy on Middle East issues, contend such prescriptions reflect a pattern of excusing Hamas's violence as reactive to occupation rather than rooted in ideological rejection of Israel's existence, evidenced by polls showing over 60% of Gazans opposing a two-state solution as of 2006.[3][34]

Empirical and Methodological Critiques

Critics of Sara Roy's research have highlighted empirical discrepancies in her assessments of Gazan preferences and economic causality. Roy has asserted that a majority of Gazans prioritize economic opportunities, such as open borders for exports, over irredentist claims to Jerusalem, estimating that 50-60% of Hamas supporters might relinquish such demands for normalized trade via Rafah. However, surveys by the Palestinian Center for Policy and Survey Research contradict this, showing that over 60% of Gazans rejected a Palestinian state limited to the West Bank and Gaza as a final solution in June 2016, indicating persistent support for broader territorial ambitions despite economic hardship.[35][3] Methodologically, Roy's fieldwork-based analyses, which draw heavily on interviews conducted in Gaza and the West Bank, have been faulted for relying on anonymous sources without transparent sampling protocols or verification of representativeness, risking selection bias toward narratives aligning with her de-development thesis. This approach, while enabling access in restricted environments, omits rigorous quantitative controls, such as stratified sampling across socioeconomic or political demographics, potentially amplifying unrepresentative voices.[3] In examining Hamas's trajectory, Roy's empirical claims of moderationâ€”citing post-2006 electoral shifts toward pragmatic state-building and a tacit two-state acceptanceâ€”face challenges from contemporaneous Hamas leadership statements rejecting Israel's existence and negotiations. For instance, senior figures like Mahmoud al-Zahar and Mousa Abu Marzouk publicly affirmed in 2006-2007 that peace talks were incompatible with Islamist principles, contradicting Roy's selective emphasis on internal political documents while downplaying jihadist rhetoric and charter adherence.[36][28][37] Roy's de-development framework, positing Israeli policies as the primary driver of Gaza's economic dysfunction since 1967, has been critiqued for empirical overreach in causal attribution, with reviewers arguing it selectively highlights restrictions (e.g., on agriculture or industry) while underweighting Palestinian-initiated disruptions, such as attacks that led to the closure of industrial zones like Erez and Karni after 2000. These facilities, intended to foster employment, were repeatedly targeted by militants, contributing to job losses independent of blockade intensification, yet Roy's narrative frames closures as unilateral Israeli sabotage rather than security responses.[38][39] Such critiques, often from analysts associated with pro-Israel research organizations like CAMERA and the Middle East Forum, underscore a perceived pattern of contextual omissionâ€”neglecting Gaza's pre-2005 integration into Israeli labor markets (where 40,000-60,000 Palestinians commuted daily) and post-Hamas aid diversion estimates (up to 60% of inflows militarized, per UN and World Bank reports)â€”which empirical data suggest exacerbated decline beyond occupation effects.[3][28]

Publications and Writings

Major Books

Sara Roy's seminal work, The Gaza Strip: The Political Economy of De-development, first published in 1995 by the Institute for Palestine Studies, analyzes the systematic economic stagnation in Gaza under Israeli occupation, arguing that policies from 1967 onward intentionally fostered dependency and prevented sustainable development, termed "de-development."[1] Updated editions in 2001 and an expanded third edition in 2016 incorporate post-Oslo data, documenting how closure regimes and restrictions on movement and trade exacerbated poverty rates exceeding 60% by the early 2000s, with unemployment peaking at over 50% in some periods.[13] The book draws on fieldwork and economic indicators to challenge narratives of Gaza as merely war-torn, emphasizing structural barriers over conflict alone. In Failing Peace: Gaza and the Palestinians after Oslo (Pluto Press, 2007), Roy critiques the post-1993 Oslo Accords era, contending that Israeli economic policies, including settlement expansion and resource control, undermined Palestinian autonomy and fueled social disintegration in Gaza, contributing to the 2006 Hamas electoral victory.[40] She presents data on aid dependency, where international inflows failed to offset restrictions that limited Gaza's GDP growth to under 2% annually in the early 2000s, while highlighting internal Palestinian governance failures.[41] Hamas and Civil Society in Gaza: Engaging the Islamist Social Sector (Princeton University Press, 2011) examines Hamas's non-militant institutions, based on Roy's fieldwork in Gaza and the West Bank during the Oslo period, revealing how Islamist charities provided essential services like education and healthcare to over 20% of Gaza's population by the mid-2000s, filling voids left by the Palestinian Authority. The book argues these networks enhanced Hamas's legitimacy through welfare provision rather than ideology alone, though Roy notes their ties to political aims without endorsing militancy.[42] More recently, Unsilencing Gaza: Reflections on Resistance (Pluto Press, 2021) compiles Roy's essays on Gaza's economic isolation post-2007 blockade, critiquing U.S. and Israeli policies for prioritizing security over development, with data showing Gaza's exports dropping 90% from pre-blockade levels and reliance on tunnel economies for basic goods.[25] It advocates recognizing Palestinian agency amid what Roy describes as engineered humanitarian crises, grounded in longitudinal economic analysis.[1]

Key Articles and Recent Contributions

Roy's influential article "The Political Economy of Israel's Occupation: Israel and the Palestinians" (1995) introduced the concept of de-development, positing that Israeli policies in the occupied territories systematically undermined Palestinian economic self-sufficiency through labor dependency, resource extraction, and infrastructural restrictions, rather than annexation or withdrawal. This framework, drawn from fieldwork in Gaza and the West Bank, emphasized causal mechanisms like border closures and settlement expansion that stifled indigenous growth, influencing subsequent analyses of occupation economics.[13] In "Hamas and the Transformation(s) of Revolutionary Islam in Palestine" (published in Current History, 2011), Roy examined Hamas's evolution from militant group to governing entity post-2006 elections, highlighting its pragmatic adaptations in social services and governance amid blockade constraints, while critiquing oversimplifications of it as purely terrorist. She argued, based on Gaza-based research, that Islamist institutions filled voids left by Fatah's corruption and Oslo's failures, fostering resilience through civil society networks, though not without authoritarian tendencies.[43] More recently, Roy's "The Long War on Gaza" (New York Review of Books, December 19, 2023) assessed Israel's post-1967 policies as cumulatively de-developing Gaza's economy and society, citing data on unemployment rates exceeding 50% and aid dependency, attributing dysfunction to deliberate exclusion rather than inherent Palestinian failings.[18] The piece, informed by decades of fieldwork, contended that military operations like Protective Edge (2014) exacerbated pre-existing structural collapse, with empirical evidence from GDP contraction and industrial shutdowns supporting claims of engineered underdevelopment.[18] Her 2021 open letter "A Letter to Biden on Gaza" (Washington Report on Middle East Affairs, June 11) urged U.S. policy shifts, drawing on 35 years of Gaza observation to argue that punitive isolationism perpetuated cycles of violence, advocating engagement with Islamist sectors for stability, while noting Hamas's internal reforms in welfare provision despite governance flaws.[44] In contributions like the co-authored "Gaza: A Model of Exclusion and Its Implications for Global Politics" (Markaz Review, October 2021), Roy and Ivar Ekeland framed Gaza as a paradigm of state-induced marginalization, using metrics such as 80% youth dependency on aid and caloric rationing under blockade to illustrate broader lessons for conflict economies, emphasizing empirical resistance patterns over ideological narratives.[45] These works underscore Roy's focus on verifiable socioeconomic data to challenge prevailing conflict framings.

Influence and Reception

Academic Impact

Sara Roy's academic contributions have primarily shaped discourse within Middle East studies, with a focus on the political economy of the Palestinian territories, particularly Gaza. As a senior research scholar at Harvard University's Center for Middle Eastern Studies since the 1990s, her fieldwork-based analyses have emphasized structural economic constraints imposed by Israeli policies, influencing specialized scholarship on development under occupation.[1] Her framework of "de-development"â€”defined as deliberate policies that prevent economic self-sufficiency and growthâ€”has become a foundational concept for examining Gaza's socioeconomic trajectory, referenced in studies analyzing displacement, resource control, and institutional stagnation.[46] [47] Roy's 2011 book Hamas and Civil Society in Gaza: Engaging the Islamist Social Sector, published by Princeton University Press, extended her impact by documenting the role of Islamist institutions in social welfare provision during the Oslo era, based on extensive primary research in Gaza and the West Bank. This work has informed academic debates on the non-militant dimensions of Palestinian Islamism, highlighting how such entities fostered stability and moderated political extremism amid economic collapse, and has been reviewed and cited in journals assessing civil society dynamics in conflict zones.[2] [48] Her publications, exceeding 100 in number, including updates to her de-development thesis through 2021's Unsilencing Gaza, continue to frame Gaza's challenges as outcomes of prolonged blockade and closure policies rather than isolated governance failures, though reception varies by institutional orientation toward the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[13] While Roy's empirical focus on economic indicators and institutional resilience has garnered citations in peer-reviewed outletsâ€”such as analyses of regional de-development parallelsâ€”her overall scholarly footprint remains niche, with modest aggregate citations reflecting the polarized nature of the field.[49] This influence is evident in how her data-driven critiques have prompted counter-studies on methodological assumptions, yet her emphasis on causal links between occupation policies and Palestinian economic regression persists as a reference point in progressive academic circles.[50]

Public and Policy Engagement

Sara Roy has served as a consultant to the US government, international organizations, human rights groups, and private businesses on Middle East issues, particularly those concerning Palestinian economic development and the Gaza Strip.[1] She also holds a position on the International Advisory Council of the American Near East Refugee Aid (ANERA), where she contributes to humanitarian and development strategies for the region.[1] Roy's public engagements include co-chairing the Middle East Seminar at Harvard's Weatherhead Center for International Affairs and the Center for Middle Eastern Studies (CMES), as well as the Middle East Forum at CMES, platforms that facilitate discussions among academics, policymakers, and practitioners on Palestinian governance and regional conflicts.[1] She delivers lectures across the US, Europe, the Middle East, and Australia, often addressing policy implications of Gaza's economic de-development and the need for sustainable reconstruction beyond short-term aid.[1] In policy-oriented writings, Roy has advocated for approaches emphasizing economic normalization and Palestinian self-determination over dependency-inducing aid models. In a 2014 analysis following conflict in Gaza, she argued that reconstruction requires restoring freedom of movement, trade, and labor access to Israel, rather than reliance on international donations that perpetuate aid economies and fail to address underlying restrictions.[51] A 2012 op-ed urged testing Hamas's governance through conditional engagement, citing its provision of social services as a potential basis for pragmatic diplomacy, while critiquing US policies of isolation.[52] More recently, in October 2025, Roy critiqued Western "day after" plans for Gazaâ€”such as those from the US, Israel, and Arab statesâ€”for presupposing Palestinian exclusion from decision-making and prioritizing security over economic viability, drawing on empirical data from decades of Gaza's aid-dependent stagnation.[26] Her book Unsilencing Gaza: Reflections on Resistance (2021) examines US foreign policy's role in enabling Israel's containment strategies, recommending shifts toward recognizing Gaza's Islamist social sector in development frameworks.[53] These contributions position her work as a counterpoint to prevailing aid-focused paradigms, though they have drawn scrutiny for perceived leniency toward Hamas governance.[33]
