Sarah Bloom Raskin

Sarah Bloom Raskin is an American attorney and financial regulator who has served in high-level roles overseeing banking and markets, including as a Governor of the Federal Reserve Board from 2010 to 2014, where she focused on consumer protection and financial stability, and as United States Deputy Secretary of the Treasury from 2014 to 2017, managing policy on capital markets, sanctions, and international finance.[1][2] Prior to these positions, she was Maryland's Commissioner of Financial Regulation from 2007 to 2010, enforcing state banking laws and addressing predatory lending practices.[1] In January 2022, President Joe Biden nominated her to serve as Vice Chair for Supervision of the Federal Reserve, a position responsible for supervising large banks and ensuring systemic risk management, but she withdrew the nomination in March 2022 after Senate Republicans, led by Senator Pat Toomey, criticized her for advocating the use of financial regulation to limit lending to fossil fuel companies as a means to address climate change, arguing it would politicize monetary policy and prioritize environmental goals over financial safety and soundness.[3][4][5] Raskin, who holds a bachelor's degree in economics from Amherst College and a law degree from Harvard Law School, currently teaches as the Colin W. Brown Distinguished Professor of the Practice of Law at Duke University School of Law, where her work emphasizes the intersection of law, economics, and public policy.[1][6]

Early Life and Education

Upbringing and Early Influences

Sarah Bloom Raskin was born on April 15, 1961, in Massachusetts.[7][8] She grew up in Indiana, where her family relocated after her birth.[8] Raskin completed high school, earning a diploma in 1979.[7] Public records provide limited details on her childhood or specific early influences shaping her interest in economics and law, with no documented mentors, pivotal events, or familial professions directly linked to her later career in financial regulation prior to her college years. Her father's background included residence in Massachusetts, but further specifics on family dynamics or socioeconomic factors remain undisclosed in available sources.[9]

Academic Achievements

Raskin earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in economics from Amherst College in 1983, graduating magna cum laude.[1][10] She received a Juris Doctor degree from Harvard Law School in 1986.[11] No additional academic honors, such as election to Phi Beta Kappa or distinctions at Harvard, are documented in primary institutional records.[1]

Government Service

Early Treasury Positions

Sarah Bloom Raskin held her initial positions within the U.S. Department of the Treasury during the early years of the Clinton administration, serving from 1993 to 1996 as Assistant to the Secretary and Counsel to the Secretary.[11] In this role, she advised Treasury Secretaries Lloyd Bentsen (1993â€“1994) and Robert Rubin (1995â€“1999) on legal aspects of financial policy, regulatory compliance, and departmental operations amid a period of economic expansion and early preparations for financial reforms.[11] [12] Her tenure coincided with key Treasury initiatives, including oversight of banking regulations and international financial coordination, though specific contributions attributed to Raskin in official records remain limited to her advisory functions rather than high-profile policy authorship.[11] Following her departure in 1996, she transitioned to the White House as Associate Counsel to the President, marking the end of her early Treasury service before subsequent roles in private practice and state regulation.[11]

Federal Reserve Governorship

The United States Senate unanimously confirmed Sarah Bloom Raskin as a member of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System on September 30, 2010.[13] She was sworn into office on October 4, 2010, alongside Janet Yellen.[14] Raskin's term was set to expire on January 31, 2016.[1] As a Governor, Raskin participated in Federal Open Market Committee meetings, contributing to decisions on monetary policy during the post-Great Recession recovery, including quantitative easing programs and forward guidance to maintain low interest rates. Her responsibilities included oversight of banking supervision, consumer protection, and financial stability initiatives under the Dodd-Frank Act.[1] Raskin delivered several public speeches highlighting distributional effects of Federal Reserve policies. In a March 1, 2012, address to the Y's Men and Y's Women of Westport, she discussed how prolonged accommodative monetary policy, while supporting employment and growth, imposed costs on savers through low yields eroding purchasing power and retirement security.[15] She argued that these trade-offs required careful calibration to avoid undue hardship on households reliant on fixed income.[15] In April 2013, at the Hyman P. Minsky Conference, Raskin examined inequality trends in the recent business cycle, noting widening income disparities despite aggregate recovery and advocating for monetary policy frameworks that account for uneven benefits across socioeconomic groups.[16] She emphasized empirical evidence from labor market data showing persistent gaps in wage growth and employment for lower-income workers.[16] Raskin resigned from the Board effective March 13, 2014, to accept nomination as Deputy Secretary of the Treasury.[1] Her departure occurred amid ongoing implementation of financial regulatory reforms, with no public dissents recorded from FOMC votes during her tenure.[1]

Deputy Secretary of the Treasury

Sarah Bloom Raskin was nominated by President Barack Obama to serve as Deputy Secretary of the Treasury on January 10, 2014, and confirmed by the U.S. Senate on March 12, 2014, by a vote of 93-3, making her the first woman to hold the position.[2] She assumed office on March 13, 2014, succeeding Neal S. Wolin, and served until January 20, 2017, at the conclusion of the Obama administration.[8] As the second-ranking official in the department under Secretary Jacob Lew, Raskin's responsibilities included overseeing the Treasury's domestic finance operations, managing interagency coordination, and advancing policies on economic growth, financial stability, and consumer protection.[17] During her tenure, Raskin focused on implementing post-financial crisis reforms, including aspects of the Dodd-Frank Act, and promoting innovative financial tools to enhance economic opportunities for Americans.[6] One key initiative she highlighted was the myRA program, a low-risk, low-cost starter retirement savings account launched by the Treasury in December 2014 to encourage saving among workers without access to employer-sponsored plans; Raskin advocated for its accessibility in public commentary, emphasizing its role in building financial security.[18] She also addressed audit regulation and investor protection, as evidenced by her December 14, 2016, remarks praising the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board's (PCAOB) efforts to improve audit quality since its establishment under Sarbanes-Oxley.[19] Raskin's leadership contributed to the Treasury's broader agenda of fostering business confidence and economic prosperity amid recovery from the 2008 crisis, though specific quantifiable achievements tied directly to her oversight remain documented primarily through departmental operations rather than standalone metrics.[2] No major controversies arose during her service in this capacity, with her departure aligning with the administration changeover.[8]

Academic and Think Tank Roles

Following her tenure as Deputy Secretary of the Treasury from 2014 to 2017, Sarah Bloom Raskin transitioned to academic roles. In July 2020, she joined Duke University School of Law as a visiting professor of the practice of law and as a distinguished fellow at the school's Global Financial Markets Center (GFMC).[25] Her work there initially focused on financial regulation, market resilience, and mentoring students through guest lectures and research projects.[6] In October 2021, Raskin was appointed the Colin W. Brown Distinguished Professor of the Practice of Law at Duke Law, a position that recognizes her expertise in policy and practice.[26] She also became faculty director of the GFMC, overseeing initiatives on global financial markets, and serves as a senior fellow at the Duke Center on Risk, where her research addresses systemic risks from financial instruments, cyber threats, and climate-related events.[6][27] In parallel, Raskin holds a senior fellow position at the Roosevelt Institute, a think tank advocating progressive economic policies.[28] There, she contributes to analyses on climate transition financing, monetary policy, financial stability, and regulatory frameworks for economic resilience, including membership in the institute's Regenerative Crisis Response Committee.[28] Her affiliations emphasize integrating financial oversight with broader economic and environmental challenges.[27]

Federal Reserve Vice Chair Nomination

Nomination and Confirmation Hearings

President Biden nominated Sarah Bloom Raskin on January 14, 2022, to serve as Vice Chair for Supervision on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors, a role overseeing bank regulation and supervision.[3] The nomination drew immediate scrutiny from Senate Republicans, who cited her prior writings and speeches advocating for financial regulators to incorporate climate change considerations into oversight, including a 2017 Duke University article where she called for "reimagining" capital markets to combat an "unlivably hot planet."[38] The Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs held Raskin's confirmation hearing on February 3, 2022, alongside nominees Lisa D. Cook and Philip N. Jefferson for Federal Reserve governor positions.[39] During her testimony, Raskin affirmed that addressing inflation would be the Federal Reserve's top priority, aligning with the nominees' prepared statements emphasizing monetary policy focus amid rising prices.[40] Republican senators, including Patrick Toomey and Tim Scott, interrogated her on climate-related views, questioning whether she would use supervisory tools to restrict lending to fossil fuel industries based on environmental policy rather than financial risk assessments.[41] Raskin responded that she would not direct the denial of credit to any sector solely due to climate considerations but maintained that climate risks, such as physical damages or transition effects, constitute legitimate financial stability threats warranting supervisory attention.[36] Partisan tensions escalated post-hearing, with Republicans releasing a staff report on February 2, 2022, highlighting Raskin's history of promoting climate-focused financial regulation, including her Treasury Department-era emphasis on integrating environmental factors into banking oversight. On February 15, 2022, all Republican members boycotted a committee vote on the Fed nominees, stalling advancement and specifically targeting Raskin's nomination as a means to protest perceived politicization of the central bank's apolitical mandate.[42] Democrats defended Raskin, arguing her expertise in financial risks from non-traditional sources, including climate, aligned with prudential regulation without overstepping into industrial policy.[43] The boycott delayed full committee consideration, underscoring divisions over the Fed's role in addressing environmental issues versus core economic stabilization.[44]

Climate Policy Focus and Opposition

During her January 2022 nomination for Federal Reserve Vice Chair for Supervision, Sarah Bloom Raskin emphasized the integration of climate-related financial risks into banking regulation and oversight, arguing that regulators must proactively address the economic costs of climate change through supervisory tools. In a September 2021 Project Syndicate article, she stated that financial regulators "will need to leave their comfort zone and act early before the problem worsens," advocating for measures to mitigate risks from carbon-intensive activities by adjusting capital requirements and disclosure standards.[45][46] Raskin's positions included calls to redirect private capital away from fossil fuel-dependent investments toward sustainable alternatives, as evidenced in her prior Treasury Department work and public writings where she described fossil fuel financing as increasingly untenable due to transition risks. A Senate Banking Committee compilation of her statements highlighted remarks such as urging a shift "toward sustainable investments that do not depend on carbon and fossil fuels," framing climate policy as integral to financial stability oversight.[47] During February 2022 confirmation hearings, she defended the Fed's role in examining climate risks within its existing mandate of promoting financial stability, while rejecting accusations of overreach but affirming the need for scenario analysis and stress testing related to physical and transition risks from climate change.[48] Opposition from Senate Republicans focused on Raskin's advocacy as an attempt to weaponize the Fed's supervisory authority for environmental policymaking, potentially discriminating against energy sector lending and exceeding the central bank's statutory focus on monetary policy, employment, and price stability. Senator Joe Manchin cited her views that fossil fuels "should have no place in the future" of energy portfolios as evidence of bias against traditional energy financing, announcing his opposition on March 14, 2022, which effectively doomed confirmation.[49] Senator Bill Cassidy similarly criticized her nomination on February 3, 2022, arguing it promoted an "anti-energy" agenda that could stifle domestic production amid global energy needs.[50] Critics, including Senator Tim Scott, contended that such climate-focused supervision blurred the line between prudential regulation and industrial policy, risking politicization of an independent institution.[47] This bipartisan resistance, joined by Manchin, led to her withdrawal on March 15, 2022.[51]

Withdrawal and Implications

Sarah Bloom Raskin announced her withdrawal from consideration for the Federal Reserve Vice Chair for Supervision position on March 15, 2022, citing a prolonged stalemate in the Senate Banking Committee that prevented advancement of her nomination and those of other nominees.[52] The decision followed unanimous Republican opposition on the committee, who boycotted votes to protest her prior advocacy for using banking supervision to discourage financing of fossil fuel projects, which critics argued would politicize the Fed's mandate beyond financial stability.[5] Key Democratic support eroded when Senator Joe Manchin stated he could not back her due to concerns that her approach risked subordinating monetary policy to climate goals, effectively lacking the votes for confirmation.[53] President Biden responded by praising Raskin's public service history while noting her withdrawal aimed to avoid further delay for the Fed's broader nomination slate, including Chair Jerome Powell's renomination.[54] Senate Republicans, including Ranking Member Pat Toomey, welcomed the move as safeguarding the Fed from regulatory overreach into energy policy, arguing it preserved the central bank's independence from ideological pressures.[5] Democrats and environmental advocates, conversely, attributed the outcome to fossil fuel industry lobbying, viewing it as a setback for integrating climate risk assessments into supervisory practices.[51] The withdrawal facilitated swift committee advancement of the remaining nominees on March 17, 2022, enabling Powell's confirmation and filling other board vacancies amid ongoing inflation pressures.[55] It delayed potential revisions to bank capital and supervision rules under the Vice Chair role, as Raskin's successor, Michael Barr, assumed the post in July 2022 with a narrower focus on financial stability rather than explicit climate linkages.[56] Broader implications underscored Senate checks on executive nominees perceived as advancing partisan agendas, reinforcing expectations that Fed supervision prioritize apolitical risk management over environmental policy enforcement, and signaling limits to the administration's climate-finance integration amid energy sector dependencies.[57] This episode also highlighted intra-Democratic tensions, with moderates like Manchin prioritizing economic pragmatism over progressive priorities.[58]

Policy Views and Criticisms

Perspectives on Financial Regulation

Sarah Bloom Raskin has emphasized financial regulations that promote systemic stability and long-term societal benefits, critiquing models prioritizing short-term profits at the expense of risk management. In a 2012 address to the Graduate School of Banking at Colorado, she distinguished "high-road" bankingâ€”characterized by strong customer relationships, prudent underwriting, and community focusâ€”from "low-road" practices that fueled the subprime mortgage crisis through opaque risks and excessive leverage.[59] She endorsed core Dodd-Frank Act provisions, such as the Volcker Rule prohibiting proprietary trading by insured banks to prevent conflicts of interest, but dissented from its 2012 implementation for lacking robust guardrails against evasion and imposing high compliance costs without clear prohibitions on manipulative metrics-based rules.[59] During her tenure as a Federal Reserve Governor from 2010 to 2014, Raskin highlighted post-2008 banking reforms' successes in bolstering resilience, noting in a May 2013 speech that U.S. banks' regulatory capital had surpassed pre-crisis levels across large, medium, and community institutions, aiding a gradual economic recovery with GDP growth of 1.75% year-over-year in Q1 2013.[60] She stressed ongoing supervisory needs to address lingering vulnerabilities, including tight mortgage credit that hindered household access despite rising home sales and prices since late 2011, and advocated for regulations fostering financial inclusion to mitigate inequality's role in amplifying crisis severity.[60][61] Raskin's perspectives prominently include incorporating climate-related financial risks into regulatory frameworks, arguing that inaction equates to heightened systemic exposure. In a September 2021 Project Syndicate commentary, she contended that escalating extreme weather eventsâ€”such as floods and heatwaves displacing communities and disrupting agricultureâ€”impose verifiable economic costs, urging U.S. regulators to proactively redirect private capital from high-emission activities toward low-carbon alternatives to avert deeper instability.[45][47] During her 2022 Federal Reserve Vice Chair nomination hearings, she affirmed that such supervision aligns with the Fed's statutory focus on stability, not direct emissions cuts, yet critics, including Senate Republicans, argued her approachâ€”evident in prior calls to scrutinize fossil fuel lendingâ€”risks subordinating monetary policy to environmental goals, potentially exceeding legal mandates and echoing biased institutional pushes for de-risking carbon-intensive sectors.[48][38]

Climate Risk and Finance Debates

Sarah Bloom Raskin has argued that climate change constitutes a systemic financial risk requiring integration into regulatory supervision to safeguard economic stability. She identifies both physical risks, such as property damage from extreme weather events, and transition risks, including asset devaluation from shifts to low-carbon policies, as threats that could lead to losses estimated at $1 trillion to $4 trillion in the energy sector alone if carbon assets lose value abruptly.[62] In testimony before the Senate Democrats' Special Committee on the Climate Crisis on March 17, 2020, Raskin recommended that regulators issue supervisory guidance mandating banks, insurers, and investment firms to manage climate risks through improved governance, data collection, scenario analysis, and disclosures aligned with the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures framework, which covers organizations with $11 trillion in market capitalization. She advocated for developing climate-specific stress testing models, drawing on precedents like the Bank of England's 2019 framework and the Dutch Central Bank's tests since 2017, to assess vulnerabilities without awaiting new legislation.[62] Raskin has proposed targeted regulatory measures to accelerate a shift from carbon-dependent activities, including higher capital requirements for fossil fuel exposures, enhanced supervision, and potential concentration limits on high-emission assets. In a June 2020 report for the environmental advocacy group Ceres, she stated that regulators should "do all they can... to bring about the adoption of practices and policies that will allocate capital and align portfolios toward sustainable investments that do not depend on carbon and fossil fuels," while recommending climate stress tests and elevated capital charges for energy assets. Similarly, at a June 2021 conference, she called for regulators, auditors, and standard-setters to "incentiviz a rapid, orderly, and just transition away from high-emission assets."[47] These positions fueled controversy during Raskin's January 2022 nomination as Federal Reserve Vice Chair for Supervision, where Senate Republicans, citing her prior statements, accused her of seeking to weaponize climate risk assessments to curtail lending to fossil fuel producers, effectively enacting environmental policy via banking rules. A May 2020 New York Times op-ed by Raskin exemplified this critique, as she faulted the Fed for pandemic-era support of fossil fuels, asserting it ignored "clear warning signs about the economic repercussions of the impending climate crisis" and propped up "a terrible investment" amid short-term risks.[63][47] Critics, including industry groups, argued such tools risked politicizing neutral supervision, denying credit to essential energy sectors based on uncertain long-term projections rather than proven prudential threats, and bypassing Congress.[51] In February 2022 confirmation hearings, Raskin maintained that climate risk mitigation aligns with the Fed's financial stability mandate, emphasizing supervisoryâ€”not monetaryâ€”tools and denying intent to target specific industries punitively, while affirming priority on inflation control. Opposition from fossil fuel stakeholders and Republican senators, who viewed her Ceres affiliations and statements as indicative of bias toward rapid decarbonization over risk-neutral analysis, led to her March 15, 2022, withdrawal.[48][51] The debates highlight tensions over climate risk's materiality: empirical instances like insured losses from hurricanes (e.g., $100 billion from 2017 events) underscore acute impacts, yet long-term modeling relies on assumptions about emissions trajectories and policy responses, inviting skepticism on regulatory intervention's net benefits versus costs to energy reliability and growth.[62] Advocacy sources like Ceres, focused on sustainability, may amplify alarmist framings, while regulatory caution reflects challenges in quantifying causal chains from climate variables to balance-sheet solvency.[47]

Evaluations of Regulatory Approach

Raskin's regulatory philosophy emphasizes proactive supervision to mitigate systemic risks, including those posed by climate change, which she has described as necessitating early regulatory action to reallocate capital away from high-emission activities. In a September 10, 2021, Project Syndicate commentary, she advocated for financial regulators to "change the climate of financial regulation" by addressing both physical climate impacts and transition risks from decarbonization policies, arguing that inaction would impose mounting economic costs.[45] This stance aligns with her prior roles, where she supported aggressive enforcement; on March 22, 2013, as a Federal Reserve Governor, she endorsed a "broken windows" approach to oversight, urging regulators to target minor infractions to prevent larger failures and strengthen consumer protections.[64] Critics have evaluated this approach as an overextension of regulatory authority, potentially subordinating financial stability to ideological priorities. During her February 3, 2022, Senate confirmation hearing for Federal Reserve Vice Chair for Supervision, Republican senators, including Patrick Toomey, challenged her views as risking the politicization of bank supervision by denying credit to fossil fuel projects under the guise of risk management, a practice they argued deviates from the Fed's core mandates of monetary policy and prudential oversight.[36][40] The U.S. Chamber of Commerce, in a January 27, 2022, letter to the Senate Banking Committee, warned that Raskin's emphasis on climate criteria for capital allocation could distort market decisions, favoring certain sectors while harming energy production and broader economic vitality.[65] Similarly, 24 state financial officers, led by Texas Comptroller Glenn Hegar, opposed her nomination on January 31, 2022, citing concerns that her framework would weaponize regulation against traditional energy financing without empirical evidence tying it directly to bank solvency.[66] Proponents, such as the advocacy organization Better Markets in a January 31, 2022, fact-check memo, defended Raskin's positions as essential for anticipating systemic threats akin to those ignored pre-2008 financial crisis, though such groups consistently push for heightened regulatory intervention across financial sectors.[67] Evaluations from conservative think tanks, like the Texas Public Policy Foundation's February 16, 2022, analysis, further critiqued her approach for conflating verifiable physical risks (e.g., asset devaluation from weather events) with speculative policy-driven transition risks, potentially enabling regulators to impose de facto industrial policy without legislative backing.[68] The intensity of these evaluations contributed to the March 15, 2022, withdrawal of her nomination, after Senator Joe Manchin announced opposition, citing insufficient assurances that climate considerations would not override the Fed's independence.[51][69] This outcome underscored broader debates on whether supervisory tools should incorporate non-traditional risks, with detractors arguing Raskin's framework risks regulatory capture by environmental advocacy rather than grounding decisions in quantifiable financial metrics. Subsequent regulatory shifts, such as the October 20, 2025, rescission of interagency climate risk guidance by the Fed, FDIC, and OCC, reflect a retreat from mandatory climate integrations in supervision, implicitly validating concerns over prescriptive mandates.[70]
