Hromada

A hromada (Ukrainian: грома́да, romanized: hromáda, lit. 'community') constitutes the primary unit of local self-government and administrative division in Ukraine, encompassing one or more settlements whose inhabitants collectively form a territorial community responsible for local affairs.[1][2] Emerging from decentralization reforms initiated in 2014 and accelerated from 2015, hromadas resulted from the amalgamation of over 12,000 smaller units into roughly 1,470 viable entities, granting them expanded fiscal autonomy, service delivery responsibilities, and decision-making powers to foster efficient governance and community resilience.[3][4][5] Governed by directly elected mayors and councils that appoint executive bodies, hromadas manage essential functions such as infrastructure, education, healthcare, and budgeting, which empirical studies link to heightened local trust and social capital compared to pre-reform structures.[6][7] These reforms have proven instrumental in wartime adaptability, enabling hromadas to coordinate crisis responses and civic initiatives amid invasion, though martial law since 2022 has deferred elections, imposed military administrations in frontline areas, and strained decentralized operations with centralized overrides.[8][9][10]

Overview and Legal Framework

Definition and Characteristics

A hromada (Ukrainian: громада), translating to "community," denotes an amalgamated territorial community (ATC) serving as the foundational unit of local self-government in Ukraine's administrative structure. These communities arise from the consolidation of contiguous villages, rural settlements, and cities into singular entities designed for fiscal and administrative viability, enabling them to handle essential services such as infrastructure maintenance, education, and primary healthcare with enhanced autonomy and resource allocation.[11][2] Distinguishing features of hromadas include their requirement for territorial adjacency to maintain operational coherence, direct election of representative councils (radys) by community residents to deliberate on local policies, and leadership by an elected head—typically a mayor in urban-oriented hromadas or starostas representing rural subunits—contrasting sharply with the pre-reform landscape of fragmented, under-resourced micro-units like individual village councils that often lacked economies of scale.[2][3] The amalgamation process culminated in 2020 with the establishment of 1,469 hromadas—comprising 409 urban, 435 settlement, and 625 rural types—that fully subsumed Ukraine's territory, supplanting the prior array of thousands of diminutive primary administrative divisions and thereby fostering more efficient, self-reliant local governance nationwide.[2][12]

Legal Foundation and Reforms

The legal foundation for hromadas derives from Article 140 of the Constitution of Ukraine, which establishes local self-government as the right of territorial communities—comprising residents of villages, settlements, or cities—to independently resolve issues of local importance within their competence and under their own responsibility.[13] This provision, adopted in 1996, guarantees the exercise of self-governance through councils and executive bodies at the local level, forming the basis for subsequent statutory developments.[14] Key legislative mechanisms emerged from the 2014 decentralization reforms, including amendments to the Budget Code enacted on December 28, 2014, which devolved fiscal resources by granting voluntarily amalgamated communities access to a larger share of taxes, such as personal income tax, to support local budgets.[7] These changes shifted revenue distribution from central and raion authorities to the community level, increasing local budget expenditures from approximately 20% of consolidated expenditures in 2014 to over 40% by 2019.[15] Complementing this, the Law on Voluntary Amalgamation of Territorial Communities, adopted on February 5, 2015, provided the statutory framework for merging primary communities into hromadas capable of performing devolved functions, designating them as legal entities of public law with independent budgets derived from local taxes like real estate property tax and state interbudgetary transfers.[16] These reforms emphasized fiscal autonomy and the principle of subsidiarity—ensuring decisions are taken at the lowest effective level—aligning with European standards while reducing central oversight over local administration.[17] Hromadas thus gained authority to manage own resources without prior raion approval for routine expenditures, though subject to national oversight for compliance with constitutional norms.[18]

Historical Development

Soviet and Post-Independence Legacy

During the Soviet era, Ukraine's administrative divisions were structured around a hierarchical system of oblasts (provinces), raions (districts), and lower-level soviets, including village soviets (selysovety) that managed rural localities but operated under strict central oversight from republican and union authorities. Power was concentrated in higher echelons, with local bodies executing directives from the Communist Party and state apparatus rather than exercising independent authority, resulting in limited local initiative and resource allocation controlled from Kyiv or Moscow. This centralization prioritized ideological conformity and industrial planning over efficient local governance.[19] Following independence in 1991, Ukraine inherited this fragmented structure with little initial reform, retaining over 12,000 territorial communities, predominantly small village and settlement councils averaging fewer than 2,000 residents each, which lacked the scale to sustain viable administrative functions. These units depended heavily on central budget transfers for funding, as local tax bases were underdeveloped and fiscal authority remained vested in Kyiv, leading to chronic under-resourcing and inefficiencies in service provision. Dependency on national directives stifled local accountability, contributing to widespread corruption where officials at raion and village levels engaged in rent-seeking amid opaque allocation of limited resources.[20][21] The proliferation of minuscule administrative entities precluded economies of scale, empirically evident in suboptimal metrics for local services: for instance, many rural councils struggled to maintain schools and health posts due to insufficient personnel and budgets, with per capita spending on infrastructure often below national averages and delivery rates for utilities like water and roads lagging in fragmented areas. This inefficiency stemmed causally from the Soviet-inherited model's emphasis on territorial proliferation—exacerbated post-1991 by politicized subdivisions in western regions—over consolidated capacity, rendering small units incapable of attracting investment or responding to demographic declines in rural populations.[21][22]

Initiation of Decentralization (2014–2015)

The Revolution of Dignity, culminating in February 2014, exposed the perils of excessive centralization under the Yanukovych administration, prompting the interim government to prioritize devolution of authority to local levels as a safeguard against authoritarian overreach.[23] This shift aligned with broader post-revolutionary commitments to European integration, including the pursuit of the EU-Ukraine Association Agreement, which emphasized governance reforms for subsidiarity and local empowerment.[3] On 1 April 2014, the Cabinet of Ministers approved the "Concept of the Reform of Local Self-Government and Territorial Organisation of Power in Ukraine," which articulated a framework for restructuring subnational governance to bring decision-making closer to citizens and reduce Kyiv's dominance in resource allocation and service delivery.[24][25] The 2014 Concept enshrined the principle of subsidiarity, positing that tasks should be handled at the lowest effective level of government capable of efficient execution, thereby establishing a causal mechanism linking local autonomy to improved responsiveness and investment in public goods.[26] It targeted the formation of capable territorial communities (hromadas) through amalgamation of existing villages, settlements, and cities, aiming to create entities with sufficient scale for fiscal viability while preserving community identity.[27] This approach countered the fragmented, under-resourced local structures inherited from Soviet-era central planning, which had perpetuated dependency on central subsidies and stifled initiative.[22] Implementation began with enabling legislation: on 5 February 2015, the Verkhovna Rada passed the Law on Voluntary Amalgamation of Territorial Communities, allowing neighboring local councils to merge voluntarily into unified hromadas with enhanced administrative capacities.[7] By September 2015, 793 communities had amalgamated to form 159 new hromadas, primarily in rural and small-town areas, serving as pilots for the reform's viability.[21] These initial hromadas gained direct access to state infrastructure subventions and pilots for expanded fiscal tools, marking the first structured effort to operationalize the Concept's vision.[28] Early outcomes demonstrated tangible fiscal empowerment: local budgets across reforming areas surged by 37.7% (UAH 44.6 billion) in the first half of 2015 alone, driven by provisional retention of higher shares of personal income tax (up to 60% versus the prior 15%) and local taxes like excise duties.[21] This revenue boost in pilot hromadas enabled initial investments in roads, schools, and utilities, establishing empirical evidence for the reform's premise that devolved resources catalyze local development without proportional central oversight.[3] Such gains, while modest in scale, validated the causal pathway from amalgamation-induced scale to self-sustaining governance, setting precedents for wider rollout despite administrative hurdles like uneven participation rates.[18]

Consolidation and Completion (2016–2020)

Between 2016 and 2019, the Ukrainian government expanded hromada formation through voluntary amalgamations, supported by fiscal incentives including state infrastructure subventions and grants that rewarded merging smaller units into larger territorial communities capable of managing broader responsibilities.[29][30] This phase added over 900 hromadas to the initial pilots from 2015, driven by legal frameworks under the 2014-2015 decentralization laws that tied amalgamation to enhanced local budgeting autonomy and direct transfers from the state budget.[11] By mid-2019, approximately 838 amalgamated hromadas had been established, covering a significant portion of the population but leaving gaps in rural areas where smaller villages resisted merger due to concerns over losing local identity.[31] To achieve nationwide coverage, the process shifted toward mandatory elements in 2020, culminating in the formation of 1,469 hromadas by year's end, which amalgamated the previous 10,977 primary local councils into a unified basic tier of self-government.[11] On July 17, 2020, the Verkhovna Rada passed legislation reforming the subnational structure by reducing raions from 490 to 136 larger districts, devolving many raion-level tasks—such as secondary education, healthcare, and social services—to hromadas and eliminating intermediate administrative layers.[10] This consolidation ensured 100% territorial coverage, enabling hromadas to plan and execute development independently while receiving guaranteed minimum state funding.[32] The reforms yielded measurable gains in local capacity, with amalgamated hromadas increasing investments in infrastructure projects like roads and utilities, leveraging economies of scale from larger populations and budgets that grew through retained taxes and grants.[33] Assessments noted improved service delivery and local economic activity, as hromadas directed resources toward priority needs, though challenges persisted in ensuring equitable amalgamation in less viable rural areas.[3]

Adaptations During the Russian Invasion (2022–Present)

Following Russia's full-scale invasion on February 24, 2022, Ukraine declared martial law, which suspended local elections and extended the terms of hromada councils and heads indefinitely to maintain governance continuity amid wartime conditions.[34][35] In frontline and de-occupied regions, particularly in the east and south, military administrations were established in approximately 13-14% of hromadas, such as those in Kherson (49 hromadas), Zaporizhzhia (37), and Donetsk (36) oblasts, where appointed military leaders assumed oversight or replacement of civilian structures to coordinate defense and security operations.[9][34] These measures compromised standard democratic processes but prioritized operational stability in high-threat areas.[9] Hromadas not under military control adapted by exercising expanded emergency powers under martial law, enabling rapid mobilization for civilian defense, humanitarian aid distribution, and infrastructure repairs, drawing on pre-invasion decentralization reforms that had enhanced local competencies and resource access.[36] Studies attribute this responsiveness to prior investments in local governance capacity, which correlated with better preparedness metrics like economic indicators and administrative readiness, allowing hromadas to organize volunteer networks and sustain essential services despite disruptions.[37] For instance, local authorities coordinated evacuations, shelter provisions, and basic welfare in the invasion's early phases, with urban-rural divides influencing but not overriding overall institutional endurance.[38] By 2024, partial recentralization emerged as the central government redirected portions of local budgets toward national war priorities, including defense procurement and mobilization, straining hromada fiscal autonomy while necessitating adaptive budgeting in over 1,400 communities.[9][39] Nonetheless, hromadas retained significant roles in resilience efforts, managing internally displaced persons (IDP) integration, community-based aid, and frontline support programs, with analyses of local budgets highlighting their contributions to shock absorption despite revenue shortfalls from population displacement and economic contraction.[39] Martial law extensions through May 2025 further entrenched these adaptations, underscoring decentralization's foundational role in enabling localized crisis management amid centralized wartime imperatives.[40][36]

Governance and Organization

Formation Mechanisms

Prior to the completion of the administrative reform in 2020, hromadas were primarily formed through voluntary amalgamation processes initiated by local councils of villages, rural settlements, and small towns. Under the 2015 Law "On Voluntary Amalgamation of Territorial Communities," adjacent primary-level administrative units merged to create larger, capable territorial entities, requiring affirmative decisions by at least two-thirds of the respective councils and alignment with government-approved perspective plans for regional development.[41] These mergers emphasized contiguous territories to ensure administrative efficiency and service delivery, with capability assessed via factors such as projected population size, economic viability, and infrastructure access.[25] A key criterion was a minimum recommended population of 5,000 inhabitants for rural or rural-urban hromadas, rising to 10,000 for settlement-based ones, though exceptions applied in mountainous or sparsely populated areas to avoid service gaps.[42] Amalgamations received final approval from regional state administrations or the Cabinet of Ministers, granting legal status and access to enhanced fiscal incentives like infrastructure grants.[43] In December 2019, amendments shifted toward capacity-based formation, prioritizing hromada viability over pure voluntarism; regional administrations were empowered to enforce mergers per perspective plans if voluntary efforts lagged.[43] This culminated in April 2020 when the Cabinet of Ministers approved a nationwide plan on May 27, 2020, establishing 1,469 hromadas to cover all territory, including non-voluntary assignments of unamalgamated units to nearby capable ones based on population thresholds (e.g., at least 5,000 for most rural types) and service sustainability.[43] Cities of oblast significance, previously standalone, were integrated as hromada centers or merged into larger units, dissolving their separate status to streamline the three-tier system (hromada, raion, oblast).[17] Post-formation, hromada boundaries are enshrined in law and fixed absent exceptional circumstances, such as parliamentary-approved mergers of under-capacity units.[43] To maintain representation of subsumed localities, hromadas designate starosta districts—former village or settlement centers—where starostas are elected to advocate rural interests in council deliberations, with one vote per district regardless of size.[25]

Representative and Executive Bodies

The representative body of a territorial community, or hromada, is the local council (rada hromady), composed of deputies elected by residents through proportional or mixed electoral systems during local elections, such as those held in 2015 for newly formed hromadas and unified elections in October 2020.[2][44] The council exercises legislative authority by adopting policies, approving annual budgets, and making binding decisions on community matters, ensuring direct democratic input from the population as a check against centralized control.[2][44] Executive functions are led by the head of the hromada (golova hromady), directly elected by a majority of community voters, who serves a five-year term and presides over the executive committee formed to implement council resolutions.[44][2] The head organizes council sessions, chairs executive committee meetings with a single vote equivalent to other members, and represents the hromada in external relations, fostering accountability through personal election rather than appointment.[44] In amalgamated hromadas, which combine multiple former villages or settlements, starostas are elected by residents of designated starosta districts to represent peripheral areas within the unified structure; they participate in council plenaries and executive meetings to voice district interests but hold no hierarchical authority over the head, operating instead through collaborative service relations under the Law on Local Self-Government (No. 280/97-VR).[44] The council may also establish standing committees for oversight of executive performance, budget execution, and policy implementation, enhancing internal checks.[2] Empirical analysis of hromada-level data from the 2020 local elections reveals that higher voter turnout positively correlates with greater expenditures on public goods, such as infrastructure and services, indicating that robust electoral participation strengthens local governance outcomes and counters inefficiencies from top-down administration.[45] This pattern, drawn from comprehensive repositories tracking over 1,400 hromadas, underscores the reform's emphasis on direct elections to align resource allocation with community priorities.[45]

Administrative Subdivisions

Within a hromada, administrative subdivisions are limited and primarily serve coordination rather than autonomous governance. The primary mechanism is the optional establishment of starostynskyi okrugs (starosta districts), which group multiple rural settlements for streamlined local administration. These districts are formed by resolution of the hromada council, typically encompassing former village or settlement councils from the pre-reform era, and are headed by a starosta elected by residents of the included settlements.[46] The starosta's role is advisory and representational, facilitating communication between remote rural areas and the hromada's central executive bodies, such as handling resident inquiries, monitoring local services, and participating in council sessions without independent decision-making authority.[47] Pre-2014 administrative entities like village councils are integrated into these districts without retaining formal autonomy, functioning instead as consultative units to preserve local input while centralizing powers at the hromada level for efficiency. This structure avoids fragmented sub-level governance, ensuring unified policy implementation across urban-rural mixes. No legal framework grants sub-hromada units separate budgets, executive powers, or electoral independence, aligning with the reform's emphasis on capable, consolidated communities capable of managing diverse territories.[46] Approximately 1,100 of Ukraine's 1,469 hromadas are rural or settlement types, predominantly relying on starosta districts to address dispersed populations and adapt pre-existing rural networks to post-amalgamation needs. Urban-centered hromadas rarely employ them, focusing instead on centralized municipal structures, though hybrid hromadas may delineate districts for outlying villages to enhance service delivery without diluting core competencies.[48] This subdivision approach supports operational efficiency, such as in coordinating infrastructure maintenance or emergency response in expansive rural zones, but remains subordinate to hromada-wide oversight.[47]

Powers and Functions

Autonomous Competencies

Hromadas possess inherent self-governing powers to address local needs, including the management of communal property such as land, buildings, and enterprises owned by the community, without requiring central government oversight.[49] These powers extend to planning and implementing local infrastructure projects, encompassing roads, utilities, water supply systems, and public spaces, enabling communities to prioritize and fund improvements based on resident demands.[1] In education, hromadas autonomously operate and maintain primary and secondary schools, including curriculum adaptation to local contexts, teacher employment, and facility upkeep, as empowered under amendments to local self-government legislation during the 2014–2020 decentralization phase.[10] Primary healthcare services fall similarly within their purview, with hromadas responsible for family medicine centers, outpatient clinics, and basic medical infrastructure to serve population health needs at the grassroots level.[50] Cultural and recreational facilities, such as libraries, museums, community centers, and sports venues, are managed independently to preserve local heritage and promote social cohesion.[51] Hromadas also exercise authority over local zoning and land-use planning, approving developments and allocating plots for residential, commercial, or agricultural purposes aligned with community strategies.[52] Fiscal independence underpins these competencies, as hromadas retain 60 percent of personal income tax revenues generated within their boundaries, alongside full proceeds from local fees like land taxes and property levies, allowing direct allocation to development initiatives such as infrastructure upgrades or service enhancements.[7] This revenue model, formalized in budget code amendments post-2014, supports discretionary spending on own-priority projects, fostering localized economic decision-making.[38]

Delegated State Responsibilities

Hromadas execute delegated state responsibilities, including the administration of social protection programs for vulnerable populations such as internally displaced persons, civil status registration (e.g., births, marriages, and deaths), and maintenance of local roads, with funding primarily provided through targeted state budget subventions while local bodies ensure compliance with national standards and reporting requirements.[53][54][55] These functions stem from the Law on Local Self-Government, which grants executive authorities' powers to local self-government bodies, but retains central oversight to enforce uniformity, creating tensions where local adaptability is constrained by fiscal dependence on transfers and potential delays in allocations that undermine efficient execution.[56][57] The 2020 administrative reform shifted most sub-regional delegated tasks from abolished raions to hromadas, encompassing expanded social services delivery and infrastructure upkeep previously managed at the district level, thereby concentrating implementation at the community tier but amplifying local burdens without proportional capacity enhancements in all cases.[19] This transition, formalized under the new territorial organization, aimed to streamline service provision but introduced causal frictions, as hromadas faced immediate execution demands amid varying resource bases, often reliant on state directives that prioritized national priorities over localized needs.[58] Under martial law imposed in 2022, delegated responsibilities expanded to include local support for mobilization measures, such as assisting in territorial defense financing and IDP welfare coordination, with hromada heads empowered to allocate budgets for these state-mandated activities, though this heightened central-local tensions as wartime exigencies prompted ad hoc recentralization risks through enhanced oversight and funding conditions.[59][10] Empirical observations indicate that while local administration improved responsiveness in frontline areas, dependency on state subventions for these expanded roles has occasionally led to execution gaps, underscoring the causal link between fiscal leverage and central control over delegated functions.[60]

Fiscal and Resource Management

Hromadas derive revenues primarily from own sources and interbudgetary transfers from the state budget. Own revenues, comprising over 65% of total budgets in 2021, include a 60% share of personal income tax (PIT) accrued in their territories, which accounted for 61% of tax revenues, and local taxes such as property and land taxes, contributing about 30% of tax revenues.[11][61] Hromadas possess limited tax autonomy to set rates within statutory limits, for instance, property tax rates up to 1.5% of the minimum wage per square meter for non-privileged properties.[62] Non-tax own revenues, including fees and fines, make up roughly 6% of own funds.[11] Interbudgetary transfers constitute approximately 35% of hromada budgets, encompassing formula-based grants for equalizing fiscal capacity and targeted subventions for delegated state functions like education and healthcare.[11] These transfers, while stabilizing, tie hromada finances to central allocations, with total aggregated hromada revenues reaching UAH 396.4 billion in 2021.[11] Pre-war average annual budgets per hromada ranged from UAH 200–500 million, varying by population and economic base, with mean own revenue per capita at UAH 6,300.[11] Post-decentralization reforms enabled hromadas to issue debt for capital projects, subject to constraints including debt service not exceeding 10% of general fund expenditures and total debt limited to 200% of development budget revenues (400% for Kyiv).[61] Financial management occurs through dedicated local treasury accounts within the state treasury system, allowing execution of expenditures and cash management, supplemented by internal financial departments.[61] External audits are conducted by the State Audit Service, ensuring compliance, though pre-war analyses indicated potential for revenue optimization via better tax collection efficiency.[11] Despite enhanced fiscal autonomy, hromadas face sustainability risks from transfer dependency, as formula adjustments or fiscal policy shifts at the central level—such as subvention revisions for delegated powers—can alter resource availability without local recourse.[63] This exposure was evident in pre-war idle cash balances averaging billions of UAH across local governments, signaling inefficiencies in resource allocation amid rigid expenditure earmarking for protected items like salaries.[61]

Impacts and Evaluations

Positive Outcomes in Development and Resilience

The decentralization reforms establishing hromadas since 2015 have facilitated substantial local investments in infrastructure and services, with amalgamated communities demonstrating enhanced fiscal capacity to fund development projects independently of central directives. Reports indicate that these reforms enabled hromadas to allocate resources more efficiently toward roads, schools, and utilities, contributing to measurable improvements in local economic activity and service delivery.[61][17] These structural changes have also fostered greater social capital and interpersonal trust within communities, reversing prior perceptions of low civic engagement in Ukraine. Empirical analysis attributes this uplift to the devolution of decision-making powers, which encouraged participatory governance and strengthened community bonds essential for collective action.[3] In the context of Russia's full-scale invasion beginning February 24, 2022, hromadas exhibited notable resilience by rapidly organizing humanitarian aid distribution, territorial defense coordination, and civilian evacuations at the local level. Studies highlight that pre-war decentralization equipped these entities with the administrative autonomy and financial buffers needed for swift, adaptive responses, particularly in frontline regions where centralized command structures proved slower.[36][38] Quantitative assessments link higher pre-invasion hromada capacities—such as revenue generation from local taxes—to reduced operational disruptions and sustained service provision amid hostilities, with economically stronger communities maintaining lower vulnerability indices during the conflict's early phases. This localized resilience mitigated broader systemic shocks, as evidenced by hromadas' ability to sustain civic networks for crisis management despite ongoing threats.[38][64]

Empirical Evidence of Effectiveness

Empirical analyses of Ukraine's territorial amalgamations, which formed hromadas starting in 2014, demonstrate systematic increases in local revenues and expenditures following the reform. A study utilizing local budget data from 2012 to 2018 found that amalgamated territorial communities (ATCs) experienced enhanced fiscal capacities, enabling greater investment in public goods provision, with dynamic treatment effects models confirming positive shifts in both revenue mobilization and spending levels compared to non-amalgamated units.[65] This scaling effect addressed inefficiencies inherent in smaller pre-reform municipalities, where fragmented administration often led to underutilized resources and suboptimal service delivery.[11] Fiscal decentralization under the hromada system improved local spending efficiency, as evidenced by higher own-revenue efforts in amalgamated communities. In 2021, own revenues reached 396.4 billion UAH, comprising 68% of local budgets and 24% of the national budget, primarily driven by personal income tax contributions. Analyses indicate an average own-revenue effort of 76.5–78.1%, with untapped potential for a 21.8% increase—equivalent to approximately 44 billion UAH or $1.5 billion—particularly in urban hromadas, which exhibited 88.5% efficiency versus 74.4% in rural ones. Capital expenditures showed positive elasticity (0.11–0.12) relative to revenue growth, supporting infrastructure and service enhancements.[11] Emerging research attributes these gains to amalgamation's role in consolidating administrative capacities, reducing small-unit failures, and fostering better revenue collection through elevated administrative spending (elasticity of 0.66–0.69). Such outcomes have bolstered local economic resilience and service provision, with studies like Nivievskyi et al. (2021) highlighting overall positive impacts on budget robustness pre-war. While regional variations persist—e.g., lower efficiency in western oblasts like Chernivetska (59.4%)—the reform's causal mechanism of resource scaling has empirically elevated hromadas' operational effectiveness.[65][11]

Criticisms and Limitations

Structural and Operational Shortcomings

The amalgamation process for hromadas, initially voluntary from 2015, resulted in significant variation in community sizes and capacities, with 35% of united territorial communities (UTCs, or hromadas) having fewer than 5,000 inhabitants by 2018, limiting economies of scale for service delivery in areas like healthcare and education.[66] Poor guidance on partner selection during mergers often led to under-capacitated units, exacerbating territorial fragmentation, particularly in rural areas where coordination costs rose due to dispersed populations and infrastructure deficits.[66] The 2020 administrative reform mandated mergers for remaining unamalgamated communities, completing the formation of 1,469 hromadas and covering over 90% of Ukraine's territory, but this shift from voluntary to compulsory processes sparked widespread local resistance, including over 100 protest actions across regions in the first half of 2020 alone.[43][67] Such forced consolidations undermined the reform's original emphasis on voluntary cooperation, fostering perceptions of top-down imposition and questioning the sustainability of merged units lacking organic consensus.[43] Operational challenges were evident in capacity gaps, especially in rural hromadas, where limited administrative expertise and staff shortages—compounded by low staff turnover rates of around 10%—hindered effective management of delegated functions like property tax administration, with real estate taxes comprising only 2% of local revenues by 2021.[66][67] These deficiencies contributed to unspent funds, totaling UAH 49 billion in 2017, due to inadequate project design and implementation skills.[66] Corruption risks heightened with decentralization, as local elites gained control over larger budgets, enabling potential embezzlement, procurement overpricing, and illegal land grabs amid weak territorial planning regulations.[33] Only 25% of hromadas had full-time anti-corruption officers by 2024, with rural areas particularly underserved in expertise, leading to superficial risk assessments and vulnerability to service capture.[33] Elections from 2015 to 2020 reflected operational disengagement, with voter turnout dropping to 46.6% in 2015 local polls and a historic low of 37% in 2020, signaling low trust and participation in newly merged rural units where administrative burdens outpaced perceived benefits.[66][68]

War-Related Disruptions and Recentralization Pressures

Following the imposition of martial law on February 24, 2022, in response to Russia's full-scale invasion, Ukraine suspended local elections, including those for hromada councils, as constitutionally prohibited during such conditions.[69] This extension of local mandates, reaffirmed by parliamentary resolutions in October 2025 declaring elections impossible amid ongoing aggression, has deferred democratic renewal in hromadas, prioritizing wartime continuity over electoral cycles.[70] [35] Military administrations, established in front-line and occupied regions, have overridden hromada autonomy in approximately 13% of communities, particularly those within 25 km of active hostilities, subsuming local decision-making under central military command for security imperatives.[39] [9] These structures, covering key eastern and southern oblasts, facilitate rapid resource allocation but erode decentralized competencies, as local bodies yield to unified defense protocols in territories comprising roughly 20% of pre-invasion land under occupation or threat.[10] Fiscal recentralization has intensified, with 2023 proposals redirecting personal income tax revenues from military personnel—previously shared with hromadas—into centralized defense funds, straining local budgets amid reduced economic activity.[71] State budget amendments, such as the 2023 shifts prioritizing defense over subnational allocations, have funneled resources upward, with total defense spending reaching 1.85 trillion hryvnias by year-end, often at the expense of hromada-level service provision.[72] Front-line hromadas face acute strains from internally displaced persons (IDPs), numbering over 4.6 million nationwide as of 2024, with many concentrated in border communities hosting up to 20% of their population as evacuees.[73] Infrastructure damage exacerbates vulnerabilities: by May 2024, 58% of front-line hromadas reported education facility destruction and 48% health infrastructure losses, hindering service delivery and fostering dependency on central aid.[74] In occupied areas, hromada functions have collapsed, with local governance supplanted by Russian proxies, leading to documented erosion of institutional resilience and capacity for post-liberation recovery.[75] These wartime adaptations, while enabling coordinated defense, generate recentralization pressures that threaten the 2014 decentralization framework's gains in local empowerment, as analyzed in 2025 studies highlighting risks of entrenched central dominance post-conflict absent deliberate safeguards.[10] [34] Empirical assessments underscore a tension: short-term centralization aids survival, yet prolonged overrides could rollback fiscal and administrative autonomy, per regional governance evaluations.[9]

Distribution and Classification

Types and Categorization

Hromadas in Ukraine are primarily classified by the type of their administrative center, distinguishing urban hromadas (centered in cities), settlement hromadas (centered in urban-type settlements, or selyshche), and rural hromadas (centered in villages). This typology accounts for variations in population density, economic profiles, and service delivery capacities, with urban hromadas typically encompassing larger populations and more diversified revenue sources from local taxes and businesses, while rural and settlement hromadas often rely more heavily on agricultural activities and state transfers.[1] The classification supports differentiated policy approaches, ensuring that resource allocation and support mechanisms address inherent disparities rather than applying uniform standards that could exacerbate inequalities between densely populated urban areas and sparsely settled rural ones.[17] Capability assessments provide a secondary layer of categorization, evaluating hromadas' financial and operational viability through indicators such as per capita own-source revenues, population size, infrastructure endowment, and administrative efficiency. The Ministry for Communities and Territories Development conducts these evaluations, grouping hromadas into high, medium, and low capability levels; for instance, high-capability hromadas demonstrate robust local revenue generation exceeding national averages, often correlated with urban centers benefiting from industrial and commercial bases.[76] Rural hromadas, by contrast, frequently fall into lower categories due to limited tax bases and higher dependence on interbudgetary transfers, with criteria emphasizing sustainable revenue potential over mere population thresholds.[11] This framework, rooted in the 2015 methodology for forming viable communities, guides targeted state interventions, such as capacity-building grants, to enhance weaker hromadas without undermining stronger ones' autonomy.[77] As of 2020, following the completion of amalgamation reforms, approximately 366 urban hromadas coexisted with over 1,100 rural and settlement variants, highlighting the predominance of less urbanized types amid Ukraine's overall territorial structure of 1,469 hromadas. These categorizations underscore causal factors like geographic contiguity and economic clustering in determining viability, informing allocations that prioritize empirical fiscal health over ideological uniformity.[11]

Current Statistics and Mapping

Ukraine's decentralization reform established 1,469 amalgamated territorial communities, known as hromadas, by July 2020, a structure that persists as of 2025.[5] These hromadas encompass 409 urban, 435 settlement, and 625 rural types, serving as the primary units of local self-government across the country's 24 oblasts and the Autonomous Republic of Crimea.[78] Distribution varies significantly by oblast, with denser rural networks in western regions like Ivano-Frankivsk and Ternopil featuring higher numbers of hromadas due to smaller settlement sizes, while eastern and central oblasts have fewer but larger urban-centered ones.[79] Pre-war average populations ranged from 20,000 to 50,000 residents per hromada, though displacement from the 2022 Russian invasion has reduced effective sizes in frontline areas, with some communities losing up to half their inhabitants.[64] Interactive mapping resources, including the official decentralization portal, delineate hromada boundaries, administrative centers, and integration with raion-level subdivisions, facilitating geospatial analysis of local governance units.[79] As of 2024, the war impacts over 100 hromadas through partial occupation or frontline status, particularly in Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts, where Russian control affects administrative functionality and resource allocation.[80] In non-occupied zones, 2025 data indicate stabilizing local budgets, bolstered by international aid including EU programs aimed at enhancing hromada fiscal autonomy and infrastructure repair.[81]
