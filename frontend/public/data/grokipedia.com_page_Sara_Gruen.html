Sara Gruen

Sara Gruen (born 1969) is a Canadian-American author best known for her historical novel Water for Elephants (2006), a New York Times #1 bestseller that has sold over 10 million copies worldwide, been translated into 43 languages, and adapted into a 2011 film directed by Francis Lawrence.[1] Born in Vancouver, British Columbia, Gruen was raised in London, Ontario, earned a bachelor's degree in English literature from Carleton University in Ottawa, and relocated to the United States in 1999, where she transitioned from technical writing to full-time fiction authorship.[2][3] Her novels, which frequently explore themes of human-animal bonds amid historical backdrops such as circuses, equestrian worlds, and World War II-era Scotland, include Ape House (2010), At the Water's Edge (2015), Riding Lessons (2003), and Flying Changes (2005), several of which have also reached USA Today bestseller lists.[4] Gruen's accolades for Water for Elephants encompass the 2007 Indies Choice Book Award for Adult Fiction from the American Booksellers Association, the Alex Award from the American Library Association for adult books appealing to young readers, and finalist status for the Audie Award in fiction.[5] She resides in Asheville, North Carolina, with her husband, three sons, and an extensive menagerie of pets including dogs, cats, horses, birds, and goats, underscoring her longstanding commitment to animal welfare organizations.[4][6] Post-success, Gruen invested years and substantial resources in advocating for the release of a man convicted of murder in Illinois, an obsession that culminated in her declaring bankruptcy and facing severe health issues by 2021.[7]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Origins

Sara Gruen was born in 1969 in Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada.[8] She grew up in London, Ontario.[9] Gruen's parents were musicologists, exposing her to classical music from an early age; she trained as a violinist and was expected to pursue it seriously. This familial emphasis on music contrasted with her developing personal interests, as she often practiced violin in her parents' library, where she discovered a preference for reading over musical study.[10] From childhood, Gruen harbored a deep affinity for animals, rooted in dreams and experiences that persisted throughout her life, alongside an enduring love for storytelling.[11][12] These early inclinations, shaped by her Canadian upbringing amid family relocations within the country, laid foundational influences without formal structure at the time.[6]

Formal Education and Early Influences

Gruen attended Carleton University in Ottawa, Canada, where she initially pursued a degree in music performance before switching to English literature, from which she graduated with a Bachelor of Arts.[6][2] Following graduation, she remained in Ottawa for approximately ten years, during which her formal education in literature provided foundational skills in analysis and narrative structure that later informed her writing aspirations.[5][2] In 1999, Gruen relocated to the United States to accept a position as a technical writer, a role that leveraged her literary background for precise communication and documentation tasks.[3][2] This early professional experience honed her ability to craft clear, structured prose under deadlines, contrasting with the creative freedom of fiction but building discipline she credited as essential preparation for novel-writing.[11] The move marked a shift from Canadian academic environments to American professional demands, exposing her to new cultural and economic contexts that subtly shaped her perspectives on resilience and adaptation in her later works.[13] Laid off from her technical writing job in 2001, Gruen, then in her early thirties, opted against re-entering the workforce, instead committing to full-time fiction writing after producing two unpublished novels during her pre-layoff years.[14][13] This pivot reflected the cumulative influence of her English literature training, which had instilled a passion for storytelling, combined with the practical rigor of technical writing, enabling her to approach fiction with both artistic intent and methodical execution.[15]

Literary Career

Pre-Breakthrough Publications

Sara Gruen's entry into publishing followed her layoff from a technical writing position in 2001, prompting her to pursue fiction full-time under a two-year agreement with her husband to support the endeavor or return to employment.[16] Her debut novel, Riding Lessons, was published in 2004 by William Morrow, an imprint of HarperCollins.[17] The story follows protagonist Annemarie Zimmer, a former competitive equestrienne whose Olympic aspirations ended in a teenage accident, as she confronts job loss, marital dissolution, and her father's terminal illness by returning to the family horse farm.[18] Centered on equestrian life, the narrative examines themes of personal healing, loss, survival, renewal, and familial tensions, particularly mother-daughter dynamics amid dysfunction.[19][20] Prior to securing publication, Gruen endured extensive rejections, issuing around 129 agent queries before obtaining representation that facilitated the deal for Riding Lessons.[13] The novel received modest commercial and critical attention, appealing primarily to readers interested in horse-centric stories but not achieving widespread acclaim.[14] In 2005, Gruen released the sequel Flying Changes, also through William Morrow, extending the equestrian focus to Annemarie's evolving challenges with a stagnating romance, her daughter's competitive riding ambitions, and the imperative to confront lingering fears.[21] The book emphasizes personal growth, balancing parental roles with individual aspirations, and navigating relational and emotional upheavals within the horse farm setting.[22] Like its predecessor, it garnered moderate reception, solidifying Gruen's early niche in equine-themed fiction without propelling her to broader recognition.[14]

Water for Elephants and Rise to Fame

Water for Elephants, Gruen's third novel, originated from her immersion in Depression-era circus history, prompted initially by a Chicago Tribune article on photographer Edward J. Kelty and expanded through months of research at institutions like the Circus World Museum in Baraboo, Wisconsin.[23][24] This groundwork enabled Gruen to authentically depict the era's traveling shows, including their hierarchical "department" structures, animal handling, and economic precarity amid the Great Depression.[1] The manuscript faced initial rejections, including from Gruen's prior editor at Avon Books, before Algonquin Books acquired it for publication in May 2006.[25] Algonquin initially planned a 15,000-copy print run but doubled it to 30,000 following strong pre-publication bookseller demand.[26] Unlike Gruen's earlier works, which had modest sales through smaller presses, Water for Elephants marked a breakthrough by leveraging targeted marketing to independent booksellers and word-of-mouth appeal, propelling it beyond niche historical fiction audiences. Commercially, the novel achieved explosive success, selling over 10 million copies worldwide and topping the New York Times paperback fiction bestseller list while maintaining a prolonged presence on the hardcover chart for more than four years.[1][27] It has been translated into 43 languages, reflecting its broad international resonance.[28] This scale of achievementâ€”far surpassing Gruen's prior titlesâ€”established her as a commercial powerhouse, with early U.S. paperback sales alone exceeding 217,000 units per Nielsen BookScan data.[25] Thematically, Water for Elephants interweaves romance, survival, and critiques of animal exploitation within a circus framework, grounded in verifiable historical practices such as veterinary improvisations and the era's "redlight" versus "white light" performer divisions.[1] Its narrative authenticity stems from Gruen's empirical sourcing, including period accounts of spectacles like the Benzini Brothers circus analogue, which mirrored real Depression outfits' instabilities without fabricating causal mechanisms for events.[29] This fidelity contributed to its cultural footprint, distinguishing it as a pivotal work that elevated Gruen from obscurity to sustained literary prominence.

Subsequent Novels and Creative Output

Gruen's second major novel, Ape House, was published in September 2010 by Spiegel & Grau.[30] The story centers on a group of bonobo apes trained in language at a research facility who escape following an explosion, leading to their exploitation in a reality television show that satirizes media sensationalism and human-ape interactions.[31] The book debuted on the New York Times bestseller list but received mixed critical reception, with reviewers noting its overcrowded plot and reliance on coincidences despite engaging animal portrayals.[32][31] In 2015, Gruen released At the Water's Edge, also through Spiegel & Grau, shifting to historical fiction set during World War II.[33] The narrative follows a disillusioned American socialite accompanying her husband and his friend to Scotland in search of the Loch Ness Monster amid wartime rationing and personal reckonings, incorporating elements of romance and self-discovery against the backdrop of the 1942 monster sighting hype.[34] It achieved commercial success as a bestseller but garnered critiques for stereotypical characters and predictable plotting, though praised for atmospheric historical details.[35][33] As of October 2025, Gruen has not published additional major novels since At the Water's Edge, with her output reflecting a pattern of extended intervals between works following her debut breakthrough.[36] Her post-2006 books maintained strong sales viability, often appearing on bestseller lists, yet they did not replicate the critical and commercial dominance of Water for Elephants, signaling a stylistic evolution toward more contemporary and historical ensemble narratives.[33][37]

Writing Themes and Critical Reception

Gruen's novels recurrently explore empathy toward nonhuman animals, drawing on documented behaviors and social structures to underscore their emotional capacities and challenge human-centered assumptions about intelligence and suffering. In works featuring circus elephants and bonobo apes, she incorporates research into species-specific traits, such as matriarchal hierarchies among bonobos and the impacts of captivity on pachyderms, to depict animals as sentient beings deserving moral consideration rather than mere spectacles or tools.[38][11][39] This animal focus intersects with broader motifs of outsider perspectives and resilience amid exploitation, often through protagonists navigating marginal social worlds like Depression-era circuses or scientific labs, emphasizing loyalty, identity, and ethical dilemmas without didactic political overlays. Her approach prioritizes observational realismâ€”grounded in historical accounts of animal handling and interspecies bondsâ€”over sentimental anthropomorphism, fostering reader insight into causal links between human actions and animal welfare.[40][41][42] Critical reception has been mixed, with praise for Gruen's immersive historical reconstructions and brisk narrative momentum, particularly in evoking the gritty authenticity of bygone eras through verified details of practices like circus logistics. Reviewers have commended her ability to weave compassion and moral complexity into accessible prose, as evidenced by the sustained commercial success and acclaim for her breakthrough novel's portrayal of human-animal dynamics.[43][32][44] However, detractors have noted formulaic romantic subplots and occasional narrative haste that amplify dramatic contrivances over subtlety, alongside critiques of pacing inconsistencies in later works where human characterizations feel uneven compared to the more rigorously researched animal elements. Some analyses highlight liberties in blending fact with fiction, potentially straining historical fidelity for emotional impact, though her empirical grounding in animal ethology remains a consistent strength amid these variances.[44][45][32]

Adaptations and Media Impact

Film Adaptation of Water for Elephants

The 2011 film adaptation of Water for Elephants, directed by Francis Lawrence, starred Robert Pattinson as the protagonist Jacob Jankowski, Reese Witherspoon as Marlena, and Christoph Waltz as the circus owner August Rosenbluth.[46] Distributed by 20th Century Fox and released theatrically on April 22, 2011, the production faced translational challenges in converting the novel's raw, Depression-era circus gritâ€”marked by animal mistreatment, economic desperation, and moral ambiguityâ€”into a visually driven romance suitable for wide audiences.[47] Screenwriter Richard LaGravenese adapted Sara Gruen's source material, emphasizing spectacle over the book's unsparing depictions of violence and exploitation, which some critics argued resulted in a perfunctory climax and diluted thematic depth.[48] Produced on a budget of $38 million, the film achieved commercial success by grossing $58.7 million domestically and $117 million worldwide, reflecting strong international appeal driven by its stars' draw and period aesthetics despite mixed critical reception.[49] Reviews praised the cinematography and production design for evoking authentic circus grandeur, but faulted the adaptation for prioritizing soapy romance and sanitized visuals over the novel's causal undercurrents of hardship and ethical compromise.[50] This softening aligned with media economics favoring broader accessibility, as evidenced by its 60% Rotten Tomatoes score, where audience metrics outpaced critics amid complaints of predictability.[47] Gruen maintained limited on-set involvement, limited to consultation and a cameo role, prioritizing her contract's stipulations for animal welfare amid the story's focus on elephant Rosie.[51] The American Humane Association monitored filming, certifying no animals were harmed through separate green-screen shots and controlled interactions, countering activist allegations of off-set elephant mistreatment via edited videos.[52][53] Such measures addressed real-world causal risks in using live animals, though critics from animal advocacy groups contended the production contradicted the novel's exposÃ© on circus abuses by normalizing captive performances.[54]

Other Media Projects

Gruen's novels have been adapted into audiobooks, expanding their accessibility through narrated formats. Water for Elephants received an audiobook release in 2006, narrated by David LeDoux and John Randolph Jones, with a runtime of 11 hours and 26 minutes, distributed by platforms including Audible and Amazon.[55][56] Subsequent works such as At the Water's Edge (2015) also feature professional narrations, available via services like Audiobooks.com, though these productions remain standard audio conversions without additional multimedia elements.[57] Beyond audiobooks, Gruen's works have seen limited exploration in other formats, with no major television series adaptations developed as of 2025. Early discussions around pitching circus-themed elements from Water for Elephants for episodic television surfaced post-2011 film but failed to materialize into productions.[51] A stage musical adaptation of Water for Elephants, with book by Rick Elice and music by PigPen Theatre Co., premiered at the Alliance Theatre in Atlanta on June 16, 2023, before transferring to Broadway's Imperial Theatre, where it opened March 21, 2024, and closed after a limited run on September 1, 2024.[58] This production incorporated circus performers into the cast for acrobatic staging but did not extend to touring or further media iterations.[59] Gruen maintains a digital presence through her official website, saragruen.com, which provides updates on publications and author events, and her X (formerly Twitter) account @SaraGruen, where she occasionally shares insights into her writing process and research.[36][60] These platforms focus primarily on literary promotion rather than interactive media projects.

Awards and Recognition

Literary Honors and Accolades

Sara Gruen's novel Water for Elephants (2006) earned the Alex Award in 2007 from the American Library Association's Young Adult Library Services Association, an honor given annually to ten books written for adults that demonstrate special appeal to readers aged 12 through 18.[61] The selection highlights the book's narrative of a young man's entry into the world of a traveling circus during the Great Depression, which resonated with younger audiences through its themes of resilience and romance despite mature content.[62] The same novel received the Book Sense Book of the Year Award for adult fiction in 2007, bestowed by the American Booksellers Association to recognize titles favored by independent booksellers and customers.[63] Originally under the Book Sense program (later rebranded as IndieBound), this accolade underscored the grassroots enthusiasm for Gruen's work among booksellers, positioning it as a standout in commercial fiction without reliance on major publisher marketing alone.[64] Additional recognitions for Water for Elephants include a nomination for the Quill Award in general fiction in 2006 and a finalist placement in the Audie Awards for fiction audiobook in 2007, reflecting acclaim in both print and audio formats.[65] Gruen's oeuvre has not garnered top-tier prizes such as the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction or the National Book Award, though its translation into over 50 languages serves as an indirect measure of global literary interest.[5] These honors primarily cluster around her breakthrough work, affirming its role in elevating her profile within popular and genre fiction circles.

Involvement in Criminal Justice Advocacy

The Charles Murdoch Murder Conviction

On May 17, 1983, Charles Franklin Murdoch, Jr., along with co-defendant Dino Dinardo and others, robbed Chris's Horseshoe Bar in Long Beach, California, during which bar patron Robert Segale was fatally shot in the head, and another patron was stabbed and critically wounded.[66][67] The robbery involved forcing patrons to the floor, demanding money from the cash register, and fleeing with approximately $300.[68] Murdoch was charged with first-degree murder under California's robbery-murder special circumstance, which elevates felony murder during robbery to first-degree if the defendant acted with reckless indifference to human life.[69] Prosecution evidence included Dinardo's trial testimony identifying Murdoch as the shooter who entered the bar wearing a jacket and carrying a revolver, a fingerprint from Dinardo on the cash register, and an identification of Murdoch as the gunman by witness Dyanne Spence, who came forward approximately 12 years after the crime based on a police sketch resemblance.[68][67] The robbery motive was established through the theft of cash and lack of personal animus toward the victim, with forensic ballistics linking the murder weapon to the crime scene.[70] The defense contended that the evidence was insufficient to prove Murdoch's participation beyond reasonable doubt, highlighting Dinardo's potential coercion in his testimony and Spence's delayed identification as unreliable due to the passage of time and initial failure to identify Murdoch in lineups.[68] In 1995, a jury convicted Murdoch of first-degree murder with the robbery-murder special circumstance true, leading to a sentence of life imprisonment without the possibility of parole; Dinardo, tried separately, received a reduced sentence of 12 years after pleading to manslaughter.[66][69] Murdoch's direct appeal to the California Court of Appeal was denied, affirming the conviction based on the sufficiency of evidence under the substantial evidence standard and rejecting claims of instructional error or prosecutorial misconduct.[67] Subsequent habeas petitions to the California Supreme Court, U.S. District Court, and Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals were denied, with federal courts deferring to state findings under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act and concluding that the trial record supported the jury's verdict without constitutional violations warranting relief.[68][69] As of 2010, the Ninth Circuit explicitly upheld the denial of habeas relief, noting no clear and convincing evidence to rebut the presumption of correctness for state court factual determinations.[67]

Gruen's Investigation and Advocacy Efforts

In June 2015, Gruen received a forwarded fan letter from Charles Murdoch, an inmate at a California prison, who praised her novel Water for Elephants and described parallels between its themes and his conviction for first-degree murder.[71] Intrigued by Murdoch's account of trial irregularities, including recanted witness testimony and prosecutorial misconduct, Gruen began independently scrutinizing the case files.[7] Gruen assembled a team of private investigators and legal experts, committing thousands of personal hours to reviewing over thousands of documents, transcripts, and forensic reports from the 1992 trial and appeals.[72] She initiated formal legal support by issuing a $7,500 retainer check in October 2015 and contracting for $20,000 monthly payments to a specialized attorney focused on post-conviction relief.[7] These efforts emphasized causal analysis of evidentiary gaps, such as alibi inconsistencies and alleged suppression of exculpatory materials, drawing partial validation from Ninth Circuit Chief Judge Alex Kozinski's prior characterization of the conviction as a "truly spectacular miscarriage of justice."[71] Financially, Gruen's investigations accrued expenditures exceeding $500,000, covering expert consultations, document acquisition, and litigation preparation, which strained her resources and prompted home refinancing.[73] To sustain funding amid depleting personal assets, she pursued unconventional methods, including bulk purchasing and reselling Hatchimals toys in late 2016, allocating 100% of proceedsâ€”publicized via national media appearancesâ€”to legal fees.[74] Gruen's advocacy extended to public campaigns, where she disseminated analyses of trial flaws, including witness deals and forensic discrepancies, through op-eds, interviews, and online statements framing the case as emblematic of systemic conviction errors.[72] These efforts aligned with innocence project paradigms emphasizing re-examination of physical evidence and incentives for false testimony, yet her interpretations of suppressed materials encountered pushback from archival records indicating corroborative witness alignments and procedural validations.[71][7]

Financial and Personal Repercussions

Gruen's advocacy efforts led to substantial financial strain, including refinancing her home and expending over $250,000 by 2016 on legal fees, investigations, and related costs, such as an initial $7,500 retainer and subsequent monthly payments of $20,000 to attorneys.[7] By 2021, these expenditures, combined with exhausted savings, had left her "absolutely broke," forcing her to borrow against her home equity, clip coupons, forgo vacations, and face debt levels comparable to bankruptcy.[7] [71] The physical toll manifested in severe stress-related health deterioration, including vertigo, chronic migraines, recurrent flu and pneumonia, profound brain fog, and adrenal malfunction with critically low cortisol levels, causing her weight to plummet to 95 pounds and daily fainting episodes. In December 2017, she experienced transient global amnesia, temporarily losing hours of memory, and by August 2019, her condition had escalated to a near-death state marked by cognitive decline and possible infection flare-ups exacerbated by stress.[7] [71] Threats intensified following the 2016 Hatchimals resale controversy, drawing death and rape threats from online fringe groups, prompting her to flee her home multiple times in 2018, relocate six times in five months, and install a safe room while using disguises amid suspicions of surveillance by individuals linked to Aryan Nation affiliates.[7] [71] Her obsession strained family dynamics, with her husband assuming case correspondence and animal care responsibilities during her absences, while her son accompanied her during relocations, disrupting household stability and contributing to her later regrets over the personal costs.[7]

Legal Outcomes and Broader Implications

The conviction of Charles Murdoch for first-degree murder in the 1983 Horseshoe Bar robbery-killing was upheld by the California Court of Appeal, with the state Supreme Court denying further review prior to Gruen's involvement.[71] Federal habeas corpus petitions were subsequently denied by the district court and affirmed by the Ninth Circuit in 2007, rejecting claims of insufficient evidence and prosecutorial misconduct despite dissenting opinions, such as Ninth Circuit Judge Alex Kozinski's characterization of the case as a "spectacular miscarriage of justice" based on unreliable witness testimony.[69] Courts emphasized the preponderance of trial evidence, including accomplice Dino Dinardo's testimony linking Murdoch to the robbery and murder, over post-conviction reinterpretations of coercion or mental health issues in identifications.[68] Gruen's 2018 submission of a 50-page evidentiary dossier to the Los Angeles County District Attorney's Conviction Review Unit, highlighting alleged coerced confessions and witness unreliability, did not result in exoneration or retrial as of the latest available records through 2025, underscoring the high evidentiary threshold required for post-appeal relief in established convictions.[7] This outcome aligns with broader judicial affirmations that prioritize original trial records and multiple layers of appellate scrutiny over supplemental private investigations, which often fail to meet standards for newly discovered evidence under precedents like Schlup v. Delo. Broader implications reveal tensions in conviction integrity processes: while private advocacy can spotlight potential flaws, such as reliance on incentivized testimony, empirical data indicate wrongful conviction rates remain low (estimated at 0.5-4.1% across studies), with most challenged cases upheld due to systemic safeguards like adversarial testing and habeas review.[75] Critics of celebrity-driven efforts argue they risk undermining due process by amplifying unvetted narratives that sway public opinion more than forensic reevaluation, potentially eroding trust in judicial finality without proportional success in overturnings.[76][77] Conversely, cases like Murdoch's illustrate how such initiatives expose gaps in initial investigations, though affirmed guilt via repeated reviews suggests causal emphasis on original evidentiary weight over retrospective advocacy.[71] Private probes, while uncovering details like witness mental health records, demonstrate limited efficacy in isolation, with success rates in generating judicial relief below 1% for post-conviction claims lacking DNA or physical exculpation.[78]

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Sara Gruen married Bob Gruen around 2000; the couple marked 15 years of marriage in 2015.[6] Bob Gruen, who pursued graduate studies in literature at the University of Chicago, supported his wife's transition from technical writing to fiction authorship.[6] Gruen and her husband have three sons, named Benjamin, Thomas, and Daniel, born between the late 1990s and early 2000s.[6] The family relocated together to Asheville, North Carolina, in 2008, shortly after the commercial success of Water for Elephants, enabling Gruen to focus on writing amid a supportive home environment.[6] This move reflected the couple's shared commitment to a rural lifestyle conducive to creative pursuits and family stability.[6] Gruen has consistently kept details of her marital and parental life private, avoiding public disclosures beyond basic family composition in occasional interviews.[63] Her husband occasionally assisted in personal matters requiring intervention, underscoring a collaborative partnership.[7]

Residence and Lifestyle

Sara Gruen relocated to Asheville, North Carolina, around 2008, citing the city's "magical energy" as a key draw, alongside its renowned artistic community and surrounding natural landscapes.[6] Her residence is situated in the rural Beaverdam area on the outskirts of Asheville, providing a secluded setting amid countryside that aligns with her preference for environments fostering focused creativity.[79] The home maintains a menagerie of animals, including dogs, cats, horses such as Tia and Fancy, goats, birds, and periodically adopted kittens, reflecting Gruen's longstanding affinity for animal companionship that parallels motifs in her novels.[10][7] This rural, animal-centric household supports immersive writing routines, marked by episodes of deep concentration that have confined her to the property for stretches as long as six weeks.[6] Gruen engages with Asheville's literary ecosystem through participation in local events, such as book readings and signings at independent venues like Malaprop's Bookstore, both before and after achieving widespread recognition.[80]

Health and Financial Status Post-Advocacy

Following her intensive involvement in the Charles Murdoch case, which spanned approximately six years and concluded without exoneration by early 2021, Sara Gruen faced profound financial collapse, having depleted her resources through expenditures exceeding $500,000 on private investigations, expert witnesses, forensic testing, and legal fees.[7] Previously, the commercial triumph of Water for Elephantsâ€”which sold over 10 million copies worldwide and inspired a 2011 film adaptationâ€”had elevated her to financial independence, enabling a comfortable lifestyle including property ownership and family support.[7] By March 2021, Gruen described herself as "absolutely broke," reliant on credit card debt and unable to meet basic obligations, marking a stark transition from prosperity to precarity driven by unchecked personal investment in the advocacy.[7] Concurrently, the sustained stress exacerbated pre-existing vulnerabilities, manifesting in acute physical decline; Gruen reported "crippling vertigo" that rendered her bedridden for periods and halted professional output, including failure to advance a contracted novel due by late 2020.[81][7] She characterized her overall condition as "seriously ill," with symptoms persisting amid the unresolved emotional toll of the case, which she linked directly to the financial ruin and obsessive focus.[7][82] This health crisis contributed to a de facto writing hiatus, as Gruen's last major publication, At the Water's Edge in 2015, preceded the advocacy's peak demands, raising questions about her capacity for future literary contributions absent intervention.[7] As of reports in 2021, no substantial recovery in health or finances was documented, with Gruen's condition underscoring the risks of individual-led justice pursuits without institutional safeguards, though she expressed no regret over the expenditures despite the fallout.[7][82]
