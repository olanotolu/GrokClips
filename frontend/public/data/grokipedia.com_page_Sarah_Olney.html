Sarah Olney


Sarah Olney (born 1977) is a British politician and chartered accountant serving as the Liberal Democrats' Member of Parliament for Richmond Park since December 2019.[1][2]
She first won the seat in a December 2016 by-election triggered by the resignation of Conservative MP Zac Goldsmith over Brexit, holding it until losing to Goldsmith in the 2017 general election, before reclaiming it in the 2019 general election and successfully defending it in 2024 amid the Liberal Democrats' national gains.[2][3]
Olney has occupied multiple shadow spokesperson roles for her party, including on the Treasury from 2022 to 2024, transport, business, and international trade, and currently serves as spokesperson for business as of October 2025.[2]
Prior to politics, she worked as a qualified accountant after studying English literature at King's College London, and she joined the Liberal Democrats in 2015 partly in response to the European Union membership referendum result.[1][4]
Her parliamentary focus includes economic policy, small business support, and constituency issues in the competitive, Remain-leaning Richmond Park area, where repeated seat flips highlight her role in the party's local resilience.[5][6]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Sarah Jane Olney, nÃ©e McGibbon, was born on 11 January 1977 and raised in Surrey Heath, England, in a suburban middle-class household.[7] Her father worked as a headteacher in the public sector, while her mother was a nurse; both parents were supporters of the Conservative Party.[7] Olney has described her family background as lacking strong political influences, with no immediate relatives or close friends initially involved in party politics.[7] Her upbringing emphasized public-sector values through her parents' professions, though their Conservative voting preferences contrasted with Olney's later alignment with the Liberal Democrats, whom she began supporting prior to formally joining in May 2015.[7] She attended a comprehensive school in Surrey before pursuing higher education.[8]

Academic qualifications

Olney attended a state comprehensive school in Surrey.[9][8] Following her A-levels, she was accepted to study English literature at King's College London, from which she graduated.[4][10]

Pre-political career

Professional roles in finance and law

Prior to entering politics, Olney pursued a career in finance, qualifying as a chartered accountant through the Association of Chartered Certified Accountants in 2016.[11][4] Following her degree in English Literature from King's College London, she initially worked in project management roles, including at Barclays Bank, before transitioning into finance management within the charity sector.[4][11] Olney held accounting positions at several organizations, including Arts & Business, Distilled Ltd, and SCi Sales Group, where she gained experience in financial management.[11] She later served as Assistant Finance Manager at the National Physical Laboratory in Teddington from February 2015 to December 2016, contributing to the finance team during her qualification period.[11][4] These roles involved handling financial operations, budgeting, and compliance in both commercial and public research settings.[6] No verified professional experience in law has been identified in Olney's pre-political career; her expertise centered on accounting and financial oversight.[11] After her brief tenure as MP in 2016â€“2017, she returned to finance as a Financial Accountant at Historic Royal Palaces from March 2018 until resuming parliamentary duties in December 2019.[6]

Transition to local activism

Prior to entering national politics, Olney transitioned from her career in accountancy by joining the Liberal Democrats in 2015, shortly after the party's significant electoral defeat in the May general election and Nick Clegg's subsequent resignation as leader.[12] This move marked her shift from passive support for the party's values to active involvement, driven by a recognition that the Liberal Democrats' reduced parliamentary presenceâ€”down from 57 seats to 8â€”necessitated greater grassroots participation to rebuild influence.[12][7] Her initial activism focused on local party efforts in Richmond Park, a constituency with strong pro-European Union sentiment that had voted 72% to remain in the 2016 referendum.[13] Olney engaged in campaigning against Brexit and local issues such as Heathrow airport expansion, aligning with the area's environmental concerns that had previously mobilized voters under incumbent Zac Goldsmith.[14] By October 2016, less than 18 months after joining, she was selected as the Liberal Democrats' by-election candidate following Goldsmith's resignation over his opposition to a second EU referendum on the issue.[15][16] This rapid elevation reflected the party's strategy to capitalize on local Remain support rather than relying on established figures, positioning Olney as a fresh, non-career politician voice.[7] In her victory speech after winning the December 1, 2016, by-election, Olney emphasized that her lack of prior political involvement demonstrated accessibility for ordinary professionals to effect change.[9]

Political rise and elections

Joining the Liberal Democrats and initial candidacy

Sarah Olney joined the Liberal Democrats in May 2015, immediately following the party's heavy defeat in the May 2015 general election, in which it lost 49 of its 57 seats.[7] [17] Her decision stemmed from dissatisfaction with the election outcome and a personal commitment to liberal values, prompting her to seek greater involvement in political activism despite no prior party affiliation.[7] Over the subsequent 18 months, Olney engaged in local party activities in Richmond Park, building support within the constituency's Liberal Democrat branch amid growing local opposition to Brexit and the proposed expansion of Heathrow Airport.[18] This period marked her transition from professional life to grassroots politics, though she held no elected office beforehand. Her initial parliamentary candidacy emerged in October 2016, when Conservative MP Zac Goldsmith resigned on 25 October to trigger a by-election over the government's support for a third runway at Heathrow.[16] The Liberal Democrats selected Olney as their candidate on 30 October 2016, with local members voting her into the position despite her relative inexperience and the party's absence of a sitting MP in the seat since 2005.[16] [19] This selection positioned her as the party's standard-bearer in a high-profile contest against Goldsmith, who ran as an independent, capitalizing on the constituency's strong Remain vote in the June 2016 EU referendum.[15]

2016 by-election victory and 2017 defeat

Zac Goldsmith, the Conservative MP for Richmond Park, resigned on 25 October 2016 in protest against the government's decision to approve expansion at Heathrow Airport, which he had pledged to oppose during his 2015 campaign.[20][21] This triggered a by-election on 1 December 2016, with Goldsmith restanding as the Conservative candidate after abandoning plans to run independently.[22] The Liberal Democrats nominated Sarah Olney, a local mother and former investment banker who had joined the party only 18 months prior and lacked prior political experience.[7] The campaign pivoted heavily on Brexit, as Richmond Park had voted 72% to remain in the European Union in the June 2016 referendum, contrasting with Goldsmith's support for Leave.[23] Olney positioned the Liberal Democrats as defenders of pro-EU voters, calling for a second referendum on any Brexit deal and criticizing the Conservative government's approach, while Goldsmith emphasized local issues like Heathrow but faced accusations of inconsistency on his resignation pledge.[24] Analysts noted significant tactical voting, with up to a third of former Conservative Leave voters switching to Olney to block Goldsmith, alongside high mobilization of Remain supporters.[13] Olney secured victory with 20,170 votes to Goldsmith's 18,538, achieving a majority of 1,632 and overturning Goldsmith's 23,015-vote lead from the 2015 general election.[22] This represented the Liberal Democrats' first by-election gain from the Conservatives since 2008 and was hailed by party leader Tim Farron as evidence of resurgence against "hard Brexit," though turnout was lower at around 53% compared to national averages.[25] Olney took her seat in the House of Commons on 5 December 2016.[26] In the snap general election of 8 June 2017, called by Prime Minister Theresa May to strengthen her Brexit negotiating position, Goldsmith regained Richmond Park from Olney by just 45 votesâ€”23,298 to 23,253â€”yielding a 0.1% majority on a high turnout of 79.1% from an electorate of 80,025.[27][28] The razor-thin margin reflected the seat's volatility, with national Conservative gains offsetting local pro-EU sentiment, though Labour's candidate Christian Wolmar took 15% of the vote, potentially splitting anti-Conservative support.[29] Olney attributed the loss to the brevity of the campaign and broader electoral dynamics favoring the Conservatives, despite retaining a strong local base.[30]

2019 general election win and subsequent re-elections

In the 2019 United Kingdom general election held on 12 December, Sarah Olney was selected as the Liberal Democrats' candidate for Richmond Park, a constituency she had briefly represented following a 2016 by-election victory before losing it in 2017.[31] She secured 34,559 votes, achieving a 53.1% vote shareâ€”an increase of 8.0 percentage points from the party's previous performanceâ€”defeating the incumbent Conservative Zac Goldsmith who received 26,793 votes (41.2%).[32] This resulted in a majority of 7,766 votes for Olney, marking the Liberal Democrats' recapture of the seat from the Conservatives amid national trends favoring the latter party overall.[32] [33] Olney retained the seat in the 2024 United Kingdom general election on 4 July, polling 28,528 votes for a 54.6% share, an increase of 1.5 percentage points from 2019.[34] Her closest challenger, Conservative candidate Sara Gezdari, received 11,373 votes (21.8%), yielding a majority of 17,155â€”more than double her 2019 marginâ€”and reflecting a significant national swing toward the Liberal Democrats in suburban Remain-voting areas like Richmond Park.[34] Labour's Laura Coryton placed third with 5,048 votes (9.7%), underscoring Olney's strengthened local position.[34] No further general elections have occurred as of October 2025, with Olney continuing as MP.[31]

Parliamentary roles and activities

Committee memberships and shadow positions

Sarah Olney served on the Public Accounts Committee from 2 March 2020 to 30 May 2024, scrutinizing government spending and public sector performance as part of the House of Commons select committee system.[2] She continued involvement with public accounts oversight as a member of the Public Accounts Commission, which supervises the National Audit Office and the committee's operations.[35] In opposition roles within the Liberal Democrats, Olney held frontbench positions as spokesperson for Business and Industrial Strategy starting in January 2020, focusing on economic policy and enterprise issues.[2] She later became Liberal Democrat Spokesperson for the Treasury on 11 July 2022, addressing fiscal policy, taxation, and public finances until 2024.[2] From 18 September 2024, she served as Liberal Democrat Spokesperson for the Cabinet Office, handling scrutiny of government efficiency, procurement, and civil service matters, with her tenure extending into 2025.[2] These roles positioned her to challenge the government on accountability and resource allocation from the Liberal Democrat perspective.[36]

Legislative contributions and voting record

Sarah Olney has co-sponsored 18 private member's bills during her tenure, focusing on areas such as electoral reform, environmental standards, and social protections.[37] Notable examples include the Minimum Energy Performance of Buildings Bill (2023), which sought to enhance building energy efficiency standards; the Gender Pay Gap Information Bill (2022), aimed at reviewing the effectiveness of pay gap reporting requirements; and the Commercial Rent Transparency Bill (2022), which proposed prohibiting upward-only rent review clauses in commercial leases.[37] She also co-sponsored the Night Flights Prohibition Bill (2024), calling for a government report on banning night flights at airports, and the Public Order (Protection of Access to Abortion Services) Bill (2020), which sought to restrict anti-abortion protests near clinics.[37] A key legislative initiative was Olney's introduction of the Elections (Proportional Representation) Bill via a Ten Minute Rule motion on 3 December 2024, proposing the adoption of proportional representation for UK parliamentary elections and local government elections in England.[38] The motion passed its first reading by a narrow margin of 138 votes to 136, marking a symbolic victory for electoral reform advocates despite limited prospects for further progress.[39] Additionally, Olney campaigned successfully for free access to sentencing transcripts for survivors of rape and serious sexual offences, a policy implemented in 2025 following her advocacy since 2023.[40] She has participated actively in committees, including scrutiny of the Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill in 2025, contributing to debates on assisted dying provisions.[41] Olney's voting record demonstrates strong alignment with Liberal Democrat positions, voting with the party in 95% of 301 divisions over the past year.[42] Across her parliamentary career, she has participated in 320 divisions, rebelling against the party majority on 59 occasions.[37] She generally supported measures to address climate change, voting in favor on 10 occasions and against on 2 between 2020 and 2025.[42] Recent votes include aye on the Sentencing Bill's second reading and support for removing the two-child limit in child poverty strategies.[43] Her record reflects Liberal Democrat priorities, such as opposition to first-past-the-post systems and advocacy for enhanced worker protections, as seen in contributions to the Employment Rights Bill debates in 2025.

Key speeches and interventions

Sarah Olney delivered her maiden speech in the House of Commons on 19 December 2016 during the debate on "Exiting the European Union and Science and Research".[44] In it, she paid tribute to her predecessor Zac Goldsmith, emphasized the historical significance of Richmond Park as London's smallest constituency and an ancient royal deer park, and advocated for sustained UK-EU scientific collaboration to maintain research funding and innovation post-Brexit.[44] She highlighted local environmental concerns, including opposition to Heathrow's third runway, and stressed the constituency's diverse, educated population reliant on cross-border partnerships.[44] On 10 March 2021, Olney led a Westminster Hall debate on maternal mental health, focusing on the exacerbated risks during the perinatal period amid COVID-19 restrictions.[45] She cited data indicating that up to 20% of women experience perinatal mental health issues, with maternal suicide as the leading cause of direct maternal deaths, and criticized inadequate service provision, including long waiting times and regional disparities.[46] [47] Olney called for ring-fenced funding, specialist community teams in every area, and integration of mental health support into routine antenatal care to prevent long-term impacts on mothers and children.[47] In a Ten Minute Rule motion on 3 December 2024, Olney introduced the Elections (Proportional Representation) Bill, arguing that the first-past-the-post system distorts representation by awarding disproportionate seats to parties with limited vote shares, as evidenced by the 2024 election where Labour secured two-thirds of Commons seats with under 34% of the vote.[48] The bill passed narrowly by 138 votes to 136, marking a symbolic win for electoral reform advocates, though unlikely to progress without government support.[49] She proposed proportional representation for parliamentary and local elections to better reflect voter intent and reduce wasted votes, drawing on international examples like those in Scotland and Wales.[50] Olney has made significant interventions on the Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill in 2025, expressing concerns over safeguards for vulnerable groups. On 13 June 2025, she argued that palliative care and assisted dying could coexist but stressed the need for robust mental health assessments to prevent coercion or misapplication in cases like anorexia or depression.[51] She tabled amendments, including one in January 2025 replacing "capacity" with "ability" to ensure applicants fully comprehend consequences, aiming to close potential loopholes for those with eating disorders or learning disabilities.[52] Another amendment sought mandatory psychosocial interventions by independent panels to evaluate eligibility beyond terminal prognosis.[53] Olney opposed online harassment during the bill's scrutiny and highlighted frustrations with the Public Bill Committee's process, including limited time for amendments.[54][55]

Political positions and policy stances

Brexit and European Union relations

Olney first gained prominence in the 2016 Richmond Park by-election, triggered by Zac Goldsmith's resignation over Heathrow expansion but framed amid Brexit tensions in a constituency that voted 72% to remain in the EU referendum.[56] She campaigned as a Liberal Democrat candidate emphasizing opposition to a "hard Brexit," advocating for parliamentary scrutiny of exit terms and keeping options open to remain if beneficial for the country.[14] In post-election remarks, she described her victoryâ€”securing 20,510 votes and a 1,872 majorityâ€”as symbolic for Remain voters disillusioned with the government's direction, stating, "My fear is that there is nothing to scrutinise, that we head for the exit without leaving the door open to perhaps remaining if that turns out to be whatâ€™s best for the country."[14] [57] Throughout her parliamentary terms, Olney maintained a voting record aligned with Liberal Democrat opposition to Brexit implementation, consistently supporting measures to preserve UK EU membership where possible and rejecting withdrawal bills.[42] In her 2016-2017 tenure, she voted for EU membership in five divisions, reflecting party instructions against exit proceedings.[42] Following her 2019 re-election, she backed Liberal Democrat policy to revoke Article 50 and halt Brexit if the party formed government, consistent with the manifesto pledge to prioritize EU single market and customs union access over full withdrawal. Her maiden speech in December 2016 underscored Parliament's role in bridging Brexit divides, advocating constructive engagement rather than unilateral exit.[58] Post-Brexit, Olney has focused on mitigating withdrawal impacts and repairing UK-EU ties, criticizing the 2020 deal as a "complete disaster" for small businesses due to billions in lost exports and trade barriers.[59] In February 2025, during a Commons debate on UK-EU relations, she urged dropping government "red lines" for a new customs union to boost growth, noting the EU as the UK's largest trading partner and closest neighbor requiring pragmatic reset.[59] She has repeatedly called for a youth mobility scheme allowing 18- to 30-year-olds reciprocal work and study rights, arguing in a January 2025 Westminster Hall debate that it would address labor shortages in sectors like hospitality, generate Â£10,000+ annual fiscal contributions per participant, and signal commitment to broader cooperation amid a 30% drop in small business EU exports post-Brexit.[60] Olney expressed disappointment at the government's rejection of such schemes, citing two-thirds public support and EU insistence on their role in security and defense partnerships.[60] [59]

Environmental policy and transport infrastructure

Sarah Olney has served as the Liberal Democrats' spokesperson for energy, climate change, and transport, emphasizing decarbonization and sustainable infrastructure in her parliamentary contributions.[61][62] In a 2022 transport debate, she highlighted decarbonization as the primary challenge for the sector, advocating for expanded local transport options to reduce emissions while addressing community needs.[63] On aviation, Olney has consistently opposed Heathrow Airport expansion, including a third runway, citing unproven economic benefits and adverse local impacts on Richmond Park constituents.[64][65] She called for a full ban on night flights at Heathrow in 2025, demanding a government inquiry into their health effects, and criticized the airport's reliance on external funding for transport upgrades without sufficient mitigation.[66][67] In October 2025, during a Heathrow review debate, she reiterated opposition to expansion amid government support for it.[68] Regarding water infrastructure, Olney objected to Thames Water's proposed Teddington Direct River Sewer Pump in a public letter, arguing it would harm local environments and communities.[69] She supported Liberal Democrat calls to place Thames Water into special administration in March 2024, proposing reform as a public benefit company to address environmental failures like sewage discharges.[70] Olney has criticized reductions in bus services, noting a 30% rise in mid-journey terminations (1.5 million instances) revealed in her 2024 Freedom of Information request, which she linked to broader accessibility issues under the London Mayor.[71][72] She endorsed sustainable aviation fuel mandates in 2025 contributions, tying them to industrial strategy for emission reductions.[73] On structural projects, she highlighted inadequate pedestrian protections on Hammersmith Bridge and welcomed a Â£1 billion fund for bridges and tunnels announced in June 2025.[74][75] In climate debates, such as the 2020 Climate Change Assembly discussion, she advocated for pathways to net zero aligned with citizen assemblies.[76]

Domestic issues including security and foreign policy

Sarah Olney has advocated for increased UK defence spending in response to global instability, welcoming the government's commitment to raise it to 2.5% of GDP while urging further escalation to address threats comparable to those during the Cold War.[77][78] In a June 2025 debate on the National Security Strategy, she emphasized the need for enhanced resilience against transnational threats, including strong supply chains, immigration controls, and protections against online harms and foreign interference in domestic politics.[78] Olney has specifically highlighted concerns over China's national security law and its erosion of freedoms in Hong Kong, calling for measures to counter increasing transnational repression targeting UK-based individuals.[79] On foreign policy, Olney has supported robust UK alignment with allies against Russian aggression, participating in Liberal Democrat conference motions affirming solidarity with Ukraine and committing to sustained military and humanitarian aid.[80] Her parliamentary voting record indicates consistent support for measures promoting EU integration on security matters, reflecting a preference for multilateral frameworks over unilateral actions.[42] Regarding domestic security implications of migration, she has pushed for comprehensive reform of the UK's asylum system to balance efficient processing with adherence to international obligations, arguing that the current framework undermines credibility and public trust.[81] Olney has also addressed foreign influence in UK elections and donations, advocating for stricter transparency rules and regulatory enhancements to safeguard democratic processes from external interference, as outlined in Liberal Democrat policy positions she has endorsed.[82][83] These stances align with broader Liberal Democrat emphases on liberal internationalism, though critics within more conservative circles have questioned the party's historical reluctance to prioritize hard power deterrence.[42]

Achievements and criticisms

Notable successes and local impacts

Olney has advocated persistently for the reopening of Hammersmith Bridge, closed to vehicles since April 2019 due to structural issues, by organizing protests with local rowing groups, launching petitions urging the Department for Transport to release repair funds, and questioning ministers on funding commitments, thereby maintaining public and governmental focus on the issue affecting cross-river connectivity for Richmond Park residents.[84][85][86] In response to the closure of all three former police stations in the constituency amid budget cuts, Olney initiated a petition for a new facility in Richmond to enhance visible policing and community safety, while conducting surveys on the future of Royal Parks Police amid proposed reductions, highlighting risks to park security and influencing local discussions on resource allocation.[87][88][3] Her interventions on environmental and transport matters have addressed constituency-specific concerns, including securing a Westminster Hall debate on aircraft noise impacts from Heathrow Airport on November 14, 2024, where she emphasized effects on local communities, and opposing the third runway expansion, which she argued would exacerbate noise and air quality issues in Richmond Park despite government approval in January 2025.[89][64] Olney's casework and local engagements, such as meetings with constituents on Thames sewage pollution and youth priorities, have facilitated representation on issues like river abstraction proposals and policing cuts, contributing to sustained advocacy that pressures authorities even without immediate policy reversals.[90][3]

Criticisms of opportunism and policy consistency

Critics have accused Sarah Olney of opportunism during her 2016 Richmond Park by-election campaign, particularly in misrepresenting her opponent Zac Goldsmith's longstanding opposition to Heathrow's third runway expansion as support for it, allegedly to appeal to local voters concerned about environmental and noise impacts.[91] Similar claims arose in the 2019 general election, where opponents alleged she repeated distortions of Goldsmith's position despite his consistent anti-expansion stance since 2015.[91] These tactics were portrayed by detractors as selective targeting of a winnable Remain-voting seat, with Olney having joined the Liberal Democrats only a year prior and lacking prior political experience in the area.[92] On policy consistency, Olney faced internal party criticism in 2021 for advertising an unpaid internship in her office, contravening Liberal Democrat policy adopted in 2019 mandating paid roles to promote fairness and access.[93] She defended the posting by citing a "maxed out" staffing budget, but activists highlighted it as hypocritical given her advocacy for equitable employment practices as a business spokesperson.[93] Allegations of inconsistent adherence to electoral rules further fueled perceptions of opportunism; in 2021, Olney was reported to police alongside fellow Liberal Democrat MP Wera Hobhouse over 2019 campaign spending returns that allegedly underdeclared local expenses by Â£2,849 by reallocating costs to national party accounts, prompting a fraud probe though no charges resulted.[94] Opponents, including LibDemWatch, framed this as sharp practice to maximize local impact while evading limits, contrasting her public emphasis on transparency.[95] Such incidents, while not leading to sanctions, were cited by conservative-leaning critics as evidence of prioritizing electoral advantage over strict policy or procedural fidelity.

Evaluations of Liberal Democrat affiliations

Sarah Olney's parliamentary record demonstrates strong adherence to Liberal Democrat positions, with a voting alignment score of 95% alongside other party MPs across more than 290 divisions in the preceding year. This consistency spans core party priorities, including opposition to increases in the state pension age, support for environmental measures, and advocacy for rejoining the European single market.[42] Her integration into party structures underscores this affiliation, as evidenced by appointments as Liberal Democrat spokesperson for the Treasury from 2019 to 2020 and for the Cabinet Office from 2020 onward. In these capacities, Olney has advanced party initiatives such as proportional representation for elections and enhanced scrutiny of executive fiscal decisions, aligning with the Liberal Democrats' emphasis on liberal economic reforms and democratic accountability.[2] On conscience matters without a party whip, Olney has occasionally diverged from the majority of her colleagues, voting against the second reading of the Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill on 29 November 2024â€”a stance shared by 11 other Liberal Democrat MPs amid broader party support for the measure. This position, articulated in committee scrutiny, emphasized insufficient safeguards against coercion, reflecting potential responsiveness to Richmond Park's demographic mix rather than ideological rupture with Liberal Democrat principles.[96][97]

Personal life

Family and relationships

Sarah Olney is married to Benjamin Olney, a town planner who works as a manager for High Speed 1.[7][8] The couple live in North Kingston with their two children: daughter Isabel and son Rufus.[7][98] In 2016, Isabel was aged seven and Rufus three, and by 2020 the children were reported as aged ten and seven, respectively.[99]

Public persona and interests

Sarah Olney presents a public persona emphasizing her identity as a working mother navigating the demands of parliamentary life alongside family responsibilities. In a March 2017 statement ahead of International Women's Day, she highlighted the intensity of motherhood, asserting that routine childcare duties exceed the rigors of her prior accountancy profession in challenge and reward.[100] Olney promotes physical wellbeing and community health initiatives, featuring resources like the NHS Couch to 5K running program on her constituency website to assist residents in adopting active lifestyles.[101] Her engagements extend to supporting local charities, exemplified by her March 2025 visit to Fight for Sight's rebranded boutique in Richmond, where she endorsed the organization's work in funding vision research and highlighted the importance of community-driven philanthropy.[102]
