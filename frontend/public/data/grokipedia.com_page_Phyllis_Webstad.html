Phyllis Webstad

Phyllis Webstad is a Secwépemc residential school survivor and activist of mixed Indigenous and European heritage who inspired the annual observance of Orange Shirt Day.[1] Born in Dog Creek, British Columbia, she attended St. Joseph's Mission Residential School starting at age six in 1973, where staff confiscated a new orange shirt given to her by her grandmother, an event she later recounted as emblematic of the cultural erasure experienced by many Indigenous children in such institutions.[1] In 2013, Webstad shared this personal experience at a commemoration event for the Williams Lake school, prompting attendees to wear orange shirts in solidarity and giving rise to the grassroots movement now observed nationally on September 30 to honor residential school survivors and promote awareness of their intergenerational impacts.[1] Webstad has since authored The Orange Shirt Story, established the Orange Shirt Society to support survivors and educate on reconciliation, and become a public speaker advocating for Indigenous healing, though the initiative's expansion has coincided with heightened scrutiny over the veracity of some associated historical claims, such as unexcavated "mass graves" reported in mainstream outlets but lacking forensic confirmation.[2][1]

Early Life and Background

Family and Heritage

Phyllis Webstad, born Phyllis Jack on July 13, 1967, in Dog Creek, British Columbia, derives her heritage from the Northern Secwepemc (Shuswap) people of the Stswecem'c Xgat'tem First Nation, also known as the Canoe Creek Indian Band.[1][3] Her ancestry includes mixed Secwepemc roots alongside Irish and French elements, reflecting intermarriages common in the region.[1] Her mother, Rose Wilson, delivered her at the family home on the Dog Creek Indian Reserve, a rural community approximately 85 kilometers south of Williams Lake.[4] Webstad's early childhood, prior to age six, unfolded on the Dog Creek Reserve under the primary care of her maternal grandmother, Lena Jack, in a modest household characterized by limited financial resources.[1][4] The reserve setting provided a close-knit Secwepemc community environment, where traditional familial bonds emphasized extended family support amid economic constraints, including the absence of modern amenities like running water in some homes.[1] This period shaped her initial cultural immersion in Secwepemc practices and reserve life, influenced by her grandmother's role as a central caregiver and her mother's presence in the household.[5]

Residential School Experience


Phyllis Webstad enrolled in St. Joseph's Mission Residential School near Williams Lake, British Columbia, for the 1973-1974 school year at the age of six. Prior to this, she resided with her grandmother on the Dog Creek Indian Reserve, part of the Stswecem'c Xgat'tem First Nation. Her grandmother had purchased a new shiny orange shirt with a laced front specifically for Webstad's first day at the school.[1][1] Upon arrival, school staff stripped Webstad of her personal clothing, confiscating the orange shirt, which she never saw or wore again. This immediate separation from her belongings symbolized to her a disregard for her individuality and feelings. Webstad has described feeling worthless and uncared for as a result, with the color orange thereafter evoking those initial emotions of insignificance.[1][1] Throughout the year, Webstad adhered to regimented daily routines enforced by the school, including making her bed each morning, using the bathroom only at scheduled times, and consuming all provided meals irrespective of taste preferences. These conditions marked her enforced adaptation to institutional life away from family. She attended for this single school year only before returning to live with her family.[1][1]

Professional and Educational Background

Formal Education

After leaving residential school, Phyllis Webstad pursued post-secondary education as an adult, obtaining a diploma in Business Administration from the Nicola Valley Institute of Technology.[1] She subsequently earned a diploma in Accounting from Thompson Rivers University in Kamloops, British Columbia.[1] These programs equipped her with administrative and financial skills relevant to her advocacy and organizational leadership in Indigenous community initiatives.[1]

Career Trajectory

Phyllis Webstad obtained a diploma in Business Administration from Nicola Valley Institute of Technology in the early 2000s, followed by a diploma in Accounting from Thompson Rivers University.[6] Prior to establishing Orange Shirt Day in 2013, she held positions in child welfare, early childhood education, and community development, fields aligned with her educational background and community involvement in British Columbia.[6] Specific timelines for these roles remain undocumented in public records, but they reflect her pre-activism professional focus on supporting Indigenous and family-oriented services.[6]

Activism and Advocacy

Founding of Orange Shirt Day

Orange Shirt Day originated from Phyllis Webstad's decision to publicly share her residential school experience during a commemoration event for the St. Joseph's Mission Residential School in Williams Lake, British Columbia, in 2013.[7][8] The initiative stemmed from discussions among survivors and community members planning the event, where Webstad recounted the confiscation of her new orange shirt upon arrival at the school in 1973, symbolizing the broader stripping of Indigenous children's cultural identity and personal belongings.[3] This personal anecdote, drawn from her entry into the St. Joseph's Mission at age six, served as the catalyst for encouraging widespread wearing of orange shirts to represent lost innocence and resilience among survivors.[1] The symbolism of the orange shirt directly mirrored Webstad's 1973 experience, when her grandmother purchased the garment as a proud new outfit for her first day of school, only for it to be removed by school authorities, leaving her feeling devalued and disconnected from her heritage.[1] Webstad later explained that the shirt's removal exemplified how residential schools systematically eroded Indigenous children's sense of self and family ties, a theme she emphasized to make the abstract harms of the system tangible through a simple, relatable object.[3] Webstad's primary motivation was to foster survivor-led storytelling, enabling others to voice their traumas without repeated institutional retellings, as she stated: "I am honored to be able to tell my story so that others may benefit and understand, and maybe other survivors will feel comfortable enough to share their stories."[1] Initial grassroots adoption occurred locally, with the first observance on September 30, 2013—chosen because it aligned with the typical return-to-school date when children were often taken—drawing community participation in Williams Lake, where participants donned orange attire to honor affected families.[3][8] This event marked an organic beginning, rooted in Webstad's direct efforts to educate through personal narrative rather than formal campaigns.[9]

Establishment of Orange Shirt Society

The Orange Shirt Society was established in 2015 as a non-profit organization in Williams Lake, British Columbia, by Phyllis Webstad to institutionalize and expand the Orange Shirt Day initiative that originated locally in 2013.[10][11] The society serves as a formal entity to manage administrative aspects of the movement, including resource allocation for awareness campaigns on the intergenerational effects of Indian Residential Schools.[10] Webstad founded the society and continues to act as its ambassador, overseeing its operations alongside a board and staff comprising both Indigenous and non-Indigenous members.[1][10] Primary activities include coordinating community events, distributing educational resources, and handling merchandise such as orange shirts and jerseys, with proceeds supporting ongoing programs like the Orange Shirt Legacy Fund.[10][12] In recent years, the society has sustained these efforts through initiatives like annual orange shirt design contests—such as the 2025 contest—and year-round reconciliation support, including speaker bureaus and activity kits for schools and communities.[12] The organization also trademarked the phrase "Every Child Matters" to protect its branding and messaging.[11]

Key Speaking Engagements and Public Roles

Webstad holds the positions of chief executive officer and ambassador for the Orange Shirt Society, through which she coordinates public outreach and travels across Canada to deliver presentations on residential school survivorship and reconciliation efforts.[1][11] Her verified speaking engagements from 2013 onward encompass keynotes and addresses at conferences, educational gatherings, and commemorative events, often centered on recounting her personal experiences to foster awareness. Notable examples include her address at the inaugural Orange Shirt Day event in Williams Lake, British Columbia, on September 30, 2013, where she first publicly shared the story of her confiscated orange shirt.[13]
  • In 2024, she delivered the keynote "The Orange Shirt Story" at the PDTCA conference, detailing her pre-residential school life and the loss of cultural items upon entry.[14]
  • Also in 2024, Webstad presented a keynote at the AFP ICON conference, emphasizing the importance of confronting residential school denialism through personal testimony.[15]
  • In late April to early May 2025, she spoke at the National Youth to Youth program hosted by Honouring Indigenous Peoples at Acadia University in Wolfville, Nova Scotia, engaging youth delegates on intergenerational healing.[16][17]
  • On May 2, 2025, during the British Columbia Historical Federation (BCHF) conference in Williams Lake (May 1–4), she provided the keynote address, discussing the historical "apocalypse" of the past 150 years and persistent denialism regarding residential schools.[16]
  • In May 2025, Webstad appeared as a speaker at the Indigenous History and Heritage Conference in Winnipeg, Manitoba.[18]
  • Later in 2025, she served as a keynote speaker at the National Gathering for Indigenous Education in Winnipeg, organized by Indspire.[19]
  • On September 24, 2025, she addressed attendees at the "A Quilt for Every Story" gala in Timmins, Ontario, honoring the Orange Shirt Society's work.[20]
These engagements reflect her commitment to direct testimony, with full-time speaking commencing around 2019.[16]

Role in National Day for Truth and Reconciliation

Origins and Development

Phyllis Webstad shared her residential school experience, including the confiscation of her new orange shirt, during a Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) community event in Williams Lake, British Columbia, on May 31, 2013.[1] This testimony, delivered as a survivor representative, highlighted the cultural erasure and personal losses inflicted on Indigenous children, resonating with attendees and prompting immediate local action.[15] The event's impact extended beyond the gathering, as Webstad's narrative fueled broader discussions on reconciliation, indirectly supporting the TRC's Calls to Action, including Call 80 for a national day of commemoration.[21] The momentum from Webstad's 2013 testimony and subsequent advocacy contributed to the evolution of September 30 observances into formalized recognitions. By amplifying survivor voices through speaking engagements and the Orange Shirt Society, Webstad's efforts aligned with growing demands for statutory acknowledgment of residential school legacies, bridging grassroots awareness to policy considerations.[22] This indirect influence manifested in provincial and territorial adoptions of the date prior to federal action, such as the Northwest Territories designating it a statutory holiday in 2017 to honor survivors.[23] These provincial precedents underscored the expanding reach of Webstad's story-driven initiative, fostering a foundation for national-level dialogue on truth and reconciliation without prescribing the specific holiday structure.[24] Her focus on personal testimony over direct policy advocacy ensured the emphasis remained on empirical survivor experiences, aiding the organic development toward broader institutional responses.[25]

Broader Adoption and Federal Recognition

In June 2021, the Parliament of Canada passed Bill C-5, amending the Canada Labour Code to designate September 30 as the National Day for Truth and Reconciliation, a statutory holiday for federal government employees and workers in federally regulated private-sector industries.[26] The legislation took effect in August 2021, with the first observance on September 30, 2021, honoring the lost children and survivors of residential schools.[23] This federal recognition built directly on Orange Shirt Day, which Webstad founded in 2013, by aligning the holiday with its annual date to amplify its symbolism of children arriving at residential schools.[27] Webstad has been acknowledged for her foundational role in this evolution, as her personal story of having her orange shirt confiscated upon entering St. Joseph's Mission Residential School in 1973 inspired the grassroots movement that pressured for national commemoration.[1] She has described the day's purpose as centered on sharing truths about residential school harms to enable reconciliation, stating, “In order for the world to be different, people need to know our truths, and truth comes before reconciliation.”[15] This emphasis on education about the systemic impacts on Indigenous children underscores the holiday's intent to foster public awareness beyond federal workplaces.[28] Following federal adoption, several provinces extended statutory holiday status to their public- and private-sector workers, including British Columbia via Bill 2 in March 2023, Prince Edward Island, Manitoba, the Northwest Territories, and Yukon.[23] These expansions reflect the holiday's growing integration into provincial labor codes, though implementation varies, with not all jurisdictions mandating time off for all employees.[29] Webstad's advocacy through the Orange Shirt Society has supported this broader uptake by promoting consistent observances tied to residential school history.[30]

Literary and Media Works

Published Books

Phyllis Webstad has authored a series of children's picture books published by Medicine Wheel Publishing, primarily drawing from her personal experiences as a survivor of the Canadian Indian residential school system and the origins of Orange Shirt Day. These works aim to educate young readers on themes of cultural loss, remembrance, and reconciliation through autobiographical narratives.[2] Her debut book, The Orange Shirt Story (2018), recounts Webstad's arrival at St. Joseph's Mission residential school in Williams Lake, British Columbia, at age six in 1973, where school authorities immediately confiscated her new orange shirt—purchased for the occasion by her grandmother—highlighting the immediate stripping of personal identity and Indigenous belongings. Illustrated by Brock Nicol, the book targets school-age children to foster awareness of residential school impacts.[31][32] Phyllis's Orange Shirt (2019), also illustrated by Brock Nicol, adapts the core narrative of the 2018 title for preschool audiences, simplifying the account of the shirt's loss to emphasize emotional resilience and cultural pride for very young readers.[33] In With Our Orange Hearts (2022), Webstad extends the theme to collective healing, incorporating stories from family and community members affected by residential schools, using the metaphor of orange hearts to symbolize ongoing remembrance and unity among survivors. Aimed at elementary-aged children, it builds on autobiographical elements to promote intergenerational dialogue.[33] Every Child Matters (2023) focuses on affirming the value of Indigenous children's experiences amid historical traumas, directly tying Webstad's story to the national discourse on unmarked graves and residential school legacies, with illustrations reinforcing messages of individual significance. Designed for children aged 4–8, it serves as an educational tool for truth and reconciliation.[34] Webstad's most recent title, Today Is Orange Shirt Day (2024), a board book illustrated by Natassia Davies, introduces toddlers to the annual September 30 observance through simple text and visuals depicting orange shirts as symbols of awareness, rooted in her foundational shirt narrative.[35][36]

Film and Other Adaptations

Returning Home, a 2021 documentary directed by Sean Stiller, centers on Phyllis Webstad's life as a residential school survivor and founder of Orange Shirt Day, interweaving her personal narrative with broader survivor stories and the ecological challenges facing her Secwepemc community, including salmon decline.[37][38] The film documents Webstad's national speaking tour and received an 8.5/10 rating on IMDb based on viewer assessments.[37] In September 2025, Phyllis Webstad's Orange Shirt Story, a one-hour documentary, premiered in Kamloops, British Columbia, featuring interviews with Webstad that detail her residential school experiences and the genesis of the Orange Shirt Day movement without aiming to re-traumatize participants.[39][40] Produced to educate audiences on the movement's origins, it screened through the Kamloops Film Society and emphasizes forward-looking pathways informed by Webstad's testimony.[41][39]

Awards and Recognitions

Major Honors Received

In 2017, Phyllis Webstad was awarded the Distinguished Alumni Award by Thompson Rivers University, recognizing her role as an accounting technician alumna and her foundational efforts in initiating Orange Shirt Day to highlight residential school survivor experiences.[42][43]

Recent Developments (Post-2020)

In January 2025, Phyllis Webstad received the 2024 British Columbia Reconciliation Award from the Office of the Lieutenant Governor, honoring her lifelong commitment to advancing reconciliation through the Orange Shirt Day movement and residential school survivor advocacy.[44][45] In recognition of her influence, the BC Achievement Foundation established the Phyllis Webstad Emerging Leader Designation as a new category within the Reconciliation Award program, aimed at young individuals demonstrating leadership in reconciliation efforts.[46] Throughout 2025, Webstad continued her role as CEO and founding ambassador of the Orange Shirt Society, promoting awareness of residential school intergenerational impacts via educational resources and public engagements.[12] In September 2025, she announced a large-scale Every Child Matters graduation event scheduled for spring 2026 at BC Place in Vancouver, targeting high school students to foster ongoing dialogue on truth and reconciliation.[47] Webstad delivered keynote addresses in 2025, including at Indspire's National Gathering for Indigenous Education in Winnipeg, where she shared her residential school experiences to support educational initiatives aligned with the Truth and Reconciliation Commission's calls to action.[19] These activities underscored her sustained influence amid heightened national focus on Indigenous history following the 2021 discovery of unmarked graves at former residential school sites.[48]

Reception and Impact

Achievements and Positive Contributions

Phyllis Webstad's founding of Orange Shirt Day in 2013 has led to its annual observance across Canadian schools and communities, fostering widespread educational engagement on residential school histories. By 2021, the initiative aligned with federal recognition as the National Day for Truth and Reconciliation, prompting Canadian Heritage to fund 340 related events and gatherings nationwide.[24] This expansion includes tailored resources for elementary, junior, and high school curricula, enabling teachers to integrate survivor narratives into lessons on reconciliation.[12] Webstad's personal storytelling, amplified through tours and the Orange Shirt Society, has encouraged intergenerational dialogue by prompting other survivors to share experiences, thereby building community healing networks. Her efforts have produced six books detailing family and individual residential school impacts, distributed to educate broader audiences on cultural loss and resilience.[15] These works support public speaking engagements that emphasize "Every Child Matters," contributing to anti-bullying and anti-racism discussions in educational settings.[1] The measurable adoption of Orange Shirt Day symbols, such as orange attire in community events and sports initiatives like the Orange Jersey Project, reflects heightened awareness of residential school intergenerational effects, with participation extending beyond Canada to international Indigenous contexts.[12]

Criticisms and Controversies

Critics of the Orange Shirt Day movement, initiated by Phyllis Webstad, have questioned the uniformity of the victimhood framing it promotes, arguing that residential school experiences varied widely and included positive outcomes such as education, vocational training, and escape from reserve hardships. For instance, anthropological analyses highlight that while abuses occurred, historical records show many students received benefits like literacy skills and health improvements, with pre-1960s complaint rates low relative to enrollment, suggesting non-systemic issues rather than inherent genocidal intent.[49] Webstad's personal account of her orange shirt's confiscation and hair-cutting at St. Joseph's Mission in 1973 has been scrutinized for inconsistencies, such as her later vagueness on staff identities (shifting from nuns to unspecified "they") and admission of no memory of the shirt's return, alongside evidence that such practices were often hygiene measures for lice-infested arrivals from impoverished reserves.[50] Moreover, the institution was classified as a hostel by 1969 federal policy, not a full residential school, complicating retrospective abuse categorizations.[50] Empirical challenges to mass trauma claims underpinning the movement include the absence of verified mass graves or evidence of widespread child deaths beyond normal historical mortality rates from diseases like tuberculosis, which affected all Canadian populations. Ground-penetrating radar detections at sites like Kamloops in 2021 were initially reported as graves but remain unexcavated anomalies, with no bodies exhumed to confirm genocide narratives amplified by media and political figures.[51] Critics, including political scientist Tom Flanagan, contend that such unverified stories fueled by confirmation bias in mainstream outlets—often aligned with institutional left-leaning tendencies—distort causal realities, ignoring data on voluntary attendance in later decades and Indigenous parental requests for schooling to counter on-reserve neglect and violence.[51] The emphasis on perpetual victimhood in initiatives like Orange Shirt Day has drawn rebuke for potentially undermining Indigenous agency and integration, as it prioritizes historical grievance over causal factors like internal community governance and cultural barriers to economic participation. Anthropologists note that this narrative, while emotionally resonant, correlates with stalled advancement, as seen in critiques of native leadership overlooking residential schools' role in producing professionals and fostering self-reliance.[52] Reconciliation efforts symbolized by September 30 observances have faced scrutiny for yielding minimal practical gains in self-sufficiency, with Indigenous on-reserve median incomes at $28,000 in 2020 versus $41,000 nationally, and poverty rates over 40% despite trillions in transfers since 1960. Government data confirm persistent gaps in labor force participation (59% for First Nations on-reserve vs. 65% overall) and housing adequacy, attributing stagnation partly to symbolic policies that sideline market-driven reforms like resource development for revenue generation.[53] Such outcomes suggest that annual rituals, while raising awareness, may entrench dependency without addressing root causes like reserve isolation and welfare disincentives to entrepreneurship.[54]

Empirical Context of Residential Schools

The Canadian Indian residential school system, established under federal policy from the 1880s until the closure of the last school in 1996, encompassed approximately 150 institutions operated primarily by Christian churches with government funding.[55] An estimated 150,000 Indigenous children attended these schools over the century, with enrollment peaking in the early 20th century before declining; by 1950, only about 32% of school-age status Indian children were enrolled, reflecting increased parental choice and alternatives like day schools in later decades.[56] The system's core objective was assimilation, aiming to educate children in Euro-Canadian norms, languages, and skills while discouraging Indigenous cultural practices, though attendance was not uniformly compulsory and many families sought the limited educational opportunities available.[57] While the schools provided basic literacy and numeracy instruction absent in many remote communities, outcomes varied; some attendees gained vocational training in trades such as sewing, carpentry, and agriculture, facilitating later employment and economic independence for a subset of graduates.[58] Documented abuses included corporal punishment—common in educational institutions of the era—and enforced cultural suppression through language bans and separation from families, though these were not consistent across all schools or periods, with experiences differing by institution, administrator, and decade.[59] Mortality rates were notably high, with over 4,000 documented deaths primarily from infectious diseases like tuberculosis and influenza, exacerbated by overcrowding, malnutrition, and the children's pre-existing vulnerabilities from reserve conditions; these rates, while tragic, aligned with broader historical patterns of child mortality in impoverished rural populations rather than evidencing intentional killing.[60] Recent ground-penetrating radar surveys at former school sites have detected soil anomalies suggestive of unmarked individual burials, but subsequent limited excavations have yielded no confirmation of mass graves or foul play, prompting critiques that framings of "genocide" overstate the assimilative policy's intent and overlook empirical evidence of disease-driven losses without systematic extermination.[51]
