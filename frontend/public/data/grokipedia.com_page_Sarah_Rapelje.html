Sarah Rapelje

Sarah Rapelje (June 9, 1625 – April 1685) was the first female child of European parentage born in the Dutch colony of New Netherland, in what is now New York.[1][2] Born at Fort Orange (present-day Albany) to Walloon settlers Joris Jansen Rapelje and Catalina Trico, who had arrived the previous year aboard the Eendracht, she grew up in the early colonial settlement amid interactions with Indigenous peoples and Dutch traders.[3][4] In 1639, she married Hans Hansen Bergen, a farmer from Bergen, Norway, with whom she had eight children before his death around 1654; she subsequently wed Tunis Gysbert Bogardus, producing five more offspring and managing family properties in Wallabout (now Brooklyn).[3][5] Rapelje's extensive progeny, estimated to number over a million descendants by the 20th century, underscores her role as a matriarch in the foundational families of New York, with notable kin including actor Humphrey Bogart.[6][4]

Family Origins

Parents and Ancestry

Joris Jansen Rapelje, Sarah Rapelje's father, was a French Huguenot born on April 28, 1604, in Valenciennes, a town in the Spanish Netherlands (modern northern France) known as a Protestant stronghold amid Catholic dominance. As a refugee from religious persecution against Calvinists, he fled to the Dutch Republic, where he worked as a textile weaver before his emigration.[7][8] Catalyntje Trico (also recorded as Jeronimus or Catalina), Sarah's mother, was a Walloon Calvinist born circa 1605, with origins traced to Paris or the nearby Picardy region in France, areas of French Protestant communities facing suppression. Like many Walloons—French-speaking Reformed believers from the southern Low Countries—she escaped persecution by relocating to Amsterdam with her sister, joining other Protestant exiles in the tolerant Dutch Calvinist environment.[9][10] Joris and Catalyntje married on January 21, 1624, at Amsterdam's Walloon Church, a Reformed congregation serving French-speaking Protestant refugees, reflecting their shared ethnic French roots and commitment to Calvinist faith amid displacement.[11]

Arrival in New Netherland

Joris Jansen Rapalje and Catalyntje Trico, both of Walloon Protestant background residing in Amsterdam, married on January 21, 1624, at the city's Walloon Church shortly before departing for New Netherland.[12][13] As part of the Dutch West India Company's initiative to populate the colony with settlers, they joined approximately 30 Walloon families recruited for permanent agricultural and trading settlement.[14][15] The couple sailed aboard the Eendracht (Unity), which departed from Texel near Amsterdam on January 25, 1624.[16] The Eendracht reached the Hudson River in spring 1624, with settlers disembarking to establish Fort Orange (present-day Albany) as a fur trading post and farming outpost.[17][18] Joris, previously a textile worker, took employment with the Company to support construction and operational needs of the fledgling outpost.[19] Catalyntje handled domestic management, vital for sustaining family units in the harsh colonial environment where interactions with Native American groups facilitated initial trade in furs and provisions.[20] These efforts laid groundwork for colony viability without immediate large-scale expansion.[21]

Birth and Early Years

Birth in Fort Orange

Sarah Jorise Rapelje, daughter of Joris Jansen Rapelje and Catalyntje Trico, was born on June 9, 1625, at Fort Orange, a Dutch trading post in the colony of New Netherland (present-day Albany, New York).[3][22] This date, drawn from family records and colonial genealogies, aligns with the timing of her baptism in the Dutch Reformed tradition, which typically occurred shortly after birth in remote outposts lacking formal vital registries.[22] Historical accounts designate Rapelje as the first child of European Christian parentage born in New Netherland, preceding any other documented births of European descent in the region, as the colony's permanent settlement began in 1624 with limited families.[1][23] Fort Orange itself was a primitive enclosure of log palisades and basic structures, positioned strategically five miles south of the Mohawk River's confluence with the Hudson to monopolize the upstream fur trade, which by 1626 yielded over 8,000 beaver pelts annually through exchanges with Mohawk and Mahican tribes.[24][25] The outpost's isolation amplified mortality risks for infants and settlers, including exposure to endemic diseases, nutritional deficiencies, and intermittent hostilities, though no specific records detail Rapelje's immediate survival circumstances beyond her recorded vitality into adulthood.[24]

Childhood in Colonial New Netherland

Shortly after her birth on 9 June 1625 in Fort Orange, Sarah Rapelje's family relocated southward to New Amsterdam in late 1626, following orders from the Dutch West India Company to consolidate and populate the burgeoning southern settlement at the tip of Manhattan Island. This move positioned the young Sarah in a frontier trading outpost of roughly 270 inhabitants, comprising Dutch, Walloon, and enslaved African residents amid rudimentary fortifications and wooden structures. The Rapeljes received a bouwery—a farm lot—for cultivation, reflecting the company's emphasis on agricultural self-sufficiency to support fur trade operations.[20][8] Sarah's early years unfolded in a Walloon-Dutch household where her father, Joris Jansen Rapelje, engaged in farming and small-scale crafting, such as tobacco pipe-making, to sustain the family amid scarce resources and seasonal hardships like harsh winters and crop failures. Her mother, Catalyntje Trico, managed domestic production, including textile work and food preservation, typical of Calvinist settler women who contributed to community resilience without formal division of labor. Education for children like Sarah was informal and religiously oriented, centered on Calvinist catechism, basic literacy from the Bible, and practical skills for colonial survival, imparted at home due to the absence of dedicated schools until later decades.[26][13] As part of a multi-ethnic hub reliant on commerce, Sarah encountered Lenape indigenous traders exchanging beaver pelts for European goods, fostering early intercultural exchanges that shaped the colony's economy but also exposed settlers to risks from sporadic conflicts and diseases. Daily life involved communal activities—children playing in unpaved streets, assisting with livestock, and navigating a landscape of mud flats, forests, and waterways—instilling adaptability in the volatile environment of New Netherland's expansion.[27][28]

Adult Life and Family

First Marriage and Children

Sarah Rapelje married Hans Hansen Bergen, a ship carpenter originally from Bergen, Norway, who had emigrated to New Netherland circa 1633, around 1639 in New Amsterdam when she was approximately 14 years old.[29] Bergen, known also as Hans Hansen Noorman, initially worked in tobacco cultivation and milling before transitioning to farming.[30] [29] The couple had eight children baptized in the New Amsterdam Dutch Reformed Church between 1640 and 1653: Anneken (baptized 22 July 1640), Brechtje (27 July 1642), Jan (10 January 1644), Hans (29 September 1647), Catalyntje (14 February 1649), Gerret (20 February 1651), and Michiel (20 April 1653), with one additional child whose records indicate early mortality.[31] Seven of these children survived to adulthood, a notably high rate given the era's typical infant mortality exceeding 20-30% in colonial settlements due to disease and harsh conditions.[29] [5] By the early 1640s, the family relocated from Manhattan to Wallabout (near present-day Brooklyn), where Bergen acquired farmland for agricultural pursuits, including grain and livestock, contributing to the demographic expansion of Dutch settlements amid rising frictions with English colonists over territorial claims.[29] This move aligned with broader patterns of New Netherland families seeking arable land on Long Island amid the colony's population growth from under 1,000 in 1640 to over 9,000 by 1664.[32]

Widowhood and Remarriage

Hans Hansen Bergen died in early 1654, likely from unspecified illness or accident common in colonial settings, leaving Sarah Rapelje, aged about 29, as a widow responsible for their farm in the Wallabout section of Brooklyn and eight children ranging from infants to preteens.[33][31] In the immediate aftermath, Rapelje demonstrated practical autonomy by selling a lot on Pearl Street in New Amsterdam on May 30, 1654, a transaction that underscores her role in liquidating assets to sustain the household amid the challenges of frontier agriculture and childcare.[31] Dutch customary law in New Netherland granted widows usufruct rights over marital property, enabling them to manage estates, act as guardians for minors, and retain economic control until heirs came of age, which positioned Rapelje to preserve family holdings rather than face destitution.[34] This framework, rooted in Roman-Dutch traditions adapted to colonial needs, encouraged remarriage as a strategy for stability, allowing incoming spouses to contribute labor and protection without supplanting the widow's inheritance claims. Rapelje's circumstances aligned with this system, as her land patent—stemming from Bergen's earlier grants—remained intact for family use. On August 19, 1654, Rapelje remarried Teunis Gysbertse Bogaert (c. 1625–1699), a Dutch immigrant from Heicop who had arrived in the colony around 1652, in a union motivated by the era's imperatives for shared labor and defense in an unstable settlement.[6] Bogaert integrated into her operations, eventually overseeing the 400-acre patent after the English takeover in 1664, though Rapelje's prior rights ensured continuity for her progeny.[22] The marriage yielded seven more children, expanding her lineage while leveraging Bogaert's skills in farming and community affairs for household resilience.[22]

Total Progeny and Family Dynamics

Sarah Rapelje produced a total of 14 children across her two marriages, comprising seven with her first husband, Hans Hansen Bergen, and seven with her second husband, Teunis Gysbertse Bogaert.[22] [35] This reproductive output aligned with patterns of high fertility in early New Netherland settlements, where women commonly bore 10 or more children to offset infant mortality rates exceeding 20-30% and to provide labor for agrarian households.[6] Of her children with Bergen, seven reached adulthood despite the loss of the youngest in infancy, underscoring resilience in family survival amid colonial hardships such as disease and resource scarcity.[6] [1] Her households exemplified economic self-sufficiency through diversified activities, including farming in the fertile Wallabout region and trade facilitated by Bergen's initial shipbuilding skills, which transitioned to land-based sustenance as the family expanded.[23] [5] As matriarch, Rapelje managed these operations, integrating stepchildren and offspring into communal labor structures typical of Dutch settler families, which emphasized patrilineal inheritance but relied on maternal oversight for daily provisioning and child-rearing.[22] Family dynamics centered on affiliation with the Dutch Reformed Church, where baptisms and communal rites reinforced kinship ties, and participation in local defense efforts, including burgher militias during the 1664 English transition, with adult sons likely contributing to household protection and civic obligations.[1] [3] This structure reflected adaptive strategies for stability in a frontier environment, prioritizing collective resource pooling over individualistic pursuits.[6]

Later Life and Death

Residence and Activities in Bushwick

Sarah Rapelje resided in Boswijck, the Dutch village that evolved into modern Bushwick in Brooklyn, during the final decades of her life, integrating into a rural community amid the transition from Dutch to English colonial administration.[1] By the 1660s, following the death of her first husband Hans Hansen Bergen around 1654, she had remarried Teunis Gysbertse Bogaert and relocated within Brooklyn's expanding settlements, where family lands supported agricultural pursuits typical of the era's Dutch farming households.[4] Her property holdings derived primarily from inheritance tied to her father's 1637 purchase of 167 morgens (approximately 335 acres) from indigenous groups in what became Brooklyn, portions of which passed to descendants amid the patroon system's land distribution. While a traditional account attributes to her a specific grant in the adjacent Wallabout area as recognition for her 1625 birth status, no contemporary documentation confirms this, though English authorities under Governor Richard Nicolls validated existing Dutch patents in nearby Breuckelen by 1667, facilitating continuity for settlers like Rapelje. These lands enabled family-based farming, with Rapelje contributing to provisioning efforts common in colonial Brooklyn villages. Under English rule after the 1664 conquest, Rapelje navigated the shift by adhering to Dutch Reformed Church practices and local governance in Boswijck, where patroon families retained influence despite oaths of allegiance to the Crown; church records show her active as a baptism witness, underscoring community ties in a maturing outpost.[4] This period reflected broader Dutch persistence in outlying areas, with limited disruption to daily agrarian life until later Anglicization pressures.[36]

Death and Burial

Sarah Rapelje died in April 1685 in Boswijck (now Bushwick, Brooklyn), at the age of approximately 60.[5][1][37] Colonial records from the period provide limited details on the precise circumstances of her death, consistent with the disruptions and incomplete documentation in post-New Netherland New York under English rule.[1] She was buried in Brooklyn, though the exact location—possibly a family plot or the Flatbush Dutch Reformed Churchyard—remains unconfirmed due to the loss of many early settler grave markers and church records from wars, fires, and neglect.[6][38]

Historical Significance

Symbolic Role in European Settlement

Sarah Rapelje's birth on 9 June 1625 at Fort Orange constituted the earliest documented instance of a European female child born within the boundaries of New Netherland.[12] This milestone, preserved through family settler accounts and compilations of 17th-century Dutch colonial documents by historical societies, highlighted the colony's emerging capacity for generational continuity.[39] Prior to her arrival, European presence in the region, initiated by the Dutch West India Company's trading post at Fort Orange in 1624, consisted primarily of male traders and short-term expeditions.[4] The event embodied the practical success of transatlantic migration strategies aimed at populating remote outposts through family units, as evidenced by the dispatch of Rapelje's parents, Joris Jansen Rapelje and Catalyntje Trico, among the initial settlers tasked with securing a demographic base.[22] By demonstrating reproductive viability in a frontier setting characterized by harsh winters, scarce resources, and isolation from Europe, her birth causally advanced the shift from extractive fur trade operations—dependent on alliances with Mohawk and other indigenous groups—to self-sustaining communities capable of withstanding wilderness pressures.[1] Contemporary records reveal no immediate displacement of native populations attributable to this singular birth; Fort Orange's foundational activities centered on negotiated beaver pelt exchanges rather than expansive land clearance.[4] This empirical primacy underscores her role as an indicator of settlement endurance, predicated on adaptive economic integration with pre-existing indigenous networks, rather than conquest-driven narratives unsupported by early colonial ledgers.[12]

Genealogical Impact and Descendants

Sarah Rapelje produced 15 children from her two marriages, fostering extensive lineage expansion among Dutch colonial families in New York and New Jersey. With Hans Hansen Bergen, she had eight offspring, including sons such as Hans, who inherited family plantations in Breuckelen (modern Brooklyn), establishing the Bergen surname as a fixture in Kings County landholding and civic life; these progeny intermarried with other early settler lines, propagating descendants across generations in urban and rural settlements.[29] The detailed tracings in Teunis G. Bergen's The Bergen Family (1876) document over 400 named descendants by the mid-19th century, underscoring the family's role in sustaining agrarian and mercantile networks from colonial times into the early republic.[29] Her union with Teunis Gysbert Bogaert yielded seven children, whose lines radiated into Bushwick and adjacent areas, linking through marriages to families like the Rapeljes (via kin networks) and others in the Dutch Reformed communities; while direct Dey connections appear in broader intermarriages among these groups, the Bogaert progeny reinforced settlement patterns in Long Island and the Hudson Valley. Genealogical compilations preserved by the Holland Society of New York, drawing from church records and probate documents, affirm this proliferation, countering fragmentary historical accounts with primary-source fidelity.[40] Descendants contributed to pivotal events, with genealogical society records noting Bergen-line participants as patriots in the American Revolution—such as militiamen from Kings and Bergen counties who engaged in local defenses—and as pioneers in post-independence expansion, including migrations to upstate New York and New Jersey frontiers; these verifiable ties, rooted in muster rolls and family Bibles, highlight causal chains from 17th-century arrivals to foundational republican stock.[29][41]
