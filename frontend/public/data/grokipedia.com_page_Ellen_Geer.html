Ellen Geer

Ellen Ware Geer (born August 29, 1941) is an American actress, theatre director, producer, and educator with a career spanning over five decades in film, television, and stage performances.[1][2] The daughter of actors Will Geer and Herta Ware, Geer debuted on screen in the 1968 film Petulia and has since appeared in numerous television series, including guest roles on Star Trek: The Next Generation as Kila Marr, Beauty and the Beast as Mary, and The Waltons.[3][4][5] Her stage work includes training at the American Shakespeare Academy and performing in repertory theater, leading to her establishment as a key figure in educational theater programs.[6][3] Since 1978, Geer has served as producing artistic director of the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum, an open-air repertory theater in Topanga Canyon, California, founded by her father, where she has directed and produced over 50 productions of works by Shakespeare, Chekhov, Tennessee Williams, and others, while expanding outreach to public school children through sponsored educational initiatives.[7][8][9]

Early Life and Family

Birth and Parentage

Ellen Geer was born Ellen Ware Geer on August 29, 1941, in New York City, New York.[6][3] She is the daughter of American actors Will Geer and Herta Ware, both of whom were involved in theater and film during the mid-20th century.[10][6] Her father, Will Geer (born William Aughe Ghere), pursued acting alongside horticulture and social activism, while her mother, Herta Ware, worked as an actress and balladeer.[6] The couple's marriage reflected their shared commitment to the performing arts, though they later separated amid personal and professional challenges.[3]

Childhood Amid Activism and Blacklisting

Ellen Geer was born on August 29, 1941, into a family steeped in theater and left-wing political activism, with her father, actor Will Geer, and mother, actress Herta Ware, both committed to supporting workers' rights and union causes.[11] The family's early years included time in West Nyack, New York, where Will Geer, holding a botany degree from the University of Chicago, maintained gardens that reflected his multifaceted interests in nature and social justice.[12] Herta Ware's upbringing, influenced by her grandmother Ella Reeve Bloorâ€”a prominent communist organizerâ€”further embedded political engagement in the household, fostering an environment of ideological commitment amid the parents' acting pursuits.[13] In the late 1940s and early 1950s, during the height of the McCarthy era, Will Geer faced scrutiny from the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) over suspected communist associations, refusing to testify and invoking the Fifth Amendment, which led to his blacklisting from Hollywood.[14] This effectively ended his film and television work, plunging the familyâ€”then residing in a comfortable Santa Monica home with domestic helpâ€”into financial distress and forcing the sale of their property.[14] As a young girl during this period, Geer witnessed the direct consequences of her father's principled stand against government inquiries into political affiliations, an event that isolated the family professionally and tested their resilience.[11] Following the blacklisting, the Geers relocated to rural land in Topanga Canyon, California, around the early 1950s, where Will applied his horticultural expertise to grow vegetables and sustain the household through self-reliant farming, marking a shift to a survivalist existence amid economic hardship.[14] There, informal theater productions emerged on the property, drawing other blacklisted performers and immersing Geer and her siblings in an artistic community that circumvented mainstream industry barriers while perpetuating the family's creative and activist ethos.[14] Will Geer maintained an optimistic outlook, framing the ordeal as a "dark period for America" rather than personal defeat, which shaped his children's exposure to perseverance in the face of ideological persecution.[11]

Professional Career

Entry into Acting and Early Roles

Geer, born into a family of actors, began her professional acting career in theater during her teenage years, reflecting the artistic environment shaped by her parents' involvement in stage and socially conscious performances. She trained at the American Shakespeare Academy in New York under instructors including John Houseman. Her stage debut occurred in 1956 as the First Fairy in William Shakespeare's A Midsummer Night's Dream at the American Shakespeare Festival.[6] That same year, Geer made her New York City debut in the title role of Alice in Wonderland at the Cricket Theatre. Additional early stage roles included Gretta in Agatha Christie's Witness for the Prosecution and Emily in Hello out There at the Robin Hood Theatre in Arden, Delaware, both in 1956; Ermengarde in The Matchmaker and Laurey in Green Grow the Lilacs at the Phoenicia Playhouse in Phoenicia, New York, in 1957.[6] These performances established her foundation in regional theater, where she continued working across the country at venues such as the Tyrone Guthrie Theatre, the American Conservatory Theatre in San Francisco, and the Old Globe in San Diego.[8] Geer's entry into screen acting began with television in 1961, portraying Susan (credited as Daughter in some records) in the episode "The Wooden Dish" of the anthology series Play of the Week, which aired on WNTA and featured Henry Hull as an aging patriarch.[6] [15] She transitioned to film in 1968 with a supporting role as a nun in Richard Lester's Petulia, starring Julie Christie and George C. Scott.[10] Her subsequent early film appearance came in 1969's The Reivers, directed by Mark Rydell, where she acted alongside her father, Will Geer, in the adaptation of William Faulkner's novel.[10] These roles marked her initial forays into on-camera work amid ongoing theater commitments.

Film and Television Appearances

Ellen Geer's screen career commenced in the late 1960s, with her film debut as a nun in Petulia (1968), followed by supporting roles in films such as The Reivers (1969) and Kotch (1971).[16] She achieved an early prominent role as Sunshine DorÃ© in Hal Ashby's cult classic Harold and Maude (1971), portraying a free-spirited character in the dark comedy.[10] Additional 1970s film credits include writing and starring in independent features Silence (1974) and Memory of Us (1974), as well as appearing in Over the Edge (1979), a drama about suburban youth rebellion.[16] In television, Geer secured a regular role on The Jimmy Stewart Show from 1971 to 1972.[16] She made frequent guest appearances on 1970s series, including Police Story, The Streets of San Francisco, Baretta, Barnaby Jones, Charlie's Angels, CHiPs, and Fantasy Island.[16] Her television film work during this period encompassed Babe (1975), The Lindbergh Kidnapping Case (1976), The Trial of Lee Harvey Oswald (1977), and A Shining Season (1979).[16] Geer's later film roles featured supporting parts in major productions, such as Bonnie in Phenomenon (1996) opposite John Travolta, a Pineview Woman in The Postman (1997) directed by Kevin Costner, and an aunt in Practical Magic (1998).[10] She continued television guest spots into the 1990s and 2000s on shows like Star Trek: The Next Generation, The Waltons, Quincy, M.E., Dallas, Falcon Crest (recurring), Beauty and the Beast (recurring), The Practice, CSI: Crime Scene Investigation, ER, NYPD Blue, and Cold Case.[16] More recent appearances include Desperate Housewives (2007), Charmed series finale as future Piper Halliwell, NCIS as Esther Daniels, and Better Things as Woman in Yellow.[16] Over her career, she has accumulated more than 125 credited roles in film and television.[17]

Theater Performances and Direction

Ellen Geer began her theater career with acting roles in regional productions before focusing extensively on repertory work at the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum. She earned the Los Angeles Drama Critics Circle Award for Best Actress for her portrayal of Mary Tyrone in Eugene O'Neill's Long Day's Journey Into Night.[8] At Theatricum Botanicum, Geer has performed lead roles in classical and modern plays, including Blanche DuBois in Tennessee Williams' A Streetcar Named Desire and the title character in Euripides' Medea, both of which she also directed.[18] She played Maude in the 2006 stage adaptation of Harold and Maude, opposite Aaron Angello as Harold, running from July 8 to September 24.[19] Geer commenced directing at Theatricum Botanicum in 1973 and, following her appointment as artistic director in 1978, has overseen more than 50 productions, spanning Shakespearean works, plays by Anton Chekhov and Thornton Wilder, and Greek tragedies.[8] Among her directing credits are the musical adaptation of George Orwell's Animal Farm (2002), her self-compiled Queen Margaret's Version of Shakespeare's War of the Roses (2023), George Bernard Shaw's Strife (2025), and an original retelling of Anton Chekhov's The Seagull (2025).[10][20][21][22] She has also directed her own adaptations, including Dracula, A Dark Cloud Came, and Merlin: The Untold Adventures.[8]

Leadership of Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum

Establishment and Expansion

Following Will Geer's death on November 22, 1978, his daughter Ellen Geer assumed the role of artistic director for the Theatricum Botanicum, leading the family to incorporate it as a nonprofit organization dedicated to preserving her father's vision of an outdoor repertory theater in Topanga Canyon, California.[23] This formal establishment built on the venue's informal origins in the 1950s as a gathering space for blacklisted performers on the Geer family property, which Will Geer had expanded into structured performances by 1973.[24] Under Ellen Geer's immediate leadership, the theater transitioned from ad hoc productions to a stable institution emphasizing classical works, folk music, and community engagement, ensuring continuity amid financial and logistical challenges typical of small regional theaters.[25] Ellen Geer oversaw significant expansion starting in the late 1970s, transforming the venue into an Actors' Equity Association-affiliated theater by the 1980s, which enabled professional contracts and higher production standards.[26] She introduced the Academy of the Classics, an educational initiative focused on Shakespearean training for actors, and developed school outreach programs that brought live performances to Los Angeles-area students, broadening the theater's audience and revenue streams.[26] By the 2000s, the annual summer repertory season had stabilized at five rotating productions, often featuring works by Shakespeare, Chekhov, and other canonical authors, alongside innovations like family-oriented adaptations and environmental integrations using the botanic garden setting.[27] This growth culminated in the theater's recognition as a Los Angeles cultural landmark, with Ellen Geer producing and directing over 50 original stagings by the 2020s, sustaining operations through grants, donations, and ticket sales despite seasonal outdoor constraints.[8] The institution marked its 50th anniversary in 2023, reflecting expanded facilities, a multi-generational ensemble including Geer family members, and awards for educational impact, though it remains a modest operation compared to larger urban venues.[20]

Key Productions and Innovations

Under Ellen Geer's leadership since 1978, the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum evolved from a modest Sunday afternoon venue into a professional repertory theater presenting full seasons of classic and socially relevant plays in an outdoor botanical setting.[7] This expansion included establishing rotating repertory productions, where ensembles perform multiple works simultaneously, allowing audiences to experience interconnected narratives and actor versatility over extended runs.[25] Geer has produced and directed over 50 productions, emphasizing Shakespearean works alongside adaptations of Chekhov, Tennessee Williams, Thornton Wilder, and lesser-performed classics that highlight labor struggles, political themes, and human resilience.[7] [9] Notable stagings include her 2023 direction of Macbeth, which opened an ambitious season blending tragedy with contemporary resonance, and the 2025 repertory featuring A Midsummer Night's Dream and Much Ado About Nothing, praised for sophisticated humor and ensemble dynamics amid the canyon's natural amphitheater.[20] [28] Innovative interpretations mark several efforts, such as Geer's relocation of Anton Chekhov's The Seagull to a sun-drenched Malibu estate in 2025, reframing the drama's interpersonal tensions through a West Coast lens while preserving the original's emotional core.[29] Co-directed with her daughter Willow Geer, the same season's revival of John Galsworthy's Strifeâ€”a rarely staged 1909 play on industrial conflictâ€”underscored overlooked perspectives on class and unionism, aligning with the theater's roots in blacklisted artists' advocacy.[21] Additional programming innovations include educational workshops, youth apprenticeships fostering multi-generational casts, and themed seasons like the 2025 "Season of Resilience," which integrated post-disaster healing through performance.[30] These elements, combined with events such as the 2024 70th-anniversary screening of the 1954 film Salt of the Earth, sustain the venue's commitment to provocative, site-specific theater drawing over 50 years of Shakespearean continuity.[31]

Personal Life

Marriages and Children

Ellen Geer married actor Ed Flanders on January 29, 1963; the marriage ended in divorce in 1968.[6][3] The couple had one child, a son named Ian Geer Flanders.[3] In 1975, Geer married singer-songwriter Peter Alsop, with whom she has remained married.[3][23] They have two daughters, Megan Geer and Willow Geer.[3]

Ongoing Family Involvement in Arts

Ellen Geer's daughter, Willow Geer, has emerged as a key figure in sustaining the family's artistic legacy, serving as Associate Producing Artistic Director at the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum and frequently performing in its productions, including roles in Shakespearean works and contemporary plays.[20][25] Born in 1981, Willow grew up immersed in the theater's environment, contributing to its operations and creative output alongside her mother, with the two often collaborating on directing and staging efforts.[25] Her involvement exemplifies the multi-generational continuity, as she represents the third generation of Geers active in the venue founded by her grandfather Will Geer in 1973.[14] Ellen's other children, including son Ian Geer Flanders and daughter Megan Geer, were raised participating in Theatricum productions, fostering a tradition where family members across branches perform and contribute to programming.[14] Her sister, Melora Marshall, holds the record for most appearances at the theater, starring in numerous repertory seasons and embodying the enduring sibling involvement initiated during the company's early years under Will Geer and Herta Ware.[32] Nephews and other extended relatives have also stepped into leadership and performance roles, supporting Ellen's direction of over 50 productions since 1978.[20] The involvement extends to younger descendants, with grandchildren performing on stage as of summer seasons in recent years, ensuring the Geer family's hands-on presence in both professional repertory and educational initiatives that reach over 8,000 students annually through programs like the Academy of the Classics.[14][7] This ongoing participation maintains the theater's status as a family-operated entity, prioritizing classical works, outdoor performances, and community outreach rooted in the original vision of artistic refuge and activism.[25]

Legacy and Impact

Contributions to American Theater

Ellen Geer has advanced American repertory theater by sustaining and innovating the outdoor performance model at the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum, where she has served as producing artistic director since 1978, expanding operations from sporadic Sunday workshops to year-round professional seasons featuring ensemble casts performing multiple roles across rotating productions.[7] This approach revives classical techniques emphasizing actor versatility and textual fidelity, countering trends toward specialized, commercial indoor theater.[25] Through directing over 50 productions, Geer has integrated classical repertoiresâ€”spanning Shakespeare, Chekhov, Tennessee Williams, Thornton Wilder, and Greek tragediesâ€”with site-specific adaptations that leverage the Topanga Canyon amphitheater's natural environment to underscore themes of human-nature interplay and social realism.[8] Notable examples include her 2014 gender-reversed King Lear, which heightened focus on patriarchal vulnerabilities, and a 2025 relocation of Anton Chekhov's The Seagull to a contemporary American context, preserving dramatic essence while addressing modern alienation.[33][29] Geer's educational outreach has democratized theater training, founding the Academy of the Classics in 1976 to offer intensive Shakespeare seminars, scene study workshops, and teen programs that have engaged thousands of Los Angeles public school students annually through subsidized outreach.[8] These efforts prioritize skill-building in verse-speaking and ensemble dynamics over ideological framing, fostering a pipeline of performers grounded in empirical stagecraft traditions.[3] By embedding such programs within a nonprofit repertory framework, she has bolstered regional theater's resilience against funding volatility, ensuring sustained access to unaltered canonical works.[34]

Influence of Family Heritage

Ellen Geer's career and leadership at the Will Geer Theatricum Botanicum were profoundly shaped by her parents' commitment to theater amid adversity. Born to actors Will Geer and Herta Ware, she grew up immersed in a household where performance was both profession and refuge; her father, blacklisted during the McCarthy era after testifying before Congress, founded the Theatricum in 1953 on 4.5 acres in Topanga Canyon, California, as a sanctuary for similarly affected artists, integrating theater with the natural landscape inspired by his botany degree.[24] Herta Ware supported the endeavor through acting and cultivating a garden for supplemental income, instilling in their childrenâ€”including Ellen and siblings Thad, Kate, and Sarahâ€”a resilient ethos of collaborative, outdoor repertory work focused on classical texts like Shakespeare.[23] [24] This heritage directly influenced Ellen's assumption of artistic directorship in 1978 following Will Geer's death, transforming the venue from informal Sunday workshops into a professional nonprofit repertory company with year-round programming, educational outreach serving 6,000 children annually via field trips, and youth camps emphasizing dramatic arts.[7] The family's tradition of activism and union advocacy, rooted in the blacklist era's challenges, informed her selections of socially resonant plays and her emphasis on accessible, nature-connected performances, echoing her father's vision while expanding international recognition for Shakespeare productions.[24] [23] Multi-generational involvement perpetuated this legacy, with Ellen's siblings and her own daughters, Willow and Megan Geer-Alsop, participating in productions, reinforcing a model of familial collaboration over commercial individualism.[23] This structure not only sustained the theater's operations but also mirrored the Geers' historical response to exclusion from mainstream industry, prioritizing artistic integrity and community education as core principles.[24]
