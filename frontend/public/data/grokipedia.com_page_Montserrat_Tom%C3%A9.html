Montse Tomé

Montserrat Tomé Vázquez (born 1982) is a Spanish football manager and former player who served as the head coach of the Spain women's national team from September 2023 until August 2025.[1][2] She was the first woman appointed to the role, succeeding Jorge Vilda following Spain's victory at the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup.[3][1] As a player, Tomé appeared for clubs such as Oviedo Moderno, Barcelona, and Levante, securing two Spanish league titles and earning four caps for the senior Spain national team.[3][4] Before her senior appointment, she managed Spain's under-20 and under-23 women's teams and assisted Vilda with the senior squad.[5] Tomé's tenure occurred amid strained relations between the Spanish Football Federation and senior players, stemming from a 2022 player protest against Vilda's management; as his assistant, she had aligned with the federation.[1][6] Her early decisions included barring Jenni Hermoso from selection, a player central to prior federation disputes, though Hermoso was later recalled.[7] Under Tomé, Spain claimed the 2024 UEFA Women's Nations League but faltered with a quarterfinal exit at the 2024 Olympics and a final loss to England at UEFA Women's Euro 2025, after which her contract expired without renewal.[8][2][9]

Playing career

Club career

Tomé began her senior playing career with Oviedo Moderno in Asturias, where she developed as a midfielder after starting in local youth teams.[10][11] She joined Levante UD in the 2006–07 season, playing four campaigns in the Superliga and contributing to their league title win in 2007–08.[12][13] In summer 2010, Tomé transferred to FC Barcelona, appearing in 54 official matches over two seasons and scoring 5 goals while accumulating 3,485 minutes.[14] With Barcelona, she secured another Spanish league championship during her tenure.[3] She returned to Oviedo Moderno for the 2012–13 season before retiring from playing to pursue coaching.[15] Across her club career, Tomé won two national league titles, reflecting her role in competitive squads despite limited international exposure at the senior level.[3]

International career

Tomé earned four caps for the senior Spain women's national team, primarily during the qualification campaign for the 2005 UEFA Women's Euro.[16] Her appearances reflected limited opportunities in a competitive squad, with no goals scored in those matches.[16]

Coaching career

Early coaching roles

Tomé transitioned to coaching after retiring as a player in 2012, initially taking roles with youth teams at clubs in her native Asturias region.[5] She served as coach for junior squads at Oviedo Moderno CF, where she had previously played, as well as Valdesoto CF and CD Romanón, focusing on developing young female players in regional leagues.[5] These positions provided her foundational experience in youth development, though they were at amateur and lower-tier levels without notable competitive achievements documented in public records. By the mid-2010s, Tomé advanced to roles within the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF), coaching Spain's women's youth national teams, including the under-19 squad. In this capacity, she contributed to talent identification and training programs for emerging players, emphasizing tactical discipline and technical skills aligned with the federation's senior team philosophy.[17] Her work with the U19 team involved preparing squads for European qualifiers, though specific match records or tournament outcomes from this period remain sparsely detailed in federation reports, reflecting the developmental rather than high-profile nature of these assignments. These roles preceded her promotion to the senior national team's staff in 2018, marking the culmination of her early coaching phase.[18]

Assistant coach roles

Tomé joined the coaching staff of the Spain women's national football team in 2018 as an assistant coach under head coach Jorge Vilda.[19][20] In this capacity, she served as Vilda's deputy for five years, contributing to tactical preparation and analysis within the senior team setup.[21] Her responsibilities included leveraging analytical skills to support match strategies, making her a central figure in the technical staff during qualification campaigns and major tournaments.[3] As assistant coach, Tomé was part of the team that achieved the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup victory, defeating England 1–0 in the final on August 20, 2023, in Sydney.[18] This triumph marked Spain's first major international title, with Tomé involved in the preparatory phases leading to the tournament success under Vilda's leadership.[2] She continued in the role through Vilda's tenure until his dismissal on September 5, 2023, amid internal federation disputes following the World Cup.[22] Prior to her senior team assistant position, Tomé had coached youth teams within the Spanish football system, but her primary assistant-level experience at the elite level was confined to the national team staff under Vilda, where she focused on continuity in player development and performance optimization.[5] No club-level assistant roles are documented in her career trajectory during this period.[23]

Head coach of Spain women's national team

Montse Tomé was appointed head coach of the Spain women's national football team on 5 September 2023, becoming the first woman to hold the position.[18] Her appointment followed the dismissal of Jorge Vilda shortly after Spain's victory at the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, where Tomé had served as his assistant.[20] Tomé's initial tenure was marked by efforts to stabilize the team amid ongoing internal tensions stemming from the prior player mutiny against Vilda's management.[3] During her time in charge, Tomé oversaw 36 matches, achieving 28 wins, 3 draws, and 5 losses.[20] Key successes included winning the 2023–24 UEFA Women's Nations League, which secured qualification for the 2024 Paris Olympics.[24] At the UEFA Women's Euro 2025, Spain under Tomé advanced to the final but lost 1–1 (3–1 on penalties) to England on 27 July 2025.[18] Her coaching emphasized maintaining the possession-based, high-pressing style established under Vilda, while integrating younger players and managing squad dynamics.[25] Tomé's contract expired on 31 August 2025, and the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) opted not to renew it following the Euro final defeat, announcing her departure on 11 August 2025.[2] She was replaced by Sonia Bermeo, head coach of the Spain U-23 team.[26] Despite the strong win rate, critics pointed to tactical inflexibility in high-stakes matches and unresolved player relations as factors in the decision.[23]

Controversies and criticisms

2023 player boycott and appointment challenges

Following the sacking of head coach Jorge Vilda on September 5, 2023, amid escalating disputes with players, the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) appointed Montse Tomé—Vilda's long-time assistant since 2018—as interim head coach of the Spain women's national team, marking the first time a woman held the position.[27][17] The decision prioritized internal continuity from the staff that had guided Spain to the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup title, despite prior player criticisms of the coaching environment as overly controlling and unsupportive of individual needs.[28] Tomé's appointment intensified an existing boycott by 81 players, who had issued a collective statement in early September 2023 refusing national team call-ups until the RFEF implemented deep structural reforms addressing cultural issues exposed by the Rubiales scandal and earlier disputes.[29] The boycott stemmed from events post-World Cup final on August 20, 2023, including RFEF president Luis Rubiales' non-consensual kiss of player Jenni Hermoso and his defiant speech on August 25, which prompted the initial team withdrawal.[28] Players viewed Tomé's promotion—without an external search—as insufficient change, associating her with the federation's contested management style that had led to prior revolts, such as the 2022 "Las 15" group exclusion.[30] On September 18, 2023, Tomé named her debut squad for UEFA Women's Nations League matches, selecting 23 players including 15 World Cup winners like Alexia Putellas and Irene Paredes, but the boycotters reiterated their stance, declaring prior statements "remain in full force and effect" and declining to report.[31][30] This standoff created logistical uncertainty, with Tomé preparing for potential absences while the RFEF, under interim president Pedro Rocha, defended the appointment as a step toward renewal.[28] The crisis resolved on September 20, 2023, after overnight talks mediated by Spain's Higher Council for Sports, where the RFEF pledged "immediate and profound changes" to protocols and governance; all but two boycotting World Cup players (Misa Rodríguez and another) then ended their protest, enabling their return for Tomé's first game against Sweden on September 22.[32][33] Players clarified they did not demand Tomé's removal but emphasized rebuilding trust would require her to address ongoing federation issues independently of prior loyalties.[34] The episode underscored causal tensions between player demands for systemic overhaul and the RFEF's incremental approach, with Tomé navigating initial matches amid fragile squad cohesion.[35]

Association with federation leadership and Rubiales scandal

Montse Tomé served as assistant coach to Jorge Vilda for the Spain women's national team, a position she held during Luis Rubiales' tenure as president of the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) from May 2018 until his resignation on September 10, 2023.[36] Under this leadership structure, the coaching staff oversaw the team's preparation and victory at the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, culminating in the final on August 20, 2023, after which Rubiales kissed player Jenni Hermoso on the lips without her consent during the victory celebrations, sparking widespread outrage and legal proceedings.[37] Vilda, appointed head coach in 2015 and retained by Rubiales despite player complaints about a controlling environment, had faced a boycott by 15 senior players in September 2022 over issues including mental health concerns and federation oversight, which the RFEF publicly contested as unsubstantiated.[38] In the immediate aftermath of Rubiales' actions, Tomé and ten other members of the coaching staff resigned on August 26, 2023, issuing a joint statement expressing distress over the federation's handling of the incident and calling for institutional change.[39] However, following Vilda's dismissal on September 5, 2023—attributed by the RFEF to ongoing discord rather than the scandal directly—Tomé was swiftly reappointed as head coach on September 6, 2023, becoming the first woman in the role.[40] This decision by the interim RFEF leadership, still operating under the shadow of Rubiales' regime, was criticized by players and observers for signaling continuity with the prior administration, as Tomé had been part of Vilda's inner circle that the boycotting players had opposed.[41] Tomé's ties to the federation drew further scrutiny during Rubiales' trial for sexual assault. On February 10, 2025, she testified before a Madrid court that she had been unaware of any RFEF protocol for addressing sexual violence at the time of the kiss and denied excluding Hermoso from subsequent national team selections as retaliation, insisting decisions were based on sporting criteria.[37] [38] Rubiales was convicted on February 21, 2025, of sexual assault—defined under Spanish law as an act against sexual freedom without violence or intimidation—and fined €10,800, though he avoided imprisonment; Tomé declined to comment on the verdict when questioned afterward, redirecting focus to team matters.[42] [43] Her testimony highlighted gaps in federation protocols under Rubiales, contributing to broader debates on accountability within Spanish football governance.[37]

2025 dismissal and performance debates

On August 11, 2025, the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) announced that Montse Tomé would not have her contract renewed as head coach of the Spain women's national team, effective after its expiration on August 31, 2025.[2][26] The decision followed Spain's penalty shootout loss to England in the UEFA Women's Euro 2025 final on July 27, 2025, marking the team's second consecutive runner-up finish in major tournaments under Tomé, after a bronze medal match defeat to Germany at the 2024 Paris Olympics.[18][44] Tomé's overall record stood at 28 wins, 4 draws, and 5 losses in 37 matches, yielding a 75.67% win percentage since her appointment in September 2023.[3] Despite this strong aggregate performance, critics highlighted tactical shortcomings in high-stakes games, including Spain's inability to convert dominance into trophies amid a generational talent pool featuring players like Aitana Bonmatí and Alexia Putellas.[45] The RFEF cited the need for a "new project" post-Euro, though reports indicated the non-renewal was predetermined before the tournament, reflecting internal evaluations of her leadership amid federation instability.[23] Debates surrounding the dismissal centered on whether performance failures justified the move or if deeper federation dysfunction prevailed. Tomé publicly claimed a "broken promise" by RFEF president Rafael Louzan, who had allegedly assured her of continuity, and stated she was not directly informed of the decision, learning it through media channels.[46] Player discontent was a recurring theme, with sources attributing tensions to Tomé's efforts to disrupt established cliques involving figures like Jenni Hermoso and Irene Paredes, exacerbating divides from prior boycotts and the Luis Rubiales scandal.[47][7] However, some analysts argued the RFEF's track record of mismanagement—evident in repeated coaching upheavals and ethical lapses—undermined claims of a merit-based sacking, positioning Sonia Bermúdez, a former player with no senior head coaching experience, as the successor despite questions over her qualifications.[22][45] The episode fueled broader discussions on Spanish women's football governance, where Tomé's exit was seen by proponents as a pragmatic response to underwhelming results against expectations set by the 2023 World Cup win under Jorge Vilda, while detractors viewed it as scapegoating amid systemic issues like player-federation rifts and inconsistent strategic vision.[7] No formal performance metrics beyond tournament outcomes were publicly detailed by the RFEF, leaving debates reliant on qualitative assessments of team cohesion and tactical adaptability.[18]

Honours

As player

  • Primera División Femenina: 2007–08 (Levante UD); 2011–12 (FC Barcelona)[48][5]

As assistant coach

In 2018, Montse Tomé joined the Spain women's national football team as assistant coach under head coach Jorge Vilda.[27][49] She served in this role for five years, contributing to the team's tactical and analytical preparation.[3] As Vilda's chief assistant, Tomé focused on performance analysis and staff coordination, helping build the squad's competitive framework amid growing international success.[3][27] During her tenure, the team achieved its first major title by winning the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup in Australia and New Zealand on August 20, 2023, defeating England 1–0 in the final.[18] Tomé's involvement included supporting Vilda's strategies that emphasized possession-based play and youth integration, which propelled Spain to a tournament record of seven wins, one draw, and 18 goals scored.[18][27] Her role ended on September 5, 2023, when she was promoted to head coach following Vilda's dismissal by the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF).[26][27]

As head coach


Montse Tomé was appointed head coach of the Spain women's national football team on September 5, 2023, becoming the first woman to hold the position. She succeeded Jorge Vilda, under whom she had served as assistant coach since 2018, amid ongoing internal federation changes following the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup victory.[17][26]
During her tenure, Tomé led Spain to victory in the inaugural UEFA Women's Nations League, defeating France 2–0 in the final on July 16, 2024. The team maintained a strong record under her management, winning 28 of 36 matches. At the UEFA Women's EURO 2025, Spain achieved a perfect group stage record, including a 3–1 win over Italy, before advancing to the final but losing to England on penalties.[3] Spain also competed at the 2024 Paris Olympics but exited earlier than expected.[50][44]
Tomé's contract was not renewed by the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF), with her departure announced on August 11, 2025, following the EURO final defeat; her deal expired on August 31. She was replaced by Sonia Bermúdez, previously coach of the Spain under-23 team. The decision came after reported dissatisfaction in the locker room and tactical critiques during key losses, though Tomé expressed disappointment over unfulfilled promises from federation leadership.[2][23][46]
