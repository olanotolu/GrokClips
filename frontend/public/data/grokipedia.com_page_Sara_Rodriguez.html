Sara Rodriguez

Sara Rodriguez is an American Democratic politician who has served as the 46th Lieutenant Governor of Wisconsin since January 2023.[1] Elected on November 8, 2022, as the running mate of incumbent Governor Tony Evers, she previously held the position of Wisconsin State Representative for the 13th Assembly District from April 2021 to January 2023.[1] A registered nurse and public health professional with more than two decades of experience in nursing, Rodriguez focused her legislative work on health care access and public health policy.[2][3] In July 2025, she became the first Democrat to announce her candidacy for Governor of Wisconsin in the 2026 election.[4] Her political career has included efforts to address health disparities and support working families, though she has faced scrutiny over campaign practices, including allegations of improper fundraising methods in prior elections.[5]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Sara Rodriguez was born in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, and raised in Brookfield in a working-class union family.[2] Her parents began their family while living in a trailer but saved to relocate to a better school district, emphasizing the value of education and stability.[2] Her mother worked as a teaching assistant for children with special needs and was a member of the Wisconsin Education Association Council (WEAC), while her father, a Vietnam War veteran, was employed as a phone repair technician at Wisconsin Bell.[2] Rodriguez's grandparents were dairy farmers in Richland County, Wisconsin, contributing to her family's deep ties to the state's rural and agricultural heritage.[2] During her upbringing in Brookfield, Rodriguez attended Brookside Elementary, Burleigh Elementary, Elmbrook Middle School (now Pilgrim Park Middle School), and graduated from Brookfield East High School in 1993.[6] She participated in extracurricular activities including softball, swimming, orchestra, forensics, drama, Key Club, Spanish Honor Society, and National Honor Society, fostering skills in leadership, community service, and academics.[6] Her family instilled values of hard work, perseverance, and mutual community support, shaped by their modest circumstances and union background.[2]

Academic and professional training

Rodriguez graduated from Brookfield East High School in Brookfield, Wisconsin, in 1993. She subsequently earned an undergraduate degree from Illinois Wesleyan University.[2][6] Rodriguez advanced her education at Johns Hopkins University, obtaining a Bachelor of Science in Nursing, a Master of Science in Nursing (MSN), and a Master of Public Health (MPH).[7][1] Her nursing training at the Johns Hopkins School of Nursing equipped her to become a registered nurse (RN), focusing on clinical and public health competencies.[7] She supplemented this with specialized professional training as an Epidemic Intelligence Service officer at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), where she conducted domestic and international outbreak investigations.[1][2]

Healthcare career

Nursing and public health roles

Rodriguez is a registered nurse who earned a Master of Science in Nursing (MSN) and a Master of Public Health (MPH) from Johns Hopkins University School of Nursing.[7] As an RN, she worked night shifts in emergency rooms, treating patients amid the opioid overdose epidemic and gun violence injuries.[8] She has over 20 years of experience in public health nursing and healthcare leadership roles.[9] Rodriguez served as an Epidemic Intelligence Service officer at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), where she led domestic and international disease outbreak investigations, including a response in Louisiana after Hurricane Katrina.[10] Early in her career, Rodriguez volunteered for two years with the Peace Corps in Samoa, focusing on health education programs in partnership with the World Health Organization.[10] In subsequent executive positions, she managed health services at Honeywell and held the role of Vice President of Population Health and Integrated Care Management at one of the largest healthcare systems in the Midwest.[10][9] She also founded Lamplight Healthcare Consulting to advise health systems and technology firms on public health strategies.[7]

Involvement in health policy and organizations

Prior to entering elected office, Rodriguez served as a public health nurse for over two decades, including roles in epidemiology and health intelligence. She worked as an intelligence officer at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), focusing on public health surveillance and response strategies.[10][11] Additionally, she held positions in public health departments in Colorado, contributing to community health initiatives and disease prevention programs.[12] From 2017 to 2020, Rodriguez was Vice President of Population Health Services and Integrated Care Management at Advocate Aurora Health, one of the largest healthcare systems in the Midwest. In this executive role, she oversaw strategies to improve population-level health outcomes, including care coordination, reduction of hospital readmissions, and integration of preventive services to manage chronic conditions and lower costs.[13][4][14] Her work emphasized data-driven interventions to address social determinants of health and enhance patient management across the system's network in Wisconsin and Illinois.[15] In 2020, following her tenure at Advocate Aurora Health, Rodriguez founded Lamplight Healthcare Consulting, a firm providing expertise in public health strategy, care integration, and workforce development for healthcare organizations. Through this venture, she advised on policy-aligned solutions for improving access to care and operational efficiency in response to emerging challenges like the COVID-19 pandemic.[16][17] These professional experiences positioned her as an advocate for nurses' involvement in health policy formulation, emphasizing evidence-based approaches to systemic improvements.[18]

Political career

Wisconsin State Assembly service (2021â€“2023)

Rodriguez was elected to the Wisconsin State Assembly in the November 3, 2020, general election, defeating one-term Republican incumbent Rob Hutton by a margin of 50.9% to 49.1% (19,318 votes to 18,584).[19] She won her August 11, 2020, Democratic primary unopposed, receiving 99.8% of the vote (5,593 votes).[19] The 13th Assembly District, which she represented from January 4, 2021, to January 2, 2023, covers suburban communities in Waukesha County, including Brookfield, Elm Grove, and parts of Wauwatosa.[19] This election flipped the seat from Republican to Democratic control in a competitive suburban district.[20] As a freshman legislator in the Democratic minority during a Republican-majority session, Rodriguez focused on healthcare access and public health policy.[19] She served on the Assembly Committee on Energy and Utilities from January 2021 to May 2022.[21] Rodriguez sponsored Assembly Bill 226 (2021), which required the University of Wisconsin System regents and technical college district boards to collect and report data on nursing degree program enrollment, retention, and graduation rates, while mandating plans to address shortages in the nursing workforce.[22] The bill aimed to inform state strategies for bolstering healthcare staffing amid ongoing workforce challenges.[22] Rodriguez co-sponsored legislation to repeal Wisconsin's pre-Civil War abortion ban (1849 law), which had been invoked following the 2022 U.S. Supreme Court Dobbs decision; the effort did not advance in the GOP-controlled chamber.[19] Her priorities included advocating for expansion of BadgerCare (Wisconsin's Medicaid program) to improve affordable healthcare coverage and addressing public health needs, such as during the COVID-19 pandemic response.[19] In July 2022, Governor Tony Evers selected her as his running mate for the gubernatorial re-election campaign, contributing to her decision to forgo re-election to the Assembly; the Evers-Rodriguez ticket's victory led to her transition to lieutenant governor in January 2023.[19]

Lieutenant Governor tenure (2023â€“present)

Rodriguez was sworn in as Wisconsin's 46th lieutenant governor on January 3, 2023, alongside Governor Tony Evers.[1] In this capacity, she serves as president of the Wisconsin State Senate, presiding over its sessions and empowered to cast tie-breaking votes when necessary, though the Republican-majority chamber has not required her intervention in such instances during her tenure.[23] Her role also includes supporting the governor's administration priorities, drawing on her prior experience in public health and state assembly service focused on health policy and workforce issues. A key aspect of Rodriguez's tenure has involved extensive statewide outreach through annual tours of Wisconsin's 72 counties, initiated in 2023 as a priority alongside Governor Evers to connect directly with local communities and address regional concerns.[24] These tours, conducted each year from 2023 to 2025, have emphasized listening sessions on persistent challenges such as affordable housing shortages, public education funding, and childcare access, with Rodriguez reporting these as recurring themes across urban and rural areas alike.[25] The 2025 tour concluded on August 28 in Calumet County, marking the third consecutive year of full coverage and facilitating discussions on economic development and small business support.[24] In August 2025, Rodriguez was appointed by fellow state lieutenant governors to chair the State Strategies Committee on Aerospace, Aviation, and Defense, a national body aimed at coordinating interstate efforts to bolster these sectors amid economic and security considerations.[26] This leadership role aligns with broader administration goals for workforce development in high-tech industries, though specific outcomes from the committee remain pending as of October 2025. Her tenure has otherwise maintained a low public profile consistent with the lieutenant governorship's constitutional limits, focusing on ceremonial duties and advisory support rather than independent policymaking authority.[23]

2026 gubernatorial campaign

On July 25, 2025, Lieutenant Governor Sara Rodriguez announced her candidacy for the Democratic nomination in the 2026 Wisconsin gubernatorial election, positioning herself as the first Democrat to formally enter the race after incumbent Governor Tony Evers stated he would not seek a third term.[27][28] In her launch, Rodriguez emphasized her background as a registered nurse and public health expert, pledging to focus on healthcare access, economic growth, and protecting democratic institutions, while criticizing former President Donald Trump for contributing to national divisions.[29] Her campaign website, saraforwi.com, outlines priorities including expanding affordable healthcare, investing in education, and addressing rural economic challenges.[30] Rodriguez's campaign strategy includes extensive grassroots outreach, highlighted by the launch of the "Rolling with Rodriguez" tour on August 25, 2025, aimed at visiting all 72 Wisconsin counties to engage voters on local issues.[31] Early stops included a Milwaukee event on August 15, 2025, focused on veterans' concerns such as mental health services and VA wait times, and a Racine visit on September 24, 2025, where she discussed workforce development and community safety.[32][33] The campaign has framed her as the strongest Democratic contender against potential Republican nominee U.S. Congressman Tom Tiffany, citing internal polling and her statewide visibility from the 2022 ticket.[34] Financial support bolstered the effort when the Democratic Lieutenants Governor Association announced a $2 million independent expenditure investment on October 1, 2025, to aid Rodriguez's bid amid a competitive primary field that may include other Democrats considering entry.[35] As of October 2025, no primary opponents had formally launched, though speculation persists around figures like state Attorney General Josh Kaul.[27] On the Republican side, candidates including Tiffany have emerged, setting up a contest in the battleground state where recent elections have been decided by narrow margins.[34]

Policy positions

Healthcare and public health

Rodriguez supports expanding Medicaid in Wisconsin to enhance healthcare access and affordability for residents, contending that such expansion would also strengthen the state's healthcare workforce by providing resources for training and retention.[36][37] This position aligns with Democratic priorities to counter Republican opposition to the expansion, which has left Wisconsin among states without full Medicaid coverage under the Affordable Care Act as of 2025.[36] As chair of Governor Tony Evers' Task Force on Healthcare Workforce, established in 2023, Rodriguez has prioritized addressing shortages in nursing and other clinical roles, informed by her two decades as a public health nurse and emergency room clinician.[9] The task force, which she led, issued recommendations on August 20, 2024, including incentives for workforce recruitment, expanded training programs, and policy reforms to reduce burnout and improve retention amid post-pandemic strains.[38] Rodriguez has highlighted the need to "shore up this industry's workforce," emphasizing practical solutions drawn from her experience in outbreak response at the CDC's Epidemic Intelligence Service.[39] On public health infrastructure, Rodriguez advocates for investments to lower overall healthcare costs and ensure equitable access, including initiatives for clean water to prevent environmental health risks irrespective of geographic location.[2] During the COVID-19 pandemic, she endorsed Assembly Democrats' comprehensive response legislation introduced on January 4, 2021, which sought to bolster testing, vaccination efforts, and economic supports tied to public health compliance.[40] Rodriguez opposes abortion restrictions, framing access to abortion procedures as essential reproductive healthcare that must be protected from legislative overreach, a stance she has reiterated in her gubernatorial campaign.[2] This includes criticism of federal policies under prior Republican administrations that she argues undermine such access.[41]

Economic and fiscal policies

As Lieutenant Governor, Sara Rodriguez has advocated for economic policies emphasizing support for small businesses, which she describes as "the lifeblood of our communities."[42] She has supported state investments in technical assistance, workforce development, and talent attraction to promote entrepreneurship and small business growth, continuing these priorities from her tenure.[42] These efforts align with broader Democratic initiatives under Governor Tony Evers to expand workforce training programs, including apprenticeships and technical education funding, aimed at addressing labor shortages in manufacturing and other sectors.[43] In fiscal matters, Rodriguez participated in the Evers administration's 2025-2027 biennial budget proposal, which Republican critics, including State Representative Ned Weski, characterized as including $3 billion in proposed tax increases and projecting a $4 billion deficit if enacted as submitted.[44] The final compromise budget, signed in August 2025 after legislative negotiations, instead provided approximately $1.5 billion in tax relief, including expansions to the second income tax bracket at a 4.4% rate and cuts benefiting middle-class families and retirees, while increasing overall state spending amid a waning surplus.[44] [45] Rodriguez has opposed federal budget cuts proposed in 2025 that would reduce funding for Medicaid, Medicare, and public services, joining nearly 900 state and local officials in a bipartisan letter arguing such reductions undermine essential programs without addressing fraud or waste effectively.[46] During her 2026 gubernatorial campaign launch, she highlighted aiding families amid economic pressures like inflation, though specific fiscal proposals on property or income taxes remain undeveloped in public statements.[15] No direct positions on property tax reforms or further state tax adjustments have been articulated, despite ongoing debates over local levies and Evers' prior vetoes criticized for perpetuating higher rates.[47]

Social and cultural issues

Rodriguez holds pro-choice positions on abortion, emphasizing that decisions about starting a family should remain with individuals rather than politicians, informed by her background as a healthcare professional. She co-authored legislation during her time in the Wisconsin State Assembly to repeal the state's 1849 law criminalizing abortion, and as Lieutenant Governor, she has advocated for protecting access to reproductive healthcare following court rulings affirming such rights in Wisconsin.[48][19] In education policy, Rodriguez supports increased funding for public schools to ensure equitable access from early childhood through higher education, portraying educators as essential to the state's future and committing to policies that aid students and teachers amid challenges like post-pandemic recovery. She has highlighted education as a recurring concern during her statewide campaign tour, linking it to broader family support systems including childcare.[49][25] Rodriguez addresses gun violence through a public health lens, drawing from her emergency room experience treating related injuries and her work combating national crises in opioids and firearms. She has participated in events like the 2025 Emergency Gun Violence Summit and received endorsements from gun violence prevention groups such as Giffords PAC, which align with her advocacy for measures to reduce such incidents without specifying legislative details beyond broader health initiatives.[8][50][51] On LGBTQ issues, Rodriguez has demonstrated support by visiting community centers like OutReach LGBTQ in Madison to discuss essential services and issuing statements affirming visibility and access to opportunities for transgender youth, framing these as part of inclusive community support. Her positions align with Democratic priorities but lack detailed policy proposals on specific legislation like nondiscrimination protections or gender-related policies.[52][53]

Controversies and criticisms

Campaign launch irregularities

On July 17, 2025, a campaign launch video announcing Sara Rodriguez's candidacy for Wisconsin governor was uploaded to YouTube, featuring her declaration of intent to run and emphasis on her background as a nurse and lieutenant governor.[54] This posting occurred one week before Governor Tony Evers publicly stated on July 24, 2025, that he would not seek a third term, with Rodriguez's official campaign announcement following on July 25, 2025.[54] [28] Rodriguez's campaign attributed the early upload to an internal error, initially stating that she had received advance notice from Evers about his decision, but later revising this to claim she learned of it simultaneously with his cabinet members.[54] When pressed by reporters on the discrepancy, a campaign staffer intervened to deflect questions, according to contemporaneous social media documentation.[54] Evers, for his part, denied providing any endorsement or prior coordination with Rodriguez, expressing a preference to avoid involvement in the Democratic primary.[54] Critics, including commentators from conservative outlets, highlighted the timeline as evidence of potential foreknowledge or premeditated preparation, questioning the shifting explanations and suggesting a lack of transparency in the campaign's rollout.[54] The incident drew limited coverage in mainstream Wisconsin media, with no formal investigations or ethics complaints reported as of October 2025, though it fueled early skepticism about the campaign's organizational readiness.[54] The MacIver Institute, a free-market think tank critical of Democratic officeholders, amplified the story via archived social media posts and timelines, while Rodriguez's team maintained the upload was inadvertent and did not alter the video's content or visibility prior to the official launch.[54]

Public health stances and COVID-19 response

Rodriguez, a registered nurse and former CDC intelligence officer, has positioned herself as a proponent of evidence-based public health interventions, drawing on her experience in emergency rooms and chronic disease management at state health departments.[1][8] In early 2021, as a state assemblymember, she supported Democratic-led efforts to bolster Wisconsin's COVID-19 response, including the introduction of comprehensive legislation on January 4, 2021, to expand testing, contact tracing, personal protective equipment distribution, and financial aid for small businesses and workers impacted by the pandemic.[40] She criticized Republican assembly members on January 27, 2021, for rejecting a bipartisan Senate-passed compromise bill that would have expedited federal relief funds for virus mitigation, accusing them of introducing partisan amendments that delayed critical resources amid rising cases and hospitalizations.[55] During her 2020 assembly campaign, Rodriguez highlighted Republican legislative challenges to Governor Tony Evers' emergency ordersâ€”struck down by the state Supreme Court in May 2020â€”as forcing residents into untenable choices between public health and economic survival, a factor she cited in deciding to run against the incumbent. Rodriguez aligned with Democratic opposition to Republican-backed bills in 2021 and 2022 that prohibited COVID-19 vaccine mandates for state employees, employers, and schoolchildren, as well as measures recognizing prior infection as equivalent to vaccination for exemption purposes; these passed along party lines in the GOP-controlled assembly.[56][57] In her lieutenant governor role, Rodriguez has focused on strengthening public health infrastructure, including co-chairing the Governor's Task Force on Healthcare Workforce since 2023 to address shortages in clinical training and recruitment.[9] On August 28, 2025, she demanded the resignation of Robert F. Kennedy Jr. from his nomination as U.S. Secretary of Health and Human Services, decrying his vaccine skepticism as a threat to established public health strategies.[58]

Personal and family-related attacks

In July 2025, five days after Sara Rodriguez announced her candidacy for governor of Wisconsin, reporters received an anonymous tip alleging that her husband was an undocumented immigrant.[59] Rodriguez's campaign immediately refuted the claim, stating that her husband is a naturalized U.S. citizen and describing the tip as part of a "racist smear campaign" orchestrated by political opponents.[60] An investigation by the reporters uncovered evidence confirming his naturalized citizenship status, leading to no further substantiation of the allegation.[61] The incident highlighted early personal attacks on Rodriguez's family amid her 2026 campaign launch, though it did not gain traction beyond initial reporting.

Personal life

Family and relationships

Sara Rodriguez is married to an immigrant who became a naturalized U.S. citizen; the couple met salsa dancing and have been wed for approximately 19 years as of 2025.[62][63] They have two children, whom they are raising in Brookfield, Wisconsin.[64][63] The family also includes a dog named Chico.[62] In July 2025, shortly after Rodriguez launched her campaign for governor, opponents circulated a false claim that her husband was undocumented, which she publicly refuted by confirming his naturalized citizenship status.[65][61][66] No prior public relationships or additional family details have been disclosed by Rodriguez.[2]

Residences and community involvement

Sara Rodriguez resides in Brookfield, Wisconsin, located in Waukesha County.[67][2] She was raised in Brookfield, attending and graduating from Brookfield East High School.[1][6] Prior to her election as lieutenant governor, Rodriguez represented Wisconsin's 13th State Assembly District, which encompasses parts of Brookfield and surrounding areas in Waukesha and Milwaukee Counties.[4] In terms of community involvement, Rodriguez has served as a leader for a local Girl Scout troop, engaging directly with youth development activities in her area.[2][1] She is also a returned Peace Corps volunteer, having completed service abroad before returning to Wisconsin to build her career in public health.[2][1] These roles reflect her pre-political commitments to community service and family-oriented initiatives.

Electoral history

2020 Wisconsin State Assembly election

In the 2020 Wisconsin State Assembly election for District 13, Democratic challenger Sara Rodriguez, a registered nurse from Brookfield, defeated one-term Republican incumbent Rob Hutton.[68] The district encompasses parts of Waukesha County, including Brookfield and portions of Milwaukee County, areas that had leaned Republican in prior cycles.[68] The general election occurred on November 3, 2020, coinciding with the presidential contest. Rodriguez prevailed by a narrow margin of 555 votes out of 38,081 cast, marking one of the tightest races in the state assembly that year.[68][69]

Rodriguez's victory flipped the seat to Democratic control for the first time since redistricting, contributing to Democrats' net gain of one assembly seat statewide despite Republicans retaining a slim majority.[68] Hutton, who had held the seat since 2013 after winning a special election, conceded shortly after the results were certified.[68] Rodriguez was sworn in on January 4, 2021, beginning her term in the 105th Wisconsin Legislature.[70]

2022 Wisconsin Lieutenant Governor election

The Democratic primary election for lieutenant governor took place on August 9, 2022. State Assembly member Sara Rodriguez, a registered nurse with prior service in the Wisconsin Legislature since 2021, secured the nomination by defeating Peng Her, a public school teacher, with 354,260 votes (76.41%) to Her's 108,766 votes (23.46%).[71] Rodriguez's campaign emphasized her healthcare expertise and advocacy for working families amid economic challenges post-COVID-19.[19] The Republican primary, also on August 9, 2022, featured a crowded field of eight candidates, including former State Senator Roger Roth, a U.S. Air Force veteran and business owner, who emerged as the nominee with the highest vote share in the fragmented contest.) Roth positioned himself as a conservative focused on election integrity and fiscal restraint, aligning with the party's gubernatorial contender Tim Michels.[72] In the general election held on November 8, 2022, Rodriguez ran jointly with incumbent Democratic Governor Tony Evers against the Republican ticket of construction executive Tim Michels and Roth. The Evers-Rodriguez ticket prevailed narrowly statewide, garnering 1,269,305 votes (51.18%) to 1,210,737 votes (48.82%) for Michels-Roth, a margin of approximately 58,568 votes reflecting Wisconsin's competitive partisan divide.[73] Voter turnout exceeded 2.4 million, with Democrats benefiting from strong performance in urban and suburban areas like Milwaukee and Dane counties. Rodriguez's victory marked the continuation of Democratic control of the executive branch despite Republican gains in the state legislature. She was sworn in as Wisconsin's 46th lieutenant governor on January 3, 2023.[19]
