Sarah Dunant

Sarah Dunant (born 8 August 1950) is a British novelist, broadcaster, and critic specializing in historical fiction set during the Italian Renaissance, with her works examining women's experiences amid periods of artistic, religious, and political upheaval.[1][2] Educated in history at Newnham College, Cambridge, Dunant initially wrote thrillers, including collaborations under pseudonyms, before achieving acclaim with her Renaissance novels such as The Birth of Venus (2003), In the Company of the Courtesan (2006), and Sacred Hearts (2009), which became international bestsellers translated into over 30 languages.[3][4][5] Her oeuvre totals 12 novels, four of which were shortlisted for literary awards, and she has edited two collections of essays.[3] In broadcasting, she presented BBC programs including The Late Show and Nightwaves, and continues to deliver lectures on history, art, and gender for organizations like the Arts Society.[3][2] Dunant received an honorary doctorate from Oxford Brookes University for contributions to education and literature, and in 2024 was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Literature.[3] Her most recent novel, The Marchesa (2025), focuses on the life of Isabella d'Este.[2]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Sarah Dunant was born Linda Dunant on August 8, 1950, in London, England, to a middle-class family.[1][6] She was the daughter of David Dunant, who began his career as an airline steward of Welsh origin before advancing to a managerial position at British Airways, and Estelle Dunant (nÃ©e Joseph), a teacher.[1][6][7] Dunant's upbringing occurred in post-World War II London, amid Britain's economic recovery and social reconstruction in the 1950s and 1960s, where her family's stability reflected the era's emphasis on professional service and education.[1] Her father's aviation background provided indirect exposure to global travel and international perspectives, though specific family dynamics emphasized domestic routines typical of the time.[8][6] From childhood, Dunant exhibited an innate interest in history, describing it as being "in her blood" and recalling a lifelong passion cultivated through the cultural environment of mid-century Britain, including early encounters with historical narratives that foreshadowed her later narrative pursuits.[9] This personal affinity, rooted in observational engagement rather than formal ideology, laid groundwork for her eventual explorations of historical and social themes, distinct from overt political influences in her family setting.[9]

Formal education and early influences

Sarah Dunant attended the Godolphin and Latymer School, a private day school for girls in Hammersmith, London, completing her secondary education there before advancing to university.[7][10] She then enrolled at Newnham College, Cambridge, to study history, entering in the late 1960s during a period of expanding access for women to the university's resources.[11][3] In 1971, as a second- or third-year student, Dunant expressed appreciation for the supportive environment at Newnham, noting that "being a woman at Cambridge spoils you" due to the focused community and academic privileges it offered.[12] Her program emphasized rigorous historical analysis, which she later described as dispelling romanticized views through intensive scholarly training.[9] Dunant graduated in 1973 with a first-class honors degree in history, reflecting strong performance in a discipline centered on evidence-based inquiry into past events and causal mechanisms.[10][11] During her undergraduate years, she participated in theatre productions, an extracurricular pursuit that complemented her academic focus by developing skills in narrative presentation and public expression.[10] This formal grounding in historical methodology, conducted within the intellectually demanding setting of Cambridge's women's college, equipped her with tools for dissecting complex human motivations, prioritizing primary sources and logical reconstruction over interpretive biases prevalent in less empirical approaches.[3]

Broadcasting career

Entry into media

After completing her history degree at Newnham College, Cambridge, in 1972, Sarah Dunant pursued a brief career as an actress, followed by extended travels in Asia, including residence and employment in Japan, before returning to London in the mid-1970s.[13] Her entry into professional media occurred through determined applications to the BBC, which she described as increasingly bold and unconventional, ultimately securing her an initial role as a producer at BBC Radio in 1974.[7] This marked her transition from academic and exploratory pursuits to the structured yet fiercely competitive British broadcasting sector, where entry-level positions demanded both qualifications and tenacity.[14] Dunant's early motivations centered on engaging with arts and cultural discourse, leveraging her historical training to explore broader societal narratives beyond conventional outlets.[7] She worked as a producer until 1976, focusing on radio content that aligned with her intellectual interests, amid an industry where women comprised a small fraction of production staffâ€”less than 10% in key BBC roles during the decadeâ€”necessitating exceptional persistence to overcome gender-based hurdles in hiring and advancement.[14] Her success in this environment underscored a pragmatic approach to breaking in, prioritizing demonstrable skills over formal networks. By 1977, Dunant shifted to freelance status as a writer and broadcaster, a move that afforded autonomy in a period of expanding independent media opportunities while navigating economic pressures on public service broadcasting.[14] This freelance phase built on her BBC foundation, enabling selective projects that tested her capacity to challenge prevailing cultural orthodoxies through informed commentary, distinct from the institutional constraints of salaried employment.[15]

Key roles and programs at the BBC

Sarah Dunant joined the BBC in the 1980s, initially contributing to programs focused on media review and cultural commentary, before taking on prominent presenting roles in arts broadcasting during the 1990s.[11] One of her early involvements was as a panelist on Did You See...?, a BBC Two series running from 1980 to 1993 that featured discussions on the week's television output, hosted by figures like Ludovic Kennedy and Jeremy Paxman, emphasizing critical analysis of broadcast content.[16] Her most notable television role came with The Late Show, BBC Two's pioneering daily late-night arts program that debuted on 16 January 1989 and ran until 1995, where Dunant served as a lead presenter alongside contributors like Tracey MacLeod and Kirsty Wark.[17] The format combined live panel debates, interviews, and segments on literature, visual arts, music, and filmâ€”such as episodes examining novelists like Patricia Highsmith or architectural innovators like Frank Gehryâ€”prioritizing intellectual discourse over populist entertainment, with Dunant often steering conversations toward rigorous cultural critique.[18][19] In parallel, Dunant presented Night Waves on BBC Radio 3, a nightly program in the 1990s dedicated to in-depth discussions on arts, ideas, and culture, fostering extended explorations of literary and historical themes that aligned with her emerging interests in narrative analysis.[11] These roles established her as a voice for substantive arts engagement, contributing to the BBC's expansion of daily cultural programming amid the channel's shift toward more specialized late-evening slots, though specific audience metrics for her segments remain undocumented in public records.[17]

Literary career

Crime and mystery novels

Dunant began her literary career in the crime genre with two collaborative thrillers co-authored under the pseudonym Peter Dunant with Peter Busby, featuring the protagonist Marla Masterson, a resilient female operative entangled in espionage and high-stakes intrigue. Exterminating Angels, published in 1984, introduces Masterson navigating covert operations amid betrayal and survival imperatives, while Intensive Care (1986) escalates the tension through intensified personal and professional perils in a shadowy underworld. These early works employ a fast-paced narrative style rooted in realistic depictions of risk and deception, emphasizing causal chains of human decision-making under duress rather than abstracted moralizing.[20][21] Transitioning to solo authorship, Dunant developed the Hannah Wolfe series, centering on a tough, independent private investigator in 1990s London who confronts urban crime through pragmatic investigation and physical confrontations. The trilogy comprises Birth Marks (1991), probing surrogacy and identity fraud; Fatlands (1993), delving into animal rights extremism and corporate malfeasance; and Under My Skin (1994), examining infidelity, addiction, and vigilante justice. Wolfe's character embodies gritty realism, as she maneuvers through London's underbellyâ€”facing assaults, ethical quandaries, and systemic corruptionâ€”grounded in observable patterns of human opportunism and vulnerability in metropolitan environments.[22][23] In standalone novels like Transgressions (1997), Dunant shifted toward psychological depth, following Elizabeth Skvorecky, a Czech-British translator whose immersion in a violent crime manuscript blurs into her own experiences of stalking, fantasy, and boundary-testing encounters. The narrative dissects transgression as a interplay of impulse and consequence, portraying violence not as sensationalism but as an emergent property of unchecked desires and isolation, with Skvorecky's arc highlighting empirical risks of urban anonymity and interpersonal trust erosion. This evolution from ensemble action to introspective solo probes reflects Dunant's refinement of moral ambiguity, where protagonists' agency emerges from navigating tangible threats without reliance on external ideologies.[24][25]

Historical fiction

Sarah Dunant's historical fiction, beginning in the early 2000s, centers on Renaissance Italy, drawing on extensive archival research to depict the era's social, artistic, and political landscapes through the lens of women's constrained yet resilient lives. Her novels integrate scholarly insights into gender dynamics, emphasizing how biological realities such as unmitigated childbirth pains and high maternal mortality shaped female agency amid patriarchal structures and religious doctrines. This approach avoids romanticization, portraying power as contingent on familial alliances, economic leverage, and intellectual maneuvering rather than inherent equality.[2][26] The Birth of Venus (2003) follows Alessandra Cecchi, a young woman in fifteenth-century Florence during the Savonarolan upheavals following Lorenzo de' Medici's death, as she navigates artistic patronage, forbidden desires, and familial duties in a city gripped by religious fervor and political instability. The narrative highlights the era's artistic ferment, with Alessandra's secret commissioning of a fresco depicting Botticelli's Birth of Venus underscoring women's indirect influence on cultural production amid sumptuary laws and moral crackdowns. Dunant grounds the story in primary sources like contemporary diaries, illustrating causal chains where economic prosperity from Medici banking enabled artistic innovation but also intensified scrutiny on female virtue.[27][28][29] Subsequent works expand this focus: In the Company of the Courtesan (2006) traces the survival of courtesan Fiammetta and her dwarf companion Bucino after the 1527 Sack of Rome, relocating to Venice where Fiammetta rebuilds her status through intellect and alliances in a republic valuing mercantile cunning over noble birth. The novel details courtesans' economic roles, supported by guild records showing their literacy and property ownership as buffers against destitution, while depicting the physical toll of aging and disease without modern palliatives. Sacred Hearts (2009), set in the 1570 convent of Santa Caterina in Ferrara during Counter-Reformation tensions, portrays novice Serafina's forced enclosure and bond with herbalist Zuana, revealing convents as refuges and prisons for noblewomen, with over 80% of Italian convents housing unwilling entrants per historical demography. Dunant incorporates convent archives to show how herbal medicine and music offered limited autonomy, constrained by vows and inquisitorial oversight.[30][31][32] The Borgia duologyâ€”Blood & Beauty (2013) and In the Name of the Family (2017)â€”shifts to papal intrigue, centering Rodrigo Borgia's ascension as Alexander VI in 1492 and his children's machinations, including Cesare's conquests and Lucrezia's marriages as diplomatic tools. Dunant eschews sensational myths, relying on Vatican dispatches and Machiavelli's observations to depict unvarnished corruption: simony in papal elections, incest rumors rooted in power consolidation, and syphilis epidemics ravaging courts due to absent antibiotics. Women's roles emerge in Lucrezia's navigation of serial annulments and fertility pressures, reflecting Renaissance dynastic imperatives where female heirs bolstered legitimacy amid 30-50% infant mortality rates. The sequel incorporates NiccolÃ² Machiavelli's Florentine embassy, grounding familial betrayals in documented events like Cesare's 1502 campaigns.[33][34][35] Dunant's most recent novel, The Marchesa (2025), profiles Isabella d'Este, Marchesa of Mantua (1474â€“1539), as a pioneering art collector and diplomat who amassed over 2,000 artworks and jewels through strategic marriages and patronage, influencing figures like Leonardo da Vinci amid French invasions and inter-state rivalries. Drawing on d'Este's 2,000+ surviving letters, the book illustrates her wielding "soft power" via cultural diplomacy, such as borrowing artworks for prestige, while enduring eight pregnancies and widowhood's vulnerabilities without legal inheritance rights. This work extends Dunant's motif of women leveraging intellect against bodily and institutional limits, corroborated by inventories from Mantua's ducal archives.[36][37][38]

Non-fiction works

Dunant's non-fiction output is limited to two edited anthologies published in the mid-1990s, both issued by Virago Press and reflecting her background in broadcasting and cultural commentary through collections of essays by diverse contributors. These works prioritize analytical essays over narrative fiction, examining societal tensions via contributions from academics, writers, and critics, often drawing on empirical observations of language, culture, and psychology rather than speculative interpretation.[39] The War of the Words: The Political Correctness Debate, edited by Dunant and published in 1994, compiles essays critiquing the rise of political correctness in Western discourse, particularly in academia and media. Contributors, including linguists and cultural analysts, dissect its implications for free speech, liberalism, and identity politics, with discussions on how evolving norms redefine acceptable language and behavior across generations. The volume challenges assumptions of PC as mere politeness, highlighting tensions between inclusivity efforts and potential censorship, grounded in case studies from educational and public spheres.[40][41] In collaboration with historian Roy Porter, Dunant co-edited The Age of Anxiety in 1996, featuring ten essays from prominent intellectuals exploring late-20th-century societal unease, including diminished personal agency amid rapid technological and economic shifts. Essays address psychological and historical roots of collective anxiety, using data from social trends and historical parallels to argue for a pervasive loss of control in modern life, distinct from earlier eras' optimism. This work extends Dunant's interest in empirical cultural diagnosis, linking individual malaise to broader structural causes without prescriptive solutions.[42][43]

Critical reception and controversies

General critical assessment

Sarah Dunant's body of work, spanning crime thrillers and historical fiction, garners praise for its accessible prose and emphasis on female agency amid historical constraints. Reviewers commend her Renaissance-era novels for evoking the era's sensory and social textures, drawing parallels to Hilary Mantel's approach in integrating bodily realism with political intrigue.[44] This focus on women's interior livesâ€”amid events like the Medici rule or convent dynamicsâ€”lends emotional depth, supported by extensive archival research that anchors fictional elements to documented customs and figures.[45] Her transition from taut psychological thrillers to expansive historical narratives demonstrates sustained narrative vigor, with critics noting seamless genre evolution that retains thriller-like momentum in plotting.[46] Empirical indicators include commercial viability, as evidenced by titles like In the Company of the Courtesan debuting at number five on the New York Times bestseller list in 2006, reflecting broad reader engagement across markets.[47] Such success underscores her skill in balancing scholarly detail with page-turning accessibility, though the format prioritizes dramatic causality over exhaustive historiography. Assessments of her fact-fiction interplay highlight strengths in "substantiated historical fiction," where invented protagonists illuminate real socio-economic pressures, yet observe that narrative libertiesâ€”such as attributing modern psychological insights to period actorsâ€”may foster reader conflations of speculative motives with causal historical drivers.[45] This approach enhances immediacy but invites scrutiny for potentially oversimplifying complex institutional or cultural evolutions, as when fictional convent intrigues stand in for broader Reformation impacts. Overall, Dunant's oeuvre prioritizes interpretive vividness over strict chronicle, earning acclaim for revitalizing under-examined female histories while prompting debates on fiction's interpretive bounds in public historical understanding.[48]

Awards and honors

Sarah Dunant was awarded the Crime Writers' Association (CWA) Silver Dagger in 1993 for her crime novel Fatlands, recognizing its excellence in suspense and plotting within the genre.[49] This accolade, conferred by a panel of crime fiction experts, elevated her standing among thriller writers and correlated with increased sales and international distribution of her Hannah Wolfe series, as evidenced by subsequent publisher promotions highlighting the win.[50] Her early crime novels achieved multiple shortlistings for the CWA Gold Dagger, the association's top honor for outstanding crime fiction, including Birth Marks (1991) and Transgressions (1997).[5] These nominations underscored the technical merits of her investigative narratives but did not culminate in a win, reflecting competitive fields dominated by established procedural styles.[6] In historical fiction, Sacred Hearts (2009) was shortlisted for the inaugural Walter Scott Prize for Historical Fiction in 2010, a Â£25,000 award judged on authenticity and narrative depth in depicting past eras.[51] Though not victorious, the recognition affirmed her research-driven approach to Renaissance Italy, boosting academic interest and adaptations in educational contexts.[52] Dunant was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Literature in 2024, an honor granted to 29 writers for sustained contributions to British literature, selected via peer nomination and review.[53] Additionally, she received an honorary Doctor of Letters from Oxford Brookes University for services to literature and education, where she had taught creative writing.[11] These distinctions have supported her shift toward public lecturing and cultural commentary, expanding her influence beyond fiction sales.

Notable controversies

Dunant's 1997 thriller Transgressions elicited significant controversy within feminist literary circles for its portrayal of sexual violence, particularly a pivotal scene in which the protagonist, a survivor of rape named Elizabeth, seduces her assailant to mitigate further harm. Critics, including some feminist scholars, argued that this depiction romanticized female masochism and perpetuated harmful stereotypes by suggesting women derive agency or empowerment through submission to male aggression, thereby undermining anti-rape advocacy and betraying core feminist tenets against victim-blaming.[54][55] This interpretation positioned the novel as potentially reinforcing the very patriarchal "syntax of violence" it ostensibly critiqued, with concerns that such narratives could influence real-world perceptions of trauma responses among actual victims.[55] In response, analyses of the work highlight Dunant's deliberate engagement with the raw mechanics of power imbalances in sexual encounters, positing that the scene exposes the pragmatic, non-idealized survival tactics available to women in asymmetrical confrontations rather than prescribing them as normative or desirable.[54] Far from anti-feminist, the narrative is interpreted as a first-principles examination of causality in gendered violenceâ€”where biological and social imperatives drive improvised agency amid existential threatsâ€”challenging sanitized portrayals that prioritize ideological purity over empirical realism in human behavior. Right-leaning commentators, though less prominently engaged, have echoed this by critiquing overly systemic framings of victimhood in favor of recognizing individual resilience and strategic adaptation, viewing the novel's unflinching realism as a counter to narratives that attribute all agency deficits to structural forces alone.[55] In her later historical fiction, such as The Birth of Venus (2003) and Sacred Hearts (2008), Dunant has faced milder scrutiny over the balance between depicting women's historical subjugation and emphasizing their navigational agency within patriarchal constraints, with some observers questioning whether recurrent motifs of adversity inadvertently amplify a victim-centric lens at the expense of unvarnished accounts of personal triumph or moral ambiguity. These debates underscore tensions in the genre between fidelity to archival power dynamicsâ€”often empirically skewed toward female vulnerabilityâ€”and modern imperatives for uplifting representations, though Dunant's defenders maintain her characterizations derive from primary sources illustrating causal pathways of resilience rather than contrived empowerment.[5]

Views and public engagement

Feminist and cultural commentary

Dunant has articulated a view of women's historical agency as rooted in pragmatic navigation of patriarchal constraints, emphasizing resilience and strategic adaptation rather than passive subjugation. In discussions of Renaissance Italy, she describes women exerting influence within limited "wiggle room," engaging in family politics and survival tactics that reflect a form of understated heroism amid male-dominated power structures.[56][57] This perspective counters narratives of uniform victimhood by highlighting diverse female experiences, from convent autonomy offering alternatives to marriage to creative pursuits in marginalized roles like courtesans or artists.[57] Her commentary extends to feminist efforts in cultural recovery, praising initiatives like Virago Press, founded in 1973, for empirically redressing gender imbalances in publishing by reprinting overlooked women authors such as Willa Cather and Rebecca West, thereby reshaping literary canons through subversion symbolized by the apple of forbidden knowledge.[58] Dunant observes persistent disparities, including underrepresentation of women in literary reviews and broadcast discussions, as evidenced by critiques of segments like BBC's Today programme lacking female perspectives as recently as 2013.[58] On gender dynamics, Dunant contends that patriarchal power structures foster male pomposity and conversational dominance, where men interrupt or overlook women, but feminism mitigates this by promoting humor and mutual engagement, ultimately benefiting men through reduced self-importance.[59] She frames these observations disinterestedly, drawing causal links between historical constraints and modern interactions without presuming inevitable oppression, as seen in her portrayal of women's varied agency across convent, court, and domestic spheres.[57][59]

Activism and public statements

Dunant has publicly advocated for the legalization of marijuana, drawing on personal experience and policy arguments. In a November 2015 essay for BBC Radio 4's A Point of View, she described cannabis as "a manifestly less dangerous drug than tobacco or alcohol," noted her own intermittent use over 40 years, and praised U.S. state-level legalization efforts as a "triumph for the American Dream" that could generate revenue for public services like education or healthcare while reducing crime associated with prohibition.[60] On reproductive rights, Dunant commented on the U.S. Supreme Court's June 2022 overturning of Roe v. Wade. In a BBC Radio 4 A Point of View broadcast, she reflected on the decision's implications by recounting a 1970s road trip across America during the original 1973 ruling, portraying the loss of federal abortion protections as a regression from an era of expanding women's autonomy.[61] As a Newnham College, Cambridge alumna (class of 1972), Dunant has supported women's higher education through institutional advocacy. In 2024, she endorsed the college's Shaping Newnham's Future fundraising campaign as its History Champion, emphasizing the role of single-sex institutions in launching women into leadership amid ongoing debates over gender-segregated education.[62] Dunant frequently delivers public lectures and broadcasts that stress rigorous, evidence-based interpretations of history, particularly challenging sanitized narratives of the past. Her talks, available through her professional site, interconnect historical research with contemporary politics and gender dynamics, urging audiences to confront unvarnished causal realities over ideological revisions.[63] For instance, in BBC appearances, she has critiqued judging historical figures by modern standards without contextual evidence, as in her 2022 essay questioning whether "the right side of history" demands ahistorical moralism.[64]

Critiques of her positions

Critics within feminist literary circles have challenged Dunant's handling of sexual violence and female agency, particularly accusing her 1997 novel Transgressions of promoting masochistic tropes that undermine core feminist opposition to rape and patriarchal exploitation.[54] The controversy stemmed from the protagonist's complex psychological response to violation, interpreted by some as eroticizing trauma and betraying second-wave feminist tenets against victim-blaming or female complicity in abuse.[65] This critique, voiced in academic analyses, highlighted tensions in Dunant's broader feminist commentary, suggesting her narrative choices risked reinforcing rather than dismantling stereotypes of women's passivity or enjoyment in subjugation.[55] Dunant's advocacy for abortion rights, expressed through public reflections on events like the overturning of Roe v. Wade and Ireland's 2018 referendum, has drawn implicit pushback from pro-life perspectives emphasizing empirical data on fetal development and post-abortion psychological impacts.[66] [67] Conservative commentators, while not targeting Dunant individually in prominent critiques, have broadly contested such positions by citing studies showing alternatives like adoption correlating with lower regret rates among women compared to abortion (e.g., 5-10% vs. up to 20% in longitudinal surveys), arguing that advocacy overlooks causal links between procedure and elevated risks of depression or relational strain. These counterarguments prioritize biological realities of gestation over autonomy narratives, a viewpoint underrepresented in mainstream media coverage of figures like Dunant due to institutional biases favoring progressive framings. In historical interpretations, some scholars debate Dunant's selective emphasis on gendered oppression in Renaissance settings, contending it privileges ideological lenses over multifaceted causal factors like economic pressures or individual agency in shaping outcomes for women. While her works are praised for research depth, detractors note potential anachronistic projections of modern feminism onto eras where biological dimorphism and kinship structures exerted undeniable influences on social roles, potentially skewing causal realism in favor of victimhood-centric readings.[45] Such academic tensions underscore broader critiques of scholarship that, per conservative historians, underweights primary archival evidence of female resilience or strategic adaptation amid patriarchal norms, instead amplifying systemic subjugation without equivalent scrutiny of parallel male constraints.[68]

Personal life

Family and relationships

Sarah Dunant has two daughters, born in 1987 and 1990.[69] She has never married, having cohabited unmarried with the father of her childrenâ€”the only partner with whom she lived for longer than a year.[59] The relationship ended in separation, after which Dunant lived apart from the father while raising her daughters in London.[70] Dunant has exercised discretion over details of her private partnerships, with no public identification of the children's father or further relational history disclosed in available sources.[59][70]

Experiences shaping her work

Dunant forwent epidural anesthesia and other pain relief during the births of her two children in 1987 and 1990, intentionally subjecting herself to the unbuffered physical intensity to empirically comprehend the ordeals of pre-anesthetic-era women, which she cited in 2025 as directly shaping her literary representations of Renaissance childbirth.[69] She characterized the pain as "excruciating" and "agonizing," akin to expelling a watermelon-sized object, with no ensuing complications but profound immediacy that eluded theoretical simulation.[69] Immediately following each delivery, Dunant recorded the sequential phases of labor pain to preserve raw details for her work, prioritizing this causal firsthand data over secondary historical empathy to ensure authentic physiological portrayals in her fiction.[69] Her approach extended to locational realism through extensive travels and immersions in Italy, where she acquired deep sensory acquaintance with Renaissance-era sites like Florence, informing the tangible environmental and societal textures of her narratives via direct observation rather than abstracted conjecture.[71][9]

Bibliography

Mystery novels

Dunant co-authored the Marla Masterson crime series with Peter Busby under the joint pseudonym Peter Dunant.[15] The series includes Exterminating Angels (1983) and Intensive Care (1986).[72] Her solo mystery output began with the standalone thriller Snow Storms in a Hot Climate (1988).[73] Dunant then wrote the Hannah Wolfe series, featuring a female private investigator in London: Birth Marks (1991), Fatlands (1993), and Under My Skin (1995).[15] Subsequent standalone mystery novels are Transgressions (1997) and Mapping the Edge (1999).[74]

Historical novels

Dunant's historical novels are set in Renaissance Italy and published as follows:
  • The Birth of Venus (2003)[48]
  • In the Company of the Courtesan (2006)[6]
  • Sacred Hearts (2009)[75]
  • Blood & Beauty (2013), the first installment of the Borgias duology[6]
  • In the Name of the Family (2017), the second installment of the Borgias duology[76]
  • The Marchesa (2025)[36]
These works have appeared in international editions, including translations into multiple languages.[77]

Non-fiction

Dunant's non-fiction publications are limited to two edited essay collections from the mid-1990s, reflecting her background in broadcasting and cultural critique. The War of the Words: The Political Correctness Debate, edited by Dunant and published by Virago Press in 1994, compiles contributions examining the political correctness controversy.[78] In 1996, she co-edited The Age of Anxiety with Roy Porter, also issued by Virago Press, which addresses pervasive cultural and psychological anxieties of the era.[43] These volumes represent her sole ventures into non-fiction authorship, with no subsequent works in the genre identified.[6]
