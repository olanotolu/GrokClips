Sarah Haskins (comedian)


Sarah Haskins (born August 3, 1979) is an American comedian, writer, and producer noted for her satirical commentary on gender-targeted advertising and media portrayals of women.[1] A Chicago native who graduated from Harvard University with a degree in American history and literature, Haskins gained prominence through her creation and performance of the "Target Women" segments on Current TV's Infomania from 2007 to 2010, where she dissected commercials and cultural tropes aimed at female consumers with deadpan humor.[2][3] Her segments critiqued the superficiality and stereotypes in products like skincare, cleaning supplies, and dating advice, often highlighting manipulative marketing tactics while avoiding overt ideological preaching.[4] Following Infomania, Haskins transitioned to writing and producing, contributing to television shows such as Black-ish and Trophy Wife, and co-writing feature films including the critically acclaimed high school comedy Booksmart (2019) with frequent collaborator Emily Halpern, as well as 80 for Brady (2023).[1][5] These works demonstrate her versatility in blending sharp wit with character-driven narratives, earning nominations for awards like the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay for Booksmart.[6]

Early life and education

Childhood in Chicago

Sarah Haskins was born on August 3, 1979, in Chicago, Illinois.[1] She grew up in the city, attending the Francis W. Parker School located in the Lincoln Park neighborhood.[2][7] Haskins was raised in a separated household, where her mother worked outside the home.[8] This setup diverged from the polished images of homemaking and family life prevalent in contemporary television advertising, which she later recalled as an "elaborate fantasy designed to make us all feel bad for not folding our laundry right away and sitting down to perfect home-cooked meals in a spotless house."[8] Such early discrepancies between personal experience and media representations contributed to her developing awareness of advertising tropes during childhood.[8]

University years and initial comedy involvement

Haskins attended Harvard College, majoring in American history and literature, and graduated in 2001.[9][10] At Harvard, she engaged in limited theater activities but concentrated on improvisational comedy as a member of the Immediate Gratification Players (IGP), the university's student-run improv troupe founded in 1986.[2][11][7] Her prior exposure to Chicago's Second City performances, attended before college, fueled her enthusiasm for improv formats emphasizing spontaneous wit and ensemble dynamics.[12] These university experiences marked Haskins' initial foray into comedy, distinct from later professional endeavors, as she experimented with sketch-like improv scenes in campus settings during the late 1990s and early 2000s.[2][13] Upon graduating, Haskins relocated to Chicago, her hometown, to continue amateur improv pursuits in the city's vibrant theater community, including minor performances at local venues that extended her Harvard-honed techniques without immediate commercial breakthroughs.[9][11] This post-academic phase, spanning several years in the early 2000s, represented a bridge from student-led experimentation to sustained scene involvement.[9]

Professional career

Early comedy work and move to Los Angeles

After graduating from Harvard College in 2001, Haskins returned to her native Chicago and immersed herself in the local improv and sketch comedy scene. She performed with prominent ensembles including the ImprovOlympic (iO Theater) and Second City, honing her skills in improvisational and satirical performance.[14][4] These experiences involved both stage work and touring, as she joined the Second City National Touring Company, where she developed foundational techniques in character-driven humor and quick-witted commentary.[15] In 2007, Haskins relocated to Los Angeles, prompted by a full-time writing position at the emerging cable network Current TV.[11][10] This move represented a transition from Chicago's theater-centric improv circuit to scripted television production, allowing her to apply her satirical edge to video content and broader media formats. Initially serving as a writer and contributor, she focused on crafting segments that blended observational humor with cultural critique, though without immediate widespread recognition.[16] The opportunity at Current TV, a platform emphasizing user-generated and innovative programming, provided her first sustained exposure to national audiences and professional writing workflows.[11]

Target Women segments on InfoMania

"Target Women" originated as a recurring segment on the Current TV satirical news program InfoMania, where Sarah Haskins served as writer, host, and performer from 2007 to 2010.[17] The format involved Haskins presenting montages of television advertisements and media clips directed at female consumers, followed by her deadpan commentary dissecting the underlying gender stereotypes and manipulative marketing strategies.[18] Segments typically ran several minutes, blending archival footage with Haskins' scripted narration to expose patterns in how products were pitched to women.[19] Early episodes focused on everyday consumer goods, such as the inaugural "Target Women: Yogurt," which aired in 2008 and lampooned yogurt commercials portraying the product as an essential, almost spiritual staple for women, complete with pseudo-empowering narratives of self-care and femininity.[19] [20] Subsequent installments targeted wedding-themed television programming, critiquing the genre's emphasis on extravagant ceremonies and bridal consumerism as a path to fulfillment.[19] Beauty product ads formed another core theme, with segments ridiculing anti-aging treatments like Botox injections and invasive devices including vibrating mascara, neck slimmers, and toning shoes marketed as quick fixes for physical insecurities.[19] [21] Later segments expanded to pharmaceutical advertising, notably a 2009 episode addressing direct-to-consumer promotions of medications for menopausal and aging women, highlighting hyperbolic claims of restored vitality and youthfulness.[17] Production occurred in collaboration with InfoMania's team, utilizing the show's on-air and online platforms to distribute content, which contributed to the series' role as a milestone in Haskins' career by establishing her satirical voice on gender-targeted media.[22] The segments concluded alongside InfoMania's run in 2011, marking the end of this phase of her television work.

Film and television writing post-2011

Following the conclusion of her work on InfoMania, Haskins transitioned to screenwriting, co-writing the screenplay for the romantic comedy-drama Celeste & Jesse Forever (2012) with Rashida Jones.[23] The film, directed by Lee Toland Krieger, follows a divorcing couple navigating post-marital friendship and starred Jones and Andy Samberg.[23] Haskins then contributed to television, serving as a writer and co-executive producer on the ABC sitcom black-ish starting in 2014.[24] She received writing credits for episodes such as "Their Eyes Were Watching Screens" (Season 3, Episode 18, aired March 22, 2017) and held the co-executive producer role for 24 episodes across Seasons 3 and 4 (2016â€“2017).[25][24] In features, Haskins co-wrote the original screenplay for Booksmart (2019) alongside her frequent collaborator Emily Halpern, with additional contributions from Susanna Fogel and Katie Silberman.[26] Directed by Olivia Wilde, the coming-of-age comedy depicts two high school overachievers cramming a lifetime of fun into one night, earning critical acclaim and nominations including a BAFTA for Original Screenplay.[27] Haskins and Halpern later penned the screenplay for 80 for Brady (2023), a sports comedy directed by Kyle Marvin and based on a true story of four elderly friends traveling to the Super Bowl to see Tom Brady.[28] The film starred Jane Fonda, Lily Tomlin, Rita Moreno, and Sally Field, grossing over $39 million at the box office.[28] In May 2024, Haskins and Halpern sold an original pitch for a mother-daughter comedy to 20th Century Studios, described as featuring two strong female leads, with Gloria Sanchez Productions attached to produce.[29] Plot details remain under wraps as of that date.[29]

Satirical content and themes

Critique of advertising and gender stereotypes

In her Target Women segments on InfoMania from 2007 to 2010, Sarah Haskins systematically deconstructed television advertisements directed at female consumers, focusing on how they exploited insecurities about appearance and body image through unsubstantiated causal claims.[21] For instance, in the "Beauty Contraptions" episode aired in 2010, she analyzed products like vibrating mascara, which advertisements from brands such as CoverGirl promoted as self-applying tools that mechanically enhanced lash length and volume via vibration, implying a direct causal mechanism for superior results over manual application.[30] Haskins highlighted the empirical implausibility of such mechanisms, noting that the device's vibration primarily aids product distribution rather than biologically altering lashes, a tactic rooted in marketing hype rather than verifiable cosmetic efficacy.[21] Similarly, Haskins critiqued shape-up shoes, exemplified by Skechers Shape-Ups launched in 2009, whose ads claimed that the rocker-bottom sole would tone leg and gluteal muscles through everyday walking, promising measurable body reshaping without exercise.[21] This portrayal linked passive consumption to active physiological change, disregarding biomechanical evidence that such instability primarily increases joint stress rather than muscle activation sufficient for toning, as later substantiated by regulatory scrutiny leading to a 2012 settlement where Skechers paid $40 million for deceptive advertising.[21] Her analysis extended to yogurt campaigns, such as those for Yoplait in the late 2000s, which tied low-calorie consumption to effortless weight loss and slimmer figures, framing dairy snacks as causal agents for metabolic transformation despite nutritional data showing minimal impact on body composition without caloric deficit.[9] These critiques underscored verifiable patterns in 2000s gender-targeted advertising, where women's product ads disproportionately emphasized decorative and domestic roles, with meta-analyses of over 64 studies from the era revealing that female portrayals were 2.5 times more likely to involve beauty enhancement or household tasks than male counterparts, perpetuating stereotypes over reflective consumer realities.[31] U.S. advertising spend on beauty and personal care, which targeted women at rates exceeding 80% of the $50 billion annual market by 2008, amplified these tactics, correlating with consumer behavior studies indicating heightened purchase intent from insecurity-driven appeals rather than product utility.[32] Haskins' method involved juxtaposing ad footage with deadpan narration to expose the disconnect between promised outcomes and empirical outcomes, such as the failure of diamond jewelry campaigns (e.g., De Beers' ongoing "A Diamond is Forever" from 1947, refreshed in the 2000s) to deliver intrinsic value beyond manufactured scarcity.[9]

Political and cultural commentary

Haskins incorporated political angles into her "Target Women" segments by analogizing campaign strategies to consumer advertising, particularly during the 2008 U.S. presidential election. In a July 2008 installment, she satirized both Democratic and Republican efforts to court female voters, equating references to shows like Army Wives by Barack Obama and John McCain to pandering tactics akin to yogurt or wedding media ads, while critiquing the media's reduction of first ladies Michelle Obama and Cindy McCain to archetypal roles under disproportionate scrutiny.[33] This segment highlighted perceived superficiality in addressing women as a uniform bloc focused on "family issues," in contrast to male-oriented discourse on national security. Following Sarah Palin's vice-presidential nomination, Haskins targeted the "hockey mom" persona in a dedicated skit, portraying it as a media-amplified fusion of homemaking and frontier toughness that exemplified tokenized appeals to women rather than substantive policy engagement.[34][8] Such commentary often mocked tropes in conservative-leaning narratives, like rugged maternalism, as contrived empowerment narratives intertwined with consumerism, aligning with her broader feminist lens that viewed political marketing as exploiting gendered insecurities for votes much like products for self-improvement. While occasionally critiquing bipartisan tactics, Haskins' satire predominantly challenged portrayals reinforcing traditional gender roles or market-driven "empowerment," normalizing progressive framings of liberation through cultural deconstruction over consumption or domesticity.[22] This approach echoed mainstream media emphases on social conditioning as the primary driver of gender disparities in politics and advertising, sidelining empirical patternsâ€”such as women's higher average responsiveness to relational and security-oriented messaging rooted in evolved preferencesâ€”observable in voter data and marketing analytics from the era.[33] Her work thus contributed to cultural discourse by amplifying left-leaning skepticism of biological or market-realist explanations for gendered political engagement, though sources praising it often stemmed from outlets with documented progressive biases.[8]

Evolution of style and collaborations

Haskins' early comedic style emerged from her improvisational training at Chicago's Second City and Harvard University's Immediate Gratification Players, fostering a spontaneous, character-focused approach that emphasized quick-witted ensemble dynamics.[35][11] This groundwork transitioned into her solo-authored "Target Women" segments from 2007 to 2010, where she employed a snarky, self-deprecating on-camera persona to dissect advertising tropes aimed at women through monologue-style satire.[9] Following the end of "Target Women," Haskins shifted toward collaborative screenwriting, most notably partnering with Emily Halpernâ€”a fellow Harvard alumnaâ€”starting with an initial draft of Booksmart in 2009, which underwent multiple revisions before its 2019 release.[35][36] This partnership marked a stylistic pivot from performative sketches to co-authored scripts, adapting her adorkable critique into integrated narrative elements driven by character arcs and relational humor.[35] Over the subsequent decade, Haskins' approach matured from concise, viral-oriented 2000s content to fuller-length features in the 2020s, such as 80 for Brady co-written in 2022 and released in 2023, broadening her humor to encompass ensemble-driven stories of female bonds while embedding satirical observations on societal expectations.[35] Her improv roots continued to influence this evolution, informing a preference for organic joke integrationâ€”"the strongest jokes are germane to both story and character"â€”over standalone punchlines, as evidenced in post-2011 television contributions like Trophy Wife (2013â€“2014) and subsequent films.[35][36] Key collaborations with Halpern extended into 2024, including the sale of a mother-daughter comedy pitch to 20th Century Studios on May 16, highlighting sustained adaptation of her foundational media-savvy wit into structured, multi-character comedies.[29] This progression reflects a deliberate move from solo, topical rants to layered, collaborative narratives that leverage improv-honed spontaneity within scripted frameworks.[9]

Reception and impact

Achievements and positive appraisals

Haskins' "Target Women" segments on Infomania achieved viral success shortly after their 2008 debut, with the inaugural episode on yogurt marketing becoming an online hit that drew widespread media attention for its incisive feminist satire.[4] The series was praised in outlets like Mother Jones for innovating in comedic critiques of gender-targeted advertising, establishing Haskins as a key voice in media analysis of stereotypes.[4] In television writing, Haskins earned a 2017 Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Comedy Series as co-executive producer on Black-ish, recognizing her contributions to the show's acclaimed seasons.[6] Her film work further highlighted her success; co-writing Booksmart (2019) with Emily Halpern yielded critical acclaim, including a 97% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes and nominations for BAFTA's Best Original Screenplay and the Writers Guild of America Award for Original Screenplay.[37] [6] [38] The screenplay was later ranked 69th on the WGA's list of the 101 greatest of the 21st century.[39] Haskins co-wrote 80 for Brady (2023), which grossed $39.3 million domestically against a $28 million budget, exceeding opening weekend projections with $12.5 million and securing the number-two spot at the box office.[40] [41] Critics and industry observers have credited her satirical style with influencing subsequent works in women-focused comedy, elevating discussions on advertising and cultural tropes.[4]

Criticisms and counterarguments

Some observers have argued that Haskins' "Target Women" segments exhibit selective outrage by concentrating exclusively on advertising tropes aimed at women, such as beauty products and domestic roles, while neglecting analogous manipulative tactics in male-targeted marketing, like those promoting rugged individualism or status symbols in automotive and sports ads. For example, a 2009 commentary on gendered food advertising suggested the format's one-sidedness warranted a parallel "Target: Men" series to examine how brands exploit male consumers' preferences for high-protein or convenience foods without equivalent scrutiny. [42] This focus has drawn counterarguments that Haskins' critiques reinforce a narrative of advertising as systematically patriarchal, potentially dismissing free-market explanations where gender-differentiated campaigns reflect empirically observed purchasing behaviors rooted in biological and cultural factors, rather than top-down oppression. However, such rebuttals remain underrepresented in mainstream discourse, with limited direct engagement from conservative commentators, who may view the satire as ideologically aligned with left-leaning cultural critiques lacking broader empirical balance on consumer agency. Haskins' humor has also been characterized as niche or preachy by some, limiting its crossover appeal beyond progressive audiences attuned to feminist media analysis. Her co-created ABC sitcom Trophy Wife (2013â€“2014), which extended her witty take on gender dynamics in family life, earned critical praise for subverting stereotypes but averaged low viewership, resulting in cancellation after one 22-episode season on May 8, 2014. Analysts attributed this to its quirky, targeted sensibility failing to resonate widely, positioning it among "canceled-too-soon" comedies that prioritize sharp observation over mass-market bombast. [43] [44]

Personal life

Marriage and family

Sarah Haskins married writer and director Geoffrey Edwards on August 6, 2010.[1] Edwards is the son of filmmaker Blake Edwards and his first wife, actress Patricia Walker, and the stepson of actress Julie Andrews.[45] The couple's relationship, involving an age gap of approximately two decades and Edwards' prior marriages, partially inspired the premise of their co-created ABC sitcom Trophy Wife, though elements were adjusted for the series.[11] Haskins and Edwards have one child together, a daughter born in late 2010.[2] Haskins is also stepmother to Edwards' two children from previous marriages; as of 2013, they were ages 9 and 19.[45] The family resides in Los Angeles, where Haskins has been based for much of her professional career following earlier work in Chicago.[46] Limited public details are available regarding their domestic life, respecting the couple's preference for privacy on personal matters.[2]

Residence and current activities

Haskins resides in Los Angeles, California, having relocated there in October 2007 for professional opportunities in the entertainment industry.[10] This location aligns with her ongoing ties to Hollywood screenwriting and production hubs.[2] As of 2025, Haskins pursues selective writing projects, including a mother-daughter comedy pitch co-developed with Emily Halpern and sold to 20th Century Studios in May 2024, with Gloria Sanchez attached as producer.[29] Her public activities remain low-profile compared to her earlier on-screen work, emphasizing behind-the-scenes collaboration over performance.[47] This shift coincides with a focus on family responsibilities, though specific details on non-professional engagements are limited in available records.
