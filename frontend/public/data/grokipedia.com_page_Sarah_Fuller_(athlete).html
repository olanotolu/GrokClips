Sarah Fuller (athlete)


Sarah Fuller is an American athlete from Wylie, Texas, best known for her roles as a goalkeeper on the Vanderbilt University women's soccer team and as a kicker for the university's football team in 2020.[1] Fuller joined the Vanderbilt soccer program as a freshman in 2017 but saw limited action in her first three seasons, appearing in just two games before earning the starting goalkeeper position as a senior in the fall of 2020.[1] In that breakout year, she started all 12 games, posting an 8-3-0 record with three shutouts and a 1.02 goals-against average, while contributing one assist in the Southeastern Conference tournament, where Vanderbilt claimed the championship.[1] Amid player shortages caused by COVID-19 opt-outs and illnesses, Fuller was recruited to the football team leveraging her kicking experience from soccer, becoming the first woman to play in a Southeastern Conference game on November 28, 2020, when she handled a squib kickoff against Missouri.[2] Two weeks later, on December 12 against Tennessee, she successfully converted extra points, marking the first time a woman scored in a Power Five conference football game.[2][3] Her participation was limited to these instances, filling a practical need rather than a long-term role on the roster.[2]

High school athletics

Sarah Fuller attended Wylie High School in Wylie, Texas, where she competed as a goalkeeper on the varsity soccer team.[9] As a high school athlete, she displayed notable leg strength, punting soccer balls up to 60 yards, a skill that underscored her potential in the position.[10] Described as a star athlete at Wylie, Fuller's goalkeeper performances highlighted her command in the net and distribution abilities, contributing to her recognition in Texas soccer circles.[11] Her high school success facilitated recruitment to Vanderbilt University's women's soccer program, where she enrolled to continue as a goalkeeper following her 2017 graduation.[1]

College soccer career

Vanderbilt Commodores

Sarah Fuller joined the Vanderbilt Commodores women's soccer team as a freshman goalkeeper in 2017 after a standout high school career at Wylie High School in Texas.[1] During her freshman season, she did not see any game action, primarily due to a broken foot injury that sidelined her and contributed to early frustrations with adapting to college-level academics and athletics.[12] As a sophomore in 2018, Fuller appeared in just one game, contributing to one shutout while serving as a backup amid ongoing team depth at the position.[1] Her junior year in 2019 followed a similar pattern, with only one appearance on September 12 against Chattanooga, where she again helped secure a combined shutout.[1] These limited opportunities reflected stiff competition from senior goalkeepers and persistent injury setbacks, including a slipped disk in her back during sophomore training that frequently kept her out of sessions.[12] Despite minimal startsâ€”none across her first three seasonsâ€”Fuller focused on team support and personal development, earning SEC Academic Honor Roll recognition each year from 2017 to 2019.[1] Fuller's transfer from Vanderbilt after her senior year in 2020 stemmed from her graduation and desire to continue soccer as a graduate transfer closer to her Texas home at the University of North Texas, where she could pursue a master's degree and additional playing opportunities.[13] This move aligned with seeking enhanced development and field time beyond her backup role at Vanderbilt, though she expressed gratitude for the program's foundational experience.[14]

University of North Texas

Fuller joined the University of North Texas women's soccer team as a graduate transfer from Vanderbilt, with the signing announced on November 13, 2020, ahead of the 2021 season.[15] As the starting goalkeeper, she anchored the defense for the Mean Green in Conference USA play, demonstrating progression in her collegiate career through consistent play amid a schedule affected by residual COVID-19 protocols.[9] In 17 appearances, all starts, Fuller logged 1,648 minutes, posting a 0.71 goals against average, eight shutouts, and 43 saves.[9] These efforts supported North Texas' 10-5-2 overall record and 5-3-2 conference mark, with her shutouts ranking second in Conference USA per game (0.47).[9] [16] Fuller earned Conference USA Defensive Player of the Week on September 14, 2021, after two shutouts in consecutive matches.[17] Her role highlighted improved command in net compared to prior seasons, focusing on distribution and shot-stopping in a competitive conference environment.[9]

Professional and club soccer

Post-college playing experience

Following her graduation from the University of North Texas in 2021, Fuller signed a contract with Minnesota Aurora FC of the USL W League, a pre-professional women's soccer league, on February 7, 2022, marking her entry into organized post-collegiate play as a goalkeeper.[18][19] She served as team captain during the league's inaugural 2022 season, which operated on a summer schedule.[20][21] In 13 appearances for Aurora FC, Fuller recorded eight goals conceded while helping the team advance to the USL W League championship match, where they lost 2â€“1 in extra time to Tormenta FC on July 30, 2022.[22] This performance in a developmental league highlighted her continued commitment to soccer amid competitive challenges, including limited spots in higher-tier professional circuits like the NWSL.[20] Fuller retired from soccer on January 3, 2023, after this single professional season, citing a desire to pursue other opportunities beyond active play.[22] Her brief tenure underscored the realities of transitioning from collegiate to professional women's soccer, where opportunities remain constrained despite growing infrastructure in lower divisions.[23]

Mental health hiatus

In spring 2022, Fuller experienced severe depression, including thoughts of self-harm and suicide, prompting her to take a hiatus from soccer.[20] [24] This decision was influenced by the suicide of Stanford goalkeeper Katie Meyer in March 2022, which led Fuller to seek an emergency session with her sports psychologist and step away from the sport starting around spring break.[20] [25] Fuller attributed the mental health challenges to soccer burnout, describing the sport's repetitiveness as draining despite her prior passion for it, compounded by external pressures from her 2020 football fame, including online criticisms and a death threat.[20] [24] In a Sports Illustrated interview, she stated, "When youâ€™re at that low of a point, the things that you loveâ€¦ tend to be sometimes the most draining," emphasizing her choice to "press pause" rather than continue under duress.[20] The hiatus lasted through the end of the spring semester, after which Fuller reported feeling improved and resumed playing by summer 2022, captaining Minnesota Aurora FC in the USL W League to a runner-up finish.[20] [24] This break shifted her focus temporarily from competitive play to personal recovery, while she later opted out of her remaining college eligibility at the University of North Texas.[20]

College football involvement

Opportunity and preparation

In late November 2020, Vanderbilt University's football team faced an acute shortage of kickers when multiple specialists, including the starting placekicker, entered COVID-19 quarantine or opted out of the season due to health protocols and exposure risks.[26][27] Head coach Derek Mason identified Sarah Fuller, a senior goalkeeper on the women's soccer team with demonstrated leg strength from free kicks and penalties, as a potential solution based on her athletic profile rather than football-specific skills.[13] Fuller, lacking any prior experience in American football, was invited to practice sessions starting around November 23, 2020, to test her suitability amid the team's urgent need for depth at the position.[28][29] She evaluated her own readiness by focusing on the practical demands, determining that her soccer-honed technique could be adapted sufficiently for kickoffs and potentially extra points despite the differences in ball shape, tee usage, and field positioning.[30][13] Training emphasized quick adjustments from soccer's instep-driven strikes to football's more precise, angled approaches, with Fuller participating in targeted drills to build familiarity under the guidance of football staff, prioritizing functionality over long-term development given the immediate roster constraints.[28] This circumstantial opportunity arose solely from the program's personnel crisis, with Fuller's selection reflecting a pragmatic assessment of available campus athletes capable of filling the gap rather than broader representational aims.[31][26]

Games played and performance

Sarah Fuller made her debut in college football on November 28, 2020, during Vanderbilt's game against Missouri, entering as the placekicker due to a shortage of available specialists amid COVID-19 protocols.[32] She executed a single squib kickoff to open the second half, directing a low line drive approximately 30 yards to Missouri's 35-yard line, where it was recovered by the Tigers with no return yardage.[33] Vanderbilt lost the contest 41-0, marking the team's eighth consecutive defeat in a winless 2020 season.[32] In her second and final appearance, Fuller played against Tennessee on December 12, 2020.[3] She successfully converted a single extra-point attempt in the first quarter following a Vanderbilt touchdown, accounting for the team's only point in a 42-17 loss.[34] No additional kicks were attempted in the game, which concluded Vanderbilt's 0-9 season.[35] Over her two games, Fuller's statistical contribution consisted of one kickoff with effective short-yardage placement and one perfect extra-point conversion (1/1), yielding 1 total point scored, with no field goal attempts or misses recorded.[36] Her role was limited to these emergency fill-in duties as a temporary specialist for a Vanderbilt team hampered by injuries, opt-outs, and pandemic-related absences, amid an overall program record of zero wins.[37]

Critics contended that Fuller's participation in Vanderbilt's football games during the 2020 season, amid the team's winless 0-7 record and ongoing COVID-19-related absences among male specialists, functioned primarily as a publicity stunt rather than a merit-based necessity.[38] Commentators such as Clay Travis described it as a "manufactured stunt" engineered to appeal to progressive media narratives, arguing that the program prioritized symbolic gender inclusion over recruiting or developing competent male kickers available during the pandemic.[39] This view posited that the move distracted from Vanderbilt's on-field struggles, including a 41-0 loss to Missouri on November 28, 2020, where Fuller executed her debut squib kickoffâ€”a low-risk, short bounce designed to minimize return yardage rather than showcase distance or power.[32] Fuller's SEC Co-Special Teams Player of the Week award, announced on November 30, 2020, for that single squib kick against Missouri, drew particular scrutiny for rewarding minimal involvement over substantive performance.[40] Detractors, including draft analyst Matt Miller, labeled it fraudulent and called for its vacating, asserting that the honor diluted the award's value by conflating historical novelty with athletic merit, as squib kicks require less leg strength and carry lower stakes than standard kickoffs or field goals.[40] Broader critiques framed her selection and accolades as emblematic of tokenism, where gender-based opportunity supplanted skill evaluation, potentially eroding the competitive integrity of men's college football by incentivizing identity-driven decisions over pure ability.[41] Such arguments highlighted that Fuller's limited roleâ€”two kickoffs across losses to Missouri and Tennessee on December 5, 2020â€”yielded no game-altering impact, yet garnered disproportionate media elevation, fostering perceptions of favoritism that undermined traditional standards of excellence in the sport.[42]

Honors and legacy

Athletic awards

Fuller earned recognition in soccer primarily through team achievements at Vanderbilt University. As starting goalkeeper in the shortened 2020 season, she helped the Commodores secure the Southeastern Conference Women's Soccer Tournament championship on November 8, 2020, with a 1.02 goals-against average that ranked 10th in program history.[12] In football, her brief stint as Vanderbilt's placekicker yielded the Southeastern Conference Co-Special Teams Player of the Week honor on November 30, 2020, shared with Florida's Kadarius Toney, after executing a 30-yard kickoff against Missouri on November 28â€”the first such participation by a woman in a Power Five conference game.[51][52] The award, based on that single onside kick returned for a touchdown by the opponent, prompted scrutiny over whether it prioritized novelty over sustained performance metrics typical for weekly honors.[51] Additional football-related honors included a nomination for the 2020 Capital One Orange Bowl Courage Award, recognizing her barrier-breaking role amid Vanderbilt's injury shortages. Cultural and media impact

Sarah Fuller's participation in Vanderbilt University's football games in November and December 2020 generated significant media attention, with coverage from major outlets including The New York Times, ESPN, and Sports Illustrated highlighting her as the first woman to play in a Power Five conference game.[27][28][53] This frenzy peaked around her kickoff against Missouri on November 28, 2020, and her extra point against Tennessee on December 12, 2020, framing the event as a historic milestone for gender integration in college football despite Vanderbilt's ongoing kicker injuries due to COVID-19 protocols.[5] Post-season coverage diminished rapidly, with minimal sustained national interest evident in subsequent years; Personal life

Education and residences

Fuller graduated from Vanderbilt University in spring 2021 with a bachelor's degree in Medicine, Health, and Society.[71] She subsequently transferred as a graduate student to the University of North Texas, where she earned a Master of Business Administration in Sports Entertainment Management from the G. Brint Ryan College of Business in 2022.[62][21] Born in Wylie, Texas, Fuller resided in Nashville, Tennessee, while attending Vanderbilt from 2017 to 2021.[9] She then moved to Denton, Texas, for her graduate studies and soccer eligibility at UNT.[71] Following completion of her MBA, Fuller relocated back to Nashville, where she is currently based in support of her professional role.[72]

Interests and challenges

Following her historic participation in Vanderbilt's football games in November and December 2020, Fuller faced significant public scrutiny and online backlash, with critics labeling the opportunity a publicity stunt amid the team's COVID-19-related absences.[73] Despite this, her family provided strong support, including her parents traveling from Dallas, Texas, to attend the Missouri game on November 28, 2020.[8] In 2022, Fuller encountered profound mental health challenges, experiencing depression that included thoughts of self-harm and suicide, prompting her to take an indefinite break from professional soccer with the USL W League's Huntsville City FC.[20] [24] These struggles, which she attributed in part to the pressures of her athletic career and public visibility, led her to "press pause" on soccer to prioritize recovery.[74] Fuller has since channeled these experiences into advocacy for mental health awareness among athletes, emphasizing the need for better support systems in sports.[20] She has expressed passions for student-athlete rights and advancing opportunities for women in sports, viewing her platform as a means to address systemic issues like mental health stigma.[21] Limited public details exist on personal hobbies beyond her athletic background, which included early involvement in basketball, volleyball, and indoor soccer leagues influenced by her 6-foot-1-inch heightâ€” a trait inherited from her 6-foot-5-inch grandfathers.[5] No documented philanthropy efforts were identified in available records.
