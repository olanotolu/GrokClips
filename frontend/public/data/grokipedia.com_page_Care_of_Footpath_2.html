Care of Footpath 2

Care of Footpath 2 (also known as Kill Them Young) is a 2015 Indian bilingual drama film directed and starring child actor Kishan Shrikanth in Kannada and Hindi, depicting the lives of juvenile delinquents in urban slums.[1] The story centers on four teenagers from impoverished backgrounds who, after a botched plan leads to the murder of a police officer, navigate arrest, trial under India's juvenile justice system, and societal demands for harsh punishment versus rehabilitation efforts by non-governmental organizations.[2] Inspired by real events involving youth crime in India, the film explores environmental influences on delinquency, including slum conditions and peer pressure, while critiquing leniency in juvenile laws following high-profile cases.[1][3] Produced by Devaraj Pande under SRS Media Vision, the film features Avika Gor and Esha Deol in supporting roles, marking Deol's return to cinema after a hiatus.[1] Shrikanth, who debuted as a director at age 10 with the predecessor Care of Footpath (2006), helmed this sequel at 19, drawing from his early experiences in Kannada cinema.[4] It received a selective theatrical release and was submitted as India's entry for the Best Foreign Language Film at the 88th Academy Awards, though it did not secure a nomination.[5] Critics noted the film's ambitious social commentary but faulted its execution as melodramatic and overly protracted, with mixed reception highlighting its intent to provoke debate on juvenile reform amid public outrage over lenient sentencing in violent crimes.[6][3] The narrative underscores causal factors like socioeconomic deprivation over innate criminality, aligning with empirical observations of crime correlates in developing urban areas, while avoiding romanticization of offenders.[1]

Synopsis

Plot Overview

Care of Footpath 2 follows four teenage friends from the slums who, under provocation from a corrupt police officer, meticulously plan and execute his murder.[7] The story unfolds against the backdrop of urban poverty in New Delhi, where the protagonists navigate a harsh environment that fosters juvenile delinquency among millions.[6] When their scheme unravels, the youths face arrest, thrusting them into India's juvenile justice system for trial.[2] The film's central conflict revolves around the legal proceedings, emphasizing procedural elements such as juvenile court protocols and the intense media scrutiny that amplifies public debate over youth crime and punishment.[7] As an emotional suspense thriller, it structures the narrative around the tension between the adolescents' impulsive actions and the inexorable machinery of justice, without delving into resolution details.[8] This setup underscores the broader societal issues of corruption and delinquency while maintaining focus on the trial's dramatic unfolding.[3]

Background and Inspirations

Real-World Basis

The film's narrative is grounded in the documented surge of juvenile involvement in violent offenses in India during the early 2000s, particularly in urban slum settings where minors engaged in confrontations with law enforcement and officials amid widespread perceptions of systemic graft. National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) reports indicate that crimes committed by juveniles under 18 constituted approximately 1% of total cognizable offenses in 2003, rising to 1.2% by 2013, with a notable uptick in serious categories such as murder, robbery, and crimes against the state that often pitted youth against authority figures.[9] This trend fueled public discourse on the limitations of treating all minors under a uniform rehabilitative framework, culminating in the 2015 Juvenile Justice Act amendments allowing prosecution of 16- to 18-year-olds as adults for heinous crimes.[10] Causal analysis of these patterns prioritizes breakdowns in familial structures over deterministic socioeconomic narratives. Empirical research consistently identifies absent or dysfunctional parentingâ€”manifesting as single-parent households, parental alcoholism, or neglectâ€”as a primary predictor of delinquent behavior among Indian youth, with studies showing significant correlations between the absence of biological parents and elevated rates of aggression and criminality.[11] A meta-analysis of global and regional data further substantiates that weak parental monitoring and inconsistent discipline erode personal accountability, fostering environments where minors rationalize violence as retribution against perceived institutional failures like corrupt policing, independent of poverty levels alone.[12] Indian-specific inquiries reinforce this, attributing a substantial portion of urban juvenile violence to intra-family discord and enabling parental attitudes that normalize defiance of authority, rather than external victimhood.[13] Such factors underscore a chain of personal and relational failures enabling escalation from petty defiance to targeted assaults on officials, as observed in pre-2015 case patterns.[14]

Development and Pre-Production

Care of Footpath 2 originated as a thematic successor to the 2006 film Care of Footpath, directed by Kishan Shrikanth, who at age 10 had helmed the original as a child actor and filmmaker focusing on slum child welfare and adoption.[15] The sequel expands into adolescent crime, centering on four teenage protagonists inspired by real juvenile delinquency cases who plot to murder a corrupt police officer, aiming to interrogate leniency in India's juvenile justice laws.[1] Shrikanth envisioned it not as a conventional sequel but as a reboot with a refreshed aesthetic and narrative independence, evolving the franchise's social commentary from early childhood vulnerability to youth criminality and legal accountability.[16] Scripting emphasized causal links between provocation, peer influence, and systemic failures, drawing from documented patterns of juvenile offenses to critique protections that Shrikanth and co-writers portrayed as enabling impunity for serious crimes.[1] Development progressed with efforts to broaden appeal through bilingual execution in Kannada and Hindi (as Kill Them Young), necessitating dual-language scripting and casting to navigate sensitive depictions of violence and trial proceedings across linguistic markets while maintaining narrative cohesion.[17] By February 2014, pre-production advanced to include high-profile cameos, such as Bollywood director Anees Bazmee, signaling momentum in assembling talent for the project's ambitious scope.[18] In September 2015, the completed film secured a lateral entry into the 88th Academy Awards general category, the first for a Kannada production outside the foreign-language slot, reflecting Shrikanth's intent to elevate regional cinema's global scrutiny of juvenile reform debatesâ€”though it advanced no further in selection.[19] [20] This phase underscored challenges in adapting provocative themes, including ethical portrayals of youth offenders, into a format viable for international advocacy without diluting the critique of legal thresholds for adult prosecution.[17]

Cast and Characters

Principal Roles

Kishan Shrikanth, also known as Master Kishan, stars as Krishna, the central juvenile delinquent whose portrayal underscores street-smart resilience and deliberate agency in orchestrating a crime amid personal hardships and peer pressures, reflecting the film's exploration of moral ambiguity in young offenders who weigh risks against perceived injustices.[1] Avika Gor portrays Geeta, a key co-conspirator driven by vendettas and group loyalty, emphasizing how interpersonal influences amplify individual choices in juvenile wrongdoing without excusing accountability.[1] [21] Deepp Pathak plays Viji, another teenage accomplice whose actions highlight the causal chain of resentment-fueled decisions, portraying offenders as active agents rather than mere victims of circumstance.[1] Esha Deol appears as Advocate Meera, an adult figure embodying legal authority and ethical confrontation, offering a counterpoint that probes the delinquents' rationalizations against societal norms and victim impacts.[1] [21]

Supporting Roles

Deepp Pathak played Viji, a corrupt police officer whose exploitative actions toward slum residents catalyze the protagonists' radical response, realistically portraying entrenched corruption within law enforcement subplots.[22] His performance underscores systemic incentives for abuse of power, drawing from the film's basis in true juvenile delinquency cases where authority figures prey on vulnerable communities.[1] Esha Deol portrayed Advocate Meera, a principled lawyer defending the accused juveniles, offering a counterpoint to institutional villainy by emphasizing individual integrity amid flawed judicial processes.[22] This role contributes to balanced ensemble dynamics, humanizing legal figures who navigate bureaucratic hurdles without endorsing the system's broader failures.[23] J. Karthik enacted Inspector Karthik, an investigating officer entangled in the murder probe, whose procedural diligence highlights routine police work separate from outright antagonism.[22] Shravanthi Sainath appeared as a TV reporter covering the case, illustrating media's role in amplifying slum narratives and public scrutiny of delinquency.[24] Dingri Naresh's Dingri represented familial anchors in the slum environment, supporting subplots of kinship ties that ground the teenagers' motivations in everyday survival pressures.[22] Child actors and local cameos further authenticated these family dynamics, depicting unvarnished slum interactions without romanticization, as evidenced by the production's emphasis on non-professional performers for realism.[3]

Production

Filming Locations and Process

Principal photography for Care of Footpath 2 began in October 2013 at Kanteerava Studios in Bangalore, India, where interior scenes, including courtroom sequences depicting the Indian legal system, were filmed to ensure realism in portraying trials of juvenile offenders.[25] Exterior shots leveraged Bangalore's urban environments to authentically represent the socio-economic conditions of the protagonists, young slum dwellers entangled in crime and corruption.[26] Filming extended through 2014, with actress Esha Deol completing her schedule in Bangalore by April of that year, focusing on sequences that highlighted legal and ethical dilemmas in juvenile justice.[27] The production faced logistical demands of capturing raw depictions of violence and poverty, necessitating careful coordination in urban settings prone to disruptions. As a bilingual film in Kannada and Hindi, shoots incorporated script adjustments for linguistic and cultural fidelity, with actors delivering lines in multiple languages to accommodate regional audiences without compromising narrative coherence.[27] This approach extended to working with young performers like Avika Gor and Master Kishan in high-stakes scenes, prioritizing controlled environments to handle the portrayal of delinquency and moral conflict responsibly.[1]

Technical Aspects

The editing of Care of Footpath 2, credited to director Kishan Shrikanth, employs a structure that interweaves the juveniles' backstory with courtroom proceedings to heighten suspense in trial sequences, drawing on recreations of Indian juvenile justice processes for procedural accuracy.[24] One review praises the editing as "par excellence" for maintaining engagement amid the narrative's emotional intensity.[28] However, the film's pacing falters in the climax, becoming incoherent and overlong, which dilutes tension despite efforts to avoid gratuitous sensationalism through grounded legal depictions.[3] [6] Cinematography by Magesh K Dev captures the gritty realism of slum and footpath environments, emphasizing the harsh underbelly of urban poverty without romanticization, as seen in sequences depicting the begging mafia and daily struggles of juvenile characters.[24] This approach relies on natural lighting and on-location shooting to convey authenticity, contributing to the film's suspense by foregrounding environmental pressures on the protagonists rather than stylized visuals.[3] Some critiques note that the overall cinematography, while functional for realism, lacks innovation and fails to elevate key dramatic moments.[29] Sound design, overseen by D. Shankar, focuses on ambient urban noises and subtle emotional cues to underscore beats in the trial and confrontation scenes, prioritizing diegetic authenticity over artificial enhancement.[24] The minimal use of post-production effects aligns with the film's commitment to verisimilitude, avoiding CGI to preserve the raw depiction of real-world juvenile delinquency inspired by true events.[1] Technical shortcomings, such as lip-sync discrepancies in dialogue-heavy sequences, occasionally undermine immersion.[3]

Music and Soundtrack

Composition

The original score and songs for Care of Footpath 2 were composed by Vivek Kar, who served as the primary music director for the 2015 Kannada-language film.[30] [31] Kar, building on his earlier work in Hindi films like Zindagi 50-50 (2013), collaborated with Manoj Srihari and director Kishan S.S. on several tracks, integrating original compositions to align with the narrative's examination of juvenile delinquency driven by socioeconomic pressures.[31] This collaborative approach produced a soundtrack released as an EP on November 2, 2015, featuring songs that emphasize tension and realism in portraying motivations like resentment toward corruption, using restrained orchestration to underscore consequences rather than sensationalize rebellion. The compositions avoid overt emotional manipulation, instead employing subtle builds in instrumentation to reflect causal links between environmental factors and delinquent behavior, consistent with the film's commitment to empirical depiction over narrative contrivance.[32]

Track Listing and Themes

The soundtrack for Care of Footpath 2 consists of five songs composed by Vivek Kar, Manoj Srihari, and Kishan S.S., released digitally on November 3, 2015, approximately one month before the film's theatrical debut on December 4, 2015, to generate promotional buzz.[33][21][34] The tracks, typical situational numbers in Kannada cinema, align with plot elements involving street youths' progression from aspiration to vengeful action against corrupt influences:
  • Bullet Nanna: Sung by Girik Aman and Shipra Goyal, with lyrics by Raghu Niduvalli; evokes aggressive familial or survival motifs amid urban hardship.[35]
  • Good Times: Underscores ironic contrasts between fleeting joys and the protagonists' grim realities of slum life and quick-money temptations.[36]
  • Kanasugala: Performed by Santhosh Venky, lyrics by K. Kalyan; centers on dreams ("kanasugala" translates to "of dreams"), depicting initial youthful hopes that fuel later defiance when thwarted by systemic corruption.[37][38]
  • Last Hope: Reflects desperation in the characters' final recourse to vigilantism, tying to the narrative's portrayal of failed legal protections for juveniles.[39]
  • Plan To Kill: Directly corresponds to the central scheme of retribution, with lyrics outlining calculated responses to exploitative adults, critiquing leniency that shields perpetrators over victims.[40]
These song purposes, inferred from titles and credited contributors, reinforce the film's focus on causal links between corruption, unchecked juvenile exploitation, and retaliatory cycles, without broader artistic embellishment.[41]

Release

Theatrical and Festival Release

Care of Footpath 2 received a limited theatrical release in the United States on November 6, 2015, qualifying it for Academy Awards consideration through required screenings in Los Angeles, including 28 showings at the Laemmle theater complex.[42] This rollout preceded its wider Indian release on December 4, 2015, primarily in Kannada, with promotional emphasis on its basis in real-life stories of juvenile offenders to provoke discussion on delinquency and legal reforms.[43][6] The film was also prepared for Hindi distribution under the title Kill Them Young, though specific Hindi theatrical dates were not prominently documented beyond the bilingual marketing push.[20] In India, the film carried a UA certification from the Central Board of Film Certification, suitable for audiences above age 12 with parental guidance, and featured a runtime of approximately 133 minutes. Promotional campaigns highlighted its independent submission to the 2016 Oscars in the Best Foreign Language Film categoryâ€”distinct from India's official entry, Courtâ€”leveraging endorsements from figures like Hema Malini to generate buzz around its examination of juvenile justice flaws.[20][44] Festival exposure remained minimal, confined largely to Oscar-qualifying runs rather than major international circuits, aligning with its strategy as a socially provocative thriller rather than a festival-circuit darling.[45]

Distribution and Box Office Performance

Care of Footpath 2 had a limited theatrical distribution primarily in Karnataka, India, with screenings commencing on December 4, 2015, targeting audiences interested in social dramas rather than mass-market entertainers.[46] The film's bilingual release in Kannada and Hindi aimed to broaden reach, but it remained confined to select urban theaters, reflecting the challenges faced by issue-based Kannada cinema in competing with commercial blockbusters.[19] International distribution was sparse, with limited screenings in the United States as part of efforts to expand Kannada films abroad, alongside showings at festivals such as the Imagine India Film Festival in Amsterdam, where it was the sole South Indian entry in 2016.[47][48] These overseas engagements highlighted niche appeal among diaspora and festival circuits but did not translate into significant global earnings. Box office performance was modest and fell short of expectations for its genre, hampered by its focus on juvenile delinquency and systemic critiques over formulaic entertainment, which restricted draw from mainstream Kannada viewers.[49] Producers anticipated stronger returns given the involvement of Bollywood actors like Esha Deol, yet the film's emphasis on realism over spectacle limited commercial viability in a market favoring high-octane narratives. Post-2015, it gained availability through satellite rights sales and potential streaming platforms, extending longevity beyond initial theatrical runs.[50]

Reception

Critical Analysis

Professional critics offered mixed assessments of Care of Footpath 2, praising its bold confrontation of juvenile delinquency and systemic corruption while faulting its execution as excessively melodramatic and structurally flawed. The film's IMDb user rating stands at 7.1 out of 10, derived from 47 votes, reflecting a niche appreciation among viewers familiar with Kannada cinema, though this limited sample size tempers its representativeness.[1] Metacritic aggregates a 0% score from a single review, underscoring the scarcity of broad critical engagement.[2] International reviewers, such as the Los Angeles Times, critiqued the film as a "bombastic, overlong melodrama" that fails to balance intensity with restraint, prioritizing emotional excess over nuanced storytelling despite its basis in real juvenile cases.[6] This perspective highlights a perceived lack of subtlety in portraying provoked teenage protagonists, rejecting overly sympathetic interpretations that might excuse their actions through socioeconomic hardship alone; instead, the narrative's hyperbolic trial sequences amplify drama at the expense of causal depth, rendering the juveniles' motivations more theatrical than empirically grounded. Domestic Indian outlets echoed concerns over excess but commended the film's audacity in dissecting legal proceedings, with Times of India awarding 2.5 out of 5 stars for partially succeeding in evoking empathy without fully resolving its ambitious scope.[3] Regional divides in criticism are evident: Indian analyses, including Deccan Chronicle's observation of an "ordinary experience" lacking innovation, emphasized procedural realism in the juvenile justice system depiction, aligning with the film's inspiration from actual Indian cases of underage offenders targeting authority figures.[51] Foreign critiques, conversely, focused on cultural authenticity, viewing the slum-based conspiracy and media frenzy as insightful yet undermined by directorial overreach from the young Kishan Shrikanth, whose choices prioritize shock over verifiable legal or psychological fidelity. Such portrayals risk romanticizing delinquency as inevitable rebellion rather than a product of individual agency intersecting with flawed institutions, a reading debunked by the film's unresolved tonal shifts that dilute its realist intent.

Audience and Commercial Response

Care of Footpath 2 experienced limited commercial success upon its December 2015 release, primarily confined to regional Kannada-speaking markets in Karnataka, with screenings extending to select international venues such as Los Angeles theaters.[52][49] The film failed to meet box office expectations, as reported by local outlets, despite its low-budget production and thematic focus on urban slum youth.[49] This underperformance aligned with its niche appeal in Kannada cinema, lacking the broad distribution or marketing push of mainstream Indian releases. Audience reception was modest, evidenced by an IMDb user rating of 7.1 out of 10 from 47 votes, indicating approval from a small viewer base familiar with the film's suspense-thriller elements drawn from real juvenile delinquency cases.[1] Viewer feedback, though sparse, highlighted emotional engagement with the story of four teenage friends drawn into crime amid slum conditions, but noted the narrative's length and melodrama as barriers to wider resonance.[1] The film's submission as India's potential Oscar entry for Best Foreign Language Film in 2016, alongside Court, generated brief festival buzz but did not translate to sustained audience traction or cultural discourse beyond advocacy groups linked to organizations like ECHO, which praised its basis in actual street children's experiences.[5][53]

Themes and Controversies

Examination of Juvenile Delinquency

In Care of Footpath 2, the juvenile protagonistsâ€”four teenagers from Bengaluru slumsâ€”are depicted as actively conspiring to murder a sadistic police inspector in retaliation for his abuses, underscoring their personal agency and grievance-driven motives rather than portraying crime as an inescapable outcome of poverty.[1] [6] The narrative highlights their calculated plotting and execution, framing delinquency as a consequence of unchecked defiance and moral lapses within dysfunctional family environments, where parental absence or failure exacerbates individual choices toward violence.[3] This portrayal diverges from environmental determinist explanations that attribute youth crime predominantly to socioeconomic hardship, instead emphasizing causal factors rooted in family breakdown and personal accountability. Empirical research corroborates the film's implicit focus on family structure: childhood family instability, such as parental separation or neglect, independently predicts adolescent delinquency and adult offending, beyond mere poverty levels.[54] [55] Studies further link broken homes to heightened risks of violent behavior, with familial discord fostering the moral hazards that enable escalatory decisions like those shown in the film.[56] The film's examination aligns with evidence on recidivism patterns in juvenile systems, where lenient approaches without robust accountability for personal agency correlate with high reoffending: 70-80% of released youth face rearrest within 2-3 years, often tied to unaddressed familial and behavioral deficits rather than isolated environmental interventions.[57] By depicting the protagonists' irreversible consequences, the movie critiques permissive frameworks that downplay individual responsibility, echoing data indicating that family-centered reforms yield stronger reductions in repeat delinquency than poverty alleviation alone.[58][59]

Critique of Corruption and Legal System

The film depicts a corrupt police officer engaging in extortion against slum-dwelling juveniles attempting a petty theft scheme, framing his misconduct as the inciting incident for their retaliatory murder. This portrayal positions institutional graft as a societal ill but stresses the protagonists' volitional escalation from confrontation to homicide, rejecting deterministic excuses in favor of accountability for disproportionate reprisal.[2][3] India's police corruption is empirically documented, with Transparency International's 2024 Corruption Perceptions Index ranking the country 96th out of 180 nations and assigning a score of 38/100, reflecting entrenched bribery and abuse of power in law enforcement.[60][61] Nonetheless, the film's narrative counters systemic monocausality by illustrating how environmental stressors like corruption interact with individual agency; data from the National Crime Records Bureau indicate that juvenile involvement in murder cases rose 13% from 2010 to 2014, often linked to multiple factors including family dysfunction and peer influence rather than corruption alone. This underscores that while graft erodes trustâ€”evidenced by low conviction rates under 30% for police-reported crimesâ€”perpetrators retain causal primacy in choosing violence over non-lethal alternatives.[62] The courtroom proceedings in Care of Footpath 2 expose limitations in India's pre-2015 legal framework for juvenile offenders, particularly under the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000, which mandated uniform treatment for all under-18s, including placement in observation homes irrespective of offence gravity. This approach drew criticism for undermining deterrence in heinous crimes, as seen in the 2012 Nirbhaya case where a juvenile co-perpetrator received lenient sentencing, sparking nationwide demands for reform.[63] The 2015 Juvenile Justice Act amendment addressed these flaws by introducing preliminary assessments for 16- to 18-year-olds accused of heinous offencesâ€”defined as those punishable by seven years or more imprisonmentâ€”potentially transferring cases to adult courts based on maturity and intent evaluation.[64] Evidence from post-amendment implementation supports targeted accountability, with over 1,500 such transfers by 2020, correlating with stabilized juvenile murder apprehensions amid broader crime declines, though full efficacy requires rigorous capacity-building in juvenile justice boards.[65] The film thus advocates evidence-driven adjustments prioritizing victim justice without wholesale abolition of rehabilitation for lesser offences.

Debates on Film's Message and Accuracy

The film's depiction of juveniles plotting against a corrupt police officer, framed within a trial exposing systemic leniency, fueled polarized interpretations of its core message. Proponents, including those emphasizing law-and-order priorities, lauded it for spotlighting failures in juvenile protections that allow recidivism and unaddressed delinquency, drawing parallels to real-world cases where minors evaded adult accountability for grave offenses. This perspective echoed broader public outrage following the 2012 Delhi gang rape, where the involved juvenile's treatment under the Juvenile Justice Act, 2000â€”resulting in release after three yearsâ€”highlighted causal gaps between rehabilitation ideals and empirical risks of reoffending. Director Kishan Shrikanth explicitly linked the film to such shifts, stating the case "changed the way how juvenile criminals are seen" by underscoring the need for scrutiny of age-based exemptions.[66] Conversely, detractors, often aligned with rehabilitation-focused advocacy, argued the narrative stigmatized slum-dwelling youth by prioritizing punitive reform over root causes like poverty and exploitative environments, potentially endorsing extralegal responses akin to vigilantism rather than institutional fixes. A Times of India review critiqued the film's execution for diluting its intent to illustrate "the near-invisible line between right and wrong," noting melodramatic excess overshadowed gritty realism in portraying begging mafias and legal defenses against vengeful authority.[3] Such views reflected concerns that sensationalized trials, mirroring media hype in actual high-profile prosecutions, could erode due process without evidence-based alternatives. On accuracy, the story aligns broadly with documented patterns of juvenile involvement in organized crime and clashes with corrupt policing in India's urban underclass, as seen in police reports on slum-based gangs, but diverges through fictionalized specifics like a targeted officer assassination, which amplify drama over verifiable sequences from inspirations. No direct misalignment with aggregated true stories of delinquent trials was contested, though the film's suspense elements, per its self-description as inspired by such events, prioritize causal narrative on provocation and fallout over forensic precision. Its release on December 4, 2015, overlapped with final Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015 deliberationsâ€”enacted December 31 amid Nirbhaya-driven demands to try 16- to 18-year-olds as adults for heinous actsâ€”but exerted no documented sway on outcomes, serving instead as a cultural footnote in preexisting reform momentum.[67][1]
