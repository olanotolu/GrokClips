Aphallia

Aphallia, also known as congenital penile agenesis, is an extremely rare congenital malformation occurring in genetic males (46,XY karyotype), defined by the complete absence of the penis due to arrested development of the genital tubercle during early embryogenesis.[1] It typically presents at birth with a well-formed scrotum containing bilateral palpable testes and an ectopic urethral opening, often perineal or recto-urinary, while internal male reproductive structures such as the prostate and seminal vesicles remain intact.[1] The condition's estimated prevalence is one in 30 million live births, with fewer than 100 cases reported worldwide, frequently associated with other anomalies including genitourinary malformations (in 80% of cases, such as urethrorectal fistulas), renal dysplasia or hydronephrosis (in 63%), and less commonly gastrointestinal or vertebral defects.[1] [2] Diagnosis relies on immediate postnatal physical examination supplemented by imaging modalities like ultrasound, cystography, and karyotyping to confirm the genetic male status and assess concomitant anomalies.[1] Management necessitates a multidisciplinary approach prioritizing urinary tract protection through initial diversion procedures (e.g., vesicostomy in 39% of reported cases), followed by delayed reconstructive interventions; contemporary guidelines favor male gender assignment with eventual phalloplasty or neophallus construction to address functional voiding, sexual potential, and body image, though outcomes remain challenging due to technical complexities and limited long-term data, with historical feminization procedures less emphasized amid evolving evidence on psychosexual development.[1] [2] Mortality, reported in 12% of cases, stems primarily from sepsis or uremia secondary to untreated associated malformations rather than the aphallia itself.[1]

Definition and Embryology

Clinical Definition

Aphallia, also termed penile agenesis, is defined clinically as the complete congenital absence of the penis in a genotypically male (46,XY) individual, resulting from failed development of the genital tubercle during embryogenesis.[3][4] This malformation manifests at birth with no visible penile shaft, corpus cavernosum, corpus spongiosum, or glans, while the scrotum is typically well-developed and contains bilaterally palpable testes.[5][6] The urethral opening is ectopically located, most commonly in the perineum or at the anal verge, necessitating alternative voiding patterns and often associating with other urogenital anomalies such as hypospadias or renal malformations, though isolated cases occur.[3][7] Diagnosis relies on physical examination confirming the absence of phallic tissue alongside normal male gonadal descent, with genetic confirmation via karyotyping to verify 46,XY status and exclude disorders of sex development.[8][9]

Embryological Development and Pathogenesis

The external genitalia arise from the genital tubercle, an initial mesenchymal outgrowth that forms during the fourth week of embryonic development, positioned cranial to the cloacal membrane and derived from intermediate mesoderm and surface ectoderm.[10] In embryos with a 46,XY karyotype, the testes differentiate by weeks 6-7 and secrete testosterone, which is locally converted to dihydrotestosterone (DHT) within target tissues, initiating masculinization of the bipotential genital tubercle.[11] This androgen-driven process causes rapid elongation of the tubercle into the phallus between weeks 8-12, accompanied by proliferation of mesenchyme to form the corpora cavernosa and corpus spongiosum precursors.[12] Concomitant with phallic growth, the urethral groove develops along the ventral surface, bordered by urethral folds that fuse midline by weeks 12-14 to enclose the penile urethra, while the genital swellings caudal to the tubercle evolve into the scrotum under androgen influence.[13] The prepuce forms later, around week 12, via ectodermal ingrowth at the glans margin.[10] These coordinated events depend on precise spatiotemporal signaling, including fibroblast growth factors (FGFs) and sonic hedgehog (Shh) from the urethral plate and cloaca, which regulate mesenchymal patterning and outgrowth.[14] Aphallia represents a profound failure in this sequence, characterized by agenesis of the penis due to non-formation or early regression of the genital tubercle, occurring within the critical 4-7 week window before androgen-dependent differentiation fully commences.[1] [4] The precise pathogenesis is idiopathic and sporadic, with no identified genetic loci, teratogenic exposures, or maternal factors consistently implicated across cases; affected individuals typically exhibit normal 46,XY karyotypes and functional testes, suggesting a localized defect in tubercle anlage proliferation or caudal migration rather than systemic hormonal insufficiency.[15] [16] Hypotheses include disrupted mesenchymal-endodermal interactions or apoptosis of the primitive tubercle, but empirical evidence remains limited to histopathological observations in rare autopsy series, underscoring the anomaly's rarity (fewer than 100 documented cases globally).[17]

Etiology and Associated Conditions

Primary Causes

Aphallia arises primarily from a failure in the embryological development of the genital tubercle, a mesenchymal prominence originating from the cloacal membrane during the fourth week of gestation.[1][18] This developmental arrest prevents the tubercle from differentiating into penile structures, including the glans, shaft, and scrotum, which normally occurs by the 12th week under the influence of androgens.[4] The condition manifests in genotypically and phenotypically male individuals with a typical 46,XY karyotype, underscoring that the defect is not chromosomal but rather a localized disruption in organogenesis.[1] The underlying mechanisms involve potential interruptions in cloacal maturation or inadequate caudal mesenchyme development, which may lead to associated proximal urethrorectal communications observed in many cases.[1] Penile elongation and virilization depend on dihydrotestosterone (DHT), derived from testosterone produced by fetal Leydig cells and stimulated via the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis; deficiencies in this axis or in 5Î±-reductase enzyme activity could contribute to tubercle regression, though direct causal links remain unestablished in most instances.[4][18] While rare associations with teratogens like etretinate or chromosomal polymorphisms (e.g., 9qh+) have been hypothesized, no specific genetic mutations or environmental factors consistently explain aphallia, rendering its precise etiology largely idiopathic and multifactorial.[4] Empirical data from case series indicate that isolated aphallia occurs without broader syndromic features in a minority of patients, suggesting intrinsic errors in mesenchymal signaling or vascular supply during early gestation as plausible but unverified contributors.[1] Further research into molecular pathways, such as those governing tubercle outgrowth, is needed to delineate causal realism beyond descriptive embryology.[19]

Commonly Associated Anomalies

Aphallia is rarely an isolated condition and is commonly associated with multiple congenital malformations, predominantly urogenital, reflecting disruptions in early embryonic development of the caudal mesoderm. A systematic review of 36 reported cases identified urogenital anomalies in nearly all instances, with fistulas present in 80% (29 cases), most frequently urethrorectal fistulas at 46% (19 cases).[1] Renal anomalies affected 63% (26 cases), encompassing hydronephrosis in 34% (14 cases), renal dysplasia in 19% (8 cases), and unilateral or bilateral agenesis in 12% (5 cases).[1] Ureteral anomalies, including vesicoureteral reflux, occurred in 29% (12 cases), while bladder anomalies such as absence or hypoplasia were noted in 21% (9 cases).[1] Cryptorchidism is a frequent genitourinary association, often bilateral, alongside urethral anomalies like blind-ending or absent urethra in approximately 21% of cases.[20][1] Prostate hypoplasia or agenesis appears in about 4% (2 cases).[1] Anorectal malformations, including imperforate anus, are also reported, sometimes as part of broader cloacal anomalies or exstrophy.[20] Non-urogenital anomalies are less prevalent but include cardiovascular defects like ventricular septal defect or patent ductus arteriosus in 12% (5 cases), respiratory issues such as lung agenesis in 9% (4 cases), and gastrointestinal anomalies like omphalocele in 9% (3 cases).[1] Other reported non-urogenital findings encompass tracheoesophageal fistula, annular pancreas, vertebral anomalies, and musculoskeletal issues like clubfoot.[20][1] Certain cases align with syndromes such as urorectal septum malformation sequence (URSMS), featuring ambiguous genitalia, imperforate anus, and urinary tract dysplasias, or cloacal exstrophy variants.[1] Literature indicates over 50% of aphallia cases involve renal agenesis or dysplasia alongside vesicoureteral reflux and cryptorchidism, underscoring the need for comprehensive multisystem evaluation.[20]

Epidemiology

Incidence and Demographic Patterns

Aphallia is an exceedingly rare congenital anomaly, with an estimated incidence of 1 in 10 to 30 million live births.[21][22][1] This figure derives from aggregated case reports and systematic reviews, as population-based registries lack sufficient data due to the condition's infrequency, resulting in fewer than 100 documented cases worldwide since its initial description in 1853.[23] The condition exclusively affects genetically male (46,XY) individuals, manifesting as complete absence of the penis at birth, often alongside other urogenital malformations that may influence ascertainment.[5] Demographic patterns show no established racial or ethnic predilections, with cases reported uniformly across global populations without evidence of geographic clustering beyond reporting biases.[5] Systematic analyses indicate higher documentation from Asia (53% of reviewed cases) and the United States, attributable to publication volume in those regions rather than elevated true prevalence. No correlations with maternal age, socioeconomic factors, or environmental exposures have been identified in peer-reviewed studies, underscoring the idiopathic nature of the defect in most instances.[1]

Historical Reporting Trends

The first modern medical report of aphallia appeared in 1853 by Imminger, describing congenital absence of the penis as a distinct anomaly.[21] Earlier anecdotal accounts exist, such as a 1701 description by French surgeon BarthÃ©lemy Saviard of a child born without a penis, but systematic documentation began in the mid-19th century.[24] By the late 1980s, the medical literature documented approximately 60 cases worldwide, primarily through isolated case reports highlighting associations with other genitourinary malformations.[25] This figure grew modestly, with fewer than 100 cases reported globally by 2010, reflecting sporadic publications rather than widespread epidemiological surveillance.[26] Post-2010 reviews maintain similar tallies, with a 2022 analysis citing under 80 cases and a 2024 systematic review identifying 41 analyzable cases from literature up to 2023, underscoring persistent underreporting consistent with an estimated incidence of 1 in 10-30 million live births.[20][5] No evidence indicates rising detection rates from improved prenatal or postnatal diagnostics, as aphallia manifests overtly at birth; instead, stable cumulative reporting aligns with the condition's intrinsic rarity and limited population-level studies.[26] Geographic trends in reporting show early 20th-century cases predominantly from Europe and North America, shifting toward Asia in recent decades, which accounts for 53% of cases in the 2024 review (22 of 41), possibly due to expanded publication from high-volume medical centers rather than incidence variation.[5] Overall, historical patterns demonstrate consistent low-volume case documentation over 170 years, without acceleration attributable to diagnostic advancements or public health initiatives.[1]

Diagnosis

Postnatal Identification

Aphallia is typically identified immediately after birth during routine physical examination of the newborn, where the complete absence of the penile shaft, corpora cavernosa, and corpus spongiosum is evident, often with the urethral meatus located along the perineal midline or in an ectopic position such as the anal or scrotal region.[1][27] In most cases, the scrotum appears normally developed with palpable testes, distinguishing aphallia from other forms of genital ambiguity, though careful palpation is required to confirm the presence of gonads and rule out conditions like buried penis or severe micropenis.[21][4] Initial clinical suspicion prompts urgent multidisciplinary evaluation, including karyotyping to confirm a 46,XY genotype, which is characteristic of penile agenesis in genetic males, alongside assessment for associated urogenital anomalies such as renal dysplasia or cloacal malformations that occur in up to 50-70% of cases.[28][1] Imaging modalities like ultrasound or voiding cystourethrography may be employed postnatally to delineate urethral anatomy and exclude fistulas, but the diagnosis remains primarily clinical and is rarely missed in term infants due to the overt external malformation.[5] Differential diagnosis involves excluding transient conditions like concealed penis, confirmed by the absence of any phallic tissue on gentle traction or dissection if necessary.[29] Challenges in postnatal identification can arise in preterm or low-birth-weight infants where genital assessment may be deferred, or in cases with severe associated anomalies masking the primary defect, though systematic neonatal screening protocols in modern obstetrics facilitate early detection in over 90% of instances at delivery.[19] Historical case reports indicate that without prompt recognition, complications like urinary obstruction from ectopic meatus can ensue, underscoring the need for immediate specialist referral to pediatric urology or endocrinology.[26]

Diagnostic Evaluation

Diagnostic evaluation of aphallia begins with a thorough physical examination at birth, revealing the complete absence of the penis while typically noting a normal scrotum containing palpable testes and a blind-ending perineal or penoscrotal hypospadiac urethral opening.[1] Karyotype analysis is essential to confirm a 46,XY genotype, distinguishing aphallia from disorders of sex development (DSD) with atypical chromosomal patterns, and is performed via peripheral blood sampling or buccal swab.[1] [4] Laboratory assessments include baseline hormone levels such as testosterone, luteinizing hormone (LH), and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which are generally within normal male ranges, supporting the diagnosis of isolated penile agenesis rather than endocrine dysfunction.[1] Renal and bladder ultrasonography is routinely conducted to detect associated urogenital anomalies, including renal agenesis or hydronephrosis, present in up to 50% of cases.[1] [6] A voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG) evaluates the urethra and bladder for fistulas or reflux, while scrotal ultrasound confirms bilateral testicular presence and epididymal structures.[1] In cases with suspected broader malformations, additional imaging such as abdominal ultrasound or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) may assess for cardiac, vertebral, or anorectal anomalies, though these are less common in isolated aphallia.[1] Echocardiography and spinal ultrasound are considered if clinical signs suggest VACTERL association.[4] Multidisciplinary input from pediatric urology, endocrinology, and genetics ensures comprehensive ruling out of syndromic features, with molecular testing reserved for atypical presentations.[30] Prenatal diagnosis is rare, as aphallia is not reliably detected by routine fetal ultrasound due to the subtle nature of external genital findings.[1]

Management

Neonatal and Initial Interventions

Upon identification of aphallia at birth, initial evaluation includes physical examination confirming the absence of penile tissue, presence of a well-developed scrotum with palpable testes, and an ectopic urethral meatus, often perineal or associated with a urethrorectal fistula.[5] Karyotyping verifies the typical 46,XY genotype, while ultrasonography assesses internal genitalia, renal structures, and rules out associated anomalies such as vertebral or cardiac defects.[4] Endocrine assessment evaluates testicular function and baseline hormone levels to inform future decisions, with multidisciplinary input from urology, endocrinology, and genetics essential due to the condition's rarity and variability.[5] Immediate interventions prioritize urinary tract management to prevent obstruction, infection, or renal damage, commonly involving vesicostomy in approximately 39% of cases for reliable bladder drainage when fistulas complicate voiding.[5] Perineal urethrostomy or temporary catheterization may address ectopic urethral openings, with fistula division deferred if stable to avoid neonatal complications.[31] No routine early gonadectomy or definitive genital surgery occurs in the neonatal phase; instead, stabilization precedes sex assignment discussions, historically favoring female rearing with orchiectomy to avert pubertal virilization, though evidence of subsequent gender dysphoria has prompted caution.[5] Contemporary approaches emphasize delaying irreversible procedures, favoring provisional male assignment to align with chromosomal sex and observed male gender identity in most reported cases, supported by parental counseling on potential phalloplasty options later.[5] Absent standardized guidelines, initial care avoids androgen suppression unless virilization risks immediate issues, prioritizing psychosexual development monitoring over hasty reassignment.[31] Associated anomalies, present in up to 80% of cases, necessitate concurrent evaluation and correction, such as cardiac assessments via echocardiography.[4]

Long-term Reconstruction

Long-term reconstruction for aphallia focuses on creating a functional neophallus to support male gender identity and standing voiding in genetic males, typically deferred until late childhood or puberty to accommodate growth and optimize tissue use.[1] Techniques often begin with temporary neophalloplasty using local flaps, such as the De Castro abdominal wall flap or scrotal flaps, to provide initial cosmesis and psychosexual development, followed by staged definitive procedures.[32] [1] Microsurgical free flaps, including radial forearm, osteocutaneous fibular, or myocutaneous latissimus dorsi flaps, are employed for more durable reconstruction, combined with urethroplasty using buccal mucosa or bladder grafts to form a neourethra.[32] Non-microsurgical options like groin or parascrotal flaps serve as alternatives when microsurgery is unavailable, though they yield shorter phalli with limited vascularity.[32] Urethral reconstruction remains a critical component, often performed via anterior-sagittal-transanorectal approaches with pedicled skin flaps augmented by buccal mucosa for durability, enabling perineal or epispadiac voiding. In a series of four 46,XY patients undergoing single-stage abdominal flap phalloplasty and urethroplasty (ages 9 months to 12 years), all achieved phallus survival with adequate male appearance, but three experienced urethral complications including partial dehiscence or necrosis, necessitating revisions or alternative voiding sites. Long-term follow-up (up to 5 years) showed one case with excellent functional and cosmetic results, underscoring the feasibility but highlighting revision needs. Complications are prevalent, with fistula rates up to 80% and dehiscence common, often requiring multiple interventions; erectile function typically requires later prosthetic implantation, while sensation remains suboptimal without nerve coaptation.[1] [32] Neophalloplasty is advocated over female assignment due to prenatal androgen imprinting and genetic maleness, with only 12% of cases opting for feminization in recent reviews.[32] [1] Multidisciplinary planning, including psychological support, is essential, though long-term data on satisfaction and function remain sparse, with phalloplasty performed in just 7% and planned in 24% of documented cases as of 2024.[1]

Supportive Care

Supportive care for aphallia primarily involves psychological counseling and a multidisciplinary team to address the emotional, social, and familial impacts of the condition. Upon diagnosis, immediate psychological counseling is provided to parents to alleviate emotional distress, facilitate informed decision-making on gender assignment and treatment options, and mitigate risks of stigma or family concealment of the affected child.[21] [8] A multidisciplinary approach is recommended, incorporating pediatric urologists, endocrinologists, psychologists, surgeons, and social workers to deliver holistic care beyond surgical interventions.[5] This team coordinates ongoing support to manage psychosocial and psychosexual challenges, evaluates family socioeconomic factors, and ensures access to detailed information on long-term outcomes such as fertility and sexual function.[5] [8] In low-resource settings, such care faces barriers like limited follow-up, underscoring the need for accessible counseling to prevent loss to treatment.[8] In the acute neonatal phase, supportive measures prioritize stabilization by addressing urinary obstruction or infection risks, such as through temporary diversion if sepsis threatens, while deferring definitive decisions until family counseling is complete.[5] Long-term elements include therapy sessions and support groups to enhance quality of life, with emphasis on reducing social isolation and preparing patients for adult psychosexual adjustment.[8] Approximately 17% of reported cases involve refusal of treatment or loss to follow-up, highlighting the critical role of sustained family engagement in supportive protocols.[5]

Prognosis and Outcomes

Surgical Success Rates

Surgical reconstruction for aphallia typically involves initial temporary neophalloplasty in infancy or childhood to support male gender identity and body image, followed by definitive procedures post-puberty using microsurgical techniques such as radial forearm free flap or De Castro flap. Success is primarily evaluated through aesthetic outcomes, voiding functionality, complication rates, and patient satisfaction, though large-scale, long-term data are limited by the condition's rarity (incidence approximately 1 in 30 million male births).[5] Scrotal flap phalloplasty, a non-microsurgical temporary approach utilizing scrotal tissue, demonstrates high aesthetic success. In a multi-institutional review of cases from 2011 to 2021 with at least one-year follow-up, all patients achieved satisfactory cosmetic results, forming a cylindrical neophallus resembling an uncircumcised penis without significant contraction, length loss, or scarring; no short- or long-term complications were reported.[33] This technique preserves distant donor sites for future definitive reconstruction and avoids the morbidity of adult microsurgical methods in prepubertal boys. Definitive phalloplasty outcomes focus on functionality (e.g., standing voiding, sensation, potential for erection via prosthesis), but systematic reviews indicate sparse long-term reporting on neophallus viability, sexual function, or psychological integration. Case series of De Castro technique modifications report good cosmetic results and patient satisfaction in isolated aphallia, serving as an initial reconstructive stage, though complication rates (e.g., late revisions in up to 47% of related pediatric neo-phalloplasties) necessitate multiple surgeries.[34]00140-6/abstract) Urethral reconstruction, often integrated, shows variable fistula rates (15% for onlay preputial island flaps, 32% for buccal mucosa grafts) and dehiscence (19% in select series), with overall satisfaction in small cohorts (e.g., all three patients in one study content with aphallia correction).[35] Microsurgical options like radial forearm flaps yield encouraging long-term aesthetics in associated conditions (e.g., exstrophy), but functional metrics such as penetrative capability or prosthesis integration remain understudied in pure aphallia.[36] Overall, while temporary procedures achieve near-100% aesthetic success in reported series, definitive surgery's functional success is constrained by high revision needs and absent robust metrics; multidisciplinary follow-up is essential, with outcomes influenced by associated anomalies (e.g., lower success in presphincteric types with higher malformation rates).[32]

Functional and Psychological Outcomes

Functional outcomes in aphallia vary based on sex assignment and surgical interventions, with urinary continence achieved in many cases through perineal urethrostomy or urethroplasty, though urethrorectal fistulas occur in approximately 46% of reported cases and may require additional management.[1] Phalloplasty in male-assigned patients enables standing urination in some instances but often necessitates multiple revisions due to inadequate growth during puberty and complications such as fistulas or strictures.[37] Sexual function remains limited, with prosthetic implantation allowing penetrative intercourse but poor sensation and erectile reliability; fertility potential exists given normal testicular histology in 97% of cases, though only isolated adult reports confirm viable sperm production and reproduction.[1][38] Psychological outcomes are influenced by gender assignment congruence with genetic sex (46,XY in nearly all cases), with female reassignment historically leading to gender dysphoria and patient-initiated reversal to male identity in multiple documented instances due to innate male-typical identity formation.[39][37] Male assignment aligns better with psychosexual development but can involve distress from cosmetic dissatisfaction, repeated surgeries, and social stigma, as seen in adolescent cases where patients exhibit embarrassment yet refuse reassignment.[40] Long-term adjustment data are scarce owing to the condition's rarity (incidence ~1 in 30 million births) and loss to follow-up in 17% of cases, underscoring the need for multidisciplinary support to mitigate risks of poor self-esteem and psychosexual maladjustment.[1][8]

Controversies and Debates

Sex Assignment Practices

In cases of aphallia, a congenital condition characterized by the complete absence of penile tissue in chromosomally male (46,XY) individuals, historical management protocols often recommended female sex assignment shortly after birth. This approach, influenced by the technical challenges of early phalloplasty and limited functional outcomes of penile reconstruction, typically involved bilateral orchiectomy, vaginoplasty, and rearing the child as female to facilitate urinary continence and perceived social adaptation.[41][40] However, longitudinal studies of such assignments reveal high rates of dissatisfaction and gender dysphoria, with many individuals later pursuing reassignment to male. In a review of 46,XY persons with penile agenesis raised as female, approximately 50-60% experienced male gender identity and sought reversal, often citing incongruence between assigned sex and innate sense of maleness, exacerbated by pubertal virilization from endogenous testosterone if gonads were retained.[39] Similar patterns emerged in related conditions like cloacal exstrophy with aphallia, where female rearing failed to align with genetic and hormonal male development, leading to psychological distress in over half of cases.[42] Contemporary guidelines increasingly advocate male sex assignment aligned with karyotype, gonadal function, and prenatal androgen exposure, deferring major interventions until adolescence or adulthood when patient autonomy can inform decisions. Successful male rearing has been documented in select cases, with perineal urethrostomy for initial voiding and delayed phalloplasty yielding functional erections and voiding in 70-80% of procedures, though fertility remains limited without gonadal preservation.[43][44] This shift reflects empirical evidence that female assignment disrupts psychosexual development in genetic males, prioritizing biological congruence over reconstructive feasibility.[5] Debates persist regarding the timing of sex assignment, with some advocating delayed decisions to assess emerging gender identity, but data indicate early male designation minimizes identity discordance, as prenatal androgen imprinting on the brain fosters male-typical cognition and behavior.[45] Multidisciplinary teams emphasize parental counseling on genetic sex, potential for virilization, and long-term outcomes, rejecting routine female reassignment absent compelling medical rationale.[4]

Ethical Considerations in Treatment

The treatment of aphallia raises profound ethical questions centered on the principles of autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice, particularly given the irreversible nature of genital surgeries performed on infants incapable of providing informed consent.[4] Historically, female sex assignment with orchiectomy and vaginoplasty was favored due to the technical challenges of phalloplasty and assumptions about easier feminization, but empirical outcomes have revealed high rates of gender dysphoria and patient-initiated reassignment to male in such cases, underscoring the harm of overriding likely male gender identity shaped by prenatal androgen exposure.[46] [47] This shift is evidenced by studies showing that 46,XY individuals with aphallia raised as females often experience dissatisfaction and seek male reassignment post-puberty, with risks amplified by the brain's masculinization from in utero testosterone.[48] [49] Contemporary ethical frameworks advocate for male sex assignment in most aphallia cases, prioritizing genetic and hormonal influences on gender identity over cosmetic normalization, while deferring non-urgent reconstructive surgeries until the patient can participate in decision-making.[50] [38] This approach mitigates non-maleficence by avoiding gonadectomy, which precludes fertility and aligns with data indicating superior psychosexual outcomes for male-reared patients, including preserved testicular function and potential paternity via assisted reproduction.[38] However, early interventions like perineal urethrostomy
