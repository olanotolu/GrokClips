Hakeem Lyon


Hakeem Lyon is a fictional character and central figure in the American musical drama television series Empire, which aired on Fox from 2015 to 2020, portrayed by actor Bryshere Y. Gray.[1] As the youngest son of hip-hop entrepreneur Lucious Lyon and his former wife Cookie Lyon, Hakeem is depicted as a brash, talented rapper whose professional ambitions within the family-run Empire Entertainment company are complicated by personal immaturity, familial betrayals, and rivalries with his brothers Andre and Jamal.[1] The character's arc highlights themes of succession struggles, artistic development, and redemption amid the high-stakes world of the music industry.[2]

Character Overview

Background and Role in Empire

Hakeem Lyon is the youngest son of Lucious Lyon and Cookie Lyon, portrayed by Bryshere Y. Gray in the Fox musical drama series Empire (2015â€“2020).[3] As Lucious' favored heir among his three sonsâ€”Andre, Jamal, and himselfâ€”Hakeem is depicted as an aspiring rapper with raw talent, signed to Empire Entertainment, the hip-hop empire Lucious built from street-level origins into a multimillion-dollar conglomerate.[3][4] His background reflects a privileged yet tumultuous upbringing under Lucious' influence, lacking close maternal guidance due to Cookie's 17-year imprisonment for drug trafficking charges shortly after his early childhood.[5] This estrangement fostered Hakeem's alignment with his father, amplifying family tensions upon Cookie's release in 2015, when she seeks to reclaim influence over Empire and her sons.[5] Within Empire Entertainment, Hakeem's role evolves from a flashy, immature artist prone to scandalsâ€”such as public feuds and reckless behaviorâ€”to a key performer driving the label's street-credible output through singles and tours.[6] In season 2, following Lucious' arrest, Hakeem ascends to CEO with assistance from girlfriend Camilla Marks, leveraging boardroom maneuvers and his celebrity to steer the company amid rival Lyon Dynasty challenges, though his tenure proves short-lived due to inexperience and betrayals.[6] His contributions include high-profile tracks like "Drip Drop" and "Empire," which underscore his commercial viability while highlighting ongoing power struggles central to the Lyon succession narrative.[6]

Core Personality and Traits

Hakeem Lyon is depicted as a prodigiously talented rapper with immense star potential, yet hindered by profound immaturity and a lack of discipline.[7] As the youngest Lyon son, he embodies entitlement, often prioritizing partying, indulgence in alcohol, and casual relationships over professional responsibilities, arriving hungover to recording sessions and flaunting a self-styled "bad boy" persona.[8] [9] His core flaw lies in aspiring to his father Lucious's mogul statusâ€”craving the power, glamour, and controlâ€”without the willingness to exert the necessary effort, as articulated by actor Bryshere Y. Gray.[7] This manifests in impulsive decision-making and unearned bravado, rendering him insufferable at times and prone to self-sabotage, though episodes reveal underlying vulnerability during family crises.[10] Hakeem's ambition drives him to seek validation and dominance within the family, yet his leadership falters due to being guided by lust and short-term desires rather than strategic pragmatism.[11] Despite these shortcomings, his raw creativity and charisma fuel standout musical performances, underscoring a tension between innate gift and personal recklessness.[12]

Creation and Development

Casting and Initial Conception

Hakeem Lyon was conceived by Empire co-creators Lee Daniels and Danny Strong as the youngest of three sons to hip-hop mogul Lucious Lyon and his ex-wife Cookie, positioned as a prodigiously talented yet immature rapper central to the family's succession struggles. The character draws from Daniels' autobiographical elements, including themes of familial dysfunction and artistic ambition in the music industry, while echoing Shakespearean dynamics akin to King Lear's favored but flawed heir. Hakeem's initial portrayal emphasizes swagger, entitlement, and raw musical prowess, reflecting influences from real hip-hop dynasties and Daniels' observations of young artists' excesses.[13][14] Bryshere Y. Gray, a 20-year-old rapper from Philadelphia known as Yazz the Greatest and formerly of the teen boy band Mindless Behavior, was cast as Hakeem in late 2014 for the series' January 7, 2015, Fox premiere. Selected for his authentic rapping ability and stage presence honed through music performances, Gray marked his acting debut with the role, bringing a lived-in edge to Hakeem's boastful demeanor despite lacking prior on-screen experience. Co-creator Danny Strong highlighted the character's appeal in scripting, noting Hakeem's vibrant personality as a favorite among writers for its dramatic potential.[8][15]

Character Evolution Across Seasons

Hakeem Lyon enters the series in its inaugural season as a prodigiously talented yet immature rapper, embodying the spoiled youngest son who leverages his father's favoritism while displaying reckless tendencies, including leaking unreleased tracks and pursuing impulsive romantic entanglements that exacerbate family tensions.[16] His early arc highlights a lack of accountability, with Lucious often mitigating the fallout from Hakeem's indiscretions, such as public feuds and professional sabotage.[17] In seasons 2 and 3, Hakeem experiences pivotal challenges that catalyze modest growth toward maturity, notably a kidnapping orchestrated by rivals that underscores vulnerabilities beyond his bravado and fosters a shift toward valuing interpersonal commitments.[18] His budding romance with Laura prompts efforts to adapt and demonstrate reliability, marking a departure from self-indulgence as he navigates the demands of impending fatherhood with Anika, though persistent impulsivity surfaces in endorsements of political figures and ongoing sibling rivalries.[19] These events compel Hakeem to balance artistic ambition with familial obligations, including co-founding Lyon Dynasty alongside his mother Cookie. By seasons 4 and 5, Hakeem's evolution manifests in heightened focus on paternal responsibilities amid custody disputes over daughter Bella, prioritizing stability and collaboration with ex-partner Tiana in raising their child while channeling personal turmoil into creative output, such as rage-fueled studio sessions yielding breakthrough tracks.[20] Despite relapses into volatility, including strikes against Empire productions, he increasingly asserts independence in business maneuvers and musical innovation. In the final season 6, Hakeem pursues acting ambitions by auditioning for the lead role of Lucious in an Empire biopic, an endeavor that idolizes his father yet requires scrutinizing the mogul's flaws and legacy, reflecting deeper self-examination and a maturation from prodigal son to reflective artist confronting inherited traits.[21] This arc culminates in attempts at leadership within the family empire, though external pressures like the biopic's demands limit full resolution of his growth trajectory.

Family and Interpersonal Dynamics

Relationships with Lyon Family Members

Hakeem's relationship with his father, Lucious Lyon, begins with favoritism, as Lucious identifies Hakeem's raw musical talent and positions him as a potential heir to Empire Entertainment, often shielding him from the harsher scrutiny given to his brothers.[22] This dynamic sours through Lucious' authoritarian control and betrayals, such as when Hakeem discovers Lucious' role in corporate maneuvers that undermine his independence, leading Hakeem to publicly diss his father and align against him in power struggles.[23] Despite these rifts, moments of reconciliation occur, particularly after traumatic events like Hakeem's shooting in Season 2, where Lucious' protective instincts resurface amid family crises.[24] With his mother, Cookie Lyon, Hakeem initially harbors resentment stemming from her 17-year imprisonment for drug charges, viewing her absence as abandonment during his upbringing in a challenging Philadelphia environment.[25] Their bond strengthens post-reunion, with Cookie advocating for Hakeem's career at her rival label Lyon Dynasty and intervening in his personal excesses, such as when she and Lucious confront him after he arrives intoxicated at Empire headquarters.[26] This maternal support fosters growth, though it is punctuated by conflicts over Hakeem's impulsive decisions, including romantic entanglements that Cookie often critiques.[27] Hakeem's interactions with his brothers, Andre and Jamal, are defined by intense sibling rivalry exacerbated by competition for Lucious' approval and control of the family empire. He frequently clashes with Jamal over artistic spotlight, as seen in jealous outbursts during Jamal's video shoots and diss tracks that escalate to physical confrontations, yet they occasionally collaborate on tracks like "Trapped" amid shared family threats.[28] With Andre, tensions arise from professional overlaps, such as Hakeem's dissatisfaction with Andre's involvement with singer Nessa, whom Hakeem had pursued, leading to public feuds and plots to undermine each other.[26] Despite betrayalsâ€”like Hakeem and Andre conspiring against Jamal after Lucious' arrestâ€”the brothers exhibit underlying loyalty, reuniting to support one another in crises, including Andre's mental health struggles and collective efforts to reclaim Empire.[29]

Romantic and Professional Entanglements

Hakeem's romantic pursuits often intertwined with the competitive dynamics of the Lyon family's music empire, creating conflicts that influenced corporate alliances, artist signings, and ownership battles. His relationships frequently involved women connected to Empire Entertainment or rival ventures, amplifying personal betrayals into professional repercussions.[30] In season 1, Hakeem maintained a year-long secret affair with the older Camilla Marks while publicly pairing with singer Tiana Brown for promotional purposes, dubbed "Takeem" to boost their joint music releases under Empire. Lucious Lyon discovered the relationship and coerced Camilla to end it and leave the country, viewing her as a distracting influence on Hakeem's career focus. Camilla resurfaced in season 2, married to investor Mimi Whiteman, whose acquisition of Empire shares threatened Lucious's control; Hakeem exposed Camilla's manipulation of Mimi by secretly recording their intimate encounter and sharing it, prompting Mimi to divest and leading to Camilla's murder of Mimi in retaliationâ€” an act that escalated family power struggles.[7][31][32] Hakeem's liaison with Anika Calhoun, formerly Lucious's fiancÃ©e and Empire's head of A&R, began in season 2 amid tensions following Hakeem's kidnapping, with the two engaging in a sexual encounter that reignited prior chemistry. Professionally, Anika partnered with Cookie Lyon to launch the rival Lyon Dynasty label, signing Hakeem as a flagship artist to challenge Empire's dominance. Their affair resulted in Anika's pregnancy and the birth of daughter Bella, sparking protracted custody disputes; Anika secured initial full custody in season 4 amid concerns over Hakeem's post-trauma instability, though Hakeem later regained primary custody through blackmail leveraging Anika's vulnerabilities.[33][24][34] Hakeem's arrangement with Tiana evolved from a contrived season 1 romance to genuine collaboration, though strained by his infidelity and her hidden same-sex relationship. As fellow Empire artists, they co-performed tracks and navigated label politics, but recurring tensionsâ€”including Hakeem's volatile behaviorâ€”undermined their partnership, contributing to Tiana's eventual wariness and professional distance.[35][7]

Musical Career and Achievements

Key Performances and Releases

Hakeem Lyon's musical output in Empire prominently features hip-hop tracks emphasizing bravado and street credibility, beginning with his season 1 single "No Apologies," performed in episode 2, "The Outspoken King," aired January 14, 2015.[36] The track, showcasing his rapid-fire delivery, appeared on the Original Soundtrack from Season 1 of Empire, highlighting his role as the label's rising rap prospect.[37] Follow-up "Drip Drop," debuted in an early episode, marked a pivotal moment by elevating Hakeem's artistic standing within the narrative, as noted for compelling listeners to view him beyond a party rapper archetype.[38] Collaborations amplified his profile, including the duet "Live in the Moment" with brother Jamal Lyon in season 1's "Our Dancing Days" episode, blending rap and R&B elements to underscore familial tensions.[37] Another standout, "You're So Beautiful," featured Hakeem alongside Jamal, released as part of the season 1 soundtrack and praised for its emotional depth amid the show's interpersonal drama.[39] "Can't Truss 'Em," performed in season 1 episode 6, "Out, Damned Spot," further demonstrated his lyrical prowess, positioning him as a contender against industry rivals.[38] In season 2, Hakeem's rivalry with rapper Freda Gatz culminated in a high-profile rap battle, a raw freestyle exchange that tested his battle skills and became one of the series' most viewed musical segments.[40] Tracks like "Armani" and "Right There," featuring his verses, contributed to the Empire: The Complete Season 2 compilation, reflecting his push for solo dominance post-label shakeups.[41] Later seasons saw performances such as the season 4 studio battle against Blake and a season 6 Bossy Fest set with Maya, integrating his music into evolving power struggles and redemptive arcs.[42][43] These releases, tied to Empire's soundtracks, often charted on Billboard, validating the in-universe success of Hakeem's catalog.[38]

Business Ambitions and Setbacks

In the second season of Empire, Hakeem Lyon co-founded Lyon Dynasty, a rival record label established by his mother Cookie Lyon after their departure from Empire Entertainment due to escalating conflicts with patriarch Lucious Lyon. This venture represented Hakeem's ambition to assert independence in the music industry, leveraging his rap skills and star potential to build a competing empire focused on signing new talent and promoting his own releases, such as collaborations with artists like Valentina.[30][44] Lyon Dynasty encountered significant setbacks, including sabotage orchestrated by Lucious, who acquired majority stakes in radio stations to limit the label's airplay and poached promising artists like Valentina for Empire. Internal challenges compounded these external pressures, such as the kidnapping of Hakeem during a raid on the label's offices in October 2015, which disrupted operations and heightened family tensions. Additionally, personal entanglements, including Hakeem's relationships and the revelation of Anika's pregnancy with his child, diverted focus from business growth.[33][18][27] By the midseason finale of season 2 in December 2015, Lyon Dynasty's viability waned amid corporate maneuvers, culminating in its acquisition by Empire Entertainment, which facilitated Cookie's return to the parent company but marked the end of Hakeem's independent label endeavor. Hakeem later ascended to CEO of Empire in subsequent seasons, influenced by alliances like that with Camilla Marks-Whiteman, though this role brought further pressures, including demands to sever ties with associates to consolidate power. These experiences underscored Hakeem's recurring pattern of ambitious pursuits undermined by familial rivalries and impulsive decisions.[45][46][47]

Storyline Arcs

Seasons 1-2: Rise and Family Conflicts

In season 1, Hakeem Lyon is introduced as the youngest and most favored son of Lucious Lyon, showcasing his rapping talent early on during a performance aboard his father's yacht in the pilot episode aired on January 7, 2015.[1] As Empire Entertainment's rising star, Hakeem performs tracks like "Drip Drop" and collaborates with artists such as Tiana Brown, building momentum toward major events including the opening of the club's Leviticus venue.[48] However, his brash personality leads to scandals, such as a public altercation at a high-end restaurant, highlighting his immaturity amid the high-stakes competition for his father's succession.[49] Hakeem's relationship with Anika Calhoun, Empire's executive, evolves into a romantic entanglement that draws Lucious's ire, culminating in betrayal when Lucious begins an affair with her, prompting Hakeem to lash out with a diss track targeting his brother Jamal.[30] Family tensions escalate as Cookie Lyon, recently released from prison, attempts to reconnect with Hakeem, who initially resists but gradually aligns with her against Lucious's favoritism toward Jamal. This rift intensifies during Lucious's ALS diagnosis revelation and the ensuing battle for control of Empire, where Hakeem's loyalty wavers, leading to physical confrontations, including Cookie slapping him for disrespecting Jamal.[50] Transitioning into season 2, premiering September 23, 2015, Hakeem defects to his mother Cookie's newly formed Lyon Dynasty label, seeking independence from Lucious's dominance and releasing music that propels his career forward with sold-out performances and viral tracks.[51] His rise is interrupted by a kidnapping orchestrated by Laz Delgado, Anika's associate, demanding ransom from the Lyons, which underscores the violent undercurrents of their empire-building ambitions.[52] Despite this trauma, Hakeem rebounds, dating singer Laura and navigating betrayals, such as Lucious's manipulations to reclaim him, while clashing with Jamal over artistic direction and company loyalty, exemplifying the persistent family conflicts that both hinder and fuel his ascent.[53]

Seasons 3-4: Power Struggles and Maturity Tests

In season 3, Hakeem recovers from the gunshot wound sustained at Lucious and Anika's wedding, initially suffering memory loss that strains his relationship with his father, whom he confronts over the incident.[54] Anika gives birth to their daughter Bella, thrusting Hakeem into fatherhood amid escalating family tensions, as Lucious marries Anika despite her pregnancy by Hakeem, leading to Hakeem's breakup with fiancÃ©e Laura.[54] These events test Hakeem's maturity, as he bonds with Bella under Lucious's guidance, learning to hold and care for her while grappling with his impulsive past.[55] Hakeem's power struggles intensify through legal repercussions from his reckless decisions, including a $50 million lawsuit against Empire stemming from a goon he hired for his birthday party assaulting Tiana's friend Kennedy, exposing his poor judgment in security arrangements.[56] The season's climax involves Diana DuBois, leveraging her influence to prompt Child Protective Services to remove Bella from Anika's custody, framing it as a safety issue tied to Lyon family volatility; Hakeem responds by organizing a "Free Bella" campaign, vandalizing DuBois properties, and negotiating fiercely to reclaim his daughter, demonstrating emerging resolve amid co-parenting disputes with Anika.[57][58] Transitioning to season 4, Hakeem's custody battle with Anika escalates into a central power struggle, as she serves him papers amid the ongoing Lyon-DuBois feud, prompting Hakeem to go on the run and employ desperate tactics to avoid losing Bella, whom he deems himself better suited to raise given Anika's history of pushing a pregnant woman down stairs.[59][60] Angelo DuBois publicly contrasts Anika's "elite pedigree" with Hakeem's "drug-and-sex-fueled criminal exploits," underscoring class-based power dynamics that challenge Hakeem's legitimacy as a parent.[61] These trials further probe Hakeem's maturity, as the custody hearing forces him to confront his vulnerabilities and past instability, while the broader Lyon family warâ€”culminating in casualties and revelationsâ€”tests his loyalty and strategic acumen against external threats like the DuBois clan.[62] Hakeem's efforts to protect Bella, including seeking comfort from Anika during abductions, reveal a shift toward accountability, though his methods remain impulsive, highlighting incomplete growth in navigating familial and legal power structures.[4]

Seasons 5-6: Leadership Attempts and Resolutions

In season 5, Hakeem recovers from a near-fatal shooting sustained at the end of season 4, which results in the removal of part of his lung and initially impairs his ability to perform.[63][64] Amid the Lyon family's efforts to reclaim control of Empire Entertainment following Lucious's presumed death and subsequent return, Hakeem asserts himself by immersing in studio work for over 24 hours at a stretch, refusing to abandon his musical output despite physical setbacks.[65] He records diss tracks targeting rivals and family tensions, rejecting Andre's suggestion for therapy as a means to process trauma, thereby demonstrating an attempt at self-directed leadership in his career recovery rather than yielding to external guidance.[66] These efforts contribute to partial resolutions within the family dynamic, as Hakeem regains his "bars" and lyrical prowess by episode 3, enabling him to support broader Lyon initiatives like venture capital pursuits and artist signings, though ultimate company leadership eludes him amid ongoing power shifts.[67] In season 6, Hakeem's leadership ambitions manifest through interventions in the production of a biopic chronicling Empire's history, where he challenges Lucious's script revisions to preserve an accurate depiction of family events, positioning himself as a guardian of the company's narrative legacy.[68] During a press conference on September 24, 2019, announcing Andre as permanent CEO, Hakeem disrupts the event by altering the run-of-show, underscoring his reluctance to passively accept hierarchical decisions.[69] He further commits to the family enterprise by portraying Lucious in the biopic on October 9, 2019, a career pivot that prioritizes legacy preservation over solo stardom but sparks internal drama.[70] Resolutions culminate in the series finale on April 21, 2020, with Hakeem persuading Andre to forge a bond with his young son, exemplifying matured interpersonal leadership that aids family unity despite unresolved business fractures and the absence of formal executive control.[71][72] This arc reflects Hakeem's evolution from impulsive assertions to constructive influence, aligning with the Lyons' overarching theme of contested authority yielding tentative harmony.

Reception and Analysis

Critical Perspectives

Critics have frequently characterized Hakeem Lyon's portrayal as emblematic of immature and entitled archetypes in depictions of young black men within hip-hop culture, arguing that his consistent displays of impulsivity and poor judgment reinforce stereotypes of irresponsibility and arrested development. For instance, reviewers noted his behavior as that of an "entitled moron with mommy issues," contributing to broader concerns that the series perpetuated negative images of black family dynamics and youth.[73] Similarly, rankings of Empire characters placed Hakeem near the bottom for likability, citing his lack of redeemable qualities amid patterns of misogyny and self-sabotage.[74] Scholarly analyses of the series extend this critique to questions of black masculinity, positioning Hakeem as a figure who navigates hip-hop's conventional emphasis on heterosexual male dominance through exaggerated bravado and relational volatility, often at the expense of nuanced growth. One study on media stereotypes in Empire identified viewer reinforcement of tropes such as black male aggression and homophobia, with Hakeem's arcsâ€” including his feuds and romantic entanglementsâ€”serving as vehicles for these portrayals rather than subversion.[75][22] Another examination of black masculinity in television highlighted how characters like Hakeem align with persistent stereotypes of hyper-masculine, underachieving black youth, limiting representational diversity despite the show's commercial success.[76] Some perspectives frame Hakeem as a deliberate "love-to-hate" antagonist designed to provoke, yet this intentionality has drawn fire for prioritizing dramatic excess over realistic evolution, with his leadership attempts in later seasons underscoring unresolved immaturity rather than maturation. Critics from outlets like The Atlantic scrutinized episodes where Hakeem's masculinity is testedâ€”such as his kidnapping and subsequent bravadoâ€” as resolving too superficially, failing to interrogate underlying cultural pressures on black male identity in entertainment.[77] Overall, these views contend that Hakeem's static traits, while entertaining, risk entrenching reductive narratives about black ambition and family conflict, echoing wider indictments of Empire as veering into "coonery" territory despite its progressive elements elsewhere.[78]

Fan and Cultural Responses

Fans of Empire often praised Hakeem Lyon's musical performances for their energy and appeal, with outlets highlighting episodes featuring his tracks as among the series' strongest musical moments.[79][40] His portrayal as the fun, brash rapper delivered entertaining antics that some viewers found to be a highlight, contributing to his status as a polarizing yet engaging figure.[80] However, others criticized Hakeem's impulsive and hotheaded traits, ranking him low in likability polls and citing a lack of redeemable qualities amid his selfish decisions.[74][81] Culturally, Hakeem's character sparked debates over stereotypes in Black representation on television, with critics arguing his bling-obsessed, lothario persona reinforced tropes of thuggish youth in hip-hop.[82][83] Academic analyses examined how Empire perpetuated such exaggerated racial and gender stereotypes through Hakeem's arc, portraying him as immature and brute-like in family power dynamics.[75][84] Despite these concerns, some responses viewed the show's depiction, including Hakeem's, as breaking down stereotypes by embracing multifaceted Black family experiences in the music industry.[85]

Portrayal Controversies and Stereotypes

Hakeem Lyon's portrayal as the youngest Lyon son and aspiring hip-hop artist has faced criticism for amplifying stereotypes of young black men as entitled, immature, and hypersexualized within the rap industry. Critics have labeled him an "entitled moron with mommy issues," pointing to recurrent depictions of him strutting shirtless amid entourages of women and cash, contrasted with moments of vulnerability where he is dominated or "punked out" by family members, evoking tropes of the insecure yet brash "thug-gangster-hoodrat."[73] This characterization, according to Jozen Cummings of Billboard, renders Hakeem the least likable figure in the series, with "hate for Hakeem know[ing] no bounds" due to his apparent absence of redeemable qualities in early seasons.[74] The character's frequent promiscuous encounters and objectification of women have drawn accusations of reinforcing misogynistic elements in hip-hop culture representations. Analyses describe Hakeem as lusting after women and engaging in casual sexual relationships, which align with exaggerated portrayals of black male hypersexuality, even as his musical success propels the narrative.[76] His romantic entanglements, such as with Camilla Marks, are tied to the "jezebel" archetype of seductive, manipulative black or mixed-race women, perpetuating gender stereotypes where female characters serve male ambitions or rivalries through sexuality.[86] Such dynamics, critics argue, prioritize dramatic excess over nuanced development, catering to non-black viewers' preconceptions of "ratchet" black behavior in entertainment.[73] Broader debates on Empire's impact question whether Hakeem's arcâ€”initially defined by recklessness and familial rebellionâ€”bolsters harmful racial stereotypes by drawing from real hip-hop figures' flaws without sufficient counterbalance in early episodes. While the series evolves his role toward business involvement and maturity, detractors contend the foundational portrayal risks entrenching views of black youth in music as inherently spoiled and undisciplined, prioritizing spectacle over realistic causal progression in character growth.[73][86] These elements have fueled discussions on media's role in shaping cultural perceptions, with some attributing the show's appeal to its unapologetic embrace of controversy-laden tropes.[76]
