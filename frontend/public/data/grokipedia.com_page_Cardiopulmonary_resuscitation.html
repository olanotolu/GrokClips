Cardiopulmonary resuscitation

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is an emergency procedure used during cardiac or respiratory arrest that involves chest compressions, often combined with artificial ventilation, to preserve brain function and maintain circulation until spontaneous breathing and heartbeat can be restored. It is recommended for those who are unresponsive with no breathing or abnormal breathing, for example, agonal respirations.[1] CPR involves chest compressions for adults between 5 cm (2.0 in) and 6 cm (2.4 in) deep and at a rate of at least 100 to 120 per minute.[2] The rescuer may also provide artificial ventilation by either exhaling air into the subject's mouth or nose (mouth-to-mouth resuscitation) or using a device that pushes air into the subject's lungs (mechanical ventilation). The 2025 American Heart Association guidelines emphasize early and high-quality chest compressions over artificial ventilation; a simplified CPR method involving only chest compressions is recommended for untrained rescuers.[2][3] With children, however, the 2025 American Heart Association guidelines indicate that doing only compressions may result in worse outcomes, because such problems in children normally arise from respiratory issues rather than from cardiac ones, given their young age.[1] Chest compression to breathing ratios are set at 30 to 2 in adults. CPR alone is unlikely to restart the heart. Its main purpose is to restore the partial flow of oxygenated blood to the brain and heart. The objective is to delay tissue death and to extend the brief window of opportunity for a successful resuscitation without permanent brain damage. Administration of an electric shock to the subject's heart, termed defibrillation, is usually needed to restore a viable, or "perfusing", heart rhythm. Defibrillation is effective only for certain heart rhythms, namely ventricular fibrillation or pulseless ventricular tachycardia, rather than asystole or pulseless electrical activity, which usually requires the treatment of underlying conditions to restore cardiac function. Early shock, when appropriate, is recommended. CPR may succeed in inducing a heart rhythm that may be shockable. In general, CPR is continued until the person has a return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) or is declared dead.[4]

Medical uses

CPR is indicated for any unresponsive person with no breathing or only occasional agonal gasps, as this indicates likely cardiac arrest.[5] If a person has a pulse but is not breathing (respiratory arrest), artificial ventilation alone may be appropriate. However, due to challenges in accurately assessing a pulse, current guidelines recommend that lay rescuers not check for one and instead start CPR if the person is unresponsive and not breathing normally; healthcare professionals may check for a pulse within 10 seconds.[5] In cardiac arrest due to trauma, initiate CPR while prioritizing rapid treatment of reversible causes such as hypovolemia, tension pneumothorax, or pericardial tamponade; withhold CPR only if there are obvious signs of irreversible death (e.g., decapitation, incineration).[5][6] For suspected opioid overdose with respiratory or cardiac arrest, administer naloxone (e.g., intranasal or intramuscular) in addition to starting CPR.[7]

Pathophysiology

CPR is used on people in cardiac arrest to oxygenate the blood and maintain a cardiac output to keep vital organs alive. Blood circulation and oxygenation are required to transport oxygen to the tissues. The physiology of CPR involves generating a pressure gradient between the arterial and venous vascular beds; CPR achieves this via multiple mechanisms, including direct compression of the heart against the spine (cardiac pump) and changes in intrathoracic pressure (thoracic pump).[8] The brain is highly sensitive to ischemia and may sustain hypoxic-ischemic injury after blood flow has been stopped for 4 to 6 minutes, with irreversible damage becoming likely after 8 to 10 minutes.[9] Typically, if blood flow ceases for one to two hours, widespread cell death occurs in less tolerant organs like the kidneys and liver, though tolerance varies by organ and conditions such as hypothermia (e.g., in near-drownings) can extend the viable window. The chances of successful resuscitation decrease significantly if CPR is not initiated promptly, ideally within minutes of arrest, with survival rates dropping by approximately 10% per minute of delay.[10] The heart also rapidly loses the ability to maintain a normal rhythm. Following cardiac arrest, effective CPR enables enough oxygen to reach the brain to delay brain death and allows the heart to remain responsive to defibrillation attempts.[11] If an incorrect compression rate is used during CPR, going against standing American Heart Association (AHA) guidelines of 100â€“120 compressions per minute, this can cause a net decrease in venous return of blood, for what is required, to fill the heart.[12] For example, if a compression rate of above 120 compressions per minute is used consistently throughout the entire CPR process, this error could adversely affect survival rates and outcomes for the victim.[12]

Order of CPR in a first aid sequence

The sequence for initiating CPR in first aid for suspected cardiac arrest follows current guidelines from organizations such as the American Heart Association (AHA) and the Red Cross.[13][5] The recommended order for a lay rescuer is:
  1. Ensure scene safety and check for responsiveness: Tap the person and shout to see if they respond. Check for normal breathing (do not mistake gasping for normal breaths).
  2. If unresponsive and not breathing normally, activate the emergency response system (e.g., call 911) immediately. If an automated external defibrillator (AED) is nearby, send someone to retrieve it or get it yourself if alone and feasible without delaying action.
  3. Begin CPR immediately with chest compressions (Hands-Only CPR for adults): Place hands on the center of the chest and compress at a rate of 100â€“120 per minute to a depth of at least 5 cm (2 inches), allowing full recoil.
  4. As soon as the AED arrives, turn it on, follow the voice prompts, and deliver a shock if advised, then immediately resume CPR.
If multiple rescuers are present, delegate tasks: one calls emergency medical services (EMS) and retrieves the AED while others start CPR without delay.

Exception to the main sequence

For certain situations, the sequence prioritizes immediate CPR to address hypoxia:
  • If alone with a drowning victim or a child/infant who is unresponsive (e.g., found unconscious), perform about 2 minutes (5 cycles) of CPR first, using conventional CPR (30 compressions to 2 breaths). Then activate EMS and retrieve an AED if available. This applies because cardiac arrest in these cases often stems from respiratory causes rather than primary cardiac issues.[13]
If a phone is not immediately available, complete the initial CPR cycles before calling, provided return to the victim or EMS arrival is swift (under 2 minutes).

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation Methods

The American Heart Association (AHA) and International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation (ILCOR) periodically update CPR guidelines based on evidence reviews. The 2025 AHA guidelines continue to emphasize high-quality CPR, with key elements including the CAB sequence (chest compressions, airway, breathing) for all age groups except newborns, where neonatal resuscitation protocols apply. High-quality CPR involves compressions at 100-120 per minute, adequate depth, full chest recoil, and minimizing interruptions to less than 10 seconds.[5] The most important aspects of CPR are few interruptions of chest compressions, sufficient speed and depth of compressions, complete recoil between compressions, and avoiding excessive ventilation. It is unclear if a few minutes of CPR before defibrillation results in different outcomes than immediate defibrillation.[5]

Methods

Compressions with rescue breaths

Standard CPR involves chest compressions combined with ventilations (rescue breaths) for victims of cardiac arrest who are unresponsive, not breathing normally, or only gasping. Ventilations may be omitted by untrained lay rescuers for adults in non-asphyxial arrests (e.g., not drowning).[14] For ventilations, use a head-tilt chin-lift to open the airway, unless cervical spine injury is suspected, in which case use jaw thrust. For infants, keep the head neutral.[15] Compressions are performed on the lower half of the sternum. Rescue breaths involve pinching the nose and delivering mouth-to-mouth breaths, allowing visible chest rise. For infants, cover mouth and nose simultaneously. The compression-to-ventilation ratio is 30:2 for single rescuers or when using bag-mask; for two rescuers on children or infants (post-neonatal), 15:2 may be used. For victims of drowning, start with 2 ventilations. In advanced airway management (e.g., endotracheal tube), provide 1 breath every 6 seconds (8-10 per minute) without pausing compressions.[16] For newborns, follow Neonatal Resuscitation Program (NRP) guidelines with a 3:1 compression-to-ventilation ratio initially. The 30:2 or 15:2 ratios apply to older infants and children.[17] Compression rate is 100-120 per minute for all ages. Depth: adults 5-6 cm (2-2.4 inches); children approximately 5 cm (2 inches) or one-third anteroposterior chest diameter; infants approximately 4 cm (1.5 inches) or one-third chest diameter. Use two hands for adults, one or two for children, and two thumbs encircling hands (preferred) or heel of one hand for infants.[14] Barrier devices (e.g., face shields) may be used to reduce infection risk.[15] If cardiac arrest is suspected, call for help and use an AED if available, as early defibrillation is critical for shockable rhythms. Continue CPR until return of spontaneous circulation, advanced care arrives, or the scene is unsafe. The CAB sequence prioritizes immediate compressions; pulse checks are limited to 10 seconds by professionals.[14]

Compression only

For untrained lay rescuers aiding adults in out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, compression-only CPR (without ventilations) is recommended, as it is simpler and equally or more effective for non-asphyxial causes. Dispatcher-assisted instructions often guide this approach.[14] Perform continuous sternal compressions at 100-120 per minute to a depth of 5-6 cm, allowing full recoil. Compression-only is less effective for children, where respiratory causes predominate; use standard CPR if trained. Exceptions include drownings and overdoses, where ventilations are preferred if feasible. For cardiac arrest due to non-cardiac causes or patients under 20, standard CPR is superior.[16] The rhythm of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees (104 bpm) or "Another One Bites the Dust" by Queen (110 bpm) approximates the ideal rate.[15]

Prone CPR

Standard CPR is performed in the supine position. Prone (reverse) CPR, compressing the back between the scapulae, may be considered when the patient cannot be safely turned supine (e.g., during surgery), but supine is preferred when possible.[18]

Pregnancy

In pregnant patients at 20+ weeks gestation, perform manual left uterine displacement (e.g., towel under right hip for 15-30 degree tilt) to relieve aortocaval compression. If ineffective, prepare for resuscitative hysterotomy immediately upon arrest recognition, aiming for delivery within 5 minutes to improve maternal and fetal outcomes.[19]

Family presence

Family presence during CPR is recommended in most situations, as it can aid psychological coping for families. Institutions should develop supportive policies and train teams to manage this. Evidence supports benefits for both adults and children.[20]

Other

Interposed abdominal compression-CPR (IAC-CPR), a three-rescuer technique adding abdominal compressions during chest recoil, may be considered as an adjunct in hospital settings and pediatric cardiac arrest (Class IIb). Evidence for pre-hospital benefit remains limited.[19] Targeted temperature management post-ROSC is standard, but intra-arrest cooling lacks clear benefit. Internal cardiac massage involves direct manual compression of the heart via thoracotomy, used in open-chest scenarios like trauma or surgery. Active compression-decompression devices have not demonstrated improved outcomes over standard manual CPR.[5]

Use of Devices

Use of devices

Defibrillators

A defibrillator is a machine that produces defibrillation: electric shocks that can restore the normal heart function of the victim. The common model of a defibrillator out of a hospital is the automated external defibrillator (AED), a portable device that is especially easy to use because it produces recorded voice instructions. Defibrillation is only indicated for some arrhythmias (abnormal heart beatings), specifically ventricular fibrillation (VF) and pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT). Defibrillation is not indicated if the patient has a normal pulse or is still conscious. Also, it is not indicated in asystole or pulseless electrical activity (PEA), in those cases a normal CPR would be used to oxygenate the brain until the heart function can be restored. Inappropriate defibrillation, such as in non-shockable rhythms or conscious patients, can induce dangerous arrhythmias like ventricular fibrillation.[21] When a patient does not have heart beatings (or they present a sort of arrhythmia that will stop the heart immediately), it is recommended that someone asks for a defibrillator (because they are quite common in the present time),[22] for trying with it a defibrillation on the already unconscious victim, in case it is successful.

Order of defibrillation in a first aid sequence

It is recommended to call for emergency medical services before a defibrillation. Start CPR immediately and apply the AED as soon as it becomes available, minimizing interruptions to chest compressions. All these tasks (calling by phone, getting an AED, and the chest compressions and rescue breaths maneuvers of CPR) can be distributed between many rescuers who make them simultaneously.[23] The defibrillator itself would indicate if more CPR maneuvers are required. As a slight variation for that sequence, if the rescuer is completely alone with a victim of drowning, or with a child who was already unconscious when the rescuer arrived, the rescuer would do the CPR maneuvers during 2 minutes (approximately 5 cycles of ventilations and compressions); after that, the rescuer would call to emergency medical services, and then it could be tried a search for a defibrillator nearby (the CPR maneuvers are supposed to be the priority for the drowned and most of the already collapsed children).[24][23][22] As another possible variation, if a rescuer is completely alone and without a phone nearby, and is aiding any other victim (not a victim of drowning, nor an already unconscious child), perform CPR for about 2 minutes, then call emergency services (leaving the victim briefly if necessary to do so). After the call, retrieve a nearby defibrillator and use it, continuing CPR as needed.[22]

Defibrillation

The standard defibrillation device, prepared for fast use out of the medical centers, is the automated external defibrillator (AED), a portable machine of small size (similar to a briefcase) that can be used by any user with no previous training. That machine produces recorded voice instructions that guide the user along the defibrillation process. It also checks the victim's condition to automatically apply electric shocks at the correct level, if they are needed. Other models are semi-automatic and require the user to push a button before an electric shock. A defibrillator may ask for applying CPR maneuvers, so the patient would be placed lying in a face-up position. Water and metals transmit the electric current. This depends on the amount of water, but it is convenient to avoid starting the defibrillation on a floor with puddles and to dry the wet areas of the patient before (fast, even with any cloth, if that is enough). It is not necessary to remove the patient's jewels or piercings, but it should be avoided placing the patches of the defibrillator directly on top of them. The patches with electrodes are put on the positions that appear at the right. In very small bodies: children between 1 and 8 years, and, in general, similar bodies up to 25 kg approximately, it is recommended the use of children's size patches with reduced electric doses. If that is not possible, sizes and doses for adults would be used, and, if the patches were too big, one would be placed on the chest and the other on the back (no matter which of them). As of the 2025 AHA guidelines, among devices for improving CPR, only defibrillators have demonstrated improved outcomes over standard CPR alone for shockable out-of-hospital cardiac arrest.[27] When a defibrillator has been used, it should remain attached to the patient until emergency services arrive.[28]

Devices for timing CPR

Timing devices can feature a metronome (an item carried by many ambulance crews) to assist the rescuer in achieving the correct rate. Some units can also give timing reminders for performing compressions, ventilating, and changing operators.[29]

Devices for assisting in manual CPR

Mechanical chest compression devices are not better than standard manual compressions.[30] Their use is reasonable in situations where manual compressions are not safe to perform, such as in a moving vehicle.[30] Audible and visual prompting may improve the quality of CPR and prevent the decrease of compression rate and depth that naturally occurs with fatigue,[31][32] and to address this potential improvement, a number of devices have been developed to help improve CPR technique. These items can be devices to be placed on top of the chest, with the rescuer's hands going over the device, and a display or audio feedback giving information on depth, force or rate,[33] or in a wearable format such as a glove.[34] Several published evaluations show that these devices can improve the performance of chest compressions.[35][36] As well as its use during actual CPR on a cardiac arrest victim, which relies on the rescuer carrying the device with them, these devices can also be used as part of training programs to improve basic skills in performing correct chest compressions.[37]

Devices for providing automatic CPR

Mechanical CPR has not seen as much use as mechanical ventilation; however, use in the prehospital setting is increasing.[38] Devices on the market include the LUCAS device,[39] developed at the University Hospital of Lund,[40] and AutoPulse. Both use straps around the chest to secure the patient. The first generation of the LUCAS uses a gas-driven piston and motor-driven constricting band, while later versions are battery-operated. There are several advantages to automated devices: they allow rescuers to focus on performing other interventions; they do not fatigue and begin to perform less effective compressions, as humans do; they can perform effective compressions in limited-space environments such as air ambulances,[42] where manual compressions are difficult, and they allow ambulance workers to be strapped in safely rather than standing over a patient in a speeding vehicle.[43] However the disadvantages are cost to purchase, time to train emergency personnel to use them, interruption to CPR to implement, potential for incorrect application and the need for multiple device sizes.[44][45] Several studies have shown little or no improvement in survival rates[46][47][48] but acknowledge the need for more study.[49] The 2025 AHA guidelines, informed by multiple RCTs, state that mechanical CPR devices do not improve survival or neurological outcomes compared to manual CPR but may be considered in scenarios where high-quality manual compressions cannot be maintained, such as during transport.[27]

Mobile apps for providing CPR instructions

To support training and incident management, mobile apps have been published in the largest app markets. An evaluation of 61 available apps has revealed that a large number do not follow international guidelines for basic life support and many apps are not designed in a user-friendly way.[50] As a result, the Red Cross updated and endorsed its emergency preparedness application, which uses pictures, text, and videos to assist the user.[51] The UK Resuscitation Council has an app, called Lifesaver, which shows how to perform CPR.[52]

Effectivity rate

CPR oxygenates the body and brain, which favors making a later defibrillation and the advanced life support. Even in the case of a "non-shockable" rhythm, such as pulseless electrical activity (PEA) where defibrillation is not indicated, effective CPR is no less important. Used alone, CPR will result in few complete recoveries, though the outcome without CPR is almost uniformly fatal.[53] Studies have shown that immediate CPR followed by defibrillation within 3â€“5 minutes of sudden VF cardiac arrest dramatically improves survival. In high-performing regions such as Seattle/King County, where CPR training is widespread and defibrillation by EMS personnel follows quickly, the survival rate is about 20% for all causes and approximately 43% for shockable rhythms (based on 2001-2020 data).[54][55] In other urban areas like New York City, recent data indicate a survival rate of approximately 20% for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest (as of 2024), influenced by response times and bystander intervention.[56][57] Similarly, in-hospital CPR is more successful when arrests are witnessed, occur in the ICU, or occur in patients wearing heart monitors.[58][59]

Consequences

Survival rates: As of 2023, in US hospitals, approximately 25% of patients who received CPR survived to hospital discharge.[73] In 2023, for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest (OHCA) in the US, overall survival to hospital discharge was 10.3%, with 13.0% for cases receiving bystander CPR.[74] Since 2003, targeted temperature management after CPR[75] and other improvements, including high-quality compressions, have contributed to gradual increases in survival rates and better neurological outcomes, though OHCA rates dipped during the COVID-19 pandemic before recovering.[76]

Organ donation

Organ donation is usually made possible by CPR, even if CPR does not save the patient. If there is a return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC), all organs can be considered for donation. If the patient does not achieve ROSC, and CPR continues until an operating room is available, the kidneys and liver can still be considered for donation.[77] 1,000 organs per year in the US are transplanted from patients who had CPR.[78] Donations can be taken from 40% of patients who have ROSC and later become brain-dead.[79] Up to 8 organs can be taken from each donor,[80] and an average of 3 organs are taken from each patient who donates organs.[78]

Mental abilities

Mental abilities are about the same for survivors before and after CPR for 89% of patients, based on before and after counts of 12,500 US patients' Cerebral-Performance Category (CPC[81]) codes in a 2000â€“2009 study of CPR in hospitals. 1% more survivors were in comas than before CPR. 5% more needed help with daily activities. 5% more had moderate mental problems and could still be independent.[82] For CPR outside hospitals, a Copenhagen study of 2,504 patients in 2007-2011 found that 21% of survivors developed moderate mental problems but could still be independent, and 11% of survivors developed severe mental problems, so they needed daily help. Two patients out of 2,504 went into comas (0.1% of patients, or 2 out of 419 survivors, 0.5%), and the study did not track how long the comas lasted.[83] Most people in comas start to recover in 2â€“3 weeks.[84] 2018 guidelines on disorders of consciousness say it is no longer appropriate to use the term "permanent vegetative state."[85] Mental abilities can continue to improve in the six months after discharge,[86] and in subsequent years.[84] For long-term problems, brains form new paths to replace damaged areas.[87][88]

Injuries

Injuries from CPR vary. 87% of patients are not injured by CPR.[89] Overall, injuries are caused in 13% (2009â€“12 data) of patients, including broken sternum or ribs (9%), lung injuries (3%), and internal bleeding (3%).[89] The internal injuries counted here can include heart contusion,[90] hemopericardium,[91] upper airway complications, damage to the abdominal viscera âˆ’ lacerations of the liver and spleen, fat emboli, pulmonary complications âˆ’ pneumothorax, hemothorax, lung contusions.[94][95] Most injuries did not affect care; only 1% of those given CPR received life-threatening injuries from it.[89][95] Broken ribs are present in 3%[89] of those who survive to hospital discharge, and 15% of those who die in the hospital, for an average rate of 9% (2009-12 data)[89] to 8% (1997â€“99).[96] In the 2009-12 study, 20% of survivors were older than 75.[89] A study in the 1990s found 55% of CPR patients who died before discharge had broken ribs, and a study in the 1960s found 97% did; training and experience levels have improved.[97] Lung injuries were caused in 3% of patients and other internal bleeding in 3% (2009â€“12). Bones heal in 1â€“2 months.[98][99] The costal cartilage also breaks in an unknown number of additional cases, which can sound like breaking bones.[100][101] The type and frequency of injury can be affected by factors such as sex and age. A 1999 Austrian study of CPR on cadavers, using a machine that alternately compressed the chest and then pulled it outward, found a higher rate of sternal fractures in female cadavers (9 of 17) than males (2 of 20), and found the risk of rib fractures rose with age, though they did not say how much.[102] Children and infants have a low risk of rib fractures during CPR, with an incidence of less than 2%, although, when they do occur, they are usually anterior and multiple.[97][103][104] Where CPR is performed in error by a bystander, on a person not in cardiac arrest, around 2% have injury as a result (although 12% experienced discomfort).[105] A 2004 overview said, "Chest injury is a price worth paying to achieve optimal efficacy of chest compressions. Cautious or faint-hearted chest compression may save bones in the individual case but not the patient's life."[97]

Other side effects

The most common side effect is vomiting, which necessitates clearing the mouth so patients do not breathe it in.[106] It happened in 16 of 35 CPR efforts in a 1989 study in King County, Washington.[107]

Survival differences, based on prior illness, age or location

The American Heart Association guidelines say that survival rates below 1% are "futility,"[108] but all groups have better survival than that. Even among very sick patients, at least 10% survive: A study of CPR in a sample of US hospitals from 2001 to 2010,[58] where overall survival was 19%, found 10% survival among cancer patients, 12% among dialysis patients, 14% over age 80, 15% among blacks, 17% for patients who lived in nursing homes, 19% for patients with heart failure, and 25% for patients with heart monitoring outside the ICU. Another study, of advanced cancer patients, found the same 10% survival mentioned above.[109] A study of Swedish patients in 2007â€“2015 with ECG monitors found 40% survived at least 30 days after CPR at ages 70â€“79, 29% at ages 80â€“89, and 27% above age 90.[110] An earlier study of Medicare patients in hospitals from 1992 to 2005, where overall survival was 18%, found 13% survival in the poorest neighborhoods, 12% survival over age 90, 15% survival among ages 85â€“89, and 17% survival among ages 80â€“84.[111] Swedish patients 90 years or older had 15% survival to hospital discharge, 80â€“89 had 20%, and 70â€“79 had 28%.[110] A study of King County WA patients who had CPR outside hospitals in 1999â€“2003, where 34% survived to hospital discharge overall, found that among patients with 4 or more major medical conditions, 18% survived; with 3 major conditions 24% survived, and 33% of those with 2 major medical conditions survived.[112] Nursing home residents' survival has been studied by several authors,[58][111][83][113][114][115][116] and is measured annually by the Cardiac Arrest Registry to Enhance Survival (CARES). CARES reports CPR results from a catchment area of 115 million people, including 23 state-wide registries, and individual communities in 18 other states as of 2019.[117] CARES data show that in health care facilities and nursing homes where AEDs are available and used, survival rates are double the average survival found in nursing homes overall.[118] As of 2023, nursing home OHCA survival was 3.9%.[74] Geographically, there is wide variation from state to state in survival after CPR in US hospitals, from 40% in Wyoming to 20% in New York (2003-2011 data), so there is room for good practices to spread, raising the averages.[119] For CPR outside hospitals , survival varies even more across the US, from 3% in Omaha to 45% in Seattle in 2001. This study only counted heart rhythms that can respond to defibrillator shocks (tachycardia). A major reason for the variation has been the delay in some areas between the call to emergency services and the departure of medics, and then arrival and treatment. Delays were caused by a lack of monitoring, and the mismatch between recruiting people as firefighters, though most emergency calls they are assigned to are medical, so staff resisted and delayed on the medical calls. Building codes have cut the number of fires, but staff still think of themselves as firefighters.

Dysthanasia

In some instances, CPR can be considered a form of dysthanasia.[122][123]

Prevalence

Chance of receiving CPR

Recent data indicate that bystanders in the US attempt CPR in approximately 40-42% of out-of-hospital cardiac arrest cases (as of 2023-2024).[124][125] Globally, bystander CPR rates vary widely, reported as low as 5% in some regions and up to 50% in others (as of 2023-2024).[126][127] However, the quality of bystander CPR is variable, with many compressions not meeting recommended depth and rate guidelines. One study found that members of the public having received CPR training in the past lack the skills and confidence needed to save lives. The report's authors suggested that better training is needed to improve the willingness to respond to cardiac arrest. Factors that influence bystander CPR in out-of-hospital cardiac arrest include:
  • Affordable training
  • Target CPR training to family members of potential cardiac arrest
  • CPR classes should be simplified and shortened
  • Offer reassurance and education about CPR
  • Provide clearer information about legal implications for specific regions
  • Focus on reducing the stigma and fears around providing bystander CPR
There is a relation between age and the chance of CPR being commenced. Younger people are far more likely to have CPR attempted on them before the arrival of emergency medical services. Bystanders more commonly administer CPR when in public than when at the person's home, although healthcare professionals are responsible for more than half of out-of-hospital resuscitation attempts. People with no connection to the person are more likely to perform CPR than family members. There is also a clear relation between the cause of arrest and the likelihood of a bystander initiating CPR. Laypersons are most likely to give CPR to younger people in cardiac arrest in a public place when it has a medical cause; those in arrest from trauma, exsanguination or intoxication are less likely to receive CPR. It is believed that there is a higher chance that CPR will be performed if the bystander is told to perform only the chest compression element of the resuscitation. Studies, including a 2017 analysis of nearly 20,000 U.S. cases and more recent global reviews (as of 2024), indicate women are approximately 6-10% less likely than men to receive bystander CPR in public cardiac arrest, potentially due to fears of sexual assault accusations.[127][128]

Chance of receiving CPR in time

As of 2024, U.S. bystander CPR rates have risen to about 41.7%, with higher survival (up to 22% if started within 1 minute). Global disparities persist, with lower rates in low-income regions.[129][125] CPR is likely to be effective only if commenced within 4-6 minutes after cardiac arrest, as brain cells begin to suffer irreversible damage from oxygen deprivation after this period without intervention.[130][131] A notable exception is cardiac arrest which occurs in conjunction with exposure to very cold temperatures. Hypothermia seems to protect by slowing down metabolic and physiologic processes, greatly decreasing the tissues' need for oxygen. There are cases where CPR, defibrillation, and advanced warming techniques have revived victims after substantial periods of hypothermia.

Society and culture

Portrayed effectiveness

CPR is often severely misrepresented in movies and television as being highly effective in resuscitating a person who is not breathing and has no circulation.[132] Recent analyses of medical dramas continue to depict inflated survival rates, with a 2020 study finding 61.9% survival to hospital discharge in televised CPR events, far exceeding real-world outcomes.[web:4] A 1996 study published in the New England Journal of Medicine showed that CPR success rates in television shows were 75% for immediate circulation, and 67% survival to discharge.[132] This gives the general public an unrealistic expectation of a successful outcome.[132] When educated on the actual survival rates, the proportion of patients over 60 years of age desiring CPR should they have a cardiac arrest drops from 41% to 22%.[133]

Training and stage CPR

It is dangerous to perform CPR on a person who is breathing normally. These chest compressions create significant local blunt trauma, risking bruising or fracture of the sternum or ribs.[134] If a patient is not breathing, these risks still exist but are dwarfed by the immediate threat to life. For this reason, training is always done with a mannequin, such as the well-known Resusci Anne model.[135] The portrayal of the CPR technique on television and film often is purposely incorrect. Actors simulating the performance of CPR may bend their elbows while appearing to compress, to prevent force from reaching the chest of the actor portraying the patient.[136]

Self-CPR hoax

A form of "self-CPR" termed "cough CPR" was the subject of a hoax chain e-mail entitled "How to Survive a Heart Attack When Alone," which wrongly cited "Via Health Rochester General Hospital" as the source of the technique. Rochester General Hospital has denied any connection with the technique.[137][138] "Cough CPR" in the sense of resuscitating oneself is impossible because a prominent symptom of cardiac arrest is unconsciousness, which makes coughing impossible.[139] The American Heart Association (AHA) and other resuscitation bodies[140] do not endorse "cough CPR", which it terms a misnomer as it is not a form of resuscitation. The AHA does recognize a limited legitimate use of the coughing technique: "This coughing technique to maintain blood flow during brief arrhythmias has been useful in the hospital, particularly during cardiac catheterization. In such cases, the patient's ECG is monitored continuously, and a physician is present."[141] When coughing is used on trained and monitored patients in hospitals, it is effective only for 90 seconds.[140]

Learning from film

In at least one case, it has been alleged that CPR learned from a film was used to save a person's life. In April 2011, it was claimed that nine-year-old Tristin Saghin saved his sister's life by administering CPR on her after she fell into a swimming pool, using only the knowledge of CPR that he had gleaned from a motion picture, Black Hawk Down.[142]

Hands-only CPR portrayal

Approximately 40% of people who experience an out-of-hospital cardiac arrest receive bystander CPR, according to 2023 data.[143] Most bystanders are worried that they might do something wrong.[144] On October 28, 2009, the American Heart Association and the Ad Council launched a hands-only CPR public service announcement and website as a means to address this issue.[145] In July 2011, new content was added to the website including a digital app that helps a user learn how to perform hands-only CPR.[146]

History

In the 19th century, Doctor H. R. Silvester described a method (the Silvester method) of artificial ventilation in which the patient is laid on their back, and their arms are raised above their head to aid inhalation and then pressed against their chest to aid exhalation.[147] The Holger Nielsen technique of artificial respiration, developed by Danish physician Holger Nielsen, revolutionized the field of emergency medical care. Introduced in 1932, this technique positions the patient face down (prone) with arms extended forward. The rescuer kneels at the patient's head, rocks backward while lifting both arms upward and forward to aid inhalation, then rocks forward to place arms beside the body and apply firm pressure on the lower back between the shoulder blades to aid exhalation. Repeat 12-16 times per minute.[148][149] It was not until the middle of the 20th century that the wider medical community started to recognize and promote artificial ventilation in the form of mouth-to-mouth resuscitation combined with chest compressions as a key part of resuscitation following cardiac arrest. The combination was first seen in a 1962 training video called "The Pulse of Life" created by James Jude, Guy Knickerbocker, and Peter Safar. Jude and Knickerbocker, along with William Kouwenhoven and Joseph S. Redding had recently discovered the method of external chest compressions, whereas Safar had worked with Redding and James Elam to prove the effectiveness of mouth-to-mouth resuscitation. Initial testing of components was conducted on animals, and the combined technique was soon applied successfully in humans. Their combined findings were presented at the annual Maryland Medical Society meeting on September 16, 1960, in Ocean City, and gained widespread acceptance over the following decade, helped by the video and speaking tour they undertook. Peter Safar wrote the book ABC of Resuscitation in 1957. In the U.S., it was first promoted as a technique for the public to learn in the 1970s.[150] Mouth-to-mouth resuscitation was combined with chest compressions based on the assumption that active ventilation is necessary to keep circulating blood oxygenated, and the combination was accepted without comparing its effectiveness with chest compressions alone. However, research in the 2000s demonstrated that assumption to be in error, resulting in the American Heart Association's acknowledgment of the effectiveness of chest compressions alone (see Compression only in this article).[151] CPR methods continued to advance, with developments in the 2010s including an emphasis on constant, rapid heart stimulation, and a de-emphasis on the respiration aspect. Studies have shown that people who had rapid, constant heart-only chest compression are 22% more likely to survive than those receiving conventional CPR that included breathing. Because people tend to be reluctant to do mouth-to-mouth resuscitation, chest-only CPR nearly doubles the chances of survival overall, by increasing the odds of receiving CPR in the first place. Subsequent updates, including the 2020 and 2025 American Heart Association guidelines, further refined protocols, incorporating evidence on dispatcher instructions, mechanical devices, and opioid-associated arrests.[152][153]

On animals

It is feasible to perform CPR on animals, including cats and dogs.[154] The principles and practices are similar to CPR for humans, with a recommended ratio of 30 chest compressions to 2 rescue breaths; however, resuscitation is usually done by covering the animal's nose (and sometimes mouth for smaller animals) rather than the mouth alone.[154] CPR should only be performed on unconscious animals with no breathing or pulse to avoid the risk of being bitten; a conscious animal would not require chest compressions.[154] As in humans, chest compressions during CPR on animals can cause rib fractures, particularly in breeds with more fragile bones.[155]

Neurological Outcome Scales in Cardiac Arrest Research

Research

The Pediatric Cerebral Performance Category (PCPC) scores are used in pediatric research as a tool to describe "good" and "poor" neurological outcomes following cardiac arrest. PCPC level 1 indicates normal function: conscious and alert. Level 2 indicates mild disability. Level 3 indicates moderate disability. Level 4 indicates severe disability. Level 5 indicates coma or persistent vegetative state. Level 6 indicates brain death or death from other causes.[156] In adult CPR research, the Cerebral Performance Category (CPC) scale is commonly used, ranging from 1 (good cerebral performance) to 5 (brain death or death), with levels 1-2 considered good outcomes and 3-5 poor.[81] Recent studies as of 2024 explore integrating CPC with tools like the modified Rankin Scale (mRS) for more comprehensive outcome assessment, improving validity in cardiac arrest survival research.[157]
