Sarah Idan

Sarah Idan (born February 4, 1990) is an Iraqi-American activist, musician, television host, and beauty queen who was crowned Miss Universe Iraq 2017 and represented her country at the Miss Universe pageant, becoming the first Iraqi woman to do so in 45 years.[1][2] Born in Baghdad as the second youngest of five children, Idan volunteered as an interpreter for the U.S. Army in Iraq at age 18 during the post-invasion period, later immigrating to the United States where she pursued modeling and music.[3][4] Her defining controversy arose from a selfie taken with Miss Israel Adar Gandelsman at the 2017 pageant, captioned "Peace and Love from Miss Iraq and Miss Israel," which drew death threats from extremists, forcing her family to flee Iraq and solidifying her role as a pro-peace advocate against antisemitism and Islamist ideology.[5][6] Idan founded the nonprofit Humanity Forward to foster dialogue between Muslims and Jews, received the UN Watch Ambassador for Peace Award for her bridge-building efforts, and in 2023 launched a congressional bid as a Democrat in California's 30th district to challenge radical influences in U.S. politics, though she did not advance to the primary ballot.[7][8][9]

Early life and background

Childhood and family in Iraq

Sarah Idan was born on February 4, 1990, in Baghdad, Iraq, into a Sunni Muslim family of five children, where she was the second youngest.[10] [3] Her parents hailed from the provinces of Babylon and Qadisiyah, and her father worked as a military engineer for Saddam Hussein's Ba'athist regime.[10] The family resided in Baghdad during Idan's early years under the authoritarian rule of Saddam Hussein, a period marked by state propaganda, economic sanctions following the 1990-1991 Gulf War, and restricted civil liberties enforced by the Ba'ath Party.[10] Daily life for the Idans involved navigating a society dominated by regime loyalty, with limited access to diverse information and opportunities due to international isolation and internal repression.[11] As Sunni Arabs living in a neighborhood increasingly influenced by Shia dynamics post-regime shifts, the family maintained tribal ties to a Sunni Arab heritage.[12]

Experiences with antisemitism and war

Idan grew up in Baghdad amid pervasive antisemitic rhetoric disseminated through Iraqi state media and education under Saddam Hussein's Ba'athist regime, which portrayed Jews and Israel as existential enemies. This propaganda fostered dehumanization of Jewish people, a pattern she later attributed to longstanding cultural and governmental indoctrination in Arab societies. As a child, she absorbed these narratives, which included school-mandated chants glorifying Hussein and suppressed criticism of the regime, leading her to internalize negative stereotypes about Israel and Jews.[10][13] The 2003 U.S.-led invasion of Iraq unleashed widespread instability in Baghdad, marked by sectarian violence, insurgent bombings, and collapsed infrastructure that severely limited access to essentials like food, water, and electricity. Idan's family, with her father employed as a military engineer for the Ba'ath Party, navigated this era as virtual "hostages" under constant threat of accusations of disloyalty, exacerbating the dangers of daily life. By her late teens, the cumulative chaosâ€”coupled with her growing realization that regime teachings were fabricationsâ€”fueled disillusionment with authoritarian extremism and its societal controls.[10] This environment prompted Idan's family to flee Baghdad around 2009, seeking escape from the persistent insecurity and violence that had defined post-invasion Iraq. The decision reflected broader patterns of displacement amid over 100,000 civilian deaths from bombings and conflict between 2003 and 2009, underscoring the war's direct toll on ordinary residents. These formative encounters with prejudice and upheaval instilled an early skepticism toward radical ideologies, prioritizing empirical observation over imposed narratives.[10][6]

Immigration and early career in the United States

Service as U.S. military interpreter

In February 2008, at age 18, Sarah Idan volunteered as an Arabic-English translator for U.S. forces in Iraq, departing her family's home in Baghdad to work in insurgency-plagued areas around the capital.[14][15] Having self-taught English during a period as a refugee in Syria amid the Iraq War's violence, Idan returned to offer her linguistic skills to Coalition troops, motivated initially by a desire to contribute amid local instability and to leverage her bilingual abilities in a conflict zone where accurate communication was critical for operations.[14][16] Idan's role involved interpreting during high-stakes interactions, including interrogations, patrols, and community engagements in Baghdad, where U.S. units faced frequent ambushes and improvised explosive device attacks from Al-Qaeda-linked insurgents.[17][18] Local interpreters like Idan were prime targets for reprisals, as collaboration with American forces marked them and their families for death threats or assassinations by extremists, underscoring the operational risks she assumed in transitioning from Iraqi civilian to embedded ally.[19] Her service extended into 2009 with U.S.-led Coalition units, facilitating real-time translation that aided tactical decisions and intelligence gathering against terrorist networks.[18] Through daily immersion, Idan observed U.S. troops' emphasis on rules of engagement, civilian protection, and disciplined counterinsurgency tactics, which she later contrasted with the corruption and inefficacy of post-Saddam Iraqi authorities.[20][4] She has recounted building rapport with soldiers by providing culturally nuanced insights, such as clarifying local dialects and customs to prevent misunderstandings during raids or negotiations, fostering mutual trust in environments where miscommunication could prove fatal.[21] This exposure highlighted American military professionalism in degrading insurgent capabilitiesâ€”evident in surged operations that reduced violence in Baghdadâ€”while exposing governance voids like militia infiltration and sectarian favoritism that undermined Iraqi security forces.[4] Idan departed Iraq for the United States around 2010, after approximately two years of service, having internalized values of accountability and anti-extremism that informed her later worldview.[18]

Path to citizenship and initial pursuits

Following her service as an interpreter for U.S. forces in Iraq, Sarah Idan relocated to the United States in 2009, initially moving to Southern California as part of the Special Immigrant Visa program available to Iraqi allies who faced threats due to their collaboration with American military operations.[22][23] This pathway, designed for individuals like Idan who provided linguistic support in high-risk environments, granted her lawful permanent residency after rigorous security vetting and documentation requirements, amid a backlog that often delayed approvals for thousands of similar applicants. She continued traveling between Iraq and the U.S. during this period, navigating ongoing personal security concerns that underscored the adaptive hurdles of resettlement.[23] Idan achieved U.S. citizenship in 2015, benefiting from expedited naturalization eligibility tied to her prior contributions as a military interpreter, which qualified under provisions for certain non-citizen service members and affiliates.[23] Upon settling in California, she pursued formal education to build her professional foundation, enrolling at the Musicians Institute in Hollywood where she graduated with a degree in performing arts. This training leveraged her self-taught English proficiencyâ€”acquired amid wartime displacementâ€”and multilingual abilities in Arabic, facilitating her integration into American society through skill-based opportunities rather than immediate high-profile roles.[22] In her early years stateside, Idan focused on personal stabilization and cultural assimilation, drawing on resilience forged from fleeing persecution to secure entry-level positions that capitalized on her interpretive expertise, though specifics of these roles remain tied to transitional linguistics work. Preceding her later public endeavors, she explored foundational creative pursuits, including initial modeling appearances and local engagements that highlighted her determination to thrive amid bureaucratic immigration delays and the isolation of immigrant adaptation in a new environment.[10]

Entertainment career

Music releases and performances

Idan pursued a career as a singer-songwriter after completing vocal performance training, composing and performing original material in English and Arabic that fuses cultural influences with themes of personal liberation and resilience against oppression.[15] Her lyrical content draws from first-hand experiences of constraint, emphasizing individual agency over collective ideologies. Performances occurred in Los Angeles venues, where she showcased stripped-down acoustic sets blending Eastern melodic structures with Western pop sensibilities.[24] An early documented performance featured Idan's cover of Roxette's "Listen to Your Heart" during a live workshop at the Musician Institute on May 31, 2011, highlighting her vocal range and interpretive style.[24] She also contributed an Arabic track titled "Just For You" (Ø£ØºÙ†ÙŠØ© ÙÙŠÙ„Ù… Ø§Ù„Ù‚Ø±Ø¯ Ø¨ÙŠØªÙƒÙ„Ù…) for the film Monkey Talks, demonstrating her bilingual capabilities in media soundtracks. Beauty pageants and Miss Universe participation

Sarah Idan first entered the pageant circuit by winning the title of Miss Iraq USA in 2016, a competition organized for the Iraqi diaspora community in the United States.[23] This victory positioned her within Iraqi-American pageant traditions, emphasizing cultural representation abroad.[25] In 2017, Idan advanced to the national level, being crowned Miss Universe Iraq on November 1 during a special ceremony at the Miss Iraq Organization headquarters in Qadisiya.[26] The selection process involved pageant officials evaluating candidates based on standard criteria such as poise, intelligence, and advocacy platforms, with Idan's background as an Iraqi immigrant highlighting her unique perspective on national representation.[27] At 27 years old, she became the designated representative for Iraq, marking a significant return for the country to international pageantry after an absence.[2] Idan competed at the Miss Universe 2017 pageant, held on November 26 at The Axis at Planet Hollywood Resort & Casino in Las Vegas, Nevada.[28] Her participation made her the first Iraqi contestant in 45 years, since the nation's last entry in 1972.[2] Although she did not advance to the top placements, her presence underscored efforts to revive Iraq's involvement in global beauty competitions, focusing on showcasing modern Iraqi womanhood amid regional challenges.[29] Idan's platform emphasized empowering women in the Middle East, drawing from her experiences to advocate for greater opportunities and visibility for Iraqi females.[30]

Television hosting and media roles

In early 2018, Idan launched and hosted Hollywood with Sarah Idan, a weekend entertainment series on WION, an Indian international news channel based in New Delhi.[31] The program, produced from Los Angeles, featured updates on Hollywood films, celebrity interviews, and industry trends, leveraging Idan's background as a model and pageant titleholder to appeal to global audiences interested in Western entertainment.[32] Episodes included segments on major releases and events, such as coverage of film premieres and actor profiles, airing regularly to provide concise, visually engaging content.[33] Beyond her hosting debut, Idan made guest appearances on international broadcasts, showcasing her on-camera presence in interview formats. On BBC's Victoria Derbyshire in 2018, she discussed her experiences as Miss Iraq amid regional tensions, highlighting her articulate delivery in English.[34] Similarly, she featured multiple times on Newsmax's Rob Schmitt Tonight starting in 2020, contributing to segments on cultural and current events with a focus on her bilingual perspective.[34] These roles emphasized her transition from scripted entertainment hosting to dynamic guest commentary, where her poise and cross-cultural insights enhanced discussions without relying on prepared scripts.[34] Idan's media engagements post-2018 increasingly involved opinion-oriented spots on U.S. networks, where her fluency in Arabic enabled nuanced takes on Middle Eastern topics within broader talk-show structures. This evolution underscored her adaptability in broadcast settings, from light entertainment to substantive interviews, while maintaining a professional demeanor suited to live television.[35]

Political and advocacy activities

Foreign policy views and criticism of extremism

Sarah Idan has advocated for the elimination of terrorist groups like Hamas, drawing parallels to the oppression she witnessed in Iraq under Saddam Hussein, where ordinary people desired freedom from authoritarian rule and extremism. In an October 2023 op-ed, she described Hamas as an "Islamic emirate" that executes LGBTQ individuals, beats journalists, and indoctrinates children into violence, arguing that Gazans, like Iraqis, yearn for liberation from such control following the group's October 7 attack that killed over 1,400 Israelis.[30] She has emphasized that supporting Palestinian freedom requires opposing Hamas's use of human shields and reprisals, citing Iran's role as the leading state sponsor of terrorism in arming such groups.[30][36] Idan attributes the Arab-Israeli conflict primarily to antisemitism embedded in educational and belief systems across Muslim countries, rather than territorial disputes or policy differences. In a 2019 statement, she asserted that the hostility stems from "anti-Semitic belief systems taught in Muslim countries," which fuel rejection of Israel's existence independent of its actions.[6] During a United Nations Human Rights Council speech that year, she criticized the failure to condemn Hamas's launches of nearly 700 rockets at Israeli civilians or its exploitation of Palestinians as shields, urging Arab states to prioritize shared security interests with Israel over alliances with militias.[37] Idan has portrayed Israel as a critical stabilizer in the region, "the only one standing against the terrorists," defending Western freedoms against Iran-backed extremism that she links to broader threats observed in her Iraqi upbringing.[13] Her critiques extend to biased coverage in Arab and international media, which she argues distorts causal realities by omitting terrorist initiations of violence and emphasizing Israeli responses as unprovoked aggression. In writings for Algemeiner, Idan contrasted progressive Arab states like the UAEâ€”benefiting from peace accords with Israelâ€”with Iran's promotion of extremism, warning that laws criminalizing normalization, such as Iraq's proposed death penalty for peace efforts, entrench antisemitic isolation.[38][39] She rejects narratives framing the conflict as mere oppression, instead grounding her analysis in empirical patterns of indoctrination and aggression, as evidenced by her personal encounters with threats for advocating dialogue.[6][37]

2024 U.S. congressional candidacy

In June 2023, Sarah Idan announced her candidacy for the Democratic primary in California's 30th congressional district, an open seat vacated by Adam Schiff's Senate run, positioning herself as a secular Muslim immigrant and U.S. military veteran seeking to inject a "voice of reason" into the party.[40][3] Her platform emphasized domestic priorities such as combating poverty, crime, and homelessness in the Los Angeles-area district, alongside a realistic approach to immigration informed by her own journey from Iraq to U.S. citizenship.[41] Idan also advocated scrutinizing foreign aid allocation and strengthening bilateral ties with Israel, drawing on her personal experiences to critique extremism within both U.S. politics and Middle Eastern contexts.[4] Idan's campaign highlighted her service as a U.S. Army interpreter in Iraq and her transition to American life, framing these as assets for addressing national security and integration challenges over ideological purity.[4] She aimed to challenge progressive influences in the Democratic Party, including figures associated with the informal "Squad" grouping, by promoting pro-American policies and rejecting what she described as divisive wokeness.[4] However, the district's primary, held on March 5, 2024, under California's top-two system, featured a crowded field of Democrats, with candidates like state Assemblymember Laura Friedman advancing. Idan did not appear on the primary ballot, having withdrawn prior to the December 2023 filing deadline, amid a competitive race requiring substantial signatures and fundraisingâ€”her committee reported limited financial activity per Federal Election Commission records.[9] The early exit underscored challenges for outsider candidates in deep-blue districts dominated by established party networks, though Idan maintained visibility through ongoing advocacy, including a December 2023 tour of Gaza-border communities in Israel.[14] By 2025, she reflected on the bid as an effort to elevate moderate, experience-based perspectives, continuing to influence discourse on U.S. foreign policy and immigrant contributions without electoral success.[42]

Founding of peace initiatives

Sarah Idan founded the non-profit organization Humanity Forward in 2019 as a platform dedicated to fostering dialogue and understanding between Arabs, Muslims, Jews, and Israelis.[2] The initiative aims to counter entrenched narratives of hate through structured online and in-person engagements, including YouTube channels focused on discussing politics, religion, and cultural intersections to promote tolerance and unity.[2][43] Headquartered in the United States, where Idan serves as founding CEO, the organization emphasizes non-partisan efforts to build bridges across divided communities by highlighting shared human values and challenging misinformation.[15] Humanity Forward's programs include virtual forums and educational content designed to connect participants from conflicting backgrounds, encouraging personal stories and rational discourse over ideological confrontation.[44] Idan has described the NGO's mission as translating her personal commitment to peace into actionable structures that prioritize empirical dialogue and mutual respect, distinct from governmental or electoral advocacy.[45] Early activities involved partnerships with interfaith groups for university speaking engagements and media collaborations to amplify voices advocating coexistence.[45] The organization's impact has been recognized through awards, such as the Ambassador for Peace Award presented by UN Watch in 2019, honoring Idan's role in promoting tolerance and unity amid regional tensions.[7][21] These accolades underscore verifiable outcomes in facilitating cross-cultural exchanges, with documented events drawing participants from diverse demographics to address root causes of division through evidence-based conversations rather than abstract appeals.[7]

Pro-Israel stance and engagements

Friendship with Miss Israel and initial controversies

In November 2017, during the Miss Universe 2017 pageant in Las Vegas, Sarah Idan, representing Iraq, posed for a selfie with Adar Gandelsman, Israel's contestant, and posted it on Instagram with the caption "Peace and Love from Miss Iraq and Miss Israel."[46][28] The image, taken amid the pageant's preliminary events, quickly ignited outrage across Iraq and broader Arab social media, where commenters accused Idan of betraying her nation by fraternizing with a representative of Israel, a country with which Iraq maintains no diplomatic relations.[47][48] The backlash escalated rapidly, with Idan receiving death threats via social media and phone calls from Iraqi nationalists who branded her a "traitor" and "Zionist agent."[28][49] Reports indicated threats originated from various quarters, including Hamas-affiliated groups, prompting Idan to alert U.S. authorities for protection while in Las Vegas.[50] The Miss Iraq organization issued an ultimatum six days before the pageant's finale on November 26, demanding she remove the photo or face disqualification, viewing it as a violation of national sentiment against normalization with Israel.[28] Idan refused to delete the post, defending the gesture as an apolitical act of personal goodwill and a call for human connection amid enmity between states, stating she had not anticipated the severity of the reaction but stood by the message of peace.[28][51] In response to mounting pressure, she clarified in a follow-up Instagram statement that the photo did not endorse Israeli government policies, emphasizing instead individual friendship over geopolitical divides.[52] This stance intensified the controversy, as Iraqi officials and online detractors dismissed her explanation, framing the interaction as normalization propaganda unacceptable under Iraq's longstanding anti-Israel position.[53]

Visits to Israel and personal encounters

In June 2018, Idan traveled to Israel on a trip organized by the American Jewish Committee, where she reunited with Adar Gandelsman in Jerusalem.[54][52] The itinerary included visits to the Babylonian Jewry Heritage Center in Or Yehuda, which documents the history and expulsion of Iraq's Jewish community, and the Western Wall in Jerusalem, sites that exposed her to narratives contrasting state-sponsored Iraqi accounts of Jewish exodus.[55] She also explored the Mahane Yehuda Market, dining at an Iraqi Jewish restaurant, and engaged with Iraqi Jewish descendants, fostering personal connections to shared heritage.[56][57] Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks, Idan conducted a solidarity tour in late December 2023 of Gaza border communities, including the kibbutz of Kfar Aza, where she observed extensive destruction from the assault, including burned homes and sites of civilian massacres.[14][58] Accompanied by local residents and security personnel, she heard firsthand accounts from survivors detailing the scale of violence, which she later described as surpassing the atrocities she witnessed under ISIS in Iraq.[58] The tour extended to other southern towns near the border, providing direct exposure to the physical aftermath and security challenges in the region.[14]

Public statements on Arab-Israeli conflict

In a 2019 speech at the United Nations Human Rights Council, Sarah Idan described the Arab-Israeli conflict as "deeply rooted" in antisemitism embedded in educational and belief systems across Muslim countries, rather than solely territorial disputes, stating that "hatred and intolerance are reinforced by biased media coverage against Israel."[6][37] She urged Arab nations to recognize shared interests with Israel against terrorist groups, noting, "I'd like to remind Arab countries that today you share more common interests with Israel than the terrorist militias."[6] Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks on Israel, Idan rejected narratives framing the violence as efforts to "free Palestine," asserting instead that the events constituted terrorism disconnected from legitimate territorial claims.[14][59] In October 2024, she reiterated Israel's role in confronting mutual threats, declaring, "Israel is the only one standing against the terrorists," and emphasized her intent to align with forces combating radicalism, drawing implicit parallels to jihadist violence she witnessed in Iraq under regimes like Saddam Hussein's and ISIS's, where pretexts masked broader ideological aggression.[13] Idan has challenged prominent critics of Israel by highlighting antisemitism's primacy over land disputes, as in her 2022 public call for a debate with Mandla Mandela, Nelson Mandela's grandson, after his advocacy for boycotting events in Israel; Mandela declined, prompting Idan to observe that "it's easier to start a war than to have the dialogue which can lead to peace."[8][60] She has consistently attributed disproportionate global support for Palestinian causes to underlying antisemitism, noting that jihadist mobilization occurs selectively against Israelâ€”unlike for Iraq or Syriaâ€”because "Free Palestine" serves as a euphemism for eliminating Jewish presence, informed by her upbringing in an environment where Jews were demonized as infidels despite their absence.[13]

Backlash and resilience

Threats and loss of Iraqi citizenship

Following her December 2017 Instagram selfie with Miss Israel Adar Gandelsman, Sarah Idan received numerous death threats from Iraqi nationals, prompting her family to flee the country for safety.[28][61] The threats escalated due to her public friendship with an Israeli, violating longstanding Arab League boycotts, and included warnings that intensified online harassment and personal endangerment, leading to heightened security measures for Idan and her relatives.[62] These attacks persisted beyond 2017, with Idan reporting a surge in threats as recently as January 2024, often tied to her ongoing pro-Israel advocacy.[63] In June 2019, Idan addressed the United Nations Human Rights Council in Geneva, criticizing Iraq's suppression of free speech and defending Israel's right to exist amid the Arab-Israeli conflict, remarks delivered on behalf of UN Watch.[64] This speech prompted the Iraqi Parliament's Security and Defense Committee to initiate proceedings to revoke her citizenship, citing her statements as treasonous normalization with Israel.[65] By mid-2019, Iraqi officials confirmed the revocation, stripping Idan of her legal status as a citizen and barring her return, a decision formalized in response to her UN testimony rather than solely the 2017 selfie.[66] Despite exile and the loss of citizenship, Idan maintained her advocacy efforts, relocating permanently to the United States where she pursued political activities, interpreting the repercussions as validation of her positions on regional extremism and peace. This resilience manifested in her continued public engagements without retreat from prior stances, even as threats required ongoing personal security protocols.[64]

Responses from Arab media and communities

Arab media outlets, such as Middle East Monitor, have portrayed Sarah Idan as promoting a misleading narrative of peace. In a June 26, 2018, opinion piece, the outlet labeled her a "false prophet of peace and coexistence," contending that her advocacy implicitly endorses Israel's portrayal of the Arab-Israeli conflict while ignoring Palestinian perspectives.[67] Public discourse within Arab and Iraqi communities has similarly reflected strong opposition, particularly on social media platforms where users have denounced her as a "traitor" and "Zionist agent" in response to her friendships and statements supporting Israel.[6][68] Following her June 2019 address at the United Nations Human Rights Council, where she criticized antisemitism and media imbalances, Iraqi parliamentary subgroups initiated discussions to revoke her citizenship, viewing her remarks as disloyalty to national interests.[69][70] These responses highlight divisions, with condemnation prevalent in Iraq's domestic media and government circles in Baghdad, contrasted by pockets of alignment among some Iraqi expatriates who share her emphasis on regional reconciliation over entrenched hostilities. Idan has rebutted such critiques by attributing them to systemic biases in Arab media, which she argues prioritize anti-Israel propaganda and evade causal factors like extremism and antisemitic indoctrination in curricula.[37][6]

Achievements in advocacy despite opposition

Despite facing death threats that forced her family to flee their home and subsequent threats of citizenship revocation by the Iraqi government, Sarah Idan established Forward Humanity, serving as its CEO to facilitate dialogue and connections between Muslims and Jews, as well as Arabs and Israelis, with the goal of advancing peace initiatives.[7] On June 13, 2019, Idan received the Ambassador for Peace Award from UN Watch in Geneva, recognizing her "courageous and extraordinary actions to promote tolerance, build bridges for peace, and spread a message of hope and unity," including her rejection of anti-Israel boycotts and public advocacy for Israel's equal treatment.[7] During sessions on June 25 and 26, 2019, at the United Nations Human Rights Council in Geneva, Idan delivered speeches criticizing the Iraqi regime's human rights abuses, its failure to condemn death threats against her following her 2017 selfie with Miss Israel, and the pervasive antisemitism embedded in educational systems across Muslim countries, which she identified as a primary driver of the Arab-Israeli conflict; she urged direct peace negotiations between Israel and Palestinian Arabs as essential to resolving regional suffering.[37][6][71] These addresses, invited by UN Watch, drew international attention to her advocacy despite immediate backlash from Iraqi authorities, who initiated proceedings to strip her citizenship for supporting Israel, yet amplified calls for Iraq-Israel normalization and highlighted media biases favoring narratives that demonize Israel.[71][72] In August 2018, amid ongoing controversies, Idan traveled to Israel for the first time, reuniting with Miss Israel Adar Gandelsman, meeting Iraqi Jewish expatriates, and publicly advocating for diplomatic relations between Iraq and Israel alongside a resolution to the Israeli-Palestinian dispute through mutual recognition.[7][6] Idan has sustained her efforts through media engagements, including multiple appearances on Egyptian television to promote Arab-Jewish understanding and a January 2021 interview on Al-Arabiya Network where, as a self-described peace activist, she called for Iraq's independence from Iranian-backed militias and coexistence among Muslims, Jews, Arabs, and Israelis.[68][73]
