Sara Britcliffe


Sara Britcliffe (born 1995) is a British former politician who served as the Conservative Member of Parliament (MP) for Hyndburn from 2019 to 2024.[1][2]
Elected at the age of 24 in the 2019 general election, she became the youngest Conservative MP in the House of Commons and the first woman to represent the Lancashire constituency of Hyndburn.[3][4]
Prior to her parliamentary career, Britcliffe worked as a local councillor for Hyndburn Borough Council and operated a sandwich shop in the area.[3]
During her tenure, she served on select committees including the Levelling Up, Housing and Communities Committee and the Women and Equalities Committee, and publicly addressed the online abuse and threats she received as a young female MP.[5][6][7][8]
She lost her seat to Labour's Sarah Smith in the 2024 general election.[1][9]

Early life

Family background and childhood

Sara Britcliffe was born on 21 February 1995 in Hyndburn, Lancashire, to Peter Britcliffe, a long-serving Conservative councillor, and Gabrielle Kroger.[10] Her father represented the Oswaldtwistle division on Lancashire County Council for over 30 years and later served as Mayor of Hyndburn.[11] [12] She has two older brothers, and the family resided in the Hyndburn area, where they continue to live.[10] [13] Britcliffe grew up in this politically active household, with her father's extensive local government service providing early exposure to community involvement and Conservative politics in Accrington and surrounding districts.[11] [3]

Mother's death and personal challenges

Sara Britcliffe's mother, Gabrielle Kroger, succumbed to alcoholism in 2004 at the age of nine for Britcliffe.[14][15] Britcliffe has described witnessing the event firsthand, emphasizing her mother's underlying need for intervention amid chronic binge drinking episodes that disrupted family life.[16][17] The alcoholism manifested in acute incidents, including Kroger's arrest for drunk and disorderly conduct on an airplane, which stranded the young Britcliffe without immediate parental support.[18] These experiences contributed to broader familial instability, with Britcliffe later highlighting in parliamentary contributions the compounded effects of her mother's alcohol dependency and associated mental health struggles.[19] In a 2020 remote maiden speech during the Domestic Abuse Bill debate, she detailed how such parental addiction eroded family cohesion and left lasting emotional imprints.[19] Britcliffe has since channeled these challenges into advocacy, urging enhanced government resources for children of alcohol-dependent parents, including specialized counseling and family intervention programs absent during her upbringing.[20][21] She maintains that earlier systemic support could have altered outcomes, reflecting on the absence of such aid as a critical gap in addressing addiction's intergenerational toll.[14]

Education and pre-parliamentary career

Formal education

Britcliffe attended St Christopher's Church of England High School in Accrington, where she later referenced her time as a pupil in parliamentary contributions.[22] [23] Following secondary school, she studied modern languages at the University of Manchester, specializing in German and Spanish, completing her degree in 2018 or 2019.[3] [24] No further higher education qualifications are recorded.

Local government involvement

Britcliffe was elected as a Conservative Party councillor to Hyndburn Borough Council on 3 May 2018, representing the St Andrew's ward in Oswaldtwistle and succeeding her father, Peter Britcliffe, who had held the seat previously.[10][25] Her official term of office extended until 26 March 2021, though records indicate active service primarily from May 2018 to May 2020, after which she resigned following her selection and subsequent election as Member of Parliament for Hyndburn in December 2019.[26][25] During her tenure, she focused on constituency-level concerns in Oswaldtwistle, a town within the Hyndburn district, leveraging her local family ties and business experience in the area.[3] Prior to her council role, her father had served as a long-standing Conservative councillor in the same ward, providing a foundation for her entry into local governance.[10]

Other professional experience

Prior to her parliamentary career, Britcliffe managed a sandwich shop in Oswaldtwistle, Lancashire, following her university graduation.[27][3] This role, described in some accounts as running a bakery, provided her with early business management experience in the local community.[28][29] No other significant professional positions outside local government and politics are recorded prior to her 2019 election.[30]

Parliamentary career

2019 election victory

Sara Britcliffe was elected as the Conservative Member of Parliament for Hyndburn in the 2019 United Kingdom general election on 12 December 2019, defeating the incumbent Labour MP Graham Jones.[31] The constituency, located in Lancashire, had been held by Labour since 1992.[32] Britcliffe received 20,565 votes, accounting for 48.5% of the valid votes cast, an increase of 8.0 percentage points from the Conservative performance in 2017.[33] Labour's vote share fell to 17,614 (41.5%), a decrease of 7.9 points, resulting in a Conservative majority of 2,951 votes.[33] Voter turnout was 59.9% among an electorate of 70,842.[34] The victory represented a notional swing of approximately 8.0% from Labour to Conservative, contributing to the national Conservative gain of seats in traditional Labour heartlands.[33] Britcliffe's win marked the first Conservative representation for Hyndburn in 27 years, aligning with broader trends in the election where the Conservatives secured an overall majority of 80 seats.[32][35]

Key roles and appointments

Britcliffe served as Parliamentary Private Secretary (PPS) to the Secretary of State for Education from 29 January 2021 until 6 July 2022.[28][36] In this unpaid role, she supported ministerial duties including policy development and constituency outreach on education matters.[37] She resigned amid the July 2022 government crisis triggered by scandals surrounding Prime Minister Boris Johnson, citing the need to restore public trust in governance.[38] On 30 September 2022, Britcliffe was appointed Vice-Chairman of the Conservative Party for Youth, tasked with engaging younger voters and promoting party policies to that demographic.[39] This voluntary position involved organizing outreach events and advocating for youth-focused initiatives within the party structure. From 24 November 2023 to 5 July 2024, she held the paid role of Deputy Chairman of the Conservative Party, with a focus on candidate selection and women's engagement, receiving an annual salary of Â£10,000 for part-time duties.[40] Britcliffe was also appointed to the Transport Select Committee on 17 July 2023, serving until the dissolution of Parliament on 30 May 2024; in this capacity, she scrutinized Department for Transport policies, including infrastructure projects and connectivity issues relevant to her constituency.[41][37]

Major parliamentary activities

Britcliffe delivered her maiden speech on 28 April 2020 during the second reading of the Domestic Abuse Bill, marking the first such address delivered remotely from outside the House of Commons due to COVID-19 restrictions; she spoke from her constituency in Lancashire and highlighted her commitment to supporting victims of domestic abuse.[4][42] She served on the Women and Equalities Committee from 2 March 2020 to 1 March 2021, contributing to scrutiny of related policy areas.[1] From 17 July 2023 until her electoral defeat on 30 May 2024, she was a member of the Transport Committee, focusing on transport infrastructure and policy oversight.[43] Britcliffe participated in several bill committees, including the Higher Education (Freedom of Speech) Bill Committee from 7 September to 22 September 2021, the Energy Bill [HL] Committee from 17 May to 29 June 2023, and the Renters (Reform) Bill Committee from 8 November to 28 November 2023.[1][43] She submitted multiple written questions to government departments, such as inquiries to the Secretary of State for Justice on communications operators and convictions under specific legislation.[44] Britcliffe also posed oral questions, including on 19 January 2021 regarding post-Brexit travel for performers from her constituency and on 24 October 2023 in departmental question sessions.[45][46] In debates, she intervened on the British Sign Language Bill on 28 January 2022, urging alignment of government guidance with deaf community needs, and supported the Commonwealth Parliamentary Association and International Committee of the Red Cross (Status) Bill during its third reading on 26 April 2024.[47][48] Her voting record included participation in 895 divisions, with only two instances of voting against the Conservative Party majority.[37]

Resignation from PPS and party leadership endorsements

On 6 July 2022, Britcliffe resigned as Parliamentary Private Secretary (PPS) to the Department for Education, joining over 50 Conservative MPs and ministers in a coordinated wave of departures triggered by Prime Minister Boris Johnson's perceived mishandling of sexual misconduct allegations against his deputy chief whip, Chris Pincher.[38][49] In her public statement on X, she described the decision as made "with a heavy heart" in a department she valued for its progress on education policy and the government's levelling up initiatives, but criticized the administration for allowing "self-inflicted" scandals to overshadow substantive delivery, stating that "this Government has achieved so much starting to deliver on levelling up but we risk becoming so subsumed in the scandal that we are unable to deliver on our promises to the British people".[50][51] Her resignation letter emphasized the need for focus amid ongoing controversies, including earlier "Partygate" violations, which she viewed as eroding public trust without necessitating a change in leadership at the time.[52] The collective resignations, including those from senior figures like Chancellor Rishi Sunak and Health Secretary Sajid Javid, precipitated Johnson's resignation announcement on 7 July 2022 and the launch of a Conservative Party leadership contest to select his successor. Britcliffe initially backed Nadhim Zahawi, a fellow education minister who entered the race but withdrew on 12 July after failing to secure sufficient MP nominations.[53] She subsequently endorsed Liz Truss, the frontrunner among "Red Wall" MPs representing northern seats, aligning with Truss's campaign emphasis on low taxes and economic growth to sustain voter gains from 2019.[53] Truss won the contest on 5 September 2022, defeating Sunak in the final MP and membership ballot, though her 49-day premiership ended amid market turmoil from unfunded tax cuts. Britcliffe's support reflected a preference for continuity in Johnson's levelling up priorities over Sunak's fiscal conservatism, consistent with her prior defense of government achievements despite scandals.[54]

2024 election defeat

In the 2024 United Kingdom general election held on 4 July 2024, Sara Britcliffe sought re-election as the Conservative candidate for Hyndburn, a constituency she had represented since 2019.[55] She secured 10,499 votes, representing 28.9% of the valid votes cast, a decline of 19.6 percentage points from her 48.5% share in 2019.[56] This result marked her defeat, with Labour's Sarah Smith emerging victorious on 12,186 votes (33.5%, down 8.0 points from 2019), achieving a majority of 1,687 votes over Britcliffe.[56] [57] The vote distribution highlighted a fragmented opposition to Labour, with Reform UK polling strongly at 7,541 votes (20.7%, up 15.6 points), the Liberal Democrats at 3,248 votes (8.9%), and the Green Party at 1,557 votes (4.3%).[56] Independent candidates and the Workers Party divided the remaining preferences, totaling under 4% combined. Turnout in Hyndburn was approximately 59.2%, with 36,374 valid votes recorded.[57] Britcliffe's loss mirrored the national Conservative collapse, where the party lost 251 seats overall amid a 23.7-point swing to Labour, though Reform UK's surgeâ€”reflecting discontent with mainstream partiesâ€”eroded Conservative support in traditional heartlands like Hyndburn. Post-election, Britcliffe's tenure ended with the dissolution of Parliament on 30 May 2024, prior to the vote, confirming her departure from the Commons after one term.[55] No public statements from Britcliffe attributing specific local factors to the defeat were immediately reported, though the constituency's industrial and working-class demographics aligned with broader trends of voter shifts toward Reform UK on issues like immigration and economic stagnation.[56]

Political positions

Support for law enforcement and security measures

Britcliffe has advocated for bolstering police resources and operational effectiveness. During a February 2023 parliamentary debate on police resources, she welcomed government efforts to increase funding by over Â£500 million and introduce flexibility for police and crime commissioners in addressing antisocial behaviour, while highlighting youth investment funds as complementary measures.[58] In the same session's oral questions, she questioned the prior 8,500-officer decline in police community support officers under the previous Labour administration, underscoring the need for sustained neighbourhood policing.[59] She actively supported legislative reforms to enhance law enforcement powers. Britcliffe contributed to Commons debates on the Police, Crime, Sentencing and Courts Bill in March 2021, backing provisions to enshrine annual reporting on the police covenant for improved officer support and welfare.[60] She voted against amendments at the bill's report stage, such as New Clause 98, and aligned with the Conservative majority in approving its third reading on July 5, 2021, which expanded police authority on public order, sentencing for serious crimes, and offender management.[61][62] On local security initiatives, Britcliffe engaged directly with enforcement operations targeting crime hotspots. In June 2020, she commended Lancashire Police for their effective implementation of COVID-19 lockdown measures in Hyndburn, emphasizing community safety amid restrictions.[63] By August 2023, she participated in Operation Centurion, a national antisocial behaviour crackdown backed by Â£2 million in government funding, joining Lancashire's Police and Crime Commissioner to observe strengthened patrols and enforcement in Hyndburn hotspots, describing it as a key rollout for local deterrence.[64][65] These efforts aligned with the Lancashire Fighting Crime Plan, which prioritizes enforcement alongside diversionary activities to reduce recidivism.[66][67]

Advocacy on local issues

Britcliffe has actively raised concerns about recurrent flooding in her constituency, particularly highlighting the impacts of the Boxing Day 2020 floods on Hyndburn communities during a June 2022 Westminster Hall debate on flooding in Irwell Vale and surrounding areas.[68] She emphasized how such events united local residents and underscored the need for enhanced flood defenses, drawing on direct constituent experiences in East Lancashire.[68] On transport infrastructure, she served on the House of Commons Transport Committee from July 2023 until her defeat in the 2024 election, focusing on regional connectivity issues.[37] Britcliffe questioned the government in January 2020 about progress on reopening the Skipton-Colne railway line, a project aimed at improving links between Colne in Pendle and Skipton in North Yorkshire, which would boost economic access for Hyndburn residents reliant on road travel.[37] She also inquired about trials of noise cameras to address local road safety and enforcement concerns in October 2022.[37] In economic matters, Britcliffe advocated for job creation through decarbonisation initiatives, asking in September 2020 about hydrogen technology opportunities in the North West to support manufacturing and energy sectors in Hyndburn's industrial base.[37] She raised constituent queries on energy bill support in November 2022 amid the cost-of-living crisis, pressing for targeted relief in areas with high household energy dependency.[37] Regarding housing, she contributed to debates on the Renters (Reform) Bill in November 2023 and the Social Housing (Regulation) Bill in 2022, emphasizing reforms to improve standards and tenant protections in local rental markets affected by post-industrial decline.[37] Britcliffe also addressed community services, questioning the launch of the youth investment fund in September 2020 to enhance opportunities for young people in Hyndburn and supporting measures for unpaid carers in September 2021, reflecting grassroots concerns over social welfare in her constituency.[37]

Economic and social policy stances

Britcliffe has emphasized economic policies focused on regional regeneration and business support, particularly in northern England. As a representative of the former "Red Wall" constituency of Hyndburn, she advocated for government investment to stimulate local growth, pledging in her April 2020 maiden speech to press ministers for resources to enhance the northern economy.[69] She successfully secured Â£26 million in levelling up funding in January 2023 for Hyndburn, earmarked for redeveloping Accrington town centre into a commercial and cultural hub, which she described as a major win for local regeneration.[70] Additionally, she proposed establishing a dedicated business champion role for the area, which Hyndburn Borough Council adopted to provide tailored assistance to enterprises.[71] Her voting record on business and economy matters generally followed Conservative lines, including opposition to proposals for lowering corporation tax rates in two 2021 divisions.[72] In social policy, Britcliffe highlighted education and health as priorities, aligning with her pre-election commitments to improve access to quality schooling and healthcare alongside job opportunities.[30] She endorsed the government's March 2022 Schools White Paper, which outlined reforms to expand educational opportunities and address attainment gaps.[73] Informed by her childhood experience of losing her mother to alcoholism at age nine, she has pushed for strengthened post-rehabilitation support, arguing in July 2023 for systemic improvements in transitional care to prevent relapse and aid recovery.[14] On welfare provision, she critiqued inefficiencies in the system during the October 2020 free school meals debate, proposing vouchers or food stamps as a targeted alternative to direct cash payments for vulnerable families.[74] Britcliffe largely supported public health measures, including social restrictions during the COVID-19 pandemic, with 20 votes in favor across 22 relevant divisions.[72] She also engaged on environmental issues with social dimensions, serving as vice-chair of the Net Zero All-Party Parliamentary Group and backing the UK's 2050 net zero target through parliamentary inquiries and reports.[37][75]

Reception and legacy

Achievements and contributions

Sara Britcliffe secured approximately Â£40 million in funding for her Hyndburn constituency during her tenure, including Levelling Up allocations that supported the regeneration of Accrington town centre through infrastructure and economic development projects.[76][77] In parliamentary roles, she served as Parliamentary Private Secretary to the Foreign Secretary from 2022, assisting in departmental operations and policy implementation on international matters.[26] She was appointed Vice Chairman of the Conservative Party for Youth in October 2022, aiming to enhance engagement with younger voters and party members, and later became Deputy Chairman focusing on candidates and women from November 2023 until July 2024.[78] These positions contributed to internal party organization and outreach efforts amid electoral challenges. Britcliffe participated in select committees and bill scrutiny, including membership of the Transport Select Committee from July 2023 to May 2024, where she examined transport policy and infrastructure issues, and the Renters (Reform) Bill Committee in November 2023, reviewing proposed housing reforms.[2] Her active involvement in debates, with recorded spoken contributions on topics such as local economic regeneration and supply chain investments, underscored her focus on constituency-specific advocacy.[79] In November 2023, she was recognized as the "Parliamentarian to Watch" in Westminster awards for her rising influence and effective representation.[76]

Criticisms from opponents

Labour opponents have accused Sara Britcliffe of engaging in immature and unprofessional conduct shortly after her 2019 general election victory in Hyndburn. The defeated Labour incumbent, Graham Jones, publicly condemned Britcliffe for wearing a T-shirt featuring his face overlaid with the phrase "Gotcha," labeling the act as "disgusting, childish and not befitting of an MP."[80] Jones further criticized her subsequent explanationâ€”that the T-shirt was put on her by friends while she was drunkâ€”as evidence of an "inability to fulfil the role responsibly," emphasizing that he received no apology despite her claims.[80][81] In 2022, amid the Partygate scandal, Britcliffe drew criticism from Labour councillors for failing to denounce breaches of COVID-19 lockdown rules by senior Conservatives. Rossendale Labour councillor Samara Barnes specifically rebuked Britcliffe's silence on Prime Minister Boris Johnson and Chancellor Rishi Sunak receiving fixed penalty notices on April 12, 2022, for attending gatherings in Downing Street, calling for her to "call out this behaviour" and demand their resignations.[82] Barnes highlighted the perceived hypocrisy of rule-enforcers flouting restrictions while constituents endured significant hardships, expressing disappointment that Britcliffe had not taken a public stand amid ongoing economic pressures like the cost-of-living crisis.[82] Local Labour figures have also sporadically targeted Britcliffe's defense of government policies perceived as prioritizing party loyalty over accountability, though such critiques often centered on broader Conservative handling of scandals rather than personalized attacks unique to her record.[83] These instances reflect opponents' portrayal of Britcliffe as emblematic of partisan defensiveness, contributing to narratives deployed during her 2024 reelection campaign, which she ultimately lost to Labour's Sarah Smith by a margin of approximately 5,000 votes on July 4, 2024.[84]

Public perception and media coverage

Sara Britcliffe garnered support among Conservative Party members and local voters in Hyndburn for her youthful energy and rapid rise, having been elected at age 24 in the 2019 general election with a 2,082-vote majority over Labour. Her debut parliamentary speech via Zoom on April 28, 2020, amid COVID-19 lockdowns, was highlighted by BBC News as a historic moment for remote participation, reflecting positively on her adaptability.[4] Endorsements from party figures and her contributions to outlets like CapX in July 2024, advocating for Conservative renewal, reinforced her image as a fresh voice within right-leaning circles.[85] Criticism emerged from political opponents, particularly over a December 2019 incident where she wore a T-shirt depicting the face of her defeated Labour predecessor, Graham Jones, prompting him to label it "disgusting" and "childish behaviour" in media statements.[80] In March 2023, her social media concerns about a Drag Queen Story Hour event at a Hyndburn literary festivalâ€”citing safety risks from online backlashâ€”led to its cancellation, drawing accusations from event performer Aida H. Dee and LGBTQ+-focused outlets of fostering unfounded fears, though Britcliffe maintained the decision prioritized child welfare amid threats.[86] Such coverage in sources like PinkNews, which align with progressive advocacy, amplified narratives of cultural insensitivity, contrasting with her framing of community protection. Her 2024 general election defeat in Hyndburn, where Labour's Delyth Jewell secured a 25.4% swing and 15,151-vote majority, signaled eroded local support amid national Conservative losses, with Britcliffe conceding a "battle" post-local polls in May 2024.[87] No comprehensive public opinion polls specifically gauged her favorability, but constituency results and sparse supporter testimonials on social media suggested loyalty among core voters for her advocacy on issues like addiction supportâ€”stemming from her public disclosure in July 2023 of witnessing her mother's alcoholism-related death at age nineâ€”yet broader appeal faltered.[14][88] Media coverage was predominantly local and event-driven, with Lancashire outlets like the Lancashire Telegraph reporting controversies and parliamentary activities, while national conservative-leaning publications such as The Telegraph emphasized her personal resilience.[89] Mainstream sources, often exhibiting left-leaning institutional biases, tended to foreground opponent critiques, as seen in Labour-aligned commentary on her net zero stance juxtaposed with a Â£5,000 donation from a motorsport executive in 2021, though no evidence of policy inconsistency was substantiated beyond partisan claims.[75] Post-defeat reflections, including her July 2025 Facebook post on ongoing advocacy, received limited attention, underscoring her niche rather than widespread profile.[90]

Post-parliamentary activities

Party roles and consultancy

Following her defeat in the 2024 general election, Sara Britcliffe co-founded Polaris Partners, a public affairs consultancy firm, alongside fellow former Conservative MP Andrew Higginbotham.[91][92] The firm provides bespoke strategic advice to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), drawing on the founders' parliamentary and party experience to navigate Westminster policy changes, Whitehall regulations, and opportunities for business growth.[92] Polaris emphasizes leveraging networks in government and politics to help clients adapt to legislative shifts and enhance engagement with policymakers.[92] Britcliffe has described her role at Polaris as involving communication, stakeholder engagement, and strategic planning, building on her prior positions within the Conservative Party, including as Deputy Chairman focused on candidate selection and women's engagement.[26] No formal ongoing roles within the Conservative Party have been reported as of October 2025, with her post-parliamentary focus shifting to private sector consultancy amid a broader trend of former MPs entering public affairs.[91] This transition aligns with her professional background in political delivery and her emphasis on practical support for businesses facing regulatory challenges.[26]

Ongoing advocacy and public engagement

Following her defeat in the July 4, 2024, general election, where she received 10,499 votes (28.9% of the total) in Hyndburn, Sara Britcliffe has sustained public engagement on addiction-related issues, particularly support for children of alcoholics (COAs). She has continued to highlight her personal experiences with her mother's alcohol dependency, initially raised in her April 2020 maiden speech, through affiliations with the National Association for Children of Alcoholics (NacOA).[20][19] In July 2025, NacOA promoted her parliamentary advocacy via social media, underscoring the ongoing need for policy changes to address hidden impacts on COAs, with Britcliffe positioned as a key voice in breaking silence on the issue.[93][94] Britcliffe remains active on platforms like Facebook and Instagram, commenting on local concerns such as fly-tipping in Hyndburn and Haslingden, which she described as a persistent blight reported frequently during her tenure.[95][77] These posts reflect continued constituent outreach, including reflections on community events and post-election adjustments, such as vacating her constituency office in September 2024.[96] In December 2024 and July 2025 social media updates, she framed her departure from Parliament as enabling more family time while expressing appreciation for her service, signaling a shift toward personal and selective public involvement rather than formal political roles.[97][98] No new partisan positions within the Conservative Party have been reported since her July 2024 exit as Deputy Chairman for Campaigning and Candidates.
