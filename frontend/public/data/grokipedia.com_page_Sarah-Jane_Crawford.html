Sarah-Jane Crawford

Sarah-Jane Crawford (born 15 July 1983) is an English television and radio presenter, actress, DJ, and entrepreneur recognized for her work across major broadcasting networks including the BBC, MTV, and Channel 4.[1][2] Born in London to parents of Irish and Caribbean heritage and adopted shortly after birth, she relocated to Cambridgeshire at age 12 and later earned a degree in marketing before entering media.[2] Crawford's career gained prominence with her debut as a stand-in DJ on BBC Radio 1Xtra in 2008, where she hosted weekday afternoon and weekend shows, including the station's chart and book club segments, while conducting interviews with figures such as Robert De Niro and Usher.[2] She expanded into television with roles on E4 as a roving reporter, MTV events like the EMA backstage coverage, and Channel 4 programs, later co-hosting Channel 5's World's Strongest Man and presenting E! News at the BAFTAs and the 2018 Royal Wedding.[3] Beyond broadcasting, she has acted in films including The Hatton Garden Job (2017) and Fedz (2013), and launched ventures such as the vegan dining app Viappi in 2017 and the Clean Curls hair care brand focused on curly hair products.[1][3]

Early life

Family background and upbringing

Sarah-Jane Crawford was born in Lambeth, London, on 15 July 1983 to a white birth mother of Irish descent and a black birth father from West Africa, giving her a mixed-race heritage.[4][5] She was adopted by white parents at two months old and raised primarily in South East London.[4][6] Her adoptive family provided a childhood characterized by love and security, though her transracial adoption shaped her experiences navigating racial identity in a white household.[7] Crawford's family relocated to Cambridgeshire when she was 12, placing her in a region that, during the 1990s, featured limited ethnic diversity amid its university-dominated, middle-class socioeconomic profile.[2] In school settings during her upbringing, she encountered peer comments making her uncomfortable about her physical appearance, such as her lips, alongside accusations of exhibiting a "black attitude" and exposure to racist jokes framed as banter.[8] Despite the absence of biological family cultural ties, Crawford has described maintaining a strong sense of Black identity, stating that she has "never not felt Black" even as elements of her heritage felt like missing pieces.[9]

Education and initial influences

Crawford completed her early secondary education at Sydenham Girls' School in London for one year in 1991â€“1992 before her family relocated.[10] She then attended Witchford Village College in Cambridgeshire, where she earned GCSEs in 1997, achieving eight grades at Aâ€“B and two at C.[10] From 1997 to 1999, she studied A-Levels at Cambridge Regional College, obtaining grades Aâ€“C.[10] Following secondary school, Crawford enrolled at Kingston University before transferring or additionally attending Anglia Ruskin University in Cambridge.[6] She graduated from Anglia Ruskin with a 2:1 Bachelor's degree in Marketing in 2003.[11][10] Her initial interests in performance and media were shaped by exposure to music within the multicultural South East London community of her early years, prior to the family move to Cambridgeshire, which cultivated an adaptability evident in her later pivot from marketing studies to broadcasting.[6] No documented self-taught DJing or local event participation predates her 2008 professional radio entry, though her university background in marketing provided foundational skills in audience engagement and promotion.[12]

Professional career

Entry into media and radio beginnings

Sarah-Jane Crawford entered the media industry through her participation in the third series of Channel 4's reality competition Shipwrecked in 2006, which served as a launching pad for her broadcasting aspirations despite her academic background in marketing.[12] Following this exposure, she secured initial presenting roles, including work on the Shopping Channel where she developed live ad-libbing skills essential for on-air performance in a fast-paced, unscripted environment.[13] By 2007, she hosted red carpet and backstage coverage for the Vodafone Live Music Awards, marking her shift from contestant to professional host amid the competitive UK media landscape where entry-level opportunities often required demonstrated versatility and persistence.[6] Her radio career began in October 2008 when she joined BBC Radio 1Xtra as a stand-in DJ, covering ad hoc shows for established presenters on the urban music station known for its rigorous selection of on-air talent.[2] This freelance-style entry allowed her to hone broadcasting techniques through irregular but high-pressure slots, building rapport with listeners and producers in a field dominated by networked insiders and where securing permanency demanded consistent energy and genre knowledge.[2] The stand-in work directly facilitated her transition to regular programming by early 2009, including early weekend breakfast shows, establishing a foothold that rewarded her prior media experience while navigating the station's emphasis on authentic urban voices.[6]

Radio presenting

Crawford joined BBC Radio 1Xtra in October 2008, initially serving as a stand-in DJ for various ad hoc shows before progressing to regular presenting roles across the network.[2] She hosted weekday afternoon programs from 1:00 p.m. to 4:00 p.m. Monday through Friday, contributing to the station's urban music and entertainment output over a seven-year tenure ending in October 2015.[10] Her work included delivering music selections, interviews, and entertainment segments tailored to the station's audience.[2] In August 2015, Crawford announced her exit from BBC Radio 1Xtra to focus on a new project in Los Angeles, with her final broadcast scheduled for October 2, 2015.[14] Following her departure from the BBC, Crawford transitioned to Hits Radio under Bauer Media, where she began hosting shows five days a week starting in 2018.[10] Her programs feature contemporary hits alongside classic throwbacks, maintaining a consistent midday presence on the network.[15] As of October 2025, she continues in this role, with daily episodes confirmed through ongoing station scheduling.[7]

Television presenting

Crawford commenced her television presenting career as a roving reporter for E4, where she contributed to music and entertainment segments, including red-carpet coverage associated with T4 and E4 programming.[16][7] In 2009, she served as the primary host for MTV's spin-off channel VIVA upon its launch, conducting interviews with prominent music artists and establishing her on-screen presence in music television.[17][12] She hosted The Xtra Factor on ITV2 for the 2014 series (the 11th season of The X Factor), commencing on 30 August 2014 and replacing prior presenters Caroline Flack and Matt Richardson; Crawford departed after one season to focus on new opportunities.[10][18] Crawford has presented live coverage of events such as the MOBO Awards on ITV2 and conducted high-profile interviews for Channel 4, including sessions with actors like Robert De Niro and RenÃ©e Zellweger.[17][16] More recently, she has hosted segments and voice-overs for E! Network and contributed presenting items to BBC One's Morning Live.[19][7]

Film, modelling, and other media work

Crawford has appeared in several British films, including the 2013 urban drama Fedz, directed by Lee Howard, where she portrayed a supporting role amid a cast featuring Ozwald Boateng and Riz Ahmed. In the same year, she played Lorna, a single mother, in It's a Lot, a comedy-drama directed by Femi Oyeniran and Darwood Grace, which premiered in the UK on October 21, 2013, and explored themes of friendship and urban life in London.[20] Her most prominent film credit came in 2017 with The Hatton Garden Job, a heist thriller directed by Ronnie Cook, in which she portrayed Detective Chief Inspector Emma Carter, investigating the real-life 2015 burglary, opposite actors including Matthew Goode and Larry Lamb. In addition to acting, Crawford has worked as a model, signing with the agency Models 1 in early 2013 for special bookings, leveraging her experience from earlier campaigns such as one for Ipanema flip-flops.[21] She featured in a British Vogue photoshoot in November 2008, highlighting her transition from modelling to media roles.[22] Crawford has extended her media presence through voice-over work, including serving as the voice for the MTV Chart Show, and occasional DJ sets at events separate from her radio commitments.[13]

Entrepreneurship

Viappi vegan app

Crawford launched Viappi in 2017 as a free mobile application to assist users in maintaining vegan and vegetarian diets through practical tools for dining and cooking.[23][24] The app stemmed from her personal shift to veganism in May 2015, following a 21-day plant-based challenge, and aimed to address challenges in finding affordable, location-independent vegan options.[25][24] Key functionalities include a UK-wide directory for locating vegetarian and vegan restaurants, complemented by a database of plant-based recipes developed with input from professional chefs.[19][26] Recipes encompass accessible home-cooked meals such as lentil bolognese, vegan mac 'n' cheese, chocolate cake, and adaptations of international dishes like pizza, lasagna, and Caribbean or African-inspired vegan twists.[25] Positioned as a competitor to apps like HappyCow, Viappi emphasized user-friendly features for budget-conscious eaters but garnered limited traction, with no publicly available download metrics and minimal presence in subsequent vegan app recommendations.[19][26] Its social media activity, including recipe updates, appears to have tapered off after initial promotion.[27]

Clean Curls beauty brand

In 2024, Sarah-Jane Crawford launched Clean Curls, a haircare brand specializing in non-toxic products for curly and textured hair, with an initial exclusive rollout in Superdrug stores across the United Kingdom on February 12.[28] [29] The brand's debut featured a range of vegan, cruelty-free formulations free from sulfates, parabens, phthalates, and other harsh chemicals, developed to hydrate, define, and maintain curl health without compromising safety or efficacy.[30] [31] Crawford formulated the products drawing from her own experiences transitioning from chemical straightening to natural curl care, aiming to address gaps in affordable, clean options for textured hair patterns often underserved by mainstream brands.[32] The lineup innovates by prioritizing ingredient transparency and performance for diverse curl types, including coily and wavy textures, with an emphasis on accessibility for everyday consumers rather than premium pricing.[28] Initial distribution began in 112 Superdrug locations, reflecting a bootstrapped approach focused on rapid market testing and consumer feedback to refine product efficacy.[33] By mid-2024, the brand expanded to additional retailers like Ocado and entered the Irish market via Superdrug on June 28, demonstrating early commercial traction through in-store and online sales channels.[34] [35] Clean Curls' growth metrics, including store footprint expansion within the first year driven by strong performance indicators, position it for further scaling in 2025, with stated goals to broaden access to non-toxic curl care amid rising demand for clean beauty alternatives.[33] [36] The brand's nomination for awards, such as Cosmopolitan UK's Reader's Choice for best shampoo in late 2024, underscores its emerging recognition in the competitive haircare sector.[37]

Personal life

Relationships and family

Crawford participated in the third series of the E4 dating show Celebs Go Dating in 2017, engaging in dates arranged by professional matchmakers to find a compatible partner.[38] She is married to Brian Barry-Murphy, a football coach and manager, and the couple has two daughters.[39] Their first child, Summer Jane Crawford-Murphy, was born in August 2020.[40] [41] Crawford and Barry-Murphy announced their second pregnancy in July 2021.[42] Their second daughter arrived on December 3, 2021.[43] [44]

Health experiences and identity

Sarah-Jane Crawford possesses mixed Irish and West African heritage, with a white mother and an Afro-Caribbean father, and has articulated a self-perception as a racial "chameleon" due to her adaptability across social environments.[9][5] She has emphasized a core identification with Black identity, stating in a 2024 interview, "Iâ€™ve never not felt Black," despite incomplete early knowledge of her full heritage, shaped by extended family dynamics where many members are Black or mixed-race.[9][4] Crawford encountered a breast lump in 2014, prompting a lumpectomy that revealed benign tissue, though the episode served as an early health alert amid her rising media career.[45][46] A subsequent cancer scare occurred in 2016, marking the second such incident within two years, with no malignancy confirmed.[47][48] Related diagnostics uncovered a uterine fibroid comparable in size to a three-month pregnancy, which necessitated emergency surgery in January 2015 to remove the mass.[45][49] She has additionally contended with endometriosis over multiple years, contributing to ongoing gynecological management without specified causal links to other conditions.[50] Prior to a successful pregnancy, Crawford experienced a miscarriage in June 2020, describing the associated grief as profound.[51] During her pregnancies, she noted heightened awareness of maternal health disparities, including elevated complication risks for women of colour, such as difficulties in childbirth.[41][8]

Advocacy and public stances

Veganism and related debates

Sarah-Jane Crawford adopted a vegan diet in 2015 after emulating BeyoncÃ©'s 21-day vegan challenge as a segment idea for her radio show, subsequently maintaining the lifestyle long-term and describing it as transformative for her health and energy levels.[52][53] As a Veganuary ambassador, she has advocated for plant-based eating through public talks and a podcast series titled The V-Word, interviewing figures in sports, music, and cuisine to raise awareness of vegan options and ethical considerations.[19] Her efforts emphasize personal benefits such as improved fitness, achieved alongside three weekly personal training sessions, positioning veganism as accessible and appealing for mainstream adoption.[24] In November 2018, Crawford appeared on Good Morning Britain to debate the sacking of a Waitrose magazine editor who had joked about "killing vegans" in a column, sparking accusations of vegan intolerance toward humor critiquing the movement.[54] The exchange with host Piers Morgan highlighted tensions, with critics arguing that demands for the editor's dismissal exemplified over-sensitivity and a reluctance to tolerate satire on veganism's perceived moral absolutism, even as Crawford defended the response as proportionate to the remark's provocation.[55] This incident underscored broader public skepticism toward vegan advocacy, where proponents like Crawford are sometimes portrayed as prioritizing ideological purity over free expression. Empirical studies reveal mixed outcomes for vegan diets, challenging universal claims of superiority. While prospective cohorts associate veganism with reduced risks of cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes, vegans exhibit 2.3 times higher hip fracture rates compared to omnivores, likely due to lower bone mineral density from inadequate calcium and protein intake without supplementation.[56][57] Vitamin B12 deficiency affects 11-90% of unsupplemented vegans, risking neurological issues like neuropathy, particularly in populations with higher baseline needs such as pregnant women or those with genetic absorption variances.[58] Meta-analyses also link veganism to elevated stroke risk and depression symptoms, attributing these to potential shortfalls in omega-3 fatty acids, heme iron, and complete proteins, outcomes that causal analysis ties to the diet's exclusion of animal-derived nutrients rather than inherent plant inferiority when balanced omnivorously.[59][60] Crawford's positive personal narrative aligns with self-reported benefits but contrasts with evidence indicating veganism's unsuitability for certain groups, such as athletes requiring high bioavailable protein or individuals with absorption disorders, necessitating individualized assessment over blanket endorsement.[61]

Discussions on race and maternal health

Crawford, of mixed West African and Irish heritage and adopted by white parents, has emphasized self-identification in racial matters, stating she classed herself as black due to extended family ties, while acknowledging the complexities of mixed-race experiences and external labeling.[4][9] In maternal health discussions, she has advocated ending taboos around fertility and pregnancy complications for black women, arguing that shame hinders awareness and support, as evidenced by her Instagram series "Mums The Word" sharing personal miscarriage trauma from a private scan.[8] She highlighted disparities, noting black women in the UK face elevated pregnancy-related death risksâ€”citing figures around five times higher at the timeâ€”and urged more research into underlying causes alongside culturally competent care attuned to community differences.[8] Recent MBRRACE-UK data for 2020-2022 shows black women remain over twice as likely to die maternally as white women (13.56 overall rate per 100,000 maternities), with persistent gaps despite some decline, and similar primary causes like cardiovascular issues but higher substandard care rates.[62][63] Crawford's push for open dialogue challenges sensitivities that may suppress race-specific risk talks, prioritizing practical interventions over avoidance.[8] While many analyses, including parliamentary reports, attribute disparities mainly to systemic racism and care biases, empirical evidence indicates multifactorial causes, including biological elements like 60% higher preeclampsia prevalence among black womenâ€”a hypertensive disorder linked to placental and genetic factorsâ€”independent of socioeconomic status in some cohorts.[64][65][66] Dissenting perspectives stress individual-level factors such as preexisting hypertension or obesity rates, which correlate with ethnicity but transcend structural explanations alone, as disparities endure even among affluent groups; for instance, U.S. data show high-income black mothers faring worse than low-income white ones, suggesting causal roles beyond access or bias.[67] Crawford's focus on community-tailored services implicitly nods to such inherent differences, favoring evidence-driven solutions over narratives that risk politicizing health data.[8]

Reception and impact

Achievements and recognition

Crawford hosted the MOBO Awards in October 2014 alongside Mel B on ITV2, marking a significant milestone in her broadcasting career.[6] She also served as red carpet host for the British Fashion Awards in 2015, providing live coverage for E! Network and engaging audiences with insights into the event's glamour.[68] These roles underscored her versatility in presenting high-profile music and fashion events across major networks like ITV2 and E!. In radio, Crawford presented the UK Chart Show on Hits Radio starting in January 2019, delivering weekly countdowns that aligned with the network's focus on contemporary hits.[69] She co-hosted two seasons of World's Strongest Man on Channel 5 and hosted The Capitalist on Capital XTRA for three years, contributing to her reputation as a dynamic voice in UK media.[7] Crawford launched Viappi, a vegan restaurant-finding app, in November 2017, aimed at simplifying access to plant-based dining options and reflecting her commitment to vegan advocacy.[25] Her beauty brand, Clean Curls, debuted products for curly and textured hair using non-toxic ingredients, achieving distribution in retailers such as Superdrug and Ocado by 2024.[28] Media outlets recognized Crawford's multi-hyphenate trajectory in early 2025, profiling her as "one to watch" for balancing broadcasting, entrepreneurship, and brand-building amid a demanding schedule.[7] She featured in Cosmopolitan's beauty segment that February, sharing expertise on hair care routines tied to her professional ventures.[70]

Criticisms and controversies

In November 2018, Crawford appeared on Good Morning Britain in a debate sparked by the resignation of Waitrose food editor William Sitwell, who had emailed a freelance writer pitching vegan recipes with counter-ideas for a series called "Kill the Vegans," including force-feeding vegans meat and telling them "we are going to kill you."[54] Crawford contended that Sitwell's remarks were unprofessional and inappropriate for someone in a position of editorial influence, arguing for measured rejection of pitches rather than inflammatory private responses, while clarifying she was not offended but highlighted the dismissal of a demographic shift toward veganism.[54] Host Piers Morgan countered by defending the comments as poor-taste jokes unfit for public outrage, labeling society "snowflakey" and accusing Crawford of perpetual anger from her vegan diet, dubbing her "hangry" and symptomatic of broader hypersensitivity to humor.[54] The confrontation underscored debates on free expression versus workplace decorum, with Morgan's camp critiquing vegan advocates like Crawford for amplifying minor jests into career-ending scandals.[55] Crawford's tenure as host of The Xtra Factor in 2014 ended after one season amid reported scheduling conflicts and program shake-ups, though no specific ratings declines were attributed to her performance; subsequent series viewership hovered around 8.61 million for main episodes without direct linkage to spin-off critiques.[71][72]
