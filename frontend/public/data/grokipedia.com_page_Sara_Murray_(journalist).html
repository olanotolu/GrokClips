Sara Murray (journalist)

Sara Murray is an American former political journalist who served as a Washington-based correspondent for CNN from 2015 onward, specializing in coverage of presidential campaigns, immigration policy, and federal investigations including those related to the Trump administration and the Justice Department.[1][2]
A graduate of the University of Maryland's Philip Merrill College of Journalism, Murray began her career at The Wall Street Journal, where she reported on Mitt Romney's 2012 presidential bid, congressional activities, and immigration issues.[3][4]
At CNN, she became a prominent figure in on-the-ground political reporting, frequently covering Donald Trump's rallies and events during his 2016 campaign, providing live analysis that positioned her as a key network voice on his rise amid criticism from Trump himself for what he deemed inaccurate portrayals.[5][6][7]
Her work extended to bipartisan scrutiny of sensitive probes, such as Russia-related inquiries, though conducted within the context of mainstream media outlets often critiqued for institutional left-leaning biases in source selection and framing.[8]
In recent years, Murray transitioned from journalism to private sector investigations, joining FTI Consulting as a managing director focused on complex, politically charged cases drawing on her reporting expertise.[8][9]

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Sara Murray was born on March 31, 1985, in Mount Pleasant, Michigan, a small city in Isabella County with a population of approximately 26,000 residents during her formative years. She was raised there in a traditional Midwestern environment characterized by community ties and practical family values.[10][11] Her parents, both certified public accountants, offered engaged and supportive guidance, instilling a strong work ethic, humility, and confidence in her abilities. They expressed skepticism about pursuing journalism as a career, instead encouraging a business-oriented backup plan reflective of their professional pragmatism. Murray's father, Paul B. Murray, continues to practice as a CPA in Mount Pleasant.[11][12] Public details on her mother and siblings remain limited, though she grew up alongside sisters in the local community, which emphasized self-reliance and ambition extending beyond regional boundaries.[11][12]

Academic background

Sara Murray earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in journalism from the Philip Merrill College of Journalism at the University of Maryland in 2007.[3][4] The Philip Merrill College of Journalism's undergraduate program focuses on developing core competencies in news writing, reporting, and multi-platform storytelling, with opportunities for specialization in areas such as investigative journalism, broadcast, and data reporting.[13][14] This curriculum prioritizes hands-on experiential learning through capstone projects, internships, and field-based courses, equipping graduates with skills for factual news production across print, broadcast, and digital formats rather than theoretical or interpretive frameworks.[14] Such training provided Murray with a foundation in empirical reporting techniques, aligning with her subsequent roles in political correspondence that demand verification of primary sources and on-the-ground verification.[3]

Professional career

Early journalism roles

Sara Murray joined The Wall Street Journal in 2008 as a reporter, immediately following her graduation from the University of Maryland's Philip Merrill College of Journalism.[4] Her initial roles involved assignments across the newspaper's politics and economics teams, where she contributed to coverage emphasizing empirical analysis of policy impacts and market dynamics.[4] This period honed her expertise in dissecting complex financial and regulatory issues through verifiable data, such as tracing the ripple effects of housing market policies on broader economic stability.[8] A key early focus was her reporting on the 2008 financial crisis, which included scrutiny of bipartisan regulatory shortcomings, including the roles of federal oversight bodies like the Federal Reserve and congressional legislation such as the Community Reinvestment Act amendments.[8] Murray's work highlighted causal links between lending practices, government incentives, and systemic risks, drawing on economic indicators and primary documents to assess accountability without partisan favoritism.[4] These assignments demonstrated her approach to impartial analysis, prioritizing first-hand sourcing from policymakers and industry data over narrative-driven interpretations. By 2012, at age 27, Murray shifted to on-the-ground political reporting, embedding with Mitt Romney's presidential campaign to cover strategy, fundraising, and voter outreach in battleground states.[5] This role involved direct observation of campaign operations, including interviews with advisors and analysis of polling data, fostering skills in real-time fact-checking and contextualizing political events amid economic recovery debates.[15] In 2013, she transitioned to the Wall Street Journal's digital video team as an anchor and reporter, producing multimedia segments on political scandals and economic policy shifts, such as early probes into government spending inefficiencies that implicated figures across party lines.[16] These experiences built a foundation in rigorous, evidence-based fieldwork that underscored bipartisan accountability in her pre-network career.[17]

Tenure at CNN

Sara Murray joined CNN in March 2015 as a political reporter based in Washington, D.C., focusing on the 2016 presidential campaign across television and digital platforms.[7][15] Her role expanded to cover the White House, Justice Department, and politically sensitive investigations, emphasizing on-the-ground reporting from primary events rather than studio analysis.[1][8] During the 2016 election cycle, Murray provided extensive coverage of Donald Trump's campaign trail, documenting direct interactions with supporters and candidates amid heightened tensions, including incidents such as vandalism against her vehicle by Trump backers.[18][19] She continued this fieldwork in subsequent cycles, contributing to CNN's political reporting through 2020 and 2024, where her access to rallies and events allowed for firsthand accounts of voter dynamics and campaign logistics.[5] Murray's tenure included bipartisan scrutiny of investigations, such as the Russia probe, where she reported on developments like the arrest of alleged foreign agents and interference attempts using court filings and official statements as primary sources.[20][21] On January 6-related matters, she covered prosecutions of participants, including Proud Boys convictions for seditious conspiracy and cases involving local politicians present at the Capitol, drawing from legal documents and committee transcripts to detail outcomes without endorsing partisan interpretations.[22][23] This approach, spanning nearly a decade until her departure in late 2024, positioned her as a key correspondent amid CNN's broader struggles with audience trust and ratings declines attributed to perceived institutional biases in mainstream media.[24]

Transition to private sector

In January 2025, Sara Murray departed CNN after nearly a decade as an investigative correspondent, where she had covered politically sensitive topics including national security and election integrity.[24] She joined FTI Consulting as a Managing Director in its Cybersecurity practice, based in Washington, D.C., to focus on complex investigations involving misinformation, disinformation, and foreign threat actors.[24][8] At FTI, Murray applies her journalism-honed skills in sourcing, analysis, and communication to assist corporate clients with risk assessments, crisis preparedness, data privacy challenges, and litigation support in cybersecurity and national security domains.[8] Her prior reporting on federal probes into election interference and global threats directly informs advisory services aimed at mitigating digital misinformation campaigns.[24] Murray maintains public engagement through expert commentary on media outlets, such as discussions on artificial intelligence risks for WMUR News 9, and active posting on X (formerly Twitter) under @SaraMurray, where she addresses ongoing political and legal developments.[25] This activity links her advisory expertise to broader discourse without resuming full-time journalism.[26]

Notable reporting and contributions

Coverage of presidential campaigns

Murray embedded with Donald Trump's 2016 presidential campaign as a CNN political correspondent, covering rallies and events across the United States for about 1.5 years.[18] Her on-the-ground reporting captured the raw intensity of supporter gatherings, including chants against media outlets and clashes with protesters, as seen at events in New Orleans in March 2016 and San Jose in June 2016 where anti-Trump demonstrators pursued attendees.[18] She documented widespread voter anger toward political establishments, media institutions, and financial elites, which stemmed from perceived failures in addressing economic displacement in rural and industrial areas.[18] This sentiment prioritized critiques of trade deals and job outsourcing over identity-based appeals, aligning with Trump's policy emphasis on renegotiating agreements like NAFTA to revive manufacturing sectors hit by globalization.[27] Murray's direct interactions, such as interviewing supporters whose campaign materials were vandalized, underscored grievances rooted in tangible losses rather than abstract cultural conflicts often highlighted elsewhere.[18] Personal encounters illustrated her immersion amid hostility: Trump named her at a November 2015 rally, calling her a "total novice reporter" to crowd jeers; her broadcast cables were cut twice during live shots; and her vehicle was keyed in Florida.[28][18][19] In the 2020 cycle, Murray shifted focus to post-election legal maneuvers, reporting on Trump's challenges to vote counts in states like Pennsylvania and Georgia, citing specific court filings and dismissals that rejected claims on procedural grounds such as standing rather than evidentiary merits.[29][30] She examined how 2020 irregularities allegations motivated Republican candidates, analyzing over 60 failed lawsuits filed by the Trump campaign and allies, which courts uniformly denied relief despite reviewing affidavits on ballot handling.[31] For 2024, her coverage addressed procedural changes, such as new voter ID requirements in Georgia effective for the cycle's early voting starting October 2024, and discrepancies in mail-in ballot processing timelines in battlegrounds, grounded in state election board data over anecdotal reports.[32][33] This sustained engagement with primary documents and local officials enabled reporting on grassroots conservative concerns, including skepticism toward expanded absentee voting, distinct from speculative narratives.[34]

Investigations into legal and political matters

Murray extensively covered the Special Counsel Robert Mueller's investigation into Russian election interference and links to the Trump campaign, reporting on key developments such as the December 2017 indictments of former campaign chairman Paul Manafort and associate Rick Gates, while noting President Trump's public statements distancing himself from the probe.[35] She detailed the January 2019 FBI raid on Trump associate Roger Stone's home, which CNN anticipated based on grand jury scheduling, and provided on-site analysis of Stone's subsequent trial, where witness testimony contradicted Trump's statements to Mueller about WikiLeaks contacts.[36] [37] In May 2023, Murray reported on the release of John Durham's review of the FBI's handling of the Russia probe origins, highlighting congressional calls for testimony on findings that critiqued investigative predications.[38] Her reporting on the Hunter Biden investigation emphasized evidentiary details from IRS whistleblowers, including a May 2023 account of tensions in a October 2022 meeting between prosecutors and Biden's defense team, where alleged interference by the Justice Department was raised, potentially affecting charging decisions on tax and gun offenses.[39] Murray covered House Republicans' November 2022 pledges to probe Biden family business dealings, citing subpoena demands for financial records tied to foreign entities like Burisma, and in May 2023 detailed bank records alleging over $10 million in payments to Biden relatives from overseas sources between 2014 and 2019, framing these as patterns warranting scrutiny beyond campaign narratives.[40] [41] She also reported on Hunter Biden's May 2023 Arkansas paternity hearing, where DNA evidence confirmed fatherhood, leading to child support obligations amid broader legal pressures.[42] In Trump-related legal probes, Murray provided courtroom and prosecutorial insights, such as her February 2022 interview with Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis, who described her election interference investigation as a "criminal" inquiry focused on evidence of pressure on officials to "find" votes, rejecting perceptions of partisan gamesmanship.[43] She tracked the August 2022 special grand jury impanelment in Georgia, reporting on its subpoena powers and focus on 2020 election subversion efforts, and in 2023 covered federal probes into classified documents at Mar-a-Lago, including witness accounts of Trump's directives to lawyers on retention.[44] [45] Murray's approach often prioritized verifiable court records and on-record statements over anonymous leaks, as seen in her live updates from sentencing hearings and DOJ filings, underscoring discrepancies between public defenses and documented evidence in cases spanning both parties.[37][46]

Reception and impact

Professional recognition and achievements

Murray earned a News & Documentary Emmy Award from the National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences in 2020 for her reporting on the Special Counsel's investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. presidential election.[9] This accolade recognized her contributions to CNN's coverage, which involved detailed analysis of primary documents and legal proceedings related to the probe.[8] She also received a White House Correspondents' Association Award for the same investigative work, highlighting her role in elucidating the investigation's scope and findings for public understanding.[8] In 2018, Murray was nominated for a News & Documentary Emmy in the Outstanding Breaking News Coverage category as part of CNN's team efforts during significant political events.[47] Her expertise in federal investigations and political reporting culminated in her recruitment by FTI Consulting in January 2025 as a Managing Director in its Cybersecurity practice, where she advises on complex probes involving national security threats, misinformation, and corporate risks drawing from her journalism-honed analytical approach.[24] This transition underscores the professional value placed on her track record in dissecting high-stakes legal and geopolitical matters.[8]

Criticisms and allegations of bias

Murray has encountered criticism primarily from former President Donald Trump and his administration officials regarding her on-the-ground reporting during his 2016 campaign and presidency. In November 2015, Trump accused her of biased coverage at a book-signing event, stating she "didn't report it very well" by failing to flip cameras to capture long lines of supporters, and labeled her "absolutely terrible."[48] Similar tensions arose in March 2017, when White House Press Secretary Sean Spicer challenged Murray's questions on anonymous sourcing in stories linking Trump associates to Russia, accusing her of promoting unsubstantiated narratives.[49] Conservative critics have alleged that, despite Murray's reputation for fact-based reporting relative to some CNN colleagues, her work reflects residual institutional bias at the network, manifested in disproportionate scrutiny of Trump-era events over analogous Democratic actions. For instance, her coverage of the so-called fake electors efforts in battleground statesâ€”such as reporting in July 2024 that seven states selected such individuals as Republican convention delegatesâ€”has drawn claims of selective framing that emphasizes Republican culpability without equivalent emphasis on disputed 2020 election procedures in Democrat-controlled jurisdictions or potential irregularities like ballot handling in Pennsylvania.[50][51] Critics, including those in conservative media, argue this approach underplays causal factors such as preelection law changes pushed by Democratic officials, which fueled Republican challenges, and instead normalizes narratives of unilateral GOP malfeasance in post-election disputes.[52] In coverage of January 6, 2021, Murray's reporting on ongoing Republican promotion of election-related claims has been faulted by some conservatives for insufficient exploration of antecedent left-wing influences, such as rhetoric from Democratic leaders questioning prior elections (e.g., Stacey Abrams' 2018 Georgia contest refusals), which paralleled but received less network dissection.[52] These critiques posit that while Murray avoids overt sensationalism, her emphasis aligns with CNN's documented left-leaning tilt, evidenced by internal leaks and external analyses showing softer treatment of scandals like Hunter Biden's foreign dealings compared to Trump probes.[51][53] Conversely, isolated left-leaning commentary has portrayed Murray as occasionally too accommodating to Trump defenses, such as her May 2024 assessment that an acquittal in his New York hush-money trial would represent a "worst-case scenario" for Democrats by validating skepticism of the case's merits, potentially undermining anti-Trump momentum.[51] This view, however, lacks widespread documentation, and Murray's defenders highlight her tough questioning in Trump interviews and consistent fact-checking as distinguishing her from peers prone to unverified narratives. Overall, allegations against her personally remain sparse compared to network-wide rebukes, with empirical reviews suggesting her output hews closer to verifiable data than ideologically driven counterparts.[49]

Personal life

Marriage and divorce

Sara Murray married Garrett Haake, a correspondent for MSNBC and NBC News, on April 22, 2017, in a private ceremony near Austin, Texas.[54][55] The event drew attendees from the journalism community, including members of the White House press pool, reflecting the couple's shared professional background in political reporting.[54] Murray and Haake divorced sometime prior to August 2020, with no public statements released on the reasons for the split or other details of the proceedings.[56] Both individuals have maintained privacy regarding their personal lives post-divorce, consistent with the limited disclosure typical among high-profile journalists navigating demanding careers in Washington, D.C.[57]

Residence and current activities

Murray resides in Washington, D.C.[58] She departed CNN in January 2025 after nearly a decade as a political correspondent, transitioning to the private sector as a Managing Director in FTI Consulting's cybersecurity practice.[24] In this capacity, Murray applies her investigative journalism background to complex cases involving national security, misinformation, and geopolitical risks, conducting bipartisan analyses for corporate and legal clients.[8][59] Her work emphasizes empirical scrutiny of sensitive political and security matters, drawing on prior coverage of high-profile investigations.[60]
