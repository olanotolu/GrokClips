New York City Police Department

The New York City Police Department (NYPD), established in 1845, is the primary municipal law enforcement agency responsible for public safety in New York City, a metropolis of over 8.5 million residents spanning five boroughs.[1] Headquartered at One Police Plaza in Lower Manhattan, it operates through 78 patrol precincts, 12 transit districts, and specialized units for counterterrorism, traffic management, and emergency response.[1] As the largest police department in the United States, the NYPD employs approximately 34,000 sworn officers and 15,000 civilian personnel.[2][3] The department has pioneered data-driven policing strategies, such as CompStat, which facilitated a substantial decline in major crimes during the 1990s and beyond, contributing to New York City's current lowest major crime rate among the 25 largest U.S. cities.[1][4] This empirical success stemmed from focused enforcement on quality-of-life offenses and misdemeanor arrests, yielding causal reductions in serious violence through deterrence and disruption of criminal networks, as evidenced by longitudinal crime data rather than correlative attributions to external factors.[5] Despite these outcomes, the NYPD has faced persistent controversies, including historical corruption scandals and low rates of officer discipline for misconduct complaintsâ€”only about 2% of over 180,000 investigated cases since 2000 resulted in penaltiesâ€”highlighting challenges in internal accountability amid high operational demands.[6][7] Recent staffing shortages, with thousands of officers departing since 2020 due to recruitment difficulties and attrition exceeding 300 per month, have strained resources even as large recruit classes are sworn in to reverse the trend.[3][8] The agency's structure emphasizes specialized bureaus for patrol, detection, and intelligence, enabling comprehensive coverage but also exposing it to scrutiny over practices like stop-and-frisk, which courts ruled unconstitutional in scale yet correlated with crime drops in targeted analyses.[1][6]

History

Founding and Early Development (1845â€“1900)

The New York City Municipal Police Department was established on May 23, 1845, through the Municipal Police Act passed by the New York State Legislature, which authorized a uniformed day force of approximately 800 officers to supplement and eventually replace the city's antiquated night watch system dating back to the Dutch colonial era.[9] [10] This reform addressed rising disorder in a city of nearly 400,000 residents amid rapid immigration and industrialization, with the force modeled on London's preventive policing principles emphasizing uniformed patrols for public order rather than reactive constables.[11] [12] George Washington Matsell, a Tammany Hall affiliate, was appointed the first chief of police, overseeing initial operations from headquarters at the Tombs prison; the department issued its first printed rules and regulations in 1845, though full uniforms were not adopted until 1853.[13] [11] Early recruits, often recent Irish immigrants lacking formal training, focused on suppressing street crime, vagrancy, and public disturbances, but the force quickly exhibited lax discipline and political favoritism.[14] By the mid-1850s, entrenched corruptionâ€”manifesting in officer shakedowns of merchants, protection of gambling dens, and election-day intimidation under Tammany influenceâ€”prompted state intervention.[15] In 1857, the New York State Legislature created the Metropolitan Police District, encompassing New York City, Westchester County, and Staten Island, and abolished the Municipal Police to centralize control and enforce liquor and anti-vice laws more rigorously; this shift installed a state-appointed board and expanded the force to over 1,000 officers.[16] [17] Resistance from the outgoing Municipal force, loyal to Democratic Mayor Fernando Wood, erupted into the Dead Rabbits Riot on June 16, 1857, a violent clash near City Hall that killed at least eight and injured dozens, underscoring partisan divisions in policing.[18] The Metropolitan Police inherited these issues, struggling during events like the 1863 Civil War Draft Riots, where officers quelled widespread anti-draft and anti-Black violence but sustained heavy casualties, including three patrolmen killed.[17] Throughout the late 19th century, Tammany Hall's machine politics deepened systemic graft, with captains routinely demanding bribes from brothels, saloons, and lottery operatorsâ€”practices tacitly enabled by political patronage in appointments and promotions.[15] [14] The Tweed Ring scandals of the 1860sâ€“1870s exemplified this fusion of police and municipal corruption, as officers shielded Boss William Tweed's embezzlements.[19] Public outcry culminated in the Lexow Committee investigation of 1894â€“1895, a New York State Senate probe chaired by Clarence Lexow, which documented extortion rackets, false arrests, and Tammany-orchestrated vice protection, leading to over 100 indictments and the ouster of Superintendent Thomas Byrnes.[20] [21] Reforms followed, including the 1895 appointment of Theodore Roosevelt as president of the restructured Board of Police Commissioners, who enforced civil service exams, night patrols, and anti-corruption measures amid the force's growth to about 3,000 officers by 1900.[22] In 1898, the department consolidated with Brooklyn's police upon the creation of Greater New York, forming the modern NYPD framework while retaining vulnerabilities to political interference.[23]

Expansion and Reforms in the 20th Century

Following the 1898 consolidation of the five boroughs into Greater New York, the NYPD absorbed forces from Brooklyn, Queens, Staten Island, and other municipal agencies, expanding its jurisdiction to cover over 360 square miles and a population exceeding 3 million by 1900. This integration necessitated organizational growth, including the standardization of ranks and procedures across former independent departments.[11] To address specific threats, the department established specialized units in the early 20th century, such as the Bomb Squad in 1905 to counter extortion bombings by Italian "Black Hand" gangs using improvised explosive devices. Additional formations included enhanced vice enforcement squads during Prohibition (1920â€“1933), which targeted illegal alcohol operations but also fueled opportunities for graft. Harbor patrols and aviation units emerged by the 1920s to manage waterfront crime and aerial surveillance, reflecting the department's adaptation to urban industrialization and immigration-driven population surges.[24][25] Corruption intensified in the 1920s amid political patronage under Tammany Hall influence, with officers engaging in bribery, shakedowns, and protection of vice operations. The Seabury investigations, launched in 1930 by Judge Samuel Seabury under Governor Franklin D. Roosevelt's directive, exposed systemic graft in the NYPD, including magistrates' courts collusion and police tolerance of gambling and prostitution rings; these probes documented over 100 instances of departmental malfeasance and contributed to Mayor Jimmy Walker's resignation in 1932.[26][27][28] Mayor Fiorello La Guardia's administration (1934â€“1945) responded with aggressive reforms under Police Commissioner Lewis J. Valentine, appointed in 1934. Valentine, a career officer, dismantled corrupt networks by revoking "courtesy cards" that shielded criminals, reorganizing the detective division for merit-based promotions, dismissing over 400 officers for misconduct, and enforcing strict discipline, which reduced reported corruption incidents and elevated departmental efficiency during the Depression and World War II eras. His tenure emphasized apolitical enforcement, including crackdowns on organized crime figures, though wartime demands strained resources and prompted expanded surveillance of potential subversives.[29][30][31] Mid-century developments included civilianization efforts to alleviate sworn personnel burdens, such as the introduction of school crossing guards in the 1950s, which outsourced non-enforcement duties and allowed officers to focus on patrol and investigation. The department's overall size grew in tandem with New York City's population peaks, incorporating technological advances like radio-equipped patrol cars by the 1930s and formalized training academies.[23][32] By the late 1960s, corruption reemerged, exemplified by plainclothes officers' involvement in drug trade payoffs and gambling protections. Whistleblower Frank Serpico's 1970 disclosures prompted Mayor John Lindsay to form the Knapp Commission in 1970, which held public hearings revealing "grass-eaters" (passive corruptors) and "meat-eaters" (active predators) across precincts; the commission's 1972 report documented over 200 cases, leading to 20 indictments, the resignation of Commissioner Patrick Murphy's predecessors, and structural changes like mandatory integrity testing and an independent prosecutor for police misconduct. These measures aimed to institutionalize oversight but highlighted a persistent 20-year cycle of scandal, reform, and relapse driven by the department's scale and enforcement discretion.[33][34][35]

Modern Era: Crime Waves, Broken Windows, and Post-9/11 Changes (1970sâ€“Present)

In the 1970s, New York City faced a severe fiscal crisis that led to budget cuts and layoffs within the NYPD, contributing to a surge in crime rates. By 1975, the city was on the brink of bankruptcy, resulting in the reduction of police personnel from approximately 34,000 officers in 1975 to under 28,000 by 1977. Homicide rates escalated, reaching 1,691 murders in 1972 and climbing further amid economic decline and urban decay.[36] The 1980s crack cocaine epidemic exacerbated violence, with murders peaking at 2,245 in 1990.[36] The introduction of broken windows policing in the mid-1990s under Mayor Rudy Giuliani and Police Commissioner William Bratton marked a pivotal shift. Drawing from the 1982 theory by James Q. Wilson and George Kelling, which posited that addressing minor disorders prevents serious crime, the NYPD targeted quality-of-life offenses like fare evasion and public urination.[37] Bratton implemented CompStat in 1994, a data-driven system mapping crime hotspots and holding precinct commanders accountable through weekly meetings.[38] Misdemeanor arrests rose 70% over the decade, correlating with a sharp decline in overall crime.[39] Empirical analyses attribute much of the 1990s crime dropâ€”homicides falling to 633 by 2000â€”to these policing innovations, as New York City's reductions outpaced national trends despite similar economic recoveries elsewhere.[40] While factors like declining lead exposure and legalized abortion have been proposed, the timing and intensity of NYPD strategies, including increased felony arrests, provide stronger causal evidence for localized impacts.[41][40] Critics argue the drop was overstated or due to statistical artifacts, but precinct-level data show sustained violent crime reductions tied to proactive enforcement.[42] The September 11, 2001, attacks profoundly altered NYPD operations, with 23 officers killed in the World Trade Center collapse. Under Commissioner Ray Kelly from 2002, the department established the Counterterrorism Bureau and expanded the Intelligence Division, deploying detectives to 11 overseas posts by 2003 to gather foreign intelligence independently of federal agencies.[43][44] This shift emphasized preventive measures, including the creation of a Demographics Unit for monitoring potential threats, though it drew criticism for overreach into Muslim communities.[45] In the 2010s, stop-and-frisk practices peaked at over 685,000 stops in 2012 but declined amid legal challenges and policy shifts under Mayor Bill de Blasio.[37] The 2020 George Floyd protests spurred "defund the police" demands, leading to a $1 billion NYPD budget cut and mass retirements, with personnel dropping below 35,000.[46] Homicides surged 46% to 468 that year, reversing prior lows.[36] Under Mayor Eric Adams from 2022, renewed focus on enforcement reduced murders to 391 by 2023, though rates remained above pre-2020 levels.[36][47]

Organizational Structure

Leadership and Bureaus

The New York City Police Department is led by the Police Commissioner, a civilian appointed by the Mayor of New York City and responsible for directing all departmental operations and policy.[48] Jessica S. Tisch serves as the 48th Commissioner, sworn in on November 25, 2024, following her nomination by Mayor Eric Adams.[49] [50] Supporting the Commissioner are senior executive roles, including the First Deputy Commissioner, who assists in overall management and assumes command in the Commissioner's absence; currently held by Tania Kinsella.[48] The Chief of Department, the highest-ranking uniformed officer, oversees field operations and patrol forces; as of October 2025, Michael LiPetri holds this position on an interim basis.[51] [48] Additional key positions include the Chief of Staff, Ryan Merola, who coordinates internal administration, and bureau chiefs who manage specialized functions.[48] The NYPD's structure divides responsibilities across bureaus and major offices encompassing enforcement, investigation, and support activities.[52] The Patrol Services Bureau supervises uniformed patrol in precincts, transit districts, and housing developments, ensuring community policing and initial response to incidents.[52] The Detective Bureau investigates major crimes, conducts surveillance, and supports prosecutions through specialized squads for homicide, narcotics, and financial crimes.[53] Other prominent bureaus include the Crime Control Strategies Bureau, which addresses quality-of-life offenses and gang activity; the Counterterrorism Bureau, focused on intelligence gathering and threat prevention; and the Transportation Bureau, overseeing traffic enforcement and port security.[52] The Special Operations Bureau commands elite units like the Emergency Service Unit for hostage rescues and hazardous incidents, as well as the Strategic Response Group for civil disorder management.[52] Administrative bureaus, such as the Information Technology Bureau and Personnel Bureau, handle logistics, training, and resource allocation to sustain operational efficiency.[52] This bureau-based organization enables coordinated responses to New York City's diverse challenges, with over 36,000 officers distributed across these units as of 2025.[1]

Precincts, Patrol Boroughs, and Field Operations

The New York City Police Department's field operations are structured around 78 police precincts, which serve as the primary units for uniformed patrol, crime prevention, and emergency response across the city's five boroughs.[54] These precincts are grouped under eight patrol borough commandsâ€”Manhattan North, Manhattan South, Bronx, Brooklyn North, Brooklyn South, Queens North, Queens South, and Staten Islandâ€”each commanded by an assistant chief responsible for coordinating resources, implementing department strategies, and ensuring operational efficiency within their geographic area.[54] This hierarchical arrangement allows for localized policing while maintaining citywide oversight, with precinct commanders (typically captains or deputy inspectors) managing daily activities such as radio runs, foot and vehicle patrols, and initial investigations.[55] Patrol borough commands facilitate borough-level planning, including resource allocation during major events or crime surges, and integrate specialized units like traffic enforcement and community affairs into precinct operations.[55] For instance, Manhattan South covers densely populated areas including the Financial District and Midtown, encompassing precincts such as the 1st and 5th, while Brooklyn North oversees high-crime zones in areas like Harlem and Washington Heights through precincts including the 28th and 32nd.[56] Queens North and South commands split oversight of the borough's diverse neighborhoods, addressing variations in crime patterns from residential Flushing to commercial Jamaica, with a total of 13 precincts between them.[56] The Bronx and Staten Island commands, each with fewer precincts (seven and three, respectively), focus on tailored responses to local challenges, such as gang activity in the Bronx's 40th Precinct or rural-suburban patrols on Staten Island.[56] Field operations emphasize proactive policing through sector patrols, where uniformed officers are assigned to specific sub-areas within precincts for regular foot, bike, or vehicle beats to deter crime and respond to 911 calls.[57] Each precinct maintains a detective squad for follow-up investigations, but core field duties prioritize immediate public safety, including quality-of-life enforcement and traffic control, supported by the Chief of Patrol's directives from headquarters.[57] This structure has evolved to incorporate data-driven deployments, such as CompStat analytics, enabling borough commands to shift personnel based on real-time crime data rather than fixed assignments.[55] As of 2025, ongoing reforms include enhanced quality-of-life divisions in select precincts to address non-violent offenses like unauthorized vending, piloted in areas such as the 1st Precinct.[58]

Administrative and Support Units

The administrative and support units of the New York City Police Department (NYPD) comprise bureaus and offices dedicated to non-enforcement functions, enabling the sustainment and efficiency of the department's 36,000 uniformed officers and thousands of civilian staff. These units handle critical backend operations, including human resources, professional development, technological infrastructure, legal counsel, fiscal management, and employee welfare, thereby freeing frontline enforcement bureaus to focus on public safety. Led by deputy commissioners and chiefs appointed by the Police Commissioner, these entities operate from headquarters at One Police Plaza and specialized facilities across the city.[59][48] The Personnel Bureau, under Chief John Benoit, manages recruitment drives, applicant screening, promotions, assignments, and overall human resources for the NYPD workforce. It coordinates hiring for both sworn officers and civilian positions, emphasizing qualifications and diversity in a department that processes thousands of applications annually to maintain operational strength.[60][48] The Training Bureau, directed by Chief Martine N. Materasso, administers all departmental education and skill-building programs, primarily through the NYPD Police Academy in College Point, Queensâ€”a 32-acre facility equipped for academic instruction, physical conditioning, firearms training, and tactical simulations. Recruits undergo a six-month program covering law, police science, behavioral studies, and physical fitness, while in-service officers receive ongoing specialized refreshers to adapt to evolving threats and protocols.[61][48][62] Information technology support falls under the Information Technology Bureau, overseen by Deputy Commissioner Yisroel Hecht, which maintains the NYPD's digital infrastructure, including data analytics for crime mapping via CompStat, cybersecurity for sensitive records, and communication systems linking precincts and specialized units. This bureau ensures technological reliability amid the department's scale, supporting real-time decision-making and evidence management.[52][48] Legal and compliance functions are managed by the Office of the Deputy Commissioner of Legal Matters, led by Michael Gerber, which provides counsel on policy interpretation, litigation defense, and regulatory adherence, including interactions with federal and state authorities. Complementing this, the Deputy Commissioner of Trials, Rosemarie Maldonado, conducts internal disciplinary proceedings for officer misconduct, ensuring due process in a force subject to high public scrutiny.[48] Fiscal and logistical backing is provided by the Office of the Deputy Commissioner of Management and Budget, under Kristine Ryan, which allocates the NYPD's multi-billion-dollar annual budget across salaries, equipment, and facilities, while the Support Services Bureau, headed by Deputy Commissioner Steven Harte, coordinates procurement, fleet maintenance, and property disposition, including the handling of seized assets through the Property Clerk's office. Employee welfare is addressed by the Deputy Commissioner of Employee Relations, Lisa D. White, who liaises with unions and handles grievances, benefits, and morale initiatives for the rank-and-file. Medical services, supervised by Chief Surgeon Eli J. Kleinman, MD, MPH, encompass occupational health screenings, injury response, and wellness programs tailored to the physical demands of policing.[48] Public-facing administrative efforts include the Office of the Deputy Commissioner of Public Information, led by Delaney Kempner, which disseminates verified data on crime statistics, operations, and transparency initiatives, such as officer profiles and use-of-force reports, to foster accountability without compromising investigations. These units collectively underpin the NYPD's capacity to sustain long-term effectiveness in a high-volume urban environment.[48]

Personnel and Ranks

Rank Hierarchy and Insignia

The New York City Police Department maintains a paramilitary-style rank hierarchy, with the civilian Police Commissioner serving as the overall head, appointed by the Mayor and responsible for departmental policy and operations. The Commissioner is supported by civilian executives including the First Deputy Commissioner and various Deputy Commissioners overseeing specialized functions such as intelligence and counterterrorism. Uniformed leadership begins with the Chief of Department, the highest-ranking sworn officer who directs patrol, investigative, and support operations across the city's 77 precincts and specialized units.[48][63] Subordinate to the Chief of Department are Assistant Chiefs and Bureau Chiefs, who command major bureaus like Patrol Services or Detectives, typically holding three gold stars as collar insignia. Deputy Chiefs, marked by two gold stars, supervise borough-level operations or large divisions, such as patrol boroughs encompassing multiple precincts. Inspectors and Deputy Inspectors form the senior command level below chiefs, with Inspectors overseeing districts or task forces; these ranks feature eagle or star-based insignia on collars and shoulders. Captains command precincts or specialized units, denoted by gold captain bars (two vertical bars with curves).[64][48] Supervisory ranks include Lieutenants, who manage shifts or squads and wear a single gold bar on collars and sleeves, and Sergeants, who directly oversee patrol officers with three upward-pointing chevrons on sleeves. The foundational rank is Police Officer, with no specific collar insignia beyond the department shield badge; detectives retain this rank but receive investigative assignments after patrol experience, distinguished by gold-colored shields rather than the standard silver. Promotions to supervisory ranks require civil service exams, while higher commands involve discretionary appointments by the Commissioner.[65][64][55]

Recruitment, Training, and Demographics

The recruitment process for NYPD police officers begins with eligibility requirements, including U.S. citizenship or permanent residency, a high school diploma or equivalent, and being at least 17 years old to take the civil service examination, with appointment requiring attainment of 20 years and 6 months of age.[66] Candidates must pass a written exam, physical agility test, medical examination, psychological evaluation, and background investigation, which includes checks for criminal history and drug use.[67] In April 2025, Mayor Eric Adams announced expanded eligibility, allowing candidates with certain sealed or dismissed convictions to apply, aiming to broaden the applicant pool amid staffing shortages, with over 1,300 individuals advancing to assessment by that point.[68] Selected recruits undergo six months of training at the NYPD Police Academy in College Point, Queens, covering law, police science, behavioral science, and physical conditioning.[69] The curriculum emphasizes practical skills such as de-escalation, communication, safe tactics, and community policing principles, alongside physical requirements including sprinting to surmount a six-foot fence, climbing stairs with equipment, and dragging a 165-pound mannequin to simulate real-world scenarios.[62][69] The Training Bureau, overseeing the 32-acre campus, also provides in-service programs for ongoing professional development.[61] As of November 2024, the NYPD employed approximately 33,475 sworn officers.[70] Demographic breakdowns by race and ethnicity showed 39.7% White, 32.5% Hispanic or Latino, 16.5% Black or African American, with the remaining roughly 11.3% consisting of Asian, Native American, or other categories.[70] These figures reflect ongoing recruitment efforts to increase minority representation, though non-Hispanic Whites remain the plurality; gender data indicates a force predominantly male, consistent with national policing trends where females comprise about 12-15% of officers, though NYPD-specific recent breakdowns are not publicly detailed in aggregate beyond interactive dashboards.[2] The department's total sworn personnel hovered around 34,000 in 2025 summaries, down from historical peaks due to retirements and attrition outpacing hires.[71]

Officer Residence Policies and Diversity Initiatives

The New York City Police Department requires sworn officers to reside within one of the five boroughs for the first two years of employment, in accordance with Administrative Code sections 12-119 through 12-121 and Local Law 48 of 2009.[72] After this period, officers may relocate to designated New York State counties including Nassau, Suffolk, Westchester, Rockland, Orange, and Putnam, but residence outside these areas, such as in New Jersey, can result in disciplinary action.[73][74] This policy aims to foster familiarity with city communities while accommodating commuting from nearby suburbs, though the proportion of officers living within city limits has declined amid high housing costs and legislative debates.[74] Proposals to mandate full-time city residency, such as New York State Senate Bill S2984 in 2021 and S6196 in 2025, have advanced to encourage greater community investment but face opposition from police unions citing salary inadequacies relative to urban living expenses.[75][76][77] The NYPD's diversity initiatives, coordinated through the Office of Equity and Inclusion established to promote supplier diversity and equity strategies targeting marginalized communities, emphasize recruitment from underrepresented groups to reflect New York City's population.[78] These efforts include partnerships with religious institutions, community organizations, and targeted advertising campaigns, such as a 2021 drive featuring LGBTQ-identifying officers to expand applicant pools.[79][80] In 2021, the department launched a broader campaign explicitly aiming for diverse candidates ahead of officer exams.[81] Despite these measures, the NYPD faces persistent staffing challenges, with over 14,000 officers departing in five years as of 2025 and monthly net losses exceeding 300, prompting initiatives like the "Back the Blue" fundraiser to bolster hiring.[8][82] Hispanic officers have reached record representation levels by late 2024, comprising a plurality among promotions and reflecting recruitment gains in that demographic, while the overall sworn force numbers approximately 34,000.[70][71] Gender composition remains predominantly male, with initiatives continuing to prioritize female and minority enlistment amid broader critiques that such programs may prioritize demographic targets over merit-based selection, potentially impacting operational effectiveness.[79] Public data dashboards track personnel by race, gender, and rank, enabling transparency but highlighting disparities relative to city census figuresâ€”whites non-Hispanic at 32%, Blacks at 24%, Hispanics at 29%, and Asians at 14%â€”as of 2023.[2][83]

Operations and Services

Uniformed Patrol and Emergency Response

Uniformed patrol officers constitute the primary frontline of the New York City Police Department's operations, conducting preventive patrols and responding to routine and emergency calls for service across the city's 77 precincts.[84] These officers, numbering approximately 33,000 sworn personnel, perform duties in uniform, including vehicle, foot, and bicycle patrols within designated sectors to enforce laws, deter criminal activity, and engage in community interactions under neighborhood policing models. [57] Responsibilities encompass traffic control, quality-of-life enforcement, and initial assessments of incidents, with steady sector assignments ensuring consistent coverage in high-crime areas.[57] Emergency response begins with the NYPD's Communications Division dispatching uniformed units to 911 calls prioritized by severity, such as crimes in progress, medical emergencies, or public safety threats.[85] Officers arriving on scene secure perimeters, render first aid if trained, preserve evidence, and coordinate with specialized units like the Emergency Service Unit for high-risk situations involving barricades, rescues, or active threats.[86] The Patrol Guide mandates procedures for body-worn camera activation, arrest processing, and incident documentation during these responses to ensure accountability and evidentiary integrity.[86] Performance metrics reveal challenges in response efficiency, with average times for crimes in progress escalating to 15 minutes and 23 seconds in fiscal year 2024 (July 1, 2023, to June 30, 2024), marking the longest durations recorded in decades amid officer attrition and procedural demands.[87] [88] Earlier 2024 data showed peaks of 16 minutes and 12 seconds, correlating with reduced active-duty ranks below 35,000 and increased non-operational tasks like paperwork.[89] These delays have raised concerns over public safety, as empirical trends link slower responses to potential escalations in ongoing threats, though NYPD strategies like CompStat continue to allocate resources dynamically based on real-time crime data.[88]

Detective and Investigative Functions

The Detective Bureau of the New York City Police Department (NYPD) is responsible for the detection and investigation of major crimes, including homicides, robberies, burglaries, and narcotics offenses, complementing the uniformed patrol's initial response to incidents.[53][90] Its operations emphasize gathering evidence, identifying suspects, and building prosecutable cases through forensic analysis, witness interviews, and surveillance, often in coordination with the Queens District Attorney's Squad and other prosecutorial units.[90] The Bureau maintains specialized squads to address patterns in violent and property crimes, prioritizing high-impact offenses that threaten public safety.[53] Detective Borough Commands oversee investigations across the five boroughsâ€”Manhattan, Bronx, Brooklyn, Queens, and Staten Islandâ€”each featuring dedicated squads for specific crime types.[91] Homicide Squads, for instance, exclusively handle homicide investigations and first-degree assault cases involving serious injuries, conducting scene processing, suspect apprehension, and coordination with medical examiners for cause-of-death determinations.[53] Robbery and Burglary Squads analyze patterns in commercial and residential break-ins, as well as armed robberies, deploying undercover operations and pattern intelligence to disrupt serial offenders.[90] Narcotics Squads target drug distribution networks, including overdose-related investigations in high-risk areas like the Bronx, through surveillance, controlled buys, and collaboration with federal task forces.[90][92] Additional investigative functions include the Cold Case Squad, which reexamines unsolved homicides and sexual assaults where leads have been exhausted, utilizing advanced DNA analysis and renewed witness canvassing to resolve decades-old cases.[53] The Special Victims Division handles sex crimes, child exploitation, and human trafficking, maintaining borough-level squads for rapid response and long-term investigations.[93] Vice-related probes, such as prostitution rings tied to organized crime, fall under narcotics and specialized enforcement squads, focusing on dismantling networks rather than isolated incidents.[94] The Crime Scene Unit provides technical support citywide, processing evidence from major incidents using latent print examination, ballistics, and digital forensics.[90] As of early 2025, the Bureau operates with fewer than 5,000 detectives due to retirements and attrition, straining capacity for thorough follow-up on non-violent felonies like grand larceny auto while prioritizing violent crimes.[95] This shortage has prompted internal reallocations, with some patrol officers temporarily assigned to investigative duties, though core functions remain focused on evidence-driven case clearance to support convictions.[95]

Specialized and Transit Operations

The NYPD's specialized operations include elite units under the Special Operations Bureau, which deploys personnel with advanced training for tactical support, rescues, and high-risk incidents across the city.[96] These units handle scenarios such as hostage situations, hazardous materials responses, and counterterrorism operations, often collaborating with federal agencies like FEMA's Urban Search and Rescue NY Task Force 1.[96] The Emergency Service Unit (ESU), operational since 1930, functions as the department's sole SWAT team and primary rescue force, addressing barricaded suspects, jumpers, elevator entrapments, and weapons of mass destruction threats.[96] ESU members receive certification in SWAT tactics, SCUBA diving, and emergency medical response, enabling comprehensive handling of urban crises including building collapses and active shooter events.[97] The unit maintains approximately 500 officers equipped for rapid deployment citywide.[98] Supporting aerial and maritime needs, the Aviation Unit operates seven helicopters and one fixed-wing aircraft within a 60-mile radius of New York City for search-and-rescue, hoist operations, fire suppression, and tactical insertions like fast-roping.[96] [99] The Harbor Unit patrols waterways, conducts diver-led searches for critical infrastructure, escorts ferries during counterterrorism efforts, and responds to waterborne emergencies involving swimmers or vessels.[96] The Counterterrorism Bureau coordinates specialized prevention measures, including the Critical Response Command's daily deployments to infrastructure sites and the Bomb Squad's mitigation of suspicious devices using tools like total containment vessels.[43] It integrates intelligence from the Domain Awareness System, which processes real-time data from cameras and sensors, and partners with the FBI's Joint Terrorism Task Force.[43] Transit operations fall under the separate Transit Bureau, tasked with securing 5.6 million daily subway riders across 472 stations, 25 lines, and 250 miles of track.[100] Organized into 12 districts and three borough commands, the bureau employs task forces for anti-terrorism, vandalism suppression, canine detection, and MetroCard fraud investigations to maintain order in the world's busiest metro system.[100]

Equipment and Technology

Firearms and Less-Lethal Weapons

The New York City Police Department authorizes patrol officers to carry 9mm semi-automatic pistols as primary duty firearms, with the Glock 17 Generation 4 serving as the standard issue model, equipped with a 17-round detachable magazine.[101] Approved alternatives include the compact Glock 19 and the SIG Sauer P226 configured in double-action-only mode to mitigate accidental discharges.[102] [103] Since 2021, new recruits receive Glocks with lighter 5-pound trigger pullsâ€”down from the department's historical 12-pound standardâ€”to improve shot accuracy under stress, though veteran officers retain heavier triggers unless requalified.[104] [105] Specialized units, such as the Emergency Service Unit, deploy long guns including 9mm submachine guns like the Heckler & Koch MP5 and .223/5.56mm rifles such as the Colt M4 carbine for high-risk operations, while patrol shotguns like the Remington 870 remain available in precincts for barricade or vehicle pursuits.[101] Officers must qualify semi-annually at firearms training simulators emphasizing precision and de-escalation before lethal force.[106] Less-lethal weapons form a core of the NYPD's use-of-force continuum, prioritized over firearms per department policy requiring reasonable alternatives and de-escalation when feasible.[107] [108] Standard equipment includes oleoresin capsicum (OC) spray, deployed via Patrol Guide Procedure 212-95 against subjects actively resisting arrest or exhibiting aggressive aggression, with documented incidents tracked in annual use-of-force reports.[109] [110] Expandable friction-lock batons provide intermediate force for control or strikes, used in compliance with guidelines prohibiting punitive application.[111] Conducted electrical weapons (CEWs), such as Tasers, are authorized for select personnel and scenarios involving active threats, with policy restricting deployment to active resistance and prohibiting use for coercion; Surveillance, Data Analytics, and Innovations

The New York Police Department's Domain Awareness System (DAS), launched in partnership with Microsoft around 2010, integrates over 9,000 public and private security cameras, approximately 500 automated license plate readers (LPRs), and radiological sensors to generate real-time mapping of potential threats across the city.[124] [125] LPRs, deployed since at least 2008, capture vehicle license plates, timestamps, and locations, storing data for up to five years to aid investigations into vehicle-related crimes such as thefts and shootings.[126] [127] Access to DAS is restricted via password-protected interfaces, with mobile integration allowing patrol officers to query data from smartphones and tablets for on-scene decision-making.[124] Data analytics within DAS and related systems support predictive policing by analyzing historical crime patterns, weather, and temporal factors to forecast hotspots and optimize resource deployment.[128] [125] Tools like Patternizr, an AI-driven platform introduced around 2019, automate pattern recognition in burglary reports by processing unstructured data from complaints, yielding over 1,000 investigative leads in its initial evaluations.[129] These analytics extend CompStat principlesâ€”originally a crime-mapping tool since 1994â€”into CompStat 2.0, an interactive online platform providing granular, precinct-level statistics for proactive patrolling.[130] Empirical assessments indicate such data-driven allocations correlate with localized crime drops, as seen in targeted surges reducing assaults and burglaries by 5-10% in affected areas, though broader causal attribution remains debated due to confounding variables like economic factors.[131] Innovations include the full rollout of body-worn cameras (BWCs) to all uniformed patrol officers by March 6, 2019, following a pilot in April 2017 that equipped 1,300 officers across 20 commands; BWCs record interactions, with studies in NYPD housing units showing 17% fewer use-of-force incidents and reduced complaints post-deployment.[132] [133] The department has also integrated facial recognition capabilities, enabling matches against over 15,000 cameras in Manhattan, the Bronx, and Brooklyn as of 2021, primarily for identifying suspects in post-incident footage rather than real-time tracking.[134] Drone usage, initiated with 14 units purchased in 2014 for aerial surveillance equipped with 4K cameras, supports tactical operations but faces policy limits under the 2021 Public Oversight of Surveillance Technology (POST) Act, which mandates annual impact assessments for technologies like LPRs and BWCs to balance efficacy with privacy constraints.[135] These tools enhance evidentiary collection, with LPR data contributing to thousands of arrests annually, though critics from privacy advocacy groups question retention practices without corresponding transparency in hit rates or false positives.[126]

Effectiveness in Crime Control

Historical Crime Trends and Statistical Data

New York City crime rates reached their zenith in the early 1990s amid the crack cocaine epidemic, with recorded murders peaking at 2,262 in 1990, equivalent to a rate of approximately 30.7 per 100,000 residents.[47][39] This marked the highest annual homicide total in the city's modern history, reflecting broader violent and property crime surges; violent crime index offenses exceeded 200,000 annually in the late 1980s, while property crimes topped 500,000.[39] From 1990 to 1999, homicides declined by 73 percent, outpacing the national drop, with overall violent crime falling more than 56 percent in the city compared to 28 percent nationwide, and property crimes tumbling by about 65 percent.[39] The downward trajectory continued into the 2000s and 2010s, stabilizing at historic lows by the late 2010s. Murders fell below 500 annually by the mid-2000s and reached 319 in 2019, a rate of roughly 3.8 per 100,000.[47][136] Major index crimes, encompassing murder, rape, robbery, felony assault, burglary, grand larceny, and grand larceny of motor vehicles, decreased steadily; for instance, from 2000 to 2015, NYPD data show consistent year-over-year reductions in these categories, with total index crimes dropping from over 360,000 in 2000 to under 250,000 by 2015.[137] The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted this trend, with murders rising 47 percent to 468 in 2020â€”the largest single-year increase in decadesâ€”alongside a near-doubling of shootings (+97 percent).[136][138] Overall major crimes increased amid lockdowns and social disruptions, though levels remained far below 1990s peaks. Subsequent years saw partial recovery: murders declined to 438 in 2022 and 391 in 2023 (rate of 4.1 per 100,000), with projections for 2024 around 375.[47] Through mid-2025, murders averaged below one per day for the first time since pre-pandemic years, totaling 149 in the first half.[139][140]

Key Strategies: CompStat, Broken Windows, and Proactive Policing

CompStat, introduced by the New York City Police Department in 1994 under Commissioner William Bratton, is a data-driven management system that compiles weekly crime statistics from precincts, maps hotspots, and convenes regular accountability meetings for commanders to address trends and deploy resources.[38][4] The approach emphasized rapid information sharing, precise problem-solving, and performance metrics tied to crime reductions, coinciding with a sharp decline in major felonies during the 1990s, including a 56.3% drop in felony assaults and a 73.6% decrease in burglaries from 1990 to 2000 levels.[141] While NYPD officials attributed much of the success to CompStat's focus on accountability and targeted interventions, econometric analyses have found no statistically significant causal link between its implementation and the overall crime drop, suggesting it amplified but did not independently drive reductions beyond broader factors like increased police staffing.[42][41] The Broken Windows theory, articulated by James Q. Wilson and George Kelling in 1982 and operationalized in NYPD practice from 1994 onward, posited that visible signs of disorderâ€”such as graffiti, fare evasion, and petty offensesâ€”signal a lack of social control, inviting more serious crimes if unaddressed.[37] Under Mayor Rudy Giuliani and Bratton, the strategy shifted enforcement toward misdemeanor arrests, subway cleaning, and quality-of-life policing, which correlated with subsequent declines in violent crimes; for instance, a 10% rise in misdemeanor arrests was associated with 2.5-3.2% reductions in robberies and 1.8-2.9% drops in vehicle thefts.[39] Proponents, including Bratton, argued this created a causal deterrent effect by restoring order and disrupting escalation pathways, contributing to New York City's homicide rate falling from 2,245 in 1990 to 633 by 1998.[37] Critics, however, contend the approach's impact was overstated, with the 1990s crime plunge more attributable to waning crack epidemics, demographic shifts, and incarceration surges rather than disorder policing alone, as similar declines occurred in cities without such tactics.[41][142] Proactive policing in the NYPD encompassed targeted interventions like hot-spot patrols and stop-question-and-frisk (SQF), which intensified in the 1990s and peaked under Commissioner Raymond Kelly from 2002-2013, aiming to preempt crime through officer-initiated contacts in high-risk areas.[143] Hot-spot strategies, focusing resources on 40-45% of violent crime locations, demonstrated effectiveness in reducing incidents without displacing them elsewhere, per diffusion-of-benefits analyses.[144] SQF, applied as a hot-spot tool, yielded recoveries of illegal weapons and correlated with localized violent crime drops, though citywide efficacy was weaker and debated, with randomized evaluations showing modest preventive effects overshadowed by multiple causal influences on the era's trends.[145][146] Overall, these strategies' integration under CompStat's umbrella fostered a feedback loop of data-informed enforcement, but empirical consensus holds that while they likely contributed incrementallyâ€”via heightened deterrence and incapacitationâ€”the 1990s crime decline stemmed primarily from exogenous factors like a 35% police force expansion and crack market stabilization, rather than any single tactic.[39][41]

Post-2020 Challenges and Recent Reductions (2021â€“2025)

Following the 2020 protests against police brutality, the NYPD faced significant operational and personnel challenges, including a sharp decline in sworn officer numbers driven by heightened retirements, resignations, and recruitment shortfalls. Between 2020 and 2025, the department lost over 14,000 rank-and-file officers, reducing sworn personnel from approximately 36,000 to around 33,000 by mid-decade, amid widespread anti-police rhetoric Accompanying state-level reforms, including 2019 bail changes and 2020 criminal discovery laws, imposed new evidentiary burdens on officers, requiring rapid documentation and disclosure that uniformed attrition hitting 8.7% in 2024 versus 4.8% in 2018, as veteran detectives retired en masse and public scrutiny deterred applicants, leaving boroughs under-resourced for investigations and response.[147] These dynamics highlight causal pressures from reduced enforcement incentives and fiscal reallocations, with empirical data indicating that staffing shortfalls directly correlated with slower case processing and elevated risks in high-crime areas.[159]

Accountability, Misconduct, and Reforms

Historical Corruption Scandals and Internal Investigations

The New York City Police Department (NYPD) has experienced recurring cycles of corruption scandals dating back to its founding in 1845, with major investigations occurring approximately every 20 years, often triggered by media exposÃ©s and involving extortion, bribery, and protection rackets in vice-related activities until the 1980s, after which narcotics trafficking became prominent.[22] Early patterns included alliances between officers and criminal elements for systematic blackmail, as documented in 19th-century probes.[160] The Lexow Committee, a New York State Senate investigation formalized as a dedicated corruption-fighting entity post-Knapp and restructured after Mollen, investigates officer misconduct through tips, audits, and field inquiries, handling thousands of cases reforms, scandals persisted into the 1990s, including the "Dirty Thirty" in the 30th Precinct (1992â€“1995), where 13 officers faced charges for drug-related thefts and protection schemes, Use of Force Incidents and Empirical Context

The New York City Police Department authorizes the use of force only when necessary to effect an arrest, prevent escape, overcome resistance, or protect life and property, in accordance with New York Penal Law Article 35 and departmental guidelines that emphasize proportionality and de-escalation.[166] Empirical data from annual NYPD Use of Force Reports, compiled via the Threat, Resistance, and Injury (TRI) system implemented in 2016, document all reportable incidents, revealing that force occurs in a small fraction of encounters. In 2023, the NYPD recorded 9,777 use-of-force incidents amid 226,875 arrests, equating to approximately 3.4% of arrests involving any level of force; this follows a 2020 rate of 2.9% (4,071 incidents out of 140,411 arrests), with variations attributable to crime volume, resistance levels, and operational tempo rather than systemic excess.[166][112] Lethal force remains exceedingly rare. Firearms discharges totaled 48 in 2023 (down 22.6% from 62 in 2022), the third-lowest on record, typically occurring in adversarial conflicts where officers faced armed threats or active resistance; non-lethal options dominated, with 1,496 conducted electrical weapon (CEW) deployments (65.7% effective in gaining compliance).[166] Subject injury rates underscore restraint: among 12,860 subjects in 2023 incidents, 97.2% sustained no or minor injuries, with only 1.7% seriously hurt, often in high-threat scenarios like crimes in progress (the most common trigger, comprising 63.3% of arrest-related force).[166] Officers, conversely, faced elevated risks, with 5,383 injuries in 2023 (18.8% of involved personnel), including 347 substantial or serious cases, amid a broader surge in assaultsâ€”4,600 officer injuries from suspects in the first nine months of 2024 alone, the highest since tracking began, and a 63% rise in assaults over six years ending 2025.[166][167][168] Civilian complaints of excessive force, tracked by the Civilian Complaint Review Board (CCRB), rose post-2020 to 2,588 in 2023, reflecting heightened scrutiny amid national debates, yet substantiation rates remained low at 5.5% (143 cases), down 59.6% from 2022 and consistent with historical averages of 3-12%, indicating most allegations lack sufficient evidence upon investigation.[166][6] Independent empirical analyses, such as economist Roland Fryer's 2016 study of large urban departments including contextual controls for encounter dynamics, found no racial bias in police shootingsâ€”blacks and Hispanics were not more likely to be shot conditional on being armed or resistingâ€”though non-lethal force showed disparities attributable to higher rates of subject noncompliance rather than officer prejudice.[169] This aligns with NYPD data patterns, where force correlates strongly with resistance (e.g., 70% of 2020 incidents tied to arrests or emotionally disturbed persons exhibiting threat), underscoring causal links to situational necessities over discretionary abuse.[112] High-profile incidents, such as the 1999 Amadou Diallo shooting (41 rounds fired at an unarmed suspect misidentified as armed) or 2014 Eric Garner case (death from chokehold restraint during resistance to arrest), represent outliers amid thousands of justified applications, with internal reviews and courts often affirming legal bounds under Fourth Amendment reasonableness standards.[169]

Oversight Mechanisms, Reforms, and Empirical Outcomes

The primary civilian oversight body for the New York City Police Department is the Civilian Complaint Review Board (CCRB), an independent agency established in 1993 with subpoena power to investigate allegations of misconduct by uniformed officers, including use of force, abuse of authority, discourtesy, and offensive language. The CCRB operates separately from the NYPD and forwards substantiated cases to the police commissioner for discipline, though the department retains final decision-making authority. Complementing the CCRB is the Office of the Inspector General for the NYPD (OIG-NYPD), created in 2014 under the Department of Investigation, which conducts systemic audits, investigates corruption and high-level misconduct, and issues policy recommendations independent of NYPD command.[170] Internally, the NYPD's Internal Affairs Bureau handles some probes, but external entities like the CCRB and OIG-NYPD were designed to mitigate potential conflicts of interest in self-policing.[171] Key reforms have addressed historical corruption and constitutional violations. The 1994 Mollen Commission, investigating 1990s graft including gun trafficking and protection rackets, recommended decentralizing internal controls, enhancing recruitment screening, and improving anti-corruption training, leading to structural changes in the NYPD's integrity systems.[162] Following the 2013 federal ruling in Floyd v. City of New York, which deemed stop-and-frisk practices unconstitutional due to racial bias and low yield (e.g., only 10% of stops yielding arrests or summonses), the NYPD implemented reforms including revised training, body-worn cameras for patrol officers by 2017, and a court-appointed monitor to oversee compliance.[172] Post-2020 George Floyd protests prompted additional measures, such as a 2021 memorandum strengthening the disciplinary matrix for substantiated complaints and expanded protest policing training, amid city council pushes for budget scrutiny though actual NYPD funding rose from $5.8 billion in 2020 to over $11 billion by 2025.[173][174] Empirical data on outcomes reveal mixed results, with increased transparency but persistent challenges in accountability. CCRB complaints surged 60% under Mayor Eric Adams (2022â€“2025), reaching the highest volume in a decade by 2024 (over 10,000 annually), yet substantiation rates for use-of-force allegations declined from 12% in 2021 to 5% in 2024, and overall force complaints substantiated at just 3% in sampled investigations.[175][176] Of substantiated CCRB cases forwarded for discipline, 38% were dismissed by the NYPD in 2024 without full review, totaling over 430 such closures that yearâ€”far exceeding prior yearsâ€”and only about two-thirds of recommended penalties were imposed historically.[177][178] Post-stop-and-frisk reforms, NYPD pedestrian stops dropped 96% from 685,000 in 2011 to under 20,000 by 2019, with no corresponding rise in felonies or violent misdemeanors; murders fell to 291 in 2017, the lowest since the 1950s, though causal attribution remains debated amid ongoing CompStat-driven strategies.[179][180] These patterns suggest oversight mechanisms have amplified reporting but yielded low misconduct validation rates, potentially indicating robust evidence thresholds or under-detection, while reforms correlated with sustained crime declines rather than spikes.[181]

Officer Risks and Line of Duty Incidents

Fatalities and Injuries in Service

The New York City Police Department has endured substantial line-of-duty fatalities, predominantly from felonious assaults involving gunfire, vehicular pursuits and crashes, and exposure-related illnesses. During the twentieth century, 585 officers died participating in law enforcement duties, with 290 attributed to intentional gunshot wounds and motor vehicle incidents comprising another major category.[182] The September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks resulted in the immediate deaths of 23 NYPD officers responding at the World Trade Center site, while hundreds more have since perished from associated illnesses such as respiratory diseases and cancers due to toxic exposure, with these cases officially classified as line-of-duty deaths.[183][184] Post-2020 fatalities underscore persistent threats from armed confrontations and impaired drivers. Detectives Jason Rivera and Wilbert Mora were killed by gunfire in an ambush on January 25, 2022, during a response to a domestic violence call in Harlem. Officer Anastasios Tsakos died on January 27, 2021, after being struck by a drunk driver with a suspended license while directing traffic following a crash in Queens.[185] Detective Jonathan E. Diller was fatally shot on March 25, 2024, by a repeat offender during an effort to enforce a parking violation in Queens.[186] Officer Didarul Islam, 36, was killed by gunfire on July 29, 2025, while working a paid security detail at a Midtown Manhattan business.[187] Injuries to officers, largely from suspect assaults, have escalated amid elevated urban violence. The department recorded 4,600 such injuries in the first nine months of 2024 alone, surpassing prior highs since tracking commenced in 2004.[167][188] Assaults on officers rose 63 percent from 2019 levels, with 970 incidents reported in the first five months of 2025.[168] These patterns correlate with recidivism among assailants, many of whom faced prior convictions for violent offenses.[167]

The New York City Police Department (NYPD) maintains several internal and affiliated programs to address officer mental health, stress, and family welfare, recognizing the high-risk nature of policing that contributes to elevated rates of post-traumatic stress and suicide among personnel. The Employee Assistance Unit (EAU), operational 24 hours a day, provides confidential peer counseling services to members of service (MOS) for issues including burnout, critical incidents, suicide prevention, and postpartum depression, accessible via phone at 646-610-6730 or in-person at 90 Church Street.[189] [190] The unit's pamphlets and outreach emphasize proactive support, with dedicated staff including peer counselors and therapy dogs to mitigate job-related stressors.[191] Complementing internal efforts, the Police Organization Providing Peer Assistance (POPPA), an independent nonprofit supported by the NYPD, offers anonymous, volunteer-driven peer support for active and retired officers and their families dealing with law enforcement-related trauma, operating continuously since its inception post-9/11.[192] [193] POPPA's services include crisis intervention and the S.A.F.E.R. Answer the Call, a nonprofit, disburses financial assistance and annual stipends to approximately 500 families of slain NYPD officers and firefighters, raising $5.4 million in a 2025 event to sustain these efforts.[197] [198] The NYPD Honor Legion offers targeted support to surviving dependents of line-of-duty fatalities, while specialized funds like the Detectives' Endowment Association Widows' and Children's Fund provide scholarships and direct aid to detectives' kin.[199] [200] These mechanisms collectively aim to reduce attrition and enhance retention by addressing the causal links between unmitigated trauma and impaired performance, though empirical evaluations of long-term efficacy remain limited.

Unions, Affiliations, and Interagency Cooperation

The New York City Police Department (NYPD) is represented by multiple labor unions structured primarily by rank, which negotiate contracts, provide legal defense, and advocate for members' interests. The Patrolmen's Benevolent Association (PBA) serves as the primary union for patrol officers, encompassing over 24,000 members and recognized as the largest municipal police union globally.[212] The Sergeants Benevolent Association (SBA) represents sergeants, focusing on supervisory roles and contributing to operational successes in crime reduction.[213] Additional unions include the Detectives' Endowment Association for investigative personnel, the Lieutenants Benevolent Association for mid-level supervisors, the Captains Endowment Association for command staff, and specialized groups such as the Traffic Squad Benevolent Association.[214] These organizations have collectively endorsed political candidates, such as in the 2025 New York City mayoral race where 13 law enforcement unions, including the PBA and SBA, supported incumbent Eric Adams.[215] The NYPD maintains affiliations with numerous fraternal organizations that foster camaraderie, professional development, and representation for members from diverse backgrounds. Interagency cooperation forms a cornerstone of NYPD operations, particularly through joint task forces with federal, state, and local partners to address transnational threats.
