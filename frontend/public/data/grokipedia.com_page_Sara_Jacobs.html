Sara Jacobs

Sara Josephine Jacobs (born February 1, 1989) is an American politician and member of the Democratic Party who has represented California's 51st congressional district in the United States House of Representatives since 2021.[1][2] A third-generation resident of San Diego, Jacobs graduated from Torrey Pines High School and earned a Bachelor of Arts in political science and a Master of Arts in international affairs from Columbia University.[3] Prior to her election, she worked in international development, advising on policy for organizations focused on conflict prevention and women's rights, and founded a nonprofit to support emerging leaders.[4] Elected in 2020 after a competitive primary where family wealth from her grandfather, Qualcomm co-founder Irwin M. Jacobs, played a significant role in campaign funding, Jacobs has prioritized legislative efforts on reproductive health privacy and global humanitarian issues.[5][6] She introduced the My Body, My Data Act to safeguard sensitive health data from misuse by tech companies and serves as founding co-chair of the bipartisan Protection of Civilians in Conflict Caucus, advocating for policies to reduce civilian harm in wars.[7] As one of the youngest members of California's congressional delegation, her tenure has included advocacy for abortion rights, leading to her arrest during a 2022 protest outside the Supreme Court, and positions on foreign aid that have drawn scrutiny from pro-Israel groups regarding U.S. support amid the Israel-Hamas conflict.[8][9]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Sara Jacobs was born on February 1, 1989, in Del Mar, California, and raised in the San Diego area as part of a third-generation San Diegan family.[6] [10] She is the daughter of philanthropist Gary E. Jacobs, a businessman who founded the Gary and Jerri-Ann Jacobs High Tech High Charter School and serves on various nonprofit boards, and Jerri-Ann Jacobs.[6] [11] Her parents established the Jacobs International Teen Leadership Institute in 2000, a program focused on youth leadership development affiliated with the Jewish Federation of San Diego.[12] Jacobs' paternal grandfather, Irwin M. Jacobs, co-founded Qualcomm in 1985, building substantial family wealth estimated at over $1 billion for Irwin and his wife Joan by 2020.[6] [5] Her uncle, Paul E. Jacobs, served as Qualcomm's CEO and chairman from 2005 to 2018.[6] The family has resided in Del Mar, where her parents continue to live, and has engaged in extensive philanthropy, including support for Jewish causes and education in San Diego.[11] [13] During her upbringing, Jacobs attended public schools in the San Diego region, graduating from Torrey Pines High School.[10] The family's Democratic affiliations and donor history, including millions in political contributions, reflect their influence in local and national politics, though Jacobs has described her early life as grounded in community involvement rather than overt privilege.[5] [14]

Academic Achievements

Sara Jacobs attended Torrey Pines High School in San Diego, California, graduating prior to pursuing higher education.[3] She enrolled at Columbia University, earning a Bachelor of Arts degree in political science in 2011.[15] [6] Jacobs continued her studies at Columbia University's School of International and Public Affairs, completing a Master of International Affairs degree with a focus on international security policy and conflict resolution in 2012.[16] [4] No public records indicate additional academic honors, such as departmental awards or membership in honor societies, associated with her undergraduate or graduate work.[16]

Pre-Congressional Career

International Policy Roles

Prior to entering Congress, Sara Jacobs held several policy positions focused on international conflict resolution, humanitarian aid, and stabilization efforts. Following her Master's degree in International Affairs from Columbia University, she worked at the United Nations Department of Peacekeeping Operations.[17] In this role, Jacobs contributed to efforts analyzing global conflicts and supporting peacekeeping initiatives.[18] Subsequently, Jacobs served for eight months at UNICEF, where she engaged in policy work related to child welfare and international development in conflict-affected regions.[19] She then joined the U.S. State Department during the Obama administration as a policy officer in the Bureau of Conflict and Stabilization Operations, focusing on strategies to prevent and mitigate violent extremism and state fragility, particularly in areas like the Sahel region of Africa.[20] In this capacity, her responsibilities included developing operational plans for stabilization and advising on U.S. foreign policy responses to emerging threats.[20] In November 2015, Jacobs transitioned to a foreign policy advisory role on Hillary Clinton's 2016 presidential campaign, where she provided expertise on international security and development issues until the campaign's conclusion.[21] These experiences informed her emphasis on evidence-based approaches to foreign policy, drawing from firsthand engagement with multilateral institutions and U.S. government operations.[15]

Domestic Advocacy Work

Prior to her election to Congress, Jacobs founded San Diego for Every Child: The Coalition to End Child Poverty in fall 2018, serving as its founding chair.[16] [22] The coalition comprised community organizations, policymakers, and leaders aimed at reducing the experience of child poverty in San Diego County by 50% by 2030 through coordinated policy advocacy, program implementation, and data-driven initiatives.[22] [3] The organization's efforts focused on addressing root causes such as housing instability, food insecurity, and lack of access to quality education and healthcare, emphasizing cross-sector collaboration to influence local and state policies.[23] Jacobs led the coalition in building partnerships with entities like the Center on Policy Initiatives and Jewish Family Service to develop strategies for systemic change, including advocacy for expanded early childhood support and economic opportunity programs.[22] This work built on local data indicating that approximately 18% of San Diego children lived in poverty at the time, prioritizing evidence-based interventions over broader redistributive measures.[22] During her tenure as chair through 2020, the coalition advanced public awareness campaigns and policy recommendations, such as integrating poverty reduction into regional planning, though measurable outcomes were nascent prior to her congressional service.[24] This domestic initiative represented a shift from Jacobs's prior international focus, targeting localized inequities through coalition-driven reform rather than federal mandates.[3]

Entry into Elective Office

2020 Special Election and Primary

In the March 3, 2020, primary election for California's 53rd congressional district, held to succeed retiring Democratic incumbent Susan Davis after her announcement in January 2019 not to seek an eleventh term, fourteen candidates competed in the state's nonpartisan blanket primary system, where the top two vote-getters advanced regardless of party. The district, encompassing parts of San Diego including urban and military-heavy areas, attracted a predominantly Democratic field reflecting its D+19 Cook Partisan Voting Index. Sara Jacobs, a 31-year-old former policy advisor at the U.S. Department of State's Office to Monitor and Combat Trafficking in Persons and executive director of the San Diego-based Center on Policy Initiatives focused on poverty reduction, led the field with 58,312 votes, comprising 29.1% of the total.[25] Her campaign highlighted expertise in international human rights, economic inequality, and national security, bolstered by over $1.5 million in fundraising, including contributions linked to her grandfather, Qualcomm co-founder Irwin Jacobs. The contest occurred amid national Republican gains but saw Jacobs hold the district's Democratic base, with turnout reflecting broader presidential election dynamics. Congressional Tenure

Committee Assignments

Upon entering the 117th Congress in 2021, Representative Sara Jacobs was assigned to the House Committee on Foreign Affairs.[32] She also received an appointment to the House Democratic Steering and Policy Committee as the freshman representative for House Democrats, a role announced by Speaker Nancy Pelosi on January 15, 2021.[33] In the 118th Congress (2023â€“2025), Jacobs retained her seat on the Foreign Affairs Committee and gained assignment to the House Committee on Armed Services, where she served on the Subcommittees on Intelligence and Special Operations and Military Personnel.[34] On the Foreign Affairs Committee, she became ranking member of the Subcommittee on Africa, later expanded to the Subcommittee on Africa, Global Health, Global Human Rights, and International Organizations.[16] These assignments carried over into the 119th Congress (2025â€“2027), reflecting her focus on international relations, defense policy, and Democratic party strategy.[15][35] The Steering and Policy Committee membership provides input on legislative priorities and committee placements for Democrats, while her Armed Services roles involve oversight of military intelligence, special operations forces, and personnel issues.[32]

Caucus Memberships

Sara Jacobs serves as the Democratic Caucus Leadership Representative, a position she was elected to in December 2022, making her the youngest member of Democratic House leadership at age 33 for the 118th Congress.[36] In this role, she represents the caucus in meetings with the White House and coordinates messaging on key issues.[36] Jacobs is a member of the Congressional Progressive Caucus, which she joined on March 26, 2021, as her ninth caucus affiliation at the time; the group advocates for policies such as Medicare for All, of which she is an original cosponsor.[37] [38] Her caucus memberships also include bipartisan groups focused on specific policy areas:
  • Congressional Animal Protection Caucus: Addresses animal welfare issues through legislation and awareness efforts.[32]
  • Bipartisan Task Force for Combating Antisemitism: Works to condemn antisemitism and promote tolerance initiatives.[32]
  • Bipartisan Task Force on Artificial Intelligence: Examines AI's implications, with Jacobs contributing to its December 2024 report on governance, sectoral regulation, and innovation safeguards.[32] [39]
  • Congressional Future Caucus: Focuses on millennial and Gen Z policy priorities, where Jacobs has co-chaired efforts on state-level AI guidance.[40] [41]
  • Congressional Caucus on International Narcotics Control: Monitors global narcotics issues and related foreign policy.[1]
Jacobs participates in the Congressional Women's Caucus, supporting women's policy advancement.[42] She is also affiliated with the California Democratic Congressional Delegation, coordinating state-specific priorities.[32]

Key Legislative Initiatives

Jacobs has prioritized legislation addressing reproductive health data privacy amid concerns over post-Roe v. Wade surveillance risks. She introduced the My Body, My Data Act (H.R. 854) in May 2023 to prohibit the collection, retention, or transfer of personal reproductive or sexual health data without explicit consent, establishing federal standards for apps and entities handling such information. The bill was reintroduced in June 2025 with Senators Mazie Hirono and Ron Wyden as cosponsors, aiming to extend bodily autonomy protections to digital realms by limiting data sales and mandating breach notifications.[43] As of October 2025, the measure remains pending in committee, though it garnered endorsements from privacy advocates like the Electronic Frontier Foundation for safeguarding users from targeted enforcement.[44] In child care policy, Jacobs has co-sponsored the Child Care for Every Community Act (H.R. 953 in the 118th Congress), first introduced in February 2023 alongside Senator Elizabeth Warren and Representative Mikie Sherrill, to establish a federally supported network of community-based child care centers serving children from infancy through age five, with caps on family costs at 7% of income.[45] [46] Reintroduced as H.R. 5658 in September 2025, the bill proposes $200 billion in mandatory funding over a decade to expand access, particularly in underserved areas. Complementing this, Jacobs sponsored the Leveraging Estate Gains for America's Children and Youth (LEGACY) Act on July 10, 2025, which would reverse recent estate tax cuts to raise $400 billion over 10 years for child care subsidies, explicitly reducing her own potential inheritance to finance the effort.[47] Neither bill has advanced beyond introduction as of late 2025. Serving on the House Armed Services Committee, Jacobs has focused on military family welfare through amendments to annual National Defense Authorization Acts (NDAAs). In July 2022, she secured passage of five priority amendments in the FY2023 NDAA, including provisions for service member mental health support and housing improvements.[48] For the FY2026 NDAA, her July 2025 amendment expanded TRICARE coverage to include in vitro fertilization and other assisted reproductive technologies for active-duty personnel, veterans, and survivors, addressing fertility challenges from deployments and service-related injuries; it passed committee markup.[49] These efforts reflect her emphasis on quality-of-life enhancements for the 70,000 active-duty troops in her district. On international affairs, as a Foreign Affairs Committee member and Africa Subcommittee Ranking Member, Jacobs sponsored H.R. 4871, the Atrocity Prevention Act, in July 2023 to institutionalize U.S. interagency coordination against genocide and mass atrocities, building on prior executive orders with mandatory reporting and funding allocations. She also introduced H.R. 4422, the Global Respect Act, in June 2023 to counter foreign discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals by conditioning certain aid on human rights compliance. In February 2025, Jacobs filed legislation to prohibit the dissolution of the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID), preserving its $50 billion annual foreign aid role amid proposed administrative reforms.[50] These initiatives, while not yet enacted, align with her pre-congressional work in conflict prevention.

Political Positions

Economic and Fiscal Policy

Sara Jacobs has advocated for increasing taxes on high-income individuals and estates to fund social programs, emphasizing that the wealthy should pay a "fair share." In July 2025, she introduced the Leveraging Estate Gains for America's Children and Youth (LEGACY) Act, which proposes lowering the estate tax exemption threshold to $14 million for joint filersâ€”compared to the current $13.61 millionâ€”and directing 15 percent of additional revenue toward universal child care subsidies.[47] This measure would reduce her own potential inheritance, given her reported net worth exceeding $70 million inherited from family business interests.[51] Jacobs has publicly stated the need to "tax rich people MORE," criticizing tax cuts for the wealthy and corporations as benefiting figures like Elon Musk and Donald Trump at the expense of working families.[52] On federal spending, Jacobs prioritizes investments in infrastructure, health care, and child care over deficit reduction through cuts to entitlements. She voted for the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (H.R. 3684) in November 2021, which authorized $1.2 trillion in spending on roads, bridges, broadband, and water systems, securing over $44 billion for California projects including $24 million for San Diego International Airport improvements.[53] [7] She also supported the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, which included $437 billion in new spending offset by revenue measures such as a 15 percent corporate minimum tax and enhanced IRS enforcement targeting high earners, projecting a $300 billion deficit reduction over a decade according to Congressional Budget Office estimates.[54] Jacobs has opposed Republican budget proposals, such as the fiscal year 2026 reconciliation bill, arguing they would increase the deficit by $2.4 trillion while slashing Medicaid, SNAP, and education funding.[55] [56] Jacobs' fiscal approach aligns with progressive Democrats, favoring revenue increases from upper-income sources to expand domestic programs without corresponding spending offsets in mandatory programs, as evidenced by her support for waiving Pay-As-You-Go rules in the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021.[57] She has rejected corporate PAC contributions, positioning herself against undue influence from business interests in policy-making.[58]

Foreign Policy

Jacobs serves on the House Foreign Affairs Committee, where she holds the position of Ranking Member of the Subcommittee on Africa, and previously worked in policy roles at the United Nations, UNICEF, and the U.S. State Department during the Obama administration.[32][20] Her legislative efforts emphasize international diplomacy, humanitarian aid, and oversight of U.S. military engagements, reflecting a progressive approach that prioritizes constraints on arms transfers and support for multilateral solutions in conflict zones.[7] In the Middle East, Jacobs has advocated for conditioning U.S. military aid to Israel on improvements in Gaza's humanitarian conditions, co-introducing the Block the Bombs Act in June 2025 with Representatives Delia Ramirez, Pramila Jayapal, and Mark Pocan to restrict certain weapons sales amid the Israel-Hamas war.[59] She endorsed the January 2025 Israel-Hamas agreement for hostage releases and a ceasefire, describing it as a critical step toward ending the conflict.[60] Jacobs, the youngest Jewish member of Congress, boycotted Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's July 2024 address to a joint session of Congress, citing concerns over his government's policies following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks, the subsequent rise in antisemitism, and inadequate protections for Palestinian civilians.[61] On broader U.S. defense policy, Jacobs has repeatedly opposed annual National Defense Authorization Acts (NDAAs) lacking sufficient restrictions, voting against the FY2024 NDAA in July 2023 due to its inclusion of what she termed "far-right extreme priorities," the FY2026 NDAA in July 2025 for insufficient limits on the military's domestic law enforcement role, and earlier versions where funding increases exceeded her priorities despite securing amendments on issues like service member quality of life.[62][63][64] She has supported defensive systems like Iron Dome for protecting Israeli civilians while distinguishing them from offensive capabilities.[65] Regarding Africa, Jacobs co-led the reintroduction of a comprehensive Sudan bill in March 2025 with Foreign Affairs Ranking Member Gregory Meeks, aiming to address the ongoing civil war through sanctions on perpetrators, humanitarian support, and diplomatic pressure for peace.[66] Her subcommittee role has focused on countering instability, promoting democracy, and enhancing U.S. partnerships to mitigate influences from adversaries like Russia and China in the region.[7]

Healthcare Policy

Jacobs has advocated for expanding access to healthcare through single-payer systems, serving as an original co-sponsor of the Medicare for All Act introduced in March 2021, which aims to establish a universal health insurance program guaranteeing coverage for all U.S. residents without private insurance alternatives.[67][68] She continued as a co-sponsor in subsequent sessions, including the 119th Congress version (H.R.3069).[69] On prescription drug costs, Jacobs has supported measures allowing Medicare to negotiate prices, capping insulin at $35 per month for beneficiaries, and preventing excessive price hikes, criticizing Republican opposition to these provisions in 2022.[70] She voted against Republican budgets in July 2025 that proposed cuts to health programs, arguing they would reduce coverage for millions.[55] A significant portion of her legislative efforts focuses on reproductive healthcare access. Jacobs co-leads the Right to Contraception Act and the Secure Access for Essential Reproductive (SAFER) Health Act to codify contraceptive rights and protect interstate travel for abortion services.[7] She introduced the My Body, My Data Act in June 2025 to prohibit companies from collecting or disclosing reproductive health data without consent, targeting apps and services tracking menstrual cycles, contraception, or abortion-related information.[43] Additionally, she has prioritized fertility treatments, co-introducing the IVF for Military Families Act in April 2025 to expand in vitro fertilization coverage under TRICARE and the Veteran Families Health Services Act in September 2023 to broaden VA and DoD fertility counseling and treatments.[71][72] Jacobs has also addressed military healthcare delivery, introducing bipartisan legislation in May 2025 with Rep. Jen Kiggans to streamline credentialing for military providers, reducing administrative barriers to care.[73] She secured provisions in the 2023 National Defense Authorization Act to improve reproductive healthcare access for service members, including abortion and contraception without undue restrictions.[74]

Social and Cultural Issues

Jacobs has been a vocal advocate for expansive abortion rights, introducing the My Body, My Data Act in 2023 to prohibit the sale or transfer of reproductive health data without consent, motivated by concerns over post-Dobbs tracking of abortion seekers.[7] She co-led the Right to Contraception Act and the Secure Access for Essential Reproductive (SAFER) Health Act to codify access to contraceptives and protect interstate travel for abortions, respectively.[7] In media appearances, she has emphasized data privacy as critical to preventing "crimes" against women seeking abortions, positioning herself as a national figure on these issues amid state-level restrictions.[75] On LGBTQ matters, Jacobs voted for the Equality Act (H.R. 5) in 2021, which would amend civil rights laws to prohibit discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity in employment, housing, and public accommodations.[76] As co-chair of the Congressional Equality Caucus's Transgender Equality Task Force, she led 155 members in 2023 to oppose National Defense Authorization Act provisions restricting federal funding for transgender-related medical treatments, including those for military dependents.[77] She co-sponsored a 2025 resolution recognizing Transgender Day of Visibility and has defended medical procedures for gender transition, equating them to breast augmentation surgeries received by cisgender women during a 2025 House floor debate with Rep. Nancy Mace.[78][79] Jacobs supports expanded access to medical interventions for transgender youth, co-sponsoring Democratic legislation in 2025 to protect and broaden availability of such treatments amid potential federal restrictions.[80] This stance aligns with her opposition to bills like the Protect Children's Innocence Act, which seeks to criminalize hormone therapies and surgeries for minors with gender dysphoria, though she has not publicly detailed her vote on it.[81] Regarding gun violence as a public health issue, Jacobs has backed comprehensive restrictions, earning endorsement from Giffords Law Center and voting for the 2022 Protecting Our Kids Act package, which included enhanced background checks, raised the assault weapons purchase age to 21, and funded red-flag laws.[82][57] In criminal justice, Jacobs has co-sponsored the Global Criminal Justice Act and pledged to address "atrocities" in the U.S. system, such as over-incarceration, during her 2020 campaign, emphasizing support for victims of flawed policies.[1][83]

through substantial federal investments in emissions reductions and clean technologies, viewing coastal districts like hers in San Diego as particularly vulnerable to rising temperatures, droughts, and intensified wildfires.[85] In August 2022, Jacobs voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which allocated approximately $369 billion for climate and clean energy initiatives, including tax credits for electric vehicles, solar and wind projects, and carbon capture technologiesâ€”the largest such investment in U.S. history according to its proponents.[86] She opposed H.R. 1, the Limit, Save, Grow Act of 2023, arguing it would exacerbate the climate crisis by providing subsidies to the fossil fuel industry and increasing the federal deficit by $2.9 billion while undermining renewable energy development.[87] Jacobs has consistently voted against measures expanding oil and gas leasing or liquefied natural gas (LNG) exports, including opposition to roll call votes in 2024 that sought to weaken federal oversight on LNG permitting and reverse oil and gas leasing reforms.[88] Her legislative record includes support for the Wildfire Response and Drought Resiliency Act in July 2022, which established a National Wildland Fire Risk Reduction Program to mitigate fire-related losses through improved forest management and drought preparedness.[85] In fiscal year 2022 appropriations, she backed provisions funding clean energy research, grants for emissions reductions, enhanced environmental protections, and climate adaptation programs.[89] She has sponsored bills targeting air quality improvements, such as one directing the Environmental Protection Agency to provide grants to local agencies for implementing cleaner air space programs in high-pollution areas.[90] The League of Conservation Voters has awarded her a lifetime scorecard score of 99%, reflecting near-perfect alignment with their criteria for pro-environment votes across 118 tracked roll calls from 2021 to 2024, though the group's advocacy focus may emphasize restrictive energy policies over cost-benefit analyses of emissions reductions.[88] In October 2025, Jacobs joined California Democrats in criticizing the cancellation of $7.6 billion in Department of Energy grants for clean energy projects, calling it an unlawful setback to emissions goals.[91]

Controversies and Criticisms

Debates on Gender-Affirming Care

In September 2025, during House floor debate on the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), Representative Sara Jacobs clashed with Representative Nancy Mace over a provision to prohibit gender-affirming care for transgender minors in military families. Jacobs argued that procedures like breast augmentations, Botox injections, and rhinoplasties qualify as gender-affirming care, stating, "Lots of my colleagues have received gender-affirming care, and let me be clear, everybody should have access to the health care that they need."[79][92] Mace responded by calling Jacobs "disgusting" and accusing her of equating cosmetic procedures with treatments for minors, highlighting Republican concerns that such care, including puberty blockers and surgeries, lacks sufficient long-term evidence of safety and efficacy for children.[79][93] Jacobs has consistently opposed legislative restrictions on gender-affirming care, including for youth, framing bans as discriminatory and harmful to military recruitment and retention. In December 2024, she criticized the FY2025 NDAA for including a ban on such care for dependents of servicemembers under 18, estimating it would affect thousands of families and exacerbate provider shortages by limiting TRICARE coverage.[94][95] She joined 155 Democratic colleagues in September 2023 to urge removal of anti-LGBTQ+ provisions from the NDAA, including those targeting transgender healthcare access.[77] Critics, including Republican lawmakers and medical skeptics, have faulted Jacobs' stance for downplaying risks associated with interventions like puberty blockers, which multiple European reviewsâ€”such as the 2024 Cass Review in the UKâ€”have deemed to have weak evidentiary support for benefits in minors, with potential harms including infertility, reduced bone density, and elevated regret rates post-transition. Jacobs counters that denying care increases suicide risks among transgender youth, citing studies from advocacy groups, though these face scrutiny for methodological limitations like short-term follow-ups and selection bias in self-reported outcomes.[96] In May 2025, Jacobs defended access for minors during committee hearings, dismissing Republican attacks on transgender youth as politically motivated and ineffective for electoral gains, while co-sponsoring resolutions affirming transgender visibility and federal support for related policies.[97] Her advocacy aligns with Democratic efforts to protect such care amid state-level restrictions in 24 U.S. jurisdictions by mid-2025, but draws fire from opponents who argue it prioritizes ideology over precautionary principles, given desistance rates exceeding 80% in untreated gender-dysphoric youth per longitudinal studies.[98]

Stances on Israel-Palestine Conflict

Sara Jacobs, who is Jewish, unequivocally condemned Hamas's October 7, 2023, attacks on Israel, describing them as "horrific" and "unprovoked," which resulted in approximately 1,200 Israeli deaths and the taking of over 250 hostages.[99] On the one-year anniversary of the attacks in October 2024, she reiterated her focus on securing the release of the remaining hostages and pursuing long-term peace and security for both Israelis and Palestinians.[100] Jacobs has consistently expressed support for U.S. defensive aid to Israel, including funding for systems like Iron Dome, while emphasizing the need to prevent civilian harm in Gaza.[101] [102] In November 2023, Jacobs called for a bilateral ceasefire between Israel and Hamas to facilitate hostage releases and humanitarian aid deliveries, criticizing Israel's planned ground operations in southern Gaza as risking further civilian casualties without addressing underlying security threats.[103] She has advocated for an immediate and permanent ceasefire on multiple occasions, including in April 2024, to enable aid to Palestinians and hostage returns, while maintaining that Hamas must be held accountable.[9] In January 2025, following a hostage-release and ceasefire deal, Jacobs described the agreement as "bittersweet," noting her ongoing processing of the October 7 trauma amid broader Jewish community grief.[60] Jacobs has opposed unconditional U.S. offensive arms transfers to Israel amid reports of potential violations of international humanitarian law in Gaza. In May 2024, she voted against H.R. 8369, the Israel Security Assistance Support Act, which aimed to compel the delivery of previously paused weapons shipments, stating that U.S. arms must comply with legal standards regardless of recipient.[104] [105] In June 2025, she co-introduced legislation with Representatives Delia Ramirez, Pramila Jayapal, and Mark Pocan to withhold offensive weapons transfers to Israel pending compliance with U.S. and international law, aiming to reduce civilian deaths while preserving defensive support.[59] She supported a broader April 2024 aid package that included $26.4 billion for Israelâ€”primarily defensive aidâ€”alongside $9 billion in humanitarian assistance for Gaza.[101] Jacobs has been critical of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's government, opting not to attend his July 2024 address to Congress due to its perceived failure to prioritize hostage recovery and its role in rising antisemitism post-October 7.[61] At a July 2025 town hall, she affirmed withholding offensive weapons from Israel under current conditions but drew criticism from some attendees for not labeling the Gaza situation a "genocide."[106] Pro-Israel advocates have accused her of echoing Hamas narratives by emphasizing Israeli responsibility for civilian casualties and pushing aid conditions, though Jacobs maintains her positions balance Israel's security with humanitarian imperatives.[107] [9]

Opposition to Defense and State Department Bills

In July 2025, Representative Sara Jacobs voted against the Fiscal Year 2026 National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) during markup in the House Armed Services Committee, citing insufficient constraints on the military's potential involvement in domestic law enforcement activities.[63] She was one of only two Democrats on the committee to oppose the bill, which advanced despite her objections.[108] Jacobs similarly opposed the $886 billion NDAA for Fiscal Year 2024, rejecting it in committee due to what she described as inclusion of "far-right extreme priorities," including provisions restricting certain military policies on social issues.[62] In December 2024, she voted against the final House-passed NDAA, attributing her stance to "poison pills" added by Speaker Mike Johnson, such as a ban on funding for gender-affirming care for transgender service members.[95] Earlier, in 2022, Jacobs voted against the FY2022 NDAA after securing some priorities but opposing a nearly $37 billion funding increase over the president's request, arguing it prioritized excessive military expansion over other needs.[64] Regarding State Department matters, Jacobs voted against the State Department authorization bill in September 2025, criticizing it for endorsing a reorganization proposed by Senator Marco Rubio that she claimed would undermine diplomatic and development tools essential for U.S. foreign policy.[109] Her opposition highlighted concerns over reduced emphasis on non-military foreign engagement amid broader debates on federal spending priorities.[110]

Border and Immigration Policy Critiques

Jacobs has drawn criticism from Republican opponents and border security advocates for voting against multiple bills aimed at enhancing enforcement at the U.S.-Mexico border. In May 2023, she opposed H.R. 2, the Secure the Border Act, which proposed resuming physical barrier construction, increasing Border Patrol personnel by 10,000 agents, and limiting asylum claims by requiring migrants to apply in transit countries before reaching the U.S. Critics contended that her rejection of the measure, which passed the House on a party-line vote, reflected a reluctance to prioritize enforcement amid escalating illegal crossings, with U.S. Customs and Border Protection recording over 2.4 million nationwide encounters in fiscal year 2023, including significant activity in her San Diego district.[111] Similarly, in April 2024, Jacobs voted against a Republican initiative to reinstate Title 42 expulsions, the Migrant Protection Protocols (Remain in Mexico), and restrictions on asylum processing, measures previously credited with reducing crossings during the Trump administration. Opponents, including House Republicans, argued that her stance favored procedural reforms over immediate security, potentially prolonging a humanitarian and security crisis marked by fentanyl smuggling and unaccompanied minors overwhelming local resources in border communities like San Diego. During a October 2024 debate, her Republican challenger, El Cajon Mayor Bill Wells, highlighted nearly 20 million cumulative encounters under Democratic policies, accusing Jacobs of enabling threats to public safety by opposing stringent controls.[112][113] Jacobs has advocated for comprehensive immigration reform emphasizing pathways to citizenship for long-term undocumented residents, such as Dreamers and agricultural workers, while criticizing mass deportations and surveillance as counterproductive to safety. Conservative groups, reflected in her 0% score on Heritage Action's 117th Congress scorecardâ€”which evaluates votes on issues including border enforcementâ€”have faulted this approach for inverting priorities, allegedly subsidizing illegal immigration through taxpayer-funded services like the $650 million she helped secure in 2024 for FEMA's Shelter and Services Program targeted at migrants, rather than bolstering deterrence. Such positions, detractors claim, disregard causal links between lax policies and rising local burdens, including over 1,000 fentanyl overdose deaths in San Diego County in 2023 linked to cross-border trafficking.[114][57][7]

Personal Life and Public Image

Family and Fertility Decisions

Sara Jacobs, a third-generation San Diegan from a prominent philanthropic family, has publicly shared her decisions to prioritize fertility preservation amid her congressional career. In September 2021, during her first year in office, she underwent egg freezing to allow flexibility in planning future parenthood, a choice she made after deciding in her late 20s that she wanted time to build her professional life before starting a family.[115][116] In the summer of 2025, Jacobs pursued additional egg freezing, a process that entailed taking estrogen pills and self-administering hormone injections, followed by daily blood work and ultrasound monitoring, culminating in the retrieval of 11 eggs.[117] She has described this step as empowering and aimed at breaking stigmas around fertility treatments, emphasizing its personal and policy relevance.[118] These experiences have directly shaped Jacobs's advocacy for broader access to assisted reproductive technologies, including introducing the IVF for Military Families Act in 2025 to mandate TRICARE coverage for infertility diagnosis and treatments like IVF for active-duty service members and their spouses, addressing disparities where one in four military families reports infertility.[71][119] As of October 2025, Jacobs has no publicly reported children.[4]

Media and Public Engagements

Jacobs has frequently engaged with national and local media outlets to discuss legislative priorities, often appearing on cable news networks aligned with Democratic viewpoints. On MSNBC's The Sunday Show on July 17, 2023, she addressed Republican efforts to politicize the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) and its implications for the military.[120] She appeared on CNN on February 14, 2021, drawing from her prior State Department experience to comment on violent extremism and the January 6 Capitol riot, describing participants as "conflict entrepreneurs."[121] Additional MSNBC segments include discussions on November 2, 2021, urging passage of infrastructure and social spending bills; June 23, 2024, announcing her boycott of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's congressional address; and February 22, 2025, advocating public pressure as a counter to President Trump's policies.[122][123][124] Locally in San Diego, Jacobs has participated in interviews with outlets such as KPBS, NBC 7, and CBS 8, focusing on district-specific issues like government shutdowns and elections. A KPBS Q&A on January 11, 2023, covered the new Congress's startup amid House Speaker negotiations.[125] On NBC 7's Politically Speaking on October 15, 2025, she attributed prolonged House recesses during a shutdown to Republican dysfunction.[126] CBS 8 aired a full interview on October 16, 2024, where she outlined her reelection platform for California's 51st district.[127] She also joined CBS News' The Takeout on July 1, 2022, reacting to the Supreme Court's overturning of Roe v. Wade.[128] In public engagements, Jacobs regularly hosts constituent-facing events, including in-person, virtual, and telephone town halls to provide updates and field questions. Her official congressional calendar emphasizes transparency through such sessions, with examples including an in-person town hall announced via her House website for direct interaction on San Diego and Washington matters.[129][130] A virtual town hall on June 19, 2025, featured California Attorney General Rob Bonta addressing constituent queries.[131] Telephone town halls occurred on October 9, 2025, focusing on the government shutdown, and February 28, 2025, targeting opposition to Trump administration actions.[132][133] Collaborative events include a April 2, 2025, webinar with Rep. Juan Vargas on immigration rights and interactions with law enforcement.[134] Community-organized appearances, such as "The Town Hall We Deserve" hosted by Indivisible North County San Diego, underscore her outreach to progressive activist groups.[135] Jacobs maintains an active presence on C-SPAN, with 117 archived videos since her first appearance in a 2020 interview, covering House sessions, committee hearings, and policy briefings.[136] These engagements reflect a pattern of leveraging media and public forums to advance her positions on reproductive rights, foreign policy, and domestic spending, often critiquing Republican-led initiatives.
