Phyllis Gardner (clinical pharmacologist)

Phyllis Irene Gardner (July 7, 1950 – September 10, 2025) was an American clinical pharmacologist and professor of medicine at Stanford University School of Medicine, specializing in ion channels, signal transduction, and cellular pharmacology.[1][2][3] Gardner earned her MD from Harvard Medical School and joined the Stanford faculty in 1984 following postdoctoral fellowships in pharmacology at Columbia University and University College London, where she developed expertise in cardiac arrhythmias, cystic fibrosis pathogenesis, and gene therapy.[1][4][5] Her research contributions included studies on cell biology and pharmacological interventions, resulting in numerous publications and over 2,000 citations, alongside administrative roles such as Senior Associate Dean for Education at Stanford and vice president for research at Alza Corporation from 1994 to 1996.[6][7][1] Gardner gained prominence as an early and vocal skeptic of Theranos, the biotechnology company founded by her former Stanford student Elizabeth Holmes; in 2003, she dismissed Holmes's proposal for microfluidic blood testing from a finger prick as physically implausible due to issues like sample dilution and chemical detection limits, a judgment vindicated when Theranos was exposed as fraudulent in 2015, leading to Holmes's conviction for wire fraud.[8][1][4]

Early life and education

Family background and formative years

Phyllis Gardner was born on July 7, 1950, in Ames, Iowa.[1] Her father, Frank Gardner, taught agronomy at Iowa State University, an institution located in Ames.[1] Her mother, Opal (Van Winkle) Gardner, managed the family home.[1] Little is publicly documented regarding her siblings or specific childhood experiences beyond this academic familial setting.

Academic training and early career influences

Gardner received a Bachelor of Science degree in biology from the University of Illinois.[4] [7] She subsequently earned a Doctor of Medicine from Harvard Medical School.[4] [7] After completing medical school, Gardner undertook residency training in internal medicine at Massachusetts General Hospital from 1976 to 1978.[9] [4] This was followed by a chief residency at Stanford University Medical Center.[4] [7] These clinical training experiences provided foundational expertise in patient care and hospital administration, influencing her subsequent emphasis on integrating clinical pharmacology with practical medical applications.[10] Gardner then pursued research fellowships at Columbia University and University College London, including a postdoctoral fellowship at the latter in 1982, where she developed skills in advanced biophysical and pharmacological techniques.[7] These fellowships honed her focus on cellular mechanisms underlying drug action, bridging basic science with therapeutic development.[1] In 1984, following these postdoctoral positions, Gardner joined the Stanford University faculty as a professor of medicine, initiating her academic career with laboratory work in cell biology, ion-channel biophysics, and cardiac electrophysiology.[1] [10] This early research environment at Stanford reinforced her commitment to rigorous empirical validation in pharmacology, shaping her long-term skepticism toward unsubstantiated biomedical claims.[10]

Academic career

Appointment and roles at Stanford University

Phyllis Gardner joined the faculty of Stanford University School of Medicine in 1984 following postdoctoral fellowships at institutions including the National Institutes of Health.[1] Her initial appointment was in the Department of Medicine, with a focus on clinical pharmacology, where she conducted research on ion channels, signal transduction, and drug delivery systems. Over the subsequent years, she advanced through the academic ranks, earning tenure as an associate professor before being promoted to full professor of medicine effective February 1, 2006.[11] In addition to her professorial roles, Gardner held administrative positions emphasizing medical education and student affairs. She served as Senior Associate Dean for Education and Student Affairs, overseeing curriculum development, admissions processes, and support for medical students during a period of expansion in Stanford's MD program.[12] In this capacity, she advocated for integrating humanist perspectives into preclinical training to foster well-rounded physicians capable of addressing patient needs beyond technical expertise.[13] Gardner later transitioned from the deanship but retained her emerita status as professor, continuing to mentor trainees in pharmacology and related fields until her death in 2025.[4]

Administrative contributions to medical education

Gardner held the position of Senior Associate Dean for Education and Student Affairs at Stanford University School of Medicine, a role documented in the school's official bulletin for the 2000–2001 academic year.[14] In this administrative capacity, she managed oversight of the MD program's educational framework, including curriculum coordination and student support services, during a period when Stanford's medical education emphasized integrating basic science with clinical training.[15] A key focus of her tenure involved advocating for infrastructure improvements to address deficiencies in educational facilities. In 1999, amid discussions on the medical school's accreditation by the Liaison Committee on Medical Education, Gardner highlighted the urgent need for modernized libraries, classrooms, and information systems, describing the existing setup as inadequate for contemporary pedagogical demands.[16] Her public statements in faculty senate meetings and media underscored how outdated infrastructure hindered effective instruction and student preparation, contributing to broader institutional pushes for capital investments in learning environments.[17] Gardner also engaged in policy advocacy linking medical education to research translation. During her 2004 testimony before the U.S. House Subcommittee on Health, she detailed how federal funding sustains university-based training of future scientists and facilitates the dissemination of foundational knowledge through medical programs and continuing education, distinguishing academic approaches—focused on open inquiry—from industry priorities like product commercialization.[15] This perspective reinforced efforts to embed translational research principles into Stanford's curriculum, promoting curricula that prepare students for bridging bench science and clinical application.[15]

Scientific research

Focus on pharmacology and drug delivery

Gardner's research in clinical pharmacology emphasized ion channels and signal transduction pathways, which underpin many pharmacological interventions by modulating cellular excitability and therapeutic responses.[2] Her laboratory investigations explored ion channel regulation in immune cells, such as T and B lymphocytes, using electrophysiological techniques to elucidate mechanisms relevant to immunomodulatory drugs.[18] A significant focus involved advancing gene therapy delivery for cystic fibrosis (CF), targeting the defective CFTR chloride channel. Gardner co-led preclinical and clinical studies employing adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors to deliver functional CFTR genes via maxillary sinus administration, bypassing systemic barriers to localized epithelial targeting. In a 1998 Phase I/II trial, tgAAV-CF vectors were delivered intranasally to CF patients with chronic sinusitis, demonstrating safety and modest vector persistence without eliciting strong immune rejection.[19] A subsequent 2002 Phase II double-blind, placebo-controlled trial confirmed tolerability but limited efficacy in improving sinus pathology, highlighting challenges in vector tropism and expression duration for sustained pharmacological correction.[20] She co-authored a 1997 review outlining vector strategies, including AAV's non-integrative delivery advantages over adenoviral alternatives, to achieve therapeutic chloride transport restoration.[21] In industry, Gardner contributed to drug delivery innovations during a 1996 leave from Stanford to ALZA Corporation, serving as Principal Scientist, Vice President of Research, and Head of the ALZA Technology Institute. ALZA specialized in controlled-release systems, such as osmotic pumps and transdermal patches, enabling precise pharmacokinetics for agents like nicotine and contraceptives.[12] [10] Her expertise extended to implantable technologies, as evidenced by her 2006 review on microfabricated nanochannel devices, which analyzed silicon-based MEMS for zero-order drug release, addressing diffusion limitations and biocompatibility issues in long-term implantation.[22] These systems leverage nanoscale channels to regulate molecular flux, offering potential for chronic disease management but constrained by fabrication scalability and biofouling.[23] Later advisory roles, such as on Delpor's scientific board in 2018, applied her pharmacology insights to long-acting injectable depots for psychiatric therapeutics.[24]

Key experimental findings and methodologies

Gardner's experimental research centered on ion channel function and regulation, particularly chloride channels implicated in cystic fibrosis (CF), utilizing electrophysiological techniques to dissect signaling pathways. She employed whole-cell patch-clamp recordings to measure chloride currents in airway epithelial cells and lymphocytes, applying stimuli such as cAMP-dependent protein kinase, protein kinase C, and calcium ionophores to assess channel activation. Fluorometric analysis complemented these methods to monitor intracellular calcium dynamics and signal transduction events.[25] A pivotal finding from her 1989 study on transformed B-lymphocyte lines from CF patients revealed a defective regulation of a cAMP-activated chloride channel, mirroring epithelial defects but extending the pathology to non-epithelial cells; normal lymphocytes exhibited robust cAMP-stimulated chloride efflux, whereas CF cells showed impaired response, supporting lymphocytes as a model for CFTR-related dysfunction diagnosis and cloning efforts.[26] In parallel work, single-channel patch-clamp recordings identified multiple K+-selective currents in chick ciliary ganglion neurons, contributing to understanding repolarization and afterhyperpolarization mechanisms, with channels displaying distinct kinetics under voltage-clamp conditions.[27] Her 1991 investigations into airway epithelial cells demonstrated that multifunctional calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase (CaM kinase) activates calcium-dependent chloride channels in both normal and CF-derived cells, bypassing defective cAMP and protein kinase C pathways; intracellular perfusion of activated CaM kinase with ATP elicited chloride currents equivalent to those from calcium ionophores, while specific peptide inhibitors of CaM kinase or calmodulin abolished activation, but protein kinase C inhibitors did not, indicating a preserved alternative regulatory route with therapeutic implications for CF via calcium-mobilizing agents.[25] These experiments used primary cultures of human nasal and tracheal epithelia, highlighting methodological rigor in isolating pathway-specific defects through pharmacological and molecular interventions.[25]

Industry and entrepreneurial activities

Advisory roles in biotechnology ventures

Gardner served on the Scientific Advisory Board of Delpor, Inc., a biotechnology company developing long-acting injectable formulations for psychiatric and neurological disorders, with her appointment announced on January 2, 2018.[24] Her role involved providing guidance on drug delivery technologies, drawing from her pharmacology expertise.[28] She also joined the scientific advisory board of Pardigm, a biotechnology startup focused on cortisol measurement diagnostics for stress monitoring in athletes and high-performers, with her involvement noted in company profiles and announcements around 2021–2022.[29][30] In September 2022, Firebrick Pharma appointed Gardner to its Scientific Advisory Board to support the development of thermostable antiviral platforms, including vaccines and antibody therapies.[31] The board's formation emphasized expertise in immunology and biomanufacturing, areas aligned with her background in cellular biophysics and drug systems.[31] NAVAN Technologies, Inc., added Gardner to its Strategic Advisory Board on May 21, 2019, to advise on plant-derived therapeutics for cell therapy and agricultural biotechnology applications.[32] Her contributions leveraged over 40 years of experience spanning academia, medicine, and industry.[33] Additionally, in November 2021, HiDOHealth appointed her as an advisor to guide digital health innovations in collaboration with Stanford University, focusing on data-driven health solutions.[34] Gardner also reviewed grant applications for small cancer-oriented biotechnology businesses as part of the Cancer Prevention and Research Institute of Texas (CPRIT) evaluation panel, associated with Avestria Ventures.[4]

Board memberships and investment decisions

Phyllis Gardner served on the board of directors for Revance Therapeutics, Inc., joining in 2006 and contributing her expertise in pharmacology and corporate governance to the aesthetics and therapeutics company's development of neuromodulator products.[35] She also held directorships at CohBar, Inc., a clinical-stage biotechnology firm focused on mitochondrial-derived peptides for metabolic and fibrotic diseases, as noted in her profile with Avestria Ventures.[4] In March 2021, Gardner was appointed to the board of MiMedx Group, Inc., a company specializing in regenerative biomaterials for wound care and surgical applications, where she participated in governance amid shareholder activism, including supporting the board's nominees against activist challenges.[36][37] Gardner joined the board of Corium International, Inc., a transdermal drug delivery company, bringing her background in drug delivery systems to oversee advancements in pharmaceutical patches and devices.[38] Her board experience extended to multiple public and private biotechnology firms, informed by her prior role as vice president of research at Alza Corporation from 1994 to 1996, where she advanced controlled-release drug technologies before returning to academia.[39][1] As a co-founder of Genomics Collaborative, Inc. (later integrated into BioServe), SKOLAR MD (acquired by Wolters Kluwer), and APEX DX, LLC, Gardner actively shaped early-stage biotechnology ventures targeting genomics diagnostics and medical decision support tools, reflecting her hands-on approach to translating pharmacological research into commercial entities.[38] In investment capacities, she served as an adjunct partner at Essex Woodlands Healthcare Partners, a venture capital firm focused on healthcare innovations, leveraging her industry governance skills to evaluate and support biotech funding opportunities.[40] Gardner's decisions emphasized rigorous scientific validation, as evidenced by her avoidance of unproven technologies; for instance, she declined involvement with Theranos early on due to skepticism over its blood-testing claims lacking empirical backing, prioritizing ventures with feasible drug delivery and pharmacology foundations.[41]

Critique of Theranos and Elizabeth Holmes

Initial assessment of the technology

In 2002, Elizabeth Holmes, then a 19-year-old Stanford University sophomore, approached Phyllis Gardner, a professor of molecular pharmacology, with a proposal for a transdermal patch designed to detect infectious diseases through skin-sourced blood samples and simultaneously administer antibiotics upon identification of microbes.[8][42] Gardner immediately dismissed the concept as scientifically unviable, stating directly to Holmes, "Oh, that’s a lovely idea, but it won’t work."[42] Gardner's skepticism stemmed from fundamental pharmacological and physiological limitations: the patch could not extract sufficient blood volume from the skin to enable accurate analyte detection for diagnostic purposes, as meaningful testing requires larger sample quantities to achieve reliable sensitivity and specificity.[8][42] Additionally, antibiotic delivery via such a device was infeasible, as these drugs demand high systemic doses—often administered through large intravenous bags due to their relatively low potency and poor transdermal absorption profiles—which no passive or simple patch could provide without risking under-dosing and treatment failure.[8][42] She emphasized that Holmes' iterative "try it until you succeed" methodology, while tolerable in low-stakes software development, was "completely ridiculous in terms of healthcare," where diagnostic and therapeutic errors could directly endanger patients.[8] This early evaluation highlighted Gardner's reliance on established principles of drug pharmacokinetics and diagnostic assay requirements, predating Theranos' pivot to finger-prick blood testing devices like the Edison machine, which retained similar volume and accuracy challenges.[43][44] Despite Holmes' persistence in pursuing the venture, Gardner's assessment underscored the absence of foundational evidence supporting the technology's claims from inception.[44]

Persistent warnings and public advocacy

Gardner maintained her skepticism toward Theranos over the subsequent decade, repeatedly advising colleagues and contacts against trusting Holmes or investing in the company, citing the fundamental scientific implausibility of the claimed technology. For instance, she informed inventor Richard Fuisz of her distrust, stating, "I don’t trust her," and shared similar concerns with others in her professional network, forming what she described as a "little cabal" of early doubters.[8][45] These private warnings persisted despite Theranos's rising valuation, as Gardner emphasized the risks of inaccurate diagnostic results, such as erroneous clotting time measurements that could endanger patients by leading to improper anticoagulation dosing.[43] Her advocacy became more public in 2015 amid investigative reporting, when she provided insights to The Wall Street Journal's John Carreyrou, contributing to articles that highlighted Theranos's operational failures and reliance on conventional equipment rather than proprietary devices.[8][42] That year, she also opposed Holmes's nomination to the Harvard Medical School board of fellows during a meeting, voicing her long-held reservations about the founder's credibility.[42] Following the company's 2018 shutdown and Holmes's indictment, Gardner amplified her critiques in media appearances, including HBO's 2019 documentary The Inventor: Out for Blood in Silicon Valley, where she attended the premiere and condemned the board's lack of expertise as "malfeasance" by "old men whose brains go to their groin."[43][8] In subsequent interviews, Gardner connected with Theranos whistleblowers and advocated for legal accountability, expressing in 2019 that she sought Holmes's conviction, envisioning her in "an orange jumpsuit with a black turtleneck accent" and refusing to support any platform for the founder, such as a potential Stanford speaking engagement.[8][42] She attributed the scandal's longevity to hype over evidence, critiquing how Holmes's persistence and charisma overshadowed pharmacological realities like insufficient blood volumes for reliable testing.[43]

Vindication following the scandal's exposure

Following the October 2015 Wall Street Journal exposé by John Carreyrou detailing Theranos' inaccurate blood-testing claims and regulatory violations, Gardner's early critiques—dating back to her 2003 encounters with Holmes—were confirmed as the company's technology proved incapable of performing as advertised, leading to its dissolution by 2018.[43] Her assessment that Holmes' proposed methods violated fundamental principles of pharmacology and microfluidics aligned with subsequent findings that Theranos relied on misrepresented commercial analyzers rather than proprietary devices for most tests.[44] In post-scandal interviews, Gardner articulated the fulfillment of her warnings, noting in 2019 that Holmes was "paying a big price" for disregarding established science, as legal proceedings exposed the fraud that ensnared investors and patients.[44] She emphasized accountability, stating to Business Insider that same year, "I just want her convicted," reflecting her view that the case exemplified the perils of unchecked hype over empirical validation in biotech.[8] Gardner's prescience elevated her profile in coverage of the affair, positioning her as a key exemplar of scientific dissent amid Silicon Valley's innovation fervor.[43] Her insights informed broader narratives, including the 2019 HBO documentary The Inventor: Out for Blood in Silicon Valley, which highlighted her as an early skeptic whose pharmacological expertise foresaw the venture's collapse, reinforcing calls for rigorous peer scrutiny in disruptive health technologies.[46] Gardner reiterated this validation in 2022, declaring, "I was proven right," amid Holmes' fraud trial and conviction on charges including wire fraud, which affirmed the infeasibility she had flagged nearly two decades prior.[47]

Public commentary and media presence

Interviews on scientific skepticism

Phyllis Gardner expressed her commitment to scientific skepticism in multiple interviews, emphasizing the necessity of grounding biomedical claims in empirical evidence and physical realities rather than hype or charisma. In a March 2019 Business Insider interview, she detailed her early rejection of Elizabeth Holmes' 2002 proposal for a transdermal patch to detect infections and deliver antibiotics, noting that the required antibiotic doses far exceeded what skin permeation could achieve, rendering the concept practically unviable.[8] Gardner underscored the ethical imperative of caution, stating, "When you have people's lives at risk, you don't do that," and refused endorsement irrespective of the proposer's gender, prioritizing verifiable science over narrative appeal.[8] Appearing on The Times Tech Podcast in March 2019, Gardner critiqued the "fake it till you make it" ethos prevalent in Silicon Valley startups, arguing that medicine demands rigorous validation incompatible with unsubstantiated promises, particularly for diagnostic technologies like Theranos' purported finger-prick blood tests.[48] She highlighted how such skepticism, informed by pharmacological expertise, exposed flaws in claims of detecting trace analytes from micro-volumes of blood without established concentration methods, which violated principles of assay sensitivity and specificity.[48] In a contemporaneous Refinery29 interview, Gardner reiterated the implausibility of Holmes' initial ideas, recalling her direct advice: "Oh, that’s a lovely idea, but it won’t work," due to antibiotics' insufficient potency for non-invasive delivery, a limitation rooted in bioavailability kinetics.[42] She lamented the cultural tendency to idolize innovators without scrutiny, warning that overlooking scientific fundamentals in biotech fosters fraud over genuine progress.[42] Gardner's June 2019 Mercury News discussion further illustrated her skeptical framework, describing collaboration with fellow critics to challenge Theranos publicly by 2015 and invoking a professional maxim against excuses: "Excuses are like assholes: Everyone has one."[45] She advocated persistent truth-telling, even amid dismissal, as essential to upholding empirical standards against institutional inertia or celebrity-driven investment.[45] Across these outlets, Gardner modeled skepticism as a duty to interrogate causal mechanisms and data gaps, cautioning that unverified hype endangers patients and erodes trust in legitimate innovation.[8][42][48]

Broader views on pharmaceutical innovation and hype

Gardner emphasized the essential role of public-private partnerships in advancing pharmaceutical innovation, highlighting the Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 as a catalyst that enabled universities to patent and license federally funded inventions, fostering over 1,100 companies from NIH and university research.[49] She noted that biotechnology firms, investing $20.5 billion annually in R&D as of 2002, prioritize novel therapies derived from living organisms rather than chemical compounds typical of traditional pharmaceuticals, contrasting with the latter's focus on blockbuster drugs.[49] This approach, she argued, generates substantial economic benefits, including trillions in healthcare savings and a thriving Silicon Valley economy driven by innovations like the Cohen-Boyer patent.[49] However, Gardner cautioned against overpromising and hype in biotech ventures, particularly where patient safety is involved, stating that a "try it until you succeed" mentality is unacceptable in healthcare.[8] She underscored the lengthy and costly realities of drug development—spanning 6 to 10 years from lab to initial clinical evaluation and up to 15 years overall, with costs exceeding $800 million per approved product—requiring rigorous preclinical testing for efficacy and safety amid complex regulatory processes unmatched in other industries.[49] In her view, intellectual property protections and confidentiality are core to industry values, but they must not enable unvalidated claims that endanger lives, as unchecked media promotion can mislead stakeholders and investors.[8][49] Gardner's advocacy for empirical rigor in innovation extended to critiquing ventures that bypass foundational science, insisting on accountability to prevent harm from premature commercialization.[8] While supportive of federal funding for basic research, she stressed that translating discoveries demands private investment and disciplined processes, warning that hype often obscures these barriers and inflates expectations unrealistically.[49]

Awards, honors, and legacy

Professional recognitions

Gardner received the Judith Pool Award in 1998 from the Northern California Chapter of the Association for Women in Science, an honor bestowed on senior scientists with over ten years post-training experience who demonstrate excellence in research and mentoring.[50] She was awarded a five-year Career Development Award by the United States Department of Veterans Affairs to advance her investigations into ion channels, signal transduction, and related pharmacological mechanisms.[51] These recognitions underscore her contributions to clinical pharmacology and biophysics, though biographical accounts frequently reference additional unspecified national honors without detailing further specifics.[4][7]

Influence on empirical standards in biomedicine

Phyllis Gardner's critiques of unproven biomedical technologies, particularly Theranos, exemplified the application of fundamental pharmacological principles to assess feasibility, thereby reinforcing the need for empirical validation prior to commercialization. In rejecting Elizabeth Holmes' early 2002 proposal for a transdermal patch to detect infections and deliver antibiotics, Gardner argued that the required dosages exceeded what such a device could practically administer, highlighting dilution and delivery limitations inherent in micro-volume systems.[1] [8] This stance extended to Theranos' later blood-testing claims, where she emphasized that multiplexing hundreds of assays from minuscule samples violated basic biochemical realities, such as signal dilution and sensitivity loss, underscoring that innovations must align with established physics and chemistry before clinical deployment.[42] [8] Gardner's public advocacy stressed that patient safety demands rigorous, evidence-based scrutiny over speculative hype, influencing discourse on accountability in biomedicine. She repeatedly warned against deploying unvalidated diagnostics, stating, "When you have people's lives at risk, you don't do that," and criticized the tolerance for technologies lacking peer-reviewed data or independent verification.[8] Her persistence, including serving as a source for investigative reporting that exposed Theranos' shortcomings, contributed to heightened investor and regulatory caution toward biotech claims predicated on charisma rather than data, as evidenced by post-scandal reforms in startup due diligence.[1] [42] Through her role as a Stanford pharmacology professor and senior associate dean, Gardner modeled empirical skepticism, educating students and peers on prioritizing causal mechanisms and testable hypotheses over narrative-driven innovation. Her vindicated foresight in the 2015-2018 Theranos unraveling served as a cautionary case study, promoting a cultural shift toward demanding reproducible results and preclinical feasibility studies in the field, thereby elevating baseline standards against fraud and overpromising in diagnostic advancements.[1][8]

Death and posthumous impact

Phyllis Gardner died of a heart attack on September 10, 2025, at age 75, while vacationing on Martha's Vineyard, Massachusetts.[1] Following her death, academic and media accounts emphasized her prescience in identifying flaws in Theranos's technology, crediting her skepticism with contributing to broader scrutiny that culminated in Elizabeth Holmes's 2022 conviction for fraud.[1] Stanford University highlighted her as a professor of medicine in clinical pharmacology and an early skeptic of the blood-testing firm, underscoring her role in upholding empirical standards amid biotech hype.[52] These tributes reinforced her posthumous reputation as a proponent of rigorous, data-driven evaluation in clinical pharmacology, influencing ongoing discussions on the risks of unsubstantiated innovation claims in the field.[1]

Selected publications

Peer-reviewed journal articles

Gardner co-authored more than 75 peer-reviewed journal articles, with research spanning ion channel physiology, cystic fibrosis mutations, signal transduction, and implantable drug delivery technologies.[53] Her work often emphasized empirical mechanisms of cellular transport and pharmacological interventions, including early investigations into chloride channel regulation relevant to genetic disorders.[6] Notable publications include:
  • Kuno, M., & Gardner, P. (1987). Ion channels activated by inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate in plasma membrane of human T-lymphocytes. Nature, 326(6110), 301–304. This study identified IP3-gated channels in T-cell membranes, advancing understanding of lymphocyte signaling.[54]
  • Gardner, P., et al. (1989). A cAMP-regulated chloride channel in lymphocytes that is affected in cystic fibrosis. Science, 244(4901), 73–75. The paper demonstrated defective cAMP-activated chloride conductance in CF lymphocytes, linking it to the disease's pathophysiology.[26]
  • Wagner, J. A., et al. (1991). Activation of chloride channels in normal and cystic fibrosis airway epithelial cells by protein kinase C. Nature, 349(6312), 793–796. Co-authored work showing PKC stimulation of chloride secretion, contrasting with CFTR defects in cystic fibrosis epithelia.[25]
  • Duan, Y. J., et al. (1994). Volume-activated chloride current is not related to P-glycoprotein overexpression. Cancer Research, 54(19), 5029–5034. Findings dissociated volume-sensitive chloride currents from multidrug resistance protein expression in tumor cells.[55]
  • Schrijver, I., et al. (2005). Genotyping microarray for the detection of more than 200 CFTR mutations in medically diverse patients. Population Health Metrics, 3, 5. Developed a microarray assay for comprehensive CFTR mutation screening, improving diagnostic yield in heterogeneous populations.[56]
  • Schrijver, I., & Gardner, P. (2006). Advances in molecular genetics and mutation analysis. Genetics in Medicine, 8(5), 275–282. Reviewed progress in genetic testing for cystic fibrosis, highlighting mutation detection technologies.[57]
  • Gardner, P. (2006). Microfabricated nanochannel implantable drug delivery devices: an alternative paradigm for drug delivery. Journal of Controlled Release, 113(1), 1–2. Explored nanotechnology for sustained-release therapeutics, proposing silicon-based implants for precise dosing.[22]
These articles reflect Gardner's contributions to foundational pharmacology and translational diagnostics, often leveraging biophysical assays and genetic tools for causal insights into disease mechanisms.[51]

Conference presentations and reviews

Gardner delivered a keynote presentation titled "Like a Salmon Swimming Upstream" at the Stanford Medicine Alumni Association's Women in Medicine and Science event on December 12, 2019, discussing her career challenges and skepticism toward unproven biomedical claims.[58][59] She presented a state-of-the-art lecture on "Gene Therapy for Cystic Fibrosis" at the joint annual meeting of the American Society for Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics (ASCPT) and the American Society for Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics (ASPET) in San Diego, California, beginning March 5, 1997. Gardner contributed to discussions at the Gordon Research Conference on Ion Channels in Muscle and Other Excitable Membranes, held in 1986, focusing on ion channel signaling relevant to her research on lymphocyte activation.[60] Among her review articles, Gardner authored "Patch-Clamp Studies of Lymphocyte Activation" in the Annual Review of Immunology (volume 8, pages 231–252, 1990), synthesizing electrophysiological data on calcium-dependent signaling in T-lymphocytes. She co-authored "Toward Cystic Fibrosis Gene Therapy" in the Annual Review of Medicine (volume 48, pages 203–216, 1997), evaluating early vector-based approaches to CFTR gene delivery and their pharmacological implications.[21]
