Elle Reeve


Elle Reeve is an American journalist and correspondent for CNN based in New York, specializing in on-the-ground reporting of political extremism, fringe movements, and related social phenomena.[1] She previously served as a correspondent for Vice News, where her immersive coverage of events like the 2017 Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia—featuring direct interactions with participants—earned critical recognition, including an Emmy Award for Outstanding Coverage of a Breaking News Story and a Peabody Award.[2][3] Reeve holds a bachelor's degree in journalism and political science from the University of Missouri, obtained in 2005, and has contributed to outlets such as The New Republic and The Atlantic prior to her television roles.[3] Her work often involves embedding with controversial groups to document their activities and ideologies firsthand, though such approaches have drawn scrutiny for potential risks in amplifying fringe voices amid broader debates over media balance in extremism coverage.[4]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

Elspeth Reeve, professionally known as Elle Reeve, was born circa 1982.[5] She grew up in Atlanta, Georgia, spending the first half of her childhood in downtown Atlanta, where the majority of her neighbors and classmates were Black.[5][6] Later, her family relocated to a 1970s housing development off Highway 109, where a dispute over a neighbor's fence escalated into years of harassment and stalking against her family, beginning when Reeve was 13 years old.[7] Details about her parents' occupations, heritage, or siblings remain private and are not publicly documented in available sources.

Academic Training

Reeve earned a Bachelor of Journalism degree from the Missouri School of Journalism at the University of Missouri in 2005.[3][4] The Missouri School of Journalism, established in 1908 as the world's first journalism school, emphasizes practical training through student-run media outlets and internships, which aligned with Reeve's early career path in reporting. Following graduation, she interned at Time magazine, applying skills developed during her undergraduate studies.[8] No advanced degrees or further formal academic training are documented in her professional biographies.

Professional Career

Early Journalism Roles

Reeve began her professional journalism career shortly after earning a Bachelor of Journalism degree from the University of Missouri School of Journalism in 2005. She first interned at Time magazine, followed by a reporting role at the Center for Public Integrity, a nonprofit investigative journalism organization based in Washington, D.C.[9] In the early 2010s, Reeve transitioned to digital media, serving as politics editor for The Wire, a politics and media-focused site operated by The Atlantic. In this capacity, she oversaw coverage of political campaigns, policy debates, and media trends, contributing bylined articles on topics such as Mitt Romney's business record at Bain Capital in 2012 and the personal dimensions of political figures' family lives.[4][10][11][12] Her work during this period emphasized data-driven analysis and cultural intersections with politics; for instance, in January 2011, she reported on OkCupid's internal data revealing counterintuitive patterns in online dating preferences, highlighting how physical attractiveness influenced match rates. Reeve also freelanced for The Atlantic proper, producing pieces on election dynamics and social phenomena that demonstrated her focus on empirical trends over narrative speculation.[13]

Vice News Period (2014–2019)

Elle Reeve joined Vice Media as a reporter around 2015, initially covering the emerging alt-right movement, a loose network of online communities promoting white nationalist ideas under the guise of internet memes and irony.[14] Her work emphasized immersive, on-the-ground reporting typical of Vice's style, which prioritized access to fringe groups over traditional detachment, though critics have noted this approach sometimes amplified sensational elements at the expense of broader context.[4] In June 2016, Reeve became a correspondent for Vice News Tonight on HBO, the network's Emmy-winning weekly newscast that launched that year and focused on underreported global stories through short-form documentaries.[3] [15] As technology correspondent, she produced segments on digital surveillance, such as China's use of facial recognition and social credit systems to monitor citizens, highlighting state control over personal data in authoritarian contexts.[16] Her reporting often delved into how online platforms enabled radicalization, including coverage of subcultures like incels and Gamergate participants, where she examined causal links between anonymous forums and real-world behaviors without endorsing platform self-regulation narratives prevalent in left-leaning media.[17] Reeve's Vice tenure ended in September 2019 when she departed for CNN, amid Vice Media's internal challenges including layoffs and shifts in HBO partnership dynamics.[18] [3] During her time, Vice News, while praised for visual innovation and access to hard-to-reach subjects, faced scrutiny for ideological tilt—systemically aligning with progressive critiques of conservatism—which influenced story selection toward narratives framing right-wing extremism as uniquely threatening, often downplaying parallel leftist radicalism.[19] Her contributions helped establish Vice's reputation for raw footage over polished analysis, contributing to multiple awards for the program, though individual segments varied in empirical rigor.[20]

CNN Correspondent Role (2019–Present)

Elle Reeve joined CNN as a correspondent in September 2019, based in New York, after serving as a correspondent for HBO's Vice News Tonight.[18][3] In this role, she focuses on on-the-ground reporting related to political extremism, online radicalization, and domestic movements, often embedding with subjects to document their perspectives and activities.[1][21] Reeve's CNN assignments have included coverage of the January 6, 2021, U.S. Capitol events, where she reported firsthand on the manifestation of online extremism in physical protests, noting the presence of groups and ideologies she had tracked digitally for years.[21][14] She has also documented supporter gatherings for former President Donald Trump, such as a September 2024 MAGA boat parade in Panama City, Florida, interviewing participants on economic concerns and election-related claims.[22] Additional reporting encompasses campus protests amid the Israel-Hamas conflict, where Reeve visited universities to speak with students and administrators during peak unrest in 2024.[23] She collaborates on broader investigations into misinformation and extremist recruitment tactics, including joint efforts with colleagues like Donie O'Sullivan to analyze how internet subcultures influence real-world politics.[24] Reeve's approach emphasizes direct access to fringe communities, continuing her prior Vice work but within CNN's platforms for television, digital, and multimedia output.[25]

Notable Reporting Assignments

Reeve's most prominent assignment came in August 2017, when she embedded with white nationalist organizers and participants during the Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia, for HBO's Vice News Tonight. Her 22-minute segment, "Charlottesville: Race and Terror," featured direct interviews with rally leaders including Christopher Cantwell, David Duke, and Robert Ray, capturing the groups' internal dynamics and chants such as "Jews will not replace us" from the participants' vantage point.[26] [27] The reporting drew widespread attention for its immersive access, though it later faced scrutiny over the portrayal of events amid the rally's violence, which resulted in one death and multiple injuries.[18] Transitioning to CNN in 2019, Reeve covered the January 6, 2021, breach of the U.S. Capitol, where she observed thousands of protesters storming the building in what she described as the real-world spillover of online extremism she had tracked for years.[21] Her on-site reporting highlighted connections between digital rhetoric from far-right forums and the physical actions, including clashes with police and the disruption of electoral certification.[28] Other significant CNN assignments include her 2024 examination of Trump supporters at a MAGA boat parade in Panama City, Florida, where she questioned participants on economic grievances and election claims amid a contest for the "most patriotic" vessel.[29] She has also reported on groups like Moms for Liberty regarding school book bans and parental rights activism, as well as broader extremism topics such as white nationalist recruitment tactics and misinformation spread.[30] [31]

Publications and Authorship

Selected Articles and Contributions

Reeve contributed to Vice with the article "Meet the YouTube star who's de-radicalizing young, right-wing men," published on March 14, 2019, which profiled transgender YouTuber Natalie Wynn (ContraPoints) and examined her efforts to counter radicalization among young conservative men through philosophical videos that addressed their ideological concerns without direct confrontation.[32] The piece highlighted Wynn's strategy of engaging audiences on topics like fascism and identity, noting that "people don't care about facts as much as they think they do," emphasizing emotional and narrative appeal over pure data in deradicalization.[32] In July 2024, Reeve published "“Weaponized Autism.” Shame, Pride, and the Making and Undoing of the Alt-Right" on Literary Hub, exploring intersections between neurodivergence, online subcultures, and alt-right recruitment, drawing from her reporting experiences to discuss how terms like "weaponized autism" were used by extremists to valorize obsessive online behaviors while pathologizing outsiders.[33] The article analyzed how shame over perceived autistic traits fueled entry into extremist communities offering camaraderie and purpose, but also contributed to the movement's internal fractures through infighting and doxxing.[33] Earlier in her career, Reeve wrote "Meet the Manliest Journalist in America: Me" for The New Republic on July 15, 2015, a satirical self-reflection critiquing gender stereotypes in journalism amid debates over male dominance in the field, using humor to underscore how physicality and bravado were overvalued in reporting environments.[34] This piece, while lighter in tone, demonstrated her engagement with media industry dynamics before shifting focus to extremism coverage.[34] Reeve's written contributions often complement her on-the-ground reporting, providing deeper analysis of the psychological and cultural drivers behind fringe movements, though her output leans more toward video and broadcast formats given her roles at Vice and CNN.[10]

Book: Black Pill (2024)

Black Pill: How I Witnessed the Darkest Corners of the Internet Come to Life, Poison Society, and Capture American Politics is Elle Reeve's debut book, published on July 9, 2024, by Atria Books, an imprint of Simon & Schuster.[35][36] The 304-page hardcover draws on Reeve's decade of on-the-ground reporting for Vice News and CNN, chronicling her immersion in online subcultures including incels, the manosphere, and alt-right forums.[37] Reeve structures the narrative as a "stranger-than-fiction odyssey," tracing the evolution of internet memes and ideologies—such as the "black pill," a fatalistic worldview positing that physical attractiveness overwhelmingly determines romantic and social success for men—into real-world political influence.[35][38] She details encounters with figures from these communities, including incels who harbor resentment toward women and society, and connects their rhetoric to events like the 2016 U.S. presidential election, the 2017 Charlottesville rally, and the January 6, 2021, Capitol riot.[39][40] The book argues that these digital spaces fostered a pipeline from ironic online humor to organized extremism, exacerbating societal polarization and contributing to violence.[36][41] Key themes include the interplay between anonymity online and offline radicalization, with Reeve emphasizing her interviews revealing how participants transitioned from forum posting to activism.[42] She portrays these groups as harboring deep misogyny and racial animus, framing their growth as a threat to democratic norms, though critics from conservative outlets have questioned the book's selective focus on right-leaning extremism amid broader ideological conflicts.[40][43] Reception has been largely positive in mainstream outlets; The New York Times called it a "chilling and insightful account" of the alt-right's trajectory, while Publishers Weekly praised its "riveting" journalism comparable to works like Why We're Polarized.[39][37] On Goodreads, it holds a 4.2 out of 5 rating from over 3,800 reviews as of late 2024, with readers noting its timely examination of online radicalization despite the subject matter's discomfort.[38]

Awards and Recognition

Key Honors for Reporting

Reeve's reporting on the 2017 Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia, for Vice News Tonight earned significant recognition from major journalism award organizations. The segment "Charlottesville: Race and Terror," in which Reeve embedded with white supremacist participants, won the George Polk Award for National Television Reporting in 2018, awarded by Long Island University for "on-the-scene coverage of the Charlottesville demonstrations."[44][45] The same coverage received a Peabody Award in 2018 from the University of Georgia's Grady College of Journalism, honoring its "fearless" on-the-ground reporting that captured raw interactions with rally attendees.[46] At the 39th Annual News & Documentary Emmy Awards in 2018, Vice News Tonight's Charlottesville segment secured four Emmys, including Outstanding Coverage of a Breaking News Story in a Newscast, Outstanding Editing: News, and Outstanding Video Journalism, recognizing the production's technical and journalistic execution under pressure.[47][48]

Controversies and Criticisms

Charlottesville Unite the Right Coverage (2017)

In August 2017, Elle Reeve, as a correspondent for VICE News Tonight on HBO, embedded with white nationalist and alt-right participants during the Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia. The event, organized by figures including Jason Kessler and Richard Spencer to protest the removal of a statue honoring Confederate General Robert E. Lee, drew hundreds of attendees who marched with tiki torches on the evening of August 11, chanting phrases such as "Jews will not replace us" and displaying swastikas. Reeve's team gained access by traveling in vehicles with rallygoers, conducting on-site interviews that captured unfiltered expressions of racial animus and preparations for confrontation, including neo-Nazi Christopher Cantwell stating his intent "to make myself more capable of violence."[26][49][27] The resulting episode, "Charlottesville: Race and Terror," aired on August 14, 2017, and focused on the rally's Friday night procession and Saturday confrontations, where participants clashed with counter-protesters using improvised weapons like shields and pepper spray from both sides. Reeve's reporting highlighted the organizers' explicit white supremacist ideology, including antisemitic rhetoric and calls for a white ethnostate, without added narration to let the subjects' words stand alone. The segment documented the escalation on August 12, when James Alex Fields Jr., a rally attendee with prior neo-Nazi affiliations, accelerated his vehicle into a crowd of counter-protesters, killing 36-year-old Heather Heyer and injuring 19 others; Fields was later convicted of first-degree murder and federal hate crimes.[26][46][49] Reeve's immersive approach drew praise for its raw exposure of extremist motivations, earning a Peabody Award citation for "fearlessly jumping onto vans and pushing microphones into already flushed faces" amid the chaos. However, it ignited debates over media platforming of hate groups, with some outlets questioning whether embedding and amplifying such voices risked legitimizing fringe ideologies rather than merely documenting them—a tension Reeve addressed in subsequent interviews by emphasizing the need to confront extremism directly rather than ignore it. Alt-right participants, including Cantwell (who faced federal charges partly due to identifications from the footage), later expressed hostility toward Reeve, viewing her as a target for doxxing and threats, while she reported being recognized and approached by neo-Nazis in public settings post-broadcast. Critics from across the spectrum, including discussions in media ethics forums, argued the coverage exemplified broader perils of "both-sides" reporting on asymmetric threats, where violent actors on the right received disproportionate access compared to counter-protesters, though Reeve maintained her method revealed causal links between online radicalization and street action without sanitizing the subjects' agency.[46][50][51][52]

Allegations of Bias in Extremism Reporting

Critics have alleged that Elle Reeve's reporting on extremism displays a selective focus on right-wing groups, potentially reflecting institutional biases in outlets like Vice News and CNN, where coverage of far-right threats predominates over equivalent scrutiny of left-wing extremism. Reeve's immersive dispatches, such as her 2017 Vice News coverage of the Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia—which featured interviews with neo-Nazis and white nationalists—and her CNN segments on the Proud Boys, including their 2020 Portland rally confrontations, have been credited with exposing internal dynamics of these movements but faulted for lacking balance.[27][53] No comparable firsthand reporting by Reeve on Antifa or other left-wing militant groups, despite their documented involvement in street violence and property destruction during events like the 2020 protests, has been prominently featured in her body of work.[53] This disparity aligns with broader analyses of media patterns, where right-wing extremism is more frequently framed as a systemic threat using terms like "extremist," while left-wing violence is often contextualized as reactive or downplayed.[54] Conservative commentators argue that such coverage, exemplified by Reeve's emphasis on online radicalization in her 2024 book Black Pill—which details incel forums and alt-right misogyny without parallel examination of leftist echo chambers—contributes to a distorted public perception of relative threats, privileging narrative over empirical parity in violence data.[42] Reeve has not publicly responded to these specific bias claims in the context of her extremism beat, though her defenders praise the rigor of her access to hard-to-reach subjects. Empirical comparisons of political violence indicate both ideological flanks pose risks, with right-wing attacks more lethal in recent U.S. data but left-wing incidents involving higher frequencies of property-focused actions.[55]

Responses to Broader Media Criticisms

Elle Reeve has pushed back against liberal-leaning criticisms of mainstream media practices, particularly accusations that journalists "platform" or humanize supporters of Donald Trump or spreaders of misinformation. In a May 2025 CNN podcast discussion, she aligned with correspondent Donie O'Sullivan in labeling such complaints "infuriating," emphasizing a moral imperative to recognize shared humanity by stating, "We're all human beings. Dehumanization is bad."[56][57] Reeve has critiqued the efficacy of no-platforming strategies employed by some media outlets and activists, declaring in the same discussion that "the no-platforming concept has completely failed" and is "totally bankrupt." She argued that avoidance exacerbates problems, observing that "the idea that if you ignore it, it goes away has completely failed," thereby defending direct engagement as essential for understanding and countering fringe ideologies.[56] In interviews addressing perceptions of media bias, Reeve has noted how far-right subjects view outlets like CNN as inherently liberal and adversarial, with one podcast appearance recounting their dismissal of her as "a tool of all that opposes them."[58] This reflects her awareness of distrust from conservative audiences, though her responses prioritize immersive reporting to illuminate causal factors in extremism over rebutting bias claims directly. Such approaches, she implies through her work, stem from empirical observation rather than ideological alignment, countering broader critiques of selective outrage in coverage.[4]

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Reeve married United States Army Private Scott Thomas Beauchamp in 2007 while he was on leave from deployment; the couple wed at a lawyer's office in Virginia without informing her employer, The New Republic, where she worked as a reporter-researcher.[59] The marriage ended in divorce prior to her subsequent union. On December 31, 2018, Reeve married Jeremy Greenfield, a content writer based in Brooklyn.[60] Reeve and Greenfield have two children, both born in 2024. Their first child arrived in February 2024, as announced by Reeve on social media.[61] Their second child was born later that year, with Greenfield publicly noting the arrival in a professional network post coinciding with the release of Reeve's book Black Pill.[62] The family resides in New York City, where Reeve balances her reporting career with parenthood.
