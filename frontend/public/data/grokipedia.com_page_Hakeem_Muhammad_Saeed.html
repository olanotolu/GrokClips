Hakeem Muhammad Saeed

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed (9 January 1920 – 17 October 1998) was a Pakistani hakim specializing in Unani medicine, medical researcher, author, philanthropist, and Governor of Sindh Province.[1][2] He is renowned for establishing the Hamdard Foundation in 1948, which developed into a major institution promoting Eastern medicine through Hamdard Laboratories, one of the world's largest producers of Unani remedies, and Hamdard University in Karachi in 1985.[1][3] Saeed authored and compiled approximately 200 books on topics including medicine, philosophy, science, health, religion, and social issues, contributing significantly to the documentation and dissemination of traditional knowledge.[1] His efforts extended to public health initiatives, educational reforms, and national think tanks like the Hamdard Shura, reflecting a commitment to integrating Greco-Arab medical traditions with modern practices in Pakistan.[4] Serving briefly as Governor of Sindh from July 1993 to January 1994, his career culminated tragically when he was assassinated by unknown gunmen in Karachi on 17 October 1998 while en route to Hamdard Laboratories, an event that underscored the political tensions of the era but did not diminish his enduring legacy in healthcare and philanthropy.[1][2][5]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was born on January 9, 1920, in Delhi, British India, into a family of Unani physicians with deep roots in traditional herbal medicine.[6][7] His father, Hakim Abdul Majeed, was a prominent practitioner who founded the Hamdard Dawakhana in 1906, establishing a legacy in Unani medicine that emphasized empirical formulations and ethical practice.[8][9] His mother, Rabia Begum, supported the family's scholarly and religious environment, which was characterized by Urdu-speaking traditions and adherence to Islamic principles.[8] The family's ancestry traced back to migrants from Kashghar in Turkestan (present-day Xinjiang, China), who arrived in the Indian subcontinent during the reign of Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II in the late 18th century, initially settling in Patna before moving to Delhi.[9][6] This heritage instilled a commitment to intellectual pursuits and medical philanthropy, with the household serving as a hub for learning Unani texts and compounding herbal remedies. Saeed's early upbringing in colonial Delhi exposed him to a blend of traditional Islamic scholarship and emerging modern influences, fostering his lifelong dedication to integrating empirical observation with classical knowledge.[7][6] Under his father's guidance, Saeed received initial training in Unani medicine from a young age, assisting in the family clinic and witnessing the direct application of treatments to patients from diverse backgrounds.[8] This hands-on immersion, combined with a disciplined home education emphasizing Arabic, Persian, and Urdu literature, shaped his foundational worldview, prioritizing evidence-based healing over unverified claims. By his late teens, he had begun formal studies, but the Partition of India in 1947 profoundly impacted his early adulthood, prompting migration to Pakistan with his wife, Naimat Begum—whom he married in 1945—and their young daughter, Sadia Rashid, amid the upheaval of communal violence and displacement.[10][7]

Medical and Scholarly Training

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was born into a family of traditional Unani practitioners in Delhi, where his father, Hakim Haji Abdul Majeed, established Hamdard Waqf Laboratories focused on herbal and Unani formulations. Saeed received initial scholarly training in classical Islamic sciences, including Arabic, Persian, Urdu, English, and Quranic studies, alongside practical exposure to Unani medicine through family apprenticeship, emphasizing empirical herbal remedies and Greco-Arabic principles.[11] In 1938, at age 18, Saeed enrolled at the University of Delhi to pursue formal medical and pharmaceutical education, blending traditional knowledge with modern scientific methods. During his studies, he contracted tuberculosis, which forced him to miss a year but did not derail his progress. He obtained a Bachelor of Pharmacy (B.Pharm) and a Bachelor of Science (BS) in medicinal chemistry in 1942, focusing on chemical analysis and formulation of herbal drugs.[11][12][13] Following his undergraduate degrees, Saeed advanced to postgraduate studies at the University of Delhi, earning a Master of Pharmacy (M.Pharm) in 1945, which deepened his expertise in pharmaceutical sciences and quality control for traditional remedies. In 1952, he traveled to Turkey and completed a PhD in Pharmacy at Ankara University, researching Eastern medicinal systems in a comparative context with Western pharmacology. This doctoral work underscored his commitment to integrating Unani principles with rigorous scientific validation.[11][10] Saeed's training emphasized causal mechanisms in herbal efficacy, drawing from first-hand experimentation at Hamdard Laboratories post-1942, where he served as a junior researcher developing standardized Unani preparations. His scholarly pursuits during this period involved compiling classical texts on Unani pharmacology, laying groundwork for later advocacy against unsubstantiated Western dismissal of empirical Eastern traditions.[11][14]

Career in Medicine and Philanthropy

Establishment of Hamdard Foundation

Following the partition of India in August 1947, Hakeem Muhammad Saeed migrated from Delhi to Karachi, Pakistan, with limited resources, including basic equipment and formulas for Unani herbal medicines inherited from his family. In 1948, he established Hamdard Laboratories (Waqf) in a modest rented premises in Karachi's Kharadar area, dedicating it as a perpetual charitable endowment (waqf) to promote Eastern medicine, produce affordable herbal remedies, and serve public health needs amid post-partition hardships.[15] The initiative built on the original Hamdard Dawakhana founded in Delhi in 1906 by his uncle Hakeem Hafiz Abdul Majeed, but Saeed adapted it to Pakistan's context, starting production of flagship products like Rooh Afza syrup and other Tibb-e-Unani formulations using local herbs.[16] The waqf structure ensured profits were reinvested into philanthropy rather than personal gain, aligning with Saeed's vision of self-sustaining welfare through medicine; by 1953, the operations were fully formalized under waqf regulations, expanding manufacturing capacity and distribution across Pakistan.[17] This establishment marked the genesis of what evolved into the Hamdard Foundation, emphasizing research in traditional Unani systems while countering reliance on imported Western pharmaceuticals, with early output reaching household popularity for remedies targeting common ailments like digestive issues and fevers. Saeed personally oversaw formulation standardization and quality control, drawing from Greco-Arabic medical texts, which laid groundwork for later institutional growth into education and nation-building projects.[1][18]

Development of Unani Medicine and Research

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed played a pivotal role in institutionalizing and scientifically advancing Unani medicine, known as Tibb-e-Unani, in Pakistan by establishing dedicated research facilities and promoting empirical validation of traditional formulations. After migrating to Karachi following the 1947 partition, he founded Hamdard Laboratories (Waqf) in 1948, transforming the family herbal enterprise into a major center for Unani drug production and research.[19] The laboratories focused on quality control, standardization of herbal extracts, and clinical testing of remedies derived from Greco-Arabic traditions, positioning Hamdard as one of the world's largest manufacturers of Unani medicines by the late 20th century.[20] Saeed's emphasis on research in medical biology distinguished his work, integrating traditional pharmacology with laboratory analysis to address efficacy and safety concerns.[19] Saeed expanded research infrastructure through the Hamdard Foundation, established in 1953, which supported systematic studies on medicinal plants and therapeutic protocols.[21] In 1957, he initiated Hamdard Medicus, a peer-reviewed journal that published original research on Unani therapeutics, history of Eastern medicine, and pharmacological evaluations, continuing as a biannual outlet for scholarly contributions.[22] By 1965, he opened the Hamdard Research Clinic and Nursing Home, where Unani treatments were combined with modern diagnostics to gather clinical data on patient outcomes.[23] These efforts culminated in the development of Madinat al-Hikmah ("City of Knowledge") in the 1980s, encompassing research centers dedicated to validating Unani principles through controlled studies and documentation of ancient texts.[4] Further institutionalizing research, Saeed founded the Faculty of Eastern Medicine at Hamdard University in 1991, offering Bachelor of Unani Medicine and Surgery (BUMS) degrees alongside postgraduate programs emphasizing evidence-based research on herbal pharmacology and holistic therapies.[24] In the early 1990s, he established the Pakistan Academy of Eastern Medicine (PAEM) to advocate for policy reforms and collaborative studies integrating Unani with allopathic systems, addressing regulatory hurdles to traditional medicine's growth.[25] Saeed's research advocacy extended to over 200 publications on Unani topics, including clinical trials and plant-based drug development, though independent verification of efficacy remains limited by the field's reliance on anecdotal and observational data rather than large-scale randomized controlled trials.[19] His initiatives elevated Unani from folk practice to a structured discipline, fostering self-reliance in Pakistan's pharmaceutical sector while prioritizing natural remedies amid skepticism from Western-oriented medical establishments.[7]

Educational and Institutional Foundations

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed established Hamdard University in Karachi in 1991 as a private institution dedicated to higher education, research, and the integration of traditional Eastern medicine with modern sciences.[26] Serving as its inaugural Vice-Chancellor, Saeed emphasized interdisciplinary programs, particularly in pharmacy, medicine, and Unani (Tibb) studies, aiming to revive intellectual traditions from historical Muslim civilizations while addressing contemporary health needs in Pakistan.[11] The university's campuses in Karachi and later Islamabad housed specialized faculties, including the Hamdard Al-Majeed College of Eastern Medicine, which offered degrees in Unani medicine, and the Hafiz Muhammad Saeed College of Pharmacy, focusing on herbal and pharmaceutical research.[11] Under Saeed's vision through the Hamdard Foundation—established in 1948 for broader philanthropic goals—he developed Madinat al-Hikmah (City of Knowledge), a comprehensive complex launched in the 1980s that functioned as an educational and research hub.[27] This initiative included libraries with over 100,000 volumes on Islamic sciences, medicine, and history; research institutes such as the Institute of History of Medicine and Medical Sciences; and facilities for scholarly seminars and publications, fostering self-reliance in knowledge production amid Pakistan's post-independence challenges.[11] Saeed's approach prioritized empirical validation of traditional remedies, funding clinical trials and pharmacological studies to bridge Unani practices with evidence-based standards.[21] Saeed's earlier academic involvement laid groundwork for these foundations; after earning his doctorate, he joined the University of Sindh in 1953 as an associate professor of pharmacy, teaching organic chemistry and advocating for expanded curricula in indigenous medical systems.[14] By the 1970s, he expanded Hamdard's educational outreach with technical institutes and scholarships, training over thousands in vocational health skills, though formal university status was achieved later. These efforts reflected his commitment to institutional autonomy, funding operations through Hamdard Laboratories' profits rather than heavy government reliance, ensuring sustainability for programs serving underprivileged students.[28]

Intellectual and Literary Works

Major Publications

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was a prolific writer, authoring and compiling over 200 books in Urdu and English on topics including Unani medicine, Islamic philosophy, health sciences, religion, literature, and social issues. His publications emphasized the practical application of traditional Eastern healing systems, often drawing on empirical observations from Unani texts and personal clinical experience, while advocating for their integration with modern knowledge to address public health needs in Pakistan. Many of his works were published through Hamdard Foundation presses, reflecting his institutional commitment to disseminating accessible medical knowledge.[1][29] One of his most enduring contributions is the multi-volume Dehati Mualij (Village Healer), first issued in the mid-20th century and subsequently reprinted and expanded. This series provides detailed, step-by-step remedies for common ailments using herbal, dietary, and Unani-based interventions, tailored for rural populations lacking formal medical infrastructure; it prioritizes low-cost, locally available materials and has been translated into Hindi and other languages, achieving broad circulation and practical influence in underserved areas.[30][31] Saeed's explorations of Islamic medical heritage include Al-Tibb al-Islami, a concise historical overview tracing the development of Tibb (medicine) from the era of Prophet Muhammad through the Islamic golden age, highlighting prophetic traditions (Sunnah) in hygiene, diet, and therapeutics as foundational to Unani principles and countering dismissals of traditional systems by advocating evidence from classical sources.[32] He also produced compilations like Hakeem Muhammad Saeed ke Tibbi Mashwaray, a collection of clinical advice derived from decades of practice, focusing on preventive care and holistic treatments for chronic conditions prevalent in South Asia.[33] In addition to original treatises, Saeed edited and contributed to scholarly works on the philosophy and history of Eastern medicine, such as travelogues documenting global medical practices (Europe Nama) and essays promoting Unani research, which served to bridge ancient Greco-Arabic knowledge with contemporary pharmacology through Hamdard's institutional lens. These efforts, grounded in Saeed's firsthand advocacy for empirical validation of herbal formulations, influenced generations of practitioners despite skepticism from Western-oriented medical establishments.[34]

Themes and Impact

Saeed's intellectual works emphasized the historical continuity and scientific validity of Unani, Arabic, and Islamic medicine, tracing its origins from the Prophet Muhammad's era through medieval Muslim advancements and Greco-Arab foundations to contemporary applications. In publications such as Al-Biruni's Book on Pharmacy and Materia Medica (1973), Al-Tibb Al-Islami (1976), and Yunani (Unani), Arabic, and Islamic Medicine and Pharmacy (1997), he advocated integrating traditional Eastern systems—like Unani and Chinese medicine—with modern scientific methods, rejecting colonial-era dismissals of Asian medical traditions in favor of a global, non-Eurocentric perspective.[35] His writings highlighted holistic approaches, including preventive care, natural remedies, and ethical principles derived from Islamic sources, as seen in explorations of concepts like cardiovascular treatments rooted in ancient texts.[35] Travelogues formed a significant portion of his literary output, with 56 works primarily targeting youth and children to instill moral values, simplicity, and intellectual discipline, often prioritizing didactic content over stylistic flair. These narratives extended themes of harmonizing classical Islamic knowledge—such as ethical living and self-reliance—with modern progress, echoing reformist traditions like those of Sir Syed Ahmed Khan, and served as tools for character-building amid Pakistan's post-independence challenges.[36] Broader philosophical themes across his corpus underscored moral development alongside scientific inquiry, promoting education that fused religious wisdom with empirical rigor.[37] The impact of Saeed's writings lay in revitalizing Eastern medicine beyond nationalist confines, influencing global discourse through the Society for the Promotion of Eastern Medicine's efforts to document and hybridize traditional practices with Western advancements.[35] In Pakistan, they underpinned institutional innovations like Hamdard University and its hospital, where integrated curricula and treatments enhanced public health outcomes by blending Unani protocols with modern diagnostics, as evidenced by stakeholder assessments of improved accessibility and efficacy.[37] His prolific output, including youth-oriented texts, fostered cultural resilience against Western medical dominance, educating generations on indigenous systems and contributing to sustained research in holistic care.[36]

Public Service and Advocacy

Role as Governor of Sindh

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was appointed Governor of Sindh on 19 July 1993 by President Ishfaq Ahmed, serving until 23 January 1994, a tenure of approximately six months amid political instability in the province.[12][38] His appointment followed the resignation of prior governors and preceded the return of Mahmoud Haroon, occurring during a period of heightened ethnic and political tensions in Karachi, dominated by conflicts involving the Muttahida Qaumi Movement (MQM).[39] Throughout his governorship, Saeed eschewed official privileges, including refusal to occupy the Governor's House in Karachi, opting instead to commute in his personal vehicle without a security convoy, reflecting his austere personal ethos rooted in public service over ceremonial pomp.[40] He prioritized administrative efficiency and public accessibility, directing efforts toward stabilizing provincial governance amid urban violence, though his short term limited broader structural reforms.[14] Saeed advocated for enhanced education access, championing reforms to ensure quality schooling for all children and emphasizing compulsory primary education as a foundational priority, aligning with his lifelong philanthropy in knowledge dissemination.[7][4] He publicly criticized the MQM for exacerbating terrorist activities in Sindh, attributing much of Karachi's insecurity to the party's militant factions, a stance that drew ire from ethnic nationalist groups and foreshadowed threats against him.[39] His governorship integrated his medical and scholarly background into public policy, with informal pushes for integrating traditional Unani practices into provincial health discussions, though no formal legislation emerged due to the brevity of his role.[10] Saeed's tenure ended abruptly with his replacement, after which he resumed focus on Hamdard Foundation activities, but his provincial service underscored a commitment to ethical leadership amid Pakistan's fractious federal-provincial dynamics.[38]

Promotion of Eastern Medicine Against Western Skepticism

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed actively advocated for Unani medicine, or Tibb, in Pakistan during a period when Western-trained physicians and colonial legacies marginalized traditional systems as unscientific or ineffective without rigorous clinical trials. He argued that Unani's emphasis on humoral balance, natural remedies, and preventive care offered complementary benefits to allopathic approaches, which he critiqued for over-reliance on symptomatic interventions and pharmaceuticals with potential side effects. Saeed's efforts focused on empirical validation through research, establishing laboratories at Hamdard to test traditional formulations, while emphasizing the need for standardization to counter dismissals of Unani as anecdotal.[41] In 1956, Saeed co-founded the Society for the Promotion of Eastern Medicine to organize hakims, document practices, and lobby for institutional recognition, directly challenging the post-colonial dominance of Western biomedicine that had eroded Tibb's status since British rule. The society published bulletins and hosted conferences to disseminate evidence from clinical observations and herbal efficacy studies, aiming to bridge traditional knowledge with modern pharmacology. By 1963, Saeed led a Pakistani delegation to China to study integrated traditional systems, fostering cross-cultural exchanges that highlighted Unani's potential in global health contexts despite Western doubts rooted in evidentiary standards like randomized controlled trials.[41][42] Saeed's advocacy peaked during President Ayub Khan's administration in the 1960s, when proposals to ban or classify Eastern medicine as quackery threatened its practitioners; through persistent articles, public lectures, and political pressure, he secured the Unani, Ayurvedic and Homoeopathic Practitioners Act of 1965, which legalized and regulated Tibb nationwide. His 1983 article published by the World Health Organization underscored Unani's role in primary care for resource-limited settings, positioning it as viable amid skepticism from allopathic lobbies prioritizing evidence-based protocols over holistic traditions. Saeed's over 200 publications, including comparative analyses in Hamdard Medicus, persistently called for hybrid models, warning that exclusive Western adoption ignored proven indigenous remedies' accessibility and lower toxicity in chronic conditions.[19][43][22]

Controversies

Conflicts with Authorities on Medical Policy

In the early years following Pakistan's independence, Hakeem Muhammad Saeed faced resistance from federal health authorities who prioritized Western allopathic medicine and viewed traditional Eastern systems, particularly Unani Tibb, as unscientific. In 1959, Lieutenant-General Wajid Ali Khan Burki, serving as Surgeon General of the Pakistan Army Medical Corps and a key official in the Ministry of Health, attempted to impose restrictions or bans on Unani practitioners, dismissing the system as quackery incompatible with modern standards. Saeed responded by mobilizing support through public advocacy, highlighting the empirical successes and historical efficacy of Eastern formulations based on herbal and holistic principles.[44] By 1963, these tensions escalated, leading Saeed to resign from his advisory or institutional role amid irreconcilable differences with the federal administration and Sindh's Health Minister, who undervalued the integration of Eastern medicine into national policy. The following year, in 1964, Saeed intensified his critique by directly challenging Burki's policies in public forums, authoring articles that exposed the bias against non-Western systems and organizing conferences to demonstrate clinical evidence from Hamdard laboratories. This campaign underscored Saeed's insistence on evidence-based validation, such as documented cure rates for chronic conditions unresponsive to allopathy, rather than ideological dismissal.[44][10] Saeed's persistent lobbying under Field Marshal Ayub Khan's regime—through writings in journals, seminars attended by policymakers, and alliances with practitioners—created sufficient political pressure to compel legislative change. In response, the government enacted the Eastern Medicine Practitioners Act (often dated to the mid-1960s), which granted legal recognition and regulatory framework for Unani, Ayurvedic, and related systems, allowing licensed hakeems to practice without persecution. This outcome reflected not capitulation to pseudoscience, as critics claimed, but a pragmatic acknowledgment of widespread public reliance on affordable Eastern remedies amid limited allopathic infrastructure. Saeed's efforts thus shifted policy from suppression to coexistence, though ongoing skepticism from medical establishments persisted.[10][45]

Scrutiny of Unani Medicine's Efficacy

Unani medicine's theoretical framework, centered on the balance of four humors (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile) and elements derived from ancient Greek, Arabic, and Persian texts, diverges significantly from contemporary biomedical understandings of physiology and pathology, which emphasize cellular, molecular, and genetic mechanisms supported by empirical observation.[46] This humoral paradigm has not been substantiated through rigorous causal testing, leading critics to classify Unani as a pre-scientific system prone to post-hoc rationalizations rather than predictive, falsifiable models. Proponents, including institutions promoting traditional systems, often rely on historical texts and anecdotal efficacy, but such claims lack the mechanistic validation required for causal inference in modern evidence hierarchies.[47] Clinical evidence for Unani interventions remains sparse and methodologically constrained, with few high-quality randomized controlled trials (RCTs) demonstrating superiority or equivalence to evidence-based alternatives across its broad therapeutic claims, such as for chronic diseases or dermatological conditions. A non-inferiority RCT involving 287 patients with psoriasis vulgaris found that a Unani polyherbal regimen achieved a PASI 75 response in 16.3% of intention-to-treat participants after 12 weeks, comparable to PUVA-sol therapy but indicative of limited absolute efficacy, as over 80% did not reach this threshold.[48] Systematic reviews, such as one evaluating Unani therapeutics for obesity, report overall suggestive benefits without adverse effects but highlight small sample sizes, heterogeneous interventions, short durations, and modest effect sizes, underscoring insufficient power to establish generalizability or rule out placebo contributions.[49] Similarly, trials on polyherbal formulations for anxiety-depressive disorders show symptom reductions (e.g., via HAM-A and HAM-D scales), yet these are often unblinded or from Unani-affiliated centers, raising concerns over bias in outcome reporting.[50] Challenges in validating Unani's efficacy stem from its holistic, individualized prescribing, which resists standardization essential for double-blinded RCTs, and the predominance of observational or open-label studies that conflate non-specific effects like patient expectations with pharmacological actions.[51] While isolated herbal components (e.g., certain anti-inflammatory agents) may exhibit pharmacological activity in vitro or in small cohorts, the synergistic claims of compound formulations lack dissection into active principles, impeding causal attribution and regulatory approval akin to pharmaceuticals. Sources from Unani advocacy bodies, such as those in India and Pakistan, frequently report positive outcomes, but independent Western reviews note epistemological mismatches and potential delays in accessing proven therapies, particularly for serious conditions where empirical data prioritizes interventions with demonstrated survival benefits.[47] Hakeem Muhammad Saeed's advocacy for Unani integration, through institutions like Hamdard, amplified these treatments amid such evidential gaps, drawing Western skepticism that prioritized biochemical plausibility over traditional authority.[46]

Assassination

Circumstances of Death

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was assassinated on October 17, 1998, in Karachi, Pakistan, by unidentified gunmen who opened fire on his vehicle.[52][53] The attack occurred as Saeed was en route to Hamdard Laboratories to oversee a medical experiment, resulting in his immediate death from gunshot wounds, along with two companions killed and three others injured.[54][55] The assailants fled the scene, leaving no immediate claims of responsibility, amid Karachi's volatile security environment marked by ethnic and political violence in the late 1990s.[52] An initial first information report (FIR) registered at the local police station described the perpetrators as unknown armed individuals who targeted Saeed's convoy in a drive-by shooting near his clinic in the Nazimabad area.[52]

Investigation and Judicial Outcomes

The assassination of Hakeem Muhammad Saeed on October 17, 1998, prompted an immediate police investigation in Karachi, with an FIR registered at Paposhnagar police station against unidentified assailants responsible for firing that killed Saeed, his driver Muhammad Iqbal, and bodyguard Aslam, while injuring three others.[52] The probe initially focused on sectarian motives amid Karachi's ethnic and political violence, but soon shifted to implicating members of the Muttahida Qaumi Movement (MQM), leading to the arrest of nine suspects, including MQM activists Muhammad Amirullah, Imran Pasha, and Muhammad Shakir.[56][57] The case proceeded in an anti-terrorism court, where the prosecution alleged MQM involvement based on witness statements and recovery of weapons, but the accused denied participation, claiming political framing.[58] An appellate bench of the Sindh High Court acquitted all nine in 2010, ruling the evidence insufficient and potentially coerced.[56] The Sindh government appealed to the Supreme Court of Pakistan, which in 2014 dismissed the appeal and upheld the acquittals, explicitly noting that prosecution evidence, including confessional statements, appeared fabricated and lacked corroboration.[52][56] The verdict highlighted procedural flaws in the investigation, such as reliance on questionable eyewitness accounts amid Karachi's unstable security environment. No alternative suspects were successfully prosecuted, and the perpetrators' identities remain unresolved in judicial records.[59]

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was the younger brother of Hakim Abdul Hameed, a prominent Unani physician who expanded the Hamdard organization in India after partition.[12] The brothers shared a family legacy in traditional medicine, with their father, Hakeem Saeed Ahmad, also practicing as a hakeem in Delhi.[11] In 1945, Saeed married his cousin Naimat Begum in Delhi, forming a partnership that supported his early career in Unani medicine.[12] The couple had one daughter, Sadia Rashid (also referred to as Sadiya Saeed), born prior to the partition of India.[12][11] Following Pakistan's independence in 1947, Saeed relocated to Karachi with his wife and daughter, where they established a new life amid the challenges of migration and nation-building.[60][10] Sadia Rashid later assumed leadership roles in the Hamdard Foundation Pakistan and Hamdard Laboratories, continuing her father's institutional work after his death.[11] No public records indicate additional children or subsequent marriages.[12][60]

Philanthropic Efforts

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed established the Hamdard Laboratories in 1948 upon migrating to Pakistan, initially focusing on herbal medicine production, and later formalized its philanthropic arm through a waqf dedication on January 1, 1964, channeling profits toward public welfare.[17] This transformation positioned Hamdard as a nonprofit entity supporting education, healthcare, and research in traditional Unani medicine, with Saeed overseeing the expansion of facilities that provided affordable or free services to underserved populations.[19] In education, Saeed's efforts included founding multiple schools and the Hamdard University of Natural and Islamic Sciences in 1991, aimed at integrating Eastern medical knowledge with modern sciences and promoting holistic learning accessible to low-income students.[7] The foundation sponsored scholarships, libraries, and intellectual forums to foster scientific discourse, including nation-building projects that hosted conferences on health and philosophy to bridge traditional and contemporary knowledge.[3] Healthcare philanthropy under Saeed involved establishing dispensaries, research centers, and welfare initiatives offering free treatments via Unani remedies, alongside provision of ambulances and community clinics targeting Pakistan's poor, particularly in urban Karachi.[61] These programs emphasized preventive care and herbal formulations, distributing millions of doses annually through subsidized outlets, while Saeed advocated for integrating Eastern practices into national health policy to reduce reliance on costly Western imports.[62] By the 1990s, Hamdard's network supported over 100 welfare projects, including orphanages and vocational training, reflecting Saeed's commitment to self-sustaining community upliftment without government dependency.[19]

Legacy

Awards and Honors

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed was conferred the Sitara-e-Imtiaz by the President of Pakistan in 1966, recognizing his pioneering efforts in promoting Unani medicine, establishing pharmaceutical enterprises, and advancing medical education through institutions like Hamdard Laboratories.[63][27] In 1983, he received the Kuwait Prize for Tibb-i-Islami from the Kuwait government, honoring his scholarly contributions to Eastern and traditional Islamic medical systems, including research on herbal formulations and their integration with modern pharmacology.[27] Following his assassination in 1998, Saeed was posthumously awarded the Nishan-e-Imtiaz, Pakistan's highest civilian honor, in 2002 by the Government of Pakistan, acknowledging his lifelong dedication to public health, philanthropy, and the establishment of enduring institutions such as Hamdard University and welfare trusts.[62][10] These accolades underscored his role in bridging traditional healing practices with national development, though they were conferred amid debates over the empirical validation of Unani treatments he championed. No other major international or national awards are documented in official records, reflecting the specialized focus of his work within Pakistan's alternative medicine sector.

Enduring Institutions and Influence

Hakeem Muhammad Saeed's most prominent enduring institution is the Hamdard Foundation Pakistan, which he established following his migration to Karachi in 1948, building on the family's pre-partition herbal medicine enterprise founded in Delhi in 1906.[64] The foundation, structured as a waqf dedicated to humanitarian service, continues to advance Eastern (Unani) medicine through research, production of herbal remedies, and public health initiatives, including free medical camps and educational outreach that have reached millions since inception.[3] Its pharmaceutical arm, Hamdard Laboratories, manufactures over 200 traditional formulations, maintaining market leadership in Pakistan's herbal sector with annual production exceeding 100 million units as of recent reports.[21] In 1991, Saeed founded Hamdard University in Karachi as part of his broader vision to integrate modern and traditional sciences, serving as its first vice-chancellor until his death.[18] The university, one of Pakistan's earliest private higher education institutions, enrolls over 6,000 students annually across faculties of Eastern medicine, pharmacy, health sciences, and social sciences, with a focus on Tibb-e-Unani research that has produced peer-reviewed studies on herbal efficacy.[26] It operates facilities like the Institute of History of Medicine and Medical Research, contributing to archival preservation and clinical trials that validate traditional remedies against contemporary standards.[26] Saeed's crowning project, Madinat al-Hikmah (City of Wisdom), established in the 1980s near Karachi, encompasses the university and allied centers for science, culture, and Islamic studies, embodying his goal of a holistic knowledge ecosystem.[37] Post-1998, these entities have sustained his influence by fostering interdisciplinary scholarship, with the complex hosting annual conferences on medical history and herbal pharmacology attended by regional experts.[40] Collectively, these institutions have institutionalized Saeed's advocacy for evidence-based traditional medicine, countering skepticism through sustained output of 50+ research publications yearly from affiliated labs, while generating employment for thousands and supporting Pakistan's alternative healthcare framework amid limited allopathic access in rural areas.[7]
