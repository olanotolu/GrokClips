Geography of Laos

Laos is a landlocked country in mainland Southeast Asia. It covers approximately 236,800 square kilometers and is surrounded by Myanmar, Cambodia, China, Thailand, and Vietnam. About 70% of its geographic area is made up of mountain ranges, highlands, plateaus, and rivers cut through.

Topography

Most of the western border of Laos is demarcated by the Mekong river, which is an artery for transportation.[2] The Khone Falls at the southern end of the country prevent access to the sea, and cargo boats travel along the entire length of the Mekong in Laos during most of the year. Smaller power boats and pirogues provide an important means of transportation on many of the tributaries of the Mekong. Prior to the twentieth century, kingdoms and principalities encompassed areas on both sides of the Mekong, and Thai control in the nineteenth century extended to the left bank. While the Mekong was established as a border by French colonial forces, travel from 1 side to the other has been more limited since the establishment of the Lao People's Democratic Republic (LPDR, or Laos) in 1975. The eastern border with Vietnam extends for 2,161 kilometres, mostly along the crest of the Annamite Chain, and serves as a physical barrier between the Chinese-influenced culture of Vietnam and the Indianized states of Laos and Thailand.[2] These mountains are populated by tribal minorities who traditionally have not acknowledged the border with Vietnam any more than lowland Lao have been constrained by the 1,754-kilometre Mekong River border with Thailand. Thus, ethnic minority populations are found on both the Laotian and Vietnamese sides of the frontier. Because of their "relative isolation", contact between these groups and lowland Lao has been mostly confined to trading. Laos shares a 555-kilometre southern border with Cambodia, and Khmer ruins at Wat Pho and other southern locations attest to the history of contact between the Lao and the Khmer. In the north, the country is bounded by a 475-kilometre border with China and shares the 238-kilometre-long Mekong River border with Myanmar.[2] The topography of Laos is "largely mountainous", with the Annamite Range in the northeast and east and the Luang Prabang Range in the northwest, among other ranges typically characterized by "steep terrain". Elevations are typically above 500 metres with river valleys, including the highest point Phou Bia at 2,817 metres.[2] This landscape extends across most of the north of the country, except for the plain of Vientiane and the Plain of Jars in the Xiangkhoang Plateau. The southern "panhandle" of the country contains areas in SavannakhÃ©t and Champasak provinces that are more suited for paddy rice cultivation and livestock raising. Most of Khammouan Province and the eastern part of all the southern provinces are "mountainous". Together, the alluvial plains and terraces of the Mekong and its tributaries cover about 20% of the land area. Approximately 5% of the total land area is classified as arable (as of 2023).[4] The forested land area has declined since the 1970s as a result of commercial logging and expanded swidden, or slash-and-burn, farming, with natural forest cover at about 78% as of 2020 but ongoing annual losses.[5]

Climate

Laos has a tropical climate, with a rainy season from May through October, a cool dry season from November through February, and a hot dry season in March and April. Generally, monsoons occur at the same time across the country, while that time may vary from 1 year to the next. Rainfall is not always adequate for rice cultivation and the average precipitation conceals years where rainfall may be half or less of the norm, causing declines in rice yields. Such droughts sometimes are regional, leaving production in other parts of the country unaffected. Agriculture

Approximately 3.8 million out of 23,080,000 hectares of Laos's land area is potentially suitable for cultivation, [18] and as of 2023, about 1.22 million hectares (5.3% of land area) is arable land. [19] Rice is a crop grown during the rainy season. Agricultural cultivation is possible with varying weather on a portion of land area apart from the Vientiane plain and the lowlands along the Mekong Valley. These cultivated areas are situated in the valleys cut by the rivers or the plateau regions of Xieng Khouang in the North and in the Bolavens in the south. Typically there are only two ways to cultivate: either the wet-field paddy system practiced among the Lao Loum or lowland Lao, or the swidden cultivation system practiced in the hills.[21]

Human geography

As of 2025, the overall population density is 34 persons per square kilometer, and some districts have densities fewer than 10 persons per square kilometer.[22] Agricultural population density is approximately 3.5 persons per cultivated hectare.[23] Urbanization remains low, with 37.9% of the population living in urban areas as of 2021.[2]

Natural resources and environmental issues

The natural resources of Laos include timber, hydropower, gypsum, tin, gold, and gemstones.[2] Laos' economy heavily depends on these, with hydropower and mining being major exports as of 2024. However, exploitation has led to environmental challenges, including deforestation from logging and soil erosion from mining activities. Gold and gemstone mining, often artisanal, contributes to water pollution through mercury use and sedimentation.[2][24] Environmental issues in Laos are significant, driven by rapid development and climate change. Deforestation remains a critical concern, with forest cover declining from 70% in the 1940s to about 40% as of 2020, exacerbated by agricultural expansion and illegal logging. Hydropower development, including over 100 dams on the Mekong River and tributaries, has sparked controversies over impacts on fisheries, sediment flow, and downstream ecosystems in neighboring countries. As of 2025, ongoing projects face criticism for inadequate environmental impact assessments.[25] Air pollution has worsened, with PM2.5 levels frequently exceeding WHO guidelines in 2025, particularly during the dry season due to forest fires, agricultural burning, and transboundary haze. The Global Forest Watch reported 7,671 fire alerts in Laos from June 2024 to June 2025, contributing to health risks and climate change.[26][27] Waste management is another growing issue, with low recycling rates and plastic pollution affecting rivers; the World Bank supported initiatives in 2025 to address solid waste and pollution.[25] The United Nations Development Programme warns: "Protecting the environment and sustainable use of natural resources in Lao PDR is vital for poverty reduction and economic growth."[28] Laos' National Socio-Economic Development Plan (2021â€“2025) emphasizes environmental protection, including biodiversity conservation areas covering 23% of land, but implementation challenges persist due to limited resources and enforcement.[24]
