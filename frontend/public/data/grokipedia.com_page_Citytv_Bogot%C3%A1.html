Citytv Bogotá


Citytv Bogotá is a Colombian free-to-air local television channel owned by Casa Editorial El Tiempo, specializing in Bogotá-focused news, entertainment, and urban lifestyle programming.[1][2]
Launched on March 19, 1999, via UHF channel 21, the station adopted its branding and format under license from the Canadian Citytv network owned by Rogers Media, emphasizing innovative, community-oriented content with live street reporting and real-time coverage of local events.[3][4]
Distinguished by its proximity to everyday life in the capital, Citytv features key programs such as Arriba Bogotá, a morning magazine covering city affairs, and Citynoticias, delivering multiple daily news bulletins with an emphasis on immediacy and viewer interaction, establishing it as a leading voice for bogotanos over its 25 years of operation.[5][1][3]

History

Founding and Launch (1999–2005)

Citytv Bogotá was founded in 1999 by the Casa Editorial El Tiempo (ETCE), Colombia's leading newspaper publisher, as a local free-to-air television channel dedicated to urban news, culture, and lifestyle content tailored to the capital's residents.[6] The initiative licensed the "Citytv" brand from CHUM Limited, a Canadian broadcaster known for its city-focused programming, adapting the format to emphasize Bogotá's daily realities without national scope.[7] Broadcasting began on March 19, 1999, over UHF channel 21 after two months of technical tests and rehearsals, marking the channel's public debut from repurposed facilities in the basement of ETCE's headquarters at Avenida Jiménez and Carrera Séptima.[8] [6] The inaugural transmission included the premiere of Citynoticias, its core news bulletin, produced in integrated studios with on-site editing to enable rapid coverage of local events, under journalism director Juan Lozano and president Iván McAllister.[9] [6] In its early years, Citytv differentiated itself through innovative, community-driven formats, including Sin Cédula, a weekday children's program from 4:30 to 5:00 p.m. that ran until 2005 and featured youth-led segments on city life.[10] Other initial offerings adapted Canadian inspirations like Mucha Música for music and Circo Eléctrico for variety, alongside Bogotá-centric shows such as Noctámbulo for nightlife, Ciudad Total for comprehensive urban reporting, and Sin Amarillo, Azul y Rojo for apolitical sports discussions.[6] This lineup, prioritizing citizen journalism and real-time proximity to viewers, helped establish the channel as a novel alternative to national broadcasters amid Colombia's expanding local TV landscape by 2005.[6]

Evolution and Key Milestones (2006–2015)

In 2006, Citytv Bogotá marked its seventh anniversary with community-oriented events, including a tree-planting initiative at Colonia Oriental led by station personalities and a mural painting collaboration with local artists at the Museo de Arte Moderno's parking lot.[11] These activities underscored the channel's ongoing commitment to participatory programming, highlighted by its pioneering Citycápsula segment, which enabled citizen-submitted video capsules, and Citynoticias, which focused on underreported local stories while adhering to an internal ethics code prohibiting graphic depictions of violence.[11] A significant structural shift occurred in 2007 when Spanish media conglomerate Grupo Planeta acquired a 55% stake in parent company Casa Editorial El Tiempo (CEET) and a 40% interest in Citytv itself through a binding agreement signed that year, injecting capital for potential expansion while retaining local operational control.[12] This partnership aimed to bolster the channel's resources amid Colombia's evolving media landscape, though it preserved Citytv's emphasis on Bogotá-centric content over national syndication. By 2009, Citytv celebrated its tenth anniversary on March 19 with on-location broadcasts of flagship programs from various Bogotá parks, reflecting a decade of growth into a consolidated local broadcaster with national cable distribution and a reputation for agile, ethics-driven journalism.[13] Under general manager Lorencita Santamaría, the channel emphasized cost-effective, high-quality production by a young team, fostering innovations in real-time community reporting that differentiated it from traditional networks.[13] Throughout the period, Citytv maintained its core model of hyper-local news and entertainment, adapting to competitive pressures by prioritizing viewer engagement over sensationalism, though specific technological upgrades like high-definition broadcasting aligned with Colombia's broader digital transition starting in 2008 without unique channel-specific mandates documented.[14] This era solidified its niche as Bogotá's voice, with steady audience loyalty evidenced by anniversary milestones rather than dramatic ratings surges.

Recent Developments (2016–Present)

In 2016–2020, Citytv Bogotá sustained its emphasis on real-time local journalism and community-oriented content amid evolving media consumption patterns, integrating more multimedia elements from its parent company, Casa Editorial El Tiempo, to bolster digital dissemination. The channel's programming retained a focus on Bogotá-specific events, traffic updates, and cultural features, with no reported structural overhauls in broadcast operations or ownership, which remained under El Tiempo's control following prior integrations.[1] The COVID-19 pandemic from 2020 onward prompted adaptations in remote reporting and heightened coverage of public health measures, urban mobility challenges, and social impacts in Bogotá, aligning with the channel's street-level reporting ethos. This period saw increased reliance on social media for viral content distribution, including user-generated stories and live streams, enhancing audience engagement without altering core analog broadcasting.[15] By 2024, Citytv marked its 25th anniversary on March 19, commemorating the original launch with retrospective programming that revisited foundational broadcasts and iconic segments, underscoring its evolution into a digital-savvy outlet while preserving proximity to local narratives. Reflections highlighted persistent viewer loyalty through unfiltered, on-the-ground coverage, distinguishing it from national competitors.[16][17] Into 2025, the channel continued operations via integrated platforms like citytv.eltiempo.com for online streaming, maintaining coverage of municipal developments and daily life without documented shifts in executive leadership or technical infrastructure.[2]

Ownership and Organizational Structure

Corporate Ownership

Citytv Bogotá operates under a local broadcast concession granted to CEETTV S.A., a specialized entity established for television operations and fully controlled by Casa Editorial El Tiempo S.A. (CEET), the publisher of Colombia's leading daily newspaper El Tiempo.[18][19] CEET's ownership structure traces to Luis Carlos Sarmiento Angulo, a Colombian financier whose Grupo Aval Acciones y Valores encompasses banking, real estate, and media assets, making him Colombia's richest individual with a net worth exceeding $14 billion as of recent Forbes assessments.[19] Sarmiento consolidated full control of CEET in 2012 by acquiring the 55% stake previously held by Spain's Grupo Planeta in both CEET and CEETTV S.A., eliminating minority foreign influence in the media holdings.[20] This structure has remained stable through 2025, with no reported changes in shareholding or divestitures.[18] The alignment of Citytv's ownership with Sarmiento's broader conglomerate raises considerations of concentrated media control in Colombia, where a few economic groups dominate news dissemination; however, regulatory filings confirm compliance with national broadcasting laws limiting cross-ownership conflicts.[19] CEETTV S.A. functions autonomously in day-to-day operations but reports strategically to CEET's board, dominated by Sarmiento family interests.[18]

Management and Key Executives

Clemencia Izquierdo Manrique serves as the executive manager (gerente ejecutiva) of Citytv Bogotá and El Tiempo Televisión, responsibilities that include developing growth strategies, sustainability initiatives, brand relaunch efforts, and production executions for the channels.[6][21] Under her leadership since ascending to the role after six years in progressively senior positions, the channel has maintained its focus on local Bogotá coverage, including extended live broadcasts exceeding 17 hours for major events.[1] Operational management is handled by figures such as Jorge Jaramillo, who holds the position of operations manager (gerente de operaciones) at Citytv Bogotá, overseeing day-to-day technical and logistical functions.[22] The channel operates within the broader structure of Casa Editorial El Tiempo S.A., where television divisions report to executive leadership including David Matoses Peraire, gerente general of the parent company since May 2022.[23] Ultimate oversight aligns with ownership by Luis Carlos Sarmiento Angulo through his control of the Casa Editorial El Tiempo group, though day-to-day decisions rest with the operational executives.[19]

Programming

News and Current Affairs

Citytv Bogotá's news and current affairs programming emphasizes local coverage of the capital, including security incidents, urban mobility, citizen complaints, and public policy impacts, often incorporating live reports and community input for immediacy.[2] The channel airs multiple daily newscasts tailored to different time slots, prioritizing verifiable events over speculation, with a focus on Bogotá-specific developments such as crime statistics, traffic disruptions, and municipal announcements.[24] The flagship evening bulletin, CityNoticias de las 8, airs at 8:00 PM weekdays and is anchored by Johnatan Nieto, delivering summaries of daily events with on-site footage from correspondents embedded in high-impact areas like neighborhoods affected by robberies or protests.[24] Complementing this, CityNoticias del Mediodía provides midday updates around noon, covering breaking developments in real-time, such as emergency responses or policy shifts by local authorities.[2] Weekend editions under CityNoticias Fin de Semana, hosted by Rafael Niño, extend coverage to include reflective segments on weekly trends, maintaining the channel's commitment to 24/7 accessibility via live streams. Morning programming includes Arriba Bogotá, led by Aníbal Alvarado, which combines news with practical assistance for viewers, such as reporting on public services and facilitating direct citizen queries to officials.[24] Additional presenters like Lina Pulido contribute across bulletins, enhancing viewer engagement through on-air interactions.[25] For current affairs, the channel features debate-oriented segments like Talk Show ¿Quién tiene la razón?, which examines contentious local issues through moderated discussions, drawing on expert input rather than unsubstantiated opinions.[2] This format, alongside opinion columns tied to parent publisher El Tiempo, aims to contextualize events empirically, though selections reflect editorial priorities favoring urban challenges over national politics unless directly impacting Bogotá.[26] Programming avoids sensationalism, grounding reports in official data and eyewitness accounts to support causal analysis of city dynamics.[27]

Entertainment and Lifestyle Programming

Citytv Bogotá features limited but longstanding entertainment programming, primarily centered on weekend variety shows that blend light-hearted content with family-oriented segments. The flagship program, Bravíssimo, airs Saturdays and Sundays from 7:30 a.m. to 12:00 p.m., delivering a mix of live performances, celebrity interviews, games, cooking recipes, and event coverage designed to engage viewers during morning hours.[28] Launched over two decades ago, the show has maintained a consistent format emphasizing accessible entertainment, including theatrical previews and humorous segments featuring guests like actors Paola Turbay and Diego Trujillo discussing productions such as Doble o nada.[29] Lifestyle elements within Bravíssimo incorporate practical advice through recipes and interviews that touch on daily life topics, though the program prioritizes spectacle over in-depth lifestyle analysis.[30] In late 2024, Citytv introduced La Corona TV, hosted by comedian El Gordo Ariel and presenter Alejandra Serje, focusing on gossip, showbiz news, and comedic commentary in a farándula-style format to expand entertainment offerings.[31] This addition reflects an effort to refresh content with high-energy, personality-driven segments amid competition from broader Colombian networks. Weekday mornings feature Arriba Bogotá, a magazín-style program that, while predominantly informative on local events and transit, includes interview-driven lifestyle glimpses into Bogotá's urban routines and community stories before the city fully awakens.[5][32] However, pure lifestyle programming remains secondary to news, with entertainment slots often filled by syndicated fare like crime dramas rather than original lifestyle series on health, fashion, or wellness. Overall, these offerings target urban audiences seeking casual diversion, though viewership data indicates stronger appeal in gossip and variety over structured lifestyle education.[2]

Special Features and Innovations

Citytv Bogotá pioneered a hyper-local broadcasting model tailored exclusively to the capital's urban dynamics, integrating news with lifestyle segments to reflect daily city experiences such as traffic disruptions and cultural events. This approach features dynamic on-location journalism, where reporters engage directly with occurrences from multiple vantage points, diverging from conventional studio-bound formats prevalent in national Colombian television.[33] A hallmark innovation lies in its real-time mobility reporting, including dedicated segments on road conditions ("Estado de las vías") and public transport updates via TransMilenio, delivered through integrated multimedia elements like live video feeds and interactive maps to aid viewer navigation in Bogotá's congested infrastructure. The channel's programming also incorporates weekend news bulletins that blend breaking developments with cultural highlights, such as live concert announcements, enhancing immediacy and relevance.[2] In recent years, Citytv has advanced its digital ecosystem by introducing podcasts in 2024, including "La Habitación," to extend narrative depth beyond traditional broadcasts and engage audiences with in-depth audio storytelling on local issues. Live streaming capabilities across platforms further enable 24/7 access to content, supporting on-demand consumption of events like public announcements and emergencies. These adaptations reflect a strategic pivot toward multi-platform delivery amid evolving media consumption patterns in Colombia.[34][27]

Operations and Technical Aspects

Broadcast Signal and Coverage

Citytv Bogotá transmits its signal via digital terrestrial television (TDT), following Colombia's nationwide transition from analog broadcasting. The channel originally launched on March 19, 1999, using analog UHF channel 21, which allowed reception via rooftop antennas in the Bogotá area.[35][36] With the adoption of TDT standards, Citytv shifted to digital multiplexing, enabling higher-quality HD transmission and potentially multiple subchannels, though specific virtual channel assignments remain tied to local Bogotá allocations such as UHF physical channel 27 for primary HD service.[37] The broadcast coverage is localized, targeting the Bogotá Capital District and its metropolitan area, extending to adjacent municipalities in the Cundinamarca department through line-of-sight propagation from transmitters positioned to optimize urban and peri-urban reception.[38] This geographic focus aligns with the channel's emphasis on Bogotá-centric news and events, with signal strength sufficient for reliable over-the-air viewing in densely populated zones but diminishing in remote or obstructed terrains typical of the Andean highlands. Viewers require compatible TDT antennas and decoders to access the free-to-air signal, as mandated by Colombia's Ministry of Information and Communications Technology (MinTIC) for post-analog households.[39] Reception quality depends on factors such as elevation, interference from buildings, and weather, with primary service radii estimated at 50-70 kilometers from central Bogotá transmitters, though exact power outputs and ERP (effective radiated power) figures are regulated by MinTIC and not publicly detailed for this licensee. Beyond terrestrial broadcast, the signal is redistributed via cable, satellite, and IPTV providers nationwide, but over-the-air access remains core to its public service mandate as a licensed open television operator.[39]

Production Facilities and Technology

Citytv Bogotá's production facilities are currently located in central Bogotá, sharing infrastructure with the El Tiempo newspaper offices, including studios for news programs such as Arriba Bogotá.[33] The master control room operates from a separate site in the city's historic center, facilitating integrated operations between print and broadcast divisions.[40] The channel maintains high-definition (HD) broadcast capabilities, established prior to 2020, supporting efficient content distribution.[40] For field production, Citytv employs shoulder-mounted cameras equipped with multipurpose broadcast lenses optimized for live reporting.[40] Audio workflows incorporate digital mixing consoles, including a Soundcraft Vi4 system acquired in 2011 for enhanced mixing during entertainment and news segments.[41] Post-production efficiency has been improved through automation, such as the 2020 implementation of Enco's enCaption system for real-time subtitle generation, projecting a 24-month return on investment.[42] Early adoption of Panasonic P2 solid-state media technology replaced traditional tape-based systems, streamlining archival and editing processes.[40] Plans for modernization included a 2020 upgrade to news studio matrices, switchers, and distribution systems, though implementation was deferred due to operational and regulatory delays.[40] In preparation for relocation, Casa Editorial El Tiempo—Citytv's parent entity—announced a new headquarters in Bogotá's Fontibón district, near El Dorado International Airport, set for completion in the first half of 2026.[43] This 15,000 m² complex features two buildings: one for printing operations and the other with six floors of offices, open workspaces, and a dedicated TV production terrace for live broadcasts and content creation, emphasizing sustainable construction technologies.[43]

Reception and Impact

Audience Metrics and Viewership

Citytv Bogotá has experienced fluctuating but notable viewership, with recent data highlighting its rise in national rankings. In November 2024, the channel reached a historic peak by becoming the third most viewed television network in Colombia, overtaking Canal 1 amid programming changes on the latter, as reported in daily audience measurements.[44] This positioned Citytv behind only the dominant Caracol and RCN networks, which together command the majority of open and pay TV audiences.[45] Key programs drove this performance; on November 21, 2024, City Noticias achieved a rating of 1.2, followed by CSI: Las Vegas at 0.7 and Amor Invencible at 0.4, contributing to the channel's overall lead over competitors that day.[46] In Bogotá, where the channel holds an over-the-air signal, similar metrics persisted into 2025, with programs recording ratings of 0.8 to 1.2 in July, reflecting sustained local engagement amid urban-focused content.[47] Within the pay television sector, Citytv captured a 6.72% audience share among cable channels as of June 2025, trailing leaders like Claro Video but ahead of sports and international outlets.[48] Historical highs include City Noticias at 8 p.m. attaining a record 6.84% rating in September 2013, ranking eighth among all Colombian programs.[49] Entertainment segments have also shown growth, with Bravíssimo reporting an 8% increase in viewership for 2023, underscoring the channel's appeal in lifestyle programming.[50] Audience data, primarily tracked by Kantar Ibope Media, indicates Citytv's strength in niche urban demographics rather than broad national dominance, with Bogotá penetration bolstered by its free-to-air availability and emphasis on local news.[51] While exact 2025 national shares remain unpublished in public reports, the channel's mid-tier status persists, benefiting from digital extensions like online streams that supplement traditional metrics.

Critical Reception and Influence

Citytv Bogotá has been praised for its innovative journalistic style, characterized by standing presenters, handheld cameras, and a dynamic, urban tone that emphasizes on-the-ground reporting and diversity in coverage. This approach, adapted from the Canadian Citytv format since its launch in 1999, was described as "innovador en ideas, diseño visual, promociones, estéticas, tono urbano" and effective in highlighting Bogotá's multifaceted realities.[52] [1] The channel's commitment to local stories has positioned it as a symbol of the city, embedding its fast-paced, reporter-driven narrative in the collective perception of Bogotá's daily life.[1] Critical acclaim is evidenced by multiple awards from the Premios India Catalina, Colombia's premier television honors. In 2025, Citytv secured four awards, including for best local or regional newscast, recognizing its role as "la voz del pueblo" amid competition from national networks.[53] [54] Earlier, City Noticias earned its twelfth India Catalina for best local news in 2023, underscoring sustained excellence in regional journalism.[55] These accolades reflect industry validation of its production quality and relevance, with seven nominations in 2025 alone across key categories.[56] The channel's influence extends to shaping audience habits in Bogotá, where it has driven significant viewership growth and maintained a strong local footprint. Its audience share rose 23.4% from 2006 to 2007, marking the highest increase among open-access channels in the capital.[4] City Noticias programs have achieved peak ratings, such as 6.84% for the 8 p.m. edition in 2013, ranking among the top-viewed shows nationally despite its regional focus.[49] With an estimated 13% national audience share emphasizing Bogotá content, Citytv has reinforced urban media trends toward localized, immediate reporting, influencing how residents engage with city-specific issues like security and mobility.[57]

Controversies and Criticisms

Journalistic Practices and Bias Allegations

Citytv Bogotá's journalistic practices are characterized by an informal, urban-oriented style that prioritizes immediacy and citizen engagement, with reporters using handheld cameras for street-level live coverage and presenters delivering news in a conversational manner while standing. This format, which emerged in the channel's early development, aims to reflect Bogotá's dynamic environment by focusing on local issues such as mobility, security, and social events, often integrating direct public testimonials to enhance relatability.[1][33] The approach emphasizes rapid production cycles, with programs like City Noticias featuring extended emissions—up to 46 minutes with multiple short segments—to cover breaking developments in real time, distinguishing it from more structured national broadcasts.[58] This hands-on methodology has solidified Citytv's role as a primary source for Bogotá-specific reporting, though it has drawn occasional criticism for potential errors in high-pressure live scenarios, as seen in a 2024 incident where a reporter misstated details on air, prompting public backlash that the journalist dismissed as minor.[59] Allegations of ideological bias against Citytv remain sparse and anecdotal, with no documented cases of systematic manipulation or editorial skew substantiated by regulatory bodies or independent audits. Some social media users have attributed perceived political leanings to the channel's affiliation with Casa Editorial El Tiempo, claiming alignment with establishment views in coverage of national figures, but these assertions lack empirical backing and appear driven by partisan discontent rather than evidence of distortion.[60] In contrast to national outlets like RCN, which faced 2012 accusations from Reporters Without Borders of lacking objectivity, Citytv has avoided similar institutional rebukes, suggesting its local focus mitigates broader ideological scrutiny.[61] Critics have instead highlighted vulnerabilities in field practices, where the emphasis on on-site reporting exposes journalists to risks, as evidenced by repeated aggressions during protests—such as a 2016 police altercation, a 2025 verbal confrontation with councilor Julián Forero, and vandalism-related threats in March 2025—raising questions about safety protocols without implicating content bias.[62][63][64] These incidents underscore operational challenges in maintaining impartiality amid volatile events, though they reflect external pressures more than inherent channel practices.

Legal and Ethical Issues

In February 2005, the Comisión Nacional de Televisión (CNTV) imposed a fine of 125 million Colombian pesos on Citytv for violating content regulations through two investigative reports aired on November 3 and 5, 2004.[65] The reports, focused on activities at La Bombonera establishment in Cartagena, were deemed by the CNTV to include pornographic material unsuitable for broadcast, infringing prohibitions on explicit imagery in open television.[65] Citytv was entitled to appeal the decision following formal notification, though the channel maintained the content served a journalistic purpose in exposing local issues.[65] The incident highlighted tensions between investigative reporting and regulatory standards on decency, with the CNTV framing the sanction as a measure to enforce responsibility rather than censorship.[65] No further major regulatory fines or legal actions against Citytv for content violations have been documented since, amid a shift to oversight by the Autoridad Nacional de Televisión (ANTV) post-2011. An administrative investigation into CEETTV S.A. (Citytv's operating entity) in 2021 concluded without merit or sanction in 2022.[66] Ethically, Citytv's fast-paced, street-level journalism has drawn academic critique for occasionally prioritizing audience engagement and speed over rigorous verification, risking errors in live broadcasts.[33] Experts such as Javier Darío Restrepo have emphasized journalism's duty to prioritize human subjects over stylistic flair, implicitly questioning formats like Citytv's that blend news with entertainment elements.[33] The channel's editorial principles affirm respect for privacy and rejection of sensationalism, yet its emphasis on real-time urban reporting has sparked debates on balancing public interest with potential intrusions.[33] No systemic ethical violations leading to professional sanctions have been upheld by Colombian journalism oversight bodies.
