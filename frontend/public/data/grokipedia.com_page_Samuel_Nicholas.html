Samuel Nicholas

Samuel Nicholas (c. 1744 – August 27, 1790) was an American military officer who became the first commissioned officer of the Continental Marines, serving as their senior leader throughout the Revolutionary War and effectively functioning as the inaugural commandant of what would become the United States Marine Corps.[1][2] Born in Philadelphia to a Quaker family, Nicholas operated the Conestoga Wagon Tavern and initially adhered to pacifist principles, but he was disowned by the Society of Friends in 1776 for pursuing military training amid rising tensions with Britain.[3][4] On November 28, 1775, the Continental Congress appointed him captain of Marines, tasking him with recruiting the initial two battalions from Philadelphia's taverns, including the famed Tun Tavern, to support naval operations against British forces.[1][5][6] Nicholas led approximately 300 Marines in the Continental Navy's first amphibious assault, the raid on New Providence in the Bahamas in 1776, where they captured military stores and demonstrated the Corps' amphibious capabilities despite limited strategic gains.[7] Promoted to major, he commanded Marine detachments in subsequent campaigns, including service under George Washington at the Battles of Trenton and Princeton in late 1776 and early 1777, contributing to key victories that bolstered the Patriot cause.[8] Remaining the ranking Marine officer until the war's end, Nicholas focused on recruitment, training, and expeditionary roles, laying foundational precedents for the Marine Corps' emphasis on versatility in combined arms operations.[9] He died in Philadelphia during a yellow fever outbreak, buried in the Arch Street Friends' graveyard.[10]

Early Life

Family Background and Quaker Upbringing

Samuel Nicholas was born in 1744 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, the only son of Anthony Nicholas, a blacksmith, and Mary Shute Nicholas.[11][12] The family belonged to the Quaker community, with Anthony Nicholas achieving sufficient prosperity through his trade to support his son's early education despite his death in 1751.[11] Raised in Philadelphia, the epicenter of colonial Quakerism, Nicholas grew up immersed in the Society of Friends' tenets of pacifism, plain living, and communal worship through monthly meetings.[4] Quaker education emphasized moral discipline and practical skills over martial pursuits, shaping a worldview centered on peace testimony that explicitly forbade bearing arms or participating in warfare.[4] The family's Quaker standing faced challenges early, as Anthony Nicholas was disowned by the Philadelphia Monthly Meeting in 1749 for "evil conduct," reflecting lapses in adherence to the society's strict behavioral codes.[4] This backdrop foreshadowed Nicholas's own expulsion from the meeting on February 23, 1776, for "associating to learn the art of war," highlighting the tension between his upbringing and emerging revolutionary commitments.[4]

Education and Pre-Revolutionary Career

Samuel Nicholas was born on March 27, 1744, in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, to Andrew Nicholas, a Quaker shopkeeper and wheelwright, and Mary Shute Nicholas.[3][13] Raised in the pacifist Quaker tradition amid Philadelphia's growing colonial society, Nicholas received his early education in local schools before attending the Philadelphia Academy, established in 1751 as a preparatory institution emphasizing classical studies, mathematics, and practical sciences.[14] Nicholas graduated from the Philadelphia Academy in 1759 at the age of 15, having completed a curriculum that prepared young men for mercantile or public life rather than advanced scholarship.[14][15] Following graduation, he secured employment with a Philadelphia fishing firm, leveraging family connections in the city's commercial networks.[14] In 1760, at age 16, he gained admission to the exclusive Schuylkill Fishing Company, a prestigious gentlemen's club founded in 1732 for sportsmanship, social networking, and fowling along the Schuylkill River, which served as an elite venue for Philadelphia's merchant class and reflected his emerging social standing.[14][15] By the mid-1760s, Nicholas had transitioned into independent business pursuits, aligning with Philadelphia's tavern culture central to colonial commerce and community life. In 1768, he married Mary Ann Jenkins, daughter of a local brewer, acquiring through her dowry the proprietorship of the Conestoga Wagon Tavern (also known as the Conestoga Wagon Inn) at Second and Norris Streets.[3][16] As tavern keeper, Nicholas managed a establishment frequented by wagoneers, merchants, and locals, handling liquor sales, lodging, and informal gatherings that foreshadowed its later role in revolutionary organizing, though prior to 1775 it primarily supported his livelihood in the pre-war economy.[3][5] His Quaker upbringing persisted in this period, with no recorded military involvement, though the society's pacifism would later conflict with his wartime service.[13]

Entry into Military Service

Commissioning as Captain of Marines

On November 5, 1775, the Second Continental Congress selected Samuel Nicholas, a Philadelphia tavern owner from a Quaker family, as the first "Captain of Marines" to lead the newly authorized marine force intended for service aboard Continental Navy vessels.[10] This appointment preceded the formal resolution passed on November 10, 1775, which authorized the raising of two battalions of marines comprising 50 companies, each with one captain, to provide shipboard security, boarding parties, and amphibious assault capabilities against British naval forces.[17] Nicholas's selection likely stemmed from his local prominence in Philadelphia, where the Congress convened, and his reputation as a reliable figure managing the Conestoga Wagon Tavern, which facilitated recruitment among working-class Philadelphians.[5] Nicholas received his official written commission as Captain on November 28, 1775, marking the inaugural officer appointment in what would become the Continental Marines.[11] In this role, he was tasked with immediate recruitment efforts, enlisting the first marines from Philadelphia's taverns and docks, emphasizing sturdy recruits suitable for naval infantry duties.[10] By early December 1775, under his command, approximately 300 marines had been raised and organized into companies, with Nicholas personally overseeing their mustering and initial training before detachment to ships like the USS Alfred under Commodore Esek Hopkins.[18] This commissioning established Nicholas as the senior marine officer, a position he retained throughout the Revolutionary War without formal peers in rank until his later promotion.[17]

Recruitment and Organization of Continental Marines

On November 10, 1775, the Second Continental Congress in Philadelphia passed a resolution to raise two battalions of Marines for service as landing forces and shipboard security in the Continental Navy, specifying a structure of one colonel, two lieutenant colonels, two majors, nine captains, sixteen first lieutenants, and sixteen second lieutenants.[5] The resolution aimed to provide disciplined troops capable of amphibious assaults and suppressing mutinies at sea, drawing from the British Royal Marines model but adapted for the revolutionary cause.[19] Samuel Nicholas, a 31-year-old Philadelphia Quaker with local prominence as a tavern proprietor, was commissioned as the first captain of Marines on November 28, 1775, by Congress president John Hancock, positioning him as the senior officer responsible for initial implementation.[20] Nicholas immediately initiated recruitment in Philadelphia, establishing operations at Tun Tavern on Water Street, where he enlisted Robert Mullan, the tavern's owner and a fellow patriot, as his first subordinate captain and primary recruiter.[21] Efforts focused on enlisting able-bodied men, skilled marksmen, and experienced seamen from the city's laboring classes and waterfront, offering daily pay of six shillings for privates to attract volunteers amid economic incentives and patriotic appeals.[22] By early December 1775, Nicholas had recruited approximately 300 Marines, forming companies rather than full battalions due to the urgent needs of naval operations under Commodore Esek Hopkins.[17] These recruits underwent basic training in musket drill, shipboard discipline, and small-unit tactics at Philadelphia barracks, with Nicholas overseeing procurement of uniforms, muskets, and bayonets from local suppliers.[18] The organization emphasized mobility and firepower for detached service on vessels like the Alfred, prioritizing combat effectiveness over rigid formation structures, which allowed rapid deployment but limited cohesive battalion-level command throughout the war.[2]

Revolutionary War Campaigns

The Nassau Expedition and Amphibious Raid

The Continental Navy's first squadron, commanded by Commodore Esek Hopkins, departed from the Delaware Capes on February 17, 1776, with Captain Samuel Nicholas leading approximately 234 Marines aboard the vessels Alfred, Columbus, Cabot, Providence, and Andrew Doria, among others.[23] The primary objective was to seize gunpowder and military stores from British-held ports in the Chesapeake or southern colonies, driven by acute shortages of munitions in the American forces; Nicholas's Marines, recently recruited and organized under congressional authorization from November 1775, were tasked with amphibious assaults to secure these supplies.[24][25] After capturing two British sloops en route, which revealed stores of gunpowder on New Providence island in the Bahamas, Hopkins redirected the fleet to Nassau, arriving offshore on March 1, 1776.[26] On March 3, Nicholas commanded a landing force of about 250 Marines and sailors who advanced under cover of naval gunfire, capturing Fort Montagu—defended by roughly 100 militia with six guns—after brief resistance that wounded one Marine and killed another, marking the first U.S. Marine combat fatality.[24][27] The force then occupied Nassau unopposed, as Governor Montfort Browne had evacuated key supplies, including 162 barrels of gunpowder, aboard the schooner Mary and HMS St. John the night before.[27] American forces secured Fort Nassau and the town for several days, loading captured materiel including 24 to 38 casks of remaining gunpowder, 72 to 88 cannons ranging from 9- to 32-pounders, 15 brass mortars, over 16,500 cannonballs, and 38,240 pounds of round shot onto the ships.[25][28] Hopkins's decision to withdraw by March 17, prompted by intelligence of approaching British reinforcements from Jamaica, limited the haul but demonstrated the Marines' capability in combined naval-amphibious operations; the raid yielded no significant British casualties beyond the initial skirmish and provided the Continental Army with critical, albeit reduced, ordnance amid ongoing powder shortages.[23][29]

Promotion to Major and Philadelphia Defense

Following the Nassau Raid in March 1776, Samuel Nicholas returned to Philadelphia, where the Continental Congress promoted him to major on June 25, 1776, and designated him the senior officer commanding the Continental Marines.[11] This elevation reflected his leadership in the recent amphibious operation and positioned him to oversee recruitment, training, and deployment of Marine forces amid escalating British threats.[17] Nicholas promptly organized three companies totaling 131 Marines, later reduced to 80 men, for field service while maintaining headquarters in Philadelphia.[17] In December 1776, he marched these units to join General George Washington's army across the Delaware River, contributing to the surprise attacks at Trenton on December 26 and Princeton on January 3, 1777, which bolstered American morale and secured the region temporarily.[13] By mid-1777, as British General William Howe's army advanced from the Chesapeake Bay toward Philadelphia, Nicholas focused on fortifying the city's defenses, particularly along the Delaware River to counter potential naval incursions.[30] Continental Marines under his command manned key positions, including river forts like Mifflin on Mud Island, where they resisted British attempts to clear the waterway in October 1777, destroying the frigate HMS Augusta and delaying supply lines despite the forts' eventual fall.[31] Four companies of Marines integrated into Washington's forces participated in the broader Philadelphia campaign, contesting Howe's movements at battles such as Brandywine and Germantown, though the city was occupied by British troops on September 26, 1777.[32] Nicholas evacuated with the Continental Congress to York, Pennsylvania, preserving Marine organization and records during the occupation, which lasted until June 1778.[13] His oversight ensured the Marines' role in riverine and coastal defense highlighted their versatility beyond shipboard duties, even as ground forces bore the brunt of the failed campaign to hold the patriot capital.[33]

Service in Continental Army Campaigns (1776–1779)

Following the Nassau Raid, Major Samuel Nicholas commanded a battalion of approximately 300 Continental Marines attached to the Continental Army under General George Washington for the New Jersey Campaign in late 1776.[8] On December 21, 1776, Nicholas marched three companies—totaling about 131 men—to rendezvous with Washington near McConkey's Ferry on the Delaware River.[33] These forces, supplemented by detachments from Continental Navy ships such as the Andrea Doria, Hancock, and Montgomery, brought the total Marine contingent to roughly 600 men operating as infantry.[8] Nicholas's Marines crossed the Delaware River with Washington's army on the night of December 25–26, 1776, enduring severe weather to surprise and defeat Hessian forces at the Battle of Trenton, capturing over 1,000 prisoners with minimal American casualties, including few confirmed losses among the Marines.[8] On January 2, 1777, during the Second Battle of Trenton (also known as the Battle of Assunpink Creek), Nicholas's battalion helped repel a British advance led by Lord Cornwallis, holding defensive positions along the creek.[33] The following day, January 3, at the Battle of Princeton, Nicholas led a spearhead assault against approximately 1,200 British regulars, contributing to a decisive American victory that inflicted over 400 enemy casualties while sustaining light losses.[8][33] By this point, his original three companies had been reduced to about 80 effectives due to detachments and attrition.[17] In the ensuing weeks, Nicholas's Marines continued skirmishing in New Jersey as both infantry and artillery support until early February 1777, when the battalion transitioned primarily to artillery duties and prisoner escort convoys, including those from Trenton and Princeton.[8][17] Some units wintered at Morristown, New Jersey, while Nicholas coordinated operations amid the British occupation of Philadelphia from September 1777 to June 1778, relocating his headquarters to New London, Connecticut.[33] By June 1778, with Philadelphia evacuated, Nicholas reestablished Marine barracks there, resumed recruiting, and served as muster master for Continental forces.[33] In October 1777, two Marine sergeants under his command, William Hamilton and Alexander Neilson, received battlefield commissions as second lieutenants—the first such promotions from the enlisted ranks.[33] Through 1779, Nicholas's Marines remained in support roles with the Army, though no major field engagements are recorded; on November 20, 1779, he unsuccessfully requested command of a Marine detachment for the frigate America.[33]

Post-War Civilian Life

Return to Philadelphia and Business Pursuits

Following the disbandment of the Continental Marines in 1783, Samuel Nicholas returned to Philadelphia and resumed management of the Conestoga Wagon, the tavern he had operated prior to the war, which his wife's family had established.[3][16] This establishment, located in the city, catered to locals and travelers, continuing as a venue for social gatherings amid Nicholas's reintegration into civilian commerce.[34] Nicholas's business activities remained centered on the tavern's operations, leveraging his pre-war experience as a proprietor to sustain his livelihood in the post-war economy, though specific financial records or expansions are not well-documented.[17] He maintained involvement in Philadelphia's mercantile and social circles until his death on August 27, 1790, during a yellow fever epidemic that claimed numerous lives in the city.[17][11]

Family and Personal Affairs

Samuel Nicholas was born around 1744 in Philadelphia to Andrew Nicholas, a blacksmith, and Mary Shute Nicholas, members of a prominent Quaker family.[17][10] His father died in 1751, leaving Nicholas as the family's primary male heir.[11] Nicholas married Mary Jenkins, daughter of Philadelphia innkeeper Charles Jenkins and Mary Gray, in a Quaker ceremony on March 5, 1778.[11][35] As part of the marriage settlement, Nicholas acquired proprietorship of the Conestoga Wagon Tavern, a popular establishment previously operated by his father-in-law.[17][13] The couple had at least four children, including sons Samuel (born 1780) and Charles Jenkins Nicholas, and daughter Mary Jenkins "Molly" Nicholas (born 1787).[36][17] Genealogical records indicate possibly additional sons such as William and daughters including Sarah, though primary historical accounts confirm the named offspring.[37][38] Prior to his military service, Nicholas pursued personal interests in equestrian and outdoor activities, joining the Schuylkill Angling Organization—a fishing and shooting club—in 1759 and founding the Gloucester Fox Chasing Club in 1766.[10] He affiliated with the Freemasons, attending meetings at Tun Tavern in Philadelphia, and later became an original member of the Pennsylvania Society of the Cincinnati after the war.[39][10] His Quaker ties ended formally on February 23, 1776, when the Philadelphia Meeting disowned him for "associating to learn the art of war."[4]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Samuel Nicholas died on August 27, 1790, in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, during a yellow fever epidemic that killed thousands in the city, including many prominent residents.[40][10] He was approximately 46 years old at the time, having returned to civilian pursuits such as innkeeping and horse trading after the disbandment of the Continental Marines in 1783.[18] His funeral occurred the following day, August 28, as noted in the diary of his friend Jacob Hiltzheimer, who attended after morning obligations.[11] Nicholas was interred in the Friends Graveyard at the Arch Street Meeting House, a Quaker burial ground reflecting his Philadelphia Quaker roots, despite prior excommunication from the Society of Friends for military involvement.[41][12] The exact location of his grave is unknown, with no original marker; a memorial stone was installed much later by the Marine Corps.[13] Nicholas died intestate, without a will, necessitating probate proceedings for his estate, which included business assets but no immediate public commemorations tied to his military service, as the Continental Marines had long been dissolved.[42] His passing received scant contemporary notice beyond local circles, amid the chaos of the epidemic that claimed over 4,000 lives in Philadelphia that year.[14]

Historical Assessment and Legacy

Role in Founding the U.S. Marine Corps

On November 10, 1775, the Second Continental Congress passed a resolution authorizing the formation of two battalions of Marines to serve as sea soldiers aboard Continental Navy ships, marking the birth of the Continental Marines, the direct predecessor to the United States Marine Corps.[21] Samuel Nicholas, a 31-year-old Philadelphia merchant with local prominence and no prior military experience, was selected to lead this new force due to his connections in the city, which facilitated rapid recruitment.[18] That same day, Nicholas began enlisting the first Marines at Tun Tavern, a prominent Philadelphia inn, establishing it as a symbolic birthplace of the Corps.[21] Nicholas received his formal commission as the first Captain of Marines on November 28, 1775, from the Continental Congress, predating other Marine officers and granting him seniority.[1] Under his command, he raised approximately 300 Marines in Philadelphia, organizing them into companies equipped for shipboard duties and amphibious operations, including providing security against mutiny and conducting boarding actions.[10] As the senior officer, Nicholas effectively served as the de facto commandant of the Continental Marines, directing their initial training and deployment, which laid the foundational structure and traditions of the force, such as emphasis on discipline and versatility in naval contexts.[18] Following the Revolutionary War, the Continental Marines were disbanded in 1783, but their reestablishment on July 11, 1798, under the Navy Department explicitly traced lineage to the 1775 battalions, with Nicholas recognized in Marine Corps tradition as the inaugural commandant by virtue of his precedence.[1] His role in assembling and leading the original contingent ensured the persistence of Marine identity as an elite naval infantry unit, influencing the Corps' enduring ethos despite the interim dissolution.[10]

Recognition and Honors

Nicholas is traditionally recognized as the first Commandant of the United States Marine Corps, a title derived from his role as the senior Continental Marine officer commissioned on November 28, 1775, and retained until the Corps' disbandment in 1783.[43][10] This honor reflects his leadership in recruiting, organizing, and commanding the initial Marine force, despite the formal Commandant position not being established until 1798.[13] In tribute to his service, three United States Navy ships have borne the name USS Nicholas: a Fletcher-class destroyer (DD-449) commissioned on March 30, 1942, and decommissioned on November 29, 1963; a Knox-class frigate (FF-1011/FFG-47) commissioned on March 11, 1979, and decommissioned on July 16, 2009; and references to additional vessels honoring his legacy in naval nomenclature.[10] Posthumous commemorations include annual wreath-laying ceremonies at his grave in the Arch Street Friends Meeting House Cemetery, Philadelphia, such as the event on October 14, 2025, as part of Navy-Marine Corps 250th anniversary observances emphasizing his foundational contributions to Marine expeditionary traditions.[44] The Marine Corps Association established the Samuel Nicholas Society in his name to recognize planned giving supporters, underscoring his enduring status as a model of Marine leadership and adaptability.[43]

Scholarly Evaluations and Debates

Historians assess Samuel Nicholas primarily as the senior Continental Marine officer whose recruitment and leadership efforts laid the groundwork for the United States Marine Corps, though his formal role as "Commandant" is a postwar tradition rather than a Revolutionary-era title. As the first commissioned Marine captain on November 28, 1775, Nicholas raised two battalions totaling around 300 men, emphasizing disciplined infantry for naval service, which scholars credit with establishing early Marine ethos despite the force's small scale and integration into broader Continental Army operations.[18] [10] Evaluations of his tactical contributions highlight the 1776 Nassau expedition as a pioneering amphibious operation, where Nicholas's 234 Marines secured gunpowder and munitions with minimal casualties, demonstrating effective coordination with naval forces under Esek Hopkins; however, analysts note the raid's limited strategic disruption to British operations, attributing greater long-term significance to its morale-boosting effect on American forces.[45] Later campaigns, including service at Trenton and Princeton in 1776–1777, saw Nicholas's Marines perform reliably in combined arms roles, yet historians like Jason W. Smith argue that their dispersal into army units diluted Marine identity, with Nicholas transitioning to quasi-administrative duties amid organizational flux.[46] [45] Debates among scholars center on the mythic elements of Marine origins, particularly the Tun Tavern recruitment narrative, which tradition ties to Nicholas but which recent works contextualize within Philadelphia's wider Quaker and merchant networks rather than a singular founding moment.[46] Some question Nicholas's postwar obscurity and financial ruin—dying indebted in 1790 after running an inn and facing creditor suits—as evidence of inadequate institutional foresight, contrasting with the Corps' 1798 revival under different leadership; others counter that wartime exigencies, not personal failing, precluded lasting structures, viewing his legacy as symbolic of adaptability over institutional permanence. These interpretations underscore a consensus on Nicholas's personal valor but diverge on whether his efforts presaged a distinct Marine service or merely supplemented naval needs.[10]
