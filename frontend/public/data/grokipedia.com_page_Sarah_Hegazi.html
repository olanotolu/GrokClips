Sarah Hegazi


Sarah Hegazi (1989 – 14 June 2020) was an Egyptian feminist and political activist known for raising a rainbow flag during a concert by the Lebanese band Mashrou' Leila in Cairo on 22 September 2017, an act that led to her arrest by Egyptian authorities on charges including joining an illegal group.[1] She was detained for three months, during which she reported experiencing torture and sexual assault, before release on bail amid ongoing legal proceedings under Egypt's anti-debauchery laws that target homosexual conduct.[1] Hegazi subsequently fled Egypt and received asylum in Canada in 2018, where she continued advocacy work but struggled with severe post-traumatic stress disorder stemming from her imprisonment.[2] Her death by suicide in Toronto drew widespread mourning among activists, highlighting the personal toll of state repression against dissent in Egypt.[1][2]

Early Life and Activism

Childhood and Education

Sarah Hegazi was born on October 1, 1989, in Cairo, Egypt, to a conservative middle-class family.[3] As the eldest of four siblings, she assumed early responsibilities after her father's death during her youth, assisting her mother in raising the younger children.[4][5] Hegazi completed a bachelor's degree in information systems in 2010, after which she worked as a software developer.[6][7] She pursued additional professional training at the American University in Cairo's Continuing Education Center in 2016.[8]

Initial Political Involvement

Hegazi's initial political engagement occurred in the post-2011 Egyptian revolutionary context, where she aligned with leftist movements emphasizing social justice and opposition to authoritarianism. She became a member of the Bread and Freedom Party (Hizb al-Ard wa al-Hurriyya), a democratic socialist organization founded by dissident activists to promote wealth redistribution, workers' rights, and democratic reforms amid the country's political turmoil.[9][10] As a party supporter, Hegazi advocated for the release of political prisoners held under President Abdel Fattah al-Sisi's administration, which had intensified crackdowns on dissent following the 2013 military coup. Her activities included criticizing Sisi's authoritarian measures, such as the 2019 constitutional amendments that extended his presidency potentially until 2030, reflecting her commitment to combating military rule and economic inequality.[10][9] Hegazi identified ideologically as a communist, integrating class-based analysis into her early activism, which laid the groundwork for her later intersectional focus on feminism and queer issues within Egypt's repressive environment. This involvement marked her transition from personal interests in social justice—pursued through self-study in feminism and diversity after her 2010 university graduation—to organized opposition against the regime's suppression of leftist voices.[11][10]

Ideological Positions

Socialist and Feminist Beliefs

Sarah Hegazi was a member of the Bread and Freedom Party, a democratic socialist organization in Egypt focused on advocating for workers' rights, political freedoms, and opposition to authoritarian rule following the 2011 revolution.[9][10] Her socialist commitments extended to solidarity with political prisoners, as she campaigned for their release and critiqued economic inequalities under Egypt's post-2013 regime.[9] Hegazi aligned with leftist groups, including the Revolutionary Socialists, who mourned her as a comrade dedicated to combating oppression through class struggle and anti-imperialist positions, such as support for Palestinian causes.[12][13] She self-identified as a feminist, integrating gender equality into her broader political activism against patriarchal structures in Egyptian society.[14] In discussions, Hegazi challenged misogynistic ideas in philosophy, such as Friedrich Nietzsche's derogatory characterizations of women, while drawing on feminist thinkers like Nawal El-Saadawi to argue for women's autonomy and resistance to cultural conservatism.[13] Her Instagram profile encapsulated this stance, declaring her as "super communist, super gay and feminist," reflecting an intersectional approach that linked feminism to anti-capitalist and queer liberation efforts.[15] Hegazi's writings and public engagements emphasized dismantling hierarchical power dynamics, including those rooted in class, gender, and sexuality, though she prioritized empirical critiques of state repression over abstract ideological debates.[11][1]

Views on LGBTQ Issues

Sarah Hegazi identified as homosexual and advocated for the normalization of LGBTQ identities in Egypt, where such expressions are criminalized under charges of "debauchery" and face widespread societal condemnation.[16] Her act of raising the rainbow flag—a symbol of sexual and gender diversity—on September 22, 2017, during a Mashrou' Leila concert in Cairo was a deliberate public endorsement of queer visibility, intended as an expression of personal and collective pride.[16] [17] In a 2018 interview reflecting on the incident one year later, Hegazi affirmed, "We were proud to hold the flag. We wouldn’t have imagined the reaction of society and the Egyptian state. For them, I was a criminal—someone who was seeking to destroy the moral structure of society."[17] She rejected portrayals of homosexuality as a disease or moral aberration, recounting how interrogators during her detention conflated it with communism or crimes like pedophilia to rationalize abuse, including electrocution.[16] This reflected her broader contention that official ignorance and prejudice served as pretexts for state violence rather than genuine ethical concerns.[18] Hegazi framed homophobia as a strategic alliance between the Egyptian regime under President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi and Islamist elements, who exploited religious conservatism to suppress political opposition and enforce conformity.[16] She argued that the state's puritanical rhetoric—"protecting religion and social morality"—aligned it with societal bigotry, particularly against non-heterosexual individuals who deviated from Sunni male norms, thereby perpetuating cycles of exclusion tied to authoritarian control.[16] [14] In her view, this dynamic extended beyond sexuality to intersect with class oppression and patriarchal structures, positioning queer rights as inseparable from her socialist and feminist commitments to dismantling intersecting forms of power.[1] While in exile in Canada after her 2018 release, Hegazi sustained her advocacy through writings and social media, critiquing Egypt's conservative middle class for amplifying stigma and endorsing global solidarity efforts, such as support for Black Lives Matter, to highlight shared struggles against systemic injustice.[14] Her persistence underscored a belief in individual freedoms as antithetical to the regime's tools of division, though she noted the limited support from civil society compared to the unified backlash from state and religious authorities.[16]

Egyptian Context for Homosexuality

Legal Framework and Prosecutions

Egypt lacks explicit legislation criminalizing consensual same-sex sexual activity between adults in private, but authorities routinely apply broader provisions of the penal code and related statutes to target individuals perceived as homosexual.[19][20] The primary instrument is Law No. 10 of 1961 on Combating Prostitution, particularly Article 9(c), which penalizes "habitual debauchery" (fujur)—interpreted by prosecutors and courts to encompass repeated homosexual acts—with imprisonment ranging from six months to three years and fines.[19] This requires evidence of at least two acts within a three-year period to establish habituality, though convictions often rely on coerced confessions rather than direct proof. Supplementary charges under Penal Code Article 178, prohibiting incitement to debauchery, carry up to two years' imprisonment and fines of 5,000 to 10,000 Egyptian pounds.[19] Prosecutions typically arise from police entrapment operations, surveillance of social media or dating applications, or raids on private gatherings suspected of involving homosexual activity, with arrests surging after the 2013 ouster of President Mohamed Morsi amid broader security crackdowns.[21][20] Authorities documented at least 250 such arrests since 2013, according to U.S. State Department reports, often leading to pretrial detention where forensic medical examinations, including forced anal probes, are used to infer guilt despite lacking scientific validity for proving sexual orientation or recent activity.[22][21] Convictions under these frameworks have resulted in sentences up to six years in some economic court cases, though appeals occasionally overturn verdicts for lack of evidence.[20] Additional laws, such as Penal Code Articles 269 bis (public incitement to indecency) and 278 (scandalous acts offending public shame), enable charges for online solicitations or public displays, with penalties up to one year in detention or fines.[19] These provisions, rooted in colonial-era codes, are selectively enforced against men, reflecting interpretations aligned with prevailing Islamic jurisprudence that views homosexual acts as immoral, though not all religious scholars advocate criminalization of private conduct.[19] Human rights organizations report systemic issues in prosecutions, including torture to extract admissions and denial of fair trials, but Egyptian courts uphold the debauchery framework as sufficient for maintaining public order.[21]

Cultural and Religious Attitudes

In Egyptian society, homosexuality faces profound cultural rejection, rooted in longstanding norms that prioritize family honor, reproduction, and traditional gender roles. Surveys indicate near-universal opposition: a 2013 Pew Research Center poll found that 95% of Egyptians believed society should not accept homosexuality, with only 3% in favor.[23] Similarly, earlier World Values Survey data from 2007 showed 95% viewing homosexuality negatively, reflecting a consensus that equates same-sex relations with moral deviance and social disruption.[24] This stigma manifests in everyday discrimination, including familial ostracism, workplace harassment, and vigilante violence, as individuals risk severe social isolation or honor-based reprisals for perceived deviance.[25] Cultural narratives often frame homosexuality as a foreign import or psychological illness, exacerbating concealment and internalized shame among those affected.[26] Religiously, attitudes are shaped by dominant Sunni Islamic interpretations, which deem homosexual acts sinful based on Quranic accounts of the Prophet Lot's people (e.g., Surah Al-A'raf 7:80-84) and prophetic traditions prescribing punishment for sodomy.[27] Egypt's premier religious authority, Al-Azhar University, has consistently condemned homosexuality as a "phenomenon" to be confronted through education and societal vigilance, issuing statements in 2021 warning against media normalization and affirming its incompatibility with Islamic ethics.[28] [29] The Grand Mufti of Egypt has reiterated that while physical harm to individuals is impermissible, homosexual behavior remains forbidden under Sharia, underscoring a doctrinal line between tolerance of persons and acceptance of acts.[30] Among the Coptic Orthodox minority, similar prohibitions prevail, viewing same-sex attraction as contrary to biblical teachings on marriage and chastity.[3] These positions, drawn from scriptural exegesis rather than modern secular frameworks, reinforce cultural taboos, though some clerics distinguish between innate inclinations (to be resisted) and voluntary acts (deemed culpable).[32]

The 2017 Incident

Concert Participation and Flag Waving

On September 22, 2017, Sarah Hegazi attended a concert by the Lebanese indie rock band Mashrou' Leila at a venue in Cairo.[33] The band's lead singer, Hamed Sinno, is openly homosexual, and their performances have frequently incorporated themes addressing social taboos, including those related to sexual orientation.[14] Hegazi, an Egyptian activist with prior involvement in leftist and feminist causes, participated as an audience member expressing solidarity with these themes.[1] During the event, Hegazi and several other attendees raised and waved rainbow flags, a multicolored banner commonly used as a symbol of pride in homosexual and broader LGBTQ identities.[18] Videos captured by concertgoers showed Hegazi holding the flag aloft while dancing and appearing joyful amid the crowd.[33] This act occurred spontaneously during the performance, reflecting Hegazi's personal identification with the flag's symbolism at a public gathering of approximately 1,000 people.[1] The flag-waving drew immediate attention from security personnel present at the concert, though no interventions happened during the show itself.[4] Footage of the incident, including Hegazi's participation, quickly spread on social media platforms, amplifying visibility of the gesture in a country where public expressions of homosexuality face severe legal and social penalties.[17] Hegazi later described the moment as one of elation and defiance against prevailing cultural norms.[1]

Arrest and Initial Detention

Sarah Hegazi was arrested in Cairo on October 2, 2017, alongside activist Ahmed Alaa, following the circulation of video footage showing her waving a rainbow flag at the Mashrou' Leila concert on September 22, 2017.[35] [6] The arrests formed part of a broader crackdown by Egyptian security forces targeting individuals identified in the footage, with over 50 people detained in the ensuing weeks under Egypt's vague "debauchery" laws and related provisions.[4] Hegazi, the only woman arrested in this specific wave, was taken to a police station and charged with inciting debauchery, a charge commonly applied to suppress perceived immorality or LGBTQ-related expression in Egypt.[37] [18] Upon arrival at the station, Hegazi was interrogated by the Supreme State Security Prosecution that same morning.[35] The prosecution ordered her detention for 15 days pending further investigation into charges including "joining an illegal group" and "planning to overthrow the constitutional order," framing the flag-waving incident as part of organized subversion rather than isolated expression.[6] During this initial phase, Hegazi later reported experiencing torture by police, including electric shocks, as well as incitement of other detainees against her, reflecting standard tactics in Egyptian state security interrogations documented by human rights monitors.[1] [21] Her detention order was renewed multiple times, extending her hold beyond the initial period amid a media blackout on LGBTQ issues that amplified the prosecutorial narrative of moral and national security threats.[6]

Imprisonment Experience

Prison Conditions and Reported Abuses

Sarah Hegazi was detained for three months following her arrest on September 22, 2017, initially in a Cairo police station before transfer to Qanater Women's Prison north of the city.[1] During initial interrogation, she reported being subjected to electric shocks by police officers as a form of torture.[14] Hegazi was placed in solitary confinement for several days upon arrival at Qanater, where she described ongoing psychological torment amid the prison's overcrowded and unsanitary conditions typical of Egyptian facilities.[16] Prison authorities and police incited fellow inmates to target Hegazi due to her perceived association with homosexuality, goading them by stating she advocated for same-sex relationships.[7] This led to physical beatings, sexual assaults, and verbal harassment by cellmates, with Hegazi sustaining visible injuries such as scratches on her shoulder during her first night of communal detention.[7] [1] As the only woman arrested in the 2017 crackdown related to the rainbow flag incident, she faced intensified abuse, including systematic harassment by both guards and prisoners, exacerbating her isolation.[1] These experiences contributed to severe post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), which Hegazi attributed directly to the prison environment and lack of accountability for the perpetrators.[14] No Egyptian officials faced prosecution for the reported abuses, aligning with broader patterns of impunity in detentions targeting perceived moral offenses.[1] Hegazi was released on bail in December 2017 after approximately 90 days, but the trauma persisted, influencing her decision to seek asylum.[7]

Trial Proceedings and Release

Hegazi was arrested on October 1, 2017, alongside activist Ahmed Alaa and others linked to the September 22 concert incident, and referred to Egypt's Supreme State Security Prosecution for investigation into charges related to waving the rainbow flag.[6] [8] The prosecution ordered her pretrial detention for an initial 15 days, accusing her of involvement in "promoting sexual debauchery" and potentially joining an undocumented group aimed at undermining public morals, offenses that carried risks of imprisonment up to 15 years or life if convicted.[6] [40] [7] Her detention was renewed periodically by the prosecution, extending her time in custody for a total of three months amid ongoing investigations into the "rainbow flag case," which encompassed dozens of arrests following public backlash against the concert display.[8] [41] During this period, no formal trial commenced, as Egyptian security cases often involve prolonged pretrial phases under state security laws allowing renewable detentions without judicial oversight.[6] On January 2, 2018, a Cairo misdemeanor court granted bail to Hegazi and Alaa, releasing them pending a future trial date in the collective case.[8] [40] The release followed advocacy from human rights groups highlighting procedural irregularities and lack of evidence tying individuals directly to organized immorality.[41] Hegazi faced no immediate conviction at this stage but remained at risk of rearrest or sentencing, prompting her subsequent departure from Egypt.[42]

Life in Exile

Asylum in Canada

Following her release from prison in early 2018, Hegazi fled Egypt out of fear of further persecution and targeting as an openly queer activist.[9] She traveled to Canada later that year, seeking asylum on the basis of the persecution she had endured, including imprisonment and torture for waving a rainbow flag at a 2017 concert in Cairo.[43] Hegazi settled in Toronto, where she lived in exile apart from her family and support network in Egypt.[37] Hegazi was granted political asylum in Canada, with reports indicating the status was obtained relatively recently prior to her death in June 2020, after approximately 18 months of residence.[43] [44] This followed her initial arrival and application process amid ongoing risks from Egyptian authorities, who had continued to harass her associates and the broader LGBTQ community.[1] From Canada, Hegazi marked the one-year anniversary of her arrest in 2018 by publishing an essay reflecting on persistent fear and societal rejection, even in exile.[1] During her time in Canada, Hegazi engaged sporadically in activism, including writing and connecting with queer and human rights networks, though her asylum status provided legal protection without fully resolving the trauma from Egypt.[42] She lost her mother to cancer shortly after departing Egypt, compounding personal isolation in her new environment.[45]

Mental Health Challenges

Following her release from prison in January 2018 and subsequent flight to Canada for asylum later that year, Sarah Hegazi endured profound mental health deterioration rooted in the trauma of her detention, where she reported enduring torture and sexual assault.[42][46] Hegazi was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), which manifested in persistent fear, hypervigilance, and intrusive memories of abuse, persisting despite physical distance from Egypt.[14][45] In a 2018 personal essay, she detailed how the prison ordeal left her in a state of chronic psychological distress, unable to fully escape the violence even in exile.[47] Exile compounded her isolation, exacerbating depression, loneliness, and recurrent panic attacks, as Hegazi lived apart from family, friends, and her cultural roots in Egypt.[14][1] She sought treatment including electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), a procedure reserved for severe, treatment-resistant cases, reflecting the intensity of her symptoms.[14] Human Rights Watch reported her accounts of ongoing "fear of everyone—family, friends, and the street"—a remnant of prison-induced paranoia that hindered social reintegration in Canada.[1] These challenges were not merely personal but emblematic of broader exile traumas for LGBTQ individuals fleeing state persecution, where safety from immediate violence does not equate to psychological recovery.[47] Hegazi's writings and communications with advocates underscored a causal link between the unprocessed prison abuses— including reported beatings and assaults—and her unrelenting mental anguish, with no evidence of resolution prior to her death.[45][46]

Suicide and Immediate Aftermath

Circumstances of Death

Sarah Hegazi, aged 30, died by suicide on June 14, 2020, in her apartment in Toronto, Ontario, Canada, where she had been granted asylum.[2][42] Her death was confirmed as suicide by her Egyptian lawyer, Khaled al-Masry, on June 15.[1] Hegazi took her own life by hanging in the bathroom of her residence.[48] Friends and supporters reported the death and shared details of a suicide note she left behind, expressing feelings of alienation and failed attempts at recovery.[14] The coroner ruled the cause as suicide, amid her ongoing post-traumatic stress disorder stemming from prior imprisonment and reported abuses in Egypt.[2]

Suicide Note and Attributions

In her suicide note, discovered after her death on June 13, 2020, Sarah Hegazi conveyed deep fatigue and addressed personal connections alongside broader entities. She apologized to her siblings for failing to persevere, writing, "To my siblings, I tried to make it, and I failed," and similarly told friends of the "harsh" experiences that exhausted her, stating she "couldn’t make it."[47] To comrades and the world at large, she expressed, "You were unbelievably cruel, but I forgive you," while poetically preferring death's release: "The sky is sweeter than the earth! And I want the sky, not the earth."[49][50] Friends and activists who circulated the note attributed Hegazi's suicide primarily to post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) stemming from her 2017 arrest, three-month imprisonment, and reported abuses in Egyptian custody, including torture and sexual assault.[2][14] These groups, such as Human Rights Watch, emphasized how the trauma persisted despite her 2018 asylum in Canada, exacerbated by prior suicide attempts, social isolation as an exile, and grief following her mother's death from cancer in early 2020.[1][18] The note's themes of forgiveness and universal cruelty have fueled debates on causation, with LGBTQ advocates linking it directly to Egypt's crackdown on perceived moral deviance—evident in her rainbow flag incident at a 2017 concert—while some analyses highlight exile's alienating effects, including cultural disconnection in the West.[47][42] Hegazi's self-described socialism and feminism, combined with the note's broad indictments, underscore multifaceted pressures beyond state persecution alone, though primary attributions remain tied to prison-induced trauma as reported by contemporaries.[14]

Reactions and Ongoing Legacy

Support from LGBTQ and Human Rights Groups

Human Rights Watch mourned Hegazi's death on June 14, 2020, describing her as a "queer feminist whose resistance centered around a deconstruction of class power and struggle" and attributing the conditions leading to her suicide to state-sponsored repression in Egypt.[1] Amnesty International criticized the Egyptian regime's role in her oppression, linking her 2017 imprisonment and subsequent trauma to broader failures in protecting LGBTI rights, and called for accountability in a statement following her suicide.[51] [45] Outright International, an organization focused on LGBTIQ rights in the Global South, expressed solidarity with the Egyptian LGBTIQ movement after her passing, highlighting the loss through statements from local partners like Bedayaa Organization, which directly mourned her as an activist.[52] The Tahrir Institute for Middle East Policy established the Sarah Hegazi Fellowship to honor her work as an intersectional queer feminist and human rights defender, supporting research on marginalized voices in the region.[9] In her legacy, LGBTQ groups in the Arab world and diaspora organized annual commemorations, including the first MENA Lesbian and Queer Woman's Pride Day on June 13, 2021, explicitly in her honor, recognizing her 2017 arrest and three-month detention for raising the rainbow flag.[53] On the first anniversary of her death in 2021, Arab LGBTQ+ communities dedicated Pride Month to Hegazi, framing her activism against Egypt's anti-LGBTQ crackdown as a pivotal moment for regional visibility.[54] Vigils, such as one held in Toronto's Barbara Hall Park on June 15, 2024, by local 2SLGBTQ+ groups, continued to pay tribute to her asylum-seeking journey and the torture she endured in Egyptian prison.[55]

Criticisms and Alternative Perspectives

Egyptian media and conservative commentators framed Hegazi's raising of the rainbow flag at the September 22, 2017, Mashrou' Leila concert in Cairo as an intentional promotion of "debauchery" and moral corruption, threatening Islamic values, family structures, and national identity. Coverage emphasized terms like "perverted," "deviant," and "sick" to describe homosexuality, portraying the incident as a foreign-influenced assault on Egyptian society rather than a simple expression of personal identity.[17][56][57] In response to her June 14, 2020, suicide, Egyptian outlets displayed the highest levels of stigmatizing frames among analyzed media (Egyptian, Lebanese, and American), with minimal sympathy and no direct attacks on the regime for her arrest or alleged abuses. Attributions often linked her death to inherent flaws in her "immoral" lifestyle or ideological pursuits, such as queer activism intertwined with leftist politics, rather than solely to detention trauma or exile isolation.[58][59] Alternative views contend that the arrests, including Hegazi's under charges of inciting debauchery and joining an unsanctioned protest, reflected legitimate enforcement of laws against public displays deemed socially disruptive in a conservative context, amplified by viral outrage rather than preemptive targeting. Some Arab reactions echoed this, criticizing her choices as self-destructive and rejecting narratives of martyrdom, while questioning why supportive exile in Canada failed to prevent suicide if state persecution were the sole cause.[60][61]

Cultural Impact and Debates

![Side-by-side image of Amman mural of Sarah Hegazi before and after][float-right] Sarah Hegazi's act of raising a rainbow flag at a 2017 concert in Cairo transformed her into a symbol of defiance against state-sponsored homophobia in Egypt and broader Middle Eastern contexts, inspiring tributes across queer activist networks.[14] Her suicide in 2020 prompted widespread mourning that cultivated a sense of "queer Arabness," with responses emphasizing her legacy as a leftist, feminist, and communist activist resisting authoritarianism and social conservatism.[62] This collective grief manifested in artistic commemorations, including street murals in cities like Amman, where a tribute to Hegazi was vandalized and subsequently repaired, highlighting tensions between commemoration and backlash; similar murals appeared in Brighton, United Kingdom, and Toronto, Canada, often accompanied by excerpts from her prison diary.[63] In recognition of her influence, the first annual MENA Lesbian and Queer Woman's Pride Day was established on June 13, 2021, honoring Hegazi's role in visibility for lesbian and queer women in the Middle East and North Africa region.[53] Fellowships, such as the Sarah Hegazi Fellowship by the Tahrir Institute for Middle East Policy, continue to support queer feminist human rights defenders, perpetuating her intersectional advocacy against class power, patriarchy, and state repression.[9] Debates surrounding Hegazi's legacy center on the framing of her death and activism within Egyptian media and society, where her suicide ignited controversy over LGBTQ rights amid public homophobia and state narratives portraying such acts as threats to national morality.[64] Analyses of news coverage reveal how her story was discursively constructed to reinforce heteronormative national identity, with terms like "deviancy" used in charges against her, contrasting with international queer solidarity that attributes her trauma primarily to prison torture including electric shocks and solitary confinement.[17] Critics within progressive circles caution against reducing Hegazi to a slogan of persecution, urging acknowledgment of her broader writings on class struggle and the myth of safety in exile, where ongoing PTSD and isolation compounded her experiences.[65] These discussions underscore causal factors in her mental health decline, prioritizing empirical accounts of state violence over unsubstantiated cultural relativism, while noting biases in mainstream human rights reporting that may amplify selective narratives.[1]
