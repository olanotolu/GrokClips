List of current United States lieutenant governors

The current United States lieutenant governors comprise the officials serving as second-in-command to the governors in 45 states and equivalents in four territories, where they typically preside over the state senate, cast tie-breaking votes, and assume gubernatorial duties upon vacancy, death, resignation, or removal of the governor.) Five states—Arizona, Maine, New Hampshire, Oregon, and Wyoming—lack the office, with gubernatorial succession instead vesting in the secretary of state or president of the senate.[1] Election methods vary across jurisdictions: candidates run jointly with gubernatorial nominees on the same ticket in most states to ensure alignment, while separate elections occur in 18 states, potentially leading to divided government within the executive branch; in Tennessee and West Virginia, the senate president ex officio holds the title, selected by legislative vote.[2] As of October 2025, Republicans hold 27 of the 45 state positions to Democrats' 18, reflecting partisan dominance in the office amid broader Republican gains in state executive roles following the 2024 elections.[1] The role's powers differ significantly by state constitution and statute, ranging from largely ceremonial in some to substantive policymaking authority in others, such as leading initiatives on economic development or emergency management.[3]

Overview of the Lieutenant Governor Position

Constitutional and Historical Foundations

The office of lieutenant governor traces its roots to British colonial administration in North America, where royal governors frequently appointed lieutenant governors as deputies to manage routine affairs, exercise executive powers during absences, and maintain order, as seen in colonies like Virginia and Massachusetts.[4] These appointments ensured governance continuity without fixed terms, reflecting monarchical delegation rather than elective principles.[5] After independence, the framers of state constitutions adapted the concept, incorporating lieutenant governors in early documents such as New York's 1777 constitution, to provide for gubernatorial succession and stability amid revolutionary uncertainties.[6] Unlike the federal vice presidency defined in Article II, Section 1 of the U.S. Constitution, no provision in the federal charter mandates the office for states, leaving its establishment to individual state sovereignty under the Tenth Amendment. This absence of uniformity allowed pragmatic variations: by the early republic, some states enshrined the position constitutionally for succession, while others initially omitted it to avoid superfluous executive layers.[7] In the 19th century, as states revised constitutions amid territorial expansion and population growth, the role formalized in most jurisdictions, with lieutenant governors assuming statutory duties like presiding over the state senate—often with tie-breaking authority—and acting governor during vacancies or travel.[8] Examples include Maryland's 1864 constitution creating the office explicitly for these functions, and Texas's 1845 framework succeeding the republic's vice presidency.[6] [9] Election modalities diverged—joint tickets with governors in some for alignment, separate ballots in others for independence—prioritizing state-specific needs over national standardization. Currently, 45 states maintain the office via constitutional provision, while five—Maine, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Oregon, and Wisconsin—eschew it, designating senate presidents or secretaries of state for analogous roles, evidencing federalism's emphasis on tailored, non-redundant structures.[10][3]

Core Duties and Variations Across Jurisdictions

The primary duty of lieutenant governors in the 45 U.S. states where the office exists is to assume the governorship upon vacancy due to the governor's death, resignation, removal, or incapacity, as specified in state constitutions. This succession mechanism ensures executive continuity, with the lieutenant governor becoming full governor rather than merely acting in most cases. In addition, lieutenant governors often serve as president of the state senate, presiding over sessions and casting tie-breaking votes where authorized, a power that can determine outcomes in divided legislatures. For instance, they preside over the senate in 25 states and break ties in 24 of those.[11][3][12] Duties vary substantially by state, ranging from ceremonial to substantive roles shaped by constitutional provisions, statutes, and gubernatorial delegation. In minimalist configurations, such as Texas, the lieutenant governor's influence centers on senate leadership and succession with few independent executive functions. In contrast, states like California grant broader authority, including tie-breaking in the senate, appointments to policy boards, and oversight of initiatives in areas like economic development and higher education. Election methods further differentiate roles: joint tickets with the governor in 26 states promote alignment and reduce post-election conflicts, while separate elections in 17 states can yield opposing party holders, potentially complicating governance though succession remains intact. These structural differences empirically affect office efficacy, with joint systems minimizing dual-executive friction observed in separate-election states.[13][14][15] In organized territories like Puerto Rico, lieutenant governors mirror state duties in succession and legislative presidency but operate under federal statutory constraints, limiting autonomy compared to states. Vacancies in the office can create temporary leadership gaps, though state lines of succession typically designate the senate president pro tempore or secretary of state as interim successors. Overall, while core functions provide stability, jurisdictional variations highlight how state-specific designs determine the office's practical influence beyond abstract succession ideals.[11][3]

Demographic and Partisan Breakdown

As of October 2025, Republicans occupy 26 of the 45 lieutenant governorships in U.S. states, comprising approximately 58% of the positions, while Democrats hold the remaining 19.[1] This partisan distribution aligns closely with gubernatorial control, with lieutenant governors sharing their governor's party affiliation in roughly 90% of cases, primarily due to joint-ticket election systems in 36 states that incentivize intra-party selection to maintain unified executive branches.[17] Separate elections for lieutenant governor, used in nine states such as Mississippi and Rhode Island, introduce potential for divergence but rarely result in cross-party pairings, as voters tend to favor co-partisans for the office's succession role.[18] Demographically, the group is overwhelmingly male and white, averaging around 55 years of age, with 14 women (31%) serving across parties—nine Democrats and five Republicans.[1] [19] Ethnic minority representation remains limited, including three African Americans (Juliana Stratton in Illinois, Austin Davis in Pennsylvania, and Winsome Earle-Sears in Virginia), two Asian Americans (Aruna Miller in Maryland and Eleni Kounalakis in California), and no current Hispanic or Native American lieutenant governors in states. This composition prioritizes experienced political insiders over quotas for demographic parity, reflecting electoral outcomes driven by voter preferences in state-specific contexts rather than institutional mandates for proportional representation. Post-2024 elections yielded minimal net partisan shifts, with Democrats gaining North Carolina's office (Rachel Hunt defeating Republican Hal Weatherman) but Republicans retaining seats in Vermont, Mississippi, and North Dakota amid broader Republican strength in state executive races.[20] [18] Joint-ticket mechanisms reinforce party discipline by tying lieutenant gubernatorial candidacies to gubernatorial nominees, reducing turnover and ideological variance compared to primary-based or separate-ballot systems, which correlate with occasional independent or cross-party outcomes like Vermont's Democratic lieutenant governor under a Republican governor.[17] Such structures promote executive cohesion but can limit checks on gubernatorial power, as evidenced by higher alignment rates post-2020.[21]

Jurisdictions Without Lieutenant Governors

States Relying on Alternative Succession Mechanisms

Five states—Arizona, Maine, New Hampshire, Oregon, and Wyoming—lack a dedicated lieutenant governor position, relying instead on their state constitutions to designate the president of the senate as the immediate successor to the governor in cases of vacancy, death, resignation, or incapacity.[27][3] This legislative officer, elected by fellow senators at the start of each session, assumes executive duties without requiring a separate statewide election, thereby integrating gubernatorial succession into the legislative process.[27] The mechanism varies slightly by state but generally prioritizes the senate president over other officials like the secretary of state or attorney general until a special election or gubernatorial appointment fills the vacancy.[28] For instance, in Arizona, the senate president serves as acting governor until the next general election.[27] This structure has persisted since statehood for most of these jurisdictions, reflecting a deliberate choice for streamlined continuity over a standalone executive role. Efforts to introduce lieutenant governors, such as Arizona's Proposition 131 in 2022—which proposed electing the position on a joint ticket with the governor—have failed at the ballot box, with voters rejecting it by a margin of approximately 52% to 48%.[29] As of October 2025, the current senate presidents positioned to succeed their respective governors are:

Federal District and Unorganized Territories

The District of Columbia lacks a lieutenant governor, operating instead under a mayor-council government established by the Home Rule Act of 1973, with executive authority vested solely in the mayor. Muriel Bowser has served as mayor since January 2, 2015, overseeing departments without a designated successor in the role of lieutenant governor. In cases of mayoral vacancy, the chairman of the Council of the District of Columbia assumes the role of acting mayor, as outlined in D.C. Code § 1-204.03, reflecting limited self-governance under congressional oversight per Article I, Section 8 of the U.S. Constitution.[36] Deputy mayors, such as the Deputy Mayor for Operations and Infrastructure, support administrative functions but hold no constitutional succession rights equivalent to a lieutenant governor.[37] The unorganized territories of the United States, primarily the nine minor outlying islands (Baker Island, Howland Island, Jarvis Island, Johnston Atoll, Kingman Reef, Midway Atoll, Navassa Island, Palmyra Atoll, and Wake Island), maintain no local executive offices, including governors or lieutenant governors. These unincorporated areas, lacking organic acts from Congress, are administered directly by federal entities like the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service for wildlife refuges or the U.S. Department of the Interior's Office of Insular Affairs, with military oversight in select cases such as Wake Island.[38] This structure enforces minimal governance focused on federal interests like conservation and defense, without the autonomous executive frameworks afforded to states or organized territories under Article IV, Section 3 of the Constitution. Vacancies in any ad hoc administrative roles, rare due to uninhabited or low-population status, are resolved federally without local succession mechanisms.[39]

Political and Electoral Dynamics

Alignment Between Governors and Lieutenant Governors

In the 25 states where governors and lieutenant governors are elected on joint tickets, party alignment is guaranteed by design, as candidates run as a unified slate following primaries or conventions.[40] This mechanism, adopted to promote executive harmony, ensures that the lieutenant governor shares the governor's partisan affiliation, facilitating coordinated governance. Examples include Texas, where Republican Lieutenant Governor Dan Patrick serves alongside Republican Governor Greg Abbott, supporting shared priorities such as fiscal conservatism and border security measures.[2] Similarly, in Democratic-led states like California, Lieutenant Governor Eleni Kounalakis aligns with Governor Gavin Newsom on progressive policies.[2] Even in the 18 states with separate elections, partisan alignment prevails in nearly all cases due to voter ticket-splitting patterns favoring co-partisans for executive roles. As of October 2025, every one of the 45 states with lieutenant governors features a same-party pairing, a norm sustained across election cycles despite the absence of structural mandates.[40] In Vermont, for instance, separately elected Republican Lieutenant Governor Joe Benning complements Republican Governor Phil Scott, demonstrating how electoral independence does not preclude ideological synergy. This high alignment rate—approaching 100%—contrasts with historical periods of greater divergence, underscoring voter preferences for unified executive leadership over divided tickets. Joint elections enhance accountability by tying the lieutenant governor's fortunes to the governor's during campaigns, incentivizing collaboration on policy agendas and reducing intra-executive friction. Proponents argue this structure streamlines legislative advocacy, as seen in red states where aligned pairs advance reforms like tax cuts without partisan veto threats within the executive branch. Critics, including some political scientists, contend that mandatory pairing may suppress diverse viewpoints and limit checks on gubernatorial power, potentially stifling debate on contentious issues. However, observable low rates of public policy defection by lieutenant governors—evidenced by consistent support for gubernatorial initiatives in aligned administrations—suggest such concerns overstate risks, with empirical patterns favoring operational efficiency over theoretical independence.[41][15]

Instances of Partisan Divergence and Their Implications

As of October 26, 2025, there are no states in which the lieutenant governor belongs to a different political party than the governor, reflecting the norm of partisan alignment in state executive leadership.[42][21] This uniformity persists across the 45 states with lieutenant governors, where election methods—joint tickets in 33 states and separate but coordinated races in others—encourage same-party pairings, reinforced by voter tendencies to support cohesive executive teams for streamlined governance.[2] Partisan divergence remains exceptional due to structural incentives and electoral dynamics favoring unity; in states with separate lieutenant gubernatorial elections (Connecticut, Delaware, Mississippi, Rhode Island), voters have historically aligned choices with gubernatorial preferences, as evidenced by post-2024 outcomes maintaining parity despite competitive races.[43] Such splits, when occurring, arise primarily in these separately elected systems and can introduce intra-executive tensions, particularly if the lieutenant governor presides over the state senate and wields tie-breaking authority. Historical instances illustrate these dynamics without current parallels. In Mississippi from 1992 to 2000, Republican Governor Kirk Fordice served alongside Democratic Lieutenant Governor Ronnie Musgrove, leading to clashes over budget priorities and legislative agendas, as the lieutenant governor's senate presiding role enabled blocking of certain Republican initiatives.[15] Similarly, in Delaware from 1973 to 1977, Democratic Governor Sherman Tribbitt coexisted with Republican Lieutenant Governor Eugene Bookhammer, resulting in policy friction and heightened reliance on negotiation for executive functions. Another case occurred in Delaware in 1985–1989 under Republican Governor Michael Castle with Democratic Lieutenant Governor Shien Bynum, where divided priorities complicated responses to state fiscal challenges. These examples, drawn from states with independent lieutenant gubernatorial contests, highlight how splits can compel bipartisan compromise but often escalate disputes over authority, such as succession protocols or veto overrides.[44] The implications of such divergences include enhanced checks within the executive branch, potentially moderating extreme policies through the lieutenant governor's legislative influence—for instance, tie-breaking votes that alter bill outcomes or senate organization. However, they frequently yield operational inefficiencies, including protracted negotiations and appeals to courts for role clarifications, as conflicting partisan directives undermine unified policy execution. Voters' aversion to these frictions, demonstrated by the infrequency of splits (fewer than 5% of lieutenant gubernatorial terms since 2000), underscores a preference for aligned leadership that facilitates decisive action, particularly in unified Republican-led states where post-2024 trifectas have bolstered legislative productivity.[21][15]

Recent Developments and Future Outlook

Outcomes from 2024 Elections and Vacancy Resolutions

The 2024 lieutenant gubernatorial elections occurred in nine states, with outcomes reflecting alignment with gubernatorial results in joint-ticket states and independent results in others. Republicans retained or gained seats in Indiana, Missouri, Montana, North Dakota, Utah, and Vermont, while Democrats held Delaware and Washington and flipped North Carolina from Republican control. In joint-ticket states, the lieutenant governor candidates succeeded at a 100% rate alongside their gubernatorial running mates, consistent with historical patterns where such tickets prevail when the gubernatorial candidate wins. Key partisan shifts included Vermont, where Republican John Rodgers defeated incumbent David Zuckerman (Progressive/Democrat) by a margin of approximately 5 percentage points, securing a Republican gain despite the Democratic gubernatorial victory.[45] In North Carolina, Democrat Rachel Hunt prevailed over Republican Hal Weatherman with 50.1% of the vote, flipping the office previously held by Republican Mark Robinson and aligning with the Democratic gubernatorial win.[46] Missouri's separate election saw Republican David Wasinger defeat Democrat Richard Brown, maintaining Republican control as the incumbent Mike Kehoe pursued the governorship.[47] Democrats preserved their hold in Delaware, with Kyle Evans Gay succeeding Bethany Hall-Long.[48] In Washington, incumbent Democrat Denny Heck won reelection in the separate race against Republican Dan Gase, retaining Democratic control amid the Democratic gubernatorial success.[49] Joint tickets in Republican gubernatorial victories—Indiana (Republican hold), Montana (Republican hold under Greg Gianforte), North Dakota (Republican hold), and Utah (Republican hold under Spencer Cox)—ensured continued Republican lieutenant governors. Voter turnout in these races correlated closely with gubernatorial turnout, averaging over 60% in states like North Carolina and Missouri, where certified results confirmed the outcomes by mid-December 2024.[50] Post-election vacancy resolutions were minimal, with no ongoing lieutenant governor vacancies reported as of October 2025; all positions were filled via certified election results or standing appointments. In Alaska, for instance, Republican Nancy Dahlstrom continued in the role following her 2022 gubernatorial appointment, with no interim changes requiring resolution.[51] These results preserved the pre-election partisan balance among the contested seats (six Republican, three Democratic), while enabling unified executive control in states like Missouri, Montana, North Dakota, Utah, and the flipped North Carolina under Democrats, potentially streamlining legislative agendas without evidence of systemic delays in implementation.

Anticipated Changes in 2026 Cycles

In the 2026 lieutenant gubernatorial elections, scheduled for November 3, 2026, voters in 31 states will select new officeholders, with additional contests in three territories. These races often align with gubernatorial elections on joint tickets in most jurisdictions, providing structural stability through shared partisan branding and campaign resources, though eight states conduct separate elections for lieutenant governor, introducing greater volatility.[2] Historical data on state executive incumbents indicate reelection success rates exceeding 75% in recent cycles, bolstered by name recognition and fundraising advantages, though outcomes hinge on local economic indicators like unemployment and GDP growth, which have favored incumbents in expansionary periods.[52] Key open or contested races underscore potential shifts. In Alabama, where the office is elected separately, incumbent Republican Will Ainsworth faces primary challenges from candidates including former quarterback A.J. McCarron, Agriculture Commissioner Rick Pate, and Secretary of State Wes Allen, signaling intra-party competition in a solidly Republican state.[53] [54] Separate-ballot states like Wisconsin exhibit higher turnover potential; while current Democratic Lieutenant Governor Sara Rodriguez's term aligns with Governor Tony Evers', former Lieutenant Governor Mandela Barnes is eyeing a gubernatorial bid, which could indirectly influence ticket dynamics if Rodriguez seeks higher office or faces recruitment gaps.[55] Regional partisan trends favor Republicans in Southern and Midwestern states comprising much of the cycle, where 20 of the 31 seats are held by GOP incumbents entering 2026, reflecting durable voter coalitions in low-population, agriculture-dependent areas less swayed by urban demographic shifts. However, national midterm headwinds—such as the incumbent president's party historically losing gubernatorial seats—could prompt flips in competitive battlegrounds like Michigan or Minnesota if economic slowdowns erode support.[56] Joint-ticket structures mitigate such risks by tying lieutenant gubernatorial fortunes to gubernatorial coattails, historically yielding retention rates above 80% for aligned pairs, though term limits in states like California (where incumbent Eleni Kounalakis is termed out and pursuing the governorship) open avenues for partisan retention or modest gains.[57] Overall, empirical predictors point to continuity over upheaval absent exogenous shocks like recessions or scandals.
