List of current ships of the Royal Canadian Navy

The list of current ships of the Royal Canadian Navy catalogs the active commissioned vessels comprising Canada's primary maritime combat and support capabilities, totaling around 30 major units as of October 2025.[1] These include four Victoria-class diesel-electric submarines for undersea operations, intelligence, and deterrence; twelve Halifax-class multi-role frigates serving as the backbone for anti-submarine warfare, air defence, and multinational task group contributions; and six Harry DeWolf-class Arctic and offshore patrol ships optimized for sovereignty assertion in northern waters, surveillance, and humanitarian missions.[2] Auxiliary and training elements feature the interim replenishment vessel MV Asterix for at-sea logistics, five Orca-class patrol craft for sailor training, and the sail training ship HMCS Oriole.[2] The fleet's composition reflects ongoing modernization amid the divestment of the Kingston-class maritime coastal defence vessels, which commenced in fall 2025 to reallocate missions to newer platforms without capability loss, while future acquisitions like Canadian Surface Combatants and submarine renewals aim to enhance blue-water projection and undersea persistence.[3][4]

Submarines

Victoria-class submarines

The Victoria-class submarines, originally the Upholder class built for the Royal Navy, form the sole submarine capability of the Royal Canadian Navy (RCN), with four vessels commissioned between 2000 and 2004 following their acquisition in 1998.[5] These diesel-electric submarines displace 2,455 tonnes surfaced, measure 70.26 meters in length with a beam of 7.2 meters and draught of 7.6 meters, and are propelled by two Paxman Valenta 16 RPA diesel generators providing approximately 3.035 MW total, driving a single shaft via a GEC electric motor rated at 5 MW for submerged speeds up to 12 knots.[6] Armament includes Mk 48 heavyweight torpedoes launched from six bow tubes, with capability to lay naval mines.[7]

Operational availability has historically been low, with typically only one of the four submarines available at high readiness due to extended refits, mechanical issues including corrosion, and propulsion failures requiring frequent dockyard maintenance.[11] HMCS Corner Brook, for instance, underwent a major overhaul lasting over a decade before returning to service in 2025.[9] Deployments remain limited, with the class contributing to NATO exercises but relying on allied submarines to fulfill broader commitments amid persistent maintenance demands.[12] The fleet is projected to remain in service until the mid-2030s.[12]

Surface Combatants

Halifax-class frigates

The Halifax-class frigates form the core of the Royal Canadian Navy's surface combatant force, consisting of 12 multi-role patrol frigates designed primarily for anti-submarine warfare with capabilities for anti-air and anti-surface operations. Commissioned between 1992 and 1996, these vessels measure 134.1 meters in length, have a beam of 16.4 meters, and displace up to 4,770 tonnes fully loaded. They achieve a maximum speed of 30 knots and possess a range of 9,500 nautical miles at 15 knots, accommodating a crew of approximately 225 personnel plus air detachment.[13][14] Equipped for blue-water operations, the frigates feature sensors including the AN/SPS-49 air search radar and support embarkation of one CH-148 Cyclone helicopter for enhanced maritime surveillance and attack roles. Armament includes Harpoon anti-ship missiles, Mark 46 torpedoes, a 57 mm Mk 2 gun, and close-in weapon systems. As the backbone of the RCN's expeditionary capabilities, they conduct independent patrols and integrate into multinational task groups, emphasizing submarine hunting in open-ocean environments.[14][15]

Offshore Patrol Vessels

Harry DeWolf-class Arctic and offshore patrol ships

The Harry DeWolf-class Arctic and offshore patrol ships (AOPS) comprise six multi-role vessels procured to bolster the Royal Canadian Navy's presence in northern waters, emphasizing sovereignty enforcement, maritime surveillance, and support for interdiction operations. Designed primarily for Arctic operations during the ice-free season (July to October), these ships facilitate extended patrols, humanitarian assistance, and integration with Canadian Rangers for territorial assertion amid increasing regional activity. With deliveries spanning 2019 to August 2025, the class addresses capability gaps in low-threat environments, prioritizing endurance over high-intensity combat.[24][25] Key specifications include a maximum displacement of 6,660 tonnes, length of 103 metres, beam of 19 metres, and a top speed of 17 knots, enabling a range of 6,800 nautical miles. These vessels possess icebreaking capability equivalent to Polar Class 5, allowing sustained progress at 3 knots through 1 metre of first-year sea ice, which supports navigation in partially iced Arctic passages. Crewed by approximately 45 personnel with capacity for up to 85 including embarked forces, the ships feature modular mission bays for small boats, unmanned vehicles, and helicopters, enhancing flexibility for patrol and response duties. Coastal Defence and Training Vessels

Kingston-class maritime coastal defence vessels

![HMCS Nanaimo and Saskatoon][float-right] The Kingston-class maritime coastal defence vessels (MCDVs) are a series of twelve multi-role patrol ships designed primarily for mine countermeasures, route survey, and support to diving operations, with secondary capabilities in coastal defence and training.[3] Commissioned between July 1996 and June 1999, these vessels measure 55.3 metres in length, displace approximately 970 tonnes, achieve a maximum speed of 16 knots, and accommodate a core crew of 37 personnel plus up to 13 clearance divers.[3] Their modular mission systems allow reconfiguration for tasks such as minehunting using autonomous underwater vehicles or supporting explosive ordnance disposal, though armament is limited to a single Bofors 40 mm/60 deck gun and small arms.[3] As of October 2025, the class is undergoing phased retirement due to obsolescence in propulsion systems, sensors, and overall platform age after nearly three decades of service, with the Royal Canadian Navy consolidating operations toward fleet modernization.[33] Eight vesselsâ€”HMCS Kingston, Glace Bay, Charlottetown, Summerside, Whitehorse, Saskatoon, Brandon, and Vancouverâ€”were formally paid off in ceremonies held in Esquimalt, British Columbia, on 29 September and in Halifax, Nova Scotia, on 3 October, marking the end of their operational deployments.[34] [33] The remaining four active shipsâ€”HMCS Moncton, Yellowknife, Edmonton, and Nanaimoâ€”have been reassigned to Maritime Forces Atlantic in Halifax, with Nanaimo transiting from the Pacific coast later in 2025; these will continue limited duties focused on training and transition support pending their scheduled retirements: Yellowknife in 2026, Edmonton in 2027, and Moncton and Nanaimo in 2028.[33] [35]

Orca-class patrol and training vessels

![Orca-class patrol vessel Wolf (PCT 59)][float-right] The Orca-class consists of eight steel-hulled patrol craft training (PCT) vessels operated by the Royal Canadian Navy (RCN) primarily for training naval personnel in basic seamanship, navigation, and coastal operations.[37] These non-commissioned training tenders, loosely based on the Australian Pacific-class patrol boat design, were constructed by Victoria Shipyards Limited between 2004 and 2008, with the first vessel, Orca (PCT 55), accepted into service on November 17, 2006.[38] [39] Each displaces 210 tonnes, measures 33 meters in length with a beam of 8.6 meters and draught of 2.5 meters, and is powered by two Caterpillar 3516B diesel engines providing a maximum speed of 23 knots.[37] [39] The vessels accommodate up to 24 personnel, including a minimum crew of five and trainees, facilitating hands-on instruction for naval warfare officers, boatswains, and engineers as part of the RCN's training syllabus.[37] Their small size and maneuverability enable cost-effective, high-volume training sorties in coastal waters, contrasting with the higher operational costs of larger warships.[37] Beyond training, they conduct patrols for regulatory violations such as illegal fishing or pollution, assist in search and rescue, support boaters, and participate in community events, though they carry no offensive armament beyond small arms.[37] All eight vessels remain in active service as of January 2025, operating year-round from bases in British Columbia's coastal waters.[40]

Auxiliary and Support Vessels

Interim auxiliary oiler

The MV Asterix serves as the Royal Canadian Navy's (RCN) interim auxiliary oiler, providing at-sea replenishment capabilities following the retirement of the previous Protecteur-class ships in 2015.[41] Originally constructed as the commercial container ship Cynthia in 2010, it underwent conversion at Davie Shipbuilding in LÃ©vis, Quebec, starting in 2015 to enable naval support functions, including underway replenishment of fuel and limited stores to surface combatants.[42] The vessel was formally accepted into RCN service in January 2018 after completing sea trials.[43] MV Asterix has a displacement of approximately 26,000 tonnes and can carry 10,500 cubic meters of marine diesel fuel (F76) and 1,300 cubic meters of aviation fuel (F44), supporting replenishment-at-sea (RAS) operations via four STREAM-type rigs for simultaneous transfer to two ships.[41] It features a hangar and flight deck for CH-148 Cyclone helicopter operations, enhancing its utility for task group sustainment, though its munitions storage is limited compared to dedicated warships.[41] Crewed by civilian mariners from Federal Fleet Services under RCN operational command, the ship maintains a mixed complement of about 36 naval personnel and 79 civilians.[44] The initial charter began in 2014 as a bridge solution, with the contract extended multiple times; in August 2024, a three-year extension was secured, ensuring service through 2028.[45] This has enabled the RCN to conduct extended deployments, such as replenishment alongside USCGC Waesche during Operation Latitude on September 9, 2025, and support for Operation Projection in the Indo-Pacific.[46][47] While filling critical gaps in fleet sustainment, MV Asterix lacks the full organic aviation and vertical replenishment capacities of purpose-built oilers, relying on alongside transfers for heavier loads.[43]

Sail training and research vessels

HMCS Oriole serves as the Royal Canadian Navy's dedicated sail training vessel, commissioned in 1954 after being built in 1921 as a yacht for the Royal Canadian Yacht Club.[48] Rigged as a ketch with 15,700 square feet of sail area and an auxiliary 261 hp Detroit Diesel engine, the 102-foot vessel is the oldest active ship in the RCN fleet.[49] She accommodates up to 20 personnel, including trainees, and operates primarily from CFB Halifax for seasonal cruises.[50] The vessel supports professional development by providing hands-on experience in traditional seamanship, navigation, and leadership under sail for junior officers, non-commissioned members, and occasionally sea cadets.[51] These cruises, often spanning thousands of nautical miles annually along Canadian coasts and international ports, build foundational maritime skills amid broader RCN challenges with powered fleet availability and crew shortages.[52] The RCN maintains no dedicated sail training or research vessels beyond Oriole and smaller auxiliary sailboats used regionally, such as CS 36s and Bavaria 45Cs on the Pacific coast for basic instruction.[52] Oceanographic and hydrographic research falls under the Canadian Coast Guard, with vessels like CCGS John Cabot handling surveys using powered multi-role platforms rather than sail propulsion.[53] RCN ships occasionally embark scientific teams for ad hoc support, but lack specialized non-powered research assets.[54]

Harbour and yard support craft

The Royal Canadian Navy maintains a fleet of harbour and yard support craft for essential base operations at Canadian Forces Base (CFB) Esquimalt on the Pacific coast and CFB Halifax on the Atlantic coast, including fire suppression, towing, berthing assistance, and diving support. These non-combat vessels feature small displacements under 300 tonnes, limited endurance for harbour duties only, and specialized equipment such as high-capacity pumps, winches, and diving platforms, without capabilities for extended blue-water operations.[55] Fireboats in the Fire class, comprising two units commissioned in the late 1970s, deliver primary harbour firefighting and rescue response with integrated pumps and monitors for foam and water delivery, alongside secondary tug functions with a bollard pull of approximately 7.5 tonnes. CFAV Firebrand (YTR 562) serves at CFB Esquimalt, while CFAV Firebird (YTR 561) operates from CFB Halifax. These vessels are slated for replacement as part of the naval large tug acquisition, which incorporates enhanced fire-fighting systems.[55] Tugboats, primarily from the Glen class of five civilian-crewed units built between 1975 and 1977, support warship movements, docking, and light salvage within dockyards, each with a bollard pull exceeding 20 tonnes and diesel propulsion for speeds up to 12 knots. The active Glen-class tugs are CFAV Glendyne (YTB 640), CFAV Glendale (YTB 641), CFAV Glenevis (YTB 642), CFAV Glenbrook (YTB 643), and CFAV Glenside (YTB 644), distributed between the two coasts.[55] Transition to four new naval large tugs under construction since 2020 includes CFAV Haro and CFAV Barkerville, delivered to CFB Esquimalt in August 2024 for Pacific operations, with CFAV Canso and CFAV Stella Maris scheduled for CFB Halifax in October 2025 and September 2026, respectively; these IMO Tier III-compliant vessels offer improved bollard pull over 50 tonnes, fire-fighting integration, and limited salvage capabilities to augment and eventually supplant the Glens.[56][57] Yard diving tenders (YDTs) facilitate underwater maintenance, mine countermeasures training, and explosive ordnance disposal by Fleet Diving Units, equipped with launch/recovery systems for divers and small submersibles. Key active units include CFAV Sooke (YDT 612) at CFB Esquimalt, utilized for hands-on naval officer training as of October 2025, and CFAV Sechelt (YDT 610) at CFB Halifax; additional tenders comprise CFAV Granby (YDT 12) and an unnamed YDT 11, converted from earlier auxiliary hulls in the late 1990s for east- and west-coast support.[58]

Operational Readiness and Challenges

Fleet availability metrics

The Royal Canadian Navy maintains fleet serviceability rates of approximately 46 to 51 percent for meeting training, readiness, and operational requirements, based on assessments through late 2024, with trends indicating further declines in deployable assets.[59][60] Submarine readiness hovers at 20-30 percent, often limiting the Victoria-class fleet to one high-readiness boat amid extended maintenance cycles, while frigate sortie generation is constrained by modernization backlogs and ageing hulls.[61] Annual sea days fall short of internal targets, averaging below NATO peer expectations for sustained maritime presence, with frigates logging around 2,100-2,500 days collectively in prior fiscal years but facing reduced output due to resource strains.[62] Personnel shortfalls exacerbate these metrics, with the Canadian Armed Forces facing a deficit exceeding 14,000 members overall and the RCN specifically short about 1,000 sailors, leading to overburdened crews and deferred training.[63][64] Maintenance backlogs, including over 76,000 outstanding third-line hours as of mid-2010s evaluations (with persistent growth reported), and supply chain inefficienciesâ€”such as late materiel deliveries and poor stock managementâ€”directly impede high-readiness postures by prioritizing reactive repairs over preventive sustainment.[65][66] These factors result in a deployability gap, where operational tempo relies heavily on allied interoperability rather than independent surge capacity. In comparison to peer navies, the RCN's availability lags the U.S. Navy's targeted 70 percent-plus readiness for major combatants and sustained global deployments, while mirroring some Royal Navy challenges in fleet understrength but with lower absolute sea day outputs relative to hull numbers.[59][67] Government audits attribute this to chronic underinvestment in sustainment, contrasting with U.S. and U.K. emphases on integrated logistics that enable higher sortie rates despite similar platform ages.[68]

Submarine operational limitations

The Victoria-class submarines, acquired second-hand from the United Kingdom as decommissioned Upholder-class vessels in the late 1990s, have experienced persistent mechanical and electrical failures that severely restrict their operational tempo. Notable incidents include the 2004 fire aboard HMCS Chicoutimi caused by seawater ingress through inadequately sealed hatches, leading to extensive electrical damage during transit, and the 2011 grounding of HMCS Corner Brook off Vancouver Island, which resulted in a 2-meter breach in the hull and prolonged repairs. More recently, in June 2025, HMCS Corner Brook sustained severe damage during dockyard testing at Seaspan Vancouver due to rushed procedures and communication lapses, exacerbating maintenance backlogs.[69][70][71] These reliability issues manifest in low fleet availability, with submarines spending more time in refit than at sea; as of 2021, only one vessel, HMCS Victoria, had emerged from extended maintenance for trials, while others remained sidelined, a pattern persisting into 2025 where modernization efforts delayed full operability of even two boats until that year. Peak operational readiness rarely exceeds one or two submarines simultaneously, far below the four-vessel fleet's potential, due to challenges adapting the aging British design to Canadian sustainment infrastructure, which lacks specialized expertise and parts supply chains optimized for these platforms.[72][73] Strategically, this unreliability undermines anti-submarine warfare deterrence across the Atlantic and Pacific approaches, compelling the Royal Canadian Navy to depend on allied partners like the United States and United Kingdom for subsurface threat coverage in joint operations. Sustainment costs have ballooned, with ongoing repairs and upgrades consuming hundreds of millions annually against minimal deployable output, as highlighted in departmental evaluations emphasizing the fleet's obsolescence by the mid-2030s.[74][68]

Surface fleet sustainment issues

The Royal Canadian Navy's surface fleet, comprising Halifax-class frigates, Kingston-class coastal defence vessels, and auxiliary oiler MV Asterix, has faced persistent sustainment difficulties stemming from maintenance backlogs, supply chain disruptions, and infrastructure constraints at key facilities like Irving Shipyard in Halifax. These challenges have reduced overall fleet readiness, with frigates and patrol vessels often sidelined for extended refits, limiting deployable assets for routine operations. In 2023, RCN Commander Vice-Admiral Angus Topshee warned of a "critical state" in fleet readiness, attributing shortfalls to systemic sustainment pressures that risked failing force posture commitments into 2024 and beyond.[75] The Halifax-class Frigate Equipment Life Extension (FELEX) modernization program, intended to extend service life through upgrades to combat systems and propulsion, experienced significant cost escalation, reaching $4.3 billion by completion in 2018â€”roughly double the original projections due to scope expansions and contractor inefficiencies. Post-FELEX, frigates have encountered reliability issues, including propulsion failures and flooding events, as evidenced by historical incidents like the 2014 collision involving HMCS Winnipeg that injured crew and required major repairs. Spare parts procurement delays have compounded these problems, with supply chain vulnerabilitiesâ€”exacerbated by reliance on international suppliersâ€”leading to prolonged downtime for vessels awaiting critical components.[76][77][78] Kingston-class vessels, designed for low-intensity coastal duties, have similarly suffered from aging hulls and outdated systems, resulting in high maintenance demands that exceed operational utilization. Irving Shipbuilding's boom-and-bust workforce cyclesâ€”marked by hiring surges for new construction followed by layoffsâ€”have periodically hampered refit throughput, forcing the RCN to defer non-critical repairs and rely on interim measures. Political reticence in allocating supplemental sustainment funds has widened capability gaps, particularly for sustained presence in contested areas like the Indo-Pacific and Arctic, where surface combatants are needed for deterrence but often unavailable due to these bottlenecks.[79][80]

Procurement and Future Fleet Composition

Submarine replacement programs

The Canadian Patrol Submarine Project (CPSP), initiated in 2021, seeks to acquire up to 12 conventionally powered submarines equipped with air-independent propulsion (AIP) systems to replace the aging Victoria-class fleet and enable persistent patrols across the Atlantic, Pacific, and Arctic oceans.[12][81] These submarines must operate in harsh Arctic conditions, including under-ice capabilities, to address strategic gaps in undersea domain awareness amid growing regional threats from adversaries like Russia and China.[82] The estimated acquisition cost exceeds $60 billion CAD, covering design, construction, and initial sustainment for the full fleet.[83] In August 2025, Public Services and Procurement Canada downselected two qualified suppliers from initial respondents: Germany's ThyssenKrupp Marine Systems (tkMS) offering the Type 212CD design, and South Korea's Hanwha Ocean proposing a variant of its KSS-III Batch II submarine.[81][83] The Type 212CD, already contracted by Germany and Norway for their own fleets, features hydrogen fuel-cell AIP for extended submerged endurance and steel hulls optimized for cold-water operations, positioning it as a mature, off-the-shelf option with potential for Canadian industrial participation.[82] Hanwha's KSS-III, also AIP-equipped with lithium-ion batteries in later variants, emphasizes rapid deliveryâ€”potentially four units before 2035â€”and proven export potential, though critics note risks in adapting it for Arctic under-ice missions compared to the Type 212CD's northern pedigree.[84] No contracts have been awarded as of October 2025, with evaluations ongoing amid requirements for domestic content and technology transfer.[81] In October 2025, Germany and Norway intensified advocacy for Canada to join their Type 212CD partnership, with defense ministers meeting Canadian counterparts in Ottawa to highlight interoperability benefits, shared production lines for up to 12 additional units, and alignment with NATO priorities in the North Atlantic.[85][86] This pitch underscores mutual strategic interests, including burden-sharing against Russian submarine activity in the Arctic, but tkMS officials acknowledged that full Canadian construction would face delays beyond initial foreign builds due to infrastructure limitations.[87] The program's evolution reflects critiques of scope creep: early efforts focused on extending Victoria-class service life into the 2030s, but persistent maintenance shortfalls and zero operational submarines at sea prompted a shift to full replacement, exacerbating delays and capability voids projected post-2030 as hulls reach end-of-life.[88] Analysts attribute these gaps to underinvestment in undersea warfare, warning that without accelerated decisions, Canada risks prolonged reliance on allied assets for maritime deterrence.[89] Final selection is anticipated in 2026, contingent on affordability reviews and geopolitical alignments.[90]

Frigate and destroyer replacements

The River-class destroyer program, previously designated as the Canadian Surface Combatant (CSC), is procuring 15 adaptable multi-mission surface combatants based on the United Kingdom's Type 26 design to replace the Royal Canadian Navy's aging Halifax-class frigates.[91] These vessels emphasize anti-air warfare (AAW) and anti-submarine warfare (ASW) capabilities, incorporating 32 Mk 41 vertical launch system (VLS) cells for missiles such as the SM-2 or SM-6, enabling enhanced defense against peer adversaries including those from China and Russia.[92] The reclassification from frigates to destroyers reflects this AAW prioritization, as the ships are engineered for high-speed, long-endurance operations with integrated sensors like the Type 997 Artemis radar and advanced combat management systems.[92][93] Program milestones include the awarding of a construction contract to Irving Shipbuilding Inc. on March 8, 2025, marking the transition to detailed build activities at the Halifax Shipyard, with full-rate production commencing on June 27, 2025.[94][95] The implementation phase advanced further on October 7, 2025, with CAE securing a subcontract from Lockheed Martin Canada for mission systems integration, though overall delivery of the lead ship is projected no earlier than 2035 due to persistent industrial bottlenecks.[96][97] These delays stem from supply chain issues and facility limitations at Irving, including inadequate climate-controlled environments that hinder precision assembly, exacerbating risks to schedule adherence.[98] Lifecycle costs for the fleet have escalated beyond initial projections, with the Parliamentary Budget Officer estimating approximately $84 billion CAD as of 2024 assessments, more than triple the original budget and driven by design modifications, inflation, and scope expansions for interoperability with NATO allies.[99] Critics, including defence analysts, highlight procurement secrecy and cost overruns as symptomatic of systemic inefficiencies in Canada's National Shipbuilding Strategy, potentially compromising fleet readiness amid rising Indo-Pacific tensions.[100][101] Despite these challenges, the destroyers' modular architecture allows for future upgrades in missile capacity and unmanned systems, aligning with strategic imperatives for credible deterrence against advanced threats.[102]

Patrol vessel and auxiliary procurements

The Royal Canadian Navy plans to phase out its 12 Kingston-class maritime coastal defence vessels between 2025 and 2028, beginning with the decommissioning of eight ships in fall 2025, without dedicated replacement vessels immediately entering service.[103][104] These vessels, commissioned from 1996 to 1999, have primarily supported coastal defence, mine countermeasures, and low-intensity operations, but their retirement aligns with broader fleet modernization under the National Shipbuilding Strategy, which prioritizes domestic construction for economic benefits over faster off-the-shelf acquisitions, contributing to extended timelines and elevated costs.[103][105] No contracts have been awarded by October 2025 for direct Kingston successors, such as proposed multi-ocean patrol vessels or light corvettes larger than the 970-tonne Kingstons, though conceptual discussions advocate for modular, 1,500-2,000-tonne designs to handle multi-role tasks including coastal patrol and limited offshore duties.[106] In the interim, the six Harry DeWolf-class Arctic and offshore patrol ships, with the final vessel accepted in August 2025, are slated to absorb select Kingston roles like counter-narcotics patrols, despite their larger 6,600-tonne displacement and Arctic-focused design limiting full equivalence for near-shore operations.[107][25] This transition risks temporary gaps in coastal defence capabilities, as the Harry DeWolf vessels cannot replicate the Kingstons' numbers or specialized minehunting equipment without additional modifications.[105] For auxiliary support, the two Protecteur-class joint support ships, intended to replace retired auxiliary oiler replenishment vessels, face delays under the National Shipbuilding Strategy's domestic build requirements at Seaspan Shipyards. The lead ship, HMCS Protecteur, launched in December 2024, is now projected for delivery and commissioning in late 2025, with sea trials in 2025, following slippage from an original 2023 target due to construction complexities and supply chain issues.[108][109][110] The second vessel, HMCS Preserver, is expected in 2026 or later, providing replenishment-at-sea, sealift, and limited air capabilities to enhance fleet sustainment, though the interim reliance on the converted commercial MV Asterix underscores procurement inefficiencies from prioritizing Canadian industry over expedited foreign options.[108][111]
