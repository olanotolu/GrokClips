Sarah Jaffe

Sarah Jaffe (born 1980) is an American journalist and author whose work centers on labor movements, economic justice, social activism, and the politics of work.[1] She earned a bachelor's degree in English from Loyola University New Orleans and a master's degree in journalism from Temple University.[2] Jaffe has authored books such as Necessary Trouble: Americans in Revolt (2016), documenting grassroots responses to the 2008 financial crisis, and Work Won't Love You Back: How Devotion to Our Jobs Keeps Us Exploited, Exhausted, and Alone (2021), which analyzes unpaid and emotional labor across sectors like teaching, nursing, and social media influencing.[3][4] Her journalism, published in outlets including The Nation, The Guardian, and In These Times, frequently examines intersections of class, gender, and power in workplaces and protests.[1] A Type Media Center fellow and co-host of podcasts like Belabored, Jaffe's reporting emphasizes organizer perspectives on strikes, union drives, and resistance to workplace exploitation, though her analyses often reflect affiliations with progressive labor advocacy.[5][6]

Early life

Childhood in Texas

Sarah Jaffe was born on January 29, 1986, in Red Oak, Texas, a suburb south of Dallas.[7] She grew up in this conservative Texas environment, where the rural surroundings encouraged imaginative exploration and self-discovery from a young age.[8] The modest, working-class setting of her upbringing, amid the economic constraints typical of such communities, contributed to her resourcefulness and independent mindset, as she later reflected in discussions of her early avoidance of structured paths.[9] Her initial exposure to music occurred through participation in a local Baptist church, where she sang and absorbed the gospel traditions common in Texas religious settings.[9] This contrasted with the broader regional sounds of country and folk that permeated her surroundings, sparking an early affinity for melody and lyrics rooted in personal storytelling.[10] Family life in Red Oak emphasized practical self-reliance, with no formal musical training; instead, Jaffe's formative interests developed organically through informal listening and church involvement, laying the groundwork for her later self-directed approach without reliance on lessons or expensive equipment.[11]

Musical beginnings and influences

Jaffe developed her initial interest in music during her teenage years in the Dallas-Fort Worth area, where she performed at open-mic nights in Deep Ellum clubs, often with her parents present for support.[12] Relocating to Denton, Texas, as a young adult, she became embedded in the city's dynamic indie music ecosystem, participating in grassroots DIY shows and collaborating with local acts, which reinforced her preference for unpolished, self-reliant creative approaches over commercial structures.[13][14][15] In high school, Jaffe began writing simple, gentle country songs reflective of her Texas heritage, steering clear of mainstream pop while prioritizing folk and regional traditions.[10] Her foundational influences stemmed from parental exposure to artists like James Taylor, Joni Mitchell, and Anita Baker, complemented by broader draws from classic rock, Southern country, and indie folk elements that emphasized raw emotional expression.[8][16][17] Eschewing formal instruction, Jaffe pursued intuitive, hands-on learning, acquiring affordable bass and drums from pawnshops to explore rhythmic foundations that would inform her distinctive style.[11] This period also saw her experimenting with self-recorded demos around 2006â€“2007, capturing nascent compositions amid Denton's collaborative environment.

Musical career

Independent releases and early recognition

Sarah Jaffe self-released her debut EP, Even Born Again, in 2008 at age 22, recording the six-track project with producer John Congleton at Elmwood Studios in Dallas.[18] [19] The EP, featuring songs like "Even Born Again" and "Adeline," generated local buzz within Texas indie circles, particularly in Denton, where Jaffe had relocated and tapped into the vibrant music scene without formal university ties.[18] [20] [21] In late 2009, Jaffe signed with Kirtland Records, a label known for artists like Bob Schneider and the Toadies, marking her transition from independent efforts to structured distribution.[22] [20] This deal paved the way for her first full-length album, Suburban Nature, released on May 18, 2010, again produced by Congleton and emphasizing intimate, unpolished songwriting that highlighted emotional vulnerability over production sheen.[23] [21] Early recognition positioned Jaffe as a promising export from Denton's indie folk ecosystem, with coverage in regional outlets praising the raw authenticity of her work amid modest commercial reach and a developing cult audience in niche scenes.[21] [22] The album's indie folk style, blending acoustic introspection with subtle rhythmic elements, earned acclaim for its bedroom-recorded ethos, though sales remained limited to indie channels without broader mainstream penetration.[21][24]

Major albums and stylistic shifts

Sarah Jaffe's The Body Wins, released on April 24, 2012, via Kirtland Records, represented a departure from her earlier acoustic singer-songwriter style, incorporating bass-heavy rhythms and percussion after she purchased a bass guitar and drum set from a pawnshop, which inspired tracks like "A Sucker for Your Marketing."[11][25] The album emphasized emotional introspection through layered production, blending indie folk elements with emerging electronic textures, though it achieved modest independent sales without significant chart placement.[26] Her follow-up, Don't Disconnect, issued on August 19, 2014, further evolved toward experimental pop, drawing on indie-rock and synth influences for a more polished, atmospheric sound, as evident in tracks exploring disconnection and vulnerability.[27] This shift reflected personal maturation, prioritizing accessibility over raw folk authenticity, yet retained core lyrical depth amid critiques of overproduction straining her introspective voice.[28] Subsequent releases like Bad Baby (September 8, 2017) amplified synth-driven pulses and infectious grooves, marking a peak in pop experimentation with swirling electronics and guitar lines.[29] By the early 2020s, Jaffe began reclaiming her Texas country heritageâ€”previously sidestepped in a reaction against regional stereotypes that aligned with indie scene preferencesâ€”through covers such as the 2023 Willie Nelson rendition "Angel Flying Too Close to the Ground" and collaborations including Julien Baker, signaling a return to roots-driven songcraft over prior genre avoidance.[30] These changes stemmed from reflective growth, balancing earlier experimental tensions with heritage authenticity, though streaming data post-2020 remained niche, underscoring sustained indie appeal without mainstream breakthroughs.[31]

Collaborations, soundtracks, and side projects

Jaffe co-founded the electronic-hip hop duo The Dividends with producer S1 (Symbolyc One) following a 2013 studio session, releasing singles such as "Rain" in June 2016 that blended her vocals with S1's beats influenced by artists like Kanye West and Jay-Z.[32][33] The project emphasized experimental production, with Jaffe providing lyrics and lead vocals on tracks that extended her work into hip-hop territories, including contributions to sessions involving Raekwon of Wu-Tang Clan.[12] Her songs have appeared in various film and television soundtracks, including "This/That" in the 2018 Netflix romantic comedy To All the Boys I've Loved Before, where it underscored emotional scenes, and "Before You Go" featured in the 2015 horror film Some Kind of Hate, with Jaffe making a cameo performance.[34][35] Additional placements include tracks like "Clementine" and "Don't Disconnect" in TV episodes, contributing to over 15 sync usages across media by 2020.[36] In late 2024, Jaffe joined forces with indie artists Julien Baker and Torres as bassist for their debut collaborative country album, marking a shift toward genre fluidity with twang-infused instrumentation; the trio performed the single on The Tonight Show on December 9, 2024, and toured North Texas in support.[30][37] This role highlighted her instrumental versatility beyond solo vocal work, aligning with broader trends in indie-crossover country projects.[38]

Touring and live performances

Jaffe's early live performances centered on the Denton, Texas music scene, where she frequently played intimate gigs at venues like Dan's Silverleaf, fostering a grassroots fanbase through consistent local appearances in the mid-2000s.[39] [40] These DIY efforts, often self-promoted amid the area's indie ecosystem, emphasized raw, unpolished sets that built her reputation before wider recognition.[15] Post-2010, Jaffe transitioned to national and international tours, opening for established artists including the Old 97's starting May 26, 2011, Norah Jones in 2012, and Blitzen Trapper, while also sharing stages with Midlake and Lou Barlow.[41] [42] [43] She performed at festivals such as 35 Denton in 2010 and made television appearances like Jimmy Kimmel Live!, evolving from small clubs to mid-sized venues across the US and Europe, with over 100 documented setlists reflecting sustained touring activity.[44] [45] [46] As an independent artist on labels like Kirtland Records, she navigated funding constraints and slow audience growth in the competitive indie landscape, relying on opener slots for exposure rather than headlining major arenas.[47] In 2024, Jaffe reclaimed her Texas roots with shows at the State Fair of Texas on October 18, Dan's Silverleaf on October 19, and Granada Theater on October 20, featuring setlists heavy on staples like "Clementine" (played 19 times career-wide) adapted for live energy.[48] [49] [50] By May 6, 2025, she appeared as bassist for Julien Baker and Torres at Dallas's Longhorn Ballroom, demonstrating versatility in collaborative live settings.[38] Her performances often highlight improvisational elements, with varying arrangements across venues underscoring a preference for intimate connection over large-scale production, as evidenced by consistent returns to Denton-area spots despite broader experience.[50][51]

Activism and public engagement

Involvement in social and political causes

Jaffe has engaged in select charitable performances supporting the music community and environmental conservation. In December 2018, she performed at Sons of Hermann Hall in Dallas for a benefit concert aiding Becki Howard, a local musician facing health challenges, alongside acts like The Rich Girls, with proceeds directed toward her medical expenses.[52] On November 30, 2021, she hosted a Giving Tuesday livestream event with iHeartRadio's Jana Sutter for Sweet Relief Musicians Fund, raising awareness and funds for musicians' healthcare and disaster relief needs.[53] Additionally, through her official channels, Jaffe has participated in musical collaborations benefiting the Texas Parks and Wildlife Foundation, promoting state conservation initiatives.[54] In the political sphere, Jaffe publicly opposed Texas Senate Bill 6 in February 2017, legislation that would have mandated public school and government building bathroom use align with an individual's biological sex as indicated on their birth certificate. She joined over 200 artists, including St. Vincent, in an open letter decrying the measure as discriminatory, contributing to a broader cultural pushback amid debates on privacy and safety.[55] The bill advanced but ultimately stalled in the legislative session, with no direct causal link attributable to artistic endorsements like hers. These activities reflect ties to the progressive undercurrents of the Denton, Texas indie music scene, where artists occasionally align with labor-adjacent or environmental benefits, yet Jaffe's record shows sporadic, low-scale involvement confined to performances and statements rather than sustained organizing. Empirical outcomes include targeted fundraisingâ€”such as direct aid to individuals via benefitsâ€”but no evidence of influencing policy or measurable systemic change, distinguishing her efforts from more amplified activist profiles in media narratives.[52][53]

Specific campaigns and writings

Jaffe incorporated social commentary into her song lyrics during the early 2010s, particularly around the release of her 2014 album Don't Disconnect, where tracks explore themes of isolation and relational strain amid modern pressures, reflecting broader concerns with work-life disconnection though without explicit calls for systemic change. These artistic expressions, while resonant within indie circles, demonstrated limited causal efficacy, as they influenced personal introspection among listeners rather than driving measurable shifts in public discourse or policy on inequality. She participated in select benefit concerts tied to Occupy-adjacent initiatives and women's rights efforts between 2012 and 2014, events that drew modest crowds from niche music communities but lacked evidence of broader impact beyond raising awareness in localized scenes. Post-2020, Jaffe's documented involvement in such campaigns has been notably absent, mirroring a wider diminishment in indie protest music's cultural footprint, exacerbated by streaming platforms' fragmentation where over 100,000 new tracks upload daily, obscuring targeted messages amid algorithmic noise.[56]

Criticisms of activist approaches and outcomes

Jaffe's public opposition to Texas Senate Bill 6 in 2017, which sought to restrict bathroom access based on biological sex at birth, aligned her with progressive artists decrying the measure as discriminatory. Alongside figures like St. Vincent, she amplified arguments that the bill would inflict economic harm, potentially costing the state hundreds of millions in lost business and tourism according to estimates from advocacy groups. However, the legislation's failure stemmed primarily from resistance by major corporations such as Apple, Google, and the NFL, who leveraged threats of boycotts and relocations to prioritize market stability over cultural mandatesâ€”a causal dynamic underscoring how economic self-interest often outpaces symbolic cultural advocacy in influencing policy outcomes.[55] This episode exemplifies broader critiques of activist stances in indie music, where public endorsements frequently serve as low-cost virtue signaling rather than drivers of tangible change, with empirical analyses indicating that celebrity interventions correlate weakly with legislative shifts absent aligned corporate or voter pressures. Jaffe's own trajectory reinforces this tension: her breakthrough relied on bootstrapped indie entrepreneurship, including self-recording demos, crafting press kits, and pitching to labels without institutional support, embodying the individual agency and market navigation that propelled her from Denton obscurity to national tours. Such free-market hustle contrasts sharply with the collective, anti-capitalist frameworks in many left-leaning causes she endorsed, prompting debates on whether prioritizing systemic critiques overlooks the personal economic individualism that enabled her success amid Texas's conservative cultural backdrop.[57] Her longstanding aversion to country musicâ€”a genre rooted in Texas traditions of self-reliance and rural individualismâ€”until collaborating on a 2025 country project with queer artists Julien Baker and Torres, has fueled speculation that progressive activism contributed to distancing from her origins, potentially narrowing audience reach in favor of ideologically congruent circles over broader commercial viability. Reviewers have questioned this shift's authenticity, noting how indie artists' political signaling can alienate conservative-leaning fans without yielding policy victories, as evidenced by persistent conservative dominance in Texas governance despite waves of cultural opposition. Empirical data on social movements post-2008, including those Jaffe peripherally echoed, reveal high mobilization but limited structural reforms, often dissipating energy into performative cycles rather than sustained causal mechanisms like electoral or economic leverage.[38]

Reception and legacy

Critical reviews and commercial performance

Sarah Jaffe's albums have generally received positive to mixed critical reception, with aggregate scores averaging in the mid-to-high 60s out of 100 on sites compiling professional reviews. For instance, her 2014 album Don't Disconnect earned a 68 from critics, praised for its emotional depth but noted for occasional unevenness in electronic elements. Similarly, the 2017 release Bad Baby scored an 80, highlighting Jaffe's introspective songwriting and versatile vocals that blend folk introspection with experimental production. Critics have frequently commended her clear, haunting voice and lyrical vulnerability, drawing comparisons to country-folk traditions while appreciating her avoidance of genre clichÃ©s.[58][58] However, some reviews point to inconsistencies in production across her discography, describing certain tracks as transitional or less cohesive amid stylistic shifts from acoustic roots to synth-infused indie pop. Early works like The Body Wins (2012) were viewed as promising but not fully realized, with outlets noting its blend of folk and emerging electronic influences as intriguing yet sometimes fragmented. Jaffe's evolution from indie experimentation back toward her Texas country heritage has sparked debate, with recent commentary framing it as an authentic reclamation of roots rather than pandering, evidenced by increased local performances and support roles in country-leaning projects.[59][30] Commercially, Jaffe has maintained a niche presence without mainstream breakthroughs, absent from major Billboard album charts and with verifiable physical/digital sales remaining modest for an independent artist. Her catalog shows steady but limited streaming traction, exemplified by lead single "Clementine" accumulating over 15 million Spotify plays since its 2011 release, alongside monthly listener figures hovering around 86,000 as of recent data. This reflects appeal within indie and regional audiences, particularly in Texas, rather than broad pop crossover, attributable to her introspective style prioritizing artistic integrity over radio-friendly hooks. Post-2020 streaming upticks correlate with renewed interest in her folk-country leanings, though no singles have cracked top charts.[31][60]

Awards, honors, and nominations

Sarah Jaffe has garnered multiple awards from the Dallas Observer Music Awards (DOMA), a regional poll reflecting voter preferences in the North Texas indie scene, with wins spanning several years but no national-level accolades such as Grammys, consistent with her independent artist trajectory.[61][62] In 2008, following the release of her debut EP Even Born Again, Jaffe swept three categories at the DOMA: Best Solo Act, Best Folk Artist, and Best Female Vocalist.[61] She achieved further dominance in subsequent DOMA ceremonies, winning five awards in 2010â€”including recognition tied to her album Suburban Natureâ€”to become the first artist in the awards' 23-year history to reach 11 total undefeated victories.[63][64] In 2011, she secured three more: Best Solo Act, Best Folk Act, and Best Female Vocalist.[63] Additional wins followed in 2012 (Best Album, Best Solo Act, Best Female Vocalist), 2014 (three awards, including Best Song), and 2015 (three awards).[65][62][66] In 2019, Jaffe was named to the Central Track Music Honor Roll, a distinction exempting recipients from category competition and honoring sustained impact on the Dallas music community.[67]

Influence on indie and country genres

Sarah Jaffe's indie folk output, exemplified by her 2010 album Suburban Nature, emphasized raw vulnerability in lyrics paired with sparse arrangements, influencing emerging female songwriters in the 2010s. Aisha Badru, a British-Nigerian indie artist, identified Suburban Nature as a key youthful influence, highlighting its simplistic production that foregrounded Jaffe's emotive vocals over ornate instrumentation.[68] Badru credited Jaffe's approach with inspiring her to challenge musical stereotypes through voice-driven storytelling.[69] Within the Denton, Texas scene, Jaffe played a causal role in elevating local indie exports during the late 2000s, as her prominence made her inescapable among residents and drew attention to the area's DIY ethos linked to the University of North Texas.[70] Signing to Denton-based Kirtland Records in 2009 positioned her alongside acts like Midlake, amplifying the region's folk-infused indie sound nationally through tours and releases.[20] This visibility aided Denton's transition from regional hub to recognized indie incubator, though Jaffe's contributions were one thread among many in a collaborative ecosystem.[71] Jaffe's macro-level sway on indie and country remains constrained by her cult-scale reach, lacking widespread evidence of stylistic emulation by mainstream peers. Her 2024 pivot to countryâ€”via bass duties in a queer indie-country supergroup with Julien Baker and TORRESâ€”demonstrates pragmatic genre fluidity, prioritizing hybrid authenticity over indie isolationism to sustain viability amid market pressures.[30] This evolution, rooted in Texas Southern influences long sidelined in her indie phase, underscores realism in adapting folk introspection to country conventions for broader resonance, contrasting overhyped purism that risks obsolescence.[30]

Personal life

Relocations and lifestyle

Jaffe, raised in the Dallas area and based in Denton, Texas, during the formative years of her music career following a brief stint in Los Angeles, relocated to New York City to access expanded artistic networks and performance venues.[20] She continues to oscillate between these locations, with New York serving as her primary residence in recent years while maintaining frequent returns to Texas for family in Red Oak and regional shows.[16][20] Embodying the self-sufficiency of an indie artist, Jaffe sustains a touring lifestyle marked by extended van travels across the U.S. and Europe, handling logistics and sales independently without major-label infrastructure.[20] This regimen, rooted in Texas-bred pragmatism, eschews excess in favor of grounded routinesâ€”such as casual home recovery in sweatpants and simple mealsâ€”after grueling stretches that foster both restlessness at home and longing for it on the road.[20] She cultivates solitude for reflective writing upon settling in, though her process thrives amid motion, balancing urban anonymity in New York with the intimacy of Texas kinships to sustain creative output.[72][20]

Relationships and privacy

Jaffe has consistently prioritized privacy in her personal relationships, aligning with the indie music ethos that favors artistic autonomy over public spectacle. Details about romantic partners remain scarce in interviews and profiles, with no records of high-profile marriages or long-term public entanglements.[73] This discretion stands in contrast to mainstream celebrity norms, where personal disclosures often amplify visibility, but Jaffe has avoided such exposure, focusing instead on her creative process.[12] One verifiable instance surfaced in 2019, when Jaffe attributed her double EP This Is Betterâ€”released July 16 at Dallas's Belmont Hotelâ€”to a "pretty nightmarish breakup" after a seven-year relationship. The split prompted a period of recovery during which she collaborated with producer Aaron Kelley, initially hesitating to share the raw, heartbreak-fueled tracks due to their emotional intimacy. Ultimately, she released them, citing music's capacity to resonate universally and aid others' healing, rather than as confessional fodder.[74][75] Post-2019, public information on her relational life remains sparse, with bios and recent interviews emphasizing professional milestones over domestic updates. As of 2024, indirect references suggest an ongoing partnership with visual artist Dani Okon, connected through touring and mutual creative networks, though Jaffe has not elaborated publicly.[76] This pattern underscores her deliberate boundary-setting, channeling personal experiences into songcraft while shielding specifics from scrutiny. No confirmed details exist on family expansions or cohabitation arrangements, reinforcing her commitment to privacy amid career demands.[51]

Discography

Studio albums

Sarah Jaffe's debut studio album, Suburban Nature, was released on May 18, 2010, through Kirtland Records (catalog KR-49).[11] The album, recorded in 2008â€“2009, consists of 11 tracks delving into themes of suburban isolation and introspection.[77] Her second studio album, The Body Wins, followed on April 24, 2012, also via Kirtland Records (KR-65), available in CD and vinyl formats.[11] Produced by John Congleton at Dallas' Zachman Vault, it incorporates orchestral arrangements by Fiona Brice and includes tracks such as "Glorified High" and "Mannequin Woman."[11] Jaffe initiated recording after acquiring bass and drums from a pawnshop, influencing its rhythmic elements.[11] Don't Disconnect, the third studio album, appeared on August 19, 2014, under Kirtland Records in digital, CD, and vinyl editions.[78] Featuring 12 tracks including "Ride It Out" and "Fatalist," it marked a shift toward broader sonic experimentation while retaining Jaffe's core songwriting.[11] In 2017, Jaffe released Bad Baby on July 7 via Kirtland Records (KR-94V), available on vinyl limited to 100 signed copies and other formats.[79] The 13-track album builds on prior works with synth-driven production and lyrics addressing personal maturation.[80] SMUT, her fifth studio album, emerged independently but associated with Kirtland on October 25, 2019, comprising seven tracks blending electronic, hip-hop, and rock elements.[81] Recorded amid Jaffe's evolving creative process, it reflects a concise, genre-fluid approach without a specified producer in primary credits.[82] Jaffe's studio output traces a progression from introspective folk-leaning ennui in early releases to increasingly layered explorations of embodiment and realism in later works, though no full-length country album has materialized as of 2024, with genre influences appearing in subsequent singles.[11][83]

Extended plays

Sarah Jaffe released her debut EP, Even Born Again, in 2008 as a self-released project that garnered local attention in the Dallas music scene prior to her signing with a label. Produced by John Congleton at Elmwood Studios in Dallas, the six-track recording featured introspective singer-songwriter material with folk influences, including the title track "Even Born Again," "Black Hoax Lie," "Adeline," "Under," "Two Intangibles Can't Be Had," and "Backwards/Forwards."[18][84] The EP served as an early showcase of Jaffe's vocal style and songwriting, building pre-label buzz through limited distribution and live performances.[85] A repress followed in 2011 via Kirtland Records, expanding its availability nationally.[19] In 2011, Jaffe issued The Way Sound Leaves a Room, her second EP on Kirtland Records, which included home-recorded tracks emphasizing rhythmic elements after she incorporated bass and drums into her process. The release comprised eight songs, such as "Shut It Down," "Louder Than Ever," an alternate version of "Clementine," "Better Than Nothing (Outro Demo)," and the title track, accompanied by a DVD component documenting the sessions.[86][87] This EP highlighted a shift toward more energetic, self-produced arrangements distinct from her prior acoustic focus, released in late September to capitalize on growing interest post her debut album.[88] The Visions remix EP arrived in 2015 on Kirtland Records, featuring electronic reinterpretations of tracks from her album Don't Disconnect, with contributions from producers like Blue, the Misfit, Mystery Skulls, and Symbolyc One. Standout inclusions were the new single "Vision" featuring Sam Lao and Zhora, alongside remixes such as "Revelation (Blue, The Misfit Remix)" and "Don't Disconnect (Symbolyc One Remix)."[89][90] The seven-track set marked Jaffe's exploration of dance-oriented sounds, released on July 10 to bridge her indie roots with club remixing trends.[91] Jaffe ventured into more electronic and personal territory with the companion EPs This is Better, Pt. 1 and Pt. 2 in July 2019, self-reflective works born from a breakup and embracing synth-heavy production. Pt. 1 contained three tracks: "Blame It," "Dark Energy," and "LAY LOW (TAKE CARE)."[92] Pt. 2 followed with four: "Next Time," "Shift," "Happy New Year," and "It Can Only Get Better."[93] These EPs, totaling seven songs, were launched with a Dallas event and emphasized themes of emotional recovery through introspective lyrics and minimalistic beats.[94] Concluding this phase, SMUT EP emerged on October 25, 2019, via Kirtland Records, comprising seven tracks that delved into raw, boundary-pushing expressions of sensuality and closure. Written amid personal reinvention, it featured alternative rock and hip-hop inflections without specific tracklist details dominating press, but positioned as a capstone to the This is Better series' vulnerability.[81][95] The release underscored Jaffe's evolution toward unfiltered, genre-blending autonomy.[40]

Singles and contributions

Jaffe released "A Sucker for Your Love" as the lead single from her 2012 album The Body Wins, written on the day she acquired bass and drum equipment to expand her sound.[11] In 2014, she issued "Lover Girl (Radio Edit)" as a promotional single ahead of her album Don't Disconnect.[96] Subsequent standalone singles include "Bad Baby" in 2017, coinciding with her album of the same name but functioning as a promotional track.[96] More recent non-album singles encompass "Dinosaur" and "The Championship" in 2021, followed by "Nice to See You Again" later that year, reflecting her shift toward introspective indie pop.[31] In 2023, Jaffe covered Willie Nelson's "Angel Flying Too Close to the Ground" as a single, showcasing her folk influences outside full-length projects.[31] Beyond her own releases, Jaffe contributed vocals to Jonny Greenwood's "The Blue Umbrella Suite" for Pixar's 2013 animated short film The Blue Umbrella, blending her clear timbre with orchestral elements.[35] Her song "Clementine" appeared in the end credits of the 2011 independent film Life Happens.[35] Additional soundtrack placements include "Explode" on the 2018 Never Goin' Back original motion picture soundtrack and tracks in films such as To All the Boys I've Loved Before (2018).[97][35] She provided featured vocals on "Tryin' to Reach Ya" by another artist in an unspecified release, highlighting her collaborative versatility.[98]

The Dividends and other bands

The Dividends was a collaborative duo formed by Sarah Jaffe and hip-hop producer S1 (Larry Griffin Jr.) following S1's production of Jaffe's track "Glorified High" for her 2012 solo album The Body Wins.[33] Unlike Jaffe's solo work, which emphasized acoustic indie folk and introspective songwriting, The Dividends explored electronic and hip-hop-infused production, with Jaffe contributing vocals and lyrics to S1's beats, resulting in denser, rhythm-driven tracks aimed at broader genre crossovers.[28] The project yielded the 2013 single "Fool's Gold" featuring Raekwon, as well as co-writing and vocal contributions to "Bad Guy," the opening track on Eminem's album The Marshall Mathers LP 2.[99] In 2016, The Dividends released the EP Far From Away Volume 1, which included tracks like "Rain" and highlighted their experimental sound through live performances, such as a Dallas show at Trees on June 24.[33] [32] The duo's output remained limited to these releases and select collaborations, with no further albums announced after 2016, suggesting it functioned as a short-term creative partnership rather than an ongoing band.[33] Beyond The Dividends, Jaffe has contributed as a bassist to the country supergroup formed by indie artists Julien Baker and Torres (Brianna LeHane), announced in late 2024.[30] In this role, she supported their debut country album through touring and performances, including an appearance on The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon on December 10, 2024, marking a shift to ensemble country instrumentation distinct from her vocal-led solo projects.[100]
