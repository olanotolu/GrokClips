Sarah Louise Delany


Sarah Louise "Sadie" Delany (September 19, 1889 – January 25, 1999) was an American educator and author best known for co-authoring the 1993 memoir Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years with her sister Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany, which detailed their lives as daughters of a former slave and became a long-running New York Times bestseller later adapted for Broadway and television.[1][2] Born in Lynch Station, Virginia, as the second eldest of ten children to Henry Beard Delany—a man born into slavery who rose to become the first African American Episcopal bishop—and Nannie Logan Delany, an educator of mixed heritage, Delany exemplified longevity and resilience, surviving to age 109 amid sweeping 20th-century transformations in American society.[3][2] Delany pursued higher education at St. Augustine's School in Raleigh, North Carolina, graduating in 1910, followed by studies at Pratt Institute and Columbia University's Teachers College, where she earned a bachelor's degree in 1920 and a master's in 1925.[3][2] Her teaching career in New York City public schools began in predominantly Black elementary institutions before she broke barriers in 1923 as the first Black woman to teach home economics—then termed domestic science—at the high school level, serving at schools including Theodore Roosevelt High School and retiring in 1960.[2][3] Beyond education, Delany's late-life authorship thrust her into public view, with Having Our Say offering firsthand accounts of racial progress and setbacks, complemented by subsequent works like The Delany Sisters' Book of Everyday Wisdom (1995) and her solo memoir On My Own at 107 (1997).[1][2] As a civil rights pioneer, she navigated segregation and discrimination while advocating through her profession and personal narrative, leaving a legacy of quiet determination and historical testimony.[3]

Early Life and Family Background

Birth and Parentage

Sarah Louise Delany was born on September 19, 1889, in Lynch Station, Virginia, at the home of her maternal aunt Eliza Logan, while her parents were traveling.[1][3] She was the second of ten children born to Henry Beard Delany and Nannie Logan Delany.[1] Her father, Henry Beard Delany (1853–1928), was born into slavery in St. Mary's Parish, Georgia, to enslaved parents Thomas Sterling Delany and Sarah Elizabeth Taylor Delany; he was freed as a young child following the Civil War and educated at Saint Augustine's College in Raleigh, North Carolina, where he later served as vice principal and instructor in agriculture and mechanical arts.[4] Ordained as an Episcopal priest in 1894, he became the first African American elected as a suffragan bishop in the Episcopal Church in 1918, serving the Diocese of North Carolina.[5] Her mother, Nannie (or Nanny) James Logan Delany (1861–1956), was born free in Pittsylvania County, Virginia, to Henry Logan, a white man, and a free African American woman; she trained as a teacher and supported her husband's ecclesiastical and educational endeavors, including at Saint Augustine's.[6][7] The couple married in 1886 and relocated to Raleigh shortly after Sarah's birth, where they raised their large family amid the post-Reconstruction challenges faced by educated Black Americans in the South.[4]

Childhood in Virginia

Sarah Louise Delany spent the initial years of her childhood in Lynch Station, Virginia, a rural community in Campbell County emblematic of the segregated South following Reconstruction. Born on September 19, 1889, at the home of her maternal aunt Eliza Logan, she was the second child—though the first had died in infancy—of ten born to her parents, reflecting the high infant mortality rates common among black families in the era.[1] [3] Her father, Henry Beard Delany, a former slave from Georgia who had gained freedom and education, had recently been ordained as a deacon in the Episcopal Church in 1889, becoming one of the first black men to achieve such status in the South; her mother, Nannie James Logan Delany, born free in Danville, Virginia, in 1861, was a graduate of St. Augustine's Normal School and emphasized literacy and self-reliance from the outset.[8] [9] These formative months in Virginia exposed Delany to the intensifying racial hierarchies of the post-Reconstruction period, as states like Virginia codified segregation through laws mandating separate facilities for blacks and whites, a system later formalized as Jim Crow. Family accounts later described everyday indignities, such as restricted access to public spaces and the constant awareness of white supremacy enforced by both law and custom, which instilled resilience amid limited opportunities for black children.[10] Despite these constraints, her parents prioritized education, homeschooling her in basic reading and arithmetic using Bibles and primers, fostering a foundation that contrasted sharply with the substandard public schools available to black Virginians, which often lacked funding and qualified teachers. The Delanys' brief residency in Virginia ended around 1890 when Henry joined the faculty at St. Augustine's Normal School in Raleigh, North Carolina, as a science and mathematics instructor, prompting the family's relocation northward.[8]

Family Dynamics and Influences

Sarah Louise Delany, known as Sadie, grew up as the second of ten children in a family that emphasized rigorous discipline, education, and moral uprightness. Her parents, Henry Beard Delany—a former slave who rose to become the first African American Episcopal bishop—and Nannie James Logan Delany, a teacher, maintained a structured household on the St. Augustine's School campus in Raleigh, North Carolina, where the family resided from Sadie's early childhood. The siblings experienced a protected yet demanding environment, with daily routines centered on academic pursuits, chores, and religious observance, fostering self-reliance and avoidance of debt as core principles.[11][10][1] Henry Delany's authoritative parenting style, informed by his own journey from enslavement in Georgia to ecclesiastical leadership, instilled in the children a profound sense of perseverance and faith, often through strict enforcement of rules and high expectations for achievement. Nannie Delany complemented this with nurturing guidance, teaching practical skills like sewing alongside academic support, which directly influenced Sadie's decision to enter teaching. The family's Episcopal devotion permeated all aspects of life, with church activities reinforcing values of integrity and community that the sisters later credited for their resilience amid racial barriers.[12][13][10] Among siblings—including her immediate younger sister Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany—the dynamics encouraged competition in scholarship while promoting mutual support, as all ten pursued higher education and professional careers, from medicine to law. Sadie's early bond with Bessie, forged in shared campus experiences and family responsibilities, highlighted a collaborative spirit amid the large household's occasional rivalries, shaping her lifelong emphasis on independence and familial loyalty over marriage. This upbringing, shielded from some external prejudices yet aware of them through parental stories, equipped Sadie with the tools to navigate Jim Crow-era challenges without bitterness.[11][1][10]

Education and Professional Training

Academic Achievements

Sarah Louise Delany, known as Sadie, completed her early education at St. Augustine's Normal and Industrial School in Raleigh, North Carolina, graduating in 1910 with training focused on teaching and domestic sciences.[3] This institution, founded for African American students, emphasized practical skills alongside academics, preparing her for a career in education.[2] In 1916, Delany relocated to New York City and enrolled at Pratt Institute, then operating as a two-year college offering associate-level programs. She completed her studies there before transferring to Teachers College at Columbia University in 1918.[2] At Teachers College, she earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in education in 1920, followed by a Master of Arts in education in 1925.[3] These advanced degrees marked significant achievements for an African American woman of her era, enabling her to pioneer teaching roles in public schools.[14] During her time as a student teacher at Columbia, Delany demonstrated resolve by insisting on completing her practice teaching in a predominantly white school rather than a segregated one, challenging prevailing norms and foreshadowing her barrier-breaking career.[2] Her academic pursuits reflected a commitment to professional development amid racial constraints, with no record of further doctoral-level study.[15]

Preparation for Teaching Career

Delany commenced her teacher training after relocating to New York City in 1916, initially enrolling in a two-year program at Pratt Institute in Brooklyn, from which she graduated circa 1918.[2] This preparatory education provided foundational skills in practical subjects, aligning with her later specialization in domestic science.[11] She subsequently entered Teachers College at Columbia University in 1918, pursuing advanced coursework in education.[2] There, Delany completed her Bachelor of Science in education in 1920, a credential that qualified her to teach in the New York City public schools.[14] As part of her program, she undertook student teaching, where she persistently advocated for—and secured—placement in a predominantly white school, defying institutional reluctance to assign Black trainees to such environments and thereby challenging early racial barriers in professional preparation.[2] Delany continued her studies post-graduation, earning a Master of Arts in education from Teachers College in 1925, which enhanced her expertise amid her nascent career.[2] This advanced degree, combined with her undergraduate training, positioned her to innovate in home economics instruction, a field then emphasizing practical domestic skills for students.[16]

Career in Education

Entry into New York City Schools

Following her graduation from Teachers College at Columbia University with a bachelor's degree in education in 1920, Sarah Louise Delany obtained her initial teaching position within the New York City public school system at Public School 119, an elementary school in Harlem primarily serving Black students.[17][10] This role marked her transition from earlier teaching experience in North Carolina's segregated schools to urban public education in the North, where racial barriers persisted despite formal opportunities.[1] Delany's early assignments were confined to predominantly Black elementary institutions, reflecting systemic preferences that limited Black educators to such settings.[1] She taught foundational subjects amid challenging conditions typical of under-resourced Harlem schools at the time, yet her persistence in pursuing advancement highlighted her determination to expand her scope.[17] In 1923, after repeated rejections due to racial prejudice, Delany achieved a breakthrough by becoming the first Black educator appointed to teach domestic science—a precursor to home economics—at the high school level in New York City public schools, specifically at Theodore Roosevelt High School in Manhattan.[2][18] This appointment required overcoming explicit denials from school officials who cited her race as a disqualifier, underscoring the causal role of discriminatory practices in delaying her professional mobility despite her qualifications.[1] Her success in this role established a precedent, though it occurred within a broader system where Black teachers' access to integrated or advanced positions remained exceptional rather than routine.[2]

Teaching Innovations and Milestones

In 1910, following her graduation from St. Augustine's School in Raleigh, North Carolina, Delany served as a circuit-riding teacher in Wake County, introducing domestic science—encompassing practical instruction in cooking, sewing, hygiene, and household management—to rural Black schools.[10] This role, part of the Jeanes Fund initiative aimed at improving education in underserved Southern communities, marked an early milestone in extending formalized home economics training to Black students, who previously lacked access to such curricula amid widespread resource disparities.[19] Delany entered the New York City public school system after earning her bachelor's degree in education from Teachers College, Columbia University, in 1920, initially teaching domestic science at P.S. 119, an elementary school in Harlem serving predominantly Black students.[2] She supplemented her salary by baking and selling cakes, reflecting the economic pressures faced by early Black educators.[1] A pivotal milestone occurred in 1923 when Delany became the first Black woman appointed to teach domestic science at the high school level in the city's public schools, securing a position at Theodore Roosevelt High School in the Bronx by leveraging her high placement on the civil service seniority list without initially disclosing her race, thereby circumventing discriminatory hiring practices.[2] [10] To adapt to urban teaching expectations, she underwent speech training to mitigate biases against her Southern accent.[10] Throughout her career, Delany taught at additional institutions including Girls' High School and Evander Childs High School, earning a master's degree from Teachers College in 1925 while continuing her professional duties.[2] Her approach emphasized rigorous, hands-on training in self-sufficient skills such as budgeting and nutritious meal preparation from basic ingredients, tailored to empower students from low-income backgrounds amid the era's racial and economic barriers.[15] Delany retired in 1960 after four decades in education, having pioneered access for Black educators in integrated high school settings and modeled resilience against systemic exclusion.[2]

Long-Term Impact on Students

Delany's tenure as the first African American to teach domestic science at the high school level in New York City public schools, beginning in 1920, broke racial barriers and set a precedent for future Black educators in integrated urban systems.[20] Her appointment at Theodore Roosevelt High School in Manhattan, secured despite initial nondisclosure of her race, demonstrated strategic navigation of discriminatory hiring practices, enabling her to instruct predominantly white and immigrant student bodies for over three decades until her retirement in 1954.[21] This milestone expanded opportunities for minority teachers, as evidenced by subsequent hires in similar roles, fostering greater diversity in faculty and curricula focused on practical life skills like nutrition, hygiene, and household management.[10] Students under Delany's guidance recalled her as exceptionally beloved, with accounts from her reflections noting that pupils competed to assist her by carrying her lunch and books, reflecting strong personal rapport and respect.[22] She emphasized self-reliance and education as pathways to empowerment, urging her charges—many from underprivileged Harlem backgrounds during the Great Depression and World War II eras—to prioritize learning and independence, skills she deemed essential for overcoming socioeconomic hurdles.[23] This approach instilled resilience, with Delany's own example of quiet persistence amid racism serving as a model; former pupils credited such influences for motivating lifelong pursuits in homemaking professions, further education, and community leadership, though quantitative tracking of alumni outcomes remains undocumented.[24] Her curriculum innovations in domestic science, informed by a 1925 master's degree in education from Columbia University, integrated scientific principles into everyday tasks, equipping students with tools for economic self-sufficiency in an era of limited welfare systems.[2] By 1930s standards, when Black women faced acute employment barriers, Delany's classes produced graduates who applied these competencies in wartime industries and postwar households, contributing to familial stability across generations.[9] While direct longitudinal studies are absent, her pioneering visibility amplified indirect effects, as Harlem's educational ecosystem—bolstered by figures like Delany—correlated with higher secondary completion rates among Black youth compared to national averages by mid-century.[19]

Personal Life and Relationships

Bond with Sister Bessie Delany

Sarah Louise "Sadie" Delany and her younger sister Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany maintained an exceptionally close bond that endured over a century, rooted in shared upbringing and mutual support through professional and personal challenges. Born on September 19, 1889, in Lynch Station, Virginia, Sadie was followed by Bessie on September 3, 1891; both grew up amid their family's ten children at St. Augustine's School in Raleigh, North Carolina, where their father served as Episcopal bishop.[10] The sisters faced early racial indignities together, such as being barred from Pullen Park around 1896 due to Jim Crow laws, fostering resilience and interdependence.[10] Sadie relocated to New York City in 1916, with Bessie joining less than two years later; they resided together for the majority of their lives, initially in Harlem during the Renaissance era and later in a Mount Vernon, New York, home from 1957 onward, where they managed daily tasks like cooking and cleaning independently into advanced age.[10] [25] Neither married, prioritizing their careers—Sadie as a pioneering domestic science teacher and Bessie as New York's second Black female dentist—and family duties, including caring for their mother until her death in 1956.[10] Their complementary personalities strengthened the partnership: Sadie's calm, accommodating approach to racism, akin to "molasses," balanced Bessie's fiery, confrontational style, described as "vinegar," which once nearly led to her lynching in Georgia in 1913 but was averted by quick intervention.[10] [26] [25] This devotion manifested in collaborative endeavors, including co-authoring Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years in 1993, drawn from interviews spanning September 1991 to 1993, where they often finished each other's sentences, and establishing a philanthropic fund at The New York Community Trust in 1994 to aid underserved communities.[10] [26] Sadie articulated their intimacy as unparalleled: "Neither one of us ever married, and we’ve lived together most all of our lives, and probably know each other better than any two human beings on this earth."[10] Bessie's death on September 25, 1995, at age 104, left 106-year-old Sadie alone for the first time, prompting profound grief—she found the loss "unbearable" at the funeral—but also inspiring On My Own at 107, which reflected on their unbreakable tie.[27] Sadie survived until January 25, 1999, reaching 109, outliving Bessie yet carrying forward their joint legacy of self-reliance and historical testimony.[10]

Decision to Remain Unmarried

Sarah Louise Delany, known as Sadie, elected to forgo marriage entirely, a choice rooted in her commitment to professional independence and familial duties rather than romantic entanglement. Born in 1889 as the eldest of ten children to parents who emphasized self-reliance and education, Delany internalized the value of dedicated singlehood early on, viewing it as an extension of her role in supporting the family unit. This perspective, cultivated amid a household where she assisted in raising siblings and tending to aging parents, led her to prioritize autonomy over marital prospects, a decision she later affirmed without regret.[19] Delany encountered multiple suitors during her years as a teacher in New York City public schools, yet she consistently declined proposals, citing the appeal of unencumbered freedom as central to her fulfillment. In reflections shared with biographer Amy Hill Hearth, she and her sister Bessie emphasized enjoying their liberty, which they believed would diminish under the constraints of matrimony and household management. This stance aligned with their broader ethos of self-determination, forged in an era when societal expectations for women often clashed with ambitious pursuits; for Delany, marriage represented a potential diversion from her trailblazing educational career, where she advanced to become one of the first Black women to teach domestic science in the city.[24] Her resolve was reinforced by a profound bond with sister Bessie, with whom she cohabited for most of her 109 years, forming a partnership that substituted for spousal ties and amplified their mutual support. The sisters articulated this preference plainly: "Neither of us ever married and the reason is that we picked careers over men," underscoring a deliberate elevation of vocation and sibling companionship above conventional domesticity. They further quipped that eschewing husbands spared them undue stress, attributing this to their exceptional longevity—a lighthearted but pointed commentary on perceived marital burdens. Such views, drawn from their oral histories, reflect not disillusionment with men but a pragmatic assessment that single life better suited their resilient, achievement-oriented temperaments.[28][29]

Daily Life and Habits

Sarah Louise Delany adhered to a structured daily routine emphasizing physical activity, nutritious eating, and moderation, practices she maintained alongside her sister Elizabeth into their centenarian years. The sisters began most days with yoga exercises, rising at 6:30 A.M. until Delany's hip fracture in June 1993 necessitated a slight adjustment to 7 A.M.[30] This regimen, which Delany credited for vitality, included poses adapted for longevity and was performed consistently in their Mount Vernon, New York, home.[31] Breakfast followed exercise and featured health tonics such as a whole clove of chopped garlic and a teaspoon of cod liver oil, consumed daily to support digestion and immunity.[32] [10] Delany's diet prioritized fresh produce from their backyard vegetable garden, avoiding processed foods and emphasizing at least seven vegetables per lunch; she limited red meat while occasionally including pork or fish.[19] [33] The sisters boiled tap water for drinking, eschewed alcohol and tobacco entirely, and practiced portion control to prevent overindulgence.[10] Beyond physical habits, Delany's routine incorporated intellectual and social simplicity: reading, gardening, and nurturing their sibling bond without marriage or children, viewing companionship as sufficient for fulfillment. She advocated boiling water for tea or coffee and maintaining an optimistic outlook, encapsulated in her maxim that "life is short, and it is up to you to make it sweet."[34] These habits, sustained post-retirement in 1960, contributed to her reaching 109 years.[1]

Views on Society and Race

Perspectives on Discrimination and Resilience

Sarah Delany candidly acknowledged racial discrimination as a persistent reality, exemplified by Jim Crow laws that enforced segregation and limited opportunities for Black Americans, which she interpreted as a backlash against early post-emancipation advancements in Black education and self-sufficiency.[35] Throughout her accounts in Having Our Say, she detailed personal encounters, such as restricted access to housing and professional barriers in New York City during the early 20th century, yet framed these not as insurmountable defeats but as challenges navigated through ingenuity and perseverance.[36] Unlike her sister Bessie's more vocal protests, Delany favored subtle strategies to evade discriminatory restrictions, such as leveraging informal networks to secure teaching positions denied by formal rules.[37] Her views on resilience emphasized individual agency, optimism, and moral fortitude over victimhood or resentment. Delany attributed her endurance to a philosophy of self-reliance instilled by her father, Bishop Henry Beard Delany, who urged his children to excel through rigorous education and ethical living regardless of external prejudices.[38] She encapsulated this in the maxim, "Life is short, and it's up to you to make it sweet," reflecting a commitment to personal joy and productivity amid adversity, evidenced by her 50-year teaching career and attainment of advanced degrees despite gender and racial exclusions from institutions like Columbia University.[39] Faith played a central role, as she credited Episcopalian principles of forgiveness and dignity for preventing bitterness, allowing her to view progress—from slavery's end to mid-century civil rights gains—as incremental triumphs won by disciplined effort rather than entitlement.[16] Delany's narratives underscore resilience as a collective family ethic, where mutual support and avoidance of self-pity enabled survival and success; she and Bessie refrained from dwelling on slights, instead highlighting achievements like homeownership in segregated Harlem as proofs of triumph over systemic odds.[40] This perspective critiques passive acceptance of discrimination while rejecting divisive hatred, promoting instead quiet determination and long-term self-improvement as antidotes to racial hostility.[41] Her longevity to age 109, marked by continued engagement until her final years, exemplified this ethos in practice.[42]

Emphasis on Self-Reliance and Family Values

Sarah Louise Delany, influenced by her father Henry Beard Delany's teachings, internalized a profound commitment to self-reliance from an early age. Her father, born into slavery and later the first African American Episcopal bishop in the United States, explicitly instructed her against accepting scholarships to avoid obligations to benefactors, advising: "Daughter, I have no money. But you must not take a scholarship. If you take a scholarship, you will be beholden to the people who gave you the money. You must make your own way."[43] He reinforced this by urging her to pursue higher education independently, declaring: "Daughter, you are college material. You owe it to your nation, your race, and yourself to go."[43] Delany adhered to these principles, funding her studies through menial jobs while rejecting domestic service for white households, ultimately becoming New York City's first Black high school teacher in domestic science—a milestone achieved through persistent self-advancement rather than external aid.[43][44] This ethos extended to the broader Delany family, where all ten children attained professional status amid widespread racial barriers, exemplifying collective self-sufficiency rooted in hard work and education over dependency. Delany credited her father's emphasis on mental independence—"They can segregate you, but they can’t control your mind"—as fostering resilience without reliance on systemic support.[43][44] Family values were equally central, with pride in the Delany name demanding respectability; Delany recalled her father's insistence that "We carried the Delany name and he wanted us to look respectable," mirrored by her parents' formal address as "Mr. and Mrs. Delany" to uphold dignity.[43] Religious faith underpinned these values, forming "the backbone of the Delany family" and instilling duty to family, community, and ethical living, as Delany and her sister reflected in their accounts of prioritizing self-improvement and civic responsibility over marital or welfare dependencies.[10][32] Delany's lifelong independence, including her choice to remain unmarried to focus on professional and familial obligations, embodied this framework, enabling her to support relatives and advance without compromising autonomy.[43]

Critiques of Modern Narratives on Race

Sarah Louise Delany's reflections in the 1990s challenged dominant racial narratives that prioritized systemic oppression and government intervention as primary solutions to inequality, instead highlighting personal discipline and moral fortitude as pathways to advancement. Having surmounted segregation without reliance on affirmative action or welfare—achieving a career as a pioneering educator in New York City public schools by 1920—she attributed her success to rigorous self-education and familial emphasis on excellence, as detailed in her co-authored memoir Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years (1993). Delany argued that dwelling on racial grievances fostered helplessness, recounting how her family ignored Jim Crow humiliations to focus on verifiable achievements, such as her father's rise from slavery to episcopal leadership by 1918.[25] This stance implicitly critiqued post-civil rights era discourses that, in her view, excused underachievement by overemphasizing historical trauma over individual agency. Delany expressed particular concern over cultural shifts in the black community during her lifetime, including elevated out-of-wedlock birth rates—which reached 68% among African Americans by 1990, per U.S. Census data—and urban violence, which she linked to eroded family structures rather than irreducible racism. In The Delany Sisters' Book of Everyday Wisdom (1994), she and Bessie advocated thrift, delayed gratification, and marital fidelity as antidotes, drawing from their own longevity (Sadie lived to 109, Bessie to 104) and financial independence without public assistance. They rejected welfare as a disincentive to effort, with Delany noting pride in never "scrubbing floors" for whites out of necessity but sustaining dignity through professional attainment, contrasting narratives that portrayed economic disparity as solely attributable to discrimination. Her alignment with Booker T. Washington's accommodationist philosophy—favoring vocational self-improvement over agitation—further underscored skepticism toward activist models that, by the 1990s, often demanded reparative policies she saw as undermining self-respect.[10] On terminology and identity, Delany critiqued evolving racial labels, dismissing "black" as insufficiently dignified and preferring "Negro" or "colored," terms tied to eras of demonstrable progress like the Harlem Renaissance. This preference, voiced in late-life interviews, rejected identity-based militancy, which she believed diverted energy from empirical metrics of success, such as homeownership rates that had climbed from under 25% for blacks in 1940 to over 40% by 1990 despite persistent bias. Regarding flashpoints like the 1992 Rodney King riots—which caused $1 billion in damages and 63 deaths—she and Bessie decried the unrest as self-sabotaging, favoring lawful resilience over reactive destruction, a position echoed in their discussions of Clarence Thomas's 1991 confirmation amid Anita Hill allegations, where they prioritized evidence over presumptive solidarity.[1][45] These views, grounded in a century of personal observation, positioned Delany as a skeptic of grievance-centric frameworks, privileging causal factors like behavioral choices—verifiable through her own barrier-breaking trajectory—over institutional excuses.

The Delany Sisters' Public Legacy

Creation of "Having Our Say"

In 1991, journalist Amy Hill Hearth, then a reporter for The New York Times, learned of Sarah "Sadie" Delany and her sister A. Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany through a neighbor's letter and interviews at a Mount Vernon, New York, community center, where the centenarian sisters resided.[46] Hearth conducted an initial three-hour interview with the sisters, aged 101 and 103 respectively, which formed the basis of a feature article published in The New York Times on September 22, 1991, titled "Two Maiden Ladies with Stories to Tell."[24] [46] The article highlighted their longevity, unmarried lives, and firsthand accounts of American history from the late 19th century onward, generating immediate publisher interest in expanding the material into a full book.[24] Following the article's success, Hearth collaborated with the Delany sisters from fall 1991 through spring 1993, conducting extensive follow-up interviews to compile an oral history rather than a traditional biography.[24] [46] Sarah Delany, then in her early 100s, played a central role by recounting personal experiences including her upbringing at St. Augustine's School in Raleigh, North Carolina, encounters with Jim Crow segregation, her career as a teacher in the Bronx, and observations of the Harlem Renaissance and civil rights movements.[46] Hearth transcribed and organized the sisters' narratives chronologically, adding contextual introductions to provide historical framing while preserving their authentic voices and colloquial expressions.[46] The title Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years derived from Bessie Delany's emphatic declaration during interviews, "We're having our say!"—reflecting the sisters' determination to document their unfiltered perspectives after a lifetime of reticence.[46] The book was published in hardcover by Kodansha America on September 19, 1993, coinciding precisely with Sarah Delany's 104th birthday.[24] [46] This collaborative effort, credited to Sarah L. Delany, A. Elizabeth Delany, and Amy Hill Hearth, emphasized the sisters' resilience and self-reliance without editorial embellishment, positioning it as a primary-source record of 20th-century African American experiences through two women's eyes.[24] Trade paperback and mass-market editions followed in 1994 via Dell, with later digital and audio releases in 2023 by Blackstone Publishing.[46]

Reception and Cultural Influence

Upon its 1993 publication, Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years garnered significant commercial success, achieving New York Times bestseller status and maintaining a position on the list for 117 weeks.[24][20] The memoir received the Christopher Award for Literature in 1993, recognizing its inspirational portrayal of the sisters' lives.[47] The book inspired multiple adaptations that extended its reach. In 1995, Emily Mann adapted it into a Broadway play, Having Our Say, which premiered to positive reviews for its intimate depiction of the sisters' century-spanning experiences and enjoyed subsequent regional productions and revivals.[48] A 1999 CBS television film, also scripted by Mann and starring Diahann Carroll as Sadie Delany and Ruby Dee as Bessie Delany, won a Peabody Award for excellence in electronic media and a Christopher Award for outstanding television programming, alongside an NAACP Image Award nomination.[49][46] Culturally, the Delany sisters' narrative exerted lasting influence by providing unfiltered, firsthand testimony on African American life from the post-Civil War era through the civil rights movement and beyond, emphasizing education, professional accomplishment, and familial bonds as antidotes to discrimination.[10] Widely adopted in educational curricula, it became a staple for exploring black history and female resilience, fostering discussions on personal agency amid historical adversity rather than perpetual victimhood.[20] Their story, as centenarians who rose from humble origins to pioneering educators and dentists, positioned the sisters as exemplars of endurance, influencing public perceptions of racial progress through individual merit and moral fortitude.[50]

Philanthropy and Enduring Funds

In 1994, Sarah "Sadie" Delany and her sister Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany established a donor-advised fund at The New York Community Trust, utilizing proceeds from their bestselling memoir Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years.[51][17] The fund supports initiatives to broaden educational opportunities, promote civil and human rights, and address systemic racism and injustice, reflecting the sisters' lifelong commitments to education and racial equity as educators and civil rights advocates.[51] Grants from this enduring fund continue to be distributed annually, enabling targeted philanthropy in line with the sisters' directives, such as scholarships and programs for underserved communities.[51][52] The sisters directed substantial royalties from Having Our Say—which sold over five million copies—and its adaptations, including a Broadway play and Emmy-winning film, toward charitable causes rather than personal enrichment.[53] This included a significant donation to St. Augustine's University, their family's historical alma mater in Raleigh, North Carolina, where their father served as the first Black Episcopal bishop in the South.[54] Additionally, they created a dedicated scholarship fund within their estate to support higher education for Delany family descendants, ensuring intergenerational access to learning amid their emphasis on self-reliance.[54] Sadie Delany's philanthropy, conducted jointly with Bessie until the latter's death in 1995, underscored a pragmatic approach rooted in their experiences overcoming Jim Crow-era barriers through education and family support, rather than reliance on external aid.[10] The New York Community Trust fund remains active post-Sadie's death in 1999, perpetuating their legacy via ongoing grants that prioritize empirical outcomes in education and rights advancement over symbolic gestures.[51][17]

Later Years and Death

Longevity and Health Practices

Sarah Louise Delany achieved remarkable longevity, reaching 109 years and 128 days before her death on January 25, 1999.[1] She credited her extended lifespan to a consistent regimen of physical activity, moderation in diet, and avoidance of unnecessary medical interventions, habits she maintained into her later years alongside her sister until the latter's death in 1995.[30] Delany incorporated daily yoga exercises into her routine starting at age 80, rising early—typically around 6:30 or 7:00 a.m.—to perform stretches and poses, often guided by television programs from Monday through Friday.[30][25] This practice, which she continued even after a hip fracture in 1993, emphasized flexibility and gentle movement over strenuous effort, contributing to her physical resilience.[55] Her diet focused on simplicity and nutrient density, featuring abundant vegetables and daily consumption of a chopped clove of garlic mixed with a teaspoon of cod-liver oil for purported health benefits, alongside light meals to avoid overeating.[32] Delany eschewed smoking and heavy drinking, advocating "clean living" as a foundational element of vitality, while expressing skepticism toward frequent doctor visits or hospitalizations, viewing them as potential risks rather than panaceas.[25] These self-reliant approaches, rooted in discipline rather than modern medical trends, aligned with her broader philosophy of self-determination.[28]

Final Contributions

Following the death of her sister Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany on September 25, 1995, Sarah Louise "Sadie" Delany collaborated with journalist Amy Hill Hearth on On My Own at 107: Reflections on Life Without Bessie, published in May 1997.[1] The book detailed Sadie's adjustment to solitude after over a century of companionship, emphasizing themes of resilience, faith, and daily routines that sustained her independence at advanced age.[27] It built on their prior works by offering personal insights into grief and self-reliance, drawing from interviews conducted in Sadie's Mount Vernon, New York, home.[1] Delany continued to support philanthropic efforts initiated with Bessie, including a donor-advised fund established in 1994 at The New York Community Trust to aid educational and community initiatives aligned with the sisters' values of self-reliance and opportunity for underserved youth.[10] In her remaining years, she directed resources from this fund toward programs promoting literacy and historical preservation, reflecting her lifelong commitment to education as a tool for empowerment.[10] These contributions extended the sisters' influence beyond their writings, ensuring ongoing support for causes they championed, such as access to quality schooling for African American students.[10] Delany's final public engagements included attending performances of the Broadway adaptation of Having Our Say, which premiered in 1995 and highlighted their shared life story; she expressed appreciation for its faithful portrayal during viewings in her later years.[1] Through these activities, she reinforced messages of perseverance and family bonds, influencing subsequent generations via media adaptations and the fund's grants, which persisted after her passing.[10]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Sarah Louise "Sadie" Delany died on January 25, 1999, at the age of 109, peacefully in her sleep at her home in Mount Vernon, New York.[1] [56] The Westchester County medical examiner's office determined the death to be from natural causes, though the case remained under review.[57] Her nephew, Harry Delany, confirmed the passing to media outlets.[56] Delany's death marked the end of the Delany sisters' shared legacy, as she was the last surviving sibling following Annie Elizabeth "Bessie" Delany's death in 1995.[1] Her burial occurred on January 31, 1999, at Mount Hope Cemetery in Raleigh, North Carolina, alongside her parents and sister Bessie.[57] A public memorial service was planned for New York in February 1999 to honor her life and contributions.[57] Media coverage highlighted Delany's extraordinary lifespan and the cultural impact of her memoir Having Our Say: The Delany Sisters' First 100 Years, with tributes emphasizing her role as a witness to American history.[1] Figures such as Camille Cosby, who was producing a CBS television adaptation of the book set to air in April 1999, called for continued remembrance of the sisters' story.[56] No immediate family disputes or legal proceedings were reported in connection with her death.
