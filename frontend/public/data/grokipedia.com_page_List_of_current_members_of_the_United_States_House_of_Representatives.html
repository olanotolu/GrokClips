List of current United States representatives

The list of current United States representatives enumerates the members of the United States House of Representatives serving in the 119th Congress, which convened on January 3, 2025, and is scheduled to conclude on January 3, 2027.[1] The House comprises 435 voting members, apportioned among the states by population and elected every two years from single-member congressional districts, plus six non-voting members consisting of delegates from the District of Columbia and five territories and the resident commissioner from Puerto Rico.[2][3] As of August 2025, Republicans hold 219 voting seats and Democrats hold 212, reflecting a narrow Republican majority retained after the 2024 elections but adjusted for vacancies arising from the deaths of two Democratic representatives in March 2025 and the resignation of one Republican representative in July 2025, with no special elections noted to have filled all positions by October.[3][4] This composition underscores the House's role in the legislative process, where a simple majority suffices for passing bills, though procedural rules and committee assignments often amplify the influence of the majority party.[2] The representatives' tenure involves legislating on federal matters, including appropriations, impeachment proceedings, and electing the president in cases of electoral college ties, with the current session marked by ongoing partisan dynamics amid these vacancies.[2]

Leadership

Speaker of the House

Mike Johnson, a Republican representing Louisiana's 4th congressional district, has served as the Speaker of the United States House of Representatives since October 25, 2023.[5] He was initially elected to the position following the unprecedented removal of predecessor Kevin McCarthy via a motion to vacate, securing the gavel on the fourth ballot amid internal Republican divisions.[5] Johnson's tenure has been marked by navigating a slim GOP majority, with the party holding 220 seats to Democrats' 215 as of the start of the 119th Congress.[6] On January 3, 2025, Johnson was re-elected Speaker for the 119th Congress on the first ballot, receiving 218 votes to 215 for House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries, after three initial Republican holdouts switched their support.[7] This narrow margin reflected ongoing intraparty tensions but affirmed his leadership, bolstered by endorsements from former President Donald Trump and key conservative factions.[6] As Speaker, Johnson controls the House floor agenda, appoints committee members, and represents the chamber in interbranch negotiations, exercising authority derived from House rules and precedents.[8] Prior to the speakership, Johnson served as vice chairman of the House Republican Conference and chaired the House Judiciary Committee's Subcommittee on the Constitution and Limited Government.[6] His election and re-election underscore the precarious dynamics of majority control, where the Speaker must balance demands from the House Freedom Caucus and moderate Republicans to maintain cohesion.[6] As of October 2025, Johnson continues in the role amid fiscal challenges, including a prolonged government funding dispute.[9]

Republican Majority Leadership

The Republican majority in the House of Representatives for the 119th Congress, which convened on January 3, 2025, holds key leadership positions responsible for coordinating legislative priorities, enforcing party unity, and managing floor proceedings.[10] These roles, elected by the House Republican Conference, support the Speaker in advancing the party's agenda amid a slim majority of 220 seats as of October 2025.[3] Majority Leader: Steve Scalise (R-LA-1) serves as Majority Leader, the second-ranking position in the Republican leadership, tasked with setting the legislative schedule and representing the conference on the House floor. Scalise, first elected to the role in 2022, retained the position following the 2024 elections.[10][11][12] Majority Whip: Tom Emmer (R-MN-6) holds the Majority Whip position, focusing on counting votes, securing member support for bills, and maintaining party discipline. Emmer assumed the role in 2022 after previous service as conference chair and was reelected for the 119th Congress.[10][11] Conference Chair: Lisa McClain (R-MI-9) chairs the House Republican Conference, overseeing internal strategy, communications, and member engagement within the party caucus. McClain was elevated to this role ahead of the 119th Congress to streamline conference operations.[11] Additional influential positions include the Republican Policy Committee Chair, held by Gary Palmer (R-AL-6), who develops policy recommendations, and the National Republican Congressional Committee Chair, Gary Palmer also serves in a leadership capacity influencing campaign and recruitment efforts, though these are not part of the core majority leadership triad.[13] The leadership structure emphasizes fiscal conservatism and deregulation, reflecting the conference's priorities post-2024 gains.[11]

Democratic Minority Leadership

The Democratic minority leadership in the 119th United States Congress, which convened on January 3, 2025, is headed by House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries of New York's 8th congressional district, who was unanimously re-elected to the position by House Democrats on November 19, 2024. Jeffries, serving in this role since January 2023, directs the party's legislative strategy, coordinates with the minority whip on floor operations, and represents Democrats in negotiations with the Republican majority.[14] Assistant Democratic Leader (also known as House Democratic Caucus Chair) Pete Aguilar of California's 33rd congressional district oversees caucus operations, messaging, and member coordination, a position he has held since 2023 with re-election for the 119th Congress.[15] Aguilar focuses on building consensus within the caucus and advancing policy priorities such as health care access and economic relief.[16] House Minority Whip Katherine Clark of Massachusetts's 5th congressional district, re-elected to the role on November 19, 2024, manages vote counting, enforces party discipline on the House floor, and assists the leader in legislative tactics; she assumed the position in 2023 following Steny Hoyer's retirement.[17] Clark's responsibilities include rallying members for key votes and communicating caucus positions publicly.[18]

Partisan Affiliation by State

In the 119th Congress, Republican majorities comprise the House delegations of twenty-nine states, while Democratic majorities comprise those of twenty-one states.[24] This distribution underscores regional divides, with Republican strength concentrated in the South, Great Plains, and parts of the Midwest, and Democratic strength in the Northeast, West Coast, and select urban-heavy states.[24] Specific post-2024 election breakdowns include Alabama (1 Democrat, 6 Republicans), Alaska (0 Democrats, 1 Republican), California (40 Democrats, 12 Republicans), Colorado (5 Democrats, 3 Republicans), Louisiana (2 Democrats, 5 Republicans), Michigan (7 Democrats, 6 Republicans), Montana (0 Democrats, 2 Republicans), New York (19 Democrats, 7 Republicans), North Carolina (4 Democrats, 9 Republicans), Ohio (4 Democrats, 10 Republicans), Oregon (4 Democrats, 2 Republicans), Pennsylvania (8 Democrats, 11 Republicans), and West Virginia (0 Democrats, 3 Republicans).[24] Subsequent personnel changes have not shifted any state's majority affiliation. Special elections filled vacancies with same-party successors: Arizona's 7th district (Democrat Adelita Grijalva elected September 23, 2025, replacing deceased Democrat RaÃºl Grijalva) and Virginia's 11th district (Democrat James Walkinshaw elected September 10, 2025, replacing deceased Democrat Gerald Connolly).[25][26] Vacancies persist in Texas's 18th district (previously Democratic, following Sylvester Turner's death on March 5, 2025; special election November 4, 2025) and Tennessee's 7th district (previously Republican, following Mark Green's resignation on July 20, 2025; special election December 2, 2025), alongside one additional unspecified vacancy.[27][19] These adjustments yield a chamber-wide total of 219 Republicans and 213 Democrats, with 3 vacancies.[4]

Vacancies

As of October 26, 2025, the United States House of Representatives has two vacancies among its 435 voting seats.[28][19] Tennessee's 7th congressional district became vacant on July 20, 2025, following the resignation of incumbent Republican Mark Green, who stepped down to focus on private sector opportunities. The state's Republican governor called a special election for December 2, 2025, to fill the remainder of the term ending January 3, 2027.[29] Texas's 18th congressional district has been vacant since March 5, 2025, when Democratic Representative Sylvester Turner died from pancreatic cancer complications at age 70. A special election is scheduled for November 4, 2025, to select a successor for the term concluding January 3, 2027; this date aligns with Texas's uniform election day for local and state races.[30] These vacancies contribute to a narrow Republican majority, with partisan counts at 219 Republicans and 214 Democrats following recent special election fillings in other districts, such as Arizona's 7th (won by Democrat Adelita Grijalva on September 23, 2025) and Virginia's 11th (won by Democrat James Walkinshaw on September 9, 2025).[31][28]

Member Lists

List of Voting Representatives

As of October 26, 2025, the House of Representatives comprises 432 voting members serving in the 119th Congress, with 219 affiliated with the Republican Party and 213 with the Democratic Party, alongside three vacancies.[4][32] These vacancies stem from the death of Representative Sylvester Turner (D-TX-18) on March 5, 2025, a special election for which is scheduled for November 4, 2025; the death of Representative Gerry Connolly (D-VA-11) on May 21, 2025; and the resignation of Representative Mark Green (R-TN-7) on July 21, 2025.[19][4] A fourth vacancy in Arizona's 7th district, resulting from the death of Representative RaÃºl Grijalva (D) on March 13, 2025, was filled by Democrat Adelita Grijalva in a special election held on September 24, 2025.[4][33] Voting representatives are elected from 435 single-member congressional districts, apportioned among the states according to the decennial census, with each serving a two-year term without term limits. Membership reflects the partisan outcomes of the 2024 general elections, adjusted for subsequent changes, and is officially documented by the Clerk of the House.[34] The current roster includes a mix of incumbents returned to office and freshmen elected in 2024, with no independent or third-party voting members.[4]

List of Non-Voting Delegates and Resident Commissioner

The non-voting delegates and resident commissioner serve the District of Columbia and unincorporated U.S. territories, representing populations without full voting rights in the House due to their non-state status. These six members, elected for two-year terms except the Puerto Rico resident commissioner (four-year term), possess legislative privileges including floor debate participation, bill sponsorship, and voting in committees, but lack votes on final House passage. They assumed office on January 3, 2025, for the 119th Congress, following 2024 elections.
