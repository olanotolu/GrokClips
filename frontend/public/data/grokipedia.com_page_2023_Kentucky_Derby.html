2023 Kentucky Derby

The 2023 Kentucky Derby was the 149th annual running of the premier Thoroughbred horse race, contested on May 6, 2023, over a distance of 1+1⁄4 miles at Churchill Downs racetrack in Louisville, Kentucky, drawing a crowd of over 150,000 spectators and marking the first leg of the American Triple Crown series.[1][2] The victor was Mage, a 15-1 longshot colt sired by Good Magic out of the mare Puca, piloted by jockey Javier Castellano in his sixth Derby attempt and conditioned by Venezuelan trainer Gustavo Delgado, who completed the classic in a time of 2:01.57 to secure the $1.86 million winner's share from a $3 million purse.[2][3][4] Runner-up Two Phil's and third-place Angel of Empire trailed by lengths, with Mage's upset paying $32.42 to win for $2 bettors, highlighting the race's unpredictability amid a field of 18 three-year-olds tested by fast conditions and a pace set by early leader Kinetic.[3][5] The event unfolded amid heightened scrutiny over equine welfare, as at least seven horses suffered fatal injuries at Churchill Downs in the 10 days leading up to and including Derby Day, contributing to a total of 12 deaths during the spring meet and prompting temporary suspension of trainer Saffie Joseph Jr. after two of his charges collapsed in training.[6][7][8] Subsequent investigation by the Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority identified no single causal factor across the fatalities, attributing patterns to factors like high workloads rather than isolated track surface or medication issues, though critics questioned regulatory oversight in an industry reliant on high-stakes racing.[8][9]

Background and Preparation

Qualification Process

The qualification for the 2023 Kentucky Derby relied on the "Road to the Kentucky Derby" points system, a structured series of prep races designed to identify top 3-year-old Thoroughbreds based on competitive performance metrics from September 2022 to April 2023.[10] Points were allocated to the top finishers in designated domestic and international events, with distributions scaled by race significance: early prep races awarded 10-4-3-2-1 to the first through fifth places, while the Kentucky Derby Championship Series featured seven mid-tier races at 50-25-15-10-5 and four final preps doubled to 100-50-30-20-10.[11] This 2023 iteration expanded points to fifth-place finishers in select races to broaden evaluation data and reward depth in fields.[12] Over 30 U.S. prep races contributed points, including the Iroquois Stakes (50-20-15-10-5 on September 17, 2022), Los Alamitos Futurity (10-4-3-2-1 on September 3, 2022), and culminating events like the Santa Anita Derby (100-40-20-10 on April 8, 2023) and Blue Grass Stakes (100-40-20-10 on April 8, 2023).[13] International paths, such as Japan's UAE Derby (100-50-30-20-10) and European trials (e.g., 20-10-6-4-2 scales), allowed up to six foreign slots, prioritizing highest earners.[10] The system emphasized empirical form, with points reflecting speed figures, track conditions, and consistency across graded stakes rather than subjective factors.[14] The top 18 point earners automatically qualified for the 20-horse field, supplemented by owner nominations and fees; if oversubscribed, also-eligible horses (ranked 19th-21st on points) filled scratches via draw order.[12] Points ties were broken by lifetime graded stakes earnings, ensuring priority to proven earners.[15] Leading the final standings was Forte with 190 points from wins in the Breeders' Futurity, Fountain of Youth, and Florida Derby, followed by Practical Move at 160 points via the Rebel Stakes and Santa Anita Derby victories—demonstrating the system's reward for sequential high-level performances before Practical Move's later withdrawal due to health issues.[14] This process finalized entrants by April 29, 2023, prioritizing data-driven selection over discretionary invites.[12]

Race Execution

Race Day Conditions and Events

The 2023 Kentucky Derby took place on May 6 under clear skies with no precipitation, temperatures ranging from a morning low of 56°F to a high of 77°F, and race-time conditions around 76°F.[24][25] The track at Churchill Downs was a fast dirt surface over 1¼ miles, providing standard footing without mud or excessive firmness that could alter pace dynamics.[25] Pre-race logistical adjustments included multiple scratches that reduced the field from an initial 20 entrants to 23 starters after earlier withdrawals. Practical Move, the Santa Anita Derby winner and a morning-line 10-1 contender, was scratched on May 4 due to fever and illness, removing a strong closer from the lineup and potentially favoring speed-oriented horses in the reduced competition.[26][27] Hours before post time on race day, morning-line favorite Forte was scratched by state veterinarians after examination revealed him unfit to compete, attributed to a foot bruise sustained in the starting gate during a prior workout; this late removal shifted betting favoritism and compressed post positions, altering tactical expectations for the remaining field.[28][29] Traditional pre-race ceremonies proceeded without reported disruptions, including the rendition of "My Old Kentucky Home," Kentucky's state song, sung by attendees as horses paraded to the post—a custom dating to the track's early 20th-century history.[30] The event drew an on-site attendance of 150,335 spectators, reflecting robust turnout amid favorable weather and heightened interest despite the scratches.[31]

The Race and Finishing Positions

The 149th running of the Kentucky Derby took place on May 6, 2023, at Churchill Downs in Louisville, Kentucky, covering a distance of 1+1/4 miles on a fast dirt track. Hit Show seized the early lead from post position 6 under jockey Joel Rosario, setting moderate fractions of :23.40 for the quarter-mile, :47.06 for the half-mile, and 1:11.39 for the three-quarter-mile mark, with Two Phil's stalking closely in second and a cluster of mid-pack runners including Mage positioned about 10 lengths off the pace.[32][33] As the field turned for home, the early leaders began to tire under the moderate tempo, allowing closers to advance; Mage, ridden by Javier Castellano for trainer Gustavo Delgado, shifted wide around the far turn and launched a strong rally from mid-pack, surging past challengers in the stretch to hit the front nearing the furlong pole. Two Phil's, under Jareth Loveberry, similarly rallied from just off the pace to challenge, but Mage held firm to win by three-quarters of a length in a final time of 2:01.57, marking Delgado's first training victory in the Derby and Castellano's first win in the event after 10 previous attempts.[32][3]

Results and Betting

Official Results and Purse Distribution

Mage crossed the finish line first in the 2023 Kentucky Derby, covering 1¼ miles in 2:01.57 on a fast track at Churchill Downs, with recorded fractional times of 22.35 seconds for the first quarter-mile, 45.73 for the half-mile, 1:10.11 for three-quarters, and 1:36.06 for the mile.[35] The victory marked the first Kentucky Derby win for both jockey Javier Castellano and trainer Gustavo Delgado.[36] The event's $3 million purse was split among the top five finishers, with $1.86 million (62%) to first place, $600,000 (20%) to second, $300,000 (10%) to third, $150,000 (5%) to fourth, and $90,000 (3%) to fifth.[37]

Payouts and Wagering Statistics

The 2023 Kentucky Derby payouts reflected Mage's status as a mid-tier longshot at 15-1 morning-line odds, delivering solid returns for straight bets under the parimutuel wagering system, where odds and payouts are calculated based on total pool contributions minus the track's takeout (typically 17-25% for exotics at Churchill Downs).[38][39] For a $2 win-place-show bet, Mage returned $32.42 to win, $14.58 to place, and $9.08 to show.[40][41] The $1 exacta on Mage over Two Phil's (positions 1-2) paid $150.22, while the $0.50 trifecta including Angel of Empire in third returned $491.18.[38][42]

Incidents and Controversies

Horse Fatalities at Churchill Downs

During the period from April 27 to May 6, 2023, encompassing the lead-up to and including Kentucky Derby Day, seven horses died at Churchill Downs, either from catastrophic injuries during training or racing or from sudden non-traumatic collapses. These included Code of Kings, which suffered a fatal neck fracture during training on April 29; Wild on Ice, euthanized after a severe workout injury on May 2; Take Charge Briana, euthanized following a training mishap the same day; and Chloe's Dream and Free Beer, both succumbing to catastrophic limb injuries during races on Derby Day, May 6.[44][45][46] Two additional fatalities involved horses trained by Saffie Joseph Jr.: Parents Pride collapsed suddenly after completing a race on April 29 without evidence of musculoskeletal trauma, and Chasing Artie similarly collapsed post-race on May 2. Initial necropsy examinations for these cases revealed no fractures or typical racing injuries, with preliminary findings pointing toward cardiac events such as possible myocarditis or arrhythmias, though definitive causes remained under investigation at the time. These clustered deaths from Joseph's stable, both occurring within 72 hours and absent overt injury, prompted immediate scrutiny of training practices.[47][48][49] The incidence of seven fatalities in this roughly ten-day window marked a deviation from historical norms at Churchill Downs, where annual fatal injury rates hovered around 1.25 to 1.32 per 1,000 race starts in recent years, typically translating to fewer deaths during Derby week. Animal welfare advocates, including organizations like PETA, attributed such events to systemic issues like breeding for extreme speed, which compromises skeletal integrity in young horses subjected to high-intensity training and racing. In contrast, industry representatives emphasized the biomechanical stresses of Thoroughbred racing— involving speeds over 40 mph on firm surfaces—as carrying unavoidable risks akin to those in other elite equine disciplines, supported by veterinary data on exercise-induced pathologies independent of track conditions.[50][51]

Trainer Suspensions and Immediate Responses

On May 4, 2023, Churchill Downs Incorporated (CDI) indefinitely suspended trainer Saffie Joseph Jr. and prohibited all horses under his care from racing or training at Churchill Downs Racetrack, citing concerns over the sudden, unexplained deaths of two horses in his barn earlier that week.[49] The affected animals included Parents Pride, which collapsed and died after a workout on April 29, and Chasing Artie, which suffered a similar fate following training on May 1; these incidents raised questions about the overall condition of Joseph's 23-horse string.[52] The suspension directly led to the scratching of Lord Miles, Joseph's entry in the Kentucky Derby, as track officials prioritized equine safety protocols amid the mounting fatalities at the venue.[53] In parallel, the Kentucky Horse Racing Commission (KHRC) supported CDI's authority to enforce such measures under state racing regulations, while initiating preliminary reviews of the incidents in coordination with veterinary experts.[54] Track officials responded to the broader cluster of deaths—totaling seven by Derby day on May 6—by intensifying pre-race veterinary inspections, expanding scratch criteria for horses exhibiting subtle lameness or irregular gait, and mandating additional diagnostic imaging for entrants on comparable training regimens.[46] These steps, grounded in existing Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) guidelines for risk assessment, aimed to mitigate potential welfare risks without halting the meet entirely, though no temporary closure of specific training surfaces was enacted at that juncture.[55] Stakeholders reacted variably to the suspension: Joseph maintained that his training methods adhered to standard practices with no evidence of medication violations or undue stress, a position echoed by owners who highlighted clean veterinary histories for the deceased horses.[56] Conversely, some industry voices and welfare advocates urged immediate enhancements to HISA's pre-existing drug testing and surface safety protocols, arguing that the fatalities underscored gaps in proactive monitoring despite regulatory frameworks.[47] The KHRC and CDI emphasized that decisions were driven by empirical observations of the deaths rather than presumptive fault, with Joseph's ban lifted on June 30, 2023, following necropsy reviews that identified no causal links to trainer negligence, though heightened oversight of his operations continued thereafter.[52][57]

Investigations and Aftermath

Official Investigations and Findings

The Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) conducted an independent investigation into 12 equine fatalities at Churchill Downs during the spring 2023 meet, including those preceding and following the Kentucky Derby on May 6. Released on September 12, 2023, the report analyzed veterinary records, necropsy results, training histories, and biomechanical data from the track surface, concluding no singular cause explained the deaths.[58][59] Necropsies revealed diverse pathologies, including complete or incomplete fractures of distal limb bones, sudden cardiac events, and other musculoskeletal failures, with no evidence of a unified external factor such as doping, toxins, or uniform medication issues. Biomechanical assessments, including sensor-equipped gallops and surface penetrometer tests, found no causal link between track conditions—including minor inconsistencies like embedded rocks in isolated areas—and the fatalities, as injury profiles did not correlate with surface metrics. HISA also identified no violations of its racetrack safety rules by trainers, veterinarians, or other covered persons that directly contributed to the incidents.[60][8][61] Statistical context from HISA's national data underscored multifactorial risks inherent to Thoroughbred racing, with the 2023 U.S. fatality rate at 1.32 per 1,000 starts—a marginal increase from 1.25 in 2022—reflecting broader variables like genetics, training regimens, and cumulative stress rather than isolated environmental or operational failures at Churchill Downs. While some deceased horses exhibited pre-existing risk factors, such as high-speed training loads or prior veterinary notations, these were not patterned across cases to support narratives of systemic "overracing" or track-specific negligence. The findings countered early media-driven speculation lacking empirical backing, emphasizing instead evidence-based regulatory enhancements over unsubstantiated alarmism.[62][50][63]

Long-Term Impact on the Industry

In response to the equine fatalities associated with the 2023 Spring Meet at Churchill Downs, the Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) implemented enhanced monitoring protocols, including mandatory veterinary examinations and surface integrity testing, as outlined in its September 2023 investigation findings and subsequent rule-making.[58] These measures, such as a proposed 30-day racing standdown following certain treatments and pilot programs for cardiac monitoring launched in 2025, aimed to address potential risk factors like exercise-associated sudden deaths without fundamentally altering longstanding racing practices.[64] [65] HISA-regulated tracks reported a 2024 fatality rate of 0.50 per 1,000 timed workouts, reflecting incremental safety advancements rather than a wholesale restructuring of the industry.[66] Economically, the Kentucky Derby continued to generate substantial revenue for the state, with the equine industry contributing $6.5 billion annually in direct and indirect activity as of 2023, including over $4.3 million in state tax revenue from Derby Day alone.[67] [68] Despite heightened scrutiny, wagering handles demonstrated resilience, rising from $288.7 million on the 2023 Derby to a record $320.5 million in 2024, alongside attendance of 156,710, indicating sustained public engagement and economic vitality exceeding $400 million in projected local impact for subsequent years.[69] [70] Industry-wide safety metrics further underscored long-term progress, with the Equine Injury Database recording a 2023 fatal injury rate of 1.32 per 1,000 starts— a 34% decline from 2.00 in 2009—and rates continuing to fall below 1.5 for five consecutive years through 2024, representing a 55% reduction overall since tracking began.[71] [72] These empirical trends, driven by data-informed interventions, have been cited by proponents as evidence of the sport's capacity for risk mitigation in high-performance equine athletics, where inherent biomechanical stresses parallel those in human elite sports, countering narratives of irredeemable systemic flaws advanced by animal welfare advocates.[73][74]
