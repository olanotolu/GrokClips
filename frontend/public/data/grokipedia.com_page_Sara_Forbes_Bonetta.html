Sarah Forbes Bonetta


Sarah Forbes Bonetta (c. 1843 – 15 August 1880) was an Egbado Yoruba girl from what is now southwestern Nigeria, orphaned and captured at age five during a slave raid by forces of the Kingdom of Dahomey in 1848, in which her parents and siblings were killed.[1][2] In 1850, she was given as a gift to Captain Frederick Edward Forbes of the British Royal Navy during his anti-slavery mission to Dahomey, who transported her to England aboard HMS Bonetta, from which she partly derived her Western name.[3][4] Presented to Queen Victoria, she impressed the monarch with her intelligence and bearing, leading to royal sponsorship of her education first in England and later at a mission school in Sierra Leone, where she learned English, arithmetic, and music while maintaining fluency in her native tongue.[3][4] Victoria became her godmother upon her baptism as Sarah Forbes Bonetta in 1851 and took a personal interest in her welfare, arranging periodic visits to court and financial support despite Bonetta's reported homesickness and health issues related to the British climate.[3][5] In 1862, at age 19, Bonetta married James Pinson Labulo Davies, a wealthy Sierra Leonean-born merchant and shipowner based in Lagos, in a ceremony at St. Nicholas Church in Brighton, England, after which the couple relocated to West Africa.[6][4] They had three children, including a daughter named Victoria after the queen, who also became her goddaughter; Bonetta educated her children in England to shield them from tropical diseases.[3][4] She died of tuberculosis at age 37 while en route to Madeira for health reasons, and was buried in Funchal's British Cemetery.[7][2] Her life exemplified the intersections of British abolitionist efforts, royal patronage, and African agency in the mid-19th century transatlantic world, though primary accounts derive largely from European observers like Forbes, with limited direct records of her own perspectives.[3][5]

Origins and Capture

Birth and Yoruba Heritage

Sarah Forbes Bonetta, originally named Omoba Aina, was born circa 1843 in Oke-Odan, a village within the territory of the Egbado (also known as Yewa) subgroup of the Yoruba people in what is now southwestern Nigeria.[8][9] The name "Aina" in Yoruba nomenclature specifically denotes a female child born with the umbilical cord wrapped around the neck, a circumstance interpreted in Yoruba cosmology as marking the individual for distinction or spiritual significance.[2][3] The Egbado Yoruba inhabited regions along the Ogun River, engaging in agriculture, trade, and localized chieftaincy systems typical of pre-colonial Yoruba polities, which emphasized patrilineal descent, orisha worship, and communal governance under obas (kings) and councils.[2][8] Aina's family held elevated status within this clan; contemporary accounts describe her as a princess or titled nobility, likely the daughter of a local chief, reflecting the hierarchical social structures where omoba (princes/princesses) were groomed for advisory or ceremonial roles.[6][3] Her early life thus embodied the cultural continuum of Yoruba identity, rooted in oral traditions, Ifá divination, and resistance to external incursions, prior to the disruptions of the mid-19th-century slave raids and imperial expansions in the region.[2]

Raid by Dahomey Kingdom

In 1848, the Kingdom of Dahomey, under King Gezo, conducted military raids into neighboring Yoruba territories as part of its expansionist and slave-acquisition campaigns, targeting villages for captives to fuel the Atlantic slave trade and internal rituals including human sacrifices.[3][10] One such raid struck Oke-Odan, an Egbado Yoruba settlement in present-day southwestern Nigeria, resulting in widespread destruction, the killing of residents, and the enslavement of survivors.[3][11] During the assault on Oke-Odan, Aina—later known as Sarah Forbes Bonetta, then approximately five years old and the daughter of a local chief—was captured after her parents and many villagers were slain.[11][10] Dahomean forces, renowned for their amazon warriors and ritualistic warfare, systematically razed the village to secure slaves, with captives like Aina transported to Dahomey's capital at Abomey for potential sacrifice or sale.[3] This event exemplified Dahomey's aggressive frontier policy, which prioritized annual "customs" wars to amass thousands of prisoners annually, though British naval pressure had begun curtailing transatlantic exports by the late 1840s.[11] Aina's survival amid the raid's carnage marked her transition from Yoruba nobility to enslavement, as she was spared immediate execution—possibly due to her youth or perceived status—and held in the Dahomean royal court, where she endured conditions including witnessing executions before her eventual transfer two years later.[3][10]

British Intervention and Rescue

Encounter with Captain Frederick Forbes

In July 1850, Captain Frederick E. Forbes of the Royal Navy arrived at the court of King Gezo in Abomey, Dahomey (present-day Benin), as part of a British diplomatic mission aimed at curbing the kingdom's involvement in the Atlantic slave trade and human sacrifices.[3][12] Forbes, commanding HMS Bloodhound, sought to persuade Gezo to reduce slave exports and abolish customs like the annual "customs" involving mass executions.[12] During his two-month stay, Forbes observed Dahomey's militaristic society, including its Amazon warriors and ritual practices, which included reserving captives like young girls for immolation or palace service.[12] On July 6, 1850, at a farewell audience, King Gezo presented Forbes with several gifts, including cowries, rum, cloth, and an eight-year-old Yoruba girl named Aina (also spelled Ina), captured two years earlier during the Okeadon war and held at court.[3][12] Aina, from a family of status among the Egbado Yoruba, had been spared immediate sacrifice but faced likely death in future rituals; Gezo offered her explicitly to Forbes as a diplomatic token, recognizing her as exceptional.[13][12] Forbes noted her precocious intellect: already fluent in English from interactions with European traders, she demonstrated musical aptitude by playing a European tune on a native instrument and engaged him in intelligent conversation, impressing him as a "perfect genius."[12] Forbes accepted Aina to avert her probable fate, viewing the transfer as a humanitarian intervention amid Dahomey's entrenched practices of enslavement and sacrifice, which claimed thousands annually.[14][12] He renamed her Sarah Forbes Bonetta, honoring himself and his previous command, HMS Bonetta, a vessel used in anti-slavery patrols.[3][12] This act aligned with British naval efforts to rescue individuals from West African potentates, though Forbes' journals emphasize her personal qualities over mere abolitionist propaganda.[12] Accompanied by Forbes, Aina departed Dahomey shortly thereafter, reaching England in November 1850 after stops including Sierra Leone.[3]

Presentation as a Diplomatic Gift

In January 1850, during his second diplomatic mission to the Kingdom of Dahomey, Captain Frederick E. Forbes encountered a young girl named Aina among captives at the court of King Gezo in Abomey; she was an Egbado princess of royal blood who had been spared immediate sacrifice due to her dignified bearing and intelligence.[3][13] Forbes, seeking to advance British anti-slavery objectives, interceded for her life and persuaded the king to present her as a diplomatic gift to Queen Victoria, proposing that "she would be a present from the King of the Blacks to the Queen of the Whites."[2][4][11] King Gezo agreed to the transfer, viewing it as a token of goodwill despite failing to commit to ending Dahomey's involvement in the slave trade during the negotiations.[3][15] The approximately seven-year-old Aina was thus removed from the Dahomean palace and placed under Forbes' protection aboard HMS Bonetta, symbolizing a rare instance of human exchange in 19th-century West African diplomacy rather than outright purchase or ransom.[11][16] Forbes later detailed the event in his 1851 account Dahomey and the Dahomans, including a sketch of Aina and noting her potential as a cultural emissary, though the gesture did not alter Dahomey's practices. This presentation not only averted her ritual death but also positioned her as a living emblem in British efforts to influence African monarchs through personal ties and prestige.[3][13]

Integration into British Society

Arrival and Initial Impressions

Captain Frederick E. Forbes transported the young girl, renamed Sarah Bonetta, to England following his diplomatic mission to Dahomey in June 1850, with the voyage concluding in late 1850.[17] Upon arrival, she was entrusted to the care of the Forbes family at their home, where she began adapting to British customs and learning the English language, demonstrating remarkable aptitude during the transatlantic journey itself.[3] Forbes expressed high regard for her intellectual capabilities, observing that she was "a perfect genius" who quickly mastered spoken English and exhibited "great intelligence."[3] This assessment aligned with her poised demeanor, which facilitated her integration into the household despite her recent enslavement and cultural dislocation.[13] On 9 November 1850, Bonetta was presented to Queen Victoria at Windsor Castle, where the monarch noted in her journal that the child, saved by Forbes from execution, proved "so intelligent & pleasant," prompting Victoria's immediate interest in her welfare.[3] These early encounters underscored Bonetta's perceptiveness and adaptability, qualities that distinguished her from typical expectations of a recently rescued West African captive.[13]

Education and Intellectual Development

Upon her presentation to Queen Victoria in November 1850, the approximately seven-year-old Sarah—renamed Sarah Bonetta—was baptized in January 1851 and placed under the Queen's sponsorship for education, with the monarch expressing admiration for her precocious intelligence.[3] Due to concerns that England's damp climate exacerbated her fragile health and persistent cough, Victoria arranged in May 1851 for her to be sent to a Church Missionary Society (CMS) school in Freetown, Sierra Leone, intending to prepare her for potential missionary service among her people.[3][13] In Sierra Leone, Sarah attended the Female Institution, a mission school for girls, where she received basic instruction despite reportedly being unhappy with the environment and discipline.[6][18] At Queen Victoria's command, she returned to England in 1855, aged about 12, to continue her studies under closer royal oversight, as the monarch deemed the African schooling insufficient for her potential.[6][3] Back in Britain, Sarah's education emphasized refinement suited to Victorian upper-class society, including fluency in English—which she had begun acquiring during her 1850 voyage from West Africa—proficiency in French, piano performance, and etiquette.[11][19] Queen Victoria personally observed and funded this phase, later recording in her journal that Sarah was "a perfect genius" who spoke English well and displayed "a great talent for music," underscoring her rapid intellectual progress and ability to win admiration from those around her.[6][20]

Royal Patronage

Relationship with Queen Victoria

Sarah Forbes Bonetta was first presented to Queen Victoria on 9 November 1850 at Windsor Castle by Captain Frederick Forbes, who had brought her to England from West Africa.[3][21] Victoria recorded in her journal that Bonetta, then about seven years old, was "a perfect little model" with "fine eyes & a forehead like a Grecian statue," noting her sharpness and intelligence during their initial encounter.[11] Impressed by the child's poise and potential, the Queen decided to oversee her upbringing, funding Bonetta's education at institutions such as a boarding school in Sierra Leone under Church Missionary Society auspices and later in England with the Romaine family in Kent.[3][2] Victoria established a formal patronage by becoming Bonetta's godmother, a role that symbolized personal investment in her ward's Christian education and social integration into British society, though Bonetta had been baptized Sarah Forbes prior to her arrival in England.[4][22] This relationship fostered ongoing correspondence and visits; Bonetta returned to court periodically, including in 1851 for further audiences that reinforced the Queen's admiration for her progress in English language and etiquette.[13] The Queen provided annual stipends—initially £40, later increased to £50—for Bonetta's maintenance, reflecting a commitment to her development as a "liberated African" exemplar of British humanitarian intervention.[11] The bond persisted into adulthood, with Victoria approving and facilitating Bonetta's 1862 marriage to James Pinson Labulo Davies, a Sierra Leonean merchant, by granting her a trousseau and wedding dress.[23] In December 1867, Bonetta visited Osborne House with her young daughter Victoria, whom the Queen also sponsored as godchild, gifting silverware upon the child's christening; Victoria described the family positively in her journal as "fine looking" and affectionate.[3][24] This patronage underscored Victoria's selective interest in Bonetta as a symbol of imperial benevolence, though it was tempered by racial attitudes of the era, with the Queen viewing her as an exceptional individual rather than broadly advocating for African emancipation.[25]

Baptism and Godparenthood

Sarah Forbes Bonetta, originally named Aina, was baptized circa 1849 by Anglican missionaries in Badagry, a coastal town in present-day Nigeria, shortly before her voyage to England aboard HMS Bonetta.[3][2] During the rite, Captain Frederick Forbes conferred upon her the name Sarah Forbes Bonetta, incorporating his surname and the vessel's name to mark her transition from enslavement to British protection.[3][26] Queen Victoria formally assumed the role of godmother following Bonetta's presentation at Windsor Castle on 9 November 1850, where the seven-year-old impressed the monarch with her deportment and quick wit.[2] This sponsorship manifested as ongoing financial provision for Bonetta's education and welfare, including placement under guardians such as the Rev. James Frederick Schön, rather than direct involvement in the West African baptism ceremony.[3][26] The arrangement underscored Victoria's patronage of select liberated Africans as exemplars of civilizing influence, though claims of a subsequent baptism in a Chapel Royal around 1852 lack supporting evidence from parish records.[26]

Marriage and Adult Life

Union with James Pinson Labulo Davies


In 1862, Sarah Forbes Bonetta, then aged 19, became engaged to James Pinson Labulo Davies, a Sierra Leone-born merchant of Yoruba descent whose parents had been freed from slavery.[3] Davies, a successful businessman and philanthropist involved in West African trade, expressed interest in marrying her, a match approved by Queen Victoria who viewed it as suitable given Sarah's African origins and Davies's wealth and status.[3] [27]
Sarah initially resisted the union, writing to her former teacher Mrs. Schoen that she could not learn to love Davies and questioned exchanging her peace of mind for financial security.[3] Despite her reluctance, she proceeded with the marriage, arranged under Queen Victoria's patronage, which funded elements of the trousseau and ensured a lavish ceremony reflective of Sarah's status as the Queen's protégée.[3]
The wedding took place on 14 August 1862 at St. Nicholas Church in Brighton, England, officiated by the Bishop of Sierra Leone.[3] [7] The event featured ten horse-drawn carriages conveying the party from West Hill Lodge, sixteen bridesmaids including four of African descent, and a diverse guest list of European and African attendees.[3] Queen Victoria commissioned wedding portraits of the couple by photographer Camille Silvy, underscoring her ongoing interest in the match.[3]

Relocation to West Africa

Following her marriage to James Pinson Labulo Davies on 16 August 1862 at St. Nicholas Church in Brighton, England, Sarah Davies accompanied her husband to West Africa to support his expanding mercantile ventures. Davies, a Sierra Leone-born merchant of Yoruba descent with significant business interests along the West African coast, initially established their residence in Freetown, Sierra Leone, where the couple settled shortly after the wedding. This relocation aligned with Davies's professional activities, including trade in commodities and shipping, which were centered in the region.[3][13] The move to Sierra Leone represented a return to African roots for Sarah, who had spent much of her childhood and adolescence in England under royal patronage, though her early years originated from the Egbado region near present-day Nigeria. In Freetown, a hub for liberated Africans and British colonial administration, the Davies established a household reflective of their status, with Sarah adapting to local social circles while maintaining ties to Britain through correspondence with Queen Victoria. Subsequently, the family relocated to Lagos, Nigeria, around 1867, where Davies owned property including a cocoa farm and resided at Wood House on Broad Street, further integrating into the elite Yoruba merchant community. This shift to Lagos capitalized on Davies's prominence as a philanthropist and industrialist in the area, though Sarah's fragile health, exacerbated by the tropical climate, prompted periodic returns to England for medical care.[3][28][29]

Family and Descendants

Children and Immediate Family

Sarah Forbes Bonetta and her husband, James Pinson Labulo Davies, had three children. Their eldest, Victoria Davies, was born in 1863 and named in honor of Queen Victoria, who agreed to serve as the child's godmother and later funded her education in England.[3] In December 1867, Bonetta presented the young Victoria to the queen during a visit to Osborne House.[3] The couple's second child, Arthur Davies, was born in 1871, followed by their third, Stella Davies, in 1873.[6] These births occurred after the family's relocation to Lagos, where Davies managed his mercantile interests.[6] By the time of Bonetta's death in 1880 from tuberculosis, Victoria was 17 years old, while Arthur and Stella were 9 and 7, respectively; the queen continued to provide support for Victoria, including an annuity.[3][11]

Continuation of Royal Ties

Victoria Matilda Davies, Sarah Forbes Bonetta's eldest daughter born in 1863, was named in honor of Queen Victoria, who served as her godmother and extended the royal patronage originally bestowed upon her mother.[3] Following Sarah's death from tuberculosis on 15 August 1880 in Madeira, where the 17-year-old Victoria learned of the loss while en route to England, Queen Victoria ensured her goddaughter's welfare by financing her education at Cheltenham Ladies' College.[3] This support underscored the continuity of the monarch's interest in the family, with Victoria maintaining correspondence and occasional visits to the royal household, reflecting Queen Victoria's personal affection documented in her journals as a "dear child" worthy of ongoing care.[1] The arrangement provided Victoria with formal schooling in England, aligning with the queen's earlier efforts to educate Sarah, though Victoria ultimately returned to West Africa, marrying Lagos physician John Randle in 1890 and establishing a prominent family there.[3][30] While direct royal involvement waned after Queen Victoria's death in 1901, the ties symbolized a rare instance of sustained Victorian-era patronage toward an African-descended family, with no verified financial or personal connections extending to subsequent British monarchs.[1]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Final Years and Illness

In the years following her relocation to West Africa with her husband James Pinson Labulo Davies, Sarah Forbes Bonetta Davies experienced persistent respiratory issues, including a chronic cough that had afflicted her since childhood and gradually worsened.[2] [4] This condition was formally diagnosed as tuberculosis, a progressive lung disease common in the 19th century, which she managed while raising her family and maintaining ties to Britain.[2] [11] Despite the illness, she bore additional children, demonstrating resilience amid deteriorating health.[2] By 1880, her tuberculosis had advanced to a critical stage, prompting travel to the island of Madeira in the Atlantic Ocean—a popular 19th-century sanatorium destination known for its mild climate believed to aid recovery from consumptive ailments.[13] [11] She arrived in Funchal, the capital, seeking convalescence, but the disease proved fatal.[7] Davies died there on August 15, 1880, at approximately 37 years of age.[7] [31]
Her death from tuberculosis was confirmed by contemporary accounts, with Queen Victoria noting the loss in her journal upon learning of it, expressing sympathy for Davies's surviving daughter Victoria.[31] The queen, who had maintained sporadic contact, viewed the event as a personal tragedy tied to her goddaughter's upbringing under British influence.[13]

Burial and Family Response

Sarah Forbes Bonetta Davies succumbed to tuberculosis on 15 August 1880 in Funchal, Madeira, at age 37, during a voyage intended to benefit from the island's temperate climate.[3] She was buried in the British Cemetery in Funchal, despite her prior request for burial at sea, which was not honored.[32] Her husband, James Pinson Labulo Davies, a Lagos-based merchant, returned to West Africa with their surviving children following the burial, continuing his commercial and philanthropic endeavors there until his death in 1906.[33] The couple's eldest daughter, Victoria Sarah Davies (born 1863), had predeceased her mother in infancy, but their younger daughter, also named Victoria (born circa 1876), received financial support from Queen Victoria for education in England, reflecting ongoing ties to the British royal family rather than direct family-led commemorations at the time.[19] The grave site, initially marked only as Plot 206 without identification, went unmarked for 139 years until 2019, when researchers and descendants facilitated the erection of a proper headstone, restoring visibility to her resting place in the cemetery.[34] This effort underscores later generations' role in preserving her memory, as immediate family records show no contemporaneous attempts to erect a monument amid Davies' focus on family relocation and business in Sierra Leone and Nigeria.[33]

Historical Context and Significance

Dahomey's Slave Trade Practices

The Kingdom of Dahomey, emerging as a powerful militarized state in the early 18th century, actively engaged in the Atlantic slave trade by conducting organized raids and wars to capture individuals from neighboring territories, such as the Mahi and Yoruba states, for sale to European traders primarily at coastal ports like Ouidah.[35] These campaigns were integral to Dahomey's economy and expansion, with the monarchy exerting control over the export trade, initially as a monopoly under kings like Agaja (r. 1718–1740), who conquered Allada in 1724 and Ouidah in 1727 to secure direct access to European buyers.[36] Captives, often numbering in the thousands per major expedition, were marched to the coast, where they were exchanged for goods including firearms, cowrie shells, and textiles, fueling further militarization.[37] Dahomey's practices extended beyond export to include domestic enslavement and ritual sacrifice, particularly during the Annual Customs (Xwetanu), a series of ceremonies honoring deceased kings that involved "yearly wars" to replenish slave supplies.[36] Unsold or designated captives faced execution, with historical accounts recording hundreds sacrificed annually; for instance, during the Grand Customs for King Gezo's (r. 1818–1858) predecessors, up to 500 prisoners were killed in ritual slayings, though numbers varied and peaked at over 1,000 during major events like royal funerals or coronations.[38] This system prioritized male captives for military use or sacrifice and females for palace labor or reproduction, reflecting a hierarchical society where slavery underpinned both economic revenue and religious obligations to ancestors.[39] Quantitative estimates indicate Dahomey's exports contributed significantly to the Bight of Benin's total of approximately 1.95 million slaves shipped between 1650 and 1900, with the kingdom's volume rising sharply after 1720 to an average of several thousand per year in the mid-18th century—early observers like d'Elbée noted 3,000 annual sales at Ouidah by 1670, predating full Dahomean control, while by the 1770s, King Kpengla (r. 1774–1789) derived an estimated £250,000 annually from the trade.[37] Exports peaked in the 1780s before declining due to British abolition efforts post-1807, shifting Dahomey toward palm oil but sustaining illegal trade into the 1850s, with raids continuing to supply Brazilian and Cuban markets.[40] Historians like Robin Law argue that while the slave trade amplified Dahomey's power, the kingdom's pre-existing militarism drove its predatory practices rather than trade dependency alone causing its rise.[35]

British Anti-Slavery Operations

The British West Africa Squadron, established in 1808 following the Slave Trade Act, conducted naval patrols along the West African coastline to intercept and seize vessels engaged in the transatlantic slave trade. Operating until 1867, the squadron captured around 1,600 ships and liberated approximately 150,000 enslaved Africans, though these efforts represented only a fraction of the ongoing trade and incurred significant costs to the Royal Navy, estimated at over £40 million by mid-century.[41][42] The operations focused primarily on maritime enforcement, including boarding suspected slavers and adjudicating captures in mixed commission courts established under treaties with other European powers. Complementing naval interdiction, British anti-slavery policy incorporated diplomatic initiatives to pressure inland kingdoms supplying slaves to coastal ports, aiming to shift economies toward "legitimate commerce" such as palm oil exports. These missions sought treaties prohibiting slave exports while tolerating internal African slavery and practices like human sacrifice, reflecting a pragmatic focus on ending transatlantic shipments rather than eradicating slavery continent-wide. In the 1840s, amid declining demand for slaves due to British abolition and naval blockades, kingdoms like Dahomey faced economic strain, prompting Britain to intensify persuasion through envoys bearing gifts and trade incentives.[3] A key example occurred in 1849–1850, when Captain Frederick E. Forbes led a diplomatic mission to Dahomey's King Ghezo, traveling inland from Whydah to Abomey to negotiate an end to slave trading and promotion of palm oil production. Forbes, documenting the journey in his 1851 publication Dahomey and the Dahomans, urged Ghezo to abolish exports in exchange for British protection and commerce, though the king resisted full compliance, citing economic dependence on slaving raids. During this mission, Forbes secured the release of a young Egbado captive, later known as Sarah Forbes Bonetta, whom Ghezo presented as a diplomatic gift; this act exemplified how such operations occasionally rescued individuals amid broader failures to halt Dahomey's annual customs involving thousands of captives.[12][3] The mission highlighted limits of diplomacy, as Dahomey continued slave exports covertly while adapting to palm oil, but it contributed to gradual pressure that reduced overt transatlantic shipments by the 1850s.[43]

Controversies and Interpretations

Views on Agency and Exploitation

Historians have interpreted Sarah Forbes Bonetta's life as a tool of British imperial propaganda, where her rescue from Dahomey in 1850 and subsequent education in England were framed to exemplify the success of Britain's anti-slavery and civilizing missions. Contemporary accounts, such as those in Victorian newspapers covering her 1862 wedding to James Pinson Labulo Davies, portrayed her as a grateful beneficiary exercising personal agency under British patronage, with Queen Victoria's involvement highlighted to underscore moral superiority over African practices like Dahomey's slave raids and human sacrifices.[44] This narrative positioned Bonetta as "Britain's African Princess," a living emblem of redemption that justified naval interventions and colonial expansion, though primary evidence from Captain Frederick Forbes's 1851 account indicates her initial presentation to the queen was as a "gift" from Dahomey's King Ghezo, commodifying her status from the outset.[44] Critiques emphasize exploitation over agency, arguing that Bonetta's trajectory was curated by British authorities with minimal input from her, reducing her to a symbolic figure devoid of autonomy. Her marriage, publicly celebrated and financially supported by Victoria, was arranged despite her youth and dependent position, reflecting broader patterns where her visibility served propagandistic ends rather than personal volition; as one analysis notes, "Bonetta’s life played out as a paradigmatic microcosm of propaganda for Victorian Britain."[44] Scholars like Samantha Pinto frame her celebrity within "conflicting genres of sovereignty," where nominal empowerment through education and royal ties masked objectification, as her body and story were deployed to legitimize imperial rights discourses while eliding the violence of her enslavement and forced assimilation.[45] This view aligns with observations that post-rescue, Bonetta's movements and public image were managed, including her repatriation attempts to Sierra Leone thwarted by health and oversight, underscoring causal constraints from her orphaned, non-citizen status in a paternalistic system.[45] Modern reinterpretations, often from black feminist perspectives, seek to reclaim agency by amplifying Bonetta's erased voice against propagandistic erasure, yet risk retrojecting contemporary anti-colonial lenses onto empirical outcomes like her literacy, entrepreneurial marriage, and maternal legacy. Authors such as Sarah Raughley contend that Britain "used her for propaganda" then "threw her away" after her 1880 death, interpreting her naming of daughter Victoria as potential "psychological costs of forced assimilation" rather than pragmatic adaptation.[46] Artistic projects since the 2010s, including photographic series by Ayana V. Jackson, reframe her portraits to "redistribute power," countering historical fetishization but sometimes prioritizing narrative subversion over verifiable details of her expressed contentment in letters to Victoria.[44] These debates reflect source biases, with academic works prone to emphasizing exploitation amid systemic skepticism toward imperial records, while first-hand Victorian documentation—despite self-interested—documents her survival from Dahomey's documented sacrificial customs as a factual baseline for assessing relative agency.[44]

Propaganda and Imperial Narratives

Sarah Forbes Bonetta's rescue from Dahomey in 1850 by Captain Frederick E. Forbes was framed in his 1851 publication Dahomey and the Dahomans as a triumph of British humanitarianism over African "barbarism," portraying her as an intelligent child "far in advance of any white child of her age" to underscore the civilizing potential of British intervention.[12] [44] Forbes' narrative, drawn from his mission to King Ghezo, emphasized Dahomey's annual "customs" of human sacrifice and slavery while highlighting Bonetta's rapid adaptation to English education, serving to legitimize Britain's anti-slavery diplomacy and naval presence in West Africa.[12] Upon her presentation to Queen Victoria in late 1850 and baptism as Sarah Forbes Bonetta in 1851, her story circulated in British society as evidence of monarchical benevolence, with Victoria's journals noting her "very intelligent" demeanor and funding her education at the expense of the crown.[44] This royal endorsement amplified the propagandistic value, positioning Bonetta as a living symbol of successful assimilation, where her portraits—such as Camille Silvy's 1862 photograph—depicted her in Victorian finery to counter prevailing racial stereotypes of African inferiority. British newspapers romanticized her as an "African princess," leveraging her trajectory to endorse anti-slavery efforts amid ongoing transatlantic trade debates, though such accounts often elided Dahomey's coerced shift to palm oil exports under British pressure.[47] Bonetta's 1862 wedding to James Pinson Labulo Davies received extensive press coverage, with outlets like the Islington Gazette describing her as "one of the prettiest coloured ladies" and reprinting excerpts from Forbes' memoir to reinforce narratives of British moral superiority and the efficacy of imperial "civilizing" missions.[44] These depictions framed her marriage—attended by elite society and funded partly by Victoria—as validation of Britain's transformative influence on Africans, aligning with broader Victorian justifications for colonial expansion in Africa despite persistent regional slave raiding.[18] While contemporary sources praised her agency and accomplishments, the selective emphasis on her "ladylike" assimilation obscured underlying power dynamics, including her initial status as a "gift" from Ghezo and the propaganda's role in sanitizing Britain's economic interests in the region.[44]

Legacy and Modern Recognition

Influence on Descendants

Sarah Forbes Bonetta's eldest daughter, Victoria Davies (born 1863), inherited her mother's close ties to the British monarchy, serving as goddaughter to Queen Victoria and receiving a lifelong annuity of £40 from the Crown, which supported her education in England before her relocation to Sierra Leone and Lagos.[3][13] Victoria maintained periodic visits to the royal household, fostering a legacy of transatlantic elite networks that elevated her family's social standing in colonial West Africa.[1] Victoria married John Randle, a Lagos-based physician and early Nigerian nationalist, in 1887, linking Bonetta's lineage to influential mercantile and professional circles; their descendants, including the Randle family, became prominent in Lagos society, with figures like J.K. Randle advancing in medicine, law, and civic leadership, reflecting Bonetta's indirect role in promoting educated African agency amid colonial structures.[30][48] Bonetta's emphasis on formal education for her children—evident in Victoria's training and the family's subsequent professional pursuits—contrasted with prevailing norms in 19th-century West Africa, contributing to a generational shift toward literacy and entrepreneurship among descendants.[49] Bonetta and her husband James Pinson Labulo Davies had at least three other children following Victoria, though records of their direct achievements are limited; the family's collective relocation to Lagos after 1862 positioned them within emerging Nigerian elite networks, where Bonetta's royal patronage and Davies's business ventures in shipping and agriculture provided economic foundations that sustained descendants through the colonial era.[3] Descendants today reside primarily in Nigeria, Sierra Leone, and Britain, with some tracing their heritage to Bonetta as a symbol of resilience, often invoking her story in personal genealogies and cultural narratives to underscore themes of African royal heritage and anti-slavery triumph.[49][11]

Cultural Depictions and Recent Honors

In 2020, English Heritage commissioned and unveiled a portrait of Sarah Forbes Bonetta by artist Hannah Uzor at Osborne House on the Isle of Wight, Queen Victoria's former residence, as part of the "Painting Our Past" series aimed at representing overlooked Black figures in British history.[16] [50] The full-length oil painting draws from a 1862 photograph of Bonetta by Camille Silvy held in the National Portrait Gallery, depicting her in Victorian attire to evoke her integration into British society while honoring her African origins.[51] This artwork has since been exhibited at sites including the Africa Centre in London, contributing to public awareness of her biography.[52] Literature has fictionalized aspects of Bonetta's experiences, notably in D.L. Murray's historical novel The Diary of Sarah Forbes Bonetta (2019), which portrays her as a seventeen-year-old in Kent beginning a diary in 1860, blending documented events with imagined personal reflections on her education and royal connections.[53] A biographical film adaptation is in development, with Cynthia Erivo set to star as Bonetta and produce, chronicling her transition from enslavement in West Africa to her role as Queen Victoria's goddaughter; the project was announced in December 2024.[54] [55] These efforts reflect renewed interest in Bonetta's life amid broader heritage initiatives, including audio essays by historians such as David Olusoga for BBC Radio 3 in 2019, which examined her story in the context of transatlantic slavery's legacies.[56]
