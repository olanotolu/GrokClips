Sarah Matthews (deputy press secretary)

Sarah Anne Matthews is an American political aide and communications specialist who served as Deputy Press Secretary in the White House during the final months of Donald Trump's presidency, from June 2020 until her resignation on January 6, 2021.[1][2] Prior to her White House position, Matthews worked as a spokeswoman for Trump's 2020 re-election campaign starting in June 2019 and held earlier roles as a Capitol Hill staffer.[1][3] A Kent State University graduate from Ohio, she rose rapidly in political communications, handling press interactions during a contentious election period.[3][4] Matthews resigned in the immediate aftermath of the January 6 Capitol breach, citing the events as a breaking point, and subsequently cooperated with investigations into the incident.[5][6] She provided public testimony to the House Select Committee on the January 6 Attack in July 2022, describing internal White House dynamics and criticizing Trump's response to the violence as inadequate.[5][1] This appearance marked her shift toward open criticism of Trump, positioning her as a rare former administration official testifying against her former employer in a politically charged inquiry led by opponents of the president.[7] Since leaving the administration, Matthews has described herself as a "Republican in exile" and has advocated against Trump's continued influence in the party, including supporting his 2024 opponent Kamala Harris.[8][9] Her post-White House commentary has focused on perceived threats to democratic norms stemming from the 2020 election challenges, though her perspective aligns with a minority view among Republican voters and officials who largely supported Trump's election-related claims.[9][10]

Early Life and Background

Upbringing and Family Origins

Sarah Matthews was raised in North Canton, Ohio, a suburb near Canton in Stark County.[3] [11] Her family maintained deep roots in local Republican politics, with both parents actively engaged in political activities.[12] Matthews' father held positions as Stark County Republican Party chairman and county elections director for multiple years, providing her early exposure to grassroots organizing and electoral processes.[13] This upbringing in a politically conservative household near Canton influenced her initial foray into Republican campaign volunteering during high school at Hoover High School in North Canton.[11] [13] The familial emphasis on party involvement fostered her interest in public relations and communications, though specific details on her mother's role or extended family origins remain limited in public records.[12]

Education at Kent State University

Sarah Matthews attended Kent State University in Kent, Ohio, where she pursued a Bachelor of Arts degree in public relations.[3][14] She enrolled around 2013 and completed her studies in 2017, though she relocated to Washington, D.C., shortly before formally receiving her diploma due to a job opportunity.[3] During her time at Kent State, Matthews engaged in political activities aligned with Republican causes, including membership in the Kent State College Republicans chapter, which facilitated early networking in conservative circles.[7][3] The university's public relations program emphasized practical skills, such as a required internship component that allowed her to gain hands-on experience; she completed three notable internships while still a student: one with former Speaker of the House John Boehner, another with U.S. Senator Rob Portman (R-OH), and a role at the 2016 Republican National Convention held in Cleveland.[3] These experiences bridged her academic training with professional political communications, including digital media work like social media management and content creation.[3] Matthews also took coursework in print beat reporting, which provided insight into journalistic practices and reporter perspectives, complementing her public relations focus.[3] Her involvement in these extracurricular and academic elements positioned her for rapid advancement in political communications upon graduation.[3]

Professional Career Prior to White House

Early Roles in Washington, D.C.

Matthews relocated to Washington, D.C., shortly before graduating from Kent State University in May 2017, marking the start of her professional career in political communications on Capitol Hill. Her initial full-time role was as a communications aide and digital manager for the U.S. House Committee on Homeland Security, beginning in the spring of 2017 and enduring for about one year. In this capacity, she handled social media management, photographed events, produced graphics, and, after advocating for expanded responsibilities, began writing content and coordinating with reporters to amplify committee messaging.[3] Transitioning to a more press-oriented position around 2018, Matthews served as press secretary for the U.S. House Committee on Energy and Commerce. She authored press releases, speeches, op-eds, and talking points for committee members, while cultivating relationships with journalists, pitching story ideas, and fielding media inquiries to shape public narratives on energy policy, health care, and commerce issues. This role honed her skills in rapid-response communications and strategic media engagement, building on her prior experience.[3] Before these professional positions, Matthews accumulated practical exposure through internships during her undergraduate years, including stints with former House Speaker John Boehner, Ohio Senator Rob Portman, and the communications team at the 2016 Republican National Convention in Cleveland. These early opportunities provided hands-on training in political public relations, from event coordination to messaging development, facilitating her swift entry into full-time Capitol Hill work upon arrival in D.C.[3]

Work as Capitol Hill Staffer

Prior to her involvement in the Trump presidential campaign, Sarah Matthews held internships on Capitol Hill for Ohio Republican lawmakers, including former House Speaker John Boehner and Senator Rob Portman.[3][7] These experiences, undertaken during her time at Kent State University and shortly after graduation in 2016, provided early exposure to congressional operations and communications.[3] Following graduation, Matthews secured her first full-time role in spring 2017 as a communications aide and digital manager for the Republican staff of the U.S. House Committee on Homeland Security, where she handled social media, graphics, photos, writing, and media relations for approximately one year.[3][14] In May 2018, she transitioned to the House Committee on Energy and Commerce as press secretary for the Republican majority, serving until June 2019 under Chairman Greg Walden (R-OR).[14][15] In this position, she managed press releases, speeches, op-eds, talking points, and relationships with media outlets covering committee activities on energy policy, health care, and commerce issues.[3][2] Her work supported the committee's legislative efforts during the 115th and 116th Congresses, including oversight hearings and bill advancements.[16]

Involvement with Trump Administration

Position in 2020 Presidential Campaign

Sarah Matthews joined Donald Trump's 2020 presidential re-election campaign in June 2019 as deputy press secretary.[1][3] In this role, she worked under national press secretary Kayleigh McEnany, supporting press operations and communications efforts.[1] As an on-the-record spokeswoman, Matthews handled media inquiries and traveled nationwide to President Trump's rallies and events featuring high-profile surrogates.[3] Her responsibilities included developing messaging strategies aligned with campaign objectives during the lead-up to the November 2020 election.[3] Matthews departed the campaign in June 2020 after McEnany, by then White House press secretary, recruited her to the administration as deputy press secretary.[1][3] This transition occurred approximately five months before Election Day, amid intensifying campaign activities.[3]

Appointment as Deputy Press Secretary

Sarah Matthews transitioned to the White House in June 2020, assuming the role of Deputy Press Secretary after serving in the same capacity for President Donald Trump's re-election campaign from June 2019 to June 2020.[1] The appointment came shortly after Kayleigh McEnany, who had been the campaign's national press secretary, was elevated to White House Press Secretary; McEnany personally offered the deputy position to Matthews as part of the administration's press office restructuring amid the ongoing 2020 presidential election.[1][17] Matthews officially joined the White House press team on June 8, 2020, where she functioned as a key communications aide, handling media inquiries and supporting briefings during a period of heightened scrutiny over the COVID-19 pandemic and election-related developments.[17][2] Her selection reflected the administration's preference for continuity from the campaign staff, leveraging her prior experience in Republican communications roles on Capitol Hill and in political outreach.[1] The role positioned her as a special assistant to the president within the press office, reporting directly to McEnany and contributing to the administration's messaging strategy through the end of Trump's term.[1][2]

Role During Final Months of Presidency

Daily Responsibilities and Contributions

In her role as deputy press secretary from June 2020 onward, Sarah Matthews functioned as a primary spokesperson for President Donald Trump, allocating significant portions of her daily schedule to engaging the White House press corps on policy matters, election developments, and administration responses to current events.[3] This involved fielding inquiries from reporters, providing on-the-record clarifications, and facilitating access to official statements amid heightened media demands in the administration's final months.[3] [17] Matthews contributed to the press office's operational framework by developing messaging strategies tailored to daily news cycles, which included monitoring sources such as POLITICO Playbook newsletters in the early morning and tracking reporters' social media activity on Twitter for emerging narratives.[3] She prepared Press Secretary Kayleigh McEnany for briefings through the assembly of detailed fact binders and "murder board" sessionsâ€”intensive rehearsals simulating adversarial questioningâ€”to refine responses and mitigate potential missteps.[3] These preparations supported the team's ability to defend administration positions under scrutiny, particularly during the post-election period marked by disputes over voting results and transition challenges.[3] Beyond briefings, Matthews staffed the president for interviews, coordinating logistics and content alignment to ensure consistent communication of priorities like economic recovery and foreign policy.[3] [17] Her involvement in these routines bolstered the press office's resilience in a polarized media environment, where traditional briefings had become infrequent, relying instead on targeted gaggles and rapid-response tactics.[3]

Interactions with Press and Key Events

As deputy press secretary during the post-election period from November 2020 to January 2021, Sarah Matthews supported Press Secretary Kayleigh McEnany in managing communications with the White House press corps amid President Trump's refusal to concede the election to Joe Biden. The press office conducted informal gaggles and issued statements asserting the presence of voting irregularities in battleground states, responding to reporter demands for evidence of fraud while highlighting affidavits from observers and ongoing lawsuits filed by the Trump campaign.[1][5] Key events included the administration's pushback against media projections of Biden's victory on November 7, 2020, with the press team, including deputies like Matthews, emphasizing incomplete vote counts and legal challenges in states such as Pennsylvania, Georgia, and Michigan. Over 60 lawsuits were filed alleging misconduct, though most were dismissed by courts for insufficient evidence or procedural issues, a reality the press office contested in interactions with skeptical reporters.[18] These engagements often devolved into tense exchanges, as outlets like CNN and The New York Times labeled the claims unsubstantiated, prompting the White House to accuse the media of bias in favor of Biden.[17] In December 2020, Matthews participated in efforts to communicate the administration's focus on Georgia's Senate runoffs alongside election disputes, coordinating responses to inquiries about state certifications and potential decertification efforts. The press operation functioned akin to a campaign war room, prioritizing rapid rebuttals to fact-checks and coverage dismissing fraud allegations, despite internal awareness among some staff that evidentiary thresholds for overturning results were unmet.[7][3]

January 6, 2021, Events and Resignation

Observations from White House on Riot Day

Sarah Matthews, serving as deputy press secretary in the West Wing, observed the unfolding violence at the U.S. Capitol around 2:00 p.m. via television and social media alongside colleagues, describing a state of shock and alarm among White House staff as the situation escalated rapidly.[19][20] She immediately urged press secretary Kayleigh McEnany to press President Trump to issue a statement condemning the violence and directing rioters to leave the Capitol, coordinating with aide Ben Williamson in efforts reflecting broader staff concern.[19][20] Matthews testified that Trump remained in the private dining room adjacent to the Oval Office from approximately 1:25 p.m. to 4:00 p.m., reportedly watching coverage on Fox News without taking immediate action to intervene.[19][20] At 2:24 p.m., Trump posted a tweet criticizing Vice President Mike Pence for lacking courage to address election irregularities, which Matthews described as "pouring gasoline on the fire" and effectively signaling approval to the rioters amid the breach.[19][20] Staff and family members, including House Minority Leader Kevin McCarthy and Ivanka Trump, reportedly pleaded with Trump to condemn the violence; he resisted such language, only agreeing to a tweet urging supporters to "stay peaceful" after Ivanka's suggestion, while dismissing McCarthy's concerns by claiming the rioters were not his supporters.[19][20] No public statement from Trump materialized for over three hours, with Matthews noting he could have reached the White House briefing roomâ€”equipped with a live cameraâ€”in less than 60 seconds to address the nation directly.[21] At 4:17 p.m., Trump released a video message instructing rioters to "go home" but also praising them as "very special" and expressing love for them, which Matthews found disturbing as it appeared to validate their actions.[19][20] Later, at 6:01 p.m., Trump tweeted that the events were a response to a "rigged election," further underscoring what Matthews viewed as his reluctance to unequivocally denounce the riot.[20] These observations, drawn from her sworn testimony before the House Select Committee investigating January 6, contributed to her decision to resign that evening, citing Trump's "indefensible" refusal to prioritize stopping the violence.[19][5]

Decision to Resign and Immediate Aftermath

Sarah Matthews decided to resign from her position as deputy White House press secretary on the evening of January 6, 2021, amid the riot at the U.S. Capitol.[6] In a public statement released that night, she expressed that she had been "deeply disturbed" by the day's events and emphasized her belief in the "power of the peaceful transfer of power," announcing her departure effective immediately.[22] [18] Matthews later recounted that she had begun losing faith in President Trump following the November 2020 election, but the events of January 6â€”particularly his refusal to unequivocally condemn the violenceâ€”led to a complete loss of confidence, prompting her decision.[13] She cited Trump's tweet criticizing Vice President Mike Pence for lacking "the courage" to alter the electoral certification as a key factor, viewing it as exacerbating the unrest rather than de-escalating it.[5] Her resignation contributed to a broader wave of departures from the administration in the riot's immediate wake, including several senior officials who similarly invoked concerns over the peaceful transition of power.[23] Matthews described the choice as deeply personal and declined to criticize colleagues who remained, stating she did not fault their decisions amid the turmoil.[24] In the days following, her action received attention in media reports, with some conservative commentators questioning the timing given her prior service through contested post-election periods, while others praised it as a stand for institutional norms.[24] Matthews maintained that her tenure had been an honor, highlighting pride in enacted policies, but prioritized the imperative of democratic continuity in her exit.[6]

Testimony to January 6 Select Committee

Preparation and Public Hearing Appearance

Sarah Matthews received a subpoena from the House Select Committee investigating the January 6, 2021, Capitol attack in early July 2022, requiring her to testify at a forthcoming public hearing.[6] She had previously cooperated with the committee through a voluntary transcribed interview conducted on February 9, 2022.[25] Following the subpoena, Matthews agreed to participate in the public proceedings, drawing on her firsthand experiences as deputy press secretary during the final months of the Trump administration.[26] Her public testimony occurred during the committee's eighth hearing on July 21, 2022, a prime-time session examining efforts to overturn the 2020 election results and the administration's actions amid the Capitol breach.[5] Matthews appeared live before the committee, delivering sworn statements alongside other witnesses, including former White House counsel Pat Cipollone, with her remarks centered on internal White House dynamics on and around January 6.[18] The hearing, chaired by Representative Bennie Thompson, featured pre-recorded clips from prior depositions to contextualize live accounts, though Matthews' contribution was presented directly.[27] Her preparation involved coordination with committee staff, review of relevant documents and timelines from her tenure, and consultation with legal counsel to ensure accuracy under oath.[19]

Specific Claims Made Under Oath

In her deposition before the House Select Committee on January 6 on February 8, 2022, Sarah Matthews testified under oath that senior White House officials, including Chief of Staff Mark Meadows, privately acknowledged to her that Donald Trump had lost the 2020 presidential election to Joe Biden, despite public claims to the contrary by Trump and his allies.[28] She stated that this internal recognition was evident in conversations where aides expressed frustration over efforts to overturn the results, describing a consensus among staff that legal challenges would fail.[28] Matthews claimed that Trump was repeatedly informed by advisors of the lack of evidence for widespread election fraud, yet he persisted in promoting the narrative of a "stolen" election, which she observed influenced the rhetoric and actions leading up to January 6, 2021.[28] During the public hearing on July 21, 2022, she reiterated under oath that Trump's 4:17 p.m. video address that dayâ€”issued after rioters had breached the Capitol for over three hoursâ€”reiterated unsubstantiated fraud allegations, failing to unequivocally condemn the violence and instead calling participants "very special," which she said blurred distinctions between peaceful supporters and assailants.[19] She testified that Trump watched coverage of the Capitol attack on television from the White House but delayed decisive action, ignoring urgent pleas from staff for him to intervene directly, as his words held unique sway over the crowd.[19] Matthews described pressing colleagues, including Press Secretary Kayleigh McEnany, to prioritize public condemnations of the riot over internal political debates, asserting that Trump's inaction was "indefensible" and contributed to prolonged unrest.[19] This led her to resign later that evening, citing in her statement the need for Trump to commit publicly to a peaceful transfer of power.[19]

Post-White House Public Positions

Evolution of Views on Trump and GOP

Matthews initially expressed support for Trump's administration and policies during her tenure as deputy press secretary from August 2020 until her resignation on January 6, 2021. In her resignation statement, she stated, "I was honored to serve in the Trump administration and proud of the policies we enacted," while citing a loss of confidence in Trump following the Capitol riot as the reason for departing.[6][25] She later recounted in a 2023 interview that her faith in Trump had begun eroding even before January 6, particularly after the 2020 election, amid efforts to challenge the results.[13] Following her resignation, Matthews' criticisms of Trump intensified through public testimony and media appearances. During her July 21, 2022, appearance before the House Select Committee on January 6, she described Trump's refusal to condemn the Capitol violence as "indefensible" and detailed internal White House discussions revealing his reluctance to disavow white supremacists unequivocally.[5] By 2023, she publicly warned of Trump's escalating rhetoric, stating in a November interview that his behavior inside the White House during the riot demonstrated a dereliction of duty, and in February 2024, she characterized his recent statements as "more unhinged," urging the Republican Party to reject him.[29][30] On the broader GOP, Matthews has positioned herself as a critic of party loyalty to Trump while maintaining a Republican identity, self-describing on social media as a "Republican in exile."[8] In March 2024 discussions with fellow former Trump staffers, she advocated for a post-Trump Republican Party focused on policy renewal rather than personal allegiance to the former president.[12] By October 2024, she participated in anti-Trump advertising campaigns, emphasizing his unfitness for office and expressing support for alternatives that align with traditional conservative principles, reflecting a shift from insider defense to external opposition against what she views as the party's Trump-dominated trajectory.[31][9]

Advocacy Roles and Media Appearances

Following her resignation from the Trump administration, Sarah Matthews aligned with anti-Trump Republican efforts, participating in the Republican Voters Against Trump (RVAT) campaign operated by the Republican Accountability PAC. On October 30, 2024, RVAT initiated a $40 million ad series featuring Matthews, where she stated that Trump's response to the January 6, 2021, Capitol events disqualified him from office, emphasizing her firsthand observations from the White House.[31] Matthews has advocated for Democratic nominee Kamala Harris in the 2024 election cycle, citing her experiences defending Trump's rhetoric as creating "cognitive dissonance" that led to her opposition.[9] She joined former Trump communications director Alyssa Farah Griffin in a March 27, 2024, University of Pennsylvania discussion on the Republican Party's future, critiquing Trump's influence and calling for a post-Trump GOP focused on policy over personality.[12] In media outlets, Matthews has appeared on MSNBC and CNN to criticize Trump's post-presidency actions and potential second term. On March 7, 2024, she told MSNBC that a Trump return would feature sycophants enabling unchecked behavior, contrasting it with the first term's partial checks from advisors.[32] In November 2024, she commented on Trump's cabinet selections, arguing they reflected power consolidation rather than strategic governance.[33] Earlier, in November 2023, she discussed Trump's legal challenges on MSNBC, describing his attacks on institutions as undermining democratic norms.[29] These appearances position her as a Republican voice warning against Trump's leadership, drawing on her administration tenure for credibility.

Controversies and Criticisms

Challenges to Testimony Credibility

Sarah Matthews' testimony before the January 6 Select Committee on July 21, 2022, drew immediate criticism from former President Donald Trump, who described her as someone "who I didn't know" and accused her of "clearly lying" without specifying particular falsehoods.[34] The House Republican Conference echoed this skepticism in a now-deleted tweet labeling her "another liar and pawn" of the Democratic-led committee, reflecting broader GOP doubts about the reliability of former Trump aides who testified publicly.[35] [36] A key challenge stems from Matthews' junior role as deputy press secretary, which afforded her limited direct access to President Trump's decision-making processes. At age 27 during the events, she primarily relayed information from press secretary Kayleigh McEnany and other staff, rendering much of her account second-hand hearsay rather than eyewitness observation.[1] For instance, her claims about Trump's resistance to stronger condemnations of the violenceâ€”such as preferring phrasing that rioters "love me"â€”were based on internal discussions she overheard or was told about, not personal conversations with Trump.[5] Critics argue this reliance on filtered reports introduces risks of distortion or incomplete context, especially given the chaotic White House environment on January 6.[37] Matthews' prolonged tenure in the administration until the evening of January 6 has also fueled questions of selective outrage or post-hoc rationalization. Commentators have noted that she continued serving despite Trump's prior controversial actions, such as his equivocal responses to the 2020 summer riots, only resigning after the Capitol breachâ€”a pattern suggesting her testimony may reflect a career-driven pivot toward anti-Trump advocacy rather than consistent ethical concerns.[38] Her subsequent media appearances and public criticisms of Trump, including labeling him a "threat to democracy" in October 2022, have intensified accusations of partisan bias undermining her objectivity.[39] These factors, combined with the committee's selective presentation of evidence favoring narratives of Trump inaction, have led skeptics to view her statements as potentially exaggerated for dramatic effect in a politically charged inquiry.[37]

Accusations of Political Opportunism and Disloyalty

Sarah Matthews faced criticism from Republican figures and conservative commentators for resigning from the Trump White House only after the January 6, 2021, Capitol riot, with detractors arguing the move was "too littleâ€”and four years too late" given her prior service in the administration.[24] This timing fueled accusations of selective disloyalty, as Matthews had publicly praised President Trump and Vice President Pence in a January 20, 2021, tweet thanking them for their service shortly after her resignation, yet proceeded to criticize Trump extensively in subsequent testimony and media appearances.[35] Critics, including voices in outlets like Talking Points Memo, portrayed such late exits by administration officials as opportunistic rather than principled, likening them to "scurrying of rats off a sinking ship" amid the administration's final days.[40][41] Her voluntary testimony before the House Select Committee on January 6 in 2022 drew sharp rebukes from House Republicans, who briefly labeled her "just another liar and pawn in Pelosiâ€™s witch-hunt" via an official tweet during the hearings, highlighting perceived betrayal by a former Trump aide still employed in Republican congressional roles at the time.[35][42] The tweet, which quoted her post-resignation praise for Trump before juxtaposing it with her committee statements on his inaction during the riot, was deleted after backlash, with the House GOP Conference attributing it to unauthorized staff action, but it underscored broader conservative claims of disloyalty against witnesses cooperating with what Republicans viewed as a partisan investigation. Some Republicans dismissed Matthews and similar ex-officials as "RINOs" (Republicans In Name Only), arguing their public opposition to Trump aided Democrats and reflected personal ambition over party fidelity.[43] Post-administration, Matthews' frequent media engagements on networks like MSNBC and CNN, where she warned of Trump's threat to democracy and critiqued Republican reluctance to oppose him, prompted accusations of political opportunism.[44] Critics contended these appearances capitalized on her insider status for visibility in left-leaning outlets, especially as she signed open letters from former Trump officials urging voters against his 2024 candidacy, moves seen by detractors as timed for career advancement in a media environment favoring anti-Trump narratives.[45] While Matthews maintained her positions stemmed from witnessing Trump's post-election conduct, conservative observers, including those in Republican leadership circles, framed her evolution from administration defender to vocal critic as emblematic of self-serving disloyalty, particularly given the lack of earlier public dissent during her tenure.[46]

Personal Life and Current Status

Residence and Personal Affiliations

Sarah Matthews was born on February 20, 1995, in North Canton, Ohio, where she grew up in Stark County. Her hometown remains North Canton, and she has described herself as a proud Ohioan. Following her education at Kent State University and early career steps in Ohio Republican politics, Matthews relocated to Washington, D.C., for roles including work on Capitol Hill and the Trump presidential campaign. She maintained a professional base in the capital during her White House tenure from 2020 to 2021. Post-administration, Matthews has continued to reside and work in Washington, D.C., engaging in congressional staffing and media commentary. Her family background includes parents active in local Republican politics, with her father involved in Stark County GOP activities. Matthews identifies with traditional Republican values but has affiliated with anti-Trump conservative groups, including appearances in campaigns by Republican Voters Against Trump.

Ongoing Political Involvement

Following her resignation from the White House on January 7, 2021, Matthews has engaged in ongoing political advocacy primarily through media commentary and public opposition to Donald Trump. She regularly appears as a commentator on CNN and programs such as The View, where she critiques Trump's post-presidency influence on the Republican Party and reiterates concerns over the events of January 6, 2021.[12] In these outlets, she has described experiencing "cognitive dissonance" while defending Trump's rhetoric during her tenure, positioning her current commentary as informed by firsthand observations.[9] In the 2024 election cycle, Matthews endorsed Vice President Kamala Harris on October 14, 2024, explicitly stating her intent to block Trump's return to the presidency, while maintaining her self-identification as a Republican.[47] [48] She participated in a Republican Voters Against Trump advertisement campaign launched on October 30, 2024, where she accused Trump of abandoning his oath of office during the January 6 Capitol riot, drawing on her experiences as deputy press secretary.[31] Matthews describes herself on social media as a "Republican in exile," reflecting her alienation from the contemporary GOP leadership while affirming her Ohio roots and prior Republican affiliations, including Capitol Hill staff roles.[8] This stance aligns with her post-White House activities, which lack formal partisan employment but emphasize public interventions against Trump-aligned policies and candidates. Earlier, from February 2021 onward, she briefly served as communications director for House Republicans on the Select Committee on the Climate Crisis, a role that overlapped with her January 6 committee testimony and drew internal GOP criticism for perceived disloyalty.[1][49]
