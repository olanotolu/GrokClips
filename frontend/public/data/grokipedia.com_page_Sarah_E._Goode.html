Sarah E. Goode

Sarah E. Goode (c. 1855–1905) was an American businesswoman and inventor who owned a furniture store in Chicago, Illinois, and developed a compact folding cabinet bed to address space limitations in urban dwellings.[1] Her design featured a desk or cabinet that unfolded into a bed with a mattress, targeted at customers in small apartments who lacked room for separate furniture pieces.[2] Goode, born Sarah Jacobs, relocated to Chicago following the American Civil War and married George Goode, with whom she operated a carpentry and furniture business catering to the city's growing population.[1] On July 14, 1885, she was granted U.S. Patent No. 322,177 for the "cabinet-bed," marking her as one of the earliest African American women to secure a patent from the United States Patent Office.[3] The invention reflected practical innovation driven by observed needs in her trade, predating modern Murphy beds and contributing to early solutions for multifunctional furniture.[4]

Early Life

Birth and Family Origins

Sarah Elisabeth Jacobs, who later became known as Sarah E. Goode, was born around 1855 in Toledo, Ohio, to parents Oliver Jacobs and Harriet Jacobs.[5][6] Her father, Oliver, worked as a carpenter, a trade that likely influenced her later familiarity with furniture construction.[5][7] The Jacobs family belonged to the free Black community in Ohio, with census records from 1860 listing them as free persons of color in Toledo.[6][7] Oliver Jacobs, born circa 1833, outlived Sarah and died in 1893.[7] While some accounts suggest her parents may have been formerly enslaved, primary records indicate the family resided as free individuals by the time of her birth, prior to the Civil War's end.[5][6]

Post-Emancipation Relocation and Upbringing

Sarah Elisabeth Jacobs, later known as Sarah E. Goode, was born in 1855 in Toledo, Ohio, to Oliver and Harriet Jacobs, the second of seven children in a family of freed individuals enumerated as mulattos in public records.[8] By 1860, at age five, she resided as a free person in Toledo, where her father worked variously as a waiter and carpenter.[1] Following emancipation via the Thirteenth Amendment in 1865—though her family had already achieved freedom status—the Jacobs family relocated to Chicago, Illinois, sometime prior to 1870, likely seeking expanded economic opportunities in the burgeoning post-Civil War urban center.[9] In Chicago, Oliver Jacobs established himself more firmly in carpentry, a trade that influenced his daughter's practical skills and entrepreneurial inclinations.[10] Goode's upbringing in Chicago emphasized self-reliance and vocational training over formal education, with records indicating limited schooling amid the era's constraints on African American access to institutions.[11] Her father directly taught her carpentry techniques, fostering an early aptitude for woodworking and furniture-related craftsmanship that later underpinned her business ventures.[12] By her late teens or early twenties, Goode had integrated into Chicago's African American community, marrying Archibald Goode, a stair builder and fellow tradesman, around 1870–1880; the couple resided in the city by the 1880 census, navigating a landscape of industrial growth and racial barriers.[1] This period honed her acumen in business operations, as she observed and participated in her father's work, preparing her for independent enterprise amid Chicago's competitive furniture market.[13]

Entrepreneurial Career

Establishment of the Furniture Business

Sarah E. Goode established a furniture business in Chicago after relocating there post-emancipation, partnering with her husband Archibald Goode, a carpenter whose skills in woodworking complemented the venture.[2][13] Historical records place the store's operation around 1885, aligning with the period of urban expansion and increasing demand for affordable home furnishings in the city.[11][1] The business, often referred to as serving customers in small urban dwellings, was situated at 513 State Street, a commercial area that facilitated access to a diverse clientele including those in nearby tenement housing.[9] This location enabled Goode to identify practical needs, such as space-saving solutions, directly from patrons facing cramped living conditions amid Chicago's rapid industrialization.[4] While the extent of the store's commercial success remains unverified in primary documents, its establishment marked Goode's entry into entrepreneurship as one of the few African American women proprietors in late 19th-century Chicago.[1]

Business Operations and Market Adaptation

Sarah E. Goode and her husband Archibald, a carpenter, established and operated a furniture store in Chicago during the 1880s, specializing in pieces suited for urban dwellers in cramped apartments.[8][2] The business addressed the practical challenges of tenement housing, where limited space made traditional furniture impractical for many customers, including working-class residents who required multifunctional items to maximize living areas.[4] Goode's operations involved retailing imported and locally crafted furniture while directly responding to client feedback on spatial constraints, such as difficulty accommodating full-sized beds.[8] This market insight prompted her to develop and patent the folding cabinet bed in 1885, a convertible design that transformed into a roll-top desk during the day, enabling the store to offer innovative solutions tailored to small-apartment lifestyles.[2][9] The store's adaptation strategy emphasized practicality over luxury, prioritizing compact, versatile products that aligned with the economic realities of Chicago's growing immigrant and labor populations in the post-Civil War era.[4] By integrating invention with sales—producing and marketing the patented bed through their enterprise—Goode demonstrated entrepreneurial responsiveness to urban market demands, filling a niche unmet by standard furnishings of the time.[13]

The Folding Cabinet Bed Invention

Design Concept and Practical Motivations

Sarah E. Goode's folding cabinet bed was conceived to resolve the spatial constraints prevalent in late 19th-century urban dwellings, where residents, particularly in cities like Chicago, struggled to fit traditional beds into compact apartments. Observing customer complaints at her furniture store about insufficient room for bulky furniture, Goode engineered a multifunctional unit that transformed from a roll-top desk into a sleeping platform.[4][11] The core design featured a wooden cabinet approximately 4 feet high, 3 feet wide, and 2 feet deep, equipped with hinged sections that unfolded to form a 6-foot-long bed frame supporting a mattress. When collapsed, it presented as a desk with pigeonholes for books, drawers for supplies, and a sloped writing surface, thereby reclaiming floor space for daytime activities. This configuration not only conserved area but also integrated storage, addressing the practical needs of small-space living without sacrificing functionality.[1][14] Motivations for the invention arose directly from the demands of urban migration and industrialization, which concentrated populations in high-density housing with limited square footage—often under 400 square feet per unit in working-class tenements. Goode's patent specification highlighted the device's purpose "to occupy less space" while serving multiple uses, a response tailored to her clientele's requirements for economical, versatile furnishings amid rising real estate costs and family sizes.[14][15]

Patent Application and Technical Specifications

Sarah E. Goode filed her patent application for the cabinet-bed on November 12, 1883, with the United States Patent Office.[16] The patent, numbered 322,177, was granted on July 14, 1885, marking her as the first African American woman to receive a U.S. patent.[16] [17] The invention comprises a sectional bedstead designed to fold compactly into a desk-like cabinet for space conservation in urban dwellings.[16] It features a central stationary rectangular section A, to which folding head section B and foot section C are hinged on opposite sides.[16] When unfolded, these sections align to form a full-length bed frame; when folded upward against A, they enclose a desk receptacle D integrated into section B, complete with pigeon-holes, drawers, and a sliding cylinder cover e for a roll-top appearance.[16] Key technical elements include a suspended woven-wire bedding support h attached to the ends of sections B and C, ensuring tautness when extended.[16] An automatic auxiliary support E, positioned centrally beneath the bed, deploys via coiled springs l to bolster the middle during use and retracts when folded.[16] Counterbalance springs f mounted on section A facilitate effortless raising and lowering of the folding sections, while toggle-joint braces o lock the assembly rigidly in the open position.[16] The patent claims emphasize the combination of the stationary and folding sections with the bedding support, auxiliary platform, and braces for operational integrity.[16] A secondary claim covers the desk configuration within the head section, incorporating the head-board a, end pieces c c, top board d, and cover e.[16] These specifications address practical needs for multifunctional furniture, enabling the device to serve as office furniture by day and sleeping quarters by night.[16]

Patents in Historical Context

Pre-Goode African American Women Inventors

Martha Jones of Amelia County, Virginia, received U.S. Patent No. 77,494 on May 5, 1868, for an "Improvement to the Corn Husker, Sheller," marking the earliest documented patent granted to an African American woman.[18][19] Her device enhanced existing corn-processing machinery by incorporating a rotating cylinder with flexible metal teeth to husk and shell corn more efficiently, addressing labor-intensive agricultural tasks in the post-Civil War South.[19] Little is known of Jones's personal background beyond her residence and the patent record, reflecting the limited documentation of Black inventors during Reconstruction.[18] Mary Jones, residing in Baltimore, Maryland, obtained U.S. Patent No. 140,253 on August 26, 1873, for a "Cooking Apparatus" designed to simplify meal preparation.[20] The invention featured a steam-based table that allowed multiple dishes to cook simultaneously over a single heat source, improving efficiency for household and small-scale commercial use.[20] This patent preceded widespread adoption of modern kitchen appliances, demonstrating practical ingenuity amid economic constraints faced by freedwomen.[21] Judy W. Reed of Washington, D.C., was awarded U.S. Patent No. 305,474 on September 23, 1884, for a "Dough Kneader and Roller" that automated bread-making processes.[22] The device combined kneading and rolling functions into a single wooden apparatus with adjustable rollers, reducing manual effort for bakers and homemakers.[19] Reed, who signed her application with an "X" indicating illiteracy, highlighted the barriers of education and access yet pursued formal protection for her innovation.[22] These pre-1885 patents illustrate rare instances of African American women navigating the U.S. patent system despite systemic discrimination, though comprehensive records remain scarce due to historical underreporting.[21]

Systemic Obstacles and Individual Agency

In the post-Civil War United States, African American women inventors faced compounded systemic barriers from racial segregation and gender discrimination, which limited access to formal education and technical training. Universities and technical institutions largely excluded women of color, perpetuating a cycle of denied opportunities for skill development essential to invention and patenting.[14] Economic disenfranchisement further hindered progress, as Black families often lacked capital for prototyping, legal fees, or market entry, while Jim Crow laws entrenched social and professional isolation.[23] Patent examiners and industry gatekeepers exhibited implicit biases, with historical records showing Black applicants scrutinized more rigorously despite legal equality post-1865.[24] Gender-specific obstacles amplified these challenges, as societal norms restricted women to unpaid domestic labor, denying them independent property rights in many jurisdictions and exposure to male-dominated trades like carpentry or mechanics. Between 1865 and the early 20th century, fewer than five patents were granted to African American women, reflecting not innate deficiency but structural exclusion from networks, funding, and recognition.[21] Sarah E. Goode demonstrated individual agency by leveraging relocation and self-directed observation to circumvent these constraints. Freed from slavery around 1855, she moved to Chicago by the 1870s, co-founding a furniture store with her husband that adapted to urban customers' spatial limitations in tenement apartments.[11] Drawing from direct business insights rather than formal education, Goode conceived and patented her folding cabinet bed in 1885 (U.S. Patent No. 322,177), transforming customer feedback into a multifunctional design that concealed the bed as a desk to maximize room utility.[15] This achievement, amid pervasive prejudice, underscored her strategic persistence: securing the patent through personal initiative without institutional backing, thereby establishing a rare precedent for Black female entrepreneurship in a hostile era.[25]

Later Life

Family Expansion and Community Role

Sarah E. Goode married Archibald Goode, a skilled carpenter and stair builder originally from Wise County, Virginia, by the time of the 1880 U.S. Census.[8][26] The couple resided in Chicago, where they raised a family amid Goode's ongoing furniture business operations. They had six children, although only three reached adulthood, reflecting high infant and child mortality rates common in late 19th-century urban African American households.[8][26] Known surviving children included daughters Estella V. "Stella" Goode Brown and Hattie Goode Conley.[7] In her later years, Goode balanced family responsibilities with her entrepreneurial pursuits, maintaining the furniture store that served Chicago's working-class residents, including many in the growing Black community on the city's South Side.[8] As one of the few African American women business owners during an era of systemic racial and gender barriers, her household exemplified self-reliance and economic contribution to local stability, though specific records of formal community leadership roles, such as church or civic organizations, remain undocumented in primary historical accounts.[26] Her family's integration into Chicago's African American networks likely amplified her influence as a model of perseverance for peers facing similar obstacles.

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Sarah E. Goode died on April 8, 1905, in Chicago, Illinois, at approximately 50 years of age.[1][13] She was interred at Graceland Cemetery in Chicago.[7] Historical records offer limited insight into the immediate aftermath of her passing. Her husband, Archibald Goode, a carpenter and upholsterer who had co-operated the family's furniture store at 513 State Street, outlived her, as did their daughters, including Estella, Inza, and Harriet.[27][28] The status of the business, known as Goode & Company and focused on custom furniture including patented cabinet beds, remains undocumented beyond her lifetime, with no verified accounts of its continuation or closure.[13][12] This scarcity of detail reflects broader challenges in tracing post-mortem trajectories for African American entrepreneurs of the era, where primary sources such as business ledgers or obituaries are often absent or incomplete.[13]

Enduring Impact

Influence on Modern Furniture Innovation

Goode's folding cabinet bed, patented on July 14, 1885 (U.S. Patent No. 322,177), anticipated key principles of modern space-saving furniture by integrating a full-size bed into a compact, multifunctional cabinet that doubled as a writing desk with compartments for supplies.[4] This design addressed the spatial constraints of urban apartments in late 19th-century Chicago, where customers of her furniture business sought versatile pieces to maximize limited room without sacrificing utility.[29] The mechanism allowed the bed frame to pivot downward from the cabinet's interior, supported by slats and a drop-leaf table, enabling seamless transitions between sleeping and daytime functions— a concept that prefigured the emphasis on adaptability in contemporary compact living solutions.[30] Her innovation is widely recognized as a precursor to the Murphy bed, patented by William Lawrence Murphy around 1911, which similarly folds a bed into a wall-mounted cabinet to reclaim floor space, though without the integrated desk feature.[4] [30] While no direct lineage exists between Goode's patent and Murphy's commercialization—driven independently by early 20th-century urban density in San Francisco—the shared focus on concealable bedding influenced the evolution of wall beds and convertible units, which became staples in studios and efficiency apartments by the mid-1900s.[29] Goode's emphasis on practical, customer-driven multifunctionality underscored causal factors like rising apartment living, paralleling how today's designs respond to similar pressures from urbanization and micro-housing trends. In broader terms, Goode's work contributed to the conceptual foundation for modern multifunctional furniture, inspiring innovations in modular systems and transformer pieces that prioritize efficiency over dedicated single-use items.[4] Examples include contemporary cabinet beds from manufacturers that echo her roll-top desk integration for home offices in small spaces, as well as sofa-bed hybrids that fold out for sleeping while serving as seating or storage.[30] These developments reflect enduring empirical demands for furniture that mitigates spatial limitations, with Goode's patent exemplifying early agency in applying mechanical simplicity to real-world constraints, though commercial scalability awaited later industrial advances.[29]

Scholarly and Cultural Recognition

Sarah E. Goode's contributions have garnered recognition in historical analyses of early African American and female inventors, particularly for her role in patenting practical furniture amid post-emancipation urban challenges. She received U.S. Patent No. 322,177 on July 14, 1885, positioning her as the second documented African American woman to secure such a grant, following Judy W. Reed's 1884 patent for a dough kneading device.[31] Popular accounts often erroneously credit Goode as the first, overlooking Reed's prior achievement, which underscores inconsistencies in historical documentation of minority inventors.[9] Scholarly discussions emphasize Goode's design as a precursor to modern space-saving furniture, addressing cramped Chicago apartments for working-class residents. A 2017 Smithsonian Magazine profile includes her among four Black women who advanced domestic technology, noting how her cabinet bed transformed multifunctional living spaces in industrial-era cities.[14] Educational materials from the National Archives feature her patent to highlight African American innovation after the Civil War, illustrating barriers like limited access to formal education and capital that she overcame through self-taught entrepreneurship.[2] Culturally, Goode is commemorated in library exhibits and archival resources focused on legal history and women's achievements, such as the University of Chicago Library's series on patent pioneers.[9] She appears in National Archives blogs during Black History Month, underscoring her as an example of individual agency in invention despite systemic exclusion from professional networks.[32] No evidence exists of formal awards, hall of fame inductions, or dedicated monuments, consistent with the era's underdocumentation of non-white, non-male patentees, though retrospective analyses affirm her influence on convertible furniture precedents like the Murphy bed.
