Hakan Şükür

Hakan Şükür (born 1 September 1971) is a Turkish former professional footballer who excelled as a centre-forward, most notably with Galatasaray S.K., where he established himself as the Süper Lig's all-time leading scorer, and with the Turkey national team, which he captained and for which he remains the top goalscorer with 51 goals in 112 appearances.[1][2] During his career, Şükür achieved prominence by scoring the fastest goal in FIFA World Cup history—11 seconds into Turkey's third-place match against South Korea in 2002—and contributed to Galatasaray's UEFA Cup victory in 2000, alongside securing eight Turkish league titles, six Turkish Cups, and four Süper Lig top scorer awards.[3][4] Following his retirement in 2008, Şükür transitioned to politics, winning election as a Member of Parliament for Istanbul representing the Justice and Development Party (AKP) in 2011 before resigning in 2013 amid family-linked corruption probes; he later expressed support for Fethullah Gülen, prompting his flight to the United States in 2016 after the failed coup attempt, where Turkish authorities have sought his extradition on charges of Gülen movement affiliation—designated by Ankara as terrorist-linked—though Şükür denies coup involvement and has resided in exile in California, working odd jobs including as a taxi driver.[5][6][7][8]

Early life

Upbringing and family influences

Hakan Şükür was born on 1 September 1971 in Sapanca, Sakarya Province, Turkey.[9] He grew up in the Sakarya region, where he developed an early interest in football through local opportunities. Şükür's family has Albanian roots, with both parents originating as immigrants from former Yugoslavia.[10] His father, Selmet Şükür, was born in Pristina, and his mother in Skopje.[11] While specific familial encouragement toward football is not extensively documented, Şükür has publicly identified with his Albanian heritage, stating in response to queries on ethnic identity, "I am Albanian." From around age 16, Şükür began playing for hometown club Sakaryaspor, marking the start of his structured involvement in the sport amid a modest provincial upbringing.[9] This local foundation propelled his rapid progression, as he impressed scouts and debuted professionally shortly after turning 17 in 1988.[12]

Initial involvement in football

Şükür began his organized football involvement through the youth system of Sakaryaspor, the club based in his home province of Sakarya.[13] Transitioning to senior professional play with the same team in 1987 at age 16, he competed in the Süper Lig during the 1987–1990 period.[9] [12] A key early milestone occurred on 26 February 1989, when Şükür substituted into a Süper Lig match against Eskişehirspor, contributing a goal in the 79th minute to secure a 2–2 draw for Sakaryaspor.[14] [13] This performance highlighted his emerging talent as a forward, building on prior impressions from youth and initial senior outings.[9]

Club career

Early professional years in Turkey

Şükür began his professional football career with hometown club Sakaryaspor, where he transitioned from the youth ranks and made his senior debut around age 17 in late 1988. In his initial professional stint, he recorded 19 goals across 41 matches, aiding the team's victory in the Turkish Cup during the 1987–88 season.[3] In August 1990, Şükür joined Süper Lig side Bursaspor on a domestic transfer, remaining with the club through May 1992. During this period, he featured in over 50 appearances, netting 11 goals, and contributed to winning the Prime Minister's Cup in 1992. His performances at Bursaspor, including consistent scoring output, established him as a promising striker and led to his first national team call-up in March 1992.[15][9] Şükür transferred to Galatasaray in June 1992 via another domestic move, marking the start of his association with the Istanbul giants. In his debut 1992–93 season, he quickly adapted to the higher competitive level, scoring prolifically and helping secure the Süper Lig title the following year in 1993–94 alongside the Turkish Cup. His early contributions at Galatasaray, totaling dozens of goals in the initial campaigns before departing for Italy in 1995, solidified his reputation as Turkey's leading goalscorer of the era.[15][9]

Spells in Italy and England

Şükür's first experience abroad came with a loan move to Torino from Galatasaray in the summer of 1995. He made 5 appearances in Serie A during the 1995–96 season, scoring 1 goal, but struggled with homesickness and returned to Turkey after just a few months.[16] In July 2000, Şükür transferred permanently to Internazionale for a reported €12.5 million, becoming one of the most expensive Turkish players at the time. Over two seasons (2000–02), he appeared in 24 Serie A matches for Inter, scoring 5 goals, but faced challenges adapting to the tactical demands of Italian football and limited playing time under managers like Marco Tardelli and Héctor Cúper.[12][2] Loaned to Parma for the 2001–02 Serie A campaign, Şükür featured in 15 league games, netting 3 goals, yet the stint failed to reignite his form amid ongoing adaptation issues and competition for places. He returned to Galatasaray in 2002 after Inter terminated his contract. On 9 December 2002, following his release from Inter, Şükür signed with Blackburn Rovers in the English Premier League on a free transfer for the second half of the 2002–03 season, managed by Graeme Souness. He played 9 league matches, scoring 2 goals and providing 1 assist, including strikes against Everton and Fulham, but a broken leg sustained in training sidelined him briefly and contributed to a modest overall impact before departing at season's end.

Return and final years at Galatasaray

Şükür rejoined Galatasaray on 8 July 2003, marking his third stint with the club following an injury-plagued and goalless loan at Blackburn Rovers, where he appeared in just five matches during the 2002–03 season.[17][12] The move allowed him to resume his role as a central figure in the team's attack, leveraging his aerial prowess and finishing ability honed over prior successful periods with the club. In his initial season back (2003–04), Şükür scored 12 league goals, helping stabilize Galatasaray's forward line amid domestic competition, though the team finished second in the Süper Lig behind Fenerbahçe.[18] Over the subsequent years, he contributed to two Süper Lig titles, scoring 10 goals in the 2005–06 championship-winning campaign and adding further strikes in the 2007–08 season, which culminated in another league triumph.[18][13] Across his final tenure from 2003 to 2008, he amassed 55 goals in 146 appearances, securing three trophies including the two league titles and reinforcing his status as the club's all-time leading scorer with a career total of 288 goals for Galatasaray.[12][19] Aging into his mid-30s, Şükür's role evolved toward mentorship for younger forwards while maintaining productivity, though injuries and competition limited his starts in later years. He announced his retirement from professional football in 2008 at age 36, shortly after the 2007–08 Süper Lig victory, his last competitive match occurring on 17 October 2007 in a 1–0 defeat to Greece for the national team, though club play extended into the domestic season.[13][12] This period underscored his enduring loyalty to Galatasaray, where he won eight Turkish championships overall, but also highlighted the physical toll of a 21-year career spanning over 540 club matches and 258 goals.[19]

International career

National team debut and key tournaments

Şükür earned his first cap for the Turkey national football team on 25 March 1992, during a 2–0 friendly victory over Luxembourg in Istanbul, managed by Sepp Piontek.[20] He netted his debut international goal in the subsequent match, a 1–1 friendly draw against Denmark on 29 April 1992 in Copenhagen.[1] Over his international tenure spanning 1992 to 2007, Şükür amassed 112 appearances and 51 goals, establishing himself as Turkey's all-time leading scorer.[2] Şükür played a pivotal role in Turkey's qualification campaign for UEFA Euro 1996, their first major tournament appearance in over four decades, by scoring multiple goals in the group stage matches, including efforts against Iceland and Hungary. In the finals held in England, Turkey competed in Group D alongside Croatia and Denmark. Şükür featured in both group fixtures: a 0–1 defeat to Croatia on 5 June 1996 at Hillsborough Stadium, where he started but failed to score amid limited attacking opportunities, and a 0–3 loss to Denmark on 19 June 1996 at St James' Park, exiting as a halftime substitute for Hami Mandıralı without contributing to the scoreline.[21] Turkey garnered zero points and were eliminated in the group stage, with Şükür's tournament output limited to no goals despite his prior qualifying form. Subsequent years saw Şükür maintain his scoring prowess in World Cup qualifiers and friendlies, including eight goals during the successful 2002 campaign that secured Turkey's qualification, though major tournament participation remained sparse until that point. His aerial prowess and positioning proved instrumental in Turkey's evolving national team strategy under coaches like Fatih Terim, who began integrating him prominently from the mid-1990s onward.[22]

World Cup 2002 and record achievements

Şükür played a pivotal role in Turkey's historic run to third place at the 2002 FIFA World Cup in South Korea and Japan, the nation's best finish in the tournament's history.[23] Entering the event with 36 goals in 73 international appearances, he featured in all of Turkey's matches, including the group stage draws against Denmark and South Korea, and the round-of-16 victory over Japan.[24] Turkey advanced to the semi-finals, where they lost 1-0 to Brazil on 26 June, before facing co-host South Korea in the third-place playoff.[25] In that 29 June 2002 match at Daegu, Şükür scored the fastest goal in World Cup finals history, heading in a cross from teammate Ümit Davala after just 11 seconds to give Turkey a 1-0 lead.[24] [26] Turkey ultimately won 3-2, with İlhan Mansız adding two more goals, securing the bronze medal and national acclaim.[27] This strike not only highlighted Şükür's clinical finishing but also symbolized Turkey's breakthrough on the global stage under coach Şenol Güneş. Beyond the tournament, Şükür holds the record as Turkey's all-time leading international goalscorer with 51 goals in 112 caps, achieved between 1992 and 2007.[28] [3] His World Cup performance contributed to his legacy as the "golden boy" of Turkish football, cementing records that underscore his enduring impact on the national team.[24]

Post-retirement transition

Business interests and media roles

Following his retirement from professional football in 2008, Hakan Şükür entered media as a football pundit, initially working for the Turkish state broadcaster TRT (Turkish Radio and Television Corporation).[29] In this role, he provided expert analysis on matches and Turkish Süper Lig developments, leveraging his extensive playing experience.[30] Şükür continued media work after entering politics in 2011, joining Lig TV (now beIN Sports Turkey) as a pundit in January 2012 while serving as an Istanbul MP for the AKP.[31] His commentary focused on domestic league games and tactical insights, though his tenure ended amid shifting political alignments.[32] Business interests during this period were not publicly detailed beyond general investments and properties in Turkey, which Şükür accumulated from his football earnings.[33] These assets, including houses and unspecified businesses, were seized by Turkish authorities after his 2016 exile, leaving him with no remaining holdings in the country.[34] In the United States, following his departure from Turkey, Şükür invested as a part-owner in Tuts Bakery and Cafe in Palo Alto, California, starting in 2016, though he later stepped away due to security concerns.[35]

Political involvement

Entry into AKP and parliamentary service

Şükür transitioned into politics following his football retirement, announcing his candidacy for the Justice and Development Party (AKP) in March 2011 ahead of the general elections.[36] He was elected as a Member of Parliament representing Istanbul in the June 12, 2011, elections, securing a seat in the Grand National Assembly as part of the AKP's landslide victory that formed the 24th Parliament.[5][37] In his parliamentary role, Şükür focused on interparliamentary relations, being elected in December 2011 as chairman of the Turkey-Italy Interparliamentary Friendship Group, leveraging his prior professional experience in Italian football.[38] His service aligned with the AKP's conservative platform, reflecting his public persona as a devout Muslim and national sports icon supportive of the party's emphasis on religious values and national pride.[30] During this period, he maintained visibility through media appearances, often bridging his athletic fame with political advocacy for youth sports development and cultural ties.[39]

Resignation amid corruption probes

Şükür, serving as an AKP member of parliament for Istanbul since the June 2011 general election, resigned from the party on December 16, 2013.[40] In his public statement, he attributed the decision primarily to the AKP government's increasingly adversarial stance toward the Gülen movement, particularly its push to shutter the movement's network of private exam preparation schools known as dershanes, which Şükür viewed as an overreach against educational institutions aligned with Islamic scholar Fethullah Gülen.[41] This resignation positioned him in open support of the Gülenists amid escalating tensions between the AKP leadership and the movement, which had previously been a key ally in Erdoğan's rise to power.[42] The timing coincided directly with the eruption of Turkey's 2013 corruption scandal, as raids and detentions began on December 17, 2013, targeting sons of three cabinet ministers, prominent businessmen, and officials accused of bribery, money laundering, and gold smuggling linked to sanctions evasion for Iran.[43] Prosecutors, later purged by the government as Gülenist infiltrators, uncovered evidence including $4.5 million in shoe boxes at a minister's home and allegations of kickbacks in public tenders.[44] Prime Minister Erdoğan dismissed the probes as a "judicial coup" orchestrated by Gülen's followers within the police and judiciary to undermine his administration, vowing purges that eventually dismantled the parallel structures.[45] Şükür, himself a longtime Gülen adherent, faced immediate pressure from party colleagues to vacate his parliamentary seat alongside the party resignation, though he initially retained it as an independent.[6] Şükür's departure highlighted fractures within the AKP, as other Gülen-linked figures distanced themselves amid the scandal's fallout, which led to the resignation of 10 ministers and calls for Erdoğan's impeachment.[46] While Şükür was not personally implicated in the corruption allegations, his alignment with the Gülen side drew accusations from AKP hardliners of disloyalty, foreshadowing his later marginalization following the movement's designation as a terrorist organization after the 2016 coup attempt.[39] The government's narrative framed such resignations as evidence of Gülenist sabotage, contrasting with Gülenist outlets' portrayal of the probes as legitimate exposures of systemic graft.[45]

Association with the Gülen movement

Historical context of Gülenist-AKP alliance

The Gülen movement, founded by Fethullah Gülen in the late 1960s as a network of religious study groups emphasizing education, interfaith dialogue, and moderate Islamism, initially maintained distance from formal politics while building influence through schools, media outlets, and business associations in Turkey.[47] By the 1990s, amid tensions with Turkey's secular Kemalist establishment, Gülen's followers—known as Hizmet or cemaat—began cultivating ties with emerging conservative forces, setting the stage for later alignments.[48] Gülen himself relocated to the United States in 1999 following investigations by secular authorities, but his movement retained significant domestic sway through informal networks in civil society and state-adjacent institutions.[49] The Justice and Development Party (AKP), established in August 2001 by Recep Tayyip Erdoğan and former Islamists from the banned Welfare Party, rose to power in the November 2002 general elections, securing 34.3% of the vote and forming a government amid economic crisis and disillusionment with secular parties.[50] An unofficial alliance between the AKP and Gülen movement coalesced shortly thereafter, driven by mutual opposition to the military-secular elite that had dominated Turkish politics since Mustafa Kemal Atatürk's founding of the republic in 1923.[51] Both groups shared conservative Islamic values and sought to curtail the Turkish Armed Forces' role as guardians of secularism, exemplified by past interventions like the 1980 coup; the AKP pursued EU accession reforms to weaken military tutelage, while Gülenists advocated educational and moral reforms aligned with these goals.[47] This partnership manifested in electoral and institutional support: Gülen-affiliated media, including Zaman newspaper and Samanyolu TV, endorsed the AKP in the 2002 and subsequent elections, amplifying Erdoğan's narrative against Kemalist "deep state" elements.[48] Gülen followers, leveraging influence in the police and judiciary—gained through AKP-era appointments and promotions—collaborated on high-profile prosecutions, such as the 2007-2011 Ergenekon trials targeting alleged secularist conspirators in the military and bureaucracy, which neutralized hundreds of opponents and consolidated AKP control.[50][51] The alliance enabled the Gülen movement to expand its "parallel state" presence, with thousands of adherents entering public service via competitive exams and loyalty networks, while providing the AKP a grassroots base beyond its urban Islamist core.[47] This cooperation peaked in the late 2000s, fostering Turkey's brief "conservative democratic" era, though underlying tensions over power-sharing and ideological nuances—Gülen's globalist moderation versus AKP's assertive nationalism—foreshadowed eventual rupture.[48]

Şükür's public endorsements and activities

Şükür publicly expressed admiration for Fethullah Gülen's teachings on education and interfaith dialogue during the period of alliance between the AKP and the Gülen movement in the early 2010s.[52] Following the 2013 corruption probes that fractured this alliance, he resigned from the AKP on December 16, 2013, criticizing the government's handling of the investigations in a manner aligning with Gülen-affiliated media narratives.[52] In the June 7, 2015, general elections, Şükür ran as an independent parliamentary candidate from Istanbul's 1st electoral district, with reported links to Gülen movement networks, though he did not secure a seat.[37] Turkish prosecutors have referenced his attendance at Gülenist gatherings and social media posts praising the movement's charitable works as indicators of endorsement, amid broader accusations of organizational membership post-2016 coup attempt.[7] Şükür has maintained that his support was limited to Gülen's pre-2013 religious and educational initiatives, denying any involvement in political infiltration or illicit activities attributed to the group by authorities.[7]

2016 coup attempt and allegations

Turkish government accusations of FETÖ membership

Following the failed coup attempt on July 15, 2016, which the Turkish government attributed to the Gülen movement, authorities launched widespread investigations into alleged supporters of the U.S.-based cleric Fethullah Gülen. On August 12, 2016, prosecutors in Sakarya province and Istanbul issued an arrest warrant for Hakan Şükür, charging him with membership in the Fethullah Terrorist Organization (FETÖ), the official designation for the Gülen network, described by Ankara as an armed terrorist group.[7][39][53] The accusations framed Şükür's prior public affiliations with Gülen-affiliated institutions, including schools and media outlets, as evidence of organizational ties, positioning him among hundreds of former AKP members and public figures targeted in the post-coup purges. Turkish officials, including Justice Minister Bekir Bozdağ, publicly linked such figures to FETÖ's purported infiltration of state institutions, with Şükür specifically accused of providing financial support to the group.[54][55] In parallel, on August 13, 2016, a Turkish court ordered the seizure of properties belonging to Şükür and his father, Sermet Şükür, as part of measures against FETÖ-linked assets, reflecting the government's broader policy of economic sanctions against designated members. These actions were justified by authorities as necessary to dismantle what they described as a parallel state structure threatening national security.[56][57]

Evidence cited by authorities versus Şükür's denials

Turkish authorities, following the July 15, 2016, coup attempt, issued an arrest warrant for Şükür on August 12, 2016, charging him with membership in an armed terrorist organization, specifically the Fethullahist Terrorist Organization (FETÖ), designated by the government as the coup's orchestrator under Fethullah Gülen's direction.[7] [58] Prosecutors cited Şükür's documented visits to Gülen in Pennsylvania on multiple occasions during his tenure as an AKP parliamentarian (2011–2013), including after his December 2013 resignation amid graft probes attributed by the government to Gülenist infiltration of institutions.[59] Additional evidence included public endorsements of Gülen's teachings, such as Şükür's statements praising the cleric's moral guidance and the Hizmet movement's educational initiatives, as well as allegations of financial support to Gülen-affiliated entities through business ties post-retirement.[37] House searches in western Turkey yielded materials purportedly linking him to FETÖ networks, though specifics remain classified in public indictments.[7] Şükür has consistently denied FETÖ membership or any role in terrorist activities, asserting in interviews that his affinity for Gülen stemmed from religious inspiration rather than organizational allegiance or political conspiracy.[7] He maintained that visits to Gülen were personal and infrequent, not indicative of operative involvement, and rejected coup orchestration claims, noting Gülen's own denials and lack of direct evidence tying him to the July 2016 events.[60] Şükür described the charges as retaliatory for his criticisms of the AKP government's post-2013 policies against the Hizmet movement, including school closures and media restrictions, which he viewed as suppressing dissent rather than countering terrorism.[61] Supporting the government's case, Şükür's father, Sermet Şükür, received a seven-year sentence in January 2021 for aiding FETÖ, based partly on testimony from a secret witness alleging familial coordination of Gülenist activities, though defense lawyers contested the absence of concrete forensic or documentary proof beyond associational claims.[62] Critics of the proceedings, including international observers, have highlighted reliance on anonymous witnesses and circumstantial links—such as school enrollments or event attendance—as hallmarks of broader post-coup purges, potentially inflating FETÖ designations without individualized evidence of violence or plotting. Şükür reiterated his innocence in a 2022 broadcast appearance, refusing extradition and framing the pursuit as politically driven exile rather than justified prosecution.[63]

Prosecution, asset seizures, and exile

Legal actions in Turkey and family consequences

In the aftermath of the July 2016 coup attempt, Turkish prosecutors in Sakarya province issued an arrest warrant for Şükür on August 12, 2016, charging him with membership in an armed terrorist organization, a reference to the Gülen movement labeled FETÖ by authorities.[45][58] On the same date, a local court ordered the seizure of properties belonging to both Şükür and his father, Selmet Şükür, as part of measures targeting alleged FETÖ affiliates.[56][64] Şükür's father, Selmet Şükür, was detained in Sakarya on August 12, 2016, in connection with the same probe into Gülenist activities.[62] He was released on November 25, 2016, under judicial control, which included a travel ban.[62] In January 2021, a Turkish court sentenced Selmet Şükür to three years and 45 days in prison for aiding a terrorist organization, based on evidence of his alleged links to the Gülen movement.[62] Separately, Şükür was tried in absentia starting in June 2016 on charges of insulting President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan through social media posts criticizing government policies, though this predated the coup-related warrant and stemmed from earlier political tensions.[58] These proceedings reflect broader patterns of familial repercussions in Turkey's post-coup crackdown, where relatives of designated FETÖ members have faced detention and asset measures irrespective of direct involvement.[65]

Flight to the United States

Şükür departed Turkey for the United States in 2015 amid escalating legal pressures stemming from his public criticisms of President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan and perceived affiliations with the Gülen movement. In February 2015, he tweeted an accusation of theft against an unnamed figure—interpreted by authorities and media as referring to Erdoğan in relation to prior corruption scandals—prompting a criminal investigation for insulting the president under Article 299 of the Turkish Penal Code.[66] Prosecutors sought a prison term of up to four years, intensifying scrutiny on Şükür, who had already resigned from the AKP in 2013 following the party's rift with Gülenists.[58] Facing imminent arrest risks and asset freezes linked to Gülen-related probes, Şükür and his family left the country discreetly, relocating to California.[35] Turkish media reported his lawyer confirming the move in June 2016, ending speculation about his whereabouts, though the departure occurred earlier to evade enforcement actions.[33] This exile preceded the July 2016 coup attempt, after which an arrest warrant was issued in August 2016 for alleged FETÖ membership, further solidifying his status as a fugitive from Turkish authorities.[58] Şükür has denied terrorism links, attributing his flight to political persecution rather than criminality.[8]

Life in the United States

Adaptation and economic activities

Following his arrival in the United States in mid-2015 on an investor EB-5 visa, Şükür settled in Palo Alto, California, where he adapted to life in exile by investing in local business ventures amid frozen assets and severed ties to Turkey.[35] He became a part-owner of Tuts Bakery and Cafe, a Mediterranean-inspired establishment that opened in 2016 near Stanford University, serving as a modest hub for his entrepreneurial efforts and community interactions.[35][8] The cafe initially provided a stable economic foothold, with Şükür involved in daily operations such as clearing tables and engaging customers, often unrecognized despite his past fame, reflecting a deliberate low-profile adaptation to American suburban life.[35] However, by 2020, facing financial pressures from his exile—including the loss of prior wealth—Şükür supplemented income by driving for Uber, relocating activities to the Washington, D.C., area while continuing to sell books related to his experiences and Turkish politics.[33][34] These gig economy roles underscored his pragmatic shift from athletic stardom to service-oriented work, prioritizing self-sufficiency over public visibility.[33] Şükür's economic adaptation has been marked by resilience, as he rebuilt from limited resources without relying on Turkish networks, though Turkish government-aligned reports have portrayed his ventures—such as the cafe—as informal gatherings for Gülen sympathizers, a claim unverified by independent U.S. sources and contested by Şükür's emphasis on legitimate business.[67] Overall, his U.S. pursuits highlight a transition to entrepreneurial and freelance sustainability, informed by visa-enabled investment and adaptive labor in a new cultural context.[8]

Ongoing criticisms of the Turkish government

From his self-imposed exile in the United States since 2016, Hakan Şükür has maintained a public stance critical of the Turkish government under President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, accusing it of authoritarian overreach, arbitrary asset seizures, and suppression of dissent linked to the Gülen movement.[8][30] In a 2020 interview, Şükür stated, "I have nothing left anywhere in the world. Erdogan took everything from me. My right to freedom, the right to explain myself, to express myself," referring to the confiscation of his properties, businesses, and bank accounts following his criticism of government policies.[68][69] Şükür has specifically alleged that Erdoğan personally sought his extradition from the U.S. as a bargaining chip in 2018 negotiations for the release of American pastor Andrew Brunson, detained on terrorism charges; he rejected any cooperation, framing it as an extortionate demand unsupported by evidence of criminality.[70] He has refused overtures from AKP officials to publicly disavow Fethullah Gülen in exchange for permission to return to Turkey, insisting such denunciations would betray his principles and lack factual basis for the government's FETÖ designations.[8] In more recent commentary, including a September 2024 interview, Şükür likened his treatment by the Turkish state to that of "a good version of Voldemort—he who shall not be named," highlighting systematic efforts to erase his public legacy, such as removing his name from stadiums, excluding him from official records, and pressuring media outlets to avoid mentioning him.[8] He has used social media to decry instances of censorship, such as the 2022 removal of a broadcaster from air for referencing him during a discussion of Erdoğan's past associates, portraying these as emblematic of broader regime intolerance for opposition voices.[71] Şükür maintains that his critiques stem from firsthand experience of post-2013 purges, which he views as politically motivated rather than justified responses to the 2016 coup attempt, though he has not been charged with direct involvement in the event itself.[72][55]

Personal life

Family dynamics and religious beliefs

Hakan Şükür's family originates from Albanian roots, with his parents having migrated from regions associated with Bulgarian territories prior to settling in Turkey. His father, Selmet Şükür, faced imprisonment in 2021 on charges linked to alleged affiliations with the Gülen movement, a network prosecutors in Turkey designate as the Fethullahist Terrorist Organization (FETÖ); Selmet received a sentence but was later reported under house arrest alongside Şükür's mother.[62][73] These legal repercussions extended to family assets, including attacks on his wife's business and harassment of his children, prompting Şükür to prioritize family protection amid escalating pressures post-2016.[74] Şükür's first marriage to Esra Elbirlik occurred in 1995 but ended in divorce after four months; Elbirlik and her family perished in the 1999 İzmit earthquake. He married Beyda Sertbaş in 1999, with whom he has three children; the family relocated to the United States together prior to the July 2016 coup attempt, anticipating risks tied to his public profile.[35] Şükür has described decisions during exile as driven by the need to sustain his wife and children, emphasizing collective resilience in adapting to economic challenges abroad.[8] Şükür identifies as a devout Sunni Muslim, publicly expressing longstanding affinity for the Hizmet movement inspired by Islamic scholar Fethullah Gülen, stating in 2013 that he had "known and loved" it for over two decades due to its emphasis on education and community service.[75] Turkish authorities, however, classify his ties—including support for Gülen-linked institutions—as evidence of FETÖ involvement, a designation Şükür rejects as politically motivated, while Gülen-affiliated outlets portray the movement as a moderate Islamic network focused on dialogue rather than extremism.[7][53] His wife Beyda adopted head covering around 2013, aligning with conservative Islamic practices observed in their household.[76]

Philanthropy and public persona

Şükür has supported humanitarian efforts through donations and public endorsements of aid organizations. In November 2014, while serving as an independent member of parliament, he announced the donation of his salary until the June 2015 elections to families impacted by the Ermenek coal mine disaster, channeling the funds via the Kimse Yok Mu association, which conducted relief operations but was later closed by Turkish authorities for alleged ties to the Gülen movement.[77] [78] He also expressed gratitude to Kimse Yok Mu for its assistance in the 2011 Van earthquake recovery and famine relief in Somalia during a 2012 event.[79] Şükür participated in the United Nations Development Programme's Match Against Poverty on March 4, 2014, in Bern, Switzerland, scoring a goal in a match featuring international football stars to raise funds for Philippines recovery after Typhoon Haiyan, which killed over 5,000 people.[80] [81] Once revered in Turkey as the "Bull of the Bosphorus" for his record 51 goals in 112 national team appearances and iconic 2002 World Cup performance—including the fastest goal in tournament history at 10.8 seconds—Şükür's public image shifted after his 2013 resignation from the AKP amid disputes over Gülen movement school closures.[30] His reputation, previously transcending club rivalries as Galatasaray's all-time top scorer, deteriorated into effective erasure from Turkish public life, with his name omitted from Galatasaray's museum exhibits, stadium tributes, and media discussions following accusations of FETÖ affiliation.[8] In the United States since 2016, Şükür has cultivated a persona of resilience and principle, coaching youth soccer, briefly owning a café near Stanford University before selling it amid reported harassment, and driving for Uber to adapt while learning English.[8] He hosts YouTube streams analyzing Turkish football, attracting thousands of viewers, and has publicly denied coup involvement, framing his exile as a stand against authoritarianism rather than self-interest, stating he prioritizes broader rights over personal security.[82] [8] Turkish pro-government outlets, such as Daily Sabah, have portrayed him as maintaining a lavish lifestyle in California, contrasting with his self-described modest adaptation, though independent reports emphasize his advocacy for rule-of-law reforms as a precondition for return.[67] [8]

Football legacy

Career statistics and records

Şükür is Turkey's all-time leading international goalscorer with 51 goals in 112 appearances for the national team between 1992 and 2007.[83][84] He holds the record for the fastest goal in FIFA World Cup history, scoring after 11 seconds against South Korea in the third-place match on 29 June 2002.[24][85] At club level, Şükür is Galatasaray's all-time top scorer with 288 goals in 539 appearances across all competitions from 1987 to 2000 and 2003 to 2008.[86] He won the Süper Lig top scorer award four times (1991–92 with 26 goals, 1994–95 with 28, 1997–98 with 24, and 2007–08 with 17).[22] In European club competitions, he scored a Turkish record 38 goals in 67 matches, primarily with Galatasaray.[87]

Honours and impact on Turkish football

Hakan Şükür amassed a collection of major honours at club and international levels, predominantly during his tenure with Galatasaray, where he played in three spells from 1987 to 2000, 2003 to 2007, and briefly in 2008. With the club, he contributed to eight Süper Lig titles, including consecutive wins in the late 1990s, six Turkish Cups, and three Turkish Super Cups. In 2000, Şükür was part of the Galatasaray squad that clinched the UEFA Cup, defeating Arsenal 4–1 in the final in Copenhagen on May 17, defeating Paris Saint-Germain in the semi-finals. His sole stint abroad yielded the 1999–2000 Coppa Italia with Torino, though his impact there was limited to nine goals in 44 Serie A appearances.[4] Individually, Şükür earned recognition as Turkey's Footballer of the Year five times and was the Süper Lig top scorer on four occasions, notably in 1996–97 with a record 38 goals in 34 matches. The International Federation of Football History & Statistics (IFFHS) named him the World's Best Top Division Goal Scorer in 1997 for his 38-league-goal haul. For the Turkish national team, he holds the record for most goals with 51 in 112 caps, captaining the side to third place at the 2002 FIFA World Cup, where he scored the fastest goal in tournament history—11 seconds against South Korea on June 29, 2002.[3][4][88] Şükür's legacy in Turkish football is marked by his status as the Süper Lig's all-time leading scorer with 249 goals in 490 appearances, surpassing predecessors like Metin Oktay and Tanju Çolak, and his 217 goals for Galatasaray in 392 games, cementing him as the club's highest scorer. His prolific output, combining aerial prowess, positioning, and finishing—earning the nickname "Bull of the Bosphorus"—elevated Galatasaray's dominance in domestic and European competitions during the 1990s and early 2000s. Şükür's achievements, including spearheading Turkey's golden generation to World Cup semi-final contention, boosted the sport's popularity in Turkey, inspiring a surge in youth participation and national pride, though his post-career political exile has overshadowed some retrospective acclaim.[19][88][12]
