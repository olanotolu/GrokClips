Let's Go Brandon

"Let's Go Brandon" is an American political slogan and chant that functions as a euphemism for the profane anti-Joseph Biden expression "Fuck Joe Biden," encapsulating widespread conservative dissatisfaction with the president's policies and leadership.[1][2] The phrase originated on October 2, 2021, during a NASCAR Xfinity Series race at Talladega Superspeedway in Alabama, where driver Brandon Brown secured his first career victory amid a crowd chanting the vulgar phrase directed at Biden; NBC Sports reporter Kelli Stavast, conducting a live post-race interview, claimed the crowd was saying "Let's go Brandon" in support of the driver, a characterization that quickly proved inaccurate and sparked viral memes highlighting the discrepancy.[1][3][4] It rapidly proliferated across conservative circles, manifesting in merchandise, vehicle decals, apparel, and public chants at sporting events, airports, and political rallies, while also inspiring adaptations targeting other figures like Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau as "Let's Go Brandonneau."[2][5][6] The slogan's endurance reflects organic grassroots opposition, often amplified on social media and alternative platforms amid perceptions of mainstream media reluctance to acknowledge the underlying sentiment, evolving into a cultural phenomenon that even charted songs and prompted legal challenges over free speech restrictions in schools.[7][8][9]

Origins

The NASCAR Event and Misreporting

On October 2, 2021, Brandon Brown won the NASCAR Xfinity Series Sparks 300 race at Talladega Superspeedway in Alabama, marking his first career victory in the series after the event concluded under caution due to darkness.[10][3] During Brown's live post-race interview with NBC Sports reporter Kelli Stavast, spectators in the stands chanted "Fuck Joe Biden," reflecting widespread frustration with President Joe Biden's policies, including the chaotic U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan completed in August 2021â€”which had contributed to his approval rating falling to around 43%â€”and rising inflation exceeding 5% annually.[11][12] Stavast interjected by describing the audible chants as "'Let's go Brandon,'" a phrase seemingly intended to celebrate the driver, thereby reframing or obscuring the vulgar anti-Biden slogan directed at the president.[13][14][11] The unaltered crowd noise persisted throughout the interview without further acknowledgment from Stavast, who proceeded to question Brown about his performance.[15][14] Unedited footage of the interview quickly circulated on platforms such as Twitter and YouTube starting October 3, 2021, prompting widespread online backlash that portrayed the incident as indicative of mainstream media efforts to sanitize or suppress expressions of public dissent against the Biden administration.[15][11][13]

Initial Viral Spread

Following the October 2, 2021, NASCAR event at Talladega Superspeedway, the phrase "Let's Go Brandon" quickly proliferated online as a coded expression of opposition to President Joe Biden, particularly amid public discontent over rising inflation rates exceeding 5% annually and ongoing COVID-19 vaccine mandates.[16] The originating video, capturing NBC reporter Kelli Stavast's interpretation of the crowd's chant, amassed millions of views across platforms like YouTube and Twitter within days, underscoring a grassroots mechanism of dissemination driven by shared skepticism toward mainstream media reporting.[17] This misattribution was widely viewed as emblematic of institutional bias, catalyzing the meme's adoption on conservative-leaning social media outlets where users repurposed it to evade content moderation while signaling anti-administration sentiment.[18] By mid-October 2021, the phrase had surfaced organically in live sports settings, including college football games such as Alabama versus Ole Miss on October 9, where spectators echoed the chant amid broader crowd frustrations.[19] Similar instances emerged at Major League Baseball events shortly thereafter, reflecting bottom-up virality from audiences rather than top-down orchestration.[20] TikTok searches for "Let's Go Brandon" surpassed 3.3 million views by late October, demonstrating exponential digital traction independent of traditional media amplification.[17] Mainstream conservative commentary further propelled the phrase, with Fox News host Tucker Carlson addressing it on October 13, 2021, explicitly linking the NBC gaffe to eroded public trust in elite narratives and framing it as a populist rebuke.[21] This early exposure on cable television intersected with organic online momentum, yet the meme's persistence stemmed from its utility as a subtle protest vehicle, evading censorship while encapsulating causal grievances like economic pressures and perceived overreach in public health policy.[19]

Popularization and Usage

Adoption in Sports and Public Gatherings

Chants of "Let's Go Brandon" emerged at NFL games shortly after the phrase's origin, including during the New York Jets versus Tennessee Titans game on October 3, 2021, at MetLife Stadium, where crowds voiced the expression audibly enough to be captured on broadcast audio. Broadcasters typically refrained from directly addressing or repeating the chants, maintaining silence or neutral commentary amid the crowd noise.[1] The expression spread to other sports venues, with reports of similar outbursts at NHL games, such as during Pittsburgh Penguins contests in late 2021, where fans integrated it into the event atmosphere.[22] Instances in NBA arenas were less frequently documented but aligned with the pattern of spontaneous crowd participation at live professional basketball events.[1] Beyond stadiums, the chant appeared in non-sports public settings like air travel; on October 30, 2021, a Southwest Airlines pilot broadcast "Let's Go Brandon" over the intercom to passengers on a flight from Houston to Columbus, Ohio, prompting audible reactions and subsequent internal airline scrutiny.[23][24] At country music concerts, crowds adopted the phrase by late October 2021, incorporating it into performances and applause breaks as a recurring element of audience engagement.[25] Media coverage tracked these occurrences across dozens of live events by year's end, highlighting the chant's penetration into venues traditionally focused on entertainment rather than overt political discourse.[1][26]

Political Endorsements and Rhetoric

Republican lawmakers quickly incorporated "Let's Go Brandon" into congressional rhetoric following its viral emergence in late October 2021. On October 29, 2021, Representative Lauren Boebert (R-CO) released a video on her official House website titled "LET'S GO BRANDON - #1 FOR A REASON," highlighting the phrase's chart-topping song as a symbol of public frustration with the Biden administration.[27] Similarly, Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene (R-GA) featured the slogan prominently at a town hall event on October 28, 2021, and displayed a sign bearing it outside her office, which was later reported stolen on November 4, 2021.[28][29] These instances positioned the phrase as a coded expression of dissent against perceived elite media suppression and policy overreach, emphasizing free speech in opposition to mainstream narratives. The slogan gained traction at high-profile conservative gatherings, including former President Donald Trump's rallies and the 2022 Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC). Trump first publicly addressed the meme on November 9, 2021, acknowledging its widespread use as a critique of President Biden during an interview.[30] His campaign further endorsed it by offering "Let's Go Brandon" T-shirts to donors contributing $45 or more, as announced in an email on October 31, 2021.[31] Chants erupted at Trump events, such as a January 30, 2022, rally in Texas.[32] At CPAC in Orlando, Florida, from February 24-26, 2022, Senator Ted Cruz (R-TX) invoked the phrase in his speech, arguing its national recognitionâ€”even in blue statesâ€”reflected broad disapproval of Biden's leadership.[33] Missouri Attorney General candidate Eric Greitens also concluded his address with "let's go Brandon," tying it to calls for political action.[34] Usage peaked during the 2022 midterm election cycle as a partisan shorthand for anti-Democratic sentiment. Candidates integrated it into campaign materials, including a Super Bowl ad by Pennsylvania Senate hopeful Dave McCormick in February 2022 and an Arizona Senate ad by Blake Masters that referenced it before airing, as reported in January 2022.[35][36] Trump rallies in midterm battlegrounds featured chants alongside criticisms of Biden's policies.[37] This rhetorical adoption coincided with Gallup polling showing Biden's approval rating at 42% in October 2021, declining amid public discontent over issues including southern border management, where migrant encounters reached record highs of over 1.7 million in fiscal year 2021.[38] The phrase thus served as a concise vehicle for articulating grievances tied to economic inflation, supply chain disruptions, and immigration enforcement lapses during this period.

Commercial and Sponsorship Attempts

In December 2021, NASCAR driver Brandon Brown, whose post-race interview inadvertently popularized the phrase, attempted to capitalize on it through a sponsorship deal with LGBcoin, a meme-based cryptocurrency launched to reference "Let's Go Brandon." The agreement, announced on December 30, 2021, aimed to brand Brown's No. 68 Chevrolet with the phrase's imagery for the 2022 Xfinity Series season, positioning the coin as a digital asset tied to the slogan's cultural momentum.[39] However, NASCAR rejected the deal on January 5, 2022, enforcing its policy against political sponsorships and citing the phrase's explicit anti-Biden undertones as incompatible with the sport's commercial standards.[40] This collapse, which later sparked investor lawsuits alleging NASCAR's inconsistent approval process, exemplified how the phrase's polarizing nature deterred institutional sponsors despite Brown's efforts to reframe it as a neutral endorsement opportunity.[41] Parallel to such high-profile failures, grassroots merchandise exploded in demand, reflecting direct consumer monetization outside corporate channels. Etsy hosted thousands of listings for "Let's Go Brandon" apparel like T-shirts and hats, alongside items from independent sellers on platforms such as Amazon, which promoted the products via ads after Walmart removed similar listings in November 2021.[42] A Georgia-based apparel firm reported generating roughly $1 million in revenue from "Let's Go Brandon"-themed wrapping paper sales in 2021, with projections to double that figure in 2022 amid sustained holiday demand.[43] Niche markets, including gun retailers marketing parts and ammunition with the slogan, further indicated robust secondary commercialization driven by partisan buyers.[44] Mainstream advertising integrations proved similarly unviable, amplifying commercial backlash. In January 2022, Yahoo rejected a campaign ad submission from Arizona Republican Senate candidate Jim Lamon explicitly because it incorporated "Let's Go Brandon," deeming the phrase a violation of platform policies on profane or divisive content.[45] These rejections, coupled with retail pullbacks, highlighted a broader hesitancy among established brands to associate with the slogan, confining its economic viability to decentralized, consumer-led outlets rather than sanctioned sponsorships or mass-market campaigns.

Cultural and Media Impact

Music and Entertainment Adaptations

Loza Alexander released the hip-hop track "Let's Go Brandon" on October 10, 2021, which quickly rose to number one on the iTunes hip-hop chart and number two on the all-genre chart, trailing only Adele's single.[46] The song debuted at number one on Billboard's Emerging Artists chart dated October 30, 2021, and also topped R&B/Hip-Hop Digital Song Sales and Rap Digital Song Sales charts.[47] Its parody style incorporated the phrase as a rhythmic chant over beats critiquing federal policies.[48] Forgiato Blow, a country rap artist, issued a "Let's Go Brandon" single on October 13, 2021, featuring explicit references to economic pressures such as rising gas prices in its lyrics.[49] This track spawned variants including "Let's Go Brandon Slide" in October 2021 and a holiday-themed "Let's Go Brandon Christmas" in November 2021, blending trap and country elements.[50] [51] Additional country adaptations emerged, such as tracks by Austin Forman and Bobby Wayne in 2021, and Tom Hanley's "Lets Go Brandon! (Official Country Song)," which adapted the phrase into twangy, upbeat melodies.[52] Entertainment integrations included a remix of Alexander's original by The Remix Bros in October 2021 and a 2022 remix version by Alexander himself.[53] [54] On television, Saturday Night Live featured a "Weekend Update" parody sketch on November 7, 2021, where cast member Kyle Mooney portrayed a confused everyman named Brandon reacting to the chant's viral spread.[55] These adaptations peaked in late 2021 with multiple iTunes entries dominating hip-hop and rap categories by October 27, reflecting the meme's brief but intense musical footprint before fading from major charts.[56]

Merchandise and Everyday Expressions

The phrase "Let's Go Brandon" proliferated into consumer-level merchandise, including T-shirts, hats, stickers, and vehicle decals, sold online via platforms like eBay and Amazon, as well as at political events. By December 2021, eBay hosted over 12,000 listings for such items.[57] Dedicated physical stores in New England, such as those in Massachusetts and Rhode Island, opened in late 2021 and reported strong sales of anti-Biden apparel bearing the slogan.[58] At the 2022 Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC), vendors sold more "Let's Go Brandon" T-shirts than any other pro-Trump merchandise, indicating peak demand among attendees.[59] Sales of these items demonstrated sustained grassroots popularity into later years, with vendors noting a surge in demand in July 2024 following the attempted assassination of Donald Trump.[60] Vehicle decals and stickers remained widely available on sites like Etsy through 2025, facilitating low-cost personalization of cars and trucks as expressions of dissent.[61] In everyday settings, the slogan appeared on signage during the 2022 trucker convoys protesting COVID-19 mandates, often integrated with messages thanking truckers or opposing restrictions, as seen in rally flags and banners.[62][63] Its use extended to casual displays at sports tailgates, such as flags at Fresno State football parties in 2021 and references persisting in NASCAR event discussions into 2025, reflecting ongoing normalization among fans despite initial expectations of transience.[64][65]

Political Interpretations

As Grassroots Dissent Against Policy Failures

The phrase "Let's Go Brandon" emerged as a surrogate for expressing public discontent with tangible policy shortcomings during the early Biden administration, linking grassroots frustration to verifiable metrics of economic strain and administrative decisions. Inflation surged to a 40-year high of 9.1% year-over-year in June 2022, as measured by the Consumer Price Index from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, eroding household purchasing power amid supply chain disruptions and fiscal spending initiatives.[66] Concurrently, the U.S. military withdrawal from Afghanistan, announced for completion by September 11, 2021, but accelerating into chaos by late August, resulted in the Taliban regaining control of Kabul and the deaths of 13 American service members in a suicide bombing at Hamid Karzai International Airport on August 26. These events, compounded by federal vaccine mandates issued via executive order on September 9, 2021, requiring inoculation for approximately 3.5 million civilian executive branch employees subject to limited exemptions, fueled perceptions of executive overreach and incompetence.[67] This organic adoption contrasted with analogous vulgar criticisms of prior presidents, such as chants against Donald Trump, which lacked equivalent coded virality or mainstream euphemistic diffusion despite similar political polarization. The specificity of the phrase's proliferation post-October 2, 2021, NASCAR incident tied to acute causal factors: policy-induced hardships absent in prior contexts, where no single misreported event catalyzed a comparable nationwide proxy for dissent. Mainstream media's initial portrayal of the crowd's vulgarity as benign support for driver Brandon Brown exemplified perceived alignment between outlets and administration narratives, diminishing trust and prompting the phrase as a workaround for unfiltered critique.[1] Further amplifying its role, the phrase circumvented platform moderation trends, where Biden administration communications reportedly pressured social media firms to suppress content questioning COVID-19 policies, including vaccine efficacy doubts, as acknowledged by Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg regarding 2021 interventions.[68] This dynamic prioritized signaling empirical realitiesâ€”such as inflation's disproportionate impact on working-class demographics and the Afghanistan exit's strategic costsâ€”over decorum, reflecting a broader rejection of sanitized discourse in favor of direct accountability for governance outcomes.

Media and Left-Leaning Criticisms

Mainstream media outlets, including CNN, have characterized "Let's Go Brandon" as juvenile and ungracious, with analyst Ron Brownstein describing it in November 2021 as an immature expression of political discontent that avoids substantive policy debate.[69] Similarly, opinion pieces in outlets like The Charlotte Observer labeled the phrase in January 2022 as a silly and immature meme masking deeper partisan hostility, often framing it as profane shorthand rather than engaging with public frustrations over economic pressures and foreign policy decisions such as the August 2021 Afghanistan withdrawal.[70] These portrayals frequently emphasize the euphemism's vulgar origins over the underlying grievances, such as rising inflation rates that reached 7% by October 2021, prioritizing critiques of tone and decorum. White House Press Secretary Jen Psaki addressed the phrase during a November 12, 2021, briefing, acknowledging it as code for a profane anti-Biden slogan and stating that President Biden viewed such rhetoric as unconstructive amid efforts to unite the country on issues like COVID-19 vaccination campaigns.[71] Critics from left-leaning perspectives, including in The Atlantic, dismissed it as juvenile own-the-libs humor in November 2021, warning that normalizing such taunts erodes civil discourse without linking it to any specific incidents of violenceâ€”empirical data shows no documented cases of the phrase directly inciting physical harm, despite occasional broader claims of toxicity in partisan contexts.[72] Surveys and usage patterns indicate limited crossover appeal, with the phrase primarily gaining traction among conservative audiences and Republican lawmakers, as evidenced by its invocation on the House floor by Rep. Bill Posey on October 21, 2021, rather than among independents or Democrats.[2] This partisan persistence reflects its role as a counter to perceived media sanitization of administration shortcomings, such as supply chain disruptions contributing to gasoline prices averaging $3.40 per gallon nationally by late October 2021, yet detractors often sidestep these causal factors in favor of euphemism-focused rebukes.[1]

Legal and Free Speech Controversies

School Discipline Cases

In February 2022, two middle school brothers at Tri County Middle School in Howard City, Michigan, wore sweatshirts displaying the phrase "Let's Go Brandon" to school, prompting administrators to order their removal under the district's dress code prohibiting vulgar or profane language.[73][74] The school interpreted the slogan as a profane euphemism equivalent to "F*** Joe Biden," a view supported by contemporaneous media coverage and public usage, leading to the students being sent to the principal's office and facing potential in-school suspension if they refused to comply.[75] Parents, represented by the Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression (FIRE), filed a federal lawsuit in April 2023 against Tri County Area Schools and administrators, alleging viewpoint discrimination under the First Amendment and seeking an injunction against the ban.[73][76] A federal district court granted summary judgment to the school district in 2024, ruling that administrators had reasonably forecast disruption and that the phrase constituted vulgarity unprotected in the school environment under Tinker v. Des Moines precedents allowing regulation of substantially disruptive speech.[77] The plaintiffs appealed to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit, where oral arguments occurred on June 12, 2025, centering on whether the phrase's political undertones outweighed its vulgar connotation and if schools could prohibit it without evidence of actual disruption.[78] On October 14, 2025, the Sixth Circuit affirmed the district court in a 2-1 decision, with Judge John Nalbandian writing for the majority that schools retain authority to suppress vulgar political speech, as "vulgarity trumps politics" in maintaining educational decorum, even absent proven disruption.[75][79] The dissenting opinion, by Judge Amul Thapar, argued the evidence did not support classifying the phrase as inherently vulgar or disruptive, potentially chilling core political expression.[80] FIRE criticized the ruling as enabling administrative overreach by deferring excessively to subjective interpretations of euphemisms.[76] Parallel incidents emerged in other districts, though outcomes diverged based on local policies and judicial review. For instance, an eighth-grade student in an unspecified district faced discipline for a "Let's Go Brandon" T-shirt in early 2025, but a federal court denied a preliminary injunction, citing similar vulgarity rationales without detailed disruption findings.[81] Legal trackers from organizations like FIRE document at least three additional reported cases nationwide between 2022 and 2025, often resolved administratively in favor of schools via dress code enforcement rather than litigation, highlighting variability tied to district-specific forecasts of interference with educational goals.[82]

Broader First Amendment Implications

The phrase "Let's Go Brandon," recognized as a coded expression of political criticism toward President Joe Biden, has exemplified tensions in First Amendment jurisprudence concerning the protection of speech that conveys vulgarity through euphemism. In regulated environments such as public schools, courts have invoked the standard from Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District (1969), which permits restrictions on student speech only if it causes substantial disruption to the educational environment or invades others' rights. In the 2025 Sixth Circuit ruling on B.A. v. Tri-County Area Schools, the court upheld a Michigan school's prohibition of sweatshirts bearing the phrase, determining that its widely understood profane connotationâ€”equivalent to an expletive directed at the presidentâ€”created a foreseeable risk of disruption sufficient to justify viewpoint-neutral regulation under Tinker and Bethel School District No. 403 v. Fraser (1986), which allows schools to proscribe lewd or vulgar speech.[75] Critics, including the Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression (FIRE), contend that mere viewer offense or discomfort does not satisfy Tinker's "substantial disruption" threshold, as no evidence of actual interference with school operations was demonstrated, positioning the phrase as core political expression akin to the armbands in Tinker.[76] Broader application extends to distinctions between unprotected "true threats" and rhetorical hyperbole, as established in Watts v. United States (1969), where the Supreme Court protected a draft protester's statement threatening the president as political exaggeration rather than imminent harm. No federal cases have classified "Let's Go Brandon" as a true threat, given its lack of direct advocacy for violence or personal endangerment; instead, it functions as satirical dissent without evidence of intent to incite lawless action under Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969). This aligns with precedents protecting offensive political slogans in public forums, where government restrictions face strict scrutiny, though limited forums like schools permit greater deference to administrators' forecasts of disruption. Litigation resurgence in 2024â€“2025, including FIRE's appeal of the Tri-County decisionâ€”supported by amicus briefs from First Amendment scholars, linguists arguing the phrase's non-vulgar surface meaning, and groups like the Buckeye Institute emphasizing anti-viewpoint-discrimination principlesâ€”underscores ongoing debates over coded speech's First Amendment boundaries.[76][83] Oral arguments occurred on June 12, 2025, with FIRE expressing optimism that the phrase's political valence warranted protection absent proven harm, though the panel ultimately affirmed the ban on October 14, 2025, highlighting judicial deference in sensitive contexts.[78][84] These proceedings illustrate the phrase's role in probing the balance between free expression rights and institutional authority to mitigate perceived vulgarity, with potential for Supreme Court review to clarify standards for euphemistic political rhetoric.[85]

Related Memes and Counter-Responses

Emergence of Dark Brandon

The "Dark Brandon" meme originated in mid-2022 on progressive online forums and social media platforms, reinterpreting the "Brandon" reference from the "Let's Go Brandon" phrase as an ironic symbol of President Joe Biden's assertive political dominance. This version portrays Biden with glowing red laser eyes, drawing from cryptocurrency meme aesthetics where such eyes signify unyielding commitment, and evoking a "dark" or Sith-like persona to celebrate perceived triumphs over Republican opposition. The meme gained traction following Biden's signing of the Inflation Reduction Act on August 16, 2022, framing him as a cunning victor in legislative battles.[86][87] By inverting the original phrase's derogatory intent, "Dark Brandon" served as morale-boosting content for left-leaning audiences, emphasizing Biden's resilience amid policy wins like student debt relief announcements and judicial appointments. It combined elements of the "Dark MAGA" templateâ€”often used satirically for Trumpâ€”with the "Brandon" motif to mock conservative frustrations. Unlike the widespread cultural penetration of "Let's Go Brandon," which inspired commercial music tracks and public chants, "Dark Brandon" remained more insular to partisan digital spaces, reflecting its niche appeal within progressive circles.[88][87] The Biden-Harris reelection campaign formally adopted the meme in 2023, integrating it into the official website launched on April 25 and featuring it in merchandise such as mugs and T-shirts depicting the laser-eyed Biden. By August 4, 2023, "Dark Brandon" items accounted for over 50% of the campaign's online merchandise sales, with promotional videos showing Biden holding themed coffee mugs. This embrace aimed to engage younger voters through meme culture but faced right-wing derision as a contrived response to electoral challenges, exemplified by 2024 social media parodies portraying it as ineffective "cope." Left-leaning supporters, however, utilized it to counter narratives of Biden's weakness, though its reach paled compared to the original phrase's mass adoption across conservative merchandise and events.[89][90][87]
