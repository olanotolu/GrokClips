New York City Cops (song)

"New York City Cops" is a song by the American rock band the Strokes, released on June 25, 2001, as the B-side to their debut single "Hard to Explain" and included on their album Is This It.[1][2] The track, written amid concerns over police brutality following the 1999 shooting of Amadou Diallo, employs satirical lyrics portraying law enforcement as inept, exemplified by the repeated refrain "New York City cops, they ain't too smart" and references to evading arrest during illicit activities.[3][2] Its raw garage rock style, driven by driving rhythms and Julian Casablancas's detached vocals, contributed to the band's role in revitalizing post-punk influences in early 2000s indie rock.[1] The song sparked controversy when, shortly after the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks, the Strokes and their label opted to exclude it from the U.S. CD edition of Is This It, replacing it with the newly recorded "When It Started" to avoid perceived insensitivity toward police heroism in the attacks' aftermath.[1][2] This decision delayed the album's American release and highlighted tensions between artistic expression and contemporary events, though the track remained on international versions and vinyl pressings.[1] Despite the omission, "New York City Cops" has endured as a fan favorite, often performed live, including notable instances amid police presence that underscored its provocative edge.[4][5]

Origins and Inspiration

Connection to Amadou Diallo Shooting

On February 4, 1999, four plainclothes officers from the New York Police Department's Street Crimes Unit approached Amadou Diallo, a 23-year-old unarmed immigrant from Guinea, in the vestibule of his Bronx apartment building, mistaking him for a serial rapist based on a vague description.[6] Diallo, who had no criminal record and was reaching for his wallet to identify himself, was fired upon with 41 shots from the officers' 9mm handguns and a shotgun, struck 19 times, and killed at the scene.[6] [7] The officers maintained that Diallo's movements appeared threatening in the dimly lit entryway, interpreting his wallet as a potential firearm, though no weapon was found on him.[7] The incident ignited massive protests across New York City, with demonstrators decrying police overreach, racial profiling, and the aggressive tactics of specialized units like the Street Crimes Unit, which had conducted high volumes of stop-and-frisk operations disproportionately targeting minorities.[8] In a state trial relocated to Albany, the four officersâ€”Sean Carroll, Richard Murphy, Edward McMellon, and Kenneth Bossâ€”were acquitted of second-degree murder and reckless endangerment charges on February 25, 2000, a verdict that intensified public outrage and scrutiny of NYPD accountability mechanisms.[9] While the acquittal hinged on arguments of reasonable fear under split-second decision-making, the event underscored empirical patterns of excessive force in certain policing contexts, including data from the Street Crimes Unit showing thousands of annual stops yielding low arrest rates relative to complaints of harassment.[8] [10] This high-profile case directly informed the song's origins, as The Strokes' frontman Julian Casablancas has stated that "New York City Cops" emerged from the news of Diallo's death and associated police brutality, fostering a broader skepticism toward law enforcement competence in handling ambiguous urban encounters.[11] Casablancas, reflecting on the era's tensions, drew from the incident's causal chainâ€”perceived threat escalation leading to lethal overreactionâ€”as emblematic of institutional failures in de-escalation and verification, shaping the track's portrayal of officers as prone to bravado over prudence.[11] The Diallo shooting thus provided a factual anchor for the song's critique, highlighting real-world precedents of misjudgment under pressure rather than abstract grievances.[11]

Songwriting by Julian Casablancas

Julian Casablancas, lead singer and primary songwriter for The Strokes, composed "New York City Cops" circa 2000 during the band's nascent phase in New York City's underground music scene. The track's lyrics articulate a pointed skepticism toward police efficacy, rooted in the songwriter's immersion in urban street dynamics where institutional enforcers appeared limited in perceptiveness and response.[11] This perspective manifests in the song's refrainâ€”"New York City cops, they ain't too smart"â€”and accompanying verses depicting a female figure engaging in illicit acts with apparent impunity, evading consequences due to officers' shortcomings.[2] The writing process prioritized unrefined, conversational phrasing to evoke causal observations of human fallibility within authority structures, eschewing heroic tropes in favor of depictions of error-prone individuals navigating chaotic city environments. Early demos likely captured this directness, aligning with Casablancas' method of distilling lived distrust of power into terse, narrative-driven lines that highlight inefficiency over systemic abstraction. Such crafting underscored a ground-level realism, portraying law enforcement as prone to oversight in petty infractions rather than infallible protectors.[1]

Musical and Lyrical Analysis

Composition and Style

"New York City Cops" showcases the raw garage rock style central to The Strokes' sound, driven by interlocking rhythm guitars from Nick Valensi and Albert Hammond Jr., steady basslines by Nikolai Fraiture, and energetic drumming by Fabrizio Moretti that propel the track's momentum.[12] The song clocks in at 3 minutes and 31 seconds, set in C major with a 4/4 time signature and a tempo of around 110 beats per minute, creating a sense of urgency suited to its punk-inflected energy.[13][14] Its composition employs a straightforward verse-chorus structure augmented by a bridge, built on repetitive chord sequences like Am-G that prioritize rhythmic drive over complexity.[15][16] This minimalist approach, evoking the lo-fi ethos of 1970s New York proto-punk bands such as Television, contrasts sharply with the slick production dominating early 2000s pop, helping to spearhead a revival of unadorned rock authenticity.[17][18]

Lyrics and Themes of Police Critique

The lyrics of "New York City Cops" depict New York City police officers as ineffective and overly aggressive figures navigating urban disorder, with the chorus repeatedly asserting, "New York City cops / They ain't too smart / They play too hard."[2] This refrain establishes a tone of casual derision toward law enforcement's competence, portraying them as enforcers who escalate situations unnecessarily rather than resolving them adeptly. Verses further illustrate this through scenarios of non-compliance and detachment, such as "They make a request but I do not arrest / You make a request but I do not arrest," suggesting a breakdown in authority where directives fail to compel obedience.[2] A recurring motif of peril underscores the song's empirical observation of police fallibility amid city chaos, exemplified by the line "Might be dead before you even get to read this," which implies indifference or incompetence could lead to fatal outcomes without heroic intervention.[2] The narrative frames officers not as omniscient guardians but as reactive participants in mechanized street lifeâ€”"Here in the streets so mechanized / Rise to the bottom of the meaning of life"â€”echoing a pre-9/11 skepticism toward institutional authority in a gritty, unpredictable metropolis.[2] This realism avoids idealizing police as infallible, instead highlighting their human limitations through detached, anecdotal vignettes rather than systemic indictment. Thematically, the song critiques romanticized perceptions of law enforcement by emphasizing bungled enforcement and personal agency in defiance, as in "When they start to lose control of you / You just say 'No, you don't know me.'"[2] Absent are prescriptive demands for policy changes or defunding; instead, the lyrics maintain an observational detachment, debunking myths of unerring duty with ironic humor, such as referencing merchandise like "They even made a t-shirt sayin' 'New York City cops.'"[2] This approach privileges a grounded view of police as flawed actors in a flawed environment, rooted in the lived realities of early-2000s New York rather than ideological polemic.[1]

Recording and Production

Sessions for Is This It Album

The recording of "New York City Cops" occurred during the spring 2001 sessions for Is This It at Transporterraum, a modest basement studio in New York City's East Village, with producer Gordon Raphael overseeing the process. Raphael utilized a 24-track analog desk and vintage microphones to impart a characteristic warmth and grit to the tracks, aligning with the band's preference for a stripped-down garage rock sound that eschewed digital polish.[19] These sessions, completed in roughly three weeks, emphasized capturing the band's live performance energy through basic tracking methods, incorporating few overdubs to retain the raw, unrefined authenticity central to the album's aesthetic. "New York City Cops" fit naturally within this framework, its driving rhythm and direct instrumentation complementing the record's overall urgency without necessitating adjustments for broader market appeal; on international editions, it concluded the tracklist as the eleventh song, amplifying the album's culminating intensity.[19][20]

Key Personnel

The recording of "New York City Cops" involved the core quintet of The Strokes, with no additional session musicians contributing to the track. Julian Casablancas provided lead vocals, Nick Valensi handled lead guitar, Albert Hammond Jr. played rhythm guitar, Nikolai Fraiture contributed bass, and Fabrizio Moretti performed on drums.[2] Gordon Raphael served as producer, adopting a minimalist, non-interventionist style that prioritized capturing the band's raw, unpolished performances in a single New York loft space equipped with basic analog gear. This approach minimized overdubs and post-production effects, allowing the quintet's interplay to dictate the song's garage rock texture and immediate energy, as Raphael focused on fidelity to the initial takes rather than studio enhancement.[19][21]

Release History

Initial Single and Album Inclusion

"New York City Cops" was released on June 25, 2001, as the B-side to the double A-side single "Hard to Explain / New York City Cops" by Rough Trade Records in the United Kingdom.[22][23] The 7-inch vinyl and CD formats featured the tracks alongside a live version of "Take It or Leave It" on some pressings, marking an early showcase of the band's raw, garage rock energy amid their rising profile in the indie scene.[24] The song appeared on international editions of The Strokes' debut album Is This It, first released on July 30, 2001, in markets including the UK and Australia via Rough Trade and RCA Records.[25] Positioned as the album's closing track, it provided a pointed critique of New York Police Department practices, drawing from real events and aligning with the record's themes of urban disillusionment.[26] This inclusion helped cement the band's breakthrough, with UK airplay and performances, such as on Top of the Pops shortly after release, amplifying its role in the post-punk revival narrative.[27]

Post-9/11 Removal and Replacement

Following the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks, which killed 23 New York Police Department officers responding at the World Trade Center, the song's lyrics portraying police as inattentive and self-interested were viewed as potentially inflammatory during a period of widespread heroization of first responders.[28] [29] The U.S. compact disc release of Is This It, initially set for September 25, 2001, was postponed to October 9, 2001, to excise "New York City Cops" and substitute it with the previously unreleased track "When It Started," recorded specifically for this purpose.[29] In contrast, the vinyl edition pressed and released domestically on September 11, 2001â€”the same day as the attacksâ€”preserved the original track listing, including the song.[30] Editions distributed outside the United States remained unaffected, retaining "New York City Cops" in its position on the album.[31] This alteration, executed jointly by the band and RCA Records, constituted a precautionary measure against expected controversy, underscoring commercial considerations in a sensitized national context over fidelity to the album's initial configuration.[31]

Band's Rationale and Public Response

The Strokes voluntarily removed "New York City Cops" from the U.S. release of Is This It following the September 11, 2001, attacks, stating that while they stood by the song, the timing was inappropriate in light of the New York Police Department's response to the tragedy.[32] Frontman Julian Casablancas later described the decision as internally driven rather than externally imposed, noting a shift in public perception where police transitioned from figures associated with brutalityâ€”such as in pre-9/11 news coverage of cases like Amadou Diallo'sâ€”to national heroes, rendering the song's critique tonally mismatched.[33] This pragmatic choice prioritized the band's nascent career viability over immediate artistic completeness, acknowledging the altered cultural context without self-censorship motives.[11] Public response to the removal was muted, with no documented lawsuits, boycotts, or organized protests against the band.[32] Fan discussions reflected division: some criticized it as concessionary pandering to post-9/11 sensitivities, while others deemed it a mature adaptation to circumstances that could have derailed the album's reception.[11] In subsequent reflections, Casablancas has framed the episode as illustrative of how temporal context can eclipse a song's original intent, diluting its political edgeâ€”in this case, inspired by police misconductâ€”and reshaping an artist's public image toward apolitical accessibility over ideological consistency.[33][11]

Commercial Performance

Chart Positions

"Hard to Explain"/"New York City Cops" was released as a double A-side single in the United Kingdom on June 25, 2001, and achieved modest commercial charting primarily in that market.[34] The single's chart performance was constrained in the United States, where "New York City Cops" was omitted from the album Is This It following the September 11, 2001, attacks, resulting in no entry on major Billboard charts such as the Hot 100 or Alternative Airplay due to limited radio promotion.[34]

Sales and Certifications

"New York City Cops," released as the B-side to "Hard to Explain" on a double A-side single in the United Kingdom on June 25, 2001, did not achieve British Phonographic Industry (BPI) certification, with sales falling short of the 200,000 units required for silver status at the time.[34] The track's limited standalone physical release contributed to its modest commercial footprint independent of the parent album Is This It. In the United States, the song's exclusion from the compact disc edition of the album following the September 11 attacks further restricted its domestic single viability, resulting in no Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) certification for the track itself. Digital streaming has provided a measure of ongoing revenue, with the song accumulating over 34 million plays on Spotify, reflecting sustained interest driven by album streams and playlist inclusions rather than initial single sales.[37] Vinyl pressings of Is This It that include "New York City Cops"â€”such as the original U.S. editionâ€”continue to circulate in collector markets, supporting niche physical demand without formalized sales certifications for the song.[38] This contrasts with the album's broader success, certified platinum by the RIAA for one million units shipped, underscoring the track's reliance on Is This It's overall popularity for its commercial longevity.

Reception and Controversies

Critical Reviews

Upon its 2001 release, New York City Cops received acclaim in contemporaneous reviews for embodying the raw, anti-establishment energy of New York City's underbelly, with critics highlighting its snarling guitar riffs and sardonic lyrics as a gritty counterpoint to polished rock trends.[39] NME awarded Is This It a perfect 10/10 score, praising the track as part of a "truly great statement of intent" that revived garage rock's urgent, street-level authenticity.[40] Rolling Stone described the song's anti-establishment vibe as capturing "gray-pavement aggression wrapped in black-leather cool," positioning it as a standout for its "sexy, snotty brilliance" amid the album's frantic pacing.[39] Pitchfork's original review of Is This It assigned it a 9.1/10 rating, underscoring the track's role in the album's tight, rhythm-driven appeal that echoed 1970s influences while asserting modern edge, though noting a lack of broader innovation.[41] This high score reflected the song's contribution to the record's critical consensus as a catalyst for indie rock revival, with its blunt portrayal of urban disillusionmentâ€”lyrics decrying cops as "ain't too smart"â€”adding provocative bite to the ensemble's lo-fi sheen.[41] In later retrospectives, the track's unfiltered provocativeness has been viewed as both a strength and a gamble, amplifying the album's prescience in depicting institutional distrust amid city malaise. A 2016 Pitchfork analysis lauded New York City Cops for evoking an "ugly New York" of aimless disappointment and forceful rebellion, its edgy sound verging on violence and resonating with contemporary abrasiveness.[42] Grammy.com's 2021 roundtable on the album's 20th anniversary credited such elements with redefining alt-rock through vital, dangerous energy, though the lyrics' raw riskiness highlighted the band's unpolished commitment to countercultural grit over mainstream caution.[43]

Debates on Anti-Police Sentiment vs. Post-9/11 Context

The lyrics of "New York City Cops," which include lines critiquing police competence such as "New York City cops, they ain't too smart," drew from real pre-9/11 incidents of perceived NYPD overreach, notably the February 4, 1999, fatal shooting of unarmed Guinean immigrant Amadou Diallo, during which four officers fired 41 shots, hitting him 19 times, despite no weapon or threat being present; the officers were acquitted of murder charges by a jury in Albany on February 25, 2000.[44][45] Supporters of the song's message have defended it as a legitimate artistic reflection of such empirically documented cases of police error or excess, arguing that abstract critiques of institutional shortcomings do not negate individual acts of bravery and predate the September 11 attacks by over two years.[46] Opponents of retaining the track post-9/11 contended that the attacks fundamentally altered public perception of law enforcement, with 23 NYPD officers dying while aiding rescue efforts at the World Trade Center site, elevating police from symbols of urban dysfunction to national heroes in a context of unified crisis response.[28][46] This causal shift, they argued, rendered the song's generalized jabs tone-deaf amid widespread empirical evidence of police self-sacrifice, including the recovery of survivors from rubble and extended shifts to maintain order; the band's decision to excise it from the U.S. CD edition of Is This It (released October 9, 2001) was framed not as censorship but as pragmatic respect for this heightened valorization, avoiding alienation in a moment when media narratives temporarily subdued chronic brutality emphases.[46] Some right-leaning commentators have pushed back against claims of over-sensitivity in the removal, positing that the song's pre-attack realism about competence gapsâ€”rooted in acquittals like Diallo's despite ballistic evidenceâ€”should not be retroactively idealized away by post-crisis sentiment, though mainstream outlets often amplified anti-police framings in later years without equivalent scrutiny of heroism data.[47] The song's 2020 performance at a Bernie Sanders rally in Concord, New Hampshire, on February 10â€”closing the set as fans rushed the stage and officers intervenedâ€”reignited discussions, with critics viewing it as ironic provocation amid contemporaneous urban protests but without the band's explicit alignment to movements advocating police defunding.[48][49] This instance highlighted enduring tensions between the track's snapshot of early-2000s policing critiques and evolving contexts, where post-9/11 unity had eroded into polarized debates, yet the performance elicited minimal backlash compared to 2001, reflecting shifted cultural tolerances rather than resolved causal realities of institutional performance.[50]

Legacy and Performances

Cultural Impact

The Strokes' "New York City Cops," released in 2001 on the vinyl edition of Is This It, contributed to the early-2000s garage rock revival by exemplifying a raw, unpolished sound rooted in New York City's underground scene, which contrasted with the era's dominant pop and electronic trends.[17] The track's driving rhythm and lyrics critiquing institutional authority aligned with the album's broader push toward authentic, lo-fi expression, helping propel indie rock's resurgence and influencing subsequent acts in the genre.[51] This authenticity symbolized a rejection of overproduced music, fostering a wave of bands emulating the Strokes' blend of post-punk energy and casual detachment.[52] In the post-9/11 context, the song's temporary removal from the U.S. CD release of Is This Itâ€”due to its portrayal of police as ineffective ("New York City cops... they ain't too smart") amid widespread recognition of first responders' heroismâ€”highlighted tensions between pre-tragedy cynicism and empirical evidence of institutional valor during crisis.[17] This adaptation underscored how real-world events can prompt reevaluation of artistic narratives, challenging absolutist anti-authority views when confronted with observable acts of bravery, yet the track's retention on vinyl and international editions preserved its role in unvarnished discourse on power structures.[53] Over time, its reemergence affirmed the resilience of candid expression against transient sensitivities, contributing to discussions on art's independence from immediate political climates. References to the song in media remain limited, with no prominent features in major films or television series, reflecting its niche status beyond live and playlist contexts.[54] It endures in curated lists of New York anthems, evoking the city's gritty underbelly and reinforcing its place in rock's tradition of provocative urban commentary.[55]

Live Performances by The Strokes

The Strokes first performed "New York City Cops" live on April 29, 2000, at Arlene's Grocery in New York City, prior to the recording and release of their debut album Is This It.[56] The track became a regular feature in their early setlists, appearing in shows during their post-release promotion, including multiple dates on their 2001 UK tour such as at London's Astoria on an early 2001 bill and the Monarch venue, where it often closed or anchored high-energy segments.[57][58] By the 2010s, the song retained its place as a setlist staple, particularly in international performances outside the United States, adapting its raw garage-rock drive to venue atmospheres from festivals to arenas.[59] A notable rendition occurred at the Reading Festival on August 27, 2011, where it followed the opener "Is This It" in a brisk, crowd-igniting sequence that highlighted the band's enduring live chemistry.[60] In a contextually ironic turn, the Strokes closed their set at a Bernie Sanders campaign rally in Durham, New Hampshire, on February 10, 2020, with "New York City Cops" amid fans rushing the stage and police officers intervening onstage to manage the crowd surge.[48][61] Frontman Julian Casablancas engaged security mid-performance, underscoring the song's detachment from its original anti-authority edge in the presence of law enforcement.[49]

Covers and Tributes

The song has inspired several tribute acts dedicated to replicating The Strokes' early sound, including the Irish-based band New York City Cops, formed to perform the group's material with fidelity to its garage rock roots.[62] This ensemble, active as of 2023, emphasizes the original's gritty instrumentation and lyrical bite in live settings, maintaining the track's anti-authority edge without significant alterations.[63] Similarly, the New York City tribute group The Strokes Tribute - NYC has incorporated "New York City Cops" into sets that evoke the band's 2001-era performances, focusing on high-energy renditions that preserve the song's raw, unpolished delivery.[64] In recorded covers, British punk outfit King Nun delivered a reinterpretation in 2021 as part of DIY Magazine's 20th-anniversary tribute album to Is This It, infusing the track with their chaotic, visceral style while retaining its core riff-driven structure and thematic cynicism toward law enforcement.[65] This version amplifies the original's punk influences, aligning with the band's heavier sonic palette, but stays true to the song's brevity and attitude.[66] Live tributes have appeared in indie circuits, notably by Canadian act The Brokes, who performed "New York City Cops" during their MTV $2 Bill Tribute show on March 14, 2025, at Toronto's Longboat Hall, channeling The Strokes' debut-era intensity to enthusiastic crowds.[67] The group reprised it at New York's Bowery Ballroom on August 9, 2025, as part of a set recreating the band's iconic early material, heightening the song's rebellious tone through amplified guitar solos and crowd interaction without deviating from its compositional framework.[68][69] Covers and tributes largely confine themselves to indie, punk, and tribute scenes, lacking mainstream commercial traction or chart performance, which underscores the song's enduring but specialized appeal among fans valuing its uncompromised edge over broader accessibility.[70]
