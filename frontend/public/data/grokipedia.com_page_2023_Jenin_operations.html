2023 Jenin operations

The 2023 Jenin operations encompassed a series of Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) counter-terrorism raids targeting militant strongholds in the Jenin refugee camp and surrounding areas of the northern West Bank, with the largest incursion occurring from July 3 to 5 under the codename Operation Home and Garden.[1][2] These actions, involving over 1,000 troops including engineering units, border police, and aerial support via drones and helicopters, sought to neutralize armed networks affiliated with Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), Hamas, and other groups responsible for launching shooting attacks and roadside bombs against Israeli targets, amid a surge of over 1,300 terror incidents in the West Bank that year.[3][1] Key outcomes included the confirmed elimination of 12 militantsâ€”all combatants per IDF assessments, with at least nine publicly claimed by PIJ and Hamasâ€”the demolition of six explosives manufacturing laboratories, eight weapon storage facilities, hundreds of improvised explosive devices (IEDs), road barricades, and tunnels used for militant operations, and the arrest of several suspects who had previously evaded capture.[4][5][1] One IDF soldier died from friendly fire during the operation's conclusion, while Palestinian medical reports recorded over 100 wounded, predominantly from militant engagements in densely fortified positions such as a mosque rigged with explosives and subterranean tunnels.[4][6] The raids inflicted significant damage to camp infrastructure, including water and electricity networks, displacing thousands temporarily and prompting claims of disproportionate force from UN officials and Palestinian authorities, though Israeli evaluations emphasized precision strikes to minimize risks to ground forces confronting booby-trapped environments that had enabled prior attacks killing Israeli civilians.[3][2]

Background

Jenin refugee camp as a hub of militancy

The Jenin refugee camp, established in 1953 by the United Nations to house Palestinian refugees displaced during the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, spans approximately 0.42 square kilometers and accommodates around 14,000 residents in densely packed conditions.[7] Over decades, the camp has evolved into a primary base for Palestinian militant groups, including Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), Hamas, and local factions like the Jenin Brigades, which operate as an umbrella network coordinating attacks against Israeli targets.[8] [9] These groups exploit the camp's narrow alleys and civilian infrastructure for manufacturing improvised explosive devices (IEDs), storing weapons, and launching ambushes, embedding operations within residential areas to complicate Israeli responses.[10] Historically, Jenin gained notoriety as a militant stronghold during the Second Intifada (2000â€“2005), serving as a launchpad for numerous suicide bombings and shootings into Israel proper.[11] Notable incidents included the March 2002 Matza restaurant suicide bombing in Haifa, perpetrated by militants from Jenin, which killed 15 Israeli civilians, alongside other attacks that prompted Israel's Operation Defensive Shield, culminating in the April 2002 Battle of Jenin where IDF forces dismantled entrenched militant positions prepared with booby traps and fortified hideouts.[12] The camp's militants, numbering in the dozens at the time, coordinated with broader networks to export violence, contributing to over 1,000 Israeli deaths during the intifada, with Jenin accounting for a disproportionate share of operational planning and recruitment.[11] This period solidified Jenin's reputation as a "capital of Palestinian resistance," where armed factions filled governance vacuums left by the Palestinian Authority, fostering a culture of confrontation over negotiation.[13] In the years preceding the 2023 operations, Jenin intensified as a hub for escalating militancy amid a surge in West Bank violence, with Israeli security assessments attributing over 50% of attacks against Israelis in 2022â€“2023 to the Jenin area, including shootings, stabbings, and IED ambushes originating from the camp.[14] The Jenin Brigades, formed around 2021 as a cross-factional alliance dominated by PIJ and Al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigades (Fatah-affiliated), claimed responsibility for multiple assaults, such as the January 2024 ramming and shooting attack near a checkpoint that killed an Israeli police officer, reflecting patterns of cross-border raids and planned "major attacks" thwarted by preemptive arrests.[15] [16] PIJ and Hamas cells in the camp manufactured explosives used in ambushes on IDF patrols and civilian routes, with documented stockpiles including assault rifles, RPGs, and labs for IED production, sustaining a cycle where local youth recruitment sustained operational capacity despite repeated raids.[17] [8] This persistent activity, unchecked by Palestinian Authority enforcement, positioned Jenin as the epicenter of a broader militant ecosystem in northern Samaria, necessitating large-scale Israeli interventions to neutralize imminent threats.[10]

Terrorist attacks and threats prompting operations

In the years leading up to 2023, the Jenin refugee camp emerged as a primary operational hub for Palestinian militants affiliated with organizations such as Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) and Hamas, who manufactured improvised explosive devices (IEDs), conducted shooting attacks, and planned assaults targeting Israeli civilians and security personnel along major roads in the northern West Bank. Israeli security forces documented that Jenin-based terrorists were responsible for the majority of deadly attacks against Israelis over the preceding two years, including vehicular rammings, stabbings, and gunfire ambushes that resulted in multiple fatalities.[14] These threats escalated in late 2022 and early 2023 amid a broader wave of terrorism originating from the Jenin area, where militants exploited the camp's dense urban layout to store weapons, train operatives, and coordinate cross-border incursions. Specific risks included the planting of explosives along Route 60 and other thoroughfares used by Israeli commuters, as well as the orchestration of drive-by shootings that killed or wounded dozens. By mid-2023, Israeli assessments indicated that nearly half of all lethal attacks in the West Bank traced back to Jenin militants, with the camp serving as a safe haven for bomb-making labs and command centers.[18] Prior to the January 26, 2023, operation, intelligence revealed an imminent PIJ-led terrorist plot by a squad operating from the camp, necessitating targeted arrests to avert an attack on Israeli targets; the raid focused on apprehending three key PIJ operatives linked to prior assaults.[19] Similarly, by July 2023, over 50 shooting incidents had been launched from Jenin since January, alongside ongoing IED threats that endangered civilian traffic and military patrols, prompting a large-scale incursion to dismantle entrenched networks before further casualties mounted. These patterns of aggression, including the use of the camp for launching rockets and coordinating with external terror groups, underscored the causal link between unchecked militancy in Jenin and the surge in Israeli victims, as verified through forensic tracing of weapons and perpetrator affiliations.[18][20]

January 2023 operation

Raid execution and tactics

The raid commenced on January 26, 2023, when Israel Defense Forces (IDF) and Yamam counterterrorism units of the Israel Border Police entered Jenin refugee camp to apprehend operatives of a local Palestinian Islamic Jihad cell suspected of plotting imminent terrorist attacks.[21][22] Initial approach relied on deception tactics, including an armoured vehicle disguised as a civilian commercial van to facilitate undetected entry and surprise arrests amid the camp's urban density.[23] When armed Palestinians detected and fired upon the disguised vehicle, IDF forces immediately returned fire, transitioning the operation into a sustained four-hour firefight involving small arms exchanges and targeted neutralization of gunmen.[23][22] Tactics emphasized rapid response and force protection in close-quarters combat, with troops leveraging superior firepower to suppress militant positions while minimizing exposure in the narrow, booby-trapped alleyways of the camp.[24] No aerial support or heavy engineering assets were reported in use, distinguishing the incursion from larger-scale operations.[19] The IDF characterized the engagement as defensive, stating forces fired only after being targeted by multiple gunmen.[22]

Casualties, arrests, and immediate outcomes

During the January 26, 2023, raid on Jenin refugee camp, Israeli forces killed nine Palestinians amid exchanges of gunfire with armed militants, according to the Palestinian Ministry of Health and reports from multiple international outlets.[22][19][23] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) stated that troops neutralized three members of a Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) cell planning an imminent attack, with eight of the deceased linked to terrorist organizations, including gunmen who fired on soldiers during the operation.[25][26] Palestinian sources described the deaths as including civilians, such as an elderly woman caught in the clashes, though the IDF maintained that the force used was in response to direct threats from armed suspects.[27][28] At least 10 to 20 Palestinians were wounded by live ammunition, with some in critical condition requiring hospitalization.[27] No Israeli casualties were reported.[25] In terms of arrests, the IDF apprehended at least one additional suspect from the targeted PIJ cell, who surrendered during the confrontation, as part of efforts to dismantle the group accused of plotting a major assault.[25][19] The operation's immediate outcomes included the elimination of key PIJ operatives, described by the IDF as averting a "ticking time bomb" terrorist attack, thereby disrupting militant infrastructure in the camp without reported destruction of broader civilian assets or prolonged presence.[29][22] This raid marked the deadliest single-day incident in the West Bank since 2005, escalating tensions but achieving the primary objective of neutralizing the planned threat according to Israeli assessments.[19][25]

July 2023 operation

Objectives and initial deployment

The July 2023 operation in Jenin, codenamed Operation Home and Garden by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF), aimed primarily to dismantle terrorist infrastructure and neutralize armed militants operating from the Jenin refugee camp, a longstanding base for groups such as Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) and Hamas affiliates responsible for planning attacks against Israeli civilians and security forces.[30][1] Israeli military officials stated the goals included destroying explosive devices, weapons caches, and manufacturing labs used for improvised explosive devices (IEDs), as well as eliminating key militants involved in recent terror activities, without intending to establish long-term ground control in the area.[31][32] The operation was framed as a preemptive measure to counter the camp's role as a command center for coordinated attacks, following a surge in West Bank terrorism earlier in 2023.[18] Initial deployment commenced in the early hours of July 3, 2023, around 1:00 AM local time, with IDF forces initiating aerial strikes using drones on multiple targets within the Jenin refugee camp to suppress militant activity and destroy suspected weapon sites.[33] This was followed by a ground incursion involving hundreds of troops, including IDF infantry brigades, combat engineering units specialized in explosive ordnance disposal, and elite counter-terrorism forces such as Yamam, who advanced into the densely populated camp to conduct targeted raids and searches.[1][34] Armored vehicles and bulldozers supported the entry to clear paths obstructed by barricades and potential IEDs, marking the largest-scale IDF deployment in Jenin since 2002.[18] The initial phase focused on securing perimeters and engaging armed resistance, with reports of exchanges of fire as forces located hidden pits containing explosives and munitions.[34]

Tactics, engagements, and duration

The July 2023 operation in Jenin, designated Operation Home and Garden by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF), initiated in the predawn hours of July 3 with Israeli Air Force drone strikes on militant infrastructure within the Jenin refugee camp, marking the first aerial bombardment in the area since the Second Intifada.[35][36] This was followed by the deployment of approximately 1,000 ground troops, comprising infantry brigades, Yahalom special engineering units, and Israel Border Police, who advanced into the camp and adjacent neighborhoods to conduct targeted raids.[37][38] Tactics emphasized intelligence-driven precision to minimize risks from the camp's dense urban layout and prevalent militant tactics, such as roadside bombs and ambushes, which had evolved to incorporate Gaza-style IED networks and tunnel precursors.[39] Armored D9 bulldozers were systematically deployed to excavate and dismantle asphalt roads, exposing and neutralizing buried IEDs that militants had embedded as defensive measures; this engineering approach, honed from prior West Bank experiences, cleared pathways for troop maneuvers while destroying at least six confirmed IED production sites.[40][41] Ground elements conducted house-to-house searches, explosive ordnance disposal, and demolition of booby-trapped structures, with UAVs providing real-time overwatch for ongoing threat detection.[2] Engagements were primarily short-range firefights and targeted eliminations rather than sustained urban combat, reflecting superior IDF intelligence penetration that preempted major militant concentrations.[42] Militants from Palestinian Islamic Jihad and affiliated groups attempted counteractions via small-arms fire and improvised explosives, resulting in the deaths of several operatives, including mid-level commanders; one IDF soldier was killed in a direct clash.[38][41] Over 100 arrest raids yielded dozens of suspects and weapons caches, with minimal reported civilian infrastructure collateral beyond tactical demolitions.[43] The incursion spanned roughly 48 hours of active operations, from the initial strikes on July 3 through intensified ground actions on July 4, with IDF forces completing withdrawals from Jenin city and the camp by early July 5, allowing for a phased disengagement without prolonged occupation.[44][36] This compressed timeline underscored the operation's focus on surgical disruption of militant networks over territorial control, contrasting with the protracted 2002 Battle of Jenin.[35]

Casualties, infrastructure destruction, and neutralized threats

During the July 2023 operation in Jenin, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) reported eliminating 13 Palestinian militants, including key figures such as a bomb-maker and members of the Jenin Brigades affiliated with Palestinian Islamic Jihad and other groups.[45] Palestinian health authorities reported 12 total deaths, with militant groups claiming at least eight of the deceased as their fighters, while IDF assessments identified all fatalities as combatants involved in recent attacks on Israelis.[44] [5] No Israeli soldiers were killed, though several were wounded by improvised explosive devices (IEDs) during engagements.[46] Infrastructure destruction targeted militant facilities, with IDF forces demolishing 14 apartments used as terror bases, three command and control centers, and sites for weapon production and explosive storage using armored bulldozers to neutralize booby-trapped structures and IEDs embedded in roads.[45] [47] These actions also severed temporary access to water and electricity in parts of the camp to limit militant mobility, though services were restored post-operation.[48] Palestinian reports highlighted damage to civilian roads and homes from airstrikes and ground maneuvers, but IDF operations focused on verified militant sites to dismantle entrenched networks.[49] Neutralized threats included the arrest of approximately 30 confirmed terrorists and over 50 suspects, alongside the seizure and destruction of explosive devices, rifles, and ammunition caches that had been used in attacks originating from Jenin.[45] [5] IDF engineering units dismantled multiple IEDs and explosive labs, reducing the camp's capacity for launching assaults, as evidenced by the prior neutralization of rocket and mortar threats from the area.[46] [20] These measures addressed Jenin's role as a primary source of over 100 terrorist incidents against Israel in the preceding months.[45]

Reactions

Israeli perspectives

Israeli officials justified the January 2023 Jenin raid as a targeted operation to apprehend suspects linked to recent terrorist attacks, including a January 26 shooting in East Jerusalem that killed seven Israelis. The IDF reported that troops came under fire from Palestinian gunmen during the raid, prompting return fire that neutralized several armed militants, with at least six of the nine killed identified as combatants.[22] Israeli authorities emphasized the raid's precision amid heightened threats from Jenin, where militants had orchestrated multiple shootings and bombings in prior weeks, underscoring the necessity of proactive measures to disrupt attack planning.[22] In the July 2023 operation, dubbed "Home and Garden," Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu described Jenin as having evolved into a "city of refuge for terrorism" over recent months, necessitating an extensive counter-terrorism effort to prevent it from reverting to that status post-operation.[50] Defense Minister Yoav Gallant highlighted the IDF's success in delivering a "heavy blow" to terror networks, noting that the two-day incursion dismantled explosive manufacturing sites, seized weapons, and arrested over 20 suspects, while most gunmen fled or concealed themselves among civilians, revealing their reliance on human shields.[51] [52] Netanyahu affirmed that forces operated with a "maximum effort to avoid harming non-combatants," framing the action as establishing a "new equation against terrorism" rather than a isolated event.[53] Gallant concluded that Jenin was no longer a "factory for the production of terrorism," crediting the operation with restoring IDF freedom of movement in the area.[54] Both operations were portrayed by Israeli leadership as defensive responses to an escalating militant threat from Jenin camp, which had launched dozens of attacks on Israeli civilians and security forces in 2022-2023, justifying the use of air support, engineering units, and infantry to neutralize entrenched threats without broader escalation.[55] Officials stressed empirical gains, such as the elimination of key figures from Palestinian Islamic Jihad and Hamas and the destruction of terror infrastructure, as evidence of operational effectiveness in safeguarding Israeli lives.[52]

Palestinian and militant responses

The Palestinian Authority suspended security coordination with Israel following the January 26 raid, citing the deaths of nine Palestinians as a violation warranting the halt in joint efforts against militants.[56][57] Palestinian officials described the operation as an escalation of Israeli aggression, threatening broader diplomatic measures while emphasizing the need for continued resistance against occupation.[58] Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ), a primary target of the raid, affirmed its fighters had engaged Israeli forces with gunfire and explosives, vowing a determined response to the elimination of key operatives planning attacks.[59][22] Hamas, alongside PIJ, pledged retaliation from Gaza, with spokesmen stating that West Bank armed groups would fulfill their duty to defend Palestinians and that the raid justified escalated clashes.[21][60] Local Jenin militants, including Al-Aqsa Martyrs Brigades affiliates, claimed the deceased as their own, framing them as martyrs resisting incursion into the refugee camp.[61] Residents expressed defiance, pledging to persist in combating the occupation despite the raid's scale, the largest since 2002.[62] In response to the July 3-5 operation, the Palestinian Authority announced a freeze on all ties with Israel beyond security coordination, which had already been suspended, attributing the incursion to systematic aggression against West Bank civilians and infrastructure.[63] PIJ and Hamas claimed most of the 12 killed Palestiniansâ€”eight by PIJ and one by Hamasâ€”as their fighters, portraying the deaths as sacrifices fueling ongoing armed resistance and condemning the destruction of militant sites as futile suppression.[64] Hamas linked subsequent Gaza rocket fire to the Jenin events, describing it as part of a unified militant reprisal against the raid's tactics, including airstrikes and bulldozing.[65] Palestinian residents and armed factions in Jenin voiced anger and resolve, rejecting the operation's aims and vowing reconstruction of militant networks amid reports of over 100 injuries and widespread camp damage.[66][64]

International reactions

The United Nations voiced concerns regarding civilian impacts during the January 2023 Jenin raid, with Special Rapporteurs condemning the Israeli military actions as part of renewed violence in the occupied West Bank that resulted in Palestinian deaths.[67] For the July operation, UN Secretary-General AntÃ³nio Guterres expressed deep alarm over the airstrikes and ground incursion, stressing that all military actions must adhere to international humanitarian law, while UN human rights experts indicated that the strikes and operations, which killed at least 12 Palestinians, may prima facie amount to war crimes due to their impact on civilian areas.[65][68] Guterres later refused Israel's demand to retract his criticism of the raid.[69] The United States supported Israel's right to counterterrorism operations in both instances. Following the January raid, which killed nine Palestinians including militants and civilians, the State Department extended condolences, affirmed Israel's defense rights against attacks originating from Jenin, and called for investigations into civilian casualties while urging de-escalation.[70] In July, U.S. officials backed the operation as a legitimate response to terrorism, contrasting with broader international condemnations.[5] European Union representatives criticized the operations' scope and proportionality. The EU High Representative expressed strong concern over escalating tensions and civilian casualties in Jenin, urging restraint from both sides.[71] After touring the Jenin camp post-July raid, EU envoy Mario Nava stated that the Israeli Defense Forces' actions violated principles of proportionality and distinction under international law.[72] Arab states and organizations issued sharp rebukes, particularly for the July incursion. The Arab League condemned Israel's use of excessive force in Jenin as tantamount to war crimes, holding an emergency session to address the airstrikes and ground assault that displaced thousands.[73] Jordan described the operation as a violation of international humanitarian law, while other Arab nations echoed calls for halting such actions despite normalization efforts under the Abraham Accords.[5][74] Countries like Egypt and Saudi Arabia similarly denounced the raids for exacerbating Palestinian suffering without addressing root security threats posed by militant networks.[16]

Controversies

Allegations of excessive force and civilian casualties

Palestinian health authorities reported 12 deaths during the July 3â€“5, 2023, operation in Jenin refugee camp, including at least two minors and claims of civilian casualties amid clashes.[75] The United Nations Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR) alleged that Israeli airstrikes and ground operations, involving explosive weapons in a densely populated area, may have constituted war crimes due to the risk to civilians, with over 100 injured and thousands temporarily displaced from damaged homes and infrastructure.[68] UN Secretary-General AntÃ³nio Guterres condemned the operations as involving excessive force, urging adherence to international humanitarian law.[65] Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) statements asserted that at least 10 of the 12 killed were militants affiliated with groups such as Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) and Hamas, including eight PIJ members claimed as "martyrs" by the Jenin Battalion, a PIJ offshoot; these individuals were linked to recent attacks on Israelis, with operations targeting command centers, explosive devices, and weapon caches embedded in civilian areas.[5][76] The IDF emphasized precision tactics, including drones and bulldozers, to neutralize booby-trapped routes and IEDs that posed immediate threats to troops, minimizing soldier casualties (one Israeli killed by friendly fire) while detaining over 100 suspects and destroying terror infrastructure like labs producing explosives.[18] Allegations of civilian deaths, particularly minors, were contested; for instance, 16-year-old Noureddin Marshoud, killed during the raid, was mourned by family as unarmed but hailed as a martyr by militants, reflecting common involvement of youth in Jenin-based armed groups operating from populated zones.[77] Palestinian sources and advocacy groups like Defense for Children International-Palestine claimed four children killed, but IDF investigations attributed some incidents to crossfire or militant explosives, with no verified evidence of deliberate targeting of non-combatants.[78] Extensive infrastructure damageâ€”affecting water, electricity, and roadsâ€”was cited by critics as collective punishment, yet IDF reports detailed it as necessary to dismantle militant networks that exploit civilian density for cover, a pattern documented in Jenin as a launchpad for over 30 attacks in 2023 prior to the operation.[79] Human rights organizations, including those aligned with Palestinian perspectives, amplified claims of disproportionate force, but empirical breakdowns from militant admissions and IDF identifications indicate most casualties were combatants in active engagements, underscoring challenges of urban counterterrorism where groups like PIJ base operations amid civilians, increasing incidental risks.[30] No independent forensic verification confirmed excessive or indiscriminate attacks, with operations framed by Israel as targeted responses to escalating West Bank terrorism rather than punitive measures.[40]

Debates on operational effectiveness and necessity

Israeli security officials and analysts argued that the July 2023 operation in Jenin was necessary to address a surge in terrorist activity originating from the Jenin refugee camp, which had become a primary hub for Iranian-backed Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) and other militant networks responsible for multiple attacks on Israeli civilians and soldiers earlier in the year.[5] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) cited the camp's role as a "refuge for terrorism," with pre-operation intelligence identifying approximately 150 wanted suspects and up to 300 potential combatants involved in manufacturing improvised explosive devices (IEDs) and planning shootings.[80] Proponents emphasized that the raid's tactical successesâ€”including the elimination of 12 militants, arrest of over 120 suspects, destruction of 10 explosive labs, and seizure of weapons, rifles, and explosive materialsâ€”directly neutralized immediate threats and disrupted operational capabilities, thereby preventing further attacks in the short term.[80][55] From an Israeli perspective, the operation's effectiveness was evidenced by minimal armed resistance from militants, many of whom fled the camp, allowing IDF forces to achieve objectives with precision engineering and aerial support while limiting broader escalation.[1] The Institute for National Security Studies (INSS) assessed it as a targeted effort to dismantle infrastructure and restore deterrence in the northern West Bank, enabling subsequent smaller-scale raids without the need for large deployments.[2] These gains were framed as essential given the 2023 spike in West Bank terrorism, with Jenin-linked groups implicated in deadly incidents, underscoring the operation's role in causal disruption of attack cycles rather than mere punitive action. Critics, including United Nations experts and Palestinian analysts, contended that the raid's necessity was overstated, portraying it as disproportionate and unlikely to yield lasting security benefits due to underlying socioeconomic and political drivers of militancy.[68] They argued that while short-term degradation occurred, the destruction of civilian infrastructure and reported civilian casualties fueled recruitment and radicalization, potentially increasing long-term threats, as evidenced by recurrent militant activity in Jenin requiring further IDF interventions in late 2023 and 2024.[41] Assessments from security think tanks noted that, absent complementary political or governance measures, such operations provide only temporary pauses in violence, with Jenin remaining a militant stronghold post-raid.[1] This view highlights debates over whether tactical successes translate to strategic necessity, given the pattern of threat regeneration in ungoverned spaces like the camp.

Media and narrative framing

International media coverage of the January and July 2023 Israeli operations in Jenin emphasized the humanitarian toll, including Palestinian casualties, displacement of approximately 3,000 residents from the Jenin refugee camp during the July incursion, and widespread infrastructure damage such as bulldozed roads and destroyed buildings.[16] Outlets like CNN described the January 26 raid, which killed nine Palestinians, as the "deadliest day in over a year" in the West Bank, focusing on the scale of violence without initially detailing the targeting of a terror cell linked to planned attacks on Israelis.[19][81] Such framing often relied on Palestinian sources, including the Health Ministry, for casualty figuresâ€”claiming civilian deaths and injuries without independent verificationâ€”while downplaying Israeli military statements that most fatalities were militants involved in recent attacks, with Jenin responsible for over 30 such incidents against Israelis earlier in 2023.[82] For instance, CBS News led with a headline amplifying Palestinian assertions of Israel "bombing innocent people" in Jenin, sidelining IDF reports of operations against explosive devices and armed groups.[83] Critiques from media watchdogs highlighted omissions of context, such as Iran's funding of Jenin-based terror groups like Palestinian Islamic Jihad and the camp's role as a militant stronghold with improvised explosive factories and smuggling routes, which justified the scale of the July operation involving airstrikes and engineering units to neutralize threats.[84][82] Arab-aligned outlets like Al Jazeera portrayed the actions as a "siege" or unprovoked assault, questioning Israel's goals and implying inevitable escalation, aligning with a narrative of resistance against occupation rather than counter-terrorism.[85] Israeli media, conversely, stressed operational successes, such as the elimination of key militants and disruption of attack networks, framing the raids as necessary responses to a surge in West Bank terrorism that had claimed multiple Israeli lives.[82] This divergence reflects broader patterns where Western mainstream reporting, drawing heavily from unverified local accounts amid institutional biases favoring sympathetic Palestinian portrayals, underemphasized verifiable militant affiliations among the deceasedâ€”later confirmed in many cases by IDF evidenceâ€”and the pre-operation civilian evacuation warnings issued to mitigate harm.[83][82]

Aftermath and impact

Security and terrorism trends post-operations

Following the major Israeli military operations in Jenin in January and July 2023, which dismantled explosive manufacturing labs and neutralized over 20 terrorists affiliated with Palestinian Islamic Jihad and other groups, security trends in the northern West Bank, including Jenin, showed a marked decline in significant terror attacks. In 2024, significant attacks across Judea and Samaria dropped 70%, from 847 in 2023 to 254, while less lethal incidents fell from 3,256 to 1,188.[86][87] This reduction was linked to sustained IDF and Shin Bet counter-terrorism efforts, including daily raids, drone strikes, and the elimination of over 800 terrorists since October 7, 2023, alongside 6,000 arrests, of which 2,350 involved Hamas affiliates.[86][87] Jenin, a persistent hub for arms smuggling and IED production, contributed disproportionately to pre-operation attacks, with many originating from its refugee camp. Post-2023, the intensified operational tempoâ€”exemplified by Operation Bayit VeGan in Julyâ€”disrupted local networks, reducing the frequency of shootings and bombings targeting Israeli civilians and forces. Shin Bet data indicated 1,040 thwarted significant attacks in 2024, including 689 shootings, reflecting proactive intelligence-driven measures that curtailed Jenin's output.[86] Israeli fatalities from West Bank terrorism stabilized at 37 in 2024, down slightly from 41 in 2023, amid 497 Palestinians killed in clashes, 97% identified as combatants by IDF assessments.[86][87] Nevertheless, terrorism in Jenin persisted, fueled by Iranian and Hamas funding for weapons and recruitment, necessitating repeated incursions into 2024 and beyond. Broader 2024 data recorded over 6,300 total incidents against Jewish targets in Judea and Samaria, including minor assaults, underscoring that while major threats diminished, low-level violence and rebuilding efforts by militants required ongoing vigilance.[88] Jenin's status as a "terror capital" prompted coordinated IDF-PA actions against local cells, though full eradication remained elusive due to entrenched militant infrastructure.[87][89]

Reconstruction efforts and ongoing challenges

Following the Israeli military's withdrawal from Jenin refugee camp on July 5, 2023, local residents initiated preliminary rebuilding of damaged structures, focusing on homes and basic roadways amid widespread destruction that affected over 1,000 residences, including 41 fully demolished and more than 70 severely damaged.[90][91] A Palestinian Authority ministerial committee estimated reconstruction costs at $15.5 million, encompassing repairs to 8 kilometers of water pipes, 3 kilometers of sewage lines, over 100 houses, and several schools.[92] UNRWA conducted urgent assessments and repairs, enabling the reopening of its four schools in the camp by July 8, 2023, to restore access for students.[93] International pledges surpassed the initial damage estimate, totaling over $45 million by mid-July 2023, including $15 million from the United Arab Emirates channeled through UNRWA and $30 million from Algeria, with additional commitments anticipated from Qatar and private donors.[92] UNRWA launched a $23.8 million flash appeal to fund emergency cash assistance for 1,300 families, rental subsidies for 41 displaced households, shelter rehabilitation for approximately 100 units, and reconstruction of a health center, in coordination with agencies like OCHA, WHO, and UNICEF.[91] However, implementation faced delays due to unexploded ordnance risks and incomplete damage assessments in the densely populated camp, home to about 18,000 residents in 0.43 square kilometers.[91] Reconstruction has been severely hampered by recurrent Israeli military operations in Jenin governorate through 2025, which have inflicted cumulative damage to 43 percent of the camp's structures according to UNOSAT assessments, alongside disruptions to water, sanitation, and electricity infrastructure affecting thousands.[94] These incursions, numbering nearly 7,500 across the West Bank by October 2025, have displaced up to 40,000 residents from Jenin and adjacent camps like Tulkarm and Nur Shams since early 2025, preventing sustained repairs and exacerbating shortages of potable water and power.[95][96] Compounding factors include a Palestinian Authority security operation launched in late 2024 against local militants, which displaced around 2,000 families by January 2025, and local fears of aid fund mismanagement by Ramallah authorities, with residents like activist Mona al-Rimawi advocating for independent oversight committees to ensure transparency.[97][92] Underfunding of broader humanitarian appeals, at less than 20 percent for the 2023 oPt emergency response, has further limited recovery efforts.[91]
