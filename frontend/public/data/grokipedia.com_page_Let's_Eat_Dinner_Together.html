Let's Eat Dinner Together

Let's Eat Dinner Together, also known as Dining Together, is a South Korean reality variety television program that aired on JTBC from October 19, 2016, to February 26, 2020.[1] The series stars comedians Lee Kyung-kyu and Kang Ho-dong as hosts who, accompanied by rotating celebrity guests, traverse unfamiliar neighborhoods to solicit invitations from residents to join them for dinner, blending documentary-style exploration with comedic interactions.[2][3] The format emphasizes the rarity of shared family meals in modern urban South Korea, where solo living and fast-paced lifestyles prevail, by documenting the hosts' door-to-door quests and the ensuing meals in everyday homes.[4] Spanning 164 episodes each approximately 100 minutes in length, the show featured diverse guests from K-pop idols to actors, fostering humorous and occasionally poignant encounters that highlighted cultural norms around hospitality and communal eating.[1] It received acclaim for its lighthearted approach to social observation, achieving consistent viewership and an IMDb user rating of 8.0 out of 10 based on viewer feedback.[2] While largely controversy-free, the program's reliance on unscripted civilian participation occasionally led to episodes being edited or withheld at participants' requests to protect privacy.[5]

Hosts and Regular Cast

Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu hosted Let's Eat Dinner Together from its premiere on October 19, 2016, until the final episode on June 17, 2020, forming the core duo known as the "Gyu-dong brothers" for their comedic synergy and on-screen rapport.[1][2] Kang, born August 11, 1970, brought his background as a retired ssireum wrestler and veteran MC from programs like 1 Night 2 Days to the role, often providing boisterous energy and physical humor.[6] Lee, born June 17, 1960, contributed sharp wit and observational comedy drawn from his experience hosting shows such as Gag Concert, frequently playing the straight man to Kang's antics.[6] The format did not feature a fixed regular cast beyond these two MCs; instead, each of the 188 episodes paired them with a rotating guest celebrityâ€”typically actors, idols, or comediansâ€”who assisted in approaching strangers for dinner invitations under strict rules limiting resources to 3,000 South Korean won (about $2.50 USD at 2016 exchange rates).[2][1] Notable recurring guests included figures like Song Min-ho and Im Soo-hyang, but no permanent ensemble existed, emphasizing the hosts' consistent presence as the program's anchor.[2] This structure highlighted the duo's improvisational skills, with episodes filmed in various South Korean neighborhoods to capture authentic interactions.[6]

Production

Development and Launch

The program was developed by JTBC's entertainment division as a reality variety show featuring veteran hosts Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu, marking their first joint project in 23 years. Chief producer Yoon Hyun-jun oversaw the initiative, with PD Bang Hyun-young handling direction, aiming to capture unscripted interactions in everyday neighborhoods through the hosts' quest for an invitation to dinner.[7][8] A press conference occurred on October 19, 2016, at JTBC's headquarters in Sangam-dong, Mapo-gu, Seoul, where the hosts discussed the format's emphasis on authentic encounters with residents, including door-knocking and casual conversations to secure a meal.[7][8] The event highlighted the duo's dynamic, with Lee Kyung-kyu noting the physical and mental demands of filming alongside Kang Ho-dong's energetic style.[8] "Let's Eat Dinner Together" premiered on the same day, October 19, 2016, airing Wednesdays at 10:50 p.m. KST on JTBC, positioning it as a late-night documentary-style variety program focused on human connections via shared meals.[9][10] Initial episodes emphasized the hosts' navigation of urban areas, relying on charisma rather than pre-arranged setups to engage locals.[11]

Filming and Logistics

The production team for Let's Eat Dinner Together selected unfamiliar residential neighborhoods across South Korea for each episode, with filming commencing in the evening after sunset to align with typical dinner times and homes displaying lit windows as indicators of occupancy. Hosts Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu, often accompanied by celebrity guests, were equipped with a single spoon each as a symbolic prop and tasked with door-to-door canvassing to solicit meal invitations from strangers, capturing the search process in a documentary-style format. Initial episodes, such as the premiere, were shot in areas like Mangwon-dong in Seoul's Mapo-gu district.[12][13] Logistical operations involved a large crew, reported by participants and observers to number in the dozens, including camera operators, sound technicians, and production staff who trailed the hosts while minimizing visible intrusion to maintain the spontaneous feel. This scale occasionally caused disruptions in confined urban or rural alleys, blocking pathways and drawing resident complaints about noise and privacy invasion during late-hour shoots, which typically lasted around three hours per episode. Staff accounts highlight variations in efficiency, with some filming wrapping up earlier under certain host dynamics, allowing quicker demobilization.[14] Special episodes extended logistics internationally, such as outings in Tokyo with guests like Sandara Park and Lee Hong-gi, requiring coordinated travel, local permissions, and adapted protocols for foreign residential areas. The crew emphasized real-time captures of interactions without scripted outcomes, though post-production editing focused on successful dinners and personal anecdotes, discarding failed searches to fit the 80-minute runtime. No compensation was publicly detailed for participating households, preserving the show's premise of organic encounters, though celebrity guests' involvement sometimes amplified on-site crowds and security needs.[15]

Broadcast and Episodes

Original Run (2016â€“2020)

The original run of Let's Eat Dinner Together premiered on JTBC on October 19, 2016, airing weekly on Wednesday evenings at 8:50 p.m. KST for approximately 100 minutes per episode.[2][1] Hosted by Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu, the program featured the duo traveling to rural or suburban areas in South Korea to seek out locals willing to share a home-cooked meal, often involving humorous interactions and challenges to secure invitations. Over its course, the show produced 164 episodes, maintaining a consistent format that emphasized spontaneous encounters and communal dining.[1] Viewership ratings for the series were stable within JTBC's variety lineup, frequently ranging from 3% to 5% in nationwide metrics, with peaks during guest appearances by popular idols or celebrities that drew higher audiences in key demographics.[20] The program's appeal lay in its low-stakes, relatable premise amid urban isolation themes, contributing to its longevity as a mid-tier hit for the cable network, though it never dominated charts like flagship JTBC dramas.[21] Production of new episodes halted on January 27, 2020, amid the emerging COVID-19 pandemic, which disrupted on-location filming reliant on public interactions. The series concluded its original airing on February 26, 2020, with pre-recorded content filling the final slots, marking the end of the initial iteration without an official renewal announcement at the time.[1] This closure reflected broader industry shifts toward safer production practices, though the show's format proved resilient for later adaptations.

Reception

Viewership Ratings

The premiere episode of Let's Eat Dinner Together on August 24, 2016, achieved competitive ratings for JTBC's Wednesday late-night slot, ranking first among cable channels per Nielsen Korea measurements, though exact figures for the debut were not publicly detailed in initial reports.[19] Subsequent episodes in the first season built momentum, with viewership frequently topping its time slot against competitors like MBC's Radio Star.[25] Viewership peaked in early 2018, reaching a series high of 7.6% nationwide on the January 31 episode, marking the program's then-record and surging past prior benchmarks with a minute-high of 9.4%.[26] Other strong performers included a January 10, 2018, outing at 6.3%, securing first place in the slot, and a February 28, 2018, installment hitting 6.6%, the year's best amid guest appearances boosting appeal.[27] [28] Episodes featuring high-profile guests, such as BTS members Jin and Jungkook on September 27, 2017, also led their slot, underscoring the impact of celebrity draw on ratings.[25] By late 2018 and into 2019, ratings trended downward amid format repetition and shifting viewer habits, with the 3-year anniversary special on October 23, 2019, drawing 3.084% among paid households.[29] Some 2019 episodes dipped to the low 2% range, a level last seen in mid-2017, reflecting a seven-week low not breached since July 2019.[30] Recovery attempts, like a April 11, 2018, episode rebounding to the mid-5% range, proved temporary, as sustained highs eluded the program in later seasons.[31] Overall, while early success positioned it as a JTBC staple, declining figures contributed to its conclusion after four seasons in 2020, with averages settling below 4% in final years per Nielsen data.[28]

Critical and Viewer Responses

Critics and viewers praised Let's Eat Dinner Together for its authentic depiction of everyday Korean social dynamics and the comedic rapport between hosts Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu, which often highlighted themes of hospitality and community in urban settings.[21] The show's format, involving unscripted door-to-door pleas for dinner invitations, was lauded for generating spontaneous humor and relatable awkwardness, contributing to its sustained popularity during its 164-episode run from 2016 to 2020.[21] Viewer aggregated ratings reflected this appeal, with an 8.0 out of 10 on IMDb from approximately 48 user reviews, emphasizing the program's documentary-like insights into family lives and regional cuisines.[2] Nevertheless, the series drew criticism for elements perceived as invasive or disruptive to participants and neighborhoods. In November 2017, episodes featuring overly personal interrogationsâ€”such as probing family finances or private habitsâ€”prompted viewer complaints about boundary violations, leading to public backlash on social platforms and news outlets.[32] Similar concerns arose over the logistical burdens on hosts and crews, including late-night filming that generated noise and unease among residents, with some online discussions highlighting the ethical issues of unsolicited home intrusions in densely populated areas.[33] A specific incident in one episode involved Lee Kyung-kyu questioning a participant's hourly wage on air, which elicited viewer rebukes for insensitivity and irrelevance to the show's premise. Additional controversies included perceived lapses in tact, such as a September 2019 segment subtly referencing the Mnet vote-rigging scandal involving boy group X1, which netizens deemed inconsiderate toward affected parties and out of step with the program's lighthearted tone.[34] The 2018 year-end special, compiling highlights into "2018 Hankki Awards," underperformed in ratings compared to regular episodes and received tepid feedback for lacking fresh content, with TNMS data showing a dip that underscored viewer fatigue with repetitive formats.[28] Despite these critiques, the show's defenders argued that its unpolished authenticity outweighed flaws, fostering discussions on evolving privacy norms in Korean variety television.[21]

Controversies

In April 2019, the program faced allegations of staging encounters after multiple episodes featured visits to residences of celebrities or public figures, prompting viewer suspicions that interactions declined to provide dinner, followed by appearances at fashion designer couple Steve J and Yoni P's home on May 8, and drummer Choi Min-hwan and singer Yul-hee's residence on November 14.[37][38][39] Producers denied manipulation, attributing the pattern to coincidental filming in urban areas with high concentrations of entertainers and the low acceptance rateâ€”around 1-2%â€”necessitating some cases, checking electricity meters to gauge occupancy without explicit permission, actions viewed by detractors as overly intrusive in densely populated Korean residential zones.[42] This practice, reported in 2019 episodes, amplified concerns over exposing ordinary citizens' living spaces and personal lives to national television, with some residents expressing discomfort at the unsolicited visits despite the show's emphasis on voluntary participation.[43] The production maintained strict protocols, including obtaining consent before filming interiors and avoiding coercion, but the inherent randomness led to occasional rejections and heightened scrutiny of ethical boundaries in reality television.[6] Additional backlash centered on the logistical strain on neighborhoods, where repeated filming crews disrupted daily life and fostered perceptions of the show as a "nuisance" to non-participants, though acceptance rates remained low due to cultural reticence toward balancing entertainment value against individual privacy.[35]

Impact and Legacy

Cultural and Social Reflections

The format of Let's Eat Dinner Together, involving unannounced visits to private homes for shared meals, underscores a cultural fascination in South Korea with peering into others' domestic lives, where residences symbolize personal achievement, aesthetic preferences, and socioeconomic status. This curiosity has propelled the program's popularity, as homes serve as intimate reflections of urban dwellers' realities amid high-density living and environmental factors like fine dust that confine people indoors, heightening interest in alternative lifestyles. Yet, the frequent rejections encountered by hosts highlight entrenched privacy norms and wariness toward strangers, contrasting traditional Korean hospitalityâ€”rooted in communal sharingâ€”with contemporary urban anonymity and safety apprehensions.[43] Episodes often reveal unscripted glimpses into family dynamics, such as generational caregiving, work-related stresses, and everyday rituals around modest home-cooked meals, fostering viewer empathy by humanizing ordinary struggles in a high-pressure society marked by long hours and declining communal ties. By facilitating dialogues on topics like retirement transitions or pet therapy initiatives born from personal loss, the show promotes interpersonal bridging, positioning shared food as a conduit for understanding in an era of social fragmentation. This aligns with broader cultural resonances, evoking concepts like sohwaehaeng (small, certain happiness) derived from simple interactions, which gained traction among younger audiences disillusioned with systemic pressures.[45][46] Over its 164-episode run from 2016 to 2020, achieving viewership ratings between 2.4% and 6.8%, the program captured a nostalgic essence of celebrating everyday resilience in working-class neighborhoods, but it also drew scrutiny for probing personal matters too invasively, exposing tensions between entertainment value and individual boundaries. Successors like One Meal Show confront amplified resistance, with declining ratings (from 3.1% premiere to 1.9% by July 2025) signaling societal shifts toward individualism, post-pandemic caution, and preference for affluent locales over relatable ones, as noted by critic Jung Duk-hyun, who views the original as an authentic extension of home-invasion formats now strained by authenticity deficits in a streaming-dominated landscape. These evolutions reflect Korea's transition from collective warmth to guarded detachment, where door-knocking symbolizes both potential hospitality and the cultural boundaries shaping interactions with outsiders.[21][47]

Post-Cancellation Developments and Revival

In 2025, JTBC launched One Meal Show as a spiritual sequel to Let's Eat Dinner Together, adapting the core concept of hosts and guests randomly visiting neighborhoods to share meals prepared from residents' refrigerators.[21] The program premiered on July 10, 2025, with new hosts Kim Hee-sun and Tak Jae-hoon, alongside celebrity chef Lee Yeon-bok, who contributed to meal preparation and storytelling segments.[48] Unlike the original's focus on everyday families and organic interactions led by Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu, the revival emphasized glamorous cooking and narrative-driven visits, debuting to a 3.1% nationwide rating.[21] The series aired weekly on Wednesdays at 8:50 p.m. KST for seven episodes, concluding on August 26, 2025, amid declining viewership that fell to 1.9% by the fourth episode on July 31.[48][49] Key challenges included frequent resident refusals to participate, attributed to heightened privacy concerns in post-pandemic South Korea, and criticism for selecting affluent districts like Seongbuk-dong and Gangnam, which distanced the show from the original's relatable, grassroots appeal.[21] Episodes featured celebrity guests such as Ahn Bo-hyun and Lee Kyu-hyung, who shared personal anecdotes during meals, but the format struggled to replicate the spontaneous charm that sustained the predecessor through 164 episodes.[49] No further revivals or direct continuations with the original hosts have materialized as of October 2025, though Kang Ho-dong and Lee Kyung-kyu briefly reunited for unrelated projects in 2023, marking their first on-screen collaboration in years without tying back to the dinner-sharing premise.[6] The One Meal Show's short run highlighted evolving viewer preferences toward more authentic or digitally native content, contributing to its early cancellation despite nostalgic intent.[21]
