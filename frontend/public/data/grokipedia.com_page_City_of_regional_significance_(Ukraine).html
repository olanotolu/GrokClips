City of regional significance (Ukraine)

A city of regional significance in Ukraine was a second-level administrative division, functioning as an urban municipality directly subordinate to an oblast (region) and equivalent in status to a raion (district), rather than being incorporated into a district's territory.[1] These entities encompassed major urban centers that exercised administrative, economic, and self-governance functions independently of raions, with approximately 187 such cities existing prior to major reforms.[1] Established under Soviet-era structures and retained post-independence, they served as hubs for local governance, concentrating significant portions of regional population and economic activity, with over 70% of Ukraine's populace residing in territorial communities or these cities.[2] The status originated from the hierarchical administrative framework inherited from the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic, where cities of oblast significance handled municipal services, budgeting, and development akin to districts but tailored to urban needs.[3] Decentralization efforts beginning in 2014 aimed to enhance local autonomy, leading to amalgamations with surrounding hromadas (territorial communities), though bureaucratic hurdles initially limited integration for many cities.[1] In July 2020, a comprehensive administrative-territorial reform abolished the distinct status of cities of regional significance, merging most of the pre-reform 118 such unitsâ€”along with legacy raionsâ€”into 136 enlarged raions to streamline governance, reduce administrative layers, and bolster fiscal capacity at the sub-regional level.[4] This restructuring integrated urban centers into broader district frameworks while preserving their roles as hromada centers, adapting to wartime resilience needs by fostering stronger local self-reliance amid ongoing decentralization.[5]

Historical Development

Origins and Evolution Pre-Independence

The administrative framework for cities of oblast subordination in the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic originated with the 1932 territorial reform, which abolished the intermediate okruha (circuit) districts established in 1923 and introduced oblasts as the principal regional units. This shift, decreed by the All-Ukrainian Central Executive Committee on February 7, 1932, initially created seven oblastsâ€”Vinnytsia, Dnipropetrovsk, Kyiv, Odesa, Kharkiv, Chernihiv, and Azov-Black Sea (the latter reorganized into Dnipropetrovsk and Odesa expansions)â€”to streamline centralized control amid collectivization and industrialization drives. Major urban centers, previously sometimes directly under republican authorities or okruhas, were reassigned to oblast soviets, bypassing raions to enable focused governance of industrial hubs and population concentrations exceeding typical raion scales.[16][17] By the 1937 Constitution of the Ukrainian SSR, this subordination was embedded in the hierarchical structure of oblasts, raions, and urban soviets, distinguishing cities of oblast level from those under raions based on their role as economic anchors. A 1938 decree further delineated these cities as entities with executive councils reporting directly to oblast bodies, reflecting Soviet priorities for urban administrative autonomy in non-agricultural sectors. Evolution continued with post-war expansions: incorporation of western territories in 1939 added oblasts like Lviv and Rivne, elevating cities such as Lviv to oblast status; by 1954, the structure stabilized at 25 oblasts, with cities like Sevastopol holding special republican-level equivalence until 1954 transfer from RSFSR. Reforms in 1962 (Decree No. 30) and 1965 (Decree No. 64) refined boundaries, creating 393 raions while preserving or granting oblast subordination to growing industrial sites like Poliske in Kyiv oblast, driven by urbanization from Five-Year Plans.[16] Designation criteria emphasized population thresholds (often above 50,000 inhabitants by later standards), industrial output, and infrastructural centrality, allowing such cities to manage budgets, services, and planning independently of rural raions to support proletarian development goals. For instance, oblast centers automatically qualified, while satellites like Kramatorsk in Donetsk oblast gained status for metallurgical significance. The 1978 Constitution reinforced this by integrating city raions within larger urban entities, culminating in approximately 142 such cities by the late Soviet period amid 434 total urban settlements, prioritizing efficiency over local fragmentation despite occasional lobbying for status elevation. This system persisted until independence, embodying Soviet causal emphasis on vertical command over horizontal equity.[16]

Post-Independence Designations Until 2020

Ukraine retained the Soviet-inherited administrative structure for cities of oblast significance following its declaration of independence on August 24, 1991, and the confirmatory referendum on December 1, 1991, where 92.3% of voters approved sovereignty. These cities operated as standalone territorial communities under direct oblast oversight, excluding them from raion administrations and granting them equivalent status to districts in terms of self-governance and resource allocation. The framework emphasized their role as regional hubs for economic, industrial, and administrative functions, with no immediate post-independence redesignations disrupting the approximately 180-190 cities classified as such by the early 1990s. The Constitution of Ukraine, ratified on June 28, 1996, formalized local self-government in Articles 140â€“146, defining territorial communitiesâ€”including those of cities of oblast significanceâ€”as bearers of self-governing rights with authority over local affairs, budgets, and property. This constitutional basis distinguished these cities from subordinate urban units, enabling city councils (miska rada) to exercise executive and legislative powers akin to raion-level bodies. Complementing this, the Law of Ukraine "On Local Self-Government in Ukraine" No. 280/97-VR, adopted May 21, 1997, specified organizational principles for city-level self-government, including council composition, executive committees, and competencies in urban planning, utilities, and social services, while affirming their independence from higher administrative tiers except for delegated state functions. Designations were conferred on cities meeting criteria of population exceeding 50,000 residents, serving as oblast administrative centers, or demonstrating substantial industrial-economic, scientific, or cultural prominence, as outlined in parliamentary classifications. This approach perpetuated Soviet-era selections, prioritizing urban centers with strategic infrastructure and population density over uniform metrics, resulting in disparities such as multiple cities per oblast in industrially dense regions like Donetsk. By January 1, 2016, the count stood at 187 such cities nationwide, distributed unevenly across 24 oblasts, Autonomous Republic of Crimea, and cities with special status.[18] From the late 1990s through 2013, amid economic privatization and hyperinflation, these designations faced minimal alteration, though fiscal constraints limited autonomy until post-2004 Orange Revolution reforms introduced modest revenue-sharing enhancements. Decentralization initiatives launched in 2014â€“2015, including voluntary amalgamation of communities under the Law on Voluntary Amalgamation of Territorial Communities (2015), bolstered capacities for cities of oblast significance by increasing local tax retention to 60% and enabling service delegation, yet preserved their distinct status without new designations or abolitions.[15] This continuity reflected pragmatic inheritance of a functional system amid geopolitical tensions, including the 2014 annexation of Crimea, which de facto removed Sevastopol and other affected cities from Ukrainian jurisdiction while maintaining de jure recognition. The structure endured until the July 17, 2020, raion consolidation law integrated them into enlarged districts.

Criteria for Designation

Population and Economic Thresholds

Cities of regional significance were typically designated based on a population threshold exceeding 50,000 residents, provided they served as key centers of economic, scientific, cultural, or administrative activity within their respective oblast or the Autonomous Republic of Crimea.[19] This criterion emphasized the city's regional centrality rather than isolated demographic size, ensuring that such municipalities could effectively manage broader territorial responsibilities independent of district-level oversight. Administrative decisions on designation were made by the Verkhovna Rada of Ukraine, often evaluating the city's capacity to support oblast-wide functions like infrastructure coordination and public services delivery. Exceptions to the 50,000-resident minimum were permitted for cities demonstrating substantial industrial output, socio-cultural prominence, or historical relevance that warranted elevated status, even with smaller populations. As of 2018, approximately 82 such smaller citiesâ€”collectively housing nearly 2 million residentsâ€”held regional significance status despite falling below the population benchmark, highlighting a flexible application prioritizing functional impact over strict numerics.[20] No codified quantitative economic thresholds, such as minimum gross regional product, industrial employment percentages, or fiscal revenue levels, were explicitly required for designation. Instead, economic suitability was assessed qualitatively through the city's role in driving regional productivity, often evidenced by concentrations of manufacturing, trade hubs, or specialized enterprises that contributed disproportionately to oblast GDP. This approach allowed for designations in economically vital locales like mining or agricultural processing centers, where population alone might understate influence, but it also invited subjective interpretations by legislative bodies.[19]

Administrative Privileges and Autonomy

Cities of regional significance in Ukraine held administrative status equivalent to raions, positioning them as second-tier units directly subordinate to the oblast level rather than integrated into a raion's structure.[10] This equivalence granted them independent local self-government bodies, including elected city councils (miska rada) and executive committees (vykonavchyi komitet), which operated autonomously in managing city affairs without oversight from a raion council.[10] [21] Under the Law of Ukraine "On Local Self-Government," these cities exercised core competencies such as approving and executing local budgets, disposing of municipal property, regulating land use within city boundaries, providing communal services (e.g., water supply, waste management, and public transport), urban planning, and delivering social services like education and healthcare infrastructure maintenance.[10] City councils, as representative bodies elected for five-year terms, held plenary powers including the adoption of statutes, programs for socioeconomic development, and decisions on administrative-territorial organization within the city, such as establishing internal city districts (miski raiony).[10] Executive committees, chaired by the directly elected mayor, implemented these decisions and handled day-to-day administration, with mayors serving as heads of both the council and executive bodies in a dual role.[10] [22] A key privilege was direct fiscal interaction with higher levels: these cities received state budget subventions, subsidies, and transfers allocated by the oblast council bypassing raion intermediaries, enabling greater budgetary autonomy and resource control compared to settlements within raions.[23] This structure supported enhanced capacities for infrastructure investment and service delivery, as evidenced by pre-reform data showing cities of oblast significance managing larger populations (often exceeding 10,000 residents) and broader functional scopes, including secondary education oversight and local policing coordination.[10] [21] Autonomy was not absolute; while self-government functions remained insulated from routine state interference, oblast state administrations could oversee delegated state powers (e.g., state registration, civil defense) and ensure compliance with national laws, with provisions for dissolution of councils or mayors by court order for violations.[10] [24] This balance reflected Ukraine's unitary framework, where local bodies derived authority from the Constitution (Articles 140â€“146) and the 1997 Law, prioritizing community interests in non-delegated matters.[10] In practice, larger such cities like Vinnytsia or Poltava demonstrated effective autonomy through independent policy-making, though smaller ones sometimes relied on oblast support for capacity gaps.[25]

2020 Administrative Reform and Abolition

Key Provisions of the Reform

The 2020 administrative reform in Ukraine, culminating in Resolution No. 807-IX of the Verkhovna Rada adopted on July 17, 2020, and effective from July 19, 2020, liquidated all 490 existing raions and established 136 enlarged raions across the mainland, plus additional units in occupied territories.[26][27] This restructuring directly abolished the category of cities of regional significance, which had previously operated as autonomous units parallel to raions, directly subordinate to oblast state administrations rather than any raion.[28] Under the reform's provisions, territories of former cities of regional significance were incorporated into the boundaries of the new raions, eliminating their standalone administrative status and privileges, such as independent raion-equivalent councils and budgets for certain functions.[28] These cities transitioned to functioning primarily as united territorial communities (hromadas) for local self-government, while state administration shifted to the raion level, with raion military administrations handling oversight in wartime contexts post-2022.[29] The Cabinet of Ministers, empowered by preceding Law No. 562-IX of April 16, 2020, had delineated hromada territories to align with this raion reconfiguration, ensuring no standalone city statuses persisted.[29] The reform mandated that new raion centers be selected based on criteria including population size, economic viability, and infrastructure, often designating former cities of regional significance or their administrative centers as such hubs.[26] This integration aimed to streamline governance by reducing fragmentation, though it reduced the number of local-level decision-making bodies and shifted certain fiscal and service delivery responsibilities upward to larger raions.[28] Pre-reform cities like Khmelnytskyi or Ternopil, previously outside raion boundaries, were explicitly mapped into encompassing raions, with their populations and lands aggregated into raion statistics and administrations.[26]

Integration into New Raions

The 2020 administrative reform abolished the status of cities of oblast significance, integrating their territories into 136 newly configured raions formed by merging the prior 490 raions. This process, approved by Verkhovna Rada Resolution No. 807-IX on July 17, 2020, encompassed approximately 118 such cities, subordinating them to raion-level administration while preserving their local self-governance through united territorial communities (hromadas).[29][28] Integration typically involved assigning each city to a specific new raion, with larger cities often designated as raion centers to leverage their infrastructure and population density for administrative efficiency. For example, in Dnipropetrovsk Oblast, Kryvyi Rih was incorporated as the center of the newly formed Kryvyi Rih Raion, encompassing the city's territory and adjacent rural areas previously under multiple old raions. Similarly, cities like Boryspil in Kyiv Oblast saw mergers of surrounding localities into their structure, enhancing their role within the Bucha Raion. These arrangements maintained operational continuity, as city councils transitioned into hromada administrations handling local services such as education, healthcare, and utilities.[28] Smaller cities of oblast significance were more frequently absorbed into broader raions without central status, leading to shared governance with rural hromadas. The Cabinet of Ministers finalized raion boundaries via detailed configurations, ensuring balanced territorial units based on population, economic viability, and geographic coherence. By January 1, 2021, all integrations were effective, eliminating direct oblast subordination and streamlining vertical administrative hierarchies. This shift reduced redundant bureaucracies but required adjustments in fiscal allocations and inter-level coordination.[28][29] In cases where cities formed independent urban hromadas, they retained autonomy over internal affairs, while raion councils oversaw supra-hromada functions like secondary roads and hospitals. Subsequent legislation, such as amendments allowing voluntary hromada accessions to city structures, facilitated further consolidations post-reform. Overall, the integration preserved urban administrative capacities amid the broader decentralization framework, adapting pre-existing city entities to the raion-centric model.[1]

Rationale and Debates

The 2020 administrative reform, enacted through Resolution No. 807-IX of the Verkhovna Rada on July 17, 2020, abolished the status of cities of regional significance to streamline Ukraine's territorial structure by reducing the number of raions from approximately 490 to 136 and integrating these cities into larger administrative units.[4] This move aimed to eliminate fragmented governance layers where such cities operated parallel to raions, directly under oblast administrations, thereby fostering more viable territorial communities (hromadas) capable of efficient service provision, resource management, and fiscal self-sufficiency as part of the ongoing decentralization process initiated in 2014.[13] Proponents, including reform architects in the Cabinet of Ministers, argued that consolidation addressed inefficiencies from overly small units, enhanced state oversight at the raion level for delegated powers like education and healthcare, and aligned with European standards for balanced local autonomy without excessive fragmentation.[30] Debates surrounding the abolition centered on trade-offs between central efficiency gains and local disruptions. Supporters highlighted empirical benefits, such as increased social capital and trust in governance post-reform, evidenced by staggered implementation data showing reformed areas exhibiting higher civic engagement during crises like the Russian invasion.[31] However, critics, including some regional analysts and local officials, contended that the top-down mandateâ€”following incomplete voluntary hromada mergersâ€”ignored geographic and demographic variances, leading to poorly configured raions that strained rural access to services and imposed financial burdens on newly formed communities lacking adequate budgets or infrastructure.[4] Cities like those in oblasts with dispersed populations argued the loss of special status diminished their administrative privileges, such as direct funding allocations, potentially exacerbating urban-rural divides without compensatory mechanisms; this was compounded by the reform's timing amid economic pressures, prompting calls for adjustments that were not fully addressed pre-implementation. While no widespread legislative reversal occurred, post-reform evaluations noted persistent challenges in occupied territories' legal status and the need for further fiscal equalization to mitigate unintended inequalities.[4]

Impact of Russian Invasion

Occupied Territories and De Facto Changes

In the wake of Russia's full-scale invasion on February 24, 2022, multiple cities formerly designated as cities of regional significance in Ukraine fell under de facto Russian military control, particularly in Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts. These included Mariupol and Berdyansk in Donetsk Oblast (captured in May 2022), Sievierodonetsk in Luhansk Oblast (July 2022), and Melitopol in Zaporizhzhia Oblast (March 2022), among others such as Nova Kakhovka and Enerhodar.[32] Kherson city itself was occupied in early March 2022 but liberated by Ukrainian forces on November 11, 2022, though significant portions of Kherson Oblast remain under Russian control as of October 2025.[33] De facto administrative changes in these cities disregarded Ukraine's 2020 decentralization reform, which had abolished the cities of regional significance category by integrating them into enlarged raions. Instead, Russia initially established military-civil administrations (MCA) to oversee occupied territories, appointing proxy officials and suppressing Ukrainian governance structures.[34] Following staged referendums in late September 2022â€”widely documented as coerced and lacking international legitimacyâ€”Russia purported to annex Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts on September 30, 2022, incorporating the cities into its federal system as municipalities within Russian-designated oblasts or the self-proclaimed Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics (recognized by Russia as federal subjects).[35] Under Russian de facto rule, these cities underwent systemic Russification, including mandatory passportization (requiring Russian citizenship for access to services), replacement of the hryvnia with the ruble, imposition of Russian-language education and media, and alignment with Russian legal codes that override prior Ukrainian autonomies. Local councils, where not dissolved, were staffed with collaborators, while resistance led to arrests or deportations; for instance, in Mariupol, Russian authorities reported resettling over 1.5 million residents by mid-2023, though independent estimates suggest forced displacements and demographic engineering.[36][37] Economic integration involved redirecting infrastructureâ€”like Mariupol's Azovstal steel plantâ€”toward Russian markets, with de facto privatization favoring Moscow-aligned entities. These shifts effectively nullified the cities' prior direct subordination to Ukrainian oblast administrations, subordinating them instead to Russian governors or MCA heads, amid ongoing militarization efforts such as youth indoctrination camps.[35][38] While de jure Ukrainian sovereignty persistsâ€”affirmed by the United Nations General Assembly resolutions condemning the annexationsâ€”the de facto reality in persistently occupied cities like Mariupol (where reconstruction has prioritized military use over civilian needs) reflects a hybrid governance model blending coercion, propaganda, and selective service provision to consolidate control. Frontline fluctuations, such as Ukrainian advances in Kharkiv Oblast in 2022, spared some areas but underscored the provisional nature of Russian holdings, with no cities of regional significance regaining full Ukrainian administration post-liberation equivalent to pre-war status.[39][33]

De Jure Status vs. Russian Claims

Under Ukrainian law, cities formerly designated as of regional significance in occupied territoriesâ€”such as Mariupol in Donetsk Oblast and Kherson in Kherson Oblastâ€”retain their integration into the post-2020 administrative framework of raions and hromadas (unified territorial communities), despite the suspension of effective governance due to occupation. The 2020 reform, enacted via Law No. 562-IX on July 16, 2020, abolished the standalone status of these cities, subordinating them to enlarged raions (e.g., Mariupol to Mariupol Raion, Kherson to Kherson Raion), with Ukraine maintaining de jure sovereignty over the entire territory as outlined in its Constitution (Article 2) and affirmed through continued administrative mappings by the Ministry of Communities and Territories Development. This legal continuity applies even in occupied areas, where Ukrainian authorities claim the right to restore pre-invasion structures upon liberation, as evidenced by provisional governance plans for de-occupied regions like parts of Kherson Oblast in late 2022.[40] Russia, by contrast, asserts de facto control and has reorganized these cities within its claimed federal subjects following the September 20-27, 2022, referendums in Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts, which it used to justify annexation decrees signed by President Vladimir Putin on September 30, 2022. Under Russian administration, Mariupol is designated as the temporary administrative center of the annexed "Donetsk People's Republic," with imposed governance structures including Russian-appointed officials and integration into Russia's federal budget and military command, while Kherson functions similarly within the claimed Kherson Oblast of the Russian Federation. These changes include forced Russification policies, such as curriculum alterations and passportization drives, but stem from occupation rather than consensual integration, with referendums conducted under duress amid active combat and lacking independent verification.[41] Internationally, the de jure Ukrainian status prevails, with the United Nations General Assembly rejecting the annexations as violations of territorial integrity via Resolution ES-11/4 (October 12, 2022) and subsequent affirmations, supported by 143 votes in favor. The United States and European Union explicitly recognize these oblasts, including their cities, as Ukrainian sovereign territory and refuse to acknowledge Russian alterations, imposing sanctions on occupation entities while providing military aid to uphold Ukraine's claims. No major state or body grants legitimacy to Russian subdivisions, viewing them as provisional outcomes of aggression rather than valid territorial changes.[34]

Autonomous Republic of Crimea

The Autonomous Republic of Crimea, de jure an autonomous republic within Ukraine comprising most of the Crimean Peninsula, historically included 11 cities designated as cities of republican subordination, functioning equivalently to cities of regional significance in Ukraine's oblasts. These cities held administrative autonomy as self-governing municipalities, detached from surrounding raions, with authority over local governance, budgeting, and urban planning under Ukraine's pre-2020 framework. This status dated to Ukraine's independence in 1991, granting them elevated privileges such as direct reporting to republican-level authorities rather than district (raion) oversight.[42] The designated cities were Alushta, Armyansk, Dzhankoi, Yevpatoria, Kerch, Krasnoperekopsk, Saky, Simferopol, Sudak, Feodosia, and Yalta. Simferopol served as the republican capital and largest such city, with a 2001 census population of 344,339, functioning as the political and economic hub.[42] Kerch, a key port city at the peninsula's eastern tip, supported industrial and maritime activities with a 2001 population of 157,000.[42] Yevpatoria and Feodosia, both Black Sea resorts, leveraged tourism and historical sites, recording 2001 populations of 106,281 and 69,009, respectively.[42] Yalta, renowned for its subtropical climate and sanatoriums, had 80,500 residents in 2001 and hosted significant diplomatic events like the 1945 Yalta Conference.[42] Smaller cities like Armyansk (28,000 in 2001) and Krasnoperekopsk (25,000) focused on chemical industry and isthmus defense infrastructure.[42] These statuses were abolished de jure by Ukraine's 2020 administrative reform, which integrated such cities into enlarged raions for streamlined governance. However, Russia's military occupation of Crimea since February 2014â€”following a disputed referendum on March 16, 2014, rejected by Ukraine and most international bodiesâ€”imposed de facto Russian administrative changes, including reclassification under Russian federal subjects. Ukraine and the United Nations General Assembly (Resolution 68/262, March 27, 2014) affirm Crimea's de jure Ukrainian status, viewing Russian actions as invalid under international law.[43] Empirical data on post-2014 demographics and economies in these cities derive largely from Russian sources, which Ukraine disputes for potential manipulation to justify annexation claims.[39]

Cherkasy Oblast

Prior to the 2020 administrative reform, Cherkasy Oblast encompassed six cities of regional significance: Cherkasy, Kaniv, Smila, Uman, Vatutine, and Zolotonosha. These cities functioned as second-tier administrative units equivalent to raions, with their city councils (miska rada) exercising authority over local executive powers, including urban planning, public utilities, education, healthcare, and fiscal management, while subordinated directly to the oblast state administration rather than a district-level intermediary. This status granted them greater budgetary autonomy and direct access to state funding allocations compared to cities of district significance.[44] The designation originated from Soviet-era divisions retained post-independence, with Cherkasy serving as the oblast center and largest such city, followed by Uman and Smila as key industrial and agricultural hubs. Kaniv, Zolotonosha, and Vatutine held similar privileges despite smaller scales, supporting regional economic activities like grain processing and light manufacturing. Populations as of recent pre-reform estimates included Cherkasy at approximately 292,000, Uman at 87,800, Smila at 68,700, Kaniv at 26,700, Zolotonosha at around 27,600, and Vatutine at 25,000. On July 17, 2020, the Verkhovna Rada of Ukraine passed resolutions forming 136 new raions nationwide, effective from that date, which eliminated the cities of regional significance category by merging their territories and governance structures into enlarged raions. In Cherkasy Oblast, this reduced the number of raions from 20 to fourâ€”Cherkasy, Uman, Zolotonosha, and Zvenyhorodkaâ€”incorporating the former cities without separate administrative layers, thereby centralizing some functions at the raion level while preserving city hromadas (territorial communities) for grassroots services. This shift aimed to streamline administration and reduce bureaucratic overlaps, though it sparked local debates over diminished urban autonomy in smaller cities like Vatutine.[29][45]

Chernihiv Oblast

Prior to the 2020 administrative reform, Chernihiv Oblast included four cities of regional significance directly subordinated to the oblast administration: Chernihiv, Nizhyn, Pryluky, and Novhorod-Siverskyi.[46][47] These cities functioned as independent administrative units outside the raion system, with their own city councils handling local governance, budgeting, and services. Population estimates for 2020 placed Chernihiv at approximately 280,246 residents, Nizhyn at 67,002, Pryluky at around 55,000 (based on prior data), and Novhorod-Siverskyi at roughly 13,000.[46] The reform, enacted via Law No. 807-IX on 17 July 2020 and effective from 18 July 2020, abolished the category of cities of regional significance nationwide, integrating them into 136 enlarged raions to streamline administration and reduce fragmentation. In Chernihiv Oblast, this reduced the number of raions from 22 to five, with the former cities serving as centers of the new entities:
  • Chernihiv: Incorporated into Chernihiv Raion as its administrative center, encompassing the city and surrounding territories previously under multiple raions.[48]
  • Nizhyn: Became the center of Nizhyn Raion, absorbing adjacent areas and former raion territories.[48]
  • Pryluky: Designated the administrative center of Pryluky Raion, integrating prior raion lands.[48]
  • Novhorod-Siverskyi: Formed the core of Novhorod-Siverskyi Raion, with expanded boundaries.[48]
The fifth raion, Koriukivka Raion, was formed without a former city of regional significance as its center. These changes preserved the cities' municipal governance but subordinated them to raion-level structures, aiming to enhance efficiency amid fiscal constraints. During the 2022 Russian invasion, Russian forces temporarily occupied northern parts of Chernihiv Oblast, including areas near Novhorod-Siverskyi, from late February to early April 2022, but withdrew without establishing de facto administrative alterations to these integrated cities; de jure, the post-reform raion structure persists under Ukrainian control.

Chernivtsi Oblast

In Chernivtsi Oblast, two cities held regional significance status prior to the 2020 administrative reform: Chernivtsi and Novodnistrovsk.[49][50] These were directly administered by the oblast authorities, separate from the 11 raions, and received budgetary preferences including 75% of personal income tax revenues allocated to their city budgets.[51]

Donetsk Oblast

The cities of regional significance in Donetsk Oblast were administrative centers directly subordinate to the oblast authorities, bypassing raion-level governance, and included key industrial hubs developed during the Soviet era for coal mining, steel production, and metallurgy. Avdiivka was encircled and captured by Russian forces in February 2024 after relentless artillery and infantry operations. Ukrainian authorities retain control over Kramatorsk, Sloviansk (retaken July 2014), Pokrovsk, Druzhkivka, and Dobropillia, though frontline advances have threatened these by late 2025, with Pokrovsk serving as a key logistical hub under intensified Russian pressure. De jure, all remain under Ukrainian sovereignty per international law and UN General Assembly resolutions condemning the occupations.[39][56]

Sumy Oblast

Sumy Oblast, in northeastern Ukraine bordering Russia's Bryansk and Kursk oblasts, encompasses cities designated as of regional significance prior to the 2020 administrative reforms: Sumy, Konotop, Okhtyrka, Romny, and Shostka. These urban centers function as key nodes for administration, industry, and transportation within the oblast, which spans 23,832 square kilometers and had an estimated population of 1,035,772 as of 2022, significantly affected by the ongoing Russian invasion.[119] The region's proximity to the border has exposed it to repeated Russian military incursions since February 2022, including occupation of border villages and artillery strikes on urban areas, leading to evacuations and infrastructure damage.[120][121] Sumy, the oblast capital founded as a fortress in 1652 on the Psel River, serves as the primary city of regional significance with an estimated population of around 268,000 in 2025 projections, down from pre-war figures due to conflict-related displacement.[122][123] As an educational and manufacturing hub hosting Sumy State University and chemical industries, it has faced frequent drone and missile attacks, including a September 2025 drone strike causing explosions across the city.[124] Konotop, a railway junction with historical ties to Cossack fortifications, supports aerospace and machinery production but endured power outages from Russian strikes in September 2025.[125] Okhtyrka, known for oil refining and situated along the Vorskla River, reported 14 injuries, including children, from a large-scale Russian drone attack in August 2025.[126] Romny and Shostka, the latter a center for chemical and rocket engineering, have similarly suffered artillery and aerial assaults, contributing to regional instability amid Russia's renewed offensives launched in April 2025 following withdrawals from Kursk Oblast.[127][128] Ukrainian forces have conducted counteroperations, recapturing villages like one reported in August 2025, highlighting the contested border dynamics.[127] These cities' strategic positions have intensified their exposure to hybrid warfare tactics, including sabotage and bombardment, as documented in military assessments.[129]

Ternopil Oblast

Prior to Ukraine's 2020 administrative reform, which enlarged raions and integrated most cities of regional significance into district administrations, Ternopil Oblast administered four such cities directly: Ternopil, Chortkiv, Berezhany, and Kremenets.[130][28] These municipalities exercised powers akin to raions, including local governance and budgeting independent of district oversight. Post-reform, Ternopil retained its status as the oblast center within Ternopil Raion, while Chortkiv became the administrative seat of Chortkiv Raion, Kremenets headed Kremenets Raion, and Berezhany integrated into Ternopil Raion. Ternopil, the largest, recorded a 2022 population of 225,004 and functions as the oblast's primary economic, educational, and transportation node.[131] Chortkiv, with 28,279 residents in 2022, historically served as a regional trade and administrative outpost. Berezhany, population 17,139 in 2022, maintained significance through its castle and pre-reform autonomy. Kremenets, at 20,476 inhabitants in 2022, hosted key educational institutions like the Kremenets Mountains National Nature Park vicinity and a historic seminary. The reform shifted these cities' oversight to raion levels, consolidating local self-government under enlarged territorial communities (hromadas).[28]

Vinnytsia Oblast

Vinnytsia Oblast encompasses six cities of regional significance, which operate as self-governing municipalities outside the raion structure, each with independent city councils responsible for local administration, budgeting, and services.[132][133] These include Vinnytsia, the oblast administrative center with a 2022 estimated population of 367,901, functioning as the primary industrial, educational, and transportation hub of the region.[134] The other cities are Zhmerynka, with 33,754 residents (2022 estimate), known for its railway significance; Khmilnyk (26,916), a resort town noted for mineral springs; Mohyliv-Podilskyi (29,925), located near the border with Moldova and serving as a trade point; Koziatyn (22,241), another key railway junction; and Ladyzhyn (22,459), centered around a major thermal power plant.[134][135] Populations reflect official estimates amid ongoing demographic shifts due to migration and the war.[135] These cities collectively contribute to the oblast's economy through manufacturing, agriculture processing, and infrastructure, maintaining distinct status under Ukraine's decentralized governance framework post-2020 reforms.[132]

Volyn Oblast

Prior to Ukraine's 2020 administrative reform, Volyn Oblast encompassed four cities of regional significance: Lutsk, Kovel, Novovolynsk, and Volodymyr-Volynskyi.[136] These municipalities were directly subordinate to the oblast administration, separate from raions, and included urban territories with associated councils handling local governance. The reform, effective from 18 July 2020, abolished this status by merging the cities into expanded raions: Lutsk and surrounding areas into Lutsk Raion, Kovel into Kovel Raion, Novovolynsk into Volodymyr Raion, and Volodymyr-Volynskyi into Volodymyr Raion. Lutsk, the administrative center of Volyn Oblast, served as the primary city of regional significance with a population of 215,986 as of 2023.[137] Located on the Styr River, it functions as a key industrial, educational, and transportation hub in northwestern Ukraine, hosting institutions like Volyn National University and significant manufacturing sectors.[138] Kovel, a major railway junction connecting Ukraine to Poland and Belarus, had a population of 67,575 in 2022.[139] Established in the 19th century, it developed around rail infrastructure, supporting logistics and trade, with the city's economy tied to transportation and light industry. Novovolynsk, founded in 1957 as a settlement for coal miners near the Zaprutba mine, recorded a population of 49,772 in 2022.[140] Primarily an industrial city focused on mining and energy production, its growth reflected post-World War II Soviet-era resource development in the region. Volodymyr-Volynskyi, one of Ukraine's ancient cities dating to the 10th century and historically known as Volodymyr, had 37,910 residents in 2022.[141] Positioned near the Polish border, it holds cultural and historical importance as a former center in medieval principalities, with economy centered on agriculture, food processing, and small-scale manufacturing.

Zakarpattia Oblast

Zakarpattia Oblast formerly included five cities of oblast significance under Ukraine's pre-2020 administrative system: Uzhhorod, Mukachevo, Beregove, Khust, and Chop. These municipalities held status equivalent to raions, with direct subordination to oblast authorities, facilitating local governance and economic functions independent of district oversight.[142][143] The 2020 decentralization reform abolished this designation nationwide, integrating the cities into six enlarged raions while preserving their roles as key urban centers, often as raion administrative hubs.[142] Uzhhorod, the oblast capital, anchors the Uzhhorod Raion and functions as the primary administrative, educational, and transport node, with a 2022 population estimate of 115,449.[139] Mukachevo, in Mukachevo Raion, supports industry and tourism, recording 85,569 residents in 2022.[139] Beregove, elevated to oblast significance in 2001 and now central to Beregove Raion, features a Hungarian-majority population of 23,325 as of 2022, emphasizing cross-border trade and cultural ties.[142][139] Khust, heading Khust Raion, had 28,039 inhabitants in 2022 and historically served as a brief capital during the 1939 Carpatho-Ukraine declaration.[139] Chop, the smallest at 8,626 residents in 2022, operates within Uzhhorod Raion as a critical railway junction bordering Slovakia, Hungary, and Romania.[139] These cities collectively drive Zakarpattia's economy through border logistics, agriculture, and thermal spas, though wartime displacement since 2022 has inflated local populations via internal migration, complicating precise demographics absent a full census.

Zaporizhzhia Oblast

Prior to the 2020 administrative reform, Zaporizhzhia Oblast encompassed five cities of oblast significance: Zaporizhzhia, Berdiansk, Enerhodar, Melitopol, and Tokmak. These entities operated as independent second-tier administrative units, directly answerable to the oblast administration rather than raions, with enhanced budgetary autonomy including a 75% share of local personal income tax.[144] Enacted through Verkhovna Rada Postanova No. 807-IX on July 17, 2020, the reform liquidated the cities of oblast significance category by amalgamating them into five enlarged raions: Berdiansk, Melitopol, Polohy (encompassing Tokmak), Vasylivka, and Zaporizhzhia. The former cities typically function as raion or hromada (united territorial community) centers, preserving local self-governance within the restructured framework. The Russian full-scale invasion commencing February 24, 2022, has profoundly altered the oblast's administration, with Russian forces occupying roughly 70% of the territory, including Berdiansk, Enerhodar, Melitopol, and Tokmak.[145] [146] Zaporizhzhia city, the oblast capital and sole major urban center under Ukrainian control, continues to administer the unoccupied northern portions amid ongoing hostilities.[147] Russian authorities have imposed parallel governance in occupied areas, including purported administrative reorganizations unrecognized by Ukraine or international bodies.[148]

Zhytomyr Oblast

Zhytomyr Oblast formerly included five cities designated as of regional significance prior to Ukraine's 2020 administrative reform: Berdychiv, Zhytomyr, Korosten, Malyn, and Novohrad-Volynskyi (renamed Zviahel in May 2022).[149] [150] These municipalities operated with administrative status equivalent to raions, bypassing subordination to district councils and reporting directly to the oblast administration, which granted them fiscal and governance autonomy focused on local infrastructure, services, and economic development.[149] The 2020 decentralization reform, implemented via Law No. 562-IX effective July 18, 2020, abolished this category nationwide to streamline governance by consolidating 490 raions and 118 cities of regional significance into 136 enlarged raions.[4] In Zhytomyr Oblast, the reform reduced the structure to four raions, integrating the former cities as follows: Zhytomyr and Malyn into Zhytomyr Raion; Berdychiv into Berdychiv Raion; Korosten into Korosten Raion; and Zviahel into Zviahel Raion. This shift emphasized territorial hromadas (communities) for local self-government while centralizing certain functions at the raion level, though the cities retained their urban cores and economic roles.[4] Key characteristics of these former cities of regional significance included their historical prominence and contributions to the oblast's economy, dominated by manufacturing, agriculture processing, and trade. Zhytomyr, the oblast capital, served as the primary hub with a 2021 population of about 275,000, featuring industries like machinery and food production alongside cultural sites dating to its 9th-century origins and 1444 Magdeburg charter.[151] [152] Berdychiv, with roots in the 14th century and a peak Jewish population before World War II, focused on light industry and had around 74,000 residents pre-war. Korosten emphasized forestry and woodworking, rooted in ancient Scythian settlements. Malyn, smaller at roughly 26,000, centered on pulp and paper production. Zviahel supported textiles and agriculture, with medieval fortifications. Post-reform and amid the 2022 Russian invasion, these centers faced displacement and infrastructure strain, but maintained administrative functions within their raions.[149]
