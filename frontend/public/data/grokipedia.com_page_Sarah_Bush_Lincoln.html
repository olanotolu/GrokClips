Sarah Bush Lincoln


Sarah Bush Johnston Lincoln (December 13, 1788 – December 10, 1869) was the second wife of Thomas Lincoln and stepmother to Abraham Lincoln, the sixteenth president of the United States.[1] Born in Elizabethtown, Kentucky, to a family of modest means, she first married Daniel Johnston in 1806, with whom she had three children before his death in 1816 left her widowed and facing debts.[1] On December 2, 1819, she wed Thomas Lincoln, a fellow widower whose first wife, Nancy Hanks, had died the previous year, thereby assuming responsibility for ten-year-old Abraham and his older sister Sarah.[2] Sarah Bush Lincoln transformed the family's rudimentary Indiana cabin by introducing order, cleanliness, and cultural elements such as books, which she gifted to Abraham and encouraged his pursuit of knowledge despite limited formal schooling.[2] Abraham reciprocated this maternal affection, referring to her as his true mother and maintaining a close bond, evidenced by his visits—including one in January 1861 prior to his presidential inauguration—and his provision of land for her use after Thomas's death in 1851.[2] In 1830, the family migrated to Coles County, Illinois, where they established a farm; she resided there until her death at age 80, outliving both Thomas and Abraham, and was buried beside Thomas in Shiloh Cemetery.[2] Her supportive role in Abraham's formative years is credited with contributing to his intellectual development amid frontier hardships.[1]

Early Life

Birth and Family Origins

Sarah Bush was born on December 13, 1788, in Hardin County, Kentucky, near what is now Elizabethtown.[1][3][4] She was the daughter of Christopher Bush, a landowner, and his wife Hannah.[5][6] The Bush family was described as prosperous and established in the region, with Christopher Bush owning property that supported a relatively stable household amid frontier conditions.[1][5] As the third daughter among nine children, Sarah grew up in a large family environment typical of early Kentucky settlers, where self-sufficiency and land-based livelihoods predominated.[6] At around age two, she relocated with her parents within Hardin County, reflecting the mobility of pioneer families seeking better farming opportunities.[3] Her family's relative affluence, derived from Christopher Bush's land holdings, contrasted with the hardships faced by many contemporaries in the Kentucky backcountry, providing Sarah with a foundation of resources before her own entry into marriage and motherhood.[1][5]

First Marriage to Daniel Johnston

Sarah Bush, born on December 13, 1788, in Hardin County, Kentucky, married Daniel Johnston on March 13, 1806, in Elizabethtown, Hardin County.[2][7] At the time of the marriage, Bush was approximately 17 years old, while Johnston, born in 1778, was a farmer in the region.[3][7] Johnston later served as the jailer of Hardin County, a position that provided modest stability amid the frontier economy.[8] The couple resided in Kentucky and had three children: daughters Elizabeth and Matilda, and son John.[2][8] Their household reflected the hardships of early 19th-century pioneer life, including land management and subsistence farming, though specific details on their property holdings remain limited in primary records.[8] Daniel Johnston died in October 1816 during a cholera outbreak, leaving Sarah Bush Johnston a widow at age 27 with three young children and mounting debts that rendered her financially destitute.[3][9] This loss forced her to manage the family amid economic precarity in Hardin County until her subsequent remarriage.[2]

Marriages and Family Dynamics

Second Marriage to Thomas Lincoln

Sarah Bush Johnston, widowed since her husband Daniel Johnston's death in 1816, resided in Elizabethtown, Kentucky, with her three children: Elizabeth, John, and Matilda.[5] Thomas Lincoln, also of Kentucky origins, had relocated his family—including eleven-year-old Abraham and younger son Thomas—to Spencer County, Indiana, following Nancy Hanks Lincoln's death from milk sickness on October 5, 1818.[10] Acquainted from their shared Kentucky community, Thomas returned to Elizabethtown in late 1819 seeking a new wife capable of managing a frontier household, selecting Sarah for her housekeeping skills and familiarity.[2] The couple married on December 2, 1819, in Elizabethtown, with the ceremony performed by Methodist minister Reverend George L. Rogers; Thomas was 41 and Sarah 31, marking the second marriage for each.[2][11] No children resulted from their union, though Sarah brought her three dependents, while Thomas contributed his surviving son Abraham after the younger Thomas's early death.[5] Following the wedding, Thomas, Sarah, and her children—accompanied by hired help for the wagons—traveled approximately 100 miles northward overland to the Lincoln homestead on Pigeon Creek in Indiana, arriving in mid-December amid winter hardships.[2] Sarah later described the Indiana site as "wild, and desolate" compared to her Kentucky home, prompting improvements to the log cabin such as added flooring, windows, and doors using materials Thomas fetched.[2] This relocation underscored the practical motivations of pioneer remarriages, prioritizing mutual support in agrarian survival over romantic ideals.[10]

Integration of Families and Household Management

Following her marriage to Thomas Lincoln on December 2, 1819, in Elizabethtown, Kentucky, Sarah Bush Lincoln integrated her three children from her previous marriage—Elizabeth, John D., and Matilda Johnston—with Thomas's surviving children, Abraham and Sarah Lincoln, into a single household.[2] The blended family, totaling five children aged approximately 7 to 12, relocated to the family's log cabin in Spencer County, Indiana, arriving around Christmas 1819, accompanied by Dennis Hanks, a cousin of Thomas's first wife Nancy Hanks who had been living with the Lincolns.[2] This arrangement resulted in a household of eight members residing in the modest one-room cabin, reflecting typical pioneer family expansions through remarriage.[2] Sarah Lincoln quickly assumed a maternal role toward her stepchildren, treating Abraham and Sarah as her own and fostering a harmonious family dynamic without reported conflicts among the siblings.[12] Abraham, then nearly 11, later described her as proving "a good and kind mother" to him, indicating successful emotional integration despite the challenges of combining orphaned and half-sibling groups in a frontier setting.[12] The Johnston children, including the older Elizabeth who soon married into the extended Hanks family, participated alongside the Lincolns in shared farm and domestic duties, contributing to a cohesive unit amid the rigors of pioneer existence.[13] In managing the household, Sarah introduced order and comfort to the previously neglected cabin, which had fallen into disarray after Nancy Lincoln's death from milk sickness in 1818.[14] She transported essential goods from Kentucky, including cooking utensils, pewter plates, a table, bedsteads, and feather bedding—items that elevated the living standards beyond the family's prior sparse furnishings of basic puncheon tables and stools.[15] Daily operations under her direction involved dividing labor for tasks such as cooking over an open hearth, sewing clothing, tending livestock, and maintaining cleanliness, with children assisting in chores like fetching water and gathering firewood to sustain the self-sufficient pioneer lifestyle.[1] Her literacy enabled her to read borrowed books aloud, subtly supporting intellectual pursuits within the household routine, though primary emphasis remained on practical survival in the Indiana wilderness.[16]

Pioneer Life in Indiana and Illinois

Sarah Bush Johnston married widower Thomas Lincoln on December 2, 1819, in Elizabethtown, Kentucky, becoming the stepmother to his children Abraham and Sarah.[2] Shortly after the wedding, she relocated with her three children—John D., Elizabeth, and Matilda—to the Lincoln homestead in Spencer County, Indiana, near Little Pigeon Creek, joining Thomas and his offspring to form a blended household of seven.[5] Upon arrival in early 1820, Sarah encountered a "wild and desolate" frontier landscape, where the existing log cabin inadequately sheltered Thomas's children amid sparse furnishings and untended surroundings.[2] In Indiana, the family pursued subsistence farming on cleared land, with Thomas and Abraham felling trees, erecting fences, and cultivating crops such as corn while raising hogs and other livestock essential for survival.[1] Sarah assumed responsibility for household management, scrubbing the cabin, organizing domestic tasks, and improving hygiene and clothing for all children, which fostered a more orderly pioneer existence despite ongoing challenges like isolation, limited resources, and periodic illnesses prevalent in the region.[2] The cabin, later upgraded with additions like a loft and better flooring through family labor, exemplified the incremental self-reliance required in backwoods Indiana, where communities relied on mutual aid for barn-raisings and harvests.[1] By 1830, seeking richer prairie soil and new opportunities, Thomas led the extended family—including adult children and relatives like Dennis Hanks—westward to Macon County, Illinois, before settling on Goosenest Prairie in Coles County later that year.[5] In Illinois, they constructed a double-cabin on 40 acres, continuing pioneer agriculture by breaking sod for wheat, oats, and corn, tending expanded livestock herds, and navigating the transitions from forested Indiana to open grasslands that demanded different clearing and plowing techniques.[2] This relocation reflected broader patterns of frontier migration for economic improvement, though the family endured similar rigors of building from scratch, weathering harsh winters, and maintaining self-sufficiency until Thomas's death in 1851, after which Sarah resided on the homestead with kin.[5]

Relationship with Abraham Lincoln

Initial Interactions and Maternal Role

Thomas Lincoln married Sarah Bush Johnston, a widow with three children—John, Matilda, and Elizabeth—on December 2, 1819, in Elizabethtown, Kentucky, following a courtship initiated after learning of her widowhood.[3] [1] The couple, both entering their second marriages, then journeyed approximately 100 miles to the Lincoln homestead in Spencer County, Indiana, arriving with Sarah's children to join Thomas's surviving children, 10-year-old Abraham and 11-year-old Sarah.[12] [17] Upon arrival, the Lincoln cabin was in a state of neglect, with filth accumulated since the death of Abraham's biological mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, in October 1818; the children had struggled to maintain the household amid grief and chores.[18] Sarah immediately took charge, directing Abraham and his sister to assist in a thorough cleaning, whitewashing walls, and organizing the space, transforming it into a more orderly home.[2] [18] This practical intervention marked her initial integration, earning quick affection from the Lincoln children, who responded positively to her energy and kindness rather than resentment toward a new maternal figure.[2] Sarah treated Abraham and his sister as her own from the outset, providing emotional support, guidance, and encouragement for Abraham's intellectual pursuits, including access to borrowed books despite Thomas's opposition to formal education.[2] Abraham reciprocated this bond, addressing her as "Mother" in later correspondence and describing her as "a good and kind mother" in adulthood, indicating a deep, enduring maternal attachment formed during these early years.[2] [3] Her role extended beyond housekeeping to fostering a stable family dynamic in the blended household of eight, where she balanced care for all children amid pioneer hardships.[2]

Educational and Personal Influence

Sarah Bush Lincoln, illiterate herself, recognized the value of education and actively supported Abraham Lincoln's intellectual development following her marriage to Thomas Lincoln on December 2, 1819, when Abraham was ten years old.[19] She brought books into the household from her prior marriage and encouraged Abraham's reading habits, often mediating with Thomas, who prioritized manual labor over study and initially resisted allowing the boy extended time for books.[19][20] This intervention enabled Abraham to pursue self-education through borrowed and owned volumes, including works like Aesop's Fables and the Bible, amid sparse formal schooling totaling less than a year.[21][22] Beyond academics, Sarah exerted personal influence by treating Abraham as her biological son, providing emotional stability after his biological mother's death in 1818 and fostering his self-assurance through consistent affection and defense against paternal criticisms.[2][22] Abraham reciprocated this bond, addressing her as "Mother" and later describing her as "a good woman—of a noble spirit," crediting her kindness for shaping his character during frontier hardships in Indiana until the family's 1830 move to Illinois.[2] Her household management also improved family dynamics, creating a more nurturing environment that indirectly supported Abraham's growth into a reflective, resilient individual.[19] Historians assess her role as foundational to his lifelong pursuit of knowledge, though Abraham's autodidactic drive remained primary.[22][21]

Long-Term Bond and Correspondence

After Abraham Lincoln left the family farm around age 22 in the late 1820s, he sustained a close and affectionate relationship with his stepmother Sarah Bush Lincoln, visiting her farm in Coles County, Illinois, approximately every year or two.[2] These visits underscored the enduring maternal bond she had fostered since her marriage to Thomas Lincoln in 1819, with Lincoln later recalling her positive influence on his early development.[2] Lincoln addressed Sarah as "Mother" in his correspondence and described her publicly as "a good and kind mother" who had treated him with genuine affection.[2] [3] While few of these letters survive in full, they reflect his ongoing regard for her, distinct from his more strained relations with his father.[2] Following Thomas Lincoln's death on January 17, 1851, Abraham took steps to ensure Sarah's financial security, providing support and maintaining a 40-acre farm for her use; he also intervened to prevent exploitative arrangements proposed by his stepbrother John D. Johnston, demonstrating protective oversight of her affairs. [23] Their mutual esteem persisted until Lincoln's final visit on January 31, 1861, when, as president-elect, he traveled from Springfield to Coles County to spend the day with her before departing for Washington, D.C.[5] This encounter marked their last meeting, after which the bond endured through intermediaries until Sarah's death in 1869.[5]

Later Life and Death

Final Years in Illinois

Following Thomas Lincoln's death on January 17, 1851, Sarah Bush Lincoln remained on the family farm in Coles County, Illinois, where she had settled with her husband in 1831.[2] [24] Abraham Lincoln assumed responsibility for her financial welfare from afar, purchasing and retaining a 40-acre plot of land in his name to shield it from tax forfeiture and ensure her security.[2] [19] Lincoln maintained contact through regular visits, occurring every year or two, which underscored their enduring bond despite his rising political career.[2] His final visit took place on January 31 to February 1, 1861, as a farewell before departing Illinois for his presidential inauguration in Washington, D.C.[2] [16] In the years that followed, including after Lincoln's assassination in 1865, Sarah Bush Lincoln reportedly recalled this parting with profound emotion, weeping as she reflected on his kindness toward her in her widowhood.[2]

Death and Burial

Sarah Bush Lincoln died on April 12, 1869, at the age of 80 in Coles County, Illinois.[4][25] She had outlived her husband Thomas Lincoln, who died in 1851, by 18 years, during which she resided primarily with her son John D. Johnston on the family farm in the area.[2] Following her death, Sarah was buried in Shiloh Cemetery near Lerna in Coles County, Illinois, adjacent to the grave of Thomas Lincoln, whose remains had been relocated there from the original Gordon Graveyard site.[26][2] The cemetery, also known locally as the Thomas Lincoln Cemetery, preserves the site as a point of historical interest related to the Lincoln family, though the graves remained unmarked for years after her burial until commemorative efforts in the late 19th century.[24] No specific cause of death is recorded in primary historical accounts, consistent with limited medical documentation from the pioneer era in rural Illinois.[1]

Legacy and Historical Assessment

Contemporary Honors and Memorials

The graves of Sarah Bush Lincoln and her husband Thomas Lincoln in Shiloh Cemetery (now known as Thomas Lincoln Cemetery) near Lerna, Illinois, received commemorative markers at their foot in 1925, installed by the Kiwanis Club of Danville, Illinois, followed by an iron fence enclosure in 1934.[24] A monument dedicated to Thomas and Sarah Bush Lincoln as Abraham Lincoln's father and stepmother was erected above their graves, with a formal dedication service held to honor their roles in his upbringing.[27] In Elizabethtown, Kentucky, the Sarah Bush Johnston Lincoln Memorial Cabin preserves a replica of her early 1800s cabin, constructed from 122-year-old logs to reflect her pre-marriage home, maintaining historical details including period gardens and serving as a site to commemorate her Kentucky origins.[6] The Lincoln Log Cabin State Historic Site near Charleston, Illinois, reconstructs and interprets the 1840s farmstead where Sarah Bush Lincoln resided with Thomas Lincoln and the blended family until Thomas's death in 1851, emphasizing her contributions to household management and family life during Abraham Lincoln's early adulthood.[28] The Sarah Bush Lincoln Health Center in Coles County, Illinois, a regional medical facility, was named in her honor upon its opening on May 10, 1977, at a construction cost of $8 million, recognizing her nurturing influence as Abraham Lincoln's stepmother in the selection process by local planners.[29][30]

Scholarly Views on Influence and Character

Historians generally regard Sarah Bush Lincoln as a pivotal maternal figure in Abraham Lincoln's formative years, providing emotional stability and intellectual encouragement following the death of his biological mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, in 1818. Michael Burlingame, a leading Lincoln biographer, notes that her arrival in the Lincoln household in December 1819 transformed the family's living conditions, as she cleaned the dilapidated cabin, improved hygiene, and brought a small library including the Bible, which supported young Abraham's self-education amid his father's opposition to prolonged reading.[20] This intervention is seen as fostering Lincoln's intellectual curiosity, with Sarah recalling in a 1865 interview with William Herndon that Abraham "read diligently" and preferred knowledge over manual labor, traits she observed and implicitly nurtured during their twelve years together in Indiana.[31] Scholarly assessments emphasize her affectionate treatment of stepchildren, treating Abraham and his sister Sarah equivalently to her own offspring, which contemporaries like her nephew Squire Hall attested to in affidavits confirming her equal devotion. Lincoln himself described her as "a good and kind mother" in correspondence and visited her regularly after leaving home, retaining a 40-acre farm for her security after Thomas Lincoln's death in 1851, actions interpreted by historians such as Louis A. Warren as evidence of profound mutual regard.[2] However, some analyses, including Burlingame's, caution against overstating her role in Lincoln's self-made ascent, attributing his achievements primarily to innate drive rather than singular external influence, though her provision of stability post-Nancy's death mitigated the frontier hardships that Burlingame describes as "little better than the life of an ox."[20] On character, sources portray Sarah Bush Lincoln as resilient, thrifty, and literate—qualities evident in her management of debts from her first marriage to Daniel Johnston (died 1818) and her adaptation to pioneer life. Her 1865 Herndon interview highlights her observational acumen, noting Abraham's contemplative demeanor around elders, while affidavits describe her as energetic and fair-complexioned, with a practical mindset that prioritized family welfare over remarriage until Thomas cleared her financial burdens via a 1819 marriage bond.[32] These traits, drawn from primary accounts rather than hagiography, underscore her causal contribution to household order, enabling Abraham's focus on learning, though academic works like those in the Journal of the Abraham Lincoln Association stress the evidentiary limits of such recollections collected decades later.[33]
