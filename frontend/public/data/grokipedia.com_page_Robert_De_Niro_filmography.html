Robert De Niro filmography

Robert De Niro's filmography documents the extensive acting credits of the American performer born Robert Anthony De Niro Jr. on August 17, 1943, in New York City, spanning more than 100 feature films across six decades from his screen debut in 1963 to contemporary releases.[1][2] His roles, often employing method acting techniques including physical transformations, have defined key entries in American cinema, particularly in crime dramas and character-driven narratives directed by Martin Scorsese, with whom he collaborated on ten films including Mean Streets (1973), Taxi Driver (1976), and The Irishman (2019).[3] De Niro's performances earned him the Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor for portraying Vito Corleone in The Godfather Part II (1974) and the Academy Award for Best Actor for Jake LaMotta in Raging Bull (1980), alongside nominations for Taxi Driver (1976), The Deer Hunter (1978), Cape Fear (1991), Awakenings (1990), and Silver Linings Playbook (2012).[1][4] Beyond acting, his filmography includes producing credits on projects like The Good Shepherd (2006), which he also directed, and Tribeca Productions ventures, reflecting a career blending commercial successes such as Meet the Parents (2000) with critically acclaimed indies.[1]

Acting credits

Feature films

De Niro made his feature film debut in the 1965 French-Italian drama Three Rooms in Manhattan, playing a minor role as a client at a diner.[1] Over the subsequent decades, he established himself through intense dramatic performances in crime and character-driven stories, notably collaborating frequently with director Martin Scorsese in films such as Mean Streets (1973), Taxi Driver (1976), Raging Bull (1980), Goodfellas (1990), Casino (1995), and The Irishman (2019). His roles often involved physical transformations and method acting techniques, including gaining weight for Raging Bull to portray boxer Jake LaMotta.[1] Later in his career, De Niro diversified into comedies like Midnight Run (1988) and the Meet the Parents series, while continuing high-profile dramatic work.[1] The following table lists his credited acting roles in feature films chronologically:

Television roles

De Niro's forays into television acting have been infrequent, with roles primarily in prestige television films and limited series rather than ongoing episodic work. His performances often draw on his established screen persona of intense, character-driven portrayals, adapted to smaller-screen formats that allow for deeper exploration of real-life figures or dramatic narratives.[6] In 2017, De Niro starred as financier Bernie Madoff in the HBO television film The Wizard of Lies, directed by Barry Levinson, depicting the Ponzi scheme operator's downfall and family impact following his 2008 arrest; the role earned him a nomination for the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Limited Series or Movie.[7][8] De Niro appeared in the 2023 Argentine mini-series Nada, a five-episode comedy-drama about an aging bon vivant adjusting to independence, where he portrayed Vicent Parisi, an old friend who arrives from abroad to assist the protagonist Manuel Tamayo Prats amid personal upheaval.[9][10] Marking his first lead in a scripted television series, De Niro played former U.S. President Warren Mullen in the 2025 Netflix limited series Zero Day, a conspiracy thriller centered on a global cyberattack investigation, in which Mullen emerges from retirement to uncover potential insider threats; the series premiered on February 20, 2025.[11][12]

Theater roles

De Niro's involvement in theater as an actor was limited, primarily confined to early experimental work and one prominent production in the mid-1980s. The play depicted the tensions of street life and family bonds in New York's Lower East Side, with De Niro's character navigating addiction, pride, and efforts to steer his son Teddy away from similar pitfalls. Directed by Michael Pressman, the production premiered off-Broadway at the Public Theater under the New York Shakespeare Festival on September 5, 1985, co-starring Ralph Macchio as Teddy and Burt Young as Jackie.[14][15] It transferred to Broadway at the Longacre Theatre, opening after previews on May 1, 1986, and closing on May 25, 1986, after a short run of 24 performances. De Niro's portrayal earned him the Theatre World Special Award, recognizing outstanding Broadway debuts.[16][17] The role marked De Niro's only major acting credit on Broadway, after which he focused predominantly on film.[18]

Directing credits

Directed feature films

A Bronx Tale (1993) marked Robert De Niro's directorial debut, a coming-of-age crime drama adapted from Chazz Palminteri's semi-autobiographical one-man play of the same name.[19] Set in the 1960s Bronx, the film explores the life of a young Italian-American boy, Calogero Anello (played by Francis Capra as a child and Lillo Brancato as a teenager), who becomes mentored by a local mob boss, Sonny (Chazz Palminteri), while navigating tensions with his honest, working-class father Lorenzo (De Niro).[19] De Niro not only directed but also produced the film through Tribeca Productions and narrated its framing device; it was distributed by Savoy Pictures and had a budget of approximately $10 million, grossing over $17 million at the box office.[20] The film earned widespread praise for its authentic portrayal of neighborhood dynamics, racial tensions, and moral conflicts, achieving a 97% approval rating from critics on Rotten Tomatoes based on 35 reviews, with consensus highlighting De Niro's assured handling of the material.[20] It received an Academy Award nomination for Best Supporting Actor for Palminteri and has been noted for its influence on later mob-themed narratives. De Niro's second directorial effort, The Good Shepherd (2006), is a historical espionage thriller chronicling the formative years of the Central Intelligence Agency from World War II through the Bay of Pigs Invasion.[21] Starring Matt Damon as CIA counterintelligence officer Edward Wilson, the film also features Angelina Jolie, William Hurt, and De Niro in a supporting role as a U.S. intelligence operative; De Niro co-produced alongside Jane Rosenthal.[21] With a reported budget exceeding $90 million, it was distributed by Universal Pictures and earned about $100 million worldwide, though it underperformed relative to expectations.[22] Critically, it holds a 56% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from 172 reviews, with some praising its ambitious scope and period detail while others critiqued its pacing and length at 167 minutes as overly deliberate.[22] The project originated from De Niro's long-developed interest in CIA history, drawing on real events and figures but fictionalizing key elements for narrative cohesion.[21] De Niro has not directed any additional feature films as of October 2025.[23]

Producing credits

Films produced without acting involvement

De Niro executive produced the crime thriller Thunderheart (1992), directed by Michael Apted and starring Val Kilmer as an FBI agent investigating ritual murders on a South Dakota Indian reservation amid tensions over land rights and sovereignty.[2] In 1993, De Niro directed and produced A Bronx Tale, adapting Chazz Palminteri's autobiographical one-man play into a narrative exploring a young boy's divided loyalties between his law-abiding father and a charismatic mobster in 1960s New York City.[2] De Niro again directed and produced The Good Shepherd (2006), a sprawling espionage drama depicting the early history of the CIA through the experiences of an intelligence officer (Matt Damon) whose personal life unravels amid Cold War secrecy and betrayals.[21]

Documentary appearances

These include short films, television specials, and episodes focused on his life, collaborations, or personal history.[1] Notable examples include the 2003 television special AFI Life Achievement Award: A Tribute to Robert De Niro, a ceremonial documentary honoring his contributions to film, directed by Louis J.
