Sarah Krasnostein

Sarah Krasnostein is an American-Australian non-fiction writer and criminal lawyer, born in Charlottesville, Virginia, who holds a PhD in sentencing and criminal procedural law from Monash University.[1][2] Admitted to legal practice in New York and Victoria, Australia, she has lectured in postgraduate criminal law and taught creative writing while contributing criticism to outlets including The Monthly and The Saturday Paper.[3][2] Her debut book, The Trauma Cleaner (2017), profiles trauma cleaning specialist Sandra Pankhurst alongside her clients' stories of decay and disaster, becoming a bestseller that won Australia's Victorian Prize for Literature, the Australian Book Industry Award for General Non-Fiction, and the NSW Premier's Literary Award for Non-Fiction.[3][2][1] Subsequent works include The Believer (2021), which examines belief through profiles of figures such as a death doula, ufologists, and a woman accused of murder, earning recognition as a Best Book of 2022 by The New Yorker; the Quarterly Essay Not Waving, Drowning (2022); and On Peter Carey (2023).[3][1] Krasnostein has received the Pascall Prize for Arts Criticism (2022) and Walkley Awards for arts criticism (2022) and long-form feature writing (2024), with her oeuvre praised for compassionate explorations of human extremes grounded in legal and empirical insight.[3]

Early Life and Background

Birth and Family Heritage

Sarah Krasnostein was born in Charlottesville, Virginia, in the United States.[1] She identifies as a fourth-generation American, reflecting her family's longstanding roots in the country, alongside third-generation Australian ties through ancestral migrations.[4] Her heritage traces to Eastern European Jewish grandparents, with paternal forebears originating from Russia and maternal ones from Romania.[4] These lineages underscore a diasporic background common among Ashkenazi Jewish families who emigrated amid historical upheavals in the early 20th century, though specific details on her parents' professions or immediate family migrations remain undocumented in public records.[4]

Upbringing and Influences

Sarah Krasnostein spent her early childhood in Virginia, United States, where she attended a conservative Jewish day school.[4] Her family, maintaining a cultural Jewish identity without strong religious observance, relocated to Melbourne, Australia, when she was 14, exposing her to contrasting American and Australian environments during formative years.[4] This move aligned with her third-generation Australian ties, stemming from ancestral migrations, while her fourth-generation American roots provided an initial base of familiarity with U.S. societal norms.[4] In Melbourne, Krasnostein enrolled in a liberal Jewish school, continuing her education within Jewish institutions across both countries.[4] She has attributed these experiences to shaping key aspects of her worldview, including a tendency to question established authority, a deepened emphasis on familial bonds, profound respect for literary traditions, and early inclinations toward social justice concerns.[4] Her secular Jewish upbringing, informed by Eastern European grandparental heritageâ€”Russia on her father's side and Romania on her mother'sâ€”further cultivated empathy for diverse belief systems and human experiences, without reliance on doctrinal faith.[4][5] Krasnostein grew up primarily under the influence of her father, David Krasnostein, a private equity investor who served as a director for the Melbourne Symphony Orchestra and the National Breast Cancer Foundation, alongside her younger brother Josh, amid estrangement from her mother.[4] This family dynamic, combined with the transcontinental shift, underscored themes of adaptation and cultural duality in her early personal history.[4]

Education and Legal Career

Academic Qualifications

Krasnostein completed a combined Bachelor of Arts and Bachelor of Laws with honours (BA/LLB (Hons)) at the University of Melbourne in 2005.[6] She later obtained a PhD in sentencing and criminal procedural law from the Faculty of Law at Monash University in 2016.[2] Her doctoral thesis, titled Pursuing consistency: the effect of different reforms on unjustified disparity in individualised sentencing frameworks, analyzed the impact of various judicial and legislative reforms on reducing unjustified disparities within discretionary, individualized sentencing systems in common law jurisdictions.[7]

Professional Legal Work and Research

Krasnostein was admitted as an attorney to the State of New York Bar in 2006, following preparation through specialized bar review programs noted for high pass rates.[8] She gained admission to practice law in the state of Victoria, Australia, in 2009, enabling professional engagement in both jurisdictions.[3][9] Her doctoral research, completed in 2016 at Monash University Faculty of Law, centered on sentencing consistency within individualized frameworks, examining reforms to reduce unjustified disparities in criminal sentencing outcomes.[2] The thesis analyzed empirical data from Australian courts, including guideline judgments and statistical variations in penalties for serious offenses, to assess procedural mechanisms for balancing discretion and uniformity.[7] Krasnostein co-authored peer-reviewed scholarship on criminal sentencing empirics, including a 2013 analysis of statistical tools and appellate interventions to mitigate disparity in Victoria's individualistic system under the Sentencing Act 1991.[10] Additional publications addressed appellate oversight in sexual offense cases and critiques of over-individualization in decisions like Bugmy v The Queen (2013), arguing for structured approaches to indigenous offender disadvantages without undermining general deterrence.[11][12] These works drew on court data and procedural law to advocate evidence-based reforms, bridging doctrinal analysis with quantitative review of justice system outcomes.[13]

Writing Career

Initial Publications and Style

Krasnostein's entry into non-fiction writing occurred through her 2014 essay "The Secret Life of a Crime Scene Cleaner," published on Longreads, which profiled Australian trauma cleaner Sandra Pankhurst and was selected for Narratively's Top 10 Stories of that year.[14][15] This piece, originating from Krasnostein's encounters during forensic research, marked her shift from legal scholarship to immersive narrative journalism, laying the groundwork for her debut book The Trauma Cleaner three years later.[16] Her early style draws directly from her criminal law doctorate, emphasizing empirical observation through prolonged fieldworkâ€”accompanying subjects on jobs, conducting extended interviews, and documenting behavioral patterns to trace causal factors in human experiencesâ€”rather than relying on secondary sources or conjecture.[16][17] This approach fosters precise, evidence-based accounts of marginal lives, prioritizing discernment of facts over moral judgment, as honed in legal fact-finding.[17] In transitioning to prose, Krasnostein adapted legal rigor into accessible storytelling that avoids sensationalism, using vivid yet restrained language to reveal underlying realities of decay, resilience, and choice without narrative embellishment or ideological overlay.[18] Her essays integrate firsthand sensory details with analytical depth, constructing portraits grounded in verifiable interactions to illuminate behavioral causation over abstract theorizing.[16]

Evolution as a Critic and Author

Krasnostein's transition into arts criticism marked a significant expansion of her professional scope beyond narrative non-fiction, beginning with her appointment as television critic for The Saturday Paper around 2020. Her reviews dissected contemporary programming with a focus on thematic depth and cultural implications, such as explorations of memory and corporate control in series like Severance.[19] This role showcased her ability to apply forensic scrutiny to visual storytelling, earning recognition for elevating television discourse in Australian media. By 2022, her contributions culminated in the Pascall Prize for Arts Criticism, awarded for the precision and insight in her Saturday Paper reviews, which judges praised for navigating complex social undercurrents in an era of fragmented viewing habits.[20] Parallel to this, her authorship matured from granular individual case studies in early works to probing societal structures, particularly around belief systems and psychological resilience. Subsequent projects, including essays in The Monthly and literary analyses like On Peter Carey (2023), reflected this pivot, integrating broader interrogations of faith, doubt, and institutional failures without relying on singular biographies. This evolution underscored her growing influence, evidenced by a 2024 Walkley Award for long-form feature writing alongside her 2022 arts criticism accolade.[3] Her legal doctorate in criminal law informed this progression, enabling a methodical dismantling of unchallenged assumptions in media portrayals of justice and human behavior, where she prioritized evidentiary coherence over sensationalism.[1] Through these developments, Krasnostein established herself as a versatile critic-author, bridging legal empiricism with cultural commentary to influence public discourse on normalized narratives in entertainment and beyond. Her work's trajectory highlights an increasing emphasis on systemic patternsâ€”such as the interplay of personal conviction and collective delusionâ€”fostering deeper engagements with audiences and peers in Australian letters.[21]

Major Works

The Trauma Cleaner (2017)

The Trauma Cleaner: One Woman's Extraordinary Life in the Business of Death, Decay and Disaster, Krasnostein's debut book, originated from her 2014 long-form essay "The Secret Life of a Crime Scene Cleaner," published on Longreads and recognized in Narratively's top stories of that year, which she expanded into a full biography following extended access to her subject.[22][23] Published in October 2017 by Text Publishing in Australia, the 272-page work details the life of Sandra Pankhurst, who endured childhood abuse and familial violence before transitioning in adulthood from a role as husband and father to identifying as a woman, engaging in drag performance and sex work, and eventually establishing a specialist business in biohazard and trauma scene cleanup in Melbourne.[24][25] The narrative centers on Pankhurst's professional routine of restoring disordered spacesâ€”such as hoarder homes, suicide sites, and murder scenesâ€”for clients often isolated by mental illness, addiction, or loss, drawing from her direct observations of over 20 such cleanups conducted alongside Pankhurst.[25] Krasnostein structures the book as an interwoven biography, alternating between Pankhurst's personal historyâ€”marked by institutionalization, family estrangement, and self-reinventionâ€”and case studies of her clients' circumstances, which reveal patterns of accumulated decay mirroring unresolved emotional wounds.[26] This approach is grounded in extensive interviews with Pankhurst, her employees, family members, and clients, providing firsthand accounts that substantiate the trajectories from trauma to coping mechanisms like hoarding or withdrawal.[24] Core themes emphasize empirically observed connections between endured adversity and subsequent capacity for detached yet compassionate intervention, as Pankhurst's own history of survival equips her to navigate clients' literal and figurative filth without judgment, fostering incremental order amid chaos.[27] These insights, derived from verified personal testimonies rather than abstract theory, highlight resilience as a pragmatic adaptation: Pankhurst's business, started after her time as a funeral director identified a gap in post-trauma remediation, processes around 100 jobs annually, addressing biohazards like blood, feces, and decomposition while addressing clients' underlying isolation.[28] The book received the Victorian Premier's Literary Award for Non-Fiction and the $100,000 Victorian Prize for Literature in 2018, underscoring its documentary rigor.[29][2]

The Believer (2021)

The Believer: Encounters with Love, Death & Faith, published in March 2021 by Text Publishing in Australia, examines human belief systems through immersive profiles of individuals and groups confronting existential uncertainties.[30] Krasnostein profiles a Buddhist death doula assisting the dying, paranormal investigators pursuing evidence of the afterlife, staff at the Creation Museum advocating young-Earth creationism, and UFO enthusiasts interpreting anomalous phenomena as extraterrestrial contact, among others.[5] These accounts highlight belief's role in providing structure amid ambiguity, drawing on direct observations and conversations to illustrate how convictionsâ€”whether religious, spiritual, or pseudoscientificâ€”address gaps in empirical knowledge about origins, mortality, and cosmic position.[31] Krasnostein's methodology emphasizes empathetic immersion without endorsement or dismissal, prioritizing descriptive fidelity to subjects' rationales and experiences over authorial judgment.[32] She traces causal links between personal historiesâ€”such as grief or intellectual isolationâ€”and the adoption of explanatory frameworks, underscoring belief's adaptive function in processing unresolvable questions like death's finality or life's purpose.[30] The narrative avoids romanticization, instead presenting beliefs as pragmatic responses grounded in subjective evidence, while noting tensions with scientific consensus, as seen in Creation Museum exhibits reconciling biblical literalism with geological data.[5] The U.S. edition, subtitled Encounters with the Beginning, the End, and Our Place in the Middle and released by Tin House Books in 2022, received acclaim for its nuanced exploration of conviction's psychological underpinnings.[33] It was named one of the best books of 2022 by The New Yorker, praised for blurring fact-fiction boundaries in revelatory ways through accounts of ghost hunting and similar pursuits.[34] Reviews highlighted empirical insights into belief persistence, such as how paranormal communities employ quasi-scientific protocols to validate experiences, reflecting broader patterns of human cognition favoring pattern recognition over probabilistic skepticism.[31] Krasnostein incorporates data on phenomena like near-death reports and creationist visitor demographics to contextualize individual stories, critiquing institutional dismissals while maintaining causal focus on belief's origins in lived exigencies rather than delusion.[32]

Essays and Other Non-Fiction

Krasnostein's essay "Not Waving, Drowning: Mental Illness and Vulnerability in Australia," published as Quarterly Essay 85 in March 2022, examines the nation's mental health crisis through portraits of three women interacting with state institutions, highlighting systemic policy failures and the erosion of moral authority in addressing human distress.[35] The piece draws on historical observations, such as 18th-century accounts of despair among early settlers, to argue that societal complacency and inadequate causal understanding exacerbate contemporary vulnerabilities, referencing empirical data like prevalence rates where nearly half of Australians report mental health issues.[35] Krasnostein critiques over-reliance on therapeutic interventions without addressing root structural and historical factors, advocating for policies grounded in evidence rather than expansive individualization of suffering.[36] In her 2023 essay "On Peter Carey," part of the Writers on Writers series, Krasnostein analyzes the Australian author's True History of the Kelly Gang (2000) to interrogate themes of home, exile, history, and identity, informed by her own relocation from the United States to Australia.[37] The work blends literary critique with personal reflection, emphasizing Carey's stylistic depth in portraying dislocation and longing, while connecting these to broader questions of national and individual belonging without uncritical endorsement of progressive historical revisions.[37] Krasnostein's "Peace in the Home," published in The Monthly in November 2023, scrutinizes the justice system's treatment of shame and trauma in child sexual abuse prosecutions, using the Malka Leifer case as a lens to expose tensions between victim support and evidentiary demands.[38] The essay challenges framings that prioritize therapeutic narratives over rigorous causal inquiry into offender accountability and institutional delays, drawing on trial specifics like Leifer's 2023 conviction after years of extradition battles.[38] These shorter non-fiction pieces, appearing in outlets like Quarterly Essay and The Monthly, underscore Krasnostein's approach to social and cultural issues by integrating firsthand reporting with critiques of empirically unsupported policies, contributing to her profile for dissecting vulnerabilities beyond dominant interpretive lenses.[3]

Reception and Critical Analysis

Awards and Recognitions

Krasnostein's debut book The Trauma Cleaner (2017) received the Victorian Prize for Literature, worth $100,000, and the Premier's Prize for Non-Fiction, worth $25,000, at the 2018 Victorian Premier's Literary Awards; these honors, among Australia's most significant state-level literary recognitions, were awarded for the work's innovative narrative non-fiction blending biography and reportage.[29] The same title also secured the Australian Book Industry Award for General Non-Fiction Book of the Year in 2018, determined by industry votes reflecting commercial and cultural impact.[39] Additionally, it earned the $5,000 Dobbie Literary Award in 2018, given annually to an Australian woman's first published book, and a joint win in the Douglas Stewart Prize for Non-Fiction at the New South Wales Premier's Literary Awards that year.[40] For her contributions to criticism, Krasnostein was awarded the 2022 Pascall Prize for Critical Writing, Australia's premier honor for arts criticism, recognizing her television reviews in The Saturday Paper.[41] In 2024, she received the Walkley Award in the Feature Writing Long category (over 4,000 words) for her essay "Peace in the Home: The Trial of Malka Leifer," published in The Monthly; the Walkleys represent Australia's highest journalism standards, judged on depth, originality, and public interest.[42]

Positive Reviews and Achievements

Krasnostein's debut book The Trauma Cleaner (2017) received acclaim for its compassionate yet unflinching examination of trauma and human resilience, with reviewers highlighting the author's ability to blend empathetic storytelling with stark realism. The Sydney Morning Herald described it as "one of the most arresting works of biography you will read in a long time," praising Krasnostein's playful yet heartfelt approach to subjects often overlooked in mainstream narratives.[43] Similarly, The Guardian commended the work's sensitivity in probing the tragedies behind scenes of decay and disaster, noting its origins in an online essay that expanded into a full-length exploration of extraordinary lives.[25] The book topped bestseller lists at the 2018 Australian Book Industry Awards Weekend, reflecting strong readership and commercial success in narrative non-fiction.[44] In The Believer (2021), Krasnostein extended her narrative style to interrogate belief systems ranging from religious faith to paranormal pursuits, earning praise for its lyrical prose and empathetic depth. Reviewers noted the book's "gorgeously written" quality and Krasnostein's rare gift for holding space for diverse viewpoints without judgment, fostering nuanced discussions on human conviction and uncertainty.[45] The Guardian highlighted its stylish and invigorating imagination, particularly in weaving personal reflection with interviews that illuminate marginalized perspectives on existence and the afterlife.[30] This work contributed to elevating immersive non-fiction by grounding abstract themes in verifiable personal testimonies, influencing broader conversations on trauma recovery and epistemological boundaries through its evidence-based empathy.[46] Krasnostein's oeuvre has achieved broader impact via adaptations and sustained readership, including a 2022 documentary film based on The Trauma Cleaner, which brought its themes of order amid chaos to visual audiences.[47] Her books' high aggregate ratingsâ€”such as 3.9 out of 5 from nearly 30,000 Goodreads reviews for The Trauma Cleanerâ€”underscore empirical acclaim for advancing narrative non-fiction's role in dissecting societal fringes with causal precision rather than sentimentality.[48]

Criticisms and Debates

Krasnostein's empathetic immersion in her subjects' lives has prompted questions about journalistic objectivity, particularly in The Trauma Cleaner (2017), where her preface describes the work as a "love letter" to Sandra Pankhurst, reflecting a close personal bond that some reviewers argue risks emotional compromise.[27] One analysis contends that this proximity leads to a loss of detachment, with facts presented in a "very biased manner" designed to elicit predetermined emotional responses rather than allowing readers independent judgment.[49] Such critiques extend to perceived overemphasis on sympathetic framings intertwined with social advocacy, as the same review accuses Krasnostein of being "swept up in gender politics and anti-heteronormative polemics," which allegedly slows the narrative with tangential societal indictments and portrays Pankhurst as a quasi-martyr figure.[49] While mainstream outlets have largely praised her nuance in handling unreliable narration due to trauma, these dissenting views highlight tensions between vivid personal storytelling and detached analysis in true crime and biography genres.[27] In The Believer (2021), Krasnostein explicitly incorporates her intuitive method and personal biasesâ€”such as influences from her own mental health experiencesâ€”into the exploration of unconventional faiths, defending it as a transparent filter for non-fiction rather than concealed subjectivity.[50] This approach invites debate on whether such self-disclosure mitigates or amplifies risks of selective connections, especially when envying subjects' certainties amid empirical absence of evidence.[46] Her academic contributions to criminal law, including critiques of sentencing practices, engage ongoing debates over individualization versus uniformity. In analyzing Bugmy v The Queen (2013), Krasnostein warned that excessive deference to offenders' disadvantaged backgrounds fosters "too much individualisation, not enough justice," potentially yielding inconsistent outcomes that undermine public trust in the system.[12] This position counters narratives prioritizing personal mitigation over standardized accountability, aligning with calls for empirical consistency to address disparities without excusing culpability.[51]

Personal Life

Marriage and Relationships

Krasnostein has been married to Australian comedian and television presenter Charlie Pickering since 2013.[52] The couple met through mutual friends at a bar and became engaged in November 2011 after dating for about a year.[53] [54] They have one child, born in 2014.[52] [55] Pickering cited the impending birth and desire for greater family involvement as key reasons for departing his co-host role on The Project in 2014, signaling the relationship's role in prioritizing work-life balance.[56] The family resides in Melbourne, where Krasnostein has conducted much of her writing from home alongside domestic responsibilities.[4]

Residence and Personal Challenges

Krasnostein was born in Charlottesville, Virginia, in the United States and later relocated to Australia for her studies in Melbourne.[1] She has since divided her time between Melbourne, where she maintains her primary residence, and New York City, reflecting her professional commitments in both countries.[1] [57] This bicoastal lifestyle aligns with her work as a writer and lecturer, having lived and worked extensively in the United States and Australia.[3] Krasnostein's early life included the challenge of her mother's departure from the family when she was young, an event that shaped aspects of her personal history and worldview.[50] She has described experiencing significant mental health difficulties, including prolonged periods of anxiety and depression that confined her largely to her room and necessitated daily medication.[50] These struggles, which she has reflected on in her writing, underscore a vulnerability to isolation that parallels themes in her examinations of belief and human resilience.[58]
