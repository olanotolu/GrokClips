Sarafina Nance


Sarafina El-Badry Nance is an Egyptian-American astrophysicist specializing in supernovae and cosmology, as well as an author and science communicator. She holds a Ph.D. in astrophysics from the University of California, Berkeley, following undergraduate degrees from the University of Texas at Austin and earlier graduate work.[1] Her research examines explosive stellar events and their implications for understanding the universe's expansion.[2] Nance gained public attention for her 2023 memoir Starstruck: A Memoir of Astrophysics and Finding Light in the Dark, which chronicles her scientific pursuits alongside personal challenges, including a preventive double mastectomy at age 26 due to a BRCA2 gene mutation that elevated her breast cancer risk to 87%; the surgery lowered it to under 5%.[3][4] The book received recognition as a finalist for the Northern California Book Awards and a longlist spot for the PEN/E.O. Wilson Literary Science Writing Award.[3] In 2022, she participated in the HI-SEAS analog mission simulating Mars habitat conditions, conducting astrophysics experiments in isolation.[3] As an advocate, Nance promotes genetic testing for hereditary cancer risks and equity for women in STEM, drawing from her experiences with family cancer history and surgical reconstruction that preserved sensation.[4] She communicates science through social media, YouTube videos, and appearances, including Forbes' 30 Under 30 in Science list in 2021, emphasizing empirical observation over narrative-driven interpretations in astronomy.[5] In 2023, she posed for Sports Illustrated's Swimsuit issue post-mastectomy, highlighting physical resilience in professional contexts.[6]

Early life and influences

Family background and childhood

Sarafina El-Badry Nance was born in the United States as the only child of an Egyptian mother and an American father, growing up in Austin, Texas.[7] Her parents placed strong emphasis on academic achievement, fostering an environment that encouraged intellectual pursuits from early childhood.[8] As an Egyptian-American in a predominantly non-Arab community, Nance faced cultural assimilation pressures intensified by events like the September 11, 2001 attacks, which heightened scrutiny and stigma toward Arab heritage in the U.S. In response, she disavowed much of her Arabic background publicly during her youth, instructing her mother not to speak Arabic to her in public and feigning ignorance of the language to avoid attention.[9] [7] This personal strategy reflected broader challenges for Arab-American families post-9/11, where data from sources like the Arab American Institute indicate spikes in discrimination reportsâ€”over 200 incidents in the immediate aftermathâ€”yet individual choices, such as Nance's focus on personal resilience, often drove adaptation over collective victimhood narratives. Nance's father played a pivotal role in countering these tensions by engaging her in regular stargazing sessions under Texas skies, using binoculars to identify constellations and stellar features, which ignited her lifelong interest in astronomy despite emerging family health concerns later in her youth.[10] [11] These experiences underscored a family dynamic prioritizing curiosity and empirical wonder, shaping her early worldview toward scientific inquiry amid cultural disconnection.[6]

Development of scientific interests

Nance's interest in astronomy originated in her childhood in suburban Austin, Texas, where she frequently stargazed with her father on clear evenings, fostering a profound curiosity about the cosmos.[10][12] These sessions, often involving simple observations of bright stars visible in the Texas sky, ignited her independent exploration of celestial phenomena, including basic concepts of star formation and the universe's scale. By age three or four, she had developed a routine of listening to NPR's StarDate radio program during drives home from school, which introduced her to astronomical events and terminology, reinforcing her self-directed fascination without formal instruction.[13] In high school, Nance's enthusiasm was further nurtured through merit-based academic engagement, particularly by her physics teacher who recognized and encouraged her aptitude for space-related topics.[13] This period marked a transition from casual observation to structured inquiry, as she improved in mathematics mid-high school and delved deeper into physics principles applicable to astrophysics, driven by intrinsic motivation rather than external programs. Her experiences culminated in a resolve to pursue formal studies in the field, grounded in personal wonder at the night sky's mysteries rather than institutional incentives.[14]

Education

Undergraduate studies

Nance completed her undergraduate education at the University of Texas at Austin, earning dual Bachelor of Science degrees in physics and astronomy in 2016 from the College of Natural Sciences.[13][15] The degrees included honors in both fields, reflecting her strong performance in foundational coursework such as classical mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum physics, stellar astrophysics, and observational astronomy techniques.[13] As a graduating senior, she received the Dean's Honored Graduate designation from the College of Natural Sciences, the institution's highest distinction for undergraduates demonstrating exceptional academic achievement, typically awarded to the top 20% of degree recipients based on GPA and other merit criteria.[16][17] Nance engaged in undergraduate research at UT Austin's Astronomy Department, focusing on Wolf-Rayet starsâ€”massive, evolved stars characterized by strong stellar winds and potential supernova progenitorsâ€”and related supernova dynamics, which provided hands-on experience with data analysis from telescopes and computational modeling essential for her subsequent astrophysics pursuits.[16] This work built a foundation in stellar evolution and explosive phenomena, aligning with core undergraduate projects in observational and theoretical astronomy.[18]

Graduate training and PhD

Nance pursued graduate studies in astronomy at the University of California, Berkeley, beginning after a gap year following her undergraduate degree.[13] She earned a Master of Science in Astronomy from Berkeley en route to her doctoral degree.[13] As a National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellow, Nance advanced to PhD candidacy under the co-advisorship of Peter Nugent and Raffaella Margutti in the Department of Astronomy.[17][19] Her dissertation research centered on Type IIP supernovae as standardizable candles for cosmology, analyzing photometric and spectroscopic data from the Zwicky Transient Facility to estimate the Hubble constant and probe tensions in cosmological models.[19] This work involved processing samples of approximately 67 Type IIP events, yielding a Hubble constant measurement of 70.2 Â± 5.9 km sâ»Â¹ Mpcâ»Â¹.[19] Nance completed her Doctor of Philosophy in Astronomy in summer 2025, with her dissertation titled Type IIP Supernovae for Cosmology.[19] During her candidacy, her contributions to supernova analysis earned selection for Forbes' 30 Under 30 Science list in 2021, recognizing her efforts to refine exploding stars as probes of cosmic expansion and composition.[20]

Scientific research

Areas of specialization

Sarafina Nance specializes in core-collapse supernovae, particularly Type IIP events, which involve the explosive deaths of massive stars and serve as standardizable candles for measuring extragalactic distances.[21] These supernovae provide empirical data on the universe's expansion rate through their light curves and spectra, enabling independent probes of cosmological parameters without relying on Type Ia supernovae.[5] Her work emphasizes modeling the physical processes driving these explosions, such as shock propagation and progenitor star properties, to refine distance estimates and address tensions in Hubble constant measurements.[22] In computational cosmology, Nance employs numerical simulations to analyze supernova populations and their implications for the large-scale structure and evolution of the universe.[5] This involves integrating observational datasets with first-principles hydrodynamic models to simulate core-collapse dynamics, focusing on causal mechanisms like neutrino-driven winds and fallback accretion that determine remnant formation, such as neutron stars or black holes.[19] These simulations ground her research in verifiable stellar evolution pathways, highlighting supernovae's role in heavy element production via rapid nucleosynthesis during explosive burning phases.[19] Her expertise extends to applying supernova observations for broader cosmological inference, prioritizing data-driven calibration over theoretical assumptions to quantify expansion history and dark energy constraints.[23] This approach leverages multi-wavelength photometry to mitigate systematic uncertainties in progenitor mass estimates and explosion energies, ensuring robust empirical foundations for universe-scale models.[21]

Key contributions and publications

Nance's doctoral research at the University of California, Berkeley, focused on Type IIP supernovae as cosmological probes, proposing methodologies to utilize their light curves for measuring the universe's expansion rate as an alternative to Type Ia supernovae, which have historically dominated such analyses due to their standardized brightness.[19] Her 2023 dissertation, Type IIP Supernovae for Cosmology, analyzed Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) light curves and spectral data from over 100 events, developing empirical calibrations for plateau luminosity and duration to infer distances and Hubble constant values, addressing limitations in sample size and plateau-phase modeling for core-collapse events.[19] Earlier contributions include modeling Betelgeuse's asteroseismic properties to constrain its evolutionary stage and potential supernova timeline, published in a 2018 Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society paper co-authored with J. Craig Wheeler's group at the University of Texas at Austin.[24] This work integrated hydrodynamic simulations with observed radial velocity variations to estimate the star's core mass and pulsation modes, providing empirical bounds on its proximity to core collapse despite its variable brightness. In collaboration with the ZTF Bright Transient Survey team, Nance contributed to a 2022 Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society study on "supernova siblings"â€”pairs or groups of explosions in the same host galaxyâ€”analyzing their light curves to probe environmental factors influencing core-collapse rates and galactic chemical evolution.[25] The analysis, drawing from 2018â€“2020 ZTF data, quantified relative rates and host offsets, validating models of stellar remnant formation and feedback in star-forming regions through statistical comparisons with simulations.[26] No peer-reviewed publications post-PhD directly extend her astrophysics work into analog astronaut simulations, though her involvement in Mars habitat isolation studies has been noted in outreach contexts without formal research outputs linking back to supernova or cosmology datasets.[22] Her pre-doctoral thesis at UT Austin, A Theoretical Investigation of Supernovae Progenitors (2016), modeled core-collapse dynamics but served primarily as foundational training rather than standalone impact.[27]

Science communication and outreach

Media engagements and public speaking

Nance has participated in numerous public speaking engagements to elucidate astrophysical principles for non-specialist audiences, emphasizing empirical observations of cosmic events such as supernovae. On June 30, 2023, she presented at Talks at Google, exploring the mechanisms of stellar explosions and their observational signatures through telescope data.[28] In a June 8, 2023, event at the Commonwealth Club, she addressed challenges in astronomical research while highlighting verifiable patterns in stellar evolution derived from spectral analysis.[29] Her media appearances include radio segments focused on space exploration and data interpretation. On June 12, 2023, Nance featured on NPR's Here & Now, discussing Betelgeuse's potential supernova and the quantitative models predicting its brightness variability based on radial velocity measurements.[30] She also appeared on The Edge podcast on July 13, 2023, analyzing the universe's expansion rates and the role of redshift data in cosmological models.[31] Additional podcast discussions, such as on Planetary Radio on June 7, 2023, centered on exoplanet detection techniques using transit photometry and radial velocity curves.[12] As an analog astronaut, Nance simulated Martian isolation to draw parallels between confined experimental conditions and astrophysical fieldwork. In August 2021, she joined a two-week HI-SEAS mission on Mauna Loa, Hawaii, where crew members conducted geological surveys and astronomical observations under resource constraints mimicking Mars, fostering insights into human factors in remote data collection for stellar studies.[2] This experience informed her communications on the causal links between environmental stressors and sustained scientific output in isolated settings.[32] Nance's op-eds reinforce method-driven narratives of cosmic inquiry. In a September 1, 2021, Nautilus piece, she detailed adapting supernova research protocols during the HI-SEAS simulation, prioritizing instrumental readings over subjective interpretations to model extraterrestrial observation challenges.[2] These platforms consistently prioritize falsifiable evidence from observations, such as light curve analyses, in explaining astrophysical phenomena to public audiences.

Personal health decisions

Family cancer history

Sarafina Nance's father was diagnosed with aggressive prostate cancer during her college years, with the disease advancing to stage IV within six months and shocking medical professionals.[4] This diagnosis, occurring around 2019, led to genetic testing that revealed he carried the BRCA2 mutation, which Nance inherited from him.[38] BRCA2 mutations in males are linked to higher incidences of aggressive prostate cancer due to impaired DNA repair mechanisms, increasing tumor aggressiveness and metastatic potential.[39] Her paternal grandmother was diagnosed with ovarian cancer when Nance was a child and battled the disease for several years before dying from pancreatic cancer.[6] Both ovarian and pancreatic cancers show elevated familial risks in BRCA2 kindreds, with transmission rates following autosomal dominant inheritance patterns where each child of a carrier parent has a 50% chance of inheriting the mutation.[4] In female carriers like those in Nance's family, the BRCA2 variant elevates lifetime breast cancer risk to approximately 87%, ovarian cancer to 30%, and prostate or pancreatic cancer risks in relatives through shared genetic predisposition.[4] Paternal BRCA2-positive prostate cancer specifically heightens daughters' hereditary breast and ovarian cancer probabilities, as evidenced by clinical guidelines urging genetic screening for female relatives of affected fathers.[39] These empirical risks, derived from large cohort studies of mutation carriers, underscore the causal role of germline BRCA2 alterations in familial cancer clustering.[38]

Preventive mastectomy and reconstruction

In 2019, at the age of 26, Sarafina Nance underwent a preventive bilateral mastectomy after testing positive for a BRCA2 gene mutation in 2017, which confers a substantially elevated lifetime risk of breast cancer estimated at 55â€“72% for carriers compared to 12â€“13% in the general population.[40][41] The surgery, performed as a prophylactic measure to mitigate this risk absent any personal history of cancer, involved removal of both breasts followed by immediate reconstruction using implants.[4][38] This multi-stage process spanned three operations, incorporating a nerve-sparing technique that preserved sensory nerves to facilitate partial retention of sensation in the reconstructed breasts.[38] Empirical data indicate that bilateral prophylactic mastectomy reduces breast cancer incidence by over 90% in high-risk individuals like BRCA2 carriers, with studies reporting risk reductions of 95% or more when combined with oophorectomy in some cohorts.[42][43] Nance's procedure aligned with this evidence-based approach, lowering her projected risk from approximately 87% to under 5%. Post-operatively, she experienced a recovery period involving physical challenges such as pain management and monitoring for fluid accumulation, alongside psychological adjustments to altered body image, though she reported regaining tactile sensation within a year via the specialized surgical method.[6][38] Notwithstanding these benefits, prophylactic mastectomy with implant reconstruction entails notable complications, including infection (up to 17%), seroma (17%), capsular contracture, implant rupture or deflation, and potential need for reoperation in 10â€“20% of cases, particularly in younger patients where long-term implant durability remains uncertain.[44][45] Critics argue that such interventions may represent over-treatment for pre-symptomatic individuals in their 20s, given the deferred nature of BRCA-associated risks and the absence of immediate malignancy, potentially outweighing benefits with cumulative surgical morbidities like flap necrosis or chronic pain.[23][46] Nance's case exemplifies the trade-offs, with sustained risk aversion achieved at the cost of irreversible anatomical change and ongoing vigilance for reconstructive failures.[47]

Public perception and controversies

Sports Illustrated appearance

Sarafina El-Badry Nance participated as a finalist in the Sports Illustrated Swim Search for the 2022 Swimsuit Issue, selected from thousands of applicants.[48][49] The competition, aimed at discovering diverse models, featured her among a class of finalists announced on March 8, 2022.[50] Her photoshoot took place on March 13, 2022, in the Dominican Republic, photographed by Yu Tsai.[51][52] Nance, then 28 years old and an astrophysicist from Austin, Texas, debuted in the issue to showcase her post-mastectomy physique, which she underwent prophylactically at age 26 due to BRCA2 gene mutation.[53][48] The feature highlighted her intent to challenge stereotypes associating scientists with conventional appearances, promoting body positivity and personal agency in displaying her reconstructed form.[6][54] The 2022 Swimsuit Issue, released in May, included Nance's images as part of the Swim Search results, emphasizing resilience and self-acceptance following her preventive surgery.[55][56]
