Queen Charlotte's and Chelsea Hospital

Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust Queen Charlotte's and Chelsea Hospital is one of the oldest maternity hospitals in Europe, founded in 1752 in London. Until October 2000,[1] it occupied sites in Marylebone Road and at 339–351 Goldhawk Road, Hammersmith, but is now located between East Acton and White City, adjacent to the Hammersmith Hospital. It is managed by the Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust. Website: Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust.

History

The hospital strictly dates its foundation to 1739 when Sir Richard Manningham established a maternity hospital of lying-in beds in a 17-room house on Jermyn Street.[2] This hospital was called the General Lying-in Hospital, and it was the first of its kind in Britain. In 1752 the hospital relocated from Jermyn Street to Marylebone Road and became one of the first teaching institutions.[3] The hospital appears to have arisen out of the 1739 foundation, but with varying degrees of recognition, developing over time.[4]: 1–18 On 10 January 1782 a licence was granted to the hospital charity by the Justices of the County of Middlesex (at that time a legal requirement for all maternity hospitals).[4]: 2 In 1809 the Duke of Sussex persuaded his mother, Queen Charlotte, to become patron of the hospital: it became, at that time, the Queen's Lying-in Hospital.[4]: 12 The queen held an annual ball to raise funds for the hospital. The medical centre moved to the Old Manor House at Lisson Green in Marylebone in 1813[4]: 20 where it was completely rebuilt to a design by Charles Hawkins in 1856.[4]: 28 Queen Victoria granted a Royal Charter to the hospital in 1885.[4]: 40 It was renamed Queen Charlotte's Maternity Hospital and Midwifery Training School in 1923.[5][6] Maternal death was a common occurrence in London throughout the 19th and 20th centuries, especially among healthy young women who were in good health prior to their pregnancies.[7] For more than a century, the maternal death rate was used to measure the effectiveness of maternity services and treatment.[7] One specific cause of maternal death, postpartum infection (then known as childbed fever, and now also as puerperal sepsis), was referred to as the doctor's plague, because it was more common in hospitals than in home births. Once the method of transmission was understood in 1931, an isolation block was created in Goldhawk Road.[5] The rest of the maternity hospital moved to Goldhawk Road to co-locate with the isolation block in 1940.[5] In 1948, following the creation of the National Health Service, the hospital linked up with the Chelsea Hospital for Women to form a combined teaching school.[5] The Chelsea Hospital for Women moved from Fulham Road to share the site under the new title Queen Charlotte's & Chelsea Hospital in 1988.[5] In 2000 the hospital moved to Du Cane Road, next to the Hammersmith Hospital.[5]

Facilities

The hospital has a specialist maternal medicine unit for London, recognising that a need existed for specialist care to be offered to pregnant women who suffered from pre-existing medical conditions, or conditions that developed during pregnancy, whose treatment might impact upon the pregnancy. The unit is known as the de Swiet Obstetric Medicine Centre.[19] The hospital's maternity services include a labour ward and a birth centre. The delivery suites in the labour ward offer women a more traditional childbirth experience, while the birth centre strives to create a more "homely" environment. The labour ward is a larger unit, and together with the birth centre, the hospital conducts approximately 5,000 births annually as of 2023.[20] Women giving birth in these areas have access to epidurals during their birth in the labour ward. The birth centre focuses on natural births, often using birth pools without epidural analgesia. Infants born in both the labour ward and birth centre have access to the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU).[21]

Research Initiatives at Queen Charlotte's and Chelsea Hospital

In 2016 the hospital partnered with Tommy's National Centre for Miscarriage Research, an organization that provides financial support for research on birth complications. This partnership established a miscarriage clinic at the hospital, which provides medical treatment for women who are participating in a research study related to miscarriages.[22] The goal of this initiative is to reduce the number of miscarriages by 50% by the year 2030 through better understanding of what causes miscarriages.[22] Tommy's created a pledge to research genetic causes of miscarriages, bacteria in miscarriages, and risk indicators of miscarriage during the first five years of the program.[23] The centre remains active as of 2025, hosting research on miscarriage causes and support services. [24] An additional 2016 initiative attempted to prevent cot death and reduce the infant mortality rate in the United Kingdom.[25] Queen Charlotte's and Chelsea Hospital sent 800 families home with foam mattresses inside of cardboard boxes for their newborn children. These boxes, popular in Finland, are designed to stop infants from rolling onto their stomachs, which is a risk factor for sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS).[25] Queen Charlotte's and Chelsea Hospital participates in several ongoing research projects through the Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust, one of five teaching and research hospitals in London.[26] Maternity-related projects include uterine fibroid ablation.[26] As of October 2024, the hospital joined the Generation Study, led by Genomics England, testing newborns for over 200 genetic conditions to identify rare diseases early; by October 2025, 500 babies had been recruited at the site. [27] [28]

COSMIC charity

COSMIC is an independent charity supporting the work of the neonatal and paediatric intensive care services of Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust, London. The charity funds a range of specialist equipment for the units, including patient monitoring systems and sensory play stations for those being treated on the wards.[29] COSMIC also funds an annual programme of training across units,[30] and provides emotional and practical support to families with babies and children in intensive care, as well as supporting research into childhood diseases such as studies Kawasaki Disease.[31]
