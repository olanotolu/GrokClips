Queen Beatrix International Airport

Source: AIP[1] Queen Beatrix International Airport (IATA: AUA , ICAO: TNCA) (Dutch: Internationale luchthaven Koningin Beatrix ; Papiamento: Aeropuerto Internacional Reina Beatrix) is an international airport located in the Dutch Caribbean island of Aruba. It features US Customs and Border Protection preclearance for flights to the United States. In April 2025, a new US Check-In Terminal soft-opened as part of the Gateway 2030 expansion project. It has flight services to the United States, Canada, several countries in the Caribbean, the northern coastal countries of South America, as well as some parts of Europe, notably the Netherlands. It is named after Beatrix of the Netherlands, who reigned as Queen of the Netherlands from 1980 to 2013.[2][3]

Overview

The airport offers United States border preclearance facilities.[4] The airport originally served as main hub for Air Aruba until its bankruptcy in 2000. Before Aruba's separation from the Netherlands Antilles in 1986 it was also one of three hubs for ALM Antillean Airlines as well as home base for Tiara Air until 2016. A terminal for private aircraft opened in 2007. Since 2013 the airport is home to Aruba Airlines. As of October 2025, the airline operates one Airbus A320-200 and one Bombardier CRJ200. The main focus of Aruba Airlines is connecting the region through its hub.[5]

History of Aviation in Aruba

Early Aviation

Early aviation in Aruba began with visits by seaplanes. On August 18, 1923, two U.S. Navy Curtiss H-16 flying boats landed in Paardenbaai harbor, Oranjestad, from Coco Solo Naval Air Station, Panama, before continuing to CuraÃ§ao.[6] On July 4, 1925, SCADTA's Dornier Do J Wal seaplane landed in Paardenbaai with Governor Brandjes and harbormaster Hoek, returning to CuraÃ§ao.[6] In 1933, the first locally based aircraft, a Curtiss-Robin, was flown by Manuel Viana, Hathaway, and Massey from a Savaneta mud-flat, later joined by pilot A.J. Viccellio.[6] In 1934, Viana launched a weekly mail and passenger service between Aruba and CuraÃ§ao using a Loening C-2H Air Yacht from a mud-flat runway. Commercial services were taken over by KLM from 24 December 1934. Later they were transferred to a graded runway known as the KLM field.[6] KLM's Snip, the PJ-AIS a Fokker tri-motor, ushered in the scheduled flying age in Aruba on 19 January 1935. On its bi-weekly Aruba-CuraÃ§ao operations, KLM transported 2,695 passengers on 471 flights.[6] The third terminal was inaugurated on March 18, 1950.[6]

World War II

During World War II, the airport was used by the United States Army Air Forces Sixth Air Force defending Caribbean shipping and the Panama Canal against German submarines.[6] The airfield was renamed Dakota Field; the terminal facilities became Dakota Airport.[6] On June 4, 1942, Dakota Airport was inaugurated with a 5,100 ft airstrip built by the U.S. Army Air Corps.[6] It remained operational until October 1945.[6] Flying units assigned to the airfield were:

Post-War Developments

In 1953-1954, the runway was upgraded to 6,445 ft with a 4,500 ft taxiway, improving the ICAO rating to Class C.[6] On 22 October 1955, the airport was named after Princess Beatrix of the Netherlands during a royal visit by Queen Juliana.[6] It was renamed Queen Beatrix International Airport in 1980 after her accession to the throne.[6] The runway was extended in 1964 to accommodate jet aircraft, with the first jet landings occurring on April 10.[6] The fourth terminal was inaugurated on November 8, 1972.[6] In 1999, the Beatrix 2000 project was completed on September 1, expanding the 1972 terminal.[6] On 3 March 2021, American Airlines celebrated its 50 years flying to and from Aruba.[7] Aruba was late to implementing baggage handling advanced enough to relieve U.S.-bound passengers of the traditional legal requirement of physically walking their baggage through U.S. customs inspection. For several decades, this forced U.S.-bound passengers to undergo a time-consuming preclearance procedure: they had to check in baggage, pass through Aruba primary airport security screening followed by Aruba exit customs, then reclaim checked baggage, walk it through immigration and customs inspections at the U.S. Customs and Border Protection port of entry, recheck their baggage, pass through a secondary security screening in accordance with U.S. standards, and then proceed to their departure gates. As part of Phase 1A of Gateway 2030, a massive airport expansion project, the airport built a new U.S. Check-In Terminal with sufficiently advanced baggage handling equipment, thereby relieving U.S.-bound passengers of the burden of reclaiming baggage and undergoing another screening. The soft opening began on April 8, 2025, with the first flights checking in at the new terminal, and the full transition for all U.S. carriers completed by May 2025.[8] A self-bag drop service was introduced in phases starting September 25, 2025, beginning with American Airlines.[9] Phase 1B, including a new concourse with additional gates, was announced in October 2025.[10]

Accidents and incidents

  • On 13 January 2010, an Arkefly Boeing 767-300 (registration PH-AHQ), operating Flight 361 from Amsterdam Schiphol Airport to Queen Beatrix International Airport, declared an emergency after a man claimed to have a bomb on board. A struggle with the flight crew ensued, and the aircraft made an emergency landing at Shannon Airport, Ireland. GardaÃ­ stormed the plane and arrested the man; he was taken to Shannon Garda police station. A passenger who had recently had surgery collapsed in the terminal while waiting for the continuation of the flight, and had to be taken to a local hospital. The replacement aircraft, PH-AHY, also a Boeing 767-300, continued the flight to Aruba.[21]
  • On 4 March 2024, an Amerijet International Boeing 767-300F freighter (registration N1993B) taxiing at Queen Beatrix International Airport blew a parked Cessna 172 Skyhawk onto its side with jet blast while exiting the ramp; the Cessna sustained damage but there were no injuries.[22]
  • On 19 March 2024, Delta Air Lines Flight 581, a Boeing 737-900 (registration N908DA), ingested a bird into one engine shortly after takeoff from Queen Beatrix International Airport bound for Atlanta; the aircraft returned safely to the airport with no injuries reported. The flight was cancelled.[23]
  • On 21 October 2025, American Airlines Flight 874, a Boeing 737 MAX 8 (registration N324RA), encountered severe hail from Tropical Storm Melissa during initial climb from Queen Beatrix International Airport to Charlotte, shattering the cockpit windshield. The aircraft returned to the airport after 2.5 hours with minor damage and no injuries.[24]
