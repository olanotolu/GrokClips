Al Franken


Alan Stuart Franken (born May 21, 1951) is an American comedian, screenwriter, author, radio host, and former United States Senator from Minnesota.[1]
Born in Manhattan, New York, to a Jewish family and raised in the Minneapolis suburb of St. Louis Park, Minnesota, Franken graduated from Harvard University with a B.A. in 1973 before joining the inaugural writing team of Saturday Night Live (SNL), where he served as a writer from 1975 to 1980 and again from 1985 to 1995, also performing on-air and earning five Emmy Awards for his comedic contributions.[1][2][3]
Transitioning to political commentary, he authored books such as Rush Limbaugh Is a Big Fat Idiot (1996) and Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them (2003), which critiqued right-wing media and policies, and hosted the liberal Al Franken Show on Air America Radio from 2004 to 2007.[1][3]
Franken won election to the U.S. Senate as a Democrat in 2008 after a prolonged recount and legal challenge against incumbent Norm Coleman, taking office in 2009 and securing reelection in 2014; he served on committees including Judiciary and Veterans' Affairs, advocating for measures like expanded mental health services in schools, service dogs for veterans with PTSD, and bipartisan reforms to foster care education under the No Child Left Behind Act.[1][4][5]
His Senate tenure ended on December 7, 2017, with his resignation following accusations of sexual misconduct from eight women, primarily involving unwanted physical contact such as groping or attempted kisses, with the most publicized incident a 2006 USO tour photo showing Franken simulating a breast grab over the clothing of a sleeping radio host Leeann Tweeden; while Franken issued apologies, described some actions as poor comedy judgment, and an Ethics Committee investigation was pending, intense pressure from Democratic colleagues amid the #MeToo movementâ€”later regretted by several senators who acknowledged insufficient due processâ€”prompted his departure to avoid partisan distraction.[6][7][8]

Early Life

Family Background and Upbringing

Alan Stuart Franken was born on May 21, 1951, in New York City to Joseph Franken, a printing salesman, and Phoebe Franken (nÃ©e Kunst), a homemaker.[9][10][11] He was raised in a Reform Jewish household alongside his brother, Owen Franken, who later became a photographer.[10][12] When Franken was four years old, his family relocated from New York to Minnesota, initially to Albert Lea in the south, where his father attempted to operate a quilting factory.[9][13][14] The venture did not succeed, and Joseph Franken subsequently worked as a printing salesman while the family settled in St. Louis Park, a Minneapolis suburb known for its Jewish community.[13][15] Franken grew up in this middle-class environment at 7931 West 25th Street, attending local public schools amid a predominantly Jewish neighborhood that shaped his early cultural and religious identity.[15][16]

Education and Early Influences

Franken was born on May 21, 1951, in New York City to Joseph Franken, who owned a printing factory, and Irene Franken; the family relocated to the Minneapolis suburb of St. Louis Park, Minnesota, when he was four years old.[9][17] Raised in a Jewish household affiliated with Temple Israel, a Reform synagogue, Franken absorbed early lessons on justice from his childhood rabbi, alongside his father's enthusiasm for comedy routines viewed together on television.[18][17] He attended the Blake School, a private college-preparatory institution in Hopkins, Minnesota, graduating in 1969 after initially starting at St. Louis Park High School.[19][11][20] There, Franken met Tom Davis, his future long-term comedy writing partner, and the two began developing satirical sketches and performances that foreshadowed his entertainment career.[19][21] His father's Republican affiliation until 1964 also shaped family political discussions, instilling an early interest in partisan dynamics.[22][23] Franken enrolled at Harvard University in 1969, majoring in political science and earning a Bachelor of Arts degree cum laude in 1973.[9][24] During his time there, he continued honing comedic material with Davis and met his future wife, Franni Bryson, a student at the nearby Simmons College.[19] These university years solidified his blend of intellectual pursuits and humor, influenced by familial exposure to political debates and comedic performers.[23][17]

Entertainment Career

Saturday Night Live Tenure

Al Franken joined the writing staff of Saturday Night Live for its debut season in October 1975, contributing to sketches that helped establish the show's satirical style.[25] Alongside writing partner Tom Davis, he co-wrote early pieces such as the "Pong" video game parody aired on November 15, 1975, and the "Little Chocolate Donuts" commercial spoof featuring John Belushi and Dan Aykroyd.[26] [27] The writing team, including Franken, received the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Writing for a Comedy-Variety or Music Special in 1976 for the first season.[28] Franken transitioned to on-air roles as a featured player from 1977 to 1980, appearing in sketches often marked by ironic consumer advocacy and political commentary, though his performing tenure was shorter than his writing contributions.[29] He departed after the 1979â€“1980 season but returned to the writing staff in 1985, continuing until 1995 while making sporadic cast appearances, particularly in 1985â€“1986 and 1988â€“1995.[26] During this period, Franken created and portrayed the recurring character Stuart Smalley, a simpering self-help guru whose "daily affirmations" segment debuted on the February 9, 1991, episode hosted by Kevin Bacon; the bit ran through 1995 and inspired the 1995 film Stuart Saves His Family.[30] Franken's request to anchor Weekend Update was denied in 1995, prompting his exit from the show.[31] He briefly returned for writing and a guest hosting stint in 2008 prior to his Senate campaign.[25] Over his two decades with SNL, Franken earned multiple Emmy nominations and wins for writing, including shared credits for variety programming.[32] His work emphasized sharp, observational satire, often targeting media and politics, though it drew mixed reviews for its intensity compared to more physical comedy styles of peers.[26]

Authorship and Political Satire Books

Franken's entry into political authorship began with Rush Limbaugh Is a Big Fat Idiot and Other Observations, published on January 1, 1996, by Delacorte Press. The book satirically targeted conservative radio host Rush Limbaugh, Republican politicians, and right-leaning media through a mix of personal anecdotes, fabricated scenarios, and direct critiques of policy positions, with Franken employing ad hominem attacks as an ironic nod to Limbaugh's rhetorical style. It achieved commercial success, spending 23 weeks on Publishers Weekly's bestseller list.[33] In 2003, Franken released Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them: A Fair and Balanced Look at the Right, published by Dutton, which expanded his satirical approach to dissecting perceived falsehoods propagated by conservative commentators, think tanks, and figures like Ann Coulter, Bill O'Reilly, and the Bush administration. The work incorporated empirical analysis, including data compiled by a team of 14 Harvard students reviewing political statements for accuracy, to challenge claims on topics such as media bias, welfare reform, and the Iraq War intelligence. It became a New York Times bestseller, though critics on the right dismissed it as partisan invective lacking objectivity.[34] Franken's 2005 book, The Truth (with Jokes), also from Dutton, focused on the 2004 presidential election, Republican campaign tactics, and allegations that the Bush administration misled the public into the Iraq War through manipulated intelligence. Blending humor with investigative elements, such as examinations of voter suppression and media complicity, the book argued that Democratic losses stemmed from failures to counter right-wing narratives effectively. It reached New York Times bestseller status and influenced progressive discourse on political messaging, though conservative outlets portrayed it as one-sided propaganda.[35] Later, Al Franken, Giant of the Senate (2017, Twelve Books) shifted toward memoir while retaining satirical elements, chronicling Franken's transition from entertainer to U.S. Senator, including his 2008 campaign recount and early legislative experiences. Published on May 30, 2017, it critiqued Senate dysfunction and partisan gridlock with self-deprecating humor but emphasized policy substance over pure polemic, earning praise for accessibility while drawing accusations of selective partisanship from opponents. The book, like its predecessors, topped New York Times bestseller lists, contributing to Franken's four such achievements in political writing.[36]

Radio Hosting and Other Media Ventures

In 2004, Franken launched The Al Franken Show as the flagship program of Air America Radio, a newly formed progressive talk radio network intended to provide an alternative to conservative-dominated airwaves. The show debuted on March 31, 2004, airing weekdays from 9 a.m. to noon Eastern Time and featuring Franken's blend of political satire, interviews with guests, and commentary on current events from a left-leaning perspective.[10] It quickly became Air America's highest-rated program, attracting an estimated audience of over 2 million weekly listeners by 2006 and establishing Franken as the top progressive radio host during its run.[37] The program often included segments like "The O'Franken Factor," a satirical take on conservative commentator Bill O'Reilly's style, and live calls from listeners, emphasizing critique of Republican policies and media figures. An edited version of the radio show was adapted for television, airing as The Al Franken Show on cable networks from 2004 to 2005, with occasional simulcasts on channels like C-SPAN in 2006.[38] [39] Despite Air America's financial challenges and internal turmoil, Franken's show sustained strong listenership until he announced his departure on January 3, 2007, citing his intent to explore a U.S. Senate candidacy in Minnesota; the final episode aired on January 12, 2007.[40] Prior to the radio venture, Franken co-hosted Indecision '96, a satirical election coverage series on Comedy Central that aired during the 1996 presidential campaign, partnering with then-conservative commentator Arianna Huffington to mock political conventions and candidates through ironic commentary and sketches. This program, which ran for several episodes across the Republican and Democratic conventions, highlighted Franken's ongoing shift toward political media satire following his Saturday Night Live tenure.[41]

Transition to Politics

Pre-Senate Activism and Advocacy

Prior to entering electoral politics, Franken engaged in political advocacy through authorship, radio broadcasting, and financial support for Democratic causes. His 2003 book Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them: A Fair and Balanced Look at the Right critiqued conservative figures and media outlets, achieving bestseller status and establishing him as a prominent liberal voice challenging perceived right-wing misinformation.[42] The work's empirical approach, including fact-checking claims by opponents like Ann Coulter and Fox News, reflected Franken's emphasis on data-driven rebuttals to ideological narratives.[43] In March 2004, Franken launched The Al Franken Show on Air America Radio, a network aimed at countering conservative dominance in talk radio with progressive commentary. Airing weekdays for three hours, the program featured interviews with Democratic leaders, critiques of the Iraq Warâ€”citing intelligence failures and rising casualtiesâ€”and advocacy for policies like expanded healthcare access, amassing a national audience of over 1 million listeners at its peak.[44] [45] Franken used satire to dismantle arguments from figures like Rush Limbaugh and the Bush administration, while promoting voter mobilization against Republican policies; the show ended in February 2007 upon his Senate candidacy announcement, having solidified his role in liberal media ecosystems despite Air America's financial struggles.[46] Franken actively campaigned for Democratic candidates, notably John Kerry's 2004 presidential bid. He spoke at rallies, such as one in Nashua, New Hampshire, where his comedic style energized crowds by highlighting Bush-era policy shortcomings, and addressed Maine delegates at the Democratic National Convention, framing the election as a rejection of incumbent failures.[42] [47] These appearances leveraged his entertainment background to boost turnout in swing states, with Franken arguing that Kerry's platform offered substantive alternatives on economic and foreign policy issues. By fall 2005, Franken formed the Midwest Values PAC, a leadership political action committee that raised over $1 million to support progressive initiatives and Democratic contenders. In the 2006 election cycle alone, the PAC distributed more than $260,000 to candidates in Minnesota and across the U.S., focusing on competitive races to counter Republican incumbents and advance priorities like labor rights and environmental protections.[48] [49] This fundraising effort marked his shift toward institutional political involvement, building alliances within the Democratic Party ahead of his own statewide run.[48]

2008 U.S. Senate Campaign and Election Dispute

Al Franken announced his candidacy for the U.S. Senate from Minnesota on February 14, 2007, seeking the Democratic-Farmer-Labor (DFL) Party nomination to challenge incumbent Republican Senator Norm Coleman.[48] Franken positioned himself as a progressive critic of the Iraq War and advocate for working families, drawing on his background in satire and radio hosting to highlight policy differences with Coleman.[50] He secured the DFL endorsement after a contentious primary process and won the nomination on June 10, 2008, defeating candidates including attorney Mike Ciresi and activist Jack Nelson-Pallmeyer.[51] The general election on November 4, 2008, featured Franken against Coleman and Independence Party candidate Dean Barkley. Initial unofficial results showed Coleman leading Franken by 215 votes out of approximately 2.9 million cast, with Barkley receiving about 8 percent.[52] Due to the margin falling within 0.5 percent, Minnesota law triggered an automatic recount, which began later in November 2008 and involved reviewing over 3,000 challenged ballots.[53] By December 19, 2008, the recount had Franken ahead by 250 votes, with final tallies after absorbing rejected absentee ballots showing Franken with 1,212,725 votes to Coleman's 1,212,500â€”a margin of 225 votes.[54][55] On January 5, 2009, the Minnesota State Canvassing Board certified Franken as the winner by 225 votes.[56] Coleman responded by filing an election contest in district court on January 6, alleging irregularities including mishandled absentee ballots and double-counted votes, seeking to include thousands of disputed ballots.[56] The trial, spanning February to April 2009, examined claims of voter fraud and procedural errors; the presiding judge rejected most of Coleman's challenges, ruling on April 13 that Franken prevailed by 312 votes.[57] Coleman appealed to the Minnesota Supreme Court, which unanimously affirmed the lower court's decision on June 30, 2009, validating Franken's victory.[57] Coleman conceded the race on July 1, 2009, ending the dispute after eight months and marking one of the longest Senate election contests in U.S. history.[58] Franken was sworn into office on July 7, 2009, providing Democrats a 60-seat filibuster-proof majority in the Senate at the time.[58] Critics, including some Republican observers, questioned the recount process and alleged potential voter fraud in absentee balloting, though courts found insufficient evidence to alter the certified results.[59]

U.S. Senate Tenure

2014 Re-election

Incumbent Democraticâ€“Farmerâ€“Labor (DFL) Senator Al Franken sought re-election to a second full term in the 2014 United States Senate election in Minnesota, facing Republican businessman Mike McFadden in the general election held on November 4, 2014.[60] Franken advanced unopposed from the DFL primary on August 12, 2014, while McFadden secured the Republican nomination by defeating three challengers with 75.1% of the primary vote. The race drew national attention as part of Democrats' efforts to defend their Senate majority amid midterm headwinds, though Minnesota's blue-leaning electorate favored Franken.[61] Franken's campaign emphasized his legislative record, including support for the Affordable Care Act and efforts to expand rural broadband access, while portraying McFadden as an out-of-touch outsider funded by wealthy donors.[60] McFadden, a private equity executive and political novice, criticized Franken as an ineffective partisan focused on Hollywood-style showmanship rather than job creation and fiscal responsibility, positioning himself as a business-oriented reformer.[61] Fundraising favored Franken significantly; by April 2014, he had raised $2.7 million in the first quarter alone, amassing $5.9 million cash on hand, compared to McFadden's early hauls of around $1.1 million in subsequent quarters.[62] [63] Franken secured a decisive victory with 1,053,205 votes (53.15%), defeating McFadden who received 850,227 votes (42.91%), a margin of 10.24 percentage pointsâ€”far wider than the protracted 2008 contest.[64] Minor candidates included Independence Party nominee Chris Diamantopoulos with 64,785 votes (3.27%) and Libertarian Kevin Terrell with 27,232 votes (1.37%).[64] The Associated Press called the race for Franken shortly after polls closed, reflecting strong turnout in Democratic strongholds and his incumbency advantages.[60] This outcome helped Democrats retain the seat but did little to stem Republican gains elsewhere in the cycle, which flipped the Senate to GOP control.[61]

Legislative Achievements and Key Votes

Franken sponsored 85 bills, 47 amendments, and nine resolutions during his Senate tenure, though few originated as standalone legislation that became law, consistent with the norm for individual senators whose priorities often advance through amendments to broader bills.[65] His efforts emphasized veterans' support, education access, and regulatory reforms targeting corporate practices. One early initiative, the Service Dogs for Veterans Act (S. 1396, introduced in 2009), established a Department of Veterans Affairs pilot program to provide service dogs for veterans with post-traumatic stress disorder and other disabilities, which was incorporated into the Veterans' Benefits Enhancement Act of 2010 (Public Law 111-275).[66] A notable success was the Franken Amendment to the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2010, adopted by the Senate on October 6, 2009, by a 68-29 vote, which prohibited the use of defense funds for contracts with companies restricting employees' ability to pursue sexual assault claims outside mandatory arbitration clauses; this targeted practices by firms like KBR amid Iraq War contractor controversies.[67][68] In financial regulation, Franken authored an amendment to the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, passed by the Senate in November 2010 (68-30 vote), creating an independent board to assign credit rating agencies to issuers, aiming to mitigate conflicts of interest exposed in the 2008 financial crisis.[69] He also secured bipartisan provisions in the Every Student Succeeds Act (reauthorizing No Child Left Behind), including expanded mental health services in schools, support for foster youth, and principal training programs, enacted in December 2015.[5] On education and health, Franken cosponsored and advocated for measures enhancing school-based mental health support, such as integrating counselors and services to address student trauma, which influenced funding allocations in appropriations bills.[4] In aviation policy, he sponsored the Airline Flight Crew Technical Corrections Act (S. 1422), which clarified Family and Medical Leave Act eligibility for flight crew members and became Public Law 111-119 on December 14, 2009.[70] Key votes aligned with Democratic priorities: Franken supported the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (December 2009, 60-39 cloture vote), enabling its passage despite procedural hurdles.[71] He opposed the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (December 20, 2017, 51-48 vote), citing concerns over deficit increases and benefits skewed toward high earners.[71] On nominations, he voted against confirming several Trump administration appointees, including those for regulatory roles, reflecting skepticism toward deregulation efforts.[72] Franken backed the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2013 (December 2013), which raised spending caps and averted sequestration cuts.[73]

Committee Assignments and Roles

Franken was assigned to three committees upon joining the Senate in July 2009 for the 111th Congress: the Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions; the Committee on the Judiciary; and the Special Committee on Aging.[74] These assignments aligned with his prior advocacy on healthcare, consumer protection, and issues affecting seniors in Minnesota.[9] In the 112th Congress (2011-2012), Franken joined the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs, appointed by Majority Leader Harry Reid to address tribal sovereignty and Native American issues relevant to Minnesota's reservations.[75] He maintained memberships on his initial committees through subsequent congresses, participating in hearings on topics such as pharmaceutical compounding oversight and mental health coverage expansions via the HELP Committee.[76][77] By the 114th Congress (2015-2016), Franken held leadership roles as ranking Democratic member of the Judiciary Subcommittee on Privacy, Technology, and the Law, focusing on data protection and surveillance reforms, and served on the HELP Subcommittee on Primary Health and Aging.[78] These positions enabled him to influence legislation on antitrust matters and elder care policy, though partisan divides limited bipartisan outputs.[79] In the 115th Congress, prior to his resignation in January 2018, his assignments remained consistent, with continued emphasis on judiciary oversight amid investigations into executive actions.[74]

Sexual Misconduct Allegations

Initial Accusation by Leeann Tweeden

On November 16, 2017, Leeann Tweeden, a Los Angeles-based radio host and former model who had participated in USO tours, publicly accused Al Franken of sexual misconduct stemming from a 2006 USO entertainment tour to U.S. troops in the Middle East.[82][83] Tweeden detailed that during a rehearsal for a comedy sketch aboard a military aircraft en route to Kuwait on December 20, 2006, Frankenâ€”then a comedian and not yet a senatorâ€”insisted on practicing a kiss scene despite her discomfort. She alleged he grabbed her shoulders, pressed his lips to hers, and "stuck his tongue in my mouth," continuing aggressively even as she turned her head away and pushed back, leaving her feeling violated and humiliated.[83][84] Later during the return flight from Qatar to the U.S., Tweeden claimed Franken took a photo of himself groping her breasts while she slept, which she discovered upon reviewing her camera and confronted him about, to which he reportedly responded dismissively.[82][85] Tweeden stated she remained silent for over a decade due to Franken's rising political prominence and her reluctance to appear weak or overly sensitive, but came forward amid the Harvey Weinstein scandal and broader #MeToo movement, emphasizing the power imbalance and her intent to highlight the incident's impact on her trust in male colleagues.[86][87]

Subsequent Accusations from Other Women

Following the accusation by Leeann Tweeden on November 16, 2017, at least seven other women publicly alleged instances of unwanted physical contact or attempted kissing by Al Franken, spanning from 2003 to 2017.[88][89] These claims, reported primarily through media outlets like HuffPost, CNN, and Politico, described a pattern of groping during photo opportunities or forcible kissing attempts in professional settings, though Franken denied recollection of most incidents and described some as misunderstandings.[7] At least three accusers remained anonymous, limiting independent verification.[88] On November 20, 2017, Lindsay Menz, a Minnesota radio news director, alleged that during a 2010 photo session at the Minnesota State Fair, Franken placed his hand on her clothed breast and pressed it toward her. Menz stated she laughed it off at the time to avoid awkwardness but later viewed it as non-consensual.[89][88] Franken responded via spokesperson that he did not remember the incident but took her account seriously.[90] HuffPost reported two anonymous allegations on November 23, 2017: one woman claimed Franken cupped her buttocks at a 2008 Minneapolis fundraiser after she praised his work; the other said that in September 2017 at the Minnesota State Fair, Franken attempted to kiss her without consent after she requested a photo, pulling away only after she resisted.[88][89] On November 30, 2017, Stephanie Kemplin, an Army veteran, alleged that during a 2003 USO tour in Iraq, Franken groped her breasts under her flak jacket while pretending to inspect her gear for a skit, leaving her feeling violated amid the tour's high-stress environment.[91][88] Kemplin emphasized the incident's lasting impact but noted it did not define her service. Franken's team expressed regret if she felt uncomfortable.[91] Rachel Smith, an employee at a Twin Cities TV station, alleged on November 30, 2017, that in 2007 during a rehearsal for Franken's radio show, he pulled her toward him and attempted to kiss her forcibly without consent, which she rebuffed.[89][88] An anonymous former congressional aide separately claimed Franken tried to kiss her after consuming alcohol at an event, though specifics on timing were not detailed publicly.[88] On December 6, 2017, another woman, identified only through Politico reporting, alleged that after a 2006 taping of Franken's radio show in Minneapolis, he attempted to kiss her forcibly in a car, which she resisted; Franken denied the claim, stating he had no memory of it.[92][88] These reports contributed to mounting pressure on Franken amid the broader #MeToo movement, though subsequent investigations, such as a 2019 New Yorker analysis, highlighted inconsistencies in some accounts and questioned the proportionality of the Senate Ethics Committee's rushed process.[7]

Franken's Responses and Apologies

On November 16, 2017, following Leeann Tweeden's public accusation of unwanted kissing during a 2006 USO tour rehearsal and the release of a photograph depicting Franken with his hands positioned over her breasts while she appeared asleep, Franken issued a statement apologizing for the incident.[93][94] He stated, "I certainly don't remember the rehearsal for the skit in the same way, but I send my sincerest apologies to Leeann," adding that the photograph was "completely inappropriate" and that "there's no excuse."[95] Franken expressed willingness to meet Tweeden in person to apologize and called for a Senate Ethics Committee investigation into the matter, emphasizing that such allegations warranted review regardless of party affiliation.[96] As additional women came forward with allegations of groping or unwanted advances between November 20 and December 2017â€”including claims from a Democratic congressional aide, a former intern, and othersâ€”Franken responded on November 23, 2017, with a statement acknowledging broader patterns in his behavior.[97] He said, "I've met tens of thousands of people and taken thousands of photographs, often in crowded and chaotic situations. I'm a warm person; I hug people. I've tried to make the case that any photographs that would make anyone uncomfortable or bring any ill will are unacceptable, and I'm prepared to take all the consequences."[98] Franken apologized specifically to those who felt he had made them uncomfortable, stating, "I take thousands of photos, I hug people, but I've made some women feel badly and that is not OK."[97] In his December 7, 2017, Senate floor speech announcing his resignation, Franken reiterated regret over the allegations but framed his departure as politically motivated amid the broader #MeToo movement, without issuing a blanket apology for all claims.[99] He noted the irony of resigning while President Trump, who had faced similar accusations, remained in office, and affirmed he would continue advocating for his beliefs outside the Senate.[100] Critics, including some media outlets, described the speech as lacking contrition and revealing bitterness toward Democratic colleagues who urged his exit.[101] Franken complied with an ongoing Ethics Committee probe before resigning but did not contest the emerging consensus among Senate Democrats that he should step down.[102]

Resignation and Immediate Aftermath

On December 7, 2017, U.S. Senator Al Franken announced his intention to resign from the Senate amid allegations of sexual misconduct from eight women, who accused him of groping or attempting forcible kisses during his time as a comedian and senator.[6] In a Senate floor speech, Franken stated that while he believed an ethics investigation could clear him of some claims, the mounting pressure from Democratic colleagues made it untenable to remain, adding that his resignation would occur "in the coming weeks" to allow time for a successor appointment.[103] He criticized Republican senators for what he described as hypocrisy in addressing similar allegations against their own members, such as those against Roy Moore and Donald Trump.[103] The decision followed intense pressure from Senate Democrats, with over 30 colleagues, including Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and Senator Kirsten Gillibrand, publicly calling for his resignation in the prior days to maintain party credibility during the #MeToo movement.[104] Franken had initially urged the Senate Select Committee on Ethics to conduct a full investigation, which had opened a preliminary inquiry on November 30, 2017, but he concluded that pursuing it would distract from Democratic priorities like opposing the Republican tax bill.[105][106] The ethics probe effectively ended without a hearing or subpoenaed evidence due to his departure.[7] Franken's resignation took effect on January 2, 2018, after which Minnesota Governor Mark Dayton appointed Lieutenant Governor Tina Smith to fill the vacancy until a 2018 special election.[8] Immediate reactions within the Democratic Party emphasized unity and forward momentum, with Schumer praising Franken's service while affirming the need for accountability, though some aides expressed private frustration over the rushed process without due process.[107] Republicans, including Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell, welcomed the move but highlighted perceived double standards in Democratic handling of allegations compared to their reticence on figures like Al Franken before the accusations surfaced.[108]

Ongoing Debates and Defenses

In the years following his 2017 resignation, Al Franken expressed profound regret over his decision to step down without a formal Senate investigation, stating in a 2019 interview that he "absolutely" wished he had allowed the Ethics Committee to review the allegations.[7] He argued that the accusations, primarily involving awkward physical interactions during comedic performances and non-criminal unwanted advances, did not warrant forfeiture of his seat, especially given his legislative record.[109] Franken maintained that the infamous 2006 USO tour photograph with Leeann Tweeden, depicting him leaning toward her chest, was intended as a satirical gag rather than an assault, and that subsequent claims from other women lacked evidence of severe misconduct.[104] Several Democratic senators who initially demanded his resignation later voiced second thoughts, with seven admitting by 2019 that the process was mishandled amid the heightened scrutiny of the MeToo movement.[110] Senate Majority Whip Dick Durbin, for instance, indicated regret over the haste, suggesting an internal probe could have clarified the extent of the allegations.[111] Defenders, including progressive commentators, contended that Franken's ouster represented a premature sacrifice of an effective legislatorâ€”known for bipartisan work on issues like infrastructure and veterans' affairsâ€”for partisan optics, particularly when contrasted with retained figures facing graver accusations.[7] Critics of the resignation, such as New York Times columnist Michelle Goldberg, reflected in 2022 that calling for Franken's exit without due process was erroneous, highlighting how the rush eroded trust in institutional fairness.[112] Ongoing debates center on whether the allegations constituted a pattern of harassment justifying expulsion or isolated, context-specific incidents amplified by media frenzy, with Franken himself positing in interviews that a measured inquiry would have vindicated his service.[113] Some analyses frame the episode as emblematic of MeToo's early overcorrections, where political pressure supplanted evidentiary standards, though opponents maintain the resignation upheld accountability for corroborated improprieties.[114]

Post-Senate Activities

Podcast Launch and Content Focus

Franken launched The Al Franken Podcast on May 14, 2019, shortly after his resignation from the U.S. Senate in January 2018.[115] The podcast, produced independently and distributed on platforms such as Apple Podcasts and Spotify, features Franken as host conducting interviews and monologues typically lasting 30 to 60 minutes per episode.[116] By early 2025, it had published over 350 episodes, maintaining a weekly release schedule.[117] The content centers on political analysis, with a emphasis on U.S. domestic and foreign policy, electoral strategies, and critiques of Republican figures and policies. Episodes frequently address Donald Trump's influence on the Republican Party, including alliances with evangelical and fundamentalist Christian groups, as well as Democratic Party challenges in messaging to younger voters and countering conservative narratives.[118] [119] Guests include progressive activists like David Hogg, discussing recruitment of young Democrats; political scientists such as Norm Ornstein, analyzing congressional dysfunction; and commentators like John Fugelsang on historical and ideological topics, such as interpretations of religious influences in politics.[120] [121] Franken incorporates humor drawn from his comedy background, blending satirical commentary with substantive discussions on issues like judicial nominations, healthcare policy, and election integrity. For instance, episodes have explored Senate procedural maneuvers, such as Harry Reid's 2013 "nuclear option" and Mitch McConnell's blockade of Merrick Garland's Supreme Court nomination, framing them as pivotal to partisan gridlock.[122] The podcast avoids non-political topics, maintaining a focus on advancing liberal policy critiques and electoral tactics, as evidenced by recurring themes of unifying Democratic efforts against perceived authoritarian tendencies in the GOP.[123]

Public Speaking and Commentary

Following his resignation from the U.S. Senate in December 2017, Al Franken resumed public speaking in 2019, initially through limited events focused on his professional experiences rather than the allegations leading to his departure. In June 2019, he delivered his first post-resignation speech to a Jewish community group in New York City, discussing his career in comedy and politics without addressing the misconduct claims.[124] That August, Franken engaged United Talent Agency to secure paid speaking opportunities, pitching himself for engagements on topics including his Senate tenure and Saturday Night Live work.[125] Later in 2019, Franken hosted a public event in Portland, Oregon, on October 15, sharing anecdotes from his time as a senator and comedian to mixed reception amid ongoing debates over his resignation.[126] He also spoke at Politicon in Nashville, Tennessee, on October 12, reflecting on his childhood, college years, and early career influences.[127] By the early 2020s, Franken expanded into university lectures and keynote circuits, often blending humor with political analysis. In February 2022, he launched "The God Spoke Tour," a live comedy show incorporating commentary on current events such as Democratic strategies and media dynamics.[128] Speakers bureaus like AAE and All American Speakers continue to book him for addresses on leadership, satire, and public policy, with fees reportedly in the range of $50,000â€“$100,000 per event as of 2025.[129][130] Recent engagements include the President's Distinguished Lecture Series at Kean University on October 30, 2024, where he discussed bipartisan challenges and comedy's role in discourse, and the Drew Forum at Drew University on February 6, 2025, offering insights on electoral politics and institutional biases.[131][132] In these forums, Franken has critiqued Trump-era policies, lamented the Senate's procedural gridlock under both parties, and argued that his 2017 resignationâ€”prompted by unverified additional accusationsâ€”hastened Democratic losses by depriving the party of a swing-state vote on key legislation like the Affordable Care Act's defense.[109] He has attributed pressure for his exit to partisan media amplification and internal Democratic conformity, contrasting it with leniency toward figures like President Biden's family associates.[7]

Political Action Committee and Endorsements

Following his resignation from the U.S. Senate in January 2018, Al Franken maintained involvement in Democratic politics through the Midwest Values PAC, a leadership political action committee he established on October 26, 2005.[133] The PAC focuses on supporting Democratic candidates for state legislatures, Congress, governorships, and other offices who advocate for progressive priorities including economic and social justice, education funding, clean energy initiatives, healthcare access, and accountability in government.[134] Franken, who contributes opinion pieces and updates via the PAC's website, has emphasized values rooted in Midwestern pragmatism, such as the principle that "we all do better when we all do better," a phrase borrowed from the late Senator Paul Wellstone.[135] The committee has remained active post-resignation, raising $1,198,680 during the 2021-2022 election cycle and continuing fundraising into subsequent cycles, while disbursing funds to aligned candidates and organizations.[136] Overall, it has donated more than $3.2 million to political efforts, primarily targeting competitive races in Minnesota and other battleground states to bolster Democratic majorities in legislatures and Congress.[134] Despite efforts to spend down reservesâ€”exceeding $100,000 in expenditures between July and December 2019â€”the PAC has sustained operations, including contributions during the 2024 cycle reported to the Federal Election Commission as of September 2025.[137][138] Franken's personal endorsements, often amplified through the PAC's network, have targeted key Democratic primaries and general elections. On April 10, 2025, he endorsed Minnesota Lieutenant Governor Peggy Flanagan in her bid for the U.S. Senate seat in the 2026 election, praising her record on education, healthcare, and working families during the primary stage.[139] This support aligns with the PAC's emphasis on electing candidates who prioritize opportunity and equality, though specific PAC-backed recipients in recent cycles are detailed in Federal Election Commission filings rather than publicly listed endorsements.[133]

Reflections on Resignation and Future Prospects

Franken has publicly stated that he regrets resigning from the Senate on December 7, 2017, without permitting a full investigation by the Senate Ethics Committee. In a July 2019 New Yorker profile, he affirmed, "Oh, yeah. Absolutely," when directly asked about his regret, emphasizing that an inquiry would have allowed him to defend against the accusations while continuing his work.[7] He has described the rapid push for his departure amid the #MeToo movement as a "rush to judgment," contrasting it with cases involving figures like Joe Biden or Andrew Cuomo, where allegations did not lead to immediate resignation.[104][109] Several Democratic senators, including Kirsten Gillibrand and others who initially called for his resignation, later expressed misgivings about the process, with seven current and former members telling The New Yorker that the case was mishandled and lacked due process.[110] Franken has defended specific incidents, such as the 2006 USO tour photograph with Leeann Tweeden, as part of a rehearsed comedic bit rather than non-consensual groping, while acknowledging poor judgment in other reported interactions like unwanted kisses or touches.[113] He has argued that his ouster weakened Democratic leverage in key votes, such as those on tax reform and judicial confirmations, contributing to tangible legislative losses.[7] As of March 2025, Franken has foreclosed on prospects for a political comeback, stating in an interview, "Iâ€™m ruling it out" and "I don't think I'll be running again," even as speculation arose around Minnesota's open Senate seat following Amy Klobuchar's potential departure.[140] Earlier comments in 2021 and 2022 had left the door ajar, with him describing a return as "tempting," but he has since committed to supporting Democratic candidates through his podcast, public commentary, and a political action committee rather than seeking office himself.[141][142] This shift aligns with his focus on external advocacy, though he continues to critique partisan dynamics and express frustration over his Senate exit's long-term impact on progressive priorities.[109]

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Al Franken married Franni Bryson on October 2, 1975, after meeting her while both attended Harvard University.[3] [143] The couple relocated from Manhattan to Minneapolis in 2005, after their children had become independent.[144] Bryson, who has worked in nonprofit organizations focused on issues such as abortion rights and consumer protection, remained supportive of Franken amid his 2017 sexual misconduct allegations, accompanying him to events and publicly defending their long-term partnership.[145] Franken and Bryson have two children: a son, Joseph (Joe) Franken, and a daughter, Thomasin Franken.[24] Thomasin married Brody Greenwald, and the couple welcomed their first child, Joseph Bryson Greenwald, on May 30, 2013, marking Franken and Bryson's entry into grandparenthood.[146] By 2019, the family had expanded to include four grandchildren.[147]

Health Issues and Personal Challenges

Franken encountered a significant personal challenge in the mid-1990s following the critical and commercial failure of his film Stuart Saves His Family (1995), which led to a period of depression and professional doubt.[148][149] Following his resignation from the U.S. Senate in December 2017 amid allegations of sexual misconduct, Franken described entering a clinical depression that persisted for months, complicating his return to public life.[150] He has not reported any major physical health conditions, stating in a 2022 interview that his health remained "pretty good."[22] Franken's family faced additional challenges, including his wife Franni's struggles with postpartum depression and alcohol dependency after the birth of their first child in the early 1980s; she credited Franken with steadfast support during her recovery.[151][152] These experiences informed his advocacy for mental health and addiction treatment access during his Senate tenure.[153]

Reception and Legacy

Achievements in Comedy and Politics

Franken's early career on Saturday Night Live (SNL) spanned two periods, from 1975 to 1980 and 1985 to 1995, during which he served as a writer, performer, and producer, contributing to hundreds of sketches that satirized politics, media, and American culture.[31] He created enduring characters like the self-affirmation guru Stuart Smalley, whose catchphrase "I'm good enough, I'm smart enough, and doggone it, people like me" became a cultural staple in sketches parodying self-help trends.[155] For his SNL work, Franken received five Emmy Awards, including for outstanding writing in variety programs in 1989, 1993, and 2003, and additional nominations across 15 instances.[32] Beyond television, Franken hosted The Al Franken Show on Air America Radio from 2004 to 2007, where he blended political commentary with humor, drawing a progressive audience critical of conservative media figures.[2] He also co-created and starred in the NBC sitcom LateLine (1998â€“1999), a workplace comedy set in a fictional news magazine show.[31] As an author, Franken wrote four books that reached #1 on the New York Times bestseller list, including Rush Limbaugh Is a Big Fat Idiot (and Other Observations) (1996) and Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them (2003), which critiqued right-wing rhetoric through data-driven satire.[2] In the U.S. Senate, representing Minnesota from 2009 to 2018, Franken focused on legislation aiding veterans, infrastructure, and consumer protections, sponsoring 85 bills and 47 amendments over his tenure.[65] A notable success was the Franken Amendment, passed unanimously in the Senate's 2009 defense authorization bill, which barred contractors from prohibiting employees' free speech about corporate fraud, directly targeting practices by firms like Kellogg Brown & Root in Iraq.[68] He secured bipartisan inclusions in the Every Student Succeeds Act (2015), the reauthorization of No Child Left Behind, providing targeted support for foster youth, school mental health services, and principal training programs.[5] Franken advocated for expanded access to health services, co-sponsoring measures like the Access to Birth Control Act (2018) and contributing to Affordable Care Act amendments enhancing community health centers.[156] His efforts extended to veterans' issues, including bills improving disability claims processing, and broadband expansion in rural areas via infrastructure investments.[19] Reelected in 2014 amid a national Democratic downturn, Franken's Senate record emphasized procedural wins through amendments rather than standalone bill passages, reflecting the challenges of minority-party influence.[5]

Criticisms from Conservative Perspectives

Conservatives have frequently criticized Al Franken's comedic and literary output for engaging in personal attacks and partisan smears against right-wing figures rather than substantive debate. In his 1996 book Rush Limbaugh Is a Big Fat Idiot and Other Observations, Franken directly insulted conservative radio host Rush Limbaugh, prompting backlash from conservatives who viewed the title and content as emblematic of liberal ad hominem tactics devoid of intellectual rigor.[157] Similarly, his 2003 book Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them targeted conservative media personalities like Bill O'Reilly and Ann Coulter, accusing them of dishonesty; National Review contributor Byron York described Franken's approach as an "ugly schtick" that prioritized mockery over fair analysis, while Fox News Channel sued Franken for using the phrase "fair and balanced," highlighting perceived hypocrisy in his critique of conservative outlets.[157][7] During the 2017 sexual misconduct allegations, conservative commentators emphasized Franken's hypocrisy, noting his prior demands for accountability from figures like Fox News executives Roger Ailes and Bill O'Reilly amid their scandals. Fox News host Sean Hannity labeled Franken a "massive hypocrite" on November 16, 2017, citing Franken's history of crude comments and the infamous 2006 USO tour photo with Leeann Tweeden, where he appeared to grope her while she slept, as evidence of selective moral outrage after Franken had publicly lectured Republicans on women's issues.[158] President Donald Trump echoed this on Twitter the same day, accusing Franken of hypocrisy for "lecturing anyone who would listen about values, integrity and respect for women" just weeks earlier, while defending his own record despite multiple accusations.[159] Conservatives argued this revealed a double standard in Democratic responses to misconduct, contrasting Franken's swift resignation with leniency toward allies like Bill Clinton.[160] In the Senate, Franken drew conservative ire for his confrontational style, including sharp interrogations of nominees and policies aligned with Republican priorities, which clashed with norms of collegiality. His 2017 memoir Al Franken, Giant of the Senate detailed such tactics, including staging a filibuster-like speech against a GOP budget resolution, which conservatives saw as performative obstructionism rather than constructive opposition; outlets like Fox News portrayed this as part of a broader pattern of liberal elitism from a former comedian ill-suited for legislative seriousness.[161] Additionally, Franken's advocacy for policies like expanded government intervention and criticism of tax cuts for high earners fueled accusations of promoting class warfare, as articulated in critiques from libertarian-leaning sources decrying his rhetoric as fostering entitlement over personal responsibility.[162]

Evaluations of Senate Impact

Al Franken's tenure in the U.S. Senate from July 7, 2009, to January 3, 2018, yielded a legislative record characterized by modest enactment of primary-sponsored bills but notable success through amendments incorporated into broader legislation. He sponsored over 500 bills and resolutions, yet only a handful, such as the Airline Flight Crew Technical Corrections Act (S. 1422), became public law as P.L. 111-119.[70] His approach emphasized attaching provisions to larger packages, with 63 Senate amendments agreed to during his service.[163] For instance, in October 2009, an amendment he championed passed to withhold defense contracts from firms restricting employee access to courts for sexual assault claims.[68] Evaluations from supporters highlight Franken's effectiveness in committee work, particularly on the Judiciary Committee, where his questioning of executive nominees drew praise for exposing inconsistencies and advancing Democratic priorities.[7] Early in his term, he secured passage of seven measures through the Senate, including amendments addressing veterans' service dogs and financial reforms.[164] Proponents, including bipartisan observers, credited him with a record of cross-aisle collaboration, such as incorporating cost-containment amendments into health care legislation.[165][166] In 2010, his amendment establishing a board to oversee credit rating agency conflicts passed the Senate 68-30.[69] Critics, particularly from conservative perspectives, assessed Franken's impact as limited and ideologically driven, pointing to his 0% score on the Heritage Action scorecard for the 115th Congress, indicating consistent opposition to conservative fiscal and regulatory reforms like the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.[72] While his amendments influenced defense and financial bills, standalone legislative achievements were sparse, aligning with broader patterns where junior senators rarely drive major enactments independently.[65] GovTrack data ranked him #56 among senators for bills advanced out of committee in 2016, suggesting middling productivity in shepherding legislation forward.[167] Conservative analyses often framed his oversight role as partisan theater rather than substantive policy influence, emphasizing rhetorical flourishes over causal legislative outcomes.[168]

Broader Controversies and Viewpoints

Al Franken's resignation from the U.S. Senate on December 7, 2017, following allegations of sexual misconduct by eight women, including an unwanted kiss and simulated groping during a 2006 USO tour documented in a photograph, generated significant partisan and ideological contention.[7][104] Conservative commentators highlighted perceived Democratic hypocrisy, noting Franken's ouster contrasted with the party's tolerance for figures like Bill Clinton, while some on the right opportunistically defended him to underscore inconsistencies in #MeToo enforcement against liberals versus conservatives like Donald Trump or Roy Moore.[169] From liberal perspectives, the rapid push for resignationâ€”driven by Democratic senators such as Kirsten Gillibrand without awaiting a full Senate Ethics Committee investigationâ€”later drew regret, with Franken himself stating in 2019 that he "absolutely" wished he had not stepped down so hastily, arguing the allegations warranted scrutiny rather than immediate capitulation.[170][109] Some feminists and progressives contended the accusations, primarily involving non-criminal unwanted advances in professional settings, did not equate to the predatory patterns seen in other high-profile cases, advocating due process to avoid chilling political discourse or punishing satire-adjacent behavior from his comedy background.[171][172] Conservative critiques extended beyond the scandal to Franken's career-long partisan satire, exemplified by his 2003 book Lies and the Lying Liars Who Tell Them, which prompted a failed lawsuit from Fox News for alleged defamation, portraying him as a relentless attacker of right-wing media and figures.[7] In the Senate, Republicans accused him of demagoguery, such as sponsoring amendments tying GOP opposition to child rape legislation, which fueled perceptions of him as a divisive operative rather than a substantive legislator.[173] These viewpoints framed his tenure as emblematic of Democratic tactics prioritizing narrative over governance, though empirical assessments of his legislative record, including co-sponsoring over 1,000 bills on issues like veterans' affairs and infrastructure, elicited mixed evaluations of effectiveness.[174] The episode underscored tensions in applying uniform standards amid cultural shifts, with post-resignation analyses revealing no further allegations emerging and Franken's expressed remorse for specific incidents, yet persistent divisions over whether his exit strengthened or undermined institutional accountability.[112][175]
