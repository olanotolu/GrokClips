Sara Nović

Sara Nović (born 1987) is an American author, translator, creative writing instructor, and deaf rights advocate whose works explore themes of war, immigration, displacement, and Deaf culture.[1][2] Her debut novel, Girl at War (2015), depicts the impact of the Yugoslav Wars on a young girl of Croatian heritage, drawing from Nović's own experiences dividing time between the United States and Croatia; it received the American Library Association's Alex Award and was a finalist for the Los Angeles Times Book Prize.[2][3] Nović's second novel, True Biz (2022), set in a school for the deaf, became a New York Times bestseller and Reese's Book Club selection, also earning an Alex Award for its portrayal of Deaf community dynamics and American Sign Language (ASL) stigma.[2][4] She followed with the nonfiction anthology America Is Immigrants (2019), compiling immigrant biographies to highlight contributions to American society.[5] Having experienced progressive hearing loss starting in middle school, Nović communicates primarily in ASL and has advocated against hearing-centric biases in education and media, including through essays and her role as an instructor in Deaf studies at institutions like Emerson College.[6][7] Nović holds an MFA in fiction and literary translation from Columbia University and resides in Philadelphia.[2]

Early Life and Background

Childhood and Onset of Deafness

Sara Nović was born in 1987 in the United States to Croatian immigrant parents.[1] She grew up hearing in her early years, with no initial indications of hearing impairment.[8] Her family background included exposure to Croatian language and culture at home, though specific details of her preschool experiences emphasize typical auditory development prior to adolescence.[9] Nović began experiencing progressive hearing loss during middle school, around age 11 to 14.[8] This onset was marked by failing a standard school hearing test, which highlighted the emerging impairment amid the social challenges of adolescence.[10] At the time, she had not encountered other deaf individuals, leading her to initially conceal the loss and perceive herself through a hearing lens rather than connecting with deaf community resources.[6] The condition was acquired rather than congenital, with no reported genetic or perinatal factors specified in her accounts.[8] This isolation delayed her immersion in deaf culture and sign language, shaping her later advocacy for early deaf education.[6]

Family Influences and Upbringing

Sara Nović was raised by Croatian immigrant parents in the United States, where she grew up dividing her time between American and Croatian environments, fostering a bicultural identity that profoundly shaped her worldview and literary themes.[11] This dual exposure included frequent visits to extended family in Croatia, which immersed her in the language, customs, and historical narratives of her heritage, including the Yugoslav Wars' aftermath.[12] Her parents' emphasis on maintaining Croatian cultural ties influenced Nović's bilingual proficiency and her later focus on themes of displacement and belonging in works like Girl at War.[13] Nović's family home prioritized music over literature, with her father amassing a vast collection of albums, cassettes, and CDs that far exceeded the household's books. This environment redirected her early literacy efforts toward reading liner notes, lyrics sheets, and album artwork, cultivating an appreciation for concise, evocative language and storytelling through non-traditional texts.[14] Despite the limited formal reading materials, her parents supported creative expression, indirectly nurturing her development as a writer by encouraging engagement with narrative forms embedded in music and family oral histories from Croatia.[9] The family's hearing status contrasted with Nović's deafness, yet they adapted by learning basic sign language and prioritizing communication, which reinforced resilience and advocacy in her upbringing. This dynamic, combined with Croatian familial storytelling traditions, provided a foundation for her exploration of identity, marginalization, and empathy in her nonfiction and fiction.[15]

Education

Undergraduate Education

Nović attended Emerson College in Boston for her undergraduate education, graduating in 2009.[6] [16] She was the first member of her family to pursue higher education.[16] During this period, Nović began writing material that contributed to her later works, including early drafts composed while traveling on Amtrak trains between Boston and her family home.[17] Her experience at Emerson represented a formative phase, which she later described as a "stretch of golden time" that reinforced her positive perceptions of academic environments.[18]

Graduate Studies and Early Influences

Nović completed a Master of Fine Arts (MFA) in fiction and literary translation at Columbia University School of the Arts in 2014. The program's emphasis on narrative craft and cross-linguistic adaptation allowed her to refine techniques for integrating personal heritage with historical events, particularly those tied to the Yugoslav Wars.[14] Her studies in literary translation profoundly shaped her approach to prose, exposing her to Croatian texts and prompting deeper engagement with themes of displacement and identity that permeated her early work.[9] This training influenced the multilingual layers in her writing, where she translated not only language but also cultural nuances, enhancing the authenticity of depictions of war-torn settings. During this period, Nović workshopped initial iterations of her debut novel Girl at War, transforming a short story into a novel-length examination of trauma and resilience.[19] The graduate environment at Columbia also intersected with her deaf experience, encouraging innovative representations of communication barriers through visual prose styles reminiscent of American Sign Language's spatial dynamics.[8] These early academic influences prioritized empirical observation of human conflict over abstracted ideologies, grounding her fiction in firsthand familial accounts of the Balkans rather than secondary media narratives.[9]

Literary Career

Debut Novel: Girl at War

Girl at War is Sara Nović's debut novel, published in hardcover by Random House on May 12, 2015.[3] The book centers on Ana Jurić, a ten-year-old girl living in Zagreb, Croatia, whose ordinary childhood is upended by the outbreak of the Croatian War of Independence in 1991 amid the dissolution of Yugoslavia.[20][21] As conflict escalates, Ana's family faces displacement, separation, and survival challenges, with her infant sister being evacuated to safety in the United States via humanitarian efforts.[22] The narrative alternates between wartime events in Croatia and Ana's later experiences as a young adult in New York in 2001, where she grapples with unresolved trauma and returns to her homeland to confront lingering personal and national wounds.[20][22] The novel draws on historical events of the Yugoslav Wars, particularly the siege and ethnic tensions in Croatia, to explore the psychological toll of violence on civilians, emphasizing a child's perspective on chaos, betrayal, and resilience.[21] Key themes include the enduring legacy of loss and grief, the fragmentation of identity in exile, the unreliability of memory under duress, and the tension between assimilation in a new country and reconnection with one's origins.[23][24] Nović, who visited Croatia as a teenager and researched the period extensively, incorporates factual elements like the role of international aid organizations and the specifics of urban warfare in Zagreb without claiming direct autobiography, focusing instead on universal human responses to atrocity.[13] Critically, Girl at War received acclaim for its vivid depiction of war's intimate horrors and its nonlinear structure that mirrors fragmented recollection, with reviewers noting its ability to humanize geopolitical upheaval.[25] The New York Times described it as an "old-fashioned page-turner" that operates on both intimate and epic scales, earning an Editor's Choice designation.[26] Other outlets praised its emotional maturity and avoidance of sentimentality, though some critiqued the adult sections as less compelling than the childhood wartime sequences.[27] The novel won the American Library Association's Alex Award in 2016 for adult books appealing to young adults, was a finalist for the Los Angeles Times Book Prize and Goodreads Choice Awards, and appeared on longlists for the Center for Fiction's First Novel Prize, International Dublin Literary Award, and Women's Prize for Fiction.[20] It has been translated into 13 languages, reflecting its international resonance in addressing overlooked aspects of the 1990s Balkan conflicts.[20]

True Biz and Focus on Deaf Themes

True Biz, Nović's second novel, was published on April 5, 2022, by Random House.[28] Set at the fictional River Valley School for the Deaf, a boarding school for deaf high school students, the narrative follows principal characters including Charlie, a late-deafened teenager transferring from mainstream education with limited prior exposure to Deaf culture; Austin, a student navigating family expectations; and February, a hearing teacher learning sign language.[29] [30] The story examines interpersonal relationships, school politics, and external crises, emphasizing the role of American Sign Language (ASL) in fostering community and identity.[31] The novel centers Deaf themes by immersing readers in the lived experiences of Deaf individuals, portraying ASL not merely as a communication tool but as a cultural cornerstone that shapes worldview and belonging.[29] Nović, drawing from her own Deaf background, depicts the contrasts between oralist approaches—relying on lip-reading and spoken language—and signing environments, highlighting how the former can isolate deaf students while the latter enables full participation and emotional expression.[30] Through Charlie's arc, who arrives with minimal ASL proficiency and family resistance to Deaf norms, the book illustrates the challenges of identity formation for those disconnected from Deaf spaces, including stigma against signing and pressure toward medical interventions like cochlear implants.[31] [15] Nović uses the boarding school setting to explore broader debates in Deaf education and advocacy, such as the historical suppression of ASL in favor of oralism, which she links to ongoing marginalization by hearing institutions.[15] The narrative critiques power imbalances, including hearing administrators' decisions affecting Deaf students' language access, while celebrating Deaf-led resistance and the richness of Deaf social networks.[29] In interviews, Nović has emphasized the novel's intent to counter underrepresentation by centering a predominantly Deaf cast and authentic signing dynamics, avoiding tokenized portrayals common in hearing-authored works.[30] This focus underscores language as a determinant of inclusion, with ASL depicted as empowering identity amid societal exclusion.[31]

Essays, Translations, and Other Works

Nović authored America Is Immigrants (2019), a non-fiction collection featuring short biographical profiles of immigrants from every country worldwide, accompanied by illustrations from Alison Kolesar, highlighting diverse immigrant experiences in the United States.[32][33] Her translation work includes rendering poems by Bosnian writer Izet Sarajlić into English, particularly selections from his 1993 collection Sarajevska Ratna Zbirka (Sarajevo War Journal), which capture wartime reflections from the Siege of Sarajevo; these appeared in Circumference Magazine and earned her the 2014 Willis Barnstone Translation Prize from the American Literary Translators Association.[34][35][36] Nović has published essays on topics ranging from deafness and identity to sports fandom. Notable pieces include "Signed Away" in The Baffler (November 2024), addressing deaf community issues; "Martha’s Vineyard Was Once a Haven for the Deaf" in Afar (August 2024), exploring historical deaf enclaves; and "How to Lose Your Hearing" in Oprah Daily (January 2023), a personal account of acquiring deafness.[37] Earlier essays cover baseball, such as "On Unconditional Love, or Why I Am a Mets Fan" in The Believer (July 2019) and contributions to Baseball Prospectus.[37][38] She contributed to anthologies including Empty the Pews: Stories of Leaving the Church (Epiphany Publishing, 2019) and Others: Writers on the Power of Words (Unbound Press, 2019).[37] As founding editor of the Redeafined blog, Nović has advanced deaf rights through opinion pieces and advocacy content since its inception.

Academic and Professional Roles

Teaching Positions

Nović serves as an assistant professor of creative writing in the English department at Stockton University, where she teaches courses in creative writing and Deaf studies, including an introductory Deaf studies class she developed for the university's Disability Studies minor.[39][8] She is also listed as adjunct faculty in the Languages and Culture Studies program at Stockton.[40] Prior to her role at Stockton, Nović held a teaching fellowship at Columbia University during her MFA studies, where she instructed in writing and composition.[41][35] In spring 2015, she served as the Kim Frank Visiting Writer at Wesleyan University.[42] Additionally, as an Emerson College alumna, Nović teaches workshops in the Popular Fiction MFA program there, serving as a thesis advisor, and offers introductory courses on Deaf studies.[6] Her teaching emphasizes Deaf culture, identity, and literary representation, drawing from her experiences as a deaf writer and advocate.[2]

Editorial and Advocacy-Related Work

Nović has served as fiction editor for Blunderbuss Magazine, a literary publication, where she has helped curate submissions and ensure equitable treatment of writers from Deaf and disabled backgrounds by applying her perspective to editorial processes.[43][14] In this capacity, she has contributed to amplifying underrepresented voices within general literary fiction, distinct from specialized advocacy platforms.[44] As founding editor of Redeafined, an online blog launched circa 2015, Nović has edited and published content focused on Deaf rights, culture, and lived experiences, with the explicit aim of countering marginalization through targeted discourse.[45][14] The platform addresses systemic issues such as audism—the discrimination against sign language users—historical oppression including institutional violence against Deaf individuals, and barriers to accessibility in education, healthcare, justice systems, and media representation.[46][47] Key topics include critiques of oralist policies historically promoted by figures like Alexander Graham Bell, which prioritized spoken language over American Sign Language (ASL) and contributed to cultural erasure, as well as contemporary calls for Deaf inclusion in theater, film, and literature.[45] Nović has personally authored posts, such as reflections on Deaf identity and sign language interpretation at events, to foster productive dialogue on these matters.[48][47] These editorial efforts intersect with Nović's broader advocacy by prioritizing empirical accounts of Deaf community challenges over idealized narratives, emphasizing causal factors like policy-driven suppression of ASL in favor of cochlear implants or speech therapy, which data from Deaf-led studies indicate can undermine linguistic acquisition if not paired with sign language exposure.[15] Through Redeafined, she has platformed Deaf contributors to challenge hearing-centric biases in institutions, including academia and publishing, where left-leaning presumptions of universal "progress" via medical interventions often overlook evidence of stronger outcomes for ASL-fluent Deaf individuals in cognitive and social development.[45] Her work underscores the need for source-critical awareness, favoring firsthand Deaf testimonies over mainstream media portrayals that may downplay cultural genocide risks in assimilationist approaches.[30]

Deaf Advocacy

Key Initiatives and Publications

Nović has advanced deaf advocacy through targeted essays critiquing medical and cultural interventions in deaf lives. In "Sign of the Times," published in Guernica on March 14, 2016, she examines cochlear implants as surgical devices that simulate hearing via electrical impulses to the auditory nerve, arguing that their portrayal as a cure often ignores risks like auditory neuropathy and the importance of early sign language acquisition to prevent language deprivation.[49] Her 2018 New York Times opinion piece, "A Clearer Message on Cochlear Implants," published November 21, advocates for nuanced discussions of the technology's benefits—such as improved speech perception for some users—while highlighting evidence-based downsides, including variable outcomes (success rates around 50-80% for open-set speech recognition in children) and the necessity of bilingual approaches incorporating American Sign Language (ASL) to support cognitive development.[50] In "Signed Away," featured in The Baffler in November 2024, Nović addresses the hearing world's commodification of signed communication, critiquing proprietary systems like Makaton for simplifying and appropriating elements of ASL without crediting deaf linguistic traditions, thereby perpetuating caricatures over authentic deaf-led sign systems.[51] These essays collectively initiative broader awareness of ASL's role in deaf identity formation and critique assimilationist pressures, drawing on historical precedents like the 1880 Milan Conference's suppression of sign language, which studies link to intergenerational literacy gaps in deaf populations.[51][49] Beyond essays, Nović's initiatives emphasize amplifying deaf narratives in media and education; as a deaf rights activist, she has expressed commitment to ensuring deaf children access full language exposure without shame, influencing discussions on policy and representation through public talks and her role in promoting ASL-integrated curricula.[6] Her writings prioritize empirical outcomes, such as longitudinal data showing bilingual deaf children outperforming implant-only peers in reading proficiency by up to 20-30 percentile points when ASL is primary.[50]

Positions on ASL, Cochlear Implants, and Deaf Identity

Sara Nović advocates for American Sign Language (ASL) as a foundational tool for deaf children's language acquisition, emphasizing its role in preventing language deprivation syndrome, which can impair cognitive and social development when deaf youth lack accessible visual language. In her 2018 New York Times opinion piece, she critiques the oversimplification of deaf communication as an "either-or battle between sign language and speech," arguing instead for multimodal approaches that include ASL regardless of technological interventions.[50] She draws from personal experience, having learned ASL as an adult after relying on lip-reading and residual hearing, and highlights empirical data showing that only about 8% of hearing parents achieve conversational proficiency in ASL with their deaf children, leaving many at risk of delayed language milestones.[30] Regarding cochlear implants, Nović views them as a neutral technology that falls short of restoring natural hearing and requires intensive auditory-verbal therapy, often spanning years, without guaranteeing full linguistic competence. She warns that promoting implants as a "cure" for deafness reinforces stigma, potentially eroding Deaf cultural identity by prioritizing assimilation into hearing norms over bilingualism.[49] In interviews, she acknowledges intra-community debates, noting that while some deaf individuals benefit from implants, denying ASL access alongside them can result in "half-languaged" outcomes, particularly if devices malfunction or provide inconsistent auditory input, as depicted in her novel True Biz through the character Charlie, whose faulty implants exacerbate communication barriers until ASL exposure.[30][15] Nović stresses that thriving deaf children, per studies she references, succeed with ASL exposure irrespective of implants or hearing aids, advocating coexistence rather than replacement to support holistic development.[15] Nović ties these positions to Deaf identity, portraying deafness not as a pathology but as a cultural and linguistic affiliation that offers a "unique perspective on the world," endangered by historical oralism and modern medicalization efforts aimed at eradication.[50][49] She argues that ASL fosters a horizontal, diverse Deaf community—encompassing variations like Black American Sign Language—against power imbalances where hearing-dominated systems impose English-centric education, marginalizing native signers.[15] In True Biz and related discussions, she illustrates how late ASL acquisition, as in her own case, enables reclamation of identity previously stifled by hearing-centric upbringing, while critiquing assumptions that technological "fixes" obviate the need for cultural preservation.[30] Her stance aligns with evidence from bilingual deaf education models, which demonstrate superior outcomes in literacy and social integration compared to implant-only protocols lacking visual language support.[49]

Broader Debates and Criticisms

Nović's advocacy intersects with longstanding tensions between proponents of American Sign Language (ASL) as a primary mode of communication for deaf children and those favoring auditory-oral approaches, often augmented by cochlear implants. In a 2018 New York Times opinion piece, she described cochlear implants as "neutral" technology rather than a universal cure, cautioning that overreliance on them without ASL access risks language deprivation syndrome, where children fail to acquire fluency in any language due to inadequate visual or auditory input during critical developmental windows.[50] This position aligns with empirical findings that deaf children denied early ASL exposure exhibit higher rates of cognitive delays and lower literacy, even with implants, as visual languages facilitate foundational linguistic structures absent in auditory-only training.[52] Critics of ASL-centric advocacy, including Nović's, argue it perpetuates cultural separatism and undervalues cochlear implants' proven benefits, such as enabling 70-90% of pediatric recipients to develop spoken language skills sufficient for mainstream education when implanted before age two.[53] Medical and parental advocates contend that framing implants as threats to "Deaf identity" dismisses parental autonomy and data showing improved employment and social outcomes for oral-communicating deaf individuals, with some labeling opposition as ideologically driven resistance to technological progress that has reduced profound deafness's isolating effects.[54] [55] Nović counters that implants' variable efficacy—dependent on factors like neural plasticity and post-surgical therapy—does not negate the need for bimodal approaches, citing cases where implant failures leave children linguistically isolated without sign language backups.[30] These debates extend to deaf identity, where Nović and like-minded advocates reject medical models viewing deafness solely as pathology, instead emphasizing cultural linguistics; detractors, however, criticize this as minimizing biological realities, noting that untreated profound deafness correlates with higher comorbidity risks like delayed neural development, independent of cultural framing.[56] Recent studies support hybrid models, finding no detriment—and potential gains—in spoken vocabulary from concurrent ASL use, challenging oralism's historical suppression of sign languages post-1880 Milan Conference.[57] While Nović's writings, such as in True Biz, illustrate these trade-offs through characters navigating implant limitations, the discourse remains polarized, with deaf culturalists wary of eroding community cohesion amid rising implant rates exceeding 50% of U.S. deaf births.[58]

Reception and Impact

Literary Awards and Critical Response

Nović's debut novel Girl at War (2015) received the American Library Association's Alex Award, recognizing books written for adults with special appeal to young readers aged 12-18. It was also a finalist for the Los Angeles Times Book Prize in Current Interest.[6] Additionally, the novel was longlisted for the Baileys Women's Prize for Fiction in 2016.[59] Her second novel, True Biz (2022), similarly won the Alex Award from the American Library Association.[29] It achieved New York Times bestseller status, was selected as a Reese's Book Club pick, and named an Editors' Choice by the New York Times Book Review.[29] The book was further designated as the 2024 One Book, One Philadelphia community reading selection.[60] Critics praised Girl at War for its portrayal of the Yugoslav Wars through a child's perspective, with the New York Times Book Review selecting it as an Editor's Choice and Booklist awarding it a starred review for its emotional depth and historical insight.[61] The Guardian commended the novel's charm in capturing youthful resilience amid trauma but noted occasional disruptions in tone from expository dialogue.[25] True Biz garnered acclaim for authentically depicting Deaf culture and education, with reviewers highlighting its balance of character development and cultural education; the Lafayette student newspaper described it as a "stunning portrait" of Deaf life.[62] The New York Times review emphasized Nović's personal insights into adolescence and Deaf identity, derived from her own experiences.[10] Some assessments, however, found the narrative occasionally lacking in depth despite strong thematic intent.[63]

Advocacy Achievements and Controversies

Nović has advanced Deaf advocacy primarily through her nonfiction essays and integration of Deaf cultural themes into her fiction, emphasizing the preservation of American Sign Language (ASL) and critique of auditory-verbal therapy mandates. Her 2018 New York Times op-ed, "A Clearer Message on Cochlear Implants," argued that promotional materials from device manufacturers and medical organizations often present the technology as a universal "miracle cure" while downplaying failure rates—estimated at up to 20% for pediatric users—and the necessity of bilingual ASL-spoken language approaches for optimal outcomes, potentially leading to language deprivation in children.[50] This piece contributed to broader discussions on informed consent in Deaf healthcare, aligning with empirical data from studies showing that early ASL exposure improves cognitive and linguistic development even among implanted children.[50] In her teaching roles at institutions like Oberlin College, where she instructs Deaf studies and creative writing, Nović has educated hearing and Deaf students on cultural linguistics and identity, fostering greater institutional awareness of ASL as a primary language rather than a remedial tool.[64] Her novel True Biz (2022), which became a New York Times bestseller and Reese's Book Club selection, dramatizes these issues through characters experiencing cochlear implant malfunctions and school policies favoring oralism over signing, thereby amplifying Deaf voices in mainstream literature and prompting public discourse on educational equity for the estimated 1 million Deaf children in the U.S.[2][15] These efforts have positioned Nović as a prominent figure in Deaf cultural preservation, with outlets recognizing her as a role model for integrating personal Deaf experiences into advocacy that challenges hearing-centric norms.[65] However, her stances have fueled controversies within divided Deaf and medical communities, particularly regarding cochlear implants, which have enabled measurable hearing restoration in over 700,000 recipients worldwide since FDA approval in 1984 but remain contentious for potentially eroding Deaf cultural identity when imposed without ASL support.[50] Critics from audiology fields, such as those affiliated with the Alexander Graham Bell Association, contend that such advocacy undervalues clinical success rates—where 80-90% of implanted children achieve spoken language proficiency—and prioritizes cultural ideology over individual choice, though Nović counters that unbalanced messaging risks uninformed parental decisions leading to suboptimal results.[30] This tension reflects ongoing causal debates: while implants address sensory deficits empirically, their deployment without linguistic safeguards can causally hinder holistic development, as evidenced by higher rates of academic underperformance among orally educated Deaf youth lacking visual language access.[15] No major personal scandals have emerged, but her work has intensified scrutiny on whether Deaf advocacy inadvertently stigmatizes adaptive technologies for subsets of users who benefit from them.[66]

Personal Life

Relationships and Current Residence

Nović married Zach Stone, a radio producer and morning-show personality at WXTU-FM in Philadelphia, in January 2018.[17] The couple has two young children, with Nović discussing the challenges of parenting amid her writing career in a 2023 interview.[67] She resides in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, with her family.[68]
