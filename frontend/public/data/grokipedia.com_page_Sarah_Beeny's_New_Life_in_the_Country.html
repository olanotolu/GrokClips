Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country

Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country is a British documentary television series that premiered on Channel 4 in 2020, following property developer and television presenter Sarah Beeny, her husband Graham Swift, and their four sons as they relocate from London to a 220-acre semi-derelict former dairy farm in Somerset, England, to renovate it into a low-carbon, self-sufficient family home.[1][2] The programme chronicles their adaptation to rural living, including farming activities, family dynamics, and ambitious construction projects aimed at sustainability, such as incorporating renewable energy and eco-friendly materials.[1] It has aired multiple series through 2025, attracting viewers interested in property renovation and countryside relocation amid urban exodus trends.[3] The series emphasizes practical challenges like restoring outbuildings, managing livestock, and integrating artistic elements from Swift's background, while showcasing Beeny's expertise in property development derived from prior shows such as Property Ladder.[3] Notable aspects include the family's efforts toward off-grid living and business diversification, though the project has faced logistical hurdles typical of large-scale rural renovations.[1] A defining characteristic has been ongoing controversies over planning permissions, with local residents and Somerset Council objecting to the scale of developments, described by critics as resembling a "mini-Downton Abbey," leading to rejections of applications and, in 2025, enforcement notices requiring demolition of unauthorized extensions built without approval.[4][5] These disputes highlight tensions between individual property ambitions and local regulatory frameworks preserving rural aesthetics, with Beeny's team arguing for the project's environmental benefits despite procedural lapses documented on air.[6][7]

Core Premise of the Series

Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country is a documentary series that chronicles the relocation of property expert and television presenter Sarah Beeny, her husband Graham Swift, and their four sons from urban London to a 220-acre semi-derelict former dairy farm in Somerset, England. Premiering on Channel 4 on 11 November 2020, the program documents their real-time efforts to transform the property through renovations, including the construction of a modern carbon-neutral home, while adapting to the demands of rural existence.[23][24][3] The series adopts an observational format centered on authentic, unscripted footage of family-driven projects, such as rewilding portions of the land, establishing animal husbandry with bees and hens, and exploring viable rural enterprises like cheesemaking and cider production. It foregrounds the integration of Beeny's professional background in property development with the empirical realities of farm operations, including logistical hurdles, material costs, and environmental dependencies that dictate outcomes.[23][1] Unlike stylized reality programming that emphasizes narrative arcs of triumph, the core premise prioritizes candid portrayals of the uncertainties inherent in off-grid living and agriculture, such as variable yields influenced by weather and the iterative trial-and-error of sustainable practices, thereby offering viewers a grounded view of countryside self-sufficiency.[1][3]

Production and Broadcast

Development and Filming Approach

The series was produced by Outline Productions for Channel 4, with executive producers including Laura Mansfield. Filming began in advance of the Beeny family's relocation to Somerset in late 2019, enabling the capture of preliminary decision-making and property search processes to depict events as they unfolded chronologically rather than through post-move reconstruction. This timing facilitated an observational documentary format that prioritized real-time progression over dramatized retrospectives, aligning with the production's emphasis on verifiable milestones such as the acquisition of the 220-acre Rise Hall farm and initial site assessments.[23][25] The filming approach relied on self-shooting producers and directors to generate raw, unpolished footage, minimizing intrusive crew presence to reflect the family's practical, hands-on ethos amid the renovation challenges. This method incorporated unfiltered depictions of setbacks, such as planning disputes and construction delays, without extensive narrative editing to impose contrived resolutions, thereby preserving causal sequences of decision-making and outcomes. Beeny contributed to shaping the focus on empirical progress indicators, including quantifiable elements like renovated square footage and sustainable infrastructure installations, to underscore tangible achievements over subjective anecdotes.[26][3]

Channel 4 Airing and Series Timeline

The first series of Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country premiered on Channel 4 on 11 November 2020, consisting of eight episodes aired weekly on Wednesdays through 29 December 2020.[27] The second series followed on 30 November 2021 with nine episodes, broadcast on Tuesdays and extending into January 2022, concluding on 25 January.[28] Series 3 began airing on 17 April 2023, comprising seven episodes shown weekly at 8:00 p.m.[29] Channel 4 renewed the series for subsequent runs based on performance metrics, including viewership exceeding slot averages for ABC1 adults and 16- to 34-year-olds, which supported commissioning of additional seasons despite mixed audience reactions.[30] The fourth series commenced on 4 January 2025, with eight episodes airing Saturdays from 5:10 p.m., ending on 22 February 2025.[1] All episodes across series have been made available on demand via Channel 4's streaming service, All 4, broadening accessibility post-broadcast without modifications to the original content.[31]

Program Content

Renovation Projects and Farm Transformations

The Beeny family acquired a 220-acre semi-derelict former dairy farm in Somerset, initiating extensive renovations to convert outdated dairy buildings and surrounding structures into functional, modern living and operational spaces. Central to the project was the overhaul of derelict barns and farm infrastructure, transforming them from disused agricultural relics into integrated elements of a self-sustaining estate, with efforts documented across multiple series of the program. These upgrades emphasized structural reinforcement and adaptation for residential use, though specific timelines for individual phases extended over several years due to construction complexities and external approvals.[32][33] Sustainable features were incorporated to pursue carbon-neutral operations, including the installation of an extensive array of solar panels on rooftops to generate surplus electricity, reducing reliance on grid power and offsetting environmental impact. Additional eco-friendly elements, such as electricity-powered cookers designed for efficient heat control and minimized running costs, supported the goal of year-round low-emission functionality. Ground stabilization using geosynthetic materials like TERRAM further lowered the carbon footprint by reducing the need for excessive hardcore layering in access paths. However, these initiatives were tempered by real-world material and energy costs, with solar installations requiring upfront investment amid fluctuating supply chain prices.[34][35][36] Profit-oriented ventures included proposals to repurpose barns into glamping-style accommodations or additional dwellings, but these encountered empirical regulatory hurdles, such as protracted planning permission processes and local opposition citing disregard for zoning rules. In one instance, plans to convert two barns into five homes were abandoned in 2024 following community backlash and resubmission failures, highlighting permitting delays that stalled potential revenue streams. Unauthorized constructions, including a substantial extension to the main farmhouse and a treehouse-style cabin built from on-site deadwood, added value through enhanced usable space but provoked enforcement actions; the extension faced a 2025 demolition order from Somerset Council for lacking prior approval, while the treehouse was retained only after a two-year legal appeal. Audience Feedback and Ratings

Audience feedback for Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country has been mixed, with an average IMDb user rating of 7.0 out of 10 based on 138 ratings as of 2025.[3] User reviews on the platform reflect divided opinions, with approximately one-third expressing appreciation for the series' portrayal of rural self-reliance and family-driven projects, such as episodes featuring cottage industries like cheese and cider production, described as "informative" and highlighting the "journey of building a classic style mansion."[66] In contrast, the majority of IMDb reviews criticize the show for its perceived unrelatability, labeling it "pretentious nonsense" due to the family's wealth and lifestyle choices that many viewers cannot aspire to.[66] Social media and forum discussions similarly show polarization, with some users on platforms like Twitter praising the aspirational elements of countryside adaptation and property renovation as motivational for rural enthusiasts, while others decry it as showcasing "rich people spending money" in an out-of-touch manner.[67][68] This feedback appeals particularly to demographics interested in property development and rural living, including ABC1 viewers and those aged 16-34, who rated episodes above the channel's slot averages.[30] Verifiable performance metrics underscore commercial viability amid the backlash, including consolidated viewing figures of 1.9 million for the first two episodes of series 2 and renewals through series 4 in 2025, positioning it as one of Channel 4's top factual brands.[69][30] Consistent Tuesday evening scheduling further indicates sustained audience draw, countering claims of widespread rejection by demonstrating ongoing broadcaster confidence in its draw for property and rural advocacy audiences over urban-focused critiques.[70]

Animal Welfare Concerns

In the first series of Sarah Beeny's New Life in the Country, which aired on Channel 4 starting in November 2020, an episode depicted the family's cat catching a wild rabbit, after which Beeny's children chose to retain it as a pet rather than releasing it.[71] This decision, shown in the context of adapting to rural life on their Somerset farm, elicited immediate viewer complaints aired via social media, with detractors describing the act as "cruel" and arguing that wild animals suffer in captivity due to risks like stress, dietary mismatches, and diminished survival instincts if later freed.[71][72] The criticisms, primarily from urban-based audiences unfamiliar with farm dynamics, emphasized theoretical welfare hazards without on-site evaluation, contrasting with the family's direct monitoring where the rabbit exhibited no visible distress or health decline during the episode's timeframe.[71] No involvement from animal welfare authorities, such as inspections or interventions, was documented, and subsequent coverage yielded no evidence of injury, illness, or euthanasia linked to the rabbit's careâ€”outcomes that would typically surface in media or regulatory reports if adverse.[71] Beyond this isolated event, the series portrayed standard rural animal management on the property's converted dairy farm, including livestock handling aligned with UK norms for free-range or pasture-based systems common in Somerset, where no verified deviations from Defra welfare guidelines or RSPCA standards were alleged by experts or officials.[41] The absence of formal complaints or enforcement actions underscores that such practices, rooted in practical causation like predator-prey interactions on working lands, generally prioritize observable vitality over precautionary prohibitions absent empirical harm.[71]
