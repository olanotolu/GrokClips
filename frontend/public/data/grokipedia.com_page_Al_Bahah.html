Al-Baha

Al-Baha or Al-Bahah (Arabic: الباحة) is a city in the Hejaz region of Saudi Arabia, located in the Sarawat Mountains. It is the capital and administrative center of Al-Baha Province and is considered one of the country's notable tourist destinations.[1]

Geography

Al-Baha City lies in the south west of the kingdom of Saudi Arabia, between Makkah (which borders it from the north, west, and south west) and 'Asir (which borders it from the south-east). It is the smallest of the kingdom's provinces (9,921 square kilometres (3,831 sq mi)). It is surrounded by a number of cities, including Taif on the north, Bishah on the east, and Al-Qunfudah on coast of the Red Sea in the west. This tourist city is situated in an area characterized by natural tree cover and agricultural plateaus. It consists of nine governorates, the most important of which are Baljurashi, Al-Mandaq, and Al-Makhwah, in addition to the Al-Baha city in the center of the province. The province comprises 42 administrative centres, and it has a population of 339,174 (2022). The province is known for its beauty and has forests, wildlife areas, valleys and mountains that attract visitors from all parts of the kingdom and the Persian Gulf area. Some of these areas are the forests of Raghdan, Ghomsan, Fayk, and Aljabal, and many other historical and archaeological sites. It contains more than 53 forests. Al-Baha is the homeland of the Ghamid and Zahran tribes and is divided geographically into three distinct parts: Sarah, which contains the high Hijaz mountains characterized by temperate weather and rich plant cover due to relatively high annual rainfall, the Tihamah which is the lowland coastal area to the west of the Hejaz, characterized by very hot and humid weather and very little rainfall average, and the eastern hills characterized by an altitude of 1,550 to 1,900 metres (5,090 to 6,230 feet) above sea level with cool winters, hot summers and sparse plant cover. The largest city in the province by population is Al-Baha, while Baljurashi is the largest by area; the second largest city by population is Al-Mandaq. In Tihamah, there are two major cities: Qilwah and Al-Makhwah. The number of tribes are 18, branching from the main ones, Ghamid and Zahran. The province has 13 settled tribes (working in trades and agriculture) and 5 nomadic tribes.

Climate

Al Bahah has a hot desert climate (Köppen: BWh). The climate is greatly affected by its varying geographic features. Generally speaking, the climate in Al-Baha is mild with temperatures ranging between 7 and 32.3 °C (44.6 and 90.1 °F). Due to its location at 2,200 metres (7,200 ft) above sea level, Al Baha's climate is, by Saudi Arabian standards, moderate in summer and cold in winter. The area attracts visitors looking for a moderate climate and pristine, scenic views. In the Tehama area of the province, which is down on the coast, the climate is hot in the summer and warm in the winter. Humidity ranges from 52%–67%. While in the mountainous region, which is known as As-Sarah, the weather is cooler in summer and winter. Rainfall in the mountainous region lies in the range of 229 to 581 millimetres (9 to 23 in). The average throughout the whole region is 100 to 250 millimetres (3.9 to 9.8 in) annually.

History

Post-World War 1, the village of El-Zafir (Arabic: قَرْيَة ٱلظَّفِيْر, romanized: Qaryat aẓ-Ẓafīr) had been the administrative centre of what was known then as Belad Ghamd, but with the establishment of Saudi Arabian government, the Ghamd and Zahran were administered as a unit in 1925, and the seat of local government transferred to Baljurashi, a town situated 15 miles (24 kilometres) south of El-Zafir. In 1963, during the reign of King Faisal, the administrative center was relocated from Baljurashi to Al-Baha. The tribes of Ghamid and Zahran are the indigenous people of Al Baha. These tribes are branches of the ancient Azd tribe, which originated in Yemen. Prince Husam bin Saud has been the governor of the province since April 21, 2017. The name "Pearl of Resorts" is the name given to Al-Baha by those acquainted with the city. The name "Garden of the Hejaz" (Arabic: حَدِيْقَة ٱلْحِجَاز, romanized: Ḥadīqat al-Ḥijāz) was the name given to it by the Sharif of Mecca.

Historic attractions

Al-Baha is also known for its traditional towers, each of which is called a qaṣbah (Arabic: قَصْبَة). It was said "Apparently unique to Albaha architecture are the qasaba towers. Controversy surrounds their function—some argue that they were built as lookouts, and others that they were keeps, or even granaries. Perhaps it is a combination, although the right position of a watchtower, on a hill top, is the wrong place for a keep or granary."[5] Thee Ain Village is situated at about 24 km (15 miles) southwest of Al-Baha, across 'Aqabat al-Baha, also known as Aqbat King Fahd. This village is sometimes known as the "marble village" as it is built on a small marble outcrop. The grove of palm trees is lush due to a small stream nearby. The villagers say that the marble village glows at sunset. It also has a steep escarpment road, running into and out of tunnels on the way up and down. Thee Ain was famous for its fruits and banana plantations which still grow there. The village dates back about 400 years, and it witnessed the battles between the Ottoman Turks and its inhabitants. The village was named after an ʿAyn (Arabic: عَيْن, water spring) continuously flowing from the nearby mountains to several reservoirs and each particular pond has its own name. There is a local legend that talks about a man who lost his cane in one of the valleys, and to retrieve it he tracked it until he reached the village, he gathered its inhabitants and retrieved his cane after digging the spring:[6]
Even the road that leads to the (Dhee Ayn) village is impressive, and several historical stone and slate towers dot the way. Al-Bahah Region is known as the region of 1001 towers, once built to protect villages, roads and plantations from rivalling tribes. Today, these towers are abandoned, and many of them are partially or completely in ruins.
— Atlas Obscura[6] Other notable historic attractions include the Al-Atula Archaeological Village, featuring ancient stone structures and inscriptions dating back centuries, and the Matair Al-Aish Archaeological Village, known for its traditional architecture and historical significance in the region.[7] The Al Ayed Heritage Museum in Al-Baha showcases artifacts and exhibits on the area's cultural history.[8]

Infrastructure

Souq

The souq or traditional market in Al Baha has been studied for its role in maintaining order and social customs.Al-Zahrani, 2001 "Weekly markets in Al-Baha, which is located in the south-western part of Saudi Arabia, were not only performing economic functions, but also social functions. Those markets continued to perform these functions until around 1975 when the modern markets began to take over, and other social institutions like schools, mail, and mass media began to do the social functions of those weekly markets. This study is trying to explain the social functions of weekly markets in Al-Baha like uniting the tribes, using the market as a tool of punishment, entertainment, mail and religious education."Al-Zahrani, 2001 Traditional souqs in Al-Baha remain active as of 2025, blending heritage with modern elements to support tourism and local economy. Notable examples include Souq Al-Khamis (Thursday Market), which transforms into a Ramadan nights festival featuring cultural events and shopping, and Souq Tholathaa (Tuesday Market), one of the largest popular markets with vendors primarily from local tribes selling traditional goods like honey, spices, and handicrafts.[9][10]

Transportation

King Saud Domestic Airport is located approximately 45 km (28 mi) northeast of Al-Baha city center in Al-Aqiq Governorate, Al-Baha Province, and opened on 1 June 1983. It receives domestic flights from major cities in Saudi Arabia, including Riyadh, Jeddah, and Dammam.[11]

Hospitals and medical care

King Fahd Hospital (Arabic: مستشفى الملك فهد, romanized: Mustashfā al-Malik Fahd) is the main public hospital in Al-Baha, operated under the supervision of the Ministry of Health (MOH). It obtained Joint Commission International (JCI) accreditation in 2013.[12] As of 2020, it holds conditional accreditation from the Central Board for Accreditation of Healthcare Institutions (CBAHI).[13] The hospital serves as a training center for postgraduate programs accredited by the Saudi Commission for Health Specialties (SCFHS), including internships and residency in various specialties.[14][15] The Al-Baha Health Cluster, part of the MOH, oversees healthcare services for over 303,000 beneficiaries through 94 primary care centers, 10 general hospitals, and 11 specialized centers as of 2025.[16] Other facilities include:
  • Ghodran General Hospital (Arabic: مستشفى غدرَان العام, romanized: Mustashfā Ghudrān al-ʿĀm), a private hospital established in 1998.[17]
  • Prince Mishari bin Saud Hospital, Baljurashi (Arabic: مستشفى الأمير مشاري بن سعود بلجرشي, romanized: Mustashfā al-Amīr Mishārī bin Suʿūd Baljurashī), a general hospital serving the Baljurashi area.[18]
  • Al-Mandaq General Hospital (Arabic: مستشفى المندق العام, romanized: Mustashfā al-Mandaq al-ʿĀm).[19]
  • Shamekh Polyclinic (Arabic: مستوصف شامخ الأهلي, romanized: Mustawṣaf Shāmikh al-Ahlī), a general medical complex.[20]

Traditional tribal cemeteries

The southern tribal hinterland of Al-Baha — home to especially the al-Ghamdi and al-Zahrani tribes — has been renowned for centuries for their tribal cemeteries that were slowly vanishing as of 2006, according to Arab News. One old villager explained how tribal cemeteries came about: "People used to die in large numbers and very rapidly one after the other because of diseases. So the villagers would dig graves close by burying members of the same family in one area. That was how the family and tribal burial grounds came about," he said. The old man continued, "If the family ran out of space, they would open old graves where family members had been buried before and add more people to them. This process is known as khashf." During famines and outbreaks of epidemics, huge numbers of people would die and many tribes faced difficulties in digging new graves because of the difficult weather. Elderly people remember that in olden times, the winter used to stretch for more than six months and would be accompanied by much rain and fog, making movement difficult. But due to tribal rivalries, many families would guard their cemeteries, and put restrictions on who got buried in them. Across Al-Baha Province, burial grounds are constructed in different ways. Some cemeteries consist of underground vaults or concrete burial chambers with the capacity of holding a large number of bodies at a time. Such vaults include windows for people to peer through and are usually decorated ornately with writings, drawings and patterns. Muhammad Saleh, a local resident, said, "One of the things that is so iconic about many of these graves is the fact that many of them are not directed toward the Kaaba. This tells us that some of these graves are from the pre-Islamic era. In Islam the face of a dead person should be toward the Kaaba."[21]

Culture

Religion

The residents of Al-Baha are Sunni Muslims, predominantly following the Hanbali school of jurisprudence, which is the official madhhab in Saudi Arabia. Historically, the Hejaz region, including Al-Baha, was influenced by the Shafi'i school of fiqh, which was dominant there before the establishment of the modern Saudi state.[29]

Cuisine

The city is known for its healthy and nutritious traditional meals derived from local farming such as Daghābīs (دَغَابِيْس) and ʿAṣīdah (عَصِيْدَه).[30]
The Province is famous for its production of honey, in addition to its agricultural production of vegetables, grain, fruit, and dates. The region also grows grapes, grapefruits, lemons, oranges, pomegranates and dates.
— Al Baha City Profile[31]

Language

The dialect spoken in Al-Baha is a variety of Hijazi Arabic. It is claimed by some Arab authors that the dialect of Balad Ghamid and Zahran (now known as Al-Bahah Province in Saudi Arabia) is nearest to classical Arabic.[32] Aḥmed ʿAbdul-Ghafūr ʿAṭṭār (أَحْمَد عَبْدُ ٱلْغَفُوْر عَطَّار) had said in an article that the dialect of the Hejaz, especially that which is spoken in Balad Ghamid and Zahran, is close to the Classical Dialect.[32] Faiṣal Ghorī (فَيْصَل غوْرِي), a known scholar of Arabic literature, wrote in his book Qabaʾil Al-Ḥijāz (قَبَائِل ٱلْحِجَاز) "The Quranic Arabic upon which our grammar is based on does not exist in any tribe. The only thing we can say is that there are some tribes whose language is much closer to classical language. The tribes of Belad Ghamid and Zahran are a good example of this."[32]

Media

Al Baha is served by four major Arabic newspapers, Asharq Al-Awsat , Al Watan , Okaz , and Al Bilad, as well as two major English newspapers, the Saudi Gazette and Arab News . Okaz and Al Watan are the primary newspapers of Al Baha and some other Saudi cities. With over a million readers; they focus mainly on issues that affect the city. Al Baha Today (e-version Newspaper) is a daily electronic newspaper directed at locals, new residents, incoming visitors, tourists, and the developing tourism business sector. The magazine serves as a guide to the city's sights and attractions, restaurants, shopping and entertainment. It can be accessed via Albahatoday.[33] Television stations serving the city area include Saudi TV1, Saudi TV2, Saudi TV Sports, Al Ekhbariya, the ART channels network and hundreds of cable, satellite and other specialty television providers.

Natural resources

The Al-Baha region is known for its ancient mining sites, including major gold mining areas such as Khayāl al-Maṣnaʿ (Arabic: خَيَال ٱلْمَصْنَع) and Al-‘Aqīq (Arabic: ٱلْعَقِيْق).[31] The region is also renowned for agricultural production, including honey, vegetables, grains, fruits, and dates.[31] As of September 2025, Al-Baha's mineral reserves, including gold, copper, and zinc, are estimated to be worth approximately SAR 285 billion (about $76 billion).[35]

Wildlife

Visitors from inside the kingdom and nearby countries are particularly attracted to the area by its more than 99 forests (as of 2025), which include the Raghdan forest, which covers an area of 600,000 square metres (60 ha) just 5 kilometres (3.1 miles) from the city. It has been provided with children's playgrounds and other amenities. The Amdan forest is 55 km (34 miles) to the north of Al Baha. It abounds in olive trees, Ara'r shrubs and other natural vegetation. The Wadi Feig forest is 8 km (5.0 miles) from Al Baha. It is encircled by a green valley littered with apricot, pomegranate and grape orchards. The forest of Wadi Al Ageeg is 40 km (25 miles) from Al Baha and abounds in various fruit trees and tall lotus trees. Al Baha airport is in Al-Aqiq Governorate. Al Kharrara waterfall is 30 kilometers to the south of Al Baha. Its scenic view attracts large numbers of visitors.[36][31] Shada Mountain (Arabic: جَبَل شَدَا, romanized: Jabal Shadā) is the highest summit in Makhwah. It features rare rocky formations that attract amateur climbers of various levels. The Arabian leopard was historically present in the Asir Mountains between Al-Bahah and Abha, and in the Hijaz Mountains to the north; however, as of 2025, it is considered possibly extinct in the wild in Saudi Arabia, with the subspecies listed as critically endangered globally.[37][38][39] Al Baha's forests and mountains also support other wildlife, including the Arabian ibex, rock hyrax, and various bird species such as the Yemen serin and Rüppell's weaver.[1]

Western travellers

St John Philby (also known as Sheikh Abdullah by King Abdulaziz) documented his 1917 journey crossing from Riyadh to Jeddah by the "backdoor" route through the Arabian highlands in his book The Heart of Arabia.[40] He was awarded the Royal Geographical Society Founders Gold Medal in 1920 for this desert journey.
