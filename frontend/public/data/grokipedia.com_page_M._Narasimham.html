M. Narasimham

Maidavolu Narasimham (3 June 1927 – 20 April 2021) was an Indian central banker renowned for spearheading financial sector reforms.[1][2] He served as the thirteenth Governor of the Reserve Bank of India from 2 May to 30 November 1977, marking the only instance of an internal RBI cadre member ascending to that position.[1][3] Narasimham's career began in 1950 when he joined the RBI as a research officer in its Economic Department, eventually rising through governmental roles including Additional Secretary in the Department of Economic Affairs.[4][5] Following his governorship, he represented India as Executive Director at the World Bank and International Monetary Fund.[2] His defining legacy stems from chairing two pivotal committees on banking reforms: the 1991 panel, which advocated deregulation of interest rates, reduced statutory preemptions, and enhanced capital adequacy to align Indian banks with global standards amid economic liberalization; and the 1998 follow-up, which further emphasized consolidation, governance improvements, and management of non-performing assets.[6][7] These recommendations formed the intellectual foundation for transforming India's state-dominated banking system into a more competitive and resilient structure, influencing policies that bolstered financial stability post the 1991 balance-of-payments crisis.[6]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Family

Maidavolu Narasimham was born on 3 June 1927 in Nellore, Andhra Pradesh, into a family originating from Maidavolu village in Guntur district of the region.[2][1] His family held prominence in legal and intellectual circles, with his father advancing to the position of judge at the Andhra Pradesh High Court.[3] Narasimham's mother, Padmavati, was the eldest daughter in her family, contributing to the household's emphasis on education and public service.[3] Limited public records detail his siblings or immediate extended kin, though the familial background underscored a tradition of scholarly and professional achievement that influenced his trajectory in public finance.[3]

Academic Background

Maithili Narasimham, commonly known as M. Narasimham, pursued his early education in Chennai, attending Loyola College followed by Presidency College, where he excelled academically by topping the university examinations.[3] These institutions provided his foundational training in economics, aligning with his later career in financial research and policy.[3] He then advanced to higher studies at St. John's College, Cambridge University, focusing on economics despite familial preference for Oxford. There, he obtained an MA degree (Cantab.), which equipped him for analytical roles upon returning to India in the 1950s.[3][8] His Cambridge tenure also involved engagement with intellectual circles, including contemporaries like I.G. Patel, fostering networks influential in his public service trajectory.[3]

Professional Career

Entry into Banking Sector

Narasimham entered the banking sector in 1950 by joining the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) in Bombay as a research officer in its Economic Department, specifically within the department of research and statistics.[3][9][10] This entry-level position marked his initial involvement in central banking functions, focusing on economic analysis and data compilation at a time when India's financial institutions were consolidating post-independence.[3] His selection for the role followed a rigorous interview process conducted by senior figures in Indian central banking, underscoring the competitive nature of recruitment into the RBI during that era.[3] From this starting point, Narasimham's work in research and statistics provided foundational experience in monetary policy and economic indicators, contributing to his eventual rise through the RBI ranks over more than two decades of service.[3][11]

Advancement in RBI and Government Roles


Maidavolu Narasimham joined the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) in 1950 as a research officer in the Department of Research and Statistics.[3] Over the subsequent decades, he progressed through the institution's ranks, demonstrating expertise in economic analysis and policy formulation.[12] His internal advancement within the RBI cadre positioned him uniquely among central bank leaders.[13] Prior to his elevation to the governorship, Narasimham served as Additional Secretary in the Department of Economic Affairs under the Ministry of Finance, where he contributed to fiscal and monetary policy coordination between the government and the central bank.[10] This governmental role bridged his RBI experience with broader policy-making responsibilities, enhancing his influence on India's economic framework during the 1970s.[11] Narasimham's career culminated in his appointment as the 13th Governor of the RBI on 2 May 1977, serving until 30 November 1977 in an interim capacity following the political transition after the Emergency period.[12] He remains the only RBI Governor selected directly from the bank's internal cadre, underscoring his merit-based rise without prior external high-level appointments typical for the role.[7] This distinction highlights the RBI's recognition of his deep institutional knowledge and technical proficiency in steering monetary policy amid economic challenges.[3]

Governorship of the Reserve Bank of India

M. Narasimham served as the 13th Governor of the Reserve Bank of India from 2 May 1977 to 30 November 1977.[14] His appointment occurred immediately following the electoral victory of the Janata Party and the end of the Emergency imposed by the previous Congress government, marking a period of political transition in India.[15] This seven-month tenure was interim in nature, providing continuity in RBI leadership during the shift to a new administration led by Prime Minister Morarji Desai.[16] Narasimham's elevation to Governor was unprecedented, as he became the first and only individual to be promoted from within the RBI's internal cadre to the position, reflecting his extensive experience accumulated over decades in the organization.[12][13] During his brief term, the RBI maintained its core functions of monetary stability and banking regulation amid economic challenges, including managing inflation and credit policies in the post-Emergency context, though no major structural reforms were enacted specifically under his direct oversight.[3] He was succeeded by I. G. Patel on 1 December 1977.[17]

Banking Reforms and Policy Contributions

Pre-Reform Context and Rationale

Prior to the 1991 economic liberalization, India's banking sector operated under a heavily regulated framework dominated by public sector banks, which controlled over 90% of deposits and advances by the late 1980s. These banks faced chronic inefficiencies due to directed lending mandates prioritizing social objectives over commercial viability, resulting in low profitability—public sector banks recorded return on assets below 0.5% on average—and escalating non-performing assets from politically influenced loans to inefficient public enterprises. Strict interest rate controls, high cash reserve ratios exceeding 15%, and branching restrictions stifled competition and innovation, fostering a system of financial repression where credit allocation favored government priorities rather than market signals.[18] The broader macroeconomic imbalances exacerbated these vulnerabilities, culminating in a severe balance of payments crisis by mid-1991. Foreign exchange reserves plummeted to cover just two weeks of imports, triggered by persistent fiscal deficits averaging 8-9% of GDP, current account deficits widening to 3% of GDP in 1990-91, and external shocks like the Gulf War's oil price surge and reduced remittances from the Middle East.[19] The Reserve Bank of India responded with drastic measures, including raising import cash margins to 200% and pledging gold reserves abroad, averting default but underscoring the unsustainability of the inward-looking, import-substituting growth model that had relied on concessional foreign aid and suppressed domestic savings mobilization through banking channels.[20] This crisis provided the impetus for structural reforms, including banking sector overhaul, as conditions attached to an IMF bailout demanded deregulation to enhance efficiency and global integration. The rationale centered on dismantling the oligopolistic, state-controlled structure to foster competition, reduce fiscal burdens from bank recapitalizations (which had already cost over 1% of GDP annually), and align incentives with prudential norms, thereby enabling credit to flow to productive private investment amid liberalization.[21] Without such changes, the sector risked perpetuating low productivity—evident in stagnant real credit growth below 10% annually—and impeding the transition to a market-oriented economy.

Narasimham Committee I (1991)

The Narasimham Committee I, formally known as the Committee on the Financial System, was established by the Government of India on 14 August 1991, chaired by M. Narasimham, with nine members tasked to evaluate the structure, organization, and operations of the banking sector and recommend measures to enhance efficiency and competitiveness amid India's economic liberalization initiated that year.[22][23] The committee's work addressed longstanding issues such as high non-performing assets (NPAs), inadequate capital adequacy, directed credit programs that distorted resource allocation, and excessive government intervention, which had led to inefficiencies in public sector banks since nationalization in 1969.[24] The committee submitted its main report on 16 November 1991, advocating a shift toward market-oriented reforms to align Indian banking with global standards.[22] Key recommendations encompassed reducing the Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR) from 38.5% to 25% over time and the Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) to free up liquidity for lending; phasing down government ownership in public sector banks to below 51% while granting operational autonomy; and mandating banks to achieve 8% risk-weighted capital adequacy as per Basel I norms by 1994 and 1997 in stages.[25][26] The report also proposed deregulating interest rates on deposits and loans, abolishing branch licensing policies to promote competition, and easing restrictions on entry for new private sector banks.[27] Further structural proposals included consolidating public sector banks through mergers to create three large international banks, four national banks, regional banks, and local banks, forming a four-tier hierarchy to optimize scale and specialization. To tackle NPAs, it suggested establishing an Asset Reconstruction Fund and shifting to a system of income recognition, asset classification, and provisioning norms closer to international practices, reducing the role of directed lending from 40% to 10% of net bank credit.[28] The committee emphasized strengthening the Reserve Bank of India's supervisory role, including on-site inspections and off-site surveillance, while advocating for the development of money and capital markets to support indirect financing.[29] These measures aimed to foster a sound, healthy financial system capable of supporting sustained economic growth without compromising financial stability.[24]

Narasimham Committee II (1998)

The Committee on Banking Sector Reforms, chaired by M. Narasimham, was established in 1998 to assess the progress of banking reforms initiated after the 1991 committee and to recommend second-generation measures aimed at enhancing efficiency, competitiveness, and resilience in the financial system amid globalization and technological advancements.[30] The report was submitted on April 23, 1998, to Finance Minister Yashwant Sinha, focusing on prudential norms, institutional restructuring, and regulatory shifts to align Indian banks with international standards. Central recommendations addressed prudential norms, including raising the capital to risk-weighted assets ratio (CRAR) from 8% to 12% over time to bolster solvency and absorb shocks, with phased implementation starting at 9% by March 31, 2000. The committee targeted reducing gross non-performing assets (NPAs) to 3-4% through stricter classification, provisioning, and recovery mechanisms, advocating mark-to-market accounting for investments and the creation of an asset reconstruction fund to handle bad loans.[25] It also proposed lowering statutory liquidity ratio (SLR) and cash reserve ratio (CRR) gradually to free up resources for lending while emphasizing risk management, internal audits, and disclosure standards. On structural reforms, the committee urged consolidating public sector banks (PSBs) into three or four large entities through voluntary mergers to achieve global scale and reduce fragmentation, alongside diluting government equity in PSBs to 33% to enable private participation and improve governance.[22] Weak banks were advised to adopt "narrow banking," restricting activities to low-risk government securities until viability was restored. For rural banking, it recommended public sector banks forming subsidiaries to handle rural operations, while consolidating regional rural banks (RRBs) from over 196 to around 30-50 viable units.[31] Regulatory proposals included granting greater operational autonomy to PSBs in staffing, branch expansion, and interest rates, with full deregulation of lending rates and a phased shift from administered deposit rates. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) was urged to transition from direct intervention to supervisory oversight, including off-site monitoring and consolidated supervision of banking groups. Legal reforms were highlighted, such as expedited debt recovery tribunals and bankruptcy laws to address delays in resolutions.[32] These measures aimed to foster a market-oriented system, though implementation varied, with some like CRAR enhancements adopted promptly via RBI directives.

Implementation and Key Outcomes

The recommendations of the Narasimham Committee I (1991) were adopted as the blueprint for initial banking sector reforms amid India's 1991 balance-of-payments crisis, with the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) introducing prudential norms for income recognition, asset classification, and provisioning in phases starting in 1992 to address weak asset quality and recapitalization needs.[33] A minimum capital-to-risk assets ratio (CRAR) of 8% was mandated for commercial banks from 1992, aligning with Basel I standards to bolster solvency.[34] Statutory liquidity ratio (SLR) was progressively lowered from 38.5% in 1991, and cash reserve ratio (CRR) from 15%, while interest rates on deposits and loans were deregulated stepwise, culminating in greater market-driven pricing by the mid-1990s. Guidelines for new private sector banks were issued in 1993, enabling licenses for entities like ICICI Bank and HDFC Bank between 1994 and 1995.[35] The Narasimham Committee II (1998) built on prior progress, prompting RBI's second-phase reforms launched in October 1998, which emphasized consolidation, risk management, and alignment with Basel norms.[36] CRAR targets were raised to 9% by 2000 and 10% by 2002 for scheduled commercial banks, with phased adoption of advanced provisioning for non-performing assets (NPAs) defined as loans overdue for 90 days.[37] Structural measures included promoting mergers among public sector banks for viability and shifting toward a market-oriented inter-bank call money market, reducing directed lending's share from over 50% of net bank credit pre-1991. Foreign bank entry was liberalized, allowing expanded branching subject to capital requirements. Key outcomes included a fortified banking system's resilience, with average CRAR for scheduled commercial banks rising from 7.8% in 1992-93 to over 12% by the early 2000s, enabling better absorption of credit risks.[38] Competition intensified as private sector banks' share in assets grew from negligible levels to about 15% by 2005, fostering efficiency gains like higher return on assets (from 0.2% pre-reforms to 0.9% by 2003) and innovation in products such as automated teller machines and retail lending. However, public sector banks, holding 75% of assets, grappled with elevated NPAs—reaching 14% gross in the mid-1990s before stricter norms curbed underreporting—due to persistent political influences on lending, underscoring incomplete autonomy despite reforms. Overall, these changes supported credit growth averaging 15-20% annually in the 1990s-2000s, integrating Indian banks into global standards while exposing vulnerabilities in state-owned entities.[39][40]

Legacy and Impact

Economic Achievements

Maidavolu Narasimham's most significant economic achievements stemmed from his chairmanship of the 1991 and 1998 committees on financial sector reforms, which provided the blueprint for liberalizing India's heavily regulated banking system amid the 1991 economic crisis. The first committee recommended reducing the statutory liquidity ratio (SLR) from 38.5% to 25% in phases, deregulating interest rates, abolishing branch licensing, and allowing entry of private sector and foreign banks to foster competition and efficiency.[3] These measures, partially implemented, shifted banking from government-directed lending to market-oriented operations, enabling public sector banks to prioritize profitability over social objectives and facilitating the emergence of new private banks like ICICI and HDFC Bank. The second committee built on this by advocating consolidation of public sector banks into fewer, stronger entities and reducing government stake to 33%, alongside stricter norms for non-performing assets (NPAs) and capital adequacy aligned with Basel standards.[3] Implementation contributed to improved bank productivity, with private banks outperforming public ones in efficiency post-1991, as competition drove technological adoption and risk management.[41] The reforms supported broader economic liberalization, aiding India's transition from a balance-of-payments crisis to sustained GDP growth averaging over 6% annually in the subsequent decades, by channeling credit more effectively to productive sectors.[42] Earlier, as RBI deputy governor and banking secretary, Narasimham spearheaded the establishment of regional rural banks in 1975 to extend credit to underserved areas, increasing rural banking penetration, and helped found the National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development (NABARD) in 1982 to coordinate agricultural financing.[3] During his brief tenure as RBI Governor from May 1977 to November 1977, he navigated inflationary pressures, though his lasting impact lay in institutional reforms that enhanced financial inclusion and stability.[3] Overall, these efforts modernized India's financial infrastructure, reducing public sector dominance from 90% of assets in 1991 toward a more diversified system.[43]

Criticisms and Controversies

The Narasimham Committee reports faced significant opposition from bank employee unions, who protested against recommendations for greater operational autonomy, reduced government intervention, and potential privatization of public sector banks, viewing them as threats to job security and the social objectives of banking. The United Forum of Bank Unions (UFBU), representing approximately 1.3 million employees, organized agitations in the late 1990s, including plans for strikes and disruptions to banking services in response to the 1998 report's emphasis on restructuring weak banks and strengthening market discipline.[44][23] Critics of the reforms argued that the committees prioritized efficiency, profitability, and capital adequacy norms aligned with Basel standards over financial inclusion and priority sector lending to agriculture and small industries, potentially exacerbating rural credit gaps and income disparities in a developing economy. Some analysts contended that the shift toward market-driven interest rates and reduced directed credit undermined the developmental role of public sector banks, though empirical data post-reforms showed persistent dominance of public banks in priority lending while NPAs reflected legacy issues from pre-reform political lending rather than liberalization itself.[23] During Narasimham's brief tenure as RBI Governor from May to November 1977, his monetary policy decision in May 1977 to maintain unchanged interest rates drew criticism from economists I.G. Patel and Sukhamoy Chakravarty, who advocated for rate cuts to stimulate investment amid economic slowdown concerns following the Emergency period.[16]

Published Works

Major Publications

M. Narasimham authored books focusing on global economics, Indian financial reforms, and personal reflections on public service. His 1988 publication World Economic Environment and Prospects for India, published by Sterling Publishers, examines international economic conditions and their potential impacts on India's growth trajectory amid evolving trade and monetary policies.[45][46] In 2002, Narasimham released Economic Reforms: Development and Finance, a UBS Publishers' Distributors title that addresses the interplay between liberalization measures, developmental goals, and financial sector evolution in post-1991 India.[47][48] That same year, he published From Reserve Bank to Finance Ministry and Beyond: Some Reminiscences, offering firsthand accounts of his tenure at the Reserve Bank of India, including executive directorship and governorship from 1977 to 1978, alongside insights into fiscal policy formulation during key transitional periods.[49][46]

Death

M. Narasimham died on 20 April 2021 in Hyderabad, Telangana, at the age of 94, from complications related to COVID-19.[50][5] He had been receiving treatment at a hospital for the illness prior to his death.[51][17]
