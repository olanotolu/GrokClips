Sarah Marshall (writer)

Sarah Marshall is an American writer, journalist, and podcaster recognized for her efforts to reassess popular misconceptions about historical events, cultural figures, and media narratives.[1] She co-created and hosts the podcast You're Wrong About, launched in 2018 with Michael Hobbes, which examines topics miscast in the public imagination, such as moral panics, the myths of serial killers' intellect, and the recurring trope of maligned women in news coverage.[2][3] The series has gained prominence for its revisionist approach to modern history and pop culture phenomena, earning features in outlets including The New Yorker, The Guardian, and Time.[2] Marshall's freelance writing, which probes similar themes of collective memory and narrative distortion, has appeared in The Believer, BuzzFeed, and the true crime anthology Unspeakable Acts.[1] Her work emphasizes empirical reevaluation over entrenched storytelling, often highlighting causal factors overlooked in initial accounts, such as socioeconomic contexts in cases like the Tonya Harding scandal.[4]

Early life

Upbringing and education

Sarah Marshall grew up in Portland, Oregon, and Honolulu, Hawaii.[5] She also spent time in rural areas of Oregon during her childhood.[6] Marshall earned a Master of Fine Arts degree in fiction from Portland State University, where she subsequently taught creative writing.[6][5]

Professional career

Early writing and journalism

Marshall earned a Master of Fine Arts degree in fiction from Portland State University, after which she began teaching writing as an adjunct instructor there while developing her nonfiction portfolio.[7][8] Her early journalism centered on long-form cultural criticism, particularly re-examinations of true crime cases and media-driven scandals involving marginalized or vilified figures, often highlighting class dynamics and narrative distortions.[5] A pivotal early publication was her January 2014 essay "Remote Control" in The Believer, an 11,000-word analysis of the 1994 Tonya Harding-Nancy Kerrigan scandal that critiqued media portrayals of Harding as a symbol of white trash excess and argued her punishment reflected broader societal discomfort with upward mobility from lower classes.[9] The piece drew on primary sources including court documents and contemporary news coverage to challenge the dominant narrative of Harding as inherently villainous.[9] It was subsequently selected for inclusion in The Best American Nonrequired Reading 2015, edited by Cheryl Strayed.[10] Marshall's freelance output soon expanded to digital and print outlets, including contributions to BuzzFeed Readerâ€”such as her February 2017 feature tracing Anna Nicole Smith's trajectory from tabloid icon to punchline, attributing her downfall to a collision of American dream mythology and punitive cultural attitudes toward female ambitionâ€”and The New Republic, where she published pieces like the April 2016 essay on political alienation in media representations.[11][12] She also wrote for The Baffler, with essays on topics like the 1992 Newsies strike adaptation (January 2018) and Disney's cultural influence, building a reputation for meticulous research into pop culture's undercurrents.[13] These works, often exceeding 5,000 words, emphasized empirical reevaluation over sensationalism, predating her pivot to podcasting.[2]

Development of podcasting

Sarah Marshall entered podcasting through a collaboration initiated by journalist Michael Hobbes, who contacted her in 2016 after reading her 2014 essay on figure skater Tonya Harding published in The Believer.[14][4] Hobbes, then working at HuffPost, proposed developing a podcast in early 2018 to explore misunderstood historical narratives, drawing on Marshall's expertise in reexamining cultural myths; she agreed, marking her shift from print journalism to audio storytelling.[14] The podcast, titled You're Wrong About, launched on May 14, 2018, with its inaugural episode on the Satanic Panic of the 1980s and 1990s, a topic aligning with Marshall's interest in moral panics and media distortions.[14] Co-hosted by Marshall and Hobbes, the biweekly series adopted a format of deep-dive discussions with guests, emphasizing primary sources, archival material, and contrarian analysis of popularly held misconceptions about events, figures, and phenomena.[15] This structure allowed Marshall to expand her written research into conversational, narrative-driven episodes, often spanning 90 minutes or more, which facilitated nuanced explorations beyond the constraints of magazine articles.[4] By May 2019, the podcast's audience growth prompted the launch of a Patreon campaign to fund production, reflecting listener support for its revisionist approach to topics like true crime and public scandals.[14] Marshall's role evolved to include solo research and hosting duties on select episodes, honing a style that prioritized empirical reevaluation over sensationalism, though the collaborative dynamic with Hobbes shaped its early investigative rigor until his departure in 2021.[15] This period established podcasting as a core medium for Marshall, enabling her to reach broader audiences while maintaining a focus on causal analysis of historical misinterpretations.[2]

Major works and projects

You're Wrong About podcast

"You're Wrong About" is a history and pop culture podcast co-created by writer Sarah Marshall and journalist Michael Hobbes, which debuted on May 2, 2018, with its inaugural episode examining the Satanic Panic of the 1980s and 1990s.[16][17] The series focuses on re-evaluating events, individuals, or phenomena that have been distorted or oversimplified in collective memory, drawing on archival materials, interviews, and primary accounts to challenge dominant interpretations.[18][19] Initially co-hosted by Marshall and Hobbes, the podcast featured conversational dissections of topics ranging from true crime scandals to media-driven moral panics, emphasizing contextual factors often overlooked in initial reporting.[20] Hobbes departed after three years in October 2021, citing a desire to pursue other projects, after which Marshall assumed the role of primary host, incorporating rotating guests such as journalists and historians for collaborative analysis.[20][21] Episodes typically run 60 to 90 minutes and are released weekly or bi-weekly, available across platforms including Apple Podcasts, Spotify, and the show's Buzzsprout feed, with over 200 installments produced by 2023.[19][22] The production remains independent, funded through listener support and merchandise sales, maintaining a format that prioritizes narrative reconstruction over sensationalism.[18]

Coverage of Tonya Harding

Sarah Marshall first examined Tonya Harding's story in her January 2014 essay "Remote Control," published in The Believer magazine.[9] The piece details Harding's working-class upbringing in Clackamas County, Oregon, where she began skating at age three at a mall rink, became the first American woman to land a triple axel in competition in 1991, and endured physical and emotional abuse from her mother and husband Jeff Gillooly.[9] Marshall recounts the 1994 scandal, including the January 6 baton attack on rival Nancy Kerrigan that sidelined her from the U.S. Championships, which Harding subsequently won, and Harding's later implication through Gillooly and bodyguard Shawn Eckhardt.[9] Convicted only of failing to report the plotâ€”claiming fear of Gillooly prevented disclosureâ€”Harding received a lifetime ban from the U.S. Figure Skating Association.[9] Marshall argues that media coverage amplified Harding's "white trash" imageâ€”highlighting her multiple residences, rifle ownership, and unpolished demeanorâ€”in stark contrast to Kerrigan's poised elegance, prioritizing sensationalism over nuance.[9] She posits Harding's account of ignorance regarding the attack as plausible, given documented abuse and the high-stakes pressures of elite skating, framing Harding as victimized by her family, spouse, and a sport intolerant of her athleticism and nonconformity.[9] In December 2017, Marshall revisited Harding in "Making an Ice Queen," an essay in The Baffler responding to the film I, Tonya.[23] She praises the movie's humanization of Harding, emphasizing her resilienceâ€”evident in her triple axel achievement and eighth-place Olympic finish in 1994 amid the scandalâ€”while critiquing 1990s media for caricaturing her as villainous to boost ratings, dismissing evidence of Gillooly's abuse documented in police reports.[23] Marshall highlights societal disdain for Harding's lower-class origins and rejection of figure skating's emphasis on ethereal grace over raw power, noting the film's invented post-ban plea scene as emblematic of her enduring sympathy.[23] Marshall extended her analysis in July 2019 through two episodes of the You're Wrong About podcast, which she co-hosts.[24] Part 1 covers Harding's early career, including her job at a mall potato restaurant and experiences of physical, emotional, and sexual abuse, positioning her rise in a sport dominated by privilege as inherently fraught.[24] Part 2 dissects the Kerrigan assault as a "low-rent conspiracy" that fueled media profits, causing disproportionate harm to participants while questioning the narrative's rush to vilify Harding.[25] In a 2022 TIME interview, Marshall described her longstanding fascination with Harding, rooted in shared Oregon roots and early exposure to tabloid media, which she credits for inspiring her 2014 essay as a defense against class-based scapegoating in a wealth-disparate sport.[26] She maintains that public condemnation punished Harding for poverty, endured abuse, and unladylike athleticism rather than definitively proving her orchestration of the attack.[26]

Explorations of the Satanic Panic

Sarah Marshall examined the Satanic Panic in a May 2, 2018, episode of the You're Wrong About podcast, co-hosted with Michael Hobbes, framing the 1980s phenomenon as driven by societal fears rather than verifiable evidence of organized satanic cults.[27] In the episode, she detailed how moral anxieties over child abuse, heavy metal music, and Dungeons & Dragons fueled widespread accusations of ritual abuse, often lacking physical evidence or independent corroboration, leading to high-profile cases like the McMartin preschool trial that ultimately collapsed under scrutiny.[28] Building on this, Marshall launched The Devil You Know, an eight-part anthology podcast series in October 2025, produced by CBC Podcasts, which focuses on firsthand accounts from individuals entangled in the Satanic Panic, including accusers, skeptics, bystanders, and those convicted on dubious testimony.[29] The series traces the panic's origins in the early 1980s, when claims of satanic infiltration in daycares and communities proliferated amid recovered-memory therapies later criticized for suggestibility, emphasizing how cultural vectors like talk shows amplified unverified narratives without empirical validation.[30] Marshall's approach in both projects underscores causal factors such as evangelical influences and media sensationalism, which investigations like the FBI's 1992 report found unsupported claims of nationwide satanic networks, attributing many allegations to confabulation rather than conspiracy.[31] She has extended these explorations in interviews, such as a 2021 Vox discussion linking recurrent U.S. satanic fears to patterns of scapegoating during social upheaval, distinct from isolated abuse cases that do not indicate ritualistic organization.[32] Originally considering a book on the topic, Marshall shifted to the podcast format to prioritize narrative depth from primary voices, avoiding synthesis until sufficient archival material could counterbalance biased contemporary sources like 1980s evangelist literature prone to exaggeration.[33] Her work highlights wrongful convictions, such as those overturned in the 1990s after forensic reexaminations revealed coerced testimonies, positioning the panic as a cautionary example of collective delusion over substantiated threat.[34]

Reception and impact

Achievements and awards

Marshall's creation and hosting of the podcast You're Wrong About earned it the Podcast of the Year award at the 2022 iHeartRadio Podcast Awards, where it prevailed over entries from major media outlets as an independent production.[35][36] In 2023, the podcast won Best History Podcast at the iHeartPodcast Awards, with Marshall recognized directly as host during the ceremony.[37] The series has received multiple nominations from the iHeartRadio Podcast Awards, including for Best Overall Host, Best Pop Culture Podcast, and Best Female Host in 2022.[38] It has also been featured in TIME magazine's list of the 100 Best Podcasts.[39] Marshall's nonfiction essays have been published in The Believer, The New Republic, and BuzzFeed, with selections anthologized in The Best American Nonrequired Reading 2015.[40] Her work contributed to the true crime anthology Unspeakable Acts.[2]

Criticisms and debates

Marshall's revisionist examinations of historical figures and events, particularly in You're Wrong About, have prompted debates over the balance between contextual empathy and accountability. Critics argue that the podcast's approach to humanizing subjects accused of serious misconduct risks excusing harmful actions by prioritizing systemic factors over individual agency. For instance, blogger Kristen Patterson contends that episodes on topics like sex offenders advocate for humane treatment in ways that may trivialize victims' trauma, potentially fostering "himpathy"â€”disproportionate sympathy for male perpetratorsâ€”as conceptualized by philosopher Kate Manne.[41] In her coverage of Tonya Harding, Marshall's 2014 essay "Remote Control" in The Believer and related podcast episodes frame Harding as a victim of class-based media prejudice and familial abuse, highlighting how her 1991 triple axel achievement and 1994 U.S. championship were overshadowed by narratives emphasizing her "trashy" persona.[9] This perspective, which posits Harding's 1994 guilty plea to conspiracy charges in the Nancy Kerrigan attack as a coerced admission amid biased scrutiny, has been controversial. Detractors, including analyses in outlets like Deadspin, view it as apologism that downplays Harding's documented ties to the assailants and her failure to report the plot promptly, despite her claims of ignorance.[42][23] Debates around Marshall's treatment of the Satanic Panic, explored in podcast arcs and her forthcoming book, center on the extent to which skepticism of 1980s-1990s mass abuse claims acknowledges real child victimization. Marshall emphasizes discredited elements like recovered memory therapy and the 1984-1990 McMartin preschool case, where seven years of investigation yielded no convictions despite allegations of ritual abuse involving hundreds of children.[24] While this aligns with FBI assessments finding no evidence of organized satanic networks, some responses, including the podcast's own disclaimer defying accusations of "loving pedophiles," reflect backlash from those who perceive such narratives as overly dismissive of corroborated abuse instances.[41] These critiques often stem from broader cultural tensions over true-crime revisionism, though Marshall maintains her intent is to correct media distortions without endorsing misconduct.

Recent activities

Projects from 2023 onward

In 2023, Sarah Marshall continued hosting You're Wrong About, transitioning to a guest-hosted format following the departure of co-host Michael Hobbes in 2022, with episodes re-examining historical misconceptions through interviews and analysis.[43] The podcast released multiple seasons, including coverage of topics such as the O.J. Simpson trial and 1990s cultural phenomena, maintaining its focus on revising public narratives with primary sources and expert commentary.[19] By 2025, it had amassed over 250 episodes, earning recognition as one of TIME's 100 best podcasts for its rigorous historical reconsiderations.[39] Parallel to this, Marshall co-hosted You Are Good (previously Why Are Dads?) with Alex Steed, a series exploring pop culture through the lens of familial dynamics and personal growth, with episodes continuing into 2025.[18] This podcast, which delves into themes like fatherhood and media representations, released ongoing installments analyzing films and books to unpack emotional and cultural insights.[44] In October 2025, Marshall launched The Devil You Know, an eight-part limited series produced by CBC Podcasts, examining the 1980s and 1990s Satanic Panic through survivor accounts, journalistic investigations, and cultural analysis.[29] The series, which Marshall developed after abandoning plans for a related book, features episodes on moral panics, alleged ritual abuse claims, and their societal impacts, drawing on archival materials and interviews to critique the era's hysteria.[45] Initial episodes, including discussions of key figures and "moral entrepreneurship," were released starting October 7, 2025, positioning the project as a standalone extension of Marshall's prior explorations of similar themes.[46]
