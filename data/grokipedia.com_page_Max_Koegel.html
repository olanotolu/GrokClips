Max Koegel

![Memorial related to Max Koegel at Ravensbrück]float-right Otto Max Koegel (1895–1946) was a Nazi SS officer and concentration camp commandant responsible for the oversight of prisoner abuse and mass deaths at several facilities during World War II.[1] Orphaned early and a World War I veteran, he joined the SS in 1932 and progressed through roles at camps including Columbia-Haus, Lichtenburg, and Sachsenhausen before assuming command of Ravensbrück women's concentration camp from January 1940 to August 1942, where conditions involved severe overcrowding, forced labor, and the onset of medical experiments.[1][2] He subsequently led Majdanek from late 1942 and Flossenbürg starting in May 1943, permitting widespread killings and exploitation in both.[3][1] Captured by U.S. forces in 1945, Koegel evaded initial prosecution by going underground but committed suicide in prison on June 27, 1946, prior to facing war crimes charges.[4]

Early Life

Family Background and Childhood

Otto Max Koegel was born on 16 October 1895 in Füssen, Kingdom of Bavaria, then part of the German Empire.[5][6] He was the fourth son of a carpenter who worked at a local furniture factory in Füssen.[7] His mother died from complications during childbirth shortly before Koegel's sixth birthday in 1901, leaving him orphaned and raised thereafter by relatives.[5] As a youth in the rural Bavarian Alps, Koegel earned his living through manual labor suited to the region's terrain, working as an Almhirte (alpine herdsman) and later as a mountain guide.[5] These early occupations reflected the modest socioeconomic circumstances of his family background, with no formal higher education recorded prior to his military service.[8] His upbringing in this environment, marked by early independence and physical hardship following his mother's death, shaped a pattern of self-reliance that persisted into adulthood.

World War I Service

Koegel volunteered for military service in a Bavarian infantry unit at the outset of World War I in August 1914.[7] Assigned to the Western Front, he participated in intense combat operations, sustaining multiple wounds over the course of the conflict.[7] One such injury occurred during the Battle of Verdun in 1916, a prolonged engagement that resulted in over 700,000 combined casualties for German and French forces.[7] [9] For his frontline service, Koegel received the Iron Cross, Second Class, a decoration awarded to recognize acts of bravery or merit in combat.[9] Accounts from personnel records indicate he was wounded a total of three times, reflecting the high risks faced by infantrymen in trench warfare.[9] His wartime experiences, including exposure to the brutal conditions of battles like Verdun and potentially the Somme, preceded his post-war struggles with unemployment and eventual turn toward radical politics.

Entry into Nazism

Joining the Nazi Party and SS

Koegel entered the Nazi movement in 1932 by joining the Nationalsozialistische Deutsche Arbeiterpartei (NSDAP), the Sturmabteilung (SA), and the Schutzstaffel (SS).[1] In his hometown of Füssen, he established the locality's inaugural SS unit, thereby initiating organized SS presence there.[1] These affiliations positioned him within the paramilitary and ideological core of the early Nazi apparatus, preceding his subsequent employment at Dachau concentration camp in April 1933.[1] [10]

Pre-War Activities in Füssen and SA

In 1932, Otto Max Koegel, born in Füssen, Bavaria, joined the National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP), the Sturmabteilung (SA), and the Schutzstaffel (SS).[1] Residing in his hometown, he founded the first SS unit in Füssen, aiding the early establishment of Nazi paramilitary structures in the rural Allgäu district amid the party's rise to power.[1] Koegel's SA involvement in Füssen centered on local organizational efforts, including recruitment and propagation of Nazi ideology through paramilitary drills and public demonstrations, consistent with the SA's role as the party's vanguard in provincial areas during the Weimar Republic's final years.[1] These activities aligned with the broader SA expansion in Bavaria, where brownshirt units enforced party discipline and intimidated political opponents prior to the Nazi seizure of power in 1933.[11] By early 1933, following the Enabling Act, Koegel transitioned from these local SA and nascent SS duties to employment at Dachau concentration camp as an administrative guard.[1]

SS Career and Concentration Camp Roles

Early Positions at Dachau

In 1937, Otto Max Koegel was assigned to Dachau concentration camp, the first facility established by the Nazi regime for the indefinite detention of political opponents and other targeted groups, where he served as adjutant to commandant Hans Loritz.[10][12] This position marked Koegel's entry into concentration camp administration within the SS-Totenkopfverbände, the guard units under SS inspector Theodor Eicke. As adjutant, Koegel assisted Loritz in managing daily operations, including personnel assignments for guards, enforcement of camp discipline, and coordination of prisoner labor details, amid a prisoner population exceeding 2,000 by mid-year, primarily consisting of communists, socialists, and other perceived enemies of the state.[10] Koegel's tenure at Dachau emphasized rigorous adherence to the camp's punitive regime, which included floggings, solitary confinement, and standing cells for infractions, as standardized by Eicke's regulations. No specific incidents directly attributed to Koegel during this period are documented in primary accounts, but his role supported the expansion of SS control over camp functions, preparing personnel for broader network deployment. By late 1937 or early 1938, Koegel departed Dachau for assignment to the Lichtenburg camp, transitioning from supportive administrative duties to more direct leadership responsibilities elsewhere.[10]

Command at Ravensbrück

Otto Max Koegel served as commandant of Ravensbrück concentration camp from January 1, 1940, to August 20, 1942.[2] He succeeded Günther Tamaschke, the initial commandant appointed in December 1938, shortly after the camp's construction began in November 1938.[13] Under Koegel's leadership as SS-Hauptsturmführer, the camp, located near Fürstenberg in northern Germany, primarily held women prisoners, with numbers growing from approximately 900 in May 1939 to around 10,000 by the end of 1942.[2] Koegel oversaw the expansion of forced labor operations, where prisoners worked in tailors' workshops producing uniforms and other items, initially under relatively less demanding indoor conditions that contributed to higher early survival rates compared to later years.[14] However, overcrowding intensified by June 1941, with about 5,000 prisoners, straining resources and leading to deteriorating conditions including poor sanitation and insufficient food rations after 1941.[14] [2] Koegel implemented strict disciplinary measures, establishing a punishment block for infractions such as repeated lateness or unkempt bedding, where women faced severe repercussions.[15] During his tenure, selections began for the elimination of weak or injured prisoners; in spring 1942, approximately 1,600 women and 300 men were transferred to Bernburg for gassing under the "euthanasia" program.[2] Medical experiments on prisoners commenced in summer 1942, targeting around 80 women, predominantly Polish, involving procedures that resulted in deaths and permanent injuries, though Koegel departed before their full escalation.[2] Additionally, in 1942, brothels were established within the camp system, coercing about 100 women into sexual labor under promises of release that were not honored.[2] Koegel's command preceded the camp's most intense phase of extermination, but these policies laid groundwork for subsequent atrocities.[14]

Command at Majdanek

SS-Obersturmbannführer Max Koegel was appointed commandant of the Majdanek concentration camp (KL Lublin) on August 8, 1942, succeeding SS-Standartenführer Karl Otto Koch, who had been arrested and removed due to charges of corruption, embezzlement, and unauthorized killings.[16][3] His tenure lasted approximately one month, ending in September 1942, though some accounts extend it to November 1942 before replacement by SS-Obersturmbannführer Hermann Florstedt.[16][3] In this role, Koegel held ultimate authority over camp administration and security, directing a structure divided into six departments: the commandant's office, political department, prisoner camp department, camp administration, sanitary department, and educational-propaganda department.[16] The garrison comprised around 1,000 personnel, including German SS men, Volksdeutsche auxiliaries, and non-German guards such as Belarusians, Ukrainians, Russians (many former Soviet POWs serving as Hiwis), and members of a Lithuanian police battalion.[16] Oversight of the women's subcamp fell to approximately 28 female overseers under Chief Overseer Else Ehrich, who reported directly to Koegel.[16] During this brief period, the camp continued operations involving prisoner intake, forced labor, and executions, consistent with its role in the broader SS system under Operation Reinhard, though no unique initiatives or major documented actions are specifically attributed to Koegel's direct orders.[3]

Command at Flossenbürg

Max Koegel was appointed commandant of Flossenbürg concentration camp in April 1943, succeeding Egon Zill, and held the position until the camp's evacuation in April 1945.[17] Under his leadership, the camp system expanded significantly to support armaments production, particularly for Messerschmitt aircraft components and other war materiel, with the main camp population growing from approximately 3,000 prisoners in early 1943 to over 30,000 by early 1945, including numerous subcamps.[17] Koegel, holding the rank of SS-Obersturmbannführer, oversaw operations that prioritized forced labor extraction, resulting in severe overcrowding, malnutrition, disease outbreaks, and high mortality rates, with an estimated 30,000 prisoners dying at Flossenbürg and its subcamps during the war's final years.[1] Koegel's command permitted widespread prisoner abuse, including beatings, arbitrary executions, and killings by guards and kapos, consistent with his prior reputation for brutality at camps like Ravensbrück and Majdanek.[1] Conditions deteriorated amid Allied bombings and resource shortages, exacerbating forced marches within the camp complex and medical experiments at subcamps such as Oberstaubling.[17] Despite his harsh internal regime, Koegel maintained a facade of civility with local civilians, earning a reputation for friendliness outside the barbed wire, which masked the camp's internal horrors.[1] As Soviet and Western Allied forces advanced in early 1945, Koegel ordered the evacuation of Flossenbürg, initiating death marches of tens of thousands of prisoners toward Dachau and other sites between April 14 and 20, during which guards shot stragglers and thousands perished from exhaustion, exposure, and starvation.[17] Koegel fled the camp ahead of U.S. liberation on April 23, 1945, going underground before his eventual arrest in 1946.[1] His tenure contributed to the camp's role in the SS's exploitative labor network, with verifiable records showing relentless demands for output that directly caused mass fatalities through overwork and neglect.[17]

Capture and Death

Allied Arrest

Following the liberation of Flossenbürg concentration camp by elements of the United States 90th and 97th Infantry Divisions on April 23, 1945, Koegel, who had assumed command there in February 1945, fled the area and evaded immediate apprehension by Allied forces.[17] He remained at large for over a year, living in hiding within Bavaria amid the Allied occupation.[18] On June 26, 1946, Koegel was located and arrested by United States Army counterintelligence personnel in Schwabach, a town near Nuremberg in southern Germany.[19][20] The capture followed tips from local informants and involved American military investigators who had been pursuing high-ranking SS fugitives; Koegel was identified through documents and physical description matching camp records. He was detained pending transfer for war crimes interrogation and potential trial.[7]

Suicide and Immediate Aftermath

On 26 June 1946, Koegel was arrested by United States Army forces in Schwabach, near Nuremberg, Bavaria, after being identified as a wanted war criminal due to his command roles in multiple concentration camps.[10][21] The next day, 27 June 1946, Koegel committed suicide by hanging himself in his prison cell, thereby evading prosecution for atrocities committed under his oversight at Ravensbrück, Majdanek, and Flossenbürg, where thousands of prisoners perished from executions, starvation, disease, and forced labor.[10][22] His death occurred prior to any formal indictment or trial, such as those conducted at Dachau for Flossenbürg personnel, leaving survivor testimonies and camp records as primary evidence of his responsibility for systemic brutality, including the implementation of harsh disciplinary measures and oversight of medical experiments at Ravensbrück. No public inquest or further official proceedings followed, and details on the disposition of his remains remain undocumented in available records.[1]
