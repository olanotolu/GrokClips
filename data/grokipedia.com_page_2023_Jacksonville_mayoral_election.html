2023 Jacksonville mayoral election

The 2023 Jacksonville mayoral election was a nonpartisan contest to select the successor to term-limited incumbent Republican mayor Lenny Curry, consisting of a general election on March 21, 2023, and a required runoff on May 16, 2023, since no candidate secured a majority in the first round.[1][2] In the general election, Democrat Donna Deegan, a former television news anchor who survived breast cancer and founded a nonprofit focused on cancer care, topped the ballot with 66,192 votes or 39.43% of the total, advancing to the runoff alongside Republican Daniel Davis, a former Jacksonville City Council president and chief executive of the JAX Chamber of Commerce, who received 41,505 votes or 24.72%.[1][3][4] Deegan defeated Davis in the runoff, earning 113,226 votes to his 104,172 for a 52.08% to 47.92% victory amid 218,028 total ballots cast and 33.07% turnout among 659,251 registered voters in Duval County, thereby becoming the first woman elected mayor of Jacksonville and flipping the office from Republican to Democratic control.[2] The race centered on priorities including public safety, pension reform, and infrastructure in Florida's largest city by population, reflecting competitive partisan dynamics in a county that supported Republican presidential candidates in 2016 and 2020.[1][2]

Background

Political landscape

The Jacksonville mayoral election operates under a nonpartisan format, where all candidates appear on a single primary ballot regardless of party affiliation, and a candidate must secure more than 50% of the vote to win outright; otherwise, the top two advance to a runoff election. In 2023, the primary was held on March 21, with the runoff on May 16, as no candidate achieved a majority in the initial round featuring seven contenders.[5][6] Duval County, which comprises Jacksonville, demonstrated partisan competitiveness amid broader Republican gains in Florida. In the 2022 gubernatorial election, Republican incumbent Ron DeSantis captured 58.9% of the vote in Duval County, defeating Democrat Charlie Crist by a margin of nearly 18 points and signaling strong GOP momentum statewide. This contrasted with local patterns, where Duval has been characterized as a purple jurisdiction, with Democratic mayoral control ending a 30-year Republican streak in the 2023 outcome despite the incumbent mayor Lenny Curry's two-term Republican administration.[7][8] Primary election turnout stood at approximately 25.7%, with 168,263 ballots cast among 655,403 registered voters, a level that often amplifies the influence of ideologically committed voters over casual participants. Republicans dominated early spending, directing over 90% of the $8.7 million in tracked advertising toward their candidates in the primary phase, yet the race's resultâ€”marked by a Democratic victory in the runoffâ€”highlighted underperformance relative to state-level Republican strength and expectations of continuity from Curry's tenure.[1][9][10]

Key city issues

Violent crime, particularly homicides, persisted as a major challenge in Jacksonville prior to the 2023 mayoral election, with the city recording 168 total homicides in 2022â€”one of its deadliest years on recordâ€”including 135 murders reported by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office.[11] [12] This contributed to broader violent crime figures of 12,071 incidents that year, fueling discussions on causal factors like gang activity and the relative efficacy of policing enhancements versus social interventions in high-risk urban areas.[13] Economic conditions highlighted disparities, with a 15% citywide poverty rate in 2022, disproportionately affecting the urban core where census tracts showed concentrations exceeding 25% and child poverty rates around 26%.[14] [15] Counterbalancing this, the Port of Jacksonville experienced robust growth, handling 1.3 million twenty-foot equivalent units (TEUs) in fiscal year 2023â€”a 7% revenue increaseâ€”and sustaining 138,000 regional jobs with $31 billion in annual economic output, underscoring the logistics sector's role in mitigating broader downturns.[16] [17] Fiscal strains from pension obligations, rooted in 1990s expansions and subsequent reforms, amounted to a $4.1 billion net liability as of September 2023, constraining budgetary flexibility. Infrastructure vulnerabilities, including recurrent tidal and stormwater flooding exacerbated by geography and development, necessitated resilience planning, as detailed in 2023 assessments projecting heightened risks.[18] Negotiations for the Jacksonville Jaguars stadium renovations remained stalled entering the election cycle, spotlighting tensions over potential public costs exceeding $1 billion, debt implications, and the need to secure the franchise amid relocation threats.[19]

Incumbent administration and term limits

Lenny Curry, a Republican, served as mayor of Jacksonville from July 1, 2015, to July 1, 2023, completing two consecutive four-year terms.[20][21] His administration prioritized pension reform, culminating in a 2017 overhaul approved by voters and the Florida Legislature, which shifted new police and firefighter hires to defined contribution plans and committed the city to $350 million in payments over 14 years, projecting $1.4 billion in savings during that period and enabling budget relief for infrastructure and reserves.[22][23] Curry described this as his signature achievement, arguing it extricated the city from unsustainable defined-benefit obligations that had strained finances for decades.[24] Downtown revitalization efforts included public-private partnerships like the 30-mile Emerald Trail connecting neighborhoods to the core, infrastructure grants for Bay Street improvements, and catalytic developments such as Lot J near the sports complex to spur economic growth in underutilized areas.[25][26][27] Despite these initiatives, Curry's tenure faced scrutiny over fiscal sustainability and public safety. The city's pension debt for police and firefighters exceeded $3 billion by 2025, with annual payments surging despite reforms, as longer amortization periods amplified interest costs and risks.[28] Overall municipal debt declined by $560 million over seven years through targeted paydowns and low-interest bond sales, yet critics highlighted vulnerabilities from legacy obligations and rising operational spending.[29][30] On crime, Duval County under Curry maintained Florida's highest murder and violent crime rates among the 20 largest counties, with homicides reaching 170 in 2020â€”the deadliest year in three decadesâ€”and no sustained declines despite programs like Cure Violence implemented in 2019.[31][32] Jacksonville's city charter, amended by voter referendum in 1991, imposes a limit of two consecutive four-year terms on the mayor, barring Curry from seeking re-election and creating an open seat that drew a crowded field of candidates across parties. This term-limit enforcement amplified competition in a city with a stable economyâ€”bolstered by diversified growth and resilience through cyclesâ€”but signs of voter fatigue with prolonged Republican dominance, as evidenced by narrow GOP margins in recent local contests.[33][34] The handover occurred amid fiscal reserves nearing $350 million and debt service savings, yet unresolved pressures like pension burdens and persistent violence shaped perceptions of continuity into the 2023 race.[29]

Candidates and platforms

Republican primary contenders


Daniel Davis, chief executive officer of the Jacksonville Chamber of Commerce and former Florida state representative, entered the Republican primary with extensive experience in local government and economic development.[35] His platform prioritized public safety through adding more police officers to the streets and boosting patrols to address crime, while committing to low taxes and controlled city spending.[35][36] Davis also proposed pension reforms, including potential reinstatement of defined-benefit plans to improve recruitment and retention of law enforcement, amid concerns over fiscal impacts.[37] Supported by business interests and Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters, he advanced from the primary with 24% of the vote.[36][38] LeAnna Cumber, a Jacksonville City Council member for District 5, small business owner, and attorney, campaigned on pragmatic fiscal conservatism and anti-corruption measures.[39] As a descendant of Cuban immigrants, she highlighted her background in public finance consulting to advocate reallocating funds from inefficient projects, such as terminating the $500 million Skyway monorail to save on maintenance costs and reduce taxes.[39] Her economic development agenda focused on infrastructure upgrades like roads and parks to attract biotech and marine industries, leveraging Jacksonville's location for growth.[39] On security, Cumber supported proactive crime prevention, including closing illegal operations and bolstering the Sheriff's Office, while tying long-term reductions to education improvements.[39] Despite significant self-funding, she placed fifth in the primary.[40] Al Ferraro, a Jacksonville City Council member for District 2 serving since 2015, emphasized aggressive action against rising violent crime as a core priority.[41][42] A native Jacksonville resident, Ferraro positioned himself as committed to campaign promises on public safety and local governance, drawing from his council tenure.[41] His platform included support for business-friendly policies, though with limited polling traction among the field.[43]

Democratic nominee

Donna Deegan served as the Democratic nominee in the 2023 Jacksonville mayoral election, marking her entry into elected politics after a career as a television news anchor. A three-time breast cancer survivor diagnosed in 1999, she founded The DONNA Foundation in 2003 to provide financial assistance to underserved breast cancer patients and fund research at institutions like Mayo Clinic.[44][45] Deegan lacked prior elected experience, which she leveraged to present herself as a non-partisan outsider capable of fostering collaboration across divides in Jacksonville's governance.[46] Deegan's platform centered on enhancing health access through initiatives like appointing a Chief Health Officer to oversee public health priorities, drawing from her foundation's focus on holistic support for families facing illness.[47] She proposed addressing poverty via expanded social services targeting root causes such as economic hardship, education gaps, food insecurity, and mental health, arguing these economic levers offer the most effective path to crime reduction.[46] Rather than emphasizing enforcement alone, Deegan advocated "whole-person" strategies incorporating prevention and community-based interventions to tackle crime's underlying drivers.[46] Her moderate-left positions avoided sharp partisan attacks, appealing to independents in a city with a history of Republican mayoral dominance by stressing practical, evidence-based solutions over ideological divides.[48]

Minor and independent candidates

Omega Allen ran as a candidate with no party affiliation (NPA) in the March 21, 2023, primary election. Holding a PhD in public administration from Northcentral University, Allen advocated for participatory municipal government, emphasizing greater community involvement in local decision-making processes.[49][50] Her campaign received 1,584 votes, comprising 0.94% of the total 167,867 votes cast.[1] Darcy G. Richardson qualified as an independent (IND) candidate, listing a platform centered on fiscal austerity and limited government intervention, but garnered negligible support with votes not itemized in official summaries, indicating fewer than reportable thresholds.[50] Brian Griffin also qualified as a write-in (WRI) candidate, though no specific platform details emerged prominently, and write-in votes totaled just 149, or 0.09%.[50][1] These entrants lacked major endorsements from political organizations, labor unions, or business groups, and campaign finance reports showed minimal fundraising, with Allen's efforts relying on personal resources rather than large donations.[51] Their combined vote share remained under 1%, exerting limited influence on the primary dynamics and failing to materially dilute the major party fields, as empirical turnout data reflected voter preference consolidation toward Democratic and Republican frontrunners.[1]

Primary election

Campaign dynamics

The campaign commenced with candidate announcements throughout 2022, including Republican Daniel Davis formally launching his bid on September 8. Qualifying for the nonpartisan ballot occurred from January 3 to 6, 2023, finalizing a field of four Republicans, two Democrats, and one no-party-affiliation candidate.[52][53] Early events included a November 21, 2022, forum hosted by the Jacksonville Rotary Club, where seven candidatesâ€”Democrats Audrey Gibson and Theresa Richardson, Republicans LeAnna Cumber, Al Ferraro, and Frankie Keasler, and no-party candidate Omega Allenâ€”outlined visions for addressing urban challenges. The primary's only televised debate aired on March 8, 2023, at Jacksonville University and moderated by WJXT-TV, featuring all seven candidates in discussions centered on crime reduction, tax policies, and infrastructure priorities.[54][55][56] Republican messaging turned negative amid intra-party competition, with attack advertisements targeting rivals' past records and decisions; for instance, ads criticized LeAnna Cumber's legislative history, prompting Daniel Davis to sue local media outlets on February 8, 2023, alleging defamatory content in broadcasts. This exchange fueled perceptions of GOP division, as resources fragmented between establishment-aligned figures like Davis and fiscal conservatives like Cumber.[57][58][59] Democrat Donna Deegan, unopposed in her party's informal primary, maintained a biography-focused approach, highlighting her experience as a former television news anchor and breast cancer survivor who founded the Donna Foundation charity, while promoting messages of unity and forward-looking governance to appeal beyond partisan lines.[60][61]

Fundraising and expenditures

Republican candidates Daniel Davis and LeAnna Cumber dominated fundraising in the lead-up to the March 21 primary, collectively raising and spending millions through their campaigns and allied political committees, far outpacing Democrat Donna Deegan's efforts.[62] By early 2023, the race had already amassed over $8.7 million in total spending, with Davis and Cumber benefiting from approximately 91% of pro-Republican advertising airings, primarily funneled through super PACs and political committees supporting their bids.[9] Deegan's campaign relied more on grassroots donations and out-of-state Democratic contributions, raising under $2 million in comparable periods, highlighting a structural funding disparity that reflected broader Republican organizational advantages in Florida local races.[63] Expenditures emphasized aggressive advertising and intra-party competition, with Davis's and Cumber's committees burning through over $2 million in January alone on TV ads, direct mail, and ground operations targeting voter turnout in a low-participation election.[62] Super PACs aligned with the Republican contenders, leveraging Florida's permissive campaign finance laws that allow indirect funding flows, dominated independent expenditures, often obscuring donor origins while amplifying attacks on rivals within the GOP primary field.[63] Deegan's spending, by contrast, focused on efficiency with targeted digital outreach and community events, but lacked the volume to match the Republican ad barrage, which comprised the bulk of race-wide media buys.[40] This fundraising imbalance underscored inefficiencies in Republican resource allocation, as Cumber's heavy investmentsâ€”despite leading early pollsâ€”yielded only fifth place, diluting the party's primary consolidation and limiting effective voter mobilization amid turnout below 30%.[40] Public filings with the Florida Division of Elections revealed that while total contributions shattered prior mayoral records, much of the GOP spending fragmented into negative intra-party ads rather than unified outreach, contributing to a split vote that advanced Davis but weakened his position heading into the runoff.[64]

Endorsements

Donna Deegan secured endorsements from key Democratic organizations and labor groups during the primary campaign. The Duval County Democratic Party Executive Committee endorsed her candidacy, emphasizing her focus on public safety and economic development. She also received support from EMILY's List, a national PAC promoting Democratic women candidates, on February 16, 2023, highlighting her pro-choice stance and leadership potential.[65] Additionally, the National Letter Carriers Association Branch 101, representing postal workers in Jacksonville, backed Deegan on February 13, 2023, citing her commitment to working families and community service.[66] Republican endorsements were fragmented across the multiple GOP contenders, reflecting intra-party competition that prevented a consolidated establishment push. Daniel Davis, advancing from the primary, obtained support from business-oriented groups, including the Northeast Florida Builders Association on October 19, 2022, which praised his economic growth agenda and ties to the Jacksonville Chamber of Commerce, where he served as CEO.[67] Other Republicans like Al Ferraro and LeAnna Cumber drew endorsements from conservative factions and local figures aligned with fiscal conservatism, but no single candidate unified major Republican leaders such as term-limited Mayor Lenny Curry prior to the March 21 vote. This division limited the influence of partisan backings, as evidenced by the split Republican vote totaling over 60% but dispersed among three main candidates.[38] Independent and crossover endorsements were minimal in the primary, with Deegan attracting some non-partisan praise for her nonprofit work through the DONNA Foundation, though these did not significantly alter the partisan contours of support. The lack of a cohesive Republican endorsement slate underscored the primary's competitive dynamics, contributing to Deegan's plurality lead despite Jacksonville's Republican-leaning electorate.[68]

Results

In the March 21, 2023, primary election for mayor of Jacksonville, Democrat Donna Deegan led with 66,192 votes, comprising 39.43% of the total votes cast for the office.[1] Republican Daniel Davis placed second with 41,505 votes (24.72%), followed by other candidates including Republican Al Ferraro (27,265 votes, 16.24%) and Democrat Audrey Gibson (14,440 votes, 8.60%).[1] No candidate secured a majority of the 167,867 valid votes for mayor, triggering a runoff between Deegan and Davis as required by Jacksonville's electoral rules.[1] A total of 168,263 ballots were cast citywide, reflecting a turnout of 25.67% among Duval County's 655,403 registered voters.[1] The results were certified without recounts, as margins exceeded thresholds for automatic verification and no challenges were filed.[1] Precinct-level breakdowns revealed geographic divides, with Deegan achieving pluralities exceeding 50% in core urban areas characterized by denser populations and higher Democratic registration, such as downtown and Northside precincts.[1] Davis, conversely, posted his strongest showingsâ€”often over 40%â€”in suburban and exurban precincts, including those in Southside and Westside regions with greater Republican leanings.[1] These patterns aligned with partisan voter distributions in Duval County, where urban cores trended Democratic and outer areas favored Republicans.[1]

Runoff election

Intensified campaigning

Following the March 21, 2023, nonpartisan primary election, campaigning for the May 16 runoff between Democrat Donna Deegan and Republican Daniel Davis escalated, with both candidates launching targeted advertisements and participating in debates focused on public safety and governance experience. Deegan's strategy emphasized unity and her personal biography as a longtime Jacksonville resident, former television news anchor, and founder of the Donna Foundation, a breast cancer nonprofit; her initial post-primary ad, titled "Unity," highlighted policy goals like improved infrastructure and inclusive economic growth while portraying her as a change agent against partisan division.[61] In contrast, Davis, a former state representative and city councilman, stressed his legislative experience and portrayed Deegan as inexperienced in elected office, arguing her outsider status ill-equipped her to handle complex issues like negotiations over the Jaguars stadium.[72] Attack ads proliferated, with the Republican Party of Duval County funding spots accusing Deegan of aligning with "defund the police radicals" by juxtaposing her comments supporting Black Lives Matter protests with footage of 2020 protest violence in Jacksonville.[73] Deegan countered by defending her support for peaceful protests and law enforcement funding while criticizing Davis's associations with outgoing Mayor Lenny Curry's administration as emblematic of entrenched favoritism.[72] Debates, such as the April 20 event at the University of North Florida, sharpened divides on crime, where Davis advocated aggressive enforcement, adding 200 police officers, and rejecting civilian review boardsâ€”citing rising crime in liberal-led cities like San Franciscoâ€”while Deegan proposed balancing enforcement with root-cause interventions like poverty alleviation and literacy programs, alongside support for accountability mechanisms.[72] The overall race, including the runoff phase, became Jacksonville's most expensive mayoral contest, raising approximately $11 million amid heightened ad buys.[74]

Additional endorsements

Following the March 21 primary election, Florida Governor Ron DeSantis endorsed Republican Daniel Davis on March 31, seeking to unify GOP voters against Democrat Donna Deegan.[75] Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters affirmed his support for Davis during the runoff campaign, highlighting potential collaboration on law enforcement priorities such as increasing officer numbers.[76] Third-place primary finisher LeAnna Cumber, a fellow Republican, did not issue a formal endorsement for Davis or Deegan, despite intra-party calls for consolidation; however, analysis of voter shifts indicated substantial movement of Cumber's primary supporters toward Davis in the runoff. Local business interests, including those tied to the JAX Chamber where Davis served as CEO, largely aligned with him but showed restraint in overt post-primary commitments amid the competitive race dynamics. Deegan received backing from former Jacksonville Sheriff Nat Glover, who on May 2 aired advertisements defending her public safety record against Republican attacks, framing them as divisive.[77] National Democratic groups contributed over $1 million in outside spending to bolster Deegan's effort, focusing on voter turnout rather than individual figurehead endorsements. These cross-party and institutional signals had marginal influence, as the race remained within 4 percentage points through the May 16 contest, underscoring endorsements' limited sway in Duval County's purple electorate.[78]

Post-election developments

Transition period

Following her election victory on May 2, 2023, Mayor-elect Donna Deegan formed a transition team to facilitate the handover from outgoing Mayor Lenny Curry's administration. On May 26, 2023, Deegan announced the team's composition, describing it as a diverse group of leaders reflecting the city's values, co-chaired by figures including Sheriff Nat Glover and business leaders Kevin Gay and David Miller.[89][90] On June 6, 2023, Deegan detailed seven transition committees tasked with reviewing key areas such as infrastructure, public safety, health care, economic development, and arts and culture, aiming to identify immediate priorities for the incoming administration.[91][92] Key appointments during this period included an executive team announced on June 14, 2023, featuring bipartisan selections such as former Republican City Council President Anna Brosche as chief of staff and Democratic operative Lakesha Burton in a senior advisory role, alongside the creation of new positions like director of equity and inclusion to promote collaborative governance.[93][94] Additional staff hires, including former council members from both parties, were revealed on June 29, 2023, incorporating experienced local figures to ensure continuity in city operations.[95] Deegan took office on July 1, 2023, inheriting the fiscal framework from the prior administration. She proposed a $1.75 billion general fund budget on July 17, 2023, which the City Council later approved; Deegan signed it into effect on October 2, 2023, without line-item vetoes, allocating over $423 million for capital projects amid ongoing transition reviews.[96][97] The process culminated in a final transition report released on November 16, 2023, summarizing committee recommendations for early administrative actions.[98]

Inauguration and early governance

Donna Deegan was sworn in as the 45th mayor of Jacksonville on July 1, 2023, becoming the city's first female mayor since consolidation in 1968.[99][100] The ceremony, held at the Jacksonville Center for the Performing Arts, featured the theme "A New Day" and emphasized unity and progress as guiding principles for her administration.[101] Deegan took office at noon, succeeding Republican Lenny Curry, and immediately outlined priorities centered on public safety, economic development, and community engagement.[102] Early governance efforts included bolstering law enforcement resources amid ongoing concerns over crime rates. In her proposed $1.7 billion budget for fiscal year 2024, Deegan allocated funding for 40 additional police officers, representing half of the 80 positions requested by Sheriff T.K. Waters to enhance patrol capabilities.[103] By June 2024, she signed a tentative labor agreement with the Fraternal Order of Police, providing double-digit pay raises for officers and corrections staff to improve recruitment and retention.[104] These measures aimed to address staffing shortages without expanding pension benefits initially, though Deegan later offered pension options for new hires as an incentive.[105] On immigration enforcement, Deegan allowed Ordinance 2025-0147-E to take effect without her signature on April 2, 2025, after expressing reservations but not vetoing it.[106][107] The ordinance criminalizes presence of unauthorized aliens in the city, permitting jail terms of up to 60 days and directing local cooperation with federal immigration authorities, thereby reinforcing Jacksonville's non-sanctuary policy despite Democratic leadership.[108] This action countered perceptions of leniency, as the city has historically avoided sanctuary designations and prioritized legal compliance in public safety matters. A University of North Florida poll conducted in May 2024 indicated Deegan's job approval at 63%, with 28% disapproval, reflecting a strong honeymoon phase driven by support from 78% of Democrats and 68% of no-party-affiliation voters, though partisan gaps persisted among Republicans.[109][110]

Policy outcomes and measurable impacts

In public safety, Jacksonville recorded 59 murders in 2024, the lowest total since at least 1995 and less than half the 126 murders in 2023, marking a 50% reduction.[111] Homicide totals reached 82 for the year, a nearly 50% drop from 2023 levels, halting a trend of exceeding 100 annually for over a decade.[112] This decline, sustained into early 2025, has been primarily attributed to intensified Jacksonville Sheriff's Office efforts, including gang unit operations and proactive enforcement, in collaboration with Mayor Deegan's administration, though the mayor has emphasized community prevention alongside policing.[113][114] Economically, JAXPORT cargo activity generated $3.1 billion in business revenue in 2024, supporting 228,100 jobs statewide and $44 billion in annual economic output, with container volumes up amid diversification into new trade lanes.[115][116] Fiscally, a 2025 audit by Florida's Department of Government Efficiency, led by CFO Blaise Ingoglia, claimed Jacksonville overspent by over $200 million across the prior five years, resulting in overtaxation of residents.[117] Affordable housing initiatives under Deegan allocated funds for development and downpayment assistance, though City Council reduced some proposals by millions; concurrent council-approved property tax cuts reduced the millage rate modestly, yielding about $13.5 million less in annual revenue despite Deegan's opposition citing minimal homeowner savings of roughly $1 monthly on average properties.[118][119] In urban development, negotiations culminated in a $1.4 billion EverBank Stadium renovation deal approved by NFL owners in October 2024 and City Council in June 2024, with construction set to begin February 2025 and conclude by August 2028, including a translucent roof and extended lease.[120][121] Homelessness programs yielded a 49% overall drop in the unsheltered population from 566 in 2024 to 290 in 2025 point-in-time counts, driven by housing assistance, shelter referrals, and enforcement under state law compliance, including 198 arrests and transport options like Greyhound tickets since mid-2024.[122][123][124] Sheltered numbers dipped slightly from 779 to 719, reflecting mixed outcomes in permanent housing transitions amid $10 million budgeted for related efforts.[125][126]

Criticisms and ongoing debates

Critics from Republican-led factions, including state officials, have accused Mayor Donna Deegan's administration of fiscal irresponsibility, pointing to specific expenditures like a $75,000 Proto Box projection of the mayor at Jacksonville International Airport as emblematic of waste.[127] [128] Governor Ron DeSantis and Chief Financial Officer Blaise Ingoglia, during a October 1, 2025, press conference, highlighted findings from Florida's Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) audits claiming Jacksonville overspent by approximately $200 million, attributing this to unchecked local tax hikes and inefficient priorities amid rising property taxes.[129] [130] Deegan countered that her $2 billion FY 2025-26 budget was "lean," emphasizing investments such as a 22% pay raise for police and firefighters, while dismissing the hologram as standard airport operations funding and the overspending claims as politically motivated distortions.[131] [132] City Council, dominated by Republicans, has repeatedly clashed with Deegan over budget allocations, stripping millions for initiatives like affordable housing, healthcare expansions, and the $237,300 State of Jax data platform, which Councilman Rory Diamond labeled as veiled diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programming lacking empirical justification.[133] [134] [135] These disputes culminated in a nearly 14-hour September 2024 council session approving a reduced millage rate and tax cut saving about $13 million in revenue, over Deegan's objections that such cuts were performative and undermined public safety funding.[136] [137] Deegan's vetoes of council-backed measures, including a June 2025 immigration ordinance requiring nonprofits to verify legal status for city funding and a $12 million "Cash for Trash" waste incentive program, further underscored gridlock, with override attempts failing 8-7 on the immigration bill.[138] [139] The December 2023 removal of the "Tribute to the Women of the Southern Confederacy" monument from Springfield Park, executed via private donations bypassing council approval, drew rebukes as an abuse of executive power and erasure of historical context, per State Representative Dean Black, prompting a January 2024 council inquiry into potential procedural irregularities.[140] [141] Deegan defended the action as affirming equality without taxpayer cost, framing subsequent council scrutiny as manufactured partisanship.[142] [143] Ongoing debates center on Deegan's mandate following her 52%-48% runoff victory, with GOP figures arguing the slim marginâ€”flipped in a city long Republican-heldâ€”does not authorize progressive policy pivots like equity-focused spending amid fiscal conservatism demands, while supporters view it as repudiation of rigid partisanship, fueling persistent veto battles and stalled initiatives.[68] [144] These tensions reflect broader causal divides: fiscal hawks prioritize verifiable cost savings and tax relief, whereas Deegan's advocates stress data-driven investments in safety and equity to address empirical urban challenges, leaving resolution unresolved as of October 2025.[145]
