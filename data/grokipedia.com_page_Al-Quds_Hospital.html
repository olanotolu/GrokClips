Al-Quds Hospital

Al-Quds Hospital (Arabic: مستشفى القدس) is a major medical facility located in the Tel al-Hawa neighborhood of Gaza City, Palestine, operated by the Palestinian Red Crescent Society (PRCS).[1][2] Established in 2001, it serves as one of the key hospitals in the Gaza Strip, specializing in advanced cardiac services such as open-heart surgery and cardiac catheterization, among only four such centers in the region.[2] The hospital's operations have been pivotal in addressing the healthcare needs of Gaza's population, which faces chronic shortages in medical infrastructure and supplies due to long-term blockade and internal governance issues.[3] However, Al-Quds has encountered significant disruptions from recurrent military engagements, including structural damage, fuel and oxygen supply failures, and temporary shutdowns, as seen in November 2023 when it ceased full operations amid escalating conflict.[4][5] Notable controversies surround allegations by Israeli authorities that the facility has been exploited by Hamas for command and control activities, prompting evacuation orders and strikes, claims contested by PRCS and international humanitarian organizations like the WHO and IFRC, which emphasize its civilian medical role while urging protection under international law.[6][1] Despite these challenges, partial resumption of services occurred in March 2025, with activation of emergency departments and outpatient clinics, underscoring its resilience amid Gaza's strained health system.[5][7]

Background and Establishment

Founding and Location

Al-Quds Hospital was established in 2001 by the Palestinian Red Crescent Society (PRCS) as a key medical facility in the Gaza Strip.[8][2] It serves as the second-largest hospital in Gaza, providing essential healthcare services to the local population.[8] The hospital is situated in the Tel al-Hawa neighborhood, an affluent area southwest of central Gaza City, approximately one mile from the Mediterranean coast.[8] This location positions it within a residential zone, facilitating access for residents while exposing it to urban conflict dynamics in subsequent years.[9] The facility operates under PRCS affiliation, emphasizing emergency and general medical care in a region with limited healthcare infrastructure.[2]

Initial Development and Capacity

The Al-Quds Hospital was established in 2001 by the Palestine Red Crescent Society in the Tel al-Hawa neighborhood of Gaza City, serving as a key medical facility in the territory's healthcare network.[2][10] This development occurred amid the Second Intifada, when the Palestinian Ministry of Health, in coordination with international support, prioritized expanding hospital infrastructure to address conflict-related disruptions, including blockades isolating communities.[11] Initially, the hospital was equipped with 60 beds to provide essential services such as emergency care, surgery, and treatment for populations in southern and western Gaza areas cut off by military actions.[11] Designed as a general referral hospital, it focused on inpatient and outpatient care for trauma and chronic conditions, with infrastructure built to support scalability during crises, though operational constraints from ongoing hostilities limited early full utilization.[2] Staffing included physicians, nurses, and support personnel drawn from the Palestine Red Crescent Society's network, emphasizing self-sufficiency in a resource-scarce environment.[12]

Pre-2023 Operations and Incidents

Services Provided

Al-Quds Hospital, operated by the Palestine Red Crescent Society (PRCS) in Gaza City, served as a key facility offering primary, secondary, and tertiary healthcare services prior to 2023.[13] It maintained a capacity of approximately 120 beds, expandable to 200 during emergencies, with 13 intensive care unit (ICU) beds and 6 operating theaters supporting inpatient admissions, outpatient consultations, and elective surgeries.[13][2] Core services encompassed emergency care through its accident and emergency department, handling trauma stabilization and triage, alongside specialized departments in cardiology, orthopedics, and general surgery.[13] Diagnostic capabilities included medical imaging with one MRI scanner, one CT scanner, one mammography unit, one panoramic X-ray, one extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL) unit, one fluoroscopy machine, and one standard X-ray device.[13] Critical care was provided via a dedicated ICU and neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) for high-risk patients and newborns.[13] The hospital also coordinated patient referrals for advanced treatments unavailable locally, such as transfers to facilities in the West Bank, and managed minor injuries and psychological first aid during escalations, as evidenced by its response to 60 cases in May 2021.[13] By July 2009, following the 2008-2009 Gaza conflict, it had fully resumed operations, including outpatient departments and inpatient services, despite prior structural damage requiring only minor repairs.[14]

2008-2009 Gaza Conflict Involvement

Al-Quds Hospital, operated by the Palestine Red Crescent Society and located in Gaza City's Tel al-Hawa neighborhood, functioned as a key medical facility during the early stages of Israel's Operation Cast Lead, which began on December 27, 2008, treating casualties from the ongoing airstrikes and ground operations amid severe shortages of fuel, medical supplies, and electricity.[3] The hospital, Gaza's second-largest at the time with a capacity of around 200 beds, absorbed patients evacuated from other damaged sites as the conflict intensified, though its operations were hampered by the broader collapse of Gaza's health infrastructure, including restrictions on aid convoys.[3][15] On January 15, 2009, the hospital sustained direct hits from Israeli artillery shelling, causing explosions, structural damage to buildings, and outbreaks of fire that threatened hundreds of patients, staff, and wounded individuals inside.[16][17] Patients were urgently evacuated to Al-Shifa Hospital in northern Gaza City, and the facility was forced to close temporarily, exacerbating the strain on remaining operational hospitals during the final days of the 22-day campaign.[18] Reports described repeated shelling on the site, with visible burns and destruction to sections of the building, though no immediate fatalities among hospital occupants were documented in contemporaneous accounts.[19][20] The United Nations Fact-Finding Mission on the Gaza Conflict, led by Judge Richard Goldstone, concluded that the Israeli Defense Forces conducted a direct and intentional attack on Al-Quds Hospital, classifying it as part of a pattern of strikes on medical facilities that violated international humanitarian law by endangering civilians and impeding healthcare delivery.[21] Israeli authorities did not publicly confirm targeting the hospital but maintained that operations aimed to neutralize Hamas rocket launchers and militants embedded in civilian areas; no specific evidence of militant activity at Al-Quds was cited by the IDF in relation to this incident.[22] Post-strike assessments by observers indicated the damage was less extensive than initially reported by Palestinian sources, with repairs to water pipes and other infrastructure allowing partial resumption of services within three to five days after the ceasefire on January 18, 2009.[23] The event contributed to the overall tally of 23 emergency service personnel killed across Gaza during the operation, highlighting the risks to humanitarian workers.[20]

Involvement in the 2023–Present Israel-Hamas War

Early War Developments and Fuel Shortages

Following the Hamas attacks on Israel on October 7, 2023, Al-Quds Hospital in Gaza City, operated by the Palestinian Red Crescent Society (PRCS), initially maintained operations to treat casualties from the escalating conflict, sheltering thousands of displaced civilians and handling an influx of wounded patients amid widespread bombardments.[24] However, the hospital, like other Gaza facilities, depended on backup generators for power after Israel's imposition of a blockade restricted fuel imports, exacerbating vulnerabilities in a region where electricity supply was already limited to 4-8 hours daily pre-war due to longstanding restrictions.[25] By November 8, 2023, the PRCS reported that the hospital had exhausted its fuel reserves, forcing the shutdown of most operations, including dialysis and intensive care units, while daily Israeli strikes near the complex compounded the crisis.[26] [27] The fuel shortage intensified as Gaza's overall humanitarian aid convoys faced delays, with no fuel trucks entering the Strip until November 15, 2023, when the first such delivery was permitted via Egypt—though this was insufficient to immediately alleviate hospital generator needs.[28] On November 11, 2023, PRCS technicians were unable to repair the hospital's failed generator due to sniper fire in the vicinity, heightening risks to patients reliant on life-support equipment.[24] By November 12, 2023, the PRCS announced that Al-Quds, Gaza's second-largest hospital with a capacity of around 220 beds, had ceased all operations due to complete fuel depletion and power failure, leaving medical staff to provide minimal care to remaining inpatients without electricity or supplies.[4] [29] This marked one of at least 18 hospital closures or evacuations in Gaza by mid-November, attributed by health officials to the blockade's effects on fuel and medical imports.[30] Israeli officials countered claims of an acute fuel crisis, asserting that Gaza's essential facilities, including hospitals, continued to function using locally produced diesel—estimated at up to 500,000 liters monthly from Hamas-controlled refineries—or smuggled supplies, and suggested that reported shortages masked diversions for military purposes by militant groups.[31] PRCS and UN reports emphasized, however, that hospital-specific fuel allocations were inadequate amid the siege, with Al-Quds facing additional pressures from overcrowding (over 14,000 displaced persons sheltered there by early November) and depleted medical stocks.[32] The shortages contributed to broader health system collapse risks, prompting international warnings from organizations like the World Health Organization about imminent patient deaths without generator fuel.[33]

Evacuation Orders and Sieges

On October 13, 2023, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) issued a broad evacuation order for northern Gaza, encompassing Gaza City and thereby affecting Al-Quds Hospital, one of the largest medical facilities in the area with over 14,000 displaced persons sheltering there at the time.[34] Subsequent specific directives followed, with the Palestine Red Crescent Society (PRCS) reporting on October 21 that Israeli forces had ordered the hospital's evacuation, a demand reiterated amid ongoing bombardments that medics described as rendering patient transfers impossible due to the presence of intensive care units, incubators, and life-support equipment.[35][36] By October 29, PRCS received "serious threats" from Israeli forces for immediate evacuation, prompting warnings from the International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) that such actions in the prevailing conditions—lacking safe corridors, fuel, and medical transport—amounted to near-impossibility and risked patient lives.[37][1] A renewed order came on October 30, coinciding with strikes that damaged hospital structures, as documented by the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA).[25] As IDF ground operations intensified in northern Gaza during early November 2023, Al-Quds Hospital came under effective siege, with Israeli tanks surrounding the facility by November 11, exacerbating fuel shortages that forced the shutdown of most operations, including dialysis and intensive care units, while daily shelling persisted nearby.[32][26] This encirclement formed part of a broader IDF cordon around Gaza City hospitals between November 10 and 16, during which PRCS declared the hospital out of service on November 12, citing depleted resources and unrelenting attacks that left staff unable to provide care.02566-7/fulltext)[4] Partial evacuations occurred amid the pressure, with PRCS facilitating the transfer of approximately 200 individuals, including 25 patients and medical staff, on November 14 via coordinated humanitarian corridors, though thousands of displaced persons had already fled the premises by November 13-14 following IDF incursions reported to involve searches for militant activity.[30][38] Renewed military actions in Gaza City during late 2024 and 2025 brought additional evacuation demands and sieges to Al-Quds. In January 2025, the surrounding area faced Israeli fire, complicating operations, while by early October 2025, ground forces again besieged the hospital, prompting the evacuation of most patients and leaving only about 20 critically ill individuals with a minimal staff amid reports of collapsing infrastructure and sniper fire.[39][40] PRCS and aid groups highlighted the siege's role in halting services, with medics noting that evacuation orders lacked feasible implementation given the intensity of surrounding combat and restricted access routes.[41] These episodes underscored recurring patterns where IDF operations targeted northern Gaza strongholds, intersecting with hospital vicinities claimed to host Hamas infrastructure, though humanitarian assessments emphasized the disproportionate risks to non-combatants and medical continuity.[42]

Recent Damage and Attacks (2024-2025)

In early 2024, Al-Quds Hospital in Gaza City faced ongoing operational disruptions due to heavy bombardment in its vicinity, leading to temporary shutdowns and widespread destruction nearby, as reported by the United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA).[43] By March 2025, the facility had completely ceased operations amid these pressures, with its surrounding area suffering extensive damage from Israeli military actions.[43] During an Israeli ground offensive in Gaza City in September 2025, Al-Quds Hospital was directly hit in attacks on healthcare facilities, contributing to 17 reported incidents over two days that included strikes on three key sites: Al-Quds, Al-Rantisi Children's Hospital, and the Medical Relief Health Centre.[41] On September 23, Israeli gunfire disabled the hospital's oxygen production station, according to the Palestinian Red Crescent Society (PRCS), which operates the facility, forcing reliance on pre-filled cylinders expected to last only three days and threatening patients dependent on oxygen.[44] Israeli ground forces positioned vehicles at the southern gate, blocking entry and exit, while access to the hospital was severed, exacerbating shortages of water, electricity, and medical supplies.[45] As Israeli troops advanced, staff at Al-Quds discharged non-critical patients in anticipation of further risks, with an American volunteer nurse documenting the facility's operational collapse, including colleagues expressing fears of imminent death amid the encirclement.[46] The offensive resulted in the shutdown of two damaged hospitals in Gaza City, including impacts on Al-Quds, as part of broader closures affecting over two dozen primary health providers.[47][48] No specific Israeli Defense Forces statements addressed these September 2025 incidents at Al-Quds, though prior justifications for actions near hospitals have cited militant usage.[49]

Controversies and Military Claims

Allegations of Militant Group Usage

Israeli authorities have alleged that Hamas utilized Al-Quds Hospital as a command and control center during the early stages of the 2023 Israel-Hamas war. On October 30, 2023, the Israeli military cited intelligence indicating that Hamas militants were operating from the facility, alongside Al-Shifa Hospital, prompting an evacuation order for the site amid advancing ground operations in Gaza City.[6] Hamas officials and hospital administrators have consistently denied these assertions, maintaining that the hospital served solely medical purposes and that no military activities occurred on its grounds.[50] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) further claimed to have documented instances of Hamas operatives firing rocket-propelled grenades (RPGs) from Al-Quds Hospital premises, as detailed in Israel's official response to a United Nations report on Gaza healthcare attacks. This allegation aligns with broader IDF assertions of Hamas embedding military operations within civilian infrastructure, including hospitals, to exploit protected status under international law. However, no publicly released visual or forensic evidence specific to tunnels, weapons caches, or command infrastructure at Al-Quds—unlike those presented for facilities such as Al-Shifa or Al-Rantisi—has been provided to substantiate the command center claim.[49][51] These accusations contributed to operational restrictions on Al-Quds, including fuel and supply limitations imposed by Israel, which the IDF justified as measures to prevent diversion to militant groups. Independent verification remains limited, with organizations like the World Health Organization expressing concerns over the impact on civilian healthcare without confirming or refuting the military usage claims. Hamas has countered that such allegations serve as pretexts for targeting medical sites, a position echoed in reports from humanitarian groups skeptical of Israeli intelligence due to historical patterns of contested claims in prior Gaza conflicts.[6][25]

Israeli Defense Forces Actions and Justifications

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) conducted targeted operations in the vicinity of Al-Quds Hospital in Gaza City during the ground phase of the Israel-Hamas war beginning in late October 2023, including surrounding the facility with tanks and engaging in clashes amid reports of Hamas militants firing from its premises.[52][53] On October 30, 2023, the IDF issued an evacuation order for Al-Quds Hospital, citing intelligence that Hamas was using it as a command center for coordinating attacks.[6] IDF justifications centered on Hamas's exploitation of the hospital for military purposes, including storing weapons inside the facility, as evidenced by photographs released on November 16, 2023, showing rifles, ammunition, and explosive devices discovered by troops within Al-Quds Hospital.[52] On November 13, 2023, the IDF published drone footage depicting a Hamas operative armed with an RPG launcher positioned at the hospital's entrance, preparing to fire at Israeli forces, which was cited as confirmation of militants using the site to launch attacks while endangering civilians and medical personnel.[53][51] These actions were framed by the IDF as necessary to neutralize Hamas infrastructure embedded in civilian sites, with broader intelligence assessments, including from U.S. sources, corroborating Hamas's pattern of operating command nodes and weapon caches under or within Gaza hospitals to exploit international legal protections.[54][55] The IDF emphasized precision targeting to minimize harm to patients and staff, contrasting this with Hamas's deliberate placement of military assets in hospitals to hinder Israeli operations and amplify propaganda narratives.[51] No large-scale IDF raid or occupation of Al-Quds Hospital itself was reported, unlike operations at nearby facilities such as Al-Shifa, with efforts focused on eliminating immediate threats from the perimeter.[52]

Palestinian and Aid Agency Perspectives

The Palestine Red Crescent Society (PRCS), which operates Al-Quds Hospital, has consistently denied Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) allegations of militant group presence or military usage within the facility, stating on November 14, 2023, that no armed gunmen were inside the hospital despite IDF claims of Hamas operatives sheltering there.[56] PRCS officials described the hospital as a civilian medical center sheltering thousands of displaced persons and treating patients exclusively, rejecting assertions of tunnels or command centers underneath as baseless.[57] PRCS reported that IDF ground operations and fire around Al-Quds Hospital in September 2025 disabled the facility's oxygen production station, severely threatening ventilator-dependent patients and medical staff, with forces positioned at the hospital gate restricting access and supplies.[44][58] Earlier, on October 29, 2023, PRCS and the International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) expressed grave concern over an IDF evacuation order for the hospital, warning that it endangered over 14,000 sheltered civilians, patients, and staff amid ongoing bombardment and fuel shortages that forced the facility to cease operations by November 12, 2023.[1][59] The Gaza Ministry of Health, controlled by Hamas authorities, has portrayed IDF actions around Al-Quds as deliberate targeting of healthcare infrastructure, contributing to over 500 medical personnel deaths across Gaza facilities by June 2024 and exacerbating a collapse in northern Gaza's medical services.[60] Palestinian officials, including those from the ministry, have labeled such incidents as war crimes, emphasizing the hospital's role in treating war-wounded civilians without military involvement.[61] Aid agencies have echoed these concerns, with the World Health Organization (WHO) condemning attacks near Al-Quds Hospital on November 3, 2023, as unacceptable violations of healthcare protections, part of 137 documented incidents damaging Gaza's medical system in the war's early months.[62][33] Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) reported losing contact with a surgeon and family sheltering in Al-Quds on November 11, 2023, amid sieges trapping patients and staff, and has described broader Israeli operations as systematically destroying Gaza's hospitals through bombardment and restrictions.[63][64] The United Nations Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR) highlighted in September 2025 that IDF attacks on and around Gaza City hospitals like Al-Quds were leaving the injured without care, urging an end to such operations to prevent further civilian harm.[45]

Humanitarian and Operational Impact

Patient and Staff Effects

During the early stages of the 2023 Israel-Hamas war, Al-Quds Hospital in Gaza City experienced critical fuel shortages that depleted its reserves, rendering the facility non-operational by November 12, 2023, as power generators failed and repairs were impossible amid surrounding bombardment and combat.[65] [24] This blackout endangered patients reliant on electrically powered equipment, such as ventilators and incubators, mirroring generator-related fatalities at nearby Shifa Hospital where two neonates died from power loss in the same period.[24] Evacuation orders from Israeli forces on October 29, 2023, targeted the hospital, which housed hundreds of injured and immobile patients, heightening risks of death or injury during transfer without adequate medical support.[1] Initial evacuation attempts failed due to heavy shelling, but by November 14, the Palestine Red Crescent Society successfully moved around 200 people, including medical staff and 25 patients, under fire that trapped others inside.[30] [66] Direct strikes on or near the hospital caused injuries, including to a man and child outside the premises during repeated attacks in November 2023, while staff reported hundreds of patients and over 12,000 displaced persons sheltering amid intensifying explosions that damaged infrastructure.[25] [67] By August 1, 2025, Al-Quds admitted 12 bodies and 90 wounded from strikes on aid queues, overwhelming staff amid ongoing hostilities that damaged support systems like oxygen supplies.[68] [69] In September 2025, remaining personnel faced evacuation pressures, abandoning much of the facility while a skeleton crew managed critical cases, including children in incubators, as colleagues communicated fears of imminent death under siege conditions.[47] [69] These events compounded broader humanitarian strains, with Gaza's health ministry—operated under Hamas control—reporting systemic risks to chronic patients like those on dialysis, though independent verification of specific Al-Quds mortality figures remains limited.[70]

International Aid and Responses

The World Health Organization (WHO) and International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) urged Israel to rescind its October 30, 2023, evacuation order for Al-Quds Hospital, citing the facility's critical role in treating over 700 patients, including newborns dependent on incubators, amid severe fuel shortages that threatened life-support systems.[6] [1] The International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) expressed alarm over the order, emphasizing the Palestinian Red Crescent Society's (PRCS) reports of limited safe evacuation routes and ongoing bombardments blocking access.[1] On November 3, 2023, WHO condemned attacks near Al-Quds Hospital, alongside Al-Shifa and Indonesian hospitals, as unacceptable violations of international humanitarian law protecting medical facilities.[62] An ICRC convoy delivering emergency supplies to Al-Quds on November 7, 2023, came under fire, highlighting risks to aid delivery amid the siege.[71] UN humanitarians reported on October 30, 2023, that northern Gaza hospitals like Al-Quds faced imminent collapse due to fuel depletion, with only days of reserves left for generators powering dialysis and ventilation.[72] By November 12, 2023, PRCS declared Al-Quds out of service after fuel exhaustion forced evacuation of remaining patients and staff under duress.[4] International responses included the World Council of Churches voicing grave concern over the evacuation directive, calling for protection of civilians and medical personnel.[73] Amnesty International issued urgent appeals in October 2023 to halt forced evacuations of Gaza hospitals, including Al-Quds, arguing they endangered trapped patients unable to relocate.[74] In 2025, amid renewed hostilities, Al-Quds partially resumed operations; on August 1, 2025, it received 12 fatalities and 90 injured from strikes on aid distribution sites, with UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) noting ongoing aid convoys but persistent access barriers.[75] Broader UN efforts, including WHO and UNRWA, focused on restoring fuel and medical supplies to northern Gaza facilities, though specific allocations to Al-Quds remained constrained by military restrictions and infrastructure damage.[76]
