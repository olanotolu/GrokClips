Orval Faubus


Orval Eugene Faubus (January 7, 1910 – December 14, 1994) was an American politician and the 36th governor of Arkansas, serving six consecutive terms from 1955 to 1967 and holding the office longer than any other individual in state history.[1][2] Born in a log cabin in rural Madison County, Arkansas, Faubus rose from humble origins through roles in journalism, the U.S. Army during World War II, and state highway administration before entering politics as a Democrat.[1][2] Faubus's governorship marked a period of substantial state development, including the paving of hundreds of miles of highways, expansion of state parks, increases in teachers' salaries, improvements to public education facilities, and support for industrialization that boosted Arkansas's economy.[1] These initiatives, alongside efforts to modernize voter registration by abolishing the poll tax and founding institutions like the Arkansas Children's Colony for the developmentally disabled, underpinned his popularity among Arkansas voters, who reelected him repeatedly despite national scrutiny.[1] His tenure is most notably defined by the 1957 Little Rock Crisis, in which Faubus ordered the Arkansas National Guard to prevent nine African American students, known as the Little Rock Nine, from entering the previously segregated Central High School, citing concerns over public safety amid threats of violence.[3][4] This defiance of a federal court order enforcing the Supreme Court's Brown v. Board of Education ruling led to a confrontation with the federal government, culminating in President Dwight D. Eisenhower federalizing the Guard and deploying the 101st Airborne Division to enforce integration.[3][4] Faubus's actions, including subsequent legislation to maintain segregation, drew widespread controversy and cemented his image as a defender of states' rights against federal overreach, though they alienated moderates and African American communities while solidifying support among segregationists.[1][3]

Early Life and Influences

Childhood and Family Background

Orval Eugene Faubus was born on January 7, 1910, in a rented log cabin on Greasy Creek in Madison County, Arkansas, situated in the rural Ozark Mountains.[1] His parents were John Samuel "Sam" Faubus, a self-educated farmer and vocal anti-capitalist, and Addie Ada Joslin Faubus, whom Sam married in 1908.[1][5] Sam, born in 1887 to William Henry Faubus and Malinda Ann Sparks Faubus, had settled in the area after his parents moved to Mill Creek in Madison County in 1886, pursuing hardscrabble hillside farming amid thin soil and frequent spring floods that limited yields.[5] As the eldest of seven children, Faubus grew up in persistent poverty, with the family alternating between a two-story log house on Standley Mountain and other rudimentary dwellings as they sought viable farmland or wage labor.[5][6] Sam's occupations included farming, crafting railroad ties for ten cents each, itinerant farm work in the Midwest and Canada, and two years of lead mining in Picher, Oklahoma, where he sent earnings home to support the household.[5] These economic pressures necessitated frequent relocations and Orval's early assistance in clearing land, farming, and other manual tasks, reflecting the subsistence existence typical of isolated Ozark communities without paved roads or modern infrastructure.[5][7] Sam Faubus's fervent socialism, adopted after Orval's birth and inspired by Eugene V. Debs, permeated family life; he named his sons—including Orval Eugene after Debs—and other children after socialist figures, exposed them to radical literature as a primary intellectual outlet, and enlisted young Orval in distributing pamphlets and engaging in ideological debates.[1][5] Sam opposed World War I, faced sedition charges in 1917, and advocated for labor unions and social justice, though he later moderated his views post-New Deal while retaining union sympathies.[5] This environment of ideological intensity amid material deprivation marked Faubus's formative years until the family's moves continued into his adolescence.[5]

Education and Early Ideological Formations

Faubus was born on January 7, 1910, in a log cabin on Greasy Creek in Madison County, Arkansas, into a family marked by poverty and socialist convictions. His father, John Samuel "Sam" Faubus, a self-educated farmer and ardent opponent of capitalism, named him after the socialist leader Eugene V. Debs and taught that economic exploitation victimized both poor whites and blacks alike, fostering an early environment of rural radicalism and racial liberalism.[1][8] His formal education occurred primarily in one- and two-room rural schools in the Ozark Mountains, supplemented by self-directed work as a farm laborer, fruit picker, and itinerant teacher after completing basic schooling. At his father's urging, Faubus attended Commonwealth College near Mena in Polk County for approximately three months during his youth; this left-wing, self-help labor institution emphasized progressive ideals and practical skills, where he received training oriented toward teaching.[1][2] No further higher education is recorded, reflecting the limited opportunities in his impoverished upbringing.[1] These formative experiences instilled a pragmatic populism influenced by New Deal-era sympathies and familial socialism, though Faubus displayed early signs of personal ambition over ideological rigidity, as evidenced by his brief college stint amid a lack of deep commitment to radical activism. This youthful radicalism later resurfaced as a political liability during the McCarthy period, highlighting tensions between his Ozark roots and emerging Democratic Party pragmatism.[8][1]

Military Service and Pre-Gubernatorial Career

World War II Experiences

Faubus enlisted in the United States Army in 1942 and served until 1946, rising to the rank of major.[2] He was assigned to the 320th Infantry Regiment of the 35th Infantry Division, where he functioned primarily as an intelligence officer.[9] The division participated in five major European campaigns: Normandy, Northern France, the Rhineland, the Ardennes (including the Battle of the Bulge), and Central Europe, during which Faubus contributed to intelligence operations amid intense combat conditions.[1][6] In his role, Faubus gathered and analyzed frontline intelligence while experiencing the division's high casualty rates; he later recalled that front-line units suffered over 400 percent turnover due to replacements, reflecting the grueling nature of sustained infantry engagements.[10] His service included direct exposure to Nazi atrocities, as his unit encountered evidence of systematic extermination efforts during advances into Germany, which he cited in postwar commentary on events like the Eichmann trial.[11] For his contributions, Faubus received the Combat Infantryman Badge and the Bronze Star Medal, honors denoting active participation in ground combat and meritorious service under fire.[9] These experiences, involving both analytical duties and proximity to battle, informed his later political emphasis on military discipline and states' rights, though he downplayed personal heroics in favor of unit-level resilience.[12]

Entry into Arkansas Politics and Administrative Roles

Following his discharge from the U.S. Army in 1946, Faubus returned to Huntsville, Arkansas, where he initially served as postmaster from 1946 to 1947, managing local postal operations and gaining familiarity with community administration.[2][13] He also engaged in local journalism by co-purchasing the Madison County Record newspaper and authoring editorials advocating improvements in education, healthcare, and highways, which exposed him to regional issues and public discourse.[1] Faubus entered partisan politics by actively campaigning for Sid McMath's successful 1948 gubernatorial bid, part of the "GI Revolt" against entrenched corruption in Arkansas state government. McMath's victory led to Faubus's appointment to the Arkansas State Highway Commission, where he served from 1949 to 1951 in an initially unsalaried role overseeing highway policy and infrastructure planning across the state.[1][2] This position, under McMath's progressive administration focused on modernization and anti-corruption reforms, connected Faubus with political activists and officials throughout Arkansas, broadening his influence beyond Madison County.[1] Subsequently, Faubus worked as an administrative assistant in Governor McMath's office, handling statewide coordination and further embedding him in Democratic Party networks. In 1952, he advanced to director of highways, leading the state's highway administration through 1953 and managing road development projects amid fiscal constraints and political scrutiny of McMath's tenure.[2][1] These administrative roles honed Faubus's expertise in state governance and resource allocation, while exposing him to the practical challenges of implementing infrastructure initiatives, ultimately positioning him as a viable candidate in the 1954 Democratic primary for governor.[13]

Path to the Governorship

1954 Gubernatorial Campaign

Faubus announced his candidacy for the Democratic nomination for governor in early 1954, positioning himself as a champion of rural and working-class interests against incumbent Governor Francis Cherry, who had ousted Faubus's former patron Sid McMath in 1952.[1] As a former state highway commissioner under McMath, Faubus campaigned on economic populism, criticizing electric cooperatives for allegedly high rates charged to rural consumers and defending welfare programs against Cherry's cuts and disparaging remarks toward recipients.[1] Despite being an underdog, Faubus garnered sufficient support in rural and mountainous areas to force a runoff after the July 27 primary, where no candidate secured a majority.[2] In the runoff, Cherry attempted to discredit Faubus by accusing him of communist sympathies due to his brief attendance at the left-leaning Commonwealth College in the 1930s, but the tactic alienated voters and backfired, enabling Faubus to prevail by approximately 7,000 votes.[1] This victory marked a shift in Arkansas Democratic politics, reflecting voter fatigue with Cherry's administration amid economic grievances rather than ideological divides.[2] Securing the Democratic nomination in a one-party dominant state virtually assured victory in the general election on November 2, 1954, where Faubus defeated Republican nominee Pratt C. Remmel, the mayor of Little Rock, in a landslide reflecting the party's entrenched control.[1] Faubus's campaign avoided racial issues, focusing instead on state development and fiscal prudence, which appealed to a broad Democratic base without alienating moderates.[14]

Democratic Primary Dynamics

In the 1954 Democratic primary for Arkansas governor, incumbent Francis Cherry faced challenges from Orval Faubus, a former state highway commissioner and newspaper publisher from Huntsville; state legislator Guy H. "Mutt" Jones; and farmer Gus McMillan.[1][15] Cherry, who had won office in 1952 by emphasizing fiscal conservatism and infrastructure, entered as the favorite backed by party establishment figures, while Faubus positioned himself as an outsider promising administrative reforms to eliminate waste and favoritism in state agencies.[13][15] The first primary on July 27 drew a field where no candidate secured a majority, with Cherry receiving approximately 48 percent of the vote, Faubus 34 percent, Jones 13 percent, and McMillan the remainder, necessitating a runoff between the top two contenders under Arkansas election law at the time.[15] Faubus's campaign gained traction in rural northwest Arkansas through grassroots efforts, leveraging his World War II veteran status and experience in state administration to appeal to voters frustrated with perceived inefficiencies under Cherry, including criticisms of patronage in highway contracts and rising state spending.[1] Cherry countered by highlighting Faubus's brief 1930s association with Commonwealth College, a labor-oriented institution in Mena with alleged communist influences, producing articles Faubus had written there to question his ideological reliability; Faubus initially downplayed the connection before acknowledging it as youthful employment, redirecting focus to his conservative record and pledges for no new taxes.[15] In the August 10 runoff, Faubus surged ahead, securing victory with about 55 percent of the vote amid strong turnout in his home region and among anti-incumbent voters, marking a significant upset against the sitting governor and propelling him toward the general election.[16][15] This outcome reflected broader dynamics of factional divisions within the Arkansas Democratic Party, where rural populism challenged urban and establishment interests, though racial integration—fresh from the May 1954 Brown v. Board of Education ruling—played minimal role in the contest compared to administrative and personal attacks.[17]

General Election Victory

In the general election on November 2, 1954, Democratic nominee Orval Faubus secured victory over Republican challenger Pratt C. Remmel, the mayor of Little Rock, capturing approximately 62 percent of the vote in a contest marked by Arkansas's entrenched Democratic dominance.[18] Remmel, campaigning on themes of municipal modernization and Republican resurgence, received 38 percent, representing the strongest showing by a Republican gubernatorial candidate in the state since Reconstruction.[18] This outcome reflected the limited viability of the GOP in mid-20th-century Southern politics, where party loyalty and the Democratic primary often determined the governorship.[1] Faubus's win propelled him into office on January 11, 1955, initiating a tenure defined initially by administrative reforms rather than the segregation conflicts that later defined his national profile.[1]

Re-elections and Political Consolidation

Faubus won re-election to a second term in 1956, defeating former Governor Francis Cherry in the Democratic primary runoff before securing a landslide victory over Republican nominee Pratt Remmel in the general election.[1] His opposition to federal intervention during the 1957 Little Rock Central High School desegregation crisis enhanced his appeal among segregationist and rural voters, contributing to his third-term victory in 1958, which featured his most convincing Democratic primary performance to date despite challenges from both segregationist and moderate factions.[19][1] Subsequent re-elections followed in 1960 against Democratic primary challenger H. E. Williams, 1962 against former Governor Sid McMath, 1964 against Republican Winthrop Rockefeller, and 1966 against Dale Alford, enabling Faubus to serve six consecutive two-year terms from January 11, 1955, to January 10, 1967—the longest gubernatorial tenure in Arkansas history.[2][1] These successes stemmed from his ability to defeat rivals across the Democratic Party spectrum, including former allies, while maintaining strong support in rural areas through a states' rights platform.[1] Faubus consolidated political power by fostering loyalty among appointees and state agency personnel, exerting unprecedented influence over government operations during his extended tenure, and chairing the Southern Governors' Conference from 1962 to 1963.[20][2] His electoral dominance reinforced Democratic control in Arkansas politics, sidelining moderate reformers and embedding patronage networks that sustained his administration amid national scrutiny.[1]

Domestic Policies as Governor (1955–1967)

Economic Development and Industrialization Efforts

During his tenure as governor, Orval Faubus prioritized industrialization to address Arkansas's heavy reliance on agriculture and high rural unemployment, viewing it as essential to replacing lost farm jobs amid mechanization and population outmigration.[21] One of his first legislative initiatives in 1955 was the creation of the Arkansas Industrial Development Commission (AIDC), tasked with attracting new manufacturing plants and expanding existing industries through promotional campaigns emphasizing low taxes, available labor, and the state's right-to-work status, which had been in place since 1944.[21][22] Faubus appointed industrialist Winthrop Rockefeller as the commission's inaugural chairman, leveraging his business acumen to market Arkansas to out-of-state firms in sectors like textiles, food processing, and wood products.[13] These efforts yielded measurable gains in industrial employment, with AIDC initiatives credited for generating approximately 90,000 new jobs in manufacturing and related fields over the subsequent decade, alongside steady expansions in payroll and state revenues from business taxes.[23] Faubus also advanced worker protections by incrementally improving the state's workmen's compensation laws, raising benefits and coverage to make Arkansas more appealing to employers wary of litigation risks.[23] Although the 1957 Little Rock school integration crisis temporarily deterred some corporate relocations due to national media scrutiny, overall economic diversification accelerated, contributing to Arkansas's integration into broader national manufacturing networks and reducing outmigration rates compared to prior decades.[24]

Infrastructure and Fiscal Reforms

During his tenure as governor, Orval Faubus oversaw significant expansions in Arkansas's highway infrastructure, including the paving of hundreds of miles of state roads, which enhanced rural connectivity and supported agricultural and industrial transport. This effort built on prior initiatives but accelerated under Faubus, aligning with broader national interstate development; for instance, in March 1965, Faubus became the first official to drive the newly completed 9.5-mile northern segment of Interstate 55 from Blytheville to the Missouri border, marking a key advancement in the state's modern highway system.[25][26] These projects were funded through dedicated state revenues and federal matching funds, contributing to reduced travel times and economic integration across the state's diverse terrain. Faubus also advanced state parks and public facilities as part of infrastructure priorities, expanding access to recreational areas amid growing post-war tourism and population mobility.[25] His administration's approach emphasized practical improvements to counter Arkansas's historical underdevelopment, with highway and park investments yielding measurable gains in accessibility without relying on unchecked borrowing. On the fiscal front, Faubus adhered to the Revenue Stabilization Act of 1945, a mechanism that allocated state funds hierarchically based on projected revenues, ensuring expenditures did not exceed inflows and averting deficits even as services expanded.[27] In his early terms, he enacted moderately progressive tax reforms, including adjustments that increased allocations for education and infrastructure while opposing broader property tax hikes favored by predecessors.[28] These measures sustained budget balance amid rising demands, as evidenced by court validations of the act's role in fiscal prudence during his governorship, allowing targeted investments without systemic insolvency.[27]

States' Rights Stance and Integration Conflicts

Philosophical Foundations of Federalism

Faubus articulated a philosophy of federalism emphasizing dual sovereignty, wherein the federal government possesses only enumerated powers, while residual authority over local matters like education resides with the states under the Tenth Amendment. He contended that the U.S. Constitution established a balanced system to prevent centralized dominance, preserving state autonomy as essential to republican governance and individual liberties. This view aligned with interpretations of the union as a compact among sovereign states, limiting federal intervention to avert tyranny and maintain diverse policy experimentation across jurisdictions.[29] In defending Arkansas's resistance to court-ordered desegregation, Faubus invoked these principles to argue that education constituted a reserved state power, free from federal dictation on operations or funding allocation. He maintained that the federal government lacked constitutional warrant to mandate public school maintenance or prescribe tax expenditures for specific social policies, positioning such overreach as a threat to the "dual system of government" that underpinned American stability through checks and balances.[30][29] During the Little Rock crisis, he framed federal troop deployment as an executive violation of state sovereignty, equating it to historical occupations that undermined local self-rule.[31] Faubus's rhetoric extended this federalist framework to warn against broader erosions of state authority, portraying judicial and executive actions in Brown v. Board of Education (1954) as precursors to an "all-powerful federal autocracy" that subordinated citizen rights to national uniformity. He explicitly rejected surrendering state prerogatives, declaring in a 1958 address that his duty as governor compelled defense of Arkansans' constitutional protections against such encroachments, even at the cost of temporary school closures to uphold local control.[29][30] This stance echoed signatories of the 1956 Southern Manifesto, which he endorsed, protesting Brown as an unconstitutional breach of federalism by imposing federal policy on state educational systems without explicit constitutional delegation.

The Little Rock Central High Crisis

In 1957, the Little Rock School Board implemented the Blossom Plan for gradual desegregation of its public schools, beginning with Central High School in compliance with the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education Supreme Court decision mandating the end of segregated education.[32] On September 2, Governor Orval Faubus issued a proclamation citing threats of violence and ordered the Arkansas National Guard to surround Central High School to maintain order and prevent integration by nine African American students known as the Little Rock Nine.[3] The next day, September 4, as school opened, the Guard blocked the students from entering under Faubus's directive, despite a federal court order requiring their admission, prompting accusations of defiance against federal authority. Faubus justified the deployment as a measure to protect public safety amid rising tensions from segregationist groups, arguing that immediate integration would incite unrest rather than stemming from ideological opposition to racial mixing.[33] Federal Judge Ronald Davies issued an injunction on September 20 prohibiting Guard interference, leading Faubus to withdraw the troops that evening after a conference with President Dwight D. Eisenhower in Newport, Rhode Island, where no resolution on integration was reached.[3] Without the Guard, a white mob of approximately 1,000 gathered outside Central High on September 23, hurling insults and threats as the Little Rock Nine briefly entered the school before being removed for safety by local police unable to control the violence.[4] The escalation, including attacks on journalists and fears of broader riots, underscored Faubus's claim of inevitable disorder, though critics attributed the mob's aggression to his earlier rhetoric challenging federal mandates.[34] On September 24, Eisenhower responded by issuing Executive Order 10730, federalizing the Arkansas National Guard and authorizing the deployment of 1,000 U.S. Army paratroopers from the 101st Airborne Division to enforce court orders and protect the students.[34] The troops arrived that night, and on September 25, the Little Rock Nine entered Central High under military escort, marking the first use of federal combat forces for domestic school integration since Reconstruction.[4] Faubus maintained that the intervention represented an unconstitutional overreach, eroding states' rights to manage local affairs, while federal officials emphasized the necessity to uphold the rule of law against gubernatorial obstruction.[35] The crisis persisted through the school year with ongoing harassment of the students, but integration proceeded under sustained military presence until November 1957, when the Army withdrew while the Guard remained federalized until May 1958.[3]

School Closures and Legal Repercussions

In response to ongoing federal court orders mandating desegregation of Little Rock's public high schools following the 1957 crisis, Arkansas Governor Orval Faubus convened an extraordinary session of the state legislature on August 26, 1958, which enacted several measures including Act 4, granting the governor authority to close schools in districts facing imminent integration to maintain public order.[36][37] On September 12, 1958, the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously rejected a request from the Little Rock School Board to postpone desegregation beyond two years, affirming the timeline set by the district's original plan.[38] Despite this, Faubus ordered the closure of all four Little Rock public high schools—Central High School, Hall High School, Technical High School, and Horace Mann High School—effective September 15, 1958, pending a voter referendum, thereby suspending education for approximately 4,000 students for the duration of the academic year.[32][36] A special election held on September 27, 1958, asked Little Rock voters whether to continue the closures or reopen the schools under desegregation; a majority approved keeping the high schools shuttered, with results reflecting strong opposition among white voters to integrated facilities rather than accepting federal mandates.[36][39] This period, dubbed the "Lost Year," saw limited private alternatives emerge, such as makeshift schools funded by segregationist groups, but these served only a fraction of displaced students, leaving most without formal instruction and prompting widespread criticism of educational disruption as a tactic to evade Brown v. Board of Education.[36][40] Legal challenges mounted swiftly, with federal courts examining the constitutionality of the closure acts. On June 18, 1959, a U.S. district court ruled the school board's continued closure unconstitutional, ordering high schools to reopen ahead of schedule with limited integration at Central and Hall High Schools.[41] The U.S. Supreme Court, in a December 12, 1959, per curiam decision, struck down two key Arkansas statutes underpinning the closures—those empowering local voters or the governor to shutter schools to block desegregation—as violations of the Equal Protection Clause, mandating compliance with prior integration rulings.[42][43] Schools reopened in August 1959 under court supervision, though Faubus's actions faced no direct personal penalties, instead bolstering his political support among states' rights advocates in subsequent elections.[36]

Broader Political Engagements

National Ambitions in the 1960 Presidential Race

In early 1960, Faubus's prominence as a defender of states' rights following the Little Rock crisis drew attention from segregationist groups outside Arkansas. On March 12, 1960, the National States' Rights Party (NSRP), a fringe organization founded in 1958 to oppose federal civil rights enforcement, nominated Faubus as its presidential candidate during a secretive convention near Miamisburg, Ohio.[44] The NSRP, known for its white supremacist rhetoric and paramilitary activities, placed Faubus's name on ballots in several states, including Florida, without his prior knowledge or endorsement.[45] Nationwide, the NSRP ticket garnered 44,977 votes, with Faubus performing strongest in Arkansas at approximately 6.8% of the total presidential vote.[46] Faubus did not accept or actively pursue the nomination, maintaining his affiliation with the Democratic Party amid his concurrent campaign for a fourth gubernatorial term. He later distanced himself from the NSRP, publicly denouncing the group in 1961 for labeling the Adolf Eichmann trial a "propaganda hoax," a stance that underscored the party's extremist ideology beyond mainstream Southern resistance to integration.[47] Instead, Faubus expressed support for Democratic nominee John F. Kennedy during the 1960 campaign, citing Kennedy's 1956 vice-presidential bid and perceived balance on federalism issues in a June 29, 1967, oral history interview.[48] This alignment reflected Faubus's pragmatic focus on Democratic loyalty and state-level power consolidation rather than a third-party bid, as Kennedy carried Arkansas in the general election. Faubus's national visibility did not translate into serious contention within Democratic circles, where primary contests centered on figures like Kennedy, Hubert Humphrey, and Lyndon B. Johnson. While some segregationist sympathizers produced campaign materials, such as buttons promoting "Faubus for President," these efforts remained marginal and did not disrupt his dominant re-election victory in the Arkansas Democratic primary on July 26, 1960, where he secured over 65% of the vote against four challengers.[49] In the general election, Faubus defeated Republican Henry M. Britt with 69.2% of the vote, solidifying his regional base without leveraging presidential speculation for broader ambitions.[49] The episode highlighted Faubus's symbolic appeal to hardline states' rights advocates but affirmed his commitment to Democratic institutional politics over quixotic national challenges.

Post-Term Political Ventures

Following his departure from the Arkansas governorship in January 1967 after six terms, Orval Faubus mounted three unsuccessful campaigns for the Democratic nomination for governor.[12] In the 1970 Democratic primary runoff on September 8, Faubus was defeated by Dale Bumpers, who secured approximately 59% of the vote to Faubus's 41%.[50] Bumpers, a political newcomer emphasizing reform and modernization, advanced to the general election and defeated incumbent Republican Governor Winthrop Rockefeller. Faubus's bid reflected his effort to leverage his long record of electoral success, but it faltered amid voter interest in generational change. Faubus ran again in the 1974 Democratic primary, challenging David Pryor, a former U.S. Representative, but lost the nomination on May 28.[51] Pryor, positioning himself as a progressive alternative, prevailed and went on to win the governorship unopposed in the general election after Republicans fielded no candidate. Faubus's campaign included criticisms of opponents' stances on issues like amnesty for Vietnam War draft evaders, yet it failed to regain his former base amid evolving state demographics and political alignments.[52] His final attempt came in the 1986 Democratic primary on May 27, where incumbent Governor Bill Clinton defeated him decisively.[53] Clinton, seeking renomination after a narrow 1984 re-election, framed the race around forward-looking governance, contrasting with Faubus's emphasis on his prior experience. Faubus, then 76, garnered support from rural and traditional Democratic voters but could not overcome the broader shift toward younger leadership, ending his political comeback efforts.[54]

Later Years and Enduring Legacy

Retirement Activities and Writings

After leaving office in January 1967, Faubus returned to Huntsville, Arkansas, his hometown, and focused on writing during his retirement. He authored In This Faraway Land in 1971, a 736-page account of his World War II infantry service in Europe.[55] He self-published the two-volume memoir Down from the Hills in 1980 through Pioneer Press in Little Rock, spanning 1,074 pages with photographs, speeches, and newspaper excerpts detailing his early governorship years, including the 1957 Little Rock crisis, alongside defenses against critics and lists of political associates.[55] A sequel, Down from the Hills II, appeared in 1985, emphasizing personal challenges such as family tragedies and relationships.[55] Faubus personally marketed his books by selling copies from his car trunk at craft fairs, the Arkansas State Capitol rotunda, and public events, often autographing them and adding buyers' names to the index for personalization; however, sales remained limited.[55] He also wrote Man's Best Friend: The Little Australian and Others, a collection of anecdotes about dogs, reflecting his interest in rural life.[56] Additionally, he produced essays commenting on public affairs and maintained involvement in veterans' causes, drawing from his military background. These writings served as a platform for Faubus to recount his career and personal history in a documentary style, incorporating primary documents and self-reflective narrative.[55] In non-literary pursuits, Faubus briefly managed Dogpatch USA, a theme park near Eureka Springs, Arkansas, inspired by Li'l Abner comics, before its financial struggles. He later accepted an appointment as Arkansas state director of veterans' affairs under Governor Frank White in the early 1980s.[2] These activities supplemented his retirement income amid modest circumstances in Huntsville, where he resided until health issues prompted moves in his final years.[23]

Assessments of Achievements Versus Criticisms

Faubus's governorship is assessed positively for fostering economic industrialization and infrastructure development in Arkansas. During his tenure from 1955 to 1967, the state experienced steady industrial growth, which enabled investments in public education and road systems, aligning with his initial campaign promises to expand spending in these areas.[1][57] His administration's focus on highway improvements built on his prior service on the Arkansas State Highway Commission from 1949 to 1951, contributing to better connectivity and economic integration with national networks.[2] These efforts, coupled with fiscal management that capitalized on post-World War II prosperity, are credited with progressive elements in his record, including enhanced educational funding and facilities.[1] Supporters highlight Faubus's enduring popularity as evidence of effective governance, evidenced by his six consecutive two-year terms—the longest in Arkansas history at the time—reflecting broad voter approval, particularly after his states' rights stance during the 1957 Little Rock crisis solidified support among segregationist constituencies.[58] His re-elections, including strong margins in primaries and generals, demonstrated alignment with Arkansas voters' priorities on local control and economic pragmatism over federal mandates.[59] Critics, however, emphasize Faubus's resistance to school desegregation as a primary failing, viewing his deployment of the National Guard to block the Little Rock Nine on September 4, 1957, and subsequent school closures as deliberate obstruction of federal court orders under Brown v. Board of Education (1954), prioritizing racial separation over constitutional compliance.[60] While Faubus justified these actions on grounds of public safety and states' rights—citing threats of violence and arguing against federal overreach—opponents contend they inflamed tensions, necessitated President Eisenhower's intervention with the 101st Airborne Division, and delayed integration, framing his legacy as one of entrenched segregationism rather than principled federalism.[10][3] Additional criticisms include allegations of political corruption, such as patronage networks and influence peddling, which undermined the transparency of his progressive initiatives.[1] Some analyses portray his shift from early moderation—evident in pre-1957 support for integrating public colleges—to hardline segregationism as opportunistic, tying his electoral successes to demagoguery rather than policy substance, though this view overlooks the causal role of federal enforcement in galvanizing local backlash.[61] Overall, assessments diverge: admirers see a defender of sovereignty who delivered tangible state benefits, while detractors prioritize his role in perpetuating division, with empirical voter data indicating the former resonated more durably in Arkansas.[59]

Electoral Record

Gubernatorial Terms Overview

Orval Faubus served as the 36th governor of Arkansas from January 11, 1955, to January 10, 1967, securing six consecutive two-year terms as a Democrat.[1] [2] He entered office after winning the 1954 Democratic primary runoff against incumbent Francis A. Cherry and defeating Republican Pratt C. Remmel in the general election, an upset victory that marked his transition from a relatively obscure state highway commissioner to the state's chief executive.[1] Faubus's initial platform emphasized fiscal conservatism and administrative efficiency, appealing to voters disillusioned with prior governance amid economic stagnation.[2] Faubus's re-elections in 1956, 1958, 1960, 1962, and 1964 demonstrated enduring popularity in a politically one-party state, where Democratic primaries effectively decided outcomes.[1] His tenure emphasized economic modernization, including aggressive industrialization campaigns that lured manufacturing firms with tax incentives and infrastructure investments, alongside paving thousands of miles of rural highways.[1] Education saw advancements such as increased teacher salaries and expanded funding, while reforms overhauled the Arkansas State Hospital, addressing longstanding mismanagement.[1] These initiatives contributed to measurable growth in employment and state revenue, though his administration later encountered credible reports of political patronage and corruption involving state contracts.[1] [2] Faubus's prolonged incumbency allowed continuity in policy execution but also entrenched his influence over state politics, including appointments to key positions and control over legislative agendas.[2] While early terms pursued broadly progressive economic measures, subsequent ones increasingly centered on resistance to federal mandates on race and education, shaping his governance amid national civil rights tensions.[2] His service on the National Governors' Conference Executive Committee from 1957 to 1958 and chairmanship of the Southern Governors' Conference in 1962–1963 extended Arkansas's voice in regional and national forums.[2] Faubus declined to seek a seventh term in 1966, citing fatigue after 12 years in office.[1]

Key Vote Margins and Voter Support Patterns

Faubus secured his initial victory in the 1954 Democratic primary runoff against incumbent Governor Francis Cherry by a narrow margin of approximately 7,000 votes out of over 200,000 cast, reflecting a contentious intra-party contest focused on administrative reforms and rural grievances.[1] He then prevailed in the general election against Republican Pratt C. Remmel in a landslide, capturing the vast majority of votes in a state dominated by Democratic loyalties. Subsequent reelections demonstrated widening margins until the mid-1960s, with Faubus routinely exceeding 70% of the vote in general elections against nominal Republican opposition, underscoring his consolidation of power within Arkansas's one-party Democratic framework.[1]

Voter support patterns revealed Faubus's reliance on rural, white Democratic constituencies, particularly in the Ozarks and southern counties, where he often secured upwards of 78% in individual races and carried 65 of Arkansas's 75 counties in 1964.[62] The 1957 Little Rock Central High School crisis markedly bolstered his appeal among segregation-minded voters opposed to federal intervention, transforming initial vulnerabilities into electoral assets and enabling four additional terms despite urban opposition.[1] In contrast, urban centers like Pulaski County (encompassing Little Rock) exhibited resistance, driven by African American voters, white liberals, and media outlets such as the Arkansas Gazette, which criticized his policies; Rockefeller, for instance, performed strongly in these areas during 1964.[63][1] This rural-urban divide persisted, with Faubus's machine leveraging county-level patronage and anti-federal rhetoric to maintain loyalty in less populated regions, even as national Republican gains loomed.[62]
