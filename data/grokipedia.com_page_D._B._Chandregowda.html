D. B. Chandregowda

Daradahalli Byregowda Chandregowda (26 August 1936 – 7 November 2023), commonly known as D. B. Chandregowda, was an Indian politician and advocate from Karnataka who achieved the distinction of serving in all four houses of the state's and national legislatures: the Karnataka Legislative Assembly, Karnataka Legislative Council, Lok Sabha, and Rajya Sabha.[1][2][3] Born in Baradahalli village, Mudigere taluk of Chikkamagaluru district, he held a B.Sc. and LL.B., practicing as an advocate before entering politics in the early 1970s.[4] Chandregowda's career spanned over five decades and multiple parties, including the Indian National Congress, Janata Party, Janata Dal, and Bharatiya Janata Party, reflecting his adaptability in Karnataka's dynamic political landscape.[2][5] He was elected to the Lok Sabha three times—first from Chikkamagaluru in 1971 and 1977 on a Congress ticket, and later from Bengaluru North in 2009 on a BJP ticket—and to the Rajya Sabha from 1986 to 1989 representing the Janata Party.[1][6] In the state legislature, he served as a three-term MLA from constituencies including Tirthahalli (1983, 1989) and Sringeri (1999), and as an MLC from 1978 to 1984, where he acted as Leader of the Opposition starting in 1980.[4][1] His tenure as Speaker of the Karnataka Legislative Assembly from 1983 to 1985 highlighted his role in legislative proceedings, while ministerial positions included handling minor irrigation under D. Devaraj Urs and law and parliamentary affairs under S. M. Krishna.[1][2] A defining moment came in June 1978 when Chandregowda resigned his Chikkamagaluru Lok Sabha seat—won in the 1977 elections—to allow Indira Gandhi to contest the by-election and reclaim her parliamentary position after her post-Emergency disqualification and defeat in Rae Bareli.[2][1] This strategic move facilitated Gandhi's political resurgence, leading to her eventual return as prime minister, and earned him a subsequent MLC nomination as reward.[2] During his parliamentary stints, he advocated for constituency development, including the realization of the Kudremukh mining project, and focused on issues affecting the poor in Chikkamagaluru.[4] Chandregowda's cross-party service and legislative versatility underscored his enduring influence in Karnataka politics until his death at age 87 from age-related causes.[1][2]

Early Life

Birth and Family

Daradahalli Byregowda Chandregowda, commonly known as D. B. Chandregowda, was born on 26 August 1936 in Daradahalli village, Mudigere taluk, Chikkamagaluru district, Karnataka.[4][1][7] He was the son of D. A. Byregowda, a local figure, and Puttamma.[8] Chandregowda married B. S. Poorna, with whom he had four daughters, including the eldest, Pallavi.[1][7][9] Limited public records detail further aspects of his immediate family background, reflecting a modest rural upbringing in the Malnad region of Karnataka.[1]

Education and Early Career

Chandregowda earned a Bachelor of Science degree from Renukacharya College in Bangalore.[4] He subsequently obtained a Bachelor of Laws degree from R. L. Law College in Bangalore.[10] During his time at Renukacharya College, he served as vice-president of the students' association, demonstrating early leadership capabilities.[4] [11] Following his legal education, Chandregowda practiced as an advocate, establishing a professional foundation before entering electoral politics in 1971.[1] His student roles, including secretary of the law college students' association, honed organizational skills that later supported his political involvement.[4] Limited public records detail his pre-political legal practice, which focused on advocacy in Karnataka amid a period of regional social and developmental challenges.[8]

Political Entry and Initial Roles

First Electoral Successes

D. B. Chandregowda entered electoral politics by contesting the 1971 Lok Sabha election from the Chikmagaluru constituency in Karnataka as a candidate of the Indian National Congress, securing victory and becoming a member of the 5th Lok Sabha.[1][12] This marked his debut success in parliamentary elections, representing a rural constituency known for its agricultural base and Congress stronghold at the time.[13] He was re-elected from the same constituency in the 1977 general election, again on a Congress ticket, during a period of post-Emergency political flux where the party sought to regain ground.[14][15] These consecutive wins established Chandregowda as a reliable Congress representative in the region, leveraging local networks and party loyalty amid national shifts following the Janata Party's brief 1977 government.[16] In November 1978, amid Indira Gandhi's efforts to re-enter Parliament after her electoral setback, Chandregowda resigned his Lok Sabha seat to enable her to contest the subsequent by-election from Chikmagaluru, which she won decisively.[1][17] This strategic exit, while ending his immediate parliamentary tenure, underscored his allegiance to Congress leadership and facilitated Gandhi's political comeback.[18]

Early Party Affiliation

Chandre Gowda began his political career with the Praja Socialist Party (PSP), serving as the General Secretary of its Chikkmagalur District unit prior to 1969.[4] In this role, he engaged in grassroots organizational activities in the region.[4] During the 1969 split in the Indian National Congress, Chandre Gowda aligned with the faction led by Indira Gandhi, drawn by its emphasis on socialist measures such as bank nationalization and the abolition of privy purses.[4] He was appointed the inaugural District Congress General Secretary for Chikkmagalur, where he restructured the party organization and successfully enrolled around 15,000 new members.[4] This affiliation with the Congress party positioned him for his debut electoral victory in the 1971 Lok Sabha election from the Chikkamagaluru constituency, where he campaigned on the party's ticket and secured the seat.[19] His early shift from the PSP to Congress reflected a broader trend among socialist-leaning leaders seeking alignment with Indira Gandhi's progressive agenda amid the factional divisions.[4]

Legislative Career

Terms as MLA

D. B. Chandregowda served three terms as a Member of the Karnataka Legislative Assembly (MLA). His first term was from 1983 to 1985, representing the Thirthahalli constituency as a candidate of the Janata Party following the 1983 state assembly elections.[1][18] The eighth Karnataka Legislative Assembly, elected in 1983, was dissolved prematurely in 1985 under President's Rule amid political instability.[8] Chandregowda returned to the assembly in 1989, securing re-election from Thirthahalli on a Janata Dal ticket for a term lasting until 1994.[11][1] During this period, he held the position of Leader of the Opposition, contributing to legislative oversight amid the Janata Dal-led coalition government.[11] His third term came in 1999, when he won the Sringeri constituency as a Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) candidate, serving until 2004.[11][18] This victory aligned with his shift toward BJP affiliations, reflecting evolving political alliances in Karnataka during the late 1990s.[13] Across these terms, Chandregowda focused on regional development issues in the Malnad districts, leveraging his experience from prior parliamentary roles.[1]

Role as MLC

D. B. Chandregowda was elected to the Karnataka Legislative Council in June 1978 as an Independent member, nominated by the Legislative Assembly, serving a term from 1978 to 1984.[4] This followed his resignation from the Lok Sabha on June 29, 1978, enabling him to prioritize state-level responsibilities after vacating his parliamentary seat.[4] During his tenure, Chandregowda held the position of Minister for Minor Irrigation, contributing to state water resource management initiatives.[4] He also served as Leader of the Opposition in the Legislative Council from January 24, 1980, to June 24, 1981, representing the Congress (U) during this leadership period, where he advocated on constituency and public welfare matters, including issues affecting the underprivileged.[20][4]

Speakership and Ministerial Positions

Chandregowda served as Speaker of the Karnataka Legislative Assembly from 1983 to 1985, having been elected as a Janata Party MLA from the Tirthahalli constituency earlier that year.[18][1] During this period, he presided over assembly proceedings amid the post-Emergency political transitions in the state.[14] In his ministerial roles, Chandregowda held the portfolio of Law and Parliamentary Affairs in the Congress government led by Chief Minister S. M. Krishna, following his election as MLA from Sringeri in 1999; he continued in this position until 2004.[1][12][11] He had previously occupied ministerial positions in other state governments, contributing to legislative and administrative functions during his terms as both MLA and MLC.[18][21]

Parliamentary Career

Lok Sabha Elections and Terms

D. B. Chandregowda was first elected to the Lok Sabha in the 1971 general election from the Chikmagalur constituency in Karnataka, representing the Indian National Congress, as part of the 5th Lok Sabha (1971–1977).[14][12] He secured victory in this rural, Congress-stronghold seat amid the national wave favoring Indira Gandhi's party following the 1969 split.[22] In the 1977 general election, held after the Emergency period, Chandregowda was re-elected from Chikmagalur to the 6th Lok Sabha, defeating the Congress candidate in a Janata Party-led alliance sweep against the ruling Congress.[4][14] His term lasted from March 1977 until November 1978, when he resigned the seat to facilitate Indira Gandhi's by-election entry following her Rae Bareli disqualification.[15] This act marked a notable instance of intra-party accommodation within Congress circles post-Emergency.[13] Chandregowda's third Lok Sabha term came in the 2009 general election, where he won the Bangalore North constituency as a Bharatiya Janata Party candidate, securing the 15th Lok Sabha seat (2009–2014) with approximately 42% of the vote share in a competitive urban contest against Congress and Janata Dal (Secular) opponents.[23] During this term, he participated in parliamentary committees on personnel, public grievances, law and justice, as well as science, technology, environment, and forests, reflecting his background in law and governance.[23] His attendance record stood at 59%, below the national average of 76% for MPs.[24]

Rajya Sabha Membership

D. B. Chandregowda served as a member of the Rajya Sabha from Karnataka, representing the Janata Party, from 1986 to 1989.[1][21] His election to the upper house occurred amid the political transitions following the Janata Party's formation, with the party later evolving into Janata Dal in 1988.[12] During his tenure, Chandregowda participated in parliamentary proceedings, including debates on budgetary matters and questions related to industrial standards.[25] Chandregowda resigned from the Rajya Sabha in late 1989 to contest the Lok Sabha election from the Chikmagalur constituency, marking the end of his upper house membership after approximately three years.[1] This move aligned with his broader parliamentary ambitions, as he had previously held Lok Sabha seats and sought to leverage his regional influence in Karnataka politics.[13] No specific committee assignments or legislative initiatives uniquely tied to his Rajya Sabha period are prominently documented in contemporary accounts, though his overall career emphasized state-level governance and opposition roles.[3]

Party Affiliations and Shifts

Congress Period

Chandregowda aligned with the Indian National Congress faction led by Indira Gandhi during the 1969 party split, motivated by her socialist policies including bank nationalization and abolition of privy purses. He served as the inaugural District Congress General Secretary for Chikkamagalur, organizing local party structures and enrolling around 15,000 members to strengthen grassroots presence.[4] His parliamentary success began with election to the Lok Sabha from Chikkamagalur in the 1971 general election, defeating opponents by a significant margin amid Congress's national resurgence under Gandhi. Re-elected in March 1977 to the sixth Lok Sabha, he secured victory with a 65,000-vote margin, reflecting strong regional support post-Emergency despite anti-Congress sentiment elsewhere.[4][1][13] Shifting to state politics, Chandregowda won election to the Karnataka Legislative Council in June 1978, representing the graduates' constituency. He held the portfolio of Minister for Minor Irrigation in the Devraj Urs-led Congress government, focusing on rural water management initiatives. From 24 January 1980, he acted as Leader of the Opposition in the Legislative Council, critiquing coalition governance while advocating Congress positions on development and federalism.[4] Chandregowda's Congress tenure emphasized loyalty to Gandhi's leadership, evidenced by his voluntary resignation from the Lok Sabha on 29 June 1978 to enable her parliamentary return via by-election. This period solidified his role as a key regional organizer and legislator until he departed the party in 1983 amid internal disagreements, transitioning to Karnataka Kranti Ranga.[4][13]

Transitions to Janata Dal and BJP

Chandregowda resigned from the Indian National Congress in 1980 amid internal factionalism, aligning with former Chief Minister Devaraj Urs to join the Karnataka Kranti Ranga, a splinter group advocating for regional interests and caste-based mobilization in Karnataka politics.[11] [6] This marked his initial shift away from the dominant national party toward smaller, state-focused outfits challenging Congress dominance post-Emergency. By 1983, he had transitioned to the Janata Party, an opposition coalition emphasizing anti-Congress sentiment and federalism, contesting and securing victory in the Tirthahalli Assembly constituency during the Karnataka elections held amid widespread dissatisfaction with Congress governance.[14] He continued this affiliation into the Rajya Sabha, elected in 1986 from Karnataka on a Janata Party nomination, a term that overlapped with the party's fragmentation and reconfiguration.[21] The Janata Party's core elements merged into the Janata Dal in October 1988 under V. P. Singh's leadership, forming a national alternative to Congress with socialist and rural-focused policies; Chandregowda maintained association with this entity, reflecting his adaptability to evolving opposition dynamics in Karnataka.[11] Decades later, after a return to Congress in the 1990s—evidenced by his 1999 assembly contest under its banner—Chandregowda again defected, resigning from Congress primary membership in 2008 amid perceptions of organizational stagnation and leadership disputes at the state level.[11] [8] He joined the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) that year, drawn by its rising influence in Karnataka under B. S. Yediyurappa's stewardship and emphasis on governance reforms and Hindu-majority consolidation.[23] This switch enabled his successful 2009 Lok Sabha bid from Bangalore North, where he defeated the Congress incumbent by over 22,000 votes, securing a term until 2014 focused on urban development and infrastructure priorities.[1] [14] His BJP tenure highlighted a pattern of pragmatic realignments toward parties gaining electoral traction in Karnataka's fragmented political landscape.

Key Events and Decisions

Vacating Lok Sabha Seat for Indira Gandhi

In 1977, D. B. Chandregowda was elected to the Sixth Lok Sabha from the Chikkamagaluru constituency as a Congress candidate, representing a region in Karnataka where he held significant local influence.[13] Following the defeat of Indira Gandhi's Congress party in the national elections after the Emergency period (1975–1977), during which Gandhi had lost her own Rae Bareli seat, party leaders sought a winnable constituency to enable her parliamentary return.[15] Chandregowda, demonstrating loyalty to Gandhi, resigned his seat in October 1978, triggering a by-election that positioned Chikkamagaluru as a strategic opportunity due to his established rapport with voters.[16] Indira Gandhi contested the Chikkamagaluru by-election on November 12, 1978, against candidates from the Janata Party and other opponents, ultimately securing victory by a margin of over 70,000 votes, which observers attributed in part to Chandregowda's groundwork and endorsement that mobilized local support.[2] This win marked a symbolic resurgence for Congress amid national opposition dominance and bolstered Gandhi's campaign for her eventual return to power in the 1980 general elections.[6] Chandregowda's decision, while praised within Congress circles for its selflessness, reflected the party's internal dynamics of prioritizing national leadership over individual incumbency in a post-Emergency landscape marked by political realignments.[18] The episode underscored Chandregowda's role as a pragmatic operator in Karnataka politics, where regional ties could sway outcomes, though it did not immediately alter his own parliamentary trajectory as he later pursued state-level roles.[1] No formal compensation or alternative seat was publicly detailed for Chandregowda at the time, highlighting the gesture's alignment with Congress discipline under Gandhi's influence.[26]

Support for Regional Leaders

Chandregowda served as Minister for Irrigation in the cabinet of Devaraj Urs, Chief Minister of Karnataka from 1978 to 1980, reflecting his alignment with Urs's focus on rural development and backward classes empowerment.[3] When Urs split from the Indian National Congress in 1979 to form the Karnataka Kranti Ranga, Chandregowda followed him into the new party, demonstrating loyalty amid internal party factionalism following the Emergency period.[27] This transition underscored his support for Urs's regional political vision, which emphasized land reforms and welfare for marginalized communities in Karnataka.[28] In his later career with the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), Chandregowda backed B.S. Yediyurappa, a dominant figure in Karnataka BJP and multiple-term Chief Minister. As Bangalore North MP in December 2011, he joined other Karnataka BJP MPs in appealing to senior party leader L.K. Advani for Yediyurappa's reinstatement as Chief Minister after his resignation amid a mining scandal investigation.[29] His steadfast support for Yediyurappa's leadership persisted even after Yediyurappa's temporary exit from BJP in 2012 to form Karnataka Janata Paksha, leading to Chandregowda's marginalization within the party.[23] This loyalty highlighted his preference for regional strongmen over national party directives, influencing his diminished role in subsequent elections.[23]

Parliamentary Contests

D. B. Chandregowda successfully contested Lok Sabha elections on three occasions, representing different constituencies and parties over his career. His first parliamentary victory came in the 1971 general election from the Chikkamagaluru constituency as a candidate of the Indian National Congress (INC), securing a seat in the 5th Lok Sabha.[8] In the 1977 general election, conducted after the end of the Emergency period, he won re-election from Chikkamagaluru as a Janata Party candidate, reflecting the anti-Congress wave that swept the Janata coalition to power nationally.[14] However, Chandregowda resigned his seat in 1978 to enable former Prime Minister Indira Gandhi to contest the resulting by-election from the same constituency, aiding her political rehabilitation following her electoral defeat in 1977.[13] Chandregowda's third Lok Sabha term began with his victory in the 2009 general election from the Bangalore North constituency, contesting on a Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) ticket after switching from Congress in 2008.[11] He defeated the veteran INC candidate and former Union Minister C. K. Jaffer Sharief, polling 452,920 votes (45.2% of the total) against Sharief's 393,255 votes (39.3%), for a margin of 59,665 votes.[34] This win contributed to the BJP's strong performance in Karnataka during that election cycle. No records indicate additional parliamentary contests or defeats for Chandregowda.

Contributions and Criticisms

Achievements in Governance

During his tenure as Minister for Minor Irrigation in the Karnataka government from 1979 to 1980, part of D. Devaraj Urs's cabinet, Chandregowda contributed to state efforts in water resource management amid regional agricultural needs.[4][1] As Speaker of the Karnataka Legislative Assembly from 1983 to 1985, he oversaw proceedings during a phase of frequent government changes following the Janata Party era, maintaining order in debates and committee functions while representing the Janata Party after his shift from Congress.[8][1] Chandregowda served as Minister for Law and Parliamentary Affairs from 1999 to 2004 in S. M. Krishna's Congress administration, where he managed legislative coordination and, in 2003, took additional responsibility for disinvestment policies aimed at state asset reforms.[1][8][35] During this period, he defended amendments to the Land Reforms Bill in assembly debates, emphasizing controls to prevent indiscriminate land sales while responding to opposition demands for modifications.[36] His role facilitated smoother passage of government business, though specific legislative outputs like enacted reforms remain tied to broader cabinet initiatives rather than individual attributions in available records.[8]

Political Legacy and Evaluations

D. B. Chandregowda's political legacy is characterized by a five-decade career marked by adaptability across multiple parties, including the Indian National Congress, Janata Dal, and Bharatiya Janata Party, and service in all major Karnataka legislative bodies: the Legislative Assembly (three terms), Legislative Council, Lok Sabha (three terms), and Rajya Sabha.[1][37] He held key positions such as Speaker of the Karnataka Legislative Assembly from 1983 to 1985, Minister for Law and Parliamentary Affairs under Chief Minister S. M. Krishna, and Leader of Opposition in the state assembly, demonstrating a deep engagement with parliamentary processes and constitutional matters.[1] A defining act was his resignation from the Chikkamagaluru Lok Sabha seat in 1978 to facilitate Indira Gandhi's post-Emergency electoral comeback, which political observers have cited as an example of party loyalty and strategic sacrifice.[13][1] Evaluations of Chandregowda emphasize his contributions to public service and legislative experience, with Prime Minister Narendra Modi describing him as a "stalwart of public service" whose work as MP, MLA, and minister left an "indelible mark," highlighting his "deep understanding of our Constitution" and "commitment to community service."[37] Upon his death on November 7, 2023, the Karnataka Legislative Assembly paid glowing tributes, reflecting bipartisan respect for his versatility and longevity in politics.[38] Leaders across parties, including D. V. Sadananda Gowda and Siddaramaiah, offered homage, underscoring his role in regional governance.[1] However, his career also drew criticism for controversial statements, such as a 2012 assembly speech alleging Lingayat dominance over Vokkaligas post-Karnataka unification, which sparked accusations of fomenting caste divisions from within the BJP.[39] Additionally, in 2012, he faced a contempt of court petition for remarks perceived as attacking the judiciary, though the case's outcome underscored tensions between political rhetoric and judicial authority.[40] These incidents highlight evaluations of him as a forthright but occasionally divisive figure in Karnataka's caste-influenced politics.

Personal Life and Writings

Family and Personal Background

Daradahalli Byregowda Chandregowda, commonly known as D. B. Chandregowda, was born on 26 August 1936 in Daradahalli village, Mudigere taluk, Chikkamagaluru district, Karnataka, to parents D. A. Byregowda, a farmer, and Puttamma.[10][41] Raised in a rural agrarian family in the Malnad region, he grew up amidst coffee plantations and traditional Vokkaliga community values, which emphasized land ownership and local governance.[4] Chandregowda pursued higher education, earning a Bachelor of Science (B.Sc.) degree from Renukacharya College in Bangalore and a Bachelor of Laws (LL.B.) from R. L. Law College, qualifying him as an advocate by profession.[4][10] During his student years, he demonstrated early leadership by serving as vice president of the students' union at Renukacharya College, honing skills that later propelled his political career.[42] On 22 May 1966, he married B. S. Poorna (also referred to as Purna Chandregowda), with whom he had four daughters; the family resided primarily in Daradahalli and Bangalore.[1][8][7] Known for his austere personal life, Chandregowda maintained strong ties to his rural roots despite his prominence in state politics, often returning to his native village for family and community engagements.[1]

Autobiography

D. B. Chandregowda published his autobiography, titled Poornachandra (Chintanegalu Mattu Aatmakathe), in Kannada through Sapna Book House in 2012.[43] The work combines personal life narrative with reflective essays on his experiences.[44] The book was released on December 30, 2011, in Bengaluru by then-Chief Minister B. S. Yeddyurappa, coinciding with Chandregowda's 75th birthday celebrations.[45] This publication provided insights into his political journey, spanning affiliations with multiple parties including the Indian National Congress and Bharatiya Janata Party, as well as his roles as a legislator and speaker.[45]

Death and Posthumous Recognition

D. B. Chandregowda died on 7 November 2023 at his residence in Daradahalli, Mudigere taluk, Chikkamagaluru district, Karnataka, at the age of 87, due to age-related illness following a prolonged period of poor health.[1][18][9] He was survived by his wife, Poornima, and four daughters.[18][3] His funeral rites were conducted on 8 November 2023 at Poornachandra Estate in Daradahalli, his hometown, with numerous admirers and political figures paying respects at his residence prior to the ceremony.[9] Following his death, Prime Minister Narendra Modi expressed condolences, stating that Chandregowda "made rich contributions to public life and will be remembered for his efforts towards the development of Karnataka."[46] Political leaders across Karnataka, including from the BJP and other parties, mourned his passing as a significant loss to the state's political landscape, highlighting his long service as a three-term MLA, MLC, three-term Lok Sabha member, assembly speaker, and former minister.[47][48] No formal posthumous awards or honors were announced in immediate reports.
