It Shoulda Been You


It Shoulda Been You is a musical comedy with music by Barbara Anselmi and book and lyrics by Brian Hargrove.[1]
The production premiered on Broadway at the Brooks Atkinson Theatre on April 14, 2015, following 31 previews, and closed on August 9, 2015, after 135 performances.[1] Directed by David Hyde Pierce, it featured an ensemble cast including Tyne Daly as the bride's overbearing mother and Sierra Boggess as the bride.[1]
The story unfolds on the wedding day of a Jewish bride and Catholic groom, where clashing families, an intrusive ex-boyfriend, and hidden secretsâ€”ranging from past relationships to unexpected affectionsâ€”create farce-like chaos that the bride's sister ultimately resolves into multiple harmonious outcomes.[2][3] Centered on intergenerational and intercultural tensions, the 90-minute show emphasizes ensemble dynamics and comedic twists rather than innovative musical forms.[3]
Though nominated for Drama Desk, Drama League, and Outer Critics Circle Awards, the musical achieved modest box office results, grossing approximately $7.88 million at 73% capacity, reflecting a reception that praised its humor but critiqued its formulaic approach to wedding tropes.[2][4] Prior to Broadway, it debuted at the George Street Playhouse in 2011, marking its path from regional to commercial theater.[5]

Creation and Development

Origins and Creative Team

It Shoulda Been You was conceived by composer Barbara Anselmi, who also composed the music, with the book and lyrics written by Brian Hargrove.[6][1] The concept originated as a comedic exploration of family dynamics at a wedding, drawing on traditional farce elements updated for contemporary audiences. Anselmi's initial vision focused on the chaos of prenuptial tensions, particularly between two families with contrasting backgrounds, which Hargrove expanded into a full script emphasizing relational conflicts and revelations.[7] The musical received its earliest public presentation at the National Alliance for Musical Theatre's (NAMT) 21st Annual Festival of New Musicals on October 6, 2009, featuring a concert-style reading with cast members including Fred Berman, Jeremy Ellison-Gladstone, and Joe Grifasi.[8][9] This showcase marked a key developmental milestone, allowing feedback from industry professionals that refined the score and narrative structure prior to further productions. Following the NAMT reading, the work progressed to its world premiere staging at the George Street Playhouse in New Brunswick, New Jersey, running from October 4 to November 4, 2011, under the direction of Gabriel Barre.[5] David Hyde Pierce, Hargrove's husband, directed the Broadway production, marking his debut in that role on the Great White Way; rehearsals began in early 2015, with Pierce emphasizing ensemble-building exercises to foster chemistry among the cast.[10] The core creative contributions remained centered on Anselmi and Hargrove, though additional lyrics were provided by Jill Abramovitz in some iterations.[6] This collaborative foundation, spanning from conception in the mid-2000s through festival exposure and regional premiere, positioned the musical for its commercial Broadway transfer in 2015.[11]

Pre-Production Workshops

The musical received early developmental exposure through workshop presentations in 2009, allowing creators Brian Hargrove and Barbara Anselmi to test material and incorporate feedback.[12] It was featured in the National Association of Musical Theatre's (NAMT) 21st Festival of New Musicals that year, where actress Lisa Howard originated the role of Jenny in a concert-style presentation attended by theater professionals.[12] These NAMT events typically involve 15- to 30-minute excerpts followed by industry discussions, aiding script refinement without full staging. A staged reading occurred at New World Stages on October 19 and 20, 2009, presenting Hargrove's book and lyrics alongside Anselmi's music in a developmental format focused on narrative and score iteration.[13] The show also participated in Village Theatre's Festival of New Musicals in 2009, another showcase for emerging works that emphasized audience and peer input to evolve the wedding-themed comedy.[14] These sessions highlighted the musical's farcical elements amid family conflicts, setting the stage for subsequent revisions before its first full production.[15]

Broadway Premiere

Casting and Rehearsals

The principal casting for the Broadway production of It Shoulda Been You was finalized following Equity principal auditions held on September 4 and 5, 2014.[16] Key roles were filled by established performers, including Tyne Daly as the overbearing mother Judy Steinberg, Harriet Harris as the groom's snobbish mother Georgette Howard, Sierra Boggess as the bride Rebecca Steinberg, Lisa Howard as Rebecca's unmarried sister Jenny Steinberg, and David Burtka as the groom Brian Howard.[1] Supporting roles included Chip Zien as father Murray Steinberg, Montego Glover, Josh Grisetti, Edward Hibbert as officiant Rabbi/Singer, Adam Heller, and Michael X. Martin.[17] [18] Rehearsals, directed by David Hyde Pierce in his Broadway directorial debut, commenced on or about January 13, 2015, ahead of previews beginning March 17 at the Brooks Atkinson Theatre.[19] To foster ensemble cohesion on the first day, Pierce initiated proceedings with a volleyball game among the cast, a technique he adopted from prior directing experiences to encourage physical interaction and break the ice.[10] Music director Larry Yurman oversaw vocal and musical preparations, with sessions focusing on the show's comedic timing and dance elements, as evidenced by rehearsal imagery capturing actors like Boggess, Burtka, Grisetti, and Howard refining choreography and blocking.[20] The process emphasized the musical's fast-paced, ensemble-driven structure, preparing the company for its 90-minute runtime filled with wedding-day hijinks.[3]

Opening and Run Details


Previews for the Broadway production of It Shoulda Been You commenced on March 17, 2015, at the Brooks Atkinson Theatre (now the Lena Horne Theatre).[1][2] The musical, directed by David Hyde Pierce, officially opened on April 14, 2015, following those initial previews.[1][2] Opening night featured appearances by celebrities including Julianna Margulies and Neil Patrick Harris in the audience, with cast members such as Tyne Daly, Lisa Howard, and Harriet Harris taking bows.[21] Originally planned as an open-ended run, the production concluded earlier than anticipated, with producers announcing its closure on June 30, 2015.[22][23] The final performance occurred on August 9, 2015, after accumulating 31 preview performances and 135 regular performances.[1][2][24] This relatively brief run reflected challenges in sustaining audience interest amid mixed critical reception, though specific box office figures were not publicly detailed in announcements.[22][25]

Content and Structure

Plot Summary

It Shoulda Been You centers on the tumultuous wedding day of a Jewish bride and her Catholic groom, where familial and cultural tensions erupt amid preparations at the ceremony site. The bride's domineering mother exerts control over proceedings, clashing with the groom's more volatile and eccentric mother, exacerbating differences in religious backgrounds and expectations.[2][3] The bride's pragmatic older sister acts as a mediator, striving to maintain order as mishaps accumulate, including the sudden arrival of the bride's ex-boyfriend, who crashes the event with intentions to halt the marriage and reclaim his former partner. This intrusion triggers a cascade of revelations, covert alliances, and exposed secrets among the wedding party, amplifying the chaos through interpersonal conflicts and dashed assumptions.[26][2] As plots unfold involving romantic entanglements, broken pacts, and humorous confrontations, the sister navigates the unraveling situation, ultimately steering the ensemble toward an unconventional yet affirming resolution that reconciles the discord.[3][2]

Characters and Themes

The musical centers on an ensemble of characters from the clashing Steinberg and Howard families, whose interactions drive the comedic conflicts during the interfaith wedding preparations. Principal figures include Rebecca Steinberg, the poised Jewish bride in her late twenties, who grapples with familial pressures and personal secrets; her fiancÃ© Brian Howard, the affable Catholic groom facing similar doubts; and Rebecca's unmarried older sister Jenny Steinberg, a cynical bridesmaid who serves as the event's reluctant fixer amid escalating chaos.[3][18] The Steinberg parentsâ€”domineering mother Judy, a controlling force fixated on tradition, and passive father Murrayâ€”contrast sharply with the Howard side, featuring boisterous mother Ruth and understated father George, highlighting generational and cultural tensions. Supporting roles, such as the disruptive ex-boyfriend Marty Kaufman, best man Greg Madison, and officiant Delia, add layers of intrigue through hidden motives and unexpected alliances.[2][17] Key themes revolve around the friction of interfaith unions, exemplified by the Jewish-Catholic divide that amplifies petty rivalries and parental meddling, underscoring how religious and ethnic differences exacerbate wedding-day absurdities without resolving into harmony.[3] The narrative probes familial dysfunction and the tyranny of expectations, where overbearing relatives prioritize appearances over individual agency, leading to comic breakdowns in communication and loyalty.[18] Central to the plot's twist is the exploration of sexual identity and authenticity, as revelations about concealed same-sex attractions challenge heteronormative assumptions, advocating resilience in pursuing genuine relationships amid judgment.[29] These elements culminate in a farce that critiques the performative nature of matrimony, emphasizing personal truth over societal scripts, though critics noted the resolution's reliance on contrivance rather than deep causal insight into character motivations.[4]

Reception and Performance

Critical Reviews

It Shoulda Been You garnered mixed-to-negative reviews from major critics upon its Broadway opening on April 14, 2015, with frequent praise for the cast's comedic execution overshadowed by consensus criticism of the book's derivative plotting, uninspired score, and dated sensibility reminiscent of 1970s sitcoms or earlier interfaith wedding farces like Abie's Irish Rose.[4][30][31] Performances by leads such as Lisa Howard as the jilted older sister Jenny, Tyne Daly as the domineering Jewish mother, and Harriet Harris as the WASP counterpart were highlighted as salvaging laughs through timing and character work, while David Hyde Pierce's direction was credited for eliciting humor from thin material via subtle gestures and farce-friendly staging.[30][32] Charles Isherwood of The New York Times described the musical as "aggressively bubbly" and overly reliant on reality-TV-style wedding chaos, deeming the book a "crumbly meringue" that failed to elevate familiar tropes beyond superficial conflict between Jewish and Catholic families.[4] He noted the lyrics' bluntness, such as lines expressing mild prejudice against gentiles, as emblematic of the show's lack of nuance or fresh insight. In Variety, Frank Rizzo acknowledged the plot's "hackneyed" length and the score's uninspiring numbersâ€”like the rhythmic but forgettable "Take a Nap!"â€”yet called the result "awfully funny" due to the ensemble's energy, with specific acclaim for Howard's heartfelt portrayal and Sierra Boggess's vocal prowess in "A Little Bit Less Than."[30] Rizzo praised Pierce's economical direction and Anna Louizos's set design for enabling slapstick door-slamming, though he faulted the book and lyrics for scant wit beyond occasional zingers. Vulture's review conceded the cast's strengths, particularly Howard's standout turn and contributions from Daly, Harris, Boggess, and Josh Grisetti as the groom, but critiqued the 19 songs crammed into 100 minutes as sitcom-like filler that stalled the farce's momentum, with twists undermined by implausible red herrings and resistance to musical integration.[32] Pierce's understated comic timing was lauded, but the overall ambition was seen as modestly amusing rather than innovative. The Wall Street Journal's Terry Teachout labeled it a "plastic statuette for the tourist trade," alternating between cloying sentiment and coarse humor in its nice-Jewish-girl-marries-nice-Catholic-boy premise, dismissing the enterprise as formulaic and unworthy of its star power.[31]

Commercial and Audience Response

The Broadway production of It Shoulda Been You ran for 135 performances following 31 previews at the Brooks Atkinson Theatre (now the Lena Horne Theatre), opening on April 14, 2015, and closing on August 9, 2015, after failing to achieve financial viability.[33] [25] [34] Its total box office gross reached $7,881,052, with an average ticket price of $64 and total attendance of 122,504, reflecting consistent underperformance relative to the venue's capacity of approximately 1,045 seats.[33] Weekly earnings fluctuated but often fell short, such as $378,180 for the week ending May 24, 2015â€”a decline from prior weeksâ€”and capacity percentages as low as 41% in early May.[35] [36] Audience response highlighted enjoyment of the musical's comedic wedding farce elements, with performers noting frequent surprises and awkward familial dynamics that engaged viewers during the run.[37] Patrons appreciated the ensemble's delivery of humorous mishaps and character interactions, often describing the show as delightful and lighthearted despite its brevity on Broadway.[38] [39] The production's old-fashioned farce style resonated with theatergoers seeking escapist entertainment, contributing to positive word-of-mouth amid the short run.[40]

Regional Productions and Licensing

Following its Broadway run, which concluded on August 9, 2015, It Shoulda Been You became available for licensing through Music Theatre International (MTI), which secured worldwide rights for amateur, educational, and professional productions.[50][51] Licensing applications opened in spring 2016, enabling regional theaters to stage the musical with access to orchestration, vocal arrangements, and production resources provided by MTI.[3][52] Post-Broadway regional productions have included a revival at Playhouse on Park in West Hartford, Connecticut, during the 2020-2021 season.[53] In 2025, Take Two Productions mounted a staging in St. Louis, Missouri, running through October 11 at Third Baptist Church's Johnson Hall.[54] That same year, Good Theater presented the musical in Portland, Maine, from October 2 to 19 as the opener for its 22nd main stage season.[55][56] Additional regional mountings have occurred at venues such as SLOC Musical Theater in San Luis Obispo, California, highlighting the show's appeal for ensemble casts in community and mid-sized theaters.[57] Prior to Broadway, the musical received developmental regional outings, including a production at George Street Playhouse in New Brunswick, New Jersey, from October 4 to November 4, 2011, and a staging at Village Theatre in Issaquah, Washington, which informed its eventual transfer.[58][59] These early runs, combined with MTI's licensing framework, have sustained the show's presence in non-Broadway circuits, though production frequency remains modest compared to more established musicals in MTI's catalog.[3]
