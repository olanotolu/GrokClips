New Right

The New Right was a diverse coalition of American conservative activists, organizations, and voters that coalesced in the late 1960s and 1970s to counter liberal dominance in politics, culture, and institutions following the Great Society programs and countercultural upheavals.[1][2] Emphasizing free-market economics, traditional family structures, anti-communism, and resistance to federal overreach in areas like education and welfare, it fused libertarian fiscal policies with neoconservative social values, distinguishing itself from the more establishment-oriented Old Right and interventionist neoconservatives by prioritizing grassroots mobilization and cultural preservation over elite-driven reforms.[1][3] Key figures such as direct-mail pioneer Richard Viguerie, strategist Paul Weyrich, and televangelist Jerry Falwell organized entities like the Moral Majority and the Heritage Foundation to amplify opposition to abortion, school busing for desegregation, and the Equal Rights Amendment, channeling discontent into electoral power.[3][4] This movement's defining achievement was galvanizing the Republican Party's "conservative ascendancy," culminating in Ronald Reagan's 1980 presidential victory and the implementation of supply-side tax cuts, deregulation, and military buildup that correlated with economic recovery from stagflation and contributed to the Soviet Union's eventual collapse.[2][1] By registering millions of previously apolitical evangelicals and suburbanites, it expanded the conservative base, leading to lasting shifts such as the 1981 Economic Recovery Tax Act and the rise of think tanks influencing policy.[2] Controversies arose from its staunch defense of traditional norms against progressive changes, drawing accusations of intolerance from left-leaning institutions, though empirical polling data from the era showed broad public support for its stances on issues like school prayer and family values.[1] Unlike paleoconservatives' isolationism or neoconservatives' focus on global democracy promotion, the New Right balanced national sovereignty with assertive anti-communism, laying groundwork for enduring debates within conservatism over immigration, trade, and cultural identity.[5]

Definition and Ideology

Core Principles

The New Right prioritizes national sovereignty and the common good over the unfettered individualism associated with classical liberalism, arguing that the liberal order has eroded social cohesion and cultural integrity.[6] This perspective critiques fusionist conservatism for subordinating social and cultural priorities to economic libertarianism, instead advocating for state intervention to safeguard traditional values and institutional neutrality in favor of substantive conservative ends.[7] Proponents contend that liberalism's emphasis on procedural fairness has enabled progressive dominance in key institutions, necessitating a reorientation toward ordered liberty guided by the nation's historic principles.[8] Central to New Right thought is economic nationalism, which favors protectionist trade policies and industrial policies aimed at bolstering domestic manufacturing and worker welfare, departing from free-market orthodoxy to prioritize national self-sufficiency.[9] On social issues, it upholds the family as the foundational unit of society, opposing policies that undermine traditional gender roles and promoting pro-natalist measures to counter demographic decline.[10] Immigration restriction forms a key tenet, viewed as essential for preserving cultural homogeneity and preventing the dilution of national identity amid mass migration.[11] In foreign policy, the New Right espouses realism, emphasizing restraint from ideological interventions and alliances that do not directly serve American interests, while maintaining military strength to deter adversaries.[12] It rejects multiculturalism and globalism, seeking to restore a politics rooted in the American Founding's emphasis on republican virtue rather than perpetual expansion of rights.[13] This framework, articulated by thinkers associated with institutions like the Claremont Institute, posits that decisive action against bureaucratic and elite capture is required to realign governance with the people's will.[14]

Philosophical Underpinnings

The New Right's philosophical foundations rest on a post-liberal critique of Enlightenment-era individualism, proceduralism, and universalism, arguing that these principles erode communal bonds, national identity, and human flourishing by prioritizing abstract rights over concrete goods. Thinkers like Patrick Deneen contend that liberalism, while promising freedom, fosters atomization, inequality, and moral relativism, necessitating a return to traditions rooted in family, faith, and locality to orient politics toward substantive ends rather than neutral mechanisms.[15][16] This perspective draws from Aristotelian notions of the common good and classical sources emphasizing natural limits on autonomy, rejecting the unlimited self-expression celebrated in liberal thought.[16] A core strand, national conservatism, privileges particularist traditions and sovereign nations against liberal globalism, viewing the latter as a solvent for cultural cohesion and demographic stability. Proponents assert that liberalism's emphasis on open borders, free trade without restraint, and supranational institutions undermines the organic communities essential for political order, advocating instead for policies that reinforce religious and familial structures as bulwarks against ideological universalism.[17] This approach echoes critiques of modernity's destabilizing effects, prioritizing empirical observations of liberal societies' outcomesâ€”such as declining birth rates and social trustâ€”over ideological commitments to equality or progress.[17] Integralism represents a religiously inflected variant, particularly among Catholic intellectuals, positing that the state must subordinate temporal authority to spiritual truths to achieve integral human development, rather than maintaining liberalism's separation of politics from ultimate ends. Influenced by figures like Adrian Vermeule and Sohrab Ahmari, it critiques liberal neutrality as illusory and coercive, insisting that governance should promote virtue and the highest good, often through directed economic and cultural policies.[17][18] Complementary ideas from RenÃ© Girard highlight mimetic rivalry in liberal societies, where scapegoating and envy proliferate without transcendent anchors, informing calls for a Christian counterculture akin to Rod Dreher's Benedict Option.[18] Neoreactionary elements, articulated by Curtis Yarvin, extend this by rejecting democratic egalitarianism as inherently unstable, favoring hierarchical governanceâ€”potentially monarchical or CEO-ledâ€”to impose order amid liberalism's predicted entropy.[17][18] Carl Schmitt's concepts, including the friend-enemy distinction and sovereign decisionism, underpin these views by framing politics as existential conflict rather than rational consensus, critiquing liberalism's depoliticization as a path to weakness and decay.[19] Collectively, these underpinnings prioritize causal realism about power dynamics and empirical failures of liberal regimes over normative ideals, seeking restoration through unapologetic particularism and authority.[20]

Distinctions from Traditional Conservatism and Other Right-Wing Movements

Economic Divergences

The New Right critiques the free-market orthodoxy of traditional conservatism, which emphasizes deregulation, low taxes, and unrestricted global trade as drivers of prosperity, arguing instead that such policies have contributed to deindustrialization, wage stagnation for native workers, and dependence on foreign supply chains vulnerable to geopolitical risks.[21][22] Proponents contend that neoliberal globalization, embraced by fusionist conservatives since the Reagan era, hollowed out manufacturing sectors in Western nations; for instance, U.S. manufacturing employment fell from 19.5 million jobs in 1979 to 12.8 million by 2010, correlating with expanded trade agreements like NAFTA in 1994.[10][23] In response, New Right economic thought favors protectionism and industrial policy to safeguard domestic industries and prioritize citizen welfare over abstract efficiency gains. This includes tariffs to counter subsidized imports and currency manipulation, as exemplified by the Trump administration's 2018 imposition of 25% tariffs on steel and 10% on aluminum, which aimed to revive U.S. production capacity amid competition from China, where state subsidies distort markets.[23][22] Advocates like those in national conservatism circles assert that such measures pursue "economic independence" by mitigating threats from adversarial nations, contrasting with traditional conservatism's faith in comparative advantage under WTO rules.[23][24] Further divergences appear in attitudes toward labor markets and social supports, where the New Right supports restricting immigration to preserve bargaining power for native workers and low-skill wages, rejecting the traditional conservative view that open borders expand the labor pool beneficially. Some post-liberal variants endorse targeted state interventions, such as child tax credits or family subsidies to boost fertility rates and counter demographic decline, while maintaining fiscal restraint through cuts to non-citizen benefitsâ€”policies framed as reintegrating economy and state for communal stability rather than individualistic market outcomes.[22][25] Critics from libertarian-leaning conservative outlets note this shift risks cronyism and reduced innovation, but New Right thinkers counter that unchecked markets erode the social fabric prerequisite for long-term prosperity.[24][10]

Cultural and Social Priorities

The New Right emphasizes the preservation of national cultural identity and social cohesion as foundational to societal stability, arguing that unchecked multiculturalism and mass immigration undermine trust and communal bonds. Advocates contend that diverse, high-immigration environments correlate with diminished social capital, drawing on research such as Robert Putnam's findings that ethnic diversity reduces interpersonal trust and civic engagement in the short term. This perspective informs calls for immigration policies prioritizing cultural compatibility and assimilation, with figures like Yoram Hazony asserting in National Conservatism principles that nations must safeguard their distinct cultural inheritances to endure.[23] Empirical data from Europe, where non-Western immigration has risen sharplyâ€”e.g., net migration exceeding 1 million annually in the EU during 2015â€“2022â€”has been linked by proponents to parallel societies and heightened social tensions, justifying restrictions to avert demographic displacement. Central to New Right social priorities is the revitalization of the family unit, viewed as the bedrock of civilizational continuity amid plummeting fertility rates. Total fertility rates in Western nations have declined to sub-replacement levels, averaging 1.5 children per woman in the European Union and 1.6 in the United States as of 2023, per United Nations data, threatening long-term population sustainability and economic viability without immigration reliance. In response, the movement promotes pro-natalist policies, including tax credits for dependents, paid family leave, and incentives for marriage and childbearing, as articulated in National Conservatism's declaration that fostering stable family life constitutes a "priority of the highest order."[23] These measures aim to counteract causal factors like delayed marriage, career prioritization, and welfare structures that subsidize single parenthood, which correlate with child poverty rates over 20% in the U.S. for fatherless households. The New Right critiques modern progressive social norms as disruptive to organic gender roles and child welfare, advocating a return to biologically informed distinctions between sexes. This includes opposition to policies enabling transgender medical interventions for minors, citing evidence from European reviewsâ€”such as the UK's Cass Report in 2024â€”that highlight insufficient long-term data on puberty blockers and elevated regret rates post-transition. Proponents prioritize protecting traditional marriage and rejecting expansive redefinitions of family, arguing that the sexual revolution's legacy, including no-fault divorce laws enacted widely since the 1970s, has doubled U.S. divorce rates and contributed to intergenerational instability. Such positions reflect a causal emphasis on empirical outcomes over ideological abstractions, with social conservative alignments reinforcing religious and communal frameworks to sustain moral order.[23]

Foreign Policy Orientations

The New Right's foreign policy orientations diverge from traditional conservatism's neoconservative emphasis on global democracy promotion and military interventions by prioritizing national sovereignty, restraint in commitments abroad, and a realist assessment of threats centered on great-power competition rather than ideological crusades.[12] This shift reflects a rejection of post-Cold War unilateralism, favoring "America First" policies that condition alliances and aid on reciprocal benefits to the United States, as articulated in the 2017 National Security Strategy under President Trump, which emphasized sovereignty and economic security over expansive humanitarian interventions.[26] Proponents argue that endless engagements, such as those in Iraq and Afghanistan costing over $8 trillion and thousands of American lives since 2001, drained resources without yielding stable outcomes, advocating instead for selective deterrence against adversaries like China and Russia while speech that proxy wars divert from domestic priorities and risk escalation without clear national gains, prioritizing instead a balance-of-power realism over moralistic interventions.[30] While not isolationist, the New Right endorses robust deterrence, such as bolstering Indo-Pacific alliances to contain Chinaâ€”evidenced by Trump's Quad initiative expansion in 2017â€”but subordinates these to potentially emboldening aggressors like Russia in its 2022 Ukraine invasion.[32] In global variants, such as European national conservatism, similar anti-globalist stances manifest in resistance to EU Post-World War II Foundations

Following World War II, conservative intellectuals in the United States sought to rebuild a coherent right-wing worldview amid the ideological dominance of liberalism and the taint of European fascism on prior rightist traditions. The war's end left American conservatism fragmented, with many associating authority and hierarchy with defeated regimes, prompting a pivot toward emphasizing ordered liberty, anti-totalitarianism, and cultural continuity rooted in Anglo-American heritage. This era's efforts focused on theoretical reconstruction rather than immediate political power, drawing on pre-war thinkers like Edmund Burke and Alexis de Tocqueville while adapting to Cold War realities.[36][2] Pivotal contributions included Friedrich Hayek's establishment of the Mont Pelerin Society on April 1, 1947, in Switzerland, which gathered economists and philosophers to advocate free-market principles against collectivism, influencing subsequent libertarian strains within conservatism. Russell Kirk's The Conservative Mind, published in 1953, articulated a traditionalist framework by chronicling a lineage of skeptical, anti-utopian thinkers from Burke to T.S. Eliot, positing that society thrives through enduring moral imaginations rather than rationalist blueprints. These works countered progressive historicism by stressing prudence, custom, and the limits of human reason in governance.[37][38] William F. Buckley's 1951 book God and Man at Yale had already assailed academic secularism and collectivism, signaling a broader campaign against institutional leftward drifts. Frank Meyer's writings in the late 1950s promoted "fusionism," reconciling individual liberty with traditional virtue as bulwarks against both atheistic materialism and unchecked state power. This intellectual infrastructure, though initially marginal, provided the doctrinal base for later mobilizations by equipping conservatives with arguments for limited government, moral order, and vigilant opposition to Soviet expansionism.[39][2]

1970sâ€“1990s Mobilization

In the 1970s, the New Right coalesced as an insurgent conservative movement within the Republican Party, driven by opposition to federal overreach, cultural liberalism, and social changes like the Roe v. Wade decision in 1973. Key architects included Paul Weyrich, who in 1973 co-founded the Heritage Foundation as a think tank to supply policy blueprints for conservative lawmakers, funded initially by the Coors family and emphasizing free-market economics alongside traditional values. Richard Viguerie revolutionized fundraising through direct-mail campaigns, compiling massive donor lists from sources like gold-record buyers and gun owners to finance New Right causes, raising millions for anti-abortion and anti-Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) efforts. Howard Phillips and Terry Dolan complemented this by establishing the National Conservative Political Action Committee (NCPAC) in 1978, one of the first groups to deploy negative television ads against liberal incumbents.[40][41][42] This infrastructure mobilized evangelical Protestants, previously apolitical, into a voting bloc; Weyrich, recognizing their potential, coined the phrase "moral majority" and urged Jerry Falwell to form the Moral Majority organization in 1979, which by 1980 claimed 4 million members focused on electing candidates opposing abortion, pornography, and gay rights. Phyllis Schlafly's Eagle Forum and STOP ERA campaign defeated the ERA ratification in key states, framing it as a threat to family structures and women's roles. These efforts fused fiscal conservatism with social issues, distinguishing the New Right from the more establishment-oriented "Old Right" and attracting suburban middle-class supporters wary of 1960s counterculture legacies.[43][42][44] The mobilization peaked with Ronald Reagan's 1980 presidential win, where New Right groups delivered turnout among white evangelicals, contributing to his 44-state landslide; Heritage's Mandate for Leadership outlined over 2,000 policy recommendations, with Reagan implementing about 60% in his first year, including tax cuts via the Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981. Throughout the 1980s, PACs like NCPAC spent over $10 million on ads, influencing Senate races and solidifying GOP control. In the 1990s, this energy fueled the 1994 Republican Revolution under Newt Gingrich's Contract with America, which recaptured the House after 40 years of Democratic dominance, prioritizing welfare reform, term limits, and balanced budgetsâ€”echoing New Right critiques of big governmentâ€”though internal tensions with neoconservatives over trade and immigration began emerging.[42][45][44]

United States

In the United States, the New Right refers to distinct phases of conservative intellectual and political mobilization, initially fusing libertarian economics with traditionalist social values in opposition to mid-20th-century liberalism, and later evolving into a nationalist critique of globalism and cultural progressivism. This movement contrasted with the establishment Republicanism of the post-World War II era by emphasizing grassroots activism, anti-statism in economics, and resistance to social changes like expanded welfare, secularism, and family law reforms.[1] Its early iteration laid the groundwork for the Reagan Revolution, while the contemporary variant challenges neoconservative foreign policy and free-trade orthodoxy in favor of national sovereignty and demographic preservation.[109]

Economic Nationalism and Trade

The New Right's economic nationalism prioritizes national self-sufficiency and worker protections over unfettered global integration, viewing trade policy as a tool for preserving domestic manufacturing and reducing strategic vulnerabilities. Empirical studies, such as the "China Shock" research, attribute up to 2.4 million U.S. job losses between 1999 and 2011 directly to import competition from China, supporting claims that free trade's aggregate gains mask concentrated sectoral harms.[179] In practice, New Right-aligned policies emphasize tariffs and targeted protections, as exemplified by the Trump administration's 2018-2019 measures imposing 25% duties on steel and 10% on aluminum imports, alongside up to 25% tariffs on $350 billion of Chinese goods to address unfair practices.
