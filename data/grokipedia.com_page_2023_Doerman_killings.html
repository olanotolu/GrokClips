2023 Doerman killings

The 2023 Doerman killings involved the execution-style shootings of three young brothers—Clayton Doerman (7), Hunter Doerman (4), and Chase Doerman (3)—by their father, Chad Doerman, at their residence in Monroe Township, Clermont County, Ohio, on June 15, 2023.[1][2] Doerman, then 32 years old, retrieved a rifle from his vehicle, lined up the boys on the couch, read from the Bible, informed them they were "going to heaven," and fatally shot each in turn before attempting to assault his wife and stepdaughter, who escaped and alerted authorities.[1][3] He surrendered to responding deputies without resistance and confessed to the acts during interrogation.[3][4] Doerman faced initial charges including three counts of aggravated murder with death penalty specifications, along with felonious assault counts for the attacks on family members.[5] Legal proceedings featured disputes over the admissibility of his confession, citing potential violations of Miranda rights and HIPAA protections during hospital evaluation, but these were resolved via a plea agreement.[6] On August 2, 2024, Doerman pleaded guilty to the three aggravated murder counts and two felonious assaults, forgoing a trial and the death penalty in exchange for three consecutive life sentences without parole.[5][2] The motive remains undetermined, with prosecutors noting no clear precipitating factors despite extensive investigation into Doerman's history of substance abuse and prior domestic incidents.[4]

Perpetrator

Chad Doerman's early life and background

Chad Christopher Doerman was born on March 1, 1991, in Ohio.[7] His father is Keith Doerman.[8] Doerman grew up in the Clermont County region of southwestern Ohio, where he later resided as an adult in New Richmond.[9] At age 19, in 2010, he was arrested on a domestic violence charge for allegedly choking his father, though the case was dismissed later that year.[8] Doerman married Laura Hensley, with whom he formed a family unit that included three young sons by 2023.[10] No public records detail the date of their marriage or specifics of his education or early career prior to adulthood.[11]

Employment and personal history

Chad Doerman worked as an insulator, a trade involving the installation of insulation materials in industrial or construction settings.[1] No public records indicate instability or disciplinary issues in his employment history leading up to June 2023.[1] On the morning of June 15, 2023, Doerman's wife, Laura, placed a 911 call reporting that he appeared suicidal after retrieving a rifle from the home, prompting a welfare check by authorities who found no immediate threat.[12] This incident reflected acute personal distress but followed no documented pattern of prior domestic disturbances or legal interventions in available records.[1] Doerman owned multiple firearms, including a .22 caliber rifle stored at the family residence in Monroe Township, Ohio, which he accessed during the morning episode and later used in the killings.[13] Ohio law at the time required no permits for long-gun ownership or possession by non-prohibited persons, and no evidence suggests Doerman was disqualified from possessing them prior to the event.[1]

Victims and Family Context

Profiles of the victims

The victims of the 2023 Doerman killings were Clayton Doerman, aged seven; Hunter Doerman, aged four; and Chase Doerman, aged three, all biological sons of Chad Doerman and his wife Laura Doerman.[1][3] The boys resided with their parents in a home located in Monroe Township, Clermont County, Ohio, at the time of their deaths on June 15, 2023.[1] Clayton, the eldest of the three, was a first-grader who had recently completed the school year prior to the incident.[14] Little additional public information exists regarding the personal lives or activities of Hunter and Chase, who were preschool-aged and not yet enrolled in formal schooling.[4] The family had been living together in the rural Monroe Township area, where the children were part of a blended household that included other siblings from Laura Doerman's previous relationship.[1]

Family dynamics prior to the incident

Chad Doerman and Laura Doerman resided together with their three biological sons—Clayton (age 7), Hunter (age 4), and Chase (age 3)—and Laura's daughter Alexis from a prior relationship, for whom Chad served as stepfather, in a home on Laurel Lindale Road in Monroe Township, Clermont County, Ohio.[11][15] The family maintained a shared living arrangement without formal custody disputes documented prior to June 15, 2023.[16] Relations within the household showed signs of strain, with neighbors reporting that Chad Doerman frequently yelled at Laura and the children on a daily basis.[17] Acquaintances described him as possessing a volatile temper, prone to explosive arguments with Laura, likening his demeanor to "a bomb ready to explode."[17] In July 2010, Chad faced a domestic violence charge for allegedly choking his father, Keith Doerman, though the case was dismissed after Keith failed to appear as a witness.[16] This incident did not involve Laura or the children directly but reflected a pattern of familial conflict predating the immediate household.[16]

The Incident

Events leading up to the killings

On the morning of June 15, 2023, Chad Doerman deviated from his routine by stopping at a Kroger store en route to work, where he browsed the vitamins section and lingered at the Little Clinic without engaging staff before departing.[1] He returned home prematurely from his job, consumed a Bud Light, and engaged in yard work near the shed.[1] After his wife and children arrived home, Doerman shared lunch with the family and commented to his wife, "This will be my last good meal," which she later described as an indicator of suicidal intent amid ongoing domestic strains.[1] [12] He placed a call to his father, remarking without context, "Clayton is going to be the hardest one."[1] Doerman then spent time playing with the boys and reading the Bible to his four-year-old son while mumbling phrases like "Chad knows what's right."[1] [18] Doerman accessed a gun safe in the master bedroom, prompting his wife to voice concern that he was scaring her; he dismissed it by saying he was "just playing" or "just kidding."[1] [18] Perceiving him as suicidal upon seeing him handle a rifle, his wife initiated a 911 call related to potential self-harm and domestic issues.[12] Doerman intercepted the phone, threw it aside, and stated, "It's too late to save those boys."[12] Exhibiting agitation, Doerman paced the residence while clutching a Bible.[18] Approximately at 4:00 p.m., he instructed his wife and three young sons to accompany him to the master bedroom under the pretense of a nap, thereby separating them from their sister, who remained in the family room viewing television.[1] [13] In the bedroom, Doerman retrieved a Marlin Model HC .22 caliber rifle from the adjacent gun safe and inserted a magazine.[13]

Detailed sequence of the murders

On June 15, 2023, around 4 p.m., at the family home on Laurel-Lindale Road in Monroe Township, Clermont County, Ohio, Chad Doerman retrieved a Marlin Model HC .22 rifle from a safe while the family was in the master bedroom following a nap.[1][13] Doerman first shot his four-year-old son Hunter multiple times in the bedroom, prompting screams from the boys' mother, Laura Doerman, who instructed the children to run and attempted to call 911, believing initially that Doerman was suicidal.[1][13] Doerman seized the phone from Laura and stated, "It's too late," before pursuing the other children.[1] Seven-year-old Clayton fled through the back door into the yard, where Doerman shot him, causing him to fall, followed by two additional close-range shots to the head in an execution-style manner.[1][13] Meanwhile, 14-year-old stepdaughter Alexis grabbed three-year-old Chase and attempted to flee toward a nearby firehouse; Doerman caught up, demanded she set Chase down, and when the rifle jammed after running out of ammunition, he reloaded before Chase ran toward Laura in the yard.[1][13] Laura intervened by blocking the rifle barrel with her thumb to shield Chase, resulting in a gunshot wound to her hand; Doerman then bit her, wrestled Chase away, and shot the toddler once in the head at close range.[1][13] In total, Doerman fired nine shots across the incident, with the killings occurring in execution-style fashion as described by prosecutors based on witness accounts and forensic evidence.[13][19]

Immediate response and arrest

Deputies from the Clermont County Sheriff's Office responded to a 911 call from the boys' mother, Laura Doerman, reporting that her husband, Chad Doerman, had shot their three sons on June 15, 2023, in Monroe Township, Ohio.[20] Upon arrival at the residence around 4:18 p.m., body camera footage captured deputies approaching Doerman, who was seated calmly outside the home adjacent to a .22-caliber rifle used in the shootings, with the victims' bodies nearby in the yard.[21] [22] Doerman complied with deputies' commands to raise his hands and drop the weapon, allowing for his arrest without incident or further resistance; he was immediately taken into custody and transported to the Clermont County Jail.[23] [24] Concurrently, emergency medical services arrived to treat Laura Doerman, who had sustained a non-life-threatening gunshot wound to her hand while attempting to intervene, and to pronounce the three boys deceased at the scene after confirming no vital signs.[20] The couple's 9-year-old daughter, who had fled unharmed to a neighbor's home during the incident, was located and evaluated by responders.[25]

Legal Proceedings

Initial charges and indictment

Following his arrest on June 15, 2023, Chad Doerman was initially charged with nine counts of aggravated murder in connection with the shootings of his three sons.[26] These charges stemmed from prosecutorial filings in Clermont County Common Pleas Court, reflecting the deliberate and execution-style nature of the killings as described in police reports and witness statements.[27] The Clermont County Prosecutor's Office indicated that the case qualified for capital punishment eligibility under Ohio law, citing factors such as the murder of multiple victims under age 13 and the offender's prior calculation and design.[28] On June 22, 2023, a Clermont County grand jury formally indicted Doerman on 21 felony counts, expanding the accusations to include the initial nine counts of aggravated murder—each incorporating specifications for prior calculation, multiple victims, and killings during felonies—along with eight counts of kidnapping and four counts of felonious assault.[26] [27] The kidnapping charges alleged restraint of the victims and the boys' mother during the incident, while felonious assault counts addressed serious physical harm inflicted, including on the surviving family members.[29] Doerman, who had confessed to investigators immediately after his apprehension, was held without bond at the Clermont County Jail pending further proceedings.[28]

Defense considerations and plea deal

Doerman's defense team initially pursued a not guilty by reason of insanity plea, entered on March 25, 2024, citing his claims of auditory hallucinations and delusions, including voices instructing him to kill his sons to prevent future harm.[30] [31] This strategy followed his indictment on 21 counts, including nine of aggravated murder, and was supported by court-ordered psychological evaluations to assess his mental state at the time of the June 15, 2023, killings.[32] Two psychiatric experts examined Doerman and concurred that he had experienced auditory command hallucinations prior to the offense, though the evaluations did not conclusively establish legal insanity under Ohio law, which requires proof that a severe mental disease or defect rendered him unable to know the wrongfulness of his actions.[33] Competency hearings in early 2024 addressed Doerman's fitness to stand trial and face potential capital punishment, with defense motions seeking to exclude the death penalty based on his mental health history.[34] These proceedings delayed the trial from its original timeline into late 2025, as evaluations confirmed Doerman's competence to proceed despite reported symptoms, allowing him to assist in his defense and understand proceedings.[35] The insanity defense faced evidentiary challenges, including a suppressed confession due to Miranda rights violations, but prosecutors maintained that Doerman's actions demonstrated premeditation and awareness.[32] On August 2, 2024, Doerman withdrew the insanity plea and entered guilty pleas to three counts of aggravated murder and two counts of felonious assault, as part of a negotiated agreement with Clermont County prosecutors that waived the death penalty in favor of three consecutive life sentences without parole.[36] [37] This shift avoided a protracted trial where mental health claims might not have succeeded, given prior competency findings and the strength of physical evidence, such as witness accounts and ballistic matches.[4] The plea was accepted after judicial verification of Doerman's voluntary understanding, reflecting a strategic pivot to mitigate the harshest penalty amid uncertain insanity outcomes.[38]

Trial avoidance and sentencing

On August 2, 2024, Chad Doerman entered a guilty plea to three counts of aggravated murder and two counts of felonious assault during a hearing in Clermont County Common Pleas Court, thereby avoiding a full jury trial.[39][3] The plea agreement, negotiated with Clermont County Prosecutor Mark Tekulve, removed the death penalty from consideration in exchange for Doerman waiving his right to contest the charges at trial.[40] Prosecutors cited the robustness of physical evidence—including body camera footage, witness statements, and Doerman's own admissions—as factors diminishing the viability of an insanity defense, which had been anticipated but deemed unlikely to succeed given the premeditated nature of the acts documented in the record.[40][4] Judge Richard Ferenc imposed an immediate sentence of three consecutive terms of life imprisonment without the possibility of parole, ensuring Doerman's incarceration for the remainder of his life.[3][41] The plea and sentencing concluded the legal proceedings without the need for prolonged evidentiary presentations or jury deliberations, which prosecutors indicated would have extended the process significantly due to the volume of forensic and testimonial material.[40] During the hearing, victim impact statements from Doerman's former wife, Laura Doerman, and his stepdaughter were presented through a court advocate, detailing the ongoing effects on the surviving family members.[42][14] Doerman remained silent throughout these presentations but displayed visible emotional response, including tearing up, as the statements concluded.[42] The agreement's finality was further underscored by the prosecutor's post-hearing statement that it spared the victims' mother additional courtroom testimony and appeals, while guaranteeing permanent removal of Doerman from society.[4][43] No appeals on the plea itself were permitted under the terms, marking the resolution of the case in state court.[44]

Motivations and Analyses

Doerman's claimed explanations

Doerman confessed during post-arrest interrogation to having planned the murders of his sons Clayton, Hunter, and Chase for several months prior to carrying them out on June 15, 2023, stating that the anticipation caused him insomnia.[4][45] In the same questioning, he invoked a biblical rationale, declaring, "The Bible says you kill your firstborn, you kill your second born, you kill your third born but first you kill your wife. I didn’t kill my wife," positioning the act as aligned with scriptural commands while noting his decision to spare his wife.[45] In a recorded jail telephone conversation with his mother shortly after his arrest, Doerman asserted full recollection of the events, including "all the reasons why," though he did not elaborate further on those motivations in the call.[4] He also referenced media coverage of the case in a call with his brother, remarking, "Hey, they thought Hitler was big, dude," but provided no additional context tying it to his actions.[4] Prior to the shootings, Doerman paced the family home while carrying a Bible and repeatedly mumbling, "Chad knows what's right," suggesting a self-justifying conviction in the necessity of his impending actions.[18] Prosecutors have described the underlying motive as ultimately indeterminate based on Doerman's accounts, with his statements emphasizing premeditation and selective religious interpretation but lacking comprehensive causal detail.[4] These claims emerged in contexts later challenged for procedural irregularities, including a court ruling deeming his interrogation confession inadmissible due to Miranda rights violations.[46]

Psychological and forensic evaluations

Doerman's defense team retained two expert psychologists who evaluated his mental state on June 15, 2023, the date of the killings, concluding that he suffered from a severe mental disease at the time.[47] These defense-commissioned assessments, cited in a motion to bar the death penalty, diverged on whether the condition impaired his appreciation of the wrongfulness of his actions, forming the basis for an initial not guilty by reason of insanity plea entered March 25, 2024.[47] [31] Prosecutors responded by requesting a court-ordered psychological examination to assess the viability of the insanity defense under Ohio law, which requires proof that the defendant, due to mental disease or defect, lacked substantial capacity to appreciate the wrongfulness of their conduct or conform to the law.[30] Public records reveal no prior documented mental health treatment or diagnoses for Doerman that would indicate chronic psychosis or instability predating the incident.[48] Toxicology results from the arrest were not publicly detailed, but investigative accounts describe no observable impairment from substances, with Doerman's actions—including retrieving a .22-caliber rifle, systematically pursuing and shooting the victims, and providing a coherent confession—aligning with premeditated intent rather than acute delirium.[45] Forensic reconstruction of the crime scene, based on witness statements, bullet trajectories, and Doerman's own admissions of planning the acts "for several months," affirmed deliberate, sequential executions: the boys were lined up or chased individually in the yard, with shots fired at close range to the head and torso, inconsistent with impulsive or hallucinatory behavior.[45] Doerman demonstrated competence to stand trial throughout proceedings, engaging in hearings, waiving speedy trial rights, and entering a guilty plea to three counts of murder on August 2, 2024, which implicitly rejected pursuing the insanity claim to trial.[49] [50] This outcome underscores that no evaluation established legal insanity sufficient to override criminal responsibility, prioritizing empirical indicators of volitional control over subjective claims of delusion.[51]

Broader interpretations and debates

Doerman's guilty plea to aggravated murder charges in August 2024, forgoing an insanity defense, has bolstered arguments emphasizing his full personal culpability, as it reflected an acknowledgment of deliberate intent without legal exoneration via mental defect.[4] Prosecutors highlighted evidence of premeditation, including Doerman's statements like "this will be my last good meal" prior to the acts and his methodical execution-style killings, which demonstrated awareness of wrongfulness rather than delusional compulsion.[52] Legal analysts, such as Vinnie Politan, have critiqued post-act claims of mental illness as strategically invoked to evade harsher penalties, noting that inconsistent narratives between Doerman's confessions to authorities and evaluations by doctors undermined assertions of impaired volition.[53] Debates persist over whether external factors, such as potential familial stressors or undiagnosed conditions, contributed to causation without absolving agency. Reports of prior domestic disturbances in the household, including neighbor accounts of frequent yelling and a 2010 charge against Doerman for choking his father, have prompted speculation about underlying relational breakdowns exacerbating tensions, though no direct causal link to the killings was established in court proceedings.[54] Substance influences appear unsubstantiated, with no verified evidence of acute intoxication or chronic abuse at the time, despite broader discussions in forensic psychology of how such factors can impair but not eliminate rational choice in similar filicide cases.[55] Critiques of mental health narratives in this context argue they risk diluting accountability by framing extreme violence as primarily symptomatic rather than volitional, particularly when evaluations fail to prove legal insanity—defined in Ohio as inability to appreciate wrongfulness or conform conduct to law.[31] Doerman's abandonment of the insanity plea after initial filings in March 2024 aligns with this view, as forensic assessments did not yield findings sufficient to negate mens rea, reinforcing first-principles emphasis on individual agency in causal chains of such acts over speculative pathologies.[5] Proponents of stricter accountability counter that invoking unproven delusions post-facto mirrors patterns in family annihilations, where perpetrators retain capacity for planning, as evidenced by Doerman's retrieval of a rifle and targeting specific victims.[56]

Aftermath and Impact

Surviving family's statements and recovery

During Chad Doerman's plea hearing on August 2, 2024, his ex-wife Laura Doerman delivered a victim impact statement through a court victim advocate, describing the profound ongoing grief experienced by her and surviving stepdaughter Alexis. She stated that their home, once filled with the boys' laughter, is now characterized by "silence and emptiness," and emphasized the persistent daily suffering from the loss of their future together, noting, "Grief will never go away, as it is all the love that is left with no place to go."[42][14] Laura expressed agreement with the plea deal resolution, which imposed three consecutive life sentences without parole, as it provided "some finality" to the criminal proceedings and ensured Doerman's lifelong incarceration, deemed "best for my family" despite no punishment restoring the lives of Clayton, Hunter, and Chase. She urged remembrance of the boys not solely for the tragedy but for their joyful pursuits like fishing, go-carting, and swimming, affirming her enduring love for them.[14] Alexis, then 15, submitted a written statement read in court, recounting her prior trust in Doerman as a father figure and the irreplaceable loss of family traditions such as Christmas with her brothers. She conveyed unresolved pain while resolving to honor their memory positively, stating, "I will never forgive you, but I will not hate you."[42] Public details on the family's recovery remain limited, with Laura highlighting Alexis's resilience throughout the process but noting no alleviation of their collective mourning, which continues daily. No specific relocations, therapeutic supports, or civil proceedings have been disclosed in available reports following the sentencing.[14]

Community reactions and media coverage

The Monroe Township and broader Clermont County communities expressed profound shock and grief following the June 15, 2023, killings, with residents describing the events as a nightmare unfolding in their quiet suburb. Neighbors reported witnessing the immediate aftermath, including the sight of the victims' bodies and the mother's distress, leading to widespread trauma among locals who characterized the scene as unforgettable horror. Youth baseball coaches remembered the victims—Clayton (7), Hunter (4), and Chase (3) Doerman—as loving, funny, and full of life, emphasizing their supportive sibling bonds and active participation in community sports.[57][58][59] In response, the community organized memorials to honor the boys, including a candlelight vigil at Monroe Elementary School—attended by the oldest victim—and a celebration of life ceremony on June 26, 2023, where family and residents gathered to celebrate the children's lives amid collective mourning. These events featured grief counseling and tributes highlighting the boys' joyful personalities, reflecting a communal effort to process the loss without delving into speculative causes. No organized protests or policy advocacy emerged prominently from local groups, with focus remaining on remembrance rather than broader activism.[60][61][62] National and local media coverage centered on the killings' brutality, frequently describing the acts as "execution-style" shootings carried out methodically with a .22 rifle in the victims' home, drawing from police bodycam footage and 911 calls released shortly after. Outlets like FOX19 and WCPO detailed graphic elements, such as the perpetrator's calm demeanor post-act and the mother's pleas, while later reports incorporated court-released documents revealing preceding behaviors like mumbling and Bible reading, though motives remained officially unclear. Coverage patterns highlighted forensic and psychological angles without uniform consensus, with some analyses attributing the incident to acute mental distress rather than external factors, amid limited ties to national debates on firearm access or domestic violence prevention specific to this case. Mainstream reporting, while fact-based, occasionally amplified sensational details from official sources, reflecting standard crime journalism practices but with restraint compared to high-profile mass shootings.[63][64][65]

Long-term legal and societal implications

Following the August 2, 2024, sentencing to life imprisonment without parole, no appeals have been filed by Chad Doerman's defense team as of October 2025, effectively finalizing the outcome and sparing the surviving family prolonged litigation.[5][66] The plea agreement, which exchanged guilty pleas to three counts of aggravated murder for removal of the death penalty, illustrates ongoing tensions in Ohio's handling of capital cases, where such deals expedite resolution but can limit victim impact testimony and jury involvement in sentencing.[37] Prosecutors justified the arrangement by noting Doerman's inevitable death in custody, avoiding the multi-year appeals process typical of death row convictions, which averaged over 15 years in Ohio prior to recent reforms.[66] Critics, including some victims' advocates, argue such bargains undermine public accountability in filicide cases, potentially eroding deterrence by substituting execution with incarceration amid Ohio's 1.5% execution rate for capital sentences since 1999.[3] On a societal level, the Doerman killings highlight empirical patterns in paternal filicide, where biological fathers account for approximately 40-50% of such acts in studied populations, with children under age 5 facing the highest per capita risk—peaking at 2.5 per 100,000 infants annually in U.S. data.[67] Causal factors, drawn from forensic reviews, frequently include untreated severe mental disorders like psychosis or substance dependence, present in over 60% of paternal perpetrators, rather than isolated stressors, underscoring the need for proactive screening in high-conflict family settings without presuming broader ideological drivers.[68] This case has prompted localized discussions on enhancing child welfare protocols for parental custody evaluations, though no statewide policy shifts have materialized by 2025.[69]
