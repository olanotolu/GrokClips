Les Ulis

1 French Land Register data, which excludes lakes, ponds, glaciers > 1 km2 (0.386 sq mi or 247 acres) and river estuaries. Les Ulis (French pronunciation: [lez‿ylis] ⓘ) is a commune in the Essonne department, 23 km (14 mi) from the centre of Paris, France. A planned community, it comprises outer suburbs (banlieues) built in the 1970s on the southwestern edge of the Paris metropolitan area. In 2022, it had a population of 25,633.

Location

Les Ulis is a new town located in the Île-de-France, in the south-west of the Parisian agglomeration and in the north-west of the Essonne department. It sits on the Courtabœuf plateau which dominates the Yvette valley, also known as the Chevreuse valley. It is 23 km to the south-west of central Paris, 20 km north-west of Évry and 14 km to the south-east of Versailles.

Geography

The town is laid out in the form of a U, approximately three kilometres by two kilometres, and covers 518 hectares. As of 2008, over 65% of the area was built-up, 17% was semi-rural and 16% was either rural or comprised communal green space.[3] The French IGN gives the co-ordinates of the town as 48°40′56″N 2°10′11″E at the central point. The source of the minor river Rouillon (river) is in the neighbouring town of Villejust, close to the communal border. The lowest point of the town, at the point where it meets Bures-sur-Yvette, is at an altitude of 87 m. Most of the town is situated at an average altitude of over 150 m. Its highest point is at 170 m. The land on which it is built consists of clay, sand and millstone. The town is divided into distinct neighbourhoods by the main roads running through it. To the east is a large industrial zone. To the west is the residential area, a large part of which consists of 33 HLM tower blocks, grouped into four neighbourhoods. The town was conceived and built in the 1970s according to the precepts of Le Corbusier by architects Robert Camelot, François Prieur and Georges-Henri Pingusson. Its tower blocks are raised on slabs so that they are accessed above street level by means of pedestrian walkways. The town incorporates a large urban park, to the north.

Transport

Line B of the RER has a branch serving the Chevreuse valley area (terminating at Saint-Rémy-lès-Chevreuse), and local bus services provide access to RER stations at Orsay, Bures-sur-Yvette, and Massy-Palaiseau. In April 2025, the local bus network was reorganized, replacing previous lines (2, 21, 22, 23) with new lines offering 30% more service, extended hours until 1:30 AM, and improved connections to these stations and the Courtaboeuf area.[4] Les Ulis finds itself at a significant meeting point of motorways. The most significant of these, the route nationale 118, meets the route nationale 104 at an interchange on the communal border with the town of Marcoussis.

History

The discovery in 1977 of Bronze Age tools in the neighbouring town of Villejust suggests the possibility of pre-historic human occupation of the area that is today Les Ulis. During the Roman era, villas rustica occupied the Courtabœuf plateau. One, the "Villa Bona" gives its name to nearby Villebon-sur-Yvette. The area was at one time wooded, but was cleared during the Middle Ages by ecobuage (slash-and-burn clearance). The name Les Ulis is attested as As Usleiz in 1231, derived from the Latin ustulare ("to burn"), via Old French usler, referring to clearance by fire; it is first recorded as the fief of Ullys in a monastic inventory of 1382. In 1712, this land is recorded as belonging to Charles Boucher d'Orsay, an important Parisian civil servant. In 1767, it was transferred to Count Pierre Gaspard Marie Grimod d'Orsay. A significant farm, the Grand Vivier, occupied much of the land. It raised cattle and sheep and cultivated wheat, potato, strawberry and beet. There was also a distillery on the land. The Ligne de Sceaux railway opened in 1867, passing just to the west of the present-day town. The development of the Courtabœuf industrial park was decided on in 1960, accompanied on 30 November that year by a decision to create a priority development zone for ten thousand homes to sit alongside it. 265 hectares of land comprising four farms, previously belonging to the communes of Bures-sur-Yvette and Orsay, were set aside for this housing project. On 13 March 1964, an urban district of Bures-Orsay was established to oversee the development of the area. Road and utilities for the industrial zone were completed by July 1966, with the first business, AtoChem (part of what is now Total S.A.), moving in the following year. Hewlett-Packard created its French headquarters in the industrial zone in 1968. In the same year, the first residents moved into Les Ulis, although running water was not yet available in all homes. The first municipal council for Les Ulis was elected in 1971. In 1973, a shopping centre, Ulis 2 , was opened under the auspices of Carrefour. A census of 1975 records 20,283 people living in Les Ulis. Because this was significantly greater than the populations of both Bures-sur-Yvette and Orsay, which shared administrative responsibility for the town, a referendum was held on 14 March 1976 in Bures-sur-Yvette and Orsay to decide between three options: maintaining the urban district; merging Bures-sur-Yvette and Orsay; or creating a new commune for Les Ulis. A majority of voters (52%, or 4,956 votes) favored the last option. Both municipal councils voted in favor of the creation of a new commune for Les Ulis. The Prefect of Essonne, Paul Cousseran, created Les Ulis as the 196th commune of the department on 17 February 1977. In March 1977, Paul Loridant was elected mayor of Les Ulis, whilst the town remained in construction. He oversaw the building of a town hall, a market, a hybrid library and a cultural centre, named after the writer Boris Vian. In 1982, the population of Les Ulis was 28,223. In 1985, it became a canton.

Les Ulis Notable People

Notable people

  • Retired football player Thierry Henry, who played for the France national team, was born and raised in Les Ulis.
  • French footballer Anthony Martial, formerly of Manchester United, grew up in Les Ulis and played for the local football team CO Les Ulis as a child.[5]
  • Footballer Patrice Evra, who played for the France national team, was raised in Les Ulis.
  • The former spokesman of the ecological French party, Stéphane Pocrain, ran for election in the constituency including Les Ulis (with the other towns of Gif-sur-Yvette, Orsay etc.) in 2002.[6]
  • Supermodel Noémie Lenoir was born in Les Ulis before her family moved first to Gif-sur-Yvette and then to Versailles where she was discovered and started her career in modeling.
  • Writer Patrick Lapeyre (author of the award-winning L'homme-sœur) was a teacher in Les Ulis' high school (Lycée de l'Essouriau).
  • Rapper Sinik, was born in central Paris, moved to Les Ulis at age four.
  • Rapper Gambino LaMG was born and raised in Les Ulis.
  • Gilbert Bayiha N'Djema, footballer
  • Tristan Dingome, footballer
  • Moussa Marega, footballer
  • Abdoulaye Cissoko, footballer
  • Patrice Vigier, self-taught luthier and founder of Vigier Guitars, was originally from Les Ulis.[7]

Economy

One of the largest tertiary business parks in Europe, the Parc d'Activité de Courtabœuf (area: 376 ha) hosts approximately 1,800 companies employing around 25,000 people (as of 2023). [8] This area is spread over three communes: Les Ulis (mainly), Villebon-sur-Yvette and Villejust. Major companies such as Hewlett-Packard (with a research center) and Ipsen have facilities in this area. [9] In 2023, a 10-year partnership project was launched to renovate the park, improving mobility, services, and attractiveness. [10]
