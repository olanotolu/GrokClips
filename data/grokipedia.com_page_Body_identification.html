Body identification

Body identification is a subfield of forensic science that uses a variety of scientific and non-scientific methods to identify deceased individuals. Visual identification by family members or acquaintances is a common initial step when the remains are recognizable and intact, often preceding or complementing scientific forensic identification techniques.[1] If a body is not badly decomposed or damaged, one or more persons who knew the deceased well can visually confirm their identity.[1] Authorities will also compare supporting materials such as personal effects, photographs, or identification documents found with the body before accepting a visual identification.[3] Scientific evidence is used to support any formal identification, allowing forensic scientists to reinforce or question the proposed identity of the deceased.[4] These methods are essential in cases where visual approaches are not feasible. Identification techniques, including anthropometry, skin analysis, dental records, and genetics, rely on the unique characteristics of each individual.[4] Factors such as body size, weight, skin patterns, and blood type serve as indicators of identity. Forensic scientists analyze these characteristics during the identification process.[4] This generally involves comparing antemortem dataâ€”such as records from relatives or missing persons reportsâ€”with postmortem data from the unidentified remains.[4]

History

Traditional scientific identification methods developed in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries which allowed forensic scientists to identify a body without formal identification. These methods included dental analysis, anthropometry and fingerprinting. Forensic dentistry was first used in 1776 by Paul Revere, who identified the fallen soldier Joseph Warren by his false teeth.[5] Anthropometry was first introduced in 1879 by Alphonse Bertillon, who developed the Bertillon System based on physical measurements.[6] His findings were overtaken by the method of fingerprinting in the 1880s.[7] Sir Francis Galton's observations of fingerprints as a means of identification proved to be more accurate.[8] Modern scientific identification techniques developed in the late twentieth century in response to the advancement of technology and research. These methods included DNA profiling. Alec Jeffreys developed DNA profiling in 1984, which was first applied in forensic cases in 1985â€“1987 and later to body identification, such as the 1990 Scandinavian Star ferry disaster and the 1991 Karen Price case.[9][10][11] Since then, DNA examination has become popular in the forensic identification field.[9]

Military

Many service members who die in combat remain unidentified due to the destructive nature of warfare and delays in recovery.[12] If unidentified remains are returned to their country, they receive formal handling in accordance with military protocols to honor the deceased.

United States

In the United States, the Department of Defense Mortuary Affairs Program oversees the recovery, identification, transportation, and disposition of service members' remains, as outlined in DoD Instruction 1300.29 (as of 2021).[13] Remains are evacuated to theater mortuary points and then to the Port Mortuary at Dover Air Force Base, Delaware, for processing and forensic examination by the Armed Forces Medical Examiner System. An active-duty escort accompanies the remains from the port mortuary to the final destination.[13] For historical cases, the Defense POW/MIA Accounting Agency (DPAA) conducts recovery and identification efforts using advanced forensic techniques.[12] Once identified, remains are provided to the family for burial with full military honors, accompanied by members of the service branch. Unidentified remains may be held for up to 48 months for further identification attempts or interred as unknowns.[13]

Traditional Research Methods

Anthropometry

Anthropometry involves examining the size, weight and dimensions of a body.[14] Analysis of the physical aspects can facilitate possible identification before any further scientific procedures take place.[14] This can reduce unnecessary expenses spent on scientific technology. If the results from anthropometric testing are not sufficient, these scientific methods will be employed to increase the accuracy of their identification process.[14]

Alphonse Bertillon

Alphonse Bertillon developed the Bertillon System in 1879.[7] This system of identifying a body has three dimensions; anthropometric information, descriptive information and the description of particular marks.[6] The combination of these categories creates a picture of the individual, which scientists attempt to match with their records.[6]

Anthropometric Information

Anthropometric information incorporates the measurement of different body parts, including the head, fingers, feet and arms.[6] The process of obtaining anthropometric information involves the use of different tools.[6] Calliper-compasses are used to measure the dimensions of the head.[6] Sliding compasses are used to measure the "foot, forearm, and middle and little fingers".[6] Small sliding compasses are used to measure the ear.[6] A vertical measure is used to measure for height and a horizontal measure is used to measure for wingspan.[6]

Descriptive Information

Descriptive information includes aspects such as eye colour, hair colour and the structure of the nose.[6] These characteristics can provide forensic scientists with indicators of identity, however, it is possible to find two individuals who have the same or similar anthropometric and descriptive information.[7] Finding peculiarities in the human body, such as particular marks, allows forensic scientists to narrow down their identification process.[6]

Description of Particular Marks

The human body has particular marks that provide more specific knowledge to forensic scientists who are attempting to determine the identity of a body. The description of particular marks involves the assessment of unique marks on the body, such as scars and birthmarks.[6] The marks of an individual are characterised by its "nature, direction, dimensions and situation".[6]

Influences on Anthropometry

The process of anthropometry can be impacted by factors that influence the perception of a body, such as sex and gender. The determination of sex is one of the first steps in identifying an individual.[14] The physical differences between the standard male and female bodies act as an indicator of identity in the forensic field.[15] Particular body parts, such as an individual's reproductive organs and the size of their breasts, are an indicator of sex.[15] Other more socially constructed ideas of gender, such as the length of oneâ€™s hair and the height of an individual, also influence the process of body identification. These assumptions about gender are more complicated in our contemporary society, where intersex and transgender individuals are becoming increasingly common.[15]

Skin

The skin provides forensic scientists with ways to identify a body.

Skin Prints

The skin has a variety of prints that are unique to the individual. Fingerprints are the most common form of print analysis in the process of body identification.[8] The analysis of palm prints is similar to that of fingerprints. However, they also provide information on the dominant hand and age of the individual, which are both key indicators of identity.[16] The hand which has the more deteriorated prints is suggested to be the dominant hand of an individual due to its more frequent use.[16] The size of one's hand can suggest the possible age range of an individual.[16] Ear prints can also be assessed in the process of body identification.[16] Ear printing is the least common method of print analysis due to the ear's malleable nature.[16] The accuracy of skin prints can be influenced by a variety of external factors, such as temperature, humidity, time and â€œnatural skin shedding.â€ These components are considered when using skin prints as a form of identification. If there is any doubt, further scientific research will take place.

Skin Defects

The skin can have defects that help identify a body, including scars, birthmarks, tattoos, moles and blemishes.[16] The nature and location of these defects are particular to the individual.

Age

The deterioration of skin over time is physically obvious to the eye.[16] A young and fit individual typically has firm and thick skin. However, when individuals age, particularly into their 60s and 70s, their skin is subject to sagging and thinning.[16] The appearance of oneâ€™s skin can be an indicator of their age, which is a significant characteristic of a personâ€™s identity.[16] Sun exposure and lifestyle choices are additional factors that forensic scientists consider alongside age when analysing the appearance of an individual's skin.[18]

Gender

Gender also affects the perception of one's skin. Common cultural and social constructs can influence a forensic scientistâ€™s determination of gender.[16] These include the expectations of facial and body hair and the length of oneâ€™s nails.[16] These factors are used in conjunction with other scientific identification methods due to its socially constructed nature.[16]

Race

An individual's race can also act as an indicator of identity. The colour of oneâ€™s skin is a visual identifier of their race.[16] These assumptions are reinforced by supportive identification documents and other scientific methods.

Dental

Dental examination is a method of body identification that involves the comparison of antemortem and postmortem dental records, such as radiographs and photographs.[19] The jaw is analysed to detect any peculiarities in the teeth or any diseases.[19] In the scenario where skin prints are unable to assist in the process of identification, dental examination can be used. The success of dental identification can be tainted if the individual has endured a physically traumatic experience that has caused damage to their teeth and jaw.[20] In this scenario, DNA identification is used in the process of body identification.

Modern Research Methods

Genetics

Alec Jeffreys is known as the "founding father of DNA identification".[9] He invented DNA fingerprinting in the 1980s to assist in the process of body identification.[9] Since then, the method of DNA typing in forensic science has advanced.[21] DNA analysis involves the comparison of DNA profiles and DNA samples.[21]

DNA profiling

The process of DNA profiling includes DNA extraction, DNA quantification and the use of PCR technology.[21]

DNA extraction

DNA can be extracted from a variety of samples, but in cases of body identification, they mostly encounter human remains and teeth, which are more resistant to damage and degradation than hair, blood and body tissues.[22] Common methods of DNA extraction include Phenol, Chelex, Silica, and Magnetic beads.[21] The Phenol process is toxic and is "not open to automation".[21] This method is primarily used to extract, from the cells, the nucleic acids necessary for purification.[23] The Chelex process is safe and is "not open to automation".[21] This method connects ions in order to "purify other compounds".[24] This is also the cheapest method.[21] The Silica process is safe and is "open to automation".[21] This method binds DNA molecules with "silica surfaces".[25] This is also the most expensive method.[21] The magnetic beads process is safe and is "open to automation".[21] Following the binding of DNA, this process involves a magnetic field paralysing beads and allowing for the washing of DNA.[26]

DNA quantification and PCR technology

The extracted DNA must be quantified to â€œensure the optimal amount of DNA template is added to a PCRâ€.[21] PCR, Polymerase chain reaction, is the technology used for the purpose of copying particular DNA in a test tube.[27] This method includes three steps; denaturation, annealing, extension.[28]

DNA markers

DNA markers are specific genetic variants (genotypes), such as short tandem repeats (STRs) or single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), that vary between individuals and enable distinction.[29] These can be used for direct genotype matching in identification or, in DNA phenotyping, to predict phenotypic traits like appearance or ancestry.[21] A genotype is the set of genes in an organism and a phenotype is an organismâ€™s characteristics that are determined by its genes and environment.[30]

Next-Generation Sequencing

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) is an advanced body identification method in the field of genetics.[21] The process of NGS includes three fundamental steps; â€œlibrary preparation, sequencing, and data interpretationâ€.[21] Its success is due to its ability to â€œtarget a larger number of PCR amplicons in a single assayâ€.[21]

Recent advances in forensic genetics

As of 2025, forensic genetics has seen further developments beyond traditional STR profiling. Forensic DNA Phenotyping (FDP) uses SNPs to predict externally visible characteristics, such as eye color, hair color, skin tone, and biogeographic ancestry, aiding identification when reference samples are unavailable.[31] Long-read sequencing technologies, including third-generation methods like PacBio and Oxford Nanopore, improve analysis of degraded or complex samples, such as those from mass disasters or ancient remains, by providing longer DNA reads that resolve structural variants missed by NGS.[32] Additionally, mRNA profiling integrates body fluid identification with individual genotyping using multiplex panels.[33]

Genetic Genealogy

Genetic Genealogy has been used to identify deceased individuals, unknown suspects as well as living individuals.[34] This method uses Genomics, Computer database technologies, Genealogy and finally Forensic DNA profiling to identify an unknown individual.[34] They start by determining the individual's DNA profile using Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) which is then uploaded onto one or more DNA databases able to match relatives.[34] Some commonly used databases include GEDmatch, FamilyTreeDNA (FTDNA) and D2C databases.[34] Once a relative is identified, closer relatives are better (3rd cousin or closer), genealogists can then determine the family tree using publicly available information, such as birth certificates, death certificates, marriage certificates, obituaries and more.[34] This provides a list of possible candidates that can then be confirmed using Forensic DNA profiling.[34] In cases of deceased individuals, this is usually done through kinship testing with a close living relative.[34] However, genetic genealogy raises significant ethical and privacy concerns, including the use of consumer DNA databases without explicit consent from all relatives, potential for familial searching without warrants, and risks of data misuse. As of 2025, its rapid adoption has outpaced regulatory frameworks, leading to ongoing legal debates and calls for oversight.[35][36]
