2023 Imo State gubernatorial election

The 2023 Imo State gubernatorial election was an off-season poll held on 11 November 2023 to elect the governor of Nigeria's Imo State for a four-year term commencing in January 2024. Incumbent Governor Hope Uzodinma of the All Progressives Congress (APC) secured re-election with a decisive majority, polling 540,308 votes across all 27 local government areas amid reports of low voter participation estimated below 40% of the 2.4 million registered voters.[1] Uzodinma's victory margin exceeded 400,000 votes over his nearest rival, Samuel Anyanwu of the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), who received 71,503 votes, while Labour Party candidate Athan Achonu garnered 64,081 votes; the remaining 14 candidates collectively obtained fewer than 10,000 votes each.[1] The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) declared the results official following collation, confirming Uzodinma's sweep of every local government area, a feat attributed to strong incumbent support in the southeastern state.[1][2] Opposition parties, including the PDP and Labour Party, raised allegations of widespread electoral malpractices, voter suppression, and irregularities such as delayed material distribution and violence in select areas, prompting calls for cancellation and subsequent legal challenges.[1] However, the Imo State Governorship Election Petitions Tribunal upheld Uzodinma's win in June 2025, dismissing petitions for lack of sufficient evidence, thereby affirming the INEC-declared outcome despite persistent claims of compromised process integrity.[3] The election underscored ongoing debates over Nigeria's electoral transparency, with observer groups noting pervasive insecurity and citizen distrust as factors influencing participation and perceptions of legitimacy.[4]

Background

Political context in Imo State

Imo State, located in southeastern Nigeria, is administratively divided into three senatorial districtsâ€”Orlu (Imo West), Owerri (Imo East), and Okigwe (Imo North)â€”which serve as political zones influencing gubernatorial rotations under an informal charter of equity aimed at equitable power sharing among the predominantly Igbo population.[5] The Orlu zone, being the most populous and historically dominant, has produced the majority of governors since 1999, fostering zonal rivalries that shape voting patterns and alliances, with Owerri emphasizing commercial interests and Okigwe focusing on agrarian concerns.[6][7] Since the return to democracy in 1999, Imo State experienced PDP dominance from 1999 to 2011 under governors Achike Udenwa (1999â€“2007) and Ikedi Ohakim (2007â€“2011, initially elected on the PPA platform), followed by a shift to APC control starting with Rochas Okorocha's 2011 victory.[8][7] The APC consolidated its hold after the 2019 election, where Emeka Ihedioha's PDP win was overturned by the Supreme Court in January 2020, installing Hope Uzodinma of the APC, marking a pattern of judicial and partisan shifts that entrenched APC as the leading party by 2023.[8][7] The state's economy, primarily agrarian with contributions from natural gas production, remains heavily dependent on federal allocations derived from national oil revenues, exacerbating vulnerability to oil price fluctuations and limiting local diversification.[9] Voter priorities leading into 2023 were shaped by persistent insecurity, including farmer-herder clashes that displaced communities and disrupted agriculture, alongside infrastructure deficits such as dilapidated roads and inadequate power supply, which hindered economic activity and heightened social tensions.[10][11]

Incumbent's prior tenure and 2019 election outcome

In the 2019 Imo State gubernatorial election held on March 9, the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) initially declared Emeka Ihedioha of the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) the winner on March 10, with 427,308 votes, while Hope Uzodinma of the All Progressives Congress (APC) placed fourth with 71,865 votes from results in 276 of the state's 388 polling units across 27 local government areas.[12][13] The election tribunal and Court of Appeal upheld Ihedioha's victory, but on January 14, 2020, the Supreme Court overturned it in a 5-2 majority ruling, declaring Uzodinma the winner with 563,134 votes after directing the inclusion of previously excluded results from the remaining 388 polling units, which were predominantly in APC strongholds.[12][13][14] This decision, which elevated the fourth-place candidate without a full vote recount, drew accusations of judicial inconsistency, as the court prioritized substantial compliance with electoral laws over strict adherence to INEC's certified results.[12][13] Uzodinma was sworn in as governor on January 14, 2020, initiating a tenure marked by infrastructure initiatives, including road rehabilitations and partnerships for health insurance programs with the World Health Organization, alongside efforts in education, youth employment, and digital economy development.[15][16][17] Critics, however, highlighted rising domestic debtâ€”from N150 billion at the end of 2020 to N205 billion by mid-termâ€”amid claims of unfulfilled promises on security and economic diversification, with opposition parties alleging mismanagement of local government allocations and failure to curb insecurity fueled by political unrest.[18][19] The Supreme Court's ruling sparked widespread protests across Imo State and beyond, including PDP-led demonstrations in Owerri and other regions, with police imposing bans on gatherings amid heightened tensions ahead of related review hearings.[20][21][22] Civil society organizations and PDP stakeholders decried the verdict as a pre-planned judicial intervention undermining electoral integrity, leading to defections, legal challenges dismissed by March 2020, and ongoing debates over Uzodinma's mandate legitimacy that persisted into the 2023 contest.[13][23][24]

Electoral framework

Electoral system

The gubernatorial election in Imo State adhered to Nigeria's first-past-the-post electoral system, as stipulated in the 1999 Constitution (as amended) and the Electoral Act 2022, under which the candidate securing the highest number of valid votes statewide is declared the winner, without requiring an absolute majority in multi-candidate contests.[25][26] Section 178 of the Constitution empowers the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) to appoint the election date, while Section 179 mandates that the elected governor and deputy serve a four-year term commencing from the date of inauguration.[27] INEC administers the process, including voter accreditation via the Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS), which employs biometric verificationâ€”fingerprint or facial recognitionâ€”to confirm voter identity against the Permanent Voter's Card and the voter register, thereby curbing multiple voting and impersonation.[28] Following voting, polling unit results are scanned and uploaded in real-time to INEC's Result Viewing Portal (IReV) for public access, facilitating transparency in aggregation before manual collation at higher levels.[29] The 2023 Imo State election operated on an off-cycle basis, decoupled from the national general elections, due to incumbent Governor Hope Uzodinma's delayed inauguration on January 14, 2020, after the Supreme Court upheld his March 2019 victory, which extended his term end to January 2024 and necessitated the poll on November 11, 2023.[30] This scheduling aligns with constitutional provisions for term continuity while allowing INEC flexibility in off-cycle states to manage logistics amid Nigeria's staggered gubernatorial cycles.[31]

Primaries and candidate selection

All Progressives Congress (APC)

Governor Hope Uzodinma, the incumbent, secured the All Progressives Congress (APC) nomination for the 2023 Imo State gubernatorial election through an unopposed primary held on April 14, 2023, in Owerri.[33][34] The event saw party delegates unanimously acclaiming Uzodinma as the candidate after all other aspirants withdrew their expressions of interest, avoiding a contested vote.[35] Prior to the primary, the APC's national leadership had established screening processes for gubernatorial aspirants, with Uzodinma cleared without issues due to his status as sitting governor.[36] Several party members, including former contenders who had acquired nomination forms, opted to decline participation following internal party reconciliations facilitated by Uzodinma's administration, which emphasized unity to consolidate support ahead of the off-cycle election.[37] This consensus approach leveraged the incumbent's control over delegate lists, drawn from local government areas and wards under APC influence, ensuring alignment with his re-election bid.[33] The unopposed outcome reflected APC's strategic use of incumbency advantages, including access to state resources for mobilization and the resolution of intra-party disputes through appointments and reconciliatory meetings, which minimized factionalism.[34] No formal challenges to the primary process were upheld by party organs or courts, affirming Uzodinma's flag-bearing status for the November 11, 2023, poll.[36]

Labour Party (LP)

The Labour Party experienced a surge in interest for its Imo State gubernatorial nomination following Peter Obi's competitive showing in the Southeast during the February 25, 2023, presidential election, which drew defectors and aspirants seeking to capitalize on the party's newfound momentum among youth and urban voters disillusioned with established parties.[38][39] The party set the cost of its governorship expression of interest and nomination forms at 25 million naira, a fee that sparked backlash for potentially limiting participation despite the LP's anti-elite rhetoric, though it reflected the heightened demand post-Obi's campaign.[40] Multiple aspirants, including former Senator Athan Achonuâ€”who had previously represented Imo North under the Peoples Democratic Partyâ€”purchased forms, signaling internal competition influenced by the Obidient movement's regional appeal.[41] On April 15, 2023, Achonu emerged as the LP's flagbearer at the primary election in Owerri, conducted by the party's national faction endorsed by the Nigeria Labour Congress and 36 state chairmen, amid reports of limited opposition due to strategic consolidations and one aspirant's reported death prior to voting.[42][43][44] Factional disputes over the process prompted legal actions from rival claimants, but the Supreme Court upheld Achonu's candidacy on September 28, 2023, resolving pre-election uncertainties.[45][46]

People's Democratic Party (PDP)

The People's Democratic Party (PDP) conducted its Imo State gubernatorial primary on April 12, 2023, in which Senator Samuel Anyanwu, the party's national secretary, emerged as the consensus candidate.[47][48] Anyanwu, a former senator from Imo East, secured the nomination amid reports of limited contestation following key withdrawals, reflecting internal efforts to unify behind a single aspirant to challenge the incumbent All Progressives Congress (APC) administration.[49] Prior to the primary, the PDP's screening committee cleared several aspirants, including former Governor Emeka Ihedioha, who had picked his nomination forms and expressed intent to contest.[50] However, Ihedioha withdrew his candidacy on March 28, 2023, stating it was to facilitate the emergence of a consensus candidate and promote party unity amid ongoing crises.[51][52] This decision, endorsed by groups like PDP Action 2023 as a strategic move to avoid fragmentation, left Anyanwu as the primary's focal point, though it underscored deeper divisions within the state chapter.[53] The PDP's primary process was hampered by internal crises and aspirant declines, with several potential contenders opting out due to factional disputes and perceived lack of viability.[54] Party executives, including seven state chapter members, resigned and defected in June 2023, citing unresolved leadership conflicts that deterred broader participation.[55] These withdrawals and exits contributed to opposition fragmentation, as PDP's historical dominance in Imoâ€”evident in its 2019 gubernatorial victoryâ€”eroded through mass defections to the APC, including three PDP federal lawmakers in April 2023.[54][56] Such shifts weakened the party's organizational strength ahead of the general election.

Other parties

The All Progressives Grand Alliance (APGA), a regional party with historical influence in southeastern Nigeria, conducted primaries to select Sir Anthony Chidi Ejiogu as its gubernatorial candidate. Ejiogu, a businessman and philanthropist, emerged from the party's internal contest amid efforts to position APGA as a viable alternative in Imo State.[57] The New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) nominated Uche Ben Odunzeh, a former House of Representatives aspirant, through its state primary process, with support from national figures like Rabiu Kwankwaso. Odunzeh's selection aimed to leverage NNPP's emerging national profile, though the party's organizational presence in Imo remained limited.[58] Other minor parties, including the Peoples Redemption Party (PRP)â€”whose candidacy was reinstated by court order on October 24, 2023â€”fielded nominees via direct selections or low-key conventions, resulting in a total of 18 candidates cleared by INEC. These parties' processes involved minimal delegate participation and public scrutiny compared to major contenders, underscoring their peripheral role in Imo State's competitive landscape.[59][60]

Campaign dynamics

Major issues and platforms

Security emerged as a paramount concern in the 2023 Imo State gubernatorial election, driven by persistent attacks from "unknown gunmen," kidnappings, and political violence that disrupted daily life and electoral activities across the southeast region. Voters and candidates frequently highlighted the need to curb abductions, including those targeting political figures and civilians, as well as the enforcement of sit-at-home orders linked to separatist agitations, which exacerbated economic stagnation. Incumbent Governor Hope Uzodinma of the All Progressives Congress (APC) defended his administration's efforts to enhance security through collaboration with federal forces, positioning it as part of his broader "3R" agendaâ€”Reconstruction, Rehabilitation, and Recoveryâ€”while opposition candidates, including Labour Party's Athan Achonu and PDP's Samuel Anyanwu, criticized the government's record as inadequate and pledged more robust local policing and community-based strategies to restore peace.[61][62][63] Economic recovery post-COVID-19 and in response to the federal fuel subsidy removal in May 2023 dominated policy debates, with candidates addressing inflation, unemployment, and the ripple effects on transportation and food prices in a state reliant on agriculture and small-scale trade. Uzodinma emphasized his 3R framework's Recovery pillar, which included industrial policy initiatives launched in November 2022 to attract investments, revive manufacturing, and mitigate subsidy-induced hardships through state palliatives and diversification into agro-processing. Opposition platforms countered by advocating for direct subsidies on essentials, youth employment schemes, and fiscal transparency to counter perceived mismanagement, arguing that the incumbent's approach had not sufficiently alleviated poverty amid rising living costs.[64][65] Infrastructure deficits, particularly in roads, power supply, and urban development, were central to voter grievances, as poor connectivity hampered commerce and service delivery in rural local government areas. Uzodinma's platform spotlighted over 100 road projects under the Reconstruction arm of his 3R agenda, claiming they laid foundations for economic growth by improving access to markets and reducing travel times, alongside efforts to upgrade power infrastructure via partnerships. Challengers promised accelerated rural electrification, maintenance of federal road allocations, and anti-corruption measures to ensure equitable distribution, framing the incumbent's projects as unevenly benefiting urban centers over peripheral zones.[64] Educational improvements featured in platforms amid concerns over funding shortfalls and infrastructure decay affecting enrollment and quality. Uzodinma highlighted investments in school rehabilitations and teacher welfare as Rehabilitation components of his agenda, aiming to recover learning losses from pandemic disruptions. Opposition candidates focused on free education policies, increased budgetary allocations, and partnerships with private sectors to address teacher shortages and outdated curricula, positioning these as correctives to systemic neglect.[66] Debates on ethnic balancing in appointments arose within narratives of Igbo marginalization at the federal level spilling into state governance, with candidates addressing equitable representation across Imo's senatorial zones to foster unity. Uzodinma's administration touted inclusive cabinet selections under his Recovery ethos to promote shared prosperity, while opponents alleged favoritism toward certain subgroups and vowed zonal rotations in key posts to mitigate perceptions of exclusion.[67]

Incumbent advantages and opposition strategies

The All Progressives Congress (APC) incumbent, Governor Hope Uzodimma, leveraged extensive control over state political structures, including mobilization networks across all 27 local government areas, to consolidate support during the campaign. This incumbency apparatus enabled efficient grassroots organization and resource distribution, providing a structural edge over challengers fragmented by weaker local apparatuses.[30] Financial disparities further amplified this, with Uzodimma's access to state funds allowing sustained visibility through patronage and infrastructure showcases, contrasting opposition reliance on sentiment-driven appeals.[68] Federal alliances under President Bola Tinubu bolstered APC's position, with Vice President Kashim Shettima publicly affirming government backing for Uzodimma's re-election on October 12, 2023, and First Lady Oluremi Tinubu campaigning in Imo State on September 1, 2023, to rally women voters. These interventions signaled national party cohesion, potentially deterring opposition defections despite APC's historically limited South-east penetration. Rally metrics underscored APC dominance, as Uzodimma's events drew larger crowds, enabling claims of effective monopoly on organized support.[69][70] Opposition parties countered with anti-incumbency rhetoric emphasizing governance failures like insecurity, positioning the election as a referendum on Uzodimma's tenure. The Labour Party (LP), led by Athan Achonu, mobilized youth through the "Obidient" movement, capitalizing on Peter Obi's over 70% South-east presidential vote share in February 2023 to frame a continuity narrative of regional resistance to APC rule. PDP candidate Samuel Anyanwu pursued zonal equity appeals, highlighting Owerri Zone's marginalization and issuing direct challenges to Uzodimma to "pack out," while hinting at post-election legal recourse through Anyanwu's senatorial litigation history. Both focused on voter sympathy over matching APC's logistical firepower, though without comparable media saturation.[30][71][72]

Election administration and conduct

Pre-election logistics

The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) distributed Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) machines to registration areas across Imo State's 27 local government areas in preparation for the November 11, 2023, gubernatorial election, following nationwide testing and mock accreditation protocols to verify functionality and address potential glitches.[73] INEC also trained electoral officers through its Electoral Institute, with sessions commencing in late September 2023 specifically for the Imo poll, focusing on BVAS operation, result transmission, and compliance with the Electoral Act.[74] However, recruitment for ad-hoc staffâ€”intended to fill roles such as presiding officers and supervisorsâ€”faced challenges, resulting in a shortfall of 6,154 positions as of mid-October 2023, prompting appeals for qualified applicants.[75] Permanent Voter Card (PVC) collection efforts intensified in the lead-up to the election, with INEC publishing detailed figures on October 30, 2023, showing high compliance rates among Imo's approximately 2.3 million registered voters; earlier data from September 20, 2023, indicated about 134,000 uncollected PVCs, leading to extended collection timelines at designated centers.[76][77] Security preparations involved joint planning between INEC, the Nigeria Police Force, and military units, including deployments to over 3,000 polling units, collation centers, and key routes, informed by pre-election threat assessments that identified vulnerabilities in violence-prone local government areas like Ohaji/Egbema and Oguta.[62] No postponements were required in low-risk polling units, as logistical compliance met INEC thresholds. INEC reiterated commitments to transparency, including real-time result uploads via the INEC Results Viewing Portal (IReV) and adherence to biometric accreditation, amid ongoing reviews of past electoral shortcomings to build public confidence.[78]

Voting day operations and reported incidents

Polling units in Imo State generally opened around 8:30 a.m. on November 11, 2023, with INEC officials arriving early and materials deployed effectively across most of the 4,720 polling units, excluding 38 units with no assigned voters.[79] The Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) functioned without major glitches in the majority of observed units, enabling accreditation in 1-3 minutes per voter, though observers noted concerns over potential pre-loading of devices in isolated cases requiring further scrutiny.[79] Voter turnout was low, estimated at around 20-30% of the 2,423,788 registered voters, attributed by observers to pervasive insecurity, citizens' distrust in the process, and enforcement of sit-at-home orders by groups like IPOB in several local government areas.[80] Local observers reported orderly conduct in most polling units, with professional handling by INEC staff, but highlighted apathy among youth, such as in Owerri North LGA where some engaged in non-voting activities due to skepticism about electoral impact.[79][81] Reported incidents included pockets of violence and coercion in Orlu Senatorial District, alongside disruptions linked to political actors. In Nkwerre LGA, NSCDC officials documented interference by politicians and a police officer at the Obinaocha polling unit, prompting accusations of electoral disruption.[82] Insecurity prevented voting in parts of Orsu LGA, an area with prior threats, though results were later announced there; similar suppression affected opposition-leaning zones like Ohaji/Egbema amid ongoing threats from unknown gunmen.[79][80] Yiaga Africa observers noted reduced overall election-day violence compared to prior cycles but emphasized persistent risks from non-state actors and inadequate security response in hotspots.[83]

Result collation process

Following the polls' closure on November 11, 2023, election results from polling units in Imo State's 27 local government areas (LGAs) underwent initial collation at the ward level before aggregation at designated LGA collation centers.[84] These LGA-level results were subsequently forwarded to the state collation center in Owerri, the Imo State capital, for final tabulation.[85] State-level collation commenced in the early hours of November 12, 2023, at approximately 2:40 a.m., supervised by Returning Officer Professor Abayomi Fasina, Vice-Chancellor of Federal University Oye-Ekiti.[85] Party agents protested the timing, citing concerns over rushed proceedings, but Fasina overruled objections and proceeded in line with INEC's electoral manual, which mandates sequential summation of votes from lower to higher levels.[85][84] Transmission of polling unit results to INEC's Result Viewing Portal (IReV) faced compliance challenges, with observer groups reporting irregularities including uploads for units where voting did not occur in LGAs such as Ideato North, Ideato South, Orlu, Orsu, Okigwe, and Oru East.[86] Nigeria Civil Society Situation Room observers noted that such discrepancies undermined process credibility, while Yiaga Africa could not fully verify outcomes due to missing IReV data and high rates of critical incidents (87 out of 101 observed), including disruptions in 12% of sampled units across affected LGAs.[83][86] INEC maintained that procedures were adhered to, though independent assessments highlighted gaps in real-time BVAS-IReV synchronization.[85] The collation process concluded with the Returning Officer's declaration of results later on November 12, 2023, at the Owerri center, certifying compliance with statutory requirements for winner announcement.[85][84]

Controversies and irregularities

Allegations of electoral malpractices

The Labour Party (LP) gubernatorial candidate, Athan Achonu, alleged that the November 11, 2023, election was marred by widespread electoral malpractices, including intimidation of voters and agents, as well as ballot box snatching in multiple locations, prompting calls for outright cancellation of the poll.[93][94] Similarly, the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) candidate, Samuel Anyanwu, claimed instances of vote buying and other irregularities, such as disruption of voting processes, occurred during the election, describing it as compromised by fraud.[95][96] Election observer Yiaga Africa, deploying its Process and Results Verification for Transparency methodology, reported that voting did not hold or commence in certain polling units across Imo State, with observers noting failures in result transmission and urging the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) to clarify the status of affected locations.[97][98] Additional claims from monitoring groups highlighted over-voting in several areas, where total votes cast exceeded the number of voters accredited via Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) machines, particularly in local government areas with strong All Progressives Congress (APC) support.[99][100] Reports also surfaced of thuggery and violence disrupting proceedings in various local government areas, including allegations of armed groups preventing access to polling units and colluding with security agents to influence outcomes, though specific counts of affected areas varied across accounts.[100][101] Non-transmission of polling unit results to INEC's IReV portal was cited as a recurrent issue, with opposition agents asserting it enabled manipulation during collation.[100]

Stakeholder responses and independent observations

The All Progressives Congress (APC) dismissed opposition allegations of rigging in the 2023 Imo State gubernatorial election as baseless and characteristic of "sore loser tactics" by the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), emphasizing that Governor Hope Uzodinma's victory reflected the electorate's will amid a substantial margin of over 540,000 votes.[102] The Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) defended its conduct, stating that Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) machines recorded accreditations aligning closely with collated results, with over-voting incidents limited and addressed through post-election audits, while rejecting calls for cancellation as unsubstantiated.[78] Independent observers, including Yiaga Africa, documented instances of irregularities such as unauthorized result alterations and polling disruptions in areas like Okigwe Local Government Area, but urged targeted investigations by INEC without asserting that these flaws altered the overall outcome, given the winner's dominant lead.[103][104] The Nigeria Civil Society Situation Room similarly highlighted result falsification concerns across collation centers but noted that logistical challenges and security lapses, while systemic, did not universally invalidate the process in a manner sufficient to overturn certified tallies.[86] Fact-checking organizations debunked several viral claims of widespread violence, verifying that recirculated footage of burning shops and vehicles predated the election by years and misrepresented isolated incidents as systemic electoral chaos, thereby countering narratives that exaggerated insecurity to undermine result legitimacy.[105][106] These assessments underscored that while flaws existed, the election's core integrity held, with disinformation amplifying perceptions of disorder beyond empirical evidence.[107]

Post-election legal proceedings

Governorship election petition tribunal

The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) candidate, Samuel Anyanwu, and the Labour Party (LP) candidate, Athan Achonu, filed separate petitions challenging the Independent National Electoral Commission's (INEC) declaration of All Progressives Congress (APC) candidate Hope Uzodimma as the winner of the November 11, 2023, Imo State gubernatorial election.[108][109] The petitions, lodged within the 21-day period mandated by Section 134(1) of the Electoral Act 2022 following INEC's announcement on November 15, 2023, alleged substantial non-compliance with electoral laws, including irregularities such as over-voting, voter suppression, and violence that purportedly affected the outcome in multiple local government areas.[110][111] Petitioners sought access to Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) data and other INEC documents to substantiate claims of discrepancies between accredited voters and votes recorded, alongside assertions that Uzodimma's victory did not reflect the will of the electorate due to these malpractices.[111][112] The three-member tribunal, chaired by Justice Olusegun Oye and sitting in Abuja, conducted hearings where petitioners presented witness testimonies and documentary evidence, while respondents, including Uzodimma and INEC, countered with defenses emphasizing compliance and the validity of INEC's collation process.[113][114] Both petitions were consolidated for adjudication, with the tribunal examining allegations under Sections 132 and 135 of the Electoral Act, which require proof of substantial non-compliance capable of altering results.[108] On May 24, 2024, the tribunal unanimously dismissed the petitions for lacking merit, ruling that the petitioners failed to provide credible, non-hearsay evidence to demonstrate over-voting or irregularities on a scale sufficient to vitiate Uzodimma's lead of over 400,000 votes.[109][108][115] It held that BVAS data and Form EC8A results aligned with INEC's declaration, dismissing witness accounts as inconsistent and unsubstantiated by empirical data, thereby upholding Uzodimma's election as governor.[113][110]

Court of Appeal ruling

The Court of Appeal, Abuja Division, considered the appeal lodged by Samuel Anyanwu, the PDP gubernatorial candidate, and his party against the Imo State Governorship Election Petition Tribunal's decision upholding Hope Uzodimma's re-election. In a unanimous judgment delivered on July 16, 2024, by a three-member panel led by Justice Elphreda Williams-Dawodu, the court dismissed all grounds of appeal, deeming them devoid of merit and affirming Uzodimma's victory as reflective of the electorate's will.[116][117][118] The appellants contended that the tribunal erred in law by failing to nullify the election due to alleged substantial non-compliance with the Electoral Act 2022, particularly claiming Uzodimma did not secure the requisite 25% of votes in at least two-thirds of Imo State's 27 local government areas amid reported irregularities. The appellate court held that the petitioners failed to adduce credible evidence demonstrating that such irregularities were widespread or pervasive enough to impact the overall result, thereby not vitiating Uzodimma's substantial compliance with the constitutional spread requirement under Section 177 of the 1999 Constitution (as amended).[116][117] This decision underscored procedural rigor in appellate review, emphasizing that mere allegations of malpractices, without proof of outcome-altering effect, do not warrant overturning certified election results from the Independent National Electoral Commission. It established a benchmark for off-cycle gubernatorial contests, where courts prioritize empirical demonstration of substantial discrepancies over unsubstantiated claims, thereby stabilizing electoral outcomes in staggered polls.[118][117]

Supreme Court affirmation

The Supreme Court of Nigeria unanimously dismissed residual appeals challenging Hope Uzodinma's victory in the November 11, 2023, Imo State gubernatorial election on August 23, 2024, thereby affirming his re-election as governor under the All Progressives Congress (APC).[119][120] A five-member panel, led by Justice Mohammed Idris in the lead judgment, upheld the concurrent decisions of the Governorship Election Petition Tribunal and the Court of Appeal, which had validated Uzodinma's mandate as declared by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC).[119] The justices reasoned that appellants Samuel Anyanwu (PDP) and Athan Achonu (Labour Party) failed to adduce credible evidence proving substantial non-compliance with the Electoral Act 2022, widespread electoral offenses, or forgery of Uzodinma's educational certificate, rendering their claims devoid of merit.[119] Allegations of irregularities, including over-voting and result manipulation, were deemed insufficient to vitiate the overall election outcome, as petitioners could not demonstrate how such issues materially impacted Uzodinma's lead of over 400,000 votes.[119][120] This affirmation diverged from the 2019 Imo election, where the Supreme Court nullified Emeka Ihedioha's (PDP) win and declared Uzodinma governor after re-evaluating excluded votes from smaller parties, a step not warranted here due to stronger evidentiary thresholds under the updated Electoral Act and INEC's deployment of Bimodal Voter Accreditation System (BVAS) for enhanced verification.[119][121] The 2023 ruling emphasized judicial restraint absent proof of pervasive fraud, reflecting procedural safeguards absent in the prior contest. Post-judgment, Uzodinma's administration proceeded uninterrupted, with the governor pledging renewed focus on Imo State's development amid sustained security and infrastructural initiatives, averting the prolonged uncertainty that marked the 2019-2020 transition.[120][119]
