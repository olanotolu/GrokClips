Finite difference

A finite difference is a mathematical expression of the form f(x+b)âˆ’f(x+a)f(x + b) - f(x + a)f(x+b)âˆ’f(x+a). Finite differences (or the associated difference quotients) are often used as approximations of derivatives, such as in numerical differentiation. The difference operator, commonly denoted Î”\DeltaÎ”, is the operator that maps a function f to the function Î”\Delta Î” defined by

A difference equation is a functional equation that involves the finite difference operator in the same way as a differential equation involves derivatives. There are many similarities between difference equations and differential equations. Certain recurrence relations can be written as difference equations by replacing iteration notation with finite differences. In numerical analysis, finite differences are widely used for approximating derivatives, and the term "finite difference" is often used as an abbreviation of "finite difference approximation of derivatives".[1][2][3] Finite differences were introduced by Brook Taylor in 1715 and have also been studied as abstract self-standing mathematical objects in works by George Boole (1860), L. M. Milne-Thomson (1933), and KÃ¡roly Jordan (1939). Finite differences trace their origins back to one of Jost BÃ¼rgi's algorithms (c. 1592) and work by others including Isaac Newton. Arbitrarily sized kernels

Using linear algebra one can construct finite difference approximations which utilize an arbitrary number of points to the left and a (possibly different) number of points to the right of the evaluation point, for any order derivative. This involves solving a linear system such that the Taylor expansion of the sum of those points around the evaluation point best approximates the Taylor expansion of the desired derivative. Such formulas can be represented graphically on a hexagonal or diamond-shaped grid.[6] This is useful for differentiating a function on a grid, where, as one approaches the edge of the grid, one must sample fewer and fewer points on one side. Finite difference approximations for non-standard (and even non-integer) stencils given an arbitrary stencil and a desired derivative order may be constructed.[7]

In differential equations

An important application of finite differences is in numerical analysis, especially in numerical solutions of ordinary and partial differential equations[8][9], which aim at the numerical solution of ordinary differential equations and partial differential equations. The idea is to replace the derivatives appearing in the differential equation by finite differences that approximate them. The resulting methods are called finite difference methods. Common applications of the finite difference method are in computational science and engineering disciplines, such as thermal engineering[9][10], fluid mechanics[3], etc.[9][10]
