Anything's Possible (film)

Anything's Possible is a 2022 American coming-of-age romantic comedy film written by Ximena García Lecuona and directed by Billy Porter in his feature-length directorial debut.[1] The story follows Kelsa, a confident transgender high school senior portrayed by Eva Reign, as she pursues a romantic interest with classmate Khal (Abubakr Ali) while dealing with family tensions and social dynamics in Pittsburgh.[2] Featuring supporting performances from Renée Elise Goldsberry as Kelsa's mother and Kelly Lamor Wilson, the film emphasizes themes of self-acceptance and youthful romance.[3] Premiering at the Tribeca Festival in June 2022 before a limited theatrical release and subsequent streaming on Prime Video, Anything's Possible garnered attention for its focus on a Black transgender protagonist in a mainstream teen romance narrative.[4] Critics praised its inclusive perspective and Porter's assured direction, with a 87% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 62 reviews, though some noted uneven pacing and underdeveloped subplots.[4] Audience reception was more divided, reflected in an IMDb score of 5.8/10 from nearly 2,000 users, highlighting appreciation for its representation alongside critiques of clichéd elements.[1] The film's release aligned with broader cultural discussions on transgender visibility in media, positioning it as a notable entry despite modest box office performance due to its limited distribution.[5]

Synopsis

Plot Summary

Kelsa Renard, a confident Black transgender high school senior in Pittsburgh, hosts a podcast discussing animals' adaptive traits and her own experiences, while aspiring to study zoology in college to pursue nature cinematography.[2] She lives with her supportive single mother, Selene, a doctor who encourages her to incorporate her identity into her college application essay, and her younger brother, who feels embarrassed by her public persona.[6] Kelsa's father had previously left the family following her transition.[7] At school, Kelsa navigates senior year with friends Em, who seeks romantic experiences before college, and Chris, a laid-back peer.[6] She initially resists dating, wary of complications arising from her transgender identity.[8] Khal, a shy, artistic Muslim classmate who secretly listens to her podcast, develops a crush and approaches her with flowers, leading to extended conversations and mutual attraction.[2] [6] They begin holding hands and dating, though Kelsa questions his motives amid broader fears of rejection.[8] Tensions emerge when Em, jealous of Khal's interest in Kelsa, stirs drama including an attempt to restrict Kelsa's bathroom access, though it resolves without escalation.[8] Khal faces pushback from his best friend Otis, who makes derogatory comments about Kelsa and physically confronts Khal, prompting Khal to defend the relationship.[6] Khal's parents assume he is gay upon learning of the romance, while Kelsa's brother inadvertently heightens family awkwardness.[6] Kelsa and Khal persevere, sharing their first kiss and progressing to physical intimacy over the school year.[6] Supported by Selene and eventually gaining acceptance from some peers, they thrive together, culminating in a positive resolution marked by a group musical performance.[2] [6]

Cast and Characters

Principal Cast

Eva Reign stars as Kelsa, the film's protagonist, a confident transgender high school senior navigating adolescence and romance.[4] This role represents Reign's feature film debut and first starring performance, following her background in journalism and theater.[9][10] Abubakr Ali portrays Khal, Kelsa's classmate who develops a romantic interest in her. Ali, an Egyptian American actor and Yale School of Drama graduate, had prior credits including television roles in Law & Order: Organized Crime before this lead film part.[11][12] Renée Elise Goldsberry plays Selene, Kelsa's supportive mother. Goldsberry, a Tony Award winner known for originating the role of Angelica Schuyler in the Broadway musical Hamilton, brings established dramatic experience to the supporting lead.[4][13]

Supporting Cast

Courtnee Carter plays Em, Kelsa's close friend who contributes to the film's depiction of peer solidarity and lighthearted banter within the high school group.[11] Kelly Lamor Wilson portrays Chris, a classmate whose interactions add layers to the ensemble's social tensions and reconciliations.[11] Grant Reynolds appears as Otis, Khal's companion who supports the narrative through scenes of male camaraderie and indirect influence on romantic pursuits.[11] Renée Elise Goldsberry stars as Selene, Kelsa's mother, offering grounded familial counsel that contrasts with the protagonists' youthful explorations.[14] These roles enhance the story's relational web without dominating the central romance, drawing on the actors' prior theater and screen experience to flesh out the supporting ensemble.[15]

Production

Development

The screenplay for Anything's Possible, originally titled What If?, was written by Ximena García Lecuona and selected for the inaugural 2019 GLAAD List, which highlights promising unproduced scripts featuring LGBTQ-inclusive narratives.[16] The script later appeared on the 2020 Black List, an annual survey of favored unproduced screenplays voted on by film executives.[17] Lecuona has described the writing process as stemming from personal inspiration that compelled her to document the story immediately.[18] Amazon Studios acquired and developed the script into a feature film production.[19] Billy Porter, whose portrayal of transgender characters in the series Pose informed his approach to authentic representation, was attached as director for his feature debut.[8] The project was produced under Amazon MGM Studios by D.J. Gugenheim, among others, focusing on a coming-of-age romantic comedy centered on a transgender protagonist.[20]

Casting

Casting director Alexa Fogel led an extensive search for the lead role of Kelsa, a Black transgender high school senior, prioritizing authenticity in representation.[21] The process included an open casting call disseminated via email, through which newcomer Eva Reign, then 26 and without an agent or manager, learned of the opportunity from a friend.[22] [23] Reign advanced through callbacks, meeting director Billy Porter, and was cast a week before principal photography began, marking her feature film debut.[21] [24] For the male lead of Khal, a Muslim American teenager from an immigrant family, Porter sought an actor to reflect the film's goals of diverse, authentic storytelling.[24] Abubakr Ali was selected, noting the rarity of such a leading role for someone of his background.[24] Porter emphasized populating the cast with performers embodying "the diversity our world demands" while focusing on human spirit over stereotypes.[24] Reign prepared by drawing on her personal experiences as a trans woman raised by a single mother, incorporating elements like fashion interests into Kelsa, under Porter's guidance to prioritize joy and self-trust rather than trauma.[22] [21] The production team reviewed the FX docuseries Pride to inform authentic portrayals of transgender experiences.[22] Challenges included the industry's limited opportunities for trans performers, with Reign having faced discouraging auditions and nearly abandoning acting prior to this role.[21]

Filming

Principal photography for Anything's Possible commenced in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, in 2021, selected for its authentic representation of urban high school environments reflective of the film's narrative.[25] The production utilized on-location shooting across various city neighborhoods, including Highland Park and areas around the Point and Downtown skyline, to integrate Pittsburgh as a visual element in the story.[26] School interiors and exteriors were primarily captured at Pittsburgh Creative and Performing Arts (CAPA) High School, enhancing the realism of adolescent daily life depicted.[27] [28] The shoot adhered to industry-standard safety measures amid ongoing COVID-19 concerns, though specific protocols for this production remain undocumented in public records; general SAG-AFTRA guidelines at the time emphasized zoning, masking, and testing on sets. Cinematography was handled by Andrei Bowden Schwartz, employing a straightforward visual approach that highlighted the city's architecture and natural light to underscore the coming-of-age intimacy without overt stylistic flourishes.[11] [29]

Themes and Representation

Core Themes

The film centers on the motif of young romance transcending social and cultural barriers, exemplified by the evolving connection between high school seniors Kelsa and Khal, who form a bond through collaborative art assignments despite differences in their family backgrounds and peer circles.[8] This relationship underscores a causal dynamic where initial mutual curiosity leads to deeper emotional intimacy, challenging conventional high school social hierarchies and emphasizing personal agency in forging connections.[3] A recurring tension lies in the interplay between family loyalty and individual growth, as Kelsa grapples with her devotion to her single mother—who provides unwavering support—against her ambition to leave Pittsburgh for college in New York or Los Angeles, illustrating how familial bonds can both anchor and constrain personal evolution.[5] Kelsa's decisions, driven by her bold self-assurance, propel this conflict forward, revealing how prioritizing dreams necessitates navigating emotional trade-offs without idealized resolutions.[2] Optimism in the face of adversity emerges as a core aspirational element, with characters embodying resilience through proactive pursuit of aspirations amid everyday high school obstacles like academic pressures and subtle peer exclusions.[5] Kelsa's confident demeanor, including her role as a content creator sharing introspective videos, causally links self-belief to plot progression, fostering a narrative that posits determination as key to expanding possibilities, tempered by realistic frictions such as social skepticism that test but do not derail resolve.[2][30]

Portrayal of Transgender Experiences

In the film, the protagonist Kelsa, portrayed by Eva Reign, is depicted as a confident and self-assured transgender high school senior whose narrative centers on romantic pursuits and personal agency rather than identity-related struggles. Transition elements are largely absent from the storyline, with no explicit focus on medical interventions, dysphoria, or familial conflict over gender identity; instead, Kelsa navigates peer dynamics and a budding relationship with minimal external adversity tied to her transgender status.[5][15] Director Billy Porter, himself a Black gay man, framed the project as a deliberate shift toward portraying transgender characters—particularly Black trans youth—as thriving entities rather than perpetual victims of survival narratives. Porter emphasized creating a "love letter" to trans communities by centering Kelsa's unapologetic boldness and romantic fulfillment, marking a departure from trauma-heavy depictions in prior media.[24][31] This optimistic lens achieves notable visibility for a Black transgender lead in a mainstream romantic comedy, positioning Kelsa as a quintessential female protagonist akin to those in traditional teen films, thereby expanding representational norms.[32][33] However, the film's portrayal contrasts with empirical data on transgender youth experiences, which document elevated challenges including widespread bullying and mental health risks. According to the CDC's 2023 Youth Risk Behavior Survey, transgender high school students reported higher prevalence of electronic bullying (32.2% vs. 14.6% overall) and physical fighting (15.9% vs. 7.8%) compared to cisgender peers, alongside persistent sadness or hopelessness affecting 69.2% of transgender respondents.[34][35] Suicide attempt rates among transgender youth reached 29% in related CDC analyses, far exceeding general adolescent figures, underscoring a reality of heightened vulnerability not reflected in Kelsa's relatively frictionless school environment.[36][37] This selective emphasis on normalcy highlights the film's aspirational intent amid documented disparities.[38]

Release

Distribution and Premiere

The world premiere of Anything's Possible took place at the 40th Outfest Los Angeles LGBTQ+ Film Festival on July 14, 2022, where it served as the opening night film.[39][40] Distributed by Amazon MGM Studios, the film received a limited theatrical release in select U.S. theaters on July 22, 2022, concurrent with its global streaming debut on Amazon Prime Video.[41][42] This day-and-date strategy aligned with Amazon's approach to hybrid releases for original content, emphasizing accessibility via its streaming platform to reach a broad international audience without specified territorial limitations beyond Prime Video's availability.[43][44] No public viewership metrics for the initial streaming rollout were disclosed by Amazon.

Reception

Critical Response

The film garnered generally positive reviews from critics, earning an 87% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 62 reviews, with the site's consensus describing it as "a smart update on the teen rom-com formula that succeeds by letting its central couple be themselves."[4] Publications praised its warmth, inclusivity, and Billy Porter's confident directorial debut, which centers a transgender Black teenager without making her identity the sole dramatic focus. For instance, Variety highlighted the film's tender exploration of self-acceptance and budding romance, noting its boundary-pushing elements in a mainstream teen comedy framework.[8] Similarly, reviews commended lead actress Eva Reign's charismatic performance and the story's optimistic tone, positioning it as a feel-good entry that expands representation in the genre. However, some critics pointed to uneven execution and artificiality in the narrative. The Guardian awarded it three out of five stars, calling it a "sweet but uneven" rom-com with patchy pacing and underdeveloped supporting elements despite its bold lead.[5] The New York Times observed that the film "never quite figures out if it wants to be distinctive or just another kid at school," critiquing its blend of familiarity and restraint in handling conflict.[3] On Metacritic, it scored 62 out of 100, reflecting mixed sentiments where tenderness was acknowledged but limited depth in character arcs and dramatic tension drew reservations.[45] These critiques emphasized that while the film's representational aims were sincere, its rom-com conventions sometimes resulted in a lack of edge or genuine stakes.

Audience and Commercial Performance

_Anything's Possible garnered mixed audience reception, evidenced by an IMDb user rating of 5.8 out of 10 from 1,950 votes as of recent data.[1] Similarly, its Rotten Tomatoes audience score registers at 58% based on over 50 verified ratings, reflecting tepid engagement relative to broader romantic comedy releases.[4] These metrics suggest niche appeal among viewers interested in transgender-centered narratives, but limited resonance with wider demographics, including purported Gen Z targets, amid a landscape favoring less specialized teen romances. The film bypassed significant theatrical distribution, premiering directly on Prime Video on July 22, 2022, which curtailed traditional box office tracking and revenue potential.[46] Financial analyses indicate no substantial domestic or international grosses reported, aligning with its streaming-first model and estimated $10 million production budget that yielded no evident breakout profitability.[47] On Amazon Prime Video, user reviews average 4.1 out of 5 from 227 contributions, but the absence of disclosed viewership metrics—unlike high-profile streaming hits—points to subdued performance, with viewer volume paling against mainstream genre counterparts that routinely amass millions of streams.[48] Empirical indicators, such as sparse rating participation across platforms (e.g., 5,800 Letterboxd averages at 3.0/5), underscore commercial underperformance driven by polarized or indifferent reception rather than widespread adoption.[49] This outcome highlights challenges for ideologically specific content in capturing scalable market share, where audience metrics reveal stronger pull among affinity groups than mass appeal.

Awards and Recognition

Anything's Possible won the GLAAD Media Award for Outstanding Film – Streaming or TV at the 34th Annual GLAAD Media Awards held on March 30, 2023, tying with Fire Island.[50][51] The film had been nominated in the category earlier that year, recognizing its portrayal of transgender experiences in a streaming release.[52] Despite promotional emphasis on its representation of a Black transgender protagonist, the film garnered no nominations from major industry awards such as the Academy Awards, BAFTA Awards, or Golden Globes.[50] It premiered as the opening night film at the 40th Outfest Los Angeles LGBTQ Film Festival on July 14, 2022, but did not receive competitive awards there.[19] No other significant festival or guild recognitions were reported for the production or its cast.

Controversies and Debates

Ideological Criticisms

Conservative critics have labeled Anything's Possible as overt propaganda advancing transgender ideology, arguing that its romantic narrative normalizes gender transition for youth while sidelining dissenting perspectives as bigotry. In a review for The American Conservative, Carl R. Trueman described the film as a "powerfully normalizing piece of LGBT propaganda" that demonizes opponents—such as a mother voicing concerns over shared facilities—by framing them as threats, thereby pressuring parents to affirm transitions without question.[53] Trueman contended that the story's emphasis on a "passable" trans protagonist pursuing uncomplicated romance exerts greater influence on adolescent viewers than institutional education, potentially encouraging irreversible decisions amid ignored medical realities like infertility from interventions, as evidenced in cases such as Jazz Jennings' documented surgical complications.[53] The film's idealized portrayal of transgender romance has drawn fire for disregarding empirical disparities in transgender experiences, including elevated risks of violence; data from the Williams Institute, drawing on the National Crime Victimization Survey (2017-2018), indicate transgender individuals face violent victimization at a rate of 86.2 per 1,000 persons—over four times the 21.7 rate for cisgender individuals—often linked to interpersonal conflicts that youth media rarely depicts.[54] Critics from right-leaning outlets, including Plugged In, highlighted scenes of student protests demanding acceptance of nontraditional gender roles as indicative of agenda-driven content targeting impressionable audiences, contrasting with broader cultural debates over the inclusion of such themes in teen-oriented entertainment absent balanced outcomes like detransition or regret, which studies estimate affect 1-8% of cases though reporting biases persist.[6] In response, progressive advocates defended the film as an exercise in artistic freedom and representation, with GLAAD commending it as a "huge step forward in authentic LGBTQ storytelling" that counters harmful stereotypes through a "radical act of love."[16] Even within left-leaning queer media, such as Autostraddle, reviewers noted tensions, critiquing side characters embodying "TERF ideology" as reductive propaganda that flattens intra-community debates, yet praised the lead's agency in pursuing happiness without the typical trauma narratives dominating trans portrayals.[55] These defenses underscore a prioritization of affirmative visibility over comprehensive risk disclosure, amid source biases in mainstream outlets that often amplify positive framings while marginalizing empirical cautions from conservative analyses.

Representation Accuracy

The film portrays its protagonist, Kelsa, a Black transgender high school senior, as confidently navigating romance and social life with minimal disruption from her gender transition, emphasizing everyday adolescent experiences over dysphoria or medical interventions.[1] This depiction aligns with director Billy Porter's intent to center a "love letter" to trans youth, focusing on empowerment and normalcy rather than adversity.[55] In contrast, empirical studies document elevated mental health risks among transgender youth, including lifetime suicide attempt rates of approximately 40%, far exceeding general population figures, as reported in analyses of large-scale surveys like the U.S. Transgender Survey.[56] These rates persist even post-transition in some cohorts, with interpersonal and minority stress models linking suicidality to factors such as family rejection and internalized distress, though causal pathways remain debated amid comorbid conditions like autism and trauma.[57][58] School-based data further underscores discrepancies: GLSEN's 2021 National School Climate Survey of over 22,000 LGBTQ+ students revealed that 75% of transgender respondents faced verbal harassment due to gender expression, 35% physical harassment, and over half felt unsafe at school, leading to absenteeism and lower academic outcomes.[59] Such pervasive rejection contradicts the film's narrative of seamless peer acceptance, where Kelsa's confidence appears unmarred by bullying or isolation common in these self-reported experiences.[60] The Cass Review, an independent analysis commissioned by England's NHS and published in 2024, critiques affirmative care models by highlighting weak evidence for long-term benefits of early interventions, noting high rates of neurodevelopmental disorders (up to 35%) and mental health comorbidities in gender clinic referrals, alongside desistance in up to 80-90% of prepubertal cases without medicalization.[61] This evidence-based appraisal prioritizes biological maturation and psychosocial causal factors—such as rapid-onset dysphoria linked to social influences—over the film's ideological emphasis on unexamined optimism, potentially underrepresenting trajectories where gender incongruence resolves without transition.[62] While advancing visibility for transgender characters of color, the portrayal thus diverges from data-driven realism, favoring aspirational narratives amid documented struggles.[5]
