New Serb Democracy

New Serb Democracy (Nova srpska demokratija, NSD) is a right-wing political party in Montenegro dedicated to safeguarding the rights and cultural heritage of the Serb minority, which comprises approximately 28.7% of the population according to the 2011 census.[1] Founded on 24 January 2009 by Andrija Mandić through the merger of the Serb People's Party and the People's Socialist Party, the party emphasizes parliamentary democracy, equality of citizens, and protection against policies perceived as eroding Serb identity, such as restrictions on the Serbian Orthodox Church and language usage.[1] The party rose to national prominence as part of opposition alliances challenging the decades-long dominance of Milo Đukanović's Democratic Party of Socialists, culminating in its participation in the 2020 "For the Future of Montenegro" coalition that secured a parliamentary majority and formed the first non-DPS government since independence.[2] In subsequent years, NSD contributed to government formations focused on EU integration, anti-corruption measures, and economic reforms, while Mandić was elected Speaker of the Parliament in October 2023 following the June elections, where the party's coalition won 13 seats.[2] Defining characteristics include its staunch defense of Serb interests amid ongoing debates over national symbols, historical narratives, and relations with neighboring Serbia, positioning it as a counterweight to Montenegrin-centric nationalism in the multi-ethnic republic.[1]

History

Formation and initial positioning (2015–2016)

The New Serb Democracy (NSD), a Serbian nationalist party, maintained its position within the opposition Democratic Front (DF) alliance during early 2015 amid internal tensions, deciding on March 29 to remain allied with the Movement for Changes despite calls for a split. This decision reinforced NSD's role as a core pro-Serb component of the DF, prioritizing unified opposition against the ruling Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS) amid allegations of electoral manipulation and governance failures.[3][4] Throughout 2015–2016, NSD aligned with DF-led protests demanding Prime Minister Milo Đukanović's resignation and the formation of a transitional expert government to ensure fair 2016 parliamentary elections, framing the crisis as a fight against DPS authoritarianism, media dominance, and suppression of opposition voices. On October 25, 2015, thousands rallied in Podgorica under DF auspices, with clashes erupting as protesters attempted to storm parliament, highlighting NSD's commitment to street mobilization for democratic reforms. The party positioned itself as a defender of Serb minority rights, critiquing the government's policies as eroding national identity and favoring Montenegrin state-building narratives over historical ties to Serbia.[5][6] NSD's stance during this period emphasized opposition to Montenegro's accelerated NATO integration, welcoming France's veto on expansion in related contexts and portraying it as incompatible with Serb interests and regional stability. Internally, the party consolidated under leader Andrija Mandić, focusing on coalition discipline to challenge DPS's 25-year rule, while avoiding fragmentation that had plagued earlier Serb parties. This positioning established NSD as a pragmatic yet ideologically firm actor in Montenegro's polarized politics, blending ethnic advocacy with broader anti-corruption demands.[7]

Rise in opposition coalitions (2016–2019)

In the 2016 Montenegrin parliamentary election held on October 16, the New Serb Democracy (NSD) participated as part of the Democratic Front (DF) coalition, which secured 20.32% of the popular vote and 18 seats in the 81-seat Assembly, positioning it as the largest opposition bloc against the ruling Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS).[8] This performance marked a consolidation of NSD's role within the DF alliance, comprising NSD and the Movement for Changes, amid widespread discontent with the long-ruling DPS under Milo Đukanović, accused of electoral irregularities and media dominance.[9] The DF's gains reflected growing Serb minority mobilization and broader anti-corruption sentiments, though allegations of a coup plot targeting the election—later linked to DF leaders including NSD's Andrija Mandić—intensified political tensions and led to their detention.[10] Post-election, NSD and DF sought to forge broader opposition coalitions to prevent DPS from forming a government, leveraging their parliamentary leverage to demand investigations into alleged fraud and push for institutional reforms.[11] Despite initial unity efforts, fragmentation persisted as smaller parties and ethnic minorities aligned with DPS, allowing the latter to secure a minority government with 36 seats plus external support. NSD's involvement in sustained protests and parliamentary boycotts during 2017 highlighted its rising profile in challenging DPS's three-decade dominance, focusing on issues like judicial independence and Serb cultural rights, even as Mandić and DF co-leader Milan Knežević faced trials for the 2016 plot, receiving five-year sentences in absentia in 2017 before acquittals in 2021.[12][13] By the 2018 presidential election on April 15, NSD contributed to an expanded opposition coalition nominating Mladen Bojanić, a civic-oriented candidate backed by DF and parties like the Social Democratic Party, achieving 33.4% of the vote against Đukanović's 53.9%.[14][15] This joint effort demonstrated NSD's strategic pivot toward inclusive anti-DPS alliances, transcending ethnic lines to capitalize on voter fatigue with Đukanović's return to power, though it fell short of forcing a runoff. Throughout 2016–2019, NSD's coalition activities amplified its opposition influence, polling consistently above 10% in DF configurations and sustaining pressure via advocacy for fair elections and minority protections, despite systemic challenges like state media bias favoring the incumbents.[16]

Breakthrough in 2020 elections and government entry

In the parliamentary elections on 30 August 2020, New Serb Democracy (NSD) participated as a constituent member of the Democratic Front within the "For the Future of Montenegro" electoral list, which garnered 133,261 votes or 32.55% of the popular vote and won 27 seats in the 81-seat Assembly of Montenegro.[17] This performance, combined with the 10 seats from the "Peace is Our Nation" list and 4 seats from the "Black on White" list, enabled the opposition to secure a slim majority of 41 seats, ousting the Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS) after its uninterrupted rule since Montenegro's independence in 2006.[18] The vote reflected widespread dissatisfaction with DPS governance, including allegations of corruption and state capture, amid heightened tensions over national identity and church property disputes.[19] NSD's role in the pro-Serb "For the Future of Montenegro" list marked a breakthrough, with the party allocated 5 seats internally from the coalition's total, building on prior opposition efforts but achieving decisive influence in the shift from DPS dominance.[20] Leader Andrija Mandić's emphasis on Serb minority rights and opposition to DPS policies on historical narratives resonated in areas with significant Serb populations, contributing to the list's strong showing despite fragmented opposition dynamics.[21] Post-election negotiations among the three opposition groups culminated in government formation on 4 December 2020, when Zdravko Krivokapić—nominated from the "For the Future of Montenegro" bloc and backed by the Serbian Orthodox Church—was elected prime minister by 41 votes in parliament, establishing the first non-DPS executive in three decades.[22] NSD entered the ruling majority as part of the Democratic Front, providing external support to the technocratic Krivokapić cabinet, which prioritized non-partisan experts over direct coalition quotas for ministerial posts to foster stability and EU integration continuity.[23] This arrangement positioned NSD to advance its priorities on minority protections and state symbols, though internal coalition frictions soon emerged over policy implementation.[24]

Developments since 2020 including 2023 elections

Following the 2020 parliamentary elections on August 30, the New Serb Democracy (NSD), as part of the Democratic Front alliance within the broader "For the Future of Montenegro" coalition, contributed to the opposition's narrow victory that ended the Democratic Party of Socialists' (DPS) 30-year rule.[22] The coalition secured 27 seats in the 81-seat parliament, enabling the formation of a minority government on December 4, 2020, led by Prime Minister Zdravko Krivokapić, with NSD leader Andrija Mandić elected as Speaker of the Parliament.[25] [1] This marked NSD's first entry into executive power, where it advocated for Serb minority rights, religious freedoms, and closer ties with Serbia amid ongoing disputes over church properties seized under the prior regime.[26] The Krivokapić government faced internal divisions, particularly between pro-Serb factions like NSD and more centrist elements, leading to its collapse via a no-confidence vote on February 4, 2022, after failing to advance EU accession or resolve judicial reforms.[26] A subsequent technocratic minority cabinet under Prime Minister Dritan Abazović struggled with legislative gridlock, prompting President Milo Đukanović to dissolve parliament on January 1, 2023, and call snap elections for June 11. In May 2023, the Democratic Front alliance, including NSD, formally dissolved after 11 years, though NSD continued collaborating with former partners in electoral coalitions.[27] In the 2023 elections, NSD participated in the "For the Future of Montenegro" coalition, which received 44,565 votes (14.74%) and won 13 seats, maintaining its position as a key pro-Serb voice with voter turnout at 56.3%.[2] [28] No single bloc secured a majority (requiring 41 seats), but the coalition joined a pro-European parliamentary majority led by the Europe Now Movement, facilitating the formation of a new government under Prime Minister Milojko Spajić on October 31, 2023. Mandić was re-elected Speaker on October 30, 2023, positioning NSD to influence debates on national identity, NATO skepticism, and regional relations despite the government's EU-oriented agenda.[2] This arrangement has sustained NSD's governmental role while highlighting tensions over historical narratives and minority protections.[29]

Ideology and political positions

Core principles on national identity and Serb minority rights

New Serb Democracy maintains that national identity in Montenegro is inherently multi-ethnic and historically intertwined, particularly between Serb and Montenegrin elements, rejecting narratives that artificially separate them as mutually exclusive. The party espouses Serbian-Montenegrin unionism, positing that shared linguistic, cultural, and religious heritage forms the basis of a cohesive civic identity, rather than imposed distinctions that marginalize Serb self-identification. This stance counters policies perceived as promoting a singular Montenegrin ethnicity at the expense of Serb heritage, advocating instead for a constitutional framework that recognizes the indivisibility of these identities within Montenegro's sovereignty.[20] On Serb minority rights, the party prioritizes protections against assimilation, including constitutional entrenchment of the Serbian language as co-official alongside Montenegrin to ensure bilingual education, signage, and public administration in Serb-majority areas. Comprising roughly 28.7% of the population per official data, Serbs face what the party describes as systemic underrepresentation in cultural institutions and media; New Serb Democracy demands proportional quotas in public broadcasting and education to preserve linguistic continuity. Religious rights form a cornerstone, with the party emphasizing the Serbian Orthodox Church's pivotal role in sustaining Serb national and cultural identity, including restitution of church properties seized under previous regimes and opposition to the state-backed Montenegrin Orthodox Church as a divisive entity.[30][31] The party's platform frames these principles as essential for reconciliation and stability, arguing that safeguarding Serb rights prevents ethnic tensions while upholding democratic pluralism. Leader Andrija Mandić has articulated that Serbs in Montenegro have robustly preserved their identity despite historical pressures, calling for policies that foster mutual respect rather than retribution, such as fair census methodologies to accurately reflect self-declared Serb affiliation without coercion. This approach aligns with broader commitments to minority protections under European standards, while critiquing domestic implementations as inadequate or biased toward majority assimilation.[32][33]

Economic and social conservatism

The New Serb Democracy (NSD) aligns with social conservatism through its advocacy for Christian values and traditional family structures, as articulated by party officials who have publicly identified the organization as a "conservative Christian party." This orientation manifests in opposition to progressive social reforms perceived as undermining national and cultural norms, including resistance to initiatives expanding LGBT rights, amid broader coalition stances against rapid liberalization in Montenegro's socially conservative context. NSD's participation in governing coalitions has reinforced priorities favoring familial and religious traditions over expansive identity-based policies, consistent with its right-wing nationalist framework that privileges ethnic Serb community cohesion. Economically, NSD supports market-oriented development and fiscal prudence, forming an Economic and Economic Council in September 2024 to strategize on growth, regional integration, and positioning Montenegro as an economic leader. While specific policy blueprints emphasize broad reforms for sustainable prosperity—such as deepening cooperation and addressing debt—the party's platform shows limited differentiation from mainstream approaches, focusing instead on national priorities over ideological economic divides. In post-2020 coalitions, NSD has endorsed rule-of-law enhancements tied to economic revitalization, including anti-corruption measures to bolster investment and reduce state dominance in key sectors.[34][35]

Views on state symbols and historical narratives

New Serb Democracy (NSD) has consistently advocated for reforms to Montenegro's state symbols, arguing that the current flag, coat of arms, and anthem inadequately represent the country's multiethnic composition, particularly the Serb community, which constitutes approximately 28.7% of the population according to the 2011 census. The party has conditioned its support for key legislative changes, such as electoral reforms in 2012, on discussions about symbol modifications, viewing the existing symbols as overly tied to a singular Montenegrin ethnic narrative that marginalizes Serb historical contributions.[36] NSD leaders, including president Andrija Mandić, have displayed the Serbian tricolor flag in official settings like parliamentary offices, prompting legal challenges under Montenegro's Law on State Symbols for allegedly violating protocols that prioritize the national flag.[37][38] While NSD publicly affirms respect for state symbols as required by law, its actions reflect a preference for inclusive alternatives, such as official recognition of the red-white-blue tricolor—historically associated with Serb identity—as a complementary flag to accommodate ethnic minorities.[39] Mandić has occasionally demonstrated symbolic deference, such as standing during the national anthem "Oy, junačka Crna Goro" at official events in 2021, though prior inconsistencies fueled accusations of selective loyalty.[40] Party officials have criticized enforced exclusivity of symbols, proposing that local authorities, like in Budva, coordinate with state institutions while honoring minority sensitivities, as articulated by NSD member Goran Ivanović in 2021.[41] On historical narratives, NSD promotes a view of Montenegro as intrinsically linked to Serb heritage, portraying Serbs as foundational to the state's development from medieval principalities through the Kingdom of Montenegro and into the Yugoslavia era.[42] The party commemorates events like the 1992 formation of the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia as fulfilling a "historical wish of our people," emphasizing continuity of Serb statehood amid post-independence efforts to impose a distinct Montenegrin identity.[42] Mandić has framed Serb positions not as vengeful but as seeking "reconciliation and equality," rejecting narratives of Serb aggression in favor of mutual coexistence, as evidenced by joint statements with Albanian parties highlighting centuries of non-conflict between Serbs and Albanians in Montenegro.[43][44] This stance counters prevailing institutional narratives in Montenegro that prioritize a separate Montenegrin ethnogenesis, often downplaying Serb roles in anti-fascist struggles or framing pre-2006 unity with Serbia as regressive. NSD's rhetoric aligns with rehabilitation of figures and groups like Chetniks as patriots, a position inferred from opposition protests labeling the 2020-2023 government—including NSD—as a "Chetnik government," though the party avoids explicit endorsement to maintain coalition pragmatics.[45] Such views prioritize empirical Serb demographic and cultural continuity over politicized separations, as articulated in party communications since the 2015 platform emphasizing minority rights without secessionism.[46]

Foreign policy stances

Relations with Serbia and regional integration

The New Serb Democracy (NSD) maintains close political and ideological ties with Serbia, advocating for enhanced bilateral cooperation in economic, cultural, and security domains to strengthen bonds between the Serb populations in both countries. Party leader Andrija Mandić has emphasized comprehensive collaboration, stating in February 2021 that the NSD would work with Serbia and Republika Srpska on all issues of mutual interest, leveraging strong relations to advance Serb minority rights in Montenegro.[47] This stance aligns with alliances between NSD and Serbia's ruling Serbian Progressive Party (SNS), which have fostered exchanges on policy and support during elections.[48] The party has also pushed for dual citizenship agreements with Serbia to facilitate cross-border movement and identity preservation for ethnic Serbs, though such proposals have faced opposition from Montenegrin nationalists.[49] In terms of regional integration, NSD supports initiatives that promote practical cooperation among Balkan states while prioritizing ties with Serbia over supranational frameworks that might dilute national or ethnic identities. The party endorses the Open Balkan initiative—a Serbia-led effort for economic liberalization, free trade, and mobility among participating countries including Albania and North Macedonia—as a means to boost Montenegro's connectivity and prosperity without awaiting full EU accession. Mandić affirmed in June 2022 that Open Balkan would prove beneficial for Montenegro in short order, countering criticisms that it undermines European integration by offering immediate regional gains.[50] This position reflects NSD's broader emphasis on Balkan-wide mechanisms that accommodate Serb interests, such as joint infrastructure projects and cultural exchanges, amid Montenegro's stalled EU path and persistent ethnic divisions.[51]

Positions on EU and NATO accession

New Serb Democracy maintains a firm opposition to Montenegro's NATO membership, rooted in the party's advocacy for Serb interests and rejection of alliances perceived as hostile to Serbia due to the 1999 NATO intervention in Yugoslavia. In June 2017, leader Andrija Mandić declared that exiting NATO would be the first priority upon gaining power, prioritizing bonds with Russia and Serbia as Montenegro's closest historical allies over Western military integration.[52] The party has pushed for a binding referendum on accession, arguing that only direct citizen input could legitimize such a decision amid low public support and bypassing parliamentary fiat.[53] This stance persisted into recent years, with Mandić abstaining from a May 2024 parliamentary vote on deploying troops to a NATO mission, citing procedural oversight but underscoring ongoing reservations within the party.[54] On European Union accession, New Serb Democracy adopts a more conditional endorsement, favoring integration provided it preserves Serb cultural and political rights without compromising ties to Serbia or requiring concessions like Kosovo recognition. Historically skeptical of EU alignment in favor of Eurasian partnerships, the party criticized rapid Western orientation under prior governments but has pragmatically joined pro-EU coalitions since the 2023 elections, pledging not to obstruct negotiations while insisting on sovereignty safeguards.[55] Mandić affirmed this approach in May 2025 discussions with Greek parliamentary counterparts, highlighting EU membership as viable with mutual support from allies, though party rhetoric continues to emphasize dialogue on minority protections during reforms.[56] This positions EU progress as subordinate to national identity concerns, distinguishing it from the outright rejection of NATO.[57]

Responses to Kosovo, Srebrenica, and other Balkan disputes

New Serb Democracy has consistently opposed Montenegro's recognition of Kosovo's independence, viewing Kosovo as an integral part of Serbia. Party leader Andrija Mandić staged a hunger strike in 2008 to protest the recognition, declaring himself the "Martyr of Kosovo" and vowing never to accept it.[58][59] As Speaker of Montenegro's Parliament in 2024, Mandić referred to Kosovo's institutions as "provisional," aligning with the party's stance that they lack legitimacy.[60][61] The party's electoral platforms and coalition agreements have included pledges to withdraw recognition, framing it as essential to Serb national interests and regional stability.[29] Regarding Srebrenica, New Serb Democracy rejects the characterization of the 1995 events as genocide, portraying international resolutions as biased efforts to demonize Serbs. In May 2024, party MP Jovan Vučurović stated that Montenegro should not support the UN General Assembly's Srebrenica resolution, arguing it targets Serbia and equates Serb actions with uncontextualized criminality while ignoring broader wartime dynamics.[62][63] Mandić echoed this in 2024, asserting that Serbs oppose affirming the "alleged genocidal nature" of the massacre, emphasizing instead the need for balanced remembrance of all victims in the Bosnian War.[64] The party supported amendments to soften the UN resolution and introduced a counter-resolution in Montenegro's Parliament on Jasenovac, a WWII Croatian camp where thousands of Serbs perished, to highlight comparative historical injustices against Serbs.[65][66] In broader Balkan disputes, the party advocates for Serb unity across borders, defending Republika Srpska's autonomy in Bosnia and Herzegovina against perceived encroachments by Bosniak-led authorities. It has criticized secessionist threats from Bosnian Serb leader Milorad Dodik while prioritizing protection of Serb rights amid ethnic tensions.[29] New Serb Democracy also opposes narratives in Croatia-Montenegro relations that downplay Serb suffering in the 1990s wars, supporting commemorations of events like the Jasenovac resolution to counter what it sees as selective historical memory favoring non-Serb victims.[67] These positions reflect the party's emphasis on empirical redress of Serb grievances, often framing disputes as defensive responses to institutional biases in international bodies like the ICTY.[68]

Organizational structure

Leadership and key figures

Andrija Mandić serves as president of New Serb Democracy, a position he has held since the party's formation on January 24, 2009, via the merger of the Serb People's Party—which he led from 2003 to 2009—and the People's Socialist Party of Montenegro.[1] Born on January 19, 1965, in Šavnik, Mandić previously held roles such as deputy minister in the federal government of Serbia and Montenegro.[1] Under his leadership, the party has emphasized Serb minority interests in Montenegro, participating in opposition coalitions against the long-ruling Democratic Party of Socialists.[69] Mandić was elected Speaker of the Parliament of Montenegro on October 30, 2023, following the pro-European coalition's victory in the June 2023 parliamentary elections, a role he continues to occupy as of 2025.[70][29] In this capacity, he has navigated legislative proceedings amid coalition tensions, including a failed no-confidence motion against him in March 2024.[29] The party's executive structure includes a main board comprising over 100 members, such as Budimir Aleksić and Nikola Baltić, responsible for policy coordination and regional organization, though Mandić remains the dominant public face and decision-maker.[71] Prominent allies in broader coalitions, like Milan Knežević of the Democratic People's Party, have collaborated closely with New Serb Democracy on electoral lists, but party leadership centers on Mandić's longstanding authority.[27]

Party membership, youth wings, and regional branches

The New Serb Democracy maintains a membership structure limited to full members, as outlined in its statutes, without provisions for a separate supporter category—a distinction from many other Montenegrin parties that accommodate both. Exact membership figures are not publicly reported or disclosed in official documents or analyses.[31] The party is organized into municipal-level bodies known as opštinski odbori (municipal committees), which handle local activities and representation in areas with notable Serb communities, including Podgorica, Nikšić, Plevlja, and Herceg Novi.[31][72] These branches engage in regional advocacy, electoral mobilization, and community events, reflecting the party's centralized yet locally adaptive framework. Youth involvement occurs primarily through the voluntary Klub mladih (Youth Club), which functions as an informal organization rather than a autonomous wing, focusing on recruitment, training, and integration of younger members into party leadership and campaigns.[73][74] The club has produced figures who advance to senior roles, such as spokespersons, underscoring its role in nurturing future cadres without a formalized separate statute.[73] The party also operates affiliated groups like the Forum Žena for women, indicating a pattern of thematic internal organizations to engage specific demographics.[75]

Electoral history

Parliamentary elections

In the 2020 Montenegrin parliamentary election held on 30 August 2020, New Serb Democracy participated as a key component of the For the Future of Montenegro coalition, which opposed the long-ruling Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS) and emphasized protection of Serb minority rights and opposition to NATO membership without consensus. The coalition secured 27 seats in the 81-seat Assembly of Montenegro, marking the first defeat for the DPS-led government since independence, with NSD allocated 8 of those seats based on internal distribution agreements.[76] This result enabled the formation of a new government under Prime Minister Zdravko Krivokapić, where NSD leaders, including Andrija Mandić, held ministerial positions and influenced policies on national identity and church-state relations.[18] The 2023 snap parliamentary election on 11 June 2023 saw NSD lead the Za budućnost Crne Gore coalition, comprising pro-Serb parties focused on cultural preservation and skepticism toward rapid EU integration without addressing historical grievances. The coalition obtained 14 seats amid a fragmented vote, with no single bloc achieving a majority; NSD effectively controlled 9 seats through its dominant position in the alliance.[77][2] Post-election negotiations positioned NSD in the opposition initially, but Andrija Mandić was elected Speaker of the Parliament in October 2023 following a broad pro-European coalition's formation under Prime Minister Milojko Spajić, reflecting NSD's leverage in balancing ethnic and reformist dynamics. Voter turnout was 56.4%, with the coalition's performance bolstered by strong support in Serb-majority municipalities.[24] Prior to 2020, NSD had contested elections since its 2012 founding as part of the Democratic Front alliance, consistently securing representation in the 10-20 seat range for the bloc while advocating against perceived anti-Serb policies of the ruling regime. This pattern underscores the party's role in mobilizing the Serb community, which comprises about 28% of Montenegro's population, though exact seat allocations to NSD in earlier cycles like 2016 (18 coalition seats total) were not publicly itemized beyond the alliance level.[78]

Presidential elections

Andrija Mandić, president of New Serb Democracy, announced his candidacy for the Montenegrin presidency on 2 February 2023, representing a coalition of pro-Serb opposition parties including the Democratic People's Party, Movement for Changes, and Workers' Party.[79][80] In the first round of the 2023 presidential election on 19 March 2023, Mandić received 19.32% of the valid votes, finishing third behind incumbent Milo Đukanović of the Democratic Party of Socialists (35.37%) and Jakov Milatović of Europe Now Movement (28.92%).[81] With no candidate securing a majority, the election proceeded to a runoff on 2 April 2023 between Đukanović and Milatović, which Milatović won with 50.95%.[81] Prior to 2023, New Serb Democracy, formed in 2009, did not field its own presidential candidates but aligned with broader opposition efforts against Democratic Party of Socialists incumbents in the 2013 and 2018 elections, though specific endorsements yielded limited national impact relative to parliamentary contests.

Local and municipal elections

In municipal elections, New Serb Democracy (NSD) has primarily competed through coalitions with allied pro-Serb parties such as the Democratic People's Party (DNP) and Socialist People's Party (SNP), targeting areas with significant ethnic Serb populations. These coalitions have achieved varying success, often reflecting national political shifts following the 2020 parliamentary elections that ended Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS) dominance. NSD's local performance has been strongest in urban centers like Nikšić and Herceg Novi, where it has secured notable council seats, though it has struggled to form majorities independently.[82] In the March 2021 Nikšić municipal elections—the first major test after the national government's formation—the coalition of parties holding the parliamentary majority, including NSD components, won a majority of seats in the 45-member assembly, defeating the incumbent DPS-led list. This outcome reinforced the post-2020 pro-Serb bloc's local influence in Nikšić, a city with a substantial Serb community.[82][83] The October 2022 Podgorica local elections saw coalitions aligned with the national government, incorporating NSD, secure a collective majority in the city assembly despite the opposition "For Our City" list (backed by DPS) topping the vote share at around 28%. NSD's involvement helped maintain control in the capital, though turnout was low at approximately 52%.[84] Subsequent elections highlighted fragmentation. In the September 2024 Podgorica snap elections, prompted by assembly dissolution, pro-Serb lists including NSD affiliates garnered limited support amid a broader defeat for the ruling Europe Now Movement (PES)-led bloc; DPS claimed the largest share with 19 of 59 seats, leaving no clear majority.[85][86] In April 2025 Herceg Novi elections, NSD's "Novska lista" secured 10 of 35 assembly seats, up from previous cycles, positioning it as a key opposition player behind the winning Democrats list (12 seats). Similarly, in concurrent Nikšić voting, the ZBNK coalition—including NSD representatives—won 15 of 41 seats, narrowly trailing DPS's 16 and outperforming PES's 5, amid claims of strong Serb voter mobilization. These results underscore NSD's reliance on ethnic solidarity but vulnerability to DPS resurgence and PES competition.[87][88]

Government participation and policy impact

Held positions in coalitions

New Serb Democracy (NSD) first entered a ruling coalition following the August 2020 parliamentary elections as part of the "For the Future of Montenegro" alliance, which won 41 seats and ousted the Democratic Party of Socialists from power after three decades. This coalition formed the government of Prime Minister Zdravko Krivokapić on December 4, 2020, with NSD contributing to the parliamentary majority but holding no dedicated ministerial portfolios during the administration's tenure, which ended amid internal disputes on April 28, 2022.[24][89] After the June 2023 elections, NSD again ran within the "For the Future of Montenegro" list, securing 9 seats, and provided external support to the minority government led by Prime Minister Milojko Spajić of the Europe Now Movement, which received a vote of confidence on October 31, 2023. On the same date, NSD leader Andrija Mandić was elected President of the Parliament, a key legislative role influencing coalition dynamics.[10][77] NSD formally joined the executive branch on July 23, 2024, via a cabinet reshuffle expanding Spajić's government to include pro-Serb parties, allocating NSD one deputy prime minister position—held by Budimir Aleksić, overseeing education, science, and relations with religious communities—and two ministries: culture and sustainable development and tourism. This integration strengthened the coalition's 46-seat majority but drew scrutiny from EU and NATO allies over the party's pro-Serb orientation.[90][91]

Key legislative achievements and initiatives

The New Serb Democracy (NSD) has primarily influenced legislation through its participation in ruling coalitions following the 2020 parliamentary elections and Andrija Mandić's election as Speaker of the Parliament in October 2023. A notable initiative supported by NSD as part of the post-2020 coalition government was the January 2021 adoption of amendments to the 2019 Law on Freedom of Religion or Belief and Legal Status of Religious Communities, which temporarily suspended provisions allowing the state to claim ownership of religious properties not registered by December 31, 2020.[92] [93] This measure addressed criticisms that the original law disproportionately affected the Serbian Orthodox Church (SOC), a key constituency for NSD, by halting forced property registrations amid ongoing disputes over historical church assets.[92] NSD has consistently advocated for the equal official status of the Serbian language alongside Montenegrin, viewing restrictions on its use in education and administration as discriminatory against the Serb community. In August 2011, the party proposed amendments to ensure absolute equality between Serbian and Montenegrin in the school curriculum, conditioning support for any agreement on such parity.[94] More recently, in 2024, NSD announced plans to initiate constitutional amendments to elevate Serbian's status, aligning with broader demands for recognition of its prevalence in Serb-majority areas.[95] These efforts reflect NSD's programmatic emphasis on protecting Serb cultural and linguistic rights, though full legislative enactment remains pending amid debates over national identity.[94] Under Mandić's speakership, the Parliament has advanced judicial reforms, including measures to combat organized crime and corruption, with NSD emphasizing vetting of judges and prosecutors to enhance accountability.[96] [97] Mandić has also overseen the alignment of domestic laws with EU standards, facilitating the passage of acquis-related legislation through bipartisan consensus, as evidenced by the adoption of multiple European directives since 2023.[98] These initiatives support Montenegro's EU accession timeline, targeting full membership by 2028, while NSD prioritizes reforms that do not compromise Serb minority protections.[99]

Criticisms of governance record

Critics of the New Serb Democracy (NSD) have focused on its role in Montenegro's governments from 2020 to 2023, arguing that the party's emphasis on Serb ethnic interests contributed to legislative gridlock and stalled reforms essential for EU accession. During this period, NSD was part of the ruling coalition under Prime Minister Zdravko Krivokapić, which promised anti-corruption measures and judicial independence but faced accusations of paralysis, failing to open new EU negotiation clusters despite initial progress on NATO alignment. Opponents, including former ruling Democratic Party of Socialists (DPS), attributed the government's collapse in 2023 to ideological rifts exacerbated by NSD's demands for recognition of Serbian language parity and cultural symbols, which delayed key bills on media freedom and rule of law.[100] NSD's advocacy for constitutional changes to equalize Serbian and Montenegrin languages has drawn sharp rebukes for undermining national unity and contradicting the 2007 constitutional framework, with detractors claiming it prioritizes Belgrade-aligned narratives over civic cohesion. In August 2024, proposals backed by NSD to amend language laws were labeled unconstitutional by legal experts and pro-EU civil society, arguing they risked reopening debates on state symbols and halting EU Chapter 23 (judiciary and fundamental rights). Such positions, attributed to NSD leader Andrija Mandić, have been linked to broader parliamentary blocks, including delays in closing EU Chapter 31 on foreign policy, security, and defense, where Serb caucus insistence on Serbia-friendly resolutions stalled consensus in summer 2024.[101][67] Foreign policy stances under NSD influence have faced international scrutiny for perceived pro-Russian tilts, complicating Montenegro's Western integration despite the party's nominal EU support. Mandić's 2024 visit to Serbian President Aleksandar Vučić's election headquarters and criticisms of Montenegro's Council of Europe vote for Kosovo's membership were cited by opposition as violations of coalition pacts, fostering perceptions of divided loyalties. In July 2024, Croatia declared Mandić and two NSD allies persona non grata over a parliamentary resolution equating Srebrenica with Jasenovac, which critics said distorted Holocaust history to appease Serb nationalists and strained regional ties needed for EU progress.[29][66][102] At the local level, NSD-led municipalities have encountered operational crises, including internal party splits and service delivery shortfalls, signaling governance weaknesses. A July 2025 analysis highlighted disintegrating NSD structures in several towns, linking them to broader coalition tensions and failure to sustain post-2020 momentum on infrastructure or economic recovery. Bosniak and Albanian minority representatives criticized NSD's 2024 entry into a reconstructed government as opportunistic, arguing it sidelined minority rights in favor of Serb-centric policies, eroding trust in multi-ethnic governance.[103][104]

Controversies

Legal cases involving party leaders

Andrija Mandić, founder and president of New Serb Democracy, faced charges in connection with the alleged 2016 coup attempt in Montenegro, where authorities accused him of participating in a conspiracy to violently overthrow the government on election day, October 16, 2016.[105] The indictment, issued by the Special State Prosecutor's Office in December 2016, claimed Mandić was part of a criminal organization involving Serbian and Russian nationalists intent on seizing the parliament building, killing Prime Minister Milo Đukanović, and derailing Montenegro's NATO accession.[106] Mandić and co-defendant Milan Knežević, a Democratic Front ally whose party later merged elements with pro-Serb opposition, denied involvement, characterizing the case as a fabricated political persecution to neutralize anti-government voices.[107] In May 2019, the Podgorica High Court convicted Mandić on counts of conspiracy to commit a criminal act, attempted terrorism, and an attack on the constitutional order, imposing a five-year prison sentence; the ruling also implicated two Russian intelligence officers suspected of orchestrating the plot.[108] The verdict drew international scrutiny, with defenders arguing it relied on circumstantial evidence and witness testimonies later questioned for coercion under the long-ruling Democratic Party of Socialists regime.[109] Montenegro's Supreme Court quashed the convictions in February 2021, citing violations of fair trial standards and ordering a retrial, a decision that acquitted two Russians and prompted broader reevaluation of the evidence against domestic defendants.[106] The retrial proceeded amid Montenegro's post-2020 political shift away from Đukanović's dominance, with the Higher Court in Podgorica ultimately acquitting Mandić and all 13 co-defendants on July 12, 2024, after determining the prosecution failed to prove intent or direct participation beyond mere association.[110][111] Mandić, who had remained active in politics and assumed the role of parliamentary speaker post-2023 elections, hailed the outcome as vindication against judicial weaponization.[112] No other significant legal proceedings against New Serb Democracy's core leadership have resulted in sustained convictions, though Mandić has faced intermittent probes related to parliamentary immunity, such as a 2024 attempt to lift it over unrelated allegations, which was overruled by the chief prosecutor.[113]

Allegations of external influence and pro-Serb nationalism

New Serb Democracy (NSD) has been accused by critics, including pro-Western analysts and Montenegrin government officials, of serving as a conduit for Russian influence in Montenegro's politics. Party leader Andrija Mandić, who has led NSD since its founding in 2009, was convicted in 2017 alongside Milan Knežević of the allied Democratic Front for plotting to overthrow the government during Montenegro's October 2016 parliamentary elections, with the plot allegedly orchestrated by Russian military intelligence (GRU) agents to prevent NATO accession.[114] Mandić received a five-year sentence but was released early and later elected parliamentary speaker in 2023, with detractors claiming the verdict evidenced ties to Moscow despite his denials of any foreign involvement.[115] Further allegations point to NSD amplifying Kremlin narratives, such as opposition to NATO and EU integration timelines, exemplified by Mandić's 2024 push for a parliamentary resolution criticizing the EU's enlargement process and echoing Russian critiques of Western expansion.[116] Reports from think tanks describe NSD as acting as a "mouthpiece" for Russian propaganda, particularly in opposing sanctions against Moscow post-2022 Ukraine invasion, though the party has publicly supported Montenegro's EU path while advocating tactical pauses. Serbian funding and media amplification of pro-Serb parties like NSD have also been cited as extending Belgrade's soft power, blending it with Russian operations to foster anti-NATO sentiment in the region.[117] Regarding pro-Serb nationalism, NSD's platform emphasizes defending the rights of Montenegro's ethnic Serb minority (around 28.7% of the population per 2011 census), promoting cultural and linguistic ties to Serbia, and rejecting what it terms artificial Montenegrin separatism from a shared Serb heritage.[118] Critics, including Montenegrin nationalists and EU observers, allege this stance undermines state sovereignty by implicitly supporting greater Serbia concepts, such as through endorsements of Serbian Orthodox Church claims over Montenegrin religious sites and use of Serbian symbols in rallies.[119] The party's participation in the 2020-2023 coalition government drew protests decrying "Serbianization" policies, with opponents attributing stalled reforms to nationalist priorities over European alignment.[117] NSD counters that its advocacy is constitutional minority protection, not irredentism, amid ongoing debates over Montenegrin identity where Serb nationalists view "Montenegrin" ethnicity as a post-2006 political construct.[118]

Internal splits and coalition tensions

In the coalition government formed following the August 2020 parliamentary elections, the New Serb Democracy (NSD) participated as part of the "For the Future of Montenegro" alliance, alongside Zdravko Krivokapić's Movement for the Statehood of Montenegro and other partners including the Civic Movement URA. Tensions surfaced early over policy divergences, particularly regarding the draft law on religious freedom, which sought to return property seized from the Serbian Orthodox Church to its pre-communist owners, and the pace of EU accession talks under Chapter 23 (judiciary and fundamental rights). NSD advocated for stronger protections of Serb minority interests and closer ties with Serbia, clashing with more pro-European factions in the coalition that prioritized rapid NATO-aligned reforms without concessions on historical grievances. These frictions intensified in mid-2022, culminating in a government crisis in September when NSD leader Andrija Mandić publicly criticized Prime Minister Krivokapić's leadership for failing to deliver on electoral promises and mishandling internal policy coordination, raising the prospect of NSD withdrawing parliamentary support.[120] The Civic Movement URA formally exited the coalition in July 2022, citing irreconcilable differences on governance and foreign policy orientation, which further destabilized the executive and prompted multiple failed attempts at no-confidence votes against Krivokapić. By November 2022, Mandić reiterated calls for renegotiating the coalition structure, underscoring NSD's frustration with perceived dilutions of the alliance's original anti-DPS platform.[121] The broader Democratic Front (DF) alliance, which encompassed NSD, the Democratic People's Party (DNP), and the Movement for Changes (PzP), dissolved on May 14, 2023, ahead of snap elections, primarily due to strategic disagreements. The PzP, under Nebojša Medojević, opted to contest independently as part of a more EU-focused bloc, arguing that continued alignment with NSD's emphasis on Serb national identity hindered broader electoral appeal and Montenegro's Western integration.[122] NSD and DNP proceeded under the "For the Future of Montenegro" banner, retaining a platform prioritizing Serb rights and constitutional recognition of the 2013 referendum's implications, but the split highlighted persistent ideological rifts within the pro-opposition front between hardline nationalists and reformists.[123] NSD itself has maintained organizational cohesion without documented major internal factions or leadership challenges since its founding in 2012, attributing stability to Mandić's consistent control and focus on unifying Serb voters against perceived Montenegrin state discrimination. Coalition strains, however, have repeatedly tested NSD's parliamentary influence, contributing to the 2022-2023 governmental paralysis that delayed EU progress and amplified opposition critiques of executive inefficacy.[101]
