Italia Coppola

Italia Coppola (Italian: [iˈtaːlja ˈkɔppola]; née Pennino [penˈniːno]; December 12, 1912 – January 21, 2004) was the matriarch of the Coppola family.[2] She appeared in non-speaking cameo roles in several of her son Francis Ford Coppola's films, including One from the Heart, The Godfather, The Godfather Part II, and The Godfather Part III.[3][4] She also wrote the lyrics for the lullaby "Ninna Nanna" featured in The Godfather.[5] She was known for her Italian cooking and published a cookbook called Mama Coppola's Pasta Book in 2000.[6][7] Francis Ford Coppola named his 1998 Edizione Pennino zinfandel after her family's name and Italian heritage, and her nickname "Mammarella" is the name of her pasta and sauce line made by him.[8][9][10]

Early life

Born in an apartment over the family's Empire Theater in Brooklyn, New York City,[11] she was the youngest of six children of Anna (née Giaquinto) and Francesco Pennino, both from Naples, Italy.[7] Her father, graduated at San Pietro a Majella, was a musician and composer of Italian songs (particularly Neapolitan songs), an importer of silent Italian films and a movie theater owner.[1][12] Her five brothers were Louis, Rosary, Alfred, Humbert and Victor.[1]

Coppola family matriarch

Italia Pennino Coppola was the wife of Carmine Coppola and the mother of academic August Coppola, filmmaker Francis Ford Coppola and actress Talia Shire.[13] She was the maternal grandmother of actors Jason Schwartzman and Robert Carmine, and writer Matthew Shire, the paternal aunt of talent manager Anthony Pennino, and the paternal grandmother of actors Nicolas Cage and Marc Coppola, and directors Roman Coppola, Christopher Coppola and Sofia Coppola. Under her maiden name, Pennino, Italia Coppola was a lyricist known for writing "Non ci lasceremo mai", Connie's wedding song from The Godfather, the Sicilian lyrics for "Ninna-nanna a Michele", consisting of "The Godfather Waltz" and "Michael's Theme", composed by Nino Rota and sung by Nino Palermo in The Godfather Part II soundtrack, "Come Back to Love (the Chief's Death)" from Apocalypse Now, and songs from Carmine Coppola themes from Napoleon, The Black Stallion, and The Outsiders.[14][15][5][16][4] Italia Pennino Coppola is buried in San Fernando Mission Cemetery alongside her husband.[13][17][18]
