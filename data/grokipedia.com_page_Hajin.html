Hajin


Hajin is a town in Deir ez-Zor province, eastern Syria, that served as a key Islamic State stronghold during the Syrian Civil War.[1][2] Captured by the Islamic State in 2014, it became the site of the group's last major urban bastion in Syria by late 2018.[2] U.S.-backed Syrian Democratic Forces, primarily Kurdish-led militias with Arab allies, launched an offensive in September 2018 to dislodge ISIS fighters entrenched there, culminating in the town's capture on December 14, 2018, amid heavy combat and coalition airstrikes.[3][4] Following the victory, Hajin fell under the control of the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria, though the area has since seen tribal clashes, ISIS sleeper cell attacks, and tensions with Syrian government forces across the Euphrates.[2]

Geography

Location and physical features

Hajin lies in the Deir ez-Zor Governorate of eastern Syria, positioned along the western bank of the Euphrates River, roughly 90 kilometers southeast of Deir ez-Zor city and near the border with Iraq.[5][6] The town's approximate geographic coordinates are 34Â°42â€²N latitude and 40Â°50â€²E longitude.[7] The terrain surrounding Hajin features flat alluvial plains in the Euphrates River valley, which contrast with the encompassing arid steppe and desert expanses of the Syrian interior.[8] At an elevation of about 180 meters above sea level, the area benefits from river irrigation enabling limited agriculture, while the broader region exhibits low-relief topography with minimal vegetation outside irrigated zones.[9][7]

Demographics

Population estimates and trends

The last official population count for Hajin, from Syria's 2004 census by the Central Bureau of Statistics, recorded 37,935 residents.[19] Due to the Syrian civil war's onset in 2011, subsequent ISIS territorial control starting in 2014, and intensified fighting, demographic data became unreliable, with no comprehensive national census conducted since.[19] The Battle of Hajin (December 2018â€“February 2019), in which the Syrian Democratic Forces ousted ISIS remnants, triggered mass displacement; over 25,500 people fled the Hajin enclave to Al-Hol displacement camp in Hasakah Governorate between December 2018 and February 2019, more than tripling the camp's population at the time.[20] [21] Initial reports indicated at least 10,000 civilians trapped in the ISIS-held pocket during early offensive stages, exacerbating outflows amid heavy bombardment and ground clashes.[22] Post-battle returns occurred, with significant numbers relocating back from temporary sites like Al-Sha'etat, though infrastructure destruction and ongoing tribal tensions limited full recovery. Recent estimates place Hajin's population at approximately 29,000 as of 2025, reflecting net declines from wartime displacement, casualties, and emigration, tempered partially by returns and limited natural growth.[23] Persistent insecurity, including ISIS sleeper cell attacks as recently as 2025, continues to drive sporadic outflows and hampers stabilization.[24] Lack of updated official data underscores reliance on humanitarian assessments, which note broader Deir ez-Zor Governorate trends of internal displacement exceeding 1,000 persons annually in recent years amid factional violence.[20]

Ethnic and tribal composition

Hajin is predominantly inhabited by Sunni Arab tribes, reflecting the broader ethnic homogeneity of eastern Deir ez-Zor Governorate along the Euphrates River.[25][26] The population consists almost entirely of Arabs, with no significant non-Arab minorities such as Kurds or Assyrians reported in the town itself, unlike more northern areas of northeast Syria.[25] The dominant tribal confederation is Al-Aqidat (also spelled Al-Akeidat or Al-Uqaydat), the largest tribe in Deir ez-Zor, encompassing hundreds of thousands of members historically semi-nomadic but now largely settled in the region east of the Euphrates, including Hajin and the Al-Bukamal district.[26][25] Within Al-Aqidat, the Al-Shaitat (Shu'aytat) clan forms the primary subgroup in Hajin, numbering approximately 150,000 members across the confederation and playing a central role in local social and conflict dynamics, including resistance against ISIS in 2014.[26][25] Sub-tribes under Al-Aqidat in the area include Al-Bujamil (Bukamil), with around 600,000 members, concentrated in Hajin and nearby eastern villages.[26] Adjacent tribes with influence in the Hajin subdistrict and eastern countryside include Al-Bakara, the second-largest confederation in Deir ez-Zor with 700,000â€“800,000 members, whose sub-clans extend into areas near Hajin and have participated in local military formations.[26] Tribal leadership follows traditional patrilineal inheritance within prominent families, fostering competition and alliances that have shaped responses to the Syrian Civil War, ISIS occupation, and subsequent SDF control.[25] These structures emphasize extended family mediation and clan-based solidarity over state institutions.[25]

Economy

Agriculture and resources

Hajin's agricultural sector depends on irrigation from the Euphrates River, enabling cultivation of staple crops including wheat, barley, cotton, corn, rice, vegetables such as cauliflower and cabbage, potatoes, and fodder crops.[27][28][29] Nearby areas like Susa and Baghuz support palm orchards for date production.[30] In Hajin, Firat, and Susa combined, irrigated farmlands cover about 286,400 acres, though yields have declined due to war-related damage to canals, pumps, and drainage systems.[31][32] Farmers face ongoing challenges from insufficient irrigation water delivery, high costs for pumping (up to 13,000 Syrian pounds per hectare in 2022), and lack of administrative support for equipment maintenance under Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) control.[33] These issues, compounded by drought and soil depletion from repeated wheat monocropping, have reduced cultivated land and contributed to broader agricultural contraction in Deir ez-Zor.[34][35] Natural resources around Hajin are sparse, with no major oil or gas fields directly within the town; however, the surrounding Deir ez-Zor province hosts significant hydrocarbon deposits, including fields like Al-Omar, which have been contested and exploited by various groups including ISIS and now SDF-affiliated entities for revenue.[36][37] Local extraction remains minimal and informal, overshadowed by agriculture as the primary economic driver.[38]

Impact of conflict on economic activity

The Syrian civil war has profoundly disrupted economic activity in Hajin, a predominantly agricultural town reliant on Euphrates River irrigation for crops such as wheat, cotton, and vegetables. Irrigation infrastructure, including canals and pumps, suffered extensive damage from prolonged fighting, leading to reduced cultivable land and lower yields; in Deir ez-Zor Governorate, where Hajin is located, the destruction of these systems has devastated food security and agricultural productivity. Soil salinization from wartime overuse and neglect of water management further degraded farmland, exacerbating desertification and forcing many farmers to abandon fields.[39][40][41] During ISIS control from 2014 to 2018, economic activity shifted toward survival-oriented practices, including forced conscription of labor for farming and extortion-based taxation on harvests, which suppressed private initiative and diverted resources to the group's military needs rather than local development. The Battle of Hajin (December 2018), involving intense urban combat between the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF), supported by U.S.-led coalition airstrikes, and ISIS holdouts, resulted in widespread destruction of homes, roads, and agricultural facilities, rendering much of the town uninhabitable and halting seasonal planting. Post-battle clearance operations revealed extensive minefields and rubble that impeded farming resumption, with stabilization efforts focusing on demining and basic repairs but facing delays due to ongoing insecurity.[42][43] In the post-liberation period under SDF administration, intermittent ISIS insurgent attacks, tribal clashes, and resource competition have continued to undermine economic recovery, including looting of infrastructure and restrictions on movement that limit market access for produce. Combined with broader regional factors like drought and fuel shortages, these disruptions have led to persistent food insecurity, with many residents relying on aid rather than local production; agricultural output in eastern Syria remains far below pre-war levels, contributing to outmigration and informal economies. Efforts by international actors to rehabilitate water systems have yielded partial successes, but governance challenges and external pressures, such as Turkish-backed incursions, perpetuate instability.[44][45][46]

History

Pre-civil war period

Hajin, a town in the Deir ez-Zor Governorate along the Euphrates River, emerged as a settlement within the broader tribal landscape of eastern Syria, where Bedouin groups transitioned to semi-sedentary life in the 19th and 20th centuries. The area was dominated by Arab tribes, including the al-Shaitat (also spelled al-ShuÊ¿aytat), a Sunni Arab confederation originating from nomadic pastoralists who settled the Euphrates valley for agriculture and trade.[47][25] These tribes maintained customary governance through sheikhs, resolving disputes via traditional mechanisms that persisted alongside state authority.[48] Under the French Mandate (1920â€“1946) and subsequent Syrian independence, Hajin integrated into national administrative divisions, becoming the center of the Hajin nahiyah (subdistrict) within the Abu Kamal district. Irrigation systems, including ancient qanats adapted in the early 20th century, supported wheat, cotton, and date cultivation, sustaining a rural economy tied to the river's fertility.[49] The Ba'athist regime, in power from 1963, pursued land reforms and collectivization in the 1960sâ€“1970s, which affected tribal land holdings but reinforced state oversight without major localized resistance in Hajin.[47] By the early 21st century, Hajin remained a peripheral town with limited infrastructure, reflecting Deir ez-Zor's status as one of Syria's poorest provinces despite its agricultural potential. Tribal loyalties shaped social structures, with the al-Shaitat comprising the majority, though inter-tribal ties extended to neighboring groups like the Bakir and Aqidat.[25] The period saw no recorded large-scale conflicts specific to Hajin, maintaining relative calm under centralized rule until protests erupted across Syria in March 2011.[47]

Rise of ISIS control (2014â€“2017)

In 2014, the Islamic State (ISIS) rapidly expanded its territorial control across Deir ez-Zor Governorate, including the capture of Hajin and surrounding areas in the Euphrates River Valley, as part of a broader offensive against rival rebel factions such as Jabhat al-Nusra and local tribal militias. This followed ISIS's declaration of a caliphate in June 2014, with the group seizing the strategic Al-Qa'im border crossing between Deir ez-Zor and Iraq on June 21, enabling cross-border logistics and reinforcements. By mid-2014, ISIS dominated much of the governorate's rural expanse, leveraging superior firepower, suicide bombings, and targeted assassinations to eliminate opposition, while exploiting Sunni Arab grievances against the Assad regime to co-opt tribal elements. Hajin, a key agricultural and smuggling hub, fell under this consolidation, serving as an administrative outpost for ISIS's wilayat al-Sham province.[50][6] From 2014 to 2016, ISIS entrenched its rule in Hajin through a hybrid governance model combining brutal enforcement and pragmatic local integration, imposing hudud punishments via religious police (Hisbah) and sharia courts, while taxing agricultural outputâ€”primarily wheat and cottonâ€”and nearby oil fields to generate revenue estimated in the millions monthly. The group maintained a robust military footprint, with foreign fighters and local recruits training in the desert terrain, and used the area to launch assaults, including a major siege on Deir ez-Zor city that isolated regime-held enclaves until 2017. Initial acquiescence from tribes like the Bakir and Uqaydat stemmed from shared sectarian animus and protection rackets, but coercive measures, such as forced conscription and public executions, eroded support, fostering underground resistance networks.[6][51] Into 2017, ISIS's control in Hajin faced mounting pressure from converging threats but remained resilient, with the group retaining a southeastern pocket amid the loss of Raqqa and partial regime breakthroughs in Deir ez-Zor city on September 5. Activities intensified, including IED deployments and ambushes that comprised about one-third of regional clashes, as ISIS repositioned families and assets eastward while embedding sleeper cells among civilians for sustained insurgency potential. Hajin's strategic position along the Euphrates solidified its role as a logistics node for cross-Euphrates operations, though internal fractures and resource strains foreshadowed vulnerabilities exploited in subsequent SDF advances starting September 2017.[51][6][50]

Battle of Hajin (2017â€“2018)

The Battle of Hajin, occurring primarily from late November to mid-December 2018 as part of the broader Deir ez-Zor campaign, involved the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF)â€”a Kurdish-led coalition primarily comprising the YPG militiaâ€”attempting to dislodge the Islamic State (ISIS) from its last major urban stronghold in eastern Syria. Hajin, located along the Euphrates River in Deir ez-Zor Governorate, served as a key ISIS command hub, logistics node, and defensive position fortified with mines, trenches, and foreign fighters. The SDF offensive, codenamed al-Jazeera Storm, was enabled by extensive U.S.-led coalition airstrikes and artillery from Combined Joint Task Forceâ€“Operation Inherent Resolve (CJTF-OIR), which conducted over 230 strikes in the preceding weeks to degrade ISIS defenses. ISIS mounted fierce resistance using counterattacks, snipers, and urban guerrilla tactics, exploiting civilian presence as shields and poor weather conditions like sandstorms to hinder SDF advances.[2] Initial SDF probes into Hajin began in October 2018, with advances stalling amid heavy mining and ISIS ambushes; by late November, SDF units pushed from the north through villages like al-Bahra, facing repeated setbacks including a thwarted assault on December 9. On that date, ISIS fighters occupied Hajin Hospital to fire on SDF positions, prompting coalition airstrikes that neutralized the threat but highlighted ISIS's tactic of embedding in civilian infrastructure. SDF forces reported killing 49 ISIS militants in early November clashes alone, while sustaining 14 fatalities in one October engagement due to counterattacks amid adverse visibility. ISIS propaganda claimed dozens of SDF casualties over several days, though such reports typically exaggerate enemy losses for morale purposes. The battle underscored ISIS's resilience despite territorial contraction, with an estimated several hundred fighters defending Hajin, bolstered by local tribal recruits and foreign contingents.[2][52][53] By December 14, 2018, SDF fighters captured Hajin after encircling the town and clearing remaining pockets, marking the effective end of ISIS's control over its final Syrian urban center and reducing the group's territory to rural pockets southeast toward Baghuz. The victory relied heavily on coalition airpower, which inflicted disproportionate ISIS lossesâ€”SDF estimates placed total militant deaths in the hundredsâ€”while SDF casualties numbered in the dozens, though independent verification remains limited due to restricted access and partisan reporting. Civilian displacement exceeded 50,000 from Hajin and environs, with unverified reports of coalition strikes causing collateral deaths amid ISIS's human shielding; CJTF-OIR investigations into such incidents emphasized ISIS's responsibility for endangering noncombatants by co-locating forces. Post-capture, sporadic ISIS sleeper cells continued low-level attacks, exploiting local Arab tribal grievances against SDF governance to undermine stabilization efforts.[3][2][52]

Post-liberation era (2019â€“present)

Following the Syrian Democratic Forces' (SDF) declaration of victory over the Islamic State (ISIS) in Hajin on December 14, 2018, the town transitioned into a period of tentative stabilization under SDF administration beginning in early 2019, marked by the return of displaced residents to a landscape of extensive destruction from prior fighting.[5] Infrastructure, including homes and public buildings, remained largely in ruins, with limited reconstruction efforts hampered by ongoing insecurity and resource constraints.[5] The SDF integrated Hajin into the Deir ez-Zor Military Council, a localized structure formed in 2019 to decentralize command and incorporate Arab tribal elements into security and governance, aiming to address ethnic tensions in the predominantly Arab area.[54] This council oversaw civil administration, including basic services and local policing, though it faced internal clashes necessitating restructurings, such as one announced after infighting in the broader Deir ez-Zor framework.[55] Tribal notables have been consulted on joint administration models, reflecting efforts to balance Kurdish-led SDF authority with local Arab input.[56] Security deteriorated into a persistent low-intensity insurgency, with ISIS sleeper cells exploiting rural terrain and nighttime SDF withdrawals to conduct ambushes, assassinations, and extortion in Hajin and nearby villages like al-Busayrah and Diban.[57] Notable incidents include the beheading of a Hajin resident by ISIS after a checkpoint abduction in Diban in 2023, and an ambush on SDF forces led by a local commander in March 2025.[57] [58] ISIS activities encompassed ushr taxes (2.5% on goods and savings), moral enforcement, and recruitment offering $150â€“$200 monthly salaries in adjacent areas since mid-2022, contributing to a decline in reported attacks from 906 in 2019 to around 208 projected for 2023, yet sustaining resident fear and underreporting.[57] SDF responses included arrests of local officials accused of enabling ISIS, such as security manager Ahmad al-Khubayl on August 27, 2023.[57] Economic activity centered on subsistence agriculture, with oil fields nearby providing some revenue to SDF-controlled entities, but insurgency and governance frictions impeded broader recovery, leaving Hajin economically stagnant amid sporadic violence.[57] By 2025, amid shifting regional dynamics, SDF leadership pursued integration into emerging Syrian security frameworks, potentially altering local control structures in Deir ez-Zor, including Hajin.[59]

Governance and administration

SDF control and local structures

Following the defeat of ISIS in Hajin during the Battle of Baghouz in March 2019, the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) consolidated control over the town as part of the eastern Euphrates region under the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria (AANES). The AANES implemented a decentralized governance model inspired by democratic confederalism, establishing local civil councils to manage administration, public services, and communal decision-making. In Hajin, this took the form of the Hajin Local Council, which oversees local affairs such as resource allocation, education, and utilities, with a structure emphasizing co-presidency (one male and one female leader) and participatory assemblies involving residents and tribal representatives.[60][61] Security structures complement the civil administration, with the Hajin Military Councilâ€”a subunit of the broader Deir ez-Zor Military Council, an Arab-majority SDF affiliateâ€”responsible for internal policing and counter-ISIS operations. This council conducts patrols, detains suspected militants, and maintains checkpoints, as evidenced by its response to ISIS attacks, including the dismantling of explosives in October 2023. The Deir ez-Zor Military Council was restructured in October 2024 following internal clashes and defections, aiming to integrate more local Arab elements amid tribal grievances over conscription and resource distribution.[62][63][55] Despite these formal structures, SDF/AANES governance in Hajin has encountered persistent challenges rooted in ethnic and tribal dynamics. Local Arab tribes, predominant in the area, have resisted what they perceive as Kurdish overreach, with the administration criticized for marginalizing clan leaders and imposing ideological policies unsuited to Sunni Arab contexts. Protests and high-profile defections, such as that of the Hajin branch commander Abu al-Harith al-Shu'ayti in late 2024, highlight simmering discontent over arbitrary arrests, forced recruitment into SDF ranks (estimated at thousands from Deir ez-Zor since 2019), and inadequate service provision, including irrigation failures affecting agriculture. Tribal insurgencies, peaking in 2023 clashes that killed over 1,000, underscore failures in balancing local autonomy with centralized SDF command, leading to temporary truces but no resolution.[37][64][65][66] As of October 2025, SDF patrols continue in Hajin amid ongoing ISIS threats, but governance legitimacy remains fragile, with proposals for "joint administration" discussed with tribal notables in July 2025 failing to quell demands for greater independence. The AANES has initiated local elections in Hajin as part of broader communal processes, though participation is limited by security concerns and boycott calls from tribes. These efforts reflect attempts to localize power, yet reliance on SDF military enforcement perpetuates tensions, as Arab communities view the structures as extensions of YPG dominance rather than genuine self-rule.[56][24][60]

Relations with external actors

The Syrian Democratic Forces' (SDF) control over Hajin has involved fraught relations with the Syrian transitional government, marked by sporadic clashes in Deir ez-Zor province. On August 14, 2025, fighting broke out between SDF units and Syrian Ministry of Defense forces in eastern Deir ez-Zor, highlighting ongoing territorial disputes east of the Euphrates River.[67] Intermittent exchanges of fire continued into October 2025, including in Mazloum Town near Hajin, amid accusations of provocations from both sides.[68] Despite these tensions, SDF commander Mazloum Abdi announced on October 17, 2025, a preliminary agreement for integrating tens of thousands of SDF fighters into the Syrian armed forces as distinct formations under the Defense Ministry, potentially reshaping local administration in Hajin and broader eastern Syria.[69] The United States-led coalition has provided critical support to SDF operations in Hajin and Deir ez-Zor, including joint actions against ISIS remnants. U.S. Central Command enabled SDF raids near Dayr az-Zawr as late as January 2â€“3, 2025, targeting ISIS networks in the Hajin vicinity.[70] However, a drawdown in American troop presence has created security vacuums, contributing to heightened ISIS attacks in the region by October 2025.[24] U.S. diplomats, including Special Envoy Tom Barrack, mediated SDF-Syrian talks in Damascus, reflecting Washington's interest in stabilizing northeastern Syria without full withdrawal.[69] Turkey maintains adversarial relations with the SDF administration in Hajin, designating its core YPG component as a terrorist extension of the Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK). President Recep Tayyip Erdogan demanded on October 7, 2025, that the SDF fully integrate into Syrian state structures or face dissolution, aligning with Ankara's broader opposition to Kurdish autonomy east of the Euphrates.[71] Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan echoed this on October 8, 2025, urging the SDF to relinquish separatist aims amid integration negotiations.[72] Turkey has signaled potential military backing for Syrian forces against non-compliant SDF elements, including in Deir ez-Zor.[69]

Controversies and criticisms

Civilian casualties from coalition operations

During the Battle of Hajin (December 2017â€“March 2019), U.S.-led coalition airstrikes supporting Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) ground operations against ISIS resulted in multiple reported civilian casualties, primarily attributed to strikes in densely populated areas where ISIS fighters embedded among non-combatants, complicating target discrimination. Airwars, an independent monitoring organization, documented at least seven alleged coalition strikes on or near Hajin between January 2018 and December 2018, with local sources reporting between 1 and 40 civilian deaths per incident, including women and children; these figures draw from SDF-aligned and opposition media, which Airwars rates as partially corroborated but potentially inflated due to lack of independent verification on the ground.[73][74][75] Notable incidents included a November 8, 2018, strike on Hajin where 12â€“40 civilians died according to local reports, described by some sources as targeting an ISIS command center amid civilian homes.[75] On November 9, 2018, 11â€“26 civilians were killed in another Hajin airstrike, with witnesses claiming it hit a residential area.[74] A November 10 strike reportedly caused 4â€“26 deaths, including a massacre-like event per activist accounts.[73] The coalition later admitted one civilian death from a January 18, 2018, Hajin strike after initial denial, and four more near Hajin on May 23, 2018, following self-reported review, though it contested higher local claims in both cases, citing insufficient evidence of coalition responsibility.[76][77] The Syrian Observatory for Human Rights (SOHR), a UK-based monitor often reliant on opposition networks and criticized for potential anti-regime bias, reported 41 civilian deaths, including Iraqi nationals, from an October 2018 coalition strike on a Hajin mosque allegedly used by ISIS; the coalition investigated but found no evidence of civilian harm, attributing discrepancies to ISIS propaganda or misattribution.[78] Overall estimates for Hajin-specific casualties vary widely, with NGOs like Airwars suggesting dozens to low hundreds across incidentsâ€”far exceeding coalition admissions of under 10â€”due to ISIS's tactic of human shielding, limited SDF evacuation capabilities, and challenges in real-time intelligence amid urban fighting.[79] The coalition maintained strikes adhered to international law, emphasizing proportionality and prior warnings via leaflets and broadcasts, though critics including local civilians accused forces of inadequate precautions given the foreseeable civilian presence.[80] No comprehensive independent tally exists, as access restrictions and crossfire from ISIS and SDF artillery further obscured attribution.

Tribal tensions and human rights concerns under SDF rule

In Deir ez-Zor province, including Hajin, Arab tribes have clashed repeatedly with the Kurdish-led Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) since 2019, driven by grievances over perceived marginalization of local Arab populations, resource control disputes, and arbitrary arrests of tribal leaders.[37] Tensions escalated in August 2023 following the SDF's detention of Ahmad al-Khubail, a prominent Arab commander in the Deir ez-Zor Military Council affiliated with the SDF, sparking widespread tribal uprisings that included attacks on SDF positions in villages east of the Euphrates River.[46] These clashes, involving tribes such as the Bakir and Aqidat, resulted in dozens of deaths on both sides and the temporary seizure of SDF-held areas like Dhiban, highlighting underlying resentments over the SDF's imposition of administrative control in predominantly Arab regions.[81] By late 2023, the insurgency had evolved into a broader challenge for SDF authority, with tribal fighters exploiting local discontent over conscription, extortion, and unequal resource distribution from oil fields near Hajin.[37] Defections from SDF ranks, including commanders from Hajin's Deir ez-Zor Military Council branch such as Abu al-Harith al-Shu'ayti in December 2024, underscored fracturing alliances and accusations of favoritism toward Kurdish elements.[64] Protests in eastern Deir ez-Zor persisted into 2024, with demonstrators demanding greater tribal autonomy and an end to SDF dominance, occasionally leading to further defections and low-level skirmishes.[65] Human rights concerns under SDF rule in Hajin and surrounding areas include widespread arbitrary detentions, enforced disappearances, and reports of torture, particularly targeting suspected Islamic State affiliates but extending to civilians and tribal figures.[82] The Syrian Network for Human Rights documented nearly 3,417 cases of enforced disappearances by SDF-affiliated forces as of 2023, with at least 67 deaths under torture, including beatings, stress positions, and electrocution in facilities across northeast Syria.[82] A 2024 UN report detailed systemic ill-treatment in SDF-run detention centers, where detaineesâ€”many arrested post-2019 ISIS defeatâ€”faced inhumane conditions, including overcrowding and denial of medical care, contributing to mass deaths estimated in the hundreds.[83] Amnesty International reported in April 2024 that thousands detained after the territorial defeat of ISIS endured systematic violations, with evidence of torture and extrajudicial killings in SDF custody, often without due process or charges.[84] Local accounts from Deir ez-Zor, including Hajin, describe SDF security forces conducting raids on tribal homes, detaining males en masse on suspicion of insurgency ties, and using collective punishment such as property seizures to suppress dissent.[81] These practices have fueled cycles of retaliation, exacerbating tribal alienation despite SDF efforts at reconciliation through "joint administration" proposals discussed with local notables in October 2025.[56]

Persistent ISIS threats and security failures

Following the SDF's capture of Hajin in December 2018, ISIS transitioned to an insurgency model, embedding sleeper cells among local populations in the Deir ez-Zor countryside, including Hajin and adjacent villages like Gharanij and Al-Sousah.[57] These cells have conducted assassinations, IED attacks, and ambushes targeting SDF patrols and checkpoints, exploiting grievances from tribal Arab communities wary of Kurdish-led governance.[37] Between January and September 2024 alone, ISIS sleeper cells executed 194 attacks across northeast Syria, with notable activity in the Hajin area contributing to persistent instability.[85] SDF security operations have yielded intermittent successes, such as the September 30, 2025, clash in Daranj village east of Hajin, where Hajin Military Council forces killed one ISIS operative and captured a cell leader during an attempted ambush.[86] However, failures in preemptive intelligence and area control have allowed ISIS to inflict steady casualties; for instance, a landmine detonation in al-Hisan village on October 21, 2025, killed two SDF fighters, while a motorcycle-borne machine-gun attack on August 7, 2025, in eastern Deir ez-Zor claimed two more.[87][88] An escalated ISIS assault thwarted east of Deir ez-Zor on September 25, 2025, resulted in five SDF deaths, underscoring gaps in rapid response amid stretched resources.[89] Civilian-targeted violence has compounded threats, with ISIS cells increasing attacks on non-combatants in Deir ez-Zor by September 19, 2025, amid SDF dismantling efforts in nearby Al-Bsera villages that netted two arrests but failed to curb broader resurgence.[90][91] Local reports from Hajin in late October 2025 describe ISIS exploiting post-Assad power vacuums for overt returns, including street-level intimidation, as SDF patrols prove insufficient against ideologically sympathetic holdouts.[92] These lapses stem from SDF overextension in Arab-majority territories, where limited buy-in from tribes enables ISIS shadow networks to sustain recruitment and logistics, perpetuating a low-intensity threat despite U.S.-led coalition airstrikes.[37][57]
