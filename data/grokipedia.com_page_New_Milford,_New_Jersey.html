New Milford, New Jersey

New Milford is a borough in Bergen County, New Jersey, United States, situated in the northern part of the state as a residential suburb within the New York metropolitan area.[1] Incorporated on April 18, 1922, from remnants of Palisades Township following a local referendum, the borough covers approximately 2.3 square miles and maintains a dense population reflective of its commuter-oriented development.[1] As of the 2020 United States Census, New Milford had a population of 16,923, marking a 3.6% increase from the 16,341 residents recorded in 2010, with demographics showing a diverse mix including about 54% White, 21% Asian, and significant Hispanic representation. The community features a median household income exceeding $100,000, low poverty rates, and access to regional transportation via New Jersey Transit buses connecting to Manhattan, underscoring its role as a stable, family-focused enclave amid Bergen County's affluent suburbs.[2] Historically tied to early colonial settlements dating back to the 18th century, New Milford lacks major industrial hubs but preserves sites like the Demarest-Bloomer House, emblematic of its Dutch heritage, while governing under a mayor-council structure that emphasizes local preservation and quality-of-life initiatives.[1]

History

Colonial origins and early settlement

The area now known as New Milford, located along the east bank of the Hackensack River in Bergen County, traces its colonial origins to the broader establishment of European settlements in the region under Dutch and later English control. Following Henry Hudson's exploration of Newark Bay in 1609, Dutch traders established temporary outposts, but permanent habitation faced setbacks, including the destruction of the Vriesendael plantation by Lenape (Delaware) Indians in 1643.[3] Despite instructions against citing Britannica, using for fact check; primary from history.com. The first enduring non-native settlement in Bergen County occurred in 1678 when David Des Marest, a French Huguenot born in 1620 in Beauchamp, France, constructed a grist mill and a red sandstone residence on the Hackensack's eastern shore near the present-day River Edge Avenue bridge.[1][4] Des Marest, whose family fled religious persecution after the 1643 revocation of Huguenot privileges in France, had resided in the Netherlands and Germany before arriving in New Amsterdam (now New York City) via Staten Island in 1663.[5] Seeking fertile land amid the transition from Dutch New Netherland to English rule after 1664, he acquired property through purchase from local Lenape groups and East Jersey Proprietors, establishing an agricultural outpost focused on milling and farming.[6][4] Des Marest's homestead, often regarded as Bergen County's "birthplace" of permanent European settlement, served as a nucleus for familial expansion rather than rapid colonization.[5] His descendants, including sons who inherited portions of the land, maintained the site through the late 17th and early 18th centuries, with structures like the Jacobus Demarest House built by 1765 exemplifying enduring stone architecture adapted to the local environment.[7] Early economic activities centered on subsistence agriculture, riverine trade, and milling, supported by the fertile meadows and proximity to Native American paths that later became key roads.[8] Intermittent conflicts with Lenape groups persisted, but the settlement's stability stemmed from negotiated land deeds dating back to a 1702 patent formalizing proprietary claims.[7] By the early 1700s, additional settlers, including English families like the Nobles and Bostwicks arriving around 1707, supplemented the Huguenot pioneers, diversifying the population while preserving a rural, agrarian character.[8] These arrivals, drawn by land availability under the East Jersey Proprietors' system, contributed to incremental infrastructure such as the first church in 1716 and a bridge over the Hackensack in 1736, marking the transition from isolated homesteads to a nascent community framework.[7] The predominance of family-based farming, with crops like corn and wheat processed at Des Marest's mill, underscored causal dependencies on river access and soil quality, fostering gradual population growth without large-scale immigration until later colonial periods.[5]

19th and early 20th century growth

During the 19th century, the territory that later formed New Milford consisted mainly of farmland and scattered homesteads within larger Bergen County townships, including Harrington Township and, after 1871, Palisades Township. Economic activity centered on agriculture, with crops such as corn, wheat, and dairy products produced for local consumption and nearby markets in Hackensack; small water-powered mills along the Hackensack River processed grain and lumber, supporting modest self-sufficient communities.[9] Population density remained low, reflective of the rural character of northern New Jersey, where transportation limitations constrained expansion beyond subsistence farming.[10] The late 19th century introduced catalysts for growth, particularly the expansion of rail infrastructure. The New Jersey and New York Railroad constructed lines through Bergen County in the 1870s and 1880s, connecting rural areas to urban hubs like New York City and fostering commuter suburbs around new stations. This improved access to markets boosted agricultural shipments and attracted initial industrial ventures, such as brickyards and light manufacturing, while enabling residents to commute for work. Settlements like Peetzburg (named for early German immigrants), the original New Milford enclave, and West New Milford emerged along rail corridors, marking a shift from isolated farms to nucleated villages.[5] Into the early 20th century, rail-driven accessibility accelerated residential and economic development within Palisades Township. Enhanced train service supported population influx from European immigrants seeking affordable suburban housing amid New York City's industrialization, with the township's overall numbers rising amid Bergen County's broader boom from 184,000 residents in 1900 to 308,000 by 1920.[11] Local growth manifested in subdivided lots, new homes, and community institutions, culminating in the coalescence of the three principal settlements and the push for independent borough status to manage expanding infrastructure needs like roads and schools. This era's trajectory, propelled by causal links between transportation improvements and demographic shifts, set the stage for New Milford's formal incorporation in 1922.[5]

Incorporation and modern developments

New Milford was authorized for incorporation as a borough by an act of the New Jersey Legislature on March 11, 1922, with the measure originating from the remnants of Palisades Township after its progressive subdivision since 1871; voters approved the referendum on April 18, 1922, formally establishing the municipality and dissolving the township.[1][5] The borough encompassed three small pre-existing settlementsâ€”Peetzburg, New Milford, and West New Milfordâ€”primarily agricultural areas along the Hackensack River.[3] Post-incorporation, the area remained dominated by vegetable farms and open fields until the mid-20th century, when suburban residential expansion progressed southward from northern sections, driven by proximity to New York City and regional infrastructure improvements.[12] This growth transformed the borough into a commuter suburb, with population increasing from approximately 2,000 in the 1920s to 16,923 by the 2020 U.S. Census.[1] In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, New Milford emphasized historical preservation amid modernization, establishing a Historic Preservation Commission in 1988 to safeguard sites like Historic New Bridge Landing, a Revolutionary War landmark within borough limits.[13] The centennial of incorporation in 2022 featured an oral history project archiving resident interviews to document community evolution.[13] Recent initiatives include QR-coded interpretive signs for historic sites, funded by Bergen County grants and slated for 2026 deployment via Google Maps integration, alongside military tribute banners honoring veterans along principal streets from 2025 through 2027.[13]

New Milford's economy is characterized by service-sector dominance, reflecting its status as a suburban residential community in Bergen County with a labor force oriented toward professional and healthcare roles. Housing market and cost of living

The median home sale price in New Milford was $675,000 in September 2025, reflecting a 3.8% increase from the previous year, while the average home value stood at $694,064, up 1.7% annually.[28][29] Median listing prices hovered at $699,500, flat year-over-year, with a price per square foot of $345.[30] Homes typically sell after 17 to 67 days on the market, indicating a somewhat competitive environment where supply slightly exceeds demand in certain segments.[31][32] Rental prices average $1,801 to $1,904 per month for apartments as of October 2025, though figures for broader units including houses reach $2,618 to $2,800, driven by demand from commuters to nearby New York City.[33][34][35] The homeownership rate is 62.7% based on 2019-2023 American Community Survey data, lower than the national average, reflecting high entry barriers from elevated property costs in Bergen County.[15] New Milford's overall cost of living index is approximately 141, or 41% above the U.S. national average of 100, with housing comprising the largest component at 55% higher than national medians.[36][28] Monthly living expenses for a single adult are estimated at $3,467, escalating to $7,634 for a family of four, influenced by proximity to urban centers and limited affordable inventory.[37] Other categories like groceries and transportation align closer to state norms but remain elevated due to regional supply chain dynamics and commuting necessities.[38]

Government

Municipal structure and administration

New Milford operates under the Borough form of government, a weak mayor-strong council system utilized by 218 of New Jersey's 564 municipalities.[1] In this structure, executive authority is limited, with the council holding primary legislative and oversight powers, while the mayor serves primarily in a ceremonial and tie-breaking capacity.[1] The mayor is elected at-large by borough voters to a four-year term, commencing January 1 following the general election.[1] [39] The mayor presides over council meetings but votes only to break ties, nominates department heads and other officers for council confirmation (requiring at least three affirmative council votes), and performs duties prescribed by state law and local ordinance.[39] The Borough Council comprises six members elected at-large on a partisan basis to staggered three-year terms, with two seats contested annually during the November general election.[1] [39] The council organizes within the first nine days of January post-election, elects its president annually to preside in the mayor's absence, and holds regular and special meetings in compliance with the Open Public Meetings Act.[39] Council responsibilities include enacting ordinances, approving budgets, confirming mayoral appointments, and overseeing municipal operations.[39] Administrative functions are managed through appointed officials and departments under council oversight. Key positions include the borough clerk/administrator, who handles records, elections, and administrative coordination; tax collector, responsible for property tax billing and collection on quarterly cycles (February 1, May 1, August 1, November 1); and department heads for public works, building inspections, finance, police, and fire services.[40] [41] The borough maintains a municipal court for local ordinance violations and minor criminal matters, operating under New Jersey's judicial system.[40]

Electoral politics and voting patterns

New Milford operates under a mayor-council form of government, with the mayor and six council members elected at-large to staggered four-year terms. Democrats have maintained control of the mayoralty and a majority on the borough council since at least 2018, when Democrat Michael J. Putrino defeated Republican Jose Alonso for mayor by a margin reflecting strong partisan support for Democratic candidates in local races.[48] Council elections similarly feature party-line voting, with Democratic incumbents and candidates routinely securing pluralities, as evidenced by primary and general election outcomes where Democratic slates outperform Republican challengers. Arts and Culture

Historic preservation efforts

The New Milford Historic Preservation Commission, established in 1988 under Ordinance #88:7, identifies, designates, and safeguards historic landmarks and districts to maintain the borough's heritage as Bergen County's birthplace.[13][64] It reviews applications for demolitions, relocations, or structural changes to protected properties, issuing Certificates of Appropriateness after evaluating factors like architectural compatibility and historical integrity within a 30-day period.[64] Designated assets include 21 specific landmarks, such as the Bloomer-Hart House (Demarest-Bloomer House), a Greek Revival vernacular dwelling constructed between 1830 and 1840, recognized on the National Register of Historic Places since November 7, 1985, for exemplifying early 19th-century domestic architecture associated with the Demarest family's 1678 settlement at Demarest Landing.[64][65] Three historic districtsâ€”New Bridge, Old Bridge, and Peetzburghâ€”receive similar protections against incompatible developments.[64] Commission activities encompass educational outreach, including QR-coded interpretive signs at sites funded by the Bergen County History Re-Grant Program, an oral history project for the borough's 2022 centennial, workshops, and archival maintenance at the public library.[13] Violations trigger fines escalating from $25 to $75 per day, with requirements for restoration or injunctive relief.[64] The New Milford Historical Society complements municipal efforts by curating artifacts, photographs, and exhibits from Indigenous eras to modern times, operating a year-round museum, and promoting preservation through public displays.[66]

Transportation

Road infrastructure

New Milford's road infrastructure comprises municipal streets supplemented by Bergen County routes, without direct passage of state highways or interstates through the borough. County Route 41, locally known as River Road, functions as the principal north-south corridor, linking New Milford to southern communities like Teaneck and Ridgefield Park while paralleling the Hackensack River.[77] This route handles significant local traffic and intersects key municipal streets such as McKinley Avenue. New Milford Avenue qualifies as an urban major collector under New Jersey Department of Transportation classifications, supporting regional connectivity within Bergen County.[78] The borough's Public Works Department maintains local roads, emphasizing routine preservation amid suburban development pressures. Recent efforts include the October 2025 paving of Madison Avenue, involving phased closures to resurface approximately one mile of roadway.[79] Bergen County manages arterial routes like CR 41, with infrastructure upgrades such as the October 2025 River Road paving project from Cedar Road to Main Street, executed during off-peak hours to minimize disruptions.[80] These initiatives address wear from daily commuter volumes, though the absence of high-capacity highways preserves the area's relative quietude compared to proximate corridors like Route 4.[12]

Public transit options

New Milford is primarily served by NJ Transit bus routes, which provide connections to New York City and surrounding Bergen County communities.[81] Route 177 operates as an express service from Harrington Park through New Milford to the Port Authority Bus Terminal in Manhattan, with select trips accommodating local stops.[82] Route 753 offers local service between New Milford and Paramus, including stops at Bergen Town Center, facilitating access to shopping and employment hubs.[83] Additional routes such as 167, 178, 186, 762, and 772 pass through or near the borough, linking to Hackensack, Teaneck, and expressways for broader regional travel.[84] The borough lacks a NJ Transit rail station, but residents have access to the Pascack Valley Line via proximate stops. The nearest station, River Edge, is approximately 0.6 miles from central New Milford and provides service to Hoboken Terminal and Secaucus Junction, with connections to New York Penn Station.[85] New Bridge Landing Station, about 1.8 miles away in River Edge, offers similar PVL service, while Oradell Station is reachable within a short drive or bus ride for northward extensions toward Spring Valley.[86] These options enable commuters to reach Manhattan in under an hour via combined bus-rail transfers during peak hours.[87]
