Elizabeth L. Gardner

Elizabeth L. Gardner (July 24, 1921 – December 22, 2011), known as Libby, was an American civilian pilot who served in the Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) program during World War II, ferrying aircraft, towing targets for aerial gunnery practice, and performing other non-combat flight duties to support the war effort amid a shortage of male pilots.[1][2] Born and raised in Rockford, Illinois, Gardner enlisted after marrying and briefly using the surname Remba; at age 22, she underwent training with WASP class 43-W-6 at Avenger Field in Sweetwater, Texas, before assignment to Dodge City Army Air Base in Kansas.[1][3] She notably piloted the Martin B-26 Marauder medium bomber, as documented in a National Archives photograph capturing her at the controls prior to takeoff.[4] Gardner's service exemplified the WASP's role in freeing male pilots for combat overseas, though the program faced initial resistance and received veteran status only in 1977, with collective recognition via the Congressional Gold Medal awarded in 2009.[1] Prior to her wartime contributions, she had been a housewife, reflecting the program's appeal to women with prior flying experience or determination to enter aviation despite societal barriers.[2]

Early Life

Birth and Childhood

Elizabeth L. Gardner was born on July 24, 1921, in Rockford, Illinois.[5][2] She grew up in the same city, part of a family that resided there during her formative years.[1] Limited public records detail her childhood experiences, though she remained in Rockford through her adolescence, attending local schools as a resident of the community.[1] No specific anecdotes or family influences from this period have been widely documented in aviation historical accounts focused on her later piloting career.[1]

Education and Pre-War Interests

Elizabeth L. Gardner graduated from Rockford High School in Rockford, Illinois, in 1939, at the age of 18.[1][6] No records indicate pursuit of higher education following her secondary schooling. Prior to the United States' entry into World War II in December 1941, Gardner's primary interests centered on domestic life; she married and became a mother, devoting herself to homemaking and family care in Rockford.[1][2] In her own account, she described this period as one in which she "stayed home to take care of my family," reflecting a conventional pre-war role for many women of her generation without documented involvement in aviation or other professional pursuits at that time.[2]

Family and Marriage

Elizabeth L. Gardner was born on July 24, 1921, in Rockford, Illinois, to parents Herbert Cleveland Gardner and Grace G. Lizer Gardner; she had at least one sibling, a brother named Robert.[7] Gardner married briefly in her early adulthood to Wilbert Bigler, though no children resulted from this union.[6] Prior to her involvement in World War II, Gardner worked as a housewife and mother, caring for her family at home in Rockford.[1][2] In a 1943 interview, she stated, "Before the war, I was a housewife and a mother who stayed home to take care of my family."[8] She later cited family obligations, including her mother's illness, as a factor in leaving her WASP duties early.[9] After the war, Gardner married Michael Remba on an unspecified date in 1951 in Manhattan, New York City.[7] The couple had one daughter, Eve Remba, born July 20, 1954.[6][10] They divorced in 1967 in the Bronx, New York, after which Gardner reverted to her maiden name.[7][6]

World War II Service in the WASP

Motivation and Application

Elizabeth L. Gardner, born in 1921 in Rockford, Illinois, developed an interest in aviation amid the escalating tensions of World War II. As a young housewife and mother prior to the war, she initially pursued education, attending two years of college as an English major and working as a secretary, but the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, prompted her to seek active involvement in the war effort. Influenced by a boyfriend who aspired to join the U.S. Army Air Corps but did not succeed, Gardner resolved not to remain sidelined, stating, "I decided to sign up for a ground school course to get my private pilot’s license."[11] To build flying experience affordably, Gardner secured employment at Piper Aircraft in spring 1942 for $12 per week, which allowed her access to a flying club with reduced-cost lessons; she soloed after just 12 hours of instruction and passed the required written tests. Answering the broader recruitment call for experienced civilian women pilots to ferry military aircraft stateside and free male pilots for combat, she applied to the Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) program, one of approximately 25,000 applicants, though only 1,830 were accepted for training and 1,074 graduated.[11][12] Her application succeeded following an interview with WASP director Jacqueline Cochran in her New York City office—Gardner's first trip to the city—and passing the Army Air Forces physical examination in Harrisburg, Pennsylvania. She received a telegram in April 1943 instructing her to report for training at Avenger Field in Sweetwater, Texas, entering Class 43-W-6 and graduating that October as a 22-year-old with a commercial pilot's license. This process reflected the program's emphasis on selecting women with prior flight hours to expedite their transition to military aircraft operations, aligning with Gardner's patriotic drive to contribute directly to the war effort through aviation.[11]

Training Period

Elizabeth L. Gardner, also known as Libby Gardner, enrolled in the Women's Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) Class 43-W-6, which commenced training on April 25, 1943, at Avenger Field in Sweetwater, Texas.[13] The program for this class involved 123 trainees undergoing a rigorous curriculum modeled after Army Air Forces flight training, including ground school instruction in subjects such as navigation, meteorology, and aircraft mechanics, followed by phases of primary, basic, and advanced flight training in progressively more complex aircraft.[13] Gardner, who had prior civilian flying experience as a requirement for WASP acceptance, completed this approximately six-month primary training regimen alongside her classmates.[11] Prior to entering the full WASP training at Avenger Field, Gardner received two days of preliminary instruction under Lieutenant Colonel Paul Tibbets, an Army Air Forces officer later known for commanding the B-29 mission that dropped the atomic bomb on Hiroshima.[1] This brief orientation likely served as an initial familiarization with military protocols and aircraft handling, though specific details of its content remain undocumented in available records. The Avenger Field training emphasized discipline, precision, and adaptation to military standards, with trainees facing high attrition rates due to the demanding physical and technical requirements.[13] On October 4, 1943, Gardner graduated from Class 43-W-6, earning her wings as one of 84 completers out of the original 123 entrants, marking the culmination of her primary WASP training.[13] Immediately following this, she was assigned to three months of transitional training on the Martin B-26 Marauder medium bomber at Dodge City Army Air Base in Kansas, a specialized phase to qualify her for towing targets and other tactical support missions in heavier twin-engine aircraft.[11] This extension honed her skills for operational duties, reflecting the WASP program's structure of building from general proficiency to type-specific expertise.[11]

Flight Assignments and Duties

![Elizabeth L. Gardner at the controls of a Martin B-26 Marauder at Harlingen Army Air Field][float-right] Following graduation from WASP Class 43-W-6 at Avenger Field in Sweetwater, Texas, in October 1943, Gardner underwent transitional training on the Martin B-26 Marauder at Dodge City Army Air Base in Kansas beginning in January 1944.[11] She was subsequently assigned to the flexible gunnery school at Harlingen Army Air Field in Texas, also in January 1944, where she flew the B-26 to support training operations.[11] Gardner's primary duties included towing aerial targets for gunnery practice, a role that utilized the B-26 and its AT-23 trainer variant to simulate combat conditions for anti-aircraft and aerial gunnery training.[1] She received specialized training as both a test pilot and flight instructor during her service, enabling her to conduct proficiency flights and evaluate aircraft performance in these multi-engine bombers.[1] On her first day reporting for B-26 duty at Harlingen, Gardner demonstrated confidence in handling the aircraft, reportedly responding to the base commander's skepticism about women pilots with, “You won’t think so after we fly for you, Sir,” before executing a successful flight.[11] These assignments contributed to the WASP program's efforts to ferry, test, and train with over 78 types of military aircraft, freeing male pilots for combat roles.[1]

Notable Incidents and Iconic Photograph

Gardner's service included qualification on the Martin B-26 Marauder, a twin-engine medium bomber notorious for its demanding flight characteristics and high pilot attrition rate during early wartime operations, earning nicknames like "flying coffin" among aviators.[1] As one of the few women authorized to fly this aircraft in non-combat roles such as target towing and proficiency training, her assignments at bases like Harlingen Army Air Field exposed her to the inherent risks of operating a type prone to stall accidents and structural failures under certain conditions.[11] No personal accidents are documented in her record, but the B-26's reputation underscored the technical proficiency required of WASP pilots.[6] A widely recognized photograph from circa 1943 captures Gardner, then 22, seated in the cockpit of a B-26 at Harlingen Army Air Field, Texas, scanning the runway prior to takeoff.[4] Preserved in the U.S. National Archives as identifier 542191, the image exemplifies the poise of WASP pilots amid routine yet hazardous missions, including ferry flights and gunnery support that demanded precision in adverse weather and mechanical stresses. This photograph has become emblematic of the program's contributions to freeing male pilots for overseas combat by handling domestic air operations.[11]

Post-War Life

Return to Civilian Roles

Following the deactivation of the Women Airforce Service Pilots program on December 20, 1944, Elizabeth L. Gardner promptly resumed civilian aviation activities. In December 1945, she served as a test pilot for General Textile Mills, evaluating an experimental whole-aircraft parachute system designed to enable safe emergency descents for small planes; during trials, she executed two bailouts after the parachute deployed but tangled, demonstrating her proficiency in high-risk evaluations.[1] Gardner then joined Piper Aircraft Corporation in Pennsylvania as a commercial pilot, where her duties included ferrying aircraft to customers, coordinating with the Department of Defense on procurement matters, and drafting speeches for company founder William T. Piper.[1] These roles leveraged her WASP-honed expertise in multi-engine aircraft and instrument flight, sustaining her professional involvement in the burgeoning postwar aviation industry amid surplus military planes and commercial expansion.[7] Her aviation career extended into subsequent decades, encompassing flight instruction and commercial operations, though specific later employers remain less documented; this continuity contrasted with many WASP contemporaries who faced barriers to sustained flying due to gender restrictions in airlines and military aviation.[7] Gardner balanced these pursuits with family responsibilities, having married prior to her WASP service and adopted the surname Remba.[1]

Advocacy for WASP Recognition

Following the deactivation of the Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) program on December 20, 1944, its approximately 1,100 members, including Elizabeth L. Gardner, received no military benefits, veteran status, or honorable discharge, despite flying over 60 million miles in domestic operations to ferry aircraft, tow targets, and test planes, thereby freeing male pilots for combat. This oversight fueled a multi-decade advocacy campaign by WASP alumni through organizations like the Order of Fifinella, involving congressional testimony, letter-writing drives, and legal challenges to affirm their service's military equivalence. Gardner, residing in New York after her post-war aviation roles, maintained involvement in these networks, as evidenced by her correspondence and participation in WASP newsletters during the 1980s, which coordinated fundraising and awareness efforts amid ongoing pushes for redress.[14] The campaign gained traction in the 1970s, culminating in the passage of Public Law 95-202 on November 23, 1977, which retroactively granted WASP members veteran status and access to benefits after persistent lobbying, including by Senator Barry Goldwater, who highlighted their hazardous duties—38 fatalities occurred in service, comparable to active-duty rates. While specific actions by Gardner in this phase are not prominently documented, her sustained affiliation with WASP groups positioned her within the cohort driving these reforms, countering initial opposition from veterans' organizations like the Veterans of Foreign Wars, which argued the WASPs' civilian-contractor status undermined combat veterans' exclusivity.[1] Renewed momentum in the 2000s focused on symbolic honors, leading to H.R. 758, signed into law by President Barack Obama on July 1, 2009, authorizing a Congressional Gold Medal for the WASP unit. Gardner actively supported this phase; in September 2009, she signed a ceremonial copy of the Gold Medal Act at the American Airpower Museum in Farmingdale, New York, underscoring her endorsement of the recognition. The following month, in October 2009, she recorded an oral history interview, detailing her B-26 Marauder ferrying experiences and contributing to archival efforts that bolstered public and legislative appreciation of WASP sacrifices. Surviving to receive the medal—presented collectively to about 300 living members at a March 10, 2010, U.S. Capitol ceremony—Gardner's participation exemplified the personal commitment of original pilots to rectifying historical oversights through direct engagement.[11][15]

Recognition, Controversies, and Legacy

Veteran Status and Awards

The Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP), including Elizabeth L. Gardner, initially served as civilians under contract with the U.S. Army Air Forces and were not granted military veteran status during or immediately after World War II. On November 23, 1977, President Jimmy Carter signed Public Law 95-202, Title IV, which retroactively recognized WASP service as active duty for veterans' benefits purposes, such as eligibility for the GI Bill and limited health care, following years of advocacy by former pilots.[16] This legislation marked the first federal acknowledgment of their wartime contributions as equivalent to military service, though full benefits parity with other veterans was not achieved until subsequent reforms. In 2009, Congress authorized the Congressional Gold Medal—the highest civilian honor—for the WASP program collectively via Public Law 111-40, signed by President Barack Obama on July 1, 2009, to honor the 1,074 women who flew over 60 million miles in support of the war effort.[17] The medal was presented in a ceremony on March 10, 2010, at the U.S. Capitol, with replicas distributed to surviving members and next of kin; Gardner, who lived until December 22, 2011, received hers as part of this group recognition.[18][19] No individual military decorations or personal awards beyond this unit citation are recorded for Gardner.

Broader Debates on WASP Program

The Women's Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) program sparked debates over its operational effectiveness and strategic necessity during World War II, with proponents arguing it addressed acute pilot shortages by enabling women to ferry over 12,650 aircraft, log 60 million flight miles, and test experimental planes, thereby freeing male pilots for combat overseas.[20] Critics, including some military leaders and returning male pilots, questioned its long-term value as male aviators became available post-1944, leading to the program's abrupt deactivation on December 20, 1944, amid claims it duplicated civilian efforts and strained resources without proportional wartime gains.[21] Empirical data, however, indicated comparable performance to male counterparts, with WASP accident rates lower overall and fatalities numbering 38 among approximately 1,074 graduates, often attributed to hazardous duties like towing targets under fire rather than pilot error.[22] Safety concerns fueled additional contention, as the program's training regimen at bases like Avenger Field resulted in 11 fatalities during instruction, prompting scrutiny over women's purported physical limits in high-risk aviation; yet analyses showed WASP fatality rates aligned with or undercut those of male pilots in similar non-combat roles, countering narratives of inherent female incompetence.[23] Opposition from male civilian pilots' unions intensified these debates, as they lobbied Congress against militarizing the WASP in 1944, fearing job displacement and viewing the initiative as an unauthorized expansion of federal authority that bypassed legislative oversight.[24] This resistance culminated in the defeat of a militarization bill by a 19-vote margin in June 1944, reflecting broader tensions over integrating women into quasi-military functions without full accountability, such as subjection to court-martial.[25] Postwar recognition debates centered on veteran status, denied initially due to the WASP's civilian contractor designation, which excluded benefits despite equivalent hazards; the Veterans Administration and groups like the Veterans of Foreign Wars argued against retroactive honors, citing lack of combat exposure and potential precedent for other non-uniformed contributors.[26] Congress granted military status in 1977 under President Jimmy Carter, following decades of advocacy, but not before highlighting systemic gender biases in evaluating women's wartime roles.[20] Gender and racial dimensions amplified controversies, as WASP faced entrenched sexism manifesting in harassment, restricted base access, and assumptions of emotional fragility, challenging first-wave feminist gains in aviation while exposing limits to wartime egalitarianism.[27] Racially, the program excluded Black women outright amid military segregation, accepting only two Asian American pilots—Hazel Ying Lee and Margaret Gee—after external pressure, underscoring how intersecting prejudices barred broader minority participation despite qualified applicants.[20][24] These exclusions reflected causal realities of the era's institutional racism, limiting the program's diversity and fueling later critiques of its homogeneity as a microcosm of selective wartime inclusion.[28]

Death and Enduring Impact

Elizabeth L. Gardner died on December 22, 2011, at the age of 90.[1][6] Gardner's legacy endures through her role in the Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) program, where she flew demanding missions including test piloting Martin B-26 Marauder bombers, contributing to the ferrying of aircraft and support operations that logged over 60 million miles collectively by WASPs and freed thousands of male pilots for combat duties.[29][30] Her service exemplified the technical competence of female aviators during World War II, helping to dismantle barriers to women's participation in military aviation; this paved the way for later integrations, such as the 1970s admission of women to U.S. Air Force pilot training.[11] Gardner's iconic 1944 photograph at the controls of a B-26, captured by U.S. Army Signal Corps photographers, has become a symbol of WASP resilience and skill, frequently reproduced in historical exhibits and media to highlight the program's overlooked contributions.[2] Posthumously, Gardner's story reinforces the WASP narrative of empirical success amid initial postwar denial of veteran benefits—status granted only in 1977 and honored with a Congressional Gold Medal in 2009—which underscores causal links between their wartime performance and subsequent policy shifts toward gender equity in aviation, unmarred by unsubstantiated narratives of inadequacy.[31] Her example continues to inspire aviation education and women's STEM pursuits, with references in Commemorative Air Force profiles emphasizing her as a trailblazer whose firsthand accounts validated the program's efficacy.[1]
