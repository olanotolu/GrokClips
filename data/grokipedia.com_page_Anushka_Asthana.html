Anushka Asthana

Anushka Asthana (born March 1980) is a British journalist and broadcaster who serves as the US editor for Channel 4 News.[1][2] She previously held the position of deputy political editor at ITV News, where she co-presented the program Peston, and was joint political editor at The Guardian, becoming one of the first women in that role at the newspaper.[3][4] Asthana has covered key political developments including the Brexit referendum, the leadership of Jeremy Corbyn, and the rise of Keir Starmer, for which she authored the 2025 book Taken As Red: The Truth About Starmer's Labour.[5] Her career also includes launching and hosting The Guardian's daily podcast Today in Focus, which won awards, and she has received recognition such as Journalist of the Year at the Asian Media Awards.[2][6] While her reporting has focused on UK and US politics, it has occasionally drawn criticism for alignment with The Guardian's editorial perspective, though no major personal controversies have defined her professional trajectory.[7]

Early life and education

Family background and early interests

Anushka Asthana was born in Scunthorpe, Lincolnshire, to parents who immigrated to the United Kingdom from New Delhi, India, in the 1970s.[8][9] Both parents worked as doctors, providing a professional household background that emphasized education and stability amid their transition to British life.[10] Raised in Stalybridge, Greater Manchester, Asthana grew up as a second-generation South Asian in a working-class town with a diverse ethnic makeup, where she observed firsthand the varying experiences of minority communities in northwest England.[11] Her childhood included frequent summer visits to relatives in Delhi and Bhopal, India, which reinforced ties to her Indian heritage and exposed her to contrasting cultural environments from an early age.[8] Asthana attended Manchester High School for Girls, an independent day school, commuting daily via the 691 bus from Stalybridge.[12] At the school, she pursued A-levels in sciences and economics, reflecting formative academic inclinations toward analytical and quantitative disciplines that later influenced her career trajectory.[12]

Academic career

Asthana obtained a BSc in Economics from the University of Cambridge.[4][12] Following her undergraduate studies, she pursued postgraduate training in Periodical Journalism at City University London around 2003.[4][13][14] In 2006, Asthana received the Laurence Stern Fellowship, arranged through City University, enabling her to spend several months at The Washington Post for hands-on journalistic immersion and skill development.[4][7][2]

Journalistic career

Initial roles at The Observer and The Times

Asthana joined The Observer in 2003 as a general reporter following completion of the newspaper's trainee scheme and her graduation from City University London.[15][4] In this entry-level role, she covered a range of topics, including lifestyle, economics, and early political stories, contributing bylines such as pieces on personal finance amid economic shifts and international features tied to her Indian heritage, like reporting on Delhi's cultural landscape.[1][8] Over her eight-year tenure at The Observer, Asthana advanced through mid-level positions, serving as education correspondent, policy editor, and deputy news editor, which involved overseeing news desk operations and specializing in policy-related investigations and analysis.[7][4] A notable assignment during this period was her 2006 Laurence Stern fellowship at The Washington Post, where she spent four months gaining experience in U.S. journalism, enhancing her reporting on transatlantic policy and political dynamics.[8][10] In 2011, amid the U.K. media industry's adaptation to digital competition and print circulation pressures, Asthana transitioned to The Times as chief political correspondent, marking a shift toward more focused political journalism with coverage of events like Labour Party conferences and Cabinet controversies.[16][17][18] This role built on her Observer experience by emphasizing in-depth Westminster reporting, including analyses of government reshuffles and party strategies.[18]

Editorship and political reporting at The Guardian

In December 2015, Anushka Asthana was appointed joint political editor of The Guardian, sharing the role with Heather Stewart in a job-share arrangement that succeeded Patrick Wintour, who transitioned to diplomatic editor.[19][20] This position placed her at the helm of the newspaper's political reporting team, overseeing coverage of major UK developments including the 2016 Brexit referendum, its implementation, and the subsequent 2017 and 2019 general elections.[7] As political editor, Asthana coordinated editorial responses to the rise of Jeremy Corbyn's leadership within the Labour Party from 2015 onward, emphasizing in-depth analysis of internal party dynamics and policy shifts amid accusations of antisemitism and electoral challenges.[7] Asthana's editorial oversight extended to shaping The Guardian's narrative on Brexit negotiations post-2016, where the outlet maintained a consistently pro-Remain stance, critiquing Conservative government handling under Theresa May and Boris Johnson through investigative pieces and opinion integration.[7] During the Corbyn era, she directed reporting on Labour's internal divisions, including leadership contests and factional battles, which influenced the paper's portrayal of the party's electability issues leading into the 2019 election defeat.[7] By 2020, as Corbyn's tenure waned and Keir Starmer ascended to Labour leadership following the December 2019 contest, Asthana contributed to coverage of Starmer's early consolidation of power, focusing on his centrist repositioning and efforts to marginalize left-wing elements, though her direct political editing role had shifted by late 2019 to editor-at-large while retaining influence over political content.[21] In September 2018, Asthana launched and hosted The Guardian's flagship daily podcast Today in Focus, which integrated political reporting with audio storytelling to amplify reach beyond print, featuring episodes on Brexit fallout, Corbyn's policies, and emerging Labour transitions under Starmer.[22] The podcast aligned with The Guardian's investigative ethos by incorporating insider interviews and data-driven critiques of political figures, such as examinations of Corbyn's electoral vulnerabilities, though listener metrics highlighted its role in broadening the outlet's audience amid declining print circulation.[22] Asthana also penned opinion pieces under her editorial purview that interrogated political leadership, including skeptical assessments of Corbyn's strategic missteps, reflecting The Guardian's broader left-leaning editorial line while prioritizing access-driven journalism over adversarial confrontation.[5] Her tenure concluded with a move to ITV News in March 2021, after which Heather Stewart assumed sole political editor duties.[23]

Deputy political editorship at ITV News

Anushka Asthana assumed the role of Deputy Political Editor at ITV News in July 2021, following her announcement in March of that year.[23][2] In this capacity, she supported Political Editor Robert Peston on the weekly ITV program Peston, delivering broadcast analysis of Westminster developments through panel discussions and interviews with key figures.[24][3] Asthana's contributions emphasized television-specific elements, such as real-time visual breakdowns of policy impacts and on-air collaborations that integrated print-sourced insights with live studio dynamics.[25] Her work on Peston included dissecting government strategies, including welfare reforms, through exchanges with ministers and civil service-adjacent sources, highlighting tensions in policy implementation.[26][27] A focal point of her tenure was ITV's coverage of the July 2024 UK general election, where she provided on-the-ground reporting and studio analysis during overnight specials, contributing to narratives on Labour's path to victory under Keir Starmer.[28] This included coordination with Peston for post-election breakdowns of seat gains and campaign internals, drawing on her access to Labour figures. Her election segments underscored broadcast demands for concise, viewer-engaged explanations of electoral mechanics and outcomes.[29] Asthana's four-year stint concluded in spring 2025, marked by a farewell from the ITV team amid her transition to a new role.[2][30] During this period, she enhanced ITV's political output by bridging detailed reporting with accessible television formats, often in tandem with Peston's investigative style.

Transition to US Editor at Channel 4 News

In April 2025, Channel 4 News announced the appointment of Anushka Asthana as its US Editor, marking her transition from deputy political editor at ITV News to a role focused on transatlantic coverage of American politics.[16] [24] The position, commencing in August 2025, positioned her to report on the unfolding US presidential election cycle and the incoming Trump administration, emphasizing on-the-ground analysis from Washington, D.C.[31] [32] Asthana relocated to Washington, D.C., in mid-August 2025, where she began settling her family while initiating coverage amid heightened geopolitical scrutiny, including meetings between President-elect Trump, Ukrainian President Zelenskyy, and European leaders.[33] [34] Her early reporting included direct engagements on Capitol Hill, such as questioning NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte on potential shifts in US policy toward Russia under Trump.[35] This work highlighted her adaptation to the US beat, involving rapid domestic travelâ€”exemplified by trips to locations like Orem, Utahâ€”and new responsibilities in editorial oversight of Trump-era developments, including NATO relations and election aftermath dynamics.[36][37] The move underscored a pivot from UK-centric political journalism to international reporting, with Asthana's initial outputs addressing transatlantic implications of US decisions amid 2025 tensions, such as Russia's reported drone activities near Poland and strains in Middle East policy.[38][39] Her role facilitated Channel 4 News' expanded focus on real-time US events, leveraging her prior Westminster experience for comparative analysis without overlapping domestic UK narratives.[40]

Broadcasting and media contributions

Presenting roles and podcasts

Asthana served as the launch presenter of The Guardian's daily news podcast Today in Focus, which debuted on November 24, 2018, aiming to deliver the day's top stories in approximately 20 minutes through a blend of analysis and personal storytelling.[41] The format emphasized originality in news coverage, drawing on Guardian journalism to engage listeners with concise, insightful breakdowns of global events.[41] At ITV News, as deputy political editor, Asthana co-presented the weekly Talking Politics podcast and video segments alongside Robert Peston and Tom Bradby, focusing on dissecting major UK political issues such as immigration policy and Labour's economic strategies.[42] Episodes, including a January 29, 2025, discussion on immigration's challenges for successive governments and a May 15, 2025, analysis of Keir Starmer's migration rhetoric as a potential liability for Labour, highlighted the program's format of direct debate and viewer questions to foster audience interaction.[43][44][45] In September 2025, following her move to Channel 4 News as US editor, Asthana co-launched the video podcast TrumpWorld with Matt Frei on September 10, 2025, designed as a weekly series examining the implications of Donald Trump's second presidency and 'America First' policies.[37] The podcast features guest experts to unpack White House developments, as in an October 2025 episode with historian Jill Lepore on constitutional themes in We the People and another assessing Trump's potential role in Middle East peace efforts.[46][47] This format innovates by combining video elements with on-location reporting from Washington, D.C., to provide unfiltered insights into US political shifts.[48]

Coverage of major political events

Asthana's reporting on the Brexit process, while serving as political editor at The Guardian from 2015 to 2021, emphasized the internal Conservative Party divisions and procedural chaos following the 2016 referendum. She documented key developments such as Theresa May's invocation of Article 50 on March 29, 2017, which triggered formal negotiations amid warnings of economic consequences for the UK, and the subsequent parliamentary gridlock that prolonged uncertainty.[49][50] Her coverage highlighted causal factors like leadership miscalculations and cross-party alliances, contributing to public understanding of how these dynamics delayed withdrawal until January 31, 2020, and shaped subsequent trade agreements.[49] In the 2019 UK general election, Asthana focused on Labour's internal dynamics under Jeremy Corbyn, including her on-the-ground reporting from within Momentum, the grassroots organization mobilizing voters for a left-wing agenda. Her analysis underscored how factional tensions and policy ambiguities on Brexit eroded Labour's credibility, enabling Boris Johnson's Conservatives to secure a 80-seat majority on December 12, 2019, by framing the vote as a mandate to "get Brexit done."[51] This outcome stemmed partly from voter fatigue with prolonged parliamentary debates, as her dispatches illustrated through interviews with activists and polling data showing a 14-point swing to the Conservatives in traditional Labour heartlands.[51] Asthana's work during the July 4, 2024, UK general election, as ITV News deputy political editor, captured the scale of Labour's 412-seat landslide under Keir Starmer, attributing it to Conservative scandals and voter disillusionment rather than overwhelming enthusiasm for Labour policies. She reported from Starmer's campaign trail, noting tactical discipline that neutralized internal left-wing challenges from figures like Corbyn supporters, allowing Labour to gain 211 seats while the Conservatives lost 251.[52] Her coverage of Starmer's pre-election maneuvering revealed how purges of antisemitism and rule changes consolidated power, mitigating factional battles that had plagued the party since 2015 and enabling a fragile coalition win with just 33.7% of the vote.[53][54] Transitioning to US editor at Channel 4 News in August 2025, Asthana covered early Trump administration initiatives, including an unprecedented August 19, 2025, meeting aimed at Ukraine peace talks amid stalled European efforts. Her reporting examined Trump's push for rapid negotiations with Russia, questioning whether it reflected genuine diplomatic leverage or concessions that could undermine NATO unity, as evidenced by Zelensky's reluctance and Putin's historical intransigence.[55] She also analyzed Trump's October 1, 2025, Gaza plan unveiling at the White House, highlighting declining US public support for Israel and potential shifts in aid policy influencing Middle East stability.[56] These pieces underscored causal risks, such as accelerated deal-making prioritizing US interests over allies' security concerns, amid broader 2025 Republican gains in Congress.[40]

Authorship and publications

Key books and writings

Anushka Asthana's most prominent authored book is Taken As Red: How Labour Won Big and the Tories Crashed the Party, published on 26 September 2024 by HarperCollins.[57] The work provides a detailed, interview-based account of the 2024 UK general election, emphasizing Labour's strategic pivot under Keir Starmer to prioritize electability over ideological purity, including a deliberate distancing from Jeremy Corbyn's tenure through membership purges and policy recalibrations that addressed voter concerns on economic competence and national security.[58] Drawing on exclusive access to over 100 insidersâ€”such as Rachel Reeves, Wes Streeting, Kwasi Kwarteng, and Nigel Farageâ€”Asthana reconstructs causal sequences of events, highlighting Labour's data-driven targeting of marginal seats and the Conservatives' self-inflicted wounds from internal divisions and policy missteps.[59] The book's analysis underscores themes of pragmatic ruthlessness in political leadership, evidenced by timelines of Labour's post-2019 introspection, where Starmer's team enforced discipline to neutralize left-wing dissent, enabling a landslide victory on 4 July 2024 with 412 seats against the Tories' 121.[60] Asthana attributes Labour's success to empirical adjustments, like abandoning expansive spending pledges in favor of fiscal restraint signals, though she notes risks of over-centralization alienating grassroots activists. A 2025 paperback edition extends coverage to Starmer's initial governance challenges, including budget constraints and coalition-building amid economic stagnation.[61] Reception has been generally positive for its sourcing depth and timeliness, with The Guardian praising the "richly sourced account of Tory dysfunction and Labour's transformation into an election-winning machine," though critics observe an underlying sympathy for Starmer's centrism reflective of Asthana's journalistic background at left-leaning outlets.[57] It earned a spot as a New Statesman book of the year, and reader aggregates show a 3.9/5 rating from over 170 reviews, commending its balance amid partisan divides.[58] [62] No other full-length books by Asthana have been published, though her pre-2024 Guardian contributions included analytical pieces on party internals, such as Brexit-era factionalism and welfare policy debates, which informed her broader insights into Labour's evolution without constituting standalone scholarly works.[49]

Personal life

Family and relationships

Anushka Asthana married Toby, a white British man raised in Oxfordshire, in 2009 at the age of 29.[63][64] The wedding incorporated Hindu traditions, including elements that highlighted cultural contrasts for her husband.[63] Asthana has publicly described the union as a cross-cultural adventure that fosters mutual enrichment across families, emphasizing its role in promoting color-blind societal dynamics.[63][65] The couple has one child, a son named Ethan born in 2012.[66] Asthana was born to Indian immigrant parents, Jagdish and Aruna Asthana.[9] She had a younger brother, Anshu Asthana, who died in 2012 at age 37 from lung disease amid struggles with bipolar disorder; Asthana has spoken about the family's experience with his condition in interviews.[67][68]

Reception and criticisms

Professional achievements and recognition

Asthana was awarded the Laurence Stern Fellowship in 2006, enabling her to spend several months working on the national desk at The Washington Post, where she contributed to reporting and traveled on Air Force One with then-President George W. Bush.[4][7] In 2021, she received a silver ARIAS award in the best speech presenter category for her hosting of The Guardian's Today in Focus podcast, recognizing her delivery and engagement in audio journalism.[69] Asthana was named Journalist of the Year at the 2024 Asian Media Awards, presented by Manchester Metropolitan University, in acknowledgment of her contributions as ITV News Deputy Political Editor.[70] In January 2025, she joined the board of the Society of Editors, a professional body advocating for editorial standards and press freedom in the UK, alongside other senior news leaders.[71] She has also served as chair of the Parliamentary Press Gallery, facilitating access and coordination for journalists covering UK parliamentary proceedings.[72]

Criticisms of bias and editorial influence

Asthana's work at The Guardian, where she served as joint political editor from 2015 to 2021, drew criticism from conservative commentators for contributing to the outlet's perceived left-leaning slant, characterized by selective emphasis on Conservative policy shortcomings and relative leniency toward Labour figures. Media watchdogs such as AllSides have rated The Guardian as Left-biased due to story choices favoring progressive narratives, while Media Bias/Fact Check notes its left-leaning editorial position and occasional use of questionable sources.[73][74] During this period, right-leaning outlets and analysts argued that Guardian coverage, including Asthana's reporting on Brexit and party leadership contests, amplified Tory infightingâ€”such as Boris Johnson's internal party battlesâ€”while framing Labour's ideological shifts under Corbyn and Starmer as pragmatic evolutions rather than ideological overhauls.[75] At ITV News, as deputy political editor from 2021 to 2025, Asthana's contributions to programs like Peston faced viewer complaints of bias, with audiences on platforms like social media accusing the show of dull, predictable left-tilting analysis that scrutinized Conservatives more harshly than Labour equivalents.[76] A Loughborough University analysis of 2024 general election TV coverage found Labour receiving significantly more airtime across broadcasters including ITV, which critics from alternative media attributed to editorial preferences favoring Starmer's campaign narrative over equivalent Conservative scrutiny, potentially amplified by Asthana's insider access to Labour sources.[77] Her 2024 book Taken As Red: How Labour Won Big and the Tories Crashed the Party has been faulted by skeptics for exemplifying this pattern, portraying Starmer's strategic discipline and Labour's organizational reforms as decisive electoral virtues while attributing Conservative defeat primarily to self-inflicted dysfunction, with limited interrogation of Labour's fiscal conservatism or policy continuities from prior governments.[57] Even some left-leaning reviewers, such as those on Labour Hub, described the account as sycophantic, lacking footnotes for key claims and uncritically echoing Starmer's framing of issues like antisemitism handling without probing deeper causal factors or alternative strategies that might have alienated voters.[78] Conservative perspectives, informed by broader distrust of Guardian alumni in broadcast roles, view such works as reinforcing a media ecosystem where Labour's 2024 landslideâ€”yielding 412 seats amid low turnout of 59.9%â€”is narrated as merit-based triumph rather than voter fatigue with 14 years of Tory rule.[79] Interactions underscoring partisan editorial divides include Asthana's 2017 public rebuke of Piers Morgan on the Andrew Marr Show regarding the Women's March, where she dismissed his feminist credentials as "bollocks" via Twitter, a stance critics associate with progressive media's prioritization of identity-based narratives over electoral pragmatism.[80][81] Right-leaning counterarguments highlight demographic skews in Guardian and ITV audiencesâ€”predominantly urban, higher-educated, and left-leaningâ€”as causal drivers of content that aligns with these viewers, potentially influencing policy discourse toward Labour-favorable frames like public sector pay disputes over fiscal restraint.[82]
