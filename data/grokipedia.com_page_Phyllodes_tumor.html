Phyllodes tumor

Phyllodes tumors (from Greek: phyllon, meaning "leaf") are a rare type of biphasic fibroepithelial mass characterized by proliferation of stromal and epithelial components, with the stromal component being neoplastic and arising from periductal stromal cells of the breast.[1][2] They account for less than 1% of all breast neoplasms.[3] They were previously termed cystosarcoma phyllodes, coined by Johannes Müller in 1838, before being renamed to phyllodes tumor by the World Health Organization in 2003.[4] Phyllon, which means "leaf" in Greek, describes the unique papillary projections characteristic of phyllodes tumors on histology.[1] Phyllodes tumors are classified as benign, borderline, or malignant based on histologic features.[2] Diagnosis is made via core-needle biopsy.[5] Treatment is typically surgical resection with wide margins (>1 cm), due to their propensity to recur.[2]

Phyllodes Tumor

Signs and symptoms

Phyllodes tumors typically present as a firm, mobile, and palpable mass that is painless in nature.[2] On physical examination, the mass can demonstrate a smooth or nodular texture depending on its size.[4] In addition, larger masses can stretch the overlying breast tissue leading to nipple retraction, chest wall fixation, and in advanced cases, ulceration from pressure necrosis.[6] Rarely, bloody nipple discharge may occur due to infarction within the tumor. Larger tumors may also cause distension of superficial veins.[2] Phyllodes tumors can grow to a variety of sizes, ranging from 0.8 to 40 cm, with the average tumor growing to between 4 and 8 cm.[7] Their growth can be slow, rapid, or demonstrate alternating growth patterns.[2] A notable feature, however, is that their growth rarely regresses, a distinguishing feature from fibroadenomas which tend to change in size based on a female's menstrual cycle.[8]

Phyllodes tumors

Causes

The exact etiology of phyllodes tumors is unknown.[2] They may represent a progression from preexisting fibroadenomas.[2] Possible growth stimuli include trauma, pregnancy, increased estrogen activity, and lactation.[2] Genetically, phyllodes tumors occur at higher incidence in patients with Li-Fraumeni syndrome.[2] Rare cases in males are often associated with gynecomastia.[2]

Phyllodes Tumor Mechanism

Mechanism

The pathogenesis behind the development of a phyllodes tumor is not well-defined.[9] Some theories suggest a genetic cause while other literature supports the involvement of hormone and growth factor receptors, cell signal transduction pathways, and cell cycle markers.[9][10] Certain receptors include estrogen/progesterone, glucocorticoid, and HER2.[9] The most well-studied cell signal transduction pathway includes the Wnt pathway, which is a highly conserved genetic pathway between species.[9] In the Wnt pathway, stabilized beta-catenin translocates to the nucleus and promotes transcription of target genes such as c-myc, c-jun, Fra-1, and cyclin D1, which contribute to cell proliferation. Dysregulation of the Wnt pathway, often through mutations in components like APC or beta-catenin, results in uncontrolled growth characteristic of phyllodes tumors.[9] In addition, vascular factors promoting angiogenesis have been shown to further promote growth of these breast masses.[9] These proteins include vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), hypoxia-inducible factor-1α (HIF-1α), platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), endothelin-1, and nitric oxide synthase.[9] The increased expression of these angiogenic factors reflects the increased angiogenesis seen in higher grade phyllodes tumors.[9] Genes that have been associated with a higher incidence of phyllodes tumors include Mediator Complex Subunit 12 (MED12) exon 2 (~70-80% of cases), Telomerase Reverse Transcriptase (TERT) promoter (~50%), and Retinoic Acid Receptor Alpha (RARA) (~20-30%); these genes regulate transcription, telomere maintenance, and differentiation, respectively, and support a clonal stromal origin.[10] Malignant phyllodes tumors often harbor additional alterations, such as EGFR amplification and PIK3CA mutations.[11][12] Recent studies indicate that phyllodes tumor development arises from dysregulated epithelial-stromal interactions, driven by genetic alterations and pathway activations (including Wnt and angiogenesis) that promote stromal proliferation.[9]

Diagnosis

Diagnostic techniques

Radiographical imaging is the first-line test for identifying a phyllodes tumor.[13] Despite their propensity to grow rapidly and deform the overlying breast tissue, approximately 20% of phyllodes tumors can present as a nonpalpable mass on screening mammography.[14] Other imaging tools used to assess the size and spread of a phyllodes tumor include ultrasound and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).[13] None of these imaging tools are definitive tests for differentiating a phyllodes tumor from a benign fibroadenoma.[15] Phyllodes tumors can only be diagnosed histologically, as they tend to have many overlapping features with other breast masses on physical examination and radiological imaging.[13] A core needle biopsy is the primary tool used to provide a definitive diagnosis for phyllodes tumors.[13] Other biopsy techniques include fine-needle aspiration and excisional biopsy; however, fine-needle aspiration is less reliable due to cytologic overlap with fibroadenoma.[5] Following biopsy, histological and gross examination is performed which helps clinicians better grade and classify the phyllodes tumor.[13]

Classification

Phyllodes tumors are part of a group of breast diseases called cellular fibroepithelial lesions.[8] This term incorporates a spectrum of diseases ranging from a benign fibroadenoma to a malignant phyllodes tumor, with numerous variants in between.[16] They are classified by ICD-O, not by MeSH.[16] Phyllodes tumors may be considered benign, borderline, or malignant depending upon their histological features, including stromal cellularity, infiltration of the epithelial-stromal interface, and mitotic activity.[17] Malignant phyllodes tumors have a propensity to metastasize and grow quickly, and are treated with consideration of their sarcomatous potential.[17] A large case series from the MD Anderson Cancer Center reported the incidence of each type of phyllodes tumor as benign (58%), borderline (12%), and malignant (30%).[18] Malignant phyllodes tumors can behave similarly to sarcomas leading to development of blood-borne metastases.[18] Distant metastases are rare, occurring in less than 5% of cases overall but in approximately 15-20% of malignant cases.[17] The most common site for distant metastases is the lung, followed by bone and abdominal viscera.[2] In more insidious cases, the parotid region has also been described in literature.[19]

Prevention and screening

There are no evidence-based recommendations for specific screening or preventive measures for the early detection of phyllodes tumors, due to their rarity and lack of known modifiable risk factors.[2] In patients with a prior history of a phyllodes tumor, follow-up recommendations are determined on a case-by-case basis depending on the grade and stage of the treated tumor, typically involving clinical breast examinations and imaging (e.g., mammography or ultrasound) every 6–12 months for the first 2–5 years, then annually thereafter, tailored to recurrence risk.[21] Patients who have not had a mastectomy should continue regular breast cancer screening based on the recommendations by the United States Preventive Services Task Force, which as of 2024 advises biennial screening mammography for women aged 40 to 74 at average risk.[22]

Treatment

Treatment for phyllodes tumors varies by histologic grade (benign, borderline, or malignant). The primary treatment is surgical excision aiming for negative margins.[17] For benign tumors, simple excision with negative margins is often sufficient, without requiring wide margins.[17] For borderline and malignant tumors, wide local excision with margins of at least 1 cm is traditionally recommended to minimize local recurrence.[17] Mastectomy may be considered for large or multifocal tumors.[17] Chemotherapy has a limited role and is typically reserved for metastatic disease, with no proven benefit in adjuvant settings.[17] Adjuvant radiation therapy after breast-conserving surgery with negative margins may reduce local recurrence rates in borderline and malignant tumors, though it does not improve survival.[17][23] The risk of local recurrence or metastases relates to the tumor's histologic grade and mitotic activity.[17] Despite appropriate surgery, incomplete margins occur in a substantial proportion of cases (approximately 28%), often necessitating re-excision.[24]

Prognosis

The prognosis of phyllodes tumor is good with an overall 87% 10-year survival rate.[2] Local recurrence rates vary by grade: 10–17% for benign, 14–25% for borderline, and 23–30% for malignant.[2] After wide-margin surgical excision, 98.7% of benign phyllodes tumors and 80% of borderline were cured.[25] In rare cases where the tumor has metastasized (less than 5% overall), the prognosis is poor.[2] This most commonly occurs in cases of malignant grade phyllodes tumor.[2]

Epidemiology

Phyllodes tumors account for less than 1% of all breast neoplasms.[3] They primarily occur in adult women, with very few examples reported in adolescents. The annual incidence is approximately 2.5 cases per million women, with higher rates observed among Hispanic and Asian women.[2][26] Incidence is most common between the ages of 40 and 50, prior to the onset of menopause.[2] Phyllodes tumors typically present about 15 years later than fibroadenoma, a common differential diagnosis.[2] Younger women have a higher chance of having a benign phyllodes tumor, while older women are more likely to have higher-grade tumors.[24]
