2023 Djerba synagogue shooting

The 2023 Djerba synagogue shooting was a terrorist attack that took place on May 9, 2023, near the El Ghriba Synagogue—one of Africa's oldest synagogues—on the Tunisian island of Djerba, during the annual Jewish pilgrimage for Lag BaOmer.[1][2] A Tunisian National Guard member, who first shot and killed a fellow officer to obtain additional ammunition, proceeded to open fire on a group of pilgrims and security personnel returning from the site, killing five individuals in total: two Jewish pilgrims (one dual Tunisian-Israeli citizen and one French national) and three Tunisian security officers, while wounding at least ten others before being killed by responding forces.[3][4] The Islamic State (ISIS) claimed responsibility for the attack, citing it as retaliation against Jews amid broader jihadist calls to target such gatherings, though Tunisian authorities initially described the perpetrator's actions as those of a lone individual motivated by personal grievances rather than organized terrorism.[5][6] This incident marked the deadliest attack on Jews in Tunisia since the 2002 synagogue bombing and highlighted ongoing vulnerabilities for the country's ancient Jewish community, numbering around 1,000, amid persistent Islamist extremism in the region despite post-Arab Spring security measures.[1][2]

Background

Jewish Community in Djerba and Tunisia

The Jewish community of Djerba maintains one of the oldest continuous presences outside the Land of Israel, with origins tracing to the 6th century BCE following the Babylonian exile or possibly earlier migrations from Judea around 3,000 years ago during Phoenician times.[7][8] This endurance spans successive invasions, including Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, Arab, Ottoman, and French colonial rule, preserving unique traditions such as distinctive prayer rites and a system of rabbinical yeshivas.[9][10] In 1948, Tunisia hosted approximately 105,000 Jews, comprising a vibrant minority engaged in commerce, crafts, and agriculture, with Djerba's community forming a core enclave in villages like Hara Sghira and Hara Kebira.[11] Post-independence in 1956, emigration surged due to Arab nationalism, economic nationalization displacing Jewish merchants, and escalating tensions from Arab-Israeli conflicts, reducing the national population to around 60,000 by 1960 and further to 20,000 by 1970.[12][1] Djerba's Jews, numbering in the tens of thousands in the mid-20th century, faced additional pressures from sporadic pogroms, the 1967 Six-Day War riots, and post-Arab Spring jihadist threats, prompting ongoing departures for Israel, France, and Canada amid economic stagnation and antisemitic incidents.[13][14] By 2023, Tunisia's Jewish population had contracted to under 1,000 on the mainland and about 1,000-1,500 in Djerba, highlighting resilience in a region marked by historical hostility yet underscoring persistent vulnerability to violence.[15][16]

Security and Jihadist Threats in Tunisia

Following the 2011 Jasmine Revolution, Tunisia experienced a marked increase in jihadist activity, as the overthrow of authoritarian controls enabled the proliferation of Salafist-jihadist networks that had been suppressed under the Ben Ali regime. This vulnerability stemmed from ideological mobilization rather than solely economic grievances, with groups exploiting newfound freedoms to recruit via mosques and online propaganda, leading to a surge in attacks targeting state institutions and foreigners. Notable incidents included the March 18, 2015, assault on the Bardo National Museum in Tunis by two gunmen affiliated with the Islamic State (ISIS), which killed 22 people, mostly European tourists, and the June 26, 2015, beachfront massacre in Sousse by a lone ISIS-inspired attacker, resulting in 38 deaths, predominantly British holidaymakers.[17][18] These operations, coordinated from Libya-based training camps, aimed to destabilize Tunisia's economy by deterring tourism, a key revenue source, and demonstrated the jihadists' ability to exploit internal radicalization pipelines.[19] Tunisia emerged as the leading source of foreign fighters for ISIS, with estimates indicating that between 5,000 and 6,000 citizens had joined the group in Syria, Iraq, and Libya by 2017, representing the highest per capita contribution globally.[20] Returnees, battle-hardened and ideologically committed, reintegrated into Tunisian society and formed sleeper cells, exacerbating domestic threats despite government amnesties and deradicalization efforts that often proved ineffective against core jihadist convictions. Porous borders, particularly the southern frontier with Libya, facilitated arms smuggling, fighter transit, and cross-border operations by groups like Jund al-Khilafah and Okba Ibn Nafaa, affiliated with al-Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM), allowing extremists to evade detection amid limited surveillance resources.[21] Although Tunisian security forces conducted extensive counterterrorism operations, arresting thousands and dismantling networks, these measures were hampered by judicial leniency, corruption, and the persistence of radical ideologies in underserved regions, sustaining a cycle of low-level violence.[22] The El Ghriba synagogue in Djerba, site of an annual Jewish pilgrimage drawing thousands, was repeatedly identified as a high-value soft target for jihadists seeking symbolic attacks on non-Muslims, building on precedents like the 2002 al-Qaeda truck bombing there that killed 20. Pre-2023 intelligence assessments highlighted elevated risks during the Lag BaOmer gathering, with foreign governments issuing advisories on potential terrorist plots against Jewish sites amid Tunisia's residual jihadist presence.[23] Lax internal security, including inconsistent perimeter checks and reliance on local policing, compounded these vulnerabilities, as jihadist cells exploited pilgrimage crowds for low-tech assaults despite sporadic military deployments.[24] This environment reflected broader failures in sealing ideological and operational gaps post-Arab Spring, where crackdowns suppressed overt violence but did little to eradicate the transnational jihadist ecosystem threatening minority communities.[25]

The El Ghriba Synagogue and Annual Pilgrimage

The El Ghriba Synagogue, situated on Tunisia's Djerba Island, is among Africa's oldest continuously active synagogues, with traditions tracing its origins to the 6th century BCE following the Babylonian destruction of Solomon's Temple in 586 BCE.[26] Local legends assert that the structure incorporates a stone or door from the ancient Temple, imbuing it with profound mystical significance in Jewish lore, akin to narratives in regional Muslim and Amazigh traditions.[27] While the present building underwent extensive 19th-century renovations, archaeological evidence and oral histories indicate Jewish worship on the site for over 1,900 years, making it a cornerstone of Djerba's Jewish community in a Muslim-majority context.[7] The synagogue anchors the annual Lag BaOmer pilgrimage, observed on the 33rd day of the Omer count between Passover and Shavuot, drawing thousands of devotees for prayers, candle lighting, and rituals honoring Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai.[28] Participants, predominantly from Israel, France, Italy, and the United States, engage in auctions of sacred items and communal feasts, fostering interfaith tolerance amid Tunisia's predominantly Sunni Muslim population.[29] Past events have attracted up to 8,000 pilgrims, bolstering Djerba's tourism economy.[30] In 2023, the pilgrimage spanned May 4 to May 9, coinciding with renewed tourism promotion after the COVID-19 downturn, and hosted over 5,000 mostly overseas visitors for the Lag BaOmer observances.[31][32] This visibility as a vibrant Jewish site in North Africa has rendered El Ghriba a symbolic target for jihadist actors, exemplified by the April 2002 al-Qaeda suicide truck bombing at its entrance, which detonated a fuel-laden vehicle and killed 21 individuals—primarily German tourists—while injuring dozens more.[23] The attack highlighted persistent security challenges for such enclaves, despite Tunisian government protections.[33]

Perpetrator

Profile of Wissam Khazri

Wissam Khazri was a 30-year-old Tunisian citizen employed as a member of the Tunisian National Guard, affiliated with a naval security center on the island of Djerba.[34][35] In this role, he had routine access to firearms as part of his duties securing coastal and island facilities.[36] Prior to the incident, Khazri had no documented criminal convictions or arrests, according to statements from Tunisian security officials.[37] Tunisian Interior Minister Lotfi Ben Romdhane described Khazri's actions as premeditated, noting that he first shot and killed a fellow National Guard officer to obtain an additional weapon before proceeding to the target site.[35][36] This sequence indicated deliberate planning and exploitation of his position for access to arms, rather than impulsive behavior.[37] Investigations by Tunisian authorities found no evidence of external coordination in the immediate lead-up, pointing to Khazri acting on personal intent.[38]

Radicalization and Preparations

Wissam Khazri, a 30-year-old Tunisian National Guardsman stationed at a naval center in Aghir on Djerba, exhibited no prior documented ties to organized terrorist groups, consistent with patterns of self-radicalized lone actors in Tunisia's post-2011 environment where exposure to Salafi-jihadist ideology proliferated via online channels following relaxed censorship after the revolution.[39][40] This ideological ecosystem, fueled by global calls for violence against perceived enemies of Islam including Jews, provided a causal pathway from personal grievance or doctrinal absorption to targeted action, though specific triggers in Khazri's case remain unelaborated in official probes.[35] Khazri's preparations centered on exploiting his insider role within the security apparatus to acquire means and access for the assault during the annual Lag BaOmer pilgrimage at El Ghriba, a period of heightened Jewish presence that he deliberately selected for maximum impact.[37] On May 9, 2023, he fatally shot a colleague at the Aghir base to seize a Kalashnikov rifle, then used a National Guard motorcycle to travel approximately 200 meters to a schoolyard overlooking the synagogue, positioning himself to observe police patrols before initiating the attack.[35][37] This sequence underscores premeditation without evident external coordination, aligning with lone-actor models where operational simplicity leverages routine duties to bypass standard security protocols.[35]

The Attack

Sequence of Events

On the evening of May 9, 2023, Wissam Khazri, a Tunisian National Guardsman, killed a colleague at his naval base station and seized ammunition before departing for the El Ghriba Synagogue in Djerba.[41][37] He arrived near the synagogue entrance via motorcycle and, at approximately 8:13 p.m., opened fire on a group of pilgrims gathered for the annual Lag BaOmer pilgrimage.[37][41] Khazri's initial shots killed two Jewish visitors immediately: Aviel Haddad, a 30-year-old Tunisian-Israeli dual national, and his cousin Benjamin Haddad, a 42-year-old French citizen.[42][37] He then pursued fleeing individuals and engaged responding security forces, killing three Tunisian security personnel in the ensuing exchange.[42][43] Tunisian security forces quickly arrived at the scene, surrounding Khazri and neutralizing him by gunfire within roughly two minutes of their intervention, ending the attack.[37] One of the wounded security officers died from injuries the following day, resulting in five total fatalities among the victims.[43][42]

Weapons and Tactics Employed

The attacker, Wissam Khazri, a Tunisian national guardsman stationed at a naval facility on Djerba, initiated the assault by using his service-issued firearm to kill a fellow guard and seize additional ammunition from him.[44][43] This enabled him to proceed directly to the El Ghriba synagogue vicinity without needing external procurement of weaponry, demonstrating the low logistical barriers posed by insider access to military-grade arms. No explosives or improvised devices were employed, distinguishing the operation from the 2002 al-Qaeda truck bombing at the same site, which killed 21 and relied on a vehicle-borne improvised explosive device.[42][37] Khazri exploited the annual Lag ba-Omer pilgrimage on May 9, 2023, when hundreds of Jewish visitors were clustered along access roads leading to the synagogue, creating a soft target density amenable to small-arms fire.[44] His tactics centered on a rapid, opportunistic shooting spree from a vehicle or on foot near the unsecured perimeter, firing indiscriminately at pilgrims before security forces neutralized him in an exchange of gunfire.[43][42] This approach evaded preliminary patrols by leveraging his uniform and local knowledge of patrol routes, underscoring how internal radicalization can subvert routine counterterrorism measures without requiring sophisticated evasion or coordination.[37] The operation's simplicity—eschewing bombs, remote detonation, or team elements in favor of solo direct action—illustrates a jihadist model adapted to constrained resources, where positional privilege substitutes for elaborate planning or external support networks.[44][43]

Casualties and Immediate Aftermath

Victims

The shooting claimed the lives of five people: two Jewish pilgrims of French nationality participating in the annual Lag Ba'Omer pilgrimage to El Ghriba, and three Tunisian national guardsmen providing security.[45][42] One of the pilgrims, Aviel Haddad, held dual French-Israeli citizenship and was buried in Netivot, Israel; his cousin Benjamin Haddad was the other pilgrim killed.[46][47] The three guards—two killed immediately and a third succumbing to wounds shortly after—were Muslim Tunisians caught in the direct confrontation with the attacker.[45][33] Eight to ten individuals were wounded, predominantly pilgrims en route to or from the synagogue, including four visitors and four security personnel.[48][49] The targeted pilgrims were primarily older adults from European and Israeli diaspora communities drawn to the event's religious significance, underscoring the attack's focus on Jewish participants amid the gathering of hundreds for the holiday observance.[1][33]

Emergency Response and Containment

Security forces engaged the attacker, a Tunisian national guardsman, shortly after he initiated the shooting near the El Ghriba synagogue, neutralizing him by gunfire and preventing escalation of the assault.[43][33] This response contained the immediate threat from the lone perpetrator, who had killed two pilgrims and wounded security personnel before being stopped.[3] The synagogue premises were promptly secured by additional police units, and the annual Jewish pilgrimage underway was halted to ensure participant safety amid the disruption.[50] No secondary attacks materialized in the vicinity, contrasting with patterns in other jihadist incidents where follow-on threats have occurred. Wounded officers received initial treatment at local Djerba hospitals, with one succumbing to injuries the following day.[1] Tunisian officials initially characterized the event as an isolated action by an internal security member, emphasizing an ongoing probe into personal motives, though subsequent findings confirmed premeditation targeting the Jewish site.[37] The swift operational handling limited the incident's scope, averting a larger massacre during the crowded pilgrimage gathering.[51]

Claim of Responsibility and Motivations

ISIS Affiliation and Propaganda

The Islamic State (ISIS) claimed responsibility for the shooting via its affiliated Amaq News Agency on May 10, 2023, one day after the attack, asserting that "a fighter of the Islamic State" had targeted Jews and Christians gathered for "infidel gatherings."[52] This statement framed the operation as part of ISIS's ongoing global jihad against religious minorities perceived as allied with Western interests, emphasizing the ritualistic nature of the victims' assembly during the annual Ghriba pilgrimage.[52] The claim's specificity to the attack's location and timing—near the El Ghriba synagogue on Djerba's coast—undermined initial Tunisian characterizations of the perpetrator as acting in isolation, pointing instead to potential ideological alignment or operational inspiration from the group's networks.[52] The Amaq attribution aligns the 2023 incident with ISIS's propaganda playbook, which routinely glorifies strikes on Jewish targets as reprisals for foreign policies in Muslim lands, including support for Israel and military interventions in Iraq and Syria.[34] Such narratives have historically amplified attacks on symbolic sites like El Ghriba, previously bombed by al-Qaeda in 2002, killing 21 and establishing the location as a recurring focal point for jihadist messaging against "crusaders and Jews."[34] ISIS's prompt endorsement via Amaq, a channel used for verifying field operations, underscores the group's interest in associating itself with the event to sustain recruitment and deterrence amid its territorial losses.[52]

Ideological Drivers and Antisemitic Context

The attacker's pledge of allegiance to ISIS reflects the organization's core Salafi-jihadist ideology, which frames Jews as an existential threat to Islam, invoking apocalyptic narratives where their elimination precedes the triumph of true believers and the reestablishment of a global caliphate. This worldview is rooted in selective interpretations of Islamic eschatology, particularly hadiths prophesying a final battle against Jews, such as the narration in Sahih Muslim stating that "the Hour will not come until Muslims fight the Jews, and the Jews will hide behind rocks and trees, which will say, 'O Muslim, there is a Jew behind me; come and kill him.'"[53] ISIS propaganda, including issues of its magazine Dabiq and videos, routinely cites these texts to depict Jewish civilians and symbols as legitimate targets for violence, portraying attacks as fulfillment of divine mandate rather than mere political retaliation.[54] Such doctrine positions synagogues like El Ghriba not as places of worship but as emblems of Jewish "occupation" and infidelity, justifying lone-actor or small-cell assaults to maximize psychological impact on dispersed Jewish minorities. The 2023 Djerba shooting exemplifies a recurring jihadist tactic of striking Jewish religious sites to assert dominance over historic Muslim lands, mirroring prior ISIS-inspired or affiliated operations, including the 2015 Paris kosher supermarket siege and attempts in Copenhagen and Mumbai, where perpetrators explicitly invoked anti-Jewish religious imperatives.[55] This pattern underscores a causal emphasis on ideological purification over opportunistic crime, with attackers often self-radicalized via online dissemination of these motifs. In Tunisia, where the state upholds a secular framework post-2011 revolution, jihadist networks exploit latent Salafi currents and cross-border propaganda to frame local Jewish communities—numbering around 1,000—as proxies for global Zionist conspiracies, bypassing official narratives of coexistence. Empirical evidence from the attacker's profile and ISIS claims highlights how this ideology sustains recruitment amid economic discontent, without reliance on transient events, as seen in the continuity from the 2002 al-Qaeda bombing at the same site, which killed 20 and was similarly motivated by anti-Jewish fervor.[56] Despite institutional secularism, the persistence of such attacks reveals an undercurrent of Islamist antisemitism that prioritizes doctrinal enmity over national pluralism.

Investigations and Legal Proceedings

Tunisian Authorities' Inquiry

The Tunisian Interior Ministry confirmed on May 12, 2023, that the attack on the El Ghriba Synagogue was premeditated, with the perpetrator, identified as 30-year-old national guardsman Wissam Khazri, deliberately targeting the site during the annual Jewish pilgrimage.[37][35] Khazri, stationed at a naval security post approximately 20 kilometers away, first shot and killed his colleague to seize the victim's service weapon and ammunition magazines before proceeding to the synagogue.[1] Ballistic analysis aligned with this sequence, confirming the use of the appropriated insider firearm in the shootings that killed two pilgrims and three security personnel.[57] Investigators conducted searches for potential accomplices in the days following the incident, but no significant arrests or networks were publicly reported, with authorities describing Khazri's actions as those of a lone actor lacking prior intelligence flags or criminal history.[35] Autopsies on the victims, including the Jewish civilians whose bodies were transported to Tunis, corroborated the cause of death as gunshot wounds, though detailed forensic reports on radicalization indicators—such as digital footprints or ideological materials—remained undisclosed.[57] With Khazri killed by responding security forces at the scene, no criminal trial ensued, limiting further judicial scrutiny into motives beyond the ministry's initial assessment of a targeted "criminal act."[37] Transparency on deeper probes into Khazri's potential jihadist influences was constrained, as Tunisian officials emphasized personal grievances over organized terrorism, despite the country's persistent jihadist threat environment evidenced by prior attacks and ongoing counterterrorism operations into 2024.[58] This approach contrasted with empirical indicators of intent, such as the attack's timing during a high-profile religious event, but aligned with the ministry's framing to avoid broader security implications.[59]

International Involvement and Accomplice Probes

The United States Department of State classified the May 9, 2023, shooting near El Ghriba synagogue as a terrorist incident in its Country Reports on Terrorism for 2023, attributing it to a National Guard member who targeted pilgrims during the annual Jewish festival.[58] This designation contrasted with Tunisian authorities' characterization of the attack as a non-terrorist act driven by the perpetrator's personal financial grievances, highlighting divergences in international assessments that prompted external scrutiny of potential broader networks.[37] French and Israeli officials, responding to the deaths of two dual French-Israeli citizens among the victims, sought greater transparency from Tunisian counterparts, including details on victim autopsies conducted by local authorities despite Jewish communal opposition on religious grounds.[60] These requests underscored concerns over limited intelligence sharing, as Tunisia maintained the gunman acted alone without external direction, while foreign probes examined possible radicalization influences amid the attacker's reported searches of jihadist materials online.[61] No confirmed foreign handlers emerged from investigations, but Western agencies continued monitoring ISIS-affiliated networks in North Africa for any indirect ties, given the group's history of antisemitic incitements and the site's symbolic value during Passover. Scrutiny extended to security protocols at Djerba–Zarzis International Airport and access checkpoints, where pilgrims arrived and were screened before heading to the synagogue; lapses in internal vetting allowed the uniformed perpetrator—posted at a nearby naval facility—to hijack a vehicle and approach unchecked.[62] International partners pressed for collaborative reviews, citing gaps in counter-radicalization within Tunisia's security forces, though cooperation remained constrained by local insistence on a isolated motive.[59] In May 2024, U.S. and French diplomats visited the site for commemorations exactly one year later, emphasizing persistent vulnerabilities and the need for vigilance against accomplice threats in unresolved ISIS ecosystems, as evidenced by subdued pilgrimage attendance amid heightened risks.[63] These engagements reflected ongoing external pressure for thorough accomplice tracing, amid reports of inadequate Tunisian follow-through on potential network links.[64]

Reactions

Tunisian Government and Domestic Response

The Tunisian government under President Kais Saied characterized the May 9, 2023, shooting at the El Ghriba Synagogue as an act driven by motives to undermine national stability and institutions, rather than terrorism or antisemitism, despite the Islamic State's subsequent claim of responsibility targeting Jewish pilgrims.[65] [59] Saied explicitly rejected an antisemitic framing on May 13, 2023, attributing the attack—perpetrated by a Tunisian national guardsman who killed two Jewish pilgrims and three security personnel—to internal discord rather than ideological hatred.[65] This stance aligned with broader efforts to contain domestic perceptions of vulnerability, prioritizing the protection of Tunisia's tourism sector, which relies heavily on Djerba's appeal to visitors including Jewish pilgrims.[36] In response, Saied met with the country's chief rabbi on May 17, 2023, pledging enhanced security guarantees for Jewish citizens and synagogues to reassure the small domestic Jewish community and signal continuity of interfaith tolerance.[66] The following day, he hosted Jewish, Muslim, and Christian religious leaders at the presidential palace, reiterating themes of national coexistence while condemning the shooting as a "cowardly" bid to sow division, without referencing Islamist extremism.[67] These gestures were accompanied by public mourning observances, though official discourse incorporated critiques of external influences, including Saied's May 16 remarks highlighting Tunisia's historical protection of Jews during World War II amid broader anti-Western sentiments.[68] Authorities implemented tighter security protocols in the immediate aftermath, including heightened patrols around religious sites, to facilitate the ongoing Ghriba pilgrimage and avert further incidents, though state-controlled media emphasized individual culpability over organized threats to mitigate tourism disruptions.[69] This approach reflected a pattern of narrative control, limiting explicit terrorism associations in domestic reporting to preserve economic stability, even as the attack's targeted nature against Jews raised questions about underlying Islamist motivations evidenced by the perpetrator's affiliations.[36]

Jewish Organizations and Israeli Stance

The European Jewish Congress issued a statement on May 10, 2023, condemning the shooting as a "deadly terror attack" at the Al-Ghriba Synagogue, with President Ariel Muzicant highlighting that "terror attacks continue to target Jews around the world even when they pray in the oldest synagogue in Africa."[70] The American Jewish Committee similarly labeled the incident a "terror attack" on the same day, expressing horror and standing in solidarity with Tunisia's Jewish community amid a pattern of threats to Jewish sites in North Africa.[1] These organizations emphasized the antisemitic motivations evident in the perpetrator's targeting of pilgrims during the Lag BaOmer observance, rejecting attempts to downplay it as non-ideological. Tunisian Jewish leaders, including community heads, insisted the attack constituted terrorism, arguing it was premeditated and preventable given prior security lapses at the site, and linked it to jihadist ideologies rather than personal grievances.[71] The Conference of European Rabbis urged European governments to denounce Tunisian authorities' minimization of antisemitism following the shooting, framing it as part of broader jihadist aggression against Jewish targets.[72] Israel condemned the attack as terrorism and raised alarms over Jewish safety in Tunisia, contributing to sharp declines in Israeli participation at the Ghriba pilgrimage; in 2024, the event was scaled back significantly due to persistent security risks from the 2023 incident and regional jihadist threats.[73] This stance reflected broader Israeli advisories cautioning against travel to high-risk areas, underscoring diaspora vulnerabilities to Islamist violence patterns in North Africa.

International Condemnations and Diplomatic Actions

The French Ministry of Foreign Affairs condemned the attack on May 10, 2023, stating that France was "appalled and deeply saddened" by the violence perpetrated at the El Ghriba synagogue in Djerba, which killed four people including French nationals, and extended condolences to the victims' families while affirming solidarity with Tunisia against terrorism.[74] The United States government responded by having its Ambassador to Tunisia visit Djerba on May 18, 2023, to express condolences directly to the victims' families and engage with the local Jewish community regarding the security of religious sites.[2] In its annual Country Reports on Terrorism for 2023, the U.S. State Department documented the Djerba incident as a terrorist attack targeting civilians near a synagogue during a Jewish pilgrimage, highlighting Tunisia's vulnerabilities to Islamist extremism despite improved counterterrorism measures.[58] On May 26, 2024, marking the first anniversary, U.S. and French diplomats jointly visited the Ghriba synagogue site to pay respects to the victims, underscoring ongoing international concern over the premeditated nature of the assault on a place of worship.[63][75] No formal international sanctions were imposed on Tunisia in direct response, though the U.S. State Department upheld its Level 2 travel advisory for the country as of October 2024, advising increased caution due to persistent terrorism risks, including potential attacks on tourist areas and religious venues like synagogues, with explicit reference to the Djerba shooting as evidence of such threats.[76] Similar advisories from other Western governments, such as the UK's, reiterated high terrorism threats in Tunisia without altering pre-existing restrictions post-attack.[25]

Controversies and Denials

Downplaying Antisemitism by Tunisian Leadership

Tunisian President Kais Saied rejected characterizations of the May 9, 2023, attack on the El Ghriba synagogue as an act of antisemitism, insisting it did not reflect broader societal prejudice against Jews in the country.[65] [77] In a May 16 statement, Saied dismissed such accusations as distortions of history by those who "conspire against Tunisia," while emphasizing that Tunisians, including his own grandfather, had protected Jews during World War II, contrasting this with criticism of Israel's actions toward Palestinians.[68] [78] This response framed the incident as an aberration rather than indicative of jihadist ideology, despite the attacker's deliberate targeting of Jewish pilgrims during the annual Ghriba pilgrimage.[59] Tunisian officials, including Interior Minister Kamel Maddouri, described the shooting as a "premeditated criminal act" stemming from personal motives, such as the perpetrator's disputes with customs colleagues, while avoiding the label of terrorism.[35] The attacker, identified as 30-year-old Tunisian national Ahmed al-Housni, a port security guard, killed five individuals—two French Jewish pilgrims, a French national, a Tunisian policeman, and another Tunisian—after hijacking a vehicle and driving toward a group of tourists near the synagogue, shouting "Allahu Akbar" during the assault.[35] This narrative conflicted with evidence of ideological intent, as the site's selection during a Jewish religious event and subsequent praise from ISIS supporters underscored antisemitic targeting over isolated grievances.[59] Saied's deflection echoed patterns of equivocation in Tunisian discourse on Jewish persecution, where claims of historical protection overlook documented antisemitic measures under Vichy French and Nazi influence during 1942-1943, including forced labor and property seizures affecting thousands of Tunisian Jews.[79] By prioritizing assertions of Tunisian benevolence amid wartime events—despite collaboration by local authorities—Saied's comments aligned with broader Arab-world tendencies to minimize recognition of antisemitism, prioritizing anti-Zionist rhetoric over acknowledgment of religiously motivated violence against Jews.[80] Such downplaying risked undermining security assurances to Tunisia's remaining Jewish community of approximately 1,000, as critics noted it signaled tolerance for extremist signals rather than confronting jihadist drivers.[81]

Media and Political Narratives on Terrorism

Initial coverage in outlets such as Al Jazeera described the May 9, 2023, incident as an "attack near synagogue," framing it in terms of a naval guard's actions against visitors and security personnel without immediate emphasis on the ideological targeting of Jewish pilgrims during the Ghriba festival.[43] This phrasing, echoed in some Western reports opting for "shooting" over "terrorist attack," reflected a broader hesitancy to apply the terrorism label in Muslim-majority contexts, potentially stemming from sensitivities around stigmatizing Islamist perpetrators amid patterns of post-9/11 narrative caution.[49] Political figures in Tunisia and aligned Arab discourse further downplayed jihadist causality, with President Kais Saied rejecting antisemitic motives and attributing the violence to individual criminality or economic pressures, a stance critiqued for ignoring the attacker's deliberate selection of Jewish targets at El Ghriba—site of a prior 2002 al-Qaeda bombing that killed 21.[80][38] Such framings prioritize "root causes" like poverty over empirical evidence of ideological drivers, as the perpetrator Wissam Khazri, a national guardsman, acted alone but chose a symbolic Jewish pilgrimage amid Tunisia's history of jihadist radicalization exporting fighters to groups like ISIS.[71][23] In contrast, conservative and Israeli-aligned media classified it unequivocally as terrorism rooted in Islamist antisemitism, highlighting the inconsistency of economic explanations given the attack's precision against Jews rather than random economic targets, and the failure of socioeconomic factors to predict or explain recurrent North African assaults on Jewish sites by ideologically motivated actors.[80][82] This divergence reveals a normalized avoidance in left-leaning institutions of attributing causality to radical Islamist ideology, despite data from repeated synagogue-targeted attacks debunking non-ideological narratives and underscoring source biases in state-influenced outlets like those in Qatar-funded media.[38][80]

Broader Implications

Impact on Jewish Life and Pilgrimages in Tunisia

The 2023 shooting at El Ghriba Synagogue occurred during the annual Lag BaOmer pilgrimage, resulting in five deaths—two Jewish pilgrims and three Tunisian security personnel—and prompting an immediate disruption to the event, with heightened security measures curtailing further activities.[1] In response, the 2024 pilgrimage was significantly scaled back, limited to a small group of about a dozen participants under heavy security, reflecting widespread fears following the prior year's violence and the ongoing Gaza conflict.[83] [84] Similarly, the 2025 pilgrimage drew only around 30 attendees, confined to minimal religious rituals inside the synagogue amid persistent safety concerns.[85] These incidents have accelerated the emigration of Djerba's Jewish community, estimated at around 1,000-1,500 prior to 2023, as residents cite escalating insecurity for relocating primarily to Israel.[86] Further attacks in 2025, including a May machete assault on a Jewish jeweler that severed fingers and an August axe attack on another Jewish store owner, have intensified these fears, leading community leaders to describe the "holiday" atmosphere of Jewish life in Djerba as over.[87] [88] While general tourism to Djerba has been affected, the primary concern for the Jewish population remains personal safety and communal survival rather than economic contributions from pilgrimages.[86]

Patterns of Islamist Antisemitism in North Africa

Despite territorial defeats of the Islamic State (ISIS) by 2019, jihadist cells affiliated with ISIS and al-Qaeda affiliates have persisted in North Africa, particularly in Tunisia, where incomplete counterterrorism efforts have allowed small networks to regroup and exploit local grievances for recruitment and operations. Tunisia, which supplied thousands of fighters to ISIS in Syria and Iraq, has struggled with radicalized returnees and border porosity with Libya and Algeria, enabling low-level attacks despite government crackdowns. This persistence underscores a causal failure to dismantle ideological underpinnings, as Salafi-jihadist doctrines view Jewish presence in Muslim lands as an affront to Islamic supremacy, framing attacks on synagogues and pilgrims as religious imperatives rather than isolated criminal acts.[22][89] In the Maghreb, Islamist antisemitism represents a fusion of historical prejudices with modern jihadist ideology, rejecting secular or nationalist explanations that downplay religious motivations; empirical evidence shows Salafi-jihadist groups invoking Quranic tropes of Jewish enmity to justify violence, as seen in propaganda from ISIS's North African branches. Post-October 7, 2023, Arabic-language online antisemitism surged, with analyses documenting a tripling of religiously rooted narratives portraying Jews as inherent enemies and conspirators against Islam, disseminated across platforms in Tunisia, Morocco, and Algeria. This rise correlates with jihadist calls from al-Qaeda and ISIS for global attacks on Jewish targets, interpreting regional events as opportunities to revive caliphate ambitions through symbolic strikes in historically Muslim territories.[90][91][92] Such patterns position soft Jewish targets in North Africa—remnant communities and pilgrimage sites—as testing grounds for jihadist operational resilience and ideological recruitment, signaling broader threats to global Jewish security by normalizing violence in areas of purported Muslim-Jewish "coexistence." Data from extremism monitors indicate that these attacks serve dual purposes: asserting dominance over infidels within dar al-Islam and inspiring lone actors or cells elsewhere, as jihadist manuals prioritize Jewish sites for their propaganda value in perpetuating a narrative of perpetual holy war. Without addressing the doctrinal core—evident in unchanged fatwas and manifestos despite military losses—these threats recur, eroding security for the few thousand Jews remaining in the region and highlighting the inadequacy of kinetic-only countermeasures.[93][94]
