Italian landing helicopter dock Trieste

The Trieste (L 9890) is a multi-purpose landing helicopter dock (LHD) serving as the flagship of the Italian Navy's Amphibious Task Group.[1] Commissioned on 7 December 2024, it represents the largest warship constructed for Italy since the Second World War, enhancing the nation's amphibious projection capabilities with integrated aviation and landing operations.[2][3] Built by Fincantieri, the vessel displaces 35,050 tons at full load and measures 245 meters in length with a 55-meter beam, enabling it to embark up to 600 troops alongside vehicles and equipment totaling 1,200 linear meters of capacity.[1] Equipped with a CODOGOL propulsion system combining gas turbines, diesel engines, and electric motors, Trieste achieves a maximum speed of 25 knots and an endurance of 7,000 nautical miles at 16 knots.[1] Its aviation facilities include a 230-by-36-meter flight deck with a ski-jump ramp and a 2,200-square-meter hangar supporting up to 20 F-35B STOVL fighters or various helicopters such as the EH-101 and NH90.[1][4] Amphibious operations are facilitated by a well deck accommodating four LC23 landing craft or one LCAC, complemented by a 900-square-meter garage for tanks and vehicles.[1] Armed with three Leonardo 76 mm guns, three 25 mm remote weapon stations, and provisions for the SAAM-ESD air defense system with Aster missiles, Trieste also features advanced C4I systems and a 770-square-meter hospital configured to Role 2E standards for medical support in expeditionary scenarios.[1] Designed for flexibility, the ship supports the Italian Expeditionary Task Force in power projection, humanitarian assistance, and disaster relief, positioning Italy among nations with robust medium-scale amphibious forces.[1]

Development and Construction

Planning and Strategic Rationale

The LHD Trieste was planned under Italy's Naval Law, a legislative framework approved by the government and parliament in May 2015 to renew and expand the Italian Navy's operational assets amid evolving maritime security threats, including the need for enhanced power projection and rapid response capabilities.[5] This initiative addressed the limitations of aging amphibious vessels, such as the San Giorgio-class ships, by prioritizing a multi-role landing helicopter dock to integrate advanced amphibious, aviation, and logistical functions into a single platform.[6] The program's strategic focus stemmed from Italy's geographic position in the Mediterranean, necessitating versatile naval forces for territorial defense, alliance commitments, and expeditionary operations. Strategically, Trieste enables the projection of a 600-man amphibious battalion, supported by landing craft, vehicles, helicopters, and command facilities, to conduct assault landings, crisis interventions, and sustained operations ashore without reliance on host-nation infrastructure.[7] [8] Its design emphasizes command and control for amphibious task groups, aligning with the Italian Navy's doctrine for sea-based force deployment in contested environments, including NATO-led missions and EU battlegroups.[9] This capability addresses causal gaps in prior fleet compositions, where smaller landing platforms limited scale and endurance, thereby enhancing deterrence and operational reach against hybrid threats like migration crises or regional instability. Beyond military applications, the rationale incorporates dual-use versatility for humanitarian assistance and disaster relief, such as providing medical support, utilities, and evacuation in natural disasters, reflecting Italy's civil-military integration priorities.[5] Trieste also serves as a contingency platform for short-takeoff vertical-landing aircraft like the F-35B during unavailability of the carrier Cavour, ensuring continuous air-maritime integration without compromising amphibious primacy.[1] Overall, the planning underscores a first-principles approach to naval architecture: prioritizing modularity, endurance (with over 1,000 personnel accommodations), and interoperability to sustain Italy's contributions to collective defense while adapting to fiscal constraints through national industrial capacity.[10]

Construction Timeline and Milestones

The contract for the construction of the Italian landing helicopter dock Trieste (pennant number L 9890) was awarded to Fincantieri on 1 July 2015 as part of Italy's naval procurement program.[11] The steel-cutting ceremony, marking the start of hull fabrication, occurred on 12 July 2017 at Fincantieri's Castellammare di Stabia shipyard near Naples.[12] Keel laying followed on 20 February 2018, initiating the formal assembly of the hull sections.[3] The ship was launched on 25 May 2019 in a ceremony attended by Italian defense officials, allowing for the transition to outfitting and integration of systems.[13] Outfitting progressed through 2020, with the vessel entering the water for initial afloat work; however, the COVID-19 pandemic caused delays in supply chains and testing.[14] Trieste conducted its maiden sea trials in August 2021, validating basic propulsion and stability.[14] Further trials and acceptance testing extended into 2024, pushing delivery from the original 2022 target.[15] Fincantieri handed over Trieste to the Italian Navy on 7 December 2024 at La Spezia, completing the build phase ahead of commissioning later that day.[1]

Technical Design and Specifications

Hull, Propulsion, and General Characteristics

The Trieste (L 9890) features a steel hull measuring 245 meters in overall length, with a beam of 36 meters at the waterline expanding to 47 meters across the flight deck, and a draft of 7.2 meters.[3][16][17] Its full-load displacement is approximately 33,000 tonnes, though some reports cite up to 38,000 tonnes reflecting operational configurations.[3][17][18] The design incorporates a floodable well deck for amphibious operations, enabling the embarkation of landing craft, alongside modular garage spaces for vehicles.[19] Propulsion is provided by a CODLOG (combined diesel-electric or gas) system, comprising two gas turbines, diesel generators, and two 2.25 MW electric motors driving twin propeller shafts.[3][20][18] This configuration delivers a maximum speed of 25 knots using gas turbines, 18 knots on diesel, and 10 knots on electric motors alone, with a range of 7,000 nautical miles at 16 knots.[3][16][20] Electrical power is generated by four 5.4 MW MAN 9L32/44CR diesel gensets, supporting both propulsion and ship systems.[1] General characteristics include accommodations for a core crew of around 450 personnel, expandable to over 1,000 with embarked troops and support staff.[3][21] The vessel's multipurpose layout supports aviation, amphibious, and humanitarian roles, with a 230 by 36 meter flight deck and extensive internal volume for logistics.[1][2]

Armament, Sensors, and Defensive Systems

The Trieste is armed with three Leonardo 76/62 mm Super Rapido multi-feeding gun mounts configured for the Strales vertically launched guided ammunition, enabling engagement of air, surface, and asymmetric threats at extended ranges.[22][23] These guns form the core of the ship's inner layer defense system, with capabilities for rapid fire rates up to 120 rounds per minute per barrel and integration with fire-control radars for precision targeting.[1] Complementing the primary guns are three remotely controlled Leonardo NA-25 dual-band radar-electro-optical/infrared (EO/IR) 25 mm autocannons for close-in defense against small boats, drones, and missiles.[24][18] Sensors on the Trieste derive from the combat management system adapted from Italy's Pattugliatori Polivalenti d'Altura (PPA) frigates, featuring Leonardo's Kronos family of active electronically scanned array (AESA) radars for multi-role surveillance, including air and surface search in L-band frequencies.[12][25] The radar suite integrates with the Leonardo SIR-M-PA identification friend-or-foe (IFF) system using conformal antennas for enhanced detection and tracking, while an electronic warfare (EW) suite from ELT Group provides electronic support measures, countermeasures, and direction-finding against radar emissions.[1] Defensive systems emphasize layered protection without vertical launch systems for missiles, relying instead on the gun-based armament for self-defense against aerial, missile, and surface threats, supplemented by decoy launchers and the ship's combat management system for threat prioritization and response automation.[23][25] The configuration prioritizes operational flexibility for amphibious missions over heavy offensive strike capabilities, aligning with the vessel's primary role in power projection and humanitarian support.[1]

Aviation Facilities and Air Wing Compatibility

The aviation facilities of the Italian landing helicopter dock Trieste (L9890) center on a expansive flight deck measuring approximately 7,400 mÂ², equipped with nine dedicated helicopter landing spots and supported by an aft-mounted island superstructure for flight operations control.[24][18] Two aircraft elevators facilitate movement between the flight deck and hangar, enabling efficient aircraft handling during sustained operations.[18] The hangar, located on Deck 5, spans 2,200 mÂ² with dimensions of 107.8 m in length by 21â€“25 m in beam and a height ranging from 7.8 to 10.7 m, allowing storage for up to 14 rotary- or fixed-wing aircraft in varied configurations, alongside space for vehicles and materiel totaling up to 1,200 tonnes.[1] This setup supports modular air wing compositions tailored to mission requirements, such as amphibious assaults or limited carrier operations. Trieste is compatible with the Italian Navy's primary rotary-wing assets, including up to eight AgustaWestland AW101 (EH101) heavy-lift helicopters or a combination of NHIndustries NH90 (SH-90A) medium helicopters and Leonardo AW129 (Mangusta) light attack helicopters for transport, utility, and anti-submarine roles.[16] In a fixed-wing configuration, the vessel can embark up to 20 Lockheed Martin F-35B Lightning II short take-off and vertical-landing (STOVL) jets alongside two NH90 helicopters, enabling air superiority and strike capabilities akin to a light aircraft carrier, though initial operations emphasize helicopter-centric amphibious support.[23] Overall air wing capacity reaches 28â€“34 aircraft depending on mix and mission profile, prioritizing flexibility for NATO interoperability and power projection.[12]

Amphibious and Logistics Capabilities

The Trieste possesses a floodable well deck at the stern, measuring 50 by 15 meters, which facilitates amphibious assault operations by accommodating NATO-standard small and medium landing craft. This configuration allows for the embarkation of up to four LC-23 Landing Craft Medium (LCM) or one LCAC (Landing Craft Air Cushion) surface-effect vessel.[23][24] The ship's garage deck, spanning 18 by 50 meters and convertible for dual use with aviation facilities, provides storage for military and civilian vehicles, including main battle tanks exceeding 60 tonnes such as the Ariete, wheeled platforms like the B1 Centauro, and tracked systems. Combined with the hangar deck, these spaces offer approximately 850 linear meters of vehicle parking capacity, enabling the transport of substantial materiel alongside combat equipment.[23][1][3] In terms of troop projection, the Trieste can embark a fully equipped amphibious battalion of up to 600 marines, in addition to its crew of around 460 personnel, for a total capacity exceeding 1,000 individuals. This supports force projection missions, including rapid deployment via landing craft or helicopters, with berthing and sustainment facilities designed for extended operations.[8][2][16]

The Italian landing helicopter dock Trieste features a fully equipped hospital module compliant with NATO Role 2E standards, spanning approximately 700â€“770 mÂ² and including surgical suites, radiology and laboratory facilities, a dental office, and accommodations for up to 27â€“28 patients.[16][1][7] This setup enables advanced trauma care, intensive treatment, and interoperability with allied forces during amphibious operations or crises. The hospital can be expanded using modular shelters in the hangar for increased capacity in mass casualty scenarios.[7][23] In humanitarian and disaster relief roles, Trieste supports ashore medical assistance, potable water production (up to 70 mÂ³ per day), and electrical power supply (approximately 2,500 kVA) to affected areas, facilitating coordination with Italy's Civil Protection Department.[1][2] These capabilities position the vessel as a forward-operating base for search-and-rescue, evacuation, and logistical aid in non-combat emergencies, drawing on its dual-use design for rapid deployment in Mediterranean or international contingencies.[5][26]

Commissioning and Service History

Commissioning Process

The commissioning process for the Italian landing helicopter dock Trieste (L 9890) involved extensive sea trials, outfitting, and acceptance testing following its launch in May 2019. Initial sea trials commenced on August 12, 2021, evaluating the ship's propulsion, auxiliary systems, and basic seaworthiness during its first sea-going period from Fincantieri's Castellammare di Stabia shipyard.[14][27] These trials, which included combat system integrations planned for later phases, extended over more than three years due to delays from the COVID-19 pandemic and additional dockyard modifications, postponing delivery from an original October 2022 target.[14][27][15] Subsequent outfitting occurred at Fincantieri's Muggiano facility near La Spezia, encompassing harbour acceptance tests, final sea trials, and verification of amphibious, aviation, and logistical capabilities.[1] The vessel underwent additional work in late 2023 to address integration issues, ensuring compliance with Italian Navy specifications for multipurpose operations.[21] Upon completion, Fincantieri formally delivered Trieste to the Marina Militare on December 7, 2024, marking the handover after nine years from the initial contract award.[15][23] The official commissioning ceremony occurred concurrently at Livorno naval base on December 7, 2024, presided over by Italian President Sergio Mattarella and attended by naval officials.[2][20] This event included the solemn oath of Naval Academy cadets aboard the ship, symbolizing its integration as the fleet's new amphibious flagship, replacing the aging Giuseppe Garibaldi.[28][29] Trieste, the largest warship constructed for Italy since World War II, entered active service immediately thereafter, certified for full operational deployment in NATO-aligned amphibious tasks.[2][20]

Early Operations and Deployments

Following its commissioning on December 7, 2024, at the port of Livorno, ITS Trieste (L 9890) entered a phase of initial sea trials and operational workups to integrate into the Marina Militare's fleet structure.[2][1] The vessel's operational debut occurred during Exercise Mare Aperto 2025, Italy's premier annual maritime training event, conducted from March 26 to April 18, 2025, across multiple regions including Sardinia.[30][31] This exercise marked Trieste's first participation, highlighting its role in the Amphibious Task Group (ATG) as part of the Expeditionary Task Force (ETF), focused on joint force projection capabilities.[32][33] Mare Aperto 2025 involved approximately 6,000 personnel from eight NATO nations, 104 surface combatants, six submarines, over 50 manned and unmanned aircraft, and integrated amphibious operations simulating high-intensity scenarios.[31][34] Trieste supported amphibious assaults, aviation operations, and command-and-control functions, validating its multi-role amphibious assault design in a multinational context.[35][36] In February 2025, Trieste conducted a ceremonial port visit to the city of Trieste, its namesake, prior to broader operational engagements.[37] Later, from July 31 to August 2, 2025, the ship made a goodwill port call to Lisbon, Portugal, docking at the Santa ApolÃ³nia Cruise Terminal and opening for public visits to showcase its capabilities.[38] During this stop, Trieste hosted interns from Naval Striking and Support Forces NATO (STRIKFORNATO), facilitating international naval cooperation and public outreach.[39] These early activities emphasized Trieste's integration into routine fleet operations, port diplomacy, and preparation for expeditionary missions within NATO frameworks.[40]

Strategic Role and Future Prospects

Integration into Italian Naval Strategy

The LHD Trieste forms a cornerstone of the Italian Navy's modernization efforts under the Legge Navale program, which aims to renew the fleet and enhance sea-based power projection capabilities. Commissioned on December 7, 2024, it replaces the decommissioned aircraft carrier Giuseppe Garibaldi (decommissioned October 1, 2024) and assumes the role of flagship for the Amphibious Task Group within the broader Expeditionary Task Force structure.[2][21] This integration aligns with Italy's "Strategic Address Lines 2019-2034," emphasizing the CapacitÃ  Nazionale di Proiezione dal Mare (CNPM), a national sea projection capability centered on units like the San Marco Marine Brigade and Lagunari Regiment.[1][7] In operational terms, Trieste enables command and control (C2) for complex amphibious missions as a Commander Amphibious Task Force (CATF) or Commander Landing Force (CLF) platform, supporting a 600-man amphibious battalion through its aviation facilities, floodable well deck for four L23-class landing craft, and garage space for vehicles up to 62 tonnes.[1][7] It facilitates over-the-horizon maneuvers exceeding 24 nautical miles, bolstering Italy's ability to conduct joint operations in the Mediterranean and beyond without dependence on fixed land bases, while complementing the carrier strike group led by ITS Cavour.[2] The vessel's multi-role design extends to humanitarian assistance and disaster relief, including NATO Role 2E medical facilities with 27 beds and C4ISR systems interoperable with alliance standards, thereby supporting evacuation, medical aid, and logistics in crisis zones.[2][21] Strategically, Trieste enhances Italy's contributions to NATO and European initiatives, such as the European Amphibious Initiative and SIAF/SILF programs, by providing a versatile platform for multinational exercises like Mare Aperto 25, the alliance's largest maritime drill hosted by Italy in 2025.[1] Its flight deck compatibility with up to 20 F-35B aircraft from 2027 further blurs amphibious and aviation roles, enabling sustained air support and potential light carrier functions during Cavour's maintenance periods, thus extending operational reach and deterrence in contested environments.[1][2] This positions the Italian Navy to address regional threats, secure sea lines, and project force ashore amid evolving geopolitical pressures in the Mediterranean and North Africa.[7]

NATO Contributions and Power Projection

The LHD Trieste has contributed to NATO operations through participation in major multinational exercises, enhancing alliance amphibious and power projection capabilities in the Mediterranean. During NATO's Neptune Strike 25-1, conducted from April to May 2025 with 13 nations and over 20,000 personnel, Italian Marines utilized Trieste as the flagship platform for executing complex amphibious assaults, demonstrating integrated joint operations including vertical envelopment and shore landings.[41] The exercise's commencement featured a media event aboard Trieste, underscoring its role in simulating high-intensity deterrence scenarios against potential adversaries.[42] Additionally, Trieste served as a central asset in Italy-led Mare Aperto 25, NATO's largest maritime exercise in the Mediterranean starting March 31, 2025, where it coordinated force projection ashore and multinational interoperability among NATO allies.[36] Trieste's design supports NATO-standard power projection by enabling rapid deployment of a 600-strong amphibious battalion, supported by aviation facilities for up to 12 helicopters or 6 V/STOL aircraft, and a floodable well deck for landing craft operations.[1] Its NATO Role 2E medical facilities, spanning 770 square meters with 28 beds and extendable capabilities, provide enhanced humanitarian and casualty evacuation support in coalition environments.[1] Interoperable C4ISR systems facilitate command and control in joint NATO, EU, or ad-hoc multinational task forces, allowing Trieste to integrate seamlessly for crisis response operations.[23] As Italy's largest combat vessel since World War II, commissioned on December 7, 2024, Trieste bolsters NATO's southern flank power projection by aligning with the Italian Navy's CapacitÃ  Nazionale di Proiezione dal Mare (CNPM), enabling sustained overseas deployments for deterrence, amphibious intervention, and humanitarian assistance.[2][1] This capability extends Italy's strategic reach beyond regional waters, supporting NATO's collective defense requirements under Article 5 and non-Article 5 missions such as maritime security and rapid reaction forces.[43]

Potential Enhancements and Second Unit Considerations

The Italian Navy has underway a modification program for the Trieste to equip it as an alternate carrier vessel, particularly to support F-35B operations during periods of unavailability for the light aircraft carrier Cavour.[44] This upgrade builds on the ship's inherent STOVL-compatible flight deck and hangar, which can accommodate up to 20 fixed-wing aircraft alongside helicopters, enhancing its flexibility for air wing deployments in high-intensity scenarios.[1] Such enhancements align with Italy's acquisition of additional F-35B Lightning II jets, with five more slated for naval aviation as part of a broader order of 25 aircraft, thereby expanding the Trieste's potential strike and support capabilities without requiring full carrier conversion.[45] Further potential upgrades could include advanced sensor integrations or defensive systems to bolster survivability, though specific details remain tied to ongoing defense planning documents like the 2024-2026 Direttiva di Programmazione (DPP), which prioritize maritime strengthening amid fiscal constraints.[44] These modifications emphasize modular adaptations, such as improved aviation fuel storage and deck reinforcements, to maximize the vessel's dual-role amphibious and aviation functions while maintaining cost-effectiveness. Although early conceptual planning for the Trieste class considered multiple units to replace aging amphibious assets like the San Giorgio-class ships, only one vessel was funded and constructed due to budgetary limitations and shifting priorities toward complementary smaller platforms, including three 16,500-tonne multi-role vessels and additional Landing Ship Standards (LSS).[46] No second Trieste-class LHD is currently programmed, with considerations for expanded amphibious capacity instead focusing on these lighter units for force projection and logistics support.[47] Longer-term prospects for a second large amphibious or carrier-type unit are speculative, with Italy initiating studies in 2026 for a nuclear-powered aircraft carrier under the Portaerei di Nuova Generazione program, potentially entering service by 2040 to address evolving threats in the Mediterranean and beyond.[48] This reflects strategic deliberations on balancing amphibious needs with airpower requirements, though realization depends on defense budgets exceeding â‚¬30 billion annually and geopolitical demands for NATO interoperability.[49]
