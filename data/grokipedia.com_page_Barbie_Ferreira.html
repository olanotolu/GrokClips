Barbie Ferreira

Barbara Linhares Ferreira (born December 14, 1996), known professionally as Barbie Ferreira, is an American actress and model of Brazilian descent.[1][2] Born in Queens, New York, and raised in New Jersey by her Brazilian mother and grandmother, she rose to prominence portraying Kat Hernandez, a high school student exploring sexuality and self-image, in the HBO series Euphoria from 2019 to 2022.[3][4] Prior to acting, Ferreira modeled for brands including Aerie and luxury labels, gaining recognition in the fashion industry despite limited prior experience.[5] Her Euphoria tenure earned nominations for awards such as the Shorty Award for Best Actor and a Queerty for TV Performance, though she exited the series in 2022 amid rumors of creative tensions with showrunner Sam Levinson, which she later denied, citing instead the completion of her character's storyline and a desire for new projects.[6][7] Subsequent roles include appearances in films like Unpregnant (2020) and Jordan Peele's Nope (2022), alongside stage work on Broadway in Cult of Love.[8] In 2025, she received a Rising Star Award at Malta's Mediterrane Film Festival.[9]

Early life

Family background and upbringing

Barbara Ferreira, born Barbara Linhares Ferreira on December 14, 1996, in East Harlem, New York City, is of Brazilian descent through both parents, who immigrated to the United States.[10][5] Her mother, Jana Seppe, emigrated from Brazil around age 18 to 20 and worked as a chef after settling in Queens, where she met Ferreira's father.[10][11] Ferreira was raised primarily by her single mother, aunt, and grandmother in a matriarchal household, with her family relocating from East Harlem to Queens during her early childhood.[5][12] The family later moved to Maywood, New Jersey, where she spent part of her formative years.[1] This upbringing in working-class immigrant communities shaped her exposure to diverse urban environments in New York and New Jersey.[13]

Education and early interests

Ferreira was born on December 14, 1996, in Queens, New York, and later relocated with her family to Maywood, New Jersey, where she attended Hackensack High School.[1][14] She has described her high school experience as challenging and isolating, citing struggles with body image and social dynamics that contributed to a sense of alienation during her teenage years.[15] From a young age, Ferreira expressed a strong interest in acting, discovering the pursuit at age seven through an after-school program at a Boys & Girls Club in Queens, which helped draw her out of her initially shy disposition.[13] This passion persisted into her high school years, during which she participated in local theater productions and enrolled in acting classes at studios to hone her skills, viewing acting as her primary career aspiration rather than modeling, which she considered only peripherally at the time.[16][17] Additionally, Ferreira developed an early hobby in makeup artistry, influenced by watching tutorials on YouTube as a child, an interest that later intersected with her professional modeling opportunities.[18]

Career beginnings

Entry into modeling

Ferreira entered the modeling industry at age 16 after discovering an open casting call from American Apparel via a message on Tumblr.[17] Skeptical but curious, she submitted a low-quality selfie taken while ill and received a response the following day, leading to her first modeling opportunities with the brand.[17] She simultaneously worked on the sales floor at an American Apparel store, modeling various clothing lines including those for the brand Petra, which helped her navigate the nascent plus-size or curve modeling niche.[19] Following her initial success with American Apparel, Ferreira signed with the Wilhelmina modeling agency, expanding her professional reach beyond informal castings.[17] This transition marked her shift from self-initiated submissions to structured agency representation, though she initially resisted plus-size categorization, preferring broader editorial work.[17] Her early experiences built resilience against online criticism, as she encountered backlash for her body type while developing a professional presence.[20] A pivotal moment came in early 2016 when Ferreira appeared in American Eagle's AerieReal campaign as its first U.S. size 12 model, featuring unretouched images that gained widespread attention for promoting body diversity without digital alterations.[21] [22] The campaign, part of Aerie's initiative launched in 2014 to showcase varied body types, highlighted her in swimwear and lingerie, solidifying her visibility in the industry.[22] This exposure contrasted with her Tumblr origins, propelling her from niche online discovery to mainstream fashion campaigns.[23]

Initial acting opportunities

Ferreira's entry into acting followed her modeling career and involved early on-camera hosting roles that showcased her presence and commentary on social issues. In the mid-2010s, she hosted the Vice web series How to Behave, appearing in 10 episodes that explored topics including sex, money, and body image; the series earned her a Webby Award for Best Web Personality/Host.[4] She also hosted Body Party for Teen Vogue, a web series focused on body positivity themes, further building her visibility in digital media.[4] These hosting gigs transitioned into scripted television work, with Ferreira securing a recurring role as Ella in two episodes of the HBO series Divorce in 2019, starring alongside Sarah Jessica Parker and Thomas Haden Church.[4] Ferreira has described auditioning for smaller roles during this period as a deliberate risk after forgoing college to pursue acting full-time, amid her modeling commitments.[24] These initial opportunities, though limited in scope, provided foundational experience and exposure leading to her casting in HBO's Euphoria later that year.[4]

Professional career

Modeling milestones

Ferreira began her modeling career as a teenager, around age 16, after submitting self-portraits taken while working at an American Apparel retail store to the brand's open casting call.[25][26] This led to her early work with American Apparel, marking her entry into professional plus-size modeling without prior industry experience or formal interest in the field.[19][27] A pivotal milestone came in early 2016 when Ferreira became the first U.S. size 12 model featured in American Eagle Outfitters' #AerieREAL campaign, which emphasized unretouched imagery to promote body diversity.[21][22] Her unedited swimwear and lingerie ads, released in January 2016, gained widespread attention for challenging conventional beauty standards in mainstream advertising.[28][29] This exposure expanded her portfolio to include campaigns for brands such as Adidas, H&M, and Target, solidifying her presence in commercial fashion.[27][3] In February 2022, Ferreira was appointed U.S. brand ambassador for YSL Beauty, appearing in digital campaigns promoting the Black Opium fragrance and makeup lines, which highlighted her transition to luxury endorsements.[30] Her most recent major achievement occurred on October 15, 2025, with her runway debut at the Victoria's Secret Fashion Show in New York City, walking for the PINK sub-brand in denim and lingerie looks.[31][32] This event represented a significant evolution, as Victoria's Secret had historically favored slimmer body types, positioning Ferreira as a key figure in the brand's recent inclusivity efforts.[33]

Acting in Euphoria

Ferreira portrayed Katherine "Kat" Hernandez, a plus-size high school student navigating body image insecurities, sexual awakening, and online sex work, in the first two seasons of HBO's Euphoria, which premiered on June 16, 2019.[34] She obtained the role through a competitive two-month audition process following her modeling career, emphasizing her personal connection to the character's struggles with self-perception and empowerment.[35] In season 1, Ferreira's performance depicted Kat's transformation from a reserved, self-conscious teen who briefly attempts weight loss to one embracing cam modeling for financial independence and confidence, drawing directly from Ferreira's own experiences with body dysmorphia and industry scrutiny.[34] Critics commended her ability to anchor emotionally raw scenes, such as Kat's confrontations with vulnerability, grounding the series' stylistic excess with authentic emotional depth.[36] Season 2, airing from January 9, 2022, shifted Kat's arc toward relational tensions, including a romance with classmate Ethan and ensuing paranoia, but featured significantly reduced screen time compared to peers like Rue and Cassie, prompting fan speculation about behind-the-scenes issues.[37] Ferreira addressed the diminished focus in interviews, noting it reflected creative choices by showrunner Sam Levinson, whom she described as writing from male perspectives that sometimes disconnected from Kat's narrative.[38] While some reviews praised her nuanced handling of Kat's internal conflicts, others critiqued the storyline for reinforcing stereotypes of fat characters as comic relief or underdeveloped sidekicks, limiting broader exploration of her agency.[39] Ferreira's portrayal earned acclaim for its unfiltered depiction of plus-size femininity amid the show's themes of addiction and identity, contributing to Euphoria's recognition for diverse body representation, though her arc's truncation drew mixed responses on narrative payoff.[36][40] On August 24, 2022, Ferreira announced she would not return for season 3, describing the exit as a mutual decision after feeling her character had "overstayed its welcome" and risked reduction to a "fat best friend" trope.[41] She denied rumors of walking off set or feuding with Levinson, emphasizing a desire for narrative evolution over prolonged sidelining, while acknowledging tensions in how Kat's story was handled in season 2.[42][38] This departure followed reports of creative differences, including limited promotion for the season, but Ferreira later clarified it allowed her to pursue roles avoiding typecasting.[43][44]

Post-Euphoria acting roles

Following her departure from Euphoria in August 2022, Ferreira transitioned toward independent cinema and theater, taking lead and supporting roles in genre-blending films and a Broadway production.[45] In 2024, she co-starred in the supernatural horror film House of Spoils, directed by Bridget Savage Cole and Danielle Krudy, portraying a line cook at a haunted estate restaurant alongside Ariana DeBose as the head chef; the film, produced by Blumhouse and Amazon MGM Studios, premiered on Prime Video on October 25, 2024.[46] Ferreira starred as Lily Trevino, a lonely caregiver who forms an unlikely online friendship with an elderly widower, in the comedy-drama Bob Trevino Likes It, written and directed by Tracie Laymon; the film, which explores themes of isolation and connection via social media, premiered at South by Southwest in March 2024 before a limited theatrical release on March 21, 2025, and earned a 95% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from critics praising her "humane" performance opposite John Leguizamo.[47][48] In November 2024, Ferreira made her Broadway debut as Loren Montgomery in Leslye Headland's Cult of Love at the Hayes Theater, depicting a dysfunctional family reunion tied to a religious cult; the play, which ran through early 2025, featured co-stars Shailene Woodley and Zachary Quinto and received mixed reviews for its dark comedy, with Ferreira's portrayal noted for adding emotional depth to the ensemble.[49][50] Later in 2025, she led the indie romantic comedy Mile End Kicks, directed by Chandler Levack, as Grace Pine, a 24-year-old music critic navigating romance and ambition in Montreal's 2011 indie scene; the film world-premiered at the Toronto International Film Festival on September 5, 2025, garnering positive early reception for Ferreira's "lived-in" lead turn and a 100% Rotten Tomatoes score from initial reviews.[45][51][52]

Public views and advocacy

Stance on body image labeling

In 2016, Ferreira indicated comfort with the "plus-size" label, stating she was "not offended by it whatsoever because I donâ€™t feel like being bigger is anything wrong," viewing it as a step toward normalizing diverse body representations in modeling.[53] By July 2020, her perspective had shifted toward rejecting such categorizations, arguing in a British Vogue interview that terms like "plus-size" create an artificial divide that "others" bodies differing from industry standards and fails to advance normalization.[54] She described persistent questioning about body positivity as "redundant," emphasizing that it distracts from treating varied sizes as standard rather than exceptional.[54] Ferreira advocated eliminating distinctions between "plus-size" and "straight-size" models, proposing they be referred to simply as "models" to dismantle outdated beauty hierarchies.[55] While acknowledging the label's early utility in fostering communityâ€”"At the beginning, it was a good way for me to identify a community"â€”she later prioritized broader disruption, stating, "now Iâ€™m kind of more like, â€˜Letâ€™s just shake this whole thing up.â€™"[54] This evolution reflects her aim to prioritize normalization over segmented advocacy, critiquing how labels perpetuate rather than resolve industry biases toward slim ideals.[55]

Rejection of activism labels

In a July 2020 interview, Barbie Ferreira described being frequently questioned about body positivity solely due to her body size as "redundant," arguing that such persistent focus hinders normalization of diverse physiques in fashion and media.[54] She rejected categorizing models as "plus-size" versus "straight-size," stating that these labels perpetuate "othering" of larger bodies and advocated instead for simply referring to all as "models" to foster integration.[55] Although she initially embraced the projection of herself as a body-positive activist during her teenage years, Ferreira later sought to broaden her influence beyond this narrow framing, emphasizing that "all aspects of her career and platform can be used as a means of making change."[56] Ferreira expressed that vocal advocacy, such as "screaming from the mountaintops" about body positivity, proved ineffective for her personally, preferring instead to "quietly do it" through professional actions like collaborations on inclusive products.[54] By 2020, she declared having "said all I have to say" in prior videos and articles on the subject, signaling a deliberate shift away from the activist label toward multifaceted contributions that avoid reductive pigeonholing.[56] This stance aligns with her broader critique of industry practices that isolate rather than mainstream varied body representations.[55]

Controversies and criticisms

Departure from Euphoria

Barbie Ferreira announced her departure from Euphoria on August 24, 2022, stating via Instagram that after four years on the series, she was saying a "very teary eyed goodbye" to her character Kat Hernandez, describing the decision as difficult but necessary for her growth.[57] She would not return for the third season, having appeared in the first two seasons of the HBO series.[58] In subsequent interviews, Ferreira explained that her exit stemmed from creative differences with showrunner Sam Levinson, particularly regarding the direction of Kat's storyline, which she felt had stalled and risked reducing the character to "the fat best friend" trope.[38] She noted that Levinson, a male writer, struggled to relate to or develop Kat's arc effectively, leading her to question whether she had "overstayed my welcome" after Kat's role diminished in season 2.[59] Ferreira emphasized that the decision was mutual and professional, denying rumors of feuds or walking off set, and clarified in 2023 and again in March 2025 that no such dramatic incidents occurred, attributing any misconceptions to a single ankle injury requiring medical attention.[60][42] The departure fueled speculation about behind-the-scenes tensions, including limited promotion for season 2 and Kat's sidelined narrative, but Ferreira maintained that her primary motivation was to avoid typecasting and pursue diverse roles, viewing the exit as beneficial for both herself and the production.[58][44]

Debates on body positivity influence

Barbie Ferreira emerged as a prominent figure in the body positivity movement during her modeling career in the mid-2010s, with outlets like Cosmopolitan describing her as a "front woman" for the cause due to her visibility as a plus-size model challenging traditional beauty standards.[61] Her influence was highlighted in 2016 when Time named her one of the "most influential teens," crediting her advocacy for reshaping perceptions of body image in fashion and media.[61] Supporters argued that her unapologetic presence in campaigns for brands like Aerie and Forever 21 normalized larger bodies, potentially reducing stigma for individuals facing similar body image struggles, as Ferreira herself recounted starting dieting in kindergarten amid early insecurities.[62] Critics of the broader body positivity movement, however, have debated Ferreira's role in promoting what some describe as unrealistic or health-detrimental ideals, particularly as her public image emphasized self-acceptance without consistent emphasis on fitness or nutrition. By 2020, Ferreira herself expressed disillusionment, stating in British Vogue that constant questions about body positivity tied to her size were "redundant" and hindered normalization rather than advancing it, signaling a shift away from being pigeonholed as an activist.[54] She elaborated in Nylon that "screaming from the mountaintops as a teenager about body positivity didn't necessarily work," preferring to move beyond the label to avoid reductive framing.[56] Ferreira's 2022 comments further fueled debates, as she critiqued "backhanded compliments" praising her for wearing form-fitting clothes like crop topsâ€”items not inherently radical for anyoneâ€”while decrying the pressure to embody perpetual self-love in a "toxic positivity space" that she said harmed her mental health, drawing from personal experience mirrored in her Euphoria character's arc.[63][64] This self-reflection contrasted with earlier advocacy, prompting discussions on whether body positivity inadvertently fosters denial of health risks associated with obesity, a concern echoed in critiques of the movement's evolution toward what some observers called performative acceptance over practical wellness. Her reported weight loss in 2024 intensified scrutiny, with viral photos sparking accusations of hypocrisy from former supporters who viewed it as contradicting her prior messaging; some speculated use of semaglutide drugs like Ozempic, while others praised the change as prioritizing health over ideology.[65] Mainstream media coverage, often aligned with body positivity narratives, framed the backlash as fatphobia, but Ferreira's own history of dieting and critique of toxicity suggested a more nuanced personal reckoning with body image pressures, underscoring debates on whether celebrity influence in such movements prioritizes empowerment or evasion of empirical health data linking sustained higher weights to conditions like diabetes and cardiovascular disease.[63][64]

Television

Ferreira first appeared on television in the HBO series Divorce, playing the role of Ella across two episodes aired in 2017.[4] Her breakthrough came with the recurring lead role of Katherine "Kat" Hernandez in HBO's Euphoria, where she appeared in all eight episodes of season 1 (2019) and all eight episodes of season 2 (2022), portraying a high school student exploring her sexuality and self-image.[70] In 2022, she guest-starred as Willow, a suspect in a murder mystery, in the episode "Danner" (season 1, episode 6) of Apple TV+'s The Afterparty. That same year, Ferreira provided voice acting as Cathy Andrews in the Robot Chicken episode "May Cause Involuntary Political Discharge" (season 11, episode 4), a sketch comedy segment featuring animated parodies.[71]

Music videos and other media

Ferreira appeared as a plus-size model in the music video for Carly Rae Jepsen's "Boy Problems," released on April 8, 2016.[72] She featured in a cameo role in Gus Dapperton's "Prune, You Talk Funny" music video, released on December 5, 2017.[73] In 2018, Ferreira made her directorial debut with the music video for Dounia's "So Cool," which she helmed as her first official produced project in the medium.[74][75]
