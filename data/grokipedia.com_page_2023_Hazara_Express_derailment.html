2023 Hazara Express derailment

The 2023 Hazara Express derailment occurred on 6 August 2023 near Nawabshah in Sindh province, Pakistan, when ten carriages of the Hazara Express—a passenger train traveling from Karachi to Rawalpindi with around 950 passengers—derailed, killing at least 30 people and injuring more than 90 others.[1][2] The incident highlighted longstanding deficiencies in Pakistan's railway infrastructure, as a preliminary investigation attributed the derailment to missing fishplates—metal plates used to connect adjacent rails—and pre-existing track damage that allowed excessive rail movement under the train's weight.[3][4] Rescue operations involved local authorities and military personnel extracting passengers from overturned coaches amid difficult conditions, with the death toll confirmed through hospital records and official tallies from Pakistan Railways.[5] Although initial statements from Railways Minister Khawaja Saad Rafiq did not exclude sabotage, the inquiry focused on mechanical failure rather than deliberate interference, contrasting with frequent militant attacks on rail lines in other regions like Balochistan.[6] In response, several senior railway officials were suspended pending further review, though they were reinstated by September 2024 after the probe concluded without charges of negligence leading to accountability.[4] The event underscored chronic underfunding and maintenance lapses in Pakistan's rail network, which has seen multiple similar accidents due to track and signaling failures.[7]

Background

The Hazara Express and Its Route

The Hazara Express operates as a daily passenger train service under Pakistan Railways, providing connectivity from Karachi City in Sindh province to Havelian in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.[8] It primarily facilitates travel for passengers heading to northern Pakistan, including routes through Punjab province en route to Rawalpindi and beyond.[9] The train's route covers approximately 1,600 kilometers, passing through Sindh, Punjab, and northern areas with around 50 scheduled stops.[10] Notable intermediate stations include Hyderabad, Nawabshah, Rohri, Khanewal, Faisalabad, Lahore, and Rawalpindi, before ascending toward Havelian near the Hazara region.[11] The full journey typically requires 32 to 33 hours, reflecting average operational speeds constrained by the network's infrastructure.[12] Launched on February 15, 2006, the service evolved from prior routes such as the Chenab Express, which previously extended to Peshawar, to better serve the Hazara Division's connectivity needs.[13] It functions mainly with economy-class seating within Pakistan Railways' legacy system, which traces its origins to the 1861 opening of the initial Karachi-Kotri line under British colonial administration.[14]

State of Pakistan Railways Infrastructure

Pakistan Railways operates a network spanning 7,791 route kilometers, much of which consists of tracks and bridges approaching or exceeding their economic lifespan, with maximum speeds limited to 105 km/h on approximately half the system.[15] By 2024, 67% of the tracks were reported as outdated and beyond their operational lifespan, reflecting decades of deferred refurbishment amid fiscal constraints that prioritize short-term allocations over long-term capital investments.[16] This aging infrastructure, often over 50 years old in key sections, stems from chronic underfunding, where maintenance budgets are insufficient to address deterioration from wear, environmental exposure, and inadequate inspections, leading to systemic vulnerabilities such as weakened joints and ballast erosion.[17] Budget shortfalls exacerbate these issues, with Pakistan Railways frequently postponing essential repairs due to allocations that fail to cover operational losses, resulting in a reliance on ad-hoc funding rather than sustained investment.[17] For instance, annual maintenance expenditures have historically lagged behind needs, contributing to a pattern where state-owned monopoly operations prioritize revenue-generating activities over preventive upkeep, unlike privatized systems elsewhere that incentivize efficiency through market competition and accountability. This structural inefficiency manifests in operational decay, where fiscal priorities divert resources from infrastructure renewal, perpetuating a cycle of reactive fixes over proactive renewal. In Sindh province, including areas near Nawabshah, tracks have long exhibited vulnerabilities to erosion and submersion from seasonal flooding and poor drainage, compounded by aging alignments in flood-prone alluvial plains that amplify risks from loose fish-plated joints and substandard sub-grade stability documented in prior engineering assessments.[18] These conditions, rooted in under-maintained embankments and delayed ballast renewal, heighten susceptibility to track failures during monsoons, as evidenced by recurring reports of weakened sections predating major incidents. The prevalence of derailments underscores the infrastructure's frailty: between 2020 and 2023, Pakistan Railways recorded 145 accidents in 2020, 87 in 2021, 62 in 2022, and 84 in 2023, many attributable to track defects, signaling failures, and unsecured alignments rather than isolated anomalies.[19] Such frequency arises from causal factors like insufficient track monitoring and resource scarcity in a monopolistic framework, where incentives for rigorous maintenance are diluted absent competitive pressures or performance-based funding.

The Incident

Timeline of the Derailment

The Hazara Express departed from Karachi City station on its routine daily journey to Havelian, carrying passengers along the main railway line through Sindh province.[20][21] On August 6, 2023, in the early afternoon local time, the train approached the section near Sarhari railway station in Nawabshah district, Sindh, traveling at an estimated speed of 45 to 50 km/h.[1][22][5] At that point, 10 of the train's approximately 16 coaches suddenly derailed from the tracks and overturned.[23][24] The derailed coaches subsequently piled up beside the line, with the train coming to a complete stop within seconds to minutes as the unaffected portions halted.[3]

Mechanical and Track Conditions at the Time

Initial site assessments at the derailment location near Sarhari railway station revealed that two fishplates, essential for securing rail joints, were missing from the track section where the Hazara Express derailed on August 6, 2023.[25] Additionally, the rails in that vicinity showed signs of damage, including a broken segment that contributed to the instability under the train's load.[25] These track deficiencies were confirmed through immediate post-derailment inspections by railway personnel, highlighting localized maintenance lapses at the precise site of the incident.[3] Examination of the locomotive following the derailment indicated that two wheels on the engine were jammed prior to departure, with railway staff having applied lubricants as a temporary measure rather than withdrawing the unit from service.[26] This pre-existing mechanical issue on the engine, which powered the train from Karachi, persisted undetected or unaddressed adequately during transit, leading to observed damage after the event.[27] No comprehensive pre-departure mechanical checks were documented as having flagged or resolved this wheel malfunction effectively.[28] The train carried approximately 950 passengers, significantly exceeding its standard capacity, which exacerbated stresses on the mechanical and track systems during the journey.[1] Overcrowding reports from survivors and officials underscored that the passenger load strained the locomotive's performance, though no formal capacity enforcement or load assessments were noted immediately before the derailment.[29] Weather conditions at the time were typical for Sindh province in early August, with no reports of rain, fog, or other adverse environmental factors influencing the derailment; accounts focused solely on track and mechanical elements.[5] This absence of external weather disruptions pointed to operational and infrastructural conditions as the primary observables tied to the event.[24]

Casualties and Immediate Response

Death Toll and Injuries

The derailment of the Hazara Express on August 6, 2023, resulted in 30 confirmed deaths, as reported by Pakistan Railways officials and corroborated by provincial authorities in Sindh.[24][30] Initial counts from the scene varied between 19 and 25 fatalities due to the chaotic conditions and ongoing rescue efforts, but hospital records and official tallies stabilized at 30 by the following day.[31][32] Injuries numbered over 90, with Pakistan Railways citing 67 hospitalized cases and additional estimates from local officials reaching 100, primarily from blunt trauma, fractures, and crush injuries sustained during the derailment of multiple carriages.[29][2] The victims were predominantly civilian passengers, including families traveling from Karachi to Rawalpindi, reflecting the train's routine service as a key long-distance route often operating near or above capacity.[5][33] Verification of the toll faced delays from the remote location near Sarhari station and nighttime occurrence, with underreporting in early hours attributed to inaccessible wreckage and incomplete passenger manifests; subsequent cross-checks via medical facilities in Nawabshah and Hyderabad confirmed the figures without significant upward revisions.[1] No official breakdown by injury severity was released, though reports indicated a subset required intensive care for complications like internal bleeding.[34]

Rescue and Medical Efforts

Rescue operations commenced shortly after the derailment at approximately 1:18 p.m. local time on August 6, 2023, involving local police, Pakistan Railways personnel, Rescue 1122 teams, Rangers, and the Pakistan Army, which joined efforts soon thereafter to extract passengers from the wreckage.[21][35][36] Rescue workers freed trapped individuals from the derailed and overturned coaches using available equipment and manual methods, with operations continuing for several hours until completion by evening of the same day.[37][38] Access to victims was hindered by the inversion of multiple carriages, complicating extrication and contributing to the intensity of on-site efforts amid the rural location near Sahara railway station.[5][38] Over 90 injured passengers were transported via ambulances to the People's Medical College Hospital in Nawabshah, where medical facilities declared an emergency to manage severe trauma cases, though staff reported challenges in handling the influx.[39][40] Some critically wounded individuals required subsequent transfers to advanced care centers in nearby cities such as Hyderabad, reflecting limitations in local capacity for complex injuries.[30] The rapid mobilization limited further casualties during extraction, with the site cleared within hours rather than days, though hospital overload underscored gaps in immediate triage efficacy; no official metrics on survival attribution to response timing were released, but the final injury count of around 100 indicated that prompt evacuation prevented a higher toll from secondary complications.[30][37][21]

Investigation

Preliminary Inquiry Findings

A six-member inquiry team headed by Federal Government Inspector of Railways Ali Mohammad Afridi submitted a preliminary report on August 8, 2023, following the derailment of the Hazara Express near Sarhari in Sindh province.[25] The departmental probe, conducted by Pakistan Railways, examined the site and involved joint input from relevant engineering and mechanical branches.[27] Its scope centered on immediate physical evidence from the tracks, locomotive, and derailed coaches, excluding broader external factors such as terrorism or sabotage despite some dissenting observations noting the possibility.[25] The report identified missing fishplates and a broken rail as the primary causes of the derailment, attributing these to track defects that led to gauge spread and instability under the train's weight.[25] [27] Secondary factors included skidding of the locomotive due to "little hitting spots" on remaining fishplates and potential axle issues, though these were deemed contributory rather than initiating.[25] Departmental oversight in maintenance was cited as enabling the defects, with the engineering branch held responsible for track conditions and the mechanical branch for locomotive performance.[25] [27] No consensus was reached on a single "prime reason" among team members, as noted in initial assessments, but the findings emphasized mechanical and infrastructural failures over operator error or external interference.[41] The report's release prompted suspensions of track maintenance staff, reflecting its attribution of negligence to specific oversight lapses.[25]

Identified Causes and Contributing Factors

The preliminary investigation determined that the derailment resulted primarily from missing fishplates at the rail joint near kilometer 266, which compromised the connection between rail segments and led to a broken track. Fishplates, essential metal splices that bolt rails together to maintain alignment and absorb vibrational stresses, were absent, allowing progressive loosening and eventual separation under dynamic loads from passing trains. This created a localized track defect, including potential widening of the gauge or vertical misalignment, which disrupted the wheel-rail interface.[3][25] At the time of the incident, the Hazara Express was traveling at approximately 45-50 km/h, a speed sufficient to generate lateral forces that, combined with the track discontinuity, caused the leading wheel flanges to climb the rails—a derailment mode driven by coning geometry failure and insufficient track restraint. Empirical rail engineering principles indicate that even modest speeds amplify such defects, as wheelset hunting or flange contact angles exceed stability thresholds when joint integrity fails, leading to progressive unloading and overturning of subsequent coaches. The damaged track section, evidenced by deep fling marks and gauge spread, further contributed by reducing lateral resistance.[1][42][25] A concurrent contributing factor was a mechanical fault in the locomotive's axle and wheels, detected earlier at Kotri station but addressed only through temporary greasing rather than engine replacement, which likely induced excessive dragging forces and exacerbated the rail joint stress. The probe team's majority report emphasized this interplay of uninspected joint hardware and locomotive sway, while dissenting views prioritized the axle jam as the initiator, citing resultant marks on residual fishplates; however, track evidence predominated in the consensus. Inadequate pre-departure checks on signaling and joint bolts, which typically prevent vibration-induced loosening in high-traffic corridors, permitted the causal chain to unfold. Initial ministerial speculation of sabotage lacked substantiation in technical findings, which instead highlighted verifiable maintenance oversights over deliberate interference.[43][44][41]

Systemic Issues and Criticisms

Historical Patterns of Neglect in Pakistan Railways

Pakistan Railways has experienced a persistent pattern of infrastructure-related failures, evidenced by recurring derailments attributable to track deficiencies. In the 2021 Ghotki incident, the derailment of the Millat Express was caused by a broken rail joint, resulting in a subsequent collision that killed at least 65 people and injured over 150, highlighting vulnerabilities in track integrity. Similarly, 2019 saw a surge in accidents, with over 100 incidents reported, many linked to inadequate maintenance of rails and signaling systems, as officials attributed the rise to superficial repairs rather than systemic upgrades. These events underscore a broader trend where deferred upkeep of aging infrastructure—much of which dates to colonial-era construction—has repeatedly compromised operational safety.[45][46] Statistical data reinforces this pattern of neglect, with Pakistan Railways recording 757 accidents between 2012 and 2017, averaging approximately 125 incidents annually, predominantly involving derailments and collisions tied to mechanical and track faults. More recent figures indicate 537 accidents from 2020 to 2025, averaging over 100 per year, with a significant portion stemming from outdated infrastructure and insufficient preventive measures. Over 120 freight train derailments occurred in the three years leading up to 2025, largely due to human error compounded by deteriorated tracks, illustrating a failure to address known risks through routine inspections and reinforcements.[47][48][49] Chronic underinvestment has exacerbated these issues, with maintenance allocations historically insufficient to rehabilitate extensive track networks in poor condition. Government audits, including those by the Auditor General of Pakistan, have repeatedly flagged irregularities in maintenance expenditures and the persistence of substandard infrastructure, contributing to widespread deterioration of rails and bridges. For fiscal year 2025, Rs12 billion was earmarked for track repairs, yet this remains inadequate relative to the scale of deferred needs, perpetuating predictable safety hazards from unaddressed wear and environmental stresses. Sector assessments confirm that years of underfunding have left much of the system vulnerable, prioritizing short-term operations over long-term resilience.[50][51][15]

Allegations of Corruption and Mismanagement

In the aftermath of the 2023 Hazara Express derailment, allegations surfaced that mismanagement and graft within Pakistan Railways contributed to inadequate track maintenance, with preliminary probes citing missing fishplates and damaged rails as direct causes potentially exacerbated by neglected upkeep.[52] Auditors have documented embezzlement of public funds and procurement irregularities in Pakistan Railways, including instances where parts for maintenance were not properly accounted for, leading to losses such as Rs 27.39 million in one reported case.[53] Separate audits uncovered misappropriations totaling nearly Rs 10 billion across various operations, underscoring systemic diversion of resources away from essential infrastructure repairs.[54] Political interference in appointments has been cited as a factor prioritizing loyalty over technical expertise, fostering inefficiencies in a sector prone to widespread corruption, with reports estimating annual losses from fare evasion and oversight failures at $3.5 million.[55] Transparency assessments highlight entrenched graft in Pakistan Railways, including misappropriations nearing USD 95 million as per a 2016 internal review, often shielded by bureaucratic unaccountability.[56] While officials like former Railways Minister Khawaja Saad Rafique attributed derailment risks to budget shortfalls, evidence of fraud in accounts—such as recent irregularities causing massive losses—suggests resource allocation failures stem more from internal pilferage than external constraints.[57][58] Accountability measures post-derailment proved fleeting: six officials, including senior track inspectors, were suspended on August 8, 2023, for negligence but reinstated on September 22, 2024, without apparent disciplinary follow-through, fueling claims of protected incompetence.[26][4] Broader probes have identified over 2,063 corruption cases involving billions of rupees in Pakistan Railways since 2020, yet penalties affected only 110 individuals, indicating lenient repercussions that perpetuate mismanagement.[59] Official responses have included denials of systemic embezzlement, but recurring audit findings of unaddressed irregularities contradict such assertions, pointing to a pattern where political cover-ups hinder reforms.[60][61]

Government and Institutional Response

Official Actions and Accountability Measures

Following the derailment on August 6, 2023, Pakistan Railways suspended six officials on August 8, 2023, under Rule-5 of the Civil Servants (Efficiency and Discipline) Rules, 2020, citing negligence in maintenance that led to missing fishplates, damaged tracks, and locomotive issues.[26] The suspended personnel included the Divisional Executive Engineer (Sukkur), Diesel Workshop Manager (Karachi), Assistant Executive Engineer (Nawabshah), Permanent Way Officer (Shahdadpur), Power Controller (Kotri), and a Keyman, with a six-member probe team and the Federal Government Inspector of Railways (grade 22) tasked to investigate further.[26] The preliminary departmental inquiry attributed the incident to failures in the engineering and mechanical branches, including absent track connectors and rail flaws, though sabotage was not excluded as a factor.[26] No criminal First Information Reports (FIRs) under negligence statutes were publicly documented with resolved outcomes by October 2025, limiting accountability to internal disciplinary proceedings.[26] Despite the inquiry's findings, all six suspended officials were reinstated effective September 22, 2024, without assignment to train operations, indicating a reversal of initial punitive measures absent further disclosed disciplinary actions or reforms tied directly to the probe's recommendations.[4] Broader pledges for track rehabilitation, such as Rs12 billion allocated in the 2025-26 fiscal year for repairs and signaling upgrades, were announced by the government but showed no verifiable implementation specific to the Nawabshah site's vulnerabilities by late 2025, with overall derailment reductions attributed to unrelated ML-1 corridor works rather than targeted post-incident interventions.[62]

Compensation and Long-Term Reforms

The Pakistani federal government announced ex-gratia payments of PKR 1.5 million to the families of each deceased victim from the derailment.[63] Pakistan Railways subsequently increased this amount to PKR 2.5 million per fatality.[64] The Sindh provincial government provided supplementary aid of PKR 1 million per deceased relative and PKR 200,000 to 500,000 for injured passengers, depending on injury severity, alongside promises of comprehensive medical support.[65] No public records confirm full disbursement rates or timelines for these funds as of late 2023, amid broader critiques of delayed victim relief in Pakistani rail incidents. Long-term reforms following the derailment included initial suspensions of six railway officials, including senior grades, for alleged negligence, though all were reinstated by September 2024 without further disciplinary action detailed.[4] Announcements emphasized track repairs near Nawabshah and signaling system upgrades, echoing unfulfilled commitments from prior accidents like the 2021 Ghotki derailment, where promised infrastructure investments stalled due to chronic underfunding—Pakistan Railways sought a PKR 35 billion bailout in October 2023 for basic maintenance alone.[66] By 2025, general safety directives mandated pre-departure braking inspections and fitness certifications for all rolling stock, but these applied network-wide rather than targeting the incident site specifically.[67] Implementation gaps persist, with no measurable reductions in derailment rates post-2023; multiple incidents occurred in 2025, including overspeed-related derailments in Punjab and Sindh attributed to aging tracks and inadequate monitoring.[68] A 2024 risk assessment of the network highlighted high vulnerability from decrepit infrastructure but recommended enhanced monitoring without evidence of executed federal follow-through tied to the Hazara event.[69] These patterns underscore systemic barriers to reform, including fiscal constraints and mismanagement, rendering announced upgrades more rhetorical than operational.[70]

Impact and Legacy

Broader Implications for Public Safety

The 2023 Hazara Express derailment exemplified the heightened public safety risks posed by chronic infrastructure deficiencies in Pakistan Railways, where track failures directly precipitated the loss of 30 lives and injuries to over 90 passengers. Missing fishplates and damaged rails, identified as primary causes, underscore how lapses in routine inspections amplify derailment probabilities, contributing to a national rail system plagued by frequent accidents that erode passenger confidence and elevate fatality exposure for daily commuters.[71][24] This event intensified scrutiny of systemic underinvestment, with Railways Minister Khawaja Saad Rafique attributing such failures to resource shortages, revealing causal links between fiscal neglect and safety outcomes that disrupt vital northern supply corridors reliant on uninterrupted service. Temporary halts in operations following the derailment near Sarhari station impeded goods and passenger flows to regions like Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, imposing indirect economic pressures that could manifest in higher transport alternatives' risks and costs, further straining public reliance on an unreliable network.[72][73] In policy terms, the derailment highlighted the imperative for shifting from bureaucratic delays to rigorous, data-verified maintenance regimens, such as mandatory component checks and track monitoring, to avert preventable cascades of failure observed here. Such protocols, grounded in causal analysis of component wear, offer a blueprint for mitigating recurrence, as evidenced by the incident's origins in overlooked defects rather than external factors alone, thereby prioritizing empirical prevention to safeguard public transit integrity.[27][72]

Public and Political Reactions

The derailment elicited widespread expressions of grief from the public, with media outlets emphasizing systemic negligence and corruption within Pakistan Railways as contributing factors to the tragedy. Reports highlighted how the incident underscored bureaucratic inefficiencies, with one analysis describing the railway system as plagued by "corruption and negligence" that prioritized political agendas over passenger safety.[61] Coverage also drew attention to the organization's financial strains, including losses exceeding Rs55 billion in the fiscal year 2022-23, amid governance issues and inadequate maintenance funding.[74] Politically, Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif conveyed deep sorrow over the loss of lives, stating he had expressed grief during a public address and sought a detailed report on the incident.[75] Railways Minister Khawaja Saad Rafique attributed the derailment primarily to departmental failures, including a damaged track section and locomotive wheel issues, while noting resource shortages as a mitigating factor in maintenance lapses; he did not rule out sabotage but stressed mechanical and oversight deficiencies over deliberate acts.[76][27] No major opposition-led demands for systemic overhaul were prominently reported, though the event amplified critiques of long-term underinvestment in rail infrastructure despite fiscal challenges. International responses remained limited, with minimal official condolences noted beyond domestic coverage.[6]
