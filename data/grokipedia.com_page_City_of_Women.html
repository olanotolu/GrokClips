City of Women

![Illustration from The Book of the City of Ladies][float-right] The City of Women, also known as The Book of the City of Ladies (French: Le Livre de la cité des dames), is an allegorical treatise composed by Christine de Pizan around 1405 as a defense of women's intellectual and moral capacities against misogynistic literary traditions prevalent in medieval Europe.[1] In the work, de Pizan, guided by allegorical figures of Reason, Rectitude, and Justice, constructs a metaphorical city populated by exemplary women drawn from history, mythology, and scripture, including figures such as Sappho, Zenobia, and the Virgin Mary, to refute claims of female inferiority articulated in texts like Jean de Meun's continuation of The Romance of the Rose.[2] This composition marks one of the earliest known systematic arguments for women's worth on their own terms, relying on empirical examples of female virtue and achievement rather than abstract philosophy alone.[1] Christine de Pizan, an Italian-born French author who became Europe's first professional female writer after her husband's death in 1380, drew from Boccaccio's De mulieribus claris for biographical material but innovated by emphasizing women's agency and rejecting deterministic views of gender.[1] The book's structure—divided into three parts corresponding to the virtues guiding the construction—culminates in a fortified city symbolizing women's collective strength and refuge from slander, underscoring causal links between historical female accomplishments and innate human potential unhindered by sex.[2] Its significance lies in challenging the scholarly consensus of the Querelle des Femmes debate, with de Pizan's firsthand experience as a widowed mother supporting her family's livelihood through writing lending authenticity to her advocacy.[1] While modern academic interpretations often frame the text through contemporary lenses, primary analysis reveals de Pizan's commitment to Christian orthodoxy and empirical exemplars over ideological abstraction, distinguishing it from later developments in gender discourse.[1] The work's enduring influence stems from its role in preserving narratives of female resilience amid institutional biases favoring male-authored histories, though its reception in the 15th century was limited by the era's patronage networks dominated by clerical and aristocratic men.[2]

Production

Development and Pre-Production

The screenplay for City of Women (La città delle donne) was co-written by director Federico Fellini, Bernardino Zapponi, and Brunello Rondi, building on an initial sketch discovered by Zapponi that outlined a man's surreal immersion in a feminine world.[3] [4] Fellini conceived the project as a dream narrative, stating that "of all my films only The City of Women is almost entirely a dream," emphasizing its reliance on subconscious imagery and personal invention rather than literal events.[5] This approach reflected Fellini's broader method of deriving stories from psychological introspection, incorporating elements of Carl Gustav Jung's dream analysis to structure the protagonist's odyssey through invented feminine archetypes.[3] Development occurred amid Italy's intensifying feminist debates in the late 1970s, with Fellini positioning the film as a male response to perceived shifts in gender relations, portraying a man's disorientation in a landscape dominated by women's assertions of power and memory.[6] Zapponi's original story contributions, adapted from an unrealized earlier project, provided the core framework of Snaporaz's journey, while Rondi assisted in refining the script's satirical tone toward feminist conventions and collective female consciousness.[7] Fellini described the work as a "fable" exploring the mystery of women through one man's projections, avoiding didactic resolutions in favor of open-ended dream logic.[8] Pre-production focused on assembling a large ensemble to evoke the film's hallucinatory scale, with Fellini casting frequent collaborator Marcello Mastroianni as Snaporaz to embody the everyman lost in reverie, echoing his role in 8½.[9] Preparations emphasized visual and performative excess, including scouting for locations to simulate a sprawling, women-dominated cityscape, though much of the aesthetic was realized through Fellini's improvisational directing style on set rather than rigid pre-planning.[8] The process aligned with Fellini's ritualistic view of filmmaking as a collective projection, prioritizing intuitive development over conventional scripting timelines, leading into principal photography in 1979.[8]

Filming and Technical Aspects

Principal photography for City of Women took place in 1979, with the majority of filming conducted at Cinecittà Studios in Rome, where elaborate sets facilitated the production's surreal and dream-like sequences.[10][11] Limited location shooting occurred in Fregene near Fiumicino for transitional exterior scenes and in rural Lazio countryside for the initial train sequence, emphasizing the film's shift from realism to fantasy.[11] Cinematographer Giuseppe Rotunno, a frequent collaborator with Fellini, captured the film in color using the Technovision process, which enhanced the visual depth and vibrancy of the production's fantastical elements.[10] The picture was composed in a 1.85:1 aspect ratio, standard for theatrical presentation at the time, allowing for wide framing of crowded, hallucinatory crowd scenes and architectural sets.[12] Production designer Dante Ferretti oversaw the construction of massive, papier-mâché-dominated interiors depicting the titular city, blending practical effects with optical illusions to evoke a burlesque, otherworldly scale without heavy reliance on post-production alterations.[13] Rotunno's lighting techniques, informed by his prior work on Fellini's color films, employed warm gels and dynamic camera movements to underscore nostalgic and satirical tones in the constructed environments.[14] The film's runtime of approximately 140 minutes reflects the unhurried pacing enabled by studio control, minimizing on-location logistical constraints.[15]

Plot Summary

Snàporaz (Marcello Mastroianni), a middle-aged businessman and habitual womanizer, dozes on a train before becoming fixated on an alluring stranger seated opposite him, whose presence arouses him intensely.[16] [17] He follows her into the train's lavatory and then disembarks to pursue her through a pitch-black tunnel and into an enveloping forest, eventually stumbling upon a sprawling hotel overrun by hundreds of women attending a fervent feminist congress.[18] [19] Discovered cowering in a restroom stall, Snàporaz faces interrogation, ridicule, and physical manhandling by the convention attendees—a diverse array of militants, lesbians, reformed homemakers, and eccentrics such as one woman presiding over six submissive husbands of varying nationalities—who assail him with accusations of patriarchal objectification.[17] [18] Amid the chaos, he flees into hallucinatory reveries and flashbacks chronicling his formative encounters with women: a domineering nanny, lascivious schoolteachers, youthful seductions, and a montage of past lovers, all underscoring his lifelong pursuit of an elusive ideal female form.[16] [18] Snàporaz's nightmarish peregrination persists through grotesque vignettes of female supremacy, including a gymnasium teeming with women engaged in synchronized aerobics and martial arts, an elevator packed with drag queens, and a villa where erotic dalliances devolve into tribunals led by symbolic archetypes of maternal and erotic authority.[18] [16] The ordeal peaks in a derelict circus tent repurposed as a courtroom, where all-female jurors—adorned in exaggerated attire—convict him of misogynistic crimes, binding him to a roller-skating apparatus for a mock execution amid collapsing scaffolding and balloon-clutching hordes.[18] As pandemonium engulfs the scene, Snàporaz awakens abruptly on the original train, now pulling into the station beside his oblivious wife, implying the entire episode transpired as a feverish phantasmagoria sparked by his initial libidinal impulse.[18] [16] Yet, as he steps onto the platform, the enigmatic woman reappears to deliver a parting slap, and the train plunges into another tunnel, yielding to an ambiguous fade to black that blurs the boundary between reverie and reality.[18]

Cast and Characters

The principal role of Snàporaz, a middle-aged businessman who pursues a mysterious woman and finds himself immersed in a surreal feminist enclave, is portrayed by Marcello Mastroianni.[20] [21] Anna Prucnal plays Elena, Snàporaz's wife, who appears in framing sequences and confronts him about his wandering.[20] [21] Bernice Stegers depicts the unnamed woman encountered on the train, whose flirtation propels Snàporaz into the ensuing adventure.[20] [21] Jole Silvani (credited as Iole Silvani in some sources) embodies the motorcyclist who escorts Snàporaz through the woods to the city of women.[20] [21] Ettore Manni portrays Dr. Sante Katzone (sometimes rendered as Xavier in English contexts), the bombastic organizer of the women's convention central to the film's climactic sequences.[22] [20] The film employs an extensive ensemble of female characters representing various archetypes in the surreal city, including feminists, secretaries, and activists, many played by non-professional actresses and extras to evoke a dreamlike multitude; notable supporting roles include Donatella Damiani as a convention participant and Fiammetta Baralla in a minor part.[20][21]

Themes and Interpretation

Surrealism and Autobiographical Elements

Fellini's City of Women incorporates surrealistic elements through its dream-derived narrative structure, where protagonist Snàporaz navigates a labyrinthine world of exaggerated feminine archetypes and impossible architectures, such as towering statues of women and a convention hall transformed into a chaotic tribunal. These sequences, which Fellini described as stemming directly from his nocturnal visions recorded upon awakening, evoke the irrational juxtapositions and subconscious eruptions akin to surrealist manifestos, prioritizing associative logic over linear causality.[18] The film's visual lexicon—featuring grotesque amplifications like a rollercoaster ride through personal memories and hallucinatory floods of memorabilia—mirrors Fellini's broader oeuvre, where empirical reality dissolves into psychic flux, as seen in his reliance on psychoanalysis-inspired improvisation during production.[23] Autobiographical threads weave through Snàporaz's odyssey, positioning the character as a Fellini surrogate confronting midlife disorientation amid Italy's feminist upheavals of the late 1970s, including encounters echoing the director's documented reminiscences of Rimini childhoods and Roman adulteries. Fellini drew from his personal archive of dream notebooks and relational dynamics—particularly tensions between male introspection and female collectivity—to infuse the script, co-written with Bernardino Zapponi, with confessional undertones, though filtered through hyperbolic fantasy rather than literal biography.[24] This semi-autobiographical impulse, evident in the protagonist's fragmented flashbacks to lovers and maternal influences paralleling Fellini's own marital reflections with Giulietta Masina, underscores a causal realism in his filmmaking: external societal shifts, like the 1970 Italian divorce law reforms and women's liberation marches, catalytically reshaped private anxieties into public spectacle.[25] Scholarly analyses attribute the film's introspective core to Fellini's habit of transmuting lived empirical data—interviews, photographs, and ephemera—into narrative proxies, distinguishing it from purely invented surrealism.[24]

Gender Dynamics and Critique of Feminism

In City of Women, gender dynamics are depicted through the surreal odyssey of protagonist Snàporaz, who transitions from a position of male pursuit—chasing a woman on a train—to one of vulnerability amid a matriarchal society where women dominate public and private spheres.[6] Women appear as multifaceted archetypes: seductive figures, maternal authorities, and aggressive collectives, often reducing Snàporaz to an object of scrutiny or ridicule, inverting traditional power imbalances.[24] This reversal highlights male projections onto women, shaped by cultural influences like Italian Catholicism and post-fascist machismo, where men impose subconscious fantasies rather than perceiving female autonomy.[24] Empirical observations from the film's narrative, such as Snàporaz's encounters with a feminist congress featuring consciousness-raising sessions and archival slides of male dominance, underscore relational emptiness in heterosexual dynamics, with women portrayed as more perceptive yet collectively overwhelming.[6] The film critiques aspects of second-wave feminism prevalent in 1970s Italy, satirizing its institutional forms through exaggerated sequences like a women's tribunal prosecuting Snàporaz for historical "crimes" of womanizing and objectification.[26] Fellini drew from real encounters with militant feminists, portraying their movement as a cult-like force that alienates men and amplifies gender polarization, with Snàporaz fleeing a "feminazi" assembly demanding accountability for male lust.[26] [6] This reflects broader causal tensions: feminism's push for deconstructing patriarchy exposes male inadequacies but risks caricature when detached from individual nuance, as evidenced by the film's dream-logic escalation from personal desire to societal indictment.[24] Fellini incorporated consultations with feminist intellectuals during production, yet the resulting portrayal—women as both empowered and grotesque—suggests a skepticism toward ideological absolutism, prioritizing male confusion over endorsement of either sex's supremacy.[24] Federico Fellini described the work as a "nightmare or fable" viewed exclusively through a male lens, defending it against charges of bias by arguing it unmasks how men have historically veiled women's reality with their own illusions, rather than claiming objective truth about femininity.[24] He rejected reductive feminist dismissals, insisting the film's impudence and humor stemmed from honest introspection into generational male anxieties, not hostility toward women.[24] Critics, particularly in Italian feminist periodicals post-release in 1980, accused it of misogyny for objectifying women as hysterical or vengeful hordes, interpreting the satire as reinforcement of patriarchal unease rather than subversion.[26] [27] However, defenders note the film's self-critique of masculinity—Snàporaz as a buffoonish cad driven by unchecked desire—aligns with causal realism in gender relations, where mutual incomprehension arises from biological and cultural divergences, not one-sided villainy.[6] This duality underscores the film's resistance to politicized narratives, favoring ambiguous fantasy over dogmatic resolution.[24]

Release

Premiere and Distribution

La città delle donne premiered theatrically in Italy on March 28, 1980.[28] The film was screened at the 1980 Cannes Film Festival later that year, eliciting a divided response from critics who viewed it as either a bold evolution in Fellini's style or a derivative work.[29] Theatrical releases followed in other European countries, including France on May 19, 1980; the Netherlands on October 2, 1980; West Germany on October 24, 1980; and Spain on December 4, 1980.[28] As an Italian-French co-production between Opera Film Produzione and Gaumont, distribution was managed primarily by Gaumont in France and for international sales.[5][30] In the United States, the film received a limited release on April 8, 1981.[15] Subsequent home video and restoration releases have been handled by entities such as Cohen Media Group in later years.[31]

Box Office Performance

City of Women was released in Italy on March 28, 1980, opening across eighty theaters and ranking 24th in the national box office for the 1979-80 season among the top-grossing films.[32][33] In the United States, it received a limited release and earned a domestic gross of $12,516, placing it among lower-performing art-house imports of the era.[34] The film's modest commercial results contrasted with the broader international success of Fellini's earlier works like La Dolce Vita (1960), signaling a shift toward niche appeal in his later career amid rising production costs and evolving audience tastes.[35] Overall, City of Women underperformed at the box office relative to expectations for a Fellini project, contributing to perceptions of his post-Amarcord (1973) films as commercially challenging despite critical interest.[36]

Reception and Criticism

Contemporary Reviews in Europe

Upon its premiere out of competition at the 33rd Cannes Film Festival on May 19, 1980, City of Women faced significant backlash from French critics and audiences, who booed the screening and dismissed it as disjointed and self-indulgent.[37] [38] Soviet director Andrei Tarkovsky labeled the film "worthless" during the festival, reflecting broader European skepticism toward Fellini's increasingly surreal style amid the era's feminist scrutiny.[39] In Italy, where the film released theatrically in October 1980, reviews were polarized, igniting heated debates in the press over its portrayal of women and perceived antifeminism. Feminist groups and commentators accused Fellini of mocking the women's liberation movement through exaggerated congress scenes drawn from real feminist gatherings, leading to widespread polemics that framed the work as a reactionary assault on gender equality.[40] [41] Despite this, the film garnered institutional recognition, winning four Nastri d'Argento awards, including for best director and best screenplay, signaling appreciation among some Italian critics for its visual audacity and autobiographical introspection.[42] Overall, European reception highlighted a divide: condemnation from progressive circles for its satirical edge on feminism, contrasted by acclaim for Fellini's technical mastery in segments like the dreamlike feminist assembly.[43]

North American and Global Response

In North America, City of Women premiered on April 8, 1981, distributed by New Yorker Films, receiving generally positive critical attention for its visual inventiveness despite critiques of its narrative coherence and thematic handling.[17] Vincent Canby of The New York Times described it as a "spellbinding" and "dazzling visual display" blending burlesque and satire, though he found it overlong even for Fellini enthusiasts.[17] Roger Ebert awarded it 2.5 out of 4 stars, praising its "bedazzling collection of images" and "graceful and fluid celebration of pure filmmaking skill," while noting its surreal excesses.[16] Aggregate critic scores reflect this ambivalence, with Rotten Tomatoes compiling a 68% approval rating from 19 reviews, highlighting stylistic strengths amid uneven pacing.[19] Commercially, the film underperformed, grossing just $6,200 in the United States, underscoring limited audience appeal amid perceptions of self-indulgence and dated gender commentary.[19] Canadian reception mirrored U.S. patterns, with festival screenings and arthouse distribution but no significant box-office breakthrough, as the film's esoteric style alienated broader viewers.[16] Globally outside Europe and North America, distribution was sparse, with scant documented contemporary responses; releases in markets like Australia and Japan occurred via limited arthouse channels in 1981–1982, yielding niche appreciation for Fellini's spectacle but echoing North American critiques of misogynistic undertones without widespread commercial or critical impact. In Latin America and Asia, the film registered minimally, often confined to retrospectives rather than initial theatrical runs, reflecting Fellini's declining international draw post-1970s.[19]

Long-Term Scholarly Analysis

Scholarly interpretations of City of Women have evolved significantly since its 1980 release, shifting from predominantly negative assessments tied to contemporaneous feminist critiques toward more nuanced examinations of its surreal structure, autobiographical undertones, and commentary on gender ideology. Early academic responses, influenced by the film's release amid Italy's heated debates on feminism, frequently characterized it as a reactionary assault on women's movements, with some Italian feminist publications decrying its portrayal of female characters as caricatures embodying male anxieties rather than authentic social critique.[27] [44] However, this framing has been challenged by later analyses emphasizing the film's dream-logic narrative as a deliberate extension of Fellini's introspective style seen in 8½ (1963), where protagonist Snaporaz's odyssey through a matriarchal phantasmagoria serves as a psychoanalytic exploration of masculine identity fragmentation under ideological pressures, not blanket misogyny. Peter Bondanella, in a 2013 review of the film's restoration, argues that initial dismissals overlooked its layered fantasy elements, interpreting the work instead as Fellini's artisanal response to cultural shifts, wherein manual production techniques underscore themes of illusion versus reality in gender relations. This perspective aligns with broader reevaluations positioning City of Women within Fellini's late-period affirmation of imaginative transcendence, contrasting it with prior films like Casanova (1976) that critiqued historical machismo; here, Snaporaz's arc from carnal fixation to visionary epiphany symbolizes a spiritual reconciliation with the feminine as an enigmatic force, rather than adversarial other. Such readings highlight causal mechanisms in Fellini's oeuvre: personal dreams processed through cinematic signs, yielding a critique of extremism—feminist or otherwise—that subsumes individuality into collective dogma, evidenced by sequences lampooning ideological rallies without endorsing subjugation.[45] Quantitative assessments of the film's enduring impact remain limited, but archival analyses reveal its prescience in anticipating polarized gender discourses, with Snaporaz's encounters prefiguring real-world tensions between biological sex roles and constructed identities. Feminist-leaning scholarship, such as in studies of Italian cinema, often persists in viewing the film through a depoliticized lens that attributes its ambiguities to Fellini's collaboration with female consultants while downplaying his intent to satirize absolutist feminisms, a bias reflective of academia's systemic inclinations toward progressive narratives over director-stated motivations.[46] [44] Empirical close readings, however, substantiate a balanced realism: women appear as multifaceted—nurturing, domineering, illusory—mirroring Fellini's documented observations of 1970s Italy, where radical groups like Rivolta Femminile exerted cultural dominance, prompting his hyperbolic inversion to expose totalitarian risks inherent in any monomaniacal ideology. Over four decades, this has cemented the film's status in film theory as a case study in auteurial resistance to epochal pressures, with restorations since 2001 facilitating renewed empirical scrutiny of its visual rhetoric.

Controversies

Accusations of Misogyny

Upon its release in Italy on April 16, 1980, City of Women provoked significant controversy among feminist critics, who accused Federico Fellini of misogyny for caricaturing the women's movement as an aggressive, monolithic force hostile to men.[47] Italian feminists contended that scenes depicting a chaotic women's congress—where protagonist Snaporaz faces verbal and physical assault amid slogans and exhibits decrying male dominance—delivered a "false, fallacious, sexist image" of women and contemporary feminists, reducing complex gender dynamics to ridicule.[47] Critics argued the film perpetuated sexist stereotypes by portraying women primarily as erotic fantasies or vengeful aggressors, with men positioned as bewildered victims navigating an overwhelming "city" of female power, thereby dismissing legitimate feminist grievances in favor of male anxiety.[47][26] This interpretation framed Snaporaz's surreal odyssey, including his trial by a tribunal of women for patriarchal sins, as a parody of gender politics that mocked rather than engaged with feminist ideology.[26] French reviewers echoed these charges, expressing displeasure at the film's handling of women and feminists, viewing the exaggerated dynamics as repellent and self-indulgent.[24] In broader scholarly discourse, the work has been faulted for a "cartoonish portrayal of feminism," with detractors claiming it distorted the movement's positions into a "feminazi" cult-like entity, prioritizing Fellini's personal resentments over balanced representation.[48][3] Such accusations persisted in later analyses, attributing the film's gender depictions to underlying biases that undermined any purported satire on both sexes.[26]

Fellini's Defense and Intent

Federico Fellini described City of Women (1980) as a dream-like narrative centered on a single man's internal voyage of self-discovery, where the protagonist, Snaporaz (played by Marcello Mastroianni), confronts an invented, metaphorical representation of womanhood rather than literal women.[8] In a 1980 interview conducted on the film's premiere day in Rome, Fellini explained that "woman has become everything for him: sky, earth, water, landscape… it’s him, woman," emphasizing the film's autobiographical roots in his own psyche, portraying woman as "his metaphor, his obscurity, the part of himself he doesn’t know."[8] He framed the story as "a voyage seeking for something that can never be met because you are in it," underscoring themes of inevitable self-confrontation amid the era's feminist movements, which he viewed as highlighting the "autonomization of sexes" leading to a perceived decline in shared human values and intimacy.[8] Fellini positioned the film not as a political treatise on feminism but as a personal, oneiric exploration of male subjectivity, insisting it was "not a film about women but a film about man… or a film about one man."[8] He anticipated critiques by noting that feminist observers might decry the absence of "one real woman," responding that this was deliberate, as "the film is really a dream, and as in a dream everything is the dreamer."[8] Against accusations of antifeminism or misogyny, Fellini defended the work as a fable or nightmare reflecting his subjective experience, rejecting interpretations that overlooked its introspective complexity.[24] This stance aligned with his broader view of cinema as a feminine, projective ritual, where creative intuition supplanted ideological agendas.[8]

Legacy

Influence on Cinema

City of Women (1980) represents a later phase in Federico Fellini's career, characterized by heightened surrealism and introspection on gender dynamics, which extended his broader stylistic legacy rather than spawning widespread direct emulation in subsequent films. Unlike earlier masterpieces such as 8½ (1963), the film's provocative dream narrative—featuring a male protagonist overwhelmed by a fantastical matriarchal society—has primarily influenced academic discourse on auteur-driven explorations of masculinity and feminism, rather than practical cinematic techniques or plots adopted by other directors. Scholarly analyses position it as emblematic of Fellini's shift toward postmodern affirmation after periods of disillusionment, contributing to understandings of how personal reverie intersects with cultural critique in late-20th-century Italian cinema.[49][50] The film's carnivalesque exaggeration of female collectivity and grotesque elements has informed interpretations of Fellini's engagement with mass culture's magical undercurrents, influencing how surrealist motifs are read in relation to societal gender tensions. In feminist film studies, it is critiqued for a depoliticized portrayal of women's movements, yet this very tension has shaped archival and theoretical examinations of Italian cinema's negotiation with 1970s-1980s gender politics, underscoring Fellini's role in challenging neorealist boundaries through fantasy. While direct homages in global productions remain scarce, its thematic boldness resonates in broader postmodern transitions, where dream-logic critiques of patriarchy persist as a reference point for self-reflexive storytelling.[51][52][53]

Restorations and Modern Availability

A high-definition restoration of City of Women was undertaken by Gaumont, enabling Blu-ray releases such as the 2013 edition from Eureka Entertainment's Masters of Cinema series, which presented the film in its original 1.85:1 aspect ratio with improved clarity from the original negative.[54][55] This effort addressed earlier transfer issues in analog formats, though minor grain inconsistencies persisted in high-contrast scenes.[55] Subsequent digital upgrades included a 2K restoration screened theatrically in the United States starting in February 2016 at venues like Film Forum, emphasizing Fellini's surreal visual style.[56] By 2021, a 4K digital restoration—overseen in collaboration with institutions like MoMA and Luce Cinecittà—facilitated international retrospectives, enhancing color saturation and detail in the film's elaborate dream sequences.[4] In contemporary distribution, the film remains accessible via physical media, including Blu-ray editions from Kino Lorber (2016) and Cohen Media Group, which incorporate the restored elements alongside extras like documentaries on production.[57][58] Digital streaming options include subscription services such as Kanopy and Fandor (via Amazon Channel), with rental or purchase available on platforms like Prime Video, Apple TV, and YouTube as of 2025.[59][60][61] Limited theatrical revivals continue periodically at arthouse cinemas, often tied to Fellini centennials or thematic series.[31]
