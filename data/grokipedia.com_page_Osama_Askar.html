Osama Askar

Lieutenant General Osama Askar is an Egyptian Army officer who served as Chief of Staff of the Armed Forces from 27 October 2021 to 4 July 2024.[1][2] Appointed to the position by President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, Askar had previously commanded the 23rd Mechanized Infantry Division and served as Chief of Staff of the Third Field Army starting in July 2014.[3] In his role as Chief of Staff, he inspected combat training systems, oversaw command post exercises, and conducted naval drills to enhance operational readiness.[4][5][6] Askar also represented Egypt in high-level military engagements, including meetings with NATO's Director of the International Military Staff and the U.S. Central Command commander to address regional security issues.[7][8] Upon replacement by Lieutenant General Ahmed Fathy Khalifa, Askar transitioned to the role of presidential adviser for military affairs.[2]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Background

Osama Askar, full name Osama Ahmed Rushdy Abdallah Askar, was born in 1957 in Dakahlia Governorate, a Nile Delta province known for its agricultural economy and dense population.[1][9] Specific details on his family background or pre-military upbringing remain undocumented in available records, reflecting the typically opaque personal histories of Egyptian military officers.[3] Early indications of Askar's career trajectory point directly to military service, with no public evidence of civilian professions or education prior to academy entry. He enrolled in the Egyptian Military Academy, graduating from its 70th class, which positioned him for initial infantry roles upon commissioning.[1] This pathway aligns with standard recruitment for Egypt's officer corps, emphasizing discipline and national service from a young age.[10]

Military Training

Osama Askar received his initial military training at the Egyptian Military Academy in Cairo, graduating in 1976 as part of the infantry branch.[3] This foundational education equipped him with core skills in tactics, leadership, and infantry operations, standard for officer cadets entering the Egyptian Armed Forces.[1] Following his commissioning, Askar pursued advanced military education, earning a Master's degree in Military Sciences.[1] He also completed fellowship training at the Nasser Higher Military Academy, Egypt's premier institution for senior officer development, focusing on strategic studies and command principles.[1] These programs emphasized operational planning, combined arms tactics, and higher-level decision-making, preparing graduates for divisional and field army roles.

Military Career Progression

Initial Infantry Roles

Osama Roshdi Askar commenced his military career in the infantry branch of the Egyptian Armed Forces following his graduation from the Egyptian Military Academy in Cairo in 1976.[3] He progressed through various positions within the infantry ranks, gaining experience in foundational infantry operations and command structures typical for junior officers in the Egyptian Army.[3] [10] During his early tenure, Askar assumed multiple leading roles in the army's infantry divisions, which involved tactical training, unit leadership, and operational readiness in conventional infantry warfare.[10] These assignments laid the groundwork for his subsequent advancements, emphasizing discipline, marksmanship, and small-unit tactics as core competencies in Egypt's ground forces.[9] His infantry background aligned with the Egyptian Army's emphasis on mechanized and combined arms capabilities, though initial roles focused primarily on dismounted infantry elements.[11]

Brigade and Divisional Commands

During his mid-career progression in the Egyptian Army's infantry branch, Osama Askar commanded a mechanized infantry brigade, overseeing combined arms operations and training in armored and foot-mobile tactics.[10] This role involved leadership positions within infantry divisions, emphasizing tactical coordination and unit readiness for defensive and rapid response missions.[10] Askar later assumed command of the 23rd Mechanized Infantry Division, a key formation within the Third Field Army tasked with securing the eastern Suez Canal zone and South Sinai governorate.[3] The division, equipped with armored vehicles and artillery, focused on border defense, counter-infiltration operations, and integration with air and naval assets for regional stability.[3] This command preceded his elevation to staff roles in the Third Army, building expertise in large-scale mechanized maneuvers and logistical sustainment in arid terrains.[3]

Third Field Army Leadership

Osama Askar previously served as Chief of Staff of the Third Field Army, succeeding his command of the 23rd Mechanized Infantry Division.[3] In August 2012, he was promoted to commander of the Third Field Army, headquartered in Suez and tasked with defending Egypt's eastern sector, including the Sinai Peninsula and areas east of the Suez Canal.[3] During his tenure, the Third Field Army addressed security challenges amid post-2011 political turmoil, including labor strikes, riots, and rising militancy in Sinai during late 2012 and early 2013.[3] Askar directed counter-terrorism operations, infrastructure protection, and community outreach initiatives to stabilize the region, with Egyptian military statements asserting that unrest had subsided by approximately 90% in Sinai by April 2014.[3] His forces also secured polling stations nationwide during the January 2014 constitutional referendum.[3] In March 2014, Askar received promotion to lieutenant general in recognition of his service, retaining command of the Third Field Army.[12] His leadership emphasized integrated infantry and mechanized operations to counter insurgent threats, though independent verification of stability claims remains limited due to restricted access in Sinai.[3] Askar's command concluded in early 2015, when Major General Mohamed Abdulla was appointed as his successor on February 1, coinciding with Askar's reassignment to lead the newly formed Unified Command for the Area East of the Canal and Counterterrorism operations.[13]

Appointment as Chief of Staff

Selection and Promotion

Lieutenant General Osama Ahmed Rushdi Askar was appointed Chief of Staff of the Egyptian Armed Forces on October 27, 2021, by presidential decree issued by President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi.[14][15] This position serves as the second-highest military role, directly under the Minister of National Defense, overseeing operational command and staff functions across the armed forces.[1] Askar's selection was preceded by his appointment as Chief of Operations for the Egyptian Armed Forces in late 2019, following his command of the Third Field Army, which is responsible for operations in the Sinai Peninsula and eastern regions.[16] His experience in these roles, particularly in counter-terrorism efforts, positioned him as a candidate aligned with the military's strategic priorities under el-Sisi's leadership.[1] The appointment replaced the previous Chief of Staff, Lieutenant General Tamer Mahmoud Radi, reflecting routine leadership transitions within Egypt's military hierarchy.[17] While Askar had been promoted to the rank of Lieutenant General in January 2015 by el-Sisi during his tenure as commander of forces east of the Suez Canal, the 2021 elevation to Chief of Staff represented a functional advancement without a change in rank, emphasizing his operational expertise and loyalty to the presidential administration.[1] Egyptian military promotions and appointments at this level are executed via executive authority, with the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces providing advisory input, though final decisions rest with the president.[18]

Key Responsibilities

Lieutenant General Osama Askar's appointment as Chief of Staff on 27 October 2021 positioned him as the deputy to the Commander-in-Chief of the Egyptian Armed Forces, with authority over operational planning, execution, and coordination across the army, navy, air force, and air defense branches.[19] His core duties involved directing military doctrine development, resource allocation for units, and ensuring inter-service integration to maintain national defense capabilities.[1] This role required him to advise President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, as Supreme Commander, on strategic military matters while implementing directives from the Ministry of National Defense.[16] A primary responsibility was overseeing combat readiness through rigorous inspections and training oversight, including evaluations of specialized units like the Thunderbolt Forces' modern systems on 8 February 2024 and naval exercises on 24 May 2024.[20] [6] Askar focused on enhancing personnel proficiency in individual fighter tactics and field army operations, such as reviewing Second Field Army tasks on 22 April 2024 to affirm commitment to diverse missions.[5] These activities underscored his mandate to sustain high operational tempo amid ongoing regional threats. Askar also held accountability for counter-terrorism leadership, particularly sustaining stability in the Sinai Peninsula via unified commands east of the Suez Canal, where he had previously commanded forces.[21] On 17 November 2021, he publicly commended the armed forces' contributions to security restoration there, reflecting his duty to integrate intelligence, logistics, and tactical operations against insurgent groups.[21] Internationally, his responsibilities extended to fostering military partnerships, as evidenced by engagements on Gaza and Red Sea security with U.S. Central Command on 7 May 2024.[8]

Tenure as Chief of Staff

Operational Oversight

Lieutenant General Osama Askar exercised operational oversight as Chief of Staff by supervising training regimens, combat preparations, and field army deployments to maintain the Egyptian Armed Forces' readiness. This involved directing command post exercises, inspecting specialized units, and ensuring adherence to operational protocols across infantry, naval, and special forces elements.[22] His responsibilities extended to evaluating tactical methods for individual fighters and larger formations, with a focus on integrating modern systems into routine drills.[4] In November 2021, shortly after his appointment, Askar commended the armed forces' contributions to stabilizing the Sinai Peninsula through sustained operational efforts, highlighting the restoration of security via coordinated military actions.[21] By May 2022, he conducted an inspection of the Second Field Army's training camp in North Sinai, reviewing preparations for frontline combatants and stressing the importance of high-intensity readiness.[23] Throughout 2024, Askar's oversight intensified on specialized and joint exercises. In February, he examined advanced combat training systems employed by the Thunderbolt special forces, assessing their efficacy in simulated scenarios.[20] In May, he observed naval training activities, evaluating maritime operational capabilities.[6] June saw him witness security implementation drills for the Third Field Army, focusing on optimal procedural responses.[24] These activities underscored a doctrine prioritizing empirical validation of tactics through repeated field evaluations, though official reports from the Ministry of Defense predominantly emphasized successes without independent verification of outcomes.[25]

International Engagements

During his tenure as Chief of Staff, Lieutenant General Osama Askar engaged in several bilateral military discussions and hosted foreign counterparts to strengthen Egypt's defense partnerships, particularly with Western allies and regional actors. In July 2023, Askar met with U.S. Central Command Commander General Michael Kurilla during the latter's visit to Egypt, where they addressed shared security concerns such as border security and enhanced joint training, including preparations for the multinational Bright Star 23 exercise.[26] [27] This engagement underscored ongoing U.S.-Egypt military cooperation, which includes annual exercises and intelligence sharing amid regional instability. In September 2023, Askar hosted Admiral Rob Bauer, Chair of the NATO Military Committee, during Bauer's official visit to Egypt from September 23 to 25, focusing on NATO's Mediterranean Dialogue and prospects for deepened collaboration on counter-terrorism and maritime security.[28] The following December, Askar conducted an official visit to Italy, concluding on December 15, 2023, where he held talks with Italian military leaders on mutual interests and avenues to bolster bilateral defense ties, including potential joint operations in the Mediterranean.[29] [30] Askar's engagements extended to improving relations with Turkey, strained for over a decade due to political differences. On May 8, 2024, he met Bahraini Chief of Staff Theyab bin Sakr al-Nuaimi to review joint initiatives.[33] In June 2024, discussions with Central African Republic representatives focused on enhancing bilateral ties amid Sudan's conflict, while similar meetings with Sierra Leone addressed capacity-building.[34] [35] These efforts reflected Egypt's emphasis on pan-African security networks under Askar's oversight.

Domestic Military Exercises

During his tenure as Chief of Staff from October 2021 to July 2024, Lieutenant General Osama Askar actively oversaw domestic military exercises emphasizing tactical proficiency, strategic coordination, and unit readiness across Egypt's field armies and naval forces.[16][36] These drills, implemented as part of routine combat training plans, involved formations from the Second and Third Field Armies, as well as specialized naval and northern zone units, focusing on small-unit tactics, field skills, and security protocols to bolster national defense capabilities.[37][38] On June 27, 2022, Askar witnessed the main phase of the "Eisar 64" exercise carried out by Third Field Army formations, which simulated operational scenarios to test coordination and execution under controlled conditions.[39][40] In April 2023, he inspected a small tactics and field skills drill in the Northern Military Zone, evaluating personnel performance in practical maneuvers and commending their preparedness.[41] Later that year, on October 1, 2023, Askar attended a demonstration of minor tactics by a Second Field Army formation, highlighting advancements in combat training methodologies.[42] Into 2024, Askar continued supervising exercises amid evolving security needs. On March 5, he reviewed the combat training system of a Second Field Army unit, aligned with updated doctrinal standards for enhanced operational efficiency.[38] In May, he inspected naval training activities focused on securing vessels during missions, including protection procedures against potential threats.[43] June saw him observing a Third Field Army drill on security measure implementation and, separately, a specialized tactical command project integrating decision-making and coordination.[24] These efforts underscored a commitment to rigorous, scenario-based training to maintain the Armed Forces' high alert status.[44]

Controversies and Criticisms

Sinai Counter-Terrorism Operations

During Lieutenant General Osama Askar's tenure as Chief of Staff from October 2021 to August 2024, the Egyptian Armed Forces maintained intensive counter-terrorism efforts in the Sinai Peninsula under the framework of Operation Sinai Province, initiated in February 2018 to combat the Islamic State-affiliated Wilayat Sinai group. These operations involved coordinated ground raids, aerial strikes, and buffer zone enforcements east of the Suez Canal, building on the unified command structure Askar had previously led as its inaugural commander since January 2015. Askar personally oversaw inspections of military units and infrastructure projects in North Sinai, such as on January 14, 2023, emphasizing enhanced security measures and development to isolate militants from local populations.[45][46] Egyptian military statements under Askar's leadership highlighted operational successes, including the neutralization of militant networks and restoration of stability, with Askar publicly praising the armed forces' contributions to curbing insurgent activities and enabling reconstruction. By 2023, official reports claimed a significant reduction in large-scale attacks compared to peak years, attributing this to tribal engagements, intelligence-driven targeting, and fortified checkpoints, which had reportedly contained Wilayat Sinai to remote areas. However, independent assessments indicated a strategy of containment rather than eradication, with militants adapting through guerrilla tactics, improvised explosives, and cross-border smuggling.[47][48][49] Persistent violence underscored limitations in effectiveness; for instance, in May 2022, Wilayat Sinai claimed responsibility for ambushes killing at least 16 Egyptian troops in separate incidents near Sheikh Zuweid and Bir al-Abd. Attacks continued sporadically through 2023-2024, including roadside bombings and assaults on security convoys, with U.S. State Department reports noting ongoing threats despite Egyptian commitments to rebuilding North Sinai post-operations. Critics, including analysts from think tanks, argued that heavy militarizationâ€”deploying up to 88 battalions by 2018, sustained under subsequent leadershipâ€”failed to address root causes like local grievances and economic neglect, potentially prolonging the insurgency by alienating Bedouin tribes through perceived overreach.[50][51][52] Controversy also arose over tactical methods, with reports of reliance on unverified intelligence leading to civilian impacts and accusations of insufficient transparency in casualty figures, as Egyptian announcements often aggregated militant and security losses without independent verification. While official narratives framed operations as decisive, empirical data from conflict trackers showed Wilayat Sinai retaining operational capacity for low-intensity strikes, challenging claims of comprehensive victory and raising questions about resource allocation amid Egypt's broader military expenditures.[53][49]

Allegations of Human Rights Abuses

During Osama Askar's tenure as Chief of Staff of the Egyptian Armed Forces from October 2021 to August 2024, human rights organizations documented ongoing allegations of abuses by Egyptian military units in North Sinai counter-terrorism operations, including forced evictions, home demolitions, and extrajudicial executions of suspected militants and civilians. These actions were framed by critics as disproportionate responses to the Sinai insurgency led by ISIS-affiliated groups, with reports citing the destruction of thousands of homes to create buffer zones along the Gaza border and against insurgent threats, often without adequate compensation or relocation support.[54] Egyptian authorities maintained that such measures were essential for security and complied with legal frameworks, rejecting claims of illegality as distortions by biased observers.[55] Amnesty International highlighted video evidence from August 2021 showing Egyptian army personnel executing unarmed individuals in Sinai, labeling the incidents as deliberate cold-blooded killings disguised as combat encounters, with calls for independent investigations that went unheeded. Similar patterns persisted into subsequent years, with U.S. State Department reports noting credible accounts of arbitrary killings and torture by security forces, including military units, amid the suppression of alleged insurgents. Human Rights Watch characterized large-scale demolitions as potential war crimes under international humanitarian law, based on satellite imagery and witness testimonies, though the Egyptian military countered that operations targeted terrorist infrastructure and spared non-combatants.[55][56][54] In September 2025, the discovery of mass graves in Sinai, containing hundreds of skeletons attributed to civilian and indigenous victims, intensified scrutiny, with groups like the Cairo Institute for Human Rights Studies implicating the armed forces in systematic extrajudicial killings over the insurgency's duration, including periods under Askar's command oversight. These findings, derived from field research and forensic analysis, were dismissed by Egyptian officials as unverified propaganda amid the conflict's complexities, where militants have also perpetrated atrocities against locals. No direct personal involvement by Askar was alleged in these reports, which focused on institutional practices within the military hierarchy he led.[57][58]

Political Influence and Loyalty Concerns

Lieutenant General Osama Askar's tenure as Chief of Staff from October 2021 onward was marked by perceptions of expanding personal influence within the Egyptian Armed Forces, particularly through longstanding alliances forged during his prior roles in counter-terrorism operations in the Sinai Peninsula. These networks, built over years of combating insurgency, positioned him as a key figure in military strategy, but also fueled speculation about his political aspirations among Egypt's elite circles. By mid-2023, reports emerged that President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi was evaluating Askar's continuation in the role amid concerns over such ambitions, which were said to threaten the centralized command structure under civilian-military fusion in Egypt.[59] Analyses from regional observers attributed Askar's eventual replacement in 2024 to the accumulation of influence that could challenge presidential authority, a common dynamic in authoritarian systems where military leaders' growing power prompts preemptive reshuffles to ensure loyalty. Opposition-leaning outlets highlighted this as part of broader efforts to curb potential rivals, though state-aligned narratives framed the transition as routine promotion cycles without explicit disloyalty accusations. Askar's prior controversial reputation, stemming from his Sinai command during escalated operations, amplified these loyalty questions, with critics questioning alignment with Sisi's directives on resource allocation and operational autonomy.[60][61] Despite these concerns, Askar's post-replacement appointment as a presidential advisor on military affairs indicated that perceived risks were contained through reassignment rather than outright purge, preserving institutional continuity while reinforcing loyalty mechanisms within the officer corps. This outcome aligned with patterns in Sisi's leadership, where high-ranking officers are rotated to prevent factional entrenchment, though independent assessments note the opacity of such decisions limits verification of underlying motives.[62]

Succession and Legacy

Replacement in 2024

On July 3, 2024, Egyptian President Abdel Fattah al-Sisi issued a decree appointing Lieutenant General Ahmed Fathy Ibrahim Khalifa as the new Chief of Staff of the Armed Forces, effective immediately, replacing Lieutenant General Osama Askar who had held the position since October 27, 2021.[63][2] Askar was simultaneously reassigned as a presidential advisor, a move that positioned him outside the direct military command structure while retaining influence within the presidential apparatus.[63][64] The replacement occurred amid a broader cabinet reshuffle announced on the same day, which included changes to the defense ministry and other key portfolios, amid ongoing economic pressures and regional security challenges in Egypt.[65] Khalifa, previously a senior officer with experience in operational commands, was promoted to the role without prior public indications of Askar's imminent departure, though earlier reports in October 2023 had noted discussions within President Sisi's inner circle about Askar's potential reappointment or succession.[59][66] Speculation in Egyptian media outlets suggested the shift might reflect efforts to refresh military leadership amid criticisms of prolonged tenures and to balance internal power dynamics within the armed forces, though official statements emphasized continuity in national security priorities.[67] Askar's transition to advisory role was described by some analysts as a strategic elevation to mitigate potential rivalries, given his prior roles in counter-terrorism operations in Sinai.[68] No official reasons for the change were detailed beyond the presidential decree, aligning with the opaque nature of high-level military appointments in Egypt.[63]
