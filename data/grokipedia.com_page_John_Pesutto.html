John Pesutto

John Pesutto (born 5 September 1970) is an Australian lawyer and politician who has represented the electorate of Hawthorn in the Victorian Legislative Assembly since 2014.[1][2] Prior to entering parliament, Pesutto practiced law after graduating from the University of Melbourne with degrees in commerce and law in 1994, operating his own legal practice and corporate advisory firm while serving as a Senior Fellow at the university's School of Government.[1][2] He assumed leadership of the Victorian Liberal Party and the role of Leader of the Opposition following the party's defeat in the 2022 state election, holding these positions from December 2022 until a leadership spill removed him in December 2024.[2] Pesutto's leadership was defined by efforts to consolidate the party amid factional strife, but it faced significant challenges, including his vote to expel MP Moira Deeming from the parliamentary party in 2023 over her participation in a controversial rally.[3] This decision led to Deeming's successful defamation lawsuit against him in 2024, resulting in a court-ordered payment of approximately $2.3 million, which as of mid-2025 threatened Pesutto with bankruptcy and potential expulsion from parliament unless resolved.[4][5] By October 2025, Pesutto and supporters were exploring refinancing options for a related party loan to avert further legal battles within the Liberal Party.[6]

Early life and professional career

Upbringing and education

John Pesutto was born on 5 September 1970 in Traralgon, Victoria, to Italian immigrant parents.[7][8] He grew up in regional Victoria, spending part of his early years in Hastings before the family relocated to Traralgon in the Latrobe Valley.[9][10] As one of five children, Pesutto was raised in a working-class household; his father worked primarily as an electrician at the local power station.[7][9] He attended Lavalla Catholic College in Traralgon and played junior soccer for local clubs Olympians and Traralgon City.[8] Pesutto moved to Melbourne for higher education, enrolling at the University of Melbourne where he completed bachelor degrees in commerce and law in 1994.[11][12] His academic focus reflected an early interest in legal and economic principles, laying the foundation for his subsequent career in law and policy advisory roles.[13]

Legal and advisory roles

Pesutto graduated from the University of Melbourne in 1994 with degrees in law and commerce.[1] Following this, he spent many years in private legal practice, specializing in industrial relations and employment matters, and operated his own legal practice.[1] [2] In addition to his legal work, Pesutto established and operated a corporate advisory firm, providing services in areas such as governance, integrity, and performance issues to government departments and public sector agencies.[2] [1] He also held the position of Senior Fellow at the University of Melbourne's School of Government during this period.[2] [1] Prior to his election to parliament in November 2014, Pesutto served in several senior advisory roles within the Victorian government, including as Senior Advisor to Premier Ted Baillieu from 2010 to 2013, Chief of Staff to the Health Minister, and Counsel to Premier Denis Napthine from 2013 to 2014.[1]

Political career

Entry into parliament and early roles (2014â€“2022)

Pesutto was preselected as the Liberal Party candidate for the safe seat of Hawthorn on 4 September 2014.[14] He was elected to the Victorian Legislative Assembly representing Hawthorn at the state election on 29 November 2014, securing 58.59% of the two-candidate-preferred vote against Labor's John McNally.[15] In December 2014, Pesutto was appointed Shadow Attorney-General in Matthew Guy's opposition frontbench, a position he held until the 2018 election.[2] [1] During this period, he focused on scrutiny of government justice policies, including criticism of Labor's handling of legal and prosecutorial matters under Premier Daniel Andrews.[3] Pesutto lost the seat of Hawthorn to Labor candidate John Kennedy at the 2018 state election on 24 November, conceding on 5 December amid a swing against the Liberals statewide.[16] Outside parliament from 2018 to 2022, he maintained involvement in public policy through his legal practice and advisory work, while expressing intent to return to politics.[17] Pesutto regained Hawthorn at the 2022 state election on 26 November, defeating Labor with a margin of 5.6%.[2]

Rise to leadership (2022)

Following the Liberal-National Coalition's defeat in the Victorian state election on 26 November 2022, where Labor secured a third consecutive term under Premier Daniel Andrews with an increased majority, Opposition Leader Matthew Guy announced his resignation on the same day.[18] The Coalition's seat count in the Legislative Assembly fell from 27 to 22, exacerbating internal divisions within the Liberal Party after two successive election losses.[19] The party room convened on 8 December 2022 to elect a new leader, with Hawthorn MP John Pesutto emerging as the candidate to challenge shadow treasurer Brad Battin. Pesutto, previously serving as shadow attorney-general and shadow minister for child protection, positioned himself as a moderate voice aiming to unify the party and rebuild its electoral standing.[20] In a ballot of 33 MPs, Pesutto secured victory by a single vote, defeating Battin in a closely contested race that highlighted factional tensions between moderate and conservative wings.[21][19] Upon his election, Pesutto was sworn in as Leader of the Opposition and pledged to lead a "consultative, constructive, and conciliatory" opposition, focusing on holding the government accountable while addressing policy failures that contributed to the election loss. David Southwick was appointed deputy leader, providing continuity from the previous leadership.[22] This narrow ascension marked Pesutto's rise from a backbench role to the party's helm amid calls for renewal and introspection within the Victorian Liberals.[10]

Tenure as Leader of the Opposition (2022â€“2024)

John Pesutto was elected Leader of the Victorian Liberal Party and Leader of the Opposition on 8 December 2022, narrowly defeating Brad Battin in a party room ballot shortly after the Coalition's loss in the 2022 Victorian state election.[20][21] He assumed leadership amid a parliamentary Liberal Party reduced to 16 seats in the 88-seat Legislative Assembly, pledging to rebuild the party's credibility through constructive scrutiny of the re-elected Labor government led by Daniel Andrews and later Jacinta Allan.[21][19] Pesutto's opposition emphasized accountability on fiscal management, with repeated criticisms of Victoria's rising state debt, which exceeded AUD 150 billion by mid-2023, and infrastructure project delays under Labor.[23] He positioned the Liberals as advocates for evidence-based governance and integrity in public administration, drawing on his legal background to highlight alleged shortcomings in government decision-making processes.[2] In parliamentary debates, his team targeted Labor's handling of crime statistics, noting a 15% rise in reported offences in 2023 compared to pre-pandemic levels, and pushed for tougher policing measures.[24] Policy platforms under Pesutto addressed housing and development barriers, including a July 2024 commitment to exempt small-scale residential projects from mandatory cultural heritage management plans to reduce approval times and boost supply amid Victoria's housing shortage.[25] In May 2024, he announced intentions to reinstate police move-on powers repealed by Labor, arguing they were essential for maintaining public order in high-crime areas.[26] These initiatives aimed to appeal to suburban voters facing cost-of-living pressures, with opposition polling showing Liberal primary vote gains to around 35% by late 2024.[27] Internally, Pesutto navigated factional tensions between moderate and conservative wings, securing moderate dominance in July 2024 state council elections that reinforced his leadership structure.[28] Despite external popularityâ€”evidenced by his approval ratings outperforming Allan's in mid-2024 surveysâ€”recurrent leadership speculation arose from party room dissent, particularly over strategic directions and candidate preselections.[24][27] His tenure, spanning two years, focused on positioning the Liberals for the 2026 election but was marked by ongoing efforts to unify a divided parliamentary party.[17]

Moira Deeming expulsion and defamation case

In March 2023, Moira Deeming, a Liberal Party member of the Victorian Legislative Assembly for Western Victoria Region, organized and spoke at the "Let Women Speak" rally on the steps of Parliament House in Melbourne, which focused on women's rights and opposition to transgender activism. The event was disrupted by individuals performing Nazi salutes, an incident Deeming publicly condemned as unrelated to the rally's purpose.[29][30] On 27 March 2023, the Victorian Liberal parliamentary party voted against expelling Deeming but imposed a nine-month suspension from the party room, with 22 votes to retain her membership and 13 in favor of expulsion.[30] Tensions escalated when Deeming threatened legal action against party leader John Pesutto over his criticisms of her association with the rally. On 12 May 2023, Liberal MPs voted 19 to 11 to expel Deeming from the parliamentary party room, requiring her to sit as an independent for the remainder of her term despite retaining her Liberal endorsement for the 2026 election.[29][31] Pesutto justified the expulsion as necessary to address damage to the party's reputation from the rally's optics, though Deeming maintained the decision reflected internal factional opposition to her conservative views on gender issues.[32] Deeming initiated defamation proceedings against Pesutto in the Federal Court in December 2023, alleging that his statementsâ€”including emails to party members, media comments, and a party documentâ€”imputed that she sympathized with neo-Nazis, associated with extremists, and was unfit to serve as a parliamentarian.[33] Pesutto defended on grounds of contextual truth, honest opinion, and public interest under the Defamation Act 2005 (NSW), arguing the imputations arose from reasonable concerns over the rally's aftermath.[34] On 11 December 2024, Justice David O'Callaghan ruled in Deeming's favor, finding that five publications by Pesutto conveyed defamatory meanings, including that Deeming was a Nazi sympathizer unfit for parliament, and rejected Pesutto's defenses due to insufficient evidence linking her directly to the disruptors.[35][34] The court awarded Deeming $300,000 in damages for reputational harm and emotional distress, with Pesutto ordered to pay her legal costs on an indemnity basis, ultimately totaling approximately $2.3 million.[36] The judgment highlighted that Pesutto's actions exacerbated intra-party divisions, contributing to subsequent leadership instability, though a December 2024 motion to reinstate Deeming in the party room failed.[37]

Leadership downfall and immediate aftermath (2024)

In December 2024, escalating internal divisions within the Victorian Liberal Party, primarily stemming from Pesutto's handling of the Moira Deeming expulsion and a related defamation lawsuit, culminated in a leadership spill. On December 13, 2024, the Supreme Court of Victoria ruled that Pesutto had defamed Deeming by implying in party communications that she was associated with neo-Nazis, awarding her damages and costs estimated at over $300,000, though the full liability reached into millions including legal fees.[38][39] This verdict intensified pressure on Pesutto, as it highlighted factional rifts between moderates supportive of his leadership and conservatives aligned with Deeming, who had been expelled from the party room in 2023 over her attendance at a Let Women Speak rally disrupted by anti-trans activists.[40] A party room meeting on December 20, 2024, narrowly voted against readmitting Deeming, with Pesutto's allies holding the line amid accusations of procedural irregularities and threats of defection from right-wing MPs.[41] By December 22, a group of Liberal MPs, including conservatives frustrated by Pesutto's resistance to Deeming's return and perceived weak opposition performance post-2022 election defeat, called a special meeting to consider both her readmission and a spill of Pesutto's leadership.[42] The spill motion proceeded on December 27, 2024, where Pesutto lost the confidence of the party room; shadow treasurer Brad Battin, a right-faction figure and former police officer, defeated moderates including Chris Crewther and Jess Wilson to become the new leader, with David Southwick retained as deputy.[39][40] Deeming was immediately readmitted to the party room following the spill, marking a victory for the conservative wing and exposing Pesutto's diminished authority after over two years as leader.[43] The new all-male leadership team under Battin faced immediate criticism for sidelining female contenders Wilson and deputy shadow minister Kim Stokes, prompting accusations of gender imbalance from within the party and media observers.[44] Pesutto retained his seat in Hawthorn and shadow attorney-general portfolio initially, but the ousting underscored broader instability in the Victorian Liberals, who held only 28 lower house seats after the 2022 landslide loss to Labor, with analysts attributing the spill to unresolved cultural wars over issues like gender ideology and party unity.[45] In the weeks following, Battin announced a shadow cabinet reshuffle emphasizing fiscal conservatism, while Pesutto's allies regrouped amid ongoing defamation cost liabilities that strained party resources.[17]

Post-leadership challenges (2025â€“present)

Following his ousting as Victorian Liberal Party leader on 27 December 2024, Pesutto encountered significant financial and political repercussions stemming from the defamation judgment awarded to former MP Moira Deeming. In April 2025, Deeming secured costs estimated at over $2 million against Pesutto personally, as the court ruled his actions in expelling her from the party constituted defamation.[46] By June 2025, Pesutto faced imminent bankruptcy proceedings unless he paid approximately $2.3 million in outstanding legal fees to Deeming, prompting warnings that failure to do so would lead to his expulsion from the Victorian Parliament under anti-bankruptcy laws.[4] On 19 June 2025, the Victorian Liberal Party's state council approved a $1.5 million interest-free loan to Pesutto, sourced from party funds, to cover the bulk of Deeming's costs and avert his bankruptcy. This intervention, described by party insiders as an "11th-hour deal," preserved Pesutto's parliamentary seat in Hawthorn but ignited internal divisions, with critics arguing it diverted resources from campaign efforts amid the party's electoral struggles.[46] [47] Deeming subsequently indicated plans to pursue further legal action against Pesutto's financial backers, including party figures who supported the expulsion, exacerbating tensions within the Liberal factional landscape.[48] By October 2025, the loan had escalated to $1.55 million amid repayment disputes, leading Pesutto and his allies to explore refinancing options through private lenders to shield the party from potential court-mandated recovery efforts by Deeming. This development risked drawing the Liberal Party into protracted litigation, further straining relations between moderate and conservative wings.[6] Concurrently, Pesutto faced whispers of a conservative preselection challenge for Hawthornâ€”a marginal seat ahead of the 2026 state electionâ€”but no formal contender had emerged by early October, despite extended party deadlines for candidate nominations.[49] Pesutto's removal from shadow ministry roles, including Multicultural Affairs, effective January 2025 under new leader Brad Battin, relegated him to the backbench and underscored his diminished influence amid ongoing party infighting over Deeming's potential readmission. These challenges compounded the Liberal Party's broader disarray, with analysts attributing intensified factional warfare to Pesutto's unresolved legal entanglements.[2] [50]

Electoral record

State elections contested

Pesutto first contested the Electoral district of Hawthorn at the 29 November 2014 Victorian state election, succeeding retiring Liberal MP Ted Baillieu and defeating the Labor candidate on a two-party preferred margin of 8.6%.[51] He retained the seat at the 24 November 2018 election with 43.89% of first-preference votes but lost to Labor challenger John Ormond Kennedy in a narrow upset, amid a statewide swing to Labor.[52][51] Pesutto reclaimed Hawthorn at the 26 November 2022 election, polling 18,728 first-preference votes (42.27%) and defeating Kennedy on a two-candidate preferred count of 22,927 votes (51.74%) to 21,383 (48.26%), for a winning margin of 1,544 votes.[53]

Political positions and ideology

Economic and fiscal policies

Pesutto has advocated for fiscal discipline to address what he describes as Labor's mismanagement, including projected net debt of $187.8 billion by 2027â€“28 and interest repayments approaching $1 million per hour.[54] In October 2024, he announced a Financial Integrity Plan featuring a legislated Charter of Budget Honesty, which would require a fiscal strategy at the start of each parliamentary term, benchmarks such as a net debt cap as a percentage of gross state product, and capped contingencies, alongside annual reporting in budget updates.[55] This plan also includes a real-time public expenditure tracker website for portfolio-level spending visibility and a Victorian Intergenerational Report, produced every five years by a new productivity commission, to assess long-term economic sustainability.[55] On taxation, Pesutto has positioned the Liberal Party to reform Victoria's systemâ€”criticized as the nation's highest taxingâ€”to enhance competitiveness and retain businesses and investment.[54] [56] Key proposals include reviewing stamp duty and land tax for long-term viability, committing to the "most attractive tax regime in the nation," and eliminating targeted levies such as Labor's Holiday and Tourism tax to lower costs for regional businesses and consumers.[56] [54] He has argued that escalating taxes under Labor drive interstate migration of jobs and capital, contrasting this with reforms to foster growth without specifying immediate rate cuts.[54] For economic growth, Pesutto proposed establishing a Victorian Productivity Commission to reduce regulatory burdens, estimated to save up to $1.6 billion annually in red tape, and boost investment.[56] [54] Additional measures include a new watchdog and code of conduct to curb project cost blowouts and union misconduct on major infrastructure, aiming to streamline processes and enforce accountability.[56] These initiatives reflect a focus on deregulation and private-sector incentives over government expansion, though implementation details remained prospective during his leadership.[56]

Social and cultural issues

Pesutto has positioned himself as a moderate on social issues, supporting policies aligned with progressive reforms while allowing conscience votes within the Liberal Party. He has expressed personal support for abortion access and confirmed that coalition members would have a free vote on proposals to enshrine such rights in the Victorian Constitution.[57][58] Despite voting against the Voluntary Assisted Dying Bill in November 2017, which legalized euthanasia for terminally ill patients after a year-long inquiry and extensive debate, Pesutto later indicated as opposition leader that he backed the existing framework and opposed efforts to relax safeguards.[59][60][61] On matters of gender and sexuality, Pesutto has taken stances emphasizing inclusivity for LGBTQI communities, including support for equal rights. This approach contributed to internal party tensions, particularly in his role in expelling MP Moira Deeming in May 2023 following her attendance at the Let Women Speak rally on March 18, 2023, which featured speakers critical of transgender ideology and women's rights concerns. Pesutto condemned the event as an "anti-trans rights protest" and moved for Deeming's removal amid allegations of neo-Nazi attendance, a decision upheld by Liberal MPs but later contested in Deeming's successful defamation lawsuit against him, settled in December 2024 with Pesutto issuing an apology without paying damages.[60][29][62] These positions reflect Pesutto's effort to steer the Victorian Liberals toward a broader electoral appeal, distancing from socially conservative elements, though critics within the party argued it alienated traditional bases. No public record details his specific stance on the 2017 federal same-sex marriage plebiscite, but the Victorian Liberal Party allowed free votes on such conscience matters without binding policy.[63][60]

Specific policy areas

Pesutto has positioned the Liberal Party to oppose the Allan Labor Government's housing targets, pledging to scrap the current plans in favor of reforms that reduce regulatory barriers, including exemptions from cultural heritage assessments for residential developments under certain thresholds.[25][64] He argued that such measures, including revisions to Melbourne Water requirements, would accelerate housing supply without compromising essential protections, citing delays in projects due to Aboriginal heritage processes approved by registered parties.[65] On youth crime, Pesutto highlighted a 934 percent surge in offences over the prior decade under Labor, criticizing the government's approach to raising the age of criminal responsibility to 12 without adequate funding for policing or youth intervention programs.[66][67] His opposition emphasized the need for stronger community safety measures amid rising incidents, particularly among 14- to 17-year-olds, which increased 20.7 percent year-on-year, while decrying reductions in youth detention capacity.[68] In health policy, Pesutto committed to abolishing Labor's payroll tax on general practitioners, which he described as a "Health Tax" driving up costs and eroding bulk billing incentives, with the policy estimated to save practices facing Victoria's higher-than-average tax rates.[69][70] This stance aimed to lower out-of-pocket expenses for patients and counteract reported government funding pressures on hospitals.[71] Regarding energy and renewables, Pesutto proposed a "locals first" framework prioritizing community input and benefits in renewable projects, including safeguards like transmission line burial underground and revenue-sharing for host communities, positioning it as a counter to Labor's centralized approach.[72] The plan included a 2-kilometer setback for wind turbines to address regional concerns, though it drew criticism for potentially reviving restrictive policies.[73] For transport infrastructure, Pesutto advocated pausing the Suburban Rail Loop to reallocate funds toward higher-priority projects like road upgrades and regional rail, reserving the right not to complete the $216.7 billion initiative if elected, while pushing for enhanced oversight and anti-corruption measures such as excluding organized crime from major sites via a new enforcement body.[74][75][76]

Personal life

Family and relationships

John Pesutto has been married to Panagiota Pesutto, known as Betty, who is of Greek Australian descent, since approximately 2001.[77][10] The couple resides in Hawthorn, Victoria, where they have lived for over 25 years.[13] They have three daughters: Chiara, Aurelia, and Claudia.[10] Pesutto has publicly expressed affection for his family, including Mother's Day tributes from himself and his daughters to Betty.[78] Pesutto was born to Italian migrant parents, Antoinetta and her husband, who emigrated to Australia and raised five children; his father worked as an electrician.[79] In September 2025, amid personal financial difficulties involving a bankruptcy notice, Pesutto transferred a 50 percent share in his consultancy firm, Hugo Benice Advisory Pty Ltd, to Betty for $1.[80][81] No other significant relationships or family details have been publicly documented in reliable sources.

Public persona and affiliations

John Pesutto is characterized as a moderate within the Victorian division of the Liberal Party of Australia, advocating classical liberal principles with an emphasis on governance integrity and evidence-based decision-making.[3][77] His public engagements include regular appearances on television and radio to analyze political and policy issues, as well as authoring a fortnightly column for The Age newspaper prior to his elevation to party leadership.[2][1] Pesutto's persona reflects a professional trajectory rooted in law and public policy, where he has prioritized community service in the Hawthorn electorate, focusing on multiculturalism, resource management, and local needs such as housing and public services.[2][1] He joined the Liberal Party at age 22 and has maintained a commitment to broadening its appeal, though internal party dynamics have tested this approach.[10][77] His primary affiliations include longstanding membership in the Liberal Party of Australia (Victorian Division), where he has held advisory roles such as Counsel to Premier Denis Napthine (2014) and Chief of Staff to the Health Minister under Premier Ted Baillieu.[1] Professionally, Pesutto served as a Senior Fellow at the University of Melbourne's School of Government and operated his own legal practice and corporate advisory firm specializing in industrial relations and employment law.[2][1] These ties underscore his expertise in governance, integrity, and performance advisory services.[1]
