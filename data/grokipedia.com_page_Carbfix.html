Carbfix

CarbFix is an Icelandic carbon capture and mineralization technology that dissolves captured CO2 in water and injects it into subsurface basalt formations, accelerating natural geochemical reactions to form stable carbonate minerals such as calcite and dolomite within months to two years, thereby achieving permanent geological storage.[1] The approach, pioneered through a collaborative pilot project launched in 2006 by Reykjavík Energy, the University of Iceland, France's CNRS, and Columbia University's Earth Institute, demonstrated feasibility with initial injections of 175 tonnes of CO2 in 2012 at a test site near Reykjavik, where isotopic and tracer monitoring verified over 95% mineralization efficiency.[2][3] Full-scale implementation began in 2014 at the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant, where the process has since mineralized CO2 and H2S emissions from plant operations, cumulatively storing more than 70,000 tonnes of CO2 as of recent reports, with ongoing seismic, fluid, and soil flux monitoring confirming containment and transformation without leakage.[4][5] This method contrasts with conventional CO2 storage in sedimentary reservoirs by leveraging the high reactivity of basalt—abundant in volcanic regions—to enable rapid, verifiable sequestration at costs around $25 per tonne, though scalability remains constrained to geologically suitable mafic rock environments.[4]

History

Founding and Early Development (2006-2014)

The CarbFix project originated in 2006, prompted by Iceland's commitments under the Kyoto Protocol and an initiative from the Icelandic President to explore economic CO₂ sequestration in basalt rocks. The first organizational meeting convened in October 2006, establishing objectives for site characterization, laboratory studies, and modeling of CO₂-basalt interactions. It was formalized in 2007 through collaboration among Reykjavík Energy, the University of Iceland, the French National Centre for Scientific Research (CNRS) in Toulouse, and Columbia University's Earth Institute, with Reykjavík Energy providing primary funding and industrial leadership. Hólmfríður Sigurðardóttir was appointed project manager that year.[6][2][7] Early efforts emphasized foundational research, including laboratory experiments on CO₂ dissolution in water and its reactive transport in basalt, alongside field investigations at the Hellisheiði geothermal site. Graduate students conducted theses on subsurface geology and hydrology, contributing to over 60 peer-reviewed publications that demonstrated rapid mineralization potential, with CO₂ forming stable carbonates within months to years under subsurface conditions. Natural analogue studies and reservoir modeling informed injection strategies, while challenges such as corrosion in gas separation processes led to abandoning distillation methods by 2011 in favor of alternative approaches. Securing more than ten environmental permits required extensive public engagement, achieving stakeholder acceptance by 2009.[7][6] A pilot gas capture facility at Hellisheiði was completed in spring 2010, initiating operations in July despite equipment setbacks. Injection infrastructure testing in March 2011 validated a sparger system for dissolving CO₂ in groundwater at 350 meters depth, mitigating clogging risks. Small-scale pilot injections commenced in January 2012, successfully delivering 175 tonnes of pure CO₂ over three months at 500 meters depth and 35°C, followed by 73 tonnes of a CO₂-H₂S mixture until August, when bio-clogging by iron-oxidizing bacteria and discontinuous flow halted operations. Post-injection monitoring revealed over 95% mineralization within one year, confirming the method's efficacy. By 2014, these developments paved the way for industrial-scale implementation, targeting annual injections of around 15,000 tonnes of CO₂-H₂S mixtures at elevated subsurface temperatures.[2][6][7]

Pilot Injection and Initial Results (2014-2018)

In June 2014, Carbfix initiated large-scale injections of CO2 and H2S emissions captured from the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant, marking the transition from small-scale pilots to operational deployment as part of the EU-funded CarbFix2 project. The gases were fully dissolved in recycled condensate water at surface conditions using a downhole mixer, with dissolution completing in under 40 seconds at a pH of approximately 5.4 and a dissolved inorganic carbon concentration of 30.9 mM. Injections targeted fractured basaltic formations at depths of 400–800 meters and temperatures exceeding 250°C, differing from earlier pilots at cooler, shallower depths. This phase began on 23 June 2014 with gas-charged water injection into a deep reservoir well (HN-3), building on prior tests including a pure CO2 injection into a shallower reservoir from January to March 2014 that verified infrastructure reliability without operational issues.[8][5] Through the end of 2017, the project injected 23,200 metric tons of CO2 and 11,800 metric tons of H2S, with operations scaling up further in 2016 and 2017 to handle a larger fraction of the plant's emissions—approximately one-third of CO2 (around 12,000 tons annually) and 60% of H2S. Monitoring involved pre- and post-injection groundwater sampling, geochemical tracers (e.g., perfluorocarbon compounds), and isotope analyses (e.g., δ¹³C and Ca isotopes) from a network of wells, confirming no detectable leakage to the surface or shallow aquifers. Initial results from the first injection sub-phase (June 2014 to July 2016) showed over 50% of injected carbon mineralizing within 4–9 months, primarily as calcite (CaCO₃), and 76% of sulfur as pyrite (FeS₂), with rates accelerated by the high-temperature reactive transport in basalt-hosted fractures. These findings exceeded lab-based predictions, attributing rapid trapping to enhanced dissolution kinetics and abundant divalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺) from basalt dissolution.[5][9] By 2018, cumulative data indicated 80–90% mineralization of injected carbon within one year in monitored zones, consistent with earlier pilot verifications but validated at industrial volumes without capacity limitations or pressure buildup issues. Reaction path modeling and field isotopes reconstructed precipitation temperatures aligning with reservoir conditions (250–300°C), supporting causal mechanisms of CO2 hydration to bicarbonate followed by cation sourcing for carbonate nucleation. No adverse environmental impacts were observed, with stored CO2 volumes estimated at over 70% efficiency in precipitated solids, primarily carbonates, as verified by core sample analyses showing secondary mineral fills in fractures. These outcomes affirmed basalt's suitability for permanent storage, prompting further expansions while highlighting the method's reliance on site-specific hydrology and reactive rock composition for scalability.[8][10][5]

Commercialization and Expansion (2019-Present)

In late 2019, Carbfix was established as a subsidiary of Reykjavík Energy, transitioning to independent operations on January 1, 2020, which facilitated its shift toward commercial-scale carbon mineralization.[2] This period saw the company refine its technology for broader industrial applicability, including pilot adaptations for sectors such as steel, iron, and cement production, while maintaining mineralization costs at US$24.8 per ton—below average EU Emission Trading System carbon quota prices.[2] By 2024, operations had scaled to include the Mammoth project, injecting 36,000 metric tons of CO2 annually into basaltic formations.[11] Expansion initiatives emphasized large-scale infrastructure and international partnerships. The Coda Terminal, a planned cross-border CO2 transport and storage hub in Iceland, received EU Innovation Fund support of €115 million and is scheduled to commence operations in 2026, targeting full capacity of 3 million tons per year by 2031 through shipment of captured CO2 from northern European industrial sites.[12][13] In May 2025, Carbfix secured Europe's first onshore geological CO2 storage permit under the EU CCS Directive for this project, enabling permanent mineralization in basaltic rock.[14] To extend beyond Iceland, Carbfix signed a December 2024 memorandum of understanding with CarbonQuest for onsite carbon capture and storage projects targeting North American industrial and manufacturing facilities, leveraging existing infrastructure to reduce deployment costs.[15] In May 2025, the company extended its partnership with Aker Carbon Capture to integrate capture technologies with mineralization for end-to-end CCS solutions.[16] These agreements, alongside ongoing Hellisheiði integrations advancing toward carbon neutrality, positioned Carbfix for global commercialization by 2025.[17]

Technical Method

CO2 Dissolution and Preparation

The CarbFix process prepares captured CO2 for injection by fully dissolving it in water at the surface or during downhole mixing, forming a dense, acidic solution that enhances solubility trapping and reactivity with basalt. This approach, developed since 2007, avoids the risks of injecting supercritical gaseous CO2, such as buoyancy-driven leakage or slow in-situ dissolution, by leveraging water as a carrier medium.[18][8] CO2 gas, sourced from industrial emissions like geothermal power plants, is separated and introduced into water via bubbling or equilibration in mixing vessels or pipelines under pressure to achieve near-complete dissolution in under 5 minutes. The solubility is governed by Henry's law, with typical flow rates such as 750 g/s water paired with 27 g/s CO2 ensuring saturation without free gas phases. Chemically, the process yields carbonic acid through the reaction CO₂(g) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₂CO₃(aq), which dissociates to bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and hydrogen ions, lowering the pH to 3–5 and promoting cation release from host rocks upon injection.[8][5][18] Water consumption averages 20–25 tons per ton of CO2 to maximize dissolved concentrations, with fresh water traditionally used but seawater increasingly viable to minimize freshwater demands in water-scarce regions. In November 2023, CarbFix conducted the world's first injection of CO2 fully dissolved in seawater at the Helguvík site, confirming comparable dissolution efficiency and mineralization potential without adverse effects from salinity. Preparation includes pressure monitoring to exceed the bubble point by at least 5 bar, preventing gas exsolution, and integration with capture systems for continuous operation, as demonstrated in the CarbFix2 project injecting over 23,200 metric tons of CO2 from 2014–2017.[19][20][5]

Injection into Basalt Formations

The injection process in CarbFix operations involves dissolving captured CO₂ in water to form a carbonated solution, which is then pumped into subsurface basalt formations via dedicated injection wells. This dissolution occurs prior to or during injection in high-pressure units, creating a mildly acidic carbonic acid solution with a pH typically ranging from 3 to 5, leveraging the reactivity of basalt's divalent cations such as Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, and Fe²⁺.[18][20] The process requires substantial water volumes, approximately 25 tons per ton of CO₂, sourced from nearby geothermal or other supplies to achieve full solubility trapping immediately upon injection.[5] Wells are drilled to target permeable and fractured basalt layers, often at depths of 400 to 800 meters in Icelandic sites, though some operations extend beyond 2,000 meters to access suitable reactive rock.[21] Injection parameters are precisely managed to maintain reservoir pressures at least 5 bar above the bubble point pressure—calculated from CO₂ and water flow rates, temperatures, and compositions—preventing CO₂ exsolution into free gas.[18] Full dissolution is verified at the wellhead using CO₂ sensors or downhole logging to detect any gas bubbles, with no cement well casing required if solubility is confirmed, reducing operational complexity while adhering to national drilling standards.[18] In the 2014 pilot project, annual injection targeted 2,200 tons of fully water-dissolved CO₂ into basalt-hosted reservoirs.[1] Operational monitoring ensures injection integrity through continuous mass flow metering (with ≤5% uncertainty), pressure logging, and periodic tracer tests using compounds like SF₅CF₃ to track fluid migration without relying on mineralization endpoints.[1][18] This approach contrasts with supercritical CO₂ injection in sedimentary formations by prioritizing reactive mafic rocks, where basalt's high surface area and mineral content facilitate rapid interaction, though site-specific permeability governs flow rates and plume extent.[5] Since 2014, CarbFix has injected over 100,000 tons of CO₂ using this method, scaling from pilot to commercial volumes at rates supporting industrial capture integration.[22]

Mineralization Mechanism and Verification

The mineralization mechanism at Carbfix involves dissolving captured CO2 in water to form carbonic acid, which is then injected into porous basalt formations at depths of 400 to 800 meters.[23] This process leverages the reactivity of basalt, rich in divalent cations such as calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺), and iron (Fe²⁺), to facilitate the precipitation of stable carbonate minerals.[3] The primary reaction produces calcite (CaCO₃) and other carbonates through the interaction of dissolved CO₂ with these cations leached from the host rock, effectively converting the injected CO₂ into solid form.[5] Unlike traditional geological storage relying on physical trapping, this in-situ mineralization achieves chemical permanence by binding CO₂ as insoluble minerals, preventing remobilization.[4] Field observations from the CarbFix pilot demonstrate rapid mineralization kinetics, with up to 95% of injected CO₂ converting to carbonates within 2 years, orders of magnitude faster than in sandstone or limestone reservoirs.[23] Laboratory experiments and reactive transport modeling support this, attributing the speed to the high surface area and reactivity of fractured basalt, enhanced by the downhole dissolution method that maximizes contact between CO₂-charged water and reactive minerals.[3] The process exploits natural groundwater flow and the alkaline nature of basalt pore waters (pH ~9-10), which promote cation dissolution and carbonate supersaturation.[5] Verification employs a multi-method approach, including the injection of conservative and reactive chemical tracers to track fluid migration and quantify mineralization rates.[4] Monitoring wells adjacent to injection sites enable regular sampling of formation waters for geochemical analysis, revealing decreases in dissolved inorganic carbon and shifts in cation concentrations indicative of precipitation.[3] Calcium isotope ratios (δ⁴⁴/⁴⁰Ca) in pre- and post-injection fluids provide direct quantification of carbonate formation, confirming near-complete CO₂ immobilization in calcite at the CarbFix site.[3] Additional techniques involve non-conventional stable isotopes (e.g., Mg, Fe) and clumped isotope thermometry to reconstruct mineralization temperatures and fluid origins, further validating the process's efficacy and permanence.[24] Core samples from the storage formation exhibit precipitated carbonate veins, offering physical evidence of mineral trapping.[4] These methods, integrated into protocols validated by third parties like DNV, ensure robust assessment of storage integrity.[25]

Key Facilities and Operations

Hellisheiði Geothermal Integration

The Hellisheiði Power Plant, operated by ON Power, integrates Carbfix technology to capture and mineralize CO₂ and H₂S emissions from geothermal fluids, reducing atmospheric release from power generation.[26] This integration began with a pilot gas capture plant constructed in spring 2010, enabling initial testing of CO₂ separation from plant emissions.[6] Full-scale operations commenced in June 2014, with CO₂-charged water injected into subsurface basalt formations adjacent to the plant, accelerating natural mineralization processes.[5][27] The capture system at Hellisheiði processes geothermal gases, achieving up to 98% CO₂ removal efficiency in the pilot phase, with an annual capacity of 3,000 tonnes of CO₂ and 1,000 tonnes of H₂S.[28] Upscaling under the CarbFix2 project increased injection to approximately 12,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually, representing about 30% reduction in the plant's CO₂ emissions.[26][27] Injections target formations exceeding 200°C, where dissolved gases react with basalt to form stable carbonates, verified through geochemical monitoring and tracer studies. By 2023, cumulative injections exceeded 70,000 tonnes of CO₂, with over 95% mineralized within two years based on site-specific observations.[21] This geothermal integration demonstrates practical application of Carbfix in an operational energy facility, leveraging the plant's proximity to injection sites for efficient transport and minimizing energy penalties associated with gas handling.[29] Ongoing enhancements, including EU-funded projects like Silverstone, further optimize capture at Hellisheiði, focusing on mixed gas handling and long-term storage integrity.[28] The site's basalt geology provides favorable conditions for rapid mineralization, distinguishing it from sedimentary storage alternatives.[30]

Direct Air Capture Collaborations (Orca and Mammoth)

Carbfix has partnered with Climeworks, a Swiss direct air capture (DAC) technology firm, since 2017 to integrate DAC with permanent mineralization storage in Iceland's basaltic formations.[31] In this collaboration, Climeworks' modular DAC units capture CO₂ from ambient air using renewable geothermal energy, after which the concentrated CO₂ is transported to Carbfix facilities, dissolved in water, and injected underground for rapid conversion to stable carbonate minerals.[31] [32] These projects leverage the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant's proximity for low-cost, carbon-neutral operations, with ON Power providing excess heat and electricity.[33] The Orca plant, Climeworks' inaugural commercial-scale DAC facility, began operations on September 8, 2021, near Hellisheiði, marking the world's first large-scale DAC and storage (DAC+S) deployment.[34] Orca comprises 16 modular collectors designed to remove 4,000 metric tons of CO₂ annually, equivalent to the emissions of approximately 870 cars per year, with the captured CO₂ piped directly to Carbfix for injection into subsurface basalt at depths of 500–2,000 meters.[33] [35] By 2023, Orca had achieved full operational capacity, demonstrating the feasibility of combining DAC with Carbfix's mineralization process, which achieves over 95% conversion to solids within two years based on prior pilot data.[31] Building on Orca, the Mammoth plant represents a significant scale-up, with groundbreaking on June 28, 2022, and initial commissioning on May 7, 2024, positioning it as the world's largest operational DAC facility to date.[36] [37] Mammoth features up to 72 modular collectors, targeting a full capacity of 36,000 metric tons of CO₂ removal per year—roughly nine times Orca's output—through phased modular deployment for iterative improvements.[38] The CO₂ undergoes pre-treatment in a Carbfix-developed absorption tower before injection, enhancing efficiency in the mineralization process, and is stored via the same basalt-hosted method as Orca, with monitoring confirming long-term permanence.[39] These collaborations have enabled over 40,000 tons of cumulative DAC-based removals by mid-2024, funded partly by corporate buyers and grants, while highlighting Iceland's unique geology for cost-effective storage at under $100–150 per ton.[36] [40]

Recent Projects and Permits (Steingerður and EU Approvals)

In June 2025, Carbfix and ON Power launched the Steingerður carbon capture facility at the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant in Iceland, marking a significant advancement in emissions removal from geothermal operations.[41] The facility employs direct water capture technology to remove approximately 95% of carbon dioxide (CO2) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) emissions from the plant's geothermal steam, with a target capacity of 30,000 tonnes of CO2 captured annually once at full operation.[42] [43] Construction on the project, also known as Silverstone in English, began in 2023, with aims to achieve full capacity by the end of 2025, enabling the Hellisheiði plant to approach a near-zero carbon footprint.[44] [45] The Steingerður initiative received support from EU funding, aligning with broader European efforts to deploy carbon dioxide removal technologies at industrial sites.[41] Captured CO2 is mineralized into stable rock formations via Carbfix's established injection process into basalt, ensuring long-term permanence without reliance on traditional monitoring wells.[42] On May 12, 2025, Carbfix obtained Europe's first onshore geological CO2 storage permit under the EU's Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) Directive, issued by Icelandic authorities and reviewed by the EFTA Surveillance Authority.[14] [46] This permit authorizes storage at the Hellisheiði site for up to 106,000 tonnes of CO2 per year, totaling 3.2 million tonnes over 30 years, representing a shift from prior EU approvals limited to offshore sites.[47] [48] The approval followed a draft permit process initiated in early 2024, emphasizing the site's basalt-hosted mineralization as a safe, permanent storage mechanism verified through prior pilot data.

Achievements and Scientific Validation

Rapid Mineralization Demonstrations

![Core sample from CarbFix injection site showing precipitated carbonate minerals][float-right] The CarbFix pilot project, initiated in 2006 and culminating in injections starting in 2012 at the Hellisheiði geothermal site in Iceland, demonstrated rapid in situ mineralization of injected CO₂. Between June 2012 and September 2012, approximately 250 tons of CO₂ dissolved in water were injected into fractured basaltic formations at depths of 400 to 800 meters.[23] Monitoring through fluid sampling and geochemical analysis revealed that 95% or more of the injected CO₂ had mineralized into stable carbonate minerals, primarily calcite, siderite, and dolomite, within less than two years.[23] This rate contrasted sharply with conventional geological storage expectations, where mineralization typically occurs over thousands to millions of years, highlighting the enhanced reactivity of the dissolution method in mafic rocks.[23] Subsequent verification involved drilling a dedicated monitoring well in 2013, from which core samples were extracted and analyzed using techniques such as X-ray diffraction, electron microscopy, and stable isotope ratios. These analyses confirmed the precipitation of carbonates within vesicle and fracture fills, with mass balance calculations indicating near-complete conversion of dissolved CO₂ to solid phases.[23] A 2019 study using calcium isotopes in pre- and post-injection waters quantified that 165 ± 8.3 tons of CO₂ had precipitated as calcite, achieving a 72 ± 5% storage efficiency specifically for that mineral phase, underscoring the method's efficacy despite incomplete accounting for other carbonates.[3] The CarbFix2 project, launched in 2014, extended these demonstrations by injecting over 10,000 tons of CO₂ and 3,000 tons of H₂S from industrial sources into the same basaltic reservoir. Reactive transport modeling and fluid monitoring showed that more than 60% of the CO₂ mineralized within four months, with overall rates exceeding 80-90% within one year, as corroborated by geochemical tracers and seismic monitoring.[5] These field-scale tests validated the scalability of rapid mineralization, with precipitation occurring primarily as calcium carbonate in the shallow injection zone and iron/magnesium carbonates deeper, demonstrating the process's dependence on local mineralogy and flow paths.[5] Independent peer-reviewed assessments emphasized the permanence of these minerals, resistant to reversal under subsurface conditions.[49]

Capacity Milestones and Global Recognition

The Carbfix project achieved its initial capacity milestone through pilot injections conducted between January and March 2012, when 175 metric tons of pure CO2 were dissolved in water and injected into basaltic formations at approximately 500 meters depth and 35°C. Subsequent injections from June to August 2012 added 73 metric tons of a gas mixture comprising 75% CO2 and 25% H2S, demonstrating the feasibility of handling mixed industrial emissions. These early tests laid the groundwork for operational scaling, with full-scale injections commencing at the Hellisheiði geothermal plant in June 2014, marking the transition from pilot to commercial application.[2][5] Capacity expanded significantly through collaborations with direct air capture (DAC) facilities. The Orca plant, operational since September 2021 at Hellisheiði, provided an initial annual storage capacity of 4,000 tons of CO2 captured directly from the atmosphere and mineralized via Carbfix processes. This was followed by the Mammoth plant's commissioning in May 2024, increasing DAC-linked capacity to 36,000 tons per year and representing a tenfold scale-up at the site. In May 2025, Carbfix secured Europe's first onshore geological storage permit for the Steingerður project, authorizing up to 106,000 tons of CO2 mineralization annually, with a cumulative potential of 3.2 million tons over 30 years. Future projections include the Coda Terminal reaching 3 million tons per year by 2031, underscoring ambitions for gigatonne-scale deployment.[38][50][51][52] Carbfix has garnered international acclaim for its mineralization technology. In 2022, it received two Milestone Prizes from the Musk Foundation's XPRIZE Carbon Removal competition for advancing scalable CO2 removal verification. BloombergNEF awarded it the Pioneers accolade for pioneering CO2-to-stone conversion. In January 2025, Carbfix was nominated for the Nordic Innovation Award following its designation as recipient of the Icelandic Innovation Award 2024 by the Icelandic Intellectual Property Office. The project's global profile peaked with the WIPO Global Awards 2025, securing the Environment category prize and honoring CEO Edda Aradóttir as Best Woman Entrepreneur for IP-driven climate innovation.[53][54][55][56]

Peer-Reviewed Evidence of Permanence

Peer-reviewed studies on the CarbFix project provide empirical evidence for the permanence of CO2 storage through mineralization in basaltic formations, demonstrating rapid conversion to stable carbonate minerals with no observed remobilization over monitored periods. A landmark field-scale experiment reported in Science involved injecting approximately 2,100 tonnes of dissolved CO2 into fractured basalt at the Hellisheiði site between 2009 and 2011, with geochemical monitoring revealing that over 95% mineralized into carbonates—primarily calcite, magnesite, and siderite—within less than two years.[23] These precipitates were verified through core sample analysis, showing spatial correlation with injection zones and thermodynamic stability consistent with permanent sequestration, as the carbonate minerals exhibit low solubility under subsurface conditions and resist reversal under typical geological pressures and temperatures.[23] Subsequent verification using calcium isotopes in pre- and post-injection groundwaters from the same site quantified carbonate precipitation directly, indicating that 165 ± 8.3 tonnes of CO2 formed calcite, achieving a 72 ± 5% storage efficiency for the monitored phase, with the minerals integrated into the host rock matrix for long-term stability.[3] Isotopic signatures confirmed the injected CO2 as the carbon source, and the absence of significant Ca depletion in fluids post-mineralization supports complete trapping without leakage.[3] Follow-up clumped isotope analyses of calcite from the site reconstructed mineralization temperatures (20–35°C) matching injection conditions, further validating the process's efficiency and the durability of the resulting carbonates against dissolution.[24] In the CarbFix2 extension, continuous injection of over 10,000 tonnes of CO2 and H2S from 2014 to 2017 at higher temperatures (up to 250°C) showed ongoing mineralization, with modeling and fluid chemistry indicating sustained carbonate formation without evidence of instability or reversal after 3.5 years.[5] Broader reviews of in situ basalt carbonation, incorporating CarbFix data, affirm that mineralized CO2 remains sequestered over geological timescales due to the exothermic nature of the reactions and the low reactivity of product phases in aqueous environments. No peer-reviewed studies report detectable CO2 remobilization from these sites, underscoring the approach's reliability for permanent storage compared to pore-filling methods prone to leakage.

Challenges and Criticisms

Geological and Scalability Constraints

The Carbfix mineralization process requires mafic rock formations, such as basalts, rich in reactive silicate minerals containing calcium, magnesium, and iron to enable the formation of stable carbonates like calcite, dolomite, and siderite.[11] These rocks must possess adequate porosity—typically through vesicles, fractures, or intergranular spaces—and permeability to facilitate the injection of CO2-dissolved water while maintaining injectivity and minimizing risks like induced seismicity or preferential flow paths.[57] At the Hellisheidi site, the host basalts meet these criteria due to their relatively fresh composition and hydrological connectivity, but variations in rock alteration, depth (ideally exceeding 500 meters for suitable temperature and pressure), and structural complexity elsewhere can slow reaction kinetics or limit fluid distribution.[11][58] Globally, geological suitability restricts deployment to regions with extensive mafic volcanism, including Icelandic rift zones, continental flood basalt provinces (e.g., Deccan Traps in India, Columbia River Basalts in the U.S.), and ophiolite sequences, which cover about 10% of the continental surface but often lack proximity to CO2 sources or require deep drilling in inaccessible terrains.[57] In non-ideal settings, such as highly deformed or metamorphosed rocks, reduced mineral reactivity and low intrinsic permeability (1-2% porosity in some cases) further constrain effectiveness, as observed in evaluations of Alpine mafic units.[58] Scalability faces volumetric limitations inherent to basaltic reservoirs, where matrix permeability is lower than in porous sedimentary formations, resulting in injection rates of 0.036 million tonnes of CO2 per annum (Mtpa) at Carbfix's Mammoth facility—orders of magnitude below 1 Mtpa per well in conventional saline aquifer storage.[11] Potential near-wellbore clogging from early carbonate precipitation exacerbates this, reducing long-term injectivity and necessitating multiple wells or hydraulic stimulation, which adds complexity and risk.[11] The requirement for roughly 25 tonnes of water per tonne of CO2 to achieve dissolution and injection imposes additional hydrological constraints, particularly in arid or groundwater-stressed areas, where sourcing and permitting large volumes could compete with other uses or trigger environmental opposition. Although theoretical global storage potential in basalts exceeds hundreds of gigatonnes, practical deployment is bottlenecked by the scarcity of co-located emission sources, rock volumes, and infrastructure, with pilot-scale demonstrations underscoring the need for extensive site characterization to avoid overestimating capacity.[57][59]

Economic Costs and Energy Demands

The Carbfix mineralization process incurs operational costs estimated at approximately $25 per metric ton of CO2 stored, encompassing dissolution in water, injection into basalt formations, and monitoring, which is lower than conventional carbon capture and storage methods that require compression to supercritical state and extensive pipeline infrastructure, often exceeding $65–$100 per ton.[60][61] This figure derives from industrial-scale implementation at the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant, where capital expenditures include drilling injection and monitoring wells, while ongoing expenses cover electricity for pumping, water sourcing, and maintenance.[62] Carbfix reports potential reductions to under $25 per ton at larger scales, with claims of as low as $10 per ton achievable through optimized operations and reduced upfront infrastructure needs in suitable basalt terrains.[27][63] Economic analyses indicate that profitability hinges on carbon pricing; for instance, a scenario modeling Hellisheiði operations required a carbon cost of €77 per ton CO2 to break even, factoring in revenues from emissions avoidance credits but offset by variable injection rates and geological variability.[64] Capital costs dominate at low injection volumes due to fixed well investments, but operational expenses scale more linearly with throughput, primarily driven by water treatment and electricity for high-pressure injection pumps.[65] These costs benefit from integration with existing geothermal facilities, minimizing transport expenses, though expansion to non-geothermal sources could elevate them through added logistics.[66] Energy demands for the mineralization phase are relatively modest compared to capture or traditional storage, focusing on mechanical processes like CO2 dissolution in water via counter-current mixing and subsurface injection, without the high-energy compression typical of supercritical CO2 handling.[60] Specific consumption figures remain limited in public data, but operations leverage surplus geothermal electricity and heat at sites like Hellisheiði, with electricity primarily used for pumps recirculating approximately 20–25 tons of water per ton of CO2 to achieve full dissolution.[52] Injection pressures, sustained by geothermal fluids, further reduce electrical needs, though scaling to megaton levels could strain local renewable capacity if not co-located with low-cost energy sources.[27] When paired with direct air capture, total system energy rises significantly due to upstream thermal and electrical inputs for sorbent regeneration, but Carbfix's mineralization adds minimal incremental demand beyond pumping.[67]

Potential Risks and Unresolved Debates

While the Carbfix method employs dissolved CO2 injection at low pressures to reduce risks associated with supercritical CO2 storage, such as induced seismicity, the potential for fault activation remains a concern in fractured basaltic formations. Monitoring at the Hellisheiði site has detected no significant seismic events attributable to injections since operations began in 2012, attributed to the aqueous phase limiting pore pressure buildup compared to gaseous injections.[4][68] However, broader reviews of CO2 storage indicate that even small leakages could trigger seismicity in overlying layers without overpressurization, particularly if scaling introduces higher volumes or proximity to faults.[69][70] Leakage risks, though minimized by rapid mineralization (up to 95% within two years in pilot tests), persist due to incomplete trapping or caprock integrity compromise. Field data from Carbfix shows near-complete dissolution and precipitation, but long-term remobilization under geochemical shifts—such as pH fluctuations or fluid migration—has not been empirically tested beyond decadal scales.[71] Critics note that while basalt's reactivity accelerates trapping, heterogeneous rock permeability could allow residual CO2 plumes to migrate, potentially contaminating groundwater with elevated alkalinity or mobilized trace metals like heavy elements from host rock dissolution.[72][8] Unresolved debates center on the method's permanence versus reliance on monitoring and verification, with some arguing that mineralization's "forever" claims overlook diagenetic reversibility over millennia, as natural carbon cycling demonstrates mineral carbonates can dissolve under changing subsurface conditions.[73] Scalability debates highlight site-specificity to mafic rocks, questioning global applicability without risking unproven adaptations in less reactive lithologies, potentially amplifying energy demands or injection failures.[74] Additionally, ecological trade-offs, including high initial freshwater use (though mitigated in later iterations with seawater), raise questions about net environmental benefits in water-stressed regions, absent comprehensive life-cycle assessments integrating indirect emissions from co-located geothermal operations.[75][76]

Economic and Policy Aspects

Funding Sources and Carbon Pricing

Carbfix was established in 2007 through collaboration among its founding partners—Reykjavík Energy, the University of Iceland, the French National Centre for Scientific Research (CNRS), and Columbia University—which provided initial project funding and research support starting from a 2006 pilot initiative.[2] These partners enabled early demonstrations of CO2 mineralization at the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant, with operational costs covered through utility revenues and academic grants. Subsequent scaling has relied heavily on public grants, including a €115 million award from the European Innovation Fund in 2022 for the Coda Terminal, a large-scale CO2 storage hub at Straumsvík capable of handling up to 250,000 tonnes annually.[77] Additional EU funding includes €3.9 million for the Silverstone project, aimed at enhancing mineralization at Hellisheiði, and synergies with Horizon Europe programs like CarbFix2 for research and deployment.[78][79] In the United States, Carbfix participated in a $3 million federal grant consortium in 2023 to assess onshore mineralization feasibility, reflecting interest in exporting the technology beyond Iceland.[80] Private investment has supplemented grants, with backers including the Musk Foundation, Urban Future Lab, and Greentown Labs, though total private funding remains undisclosed and secondary to public sources.[81] These investments target commercialization, such as partnerships with emitters like Climeworks for direct air capture storage. Carbfix's revenue model centers on service contracts for CO2 injection and mineralization, charging clients for permanent storage that qualifies for carbon removal credits, rather than direct participation in cap-and-trade systems.[82] The economic viability of Carbfix's operations is closely linked to carbon pricing mechanisms, particularly in the European Economic Area, where Iceland aligns with the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). Higher carbon prices—reaching €100 per tonne of CO2 in the EU ETS by 2023—increase demand for verified permanent storage to offset compliance costs or voluntary removals, enabling Carbfix to monetize its capacity at rates competitive with geological sequestration alternatives.[83] For instance, storage services support premium carbon credits sold at $600–1,200 per tonne in voluntary markets, as seen in partnerships with direct air capture providers, though Carbfix's costs (estimated at $25–50 per tonne for mineralization) yield margins dependent on sustained price floors above $100 per tonne for scalability. Without robust pricing signals from policies like the EU ETS or emerging US mechanisms, reliance on grants could persist, as commercial contracts alone have generated under $13 million annually as of recent estimates.[84][85]

Cost-Benefit Analysis and Market Viability

The mineralization component of Carbfix's process incurs costs estimated at $10 to $25 per metric ton of CO2 injected, primarily covering dissolution in water, injection infrastructure, and monitoring, which compares favorably to traditional geological storage methods that often exceed $50 per ton due to higher compression and transport needs.[63][60] These figures derive from operational data at the Hellisheidi site, where economies of scale from co-locating with geothermal sources reduce energy inputs for CO2 capture and dissolution to under 20% of total injected volumes.[60] Independent techno-economic modeling for integration with a 500 MW combined-cycle power plant in the US projects levelized costs of $40-60 per ton for the full chain, factoring in capital expenditures of approximately $20-30 million for injection wells and pumps.[86] Benefits encompass not only cost efficiency but also enhanced safety and permanence, with field demonstrations showing 95% of injected CO2 mineralizing into carbonates within 1-2 years—far faster than natural processes—eliminating leakage risks associated with supercritical CO2 storage in saline aquifers.[60] This rapidity mitigates long-term monitoring expenses, estimated at $1-2 per ton annually, and supports verifiable credits under standards like those from the IPCC, potentially yielding net environmental gains by preventing atmospheric re-release over millennia.[63] Economically, the method's low upfront capital—under $5 million for initial pilot-scale facilities—enables deployment in basalt-rich regions without extensive site characterization, contrasting with sedimentary basin storage requiring billions in pipeline infrastructure.[87] Market viability remains contingent on carbon pricing exceeding $50 per ton, as evidenced by CarbFix2's design to achieve break-even at full-scale CCS integration, though current voluntary markets price mineralization credits at $100-200 per ton when bundled with direct air capture.[27][88] Partnerships, such as the 2024 collaboration with CarbonQuest for North American point-source deployment, signal scalability potential, projecting 1-5 million tons annual capacity by 2030 in suitable geologies, but global applicability is limited to 10-20% of emissions sources lacking widespread basalt availability.[84] Policy incentives, including US 45Q tax credits up to $50 per ton and EU ETS escalations toward $100 by 2030, could render it competitive against alternatives like bioenergy with CCS, though unresolved debates over additionality and lifecycle emissions from water acidification may temper investor confidence.[86][89] Overall, while site-specific advantages confer niche viability, broader adoption demands sustained high carbon prices and geological mapping to offset deployment risks.

Regulatory Developments and Incentives

In April 2021, the Icelandic parliament enacted legislation establishing a regulatory framework for the safe capture, transport, and geological storage of carbon dioxide (CO2), explicitly designed to incentivize private sector investment in innovative storage technologies like Carbfix's rapid mineralization process rather than relying solely on reforestation or afforestation.[90] This law aligns with Iceland's transposition of the European Union's Directive 2009/31/EC on the geological storage of CO2, integrated into national climate legislation to enable commercial-scale carbon capture and storage (CCS) projects.[91][20] A pivotal regulatory advancement occurred on May 12, 2025, when Icelandic authorities granted Carbfix Europe's first onshore geological storage permit under the EU CCS Directive, authorizing permanent CO2 mineralization in basaltic formations at the Hellisheiði site with an initial capacity exceeding 100,000 tonnes annually, subject to rigorous monitoring and exemption thresholds for research-scale injections below that volume.[14][92][51] This permit underscores Iceland's compliance with EU standards for site selection, risk assessment, and long-term liability transfer to the state post-closure, marking a shift from experimental to operational onshore CCS in the European Economic Area.[93] In terms of incentives, the EU Innovation Fund provided a €115 million grant to Carbfix's Coda Terminal project in 2023, supporting the development of a cross-border CO2 transport and storage hub with mineralization capacity targeting industrial emitters across Europe.[94] Additional EU funding, approximately $4.4 million, was allocated in June 2025 to Project Silverstone, a collaboration between ON Power and Carbfix for direct air capture integration at geothermal plants, recognizing the project's potential for verifiable carbon dioxide removal.[41] Iceland currently lacks dedicated national deployment incentives for carbon dioxide removal via mineralization, relying instead on EU mechanisms like emissions trading under the EU ETS, where stored CO2 can offset allowances, though critics note the absence of systematic financial supports for non-point-source capture.[91][93] These developments position Carbfix as a test case for scaling CCS under harmonized regulations, potentially influencing broader EU policy revisions to include permanence certifications for mineralized storage.[95]

Current Status and Future Outlook

Ongoing Operations and Capacity (as of 2025)

As of October 2025, Carbfix maintains active CO2 mineralization operations at ON Power's Hellisheidi and Nesjavellir geothermal power plants in Iceland, where captured gases are dissolved in water and injected into basaltic formations for rapid mineral storage. The Silverstone facility at Hellisheidi, officially opened on June 19, 2025, integrates advanced capture technology to sequester approximately 34,000 tonnes of CO2 annually, representing the bulk of the plant's emissions previously estimated at around 36,000 tonnes per year.[96][97] This expansion follows the receipt of Europe's first EU permit for onshore CO2 storage in May 2025, authorizing up to 106,000 tonnes annually under strict monitoring protocols.[51] In parallel, Carbfix handles storage for direct air capture (DAC) sources, notably Climeworks' Mammoth plant operational since 2024 with a capacity of 36,000 tonnes of CO2 per year, alongside smaller contributions from the earlier Orca facility (4,000 tonnes annually). At Nesjavellir, a pilot-scale CCS plant initiated injections in March 2023, capturing a fraction of the site's emissions on the order of thousands of tonnes yearly. Combined, these efforts yield a total mineralization rate of approximately 90,000 tonnes of CO2 per year, verified through ongoing seismic and geochemical monitoring to confirm permanence without leakage.[48][11][98] Capacity enhancements stem from phased infrastructure upgrades, including additional injection wells drilled since 2022, enabling sustained operations amid Iceland's basalt-rich geology. While the EU-funded Silverstone targets near-zero emissions at Hellisheidi by fully abating non-condensable gases, scalability remains tied to water and energy inputs for dissolution, with current throughput below the permitted maximum to prioritize safety and verification.[45][44]

Expansion Plans and International Partnerships

In May 2025, Carbfix obtained Europe's first permit for onshore geological storage of CO2 under EU directives, enabling expansion of mineralization operations at the Hellisheiði geothermal power plant in Iceland through the Silverstone project, in partnership with ON Power and funded by the European Innovation Fund.[92][41] This initiative builds on existing capacity to capture and inject larger volumes of CO2, with the facility operational as of June 2025 and targeting increased annual storage beyond the current 90,000 tonnes.[48] A key domestic expansion is the Coda Terminal, a planned hub in southwest Iceland for receiving, transporting, and mineralizing imported CO2, with preparatory works advancing toward operational commencement in 2025 and full scale by 2030; drilling of initial injection wells began in 2022.[99][52] This project positions Iceland as a cross-border storage site, leveraging basaltic rock formations for permanent sequestration.[48] Internationally, Carbfix signed a memorandum of understanding with CarbonQuest in December 2024 to deploy mineralization technology at industrial sites in North America, focusing on hard-to-abate sectors like manufacturing and enabling onsite CCS for both new and retrofit applications.[84][100] In December 2024, it partnered with Italy's National Research Council (CNR) to advance mineral carbon capture research, emphasizing interdisciplinary networks for global energy transition applications.[101] Carbfix has expressed intent to collaborate with Indian firms in basalt-rich regions for sector-specific CCS pilots, as outlined in November 2023, while the GECO project explores demonstration sites beyond Iceland to validate scalability in diverse geologies.[102][28] These efforts aim to commercialize the technology globally, though no large-scale deployments outside Iceland were operational as of October 2025.[83]

Broader Implications for Carbon Removal

The CarbFix mineralization process achieves permanent sequestration of CO2 by converting it into stable carbonate minerals within basalt formations, typically within months to two years, contrasting with geological storage methods that rely on physical trapping and face potential leakage over centuries.[60] This rapid mineral trapping mechanism addresses a core limitation in carbon dioxide removal (CDR) portfolios, where biological approaches like afforestation offer only temporary storage vulnerable to reversals from fires, decay, or land-use changes.[103] Empirical data from CarbFix injections at Hellisheidi, Iceland, confirm over 95% mineralization rates, providing verifiable evidence of durability without the monitoring burdens associated with supercritical CO2 plumes in sedimentary reservoirs.[49][18] Globally, the approach highlights geological constraints on CDR scalability, as suitable mafic and ultramafic rocks cover substantial land areas but viable injection sites are concentrated in regions like Iceland, parts of India, and oceanic basalts, limiting widespread deployment without extensive drilling.[104] Capacity estimates suggest potential for gigatonne-scale storage if expanded, yet energy demands for CO2 dissolution and injection—around 1-2 GJ per tonne—underscore trade-offs with renewables integration, unlike lower-energy ocean alkalinity enhancement but with fewer permanence guarantees.[105] CarbFix's model influences CDR economics by demonstrating costs as low as $25 per tonne in pilots, potentially competitive under carbon pricing above $50 per tonne, though scaling to teratonne needs requires site-specific assessments to avoid over-optimism from lab extrapolations.[60] Integration with direct air capture (DAC) exemplifies broader synergies, as seen in partnerships like Climeworks-CarbFix, which standardize verifiable permanent removals and support IPCC-recognized pathways for 5-15 GtCO2/year by 2050, filling gaps left by bioenergy with CCS (BECCS) amid land competition.[106] However, unresolved debates on induced seismicity and water use in arid basalt regions temper enthusiasm, emphasizing mineralization's role as a complementary rather than dominant CDR strategy in diverse portfolios.[107] This evidence-based permanence bolsters policy frameworks prioritizing durable removals, countering hype around reversible methods while highlighting the need for empirical validation over modeled potentials in academic projections often critiqued for optimism bias.[108]
