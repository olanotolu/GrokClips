Orthognathic surgery

Orthognathic surgery (/ˌɔːrθəɡˈnæθɪk/), also known as corrective jaw surgery or simply jaw surgery, is surgery designed to correct conditions of the jaw and lower face related to structure, growth, airway issues including sleep apnea, TMJ disorders, malocclusion problems primarily arising from skeletal disharmonies, and other orthodontic dental bite problems that cannot be treated easily with braces, as well as the broad range of facial imbalances, disharmonies, asymmetries, and malproportions where correction may be considered to improve facial aesthetics and self-esteem.[1] The origins of orthognathic surgery belong in oral surgery, and the basic operations related to the surgical removal of impacted or displaced teeth – especially where indicated by orthodontics to enhance dental treatments of malocclusion and dental crowding. One of the first published cases of orthognathic surgery was the one from Dr. Simon P. Hullihen in 1849. Originally coined by Harold Hargis, it was more widely popularised first in Germany and then most famously by Hugo Obwegeser who developed the bilateral sagittal split osteotomy (BSSO). This surgery is also used to treat congenital conditions such as cleft palate.[2] Typically surgery is performed via the mouth, where jaw bone is cut, moved, modified, and realigned to correct malocclusion or dentofacial deformity. The word "osteotomy" means the division of bone by means of a surgical cut. Surgery is generally performed after completion of facial growth, typically in late adolescence or early adulthood.[3] The "jaw osteotomy", either to the upper jaw or lower jaw (and usually both) allows (typically) an oral and maxillofacial surgeon to surgically align an arch of teeth, or the segment of a dental arch with its associated jawbone, relative to other segments of the dental arches. Working with orthodontists, the coordination of dental arches has primarily been directed to create a working occlusion. As such, orthognathic surgery is seen a secondary procedure supporting a more fundamental orthodontic objective. It is only recently, and especially with the evolution of oral and maxillofacial surgery in establishing itself as a primary medical specialty – as opposed to its long term status as a dental speciality – that orthognathic surgery has increasingly emerged as a primary treatment for obstructive sleep apnoea, as well as for primary facial proportionality or symmetry correction. The primary use of surgery to correct jaw disproportion or malocclusion is rare in most countries due to private health insurance and public hospital funding and health access issues. Nearly 5% of the general population is affected by dentofacial deformities that may require jaw correction procedures,[3][4][5] such as maxillary prognathisms, mandibular prognathisms, open bites, difficulty chewing, difficulty swallowing, temporomandibular joint dysfunction pains, excessive wear of the teeth, and receding chins. Increasingly, as people are more able to self-fund surgery, 3D facial diagnostic and design systems have emerged, as well as new operations that enable for a broad range of jaw correction procedures that have become readily accessible; in particularly in private maxillofacial surgical practice. These procedures include IMDO, SARME, GenioPaully, custom BIMAX, and custom PEEK procedures. These procedures are replacing the traditional role of certain orthognathic surgery operations that have for decades served wholly and primarily orthodontic or dental purposes.[6] Another development in the field is the new index called the index of orthognathic functional treatment need (IOFTN) that detects patients with the greatest need for orthognathic surgery as a part of their comprehensive treatment.[7] IOFTN has been validated internationally and detected over 90% of patients with greatest need for orthognathic surgery.[8]

Medical uses

Studies indicate that at least 5% of the US and UK general populations have dentofacial deformities associated with severe malocclusion and esthetic compromise, for which orthognathic surgery may be considered in a subset of cases.[9] Orthognathic surgery can be used to correct:
  • Gross jaw discrepancies (anteroposterior, vertical, or transverse discrepancies)[10]
  • Skeletofacial discrepancies associated with documented sleep apnea, airway defects, and soft tissue discrepancies
  • Skeletofacial discrepancies associated with documented temporomandibular joint pathology
A disproportionately grown upper or lower jaw causes dentofacial deformities. Chewing becomes problematic, and may also cause pain due to straining of the jaw muscle and bone. Deformities range from micrognathia, which is when the mandible does not grow far forward enough (over bite), and when the mandible grows too much, causing an under bite; all of which are uncomfortable. Also, a total maxilla osteotomy is used to treat the "long face syndrome", known as the skeletal open bite, idiopathic long face, hyper divergent face, total maxillary alveolar hyperplasia, and vertical maxillary excess. Prior to surgery, surgeons should take x-rays of the patient's jaw to determine the deformity, and to make a plan of procedures. Mandible osteotomies, or corrective jaw surgeries, benefit individuals who have difficulty chewing, swallowing, TMJ pains, excessive wear of the teeth, open bites, overbites, underbites, or a receding chin. The deformities listed above can be perfected by an osteotomy surgery of either the maxilla or mandible (whichever the deformity calls for), which is performed by an oral surgeon who is specialized in the working with both the upper and lower jaws.[12] Orthognathic surgery is also available as a very successful treatment (90–100%) for obstructive sleep apnea.[13]

Cleft lip and palate

Orthognathic surgery is a well established and widely used treatment option for insufficient growth of the maxilla in patients with an orofacial cleft.[14] Studies show that approximately 48% of patients with repaired complete unilateral cleft lip and palate and 63% with bilateral may require orthognathic surgery.[15] There is some debate regarding the timing of orthognathic procedures, to maximise the potential for natural growth of the facial skeleton.[16] In patients with cleft lip and palate, maxillary hypoplasia often results from impaired growth due to scar tissue from primary repair surgeries, which orthognathic surgery corrects.[15] Patient reported aesthetic outcomes of orthognathic surgery for cleft lip and palate appear to be of overall satisfaction,[17][18] despite complications that may arise. A 2013 systematic review comparing traditional orthognathic surgery with maxillary distraction osteogenesis found that the evidence was of low quality; it appeared that both procedures might be effective, but suggested distraction osteogenesis might reduce the incidence of long-term relapse.[19] The most common causes of cleft lip and palate are genetic and environmental factors. Clefts are known to occur due to folic acid deficiency.[20]

Risks

Although infrequent, there can be complications such as bleeding, swelling, infection, nausea and vomiting.[21] Infection rates ranging from approximately 2% to 15% are reported after orthognathic surgery; antibiotic prophylaxis reduces the risk of surgical site infections when the antibiotics are given during surgery and continued for longer than a day after the operation.[22] There can also be some post operative facial numbness due to nerve damage.[21] Sensory disturbances occur in up to 85% of patients initially, with 10-20% experiencing persistent numbness.[21] Diagnostics for nerve damage consist of:
  • brush-stroke directional discrimination (BSD)
  • touch detection threshold (TD)
  • warm/cold (W/C)
  • sharp/blunt discrimination (S/B)
  • electrophysiological tests (mental nerve blink reflex (BR), nerve conduction study (NCS), and cold (CDT) and warm (WDT) detection thresholds.[23]
The inferior alveolar nerve, which is a branch of the mandibular nerve, must be identified during surgery and worked around carefully in order to minimize nerve damage. The numbness may be either temporary, or more rarely, permanent.[24] Recovery from the nerve damage typically occurs within the first three months after surgery. Some 3D movements are considered riskier than other ones, such as maxillary impaction.[25]

Surgery

Orthognathic surgery is performed by maxillofacial or an oral surgeon or a plastic surgeon in collaboration with an orthodontist. It often includes braces before and after surgery, and retainers after the final removal of braces. Orthognathic surgery is often needed after reconstruction of cleft palate or other major craniofacial anomalies. Careful coordination between the surgeon and orthodontist is essential to ensure that the teeth will fit correctly after the surgery.

Orthognathic Surgery

Planning

Planning for the surgery usually involves input from a multidisciplinary team, including oral and maxillofacial surgeons, orthodontists, and occasionally a speech and language therapist. Although it depends on the reason for surgery, working with a speech and language therapist in advance can help minimize potential relapse. The surgery usually results in a noticeable change in the patient's face; a psychological assessment is occasionally required to assess patient's need for surgery and its predicted effect on the patient. Radiographs and photographs are taken to help in the planning. There is also advanced software that can predict the shape of the patient's face after surgery,[26][27][28][29][30] which is useful for the planning and also explaining the surgery to the patient and the patient's family.[31] Modern planning often incorporates virtual surgical planning (VSP) using 3D imaging for precise simulation and custom guides/plates.[32] Great care needs to be taken during the planning phase to maximize airway patency. Orthodontics are a critical component of orthognathic surgery. Traditionally the presurgical orthodontic phase can take as long as one year and undertaken with conventional metal braces.[33] However, these days new approaches and paradigms exist including surgery-first [34] And using clear aligner orthodontia (like Invisalign)[35][36]

Sagittal split osteotomy

This procedure is used to correct mandible retrusion and mandibular prognathism (over and under bite). First, a horizontal cut is made on the inner side of the ramus mandibulae, extending anteriorly to the anterior portion of the ascending ramus. The cut is then made inferiorly on the ascending ramus to the descending ramus, extending to the lateral border of the mandible in the area between the first and second molar. At this time, a vertical cut is made extending inferior to the body of the mandible, to the inferior border of the mandible. All cuts are made into the middle of the bone, where bone marrow is present. Then, a chisel is inserted into the pre existing cuts and tapped gently in all areas to split the mandible of the left and right side. From here, the mandible can be moved either forwards or backwards. If sliding backwards, the distal segment must be trimmed to provide room in order to slide the mandible backwards. Lastly, the jaw is stabilized using titanium plates and screws for rigid fixation, allowing limited mouth opening during recovery. Elastics may be used to guide occlusion, but wiring shut is rarely required in modern practice. Recovery involves a soft/liquid diet for 4-6 weeks.[37]

Genioplasty osteotomy (intra-oral)

This procedure is used for the advancement (movement forward) or retraction (movement backwards) of the chin. A horizontal vestibular incision (2-3 cm) is made inside the mouth between the lower lip and gums, exposing the anterior mandible. Then, soft tissue of the mandible is detached from the bone; done by stripping attaching tissues. A horizontal osteotomy is then made inferior to the tooth roots, bilaterally, where bone cuts are made vertically inferior, extending to the inferior border of the mandible, thereby detaching the bony segments of the mandible. The bony segments are stabilized with titanium plates; no fixation (binding of the jaw) necessary. If advancement is indicated for the chin, there are inert products available to implant onto the mandible, utilizing titanium screws, bypassing bone cuts.[38][39]

Rapid palatal expansion osteotomy

When a patient has a constricted (oval shape) maxilla, but normal mandible, many orthodontists request a rapid palatal expansion. This consists of the surgeon making horizontal cuts on the lateral border of the maxilla, extending anteriorly to the inferior border of the nasal cavity. At this time, a chisel designed for the nasal septum is utilized to detach the maxilla from the cranial base. Then, a pterygoid chisel, which is a curved chisel, is used on the left and right side of the maxilla to detach the pterygoid plates. Care must be taken as to not injure the inferior palatine artery. Prior to the procedure, the orthodontist has an orthopedic appliance attached to the maxilla teeth, bilaterally, extending over the palate. After surgery, following a 5-7 day latency period, the patient or orthodontist activates the jackscrew laterally by turning it 0.25 mm twice daily (total 0.5 mm/day) until the desired expansion (typically 7-10 mm over 2-4 weeks) is achieved. The device remains in place for retention (3-6 months).[40] The expansion of the maxilla may take up to eight weeks with retention.

Post Operation

After orthognathic surgery, patients typically follow an initial all-liquid diet for 1-2 weeks, progressing to soft, no-chew foods for a total of 6-8 weeks to support healing and prevent strain on the surgical sites.[22] A high-calorie, high-protein intake is recommended during this period. Weight loss is common due to reduced appetite and dietary restrictions. Recovery time varies: initial healing for minor procedures may take a few weeks, while more complex surgeries can require up to 12 months for full bone remodeling and orthodontic completion.[21] Pain is managed with prescribed medications, though some patients experience minimal discomfort due to temporary numbness from nerve involvement (e.g., inferior alveolar nerve paresthesia, affecting sensation in the lower lip/chin for months to a year).[23] Prophylactic antibiotics are routinely prescribed to reduce infection risk.[22] Swelling is prominent around the jaw, peaking at 2-3 days post-op, with possible bruising; apply ice packs and elevate the head for the first 48-72 hours to minimize it. Most swelling resolves within 2-4 weeks, though residual effects may persist for 3-6 months.[21]

Recovery

All dentofacial osteotomies require an initial stabilization period of 2–6 weeks, during which swelling and discomfort subside. Complete bony union typically occurs within 2–3 months, with full remodeling taking 3–12 months depending on the procedure and individual factors.[41][42] The jaw may sometimes be restricted in movement using elastics (wires are rarely used in modern practice) for 1–4 weeks to guide occlusion.[43] Patients should follow a liquid or soft diet for the first 4–6 weeks to support healing and avoid complications. Swelling peaks at 2–3 days post-surgery and can be managed with ice packs for 24–48 hours, head elevation, and prescribed medications. Pain is typically controlled with analgesics for the first week, though milder discomfort may persist longer.[44][45] Immediately after surgery, adhere to infection prevention measures, including oral hygiene with gentle rinsing or brushing and a course of prophylactic antibiotics as prescribed. Regular mouth cleaning is essential for oral health regardless of surgery. Patients should not drive or operate machinery while taking strong painkillers or until cleared by their surgeon. Most patients return to work or school in 1–3 weeks for light activities, or 2–6 weeks for more demanding roles, but must follow recovery guidelines for 6–8 weeks. Sensory changes like numbness may last months but usually resolve.[41][42]

History

Mandible and maxilla osteotomies have been performed since the mid-19th century, but modern techniques emerged in the mid-20th century. A key advancement was the bilateral sagittal split osteotomy (BSSO) for the mandible, developed by R. Trauner and H. Obwegeser in 1955, which became a standard for correcting dentofacial deformities such as malocclusion and prognathism.[12] Advances have been made in the procedures and anesthesia used. By the 1980s, these osteotomies were commonly applied to address a range of deformities, including receding chins, and to alleviate pain associated with temporomandibular joint disorder (TMJ). Prior to 1991, some patients undergoing dentofacial osteotomy still had third molars (wisdom teeth) and had them removed during surgery. A study by Lacey and Colcleugh involving 83 patients from 1987 to 1991 examined the risks of combining the procedures.[46] Patients were divided into two groups: those who had and those who did not have third molar extraction during the osteotomy. The study found that 73% of infections (11 out of 15 cases of hardware infection or exposure) occurred in patients who had third molars removed simultaneously, indicating an increased risk of infection (approximately 30% infection rate in the extraction group compared to lower rates without).[46] The data suggested that performing osteotomy and third molar extraction at the same time significantly heightens infection risk. Advances in surgical techniques have allowed performance under local anesthesia with intravenous sedation. Raffaini et al. reported on this approach in 2002, based on four years of multicenter experience.[47] Prior to such developments, patients were fully sedated and typically hospitalized for 2–3 days post-surgery for anesthesia recovery. These advancements enable broader application to various jaw regions with faster recovery, less pain, and no need for hospitalization, improving overall efficiency.[47] Jaw osteotomy procedures have evolved from techniques established in the mid-20th century. The original mandible and maxilla osteotomy methods remain foundational, valued for their simplicity and effectiveness in correcting dentofacial deformities.
