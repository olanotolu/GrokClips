City of Djinns

City of Djinns: A Year in Delhi (1993) is a travelogue by Scottish writer William Dalrymple recounting his experiences residing in India's capital city while investigating its historical layers, cultural diversity, and folklore centered on djinns—pre-Islamic spirits believed to inhabit the region.[1]
Published by Penguin Books, the work blends personal narrative with archival research to trace Delhi's evolution across its seven successive ancient cities, from the legendary Indraprastha of the Mahabharata epic to the Mughal and British eras, culminating in the modern metropolis shaped by the 1947 Partition.[1][2]
Dalrymple encounters a range of figures, including Sufi mystics, eunuchs guarding imperial tombs, and Partition survivors, to illuminate the city's enduring mystical and social undercurrents.[1]
The book received the Thomas Cook Travel Book Award in 1994 and the Sunday Times Young British Writer of the Year Award, recognizing its scholarly yet accessible portrayal of Delhi's palimpsest-like history.[3][2]

Publication and Background

Author and Context

William Dalrymple, born William Benedict Hamilton-Dalrymple on 20 March 1965 in Scotland, is a historian, travel writer, and broadcaster specializing in South Asian history and culture. Educated at Ampleforth College and Trinity College, Cambridge, where he studied history, Dalrymple's early career focused on travel literature, with his debut book In Xanadu (1989) recounting a journey retracing Marco Polo's route from Jerusalem to China. His move to Delhi marked a pivotal shift toward deeper engagement with Indian history. [4] In 1989, at age 24, Dalrymple arrived in Delhi on a whim during a period of personal exploration following university, captivated by the city's chaotic vibrancy and historical depth. He settled there, living modestly and conducting extensive research amid the urban sprawl, which spanned six years and informed his second book, City of Djinns: A Year in Delhi (1993). The work emerged from this immersive experience, combining autobiographical elements of daily life in contemporary Delhi—marked by pollution, Partition-era migrants, and bureaucratic hurdles—with archival dives into the city's millennia-spanning past, from Indraprastha of the Mahabharata to Mughal ruins. Dalrymple's approach privileged firsthand observation and oral histories from locals, including Sufi dervishes and elderly residents, over purely academic sources.[5] [6] [7] The book's context reflects the late 1980s transition in India, just before economic liberalization in 1991, when Delhi grappled with overpopulation exceeding 8 million and the lingering scars of the 1947 Partition, which displaced millions and reshaped demographics. Dalrymple, an outsider yet empathetic chronicler, sought to pierce the "veil" of the city's djinns—metaphorical spirits embodying forgotten histories—through rigorous on-the-ground inquiry, distinguishing his narrative from colonial-era Orientalist tropes by emphasizing causal continuities in Delhi's cycles of rise and ruin.[5] [8]

Research and Writing Process

Dalrymple's research for City of Djinns spanned six years during his extended residence in New Delhi, beginning with an initial year-long immersion in 1989 alongside his wife, Olivia Fraser.[6][9] This period involved renting a flat in the city and systematically uncovering its historical strata through on-site explorations of narrow alleys, mosques, abandoned ruins, tombs, and other sites that embodied Delhi's layered past.[10] A core method was direct engagement with Delhi's residents, including interviews with diverse figures such as pigeon fanciers, Sufi mystics, Muslim healers, musicians, calligraphers, philosophers, eunuchs (hijras), and survivors of events like the 1947 Partition.[10][11][12] These oral accounts provided firsthand insights into cultural practices, supernatural beliefs, and social dynamics, complementing Dalrymple's readings of historical texts, including medieval chronicles by observers like Ibn Battuta.[11] While primary archival work in institutions like India's National Archives featured more prominently in his later books, City of Djinns emphasized ethnographic fieldwork and secondary historical synthesis over unpublished documents.[6] The writing process integrated autobiography, travel observations, and historical vignettes into a non-linear narrative that reverses chronology, commencing with contemporary Delhi and regressing through epochs to ancient origins, evoking a "memento" structure to mirror the city's palimpsest-like accumulation of eras.[13][14] This approach allowed Dalrymple to juxtapose personal anecdotes—such as encounters with eccentric landlords or urban mystics—with broader causal analyses of Delhi's cycles of rise, ruin, and rebirth, grounded in verifiable historical patterns rather than speculative folklore alone.[11] The result was a cohesive portrait prioritizing empirical traces of human agency over unverified supernatural attributions, though djinn lore served as a cultural lens for interpreting persistent local beliefs.

Publication Details

City of Djinns: A Year in Delhi was first published in 1993 by HarperCollins in London.[15] The initial hardcover edition bore the ISBN 000215725X and comprised 352 pages, including drawings by Olivia Fraser.[16] A paperback edition followed shortly thereafter, released on 20 September 1993 with ISBN 9780006375951.[15] Subsequent editions have been issued by various publishers, reflecting the book's enduring availability. Penguin Books released a reprint in the United States on 25 March 2003, with ISBN 9780142001004 and 352 pages.[2] Bloomsbury Publishing brought out an edition on 20 January 2017, assigned ISBN 9385936557.[4] These reissues maintain the core content while updating formats for contemporary readers.

Content and Structure

Narrative Framework

City of Djinns: A Year in Delhi employs a hybrid narrative framework that fuses personal memoir with historical inquiry and travelogue elements, centering on William Dalrymple's experiences during 1989–1990 while residing in the city with his wife Olivia. This first-person account serves as the chronological spine, documenting daily life in a dilapidated haveli in Old Delhi, interactions with diverse residents such as eunuchs and Sufi mystics, and participation in local customs like Diwali celebrations, which provide organic transitions into broader explorations.[17] The structure eschews strict linearity, instead layering contemporary observations atop excavated historical vignettes to portray Delhi as a palimpsest of eras coexisting uneasily.[18] The historical narrative unfolds in approximate reverse-chronological fashion, commencing with 20th-century upheavals such as the 1947 Partition riots—which displaced over 12 million people and killed up to 2 million—and the 1857 Indian Rebellion, before regressing through the British Raj, Mughal dynasty, and medieval sultanates like that of Muhammad bin Tughlaq, who shifted the capital from Daulatabad in 1327. This archaeological approach, likened to peeling an onion, integrates archival research, site visits to ruins like the Red Fort (built 1639–1648), and interviews with surviving eyewitnesses or descendants, revealing causal chains from imperial decays to modern urban decay.[18][11] Dalrymple's method prioritizes primary evidence, such as British colonial records and oral histories, over secondary interpretations, underscoring how events like the 1857 siege of Delhi—where rebels briefly held the city before British recapture on September 20, 1857—reshaped demographic and architectural landscapes.[17] Mythical and supernatural motifs, embodied in the djinns of Islamic folklore—invisible spirits believed to inhabit ruins and influence human affairs—infuse the framework with a speculative dimension, contrasting empirical history with popular beliefs that attribute unexplained phenomena, such as hauntings in the Lutyens' Delhi bungalows, to these entities. Dalrymple collects such tales from locals, including accounts of djinns aiding or cursing rulers, to illustrate cultural persistence amid modernization, without endorsing supernatural causation but noting their role in shaping collective memory. Anecdotal digressions, drawn from encounters like those with Partition refugees or bazaar storytellers, maintain narrative momentum, ensuring the framework remains engaging rather than didactic, while cross-referencing verifiable facts like the city's seven successive incarnations—from Indraprastha in the Mahabharata epic (circa 1000 BCE) to Shahjahanabad founded in 1639.[19] This interwoven style yields a comprehensive yet intimate depiction, where personal epiphanies illuminate causal realities, such as how Mughal opulence funded by land revenue systems precipitated later economic fragilities.[18]

Key Historical Episodes Covered

Dalrymple recounts the ancient origins of Delhi, linking it to the legendary city of Indraprastha from the Mahabharata epic, where archaeological evidence and oral traditions suggest early settlements dating back over 3,000 years, though he notes the blend of myth and sparse material proof in these foundations.[11] In the medieval period, the narrative covers the Delhi Sultanate's successive capitals, including Qutb-ud-din Aibak's Qila Rai Pithora around 1193, Alauddin Khilji's Siri in the early 14th century, and Muhammad bin Tughlaq's ill-fated shift to Daulatabad in 1327 followed by his return and construction of Jahanpanah, highlighting the sultans' architectural ambitions amid political instability and famines that killed thousands.[11][18] The Mughal era features prominently, with emphasis on Shah Jahan's founding of Shahjahanabad in 1639, a walled city that became the empire's cultural zenith before its decline; Dalrymple details devastating invasions, such as Timur's 1398 sack that massacred over 100,000 inhabitants and razed structures, and Nadir Shah's 1739 plunder, where Persian forces killed an estimated 30,000 Delhi residents in a single day, looted treasures including the Peacock Throne valued at £87.5 million, and triggered further Mughal fragmentation.[20][17] British colonial episodes include the 1857 Indian Rebellion, during which rebels seized Delhi on May 11, holding it for three months before British forces recaptured it on September 20, executing thousands in reprisals and demolishing much of the city, reducing its population from 600,000 to under 100,000; Dalrymple also touches on the later imperial capital's design by Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker, inaugurated in 1931 after displacing 40,000 residents.[17][21] Post-independence traumas are depicted through the 1947 Partition, which caused over 1 million deaths across India and Pakistan, with Delhi absorbing 500,000 Hindu and Sikh refugees from Pakistan while 350,000 Muslims fled to Pakistan amid riots that killed thousands locally, inverting the city's Muslim-majority demographics; Dalrymple further addresses the 1984 anti-Sikh pogroms following Indira Gandhi's assassination on October 31, resulting in at least 3,000 deaths in Delhi alone, as recounted via survivor testimonies.[17][22][11]

Themes and Elements

Delhi's Historical Layers

Dalrymple depicts Delhi as a stratified urban palimpsest, where successive rulers constructed new capitals atop the ruins of predecessors, creating a tangible archive of conquest and decay spanning over two millennia. The deepest layer referenced is the mythical Indraprastha, the Pandavas' capital in the Mahabharata epic, traditionally dated to around 1400 BCE, though Dalrymple notes its invocation in local lore as the origin of settled life amid the Yamuna River's floodplains.[23] [24] This pre-Islamic foundation gave way to Rajput strongholds, notably the Tomara dynasty's Lal Kot fortress established in 736 CE, which Anangpal Tomar expanded into a walled city symbolizing Hindu resistance before its eclipse by Muslim incursions.[25] The Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) then imposed five overlapping cities: Qutb-ud-din Aibak's foundational Qila Rai Pithora, Alauddin Khilji's Siri (1303) with its famine-defying granaries, and the Tughlaq expansions including Tughlaqabad (1321) and Firoz Shah Kotla (1354), whose ruins Dalrymple traverses to evoke the sultans' transient tyrannies and engineering feats.[26] [11] The Mughal overlay, peaking under Shah Jahan (1628–1658), birthed the seventh city of Shahjahanabad (1639–1648), encompassing the Red Fort, Jama Masjid, and Chandni Chowk bazaar, whose opulent yet decaying vestiges Dalrymple animates through accounts of court eunuchs and imperial intrigues drawn from Persian chronicles and traveler testimonies like those of François Bernier.[27] [28] British intervention superimposed an eighth layer with the 1911 announcement of New Delhi, realized between 1911 and 1931 under architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker, whose neoclassical avenues and domes contrasted Mughal arabesques while incorporating indigenous motifs; Dalrymple critiques this imperial hybridity amid remnants of the 1857 Rebellion, when sepoys besieged Delhi, toppled Mughal nominal rule, and prompted brutal reprisals that razed much of Shahjahanabad.[17] [18] The 1947 Partition violence forms the most recent stratum, displacing 800,000 Muslims from Old Delhi and influxing equivalent Punjabi Hindus and Sikhs, fracturing communal fabrics and swelling refugee camps that Dalrymple witnesses as echoes of prior cataclysms, underscoring cycles of migration, massacre, and reconstruction.[17] [29] Through archival dives and encounters with living relics—like hijra communities linked to Tughlaq-era precedents and faded Anglo-Indians evoking Raj nostalgia—Dalrymple argues these layers persist not as inert stone but as spectral influences shaping contemporary Delhi's chaotic vitality.[30]

Mythology of Djinns and Supernatural Beliefs

In Islamic tradition, djinns (or jinn) are supernatural beings created by God from smokeless fire, predating humanity and possessing free will akin to humans, with the capacity for moral choice, including obedience or rebellion against divine will.[31] They inhabit a parallel realm called al-Ghaib, invisible to humans yet capable of interacting with the physical world through shapeshifting, possession, or influence over events, as referenced in the Quran (e.g., Surah Al-Jinn, which describes their creation and a group encountering the Prophet Muhammad).[31] Some djinns serve benevolent purposes, such as aiding King Solomon in construction per Quranic accounts, while others, led by Iblis (a disobedient djinn cast out for refusing to bow to Adam), engage in deception or harm, including inducing mental or physical affliction through possession.[31][32] William Dalrymple's City of Djinns integrates these mythological elements into Delhi's layered history, portraying the city as a repository of djinn lore where supernatural entities are believed to persist in the ruins of its seven successive incarnations, ensuring cyclical regeneration amid repeated conquests and demolitions.[1] Local folklore, as encountered by Dalrymple during his research in abandoned tombs, mosques, and alleys, attributes Delhi's endurance to fire-formed djinns who safeguard its phoenix-like rebirth, a motif blending pre-Islamic Arabian roots with Indo-Islamic syncretism.[33] These beliefs reflect causal interpretations of historical cataclysms—such as invasions by Timur in 1398 or Nadir Shah in 1739—as djinn-orchestrated trials rather than mere human agency. Contemporary supernatural practices in Delhi, which Dalrymple observes and documents, center on sites like the 14th-century Feroz Shah Kotla fortress, where djinns are venerated as "jinn-saints" or protective babas residing in niches and alcoves.[34] Devotees, spanning Muslims and Hindus, gather weekly—often Thursdays—to inscribe petitions (shikwa) on walls, seeking intervention for ailments, legal disputes, or financial woes, later burning granted wishes' remnants and distributing food like biryani in thanks.[34] Such rituals echo broader Indo-Islamic convictions in djinns' apotropaic powers, with historical precedents in Sufi traditions of exorcism or capture, as Dalrymple recounts through meetings with ascetics claiming dominion over these entities.[13] While empirical evidence for djinn agency remains absent, these persistent beliefs underscore a cultural realism wherein supernatural causality fills explanatory gaps in personal and historical narratives, undiminished by modern skepticism.[32] Other Delhi landmarks, including the 16th-century Jamali Kamali tomb, harbor tales of hauntings and possessions, where djinns purportedly manifest as whispers or apparitions amid overgrown ruins, reinforcing the city's aura as a liminal space between worlds. Dalrymple's narrative highlights how these folklore elements—rooted in Quranic ontology yet adapted to local ecology—coexist with tangible history, as residents attribute unexplained misfortunes or fortunes to djinn influence rather than coincidence.[35] This interplay of myth and materiality, drawn from oral traditions and eyewitness accounts, illustrates Delhi's supernatural worldview as a resilient framework for interpreting causality in a city marked by impermanence.[36]

Social and Cultural Dynamics

Dalrymple portrays Delhi's social fabric as a palimpsest shaped by successive invasions, migrations, and upheavals, with the 1947 Partition serving as a pivotal rupture that decimated the indigenous Muslim aristocracy and Urdu-speaking elite, driving out an estimated two-thirds of the city's Muslim population in the ensuing violence and mass exodus.[17][14] This demographic cataclysm, coupled with the influx of over 500,000 Punjabi Hindu and Sikh refugees fleeing reciprocal massacres in Pakistan, fundamentally realigned social hierarchies, supplanting traditional Mughal-linked families with a more mercantile, Punjabi-dominated middle class that infused the city with new linguistic, culinary, and entrepreneurial norms.[17] The resulting cultural friction manifests in Dalrymple's accounts of faded nawabs clinging to decayed havelis and rituals, juxtaposed against the brash vitality of refugee-descended traders reshaping Old Delhi's bazaars.[29] The book underscores the marginal yet enduring role of subaltern communities, such as the hijras—India's traditional third-gender eunuchs—who operate in tightly knit guilds enforcing territorial "parishes" and deriving income from ritual blessings at births and weddings, a practice rooted in their historical guardianship of Mughal harems.[27][37] Dalrymple's immersion reveals their insular world of elective surgeries, devotional cults to figures like Guru-ji, and social ostracism, highlighting a continuum of gender ambiguity and spiritual liminality that persists amid Delhi's stratified castes and sects.[38] Parallel encounters with Sufi mystics and folk healers illustrate syncretic cultural undercurrents, where Hindu and Islamic practices interweave in qawwali sessions and shrine visitations, fostering a resilient, if beleaguered, cosmopolitanism in the face of post-Partition homogenization.[38] Supernatural beliefs in djinns—fiery spirits from Islamic lore said to inhabit ruins and influence human affairs—permeate everyday social interactions, as evidenced by Dalrymple's dialogues with exorcists and residents attributing misfortunes to jinn possession, a worldview that bridges pre-Islamic folklore with contemporary urban anxieties.[17] This ethereal dimension underscores causal attributions in Delhi's underclass, where economic precarity and bureaucratic inertia amplify reliance on amulets and pirs over rational remedies, reflecting a cultural psychology attuned to the city's layered traumas rather than progressive secularism.[14] Overall, Dalrymple depicts social dynamics as a tension between archival grandeur and chaotic present, with class, religion, and gender intersecting in ways that privilege adaptive resilience over uniform integration.[29]

Reception and Analysis

Critical Reviews

Critics praised City of Djinns for its engaging blend of personal memoir, historical research, and vivid portrayal of Delhi's multifaceted identity. Kirkus Reviews described it as "a charming portrait of the ancient Indian capital of Delhi by a talented young British travel writer," highlighting Dalrymple's affable style and explorations of the city's post-Partition changes, hidden customs, and religious coexistence.[30] The New York Times noted its "striking evocation" of Delhi's many faces across centuries, crediting the book with uncovering historical layers through the author's year-long immersion.[39] Reviewers commended Dalrymple's narrative skill in evoking Delhi's past via topographical details and anecdotes, particularly his sympathetic engagement with the city's mystical and cultural elements, such as beliefs in djinns and encounters with eunuchs and squatters.[40] The Independent observed that the book avoids the pitfalls common to sophomore efforts following Dalrymple's debut In Xanadu, succeeding as a pilgrim-like response to Delhi rather than a conventional travelogue.[40] Some critiques pointed to limitations in scope and tone. Kirkus characterized the work as a "warm look" suitable for newcomers but "not a heavyweight experience," suggesting it prioritizes accessibility over exhaustive depth.[30] The Independent faulted occasional patronizing attitudes toward Delhi's simpler residents, an overemphasis on the author's wife's colonial ancestor William Fraser—including a tangential Scottish detour—and a concluding episode that verges on fictional invention, introducing uncertainty to the memoir's authenticity.[40] Despite these reservations, the book was widely regarded as an insightful entry into Delhi's enigmatic character, bolstered by 16 pages of black-and-white illustrations.[30]

Awards and Recognition

City of Djinns received the Thomas Cook Travel Book Award in 1994, recognizing its excellence in travel literature. This accolade highlighted the book's immersive exploration of Delhi's history and culture, distinguishing it among contemporary works in the genre.[1] The book also won the Sunday Times Young British Writer of the Year Award in 1994, awarded to Dalrymple for his debut non-fiction work's narrative depth and scholarly approach.[41] These honors underscored the text's critical acclaim for blending personal memoir with rigorous historical research, establishing Dalrymple's reputation in travel writing.[42]

Criticisms and Viewpoints

Some postcolonial scholars have critiqued City of Djinns for employing orientalist tropes that exoticize Delhi as a chaotic, labyrinthine space of mystery and decay, thereby reinforcing a Western gaze of Eastern inferiority.[43] For example, the narrative's descriptions of the city as "a labyrinth, a city of palaces, an open gutter" are seen as constructing India as inherently backward and in need of external order, while portraying locals as indifferent to their historical heritage, such as the claim that "no one is thinking too much about these old historical places."[43] The book has also been accused of evoking imperialist nostalgia by lamenting post-independence demographic shifts, such as the population surge from 918,000 in 1941 to 1,800,000 in 1951 due to Punjabi influx, which Dalrymple depicts as vulgarizing the city's elegance, and through interviews that probe the justification of British rule, as with Iris Portal.[43] These elements, critics argue, selectively emphasize the Raj's perceived civility to foster a longing for colonial stability over objective analysis of empire's disruptions.[43] [44] Countering such views, other analyses portray Dalrymple's work as post-orientalist, drawing on empirical immersion and local voices to challenge rather than perpetuate colonial binaries, with his dismissal of rigid postcolonial theory underscoring a focus on lived historical continuities.[45] Reviews from established outlets like Kirkus have highlighted the book's affable style and vivid depiction of Delhi's resilient layers—from eunuchs to colonial remnants—without noting structural flaws, positioning it as an accessible entry to the city's multifaceted past rather than a rigorous academic treatise.[30] Among Indian readers, viewpoints vary: some appreciate the unvarnished acknowledgment of invasions' atrocities against Hindus, supported by archival evidence, while others question an overemphasis on Mughal syncretism at the expense of pre-Islamic Hindu foundations, though such concerns often stem from broader skepticism of Dalrymple's oeuvre rather than specific textual errors in City of Djinns.[46]

Legacy and Adaptations

Cultural Influence

City of Djinns has shaped international understandings of Delhi's cultural identity by highlighting its stratified history and persistent folklore, including beliefs in djinns as guardians of the city's ruins, thereby reinforcing the metropolis's reputation as a repository of layered civilizations.[47] This narrative approach, blending archival research with eyewitness accounts from the early 1990s, has informed subsequent cultural studies examining Delhi's ecological and social interconnections.[48] The book's vivid depictions of Mughal remnants, Partition-era migrations, and contemporary urban life have influenced travel recommendations, encouraging visitors to explore lesser-known historical layers beyond major monuments like the Red Fort. For instance, it has been cited in guides for enriching trips through India by animating Delhi's past for modern explorers.[49] Such portrayals underscore causal continuities between ancient invasions—spanning Tughlaq displacements in the 14th century to British colonial overlays—and present-day social dynamics, countering oversimplified views of the city as merely chaotic.[9] In literary circles, Dalrymple's work exemplifies a revival of immersive travelogues that prioritize empirical historical inquiry over romantic exoticism, impacting genres focused on South Asian urbanism. Academic engagements, such as those analyzing its fusion of biography, memoir, and historiography, attest to its role in prompting reevaluations of Delhi's cultural resilience amid repeated reconstructions.[50] While not without critiques of selective emphasis on elite narratives, its enduring citations in cultural discourse affirm a legacy of fostering appreciation for verifiable historical causalities over mythic distortions.[44]

Adaptations

"City of Djinns" served as the basis for an episode in the BBC documentary series Indian Journeys, presented by author William Dalrymple.[51] The series, consisting of three parts, explores India's sacred geography, with the "City of Djinns" episode focusing on Delhi's layered history, supernatural lore, and cultural intricacies as depicted in the book.[52] First broadcast on BBC World on August 20, 2002, the program features Dalrymple retracing the book's narrative through on-location footage of the city's ruins, tombs, and contemporary life.[51] No feature films, stage productions, or radio adaptations of the book have been produced.[26] The documentary remains the primary audiovisual rendition, emphasizing empirical exploration over dramatization and aligning with the book's travelogue style.
