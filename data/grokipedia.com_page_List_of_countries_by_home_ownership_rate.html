A list of countries by home ownership rate compiles statistics on the proportion of households or dwellings that are owner-occupied rather than rented or otherwise non-owner tenured, typically measured through national censuses, surveys, or housing registries and expressed as a percentage.[1] These rates vary dramatically worldwide, exceeding 95% in nations such as Romania, Cuba, and Vietnam due to historical factors like rapid post-socialist privatizations that distributed state housing to residents, while falling below 50% in Switzerland (around 42%), Germany (approximately 51%), and Hong Kong (about 22%), where stringent land-use regulations, high urban densities, and cultural preferences for renting predominate.[2][3] Across OECD countries, average rates hover around 70%, with empirical decompositions showing that easing mortgage down-payment constraints and favorable tax treatments for owners have driven increases over decades, though persistent cross-country gaps underscore the role of cultural norms and institutional barriers like zoning restrictions that limit supply and inflate prices in low-ownership economies.[4][5] High ownership correlates with greater household wealth accumulation via property appreciation but also exposes owners to market risks, whereas low rates in rental-heavy societies facilitate labor mobility yet contribute to intergenerational asset divides absent compensatory policies.[6][7]

Methodology and Definitions

Home Ownership Rate Definition

The home ownership rate quantifies the prevalence of owner-occupancy in a given population or jurisdiction, typically expressed as the proportion of occupied residential dwellings or households where the residents hold legal title to the property. This includes properties owned outright without outstanding debt as well as those subject to mortgages or other secured loans, but excludes rental arrangements, squatting, or other non-ownership tenures.[8] In practice, owner-occupancy requires the title holder to reside in the dwelling as their primary residence, distinguishing it from investment properties or secondary homes.[9] Measurement approaches vary by data provider, affecting precise comparability across countries. The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) defines the rate as the share of households owning their dwelling, either outright or with a mortgage, derived from national household surveys or censuses focused on tenure status.[8] Eurostat, for European Union statistics, calculates it as the percentage of individuals aged 15 and over living in owner-occupied dwellings, where ownership is confirmed by a title deed regardless of payment status.[9] Similarly, the U.S. Census Bureau measures it as the ratio of owner-occupied housing units to total occupied housing units, emphasizing primary residences.[10] These definitions prioritize empirical tenure data but may diverge in unit of analysis (households versus individuals) or inclusion criteria, such as treatment of subsidized housing or informal ownership in developing economies.

Data Sources and Collection Methods

Home ownership rates are primarily derived from national population censuses and household surveys conducted by official statistical agencies in each country. These sources capture tenure statusâ€”typically categorizing dwellings as owner-occupied (with or without a mortgage), rented (market or subsidized), or other arrangementsâ€”through self-reported data from representative samples or full enumerations. EU-SILC defines owner-occupancy as households where at least the respondent or their spouse/partner owns the dwelling, with data weighted for population representativeness and adjusted for non-response. National adaptations ensure consistency, but base questions on tenure are standardized to enable cross-country aggregation by Eurostat.[12][13] The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) compiles and harmonizes home ownership data for its 38 member countries through the Affordable Housing Database, drawing on EU-SILC for European nations (e.g., 2022 data for most) and national equivalents elsewhere, such as Australia's Survey of Income and Housing (2019-20), Canada's Survey of Financial Security (2019), Japan's Comprehensive Survey of Living Conditions (2021), and the United States' Survey of Income and Program Participation (2021). OECD calculations adjust for definitional differences, like inclusion of mortgages in ownership, and focus on the household head's age (often 25-64) to enhance comparability, though coverage varies by survey frequency and sample size.[11][4] For non-OECD and developing countries, data often stem from national household income and expenditure surveys or demographic health surveys, aggregated by bodies like the United Nations or World Bank, though coverage is sparser and less frequent. These rely on probabilistic sampling frames from censuses, with tenure questions embedded in broader modules on living standards, but methodological variancesâ€”such as differing thresholds for "ownership" (e.g., freehold vs. usufruct rights)â€”limit global standardization without secondary harmonization.[14]

Comparability Challenges and Limitations

Comparisons of home ownership rates across countries face significant challenges due to variations in the definition of tenure. For instance, subsidized rental data is unavailable in countries including Australia, Canada, Chile, and the United States, leading to potential undercounting of non-market tenures and overestimation of ownership where social housing is reclassified.[11] External shocks, such as the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020-2021, further compromised data quality through reduced interview response rates and inconsistent timing, particularly in Europe.[11] Temporal misalignmentâ€”data from different yearsâ€”compounds issues, as rates fluctuate with economic cycles, preventing direct cross-sectional analysis without adjustments.[16] Classification inconsistencies in subsidized or social housing pose additional hurdles; Austria and Denmark, for example, attribute all such tenants to market (private) rent, unlike other OECD peers, distorting tenure distributions.[11] Income-based quintile analyses, often used to contextualize rates, employ gross income in Chile, Colombia, Mexico, and the US, while net income remains unadjusted for taxes or benefits in the UK and TÃ¼rkiye due to data constraints, undermining subgroup comparability.[11] Overall, the absence of standardized international protocols results in datasets where apparent ownership disparities may reflect methodological artifacts rather than substantive differences, necessitating caution in policy inferences.[16][5]

Historical and Temporal Trends

Post-Communist Privatization Effects (1990s-2000s)

Following the dissolution of communist regimes in 1989â€“1991, governments across Central and Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union enacted mass housing privatization programs, typically allowing sitting tenants to acquire state- or municipal-owned apartments at nominal fees or free of charge. These initiatives, often legislated within the first two years of transition (e.g., Russia's 1991 Fundamentals of Housing Policy and Hungary's 1990 compensation-based sales), aimed to establish private property rights, reduce state fiscal burdens, and align housing systems with market economies. This contrasted with gradual market-driven accumulation in Western economies and aligned with first-principles incentives for individuals to claim property under uncertainty. By 1995, over 90% of urban housing in many post-communist states had been privatized, per tenant-led processes that minimized transaction costs but often excluded non-tenants, such as recent migrants.[24][28] However, the absence of robust regulatory frameworks for multi-unit buildingsâ€”prevalent in panel-block estates housing 30â€“50% of urban populationsâ€”led to fragmented ownership, where individual proprietors underinvested in shared infrastructure due to collective action problems. Determinants of Variation

Economic and Market Factors

Economic factors such as household income levels, housing affordability, access to mortgage credit, and prevailing interest rates exert significant influence on cross-country variations in home ownership rates. Higher per capita income generally facilitates greater savings for down payments and mortgage servicing, correlating positively with ownership in empirical analyses of OECD nations, though the relationship is moderated by local market conditions; for instance, mortgage market depth explains over half of the variation in lending tied to GDP per capita levels above certain thresholds.[42] Housing affordability, measured by price-to-income ratios, inversely affects ownership probabilities, as elevated ratiosâ€”often stemming from constrained supplyâ€”deter potential buyers, particularly in urbanized economies where demand outpaces construction.[43] Access to mortgage financing represents a critical market determinant, with liberalization of credit constraints demonstrably elevating ownership rates among previously restricted households. Panel data from selected OECD countries indicate that reductions in down-payment requirements and expansions in loan-to-value ratios have boosted homeownership by easing entry barriers for younger and lower-wealth demographics, with effects persisting even after accounting for tax incentives on debt.[5] In contexts of underdeveloped financial systems, limited mortgage availability perpetuates renting, as seen in comparisons where countries with shallower credit markets exhibit 10-20 percentage point lower ownership despite comparable incomes.[44] Interest rates directly impact the cost of home financing, with empirical evidence from structural models showing that monetary policy expansionsâ€”lowering ratesâ€”raise homeownership by altering tenure choices and increasing demand for owner-occupied units. A one percentage point decline in rates can elevate ownership rates by up to 2-3 points in the short term, driven by reduced monthly payments that shift marginal renters into buyers, though this effect diminishes in supply-inelastic markets where prices absorb the stimulus.[45][46] Housing supply elasticity further modulates these dynamics through its effect on prices; jurisdictions with low elasticityâ€”often due to regulatory barriersâ€”experience amplified price responses to demand shocks, eroding affordability and suppressing ownership rates relative to more elastic peers. Cross-state U.S. analyses reveal that inelastic supply correlates with 5-10% lower ownership amid equivalent income growth, a pattern extensible to international contexts where land-use restrictions hinder construction.[47][48]

Policy and Institutional Influences

Government policies that subsidize homeownership, such as tax deductions for mortgage interest, aim to lower the effective cost of borrowing but primarily benefit higher-income households and have limited impact on aggregate ownership rates. In the United States, the mortgage interest deduction, which allows itemization of interest on up to $750,000 of debt for loans after December 15, 2017, disproportionately aids those with expensive homes and higher marginal tax rates, yet empirical studies indicate it does not significantly elevate overall homeownership.[49][50] Regulations in rental markets, including tenant protections and rent controls, influence tenure choices by altering the relative attractiveness of renting versus owning. Relaxing down-payment requirements through higher LTV limits in OECD countries has boosted ownership by 1.9 to 4.4 percentage points for credit-constrained households, countering demographic pressures like aging populations. Tighter collateral constraints, prevalent in places like Germany and the US, further suppress rates among the young by limiting borrowing against home equity.[53] Land-use policies and zoning restrictions constrain housing supply, elevating prices and impeding ownership affordability. Restrictive zoning, by limiting density and new construction, has been linked to reduced homeownership rates in US jurisdictions, as it inflates entry costs for first-time buyers without proportionally increasing supply.[54][55] Public housing frameworks can promote ownership when designed for eventual private tenure, as exemplified by Singapore's Housing and Development Board (HDB) system. Since the 1960s, HDB has provided subsidized apartments on 99-year leases with ownership transferability, raising the national homeownership rate to over 90% by 2024 through centralized land acquisition, low-cost financing, and mandatory savings via the Central Provident Fund.[56][57] In contrast, rental-oriented social housing in Europe, such as in the Netherlands (30% public stock), correlates with persistently lower private ownership by prioritizing tenure security over asset-building.[58]

In OECD countries, ownership probability increases with age, reaching a peak for those aged 45-64, with population aging contributing an average uplift of 0.75 to 1 percentage point to national rates between the 1980s and 2000s, particularly in Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, and Canada.[61] Conversely, rising shares of single-person householdsâ€”often linked to delayed family formation and higher divorce ratesâ€”have depressed rates in nations like Germany, Italy, and Australia by favoring renting among smaller units.[61] Immigrant demographics further modulate aggregates, as first-generation households typically exhibit lower ownership due to credit constraints and mobility needs, though this gap narrows over generations absent cultural overrides.[61] Geographic conditions, such as terrain, land scarcity, and settlement patterns, shape ownership by influencing supply costs and suitability for self-built or affordable housing. High-density urban environments, prevalent in countries like Switzerland (44% ownership) and Germany, correlate with subdued rates due to constrained land availability and elevated construction expenses, promoting rental markets suited to transient populations.[60][61] In contrast, nations with dispersed rural geographies or abundant arable land, such as Romania (96%) or Laos (95.9%), sustain elevated rates through easier access to buildable plots and lower density-driven barriers.[2] Empirical decompositions using regional proxies confirm that non-urban locales yield higher ownership, as geographic peripheries avoid the cost premiums of core cities.[61]

Implications and Empirical Outcomes

Wealth Accumulation and Economic Stability

Home ownership serves as a primary vehicle for household wealth accumulation, enabling equity buildup through mortgage principal payments that function as enforced savings, supplemented by potential property appreciation. Empirical analyses indicate that homeowners consistently exhibit higher net worth than renters, with median homeowner net worth reaching $255,000 in 2019 compared to substantially lower figures for non-owners, largely attributable to housing equity comprising over half of net wealth for many households.[62] [63] Longitudinal studies controlling for income, education, and other factors confirm that home ownership positively contributes to long-term wealth gains, particularly for lower-income families where it accounts for a majority of assets.[64] [65] Cross-country comparisons further reveal that variations in home ownership rates explain significant portions of differences in equalized net household wealth, underscoring housing's role in bridging wealth gaps when ownership is widespread.[7] At the national level, elevated home ownership rates correlate with more resilient household balance sheets, as property serves as a tangible asset hedge against inflation and a transmissible inheritance, fostering intergenerational wealth transfer. In countries with high ownership, such as those exceeding 80% rates in parts of Eastern Europe and Asia, housing equity has driven recent wealth disparities amid price surges, yet it provides a buffer during downturns by allowing equity access via refinancing or sales.[66] This mechanism enhances economic stability by reducing reliance on volatile liquid assets; for instance, home equity represented approximately 21% of U.S. household net worth in early 2000s data, a share that has grown with appreciation, stabilizing consumption patterns.[67] However, outcomes depend on mortgage structuresâ€”nations with predominant fixed-rate loans experience muted transmission of interest rate shocks to activity, preserving owner stability compared to variable-rate systems.[68] Economically, high home ownership mitigates broader instability by anchoring populations to localities, curbing excessive labor mobility that can exacerbate unemployment volatility, though it risks amplifying housing market corrections. Studies link ownership to lower household leverage sensitivity in recessions, as equity cushions defaults, contributing to faster recoveries in high-ownership economies.[69] While not universally accelerating wealth beyond alternative investments like stocks, the empirical premium of ownership persists in diverse contexts, supporting stability through diversified national asset bases less prone to rentier extraction.[70][71]

Social Cohesion and Family Formation

Higher rates of home ownership across countries correlate with enhanced social cohesion through mechanisms of residential stability and reduced mobility, which encourage long-term community investment and interpersonal trust. Empirical analyses, such as those using household survey data, find that homeowners exhibit greater social connectedness compared to renters, as ownership fosters participation in neighborhood activities and local governance.[72] This effect persists after controlling for income and demographics, with homeowners reporting higher levels of trust in neighbors and voluntary association membership.[73] In contrast, prevalent renting environments, often observed in low home ownership nations like Germany and Switzerland, are associated with higher transience, which can erode collective efficacy and increase social fragmentation. Cross-sectional studies from diverse contexts, including Indonesia and Australia, confirm that home ownership positively influences social capital dimensions, such as reciprocal norms and network density, independent of economic confounders.[73][74] Regarding family formation, home ownership facilitates earlier marriage and childbearing by signaling financial security and enabling spatial commitment to family life. Quasi-experimental evidence from large financial windfalls, such as lottery wins, shows that transitions to ownership increase marriage probabilities by up to 10% for singles and modestly elevate fertility rates, as households prioritize family expansion post-purchase.[75] Longitudinal data further indicate that ownership stabilizes marital unions, with homeowners experiencing lower divorce risks due to sunk costs and shared equity incentives.[76] Countries with elevated home ownership, particularly those achieving rates above 80% through privatization or subsidies, demonstrate patterns of sustained family structures and higher completed fertility compared to rental-dominant economies. For instance, access to affordable ownership in contexts like post-1990s Eastern Europe has supported traditional family metrics, contrasting with delays in family milestones in high-rent Western markets where first births are postponed by 3-4 years amid housing unaffordability.[77] Rising house prices in low-ownership settings globally suppress fertility by 0.01-0.03 births per woman per 10% price increase, disproportionately affecting non-owners and exacerbating generational delays in household formation.[78] These outcomes underscore ownership's role in causal pathways to demographic stability, beyond mere correlation with wealth.[79]

Criticisms, Risks, and Alternative Perspectives

High home ownership rates pose macroeconomic risks by curtailing labor mobility, as homeowners incur substantial transaction costsâ€”including moving expenses, taxes, and potential capital gainsâ€”when changing residences for employment. Empirical analyses across U.S. states and European regions confirm that ownership reduces household relocation rates by 1.5 to 3 percentage points annually compared to renters, limiting access to distant job markets and exacerbating skill mismatches.[80] This reduced mobility underpins the Oswald hypothesis, which links elevated ownership to higher aggregate unemployment through inefficient labor allocation. Cross-country regressions using OECD data from 1960 to 2011 show that a 10 percentage point rise in the home ownership rate correlates with a 1 to 2 percentage point increase in unemployment, persisting after controlling for demographics and institutions; within-country variations, such as U.S. states, yield similar lagged effects.[81] While individual-level studies often find no prolongation of personal joblessness spells, the aggregate evidence highlights systemic frictions, including longer commutes and fewer startups, as ownership discourages risk-taking. Leveraged home purchases amplify household vulnerability to economic shocks, with mortgage debt exposing owners to interest rate hikes and price corrections. During downturns, such as the 2008 crisis, widespread foreclosures eroded equity for millions, correlating with elevated anxiety and depression rates among affected owners.[82] Policy-driven ownership promotion, via tax incentives or guarantees, can foster overinvestment in housing, inflating bubbles and diverting capital from higher-yield sectors, as critiqued by analyses of U.S. subsidies that disproportionately aid affluent buyers without net wealth gains for lower-income groups.[71][83] Alternative views favor rental-dominant systems for enhancing adaptability, as exemplified by Germany (51% ownership in 2023) and Switzerland (42%), where low rates stem from regulated private rentals offering long-term security and affordability without ownership burdens. Germany's framework, with rent controls and non-profit housing, sustains vacancy rates below 2% and median rents at 30% of income, enabling fluid job transitions and entrepreneurship amid low unemployment (around 3% in 2024).[51][84] These models demonstrate that prioritizing supply-side deregulation and tenant rights can yield stability and growth sans high ownership, countering narratives that equate ownership with societal progress.[85]

Policy Debates and Controversies

Subsidies, Regulations, and Market Distortions

Government subsidies for homeownership, such as mortgage interest deductions, purchase grants, and loan guarantees, are prevalent in many OECD countries, including the United States, Canada, and several European nations, aiming to boost ownership rates by reducing borrowing costs.[86] However, empirical analyses indicate these measures often fail to substantially elevate ownership rates and instead inflate housing prices, as benefits accrue to sellers and existing owners rather than new buyers.[49] [87] For instance, in the U.S., where such tax subsidies are generous, the homeownership rate remains lower than in numerous unsubsidized developed economies, suggesting subsidies distort markets by encouraging over-leveraging without addressing underlying supply constraints.[49] Regulatory frameworks, particularly zoning and land-use restrictions, significantly impede housing supply and affordability, thereby suppressing homeownership rates in high-regulation jurisdictions. In the United States and parts of Europe, stringent zoning laws limit density, mandate large lot sizes, and impose environmental or aesthetic requirements, which elevate construction costs and curtail new builds, driving up prices and excluding lower-income households from ownership.[88] [89] Evidence from U.S. metropolitan areas shows that land-use controls account for the majority of housing cost premiums in expensive regions, with deregulation in select locales correlating to increased supply and modestly higher ownership among younger cohorts.[90] Internationally, countries like Germany and Switzerland, with relatively flexible land-use policies favoring rentals, exhibit lower ownership rates but stable affordability, contrasting with subsidized high-ownership nations where regulations exacerbate shortages.[5] Central bank monetary policies introduce further distortions by lowering interest rates and expanding credit, which amplify housing demand and fuel price bubbles without proportionally increasing ownership access. Post-2008 quantitative easing in the Eurozone and U.S. correlated with surging home prices, benefiting incumbent owners through wealth effects while raising barriers for first-time buyers via elevated down payments and debt burdens.[91] [92] OECD panel data reveal that eased credit constraints, often policy-induced, temporarily boost ownership among credit-limited groups but contribute to long-term imbalances, as low rates shift investments toward real estate speculation over productive capital.[93] These interventions, while stabilizing financial systems short-term, embed inefficiencies, with cross-country variations showing higher ownership in less interventionist credit regimes like those in Eastern Europe.[5] Combined, these factors create path dependencies: subsidies and loose policy entrench high prices, while regulations stifle supply responses, perpetuating low ownership in distorted markets despite interventions. Rigorous decompositions of OECD trends attribute up to 15-20% of ownership variance to policy-induced credit availability versus structural barriers like regulation, underscoring the need for supply-focused reforms over demand stimuli.[93] [5]

Generational and Inequality Concerns

In many OECD countries, younger generations such as Millennials and those born in the 1980s exhibit homeownership rates 10-20 percentage points lower than previous cohorts at comparable ages, primarily due to rising housing costs relative to incomes and increased student debt burdens.[94][95] This gap persists across Europe, where the share of homeowners among young adults has declined despite overall national rates stabilizing or rising among older populations, reflecting barriers like elevated house-price-to-income ratios now averaging 16% above historical norms in high-income OECD nations.[96][97] Intergenerational wealth transfers via housing exacerbate these disparities, as parental home equityâ€”amplified by decades of appreciationâ€”enables down-payment assistance or direct inheritance that boosts adult children's ownership likelihood by up to 15-20% in studies from Norway and the United States.[98][99] Such mechanisms create a "housing channel" of wealth persistence, where children of homeowners inherit not only assets but also advantages in navigating credit markets, while those from renter households face compounded entry barriers, widening the divide in net worth accumulation.[100] Empirical models indicate this transmission strengthens during early and middle childhood exposure to parental housing gains, influencing long-term outcomes like educational attainment and financial independence.[99] Homeownership policies, including tax subsidies and zoning restrictions, intensify inequality concerns by favoring existing ownersâ€”often older and higher-income groupsâ€”who benefit from untaxed imputed rents and capital gains, while locking out lower-wealth entrants and inflating asset bubbles that disproportionately reward incumbents.[101] Cross-country analyses show that while higher aggregate homeownership correlates with reduced overall wealth inequality (e.g., Gini coefficients 5-10 points lower in high-ownership nations like Spain versus low-ownership ones like Switzerland), the owner-renter wealth gap has surged 70% since the 1990s, with median homeowner net worth now exceeding renters' by factors of 40-50 in the United States.[102][66] Within generations, this manifests as greater housing asset inequality among working-age adults, as restricted supply and credit access concentrate ownership among the affluent, potentially polarizing labor markets in urban centers.[97][103] Critics argue that subsidizing homeownership distorts markets toward over-investment in illiquid assets, trapping younger cohorts in renting amid stagnant wages and high leverage risks, though evidence from panel data across Germany, France, Italy, and Spain suggests ownership still nets positive wealth effects for participants despite these frictions.[7] Reforms targeting supply expansion over demand stimuli could mitigate these issues without undermining the empirical link between ownership and intergenerational mobility, as restrictive land-use policies have been shown to casually boost net wealth for regulators' beneficiaries at the expense of broader access.[101][102]

Evidence-Based Reforms

Evidence-based reforms to elevate home ownership rates emphasize increasing housing supply through regulatory liberalization and enhancing credit access via financial market adjustments, as these interventions have demonstrated causal links to higher ownership in empirical analyses. In the United States, interstate banking deregulation during the 1980s and 1990s expanded mortgage availability, resulting in a statistically significant rise in homeownership rates by facilitating loans to previously underserved households.[104] Similarly, relaxing down-payment requirements on mortgages has empirically boosted ownership among credit-constrained households in OECD countries, with panel data showing positive effects on tenure transitions without immediate evidence of widespread default risks in stable economic conditions.[93] Supply-side deregulation, particularly easing zoning and land-use restrictions, addresses root causes of affordability barriers by curbing artificial price inflation. Econometric studies indicate that stringent zoning regulations, such as minimum lot sizes, elevate housing costs by limiting construction, with deregulation in comparable markets correlating to price stabilization and increased ownership potential through greater inventory.[88] In New Zealand, post-2016 reforms including upzoning in major cities like Auckland generated over 22,000 additional housing consentsâ€”equivalent to 4% of existing stockâ€”enhancing supply responsiveness and contributing to affordability gains that supported a rebound in homeownership rates to levels above pre-reform declines.[105][106] These outcomes contrast with persistent restrictions elsewhere, where regulatory barriers explain up to dominant shares of price premiums, underscoring deregulation's role in enabling broader access without relying on demand-side subsidies prone to market distortions.[89] Mass privatization of state-owned housing stock represents another empirically validated approach, particularly in post-communist transitions. In Central and Eastern European countries, rapid privatization programs from the early 1990s transferred apartments to occupants at nominal costs or via restitution, propelling homeownership rates above 80-96% in nations like Romania and Slovakiaâ€”rates far exceeding Western European averagesâ€”while fostering wealth accumulation absent from rental-heavy systems.[23][107] This shift created quasi-experimental variation confirming homeownership's causal benefits for stability, though long-term maintenance challenges emerged without accompanying private finance development.[108] Such reforms prioritize tenure security over subsidized rentals, aligning with data showing outright ownership correlates with reduced mobility frictions and higher economic engagement compared to regulated public housing models.[109] While targeted subsidies like down-payment assistance have shown marginal gains in specific demographics, broader evidence favors supply-focused measures to avoid inflating demand without corresponding builds, as seen in cycles of ownership booms followed by corrections.[110] Reforms must account for local institutional contexts, with meta-analyses of regulatory impacts revealing that overly permissive credit expansions alone risk leverage bubbles, whereas combined supply liberalization sustains gains.[111] Ongoing evaluations in deregulated markets continue to affirm these pathways as scalable for countries with middling ownership rates, provided implementation counters entrenched interests favoring scarcity.[112]
