2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival

The 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival was the sixteenth edition of the winter European Youth Olympic Festival, a biennial multi-sport competition organized by the European Olympic Committees for athletes aged 14 to 18, held from 21 to 28 January 2023 in the Friuli-Venezia Giulia region of Italy, with select events extending into neighboring Slovenia and Austria.[1][2] The event encompassed twelve winter sports disciplines, including alpine skiing, biathlon, cross-country skiing, curling, figure skating, freestyle skiing and ski cross, ice hockey, Nordic combined, short track speed skating, ski jumping, ski mountaineering, and snowboarding, drawing roughly 1,800 young competitors from 47 European nations.[1][3] France dominated the medal table, securing seventeen medals including seven golds, ahead of Italy and Switzerland, while the host country set a national record for podium finishes across the disciplines.[4][3][5] Designed to foster Olympic values among emerging talents, the festival provided a platform for high-level competition and cultural exchange, with venues leveraging the region's alpine terrain and ice facilities.[6]

Background and Host Selection

Event Overview and Historical Context

The European Youth Olympic Winter Festival is a biennial multi-sport competition organized by the European Olympic Committees (EOC) for athletes aged 14 to 18, emphasizing athletic excellence alongside cultural and educational initiatives to foster Olympic values and talent development. The event's winter edition, featuring disciplines such as alpine skiing, biathlon, and figure skating, debuted in 1993 as a counterpart to the summer festival initiated in 1991, providing a structured pathway for young Europeans toward senior international competitions.[7] Over its history, the festival has grown into Europe's premier youth winter sports gathering, with editions hosted across various nations to promote regional infrastructure and youth engagement in winter disciplines.[7] The 2023 edition, the 16th overall, took place from January 21 to 28 in Italy's Friuli-Venezia Giulia region, marking the country's second time hosting the winter festival after the inaugural 1993 event in Aosta Valley. Approximately 1,300 athletes from 47 National Olympic Committees competed in 109 events across 13 disciplines, with venues distributed in cities including Trieste, Tarvisio, and Pontebba.[1] [8] The opening ceremony occurred on January 22 in Trieste's Piazza UnitÃ  dâ€™Italia, highlighting multicultural themes amid the region's alpine terrain suitable for winter sports.[6] This iteration underscored the festival's role in nurturing emerging talents, with competitions adhering to formats mirroring senior European and Olympic standards to prepare participants for future elite levels, while logistical preparations benefited from Italy's established winter sports heritage.[1] The event's scale, involving 1,200 volunteers and extensive media coverage, reinforced its status as a key developmental platform, though independent analyses note occasional challenges in balancing youth participation with competitive intensity across disciplines.[8]

Bidding and Selection Process

The bidding process for hosting the European Youth Olympic Winter Festival is managed by the European Olympic Committees (EOC), with National Olympic Committees (NOCs) responsible for submitting formal applications on behalf of candidate regions or cities.[9] Applications typically include details on venues, infrastructure, budget, and alignment with EOC criteria emphasizing youth development, sustainability, and Olympic values, followed by evaluation by the EOC Executive Committee before final approval at the EOC General Assembly.[10] For the 2023 edition, Friuli Venezia Giulia in northern Italy emerged as the sole candidate, proposed by the Italian NOC.[11] The region's bid highlighted its established winter sports facilities across multiple municipalities, including Tarvisio, Sappada, and Piancavallo, and its capacity to host events in alpine skiing, biathlon, and other disciplines.[12] Although initial interest was expressed by other entities, such as Slovenia's consideration of a bid estimated at â‚¬9.38 million, no competing application advanced to the final stage.[13] EOC members unanimously approved Friuli Venezia Giulia as host during the 47th EOC General Assembly held in Marbella, Spain, on November 9-10, 2018.[14][11] This marked Italy's second hosting of the winter festival, following the inaugural 1993 event in Aosta Valley.[11] The host contract was formally signed between the EOC and regional authorities on February 14, 2020, solidifying preparations amid the region's emphasis on cross-border cooperation with neighboring Austria and Slovenia for select events.[12]

Organization and Preparation

Local Organizing Committee

The Local Organizing Committee (LOC) for the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival, officially named Comitato Organizzatore EYOF FVG 2023, was established to coordinate all aspects of the event's preparation and execution in the Friuli Venezia Giulia region of Italy.[15] Headquartered at Piazza UnitÃ  d'Italia 1 in Trieste, with an operational office in Amaro, the committee operated under the statutory framework of the regional autonomous government and collaborated closely with the Italian National Olympic Committee (CONI).[15][16] Its formation emphasized integration of local mountain communities, including Tarvisio, Piancavallo, and Sappada, to leverage regional infrastructure for the multi-venue event held from January 21 to 28, 2023.[17] Leadership of the LOC was provided by President Maurizio Dunnhofer, who oversaw strategic direction, and General Director Giorgio Kaidisch, responsible for operational and marketing activities.[18][19] Dunnhofer, in coordination with Friuli Venezia Giulia Regional President Massimiliano Fedriga, managed key milestones such as funding confirmations and coordination commission visits, ensuring alignment with European Olympic Committees (EOC) standards.[20][21] The committee handled logistics for approximately 1,800 athletes from 46 nations, including accreditation, transportation, and temporary importations for equipment like biathlon rifles.[22][23] The LOC's responsibilities extended to volunteer recruitment and management, engaging over 2,000 participants for event support, as well as securing partnerships with regional entities for cultural and educational programs integrated into the festival.[24] It also issued public tenders for services such as accredited transport and event infrastructure, adhering to Italian procurement regulations.[25] Following the event's conclusion, the committee formalized its dissolution by June 30, 2024, transferring knowledge to future hosts like the 2025 edition in Georgia.[16][3]

Competition Format and Rules

The competitions at the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival were structured across 14 disciplines, encompassing approximately 100 events that followed the technical rules of the respective international federationsâ€”such as the International Ski Federation (FIS), International Skating Union (ISU), and International Ice Hockey Federation (IIHF)â€”with modifications for youth athletes.[32] Events emphasized individual and team performances, including formats like slalom and giant slalom in alpine skiing, heats and relays in short track speed skating, and knockout tournaments in ice hockey, often incorporating mixed-gender team competitions to foster collaboration.[32] [1] Eligibility was restricted to athletes aged 14 to 18, corresponding to birth years 2005â€“2008, with minor variations by discipline (e.g., 2005â€“2006 for alpine skiing and ski jumping; 2006â€“2007 for curling).[32] Each National Olympic Committee (NOC) faced quotas limiting entries to two males and two females per discipline, supplemented by up to four officials, to balance participation and competitiveness; team sports like ice hockey permitted squads of 18 players (including two goalkeepers) plus six officials.[32] NOCs nominated athletes via their European Olympic Committees affiliation, requiring valid federation licenses and, for certain sports like biathlon, customs approvals for equipment such as rifles.[32] Anti-doping protocols, managed by the International Testing Agency (ITA), aligned with World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) standards, including random testing without prior notice and mandatory chaperones for minors during procedures; therapeutic use exemptions were processed in advance.[32] [33] Uniform and equipment regulations prohibited commercial advertising and restricted podium displays to national flags and anthems, per European Olympic Committees guidelines.[32] Debut disciplines like freestyle skiing and ski mountaineering introduced youth-oriented events such as ski cross and sprint races, adhering to federation-specific tiebreakers (e.g., goal difference in ice hockey, seeding in short track).[32] All athletes were required to remain on-site from January 21 to 28, 2023, to ensure full event immersion.[32]

Schedule and Ceremonies

Overall Timeline

The 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival occurred from January 21 to 28, 2023, encompassing athlete arrivals, ceremonies, and competitions across 14 winter sports disciplines in the Friuli Venezia Giulia region of Italy, with some events extending into neighboring Slovenia and Austria.[1][31] The opening ceremony took place on January 21 in Trieste, where Italian track cyclist Jonathan Milan, a Tokyo 2020 Olympic gold medalist, lit the cauldron using the Flame of Peace, marking the formal start amid performances highlighting regional culture and Olympic values.[34][32] Competitions began on January 22 and continued daily through January 28, with medal events distributed to ensure a steady progression of results; for instance, January 22 featured initial medals in biathlon (Forni Avoltri), alpine skiing (Tarvisio), and cross-country skiing (Tarvisio), while later days included ice hockey semifinals, freestyle skiing, and short track speed skating finals.[1] The schedule accommodated 1,300 athletes aged 14-18 from 47 nations, prioritizing youth development alongside competitive outcomes, with 109 events yielding medals in disciplines such as ski mountaineering (introduced for the first time) and curling.[8][35] The festival concluded on January 28 with the closing ceremony in Udine, following final medal events in biathlon and alpine skiing, where France topped the overall medal table with 19 golds among 47 total.[1][4] This eight-day structure aligned with European Olympic Committees guidelines for balancing sport, education, and cultural programs, including the Flame of Peace relay initiated in November 2022.[36][32]

Opening Ceremony

The opening ceremony of the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival took place on January 21, 2023, in Piazza UnitÃ  dâ€™Italia in Trieste, Italy, marking the official start of the 16th edition of the event.[6][37] Over 1,300 athletes aged 14â€“18 from 47 European National Olympic Committees participated, along with approximately 1,200 volunteers.[6] The ceremony featured a parade of nations, including delegations from all participating countries, and emphasized themes of friendship, inclusion, sharing, and environmental responsibility to commemorate 30 years since the inaugural Winter EYOF in Aosta Valley in 1993.[6][38] Proceedings included addresses from key figures such as Italian Minister of Sport Andrea Abodi, who formally declared the festival open; Italian Olympic Committee President Giovanni MalagÃ²; EYOF 2023 President Massimiliano Fedriga; European Olympic Committees President Spyros Capralos; and EOC Secretary General Raffaele Pagnozzi.[6] The Olympic cauldron was lit by Jonathan Milan, an Italian track cyclist and gold medalist at the Tokyo 2020 Olympics.[6] The event's Olympic torch relay had begun on November 4, 2022, at Rome's Ara Pacis.[38] Due to strong winds, the ceremony was shortened from its planned duration.[6] Competitions commenced the following day across 14 winter sports disciplines.[6]

Closing Ceremony

The closing ceremony of the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival occurred on January 28, 2023, at the Fiera di Udine in Martignacco, near Udine, Italy, marking the conclusion of the event after eight days of competition across 14 winter sports disciplines.[4][39] Held indoors at the ICE Arena in Hall 6, the ceremony gathered athletes, officials, and delegations from 47 European National Olympic Committees to celebrate the festival's achievements, including the distribution of 171 medals.[4][3] The program commenced with a parade of participating athletes and delegations marching together, symbolizing unity among the young competitors.[39][3] Italy's delegation entered led by flag-bearers Ludovica Righi, who won gold in the girls' alpine skiing giant slalom, and Erik Canovi, gold medalist in ski mountaineering individual and sprint events.[3] Speeches followed, including remarks from European Olympic Committees President Spyros Capralos, who emphasized the festival's role in providing opportunities for young athletes, and Friuli Venezia Giulia Region President Massimiliano Fedriga, who described the hosting as a resounding success that showcased the region internationally.[4][39] A key symbolic act was the handover of the European Olympic Committees flag from Fedriga to Capralos, then to Otar Arbolishvili, Mayor of Borjomi Municipality in Georgia, designating Borjomi and Bakuriani as hosts for the 2025 Winter EYOF.[4][39][3] The European Fair Play Movement also presented trophies to individuals including Roberto Bruni, Eni Netaj, Fausto Pedergnana, and the Ukrainian athletes' delegation, recognizing exemplary conduct during the festival.[4][3] The event underscored the festival's focus on youth development, with France leading the medal table and Italy achieving a national record of 21 podiums.[4][3]

Participation

Participating Nations

Athletes from 47 European nations, primarily members of the European Olympic Committees, participated in the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival held in Friuli Venezia Giulia, Italy.[1] [32] These delegations comprised young competitors aged 14 to 18, with team sizes varying significantly based on each nation's winter sports infrastructure and Olympic qualification pathways; for instance, winter sports-dominant countries like Austria fielded larger contingents exceeding 80 athletes, while others such as Albania and Azerbaijan sent minimal representations of one athlete each.[40] The host nation Italy competed across all disciplines, deploying 109 athletes (56 male and 53 female) to maximize participation in the 14 sports program.[41] This broad representation underscored the event's role in fostering continental youth development in winter disciplines, though smaller nations often focused entries on alpine skiing or biathlon to align with available talent and facilities.[40]

Athlete Qualifications and Numbers

Approximately 1,300 athletes aged 14 to 18 competed in the 2023 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival, representing 47 National Olympic Committees (NOCs) from across Europe.[36][27] The event's scale reflected its role as a premier multi-sport gathering for young European talent, with participation limited to athletes nominated by their NOCs in accordance with established age eligibility and sport-specific quotas.[6] Athlete selection occurred at the national level, where each NOC applied its own criteriaâ€”typically encompassing recent competitive performance, national rankings, and developmental potentialâ€”while adhering to the European Olympic Committees' overarching framework.[32] Age requirements varied slightly by discipline but generally restricted participation to those born no earlier than 2005, ensuring competitors were in the 14â€“18 age bracket as of the event dates in January 2023.[36] For team sports such as ice hockey and curling, quotas prioritized mixed or gender-specific teams from qualifying NOCs, with additional emphasis on prior international youth results where applicable.[32] Italy submitted the largest delegation, with 109 athletes (56 males and 53 females) across all 14 disciplines, underscoring the host nation's investment in youth winter sports development.[3] Other prominent teams included those from traditional winter sports powerhouses like Austria, France, and Switzerland, though exact breakdowns per nation were not centrally mandated beyond quota limits per event.[27] This structure promoted broad accessibility while prioritizing competitive readiness, as determined by NOC evaluations rather than universal qualifying standards.[32]

Notable Performances and Records

In short track speed skating, Hungarian athlete Dominik Major secured three gold medals, contributing significantly to his nation's strong showing in the discipline.[4] Similarly, teammate Dora Szigeti claimed four medals overall, including two golds, highlighting Hungary's dominance on the ice.[26] Polish skater Hanna Mazur also excelled with four medals in short track events.[4] Slovenian ski jumper Nika Prevc won three gold medals across individual and team events, marking a standout performance in the sport.[4] In alpine skiing, Swiss racer Laura Huber took gold in the girls' Super-G, finishing 0.63 seconds ahead of the field, while Hungarian Attila Banyai edged out competitors by 0.04 seconds for boys' Super-G victory.[4] Spanish athlete Laia Selles Sanchez achieved three golds in skiing disciplines.[4] Host nation Italy set a national record with 21 total medals, including six golds, surpassing their previous best of 20 from the 2022 edition.[3] The festival featured a record 59 events across 12 sports, expanding from 39 events in nine sports at the prior edition, though no individual event-specific time or distance records were widely reported beyond these multi-medal feats.[4]
