2023 Karachi local elections

The 2023 Karachi local elections were held on 15 January 2023 to elect chairpersons, vice-chairpersons, and general members to the city's 246 union councils, serving as the foundational tier of local governance under the Sindh Local Government Act 2013.[1][2] These polls, part of the second phase of Sindh province's local government elections, determined the composition of higher bodies including district councils and the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation, amid Karachi's role as Pakistan's largest metropolis and economic center housing over 16 million residents. Official results from the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP), released after extended delays and legal challenges, showed the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) and Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) securing an equal number of union council seats, with winners announced for 215 out of 235 contested councils after withholding results for 20 due to disputes.[3][4] The elections featured competition among major parties including the PPP, JI, Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), and Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), but were overshadowed by significant logistical failures, including a nearly 30-hour delay in initial result compilation and weeks-long resolution of recounts for disputed seats.[5] Opposition parties, particularly PTI and MQM-P, alleged widespread rigging, ballot stuffing, and misuse of state resources by the PPP-led Sindh government, claims the ECP partially addressed through re-polling and tribunal reviews, though critics questioned the electoral body's independence given provincial oversight dynamics.[6] Voter turnout remained low at around 30-40% in urban areas, reflecting disillusionment with local governance failures in infrastructure, water supply, and waste management that have plagued Karachi for decades.[7] These outcomes facilitated the indirect election of the Karachi mayor in June 2023, where PPP's Barrister Murtaza Wahab narrowly defeated JI's Hafiz Naeem ur Rehman with 173 votes to 169 in the city council, underscoring the finely balanced power distribution from the local polls.[8] The results represented a departure from historical ethnic-based voting patterns, with PPP expanding beyond its rural Sindhi base into urban and Muhajir-dominated neighborhoods traditionally held by MQM, attributed by analysts to voter fatigue with PTI's national turmoil and MQM's internal fractures, though substantiated causal links remain debated amid unverified claims of inducements.[9] Controversies persisted post-election, including ECP's withholding of results in violence-affected or contested units, highlighting systemic challenges in ensuring transparent urban elections in Pakistan's most diverse city.[2]

Background

Historical Context of Karachi's Local Governance

Karachi's local governance originated in the mid-19th century under British colonial rule. In 1846, a Conservancy Board was formed to address cholera epidemics and basic sanitation.[10] This body was upgraded to a Municipal Commission in 1852, followed by its reorganization as a Municipal Committee in 1853, introducing limited elected representation.[10] By 1933, the City of Karachi Municipal Act established a Municipal Corporation with an elected mayor, deputy mayor, and 57 councillors, expanding authority over urban planning, taxation, and services amid growing population pressures.[10] [11] Following Pakistan's independence in 1947, Karachi served as the federal capital until 1959, leading to centralized federal oversight that diminished local autonomy, with the Karachi Municipal Corporation (KMC) handling core functions like water supply and waste management.[10] The 1959 Basic Democracies Ordinance under President Ayub Khan introduced tiered elected councils, including urban municipal bodies via the 1960 Municipal Administration Ordinance, but these served more as mechanisms for regime legitimacy than genuine devolution, with rural elites dominating and urban areas like Karachi retaining limited fiscal powers.[12] In 1976, amid rapid urbanization, the KMC was elevated to the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC), incorporating zonal committees to manage expanded districts, though political interference persisted.[10] The 1979 Local Government Ordinance under General Zia-ul-Haq further structured urban councils, but implementation in Karachi was hampered by ethnic mobilization, particularly the rise of the Mohajir Qaumi Movement (later Muttahida Qaumi Movement, MQM) in the 1980s, which secured control over local bodies through Muhajir-majority representation, often prioritizing constituency patronage over infrastructure.[12] Zonal municipal committees were established in 1987 to decentralize administration but dissolved in 1994 amid governance disputes.[10] Under General Pervez Musharraf's 2001 Devolution Plan via the Local Government Ordinance, the KMC was superseded by the City District Government Karachi (CDGK), a unified district-level entity led by an elected Nazim (mayor) with enhanced powers over budgeting and services, aiming to bypass provincial dominance; this system operated until 2010, during which MQM-affiliated Nazims like Naimur Rehman and Mustafa Kamal managed operations but faced criticism for inefficiencies in sewage and housing.[13] [14] The 18th Constitutional Amendment in 2010 restored provincial authority, prompting Sindh to enact the Sindh Local Government Act 2013, which reconfigured Karachi into multiple District Municipal Corporations (DMCs) across its districts (initially five, later seven), union committees, and town committees, emphasizing provincial oversight and reducing direct mayoral powers.[15] This framework has perpetuated cycles of delayed elections and fiscal dependency on the Sindh government, contributing to persistent challenges in urban service delivery despite Karachi's economic significance.[11]

Pre-Election Political Dynamics

The political landscape in Karachi preceding the January 15, 2023, local government elections was characterized by fragmentation, ethnic tensions, and shifting alliances following the 2013 military operation against extremism and organized crime, which significantly weakened traditional power structures. The Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), long the primary representative of the Muhajir (Urdu-speaking migrant) community comprising about 50% of Karachi's population, abruptly boycotted the polls on January 14, 2023, alleging pre-planned rigging, unfair delimitation of constituencies favoring rural Sindhi interests, and failure to address urban governance grievances despite its alliance with the ruling Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) in Sindh.[16][17][18] This boycott, announced hours before voting, stemmed from unresolved demands for administrative autonomy and resource allocation, exacerbating ethnic divides between Muhajirs and the PPP's Sindhi base while creating opportunities for other parties to capture disenfranchised urban votes.[19] The PPP, controlling Sindh province since 2008, sought to consolidate its influence in Karachi by emphasizing infrastructure improvements, water scarcity resolution, and anti-encroachment drives, positioning itself as a pragmatic alternative amid perceptions of MQM decline and PTI's national-level turmoil post-April 2022 ouster of Imran Khan.[19] As part of the federal Pakistan Democratic Movement coalition, the PPP leveraged provincial resources for campaign logistics but faced criticism for delays in local polls attributed to legal challenges over voter lists and boundaries.[6] Meanwhile, Jamaat-e-Islami (JI), an Islamist party with roots in urban welfare networks, intensified grassroots mobilization through mosque-based outreach and digital campaigns highlighting corruption and service delivery failures, aiming to erode PPP strongholds in low-income areas like Orangi and Liaquatabad.[20] JI's strategy capitalized on voter fatigue with ethnic politics, drawing support from Pashtun and lower-middle-class segments disillusioned by post-2013 instability.[19] Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), buoyed by Khan's anti-corruption narrative and 2018 national gains in Karachi (securing 14 of 21 National Assembly seats), mounted an aggressive campaign targeting youth and middle-class voters with promises of transparent governance and opposition to "dynastic" parties like PPP.[19] However, PTI grappled with internal dissent and the absence of formal pre-poll pacts, though informal coordination with JI emerged in select districts amid shared opposition to PPP dominance.[21] The Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) played a marginal role, focusing on pockets of Punjabi expatriate support without significant alliances. Overall, the dynamics reflected a contest over Karachi's 246 union councils, where control promised leverage in the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation, amid low anticipated turnout due to apathy and security concerns.[22]

Merger of MQM Factions and Ethnic Politics

On January 12, 2023, three days before the Karachi local government elections, the Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), the Pak Sarzameen Party (PSP) led by Syed Mustafa Kamal, and the MQM Organization Restoration Committee faction under Dr. Farooq Sattar announced their merger to form a unified MQM-P.[23] [24] This reunification aimed to consolidate support among Karachi's Muhajir community, the Urdu-speaking descendants of Muslim migrants from India following the 1947 partition, who form the core base of MQM's ethnic nationalist politics.[25] The move followed years of fragmentation since 2016, when internal rifts, exacerbated by Altaf Hussain's exile in London and his inflammatory speeches, led to the party's split into competing factions.[26] The merger was positioned as a strategic response to perceived marginalization of Muhajirs in Karachi's multi-ethnic political landscape, where voting patterns remain heavily influenced by ethnic identities rather than policy platforms.[27] MQM-P convener Khalid Maqbool Siddiqui emphasized that the unified party would prioritize Karachi's urban governance issues, such as infrastructure and revenue generation, while advocating for Muhajir rights against dominance by Sindhi-led Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) in rural Sindh and Pashtun-supported groups like Jamaat-e-Islami (JI).[24] Critics, including rival parties, dismissed the alliance as "political engineering" orchestrated by establishment influences to counter PPP's influence in Sindh, though MQM leaders denied external coercion.[26] Altaf Hussain, founder of the original MQM and leader of the London-based faction excluded from the merger, described the unification as a "forced marriage made for divorce," expressing skepticism about its longevity while noting it simplified directing criticism toward a single entity.[28] [29] In Karachi, ethnic politics have historically fueled MQM's mobilization of Muhajir voters through narratives of discrimination and demands for proportional representation, sustaining bloc voting despite the party's declining electoral fortunes amid urban demographic shifts and competition from PTI and JI.[27] The merger sought to reverse this erosion by presenting a cohesive front, though its immediate impact on the January 15 elections was limited, with JI emerging as a strong contender in Muhajir areas.[25]

Electoral Framework

Local Government Structure in Karachi

Karachi's local government operates within a multi-tiered framework established by the Sindh Local Government Act, 2013 (SLGA 2013), which designates the city as a metropolitan area under the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation while incorporating subsidiary local bodies for decentralized administration.[15] This structure emphasizes urban municipal functions such as infrastructure maintenance, public health, and service delivery, though provincial oversight limits full autonomy.[30] The apex body, the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC), holds jurisdiction over city-wide strategic planning, major roads, parks, and coordination with provincial agencies on water and sewerage.[31] It comprises a City Council of directly elected general councilors from territorial constituencies, supplemented by reserved seats for specified categories including women, youth, and minorities, with leadership provided by a Mayor and Deputy Mayor selected through council processes.[30] The KMC's administrative apparatus includes departments for engineering, finance, and enforcement, but its effectiveness is constrained by shared responsibilities with entities like the Karachi Development Authority.[32] Subordinate to the KMC are 25 Town Municipal Corporations (TMCs), distributed across Karachi's seven districtsâ€”Keamari, Central, West, South, East, Korangi, and Malirâ€”each managing localized services such as solid waste collection, street lighting, and community facilities.[33][34] TMCs are governed by elected Chairmen and Vice Chairmen, supported by councils drawn from underlying units, enabling responsiveness to area-specific needs while reporting to the KMC.[30] At the grassroots level, 246 Union Committees (UCs) form the foundational tier, handling neighborhood-level tasks including drainage, local taxation collection, and citizen grievance redressal.[35] Each UC features an elected Chairman and general members representing sub-wards, fostering direct community engagement but often hampered by inadequate funding devolution from higher tiers.[36] This arrangement under SLGA 2013 replaced prior systems like the 2001 ordinance, aiming for enhanced devolution, yet persistent provincial interventions undermine local fiscal independence.[37]

Delimitation Process and Voter Registration

The delimitation of local government constituencies in Karachi for the 2023 elections followed the framework established by the Sindh Local Government Act, 2013 (SLGA 2013), which mandates the division of districts into union committees (UCs), wards, and other units based on population density, geographical contiguity, and administrative viability to ensure equitable representation. Section 10 of the Act requires the Provincial Local Government Commission to conduct this process, preventing boundaries from crossing revenue taluka limits and prioritizing compact, coherent units. For the phase II elections on January 15, 2023, which covered 229 of Karachi's 235 UCs, the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) utilized pre-existing delimitations inherited from prior cycles, as fresh redrawing was not mandated prior to polling despite ongoing disputes.[15][38][39] Controversies arose over alleged gerrymandering in these delimitations, particularly in urban areas of Karachi and Hyderabad divisions, where opposition parties claimed boundaries were adjusted to consolidate Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) strongholds by diluting ethnic minority concentrations in Muhajir-dominated neighborhoods. The Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P) boycotted the elections, citing the lack of new delimitation as evidence of bias favoring rural and Sindhi-majority influences in urban seats, a grievance rooted in historical tensions over urban-rural power imbalances in Sindh's local governance. The ECP proceeded without revisions, arguing that the existing setup complied with SLGA provisions and prior court validations, though this fueled accusations of procedural opacity.[16][40][41] Voter registration for the elections drew from the ECP's national electoral rolls, integrated with the computerized system requiring a valid Computerized National Identity Card (CNIC) for eligibility, ensuring universal adult suffrage for residents aged 18 and above within the relevant UCs. Updates to rolls occurred periodically via door-to-door verification and applications to district election commissioners, with the ECP finalizing lists ahead of the January 15 polling date to reflect recent migrations and demographics in Karachi's dense urban landscape. Specific turnout data indicated variable participation, but comprehensive registered voter counts for Karachi's phase II were not publicly disaggregated by the ECP; broader Sindh LG phase II encompassed over 3,500 councilor seats across Karachi and Hyderabad, underscoring the scale of urban enfranchisement amid logistical challenges like outdated rolls in informal settlements.[42][43][39]

Election Delays and Legal Challenges

The local government elections in Karachi, part of Sindh's broader polls, faced repeated delays stemming from disputes over the delimitation of constituencies conducted by the PPP-led Sindh government, which opposition parties alleged was manipulated to favor rural and PPP-dominated areas at the expense of urban centers like Karachi. Originally anticipated after the expiry of previous terms around 2020, the polls were pushed back due to legal amendments to the Sindh Local Government Act (SLGA) 2013 and subsequent challenges, with the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) notifying schedules only in late 2022 following court hearings. These delays were exacerbated by petitions from parties including PTI and MQM-P, which argued that the delimitation violated principles of fair representation and required fresh boundaries based on population data from the 2017 census.[44][45] In July 2022, PTI leader Ashraf Jabbar Qureshi petitioned the Sindh High Court (SHC) against an ECP notification postponing polls, seeking immediate elections on the grounds that further delays undermined democratic mandates and allowed the provincial government to retain administrative control. Similarly, MQM-P filed applications in November 2022 to halt proceedings until legislative tweaks empowered local bodies adequately and addressed delimitation flaws, claiming the process fragmented urban constituencies to dilute ethnic minority representation. The SHC dismissed multiple postponement pleas, including MQM-P's bid in June 2022 and another in January 2023 just before Phase II polling, ruling that such requests contravened election laws and emphasizing the ECP's authority to proceed.[45][46][47] Despite these rulings, the legal wrangling contributed to phased votingâ€”rural Sindh in December 2022 and urban areas including Karachi on January 15, 2023â€”with the ECP directing foolproof security and preparations to counter ongoing objections. Chief Election Commissioner Sikandar Sultan Raja affirmed in January 2023 that no further postponements would occur, underscoring the commission's efforts to enforce constitutional timelines amid provincial resistance. These challenges highlighted tensions between federal electoral oversight and provincial autonomy, with critics attributing delays to the Sindh government's strategic maneuvering rather than procedural necessities.[48][49][50]

Campaign

Major Campaign Issues

The campaigns for the 2023 Karachi local elections prominently featured the city's entrenched municipal service deficiencies, with candidates emphasizing improvements in water supply, sanitation, waste management, and infrastructure as core priorities. Karachi residents have long grappled with irregular water distribution, forcing dependence on private tankers amid shortages exacerbated by outdated pipelines and population pressures, issues repeatedly underscored by campaigning parties as symptomatic of provincial mismanagement.[51][52] Sanitation and sewage systems emerged as another focal point, with overflowing drains and untreated wastewater contributing to public health risks and urban flooding during monsoons; reports from the period highlighted how these failures, affecting over 59% of surveyed residents, were leveraged in voter appeals for accountable local oversight.[52][53] Waste disposal inefficiencies, including uncollected garbage piling up in streets, were criticized as evidence of weak enforcement and corruption in solid waste authorities, prompting promises of streamlined collection and recycling initiatives.[51] Infrastructure decay, encompassing potholed roads, inadequate public transport, and traffic congestion, was tied to broader demands for devolved powers to local bodies, with 71% of residents in contemporaneous polls identifying it as a top grievance amid stalled provincial projects.[52] Power outages and load-shedding, though partially federal, were linked locally to distribution failures, fueling campaigns for enhanced utility coordination.[54] These issues reflected causal breakdowns in governance accountability rather than mere resource scarcity, as ethnic-based parties like JI positioned themselves as alternatives to PPP-dominated provincial control, while PTI stressed anti-corruption reforms to enable service delivery.

Pakistan Peoples Party Strategy

The Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP), benefiting from its control of the Sindh provincial government, pursued a strategy of showcasing pre-election development projects to underscore its capacity for governance in Karachi. This included the launch of new transport services and infrastructure initiatives, positioned as evidence of commitment to urban improvements amid longstanding issues like water scarcity and mobility challenges.[7] Such efforts were concentrated in districts like the newly created Keamari and the expanded Malir, where administrative boundary changes aligned with PPP's support among Pashtun, Baloch, and Sindhi communities, diluting the dominance of Urdu-speaking Muhajir voters traditionally aligned with rivals like the Muttahida Qaumi Movement.[7] PPP's campaign emphasized grassroots organization and advanced planning, with party leaders conducting targeted outreach in union councils to mobilize voters disillusioned by fragmented opposition efforts.[7] Chairman Bilawal Bhutto Zardari framed the party's pitch around collective responsibility for the city's revival, stating via social media that "Together we will build the Karachi we deserve" in reference to collaborative urban development under the slogan #KarachiHumSabKa.[7] This messaging aimed to broaden appeal beyond core ethnic bases by portraying PPP as a pragmatic alternative focused on service delivery rather than ideological confrontation. As the incumbent, PPP effectively deployed provincial resources for voter engagement, including door-to-door canvassing and highlighting contrasts with competitors' past records on civic amenities. Analysts attributed the approach's efficacy to disciplined constituency management rather than widespread popularity gains, enabling the party to secure 93 union council seats despite low overall turnout of approximately 20-25%.[7][5] The strategy also involved countering delays sought by opponents through legal challenges, insisting on proceeding with polls to capitalize on momentum from rural Sindh strongholds spilling into urban fringes.[49]

Jamaat-e-Islami Platform

Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) in Karachi, under the leadership of Hafiz Naeem ur Rehman, centered its platform for the 2023 local government elections on resolving the city's entrenched civic infrastructure deficits, distinguishing its campaign from religious mobilization by prioritizing practical urban governance reforms.[20] The party highlighted chronic issues including acute water shortages, dysfunctional sewerage networks, inefficient solid waste disposal, and unregulated encroachments that exacerbate flooding and habitat loss, attributing these failures to decades of mismanagement and corruption by incumbent administrations.[55] JI pledged enhanced transparency and accountability in local administration to curb graft, promising streamlined service delivery through community oversight mechanisms and efficient resource allocation from municipal revenues.[55] It advocated for inclusive urban planning that extends basic amenities to all residents, encompassing non-Muslims, transgender individuals, and low-income neighborhoods often sidelined in policy execution.[55] The platform emphasized reviving stalled infrastructure projects, such as improved drainage systems and public transport integration, to mitigate Karachi's vulnerability to seasonal monsoons and daily mobility challenges. Complementing ground-level rallies and door-to-door canvassing, JI leveraged social and digital media to amplify its message, focusing on voter grievances over livability rather than doctrinal appeals, which resonated with an electorate disillusioned by partisan neglect.[20] This approach underscored a commitment to evidence-based governance, critiquing the provincial government's tax collection without corresponding investment in urban upkeep.

Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf Efforts

Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) initiated an aggressive campaign in Karachi for the local government elections scheduled on January 15, 2023, aiming to challenge the dominance of established parties amid political instability following the ouster of its founder Imran Khan in April 2022.[56] Party leaders positioned PTI as an alternative to backroom political deals, with senior figure Asad criticizing rivals for secretive negotiations that undermined transparent politics.[56] Imran Khan directly appealed to Karachi residents through a video message on January 13, 2023, urging high voter turnout to support PTI candidates against parties accused of exploiting the city for decades.[57] [58] He emphasized PTI's role in confronting entrenched interests that had "sucked the nation's blood" over 30 years, framing the elections as an opportunity for change in municipal governance.[57] PTI fielded candidates across numerous union councils, focusing on grassroots mobilization despite legal and administrative hurdles, including challenges to the appointment of city administrators perceived as favoring rivals.[59] Post-polling, PTI explored alliances, such as contacts with Jamaat-e-Islami, indicating a strategy to consolidate opposition seats in the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation.[60] This approach reflected PTI's broader effort to penetrate urban Sindh electorates traditionally loyal to ethnic-based parties.[56]

Muttahida Qaumi Movement Positions

The Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P) centered its campaign stance on demands for electoral reforms prior to the 2023 Karachi local government elections, emphasizing the need for fresh delimitation of constituencies to address perceived underrepresentation of urban populations. The party argued that boundaries drawn from the 2017 census data failed to account for Karachi's demographic shifts, particularly disadvantaging densely populated Muhajir-majority areas.[61] This position stemmed from MQM-P's advocacy for constituencies aligned with the digital census conducted in 2022-2023, which they claimed would better reflect urban-rural population balances in Sindh.[62] MQM-P insisted that elections should be deferred until voter lists were verified and updated, citing discrepancies that could enable manipulation favoring rural-based parties like the Pakistan Peoples Party.[63] Party leaders, including convener Khalid Maqbool Siddiqui, warned of street protests if these demands were ignored, framing the polls as inherently flawed without procedural corrections.[64] The party lobbied the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) to postpone voting scheduled for January 15, 2023, but received assurances that delimitation changes would not occur in time.[16] On January 14, 2023, MQM-P announced a boycott of the elections in Karachi and Hyderabad, describing the process as "already rigged" due to unresolved reservations over delimitation and voter rolls.[18] This withdrawal effectively curtailed detailed policy campaigning, though the party continued to highlight governance failures such as water shortages and infrastructure deficits in urban centers as underlying motivations for seeking fair representation.[65] Post-election, MQM-P pointed to low voter turnoutâ€”estimated below 20% in many areasâ€”as validation of their claims of systemic irregularities.[65]

Other Parties and Independents

The Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) fielded candidates across select union councils in Karachi, emphasizing infrastructure development and urban governance reforms amid its national coalition dynamics, though its campaign presence remained subdued compared to dominant parties.[5] The party secured 7 union council seats in the January 15, 2023, polls, primarily in areas with historical Punjabi voter concentrations.[5] Jamiat Ulema-e-Islam Fazl (JUI-F) and Tehreek-i-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP) contested on platforms centered on religious observance and anti-corruption appeals to conservative segments, with JUI-F targeting Pashtun and religious enclaves and TLP mobilizing through protests against perceived elite dominance. JUI-F won 3 seats, while TLP obtained 2.[5] These outcomes reflected limited organizational reach in Karachi's diverse urban landscape, where broader parties overshadowed niche religious mobilization. Independent candidates, often local notables or defectors from major parties, ran on personalized pledges addressing neighborhood-specific woes like sewage and electricity shortages, bypassing formal party structures. They captured 3 union council seats, underscoring sporadic reliance on grassroots ties rather than structured campaigns.[5] Overall, these entities accounted for a marginal share of the 246 union councils, highlighting the elections' consolidation around established players.[5]

Conduct of Elections

Timeline of Key Events

  • November 22, 2022: The Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) announced that the delayed local government elections in Karachi and Hyderabad divisions, part of Sindh's second phase, would be held on January 15, 2023.[66]
  • January 13, 2023: The Sindh provincial government issued a notification attempting to postpone the polls in Karachi, Hyderabad, and Dadu districts citing security concerns, but the ECP rejected the move and ordered elections to proceed as scheduled, directing authorities to ensure foolproof security arrangements.[67][49]
  • January 14, 2023: The Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), a coalition partner of the ruling Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) at the provincial level, withdrew its candidates from the Karachi and Hyderabad polls, protesting perceived rigging and lack of fair conditions.[16]
  • January 15, 2023: Polling occurred across 503 union councils in Karachi and Hyderabad divisions to elect over 3,500 councilors, with observers noting generally peaceful operations despite isolated complaints of mismanagement at some stations; voter turnout varied but was reported as moderate in urban areas.[6][39]
  • January 16, 2023: The ECP released the complete results nearly 30 hours after polls closed, showing the PPP securing 93 union council seats in Karachi, followed by Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) with 40; delays were attributed to compilation issues and verification processes.[5]

Polling Day Operations

Polling for the 2023 Karachi local government elections occurred on January 15, 2023, utilizing approximately 5,000 polling stations across the city, secured by more than 25,000 police personnel deployed to maintain order.[68] The Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) had instructed Sindh authorities to implement foolproof security and logistical measures, with security forces present at 92% of stations before voting began and maintaining coverage both inside (73%) and outside (89%) during the process.[49][69] Observers from the Free and Fair Election Network (FAFEN), which monitored 10% of polling stations in Karachi and Hyderabad divisions, reported the voting as largely peaceful and orderly, with 74% of stations operational by 8:00 AM and the median first vote cast at 8:41 AM.[69] Facilities included 30,399 polling booths province-wide for the phase, with 86% in separate rooms and 92% fitted with secrecy screens; 24% of booths had CCTV surveillance, while 29% of stations were classified as highly sensitive.[69] Staffing comprised 94% trained presiding officers, though 8% of booths lacked assistant presiding officers and gender staffing gaps persisted in 18-19% of female sections.[69] Voter processing followed standard protocols, averaging four minutes per individual: 98% were checked for prior indelible ink marks, 99% had names struck from registers, and 88% received ballots per procedure (86% stamped, 85% signed by staff).[69] Minor issues included 77% of stations near unauthorized campaign camps and brief staff breaks at 3% of booths (averaging 15 minutes), but these did not broadly disrupt operations. In Karachi specifically, eight verbal altercations or minor incidents in districts like Central, South, and Kemari caused temporary pauses of 10-20 minutes, yet no widespread violence or systemic failures occurred on polling day.[69] FAFEN noted the day's management as effective, contrasting with later tabulation challenges.[69]

Reported Irregularities and Management Issues

The second phase of the Sindh local government elections, held on January 15, 2023, in Karachi and other districts, proceeded peacefully overall, with no major incidents of violence reported at polling stations, though operational challenges included delays in the distribution of polling materials, which were eventually provided by evening.[70][5] Lax compliance with ballot issuance procedures was observed, alongside persistent violations of campaigning restrictions in the lead-up to polling day.[6] Results management emerged as a primary issue, with the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) delaying the release of consolidated outcomes for Karachi's 235 union committees by approximately 30 hours after polls closed at 5 p.m., attributing this to the manual compilation process involving Forms 11 and 12 from multiple polling stations per union committee, without deployment of the Result Transmission System (RTS).[5] The Sindh Election Commissioner explained the complexity arose from returning officers overseeing results for several union committees simultaneously, leading to provisional results taking up to two days in Karachi while consolidated figures for some areas remained pending longer.[71][5] These delays fueled allegations of deliberate manipulation from opposition parties; Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) Karachi chief Hafiz Naeemur Rahman accused deputy commissioners of interfering on behalf of the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP)-led Sindh government, claiming JI agents were denied access to Forms 11 and 12, enabling the "stealing" of their mandate, and demanded immediate result announcements under threat of citywide sit-ins.[70] JI further alleged tampering in 94 seats, asserting evidence from Form 11s showed eight victories wrongly awarded to PPP, and called for recounts in 10 union committees.[5] Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) rejected the outcomes entirely, charging the ECP, PPP, and local administration with rigging through political engineering and demanding resignations of involved officials.[5] The Free and Fair Election Network (FAFEN) highlighted transparency deficits, noting that 15% of examined Form XI statements (detailing vote counts) contained omissions such as missing polling station identifiers, voter statistics, or signatures, which compounded public distrust amid the delays and raised concerns about procedural integrity without evidence of systemic fraud.[71] The ECP maintained that elections were conducted transparently and promised completion of all results, though critics pointed to underlying legislative weaknesses in results handling as a contributing factor to such issues.[5][71]

Results

Union Council Outcomes

In the 2023 Karachi local government elections held on January 15, union council (UC) chairmanships were directly elected in 235 of the city's 246 UCs, with the remaining 11 postponed due to candidate deaths. The outcomes, finalized by the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) in March 2023 following multiple recounts and complaints in 20 UCs, resulted in the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) and Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) securing an equal number of UC chairmanships, marking a closely contested balance between the two parties despite PPP's provincial dominance in Sindh. Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) obtained 40 chairmanships, Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) 5, and Jamiat Ulema-e-Islam (Fazl) (JUI-F) 1, with independents and smaller parties accounting for the rest. These chairmanships, each representing control over local administrative functions like sanitation, water supply, and community development within their UC boundaries, also served as the electoral college for higher-tier positions including town municipal corporations and the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC) mayor. Initial results announced by the ECP approximately 30 hours post-polling showed PPP ahead with 93 UC wins, PTI with 40, PML-N with 7, and JI trailing but strong in urban strongholds like District Central and East; however, recountsâ€”prompted by petitions from losing candidates, particularly JIâ€”flipped several UCs, including at least two from PPP to JI in District Central, narrowing the gap to near parity by the final tally. By early March, prior to full resolution of withheld results, media reports indicated PPP at 84 and JI at 82 among decided UCs, with the remaining outcomes confirming equality. Subsequent by-elections in May 2023 for 11 UCs saw PPP win 7 chairmanships and JI 4, further solidifying PPP's edge in aggregate control but not altering the main poll's equalized chairman distribution. PTI retained pockets in Korangi and Keamari but underperformed relative to 2015 results amid internal party fractures. Voter turnout for UC chairman polls averaged around 25-30% citywide, lower in opposition bastions due to reported apathy and irregularities.

Voter Turnout Statistics

Voter turnout in the 2023 Karachi local government elections, held on January 15, was markedly low, estimated at less than 20% overall across the city's districts, with the exception of Malir district where participation was relatively higher.[69] This figure represented a significant decline from the 36% turnout recorded in the 2015 Karachi local elections.[69] The Free and Fair Election Network (FAFEN), which deployed observers to over 1,000 polling stations in Karachi and Hyderabad divisions, attributed the subdued participation primarily to the boycott by the Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), which cited concerns over electoral roll inaccuracies and delimitation issues as justification for abstaining.[69] [72] Turnout varied by district within Karachi, remaining relatively lower in Central, East, West, South, Korangi, and Kemari districts compared to Malir.[69] Across Karachi and Hyderabad divisions combined, approximately 13.3 million voters were registered, but the boycott's impact was most pronounced in urban areas like Karachi, where MQM-P traditionally holds sway.[69] Additional factors contributing to the low engagement included inclement weather on polling day and widespread perceptions of pre-poll manipulation, as voiced by boycotting parties and some observers, though FAFEN noted that polling itself proceeded peacefully in observed stations.[72] [73] The depressed turnout raised questions about the representativeness of the results, particularly in union councils where PPP secured majorities amid reduced participation from urban ethnic constituencies.[69] Independent analyses, including from FAFEN, emphasized that while no comprehensive official turnout data was promptly released by the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP), observer estimates underscored systemic challenges in mobilizing voters for local polls, contrasting with higher participation rates in rural Sindh phases.[69] MQM-P leaders interpreted the low figures as a de facto rejection of the process by Karachi's populace, aligning with their claims of electoral irregularities prior to voting.[74]

Controversies

Allegations of Rigging and Manipulation

Opposition parties, including Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), Jamaat-e-Islami (JI), and Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P), raised allegations of pre-poll manipulation ahead of the January 15, 2023, polling in Karachi, claiming the ruling Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) influenced voter lists and administrative processes to favor its candidates.[77] MQM-P convener Khalid Maqbool Siddiqui specifically accused authorities of "pre-poll rigging" through biased delimitation and exclusion of Karachi's urban representation.[77] JI echoed these concerns, alleging interference by the Sindh provincial government in electoral preparations.[77] On polling day, JI leader Hafiz Naeem ur-Rahman claimed ongoing rigging, asserting that the Sindh government was "interfering" in vote counting and tampering with results at union council levels.[78] PTI reported similar issues, including violations of the Election Commission of Pakistan's (ECP) code of conduct, such as PTI candidate Firdous Shamim Naqvi being booked for alleged ballot box tampering, though PTI dismissed this as politically motivated.[79] The Free and Fair Election Network (FAFEN), which deployed observers, noted peaceful and well-managed polling overall but highlighted lax compliance with ballot issuance procedures and campaigning restrictions, contributing to perceptions of irregularity.[6] Post-polling delays in result announcementsâ€”extending nearly 30 hours in some casesâ€”intensified claims of manipulation, with JI alleging returning officers (ROs) altered Form 11 tally sheets to award seats to PPP candidates despite JI securing majorities in over 100 union councils.[5] [80] JI submitted evidence, including signed Form 11s, to the ECP demanding recounts in at least 10 contested seats where discrepancies showed JI candidates receiving 153 votes against rivals' lower tallies before alleged adjustments.[81] PTI rejected the overall results, accusing the PPP, Sindh administration, and ECP of collusion, claiming mandate theft in approximately 40 union councils and calling for resignations of the ECP chief, Sindh Inspector General of Police, and Karachi Additional Inspector General.[5] In response, PTI and JI formed a joint committee to challenge the outcomes, later expanding accusations of "massive rigging" by PPP across Sindh's local polls.[82] [83] These claims led to clashes during recounts, injuring workers in multiple Karachi areas.[84] The PPP countered by accusing PTI of attempting to sabotage the process and affirmed its victories in over 100 union councils based on official tallies.[5] FAFEN's analysis emphasized that results management flaws, including delays and unaddressed complaints, fueled rigging perceptions and undermined transparency, though it found no systemic evidence of widespread ballot fraud during polling.[85] [73] The ECP initiated probes into specific complaints, reserving decisions on cases involving potential RO misconduct and dismissing at least one officer amid evidence-gathering on electoral fraud.[86]

Evidence and Independent Observations

The Free and Fair Election Network (FAFEN), deploying 104 observers across 343 sampled polling stations in Karachi and Hyderabad divisions, documented a largely peaceful polling process on January 15, 2023, with only 14 verbal altercations recorded compared to 55 violent incidents in the prior phase of Sindh local government elections.[87] [6] FAFEN's observations indicated orderly voter facilitation and minimal disruptions, attributing the calm to adequate security deployment by provincial authorities.[88] Results management, however, drew criticism from FAFEN for substantial delays, with full outcomes for Karachi's 235 contested union committees released over 36 hours after polls ended, far exceeding standard timelines.[87] [5] Observers identified procedural lapses, including incomplete Form-XI result sheets lacking essential details such as polling station names and voter turnout figures, which hindered transparency in aggregation at returning officer offices.[87] These issues, compounded by prior postponements of the pollsâ€”originally slated for July 2022 but delayed thrice due to floods and security constraintsâ€”fueled perceptions of mismanagement without FAFEN confirming systemic fraud.[87] Voter turnout estimates from FAFEN placed Karachi below 20% overall (higher in Malir district), a decline from 36% in the 2015 local polls, potentially reflecting urban apathy or logistical barriers rather than overt suppression.[87] No independent audits or forensic analyses of ballots were reported by observers, limiting verification to procedural compliance; FAFEN emphasized that while campaigning violations persisted pre-poll, polling-day operations met basic standards, though lax ballot issuance protocols warranted scrutiny.[6]

Official Responses and Legal Proceedings

The Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) responded to allegations of irregularities in the 2023 Karachi local elections by taking notice on January 19, 2023, of reported issues in six union councils: UC-3 (Orangi Town 1), UC-7 (Orangi Town 2), UC-8 (Orangi Town 3), UC-3 (Mominabad 4), UC-12 (Manghopir 5), and UC-1 (Gulshan-i-Iqbal).[89][90] This action followed complaints from Jamaat-i-Islami (JI) and Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), who claimed post-polling manipulation favoring the Pakistan People's Party (PPP). The ECP scheduled a hearing for January 23, 2023, issuing notices to the Sindh election commissioner, district returning officers, returning officers, and candidates from the affected union councils to address delays in result compilation exceeding 36 hours and alleged tampering.[89][90] On February 8, 2023, JI submitted documentary evidence to the ECP alleging rigging, including a case where a JI candidate reportedly received 153 votes per Form-11 but was recorded as zero by the returning officer, with similar claims of result alterations in Orangi Town pushing JI to third place.[81] The ECP directed retrieval of original Form-11 documents, summoned the presiding officer for a February 16, 2023, hearing, and expressed concerns over officer performance in related Sindh cases, though it noted that political parties lacked direct standing to file such claimsâ€”requiring individual candidates to do so. PTI separately lodged 14 petitions supported by Form-11 discrepancies from 18 union councils, accusing PPP influence via external officials.[81] Legal challenges extended to the Sindh High Court, where petitions such as Constitutional Petition No. 1504 of 2023 contested results from specific Karachi union councils in the second phase of Sindh local government elections.[91] The court directed formation of an inquiry committee under section 55(4) of the Elections Act, 2017, comprising the Provincial Election Commission Sindh and Regional Election Commissioner Karachi, to investigate complaints against returning officers and potential tribunal decisions. Additional filings invoked election tribunals for recounts or nullifications, but no widespread invalidations of results were reported from these proceedings, with hearings focusing on procedural lapses rather than systemic fraud.[91]

Mayoral Election

Nomination and Campaign Phase

The nomination phase for the Karachi mayoral election commenced following the allocation of reserved seats in the Karachi City Council on June 5, 2023, which confirmed the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) as the largest bloc with 155 seats, including reserved ones for women and minorities, while Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) held 130 seats.[75] [76] The PPP nominated Barrister Murtaza Wahab, a party leader and former adviser to the Sindh chief minister, as its candidate for mayor of the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC).[92] JI selected Hafiz Naeem ur Rehman, the party's Karachi emir, leveraging its strong performance in the underlying January 2023 local council elections.[92] On May 20, 2023, Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), holding approximately 61 seats in the council, announced its unconditional support for Rehman, framing it as an opposition alliance to counter PPP dominance amid allegations of pre-poll irregularities in the local elections.[93] [94] PTI Chairman Imran Khan endorsed this backing on June 1, 2023, urging PTI councilors to vote for Rehman and projecting a combined JI-PTI tally of 191 votes against PPP's estimated 145-147.[95] [96] This alliance shifted the dynamics, as PTI abstained from fielding its own candidate despite earlier considerations.[96] Nomination papers for the June 15, 2023, election were filed on June 11, 2023, with both Wahab and Rehman among numerous aspirants submitting to the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP); scrutiny and acceptance followed standard procedural requirements under the Sindh Local Government Act.[92] The campaign, conducted among the 333 city council members rather than the general public due to the indirect electoral process, centered on behind-the-scenes lobbying, alliance negotiations, and promises of cooperation. JI emphasized anti-corruption and urban service delivery platforms, bolstered by PTI's endorsement, while PPP highlighted its provincial government's resources for infrastructure and development in Karachi.[96] Tensions arose as some PTI councilors reportedly defied party directives, contributing to later expulsions.[97]

Voting Process and Results

The mayoral election for the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC) was conducted on June 15, 2023, at the Arts Council of Pakistan in Karachi.[98] The vote involved elected representatives from the city's local councils, with 333 members participating.[99] Voting proceeded via a show of hands among the councilors, forgoing a secret ballot as stipulated in the procedure overseen by the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP).[100][101] The primary contestants were Barrister Murtaza Wahab, representing the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP), and Hafiz Naeem ur Rehman of Jamaat-e-Islami (JI).[102][8] Wahab won with 173 votes, surpassing Rehman's 160 votes, thereby becoming the first PPP-affiliated mayor in Karachi's history.[99][100][102]

Immediate Disputes and Challenges

The mayoral election on June 15, 2023, proceeded via a show of hands at the Karachi Arts Council, with media access barred, prompting immediate procedural concerns raised by opposition parties.[102] Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) alleged that over 30 of its union council members were subjected to "abuse of power" by the Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP)-controlled Sindh government, including forcible disappearances and detention in safe houses to prevent them from voting in support of Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) candidates.[103] This abstention reduced JI's anticipated tally from 192 votes to 160, enabling PPP candidate Murtaza Wahab to secure victory with 173 votes despite falling short of the 184-vote simple majority threshold under the Sindh Local Government Act.[102][104] JI immediately rejected the results as rigged, claiming that 31 PTI union council chairmen were not produced for voting despite court directives, and accused the PPP of coercing PTI members to withhold support.[102] JI leader Hafiz Naeemur Rehman described the election as "fake," asserting that the PPP had "robbed" seats in prior local polls and imposed the mayor through manipulation of PTI votes and reserved seats.[105] The party demanded that the Election Commission of Pakistan annul the poll and schedule a re-election.[104] PPP representatives, including Saeed Ghani, countered that PTI abstentions stemmed from internal party dissent rather than government interference, with no evidence of coercion provided by PTI.[102] Post-voting violence erupted outside the polling venue, as PPP and JI workers engaged in stone-pelting clashes, leading to a police baton charge and the arrest of eight JI activists.[103][104] JI responded by declaring June 16 a "Black Day" for nationwide protests and vowing to pursue legal and democratic challenges against the outcome.[102][103] These events underscored deep partisan distrust, with opposition claims of state-backed manipulation unverified by independent observers at the time, though JI announced plans for further strategy disclosure amid ongoing tensions.[104]

Aftermath

Formation of Local Government

Following the January 15, 2023, Union Committee elections, councilors for the Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC) were elected indirectly through proportional representation based on party performance in the 235 urban Union Committees. The Pakistan Peoples Party (PPP) secured the highest number of seats in the KMC, with 94 councilors, ahead of Muttahida Qaumi Movement-Pakistan (MQM-P) with 41, Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) with 28, and Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) with 20.[5][106] This composition enabled PPP to claim the right to form the local government despite lacking an absolute majority, relying on support from independents and smaller parties.[102] The mayoral election, delayed for months amid disputes over polling irregularities, took place on June 15, 2023, at the Arts Council of Pakistan. PPP nominee Barrister Murtaza Wahab defeated JI candidate Hafiz Naeem-ur-Rehman in a vote conducted by show of hands, securing 173 votes to Rehman's 161 out of approximately 334 participating councilors.[102][8][100] Wahab was sworn in as mayor the following day by Sindh Local Government Minister Syed Nasir Hussain Shah, becoming the first PPP-affiliated mayor in Karachi's history and marking the party's control over the city's executive.[102][107] Simultaneously, PPP's Salman Murad was elected deputy mayor.[99] The formation proceeded with Wahab assuming office amid protests from opposition parties, who alleged vote manipulation and abstentions by some councilors.[108][109] PPP subsequently filled key administrative positions, including the appointment of municipal commissioners and oversight of town administrations, consolidating its governance structure over Karachi's local bodies.[110] By September 2023, Wahab and Murad contested by-elections in PPP strongholds to bolster their representation further.[110]

Initial Governance Actions

Following his election on June 15, 2023, and oath-taking as mayor on June 19, 2023, Murtaza Wahab prioritized addressing Karachi's chronic infrastructure deficits, identifying water supply, sanitation, sewerage systems, road networks, and transport as immediate focus areas.[111] [112] He pledged to complete the majority of ongoing development projects within the first 100 days, emphasizing collaboration with opposition parties to foster upliftment without discrimination.[113] Wahab also committed to making the city "clean and green," aligning with provincial government initiatives to enhance urban hygiene and aesthetics.[113] [114] The inaugural Karachi City Council meeting on July 17, 2023, under Wahab's leadership, aimed to advance these agendas but quickly devolved into disorder amid opposition protests over alleged electoral irregularities and procedural disputes, leading to its postponement.[115] [116] Despite such hurdles, the administration moved forward with vows to "rebrand" Karachi through targeted interventions in public services, though implementation faced constraints from limited municipal funding and reliance on provincial allocations.[112] Early efforts included directives for improved waste management and sewerage maintenance, reflecting PPP's broader electoral promises to rectify decades of neglect in municipal governance.[114]

Ongoing Criticisms and Performance Metrics

The PPP-led Karachi Metropolitan Corporation (KMC), established after the 2023 local elections with Murtaza Wahab as mayor, has encountered sustained criticisms for limited progress in addressing chronic urban challenges, including water scarcity, waste accumulation, and infrastructure decay. Opposition lawmakers and civic observers have highlighted the administration's failure to distribute development funds to union councils despite Karachi's revenue generation capacity, attributing this to mismanagement and political priorities.[117] Road networks frequently suffer damage from seasonal rains, exacerbating mobility issues and prompting assembly-level rebukes for inadequate maintenance.[118] Water supply deficiencies persist as a core complaint, with the Karachi Water and Sewerage Corporation delivering roughly 665 million gallons per day (MGD) against a demand ranging from 820 to 1,200 MGD, a shortfall unchanged over 17 years of PPP control in Sindh province.[119] [120] Solid waste management lags, contributing to sanitation breakdowns and descriptions of the city as "unliveable" amid pollution and untreated effluents.[121] Governance lapses, such as potential millions wasted on unverified "ghost employees" within KMC payrolls, have fueled audits and accountability demands.[122] Performance indicators show mixed results: KMC revenue reportedly doubled to 2.8 billion rupees in the mayor's first year through enhanced collections.[123] A World Bank assessment awarded KMC a 76 percent score in its 2024 annual review under the Competitive and Livable City project, qualifying it for further funding disbursements.[124] Nonetheless, public backlash intensified over 2025 utility charge hikes perceived as disconnected from service upgrades, leading to trader protests and payment boycotts.[125] Structural constraints on the mayor's authority, amid overlapping provincial and federal jurisdictions, further limit municipal efficacy in Pakistan's megacity.[126] Transparency International Pakistan's 2024 Sindh Local Government District Performance Index underscores citizen feedback on these gaps, advocating greater local empowerment for measurable gains.[53]
