Orthomyxoviridae

Orthomyxoviridae (from Ancient Greek ὀρθός (orthós) 'straight' and μύξα (mýxa) 'mucus') is a family of negative-sense RNA viruses. It includes nine genera: Alphainfluenzavirus, Betainfluenzavirus, Gammainfluenzavirus, Deltainfluenzavirus, Isavirus, Mykissvirus, Quaranjavirus, Sardinovirus, and Thogotovirus.[1] The first four genera contain viruses that cause influenza in birds (see also avian influenza) and mammals, including humans. Isaviruses infect salmon; Mykissvirus infects rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss); Sardinovirus infects pilchards and other fish. The thogotoviruses and quaranjaviruses are arboviruses, infecting vertebrates and invertebrates (such as ticks and mosquitoes).[2][3][4] The four genera of influenza virus that infect vertebrates, which are identified by antigenic differences in their nucleoprotein and matrix protein, are as follows:
  • Alphainfluenzavirus infects humans, other mammals, and birds, and causes all flu pandemics
  • Betainfluenzavirus infects humans and seals
  • Gammainfluenzavirus infects humans and pigs
  • Deltainfluenzavirus infects pigs and cattle.

Structure

Virions of viruses in the Orthomyxoviridae family are pleomorphic enveloped particles that can adopt spherical or filamentous forms. Spherical virions are typically ellipsoidal with diameters of 80–120 nm, while filamentous forms have diameters of 80–100 nm and lengths up to 20 μm.[5] The envelope, derived from host cell membranes, contains embedded virus-encoded glycoproteins that mediate attachment and entry. In the influenza genera (Alphainfluenzavirus to Deltainfluenzavirus), these include hemagglutinin (HA) for receptor binding and fusion, and neuraminidase (NA) for release, with approximately 500–1000 surface spikes (10–14 nm in length) and an HA:NA ratio of about 10:1 in influenza A viruses.[6] The lipid bilayer envelope encloses the viral ribonucleoprotein complexes (vRNPs), which consist of the genomic RNA segments (6–8 per virion, varying by genus) bound to nucleoprotein (NP) and polymerase components. These vRNPs are filamentous structures, 50–130 nm long and 9–15 nm in diameter, exhibiting helical symmetry and often featuring loops at one or both ends; their arrangement within the virion is not fully resolved. In non-influenza genera, such as Thogotovirus, the envelope features a single major glycoprotein instead of separate HA and NA.[7][8]

Genome

For an in-depth example, see H5N1 genetic structure. Viruses of the family Orthomyxoviridae contain six to eight segments of linear negative-sense single stranded RNA. The number of segments varies by genus: six in Thogotovirus, seven in Gammainfluenzavirus (influenza C) and Deltainfluenzavirus (influenza D), and eight in the others. They have a total genome length that is 10,000–14,600 nucleotides (nt).[9] The influenza A genome, for instance, has eight pieces of segmented negative-sense RNA (13.5 kilobases total).[10] The best-characterised of the influenzavirus proteins are hemagglutinin and neuraminidase, two large glycoproteins found on the outside of the viral particles. Hemagglutinin is a lectin that mediates binding of the virus to target cells and entry of the viral genome into the target cell.[11] In contrast, neuraminidase is an enzyme involved in the release of progeny virus from infected cells, by cleaving sugars that bind the mature viral particles. The hemagglutinin (H) and neuraminidase (N) proteins are key targets for antibodies and antiviral drugs,[12][13] and they are used to classify the different serotypes of influenza A viruses, hence the H and N in H5N1. The genome segments have conserved terminal sequences at the 5' and 3' ends that are partially complementary, facilitating replication and packaging. The 5'-terminal sequence is 13 nucleotides long, and the 3'-terminal sequence is 12 nucleotides long. These sequences are highly similar across segments and genera within the family.[14] Encapsidated nucleic acid is solely genomic. Each virion may contain defective interfering copies. In influenza A viruses, PB1-F2 is produced from an alternative reading frame in the PB1 segment. The M and NS genes produce two proteins each (four proteins total) via alternative splicing.[15]

Orthomyxovirus Replication Cycle

Replication cycle

The virus interacts with the host cell via its surface hemagglutinin (HA) glycoprotein, which binds to sialic acid residues on the surfaces of epithelial cells in the respiratory tract.[16] The cell imports the virus by endocytosis. In the acidic pH environment of the endosome, conformational changes in the HA protein fuse the viral envelope with the endosomal membrane, releasing the viral ribonucleoprotein complexes—containing the viral RNA (vRNA) segments, nucleoprotein (NP), and viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (vRdRp)—into the host cell's cytoplasm (Stage 2).[17] These viral ribonucleoproteins (vRNPs) are transported into the host cell nucleus, where the vRdRp transcribes the negative-sense vRNA into positive-sense mRNA (for protein synthesis) and complementary positive-sense cRNA (as a template for new vRNA synthesis) (Steps 3a and b).[18] The viral mRNA is exported to the cytoplasm and translated by host ribosomes (step 4). Some newly synthesized viral proteins, such as HA and neuraminidase (NA), are processed through the Golgi apparatus and transported to the host cell surface (step 5b), while nuclear proteins like NP and polymerase subunits return to the nucleus to bind newly synthesized vRNA and assemble vRNPs (step 5a). Other viral proteins, such as non-structural protein 1 (NS1), interfere with host antiviral responses, including inhibition of host mRNA processing and export.[19] A new virion assembles as negative-sense vRNPs (forming the genomes of progeny viruses), vRdRp, and other viral proteins associate with the plasma membrane, where HA and NA cluster. The vRNPs migrate to this site and are incorporated as the virus buds from the host cell, acquiring a host-derived phospholipid envelope studded with HA and NA (steps 6 and 7).[20] The progeny viruses are released from the cell surface as NA cleaves sialic acid residues, preventing reattachment.[16] The infected host cell typically dies by apoptosis or lysis, allowing spread to adjacent cells. Orthomyxoviruses are negative-sense RNA viruses that replicate their genome in the host cell nucleus. Their vRdRp cannot initiate de novo RNA synthesis and instead uses a process called cap snatching to prime viral mRNA transcription.[21] In the nucleus, the PB2 subunit of vRdRp binds the 5′ cap of host pre-mRNAs, while the PA subunit's endonuclease activity cleaves the cap-containing oligonucleotide (10–13 nucleotides). This capped primer is then used by PB1 to transcribe viral mRNA from the vRNA template, ensuring the viral mRNA has a 5′ cap for efficient host ribosome recognition and translation.[22] Without RNA proofreading enzymes, the vRdRp has a high error rate of approximately 10^{-5} mutations per nucleotide incorporated, leading to frequent genetic variation in progeny viruses.[23] In genera with segmented genomes, such as the eight segments in influenza A and B viruses, co-infection of a cell by multiple strains enables reassortment of genome segments. This can generate progeny with novel combinations of genes, potentially altering host range, virulence, or antigenicity (known as antigenic shift when evading immunity).[12] While detailed here for influenza viruses (genera Alphainfluenzavirus to Deltainfluenzavirus), replication in other genera (e.g., Thogotovirus, Isavirus) follows similar nuclear transcription and cap-snatching mechanisms but with genus-specific variations in genome segmentation and host interactions.[9][1]

Influenza Types

There are four genera of influenza virus, each containing only a single species, or type. Influenza A and C infect a variety of species (including humans), while influenza B almost exclusively infects humans, and influenza D infects cattle and pigs.[24][25][26]

Influenza A

Influenza A viruses are further classified, based on the viral surface proteins hemagglutinin (HA or H) and neuraminidase (NA or N). 18 HA subtypes (or serotypes) and 11 NA subtypes of influenza A virus have been isolated in nature. Among these, the HA subtype 1-16 and NA subtype 1-9 are found in wild waterfowl and shorebirds and the HA subtypes 17-18 and NA subtypes 10-11 have only been isolated from bats.[27][28] Further variation exists; thus, specific influenza strain isolates are identified by the Influenza virus nomenclature,[29] specifying virus type, host species (if not human), geographical location where first isolated, laboratory reference, year of isolation, and HA and NA subtype.[30][31] Examples of the nomenclature are:
  1. A/Brisbane/59/2007 (H1N1) - isolated from a human
  2. A/swine/South Dakota/152B/2009 (H1N2) - isolated from a pig
The type A influenza viruses are the most virulent human pathogens among the three influenza types and cause the most severe disease. It is thought that all influenza A viruses causing outbreaks or pandemics originate from wild aquatic birds.[32] All influenza A virus pandemics since the 1900s were caused by Avian influenza, through Reassortment with other influenza strains, either those that affect humans (seasonal flu) or those affecting other animals (see 2009 swine flu pandemic).[33] The serotypes that have been confirmed in humans, ordered by the number of confirmed human deaths, are:
  • H1N1 caused "Spanish flu" in 1918 and "Swine flu" in 2009.[34]
  • H2N2 caused "Asian Flu".
  • H3N2 caused "Hong Kong Flu".
  • H5N1, "avian" or "bird flu", involved in an ongoing multi-species outbreak since 2020 with 972 confirmed human cases and 468 deaths as of February 2025.[35]
  • H7N7 has unusual zoonotic potential.[36]
  • H1N2 infects pigs and humans.[37]
  • H9N2, H7N2, H7N3, H10N7.

Influenza B

Influenza B virus is almost exclusively a human pathogen, and is less common than influenza A. The only other animal known to be susceptible to influenza B infection is the seal.[45] This type of influenza mutates at a rate 2–3 times lower than type A[46] and consequently is less genetically diverse, with only one influenza B serotype.[24] The B/Yamagata lineage has not been detected globally since March 2020 and is considered extinct as of 2025, leading to recommendations for trivalent vaccines.[47][48] As a result of this lack of antigenic diversity, a degree of immunity to influenza B is usually acquired at an early age. However, influenza B mutates enough that lasting immunity is not possible.[49] This reduced rate of antigenic change, combined with its limited host range (inhibiting cross species antigenic shift), ensures that pandemics of influenza B do not occur.[47]

Influenza C

The influenza C virus infects humans and pigs, and can cause severe illness and local epidemics.[50] However, influenza C is less common than the other types and usually causes mild disease in children.[51][52]

Influenza D

This is a genus that was classified in 2016, the members of which were first isolated in 2011 from pigs; primarily infects cattle but also detected in pigs, sheep, goats, horses, and camels, with no confirmed human cases as of 2025.[53][54][55][56] This genus appears to be most closely related to Influenza C, from which it diverged several hundred years ago.[54] There are at least two extant strains of this genus.[55]

Viability and disinfection

Mammalian influenza viruses tend to be labile, but they can survive several hours in a host's mucus.[57] Avian influenza viruses can survive up to 100 days in distilled water at 17 °C (63 °F), with shorter survival at higher temperatures.[58] The avian virus is inactivated more quickly in manure but can survive up to 30–35 days in feces at 4 °C (39 °F) and up to 7 days at 20 °C (68 °F).[57] Avian influenza viruses can survive indefinitely when frozen.[57] Influenza viruses are susceptible to bleach, 70% ethanol, aldehydes, oxidizing agents and quaternary ammonium compounds. They are inactivated by heat at 133 °F (56 °C) for minimum of 60 minutes, as well as by low pH <2.[57]

Vaccination and prophylaxis

Vaccines and drugs are available for the prophylaxis and treatment of influenza virus infections. Seasonal influenza vaccines include inactivated (egg- or cell-based), live attenuated, and recombinant formulations targeting influenza A(H1N1pdm09), A(H3N2), and B/Victoria lineage viruses. For the 2025–2026 season, all vaccines are trivalent. Because the antigenicities of the wild viruses evolve, vaccines are reformulated annually by updating the seed strains.[59][60] More specifically, many flu vaccines are made using the reassortment method, which has been used for over 50 years. In this method, scientists create reassortant seed strains by co-infecting cells with a high-yield, non-pathogenic strain and a contemporary strain possessing the desired hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) surface antigens. These antigens are selected based on surveillance of circulating viruses expected to predominate in the upcoming season. The reassortant viruses are then propagated in chicken eggs (for egg-based vaccines) or mammalian cells (for cell-based vaccines) to produce the vaccine antigens.[61] Recombinant vaccines, such as Flublok, are produced by inserting the HA gene from circulating strains into a baculovirus vector, which is used to express the protein in insect cells, avoiding the need for egg or cell culture of live virus.[61] When the antigenicities of the seed strains and wild viruses do not match, vaccines may provide reduced protection.[59] Drugs recommended by the CDC for the treatment and prophylaxis of influenza include oseltamivir (Tamiflu), zanamivir (Relenza), peramivir (Rapivab), and baloxavir marboxil (Xofluza). Oseltamivir, zanamivir, and peramivir are neuraminidase inhibitors that prevent the release of virions from infected cells. Baloxavir marboxil inhibits the cap-dependent endonuclease activity of the viral polymerase, blocking viral replication. These drugs are most effective when initiated within 48 hours of symptom onset and are prioritized for high-risk patients. Resistance to neuraminidase inhibitors occurs but is less common than historical resistance to adamantanes (amantadine and rimantadine), which are no longer recommended due to widespread resistance in influenza A viruses.[62]
