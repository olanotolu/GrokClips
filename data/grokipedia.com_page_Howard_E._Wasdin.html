Howard E. Wasdin


Howard Eugene Wasdin (November 8, 1961 – April 6, 2023) was an American Navy SEAL sniper who served with SEAL Team Six, earning the Silver Star for gallantry and the Purple Heart for wounds sustained during the Battle of Mogadishu in Somalia in 1993.[1][2] After enlisting in the U.S. Navy and graduating from Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL (BUD/S) training with Class 143, Wasdin completed 12 years of service, including nine as a SEAL, with deployments supporting operations in the Gulf War and other special operations.[3][4] Medically retired following injuries from combat, he transitioned to civilian life as a chiropractor, motivational speaker, and co-author of bestselling memoirs such as SEAL Team Six: Memoirs of an Elite Navy SEAL Sniper, which detailed his rigorous selection, sniper training, and experiences in elite counterterrorism missions.[5][6] Wasdin died in a plane crash near Jesup, Georgia, at age 61.[4]

Early Life

Childhood Adversity and Family Dynamics

Howard E. Wasdin was born on November 8, 1961, in Boynton Beach, Florida, under impoverished conditions that included his parents' extreme financial hardship.[7] His biological father abandoned the family early, prompting his mother to flee with the infant Wasdin to start anew.[8] She remarried when Wasdin was seven to a man named Leon, who assumed the role of stepfather and exerted harsh control over the household.[8] The family settled in Screven, Georgia, in rural South Georgia, where Wasdin endured routine physical abuse from his stepfather, including severe beatings that left lasting psychological scars.[9][10] Family dynamics were dominated by the stepfather's authoritarian presence and the mother's apparent inaction despite awareness of the violence, fostering an environment of fear and instability for Wasdin.[10] As a child, he contributed to the household by performing demanding farm labor in the fields, which instilled early discipline amid the adversity but did little to mitigate the emotional toll.[9] These experiences, detailed in Wasdin's autobiography SEAL Team Six: Memoirs of an Elite Navy SEAL Sniper, contributed to his development of resilience, though they also precipitated childhood trauma akin to post-traumatic stress.[11] The absence of intervention from family or authorities underscored the isolated rural context, where such domestic hardships were often unaddressed.[12]

Education and Formative Influences

Wasdin experienced a challenging childhood marked by physical abuse from his adoptive father in Screven, Georgia, where the family relocated when he was four years old.[12][9] This environment of repeated beatings and hardship instilled a drive for self-reliance and resilience, which Wasdin later described as forging his mental toughness for future endeavors.[13][14] He graduated from Wayne County High School in Screven, Georgia.[4] Following high school, Wasdin briefly attended college to pursue higher education but left after approximately one and a half years to enlist in the U.S. Navy, seeking financial stability and an opportunity to continue his studies through military benefits.[9][14] This period of academic interruption underscored his formative shift toward military service as a pathway out of personal adversity and toward structured achievement.

Military Enlistment and Training

Initial Navy Service

Wasdin enlisted in the United States Navy in 1983, motivated by financial needs following attempts at college, and initially trained as a search-and-rescue swimmer.[15][16] He served as an antisubmarine warfare operator and rescue swimmer with Helicopter Anti-Submarine Squadron 7 (HS-7), a unit focused on anti-submarine operations from helicopter platforms.[17][18] Assigned to HS-7, Wasdin deployed aboard the aircraft carrier USS John F. Kennedy in 1986, participating in naval operations during the squadron's Mediterranean cruise.[17] He performed duties including sonar operations and rescue missions, earning recognition for his performance in high-stakes environments such as tracking submarine contacts.[18] His service in this role demonstrated proficiency in aviation rescue and anti-submarine tactics, contributing to the squadron's operational readiness.[17] Wasdin completed his initial enlistment contract with HS-7, during which he advanced in rank and skill, before re-enlisting with the explicit goal of attending Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL (BUD/S) training.[18] This period of conventional Navy service, spanning approximately four years, provided foundational experience in maritime operations and rescue that informed his later special warfare pursuits.[15]

BUD/S and SEAL Qualification

After serving his initial enlistment with Helicopter Anti-Submarine Squadron 7 (HS-7), Wasdin re-enlisted in the U.S. Navy to pursue qualification as a SEAL, reporting to the Naval Special Warfare Center in Coronado, California, for Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL (BUD/S) training.[17] BUD/S Class 143 emphasized extreme physical and mental endurance, beginning with pre-training conditioning such as ocean swims and beach calisthenics in cold Pacific waters.[3][19] Candidates underwent physical screening tests requiring specified numbers of push-ups, sit-ups, chin-ups, a timed run, and a swim, followed by surf immersion with inflatable boats (IBS) to foster teamwork amid wave capsizing.[19] The pinnacle of first-phase training was Hell Week, a 5.5-day ordeal with fewer than four hours of sleep, exposing participants to hypothermia, simulated combat stressors like M-60 machine gun fire and artillery blasts during breakout, and continuous evolutions such as the "Lyon’s Lope" paddling drill.[19] Wasdin struggled with four-mile timed beach runs, incurring "goon squad" punishments from instructors, but outperformed peers on the obstacle course (O-course), scaling a 60-foot cargo net and descending a three-story tower.[19] In drown-proofing exercises, he navigated a 50-meter underwater swim with hands and feet bound, nearly blacking out but succeeding.[19] Tempted to quit amid hypothermia—rejecting offers of hot chocolate and medical evacuation—Wasdin persisted, completing Hell Week under Captain Bailey's oversight.[19] Wasdin graduated BUD/S Class 143, advancing to SEAL Qualification Training (SQT) for specialized tactical instruction, including advanced combat skills, before earning the SEAL Trident insignia.[3][6] This qualification process, rooted in BUD/S's attrition rate exceeding 75%, prepared him for operational roles by instilling unbreakable resolve and proficiency in small-unit tactics.[19]

Service with SEAL Team Six

Roles and Operations Prior to 1993

Following combat deployment with SEAL Team Two during Operation Desert Storm in early 1991, where Wasdin participated in missions including the destruction of an Iraqi command post, he sought assignment to the Naval Special Warfare Development Group (DEVGRU), also known as SEAL Team Six.[20][21] Wasdin successfully completed the rigorous Green Team selection process, DEVGRU's operator assessment course emphasizing close-quarters battle, advanced tactics, and endurance under stress, typically lasting several months with high attrition rates.[21] Upon selection, he joined SEAL Team Six, transitioning from SEAL Team Two around 1991-1992.[22] In SEAL Team Six, Wasdin's primary role was as a sniper, involving specialized training in precision long-range marksmanship, reconnaissance, and high-value target engagement using rifles such as the Remington 700 and modified M40A1 systems.[21] He underwent the unit's sniper course, honing skills in urban and field environments for counterterrorism and direct action raids, earning acclaim as one of DEVGRU's elite shooters.[22][23] Public details on specific SEAL Team Six operations involving Wasdin prior to August 1993 are limited due to classification, with his pre-Somalia service focused on operational readiness, joint exercises, and potential contingency preparations rather than disclosed combat deployments.[21] This period solidified his expertise in sniper overwatch and assault support roles within DEVGRU's squadrons.[22]

Operation Gothic Serpent and the Battle of Mogadishu

In August 1993, Howard E. Wasdin deployed to Mogadishu, Somalia, as a sniper with a four-man SEAL Team Six element attached to Task Force Ranger for Operation Gothic Serpent, a U.S. military effort to neutralize the Somali National Alliance led by warlord Mohamed Farrah Aidid by capturing key figures in his network.[18][24] The SEAL snipers, including Wasdin, operated under Commander Eric Thor Olson and provided specialized overwatch and support alongside Delta Force and Army Rangers, focusing on high-value target raids in the city's hostile urban environment.[18] On October 3, 1993, during a daytime raid targeting two of Aidid's top lieutenants in the Bakara Market district, Wasdin participated as part of a blocking force positioned to interdict militia reinforcements.[18] Approximately 37 minutes into the operation, a Black Hawk helicopter designated Super Six One was struck by an RPG and crashed, prompting Task Force Ranger elements, including the SEAL snipers, to shift to rescue and recovery missions amid escalating Somali militia assaults involving automatic weapons, RPGs, and technicals. Wasdin initially engaged enemy snipers with his CAR-15 rifle for about 30 minutes before rejoining the convoy response.[18][25] As the convoy pushed toward the crash site, it encountered an ambush by Aidid's militia, some disguised as civilians, leading to intense close-quarters fighting; Wasdin, driving a vehicle while returning fire, eliminated nine Somali fighters in full-automatic mode with his CAR-15 before exhausting its ammunition and switching to a 9mm Sig Sauer pistol to continue suppressing threats.[18] Despite sustaining multiple wounds—including a ricochet to his left knee during the initial sniper exchange, a bullet penetrating his right shinbone in a direct firefight, and a round striking his left ankle—he persisted in combat until the vehicle struck a landmine, further complicating the extraction.[18][24] A Quick Reaction Force eventually evacuated the beleaguered elements, with Wasdin receiving immediate medical attention; the battle resulted in 18 U.S. fatalities, over 70 wounded, and the deaths of more than 300 Somali combatants, ultimately contributing to the withdrawal of U.S. forces from Somalia by March 1994.[18] For his gallantry in sustaining effective fire against superior numbers despite severe injuries, Wasdin was awarded the Silver Star, recognizing actions that exemplified "conspicuous gallantry and intrepidity" against an armed enemy.[24][18] He also received the Purple Heart for the wounds sustained, which included near-amputation of his right leg due to the shinbone injury and subsequent complications, foreshadowing his eventual medical retirement from the Navy.[24] These injuries stemmed from small-arms fire and shrapnel in the chaotic urban melee, where Somali forces exploited the downed helicopters to encircle and probe U.S. positions with hit-and-run tactics.[18]

Military Awards and Retirement

Key Decorations and Recognition

Wasdin received the Silver Star for conspicuous gallantry and intrepidity in action against an armed enemy during Operation Gothic Serpent in Mogadishu, Somalia, on October 3–4, 1993. As a member of Task Force Ranger, he supported an assault force that apprehended two high-value militia targets and 22 others, then engaged intense enemy fire while wounded three times in the leg, shoulder, and face. Despite his injuries, Wasdin continued to suppress enemy positions with accurate sniper fire, protecting the Ranger blocking force and aiding the evacuation of a downed helicopter crash site, thereby inspiring his teammates to maintain combat effectiveness under severe duress.[1] For the wounds sustained in the same engagement, Wasdin was awarded the Purple Heart, recognizing his sacrifice in direct combat with hostile forces.[3] These decorations highlight Wasdin's role as a sniper with SEAL Team Six, where his actions exemplified valor in one of the most intense urban battles involving U.S. special operations forces during the early 1990s.[3]

Wounds, Recovery, and Medical Discharge

During the Battle of Mogadishu on October 3, 1993, as part of Operation Gothic Serpent, Wasdin sustained three gunshot wounds to his right leg from AK-47 fire while serving as a sniper with SEAL Team Six.[9][26] The injuries shattered his femur and nearly resulted in amputation of the leg, yet he continued to engage enemy forces and assist in vehicle operation amid intense combat.[27][15] Following evacuation from Somalia, Wasdin underwent multiple surgeries upon return to the United States to address the severe damage, including attempts to repair the non-healing leg wound and restore functionality.[28] Despite these interventions, chronic pain and impaired mobility persisted, rendering him unfit for continued high-intensity SEAL operations.[28][3] Wasdin received a medical discharge from the U.S. Navy in 1995 after 12 years of service, primarily due to the cumulative effects of his Mogadishu injuries, which precluded his return to active duty.[9][3][24]

Post-Military Professional Life

Transition to Chiropractic Practice

Following his medical discharge from the U.S. Navy in the mid-1990s due to severe injuries sustained during the Battle of Mogadishu, Wasdin experienced chronic pain and sought non-surgical relief, initially resisting but eventually consulting a chiropractor in South Florida whose adjustments significantly improved his quality of life and mobility.[22][28] This personal experience, combined with his post-military depression and desire to continue helping others in a capacity akin to his SEAL service, motivated him to pursue chiropractic as a profession.[29][30] Wasdin enrolled in chiropractic college to formalize his training, attending Life University in Georgia, from which he graduated with a Doctor of Chiropractic (D.C.) degree in 2009.[31] His military background emphasized precision and discipline, which he applied to his studies and later practice, viewing chiropractic as a means to address root causes of pain through spinal adjustments rather than symptom management alone.[17] Upon graduation, Wasdin established Absolute Precision Chiropractic in Jesup, Georgia, opening the clinic in 2009 or 2010 to serve patients with conditions ranging from musculoskeletal injuries to those exacerbated by high-stress lifestyles, drawing on his own recovery to inform patient care.[9][4] The practice focused on evidence-based adjustments tailored to individual needs, reflecting Wasdin's commitment to helping veterans and civilians achieve functional restoration without reliance on pharmaceuticals.[29] He continued operating the clinic until his death in 2023, integrating motivational speaking on resilience into his professional outreach.[22]

Business Ventures and Public Engagement

Following his transition to chiropractic care, Wasdin established Absolute Precision Chiropractic in Jesup, Georgia, a practice emphasizing advanced techniques for pain management and overall health optimization, staffed by a team under his leadership.[32] The clinic, located at 936 South First Street, served the local community with services including chiropractic adjustments and related therapies until Wasdin's death in 2023.[29][33] Wasdin expanded his professional reach through public speaking, delivering motivational keynotes on leadership, team building, overcoming adversity, and resilience drawn from his military experience.[23] His engagements included addresses to corporate audiences such as GE and Microsoft, professional groups like the American Chiropractic Association, and educational institutions including Palmer Chiropractic College and Texas Chiropractic College.[24][34] Speaking fees for his presentations typically ranged from $15,000 to $20,000.[17] In community involvement, Wasdin focused on youth development, contributing to programs for children and teens through the Boys and Girls Clubs of America, which recognized his efforts with induction into The Jeremiah Milbank Society in 2012.[24] He also participated in volunteer aviation with Angel Flight, piloting missions to transport medical patients.[35] These activities complemented his speaking career, emphasizing personal growth and service.[6]

Authorship and Public Commentary

Major Publications

Wasdin co-authored the memoir SEAL Team Six: Memoirs of an Elite Navy SEAL Sniper with Stephen Templin, published on May 24, 2011, by St. Martin's Press.[21] The book details his recruitment into the U.S. Navy SEALs, BUD/S training, selection for SEAL Team Six (DEVGRU), sniper operations during Operation Desert Storm, and his wounding during the Battle of Mogadishu in 1993.[36] It achieved New York Times bestseller status, remaining on the list for multiple weeks.[37] In 2012, Wasdin and Templin released I Am a SEAL Team Six Warrior: Memoirs of an American Soldier, a young adult adaptation of the prior memoir published by St. Martin's Griffin.[38] The work emphasizes his childhood challenges, including abuse, and path to elite special operations, tailored for younger readers while retaining core autobiographical elements from his SEAL career.[39] Wasdin collaborated with Templin on the fictional SEAL Team Six Outcasts series, beginning with Outcasts: A SEAL Team Six Novel in November 2012, followed by Easy Day for the Dead in 2013, both published by St. Martin's Press.[5] These thriller novels feature disgraced SEAL operators conducting covert missions against terrorist threats.[2] In 2014, Wasdin co-authored The Last Rescue: How Faith and Love Saved a Navy SEAL Sniper with his wife Debbie Wasdin, published by Thomas Nelson on October 28.[40] The book recounts his post-military struggles with injury recovery, divorce, renewed faith, and second marriage, framing personal redemption amid war's aftermath.[41]

Themes and Reception of His Works

Wasdin's primary work, SEAL Team Six: Memoirs of an Elite Navy SEAL Sniper (2011, co-authored with Stephen Templin), emphasizes the grueling physical and psychological demands of Navy SEAL training, including Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL (BUD/S) and sniper school, portraying these as transformative processes that forge elite warriors capable of operating under extreme stress.[42] The narrative highlights themes of unyielding discipline, team loyalty, and tactical precision, with Wasdin recounting operations in Grenada, the Persian Gulf, and Somalia, where he served as a sniper for SEAL Team Six (later redesignated DEVGRU). Central to the book is his firsthand account of the October 3, 1993, Battle of Mogadishu, detailing sniper overwatch, intense urban combat, and personal wounding by multiple gunshot impacts, underscoring themes of heroism amid chaos and the raw brutality of close-quarters fighting against Somali militiamen.[43] Post-combat struggles, including depression, social isolation, and difficulty reintegrating into civilian life, emerge as recurring motifs, reflecting the long-term costs of special operations service without delving deeply into introspective analysis of personal backstory influences.[44] [45] The memoir's style is visceral and action-oriented, akin to military thrillers, prioritizing operational details over philosophical reflection, which conveys a sense of immediacy but draws criticism for occasional boastfulness in depicting SEAL superiority over other forces.[46] Themes of resilience and adaptation persist in Wasdin's later adaptation, I Am a SEAL Team Six Warrior (2012, aimed at younger readers), which condenses the original's training and combat elements to inspire fortitude and perseverance, though it alternates between high-stakes excitement and slower biographical passages.[47] Reception was broadly positive, with the book achieving commercial success, including a boosted release timing shortly after the May 2011 Osama bin Laden raid by SEAL Team Six, which heightened public interest in the unit's history and capabilities; it garnered over 18,000 Goodreads ratings averaging 4.1 out of 5, praised for its engaging, behind-the-scenes access to elite operations.[36] Critics noted its timeliness and thrilling pace, positioning it within a surge of post-9/11 military memoirs that emphasize heroism and operational grit, though some found it less introspective or entertaining than comparable accounts like Richard Marcinko's Rogue Warrior.[48] [42] No widespread challenges to the memoir's factual accuracy emerged, with reviewers appreciating its alignment with established events like the Mogadishu battle as depicted in Black Hawk Down, though its plainspoken tone limits broader psychological or ethical explorations.[49] [43]

Personal Life and Legacy

Family and Relationships

Howard E. Wasdin's first marriage was to Laura Wasdin, which ended in divorce; the couple had two children together.[50] The dissolution stemmed in part from the demands of his SEAL career, which he later reflected left him more committed to his team than to his family.[10] Following the divorce, Wasdin married Debbie Wasdin (née Snyder), an accountant, with whom he shared a close partnership; the couple co-authored The Last Rescue: A Story of Love, Faith and Spiritual Pursuit in 2015, detailing their personal trials and faith journey.[51] [52] Debbie Wasdin was characterized in his obituary as a loyal, encouraging, and devoted spouse to whom Howard was a loving, hard-working, and doting husband who prioritized her needs exceptionally.[4] The couple resided in Fernandina Beach, Florida, and maintained a strong bond, with Wasdin describing her as his best friend.[10] Wasdin also repaired his strained childhood relationship with his stepfather later in life.[10] At the time of his death on April 6, 2023, Wasdin was survived by his wife Debbie and five children: son Blake Wasdin (married to Jordan) and daughters Eryn Graeter (married to Paul), Rachel Wasdin, and two others not detailed in primary accounts.[4] [53]

Death and Posthumous Impact

Howard E. Wasdin died on April 6, 2023, at age 61, when the single-engine aircraft he was piloting crashed shortly after takeoff from William A. Zorn Airport in Wayne County, Georgia.[3] [4] His canine companion, Tango, also perished in the incident.[4] The National Transportation Safety Board conducted an investigation, issuing a preliminary report that detailed the crash sequence but did not specify a cause, with wreckage examination ongoing as of April 2023.[54] Funeral services for Wasdin were held on April 15, 2023, in Jesup, Georgia, attended by family, fellow veterans, and community members who acknowledged his military service and post-service achievements.[55] [53] Following his death, the National Navy UDT-SEAL Museum published a tribute highlighting Wasdin's BUD/S Class 143 graduation, SEAL Team Six sniper role, wounds sustained in the 1993 Battle of Mogadishu, and subsequent career as a chiropractor and author, framing his life as emblematic of resilience and service.[3] Contemporaries, including former SEAL training classmate Stephen Templin, publicly mourned the loss, recalling personal connections from their shared military experiences.[56] His publications, such as SEAL Team Six: Memoirs of an Elite Navy SEAL Sniper (co-authored with Stephen Templin), have endured as primary accounts of DEVGRU operations, sustaining interest in his firsthand perspectives on special warfare amid limited posthumous events or institutional commemorations reported to date.[57]
