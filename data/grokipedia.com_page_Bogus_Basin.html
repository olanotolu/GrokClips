Bogus Basin

Bogus Basin Mountain Recreation Area is a ski area in the western United States, located in Boise County, Idaho, sixteen road miles (26 km) north-northeast of the city of Boise. Bogus Basin is operated by the Bogus Basin Recreation Association, a non-profit organization, on private[1] and leased land in the Boise National Forest. In 2024, two new high-speed quad chairlifts were installed, enhancing terrain access. Ski season typically runs from late November to mid-April, depending on snow conditions (as of the 2025 season). The area also has cross-country skiing on 23 miles (37 km) of Nordic trails.

History

The area probably got its name during the 19th-century gold rush. Crooks in the hills above Boise City, known as "spelterers", would make bogus gold dust by heating lead filings with a bit of real gold dust.[2] Alf Engen, the father of the American powder technique, selected the site for the ski area at Bogus Basin in 1939.[3] It opened to the public 83 years ago in December 1942 with a 500-foot (150 m) rope tow, and a 3,300-foot (1,010 m) T-bar was installed in 1946.[4][5] In the early 1950s, Bogus had a 30-meter Nordic ski jump, designed by Corey Engen,[6] and his brother Sverre was Bogus' ski instructor.[7] The first chairlift at Bogus was installed in the fall of 1959 at Deer Point,[8] and night skiing debuted in December 1964.[4] The resort operates 7 chairlifts and 4 magic carpets. Four of the chairlifts are high-speed quads (#1 Deer Point , and #6 Pine Creek) were installed in 1996 and 1999, #3 Superior in 2011,[9] and #2 Morning Star in 2019. Bogus Basin has 2,600 acres (4.1 sq mi; 10.5 km2) of mixed runs, bowls, and glades, with 900 acres (3.6 km2) groomed. The lift-served vertical drop is 1,790 ft (546 m) on the east-facing "back side," with a summit elevation of 7,582 ft (2,311 m) above sea level at the top of Shafer Butte, the highest point of the Boise Ridge mountains. This back side of Shafer Butte was opened in January 1977, following the installation of Pine Creek (#6), a double chairlift, the previous summer. A fixed-grip double for 23 seasons, it became a high-speed quad in the summer of 1999.[10][11] On the front side, Bogus Basin's southern lift-served summit is at "Doe Point," adjacent to Deer Point, which is slightly higher and covered with communications towers at an elevation of 7,070 feet (2,155 m). Both vantage points overlook Boise and the entire Treasure Valley, over 4,000 vertical feet (1,220 m) below. Bogus' base area and main day lodge (J. R. Simplot Lodge, formerly Bogus Creek) are at 6,150 ft (1,875 m), at the base of the north-facing slopes served by the Deer Point Express (#1), a high-speed quad installed in the summer of 1996. The original double chairlift on #1 was installed in 1959 and upgraded in 1981. Showcase (#4), a double chairlift that had replaced a surface poma lift in 1972, is east of and parallel with the Deer Point Express. The original Deer Point lift was relocated and renamed Coach (#7) in 1996, servicing the beginner learning area. It honors Bill "Coach" Everts, an early area manager (1953â€“58) and longtime director.[4] At mid-mountain, a second day lodge (Pioneer Lodge - 1973) sits at 6,800 feet (2,070 m) with a sizable parking lot, a cluster of condominia (1975),[4] and the Jason Harper Training Center. From the Pioneer area, there is direct access to the gentle south-facing slopes served by a high-speed quad chairlift, Morning Star (#2) and the north-facing slopes of the Bitterroot (#5), a fixed-grip quad chairlift installed in 2024 (replacing a 1970s double; vertical: 525 ft (160 m)), which operates seven days a week. In addition, there is connecting trail access to the base of the Superior Express (#3) lift. With its 1,500-foot (457 m) vertical rise, the Superior Express serves the advanced and expert terrain on the northern face of Shafer Butte, unloading at 7,480 feet (2,280 m). It replaced a Riblet double chairlift built in 1965,[12][13] and cut the ride time of the original lift in half.[14] Night skiing was added to the Superior area with the installation of lights in the summer of 1986, and Morning Star was converted from a double to a triple chairlift in 1999. Historically, Bogus Basin's average annual snowfall is 200â€“250 inches (510â€“640 cm).[15] Due to limited water resources, there is no significant snow making, only small portable units for patching. Night skiing is available on 165 acres (0.67 km2) on runs served by five of the chairlifts (none on #5 or #6). The main day lodge at Bogus Creek was built in 1962 and expanded in 1991; its ground floor contains the ticket office and ski lockers.[4] In 2002 it was named for agribusiness magnate J. R. Simplot (1909â€“2008), because without him there might not be a Bogus Basin. When the fledgling ski area was struggling to pay its debts in 1953,[16] Simplot bought its ski lifts and other mountain improvements from the Kingcliffe Co.[3] and leased them back to the Bogus Basin Recreational Association for $1,500 per year for ten years.[4] His intervention averted almost certain financial demise and won the everlasting gratitude of a generation of skiers. Simplot was later the driving force behind Brundage Mountain northwest of McCall, which opened in November 1961. In summer 2024, the Coach (#7) and Bitterroot (#5) chairlifts were replaced with new fixed-grip quad chairlifts manufactured by Skytrac to improve capacity and access for beginner and intermediate terrain. Other activities

The GoldRush Tubing Hill opened in the fall of 2003, constructed just west of the main parking lot for about $100,000. Annual revenues from the hill were expected to be four to five times that figure; revenues for its fourth season (2006â€“07) were just under $140,000. The Glade Runner is a year-round operating mountain coaster that opened in December 2017.[19] The base station was constructed a few yards from the J. R. Simplot Lodge, and the mountain coaster features 4,330 feet (1,320 m) of track. Other summer activities are available at Bogus, including climbing, tubing, hiking, mountain biking, and a disc golf course. The Two Deer Disc Golf Course, an 18-hole technically challenging course, opened in 2023. It starts near the Frontier Point Lodge at the base area and features blind drives, bends, downhill and uphill holes, and is designed for various throwing techniques.[20]

Bogus Basin Road

Bogus is accessed by Bogus Basin Road (an extension of Harrison Boulevard), which twists 16 miles (26 km) from the Boise city limits to the resort, only ten miles (16 km) NNE as the crow flies. The two-lane road turns 172 times[3] and gains 3,400 feet (1,040 m) in elevation as the terrain changes from dry sagebrush foothills to snow-laden mountain forest. Originally a gravel road constructed by CCC crews (funded by the WPA) from 1938 to 1940, Bogus Basin Road was first paved in 1962, widened in 1974,[21] and improved in 1998. Before its paving, it was a one-way road in ski season, with the direction changed to downhill in the early afternoon.[4]

Season passes

In March 1998, Bogus' general manager Mike Shirley reduced the cost of an adult season pass from $500 to $199, lowering the break-even point to just seven visits (and kids' season passes fell to just $29). Bogus Basin sold nearly nine times as many passes for the 1998â€“99 season versus the previous year, halting the pass sales at 25,000 (2,854 for the 1997â€“98 season).[22] The new pricing strategy generated almost four times as much revenue (nearly $3.6 million) from season pass sales, all before June, six months before the season would begin. Total skier visits went from under 192,000 to over 303,000 (up 58%). Although the sales of day tickets ($31 each) expectedly fell (almost 50%), Bogus' total revenue increased by $2.6 million (up 55%) to $7.3 million for the 1998-99 ski season.[22] Shirley's deep-discount strategy was effective: resorts from coast to coast lowered their prices for multi-day, multi-area, and season passes.[23] Locally, ski equipment sales increased significantly as skiers upgraded their gear.[22][24][25][26] The $199 price was in effect for fifteen years, until raised to $229 for the 2013â€“14 season.[27] Seniors (age 70 and over)[28] skied for free at Bogus through March 2015; the policy was changed that summer. For the 2015â€“16 season, season passes for seniors were priced at $199 and one-day lift tickets at $44, a discount of ten dollars off the regular daily rate. Since the 2015â€“16 season, prices have increased progressively. By the 2025â€“26 season, the unlimited Winter Season Pass for adults (ages 25â€“69) costs $749 at full price (or $549 during the early sale period from February 28 to March 9, 2025), while seniors (70+) pay $599.[29][30] Bogus Basin has limited season pass sales since the 2020â€“21 season to manage capacity and preserve guest experience.[31]

General managers

After 21 seasons, Mike Shirley announced in February 2012 that he was stepping down as general manager and president, and was succeeded by Alan Moore, the former vice president of finance.[35] Shirley was a former vice president with Morrison-Knudsen Co.[36] and initially hired as an interim manager after the resignation of Terry Lofsvold in November 1991. After the search committee determined he was the best candidate, he was hired in April, the first general manager at Bogus with an extensive background in finance.[4] Moore stepped down in 2015 and was succeeded in November by Brad Wilson, formerly the general manager at Diamond Peak ski area, Nevada, at northeast Lake Tahoe.[37]
