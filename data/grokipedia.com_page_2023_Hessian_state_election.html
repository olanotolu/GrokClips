2023 Hessian state election


The 2023 Hessian state election was held on 8 October 2023 to elect the 133 members of the 21st Landtag of Hesse, the state legislature of the German state of Hesse.[1]

The Christian Democratic Union (CDU), incumbent governing party led by Minister-President Boris Rhein, won 34.6 percent of the second votes, an increase of 7.6 percentage points compared to 2018, translating to 52 seats including overhang mandates from strong performances in direct constituencies.[2][3][1] The election ended the CDU-Greens coalition, as the Greens fell to 14.8 percent and 22 seats, prompting the CDU to form a grand coalition with the Social Democratic Party (SPD), which obtained 15.1 percent and 23 seats, securing a combined majority of 75 seats.[2][1]
The Alternative for Germany (AfD) achieved notable growth to 18.4 percent of the voteâ€”up from 12.8 percent in 2018â€”earning 28 seats and establishing itself as the second-largest parliamentary group, reflecting voter shifts away from established parties.[2][3] The Free Democratic Party (FDP) barely surpassed the five-percent threshold with exactly 5.0 percent, gaining 8 seats, while parties outside the Bundestag's traffic light coalition faced exclusion.[2][4] Voter turnout was 66.0 percent.[2]

Background and context

Pre-election political landscape

The pre-election political landscape in Hesse was dominated by the incumbent coalition government of the Christian Democratic Union (CDU) and the Greens, which had governed the state continuously since 2013 following the CDU's victory in the 2013 state election and renewal after the 2018 poll where the CDU secured 27.0% and the Greens 19.8%.[5] This "black-green" alliance, led initially by Minister-President Volker Bouffier until his resignation announced in October 2021 and effective in 2022, transitioned to Boris Rhein, who was elected to the position on May 31, 2022, maintaining the coalition's focus on economic stability and environmental policies amid Hesse's strong performance, with 0.4% GDP growth ranking it among Germany's top states driven by the Frankfurt financial hub.[6] [5] Opinion polls throughout 2023 consistently showed the CDU leading with 29-32% support, positioning it for re-election, while the Alternative for Germany (AfD) rose to 11-17%, reflecting voter frustration with immigration policies.[7] The Greens polled at 16-22%, with a peak in March before stabilizing lower due to national controversies, and the Social Democrats (SPD) hovered at 16-21%, undermined by federal leadership under Chancellor Olaf Scholz.[7] The Free Democrats (FDP) and The Left (Linke) remained marginal at 5-7% and below, respectively, indicating limited viability for broader coalitions.[7] Over 50% of voters linked their state preferences to dissatisfaction with the federal traffic-light coalition's handling of economic pressures and energy costs following Russia's invasion of Ukraine.[5] Central issues included migration management, with calls for stricter border controls and faster deportations amid high asylum inflows straining local resources, a concern amplified by federal Interior Minister Nancy Faeser's perceived ineffective policies.[5] The AfD leveraged this discontent, advocating rigorous enforcement, while the CDU under Rhein emphasized pragmatic security measures without partnering with the AfD.[5] Additional scrutiny arose from rising right-wing extremism reports by state intelligence and lingering effects of the 2020 Hanau attack, alongside debates over police efficacy, though Hesse's relative economic resilience contrasted with national inflationary woes.[5]

Incumbent government and leadership transition

The incumbent government of Hesse entering the 2023 state election was a coalition between the Christian Democratic Union (CDU) and Alliance 90/The Greens, formed following the 2018 Landtag election and renewed after leadership changes. This black-green coalition had governed the state continuously since 2019, with the CDU holding the position of Minister-President. Volker Bouffier of the CDU had led the government as Minister-President since January 31, 2010, succeeding Roland Koch amid a prior CDU-FDP coalition that transitioned to CDU-Greens after the 2013 election loss of an absolute majority. On February 25, 2022, Bouffier announced his resignation effective May 31, 2022, citing personal reasons after over a decade in office.[8] Boris Rhein, the CDU's Landtag President since 2013, was elected as Bouffier's successor on May 31, 2022, securing the necessary majority in the state parliament. The coalition partners agreed to continue their partnership under Rhein, who retained key policy continuities from the Bouffier era while emphasizing stability amid national economic pressures. This internal CDU leadership transition occurred without triggering early elections, allowing the government to serve its full term until October 2023.

Major policy challenges driving voter discontent

The primary policy challenge fueling voter discontent in the 2023 Hessian state election was the federal government's perceived lax approach to migration control, amid a record 334,000 asylum applications nationwide that strained reception facilities, housing, and welfare systems in Hesse as a transit and economic hub.[9] Irregular border crossings surged, with over 200,000 unauthorized discourse, as evidenced by the Alternative for Germany (AfD) securing 16.1% of the voteâ€”up from 12.8% in 2018â€”by emphasizing border closures and deportation of rejected claimants, reflecting empirical voter prioritization of security and resource allocation over humanitarian expansions.[11][12] Economic pressures, including persistent inflation and the energy crisis precipitated by the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, amplified dissatisfaction in Hesse's export-dependent economy centered around Frankfurt.[13] Germany's annual inflation averaged 5.9% in 2023, with household energy costs rising sharplyâ€”gas prices peaking at over â‚¬300 per megawatt-hour in 2022 before partial stabilizationâ€”due to the abrupt cutoff of Russian supplies and the prior nuclear phase-out, which critics argued exacerbated industrial competitiveness losses without adequate alternatives.[14] These factors contributed to a 0.3% GDP contraction in Hesse for 2023, heightening concerns over deindustrialization and living standards under federal green transition mandates perceived as ideologically driven rather than pragmatically balanced.[15] Associated security worries, including elevated crime rates in migrant-heavy areas, further eroded trust in state and federal handling, with non-German nationals comprising 41% of suspects in violent crimes nationwide despite representing 14% of the population.[5] In Hesse, localized incidents of gang violence and property crime linked to asylum accommodations underscored causal links between unchecked inflows and public safety breakdowns, driving support toward parties advocating stricter enforcement over expansive reception policies.[16] This confluence of migration-induced strains and economic hardships manifested in punitive voting against the federal coalition partners (SPD and Greens), who lost ground in Hesse, signaling a broader rejection of policies prioritizing international obligations over domestic Electoral framework

Election date and scheduling factors

The 2023 Hessian state election was held on Sunday, 8 October 2023, to elect the 21st Landtag.[18] This date marked the conclusion of the standard five-year term following the previous election on 28 October 2018, as stipulated by Article 75 of the Hessian Constitution, which mandates Landtag elections every five years. The Hessian Election Act (Landtagswahlgesetz) further requires that elections occur on a Sunday or public holiday, with the precise date set by the state government no later than six months prior to the end of the incumbent Landtag's term to ensure timely convocation of the new assembly. The selection of 8 October aligned with Bavaria's concurrent state election, creating a unified "super election Sunday" for two of Germany's most populous states and amplifying national media coverage and political stakes.[19] Hessian authorities formally announced the date in early 2023, with the official call to election published on 20 February 2023 in the State Gazette, initiating the campaign period.[20] No advancements or delays were enacted, reflecting adherence to the fixed constitutional cycle without extraordinary circumstances such as government collapse or legal mandates for early voting, unlike irregular "snap" elections in other German states.[21] Key scheduling considerations included logistical coordination for voter rolls, polling stations, and absentee voting across Hesse's urban and rural districts, as well as compliance with federal data protection and accessibility standards under the Federal Election Act's supplementary provisions for state polls.[22] The timing in early autumn avoided conflicts with major holidays or federal events, while the synchronization with Bavaria minimized sequential spillover effects on voter behavior between the contests.[19]

Electoral system and district allocation

The electoral system for the Landtag of Hesse employs a mixed-member proportional representation framework, whereby voters cast two ballots: a first vote for a candidate in one of the state's 55 single-member constituencies and a second vote for a party list at the state level.[23][21] The first vote determines the winner in each constituency via plurality, securing 55 direct mandates.[24] The second vote establishes the overall proportional distribution of the remaining seats, with parties required to surpass a 5% threshold of valid second votes to qualify for representation.[25] Seat allocation begins with assigning the 55 constituency winners their seats, regardless of their party's second-vote performance. The remaining seatsâ€”initially targeted at 55 to reach a base total of 110â€”are then distributed proportionally based on second-vote shares among qualifying parties, drawing from their state lists in descending order of preference.[21] If a party's direct mandates exceed its proportional entitlement, overhang mandates are created for that party, and compensatory leveling seats are added for other parties to restore proportionality, potentially expanding the parliament beyond 110 members.[25] This mechanism ensures broad proportionality while preserving local representation through constituencies.[24] In the 2023 election, the system resulted in 133 seats due to overhang and leveling adjustments, with the 55 constituencies electing direct deputies and the balance filled from lists to reflect second-vote outcomes.[1] Constituency boundaries, fixed prior to the election, adhere to principles of approximate population equality across Hesse's districts, though urban-rural disparities influence competition dynamics.[23]

Parties and candidates

The 2023 Hessian state election featured 21 parties and electoral associations that submitted state lists to contest the 133 seats in the Landtag.[26] Only five parties surpassed the 5% electoral threshold to gain representation: the Christian Democratic Union (CDU) with 52 seats, Alternative for Germany (AfD) with 28 seats, Social Democratic Party of Germany (SPD) with 23 seats, Alliance 90/The Greens (Die GrÃ¼nen) with 22 seats, and Free Democratic Party (FDP) with 8 seats.[27] These parties represented the primary ideological spectrum, from center-right conservatism to left-liberal environmentalism, with the CDU and Greens forming the incumbent coalition prior to the vote.[1] The CDU, a center-right party rooted in Christian democratic principles, prioritizes market-oriented economics, family policies, and internal security; it has held the state premiership since 1999 under leaders emphasizing fiscal discipline and regional infrastructure investment. Led by Boris Rhein, the incumbent Minister-President, the CDU campaigned on continuity and critiques of federal energy policies.[28] The AfD, established in 2013 as a eurosceptic and anti-immigration party, advocates for national sovereignty, reduced welfare for non-citizens, and opposition to climate regulations perceived as economically burdensome; its Hessian branch focused on migration controls and crime reduction amid rising voter turnout in rural and eastern districts. The SPD, Germany's oldest social democratic party, promotes workers' rights, progressive taxation, and social welfare expansion; under federal Interior Minister Nancy Faeser as lead candidate, it sought to defend labor market reforms while addressing affordability concerns. The Christian Democratic Union (CDU), ideologically rooted in Christian democracy with center-right emphases on family values, fiscal responsibility, and strong state institutions, positioned itself as a bulwark against federal coalition policies, advocating for Hesse as an "Ampel-free zone" free from SPD-Green-FDP influences. Leading candidates and their backgrounds

Boris Rhein served as the lead candidate for the Christian Democratic Union (CDU), having assumed the role of Minister-President of Hesse in May 2022 following Volker Bouffier's resignation. Born on 2 January 1972 in Frankfurt am Main, Rhein trained as a lawyer after studying at the universities of Frankfurt and Heidelberg. He entered politics with the CDU's youth wing in the 1990s, becoming a member of the Hessian Landtag in 2003 and advancing through roles including parliamentary state secretary for the interior and justice before his ministerial appointments as Minister of the Interior and Sport from 2010 to 2014 and Minister of Economics from 2014 to 2022.[35][36] Tarek Al-Wazir was the top candidate for the Greens (Alliance 90/The Greens), continuing as deputy Minister-President and Minister of Economics, Energy, Transport, and Regional Development in the incumbent coalition government. Born on 4 February 1971 in Heidelberg to a German schoolteacher mother and a Yemeni diplomat father, Al-Wazir grew up bilingual and joined the Greens in the early 1990s, entering the Hessian Landtag in 2003 after roles in local politics and environmental activism. His ministerial tenure since 2014 emphasized economic policy alongside green priorities like renewable energy expansion.[37][38] Nancy Faeser led the Social Democratic Party (SPD) campaign while holding the federal position of Minister of the Interior and Homeland since December 2021. Born on 13 July 1970 in Bad Soden am Taunus, Hesse, Faeser qualified as a lawyer after secondary school and joined the SPD in 1988, serving in the Hessian Landtag from 2003 to 2021 where she chaired the SPD parliamentary group from 2010 and focused on interior affairs and anti-extremism efforts. Her state-level candidacy drew on her long-standing ties to the region despite her national role.[39][40] Robert Lambrou co-led the Alternative for Germany (AfD) as one of its Hessian state chairmen and top candidate, contributing to the party's strong performance. Of Greek immigrant heritage, Lambrou has represented AfD in the Landtag since 2013, advocating positions on migration restriction and economic deregulation aligned with the party's platform.[41][42]

Role of federal politics in state-level dynamics

The 2023 Hessian state election was widely interpreted as a verdict on the federal traffic light coalition government led by Chancellor Olaf Scholz, comprising the SPD, Greens, and FDP, with exit polls indicating widespread voter dissatisfaction driving support toward opposition parties.[15] Approximately 50% of Hessian voters reported considering national politics in their decisions, amid polls showing less than half of Germans satisfied with the federal coalition's performance.[5] This discontent manifested in significant losses for the coalition parties at the state level, despite Hesse's relatively strong economic growth of 0.4% contrasting with national stagnation.[5] Migration policy emerged as a pivotal national issue spilling into the state campaign, with federal Interior Minister Nancy Faeserâ€”a Hessian SPD candidateâ€”facing criticism for the government's inability to reduce irregular arrivals and expedite deportations.[5] CDU leader Boris Rhein capitalized on this by advocating stricter border controls and designating additional safe countries of origin, such as Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, and India, to address perceived federal laxity.[5] Economic pressures, including inflation and high energy costs under federal policies, further amplified frustration, contributing to a protest dynamic that boosted the CDU to 35.5% of the vote per exit polls, up from prior levels.[15][52] The AfD benefited substantially from federal migration handling and broader governance critiques, surging to around 16%â€”a gain of over 3 percentage points from 2018â€”positioning it as the second-largest force and underscoring anti-establishment sentiment.[15][52] In contrast, the SPD recorded its worst-ever result at 16%, a personal blow to Faeser, while the Greens and FDP also declined sharply, reflecting the federal coalition's eroded trust among Hessian voters who prioritized national competence over state-specific matters.[15][5] This pattern highlighted how federal policy failures, rather than purely local dynamics, shaped the election's outcome, with the CDU leveraging opposition status to consolidate conservative support.[52]

These patterns suggest gender-specific sensitivities to issues like asylum policy and security, with female voters showing greater AfD affinity possibly linked to localized safety perceptions.[61]

Shifts from 2018 election and voter migration patterns

The Christian Democratic Union (CDU) increased its share of the second votes from 27.0% in 2018 to 34.6% in 2023, marking a gain of 7.6 percentage points and restoring much of its pre-2018 strength.[62][3] The Alternative for Germany (AfD) advanced from 12.8% to 18.4%, a rise of 5.6 percentage points, securing second place ahead of the Social Democratic Party (SPD) and Greens.[3][63] The SPD declined from 19.8% to 15.1% (-4.7 pp), while the Greens fell from 19.8% to 14.8% (-5.0 pp); the Free Democratic Party (FDP) slipped from 7.5% to 5.0% (-2.5 pp), barely clearing the 5% threshold; and The Left dropped from 6.3% to 3.1% (-3.2 pp), failing to enter the Landtag.[3]

Government formation process

Post-election negotiations and challenges

Following the October 8, 2023, election, Hessian Minister-President Boris Rhein announced on November 10 that the CDU would pursue coalition negotiations with the SPD rather than renewing the prior CDU-Greens partnership, citing the need for policy shifts amid the AfD's electoral gains and public concerns over migration and security.[67][68] This decision reflected the CDU's strategic aim to form a majority without relying on the weakened Greens, who had lost seats, while adhering to the established cordon sanitaire against the AfD despite its 16% vote share.[69] Formal talks between the CDU and SPD commenced on November 15, approximately five weeks post-election, with both parties expressing optimism and no anticipation of collapse.[70] Negotiations centered on internal security, economic policy, and education, but the primary challenge emerged in migration control, where the CDU pushed for stricter measures to address voter dissatisfaction evidenced by AfD's surge, while the SPD sought balances compatible with federal obligations under the Scholz government.[69][71] This tension stemmed from empirical pressures, including irregular arrivals straining state resources, which federal policies had failed to curb effectively, prompting Hesse to prioritize repatriations and border enforcement independently.[69] Despite these hurdles, the parties reached a coalition agreement on December 18, 2023, titled the "Hessenvertrag der demokratisch-christlich-sozialen Koalition 2024â€“2029," which emphasized a "hard line" on unauthorized migration through enhanced deportations and asylum processing while committing to social investments.[69][72] Ongoing challenges included implementing these amid federal-state tensions and maintaining intra-coalition unity on fiscal restraint, as Hesse faced budgetary pressures from energy costs and infrastructure needs without increasing debt. The swift formation, completed within two months, contrasted with longer processes in other states, underscoring the ideological proximity between CDU and SPD on core state competencies despite national divides.

Formation of the new coalition and cabinet

Following the October 8, 2023, election, the Christian Democratic Union (CDU), led by incumbent Minister-President Boris Rhein, secured 52 seats in the 137-seat Landtag, falling short of a majority but enabling potential coalitions.[73] Although renewing the prior CDU-Greens alliance (with the Greens holding 19 seats, yielding 71 seats total) was arithmetically feasible, Rhein opted for negotiations with the Social Democratic Party (SPD), which obtained 23 seats, to achieve a more stable grand coalition of 75 seats.[43] This decision, endorsed by CDU federal leader Friedrich Merz as prudent, reflected preferences for political reliability amid policy divergences with the Greens, particularly on migration and economic issues highlighted in the campaign.[74] Talks commenced in early November 2023, focusing on shared priorities such as internal security, infrastructure, and fiscal conservatism, while addressing the electoral backlash against federal coalition policies.[74] By December 2023, the parties finalized a coalition agreement, replacing the previous CDU-Greens partnership that had governed since 2019.[75] The new cabinet, designated the second Rhein cabinet, was sworn in on January 18, 2024, following Rhein's re-election as Minister-President by the Landtag with support from the coalition partners.[76] Rhein retained leadership, with CDU members holding key portfolios including interior, finance, and justice, while SPD allocated roles in social affairs, environment, and energyâ€”exemplified by appointee Kaweh Mansoori as energy minister.[76] This configuration emphasized continuity in conservative governance while incorporating SPD input on social policy.[75]

Analysis of outcomes

Causal factors behind party performances

The Christian Democratic Union (CDU) achieved a vote share of 34.6%, an increase of 7.6 percentage points from 2018, primarily due to its effective positioning against the federal government's handling of migration and economic stagnation, with over 50% of voters viewing the election as a de facto referendum on Chancellor Olaf Scholz's coalition.[43] CDU leader Boris Rhein's high personal approval ratings and the party's record of stable governance in Hesse, including distancing itself from federal CDU leadership controversies, further bolstered support, enabling it to attract voters disillusioned with the national coalition's internal divisions.[43] The campaign's emphasis on stricter migration controls resonated amid a surge in asylum arrivals earlier in 2023, which heightened public concerns over border security and integration strains.[52] The Alternative for Germany (AfD) secured approximately 16% of the vote, marking its strongest performance in western Germany and drawing support from non-voters (46,000 in Hesse) as well as former SPD and CDU backers, driven by perceptions of its competence on asylum and refugee policyâ€”cited by 17% of voters as the party's strength.[77][43] With migration ranked as Germany's top political issue by 44% of the electorate, AfD's advocacy for reduced immigration and enhanced security measures appealed to conviction voters frustrated by the establishment's perceived inaction, including 29,000 former SPD supporters shifting amid broader discontent with federal policy implementation.[77][43] Economic pressures, such as rising costs of living, played a secondary but reinforcing role, amplifying protest elements against the ruling coalition's economic management.[77] The Social Democratic Party (SPD) fell to 15.1%, while the Greens declined significantly, with losses attributed to their entanglement in the unpopular federal traffic-light coalition (SPD-Greens-FDP), whose disputes over migration enforcement and climate measures alienated voters.[43][52] For the Greens, federal initiatives like the controversial heating lawâ€”aimed at accelerating the fossil fuel phase-out but criticized for increasing energy costsâ€”contributed to a net loss of 57,000 voters to the CDU in Hesse, exacerbating perceptions of policy overreach amid economic slowdown.[43] The SPD's campaign suffered from incoherence, including federal Interior Minister Nancy Faeser's reluctance to prioritize the state race, further eroding trust in the party's ability to address core voter priorities like security and fiscal stability.[43]

Rise of AfD: Empirical drivers and mainstream critiques

In the 2023 Hessian state election held on October 8, AfD secured 15.1% of the vote, an increase of 2.3 percentage points from its 12.8% share in 2018, positioning it as the second-largest opposition party behind the SPD.[43][4] This gain reflected broader voter shifts, with exit polls indicating significant migration from CDU and SPD supporters dissatisfied with the federal coalition's handling of national issues spilling into state-level voting.[4] Empirical drivers of AfD's advance centered on migration policy failures and economic pressures. Pre-election surveys identified immigration as the dominant voter concern in Hesse, with over 40% of respondents prioritizing stricter border controls amid Germany's record 351,000 asylum applications in 2023, many straining local resources in the state.[78] AfD capitalized on documented integration challenges, including disproportionate migrant involvement in violent crimesâ€”such as a 2023 spike in knife attacks linked to asylum seekers in urban areas like Frankfurtâ€”where federal data showed non-citizens committing offenses at rates three to five times higher than natives per capita.[5] Economic factors compounded this, as Hesse voters grappled with inflation exceeding 8% in 2022-2023 and energy prices doubled post-Ukraine invasion, eroding trust in the CDU-Green state government's alignment with Berlin's green energy transition, which polls linked to deindustrialization fears in manufacturing-heavy regions.[52] Voter demographics further underscored causal patterns: AfD support surged in rural and eastern Hesse districts with lower incomes and higher perceived migration impacts, drawing from former CDU voters (up to 20% crossover per post-election analysis) seeking alternatives to establishment inaction.[4] Mainstream critiques framed AfD's rise as a product of extremist agitation rather than substantive grievances, with outlets like Politico and The Guardian attributing gains to the party's "populist" exploitation of fears, while emphasizing its classification by domestic intelligence as a "suspected right-wing extremist" entity due to isolated radical statements from members.[52][77] Such analyses, often from center-left perspectives, downplayed empirical migration costsâ€”e.g., Hesse's â‚¬2.5 billion annual welfare expenditure on non-citizensâ€”and instead highlighted AfD's rhetoric as a democratic threat, advocating "firewalls" against cooperation.[12] This narrative aligns with institutional biases observed in German media and academia, where migration critiques are frequently recast as xenophobia, potentially understating policy-driven causal factors like unchecked inflows exacerbating housing shortages (Hesse vacancy rate below 1%) and public service overload.[43] In contrast, AfD's platform resonated by prioritizing verifiable border enforcement and economic sovereignty, appealing to voters prioritizing data over ideological dismissal.

Broader implications for German federalism and policy

The 2023 Hessian state election underscored tensions within Germany's cooperative federalism, where state-level outcomes influence federal policy through the Bundesrat and public signaling. Hesse's delegation of five votes in the Bundesrat, previously aligned under a CDU-Greens coalition against key federal initiatives, shifted to a CDU-SPD grand coalition following the vote, preserving an oppositional posture toward the federal Ampel government's (SPD-Greens-FDP) legislative agenda. This continuity limited the federal coalition's ability to secure consent for contentious laws requiring Bundesrat approval, such as those on fiscal equalization and administrative reforms, amplifying state-federal frictions on resource allocation.[79][80] The results, mirroring Bavaria's concurrent election, signaled widespread dissatisfaction with federal policies on migration and energy transition, as CDU gains and AfD's surge to second place (with approximately 16.9% of the vote) stemmed from voter prioritization of border controls and economic burdens over green mandates. Hesse, as a financial hub with Frankfurt, exemplified how economically robust states could diverge from federal directives, with incoming Minister-President Boris Rhein advocating stricter asylum processing and resistance to the federal Building Energy Act, thereby constraining uniform national implementation.[17][43] These dynamics reinforced federalism's role in policy experimentation, where Hesse's conservative tilt pressured the Ampel coalition to address empirical pressures like overburdened state welfare systems from sustained high net migration (over 1 million asylum seekers federally in 2022-2023), potentially foreshadowing adjustments ahead of the 2025 federal election. The AfD's breakthrough, driven by localized grievances rather than ideology alone, highlighted causal gaps in federal-state coordination on concurrent competences like integration, challenging mainstream parties to confront voter realities without relying on exclusionary tactics.[81][5]
