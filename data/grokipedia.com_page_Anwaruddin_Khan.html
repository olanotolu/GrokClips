Anwaruddin Khan


Anwaruddin Muhammad Khan (died 3 August 1749) was a Mughal military officer and administrator who became the first Nawab of the Carnatic, ruling from Arcot between 1744 and 1749.[1] Appointed to the position by Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I after demonstrating loyalty in service under the Mughals and Deccan Sultanates, he governed the region amid rising European colonial rivalries.[2] Khan's tenure coincided with the outbreak of the First Carnatic War (1746–1748), during which he allied with the British East India Company, dispatching forces to repel a French attack on Madras in the Battle of Adyar and securing the return of captured territories.[2] His support for British interests stemmed from obligations to protect trade settlements, though it drew French opposition backed by claimants to the Carnatic throne like Chanda Sahib.[3] In 1749, following the Nizam's death and a shift in alliances, Khan's army was decisively defeated at the Battle of Ambur by a Franco-Indian coalition led by Muzaffar Jang and Chanda Sahib, resulting in his death on the battlefield. This event precipitated a succession crisis between his son Muhammad Ali, whom the British championed, and Chanda Sahib, escalating the broader conflict into the Second Carnatic War and solidifying Anglo-French struggles for dominance in southern India.[2]

Early Life and Origins

Birth and Family Background

Anwaruddin Khan was born in 1674 in Gopamau, a locality in the Hardoi District of present-day Uttar Pradesh, India.[1] He was the son of Haji Muhammad Anwar, a figure from whom limited historical details survive beyond this paternal link.[1] Anwaruddin's family originated from the Qannauji Shaikh community, a group of Muslim landholders and nobles tracing roots to the Kannauj region in northern India, known for service in Mughal administration during the late 17th and early 18th centuries.[4] This background positioned him within the broader network of Mughal-affiliated families that supplied military and administrative talent, though specific ancestral claims, such as generational ties to early Islamic figures, appear in later dynastic records without independent contemporary corroboration.[5]

Entry into Mughal Service

Born in Awadh around 1672 to a family of Qannauji Sheikhs, Anwaruddin Khan traveled to Delhi in his youth and enlisted in the Mughal imperial army during the reign of Emperor Aurangzeb (1658–1707).[6] His recruitment occurred amid the ongoing Deccan campaigns, where the Mughals sought capable officers from northern India to bolster forces against regional powers like the Marathas and Deccan Sultanates.[2] Anwaruddin demonstrated early prowess in military duties, earning recognition for courage and loyalty that facilitated his rapid ascent through the ranks.[2] By leveraging these qualities, he secured appointments that positioned him as a trusted subordinate, setting the foundation for his later prominence under key Mughal viceroys in the Deccan.[6]

Military and Administrative Career

Service Under Aurangzeb and Early Assignments

Anwaruddin Khan entered Mughal imperial service following the death of his father, Muhammad Anwar (also known as Shaikh Aqdas), who had served Emperor Aurangzeb and accompanied him to the Deccan in 1683 before succumbing shortly thereafter in Aurangabad.[5] Appointed initially as Peshkar to the Darugha of the Diwan Khana, Anwaruddin demonstrated administrative competence in the imperial bureaucracy during the latter phases of Aurangzeb's protracted Deccan campaigns, which spanned from 1681 until the emperor's death in 1707.[5] Aurangzeb recognized Anwaruddin's loyalty and capabilities by granting him the title of Bahadur and a mansab rank of 2,000 zat with 2,000 sowar (cavalry), entitling him to command a contingent of 2,000 horsemen.[5] This assignment placed him in active military service amid the emperor's efforts to subdue resistant Maratha forces and consolidate control over the southern provinces, where Mughal forces faced persistent guerrilla warfare and logistical strains. Anwaruddin's role involved participation in routine garrison duties and skirmishes, contributing to the maintenance of imperial authority in a region marked by fiscal exhaustion and strategic overextension.[5] Following Aurangzeb's demise, Anwaruddin's early post-imperial assignments continued to build on his Deccan experience, though specific details from this transitional period remain limited in contemporary records. His established reputation for fidelity to the Mughal throne facilitated subsequent elevations under later emperors, including titles such as Shahamat Jang conferred by Shah Alam I.[5] These initial roles underscored a career trajectory rooted in proven martial and administrative reliability amid the empire's decentralizing tendencies.[2]

Roles in the Deccan and Under Nizam-ul-Mulk

Anwaruddin Khan joined the service of Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I in the Deccan in 1724, shortly after the Nizam's victory over Mubariz Khan and consolidation of power in Hyderabad.[5] As a capable Mughal officer with prior experience, he quickly became a trusted deputy, assisting in the restoration of order amid regional instability following the Nizam's campaigns against local chieftains and zamindars.[5] His role emphasized military enforcement and administrative oversight, reflecting the Nizam's strategy to integrate loyal Mughals into Deccan governance to counter Maratha incursions and internal rebellions.[7] In 1724–1725, Anwaruddin was tasked with pacifying the coastal sarkars of Chicacole, Rajahmundry, and Masulipatam, key revenue-yielding territories prone to zamindar uprisings.[5] He decisively eliminated Subna, a disruptive local leader, and subdued Ramraz, another turbulent zamindar, thereby securing these areas for Nizam's administration and ensuring tribute flows to Hyderabad.[5] By 1725, he had been appointed governor of Eluru and Rajahmundry, roles that involved collecting revenues, maintaining garrisons, and mediating with Hindu poligars, while also serving as faujdar in districts like Korah.[5] These positions underscored his rising influence as Yamin-us-Sultanat, or right-hand man, to the Nizam, a title denoting high administrative and military counsel.[1] From 1732 to 1739, Anwaruddin oversaw Rajahmundry through his deputy Rustam Khan but later intervened to suppress Nuru'd-din, Rustam Khan's son, who had usurped local authority, demonstrating his commitment to centralized control under the Nizam.[5] By the early 1740s, he had advanced to governor of the Hyderabad subah, the Deccan's premier province, where he enforced discipline among Nasir Jang's retainers during the Nizam's absence in the Carnatic.[7] In this capacity, he repelled a Maratha raid led by Bapu Nayak (also known as Babuji Naik) at Andol (near Hindoli) around 1743, defeating the invaders with a modest force and refusing tribute demands, which bolstered Hyderabad's defenses and affirmed his loyalty amid growing external pressures.[2] These actions solidified his reputation as a reliable enforcer, earning imperial titles like Shamsat Jang from the Mughal court while prioritizing Nizam's interests over personal ambition.[5]

Appointment as Nawab of Arcot

Context of Nizam's Campaigns

In the wake of Nawab Dost Ali Khan's death on October 15, 1740, during a battle against Maratha forces led by Raghoji Bhonsle near Damalcherry, the Carnatic region descended into instability marked by succession disputes and further incursions.[8] Dost Ali's son-in-law, Safdar Ali Khan, briefly assumed the nawabship but was assassinated in late 1740 or early 1741, reportedly by his cousin or amid palace intrigue, leaving a minor heir and opening claims to figures like Muhammad Saiyid, a relative of the previous dynasty.[2] This power vacuum invited renewed Maratha raids, including occupations of Trichinopoly (Tiruchirappalli) and Madurai, exacerbating local feuds with Nayak rulers and weakening Mughal subedari authority.[1] Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I, viceroy of the Deccan and de facto independent ruler of Hyderabad, viewed the Carnatic's turmoil as a threat to his regional dominance and a opportunity to reassert nominal Mughal suzerainty amid the empire's fragmentation post-Aurangzeb. In January 1743, he mobilized an army from Hyderabad, marching southward to intervene directly, with the dual aims of quelling Maratha influence and installing loyal administrators.[5] Over the ensuing campaign, lasting more than a year, Nizam forces besieged and recovered Trichinopoly from Maratha control by mid-1743, compelled the withdrawal of Bhonsle raiders through tribute demands totaling around 20 lakh rupees, and mediated or suppressed rival claimants, including deposing or sidelining Muhammad Saadatullah Khan II in Arcot.[5] [8] These expeditions reflected Nizam-ul-Mulk's broader strategy of centralizing Deccan polities under Hyderabad's oversight, leveraging his military superiority—estimated at 50,000 cavalry and artillery—to extract oaths of fealty from Carnatic zamindars and European trading enclaves while avoiding prolonged entanglement. Following Muhammad Saiyid's death in early 1744, Nizam appointed Anwaruddin Khan, a veteran Mughal faujdar in his service since Aurangzeb's era, as deputy governor and Nawab of Arcot on March 28, 1744, bypassing the Navayat lineage to install a trusted Arcot-born subordinate capable of maintaining order.[1] [5] This move stabilized the subah temporarily but sowed seeds for future Franco-British meddling, as Anwaruddin's authority derived from Hyderabad rather than independent legitimacy.[2]

Establishment of Authority in the Carnatic

In early 1743, following the Nizam of Hyderabad's military intervention to restore order in the Carnatic after the Maratha incursions and the death of Nawab Dost Ali Khan in 1740, Anwaruddin Khan was appointed as regent for the infant Nawab Muhammad Sa'id Khan of the Nawabat dynasty.[5] [2] The Nizam's campaign, which culminated in the recovery of Trichinopoly on 29 August 1743, provided the backdrop for this appointment, enabling Anwaruddin to leverage imperial backing to address the region's fragmented authority and ongoing disorders.[2] Upon assuming regency on the 13th of Safar 1743 (approximately March), Anwaruddin swiftly subjugated the rebel Mir Asadullah and compelled Raja Pratap Singh of Tanjore to remit long-overdue tribute arrears, thereby reasserting fiscal control over key polities that had evaded central oversight amid the post-1740 vacuum.[5] The assassination of Muhammad Sa'id Khan in 1744 by Pathan elements prompted Anwaruddin to expel the Afghan perpetrators and punish the Tahirans involved, solidifying his position and leading to his formal elevation as Nawab of the Carnatic on 28 March 1744 by the Nizam, who designated him viceroy and founder of the Walajahi line.[5] [1] To further entrench authority, Anwaruddin undertook military campaigns against persistent threats, notably defeating Bapu Nayak's Maratha forces at Baswapattan in 1746 with an army of 69,000, including contingents from allied principalities such as Cuddapah and Kurnool.[5] Administratively, he appointed Raja Sampat Rai as diwan and Rai Manulal as mir munshi to manage revenues and correspondence, while parceling the Carnatic into manageable divisions among his sons—Hazrat Ala for Kanchipuram and Trichinopoly, Mahfuz Khan for Trichinopoly, Abdul Wahab Khan for Nellore, and Najibullah Khan for South Arcot—by 1746, fostering loyalty and decentralized enforcement.[5] These measures, combined with repelling early French encroachments at the Adyar River in 1746 alongside his son Mahfuz Khan, restored a degree of stability until the outbreak of succession disputes.[5]

Reign and Governance

Administrative Policies and Reforms

Upon his appointment as Nawab of the Carnatic in 1744 by Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I, Anwaruddin Khan established a civil administration modeled on Mughal principles, appointing Diwan Raja Sampat Rai and Mir Munshi Rai Manulal to oversee key fiscal and secretarial functions.[5] He divided the Carnatic into two subahs (provinces)—Arcot, encompassing Nellore, Chenglepet, and Arcot districts, and Trichy, covering Tirunelveli, Madurai, and Trichy districts—each governed by subahdars, often royal relatives, tasked with maintaining law and order, public welfare, and infrastructure like sarais and wells for approximately three-year terms.[9] In revenue administration, Anwaruddin systematized collection by conducting land surveys to classify holdings as good, moderate, or fertile, drawing from primary sources such as land revenue, rural taxes, customs duties, salt monopolies, and mint operations; provincial diwans and amalguzars supervised assessments, with exemptions granted to peasants during famines or calamities.[9] To enforce compliance, he subjugated refractory elements like Mir Asadullah, recovered arrears of tribute from Raja Pratap Singh of Tanjore, and expelled disruptive Afghan groups while releasing imprisoned Navayats, thereby stabilizing fiscal inflows.[5] In regions like the Northern Circars (during prior assignments from 1732–1739), he replaced inefficient zamindars with ameens to enhance agricultural productivity and revenue efficiency among retainers.[5] Judicial authority rested with the chief qazi-ul-quzzat, who applied Shariath law across civil and criminal matters, with subordinate qazis stationed at subah, sarkar, pargana, and village levels to adjudicate disputes, property transfers, and local governance.[9] Anwaruddin further decentralized management by assigning sub-provinces to his sons—Hazrat Ala to Kanchi, Mahfuz Khan to Trichinopoly, Abdul Wahab Khan to Nellore, and Najibullah Khan to South Arcot—aiming to consolidate familial control and administrative oversight amid regional instability.[5] These measures prioritized order and revenue extraction over sweeping innovations, reflecting the transitional Mughal framework in a fragmented Deccan polity.[9]

Relations with Local Powers and European Traders

Upon his appointment as Nawab of the Carnatic on 28 March 1744 by Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I, Anwaruddin Khan asserted authority over local zamindars and chieftains by subduing figures such as Subna and Ramraz in districts including Chicacole, Rajahmundry, and Masulipatam, thereby securing revenue streams previously contested.[5] He further compelled the recovery of overdue tributes from Raja Pratap Singh of Tanjore and subdued Mir Asadullah, demonstrating a policy of direct military intervention to enforce fiscal obligations from semi-autonomous Hindu rulers.[5] In response to Maratha incursions under Bapu Nayak seeking chauth collections, Anwaruddin mobilized 12,000 cavalry, augmented by 69,000 troops from allied local rulers, to defeat the invaders at Andol and Baswapattan in the mid-1740s, repelling external threats and reinforcing his suzerainty over border regions.[5] These actions reflected a governance approach prioritizing the subjugation of recalcitrant local powers to stabilize the Carnatic's fragmented polity, where poligar chieftains and zamindars often resisted central exactions; Anwaruddin's campaigns yielded consolidated control, enabling him to divide administrative responsibilities among his sons—assigning Kanchipuram to Muhammad Ali, Trichinopoly to Mahfuz Khan, Nellore to Abdul Wahab, and South Arcot to Najibullah—prior to his death in 1749.[5] Regarding European traders, Anwaruddin permitted settlements and trade along the Carnatic coast by companies such as the British and French East India Companies, recognizing their economic utility amid Mughal decline, though he monitored their activities to prevent territorial encroachments.[5] Initial diplomacy included negotiations with French authorities following their 1746 capture of Madras, securing temporary peace through concessions and gifts, while maintaining cordial ties with the British at Fort St. David.[5] By late 1746, he tilted toward the British, dispatching forces under Mahfuz Khan to support their defense and requesting artillery aid against French-aligned rivals, a pragmatic alignment driven by the need to counterbalance French ambitions in the region rather than ideological affinity.[5][1] This selective favoritism strengthened his position against internal challengers like Chanda Sahib but exposed vulnerabilities when European rivalries escalated into open conflict.[1]

Involvement in the Carnatic Wars

First Carnatic War Engagements

The First Carnatic War (1746–1748) arose from the French East India Company's capture of the British-held Fort St. George (Madras) on 7 September 1746, prompting Nawab Anwaruddin Khan to intervene on behalf of regional stability and his alignment with British trading interests. As the Mughal-appointed governor of the Carnatic, Anwaruddin demanded the restitution of Madras from French Governor-General Joseph François Dupleix, viewing the seizure as a violation of local sovereignty, but Dupleix refused, citing European hostilities.[10][11] In response, Anwaruddin dispatched an initial force of approximately 10,000 cavalry under his second son, Mahfuz Khan, to besiege the French position and compel evacuation.[12][13] The primary engagement occurred at the Battle of Adyar on 24 October 1746, near the banks of the Adyar River outside Madras, where Mahfuz Khan's cavalry attempted to cross the river to assault the outnumbered French defenders—roughly 200–300 European troops and sepoys commanded by Captain Louis Paradis. Despite the vast numerical superiority of the Carnatic forces, French infantry discipline, coordinated volley fire, and effective use of field fortifications repelled multiple disorganized charges, exploiting the Indians' difficulties in maneuvering across the shallow but swift-flowing waters.[13][12] Mahfuz Khan was killed during the rout, with heavy Carnatic casualties estimated in the thousands, while French losses were minimal, highlighting the tactical edge of European linear tactics over traditional cavalry tactics in open terrain.[13][11] Undeterred, Anwaruddin mobilized a larger secondary force, including elements of his personal guard and levies from Arcot, to renew pressure on Madras, but Dupleix pursued diplomatic overtures, leading to a temporary truce by late 1746 that withdrew active Carnatic troops from the siege.[10] This de-escalation allowed French resources to shift toward operations against British holdings at Fort St. David, though Anwaruddin's stance remained pro-British, providing indirect logistical support amid ongoing skirmishes.[11] The war concluded with the Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle on 18 October 1748, restoring Madras to British control without further direct Carnatic involvement, though the engagements underscored Anwaruddin's commitment to curbing European overreach in Mughal territories.[10]

Second Carnatic War and Alliances

The Second Carnatic War erupted in 1749 amid succession crises following the death of Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I on 1 June 1748, pitting rival claimants for the Hyderabad throne and the Carnatic subah against each other. Anwaruddin Khan, as the Nizam-appointed Nawab of Arcot since 1744, pledged loyalty to Nasir Jung, the Nizam's designated successor and second son, rejecting the pretensions of Muzaffar Jung, the Nizam's grandson. This alignment drew Anwaruddin into conflict with Muzaffar Jung's coalition, which included French backing from Governor-General Joseph François Dupleix, who sought to expand French influence by undermining British positions in the region.[14][15] To counter the French-supported challengers, Anwaruddin forged a strategic alliance with the British East India Company, whose Madras Presidency forces had previously benefited from his mediation during the First Carnatic War. The British, under Governor Nicholas Morse, provided military aid and recognized Anwaruddin's authority, viewing him as a bulwark against French expansionism and the pretender Chanda Sahib (Husayn Dost Khan), a former deputy governor imprisoned by the Marathas until his release with French subsidies in 1748. Chanda Sahib, claiming the Carnatic throne through familial ties to the prior Nawab Dost Ali Khan, joined Muzaffar Jung and Dupleix in a tripartite pact that aimed to oust Anwaruddin and install Chanda Sahib as Nawab, thereby securing French trading privileges and territorial concessions.[1][16] Anwaruddin's British alliance was pragmatic, rooted in mutual interests: he supplied troops and intelligence to British garrisons, while the Company offered artillery and infantry support against the numerically superior Franco-Indian forces. This partnership reflected broader Anglo-French rivalries extending from the War of the Austrian Succession, with local Indian polities leveraged as proxies. However, Anwaruddin's divided loyalties—balancing Deccan overlords, local zamindars, and European powers—strained his resources, as Nasir Jung's delayed campaigns left him exposed to Muzaffar Jung's incursions from the north.[14][15]

Battle of Ambur and Defeat

The Battle of Ambur took place on August 3, 1749, near Ambur in Tamil Nadu, as part of the Second Carnatic War, where Nawab Anwaruddin Khan confronted the invading allied forces of Muzaffar Jang, Chanda Sahib, and French troops seeking to overthrow his rule in the Carnatic.[17] Anwaruddin's army, numbering 20,000 troops including 12,000 cavalry and 8,000 infantry, faced an opposing force of approximately 36,000, bolstered by French military expertise under Captain d'Auteuil.[17] Despite Anwaruddin's alignment with the British East India Company, their support was limited, leaving his forces to engage the numerically superior and better-equipped alliance.[14] The battle unfolded with the allied forces launching an assault on Anwaruddin's positioned army, where French artillery and disciplined infantry proved decisive in breaking the Nawab's lines despite his troops' initial resistance.[17] Anwaruddin, aged approximately 77, personally led his cavalry charge but was struck down during the fighting, reportedly wounded by a sabre blow to the forehead from an enemy elephant mahout and an arrow to the chest.[18] His death marked a critical turning point, leading to the collapse of his army and a reported 300 Indian casualties on the allied side, contrasted with minimal French losses of 12 killed and 63 wounded.[17] The defeat at Ambur enabled Chanda Sahib to seize Arcot and proclaim himself Nawab of the Carnatic, installing Muzaffar Jang's authority in the region while Anwaruddin's son, Muhammad Ali Khan, escaped to Trichy to rally opposition with British aid, prolonging the succession conflict.[15] This outcome underscored the pivotal role of European military intervention in shifting local power dynamics, as French support overcame Anwaruddin's established governance despite his forces' size.[14]

Death, Succession, and Immediate Aftermath

Final Moments and Burial

Anwaruddin Khan, aged 77, led his forces of approximately 20,000 against the invading army of Muzaffar Jang, Chanda Sahib, and French allies numbering around 36,000 with 400 European troops at Ambur on 3 August 1749.[19] The battle commenced with treachery from some risaldars in his command, compounded by Muhammad Husain Khan Tahir directing his artillery against Anwaruddin's own lines, leading to the routing of the vanguard under Mahfuz Khan.[5] Anwaruddin's troops were shattered amid the chaos, and he was slain in the ensuing conflict on the 16th of Shaban 1162 AH, corresponding to 3 August 1749.[5] Following his death, Anwaruddin Khan was initially interred at the Juma Masjid in Arcot. His remains were later exhumed and reburied beside the shrine of Murshid Shah Wali-ulla in Hyderabad.[5]

Succession Dispute Between Sons

Following Anwaruddin Khan's death on 3 August 1749 at the Battle of Ambur, where he was slain during the defeat by allied forces of Chanda Sahib, Muzaffar Jang, and the French, succession to the Nawabship of the Carnatic fell into contention among his sons, compounded by external rival claims. The eldest son, Mahfuz Khan, who had previously served as deputy Nawab and governed districts including Trichinopoly and Madura, was captured alongside the youngest son, Muhammad Najibullah Khan, in the aftermath of the battle; Mahfuz's prior military leadership, such as commanding 10,000 troops against French forces at the Battle of Adyar in October 1746, positioned him as a potential successor, but his captivity sidelined his claim.[5][20] In contrast, the second son, Muhammad Ali Khan (also styled Hazrat Ala Muhammad Ali), escaped the Ambur rout and retreated to Trichinopoly (Tiruchirappalli), where he asserted his right to the throne, bolstering his position through an alliance with the British East India Company, who provided military aid against Chanda Sahib's occupation of Arcot.[5] This fraternal rivalry echoed earlier internal tensions, including a serious dispute at Trichinopoly fort between Mahfuz Khan and a rival sibling (identified in some accounts as Anwar Ali), which Anwaruddin had personally resolved by intervening to install Muhammad Ali in governance roles like Kanchi taluq and later Trichinopoly itself.[5] The third son, Abdul Wahab Khan, held Nellore district but played no prominent role in the contest.[5] Muhammad Ali's British-backed campaign ultimately prevailed, leading to his recognition as Nawab by 1750, though the broader conflict with Chanda Sahib persisted until the latter's execution in 1752; Mahfuz Khan was ransomed, resettled in Madras by 1759, and ceased contending for the title, while Najibullah Khan's capture diminished his influence.[5][21] This intra-family strife, amid European interventions, underscored the fragility of Mughal-appointed authority in the Carnatic, with Muhammad Ali's success hinging less on primogeniture than on strategic alliances rather than Mahfuz's administrative precedence.[5]

Legacy and Historical Assessment

Achievements in Loyalty and Stability

Anwaruddin Khan's appointment as Nawab of the Carnatic on March 28, 1744, by Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I, reflected his longstanding loyalty to the Nizam's administration, forged through decades of service in the Mughal military apparatus since Aurangzeb's era.[2] This fidelity positioned him as a trusted agent to govern a region plagued by disorder following the assassination of the prior Nawab, Dost Ali Khan, and rival claims by figures like Chanda Sahib.[22] By upholding allegiance to the Nizam's designated successor, Nasir Jung, against the pretender Muzaffar Jung, Anwaruddin preserved nominal Mughal suzerainty amid Deccan power struggles.[21] In stabilizing the Carnatic, Anwaruddin prioritized quelling internal disruptions and enforcing administrative order, duties central to his role as viceroy.[9] He intervened to suppress potential rebellions among local poligars and revenue defaulters, restoring fiscal discipline and agricultural productivity in a territory fragmented by prior Nawabi mismanagement.[5] Efforts included reorganizing revenue collection to curb jaghir abuses and compelling obedience from semi-autonomous chieftains, which temporarily mitigated the anarchy that had prompted Nizam-ul-Mulk's 1743 expedition.[22] These measures fostered short-term equilibrium, enabling the Nawab to collect tribute and maintain Hyderabad's oversight without immediate large-scale revolts. Anwaruddin's alliances with European traders further bolstered regional stability by countering external threats. In the First Carnatic War, his forces, led by his son Mahfuz Khan, repelled a French incursion at the Battle of Adyar on December 24, 1746, securing a victory that recaptured Madras and nearby territories from Bertrand-François Mahé de La Bourdonnais's troops.[1] This action not only checked French expansion but also reaffirmed British commercial footholds, aligning with Anwaruddin's strategy to leverage European rivalries for internal order rather than allowing unchecked foreign footholds to exacerbate local instability.[5] Such pragmatic engagements underscored his commitment to balanced governance, prioritizing loyalty to overlords while safeguarding the Carnatic's fragile cohesion against multifaceted pressures.

Criticisms and Strategic Shortcomings

Anwaruddin Khan's tenure as Nawab of Arcot faced criticism for his perceived lack of deep-rooted local alliances, stemming from his appointment by Nizam-ul-Mulk Asaf Jah I in 1744 as an administrator from Hyderabad rather than a native Carnatic figure. This outsider status fueled opposition from regional claimants, notably Chanda Sahib, son-in-law of the previous Nawab Dost Ali Khan, who contested Anwaruddin's legitimacy and garnered support from disaffected local elites.[15][23] Such resentment weakened Anwaruddin's base, as his reliance on Nizam-backed authority failed to fully integrate Carnatic polities, contributing to internal divisions that adversaries exploited during succession conflicts.[2] Strategically, Anwaruddin's alignment with Nasir Jung in the Hyderabad succession dispute after Nizam-ul-Mulk's death in 1748 positioned him against the French-supported Muzaffar Jung and Chanda Sahib coalition, underestimating the transformative impact of European military intervention. By endorsing Nasir Jung, Anwaruddin instigated confrontation without adequate countermeasures against Joseph François Dupleix's forces, whose disciplined infantry and artillery outmatched traditional Indian cavalry-heavy tactics.[14][22] This miscalculation exposed the Carnatic to invasion, as Anwaruddin's appeals to the British East India Company yielded limited immediate aid due to their neutrality policy at the time.[23] The Battle of Ambur on August 3, 1749, exemplified these shortcomings, where Anwaruddin's approximately 10,000-strong force, primarily horsemen, suffered a decisive defeat against the numerically inferior but technologically superior alliance of Muzaffar Jung, Chanda Sahib, and French troops numbering around 400 Europeans with artillery. Internal betrayal compounded the failure, as subordinate commander Hussein Khan Tahir defected mid-battle, directing cannon fire against Anwaruddin and fracturing command cohesion.[21][15] Historians attribute the rout to Anwaruddin's inadequate adaptation to combined arms warfare, with his reliance on outdated Mughal-style formations unable to withstand French volley fire and grapeshot, resulting in his death and the rapid collapse of his regime.[24] Governance critiques highlight Anwaruddin's challenges in revenue administration and debt management, which strained resources amid ongoing conflicts, though direct evidence of personal mismanagement is sparse compared to later Nawabs. His short rule (1744–1749) prioritized loyalty to Hyderabad over fiscal reforms or military modernization, leaving the Carnatic vulnerable to European ascendancy and post-defeat power vacuums.[25] These factors underscore a broader strategic oversight in navigating the shifting dynamics of Indo-European rivalries, prioritizing nominal suzerainty over pragmatic power consolidation.[26]

Long-Term Impact on South Indian Politics

The death of Anwaruddin Khan at the Battle of Ambur on August 3, 1749, precipitated a succession crisis that entrenched European powers in Carnatic governance, fundamentally altering South Indian political structures by prioritizing foreign alliances over indigenous authority. With Anwaruddin's elder son, Muhammad Ali Khan Walajah, fleeing to Trichinopoly under British protection, French-backed Chanda Sahib briefly claimed the nawabship, intensifying the Second Carnatic War and drawing the British East India Company into direct military commitments to secure their protégé.[14][24] This conflict, resolved in Muhammad Ali's favor by 1752 through British victories at battles like Arcot and Trichinopoly, established a precedent for nawabi legitimacy contingent on Company support, eroding the autonomy of Hyderabad's subahdars and fragmenting Mughal oversight in the Deccan.[25] Over subsequent decades, Muhammad Ali's reliance on British loans and troops to maintain power—totaling millions of rupees by the 1760s—fostered economic dependency that translated into political subordination, as revenues from Carnatic districts were increasingly pledged to service debts accrued during these wars.[25] The 1765 grant of diwani rights to the Company by the Mughal emperor further centralized British fiscal control, sidelining local zamindars and nawabi intermediaries in favor of Company-appointed collectors, which stifled indigenous revenue systems and contributed to agrarian distress across Tamil territories.[27] This model of subsidiary alliances, prototyped in the Carnatic, prefigured British paramountcy over much of southern India, as seen in the 1792 subsidiary treaty with Muhammad Ali and the 1801 annexation of administrative powers, rendering the nawabship a ceremonial entity under Company veto.[28] Anwaruddin's failed bid for stability under Nizam-ul-Mulk's aegis thus inadvertently accelerated the decline of Persianate Muslim polities in the south, replacing them with a hybrid system where European commercial interests dictated alliances and successions, a dynamic that persisted until direct Crown rule post-1857 and reshaped regional power from decentralized nayak-nawab coalitions to centralized colonial administration.[24] The ensuing debts and military dependencies, estimated to have drained over 10 million pagodas from Carnatic revenues by the late 18th century, not only weakened rival French influence but also precluded unified resistance to British expansion, enabling the Company's southward push into Mysore and beyond by the 1790s.[25][27]
