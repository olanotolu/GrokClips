Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As

Al-Ḥakam ibn Abī al-ʿĀṣ ibn Umayya (died c. 655 CE) was a member of the Quraysh tribe's Banu Umayya clan, serving as the father of Umayyad caliph Marwan I—the founder of the Marwanid branch of the dynasty—and the paternal uncle of caliph ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān.[1][2] Initially a vocal opponent of Muḥammad who publicly mocked the Prophet and early Muslims, al-Ḥakam was exiled from Mecca to Ta'if following its conquest by Muslim forces in 630 CE due to his persistent irreverence.[3] He later professed conversion to Islam after the conquest and was eventually recalled to Medina by his nephew ʿUthmān during the latter's caliphate, though his early antagonism remained a point of contention among contemporaries.[4] Al-Ḥakam married ʿĀʾmina bint ʿAlqāma al-Kinānīyya, who bore his son Marwān, thereby linking the pre-Islamic Umayyad lineage to the caliphal succession that shaped the early Islamic empire's political structure.[1]

Ancestry and Early Life

Umayyad Clan Origins

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-ʿĀṣ traced his paternal lineage to the Banu Umayya clan within the Quraysh tribe, a dominant group in pre-Islamic Mecca. He was the son of Abū al-ʿĀṣ ibn Umayya, positioning him as a direct grandson of Umayya ibn ʿAbd Shams, the eponymous progenitor of the Umayyad clan whose descendants formed a powerful branch of Quraysh society.[2][5] This descent placed Al-Hakam among the clan's core members, benefiting from its established status in Meccan trade networks and tribal leadership. Through his father Abū al-ʿĀṣ, Al-Hakam was the brother of ʿAffān ibn Abī al-ʿĀṣ, thereby serving as the paternal uncle to ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān, the third Rashidun caliph whose election in 644 CE highlighted the Umayyads' emerging influence in early Islamic governance.[6][7] This kinship tie exemplified the clan's internal cohesion and its strategic familial links, which later propelled Umayyad figures into positions of authority following the Prophet Muhammad's death in 632 CE. In pre-Islamic Arabia, the Umayyads functioned primarily as merchants, leveraging Mecca's position as a commercial hub to amass wealth through caravan trade to Syria and Yemen while upholding the tribe's polytheistic traditions centered on the Kaaba.[8] As a leading Quraysh subclan, they contributed to the political equilibrium among Meccan factions, often mediating disputes and sponsoring pilgrimages that reinforced their custodianship role over sacred sites.[9] This socioeconomic prominence afforded clan members like Al-Hakam a high social standing, insulated by networks of kinship and economic interdependence in a tribal confederation.

Pre-Islamic Activities and Status

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As was a member of the Banu Umayya clan within the Quraysh tribe, which dominated pre-Islamic Mecca economically and politically as custodians of the Kaaba and organizers of trade caravans to Syria and Yemen.[10] [11] Born in Mecca around the late sixth century CE to Abi al-As ibn Umayya, he belonged to a lineage that included his brother Affan ibn Abi al-As, father of the merchant Uthman ibn Affan, underscoring the family's embedded position among the city's merchant nobility.[6] [12] The Banu Umayya derived their elite status from commerce and money-lending, activities that fueled Meccan prosperity amid a polytheistic tribal order resistant to monotheistic challenges from Jewish and Christian traders along caravan routes.[12] [11] Al-Hakam's familial role in this merchant class involved upholding Quraysh interests in tribal alliances and market oversight, where economic dominance intersected with defense of traditional idol worship against ideological incursions.[10] As such, he exemplified the pre-Islamic Umayyad profile of wealth accumulation through trade partnerships and mediation in intertribal disputes, setting the context for clan-wide preservation of polytheistic hegemony.[12]

Opposition to Muhammad and Islam

Initial Hostility in Mecca

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-ʿĀs, a notable figure from the Banu Umayya clan of Quraysh, emerged as a staunch opponent of Muhammad's prophethood during the early Meccan period, spanning roughly 610 to 622 CE, when the nascent Muslim community faced systematic persecution. Traditional biographical accounts, drawing from sirah literature, depict him actively resisting the da'wah efforts, aligning with the broader Quraysh elite's strategy to safeguard tribal polytheism and commercial interests threatened by Islam's monotheistic challenge. His hostility reflected causal motivations rooted in preserving clan prestige and economic dominance in Mecca's pilgrimage-based trade, rather than abstract theological dispute.[13] Specific instances of his opposition included verbal harassment directed at Muhammad, such as ridiculing the Prophet's message and public proclamations, which contributed to the mocking atmosphere that intimidated potential converts. Sunni historical narratives identify Al-Hakam among prominent Quraysh ridiculers who undermined early preaching by deriding revelations as fabrications or follies, thereby hindering propagation among kin and allies. This phase of resistance coincided with intensified tribal boycotts and physical torments against converts, though direct evidence ties Al-Hakam more to ideological sabotage than overt violence.[14] His stance likely intersected with the faction under Amr ibn Hisham (Abu Jahl), a leading antagonist from Banu Makhzum, in coordinated efforts to isolate Muhammad socially and economically pre-Hijra. Quraysh assemblies, where opponents like Al-Hakam voiced dissent, aimed to delegitimize Islam as a Hashimite innovation disruptive to ancestral norms, evidenced by reports of collective oaths against aiding the Prophet. These actions empirically prolonged the Meccan hardship, forcing migrations to Abyssinia by 615 CE, without Al-Hakam's personal involvement in documented expulsions or assaults.[3]

Role During Key Events Pre-Conquest

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As emerged as a prominent antagonist to Muhammad in the early Meccan period, actively ridiculing the Prophet through public imitation of his gait and mannerisms, a deliberate act aimed at discrediting his claims and impeding the spread of Islam among Quraysh society. This form of derision, conducted in the streets of Mecca prior to the Hijrah in 622 CE, contributed to the broader campaign of psychological and social pressure exerted by polytheistic leaders to suppress the monotheistic message, which challenged the established religious and commercial order reliant on pilgrimage to the Kaaba's idols. Such tactics exacerbated the persecution of early converts, fostering an environment of hostility that culminated in the migration to Medina and the onset of armed conflicts. No historical records indicate Al-Hakam's direct participation in major battles such as Badr on 17 Ramadan 2 AH (March 624 CE), where Quraysh forces suffered defeat, or Uhud on 7 Shawwal 3 AH (March 625 CE), despite his alignment with the Umayyad clan's leadership in opposing the Muslim exodus and subsequent raids. His role appears confined to ideological resistance, reinforcing Meccan unity against prophetic disruption through persistent mockery and verbal antagonism, which sustained polytheist cohesion and delayed Islam's consolidation in the Hijaz. This opposition, grounded in defending entrenched economic privileges tied to idolatrous trade networks, underscored the causal link between elite self-interest and the prolonged pre-conquest strife. Following the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah in Dhu al-Qa'dah 6 AH (March 628 CE), Al-Hakam maintained his hostility amid Umayyad skepticism toward the pact's provisions, which granted Muslims pilgrimage rights and a ten-year truce, potentially eroding Quraysh authority over allied tribes. Though specific interventions by him in treaty-related tensions—such as disputes over conversions or border incidents—are undocumented, his unrepentant stance exemplified the internal divisions that weakened Meccan resolve, indirectly facilitating the violations leading to the conquest in 630 CE.[15]

Conversion and Exile

Circumstances of Acceptance of Islam

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As accepted Islam following the Muslim conquest of Mecca in 630 CE, during which Muhammad entered the city with an army of approximately 10,000 followers after the Quraysh surrendered with minimal resistance.[4] This event, known as Fath Makkah, marked a turning point where many former opponents, including members of the Umayyad clan, publicly declared their submission to avoid execution or enslavement, as Muhammad had granted general amnesty but reserved judgment on persistent adversaries.[3] Historical accounts indicate Al-Hakam's conversion occurred specifically after Muhammad's entry into the Kaaba, aligning him with a cohort of "late converts" who professed faith post-victory rather than during the earlier Meccan persecutions.[16] In Sunni tradition, Al-Hakam is classified as a sahabi (companion of the Prophet) due to his acceptance during this period, though distinguished from early converts by the timing after military subjugation of Mecca.[4] Empirical reports from biographical compilations emphasize the pragmatic incentives: the decisive defeat of Quraysh forces at events like the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah's aftermath compelled survival-oriented pledges, with little evidence of prior personal conviction in Al-Hakam's case given his documented pre-conquest opposition.[3] Some narratives suggest his declaration was outwardly compliant to evade retribution, as Muhammad's policy allowed integration for those swearing allegiance without immediate probing of inner motives, though later behaviors fueled retrospective debates on authenticity.[16] Causal analysis points to the conquest's overwhelming success as the primary driver, where the collapse of polytheist resistance—evidenced by the destruction of 360 idols in the Kaaba—shifted power dynamics, prompting opportunistic conversions among elites like Al-Hakam to preserve status and kin ties within the emerging Muslim polity.[3] While Sunni sources affirm the validity of such post-Fath conversions based on prophetic acceptance without revocation at the time, variant historical interpretations, often from sectarian critics, highlight potential insincerity inferred from subsequent actions rather than contemporaneous testimony.[4] No primary hadith directly narrates Al-Hakam's pledge, but the broader context of amnesty declarations supports his inclusion among those granted provisional community entry.[17]

Exile from Medina and Return

According to narrations in historical accounts, Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As faced exile from Medina around 630-631 CE, shortly after the Muslim conquest of Mecca, due to allegations of mocking the Prophet Muhammad or deriding Muslim practices.[18] [3] These reports describe the decree as a response to persistent disruptive behavior following his nominal acceptance of Islam, with the Prophet instructing him to depart and prohibiting his return.[19] Al-Hakam relocated to Ta'if during this period of banishment, residing there amid the expanding Muslim polity.[19] The exile's duration spanned over a decade, aligning with the final years of Muhammad's life and the early caliphates of Abu Bakr and Umar, though no records indicate appeals or reversals until later.[3] Under Caliph Uthman ibn Affan (r. 644-656 CE), Al-Hakam received permission to return to Medina, reportedly through familial intercession given Uthman's Umayyad kinship.[20] Some interpretations in sirah-derived sources affirm this reinstatement, while others question the Medina-specific exile or portray the return as unproblematic, suggesting any prior restrictions applied only to Mecca.[21] This sequence reflects variances in early Islamic historiography, with empirical timelines anchored to the post-conquest era but lacking uniform corroboration across accounts.[3]

Later Life in the Muslim Community

Activities Under Caliph Uthman

During the caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan, which began in 644 CE, Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As was recalled from exile in Ta'if and permitted to resettle in Medina, a decision enacted by his nephew the caliph without recorded opposition from prominent companions.[22] This relocation facilitated Al-Hakam's proximity to Uthman, enabling informal advisory influence within the Umayyad kinship network that supported the caliph's governance amid administrative expansions into new provinces.[23] Al-Hakam received financial stipends from Uthman, totaling around 100,000 dirhams according to historical reports, which underscored his reintegration and role in bolstering familial loyalty during a era marked by tribal tensions and precursors to the First Fitnah (656 CE).[23] His presence in Medina contributed to stabilizing Umayyad ties in the capital, as evidenced by Uthman's extension of symbolic honors to him alongside relatives like Abu Sufyan ibn Harb, helping to reconcile pre-Islamic tribal elements with the expanding Muslim polity. No accounts indicate Al-Hakam's involvement in provincial governance or military campaigns, focusing instead on his sustained residence and alignment with the caliph's authority until the mid-650s CE.[22]

Relocation and Integration

Following his conversion shortly after the Conquest of Mecca in 630 CE, Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As initially resided in Mecca, maintaining settlement there in the immediate post-conversion period. Under the caliphate of his nephew Uthman ibn Affan (r. 644–656 CE), he relocated to Medina, where he had previously been prohibited from entering due to his earlier antagonism toward the Prophet Muhammad. This relocation, occurring amid Umayyad influence in the caliphal administration, marked a key step in his reintegration into the central Muslim community.[3] Integration proceeded through robust kinship networks, particularly his relation to Uthman as uncle, which provided social and political leverage within Quraysh circles in Medina. Sunni historical narratives portray Al-Hakam engaging with the sahabah in communal affairs, affirming his role in the Muslim polity as a late convert recognized as a companion (sahabi) for having met the Prophet. These ties enabled participation alongside established companions, leveraging clan solidarity to bridge his prior outsider status.[4][3] Sunni sources document no verifiable major disruptions or conflicts attributed to Al-Hakam following this settlement, with records emphasizing continuity in community life until his death in Medina around 655–656 CE. This absence of post-relocation incidents in primary accounts contrasts sharply with his documented pre-conversion opposition, indicating effective assimilation via familial and tribal mechanisms in the expanding ummah.[3][21]

Family and Descendants

Marriages and Immediate Family

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As married Amina bint Alqama ibn Safwan al-Kinaniyya, who had previously been divorced from his brother Affan ibn Abi al-As.[1] With this wife, he fathered at least one son, Marwan ibn al-Hakam, born circa 623 CE.[2][24] As the brother of Affan ibn Abi al-As—the father of Uthman ibn Affan—al-Hakam held the position of paternal uncle to the third Rashidun caliph, Uthman.[2] No other wives or children are verifiably documented in primary genealogical accounts.

Lineage to Umayyad Rulers

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As served as the direct father of Marwan ibn al-Hakam, who ascended as the fourth Umayyad caliph in June 684 CE and ruled until his death in April 685 CE, thereby founding the Marwanid branch that dominated the dynasty thereafter. This paternal link positioned Al-Hakam as the progenitor of the Marwanid line, which succeeded the Sufyanid branch descended from Abu Sufyan and maintained Umayyad control over the caliphate from 684 to 750 CE.[25] Al-Hakam's grandson, Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan, inherited the caliphate immediately after his father's brief reign, governing from 685 to 705 CE and overseeing expansions that solidified the dynasty's authority across the Islamic empire. Subsequent Marwanid caliphs, including Al-Walid I (r. 705–715 CE), Sulayman (r. 715–717 CE), and others down to Marwan II (r. 744–750 CE), traced their legitimacy through this unbroken paternal descent from Al-Hakam, emphasizing clan continuity within the Banu Umayya.[25] This lineage empirically bolstered Umayyad claims to rule post the assassination of Caliph Uthman ibn Affan in 656 CE, as Al-Hakam's bloodline—sharing a common grandfather, Abi al-As ibn Umayya, with Uthman—provided a genetic tether to the earlier caliphal authority amid rival challenges from figures like Abd Allah ibn al-Zubayr.[26] The Marwanid succession thus represented not merely political inheritance but a consolidation of Umayyad familial hegemony, with Al-Hakam's descendants numbering among the most enduring rulers of the dynasty until its overthrow by the Abbasids in 750 CE.

Controversies and Differing Sectarian Views

Allegations of Mockery and Prophetic Curse

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As, having accepted Islam after the conquest of Mecca in 630 CE, faced accusations of post-conversion misconduct involving imitation of the Prophet Muhammad's speech patterns, walking style, and gestures, such as shaking his head and hands in mockery while trailing behind the Prophet.[27] [28] These reports, drawn from early biographical and hadith literature, describe such actions as occurring in Medina, where Al-Hakam resided briefly post-conversion, potentially stemming from lingering tribal affiliations with Quraysh opponents.[19] In response, the Prophet issued a decree banishing Al-Hakam from Medina, directing him toward Ta'if, with explicit orders prohibiting his return.[19] [3] This expulsion is narrated in multiple chains, including those preserved in Sunni biographical works, though the precise motivations—ranging from overt mockery to general abusive conduct toward Muslims—vary across transmitters.[18] Certain narrations further claim the Prophet invoked a curse upon Al-Hakam and his progeny, stating phrases akin to "woe to my ummah from the loins of this man" or directly naming him in maledictions.[18] [29] These appear in collections like Musnad Ahmad ibn Hanbal and biographical dictionaries such as Al-Isti'ab by Ibn Abd al-Barr, but often as solitary reports (ahad) with isnads criticized for weakness or omission of explicit names in some variants.[30] [3] No equivalent appears in the rigorously authenticated Sahih al-Bukhari or Sahih Muslim, highlighting discrepancies in chain reliability that invite scrutiny of transmitter biases, particularly in sectarian contexts favoring or critiquing Umayyad lineages.[3]

Sunni Affirmation as Sahabi vs. Critical Narratives

In Sunni Islamic scholarship, Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As is affirmed as a sahabi (companion of the Prophet Muhammad) based on reports of his conversion to Islam during the Prophet's lifetime, specifically after the conquest of Mecca in 630 CE, followed by no recorded apostasy, thereby fulfilling the core criteria articulated by major jurists: physical companionship with the Prophet while in a state of faith and persistence in Islam until death.[4] Biographical compendia of companions, such as those compiling lists of early converts from Quraysh clans, include him among those who embraced Islam post-conquest and integrated into the community, reflecting a consensus among historians like Ibn Sa'd that prioritizes verifiable meeting and belief over later disputes.[3] Contrasting narratives, predominantly from Shi'i historiographical traditions, challenge this status by emphasizing accounts of Al-Hakam's alleged mockery of the Prophet, permanent exile from Medina around 630-632 CE, and a prophetic curse extending to his progeny, which purportedly render him unfit for the unassailable virtue accorded to sahaba in Sunni doctrine.[18] These critiques often draw on reports in works like al-Tabari's history, interpreting exile as evidence of irredeemable hostility tied to Umayyad lineage, and cite hadiths of cursing (e.g., in Musnad Ahmad variants) as disqualifying factors that undermine blanket sahabi affirmation. However, such accounts exhibit authenticity issues, including reliance on narrators deemed weak or fabricated by Sunni muhaddithun like al-Dhahabi and Ibn Hajar, such as chains involving al-Waqidi or omitted names suggesting tampering.[3] An empirical tally of hadith corpora reveals stronger chains supporting conversion and community participation—e.g., via Uthman ibn Affan's kinship ties facilitating recall—against fewer, contested narrations of curse or mockery, which Shi'i sources amplify amid sectarian polemics against Umayyad legitimacy but lack the rigorous isnad scrutiny standard in Sunni methodology.[3] This disparity underscores how critical views, while preserving alternative transmissions, often prioritize interpretive hostility over hadith grading, reflecting institutional biases in Shi'i historiography that retroactively politicize companionship criteria absent in early Sunni compilations. Sunni affirmation thus rests on first-order evidentiary standards, whereas critiques introduce causal assumptions of perpetual enmity unsubstantiated by consensus sahaba rosters.

Death and Historical Assessment

Date and Circumstances of Death

Al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As died in 31 AH (approximately 652 CE) in Medina, during the caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan. Other biographical accounts place the date slightly later, in 35 AH (655 CE).[31] Classical sources such as al-Dhahabi's Siyar A'lam al-Nubala record no extraordinary circumstances surrounding his passing, indicating a natural death without martyrdom or violence. Given estimates of his birth around 600 CE, Al-Hakam was in his mid-50s at death, consistent with attributions of advanced age but not extreme longevity in some variant reports claiming up to 88 years. No primary accounts detail illness or specific causes, reflecting the routine nature of his end amid the stability of Uthman's later years prior to the caliph's assassination in 35 AH.[31]

Legacy in Islamic Historiography

In Sunni historiographical traditions, al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As is recognized as a sahabi whose late conversion after the conquest of Mecca in 630 CE positioned his lineage as a stabilizing force in the Umayyad caliphate, particularly through his son Marwan I, who founded the Marwanid branch in 684 CE and restored order amid the second fitna.[4] Scholars like Ibn Taymiyyah rejected unsubstantiated claims impugning his character, arguing that reports of persistent enmity were fabricated or exaggerated by later opponents of Umayyad rule, thereby affirming his role in the broader continuity of Quraysh leadership within the caliphate.[21] This portrayal emphasizes causal outcomes: his integration under Caliph Uthman (r. 644–656 CE) enabled progeny who expanded Islamic governance across provinces, prioritizing empirical dynastic resilience over retrospective purity tests.[3] Shia and anti-Umayyad narratives, conversely, depict al-Hakam as emblematic of insincere or nominal conversions among Meccan elites, whose familial ambitions exacerbated divisions in the early ummah and symbolized the shift from prophetic ideals to hereditary monarchy.[18] Such views, rooted in traditions attributing prophetic disapproval to his pre-exile conduct, frame his legacy as a cautionary marker of fitnah's origins, where clan loyalty undermined communal equity, though these accounts often reflect sectarian polemics against Umayyad legitimacy rather than uniform consensus.[15] Overall, Islamic historiography balances al-Hakam's foundational contributions to caliphal endurance—evident in the Marwanids' administrative reforms and territorial consolidations under successors like Abd al-Malik (r. 685–705 CE)—against debates on conversion authenticity, with Sunni sources crediting his line for pragmatic governance that sustained Islamic expansion, while critical traditions highlight opportunity-driven motives as diluting early doctrinal rigor.[5] This duality underscores historiography's reliance on isnad chains, where source credibility varies: pro-Umayyad reports prioritize verifiable post-conversion actions and dynastic impacts, whereas oppositional ones amplify anecdotal curses to critique monarchical deviations from elective succession.[3]
