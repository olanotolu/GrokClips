Killarney, Ontario

Killarney is a municipality located on the northern shore of Georgian Bay in the Sudbury District of Ontario, Canada. The area has been inhabited by Indigenous peoples for thousands of years prior to European settlement. Killarney is commonly associated with Killarney Provincial Park, which is a large wilderness park located to the east of the townsite and occupies much of the municipality's expanded boundary. In addition to the community of Killarney itself, the community of Hartley Bay, and the ghost towns of Collins Inlet and Key Harbour, are also located within the municipal boundaries.[1] The eastern end of the La Cloche Mountains is also located within the municipality of Killarney. History

Marie, Michigan, along with his wife Josephte Sai-sai-go-no-kwe, an Odawa woman from Michigan and the niece and adopted daughter of Chief Kitchi Bashigigan. Soon after the arrival of the de la MorandiÃ¨res, many French-Canadian families started to settle in Killarney, starting many fishing businesses in the process. Although there was already an existing settlement here at that time, they are credited with the founding of the settlement. The population increased when the logging industry took off in Collins Inlet in the late 1800s. Hundreds of people from around Ontario went to Collins Inlet for work and established a large community in the area. The logging industry collapsed in the early 1900s because of the low demand and the population declined. Remains of the large village can be seen across from the present-day Mill Lake lodge. In 1929, the place was originally incorporated as the Township of Rutherford and George Island.[5] Another key industry that had a major impact on Killarney's economy was the fishing industry. There were many fishing companies in Killarney including C.W.L (Charles William Low), The Nobles, Charles Low/Joseph Roque partnership, and Albert Lowe. The collapse of the fishing industry at the end of the 1950s affected many families in Killarney including the Low, Roque, Jackman, Proulx, and Herbert families. Recreational fishing was also an industry. A fishing camp was run by the Fruehauf Trailer Company out of Detroit, Michigan. Roy Fruehauf, president of the company from 1949 to 1961, was primarily responsible for operating Killarney Mountain Lodge. Clients and guests would be flown in via Mallard seaplanes for vacations during the warmer summer months. A quarry in Killarney Bay provided employment opportunities for many families after the downfall of the fishing industry. The quarry is located on the west side of the Lansdowne Channel and operated efficiently for several decades. Operations eventually ceased, with tourism becoming the dominant industry. The major tourism businesses in Killarney were the Killarney Mountain Lodge, the Sportsman's Inn, the Killarney Bay Inn, the Pines Inn, Herbert Fisheries, and Pitfield's General Store. The booming tourism industry brought hundreds of thousands of dollars to the businesses of Killarney as well as the many families that contributed to it. The current tourism businesses of Killarney (as of 2025) include the Killarney Mountain Lodge, the Sportsman's Inn, Gateway Marina, Herbert Fisheries, and Pitfield's General Store.[6][7][8] The place name Shebahonaning (Ojibwe for "canoe passage") was changed to Killarney in the mid-1840s, reportedly at the suggestion of merchant Robert Johnston, who named it after his Irish hometown; the post office officially adopted "Killarney" in 1854.[9] Lady Dufferin, wife of the Governor-General of Canada, has often been credited with the name change, but the passage in her journal which describes their stop in Killarney is dated 1874â€”almost twenty years after the Post Office had replaced the Shebahonaning postal stamp with one reading Killarney. In July 1962, Highway 637 was established, connecting Killarney to major highways like Highway 69 and Highway 17, making it easier for people to travel to major cities like Toronto or Sudbury. Before that, the people of the town had to either go by boat in the summer to Little Current and then take a train to Sudbury or other areas to obtain food and resources or by horse and buggy in the winter. On January 1, 1999, the Ontario provincial government merged the Township of Rutherford and George Island (having an area of 31.6 km2, 12.2 sq mi in the 1996 census) with large tracts of surrounding unincorporated areas, forming the new Municipality of Killarney (with an area of 1,513.30 km2, 584.29 sq mi in 2001 census). The municipality was also transferred from the Manitoulin District to the Sudbury District at that time. In 2006, the municipality was enlarged again (to 1,654.58 km2, 638.84 sq mi) when it annexed the unorganized mainland portion of Manitoulin District. By the 2021 census, its territory was reduced to 1,469.40 km2 (567.34 sq mi) due to the incorporation of Point Grondine 3 reserve.

Geography

The larger municipality of Killarney now encompasses virtually all of Killarney Provincial Park and the French River delta, with its western boundary approximately 13 km (8 mi) east of Highway 69, and the townsite over 68 km (42 mi) from the highway. The municipality spans parts of the Sudbury, Parry Sound, and Manitoulin Districts. Despite the municipality's geographic size, most of its population continues to reside in the community of Killarney itself, although smaller settlements also exist at Hartley Bay and Bigwood.

Demographics

In the 2021 Census of Population conducted by Statistics Canada, Killarney had a population of 397 living in 193 of its 443 total private dwellings, a change of 2.8% from its 2016 population of 386. With a land area of 1,469.4 km2 (567.3 sq mi), it had a population density of 0.3/km2 (0.7/sq mi) in 2021. In 2021, 89.4% of residents reported European ethnic origins, 5.0% Indigenous, and 2.5% visible minorities. Economy

The economy of Killarney has historically been based on fur trading, logging, commercial fishing, and mining, but is now primarily based on tourism, consisting mainly of wilderness lodges, campgrounds, and retail services geared towards campers and other visitors to Killarney.[11] A community museum, the Killarney Centennial Museum, is located in Killarney. Opened in 1967, it traces the area's history from the fur trade to the present.[12]

Fishing industry

Commercial fishing was a major industry in Killarney from the mid-19th century, with operations led by families such as the Lowes (including Charles William Lowe) and Roques (including Joseph Roque partnerships). Many fishing families built outposts around Georgian Bay to sustain the community. The industry collapsed at the end of the 1950s due to overfishing and technological changes, affecting families including the Low, Roque, Jackman, Proulx, and Herbert.[13][14] This led many to seek alternative employment, shifting the economy towards tourism. Recreational fishing remains popular, and recent waterfront expansions, including a 2022 wharf upgrade, support fish processing and create seasonal jobs.[15]

Government

The Mayor of Killarney is Michael Reider, and the councillors are Ward 1: Rob Campbell, Dave Froats, and Peggy Roque; Ward 2: Mary Bradbury and Nikola Grubic. Although not officially part of the Sudbury East region, Killarney participates in the regional Sudbury East Planning Board with the municipalities of French River, St. Charles and Markstay-Warren. Tourism

One tourism website lists 21 sites, sights, and things to do in the area, though much of the content relates to provincial parks.[16] Killarney features several campgrounds, including those in Killarney Provincial Park, Rocher Rouge Campground, and Roque's Marina. Roque's Marina offers camping with 15 or 30 amp power, EV charging, transient and seasonal dockage, a 24-hour coin-operated laundromat, showers, indoor toilets, and block and cube ice.[17][18] Prominent accommodations in Killarney include the Killarney Mountain Lodge and the Sportsman's Inn, which are sister resorts. Guests from either can use the amenities of both. Other options include the Avalon Eco Resort, Bearskin Lodge & Outfitters, Lang Lake Resort, yurts in Killarney Provincial Park, and cottages such as Hartley Bay Cottages.[19][6][20] Killarney is part of the Sudbury East region and participates in the Sudbury East Planning Board with the municipalities of French River, Markstay-Warren, and St.-Charles.[21] During the spring and summer of 2020, the area's tourism was severely impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic and international travel restrictions, including from the United States. Tourism has since recovered, with increased visitation reported in subsequent years.[11]

Infrastructure

Transportation

Killarney is accessible from Highway 637, which is the only highway in and out. Killarney Airport is located in the municipality. Killarney is also frequently visited by boat.
