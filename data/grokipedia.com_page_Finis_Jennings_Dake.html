Finis Jennings Dake

Finis Jennings Dake (October 18, 1902 – July 7, 1987) was an American Pentecostal minister and evangelist whose primary legacy is the Dake Annotated Reference Bible, a King James Version study edition published in 1963 that features over 35,000 commentary notes, 500,000 cross-references, and 9,000 outline headings emphasizing a hyper-literal interpretation of Scripture.[1][2] Born in Iberia, Missouri, to James Henry and Mary Ellen Dake as one of eleven children, Dake converted to Christianity in May 1920 at age 17 after rejecting it in his youth, subsequently dedicating approximately 100,000 hours to Bible study and vowing to teach only what could be scripturally proven.[2][3] Dake's ministry included pastoring a Pentecostal church in Zion, Illinois, from 1932, where he gained a following for his emphasis on spiritual gifts and biblical literalism, though it was disrupted in 1937 when he pleaded guilty to violating the Mann Act by transporting a 16-year-old girl across state lines for immoral purposes, resulting in a six-month prison sentence during which he reportedly advanced work on his Bible annotations.[4][5][6] His church supported him through the ordeal, but the incident severed his affiliation with the Assemblies of God.[4] The Dake Bible achieved significant popularity in Pentecostal and charismatic circles, selling tens of thousands of copies and influencing figures in the Word of Faith movement through its detailed topical indexes and endorsements of prosperity, healing, and end-times teachings.[5][1] Despite its reach, Dake's work has faced substantial criticism from evangelical scholars for doctrinal deviations, including portraying God as possessing a literal spirit body with human-like parts, depicting the Trinity as three separate beings each with body, soul, and spirit, and conditioning salvation on works alongside faith—views seen as echoing non-orthodox theologies like Mormonism and undermining core Protestant tenets of God's immaterial nature and justification by faith alone.[4][5] Additional controversies include annotations supporting racial segregation as biblically mandated and assertions that physical healing is guaranteed for believers, which critics argue impose unbiblical guilt and contradict scriptural evidence of suffering among the faithful.[5][4] These elements, combined with Dake's unrepented personal scandal, have led organizations like the Christian Research Institute and Christianity Today to caution against its use, even as it persists in print and online formats for those prioritizing exhaustive scriptural referencing over theological orthodoxy.[4][5]

Early Life

Birth and Family Background

Finis Jennings Dake was born on October 18, 1902, in Iberia, a small rural town in Miller County, Missouri, to parents James Henry Dake (1869–1912) and Mary Ellen Hicks Dake (1870–1951).[7][8] The family lived in humble circumstances, with Dake later describing his birthplace as a dugout featuring a dirt floor and dirt walls, reflecting the modest agrarian lifestyle common in early 20th-century rural Missouri.[9] James Henry Dake, a farmer by occupation, had been born in Missouri to parents originating from Tennessee who had relocated eastward by the time of the 1870 census, establishing the family's roots in the American Midwest with ties to Southern migration patterns.[9] No records indicate siblings for Dake, suggesting he grew up as an only child amid the economic constraints of a working-class household in a sparsely populated county.[7][8]

Conversion and Ministry Beginnings

Religious Conversion

Finis Jennings Dake, born on February 25, 1902, grew up in a rural Missouri family with limited exposure to organized religion and initially rejected Christianity, viewing it skeptically amid what he perceived as inconsistent examples from professed believers.[3] This rejection persisted through his teenage years, during which he pursued secular interests including aspirations of becoming a cowboy actor and brief experiences as a hobo.[10] At age 17 in 1919, while in Tulsa, Oklahoma, Dake experienced a profound religious conversion, describing it as being "born again" after encountering Christians whose lives authentically demonstrated their faith, which confronted him with undeniable evidence of biblical truth.[4] [3] He underwent water baptism shortly thereafter, marking his public commitment to the faith.[11] This event shifted his trajectory decisively toward Pentecostal Christianity, with Dake later attributing an immediate spiritual empowerment that facilitated rapid Scripture memorization and preaching ability, though such claims of supernatural anointing remain interpretive and tied to his self-reported testimony.[4]

Ordination and Early Evangelistic Work

Dake first began preaching publicly in 1925, following his attendance at Central Bible Institute in Springfield, Missouri, during the mid-1920s.[4][12] He entered full-time ministry around 1924, sensing a call to evangelism at age 21.[13] In 1927, at the age of 24, Dake received ordination from the Assemblies of God in the New Mexico-Texas district while residing in Amarillo, Texas.[4][12][14] This credentialed him within Pentecostal circles, where he initially pursued pastoral roles before emphasizing itinerant evangelism.[4] From 1928 to 1931, Dake conducted evangelistic campaigns, basing himself in Tulsa and Enid, Oklahoma, during this period.[11][14] His early efforts focused on Pentecostal revival meetings, aligning with the movement's emphasis on spiritual gifts and literal biblical interpretation, though specific attendance figures or outcomes from these campaigns remain undocumented in primary records.[15] By 1927, he also took on administrative roles at Pentecostal Bible schools, blending teaching with evangelistic outreach.[15]

Legal Challenges

1937 Conviction and Imprisonment

In May 1936, Finis Jennings Dake, then pastor of Christian Assembly Church in Zion, Illinois, was arrested at his home by federal agents for violating the Mann Act.[16] The charge stemmed from Dake transporting a 16-year-old hitchhiker named Emma Barelli across the Wisconsin-Illinois state line, with evidence including hotel registrations in multiple cities listing them as husband and wife, indicating intent for "debauchery and other immoral practices."[4] [17] Dake admitted to the transportation but denied any immoral conduct, asserting he was assisting Barelli in finding employment; he attributed the accusations to spiritual opposition.[16] On February 9, 1937, before Federal Judge F. A. Geiger in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, Dake entered a guilty plea to the federal charge.[16] [4] He received a sentence of six months' imprisonment in the Milwaukee House of Correction, facing a potential maximum of ten years and a $10,000 fine under the Mann Act provisions.[18] Dake served approximately five months, gaining early release on July 18, 1937, after which he returned to his family and supportive congregation in Zion.[16] During his incarceration, he continued work on annotating the Bible, a project that later formed the basis of his reference edition.[17] The conviction resulted in the Assemblies of God revoking Dake's ordination credentials, severing his formal ties with the denomination and prompting a relocation to Cleveland, Tennessee, to resume independent Pentecostal ministry.[4][2]

Theological Writings

Development of Key Texts

Dake's theological writings emerged from his extensive personal Bible study, estimated at over 100,000 hours, which informed his preaching and teaching during evangelistic campaigns starting in the 1920s.[19] He initially produced tracts and pamphlets to articulate his interpretations, gradually expanding into full-length books that systematized his dispensational and literalist views. These early efforts laid the groundwork for more comprehensive texts by compiling scriptural cross-references, topical indexes, and explanatory notes derived from direct exegesis rather than secondary sources.[4] A pivotal early work, God's Plan for Man, originated from Dake's development of a large dispensational chart titled "Plan of the Ages," measuring 4 feet by 18 feet, which visually mapped God's progressive revelations across biblical history. First published in 1949, the book structured its content as 52 weekly lessons covering creation through eternity, emphasizing literal fulfillment of prophecies and divine covenants without allegorization.[19] [20] This text drew directly from Dake's sermon outlines and chart-based teachings, prioritizing scriptural harmony over traditional creeds.[21] Subsequent books, such as Revelation Expounded and Bible Truths Unmasked, built on this foundation by delving into eschatology and doctrinal clarifications, often incorporating hundreds of proof-texts to support positions like premillennialism and separation of divine dispensations. Dake's method involved exhaustive verse-by-verse analysis, rejecting interpretive traditions he viewed as extra-biblical, and instead favoring a plain-sense reading informed by his Pentecostal experiences. These works were self-published or issued through emerging outlets like Dake Bible Sales, reflecting his independence from denominational oversight.[19] The annotations later formalized in the Dake Bible evolved from these writings, as Dake began compiling study aids during his itinerant ministry to provide believers with tools equivalent to multiple reference volumes in one. This process, spanning decades, culminated in over 35,000 notes and 500,000 cross-references, achieved through seven years of intensive labor on the manuscript alone.[18] [22] His approach privileged empirical scriptural patterns—such as recurring themes of angelic activity and supernatural realms—over philosophical speculation, ensuring annotations served practical exposition for lay readers.[23]

Dake Annotated Reference Bible

The Dake Annotated Reference Bible is a study edition of the King James Version (KJV) Bible featuring extensive annotations by Finis Jennings Dake, first published in 1963 after over 100,000 hours of study spanning 43 years of his life.[24] [1] It includes more than 35,000 commentary notes, 500,000 cross-references, and approximately 9,000 outline headings for chapters and verses, designed to facilitate detailed scriptural analysis.[1] [24] The notes were compiled primarily from the late 1950s to 1963, drawing on Dake's prior works and emphasizing a literal interpretation of the text where possible.[25] Structurally, the Bible employs a two-column format for the scriptural text atop each page, with three columns of notes below, incorporating chain references, summaries of every book, and explanations of Hebrew and Greek terms, ancient customs, geography, and dispensational divisions.[1] These elements aim to provide comprehensive aids for readers, including Pentecostal perspectives on spiritual gifts and end-times prophecy, while avoiding allegorization in favor of direct, word-for-word rendering unless symbolism is explicitly indicated.[1] Distinctive annotations address topics such as the anthropomorphic attributes of God—depicting Him with bodily features like eyes, hands, and a form akin to man's—and elaborate on doctrines like pre-Adamic races and angelic interactions, reflecting Dake's commitment to unfiltered biblical literalism.[4] Subsequent editions expanded availability, including a New King James Version (NKJV) release in the 2010s and large-print KJV variants, published by Dake Publishing, with ongoing reprints maintaining the original notes' volume and detail.[1] The work's scope, equating to thousands of pages of supplemental material, positions it as one of the most annotated study Bibles, though its interpretations have drawn scrutiny for extending literalism to unconventional conclusions on creation chronology and human origins.[24]

Core Theological Positions

Dispensational Framework and Literalism

Finis Jennings Dake's dispensational framework structured biblical history into distinct periods of divine administration, each defined as "a moral or probationary period in angelic or human history during which God dealt with angels or human beings according to a particular test or responsibility, under which each was to remain true to his trust of administering affairs for God under His direction."[26] This approach emphasized progressive revelation, wherein God tests moral agents—angels or humans—with specific responsibilities, typically resulting in failure, judgment, and a new dispensation marked by adjusted revelation and government. Dake identified nine such dispensations, extending beyond the conventional seven to include pre-human angelic probation and eternal aspects: (1) God's rule in eternity past (angelic dispensation), (2) Innocence (Edenic period), (3) Conscience (post-Fall to Flood), (4) Human Government (post-Flood), (5) Promise (Abrahamic covenant), (6) Law (Mosaic era), (7) Grace (Church age), (8) Tribulation (future judgment), and (9) Millennial Kingdom (eternal phase).[27] [12] Each dispensation featured primary elements of stewardship, responsibility, and revelation, alongside secondary patterns of testing, failure, and divine judgment, enabling a systematic tracing of God's dealings across Scripture.[26] Central to Dake's dispensationalism was a commitment to literal hermeneutics, rejecting allegorization in favor of interpreting Scripture "literally wherein it is at all possible; if symbolic, figurative or typical language is used, then look for the literal truth it intends to convey."[1] This principle underpinned his treatment of prophetic texts, historical narratives, and doctrinal statements, treating them as factual unless explicitly figurative, which preserved distinctions between dispensations—such as the separation of Israel and the Church—and supported premillennial eschatology with a future literal thousand-year reign.[1] Dake applied this literalism rigorously to Old and New Testament prophecies, viewing unfulfilled predictions (e.g., restoration of Israel to the land) as awaiting precise, non-spiritualized fulfillment rather than symbolic of the Church. His annotated notes in the Dake Bible, exceeding 35,000 entries, consistently exemplified this method by clarifying terms, cross-referencing literal fulfillments, and avoiding spiritualizing interpretations that blurred dispensational boundaries.[1] Dake's framework critiqued covenant theology for conflating dispensations through overarching covenants, arguing instead for discrete administrations revealed progressively, each sovereignly terminated by God upon failure. This literal-dispensational lens extended to anthropology and creation, positing literal six-day creation and angelic prehistory, but remained anchored in empirical biblical data over speculative theology. While some dispensationalists limit to seven human-focused periods, Dake's inclusion of angelic and eternal dispensations reflected his exhaustive literal reading of passages like Genesis 1:1-2 and Isaiah 14, attributing cosmic judgments (e.g., pre-Adamic chaos) to prior angelic rebellion.[26] [27] His approach, detailed in works like God's Plan for Man (1949), prioritized scriptural consistency over traditional partitions, fostering a timeline of over 11,000 years from creation to eternity.[12]

Distinctive Doctrines on Creation and Anthropology

Dake's doctrine of creation diverged from young-earth interpretations by positing a "gap" between Genesis 1:1 and 1:2, viewing the former as the initial perfect creation of the heavens and earth and the latter as describing a chaotic state resulting from divine judgment on a pre-Adamite world.[12] This cataclysm, linked to Lucifer's rebellion (Isaiah 14:12-14; Ezekiel 28:11-17), destroyed an earlier inhabited order, including a pre-Adamite race of humans or beings, as inferred from passages like 2 Peter 3:5-6, which Dake interpreted as referencing a prior world that "perished, being overflowed with water."[12] [4] The six-day recreation in Genesis 1:3–2:25 then prepared the earth for Adam's dominion, with the command to "replenish the earth" in Genesis 1:28 indicating prior depletion from destruction.[12] This framework allowed Dake to accommodate geological evidence of ancient earth layers and fossils within a literal biblical timeline, attributing them to the pre-Adamite era rather than Noah's flood alone.[12] Adam, as the first man of the current creation, replaced fallen angelic rulership (exemplified by Lucifer) over the earth, restoring human oversight in God's plan.[4] Dake's annotations emphasized literal Hebrew terms like tohu (formless) and bohu (void) in Genesis 1:2 as evidence of ruin by judgment, not original creation, rejecting the idea of instantaneous divine formation without historical precedence.[12] In anthropology, Dake advocated a trichotomous composition of humanity—body, soul, and spirit—as distinct, separable elements, aligning with his literal hermeneutic applied to verses like 1 Thessalonians 5:23 ("spirit, soul, and body").[4] The spirit facilitates direct communion with God and eternal life; the soul encompasses the intellect, emotions, volition, and personality; and the body serves as the material vessel for earthly senses and actions.[4] This view positioned unfallen humanity as a "miniature" of God, endowed with limited divine attributes such as dominion power and moral agency, reflecting creation in God's "image and likeness" (Genesis 1:26-27).[4] Dake extended this to argue that humans, like God, possess tangible form, with the fall introducing sin into the soul and spirit but not altering the tripartite structure, which persists post-redemption for holistic sanctification.[4] Original sin originated solely with Adam's race, not pre-Adamites, preserving federal headship and accountability for contemporary humanity.[12] His annotations in God's Plan for Man detailed man as God's crowning work, designed for eternal rulership, with the body-soul-spirit division enabling progressive restoration through faith.[4]

Later Career and Legacy

Post-Imprisonment Ministry

Following his release from the Milwaukee House of Correction on July 18, 1937, after serving approximately five months for violating the Mann Act, Dake immediately resumed pastoral duties at Christian Assembly Church in Zion, Illinois.[16] That same evening, he conducted a church service attended by supporters, during which he performed a reported healing on a congregant suffering from a neck crick and exhorted the audience to "forget the past."[16] The Assemblies of God had revoked his credentials in March 1937 prior to his sentencing.[28] By 1939, Dake had relocated from St. Paul, Virginia—where he had been pastoring—and affiliated with the Church of God (Cleveland, Tennessee) denomination.[29] He attended his first Church of God General Assembly in 1940 and was appointed a bishop there.[2] In 1942, he pastored a Church of God congregation in Saxton, Virginia.[2] Dake received formal ordination from the Church of God in 1948 and launched a radio ministry that year.[28] From 1947 to 1948, Dake engaged in evangelistic travels, a pattern he maintained into the 1970s and 1980s alongside his writing and publishing efforts.[28] He remained credentialed with the Church of God for a significant portion of his later career, eventually retiring as an attendee of a Church of God congregation.[2] Despite the earlier conviction, Dake's post-imprisonment work sustained influence within Pentecostal circles, though it drew ongoing scrutiny for lacking public repentance.[16]

Death and Enduring Influence

Finis Jennings Dake died on July 7, 1987, at his home in Lawrenceville, Illinois, at the age of 84, from complications of Parkinson's disease.[7][4] Following his death, Dake's Annotated Reference Bible, first published in 1963 after decades of compilation, continued to circulate widely among Pentecostal and charismatic audiences, with ongoing sales reflecting sustained demand.[4] His annotations, encompassing over 35,000 entries, topical indexes, and literalist interpretations, have shaped study habits in conservative Pentecostal circles, where the volume serves as a primary reference for dispensational premillennialism and emphasis on spiritual gifts.[4][13] Dake's doctrinal framework, including assertions of God's anthropomorphic form and a multi-stage creation account derived from Genesis 1–2, persists in influencing independent Pentecostal ministries and Bible colleges, despite critiques from Trinitarian evangelicals who identify modalistic tendencies in his notes on the Godhead.[4] The Dictionary of Pentecostal and Charismatic Movements describes his impact on conservative Pentecostalism as profound and incalculable, attributing this to the Bible's comprehensive cross-references and rejection of allegorical exegesis in favor of plain-sense readings.[4] Posthumously, Dake Publishing, managed by family members, revised certain annotations to mitigate perceived excesses, yet the original edition retains a dedicated readership valuing its exhaustive detail over mainstream commentaries.[13] This enduring appeal stems from Dake's self-taught methodology—claiming over 100,000 hours of study—which prioritized scriptural self-sufficiency amid skepticism toward institutional theology.[4]

Reception and Criticisms

Dake's Annotated Reference Bible and other writings have exerted significant influence within Pentecostal and charismatic communities, where they are valued for providing extensive literal interpretations, topical indexes, and detailed scriptural cross-references that aid in-depth study.[4] Figures in the Word of Faith movement, such as Kenneth Copeland, have drawn from Dake's emphasis on faith, prosperity, and divine healing, contributing to its popularity, with approximately 30,000 copies sold in 1992 alone.[5] Supporters, including Dake's family, defend the work as offering a unique, Scripture-centered theological perspective unencumbered by denominational biases.[5] Critics from evangelical and broader Pentecostal circles, however, have lambasted Dake's doctrines as heretical and dangerous, arguing they distort core Christian tenets through hyperliteralism and anthropomorphic excesses.[4] The Christian Research Institute highlights Dake's portrayal of God as possessing a "spirit body with bodily parts like man," including estimates of divine dimensions (e.g., six feet tall, 190 pounds), as a rejection of God's immaterial essence in favor of materialistic interpretations of anthropomorphisms.[4][5] Similarly, his Trinitarian views—depicting three separate beings each with body, soul, and spirit, or even nine persons in the Godhead—have been deemed unorthodox and cultic by scholars like Michael Horton and Gleason Archer.[4][5] Further theological controversies include Dake's denial of Jesus' physical resurrection, claiming instead a non-physical "spiritual body," and his inconsistent salvation requirements, which enumerate varying conditions (e.g., 7 or 23 steps) blending grace with works, thus undermining sola fide.[4] His notes on healing and prosperity equate sickness with sin and promise material blessings through faith, influencing prosperity gospel teachings but criticized for lacking biblical warrant.[4] Bizarre annotations, such as Jonah dying inside the whale or angels governing planets, exemplify what detractors call speculative eisegesis.[5] Dake's annotations on racial matters, particularly in Acts 17:26 listing 30 reasons for segregation, drew condemnation for promoting racial separation, prompting Dake Publishing to issue apologies in the 1990s amid charges of racism from African-American leaders and others.[5] The Assemblies of God has distanced itself from his works, reflecting broader institutional wariness despite grassroots appeal.[5] Overall, while Dake's literalist approach resonates with those seeking exhaustive biblical detail, its reception underscores tensions between populist Pentecostalism and orthodox evangelicalism.[4]
