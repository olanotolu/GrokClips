Anything (film)

Anything is a 2017 American romantic drama film written and directed by Timothy McNeil.[1] The story centers on Early Landry (John Carroll Lynch), a middle-aged insurance agent from Mississippi who, devastated by his wife's suicide, relocates to Los Angeles to live with his sister and subsequently forms an unconventional romantic relationship with Freda (Matt Bomer), a transgender sex worker.[2][3] Premiering at the Los Angeles Film Festival in June 2017 before a limited theatrical release in May 2018, the film received mixed reviews, with critics praising its earnest exploration of grief and human connection but critiquing its contrived narrative and melodramatic elements.[3][1] The casting of openly gay cisgender actor Matt Bomer in the transgender role of Freda sparked significant controversy, with transgender activists arguing it perpetuated the erasure of trans performers from opportunities to portray their own experiences, though the director defended the choice as rooted in the character's emotional authenticity and the actor's commitment.[4][5][6]

Production

Development and writing

The screenplay for Anything was adapted by Timothy McNeil from his own one-act play of the same name, which he originally wrote and starred in during a 2007 production at the Elephant Theatre Company in Los Angeles.[7] McNeil, marking his feature directorial debut, expanded the stage material into a full-length script centered on themes of grief and unconventional romance, retaining core elements like the Mississippi widower protagonist relocating to Hollywood.[3] Early iterations of the play featured co-performances with actors including Mark Ruffalo, who briefly attached as a producer before departing the project, influencing McNeil's decision to helm the adaptation independently.[8] The writing process emphasized intimate, dialogue-driven character interactions reflective of the play's theatrical roots, with McNeil prioritizing emotional authenticity over expansive plot mechanics, as noted in post-production reflections on transitioning the material to screen.[9] No extensive rewrites or external script consultations were publicly documented, underscoring McNeil's singular authorship throughout development.[1]

Casting decisions and early controversies

The lead role of Early Landry, a transgender sex worker, was offered to Matt Bomer early in development without open auditions, as the actor became attached to the project during script revisions by director Timothy McNeil.[4] McNeil, who adapted his own play for the screen, prioritized Bomer's prior involvement and perceived fit for the character's emotional depth over broader casting calls.[4] Supporting roles included trans actresses Roxy Wood and Gia Ryan in smaller parts, alongside cisgender actors like John Carroll Lynch as the suicidal uncle Bill. The September 2016 announcement of Bomerâ€”a cisgender gay manâ€”in the trans lead role sparked immediate backlash from transgender activists and LGBTQ+ commentators, who argued it perpetuated the exclusion of trans performers from authentic representation.[10] Critics, including voices on platforms like Out magazine, highlighted patterns seen in prior films such as Dallas Buyers Club (2013) and The Danish Girl (2015), where cis actors like Jared Leto and Eddie Redmayne won acclaim for trans roles amid similar objections. Producer Mark Ruffalo, who executive produced via his company, acknowledged the concerns on social media, stating he understood the push for trans casting but believed the film's intent was sincere after consultations with trans advisors.[10] McNeil defended the choice in 2018 interviews, emphasizing collaboration with trans consultants to refine Bomer's portrayal for authenticity, though he maintained that artistic merit, not identity politics, guided the decision.[4] The controversy reignited with the film's June 2017 Los Angeles Film Festival premiere and a first clip release, drawing renewed criticism for Bomer's visible presentation as a trans woman via wig, makeup, and mannerisms, which some deemed caricatured or insensitive.[3] No formal boycotts materialized, but the debate contributed to industry-wide scrutiny, influencing later high-profile cases like Scarlett Johansson's withdrawn role in Rub & Tug (2018).[11]

Filming and technical aspects

Principal photography for Anything occurred in California.[12] Cinematography was handled by James Laxton, whose visual style emphasized intimate framing to highlight character interactions and emotional nuance, drawing from the film's stage play origins.[3][13] Editing duties were shared by Geraud Brisson and Andy Keir, focusing on maintaining a naturalistic pace that preserved the dialogue-driven rhythm of the source material.[3] The production utilized color film stock, resulting in a 94-minute runtime.[14]

Plot

Early Landry, a middle-aged insurance agent from Mississippi, becomes deeply despondent after his wife's death and attempts suicide by stepping in front of a truck, but survives.[2] Relocating to Los Angeles to stay with his sister Laurette and her husband Jack, he seeks a fresh start amid his grief.[15] Craving independence, Early moves into a rundown apartment building in Hollywood and places a personal ad seeking companionship from "anything human."[3] This leads him to meet Freda Von Rhenburg, a charismatic transgender woman working as a prostitute, who responds to his advertisement.[13] Despite their stark differencesâ€”Early's conservative Southern background contrasting Freda's bold urban existenceâ€”the pair form an unlikely bond, beginning with casual conversations that deepen into emotional intimacy and mutual support.[15] As Early grapples with isolation and suicidal ideation, Freda shares glimpses of her own hardships, including family estrangement and the challenges of her profession. Their relationship evolves into a romance, challenging Early's worldview and prompting Laurette's concern over its unconventional nature.[16] The narrative explores their shared vulnerability, with scenes depicting late-night talks, small acts of kindness, and confrontations with external judgments from neighbors and family.[17]

Cast and characters

John Carroll Lynch stars as Early Landry, a middle-aged insurance agent from Mississippi devastated by his wife's suicide, who relocates to Los Angeles in a state of profound grief and attempts to rebuild his life.[2][3] Matt Bomer plays Freda Von Rhenburg, Early's transgender neighbor and sex worker whose bold personality and vulnerability lead to an unexpected romantic connection with him, challenging both characters' isolation.[2][15] Maura Tierney portrays Laurette Sachman, Early's supportive yet pragmatic sister who provides him temporary housing upon his arrival in California and encourages his adjustment to urban life.[2] Supporting roles include Margot Bingham as Brianna, Laurette's assistant, and Michael Boatman as Charles, Laurette's husband, both of whom offer glimpses into the familial dynamics aiding Early's recovery.[2] Additional cast members feature Tanner Buchanan as Jack, Melora Hardin in a minor role, and Micah Hauptman as David, contributing to the ensemble of Early's new Los Angeles environment.[2]

Themes and analysis

Grief, suicide, and personal reinvention

Early Landry, portrayed by John Carroll Lynch, experiences profound grief following the sudden death of his wife of 26 years in a car accident, which leaves him emotionally vacant and disoriented in his Mississippi life as an insurance agent.[1][17] This loss manifests in his inability to engage with daily routines, culminating in a deliberate suicide attempt by stepping into oncoming traffic, an act that underscores the film's depiction of untreated bereavement as a pathway to self-destruction rather than mere melancholy.[2][19] Director Timothy McNeil, drawing from personal insights into suicide loss, presents Early's despair without romanticization, emphasizing the raw isolation that follows spousal bereavement in a traditional rural setting.[20] After surviving the attempt and facing institutionalization, Early relocates to Los Angeles under his sister Laurette's care, marking an initial rupture from his past and a tentative step toward reinvention amid urban anonymity.[3][21] In a dilapidated Hollywood apartment building, he encounters Freda, a transgender sex worker played by Matt Bomer, whose own experiences of marginalization mirror Early's loneliness, fostering a bond built on mutual vulnerability rather than physical attraction alone.[1] This relationship evolves into a catalyst for Early's personal transformation, as he confronts societal norms around love and identity, ultimately finding purpose in defying expectations of conventional mourningâ€”shifting from suicidal resignation to an acceptance of unconventional companionship as a form of renewal.[19] McNeil's narrative posits reinvention not as erasure of grief but as its integration into a redefined existence, though critics note the film's idealized resolution risks oversimplifying the causal links between loss, mental health crises, and relational healing.[22][23] The film's treatment of suicide aligns with empirical patterns of widower bereavement, where sudden spousal death via accident correlates with elevated risks of suicidal ideation due to disrupted attachment and lack of preparatory coping, yet it avoids clinical exposition in favor of character-driven causality.[2] Early's arc illustrates reinvention through geographic and relational displacement, echoing real-world observations that environmental change can interrupt grief's inertia, though the story's reliance on an improbable romance invites scrutiny for prioritizing narrative closure over sustained psychological realism.[1][3]

Transgender representation and interpersonal dynamics

In the film, the primary transgender character is Freda Von Rhenburg, depicted as a Los Angeles-based sex worker who engages clients through flirtatious, direct interactions that blend professional detachment with personal vulnerability. Freda, who transitioned from living as a man named Jack, presents as confident and sexually assured, often initiating conversations with bold propositions and physical proximity, as seen in her initial encounter with the protagonist where she offers companionship for hire.[1] This portrayal emphasizes her agency in navigating urban survival via sex work, with interpersonal exchanges revealing glimpses of her past, including a mention of prior male-presenting relationships, though the narrative subordinates these details to immediate relational needs.[3] The core interpersonal dynamic centers on the evolving romance between Freda and Early Landry, a cisgender heterosexual widower in his 60s who relocates to Hollywood following his wife's death and a suicide attempt. Their relationship begins transactionallyâ€”Early responds to Freda's online ad seeking a clientâ€”but shifts toward mutual emotional support, with dialogues exploring shared themes of loss and reinvention, such as Early's grief mirroring Freda's implied regrets over past life choices.[1] Physical intimacy escalates gradually, framed not as a taboo but as a natural progression driven by loneliness, with Early expressing acceptance of Freda's transgender status early on, stating it does not alter his attraction rooted in her personality and empathy.[21] Critics have described this bond as contrived, arguing it glosses over realistic barriers like societal stigma or mismatched expectations in cross-gender pairings, yet the film posits compatibility through complementary needs: Early's stability offering Freda security, and her vivacity countering his despair.[24] Freda's interactions with secondary characters, such as neighbors and clients, highlight dynamics of superficial urban tolerance versus underlying objectification, where she maintains a performative femininity to facilitate transactions but reveals authenticity in private moments with Early.[23] The representation avoids centering transgender identity as a source of perpetual victimhood, instead integrating it into broader human frailties like economic precarity and relational improvisation, though some analyses fault this for stereotypical mannerisms that reduce Freda to a "swishy" archetype rather than a fully dimensional figure.[19] Overall, the film's approach to these dynamics prioritizes individual agency and cross-personal chemistry over identity-based conflict, portraying attraction as emergent from circumstantial proximity and emotional reciprocity rather than ideological alignment.[25]

Critiques of urban vs. rural cultural contrasts

The film juxtaposes the protagonist Early Landry's origins in small-town Mississippi, where he worked as an insurance salesman in a conventional, strait-laced environment, against the chaotic and diverse urban milieu of Los Angeles following his relocation after his wife's death.[26] This shift underscores a cultural chasm, with Early initially experiencing bewilderment amid Hollywood's derelict apartments, quirky neighbors, and encounters with sex work and transgender identities that clash with his rural norms.[1] Reviewers have highlighted how Early's Southern gentility and grief-stricken isolation amplify this "fish out of water" dynamic, as he navigates a gritty urban landscape marked by off-screen violence and eccentricity, contrasting the implied stability of his prior life.[19][1] Critiques of the film's handling of these contrasts often center on its perceived contrivance in resolving cultural tensions through romance, portraying urban LA as a redemptive space for personal reinvention despite Early's evident unease and the protagonist's confusion over his love interest's transgender status stemming from his "middle-aged country bumpkin" background.[1] Variety noted that the narrative reduces the urban transgender character to a "colorful plot device" facilitating the rural man's emotional recovery, potentially glossing over deeper incompatibilities between conservative small-town values and city libertinism.[1] Conversely, some analyses commend the depiction for subverting stereotypes, presenting a deep-South figure who forms genuine connections without descending into bigotry or caricature, thus critiquing media tendencies to vilify rural conservatism uniformly.[22] Further scrutiny arises from familial dynamics, where Early's urban-dwelling sister, a studio executive, exhibits transphobia toward his partnerâ€”challenging the assumption of inherent urban progressivism and highlighting intra-urban cultural rifts akin to rural ones.[19] This element suggests the film implicitly critiques oversimplified binaries, showing prejudice as personal rather than geographically determined, though detractors argue it strains plausibility by having the rural widower adapt swiftly to urban outliers while urban kin resist.[24] Overall, the contrasts serve thematic ends of loneliness and acceptance but invite rebuke for implausibility, with the rural-to-urban arc seen as sentimental rather than causally grounded in empirical cultural divergences.[1][24]

Release

Festival premiere and theatrical distribution

Anything had its world premiere at the Los Angeles Film Festival on June 17, 2017, at the ArcLight Cinema in Santa Monica, California.[3][27] At the festival, the film received a Special Mention for Acting awarded to John Carroll Lynch for his portrayal of Bill.[28] The premiere screening highlighted the film's intimate drama, drawing attention from industry outlets despite its independent production scale.[13] Following its festival debut, Anything secured a limited theatrical release in the United States on May 11, 2018, distributed by Paladin.[29][15] The rollout targeted select markets, reflecting the film's modest budget and niche appeal as an indie drama focused on unconventional romance and personal loss.[2] Additional screenings occurred at events like FilmOut San Diego on June 10, 2018, expanding visibility within LGBTQ+-oriented festivals.[27] No wide international theatrical distribution was reported, with emphasis instead on domestic limited engagement and subsequent home media transitions.[30]

Home media and digital availability

"Anything" has not received a commercial physical home media release on DVD or Blu-ray formats. The film became available for digital video on demand (VOD) rental and purchase following its limited theatrical run in May 2018.[31] Platforms offering rental typically charge $3.99 for a 48-hour viewing period, while outright digital purchases range from $9.99 to $14.99, depending on the retailer and any promotional pricing.[32][33] As of October 2025, "Anything" is accessible for streaming via subscription on Starz Apple TV Channel.[33] It remains available for rent or purchase on Amazon Video, Apple TV, and Fandango at Home, with no confirmed availability on major free ad-supported platforms or other subscription services like Netflix or Hulu.[34][35] Digital distribution rights are managed through standard VOD licensing, reflecting the film's independent production and limited mainstream appeal.

Reception

Critical reviews

Anything received mixed reviews from critics, earning a 65% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 23 reviews, with the consensus noting John Carroll Lynch's standout performance as carrying the film despite its contemplative tone.[15] On Metacritic, it scored 60 out of 100 from 10 critics, indicating generally favorable but divided opinions on its handling of grief and romance.[36] Critics frequently praised Lynch's portrayal of Early Landry, a widower navigating loss and reinvention in Los Angeles, describing it as quiet and authentic, with The Hollywood Reporter highlighting the film's small-scale drama on love and kindness among isolated characters.[3] Dallas Film Now echoed this, calling Lynch's work naturalistic and the overall focus effective, though conceding it fell short of greatness.[37] Maura Tierney's supporting role as Early's sister also drew commendations for adding emotional depth to familial dynamics.[38] However, Matt Bomer's performance as the transgender sex worker Freda Von Rhenburg faced scrutiny, with IndieWire deeming the film offensive in its melodramatic approach, arguing the character felt overly refined and implausible despite Bomer's efforts.[19] The New York Times critiqued the narrative's bafflement at urban life, suggesting it prioritized emotional beats over deeper exploration of cultural clashes.[26] Some reviewers, like those aggregated on Metacritic, appreciated its lessons on transgender unfamiliarity but noted risks in viewer buy-in to its gentle sentimentality.[36] Mainstream outlets' emphasis on representational issues often reflected broader institutional preferences for authenticity in casting, potentially overshadowing artistic choices grounded in narrative suitability.[19]

Audience reactions

On Rotten Tomatoes, the film holds an audience score of 70% based on over 50 ratings, indicating generally favorable reception among viewers who rated it, with some praising its romantic elements as "one of the loveliest" they had seen in years.[15] In contrast, IMDb users rated it 6.1 out of 10 from 879 votes, reflecting a more mixed response typical of its limited theatrical release on May 11, 2018, following a premiere at Outfest on July 23, 2017.[2] Letterboxd logs show an average of 3.2 out of 5 stars from 291 users, aligning with moderate appreciation for its intimate scale but highlighting polarization.[30] Audience members frequently commended the performances of John Carroll Lynch as the grieving widower Bill and Matt Bomer as the transgender sex worker Early, describing the film as "sweet and tough, tender and honest" for its emotional authenticity and avoidance of clichÃ©s in depicting unconventional romance.[39] Reviewers noted the leads' chemistry evoked laughter and tears, with one stating it felt "surprisingly intimate and emotional."[39] However, criticisms centered on the screenplay's perceived sentimentality and abrupt resolution, with some viewers questioning the plausibility of the central relationship's rapid development.[39] The casting of cisgender actor Matt Bomer in the transgender role drew backlash from portions of the audience, who viewed it as inauthentic or emblematic of broader representational issues in Hollywood, contributing to lower scores amid debates on artistic choice versus lived experience.[39] Politically charged reactions emerged, including dismissals of the narrative as "progressive-left wing ridiculousness," reflecting discomfort with its themes of suicide, reinvention, and cross-cultural romance between rural Mississippi and urban Los Angeles.[39] Despite these divides, a subset of viewers appreciated its unflinching exploration of vulnerability, with one calling it a "great romantic drama" worth recommending for its heartfelt intent.[39] Overall, reactions underscore the film's niche appeal to those valuing character-driven indie dramas over mainstream polish, with limited box office Awards recognition

Anything garnered recognition primarily at LGBTQ-focused and independent film festivals following its premiere. At the 2017 Los Angeles Film Festival, the film received a Special Mention for Acting awarded to John Carroll Lynch for his portrayal of Early Landry.[40][41] The film achieved greater success at the 2018 FilmOut San Diego LGBTQ Film Festival, where it won three awards: Best Narrative Feature, Best Director for Timothy McNeil, and Best Actor for John Carroll Lynch.[42][43] These accolades highlighted the film's handling of themes related to transgender identity and personal loss within an indie drama context. No major industry awards, such as Academy Awards or Golden Globes, were nominated or won by the production.

Controversies

Casting of cisgender actor in transgender role

The film Anything (2018), directed by Timothy McNeil in his feature debut, cast cisgender actor Matt Bomer in the role of Frida, a transgender woman and sex worker who enters a romantic relationship with the protagonist, Early Landry, played by John Carroll Lynch.[13] Bomer, known for roles in Magic Mike and The Normal Heart, portrayed Frida post-transition, requiring him to perform in drag and adopt mannerisms associated with the character.[10] The casting decision, announced on August 30, 2016, drew immediate criticism from transgender activists and advocacy groups, who argued it perpetuated the exclusion of trans performers from roles depicting their lived experiences and reinforced stereotypes of trans women as men in disguise.[44] Transgender actress and activist Laverne Cox, in a guest column for The Hollywood Reporter, highlighted the disparity, noting that trans women are rarely cast in cisgender female leads due to industry biases, while cisgender men frequently secure trans female roles, limiting opportunities for trans talent. Similar objections echoed prior controversies, such as the casting of cisgender actors Jared Leto in Dallas Buyers Club (2013) and Eddie Redmayne in The Danish Girl (2015), where awards recognition amplified calls for authenticity over performative transformation.[45] Critics contended that such choices prioritize star power and box-office appealâ€”Bomer's involvement helped secure financingâ€”over representational fidelity, potentially marginalizing trans actors who face higher unemployment rates in Hollywood, estimated at over 70% for trans performers seeking regular work.[9] Defenders, including co-star Mark Ruffalo, emphasized artistic merit over identity matching, arguing on September 1, 2016, that roles should be awarded based on acting skill rather than personal biography, and that trans actors could similarly play cisgender parts without restriction.[10] Director McNeil, in a May 2018 Entertainment Weekly interview following the film's limited release, maintained that Bomer was selected after extensive auditions for his emotional depth in conveying vulnerability, not despite his cisgender status, and rejected accusations of insensitivity by noting consultations with trans consultants during production.[4] McNeil further asserted that mandating demographic congruence for every role could stifle creative freedom, a view aligned with longstanding acting traditions where performers embody diverse identities through craft alone.[46] The debate underscored broader tensions in representation: while empirical data from casting reports indicate cisgender actors dominate trans roles (e.g., over 90% in major films from 2010-2020 per industry analyses), proponents of the practice cite successful precedents like Hilary Swank's Oscar-winning portrayal of a trans man in Boys Don't Cry (1999), arguing that talent transcends identity without empirical evidence of harm to trans visibility.[47] Detractors, however, pointed to Anything's poor receptionâ€”holding a 0% on Rotten Tomatoes from 14 reviewsâ€”as partly attributable to perceived inauthenticity, with outlets like IndieWire labeling Bomer's performance effortful but the narrative "offensive" for reducing trans experiences to melodramatic tropes.[19] Sources critiquing the casting often stem from advocacy-aligned media, which may amplify activist voices amid systemic underrepresentation, whereas defenses appear in trade publications focused on production logistics.

Broader debates on artistic freedom vs. representational authenticity

The casting of cisgender actor Matt Bomer in the role of transgender sex worker Early Gray in Anything elicited criticisms from segments of the LGBTQ community, who argued that such decisions perpetuate the erasure of transgender performers by prioritizing conventionally attractive cisgender actors over those with lived experience of the portrayed identity.[48] This backlash framed the choice as emblematic of broader industry patterns, where cisgender actors have historically dominated transgender roles, as seen in films like Dallas Buyers Club (2013), potentially reinforcing stereotypes through performances lacking authentic embodiment.[47] Proponents of representational authenticity contended that transgender characters demand actors who can convey the specific social, psychological, and physical realities of trans experiences, citing risks of misrepresentation that could harm public perceptions or exacerbate marginalization. Advocates, including some activists quoted in LGBTQ media, emphasized that authentic casting fosters genuine visibility and counters the underrepresentation of trans actors, who comprise less than 1% of speaking roles in major films despite higher rates of employment barriers in the industry.[49] Executive producer Mark Ruffalo acknowledged these concerns on Twitter in September 2016, stating, "I hear you. We are listening. We want to get this right," signaling an intent to engage with feedback while defending the production's collaborative process with trans consultants.[50] In response, defenders of artistic freedom asserted that restricting roles to actors matching the character's identity undermines the craft of acting, which fundamentally involves empathetic transformation across differences in age, gender, ethnicity, and backgroundâ€”a principle upheld throughout theatrical history without mandated biographical congruence. Director Timothy McNeil justified Bomer's selection based on the actor's ability to capture the character's emotional depth, noting preparations including consultations with transgender individuals to inform the portrayal, arguing that talent and directorial vision should supersede identity quotas to avoid constraining creative output.[1] Critics of authenticity mandates, including some industry commentators, warned that such requirements could devolve into performative tokenism, empirically limiting opportunities in a competitive field where trans actors, while deserving more roles, must still audition against peers on merit to sustain career viability rather than identity alone.[3] The Anything debate thus illuminated tensions between these paradigms, with representational advocates prioritizing equity in hiring to address systemic disparitiesâ€”evidenced by SAG-AFTRA data showing transgender actors in under 0.5% of union-covered roles from 2010â€“2020â€”while freedom advocates invoked first-principles of performance art, positing that coerced casting erodes narrative integrity and innovation, as superior acting elevates representation more effectively than demographic matching.[49] Sources critiquing the authenticity push, often from independent film outlets, highlighted potential biases in activist-driven narratives that overlook how cisgender performances, when rigorously prepared, have garnered awards and shifted cultural dialogues, as with Jared Leto's Oscar-winning role in Dallas Buyers Club. Ultimately, the film's release without recasting underscored persistent industry resistance to prescriptive rules, fueling ongoing discourse on whether authenticity enhances truthfulness or imposes ideological barriers to storytelling.[47]
