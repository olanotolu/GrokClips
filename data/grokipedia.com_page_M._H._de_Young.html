M. H. de Young

Michael Henry de Young (September 30, 1849 – February 15, 1925) was an American journalist, newspaper publisher, and civic promoter best known for co-founding the San Francisco Chronicle and spearheading the 1894 California Midwinter International Exposition, which led to the creation of the museum now named in his honor.[1][2] Born in St. Louis, Missouri, de Young moved to San Francisco as a child and, at age 16, partnered with his brother Charles to launch the Daily Dramatic Chronicle in 1865 using a borrowed $20 gold piece to print a simple theater program sheet that evolved into a full-fledged daily newspaper under his leadership.[3][4] Over decades, he transformed the Chronicle into a powerful voice in California politics and society, serving as a director of the Associated Press and influencing public opinion through investigative reporting and editorial stances.[5] As a philanthropist, de Young loaned $75,000 worth of artwork to furnish the Fine Arts Building at the Midwinter Exposition in Golden Gate Park, and his promotional efforts for the event—modeled after the Chicago World's Fair—resulted in the structure's repurposing as a public museum; it was renamed the M. H. de Young Memorial Museum in 1921 to recognize his foundational contributions.[6][7][8] His career also involved notable rivalries with other publishers, including a fatal 1880 shooting of his brother Charles by a competitor's editor, after which Michael assumed full control of the paper.[9]

Early life

Birth and family background

Michael Henry de Young was born on September 30, 1849, in St. Louis, Missouri.[1][10] He was the son of Amelia (née Morange; 1809–1881) and Miechel (or Michel Henri) de Young (c. 1790–1854), a jeweler and dry goods merchant whose family had Jewish roots tracing to immigrants from the Netherlands and France.[11][12] The de Young family's early history remains somewhat obscure, with Michael later asserting his father was a French-origin banker from Baltimore, though primary records indicate Dutch-Jewish mercantile origins.[13] His older brother, Charles de Young (1846–1880), born in Natchitoches, Louisiana, shared the entrepreneurial path, and the siblings, along with brother Gustavus, later co-founded the Daily Dramatic Chronicle in San Francisco after the family's relocation westward around 1854.[10] The family's Jewish heritage influenced their early mercantile pursuits amid mid-19th-century American frontier migrations.[11]

Move to San Francisco and early influences

De Young was born on September 30, 1849, in St. Louis, Missouri, to Jewish parents of Dutch descent, Amelia Morange and Miechel de Young, the latter a dry goods merchant and jeweler whose background involved immigration from the Netherlands.[10][11][13] In approximately 1854, during the ongoing California Gold Rush migration, his family relocated to San Francisco when he was about five years old, settling amid the city's rapid growth as a commercial hub.[5] Miechel de Young died shortly after the move, leaving the family to navigate economic challenges in the burgeoning Western metropolis.[13] Upon arrival, de Young received a limited formal education, attending San Francisco public schools for a brief period before transitioning to practical training.[5] He later graduated from Heald College, a local business institution focused on commercial skills such as bookkeeping and accounting, which equipped him with foundational knowledge in finance and operations relevant to his future ventures.[10] These early experiences in a frontier city marked by entrepreneurial opportunism and ethnic immigrant networks shaped his pragmatic outlook, emphasizing self-reliance and market-driven adaptation over extended academic pursuits. De Young's initial foray into journalism stemmed from familial and amateur influences, as he and his older brother Charles began as typesetters for an educational amateur newspaper, honing skills in printing and content production amid San Francisco's vibrant, competitive press environment.[5] The brothers' collaboration reflected the era's informal entry paths into media, influenced by the city's demand for local reporting on gold prospects, politics, and theater, rather than structured mentorship from established outlets.[14] This hands-on start, combined with exposure to his father's mercantile background, fostered de Young's emphasis on accessible, sensationalist content to capture readership in a diverse, transient population.[11]

Journalistic career

Founding the Daily Dramatic Chronicle

In 1865, brothers Charles de Young and Michael H. de Young, recent immigrants from Missouri, launched the Daily Dramatic Chronicle in San Francisco as a modest four-page publication focused primarily on theater listings, playbills, and entertainment advertisements.[3][5] The inaugural issue appeared on January 16, 1865, initially distributed for free in hotels and theaters as a handbill-style sheet to attract advertisers and readers interested in the city's burgeoning performing arts scene.[15][4] At the time, Charles, aged about 20, and Michael, aged 16, operated with limited resources, securing operations by borrowing or raising just $20 to rent basic printing equipment and desk space in a shared office.[16] Their venture capitalized on San Francisco's post-Gold Rush cultural vibrancy, positioning the paper as "the best advertising medium on the Pacific Coast" to draw business from theaters and promoters.[14] The brothers handled much of the production themselves, printing on a small hand press and expanding content beyond pure drama listings to include brief local news snippets, which laid the groundwork for broader journalistic ambitions.[5] The Daily Dramatic Chronicle's early success stemmed from its timely coverage of performances and its aggressive promotion, quickly gaining traction among hotel guests and theatergoers despite competition from established dailies like the Alta California.[3] By emphasizing sensational theater critiques and advertisements, the de Youngs built a circulation base that allowed gradual evolution from a niche entertainment rag to a more comprehensive newspaper, though it remained a penny paper reliant on volume sales and ad revenue in its founding phase.[15]

Expansion and rebranding as San Francisco Chronicle

On September 1, 1868, the de Young brothers transformed the Daily Dramatic Chronicle from a specialized four-page theater listings sheet into a full-fledged daily newspaper, dropping the "Dramatic" moniker and adopting the name Morning Chronicle to reflect its broadened scope beyond entertainment news to general reporting, editorials, and local affairs.[4] This rebranding marked a pivotal shift, with the inaugural expanded issue proclaiming the paper would be "bright, bold, fearless" and independent in politics, aiming to cover San Francisco's commercial, social, and political developments amid the city's post-Gold Rush boom.[4] The expansion capitalized on the brothers' early success in free distribution to hotels and theaters, which had built advertising revenue from $20 initial borrowing to sustainable operations by 1868, allowing investment in additional staff, telegraph services for timely news, and printed content that appealed to a wider readership of merchants, immigrants, and civic leaders.[16] Circulation surged as the paper transitioned to paid subscriptions, reaching thousands daily within months and establishing it as a competitor to established dailies like the Alta California, with aggressive coverage of scandals, boosterism for California growth, and sensationalism that differentiated it in the competitive market.[17] By the mid-1870s, under continued management by Charles and M. H. de Young, the San Francisco Chronicle—as it became commonly known—achieved the largest circulation of any newspaper on the Pacific Coast, exceeding 20,000 copies daily through innovations like illustrated supplements and relentless promotion of urban expansion, though this growth also invited rivalries and legal challenges from competitors accusing it of monopolistic tactics.[18] The rebranding solidified the paper's role as a chronicle of San Francisco's maturation, with M. H. de Young's emerging influence steering content toward pro-development editorials that aligned with his civic ambitions, setting the stage for its dominance into the 1880s following Charles's death in 1880.[3]

Editorial stance, business practices, and market dominance

Under M. H. de Young's leadership following his brother Charles's assassination in 1880, the San Francisco Chronicle maintained a strong Republican editorial stance, aligning with pro-business interests and civic boosterism to promote San Francisco's growth as a major metropolitan center.[19] The paper advocated for policies favoring urban development and economic expansion, such as endorsing the Spanish-American War in 1898 as a boon comparable to the Gold Rush, while opposing measures perceived as hindrances to progress, including certain municipal amendments during World War I.[20][21] This boosterist approach framed the city as the "Paris of the Pacific," emphasizing culture, refinement, and commercial vitality to attract investment and residents.[22] De Young's business practices emphasized aggressive expansion and competitive tactics to build readership and influence. Initially launched in 1865 as a free theater listings sheet distributed in hotels and streets, the Chronicle evolved into a full daily by leveraging sensational and confrontational reporting, as evidenced by its 1884 publication of articles accusing sugar magnate Adolph Spreckels of market manipulation, which prompted Spreckels to shoot de Young.[23][16] De Young focused on operational efficiency and acquisitions, purchasing rival the San Francisco Call in 1913 to consolidate market position, while investing in infrastructure like the 1890 Chronicle Building, San Francisco's first skyscraper.[23][24] These strategies prioritized circulation growth over restraint, reflecting a pragmatic, profit-driven model amid fierce rivalry with papers like William Randolph Hearst's Examiner. By the 1890s, the Chronicle achieved market dominance in San Francisco, securing the largest circulation of any newspaper west of the Mississippi River within its first decade and eclipsing local competitors through sustained expansion under de Young's direction.[24][17] This preeminence persisted into the early 20th century, with the paper serving as the city's primary voice for Republican business interests and shaping public opinion on key issues like graft prosecutions and urban infrastructure.[19] De Young's tenure until his death in 1925 solidified the Chronicle's role as the dominant daily, outlasting challenges from Democratic-leaning outlets and establishing a legacy of influence that endured until the family's sale to Hearst in 2000.[17]

Public roles and civic engagement

Political involvement and boosterism

De Young aligned closely with the Republican Party throughout his career, serving as a delegate to the Republican National Convention on three occasions and emerging as a leader of national prominence within the party.[3][25] The San Francisco Chronicle, under his ownership, consistently advanced a strong Republican editorial bias, shaping public opinion on issues ranging from local governance to national policy.[19] This influence extended to alliances with the conservative wing of San Francisco's Republican establishment, including figures like William H. Crocker and Herbert Fleishhacker, in opposition to progressive reformers.[26] De Young's political engagement often merged with boosterism for San Francisco, leveraging his platform to counter negative perceptions and advocate for economic and infrastructural advancement. As a prominent civic booster, he used the Chronicle to promote the city's resilience and opportunities, particularly in the wake of disasters and scandals that threatened its reputation.[27] His efforts paralleled broader campaigns by business and political elites to secure federal support for expositions and urban projects, framing San Francisco as a hub of progress under Republican-led governance.[28] This advocacy, while rooted in self-interest as a publisher reliant on local prosperity, contributed to the city's narrative of recovery and expansion in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.[29]

Leadership in expositions and urban development

M. H. de Young spearheaded the California Midwinter International Exposition of 1894, conceiving the idea in 1893 after attending the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, where he served as vice president of California's exhibit.[27][30] As director-general, he organized the event with private investors, securing federal endorsement and constructing temporary structures across 30 acres in Golden Gate Park, drawing over 2 million visitors from January 27 to July 23, 1894.[27][31] The exposition promoted California's agriculture, industry, and tourism, with permanent legacies including the Fine Arts Building—later expanded into the de Young Memorial Museum—and the Japanese Tea Garden, catalyzing Golden Gate Park's development into a major urban green space.[7][32] De Young also contributed to the Panama-Pacific International Exposition of 1915, joining civic leaders like Franklin G. Moore and William Bourn in advocating for San Francisco to host the event as a symbol of post-1906 earthquake recovery, securing congressional approval in 1910.[33] Through the San Francisco Chronicle, he promoted the fair, which spanned 636 acres and attracted nearly 19 million visitors from February 20 to December 4, 1915, showcasing rebuilt infrastructure and fostering urban renewal along the waterfront.[34] De Young's involvement extended to presenting exhibits, such as "Novagems" on Idaho Day, underscoring his role in leveraging expositions for economic and cultural advancement.[35] As a civic booster, de Young used his journalistic platform to advocate for San Francisco's urban expansion, including post-1906 earthquake rebuilding efforts; the Chronicle published daily editions from makeshift presses amid the ruins, emphasizing rapid reconstruction and attracting investment to restore the city's commercial core.[36] His expositions directly influenced land use, transforming underutilized areas like Golden Gate Park into enduring public assets, while his editorial campaigns supported infrastructure projects that enhanced the city's global profile and population growth from 344,000 in 1900 to over 416,000 by 1910.[7][27]

Philanthropic contributions

Midwinter Fair and cultural initiatives

In 1893, following his attendance at the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago as California's representative, M. H. de Young conceived the California Midwinter International Exposition to promote the state's resources and counteract the economic downturn from the Panic of 1893.[27] He announced the project on June 1, 1893, securing approval for 140 acres in Golden Gate Park despite initial resistance from park superintendent John McLaren.[27][37] As director-general, de Young oversaw groundbreaking on August 24, 1893, with the fair opening to the public on January 27, 1894, and closing on July 4, 1894.[27][38] The exposition featured over 100 buildings showcasing agricultural, mechanical, and fine arts exhibits, alongside international pavilions from nations including Japan and Egypt, drawing more than 2 million visitors at a 50-cent entry fee.[37][27] Architectural highlights included structures in Indian, Egyptian, and Moorish styles, a 270-foot electric tower with the world's largest searchlight at the time, and cultural attractions such as the Vienna Prater amusement area and a Turkish Village performance by Little Egypt.[27][37] These elements emphasized California's agricultural prowess—symbolized by exhibits like the Prune Knight—and aimed to attract settlers and investors by highlighting the region's mild winter climate and productivity.[37] De Young's initiative extended cultural promotion through permanent legacies like the Japanese Tea Garden, which originated as a fair exhibit and remains in Golden Gate Park, fostering ongoing public engagement with international aesthetics.[37] The event's success, generating profits amid national depression, underscored de Young's civic boosterism, influencing subsequent regional celebrations such as the 1894 Fiesta de Los Angeles.[27]

Establishment and support of the M. H. de Young Memorial Museum

The M. H. de Young Memorial Museum traces its origins to the Fine Arts Building erected for the 1894 California Midwinter International Exposition in Golden Gate Park, an event for which Michael H. de Young served as director general.[39] After the exposition concluded, de Young championed its repurposing as a permanent public institution, with the museum opening on March 25, 1895, constructed using $75,000 in fair profits.[40] The inaugural exhibits featured paintings, sculptures, decorative arts, global historical artifacts, and an assemblage of approximately 6,000 objects drawn from exposition remnants, de Young's personal holdings, and contributions by San Francisco families safeguarding Gold Rush relics.[40] The structure suffered extensive damage in the 1906 San Francisco earthquake but was repaired and reopened to the public in November 1907.[39] De Young sustained the museum's growth through direct financial contributions and relentless artifact acquisition; in a 1916 account, he noted personally securing over 300,000 items for display during his two decades of involvement.[41] His philanthropy extended to funding a major expansion, including a new wing whose construction commenced in 1917, followed by further additions in 1925 after the original building's condemnation in 1926.[40] In acknowledgment of de Young's foundational leadership and sustained benefaction, the institution was redesignated the M. H. de Young Memorial Museum, with a rebuilt core facility solidifying its role as a cornerstone of San Francisco's cultural landscape through the early 20th century.[42][7]

Personal life

Marriage and immediate family

Michael Henry de Young married Katherine Isabel Deane on an unspecified date in 1881.[43] [44] The couple resided in San Francisco and had five children: one son and four daughters.[14] Their son, Charles de Young (born 1881), served as general manager of the San Francisco Chronicle until his death on September 18, 1913, at age 32 from typhoid fever and related complications.[45] Katherine de Young died in 1917.[46] Among the daughters was Helen Margaret de Young (1883–1969), who married newspaper publisher George T. Cameron (1865–1918) and remained connected to the family's media enterprises.[47] The other daughters—Constance, Kathleen, and Phyllis—also married into prominent San Francisco families, with some descendants later involved in the Chronicle's operations.[48]

Residences and lifestyle

M. H. de Young primarily resided in San Francisco's Pacific Heights neighborhood, in a Victorian mansion on California Street that reflected his status as a leading newspaper publisher and civic leader.[49] His home at 1919 California Street was featured in an 1887 publication documenting elite residences.[50] This property, situated amid the city's affluent enclaves, served as the family seat during much of his adult life.[51] In 1911, de Young acquired adjacent lots south of his estate to commission a mansion for his daughter Constance and her husband Joseph E. Tobin, designed by architect Willis Polk in Tudor Gothic Revival style at 1969 California Street.[49] Intended as one-half of a symmetrical pair, the Tobin House—completed around 1915—highlighted de Young's influence in shaping Pacific Heights' architectural landscape, though the matching counterpart was never built due to family disputes.[52] His own mansion, located down the street, was maintained by his four daughters for family gatherings into the late 1930s following his death.[53] The structure was demolished in 1941.[51] De Young's lifestyle embodied the era's elite San Francisco society, centered on his California Street estate where social and familial events underscored his role as patriarch of a prominent family.[54] As head of the Chronicle, he balanced professional demands with civic boosterism, residing in opulent surroundings that hosted gatherings reflective of his wealth and connections, though specific daily routines remain sparsely documented beyond his public engagements.[55]

Controversies

Political feuds and the assassination of Charles de Young

The de Young brothers, proprietors of the San Francisco Chronicle, frequently engaged in acrimonious political disputes through the newspaper's editorials, which championed Republican interests and criticized populist and Democratic figures amid San Francisco's turbulent late-19th-century politics, including anti-Chinese agitation and labor unrest.[14][5] In the 1879 mayoral election, the Chronicle—under Charles de Young's editorial direction—launched personal attacks on Reverend Isaac S. Kalloch, a Baptist minister and candidate for the Workingmen's Party, exposing his prior identities (including J.I. B. Smith and I.S. Kalloch) and alleging bigamy, seduction, and other moral failings based on public records from his earlier life in Massachusetts and elsewhere.[9][56] Kalloch retaliated in speeches, denouncing Charles de Young as a "pimp" and libertine, escalating the feud into personal invective.[9][57] On August 23, 1879, Charles de Young drove to the Metropolitan Temple after Kalloch's sermon and fired at the departing candidate near the San Francisco Mint, wounding him in the lungs and neck; Kalloch survived after weeks of recovery and proceeded to win the mayoralty on a platform blending moral reform with Sand Lot oratory.[9][58] Despite Charles facing charges of assault with intent to murder, the dispute intensified when Kalloch's son, Isaac M. Kalloch, a 24-year-old lawyer, sought vengeance.[59] On April 14, 1880, at approximately 8:00 p.m., Isaac M. Kalloch entered the Chronicle's offices at 1326 Market Street, where Charles was working alone in the counting room; he shot Charles three times at close range—once in the chest and twice in the head—killing him instantly before surrendering to police.[9][60] M. H. de Young, who had been alerted by noise but arrived too late, continued publishing the paper uninterrupted, maintaining its critical stance against the Kalloch administration.[9][61] Isaac M. Kalloch's trial began in June 1880; he claimed the shooting as justifiable retaliation for his father's maiming, and after 40 days of testimony, the jury acquitted him on October 20, 1880, reflecting public sympathy for family honor amid the era's code of personal redress for public insults.[61][62] The assassination underscored the volatile intersection of journalism and politics in Gilded Age San Francisco, where editorial broadsides often provoked violence, though the Chronicle's exposés on Kalloch drew from verifiable court documents and Kalloch's own admissions of reinvention.[56][57]

Accusations of sensationalism and ethical lapses in journalism

The San Francisco Chronicle, originally founded by M. H. de Young and his brothers as the sensationalist Daily Dramatic Chronicle in 1865 with a focus on theater scandals and gossip, retained a reputation for lurid coverage even after de Young's assumption of full control following his brother Charles's assassination in 1880. Critics accused the paper of prioritizing circulation-boosting headlines over factual restraint, particularly in political exposés that blurred journalism with personal vendettas, such as the 1879 attacks on mayoral candidate Isaac Kalloch, where it detailed alleged moral indiscretions including bigamy and illicit affairs to undermine his campaign.[9] A notable ethical lapse occurred during the 1900–1904 bubonic plague outbreak in San Francisco, when the Chronicle under de Young's ownership systematically downplayed the epidemic's severity, publishing biased articles and editorials that dismissed federal health reports as a Democratic conspiracy against Republican business interests, with plague stories appearing only once on the front page despite confirmed cases. This coverage, aimed at averting economic quarantines that could harm the city's—and de Young's own—boosterism efforts, was later condemned for endangering public health by suppressing accurate information in favor of commercial optimism.[19] Further accusations arose from the paper's aggressive investigative tactics, exemplified by its 1883–1884 series alleging shareholder fraud at Claus Spreckels's sugar refinery, which prompted Spreckels to shoot de Young outside the Chronicle offices on February 20, 1884; while the allegations drew from public records, detractors argued the framing exaggerated unproven claims for dramatic effect, exemplifying a pattern of "crusading" journalism that prioritized confrontation over balanced verification.[14]

Family and business disputes

In the decades following M. H. de Young's death in 1925, his descendants faced protracted internal conflicts over the management and ownership of Chronicle Publishing Company, the media conglomerate he co-founded with his brothers in 1865. These disputes often centered on strategic decisions, political ideologies, and the eventual sale of family assets, exacerbating generational rifts among roughly three dozen heirs.[63][64] A prominent feud erupted in 1995 when Nan Tucker McEvoy, de Young's granddaughter and former board chair of the San Francisco Chronicle from 1981 to 1995, opposed her cousins' push to diversify and potentially sell parts of the business, including television stations and magazines. McEvoy, known for her liberal views, accused relatives of conservative leanings and mismanagement, leading to her ouster from the board amid accusations of favoritism toward certain family branches. The conflict, rooted in differing visions for the company's future, culminated in the 2000 sale of the Chronicle and KRON-TV to Hearst Corporation for approximately $660 million, ending direct family control.[65][63][64] Post-sale litigation further strained family ties, particularly over tax strategies employed by heirs. In a 2014 Ninth Circuit ruling, the court disallowed a $318 million deduction claimed by a subgroup of heirs, including Consuelo Martin (de Young's granddaughter) and her five children, who had structured the transaction through complex trusts and partnerships to minimize capital gains taxes on the Chronicle's sale. The IRS challenged the setup as lacking economic substance, imposing additional liabilities estimated in the tens of millions and highlighting ongoing divisions in how the family interpreted de Young's original business legacy.[66][67]

Death and legacy

Final years and death

In the years leading up to his death, M. H. de Young continued to direct the San Francisco Chronicle as its proprietor, maintaining his long-standing influence over the publication amid ongoing civic and cultural engagements in the city.[68] De Young died on February 15, 1925, at age 75 in San Francisco, following surgery that afternoon for intestinal complications.[29][1] A funeral mass was conducted at St. Mary's Cathedral, consistent with his conversion to Catholicism after his marriage.[10] He was interred at Holy Cross Catholic Cemetery in Colma, San Mateo County, California.[1]

Long-term impact on San Francisco and journalism

De Young's most enduring contribution to San Francisco lies in the establishment of the M. H. de Young Memorial Museum, which originated from his advocacy to repurpose the Fine Arts Building of the 1894 California Midwinter International Exposition as a permanent cultural institution. Opened on March 23, 1895, in Golden Gate Park, the museum initially featured exhibits from the exposition and grew through de Young's subsequent expansions, including a major addition in the early 20th century that formed the basis of its structure until a 2005 rebuild. Today, as part of the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco, it houses over 27,000 objects spanning American art, textiles, African and Oceanic artifacts, arts of the Americas, and international contemporary works, serving as a key venue for exhibitions that attract approximately 1 million visitors annually and bolster the city's identity as a global arts hub.[42][69][70] The museum's long-term significance extends to enhancing San Francisco's tourism economy and cultural prestige; its location in Golden Gate Park integrates it with the city's natural and recreational assets, promoting public engagement with art through programs that have educated generations since 1895. De Young's vision positioned the institution as a memorial not only to his family but to broader civic aspirations, influencing subsequent developments like the 1915 Panama-Pacific International Exposition's art legacies and modern collaborations, such as the 1978 donation of John D. Rockefeller's American art collection, which solidified its role in preserving national heritage amid urban growth.[7][70] In journalism, de Young's transformation of the Daily Dramatic Chronicle—founded with his brother Charles in 1865 as a theater-focused sheet—into the San Francisco Chronicle, the West's leading daily by the late 19th century, established a model of aggressive, opinion-shaping reporting that dominated local discourse during pivotal events like the Civil War era and post-earthquake reconstruction. The paper's partisan Republican stance and focus on business interests amplified elite viewpoints, often prioritizing circulation through sensational tactics over strict factual rigor, as evidenced by its early reputation for investigative scoops intertwined with alleged blackmail practices that boosted popularity but eroded trust in objective news.[71][14][19] The Chronicle's longevity under de Young's direction until 1925 laid groundwork for its evolution into a multimedia enterprise, influencing Bay Area journalism by setting precedents for daily opinion leadership and infrastructure innovation, such as the 1890 construction of San Francisco's first skyscraper as its headquarters. While later ownership shifts diluted family control, the paper's foundational emphasis on local power dynamics persists in shaping political narratives, underscoring de Young's role in embedding journalism as a tool for civic influence rather than detached observation.[5][3]

Descendants and continuation of family enterprises

M. H. de Young and his wife Katherine A. de Young had five children, including a son, Charles de Young (1881–1913), and four daughters: Helen Mary de Young Cameron (1883–1969), Constance Marie de Young Tobin (1885–1968), and Kathleen Yvonne de Young (1887–1970).[1] The son's early death in 1913 left no direct male heir to assume leadership of the family enterprises, prompting de Young to groom his daughters and their descendants for involvement in the Chronicle Publishing Company, which controlled The San Francisco Chronicle and later diversified into broadcasting assets like KRON-TV.[17] Following de Young's death in 1925, the publishing business remained under family stewardship, with management passing to relatives through the female lines; de Young had structured the company to prioritize family continuity despite the absence of surviving sons.[17] Granddaughters and other heirs, including Nan Tucker McEvoy (1919–2015)—daughter of Helen de Young Cameron—played key roles, with McEvoy serving as board chair of Chronicle Publishing from 1981 to 1995 and advocating for editorial independence amid internal family debates over modernization.[72] The enterprise expanded in the mid-20th century to include television and other media, generating substantial wealth for descendants, though operational control gradually shifted toward professional executives while retaining family oversight.[17] Family control persisted until 1999, when Chronicle Publishing's shareholders—primarily de Young descendants—sold The San Francisco Chronicle to Hearst Corporation for approximately $660 million, marking the end of direct family operation of the core newspaper enterprise; other assets, such as KRON-TV, were divested separately to independent buyers.[73] Post-sale, heirs pursued individual ventures, with McEvoy notably establishing an olive oil ranch and philanthropy initiatives funded by her inheritance, though no unified family enterprise supplanted the publishing legacy.[48] Legal disputes among heirs, including a 2014 federal ruling invalidating a $318 million tax deduction claimed after the sale, highlighted tensions over asset distribution but did not revive collective business activities.[67]
