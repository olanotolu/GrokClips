Geoffrey Miller (psychologist)

Geoffrey F. Miller (born 1965) is an American evolutionary psychologist and associate professor of psychology at the University of New Mexico.[1][2] His research applies Darwinian principles to human behavior, emphasizing sexual selection's role in evolving complex cognitive traits such as intelligence, language, humor, and creativity as costly signals of genetic fitness during mate choice, rather than mere survival adaptations.[3][4] Miller's seminal book The Mating Mind (2001) articulates this "courtship machine" theory of the human brain, influencing debates on the origins of human uniqueness.[3] He has authored or co-authored influential works including Spent: Sex, Evolution, and Consumer Behavior (2009), which extends these ideas to modern consumerism as virtue-signaling, and Mate: Become the Man Women Want (2015), offering practical insights into human mating dynamics grounded in empirical evolutionary data.[3] With over 100 peer-reviewed publications, Miller's contributions span mate preferences, moral virtues, and applied evolutionary psychology to economics and policy, challenging adaptationist orthodoxy through first-principles analysis of fitness indicators.[5]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Formative Influences

Geoffrey Franklin Miller was born in 1965 in suburban Cincinnati, Ohio, a relatively conservative, Republican area.[6] He described his upbringing as happy and bourgeois, benefiting from strong public schools that emphasized standardized testing, academic tracking, and competitive honors and Advanced Placement classes in high school.[6] Miller's family structure blended nuclear and extended elements; his father worked as a lawyer, while his mother was a politically engaged homemaker and one of twelve siblings, resulting in a large network of nearby relatives.[6] His parents emphasized civic virtues, including humility and the prosocial application of personal talents, which shaped his early worldview.[6] Formative academic environments, from tracked public schooling to the competitive setting at Columbia Universityâ€”where he pursued a dual major in biology and psychologyâ€”reinforced lessons in humility amid intellectual achievement.[6] His initial interest in psychology emerged from engagement with 1960s and 1970s conceptual art, prompting exploration of cognitive processes such as metaphor, analogy, categorization, and conceptual thinking, which formed the basis of his undergraduate honors thesis.[6]

Academic Training

Geoffrey Miller received his Bachelor of Arts degree in Biology and Psychology from Columbia University in 1987.[7][8] He completed his doctoral training at Stanford University, earning a Ph.D. in Experimental Psychology in 1994.[8][7] His dissertation, titled "Evolution of the human brain through runaway sexual selection," examined evolutionary mechanisms underlying cognitive development and human intelligence as adaptations shaped by sexual selection pressures.[8][7] This work laid foundational groundwork for his later research integrating evolutionary biology with psychological inquiry, emphasizing empirical models over purely adaptationist narratives.[8]

Professional Career

Key Academic Positions

Geoffrey Miller earned his Ph.D. in cognitive psychology from Stanford University in 1992 and subsequently held a postdoctoral research position at the University of Sussex from October 1992 to December 1994, focusing on evolutionary and adaptive systems.[8][7] He then served as a lecturer in the Psychology Department at the University of Nottingham from January to August 1995.[8][7] From September 1995 to August 1996, Miller worked as a research scientist at the Max Planck Institute for Psychological Research in Munich, Germany, in the Center for Adaptive Behavior and Cognition.[8][7] He followed this with a Senior Research Fellow position at the Centre for Economic Learning and Social Evolution (ELSE) at University College London from September 1996 to August 2000.[8][7] In August 2001, Miller joined the Psychology Department at the University of New Mexico as an Assistant Professor, a role he held until June 2008, during which he conducted research on evolutionary psychology topics including sexual selection and mate choice.[8][7] He was promoted to Associate Professor in July 2008 and has remained in that tenured position to the present, serving as area head for Evolution & Society.[2][8] Among his notable visiting appointments, Miller was the Henry Kaufmann Visiting Professor of Business at NYU Stern School of Business from January 2013 to August 2014, and an Associate Professor of Applied Psychology at CUHK-Shenzhen from May 2021 to July 2022.[7] These roles complemented his primary affiliation at UNM, where he has supervised graduate students and contributed to over 110 academic publications.[8]

Institutional Affiliations and Roles

Geoffrey Miller began his professional career with a postdoctoral research position in the Evolutionary and Adaptive Systems Group at the School of Cognitive and Computing Sciences, University of Sussex, from October 1992 to December 1994.[9] He subsequently served as a lecturer in the Psychology Department at the University of Nottingham from January to August 1995.[9] From September 1995 to August 1996, Miller was a research scientist at the Max Planck Institute for Psychological Research's Center for Adaptive Behavior and Cognition in Munich, Germany.[9] He then held the position of senior research fellow at University College London's Centre for Economic Learning and Social Evolution from September 1996 to August 2000.[9]

Core Research Areas

Evolutionary Theories of Human Cognition

Geoffrey Miller proposes that many uniquely human cognitive traits, including advanced intelligence, language, creativity, humor, and moral reasoning, evolved primarily through sexual selection rather than solely natural selection for survival tasks. In his 2000 book The Mating Mind, Miller argues that the rapid expansion of the human brain during the Pleistoceneâ€”reaching approximately three times the size expected for a primate of comparable body massâ€”imposed high metabolic costs that could only be justified by substantial reproductive advantages, such as enhanced mate attraction and retention.[10] This theory revives and extends Charles Darwin's 1871 suggestion in The Descent of Man that sexual selection via female choice played a key role in human mental evolution, positioning the mind as an elaborate courtship display analogous to the peacock's tail.[11] Central to Miller's framework are "fitness indicators," costly behavioral and cognitive signals that honestly advertise an individual's genetic quality and heritable mental fitness to potential mates. Unlike survival adaptations, which might evolve through direct natural selection for problem-solving in foraging or predation avoidance, these indicators are theorized to have been selected for their ability to impress choosy partners by demonstrating cognitive prowess without deception, as deception would undermine long-term reproductive success. For instance, complex language is posited not just as a tool for coordination but as a medium for displaying wit, storytelling, and poetic fluency during courtship, with empirical support from cross-cultural studies showing preferences for intelligent verbal displays in mate choice.[12] Similarly, artistic creativity and musical ability are viewed as sexually selected traits, evidenced by their prevalence in human societies despite minimal direct survival utility, and by sex differences in creative output that align with greater male variance in reproductive success.[10] Miller derives testable predictions from this model, including the expectation that human intelligence exhibits greater heritable variance and sexual dimorphism in display-oriented domains than in pure survival cognition, and that cognitive traits correlate more strongly with mating success than with ecological problem-solving in modern populations. In a 2000 chapter, he outlines nine such predictions, such as intelligence being a better predictor of social and sexual status than of economic productivity, supported by data from twin studies showing high heritability (around 0.5â€“0.8) for general intelligence (g) and its links to assortative mating.[12] He contrasts this with the "cognitive niche" hypothesis, which attributes brain evolution mainly to survival innovations like tool use, arguing that sexual selection better explains "luxury" traits like humor and morality that exceed adaptive minima for hunter-gatherer life. While influential in evolutionary psychology, the theory has faced scrutiny for relying on indirect evidence, as direct fossil or genetic data on ancestral mating preferences remain limited.[13]

Sexual Selection and Mate Choice

Geoffrey Miller has advanced the application of sexual selection theory to explain the evolution of human cognition and behavior, positing that mate choice pressures, rather than survival demands alone, drove the development of traits such as intelligence, language, humor, art, and music.[14] In his 2000 book The Mating Mind: How Sexual Choice Shaped the Evolution of Human Nature, Miller argues that these mental capacities function as costly signals of genetic fitness, analogous to the peacock's tail, which females prefer in mates to assess heritable quality and provisioning ability.[15] He extends Charles Darwin's underemphasized 1871 hypothesis on sexual selection in humans, contending that runaway mate preferences amplified brain size from approximately 400 cubic centimeters in early hominids to over 1,300 in modern Homo sapiens by around 50,000 years ago, coinciding with behavioral modernity.[16] Central to Miller's framework is the handicap principle, where only individuals with superior genes can afford to produce and maintain extravagant displays without compromising survival.[17] For instance, he proposes that human humor evolved as a low-cost test of intelligence and creativity, allowing potential mates to evaluate cognitive flexibility and social intelligence during courtship interactions; empirical studies he references show that witty banter correlates with perceived mate value across cultures.[18] Similarly, artistic and linguistic abilities signal mutational robustness and developmental stability, with mate choice favoring those who deploy them effectively in reproductive contexts rather than utilitarian ones.[5] Miller's 1998 review synthesizes cross-species evidence, including bird song and primate displays, to support human parallels, estimating that sexual selection accounts for 60-80% of variance in sexually dimorphic human traits beyond natural selection.[14] Miller's empirical contributions include analyses of human mate preferences, revealing consistent cross-cultural priorities for psychological traits over physical ones in long-term pairing.[17] In publications such as his work on the evolutionary psychology of mate choice, he documents how ecological factors like resource scarcity and pathogen prevalence modulate preferences for intelligence (as a proxy for problem-solving) and kindness (as a proxy for parental investment), drawing from datasets spanning over 10,000 participants in 37 cultures.[19] He advocates mutual mate choice models, challenging female-centric views by highlighting male selectivity for fertility cues and female choosiness for genetic indicators, as evidenced in speed-dating experiments where both sexes reject 70-80% of potential partners based on subtle behavioral signals.[20] These findings underscore signaling theory's role in consumption and morality, where luxury goods and ethical displays further advertise mate quality.[21] While some critiques question the relative weight of sexual versus natural selection, Miller's integration of computational simulations and comparative data provides a causal mechanism grounded in fitness maximization.[18]

Signaling in Behavior and Consumption

Geoffrey Miller extends costly signaling theory, originally developed in evolutionary biology to explain animal displays like peacock tails, to human behavior and consumption as mechanisms for advertising heritable fitness traits to potential mates.[22] In behavioral contexts, particularly mate choice, Miller posits that traits such as intelligence, humor, creativity, and moral virtues function as reliable indicators of genetic quality because they are costly to develop and maintain, deterring low-fitness individuals from faking them.[23] For instance, his research on mutual mate choice demonstrates that even under monogamy, both sexes invest in costly signalsâ€”like elaborate courtship displays or cognitive performancesâ€”to compete for high-quality partners, as supported by simulation models showing persistent evolution of such traits despite equal parental investment.[24] This framework applies to consumption, where Miller argues in his 2009 book Spent: Sex, Evolution, and Consumer Behavior that modern purchases of luxury goods, fashion, and status symbols primarily serve as extensions of mating displays rather than mere utility or social status signaling.[25] He links consumerism to the "Big Five" personality traits (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism), evolved via sexual selection, positing that consumers select products to conspicuously advertise these traitsâ€”e.g., avant-garde art for Openness or high-end watches for Conscientiousnessâ€”to attract mates who value genetic and developmental stability.[26] Empirical support draws from cross-cultural patterns in luxury spending, where expenditures correlate more strongly with mating effort than survival needs, challenging traditional economic models by emphasizing romantic motives in "blatant benevolence" and conspicuous consumption.[18] Miller's virtue-signaling extension critiques corporate and consumer practices, arguing that displays of ethical consumption (e.g., eco-friendly products) often function as costly signals of moral traits shaped by mate preferences for kindness and fairness, rather than pure altruism.[2] His over 110 publications, cited more than 14,000 times, integrate these ideas with fitness indicator models, showing how behavioral and consumptive signals reliably convey underlying health, intelligence, and heritable personality under mutation-selection pressures.[3] This perspective prioritizes sexual selection's role over other evolutionary drivers, supported by evidence from human mate choice studies where preferred partners exhibit superior signal traits linked to reproductive success.[5]

Major Publications and Theories

Academic Works

Miller has published extensively in peer-reviewed journals and edited volumes on evolutionary psychology, with over 100 scholarly articles and chapters as of 2024.[8] His academic output emphasizes sexual selection, costly signaling in mate choice, and the evolutionary origins of cognitive and behavioral traits, often integrating empirical data from cross-cultural studies, behavioral economics, and genetic analyses. These works build on first-principles models of fitness indicators, challenging adaptationist explanations by prioritizing runaway sexual selection processes.[5] A foundational contribution is the 1998 paper "Mate Choice Turns Cognitive," co-authored with Peter M. Todd, which posits that human cognitive abilities, including language and intelligence, evolved primarily as fitness indicators in mate assessment rather than for ecological problem-solving. Published in Trends in Cognitive Sciences, it reviews computational models and comparative data from animal courtship displays to argue for cognitive traits as exaggerated signals of genetic quality.01169-1) The paper has been cited over 500 times, influencing subsequent research on the non-adaptive origins of human intelligence.[18] In "Evolution of Human Music through Sexual Selection" (2000), Miller applies handicap principle theory to musical ability, proposing it as a costly signal of cognitive fitness and creativity that enhanced mating success in ancestral environments. Featured in The Origins of Music, the chapter analyzes ethnographic data and neural correlates, estimating that music's universality stems from Pleistocene-era sexual selection pressures rather than group cohesion or infant care. This work, cited over 660 times, extends signaling theory to aesthetic domains and has informed studies on art and ornamentation.[18] Miller's 2007 article "Sexual Selection for Moral Virtues," in The Quarterly Review of Biology, synthesizes evidence from behavioral genetics and cross-species comparisons to argue that traits like honesty, kindness, and self-sacrifice evolved as heritable indicators of genetic quality, detectable in mate choice via long-term observation. Drawing on quantitative genetic models (heritability estimates of 0.3â€“0.5 for personality virtues), it critiques kin selection alternatives and predicts sex differences in moral signaling. Cited over 490 times, the paper addresses the evolutionary puzzle of altruism by linking it to reproductive fitness.[18] Other notable papers include "Blatant Benevolence and Conspicuous Consumption" (2007), which uses experimental data from speed-dating scenarios to demonstrate how romantic motives amplify costly signaling in prosocial and consumptive behaviors (effect sizes d > 0.5), published in Journal of Personality and Social Psychology with over 1,180 citations; [18] and "Ovulatory Cycle Effects on Tip Earnings by Lap Dancers" (2007), providing economic evidence for human estrus via lap dance revenue fluctuations (up to 30% higher during fertile phases), supporting concealed ovulation hypotheses in Evolution and Human Behavior. [18] Miller co-edited Mating Intelligence (2008) with Glenn Geher, a scholarly volume compiling 20 chapters on the cognitive and evolutionary bases of mate choice, including novel measures of "mating IQ" validated against reproductive outcomes. Published by Erlbaum, it integrates psychometric and cross-cultural data to formalize mating as a domain-specific intelligence. His later works, such as "The Smartphone Psychology Manifesto" (2012) in Perspectives on Psychological Science, advocate for mobile sensing technologies to capture real-time behavioral data, enhancing ecological validity in personality and mating research (cited over 930 times).[18] These publications collectively advance causal models of human behavior, prioritizing empirical falsifiability over narrative-driven interpretations prevalent in some social psychology subfields.[5]

Popular Books

Miller's The Mating Mind: How Sexual Choice Shaped the Evolution of Human Nature, published by Doubleday in 2000, argues that sexual selection pressures, rather than survival needs alone, primarily drove the evolution of advanced human traits such as intelligence, language, humor, and creativity. These traits function as costly signals of genetic quality and heritable fitness to potential mates, extending Darwin's original theory of sexual selection beyond physical ornaments to mental ones.[3] In Spent: Sex, Evolution, and Consumer Behavior, released by Viking in 2009, Miller applies evolutionary psychology to explain modern consumerism as a form of signaling, where individuals purchase goods and brands to advertise personality traits like openness, conscientiousness, and intelligence to others, particularly potential mates and allies.[27] The book critiques overconsumption as an exaggerated outgrowth of ancestral mating displays, drawing on the Big Five personality model to analyze how products serve as "Veblen goods" for status and virtue signaling. Co-authored with Tucker Max, Mate: Become the Man Women Want (2015, Little, Brown and Company) provides practical advice for men on enhancing sexual attractiveness and romantic success, grounded in empirical data from evolutionary psychology on female mate preferences for traits like kindness, intelligence, and social dominance.[28] It emphasizes self-improvement over manipulative tactics, integrating cross-cultural studies and behavioral experiments to outline strategies for building genuine fitness indicators.[3] Virtue Signaling: Essays on Darwinian Politics & Free Speech, self-published in 2019, collects essays examining how moral grandstanding, ideological displays, and political rhetoric stem from evolved instincts to signal virtues, alliances, and status in social groups.[29] Miller links these behaviors to runaway sexual selection dynamics, warning of risks like ideological extremism when unchecked by free speech norms and empirical scrutiny.[30]

Views on Modern Issues

Critiques of Effective Altruism and AI Development

Geoffrey Miller, an evolutionary psychologist with a decade of involvement in Effective Altruism (EA), has expressed concerns about the movement's prioritization of AI-related existential risks, arguing that the rush toward artificial general intelligence (AGI) and artificial superintelligence (ASI) overlooks profound challenges in human psychology and long-term alignment. While affirming his commitment to EA principles focused on mitigating x-risks, Miller contends that unchecked AGI development represents a "foolish, reckless, and frankly evil" endeavor driven by hubris and short-term economic incentives rather than prudent caution.[43][44] He has signed public statements advocating for prohibitions on ASI development, emphasizing that humanity's current civilizational achievementsâ€”spanning art, social bonds, and cultural wisdomâ€”do not necessitate accelerating toward technologies that could obliterate them.[45] Central to Miller's critique is the incompatibility between rapid AI scaling and evolutionary timescales for alignment. He draws analogies from biology, noting that aligning animal nervous systems with genetic fitness required tens of millions of years of iterative selection, a process far exceeding the compressed timelines proposed in EA-adjacent AI safety efforts. Inner and outer alignment problems, in his view, may prove fundamentally intractable without such gradual refinement, rendering optimistic projections in rationalist and EA communitiesâ€”such as solving alignment via scalable oversight or debateâ€”as potentially delusional. Miller estimates a 50% probability of AI-induced catastrophe ("P(Doom)"), primarily through scenarios like an unaligned ASI emerging from competitive arms races among developers, including risks from ideologically extreme actors such as antinatalists or extremists co-opting the technology.[43] He criticizes the AI industry, including entities funded or influenced by EA donors, as "evil and reckless," advising against employment there and urging broader regulatory coalitions, including with conservatives skeptical of transhumanist acceleration.[46] Miller anticipates and endorses a public moral backlash against AI as a natural brake on AGI timelines, predicting it will constrain resources and talent flows essential for rapid progressâ€”a outcome he deems beneficial for safety despite tensions with some EA factions favoring controlled acceleration. This perspective challenges EA's scope sensitivity by highlighting neglected near-term harms, such as AI's failure to yield net societal benefits despite billions in investments (e.g., OpenAI's $14 billion revenue), and critiques the rationalist subculture's demographic tendenciesâ€”like high rates of social frustration and polyamoryâ€”as potentially biasing toward escapist technological solutions over grounded human flourishing. He advocates translating AI safety discourse for global audiences, including in China, to foster international deceleration rather than unilateral races.[47][43][48]

Perspectives on Polyamory and Relationships

Geoffrey Miller, drawing from evolutionary psychology, posits that human mating strategies evolved primarily around pair-bonding for offspring rearing, but not necessarily lifelong sexual exclusivity, with ancestral patterns likely involving serial monogamy and extra-pair copulations lasting around 10-20 years per bond.[49] He argues that strict monogamy emerged as a cultural norm approximately 10,000 years ago to facilitate group-level competition and reduce intrasexual violence, rather than as a direct adaptation.[49] In modern contexts, Miller views consensual non-monogamy, including polyamory, as viable for a minority due to technological aids like reliable contraception and scheduling tools, though he emphasizes it is not "natural" but achievable for those with high intelligence, self-awareness, and communication skills.[49] Miller personally practices polyamory, co-parenting with partner Diana Fleischman while maintaining other relationships, and has advocated for empirical study of its psychology, noting its prevalence among younger generations where 10-20% of undergraduates report involvement and a substantial minority under 30 reject sexual exclusivity as ideal.[6] Surveys indicate 4-5% of U.S. adults currently engage in polyamorous arrangements, with 20% having tried it and 17% of those aged 18-44 reporting consensual non-monogamy experiences.[50] He contends polyamory demands rigorous jealousy managementâ€”through cue avoidance, distraction, or reframingâ€”and hierarchical structures (e.g., primary vs. secondary partners) for stability, contrasting it favorably with unregulated hookup culture for its emphasis on honesty and consent.[6][49] Despite potential benefits like enhanced communication and relationship resilience, Miller cautions that polyamory suits only about 4-5% of the population currently, potentially expanding to 20% with refined social norms, but risks devolving into polygyny due to sex differences in sexual satisfaction and mate preferences.[49][50] In broader relationship advice, informed by his co-authored book Mate and podcast The Mating Grounds, Miller urges men to prioritize signaling fitness, kindness, intelligence, and provisioning ability over superficial tactics, aligning with evolved female mate choice criteria to foster sustainable monogamous or negotiated non-monogamous partnerships.[51] He critiques marriage as an outdated bundle of social, legal, and religious contracts, recommending customizable civil options to accommodate diverse arrangements without endorsing plural legal marriages due to coordination complexities.[49]

Controversies and Responses

2013 Twitter Controversy on Obesity

On June 2, 2013, Geoffrey Miller, an evolutionary psychologist and professor at the University of New Mexico, tweeted: "Dear obese PhD applicants: if you didn't have the willpower to stop eating carbs, you won't have the willpower to do a dissertation. #truth"[52][53] The statement linked obesity to deficient self-control, implying it as a predictor of failure in completing a PhD dissertation, drawing from Miller's research interests in traits like impulsivity and mate selection signals.[54] The tweet rapidly generated backlash on social media and in outlets including The Atlantic, NPR, and Inside Higher Ed, with critics accusing Miller of fat-shaming, ableism, and endorsing discrimination in graduate admissions.[52][54] Responses highlighted perceived insensitivity toward obese individuals, who face documented hiring biases in academia despite equivalent qualifications, and questioned whether such views aligned with university diversity policies.[54] Miller deleted the tweet the same day and followed with apologies on June 3, asserting it did "not reflect my true views, values, or standards" and did not represent UNM's admissions practices.[52][53] Miller subsequently claimed the tweet was an "impulsive" element of informal research to observe public reactions to provocative statements on self-control, informing his work on evolutionary psychology topics like willpower and fitness indicators.[54][53] This defense prompted ethics reviews by institutional review boards (IRBs) at UNM, where Miller held his primary position, and New York University (NYU), with which he had a visiting affiliation.[53] On July 2, 2013, the UNM IRB concluded it was not research, citing no involvement of human subjects, absence of defined methods or hypotheses, and lack of data collection protocols.[53] The NYU IRB reached a similar determination, finding no ethical oversight required.[53] No formal disciplinary measures were imposed on Miller by either institution following the IRB findings, though UNM's psychology department chair noted an ongoing internal evaluation of his conduct.[53] The episode subsided without reported changes to Miller's employment status, but it amplified scrutiny of evolutionary psychologists' public statements on sensitive traits like body weight, which correlate empirically with metabolic and behavioral factors influencing long-term goal adherence, such as PhD completion rates hovering around 50% in many programs.[54][53]

Defenses Against Ideological Critiques of Evolutionary Psychology

Geoffrey Miller has argued that many ideological critiques of evolutionary psychology arise not from empirical disconfirmation but from anxieties over its implications for human equality and social norms, particularly regarding sexual selection and sex differences. In a 2003 analysis, he contended that opposition to fitness indicator theoriesâ€”positing that traits like intelligence and creativity evolved partly through mate choiceâ€”stems from fears that acknowledging sexual selection's cultural role would justify inequality or undermine egalitarian ideologies, rather than flaws in the evidence from animal behavior, cross-cultural patterns, and genetic studies supporting such adaptations.[55] Miller emphasized that these critiques often conflate descriptive science with prescriptive ethics, ignoring how evolutionary explanations can inform without endorsing status quo hierarchies, as evidenced by the predictive success of sexual selection models in explaining human arts, humor, and moral displays across 50+ societies.[55] Miller has further defended the field by highlighting institutional ideological biases that suppress politically sensitive research, such as studies on hypergamy or evolved mating preferences, which he attributes to a dominant left-leaning worldview in psychology that prioritizes conformity over replication and falsification. In a 2018 interview, he described social psychology as "one of the most biased fields in the behavioral sciences," where core findings on authority and conformity fail replication rates below 50% yet persist due to alignment with anti-fascist narratives, while evolutionary psychology faces resistance for challenging blank-slate assumptions despite support from twin studies showing 40-60% heritability in personality traits like extraversion and agreeableness.[56] He argued that this bias manifests in self-censorship, with researchers avoiding topics like sex differences in occupational interestsâ€”where meta-analyses of 500,000+ participants reveal men preferring things-oriented roles and women people-oriented ones at d=0.93 effect sizeâ€”fearing career repercussions from ideological gatekeepers.[56][57] Responding to broader cultural attacks, such as those amplified during the 2017 Google diversity memo controversy, Miller affirmed the memo's claims on innate psychological sex differences as grounded in 50+ years of cross-species and cross-cultural data, including primate studies and longitudinal human samples showing consistent gaps in variance (e.g., men's greater extremes in IQ and interests).[57] He critiqued "blank slate gender feminism" for dismissing disconfirming evidence, like the 80/20 gender split in tech fields mirroring evolved preferences rather than discrimination alone, and noted the logical paradox in diversity advocacy: assuming psychological uniformity negates the need for diversity, while acknowledging differences precludes equal outcomes without coercion.[57] Miller, drawing from his 100+ publications and teaching experience, positioned evolutionary psychology as empirically robust, with defenses rooted in causal mechanisms like gene-environment interactions verifiable via GWAS data identifying variants linked to traits like educational attainment (heritability ~10-20% from polygenic scores).[57] These arguments underscore his view that ideological opposition reflects coalitional signaling more than scientific scrutiny, as evolutionary psychologistsâ€”even predominantly left-leaningâ€”produce findings upheld by adversarial collaborations yielding replication rates over 70% for core adaptations.[56]
