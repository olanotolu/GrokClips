Geoffrey Berman

Geoffrey S. Berman is an American attorney who served as United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York from January 2018 until June 2020.[1][2] Appointed as a Republican by President Donald Trump on an interim basis and later confirmed by the Senate, Berman led an office of over 230 prosecutors handling federal criminal and civil matters in the nation's financial capital, including investigations into terrorism, public corruption, and white-collar crime.[1][3] Prior to this role, Berman had served as an Assistant United States Attorney in the same district from 1990 to 1994, where he prosecuted fraud cases, and as associate counsel in the Office of Independent Counsel for the Iran-Contra affair, successfully securing the conviction of a former CIA employee, Thomas G. Clines, for tax fraudâ€”the only such prison sentence from that probe.[3][1] His tenure drew attention for overseeing prosecutions of figures connected to Trump, such as Michael Cohen, as well as other significant cases, and concluded with his resignation following a public dispute with Attorney General William Barr over leadership transition and case priorities, after which he emphasized the office's commitment to independence.[2][3] Since leaving government service, Berman has practiced as a partner at Fried, Frank, Harris, Shriver & Jacobson LLP, where he chairs the global litigation department and heads the white-collar defense and investigations practice; he also authored the 2022 memoir Holding the Line, recounting his time at the helm of the Southern District.[3]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Geoffrey Steven Berman was born in Trenton, New Jersey, to Ronald Berman, a real estate developer and attorney, and Marie Edelman Berman.[4][5][6] Ronald Berman founded R. Berman Development Corporation, which developed over 10 million square feet of properties, and served as a founding partner in the Trenton law firm Warren, Goldberg & Berman.[7][8] The family maintained ties to Trenton, where Berman grew up amid his father's local business activities in real estate and legal practice.[9][10]

Academic and early professional influences

Berman earned a Bachelor of Arts in political science and a Bachelor of Science in economics from the University of Pennsylvania in 1981, graduating magna cum laude from the Wharton School.[11] He then attended Stanford Law School, receiving his Juris Doctor in 1984, where he served as Note Editor of the Stanford Law Review, indicating significant engagement with scholarly legal analysis during his studies.[12] [3] Following law school, Berman clerked for Judge Leonard I. Garth on the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, an experience that provided foundational exposure to federal appellate practice and judicial decision-making in complex cases.[13] Subsequently, as an associate at Davis Polk & Wardwell, he received mentorship from Robert Fiske, Jr., a prominent partner and former United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York, whose guidance emphasized rigorous litigation and white-collar defense strategies.[13] This period honed Berman's skills in high-stakes legal work prior to his entry into public service. From 1987 to 1990, Berman served as an associate counsel in the Office of Independent Counsel Lawrence E. Walsh during the Iran-Contra investigation, where he successfully prosecuted a former Central Intelligence Agency employee for tax fraud related to the affair.[1] [10] This role immersed him in probing executive branch actions and international arms dealings, fostering expertise in politically sensitive federal prosecutions and the mechanics of independent counsel inquiries.[14] These early positions under seasoned figures like Garth, Fiske, and Walsh shaped Berman's approach to federal criminal law, emphasizing independence, thorough investigation, and courtroom precision in subsequent career phases.[15]

Pre-U.S. Attorney legal career

Service as Assistant United States Attorney (1990â€“1994)

Geoffrey S. Berman served as an Assistant United States Attorney in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York from 1990 to 1994, focusing on complex criminal prosecutions.[1][3] In this role, he handled cases involving tax fraud, computer hacking, and organized crime, contributing to the office's efforts against sophisticated white-collar and technological offenses during a period when federal prosecutions of emerging cyber threats were nascent.[3][13][10] A key achievement was Berman's prosecution of the Masters of Deception, a New York-based hacking group that infiltrated telephone networks, including New York City systems, to commit fraud and disrupt services in the early 1990s.[15][16] He built rapport with group members to secure their cooperation and testimony, simplifying technical evidence for juries, which led to multiple guilty pleas and a one-year prison sentence for the leaderâ€”among the first significant federal penalties in a computer hacking case.[10][17][15] This effort, described as cutting-edge by contemporaries, established Berman's reputation for tenacity in innovative investigations.[18] He also pursued members of a prominent New York organized crime family on fraud-related charges, bolstering the district's aggressive stance against racketeering intertwined with financial schemes.[10][3]

Private practice and firm roles (1994â€“2018)

Following his tenure as an Assistant United States Attorney, Berman entered private practice in 1994 by joining Latham & Watkins, where he worked under Michael Chertoff and contributed to the court-ordered monitoring of the Bank of Credit and Commerce International (BCCI) scandal.[10][18] He advanced to partner at the firm, focusing on litigation matters informed by his prosecutorial experience.[13] Berman later joined his father's real estate development firm and established his own practice before transitioning to Greenberg Traurig in 2006.[13] At Greenberg Traurig, he specialized in white-collar defense, commercial litigation, and criminal and regulatory investigations, representing clients in high-stakes matters.[10][15] Within Greenberg Traurig, Berman served as a principal shareholder in the New York and New Jersey offices and co-managing shareholder of the New Jersey office, roles that underscored his leadership in the firm's regional operations until his departure in 2018.[1][18] His practice emphasized defending individuals and entities in complex regulatory enforcement actions, drawing on over two decades of legal expertise.[12]

Key prosecutions and investigations

During Berman's tenure, the Southern District of New York (SDNY) office under his leadership brought charges against financier Jeffrey Epstein for sex trafficking of minors and conspiracy to commit sex trafficking of minors on July 8, 2019.[26] The indictment alleged that Epstein, from 2002 to 2005, enticed and recruited dozens of minor girls, some as young as 14, to engage in sexual acts at his properties in Manhattan and Palm Beach, Florida, paying victims hundreds of dollars per encounter and additional sums to recruit others.[26] Epstein was arrested on July 6, 2019, and died by suicide in federal custody on August 10, 2019, while awaiting trial; Interactions with Department of Justice leadership

Berman's interactions with Department of Justice (DOJ) leadership were marked by escalating tensions, particularly with Attorney General William Barr, over prosecutorial independence in politically sensitive cases. In his 2022 memoir Holding the Line, Berman recounted repeated directives from Barr and senior DOJ officials to initiate investigations targeting perceived political adversaries of President Trump, including former Secretary of State John Kerry for alleged violations related to the Iran nuclear deal, as well as former FBI Director James Comey and Deputy Director Andrew McCabe.[35] [36] Berman described these requests as lacking evidentiary support and driven by partisan motives, which his office resisted by declining to open formal probes without probable cause.[37] Additional friction arose in cases involving international sanctions enforcement, such as the prosecution of Turkey's Halkbank for evading U.S. sanctions on Iran, where Berman alleged Barr sought to intervene to secure a favorable resolution amid diplomatic pressures from the Turkish government.[38] Berman maintained that such influences compromised the DOJ's commitment to impartial justice, a stance he attributed to broader efforts by Washington leadership to align SDNY actions with administration priorities.[39] Barr's office, in response, denied any improper political interference, asserting that oversight of U.S. Attorneys is standard and that Berman's decisions were subject to review by the Attorney General.[40] These disputes peaked in mid-June 2020. On June 18, Barr met Berman for approximately 45 minutes at the Pierre Hotel in New York City, pressing him to resign voluntarily and accept a role as head of the DOJ's Civil Division or a position focused on civil rights enforcement.[41] [42] Berman refused, citing his Senate confirmation and unwillingness to abandon ongoing investigations.[43] The following evening, June 19, Barr issued a DOJ press release announcing Berman's resignation effective immediately, with Acting Deputy Attorney General Jeffrey Rosen named as interim replacement; Berman promptly rebutted this in a public statement, declaring he had not resigned and would not leave until a Senate-confirmed successor assumed the role.[44] On June 20, 2020, President Trump announced Berman's removal via Twitter, prompting Berman to step down that day after confirming Rosen's readiness to serve.[45] [46] In subsequent House Judiciary Committee testimony on July 9, 2020, Berman characterized Barr's overtures as enticements to sideline him amid policy disagreements, including over case dispositions and resource allocation.[47] Barr later dismissed Berman's accounts as exaggerated, claiming the removal stemmed from performance issues rather than conflicts, though Berman's detailed recollections in Holding the Lineâ€”corroborated by contemporaneous communicationsâ€”highlighted a pattern of attempted subordination of SDNY autonomy to Main Justice directives.[48][49]

Removal from office

On June 19, 2020, Attorney General William Barr released a statement announcing that Geoffrey S. Berman had "stepped down" as United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York, effective immediately, and would be replaced on an interim basis by Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Jay Clayton, pending Senate confirmation. In the same announcement, Barr praised Berman's tenure but indicated the Department of Justice sought new leadership with expertise in financial regulation.[50] Berman promptly refuted the claim in a public statement released that evening via the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York, asserting, "I have not resigned, and have no intention of resigning," and noting he had learned of the purported resignation only through Barr's press release.[44] He affirmed he would continue overseeing ongoing investigations and prosecutions until a presidentially appointed successor was confirmed by the Senate, emphasizing the office's independence.[51] This public refusal triggered a standoff, as U.S. attorneys appointed with Senate confirmation serve four-year terms and removals mid-tenure are atypical without cause, though the position is subject to executive authority under 28 U.S.C. Â§ 541.[29] The following day, June 20, 2020, Barr responded with a letter to Berman expressing disappointment in the refusal and stating he had requested President Donald Trump to remove Berman effective immediately, which Trump did.[52] Trump confirmed the action in a public statement, later tweeting support for the decision.[53] Berman then vacated the office, with Deputy U.S. Attorney Audrey Strauss assuming the acting role and announcing no planned changes to ongoing cases.[45] Barr cited Berman's reluctance to pursue certain non-criminal matters as a factor but maintained the removal aligned with departmental priorities.[52]

Notable cases and their implications

High-profile financial and corruption cases

During his tenure, the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York (SDNY) under Geoffrey Berman prosecuted several significant cases involving international bribery, securities fraud, and related financial misconduct, often targeting schemes that spanned multiple jurisdictions and involved substantial sums. These efforts built on prior investigations but advanced to indictments, guilty pleas, and sentencings during 2018â€“2020, emphasizing accountability for corruption that undermined financial institutions and global markets.[24] A key example was the continuation and resolution of prosecutions tied to the 1Malaysia Development Berhad (1MDB) scandal, a massive embezzlement and money laundering operation that defrauded the Malaysian sovereign wealth fund of approximately $4.5 billion. In January 2018, Joo Hyun "Steve" Lee, a New Jersey-based real estate broker, pleaded guilty to conspiring to launder at least $35 million in proceeds from foreign bribery schemes linked to 1MDB, including funds used to influence Malaysian officials and acquire luxury assets in the U.S.[54] The case highlighted SDNY's role in tracing illicit flows from overseas corruption, with Berman's office securing cooperation that aided broader recovery efforts, though fugitive Malaysian financier Jho Low remained at large despite sealed indictments unsealed in 2019 for related wire fraud and money laundering charges exceeding $1 billion. In domestic financial fraud, the SDNY secured a guilty plea from former U.S. Congressman Christopher Collins in August 2019 for securities fraud and insider trading related to a biotech company's unsuccessful drug trial. Collins, who lied to investigators and obstructed justice, was sentenced to 26 months in prison in January 2020 after tipping off family and associates, allowing them to sell shares before a public disclosure that caused a 92% stock drop and over $8 million in avoided losses.[55] This prosecution demonstrated the office's focus on elite insider misconduct, with Berman emphasizing the betrayal of public trust in capital markets. Other notable actions included a February 2020 guilty plea by a banker who accepted bribes to approve fraudulent loans and facilitate money laundering at a scheme targeting over $10 million in illicit transactions through U.S. banks.[56] Additionally, in May 2018, the CEO and president of a New York credit union were charged with embezzling over $9.1 million for personal use, including luxury purchases, underscoring SDNY's vigilance against institutional corruption.[57] These cases collectively recovered millions in penalties and reinforced deterrence against financial crimes integral to broader economic integrity.

Political figures and election-related probes

During Geoffrey Berman's tenure as United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York (SDNY), the office prosecuted several cases involving political figures and potential election law violations, particularly those linked to the 2016 presidential campaign and subsequent political activities. One prominent case was the August 2018 guilty plea of Michael Cohen, President Donald Trump's former personal attorney, to charges including making unlawful campaign contributions exceeding federal limits. Cohen admitted to arranging $130,000 and $150,000 payments to women alleging affairs with Trump to suppress damaging information ahead of the 2016 election, actions prosecutors described as intended to influence the vote.[62] The SDNY filing identified Trump as "Individual-1," though Berman had recused himself from direct oversight due to his prior pro bono work for the Trump inaugural committee.[37][63] In October 2019, the SDNY indicted Lev Parnas and Igor Fruman, associates of Trump's personal lawyer Rudy Giuliani, for conspiring to violate campaign finance laws by routing foreign funds into U.S. elections. The charges alleged the pair funneled over $1 million from foreign sources, including Ukrainian oligarchs, through straw donors to Republican candidates and committees, aiming to influence federal and state races, such as a 2018 congressional bid in Florida.[30][64] Parnas and Fruman were arrested at Dulles International Airport while attempting to flee the country with one-way tickets.[65] These actions tied into broader efforts to pressure Ukraine for investigations into Trump's political rivals, though the indictment focused on finance violations rather than direct foreign influence on policy.[66] Fruman later pleaded guilty in 2021 to one count, while Parnas was convicted on related charges in 2021.[67] These probes underscored the SDNY's role in enforcing federal election laws against high-profile figures connected to the Trump orbit, amid reported tensions with Department of Justice leadership over case handling. For instance, main Justice Department officials sought to excise Trump references from Cohen's plea agreement and explored avenues to investigate Trump's critics, which Berman's office resisted.[63][68] Critics from conservative outlets argued the investigations selectively targeted Republicans, citing a lack of parallel scrutiny into Democratic figures like those linked to the Clinton Foundation, though no formal probes into such matters advanced under Berman.[69] The cases contributed to perceptions of SDNY independence but also fueled debates on politicization, as evidenced by Berman's later accounts of White House pressure to deploy resources against perceived enemies.[37][70]

Post-tenure career and contributions

Return to private legal practice

Following his removal from the United States Attorney position for the Southern District of New York on June 20, 2020, Geoffrey Berman returned to private legal practice by joining the law firm Fried, Frank, Harris, Shriver & Jacobson as a partner on December 1, 2020.[71][55] In this capacity, he assumed leadership of the firm's White Collar Defense, Regulatory Enforcement, and Investigations Practice, focusing on representing clients in high-stakes matters such as Department of Justice and Securities and Exchange Commission probes, congressional inquiries, and internal corporate investigations.[3][72] Berman also serves as Global Chair of the firm's Litigation Department, drawing on his prior experience as a prosecutor to advise on complex commercial litigation and defense strategies in white-collar cases.[3] Examples of his work include counseling a Fortune 500 medical technology company during an SEC investigation, assisting a global financial institution in a DOJ inquiry, and representing the former president of Signature Bank in connection with congressional hearings related to the bank's collapse.[3] His expertise has earned recognition from Chambers USA and The Legal 500 for white-collar crime and government investigations.[3] As of 2025, Berman remains affiliated with Fried Frank in these leadership roles.[3]

Academic roles and publications

Following his tenure as United States Attorney, Berman served as a visiting professor of law at Stanford Law School for the fall 2020 quarter, teaching a course on federal criminal law.[73] This role leveraged his extensive prosecutorial experience, including prior service as an Assistant United States Attorney in the Southern District of New York handling complex fraud and public corruption cases.[13] No subsequent or ongoing academic appointments at Stanford or other institutions have been reported.[3] Berman's primary publication is the 2022 memoir Holding the Line: Inside the Nation's Preeminent U.S. Attorney's Office and Its Battle with the Trump Justice Department, published by Penguin Press.[74] The book provides an insider account of high-profile investigations under his leadership, such as those involving Jeffrey Epstein and Michael Cohen, alongside alleged pressures from Department of Justice officials during the Trump administration.[75] It draws on his firsthand observations but has drawn mixed evaluations, with some legal analysts questioning its selective emphasis on conflicts with political leadership while defending the office's independence.[76] Prior to this, Berman contributed as note editor to the Stanford Law Review during his time as a student in the early 1980s, though no additional scholarly articles or peer-reviewed works in legal journals are documented.[73]

Controversies and differing interpretations

Allegations of political interference in prosecutions

Geoffrey Berman, in his 2022 memoir Holding the Line, alleged that Attorney General William Barr and senior Department of Justice (DOJ) officials exerted pressure on the Southern District of New York (SDNY) to initiate or alter prosecutions for political purposes during the Trump administration.[37] [63] Berman claimed this interference aimed to target Trump's perceived adversaries, such as former FBI Director James Comey, former Deputy Director Andrew McCabe, former Secretary of State John Kerry, and cybersecurity lawyer Michael Sussmann, while seeking leniency for Trump associates.[77] [35] He described these efforts as unprecedented attempts to weaponize federal prosecutions, asserting that Barr personally directed him to pursue cases lacking sufficient evidence to satisfy SDNY's independent standards.[37] Specific instances cited by Berman include the Michael Cohen prosecution, where Barr allegedly intervened to soften the SDNY's sentencing recommendation after Cohen cooperated against Trump, prompting Berman to proceed without DOJ approval to avoid perceived favoritism.[62] In the Roger Stone case, Berman alleged pressure from Barr to reduce recommended sentencing for the Trump advisor convicted of lying to Congress about WikiLeaks contacts.[48] Regarding the Halkbank investigation into sanctions evasion on behalf of Iran, Berman claimed Barr ordered him to un-recuse the office following a December 2018 phone call between Trump and Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan, pushing for a deferred prosecution agreement that Berman viewed as unduly favorable to Turkey's state-owned bank.[78] [79] The SDNY indicted Halkbank in October 2019 despite these overtures.[80] These allegations prompted the Senate Judiciary Committee, under Democratic leadership, to launch an investigation on September 12, 2022, into potential political meddling in DOJ processes, requesting documents related to the cited cases.[81] [49] Berman linked the pressures to his abrupt removal from office on June 20, 2020, after he publicly refused to resign amid ongoing SDNY probes into Trump associates like Rudy Giuliani.[82] [29] Barr and Trump administration officials denied the claims of improper interference, characterizing Barr's involvement as standard oversight of U.S. Attorneys by the Attorney General.[83] Barr described Berman's firing as his own decision, unrelated to specific cases, and dismissed post-tenure accounts like Berman's book as self-serving distortions of routine supervisory actions.[84] Trump, who had publicly criticized SDNY as politically biased against him, asserted the office pursued investigations into his circle without merit, though no formal allegations of prosecutorial misconduct by Berman were substantiated in court or congressional findings.[85] No independent probes have confirmed Berman's assertions of systemic interference, with DOJ policies permitting Attorney General review of high-profile matters.[86]

Debates over prosecutorial independence

Geoffrey Berman's tenure as acting United States Attorney for the Southern District of New York (SDNY), from January 2018 to June 2020, became a focal point for debates on prosecutorial independence, particularly following his abrupt removal from office. Appointed on an interim basis by President Donald Trump, Berman oversaw high-profile investigations into Trump associates, including the prosecution of Michael Cohen in 2018 for campaign finance Republican figures while allegedly delaying or ignoring probes into Democratic targets, such as the Clinton Foundation or Governor Andrew Cuomo's handling of nursing home deaths during the COVID-19 pandemic.[46] Attorney General William Barr, in defending the decision to seek Berman's resignation, emphasized that Berman had served over two years without Senate confirmation, justifying the push for a permanent appointee rather than any erosion of independence.[88] Berman, in his 2022 memoir Holding the Line, asserted that the Department of Justice (DOJ) under Barr exerted unprecedented political pressure on the SDNY to initiate investigations against Trump's perceived adversaries, including former Secretary of State John Kerry and Cuomo, while seeking to curtail probes into Trump allies like Rudy Giuliani.[48] He described instances where Barr summoned him to meetings to demand action on politically motivated cases, framing his resistanceâ€”including refusal to reassign Giuliani-related investigationsâ€”as a defense of the office's historic autonomy.[37] Supporters of this view, including congressional Democrats, cited Berman's March 2020 public statement affirming the SDNY's independence from political influence amid the Cohen sentencing, portraying his ouster as retaliation for upholding non-partisan standards.[89] However, these claims rely heavily on Berman's personal account, which lacks independent corroboration in public records and has been contested by Barr, who denied any improper interference and attributed tensions to routine oversight of a powerful U.S. Attorney's office.[46] The June 20, 2020, firingâ€”precipitated by Berman's refusal to resign after Barr announced corporate lawyer Jay Clayton as his replacementâ€”intensified partisan divides. Trump publicly stated he had fired Berman personally, later clarifying he deferred to Barr, while Republican senators like Lindsey Graham defended the president's authority over appointees without evidence of misconduct.[69] Detractors, including legal scholars at Stanford Law School, argued the episode exemplified executive overreach, noting the SDNY's tradition of relative insulation from Washington politics, as evidenced by its handling of cases like the 1MDB Malaysian corruption scandal under Berman.[90] Conversely, analyses from conservative perspectives highlighted Berman's interim status and the DOJ's legitimate role in ensuring alignment with departmental priorities, questioning whether his office's focus on Trump-related matters reflected institutional bias rather than pure independence. Empirical data on case outcomes shows the SDNY secured over 3,000 convictions during Berman's tenure, including non-political matters like opioid prosecutions, but debates persist over whether political considerations influenced prioritization.[91] These contentions underscore broader tensions in U.S. federal prosecution: the balance between a U.S. Attorney's operational discretion and accountability to the executive branch. While Berman's post-firing testimony before Congress in 2020 reinforced narratives of DOJ politicization, Barr's congressional appearances maintained that no directives violated norms, attributing friction to Berman's prolonged interim role amid national security priorities.[63] Mainstream media coverage, often aligned with critiques of the Trump administration, amplified Berman's perspective, potentially overlooking prior SDNY actions under Democratic appointees that similarly targeted opponents, thus warranting scrutiny of source incentives in evaluating claims of interference.[92]
