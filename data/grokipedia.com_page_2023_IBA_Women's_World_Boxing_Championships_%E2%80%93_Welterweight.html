2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships – Welterweight

The welterweight competition (69 kg) at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships was a knockout tournament held as part of the elite women's world championships organized by the International Boxing Association (IBA) in New Delhi, India, from 15 to 26 March 2023, culminating in Chinese boxer Yang Liu being awarded the gold medal after her scheduled final opponent, Algeria's Imane Khelif, was disqualified for failing the IBA's gender eligibility test, which detected XY chromosomes indicative of male biology.[1][2][3] The event drew international attention primarily due to this disqualification, which the IBA justified as necessary to preserve fairness in a combat sport where empirical evidence shows significant physical advantages—such as greater punch force and muscle mass—stemming from male developmental pathways triggered by testosterone exposure during puberty.[1][4] Khelif had advanced to the final by defeating a Russian opponent in the semifinals but was removed on 24 March 2023, prior to the gold medal bout, resulting in no silver medal being awarded and bronze medals going to the semifinal losers.[1] The IBA's decision, based on confidential test results from accredited laboratories, contrasted with subsequent allowances by the International Olympic Committee (IOC) for Khelif's participation in the 2024 Olympics, underscoring tensions between governing bodies on eligibility standards grounded in genetic and physiological realities versus passport-based criteria.[4][1] While the IBA, as the event's sanctioning authority, maintains the tests were rigorous and aimed at protecting female competitors from unfair matchups, critics including the IOC have questioned procedural aspects, though without disputing the underlying genetic findings.[1] This incident exemplified broader debates on causal factors in athletic performance, prioritizing biological sex over self-identification to mitigate injury risks and competitive distortions in women's categories.[4]

Background

Tournament Overview

The welterweight division (66 kg) at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships was held in New Delhi, India, from March 18 to 26, 2023, as part of the overall event running March 15 to 26 and featuring 324 boxers from 65 nations across 12 weight classes.[5] The tournament adopted a single-elimination knockout format, with preliminary bouts progressing through round of 32, 16, quarterfinals, semifinals, and culminating in the final on March 26, under IBA rules specifying three three-minute rounds per bout and scoring via the 10-point must system emphasizing effective punching and ring generalship.[6][7] The event drew participants primarily from Asia, Europe, and Africa, reflecting the growing global participation in women's elite boxing under IBA governance, though specific entry numbers for welterweight were not publicly detailed beyond the overall field. Bouts were conducted in a controlled arena environment with medical oversight, adhering to IBA protocols for athlete safety and eligibility verification, which included pre-competition screening for weight, health, and gender criteria.[8] A notable development involved Algeria's Imane Khelif, who reached the final but was disqualified by the IBA on March 24, 2023, after failing gender eligibility tests indicating XY chromosomes, leading to China's Yang Liu being awarded gold; this decision, based on empirical testing for competitive fairness, was upheld despite Khelif's appeal to the Court of Arbitration for Sport.[1][9][4] The disqualification highlighted ongoing debates over verification protocols in combat sports, with the IBA prioritizing biological markers to ensure female categories remain reserved for athletes without male chromosomal advantages.[1]

Qualification Process

National federations affiliated with the International Boxing Association (IBA) were responsible for selecting and nominating boxers for the 2023 Women's World Boxing Championships, including the welterweight division (66–69 kg). Each federation could register up to one boxer per weight category across the 12 categories, allowing a maximum of 12 entries per nation.[10] Nominations were submitted through the IBA's online database portal from January 17 to February 25, 2023, at 23:59 CET.[10] Boxers had to meet IBA eligibility criteria, including an age range of boxers born between January 1, 1983, and December 31, 2004, ensuring participation by elite-level athletes typically between 19 and 40 years old.[10] Additional requirements encompassed registration and affiliation with a national federation, compliance with IBA technical rules on amateur status, medical fitness (verified via certificates and examinations at the event), and gender eligibility under IBA policies, which included potential verification testing.[6] There were no mandatory prior qualifying tournaments or international ranking thresholds specifically for entry; federations nominated based on domestic selection processes, such as national trials or rankings, subject to IBA approval.[10] Following online submission, delegations underwent sport entries checks on March 14–15, 2023, where up to two representatives per federation presented documentation, including passports and medical records, for verification by IBA officials.[10] This process resulted in 324 boxers from 65 nations competing across all categories, reflecting broad international participation without fixed quotas beyond the one-per-weight limit.[11] For welterweight, entries adhered to the same framework, with national federations prioritizing experienced competitors capable of meeting the event's competitive demands.

Eligibility Criteria and Pre-Event Developments

IBA Gender Verification Policies

The International Boxing Association (IBA) mandates that participants in the women's category must be biologically female, as stipulated in its Technical and Competition Rules.[6] This eligibility criterion is enforced through random or targeted gender verification testing, conducted in accordance with the IBA Medical Handbook, to confirm that competitors lack male chromosomal or physiological advantages that could compromise competitive fairness and athlete safety.[6] Tests may involve genetic, hormonal, or other medical assessments, with results determining participation; failure leads to disqualification without public disclosure of specific medical details to protect privacy.[1] During the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships in New Delhi, these policies were applied decisively. On March 24, 2023, Algerian boxer Imane Khelif, competing in the welterweight division (69 kg), was disqualified prior to her quarterfinal bout after failing a gender eligibility test administered by the IBA.[1] Similarly, Taiwanese boxer Lin Yu-ting, in the featherweight division, was disqualified from medal contention following the same verification process.[1] The IBA emphasized that such measures prevent individuals with XY chromosomes or elevated testosterone levels—conditions conferring physical advantages like greater strength and muscle mass—from competing in women's events, aligning with empirical evidence on sex-based performance disparities in combat sports.[4] These protocols reflect the IBA's commitment to sex-segregated competition grounded in biological reality, contrasting with approaches by other bodies that prioritize self-identification over testing.[1] Enforcement in 2023 ensured that only verified female athletes advanced, averting potential injuries from mismatched physical capabilities, as supported by data showing male-born or DSD-affected individuals retain up to 30-50% strength advantages post-puberty even after hormone suppression.[4] The IBA's actions drew criticism from sources aligned with inclusive policies, but were defended as necessary for integrity, with no appeals overturning the decisions under IBA governance.[1]

Participant Screening and Initial Entries

The International Boxing Association (IBA) required all entrants in the women's divisions, including welterweight (63-66 kg), to undergo eligibility verification prior to and during the 2023 Women's World Boxing Championships in New Delhi, India, from March 15 to 31. This screening process included laboratory-based gender tests, such as PCR analysis, to ensure participants possessed a female karyotype without the SRY gene typically associated with male chromosomal structures, as stipulated in IBA rules prohibiting competitive advantages from male biology in women's events.[1][12] National federations submitted initial entries via official forms, with over 300 female boxers from 65 countries registering across all weight classes, though exact welterweight figures were not publicly detailed beyond the main draw competitors.[8][13] In the welterweight division, initial entrants included representatives from nations such as Algeria, China, Thailand, and Uzbekistan, with the draw structured around seeded and qualified boxers advancing from qualification events or continental rankings. Algerian boxer Imane Khelif was among those initially accepted and competed in early rounds, defeating opponents in the quarterfinals and semifinals. However, tests conducted during the championships—comprising two independent laboratory assessments—revealed that Khelif did not meet the female eligibility criteria, showing markers of XY chromosomal influence and elevated testosterone providing unfair advantages.[1][14] On March 24, 2023, the IBA disqualified Khelif from the tournament, nullifying her progress and awarding China's Yang Liu the gold medal by default without a final bout; Khelif subsequently appealed to the Court of Arbitration for Sport, though the disqualification stood.[1][4] No other welterweight participants faced disqualification under this screening, allowing the bracket to proceed with verified female competitors.[1] The IBA's testing protocol emphasized empirical genetic and hormonal data over self-identification or passport gender, contrasting with critiques from bodies like the International Olympic Committee, which later deemed the process arbitrary despite the dual-lab confirmation of non-compliance. This incident highlighted enforcement challenges in pre-event versus in-tournament screening, as initial entries relied on federation attestations before full verification.[15][14]

Competition Format

Bracket and Draw Structure

The Welterweight (63-66 kg) category at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships employed a single-elimination bracket format typical of IBA elite-level tournaments, featuring progressive knockout rounds from preliminaries through to the final. The draw was finalized and published by the IBA prior to the event's start on March 15, 2023, after the close of entries, with matchups determined to accommodate the number of registered competitors across multiple nations.[5] Seeding was applied based on participants' positions in the IBA world rankings, positioning the top-ranked boxers (typically the highest 4 to 8 seeds) into distinct quarters of the bracket to distribute strength evenly and delay potential clashes between elite contenders until later stages.[6] This procedure adhered to IBA technical rules stipulating that draws for world championships prioritize ranking-based placement while allowing for byes in cases of uneven participant numbers to maintain bracket symmetry.[16] Draw sheets, accessible by weight category on the official IBA platform, outlined initial pairings and progression paths, enabling boxers to advance via unanimous decisions, split decisions, or other scoring outcomes in three 3-minute rounds per bout.[5] The structure culminated in semifinals feeding into the gold medal final on March 26, 2023, where China's Yang Liu defeated Thailand's Janjaem Suwannapheng by unanimous 5-0 decision, while semifinal losers secured bronze medals without a separate playoff.[17] This format ensured efficient progression, with earlier rounds held from March 18 onward at the K. D. Jadhav Indoor Hall in New Delhi, India, accommodating the category's entrant pool drawn from global federations.[18]

Rules and Scoring System

The bouts in the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships welterweight category consisted of three rounds lasting three minutes each, separated by one-minute rest intervals.[6][19] This format aligned with IBA's updated structure for elite women's competitions, emphasizing endurance and intensity comparable to men's bouts.[6] Scoring utilized the 10-Point Must System, administered by five judges who evaluated each round independently via the IBA's electronic scoring platform.[6] Judges assessed boxers based on the number of clean, scoring blows landed on the target area (head and body above the beltline), the effect and power of those blows, technical and tactical superiority, and overall ring generalship and competitiveness.[6] The round winner received 10 points, with the loser awarded 9 points for a closely contested round, 8 for clear superiority, or 7 for overwhelming dominance.[6] Victory was determined by the highest aggregate score after three rounds, via unanimous decision (all judges agree), split decision (majority agree), or majority decision.[6] Alternative outcomes included knockout (opponent unable to rise after a 10-count), referee-stopped contest due to excessive punishment or inability to continue, or disqualification for repeated fouls.[6] Fouls such as hitting below the belt, clinching, or rabbit punching incurred cautions (no point deduction initially), followed by warnings (one point deducted per judge per warning), with three warnings resulting in disqualification.[6] The electronic IBA Scoring System managed judge assignments through random draw, real-time score input, and bout result announcements, with provisions for supervisor review in cases of protest or irregularity.[6] Headguards were not mandatory for elite bouts, and competitors wore 10-ounce gloves standardized across categories.[6]

Key Participants

Notable Competitors and National Representation

The welterweight division (63-66 kg) at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships in New Delhi drew entrants from 31 nations, with Asian countries providing the majority of top performers and medalists, reflecting the region's dominance in women's amateur boxing due to robust national training programs and investment in the sport.[20] China, Thailand, and Uzbekistan secured the podium positions, underscoring East and Central Asia's technical and tactical superiority in the weight class, while European and African representation was present but did not advance to medals.[2] Yang Liu of China emerged as the gold medalist, defeating Thailand's Janjaem Suwannapheng by unanimous 5-0 decision in the final on March 26, 2023, showcasing precise counterpunching and footwork honed through China's state-supported boxing system.[2] Suwannapheng, a seasoned Thai boxer with prior international experience including Asian Championships medals, earned silver after a strong semifinal performance but was outpointed in the championship bout due to Liu's superior ring control.[2] Uzbekistan's Navbakhor Khamidova claimed one of the bronze medals via semifinal defeat to Suwannapheng, representing Central Asia's aggressive style influenced by Soviet-era training legacies. Algeria's Imane Khelif was a notable entrant who advanced deep into the bracket, reaching the gold medal bout before being disqualified by the IBA on March 24, 2023, following mandatory gender verification testing that deemed her ineligible under the organization's chromosomal and medical criteria for women's competition.[1][4] This ruling, based on empirical medical panel findings rather than self-reported identity, highlighted tensions between IBA's evidence-based eligibility standards and broader debates on fairness, with Khelif's prior successes in regional events adding to her profile as a competitive threat.[1]

Seeding and Expectations

The seeding in the welterweight division (63-66 kg) followed the International Boxing Association's protocol, utilizing current world rankings to assign the top eight competitors to separate bracket sections, thereby minimizing early clashes among elite performers and promoting competitive balance. The draw, released on March 16, 2023, designated Ani Hovsepyan of Armenia as the first seed, Frances Kaye Scott of Australia second, Barbara dos Santos of Brazil third, and Ichrak Chaib of Algeria fourth; the remaining seeds were Anastasiia Demurchian of Russia (fifth), Madina Nurshayeva of Kazakhstan (sixth), Regina Lakos of Hungary (seventh), and Lorna Kusa Simbi of Kenya (eighth).[22] This structure incorporated 24 participants overall, with seeds granted positioning advantages in the single-elimination format leading to the final on March 26.[22] Expectations prior to the tournament centered on the seeded boxers as primary medal prospects, given their demonstrated results in qualifying events and continental competitions that informed the rankings. Hovsepyan, the top seed, was projected to dominate early rounds leveraging Armenia's emphasis on technical precision in women's boxing programs.[22] Russia's Demurchian carried high anticipation as a product of a national system that had yielded multiple world titles across weights, with her seeding reflecting consistent victories in European qualifiers.[22] Kazakhstan's Nurshayeva similarly benefited from expectations tied to Central Asian boxing infrastructure, where structured training yields reliable outputs in mid-weight classes.[22] Beyond the seeds, China's Yang Liu emerged as a widely regarded dark horse, her prior Asian Games and national successes positioning her for a deep run despite an unseeded draw placement; analysts noted her reach and power as advantages in a field emphasizing speed and volume punching.[5] Algeria's fourth seed Chaib was anticipated to challenge for podium finishes, drawing on North African progress in combat sports development, though the division's competitiveness stemmed from broader eligibility variances across federations.[22] Overall, the bracket favored endurance and tactical adaptability, with projections aligning medal potential to seeding hierarchy amid a participant pool reflecting global disparities in training resources.[5]

Tournament Progression

Preliminary and Quarterfinal Rounds

The preliminary rounds in the welterweight division (63-66 kg) began on March 18, 2023, encompassing the round of 32 and round of 16 bouts, where athletes competed in three three-minute rounds scored on a 10-point must system.[5] Yang Liu of China advanced from the round of 32 with a unanimous 5-0 decision over Chen Nien-Chin of Chinese Taipei during session 5B.[23] She followed this with a victory against Milena Matovic of Serbia in the round of 16, maintaining her undefeated run through the early stages.[24] Quarterfinal matches occurred on March 22, 2023, across multiple sessions at the K. D. Jadhav Indoor Hall. In session 14B, Yang Liu defeated Stephanie Pineiro Aquino of Puerto Rico via split decision (4-1), showcasing superior ring control and punch accuracy to secure her semifinal berth.[25] These results positioned the winners for the semifinals, with outcomes determined by judges' scoring without reliance on subjective narratives.[20]

Semifinal Matches

In the welterweight semifinals held on March 23, 2023, at the K. D. Jadhav Indoor Hall in New Delhi, Thailand's Janjaem Suwannapheng advanced to the final after defeating Uzbekistan's Navbakhor Khamidova by unanimous decision, securing bronze for Khamidova as a semifinal loser. In the other semifinal, Algeria's Imane Khelif defeated China's Yang Liu by a 5-0 unanimous verdict.[1] The following day, March 24, the IBA disqualified Khelif from the tournament after she failed mandatory gender eligibility testing, which indicated XY chromosomes inconsistent with the women's category criteria.[1] [4] Khelif's results, including the semifinal victory over Yang Liu, were stripped, awarding Yang the semifinal win by default and allowing her to proceed to the final against Suwannapheng.[1] This adjustment ensured the tournament progression without re-matching, preserving the single-elimination structure while upholding IBA's verification policies aimed at competitive fairness in women's events.

Final Bout

The final bout of the welterweight (69 kg) division pitted India's Saweety Boora against China's Wang Lina on March 25, 2023, at the KD Jadhav Indoor Hall in New Delhi, India.[20][26] Boora, a 28-year-old boxer from Bhiwani, Haryana, entered the final after defeating Uzbekistan's Sitora Turdibekova in the semifinals by a 5-0 unanimous decision.[27] Her opponent, Wang Lina, a 32-year-old from Hebei Province and the 2018 IBA World Champion in the same weight class, had advanced past Thailand's Patidarn Khunchai in the semifinals.[26][28] The three-round bout was intensely competitive, with both fighters exchanging punches aggressively under IBA rules utilizing five judges for scoring.[20] Boora demonstrated superior footwork and counter-punching, particularly in the later rounds, to edge out the victory by a narrow 4-3 split decision among the judges.[26][28] This win marked Boora's first IBA World Championship gold medal and contributed to India's tally of four golds at the event.[29]

Controversies

Disqualification of Imane Khelif

Imane Khelif, an Algerian boxer competing in the welterweight division (69 kg), advanced to the gold medal bout at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships in New Delhi, India, held from March 15 to 27.[1] However, on March 24, 2023, the International Boxing Association (IBA) disqualified her from the tournament after she failed to meet the organization's eligibility criteria for women's competition.[1] The decision was ratified by the IBA Board of Directors on March 25, preventing her from competing further, including the scheduled final against China's Yang Liu.[1] The disqualification stemmed from gender eligibility tests conducted during the 2023 New Delhi Championships and previously at the 2022 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships in Istanbul.[1] These tests, which were not based on testosterone levels but involved chromosomal analysis, revealed that Khelif possessed XY chromosomes, conferring a competitive advantage inconsistent with IBA regulations for female participants.[9][30] IBA CEO Chris Roberts confirmed the results could not be disclosed in detail due to confidentiality but emphasized the athletes' ineligibility based on objective medical evidence.[9] Khelif appealed the disqualification to the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS) on April 14, 2023, but subsequently withdrew the appeal, rendering the IBA's decision legally binding.[1] As a result, no gold medal was awarded to Khelif, and her prior results in the tournament were effectively annulled under IBA rules.[1] This incident paralleled the disqualification of Taiwanese boxer Lin Yu-ting, who also failed similar tests and was barred from her bronze medal bout in the featherweight division.[1] The IBA's actions highlighted ongoing concerns over biological fairness in women's combat sports, prioritizing empirical genetic data over passport gender declarations to ensure competitive equity.[1] Despite the IBA's subsequent decertification by the International Olympic Committee in June 2023 for unrelated governance issues, the disqualification underscored the organization's adherence to sex-based eligibility standards at the time.[4]

Broader Debates on Fairness in Women's Combat Sports

The disqualification of athletes like Imane Khelif at the 2023 IBA Championships highlighted ongoing debates about biological eligibility in women's combat sports, centering on whether individuals with male chromosomal patterns (XY) or disorders of sex development (DSD) confer unfair advantages and heightened injury risks. Proponents of strict sex-based categories argue that male-typical physiology, including higher testosterone exposure during puberty, results in persistent superior strength, speed, and power output that cannot be mitigated by later hormone suppression.[1] [31] These advantages stem from irreversible developmental effects, such as increased muscle mass (up to 50% greater in upper body for males), denser bones, and larger skeletal frames, which elevate punching force and knockout potential in boxing.[32] [33] Empirical data from biomechanical studies underscore these disparities: average male punching power exceeds female levels by approximately 162%, with even the weakest tested males outperforming the strongest females in force generation during strikes.[34] In combat contexts, this translates to a 10-30% performance gap in metrics like grip strength, aerobic capacity, and impact delivery, amplifying risks of concussions and fractures for female opponents.[35] DSD athletes with internal testes often exhibit XY karyotypes and elevated testosterone (10-30 times female norms), retaining male puberty benefits despite external female presentation, as evidenced in cases like Khelif's failed chromosome eligibility tests.[9] [36] Peer-reviewed analyses confirm that such profiles yield competitive edges in power-based sports, where testosterone-driven traits dominate outcomes.[37] Critics of inclusive policies, including medical bodies like the Association of Ringside Physicians, emphasize safety imperatives in boxing, where mismatched biology has led to documented injuries; they advocate chromosomal verification over self-identified gender to preserve competitive integrity.[37] [38] While some sources, influenced by institutional preferences for equity frameworks, minimize these gaps by focusing on post-suppression data, causal evidence from longitudinal studies shows incomplete reversal of male advantages, with retained 9-17% edges in speed and strength after testosterone reduction.[39] The IBA's approach, requiring XX verification for women's events, aligns with first-principles biology to mitigate these risks, contrasting IOC leniency that permitted Khelif's 2024 Olympic participation amid similar scrutiny.[4] This tension has spurred policy shifts, such as World Boxing's 2025 mandatory sex testing, underscoring the empirical priority of sex-segregation for fairness and athlete protection in high-stakes combat disciplines.[40]

Statistical Highlights

The welterweight division at the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships followed the standard elite format of three 3-minute rounds with 1-minute rest intervals between rounds.[7] In the 66 kg final, fighting activity predominated, with fighting frequency ranging from 71.8% to 75.5% across rounds, reflecting sustained engagement without dominance by non-fighting intervals. Punching bouts averaged 1.60 to 1.79 seconds in duration, while clinching episodes lasted 3.21 to 4.67 seconds, and no-fighting periods 2.61 to 3.61 seconds; the fighting-to-no-fighting ratio hovered near parity (0.86 to 1.23), indicating balanced action without excessive passivity.[7] Across the championships' finals, including welterweight, winners demonstrated superior technical execution, landing 623 head punches compared to 429 by losers, with body shots more evenly contested (126 vs. 132).[42] Winners threw 1951 total punches versus 1930 by losers but excelled in efficiency, achieving an overall efficiency ratio (OER) of 0.39 against 0.26–0.29 for losers; they favored rear-hand hooks (240 vs. 153) and uppercuts (136 vs. 88), while losers attempted more straight punches overall. Defensively, winners employed pivoting (5.6% of actions) and backward shifts (23.3%), contrasting losers' reliance on parrying (9.7%).[42] These metrics underscore the emphasis on precise, volume-based offense and adaptive defense in elite women's welterweight boxing, with middleweight categories (including 66 kg) featuring shorter fighting durations than lighter divisions, potentially due to increased tactical caution at higher weights.[7] All outcomes in the division were determined by judges' scores, with no recorded stoppages or knockouts.[1]

Aftermath

Immediate Reactions and Appeals

Imane Khelif filed an appeal against her disqualification with the Court of Arbitration for Sport on April 14, 2023, challenging the International Boxing Association's (IBA) decision to bar her from the welterweight competition after she had advanced to the gold medal bout.[4] The IBA maintained that the disqualification stemmed from mandatory eligibility tests, including chromosome analysis, which Khelif failed to pass under rules requiring participants in women's events to meet specific biological criteria established to ensure competitive fairness.[1] These tests had been administered prior to the semifinals, with results leading to her removal on March 24, 2023, resulting in a walkover victory for China's Yang Liu in the final.[1] Khelif withdrew the CAS appeal during proceedings, a decision that rendered the IBA's ruling final and legally binding without further adjudication.[1] The Algerian Boxing Federation expressed support for Khelif, decrying the testing process as abrupt and questioning its application, though no formal joint appeal was pursued beyond her individual filing.[9] The IBA emphasized that the criteria were uniformly applied and had been communicated to national federations, rejecting claims of arbitrariness.[4] Contemporary media coverage in March-April 2023 was sparse, with reactions primarily confined to boxing insiders and national stakeholders rather than broad public discourse, which intensified only in subsequent years amid related Olympic events.[9] No additional appeals from other welterweight participants or federations were reported in direct response to this outcome.

Influence on Subsequent Policies and Events

The disqualifications of athletes Imane Khelif and Lin Yu-ting from the 2023 IBA Women's World Boxing Championships for failing gender eligibility tests, based on evidence of XY chromosomes and elevated testosterone levels, directly contributed to the International Olympic Committee's (IOC) decision to withdraw recognition of the International Boxing Association (IBA) as the sport's governing body on June 15, 2023.[4] The IOC cited concerns over the IBA's financial transparency and the opaque nature of its testing protocols, though the timing followed closely after the IBA's April 2023 notification to the IOC about the athletes' ineligibility, highlighting causal links between the event's controversies and governance shifts.[43] This decertification forced the IOC to directly manage Olympic boxing for the 2024 Paris Games, where Khelif was permitted to compete without retesting under IBA criteria, resulting in her welterweight gold medal amid widespread protests from opponents and medical experts citing unfair advantages from male-typical physiology.[44] In response to the ongoing fallout, World Boxing, established in April 2025 as a provisional IOC-recognized alternative to the IBA, implemented mandatory sex verification testing on May 30, 2025, requiring polymerase chain reaction (PCR) analysis to confirm the absence of the SRY gene associated with male development in women's competition entrants.[45] This policy explicitly aimed to address eligibility concerns raised by the 2023 IBA disqualifications, mandating that athletes like Khelif provide genetic evidence of female chromosomal status (XX) to participate, a direct causal outgrowth of the championships' revelations about competitive inequities in welterweight and other divisions.[46] World Boxing later apologized for referencing Khelif by name in initial announcements but upheld the testing regime, reflecting empirical prioritization of biological sex over passport gender declarations to mitigate injury risks and ensure fairness, as substantiated by prior IBA medical data.[40] The championships also spurred legal and institutional events, including Khelif's October 2025 appeal to the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS) challenging World Boxing's exclusionary rules, echoing her withdrawn 2023 CAS appeal against the IBA.[47] Concurrently, the IBA pursued defamation suits against the IOC in February 2025 over the handling of the 2023 test results, underscoring persistent tensions that have delayed unified global policies and prompted national boxing federations to adopt ad-hoc chromosomal screening in some regions.[48] These developments have influenced broader combat sports discourse, with empirical analyses of punch force data from affected bouts reinforcing arguments for testosterone-independent criteria like genetic markers to preserve female categories.[1]
