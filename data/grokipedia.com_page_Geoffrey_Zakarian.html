Geoffrey Zakarian


Geoffrey Zakarian (born July 25, 1959) is an American chef, restaurateur, television personality, and author recognized for his mastery of modern American cuisine and leadership in high-profile New York City dining establishments, including The Lambs Club and The National.[1][2] Born in Worcester, Massachusetts, to Armenian-American and Polish-American parents, Zakarian honed his skills at the Culinary Institute of America and advanced through executive chef positions at acclaimed venues such as Country and Town, earning consistent high praise from critics like The New York Times.[3][4] His television prominence includes winning the Iron Chef title on Food Network's The Next Iron Chef: Super Chefs in 2011, serving as a judge on Chopped, and co-hosting The Kitchen.[1] Zakarian's career has also involved notable disputes, such as employee class-action lawsuits claiming wage theft, overtime denial, and unauthorized deductions, as well as a high-profile fallout with the Trump Organization in 2015 after he withdrew from a planned restaurant at Trump International Hotel in Washington, D.C., citing Donald Trump's comments on Mexican immigrants, resulting in a settled breach-of-contract suit.[5][6][7]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood Influences

Geoffrey Zakarian was born on July 25, 1959, in Worcester, Massachusetts, to Armenian-American father George Zakarian, a musician, and Polish-American mother Viola Hekowicz.[3][8] His family's ethnic heritageâ€”Armenian from his paternal side and Polish from his maternal sideâ€”shaped early cultural exposures, including traditional foods that emphasized hearty, home-cooked meals.[9][10] Zakarian grew up in a modest household in Worcester alongside a brother and a sister, where financial constraints were present but did not hinder a supportive upbringing focused on family values.[11] Food played a central role in daily life, with his parents preparing ethnic dishes rooted in their heritages, fostering an early appreciation for quality ingredients and communal eating despite neither being professional cooks.[9] This environment instilled discipline and a respect for culinary traditions, influenced by his father's Armenian background and the couple's traditional parenting style.[10] These childhood experiences laid foundational influences for Zakarian's career path, sparking an initial interest in cooking through family rituals rather than formal training, and highlighting the role of immigrant-descended households in preserving cultural gastronomy amid American assimilation.[12][9]

Formal Education and Initial Culinary Training

Zakarian attended Thorndyke Road School and Burncoat Junior-Senior High School in Worcester, Massachusetts, graduating from Burncoat High School in 1977.[13][3] He subsequently pursued higher education in economics at Worcester State College (now Worcester State University), earning a degree in the field.[14][4] Zakarian's formal culinary training occurred at the Culinary Institute of America (CIA) in Hyde Park, New York, from which he graduated in 1983 with an associate degree in culinary arts.[4][2] This program provided foundational skills in professional cooking techniques, kitchen management, and food preparation, marking the start of his specialized training in the culinary field before entering high-end restaurant kitchens.[4][12]

Culinary Career

Early Professional Positions

Following his graduation from the Culinary Institute of America in 1983, Zakarian obtained his initial professional role in a high-end kitchen at Le Cirque in New York City, where he approached the staff unannounced and began work the following day as a line cook.[2] Over the subsequent four years, he progressed to chef de cuisine under executive chef Alain Sailhac, gaining expertise in French haute cuisine techniques.[2] During summer intervals from Le Cirque, Zakarian staged at prominent European establishments, such as Au Quai des Ormes in Paris, Auberge de l'Ill in Alsace, the Dorchester in London, Le Chantecler in Nice under Jacques Maximin, and Pierre Orsay in Lyon, which refined his international culinary perspective.[2] In 1987, Zakarian relocated with Sailhac to the 21 Club in New York City, assuming the position of executive chef in what marked his debut in that leadership capacity at the historic venue.[15] [16] Two years afterward, in 1989, he advanced to executive chef at the newly launched 44 within the Royalton Hotel, partnering with developer Ian Schrager to establish a celebrated dining destination that emphasized innovative American fare.[2] These roles solidified Zakarian's reputation for precision and elevated service amid New York's competitive fine-dining landscape during the late 1980s.[17]

Key Restaurants and Business Expansions

Zakarian opened his first restaurant as chef and owner, Town, in 2001 at the Chambers Hotel in New York City, where it received three stars from The New York Times.[2] In 2005, he expanded with Country at the Carlton Hotel in Manhattan, which also earned three New York Times stars and a Michelin star for its modern American cuisine.[2] These venues established his reputation for refined, ingredient-focused dining but closed amid his 2011 bankruptcy filing.[2] In 2010, Zakarian broadened his portfolio through hotel partnerships, launching The Lambs Club at The Chatwal Hotel and The National at The Benjamin in New York City, emphasizing classic American fare with contemporary twists.[2] The following year, he extended operations to Florida with Tudor House at the Dream South Beach in Miami, which was named the best hotel restaurant of 2012 by Travel + Leisure.[2] These openings marked a shift toward collaborative ventures with hotel properties, leveraging his expertise in high-volume, upscale settings. Zakarian's business grew via the formation of Zakarian Hospitality Group around 2005, enabling consulting and management roles beyond full ownership.[18] Notable later projects include Point Royal at The Diplomat Beach Resort in Hollywood, Florida, opened following the resort's renovation, focusing on coastal seafood and steaks under his operational oversight.[19] More recently, in September 2025, he announced plans to develop and helm a flagship restaurant at The Central hotel in St. Petersburg, Florida, as part of ongoing expansions into emerging markets.[20] His consulting arm has also supported ventures like Mediterranean-focused Layla and casual The Porch in Sonoma, California, adapting his principles to regional hospitality needs.[21]

Awards, Achievements, and Culinary Philosophy


Zakarian's restaurant Country received a Michelin star in its inaugural year of 2005, alongside three stars from The New York Times.[17] His earlier role as executive chef at Patroon in 1998 also garnered three stars from The New York Times critic Ruth Reichl.[2] Town, which he opened in 2001 as chef-owner, similarly earned three New York Times stars and contributed to month-long reservation wait times.[2] In 2011, Zakarian won the Food Network competition The Next Iron Chef: Super Chefs, earning the Iron Chef title.[1] [2] He received the Culinary Institute of America's Augie Award in 2018 for exemplifying leadership values.[4] As a co-host of The Kitchen on Food Network, he earned a Daytime Emmy nomination in 2017 for Outstanding Informative Talk Show Host.[22] Zakarian's culinary philosophy emphasizes classical French and Italian training applied to high-quality, simply prepared ingredients, evolving into what he terms "Dynamic American" cuisine.[23] He balances tradition with innovation, stating, "I am 20 percent dynamic and 80 percent classic," to reflect constant change while rooted in technique.[23] Influences include European apprenticeships in Paris, precision from family baking, and Armenian heritage incorporating Mediterranean elements like spices.[23] This approach prioritizes authenticity, timeless quality, and professional standards adapted for home cooking.[2]

Television and Media Involvement

Competitive Judging Roles

Geoffrey Zakarian began serving as a recurring judge on the Food Network's Chopped in 2009, evaluating contestants' ability to create dishes from mystery basket ingredients under strict time constraints across appetizer, entrÃ©e, and dessert rounds.[2][1] As a panelist alongside other chefs, he assesses criteria including taste, creativity, plating, and adherence to challenge rules, often providing pointed feedback on technique and flavor balance drawn from his experience in fine dining.[24] His tenure on the show, spanning over 15 seasons by 2025, has established him as a staple judge known for his discerning palate and emphasis on precision in modern American cuisine.[25] Beyond Chopped, Zakarian has made guest judging appearances on other competitive formats. In season 3 of Cutthroat Kitchen, he judged the episode "Well, Hot Clam!", critiquing dishes sabotaged by contestants' auctioned disadvantages while prioritizing culinary execution amid chaos. He also judged the 2024 Tournament of Champions, Food Network's high-stakes bracket-style event featuring top culinary talents, where his role involved scoring semifinal and final battles on innovation and skill.[26] These selective engagements highlight his expertise in competitive settings, though his primary judging focus remains Chopped's high-volume format.[1]

Talk Show and Hosting Appearances

Zakarian has served as a co-host on Food Network's The Kitchen, a daytime talk show featuring cooking demonstrations, culinary tips, and guest segments, since its early seasons in the mid-2010s.[26] The program, which aired over 500 episodes across 40 seasons, concluded its run in December 2025 after Food Network announced its cancellation in October of that year.[27] Alongside co-hosts Sunny Anderson, Katie Lee Biegel, and Jeff Mauro, Zakarian contributed segments on techniques, recipes, and food trends, earning the show Emmy nominations for its format blending conversation and practical cooking advice.[28] Beyond hosting, Zakarian has made guest appearances on various daytime and late-night talk shows, often demonstrating quick recipes or sharing culinary insights. On Late Night with Seth Meyers in January 2019, he prepared a steamed bass with zucchini and spinach, offering tips on selecting fresh fish.[29] He appeared on Late Night with Jimmy Fallon in January 2012, promoting his work during a cooking segment.[30] In January 2025, Zakarian joined The Drew Barrymore Show to demonstrate a five-minute cooking hack for bistro hanger steak.[31] Zakarian has been a recurring guest on Rachael Ray, appearing multiple times to showcase gadgets, pasta techniques, and skillet meals, including episodes in 2016, 2019, 2020, and 2023.[32] He has also featured as a guest on ABC's The Chew, CBS's The Dish, NBC's Today, and ABC's Good Morning America, typically in segments focused on restaurant updates or seasonal recipes.[33][34] These appearances highlight his role in bridging professional chef expertise with accessible home cooking discussions.

Production and Authorship Ventures

Zakarian authored his debut cookbook, Geoffrey Zakarian's Town/Country: 150 Recipes for Life Around the Table, published by Clarkson Potter in 2006, which presents 150 recipes drawing from his experiences in urban and rural settings to emphasize accessible, high-quality home cooking.[1] His second major work, My Perfect Pantry: 150 Easy Recipes from 50 Essential Ingredients, released by Clarkson Potter on October 7, 2014, focuses on efficient pantry-based meal preparation using a core set of versatile staples, promoting simplicity without sacrificing flavor.[35] In 2017, Zakarian co-founded Corner Table Entertainment, a full-service multimedia production company specializing in culinary lifestyle content, in partnership with media executive Jaret Keller.[36][37] The venture operates from a midtown Manhattan facility equipped with a professional kitchen, enabling the development of podcasts, televised charity events, and prime-time television programs.[36] Among its early outputs is the SiriusXM radio series Food Talk with Geoffrey Zakarian, which features discussions on food trends and industry insights.[36] By 2021, the company had expanded its scope, positioning Zakarian as an executive producer alongside his on-air roles, with a focus on authentic storytelling in food media.[38]

Legal and Business Controversies

Partnership Disputes and 2011 Bankruptcy

In 2005, Geoffrey Zakarian partnered with investors Adam Block and Moshe Lax (through CML Restaurant Fund, LLC) to open Country, a modern American restaurant in the Carlton Hotel in New York City, which operated until its closure in summer 2008 despite annual gross revenues of approximately $9 million.[39] The venture never achieved profitability, leading to acrimonious disputes among the partners. Block publicly attributed the failure in part to Zakarian's "narcissistic behavior," while Lax, alongside CML, pursued legal action alleging Zakarian's mismanagement, including breaches of fiduciary duty, improper extraction of fees, failure to pay taxes, and labor law violations that treated Country as Zakarian's "personal piggy bank."[39][40] Zakarian countersued Lax in 2008 for unpaid obligations under their separation agreement, securing a judgment of $363,945.48 in March 2011, which a New York court later enforced in July 2013 after confirming an arbitration award that Zakarian's conduct did not rise to "willful misconduct."[41] The partnership conflicts intersected with a class-action lawsuit filed by 152 former kitchen staff from Country, who claimed Zakarian and his management firm violated federal and New York labor laws by falsifying pay records, denying overtime wages at time-and-a-half rates, and deducting pay for staff meals that were never provided, seeking $1 million in damages and $250,000 in penalties.[39][40] Notably, Block and Lax supported the employees' allegations by submitting affidavits and settling separately with the cooks for $200,000, escalating the pressure on Zakarian amid the ongoing partner litigation.[39][40] Zakarian denied the claims, asserting compliance with labor requirements, but the combined legal exposures exceeded $2 million from Lax alone plus the employee suit.[39] On April 6, 2011, Zakarian filed for personal Chapter 7 bankruptcy in federal court in Bridgeport, Connecticut, listing assets of no more than $50,000 and liabilities of up to $1 million across 179 creditors, including the 152 former cooks.[39][40] The filing automatically stayed the class-action and partner claims, providing a mechanism to discharge personal debts and halt collections, though it did not resolve underlying business disputes.[39] Subsequent arbitration and court rulings in Zakarian Management Group, Inc. v. Lax affirmed aspects of Zakarian's position against Lax, vacating stays on judgments in his favor by 2013.[41] The bankruptcy insulated Zakarian's personal finances during the turmoil but drew scrutiny for occurring amid unresolved operational failures at Country, where partner accusations centered on financial improprieties rather than culinary execution.[39][40]

Employee Wage and Labor Lawsuits

In early 2011, a class action lawsuit was filed in New York federal court by 152 former employees of Geoffrey Zakarian's restaurant Country, alleging violations of the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and New York Labor Law for failure to pay overtime wages.[42] [43] The plaintiffs claimed they were required to perform off-the-clock work, such as pre-shift preparations and cleaning, resulting in unpaid overtime exceeding 40 hours per week, with total damages sought over $1 million.[39] [44] The suit further accused Zakarian's management of manipulating payroll records to underreport hours, making unauthorized deductions from wages, and systematically ignoring applicable labor laws to reduce costs amid Country's financial struggles.[5] [43] One affidavit from a former line cook detailed mandatory unpaid hours for tasks like stocking and setup, contributing to the class certification efforts.[40] Zakarian's Zakarian Management Group responded by filing for Chapter 7 bankruptcy on April 26, 2011, listing liabilities of approximately $1.8 millionâ€”including the labor claimsâ€”and assets between $500,000 and $1 million, which effectively stayed the lawsuit and allowed discharge of many unsecured debts.[39] [44] In a related 2013 state court proceeding, Zakarian Management Group, Inc. v. Lax, Zakarian sought summary judgment against individual employee claims of willful violations; the court granted partial relief, finding insufficient evidence for liquidated damages under NY law but upholding potential liability for base overtime pay where off-the-clock work was documented.[43] No full public settlement details emerged post-bankruptcy, though the proceedings highlighted ongoing disputes over proof of hours worked versus recorded payroll.[43]

2016 Trump Hotel Lease Dispute

In February 2015, Geoffrey Zakarian, through his entities CZ-National, LLC and BVS Acquisition Co., LLC, signed a 20-year sublease with Trump Old Post Office, LLC for 9,344 square feet of space in the Old Post Office Pavilion in Washington, D.C., to operate a restaurant outpost of his New York establishment, The National, upon the opening of the Trump International Hotel in mid-2016.[45][46] On July 17, 2015, approximately one month after Donald Trump's June 16 presidential campaign launch speech in which he characterized some Mexican immigrants as bringing "drugs," "crime," and being "rapists," Zakarian notified Trump Old Post Office, LLC of his intent to terminate the sublease for cause.[45][47] He asserted that Trump's remarks violated implied covenants of quiet enjoyment and good faith and fair dealing, materially impairing his ability to secure financing, staffing, and customer patronage due to conflicts with his personal values and the inclusive ethos of his business.[45] Trump Old Post Office, LLC responded by filing a breach-of-contract lawsuit against Zakarian's entities on August 3, 2015, in the Superior Court of the District of Columbia, seeking approximately $10 million in damages for lost rent, fit-out costs, and related expenses.[48][45] Zakarian countersued, reiterating claims of interference and seeking declaratory relief to void the agreement.[45] The litigation included depositions, such as Donald Trump's on June 14, 2016, where he maintained that the contract obligated performance irrespective of external statements.[49] The parties reached a confidential out-of-court settlement on April 10, 2017, shortly before a scheduled pretrial conference, with no admission of liability by either side.[48][50]

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Geoffrey Zakarian was previously married to Heather Karaman, though details regarding the duration or circumstances of their divorce remain limited in public records.[51] Zakarian married Margaret Anne Williams, a marketing executive, on July 30, 2005, in a ceremony that marked his second marriage.[51] The couple, who have collaborated professionally in restaurant ventures, celebrated their 20th anniversary in 2025.[52] Williams has been described by Zakarian as integral to his personal and professional success, emphasizing her qualities of honesty, kindness, and empathy in a 2021 social media post.[53] Zakarian and Williams have three children: daughters Madeline and Anna, and son George.[54] [10] The family has occasionally appeared together in media, including cooking segments where the daughters contributed recipes alongside Zakarian.[55] As of 2023, the daughters were reported to be approximately 14 and 12 years old, with George around 7, reflecting a family-oriented dynamic centered in New York.[10]

Philanthropic Efforts and Public Persona

Zakarian has been actively involved in philanthropy, particularly in efforts to address food insecurity in New York City. He serves as Chairman of the City Harvest Food Council, a group of chefs that supports the organization's food rescue initiatives by redistributing surplus food from restaurants and suppliers to community programs serving those in need.[2][4] Appointed to this leadership role in January 2014, Zakarian has leveraged his culinary expertise to advocate for hunger relief, including participation in events like City Harvest's Summer in the City galas.[56] His family's engagement extends these efforts; in 2020, Zakarian's daughters, Madeline and Anna, co-founded the Junior Food Council under City Harvest and co-host its annual Winter Family Carnival, which raises funds and awareness for child-focused hunger programs.[2] Beyond City Harvest, Zakarian has supported broader anti-hunger causes through his platform as a restaurateur and media figure, aligning with organizations focused on culinary education and community food access.[12] In his public persona, Zakarian projects an image of refined sophistication and culinary authority, often described as embodying modern American hospitality with a focus on authenticity and precision in dining experiences.[2] This demeanor, influenced by his father's emphasis on personal style, manifests in his elegant on-screen presence as an Iron Chef and co-host of Food Network's The Kitchen, where he is known for discerning critiques and expertise in competitive formats like Chopped.[57][2] His reputation as a restaurateur underscores a business-oriented approach, balancing high-end ventures with humanitarian commitments, though some observers have noted a perceived aloofness in his television interactions.[58][10]
