It Takes Two (American TV series)

It Takes Two is an American sitcom television series created by Susan Harris that premiered on ABC on October 14, 1982, and concluded after one season on April 28, 1983, comprising 22 episodes.[1][2] The program centered on Sam and Molly Quinn, portrayed by Richard Crenna and Patty Duke respectively, a high-achieving married couple—a surgeon and a lawyer—grappling with the demands of their demanding careers alongside raising two teenage children and managing household duties often delegated to their housekeeper, played by Billie Bird.[1][3] Produced by Witt/Thomas/Harris Productions, the series highlighted the tensions of dual-income professional households in the early 1980s, drawing on familiar sitcom tropes of work-life imbalance but failing to achieve sustained popularity, as evidenced by its mid-season cancellation amid mediocre ratings.[1] Notable for providing early television exposure to future stars Helen Hunt and Anthony Edwards in the roles of the Quinn teenagers, It Takes Two represented a transitional effort by Harris following her successes with Soap and preceding The Golden Girls, though it garnered limited critical or commercial acclaim during its brief run.[4]

Premise and Characters

Synopsis

It Takes Two centers on the Quinn family, consisting of husband Sam Quinn, a pediatric surgeon, and wife Molly Quinn, a lawyer, who navigate the challenges of balancing high-powered careers with raising their three children in a downtown Chicago high-rise apartment.[5][3] The series portrays the couple's frequent comedic struggles as their professional demands—such as Sam's hospital shifts and Molly's legal cases—often lead to oversights in family responsibilities, including school events, meals, and discipline for their teenage daughter Lisa, adolescent son Andy, and young son Bobby.[6][7] The narrative emphasizes the Quinns' over-achieving personalities and the resulting tensions, where career successes inadvertently strain marital and parental bonds, prompting ongoing efforts to prioritize family amid chaotic domestic scenarios.[8] Recurring themes include generational clashes, with the parents learning to delegate to each other and extended family, such as Molly's mother, while the children exploit parental absences for mischief or independence.[3]

Main Cast and Roles

The principal roles in It Takes Two center on the Quinn family, depicting the challenges of balancing demanding professional lives with family responsibilities. Richard Crenna portrays Dr. Sam Quinn, a dedicated surgeon whose long hours at the hospital often strain family dynamics.[1] Patty Duke plays Molly Quinn, Sam's wife and a newly practicing attorney whose career ambitions similarly pull her away from home.[9] Their teenage daughter, Lisa Quinn, is enacted by Helen Hunt, while Anthony Edwards assumes the role of their son, Andy Quinn, highlighting adolescent perspectives amid parental absences.[1] Billie Bird appears as "Mama," the Quinns' live-in grandmother who provides comic relief and practical support in managing the household.[9] Richard McKenzie recurs as Judge Bentley, a colleague in Molly's legal circle who influences family storylines through professional interactions.[1]

Production

Development and Creative Team

It Takes Two was created by Susan Harris, a television writer and producer renowned for developing boundary-pushing sitcoms such as the satirical Soap (1977–1981) and its spin-off Benson (1979–1986).[10] Harris, who penned the pilot and multiple episodes, centered the series on the domestic challenges faced by high-achieving spouses, drawing from themes of work-family conflict prevalent in early 1980s American media.[11] The production was handled by Witt/Thomas/Harris Productions, a partnership formed by Harris, director Paul Junger Witt (whom she later married in 1983), and producer Tony Thomas, son of entertainer Danny Thomas.[7] This team had established a track record for ensemble-driven comedies, with Witt and Thomas contributing executive oversight to ensure the series' focus on relatable marital dynamics between leads Richard Crenna (as surgeon Sam Quinn) and Patty Duke (as lawyer Molly Quinn).[1] The creative pairing of Crenna and Duke was intentional, leveraging their established dramatic credentials to ground the sitcom's humor in authentic interpersonal tensions.[1] Directorial duties were shared among television veterans, including Norman Campbell and Peter Bonerz, who helmed episodes to maintain a consistent tone blending light farce with character-driven scenarios.[12] Harris's hands-on involvement extended to scripting oversight, emphasizing scripts that critiqued professional ambition's toll on personal relationships without resorting to overt preachiness.[7] The development process culminated in a 22-episode order for the 1982–1983 season, reflecting ABC's initial confidence in the concept amid a competitive Thursday-night slot.[1]

Episode Production and Structure

The episodes of It Takes Two were produced under the banner of Witt/Thomas/Harris Productions, with creator Susan Harris overseeing the scripting process alongside a team that emphasized comedic explorations of domestic dysfunction. A total of 22 episodes were completed for the series' sole season, aligning with the typical output for a mid-season network sitcom in the early 1980s.[7][8] Filming took place at the ABC Television Center, located at 4151 Prospect Avenue in Los Angeles, California, utilizing a studio setup standard for multi-camera sitcoms of the period. Technical specifications included a 30-minute runtime per episode, color filming, mono sound mix, and a 1.33:1 aspect ratio, designed to fit broadcast slots with room for advertisements.[13][14] Structurally, each episode adopted the conventional half-hour sitcom framework: a brief teaser or cold open introducing a dilemma, followed by two primary acts separated by commercial breaks, and concluding with a tag or resolution. Narratives centered on self-contained plots where the Quinn parents' high-pressure jobs—a surgeon father and lawyer mother—clashed with family obligations, often amplified by interactions with their three children, live-in relatives, and housekeeper, leading to resolutions that underscored work-life tensions without overarching serialization.[1][15]

Theme Song and Music

The theme song for It Takes Two, titled "Where Love Spends the Night," featured music composed by Charles Fox and lyrics by Paul Williams.[4] Performed as a duet by Paul Williams and Crystal Gayle, the track played over the opening credits sequence depicting family scenes aligned with the show's premise of divorced parents navigating shared custody.[16][17] Produced by the Witt/Thomas/Harris team, the theme's country-inflected style reflected the vocalists' backgrounds, with Gayle known for her hits like "Don't It Make My Brown Eyes Blue."[18] Williams, a prolific songwriter for television and film, contributed to multiple series themes during this period.[19] Incidental music and scoring for episodes drew from standard sitcom conventions of the era, though no dedicated composer is prominently credited beyond the theme; production records indicate reliance on library music or per-episode arrangements typical for one-season network comedies. No commercial soundtrack release accompanied the series during its 1982–1983 ABC run.[4]

Broadcast and Performance

Airing Details and Scheduling

It Takes Two premiered on ABC on October 14, 1982, occupying the network's Thursday night time slot at 9:30 p.m. ET.[3] [9] The sitcom aired weekly episodes, each approximately 25 minutes in length excluding commercials, through its series finale on April 28, 1983.[20] [5] All 22 episodes were broadcast during this single-season run without renewal for additional seasons.[9] [5] The scheduling aligned with ABC's fall-to-spring programming cycle, though specific preemptions for specials or events may have occurred as typical for network television of the era.[3]

Television Ratings and Viewership

"It Takes Two" broadcast 22 episodes across a single season on ABC, premiering on October 14, 1982, and concluding on April 28, 1983.[1] The sitcom failed to achieve strong viewership, leading to its cancellation prior to a second season despite the production of a full complement of episodes typical for 1980s network schedules.[21] Industry commentary from the period grouped the series with other underperformers like "Tales of the Gold Monkey" and "The New Odd Couple" as expected casualties in ABC's lineup, reflecting modest audience draw in a competitive Thursday-night slot against established CBS and NBC programming.[21] Detailed episode-specific Nielsen data remains scarce in public records, underscoring the show's inability to sustain advertiser-attractive demographics amid the era's emphasis on high household ratings for renewal decisions.

Reception and Analysis

Critical Reviews

It Takes Two received limited critical coverage during its brief run, reflecting its position amid a crowded 1982-1983 television landscape dominated by established hits. A review in The New York Times on October 28, 1982, commended the series for its talented cast, including Richard Crenna as Dr. Sam Quinn and Patty Duke Astin as Molly Quinn, portraying appealing characters grappling with the strains of dual-career parenthood in an upper-middle-class setting.[22] The piece highlighted creator Susan Harris's evolution from the satirical Soap to more grounded explorations of marital habits and gender role adjustments, noting how Sam views Molly as "a habit that's hard to break" amid her professional independence. Billie Bird's portrayal of Molly's eccentric mother was singled out as "nutty perfection," contributing to the show's comedic warmth without overt criticism of the premise or execution.[22] Subsequent mentions in television commentary echoed this favorable tone, describing the lead performances as executed "very nicely" by Crenna and Duke Astin, positioning the series as a competent entry from the Soap production team amid network efforts to capture evolving family dynamics.[23] However, the absence of aggregated critic scores on platforms like Rotten Tomatoes or Metacritic underscores the show's muted profile among reviewers, likely due to its competition against high-rated programs and its eventual mid-season cancellation after 18 aired episodes. No major detractors emerged in contemporary print sources, suggesting reception leaned toward appreciation for its relatable, low-key humor over innovative acclaim.

Thematic Content and Social Commentary

It Takes Two centers on the Quinn family's efforts to reconcile demanding professional lives with familial obligations, reflecting the rising prevalence of dual-career households in the early 1980s. Dr. Sam Quinn, a surgeon, and his wife Molly, an assistant district attorney, frequently grapple with time shortages that strain their marriage and parenting of six children, underscoring the causal trade-offs between career advancement and relational intimacy.[1] The series portrays these conflicts through comedic scenarios, such as scheduling mishaps and neglected anniversaries, while emphasizing mutual support as essential for marital endurance.[7] The show engages with evolving gender roles, particularly through Molly's shift from homemaker to litigator, which introduces tensions with traditional domestic expectations. Her courtroom successes often clash with home demands, like coordinating childcare amid a household supported by the couple's live-in mother-in-law, highlighting practical barriers for working mothers in high-stakes professions.[7] Sam's initial discomfort with Molly's absences evolves into endorsement, illustrating a pragmatic adaptation to women's increasing workforce participation without overt ideological advocacy. This depiction aligns with creator Susan Harris's intent to infuse mainstream sitcoms with contemporary social frictions, drawing from her prior work addressing family disruptions.[7] Episodes incorporate pointed social commentary on ethical dilemmas, such as the "Death Penalty" installment where Molly prosecutes a serial killer and defends capital punishment against Sam's opposition rooted in medical ethics.[24] This narrative probes retributive justice versus rehabilitation, with Molly confronting the defendant's mother in a debate that exposes ideological divides within marriages over punitive policy.[7] Similarly, "Inside Lisa Quinn" addresses teenage anxiety over nuclear proliferation, as daughter Lisa confronts existential fears through interactions with societal outcasts, mirroring broader Cold War-era apprehensions.[7] Everyday concerns like daycare shortages further ground the commentary in relatable domestic strains exacerbated by parental absences.[25] Overall, the series employs humor to dissect causal linkages between professional pursuits and familial erosion, advocating balance through compromise rather than prescribed reforms, while occasionally venturing into weightier debates on justice and global threats.[7]

Criticisms and Limitations

Despite a capable cast including Richard Crenna and Patty Duke, It Takes Two was critiqued for lacking compelling humor and originality, with reviewers noting it as a "mild sitcom" that felt unremarkable and formulaic.[26][15] Audience feedback highlighted episodes as "well-acted and looked good, but wasn't all that funny or interesting," contributing to its average IMDb rating of 6.5/10 from 129 users.[1][26] Character portrayals drew specific complaints for relying on stereotypes, such as the grandmother role played by Billie Bird, depicted as a "senile stereotype" that some found potentially offensive to older viewers.[26] Supporting roles, including the "oblivious mom" and "dweebish friend," were seen as bland and predictable, undermining the show's intent to realistically explore dual-career family dynamics.[15] These shortcomings manifested in poor audience retention, limiting the series to a single season of 22 episodes before cancellation on April 28, 1983, amid ABC's competitive Thursday-night schedule.[7] The program's failure to evolve beyond standard situational gags, despite creator Susan Harris's track record with more character-driven fare, underscored its structural limitations in capturing sustained viewership.[15]

Cancellation and Legacy

Reasons for Cancellation

The series concluded after one season, with its final episode airing on April 28, 1983, following the production and broadcast of 22 episodes. ABC executives opted not to renew It Takes Two primarily due to declining ratings, as stated by network spokesman Richard Connally in May 1983. This decision aligned with broader programming shifts, where the network cleared slots for new fall lineup entries amid underwhelming viewership performance across several mid-season shows.[27] No public controversies, cast departures, or production disputes were cited as factors in the cancellation; the focus remained on audience metrics, which failed to sustain competitive levels against rival network offerings during the 1982–1983 television season. Creator Susan Harris's subsequent projects, including The Golden Girls, achieved far greater longevity, underscoring that the issue was not inherent creative shortcomings but rather market fit and scheduling challenges at ABC.[27]

Post-Cancellation Reuse and Availability

Following its cancellation by ABC after the 1982–1983 season, It Takes Two entered off-network syndication during the 1983–1984 television season, with reruns distributed by Witt/Thomas Productions to local stations despite the series' single-season run.[28] This reuse allowed episodes to air beyond the original network broadcast, though the extent of syndication was limited and primarily confined to the mid-1980s. No evidence exists of widespread or sustained reruns in later decades, reflecting the show's modest viewership and lack of enduring syndication appeal compared to longer-running contemporaries. Episodes have never been commercially released on home video formats such as VHS or DVD. As of 2025, the series remains unavailable for legal streaming, rental, or purchase through major platforms, with no digital restoration or licensing deals reported.[29] Archival access is restricted to potential private collections or unauthorized uploads, underscoring the production's limited post-broadcast preservation efforts by distributor Sony Pictures Television.[30]
