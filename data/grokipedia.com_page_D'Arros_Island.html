D'Arros Island

D'Arros Island is part of the Amirante Islands group, which are in the Outer Islands (Coralline Seychelles) coral archipelago of the Seychelles islands and nation. The island is located west of the granitic Inner Seychelles archipelago, approximately 250 km southwest of Victoria, Seychelles. It is owned and managed by the Save Our Seas Foundation as a nature reserve and no-take marine protected area since 2012.[2]

History

The island was discovered in 1771 by the French explorer M. de la Biollière during an exploratory mission from Mauritius.[3] D'Arros Island bears the name of the Baron d'Arros, Marine Commandant of Île de France (now Mauritius), from 1770 to 1771.

Private island

Prior to 1975, the island was owned by a prominent local Seychellois family. In 1975, the island became the property of Prince Shahram Pahlavi Nia of Iran.[4] In 1998, it was bought by French billionaire Liliane Bettencourt (daughter of the founder and main shareholder of L'Oréal) for US$18 million.[5][6]

Nature preserve

In August 2012, it was revealed that the island had been sold, for US$60 million, to be managed by the Save Our Seas Foundation.[7][8] In 2014, it was officially designated a nature reserve.[9] As of 2025, it remains a protected nature reserve managed by the Save Our Seas Foundation.[10]

Geography

D'Arros Island is located only 2.3 kilometres (1.4 mi) west of the northern part of St. Joseph Atoll, but it is a separate geographical unit, separated by 60–62 metres (197–203 ft) deep water of a 1 kilometre (0.62 mi) wide channel, which is deeper than much of the Amirantes Bank. The land area of the island occupies 1.71 square kilometres (0.66 sq mi). D'Arros Island is an oval-shaped, flat coral sand cay, oriented northeast–southwest, 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) long and 1 kilometre (0.62 mi) wide, and nowhere higher than 3 metres (9.8 ft).

Coral reefs

The island stands on a detached patch coral reef similarly orientated, with maximum dimensions of 2.6 kilometres (1.6 mi) and 1.4 kilometres (0.87 mi). A shallow sand spit extends 0.2 kilometres (0.12 mi) northeast from it. The island stands on the northern sector of the reef atoll. There are drying reef flats 200–370 metres (660–1,210 ft) wide on its south side, but only a narrow fringing reef about 75 metres (246 ft) wide on its north side.

Administration

The island belongs to Outer Islands District.[11] It is designated as a Marine Protected Area, gazetted by the Seychelles government on 26 March 2020 under the Nature Parks and Conservancy Act, with management entrusted to the Save Our Seas Foundation (SOSF) since 2012 in collaboration with the Ministry of Environment, Energy and Climate Change.[12][13] Being an island with a small population, there are not any government buildings or services. For many services, people have to go to Victoria, which is a difficult task.

Demographics

The island has a permanent population of approximately 30 to 40.1 There are several buildings on the island, with one large house used by the owner and a number of smaller, permanently occupied houses for the staff. A small area of the island is cultivated for personal consumption of the residents. The small village is on the north coast.[14]

Flora and Fauna

Much of the island area is covered by vegetation. The tree tops reach heights of 27 metres (89 ft). The vegetation is dominated by the introduced coconut palm (Cocos nucifera) and introduced horsetail beefwood (Casuarina equisetifolia) trees.[15] Approximately 90% of the vegetation consists of coconut palms, though efforts are underway to restore native plant species as part of the island's nature reserve status since 2012.[16] In 1965, five Seychelles fody (Foudia sechellarum) birds from Cousin Island were introduced to D'Arros and they have since increased to over 100 by 1990.[17][18][19] Other terrestrial birds include the native Seychelles turtle dove (Streptopelia picturata coppelli) and introduced species such as the house sparrow (Passer domesticus) and Madagascar fody (Foudia madagascariensis).[16] The beaches serve as important nesting sites for hawksbill (Eretmochelys imbricata) and green (Chelonia mydas) turtles, with hundreds nesting annually.[20] The Save Our Seas Foundation D'Arros Research Centre mainly undertakes research on sharks, rays and turtles year-round but the centre also hosts visiting research groups and university students.[21]

Transport

A 800 metres (2,625 ft) unpaved airstrip (ICAO: FSDA) bisects the island in the southwest. The island is serviced by charter flights from the Islands Development Company Ltd. (IDC), Air Seychelles, or Zil Air from Mahé. Access is limited to charters due to the island's status as a protected nature reserve.[22][23]

Economics of D'Arros Island

Economics

D'Arros Island's economy is primarily driven by conservation and scientific research activities managed by the Save Our Seas Foundation D'Arros Research Centre, which funds operations and employs the island's residents, mainly as research staff and support personnel.[10] In addition to these professional activities, inhabitants engage in very small-scale farming and fishing, mainly for the island's self-consumption and to supplement food supplies. Historically, small communities on nearby islands grew coconuts and caught fish, a practice that continues on a limited basis for self-sufficiency.[20] Limited eco-tourism and educational programs, such as the D'Arros Experience for young ocean leaders, also contribute to the economy, promoting conservation awareness.[24][25]
