Howard Berman

Howard Lawrence Berman (born April 15, 1941) is an American attorney and retired politician who represented California's 26th and later 28th congressional districts as a Democrat in the United States House of Representatives from 1983 to 2013.[1][2] Prior to Congress, Berman served in the California State Assembly, where he authored significant labor reforms, including the Agricultural Labor Relations Act of 1975, which established collective bargaining rights for farmworkers, and contributed to other protections amid the era's union organizing efforts.[2][3] In the House, he held senior positions on the Foreign Affairs Committee as ranking member and on the Judiciary Committee, chairing its Subcommittee on Courts, the Internet, and Intellectual Property, where he advanced legislation strengthening copyright enforcement, international sanctions against Iran, and amendments to the False Claims Act to combat government fraud.[4][5][6] Berman, often described as a skilled legislative deal-maker with deep ties to Los Angeles' entertainment industry, faced criticism from technology advocates for provisions in intellectual property bills perceived as overly protective of media conglomerates, such as elements of the proposed PRO-IP Act.[4][7] His congressional tenure concluded after a contentious 2012 primary loss to fellow incumbent Brad Sherman following redistricting, amid mutual accusations of financial improprieties in campaign practices.[8][9] Since retiring, Berman has worked as a lobbyist, including representing Hollywood interests on policy matters.[10]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Howard Lawrence Berman was born on April 15, 1941, in Los Angeles, California, to Jewish parents Joseph Berman and Eleanor Berman (nÃ©e Schapiro).[11][12] His maternal grandparents had immigrated from Russia, reflecting the Eastern European Jewish heritage common among many Los Angeles Jewish families of the era.[12] Berman grew up in the Beverlywood neighborhood of Los Angeles alongside his younger brother, Michael Berman, who later became a prominent Democratic political consultant.[13][14] The family maintained a traditional Orthodox Jewish home, with Berman describing it as "pretty Orthodox" in his youth; they belonged to an Orthodox synagogue, and religious observance shaped early family life.[15] His father, a Polish immigrant, emphasized Orthodox practices, contributing to a culturally insular yet community-oriented upbringing typical of mid-20th-century Jewish immigrant-descended households in the area.[16] Berman attended local public schools, graduating from Hamilton High School in Los Angeles in 1959, where he developed interests in politics and public service amid the post-World War II suburban expansion of the Westside Jewish community.[17] This environment, marked by strong familial ties and communal institutions, fostered his early involvement in Democratic activism, including through groups like the Beverlywood Young Democrats.[18]

Academic pursuits and early influences

Berman received a Bachelor of Arts degree in international relations from the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) in 1962.[17] This undergraduate focus on international relations aligned with emerging global Cold War dynamics and U.S. foreign policy debates of the era, though specific coursework details remain undocumented in primary records.[19] He continued at UCLA School of Law, earning a Bachelor of Laws (LL.B.) in 1965, the standard pre-J.D. credential at the time for legal practice.[9] The LL.B. program emphasized constitutional law, civil procedure, and statutory interpretation, providing foundational skills for Berman's subsequent entry into California politics and legislative drafting.[20] Berman's early academic influences included active participation in the Bruin Democrats, UCLA's student Democratic organization, where he engaged in campus political activism during the early 1960s.[11] In 1960, as undergraduates, he formed a enduring friendship with Henry Waxman through these activities, fostering shared commitments to progressive Democratic ideals that later underpinned their joint political machine in California.[11] This student-era involvement introduced Berman to grassroots organizing and policy advocacy, diverting his pursuits from pure international affairs toward domestic legal and electoral strategies.[16]

Pre-political legal career

Legal education and bar admission

Berman earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in international relations from the University of California, Los Angeles in 1962.[17] He then attended the University of California School of Law at Los Angeles (now UCLA School of Law), receiving a Bachelor of Laws (LL.B.) in 1965, the standard degree designation at the time prior to the widespread adoption of the Juris Doctor (J.D.).[2] Following graduation, Berman was admitted to the State Bar of California on January 5, 1966.[21] His bar license remained active until at least 1981, after which it transitioned to inactive status, reflecting his shift toward full-time public service rather than private practice.[21] Prior to entering politics, he briefly engaged in legal practice in Los Angeles from 1967 to 1973, following a period as a VISTA volunteer in 1966â€“1967.[2]

Professional practice and notable cases

Following his graduation from the University of California School of Law in 1965, Howard Berman pursued a brief career in private legal practice. He initially volunteered with the Volunteers in Service to America (VISTA) program from 1966 to 1967, focusing on community service initiatives.[22] Berman then joined a Los Angeles law firm, where he practiced as a labor attorney from 1967 until 1973. His work emphasized representation in labor-related matters, aligning with his early involvement in Democratic politics and union-aligned activities during that era.[23][24] Public records do not highlight specific notable cases from this period, likely due to the brevity of his pre-political practice and its focus on routine labor disputes rather than high-profile litigation. Berman's legal tenure ended upon his successful campaign for the California State Assembly in 1972, after which he transitioned fully into elected office.[25]

California State Assembly service

Elections and initial victories

Howard Berman secured the Democratic nomination for California's 57th State Assembly district in the June 6, 1972, primary election, prevailing in a competitive field of nine candidates.[26] The district, encompassing parts of the San Fernando Valley in Los Angeles, had been held by Republican incumbent Charles Conrad since 1966.[27] In the general election on November 7, 1972, Berman defeated Conrad, along with Peace and Freedom Party candidate Grover Howard, to flip the seat. Berman received 65,290 votes, achieving victory amid a voter turnout of approximately 56.6%.[28][27] This win marked Berman's entry into state politics, reflecting strong support in a Democratic-leaning district amid broader shifts favoring Democrats in California that year.[29] Berman was sworn into the Assembly on December 4, 1972, beginning a decade of service in the district. He faced no serious challenges in subsequent elections through 1980, consistently securing re-election with wide margins that solidified his position as a rising Democratic leader.[28] These early victories established Berman's influence within the Assembly's Democratic caucus, paving the way for his rapid ascent to majority floor leader by 1974.[30]

Key legislative initiatives

During his tenure in the California State Assembly from 1973 to 1983, Howard Berman authored or played a pivotal role in advancing several significant pieces of legislation, particularly in labor rights, environmental conservation, and procedural reforms. One of his most prominent initiatives was the Agricultural Labor Relations Act (ALRA) of 1975, which established the Agricultural Labor Relations Board to oversee union elections and collective bargaining for farmworkers, marking the first state law granting agricultural employees such protections amid ongoing disputes between growers and unions like the United Farm Workers.[31][2] Berman also spearheaded the Santa Monica Mountains Conservancy Act of 1979 (Assembly Bill 1312), creating a state agency to acquire and preserve over 300,000 acres of open space in the Santa Monica Mountains, addressing urban sprawl and habitat loss in the Los Angeles region through public-private partnerships and bond funding.[9] Additional key bills under Berman's authorship included measures enhancing patient access to personal medical records, reforming the bail system to prioritize public safety over revenue generation, bolstering employee protections in non-agricultural sectors, and addressing environmental concerns such as pollution controls in industrial areas.[31] These initiatives reflected Berman's focus on balancing economic interests with worker and public welfare, often navigating opposition from business lobbies during his time as Majority Floor Leader from 1974 to 1980.[32]

Leadership positions and internal influence

Berman was appointed Majority Floor Leader of the California State Assembly in 1974, a position he held until 1980, during which he directed the Democratic majority's floor operations and legislative priorities.[31][33] In this capacity, he emerged as a pivotal internal figure, known for masterful procedural maneuvering and coalition-building that advanced key bills, including farm labor reforms and medical malpractice measures.[34] His influence extended through extensive fundraising efforts, often coordinated with his brother Michael Berman, which bolstered Democratic campaigns across the state and solidified his role as a de facto power broker within the caucus.[34] In late 1979, Berman challenged incumbent Speaker Leo McCarthy for leadership of the Assembly, citing concerns over McCarthy's fundraising for a potential U.S. Senate run and ties to Governor Jerry Brown that allegedly undermined party discipline.[35][36] Though unsuccessful in that bid, Berman continued exerting significant sway as a close ally of Brown, steering moderate Democratic votes on contentious issues and contributing to redistricting strategies that preserved party strongholds.[34] He assumed the role of Democratic Caucus Chairman in 1981, further amplifying his internal authority until his departure for Congress in 1982.[31] Berman's tenure highlighted his preference for behind-the-scenes leverage over public-facing roles, earning him a reputation as an "accomplished operator" who prioritized pragmatic deal-making amid factional tensions between liberal reformers and establishment Democrats.[34] This approach, while effective in passing legislation like the Agricultural Labor Relations Act, also drew criticism for entrenching insider dynamics that sidelined newer members.[2]

U.S. House of Representatives tenure

Elections and redistricting challenges

Berman was elected to represent California's 26th congressional district in the November 2, 1982, general election, defeating Republican Paul Peters after winning the Democratic primary with 83.2% of the vote.[37][2] He secured reelection in 14 succeeding terms through 2010, typically facing Republican challengers in general elections and winning with margins exceeding 70% in most cycles, such as 69.5% against Merlin Froyd in 2010.[9][38] Following the 1990 census, California's redistricting process sparked intense partisan battles, with Berman playing a central role in Democratic efforts to draw lines protecting incumbents in Los Angeles-area districts.[39] Proposed maps threatened to dismantle strongholds held by Berman and allies like Henry Waxman, prompting court interventions and negotiations that ultimately preserved his seat, renumbering it as the 28th district for the 1992 election.[40] Berman won the 1992 Democratic primary against challenger Jesse Rodriguez amid accusations of gerrymandering favoritism toward established politicians, then prevailed in the general with 62% of the vote.[41] The most consequential redistricting challenge for Berman arose after the 2010 census, when California's independent Citizens Redistricting Commission redrew boundaries to eliminate gerrymandering, merging parts of Berman's 28th district with Brad Sherman's 27th into the new 30th district and forcing the incumbents into direct competition.[42] Under the state's top-two primary system implemented in 2012, both advanced from the June 5 primaryâ€”Sherman with 35.2% (42,777 votes) and Berman with 33.4% (40,396 votes)â€”setting up an intra-party general election.[43] In the November 6 general election, Sherman defeated Berman 60.3% (149,456 votes) to 39.7% (98,395 votes), ending Berman's congressional career.[44] Despite outspending Sherman nearly 2-to-1 with over $5.7 million raised, Berman's campaign faltered amid voter preference for Sherman's local ties and criticisms of Berman's national focus and ethics issues.[45][46] The contest featured aggressive negative ads between the former allies, highlighting tensions over foreign policy and district priorities.[47]

Committee roles and assignments

Upon entering the U.S. House of Representatives in January 1983, Howard Berman was assigned to the Committee on the Judiciary, where he served continuously for his entire tenure until 2013.[9] As a senior member, he participated in key proceedings, including the 1998 impeachment inquiry against President Bill Clinton.[9] Berman chaired the Judiciary Committee's Subcommittee on Courts, the Internet, and Intellectual Property, overseeing legislation on patent reform, copyright enforcement, and digital rights issues.[48] [49] He also held membership on the Subcommittee on Immigration and Claims, which evolved into the Subcommittee on Immigration, Citizenship, Refugees, Border Security, and International Law, addressing topics such as asylum policies and border enforcement.[48] [9] In parallel, Berman served on the Committee on International Relationsâ€”renamed the Committee on Foreign Affairs in 2007â€”including as a member of its Subcommittee on the Middle East and South Asia (formerly Middle East and Central Asia).[48] He ascended to chair the full Foreign Affairs Committee during the 110th and 111th Congresses (2007â€“2011), following the death of Tom Lantos in 2008, during which he influenced authorizations for international aid, sanctions, and diplomacy in regions like the Middle East.[2] [50] In the subsequent 112th Congress, after Republicans assumed the majority, Berman served as ranking minority member.[51]

Foreign policy stances and votes

Berman chaired the House Committee on Foreign Affairs from 2007 to 2011, during which he prioritized legislation countering threats from Iran, bolstering alliances with Israel, and authorizing foreign assistance programs.[52] He introduced and advanced bills enhancing U.S.-Israel military ties, including H.R. 2480 in April 2009, which passed the House and authorized expanded joint exercises, intelligence sharing, and defense cooperation to address regional security challenges.[53] On major conflicts, Berman voted yes on October 10, 2002, for H.J. Res. 114, the Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Iraq, supporting President George W. Bush's request to enforce UN resolutions and address weapons of mass destruction threats.[54] He backed supplemental funding for operations in Iraq and Afghanistan, including the July 2010 war funding bill that passed the House 308-114, emphasizing sustained military efforts against terrorism.[55] Berman took a hawkish stance on Iran, spearheading new sanctions legislation in April 2009 amid nuclear concerns and regional meddling, while conditionally favoring normalized relations if Tehran made verifiable concessions on its nuclear program and support for militias.[56][57] He opposed Palestinian unilateral bids for statehood at the UN, such as the 2011 resolution push, arguing they bypassed direct negotiations and undermined peace prospects, and urged the U.S. to veto measures perceived as anti-Israel.[58][59] In broader policy, Berman defended foreign aid budgets against cuts, warning in January 2011 that Republican-proposed reductions risked U.S. national security by weakening diplomacy and counterterrorism capabilities in volatile regions.[60] As ranking member in 2012, he incorporated reforms into the Foreign Relations Authorization Act, including satellite security measures and aid oversight, though the full rewrite of the 1961 Foreign Assistance Act he proposed stalled.[61][62]

Domestic policy positions

Intellectual property and anti-piracy efforts

During his tenure in the U.S. House of Representatives, Howard Berman chaired the Subcommittee on Courts, the Internet, and Intellectual Property under the House Judiciary Committee, where he prioritized legislation to combat digital piracy and bolster copyright enforcement.[4] In this role, Berman advocated for measures enabling copyright holders to deploy technological countermeasures against unauthorized online distribution, reflecting the entertainment industry's push to address peer-to-peer (P2P) file-sharing networks that facilitated widespread infringement.[63] In July 2002, Berman introduced H.R. 5211, the Peer-to-Peer Piracy Prevention Act, which sought to immunize copyright owners from liability when using "reasonable" methodsâ€”such as disrupting file transmissions or interfering with P2P softwareâ€”to prevent the illegal sharing of protected works, without authorizing data alteration or system damage.[64] The bill targeted the proliferation of piracy on platforms like Napster successors, aiming to close legal gaps that left content creators vulnerable to technological circumvention of traditional enforcement.[65] Although it did not pass, it underscored Berman's focus on proactive, industry-friendly tools for digital rights management.[66] Berman also advanced international antipiracy initiatives, including hearings in 2007 on global IP theft and a 2009 proposal to integrate intellectual property enforcement into U.S. foreign policy, such as through dedicated State Department resources and trade negotiations.[67] Domestically, he co-sponsored the Prioritizing Resources and Organization for Intellectual Property (PRO-IP) Act of 2008 (H.R. 4279), which passed the House and established a White House Intellectual Property Enforcement Coordinator to streamline federal antipiracy efforts across agencies like the FBI and Department of Justice, while expanding investigative units and penalties for counterfeiting.[68] This legislation allocated additional funding for IP protection, including training programs and border seizures, to counter both domestic digital piracy and overseas manufacturing of infringing goods.[69] Additionally, Berman supported reforms like a 2005 bill with Representative Lamar Smith for mandatory blanket licensing of digital audio performances, designed to compensate artists amid rising online streaming and downloads by streamlining royalties through collective rights organizations.[70] His efforts consistently emphasized empirical evidence of economic losses from piracy, estimated in congressional testimony at billions annually for the U.S. creative sector, prioritizing causal links between lax enforcement and reduced incentives for content production.[71]

Immigration and border security views

Howard Berman supported comprehensive immigration reform legislation that paired pathways to legalization for undocumented immigrants with commitments to enhanced border enforcement. As a member of the House Judiciary Committee, he backed bills emphasizing family reunification, worker programs, and conditional status adjustments while advocating for increased border resources as part of broader packages rather than standalone enforcement measures.[72][73] In 1986, Berman voted in favor of the conference report on the Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA, S. 1200), which legalized approximately 3 million undocumented immigrants through amnesty provisions and imposed employer sanctions, though subsequent enforcement proved limited.[74] The act aimed to deter future illegal entries via sanctions but included no immediate border wall expansions or significant personnel increases, reflecting Berman's preference for reform balancing humanitarian and control elements.[75] Berman co-sponsored H.R. 6497, the DREAM Act of 2010, which sought to grant conditional permanent residency to undocumented youth meeting education or military service criteria, passing the House 216-98 on December 8, 2010.[76][77] He defended the measure against amendments tightening eligibility, arguing it addressed long-term integration without sufficient standalone border focus.[78] On border security, Berman endorsed reform frameworks incorporating infrastructure and manpower boosts, as in discussions tying legalization to federal commitments for fencing and patrols.[79] In 2007, amid House Democratic debates, he supported pairing immigration overhaul with security enhancements but opposed measures prioritizing deportation over regularization.[80] He participated in a 2009 White House meeting with President Obama to advance such comprehensive approaches.[81] Earlier, Berman critiqued restrictive legal immigration curbs, as in 1995 efforts to bifurcate bills separating legal from illegal flows, and 1996 opposition to GOP provisions complicating low-income family sponsorships under the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act.[82][83] His positions aligned with Democratic priorities favoring expanded legal channels and amnesty-like relief, often subordinating rigorous border enforcement to overall system modernization.[84]

Ethics and financial disclosures

In 2009, Berman amended his annual personal financial disclosure statement to report previously omitted capital gains from Exxon Mobil Corp. stock, estimated at a slight amount, as required under House ethics rules for reporting assets over $1,000.[85] No penalties were imposed, and such amendments were common for inadvertent omissions among members, though critics argued they highlighted lax initial compliance.[85] Berman's campaign committees filed regular reports with the Federal Election Commission (FEC), disclosing contributions and expenditures totaling over $1.5 million raised in his final 2011-2012 cycle, primarily from lawyers, real estate, and entertainment sectors aligned with his legislative focus on intellectual property.[86] His estimated net worth, based on disclosed assets like mutual funds and real estate, stood at approximately $2.5 million by 2012, reflecting typical accumulation for a long-serving member without reported irregularities beyond routine filings.[87] During the 2012 Democratic primary against Brad Sherman, Sherman's campaign filed an FEC complaint alleging that Berman's committees had overpaid Berman's brother, Michael Berman, and his consulting firm, Strategic Vision, for services from 2000 to 2010, potentially exceeding fair market value and violating contribution limits by funneling excess funds.[88][89] The FEC opened a Matter Under Review (MUR) into Michael Berman, but no public resolution found violations by Howard Berman himself, and the allegation appeared tied to the acrimonious redistricting-forced contest rather than prior enforcement actions.[90] Berman denied impropriety, asserting payments reflected standard consulting rates for redistricting and voter targeting expertise.[88] As ranking member of the House Ethics Committee from 1997 to 2003, Berman oversaw investigations into colleagues' financial and conduct issues, advocating for stricter travel disclosure rules while maintaining no personal probes against him during his tenure.[1] Overall, Berman faced no substantiated ethics violations or FEC fines, contrasting with more severe cases among peers, though detractors cited industry ties influencing policy as indirect ethical concerns without formal breach.[86]

Major controversies

Support for SOPA and PIPA backlash

Howard L. Berman, as chairman of the House Judiciary Subcommittee on Intellectual Property, Rights, and Internet, served as an original co-sponsor of the Stop Online Piracy Act (SOPA), introduced by Representative Lamar Smith on October 26, 2011, to combat foreign websites facilitating copyright infringement by authorizing domain name seizures and payment processor blocks. Berman defended the bill in public forums, including a January 7, 2012, town hall where he argued it targeted rogue sites without broadly censoring the internet, emphasizing protections for U.S. intellectual property against overseas piracy that cost industries billions annually.[91] He similarly backed the Senate's companion PROTECT IP Act (PIPA), viewing both as essential tools for rights holders, though critics contended the measures' provisions for DNS blocking and private right-of-action lawsuits would enable overreach, stifle innovation, and undermine free speech by allowing preemptive site blacklisting without due process.[92] Opposition to SOPA and PIPA escalated through late 2011 and early 2012, with tech firms including Google, Facebook, and Mozilla, alongside advocacy groups like the Electronic Frontier Foundation, decrying the bills as threats to an open internet; a coordinated blackout of major sites on January 18, 2012, amplified public outcry, prompting over 10 million emails to Congress and leading more than 100 House members to withdraw support.[93] Berman, dubbed "Hollywood's congressman" for his long ties to the entertainment sectorâ€”which donated over $1 million to his campaignsâ€”faced targeted criticism for prioritizing content industry interests over tech sector concerns, with detractors highlighting his receipt of significant contributions from motion picture and music groups amid the debate.[94] Despite the mounting pressure, Berman maintained SOPA was a "perfect document" calibrated to address piracy without unintended harms, refusing to amend core elements even as the bill stalled in January 2012.[95] The backlash directly influenced Berman's 2012 reelection bid, pitting him against fellow incumbent Brad Sherman in a redistricted California district with a tech-oriented electorate; Sherman, who opposed SOPA and voted against advancing it, leveraged the issue in debates, portraying Berman's stance as out of touch with internet users' preferences for minimal regulation.[92][96] In the June 5, 2012, Democratic primary, Sherman secured 57% of the vote to Berman's 43%, a margin analysts attributed partly to voter discontent with SOPA advocacy among younger, online-savvy demographics; Berman's subsequent general election loss on November 6, 2012, marked the end of his congressional tenure, with post-mortems noting SOPA as a factor in defeats for several bill sponsors.[97][46] Internet freedom advocates extended criticism beyond the election, opposing Berman's rumored consideration for Secretary of State in 2013 due to his role in the legislation, arguing it demonstrated disregard for global digital rights.[98]

2012 primary against Brad Sherman

The 2012 election for California's 30th congressional district pitted Democratic incumbents Howard Berman and Brad Sherman against each other following redistricting by the state's independent Citizens Redistricting Commission, which redrew boundaries after the 2010 census and combined substantial portions of Berman's 28th district and Sherman's 27th district into the new San Fernando Valley-based 30th district.[99] This matchup, one of only a few incumbent-versus-incumbent contests nationwide, unfolded under California's newly implemented top-two primary system, adopted via Proposition 14 in 2010, where the top two vote-getters in the June 5 primary advanced to the November general election regardless of party affiliation.[46] In the June 5 primary, Sherman received 46,606 votes (35.3 percent), edging out Berman's 42,693 votes (32.3 percent), with the remaining votes split among minor candidates, allowing both Democrats to advance in the heavily Democratic district.[97] The campaign escalated into one of the nation's most acrimonious, marked by heavy negative advertising and personal attacks; Sherman portrayed Berman as a Washington insider disconnected from local needs, accusing him of past involvement in gerrymandering to protect his seat, while Berman highlighted Sherman's volatile temperament and questioned his fitness for office based on prior public outbursts.[100] Spending reached record levels for a California House race, exceeding $9 million by October 2012 through candidate expenditures, outside groups, ads, robocalls, and mailers, with Berman benefiting from national Democratic leadership support including endorsements from House Minority Leader Nancy Pelosi and contributions from Hollywood donors, contrasted by Sherman's reliance on local grassroots funding and portrayal as a more independent, district-focused advocate.[101][102] Tensions peaked during an October 11, 2012, debate at Pierce College in Woodland Hills, where Sherman physically confronted Berman by grabbing his neck and pulling him into a headlock amid a dispute over campaign signs and microphone usage, an incident captured on video that drew widespread media attention and condemnation from Berman's camp as evidence of instability, though it did not derail Sherman's momentum.[103][104] In the November 6 general election, Sherman secured victory with 126,111 votes (59.7 percent) to Berman's 85,007 votes (40.3 percent), prompting Berman to concede the following day.[46][105] Sherman's win was attributed to the district's configuration favoring areas from his original territory, voter preference for his anti-establishment image over Berman's three-decade tenure, and effective mobilization of local Democratic clubs despite Berman's institutional backing.[47][106] This defeat marked Berman's sole electoral loss in a career spanning state assembly, city council, and 15 House terms.[47]

Allegations of gerrymandering and insider dealings

In the early 2000s, Howard Berman, alongside his brother Michael Berman, a prominent Democratic political consultant, played a central role in California's redistricting process following the 2000 census, which critics described as an incumbent-protection gerrymander designed to minimize competitive districts and safeguard Democratic seats.[107] Michael Berman, hired by nearly all 32 Democratic incumbents including Howard for a flat fee of $20,000 each to represent their interests in map-drawing negotiations, openly advocated for strategies that prioritized partisan advantage, stating in interviews that such tactics were essential to maintaining Democratic control in the state legislature and congressional delegation.[108] [109] This arrangement drew allegations of conflicts of interest, as Howard Berman, then a senior member of the House, benefited directly from districts engineered to preserve his own San Fernando Valley seat, which featured convoluted boundaries to exclude Republican-leaning areas while incorporating favorable Democratic voters.[110] Critics, including reform advocates, argued that the process exemplified systemic insider manipulation, though it was conducted under bipartisan agreement with Republicans to similarly protect their incumbents, resulting in only one competitive congressional district statewide by 2002.[111] The 2010 establishment of California's independent Citizens Redistricting Commission disrupted this model, redrawing the 28th and 30th districts in a way that pitted Berman against fellow Democrat Brad Sherman in the 2012 primary for the new 30th District, eliminating the safe seats both had previously enjoyed.[108] During the heated campaign, Sherman filed a complaint with the Federal Election Commission (FEC) on August 23, 2012, accusing Howard Berman of illegally overpaying his brother Michael's consulting firm, Berman & D'Agostino, for campaign services as a means to funnel excessive funds or evade contribution limits.[88] Specifically, the complaint highlighted payments totaling over $130,500 between 2005 and 2012, including $80,500 in 2008 for "campaign management" despite Berman facing no primary opponent, and alleged these fees far exceeded fair market value for the work performed, potentially violating FEC rules on excessive payments to relatives.[90] The FEC investigated the matter as part of MUR 6631, examining whether the payments constituted improper corporate contributions or circumventions of federal election laws, but ultimately closed the case in 2013 without finding violations by Howard Berman, determining the fees were for legitimate consulting services amid the lack of contemporaneous documentation.[112] Sherman, in turn, faced counter-allegations from Berman's campaign regarding high-interest loans Sherman made to his own committees, but these did not result in formal sanctions against either.[113] No criminal charges arose from the redistricting or payment disputes, though the episodes underscored broader concerns about family ties in political consulting, with Michael Berman's firm having received millions from Democratic campaigns over decades for redistricting and strategy work.[8]

Post-Congress activities

Lobbying and advisory roles

Following his departure from Congress in January 2013, Howard Berman joined the Washington, D.C. law firm Covington & Burling as a senior policy advisor, commencing on March 25, 2013.[114] Federal ethics rules barred him from direct lobbying for one year, restricting his initial role to advisory services for international clients on U.S. and European Union regulations, as well as domestic issues including tax policy, intellectual property, health care, and immigration.[114] Berman's advisory work encompassed foreign affairs, defense, national security, and intellectual property matters, drawing on his prior congressional experience.[115] After the lobbying prohibition expired in early 2014, he registered as a lobbyist at Covington & Burling, representing clients such as the Motion Picture Association of America (MPAA) beginning June 30, 2014.[10] His efforts for the MPAA focused on strengthening intellectual property protections to counter illegal piracy, which the organization quantified as inflicting over $6 billion in annual losses on the film industry.[10] Lobbying records document Berman's involvement with at least eight clients in 2020 alone, consistent with his firm's emphasis on policy advocacy in areas aligned with his expertise.[116] This transition positioned him among former lawmakers who pursued K Street roles shortly after leaving office, as noted in contemporaneous analyses of post-Congress career paths.[9]

Personal life

Family and relationships

Berman was born on April 15, 1941, in Los Angeles to a Jewish family.[33] His younger brother, Michael Berman (1942â€“2023), was a prominent Democratic political strategist and lobbyist who, alongside Berman and Henry Waxman, formed the core of the influential "Berman-Waxman machine" that shaped California Democratic politics for decades.[121] [122] Berman married Janis Gail Schwarz in 1979.[33] [123] Janis Berman, active in Democratic politics and supportive of her husband's career since the late 1970s, briefly served in a public affairs role under Los Angeles Mayor Richard Riordan in 1993 before resigning later that year.[124] [125] The couple has two daughters, Brinley and Lindsey.[33] [123]

Later years and health

After retiring from the U.S. House of Representatives on January 3, 2013, following his defeat in the 2012 Democratic primary election to Brad Sherman, Howard L. Berman returned to private life in California.[9][1] He has resided in Valley Village, California, since at least the early 2010s.[17] Berman, born April 15, 1941, turned 84 in 2025 and has not been the subject of public reports detailing significant health challenges or medical conditions in retirement.[9] His post-Congress engagements, including advisory work on foreign policy, suggest continued professional involvement without indications of debilitating health impediments.[115]
