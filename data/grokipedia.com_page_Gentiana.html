Gentiana (/ˌdʒɛntʃiˈeɪnə/)[2] is a genus of flowering plants belonging to the gentian family (Gentianaceae), the tribe Gentianeae, and the monophyletic subtribe Gentianinae. With over 300 species, it is considered a large genus. Gentians are notable for their mostly large trumpet-shaped flowers, which are often of an intense blue hue, though some are also bright yellow.[3] The type species of the genus is Gentiana lutea.[4] The genus name is a tribute to Gentius, an Illyrian king who may have discovered tonic properties in gentians.[5]

Habitat

Gentiana is a cosmopolitan genus occurring primarily in alpine and montane habitats in temperate regions of Asia, Europe, and the Americas. Some species also occur in northwestern Africa, eastern Australia, and New Zealand. They inhabit a variety of environments including meadows, forests, marshlands, and scree. They are annual, biennial, and perennial plants. Some are evergreen, others are not.[6]

Gentian

Uses

Many beverages are made with gentian root.[7] Gentiana lutea is used to produce gentian, a distilled beverage produced in the Alps and in the Auvergne.[8] Some species are harvested for the manufacture of apéritifs, liqueurs, and tonics. Gentian root is a common beverage flavouring for bitters. The soft drink Moxie contains gentian root.[9] The French apéritif Suze is made with gentian. Americano apéritifs contain gentian root for bitter flavoring.[10] It is an ingredient in the Italian liqueur Aperol. It is also used as the main flavor in the German after-dinner digestif called Underberg, and the main ingredient in Angostura bitters and Peychaud's Bitters. The principal bitter component of gentian root is gentiopicrin (also called gentiopicroside),[11] a glycoside. A 2007 paper by a Japanese group identified 23 compounds in fresh gentian root.[12] Gentiopicrin was absent from the fresh root, so it possibly developed during drying and storage of the root. The young plant and old leaves of at least one species, Gentiana scabra, are edible when cooked and have historically been used as a famine food when other food sources were scarce.[13]

Pharmacological uses

Great yellow gentian (Gentiana lutea) is used in herbal medicine for digestive problems, loss of appetite, inflammation, acid reflux, sinus infections, and hypertension,[14] Gentian has been shown to manage dyspepsia by eliciting cephalic responses that increase vascular resistance and reduce the workload of the heart during digestion.[15] Gentiana punctata leaves and roots have been used in traditional Austrian medicine internally and externally as liqueur or tea for disorders of the gastrointestinal tract, skin, locomotor system, liver, and bile, and for pediatric problems, fever, flu, rheumatism, and gout.[16] Gentiana purpurea, Gentiana punctata, and Gentiana pannonica are used to produce gentian schnapps, traditionally used as a digestive aid. In Ayurvedic medicine, the endangered herb Picrorhiza kurroa (Kutki) is traditionally used as a medical herb, but due to scarcity, it is often substituted with the Indian gentian Gentiana kurroo (Trayamana). Picrorhiza kurroa is also used in traditional Chinese medicine (as Picrorhiza scrophulariiflora).[17]

Symbolism

The gentian flower was used as the emblem of the Minamoto clan, one of the four great clans that dominated Japanese politics during the Heian period and went on to establish the first Shogunate in the aftermath of the Genpei War. It is the official flower (called Enzian) of the German-speaking community of Belgium.[19]

Species

General

Gentians have oppositely arranged leaves, sometimes in a basal rosette. The trumpet-shaped flowers are usually deep blue or azure but can be white, cream, yellow, or red. Many species are polymorphic concerning flower color, bearing flowers of different colors. Most flowers are pentamerous, with five lobes in the corolla and five sepals. A few species have four to seven flower parts. The corolla has folds called plicae between the lobes. The style is short or absent. The ovary is mostly sessile and has nectary glands.

Cultivation

Several gentian species may be found in cultivation, and are valued for the unusual intensity of their blue flowers. They have a reputation for being difficult to grow. All require similar conditions – moist, rich, free-draining soil with an acid to neutral pH. They include:[3]
  • G. acaulis
  • G. asclepiadea
  • G. paradoxa
  • G. septemfida
  • G. sino-ornata
In addition, the following cultivars, of mixed or uncertain parentage, have gained the Royal Horticultural Society's Award of Garden Merit:[23]
  • 'Blue Silk'
  • 'Strathmore'
