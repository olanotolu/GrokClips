Elizabeth MacDonald

Elizabeth MacDonald (born January 2, 1962) is an American financial journalist and television host renowned for her investigative reporting on corporate accounting scandals, tax policies, and IRS practices.[1][2] She currently anchors The Evening Edit weekdays at 5 PM ET on Fox Business Network, a role she assumed after joining the network in 2007 as stocks editor and contributor.[3] With over three decades in journalism, MacDonald previously served as a senior editor at Forbes, where she covered IRS operations, corporate fraud, and created the magazine's "World's 100 Most Powerful Women" list, and reported for The Wall Street Journal on front-page stories involving tax evasion and audits, including revelations about the Church of Scientology's IRS settlement and political misuse of IRS powers by the Kennedys.[3] Her investigative series on IRS abuses garnered national attention, contributing to taxpayer rights reforms and leading to her testifying before Congress on two occasions regarding IRS accountability and tax policy improvements.[3][4] MacDonald has received 14 journalism awards, including the Gerald Loeb Award for Distinguished Business Journalism for her collaborative series on IRS issues at Money magazine, as well as honors from the Society of Professional Journalists and the Newswomen's Club of New York.[3][5] She is also an author, notably of Skirting Heresy: The Life and Times of Margery Kempe (2014), and has contributed to publications like Worth and Money magazines on financial editing and tax topics.[3]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Elizabeth MacDonald was born on January 2, 1962, in Rockville Centre, New York, into a family of eight children.[6][7] Raised in this Long Island suburb, MacDonald grew up in an environment where education was prioritized, as evidenced by her mother, sister, and several nieces working as teachers.[1] The family maintains Irish heritage, which MacDonald has publicly acknowledged during discussions of cultural celebrations such as St. Patrick's Day.[8] Details of her early childhood remain limited, reflecting MacDonald's preference for privacy regarding personal family matters.[9]

Academic Background

Elizabeth MacDonald earned a Bachelor of Arts degree from Canisius College in Buffalo, New York, graduating in 1984.[10][2] Canisius College, a private Jesuit university founded in 1870, lists MacDonald as a member of the class of 1984 and has recognized her as a distinguished alumna for her achievements in financial journalism.[11] Multiple biographical accounts specify that she graduated with honors, though the specific field of study within the B.A. program is not detailed in available records.[12] No further postgraduate education is documented in professional profiles or institutional records.

Print Journalism Career

Initial Roles at Major Outlets

MacDonald began her print journalism career as a financial editor at Worth magazine, where she authored cover stories on financial topics.[3] She later covered the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and taxation issues for Money magazine, producing an award-winning investigative series on abusive tax shelters that highlighted complex financial schemes exploiting tax loopholes.[3][13] Transitioning to The Wall Street Journal, MacDonald focused on the Money & Investing section, reporting on stock market developments, corporate earnings, and accounting irregularities.[14] Her contributions included front-page articles, Heard on the Street columns analyzing market trends, editorials on economic policy, and Economic Beat pieces dissecting fiscal matters.[3] This period established her expertise in scrutinizing corporate practices and government financial oversight prior to her tenure at Forbes.[10]

Key Contributions at Forbes

During her tenure as senior editor at Forbes magazine starting in 2000, Elizabeth MacDonald specialized in covering stock market developments, corporate earnings reports, and accounting practices.[14] Her reporting emphasized scrutiny of financial irregularities, including an early 2001 analysis questioning the accounting methods of Irish pharmaceutical firm Elan Corporation, published months before the company's stock value dropped sharply amid related concerns.[15][16] MacDonald extended her investigative focus to the Internal Revenue Service (IRS), producing coverage that highlighted agency operational issues and taxpayer protections as part of an award-winning series on abuses, which influenced congressional reforms and her own testimony before U.S. Congress on two occasions.[3][4] This work built on her prior experience but aligned with Forbes' emphasis on fiscal accountability, contributing to broader discussions on IRS oversight and taxpayer rights enhancements.[17] A signature achievement was her creation and editorial oversight of Forbes' annual "The World's 100 Most Powerful Women" list, launched in 2004 to rank influential female figures in government, business, and nonprofit sectors based on metrics like economic impact and media reach.[18][3][4] The ranking, which she co-edited in subsequent years, provided data-driven profilesâ€”such as ranking German Chancellor Angela Merkel at number one in early editionsâ€”and established a recurring platform for evaluating global female leadership.[19][20] Her efforts earned recognition, including a Gerald Loeb Award for distinguished business and financial journalism in magazines.[21]

Transition to Television and Fox Business

Entry into Broadcast Media

MacDonald transitioned from print journalism to broadcast media in September 2007, when she joined the newly formed Fox Business Network (FBN) as its stocks editor.[3] This role marked her entry into on-air financial reporting, coinciding with FBN's launch on October 15, 2007, where she provided real-time coverage of stock market movements, earnings reports, and emerging business stories.[13] Her prior experience as a senior editor at Forbes magazine, focusing on corporate accounting and market analysis, equipped her to adapt her investigative skills to the faster-paced television format, though she later described the shift as demanding adjustments in presentation and immediacy.[22] The move required overcoming personal reservations about on-camera work; MacDonald has noted the challenge of prioritizing appearanceâ€”such as hair and makeupâ€”over the anonymity of print, while maintaining rigorous accuracy under broadcast scrutiny.[22] In her initial years at FBN, she expanded beyond stocks to report on broader economic issues, including Wall Street derivatives and health care reform, establishing herself as a regular contributor to programs like Varney & Co..[23] This period solidified her presence in television, leveraging her print-honed expertise in breaking news on fiscal matters to inform viewers amid the 2008 financial crisis.[13]

Development of The Evening Edit

The Evening Edit evolved from Fox Business Network's post-market programming, which launched on March 31, 2014, under the title Risk & Reward, initially hosted by Deirdre Bolton.[24] Elizabeth MacDonald, who joined Fox Business as stocks editor in September 2007, periodically filled in as host for Risk & Reward, leveraging her expertise in corporate accounting, taxes, and IRS coverage to deliver segments on economic risks and financial news.[3][25] In 2018, following Bolton's exit from the network, MacDonald became the permanent anchor, prompting a rebranding to The Evening Edit with Elizabeth MacDonald to reflect its emphasis on post-market analysis of daily headlines affecting investors and consumers.[26] This transition included updated motion graphics tailored for business news delivery, enhancing the program's visual focus on data-driven breakdowns of market impacts.[26] Under MacDonald's stewardship, the show shifted toward in-depth interviews with industry experts on fiscal policy, corporate developments, and personal wealth strategies, airing weekdays from 5:00 to 6:00 PM ET.[27] The program's format solidified its role in Fox Business's lineup, contributing to consistent ratings dominance over competitors like CNBC's Fast Money.[28] By November 2024, it averaged 210,000 total viewers, underscoring its appeal amid ongoing economic volatility.[29] In December 2022, The Evening Edit was integrated into a broader schedule revamp aimed at addressing key financial issues for American audiences, further refining its content to prioritize actionable insights over general market recaps.[30]

Notable Investigative Reporting

Early Warnings on Corporate Scandals

In the mid-1990s, MacDonald was among the earliest journalists to alert the public to an emerging pattern of aggressive accounting practices that would culminate in widespread corporate scandals, including those involving Enron and WorldCom in 2001â€“2002. Her reporting emphasized vulnerabilities in financial disclosures and auditor oversight, drawing on analyses of earnings management and off-balance-sheet entities that later proved central to these collapses.[13][31] At Forbes, MacDonald scrutinized specific companies' financial maneuvers ahead of their unraveling. In a September 17, 2001, article in Forbes Global, she raised doubts about Irish pharmaceutical firm Elan's funding of research through questionable structured vehicles and its reliance on non-standard accounting, prompting investor scrutiny just before the company's stock value dropped sharply amid revelations of overstated assets and hidden debt exceeding $1 billion.[15][16] This piece highlighted Elan's use of special-purpose entities to mask liabilities, a tactic echoed in larger scandals like Enron's. MacDonald's contributions extended to Enron coverage, where she co-authored pieces exposing the company's evasion of debt transparency. In one such report, she and Seth Lubove detailed how Enron shifted billions in obligations off its balance sheet via rapid-payoff swaps and partnerships, understating true leverage by over $13 billion as of mid-2001â€”disclosures that preceded the firm's bankruptcy filing on December 2, 2001.[32] Her work underscored systemic issues in GAAP compliance and auditor independence, influencing post-scandal reforms like the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002.[33]

Coverage of Taxes, IRS, and Fiscal Policy

MacDonald conducted an award-winning investigative series on IRS abuses during her time at Money and Worth magazines in the early 1990s, documenting inconsistent enforcement practices, taxpayer harassment, and systemic overreach that disadvantaged ordinary citizens.[3] This reporting highlighted how IRS agents wielded unchecked power, often leading to disparate treatment across regions and cases, as evidenced by her August 1994 Money article critiquing nationwide policy variances.[34] The series earned recognition from the Society of Professional Journalists and contributed to legislative pushes for accountability, including her congressional testimony on two occasions advocating for taxpayer bill of rights expansions and IRS restructuring.[4] These efforts influenced the IRS Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998, which implemented protections like the Office of the Taxpayer Advocate and limits on agent authority.[3] At The Wall Street Journal, MacDonald continued probing IRS operations, reporting on November 11, 1996, how lawmakers lambasted the agency for inadequate pursuit of housing subsidy fraud amid evidence of widespread abuse by developers and nonprofits.[35] She also exposed historical politicization, including a January 28, 1997, front-page story detailing the Kennedy administration's deployment of IRS audits against political foes, such as the Fair Play for Cuba Committee associated with Lee Harvey Oswald, illustrating patterns of executive branch weaponization transcending party lines.[36] Additionally, her investigations uncovered the Church of Scientology's clandestine 1993 settlement with the IRS, granting tax-exempt status after years of litigation, which she revealed through leaked documents and insider accounts.[3] Transitioning to Fox Business, MacDonald covered the 2013 IRS targeting scandal, where the agency delayed tax-exempt approvals for conservative groups using criteria like "Tea Party" affiliations, as confirmed by Treasury Inspector General audits; she hosted discussions questioning accountability gaps and potential White House involvement.[37] [38] Her reporting emphasized empirical evidence of bias, including Lois Lerner's admissions and destroyed emails, contrasting with mainstream outlets' initial downplaying.[39] On broader fiscal policy, MacDonald's work has scrutinized high-tax regimes' disincentives, such as her 2013 analysis of a White House CEO tax report that omitted effective rates paid by executives amid deductions, arguing it distorted debates on corporate contributions.[40] She has reported warnings from tax officials that confiscatory rates and wealth taxesâ€”projected to raise trillions but risking capital flightâ€”could precipitate economic stagnation akin to historical socialist experiments, drawing from her decades tracking IRS data.[41] These critiques prioritize revenue realities over redistributive ideals, citing empirical outcomes like reduced investment under prior hikes.[42]

Authorship and Other Works

Skirting Heresy: The Life and Times of Margery Kempe

Skirting Heresy: The Life and Times of Margery Kempe is a 192-page biographical work published by Franciscan Media on June 23, 2014.[43] The book centers on Margery Kempe (c. 1373â€“1438), an English mystic from Bishop's Lynn (modern King's Lynn) whose dictated The Book of Margery Kempe (completed around 1436â€“1438) is recognized as the first autobiography in English.[44] MacDonald portrays Kempe as a defiant figure who pursued intense religious devotion amid the perils of 15th-century England, where the 1401 statute De heretico comburendo imposed death by burning for heresy and ecclesiastical authorities prohibited unauthorized preaching, particularly by women.[45] MacDonald reconstructs Kempe's life trajectory, from her marriage to John Burnham, with whom she had 14 children, to her post-childbearing mystical conversion involving visions of Christ, uncontrollable weeping as a sign of piety, and a negotiated vow of celibacy that her husband eventually endorsed.[43] Key events detailed include Kempe's 1413 pilgrimage to Jerusalem, where she experienced heightened ecstasies leading to public disruptions; travels to Rome and Santiago de Compostela; and a meeting with the anchoress Julian of Norwich.[46] Despite multiple arrests and heresy trialsâ€”often triggered by her white attire symbolizing purity and her lay preachingâ€”Kempe evaded conviction through verbal agility and influential allies, embodying the title's theme of navigating doctrinal boundaries without crossing into outright heresy.[44] The narrative blends historical analysis with fictionalized elements, such as reconstructed dialogues and scenes, to evoke medieval England's social and religious strictures, where women held subordinate status under canon law and St. Paul's injunctions against female teaching.[46] MacDonald, drawing parallels to her own investigative journalism, emphasizes Kempe's role as a truth-teller challenging norms, supported by detailed research into primary sources like Kempe's own text.[45] Reviews commend the elegant, transporting prose and vivid contextualization but critique the hybrid biography-fiction format for lacking rigorous scholarly depth, potentially limiting appeal to non-experts seeking unadulterated history.[46]

Additional Publications and Contributions

MacDonald published the children's fantasy book Tideon: A New Myth in October 2020, illustrated by Bron Williams, which depicts a young boy's discovery of hidden magic and self-acceptance amid feelings of alienation.[47] The narrative draws on themes of wonder and resilience, diverging from her primary focus on financial journalism and historical biography.[48] In her tenure at Forbes magazine as a senior editor, MacDonald developed and oversaw the annual "World's 100 Most Powerful Women" ranking, launched in 2004, which evaluated global female leaders based on economic influence, media visibility, and impact across sectors like business, politics, and philanthropy.[3] This list became a widely referenced benchmark, appearing yearly and influencing discussions on gender and power dynamics in professional spheres.[49] Her investigative articles at Forbes and earlier outlets, including coverage of corporate governance and fiscal policy, earned her the Gerald Loeb Award for Distinguished Business and Financial Journalism in magazines, recognizing excellence in exposing accounting irregularities and tax-related issues.[14] MacDonald has accumulated 14 journalism honors overall, underscoring her role in advancing rigorous reporting on economic transparency.[10]

Political and Economic Views

Advocacy for Fiscal Conservatism

MacDonald has advocated for tax code simplification to reduce barriers to economic growth, arguing that the "lunatic complexity" of the U.S. tax system hampers business hiring and investment. In a 2012 analysis, she highlighted how the impending expiration of Bush-era tax cuts could exacerbate this issue, potentially acting as a "tax time bomb" that threatens job recovery by increasing compliance burdens and uncertainty for employers.[50] She has supported alternative tax structures, such as replacing the federal income tax with a national sales tax, describing the proposal as compelling for its potential to streamline revenue collection while broadening the tax base and eliminating distortions from income-based levies.[51] In discussions of fiscal policy, MacDonald has critiqued tax hikes as counterproductive, citing bipartisan studies from states like Maryland and Illinois where revenue increases following rate hikes ultimately led to economic slowdowns and shortfalls.[52] She has endorsed extending tax cuts, including expansions of zero-tax brackets for low-income earners, as a means to foster growth without expanding government revenue dependency.[53] On her program The Evening Edit, she has platformed economists like Art Laffer to underscore the dangers of unchecked government spending, which reached $38 trillion in national debt by 2019, warning of unsustainable fiscal paths that risk inflation and reduced private sector vitality.[54] MacDonald extends her fiscal conservatism to scrutiny of government waste, labeling issues like USAID fraudâ€”uncovered by inspectors general, GAO reports, and congressional probesâ€”as emblematic of broader inefficiencies requiring oversight and cuts.[55] She has supported initiatives to review and eliminate such abuses, aligning with efforts to prioritize spending restraint over expansion, as seen in her coverage of IRS targeting scandals that disproportionately burdened conservative nonprofits seeking tax-exempt status.[56] This stance reflects a consistent emphasis on accountability and limited government intervention to maintain balanced budgets and economic stability.

Critiques of Government Overreach and Media Bias

MacDonald has extensively critiqued government overreach through her investigative reporting on Internal Revenue Service (IRS) abuses, highlighting instances where the agency targeted political opponents and violated taxpayer rights. Her award-winning series exposed systemic misconduct, including the IRS's secret settlement with the Church of Scientology in 1993 and audits of groups linked to political enemies, such as the Fair Play for Cuba Committee associated with Lee Harvey Oswald under the Kennedy administration.[3][57] These revelations contributed to congressional reforms that strengthened taxpayer protections and curtailed IRS politicization.[3] She testified before the House Ways and Means Committee on two occasions regarding IRS abuses and proposed reforms, emphasizing the need for structural changes like enhanced oversight to prevent future targeting of citizens based on political affiliations.[58] In recent years, MacDonald has opposed expansions of IRS authority, arguing they enable further bureaucratic intrusion into private finances amid unchecked federal spending. She criticized the allocation of $80 billion in the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act to hire approximately 87,000 additional IRS personnel, including auditors, as a means to intensify scrutiny on ordinary taxpayers rather than addressing elite tax evasion or waste.[59][60] MacDonald highlighted risks from IRS data access, citing the 2023 case of contractor Charles Littlejohn, who leaked sensitive tax records of high-profile figures, as evidence of vulnerabilities in an overexpanded agency prone to abuse.[60] Her commentary frames such expansions as symptomatic of broader fiscal irresponsibility, where trillions in government waste precede demands for increased enforcement on individuals.[61] Regarding media bias, MacDonald has accused major outlets of partisan slant that distorts coverage of economic and governmental issues, often prioritizing narratives over factual scrutiny. In 2019, she described The New York Times as partisan following a leaked transcript of its editor admitting heavy focus on President Trump while sidelining other stories, contrasting it with Fox Business's balanced reporting on fiscal matters.[62] She has argued that mainstream media's selective emphasis amplifies government-friendly views, such as downplaying IRS scandals or overspending, while thriving on sensationalism that benefits political elites over public accountability.[63] This perspective aligns with her broader advocacy for rigorous, data-driven journalism to counter institutional biases in reporting on taxpayer burdens and regulatory excess.

Controversies and Criticisms

Responses to Accusations of Partisanship

MacDonald has been accused of partisanship by left-leaning media watchdogs, such as Media Matters for America, which critiqued her 2021 interview with former Trump chief of staff Mark Meadows as obscuring debates over Republican leadership loyalty in favor of Trump-aligned views.[64] Similar claims arise from her association with Fox Business, a network frequently labeled conservative by outlets like The Washington Post, particularly when she highlights fiscal policy critiques or IRS controversies involving Democratic figures.[65] In response, MacDonald points to her pre-Fox career at Reuters, Forbes, and The Wall Street Journal, where she conducted independent investigations into corporate scandals and government audits without partisan framing, earning the Gerald Loeb Award for distinguished business journalism in recognition of fact-driven reporting.[3] Supporters, including Forbes contributors, describe her as an outlier in New York media for resisting left-leaning currents through rigorous, evidence-based analysis rather than ideological alignment.[66] She has also demonstrated cross-aisle scrutiny, such as criticizing former President Trump's lack of civility in public discourse, underscoring a commitment to accountability over party loyalty.[67] These defenses emphasize that her work prioritizes empirical documentation, like exposÃ©s on IRS political targeting under the Clinton administration, over narrative-driven bias.[3]

Public Statements on Social and Border Issues

MacDonald has criticized perceived weaknesses in border security policies, particularly those under the Biden administration, framing them as contributors to national security risks and fiscal strain on American taxpayers. In an October 18, 2024, Fox Business article authored by her, she detailed how federal and state actions have expanded Medicaid eligibility for undocumented immigrants in hospital settings, citing data from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services showing increased uncompensated care costs exceeding $40 billion annually, which she argued incentivizes illegal crossings and diverts resources from citizens.[68] On her program The Evening Edit, she has amplified concerns about the migrant crisis, including a January 4, 2024, interview with Texas Gov. Greg Abbott, who described open border policies as "destroying" the country through overwhelmed infrastructure and crime spikes linked to over 8 million encounters since 2021 per U.S. Customs and Border Protection data.[69] In April 2019, MacDonald directly commented on international figures' stances, stating on Fox Business that Pope Francis was "weak" on building a border wall and confronting socialism, in response to the pontiff's remarks downplaying physical barriers amid rising Central American migration.[70] She has also hosted discussions underscoring enforcement priorities, such as a September 15, 2023, segment with Rep. Jeff Van Drew (R-N.J.), who asserted that sanctuary cities are reaping the consequences of lax federal policies, with FBI data indicating over 13,000 migrant-related arrests for serious crimes in fiscal year 2023.[71] Additionally, in November 2024 episodes, MacDonald questioned incoming border czar Tom Homan on deportation plans, defending targeted removals of criminal non-citizens while clarifying that legal immigrants remain protected, countering media claims of broad overreach.[72] Regarding broader social issues, MacDonald's commentary has been less frequent and more indirect, often through guest interviews critiquing progressive cultural shifts rather than explicit personal positions on topics like abortion or gender policies. For instance, in a June 3, 2023, Evening Edit appearance, she featured actor Kirk Cameron discussing his library story hours aimed at promoting traditional family values and "waking parents up from the woke matrix," highlighting parental pushback against school curricula perceived as undermining parental authority.[73] Such segments align with her broader conservative lens but lack detailed articulation of her own views on reproductive rights or identity politics, focusing instead on their intersections with economic and security concerns.

Personal Life and Philanthropy

Military Service Mentorship

Elizabeth MacDonald serves as a mentor to post-9/11 military veterans through the American Corporate Partners (ACP) program, a nonprofit initiative that pairs corporate executives with transitioning service members and their spouses for personalized career guidance.[74][75] ACP, established in 2008, facilitates one-on-one virtual mentorships focusing on skills such as resume building, networking, leadership development, and industry-specific advice, with mentors committing to at least 12 months of engagement including six sessions annually. MacDonald's participation leverages her over three decades of experience in financial journalism, including investigative reporting at Forbes magazine from 1997 to 2006 and her current role anchoring The Evening Edit on Fox Business Network since 2018, to assist veterans in adapting military-acquired competencies to civilian sectors like media, finance, and business operations.[3] This mentorship role aligns with ACP's reported outcomes, where 90% of veteran mentees indicate improved career confidence and 80% secure new employment or promotions within a year of program completion, based on internal surveys. MacDonald has consistently highlighted her involvement in professional bios across platforms, emphasizing support for veterans' reintegration amid broader economic challenges, though specific details on her mentees or session topics remain private to protect participant confidentiality. Her efforts reflect a targeted commitment to veteran empowerment, distinct from her journalistic coverage of military policy and veteran entrepreneurship on Fox Business.[76]

Involvement in Charitable Initiatives

MacDonald serves as a board member and advocate for The Beautiful Lives Project, a nonprofit organization that empowers individuals with disabilities by creating inclusive opportunities in sports, arts, and community events to foster confidence, break barriers, and combat social isolation.[77][74] The initiative partners with sports teams, companies, and local groups to host immersive, free events enabling participantsâ€”often children and adults with physical or mental challengesâ€”to actively engage in activities previously inaccessible, such as playing alongside professional athletes to build self-esteem.[78][79] In her role, which includes serving as an ambassador at large, MacDonald has actively promoted the organization's efforts through public endorsements and media exposure, including featuring co-founder Bryce Weiler on her Fox Business program The Evening Edit to highlight the project's impact.[80][81] She has spotlighted specific events, such as the second annual Field of Dreams collaboration with the Albany Firebirds, which provided football field experiences for 100 individuals with disabilities.[82] MacDonald has described the project's mission as essential for helping participants "feel beautiful inside and out," aligning her advocacy with its goal of sparking meaningful community integration.[83]
