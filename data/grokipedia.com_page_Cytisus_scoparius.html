Cytisus scoparius

Cytisus scoparius (syn. Sarothamnus scoparius), the common broom or Scotch broom, is a deciduous leguminous shrub native to western and central Europe.[1] In Great Britain and Ireland, the standard name is broom;[2][3] this name is also used for other members of the Genisteae tribe, such as French broom or Spanish broom; and the term common broom is sometimes used for clarification.[5][6] In other English-speaking countries, the most common name is "Scotch broom" (or Scots broom);[7] however, it is known as English broom in Australia.[8] Though this plant is native to Europe, it has spread to many other parts of the world. Scotch broom is now common in certain areas of North America and South America. This is because people started introducing Scotch broom to different areas of the world. After it was introduced in North America, Scotch broom was frequently planted in gardens. Later, it was used for erosion control along highway cuts and fills.[9] There are other problems with Scotch broom as well. It is slightly toxic and unpalatable to livestock.[10] It also has seeds that are viable in the soil for 30â€“80 years, making them able to grow even after years of extermination.[11]

Description

Plants of C. scoparius typically grow to 1â€“3 metres (3+1â„2â€“10 feet) tall, rarely to 4 m (13 ft), with main stems up to 5 centimetres (2 inches) thick, rarely 10 cm (4 in). Stems are ridged and green.[13] The shrubs have green shoots with small deciduous trifoliate leaves 5â€“15 millimetres (1â„4â€“5â„8 in) long, and in spring and summer are covered in profuse golden yellow flowers 20â€“30 mm (3â„4â€“1+1â„8 in) from top to bottom and 15â€“20 mm wide. Flowering occurs after 50â€“80 growing degree-days. The seed pods have long hairs only along their seams.[13] In late summer, its legumes (seed pods) mature black, 2â€“3 cm (3â„4â€“1+1â„4 in) long, 8 mm (3â„8 in) broad and 2â€“3 mm thick; they burst open, often with an audible crack, forcibly throwing seed from the parent plant. This species is adapted to Mediterranean and coastal climates, and its range is limited by cold winter temperatures. It also adapts to windy oceanic climates. The seeds, seedlings, and young shoots are sensitive to frost; adult plants are hardier, and branches affected by freezing temperatures regenerate quickly.[2][14] C. scoparius contains toxic alkaloids that depress the heart and nervous system. A legume, this shrub can fix nitrogen in the soil through a symbiotic relationship with Rhizobium bacteria.

Phytochemicals

One of the main alkaloids of this plant is cytisine. The characteristic constituents are biogenic amines (mostly tyramine in the young shoots), flavonoids (spiraeoside and scoparoside), isoflavones and their glycosides (genistin), as well as allelopathic quinolizidine alkaloids (mostly sparteine, lupanine, scoparin and hydroxy-derivatives), which defend the plant against insect infestation and herbivores (with the exception of the resistant aphid species Aphis cytisorum).[16][17]

Classification

There are four recognized subspecies of Cytisus scoparius:[1][2][18]
  • Cytisus scoparius subsp. scoparius - throughout most of the species' range in Europe, including Britain and Ireland; erect or arching branches 150â€“200(â€“250) cm tall, glabrous or sparsely sericeous leaves and twigs.
  • Cytisus scoparius subsp. maritimus (Rouy) Heywood - western European maritime cliffs and associated environments, including Britain and Ireland; procumbent growth up to 40(â€“50) cm tall; densely silky sericeous leaves and twigs.
  • Cytisus scoparius subsp. insularis (S.Ortiz & Pulgar) Auvray - endemic to islands off northwest Spain (Isla de Ons and Isla Salvora).
  • Cytisus scoparius subsp. reverchonii (Degen & Hervier) Rivas Goday & Rivas Mart. - central and southeastern Spain.

Distribution and habitat

Cytisus scoparius is native to northern Africa, western and central Europe,[1] being common in Great Britain and Republic of Ireland.[19][20] It is found in sunny sites, usually on dry, sandy soils at low altitudes, tolerating very acidic soil conditions.[2] It has been widely introduced and naturalized outside its native range, particularly in temperate regions of North America, South America, Australia, and New Zealand.[11]

Ecology

As an invasive species

Cytisus scoparius has been introduced into several other countries and continents, outside of its native range, and is commonly classified as a noxious invasive species in western North America, mainly in British Columbia (including on Vancouver Island), California, Oregon, Washington (west of the Cascades), the Sierra Nevada range,[21] fragmented areas of North America's eastern seaboard,[22] as well as Australia (where it is a declared weed),[23][24] New Zealand,[25] and in India.[26] These shrubs are sometimes utilized for civic and public landscaping projects in the American west due to their hardiness, and will seasonally reseed themselves, growing in disturbed areas, and along utility and transportation rights-of-way. The prolific growth of the plants after timber harvest or wildfire events clearly inhibits reforestation efforts, the sprouts competing with native plant seedlings and trees.[27] It is estimated that broom is responsible for $39.5 million in lost personal income annually (as of 2025) in the state of Oregon.[28] In New Zealand, broom is estimated to cost the forestry industry NZ$90 million, and to cost farmers an additional NZ$10 million in losses.[29] Biological control for broom has been investigated since the mid-1980s, with a number of insect species being tested. Some of the tested invertebrates include the broom twig miner (Leucoptera spartifoliella), broom seed beetles (Bruchidius villosus), broom gall mites (Aceria genistae), sap-sucking broom psyllids (Arytainilla spartiophila), the Scotch broom seed weevil (Exapion fuscirostre), the broom leaf beetle (Gonioctena olivacea) and broom shoot moths (Agonopterix assimilella).[30][31] In North America, three biological control agents have been released: the broom seed beetle (Bruchidius villosus), the Scotch broom seed weevil (Exapion fuscirostre), and the broom twig miner (Leucoptera spartifoliella).[11]

Eradication

The method used to remove broom is dependent on its prolific seed cycle. Care should be taken to avoid disturbing (aerating, loosening, etc.) the adjacent ground, the sprouting and seedling plants between late spring and mid-fall; late fall through winter (possibly to mid-spring) are preferred times to eradicate mature plants.[32] There are several methods, cutting, pulling, burning, herbicide or introducing chickens and goats.[33] Drought areas respond well to cutting while the seed pods are young and still green. In cooler, wetter areas pulling is the preferred method, and hand-operated broom pullers are available.[34] Low temperature fires, such as a grass fire, will increase seed germination and new sprouts may form on the burned stumps of mature broom. A spring fire followed by drought conditions will reduce seedling survival.[35] Often new plants will grow from roots or seed, requiring repeated treatments.

Cultivation

Cytisus scoparius is widely cultivated as an ornamental plant, with several cultivars selected for variation in flower colour, including "Moonlight" with pale yellow flowers[36], "Andreanus" with yellow flowers and red wings[37], and "Firefly" with golden yellow flowers and reddish-bronze wings, and growth habit, including "Pendula" with pendulous branchlets.[14] However, due to its invasive potential in regions outside its native range, such as parts of North America, cultivation is discouraged in favor of non-invasive alternatives.[9]

Scotch Broom

Uses

Historically, broom has been used in traditional medicine. It contains scoparin, a glucoside credited with diuretic properties. The plant was also employed as a cathartic and cardiac stimulant, attributed to the alkaloid sparteine.[39][40] A decoction or infusion of broom was used to treat dropsy (edema) due to its purported diuretic action.[41] An ointment made from the flowers was applied to treat gout.[42] Oxysparteine, a derivative produced from sparteine by acid action, was used as a cardiac stimulant and noted for not accumulating in the body like digoxin.[40] Additionally, a medicinal beer known as broom ale was brewed from the herb as a remedy for dropsy.[43] However, Scotch broom is considered toxic and unsafe for medicinal use. It contains alkaloids such as sparteine that can cause serious adverse effects, including heart problems, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and potentially life-threatening poisoning. Long-term use may lead to neurological symptoms like twitching and hyperreflexia. It is contraindicated during pregnancy due to risks of uterine contractions. Modern medical authorities do not recommend its use, and it should only be handled with caution or avoided altogether.[44][45][46]

Culture

In Welsh mythology, Blodeuwedd is the name of a woman made from the flowers of broom, meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria) and the oak by Math fab Mathonwy and Gwydion to be the wife of Lleu Llaw Gyffes. Her story is part of the Fourth Branch of the Mabinogi, the tale of Math son of Mathonwy.[42] Broom was considered a sign of plenty when it bore many flowers.[47] However a traditional rhyme from Sussex warns: "Sweep the house with blossomed broom in May/sweep the head of the household away."[42] Broom was also used in a decorated bundle of broom at weddings in place of rosemary when that was scarce,[47] and its strong smell was said to be able to tame wild horses and dogs.[48] In Italy, the shrub was burnt with the intent of stopping witches.[42]

Royal symbols

The name of the House of Plantagenet, rulers of England in the Middle Ages, may have been derived from common broom, which was then known as planta genista in Latin.[49]: 9 [50]: 1 The plant was used as a heraldic badge by Geoffrey V of Anjou and five Plantagenet kings of England as a royal emblem.[51] The broomscod , or seed-pod, was the personal emblem of Charles VI of France.[52]
