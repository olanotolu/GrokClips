Howards End (TV series)

Howards End is a British-American television miniseries that adapts E.M. Forster's 1910 novel of the same name, examining social class divisions in Edwardian England through the interactions of three families: the affluent Wilcoxes, the cultured Schlegel sisters, and the struggling Basts.[1][2] The four-episode series, written by Kenneth Lonergan and directed by Hettie Macdonald, premiered on BBC One in the United Kingdom on 29 March 2017 and on Starz in the United States on 8 April 2018 as a co-production between the two networks.[3][1] It features Hayley Atwell as Margaret Schlegel, Matthew Macfadyen as Henry Wilcox, Philippa Coulthard as Helen Schlegel, and Alex Lawther as Leonard Bast, with supporting performances by Julia Ormond, Eleanor Bron, and Joseph Quinn.[4][5] Critics praised the production for its strong ensemble acting, period-accurate costumes, and cinematography, earning a 94% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 69 reviews.[6][3] The series received nominations including a Golden Nymph for Hayley Atwell at the Monte-Carlo TV Festival and won the South Bank Sky Arts Award for Best TV Drama.[7][8]

Synopsis

Plot Overview

Howards End is a four-part British television miniseries that unfolds in Edwardian England, centering on the intersecting lives of three families: the cultured, half-German Schlegel sisters Margaret and Helen; the affluent, pragmatic Wilcoxes; and the struggling lower-class Basts. The story initiates in 1905 when Helen Schlegel, during a visit to the Wilcoxes' country home Howards End following a holiday acquaintance, impulsively pursues a romance with the younger son Paul, only to face rejection that complicates family ties.[9][10] Margaret develops a profound friendship with Ruth Wilcox, the matriarch, who privately bequeaths the symbolic Wilcox estate Howards End to her in her will, an act that shocks the family upon revelation after Ruth's death from cancer.[10] This inheritance draws Margaret deeper into the Wilcox orbit, culminating in her marriage to the widowed Henry Wilcox despite warnings from Helen about the family's emotional reserve. Meanwhile, the Schlegels encounter Leonard Bast, a sensitive insurance clerk, and his wife Jacky; Margaret and Helen advocate for him, but Henry's inadvertent career adviceâ€”advising Bast to leave his firm for a riskier one that subsequently failsâ€”precipitates Bast's financial ruin.[10][11] Tensions escalate as Helen, disillusioned with class inequities exacerbated by the Wilcoxes' influence on the Basts, withdraws temporarily to Germany before returning amid family crises, including the Schlegels' eviction from their London home. A wedding gathering exposes Henry's prior infidelity, testing Margaret's resolve, while her refusal to condemn him underscores personal ethical dilemmas. The narrative arcs toward a climactic gathering at Howards End, where revelations and confrontations among the families highlight enduring conflicts over property, morality, and social barriers.[10][12]

Cast and Characters

Main Characters

Margaret Schlegel (Hayley Atwell) serves as the series' central protagonist, depicted as a passionate, contradictory, intelligent, and idealistic woman ahead of her time, who navigates complex social dynamics by forming an unlikely romantic and marital bond with the widowed Henry Wilcox following her friendship with his late wife.[13][4] Helen Schlegel (Philippa Coulthard) is Margaret's younger sister, characterized as youthful, impulsive, headstrong, and passionately idealistic with strong romantic notions, whose encounter with Leonard Bast at a concert sparks pivotal class-crossing entanglements that influence the Schlegel family's interventions in the lives of others.[13][4] Henry Wilcox (Matthew Macfadyen) embodies the pragmatic, conservative, and wealthy patriarchal businessman of the Wilcox family, whose traditional Edwardian values and athletic demeanor are progressively challenged by his evolving emotional attachment to Margaret amid familial and societal pressures.[13][4] Leonard Bast (Joseph Quinn) represents the struggling lower-middle-class insurance clerk with intellectual aspirations and a poetic sensibility, driven by a desperate desire to transcend his impoverished origins through cultural pursuits, only to become ensnared in the Schlegels' and Wilcoxes' worlds with tragic consequences.[13][4] Tibby Schlegel (Alex Lawther) is the youngest Schlegel sibling, portrayed as an over-indulged, hypochondriac academic immersed in intellectual studies at Oxford, often detached from the more active emotional conflicts of his sisters while providing a contrasting lens of scholarly non-action.[13][4]

Supporting and Guest Roles

Joseph Quinn portrayed Leonard Bast, the struggling lower-middle-class clerk whose ill-fated connections to the Schlegel sisters underscore themes of class disparity and social mobility in Edwardian England.[13][5] Julia Ormond played Ruth Wilcox, the reserved and ailing wife of Henry Wilcox, whose attachment to the family estate Howards End influences the narrative's resolution following her death in 1907.[4][14] Tracey Ullman depicted Aunt Juley (Mrs. Margaret Mund), the Schlegel sisters' fretful and meddlesome relative who provides comic relief and familial support amid the protagonists' upheavals.[4] Joe Bannister appeared as Charles Wilcox, Henry Wilcox's ambitious eldest son, whose impulsive actions contribute to the story's tragic elements, including a fatal confrontation in 1910.[15] Jonah Hauer-King took the role of Paul Wilcox, the younger Wilcox son whose brief romantic entanglement with Helen Schlegel in Germany sparks early conflicts over class and propriety.[16] Rosalind Eleazar portrayed Jacky Bast, Leonard's common-law wife, whose past ties to Henry Wilcox reveal hypocrisies in the upper class and exacerbate the Basts' misfortunes.[17] Guest roles included minor characters such as Evie Wilcox's suitor and various society figures, often filled by actors like Bessie Carter as Evie Wilcox in select episodes, emphasizing the Wilcoxes' social circle without central plot advancement.[15]

Production

Development

The development of the Howards End television miniseries began in October 2013, when BBC announced that Academy Award-nominated screenwriter and playwright Kenneth Lonergan had been attached to adapt E.M. Forster's 1910 novel into a four-part series.[18] Lonergan, known for works such as You Can Count on Me (2000) and Manchester by the Sea (2016), was selected for his ability to explore complex interpersonal dynamics and social themes, aligning with Forster's narrative of class intersections in Edwardian England.[19] By December 2015, BBC One formally commissioned the project, greenlighting production under the oversight of producer Colin Callender through his company Playground Entertainment.[19] This followed a period of scripting and refinement, positioning the adaptation as a prestige drama to capture the novel's examination of social mobility, inheritance, and cultural clashes without prior screen versions dominating recent memoryâ€”the last major adaptation being the 1992 Merchant Ivory film.[19] The commissioning emphasized fidelity to Forster's text while allowing Lonergan to incorporate contemporary resonances in dialogue and pacing for television format.[20] In early 2017, BBC and U.S. network Starz confirmed a co-production partnership, expanding the project's international reach and budget for period authenticity.[20] This collaboration, announced alongside initial casting details, built on Playground's prior successes in literary adaptations and aimed to deliver a visually rich series set against early 20th-century backdrops.[21] Development concluded with finalized scripts emphasizing character-driven storytelling, paving the way for principal photography later that year.[20]

Casting

The principal roles in the 2017 BBC One and Starz miniseries Howards End were filled by established actors, with announcements beginning on 15 February 2017. Hayley Atwell, known for portraying Peggy Carter in the Marvel Cinematic Universe, was cast as the idealistic and pragmatic Margaret Schlegel.[22][4] Matthew Macfadyen, previously starring as Mr. Darcy in the 2005 film adaptation of Pride and Prejudice, took the role of the conservative businessman Henry Wilcox.[22][4] Tracey Ullman, a comedian and actress recognized for The Tracey Ullman Show, was selected as the fussy Aunt Juley Mund.[22][4] Further casting was revealed on 17 February 2017, including Philippa Coulthard as the impulsive Helen Schlegel, Julia Ormond as the gentle Mrs. Wilcox, and Joseph Quinn as the aspiring clerk Leonard Bast.[5][4] Additional supporting roles featured Alex Lawther as the intellectual Tibby Schlegel, Joe Bannister as Charles Wilcox, and Rosalind Eleazar as Jacky Bast.[4] The ensemble drew from performers with experience in period dramas and literary adaptations, emphasizing contrasts in class and temperament central to E. M. Forster's novel.[20]

Filming

Principal photography for the four-part miniseries commenced in summer 2016 and occurred across multiple sites in England, substituting Surrey and Dorset landscapes for the novel's Hertfordshire settings to evoke the Edwardian countryside.[23][24] Urban scenes centered on London, with Myddleton Square in Clerkenwell serving as the exterior for the Schlegel sisters' Wickham Place residence.[25][26] Other London locations included Albert Embankment for riverside sequences.[27] In Surrey, the production utilized Hambledon properties near Godalming: an expansive house for the Wilcox family's Oniton Grange and the timber-framed Vann estate, dating to the 16th century, as the titular Howards End farmhouse.[28][29][25] Dorset provided coastal exteriors, including Harry Warren House on Studland Bay for beachside moments, Swanage Pier, and Ballard Down for rural walks and panoramic shots.[28][27]

Adaptation and Themes

Fidelity to E.M. Forster's Novel

The 2017 BBC and Starz miniseries adaptation of Howards End, scripted by Kenneth Lonergan, remains largely faithful to E.M. Forster's 1910 novel in its core plot structure, character arcs, and exploration of class divisions among the intellectual Schlegel sisters, the pragmatic Wilcox family, and the struggling Basts.[24] The narrative follows the novel's key events, including Margaret Schlegel's marriage to Henry Wilcox, Helen Schlegel's affair with Leonard Bast, and the symbolic inheritance of Howards End as a nexus of social connection ("only connect") versus disconnection.[3] Period details, dialogue drawn from the source, and the Edwardian setting are rendered with precision, preserving Forster's critique of liberal hypocrisy and economic inequality.[24] However, the series diverges by incorporating racial diversity absent from the novel, casting supporting roles such as Jacky Bastâ€”a character depicted as white and working-class in Forster's textâ€”as a black woman played by Rosalind Eleazar, alongside other non-white figures like an Indian doctor and a black maid.[3][30] These additions layer a contemporary racial dimension onto the class-focused original, linking themes of inheritance and exclusion to modern immigration and ethnic marginalization in England, which Forster does not address.[30] While enhancing timeliness for 21st-century viewers, this alters the novel's historical specificity, shifting emphasis from purely socioeconomic barriers to intersectional ones.[3] The adaptation also condenses and streamlines Forster's intricate, subtle interconnections, employing montage, time jumps spanning weeks to years, and a more fable-like tone that reduces logistical depth and tragic disconnection in favor of sentimental progression.[3] Relationships, such as that between Margaret and the dying Mrs. Wilcox, receive expanded development for dramatic credibility, diverging from the novel's terser prose.[30] These modifications prioritize visual and emotional accessibility over the source's nuanced irony, though the series retains fidelity to enduring motifs like women's social constraints and the fragility of cross-class empathy.[24]

Thematic Interpretations and Alterations

The 2017 television adaptation of Howards End, scripted by Kenneth Lonergan, interprets E.M. Forster's core themes of class stratification and interpersonal disconnection through the interactions of the intellectual Schlegel sisters, the pragmatic Wilcox family, and the impoverished Basts, portraying Edwardian England's social rigidities as precursors to modern inequalities.[24] Class is depicted not merely as economic disparity but as ideological entrenchment, with the Wilcoxes embodying entitled capitalismâ€”exemplified by their imperial rubber investmentsâ€”and the Schlegels advocating cultural uplift for the lower classes, as seen in their misguided advice to Leonard Bast that exacerbates his downfall.[31] The motif of "only connect," central to Forster's novel, is rendered as a fragile imperative against isolationism, emphasizing the Schlegels' idealism clashing with Wilcox pragmatism, yet the series underscores causal failures in cross-class empathy, such as Bast's exploitation amid shifting economic landscapes.[32] Alterations in the adaptation amplify themes of gender roles and equality, presenting Margaret and Helen Schlegel as proto-feminist figures whose agency challenges Edwardian norms more assertively than in the novel. Lonergan heightens Helen's defiance, including a scene where she explicitly blames patriarchal structures for societal ills, framing women's independenceâ€”such as Margaret's unmarried status at age 28â€”as a rebellion against marital and social constraints.[31] This shift infuses contemporary resonance, critiquing sexual hypocrisy and female subjugation with a bracing directness, while retaining Forster's subtlety in inheritance symbolism: Howards End as England's rural soul bequeathed to the culturally attuned over materialists.[24] Unlike the novel's more ambiguous resolution, the series disrupts staid dialogue with modern pacing and overt class critiques, such as references to exploitative colonial labor, to highlight enduring divides without resolving them idealistically.[31] These changes prioritize thematic vitality over strict fidelity, interpreting Forster's liberal humanism as a caution against unexamined privilege rather than unalloyed optimism.[33]

Release

Premiere and Marketing

The four-part miniseries Howards End premiered on BBC One in the United Kingdom on 12 November 2017, airing weekly at 9:00 p.m.[36][37] In the United States, Starz broadcast the series starting 8 April 2018, also in four weekly episodes.[38][39] Promotion in the UK centered on BBC platforms, including a 40-second trailer released online on 10 November 2017, highlighting the adaptation's themes of class and connection through key scenes featuring leads Hayley Atwell and Philippa Coulthard.[40] Starz announced the US premiere date on 12 January 2018 during its Television Critics Association panel, positioning the series as a prestige drama alongside new originals like Vida and Sweetbitter.[38] The network released promotional materials emphasizing E.M. Forster's novel and the script by Kenneth Lonergan, with social media teasers focusing on character-driven narratives of Edwardian society.[41]

Broadcast and Distribution

The four-episode miniseries premiered on BBC One in the United Kingdom on November 12, 2017, airing subsequent episodes weekly on November 19, November 26, and December 3.[42] In the United States, it debuted on Starz on April 8, 2018, with episodes released weekly thereafter.[42] The production was a co-venture between BBC Studios and Starz, with Starz acquiring all U.S. multiplatform pay TV rights and worldwide multiplatform distribution rights outside the UK, while BBC retained domestic UK rights.[43] International distribution was handled by Lionsgate Television, enabling availability on various platforms globally, including streaming services like Amazon Prime Video (via Starz add-on) and Apple TV in select regions.[44] Later U.S. broadcasts included reruns on PBS Masterpiece, beginning March 21, 2021, expanding accessibility through public television.[45]

Reception

Critical Analysis

The 2017 miniseries adaptation of Howards End received strong critical acclaim, earning an 87% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 46 reviews and a Metacritic score of 86 out of 100 from 15 critics.[46][47] Reviewers praised its high production values, including sumptuous period visuals and costumes, as well as its unhurried pacing enabled by the four-episode format, which allowed deeper exploration of character dynamics than the 1992 Merchant Ivory film.[3] Hayley Atwell's portrayal of Margaret Schlegel was widely highlighted for its intelligence and understated wit, distinguishing it from Emma Thompson's earlier interpretation by emphasizing the character's pragmatic agency.[48][3] Critics commended the series for retaining approximately 75% of E.M. Forster's original dialogue, preserving the novel's focus on class divisions, social mobility, and the tension between intellectual idealism and materialist pragmatism, while adapting these themes to underscore progress over nostalgia in Edwardian England.[32][3] Director Hettie Macdonald and screenwriter Kenneth Lonergan emphasized subtle interpersonal scenes, such as extended family breakfasts, to prioritize ideas and human connections over sensational drama, aligning with Forster's ironic humanism rather than conventional period-drama spectacle.[32] However, some alterations introduced a contemporary lens, notably casting Jacky Bastâ€”a marginal figure in the novelâ€”as a Black woman (Rosalind Eleazar), which layered racial and colonial critiques onto the class narrative, referencing British imperialism in Africa to expand Forster's Edwardian context beyond its original scope.[49][3] This change, while praised by some for highlighting intersecting oppressions, shifted emphasis from the novel's purer socioeconomic satire, potentially diluting its first-principles examination of cultural disconnection.[49] Despite these strengths, detractors argued the adaptation occasionally sacrificed Forster's nuanced irony for melodrama, smoothing rough edges in character motivations and plot logisticsâ€”such as abrupt transitions in relationshipsâ€”that the novel leaves ambiguous to underscore moral complexities.[3] Matthew Macfadyen's Henry Wilcox was critiqued as overly insufferable, amplifying unlikability at the expense of the restrained ambiguity in Forster's portrayal and Anthony Hopkins' film version.[49] The score's intrusive presence was also faulted for undermining the quiet humanism central to the source material.[32] Overall, while the series succeeded in revitalizing Forster's fable-like structure for modern audiences, its interpretive liberties risked prioritizing emotional accessibility over the novel's causal realism in depicting societal fractures.[49][3]

Audience Response and Commercial Aspects

The four-part miniseries premiered on BBC One on November 12, 2017, drawing 6.8 million overnight viewers for its opening episode in the 9:00 p.m. slot, marking a strong performance for a Sunday evening period drama on the public broadcaster.[50] While consolidated figures incorporating seven-day catch-up viewing were not publicly detailed for the full run, the initial ratings positioned it as a solid performer amid competition from commercial channels, aligning with BBC's strategy for high-end literary adaptations to bolster linear TV audiences.[50] Audience reception, as aggregated on IMDb, averaged a 7.2 out of 10 rating from approximately 7,200 user votes, with reviewers frequently commending the visual production quality, period authenticity, and performances by leads Hayley Atwell and Matthew Macfadyen, though some critiqued pacing and deviations from the source novel.[2] On Rotten Tomatoes, verified audience scores hovered around 64%, reflecting a more divided response compared to critics, potentially influenced by expectations set by the 1992 Merchant Ivory film adaptation.[46] Commercially, the series benefited from co-production between BBC Studios and Starz, enabling U.S. distribution via Starz and PBS's Masterpiece anthology, which expanded its reach to international markets including streaming platforms.[51] This partnership facilitated global licensing deals, contributing to the viability of upscale period dramas in an era of fragmented viewing, though specific revenue figures from international sales remain undisclosed; its alignment with BBC's export-driven content model underscored the commercial leverage of Forster's enduring literary appeal.[52]

Cultural Impact and Comparisons

The 2017 miniseries adaptation of Howards End has been frequently compared to the 1992 Merchant Ivory film, which earned three Academy Awards, including Best Actress for Emma Thompson's portrayal of Margaret Schlegel, for its concise distillation of E.M. Forster's novel into a two-hour feature emphasizing emotional restraint and visual elegance.[57] In contrast, the four-part television format enabled director Hettie Macdonald and screenwriter Kenneth Lonergan to expand on subplots, internal monologues, and socioeconomic tensions, such as the Wilcox family's insulation from the repercussions of their decisions toward the working-class Basts, offering a more analytical lens on Forster's themes of class friction and inheritance.[48] While some reviewers deemed the miniseries an unnecessary reiteration overshadowed by the film's "life-changing" impact, others praised its fidelity to the novel's ambiguities alongside modern interpretive depth, particularly in Hayley Atwell's vigorous depiction of Margaret as a bridge between idealism and pragmatism.[58][59] Relative to Forster's 1910 novel, which critiques Edwardian England's rigid social hierarchies through the interplay of the cultured Schlegels, pragmatic Wilcoxes, and aspirational Basts, the series preserves the imperative to "only connect" disparate worlds but amplifies visual and dialogic explorations of moral disconnection, rendering Leonard Bast's downward spiral more visceral amid urban poverty.[60] This approach enhances the source material's relevance for contemporary audiences by underscoring causal links between elite detachment and proletarian precarity, diverging from the novel's subtler irony in favor of explicit class critique.[61] Culturally, the miniseries prompted reflections on enduring class dynamics, with commentators observing that a modern analogue to Leonard Bastâ€”intelligent yet economically marginalizedâ€”might evade literal starvation but face amplified cultural and informational deprivation in an era of gated knowledge and gig economies, contrasting Forster's pre-welfare state context.[62] It also drew parallels between the Schlegel sisters' era-bound pursuit of interpersonal "connections" via letters and telegrams and today's digital social networks, where superficial linkages exacerbate isolation rather than foster genuine solidarity, thereby updating the novel's caution against fragmented human relations.[63] Positioned amid a surge in prestige period dramas, the production was marketed as a potential successor to Downton Abbey for its blend of opulent aesthetics and social commentary, though it exerted limited broader influence beyond niche literary and televisual circles, primarily sustaining interest in Forster's oeuvre among adaptation enthusiasts.[64]
