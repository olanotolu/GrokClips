New Trial (film)

New Trial (Korean: 재심; RR: Jaesim) is a 2017 South Korean crime drama film written and directed by Kim Tae-yoon.[1] Starring Kang Ha-neul as Hyun-woo, a teenager who witnesses a taxi driver's murder but is tortured by police into a false confession and imprisoned for ten years, and Jung Woo as an opportunistic lawyer who pursues his retrial for personal gain that evolves into genuine advocacy, the film dramatizes the wrongful conviction process and systemic flaws in investigations.[1] Loosely inspired by the 2000 Iksan taxi driver stabbing case, where 16-year-old Choi was convicted based on a coerced admission, served a decade in prison, and was later exonerated with state compensation after the real perpetrator, Kim, was identified and sentenced to 15 years in 2018.[2][3] The narrative critiques coercive interrogation tactics and judicial inertia, reflecting documented issues in South Korean law enforcement where physical abuse led to the initial miscarriage of justice, as the victim's stabbing occurred during a robbery by the actual culprit who evaded capture for 18 years. Released amid ongoing real-world appeals in the basis case, New Trial garnered praise for highlighting empirical failures in evidence handling and confession reliability, though some critiques noted its linear storytelling over deeper systemic analysis.[4]

Background and real-life inspiration

The Iksan murder case of 2000

On August 2000, a 42-year-old taxi driver was stabbed to death in the Yakchon Ogeori area of Iksan, North Jeolla Province, South Korea.[2] The victim's body was found by a 16-year-old high school student surnamed Choi, who was making a food delivery nearby and alerted authorities after discovering the scene.[3] Police quickly focused on Choi as the prime suspect, announcing that he had confessed to killing the driver in a rage over an unpaid delivery fee.[5] Choi was interrogated without the presence of parents or legal counsel and later alleged that his confession was extracted through coercion and prolonged questioning.[5] The conviction rested primarily on this statement, as physical evidence directly tying Choi to the stabbing—such as fingerprints, blood matches, or eyewitness accounts—was absent or inconclusive.[6] He was tried as a juvenile, convicted of murder, and sentenced to detention, ultimately serving about 10 years before release around 2010.[2] Following his release, Choi faced ongoing difficulties, including social stigma and employment barriers, while maintaining his innocence and demanding state compensation.[7] In 2013, after public pressure and his formal retrial petition, Iksan police reopened the case for reinvestigation, acknowledging potential flaws in the original probe.[5] The retrial proceeded, resulting in Choi's acquittal in 2016, which affirmed investigative misconduct and the unreliability of the coerced confession.[2] New inquiries identified a man surnamed Kim as the perpetrator, who had robbed the driver before stabbing him multiple times during a struggle.[8] In March 2018, South Korea's Supreme Court upheld Kim's conviction for robbery-murder, sentencing him to 15 years in prison based on circumstantial evidence and his partial admissions.[3] The state compensated Choi with roughly 840 million South Korean won (about $740,000 USD at the time) in July 2017 for the wrongful conviction and lost years.[7]

Development and production

Script development and key personnel

Director Kim Tae-yoon, who also penned the screenplay, drew inspiration from the documented investigative shortcomings in the 2000 Iksan murder case, particularly the use of prolonged interrogations—lasting up to 100 hours without counsel—that yielded confessions later deemed unreliable due to physical and psychological coercion.[9] His script methodically traces causal pathways from these procedural failures, including suppressed exculpatory evidence and reliance on tainted witness statements, to the resulting wrongful conviction, aiming to expose systemic vulnerabilities in evidence evaluation rather than sensationalize the events.[10] To construct the narrative arc—from initial accusation through imprisonment to retrial advocacy—Tae-yoon incorporated research from publicly available court transcripts and case analyses, while diverging from the real proceedings to focus on thematic transformation of an ordinary individual into a justice advocate; he noted the script borrowed only the core motive from the actual events, as the real retrial remained unresolved during development.[11][12] The screenplay was completed ahead of principal photography, which began in late 2016 under producers Yoon Soo-jin, Park Sung-il, and Yoon Ki-ho, with Idioplan handling production to prioritize factual restraint over commercial embellishment.[12][13] Financing emphasized modest independent backing suited to a character-driven legal drama, avoiding high-budget spectacle to maintain authenticity in depicting institutional inertia and evidentiary mishandling. The project aligned with rising public scrutiny of the case, culminating in the film's February 2017 release shortly after the real-life retrial petition gained traction.[14]

Casting process

Director Kim Tae-yoon cast Jung Woo in the lead role of Lee Joon-young, the struggling lawyer taking on the pro bono case, selected for his ability to embody a character who begins as opportunistic rather than inherently righteous, diverging from conventional legal drama archetypes.[15] The choice emphasized Woo's dramatic versatility, honed in prior roles, to depict moral evolution driven by case specifics over initial self-interest.[16] For the role of Jo Hyun-woo, the wrongfully convicted youth, Tae-yoon chose Kang Ha-neul immediately after viewing his performance in Dongju: The Portrait of a Poet (2016), prioritizing an actor who could subvert audience preconceptions of innocence.[17] The director specifically sought someone with a "rough" edge to authentically capture the character's post-incarceration challenges and perceived delinquency, citing Kang's sharp facial features and eye expression as avoiding an overly "clean" or sympathetic look that might undermine the role's realism tied to the real-life case's investigative biases.[18] [19] Supporting roles, including Kim Hae-sook as the protagonist's mother Soon-im, were finalized to anchor emotional depth; Tae-yoon noted that securing Hae-sook instilled immediate confidence across the production team due to her proven gravitas in maternal figures confronting injustice.[20] Authority figures, such as those played by Han Jae-young, were cast to reflect investigative dynamics from the underlying Iksan case testimonies, ensuring portrayals grounded in procedural realism without exaggeration.[21] Overall, selections prioritized actors' physical and expressive alignment with the real individuals' documented traits—youthful resilience amid scrutiny for Kang, weathered conviction for Woo—to maintain fidelity to the case's causal elements of coercion and redemption.[22]

Filming

Principal photography and locations

Principal photography for New Trial utilized on-location shoots across South Korea to recreate the early 2000s atmosphere of the Iksan murder case, emphasizing rural police interrogation rooms and modest urban court facilities without relying on extensive studio sets. Key residential sequences, including the young protagonist's family home, were filmed along the coastal areas of Boryeong in Chungcheongnam-do province, selected for their period-appropriate rural seaside vernacular that paralleled the original crime scene's provincial setting despite the geographical shift from Jeollabuk-do.[23] Logistical choices prioritized authenticity in depicting prolonged interrogations, incorporating practical effects for confined spaces like juvenile holding areas to simulate the physical and psychological toll of extended detentions as documented in the real case. These elements were executed amid a compressed production timeline concluding in late 2016, enabling post-production completion ahead of the film's February 2017 release and highlighting persistent investigative practices in Korean provincial justice systems.

Synopsis and cast

Plot summary

In 2000, fifteen-year-old Hyun-woo, after taking a taxi, witnesses the driver step out during a phone call and get stabbed to death by an assailant who then flees the scene.[24] Hyun-woo attempts to report the crime but is immediately apprehended by police detectives who suspect him as the perpetrator based on his presence at the scene and possession of a knife inconsistent with the victim's wounds.[24] [12] Under prolonged and brutal interrogation involving physical torture and psychological pressure, Hyun-woo, overwhelmed and exhausted, provides a coerced false confession to the murder.[25] [24] He is subsequently convicted of the crime and sentenced to fifteen years in prison, serving ten years before early release on parole.[13] [12] Ten years later, upon release, Hyun-woo struggles with social stigma from neighbors and employers who view him as a convicted killer, forcing him to live in isolation with his aging mother who bears the financial burden of victim compensation payments accrued with interest.[24] [13] His mother enlists the aid of lawyer Lee Joon-young, an ambitious attorney initially seeking publicity, who begins investigating the case for a retrial by re-examining original evidence and uncovering signs of police fabrication.[24] [26] As Joon-young delves deeper, he identifies overlooked forensic discrepancies, such as mismatched DNA and witness testimonies suppressed during the original trial, while facing resistance from implicated investigators.[12] [24] The narrative culminates in a retrial courtroom where evidence of investigative misconduct and coerced testimony is presented, offering Hyun-woo a chance at vindication through persistent legal challenge.[26] [25]

Principal cast and roles

Kang Ha-neul portrays Jo Hyun-woo, a young man wrongfully convicted of murder whose role encompasses enduring coercive police interrogation and years of imprisonment within the penal system.[12] Jung Woo plays Lee Joon-young, an attorney whose function centers on spearheading the retrial process through ethical commitment to uncovering judicial irregularities.[27] Kim Hae-sook appears as Soon-im, serving as Hyun-woo's mother and representing steadfast family support amid prolonged legal adversity.[28] In a key supporting capacity, Han Jae-yeong depicts Cheol-gi, a detective whose involvement illustrates the exercise of investigative power in the case's early stages.[24] Sung Do-hyun assumes the role of Detective Cha, further embodying law enforcement's operational dynamics without evaluative framing.[29]

Release and commercial performance

Premiere and distribution

New Trial premiered in South Korea on February 15, 2017, shortly after the real-life Iksan case defendant was acquitted in a retrial on November 17, 2016, allowing the film to leverage heightened public interest in the miscarriage of justice.[30][31][32] The domestic distribution was managed by Opus Pictures and CGV Arthouse, targeting mainstream theaters amid the case's recent resolution to emphasize factual parallels over dramatization.[12] Internationally, the film received limited theatrical rollout, prioritizing arthouse circuits and festival circuits rather than wide commercial release. It screened at the 19th Far East Film Festival in Udine, Italy, in April 2017, highlighting its narrative on retrial proceedings.[33] Global sales were facilitated by United Pictures at markets like the European Film Market in Berlin, securing acquisitions in territories including those handled by New Select for select distributions.[34] Marketing efforts centered on the film's basis in the timely Iksan retrial outcome, promoting it as a grounded exploration of judicial flaws without sensationalizing fictional elements.[31]

Box office results

New Trial premiered in South Korea on February 15, 2017, achieving the number one position at the box office on its opening day with strong initial attendance.[35] Over its first five days, the film generated $7.3 million in revenue from 1 million admissions, capturing 34% of the total weekend box office share.[36] Domestically, the film accumulated a total of 2,421,197 admissions.[32] Its gross earnings reached approximately 18.5 billion KRW (equivalent to $13.4 million USD at contemporaneous exchange rates).[32] International distribution contributed minimally to overall revenue, with the vast majority derived from the South Korean market.[1]

Reception

Critical reviews

Critics commended New Trial for its realistic portrayal of coercive interrogation techniques employed by police, drawing from the real-life case of a forced confession that led to a decade-long wrongful imprisonment, which underscored systemic flaws in South Korea's justice system during the 1990s and early 2000s.[14] The film's depiction of legal obstacles in securing a retrial, including evidentiary hurdles and institutional resistance, was highlighted as a strength, with reviewers noting how it effectively dramatized the protagonist's struggle against fabricated evidence chains without relying solely on courtroom theatrics.[24] Kang Ha-neul's performance as the lead, conveying quiet resilience amid psychological torment, received particular acclaim for grounding the narrative in emotional authenticity rather than melodrama.[26] The film holds an average user rating of 6.8 out of 10 on IMDb, based on over 1,400 votes, reflecting a generally favorable but not exceptional reception among viewers who appreciated its focus on procedural realism over sensationalism.[1] On Rotten Tomatoes, it scores 55% from a limited set of four critic reviews, indicating divided professional opinions.[13] Some critiques pointed to the film's reliance on familiar retrial tropes common in Korean thrillers, resulting in a somewhat predictable structure that prioritizes linear progression over nuanced exploration of evidentiary ambiguities.[24] Pacing issues were noted, with certain sequences—particularly violent confrontations—dragging or feeling gratuitous, potentially undermining the otherwise restrained tone.[26] A minority of observers questioned the narrative's unqualified assertion of the protagonist's innocence, arguing that while the real case involved exoneration via DNA evidence, the film simplifies causal links in the investigation, potentially glossing over alternative interpretations of witness testimonies and forensic gaps that warrant independent empirical verification beyond dramatic convenience.[14]

Audience and cultural impact

The film resonated strongly with South Korean audiences troubled by instances of judicial miscarriage, as demonstrated by its commercial performance exceeding the break-even point of approximately 2.26 million admissions by March 5, 2017, shortly after its February 15 release.[37] Viewer discussions on platforms like Naver blogs and review aggregators highlighted empathy for the protagonist's wrongful conviction, drawing parallels to real-world flaws in investigative coercion and evidence handling, with many expressing outrage over systemic failures that prioritize closure over accuracy.[38] This response aligned with a broader pattern in Korean cinema, where films addressing court injustices, such as New Trial, mobilize netizens focused on social inequities, fostering online forums debating the reliability of confessions extracted under duress.[39] Culturally, New Trial amplified public scrutiny of confession validity in South Korea, contributing to heightened skepticism regarding police interrogation tactics post-release, as evidenced by media analyses linking the film to ongoing conversations about retrial advocacy in cases like the Yakchon murder on which it was based.[40] Its portrayal of a decade-long wrongful imprisonment spurred discourse on the need for evidentiary reforms, with coverage noting how the narrative encouraged viewers to question authority-driven narratives in high-profile crimes, indirectly supporting real-life pushes for retrials amid persistent low reversal rates in the justice system.[41] The film's timing near the actual 2017 retrial exoneration of the Yakchon case defendant further embedded it in national debates on accountability, prompting reflections on how coerced admissions undermine causal chains of proof.[42] Counterperspectives emerged among some viewers who critiqued the film for excessive dramatization, arguing it fictionalized elements to heighten anti-authority sentiment at the expense of nuanced perpetrator responsibility, potentially glossing over evidentiary ambiguities in favor of a redemption arc. These reactions, documented in early audience feedback, suggested the narrative's focus on innocence advocacy might inadvertently downplay the imperative of verifying guilt beyond systemic critiques, though such views remained minority amid predominant acclaim for raising awareness.[43]

Awards and nominations

Major award recognitions

New Trial earned nominations at prominent South Korean film awards in 2017, highlighting aspects of its production and acting, though it did not win any major categories. At the 54th Grand Bell Awards, the film received a nomination for Best Planning, recognizing its preparatory and developmental efforts.[44] Kim Hae-sook was nominated in the Best Supporting Actress category for her portrayal of the protagonist's mother.[45] The 38th Blue Dragon Film Awards similarly nominated Kim Hae-sook for Best Supporting Actress.[46] At the 53rd Baeksang Arts Awards, Han Jae-young, who played Baek Chul-gi, was nominated for Best New Actor in the film division, acknowledging emerging talent in the cast.[47] These recognitions underscored the film's technical merits and select performances amid a competitive field of 2017 releases.

Themes and analysis

Portrayal of the justice system

The film depicts the South Korean justice system as vulnerable to investigative abuses, particularly through prolonged and coercive interrogations that elicit false confessions from vulnerable suspects, such as a 15-year-old boy subjected to violent questioning without oversight or recording.[12][26] This portrayal frames extended interrogations—lasting days without breaks or legal counsel—as the primary causal mechanism for miscarriages of justice, enabling police to fabricate evidence and secure convictions through duress rather than substantive proof.[26] The narrative contrasts this initial systemic failure with the retrial process, which succeeds via persistent legal advocacy, forensic reexamination, and judicial reevaluation, suggesting inherent self-corrective potential within the framework despite entrenched flaws.[48] From a causal standpoint, the film's emphasis on interrogation coercion aligns with pre-reform realities in South Korea, where the absence of mandatory video recording until the 2010s allowed procedural oversights and isolated abuses to undermine reliability, contributing to documented false confessions in wrongful conviction cases.[49] However, post-2000s legal reforms, including bans on physical coercion after 1990 and subsequent adoption of audiovisual recording for custodial interrogations, have addressed these vulnerabilities by enhancing transparency and reducing opportunities for unverified duress, thereby shifting causal risks from unchecked human malice to verifiable procedural gaps.[49][50] Empirical evidence indicates that while false confessions remain a factor in Asian wrongful convictions, including South Korea, their incidence has declined with these safeguards, underscoring the system's adaptability rather than irreducible corruption.[51] Critics of the portrayal argue it potentially exaggerates police intentionality over inadvertent errors, such as confirmation bias in high-pressure investigations, while underplaying the justice system's overall integrity, evidenced by South Korea's high rule-of-law ranking and low proven miscarriage rates relative to its caseload exceeding millions annually.[52] The retrial mechanism's efficacy in the film reflects real institutional strengths, where appeals and reinvestigations have overturned errors without systemic collapse, supporting a view of causal realism: isolated failures stem from remediable lapses, not inherent design flaws, as bolstered by conviction rates above 99% sustained by prosecutorial rigor rather than unchecked fabrication.[53][54] This balanced depiction affirms the value of empirical safeguards in minimizing errors, though the film's dramatic lens prioritizes human agency in reform over institutional evolution.[48]

Factual discrepancies with real events

The film condenses the timeline of the Iksan taxi driver murder case, portraying the sequence from the 2000 crime to the protagonist's retrial efforts as occurring in a more streamlined manner over a decade, whereas the real events spanned from the August 10, 2000, stabbing death of the 42-year-old driver to the victim's first-person witness (a 15-year-old delivery boy named Choi) filing for retrial in 2013, with the Jeonbuk District Court granting and conducting the retrial leading to acquittal on November 17, 2016.[12][3][2] This compression amplifies dramatic pacing but omits the protracted post-release legal battles, including appeals delaying final resolution until after Choi had already served 10 years (from conviction shortly after the murder to parole around 2010).[55][56] The film's emphasis on a single transformative lawyer's role in uncovering evidence and securing retrial overstates individual heroism; in the actual case, Choi's exoneration relied on documented police coercion during interrogation— including physical abuse leading to a false confession—corroborated in the 2016 retrial, but involved collaborative efforts from Choi's legal team, media scrutiny, and judicial reinvestigation rather than one attorney's solitary crusade modeled after real lawyer Park Jun-yeong.[31] The narrative simplifies investigative misconduct to a clear framing conspiracy, whereas real proceedings affirmed coercion but noted initial suspicions arising from Choi's proximity as the crime scene's discoverer and sole early witness, with some evidentiary debates on his account's consistency persisting in public discourse despite ultimate acquittal.[33][3] Unlike the film's conclusive redemptive arc ending in vindication, the real case's post-acquittal developments included a 2018 Supreme Court conviction of an alternative suspect for the murder following reinvestigation—establishing a different perpetrator without reliance on DNA evidence as sometimes dramatized—yet full state compensation for wrongful imprisonment was not awarded until a 2021 court ruling ordering reparations, highlighting ongoing bureaucratic hurdles rather than immediate closure.[3][2] No significant updates to the case facts have emerged as of 2025, underscoring that while coercion was central, the exoneration reflected specific procedural failures rather than an overarching systemic plot, with the alternative conviction providing empirical closure absent in the film's portrayal.[56]
