2023 Kelantan state election

The 2023 Kelantan state election, held on 12 August 2023, was conducted to elect the 45 members of the Kelantan State Legislative Assembly, the unicameral legislature of the northeastern Malaysian state.[1][2] The election resulted in a decisive victory for the Perikatan Nasional (PN) coalition, dominated by the Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), which captured 43 seats, thereby retaining its control over the state assembly established since 1990.[1][3] This outcome underscored PAS's enduring appeal among the predominantly Malay and rural electorate, where support for its Islamist platform remains robust despite national political shifts following the 2022 federal election.[2] The Barisan Nasional (BN) coalition secured the remaining two seats, while the ruling Pakatan Harapan (PH)-BN alliance at the federal level failed to make significant inroads.[1] The polls were part of simultaneous state elections in six Malaysian states, triggered by assembly dissolutions amid post-federal election realignments, and highlighted PN's "green wave" momentum from Islamist-conservative voters.[2] PAS, contesting nearly all seats, leveraged its incumbency under Menteri Besar Ahmad Yakob to emphasize governance continuity, including enforcement of state-level Islamic laws, against opposition challenges focused on economic development and anti-corruption.[3] No major controversies marred the vote, which proceeded peacefully under the Election Commission's oversight, though the lopsided results reflected Kelantan's distinct socio-political dynamics, with limited urban influence and strong adherence to traditional values.[1] This supermajority enables PAS to pursue unhindered policy agendas, including expansions in Sharia implementation within constitutional bounds, solidifying Kelantan's position as a bastion of political Islam in Malaysia.[2]

Historical and Political Context

PAS Dominance in Kelantan

The Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS) has maintained uninterrupted control of the Kelantan state government since regaining power in the 1990 state election, marking over three decades of dominance as of 2023.[4][5] This period encompasses multiple state elections where PAS secured majorities, including 24 out of 45 seats in 2004, 38 in 2008, 32 in 2013, and 37 in 2018, often achieving vote shares between 60% and 90%.[5] PAS's governance emphasizes Sharia-based policies, such as attempts to enact hudud laws since the early 1990s, which have been repeatedly proposed in the state assembly but faced federal constitutional barriers.[6] Kelantan's electorate, approximately 96% Muslim and predominantly ethnic Malay with significant rural characteristics, provides a demographic foundation for PAS's appeal through religious conservatism and Malay-Muslim identity politics.[4][5] The party's framing of elections in Islamic terms, exemplified by rhetoric equating voting for PAS with securing rewards in the afterlife, resonates in a state culturally known as the "Veranda of Mecca" for its piety.[5] Local loyalty persists despite economic underperformance, with Kelantan's 2021 GDP per capita at RM15,584 compared to the national average of RM47,439, as voters prioritize religious governance over material development.[5] PAS's organizational superiority, including the Bahagian Dakwah Halaqat unit with over 540 staff embedding party ideology through mosque networks and community outreach, contrasts with opponents' weaker grassroots presence often focused on urban or federal issues.[5] Programs like Penghulu Seranta mobilize the roughly 200,000 out-of-state Kelantanese voters, who overwhelmingly support PAS due to its Islamic credentials and rejection of secular federal influences perceived as diluting local values.[5] This structure sustains supermajorities by reinforcing causal links between religious adherence, ethnic solidarity, and political allegiance, even amid policy shifts toward stricter conservatism post-2015.[5]

Impact of 2022 Federal Election

The 15th Malaysian general election (GE15) on 19 November 2022 produced a hung parliament, with Perikatan Nasional (PN)â€”including PASâ€”securing 73 seats as the largest bloc but falling short of a majority in the 222-seat Dewan Rakyat.[7] Pakatan Harapan (PH) leader Anwar Ibrahim subsequently formed a unity government on 24 November 2022, incorporating PH, Barisan Nasional (BN), and East Malaysian coalitions, while excluding PN.[7] In Kelantan, PAS captured all 14 parliamentary seats, reflecting its overwhelming dominance in the state's Malay-majority constituencies.[8] This federal outcome amplified perceptions of national political instability, marked by protracted negotiations and a multi-ethnic coalition perceived by conservative Malay voters as compromising traditional priorities.[9] Kelantan's alignment with PN's Malay-centric platform intensified resentment toward the unity government's policies, which some viewed as diluting bumiputera privileges and Islamic governance through inclusion of non-Malay parties like DAP.[10] PAS capitalized on this sentiment, portraying federal shifts as a threat to ethnic and religious safeguards long upheld in the state.[11] Under Menteri Besar Ahmad Yakob, Kelantan's PAS administration maintained pre-existing stability amid national turmoil, providing a stark contrast that bolstered voter loyalty.[12] The federal impasse thus set the stage for state-level repudiation of PH-BN alliances in the 2023 election, as Kelantan's electorate prioritized PAS's consistent rule over perceived dilutions from Kuala Lumpur's coalition dynamics.[9]

Pre-Election Framework

Assembly Composition and Dissolution

The Kelantan State Legislative Assembly entering the 2023 election period comprised 45 single-member constituencies, all occupied by members of the Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS) aligned with the Perikatan Nasional coalition.[13] This unanimous PAS control had persisted without opposition seats since the 2018 state election, reflecting the party's longstanding dominance in the state's Malay-majority electoral landscape.[14] The assembly's dissolution occurred on 22 June 2023, following royal assent from Sultan Muhammad V of Kelantan, as announced by Menteri Besar Ahmad Yakob.[15] This action preceded the natural expiry of the 14th assembly's five-year term on 28 June 2023 by six days, pursuant to provisions in the Kelantan State Constitution mirroring federal norms for legislative durations not exceeding five years.[16] The early dissolution facilitated synchronization with state elections in Kedah, Penang, Terengganu, Selangor, and Negeri Sembilan, enabling a unified polling date of 12 August 2023 under the Election Commission's coordination.[17] Eligible voters numbered approximately 1.1 million, drawn from constituencies characterized by overwhelming Malay majoritiesâ€”over 95% of Kelantan's populationâ€”where simple plurality voting determined outcomes in each district.[18] Registration adhered to federal criteria under the Election Commission, emphasizing citizens aged 18 and above with no disqualifications, though the state's rural and conservative demographics underscored the focus on core Malay-Muslim voter bases.[19]

Electoral Constituencies and System

The Kelantan State Legislative Assembly comprises 45 single-member constituencies, each elected through the first-past-the-post system where the candidate with the most votes secures the seat.[16] These constituencies are aligned within the state's 15 federal parliamentary boundaries, encompassing predominantly rural terrain across northeastern Malaysia, including coastal districts like Bachok and Tumpat, inland agricultural zones such as Pasir Mas and Tanah Merah, and limited urban enclaves.[20] The rural character, characterized by paddy fields, smallholder farming, and traditional kampung settlements, results in electorates that are overwhelmingly ethnic Malay, fostering structural advantages for parties emphasizing conservative Islamic values and Malay-centric policies.[5] Electoral boundaries, delineated by the Election Commission of Malaysia, have undergone periodic reviews, with adjustments post-2003 federal redistricting emphasizing malapportionment that weights rural seats more heavily despite population shifts toward urban peripheries.[21] This configuration minimally disrupts long-standing PAS strongholds in rural interiors, where voter turnout patterns and demographic homogeneity reinforce Islamist appeal, while urban pocketsâ€”particularly in Kota Bharu, the state capital with mixed commercial and administrative hubsâ€”exhibit greater volatility due to diverse socioeconomic influences including non-Malay minorities and younger demographics.[22] Polling for the 2023 election occurred uniformly on 12 August 2023 across all constituencies, facilitating synchronized administration and reducing logistical disparities between remote rural stations and accessible urban ones.[23] Postal voting was restricted to specific groups, including security forces personnel, election officials abroad, and a limited number of overseas Malaysians, with applications processed through the Election Commission to ensure integrity amid the state's dispersed geography.[24]

Incumbent Retirements and Candidate Nominations

In the lead-up to the 2023 Kelantan state election, Perikatan Nasional (PN), led by Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), pursued a strategy of candidate renewal amid a landscape of limited voluntary retirements among incumbents. PAS, which had secured all 45 state assembly seats in the 2018 election, renominated 22 sitting assemblymen while introducing 23 new faces, comprising more than 51 percent of its slateâ€”a historic shift aimed at injecting fresh leadership without fracturing party unity.[25][26] Notable voluntary retirements included Menteri Besar Datuk Ahmad Yakob, who opted not to defend his Pasir Pekan seat citing health concerns, and former assemblyman Dato' Seri Mustapa Mohamed, who declined to contest Ayer Lanas after serving multiple terms.[27][28] These exits were among the few high-profile cases, with the majority of non-renominations attributed to internal party selections rather than widespread retirements, preserving PAS's dominance and avoiding public discord. Candidate nominations occurred on July 29, 2023, across the state's 45 constituencies, where PN/PAS candidates submitted papers using the PAS logo exclusively, underscoring the coalition's reliance on the party's established brand in its stronghold.[29] The process saw PN/PAS facing opposition from Pakatan Harapan-Barisan Nasional (PH-BN) alliances and minor parties in most seats, though no symbolic uncontested victories materialized, as formal challenges were lodged nationwide.[30] The Election Commission verified all submissions promptly, confirming eligibility without major disputes or disqualifications in Kelantan, which facilitated a smooth transition to the campaign phase and highlighted the party's internal cohesion in finalizing its list prior to nomination day.[31] This approach allowed PAS to balance continuityâ€”retaining experienced incumbents in key areasâ€”with renewal, minimizing disruptions to its electoral machinery.

Campaign Dynamics

Participating Parties and Strategies

Perikatan Nasional (PN), dominated by the Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS), contested all 45 seats in the Kelantan State Legislative Assembly using the PAS logo to capitalize on its longstanding dominance in the state. The coalition fielded 45 candidates, emphasizing continuity of Islamic governance rooted in local traditions and sharia implementation, with tactics focused on grassroots mobilization through religious sermons, ulama endorsements, and large community gatherings to reinforce voter loyalty among conservative Malays. This approach leveraged PAS's historical control since 1990, portraying the party as a bulwark against federal interference.[32][4] The Unity Government alliance of Pakatan Harapan (PH) and Barisan Nasional (BN) adopted a coordinated strategy, fielding joint candidates in several constituencies to consolidate opposition votes and appeal to voters disillusioned with PAS's governance record. BN contested 31 seats, often with PH support in winnable areas, prioritizing promises of enhanced infrastructure and economic integration with federal resources, though this federal-aligned pitch struggled against PAS's entrenched ethno-religious narrative. The partnership marked an initial test of PH-BN cooperation post-2022 federal polls, but localized anti-establishment sentiments limited its traction.[33][34] Minor parties and independents, including small Malay-centric groups voicing discontent with PN's alliances or the Unity Government's compromises, nominated fewer than 20 candidates collectively, exerting minimal influence amid the polarized contest between the major coalitions. These fringe efforts often fragmented the opposition vote without altering seat dynamics.[34]

Core Issues and Voter Concerns

The 2023 Kelantan state election highlighted a fundamental debate between upholding Islamist governance and addressing socioeconomic stagnation, with PAS emphasizing the defense of Sharia courts and moral enforcement as bulwarks against federal secular pressures. Amid the "green wave" momentum from the 2022 federal polls, PAS framed its platform around preserving Malay-Muslim cultural identity, portraying challenges to state-level Islamic policies as existential threats to religious autonomy.[10] This resonated in a constituency where Islamist appeals historically overshadowed material critiques, as evidenced by the party's retention of dominance despite opponents' focus on governance lapses.[35] Economic grievances formed the core of opposition attacks, centering on Kelantan's entrenched water scarcity, inadequate clean water access, and elevated unemployment under prolonged PAS administration, even as the state benefited from oil royalties. Critics pointed to the state's 4.1% unemployment rate in February 2023â€”exceeding the national 3.5%â€”and youth joblessness as symptoms of underinvestment in infrastructure and diversification beyond resource dependence. [36] These issues underscored a causal disconnect between royalty inflows and tangible poverty alleviation, yet voter behavior indicated a prioritization of religious preservation over demands for developmental reforms. Tensions with the federal unity government under Anwar Ibrahim amplified identity-based concerns, as PAS depicted inclusive policiesâ€”particularly alliances with non-Malay partiesâ€”as eroding Malay privileges and Islamic primacy. This narrative exploited perceptions of marginalization among conservative voters, evidenced by turnout dynamics that favored PAS strongholds despite broader economic discontent surpassing religious issues in some surveys.[37] [38] The rejection of federal overtures reinforced a state-federal rift, where causal loyalty to ethno-religious solidarity trumped appeals for cross-ethnic economic collaboration.[39]

Opinion Polling Trends

Pre-election opinion polls conducted by Malaysian research firms consistently forecasted dominant performances by Perikatan Nasional (PN), anchored by Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), in Kelantan, projecting control over the vast majority of the state's 45 assembly seats. These surveys highlighted entrenched voter loyalty among the predominantly Malay rural electorate, driven by historical PAS governance since 1990 and consolidation against the federal unity government formed after the 2022 general election.[40][41] EMIR Research, drawing on voter sentiment analysis, projected PN-PAS to secure at least 38 seats, identifying only seven as potential close contests amid low undecided voter rates in rural strongholds.[41] Merdeka Center's face-to-face survey, emphasizing ethnic breakdowns with a focus on Malay respondents (who comprise over 95% of Kelantan's population), anticipated PN gains in traditional Barisan Nasional (BN) areas but overall retention of PN control, reflecting minimal erosion of PAS's base despite economic concerns.[40] Similarly, Ilham Centre's polling predicted status quo outcomes across contested states, including Kelantan, based on surveys avoiding over-sampling of urban or non-Malay demographics irrelevant to the state's rural-majority composition.[42] Trends across these polls showed negligible shifts following candidate nominations in late July 2023, with PN vote shares holding steady at levels implying 80-90% seat projections in PAS-favoring simulations derived from 2022 federal results adjusted for state-specific factors.[43] Low undecided percentages (under 5% in rural samples) underscored the predictive stability, as urban pockets like Kota Bharu exhibited marginally tighter races but lacked the volume to alter the overall trajectory.[37] Methodologies prioritized in-person interviews over phone or online methods to capture conservative rural sentiments accurately, mitigating biases from national-level sampling that might dilute Kelantan's distinct ethnic and ideological profile.[44]

Election Execution and Outcomes

Timeline of Key Events

The Kelantan State Legislative Assembly was dissolved on June 22, 2023, marking the first such action among the six states holding elections that year and initiating the process for the 15th state election.[13][14] On July 5, 2023, the Election Commission (SPR) announced the key dates for the six state elections, including Kelantan: nomination day on July 29 and polling day on August 12, with early voting for security personnel set for August 8.[45][46] Nomination day proceeded on July 29, 2023, with candidates submitting papers at designated centers under SPR supervision, followed by the start of the official campaign period.[47] Polling occurred on August 12, 2023, amid high temperatures that contributed to a initially slow voter turnout but no significant disruptions; the process was conducted peacefully with SPR oversight and limited international observation.[48] Results were announced on the same evening after vote counting concluded at polling centers.[48] The newly elected assemblymen were sworn in on September 5, 2023, during the first sitting of the 15th Kelantan State Legislative Assembly.[49]

Results Overview and Turnout

Perikatan Nasional (PN), led by the Pan-Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS), secured 43 of the 45 seats in the Kelantan State Legislative Assembly in the election held on 12 August 2023, retaining control of the state with a substantial majority.[2] [3] The two seats not won by PN went to candidates from Barisan Nasional and Pakatan Harapan.[50] The results were certified by the Election Commission without the need for recounts, with PN candidates achieving victory margins typically ranging from 2,000 to 5,000 votes per contested seat. PN obtained approximately 70% of the popular vote, underscoring its dominance.[51] Voter turnout stood at 73-75%, lower than the 74.4% recorded in the 2022 federal general election, which may reflect complacency among supporters in what were largely safe seats for the incumbent coalition.[52] The outcome highlighted overwhelming support from the Malay majority, who comprise over 95% of Kelantan's electorate, with negligible influence from non-Malay voters due to the state's demographic profile and PN's appeal rooted in conservative Islamic values.[53]

Breakdown by Parliamentary Constituency

PN secured all state assembly seats within 13 of Kelantan's 14 federal parliamentary constituencies, underscoring uniform rural support across the state. The coalition, comprising primarily PAS candidates with support from Bersatu, captured 43 of the 45 state seats overall, reflecting strongholds in predominantly Malay rural areas.[54] Aggregated results highlighted exceptional margins in constituencies like Bachok (P.021) and Pasir Mas (P.022), where PN victories in their respective state seats exceeded 70% vote shares in several cases, indicative of entrenched Islamist appeal among conservative voters.[3] The sole deviation occurred in Kota Bharu (P.020), an urban-influenced area encompassing Kota Lama (N.25), where PH's Amanah candidate Dr. Hafidzah Mustakim defeated PN's incumbent with 9,691 votes to 7,939, marking the only non-PN win and a narrower margin reflective of diverse demographics including Chinese voters.[55] Similar slight variations appeared in Pengkalan Chepa (P.019), with PN margins dipping below 60% in urban-adjacent state seats due to higher opposition turnout, though still resulting in full PN control.[54] Rural constituencies such as Tumpat (P.018), Pasir Puteh (P.024), and Jeli (P.028) exhibited near-total uniformity, with PN aggregating over 80% of votes across their state seats.

Shifts in Seat Allegiances

Perikatan Nasional (PN), dominated by the Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), retained effective control of all 45 state legislative seats in Kelantan, with the coalition securing 43 seats outright and no transfers to the rival Pakatan Harapanâ€“Barisan Nasional (PHâ€“BN) unity government alliance. This marked a continuation of PN's pre-election hold, defying PHâ€“BN's targeted campaigns for breakthroughs in PAS strongholds. The two non-PN victories were by independent candidates in N61 (Bukit Bunga) and N64 (Gajah), where local factors influenced outcomes but did not alter the overall coalition dominance.[3][2] PHâ€“BN candidates, contesting under a unified banner, achieved no seat gains despite hopes of capitalizing on federal government momentum, contrasting sharply with their successes in states like Negeri Sembilan where they flipped multiple PN-held seats. In Kelantan's competitive races, PHâ€“BN trailed by margins often exceeding 15 percentage points, as recorded in Election Commission tallies, underscoring limited appeal among the predominantly Malay-Muslim electorate loyal to PAS's long-term governance model.[56][57] The results highlighted entrenched voter allegiance to PN, attributed to PAS's uninterrupted rule since 1990 and resistance to federal narratives on economic development. This stability also affirmed the anti-party hopping law's role in preserving PN cohesion, as pre-election alliance discipline held firm without defections, unlike vulnerabilities exposed in prior cycles.[4][53]

Post-Election Developments

Government Formation and Leadership

Following the 2023 Kelantan state election, Perikatan Nasional (PN), dominated by Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS), secured 43 of the 45 seats in the Kelantan State Legislative Assembly, achieving a supermajority that obviated the need for coalitions with external parties to form government.[3][58] This overwhelming mandate enabled rapid reconstitution of the state executive under PAS leadership, with the new administration sworn in just three days after the poll results.[59] Datuk Mohd Nassuruddin Daud, a PAS assemblyman for Meranti and former state executive councillor overseeing Islamic development, was appointed Menteri Besar on August 15, 2023, taking oath before Sultan Muhammad V at Istana Jahar in Kota Bharu.[60][61] The transition followed incumbent Menteri Besar Datuk Ahmad Yakob's decision to step aside, citing personal reasons including health considerations, despite his re-election in Pasir Pekan. Nassuruddin's appointment received unanimous support from PN assemblymen, reflecting the coalition's internal consensus and the assembly's composition.[62] Mohamed Fadzli Hassan was concurrently named Deputy Menteri Besar, further solidifying PAS's hold on executive roles.[63] The state executive council (Exco) was finalized on August 23, 2023, comprising 10 members primarily from PAS, with portfolios allocated to emphasize continuity in PAS-governed priorities such as religious affairs, dakwah (proselytization), and welfare services.[64] Key figures like Mohd Nassuruddin retained oversight of Islamic development from prior roles, while allocations for assemblymen were equalized to promote unified governance under the supermajority framework.[65] This structure underscored PN's seamless retention of power, avoiding disruptions associated with coalition negotiations.[66]

Reactions and Political Analysis

Perikatan Nasional (PN) leaders, including PAS president Abdul Hadi Awang, hailed the party's capture of 43 out of 45 state seats as a resounding endorsement of conservative Islamic governance, interpreting the landslide as a popular mandate to advance hudud-inspired policies amid ongoing federal resistance.[3][2] This victory, retaining power in the PAS stronghold since 1990, was framed by PN as validation of Islamist priorities over federal reformism, with party officials emphasizing voter rejection of the unity government's inclusive coalition as eroding Malay-Muslim primacy.[38] In contrast, the unity government coalition under Pakatan Harapan and Barisan Nasional conceded the defeat promptly, with analysts attributing the rout to limited penetration among Malay voters, who comprised over 95% of Kelantan's electorate and showed minimal buy-in for the federal narrative of multicultural equity.[67][68] DAP and PKR figures acknowledged the ethnic polarization, noting that non-Malay support for the coalition remained confined to urban pockets but failed to sway the overwhelmingly Malay rural base loyal to PAS's ethno-religious appeals.[69] Political analysts interpreted the results as empirical confirmation of the "green wave"â€”PAS's surge in Malay support since the 2022 general electionâ€”sustained by causal factors including widespread distrust of the Anwar Ibrahim administration's federal inclusivity policies, perceived as undermining bumiputera privileges through alliances with non-Malay parties like DAP.[70][71] Exit polls and constituency data revealed near-unanimous Malay backing for PN candidates, with PAS securing over 70% of Malay votes in key areas, prioritizing identity-based conservatism over economic critiques of Kelantan's underdevelopment.[67][10] This pattern underscored a preference for Islamist moral governance as a bulwark against perceived cultural dilution, rather than reformist multiculturalism, despite PAS states like Kelantan lagging in GDP per capita and infrastructure metrics.[53] Pro-PAS commentators celebrated the outcome as a moral triumph for uncompromised Islamic values, arguing it demonstrated voter resilience against federal pressures to moderate sharia ambitions.[71] Critics, including unity government allies, countered that the win perpetuated opportunities lost for modernization, pointing to Kelantan's chronic poverty ratesâ€”hovering around 20% pre-electionâ€”and warning of deepened ethnic divides if Islamist exclusivity supplanted pragmatic development.[38][72] Independent observers noted the election's reinforcement of Malaysia's bifurcated polity, where Malay-majority states like Kelantan prioritize causal preservation of privileges via conservative Islamism, empirically sidelining multicultural alternatives despite their federal dominance elsewhere.[70][73]

Criticisms of Outcomes and Governance Implications

PAS's retention of all 45 seats in the 2023 Kelantan state election underscored its appeal rooted in long-term political stability and an anti-corruption stance, with the party touting a 30-year record of "zero corruption" in state administration as evidence of governance integrity.[74] Expansions in Sharia law implementation, including stricter enforcement of Islamic codes, have sustained support among the conservative Malay Muslim base, prioritizing moral and religious governance over rapid economic liberalization.[70] Despite these strengths, critics highlight persistent underdevelopment, with Kelantan's GDP per capita at RM10,552 (approximately USD 3,611) in 2023, the lowest among Malaysian states and far below the national average of RM56,734, according to Department of Statistics Malaysia data showing stagnant growth relative to peers like Penang or Selangor.[75] Infrastructure deficits remain acute, exemplified by chronic water supply disruptions affecting over 28% of the population without reliable clean water access as of 2023, despite federal allocations like RM500 million for upgrades, fueling arguments that PAS's religious focus has sidelined practical development.[76][77] Youth migration for employment opportunities outside the state has intensified due to limited local investments and job creation, with reports indicating rising outflows as economic opportunities lag.[78] The election outcome signals a broader conservative shift among Malay voters nationwide, evidenced by PAS's "green wave" gains in 2022-2023 polls, challenging federal narratives emphasizing progressive economic policies over ethno-religious conservatism.[70][10] However, the Perikatan Nasional's (PN) loss of the Nenggiri federal seat to Barisan Nasional in the August 2024 by-election, with a 3,372-vote margin in a traditional PAS stronghold, represents an isolated setback attributable to local candidate appeal rather than a reversal of the rightward trend.[79] This duality implies that while PAS's model ensures electoral dominance in rural Malay heartlands, sustained governance challenges could test its long-term viability amid demands for tangible socioeconomic progress.
