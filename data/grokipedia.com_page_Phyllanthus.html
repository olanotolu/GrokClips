Phyllanthus is the largest genus in the plant family Phyllanthaceae. It has approximately 1,065 accepted species.[1] Phyllanthus has a remarkable diversity of growth forms including annual and perennial herbs, shrubs, climbers, floating aquatics, and pachycaulous succulents. Some have flattened leaflike stems called cladodes. It has a wide variety of floral morphologies and chromosome numbers and has one of the widest range of pollen types of any seed plant genus. Despite their variety, almost all Phyllanthus species express a specific type of growth called "phyllanthoid branching" in which the vertical stems bear deciduous, floriferous (flower-bearing), plagiotropic (horizontal or oblique) stems. The leaves on the main (vertical) axes are reduced to scales called "cataphylls", while leaves on the other axes develop normally.[2] Phyllanthus is distributed in all tropical and subtropical regions on Earth. Phyllanthus was first described by Carl Linnaeus in 1753,[3]

Species

The circumscription of this genus has been a cause of much confusion and disagreement. Molecular phylogenetic studies have shown that Phyllanthus is paraphyletic over Reverchonia , Glochidion , Sauropus , and Breynia. A 2006 revision of the family Phyllanthaceae subsumed all four of these genera into an enlarged Phyllanthus. Subsequent studies suggested potential division into smaller genera.[6] A 2022 phylogenetic classification of tribe Phyllantheae revised the taxonomy, splitting the broad Phyllanthus into 10 monophyletic genera, including a core Phyllanthus and reinstatements such as Breynia, Cicca, Emblica, Glochidion, and others, based on molecular data combined with morphology.[8] Under this classification, core Phyllanthus includes around 220 primarily American species, while the total across the tribe exceeds 1,000. The Flora of China recognizes 32 species under the prior broad circumscription (as of 2008).[9]

Fossil record

Two fossil seeds of a Phyllanthus species have been extracted from borehole samples of the Middle Miocene fresh water deposits in Nowy Sacz Basin, West Carpathians, Poland. The seeds are similar to seeds of the fossil species † Phyllanthus triquetra and † Phyllanthus compassica from the Oligocene and Miocene of West Siberia. Phyllanthus fossils are known from several Miocene sites in Poland.

Pollination biology

Phyllanthus is of note in the fields of pollination biology and coevolution because some but not all species in the genus have a specialized mutualism with moths in the genus Epicephala (leafflower moths), in which the moths actively pollinate the flowers. This obligate mutualism, which has evolved independently at least five times, involves more than 500 species in the tribe Phyllantheae. While ensuring that the plant may produce viable seeds, the moths also lay eggs in the flowers' ovaries where their larvae consume a subset of the developing seeds as nourishment. Other species of Epicephala are pollinators of certain species of plants in the genera Glochidion and Breynia, both of which are phylogenetically nested within Phyllanthus.

Research and traditional medicine

Various species of the genus Phyllanthus have been used in traditional medicine across tropical and subtropical regions for treating ailments such as jaundice, diabetes, liver disorders, digestive issues, and urinary conditions. For example, P. niruri and P. amarus are employed in Ayurvedic and African traditional medicine for hepatoprotective and antidiabetic purposes, while P. emblica (Indian gooseberry or amla) is valued in Ayurveda for its antioxidant properties and use in rejuvenation therapies.[18][19] P. emblica fruit, particularly rich in hydrolyzable tannins, has a long history of use in traditional medicine and continues to be studied for potential biological properties including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects, as documented in research up to 2014.[20] More recent studies (2020–2025) have explored enhanced bioavailability formulations of P. amarus for anti-inflammatory and antiviral applications, and anti-inflammatory activities of P. niruri extracts.[21][22][23] Leaves, roots, stems, bark, and fruits of several species, such as P. niruri, contain lignans (e.g., phyllanthin and hypophyllanthin) and other phytochemicals including flavonoids, alkaloids, and terpenoids, which contribute to their pharmacological properties.[24][25][26]
