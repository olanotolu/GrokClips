Gentner Drummond

Gentner F. Drummond is an American attorney, rancher, and Republican politician serving as the 19th Attorney General of Oklahoma since January 9, 2023.[1][2] A seventh-generation Oklahoman with nearly 30 years of legal experience, Drummond previously worked as an assistant district attorney in Pawnee and Osage Counties and founded his own law firm specializing in banking, business, and energy law.[1][3] He earned a bachelor's degree from Oklahoma State University and a law degree from Georgetown University.[4] Drummond is a former U.S. Air Force captain and fighter pilot who led combat missions during the Gulf War, for which he received the Distinguished Flying Cross.[5][6] Elected in 2022 after defeating incumbent John O'Connor in the Republican primary and Democrat Angela Monson in the general election, Drummond has prioritized combating organized crime, including establishing Oklahoma's first Organized Crime Task Force to address illegal marijuana operations and launching a public tip line that has facilitated numerous arrests.[1][7] His tenure has involved multi-state legal efforts on issues such as parental rights in education and child protection, as well as ordering an investigative audit of the Oklahoma Department of Education.[8][9] Drummond has also pursued actions against deceptive fundraising schemes and engaged in public disputes, including a feud with State Treasurer Todd Russ over investment decisions in the state endowment fund.[10][11] In 2025, he announced his candidacy for governor of Oklahoma in the 2026 election.[12]

Early life and military service

Upbringing and family background

Gentner Frederick Drummond was born on October 1, 1963, in Stillwater, Oklahoma, into the Drummond family, a longstanding ranching lineage in Osage County.[13] As a seventh-generation Oklahoman, Drummond descends from Frederick Drummond, who arrived in the former Osage Nation in Indian Territory in 1886 and established early trading and ranching operations that laid the foundation for the family's agricultural holdings.[1][14][15] Raised on the family ranch in Osage County near Hominy, Drummond grew up immersed in daily ranch operations, performing labor from early morning until evening alongside relatives.[16] This environment exposed him from childhood to the practical demands of cattle ranching, including land management and livestock handling, which characterized the family's multigenerational commitment to agriculture in rural Oklahoma.[14] He has two younger brothers, David and Jonathan Drummond, who also pursued paths tied to the family's Osage County heritage.[17] The Drummond family's historical involvement in local commerce and community structures, such as operating trading posts and early municipal roles in Hominy, provided Drummond with firsthand observation of rural governance and economic self-reliance amid agricultural fluctuations.[14] These experiences fostered an early appreciation for disciplined work and resource stewardship, core elements of the family's ranching traditions without external idealization.[16]

Education

Drummond earned a Bachelor of Science in agricultural economics from Oklahoma State University, graduating as the institution's top student.[18][19] After completing nearly eight years of active duty in the U.S. Air Force, he attended Georgetown University Law Center, where he received a Juris Doctor in 1995.[20][17]

U.S. Air Force career and Gulf War service

Gentner Drummond was commissioned into the U.S. Air Force following his graduation from Oklahoma State University in 1985, where he trained as a fighter pilot through the Euro-NATO Joint Jet Pilot Training program.[19] He piloted F-15 Eagle fighter jets during his service.[17] On January 17, 1991, Drummond led the first U.S. combat strike package into Baghdad as part of Operation Desert Storm, commanding over 40 aircraft in the initial aerial assault of the Gulf War air campaign.[5] His leadership in this mission earned him the Distinguished Flying Cross for extraordinary achievement in aerial flight, recognizing his decision-making under fire that avoided friendly fire incidents despite initial reports of an enemy aircraft.[1] [6] Drummond completed 32 combat missions, accumulating 190 hours of flight time during the Gulf War conflict.[21] For his operational contributions, he received three Air Medals, along with additional commendations including Aerial Achievement Medals.[1] [5] Drummond continued his Air Force service post-Gulf War, attaining the rank of Captain before resigning in 1993 after eight years of active duty.[21] His combat experience highlighted precise execution and restraint in high-stakes engagements, contributing to the coalition's air superiority objectives.[5]

Legal and business career

Early legal practice as prosecutor

Following his military service, Gentner Drummond served as an assistant district attorney in Pawnee and Osage Counties, rural jurisdictions in northeastern Oklahoma, during the mid-1990s.[22][1] In this role, he prosecuted criminal cases as part of the local district attorney's office, contributing to law enforcement efforts in areas with limited resources and populations under 20,000 each at the time.[22] This early prosecutorial experience provided foundational involvement in the criminal justice system, handling matters typical of county-level dockets in such settings.[1]

Private law firm and banking involvement

In 1998, Gentner Drummond established his private legal practice through The Drummond Law Firm in Tulsa, Oklahoma, specializing in banking, business, and entertainment law.[3] The firm, known regionally for its expertise in banking law, provided advocacy in complex financial and commercial matters, reflecting Drummond's shift from public prosecution to entrepreneurial litigation and advisory services.[23] As principal shareholder and director of Blue Sky Bank since 2003, Drummond played a central role in its operations as a community-focused institution serving northeastern Oklahoma.[24] He advanced to chairman of the board, overseeing strategic expansions that enhanced the bank's footprint and stability amid regional economic challenges.[25] Notable initiatives under his leadership included the 2021 acquisition of The American Bank in Wagoner, which expanded access to seven branches across key communities and supported ongoing growth in lending and deposits.[26] A subsequent 2022 merger with First National Bank in Cordell further demonstrated risk-managed consolidation, prioritizing service to local businesses and agriculture while maintaining the bank's over-century-old Oklahoma roots.[27] These moves underscored Drummond's acumen in navigating regulatory and market risks to foster financial resilience without reliance on external subsidies.[17]

Political entry and campaigns prior to Attorney General

Political donations and party involvement

Prior to his 2018 campaign for Oklahoma Attorney General, Gentner Drummond's recorded political donations were limited in scope and primarily directed toward Republican candidates. In 2017, he contributed to the campaign of G.T. Bynum, a Republican running for mayor of Tulsa.[28] Drummond also made a $500 donation to the 2017 campaign of John Waldron, a Democratic candidate for the Oklahoma House of Representatives. This contribution, verified through campaign finance records, has been highlighted by critics as inconsistent with Drummond's Republican affiliation, especially after Waldron later served as chair of the Oklahoma Democratic Party.[29][30] No other significant pre-2018 donations to PACs supporting energy or law enforcement causes appear in public filings from the Oklahoma Ethics Commission or related disclosures.

2018 Attorney General campaign

In the 2018 Republican primary for Oklahoma Attorney General, Gentner Drummond challenged incumbent Mike Hunter, who had been appointed to the position in 2017 following Scott Pruitt's resignation.[31] The initial primary on June 26, 2018, advanced both candidates to a runoff, as neither secured a majority.[32] Drummond, a Tulsa-based attorney and rancher, positioned his campaign as an outsider bid emphasizing accountability and criticizing the office's handling under Hunter, amid a backdrop of negative advertising from both sides focused on fundraising practices and integrity.[31] Drummond self-financed much of his effort, loaning over $2 million to his campaign by late August 2018, one of the largest personal investments in an Oklahoma statewide race at the time.[33] The runoff election on August 28, 2018, drew approximately 296,567 votes, reflecting low turnout typical of Oklahoma runoffs. Hunter prevailed by a razor-thin margin of 271 votes, receiving 148,419 (50.05%) to Drummond's 148,148 (49.95%).[34] This outcome highlighted a split among Republican primary voters, with Hunter benefiting from incumbency and institutional support despite Drummond's significant financial push and attacks portraying the office as compromised.[35] Drummond conceded the race on August 29, 2018, acknowledging the results and calling for unity within the party.[36] The narrow defeat underscored voter priorities favoring continuity in the AG role amid ongoing state challenges like the opioid crisis, where the office was actively litigating against pharmaceutical companies, over Drummond's reform-oriented challenge.[34] No recount was pursued, as the margin exceeded provisional ballot uncertainties.[37]

Tenure as Oklahoma Attorney General

2022 Attorney General election

In the Republican primary election on June 28, 2022, Gentner Drummond defeated incumbent Attorney General John O'Connor by a narrow margin of approximately 50.3% to 49.7%, with total turnout among registered Republicans exceeding 180,000 votes.[38]) Drummond's victory reflected voter dissatisfaction with O'Connor's perceived alignment with Governor Kevin Stitt, positioning Drummond as a more independent candidate focused on governmental accountability and challenging federal overreach, particularly in cases like the McGirt decision on tribal jurisdiction.[39][40] Drummond's platform emphasized defending Second Amendment rights against potential federal encroachments and pursuing aggressive legal actions to hold accountable entities contributing to public harms, including commitments to litigate against pharmaceutical companies involved in the opioid epidemic.[39] These promises resonated with conservative voters seeking stronger resistance to external mandates and a prosecutor-oriented approach prioritizing state sovereignty.[41] In the general election on November 8, 2022, with no Democratic candidate on the ballot, Drummond faced Libertarian Lynda Steele and secured a decisive win, garnering 792,466 votes (73.76%) to Steele's 281,923 (26.24%), amid statewide turnout of about 58% for the midterm contests.[42][43] The outcome underscored a Republican preference for Drummond's emphasis on law enforcement integrity and Second Amendment protections over the Libertarian's narrower focus.[44] Drummond was sworn into office as Attorney General on January 9, 2023, marking a smooth transition from the primary upset to statewide leadership.[45]

Major achievements and policy actions

As Oklahoma's Attorney General, Gentner Drummond pursued legal actions against opioid distributors and manufacturers to hold them accountable for contributing to the state's crisis, securing a $32 million settlement with CVS Caremark in September 2025 over allegations of improper practices in controlled substance distribution.[46] His office facilitated the distribution of over $5 million in prior opioid settlement funds to nearly 40 cities and counties in August 2024 for abatement efforts, prioritizing community-level interventions based on demonstrated needs.[47] These recoveries supported empirical responses to overdose data, with Oklahoma reporting over 1,100 opioid-related deaths in 2023, underscoring the causal link between manufacturer conduct and public health burdens.[48] Drummond advocated for the enforcement of capital punishment in cases involving heinous crimes, personally attending each execution since taking office in January 2023 to oversee procedural integrity and support victims' families.[49] He pushed for a measured pace of executions to ensure sustainability and thorough clemency reviews, requesting delays such as a 30-day postponement for inmate Tremane Wood in August 2025 to allow additional scrutiny of evidence.[50] This approach balanced deterrenceâ€”Oklahoma executed seven inmates in 2024 amid a backlog of over 40 death sentencesâ€”with procedural safeguards, rejecting moratorium proposals while affirming the penalty's role in justice for aggravated murders.[51] In challenging federal overreach, Drummond prevailed in a 2025 lawsuit vacating a Biden administration Department of Health and Human Services rule that would have mandated state and provider funding for gender transition procedures by redefining sex discrimination under Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act.[52] He also filed suits against Environmental Protection Agency regulations, including a March 2024 challenge to methane emission rules deemed an assault on domestic energy production, and joined coalitions opposing California's truck emission standards and Vermont's Climate Superfund Act in May 2025 to safeguard Oklahoma's oil and gas sector, which accounts for over 10% of state GDP and thousands of jobs.[53][54] These efforts halted mandates prioritizing ideological environmental goals over verifiable energy reliability and economic data. Drummond advanced parental rights in education by joining multistate actions in September 2025 to require schools to notify parents of student gender transition discussions and protect restroom privacy, arguing that withholding such information undermines family authority and child welfare.[8] He opposed discriminatory diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives, urging Governor Kevin Stitt in September 2025 to seek a federal waiver from the Disadvantaged Business Enterprise program for inflating costs through race-based preferences and calling for removal of housing agency appointees enforcing similar practices.[55] Additionally, he co-signed a June 2024 letter to the American Bar Association demanding elimination of DEI accreditation standards lacking empirical ties to legal competence.[56] These positions emphasized merit-based systems, countering programs that, per state analyses, diverted resources without measurable benefits in outcomes like graduation rates or professional efficacy.

Controversies and legal challenges

In 2023, Oklahoma Attorney General Gentner Drummond initiated a lawsuit against the Statewide Virtual Charter School Board to challenge its approval of the St. Isidore of Seville Catholic Virtual School, the proposed nation's first publicly funded religious charter school.[57] Drummond argued that the charter violated Article II, Section 5 of the Oklahoma Constitution, which prohibits the use of public funds to support religious institutions, and potentially infringed on the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment by entangling the state with a Catholic entity that would require hiring based on religious criteria and teaching faith doctrines.[58] Supporters of the school, including the Catholic Church and religious liberty advocates, countered that charter schools operate independently as public schools under state law and that excluding religious providers discriminates under the Free Exercise Clause, citing precedents like Espinoza v. Montana Department of Revenue (2020).[59] The Oklahoma Supreme Court ruled in favor of Drummond in June 2024, holding that the charter established religion in violation of state law, a decision affirmed by an equally divided U.S. Supreme Court in May 2025 without setting national precedent.[60][61] Drummond's enforcement of state criminal jurisdiction post-McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020), which affirmed the existence of Indian reservations in eastern Oklahoma, has sparked multiple lawsuits from tribes alleging discriminatory prosecution practices that undermine the U.S. Supreme Court's ruling.[62] In a March 2023 letter, Drummond directed district attorneys to continue prosecuting non-citizens on reservations for certain offenses where state authority persists, prompting tribes such as the Modoc, Miami, Ottawa, and Eastern Shawnee to file federal suits in 2023 claiming the policy creates jurisdictional disparities and seeks to erode McGirt by appealing convictions or selectively enforcing laws.[63][64] Drummond defended these actions as upholding state sovereignty in areas outside federal or tribal exclusive jurisdiction, such as prosecuting non-Indians for crimes against Indians, and interceded in related gaming compact disputes to represent the state against gubernatorial overreach.[65] Critics, including tribal leaders, accused Drummond of overreach in undermining federal Indian law, though federal courts have upheld mixed jurisdiction models without fully resolving the disputes as of 2025.[66] Drummond has faced accusations of ethical lapses in high-profile prosecutions, including claims of prejudicing cases through public statements and reneging on agreements. In the 2025 Swadley BBQ criminal case involving state contract fraud, defense attorneys alleged Drummond violated Oklahoma Rules of Professional Conduct by declaring the defendant guilty in media interviews and predicting imprisonment, portraying it as a symbolic crackdown on corruption.[67] Drummond rejected these as baseless, asserting his comments reflected prosecutorial discretion and public accountability without influencing judicial outcomes. Similarly, in the Richard Glossip murder case, defense filings in July 2025 accused Drummond of breaching a February 2023 agreement to recommend Glossip's release after evidence of prosecutorial misconduct surfaced, instead pursuing non-capital retrial; Drummond maintained the deal was contingent on full evidentiary review and affirmed innocence claims required judicial vetting.[68] These challenges highlight tensions between aggressive law enforcement and due process, with no formal sanctions imposed as of October 2025.

Recent developments (2023â€“2025)

In October 2025, Attorney General Drummond demanded accountability from the Oklahoma Health Care Authority (OHCA) over the failed rollout of managed Medicaid care under SoonerCare, highlighting reimbursement delays for pediatric services that led to widespread provider complaints and risks to children's access to care. He cited empirical evidence of bureaucratic inefficiencies, including claims processing backlogs exceeding 90 days in some cases and a 20-30% drop in pediatric provider participation compared to fee-for-service models, arguing these failures stemmed from inadequate transition planning and vendor mismanagement. Drummond's letter to OHCA emphasized the need for immediate corrective actions to avert a statewide collapse in pediatric services, underscoring how state-contracted managed care organizations prioritized administrative costs over patient outcomes.[69][70] Drummond continued pursuing litigation against Meta Platforms, initiated in October 2023, alleging the company designed addictive features on Instagram and Facebook that exacerbated youth mental health issues through excessive screen time and harmful content algorithms. The suit claims Meta knowingly deployed tools like infinite scrolling and notifications to retain minors despite internal research showing links to anxiety, depression, and sleep disruption in users under 18, with Oklahoma reporting a 15% rise in adolescent emergency visits for mental health crises from 2019 to 2022 correlating with platform usage spikes. In September 2025, Drummond extended scrutiny to AI firms, warning companies including Meta to cease predatory interactions with children, such as flirtatious chatbots, to prevent further exploitation.[71][72] To defend Oklahoma's energy sector, Drummond in July and August 2025 opposed out-of-state lawsuits and radical net-zero standards that he argued threatened the state's oil and gas industry, which accounts for over 10% of GDP and 300,000 jobs. He filed amicus briefs and demanded transparency from climate organizations imposing emissions targets incompatible with reliable baseload power, citing data on wind and solar intermittency causing grid instability in states adopting similar policies, with Oklahoma's fossil fuels providing 70% of electricity generation. These actions aimed to preserve economic sovereignty against what Drummond described as ideologically driven overreach.[73][74] Drummond successfully halted a Biden-era federal rule in October 2025 that would have mandated states and providers to fund gender transition procedures, securing a preliminary injunction against the Department of Health and Human Services' reinterpretation of Medicaid requirements. The ruling prevented the imposition of an estimated $5-10 million in additional annual costs to Oklahoma taxpayers for procedures lacking long-term empirical support for efficacy in minors, based on reviews showing high regret rates (up to 30% in some cohorts) and complications from interventions like puberty blockers. This resolution exemplified Drummond's pattern of challenging federal overreach, resolving the mandate without protracted litigation.[75]

2026 Oklahoma gubernatorial campaign

Campaign announcement and platform

On January 13, 2025, Gentner Drummond formally announced his candidacy for the Republican nomination in the 2026 Oklahoma gubernatorial election, becoming the first major candidate to declare for the June primary.[76][22] The event took place in Pawhuska, Osage County, at the Osage County Fairgrounds, where Drummond, a member of a prominent local ranching family, gathered with supporters to emphasize returning Oklahoma to foundational principles of self-reliance and effective governance.[77][78] Drummond's platform centered on fiscal discipline to reduce living costs and expand economic opportunities for residents, pledging to prioritize policies that deliver bigger paychecks through streamlined state operations rather than expansive government programs.[79] He highlighted education reform as a core priority, aiming to elevate Oklahoma from its 50th national ranking by addressing the teacher shortage affecting nearly 5,000 classrooms staffed by emergency-certified personnel and positioning the state as a leader in student outcomes.[80][81] This approach rejected reliance on increased welfare-style interventions, favoring incentives for individual achievement and local control over centralized mandates. Drummond positioned his military service as a U.S. Air Force fighter pilot and experience in private enterprise as essential qualifications for executive decision-making, arguing these instilled discipline and results-oriented leadership needed to safeguard Oklahoma's sovereignty against external overreach.[12] His announcement underscored conservative commitments to justice, family protection, and state values, framing the campaign as a defense of empirical governance over elite-driven agendas.[12]

Fundraising, polling, and primary competition

Drummond's campaign for the 2026 Republican gubernatorial nomination raised $1.6 million in the first quarter of 2025, setting a record for the highest amount collected by any Oklahoma gubernatorial candidate at that stage.[82][83] This haul, reported on April 2, 2025, reflected strong early donor support amid an open primary following term limits for incumbent Governor Kevin Stitt.[84] Polling in the Republican primary has indicated volatility, with early surveys favoring Drummond but recent data showing a tightening contest against primary rival Charles McCall, former Speaker of the Oklahoma House. An August 2025 poll by Cole Hargrave Snodgrass & Associates placed Drummond at the lead with significant margins over McCall and State Superintendent Ryan Walters.[85] By late September, a survey reported the race as a dead heat.[86] A Stratus Intelligence poll conducted October 10â€“15, 2025, among 806 likely Republican primary voters and commissioned by McCall's campaign, showed McCall ahead of Drummond by 8 percentage points.[87][88] Such shifts underscore the influence of campaign spending and internal dynamics in an early-heating primary, where over $3 million had already been expended by early October.[89] McCall has emerged as Drummond's primary challenger, launching attacks portraying Drummond's record as Attorney General as insufficiently aligned with core Republican priorities despite his party affiliation.[90] McCall's campaign ads have highlighted his own legislative successes on issues like transgender policies while criticizing Drummond for perceived leniency, such as in handling certain cultural debates.[91] Drummond has responded by emphasizing his executive experience in the AG's office, including high-profile enforcement actions, as giving him an advantage over legislative-focused rivals like McCall in governing the state.[92] Other declared candidates, such as former state Senator Mike Mazzei, trail in fundraising and polling visibility.[84]

Personal life and public image

Family and residences

Gentner Drummond is married to Wendy Drummond, an attorney, businesswoman, and nonprofit leader experienced in corporate governance, employment law, and technology sectors.[93] The couple has a blended family with six children and seven grandchildren, as Drummond publicly stated in a 2025 campaign-related post emphasizing their commitment to public service in Oklahoma.[94] Drummond maintains professional residence in Oklahoma City, the seat of the Attorney General's office, while his family heritage ties to ranching operations in the Hominy area of Osage County, where ancestors like Frederick Drummond established the foundational Drummond Ranch and related enterprises in the early 20th century.[1][17] The historic Fred and Addie Drummond Home in Hominy, built in 1905, serves as a preserved site reflecting the family's longstanding community roots, though it operates as a museum rather than a private dwelling.[95]

Awards, honors, and civic engagements

Drummond received the Distinguished Flying Cross for "extraordinary achievement" in leading the first U.S. combat mission of the Gulf War on January 17, 1991, during Operation Desert Storm.[1] This decoration recognized his airmanship and decision-making under fire, where he opted not to engage targets immediately to avoid potential civilian casualties, a choice that initially led to his temporary arrest but was later vindicated.[6] In addition to the Distinguished Flying Cross, Drummond earned three Air Medals, denoted with two Oak Leaf Clusters, for meritorious achievement in aerial flight during combat operations.[5] He also received four Aerial Achievement Medals for sustained meritorious service in non-combat roles.[96] These honors underscore his extensive flight experience, exceeding 2,000 hours in the F-15 Eagle fighter jet.[5] Beyond military accolades, Drummond has engaged in civic roles supporting local justice and economic oversight in Oklahoma. As a former assistant district attorney in Osage County, he contributed to community-level prosecutions, emphasizing accountability in rural legal systems.[97] His family ranching background has involved stewardship of land and resources, aligning with broader civic commitments to agricultural sustainability, though specific board or initiative participations remain tied to professional rather than honorary capacities.[17] No formal state-level honors for his Attorney General tenure, such as dedicated officer awards, have been publicly documented as of 2025.

Electoral history

2018 Oklahoma Attorney General election

In the Republican primary for the 2018 Oklahoma Attorney General election, held on June 26, 2018, incumbent Mike Hunter and challenger Gentner Drummond finished first and second, respectively, advancing to an August 28 runoff due to no candidate receiving a majority.[98] The primary reflected internal GOP divisions, with Drummond positioning himself as an outsider critic of state government practices.[99] The runoff proved highly competitive, with low voter turnout typical of Oklahoma's summer primaries exacerbating the closeness. Hunter secured victory by a narrow margin of fewer than 300 votes out of approximately 182,000 cast statewide, bolstered by strong performance in absentee and early voting despite Drummond leading on election-day ballots.[32][99] Drummond conceded the race on August 29, 2018, praising the democratic process while noting the razor-thin result underscored voter dissatisfaction with the incumbent.[36]

2022 Oklahoma Attorney General election

In the Republican primary election on June 28, 2022, Gentner Drummond defeated incumbent Attorney General John O'Connor by a narrow margin of 180,338 votes (50.9%) to 173,930 votes (49.1%), marking an upset against the appointed officeholder who had assumed the role in 2021 following Mike Hunter's resignation.[103][104] This result reflected voter preference for Drummond's emphasis on combating government overreach and prioritizing law enforcement support, resonating particularly among rank-and-file Republicans disillusioned with the incumbent's handling of issues like opioid litigation settlements.[105] With no Democratic candidate in the race, Drummond faced only Libertarian Lynda Steele in the general election on November 8, 2022, securing a decisive victory with 792,466 votes (73.76%) to Steele's 281,923 (26.24%).[42] The outcome underscored strong Republican turnout in a midterm cycle where Oklahoma's overall voter participation reached approximately 53% of registered voters, with Drummond's margins expanding in rural countiesâ€”often exceeding 80%â€”compared to closer contests in urban areas like Oklahoma County, indicating his platform's appeal to conservative base voters focused on fiscal conservatism and anti-corruption measures.[43] Official results were certified by the Oklahoma State Election Board on November 22, 2022, without requests for recounts.[102]
