Aníbal Milhais

Aníbal Augusto Milhais (9 July 1895 – 3 June 1970), nicknamed Soldado Milhões ("Soldier Millions"), was a Portuguese infantryman who gained renown for his exceptional valor during the Battle of La Lys in World War I, single-handedly repelling German advances with prolonged machine-gun fire to cover his unit's retreat.[1][2] A farmer from the rural village of Valongo in northern Portugal before his 1915 conscription, Milhais served in the Portuguese Expeditionary Corps' 2nd Division, deploying to the Western Front in 1917.[1][2] On 9 April 1918, amid the German Spring Offensive's Operation Georgette, Milhais manned a Lewis gun position alone after his comrades withdrew under heavy assault by two German divisions; he fired continuously for hours, expending 400 rounds and disrupting the enemy advance despite jamming and exhaustion.[1][2] Subsequently traversing enemy-held territory on foot for three days—armed only with rifle and bayonet—he rescued a wounded Scottish major and rejoined Portuguese lines, later aiding a Belgian unit in similar fashion.[1][2] His actions earned praise from Major Ferreira de Amaral, who declared Milhais "worth one million soldiers," originating his moniker.[2] As the most decorated Portuguese soldier of the war, Milhais received Portugal's paramount military distinction, the Order of the Tower and Sword—the sole battlefield award of its kind—and France's Légion d'honneur.[1][2] Postwar, despite his heroism, he faced economic hardship farming in his renamed hometown of Valongo de Milhais (honored in 1924), fathering nine children and briefly emigrating to Brazil before securing a state pension.[1][2] Milhais remains a symbol of Portuguese resilience in national memory.[1][2]

Early Life and Background

Birth and Family

Aníbal Augusto Milhais was born on July 9, 1895, in the rural village of Valongo de Milhais, a small parish in the municipality of Murça, located in the Vila Real district of northern Portugal.[3] He was the son of António Manuel Milhais and Umbelina Rosa Milhais, both small-scale farmers whose livelihood depended on subsistence agriculture in a region characterized by hilly terrain and modest agrarian communities.[3] Raised in this impoverished farming household amid the economic constraints of late 19th-century rural Portugal, Milhais contributed to family labor from an early age, tending crops and livestock, which fostered physical endurance suited to demanding fieldwork.[1][4] Formal education was minimal, as was common for children in such isolated, agrarian settings where primary schooling often ended after basic literacy, prioritizing practical skills for survival over academic pursuits.[2]

Pre-Military Occupation and Draft into Service

Aníbal Milhais, born on July 9, 1895, in the rural village of Valongo de Milhais within the parish of Murça in northern Portugal, spent his early adulthood engaged in agricultural labor typical of the region's agrarian economy.[1] As a farmer from a humble background with no formal education, he worked the land in his hometown, supporting himself through manual farm duties amid Portugal's pre-war rural subsistence lifestyle.[5] This occupation reflected the limited opportunities available to young men in remote Trás-os-Montes province, where most residents depended on small-scale farming and seasonal labor.[6] Portugal, initially neutral in World War I following the conflict's outbreak in 1914, faced escalating pressures from Allied obligations and German actions, such as the seizure of Portuguese merchant ships in 1916, prompting early conscription efforts to bolster defenses.[2] On July 30, 1915, at the age of 20, Milhais was involuntarily drafted into military service as part of Portugal's expanding mobilization, preceding the nation's formal declaration of war on Germany on March 9, 1916. This conscription wave aimed to prepare reserves for potential involvement, drawing from civilian populations like Milhais's rural cohort despite the country's neutrality at the time.[7] Upon enlistment, Milhais was assigned to the Infantry of Bragança, specifically the 30th Infantry Regiment, where he underwent initial basic training focused on infantry fundamentals in anticipation of broader deployments.[8] This unit, rooted in the northeastern district, served as a key component of Portugal's army restructuring, which later contributed personnel to the Portuguese Expeditionary Corps (CEP) mobilized in 1917 for the Western Front.[1] His service thus represented the involuntary transition from civilian agrarian life to military obligation, driven by national imperatives rather than personal volition.[6]

World War I Service

Training and Deployment to France

Aníbal Milhais was conscripted into the Portuguese Army's Infantry Regiment of Bragança on July 30, 1915, and completed initial basic training in Portugal focused on infantry fundamentals such as marching, bayonet drills, and rifle handling with the standard Mauser-Vergueiro model. This domestic preparation occurred amid Portugal's gradual military mobilization following its declaration of war on Germany in March 1916, as the nation organized the Corpo Expedicionário Português (CEP) for overseas deployment despite limited prior experience in modern industrialized warfare.[1] [6] By early 1917, Milhais joined the CEP's 2nd Division, which shipped to France starting in February as part of the Allied reinforcement effort on the Western Front. Upon arrival at ports like Brest, Portuguese contingents received British-supplied equipment, including Lewis light machine guns, and underwent intensive supplemental training under British instructors—both during transit stops in southern England and at facilities in France such as the Infantry Training School at Marthes. This regimen emphasized trench construction, gas defense, and weapons proficiency, with documented Lewis gun firing drills conducted as late as June 23, 1917, to bridge gaps in the troops' prior exposure to static frontline tactics.[9] [10] [11] Integration into the British sector in French Flanders exposed arriving units like the 2nd Division to immediate logistical strains, including shortages of seasoned officers, unfamiliarity with coordinated artillery-infantry assaults, and the physical toll of trench environments marked by waterlogged earth, vermin infestations, and routine bombardment. British mentorship facilitated sector handovers, with the CEP eventually holding an 18-kilometer front by November 1917, allowing phased adaptation amid high attrition from illness and desertion rates exceeding 10 percent in some battalions due to these harsh conditions.[11]

Actions in the Battle of La Lys

On 9 April 1918, during the initial phase of the German Operation Georgette in the Battle of La Lys, Aníbal Milhais, serving with the Portuguese Expeditionary Corps' 2nd Infantry Division, Trás os Montes Brigade, found himself isolated after a massive artillery barrage commencing at 4 a.m. overwhelmed his sector near La Couture.[1] [2] His battalion, alongside adjacent Scottish units, retreated under the intensity of the assault, leaving Milhais alone on a forward hill position armed with a Lewis light machine gun.[1] Milhais manned the gun single-handedly, firing continuously and shifting positions across the hill to mimic the presence of an entire regiment, thereby repulsing multiple German assaults and pinning down advancing enemy infantry.[1] He depleted several ammunition belts—estimated at around 400 rounds—sourced from his own supply and nearby casualties, sustaining fire for approximately three to four hours into the evening and night of 9–10 April.[1] [2] This prolonged defense deceived the Germans into believing they confronted a larger, organized force, delaying their push and affording retreating Portuguese and British troops critical time to reorganize and withdraw without encirclement.[1] [2] Once ammunition exhausted, Milhais abandoned the position and undertook a perilous solo traversal of no-man's-land over three days, navigating shell craters, barbed wire entanglements, and intermittent enemy fire while evading patrols.[1] [2] During this journey, he crossed contested lines twice, sustaining minor shrapnel wounds but avoiding capture, and encountered and assisted a stranded Scottish officer, with whom he eventually rejoined allied lines.[1] [2] British and Belgian officers later corroborated the effectiveness of his stand through frontline reports, noting its localized but decisive disruption of the German momentum.[2]

Immediate Recognition and Military Awards

Immediately after the Battle of La Lys on April 9, 1918, Aníbal Milhais earned the nickname "Soldado Milhões" from his commander, Major João Ferreira do Amaral, who proclaimed that Milhais's solitary defense equated to the efforts of millions of soldiers in staving off the German assault.[1][12] Though his actions went initially unnoticed during the chaotic Portuguese retreat, they were corroborated by eyewitness accounts from comrades and a Scottish officer's report, prompting official acknowledgment that summer in a ceremony before 15,000 Allied troops.[1] Milhais received the Military Order of the Tower and Sword (Ordem Militar da Torre e Espada), Portugal's preeminent military decoration for valor, directly from Major do Amaral; he stands as the sole enlisted Portuguese soldier awarded this honor for World War I service.[1][13] He was further decorated with the first-class Portuguese War Cross (Cruz de Guerra), affirming the tactical delay his machine-gun fire imposed on two German regiments.[13]

Post-War Challenges and Life

Return to Portugal and Economic Hardships

Following the Armistice on 11 November 1918, Milhais was demobilized and repatriated to Portugal with elements of the Portuguese Expeditionary Corps (CEP), arriving in Lisbon on 2 February 1919 aboard the transport ship Helenus as part of the 15th Infantry Battalion.[8] He returned to his native village of Valadares in the municipality of Murça, resuming subsistence farming on family land as his primary means of livelihood, a common path for rural CEP veterans lacking other skills or opportunities.[2] Portugal's post-war economy was severely strained, teetering on the brink of bankruptcy due to wartime expenditures, disrupted trade, and fiscal mismanagement under the First Republic. Inflation surged dramatically from 1918 onward, with prices escalating to approximately 24 times pre-war levels by 1924, eroding purchasing power and wages amid declining agricultural output and industrial stagnation.[14] This economic turmoil exacerbated unemployment and poverty, particularly in rural areas dependent on farming, where veterans like Milhais faced diminished returns from crops and livestock amid high costs for essentials.[15] Milhais married his pre-war sweetheart, Teresa de Jesus, shortly after his return, and they began raising a family that would eventually include nine children, efforts at self-reliance hampered by the absence of structured post-war support for CEP personnel.[2] Despite his battlefield renown, he encountered the same neglect afflicting many returning soldiers, including inadequate reintegration policies that left thousands—over 8,000 of whom were disabled—struggling without pensions, land grants, or vocational aid, reflecting broader governmental priorities favoring political instability over veteran welfare.[16] Agricultural hardships, compounded by inflation and lack of credit access, forced reliance on meager harvests, underscoring the disconnect between military heroism and civilian economic reality for rural ex-servicemen.[17]

Efforts to Secure Support and Government Response

Following his return to Portugal in 1919, Aníbal Milhais faced persistent financial difficulties despite his wartime acclaim, prompting him to publicly appeal for support through newspapers and veteran networks in the 1920s to draw attention to the neglect of soldiers like himself.[18] These efforts highlighted the contrast between his heroic reputation and his inability to secure stable income from farming or prior one-time payments totaling around 18,419 escudos plus scattered additional sums of three to four contos, which proved insufficient amid postwar economic instability.[18] Media coverage of Milhais's plight generated public outcry, pressuring the Portuguese government to respond by granting him a modest pension in the late 1920s, calibrated to provide basic sustenance as a national figure rather than comprehensive compensation for his service.[7] This award, while acknowledging his status, reflected administrative inertia, as years elapsed between his demobilization and formal relief, with the amount falling short of ensuring long-term security for his growing family.[7] In 1928, still seeking better prospects, Milhais emigrated briefly to Brazil with his family, hoping to leverage opportunities abroad unavailable at home. The Portuguese expatriate community there, recognizing his fame, rallied to provide funds that enabled his prompt return to Portugal later that year, prioritizing familial obligations and domestic stability over permanent relocation.[7] This episode underscored the limits of individual initiative against entrenched economic barriers, as government aid remained reactive and minimal despite his symbolic value to the nation.

Family Life and Later Career

Following his return from World War I, Aníbal Milhais married Teresa de Jesus on 2 February 1919 and fathered nine children with her, establishing a large family in his rural hometown of Valongo.[2] The couple faced ongoing challenges in raising their children, compounded by Milhais's lingering health issues from combat wounds, which limited his physical capacity for demanding work.[6] Milhais sustained his family through agricultural labor, working as a farmer in the Trás-os-Montes region, a continuation of the rural occupation common to his background despite the physical toll of age and injuries.[19] Portugal's post-war economy, strained by near-bankruptcy and inflation, provided little stability, with his military pension offering only minimal support against these interwar fluctuations.[2] In response to acute financial pressures, Milhais briefly emigrated to Brazil in March 1928 seeking better opportunities but returned to Portugal on 29 August of the same year, resuming local farming upon receiving his pension arrears.[20][7] Throughout his later years, Milhais remained apolitical, prioritizing family provision over public or partisan engagement, reflecting his duty-focused character forged in military service rather than seeking leverage from his wartime reputation.[19] This resilience enabled him to maintain household stability in Valongo amid persistent rural poverty, without documented involvement in political movements or controversies of the era.[18]

Death and Enduring Legacy

Final Years and Death

In his final years, Aníbal Milhais continued to reside in Valongo de Milhais, the rural village in northern Portugal that had adopted his surname in recognition of his wartime exploits, where he sustained himself through modest agricultural labor and a limited pension tied to his military honors, reportedly yielding around 1,000 escudos monthly in the late 1960s.[21] Despite his status as Portugal's most decorated World War I veteran, Milhais lived in relative seclusion, with little public attention or material support from the state beyond basic entitlements.[22] Milhais died on June 3, 1970, at the age of 74, in his hometown of Valongo de Milhais.[19] The cause of death was not publicly detailed in contemporary records, consistent with natural decline in advanced age absent specific ailments noted. He was buried in the local Cemitério de Valongo de Milhais without a state funeral or elaborate ceremony at the time, underscoring the disconnect between his heroic legacy and post-war governmental engagement.

National Honors and Commemorations

Following Aníbal Milhais's death on June 3, 1970, Portugal established enduring tributes to his World War I heroism, including the renaming of his native parish from Valongo to Valongo de Milhais by government decree on July 8, 1924, a designation that persists as official recognition of his valor.[23] In Murça, a bust of Milhais stands as a public monument honoring the soldier from the municipality who earned the nickname "Soldado Milhões" for his stand at La Lys.[24] The Casa Museu Soldado Milhões, housed in Milhais's former residence at Rua Herói Milhões in Valongo de Milhais, was inaugurated on April 10, 2023, by President Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa, who described him as a "unique hero" whose actions exemplified individual courage in national defense; the museum preserves artifacts and narratives of his service to educate on Portugal's WWI contributions.[25][26][27] Annually on April 9, Portugal observes National Ex-Combatants' Day, commemorating the Battle of La Lys where Milhais distinguished himself; military ceremonies on this date affirm his role as an exemplar of Portuguese resilience, with references to his feats integrated into official remembrances of the expeditionary force's sacrifices.[28]

Historical Assessment and Cultural Depictions

Aníbal Milhais holds a prominent place in military historiography as Portugal's most decorated soldier of the First World War, with his sustained machine-gun fire during the Battle of La Lys on April 9, 1918, credited by Allied eyewitnesses for delaying two German regiments and enabling the orderly retreat of Portuguese and Scottish units amid a broader German offensive that inflicted heavy casualties on the Portuguese Expeditionary Corps.[1][2] This empirical demonstration of individual tenacity—firing over 400 rounds while isolated for three days—countered immediate routs and earned battlefield commendations, including the rare immediate bestowal of Portugal's Order of the Tower and Sword, as documented in military dispatches rather than postwar retrospectives.[29] Historians assess such actions as causally significant in localized defensive stands, resisting pacifist-leaning narratives that minimize personal agency amid industrialized warfare's aggregate toll, where Portuguese forces suffered over 7,000 casualties in days due to inadequate preparation and positioning.[1] Cultural depictions of Milhais reinforce his "Soldado Milhões" epithet, portraying his solo defense as pivotal to unit survival and emblematic of unyielding resolve against overwhelming odds. The 2018 Portuguese film Hero on the Front (original title Soldado Milhões), directed by Afonso Oliveira, dramatizes his La Lys exploits through flashbacks and veteran recollections, emphasizing the machine-gunner's isolation and suppressive fire's tactical impact on the battle's flank.[30] Children's literature, such as José Jorge Letria's illustrated biography Aníbal Milhais, adapts his story for younger audiences, highlighting bravery amid illiteracy and humble origins without romanticizing war's mechanics.[31] Recent media, including 21st-century podcasts and articles, perpetuate this narrative, often citing his nickname—coined by a Belgian officer—as shorthand for outsized individual contribution in a conflict dominated by mass conscription and attrition.[32] While historiographic praise centers on verifiable feats that merited four military medals and foreign honors like the French Croix de Guerre, assessments temper hero veneration with recognition of war's disproportionate costs, including the decimation of Portugal's expeditionary forces and postwar neglect of veterans through economic instability and delayed pensions.[2] Milhais's own trajectory—postwar poverty despite accolades—illustrates how societal responses prioritized symbolic commemoration over sustained merit-based support, challenging myths of uniform welfare for contributors in collective endeavors and underscoring causal disconnects between battlefield efficacy and institutional reciprocity.[1] This duality positions him as a case study in selective historical memory, where empirical valor endures amid critiques of militarism's human ledger.
