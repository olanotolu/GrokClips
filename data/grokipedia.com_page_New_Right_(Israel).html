New Right (Israel)

The New Right (Hebrew: הימין החדש, HaYamin HeHadash) is a right-wing political party in Israel, established on 29 December 2018 by Naftali Bennett and Ayelet Shaked following their departure from the Jewish Home party to broaden appeal among both secular and religious Zionist voters.[1][2] The party advocates a platform centered on national security, economic liberalism, and limiting judicial overreach, explicitly opposing the creation of a Palestinian state while supporting the annexation of Area C in the West Bank and granting limited citizenship to its Palestinian residents.[1][2] In its debut April 2019 election, the New Right secured 3.2% of the vote but failed to cross the electoral threshold, resulting in no Knesset seats; it subsequently merged into broader right-wing alliances, contributing to the Yamina list that won seven seats in 2021 and enabled Bennett to serve as prime minister from June 2021 to June 2022.[2][1] During Bennett's tenure, the government advanced bipartisan U.S. relations, securing a multi-billion-dollar military aid package, though it faced internal collapse amid coalition fractures over judicial reforms and security policies.[3] As of 2025, the party holds no independent seats in the Knesset, with Bennett preparing a potential political return under a new banner amid ongoing right-wing dynamics in Israeli politics.[4][5]

Origins and Historical Development

Founding and Split from Jewish Home

Naftali Bennett and Ayelet Shaked, respectively the leader and No. 2 of the Jewish Home party, announced their resignation from the party and the formation of the New Right (HaYamin HeHadash) on December 29, 2018.[6] The move came amid tensions within Jewish Home, which had been aligning more closely with hardline settler groups and facing constraints under Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's influence, diminishing the faction's independent power.[6] Bennett stated that the party had been "forced to fold and remain in the government despite what we believe," highlighting a desire for greater autonomy and broader leadership ambitions unfeasible within a niche religious framework.[6] The split reflected ideological disagreements over Jewish Home's increasing emphasis on religious Zionism and potential mergers with extremist elements, such as the proposed alliance with the ultranationalist Otzma Yehudit, which Bennett and Shaked opposed as it risked alienating moderate voters.[7] Their new venture aimed to refine right-wing politics toward pragmatic secular nationalism, targeting non-religious voters disillusioned by fragmented coalitions dominated by ultra-Orthodox interests or settler priorities.[7] Shaked emphasized creating "a full and equal partnership" between Orthodox and secular Israelis, with plans for an alternating candidate list to symbolize unity.[6][7] At its launch, the New Right's platform centered on uncompromising territorial claims, rejecting a Palestinian state and advocating full sovereignty over the Land of Israel to address Israel's security imperatives.[6] This nationalist core was positioned as a first-principles response to geopolitical threats, while seeking to recapture right-wing mandates lost to centrist parties by appealing to a wider electorate beyond traditional religious bases.[6]

Initial Challenges and 2019 Elections

The New Right faced significant initial obstacles in Israel's proportional representation system, where parties must secure at least 3.25% of the national vote to gain Knesset seats, amid a crowded right-wing field dominated by Benjamin Netanyahu's Likud party. In the April 9, 2019, legislative elections, the party garnered 139,527 votes, equivalent to 3.22% of the total valid votes cast, narrowly missing the threshold and resulting in zero seats.[8] This outcome stemmed primarily from vote fragmentation on the right, as supporters of secular nationalism dispersed their ballots across multiple lists, including Likud (which received 26.46%) and the Union of Right-Wing Parties (5.0%), rather than a wholesale rejection of the New Right's platform.[9] The September 17, 2019, repeat elections exacerbated these challenges, with the New Right obtaining 133,941 votes or 3.00% of the vote share, again failing to cross the threshold despite a similar voter turnout of approximately 71%.[10] Netanyahu's continued electoral strength, bolstered by his personal appeal and Likud's organizational machine, drew away potential voters, while the absence of pre-election alliances with other right-wing factions amplified the risks inherent to launching a new party without a consolidated bloc.[11] Internal party assessments later attributed the shortfall to strategic miscalculations in not hedging against threshold volatility in a system prone to small margins determining representation, though polling had suggested viability close to the cutoff.[12] Data on voter preferences indicated the New Right's appeal lay among urban, secular right-wing demographics, particularly in central Israel locales like Herzliya, where it captured up to 57% of its national support in select polling stations, reflecting traction with educated, non-religious nationalists disillusioned with religious-leaning alternatives but unwilling to consolidate behind Likud.[13] This base's fragmentation underscored empirical barriers for emerging parties in Israel's multiparty dynamics, where loyalty to established leaders like Netanyahu often overrides ideological affinity, rather than inherent flaws in the New Right's positioning.[13]

Shifts in Alliances and 2021-2022 Electoral Cycles

In the 2021 Israeli legislative election held on March 23, Yamina, led by Naftali Bennett and drawing primarily from New Right candidates, obtained 240,686 votes, equating to 5.0% of the total and securing 7 seats in the 120-member Knesset.[14] This performance positioned Bennett as a pivotal figure in post-election negotiations, culminating in a diverse coalition agreement on June 2, 2021, that excluded Benjamin Netanyahu's Likud and included parties from right-wing, centrist, left-wing, and—for the first time—an Arab Islamist faction (Ra'am). Bennett assumed the premiership on June 13, 2021, under a rotation deal with Yesh Atid leader Yair Lapid, marking the New Right's indirect influence through Yamina in ousting Netanyahu after 12 consecutive years in power.[15] Ayelet Shaked, co-founder of the New Right, was not placed on Yamina's electoral list for 2021, reflecting internal tensions and Bennett's strategic focus on consolidating support without her prominence, which led her to abstain from the ballot while later joining the coalition as Minister of the Interior. The coalition's fragility, reliant on slender margins and ideological compromises, eroded amid disputes over judicial reforms and security issues, prompting Bennett to announce on June 20, 2022, that he would not contest the ensuing election and dissolving the Knesset on June 30, 2022, after a member defected. This brief tenure from June 2021 to June 2022 demonstrated the New Right's adaptive alliance tactics but also exposed vulnerabilities in sustaining power without a unified right-wing base.[16] For the November 1, 2022, election, Shaked sought to independently revive the New Right by heading a joint list with the Jewish Home party, targeting secular right-wing voters disillusioned with larger blocs. The slate garnered 86,803 votes, or 2.08% of the total—below the 3.25% electoral threshold—resulting in zero seats and vote wastage that analysts attributed to persistent fragmentation among smaller right-leaning parties.[17] This marginalization underscored how repeated alliance shifts, while enabling short-term gains like Bennett's premiership, exacerbated vote splitting; data from the election showed that consolidating New Right and similar splinter votes into Netanyahu's bloc could have added 2-3 seats, potentially altering bloc dynamics given the razor-thin 64-52 right-wing majority. Such patterns of dispersion, driven by personal leadership rivalries over programmatic unity, diminished the party's leverage within broader right-wing coalitions.[18]

Post-2022 Trajectory and Revival Attempts

Following the New Right's failure to cross the 3.25% electoral threshold in the November 1, 2022, Knesset elections, where it garnered approximately 2.9% of the vote, the party entered dormancy without fielding candidates in subsequent cycles or formally reconstituting. Merger discussions with Likud, initiated by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu shortly after the vote, collapsed when co-founder Ayelet Shaked rejected integration, prioritizing independent right-wing alignment over absorption. Shaked shifted to individual public advocacy, critiquing government strategies on judicial reforms and security without advancing party revival efforts through 2025. The October 7, 2023, Hamas attack, which killed 1,195 Israelis and prompted a surge in support for assertive territorial and security policies—rising from 34% to 47% approval for expanded West Bank control by October 2024—amplified broader right-wing sentiment but left the New Right marginalized.[19] Shaked called for the total elimination of Hamas assets in Gaza in immediate post-attack statements, aligning with hawkish positions, yet the party's absence from Knesset representation hindered organized response.[20] Empirical voter data indicates shifts toward consolidated right-wing lists like Religious Zionism, which secured 14 seats in 2022 by unifying factions, reflecting the electoral arithmetic where ideological fragmentation below the threshold yields zero representation despite niche appeal. By 2025, opinion polls registered no distinct New Right slate above the threshold, with hypothetical support lingering at 2-3% amid discussions of broader right-wing unions that Shaked endorsed, including potential Bennett-Lieberman pacts, but without materializing as party revival.[21] Naftali Bennett's separate "Bennett 2026" initiative polled up to 23 seats in September 2025 surveys, drawing former New Right voters and underscoring the pragmatic consolidation overriding purist independence.[22] This trajectory highlights how post-October 7 security imperatives reinforced voter preference for viable blocs over dormant entities, as evidenced by Religious Zionism's sustained polling strength in fragmented right-wing landscapes.[23]

Ideological Foundations and Policy Positions

Nationalist Security and Territorial Stance

The New Right party supports the extension of full Israeli sovereignty over Judea and Samaria, positing that territorial concessions have empirically heightened security risks rather than fostering stability. This stance draws on the 2005 Gaza disengagement, which enabled Hamas to consolidate control and escalate rocket fire; prior to the withdrawal, annual rocket launches from Gaza were limited, but post-disengagement, they surged, with thousands fired in subsequent years, necessitating repeated military operations to mitigate threats to southern Israeli populations.[24][25] Party leaders argue that similar withdrawals from Judea and Samaria would replicate this pattern, empowering adversarial groups and eroding deterrence without reciprocal security gains from Palestinian counterparts.[26] In critiquing concession-oriented frameworks like the Oslo Accords, the party highlights their causal failure to deliver peace, as evidenced by the accords' aftermath: increased Palestinian terrorism, the Second Intifada's 1,000-plus Israeli fatalities, and persistent rejectionist violence despite phased territorial handovers.[27][28] Instead, New Right doctrine prioritizes unilateral security actions—such as settlement consolidation and border fortification—over negotiations that yield land without verified behavioral changes, viewing the latter as incentivizing further aggression based on historical patterns of non-compliance.[29] On broader defense, the party underscores military superiority and IDF modernization to counter existential threats, particularly from Iran and Hezbollah, which it identifies as orchestrating up to 70% of Israel's regional security burdens through proxy arming and incursions.[30] Empirical backing includes operational successes in disrupting Iranian entrenchment in Syria and preempting Hezbollah buildups along the northern border, advocating sustained investment in intelligence, precision weaponry, and rapid-response capabilities to maintain qualitative edges amid escalating proxy conflicts.[31] This hawkish posture rejects de-escalatory gestures toward adversarial states, favoring proactive deterrence to prevent multi-front wars, as demonstrated by Hezbollah's 150,000-rocket arsenal amassed post-2006 Lebanon War despite ceasefires.[32]

Economic Liberalism and Reforms

The New Right advocates for economic liberalism through deregulation and tax reductions to foster innovation and growth, positioning itself as a proponent of Israel's high-tech sector expansion. Party co-founder Naftali Bennett has called for "a drastic tax cut and a drastic regulation cut" to stimulate entrepreneurship, drawing on Israel's empirical record as a "Startup Nation" where per capita venture capital investment reached $1,068 in 2022, among the world's highest, correlating with periods of right-leaning governance that prioritized market freedoms over state intervention.[33][34] This approach contrasts with statist models, emphasizing causal incentives for private investment that have driven Israel's GDP per capita to approximately $53,810 in purchasing power parity terms by 2023, with average annual growth exceeding 6% over the prior three years under policies reducing bureaucratic hurdles.[34] A core reform focus involves integrating the haredi (ultra-Orthodox) population into the workforce to mitigate demographic-economic strains, where haredi male employment hovered around 44.5% in early 2014, below national averages, exacerbating dependency on subsidies amid a community comprising over 12% of Israel's population by 2023. Bennett has championed affirmative action and incentives for haredi participation in public and private sectors, arguing that workforce entry via skill-training programs—rather than perpetual exemptions—addresses fiscal imbalances through productivity gains, as evidenced by gradual rises in haredi employment rates to over 50% for men by the late 2010s following targeted initiatives.[35][36] This policy underscores first-principles incentives over welfare perpetuation, aiming to bolster overall GDP contributions from a growing demographic projected to reach 16% of the population by 2030. The party opposes expansive welfare measures, contending that they foster dependency and stagnation, with historical data linking liberal reforms—such as privatization and fiscal restraint under right-wing administrations—to sustained GDP expansions averaging 3-4% annually in the 2010s, outpacing welfare-heavy alternatives in neighboring states.[37] Ayelet Shaked and Bennett's platform prioritizes self-reliance, critiquing subsidy-driven models for distorting labor markets, as seen in Israel's shift from socialist-era controls to market-oriented policies that correlated with inflation dropping below 2% and tech exports surpassing $50 billion yearly by 2022.[2][38] This stance reflects a causal realism wherein reduced state redistribution incentivizes human capital development, supporting Israel's ranking as the fourth most innovative economy globally per indices tracking R&D spending at 5.4% of GDP.[39]

Domestic Social and Institutional Policies

The New Right supports judicial reforms to limit the Supreme Court's activist tendencies, which the party argues have led to the invalidation of democratically enacted laws without sufficient constitutional basis, thereby undermining the separation of powers. As articulated in its platform, the party prioritizes "judicial restraint" to restore balance between elected branches and the judiciary, countering what it views as an overreach by unelected judges influenced by left-leaning elites.[2] During Ayelet Shaked's service as Justice Minister from 2015 to 2019, the party advanced measures to diversify judicial appointments, appointing judges from varied backgrounds and negotiating changes to the selection committee to incorporate more political input while operating within existing frameworks, resulting in a broader ideological representation on the bench.[40][41] On integration and national service, the New Right advocates for mandatory enlistment of ultra-Orthodox (haredi) men into the Israel Defense Forces or civilian alternatives, rejecting exemptions that perpetuate dependency on state welfare and weaken collective defense burdens. Party co-founder Naftali Bennett has publicly opposed legislative efforts to reduce haredi recruitment quotas, arguing such policies exacerbate inequalities in service obligations amid ongoing security threats, as evidenced by his June 2025 criticism of a proposed bill that would diminish enlistment incentives and workforce integration.[42] This stance aligns with the party's goal of fostering societal cohesion by incentivizing haredi participation in education reforms that mandate core secular curricula—such as mathematics, science, and English—in yeshiva schools to equip graduates for economic productivity.[43] In governance, the New Right promotes civil service efficiencies to combat bureaucratic inertia and corruption risks stemming from entrenched interests, drawing on leaders' high-tech backgrounds to advocate streamlined regulations and performance-based incentives. Bennett's policy visions emphasize doubling public service quality through market-oriented reforms, including reduced red tape in permitting and procurement to minimize opportunities for graft, as seen in his critiques of inefficient state apparatuses during coalition negotiations.[44] These measures aim to realign institutions toward pragmatic, results-driven administration rather than ideological entrenchment.[45]

Distinctions from Religious Right Parties

The New Right differentiates itself from religious right parties like Jewish Home and Religious Zionism by rejecting religious coercion and veto power over secular policy domains, prioritizing instead a unified Jewish-Israeli national identity that transcends strict halakhic observance. Formed in December 2018 by Naftali Bennett and Ayelet Shaked after splitting from the more religiously infused Jewish Home, the party explicitly opposes imposing religious mandates on non-observant citizens, as articulated by Bennett in January 2019 when he criticized such coercion in an apparent reference to his former party.[46][1] This stance contrasts with Religious Zionism's advocacy for advancing Torah study as a quasi-constitutional priority, as seen in its December 2022 agreement with Likud to promote a Basic Law elevating religious education.[47] By emphasizing pragmatic nationalism over theocratic elements, the New Right seeks to forge coalitions that include secular professionals and urban voters who support robust security and territorial policies but resist religious primacy in governance. Its platform promotes cooperation between religious and secular Jews without subordinating state decisions to rabbinic authority, enabling appeal to a broader right-wing electorate alienated by the insularity of parties focused on halakhic enforcement.[2] This secular pragmatism facilitates policy flexibility, as evidenced by the party's economic liberalism and judicial restraint positions, which avoid the religious vetoes that can stall reforms in alliances dominated by theocratic factions.[1] Causal factors underscore how religious parties' emphasis on doctrinal priorities limits their scope for addressing economic and security imperatives, with data showing persistent income and employment gaps in communities prioritizing religious study over workforce integration—Haredi men earn 49% of non-Haredi Jewish men's monthly income (NIS 9,929 versus NIS 20,464 as of 2024), reflecting lower participation that constrains broader growth.[48] In contrast, the New Right's rejection of such insularity aligns with higher-productivity regions, where non-religious mandates correlate with sustained economic expansion and wider voter coalitions unburdened by faith-based constraints.[49]

Leadership and Organizational Structure

Primary Leaders: Bennett and Shaked

Naftali Bennett, born on March 25, 1972, in Haifa, built a career as a high-tech entrepreneur before entering politics, co-founding the cybersecurity firm Cyota in 1999, which he sold for millions, establishing him as a self-made millionaire in Israel's innovation sector.[50][51][52] As co-founder of the New Right party in December 2018 alongside Ayelet Shaked, Bennett brought operational expertise from his IDF service in the elite Maglan unit and prior ministerial roles, including serving as prime minister from June 13, 2021, to June 30, 2022.[53] Post the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks, Bennett advocated for decisive security measures, criticizing Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's leadership for fostering complacency and blocking ultra-Orthodox military enlistment, which he argued hindered Israel's victory over Hamas.[54][55] Ayelet Shaked, born in 1976 in Tel Aviv, holds a degree in electrical engineering and computer science from Tel Aviv University and worked at Texas Instruments, embodying a pragmatic, tech-oriented approach to policy innovation.[56] As Justice Minister from 2015 to 2019, she advanced judicial reforms emphasizing sovereignty over settlements and opposition to a Palestinian state, later shaping the New Right's platform to prioritize annexing Area C of the West Bank while fostering secular-religious balance through mutual agreements rather than ideological rigidity.[1] Her legislative tenacity during this tenure positioned her as a key architect of the party's nationalist yet economically liberal stance upon its founding in December 2018.[53] The duo's leadership synergy combined Bennett's charismatic public appeal—rooted in his entrepreneurial success and security background—with Shaked's methodical legislative drive, appealing to secular right-wing voters disillusioned with religious Zionism's orthodoxy. Pre-founding polls in late December 2018 projected their New Right list at 6 to 14 Knesset seats, reflecting their individual popularity as outgoing Jewish Home ministers amid internal party fractures.[57] This partnership symbolized a modern, innovation-driven right, distinct from traditional religious frameworks, though internal dynamics later clarified Bennett as formal chairman.[58]

Key Supporting Figures and Internal Dynamics

Matan Kahana, a former IDF fighter pilot and Sayeret Matkal veteran who served alongside Naftali Bennett, emerged as a prominent supporting figure in the New Right, contributing expertise in military affairs and religious institutional reform during his tenure as Minister of Religious Affairs from June 2021 to June 2022.[59][60] Kahana's initiatives challenged the ultra-Orthodox monopoly on religious services, including digitizing marriage registrations and promoting competition in kosher certification, reflecting the party's push for pragmatic liberalization in domestic institutions without diluting nationalist priorities.[61] The party's limited cadre of secondary leaders, such as Kahana, underscored its boutique operational model, prioritizing a compact team of aligned professionals over broad recruitment, which constrained scalability but ensured ideological consistency in a fragmented right-wing landscape.[62] Internal dynamics within the New Right exhibited strong cohesion on core tenets like territorial security and economic deregulation, yet revealed strains over electoral tactics and alliance purity, particularly evident in the rift following Bennett's June 2021 decision to form a broad anti-Netanyahu coalition incorporating centrist and Arab parties.[63] Ayelet Shaked voiced reservations about diluting right-wing mandates through such partnerships, advocating stricter bloc discipline, which contrasted with Bennett's pragmatic maneuvering to assume premiership and highlighted tensions between short-term power grabs and long-term ideological fidelity.[64] These debates did not fracture the leadership core pre-2022, as evidenced by the party's merger into Yamina for the March 2021 elections, preserving operational unity amid external pressures.[58] The New Right's empirical record of low personnel turnover—minimal defections compared to rivals like the Jewish Home party, which splintered into multiple factions post-2019—bolstered its internal stability and enabled consistent messaging on security hawkishness and judicial restraint.[65] This resilience, despite the party's small scale (typically 4-7 Knesset seats in active periods), stemmed from selective candidate vetting and shared elite backgrounds, allowing focused policy advocacy without the dilution seen in larger, heterogeneous right-wing entities.[66] Such dynamics positioned the New Right as a disciplined outlier in Israel's volatile party system, where ideological unity facilitated targeted influence despite electoral volatility.[67]

Party Organization and Membership Base


The New Right (HaYamin HeHadash) employs a centralized leadership model co-headed by Naftali Bennett and Ayelet Shaked, who oversee candidate selection and strategic decisions without formal primaries, enabling responsive maneuvering amid Israel's volatile coalition politics. This top-down approach stems from the party's formation in December 2018 as a splinter from the Jewish Home party, prioritizing agility over decentralized input.[68][69] The party's membership and supporter base primarily consists of secular, middle-class urban dwellers, including professionals in high-tech and business sectors concentrated in areas like Tel Aviv suburbs, attracted to its fusion of nationalism and economic liberalism detached from religious Zionism. This demographic aligns with the party's explicit aim to bridge secular and religious right-wing elements while appealing to educated voters disillusioned with traditional party apparatuses.[70][71] In contrast to mass-mobilization parties like Likud, the New Right features limited formal grassroots infrastructure, emphasizing leader-centric campaigns and digital engagement to cultivate support among tech-savvy nationalists rather than expansive branch networks.[72]

Electoral Performance and Representation

Detailed Election Results 2019-2022

In the April 9, 2019, Knesset election, the New Right (HaYamin HeHadash) obtained 139,681 votes, equivalent to 3.20% of the valid votes cast from a total of approximately 4.36 million, falling 1,400 votes short of the 3.25% electoral threshold required for representation and thus winning 0 seats.[73][74] The party's performance contributed marginally to the broader right-wing bloc, which collectively secured around 65 seats in the 120-seat Knesset.[75] The September 17, 2019, election saw the New Right receive 130,628 votes, or 3.00% of the valid votes from over 4.38 million cast, again failing the 3.25% threshold and obtaining 0 seats.[76] This outcome mirrored the prior election's shortfall pattern, with the right-wing bloc maintaining a combined total of approximately 55 seats amid inconclusive government formation.[11]

Knesset Representation and Notable Members

The New Right secured indirect Knesset representation via the Yamina electoral alliance in the March 2021 election, with its leaders occupying key seats and ministerial positions in the ensuing coalition government. Naftali Bennett served as Prime Minister from June 13, 2021, to June 30, 2022, leading a diverse anti-Netanyahu bloc that enacted policies on economic recovery and security amid the COVID-19 aftermath. Ayelet Shaked held the Interior Ministry portfolio from October 2021, overseeing immigration and local governance, while Matan Kahana managed Religious Affairs, implementing reforms to religious services including kosher certification liberalization. Other notable figures included Ofir Sofer, who served as Minister for the Development of the Negev and Galilee, focusing on regional infrastructure. Despite comprising a modest faction within Yamina's seven seats, New Right members demonstrated targeted legislative productivity; Shaked, drawing from her prior Justice Ministry experience, supported transparency initiatives that built on the 2016 NGO Law requiring disclosure of foreign government funding for advocacy groups, enhancing public scrutiny of entities receiving over 50% of budgets from abroad. Kahana advanced empirical reforms reducing rabbinical bureaucracy, evidenced by streamlined conversion processes that increased approvals by streamlining administrative hurdles. This output contrasted with larger parties' diffusion, prioritizing efficiency in niche areas like judicial restraint and institutional accountability.[83] Following the government's collapse and the November 2022 election, the New Right dissolved its formal ties and failed to retain independent seats, leaving no dedicated faction in the 25th Knesset as of 2025. Party alumni have since transitioned to extraparliamentary influence, with Bennett engaging in security advisory consultations and international diplomacy on counterterrorism, while Shaked has critiqued coalition judicial policies from opposition platforms. No current MKs affiliate directly with the defunct party, though former members occasionally advise on defense strategies amid ongoing conflicts.[4]

Factors Influencing Electoral Outcomes

The 3.25% electoral threshold in Israel's proportional representation system has structurally disadvantaged smaller right-wing parties, including the New Right, by necessitating a minimum national vote share equivalent to roughly 3.91 seats out of 120 to gain representation. In the April 2019 Knesset election, the New Right secured 156,162 votes, or 3.22%, placing it 0.03% below the threshold and resulting in zero seats despite a voter base comparable to parties that passed, such as Kulanu with 4.43%.[84][76] This outcome exemplifies how threshold mechanics amplify the costs of fragmentation, as right-wing vote splintering—evident in the simultaneous failure of parties like Zehut (1.3%)—wasted an estimated 4.5-5% of conservative-leaning ballots that year.[85] Vote distribution analyses suggest that merging these splinter groups prior to the election could have yielded 4-5 additional seats for the broader right bloc, based on their combined share translating to approximately 5.5% of total votes under unified slating.[86] Opposition from mainstream media outlets and judicial institutions, frequently characterized by analysts as exhibiting left-leaning biases that disproportionately scrutinize right-wing initiatives, has further eroded support for independent secular-right lists by magnifying perceptions of electoral risk.[87] Pre-election polling data from 2019 onward indicates that secular right-wing voters, wary of threshold shortfalls, engaged in strategic voting toward Likud, with surveys showing up to 10-15% shifts from smaller parties to Netanyahu's list in the final weeks to consolidate the bloc and prevent wasted votes.[88] This tactical behavior, driven by causal incentives for maximizing representation amid polarized coverage, reduced the New Right's effective base, as polls captured voter preferences for "safe" larger parties over ideologically aligned but vulnerable ones.[89] The October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks intensified national security priorities, empirically boosting right-wing parties' aggregate poll standings by 5-10 points in subsequent surveys due to demands for robust defense policies.[90] However, prolonged war efforts and resultant fatigue—reflected in 2025 polls where 64% of Israelis favored ending the Gaza conflict—have causally favored electoral consolidation, as voters prioritize stable coalitions over fragmented lists prone to gridlock during crises.[91] This dynamic disadvantages niche parties like the New Right's model, with data showing increased bloc discipline on the right post-attack, where unified slates under Likud or Religious Zionism captured strategic majorities amid public exhaustion with instability.[92][93]

Reception, Impact, and Controversies

Achievements and Contributions to Policy

During Naftali Bennett's tenure as prime minister from June 2021 to June 2022, his government achieved economic recovery post-COVID-19, reducing unemployment and bringing the budget deficit to zero through fiscal measures and growth-oriented policies.[94] The administration also maintained relative calm along the Gaza border by deducting payments to Hamas equivalent to riot damages, contributing to fewer incendiary attacks compared to prior years.[31] Furthermore, Bennett's coalition sustained and advanced the Abraham Accords, fostering ministerial visits and economic ties with signatory states like the UAE, while integrating the accords' framework into broader regional security dialogues.[95][96] Ayelet Shaked, as justice minister from 2015 to 2019 and a New Right co-founder, spearheaded the 2016 NGO Transparency Law, which mandates disclosure for organizations receiving over 50% of funding from foreign governments, applying to fewer than two dozen groups and enhancing oversight of external influences on domestic advocacy.[83][97] This reform empirically increased reporting requirements without prohibiting operations, addressing asymmetries in funding transparency between left- and right-leaning entities.[98] Bennett's government accelerated COVID-19 booster campaigns in mid-2021, administering third doses to over 2 million eligible individuals by August with no reported severe adverse events in initial immunosuppressed cohorts, sustaining Israel's high vaccination coverage amid Delta variant pressures.[99][100] The New Right's emphasis on pragmatic governance also normalized secular-right advocacy for haredi workforce integration, influencing budgetary incentives that correlated with rising male haredi employment from 48% in 2014 to higher participation rates by 2022, bolstering overall GDP contributions from the sector.[101]

Criticisms from Left-Leaning and Centrist Perspectives

Left-leaning outlets such as Haaretz have accused Ayelet Shaked, co-founder of the New Right, of spearheading efforts to weaken Israel's judicial independence during her tenure as Justice Minister from 2015 to 2019, portraying her advocacy for reforms like the override clause as part of a broader "war on the High Court" that prioritizes political control over democratic checks.[102] Critics, including contributors to Haaretz, further contend that Shaked's 2014 Facebook post endorsing an essay calling for the elimination of Palestinian terrorist networks—including references to "unborn" supporters—exemplifies rhetoric that normalizes extremism and erodes liberal democratic norms.[103] These perspectives frame the party's push for Israeli sovereignty over West Bank settlements, without granting full citizenship to Palestinians, as inherently undemocratic, potentially entrenching military rule over millions and sidelining prospects for a two-state solution.[104] Centrist and left-leaning commentators have similarly criticized Naftali Bennett's leadership for embedding nationalist priorities that, in their view, subordinate democratic pluralism to Jewish primacy, as evidenced by the party's support for the 2018 Nation-State Law, which opponents argue marginalizes non-Jewish citizens.[105] A Haaretz opinion piece from March 2025 described Bennett as "no less dangerous" than Benjamin Netanyahu, attributing his political maneuvers—including the 2021-2022 anti-Netanyahu coalition—to a core motivation of perpetuating policies of Palestinian containment rather than advancing egalitarian reforms.[106] Such critiques often highlight the New Right's alliances with religious Zionist factions as amplifying extremism, with Shaked's post-2019 partnerships seen as compromising judicial integrity for ideological gains.[107] From an economic standpoint, left-leaning analyses have faulted the New Right's pro-market orientation—rooted in Bennett's tech-sector background and the party's emphasis on deregulation—for contributing to Israel's widening inequality, arguing that prioritizing growth in high-tech enclaves neglects peripheral communities and exacerbates social divides amid rising living costs.[108] Critics in outlets like Haaretz contend this approach aligns with broader right-wing policies that favor privatization over robust social safety nets, potentially deepening disparities in a nation where Gini coefficients have hovered above OECD averages since the mid-2010s.[109] These viewpoints, often voiced amid protests against judicial reforms, portray the party's platform as favoring elite interests over inclusive prosperity.[110]

Right-Wing Defenses and Empirical Counterarguments

Proponents of the New Right's security policies argue that empirical outcomes from prior territorial concessions demonstrate the risks of appeasement, vindicating a deterrence-through-strength approach. The 2005 Gaza disengagement, which involved evacuating 21 settlements and displacing over 8,000 settlers, correlated with a sharp escalation in rocket and mortar attacks; from 2005 to 2006 alone, incidents rose 42% to 1,777 launches, enabling Hamas to consolidate power and fire thousands of rockets into Israel thereafter.[111][112] Similarly, Israel's 2000 withdrawal from southern Lebanon allowed Hezbollah to expand its arsenal and operational capacity, leading to sustained cross-border attacks that culminated in the 2006 war, rather than fostering stability.[113][114] The October 7, 2023, Hamas assault, which killed over 1,200 Israelis and took hundreds hostage, exposed flaws in reliance on containment without decisive action, as analyses attribute it to eroded deterrence from policies perceived as restraint, empowering militant groups to plan large-scale incursions.[115][116] New Right leader Naftali Bennett has advocated an "iron fist" response to provocations, positioning such stances as causally linked to preventing escalation by maintaining credible threats of overwhelming retaliation.[117] Defenders of the New Right's support for judicial reforms contend that Israel's Supreme Court exhibits disproportionate influence relative to democratic mandates, unlike balanced systems elsewhere, justifying curbs on overreach to align judiciary with elected branches. The Court has invalidated Basic Laws—quasi-constitutional statutes—multiple times, a power absent in the U.S., where the Supreme Court cannot override amendments, or in most European constitutional courts, which defer more to parliamentary sovereignty amid codified constitutions.[118][119] Ayelet Shaked, as former Justice Minister, argued reforms like limiting the "reasonableness" doctrine would restore public trust by preventing unelected judges from overriding voter-backed policies, citing data on judicial vetoes of Knesset decisions as evidence of elitist insulation from accountability.[120][121] Comparative metrics show Israel's judiciary intervening in policy at rates exceeding OECD peers, where elected legislatures retain primacy, supporting claims that reforms promote equilibrium without undermining rule of law.[122] On economics, right-wing governance, including periods aligned with New Right priorities, has driven Israel's high-tech surge and sustained growth, contrasting with higher structural unemployment under prior left-led administrations. Under Benjamin Netanyahu's tenure from 2009 to 2021, which overlapped with New Right figures' influence, annual GDP growth averaged around 3.5-4%, fueled by tech exports comprising over 50% of goods, while unemployment fell from 7.6% in 2010 to historic lows near 3.4% by 2019.[123][124] Earlier Labor-dominated governments in the 1990s faced double-digit unemployment peaks amid intifada disruptions and inflation legacies exceeding 10%, whereas right-leaning policies emphasized deregulation and innovation hubs, yielding per capita GDP rises from $30,000 in 2009 to over $50,000 by 2022.[125] Bennett, with his tech CEO background, championed such pro-market reforms, arguing they empirically outperform concession-oriented alternatives by prioritizing self-reliance over state-heavy interventions.[126]

Broader Influence on Israeli Political Landscape

The New Right's emphasis on a secular-nationalist platform catalyzed efforts to consolidate non-religious voters within the broader right-wing camp, highlighting the electoral risks of fragmentation as seen in its own failure to cross the 3.25% threshold in the April 2019 election, where it garnered 3.22% of the vote. This outcome underscored the need for unified right-bloc strategies, indirectly contributing to voter discipline in the 2022 election, where the national camp secured 64 seats by avoiding similar splits and prioritizing anti-left coalitions over ideological purity. Analysts note that such experiences educated secular right-leaning voters on the perils of splinter parties, fostering a pragmatic consolidation that bolstered Benjamin Netanyahu's return to power despite ongoing corruption trials.[2][127] Elements of the New Right's policy agenda, including judicial reform to curb perceived activist overreach and integration of technology in security frameworks, have permeated platforms of larger parties like Likud and Religious Zionism. Ayelet Shaked's tenure as justice minister (2015–2019) advanced reforms later amplified by Netanyahu's 2023 push against judicial supremacy, reflecting a bleed of New Right ideas into mainstream right-wing governance. Similarly, Naftali Bennett's advocacy for innovation-driven defense—drawing from his high-tech background—aligned with Religious Zionism's adoption of hybrid tech-security measures in coalition agreements, enhancing the bloc's appeal to urban, secular professionals without diluting nationalist priorities.[128] In the 2025 post-war context, the New Right's legacy sustains a niche role in debates over governance and security, with polls indicating 10–15% enduring support among secular Israelis for nationalist parties emphasizing equal civic burdens like Haredi conscription and robust Gaza policies. Bennett's registration of a new party in April 2025 and ongoing public interventions on hostage deals and territorial control reflect this persistent secular-right constituency, which favors pragmatic sovereignty over religious exclusivity, even as Likud dominates broader right-wing polls. Surveys show secular respondents aligning with center-right views on military service and post-conflict administration, preventing a full religious monopoly on right discourse.[129][130][131]
