Howler Monkey

Howler monkeys are the most widespread primate genus in the Neotropics and are among the largest of the platyrrhines along with the muriquis (Brachyteles), the spider monkeys (Ateles) and woolly monkeys (Lagothrix). The monkeys are native to South and Central American forests. They are famous for their howls, which can be heard from a distance through dense rain forest.[1] Fifteen species are recognized. Previously classified in the family Cebidae, they are now placed in the family Atelidae. They are primarily folivores but also significant frugivores, acting as seed dispersal agents through their digestive system. Threats include human predation, habitat destruction, illegal wildlife trade, and capture for pets or zoo animals.

Anatomy and Physiology

Howler monkeys have short snouts and wide-set, round nostrils. Their noses are very keen, allowing them to locate food such as fruit and nuts. Their noses are usually roundish snout-type, and the nostrils have many sensory hairs growing from the interior. They range in size from 40 to 70 cm (16 to 28 in) in head-body length, excluding their tails, which are typically as long as or slightly longer than the body. Adults weigh 4-12 kg (8.8-26.5 lb), with males typically 1.5-2 times heavier than females. This is a prime characteristic. Like many New World monkeys, they have prehensile tails, which they use while picking fruit and nuts from trees. Unlike other New World monkeys, both male and female howler monkeys have trichromatic color vision.[5] This has evolved independently from other New World monkeys due to gene duplication.[6] In the wild, they have lifespans of 15 to 20 years, and up to 25 years in captivity. Howler species are dimorphic and can also be dichromatic (i.e. Alouatta caraya).

Skull

Males experience an evolutionary trade off between investments in precopulatory traits, larger hyoids but smaller testes, or post-copulatory traits, larger testes and smaller hyoids. The hyoid of Alouatta is pneumatized, one of the few cases of postcranial pneumaticity outside the Saurischia. The volume of the hyoid of male howler monkeys is negatively correlated with the dimensions of their testes, and with the number of males per group. Larger hyoids decrease space between formant, and offers impression of larger body size.[7] They have a flat cranial shape due to a folivorous diet and an advanced vocal system. Their brain growth is posterior rather than superior or inferior as in other platyrrhines.[8]

Locomotion

Howler monkeys generally move quadrupedally on the tops of branches, usually grasping a branch with at least two hands or one hand and the tail at all times. Their strong prehensile tails are able to support their entire body weight. Fully grown adult howler monkeys do not often rely on their tails for full-body support, but juveniles do so more frequently. In certain habitats, such as forest-savanna interfaces, a portion of their travel may occur on the ground, though they are primarily arboreal, with sitting and resting being their most frequent postures.[9]

Behaviour

Social Systems

Most howler species live in groups of six to 15 animals, with one to three adult males and multiple females, though this varies by species. Mantled howler monkeys are an exception, commonly living in groups of 15 to 20 individuals with more than three adult males. The number of males in a given group is inversely correlated with the size of their hyoids and is positively correlated with testes size. This results in two distinct groups, wherein one male with a larger hyoid and smaller testes copulates exclusively with a group of females, suggesting precopulatory vocal competition. The other group has more males, which have smaller hyoids, and larger testes. The larger the number of males, the smaller the hyoid, and the larger the testes.[7] Female howler monkeys breed with multiple males within their group, with males in neighboring groups, and with solitary males. In multi-male groups, dominant males often monopolize mating opportunities with estrous females.[10] Unlike most New World monkeys, in which one sex remains in natal groups, juveniles of both sexes emigrate from their natal groups,[11] such that howler monkeys could spend the majority of their adult lives in association with unrelated monkeys. Physical fighting among group members is infrequent and generally of short duration, but serious injuries can result. Both males and females rarely fight with each other, but physical aggression is even more rare between sexes.[11][12] Group size varies by species and by location, with an approximate ratio of one male to four females.[11]

Communication

As their name suggests, vocal communication forms an important part of their social behavior. They each have an enlarged basihyal or hyoid bone, which helps them make their vocalizations. Group males generally call at dawn, as well as interspersed times throughout the day. Their main vocals consist of deep, guttural growls or "howls". The function of howling is thought to relate to intergroup spacing and territory protection, as well as possibly to mate-guarding. Howlers call usually when they are in areas with major feeding sites, which in some way leads to advertising major feeding sites and their willingness to defend locally available fruit trees. Diet and Feeding

These large and slow-moving monkeys are among the most folivorous of the New World monkeys. Howlers eat mainly top canopy leaves, together with fruit, buds, flowers, and nuts. They need to be careful not to eat too many leaves of certain species in one sitting, as some contain toxins that can poison them.[14] Howler monkeys are also known to occasionally raid birds' nests, chicken coops, and consume the eggs.[15] When in smaller groups (up to twelve individuals) and low rainfall (up to 2,200 mm (87 in)), they are more frugivorous. In larger groups and increased rainfall, frugivory decreases as a result of competition and fast food depletion.[16] As they digest fruit, more than 90% of the fruits' seeds are excreted without damage, which results in seed dispersal and distribution in tropical forests.[17]

Sleeping

In species such as the black-and-gold howler (Alouatta caraya), howlers use the upper-middle part of their sleeping tree and large branches on 70% of nights, potentially allowing for grouped sleeping or resistance to weather conditions and risk of branch breaking. Their sleeping sites are usually close to morning feeding sites.[18]

Relationship with Humans

Howler monkeys are not usually aggressive toward humans but generally do not adapt well to captivity due to dietary needs, stress, and vocalization challenges. The black howler monkey (Alouatta caraya) is sometimes kept illegally as a pet in Argentina despite legal protections, owing to its relatively gentle nature compared to more aggressive species like capuchin monkeys, though their large size and loud vocalizations pose significant challenges. John Lloyd Stephens described the howler monkeys at the Maya ruins of CopÃ¡n as "grave and solemn, almost emotionally wounded, as if officiating as the guardians of consecrated ground". To the Mayas of the Classic period, they were the divine patrons of the artisans, especially scribes and sculptors. They were seen as gods in some tribes, and the long, sleek tail was worshipped for its beauty. CopÃ¡n, in particular, is famous for its representations of howler monkey gods. Two howler monkey brothers play a role in the myth of the Maya Hero Twins included in the Popol Vuh, a sacred K'iche' creation myth. Human activities pose significant threats to howler monkeys, including habitat destruction from deforestation, hunting for meat (known as "monkey hunting" in some regions), and the illegal wildlife trade for pets and zoos. Conservation efforts include rehabilitation centers in Argentina, such as those rescuing individuals from the pet trade, and reintroduction programs; for example, in 2025, multiple howler monkeys were successfully released back into the wild in Belize's protected areas. These initiatives aim to mitigate population declines and promote coexistence with human communities.
