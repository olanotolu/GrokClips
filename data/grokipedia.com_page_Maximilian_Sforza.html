Maximilian Sforza

![Portrait of Massimiliano Sforza by Giovanni Ambrogio de Predis]float-right Massimiliano Maria Sforza (25 January 1493 – 25 May 1530) was an Italian nobleman who served as Duke of Milan from 1512 to 1515 as a member of the Sforza dynasty.[1] The son of Ludovico Sforza, known as "il Moro," and Beatrice d'Este, he was installed as duke by the Holy League after Swiss forces expelled the French occupiers led by Louis XII from the duchy in 1512. His reign depended heavily on Swiss mercenaries, whom he funded through burdensome taxes on the Milanese populace, while maintaining an extravagant court that alienated local support. In 1513, Sforza participated in the Battle of Novara, repelling a French counteroffensive, but his fortunes reversed decisively at the Battle of Marignano in 1515, where French forces under Francis I overwhelmed the Swiss defenders, leading to his capture. Imprisoned initially at the Château de Blois, he later renounced his claims to Milan via the Treaty of Noyon in 1516 for an annual pension of 30,000 ducats, residing in relative luxury in France until his death.[2] Lacking significant independent achievements, Sforza's tenure exemplified the fragility of mercenary-dependent rule amid the Italian Wars' shifting alliances and invasions.

Early Life

Birth and Parentage

Maximilian Maria Sforza was born on 25 January 1493 in Milan to Ludovico Sforza and Beatrice d'Este.[1][3] As the first surviving son of the union, he was positioned as heir to the Sforza dynasty's claims in Milan.[4] His father, Ludovico Sforza (1452–1508), known as il Moro, effectively controlled the Duchy of Milan as regent for his nephew Gian Galeazzo Sforza from 1480 and later as duke from 1494 until his overthrow in 1499.[1] Ludovico's marriage to Beatrice in 1491 strengthened alliances with the Este family of Ferrara.[4] His mother, Beatrice d'Este (1475–1497), was the daughter of Ercole I d'Este, Duke of Ferrara, and Eleonora of Aragon; she brought cultural and political prestige to the Sforza court through her patronage of arts and her Ferrara connections.[3] Beatrice died at age 21 from complications of childbirth with her third child, leaving Maximilian and his brother Francesco under their father's influence amid rising political instability.[1]

Childhood in Exile

Massimiliano Maria Sforza, born on 25 January 1493 in Milan as the eldest legitimate son of Duke Ludovico Sforza and Beatrice d'Este, faced upheaval early in life due to the French invasion during the Italian Wars. His mother died on 2 January 1497, leaving him under his father's guardianship amid political instability. Ludovico's rule ended with the French conquest of Milan on 2 September 1499, prompting the duke's flight northward; although Ludovico briefly reentered the duchy in early 1500 with Imperial and Swiss aid, he was decisively defeated and surrendered at the Battle of Novara on 10 April 1500, after which he was imprisoned in France until his death on 27 May 1508.[1] At age seven, Massimiliano and his younger brother Francesco entered exile, finding refuge under the protection of Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I, Ludovico's sometime ally and the child's namesake. The brothers spent their youth at the Imperial court in Innsbruck, Tyrol, away from French-controlled Italy, where they were raised amid the Habsburg entourage. This period of displacement, lasting until Massimiliano's restoration in 1512, severed direct ties to Milanese governance and exposed the boys to German-speaking Imperial politics and culture, shaping their later alliances with Swiss and Imperial forces against France.[5] During exile, Massimiliano received an education befitting his station, though details remain sparse; he was later described as indolent and unprepared for rule, attributes possibly traceable to the insulated courtly environment rather than rigorous ducal training. The Sforza lineage's legitimacy persisted through such patronage, as Emperor Maximilian I leveraged the young heir's claim to counter French dominance in Lombardy, foreshadowing the 1512 Swiss intervention that briefly returned Massimiliano to power.[5]

Rise to the Dukedom

Context of the Italian Wars

The Italian Wars (1494–1559) represented a protracted struggle among European powers for dominance in the fragmented Italian peninsula, primarily pitting France against coalitions involving Spain, the Holy Roman Empire, Swiss cantons, and Italian states, with Milan serving as a key contested territory due to its strategic wealth and position. Triggered by Charles VIII's invasion in 1494 to assert Angevin claims on Naples, the conflicts escalated under his successor Louis XII, who in 1499 leveraged a Visconti inheritance claim to occupy Milan. French forces under condottiero Gian Giacomo Trivulzio entered Lombardy in July 1499, capturing the city on September 2 amid minimal resistance, as Duke Ludovico Sforza fled to imperial territories. Ludovico's subsequent recruitment of 10,000 Swiss mercenaries enabled a brief recapture of Milan in February 1500, but betrayal by the Swiss—induced by French bribes—at the Battle of Novara on April 11, 1500, led to his surrender and imprisonment in France, where he died in 1508.[6] French dominion over Milan endured for over a decade, marked by administrative exploitation and military garrisons that strained local resources, while broader war dynamics shifted through opportunistic alliances. Louis XII's expansions into Genoa and Naples initially succeeded but faltered against Spanish counteroffensives by 1504, confining French holdings to Lombardy. The 1508 League of Cambrai, uniting Pope Julius II, Louis XII, Emperor Maximilian I, and Ferdinand II of Aragon against Venetian expansion, temporarily stabilized French positions but dissolved as Julius II prioritized expelling Gallic influence from the Papal States. On October 4, 1511, Julius II formalized the Holy League, enlisting Venice, Spain, England, and the Swiss Confederation to counter French aggression, framing the alliance as a crusade to liberate Italy.[7] The League's momentum peaked in 1512 following the Battle of Ravenna on April 11, where French troops under Gaston de Foix routed a Spanish-Papal force, inflicting 20,000 casualties but losing their commander and cohesion. Swiss intervention, motivated by Milanese arrears in pension payments and opportunistic expansionism, proved decisive: 20,000 pikemen advanced from the Alps, defeating French remnants and occupying Milan by late December after minimal opposition from evacuating garrisons under Jacques de La Palice. On December 29, 1512, the Swiss Diet proclaimed Maximilian Sforza, Ludovico's exiled son, as duke, restoring nominal Sforza rule as a protectorate to legitimize their control over Lombard revenues and passes. This installation reflected the wars' pattern of proxy rulers amid mercenary-driven power vacuums, though Swiss overlordship extracted heavy tribute, foreshadowing Milan’s volatility.[8][7]

Swiss Intervention and Installation

In the wake of the Battle of Ravenna on April 11, 1512, where French forces under Gaston de Foix achieved a tactical victory but suffered heavy losses including the death of their commander, the Holy League—comprising Pope Julius II, the Republic of Venice, and other allies—sought to exploit French vulnerabilities in Lombardy.[9] Pope Julius II, aiming to expel French influence from Milan, recruited Swiss mercenaries through Cardinal Matthäus Schiner, Bishop of Sion, who negotiated a Papal-Swiss alliance to mobilize troops against the French occupation.[10] Approximately 24,000 Swiss soldiers assembled near Chur in May 1512 under commanders including Ulrich von Hohensax, though initial operations focused on securing eastern approaches before a concentrated push into the Duchy of Milan later that year.[10] The decisive Swiss intervention commenced in late autumn, as French garrisons in Milan weakened amid logistical strains and desertions. On December 1, 1512, a Swiss army of about 16,000 men invaded the duchy from the north, advancing swiftly through Alpine passes and encamping within striking distance of Milan by mid-December.[9] Overwhelmed and demoralized, the remaining French forces under commanders like Jean de La Trémoille abandoned the city without a prolonged siege, allowing Swiss troops to occupy Milan unopposed by the end of the month.[11] This rapid expulsion marked a high point of Swiss military influence in Italy, driven not solely by league objectives but by the confederation's desire for territorial gains and mercenary payments.[10] On December 29, 1512, the Swiss formally installed Maximilian Sforza, the 21-year-old son of the deposed Ludovico Sforza who had been raised in exile in the Holy Roman Empire, as Duke of Milan.[11] [12] Accompanied by Swiss escorts during the campaign, Maximilian's enthronement served as a nominal restoration of Sforza rule to legitimize league control, but he functioned as a puppet sovereign under Swiss oversight.[10] In exchange for their protection, the Swiss Confederation extracted an annual tribute of 40,000 ducats from the duchy, along with permanent control over strategic enclaves including Lugano, Locarno, Mendrisio, Chiavenna, Bormio, and Domodossola, as well as toll exemptions on trade routes—arrangements that prioritized Swiss economic and political dominance over Milanese autonomy.[10] This installation temporarily stabilized anti-French alliances in northern Italy but sowed seeds of dependency, as Swiss garrisons remained entrenched to enforce compliance.

Reign as Duke of Milan

Administration and Policies

Massimiliano Sforza assumed the ducal throne of Milan on December 29, 1512, at the age of 19, following the Swiss-led expulsion of French forces, but his administration operated under de facto Swiss domination, rendering him a nominal sovereign with limited autonomous authority. The Swiss Confederation, having installed him with approximately 10,000 troops, extracted territorial concessions—including Domodossola, Lugano, and Locarno—along with toll exemptions, in return for providing military defense against French resurgence. This arrangement subordinated Milanese governance to Swiss strategic interests, particularly control over Alpine passes, while perpetuating the presence of Swiss garrisons that enforced order but fueled local resentment due to their unpopularity and exactions.[13] Fiscal policies centered on sustaining this Swiss protectorate through burdensome taxation, including a salt tax and compulsory loans, which strained the duchy’s economy and incited tax revolts amid ongoing war costs. These measures prioritized mercenary payments over domestic investment, reflecting the administration's defensive orientation rather than expansion or reform. Sforza's personal extravagance, exemplified by expenditures of 30,000 ducats on wardrobe and festivities, further eroded fiscal prudence and public support, underscoring the weaknesses in executive leadership despite his reported serious demeanor and intent to govern effectively.[13] In a conciliatory gesture amid rising discontent, Sforza conceded municipal privileges to Milanese citizens in July 1515, permitting the election of local officials and oversight of canal management to mitigate revolts and bolster loyalty. However, these limited concessions failed to offset the overarching Swiss veto on policy, as evidenced by the Confederation's role in key military actions, such as the victory at Ariotta on June 6, 1513, which temporarily secured the duchy. Overall, the administration preserved vestiges of Sforza institutional continuity—such as reliance on existing chancery and fiscal apparatuses—but innovated little, prioritizing survival in the volatile context of the Italian Wars over structural overhaul.[13]

Military and Diplomatic Efforts

During his brief reign as Duke of Milan from December 1512 to September 1515, Massimiliano Sforza's military strategy centered on reliance upon Swiss mercenaries for defense against French incursions, as his own forces were insufficient without external support. The Swiss Confederacy, having expelled the French from Lombardy earlier in 1512, provided the core of his army through a pre-existing treaty that obligated Milan to furnish troops, payments, and territorial concessions such as Domodossola, Lugano, and Locarno in exchange for protection. This arrangement proved effective initially at the Battle of Ariotta on June 6, 1513, where approximately 10,000 Swiss troops under Sforza's nominal command routed a French force, inflicting nearly 12,000 casualties and securing Lombardy's eastern frontiers temporarily. However, Sforza exercised little direct command, functioning more as a figurehead while Swiss leaders dictated operations, a dynamic that strained Milan's finances through heavy taxation and forced loans to sustain the mercenaries. The pivotal military reversal occurred at the Battle of Marignano on September 13–14, 1515, where Sforza's Swiss allies, numbering around 20,000, clashed with King Francis I's invading French army of comparable size supported by Venetian contingents; the Swiss suffered approximately 10,000 casualties in a two-day engagement marked by fierce pike combat and French artillery superiority, leading to their retreat and the collapse of Sforza's defenses. Following the defeat, Sforza ordered the surrender of Milan's Castello Sforzesco on October 6, 1515, after a brief siege, effectively ending his rule without further resistance. These events underscored the limitations of Sforza's military posture, which prioritized Swiss dependency over independent mobilization, contributing to his rapid downfall amid widespread unpopularity from the economic burdens imposed to fund the alliance. Diplomatically, Sforza maneuvered within the framework of the Holy League, formed in October 1511 by Pope Julius II against France, aligning Milan with papal, Spanish, and imperial interests to legitimize his restoration and counter French claims. He received nominal support from Pope Leo X after Julius II's death in 1513, though actual aid was sparse, and maintained ties with the Swiss as the linchpin of his regime, granting them administrative influence through ambassadors who effectively controlled policy. Efforts to broaden alliances faltered; Venice shifted toward France in 1513, isolating Milan further, while Sforza's overtures to Emperor Maximilian I yielded little beyond rhetorical endorsement. Post-Marignano, his diplomatic capitulation culminated in the Treaty of Noyon negotiations, formalized by October 6, 1515, wherein he renounced the Milanese ducal claim in exchange for a French pension of 36,000 crowns annually, a concession reflecting the exhaustion of his league-based strategy. These maneuvers, constrained by puppet-like subordination to Swiss overlords, failed to forge a stable coalition, highlighting Sforza's dependence on transient mercenary pacts rather than enduring diplomatic architecture.

Downfall and Captivity

Battle of Marignano

The French invasion of Lombardy in 1515, led by King Francis I, posed an existential threat to Maximilian Sforza's dukedom, prompting him to mobilize Swiss mercenaries as the primary defensive force while he remained in Milan.[14] Allied with Pope Leo X and Emperor Maximilian I, Sforza's strategy hinged on the Swiss pikemen's reputation for invincibility, with commanders like Cardinal Matthäus Schiner directing operations independently of the duke's direct input.[15] The Swiss army, retreating from earlier positions, confronted the advancing French near Marignano (modern Melegnano) on September 13, establishing defensive lines in marshy terrain to counter the enemy's numerical superiority and artillery.[16] The engagement began on September 13 with Swiss advances repelling initial French probes, inflicting significant casualties through disciplined pike formations, but nightfall halted major gains.[16] Renewed fighting on September 14 saw French heavy cavalry and cannon fire erode Swiss cohesion, exacerbated by the late arrival of Venetian allies under Bartolomeo d'Alviano, which flanked the defenders and precipitated a disorderly retreat; Swiss losses exceeded 10,000, shattering their aura of dominance in Italian warfare.[16] Sforza, lacking a personal field command role and facing the collapse of his mercenary backbone, received reports of the rout from fleeing troops, compelling him to prioritize the defense of Milan over further resistance.[17] In the battle's immediate aftermath, the demoralized Swiss evacuated Milan on September 15, abandoning Sforza to negotiate surrender terms with Francis I's envoys to avert siege and bombardment.[18] On September 16, Sforza capitulated the city and its key fortresses, including those of Milan and Cremona, in exchange for personal safety and a pension, marking his effective deposition as duke.[16] Captured without resistance, he was conveyed to France under honorable confinement rather than harsh imprisonment, reflecting Francis I's strategic leniency toward puppet rulers to legitimize territorial gains.[17] This outcome underscored Sforza's dependency on external allies, as the Swiss defeat not only ended his reign but also prompted their permanent withdrawal from Milanese affairs.[14]

Imprisonment and Treaty Negotiations

Following the decisive French victory at the Battle of Marignano on September 13–14, 1515, Sforza's Swiss allies evacuated Milan, allowing French forces under King Francis I to enter the city on September 17. Sforza, unable to mount effective resistance, initiated surrender negotiations and formally capitulated on October 4, 1515. He was immediately taken prisoner by the French and transported to France, where he received an initial compensation of 100,000 écus in exchange for yielding control of the duchy.[19] The terms of Sforza's captivity were formalized in the Treaty of Noyon, signed on August 13, 1516, between France and Spain. Under this agreement, Sforza renounced all claims to the Duchy of Milan in perpetuity, receiving in return an annual pension of 30,000 ducats from Francis I to support his maintenance in France.[19] The treaty reflected France's strategic aim to secure lasting control over Lombardy without ongoing Sforza opposition, while providing Sforza with financial security amid his loss of sovereignty. Sforza's imprisonment, though not in harsh conditions, persisted without release; he was confined at royal châteaus including Loches, subject to French oversight to prevent any resurgence of Sforza influence in Italy. During this period, he maintained nominal ties to anti-French diplomacy, including a 1521 agreement with Holy Roman Emperor Charles V and Pope Leo X aimed at countering French dominance, though it yielded no restoration for him personally. He died in captivity in Paris on June 4, 1530.[19]

Later Life and Death

Release and Return

Following the French victory at the Battle of Marignano on 13–14 September 1515, Maximilian Sforza surrendered control of Milan on 4 October 1515, yielding the city's castles and allowing French troops to occupy the duchy.[16] Initially taken into custody, he negotiated terms that permitted his release from strict imprisonment shortly thereafter, in exchange for renouncing his ducal rights and accepting a compensation payment reported as 100,000 écus.[16] This arrangement facilitated his relocation to France, where he entered a form of exile rather than returning to Italy or attempting to reclaim power, as Milan remained under French administration.[1] The Treaty of Noyon, concluded on 13 August 1516 between Francis I of France and Charles I of Spain, formalized Sforza's abdication of Milan, with France retaining possession of the duchy in recognition of Spanish claims elsewhere in Italy.[16] Under these terms, Sforza received an annual pension equivalent to 30,000 ducats, enabling a comfortable existence in France without further political involvement. He nominally adopted a monastic lifestyle, residing in a French abbey, though accounts describe it as lax, with continued indulgence in luxuries unburdened by religious discipline.[1] Sforza's release marked the effective end of his ducal ambitions, as subsequent conflicts in the Italian Wars saw his half-brother Francesco II Sforza briefly restore Sforza rule in Milan from 1521 onward, bypassing Maximilian entirely.[19] No efforts were made to reinstate him, reflecting the diminished legitimacy and support his brief reign had garnered amid Swiss mercenary dependencies and French imperial rivalries.[16]

Final Years

Following the Treaty of Noyon in 1516, Massimiliano Sforza formally renounced his claims to the Duchy of Milan in exchange for a French pension of 30,000 ducats annually, allowing him to live in comfortable exile rather than strict captivity.[20] He resided under French supervision, primarily in royal domains, maintaining a lifestyle of relative luxury that included artistic patronage and courtly indulgences, though without political authority or attempts to reclaim his former title.[1] Throughout the 1520s, amid the Italian War of 1521–1526, Sforza remained sidelined in France as Habsburg and papal forces briefly ousted the French from Lombardy; his half-brother Francesco II Sforza was instead recognized as duke by Emperor Charles V, underscoring Massimiliano's diminished legitimacy and lack of alliances.[21] Some accounts describe him adopting a nominal monastic habit, possibly at a French abbey, yet exempt from religious vows, enabling continued secular pursuits.[22] Massimiliano Sforza died on 4 June 1530 at the Château de Fontainebleau, aged 37, from unspecified causes, marking the effective end of his direct line without issue or notable endeavors in his later years. His obscurity in exile reflected the Sforza dynasty's broader fragmentation amid shifting Franco-Imperial rivalries.[23]

Legacy

Historical Assessment

Maximilian Sforza's brief tenure as Duke of Milan from December 1512 to September 1515 is typically evaluated by historians as a period of nominal Sforza restoration overshadowed by foreign dependency and internal weakness, marking the effective decline of the dynasty's autonomous rule. Installed at age 19 following the expulsion of French forces by the Holy League and Swiss mercenaries, his authority was subordinate to Swiss influence, rendering him a figurehead or "mere dummy" in the eyes of contemporaries and later analysts; this arrangement stemmed from the Battle of Novara on June 6, 1513, where Swiss troops secured his position but extracted heavy financial concessions, including control over Milan's revenues.[13][10] His personal limitations—described as intellectually underdeveloped, barely literate, and prone to extravagance, such as expending 30,000 ducats on wardrobe—further eroded his legitimacy, fostering unpopularity among Milanese subjects who viewed him as an ineffective puppet unable to assert control amid fiscal strain and factional unrest.[13] Efforts to consolidate power, such as granting municipal privileges in 1514 allowing Milanese election of officials and canal management to quell discontent, yielded limited results and were undermined by ongoing Swiss dominance and external threats. Military reliance on mercenaries proved double-edged: while they enabled defense of the Castello Sforzesco against French incursions in 1513, the decisive defeat at the Battle of Marignano on September 13–14, 1515, exposed the fragility of this system, leading to his surrender on October 4 and the Treaty's renunciation of ducal claims for a 36,000-crown pension. Historians note this outcome not only terminated Sforza governance in Milan—shifting control to France and later Habsburgs—but also curtailed Swiss ambitions in Italy, with Maximilian's exile to France underscoring his incapacity for independent diplomacy or sustained rule.[13][24] In broader assessments, Maximilian embodies the terminal phase of Renaissance princely rule in Lombardy, where dynastic legitimacy clashed with mercenary politics and great-power rivalries, yielding no enduring administrative or cultural legacies beyond transitional privileges and artistic portrayals. Post-exile life in Paris until his death on June 4, 1530, remained obscure, with unfulfilled ecclesiastical ambitions highlighting personal marginalization; evaluations emphasize causal factors like inherited instability from his father Ludovico's fall and the era's balance-of-power dynamics over any innate leadership deficits, though his reign's brevity precludes substantive policy impacts.[13][25]

Role in Sforza Dynasty

Maximilian Sforza, born Ercole Massimiliano on January 25, 1493, to Ludovico Sforza (il Moro) and Beatrice d'Este, emerged as the designated legitimate heir to the Sforza ducal claims in Milan after his father's capture by French forces in April 1500 and subsequent imprisonment until his death in 1508.[13] As the eldest son from Ludovico's marriage, he symbolized continuity for the dynasty founded by Francesco I Sforza in 1450, which had elevated Milan to a major Renaissance power through condottiero origins and strategic alliances, though by Maximilian's time, repeated foreign invasions had eroded its independence.[13] His installation as Duke on December 29, 1512, following Swiss mercenaries' expulsion of French occupiers after the Battle of Novara in April 1512, marked a fragile restoration of Sforza rule after twelve years of foreign domination.[13] Supported by the Holy League and Pope Julius II, Maximilian's entry into Milan relied on a 1512 treaty conceding Swiss demands for pensions, trade privileges, and military exemptions, underscoring the dynasty's shift from autonomous power to dependency on confederate mercenaries who viewed him as a puppet sovereign.[13] This phase included a tactical victory at the Battle of Ariotta on June 6, 1513, against French forces, temporarily affirming his position, yet heavy taxation to fund Swiss allies fueled domestic unrest and highlighted his administrative weaknesses at age 19.[13] The dynasty's precarious state under Maximilian culminated in defeat at the Battle of Marignano on September 13–14, 1515, where French King Francis I's artillery and infantry overwhelmed Swiss pikemen, leading to Milan's surrender on October 6, 1515, and Maximilian's abdication via the Treaty of Noyon.[13] Exiled to France with a pension of 30,000 ducats annually, he renounced claims but lived until May 4, 1530 (or June 4 per some accounts), childless, shifting dynastic hopes to his half-brother Francesco II Sforza, who briefly reclaimed Milan from 1521 to 1535 before Spanish Habsburg dominance ended Sforza rule in 1535.[13] [26] Within the Sforza lineage, Maximilian's tenure exemplified the dynasty's terminal vulnerabilities: overreliance on transient mercenary alliances eroded sovereignty, while his reputed extravagance and political inexperience—advised remotely by relatives like aunt Isabella d'Este—failed to rebuild institutional strength or secure heirs, accelerating the transition from Italian princely autonomy to Habsburg imperial control.[13] [26] Contemporary and later evaluations, such as in Cecilia Ady's historical analysis, depict him as mercurial and ineffective, a "mere dummy" whose lapse precluded lasting revival, contrasting the earlier Sforza achievements in patronage and statecraft under Ludovico.[13] This role thus encapsulated the causal interplay of internal frailty and external predation that dissolved the dynasty's century-long grip on Milan.[13]

Family

Ancestors

Maximilian Sforza's paternal lineage traces to the Sforza family, originally of peasant origins in Lombardy, who rose to prominence through condottieri service. His great-grandfather, Muzio Attendolo Sforza (1369–1424), adopted the surname meaning "force" and amassed wealth as a mercenary captain, founding the dynasty's military tradition.[27] [28] His grandfather, Francesco Sforza (1401–1466), illegitimate son of Muzio, continued as a renowned condottiere, marrying Bianca Maria Visconti (1425–1468), sole heir of the Visconti dukes of Milan, which legitimized his claim; he entered Milan in 1450 as duke after the Visconti line's extinction, transforming the family's mercenary status into sovereign rule.[27] [28] Maximilian's father, Ludovico Sforza (1452–1508), fourth son of Francesco and Bianca Maria, effectively governed Milan from 1480 and formally duked from 1494, known as "Il Moro" for his dark complexion and political acumen.[29] [30] On his maternal side, Maximilian's mother, Beatrice d'Este (1475–1497), connected him to the ancient Este dynasty, rulers of Ferrara since the 13th century with roots in the 10th-century margraviate of Este. Her father, Ercole I d'Este (1431–1505), succeeded as duke of Ferrara and Modena in 1471, expanding influence through alliances. Immediate Relatives

Massimiliano Sforza was the eldest son of Ludovico Sforza (1451–1508), known as "il Moro," who served as effective ruler of Milan from 1480 and as Duke from 1494 until French invasion forces deposed him in 1499, and Beatrice d'Este (1475–1497), daughter of Ercole I d'Este, Duke of Ferrara, whose marriage to Ludovico in 1491 allied the Sforza with the Este dynasty.[4][3] Beatrice died in 1497 shortly after giving birth to Massimiliano's only full sibling, Francesco Maria Sforza (1495–1535), who briefly succeeded Massimiliano as Duke of Milan in 1522 before the duchy fell permanently to Habsburg-Spanish control.[35][28] Ludovico Sforza fathered several illegitimate children prior to and during his marriage, resulting in Massimiliano having multiple half-siblings, including half-brother Giovanni Paolo I Sforza (1497–1535), Lord of Milan in 1535 after Francesco's death without issue, and half-sister Bianca Maria Sforza (1472–1510), who became Holy Roman Empress through marriage to Maximilian I of Habsburg in 1494.[4][35] Other documented half-siblings included Alessandro Sforza and Orsino Sforza from Ludovico's relationships with concubines such as Cecilia Gallerani and Lucrezia Landriani.[4] Massimiliano Sforza never married and produced no legitimate or illegitimate offspring, as evidenced by contemporary genealogical records and the succession passing to his brother Francesco upon his death in 1530.[3][4]
