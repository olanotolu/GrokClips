M-15 motorway (Pakistan)

The M-15 motorway, also known as the Hazara Motorway, is a 180-kilometre controlled-access highway in Pakistan linking the Burhan Interchange near Hasan Abdal in Punjab province to Thakot in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province.[1] Constructed in phases starting from 2017, it features six lanes from Burhan to Haripur, four lanes to Mansehra, and two lanes to Thakot, designed to improve safety and efficiency over the previous narrow Karakoram Highway route (N-35).[2] The motorway significantly shortens travel times to northern regions such as Abbottabad, Mansehra, and eventually Gilgit-Baltistan, boosting tourism, trade, and regional connectivity as part of Pakistan's broader infrastructure network under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor.[3] By 2020, key sections were operational, with full completion targeted to enhance economic integration and reduce reliance on congested alternative paths.[4] Maintained by the National Highway Authority, it exemplifies modern engineering with bridges, tunnels, and scenic alignments through hilly terrain, though delays in certain extensions have been noted due to funding and logistical challenges.[5]

Background and Planning

Origins and Strategic Objectives

The Hazara Motorway, designated M-15, originated as a priority infrastructure project under Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif's administration, with groundbreaking ceremonies held on November 29, 2014, at a projected cost of 31 billion Pakistani rupees for initial phases.[6][7] Planning emphasized replacing the hazardous and congested National Highway N-35 (Karakoram Highway) through the Hazara Division of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, incorporating feasibility assessments for terrain challenges like mountainous passes and seismic zones.[8] The initiative built on Pakistan's broader motorway expansion since the 1990s but accelerated post-2013 with the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) framework, designating the Havelian-Thakot segment for foreign funding and engineering input to extend connectivity northward.[9] Strategic objectives centered on enhancing national transport efficiency by shortening the Islamabad-Mansehra route from approximately six hours to two hours, thereby reducing accident risks on the narrower N-35 prone to landslides and overloading.[8] Economically, it sought to integrate the underdeveloped Hazara region into trade networks, fostering local industries, agriculture exports, and job creation through improved access to Punjab's markets and ports.[7] As a CPEC artery, the motorway facilitates overland cargo movement to China's Xinjiang via the Karakoram Highway, supporting bilateral trade volumes exceeding $20 billion annually by 2020 and countering logistical bottlenecks in Pakistan's north-south corridor.[9] Further aims included tourism promotion by easing access to northern attractions like the Swat Valley and Gilgit-Baltistan, with projections of increased visitor inflows boosting ancillary sectors such as hospitality and transport.[10] Geostrategically, it strengthens internal security and economic resilience in a volatile border-adjacent area by enabling faster military logistics and civilian mobility, while diversifying routes away from single-point vulnerabilities on existing highways.[11] These goals aligned with Pakistan's National Trade Corridor vision, prioritizing high-capacity infrastructure to handle growing freight demands without relying on unsubstantiated regional equity narratives.

Integration with CPEC and National Infrastructure Goals

The M-15 motorway, known as the Hazara Motorway, forms a critical segment of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) by providing an alternative alignment to the Karakoram Highway (N-35), facilitating faster and safer transit for goods and passengers from China's Xinjiang region to Pakistan's central areas and onward to Gwadar Port. This 180-kilometer route connects Burhan Interchange on the M-1 motorway to Thakot, bypassing hazardous sections of N-35 through the Hazara region in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, thereby reducing travel time from Islamabad to Gilgit by approximately four hours and enhancing logistical efficiency for CPEC's overland trade volume, which exceeded 100 million tons cumulatively by 2023.[12][13] While the initial Havelian-Thakot sections received partial funding under CPEC frameworks, with the Mansehra-Thakot portion alone costing 133 billion Pakistani rupees (about $480 million USD at 2018 rates), the project aligns with CPEC's emphasis on infrastructure-led regional connectivity rather than direct Chinese financing for the entire length. This integration supports CPEC's western route objectives by linking to the Raikot-Thakot section of N-35, enabling diversified freight paths that mitigate risks from terrain vulnerabilities on the primary Karakoram alignment, as analyzed in freight capacity studies projecting up to 20% efficiency gains in trade flows.[14][15] In broader national infrastructure goals, the M-15 advances Pakistan's National Transport Policy by strengthening north-south linkages, promoting economic decentralization in underdeveloped Khyber Pakhtunkhwa districts, and fostering tourism through improved access to sites like Naran and Babusar Pass, with interchanges at key points such as Shinkiari and Chattar Plain designed to stimulate local commerce. The motorway's development, initiated in phases from 2013, reflects priorities for resilient transport networks amid Pakistan's geographic challenges, including seismic zones and mountainous terrain, contributing to a national motorway expansion targeting 2,500 kilometers by 2030 to support GDP growth via enhanced freight mobility and reduced accident rates compared to two-lane highways.[10][16]

Route Description

Overall Path and Length

The M-15 motorway, also known as the Hazara Motorway, extends 180 kilometers from the Burhan Interchange on the M-1 motorway near Hasan Abdal in Punjab province to Thakot in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province.[1][3] This controlled-access highway serves as a key link in Pakistan's northern road network, bypassing congested sections of the N-35 Karakoram Highway.[2] The route traverses the Hazara region, passing through districts such as Haripur, Abbottabad, and Mansehra, while contending with rugged mountainous terrain that includes elevations up to approximately 1,000 meters.[1][2] It features a variable cross-section: six lanes from Burhan to Haripur, four lanes from Haripur to Mansehra, and two lanes from Mansehra to Thakot, designed to balance capacity with the challenging topography.[2]

Major Interchanges and Terrain Challenges

The M-15 motorway features key interchanges designed to integrate with national highways and local roads, enhancing accessibility across Punjab and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa provinces. The southern terminus is the Burhan Interchange, which connects directly to the M-1 motorway near Hasan Abdal, allowing seamless traffic flow from Islamabad and Rawalpindi.[2] Further north, the Haripur Interchange provides access to the district of Haripur and links to provincial roads, while the Havelian Interchange facilitates connections to the Grand Trunk Road (N-5) and routes toward Abbottabad city.[1] The Mansehra Interchange serves as a critical junction for the Mansehra district, enabling onward travel to the Karakoram Highway (N-35) via Balakot and Thakot, with the northern end at Thakot linking to the Indus River valley and CPEC extensions.[17] Navigating the Hazara region's rugged topography presented significant engineering hurdles, including steep gradients, narrow valleys, and over 40-meter-deep gorges that delayed construction phases.[18] The motorway's alignment through the Himalayan foothills required extensive earthworks and stabilization measures to mitigate landslide risks in seismically active zones.[18] To overcome these obstacles, the Havelian-to-Thakot segment incorporates 6 tunnelsâ€”such as the 1.75 km Abbottabad Shimla Hill Tunnelâ€”and 47 bridges spanning ravines and rivers, with viaducts elevating the roadway above flood-prone areas.[19] These features demanded advanced geotechnical surveys and reinforced designs to ensure stability amid variable soil conditions and heavy monsoon runoff.[8]

Construction Process

Phased Development and Key Milestones

The construction of the M-15 motorway, spanning 180 kilometers from Burhan Interchange near Hasan Abdal to Thakot, proceeded in multiple phases under the oversight of Pakistan's National Highway Authority, with work divided into seven packages to address challenging mountainous terrain and integrate with the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor. Initial contracts for early packages were awarded starting in 2013, though substantive ground construction accelerated from 2016 onward, focusing on four-lane configurations with provisions for expansion.[20] The first key milestone occurred with the operational opening of the Burhan to Shah Maqsood section, covering approximately 60 kilometers, on 12 December 2017, followed by its formal inauguration later that month on 27 December.[20][21] This phase connected Punjab's motorway network to northern routes, reducing travel time and enhancing access to Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. The subsequent Shah Maqsood to Mansehra segment, part of the initial four packages reaching Mansehra, became operational on 20 November 2019, completing the core Burhan-Mansehra alignment of about 120 kilometers.[20][22] The final phases encompassed the more rugged Mansehra to Thakot extension, roughly 80 kilometers with 11 tunnels and multiple bridges to navigate the Hazara region's topography. This section achieved completion and public opening in July 2020, with the Havelian-Thakot stretch formally inaugurated on 30 July, enabling end-to-end connectivity along the full route.[23] These phased openings prioritized high-traffic southern segments first, allowing partial utilization during northern construction amid delays from geological complexities and funding tied to CPEC financing.[20]

Contractors, Costs, and Engineering Innovations

The construction of the M-15 motorway, also known as the Hazara Motorway, was divided into multiple packages managed by the National Highway Authority (NHA), involving joint ventures of international and local firms. For the Hassanabdal-Havelian section (E-35 Expressway), the primary contractor was a joint venture between Turkey's Limak Construction and Pakistan's ZKB Construction for Package III, focusing on key segments including bridges and earthworks in challenging terrain.[24] Other sections, such as Havelian to Thakot, utilized similar public-private partnerships, though specific contractors for each package beyond the initial ADB-financed portion remain documented primarily through NHA procurement records.[25] Costs for the project varied by section and escalated due to terrain complexities and scope adjustments. The initial 59 km Havelian-Mansehra segment, approved in 2014 at Rs 33 billion, saw its budget revised to Rs 34.37 billion by 2019 to account for overruns in materials and construction delays.[26][27] The Hassanabdal-Havelian portion received financing from the Asian Development Bank alongside a Rs 10.021 billion (US$35 million) grant from the United Kingdom, covering approximately 40 km of four-to-six-lane controlled-access road.[24] Overall, the full 180 km alignment's expenditures reflect phased funding under national and multilateral sources, with no aggregated total publicly finalized beyond sectional figures exceeding Rs 70 billion collectively for core segments.[28] Engineering innovations on the M-15 addressed the rugged Himalayan foothills through extensive use of tunnels and elevated structures to minimize environmental disruption and ensure stability. The Havelian-Thakot section incorporates six tunnels totaling several kilometers, including the 1.75 km Abbottabad-Shimla Hill Tunnel, engineered with reinforced concrete linings for seismic resilience in a high-risk zone.[8] Complementing these are 47 bridges, many multi-span viaducts spanning valleys up to 110 feet high, constructed using girder launching techniques for efficient assembly in remote areas.[29] These features enable a fenced, access-controlled design with speeds up to 100 km/h, reducing landslide vulnerabilities compared to the parallel N-35 Karakoram Highway.[30]

Inauguration and Operationalization

Sectional Openings (2017â€“2020)

The initial operational section of the M-15 motorway, spanning 47 kilometers from Burhan Interchange to Shah Maqsood near Havelian, was inaugurated on December 27, 2017, by Prime Minister Shahid Khaqan Abbasi.[31][32] This segment, part of the broader 180-kilometer route from Hassanabdal to Thakot, alleviated congestion on the parallel N-35 Karakoram Highway and improved connectivity between Punjab and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa provinces.[31] Subsequent progress focused on the adjoining Havelian to Mansehra stretch, approximately 39 kilometers long, which faced delays due to terrain challenges and construction quality issues.[33] Despite a planned opening in August 2019 coinciding with Pakistan's Independence Day, the section was not made fully operational until mid-November, with formal inauguration by Prime Minister Imran Khan on November 18, 2019.[33][34] Local reports highlighted incomplete finishing work, including pending interchanges at Havelian, Mangal, and Baidadi, raising safety concerns for initial users. In July 2020, the Mansehra to Thakot extension, covering 68 kilometers through rugged Himalayan terrain, was completed and inaugurated on July 29 by Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Chief Minister Mahmood Khan.[35] This phase linked the motorway to the Karakoram Highway at Thakot, enhancing access to northern areas and facilitating China-Pakistan Economic Corridor logistics, though full integration required ongoing viaduct and tunnel completions.[35] By the end of 2020, approximately 154 kilometers of the M-15 were operational in phases, marking significant advancement from prior single-lane alternatives.[35]

Full Completion and Initial Operations

The M-15 Hazara Motorway reached full completion with the opening of its northern Mansehra-Thakot section in mid-2020, following a comprehensive safety audit directed by Prime Minister Imran Khan to ensure structural integrity amid challenging mountainous terrain.[35] This final segment connected the previously operational portions, spanning approximately 40 kilometers through seismically active and ecologically sensitive areas, marking the end of phased construction that began in 2014 under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor framework.[21] Initial operations integrated the entire 180-kilometer route from Burhan Interchange near Hasan Abdal to Thakot, enabling seamless four-to-six-lane access for heavy vehicles and reducing transit times from Islamabad to Khyber Pakhtunkhwa's northern districts by up to three hours compared to the parallel N-35 Karakoram Highway.[36] The National Highway Authority (NHA) assumed full oversight, with toll collection commencing at strategic plazas to fund maintenance, while the National Highways and Motorway Police enforced speed limits, vehicle standards, and emergency protocols tailored to the route's tunnels and viaducts.[37] Early operational data highlighted improved freight efficiency for CPEC-related logistics, though initial challenges included adapting to high-altitude weather variations and conducting routine inspections on bridges like the 1.5-kilometer-long spans over the Indus River tributaries.[35] No major disruptions were reported in the first year, with the motorway handling increased commercial traffic while prioritizing passenger safety through real-time monitoring systems installed during final fit-out.[21]

Technical Specifications

Design Standards and Capacity

The M-15 motorway follows the geometric design criteria established by Pakistan's National Highway Authority for high-speed controlled-access highways, including minimum curve radii, superelevation rates, and sight distances calibrated for a design speed of 120 km/h in its primary sections.[24] Lane widths adhere to standard motorway specifications of 3.65 meters, with paved shoulders typically 2.5 meters wide on each side and a central median varying from 4 to 12 meters depending on the terrain and section. The right-of-way is fenced to restrict access, incorporating grade-separated interchanges, overpasses, and underpasses to eliminate at-grade crossings, enhancing safety and flow efficiency. Cross-sections vary by segment to accommodate topographic challenges in the Hazara region: the initial 59 km from Burhan to Havelian features a six-lane configuration (three lanes per direction) with provisions for future expansion, transitioning to four lanes (two per direction) from Havelian to Mansehra, and narrowing to two lanes (one per direction) from Mansehra to Thakot.[27] This phased lane reduction reflects cost and terrain constraints in mountainous areas, while maintaining divided carriageways with concrete barriers. The pavement structure employs flexible asphalt overlays over granular bases, designed for heavy axle loads up to 10 tons per axle, in line with Pakistan's motorway loading standards. Traffic capacity is governed by the lane configuration and design speed, with the six-lane Burhan-Havelian segment capable of handling up to approximately 6,000 vehicles per hour in both directions under optimal conditions (level of service C, per highway capacity methodologies adapted for Pakistan). The four-lane and two-lane sections offer reduced capacities of about 4,000 and 2,000 vehicles per hour total, respectively, sufficient for projected regional freight and tourist traffic from the Karakoram Highway linkage but potentially requiring upgrades as volumes grow.[25] These capacities assume standard vehicle mixes dominated by cars and light trucks, with enforcement of speed limits at 120 km/h for cars to prevent congestion from speed differentials.[5]

Safety, Environmental, and Maintenance Features

The M-15 motorway incorporates standard safety features for controlled-access highways in Pakistan, including a divided carriageway with median barriers to prevent head-on collisions and W-beam steel guardrails along curves and embankments to redirect errant vehicles.[38] These elements adhere to National Highway Authority (NHA) design norms, which emphasize crash-tested barriers capable of containing impacts from vehicles traveling at design speeds up to 120 km/h. The route's five tunnelsâ€”located in Abbottabad (two), Battal, Karmong, and Mansehraâ€”feature semi-transverse ventilation systems for smoke extraction and air quality maintenance during incidents, alongside embedded emergency lighting and signage for evacuation.[39] National Highways and Motorway Police (NHMP) patrols enforce speed limits and provide rapid response, contributing to reduced accident rates compared to the superseded N-35 Karakoram Highway. Environmental mitigation follows Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) protocols mandated by Pakistan's Environmental Protection Agency, with measures implemented during construction of the initial E-35 section (Hassanabdal-Havelian) including regular water sprinkling to suppress dust from earthworks and erosion control on slopes in the hilly Hazara terrain.[40] The design prioritizes elevated viaducts and tunnels over extensive land clearing, minimizing habitat fragmentation and soil disturbance in forested and seismically active areas; ongoing monitoring requires adherence to unanticipated impact protocols, such as revegetation of borrow pits.[24] No dedicated wildlife crossings are documented, though the alignment avoids major protected zones, reflecting a pragmatic balance between connectivity needs and baseline ecological preservation amid CPEC priorities.[41] Maintenance is funded primarily through toll revenues collected at five interchanges via electronic M-Tag systems, which automate deductions to streamline operations and support routine upkeep like resurfacing and debris clearance.[42] The NHA oversees periodic inspections, with NHMP responsible for surface patrols addressing potholes and signage integrity; recent policy updates impose surcharges on non-M-Tag users to incentivize adoption and bolster funds for deferred maintenance on high-traffic segments.[43] Service areas provide rest stops for vehicle checks, though incomplete sections have prompted localized critiques of uneven enforcement.

Economic and Strategic Impacts

Connectivity and Trade Enhancements

The M-15 motorway, spanning 180 kilometers from the Burhan Interchange on the M-1 to Mansehra, establishes a controlled-access link that integrates central Pakistan's motorway network with the northern transportation corridors of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province. This alignment bypasses narrower, more vulnerable segments of the pre-existing N-35 national highway, offering a more reliable pathway for vehicular traffic toward the Karakoram Highway and Gilgit-Baltistan. By connecting to the broader Burhan-Khunjerab route, it bolsters the western variant of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), providing an alternative to the eastern routing via Abbottabad and reducing dependency on single-path infrastructure prone to disruptions from landslides or congestion.[15][44] Operational since phased openings between 2017 and 2020, the motorway has curtailed travel durations and logistics expenses along its corridor, enabling expedited movement of freight such as minerals from northern quarries, agricultural outputs from Hazara farmlands, and manufactured goods from Punjab's industrial hubs. This efficiency gain supports intra-provincial trade by streamlining supply chains that previously relied on slower, less secure roads, while also facilitating access to dry ports and export terminals linked via the M-1. In the CPEC context, enhanced route capacity correlates with potential expansions in bilateral trade volumes, as econometric models indicate that infrastructure upgrades like those on the M-15 can accommodate incremental freight demands tied to annual trade increments of up to 1 million tons.[45][46][47] Beyond domestic flows, the M-15's integration with the Karakoram Highway via N-35 upgrades positions it as a conduit for trans-border commerce, particularly for commodities traversing the Khunjerab Pass into China, including Pakistani exports like textiles and imports of machinery or raw materials. Freight analyses of the full Burhan-Khunjerab alignment, incorporating the M-15 segment, project that capacity enhancements could yield up to 1.3 million ton-kilometers of additional annual throughput per million dollars in trade growth, underscoring its role in elevating Pakistan's position as a regional transit node. These developments, while promising for volume increases, hinge on sustained maintenance and complementary investments in border facilities to realize full trade potential amid variable geopolitical and terrain-related risks.[47][15]

Tourism Boost and Regional Development

The completion of the M-15 Hazara Motorway has significantly reduced travel times from Islamabad to Mansehra from approximately four hours to 1.5 hours, facilitating safer and faster access to northern tourist destinations such as Kaghan Valley, Naran, Nathia Gali, and Swat.[48] This enhanced connectivity has directly contributed to increased tourist inflows, with surveys indicating that 90.83% of 120 respondents in Abbottabad and Mansehra reported a rise in tourism under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), of which the M-15 forms a key segment.[49] Improved road infrastructure has also spurred demand for accommodations, as evidenced by 67.85% of 112 hotel managers in the region attributing higher visitor numbers to the motorway's role in linking Hazara to scenic alpine areas and cultural sites.[50] Regional development benefits extend beyond tourism, with the M-15's 180 km alignmentâ€”featuring interchanges at key points like Chattar Plain, Battal, and Shinkiariâ€”fostering local economic activity through job creation in construction, maintenance, and ancillary services such as roadside eateries and shops.[41] Integration with CPEC initiatives, including the Havelian Dry Port and Rashakai Special Economic Zone, has amplified these effects by improving logistics and trade links to northern Pakistan, thereby supporting small businesses and infrastructure upgrades in districts like Haripur, Abbottabad, and Battagram.[41] Surveys among tourists project further growth, with 83.33% anticipating sustained expansion in the sector due to the motorway's role in overcoming previous barriers like poor road conditions on the parallel N-35 highway.[49] In the hotel sector specifically, 76.78% of managers foresee industry expansion tied to CPEC-enhanced tourism, driven by better access to high-altitude attractions and a shift toward international-standard facilities to accommodate rising demand.[50] These developments have positioned the Hazara region as a suburban gateway under CPEC, promoting balanced growth by diversifying local economies historically reliant on agriculture and remittances.[41]

Controversies and Challenges

Corruption Allegations and Investigations

The construction of the M-15 Hazara Motorway, initiated under the PML-N government as part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, faced allegations of financial mismanagement and embezzlement during subsequent audits by the PTI-led administration. In November 2019, Minister for Communications Murad Saeed announced that an internal inquiry by the Ministry of Communications had uncovered irregularities in project expenditures, leading to the recovery of Rs. 10.8 billion from contractors and involved parties. Saeed attributed the embezzlement to cost overruns and improper payments during the PML-N era, emphasizing that the recoveries were achieved through rigorous scrutiny of contracts awarded to the China Communication Construction Company and local subcontractors.[51][52] The National Accountability Bureau (NAB) reviewed related audit paras raised by the Auditor General of Pakistan concerning the Hazara Motorway but concluded there was no evidence of misconduct or corruption warranting formal charges, allowing the National Highway Authority (NHA) to settle the issues administratively. NHA Chairman Shahid Ashraf Tarar described the audit objections as minor procedural lapses exaggerated by auditors, rather than systemic graft, and noted that similar disputes were common in large-scale infrastructure projects. No criminal investigations or prosecutions by NAB or other anti-corruption bodies specifically targeting M-15 executives or officials have been publicly documented, distinguishing it from more protracted scandals in other motorway segments like M-6.[53] Local stakeholders in Mansehra district have periodically alleged misuse of toll revenues generated from the motorway's plazas, claiming funds intended for road maintenance were diverted, though these complaints pertain more to operational upkeep than construction-phase corruption and remain unverified by federal probes. Overall, while the PTI's recoveries highlighted accountability gaps in project oversight, independent corroboration of the embezzlement scale has been limited to ministerial statements, with NHA audits focusing on procedural compliance rather than proven malfeasance.

Construction Delays, Costs, and Performance Critiques

The construction of the Hazara Motorway, designated as M-15, experienced notable delays during its development phase. In May 2019, work on the project, part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, was delayed by three months primarily due to unresolved land acquisition issues, with experts warning of potential further setbacks if disputes persisted.[18] An additional three-month delay was reported in January 2019 for the Hassanabdal-Havelian section, exacerbating concerns over timeline adherence.[54] Landowners also protested delays in compensation payments as late as April 2022, highlighting ongoing administrative hurdles in securing right-of-way.[55] Cost overruns materialized through revisions to project estimates. The National Highway Authority revised the PC-1 document for the Hassanabdal-Havelian segment upward from Rs 30.97 billion to Rs 34.37 billion, signaling escalation driven by delays and associated adjustments.[54] Such increases reflect broader patterns in Pakistani infrastructure projects, where initial budgeting often underestimates complexities like terrain challenges in the Hazara region and procurement inefficiencies. Post-completion performance has drawn critiques regarding maintenance and safety. In July 2025, the Peshawar High Court mandated the National Highway Authority to repair the motorway within three months, citing deteriorating conditions that compromised usability.[56] Ongoing repair efforts at five major sites were reported in October 2025, with three completed, indicating persistent structural wear possibly linked to construction quality or heavy usage.[57] Safety concerns emerged in 2021 when road experts highlighted accidents stemming from inadequate road grip, causing vehicles to lose control at speeds above 100 km/h, prompting calls for construction reviews.[58] A January 2024 incident, where a passenger van collided with construction machinery resulting in six deaths and eight injuries, underscored unresolved hazards from incomplete or adjacent works.[59]
