List of current state governors in Nigeria

The list of current state governors in Nigeria enumerates the chief executives of the country's 36 states, each wielding constitutional authority over subnational governance, including budget allocation, infrastructure development, and local security coordination within the federal framework.[1] Elected primarily through the 18 March 2023 general elections and sworn in on 29 May 2023 for four-year terms, these governors operate under the 1999 Constitution, balancing state autonomy with federal oversight amid economic challenges and partisan dynamics.[2] As of October 2025, the All Progressives Congress holds 25 governorships, reflecting gains from recent defections that diminished the Peoples Democratic Party to 8 seats, while the All Progressives Grand Alliance, Labour Party, and New Nigeria Peoples Party each control one state.[2][3] This distribution underscores the APC's consolidated influence in state-level politics, influencing national policy negotiations via forums like the Nigeria Governors' Forum.

Constitutional Framework

Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for election as governor of a state in Nigeria, a person must satisfy the criteria outlined in Section 177 of the 1999 Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria (as amended).[4] These include being a citizen of Nigeria by birth; having attained the age of 35 years; being a member of a political party and receiving sponsorship from that party for the election; and having received education up to at least School Certificate level or its equivalent.[4][5] The citizenship requirement emphasizes birthright to ensure undivided national loyalty, while party sponsorship enforces the constitutional framework mandating political party-based contests for executive offices.[4] The educational threshold, set at secondary school completion, has remained unchanged despite repeated legislative proposals in 2024 and 2025 to raise it to higher diploma or university degree levels, none of which have been enacted into law as of October 2025.[4][6] Section 182 specifies disqualifications that bar otherwise qualified individuals from contesting.[7] These encompass voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship (subject to exceptions under Section 28); prior election as governor in two consecutive terms; adjudication as a lunatic or of unsound mind under Nigerian law; a sentence of death imposed by a competent court; conviction and sentencing for offenses involving dishonesty or fraud; conviction for any other offense with a sentence of imprisonment exceeding six months (or fines in such cases); being an undischarged bankrupt; employment in public service without resigning at least 30 days prior to the election; membership in a secret society; indictment for embezzlement or fraud by a government-accepted judicial or administrative inquiry; presentation of a forged certificate to the Independent National Electoral Commission; or conviction for election-related offenses.[7][8] Additional disqualifications may be prescribed by National Assembly legislation.[7] Certain disqualifications related to mental incapacity, death sentences, dishonesty convictions, or bankruptcy do not apply if an appeal is pending in a Nigerian court until its final determination, lapse, or abandonment.[7] These provisions aim to safeguard executive integrity by excluding those with demonstrated ethical lapses, financial irresponsibility, or divided allegiances, though enforcement has occasionally hinged on judicial interpretation amid disputes over certificate authenticity or prior convictions.[7][8]

Election and Tenure

State governors in Nigeria are elected through direct popular vote in elections organized by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC), with each state treated as a single constituency for this purpose. Candidates must meet specific eligibility criteria outlined in the 1999 Constitution, including being a Nigerian citizen by birth, attaining the age of 35 years, possessing at least a school certificate or its equivalent, and being sponsored by a political party as its candidate.[4] Elections occur no earlier than 150 days and no later than 30 days before the expiration of the incumbent's term, aligning with the national electoral calendar, though off-cycle polls may be scheduled for states affected by court rulings or vacancies. The process employs a first-past-the-post system, where the candidate receiving the highest number of valid votes is declared the winner; in cases of a tie, INEC conducts a fresh election between the tied candidates within seven days.[9] A governor's tenure commences on the date of taking the oath of office following the election declaration and lasts for four years, subject to provisions for removal via impeachment or other constitutional disqualifications.[10] Incumbents are limited to a maximum of two consecutive four-year terms, after which they are ineligible to contest for the same office until at least one full term has elapsed, ensuring periodic turnover while allowing for tested leadership continuity.[11] Vacancies arising from death, resignation, impeachment, or permanent incapacity trigger the deputy governor's assumption of office for the remainder of the term, with a new deputy appointed by the governor and approved by the state House of Assembly within seven days.[12] This framework, rooted in the 1999 Constitution (as amended), balances executive stability with democratic accountability, though implementation has faced challenges such as electoral disputes resolved by the Governorship Election Tribunals.[13]

Oath of Office

The Oath of Office for a state governor in Nigeria is prescribed in Part I of the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, 1999 (as amended).[14] This oath, along with the separate Oath of Allegiance, must be taken before the governor-elect can commence performing the functions of the office, as stipulated in Section 185(1) of the Constitution.[14] The oaths are typically administered on the inauguration dateâ€”often May 29 for states aligning with the federal cycle or immediately following off-cycle election victories certified by the Independent National Electoral Commissionâ€”ensuring a seamless transition without a vacancy in executive authority.[10] The administration of the oaths is conducted by the Chief Judge of the state or the individual temporarily exercising those judicial functions, per Section 185(2) of the Constitution.[7] This ceremonial act formalizes the governor's entry into office, with the deputy governor undergoing a parallel process under Section 187(2). The text of the Oath of Office reads:
I, [name], do solemnly swear/affirm that I will be faithful and bear true allegiance to the Federal Republic of Nigeria and that I will faithfully discharge the duties of my office to the best of my ability and conscience, in accordance with the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria; that I will abide by the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria; that I will not allow my personal interest to conflict with the national interest; that I will not allow any consideration of sectional, tribal, ethnic, religious or party interest to influence my decisions or actions; that I will not exercise any power of office to the detriment of the Federal Republic of Nigeria or its people; that I will uphold the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria in the discharge of my duties; that I will do right to all manner of people, according to law, without fear or favour, affection or ill-will; that I will not directly or indirectly communicate or reveal to any person any matter which shall be brought under my consideration or the knowledge of which I shall become acquainted with in the course of my official duties, except as required by law or authorised by the President or as may in the opinion of the Head of the National Executive Council be in the public interest; So help me God.[14]
This formulation permits either a religious swearing or a secular affirmation, accommodating diverse beliefs while imposing binding obligations on fidelity to the nation, constitutional supremacy, avoidance of personal or parochial biases, impartial administration of justice, preservation of official secrets, and rejection of corrupt practices.[14] Breaches of these commitments can underpin legal challenges, impeachment proceedings under Sections 188â€“189, or judicial interpretations of gubernatorial accountability, though enforcement has historically varied due to political dynamics. The oath's emphasis on national over sectional loyalty reflects Nigeria's federal structure, prioritizing unity amid ethnic and regional diversity, yet it has prompted debates on its implications for state autonomy versus federal oversight.[15]

Roles and Powers

Executive Authority

The executive powers of a state in Nigeria are vested in the governor, who may exercise them directly or through the deputy governor, commissioners appointed pursuant to Section 192 of the 1999 Constitution, or other subordinate officers subordinate to the governor.[16][5] These powers encompass the administration and execution of laws within the state's legislative competence, primarily over residual matters not assigned to the federal or concurrent legislative lists in the Second Schedule to the Constitution.[14] As the chief executive of the state under Section 176(2), the governor directs the state civil service, convenes and presides over the State Executive Council, and ensures the implementation of state policies and programs funded through the state's budgetary allocations.[16][5] The governor holds authority to appoint key officials to support executive functions, including commissioners to head ministries and special advisers under Sections 191 and 192, subject to confirmation by the state House of Assembly where required.[13] These appointments enable the delegation of administrative responsibilities across sectors such as health, education, and infrastructure, which fall under state jurisdiction. The governor also possesses oversight over state finances, including the preparation and presentation of the annual budget estimate to the legislature for approval, as well as the allocation of lands and resources under state control, such as granting rights of occupancy pursuant to land use laws derived from constitutional frameworks.[17] Among specific enumerated powers, the governor must assent to or withhold assent from bills passed by the state House of Assembly within 30 days under Section 100(4); if assent is withheld and the bill is repassed by a two-thirds majority, it becomes law without further gubernatorial approval.[18] Additionally, under Section 212(1), the governor exercises the prerogative of mercy for offenses against state laws, including granting pardons, reprieves, or sentence substitutions, following consultation with an advisory council comprising the attorney-general and up to three other members.[19] These provisions ensure gubernatorial involvement in clemency decisions, applied in practice to alleviate sentences for convicts serving terms in state correctional facilities.[20]

Legislative and Judicial Interactions

State governors in Nigeria interact with the state House of Assembly primarily through the legislative process outlined in Section 100 of the 1999 Constitution, where bills passed by the assembly require gubernatorial assent to become law.[18] If a governor withholds assent, the House of Assembly may override the veto with a two-thirds majority vote, enacting the bill without executive approval.[12] This mechanism embodies checks and balances, allowing the legislature to counter executive resistance while enabling governors to influence policy by proposing bills or using veto power strategically.[21] Governors also submit annual budgets to the House of Assembly for approval, which scrutinizes expenditures and can amend or reject proposals, ensuring fiscal oversight.[22] The assembly confirms key gubernatorial appointments, such as commissioners and auditors-general, providing legislative vetting to prevent unchecked executive patronage.[23] In turn, governors may prorogue or dissolve the assembly under exceptional circumstances, such as functional paralysis, though this power is rarely invoked and subject to constitutional limits.[13] Regarding judicial interactions, governors appoint the Chief Judge of the state High Court and Presidents of the Customary Court of Appeal upon recommendation by the National Judicial Council (NJC), with subsequent confirmation by a two-thirds majority of the House of Assembly.[23] This process integrates executive initiative with judicial advisory input and legislative approval, aiming to balance influence while insulating appointments from sole gubernatorial discretion.[24] The state judiciary maintains independence in adjudicating disputes involving executive actions, such as veto overrides or budget implementations, thereby checking gubernatorial overreach through judicial review.[25] These interactions reflect the federal constitution's adaptation of separation of powers at the state level, where the governor's executive authority is constrained by legislative confirmation powers and judicial oversight, preventing dominance by any branch.[26] Instances of tension, such as assembly impeachments or NJC-directed reversals of appointments, underscore the system's operational checks, though enforcement varies by political alignment.[27]

Political Landscape

Party Composition

As of October 2025, the All Progressives Congress (APC) controls governorships in 25 of Nigeria's 36 states, consolidating its dominance established in the 2023 general elections through subsequent opposition defections.[2] The Peoples Democratic Party (PDP), once a major opposition force, now holds only 8 states following the October 2025 defections of governors from Bayelsa, Enugu, and Delta states, among others earlier in the year.[2][28] These shifts reduced PDP's representation from 11 states post-2023 to its current low, highlighting the fluidity of party affiliations in Nigerian politics where governors often switch to align with the federal ruling party for resource access and political survival.[29] The remaining states are governed by parties outside the APC-PDP duopoly: Anambra under the All Progressives Grand Alliance (APGA), Abia under the Labour Party, and Kano under the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP).[30] No defections have been reported from these minority parties as of late October 2025. This composition reflects APC's strategic gains, with 25 governors enabling coordinated support for federal policies and influencing the Nigeria Governors' Forum, where APC members form the majority.[2]

Regional and Demographic Patterns

The All Progressives Congress (APC) holds a commanding position across Nigeria's northern geopolitical zones, controlling all seven states in the North West except Kano (NNPP) and Zamfara (PDP), five of six in North Central, and three of six in North East as of October 2025.[31] In the South West, APC governs four of six states, with PDP holding Osun and Oyo.[31] This northern and southwestern dominance reflects APC's consolidation of power following the 2023 elections and subsequent PDP defections, including in Delta, Akwa Ibom, and Edo states in the South South zone.[32] In contrast, the South East exhibits the most fragmented control, with APC in Ebonyi and Imo, PDP in Enugu, Labour Party in Abia, and APGA in Anambra, underscoring persistent opposition strongholds amid ethnic and regional voting patterns favoring non-APC parties.[31] The South South zone, traditionally PDP-leaning, has seen APC gains through defections, controlling Cross River outright and newly aligned states like Delta, Akwa Ibom, Edo, leaving PDP with Bayelsa and Rivers as of late October 2025.[32][33] Overall, APC governs 24 states, PDP 9, with one each for APGA, Labour, and NNPP, indicating a shift toward APC hegemony driven by federal influence and party-switching.[29] Demographically, all 36 governors are male, reflecting broader gender disparities in Nigerian elective politics where no woman has served as state governor.[34] Ethnic representation favors major groups, with Fulani holding approximately nine governorships, Yoruba seven, and Igbo six, concentrated in their respective northern, southwestern, and southeastern bases, though Fulani influence extends beyond the North West due to political alliances.[35] Religiously, the composition approximates national divides, with about 18 Muslim governors (predominantly in northern states) and 18 Christian governors (mainly in southern states), aligning with zonal Muslim majorities in the north and Christian majorities in the south.[36] Ages typically range from the mid-40s to 70s, with an average around 56, as younger candidates face barriers from incumbency and financial requirements.[34]

Key Statistics and Trends

As of October 2025, the All Progressives Congress (APC) controls governorships in 24 states, representing the largest bloc among Nigeria's 36 state executives, followed by the Peoples Democratic Party (PDP) with 9 states, and single states held by the All Progressives Grand Alliance (APGA) in Anambra, the Labour Party in Abia, and the New Nigeria Peoples Party (NNPP) in Kano.[29] This distribution reflects APC gains through defections from PDP ranks in 2025, contributing to a trend of consolidation under the ruling party at the state level.[39] All current governors are male, with no female representation among the 36 state leaders, underscoring persistent gender disparities in Nigerian executive politics despite broader calls for inclusivity.[40] Ages range from 43 (Usman Ododo of Kogi State) to over 70 in some cases, with only one governor under 50 and the majority exceeding that threshold, indicating a leadership cadre skewed toward older demographics despite youth agitation for representation.[41] Most governors are serving their first term under the 2023 elections, with terms extending to 2027 barring unforeseen vacancies; re-election rates from 2023 saw about half of incumbents retain office, highlighting competitive yet incumbency-favoring electoral dynamics.[34] A notable trend is the APC's expansion via post-election defections, reducing PDP's share from 11 states immediately post-2023 to 9 by late 2025, which critics attribute to federal influence but supporters view as ideological realignment.[29]
