Al-Shifa Hospital

Al-Shifa Hospital is the largest and most prominent medical complex in the Gaza Strip, situated in the northern Rimal neighborhood of Gaza City, with a pre-war bed capacity of around 700 to 800, functioning as the primary referral center for healthcare in northern Gaza.[1][2][3] Established in 1946 from former British army barracks during the Mandate period, it has historically served as a cornerstone of Gaza's healthcare system, providing specialized services amid chronic resource shortages exacerbated by governance under Hamas since 2007.[4][5] The facility gained international notoriety during the 2023-2024 Israel-Hamas war due to documented instances of Hamas embedding military operations within its premises, including the use of patient areas for detaining hostagesâ€”as evidenced by footage of gunmen dragging captives through the hospital foyerâ€”and the storage of weapons and explosives in wards and corridors.[6] Israeli Defense Forces raids in November 2023 and March 2024 uncovered assault rifles, grenades, and tunnel entrances connected to an extensive underground network beneath the complex, confirming Hamas's exploitation of the site for command and control functions while endangering civilians and medical staff.[7][8] These operations resulted in severe structural damage, rendering much of Al-Shifa an empty shell by mid-2024, with critical infrastructure like oxygen systems, electrical grids, and surgical suites destroyed or looted, severely impairing its capacity to deliver care amid Gaza's broader healthcare collapse.[1] As of 2025, the hospital operates at minimal functionality, struggling to accommodate surging wounded from ongoing conflict with limited beds and supplies.[9]

Location and Facilities

Architectural Design and Infrastructure

The main complex of Al-Shifa Hospital in Gaza City was redesigned and expanded in the 1980s under Israeli administration by Tel Aviv-based architects Gershon Zippor and Benjamin Idelson, who specialized in medical facilities.[10] [11] The structure features a central four-story building with reinforced concrete construction, courtyards, and adjacent specialized wings, forming a sprawling campus spanning approximately 50,000 square meters.[4] This layout prioritized operational efficiency, with wide corridors, modular patient wards, and integrated administrative blocks to support high-volume care in a densely populated urban area.[12] A key infrastructural element was the incorporation of extensive sub-basement levels beneath the main buildings, designed for critical utilities to ensure redundancy and protection from surface disruptions.[4] These included dedicated spaces for backup generators, oxygen production and distribution systems, fuel storage tanks, and maintenance workshops, connected via reinforced tunnels and shafts for secure access.[10] The below-ground network, totaling multiple levels in some sections, was engineered with thick concrete slabs and ventilation systems to withstand seismic activity and aerial threats common in the region, reflecting first-principles engineering for infrastructural resilience.[12] Subsequent architectural additions prior to 2007 included dedicated wings for cardiology and maternity services, integrated into the core complex with specialized plumbing, electrical, and HVAC systems to meet clinical demands.[13] These expansions, funded in part by U.S. aid and other international donors during the Israeli era, extended the footprint eastward and added rooftop heliports for emergency evacuations, enhancing the facility's self-sufficiency without altering the foundational underground utility framework.[13] The overall design emphasized durability, with load-bearing columns and blast-resistant glazing in key areas, positioning the hospital as Gaza's premier medical infrastructure at the time of completion.[4]

Capacity, Services, and Role in Gaza Healthcare

Al-Shifa Hospital served as the largest and most advanced medical facility in the Gaza Strip prior to October 2023, with a reported capacity of approximately 900 beds and a staff of around 1,656 employees, including doctors, nurses, and administrative personnel.[14] Managed by the Palestinian Ministry of Health, it functioned as the primary referral center for the region's over 2 million residents, handling complex cases that smaller clinics could not accommodate.[15] The hospital's emergency department alone managed up to 1,000 visits daily, contributing to an annual total exceeding 300,000 emergency encounters, while outpatient services addressed around 800 cases per day.[14] Key services included specialized departments for surgery (with over 20 operating theaters), pediatrics, internal medicine, and emergency care, enabling the facility to perform approximately 28,000 major surgeries annually before the escalation of conflict.[16] It also provided treatment for chronic conditions such as cardiovascular diseases and renal failure through dialysis units, despite chronic shortages of equipment and pharmaceuticals exacerbated by import restrictions under the Gaza blockade.[17] Oncology services were available on a limited basis, often requiring coordination for advanced chemotherapy or radiotherapy referrals outside the Strip when local resources proved insufficient.[18] In Gaza's constrained healthcare landscapeâ€”where the Ministry of Health operated 36 hospitals with a total of roughly 2,243 bedsâ€”Al-Shifa accounted for a disproportionate share of specialized capacity, employing about a quarter of the Strip's hospital workforce and serving as the backbone for trauma, maternity, and inpatient care.[19][15] This central role persisted amid logistical dependencies on external aid for supplies, allowing the hospital to maintain operations for routine and emergency needs, though elective procedures like non-urgent surgeries faced backlogs exceeding standard thresholds due to resource limitations.[20]

Historical Development

Establishment Under Egyptian Administration (1948â€“1967)

Al-Shifa Hospital was established in 1946 during the British Mandate for Palestine as a medical facility on the site of a former British Army outpost in Gaza City.[15] [21] The initial setup consisted of basic infrastructure designed to provide primary care, reflecting the limited healthcare resources available in the region at the time.[4] Following the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, Egypt occupied the Gaza Strip and assumed administrative control, placing the hospital under Egyptian authority.[22] [11] This transition enabled its conversion into Gaza's central hospital, serving the local Palestinian population amid a sharp increase in demand due to the displacement of approximately 200,000 refugees into the Strip.[11] Under Egyptian rule, the facility saw initial expansions, including the establishment of an adjacent hospital for contagious diseases shortly after 1948, though overall development remained modest with basic wards focused on general and emergency care.[11] [23] The period was marked by operational constraints, including overcrowding from the refugee crisis and shortages of medical supplies and personnel, as Gaza's healthcare system strained under Egyptian military governance without significant international investment.[24] By 1967, prior to the Six-Day War, Al-Shifa had grown into the Strip's principal medical institution but retained a capacity of around 100-200 beds, emphasizing outpatient services and rudimentary inpatient treatment amid ongoing resource limitations.[25]

Expansion Under Israeli Administration (1967â€“2005)

Following Israel's capture of the Gaza Strip during the Six-Day War in June 1967, Al-Shifa Hospital underwent initial upgrades to its existing infrastructure, including enhancements to wards and basic equipment to handle increased demand from the local population.[10] These modifications laid the groundwork for transforming the facility from a modest clinic into Gaza's primary medical center, with Israeli authorities prioritizing operational reliability amid regional tensions.[4] A major expansion occurred in the 1980s, directed by Israel's Department of Public Works, which commissioned Israeli engineers and architects to design and construct key structures such as the central five-story surgical building and specialized pavilions for departments including internal medicine, pediatrics, and cardiology.[26][10] This project, supervised by military engineering units, added hundreds of bedsâ€”elevating capacity from around 100 in the pre-1967 era to over 300 by the late 1980sâ€”and introduced advanced equipment like X-ray machines and operating theaters, funded through Israeli civil administration budgets aimed at improving public health services.[4] Underground systems, including basements housing power generators and oxygen production units, were incorporated for redundancy against outages, ensuring continuous operation during disruptions.[13][10] By the early 2000s, these developments had positioned Al-Shifa as the backbone of Gaza's healthcare, treating tens of thousands of patients annually across inpatient, outpatient, and emergency services, with specialized units handling complex cases like trauma and infectious diseases that smaller facilities could not.[26] The hospital's expanded role included serving as a referral center for the Strip's 1.3 million residents by 2005, supported by imported medical supplies and trained staff under joint Israeli-Palestinian oversight.[10]

Transition to Palestinian Authority and Hamas Control (2005â€“2007)

In August 2005, following Israel's unilateral disengagement from the Gaza Strip, Al-Shifa Hospital transitioned to full administrative oversight by the Palestinian Authority's Ministry of Health, marking the end of direct Israeli military presence in Gaza's internal affairs. Operations at the facility maintained continuity in the immediate aftermath, with the hospital continuing to function as Gaza's primary medical center under established PA protocols, handling inpatient care, surgeries, and emergency services without reported structural disruptions tied to the handover itself.[4] The January 2006 Palestinian legislative elections, in which Hamas secured a majority, introduced initial governance tensions but did not immediately alter hospital administration, as a unity government briefly persisted until escalating factional strife culminated in June 2007. During the violent Hamas seizure of Gaza that month, Hamas forces assumed control of Al-Shifa Hospital, including specific sections used for operational purposes, effectively shifting authority from PA-aligned elements to Hamas-led structures. This takeover involved targeted measures against perceived Fatah loyalists, including the dismissal and arrest of medical staff suspected of opposing affiliations; for instance, prominent Al-Shifa surgeon Jumaa al-Saqqa was detained by Hamas paramilitary police in August 2007.[27][28][29] These administrative purges contributed to early workforce instability, with Fatah-leaning health sector employees facing intimidation or removal, prompting protests and work slowdowns in Gaza's public hospitals, including Al-Shifa. The resulting PA-Hamas schism fragmented healthcare governance, as the West Bank-based PA withheld salaries from thousands of Gaza civil servants unwilling to resign, straining hospital staffing and operational funding. Supply chains for essentials like medicines and equipment began showing vulnerabilities due to the internal divide, compounded by tightened border restrictions following the takeover, which hindered imports and maintenance efforts despite international aid efforts.[30][31][32]

Involvement in Gaza Conflicts Pre-2023

Impact of 2007 Fatahâ€“Hamas Conflict

During the Battle of Gaza from June 10 to 15, 2007, Al-Shifa Hospital served as both a treatment center for casualties of the Fatah-Hamas clashes and a site of direct combat, marking an early instance of its dual civilian-military utilization. Hamas fighters positioned themselves inside the facility, prompting Fatah gunmen to launch mortar and rocket-propelled grenade attacks after the killing of a Fatah intelligence officer nearby; Hamas returned fire from within the hospital, resulting in the deaths of one Fatah fighter and one Hamas militant.[33] Such actions by both factions violated international humanitarian law protections for medical sites, endangering patients, staff, and the wounded seeking refuge. The hospital admitted numerous combatants and civilians injured in the fighting, which overall claimed approximately 160 Palestinian lives and wounded hundreds more, straining its resources amid Gaza's limited healthcare infrastructure.[32][34] Reports documented the hospital's role in receiving victims of post-clash abuses, including bodies exhibiting torture marks from Hamas forces targeting Fatah affiliates, though specific executions within Al-Shifa itself were not detailed in primary accounts; broader patterns included summary killings and detentions by Hamas in medical vicinities, as seen at other Gaza hospitals like Kamal Adwan.[32][34] The violence contributed to physical damage from gunfire and explosions around the complex, though less severe than in subsequent conflicts, while overwhelming staff and supplies set a precedent for operational disruptions during intra-Palestinian strife. Human Rights Watch noted that both groups' misuse of hospitals for military purposes hindered access to care and foreshadowed recurring militarization of civilian sites under Hamas governance.[33] Following Hamas's victory and consolidation of power in Gaza by mid-June 2007, the group assumed administrative control over Al-Shifa and other health facilities, dismissing or reassigning hundreds of Fatah-loyal employees from senior positions to install aligned personnel.[30][35] This purge, reported by Palestinian health workers and rival authorities, ensured Hamas dominance over medical supplies, staffing, and operations, integrating the hospital more tightly into its territorial control while exacerbating factional divisions in healthcare delivery. The events entrenched Al-Shifa's pattern of serving both humanitarian needs and militant objectives, a dynamic rooted in the conflict's causal outcome of Hamas's unchallenged rule.[32]

Operations During 2008â€“2009 and 2014 Wars

During Operation Cast Lead from December 27, 2008, to January 18, 2009, Al-Shifa Hospital faced acute operational challenges as Gaza's primary trauma center, overwhelmed by a surge in casualties from the conflict. The facility treated a high volume of wounded Palestinians, with United Nations officials describing "horrific" scenes of injuries, including amputations and burns, reflecting its role in absorbing the majority of severe cases amid limited alternatives due to damaged infrastructure elsewhere.[36] Fuel shortages compounded the strain, with the hospital possessing generator fuel for only three days as of January 7, 2009, threatening blackout of critical equipment like incubators and operating rooms, in a context of pre-existing blockade restrictions on imports.[37] The hospital's dependency on external aid for diesel and medical supplies was evident, as Gaza's health system relied heavily on international donors to sustain basic functions, though delivery was intermittently halted by border closures. In the 2014 Operation Protective Edge, spanning July 8 to August 26, Al-Shifa again operated under extreme pressure, admitting 1,808 live patients after triage between July 21 and August 3 alone, of whom 78 (4.3%) died in-hospital, with most injuries attributed to aerial bombardment.32077-9/fulltext) Overall, the hospital managed approximately 3,500 of the roughly 9,986 injured across Gaza by mid-August, highlighting its central role in handling trauma surges that exceeded routine capacity. Fuel scarcity persisted, affecting generator-dependent wards and contributing to broader system vulnerabilities, while the facility sheltered nearly 2,000 displaced civilians, blurring lines between medical and refuge functions.[38][39] Reports emerged of Hamas utilizing tunnels beneath the complex for cover and weapons storage during this period, potentially complicating operations by embedding military activity near patient areas.[40] These conflicts underscored recurring patterns at Al-Shifa: massive patient influxes straining beds, staff, and triage processes; chronic reliance on international humanitarian aid for essentials like fuel, which blockades and hostilities disrupted; and operational disruptions from nearby combat, including structural damage such as to an external wall in 2014.[41] Empirical records from hospital data indicate high in-patient mortality rates under surge conditions, with limited surge capacity in Gaza's isolated health network amplifying risks of secondary complications like infections from overcrowding.32077-9/fulltext)

Allegations of Military Use by Hamas

Evidence of Tunnels and Command Infrastructure

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) discovered multiple tunnel shafts beneath and adjacent to Al-Shifa Hospital during the November 2023 raid, including one entrance located under a parking lot on the hospital grounds.[7] These shafts connected to an underground network spanning at least 270 meters, featuring reinforced concrete walls, electrical wiring, and ventilation systems linked directly to the hospital's infrastructure.[40] IDF engineers traced the tunnel's power supply through ducts originating from rooftop air-conditioning units powered by the hospital's electricity grid, while separate piping diverted the facility's water supply into the tunnel for sustained operations.[7] Ventilation shafts were similarly integrated, drawing from hospital systems to maintain air flow in the subterranean structure.[42] Video footage released by the IDF on November 19, 2023, depicted the interior of a primary tunnel shaft, approximately 10 meters deep, with blast doors and metal reinforcements indicating fortification for prolonged use.[8] Further explorations revealed adjacent bunkers equipped with living quarters, including bathrooms, a kitchenette, and areas for operational activities, extending roughly 160 meters horizontally beneath the hospital complex.[43] These spaces contained communication equipment, server racks, and hard drives, suggesting their role as a command and control hub rather than mere smuggling passages.[44] Documents and electronic devices recovered from the site, analyzed by IDF intelligence, corroborated Hamas operational logs linking the tunnels to regional command functions.[40] The tunnel network's design, including multiple branching paths and secure entry points, aligned with Hamas's broader underground infrastructure for concealing command activities and personnel movement, as evidenced by the IDF's mapping of over 800 meters of connected passages in the vicinity.[7] Prior intelligence, including Hamas communications intercepted during the 2014 Gaza conflict, indicated similar hospital-adjacent tunnels used for military logistics, though specific admissions regarding Al-Shifa were not publicly detailed at the time.[45] The IDF demolished portions of the network before withdrawing on November 24, 2023, to neutralize its military utility.[40]

Weapons Caches and Hostage Holdings

Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) personnel discovered caches of weapons and military equipment during inspections of Al-Shifa Hospital, including rifles, grenades, ammunition, and combat gear concealed in various locations such as patient wards and medical storage areas.[6] [46] Videos released by the IDF on November 15, 2023, depicted these items, with serial numbers on some firearms linked to Hamas stockpiles by Israeli intelligence assessments.[47] The quantities and placement suggested organized storage rather than incidental presence, as evidenced by sealed boxes and hidden compartments integrated into hospital infrastructure.[48] Evidence of hostage holding emerged from security footage and interrogations of captured militants. On November 19, 2023, the IDF published closed-circuit television (CCTV) recordings from October 7, 2023, showing Hamas gunmen forcibly transporting at least two hostages through the hospital's foyer, surrounded by armed fighters.[49] [50] One individual was later identified as Elad Katzir, an Israeli hostage killed by his captors while in custody.[51] Interrogations of detained Hamas operatives confirmed that captives were held in secure areas beneath the facility, including basement levels repurposed for confinement, contradicting assertions of mere transient sheltering.[52] Forensic examination of the sites revealed traces consistent with prolonged occupancy, such as personal effects and restraint implements.[53]

Hamas Denials and Counterclaims

Hamas officials, including spokesman Izzat al-Risheq, issued categorical denials of any military utilization of Al-Shifa Hospital, asserting that claims of underground command structures or operational bases lacked any factual basis.[54][55] These statements positioned the facility exclusively as a civilian medical center, with Hamas warning that Israeli allegations foreshadowed unjustified attacks on protected infrastructure.[54] In response to discoveries of weapons caches and tunnel entrances during the November 2023 raid, Hamas counterclaimed that all evidence was fabricated or staged by Israeli forces post-entry, labeling the findings as props planted to rationalize the operation's civilian toll.[6] Gaza's government media office, operated under Hamas administration, echoed this by accusing Israel of disseminating falsehoods about military equipment to obscure destruction of the health sector.[56] Such assertions dismissed visual documentation, including timestamped videos of arms and infrastructure, as manipulated despite metadata indicating pre-raid origins verifiable through forensic analysis.[6] Regarding tunnel networks, Hamas maintained that any subterranean features beneath or adjacent to the hospital served solely civilian purposes, such as emergency shelters or maintenance access, rejecting characterizations of military-grade reinforcements like blast doors and ventilation systems suited for prolonged combat operations.[45] These counterclaims conflicted with assessments from engineering experts highlighting construction techniquesâ€”such as reinforced concrete linings and electrical cabling inconsistent with shelter utilityâ€”aligned with Hamas's broader tunnel network for offensive capabilities, as corroborated by U.S. intelligence independently confirming a command node exploiting hospital premises.[57][7] Hamas amplified these narratives through affiliated media outlets, framing the hospital solely as a victim of disproportionate aggression and inflating reports of civilian casualties during raids without supporting forensic data, such as autopsies distinguishing combatants from non-combatants.[58] This approach persisted despite empirical discrepancies, including pre-raid intelligence from multiple agencies documenting systematic militarization, underscoring a pattern where Hamas denials prioritized narrative control over alignment with observable infrastructure and logistical traces.[59]

Israeli Military Operations (2023â€“2024)

November 2023 Raid: Operations and Findings

On November 15, 2023, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) encircled Al-Shifa Hospital in Gaza City following intelligence indicating Hamas military use of the site, issuing prior warnings via leaflets, calls, and announcements for civilians, patients, and staff to evacuate southward along designated routes to reduce risks to non-combatants.[60] Ground troops, including tanks and infantry, conducted a limited entry into the complex, prioritizing searches of suspected areas while Arabic-speaking personnel coordinated with hospital staff and delivered humanitarian aid such as incubators, infant formula, and medical equipment to support remaining patients.[60] No direct engagements with civilians or medical personnel occurred inside the buildings during initial sweeps, though IDF forces reported confrontations with Hamas fighters positioned nearby and firing from within the hospital grounds.[60][61] Searches uncovered weapons caches in key structures, including automatic rifles, grenades, ammunition, flak jackets, and combat gear stored in the MRI building, alongside a Hamas operational command center equipped with computers and other technological assets.[62][6] On November 19, engineering units exposed a fortified tunnel entrance beneath the hospital complex's runway area, measuring 55 meters in length and 10 meters deep, featuring a blast-proof door, firing ports, electrical wiring, and ventilationâ€”consistent with Hamas underground infrastructureâ€”nearby weapons such as RPGs, explosives, and Kalashnikov rifles were also seized from an adjacent vehicle.[63] The IDF stated these discoveries validated prior assessments of the site's dual use for medical and military purposes.[62] Over the course of the raid, which lasted until approximately November 23, IDF operations neutralized around 200 Hamas gunmen in and around the hospital vicinity, with no reported civilian casualties attributed to ground forces inside the facility.[64] More than 300 patients and staff were evacuated, including 31 premature infants transferred by a WHO-led UN and Red Crescent convoy on November 19, amid ongoing reports of Hamas militants using the premises for firing positions.[65][66]

Marchâ€“April 2024 Raid: Operations and Findings

On March 18, 2024, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) units re-entered the Al-Shifa Hospital complex in Gaza City following intelligence indicating that Hamas operatives had returned to the site and were using it as a base for regrouping and launching attacks.[67] [68] The operation involved ground troops supported by drones and armored vehicles, with initial exchanges of fire reported from within hospital buildings; IDF-released drone footage captured militants firing from specific structures, prompting targeted responses.[69] [70] Prior to entry, the IDF issued evacuation warnings to civilians and medical personnel via announcements and leaflets, directing them to safer areas south of the complex.[70] [71] The two-week operation, which concluded with IDF withdrawal on April 1, 2024, resulted in the elimination of approximately 200 Hamas and allied militants within the hospital grounds, according to IDF statements.[72] [73] Troops also detained over 900 suspected militants, including more than 500 affiliated with Hamas or Palestinian Islamic Jihad, during searches of the premises.[73] Precision tactics were employed, focusing on buildings from which fire originated, with IDF reports emphasizing efforts to minimize damage to non-combatant areas through directed strikes and controlled demolitions of identified threat sites.[67] [69] Searches uncovered multiple weapons caches hidden throughout the facility, including rifles, grenades, and explosive devices concealed in locations such as the MRI center, bathrooms, and maternity ward.[74] [75] These discoveries, documented via IDF photographs and videos, indicated ongoing militant exploitation of the site post the prior November operation, with arms stored in proximity to patient areas.[74] No extensive new tunnel networks were publicly detailed from this raid, though prior subterranean infrastructure linked to the hospital was noted in operational contexts.[40]

Justifications and Precautions Taken

Israel justified targeting Al-Shifa Hospital during its 2023â€“2024 operations on intelligence assessments that Hamas operated a subterranean command center there, constituting military use of a protected medical facility and forfeiting its immunity under international humanitarian law.[76] Article 19 of the Fourth Geneva Convention provides that hospitals lose special protection if used for "acts harmful to the enemy," such as hosting command infrastructure, which Israel asserted applied due to Hamas's integration of tunnels, weapon storage, and operational hubs beneath the site.[77] This rationale aligns with the principle of distinction in Additional Protocol I, requiring attacks only on military objectives while assessing proportionality by weighing anticipated military advantage against potential civilian harm.[78] To mitigate risks to civilians and maintain proportionality, Israeli forces issued multiple advance warnings via phone calls, leaflets, and public announcements directing evacuation from the hospital complex, facilitating the departure of non-combatants including patients and staff.[77] Humanitarian corridors were designated to allow safe passage, and IDF units incorporated field medical teams to treat and evacuate the wounded, with records indicating support for over 200 evacuees coordinated with the Palestine Red Crescent Society in the November 2023 phase.[79] These measures reflect established IDF protocols for operations near protected sites, prioritizing verification of military use while enabling humanitarian access where feasible.[76] In contrast, Hamas's embedding of military assets in Al-Shifa exemplifies prohibited human shielding under Article 51(7) of Additional Protocol I, leveraging civilian presence to shield combatants and infrastructure, as corroborated by interrogations of captured operatives and documents from Hamas sites detailing such tactics across Gaza facilities.[80] This practice, which incentivizes Hamas to maximize civilian exposure for propaganda leverage, directly contravenes Israel's obligation to target only verified military objectives and underscores the causal role of militant exploitation in complicating urban operations.[81]

Post-Raid Aftermath and Controversies

Physical Damage and Reconstruction Efforts

The Israeli Defense Forces' raids in November 2023 and Marchâ€“April 2024 resulted in severe structural damage to Al-Shifa Hospital, with most surface buildings extensively destroyed or rendered unusable. A World Health Organization-led multi-agency mission on April 5, 2024, documented widespread devastation from explosives, fire, and heavy weaponry, including the collapse of walls in the emergency department and neonatal intensive care unit (NICU), burning of 115 emergency beds, and destruction of 14 NICU incubators along with the oxygen production plant.[1] The assessment concluded that the facility was completely non-functional, lacking capacity for CT scans, laboratory operations, or basic medical services, and requiring clearance of unexploded ordnance before any safe re-entry.[1] Damage was concentrated in areas the IDF identified as hosting Hamas military infrastructure, such as weapon storage and operational hubs, while underground tunnelsâ€”spanning over 250 meters in the November raidâ€”were demolished without direct surface disruption in some cases, though adjacent structures suffered secondary effects from demolition activities.[82] Post-withdrawal inspections by BBC reporters in April 2024 revealed charred main buildings with large sections of walls missing, burned wards, and surrounding grounds bulldozed, leaving the complex and nearby neighborhood largely flattened.[83] Reconstruction efforts have been severely limited as of late 2024, with the hospital remaining inoperable due to persistent security threats, structural instability, and the need for comprehensive engineering evaluations.[1] Gazan authorities under Hamas control have initiated no large-scale repairs, hampered by diversion of humanitarian aid materials toward military purposes, as documented in broader Gaza assessments.[84] International proposals for Gaza-wide healthcare rebuilding, including from U.S. plans, have emphasized conditions such as Hamas demilitarization and oversight to prevent reuse of sites for non-medical activities, though no dedicated Al-Shifa projects have advanced.[85] Minimal partial functionality in isolated units was reported by September 2024, but overall restoration remains implausible without demilitarization and sustained aid flows.[1]

Mass Graves Claims and Investigations

Following the Israeli Defense Forces' (IDF) withdrawal from Al-Shifa Hospital on April 1, 2024, after a two-week raid beginning March 18, Palestinian health officials and civil defense teams reported discovering shallow mass graves on the hospital grounds, primarily in the courtyard and surrounding areas. These included at least two graves containing approximately 30 bodies, with claims of additional burials nearby, though exact numbers for Al-Shifa remain lower than those reported at Nasser Hospital in Khan Younis. Exhumations began on April 9, 2024, revealing bodies described as decomposed, some with hands bound behind their backs, blindfolds, and gunshot wounds to the head suggestive of close-range executions. Gaza's Hamas-controlled Civil Defense attributed these to Israeli forces, alleging victims were patients, staff, or displaced civilians executed and hastily buried during the raid, with some reportedly buried alive or stripped naked.[86][87] The IDF categorically denied involvement in any burials or executions at Al-Shifa, stating that troops did not dig graves or inter bodies during the operation, which focused on targeting Hamas militants while providing aid corridors and evacuating patients under coordination with international observers. Israeli officials asserted that the graves pre-dated the March-April raid, likely originating from makeshift burials amid the hospital's collapse during prior fighting, including the November 2023 siege when the facility was overwhelmed by casualties and Hamas operations. No video or photographic evidence from IDF sources shows soldiers burying bodies, contrasting with extensive footage of other raid activities. Hamas-affiliated sources provided no timestamps, autopsies, or chain-of-custody documentation for the remains, limiting verifiability; independent access for forensic experts was restricted by ongoing hostilities and Gaza authorities.[88][89] Alternative explanations point to Hamas' documented pattern of executing suspected collaborators, often by binding, blindfolding, and shooting at close range, a method consistent with the described wounds and which intensified during the conflict. Historical reports, including from Amnesty International, detail Hamas abductions and killings of Palestinians accused of aiding Israel, with bodies disposed in shallow pits during wartime chaos to avoid scrutiny. Recent footage from Gaza, including near Al-Shifa, shows public executions of alleged spies by Hamas units in 2024-2025, mirroring the grave findings. Gaza's health ministry, operated by Hamas, has a record of unsubstantiated atrocity claims, such as prior exaggerations of hospital casualties without evidence, undermining source credibility absent third-party forensics. Limited media observations post-withdrawal noted hasty, unmarked burials likely from infighting or summary justice amid Hamas' control of the area pre-raid, rather than systematic IDF action. Calls for ICC and UN investigations persist, but as of October 2025, no conclusive forensic reports attribute responsibility to Israel, with examinations hampered by site disturbance during exhumations.[90][91][92]

Humanitarian Impact and Ethical Debates

The Israeli military operations at Al-Shifa Hospital in November 2023 and Marchâ€“April 2024 resulted in severe humanitarian challenges, including patient deaths attributed to crossfire, fuel shortages, and disrupted medical services amid the siege and raids. Gaza's Ministry of Health reported burying 179 bodies from the hospital on November 15, 2023, during the initial encirclement, though verification was limited due to the chaotic conditions controlled by Hamas. These impacts stemmed primarily from Hamas's documented embedding of military infrastructure, including command centers and tunnels, within the hospital complex, which transformed a civilian medical facility into a contested military site and precluded unimpeded humanitarian access.[79][93][7] Ethical debates centered on the balance between Israel's right to self-defense against Hamas's use of protected sites and the imperative to minimize civilian harm under international humanitarian law. Hospitals retain protection unless exploited for "acts harmful to the enemy," such as harboring fighters or command operations, a threshold met at Al-Shifa per U.S. intelligence assessments confirming Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad's operational use of the facility. Critics, including some UN officials, argued the raids disproportionately endangered patients despite Israeli precautions like evacuation warnings via leaflets and calls, which hospital administrators under Hamas influence rejected, citing inability to relocate the critically ill. Proponents of the operations emphasized that Hamas's deliberate strategy of dual-use violationsâ€”placing military assets in civilian infrastructureâ€”forces attackers into dilemmas where inaction perpetuates threats, while targeted raids with precision munitions and post-operation aid provisions aimed to limit collateral damage.02632-6/fulltext)[93][94] Long-term repercussions exacerbated Gaza's healthcare collapse, with Al-Shifa rendered largely inoperable after the raids, its buildings extensively damaged and equipment destroyed, contributing to the shutdown of most northern facilities by early 2024. This strain was compounded by Hamas's verified diversion of humanitarian aid, including commandeering supplies and tracking aid workers for exploitation, which impeded reconstruction and sustained medical operations across the Strip. While overall aid theft claims remain contested, evidence from leaked documents and militant activities underscores how such tactics prolonged shortages, prioritizing military sustainment over civilian welfare in a system already overburdened by the group's governance.[1][80][95]
