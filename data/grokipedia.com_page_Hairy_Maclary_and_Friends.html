Hairy Maclary and Friends is a series of children's picture books created by New Zealand author and illustrator Dame Lynley Dodd. The popular series has sold over 10 million copies worldwide as of 2019.[1] The character Hairy Maclary made his first appearance in 1983 in the book titled Hairy Maclary from Donaldson's Dairy.[2] He is the protagonist in twelve books in the series, and there are a further nine books about his friends.[2] Hairy Maclary's adventures are usually in the company of his other dog friends who include an English mastiff named Hercules Morse, a dalmatian named Bottomley Potts, an old English sheepdog named Muffin McLay, a greyhound named Bitzer Maloney and a dachshund named Schnitzel Von Krumm. The series also features a belligerent soft grey tabby named Scarface Claw, their formidable opponent, a black cat named Slinky Malinki and a duckling named Zachary Quack.[3] According to the books' website, Hairy Maclary is "a small dog of mixed pedigree".[4] Dodd has described him as a mix of terriers that she knows,[5] "an animated bottlebrush"[5] and "a caricature rather than a real dog".[6] However, Hairy Maclary does strike some resemblance to a Scottish terrier.

Hairy Maclary

Hairy Maclary books are designed to be read by an adult to a child. The plots are simple, in keeping with the comprehension level of the age group for which they are written. They generally involve Hairy and his friends in adventurous scenarios pitched against local cats. The animals in this series are not given human thoughts and motives;[6] their actions tell the stories, and reflect their animal natures. Each double-sided page has an illustration on one side and text on the other. The pictures and the written words together tell the story, and the illustrations and their meanings are as important as the text. Dodd emphasized the importance of text and illustrations forming an integrated partnership in picture books, where each enhances the other.[5][6] The books have a wide format that enables a child seated beside an adult to have a full view of the picture page while the adult reads. The text is written in rhythmic verse with simple rhymes like "Bottomley Potts covered in spots, Hercules Morse as big as a horse". Characters, events and language are repetitive and cumulative, in the manner of "Old MacDonald had a farm". Each book contains a twist or some sort of conclusion at the end.[5] Although the books are designed to entertain young children, they are not intended as "early readers". The Hairy Maclary books, despite their simple stories, introduce the listening child to some long but very expressive words, which are not part of the average pre-schooler's vocabulary but must be understood by the child in the context in which they occur. For example, the noise made by a stranded cat and the excited dogs who discover it is described as a "cacophony". Dodd enjoys using unusual words in the books. Criticised once by a reviewer for using the word 'bellicose' about Hairy Maclary, Dodd replied that she was 'unrepentant', saying "if we spend our time writing only language appropriate to the age of the reader, they will never learn anything new. How boring!"[6] Lynley Dodd's illustrations are closely observed from life.[5] The breeds of dogs, the types of houses and the plants growing in each garden can generally be identified. In the first book of the series the repeated lines "...and Hairy Maclary from Donaldson's Dairy" accompany illustrations of Hairy Maclary and his friends during their walk, including sniffing at the bottom of a lamppost, burrowing into a hedge, and barking at some birds, before they encounter Scarface Claw.[5]

Adaptations and legacy

In the 1990s, a television series featuring ten five-minute episodes based on the series premiered. The series has been adapted into stage productions, including shows at the Edinburgh Fringe Festival, Sydney Opera House, and a 2025 performance by the Auckland Philharmonia.[7] In 2015, a sculpture of Hairy Maclary and other characters from the books was officially unveiled on the waterfront in Tauranga (the home of Lynley Dodd) by former prime minister John Key.[8][9] In the 2019 TVNZ series Goodnight Kiwi, prime minister Jacinda Ardern read Hairy Maclary from Donaldson's Dairy; the episode aired on TVNZ 2 on Christmas Day.[10][11] Foreign language editions of the Hairy Maclary books have been published in Sweden, Japan, Slovenia, Russia, Korea and China.[5]
