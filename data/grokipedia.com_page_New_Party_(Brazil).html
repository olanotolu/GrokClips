New Party (Brazil)


The New Party (Partido Novo; NOVO) is a Brazilian political party founded in 2011 by citizens with clean records and no prior involvement in politics, who mobilized to address systemic inefficiencies through principled action, and was officially registered by the Superior Electoral Court in 2015.[1]
The party emphasizes core principles such as meritocracy, anti-corruption measures, elimination of undue privileges, and a long-term vision for reforming the state to foster greater individual opportunities and economic freedom.[1]
Notable for its focus on selecting candidates based on competence rather than political connections, NOVO has secured key electoral victories, including the governorship of Minas Gerais in 2018 with Romeu Zema, re-elected in 2022, along with federal deputies, state legislators, and local officials in multiple elections from 2016 onward, while consistently opposing clientelist practices and advocating fiscal responsibility.[1]

Ideology and Principles

Economic Policies

The New Party endorses classical liberal economic principles, emphasizing free markets, fiscal discipline, and a reduced role for the state to address Brazil's chronic inefficiencies in public spending and resource allocation. It argues that excessive government intervention, including through state-owned enterprises and redistributive programs, has exacerbated fiscal imbalances, with Brazil's gross public debt forecasted to peak at approximately 98% of GDP in 2025.[2] The party prioritizes private sector initiative and merit-based incentives as drivers of sustainable growth, contrasting these with the inefficiencies observed in entities like Petrobras, which have incurred multibillion-real losses amid political interference and mismanagement, such as over R$7 billion in negative saldo during recent left-leaning administrations.[3] Central to its platform is the advocacy for widespread privatization of state assets to eliminate subsidies, curb corruption risks, and improve operational efficiency. Party figures, including 2018 presidential candidate JoÃ£o AmoÃªdo, have explicitly called for privatizing all public banks except the National Development Bank (BNDES), viewing such measures as essential to dismantling monopolistic structures that distort competition and burden taxpayers.[4] [5] This stance extends to opposing expansions in welfare programs and targeted subsidies, which the party contends perpetuate dependency and fiscal deficits without addressing root causes of poverty, such as regulatory barriers to entrepreneurship. Deregulation and tax simplification form another pillar, aimed at streamlining bureaucracy to foster innovation and attract investment. The party has backed initiatives like the Economic Freedom Act, intended to reduce administrative hurdles and promote open markets in sectors like aviation and services.[6] By limiting state overreach, these policies seek to replace redistributive approachesâ€”often linked to prior leftist governmentsâ€”with meritocratic systems that reward productivity, grounded in evidence of state-led models' failure to deliver broad prosperity amid Brazil's entrenched public sector bloat.[3]

Governance and Anti-Corruption Stance

The New Party (Partido Novo) advocates for institutional reforms emphasizing meritocracy in the public sector, proposing the replacement of tenure-based job security with performance evaluations linked to clear metrics for promotions, dismissals, and incentives. This stance supports the 2021 approval of Constitutional Amendment Proposal (PEC) 32/20, which aimed to modernize public administration by introducing flexibility in hiring, reducing privileges like lifetime stability for new entrants, and prioritizing results-oriented management to address Brazil's inefficient bureaucracy, where personnel costs consume approximately 13% of GDPâ€”among the highest globally for emerging economies.[7][8] In anti-corruption efforts, the party has introduced legislative packages targeting impunity and systemic graft, including measures to hold political parties accountable for crimes committed by their affiliates, thereby disrupting clientelist networks that enable corruption. Specific initiatives include proposals for enhanced transparency in public procurement and social benefits, such as real-time tracking of pension fraud in the National Social Security Institute (INSS) and mandatory disclosures to curb irregularities estimated at billions of reais annually. The party critiques judicial and political privileges, like forum privileges that delay prosecutions, as enablers of entrenched corruption, advocating for streamlined processes and stricter penalties to enforce accountability without reliance on politically influenced institutions.[9][10][11] On campaign finance, Novo rejects corporate and public funding, refusing allocations from the Electoral Fund (Fundo Eleitoral)â€”which totaled R$4.9 billion in 2022â€”and has challenged its expansions in court, arguing that taxpayer-funded campaigns perpetuate dependency and opacity, echoing pre-2015 corporate donation scandals. Instead, the party promotes self-financing through private individual contributions with full disclosure requirements, positioning this as a first-principles antidote to quid pro quo arrangements in a system where public resources often subsidize entrenched interests. This approach aligns with broader demands for asset declarations by officials to preempt conflicts, fostering a governance model rooted in verifiable integrity over discretionary power.[12][13][14]

Social and Individual Liberties

The New Party (Partido Novo) advocates for expansive individual liberties grounded in personal responsibility, opposing expansive state intervention in private life. The party emphasizes self-reliance over paternalistic policies, permitting members flexibility on bioethical matters such as abortion and drug decriminalization while prioritizing protections for core freedoms like self-defense and expression.[1] This stance aligns with the party's classical liberal framework, which critiques government overreach as undermining autonomy amid Brazil's challenges, including elevated violent crime rates exceeding 20 homicides per 100,000 inhabitants annually in recent years.[15] In education, the party supports school choice mechanisms, including voucher systems that enable parents to direct public funds toward preferred providers, including private institutions, to foster competition and elevate standards. Proponents argue this counters Brazil's poor performance in international assessments like PISA, where the country ranks near the bottom in reading, math, and scienceâ€”scores lagging behind voucher-implementing nations such as Chile, which saw modest gains in enrollment equity and test outcomes post-reform.[16] The approach seeks to diminish teachers' union dominance, viewed as a barrier to merit-based reforms, by tying funding to performance rather than enrollment rigidity, thereby incentivizing innovation without mandating homeschooling, though party affiliates have endorsed its legalization for familial discretion.[17] On self-defense, Partido Novo defends easing restrictions on civilian firearm possession, framing it as an extension of individual rights against disarmament policies that disproportionately disadvantage law-abiding citizens in high-crime contexts. Candidate JoÃ£o AmoÃªdo articulated this as rooted in self-protection principles, rejecting blanket bans given empirical links between legal ownership and reduced victimization in comparative studies, while cautioning it complementsâ€”not substitutesâ€”broader security enhancements.[18] Similarly, the party champions unrestricted free speech, vehemently opposing judicial overreach and legislative threats like the "PL da Censura" (PL 2630), which it deems erosive to open discourse; members have filed measures classifying censorship as abuse of authority, citing cases where Supreme Federal Court actions suppressed content absent criminal thresholds.[19][20] Regarding criminal justice, the party favors reforms prioritizing deterrence through stringent penalties for heinous acts, such as elevating elderly fraud to hediondo status, over rehabilitative leniency amid persistent impunityâ€”evidenced by clearance rates below 10% for homicides in major states. It endorses private security alternatives and independent federal policing to bolster efficacy, critiquing expansions that dilute accountability, as in opposition to certain constitutional amendments perceived as softening enforcement.[21][22] This reflects a causal view that robust individual agency, including minimal behavioral regulations absent harm to others, outperforms state-centric models in curbing disorder.[15]

In the 2018 Brazilian presidential election, the New Party fielded JoÃ£o AmoÃªdo, a banker and one of the party's founders, as its candidate. Romeu Zema, the New Party's candidate, won the 2018 Minas Gerais gubernatorial election in the second round, defeating incumbent Fernando Pimentel with a decisive margin that reflected voter demand for fiscal discipline amid the state's inherited debt crisis.[60] Zema was reelected in 2022, securing 56% of valid votes in the first round without needing a runoff, underscoring sustained support for his reform agenda in Brazil's second-most populous state.[61] Under Zema's governance since January 2019, Minas Gerais has achieved fiscal surpluses, including R$2.2 billion in 2022â€”the first in decadesâ€”while maintaining zero primary deficit annually from 2021 onward, even after repaying over R$8 billion in legacy debts.[62] [63] These outcomes stemmed from spending cuts, privatization pushes like the proposed sale of state utility Cemig shares, and debt renegotiation efforts that aimed to lower servicing costs from inflation-plus-4% to inflation-plus-1%.[64] Empirical indicators include S&P Global Ratings' upgrade of the state's long-term rating to 'B-' from 'CCC+' in October 2025, citing improved liquidity and budgetary performance.[65] Beyond Minas Gerais, the New Party has limited direct gubernatorial wins, holding no other state governorships as of 2025, though it has exerted influence through selective alliances with center-right coalitions to amplify liberal economic policies in state assemblies.[59] This focused strategy prioritizes quality governance over broad expansion, yielding measurable fiscal stabilization in its sole stronghold amid Brazil's fragmented state-level politics.

Influence on Brazilian Politics

The New Party (Partido Novo) has contributed to Brazil's political discourse by emerging as a principled alternative amid the 2010s anti-corruption surge, aligning its founding ethos of ethical governance and rejection of clientelism with broader public demands for accountability that fueled movements like Operation Car Wash (Lava Jato). Established in 2011 by civil society actors disillusioned with entrenched corruption, the party provided an institutional framework for demands that legacy parties had long ignored, emphasizing candidate vetting and voluntary funding to sidestep state capture.[120] This stance resonated in a context where scandals eroded trust in traditional establishments, positioning Novo as a catalyst for renewed scrutiny of political practices without relying on populist appeals.[59] In the polarized landscape dominated by the Workers' Party (PT)'s statist policies and Jair Bolsonaro's nationalist populism, Novo has carved out space for classical liberalism, advocating market-oriented reforms and individual liberties as counterpoints to both collectivist interventionism and authoritarian tendencies. Its 2025 National Meeting in SÃ£o Paulo, attended by over 2,000 members, underscored this influence by energizing youth engagement with liberal principles, fostering a generational shift toward ideas of limited government and entrepreneurship that challenge the ideological binaries of recent decades.[38] By maintaining operational independenceâ€”such as renouncing electoral funds in 2022 and launching a transparency portal in 2023â€”Novo exerts pressure on established parties to adopt similar standards, indirectly elevating demands for fiscal responsibility and anti-corruption measures across the spectrum.[121][122] Empirically, Novo's persistence in a fragmented multi-party system has normalized critiques of state overreach and inefficiency, with free-market arguments gaining echoes in opposition rhetoric and policy debates, as seen in broader acceptance of privatization discussions post-2018 elections. This intellectual legacy manifests in cross-aisle acknowledgments of liberal economics' role in addressing Brazil's chronic fiscal imbalances, though diluted by pragmatic alliances, demonstrating the party's outsized discursive impact relative to its electoral size.[59]
