2023 Henryetta killings

The 2023 Henryetta killings were a mass murder-suicide perpetrated by Jesse McFadden, a convicted rapist, who on or around May 1, 2023, fatally shot six individuals—his wife Holly Guess and five teenagers—before killing himself at a rental property near Henryetta in Okmulgee County, Oklahoma.[1][2] The victims included Guess's three children, Rylee Elizabeth Allen (17), Michael James Mayo (15), and Tiffany Dore Guess (13), as well as two teenage friends of the family, Ivy Webster (14) and Brittany Brewer (15); all suffered multiple gunshot wounds to the head, with autopsies confirming the manner of death as homicide for the six and suicide for McFadden.[1][3] The killings occurred during what began as a sleepover gathering at the property between April 29 and May 1, 2023, after which Webster and Brewer were reported missing, prompting a search that uncovered the bodies in a nearby ravine; the Oklahoma State Bureau of Investigation (OSBI) later confirmed McFadden as the sole perpetrator based on ballistic evidence and scene analysis.[1][4] McFadden, who had been convicted of forcible rape in 2003 and sentenced to 20 years but released early in October 2020 after serving about 17 years, was facing active charges for child pornography and related offenses at the time, including possession of explicit images of minors, which may have factored into his actions amid fears of re-incarceration.[1][2] Post-incident investigations revealed additional details, including evidence of sexual assault on Webster and Brewer, and over 30 cell phones linked to the scene, underscoring McFadden's ongoing predatory behavior despite his registered sex offender status and prior supervision.[1] The case drew scrutiny over systemic failures in monitoring high-risk offenders, as McFadden's delayed trial and early parole release—enabled by Oklahoma's 85% rule for certain sentences—allowed him unsupervised access to minors, prompting victims' families to advocate for reforms like "Knights Law," which seeks stricter sentencing and tracking for sex crimes against children.[1][3] While local authorities closed the case as a murder-suicide without further prosecutions, the event highlighted empirical patterns of recidivism among violent sex offenders, with families criticizing lenient policies that prioritized release over public safety.[4][5]

Background

Perpetrator's Criminal History

Jesse McFadden began his documented criminal activity in Oklahoma as early as December 2002, while residing in McAlester.[6] In 2003, he was convicted of first-degree rape for assaulting a 16-year-old girl, Krystle Strong, receiving a 20-year prison sentence of which he served nearly 17 years before release around 2020.[7] [8] Upon parole, McFadden registered as a sex offender as required by state law, but he concealed his full criminal past from his wife, Holly, and her family, who only learned of it after the killings.[9] [10] Following his release, McFadden faced new felony charges in 2022 for possession of child pornography and child sexual exploitation material, offenses allegedly committed after his parole.[6] [1] He was released on bond despite these pending sex crime charges and was scheduled for a jury trial on May 1, 2023—the same day as the killings—but failed to appear.[11] No prior arrests beyond the 2003 rape conviction are publicly detailed in court records or investigations prior to his release.[12]

Victims and Family Relationships

The victims of the 2023 Henryetta killings included Jesse McFadden's wife, Holly Guess, aged 35, who was the mother of the three minor victims present at the property.[13][14] McFadden, her husband since their marriage in 2018, acted as stepfather to Guess's three children from prior relationships: Michael Mayo, 15; Rylee Allen, 13; and Tiffany Guess, 13.[15][1] In addition to the immediate family, two teenage girls were present as sleepover guests, invited by Guess's daughters for the night of April 30, 2023: Ivy Webster, 14, a friend of Rylee Allen, and Brittany Brewer, 15, a friend of one of the Guess children.[16][17] These guests had no direct familial ties to McFadden or Guess but were connected through the children's social circle at Henryetta High School and local activities.[18] Holly Guess's family extended beyond the immediate victims; her mother, Janette Mayo, later spoke publicly about the loss of her daughter and grandchildren, highlighting the broader familial devastation.[19] The children's biological fathers were not residing at the property and survived the incident, with one, the father of Ivy Webster, actively advocating for legislative changes in response.[20] No evidence indicates McFadden had biological children with Guess or prior familial connections to the sleepover guests.[21]

The Killings

Sequence of Events

On April 30, 2023, Jesse McFadden, a convicted sex offender, picked up 15-year-old Brittany Brewer from school around noon in Henryetta, Oklahoma, as part of arrangements for a sleepover involving Brewer, 14-year-old Ivy Webster, and McFadden's fiancée Holly Guess's three teenage children: 17-year-old Rylee Allen, 15-year-old Michael Mayo, and 13-year-old Tiffany Guess.[1] The group had planned activities including a trip to a nearby mall, and Webster sent a Snapchat to her mother that evening indicating she was at the residence.[1] The murders occurred sometime overnight between April 30 and May 1, 2023, at the rental property where the group was gathered. McFadden fatally shot all six victims in the head, with each receiving between one and three gunshot wounds; Ivy Webster and Brittany Brewer also sustained blunt force injuries consistent with sexual assault prior to their deaths.[1] [22] McFadden then died by suicide from a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the head.[4] The specific order of the killings remains undetermined based on available public evidence.[1]

Discovery and Initial Response

On May 1, 2023, Okmulgee County authorities, searching for two missing teenage girls—Ivy Webster, 14, and Brittany Brewer, 16—discovered seven bodies on a rural property near Henryetta, Oklahoma, owned by resident Jesse McFadden.[2][23] The search was prompted by the girls' failure to return from a sleepover at the McFadden home and McFadden's absence from a scheduled court appearance that day.[23] Three bodies were found approximately 150 yards apart and a quarter-mile from the residence, while the remaining four were located together about 450 yards farther away.[23] Okmulgee County Sheriff Eddy Rice immediately confirmed the deaths and identified the victims as McFadden, his wife Holly McFadden, her three children (Rylee Allen, Tiffany Guess, and Michael Mayo), and the two missing teens.[2][23] Rice noted McFadden was a convicted rapist and sex offender but withheld details on causes of death pending autopsy results from the state medical examiner.[2] No arrests were made, as initial evidence pointed to McFadden as the sole perpetrator in a murder-suicide.[23] By May 3, 2023, investigators, including Okmulgee Police Chief Joe Prentice, publicly stated that all victims had sustained one to three gunshot wounds to the head from a 9mm handgun legally purchased by Holly McFadden in January 2022, and that McFadden had fatally shot himself.[23] Prentice emphasized that the evidence supported McFadden murdering the six others before his suicide, though no motive was established at that stage.[23] The scene was secured, and the Oklahoma State Bureau of Investigation later assisted with forensics, but the case was ruled closed as a murder-suicide with no further suspects sought.[3]

Investigation and Evidence

Forensic Findings

The autopsies conducted by the Oklahoma Office of the Chief Medical Examiner determined that all six homicide victims—Holly McFadden, Ivy Webster, Brittany Brewer, Tiffany Guess, Rylee Allen, and Michael Mayo—died from gunshot wounds to the head, with the manner of death ruled as homicide in each case.[24][22] The perpetrator, Jesse McFadden, died from a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the mouth, classified as suicide.[24][22] Forensic examination revealed additional injuries on two female victims, Ivy Webster (age 14) and Brittany Brewer (age 15), including blunt force trauma to the genital regions consistent with sexual assault prior to their deaths.[24][22] Other victims, such as Holly McFadden, exhibited abrasions on the arms and legs alongside the fatal head wounds, suggesting possible restraint or struggle, though no such injuries were detailed for all cases.[24] Ballistic evidence recovered from the scene, including spent casings and the weapon—a handgun—linked the headshots to McFadden's firearm, corroborating the sequence of execution-style killings followed by his suicide.[22] Toxicology reports for select victims, such as Holly McFadden and Tiffany Guess, showed no significant substances that would have impaired their awareness during the events.[25] The bodies were discovered in a metal shed on McFadden's property, bound with cables and locks he had purchased days prior, indicating premeditation supported by physical evidence of staging.[26]

Staged Crime Scene and Motive Analysis

Investigators determined that the crime scene had been manipulated by McFadden to obscure the sequence of events. Okmulgee Police Chief Joe Prentice stated that the arrangement of the bodies suggested staging, specifically noting that "the bodies had been moved from where they were originally killed," based on inconsistencies observed during the initial survey of the property.[27] This assessment was supported by the spatial distribution of remains: three bodies discovered approximately 150 yards apart and a quarter-mile from the residence, with four others found 450 yards farther, indicating post-mortem relocation rather than random placement.[23] All victims, including McFadden, had been shot multiple times in the head with a 9mm handgun legally purchased by his wife in January 2022, and the absence of defensive wounds or evidence of a struggle among victims further aligned with a controlled, perpetrator-orchestrated setup.[23] Forensic analysis reinforced the staging conclusion, as the lack of a living suspect limited deeper ballistic or trace evidence pursuits, but the deliberate positioning delayed immediate recognition of the murder-suicide dynamic. Prentice emphasized following physical evidence, which pointed unequivocally to McFadden as the sole actor, with no indications of external involvement.[27] The staging likely aimed to confuse timelines or deter prompt identification, buying time amid McFadden's impending court appearance on May 1, 2023, for child sexual abuse charges stemming from prior convictions.[23] Analysis of motive remains inferential, drawn primarily from McFadden's communications and legal context, as authorities reported no explicit confession or documentation of intent. Search warrants revealed text messages sent to his mother on April 30, 2023, in which McFadden stated he would not return to prison and had resolved to end his life, implying a premeditated extension to eliminate witnesses or dependents ahead of his jury trial.[28] Cell phone data corroborated this timeline, with his wife's device pinging active on the property at 12:45 p.m. on May 1 before signal loss, aligning with the estimated time of killings.[28] Earlier texts blamed a young accuser for his legal woes, suggesting resentment toward scrutiny of his history as a registered sex offender, though no direct link to specific victims was established beyond familial ties and the presence of teenage girls at the home.[23] Prentice noted an inability to discern McFadden's full thought process absent further records, but the convergence of suicidal ideation, avoidance of incarceration, and targeted executions of household members points to a causal chain rooted in self-preservation against reimprisonment.[23] Warrants contained inaccuracies, such as misidentifying alerts and interviewees, but core evidentiary elements on motive—McFadden's prison aversion—held without contradiction from official probes.[28] This framework aligns with patterns in similar cases involving offenders facing revocation, where elimination of potential testifiers or burdens accompanies suicide to evade accountability.

Aftermath

Community and Familial Impact

The mass murder-suicide shattered multiple families in Henryetta, leaving parents, siblings, and extended relatives to cope with the sudden loss of six individuals, including five teenagers and one adult woman. Ivy Webster's father, Justin Webster, described the profound personal devastation, noting the killings connected three families "in the most unimaginable way" as they navigated shared grief over the sleepover tragedy that claimed his daughter and Rylee McDonald.[29] Rylee McDonald's family similarly expressed ongoing trauma, with her father advocating amid revelations of McFadden's manipulative control over the household, which had isolated victims from external support.[30] The McFadden family, including relatives of Holly McFadden and her three children, faced compounded loss, though public statements from that side were limited due to the perpetrator's actions.[21] In the close-knit community of Henryetta, a town of approximately 5,700 residents in Okmulgee County, the events triggered widespread shock and sorrow, with local residents reporting lingering fear and distrust toward unchecked sex offenders living among them.[31] Families of the victims organized memorial events and healing gatherings to foster communal support, while fathers like Justin Webster campaigned for awareness, emphasizing the need for parents to recognize predatory behaviors in relationships involving children.[32] The crime scene property's suspicious fire in December 2024 drew brief attention but was dismissed by affected families as secondary to their advocacy efforts, with Webster stating it held no emotional closure compared to legal reforms.[20] The tragedy spurred grassroots pushes for policy changes, amplifying familial voices into broader community action; for instance, Webster announced his candidacy for Oklahoma Senate District 8 in 2025 to prioritize child protection laws.[33] Legislative responses, such as House Bill 2422—offering chemical castration as a parole option for sex offenders—and "Knight's Law," named after Henryetta High School's mascot to honor the teen victims, reflected the incident's ripple effects on state-level discourse around offender monitoring and recidivism prevention.[34][35] These efforts underscored a collective resolve to convert personal anguish into systemic safeguards, though families acknowledged the enduring psychological toll on survivors and the town.[26]

Policy and Legal Responses

In the wake of the killings, the Oklahoma Department of Corrections proposed reforms to its earned credits program, which had enabled Jesse McFadden to secure early release after serving approximately six years of a 20-year sentence for second-degree rape, despite subsequent probation violations and pending child sex abuse charges.[36] These changes aimed to limit the accumulation of credits—up to 60 days earned per 30 days served—for high-risk offenders, addressing systemic leniency that contributed to McFadden's freedom on May 1, 2023.[36] Victims' families initiated petitions and public campaigns to strengthen laws protecting minors from convicted sex offenders, emphasizing failures in monitoring and sentencing that allowed McFadden, a registered sex offender, to host unsupervised teenage sleepovers.[37] Advocacy focused on enhanced registration requirements, stricter probation oversight, and barriers to early release for those with child victimization histories.[38] Oklahoma legislators responded with "Knight's Law" (House Bill 3992), sponsored by Rep. Scott Fetgatter (R-Okmulgee), named for the Henryetta High School mascot to honor teenage victims Ivy Webster, Brittany Brewer, Rylee Allen, and Michael Mayo.[39] The legislation sought to mandate 100% sentence service without parole for convictions involving child rape, sexual abuse, human trafficking, or child pornography, targeting recidivism risks exemplified by McFadden's record.[40] It passed the House on March 11, 2024, but stalled in the Senate.[39] A revised iteration advanced through the House again on March 11, 2025, introducing an option for offenders to undergo chemical castration in exchange for parole eligibility, while retaining full-sentence requirements for non-compliant cases.[41] Concurrently, House Bill 2422, mandating chemical castration for child sex offenders before parole consideration, also cleared the House that month, reflecting broader momentum for punitive measures despite no enacted statewide overhauls by late 2025.[42] Fathers of victims Webster and Brewer sustained pressure for comprehensive criminal justice reforms, including reevaluation of bond practices for offenders facing new sex charges, arguing that procedural lapses enabled the tragedy.[43] No civil lawsuits against state agencies were publicly filed, though attorneys highlighted evidentiary gaps in McFadden's pre-release supervision as indicative of systemic deficiencies.[44]

Recent Developments

In April 2025, the Oklahoma State Bureau of Investigation (OSBI) provided a detailed briefing to the family of victim Ivy Webster, revealing a timeline of events leading to the May 1, 2023, killings and concluding that Jesse McFadden acted as the sole shooter, though he may have received assistance from one unidentified victim in staging elements of the scene.[14] [26] The briefing included audio recordings from the investigation, which offered previously unreleased insights into McFadden's actions, such as his movements in the days prior and interactions with the victims, emphasizing his predatory history as a convicted sex offender.[21] Family members described the disclosures as aimed at educating parents on recognizing offender tactics, without altering the determination of McFadden's sole responsibility for the murders.[26] On December 10, 2024, the Henryetta residence where the bodies were discovered—often referred to locally as the "house of horrors"—sustained significant fire damage, primarily to the roof, prompting an arson investigation by the Okmulgee County Sheriff's Office and OSBI.[45] [46] The father of one victim stated that the fire held little evidentiary value for the closed case, redirecting attention to advocacy for stricter sex offender monitoring laws rather than speculating on potential interference with remnants of the scene.[20] In May 2025, Justin Webster, father of victim Ivy Webster, announced his candidacy for Oklahoma House District 8, campaigning on reforms to enhance child protection measures, including expanded oversight of registered sex offenders and improved inter-agency communication to prevent similar tragedies.[33] This effort reflects ongoing familial pushes for policy changes in response to investigative findings that highlighted lapses in monitoring McFadden's compliance with parole conditions prior to the incident.[33]
