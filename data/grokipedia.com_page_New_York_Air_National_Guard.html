New York Air National Guard

The New York Air National Guard (NYANG) is the aerial warfare arm of the New York National Guard, operating as a federally recognized reserve force of the United States Air Force to execute state defense, disaster response, and federal air operations.[1][2] It maintains five primary flying wings equipped with aircraft for strategic airlift, combat search and rescue, and unmanned attack missions, supporting both domestic emergencies like hurricane relief and international deployments.[1] Key units include the 105th Airlift Wing at Stewart Air National Guard Base in Newburgh, operating C-5M Super Galaxy transports; the 106th Rescue Wing at Francis S. Gabreski Air National Guard Base in Westhampton Beach, specializing in pararescue and helicopter recoveries; the 107th Attack Wing at Niagara Falls Air Reserve Station; the 109th Airlift Wing at Stratton Air National Guard Base in Scotia, unique for its ski-equipped LC-130 Hercules conducting polar resupply to Antarctica and Greenland; and the 174th Attack Wing at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse, focused on MQ-9 Reaper drone operations.[1][2][3] Tracing its origins to the 1st Aero Company organized in 1915 as the first National Guard aviation unit, the NYANG gained modern federal recognition in 1947 and has since achieved milestones such as the 105th becoming the first Air National Guard unit to operate the C-5 Galaxy in 1985, alongside repeated unit commendations including Meritorious and Outstanding Unit Awards from the Air Force for operational excellence in support and combat roles.[4][5][6][7]

Mission and Role

National Defense Contributions

The New York Air National Guard contributes to national defense through federal missions including strategic airlift, combat search and rescue, remotely piloted aircraft operations, and logistical support in extreme environments.[8] Units such as the 105th Airlift Wing operate C-17 Globemaster III aircraft for rapid deployment of troops, cargo, and aeromedical evacuation, supporting global operations including overseas medical missions concluded in November following four months of service.[9] The wing participated in Exercise Air Defender 2023 by flying 10 missions to transport personnel and supplies from multiple states to Europe, enhancing NATO interoperability.[10] The 106th Rescue Wing specializes in personnel recovery, deploying HH-60G Pave Hawk helicopters and HC-130J Combat King II aircraft for combat search and rescue in hostile environments, providing coverage for U.S. and allied forces worldwide.[11] It supported real-world rescue operations during the REFORPAC exercise in 2025, integrating with joint forces at Misawa Air Base, Japan.[12] The wing's special warfare elements, including pararescuemen, enable day-night recoveries, marking 50 years of such capabilities as of September 2025.[13] In intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance, the 174th Attack Wing operates MQ-9 Reaper drones, with over 300 personnel deployed between 2002 and 2008 in support of Operations Noble Eagle, Enduring Freedom, and Iraqi Freedom.[14] These missions provided persistent overhead surveillance and strike capabilities in combat zones.[15] The 109th Airlift Wing uniquely supports national interests via LC-130H Hercules ski-equipped aircraft, delivering cargo and personnel to Antarctica for National Science Foundation research programs, completing 242 missions and transporting 2,100 individuals during the 2018-2019 season alone.[16] This logistical role sustains U.S. scientific presence in polar regions critical for strategic environmental monitoring and resource assessment, with the 37th consecutive deployment launching in October 2024.[17]

State Support and Emergency Response

The New York Air National Guard executes state missions under the authority of the Governor, focusing on emergency response including disaster relief, search and rescue, and support for civil authorities during crises such as natural disasters and public health emergencies.[18] These operations leverage air assets for rapid deployment, aerial reconnaissance, and logistics, complementing local and state agencies.[19] In hurricane responses, units like the 106th Rescue Wing have conducted personnel rescues using HH-60G Pave Hawk helicopters and HC-130J Combat King II aircraft. For instance, during Hurricane Harvey in 2017, 106th pararescuemen evacuated families from flooded areas in Houston.[20] Similarly, in the 2024 responses to Hurricanes Helene and Milton, New York Air National Guard airmen, trained as emergency medical technicians, supported search and rescue in Florida with watercraft and helicopters, contributing to over 6,600 Guard personnel rescuing hundreds across affected states.[21] [22] The Guard's involvement in the September 11, 2001, attacks marked an immediate air defense and recovery effort; Technical Sergeant Jeremy Powell of the New York Air National Guard was the first U.S. military member to recognize the attacks' severity while on duty at an air defense headquarters, facilitating rapid mobilization of over 8,000 New York National Guard members by day's end.[23] [24] During the COVID-19 pandemic, more than 1,300 New York National Guard members, including Air National Guardsmen, remained on duty for over 225 days starting March 2020, staffing testing and vaccination sites, distributing meals, and producing over 35 million tests while administering 4 million vaccines across 200 mission sites.[25] [26] This effort represented the largest domestic mobilization in U.S. National Guard history for a public health crisis.[27]

Federal Mobilization and Gained Commands

The New York Air National Guard has undertaken numerous federal mobilizations, particularly in support of global operations following the September 11, 2001, attacks. Units such as the 105th Airlift Wing have conducted strategic airlift missions, including aeromedical evacuations from Africa and the Middle East to facilities in Germany and the United States as part of ongoing federal taskings.[9] In 2020 alone, the wing supported eight military operations, executing 73 combat and combat support missions while transporting 9.2 million pounds of cargo.[28] The 106th Rescue Wing has been federalized for combat search and rescue roles in theaters like Iraq and Afghanistan, aligning with its primary federal mission under Air Combat Command. More than 150 wing members were mobilized in various capacities during key periods, contributing to personnel recovery operations.[29] These deployments underscore the wing's readiness for Title 10 activations, where it integrates into active-duty rescue frameworks.[30] In a significant gained command, the New York Air National Guard assumed operational control of the Northeast Air Defense Sector (later redesignated Eastern Air Defense Sector) on December 1, 1994, marking the first such transition from active-duty Air Force to Air National Guard for air defense responsibilities.[31] This sector, manned primarily by the 224th Air Defense Group, provides vigilant detection, warning, and tactical control for NORAD and USNORTHCOM over the eastern United States, enhancing federal air sovereignty missions.[32] The 109th Airlift Wing has secured a unique federal role in Operation Deep Freeze, the U.S. military's logistical support to Antarctic scientific research managed by the National Science Foundation. Since assuming the mission, the wing's 139th Airlift Squadron operates the world's only ski-equipped LC-130 Hercules fleet, conducting annual resupply flights to remote ice stations. In the 2024-2025 season, it executed 79 missions, delivering personnel, cargo, and fuel across Antarctica.[33] This specialized capability, gained through federal designation, positions the wing as the sole provider for polar airlift under U.S. Transportation Command.[17]

Organizational Structure

Headquarters and Command

The headquarters of the New York Air National Guard (NYANG) is located at 330 Old Niskayuna Road in Latham, New York, within the facilities of the New York State Division of Military and Naval Affairs (DMNA).[34] This site serves as the central administrative and operational hub for coordinating the state's air defense, rescue, airlift, and attack missions across approximately 5,800 personnel.[35] The NYANG operates as a dual federal-state force, reporting to both the Governor of New York for state missions and the President for federal activations, with command authority aligned under the Adjutant General of New York, Major General Ray Shields.[36] Command of the NYANG is vested in Major General Michael W. Bank, who assumed the role on June 3, 2024, succeeding Major General Denise Donnell.[35] Bank, promoted to major general on October 9, 2024, is a command pilot with over 5,000 flight hours, including experience as a senior navigator on B-52H strategic bombers and KC-135R tankers.[37][36] The command staff includes Colonel Tammy Wells-Crannell as Chief of Staff, responsible for directing headquarters operations, personnel management, and logistical support.[38] Brigadier General Gary R. Charlton II serves as Assistant Adjutant General for Air, providing oversight on policy and integration with DMNA structures.[39] The NYANG's command structure emphasizes readiness for rapid mobilization, with headquarters elements focused on planning, training standardization, and resource allocation across five primary wings and specialized units like the Eastern Air Defense Sector.[1] This framework ensures alignment with U.S. Air Force standards while addressing New York-specific threats, such as disaster response and homeland defense.[40]

Major Wings and Squadrons

The New York Air National Guard's major wings focus on airlift, rescue, and attack missions, supported by specialized squadrons equipped with strategic airlifters, combat rescue platforms, and remotely piloted aircraft. These units operate from bases across the state, providing both federal rapid response capabilities and state emergency support. The core flying wings are the 105th Airlift Wing at Stewart Air National Guard Base in Newburgh, the 106th Rescue Wing at Francis S. Gabreski Air National Guard Base in Westhampton Beach, the 107th Attack Wing at Niagara Falls Air Reserve Station, the 109th Airlift Wing at Stratton Air National Guard Base in Scotia, and the 174th Attack Wing at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse.[1] The 105th Airlift Wing conducts strategic and tactical airlift operations using Boeing C-17 Globemaster III aircraft, primarily through the 137th Airlift Squadron, which handles worldwide cargo and troop transport missions, including support for humanitarian efforts and contingency deployments. The wing's 213th Airlift Squadron provides aeromedical evacuation capabilities with dedicated C-17 configurations for patient transport. Established with a focus on heavy airlift since receiving C-5 Galaxy aircraft in the 1980s before transitioning to C-17s in 2011, the 105th has logged over 1,000 combat sorties in operations like Enduring Freedom and Iraqi Freedom.[41][42] The 106th Rescue Wing specializes in personnel recovery and combat search and rescue, operating Lockheed Martin HC-130J Combat King II fixed-wing aircraft via the 102nd Rescue Squadron for long-range infiltration, refueling, and extraction, alongside Sikorsky HH-60W Jolly Green II helicopters flown by the 101st Rescue Squadron for tactical recovery in hostile environments. The wing received its first HC-130J in 2019, enhancing endurance with aerial refueling and advanced sensors for missions extending up to 1,700 miles offshore, as demonstrated in real-world rescues like the 2017 Atlantic recovery operation involving seven airmen.[43][44][45] The 107th Attack Wing executes intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, and precision strike missions with General Atomics MQ-9 Reaper remotely piloted aircraft, managed through squadrons like the 147th Attack Squadron, enabling persistent overwatch and kinetic operations in contested airspace. Transitioning from fighter roles to unmanned systems in the 2010s, the wing supports global vigilance across air, space, and cyber domains while maintaining readiness for state missions.[46][47] The 109th Airlift Wing uniquely operates ski-equipped Lockheed LC-130H Hercules aircraft through the 139th Airlift Squadron, facilitating year-round operations in polar regions, including resupply to remote Antarctic and Greenland stations under Operation Deep Freeze since 1988. The wing's eight LC-130Hs, modified with skis for unprepared snow and ice runways, have transported over 120,000 pounds of cargo per mission to sites like Summit Station, supporting scientific research and national interests in extreme environments.[48][49][50] The 174th Attack Wing mirrors the 107th in conducting MQ-9 Reaper operations for attack and ISR, with squadrons focused on remote split operations and data analysis to support joint forces. Based in Syracuse, it emphasizes multi-domain integration, including cyber and space elements, and has participated in unit-wide readiness exercises simulating full-spectrum contingencies.[1][51]

Air Defense and Support Elements

The Eastern Air Defense Sector (EADS), based in Rome, New York, functions as the core air defense component of the New York Air National Guard, coordinating aerospace warning and control for the eastern United States under North American Aerospace Defense Command (NORAD).[52] Operated by the 224th Air Defense Group, which comprises approximately 400 personnel including active-duty, Guard, and Canadian forces, EADS maintains continuous 24/7 surveillance of airspace east of the Mississippi River, identifying threats and directing responses such as fighter intercepts.[52][32] The group includes the 224th Air Defense Squadron for battle management operations and supports federal missions by integrating radar data, satellite inputs, and fighter alerts to ensure rapid threat neutralization.[52] Support elements augment operational wings through specialized groups focused on logistics, security, command and control, and medical readiness. The 152nd Air Operations Group, stationed at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse, delivers air operations planning by embedding personnel into U.S. Air Force Air Operations Centers, such as the 603d at Ramstein Air Base, Germany, to manage air tasking orders, weapon system checks, and combat planning cycles.[53][54] Mission support groups within major wings, such as the 105th Mission Support Group at Stewart Air National Guard Base, provide essential base-level functions including civil engineering, security forces, logistics readiness, and personnel services to sustain flying operations and emergency deployments.[55] Additional support includes medical detachments like the 107th Medical Group, which delivers aeromedical evacuation training and clinical services, as demonstrated in joint exercises conducting over 3,500 patient encounters in 2025.[56] These elements, including four geographically separated units under Joint Force Headquarters-Air oversight, ensure total force integration with a strength exceeding 5,800 personnel across the state.[57] In December 2023, the 224th Air Defense Group activated the 224th Security Forces Squadron to bolster force protection and base defense capabilities aligned with Air Force standards.[58]

Historical Development

Origins in Aviation Militia

The origins of aviation in the New York National Guard trace to May 30, 1908, when the First Aero Company, Signal Corps, was formed at the Park Avenue Armory in New York City, marking one of the earliest militia aviation units in the United States.[68] This unit emerged shortly after the U.S. Army's acquisition of its first powered aircraft in 1908, reflecting the rapid integration of emerging aviation technology into state militia structures.[68] In November 1915, Captain Raynal Cawthorne Bolling organized and took command of the 1st Aero Company, New York National Guard, based in Mineola, establishing it as the first dedicated Guard aviation squadron.[69][4] The unit acquired its initial aircraft in 1911 through a loan from New York aviator Glenn Curtiss, who provided one of his Hammondsport-manufactured planes to support early training and operations.[70] By 1916, the company had progressed to operational status, becoming the first militia aviation unit mustered into federal service during the Mexican Border Crisis in June of that year.[71] These early efforts laid the groundwork for New York militia aviation, emphasizing reconnaissance and signaling roles aligned with Signal Corps functions, though limited by primitive aircraft and rudimentary infrastructure.[68] The 1st Aero Company's lineage persists in modern units like the 102nd Rescue Squadron, underscoring its foundational role in the evolution toward the Air National Guard.[69]

Establishment Post-World War II

The establishment of the New York Air National Guard followed the demobilization of World War II-era Army Air Forces units and aligned with the federal reorganization of reserve aviation forces. Prior to the war, New York maintained aviation elements within its Army National Guard, such as observation and fighter squadrons, which were federalized and contributed to combat operations. Postwar efforts to rebuild these units gained momentum through lobbying by National Guard leaders to ensure inclusion in the emerging independent U.S. Air Force structure, averting exclusion from the peacetime military establishment.[72] The pivotal legislation was the National Security Act of 1947, signed by President Harry S. Truman on July 26, 1947, which created the U.S. Air Force as a separate service branch and formally authorized the Air National Guard as its reserve component, effective September 18, 1947. This act enabled states to reorganize existing aviation militia into federally recognized ANG units, with New York achieving near-complete federal recognition for its National Guard elements by the end of 1947. The New York Air National Guard's operational birthday is thus tied to this date, marking the transition from Army oversight to Air Force integration while retaining dual state-federal missions.[4][73] Key initial units included the 138th Fighter Squadron, federally recognized on October 28, 1947, at Hancock Field (now Syracuse Hancock International Airport), Syracuse, New York, as one of the earliest ANG fighter elements in the state. Assigned to air defense roles, it formed the nucleus of what became the 174th Fighter Wing. Additional units, such as elements of the 106th and 107th Groups, received recognition in subsequent months, establishing a statewide network focused on fighter interception and tactical support with initial strengths drawn from wartime veterans. By 1948, further activations like the 152d Air Operations Group at Westchester County Airport expanded command and control capabilities, setting the foundation for Cold War-era growth.[14][74][75]

Cold War Expansions and Readiness

During the early Cold War period, the New York Air National Guard expanded its capabilities to meet growing air defense requirements against potential Soviet bomber threats, transitioning from propeller-driven fighters to jet aircraft. In January 1950, the 138th Fighter Squadron at Hancock Field in Syracuse became the first jet-equipped flying unit in the state, receiving the F-84B Thunderjet, which enhanced interception speeds and operational readiness for Air Defense Command (ADC) missions.[14] This shift aligned with broader ANG efforts to integrate into national air defense networks, including radar-directed intercepts and quick-reaction alerts. By the mid-1950s, units like the 107th Fighter Group at Niagara Falls had adopted the F-94B Starfire all-weather interceptor, enabling 24-hour operations and participation in continental defense exercises.[47] The 138th Fighter Interceptor Squadron pioneered ANG contributions to active air defense in March 1953, assuming a classified alert mission at Hancock Field to test Guard units' viability for federal ADC roles, marking the first such peacetime commitment with armed aircraft on 15-minute readiness postures.[76] This experiment proved successful, leading to expanded alert duties across NY ANG squadrons, which by 1954 included standing alerts with F-86 Sabre variants for rapid scrambles. Further expansions involved personnel growth and infrastructure upgrades, such as at Stewart Air National Guard Base, which supported fighter operations and hosted ADC-directed training. The 107th transitioned to F-86H Sabres in the late 1950s, emphasizing high-speed interception, while maintaining tactical flexibility for potential mobilizations.[77] Readiness was bolstered through rigorous training and federal activations, including the 1961 Berlin Crisis mobilization, where select NY ANG fighter squadrons contributed to Operation Stair Step, deploying aircraft and personnel to reinforce NATO airpower in Europe amid heightened Soviet tensions.[78] Units conducted frequent intercepts of unidentified aircraft and participated in exercises simulating nuclear-era threats, achieving high sortie rates and integration with active-duty forces via shared ADC sectors like the New York Air Defense Sector.[31] Concurrently, airlift elements expanded; the 109th Troop Carrier Squadron, reoriented from fighters, operated C-97 Stratofreighters and later C-124 Globemasters for logistical support, including resupply missions to Arctic radar sites under the Distant Early Warning (DEW) Line, critical for early detection of transpolar attacks.[79] These efforts ensured the NY ANG's dual state-federal posture, with over 2,000 personnel by the late 1950s maintaining combat-effective status through annual inspections and equipment modernizations.

Post-Cold War Operations

Gulf War and 1990s Engagements

The New York Air National Guard's 174th Tactical Fighter Wing mobilized for Operation Desert Storm in response to Iraq's invasion of Kuwait on August 2, 1990.[80] On January 3, 1991, twenty-eight maintenance personnel from the wing's munitions maintenance squadron deployed to the theater via C-141 aircraft, supporting the forthcoming air campaign.[81] The wing's F-16 Fighting Falcon aircraft followed, arriving intact at their assigned base, where they participated in coalition strikes against Iraqi military targets during the January 17 to February 28, 1991, air and ground offensive.[81] This marked the 174th's first major combat deployment, with aircrews logging sorties in enforcement of no-fly zones and direct attacks, contributing to the rapid degradation of Iraqi air defenses and command infrastructure.[82] In the post-Gulf War decade, New York Air National Guard units shifted toward humanitarian and peacekeeping support operations amid emerging global contingencies. The 105th Military Airlift Wing, equipped with C-5 Galaxy transports, conducted resupply missions to Bosnia-Herzegovina from December 1992 to April 1993, aiding United Nations efforts to deliver relief supplies amid ethnic conflicts following Yugoslavia's dissolution.[83] These airlift operations facilitated the transport of over 100 tons of cargo, including medical aid and food, to support civilian populations and stabilization forces under Operation Provide Promise.[83] Concurrently, the 107th Air Refueling Wing provided aerial refueling support for NATO operations in the Balkans, extending the range of fighter aircraft enforcing Operation Deny Flight no-fly zones over Bosnia starting in April 1993.[84] Domestic and contingency readiness exercises dominated other 1990s activities, with units like the 109th Tactical Airlift Group conducting flood relief sorties using C-130 Hercules aircraft, such as 105 missions in early 1990 for regional disasters.[85] By mid-decade, the New York Air National Guard assumed enhanced air defense roles, with the 109th Fighter Group transitioning to alert duties and the establishment of the Northeast Air Defense Sector under New York ANG control in December 1994, bolstering continental surveillance amid reduced overseas commitments.[31] These engagements underscored the Guard's pivot from large-scale conventional warfare to versatile expeditionary and defensive postures.[80]

Post-9/11 Deployments and Global War on Terror

Following the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks, the New York Air National Guard was rapidly mobilized under Operation Noble Eagle to secure U.S. airspace, with F-16 Fighting Falcons from the 174th Fighter Squadron at Niagara Falls Air Reserve Station among the first aircraft scrambled to patrol New York City and enforce no-fly zones.[86][87] This homeland defense mission involved continuous combat air patrols over major population centers, including New York, contributing to tens of thousands of sorties flown nationwide to deter aerial threats.[88] The 107th Air Refueling Wing, also at Niagara Falls, supported these efforts with KC-135 Stratotanker aircraft providing aerial refueling for fighter patrols, marking an early shift from traditional state missions to sustained federal operations.[89] Overseas, the 105th Airlift Wing at Stewart Air National Guard Base deployed C-17 Globemaster III aircraft and personnel in support of Operations Iraqi Freedom and Enduring Freedom, with over 500 airmen accumulating at least 30 days of active duty in theater by 2009 for strategic airlift of troops and equipment.[90] By 2011, an additional 200 wing members had served in these campaigns, flying combat missions to deliver cargo directly to forward bases in Iraq and Afghanistan.[91] The wing's contributions emphasized rapid global deployment capabilities, including resupply operations under hazardous conditions.[92] The 106th Rescue Wing at Francis S. Gabreski Airport conducted combat search and rescue (CSAR) missions across multiple theaters, deploying HH-60G Pave Hawk helicopters and pararescue teams to Afghanistan, Iraq, and Libya, where crews recovered hundreds of U.S., coalition, and allied personnel amid active combat.[93] Wing airmen rotated regularly from 2001 onward, supporting personnel recovery in high-threat environments, though operations incurred losses, such as the 2018 crash of an HH-60G near Al-Qa'im, Iraq, claiming four members.[94] These efforts integrated with broader special operations, leveraging the wing's expertise in austere extractions.[95] The 109th Airlift Wing at Schenectady County Airport provided logistical support to Global War on Terror operations, deploying LC-130 Hercules crews to Afghanistan and bases like Al Udeid, Qatar, for sustained airlift in combat zones since 2001.[96] Individual wing leaders logged multiple tours, including flights to Bagram Airfield, facilitating troop movements and resupply in rugged terrains beyond standard C-130 capabilities.[97] This marked the unit's expansion from polar missions to direct GWOT sustainment, with continuous rotations underscoring New York ANG's role in expeditionary logistics.[98]

Recent Missions (2010s-2025)

The 105th Airlift Wing conducted patient evacuations from conflict zones in Africa and the Middle East, transporting casualties to U.S. medical facilities via Ramstein Air Base in Germany as part of ongoing aeromedical operations supporting U.S. Central Command.[9] In June 2023, the wing executed 10 airlift sorties during the NATO-led Air Defender exercise, ferrying personnel and equipment from multiple U.S. states to European staging areas to simulate rapid reinforcement of allies amid potential aggression.[10] The 109th Airlift Wing maintained its unique ski-equipped LC-130 Hercules fleet for annual logistical support to National Science Foundation research in polar regions, including Operation Deep Freeze in Antarctica and operations in Greenland. In the 2024-2025 Antarctic season, five LC-130H aircraft and 110 personnel flew 79 missions, delivering over 1.5 million pounds of cargo, fuel, and personnel to remote ice stations across the continent before concluding operations on March 18, 2025.[33] For Greenland, the wing launched its 2025 support mission on April 18, deploying aircraft to Thule Air Base and Summit Station for science resupply, ending successfully in September after multiple sorties amid harsh Arctic conditions.[99][50] The unit also participated in Arctic Edge 24, airlifting special operations forces and equipment to Alaska for homeland defense training.[100] In March 2025, 109th crews achieved a first by landing an LC-130 on fresh water ice during Operation Nanook-Nunalivut in Canada's Northwest Territories, enabling cargo drops of 23,000 pounds over 17 support flights.[101] The 106th Rescue Wing focused on combat search and rescue, deploying over 100 Airmen with HC-130s and HH-60s to Misawa Air Base, Japan, in August 2025 for REFORPAC, a U.S. Pacific Air Forces exercise simulating downed pilot recovery in contested environments.[102] Earlier, in 2017, the wing surged two HC-130s, three HH-60s, three zodiac boats, and 124 personnel for Hurricane Irma response in the Caribbean, conducting personnel recovery and flood zone extractions.[103] During the COVID-19 response from 2020 to 2022, 106th assets provided overwatch and potential medevac support alongside polar logistics and Middle East air cover missions.[104] Across the period, New York Air National Guard units supported domestic emergencies and training, including air sovereignty patrols, wildfire suppression, and exercises like Future Flag for close air support integration.[105] The 174th Attack Wing's MQ-9 Reapers contributed to intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance in federal operations, though specific deployment details remain classified.[106] These missions reflected a shift toward great power competition readiness, scientific sustainment, and expeditionary support amid reduced large-scale counterinsurgency deployments post-2014.

Specialized Capabilities

Airlift and Logistics Operations

The New York Air National Guard conducts airlift and logistics operations primarily through the 105th Airlift Wing and the 109th Airlift Wing, providing strategic, tactical, and specialized polar airlift capabilities to support federal, state, and international missions. These operations involve the transportation of troops, equipment, supplies, and personnel, often in austere environments, contributing to global mobility and logistical sustainment for U.S. forces and scientific endeavors.[107][3] The 105th Airlift Wing, based at Stewart Air National Guard Base in Newburgh, operates C-17 Globemaster III aircraft for rapid strategic and tactical airlift, enabling delivery of troops and cargo to main operating bases or forward deployment areas. Its 137th Airlift Squadron supports inter-theater airlift, aeromedical evacuation, and expeditionary combat operations, including converting aircraft into flying hospitals during medical missions. In one series of operations, wing aircrews executed 17 aeromedical evacuation flights totaling 759 hours, transporting 560 patients, 2,040 passengers, and 2.1 tons of cargo. The wing transitioned from C-5 Galaxy to C-17 platforms to enhance flexibility in austere field operations, with the first dedicated C-17 arriving to replace retiring strategic airlifters. Recent modernization efforts include installing microvanes on C-17s, projected to save the Air Force over $14 million annually in fuel costs as of March 2025.[107][9][108] The 109th Airlift Wing, stationed at Stratton Air National Guard Base in Scotia, specializes in polar airlift using ski-equipped LC-130H Hercules aircraft, the only such platforms in U.S. military service, for operations on unprepared snow and ice runways. It provides logistical support to the National Science Foundation's Antarctic Program and Arctic missions, including resupply of remote research stations with personnel, cargo, and fuel. During the 2024-2025 Antarctic season, the wing completed 79 missions across the continent from McMurdo Station between October 2024 and February 2025. In Greenland, operations from Kangerlussuaq supported science and defense sites, culminating in a successful season ending September 22, 2025, with multiple rotations of aircraft and personnel. The wing also conducts traditional C-130 airlift, airdrops, aeromedical evacuations, and aerial port services, earning recognition for logistics readiness in 2018. These capabilities extend to state disaster response, such as Hurricane Sandy recovery in 2012.[3][33][50]

Air Defense and Surveillance Roles

The New York Air National Guard's air defense roles originated in the early Cold War era, when several of its fighter squadrons were equipped for interceptor missions to counter potential aerial threats. The 138th Fighter Squadron, based at Hancock Field in Syracuse, became the first Air National Guard unit to assume an air defense alert posture on March 1, 1953, operating from Hancock Field as part of the national air defense network.[70] In 1954, the squadron transitioned to the F-94 Starfire, a radar-equipped interceptor designed for all-weather interception of bombers.[14] Similarly, the 137th Fighter-Interceptor Squadron under the 105th Fighter Group at Stewart Airfield received F-51 Mustang aircraft in 1952 specifically for air defense duties, marking an early emphasis on rapid response capabilities within the state.[5] These units evolved through subsequent decades, adopting advanced interceptors such as the F-86 Sabre, F-102 Delta Dagger, and F-101 Voodoo to maintain readiness against Soviet bomber incursions, participating in exercises like runway alerts and contributing to the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) system for coordinated intercepts.[122] The 102nd Fighter-Interceptor Squadron, part of the 106th Fighter-Interceptor Wing at Suffolk County Air Force Base, operated F-94B Starfires from 1954 to 1958 before shifting to rescue missions, reflecting the broader ANG transition from pure interception as strategic threats diminished. By the late 1960s and 1970s, many New York ANG interceptor squadrons, including the 137th and 138th, relinquished fighter roles in favor of tactical fighters or other missions, with the last dedicated air defense fighters phased out as ground-based radar and missile systems assumed primacy.[5] In the post-Cold War period, the New York ANG shifted focus to command-and-control and surveillance functions, primarily through the Eastern Air Defense Sector (EADS) at Rome, New York, established with roots in the 1956 activation of the 4621st Air Defense Wing.[31] The EADS, under North American Aerospace Defense Command (NORAD), is responsible for aerospace warning and control over the airspace east of the Mississippi River, employing vigilant detection via integrated radar networks, rapid threat assessment, and tactical direction of response forces.[52] This mission is executed by the 224th Air Defense Group (224th ADG), a New York ANG unit comprising the 224th Air Defense Squadron and support elements, which provides the core personnel for 24/7 operations, including monitoring commercial and military air traffic, identifying unidentified aircraft, and coordinating intercepts if required.[52] The 224th ADG formally organized as a group in December 2014, enhancing its role in bi-national NORAD operations with Canadian forces.[32] Contemporary surveillance emphasizes non-kinetic measures, such as data fusion from general surveillance radars and integration with national sensors to provide early warning of air-breathing threats, including drones and cruise missiles.[123] During events like the COVID-19 pandemic, the 224th ADG maintained uninterrupted monitoring of eastern U.S. airspace, demonstrating resilience in sustaining detection and control functions.[124] The unit's contributions extend to specialized tasks, such as supporting NORAD's annual aircraft tracking operations, underscoring its ongoing commitment to air domain awareness without reliance on organic fighter assets.[125]

Equipment and Technology

Aircraft Fleets

The New York Air National Guard's aircraft fleet consists primarily of fixed-wing transport and rescue platforms operated by its airlift and rescue wings, supporting strategic mobility, polar operations, and personnel recovery missions. The 105th Airlift Wing, based at Stewart Air National Guard Base, fields the Boeing C-17 Globemaster III for rapid global deployment of troops, equipment, and humanitarian aid, having transitioned from the C-5 Galaxy in the early 2010s to enhance flexibility and efficiency.[126][57] This aircraft's ability to operate from short, unprepared runways aligns with the wing's role in Air Mobility Command-gained operations.[41] The 109th Airlift Wing at Stratton Air National Guard Base operates a unique inventory of Lockheed LC-130H Hercules variants, the only ski-equipped C-130s in U.S. military service, alongside standard wheel-equipped C-130H models. These "Skibirds" enable landings on snow and ice, critical for Operation Deep Freeze Antarctic resupply and Greenland scientific missions, with the wing deploying up to four LC-130s seasonally for such tasks.[3][127] The LC-130H's modifications, including retractable skis, support heavy cargo delivery to remote polar sites where wheeled aircraft cannot operate.[128]

Unmanned Systems and Drones

The New York Air National Guard's involvement with unmanned systems centers on the MQ-9 Reaper, a medium-altitude, long-endurance remotely piloted aircraft used for intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, and precision strikes. The 174th Attack Wing, based at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse, transitioned to the MQ-9 mission in 2010, becoming one of the first Air National Guard units to operate the platform for both training and operational deployments.[57] This wing maintains and flies MQ-9s for combat support, including remote piloting from New York for missions in active theaters, and has accumulated 174th Attack Wing has demonstrated enhanced capabilities, such as remote landing and takeoff procedures tested in 2023, allowing operations into austere airfields without on-site pilots, thereby operators to assist California wildfire surveillance in 2017, highlighting the MQ-9's role in rapid response with its ability to loiter for extended periods while streaming high-definition video feeds.[136] Beyond large-scale RPAs, the New York ANG has explored smaller unmanned systems for training, including integration with ground forces for tactical surveillance, though primary emphasis remains on the MQ-9 fleet for strategic capabilities.[120]

Modernization Initiatives

The 105th Airlift Wing has led efforts to modernize the C-17 Globemaster III fleet through the installation of microvanes, small aerodynamic devices affixed to the aircraft's rear fuselage that reduce drag and fuel consumption by 1 percent per aircraft. This initiative, implemented as of March 2025, positions the wing as the first Air National Guard unit to complete the upgrade on its C-17s, enabling broader fleet-wide adoption and projected savings exceeding $14 million in fuel costs over the service life of the modifications.[137] In October 2024, the 106th Rescue Wing received its initial HH-60W Jolly Green II helicopters, replacing older HH-60G Pave Hawk models with advanced features including improved avionics, enhanced survivability systems, and integrated defensive measures for combat search and rescue missions. This transition enhances the wing's personnel recovery capabilities in contested environments, aligning with Air Force-wide Jolly Green II fielding goals.[131] The 109th Airlift Wing's LC-130 Hercules fleet, specialized for ski-equipped operations in polar regions, is undergoing a recapitalization program to extend airframe service life and incorporate avionics upgrades, with completion targeted for 2026 under New York state strategic objectives. This effort addresses aging infrastructure challenges while maintaining the wing's unique role in Antarctic and Arctic logistics support.[65] At Hancock Field Air National Guard Base, the 174th Attack Wing has integrated software and sensor upgrades to the MQ-9 Reaper unmanned aerial system, improving real-time intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance data processing during test evaluations conducted in collaboration with the Air Attack Training Center. These enhancements, described as transformative for Air National Guard remotely piloted aircraft operations, focus on multi-domain awareness without requiring hardware overhauls.[138] Broader modernization priorities outlined in the New York Air National Guard's strategic planning emphasize sustained investment in facilities and equipment to counter deferred maintenance and adapt to evolving threats, including advocacy for next-generation airlift and cyber integration capabilities.

Personnel and Readiness

Force Size and Composition

The New York Air National Guard (NYANG) maintains an assigned strength of approximately 5,800 Airmen, making it the largest state Air National Guard organization in the United States.[139] This end strength encompasses traditional part-time Guard members, full-time Active Guard and Reserve personnel, and dual-status technicians who support both state and federal missions.[140] Personnel are distributed across aviation, maintenance, logistics, security, medical, and administrative roles, with a focus on operational readiness for airlift, rescue, attack, and support functions. NYANG's force is structured into five primary wings, each headquartered at distinct bases throughout the state: the 105th Airlift Wing at Stewart Air National Guard Base in Newburgh; the 106th Rescue Wing at Francis S. Gabreski Air National Guard Base in Westhampton Beach; the 107th Attack Wing at Niagara Falls Air Reserve Station; the 109th Airlift Wing at Stratton Air National Guard Base in Scotia; and the 174th Attack Wing at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse.[15] These wings include squadrons dedicated to flying operations, aircraft maintenance groups, mission support squadrons, and specialized units such as communications flights and security forces. For instance, the 105th Airlift Wing alone assigns over 1,000 Airmen to airlift and sustainment tasks.[141] The composition emphasizes a balance between combat and support elements, with roughly 15-20% officers including pilots and commanders, and the remainder enlisted Airmen in technical and operational specialties.[142] Recruitment and retention efforts have sustained this force level amid national Guard challenges, with NYANG achieving high marks in accessions relative to authorized positions as of fiscal year 2024.[140]

Training Regimens and Exercises

The New York Air National Guard maintains rigorous training regimens aligned with U.S. Air Force standards, emphasizing mission-specific qualifications for airlift, rescue, and polar operations. Airmen complete initial technical training at Air Force schools, followed by unit-level proficiency checks and annual recurrent training to ensure combat readiness, including simulator sessions, live-flight evaluations, and emergency procedure drills. Maintenance personnel undergo specialized courses on aircraft systems, with recent initiatives incorporating virtual reality for C-17 Globemaster III familiarization at the 105th Airlift Wing.[143] The 109th Airlift Wing's LC-130 Hercules crews focus on polar-specific regimens, including cold-weather survival, ski-landing techniques, and airdrop operations in extreme conditions, conducted at facilities like Raven Camp for ice runway construction and snow operations. These airmen participate in annual exercises such as Canadian Armed Forces Arctic training, deploying up to two ski-equipped aircraft and 40 personnel to demonstrate tactical airlift and resupply in the High Arctic, as seen in operations supporting remote camp establishment and fuel delivery from April 1 to 22 in prior years.[144][145] The wing also simulates crisis deployments, mobilizing 228 personnel and cargo over three days in August exercises to test rapid response capabilities.[146] Rescue operations training at the 106th Rescue Wing centers on combat search and rescue (CSAR), with HH-60G Pave Hawk and transitioning HH-60W Jolly Green II crews practicing hoist recoveries up to 600 pounds from 200 feet, personnel locating, and scene direction in simulated combat environments. Recent exercises include mass accident response drills involving crash-damaged aircraft recovery (CDDAR) on November 7, 2024, and comprehensive CSAR scenarios on May 4, integrating pararescue teams for downed pilot recovery.[93][147][148] The wing supports real-world contingencies during large-scale events like REFORPAC 2025, maintaining on-call status for personnel recovery.[149] Airlift and attack units, including the 105th Airlift Wing's C-17 operations, incorporate joint exercises for airdrop, medevac, and logistics, such as coordinated training with Vermont Air Guard to enhance multi-platform response. The 174th Attack Wing integrates Agile Combat Employment in exercises like Advanced Wrath 25, focusing on MQ-9 Reaper deployment and precision strikes. Support roles, like firefighters from the 109th, extend to international training in South Africa for structural fire suppression over two weeks in 2019.[150][151][152]

Achievements and Impacts

Combat and Humanitarian Successes

The 105th Airlift Wing has conducted strategic airlift operations in support of combat missions, including the transport of troops, equipment, and supplies to forward operating bases in theaters such as the Middle East. In 2020, wing personnel executed 73 combat and combat support missions, delivering 9.2 million pounds of cargo across eight military operations.[28] The unit earned the Air Force Meritorious Unit Award for its 2020 performance, recognizing effective sustainment of deployed forces despite operational demands.[28] It received the same award for 2021, highlighting continued proficiency in expeditionary airlift and aeromedical evacuation, including a four-month deployment supporting patient transport from Ramstein Air Base, Germany.[6][159] The 106th Rescue Wing has provided combat search and rescue (CSAR) coverage in Iraq and Afghanistan since 2001, recovering downed pilots, isolated personnel, and civilians in hostile environments using HH-60G Pave Hawk helicopters and HC-130J Combat King aircraft.[160] Wing pararescue teams and combat rescue officers have executed personnel recovery missions by air, land, and sea, contributing to the preservation of U.S. and allied forces amid active threats.[160] These operations underscore the wing's role in mitigating casualties during extended conflicts, though they have incurred losses, such as the 2018 HH-60G crash near Al-Qa'im, Iraq, which claimed four airmen.[160] In humanitarian efforts, New York Air National Guard units responded to the September 11, 2001, attacks with immediate air defense alerts and recovery support at Ground Zero, mobilizing over 8,000 total National Guard members by day's end for security and rescue augmentation.[24] Technical Sgt. Jeremy Powell of the New York Air National Guard was the first U.S. military member to confirm the attacks' implications, initiating NORAD's defensive posture.[23] For natural disasters, the 105th Airlift Wing has delivered relief supplies via C-17 Globemaster III aircraft, including missions supporting post-hurricane recovery in the Caribbean. More than 300 wing airmen flew federal cargo operations following Hurricanes Irma and Maria in 2017, transporting essentials to affected regions.[161] The 106th Rescue Wing has conducted search and rescue in events like Hurricane Florence in 2018, saving 19 civilians alongside Alaska ANG teams, and contributed to broader disaster responses such as wildfires and maritime incidents.[162] These efforts align with the Air National Guard's peacetime mission of emergency assistance, emphasizing rapid deployment and specialized recovery capabilities.[30]

Polar and Scientific Support Missions

The 109th Airlift Wing of the New York Air National Guard provides specialized tactical airlift support to scientific research in polar regions using ski-equipped LC-130H Hercules aircraft, the only such Department of Defense capability for operations on unprepared snow and ice runways.[79] These missions enable resupply and personnel transport to remote Antarctic and Arctic sites where conventional fixed-wing aircraft cannot operate, supporting the National Science Foundation's (NSF) United States Antarctic Program and Arctic research initiatives.[33] In Antarctica, the wing participates in Operation Deep Freeze, deploying annually from October to March during the austral summer to deliver cargo, fuel, and passengers to stations including McMurdo and the Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station.[163] For the 2024-25 season, the 109th executed 79 missions with LC-130s, transporting essential supplies to key research sites across the continent before concluding operations on March 18, 2025.[33] This marked the unit's 37th year of continuous support, involving up to 400 airmen rotating through forward operating locations like Williams Field near McMurdo Station.[164] In Greenland, missions run from April to August, aligning with Arctic summer conditions, to sustain NSF-funded research at sites such as Summit Station and Thule Air Base.[99] The 2025 Greenland season began on April 18, focusing on logistical resupply of personnel, equipment, and fuel to isolated scientific outposts, with operations concluding by late August or early September.[165] These efforts, ongoing since the mid-1970s, also include training for polar airdrops and ice runway operations at sites like Raven Camp.[166]

Recognition and Awards

The 105th Airlift Wing received the Air Force Meritorious Unit Award for exceptional conduct in executing combat support operations over 90 consecutive days, marking its second consecutive such honor following similar recognition for prior periods.[167][168] This award underscores the wing's direct support to combat operations, including airlift missions that earned individual aircrews the Distinguished Flying Cross with Valor device for heroism in high-risk environments.[169] The 109th Airlift Wing's 139th Aeromedical Evacuation Squadron was named the top unit in the Air National Guard for patient care and evacuation proficiency, securing the award for the second consecutive year based on operational evaluations.[170] The wing's Maintenance Group also won the Air Force Maintenance Effectiveness Award in the medium aircraft category for 2023, recognizing superior aircraft readiness and sustainment in polar operations.[171] Additionally, the 109th Operations Center earned an Air Force logistics award for outstanding mission contributions.[172] The 106th Rescue Wing claimed the top squadron prize at the Air Force's Guardian Challenge rescue competition in 2024, excelling in timed rescue scenarios, pilot performance, and special missions aviator skills across combined team scores.[173] Wing personnel involved in a 2017 maritime rescue operation received Air Force Commendation Medals with Valor for heroism in adverse conditions, highlighting the unit's pararescue expertise.[110] State-level recognitions include the New York Adjutant General's awards for outstanding personnel management and recruiting, such as the 2024 honor given to a 105th Airlift Wing captain for administrative excellence in supporting over 1,000 Guardsmen.[174] Individual Airmen from across the New York Air National Guard have also earned Air Force-wide distinctions, including selections as top recruiters and enlisted aircrew members based on national performance metrics.[175][176]

Challenges and Criticisms

Operational and Logistical Hurdles

The New York Air National Guard's 109th Airlift Wing faces pronounced operational hurdles in polar missions due to the unforgiving Arctic and Antarctic environments, where whiteout conditions, high winds, and extreme cold frequently disrupt flight schedules and compromise aircraft performance. During Exercise Arctic Edge 24 in May 2024, unforgiving weather and mechanical issues hindered transport operations for Joint Special Operations Forces, underscoring the persistent risks of low-visibility landings on unprepared snow and ice surfaces.[177] These challenges demand rigorous pre-mission preparations, including specialized ski gear testing and crew acclimatization, yet still result in mission aborts or delays when visibility drops below operational thresholds.[178] Logistical strains are exacerbated by the aging LC-130H Hercules fleet, which has exceeded 30 years of service and incurs elevated maintenance costs from corrosion, specialized ski undercarriage wear, and cold-weather component failures. In December 2022, an LC-130 became stranded at Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station after a tail elevator control cable snapped during a routine supply run, requiring a multi-state team of National Guard Airmen to conduct repairs in subzero temperatures without standard hangar facilities.[179] Reliability concerns persist, with reports indicating that only five of the ten aircraft in the fleet were fully operational at times, prompting U.S. Senator Chuck Schumer to advocate for $200 million in upgrades in August 2024 to address these deficiencies.[180] Parts scarcity for these unique ski-equipped variants further complicates sustainment, as the U.S. operates the world's only such heavy airlift capability, limiting supplier options and extending downtime.[181] Across Antarctic science support missions, such as Operation Deep Freeze, logistical hurdles include coordinating remote supply chains for fuel, tires, and hydraulics ill-suited to permafrost and ice runways, often leading to deferred maintenance and reduced sortie rates.[178] While the 105th Airlift Wing's C-17 operations encounter fewer environment-specific issues, broader Air National Guard equipment readiness reports highlight systemic delays in modernization, indirectly affecting New York units through shared logistics pipelines and fiscal constraints. These factors collectively strain the Guard's ability to maintain high mission-capable rates in dual state-federal roles.

Impacts of Frequent Deployments

Frequent deployments in the New York Air National Guard, especially the recurring polar missions of the 109th Airlift Wing, place substantial physical and operational demands on personnel, including exposure to extreme cold, high-altitude operations with ski-equipped LC-130 Hercules aircraft, and extended rotations totaling up to five months annually for Antarctic support. These operations involve deploying around 400 Airmen and five aircraft per season to McMurdo Station, executing over 200 missions and accumulating thousands of flight hours in harsh environments that accelerate equipment wear and require heightened vigilance to mitigate risks like frostbite and mechanical failures.[33][182] The cumulative effect of such high operational tempo, compounded by additional global airlift commitments from units like the 105th Airlift Wing's C-17 Globemaster III operations, contributes to personnel fatigue and family separation stresses inherent to reserve component service. National Guard studies indicate that repeated deployments correlate with elevated psychological issues, relationship dissatisfaction, and reintegration difficulties upon return, as civilian jobs and family responsibilities resume amid lingering deployment-related disruptions.[183][184][185] These strains manifest in broader readiness challenges, with research on reserve forces showing that multiple deployments can degrade mental and physical health, increasing error rates and reducing performance in subsequent training or operations, as evidenced in analogous National Guard units facing similar optempo.[186][187] While the New York Air National Guard has maintained relatively strong retention amid national trends of reserve component shortfalls, internal discussions highlight ongoing pressures from deployment frequency on recruiting and sustaining skilled maintainers and aircrew.[188][189] High deployment rates also exacerbate equipment maintenance burdens, as seen in the need for specialized platforms to reduce technician fatigue during C-17 sustainment, underscoring causal links between optempo and logistical wear that could compromise long-term unit preparedness if not addressed through targeted recovery periods.[190][191]

Domestic Political Uses and Debates

The New York Air National Guard has been deployed domestically under state authority for disaster response, including airlift operations and search-and-rescue missions during events such as hurricanes and wildfires, providing logistical support without direct ground combat roles.[136] In the COVID-19 pandemic, over 7,000 New York National Guard members, including Air National Guard personnel from units like the 105th and 109th Airlift Wings, supported vaccination sites, testing, and logistics, administering more than 4 million doses in what became the largest domestic mobilization in U.S. history.[26] [27] These efforts were directed by Governor Kathy Hochul, who in 2021 considered using Guard members to backfill hospital staffing shortages caused by vaccine mandates for healthcare workers.[192] Political debates have centered on the Guard's drone operations, particularly the 174th Attack Wing's MQ-9 Reaper program at Hancock Field Air National Guard Base in Syracuse. Since 2010, anti-war activists from groups like Upstate Drone Action have conducted monthly protests against the wing's role in remote-piloted strikes abroad, labeling the drones "killer drones" and linking them to post-9/11 targeted killings, with demonstrations blocking base gates as recently as 2022.[193] [194] Local concerns have included the basing of armed unmanned aircraft systems domestically, raised by residents and officials questioning safety and oversight, amid incidents such as a 2020 Reaper crash at Syracuse Hancock International Airport due to operator errorâ€”costing $6 million and initially undisclosed to the publicâ€”and a 2013 crash into Lake Ontario.[195] [196] [197] Wing commanders have defended the program, emphasizing training for precision operations that minimize civilian casualties compared to manned strikes, while noting domestic applications like wildfire surveillance support to states such as California.[136] Vaccine mandates introduced by the Department of Defense in 2021 sparked internal debates affecting retention, with national data showing about 10% of National Guard members non-compliant by mid-2022, leading to discharges including in the Air Force and Guard components.[198] [199] In New York, the Guard's frontline role in promoting vaccinations amplified tensions, as some members resisted the policy amid broader conservative critiques of mandates as infringing on personal liberty and harming unit cohesion; by 2025, Republican legislation sought reinstatement and back pay for those separated.[200] Administrative lapses, such as the 174th Attack Wing's failure to pay $2 million in airport rent from 2016 to 2021 during drone expansions, drew criticism for fiscal oversight but prompted no major partisan rift.[201] These issues highlight tensions between operational necessities and civil liberties concerns, with Guard leadership prioritizing mission readiness over activist demands.
