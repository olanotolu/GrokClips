New Russia Party

The New Russia Party, also known as the Novorossiya Party (Russian: Партия Новороссии), is a pro-Russian separatist political organization founded on 13 May 2014 in Donetsk, Ukraine, by Russian-backed separatists including Pavel Gubarev, who also led the People's Militia of Donbass.[1] The party emerged amid the escalation of conflict in eastern Ukraine following the Euromaidan Revolution, positioning itself as a vehicle for regional autonomy or independence from the Ukrainian government.[1] At its first congress held on 23 May 2014, the party proclaimed an official program outlining a prospective government structure for the unrecognized Federal State of Novorossiya, a proposed confederation encompassing the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics (DPR and LPR) along with additional southeastern Ukrainian oblasts such as Kharkiv, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, and Odesa.[1] This initiative sought to establish a pro-Russian entity modeled on historical imperial concepts of "New Russia," with aspirations for close ties or union with the Russian Federation.[1] Although the formal Novorossiya confederation was effectively abandoned by November 2014 as separatist focus shifted to the individual DPR and LPR entities, the party's ideological framework influenced the governance and political rhetoric in these self-proclaimed republics, which Russia annexed in September 2022.[2] The New Russia Party has been active primarily in the Russian-occupied portions of Donetsk and Luhansk oblasts, where it contributes to the administration and propaganda efforts supporting separatist control.[3] It faces international sanctions from the United States for its involvement in destabilizing activities related to the Ukraine conflict, reflecting its designation as an entity undermining Ukraine's sovereignty.[3] Key characteristics include its emphasis on Russian cultural and linguistic preservation in the region, opposition to Ukrainian central authority, and alignment with Moscow's geopolitical objectives, though its practical influence remains confined to contested territories without broader recognition.[1]

Ideology and Objectives

Core Political Goals

The New Russia Party, founded on May 13, 2014, in Donetsk, articulated its central aim as the establishment of an independent federal state called Novorossiya, comprising territories historically designated as part of the Russian imperial province of the same name, including the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics and extending to regions such as Kharkiv, Odesa, Dnipropetrovsk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson oblasts.[4][5] This objective was formalized at the party's inaugural congress on May 22, 2014, where over 500 delegates endorsed the unification of pro-Russian separatist efforts to secede from Ukraine amid opposition to the post-Euromaidan government in Kyiv.[6][7] The party's program positioned Novorossiya as a sovereign entity to safeguard ethnic Russian populations and Russian-speaking communities from perceived cultural and political marginalization under Ukrainian central authority, emphasizing federalism to grant substantial autonomy to regional republics while maintaining a unified state structure.[4][8] Proponents, including self-proclaimed "People's Governor" Pavel Gubarev, framed this as a restoration of historical Russian lands lost after the Soviet Union's dissolution, with an implicit orientation toward strategic alignment with Russia, including potential integration into the Eurasian Economic Union.[9][10] Secondary goals included the promotion of Russian language and Orthodox Christian cultural dominance in governance and education, rejection of what the party described as "Banderite" Ukrainian nationalism, and the development of a socio-economic model prioritizing industrial revival in Donbas through state intervention and resource nationalization.[11] These positions were advanced through alliances with other pro-separatist groups, aiming to legitimize the confederation via referendums held on May 11, 2014, in Donetsk and Luhansk, which reported overwhelming support for independence despite international non-recognition.) The initiative sought to counter Kyiv's unitary state model by advocating decentralized power-sharing, though implementation stalled after Russia's de facto suspension of the broader Novorossiya project in late 2014.[12]

Nationalist and Separatist Positions

The New Russia Party advanced separatist objectives centered on the creation of a sovereign confederation known as Novorossiya, encompassing southeastern Ukrainian oblasts including Donetsk, Luhansk, Kharkiv, Dnipropetrovsk, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, Mykolaiv, and Odesa. This vision, articulated at the party's inaugural congress on May 22, 2014, sought to detach these territories from Ukrainian control following the 2014 referendums in Donetsk and Luhansk, which the party supported as expressions of local self-determination.[13] The confederation was proposed as a federal union potentially aligned with or integrated into Russia, reflecting a rejection of Kyiv's authority post-Euromaidan events, which party leaders described as an illegitimate coup suppressing Russian-speaking populations.[14] Nationalist positions emphasized the historical and ethnic Russian character of Novorossiya, invoking the 18th-century imperial designation for territories acquired by Russia from the Ottoman Empire and Crimean Khanate. Party founder Pavel Gubarev asserted that these regions "have always been Russian," framing separatism as a restoration of cultural and linguistic unity among East Slavic peoples divided by modern borders.[15] The platform prioritized protection of Russian language rights, opposition to Ukrainian-language mandates perceived as discriminatory, and resistance to Western integration efforts like EU association, which were viewed as threats to Russian identity.[10] In practice, these stances manifested in calls for armed self-defense militias and alliances with pro-Russian forces to secure territorial control, positioning the party as a vanguard against what it termed Ukrainian nationalism's aggressive centralization. U.S. authorities sanctioned the party in December 2014 for destabilizing actions that undermined Ukraine's sovereignty, underscoring its role in fomenting division.[14] While rooted in irredentist narratives from Russian conservative circles rather than grassroots Ukrainian separatism, the ideology drew on grievances over economic decline and cultural marginalization in Donbas since Ukraine's independence.[10]

History

Founding in 2014

The New Russia Party was announced on May 13, 2014, in Donetsk by Pavel Gubarev, a pro-Russian activist who had seized administrative buildings in the city earlier that spring and declared himself the "people's governor" of the self-proclaimed Donetsk Republic.[9] The organization, initially structured as a public-political movement, established its headquarters in Donetsk and positioned itself as a vehicle for consolidating separatist forces amid the escalating crisis in eastern Ukraine following the Euromaidan Revolution.[9] The party's inaugural congress convened on May 22, 2014, in Donetsk, where delegates formalized its structure and leadership, with Gubarev serving as a co-chair.[16] This event marked the official launch, drawing participants from pro-Russian groups seeking to advance regional autonomy or independence.[16] The founding document outlined the party's objective as uniting like-minded individuals to establish a confederation known as Novorossiya, envisioned as a federal entity incorporating Donetsk, Luhansk, and several southeastern Ukrainian oblasts including Kharkiv, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, and Odesa.[17] According to its program, the party prioritized the creation of this sovereign Novorossiya state as a means to exit Ukrainian jurisdiction, emphasizing ethnic Russian interests and opposition to the post-Euromaidan Kyiv government.[17] U.S. authorities later described the party as a unifying force for separatist supporters aiming for independence and alignment with Russia, leading to sanctions against its leadership in December 2014.[14] The initiative reflected broader irredentist aspirations revived from historical Russian imperial concepts of Novorossiya, though it operated primarily within the nascent Donetsk People's Republic amid ongoing armed conflict.[14]

Involvement in Donbas Referendums and Early Conflict

The New Russia Party emerged directly in response to the separatist referendums conducted on May 11, 2014, in the Donetsk and Luhansk regions, where local authorities aligned with pro-Russian militants organized votes on self-determination that reportedly garnered over 89% support for sovereignty from Ukraine among claimed turnouts exceeding 70%. Pavel Gubarev, a key architect of the early unrest, announced the party's founding on May 13, 2014, positioning it as a vehicle to unite "patriots and fighters" who had backed the referendum and to establish a structured political apparatus for the Donetsk People's Republic (DPR).[9][18] Gubarev's prior actions laid the groundwork for the party's separatist orientation; in late March 2014, he proclaimed himself "People's Governor" of Donetsk amid protests involving the seizure of regional administration buildings, contributing to the declaration of the DPR on April 7, 2014.[19] Detained by Ukraine's Security Service on March 6, 2014, for alleged subversive activities, Gubarev was released in early May, resuming his role in mobilizing support for the referendum and subsequent independence claims.[14] The party's inaugural congress, held later in May, focused on drafting a charter and ideology centered on regional autonomy or confederation under the Novorossiya banner, aligning with DPR efforts to legitimize parallel governance amid escalating clashes following Ukraine's Anti-Terrorist Operation initiated on April 14, 2014.[9] In the initial phase of the Donbas conflict, marked by skirmishes between Ukrainian forces and DPR militias through summer 2014, the New Russia Party provided ideological backing and organizational recruitment for separatist structures, though its formal influence was limited by the primacy of armed groups and ad hoc leadership.[14] U.S. authorities later sanctioned the party in December 2014 for actions undermining Ukraine's territorial integrity, citing its role in promoting instability post-referendum.[14] The party's platform emphasized resistance to Kyiv's central authority, framing the early fighting as defense of referendum outcomes against perceived aggression.[18]

Electoral Engagements and Post-2015 Developments

The New Russia Party attempted to engage in the parliamentary elections of the Donetsk People's Republic scheduled for November 2, 2014, by fielding candidates under its banner.[20] Initially, party representatives announced plans to participate as part of the "Free Donbass" electoral bloc, aiming to represent radical separatist interests amid the consolidation of power in the DPR.[20] However, on October 12, 2014, the DPR's Central Election Commission rejected the party's registration, stating that it had failed to convene a mandatory conference to nominate candidates and submit required documentation.[21] [22] This denial highlighted tensions between Gubarev's faction and the emerging DPR establishment, which favored candidates aligned with Alexander Zakharchenko's leadership following his appointment as prime minister in August 2014.[22] The party's exclusion from the 2014 vote prevented any formal representation in the DPR's People's Council, where winning lists included the Free Donbass bloc and others supportive of integration with Russia. No records indicate subsequent participation in DPR elections, such as the 2018 legislative vote or the 2023 assembly elections, reflecting its effective sidelining as power centralized under figures like Zakharchenko (assassinated in 2018) and Denis Pushilin.[23] Post-2015, the party's activities waned following the termination of the Novorossiya confederation project between the DPR and Luhansk People's Republic in May 2015, which eroded its core objective of establishing a broader separatist federation. Gubarev sought limited local influence, including an unsuccessful bid for control of Yasynuvata raion in 2015 and an appointment as its head in February 2016 that faced resistance from DPR authorities.[23] By 2017, the party's political wing had largely lost its capacity to shape DPR governance, overshadowed by militarized structures and pro-Russian administrative consolidation.[24] Gubarev shifted focus to Russian-based nationalist endeavors, including leading a Novorossia movement advocacy group, though these efforts yielded no electoral gains and culminated in his detention in Moscow in July 2023 for unauthorized picketing against military policies.[25] The party itself registered no verifiable electoral or organizational initiatives after 2014, remaining sanctioned by Western entities and dormant amid the frozen Minsk framework.[26]

Leadership and Organization

Pavel Gubarev and Key Founders

Pavel Gubarev, born on February 10, 1983, in Severodonetsk, Ukraine, emerged as the primary founder and leader of the New Russia Party, also known as the Novorossiya Party. Prior to the 2014 events in Donbas, Gubarev had affiliations with the Russian National Unity movement, a Russian nationalist organization, which provided him with activist experience in Donetsk. On March 3, 2014, he proclaimed himself the "People's Governor" of the Donetsk Republic amid pro-Russian protests following the Euromaidan Revolution, positioning himself as a key figure in the separatist movement seeking greater ties with Russia.[9] Gubarev announced the creation of the New Russia Party on May 13, 2014, stating it would unite supporters of establishing an independent federal state of Novorossiya, encompassing Donetsk and Luhansk regions along with other southern and eastern Ukrainian territories historically associated with Russian influence. The party's founding congress was held the following day, May 14, 2014, in Donetsk, formalizing its structure as a social-political movement aimed at separatist objectives. The initiative reflected Gubarev's vision of countering Ukrainian central authority through nationalist mobilization, drawing from earlier pro-Russian demonstrations he helped organize.[9][14] Ekaterina Gubareva, Pavel Gubarev's wife, served as a co-leader of the party and played a prominent role in its early operations. On May 16, 2014, shortly after the founding, she was appointed as the self-proclaimed Minister of Foreign Affairs for the Donetsk People's Republic, indicating her involvement in the broader separatist governance tied to the party. Following an assassination attempt on Pavel Gubarev in October 2014, Ekaterina temporarily assumed leadership of the New Russia Party, underscoring her status as a key foundational figure. No other individuals are prominently documented as co-founders in contemporaneous reports, with the party's inception centered on the Gubarevs' initiative.[9]

Internal Structure and Alliances

The New Russia Party maintained a centralized leadership structure dominated by Pavel Gubarev, its founder and chairman, who directed operations from Donetsk amid the 2014 separatist uprising. The organization originated as a social-patriotic entity formed by pro-Russian activists, with Gubarev—formerly affiliated with the Russian National Unity movement—exercising primary control over decision-making and propaganda efforts.[27] Limited public documentation exists on formal hierarchies or membership tiers, reflecting the party's origins as a wartime political initiative rather than a conventional bureaucratic entity; it prioritized ideological mobilization over institutional complexity, incorporating media assets like the Novorossiya television channel and radio station owned by Gubarev to disseminate its platform. Following an assassination attempt on Gubarev in October 2014, which injured him and prompted temporary leadership shifts, the party adapted by aligning with electoral blocs such as Free Donbas for participation in Donetsk People's Republic assemblies, indicating flexible internal arrangements to sustain influence amid conflict.[28] Ekaterina Gubareva, Gubarev's wife, emerged as a co-leader, contributing to continuity in the party's operations within the self-proclaimed Federal State of Novorossiya. These adaptations underscored a pragmatic, leader-centric model vulnerable to targeting but resilient through familial and activist networks. In terms of alliances, the party forged ties with broader pro-Russian separatist structures in the Donbas, including coordination with the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics under the Novorossiya confederation framework announced in 2014.[29] Its founding congress in late May 2014 drew participants from Russian nationalist circles, including former members of the Russian National Unity, fostering red-brown coalitions that blended far-right ethnonationalism with leftist anti-Western rhetoric to advance secessionist aims.[30] These partnerships extended to joint electoral efforts and military support networks, though tensions arose from ideological divergences and Russian state influence, as evidenced by the party's designation alongside groups like the Communist Party of the DPR in international sanctions lists. Gubarev's personal connections to Russian imperialists and Donbas militias further embedded the party in a web of informal alliances prioritizing territorial control over unified doctrine.[31]

Electoral Performance and Representation

Participation in Separatist Polls

The New Russia Party was established on May 13, 2014, two days after separatist authorities in the self-proclaimed Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics conducted referendums on independence from Ukraine, limiting direct party involvement in those votes.[9] Its founder, Pavel Gubarev, had been a leading figure in the preceding pro-Russian unrest since March 2014, including occupations of government buildings that facilitated the referendum organization, though the party itself emerged to consolidate the Novorossiya project in response to the plebiscites' outcomes.[32] Separatist officials claimed turnout exceeded 75% with 89.07% approval for sovereignty in Donetsk and 96.2% in Luhansk, figures disputed internationally for lacking independent verification and occurring amid armed conflict.[32] In the subsequent unrecognized general elections held on November 2, 2014, in the Donetsk and Luhansk entities—intended to form parliaments and head of republics—the New Russia Party sought participation but was denied registration by local authorities. DPR leader Alexander Zakharchenko's electoral framework explicitly excluded the party, citing failure to meet formal requirements such as holding a founding conference, alongside barring the Communist Party of Ukraine. Gubarev publicly condemned the process as rigged to favor Kremlin-aligned factions, arguing it suppressed broader nationalist voices advocating fuller Novorossiya integration beyond just Donbas. These polls, conducted under military conditions with restricted access for observers, yielded victories for pro-separatist blocs but drew widespread rejection from Ukraine and Western governments as illegitimate. The party's exclusion reflected internal separatist power dynamics, where Russian influence prioritized compliant structures over independent actors like Gubarev's group, which emphasized ideological purity in reviving historical Novorossiya territories. No subsequent separatist-wide polls occurred before the Novorossiya confederation's informal dissolution in 2015, shifting focus to DPR/LPR autonomy under Minsk agreements, though the party maintained advocacy for unification polls in aligned circles.[27]

Results in Donetsk People's Republic Assemblies

The New Russia Party, led by Pavel Gubarev, attempted to register for the 2 November 2014 general elections to the People's Council of the Donetsk People's Republic but was denied by the republic's Central Election Commission for failing to meet procedural requirements, such as holding a founding conference.[33] In response, Gubarev and the party urged residents to boycott the vote, criticizing it as illegitimate and controlled by rival factions aligned with Alexander Zakharchenko's Donetsk Republic organization, which secured a parliamentary majority.[34] As a result, the party obtained no seats in the 100-member assembly. Similar exclusion occurred ahead of the 11 November 2018 elections for the People's Council, where Gubarev's candidacy was rejected due to insufficient valid signatures, preventing party participation.[35] The Donetsk Republic again dominated, winning 71 of 100 seats, while limited opposition from blocs like Free Donbass—initially linked to New Russia elements but restructured without them—gained minimal representation. The party's marginalization reflected broader consolidation of power by Russia-backed leadership in the DPR, sidelining early separatist nationalists like Gubarev who advocated broader Novorossiya ambitions. No subsequent DPR assembly elections were held following Russia's 2022 annexation of the region, integrating it into Russian federal structures without distinct party contests.

Controversies

Associations with Far-Right and Nationalist Groups

The New Russia Party, established in May 2014 amid the Donetsk separatist movement, attracted affiliations from Russian nationalist factions, including elements aligned with far-right ideologies. Party figures, such as local activists in Donetsk, publicly claimed leadership over sections of the Russian National Unity (RNU), a neo-Nazi organization founded in 1990 known for its skinhead militancy, antisemitism, and advocacy of a totalitarian "Russian Slavic Republic." These claims were bolstered by acknowledgments of RNU-provided military training to party members in the early 2000s, reflecting the party's integration of extremist nationalist networks into its separatist agenda.[36] The party's inaugural congress in Donetsk drew attendees from diverse nationalist currents, including monarchists, Orthodox radicals, and far-right groups, positioning Novorossiya as a symbolic unifier of imperial white, fascist brown, and spiritualist black nationalist strands in Russian irredentism. Prominent ideologues like Aleksandr Dugin, architect of Eurasianist nationalism with ties to traditionalist and anti-Western extremism, and Aleksandr Prokhanov, a Soviet-nostalgic nationalist writer, participated, signaling the party's role in bridging intellectual and militant nationalist spheres.[36][37] These associations extended to operational alliances in the Donbas conflict, where New Russia Party structures overlapped with volunteer battalions and ideological cells promoting ethnic Russian supremacy and anti-Ukrainian separatism, often echoing RNU's rhetoric of Slavic unity against perceived Western encroachment. While the party framed such ties as patriotic resistance, critics from Ukrainian and Western analyses highlight how they facilitated recruitment from transnational far-right volunteers, including European extremists fighting under Novorossiya banners in 2014-2015.[36]

Criticisms from Ukrainian and Western Perspectives

Ukrainian authorities have condemned the New Russia Party as an illegitimate separatist organization that promoted the violent overthrow of the constitutional order and territorial dismemberment of Ukraine, classifying its activities as treasonous under Article 109 of the Ukrainian Criminal Code.[38] The party's involvement in the May 2014 "referendums" in Donetsk and Luhansk regions was dismissed by Kyiv as a sham conducted under armed coercion, lacking any legal basis and serving Russian geopolitical aims rather than reflecting local will.[38] Ukrainian officials, including those from the Security Service of Ukraine (SBU), have described the party as a vehicle for Russian hybrid warfare, with its founders like Pavel Gubarev accused of coordinating with Russian intelligence to incite unrest and facilitate annexation.[39] From a Western perspective, the party has been criticized for undermining Ukraine's sovereignty and contributing to regional instability, prompting sanctions from the United States and European Union. In December 2014, the U.S. Treasury Department designated Gubarev and associated separatist entities under Executive Order 13660 for actions threatening Ukraine's peace, security, and stability, viewing the party as part of Russian-backed efforts to create frozen conflicts.[14] The EU similarly added Gubarev to its sanctions list in July 2014, citing his role as self-proclaimed "people's governor" of Donetsk and leader of the party in fomenting rebellion and rejecting Ukraine's territorial integrity, with measures including asset freezes and travel bans.[40] [41] Western analysts, including those at institutions like the University of Cambridge, have highlighted the party's promotion of the "Novorossiya" concept as a failed propaganda construct with minimal empirical support among Russian-speaking Ukrainians, evidenced by surveys showing low endorsement even in Donbas before the escalation.[42] Critics in both Ukrainian and Western sources have pointed to the party's associations with extremist rhetoric, exemplified by Gubarev's September 2023 statements advocating the "destruction" of Ukrainians through military force and re-education camps, which were widely decried as genocidal and reflective of irredentist ideology driving the conflict.[43] Ukrainian perspectives emphasize the human cost, attributing thousands of civilian deaths and displacement since 2014 directly to separatist actions enabled by such groups, while Western commentary frames the party as emblematic of Russia's broader strategy to destabilize post-Maidan Ukraine through proxy militias and disinformation.[19] These views underscore a consensus that the party's pursuits lacked democratic legitimacy, relying instead on external military support amid documented limited grassroots backing.[39]

Debates on Legitimacy and Russian Influence

The New Russia Party, established on May 13, 2014, in Donetsk amid the pro-Russian separatist uprising, has faced persistent scrutiny over its legitimacy as a grassroots political entity versus a vehicle for external orchestration. Ukrainian authorities and Western analysts have characterized it as lacking authentic local mandate, arguing that its formation coincided with Russian military incursions and intelligence operations that enabled the separatist takeover of regional institutions. For instance, declassified communications attributed to Vladislav Surkov, a key Kremlin aide, reveal directives for staging the party's inaugural congress in Donetsk on May 24, 2014, with attendees purportedly drawn from southeastern Ukraine but under Moscow's coordination to propagate the Novorossiya confederation concept.[44] This aligns with empirical patterns of Russian proxy governance in the Donetsk People's Republic (DPR), where party affiliates like Pavel Gubarev assumed roles amid the displacement of Ukrainian administrative control by June 2014, facilitated by unmarked Russian forces documented in OSCE reports.[45] Russian influence is evidenced by the party's ideological alignment with Moscow's irredentist narrative of historical Novorossiya, a term revived in 2014 propaganda to justify territorial claims, despite pre-uprising surveys indicating limited separatist sentiment in eastern Ukraine. A 2014 study of public opinion in Donetsk and Luhansk oblasts found that while preferences for federalization or autonomy within Ukraine hovered around 30-40%, outright secession or union with Russia garnered under 20% support before the escalation of violence, suggesting the party's platform amplified rather than reflected baseline local views.[45] Critics, including reports from the U.S. Treasury, highlight sanctions imposed on Gubarev and associated figures in December 2014 for undermining Ukraine's sovereignty through Russian-backed destabilization, underscoring financial and logistical dependencies that undermined claims of independence.[14] Proponents within Russian-aligned circles counter that the party's emergence responded to the Euromaidan events' perceived disenfranchisement of Russian-speaking populations, citing the May 11, 2014, DPR referendum—where over 89% reportedly endorsed sovereignty—as validation, though international observers dismissed it due to coercive conditions, absence of verifiable voter rolls, and parallel Russian troop movements.[42] Debates intensify over the party's post-2015 electoral engagements in DPR assemblies, where it secured representation despite operating in a non-democratic environment shaped by martial law and media monopolies. Western assessments, such as those from the Atlantic Council, portray these polls as shams engineered to legitimize Russian control, with turnout figures inflated and opposition suppressed, rendering outcomes non-representative of unmanipulated will. In contrast, DPR officials and party leaders maintain legitimacy derives from sustained local participation, pointing to alliances with trade unions and veteran groups as organic bases, though independent verification remains elusive amid restricted access for external monitors. By 2022, following Russia's annexation of Donetsk oblast territories, the party's framework merged into Russia's United Russia-dominated system, further fueling arguments that its prior autonomy was illusory, sustained only by Moscow's patronage rather than enduring regional consensus. These contentions reflect broader geopolitical causal dynamics, where Russian strategic imperatives—evident in leaked planning documents—prioritized hybrid warfare outcomes over verifiable popular sovereignty, as corroborated by sequential events from the party's founding to its integration into annexed structures.[44]

International Response

Sanctions and Designations

The New Russia Party, formally known as the Social-Political Movement "New Russia," was designated by the United States Department of the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) on December 19, 2014, as a Specially Designated Global Terrorist (SDGT) and blocked entity under Executive Order 13660.[14] This action targeted the party for its role in supporting separatist activities in eastern Ukraine, including efforts to undermine the country's territorial integrity through the promotion of the Novorossiya confederation project.[14] The designation froze any U.S.-linked assets and prohibited transactions with the party by U.S. persons. Pavel Gubarev, the party's founder and former leader, was individually sanctioned by the U.S. on the same date for orchestrating the seizure of government buildings in Donetsk and leading pro-Russian protests that escalated into armed separatism.[14] The European Union imposed asset freezes and travel bans on Gubarev effective March 6, 2014, citing his responsibility for occupying regional administration buildings and declaring himself "People's Governor" of Donetsk, actions deemed to destabilize Ukraine.[46] While the EU directly sanctioned Gubarev and the related Novorossiya Movement established in November 2014, the party's activities were encompassed in broader restrictive measures against entities supporting Russian-backed separatism. Ukraine's government classified the party as an illegal separatist organization, banning its operations within Ukrainian territory as part of countermeasures against pro-Russian groups following the 2014 annexation of Crimea and conflict in Donbas. These designations have persisted into 2025, with no reported delistings, reflecting ongoing international efforts to counter entities aligned with Russian influence in the annexed regions of Donetsk and Luhansk.[47]

Support from Russian-Aligned Views

Russian state media and officials have portrayed the New Russia Party's advocacy for the Novorossiya confederation as a defensive response to the 2014 Ukrainian government's alleged suppression of Russian-speaking populations in Donetsk and surrounding regions, framing it as self-determination rooted in historical Russian imperial territories annexed by Ukraine under Soviet rule.[48] [49] President Vladimir Putin referenced "Novorossiya" in a February 2022 statement, noting its tsarist-era status as Russian land encompassing southeastern Ukraine, implicitly endorsing the separatist narrative the party promoted since its May 2014 founding congress in Donetsk.[50] In his September 2022 annexation speech, Putin invoked Novorossiya's historical figures like Rumyantsev, Suvorov, and Catherine the Great to justify reintegration, aligning with the party's original goal of establishing an independent federal state separate from Kyiv's control, which Russian-aligned commentators viewed as liberation from post-Euromaidan nationalism.[51] State broadcaster RT has consistently depicted the party's efforts and the broader "Russian Spring" protests as organic resistance against Ukrainian "Nazification," emphasizing industrial and cultural ties to Russia predating Bolshevik redrawings of borders.[52] [53] Russian political leaders have echoed this support; A Just Russia party chairman Sergei Mironov stated in September 2025 that the 2022 reunification of Donbas and Novorossiya regions marked the "beginning of the epoch of reviving the Russian world," crediting it with restoring unity disrupted by 20th-century events.[54] Nationalist and conservative figures, including those linked to early party congresses, received coverage in pro-Russian outlets as champions of ethnic Russians facing discrimination, with the party's platform seen as aligning with Moscow's strategic interests in countering NATO expansion.[55] These views, propagated through outlets like RT and Sputnik, prioritize causal narratives of historical continuity and ethnic kinship over Western characterizations of separatism as Russian-orchestrated aggression, though Russian media's state control introduces a pro-Kremlin lens that amplifies unifying rhetoric while downplaying internal separatist factionalism post-2014.[56] By 2022, with formal annexation, Russian-aligned perspectives recast the party's initial aims as prescient, integrating its territories into federal structures like United Russia party branches in Donetsk.[57]

Current Status

Post-Annexation Integration

Following Russia's formal annexation of the Donetsk People's Republic (DPR), Luhansk People's Republic (LPR), Kherson Oblast, and Zaporizhzhia Oblast on September 30, 2022, the New Russia Party ceased independent electoral participation, as political structures in the territories aligned with Russia's federal system under parties such as United Russia. Local elections held in these regions on March 10–18, 2023, for city councils and regional assemblies featured established Russian parties like United Russia, which secured majorities (e.g., 68% in DPR assemblies), with no registration or candidacy from the New Russia Party reported. This integration reflected a broader consolidation of power by Moscow, sidelining pre-annexation separatist entities in favor of centralized governance. Party co-founder Ekaterina Gubareva briefly held an administrative role as deputy head of the Kherson region's military-civilian administration from June to November 2022, overseeing aspects of occupation governance before her detention by Russian authorities on November 15, 2022, amid internal purges, followed by release days later. She was subsequently discharged from the position in early 2024. Pavel Gubarev, the party's leader, maintained influence outside formal politics through media ownership, including the "Novorossiya" TV channel, radio station, and DNR LIVE portal in Donetsk, where he broadcast pro-annexation content supporting Russia's special military operation. In April 2023, Gubarev co-initiated the "Club of Angry Patriots," a nationalist group criticizing perceived Kremlin restraint and urging intensified military action in Ukraine. By 2025, the party's original nationalist platform advocating Novorossiya confederation had no verifiable organizational revival, with Gubarev publicly decrying peace negotiations as capitulation while operating from Russian-controlled Donetsk. Sanctions imposed by the U.S. in December 2014 under Executive Order 13660 persisted, designating the party for undermining Ukraine's sovereignty, limiting any formal reintegration into Russian party politics. Ideological remnants persisted informally among pro-Russian activists, but empirical evidence shows no seats, funding, or congresses post-annexation, indicating effective absorption or dissolution into broader Russian administrative and media ecosystems.

Activities in Annexed Territories as of 2025

The New Russia Party, established in May 2014 to advocate for a confederation spanning southeastern Ukraine including the eventually annexed regions, has seen its operations in Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics adapt to Russia's 2022 annexation framework. By 2025, party-affiliated groups maintain involvement in local social initiatives, such as veteran support networks tied to Donbas militias, reflecting the original separatist ethos amid ongoing military integration into Russian forces. These efforts prioritize ideological continuity, including promotion of historical Novorossiya narratives to bolster territorial claims, though subordinated to federal oversight. In Kherson and Zaporizhzhia regions, residual party branches focus on grassroots mobilization for Russian administrative embedding, including documentation drives for residency and participation in anti-Ukrainian information campaigns, as evidenced by localized online organizing. However, electoral influence remains marginal, with United Russia dominating 2023 local polls in these territories by over 70% of votes, underscoring the party's niche role in a Kremlin-controlled political landscape. No major independent initiatives were reported in 2025, consistent with tightened controls post-annexation to prioritize war sustainment over autonomous movements.[58][27]
