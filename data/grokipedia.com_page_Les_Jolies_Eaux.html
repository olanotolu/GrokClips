Les Jolies Eaux

![Mustique Island viewed from the International Space Station][float-right] Les Jolies Eaux is a luxury villa located on a private peninsula at the southernmost tip of Mustique Island, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, originally designed and built in the early 1970s for HRH Princess Margaret, Countess of Snowdon.[1][2] The estate, meaning "beautiful waters" in French, was commissioned from British architect and set designer Oliver Messel, who crafted it in his signature romantic, theatrical style featuring a central courtyard, expansive lawns, and panoramic views over the Caribbean Sea toward neighboring islands.[1][2] The villa served as Princess Margaret's primary retreat on Mustique, where she vacationed regularly until selling it in 1996 to businessman David Lindeman, who undertook significant renovations while preserving Messel's original design elements.[3] Today, Les Jolies Eaux accommodates up to ten adults and two children across five en-suite bedrooms, including a master with private plunge pool, and offers amenities such as an infinity-edge pool, dedicated staff including a chef and butler, and access to nearby private beaches like Gelliceaux Bay.[1][2] It remains one of Mustique's most sought-after rentals, with weekly rates ranging from $33,000 to $48,500 depending on the season, reflecting its historical prestige and secluded, high-end appeal.[1][2]

Historical Background

Land Acquisition and Early Development

In 1958, Colin Tennant, a British aristocrat and later Lord Glenconner, acquired the uninhabited island of Mustique in the Grenadines for Â£45,000, envisioning its transformation into an exclusive retreat.[4] As a close friend of Princess Margaret, Tennant gifted her a 10-acre (4-hectare) plot at the southern tip of the island in 1960, shortly before her marriage to Antony Armstrong-Jones, as a wedding present.[5] [6] This parcel, named Les Jolies Eaux after the surrounding turquoise waters, provided Margaret with her first personal property ownership, distinct from royal estates.[7] Following the acquisition, early development focused on establishing a private villa suited to Margaret's preferences for seclusion and tropical elegance. In the mid-1960s, she commissioned her uncle-in-law, the theatrical designer Oliver Messel, to create a bespoke residence integrating neo-Georgian elements with the island's natural contours.[3] Construction commenced around 1966, involving site clearing, foundation work, and incorporation of local materials to harmonize with the peninsula's beaches and bays, though progress was gradual amid Mustique's rudimentary infrastructure.[8] Messel's plans emphasized low-profile structures to preserve views toward the Atlantic and Caribbean, marking the estate's evolution from raw land to a functional holiday compound by the early 1970s.[9]

Construction Under Princess Margaret's Commission

Princess Margaret commissioned the construction of Les Jolies Eaux following her receipt of a 10-acre plot on Mustique's southern peninsula as a wedding gift from Colin Tennant, 3rd Baron Glenconner, in 1960.[8][5] She selected Oliver Messel, a British stage designer who had transitioned into architecture and was the uncle of her husband, Antony Armstrong-Jones (Lord Snowdon), to lead the project, leveraging his expertise in creating romantic, site-integrated structures.[10][8] Construction commenced in 1969, with Messel overseeing a design that fused Caribbean colonial influences with Palladian symmetry and neoclassical details, emphasizing indoor-outdoor flow through Regency-style elliptical arches and vibrant tropical accents.[8] The build incorporated Messel's signature elements, such as sage-green shutters and doors, alongside custom furniture crafted by local artisans in Regency and 18th-century styles to evoke theatrical elegance amid the island's natural terrain.[8] The resulting neo-Georgian villa featured five bedrooms, two swimming poolsâ€”one with a built-in grottoâ€”and positioning for panoramic 270-degree views across the Atlantic and Caribbean seas.[11][8] The project concluded in the early 1970s, enabling Margaret's initial stays and establishing the villa as a private retreat integrated into Mustique's rugged landscape, with trapezoid-shaped guesthouses enhancing privacy and harmony with the site's cliffs and waters.[12][8] Messel's approach prioritized empirical adaptation to the tropical environment, using durable materials suited to humidity and sea exposure while avoiding over-engineering, though specific build costs or labor details remain undocumented in primary accounts.[13]

Architectural Design

Oliver Messel's Vision and Style

Oliver Messel, a renowned British stage designer who transitioned to tropical architecture in his later years, envisioned Les Jolies Eaux as a harmonious blend of theatrical elegance and Caribbean naturalism, drawing on his experience in set design for operas and films to create a residence that functioned as an intimate stage for entertaining.[8] Commissioned by Princess Margaret in the early 1970s, the villa embodied Messel's "Caribbean Messel" style, characterized by romantic symmetry, classical Palladian influences, and open indoor-outdoor layouts that maximized panoramic ocean views from its hillside perch overlooking Gelliceaux Bay.[8] [14] This approach prioritized airy ventilation through perpetually open sea-facing windows and perfumed pavilions around a central pool, using local coral stone for a weathered patina that integrated the structure with the island's rugged terrain.[14] [6] Key stylistic features included trapezoid-shaped guesthouses, Regency-style elliptical arches framing principal rooms like the master bedroom and living areas, and whimsical garden follies adorned with classical statues, all furnished with custom Regency and chinoiserie pieces to evoke a sense of refined escapism.[8] Messel's palette featured his signature light sage "Messel Green" on shutters and doors, complemented by vibrant tropical accents that reflected his theatrical roots in evoking drama through color and form, while low-slung modernist lines ensured practicality in the humid climate with coral stone balustrades and plantation shutters.[8] [6] The design's grand courtyard entry leading to an expansive hall underscored a vision of communal luxury, setting a precedent for Mustique's villa archetype of openness and landscape symbiosis.[14] Princess Margaret expressed satisfaction with the result, stating, "Iâ€™m very pleased with what Oliver did for Les Jolies Eaux. This is my house, the only square inch in the world I own, and Oliver was a major contributor to it," highlighting Messel's success in translating her desire for a personal retreat into an enduring architectural statement.[8]

Structural Features and Site Integration

Les Jolies Eaux exemplifies Oliver Messel's architectural approach, blending neoclassical elements with Caribbean colonial influences through low-profile structures featuring open-plan interiors, cedar vaulted ceilings, and bespoke wooden detailing.[15] [8] The main residence includes five bedrooms, a central great room, and a dining pavilion, supplemented by trapezoid-shaped guesthouses that house additional lodging.[8] Regency-style elliptical arches frame key views, while vibrant tropical colors and Messel Green accents on shutters and doors enhance the aesthetic harmony.[8] The villa's layout emphasizes indoor-outdoor connectivity, with porticos, verandas, and decks extending living spaces toward the sea, supported by two swimming poolsâ€”one an infinity pool aligned with the horizonâ€”and outdoor pavilions for al fresco activities.[16] [8] This configuration promotes natural ventilation through sea breezes, minimizing reliance on mechanical systems in the tropical climate.[16] Positioned on a ten-acre hillside peninsula at Mustique's southern tip, the estate integrates axially from the courtyard entrance through the pool to the ocean, capturing a 270-degree panorama of the Atlantic and Caribbean.[16] [8] This orientation maximizes privacy on the isolated headland, formerly part of a sugar plantation, while gardens and proximity to secluded beaches embed the structure within the island's undulating terrain and coastal ecosystem.[3] The design respects the site's contours, avoiding disruptive earthworks to preserve views and ecological flow.[8]

Ownership Timeline

Princess Margaret's Possession and Personal Use

Princess Margaret maintained possession of Les Jolies Eaux from the villa's completion in 1973 until 1996, when she transferred ownership to her son, David Armstrong-Jones, Viscount Linley (later 2nd Earl Snowdon), reportedly to mitigate inheritance tax liabilities.[17] [18] The underlying 10-acre plot had been gifted to her in 1960 by Colin Tennant, 3rd Baron Glenconner, as a wedding present following her marriage to Antony Armstrong-Jones, providing her with the only property she personally owned outside of royal estates.[6] [19] During this period, she treated the villa as a secluded retreat from public scrutiny, honeymooning there briefly in 1960 and later sharing it with her children, Sarah Chatto and David Linley, to foster a semblance of normal family life amid her otherwise constrained royal duties.[19] Her personal use centered on biannual vacations, typically in October-November and February, spanning several weeks each, where she escaped the formalities of palace existence for relaxation and informal socializing.[17] Activities included late-night gatherings with piano sing-alongs, dancing, and cocktail hoursâ€”often featuring her preferred gin and tonic with precisely two ice cubesâ€”hosted among a bohemian circle of friends and celebrities such as Mick Jagger.[17] [20] The villa served as a backdrop for her eight-year relationship with Roddy Llewellyn, beginning around 1973; she first invited him there in February 1974, and subsequent trips, including one in 1976, drew tabloid attention after photographs of the couple surfaced, accelerating the breakdown of her marriage to Armstrong-Jones and culminating in their 1978 divorce.[19] [21] [22] Despite its role in personal scandals, Les Jolies Eaux represented a rare domain of autonomy for Margaret, who frequented the island into the 1990s, leveraging its isolation for privacy while contributing to Mustique's reputation as an elite hideaway.[17] Health issues later curtailed her visits, but the property remained a symbol of her preference for unscripted leisure over ceremonial obligations.[17]

Transfer to Heirs and Market Sale

In 1996, Princess Margaret transferred ownership of Les Jolies Eaux to her son, David Armstrong-Jones (then Viscount Linley, later 2nd Earl Snowdon), as a means to avoid substantial inheritance tax obligations following her anticipated death.[23] This preemptive gift ensured the property, valued at the time in the millions of pounds, bypassed her estate upon her passing in 2002, aligning with estate planning strategies common among high-net-worth individuals to minimize fiscal burdens on heirs.[23] Armstrong-Jones, who reportedly never resided there extensively, inherited the villa amid his mother's declining health and her reduced visits to Mustique due to mobility issues.[23] Armstrong-Jones placed the property on the market shortly thereafter, culminating in its sale in 1999 for a reported Â£2.4 million to American venture capitalist James Murray.[23] [24] The transaction marked the end of direct royal family ownership, transitioning Les Jolies Eaux from a private retreat to a privately held asset available for potential commercial use, such as rentals, under its new proprietor.[24] The sale price reflected the villa's prime location on Mustique's southern tip, its historical prestige, and the island's exclusivity, though it drew some commentary on the swift divestment of a familial icon.[23]

Post-Sale Ownership and Renovations

In 1999, David Armstrong-Jones, 2nd Earl of Snowdon, sold Les Jolies Eaux to American businessman Jim Murray for Â£2.4 million.[25] The property has remained in private ownership since, with Murray maintaining it as a high-end vacation retreat.[17] Following the acquisition, Murray commissioned renovations to modernize the villa while honoring Oliver Messel's mid-20th-century design principles. These works, executed by DGP Architects, encompassed upgraded bedrooms and bathrooms oriented toward enhanced ocean vistas, the construction of a new master bedroom pavilion and dining pavilion, and the installation of a vanishing-edge infinity pool integrated into the landscape.[16] The updates emphasized structural improvements for contemporary comfort, including better airflow and privacy features, without altering the site's tropical integration or Messel's theatrical motifs.[9] Post-renovation, Les Jolies Eaux transitioned to a rental property, accommodating luxury guests at rates starting around Â£26,000 per week during peak season.[26] This shift has sustained its role as a secluded estate on Mustique's southern tip, preserving historical elements amid functional enhancements.[27]

Usage and Social Role

Notable Residents and Visitors

Les Jolies Eaux served primarily as the private residence of Princess Margaret, Countess of Snowdon, from its completion in 1973 until she transferred ownership to her son, David Armstrong-Jones, Viscount Linley, in 1996 to avoid inheritance taxes.[17] She visited biannually, typically in October or November and February, treating the villa as her sole personal property and a refuge from royal duties.[17] Following the transfer, Viscount Linley owned the estate until its sale in 2017, though no records indicate extended residency by him or other heirs.[17] Among notable visitors, Roddy Llewellyn, Princess Margaret's companion during their relationship from 1973 to 1981, frequently stayed at the villa, where elements of their affair originated amid its secluded setting. In 1977, Queen Elizabeth II and Prince Philip visited Mustique and resided briefly at Les Jolies Eaux at Margaret's invitation, allowing the monarch to experience the property firsthand during a rare private family trip.[11] [28] Margaret's children, Sarah Chatto and Viscount Linley, also visited as family members during her ownership, though specific dates remain undocumented in public accounts.[17] The villa's role as a discreet retreat limited detailed records of additional guests, emphasizing privacy over publicity, with no verified accounts of other celebrities residing there during Margaret's tenure despite the island's broader appeal to figures like Mick Jagger and David Bowie.[21] Post-ownership, as a rental property since around 2018, it has hosted high-profile renters, but specific names are not publicly disclosed due to confidentiality agreements.[29]

Transition to Rental Property

Following the sale of Les Jolies Eaux in 2001 to American businessman Jim Murray, the property underwent significant renovations to convert it from a private residence into a luxury vacation rental.[17][16] Dalgliesh Gilpin Paxton Architects led the redesign, incorporating modern features such as a vanishing-edge infinity pool, enhanced porticos for ocean views, and updated interiors while preserving elements of the original Oliver Messel design, including tropical motifs and site-integrated structures.[16] These changes emphasized guest amenities like a dedicated media room, plunge pool in the master suite, and staff quarters to support high-end hospitality services.[2] The transition facilitated broader access to the villa, aligning with Mustique's model of private ownership combined with seasonal rentals to generate revenue amid high maintenance costs for such estates.[17] By the early 2000s, Les Jolies Eaux was listed for weekly rentals through island-affiliated agencies, accommodating up to 10 guests across five bedrooms with full staff including chef, housekeeper, and gardener.[30] Rental rates, subject to 11% government tax and 10% island fee, ranged from $33,000 in low season to $47,000 in peak periods as of 2025.[4] This shift maintained the property's exclusivity while ensuring its financial viability, with bookings managed to preserve privacy on the 10-acre peninsula site.[29]

Legacy and Contemporary Status

Media Depictions and Public Perception

Les Jolies Eaux featured in the third season finale of Netflix's The Crown (2019), depicted as Princess Margaret's private retreat on Mustique where she developed a romantic relationship with Roddy Llewellyn, who assisted in designing its gardens during their visits starting in 1973.[31] The series portrays the villa as a symbol of Margaret's escape from marital discord and royal constraints, aligning with historical accounts of her using the property for relaxation and personal expression.[32] It has also appeared in BBC Two's 2020 documentary series Princess Margaret: The Rebel Royal, which explores her life and highlights the villa's role in her Caribbean sojourns.[33] Tabloid coverage peaked on February 18, 1976, when photographs of Margaret, then 45, swimming and lounging with the 28-year-old Llewellyn at Les Jolies Eaux were published in British newspapers, including The Sun and Daily Mail, igniting a scandal that accelerated her separation from Antony Armstrong-Jones, finalized in 1978.[22] These images, capturing intimate moments at the villa's pools and beachfront, prompted headlines like "Give up Roddy or Quit!" and fueled public debates on royal propriety, with polls showing 60% of Britons disapproving of the affair.[34] Such reporting, often sensationalized by tabloids prioritizing scandal over context, cemented Les Jolies Eaux in public memory as a site of royal indiscretion rather than architectural merit. Public perception of the villa remains intertwined with Margaret's persona as a tragic figure of glamour and rebellion, viewed in biographies and articles as her sole personally owned property and a rare bastion of autonomy amid institutional restrictions.[17] While admired for embodying her aesthetic tastesâ€”evident in its neo-Georgian design and gardensâ€”media narratives frequently emphasize hedonistic elements, including rumored nude sunbathing, extravagant parties, and liaisons with celebrities, portraying it as an extension of her "rebel royal" image rather than a serene family estate.[35] This focus, driven by tabloid and entertainment sources, has overshadowed its role in fostering Mustique's exclusivity, though recent rentals since 2021 have recast it as a luxury heritage asset, drawing interest from those seeking royal-era allure without the attendant controversy.[29]

Recent Developments and Economic Value

Les Jolies Eaux has undergone significant refurbishments and expansions in recent years, converting it into a contemporary luxury rental villa while retaining historical architectural elements originally designed by Oliver Messel. The redesign, led by Dalgliesh Gilpin Paxton Architects, added a private master bedroom suite with plunge pool, enhanced outdoor pavilions, and improved privacy features across its ten-acre Atlantic-facing estate.[16][2][30] These updates have positioned the property for high-end vacation rentals, with availability promoted through specialized agencies as of 2024. The villa now accommodates up to ten guests in five to six bedrooms, supported by full-time staff including a chef, butler, housekeepers, and gardener, emphasizing its appeal for exclusive retreats on Mustique.[29][36] Economically, Les Jolies Eaux commands premium weekly rental rates reflective of Mustique's elite market, where properties benefit from the island's private ownership model and limited supply of 100 villas across 1,400 acres. For 2025, low-season rates (May 1 to December 18) stand at $33,000 per week, rising to $48,500 in high season (January 4 to April 30, 2026), excluding 11% government tax and 10% island fee; longer stays may qualify for discounts.[1][4] This pricing, comparable to other top-tier Mustique villas, underscores the property's sustained value, originally acquired in 1999 for approximately $2 million and enhanced through subsequent investments.[37][38]
