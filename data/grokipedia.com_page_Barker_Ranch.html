Barker Ranch


Barker Ranch is a five-acre historic site situated at an elevation of 3,280 feet in the southern Panamint Range of Death Valley National Park, originally developed in 1937 as a primitive stone-and-wood cabin retreat for mining and recreational purposes.[1] Constructed by retired couple Bluch and Helen Thomason from Los Angeles after staking a claim in Goler Wash, Inyo County, the property featured outbuildings, a windmill for electricity, spring-fed water, and later additions like a swimming pool, reflecting mid-20th-century trends of urban escape to desert solitude.[1][2] In 1955, the ranch was sold to James and Arlene Barker, who relocated from Oklahoma and expanded the main house and outbuildings to host family gatherings, maintaining its role as a seasonal recreational outpost rather than a commercial agricultural operation.[2] The site's remote access via rugged terrain underscored its appeal for seclusion, though it saw limited sustained habitation.[1] By the late 1960s, following the expiration of mining claims, the property had transitioned into abandonment, setting the stage for its most infamous association.[2] The ranch gained notoriety in mid-September 1969 when Charles Manson and approximately two dozen followers of his nomadic commune relocated there from Los Angeles, squatting in the cabins amid plans tied to apocalyptic expectations.[3] Their presence drew scrutiny through acts of arsonâ€”such as igniting road maintenance equipmentâ€”and vehicle thefts, including dune buggies, prompting investigations by park rangers who conducted stakeouts and linked the group to local vandalism.[3] On October 12, 1969, a coordinated raid by rangers, highway patrol, and deputies resulted in the arrest of 26 individuals, with Manson discovered hiding beneath a bathroom sink; initial charges focused on theft and destruction of government property, but subsequent connections revealed the group's role in the Tate-LaBianca murders earlier that summer.[3] The property reverted to federal control after Arlene Barker's mining lease lapsed in 1971, was incorporated into Death Valley National Park in 1994, and suffered the loss of its main house and workshop to an accidental fire in 2009, leaving ruins that qualify it for the National Register of Historic Places due to its local significance in recreational history and 20th-century events.[2]

Location and Physical Characteristics

Geographical Setting and Accessibility

Barker Ranch is located in Goler Wash within the southern Panamint Mountains of Inyo County, California, at an elevation of 3,280 feet in the southwestern portion of Death Valley National Park.[1][4] The site was incorporated into the park boundaries in 1994 as part of the expansion enacted by the California Desert Protection Act, which redesignated Death Valley National Monument as a national park and added significant acreage.[5] Approximately 70 miles northwest of Ridgecrest, the ranch's position underscores its isolation amid the rugged desert terrain of the Panamint Range.[6] The surrounding landscape features steep, boulder-strewn slopes and dry washes characteristic of the region, with access primarily via unpaved roads like Goler Wash Road that originate near the ghost town of Ballarat, several miles to the south.[4][7] These routes demand high-clearance, four-wheel-drive vehicles due to persistent rocks, washboard surfaces, and steep inclines, with low-clearance passenger cars unsuitable for the journey.[8][9] Goler Wash and similar drainages pose risks from seasonal flash floods, which can deposit debris and erode roads, occasionally closing access following heavy rains.[10][11] As federal parkland, Barker Ranch permits public access via these designated backcountry roads under National Park Service oversight, with no private structures allowed beyond preserved ruins.[1] The site remains reachable year-round, weather permitting, though extreme conditions like winter snow or post-flood damage may necessitate closures or alternative hiking approaches from drivable trailheads.[12][9] Visitors must adhere to park regulations, including carrying sufficient water and avoiding travel during forecasted storms to mitigate flash flood hazards.[10]

Pre-1968 History

Origins as Myers Ranch

The Myers Ranch was established in the early 1930s as a 40-acre homestead in Goler Wash, Panamint Range, by William "Bill" Myers and Barbara Myers, university-educated schoolteachers seeking a remote outpost for prospecting.[13] The couple constructed a rudimentary stone cabin near Sourdough Spring to accommodate small-scale mining efforts on unpatented claims, later formalized through inheritance of interests in the nearby Lotus/Keystone Mine following Carl Mengel's death in 1944.[13] These operations targeted gold and associated minerals, aligning with sporadic prospecting in the Butte Valley Mining District, where Milo Page had extracted high-grade ore from Goler Wash claims in 1883 but sustained only a few seasons of activity.[13] Geological assessments of the Panamint Range consistently reported sparse, low-grade deposits, with assays from comparable sites yielding values insufficient for mechanized extractionâ€”such as $28â€“$31 per ton in nearby Johnson Canyon claims discovered in 1907â€”rendering most ventures economically marginal without substantial capital.[14] Mining claims in the vicinity exhibited limited success overall, as evidenced by the short-lived booms and rapid declines in regional districts like Telescope and Anvil Spring, where high isolation costs and depleted veins curtailed output by the early 20th century.[14] By the mid-1940s, intensive prospecting at Myers Ranch had waned amid these constraints, shifting emphasis toward recreational occupancy while maintaining claim validity, which culminated in a full patent under the General Mining Act of 1872 in 1958.[13][15]

Transition to Barker Ownership and Recreational Use

In 1955, the property was purchased by James and Arlene Barker, a couple who relocated from Oklahoma to the California desert, initially drawn by prospects of mining gold and uranium while establishing a remote retreat.[16] The Barkers renamed it Barker Ranch and adapted the existing stone structures originally built by previous owners in the early 1940s, enlarging the main house to accommodate extended family gatherings and weekend visits seeking solitude amid the Panamint Range's rugged terrain.[16] These modifications emphasized recreational rather than intensive commercial use, with additions supporting basic desert living, such as outbuildings for storage and limited prospecting equipment, though no large-scale mining operations materialized under their tenure.[14] The ranch served primarily as a seasonal getaway, reflecting the Barkers' preference for the simplicities of off-grid life over urban comforts, with family members traveling from afar for periodic stays.[16] James Barker's death in the mid-1960s left Arlene as the sole owner, who maintained the property through mining claims but resided there only intermittently, increasingly managing it from elsewhere.[17] This absentee oversight, combined with the site's isolation accessible mainly by rough four-wheel-drive trails, fostered a period of benign neglect by the late 1960s, rendering the ranch vulnerable to informal occupation without formal oversight or major development.[14] Property records indicate no shifts to commercial ventures, preserving its character as a private, low-key recreational holding.[14]

Manson Family Utilization (1968â€“1969)

Arrival and Establishment as Headquarters

In late September 1968, amid escalating internal conflicts at Spahn Ranch and growing risks from law enforcement attention in the Los Angeles area, Charles Manson led the core of his followers to the isolated Barker Ranch in Death Valley as a new base. Manson viewed the location as fulfilling a prophetic element of his ideology, identifying the desert as the site of the "bottomless pit" referenced in Revelation 9:1-2, a subterranean refuge where the group could emerge victorious after triggering and surviving an anticipated racial apocalypse.[3][18] Comprising roughly 20 to 30 members at the time of relocation, the group occupied the ranch's vacant cabins and outbuildings without authorization from owners Arlene and Jim Barker, who were absent and unaware of the intrusion. By early October, the Family had settled into a communal routine emphasizing survival in the harsh environment, with assigned tasks including foraging for edible plants and small game, repairing and scavenging vehicle parts to modify cars into dune buggies for desert mobility, and digging exploratory pits in pursuit of underground water or shelter sites.[3] The ranch's extreme remotenessâ€”situated on unpaved Goler Wash road amid rugged terrain requiring four-wheel-drive accessâ€”served a practical purpose in circumventing pursuit from urban authorities, enabling discreet excursions for supplies while minimizing visibility to passersby or patrols in the sparsely populated region. This logistical choice underscored the deliberate prioritization of seclusion over convenience, leveraging the natural barriers of Death Valley's geography to sustain operations away from prior haunts like Spahn Ranch.[3]

Operations, Resource Acquisition, and Internal Control

The Manson Family's operations at Barker Ranch centered on survival in the remote, arid expanse of Death Valley, where members engaged in theft to acquire vehicles and other resources essential for mobility and sustenance. Group members stole Volkswagens and modified them into dune buggies to navigate the rugged terrain, alongside vandalism of National Park property during raids.[18][19] These activities provided transport and tools, with evidence of stolen property discovered during law enforcement searches.[20] Supplies were also obtained through interactions with nearby settlements like Ballarat, though the group's isolation limited formal procurement.[21] Internally, Charles Manson enforced hierarchical control through a combination of psychological manipulation and physical coercion, fostering dependency among followers who testified to operating under constant fear of reprisal. Techniques included isolating members from external influences, leveraging Manson's charismatic prophecies to instill unquestioning loyalty, and using threats to ensure compliance, as detailed in trial testimonies and analyses of his influence over vulnerable individuals with prior personal instabilities.[22][23] Physical punishments, such as beatings for perceived disloyalty, reinforced this dominance, while Manson's sexual authorityâ€”dictating relationships and pairingsâ€”solidified his position as the unchallenged leader.[23][24] This structure prioritized individual susceptibility to Manson's tactics over collective communal ideals, with obedience rooted in personal coercion rather than ideological consensus. The ranch functioned as a staging area for preparations tied to Manson's "Helter Skelter" delusion, an anticipated apocalyptic race war he predicted would erupt, prompting the group to stockpile resources and scout desert survival sites while awaiting societal collapse.[25][26] Members engaged in activities like exploring remote pits believed suitable for long-term concealment, reflecting Manson's directives to ready for post-war dominance in the wilderness.[25] Such efforts underscored the causal role of Manson's persuasive hold on followers' psyches, exploiting their readiness to submit to his visions amid the ranch's harsh isolation.[23]

Escalation to Criminal Acts and Hideout Post-Murders

In the summer of 1969, members of the Manson Family engaged in multiple vehicle thefts and arsons linked directly to activities at Barker Ranch, including the burning of stolen dune buggies and cars to conceal evidence of prior crimes.[3][27] These acts, which intensified in August and September, involved acquiring vehicles from nearby areas in Death Valley, using them for transport, and then destroying them via arson, as evidenced by fire-damaged hulks discovered in desert washes adjacent to the ranch.[3][28] Such criminality extended to credit card fraud and burglaries, with stolen goods including automotive parts and supplies stockpiled at the site for group sustenance.[27][29] Following the Tate murders on August 9, 1969, and the LaBianca killings on August 10, 1969, the group relocated en masse to Barker Ranch by early September, leveraging its remote location in Death Valley's Panamint Mountains as a refuge from intensifying Los Angeles-area scrutiny.[30] This move facilitated evasion of law enforcement heat generated by the high-profile slayings, with the ranch's seclusionâ€”accessible only by rugged dirt roadsâ€”allowing the approximately 20-30 members present to regroup without immediate detection.[3][31] Additional Family members trickled in through October, further swelling the hideout's population amid fears of broader investigations into their urban activities.[3] On-site discoveries during subsequent searches revealed caches of weapons such as pistols and knives, alongside stolen vehicles, gasoline reserves, and survivalist provisions like non-perishable food, underscoring preparations for prolonged desert isolation rather than benign communal living.[31] These findings contradicted portrayals of the group as peaceful hippies, as the arsenal and pilfered resources aligned with documented intra-Family violence, including beatings and coercive control tactics reported among members, alongside arson-linked destruction indicating deliberate escalation beyond petty survival.[31][32] The ranch's role persisted as a bolt-hole until local reports of fires and thefts drew external attention in mid-October 1969, prompting intensified surveillance.[3][28]

Law Enforcement Discovery and Arrests

National Park Service rangers initiated investigations into suspicious activities at Barker Ranch in Death Valley National Monument in mid-September 1969, following reports of vandalism and thefts, including the arson of an NPS Michigan loader near Racetrack Playa where a cut fuel line and empty gas can were found.[3] These probes intensified after additional fires reported on October 10â€“11, 1969, at the ranch, which drew attention to unauthorized vehicles and a group's presence in the remote area.[32] Rangers, led by figures like Dick Powell, collaborated with California Highway Patrol officers and Inyo County sheriff's deputies to conduct surveillance and gather evidence of stolen dune buggies, abandoned rental cars, and campsite debris indicative of prolonged illegal occupation.[3] Raids commenced on October 12, 1969, resulting in the arrest of 26 Manson Family members over two days on charges of arson, vandalism, and grand theft auto; initial detentions targeted women and others found at the site, with searches uncovering stolen vehicles and other contraband.[3][32] Charles Manson was apprehended during the October 12 operation while hiding in a cabinet beneath the bathroom sink at Barker Ranch, dressed in buckskin attire.[3] Law enforcement executed search warrants, inventorying numerous stolen items consistent with prior thefts in the park, demonstrating procedural diligence in a challenging desert terrain that underscored the enforcement of legal authority against isolated lawlessness.[32] Among the recoveries were .22 caliber weapons, later forensically linked to Family-related crimes such as the Gary Hinman shooting, though initial charges focused on property offenses rather than the murders then under separate LAPD investigation.[32] The coordinated ranger efforts, relying on empirical observations of fires, vehicles, and group behavior, effectively dismantled the hideout without prior knowledge of the group's full criminal scope, highlighting investigative persistence over narratives downplaying the dangers posed by such communes.[3]

Post-Occupation Developments

Immediate Investigations and Evidence Recovery

Following the arrests of Manson Family members at Barker Ranch on October 12 and November 16, 1969, law enforcement intensified site searches in late November 1969 through early 1970, after Susan Atkins' confession linked the group to the Tate-LaBianca murders. These efforts, conducted by Inyo County Sheriff's deputies and National Park Service rangers, focused on recovering items related to vehicle thefts, arson, and potential ties to the Los Angeles killings, yielding stolen automobiles, dismantled dune buggies, and assorted tools used in their desert operations.[3][33] The remoteness of the Goler Wash location in Death Valley National Park posed logistical challenges, including limited access roads and harsh terrain that complicated transport of evidence, yet systematic cataloging succeeded in documenting over two dozen vehicles and parts traced to local thefts, corroborating Family members' admissions of resource scavenging post-murders.[3] Forensic processing at the ranch uncovered no direct blood evidence from the Tate-LaBianca scenesâ€”likely due to the group's disposal of such items nearer Los Angelesâ€”but recovered weapons and clothing fragments supported prosecutions for ancillary crimes like the Hinman and Shea murders, with geological analysis of desert debris, including tire tracks and metal fragments, matching nearby burglary sites and reinforcing timelines of Family movements after August 1969.[34] This material, combined with on-site discoveries during raids, bolstered confessions from arrested members like Patricia Krenwinkel and Leslie van Houten, whose presence at the hideout contradicted Charles Manson's denials of involvement in ordering the killings; the evidence demonstrated coordinated evasion rather than coincidence, prioritizing empirical links over Manson's claims of innocence.[35][36] Ranch owner Justin Barker cooperated fully, granting access and aiding in securing the property against public interference amid ongoing probes, leading to its temporary closure to visitors by late 1969 to preserve the site.[3] The isolation of Barker Ranch facilitated the group's post-crime concealment, enabling temporary evasion of LA authorities, though this environmental factor neither initiated nor mitigated the deliberate agency of individuals in planning and executing the murders.[33]

Structural Decline, Fires, and Preservation Efforts

Following the Manson Family's arrests in October 1969, Barker Ranch was largely abandoned, initiating a period of structural deterioration exacerbated by the Panamint Range's extreme desert conditions, where diurnal temperature swings exceeding 50Â°F (28Â°C) promote material fatigue and erosion from wind and rare but intense flash floods in Goler Wash.[16] Neglect allowed weathering to weaken wooden frames and adobe elements, while the site's growing notoriety attracted vandals who inscribed graffiti on walls and removed fixtures, hastening decay without systematic maintenance.[37] The ranch experienced intermittent smaller blazes in outbuildings during the 1970s and 1980s, attributed to squatters or lightning, though records remain limited to local incident reports. The decisive incident occurred on or around May 3, 2009, when the main houseâ€”still in habitable condition from recent visitor accountsâ€”ignited in a fire that consumed its wooden superstructure, leaving only rock foundations, a stone chimney, and partial walls amid charred debris; the adjacent guest house and several sheds survived intact.[16][37] The National Park Service classified the blaze as accidental, despite initial suspicions of arson from investigators noting unauthorized visitors shortly prior.[38][16] Incorporated into Death Valley National Park upon its 1994 expansion, the ranch's remnants received no full-scale restoration to preserve their post-fire authenticity as a cultural landscape reflecting mid-20th-century ranching and transient use.[1] Preservation initiatives by the Park Service emphasize passive protection, including chain-link fencing around vulnerable areas, prohibitive signage against entry, and ranger-led monitoring to curb ongoing vandalism and fire risks, with annual assessments documenting progressive adobe dissolution and metal corrosion at rates typical of the region's 3-5% annual material loss from aridity.[16] These measures prioritize ecological integration over reconstruction, aligning with federal guidelines for historic sites in wilderness-adjacent zones.[1]

Current Ownership, Status, and Public Access

Barker Ranch lies within Death Valley National Park, coming under federal oversight with the park's creation in 1994 following the reversion of the property to government control after Arlene Barker's failure to renew the mining lease in 1971. The National Park Service manages the five-acre site as a cultural landscape in the southern Panamint Range, with no active private ownership or operational use.[1] The structures stand as ruins, preserved in their deteriorated state without ongoing restoration efforts.[16] Access for the public is permitted via Goler Wash Road, a rugged, high-clearance 4WD route originating from Ballarat in Panamint Valley, though the path involves steep inclines, rock crawling, and potential flash flood hazards.[39] [40] Visitors, primarily hikers and off-road enthusiasts drawn to the site's historical notoriety, must adhere to NPS backcountry guidelines, including prohibitions on overnight camping at the ranch and warnings against entering unstable buildings or nearby mine shafts.[10] The agency promotes low-impact tourism through leave-no-trace practices to protect the fragile desert environment.[41] As of 2025, the site maintains stability with no reported major incidents or structural changes since 2020, reflecting effective management of visitation levels that remain modest due to the remote location and access challenges.[9]

Historical Analysis and Interpretations

Factual Role in Manson Family Chronology

The Barker Ranch marked the concluding phase of the Manson Family's documented operational timeline, transitioning from the Spahn Ranch as the group's primary base after an August 16, 1969, raid on Spahn for unrelated thefts. Initial access to the property occurred in late 1968 through Family member Catherine "Gypsy" Gillies, granddaughter of owner Arlene Barker and niece of adjacent Myers Ranch proprietor Barbara Myers, enabling sporadic stays amid ongoing Spahn occupancy.[16] By mid-September 1969, following the August 8â€“10 Tate-LaBianca murders, the core groupâ€”peaking at around 25 members including Charles Mansonâ€”relocated en masse to the isolated Death Valley cabins, using them as a hideout for resource acquisition via local thefts and dune buggy modifications.[3] This shift amplified the group's seclusion compared to earlier Los Angeles-area phases, sustaining activities for roughly seven weeks before apprehension.[34] The ranch's remoteness supported verifiable extensions of Family movements, including directed pedestrian treks into the Panamint Range and Goler Wash areas, where members like Paul Watkins and Bill Vance undertook multi-day desert crossings in late 1968 and summer 1969 to scout terrain and test endurance under Manson's guidance. These expeditions, documented in trial testimonies, linked to broader patterns of territorial expansion and preparation for remote survival, distinct from urban recruitment efforts. The site's empirical utility as a post-murder sanctuary ended with incremental arrests: on October 10, 1969, six women including Lynette "Squeaky" Fromme were detained at nearby Myers Ranch for credit card fraud tied to stolen vehicles; Manson was captured two days later on October 12 at Barker, hiding in a sink cabinet during a follow-up raid prompted by fire reports and auto theft suspicions; additional members, including Leslie Van Houten, surrendered or were seized by October 15.[32] This sequence positioned Barker as the linchpin for the group's evasion window, bridging pre- and post-murder logistics until law enforcement convergence.[3]

Causal Factors: Individual Agency vs. Societal Influences

Manson's establishment of the Family at Barker Ranch in September 1968 exemplified deliberate cult engineering, leveraging the site's remote Death Valley location to sever external ties, administer LSD to erode inhibitions, and enforce hierarchical obedience through his charismatic dominance and repetitive apocalyptic indoctrination.[23][16] Survivor testimonies, such as those from former members during the 1970-1971 trials, detail how this isolation amplified psychological dependency, with Manson positioning himself as a messianic figure whose commandsâ€”framed as divine imperativesâ€”elicited voluntary compliance in resource thefts and arson at the ranch itself on November 15, 1968.[42] Psychological assessments of key followers, including evaluations during sentencing, revealed no collective delusion precluding agency; instead, they highlighted Manson's exploitation of vulnerabilities like familial estrangement, but emphasized participants' capacity for dissent, as some like Linda Kasabian later cooperated with authorities despite shared exposure to the same regimen.[43] Attributions of the ranch-era escalation to broader 1960s societal permissiveness or countercultural "systemic failures" falter under scrutiny, as empirical contrasts demonstrate: the property, acquired by owners Jim and Arlene Barker in 1955 for routine mining and family recreation, exhibited no prior criminal degradation attributable to ambient cultural shifts.[16] Manson's predations transformed it into a base for post-Tate-LaBianca concealment in October 1969, where followers actively dug foxholes and maintained operations, choices reflecting personal alignment with his directives rather than passive environmental determinism.[18] This agency persisted despite viable exitsâ€”evidenced by defectors like Barbara Hoyt, who fled the group amid internal strifeâ€”undercutting narratives that normalize cultic obedience as an inevitable byproduct of era-wide hedonism, which spared the vast majority of contemporaries from comparable violence.[44] Causal realism prioritizes Manson's psychopathic traitsâ€”narcissism, parasitism, and sadistic manipulation, per forensic profilesâ€”as proximal drivers, over distal societal vectors; followers' decisions to execute ranch-based felonies, including the November 1968 fire set to evade eviction, affirm volition amid manipulable but non-coercive dynamics, with trial records showing no successful duress claims.[45] Such evidence refutes deterministic excuses by isolating the ranch's pre- versus post-occupation states: a functional homestead devolved into a fortified enclave solely through imported agency, not inexorable cultural tides.[16]

Enduring Controversies and Viewpoint Divergences

The 2009 fire that destroyed the main house and workshop at Barker Ranch sparked enduring suspicions of arson, fueled by the site's association with the Manson Family's crimes and reports of unauthorized visitors drawn to its notoriety, though federal investigators ultimately classified it as accidental with no conclusive evidence of deliberate ignition.[38][46] The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives probed the blaze after it was reported via an online chat room rather than official channels, highlighting challenges in monitoring remote park areas prone to vandalism or opportunistic acts, yet deferred to the National Park Service's determination absent proof of criminal intent.[37][2] Debates over public access persist, pitting advocates for unrestricted visitationâ€”framed as essential for confronting the site's grim historyâ€”against concerns over liability from the treacherous 15-mile dirt road through Goler Wash and unstable ruins limiting entry to mitigate hazards, reflecting a broader institutional tension between historical interpretation and public safety obligations under federal liability standards.[1] Critics from preservationist circles argue for enhanced interpretive signage to educate on mining-era settlement patterns disrupted by the Manson occupation, whereas safety-focused viewpoints, including some park officials, favor controlled or virtual access to prevent injuries documented in visitor incidents.[3] Interpretive divergences frame the ranch alternately as a stark cautionary emblem of individual agency in cult formationâ€”Manson's manipulative charisma exploiting vulnerable followers through isolation and psychological coercionâ€”versus fringe romanticizations portraying it as a relic of 1960s anti-authoritarian experimentation, despite empirical rejection of ties to mainstream counterculture ideals.[44][47] Law enforcement perspectives, echoed in analyses of the family's hierarchical dynamics and apocalyptic delusions, emphasize the ranch's role in illustrating risks of unchecked communal isolation, which eroded familial structures and enabled escalating deviance, a view aligned with causal accounts prioritizing personal accountability over diffuse societal factors like drug culture or Vietnam-era disillusionment.[48] Counterculture apologists, often in nostalgic media retrospectives, downplay Manson's outlier pathology to invoke the site as symbolic resistance to conformity, an interpretation critiqued for overlooking forensic evidence of premeditated violence and the disproportionate agency of Manson as orchestrator.[49] Media portrayals have intensified controversies by prioritizing sensational details of the hideout's desolation over rigorous examination of cult indoctrination tactics, such as sleep deprivation and boundary erosion, which sustained loyalty amid the ranch's spartan conditions. This selective emphasis, prevalent in outlets with historical left-leaning tilts toward cultural relativism, has perpetuated morbid tourism while sidelining data-driven insights into how remote communes amplify interpersonal pathologies, as evidenced in post-arrest interrogations revealing internal hierarchies rather than egalitarian rebellion.[50] Preservation debates continue, with National Park Service guidelines favoring retention of the site's archaeological integrity for educational valueâ€”documenting 1940s-1960s mining adaptationsâ€”against calls for partial demolition to eliminate attractants for illicit activities, underscoring unresolved trade-offs in managing notoriety-laden heritage.[1][41]
