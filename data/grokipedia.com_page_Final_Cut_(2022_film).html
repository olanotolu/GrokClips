Final Cut (2022 film)

Final Cut (French: Coupez!) is a 2022 zombie comedy film written and directed by Michel Hazanavicius.[1] The film serves as a remake of the 2017 Japanese cult hit One Cut of the Dead, reimagining its premise of a low-budget film crew shooting a one-take zombie movie that descends into chaos when actual undead threats emerge, delivered through a meta structure blending horror, farce, and behind-the-scenes satire.[2] Starring Romain Duris as the beleaguered director Rémi, alongside Bérénice Bejo, Grégory Gadebois, and Finnegan Oldfield, it explores the frictions of independent filmmaking amid escalating absurdity.[1] Premiering out of competition as the opening-night selection at the 2022 Cannes Film Festival, Final Cut received mixed critical reception, praised by some for its gory ingenuity and tribute to cinema while critiqued by others as grating or uneven in execution.[3][4] Hazanavicius, an Academy Award winner for directing the silent film The Artist (2011), employs the project to amplify themes of creative desperation and collaborative mayhem inherent in genre production.[2]

Background and development

Origins in the original Japanese film

One Cut of the Dead (original Japanese title: Kamera o tomeru na!, meaning "Don't Stop the Camera!") is a 2017 zombie comedy film written and directed by Shin'ichirō Ueda.[5] Released on November 4, 2017, in Japan, the film follows a low-budget film crew led by an eccentric director attempting to shoot a one-take zombie movie in an abandoned water filtration plant, only for the production to descend into chaos as apparent real zombies emerge.[6] The narrative unfolds in a single 37-minute continuous shot for its opening act, mimicking the in-universe film's style, before shattering the fourth wall to reveal a meta-commentary on the filmmaking process itself, including improvisational mishaps, actor dynamics, and budgetary constraints during reshoots.[7] This structure blends horror tropes with backstage farce, emphasizing the perseverance required in independent cinema.[6] Produced on a micro-budget of approximately ¥3 million (around $25,000–$27,000 USD), the film was shot primarily in one location with a small cast of mostly theater actors, many of whom improvised extensively under Ueda's guidance to capture authentic frustration and creativity.[8] Ueda, drawing from his experience in low-budget projects, conceived the story as a tribute to the trials of guerrilla filmmaking, incorporating elements like a undead actress and a producer's interference to highlight real-world production tensions.[6] Despite opening on just two screens in Japan with minimal promotion, it achieved extraordinary commercial success, grossing ¥3.12 billion domestically (equivalent to about $28 million USD at contemporary exchange rates), returning over 1,000 times its production cost through word-of-mouth and repeat viewings.[6] This performance elevated it to cult status internationally, inspiring remakes and underscoring the viability of innovative, constraint-driven storytelling in genre films.[9] The success of One Cut of the Dead directly prompted its adaptation into Final Cut, with French director Michel Hazanavicius acquiring remake rights to reimagine the premise for a new audience while retaining core structural twists.[10] Hazanavicius cited admiration for Ueda's economical ingenuity and meta-humor, incorporating narrative links such as a Japanese producer character (played by the original film's actress Takayuki Hirata's colleague) commissioning a live broadcast version, framing Final Cut as a pseudo-sequel in spirit.[7] This origin reflects broader trends in global cinema where high-ROI Japanese indies influence Western interpretations, though Final Cut adapts cultural specifics—like French theater troupe dynamics—for its setting.[11]

Adaptation process and creative decisions

Michel Hazanavicius first encountered the Japanese film One Cut of the Dead (2017), directed by Shin'ichirô Ueda, during the COVID-19 lockdown in 2020 while developing a separate script about a chaotic film production set. Producer Vincent Maraval had acquired remake rights to the low-budget cult comedy, which prompted Hazanavicius to adapt it, viewing the original's structure—a 37-minute opening single take followed by a meta reveal—as a personal challenge that aligned with his interest in filmmaking mishaps and collective creativity.[12][13] Unlike typical remakes that obscure their source material, Hazanavicius deliberately embraced the homage, publicly acknowledging One Cut of the Dead to honor Ueda's ingenuity and boost its visibility in France, where pre-remake viewership was under 2,000. He retained the core script fidelity, including the plot of a zombie film shoot devolving into real chaos during a mandated one-take, but infused French cultural specifics, such as family tensions involving school pressures and workplace dynamics reflective of European cinema production. Creative alterations included adding a musician character absent in the original and incorporating meta-layers like Japanese producers observing the shoot, drawing from Hazanavicius's 35 years of on-set experience to emphasize ego clashes, improvisation, and the "miracle" of collaborative filmmaking.[12][14][15] For visual distinction, Hazanavicius opted for digital cinematography with high-saturation color grading to separate the acts, contrasting the original's uniform aesthetic, while selecting an abandoned horse racing venue to evoke a George A. Romero-inspired zombie atmosphere enhanced by John Carpenter-esque scoring. Casting emphasized personal ties: his wife, Bérénice Bejo, as the harried producer; their daughter, Simone, as an assistant; and Romain Duris as the obsessive director, shifting from the original's unknown Japanese actors to established French stars for broader appeal. The 35-minute opening sequence demanded 5.5 weeks of rehearsals and four filming days to achieve choreographed stunts, practical effects, and intentional "sloppiness" mimicking a flawed low-budget production, with an on-set injury to Bejo causing a one-week delay that serendipitously aligned with weather needs.[15][13][12] These decisions prioritized subverting audience expectations through form-content interplay, amplifying the comedy's cheesiness while critiquing professional filmmaking pretensions, though Hazanavicius noted the remake's higher production values risked diluting the original's raw DIY charm.[14]

Synopsis

Film-within-a-film sequence

The film-within-a-film sequence forms the opening 37 minutes of Final Cut and is presented as a single, unbroken take depicting the production of a low-budget zombie horror film titled One Cut.[16][17] This segment, filmed in an abandoned water purification plant outside Paris, follows director Rémi Camerlynck (Romain Duris), who insists on capturing the entire movie in one continuous shot to impress a Japanese streaming executive and secure funding amid financial desperation.[16][18] The inner narrative centers on special forces agent Ava (Matilda Lutz), who battles her zombified ex-boyfriend (Finnegan Oldfield) with an axe in the plant's dimly lit corridors, only for the scenario to spiral into chaos as "real" undead—triggered by Rémi unlocking an ancient curse for authenticity—invade the set.[19][17] Rémi's abusive demands exacerbate tensions: his wife Nadia (Bérénice Bejo), doubling as lead actress and makeup artist, is bitten and transforms into a zombie, forcing her to improvise attacks on their real-life daughter Luna (Simone Hazanavicius), cast as a hidden undead child.[16][17] Intoxicated cinematographer Philippe (Grégory Gadebois) struggles to maintain focus while capturing the mayhem, as prima donna actor Raphaël (Théo Cholbi) panics and the crew resorts to graphic, on-the-fly violence involving severed limbs and blood sprays.[16][20] Rémi's obsession with completing the take leads to escalating improvisation, including producer Marie (Marianne Basler) wielding a cleaver and assistant director Jérôme (Nicolas Manhet) coordinating amid screams and gore, blurring lines between scripted horror and apparent genuine peril.[18][21] The sequence builds to a frenetic climax of mass "deaths," with Rémi himself perishing on camera, before abruptly rolling faux credits, suggesting the production has catastrophically failed.[17][22] This raw, handheld-style footage emphasizes technical bravado—a genuine 32-to-37-minute single shot with minimal cuts—highlighting the crew's desperation and Rémi's tyrannical control.[23][18]

Behind-the-scenes revelation

Following the chaotic conclusion of the opening sequence, Final Cut discloses that the preceding 37-minute segment constitutes the raw, unedited footage from a live television broadcast of the film's internal production: a low-budget French remake of the 2017 Japanese cult hit One Cut of the Dead. This gimmick—shooting an entire zombie horror episode in one continuous take at an abandoned warehouse—was devised by producer Nadia (Bérénice Bejo) as a desperate cost-cutting measure to fulfill contractual obligations to an obscure cable channel and salvage a project plagued by overruns.[2][24] The revelation reframes the apparent supernatural outbreak as the byproduct of human dysfunction, emphasizing the perils of improvised filmmaking under duress. The narrative rewinds one month to unpack the pre-production backstory, centering on director Rémi (Romain Duris), depicted as a tyrannical, cash-strapped visionary whose obsession with the one-take format stems from idolizing the original Japanese film's success. To staff the production, Rémi coerces his estranged family into participation, casting his adult daughter Justine (Matilda Lutz) as the axe-wielding lead actress despite her acting inexperience and personal reservations, and his wife as a zombie extra amid their strained marriage. Interpersonal conflicts escalate: the sound engineer harbors unrequited feelings for Justine, leading to off-script sabotage; a shady investor pressures the crew; and rehearsals devolve into farce, underscoring the causal chain from creative hubris to logistical collapse.[10][9] This meta-dissection occupies roughly two-thirds of the runtime, transitioning into a mockumentary-style epilogue set one year later that traces the viral afterlife of the botched take. The raw footage's authenticity—born of genuine mishaps like simulated decapitations turning perilously real and betrayals mimicking undead assaults—propels the remix to unexpected commercial triumph, healing familial rifts and validating the director's methods. Hazanavicius layers additional self-referentiality by drawing from his own life, with Bejo (his real-life spouse) embodying the producer and Lutz channeling familial unease, critiquing the exploitative dynamics of indie cinema while celebrating resilience in collaborative chaos.[25][26] The structure thus privileges empirical realism over horror tropes, attributing the "revelation" to verifiable production pressures rather than fantasy, though detractors note its fidelity to the source mutes the original's improvisational spark.[27]

Cast and characters

Romain Duris portrays Rémi, a struggling director attempting to complete a low-budget zombie film in a single take, who also plays the zombie-controlling Higurashi within the film's inner narrative.[2][4] Bérénice Bejo stars as Nadia, Rémi's wife and a key crew member, doubling as Natsumi, a character in the zombie sequence who faces undead threats alongside her on-screen husband.[2][4] Grégory Gadebois appears as Philippe Rolland, the film's producer exerting pressure on the production, who embodies Hosoda, a military figure in the embedded zombie story.[2] Finnegan Oldfield plays Raphaël Barrelle, an arrogant lead actor cast as the zombie-controlling Ken in the inner film, contributing to the chaotic on-set dynamics.[2] Matilda Lutz is cast as Ava, an inexperienced actress serving as the female lead, who performs as Chinatsu, the daughter character central to the zombie plot's family reunion motif.[2] Supporting roles include Sébastien Chassagne as a sound technician and Raphaël Quenard in additional crew positions, enhancing the portrayal of the amateur production team's dysfunction.[28]

Production

Pre-production challenges

Producer Vincent Maraval acquired the remake rights to the 2017 Japanese film One Cut of the Dead during the first COVID-19 lockdown in France in early 2020. At the same time, Michel Hazanavicius was independently developing a comedy screenplay centered on the chaos of a low-budget film shoot, drawing from his over 30 years of industry experience. Upon viewing the original via a link from Maraval and deeming its structure "clever" yet "challenging," Hazanavicius pivoted to adapt it, merging his initial concept with the acquired property.[13] Hazanavicius penned the screenplay solo, relocating the action to a derelict Paris water treatment plant and infusing French cultural references, such as bureaucratic funding woes and remake oversight by Japanese producers—a meta nod to the film's own status as an adaptation. Key challenges arose in scripting a narrative that honored the original's one-take frenzy and "bad movie" vibe while layering in satire on remakes and professional egos; he aimed to "be faithful, while adding some things here and there," but resisted polishing awkward dialogue to sustain the intentional amateurism. The pandemic's isolation facilitated focused writing but constrained collaborative feedback and scouting, compressing early development into remote efforts.[12][13] Casting demanded actors versed in comedy who could convincingly underperform as hack filmmakers before unveiling skill in behind-the-scenes segments. Hazanavicius enlisted regulars Romain Duris as harried director Rémi and Bérénice Béjo—his wife and frequent muse—as ambitious producer Nadia, alongside Matilda Lutz and Finnegan Oldfield, prioritizing stamina for stunts over star polish. Pre-production planning emphasized choreography for the 35-minute opening sequence, necessitating over five weeks of rehearsals to synchronize improvised mishaps, practical effects, blood squibs, and crane shots without betraying the scripted ineptitude.[12][13]

Principal photography

Principal photography for Final Cut commenced in late April 2021 on the outskirts of Paris, with filming wrapping up by May 2021.[29][30] The production primarily took place at an abandoned racecourse in Bondoufle, France, selected for its 1970s-era architecture featuring blue plastic ceilings and multi-level glass-and-wood structures that facilitated the complex scenography required for the film's zombie movie sequence.[30] A defining element of the shoot was the recreation of the film's opening 30-minute one-take sequence, captured in a genuine continuous shot without invisible cuts or splicing.[30] Cinematographer Jonathan Ricquebourg employed a shoulder-mounted Sony FX6 camera equipped with Sony 24-70mm photo zoom lenses, incorporating autofocus tracking and a motorized variable neutral density filter to handle dynamic movements and variable lighting.[30] For tracking shots, a motorized rickshaw was used, while lighting involved concealed LEDs, mobile Dinolights with half-CTB gels for warmth, and tungsten setups choreographed to shift seamlessly during the take.[30] The sequence demanded precise coordination among actors, special effects teams, camera operators, and lighting technicians, compounded by unpredictable April-May weather that affected outdoor segments.[30] To execute the one-take, the production conducted one month of full-cast rehearsals beforehand, breaking the sequence into a progression of virtual "edited" shots rehearsed as a fluid continuum.[30] Filming of this segment spanned four days, yielding 3-4 takes per day for a total of 15 attempts, with the final cut selecting a single unbroken take.[30] The behind-the-scenes portions of the film, contrasting the faux-documentary style of the opening, were shot using an Alexa LF camera paired with Leitz Thalia prime lenses, such as the 55mm, for a more polished aesthetic.[30]

Release

Festival premiere

Fin de Cut world premiered at the 75th Cannes Film Festival on May 17, 2022, serving as the opening-night selection out of competition.[18][31] The screening took place at the Palais des Festivals, drawing a high-profile audience including festival jurors and celebrities for the red-carpet event that kicked off the 18-day event.[32][33] The choice of Final Cut to open Cannes marked a departure from the festival's typical preference for dramatic or auteur-driven fare, opting instead for Hazanavicius's comedic zombie genre homage, which elicited mixed but generally positive reactions from early critics for its inventive structure mirroring the original Japanese film.[18][34] Although initially slated for a premiere at the Sundance Film Festival in January 2022, the film was withdrawn amid changes to that event's programming, redirecting focus to Cannes where it generated buzz for its meta-narrative and low-budget charm.[35] The premiere screening highlighted the film's bilingual French-Japanese production elements, with key cast members like Romain Duris and Bérénice Bejo in attendance.[36]

Theatrical and home media distribution

The film premiered theatrically in France on May 18, 2022, following its world premiere at the Cannes Film Festival earlier that month.[9] In the United States, Kino Lorber acquired all distribution rights and released it theatrically on July 14, 2023, in a limited engagement.[37][3] For home media, Kino Lorber issued the film on Blu-ray and DVD in the US on September 12, 2023, featuring English subtitles and bonus materials including interviews with director Michel Hazanavicius.[38][39] The same date marked its availability for digital streaming and video-on-demand rental or purchase in the US.[3] Physical editions included a standard Blu-ray pressing, with later limited slipcover variants announced for select markets.[40]

Commercial performance

Box office results

_Following its premiere at the Cannes Film Festival on May 17, 2022, Final Cut opened theatrically in France on the same date, accumulating a total gross of $2,045,734 from that market alone.[41] The film's performance was driven primarily by domestic audiences, with international releases in territories such as Romania (October 21, 2022) and limited screenings elsewhere contributing modestly to overall earnings.[41] In North America, Final Cut received a limited release beginning July 14, 2023, generating $15,124 in the United States and Canada, representing less than 1% of its global total.[1] The opening weekend domestically earned $6,455, with a legs ratio of 2.34, indicating sustained but minimal interest over its theatrical run.[42] Worldwide, the film grossed $2,078,101, falling short of its reported $4.3 million production budget and underscoring a reliance on French box office for viability amid sparse international distribution.[1][43]

Home video and streaming

In the United States, Final Cut was released on Blu-ray and DVD by Kino Lorber on September 12, 2023, following their acquisition of North American distribution rights in January 2023.[37][38] The editions include extras such as a making-of documentary and the theatrical trailer.[39] Internationally, a Japanese Blu-ray edition was issued on December 2, 2022.[44] For digital distribution, the film became available for purchase and rental on platforms including Amazon Video, Apple TV, and Fandango at Home shortly after its limited theatrical run.[45] By 2024, free streaming options expanded to services like Hoopla, Kanopy, and Roku Premium Channel in select regions, with ad-free access on Shudder starting September 9, 2024.[45][46][47] Availability varies by territory, with rentals typically priced at standard video-on-demand rates.

Reception and analysis

Critical reviews

On review aggregator Rotten Tomatoes, Final Cut received a 71% approval rating from 75 critic reviews, with an average score of 6.4/10.[3] Metacritic assigned the film a weighted average score of 51 out of 100 based on 19 reviews, reflecting "mixed or average" reception, with 37% positive, 37% mixed, and 26% negative assessments.[48] Critics frequently highlighted the film's comedic ingenuity and the performances, particularly Romain Duris as the frantic director, while noting its structure as a faithful remake of the 2017 Japanese original One Cut of the Dead. Keith Watson of Slant Magazine described it as "ingenious, funny and surprisingly touching," praising its meta-humor on low-budget filmmaking chaos.[49] Siddhant Adlakha of IGN commended its self-reflexive take on zombie tropes, awarding it 7/10 for delivering laughs through escalating absurdity in the single-take premise.[50] Keith Uhlich echoed this in a blog review, calling it a "consistently clever" adaptation with a French twist on the source material's behind-the-scenes revelations.[51] Detractors argued the remake lacked originality, merely replicating the original's twists without sufficient innovation or cultural adaptation. Simon Abrams of RogerEbert.com gave it 1.5/4 stars, criticizing the first act's unadorned presentation of the zombie shoot as derivative and the film's reliance on the source's gimmick, stating it "follows the making of a Z-grade horror movie... presented without commentary."[16] A review on Horror Obsessive deemed it "fine" for newcomers but redundant for those familiar with the Japanese version, emphasizing that its appeal hinges on ignorance of the prior film's structure.[52] Overall, reception pivoted on viewers' prior exposure to the original, with praise centered on execution of humor and ensemble energy amid critiques of unoriginality.

Audience and fan responses

Audiences gave Final Cut a generally positive reception, with a 74% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from over 50 verified user reviews, averaging 3.9 out of 5 stars.[3] On IMDb, the film earned a 6.3 out of 10 rating from 5,431 users as of late 2023, reflecting appreciation for its humorous take on low-budget horror production.[1] Letterboxd users rated it 3.4 out of 5 based on more than 23,000 logs, indicating solid but not exceptional fan engagement.[53] Viewers frequently commended the film's meta twist structure, where the initial 37-minute "one-take" zombie sequence—initially perceived as amateurish and chaotic—unfolds into a satirical reveal about the crew's real-time struggles, eliciting laughs from the absurdity and energy.[54] Fans highlighted the cast's committed performances, particularly Romain Duris as the obsessive director, and the gore-filled homage to zombie tropes as entertaining diversions that reward patience with the setup.[55] One IMDb reviewer noted the "huge amount of fun" in its blend of comedy and horror, praising the B-movie self-awareness.[54] However, responses were tempered among those aware of its origins as a remake of the 2017 Japanese film One Cut of the Dead, with some audiences finding it derivative and less innovative, though still "funny and heartfelt" in execution. A Rotten Tomatoes user described it as an "interesting take" with a different feel from the original, mixing positives like cinephile flair with critiques of uneven pacing.[55] Newcomers to the concept often reported higher enjoyment, calling it "endearing" and effective as a standalone comedy about filmmaking chaos.[56] Overall, fan discourse emphasized its appeal to genre enthusiasts willing to embrace the deliberate flaws for the payoff, rather than expecting polished horror.[57]

Comparisons to One Cut of the Dead

Final Cut (2022), directed by Michel Hazanavicius, is a French remake of the 2017 Japanese independent film One Cut of the Dead, directed by Shin'ichirō Ueda, replicating its core structure of a faux 37-minute one-take zombie horror sequence followed by a meta reveal of the low-budget production's behind-the-scenes chaos.[58][59] The remake adapts the original's premise—a ragtag film crew shooting a zombie movie in an abandoned warehouse encounters real undead threats—transplanting it to a Parisian industrial site with French dialogue and actors, while preserving the shot-for-shot fidelity of the initial long take and subsequent narrative twists.[60] Critics have highlighted structural parallels but noted divergences in execution due to production scale: One Cut of the Dead was produced on a budget of approximately 3 million yen (around $25,000 USD) using mostly unknown performers and improvisational energy, yielding a raw, unpredictable charm that resonated through its cult status and unexpected box office success in Japan.[58] In contrast, Final Cut benefits from Hazanavicius's Oscar-winning pedigree (The Artist, 2011), a higher budget enabling polished cinematography and stars like Romain Duris and Bérénice Bejo, which some reviewers argue dilutes the original's lo-fi authenticity and comedic spontaneity in favor of a more theatrical, self-aware polish.[59][60] Audience and critic comparisons often deem Final Cut redundant for viewers familiar with the original, as its surprises hinge on the same reveals, potentially evoking an "uncanny" familiarity rather than fresh innovation; however, proponents praise its cultural transposition, emphasizing French comedic timing and ensemble dynamics that inject new relational humor into the familial and professional tensions among the crew.[59] Where One Cut thrives on genuine amateurish constraints—such as real-time problem-solving without extensive reshoots—Final Cut leans into meta-commentary on remake culture itself, with added layers of irony about adapting a foreign indie hit into a star-driven vehicle, though this self-reflexivity does not universally elevate it above Ueda's version.[58][51]
