John Pavlovitz

John Pavlovitz is an American pastor, author, and activist based in Wake Forest, North Carolina, with more than 25 years of experience in local church ministry.[1] His writings, disseminated through the blog Stuff That Needs To Be Said, focus on faith, politics, and social issues from a progressive Christian viewpoint, emphasizing themes of inclusion, justice, and critique of conservative religious trends.[2] Pavlovitz has published multiple books, including A Bigger Table: Building Messy, Authentic, and Hopeful Spiritual Community (2017), which promotes expansive approaches to church fellowship, and If God Is Love, Don't Be a Jerk: Finding a Faith That Makes Us Better Humans (2021), which urges compassionate application of Christian principles in daily life.[3] A defining event in his career occurred in 2014 when he was terminated as a pastor at North Raleigh Community Church after posting online in support of his son identifying as gay, an action that underscored conflicts between personal convictions and institutional expectations in evangelical settings.[4] Subsequently, his platform expanded amid broader cultural debates over Christianity's role in politics, particularly criticism of evangelical alignment with certain political figures and movements, though this has drawn rebukes from traditionalist observers for prioritizing activism over scriptural literalism.[5]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

John Pavlovitz was born in Syracuse, New York, in 1969 to a middle-class family of Italian and Russian descent.[6][7] He grew up in a Catholic household, attending church regularly during his formative years.[4][5] Pavlovitz has described his childhood as a mainstream suburban experience, shaped by traditional Italian Catholic family dynamics.[4] In reflections on his early life, he characterized the cultural influences as emphasizing familial bonds, religious observance, and a blend of hearty meals with moral expectations, quipping that he was "raised on gluten and guilt" in line with Roman Catholic traditions common among Italian-American communities.[8] His father's sudden death on his 70th birthday in 2013 marked a significant personal loss, which Pavlovitz later referenced as a pivotal shift in his worldview, though details of his parents' backgrounds remain limited in public records.[9]

Formal Education and Influences

Pavlovitz pursued undergraduate studies at the University of the Arts in Philadelphia, arriving with a scholarship to major in graphic design.[4] [10] During this period, he initially drifted from his Catholic upbringing but experienced a spiritual shift that broadened his worldview through exposure to diverse stories and perspectives in a visual and performing arts environment.[11] [12] After completing his undergraduate degree, Pavlovitz attended Eastern Baptist Theological Seminary (now Palmer Theological Seminary) in Philadelphia, where he received formal training for ministry.[13] [6] This enrollment followed an initial sense of calling to vocational Christian service that emerged during his college years, leading him to transition from graphic design aspirations toward pastoral work.[14] The seminary's Baptist framework provided his primary theological education, contrasting with his early Catholic formation and shaping his approach to church leadership.[15] Key influences on Pavlovitz's development included his Catholic family background in Syracuse, New York, which instilled initial religious foundations before his Protestant seminary pivot.[6] The progressive-leaning environment of Eastern Baptist Theological Seminary, known for emphasizing social justice within evangelical traditions, likely informed his later emphases on inclusivity and empathy in ministry, though specific mentors or texts are not prominently documented in available accounts.[13] His arts education further contributed by fostering creative expression and openness to human diversity, elements evident in his writing and public theology.[8]

Pastoral Ministry

Early Ministry Roles

Pavlovitz began his pastoral career as a youth pastor at a small Methodist church in suburban Pennsylvania shortly after his marriage and completion of college studies in graphic design.[4] This initial role marked his transition from a career in art direction to ministry, which he described as being "drafted" into service while still employed in design.[16] Following a period of seminary training at Eastern Baptist Theological Seminary in Philadelphia, Pavlovitz continued involvement with the Pennsylvania church before relocating with his family to Charlotte, North Carolina.[4] There, he assumed the position of youth pastor at Good Shepherd United Methodist Church, a congregation with an evangelical orientation that he characterized as a megachurch.[17] He served in this capacity for eight years, overseeing a youth program that engaged several hundred students through events, retreats, and educational initiatives focused on faith formation.[17] During his tenure at Good Shepherd, Pavlovitz expanded his responsibilities, eventually transitioning to associate pastor while maintaining emphasis on youth and family ministries.[4] The role involved preaching, counseling, and community outreach within a conservative United Methodist framework, where he navigated tensions between traditional doctrine and emerging personal convictions on inclusivity.[17] His time in Charlotte, spanning nearly a decade overall, laid foundational experience in large-scale church operations and youth engagement before his move to Raleigh-area ministries.[4]

Tenure at North Raleigh Community Church

John Pavlovitz served as a youth pastor at North Raleigh Community Church, a nontraditional congregation in Raleigh, North Carolina, following his dismissal from a prior megachurch role in 2013 over blog posts deemed provocative by church leadership.[4][5] He joined the staff amid a period of personal transition after unemployment in 2014, continuing in ministry focused on youth engagement while the church experienced growth.[13][18] During his tenure, which extended through 2017, Pavlovitz contributed to the church's efforts to foster a compassionate environment, aligning with his evolving views on inclusivity expressed in public writings.[19] The role allowed him to maintain local pastoral duties, including youth programming, at a time when his blog "Stuff That Needs To Be Said" began attracting wider attention for critiques of evangelical norms.[20][21] North Raleigh Community Church, located downtown, operated as an independent body emphasizing relational ministry over rigid doctrinal adherence, though specific programs under Pavlovitz's direct oversight remain sparsely documented in public records.[5]

Departure from Ministry in 2017

In 2017, John Pavlovitz transitioned away from his primary role as youth pastor at North Raleigh Community Church in Raleigh, North Carolina, toward a full-time focus on writing, blogging, and public advocacy.[4][22] This shift coincided with the publication of his debut book, A Bigger Table: Building Messy, Authentic, and Hopeful Spiritual Community, released on October 26, 2017, by Westminster John Knox Press, which emphasized themes of inclusivity, hospitality, and critiquing exclusionary practices in Christianity. This departure reflected broader patterns in Pavlovitz's career, where his advocacy for authenticity and inclusivityâ€”rooted in personal experiences like supporting his gay brotherâ€”clashed with more conservative church structures, prompting self-censorship in prior positions.[17] He later reflected that institutional ministry often demanded editing one's voice to maintain unity, stating, "I was never fully authentic... I started to censor and edit myself."[17] Post-2017, Pavlovitz maintained loose ties to North Raleigh as a congregant but prioritized independent platforms, marking the end of his 25-year tenure in traditional local church leadership.[1][2] Critics from conservative circles viewed his exit as self-inflicted due to ideological drift, while supporters saw it as a necessary evolution toward a "bigger table" of spiritual community unbound by doctrinal rigidity.[24]

Writing and Media Presence

Blog "Stuff That Needs To Be Said"

"Stuff That Needs To Be Said" is an online blog authored by John Pavlovitz, launched in 2012 as a personal platform for reflections on faith, family, and ministry. Initially targeted at parents in his church community, it featured essays on diverse subjects including grief, parenting, and church experiences.[25][26] The blog's content evolved to emphasize social and political commentary viewed through a Christian lens, with posts often advocating for empathy, inclusivity, and critiques of conservative evangelical positions on issues such as LGBTQ rights, racial justice, and responses to political events. Notable entries include examinations of gender imagery in theology and responses to cultural figures, framed as calls for compassionate discourse.[27][28] Following the 2016 U.S. presidential election, Pavlovitz's writings gained viral traction, particularly those addressing evangelical support for Donald Trump, which broadened its appeal and readership. By 2022, the blog had accumulated over 100 million page views from a global audience seeking alternative perspectives within Christianity.[25][4] Pavlovitz maintains the site at johnpavlovitz.com, where it continues to publish regular posts alongside promotions for his books, speaking engagements, and newsletters; select essays have been compiled into published collections. The blog's style is characterized by direct, emotive language intended to provoke reflection and challenge perceived hypocrisies in religious and public life.[29]

Published Books

John Pavlovitz has authored multiple books primarily addressing themes of faith, grief, inclusivity, politics, and personal spirituality, often drawing from his pastoral experiences and blog writings. His publications span self-published works, devotionals, and titles from established Christian presses, with a focus on challenging traditional evangelical boundaries and promoting compassion amid cultural divides.[3] His debut book, A Year in the Grief Valley: 365 Days of Love and Loss, a collection reflecting on personal bereavement, was published in 2014 by Parsons Porch Books.[30] In 2017, Westminster John Knox Press released A Bigger Table: Building Messy, Authentic, and Hopeful Spiritual Community, which critiques exclusionary church practices and calls for broader acceptance, including of LGBTQ individuals.[31] This was followed by an expanded edition with a study guide in 2021.[32] Subsequent releases include Hope and Other Superpowers: A Life-Affirming, Love-Defending Journey Toward Meaning and Belonging in 2018 from Simon & Schuster, emphasizing resilient faith in politically charged times.[33] Stuff That Needs To Be Said: Essential Words on Life, Death, Faith, Politics, Love, and Giving a Damn, a compilation of essays, appeared in 2020 under his own imprint.[34] If God Is Love, Don't Be a Jerk: Finding a Faith That Makes Us Better Humans was published in 2021 by Westminster John Knox Press, advocating ethical living rooted in divine love over rigid doctrine.[35] Later works feature shorter devotionals such as Rise: An Honest Lenten Devotional in 2022 from Chalice Press, exploring spiritual renewal through vulnerability.[36] Most recently, Worth Fighting For: Finding Courage and Compassion When Cruelty Is Trending was issued in 2024 by Westminster John Knox Press, urging action against perceived social injustices like rights erosions and violence.[37] Additional titles, including Low: An Honest Advent Devotional and Here and Now and Small and Close, appear on his official site but lack specified publication dates in available records.[3]

Social Media and Public Speaking

Pavlovitz maintains an active presence across multiple social media platforms, where he shares writings, commentary on current events, and promotions for his books and speaking engagements. Recent 2025 appearances include a keynote for Grandparents for Gun Safety in Kansas City on October 14, a discussion on authoritarian threats at First Congregational Church in Madison, Wisconsin, on October 19, and an afternoon event at First Congregational UCC on October 25.[43][46][47] Earlier tours, such as the 2019 "Hope is Here!" series spanning January to April, visited multiple communities for similar inspirational talks.[48] These events position him as a sought-after speaker for audiences seeking alternatives to conservative evangelicalism, with recordings and live streams extending his reach.[49]

Theological and Political Views

Positions on LGBTQ Issues and Inclusivity

John Pavlovitz maintains that homosexual orientation, as well as lesbian, bisexual, and transgender identities, do not constitute sin, asserting that the Bible contains no explicit condemnation of such identities and that traditional interpretations misapply ancient cultural contexts to modern consensual relationships.[50] He argues this position stems from a prioritization of Jesus' emphasis on love over selective Old Testament prohibitions, which he views as addressing exploitative practices rather than innate orientations.[50] Pavlovitz supports same-sex marriage, contending that if God embodies love, divine intent aligns with mutual, consensual unions between adults regardless of gender, and that opposition to marriage equality violates the biblical mandate to love one's neighbor.[51][52] In a 2021 blog post, he frames such relationships as reflective of God's relational nature, dismissing hierarchical or procreative-only models of marriage as culturally imposed rather than scripturally absolute.[51] Regarding family and parenting, Pavlovitz has outlined four promises he would make to any gay children: public acknowledgment without shame, prayer not for alteration of their orientation but for their well-being and wisdom, unwavering love irrespective of church pressures, and active advocacy against bullying or rejection they might face.[53] This stance, first articulated in a September 2014 blog entry, rejects conversion efforts or conditional acceptance, positioning parental affirmation as aligned with Christ's compassion over doctrinal conformity.[53] On church inclusivity, Pavlovitz criticizes partial or performative affirmation, insisting that true inclusion requires LGBTQ participation in leadership, sacraments, and decision-making, not mere tolerance from heterosexual majorities.[54] In his 2016 "Church Beloved" manifesto, co-authored with affirming Christians, he commits to creating safe spaces for LGBTQ individuals, rejecting exclusionary doctrines as antithetical to the gospel's inclusive ethos.[55] He has praised denominational shifts, such as the United Methodist Church's 2024 reversal of its ban on LGBTQ clergy, as steps toward repentance for prior discrimination.[56] Pavlovitz also urges stronger allyship during events like Pride Month, calling for vocal opposition to discrimination rather than passive respect.[57] In his 2018 book A Bigger Table, Pavlovitz addresses LGBTQ exclusion as a core failure of evangelical churches, advocating for expanded communal tables that prioritize human dignity over rigid orthodoxy.[12] He has issued open letters to church leaders, decrying bullying of LGBTQ youth as incompatible with faith and demanding self-examination on whether institutional practices embody love or harm.[58] These views position Pavlovitz as a proponent of theological revisionism, where inclusivity overrides traditional prohibitions, though he frames such positions as fidelity to Jesus' relational priorities over cultural conservatism.[59]

Critiques of Evangelicalism and Conservatism

Pavlovitz has argued that white evangelicalism exhibits profound hypocrisy in its political engagements, particularly in demonizing Democratic figures like Barack Obama through unsubstantiated claims and racial stereotypes while granting unconditional absolution to Republican leaders with documented moral failings, such as Donald Trump.[60] He contends this selective mercy prioritizes pigmentation, party affiliation, and a distorted "white, Republican Jesus" over the compassionate teachings of the historical Jesus of Nazareth, eroding the church's moral authority and driving millions away from Christianity.[60] In broader terms, Pavlovitz describes the white evangelical church as having failed America and Jesus by forging a marriage to exclusionary politics that nurtures inequity and supremacy, enabling actions like family separations at the border and support for unhinged political rallies while excluding the poor, women, people of color, gender-fluid individuals, immigrants, refugees, and non-Christians.[61] He attributes an exodus from Christianity to evangelicals' self-righteous hypocrisy, which provides evidence for skeptics that Christians are hateful and untrustworthy, rendering organized evangelicalism toxic and irreparable due to its embrace of human rights violations, overt racism, and rabid nationalism.[62][25] Pavlovitz extends these critiques to conservatism, portraying it as a relentless "war on everything" that targets the LGBTQ community, people of color, women, immigrants, Muslims, Jewish people, scientists, and medical professionals, often weaponizing religion to fuel fear and enmity rather than fostering empathy or collaboration.[63] He asserts that conservative Christians hoard perceived divine provision out of a "lie of lack," defying Jesus' emphasis on generosity, and engage in fear-mongering that brutalizes society, rendering their faith antithetical to Christ's inclusive message.[64] In his books, such as If God Is Love, Don't Be a Jerk, Pavlovitz reinforces that evangelical alignment with such conservatism distorts Christianity into an unrecognizable form dominated by homophobic politicians and predatory figures, prioritizing power over love.[25]

Views on Politics and Donald Trump

Pavlovitz has consistently articulated a progressive political outlook, emphasizing social justice, inclusivity, and opposition to conservative policies he views as discriminatory. He frames political engagement through a lens of Christian ethics, arguing that true faith demands advocacy for marginalized groups over partisan loyalty. In particular, his commentary portrays American conservatism, especially its evangelical strains, as having devolved into a form of cultural nationalism incompatible with Jesus' teachings on humility, empathy, and peacemaking.[1][4] Pavlovitz's criticism of Donald Trump intensified following the 2016 election, positioning Trump as a symbol of moral decay within Christianity. In an April 2017 opinion piece, he deemed it "ludicrous" to regard Trump as Christian, citing Trump's professed "finding Jesus" as a politically expedient conversion lacking evidence in his conduct, which Pavlovitz contrasted with Jesus' emphasis on "humility, generosity, respect, empathy, kindness, peace."[22][65] He has repeatedly labeled Trump "cruel, immoral, vile, racist, misogynistic, narcissistic, vulgar, criminal, hateful," asserting in a July 2024 post that Trump "is not Christian and never has been."[66] Central to Pavlovitz's critique is the evangelical embrace of Trump, which he describes as a "bastardized version of the Christian faith" akin to "Trumpism" rather than genuine evangelicalism. In an August 2018 blog entry, he contended that "Christians supporting Trump aren't Christians," likening modern Trump adherents to ancient Romans who prioritized imperial power over Jesus' radical ethic of love for enemies.[67][68] By January 2025, he escalated this to declaring "MAGA Christianity is Anti-Jesus," arguing that devotion to both Jesus and Trump represents an irreconcilable contradiction, as Trump's rhetoric and policies foster division rather than the unity Pavlovitz associates with the Gospels.[69] Pavlovitz extends this scrutiny to Trump supporters, rejecting notions of respectful disagreement. In an October 2020 post, he stated, "No, I won't agree to disagree about you supporting Trump. You're just wrong," viewing such support as a fundamental moral failing that endorses harm to vulnerable populations through policies on immigration, healthcare, and LGBTQ rights.[70] He has claimed in 2025 writings that Trump backers prioritize "racism and homophobia" over economic prosperity and social welfare, and that aligning with Trump reflects a "hateful heart" antithetical to goodness.[71][72] This stance, echoed in his November 2016 enumeration of "7 things Christians are giving up by supporting Donald Trump"â€”including integrity, compassion, and witnessâ€”underscores his belief that evangelical political allegiance to Trump sacrifices core Christian principles for temporal power.[73]

Controversies and Criticisms

Church Division and Doctrinal Disputes

Pavlovitz's pastoral tenure ended amid doctrinal tensions centered on human sexuality and biblical interpretation. In 2015, after five months as youth pastor at a Southern Baptist startup church in Raleigh, North Carolina, he was dismissed by the lead pastor, who cited a lack of fit without a specific incident but acknowledged a widening theological gap.[17] This disconnect primarily involved Pavlovitz's evolving support for LGBTQ inclusion and women's leadership roles, influenced by personal experiences such as his brother's homosexuality, which clashed with the congregation's adherence to traditional evangelical prohibitions on same-sex relationships and complementarian gender norms.[17][4] His June 2015 blog post, "If I Have Gay Children: Four Promises From a Christian Pastor/Parent," exemplified these disputes by pledging unconditional love, affirmation of identity, and rejection of conversion therapy for any hypothetical gay offspring, framing such acceptance as aligned with Jesus' compassion over strict doctrinal exclusion. The post, which garnered over 100,000 views rapidly, intensified rifts, as it implicitly prioritized experiential empathy and social justice interpretations of scripture over literal readings of passages like Leviticus 18:22 or Romans 1:26-27, which conservative theologians cite as condemning homosexual acts. Critics within evangelical circles argued this stance diluted biblical authority on sin, potentially eroding church unity by accommodating cultural shifts rather than upholding timeless moral standards derived from first-principles exegesis of canonical texts.[74] These views fueled broader doctrinal fractures, with conservative responders like Pastor Gabe Hughes labeling Pavlovitz a "false teacher" for allegedly undermining core tenets such as the exclusivity of salvation through orthodox Christology and the immutability of scriptural ethics on sexuality.[74] Hughes critiqued Pavlovitz's writings for promoting a therapeutic, anthropocentric gospel that sidesteps judgment and repentance, contributing to schisms where progressive-leaning members depart traditional congregations while accusing orthodoxy of legalism.[24] Pavlovitz, in turn, attributed such divisions to evangelical rigidity, claiming in interviews that suppressing doubts on inclusivity drives congregants away, though empirical data on church exits often correlates more strongly with secularization trends and internal progressive-conservative polarizations than individual voices alone. No formal church split directly under Pavlovitz's leadership is documented, but his public advocacy amplified existing fault lines, prompting denominational-level debates, such as those in Southern Baptist circles over affirming therapies and ordination standards.[17] The disputes underscore causal tensions between doctrinal fidelityâ€”rooted in historical creeds and scriptural inerrancyâ€”and adaptive inclusivity, where Pavlovitz's positions, while resonating with deconstructing believers, have been rebutted by conservatives as fostering relativism that fragments ecclesial cohesion.[74][75] Sources critiquing Pavlovitz, often from confessional evangelical outlets, emphasize empirical adherence to patristic and Reformation-era boundaries on heresy, contrasting with progressive narratives that frame exclusion as the true schism.[24]

Accusations of Political Activism Over Theology

Critics within evangelical and conservative Christian communities have accused John Pavlovitz of elevating political activism against conservatism and figures like Donald Trump above core theological concerns, portraying his ministry as more aligned with partisan resistance than biblical exposition. Pastor Gabe Hughes, in a December 2014 blog post, warned Christians against sharing Pavlovitz's articles, describing him as a "rogue pastor" lacking biblical authority and prioritizing personal experiences and social critiquesâ€”such as defenses of figures like Rob Bellâ€”over scriptural fidelity and doctrinal soundness.[24] Hughes argued that Pavlovitz's emphasis on carnal appeals and church-as-social-club narratives undermines the gospel's call to reconciliation through Christ, instead fostering division through activist rhetoric.[24] These accusations gained traction through Pavlovitz's own writings, such as his June 5, 2021, blog post "I'm a Christian Pastor. Evangelicals Have to Be Defeated," where he explicitly called for political efforts to block white evangelicals from congressional power, claiming they would "decimate this nation" if unchecked.[76] Critics contended this framing subordinated spiritual exhortation to electoral strategy, reducing theology to a tool for opposing Republican dominance rather than proclaiming salvation or church unity. In a January 2016 follow-up, Hughes labeled Pavlovitz a "false teacher" whose rejection of the Bible as a guide for public policyâ€”coupled with generalizations appealing to secular audiencesâ€”reflected a politically driven heresy that led believers astray from God's word.[74] More recently, on August 17, 2025, Everett Piper, a conservative commentator and former college president, published an opinion piece in The Washington Times urging readers to "shun 'pastors' like John Pavlovitz," citing his self-description as a "writer, pastor, and activist" and an August 2, 2025, Substack essay that branded Trump "the very worst humanity has produced" and a "moral bottom-feeder."[77] Piper argued this exemplified hypocritical judgmentalism, where Pavlovitz weaponized his platform for political condemnation rather than embodying Christian humility or truth as outlined in texts like 2 Timothy, effectively prioritizing anti-Trump activism over pastoral shepherding.[77] Such critiques, often from sources skeptical of progressive theological shifts, highlight Pavlovitz's blend of faith and politics as eroding traditional evangelical priorities.

Conservative Rebuttals and Heresy Claims

Conservative critics, particularly from evangelical circles, have rebutted John Pavlovitz's theological positions by arguing that they undermine the authority and inerrancy of Scripture, constituting a departure from orthodox Christianity. In a 2016 response to Pavlovitz's essay "10 Things This Christian Doesn't Believe About the Bible," Pastor Gabe Hughes labeled him a "false teacher" for rejecting the doctrine that the Bible was dictated by God, asserting instead that it reflects human biases and cultural limitations rather than divine inspiration.[74] Hughes cited 2 Timothy 3:16 to counter this, emphasizing that all Scripture is "God-breathed" and not merely human opinion, and accused Pavlovitz of heresy for prioritizing scientific consensus over the literal Genesis creation account, which Hughes defended using Hebrews 11:3.[74] Further rebuttals targeted Pavlovitz's views on gender roles and sexuality, where he dismissed biblical prohibitions on same-sex relations as culturally conditioned rather than timeless truths. Hughes rebutted this by referencing 1 Corinthians 6:9-10, arguing that Pavlovitz's affirmation of LGBTQ identities promotes unrepentant sin, aligning with false teaching warned against in Jude 1:4.[74] Critics also challenged his rejection of sola scripturaâ€”the sufficiency of Scripture as the ultimate authorityâ€”claiming it opens the door to subjective interpretations influenced by modern politics and culture, as seen in Pavlovitz's integration of progressive activism into theology.[74] [24] Heresy claims extend to Pavlovitz's apparent softening of doctrines like eternal punishment, with conservatives arguing his emphasis on God's universal love eclipses biblical warnings of judgment, resembling universalism historically condemned at councils like Constantinople in 543 AD. In broader critiques, figures like Hughes warned that Pavlovitz's writings, while emotionally appealing, lack scriptural fidelity and serve to divide the church by portraying conservative evangelicals as pharisaical, contrary to Jesus' model of confronting sin directly.[24] These rebuttals frame Pavlovitz not as a reformer but as a deconstructor of core Christian tenets, urging believers to avoid his materials to prevent doctrinal erosion.[74]

Personal Life and Legacy

Family and Residence

Pavlovitz resides in Wake Forest, North Carolina, a suburb of Raleigh.[4][22][78] He has been married to his wife, Jennifer, since the early 2000s, following their meeting during his time in a Methodist church after college.[79][5] The couple has two children: a son, who was 16 years old as of 2021, and a daughter.[79][80] Pavlovitz has publicly discussed parenting challenges, including open conversations with his children about topics such as vaccination, health, and social issues.[79][80]

Current Activities and Influence

As of 2025, John Pavlovitz maintains an active schedule of writing, podcasting, and public speaking focused on progressive interpretations of Christianity, social justice, and critiques of political conservatism. He publishes frequent essays on his website, johnpavlovitz.com, covering topics such as faith amid political division, support for LGBTQ events, and opposition to figures like Donald Trump and JD Vance, with recent posts including commentary on a local pride festival and perceived authoritarian trends in Republican politics.[29][81][82] Pavlovitz hosts The Beautiful Mess by John Pavlovitz podcast, which has produced episodes through October 2025, featuring discussions on empathy, grief, and cultural issues from a pastoral perspective.[83] In the same year, he has conducted speaking engagements at events like the Wake Forest Pride Fest on October 11 and an afternoon session at First Congregational United Church of Christ in Madison, Wisconsin, on October 25, where topics included Christian nationalism and compassion in polarized contexts.[84][85][47] His most recent book, Worth Fighting For: Finding Courage and Compassion When Cruelty is Trending, released on April 2, 2024, encourages engagement with social and political challenges through a lens of empathy and justice.[86][87] Pavlovitz directs the Empathetic People Network, an organization aimed at fostering dialogue and compassion in divided communities, and continues to build influence through social media platforms including Facebook, X (formerly Twitter), and Bluesky, where he shares daily reflections reaching audiences critical of evangelical conservatism.[88][38] His work resonates primarily with progressive Christian demographics, promoting inclusivity and activism, though it draws rebuttals from conservative theologians for prioritizing political commentary over traditional doctrine.[89][45] This influence is evident in his role as a speaker at faith-based events and his contributions to discussions on church evolution, as noted in outlets like United Methodist Communications.[89]
