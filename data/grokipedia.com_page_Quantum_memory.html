Quantum memory

In quantum computing, a quantum memory is the quantum-mechanical version of ordinary computer memory. Whereas ordinary memory stores information as binary states (represented by 1s and 0s), quantum memory stores a quantum state for later retrieval. These states hold useful computational information known as qubits. Unlike the classical memory of everyday computers, the states stored in quantum memory can be in a quantum superposition, providing greater flexibility for quantum algorithms than classical information storage. Quantum memory is essential for the development of many devices in quantum information processing, including a synchronization tool that can match the various processes in a quantum computer, a component that maintains the identity of any state, and a mechanism for converting predetermined photons into on-demand photons. Quantum memory can be used in many aspects, such as quantum computing and quantum communication. Continuous research and experiments have enabled quantum memory to realize the storage of qubits.[1]

Quantum Memory

Background and history

The interaction of quantum radiation with matter, particularly atomic ensembles, has been a focus of research in quantum optics since the early 1990s. Quantum memory involves mapping the quantum state of light onto atoms and retrieving it faithfully, preserving quantum coherence. It serves as a key component for quantum information processing, including optical quantum computing and quantum communication, and advances understanding of light-matter interactions. Challenges in achieving high-fidelity storage and retrieval persist, though significant progress continues.[2] Theoretical proposals for quantum memory, such as storage of single-photon states via controlled reversible inhomogeneous broadening (CRIB), were introduced by Kessel and Moiseev in 1993.[3] Further analysis of photon-echo-based protocols appeared in 1998, with experimental demonstrations of single-photon storage achieved in 2003 using atomic gases.[4] These developments built partly on earlier classical optical data storage concepts using photon echoes, proposed in 1979 and 1982, but extended them to coherent quantum states. Key advancements include the use of electromagnetically induced transparency (EIT) for light slowing and storage, first proposed in 1990[5] and demonstrated for coherent pulses in 2001. By the 2010s, efficiencies exceeded 90% in solid-state systems, with ongoing work as of 2025 focusing on room-temperature operation and integration with quantum networks.[6]

Types

Atomic gases

Classical optical signals are typically transmitted by varying the intensity (proportional to the square of the amplitude) of light, which can be stored using devices like photographic film or digital photodetectors. In quantum information processing, however, the information is often encoded in both the amplitude and phase of the light field, forming coherent quantum states. Due to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle, measuring both amplitude and phase simultaneously disturbs the quantum state. Thus, storing quantum information requires mapping the light's quantum state onto a medium without performing a destructive measurement. Atomic gas quantum memories achieve this by transferring the state of light into collective excitations of an atomic ensemble, often using techniques like electromagnetically induced transparency (EIT) or stimulated Raman scattering. The relative amplitude and phase of the input light are preserved in the atomic cloud and can be retrieved on demand.[7][6]

Solid state

Solid-state quantum memories leverage the stability of material lattices to store quantum information, addressing limitations in processing qubits where coherence times are short due to environmental interactions. Unlike classical computing, where memory states can be cloned and stored indefinitely, quantum states cannot be perfectly copied per the no-cloning theorem, and storage is constrained by decoherence without error correction. In solid-state systems, quantum information is mapped from photons or processing qubits to more robust "storage qubits," such as nuclear spins or electronic defects, which offer longer coherence times (often milliseconds to seconds) resistant to noise. Common implementations include rare-earth-ion-doped crystals (e.g., using praseodymium or europium ions for spin-wave storage via atomic frequency comb protocols) and defect centers like nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers in diamond or phosphorus donors in silicon. The stored information can later be transferred back to processing qubits for operations.[8][3]

Optical Quantum Memory

Discovery

Optical quantum memory is used to detect and store single-photon quantum states. However, achieving high efficiency has been a significant challenge. A single photon has low energy and can be lost in complex light backgrounds. Prior to 2019, these issues had kept quantum storage efficiencies below 50%. In 2019, a team led by Professor Du Shengwang from the Department of Physics and the William Mong Institute of Nano Science and Technology at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology found a way to increase the efficiency of optical quantum memory for single-photon polarization qubits to more than 85% with fidelity over 99%.[9] This milestone advanced progress toward practical quantum computers and repeaters for quantum networks, laying groundwork for a quantum internet. Subsequent research has pushed efficiencies above 90% as of 2025.

Research and Application

Quantum memory is an important component of quantum information processing applications such as quantum networks, quantum repeaters, linear optical quantum computation, and long-distance quantum communication.[10]

Microwave Storage and Light-Microwave Conversion

The nitrogen-vacancy center in diamond has attracted significant research interest over the past decade due to its excellent performance in optical nanophotonic devices. In a 2013 experiment, electromagnetically induced transparency was implemented on a multi-pass diamond chip containing nitrogen-vacancy centers to achieve all-optical electromagnetic field sensing. Despite these related demonstrations, efficient optical storage of photons in diamond nitrogen-vacancy centers has yet to be fully implemented in practice, though the energy level structure of charged and neutral nitrogen-vacancy centers makes it feasible. The coupling between nitrogen-vacancy spin ensembles and superconducting qubits enables microwave storage in superconducting systems. Combining optical storage with spin-superconducting qubit interactions allows nitrogen-vacancy centers in diamond to facilitate coherent light-microwave conversion in hybrid quantum systems.[6]

Orbital Angular Momentum Is Stored in Alkali Vapor

Large resonant light depth is the premise of constructing efficient quantum-optical memory. Alkali metal vapor isotopes provide large optical depth at near-infrared wavelengths due to their relatively narrow spectral lines and high density at warm temperatures of 50â€“100 Â°C. Alkali vapors have been used in many key memory developments, from early research to recent results, owing to their high optical depth, long coherence times, and accessible near-infrared optical transitions. Due to its high information-carrying capacity, there is growing interest in applying structured light, which carries orbital angular momentum, to quantum information. Such light must be stored in memory to faithfully reproduce the structured photons upon retrieval. An atomic vapor quantum memory is ideal for storing these beams, as the orbital angular momentum of photons can be mapped onto the phase and amplitude of distributed collective excitations. Diffusion limits this technique, as the motion of hot atoms destroys the spatial coherence of the stored excitations. Early successes included storing weakly coherent spatially structured pulses in warm and ultracold atomic ensembles. In one experiment, scientists stored and retrieved vector beams at the single-photon level in a caesium magneto-optical trap.[11] The memory preserved the rotation invariance of the vector beam, enabling its use with rotationally invariant qubit encodings for quantum communication. The first storage of a structured single photon was achieved using electromagnetically induced transparency in a rubidium magneto-optical trap. A heralded single photon generated by spontaneous four-wave mixing in one magneto-optical trap was prepared with an orbital angular momentum state using spiral phase plates, stored in a second magneto-optical trap, and retrieved. This dual-trap setup also demonstrated coherence in multimode memory, storing an orbital angular momentum superposition state of a heralded single photon for 100 nanoseconds.[6]

GEM

GEM (Gradient Echo Memory) is a protocol for storing optical information applicable to both atomic gas and solid-state memories. The concept was first demonstrated by researchers at the Australian National University. An experiment in a three-level system based on hot rubidium atomic vapor achieved coherent storage with up to 87% efficiency.[12]

Electromagnetically Induced Transparency

Electromagnetically induced transparency (EIT) was first introduced by Harris and colleagues at Stanford University in 1990.[5] The technique modifies the optical response of an atomic medium through quantum interference between excitation paths induced by a laser beam, eliminating absorption and dispersion at resonant frequencies of atomic transitions. EIT enables slow light, optical storage, and quantum memories. Compared to other methods, EIT offers long storage times and is relatively simple and cost-effective to implement, not requiring high-power control beams as in Raman schemes or cryogenic temperatures like liquid helium. Additionally, photon echo readout can be delayed while spin coherence persists in inhomogeneously broadened media. Although limited in operating wavelength, bandwidth, and mode capacity, advancements have made EIT-based quantum memories valuable for quantum telecommunications.[6] In 2018, an EIT-based optical memory in a cold atomic ensemble demonstrated 92% storage-and-retrieval efficiency in the classical regime with coherent beams[13] and 70% efficiency for polarization qubits in weak coherent states, surpassing classical benchmarks.[14] Subsequently, single-photon polarization qubits were stored via EIT in an \, ^{85} \mathrm{Rb} \, cold atomic ensemble and retrieved with 85% efficiency.[9] Entanglement transfer between two cesium-based quantum memories was achieved with nearly 90% overall efficiency.[15] As of 2025, EIT protocols continue to advance, with demonstrations of multiplexed storage in cold atoms achieving efficiencies over 90% for multi-mode operations, enhancing scalability for quantum networks.[16]

Crystals Doped with Rare Earth

The mutual transformation of quantum information between light and matter is central to quantum informatics. Interactions between single photons and crystals doped with rare-earth ions at cryogenic temperatures are a focus of research. Rare-earth-doped crystals offer broad prospects for quantum storage due to their unique material systems.[17] In 2018, a team led by Li Chengfeng at the Key Laboratory of Quantum Information, University of Science and Technology of China (Chinese Academy of Sciences), developed a multi-degree-of-freedom multiplexed solid-state quantum memory in a rare-earth-doped crystal, demonstrating storage and manipulation of photonic qubits in spatial, temporal, and spectral dimensions with fidelities around 89%.[3] This enables multimode capacity and high-fidelity quantum state conversion, supporting large-scale quantum networks via quantum repeaters.[18]

Raman Scattering in Solids

Diamond exhibits high Raman gain in its 40 THz optical phonon mode and a wide transparency window in the visible and near-infrared bands, making it suitable for broadband optical memory. After Raman storage, the optical phonon decays into a pair of phonons with a 3.5 ps lifetime, rendering diamond memory unsuitable for communication protocols requiring longer storage. Nevertheless, diamond-based Raman memory has enabled studies of light-matter interactions at the quantum level, including emission quantum memory, macroscopic entanglement, heralded single-photon storage, and single-photon frequency manipulation.[6] Recent progress as of 2025 includes hybrid diamond photonic integration with nitrogen-vacancy centers for improved spin-photon interfaces, advancing toward practical quantum repeaters, though full optical memory remains under development.[19]

Future development

Quantum communication and cryptography remain key future research directions for quantum memory. Building a global quantum network faces significant challenges, including developing memories capable of storing quantum information carried by light over long distances. In 2018, researchers at the University of Geneva in Switzerland, collaborating with France's CNRS, demonstrated enhanced coherence in ytterbium-doped crystals (such as 171Yb3+:Y2SiO5^{171} \mathrm{Yb}^{3+} : \mathrm{Y}_2 \mathrm{SiO}_5171Yb3+:Y2â€‹SiO5â€‹), where the insulating host material enables storage and protection of quantum information at optical and microwave frequencies.[1] This positions ytterbium-doped systems as candidates for quantum repeaters in future networks. Due to the no-cloning theorem, which prevents replication of quantum signals, ongoing research focuses on extending storage distances by efficiently capturing and synchronizing photons. As of 2025, advances include millisecond-duration storage in europium-doped crystals and integration of quantum memories with cryptographic protocols for scalable entanglement distribution.[20][21][22]
