Al-Azhar University – Gaza

Al-Azhar University–Gaza (AUG) is a public, non-profit Palestinian university in Gaza City, established in 1991 by presidential decree under Yasser Arafat as an independent higher education institution emphasizing Islamic values and national development.[1] It began with faculties of law and education, expanding to 12 faculties by offering programs in medicine—the first such faculty in the Gaza Strip, opened in 1999—pharmacy, dentistry, engineering, sharia, sciences, agriculture, and administrative fields, serving approximately 16,000 students through 121 accredited degrees including bachelor's, master's, and PhDs.[1][2] The university's mission centers on providing quality education aligned with Palestinian societal needs, fostering research, and promoting Sharia awareness while preparing graduates for leadership amid regional challenges.[1] AUG has achieved recognition as one of Gaza's largest public universities and ranks among top institutions in the Arab region, contributing to knowledge creation despite operating in a conflict-prone environment that has repeatedly damaged its infrastructure, as noted in recent accounts from its leadership.[3][4] Its independent status distinguishes it from the Cairo-based Al-Azhar, focusing on local empowerment through empirical advancements in medical and technical disciplines rather than rote traditionalism.[2]

Establishment and Institutional Development

Founding and Early Years

Al-Azhar University–Gaza was established in 1991 in Gaza City through a presidential decree issued and signed by Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat, marking it as a public, non-profit, and independent Palestinian higher education institution.[1][4][3] The founding aimed to expand access to higher education in the Gaza Strip amid limited local options, serving as an alternative academic center during a period when many Palestinian students previously sought studies abroad, particularly in Egypt.[1][5] The university began operations modestly with two initial faculties: the Faculty of Law and the Faculty of Education.[1] By 1992, it rapidly expanded to six faculties, incorporating the Faculty of Pharmacy, Faculty of Agriculture and Environment, Faculty of Science, and Faculty of Arts and Human Sciences, reflecting an effort to broaden disciplinary offerings in sciences, humanities, and professional fields.[1] This early growth occurred in the context of internal academic shifts, including resignations of faculty from the nearby Gaza Islamic University, which contributed to Al-Azhar's formation as an administratively independent entity.[5] The establishment was initiated by elements aligned with the Fatah movement, positioning the university as a counterweight to the more Islamist-oriented Gaza Islamic University, founded earlier in the 1970s with ties to the Muslim Brotherhood.[6][5] This political dimension underscored early efforts to diversify higher education governance in Gaza, though specific initial enrollment figures remain undocumented in available records, with growth evident in subsequent infrastructure developments.[1]

Expansion of Infrastructure and Enrollment

Al-Azhar University–Gaza commenced operations on October 18, 1991, in a modest two-story building accommodating 725 students across two initial faculties: Education and Sharia and Law.[1] [4] The institution, founded by presidential decree under Yasser Arafat, began with limited infrastructure equivalent to just two rooms, reflecting its origins as a response to local demand for higher education amid constraints in Gaza.[4] [5] Over subsequent decades, the university expanded significantly, establishing branches starting with one in Gaza in 1992 and another in Khan Younis, evolving into three distinct campuses by the 2010s: the Eastern Campus for sciences and technology, the Western Campus for medical and economic programs, and the Southern Campus for law, humanities, agriculture, and veterinary sciences.[7] [4] This infrastructural growth included the construction of state-of-the-art facilities on the Southern Campus, supporting a broader academic scope with 12 faculties offering 121 accredited programs, including 80 bachelor's, 36 master's, and 5 PhD degrees.[4] Enrollment surged correspondingly, reaching approximately 15,000 students by the early 2020s, with around 290 academic staff, marking the university's transformation into one of Gaza's largest higher education institutions.[4] This expansion aligned with broader efforts to meet regional needs for professional training, though it occurred amid intermittent disruptions from regional conflicts that periodically halted development.[8]

Academic Structure and Programs

Faculties and Degree Offerings

Al-Azhar University–Gaza encompasses 12 faculties that deliver undergraduate and postgraduate education across humanities, sciences, engineering, and medical fields, with a foundational emphasis on Islamic scholarship derived from its affiliation with Al-Azhar University in Cairo. These faculties collectively support 121 accredited programs, comprising 80 bachelor's degrees, 36 master's degrees, and 5 doctoral programs, as reported by university leadership in early 2025.[4] [9] Bachelor's programs typically require completion of 144 credit hours over four years, excluding specialized tracks in medicine, dentistry, pharmacy, engineering, and applied medical sciences, which extend to five or six years and incorporate clinical or practical training.[10] Postgraduate offerings focus on advanced research and professional specialization, such as master's degrees in pharmaceutical sciences, clinical nutrition, physical therapy, and water and environmental chemistry, alongside PhDs in select areas like chemistry.[11] [12] The faculties and their primary degree offerings are as follows:
  • Faculty of Sharia and Islamic Studies: Bachelor's and master's in Islamic jurisprudence, Quranic studies, and related theological disciplines, prioritizing traditional Sunni scholarship.[13]
  • Faculty of Law: Bachelor's in public and private law, with master's options emphasizing legal theory within an Islamic framework.[13]
  • Faculty of Medicine: Six-year Doctor of Medicine (MD) program, including surgery training; limited postgraduate residencies.[14]
  • Faculty of Dentistry: Five-year Bachelor of Dental Surgery (BDS).[14]
  • Faculty of Pharmacy: Five-year Bachelor of Pharmacy (BPharm), with master's in pharmaceutical sciences.[14] [11]
  • Faculty of Applied Medical Sciences: Bachelor's in fields like physical therapy, medical imaging, and clinical nutrition; master's programs in these areas.[11]
  • Faculty of Sciences: Bachelor's in biology, chemistry (with minors in applied, food, or environmental chemistry), mathematics, and physics; PhD in chemistry and environmental sciences.[14] [12]
  • Faculty of Agriculture and Veterinary Medicine: Bachelor's in agricultural sciences, environmental studies, and veterinary medicine.[15]
  • Faculty of Engineering and Information Technology: Bachelor's in civil, electrical, mechanical, and computer engineering; information technology specializations.[15]
  • Faculty of Economics and Administrative Sciences: Bachelor's in economics, business administration, and accounting (144 credit hours).[16]
  • Faculty of Education: Bachelor's in education sciences, including teaching methods for various subjects.[15]
  • Faculty of Arts and Human Sciences: Bachelor's in Arabic language, English, history, and social sciences.[15]
Admission to competitive programs like medicine and engineering requires secondary school averages above 65%, though broader access exists for other faculties.[17] Programs integrate Islamic ethical principles across curricula, reflecting the university's mission to blend religious and secular knowledge.[9]

Curriculum Focus and Educational Approach

The Faculty of Sharia and Law at Al-Azhar University-Gaza centers its curriculum on traditional Sunni Islamic disciplines, requiring students to complete 144 credit hours for a bachelor's degree in areas such as Islamic jurisprudence (Fiqh), Quranic exegesis (Tafsir), Hadith authentication and interpretation, and Islamic creed (Aqidah).[18] This structure mirrors the broader Al-Azhar tradition of preserving orthodox Sunni scholarship across the four major schools of jurisprudence (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali), with coursework emphasizing textual analysis of primary sources like the Quran and Sunnah.[18] Across the university's 12 faculties, which encompass both religious and applied sciences—including medicine, pharmacy, dentistry, engineering, agriculture, and economics—the curriculum integrates modern academic standards with an underlying commitment to Islamic ethical principles.[2] For example, the Faculty of Education aligns its programs with the Palestinian national curriculum, updating course content to include subjects like educational psychology, curriculum development, and classroom management, while allocating pedagogy within teaching materials to emphasize practical application.[16] The educational approach employs a mix of lecture-based theoretical instruction, seminar discussions on Islamic texts, and hands-on training, such as micro-teaching simulations in education programs, to develop both scholarly depth and vocational skills.[16] Faculty goals include diversifying evaluation methods and creating field training opportunities, though operations have been disrupted since October 2023 due to conflict-related destruction of infrastructure.[16] Analyses of affiliated Al-Azhar systems highlight a dual emphasis on rejecting intra-Islamic extremism while framing resistance to perceived occupation as a religious duty, potentially influencing interpretive lenses in Sharia courses.[19]

Societal Impact and Operations

Contributions to Higher Education in Gaza

Al-Azhar University-Gaza has expanded higher education capacity in the Gaza Strip by enrolling around 16,000 students across 12 faculties, offering bachelor's degrees in 80 disciplines and master's degrees in 36 programs prior to major disruptions in 2023.[2][7] These offerings span critical fields such as medicine, engineering, agriculture, education, and applied sciences, addressing local demands for skilled professionals in healthcare, infrastructure, and teaching amid limited alternatives in the region.[3] The institution's growth from initial limited disciplines to a comprehensive structure has enabled successive cohorts to pursue specialized training, with faculties like Education adapting Egyptian Al-Azhar curricula to local contexts for teacher preparation.[16] By maintaining operations through multiple conflicts, including resuming online instruction as the first Gaza university to do so after the 2023 escalation, Al-Azhar ensured partial continuity of learning for thousands of students despite infrastructure losses estimated at $70 million.[4][20] This adaptability, including fee waivers for exam registrations during crises, mitigated educational disruptions and supported graduate output in essential sectors like medicine, where alumni have filled roles in Gaza's understaffed health system despite overall shortages.[4][21] Collaborations with Gaza's other non-profit universities, such as Al-Aqsa and Islamic University, have amplified these efforts through joint advocacy for international aid, online platforms, and rebuilding pledges, fostering a coordinated response to preserve higher education's role in societal resilience.[22][23] Such initiatives have sustained programs serving displaced students, including partnerships like UNESCO's Jusoor Learning Space for southern Gaza access.[24]

Student Life and Administrative Governance

The administration of Al-Azhar University–Gaza is led by a president, currently Dr. Omar Melad, who directs operations through the Board of University Presidency, responsible for managing correspondence, scheduling, and event organization.[25][4] The university maintains a decentralized structure across multiple campuses in Gaza City and southern areas, with administrative efforts focused on performance effectiveness and decision-support systems to enhance operational efficiency, as evaluated in empirical studies of Palestinian higher education institutions.[26][22] Governance operates within the broader context of Hamas's de facto control over Gaza since 2007, which has imposed constraints on institutional autonomy, including resource allocation and security protocols, though specific administrative alignments remain undocumented in primary sources.[27] Student life at the university emphasizes extracurricular engagement to complement academic pursuits, with the Deanship of Student Affairs organizing sports programs and physical activities tailored to student and staff participation under specialist guidance.[28] Prior to escalations in conflict, the campus environment fostered exploration and discovery, including opportunities for social and cultural involvement across its urban Gaza City locations, serving an enrollment of approximately 14,000 students.[29][22] Political tensions have historically permeated student activities; for instance, in 2008, Hamas supporters raided the campus, disrupting lectures amid perceptions of Fatah influence, highlighting factional divisions within Gaza's higher education sphere.[27] Such incidents underscore how administrative governance and student life are shaped by the territory's Islamist political dynamics, with extracurriculars often reflecting prevailing ideological currents rather than neutral civic engagement.

Political and Ideological Dimensions

Ties to Islamist Networks and Hamas

Al-Azhar University–Gaza, established in 1991 under a decree by Yasser Arafat during the First Intifada as a Palestinian national institution, has operated in Hamas-controlled Gaza since the group's 2007 takeover of the territory. Despite its origins aligned with Fatah-led Palestinian Authority structures, the university has faced documented instances of Hamas operational exploitation and influence. In December 2023, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) uncovered weapons caches, explosive devices, and an underground tunnel shaft beneath the university's campus in Gaza City, stating that Hamas had utilized the site for military activities against Israeli forces.[30] Supporting evidence included Hamas tunnel construction incorporating sacks marked with UNRWA and USAID logos, found lining the tunnel under the campus.[31] Prior to the 2023-2025 conflict, similar allegations surfaced during Operation Protective Edge in 2014, when Israeli airstrikes targeted the university, with the IDF claiming it served as a Hamas fundraising hub for donations and a command center for military operations.[32] Hamas-affiliated gunmen further demonstrated influence in 2021, when on September 21, they fired on protesters at the campus following a demonstration against restrictions imposed by Palestinian security forces, highlighting tensions but also the group's on-site enforcement capabilities.[33] Earlier, in 2008, Hamas supporters raided the university—perceived as a Fatah remnant—disrupting classes and underscoring ideological contests within Gaza's academic spaces under Islamist governance.[27] The university's ties extend indirectly to broader Islamist networks through its namesake affiliation with Egypt's Al-Azhar University, a historic center of Sunni scholarship that has provided religious endorsements for Hamas actions, including fatwas and statements post-October 7, 2023, framing the group's attacks as legitimate resistance.[34] While Al-Azhar–Gaza maintains a curriculum emphasizing Islamic studies and Sharia faculties, distinct from the more overtly Muslim Brotherhood-aligned Islamic University of Gaza, its location in Hamas territory has facilitated de facto integration into the group's ecosystem, including potential recruitment and ideological propagation amid limited oversight.[35] No public evidence confirms direct institutional funding of Hamas by Al-Azhar–Gaza leadership, such as President Omar Milad, but the repeated military utilization underscores operational convergence with the Islamist militant network.[22]

Criticisms of Indoctrination and Bias in Teaching

Critics have accused Al-Azhar University-Gaza of incorporating indoctrination into its teaching practices, particularly through faculty sermons and interpretations of Islamic texts that promote anti-Semitic narratives and glorify violence against Israel. For instance, Muhammad Mustafa Najem, a lecturer in Koranic interpretation at the university, delivered a televised Palestinian Authority sermon citing Quranic verses to describe Jews as "apes and pigs," attributing divine curses to them for alleged treachery.[36] This reflects a pattern where religious instruction frames Jews as inherent enemies, drawing from selective hadith and tafsir traditions that emphasize historical conflicts over contextual exegesis.[36] Additional reports document professors at Al-Azhar-Gaza employing Koranic passages to portray Jews as betrayers of covenants and adversaries of the Prophet Muhammad, reinforcing a worldview that justifies hostility as religiously mandated.[37] Such teachings occur amid Hamas's governance of Gaza since 2007, which has integrated the university into broader efforts to "re-Islamize" society from below, prioritizing ideological alignment with Islamist resistance over pluralistic scholarship.[6] Hamas-affiliated institutions, including universities, distribute factional materials that extol martyrdom and armed struggle, turning campuses into arenas for political indoctrination rather than detached learning.[38] The university's curriculum, rooted in Sharia and affiliated with Egypt's Al-Azhar system, exhibits biases that reject global jihadist extremism—such as ISIS—while endorsing "defensive jihad" and martyrdom in the Palestinian context, portraying the conflict as a cosmic battle against Zionist occupation.[19] Analyses of Al-Azhar educational materials reveal persistent anti-Israel hostility, including depictions of Jews as conspiratorial aggressors, despite nominal reforms in affiliated Egyptian programs.[39] Under Hamas oversight, this fosters a teaching environment where empirical historical analysis yields to causal narratives of perpetual religious warfare, sidelining evidence-based alternatives.[34] Faculty ties to pro-Hamas advocacy groups further embed these biases, as seen in professors associated with organizations defending "violent resistance."[40] These practices contrast with claims of academic neutrality, as Hamas leverages higher education for societal mobilization, evidenced by university roles in ideological campaigns post-2007 takeover.[41] While sources documenting these issues often stem from Israeli or Western monitoring bodies—potentially subject to counter-narratives of overstatement—their reliance on verbatim translations of sermons and texts provides verifiable empirical grounding, unlike unsubstantiated denials from Gaza authorities.[36] [37]

Involvement in Armed Conflicts

Pre-2023 Incidents and Military Utilization

Prior to 2023, Al-Azhar University in Gaza experienced several incidents involving political violence and internal factional clashes, primarily between Hamas and Fatah supporters. In April 2008, Hamas police and supporters assaulted professors and students at the campus, which was regarded as a remaining Fatah stronghold in Gaza following Hamas's takeover of the territory.[42] This violence included beatings and disruptions to academic activities as Hamas sought to consolidate control over educational institutions.[27] On August 26, 2008, an explosive device detonated in a classroom on the western side of the Gaza City campus, causing property damage but no injuries or fatalities; the incident's perpetrators and motives remained unclear, though it occurred amid heightened inter-factional tensions.[43] In September 2021, Hamas security forces raided the Al-Azhar University Gaza (AUG) campus, attacking and detaining students and faculty who resisted orders to participate in a pro-Hamas demonstration or related activities.[44] These actions highlighted ongoing efforts by Hamas to enforce ideological alignment and suppress dissent within higher education settings perceived as less supportive of the ruling group. Regarding military utilization, Hamas positioned weapons production sites, arms depots, and tunnel networks in close proximity to the Al-Azhar University campus as part of broader efforts to rebuild its arsenal during periods of relative calm. In July 2022, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) reported the detection of such installations near the university in Gaza City, alongside other civilian sites like schools, hospitals, and mosques, which Hamas exploited to shield military infrastructure from potential strikes.[45][46] This pattern of embedding military assets adjacent to educational facilities reflected Hamas's tactical doctrine of leveraging civilian density for operational advantage, though direct evidence of on-campus weapon storage or launches specific to Al-Azhar prior to 2023 remains limited in public reports.[45]

Destruction During the 2023-2025 Israel-Hamas War

Israeli airstrikes on November 6, 2023, targeted the Al-Azhar University campus located south of Gaza City, resulting in the destruction of multiple buildings amid broader military operations against Hamas infrastructure in the Gaza Strip.[47] In early December 2023, Israel Defense Forces (IDF) ground operations at the university's Rimal neighborhood site uncovered Hamas military assets, including caches of weapons, explosive charges, and rocket components stored in campus compounds. Troops also located an underground tunnel originating in the university yard and extending approximately one kilometer to an adjacent school, which the IDF described as part of Hamas's extensive tunnel network used for militant operations.[48][30][49] These discoveries prompted IDF strikes on the identified terror infrastructure, contributing to further structural damage.[48] The cumulative impact rendered much of the campus inoperable, with infrastructure losses estimated at around $70 million by early 2025, prior to any additional reported incidents.[20] By October 2025, the site's ruins had been repurposed as a refugee center amid ongoing displacement in Gaza.[50] Palestinian sources, including the university administration, have attributed the destruction primarily to indiscriminate Israeli bombardment, while Israeli military statements consistently cited the presence of Hamas command-and-control elements embedded within civilian educational facilities as the operational rationale for targeting.[47][48]

Notable Associates

Prominent Alumni

Motaz Azaiza, a Palestinian photojournalist born in 1998, graduated from Al-Azhar University–Gaza in 2021 with a degree in English language and literature.[51] His work documenting daily life and the impacts of conflict in Gaza has garnered international recognition, including features in major outlets and millions of social media followers for exposing conditions during the 2023–ongoing war.[52] Loay Ayyoub, a freelance photojournalist based in Gaza, earned his bachelor's degree in media, journalism, and public relations from Al-Azhar University–Gaza in 2017.[53] He has contributed to publications such as The Washington Post, The Guardian, and NPR, focusing on the humanitarian crisis in Gaza, and received awards like the ICRC Foundation Prize for his coverage of civilian suffering.[54] Hiba Abu Nada (1991–2023), a Palestinian poet, novelist, and nutritionist, obtained her master's degree in clinical nutrition from Al-Azhar University–Gaza.[55] Her works, including poetry collections and novels, addressed themes of displacement and resilience; she was killed in an Israeli airstrike in Khan Yunis on October 20, 2023.[56]

Influential Faculty Members

Mkhaimar Abusada, an associate professor of political science, has emerged as a prominent voice on Palestinian governance and Islamist movements, offering analyses of Hamas's strategic positions following the October 7, 2023, attacks and subsequent war. Educated in the United States with a PhD from the University of Missouri-Columbia, Abusada has critiqued Hamas's governance failures while noting public support dynamics in Gaza, drawing on surveys of Palestinian attitudes toward resistance and statehood.[57][58][59] His work, including as a visiting lecturer at institutions like Northwestern University after evacuating Gaza, underscores the faculty's role in bridging local Islamist contexts with international discourse, though his commentary reflects empirical polling rather than endorsement of militant ideologies.[60] In scientific fields, Samy S. Abu-Naser holds a professorship in artificial intelligence, contributing peer-reviewed papers on machine learning applications, deep learning models, and expert systems for education, with over 100 publications cited in academic databases.[61] His research emphasizes practical AI tools like intelligent tutoring systems tailored to resource-constrained environments, aligning with Gaza's academic priorities amid infrastructural challenges.[62] Mahmoud Sirdah, a professor of hematology and molecular genetics, gained international recognition by winning the 2023 Global Faculty Award for contributions to blood pathophysiology research, including genetic studies relevant to regional health issues like thalassemia prevalence in Palestinian populations.[63] Earlier, in 2016, he received a distinguished researcher award from Al-Azhar for advancements in molecular diagnostics.[64] Such accolades highlight isolated pockets of rigorous, empirically grounded scholarship within the faculty, despite broader institutional disruptions from conflict.[64] Deans like Ahmed Abu Shaban, associate professor and head of the Faculty of Agriculture and Veterinary Medicine, have influenced applied sciences by documenting war-induced devastation of Gaza's food systems, advocating for resilient agricultural models based on pre-2023 data from local crop yields and veterinary practices.[65] These efforts reveal causal linkages between academic output and survival imperatives in blockaded areas, though sourced primarily from faculty testimonies amid limited peer-reviewed outlets.[65]

Current Status and Reconstruction Efforts

Damage Assessment and Operational Halt

Al-Azhar University in Gaza sustained initial damage from Israeli airstrikes on October 11 and 21, 2023, targeting its headquarters in Gaza City and a branch in Al-Maghraqa south of the city, with reports of shelling causing structural harm to facilities.[32] Further bombardment on November 6, 2023, destroyed multiple buildings on the main campus south of Gaza City, as confirmed by eyewitness accounts and satellite imagery analysis.[47] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) justified these strikes by stating that the university compounds housed Hamas terror infrastructure, including weapons caches discovered during ground operations in the Rimal neighborhood campus in December 2023.[48] Subsequent assessments indicate severe cumulative destruction across the university's multiple campuses—main, eastern, western in central Gaza City, and southern branches—with infrastructure losses estimated at approximately US$70 million prior to the final major strikes.[20] A UN report notes the demolition of one campus in December 2023, contributing to the broader pattern of higher education facilities being targeted amid allegations of military exploitation by Hamas.[66] By October 6, 2025, intensified Israeli airstrikes reportedly leveled remaining structures in Gaza City, rendering physical operations impossible and aligning with the near-total destruction of Gaza's university infrastructure.[67] Independent analyses, including those from academic networks, describe the campus as reduced to rubble, with no intact buildings suitable for in-person activities.[68][69] Operations halted entirely for on-site classes following the early 2023 strikes, with the university shifting to online instruction by late 2023 to serve its approximately 20,000 students, though connectivity disruptions and faculty displacement limited efficacy.[20] Limited remote milestones, such as a master's thesis defense in March 2025, occurred under emergency conditions, but these did not restore physical functionality.[70] The October 2025 leveling effectively ended any residual operational capacity, contributing to the loss of higher education access for over 90,000 students across Gaza, as physical reconstruction remains infeasible amid ongoing conflict.[71][20]

International Responses and Future Challenges

International organizations have provided limited but targeted support for Gaza's higher education sector in response to the destruction of Al-Azhar University and similar institutions. UNESCO launched the Gaza Virtual Campus initiative in 2025 to deliver online courses to approximately 20,000 displaced students, including those from Al-Azhar, addressing immediate disruptions from campus demolitions during the 2023-2025 Israel-Hamas war.[24] This effort focuses on preserving academic continuity amid widespread infrastructure loss, with Al-Azhar's southern campus—housing advanced laboratories and faculties—reportedly obliterated by airstrikes in late 2023 and subsequent operations.[4] Responses from Arab and Muslim-majority entities have emphasized condemnation of the strikes, often framing them as deliberate attacks on education rather than responses to military use. Egypt's Al-Azhar institution (distinct from the Gaza branch) described global reactions to Israel's actions in Gaza as "disappointing" in March 2024, calling for unified Arab-Muslim opposition while highlighting displacement affecting 85% of Gaza's population and destruction of 60% of housing.[72] Palestinian university presidents, including Al-Azhar's leadership, issued an open letter in July 2025 urging practical international aid, asserting the bombings constituted a systematic effort to dismantle higher education foundations.[73] Western responses have been more restrained, with reports from outlets like Scholars at Risk documenting the November 6, 2023, airstrike on Al-Azhar's campus without equivalent calls for immediate rebuilding, amid evidence of prior Hamas operational ties at Gaza universities that informed Israeli targeting decisions.[47][32] Future challenges for Al-Azhar's reconstruction include severe logistical barriers in a contested zone lacking heavy machinery, as noted in October 2025 assessments where only minimal rubble clearance has begun across Gaza using a handful of bulldozers.[74] Hamas's governance and historical utilization of university sites for military purposes—such as donation collection and operational centers documented in 2014 incidents—complicate neutral funding, potentially deterring Western donors wary of indirect militant support while relying on sources like Qatar or regional actors.[32] Ongoing hostilities, including October 2025 strikes on sheltering sites at Al-Azhar, exacerbate risks of repeated damage, alongside internal Palestinian rivalries that have historically disrupted institutions like Al-Azhar during factional clashes.[75][27] Establishing security and a non-militarized academic environment remains prerequisite for viable rebuilding, as emphasized in analyses of Gaza's postwar needs.[74]
