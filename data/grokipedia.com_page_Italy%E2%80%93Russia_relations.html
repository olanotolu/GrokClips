Italy–Russia relations

Italy–Russia relations denote the bilateral diplomatic, economic, cultural, and strategic interactions between the Italian Republic and the Russian Federation, formalized following Russia's recognition of the unified Kingdom of Italy in 1862 and evolving through periods of cooperation and tension, including severance during Italy's alignment with Nazi Germany in World War II and restoration in 1944.[1][2] Despite Italy's post-war integration into NATO in 1949 and the Soviet Union's opposing Warsaw Pact membership, relations warmed after the Cold War, fostering a strategic partnership emphasized by frequent high-level summits and economic ties, particularly Italy's pre-2022 dependence on Russian natural gas imports comprising around 40% of its total supply.[3] Economic interdependence defined much of the post-1991 era, with bilateral trade reaching significant volumes driven by energy deals between entities like Italy's ENI and Russia's Gazprom, alongside cultural affinities rooted in historical exchanges and tourism, yet these were overshadowed by geopolitical divergences, notably Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea, which prompted Italy's adherence to EU sanctions despite domestic business lobbying for exemptions.[4] Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 intensified strains, leading Italy to condemn the aggression as a violation of international law, impose sanctions, provide military aid extended through 2025, and drastically reduce Russian gas imports to under 2% of its total by 2025 through diversification efforts.[5][6][7] Under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni's government since 2022, Italy has maintained firm support for Ukraine while advocating for negotiated resolutions, reflecting a pragmatic balance between transatlantic alliances and residual economic pragmatism, though coalition dynamics reveal ongoing domestic debates over escalation risks and the war's attribution, with some public sentiment questioning full culpability on Russia's side.[8][9][10] This evolution underscores causal factors like energy security imperatives and alliance commitments overriding historical sympathies, amid broader EU efforts to phase out Russian fossil fuels by 2027.[11]

Historical Background

Early Diplomatic and Cultural Ties (15th–19th Centuries)

Diplomatic contacts between Russian principalities and Italian states emerged in the mid-15th century, exemplified by the participation of a Russian delegation, including Metropolitan Isidor and Ambassador Foam of Tver, in the Council of Florence from 1437 to 1440, which facilitated re-engagement with Western Europe.[12] In the late 16th century, the Grand Duchy of Tuscany dispatched an embassy to Moscow in 1587 under Boris Godunov to negotiate free trade privileges in Novgorod and Pskov.[12] The 17th century saw intensified exchanges, with Tsar Alexei Mikhailovich sending multiple embassies to Tuscany, including missions in 1656, 1659–1660, and 1687, aimed at expanding trade in goods like caviar and forging an anti-Ottoman alliance; the 1659 embassy specifically sought formal recognition of the Tsar's hereditary titles through diplomatic correspondence and gifts.[12][13] These efforts were documented in contemporary accounts, such as Jacob Reutenfels' 1680 publication on Muscovy, based on his observations in Russia from 1670 to 1673.[12] Cultural ties during this period involved limited but notable migrations of artists and scholars. Russian embassy receptions in Tuscany, like that of 1660 featuring ambassadors Likhachev and Fomin, were artistically recorded, as in a Palazzo Pitti painting.[12] By the 18th century, Peter the Great's westernization policies spurred Italian influxes, with sculptor and architect Carlo Bartolomeo Rastrelli arriving in 1716 to contribute works such as the bust of Alexander Menshikov, laying foundations for Baroque influences in St. Petersburg.[12] His son, Francesco Rastrelli, extended this legacy by designing the Winter Palace, embodying Italian architectural expertise in Russian imperial structures.[14] Diplomatic momentum continued under Catherine II, who dispatched the Orlov brothers on a secret mission to Italy, including Tuscany, in 1767, while appointing Count Demetrio Mocenigo as chargé d'affaires in Florence in 1780; Tuscan immigrants like Frediano Quillici introduced cotton processing techniques in Russia by 1782.[12] In the late 18th and 19th centuries, relations diversified across Italian states, with formal diplomatic ties established between the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies and Russia in 1779 via the appointment of Count Andrey Kirillovich Razumovsky as the first Russian ambassador to Naples.[15] Architectural exchanges persisted, as seen in Giovanni Pietro Toscani's designs for public buildings in Simbirsk Vicariate (1784–1793) and Alexander Digby's late classicism projects in Astrakhan (1786–1803).[12] Russian elites increasingly engaged Italy culturally, with painter Orest Kiprensky studying in Florence in 1816 and producing a Uffizi self-portrait, and philanthropist Nikolay Demidov settling there in 1822 to fund institutions like a pharmacy and school; his son Anatoly later commissioned Karl Bryullov's 1833 painting The Last Days of Pompeii.[12] Architect Carlo Rossi furthered Italian stylistic impacts in St. Petersburg through Empire-style works, including the General Staff Building arch.[14] These interactions underscored a pattern of mutual artistic borrowing, driven by Russian modernization and Italian expertise, amid fragmented Italian polities.

20th-Century Relations: Tsarist Russia, World Wars, and Early Soviet Era

Diplomatic relations between the Kingdom of Italy and the Russian Empire were formalized after Italy's unification in 1861. Initially, Russia severed ties with the Kingdom of Sardinia-Piedmont in response to unification, but officially recognized the new Kingdom of Italy in 1862.[1] A bilateral trade treaty followed in 1863, fostering economic exchanges despite underlying tensions from Russia's alignment with Austria-Hungary, Italy's rival in territorial disputes.[1] Cultural and regional ties predated unification, with diplomatic contacts between Tuscany and Russia established as early as 1785, involving exchanges in art, literature, and nobility.[12] During World War I, Italy's position evolved from nominal membership in the Triple Alliance (with Germany and Austria-Hungary) to active alliance with the Entente Powers, including the Russian Empire, after declaring war on Austria-Hungary in May 1915 under the Treaty of London.[16] This shift aligned Italy with Russia against the Central Powers, though direct military cooperation remained limited due to geographical separation and divergent fronts—Italy focused on the Alps, while Russia engaged in the East.[17] Pre-war diplomacy had preserved cordial Italian-Russian ties, contrasting with Italy's strained relations with Austria-Hungary, but the Bolshevik Revolution in November 1917 disrupted this wartime partnership as Russia exited the conflict via the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk in March 1918.[18] The early Soviet era marked a sharp deterioration, with Italy joining the Allied intervention in the Russian Civil War (1918–1920) to oppose Bolshevik forces and safeguard wartime supplies from German capture.[19] Italian contributions centered on Siberia, where expeditionary forces, including the Legione Redenta (comprising Italian prisoners of war repatriated from Russia), operated from 1918 to 1919 alongside Czechoslovak legions and other Allies, aiming to support anti-Bolshevik Whites and secure the Trans-Siberian Railway.[20] These units, totaling around 2,000–3,000 personnel at peak, engaged in skirmishes but achieved limited strategic impact amid logistical challenges and Bolshevik advances; operations wound down by 1920 as Italy prioritized domestic postwar recovery.[21] No formal diplomatic recognition occurred until a preliminary de facto accord in December 1921, reflecting Italy's cautious stance toward the Soviet regime amid ideological opposition and fears of communist subversion in Europe.[22] In World War II, Fascist Italy's alignment with the Axis Powers led to direct conflict with the Soviet Union following Benito Mussolini's declaration of war on June 22, 1941, concurrent with Germany's Operation Barbarossa. The Italian Expeditionary Corps in Russia (CSIR), deployed in July 1941 with approximately 62,000 troops, supported German advances in Ukraine, capturing territories and conducting anti-partisan operations.[23] Expanded to the 8th Army (ARMIR) in 1942 with over 220,000 men, Italian forces suffered catastrophic losses—around 85,000 casualties—during the Soviet winter counteroffensive at Stalingrad, prompting withdrawal by early 1943 and exposing vulnerabilities in equipment and high command.[23] These campaigns underscored the asymmetry in Italy's military commitment to the Eastern Front, driven by ideological affinity with Nazi Germany but hampered by inadequate preparation for Soviet conditions.[23]

Italian-Soviet Relations (1920s–1991)

Diplomatic relations between Italy and the Soviet Union were formally established on February 7, 1924, when Italy granted de jure recognition to the Bolshevik regime, becoming one of the earliest Western nations to do so under Benito Mussolini's pragmatic foreign policy approach.[22][24] This recognition facilitated initial trade exchanges, though ideological tensions persisted amid Mussolini's suppression of domestic communists and the Soviet Union's promotion of world revolution through the Comintern, which influenced the nascent Italian Communist Party (PCI).[25] Relations warmed temporarily in the early 1930s amid mutual wariness of rising German power, culminating in the Italo-Soviet Pact of Friendship, Non-Aggression, and Neutrality signed on September 2, 1933, in Rome.[26] The agreement committed both parties to non-aggression, neutrality in case of third-party conflicts, and economic cooperation, remaining in effect until Italy's entry into war against the USSR.[27] Military-technical exchanges followed, including Soviet purchases of Italian naval designs and joint concerns over German rearmament, though underlying fascist-communist antagonism limited depth.[28] These ties unraveled with Italy's alignment to the Axis powers. Mussolini declared war on the Soviet Union on June 22, 1941, coinciding with Germany's Operation Barbarossa, and dispatched the Italian Expeditionary Corps in Russia (CSIR), comprising three divisions with approximately 62,000 troops, 5,500 vehicles, and 220 artillery pieces.[29] The CSIR advanced in Ukraine, securing victories like the Battle of Petrikowka (September 27–30, 1941), where Italian forces captured 10,000 Soviet prisoners and significant materiel.[23] In 1942, Italy expanded its commitment to the 8th Army (ARMIR) with 235,000 men, but Soviet counteroffensives inflicted catastrophic losses—over 85,000 casualties by early 1943, including during the Don River retreats and Stalingrad encirclement—exposing Italian logistical and equipment deficiencies against harsh winter conditions and superior Soviet numbers.[30] Post-World War II, full diplomatic relations resumed in October 1944 after Mussolini's regime collapsed, amid Italy's transition to a republic and Allied liberation.[22] The Cold War framework positioned NATO-member Italy (joined 1949) in opposition to the Soviet-led Warsaw Pact, with mutual suspicions heightened by PCI activities; the party, peaking at 1.7 million members in the 1948 elections, received covert Soviet funding estimated in millions of dollars annually until the 1980s and aligned with Moscow on foreign policy until Khrushchev's 1956 de-Stalinization speech prompted internal PCI debates.[31][32] Despite geopolitical rivalry, economic pragmatism prevailed, driven by Italy's energy needs and Soviet resource exports. A 1948 Italian-Soviet Treaty of Commerce and Navigation regulated trade, payments, and reparations, with bilateral turnover reaching $100 million by the mid-1950s.[33] The landmark 1960 ENI-Soviet barter deal exchanged 12 million tons of Soviet crude oil over five years for Italian pipes, machinery, and synthetic rubber, bypassing Western oil majors and sparking U.S. concerns over bloc discipline.[34][35] Subsequent agreements expanded machinery, agricultural, and chemical trade, with Soviet purchases of Fiat vehicles and Italian tourism to the USSR growing; by 1989, annual trade volume exceeded $5 billion, reflecting Italy's policy of Ostpolitik-like engagement despite PCI's waning Soviet loyalty amid Eurocommunism's rise in the 1970s.[36] Relations persisted until the Soviet Union's dissolution in December 1991, marking the end of this era.[22]

Post-Soviet Era and Russian Federation (1991–2010s)

Following the dissolution of the Soviet Union, Italy promptly recognized the Russian Federation as its successor state in 1991, facilitating the continuity of diplomatic and economic ties established during the Soviet era.[1] This recognition underpinned early post-Soviet normalization, with both countries emphasizing pragmatic cooperation amid Russia's economic transition. In 1994, the Treaty on Friendship and Cooperation was signed, outlining fundamental principles for bilateral relations, including mutual respect for sovereignty and non-interference.[2] [37] The agreement fostered institutional dialogue, such as the establishment of a joint council co-chaired by deputy prime ministers to promote business collaboration.[37] Economic relations gained momentum in the mid-1990s, highlighted by the 1996 Bilateral Investment Treaty, which aimed to protect and promote investments between the two nations.[38] Energy cooperation remained a cornerstone, building on the 1969 long-term gas supply contract between Italy's Eni and Soviet entities, which transitioned seamlessly to dealings with Gazprom post-1991.[1] By the 2000s, under Russian President Vladimir Putin and Italian Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi, personal rapport drove intensified engagement; Berlusconi's center-right governments (2001–2006 and 2008–2011) prioritized strategic partnership with Moscow, contrasting with more cautious European stances.[39] Key diplomatic visits included Berlusconi's trip to Moscow in October 2001 and Putin's reciprocal engagements in Italy, such as the 2003 Sardinia summit, where discussions advanced energy and trade agendas.[40] [41] The 2006 strategic partnership between Eni and Gazprom formalized joint ventures in upstream gas exploration, midstream infrastructure, and technology exchange, extending a collaboration that by 2009 marked 40 years of Russian gas deliveries to Italy.[42] This culminated in the 2007 memorandum for the South Stream pipeline, intended to diversify gas routes to southern Europe, including Italy, bypassing Ukraine.[42] [43] Bilateral trade expanded notably in the 2000s, with Russia exporting energy resources and Italy supplying machinery, vehicles, and pharmaceuticals; by 2010, Italy ranked among Russia's top European trading partners, reflecting mutual economic interdependence despite global fluctuations.[44] Italy's approach emphasized Russia's integration into Euro-Atlantic structures, as evidenced by hosting the 2002 NATO-Russia Council founding in Rome, balancing energy pragmatism with alliance commitments.[45] Divergences emerged over issues like the 2008 Russo-Georgian war, where Italy urged restraint but avoided severe sanctions, prioritizing sustained dialogue.[46]

Economic and Trade Relations

Overview of Bilateral Trade

Bilateral trade between Italy and Russia has historically been characterized by Italy's export of manufactured goods and Russia's supply of raw materials, particularly energy products. Prior to the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, total trade volume exceeded €20 billion annually, with Italy exporting machinery, pharmaceuticals, and vehicles while importing natural gas, oil, and metals. In 2021, Italian exports to Russia reached approximately US$12 billion, reflecting strong demand for high-value Italian industrial products. However, European Union sanctions imposed since February 2022, including bans on certain technology exports and restrictions on Russian energy imports, led to a sharp contraction, reducing overall trade to around €9 billion in 2023.[47][48] By 2024, Italian exports to Russia stabilized at US$4.65 billion, primarily consisting of permitted goods like food products and non-sanctioned machinery, while imports fell to US$3.78 billion, dominated by residual energy purchases despite diversification efforts. This shift resulted in a trade surplus for Italy of about US$0.87 billion. Energy imports, once accounting for over 40% of Italy's natural gas supply in 2021 (72.6 billion cubic meters), dropped below 5% by mid-2023 as Italy sourced alternatives from Algeria, Azerbaijan, and LNG markets. Quarterly data for 2025-Q2 shows exports at €809 million and imports at €349 million, yielding a €459 million surplus, though exports declined 19.4% from the prior quarter amid ongoing compliance with sanctions.[49][50][51][3][52]

Energy Sector Cooperation and Dependence

Italy's energy cooperation with Russia has historically centered on natural gas imports, facilitated by long-term contracts between Eni, Italy's state-controlled energy giant, and Russia's Gazprom. This partnership dates back to the 1960s, when Italy became one of the first Western European nations to import Soviet natural gas via the Trans-Austrian Gasleitung (TAG) pipeline, which transported Russian gas through Ukraine and Austria.[55] By the 2000s, bilateral agreements expanded, including joint ventures like the South Stream pipeline project announced in 2007 to bypass Ukraine, though it was later canceled in favor of TurkStream.[56] Eni and Gazprom also collaborated on upstream projects, such as gas supply contracts revised in 2012 to adjust prices amid market fluctuations, underscoring a strategic interdependence that positioned Russia as a reliable, low-cost supplier.[57] Prior to Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, Italy exhibited significant dependence on Russian natural gas, which accounted for approximately 40% of its total gas imports in 2021, equivalent to about 29 billion cubic meters annually out of 72.6 billion cubic meters consumed.[3] This reliance stemmed from geographic proximity to pipelines, competitive pricing, and limited domestic production, with Russia providing stable volumes via multiple routes including the Ukraine transit system and, increasingly, the TurkStream pipeline operational since 2020.[58] Eni's involvement in joint ventures, such as the Blue Stream pipeline to Turkey (co-owned until Eni's planned divestment in 2022), further entrenched this cooperation, enabling Russia to capture a substantial share of Italy's energy market despite EU diversification efforts in the 2010s.[59]

Other Sectors: Industry, Machinery, and Agriculture

Italian exports of machinery and mechanical appliances to Russia constituted a notable non-energy component of bilateral trade prior to 2022, with Italy leveraging its position as Europe's second-largest machinery exporter to supply high-precision equipment for Russian manufacturing and infrastructure projects. In 2021, Russia imported approximately €1.2 billion in machinery from Italy, including industrial automation systems and components for automotive and aerospace sectors, supported by joint ventures such as those involving Italian firms like Pietro Fiorentini in gas processing equipment production.[64] These ties were reinforced by intergovernmental agreements on industrial cooperation, including a 2019 pact between Rosneft and Italian partners for mechanical equipment development, aimed at localizing production in Russia to reduce import dependency.[64] Post-2022 EU sanctions in response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine drastically curtailed direct machinery trade, reducing Italy's exports of machines with individual functions to Russia to $4.5 million in 2024 and specialized printing machinery to under $1 million.[65] [66] Despite compliance declarations from Italian industry associations, investigations revealed circumvention via third countries, with Italian companies shipping $2.3 million in dual-use equipment—including CNC grinding machines and hydraulic presses with potential military applications—to Russia through Turkey and Kazakhstan in 2024-2025.[54] Broader industrial collaboration, such as in hydrogen technologies discussed in 2021, has stalled amid geopolitical tensions, though legacy investments in Russian metallurgy (e.g., Italian stakes in smelters supplying defense sectors) persist.[67][68] In agriculture, bilateral exchanges have been modest and asymmetrical, with Italy exporting processed food and beverages—valued at €132 million in Q2 2025—derived from its agrarian output, including wine, cheese, and pasta products, while importing negligible volumes of raw Russian grains or fertilizers directly for farming use.[51] Italian agricultural machinery exports to Russia, such as tractors and harvesters from firms like CNH Industrial, demonstrated resilience through 2023, holding steady amid a slowdown in Russia's domestic ag equipment market, which saw tractor sales decline after a post-2020 recovery.[69] Sanctions have since limited new deals, though pre-war frameworks facilitated technology transfers for precision farming tools, reflecting Italy's expertise in mechanized viticulture and horticulture ill-suited to Russia's vast grain belts but applicable in specialized crop regions. No major agricultural investment treaties exist beyond general trade pacts, with volumes dwarfed by energy flows and vulnerable to logistical disruptions from the Ukraine conflict.[47]

Bilateral Agreements and Institutional Frameworks

Investment and Economic Treaties

The bilateral investment treaty between Italy and the Russian Federation, formally the Agreement between the Government of the Italian Republic and the Government of the Russian Federation on the Promotion and Reciprocal Protection of Investments, was signed on April 9, 1996, and entered into force on June 20, 2000.[70] This treaty provides for fair and equitable treatment of investments, protection against expropriation without compensation, and mechanisms for investor-state dispute settlement, applying to investments made after January 1, 1996.[70] It remains in force as of 2025, offering Italian investors in Russia recourse under international law despite geopolitical tensions, including potential arbitration for breaches related to countermeasures post-2022.[71] Complementing the investment framework, the Convention between the Government of the Italian Republic and the Government of the Russian Federation for the Avoidance of Double Taxation with Respect to Taxes on Income and Capital was signed on April 9, 1996, and entered into force on January 18, 1997.[72] This agreement allocates taxing rights between the two states, provides reduced withholding tax rates on dividends (typically 10-15%), interest, and royalties, and includes provisions for mutual agreement procedures to resolve disputes.[72] However, following Russia's designation of Italy as an "unfriendly" country, Presidential Decree No. 585 of August 8, 2023, partially suspended certain provisions of the convention, such as those on dividends, interest, and royalties, effective from that date, though core elimination of double taxation mechanisms persist subject to Italian interpretations.[73][74] These treaties form the core of the post-Soviet economic pact, facilitating Italian direct investment in Russia, which peaked at sectors like energy and manufacturing prior to 2022 sanctions, with cumulative flows exceeding €10 billion by 2014 under the BIT's protections.[71] No major new economic treaties have been concluded since, amid EU-wide restrictions, though the existing frameworks continue to underpin residual trade and investment ties where compliant with sanctions.[70]

Sector-Specific Agreements (Health, Nuclear, and Technology)

In the nuclear sector, Italy and Russia pursued cooperation primarily through energy firms rather than state nuclear programs, given Italy's 1987 referendum ban on nuclear power generation. In March 2007, Italian utility Enel signed a memorandum of understanding with Russian state nuclear corporation Rosatom to collaborate on the design, construction, and operation of new nuclear power plants, focusing on potential joint ventures in third countries and technology exchange.[75] This was followed in April 2010 by a broader agreement between Enel and Russia's Inter RAO for cooperation on nuclear units in Russia, including feasibility studies for Enel's participation in projects like the Leningrad and Novovoronezh plants.[76] These initiatives reflected Russia's export-oriented nuclear strategy and Italy's interest in diversifying energy partnerships, though no operational plants resulted from them in Italy due to domestic policy constraints and later geopolitical tensions. Post-2022 EU sanctions following Russia's invasion of Ukraine suspended such nuclear-related engagements, aligning with broader restrictions on dual-use technologies.[77] Health sector agreements emphasized practical collaboration amid shared challenges like pandemics, though implementation varied. A key development occurred during the COVID-19 crisis in March 2020, when Russia dispatched medical aid to Italy—including ventilators, protective gear, and over 100 healthcare personnel—under Operation "From Russia with Love," coordinated via military channels to support overwhelmed Lombard regions.[78] In March 2021, the Russian Direct Investment Fund signed a deal with Italy's Leonardo and the Italian-Russian Chamber of Commerce to produce the Sputnik V vaccine at a facility near Kiruna, marking the first such agreement in the EU; production was slated to begin after 60 million doses but was halted by Italian authorities pending full European Medicines Agency validation, which was not granted.[79] These efforts highlighted opportunistic bilateral ties during emergencies but were critiqued for lacking transparency and overlapping with EU regulatory frameworks, with Russia's aid often framed by Moscow as goodwill despite questions over its strategic motives.[80] Ongoing sanctions have curtailed further health-specific pacts, shifting Italy's focus to EU-wide initiatives. Technological cooperation centered on scientific research and innovation frameworks, with agreements targeting aerospace, information technology, and advanced materials. An executive program under the 2002 Italy-Russia scientific and technological cooperation agreement outlined joint projects for 2020–2022, prioritizing areas like nanotechnology, biotechnology, and space technologies through bilateral calls for proposals managed by Italy's Ministry of Education and Russia’s Ministry of Science.[81] For instance, collaborative efforts included exchanges on high-tech manufacturing and digital infrastructure, with Italian firms like Leonardo exploring partnerships in defense-adjacent technologies pre-2022. However, EU sanctions since February 2022, including export controls on dual-use goods, have effectively frozen these activities, prohibiting transfers of sensitive technologies and limiting academic exchanges.[82] Prior to restrictions, such pacts contributed modestly to bilateral R&D, with Italy viewing Russia as a partner for applied innovations complementary to EU programs, though outputs remained limited by mismatched priorities and funding scales.

Cultural and Educational Exchanges

Cultural exchanges between Italy and Russia trace back to the 15th century, when Italian architects, artists, and engineers influenced Russian development under Ivan III, including the construction of the Kremlin's Italian-style fortifications by architects like Pietro Antonio Solari and Marco Ruffo.[83] This early period laid the foundation for mutual artistic admiration, with Italian Renaissance techniques shaping Russian iconography and architecture, while Russian elites later patronized Italian opera and ballet in the 18th and 19th centuries.[12] Post-World War II, exchanges revived under Soviet-Italian cultural pacts, featuring touring performances by the Bolshoi Ballet in Italy and exhibitions of Italian masters like Leonardo da Vinci in Moscow, fostering a reciprocal appreciation evident in events such as the 2011 Year of Russian Culture in Italy, which included over 400 initiatives like museum shows and film festivals.[84][85] Formal bilateral agreements underpin these ties, including the 1998 intergovernmental pact on cultural and educational cooperation, which promotes joint artistic projects, language promotion, and heritage preservation.[86] The 2001 youth exchange agreement facilitates direct people-to-people interactions, emphasizing comprehensive programs for adolescents and young adults to build long-term goodwill through shared experiences in arts, sports, and civic activities.[87] Scientific-cultural overlaps, such as the Russian-Italian Agreement on scientific-technological cooperation, have supported collaborative research in humanities, including linguistics and history, with institutions like the University of Turin partnering with Russia's Lobachevsky University for joint seminars and mobility since the early 2010s.[1][88] Educational exchanges have emphasized academic mobility and specialized training, with Russian students accessing Italian scholarships for studies in design, engineering, and classical arts, while Italian scholars engage in Russian language immersion at institutions like Novosibirsk State University.[89] Pre-2022 initiatives included dual-degree programs in cultural heritage management and annual quotas for student exchanges under EU-Russia frameworks adapted bilaterally, numbering in the hundreds annually.[90] However, following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, Italy suspended research collaborations with Russian entities, redirecting resources to host Ukrainian academics and halting joint educational projects to align with EU sanctions, effectively freezing most bilateral student and faculty exchanges as of 2022.[91] This disruption has limited ongoing programs, though historical ties persist in private cultural associations promoting language courses and literature translations.[5]

Political and Diplomatic Engagement

High-Level Summits and Visits

High-level diplomatic engagements between Italy and Russia featured frequent personal interactions during Silvio Berlusconi's tenures as Prime Minister, fostering a notably warm rapport with President Vladimir Putin. Their first encounter occurred at the G8 summit in Genoa on July 22, 2001, marking the start of multiple bilateral meetings that emphasized economic cooperation and energy ties.[92] Subsequent visits included Putin's trip to Berlusconi's Sardinia villa in April 2003 for informal talks on trade and security, followed by another in April 2008 amid political transitions in both nations, where they discussed EU-Russia relations and investment projects.[93] These encounters, often outside formal settings, underscored Berlusconi's advocacy for closer Italy-Russia ties, contrasting with broader EU skepticism toward Moscow's policies. In the 2000s, Russian leaders also visited Italy; President Dmitry Medvedev traveled to Bari on March 1, 2009, meeting Berlusconi to advance bilateral agreements on energy and small business cooperation, highlighted by joint appearances at local events.[2] Later, under Matteo Renzi's premiership, a sideline meeting with Putin at the St. Petersburg International Economic Forum on June 17, 2016, addressed trade amid EU sanctions following Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea, though Renzi maintained a more cautious stance aligned with European partners.[94] The Conte government marked a brief resurgence in high-level visits. Prime Minister Giuseppe Conte visited Moscow on October 24, 2018, engaging Putin in discussions on expanding economic ties and urging an end to EU sanctions, resulting in the signing of 13 agreements covering trade, culture, and technology sectors.[64] Reciprocally, Putin conducted an official state visit to Rome on July 4, 2019, meeting Conte and President Sergio Mattarella to reinforce bilateral dialogue, including a Vatican audience with Pope Francis, before relations deteriorated with the 2022 Russo-Ukrainian War.[95] No in-person summits have occurred since, limited to telephone exchanges under Mario Draghi.[96]

Italy's Stance on Russian Foreign Policy Priorities

Italy maintains a firm opposition to Russia's territorial claims in Ukraine, refusing to recognize the 2014 annexation of Crimea as legitimate and condemning it as a breach of international law and Ukraine's sovereignty.[97] This position persisted through subsequent governments, with Italy supporting EU sanctions imposed in response and later providing military assistance to Ukraine following the full-scale invasion on February 24, 2022, including artillery systems and protective equipment valued at over €1 billion by mid-2023.[98] Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, in office since October 2022, has articulated that the West erred in its tepid response to the Crimea annexation, arguing it emboldened further aggression, and has framed Russia's actions as disrupting global peace while committing Italy to Ukraine's defense within NATO and EU frameworks.[99] Regarding Russia's military intervention in Syria starting in September 2015 to bolster the Assad regime, Italy has adopted a more restrained critique, emphasizing the need for UN Security Council authorization for any external military actions and expressing unease over unilateral interventions that risk escalating regional instability.[100] Italian policymakers have acknowledged Russia's strategic objectives in Syria—such as countering perceived Western overreach and securing Mediterranean footholds—but prioritized pragmatic engagement on shared concerns like counterterrorism, migration flows, and post-conflict stabilization over direct confrontation with Moscow's role.[101] This approach reflects Italy's Mediterranean-oriented foreign policy, where Russia's presence has been tolerated as a de facto stabilizer against jihadist threats, though Rome has advocated for inclusive political settlements excluding Assad's dominance.[102] Following Assad's ouster in December 2024, Italy swiftly reopened its embassy in Damascus in early 2025, signaling a willingness to engage the new authorities while urging EU partners to ease sanctions for reconstruction, underscoring a continuity in favoring diplomatic leverage over ideological opposition to prior Russian-backed policies.[103] Broader Russian priorities, such as challenging NATO expansion and leveraging energy exports for geopolitical influence, have drawn Italian rebukes framed through alliance solidarity. Meloni's administration has highlighted Russia's hybrid tactics—including disinformation and migration weaponization—as existential threats to European security, beyond conventional military risks, while insisting that any EU measures targeting frozen Russian assets adhere strictly to international legality to avoid precedent-setting vulnerabilities.[104][105] Despite historical economic interdependencies fostering occasional pragmatism under prior coalitions, Italy's official stance under Meloni aligns resolutely with transatlantic deterrence, viewing unchecked Russian revisionism as incompatible with post-Cold War order stability.[106]

Coordination Within EU and NATO Frameworks

Italy has aligned its policies toward Russia with European Union frameworks, implementing all sanctions packages adopted since Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea and intensifying enforcement after the 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine. These measures include freezing over €2.3 billion in assets linked to Russian oligarchs and entities, excluding Russian banks from the SWIFT system, and prohibiting exports to Russia valued at €43.5 billion, primarily in dual-use goods and luxury items.[5] Italy has supported the EU's 19th sanctions package in October 2025, aimed at curbing Russia's war economy through restrictions on energy revenues and technology transfers, while contributing €390 million to the European Peace Facility for reimbursing member states' military aid to Ukraine.[107] [5] In diplomatic coordination, Italy backs Ukraine's EU candidacy status and has pushed for unified EU pressure on Russia to enable credible peace negotiations under Kyiv's terms, rejecting Moscow's preconditions as stalling tactics. Foreign Minister Antonio Tajani emphasized in September 2025 that additional sanctions are essential to compel Russian President Vladimir Putin to the table, reflecting Italy's role in consensus-building among the 27 member states despite initial domestic concerns over energy costs.[108] [107] Within NATO, Italy contributes to the alliance's collective defense against Russian threats, deploying 250 troops to the enhanced Forward Presence multinational battlegroup in Latvia and 570 personnel to the Very High Readiness Joint Task Force in 2023. The government under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni has pledged to meet NATO's 2% of GDP defense spending target by 2025, increasing budgets to enhance capabilities amid Russia's persistent aggression, as warned by Defense Minister Guido Crosetto. Italy has coordinated 12 military aid packages to Ukraine by October 2025, including air defense systems like the SAMP/T, often aligned with NATO allies' efforts such as the Presidential Ukraine Reassurance Logistics initiative for U.S. weapons procurement.[5] [109] [110] Italy has proposed extending NATO's Article 5 mutual defense umbrella to Ukraine without granting full membership, a position reiterated by Tajani to provide security guarantees while avoiding escalation, and has emphasized strengthening NATO's European pillar complementary to transatlantic ties. This coordination reflects a shift from earlier governments' occasional advocacy for Russian engagement—such as under Silvio Berlusconi or Giuseppe Conte—to post-2022 hawkishness, with NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte praising Italy's contributions to Euro-Atlantic security in 2024 and 2025.[108] [107] [111]

Impact of the Russo-Ukrainian War

Italy's Alignment with EU Sanctions and Military Aid to Ukraine

Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, the Italian government under Prime Minister Mario Draghi aligned closely with the European Union's rapid adoption of sanctions, endorsing the first package on February 25 that targeted over 500 individuals and entities, including asset freezes and travel bans on Russian oligarchs and officials.[5] Italy participated in subsequent EU measures, including bans on exports of dual-use goods and technologies worth over €48 billion annually to Russia, as well as restrictions on Russian energy imports such as the oil embargo implemented in December 2022.[112] This support extended to the EU's 17th sanctions package by mid-2025, with Italy advocating for further measures to pressure Russia into peace negotiations.[108] Under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, who assumed office in October 2022, Italy maintained this alignment despite domestic economic pressures from reduced Russian gas supplies, upholding EU sanctions packages and pushing for their enforcement, including proposals in July 2025 to exclude Russia-linked firms from Ukraine's reconstruction efforts.[113] Meloni's administration emphasized transatlantic unity, signing a 10-year bilateral security cooperation agreement with Ukraine in 2024 to formalize long-term support.[114] While coalition partner Matteo Salvini's Lega party expressed initial reservations about escalation, the government consistently voted in favor of EU decisions, including the January 2023 approval of €500 million in additional military financing for Ukraine through the European Peace Facility.[5] On military aid, Italy has delivered 11 packages totaling €2.5–3 billion by October 2025, including artillery systems, ammunition, and two SAMP/T air defense batteries co-developed with France.[115] The 12th package, under preparation as of late October 2025, focuses on further munitions and air defense enhancements, reflecting Italy's commitment to bolstering Ukraine's capabilities within NATO and EU frameworks.[110] This aid aligns with EU-wide efforts, such as the €5 billion ammunition initiative, and has been financed partly through Italy's defense budget reallocations, prioritizing systems interoperable with Western allies.[5] Despite pre-invasion energy dependencies, Italy's shift to diversified supplies enabled sustained support without major deviations from collective EU positions.[98]

Economic Costs and Energy Transition Challenges

Prior to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Italy relied on Russian natural gas for approximately 40% of its total imports, equivalent to a significant portion of its energy supply critical for industry and power generation.[3] The subsequent EU sanctions and voluntary reductions in Russian pipeline supplies led to a sharp decline, with direct imports from Russia dropping to negligible levels by late 2022, primarily through pipelines like the Trans Austria Gasleitung (TAG).[116] This disruption exacerbated an energy crisis, as Italy lacked sufficient domestic production or immediate alternatives, resulting in benchmark European gas prices (TTF) surging to over €300 per megawatt-hour in August 2022 from pre-war levels around €20-€50.[117] The economic toll included heightened inflation and industrial strain, with energy-intensive sectors such as steel, chemicals, and ceramics facing production curtailments and relocations. Italy's overall import bill for fossil fuels rose sharply in 2022, contributing to a public deficit widening to 7.2% of GDP amid emergency subsidies and support measures totaling tens of billions of euros.[118] Despite GDP growth of 3.7% in 2022 driven by construction and services, the energy shock slowed manufacturing output by up to 5-10% in affected subsectors and fueled household energy poverty, with gas prices for consumers increasing by around 47% year-on-year in early 2023.[119] [120] These costs were compounded by global LNG market tightness, where spot prices exceeded pipeline equivalents by 2-3 times, underscoring the causal link between sanction-induced supply cuts and elevated European energy expenses without proportionally impairing Russian export revenues through redirection to Asia.[121] Italy's energy transition accelerated through diversification, securing long-term contracts with Algeria (now supplying over 30% of imports), Azerbaijan via the Trans-Adriatic Pipeline, and increased U.S. LNG volumes, which rose from minimal pre-war levels to cover roughly 10-15% of needs by 2023.[58] Infrastructure expansions, including new floating storage regasification units (FSRUs) at Piombino and Ravenna operational by 2023-2024, aimed to boost regasification capacity to 70 billion cubic meters annually.[122] However, challenges persist: LNG's higher landed costs—due to liquefaction, shipping, and regasification premiums—sustain elevated wholesale prices, delaying industrial competitiveness recovery and straining the trade balance.[123] Geopolitical risks in new suppliers, such as Algeria's domestic demand fluctuations, and the slow pace of renewables integration (with solar and wind still under 20% of electricity mix in 2023) hinder a seamless shift, while coal phase-out by 2025 relies on gas bridging, perpetuating fossil dependence amid net-zero goals.[124] Domestic debates highlight these tensions, with proposals to reconsider Russian supplies reflecting recognition of transition frictions over ideological commitments.[125]

Domestic Political Divisions and Public Skepticism

The Italian government under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni has maintained alignment with EU sanctions against Russia and provided military aid to Ukraine since the 2022 invasion, yet this stance has exacerbated divisions within the ruling coalition.[126][127] Meloni's Fratelli d'Italia party has advocated for increased pressure on Russia, including tougher sanctions and exclusion of Russia-linked firms from Ukraine's reconstruction, while rejecting troop deployments.[113][128] In contrast, coalition partner Lega, led by Deputy Prime Minister Matteo Salvini, has pushed for negotiations to end the conflict, arguing that Ukraine cannot achieve victory militarily and criticizing escalation such as long-range weapons or security guarantees.[10][129] Salvini's positions, including public rebukes of French President Emmanuel Macron's calls for European troop considerations in Ukraine on August 23, 2025, have strained coalition unity and drawn diplomatic repercussions, such as France summoning the Italian ambassador.[130] Opposition parties, particularly the Five Star Movement under Giuseppe Conte, have aligned with Lega's skepticism, opposing further arms shipments and echoing calls for ceasefires influenced by figures like U.S. President Donald Trump.[129] These populist factions, historically linked to pro-Russia sentiments, represent a broader far-right and anti-establishment divide that prioritizes domestic economic burdens over sustained Western support for Kyiv, with Lega supporters showing particularly low enthusiasm—60% opposing post-war security guarantees for Ukraine per a September 2025 Youtrend poll.[131][10] This internal friction has manifested in mixed messaging, complicating Meloni's efforts to balance EU commitments with coalition stability amid fears of U.S. policy shifts under Trump.[126] Public opinion in Italy reflects similar wariness, with skepticism toward prolonged sanctions and aid growing due to energy price spikes and economic fallout.[132] A February 2023 European Council on Foreign Relations report found 23% of Italians viewing Russia as an ally or necessary partner, lower than adversary perceptions but indicative of lingering ties from pre-war energy dependence.[133] By September 2025, support for Ukraine had declined, with rising neutrality and no clear majority favoring weapons deliveries, as shown in Eupinion surveys; this shift correlates with populist voter bases and broader fatigue over sanctions' costs without decisive battlefield outcomes.[134][132] Despite initial 80% attribution of blame to Russia in 2022 polls, recent data highlights 64% support for engaging non-aligned powers, underscoring a pragmatic public preference for de-escalation over ideological confrontation.[131][135]

Security Dimensions and Controversies

Russian Hybrid Threats and Influence Operations in Italy

Russia has conducted hybrid operations in Italy aimed at undermining support for EU sanctions against Moscow, amplifying domestic political divisions, and exploiting energy vulnerabilities, particularly since the 2022 escalation of the Russo-Ukrainian War. These efforts encompass disinformation campaigns, attempts at political infiltration, and cyber intrusions, often leveraging Italy's historical Euroskeptic and populist movements to foster sympathy for Russian narratives. Italian intelligence assessments have identified a marked increase in such threats, with Russia positioned as a primary actor alongside China, targeting critical infrastructure and public opinion to erode transatlantic unity.[136] Political influence operations have focused on cultivating ties with right-wing parties, notably the Lega (League), led by Matteo Salvini, which maintained ideological affinities with Russia prior to 2022. In 2017, Lega established a cooperation agreement with Russia's United Russia party, facilitating exchanges on policy and ideology, though Salvini's party formally disavowed it in April 2024 amid heightened scrutiny. Allegations of covert funding surfaced in 2019, when Italian prosecutors investigated claims that Russian energy firm Eni transferred approximately €65 million to a bank account linked to Lega via a third party, purportedly for electoral support; Salvini denied the accusations, asserting the funds were legitimate loans, but the probe highlighted potential vulnerabilities to foreign interference. More recently, Salvini's September 2025 greeting of Russian Ambassador Alexei Paramonov at a Rome event drew criticism for signaling lingering rapport, despite the Meloni government's pro-Ukraine stance. These activities reflect Russia's strategy of backing Euroskeptic factions to hinder collective Western responses, though concrete evidence of ongoing direct funding remains limited post-2022.[137][138][139] Disinformation efforts have intensified through state-affiliated media and proxies, evading EU bans on outlets like RT by rebranding and utilizing local amplifiers. RT's documentary channel, RT Doc, launched billboard campaigns in major Italian cities in August 2025, promoting content that frames the Ukraine conflict as a Western provocation and portrays Russia as a victim of "censorship," aiming to normalize pro-Kremlin views amid public fatigue with sanctions. Pro-Russian networks have disseminated visuals and narratives denying Ukrainian agency in the war, such as false claims of NATO aggression, tailored to resonate with Italian audiences skeptical of military aid. In July 2025, Kremlin-linked lists designated Italian President Sergio Mattarella and key ministers as "Russophobes," part of a broader smear campaign to delegitimize pro-Ukraine leadership. Events accused of propagating such material, including pro-Russian gatherings in 2024, have persisted despite calls for cancellation, exploiting Italy's fragmented media landscape where alternative outlets amplify Moscow's line. These operations seek to erode consensus on sanctions, with studies noting Italy's exposure due to pre-war energy ties and populist discourse.[140][141][142] Cyber threats attributed to Russian actors or affiliates have targeted Italian entities to disrupt operations and signal retaliation. In February 2025, pro-Russian hackers, identified by Italy's cybersecurity agency, defaced around 20 websites, including those of banks and airports, in apparent reprisal for Italy's Ukraine support. The group Noname057(16), linked to Russian interests, claimed a December 2024 distributed denial-of-service attack on the Foreign Ministry and airport systems, explicitly citing Italy's "Russophobe" policies. While direct GRU attribution to Italy-specific incidents is rarer than in broader European campaigns, Russia's military intelligence has employed similar tactics in logistics and tech sectors since 2022, using supply-chain compromises like IP cameras for espionage, underscoring hybrid escalation. Italy's responses include enhanced NATO-EU coordination, but vulnerabilities persist due to reliance on imported energy and uneven public resilience to information warfare.[143][144][106]

Espionage Incidents and Diplomatic Expulsions

In March 2021, Italian authorities arrested Navy Captain Walter Biot on charges of espionage after he was caught attempting to hand over classified NATO documents to a Russian military attaché at his Rome apartment.[145] Biot, who commanded a signals intelligence unit, allegedly received €150,000 from Russian contacts for sensitive materials on NATO exercises and Italian military capabilities, prompting the expulsion of two Russian diplomats—military attachés Alexey Nemudrov and Dmitry Ostroukhov—deemed persona non grata for their involvement.[146] Italian Foreign Minister Luigi Di Maio described the incident as an "extremely serious" breach of national security, marking a rare public acknowledgment of ongoing Russian intelligence activities in the country.[147] Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Italy expelled 30 Russian diplomats on April 5, citing national security risks amid broader European efforts to reduce Russian diplomatic presence.[148] This action aligned with similar moves by Denmark, Sweden, and Spain, totaling over 70 expulsions across Europe that week, and reduced Russia's diplomatic staff in Italy to below pre-invasion levels.[149] In retaliation, Russia expelled 24 Italian diplomats—along with staff from France and Spain—on May 18, 2022, as part of a tit-for-tat measure affecting 85 Western personnel, further straining bilateral ties.[150] Subsequent investigations uncovered additional espionage efforts, including a 2022 probe into a Russian intelligence network in Naples targeting NATO's Joint Force Command and local industries, led by figures like Alexander Korshunov, a former SVR operative.[151] In November 2024, Italian prosecutors opened cases against two entrepreneurs—a 34-year-old and a 60-year-old—for allegedly procuring and selling sensitive commercial and military information to Russian agents, framing it as terrorism-related espionage.[152] By March 2025, repeated flyovers by a suspected Russian-made drone over the European Commission's Joint Research Centre near Lake Maggiore triggered a joint espionage and terrorism inquiry, highlighting persistent hybrid threats despite diplomatic reductions.[153]

Mutual Accusations and Propaganda Efforts

In February 2025, Italian President Sergio Mattarella compared Russia's invasion of Ukraine to the expansionism of Nazi Germany's Third Reich, prompting sharp rebukes from Russian officials who described the remarks as "bizarre and insane" given Italy's historical experience with fascism under Mussolini.[154][155] Russian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Maria Zakharova emphasized that the Soviet Union had liberated Italy from fascism during World War II, framing Mattarella's statement as ungrateful and propagandistic.[156] Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni defended Mattarella, accusing Russia of offending the Italian nation and rejecting the Kremlin's historical revisionism as a deflection from its aggression in Ukraine.[157] Russia has accused Italian leaders of Russophobia, exemplified by a July 2025 blacklist published by pro-Kremlin outlets labeling President Mattarella, Meloni, and other officials as anti-Russian for supporting Ukraine, which prompted Italy to summon the Russian ambassador and denounce it as Kremlin-orchestrated propaganda aimed at sowing division.[158][159] In response, Italian authorities have highlighted Russian state media's targeted smears, such as a June 2025 RT video falsely implying Meloni's drug use during a meeting with Ukrainian President Zelenskyy, reviving tactics previously used against Western leaders to undermine support for Kyiv.[160] Italian officials have reciprocated by condemning Russian disinformation as a hybrid threat, with Foreign Minister Antonio Tajani in 2024 urging the cancellation of pro-Russian events featuring figures like Alexander Dugin, whom the Ukrainian embassy accused of promoting propaganda that justifies aggression.[161] In June 2022, Italy summoned the Russian ambassador over Moscow's criticism of domestic media coverage portraying the Ukraine invasion as unprovoked, viewing it as an attempt to influence public opinion.[162] Defense Minister Guido Crosetto sued commentator Diego Fusaro in June 2025 for spreading Kremlin-aligned narratives that allegedly endangered national security by manipulating facts on the war.[163] Russian propaganda efforts in Italy have intensified post-2022, with RT evading EU sanctions through offline events like documentary screenings in multiple cities by May 2025 and billboard campaigns promoting narratives that portray Ukraine's government as fascist while depicting Russia as a victim of Western aggression.[140][141] Visual materials distributed via social media and posters have appealed to Italian cultural affinities, emphasizing anti-NATO sentiments and economic grievances from sanctions, as analyzed by the German Marshall Fund's Securing Democracy initiative in August 2024.[164] Italian counter-efforts include parliamentary debates on curbing pro-Russian outlets and think tank recommendations, such as a 2023 European Council on Foreign Relations report advocating enhanced media literacy to combat pervasive Kremlin influence in domestic discourse.[165] These exchanges reflect broader tensions, where state-backed narratives from Moscow seek to exploit Italy's political fragmentation, while Rome prioritizes alignment with EU fact-checking mechanisms to refute hybrid operations.[166]

Current Representation and Future Prospects

Diplomatic Missions and Consular Relations


Italy maintains an embassy in Moscow at Denezhny Pereulok 5, 119002, which serves as the primary diplomatic representation in Russia, alongside a consulate general in Saint Petersburg and honorary consulates in cities including Krasnodar, Yekaterinburg, and Sochi.[167][168] The Moscow embassy handles core diplomatic functions, while consular services, such as visa processing, continue through affiliated visa centers operational as of 2025.[169] Russia operates an embassy in Rome at Via Gaeta 5, 00185, with consulate generals in Milan, Genoa, and Palermo, providing consular support to Russian citizens in Italy.[170][171] These missions facilitate visa issuance and citizen services, though activities have been constrained by bilateral tensions.[172] Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, Italy expelled 30 Russian diplomats on April 5, 2022, citing national security risks.[149] Russia reciprocated on May 18, 2022, by declaring 24 Italian diplomatic and trade personnel persona non grata, reducing Italy's presence in Russia.[173][150] These mutual expulsions, part of broader Western responses, diminished staffing levels at both nations' missions but did not result in closures, with essential consular relations persisting amid ongoing geopolitical strains.[174]

Prospects for Normalization Amid Ongoing Tensions

Italy's government under Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni has maintained a firm commitment to EU sanctions against Russia as of October 2025, viewing them as essential leverage to compel Moscow toward negotiations rather than a pathway to unilateral normalization. Foreign Minister Antonio Tajani stated on September 11, 2025, that additional sanctions are required to intensify pressure on President Vladimir Putin and bring him to the negotiating table for a resolution in Ukraine.[108] This approach aligns with Italy's participation in the EU's 19th sanctions package, approved on October 23, 2025, which targets Russian energy sectors, third-country banks, and cryptocurrency providers to further restrict war financing.[175] Tajani emphasized on September 24, 2025, that Italy is not at war with Russia but prioritizes a "just and lasting peace" achievable only through Russian concessions at the table.[9] Prospects for broader normalization remain dim amid Russia's ongoing military actions and refusal to engage constructively, as perceived by Italian leadership. Meloni warned at the UN General Assembly on September 24, 2025, that Russia's aggression has disturbed global peace, underscoring the need for deterrence over détente without accountability.[176] While historical economic interdependence, particularly in energy, has fueled calls for pragmatism—evident in public opinion where 57 percent of Italians expressed neutrality toward Russia or Ukraine in a September 2025 survey—government policy prioritizes security alignment with NATO and the EU over resuming pre-2022 ties.[132] Italian officials have rejected premature lifting of sanctions, insisting on verifiable Russian withdrawal from Ukrainian territories as a precondition for any thaw, a stance reinforced by Meloni's advocacy for sustained Western unity in supporting Kyiv during talks.[177] Any normalization would hinge on a sustainable Ukraine peace deal, yet Italian assessments highlight Putin's stalling tactics as obstructive, with no bilateral initiatives signaled as of late October 2025.[178] Russia's Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov expressed expectations for continued pragmatic ties in prior statements, but these have not translated into reciprocal Italian overtures amid espionage incidents and hybrid threats.[179] Instead, Italy's focus on lawful handling of frozen Russian assets—up to 210 billion euros—reflects caution against escalation while upholding sanctions integrity, signaling that economic incentives alone cannot override geopolitical realities without Russian behavioral change.[105]
