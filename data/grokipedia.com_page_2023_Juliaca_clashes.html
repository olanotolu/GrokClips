2023 Juliaca clashes

The 2023 Juliaca clashes occurred on January 9 in the highland city of Juliaca, Peru, when security forces confronted anti-government protesters demanding President Dina Boluarte's resignation and early elections, leading to the deaths of 18 civilians mostly from gunshot wounds inflicted by police using excessive force.[1][2] These events marked the deadliest single day of a broader wave of unrest that erupted after the December 2022 impeachment and arrest of former President Pedro Castillo, with demonstrators from predominantly indigenous Aymara communities in the south expressing long-standing grievances against perceived Lima-centric governance and economic marginalization.[3][4] While the government maintained that police acted in self-defense against violent, sometimes armed protestersâ€”who in isolated cases killed one officerâ€”the preponderance of forensic and eyewitness evidence documented disproportionate and indiscriminate lethal force by state agents, refuting official narratives of restraint and prompting accusations of extrajudicial killings from international observers.[1][2] The clashes, which injured dozens more, intensified calls for accountability, including investigations into the chain of command up to Boluarte, amid a national protest toll exceeding 60 deaths and highlighting Peru's deep regional and ethnic divides.[5][6]

Historical and Political Context

Origins of the national protests

On December 7, 2022, Peruvian President Pedro Castillo announced the dissolution of Congress and called for a new constitution and legislative elections, an move interpreted as an attempted self-coup amid an impending third impeachment vote against him.[7][8] Congress swiftly rejected the decree, impeached Castillo for "permanent moral incapacity," and removed him from office, resulting in his immediate arrest on rebellion charges under Article 346 of Peru's Penal Code.[9][10] Vice President Dina Boluarte assumed the presidency later that day, marking the sixth leadership change in Peru since 2018 and intensifying political instability.[11] The transition provoked nationwide protests, primarily from Castillo's rural and indigenous base, who perceived it as a power grab by Lima's political establishment overriding the 2021 electoral mandate.[12] Demonstrators rallied for Castillo's reinstatement or release, the dissolution of Congress, and early general elections by April 2024, framing Boluarte's ascension as undemocratic despite its constitutional basis.[13][14] While underlying grievances such as entrenched corruption, economic disparities between coastal urban centers and Andean regions, and longstanding distrust of centralized Lima institutions contributed to mobilization, the protests originated directly from the fallout of Castillo's authoritarian bid to retain power rather than from Boluarte's policies or broader democratic erosion.[15] Supporters' actions reflected loyalty to Castillo's leftist agenda and resentment toward elite intervention, yet the causal sequence began with his unconstitutional decree, which alienated even some initial sympathizers and justified Congress's response under Peru's multiparty system designed to check executive overreach.[16][8]

Regional dynamics in Puno

Puno, located on the shores of Lake Titicaca and bordering Bolivia, is characterized by a predominantly indigenous Aymara population, estimated at comprising a significant portion of the region's over 1.2 million inhabitants, with Aymara communities maintaining distinct cultural practices and languages that reinforce local identities separate from coastal Peruvian norms.[17] This demographic reality has historically fueled sentiments of marginalization from Lima's centralized governance, including sporadic calls for greater regional autonomy or even separation, as voiced by some southern protest leaders who frame the capital as exploitative of highland resources without equitable returns.[18] Such dynamics stem from long-standing grievances over resource extraction policies that prioritize national exports over local benefits, exacerbating anti-central government hostility rather than fostering integrative dialogue.[19] Economically, Puno relies heavily on subsistence agriculture, pastoralism around Lake Titicaca, and informal artisanal mining, sectors plagued by informality rates exceeding 70% regionally and vulnerability to fluctuating commodity prices without robust state support.[20] Informal mining, in particular, involves small-scale operations often operating outside formal regulations, leading to environmental degradation and conflicts with central authorities enforcing crackdowns that locals perceive as threats to livelihoods essential for survival in an area with high poverty levels.[21] This economic precarity, combined with limited infrastructure investment from Lima, cultivates a baseline resentment toward federal policies seen as favoring urban and coastal elites, priming communities for mobilization against perceived neglect.[16] A pattern of prior confrontations, including frequent use of road blockades as a protest tactic in the southern Andes, reflects a preference for disruptive direct action over negotiated reforms, rooted in experiences of unaddressed abuses by security forces during earlier mobilizations.[22] Instances of impunity for state repression in past highland unrest, such as delayed investigations into excessive force, have eroded trust in institutional channels, encouraging escalation from strikes to sustained blockades that isolate regions and amplify demands for self-determination.[4] This confrontational approach, while effective for visibility, often hardens divisions, as communities prioritize symbolic assertions of autonomy amid cycles of unpunished violations by authorities, setting conditions for protests to evolve into militant standoffs without intermediaries.[23]

Immediate triggers in Juliaca

In the Puno region, where Juliaca serves as a key commercial and transport hub near the Bolivian border and Lake Titicaca, local organizations announced an indefinite general strike in early January 2023 amid escalating national protests against President Dina Boluarte's government.[24] This strike involved widespread road blockades that disrupted trade routes and commerce, amplifying economic pressure on the area as protesters sought to paralyze regional activities in solidarity with demands for political change.[25] On January 7, 2023, dozens of protesters advanced toward Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport in Juliaca, attempting to seize the facilityâ€”a critical gateway for passengers and goodsâ€”as part of the strike's intensification.[24] Initial confrontations erupted as demonstrators threw rocks at police lines and erected barricades, while security forces responded with non-lethal measures including tear gas to protect the airport perimeter.[26] These early skirmishes forced the temporary closure of the airport, halting flights and exacerbating local disruptions.[27] Protesters explicitly called for Boluarte's resignation, the dissolution of Congress, and the withdrawal of military deployments from protest hotspots, framing the actions as a direct challenge to perceived illegitimacy in Lima following Pedro Castillo's ouster.[25] [26] Police reinforcements were rapidly deployed to safeguard infrastructure, setting the stage for heightened tensions in the days ahead as local mobilizations merged with broader regional unrest.[24]

Sequence of Events

Build-up on January 9, 2023

On January 9, 2023, protests in Juliaca resumed with hundreds of demonstrators gathering north of the city around 9:30 a.m., crossing the Wonders of Juliaca bridge and marching south toward the city center.[28] By 10:30 a.m., groups reached the east end of Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport, linking up with others at the perimeter fences, while additional crowds formed along the Juliaca Bypass highway near Avenida Independencia and the south end of the airport runway.[28] Approximately 1,000 protesters remained positioned near the airport on Avenida Independencia by late morning, with organized elements employing slingshots (known locally as huaracas), stones, and homemade fireworks (avellanas) directed at police positions.[28] [29] Police responded by forming defensive lines, with about 75 officers initially stationed at the airport's east end behind damaged fences by 10:34 a.m., expanding to over 450 personnel including military support by 12:20 p.m.[28] As protesters advanced and launched projectiles targeting officers, police deployed tear gas starting at 11:38 a.m. along Avenida Independencia to disperse the crowds, followed by additional canisters at 12:28 p.m. near the airport as some attempted to breach entry points.[28] Eyewitness accounts and social media videos captured these targeted attacks on security forces, contributing to heightened tensions amid the police efforts to maintain control over key infrastructure like the airport and adjacent highways.[28] De-escalation efforts, including local radio reports of attempted dispersals, faltered as crowds surged forward in response to tear gas volleys between 12:45 p.m. and 1:05 p.m., with protesters continuing to press against police lines without successful negotiations to halt the advance.[28] Video evidence from Facebook livestreams and over 10 hours of social media footage documented the mutual escalations, showing protesters regrouping and advancing despite non-lethal countermeasures, setting the stage for intensified confrontations in the early afternoon.[28]

Escalation of confrontations

In the afternoon of January 9, 2023, demonstrations in Juliaca escalated into intense confrontations as groups of protesters from surrounding districts converged on police positions near the Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport and extended into the city's urban zones.[30] Protesters pressed forward against barricades, engaging in close-quarters exchanges that involved hurling projectiles and attempting to overrun security lines.[31] [32] Responding to mounting pressure, including assaults on police vehicles where protesters seized weapons and ignited patrol cars, security forces reported injuries among officers, prompting the use of live ammunition to repel advances.[33] [28] These exchanges occurred at short range, with police firing handguns and rifles amid the chaos, while protesters employed improvised explosives and direct physical assaults on law enforcement outposts.[32] [23] The unrest rapidly propagated through Juliaca's streets, encompassing attacks on government facilities such as police stations, described by Peruvian officials as coordinated efforts involving thousands of demonstrators armed with stones, sticks, and incendiary devices.[31] [34] One police officer perished in the fray, killed after protesters set a police vehicle ablaze during the assaults.[35] This reciprocal escalation marked the day's peak violence, with both sides sustaining immediate harm in the fluid, urban melee.[36]

Key incidents and tactics employed

Protesters in Juliaca employed barricades constructed from vehicles, debris, and other materials to obstruct key routes, including highways leading to the Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport, as part of broader efforts to enforce roadblocks that disrupted regional transportation starting from earlier demonstrations on January 4.[28] These coordinated blockades aimed to isolate the city and amplify economic pressure by halting commercial traffic. Additionally, demonstrators hurled rocks using slingshots and fired homemade fireworks, known locally as avellanas, toward police lines and a security helicopter, while some advanced with improvised handmade shields after breaching the airport's perimeter fence.[28] [37] Peruvian National Police (PNP) responded with organized formations involving over 450 officers, including Special Operations Teams equipped with riot shields to advance and push back crowds along avenues like Independencia.[28] Initial tactics included deploying tear gas canisters via grenade launchers and from a helicopter at approximately 11:38 a.m. and 1:41 p.m., respectively, to disperse gatherings near the airport.[28] [37] This escalated around 12:28 p.m. to the use of shotguns firing rubber and lead pellets, followed by a transition to live ammunition from assault rifles such as 7.62mm AKM and FAL models, with documented instances of officers taking aimed shots at individuals and groups rather than solely suppressive fire.[28] [37] Notable events included the breaching of airport fences by protesters around midday, prompting police gunfire as clashes spread citywide into the evening, and disputes over video evidence where verified footage from independent analyses showed police initiating lethal force amid protester stone-throwing, while no recordings captured demonstrators possessing firearms.[28] [37] Security camera footage near one confrontation site was reported damaged prior to the main clashes on January 6, complicating reconstructions of initiation sequences.[37] Peruvian military statements attributed initial violence to a minority of agitators among protesters using weapons and homemade explosives, contrasting with human rights analyses emphasizing police escalation.[38]

Casualties and Damage

Breakdown of deaths and injuries

The clashes on January 9, 2023, in Juliaca resulted in 18 civilian deaths, all protesters or bystanders, primarily from gunshot wounds inflicted by police firearms, as confirmed by autopsy reports documenting entry wounds to the thorax, abdomen, and head from long-range shots.[28][36] One police officer died in unclear circumstances during an early morning attack by protesters on January 10, with no autopsy details attributing it to protester weapons like stones or blunt objects.[28][36] ![Necropsias for the Juliaca massacre][center] Civilian injuries numbered approximately 226 between January 6 and 10, including pellet wounds from police shotguns and trauma from confrontational tactics, per regional prosecutorial data; many victims were young males of indigenous Aymara or Quechua descent, reflecting the demographic composition of Puno region's protesters.[39][40] Police sustained injuries nationwide exceeding hundreds from protester-thrown rocks and debris, though specific Juliaca figures remain undocumented beyond one officer wounded in the fatal incident.[1][36]

Damage to infrastructure and property

During the clashes on January 9, 2023, protesters set fire to a police patrol vehicle in Juliaca, resulting in the death of Officer JosÃ© Luis Soncco Quispe inside the burning vehicle.[23] Protesters also vandalized perimeter fencing around the Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport and accessed the tarmac, forcing the suspension of flight operations and disrupting regional air travel.[23] [41] Road blockades established by protesters in Juliaca and surrounding Puno region areas halted ground transportation, exacerbating shortages of fuel, food, and goods, which impeded local trade and contributed to economic stagnation in an already impoverished highland area.[23] These actions formed part of broader protest-related vandalism in Puno, including attacks on public buildings such as prosecutor's offices, though specific repair costs for Juliaca infrastructure were not itemized in official tallies amid national estimates exceeding 2 billion Peruvian soles (approximately $517 million) in total damages from the unrest.[23] [42]

Medical and emergency responses

The Carlos Monge Medrano Hospital in Juliaca, the city's main public medical facility, was overwhelmed by the influx of casualties from the January 9, 2023, clashes, treating dozens of patients primarily for gunshot wounds and blunt trauma injuries.[43] The hospital reported a collapse in capacity, with emergency services strained by at least 68 injuries documented that day, leading to coffins placed outside the facility as morgue space filled.[44][45] Local staff implemented basic triage protocols amid the chaos, prioritizing critical cases despite the volume exceeding routine operational limits.[46] National emergency resources were mobilized to supplement local efforts, with the Ministry of Health activating the Mobile Emergency Care Service (SAMU) to coordinate referrals for severe injuries.[47] Four of the most critically wounded patients were transferred from Carlos Monge Medrano to specialized hospitals in Lima for advanced interventions, supported by the Integral Health Insurance (SIS) system.[47] These evacuations, likely involving air transport given the regional isolation, encountered logistical challenges from protester roadblocks that impeded ambulance access and broader emergency mobility.[36] Coordination between regional health authorities and central government teams focused on immediate stabilization, though the persistent unrest around key sites like the airport limited rapid deployment of additional personnel and equipment.[28] Prosecutors and health officials verified on-site medical attention at the hospital to ensure continuity of care for the injured, amid reports of sustained violence complicating frontline response.[48]

Investigations and Accountability

Peruvian government probes

The Public Prosecutor's Office (FiscalÃ­a de la NaciÃ³n) initiated probes into the January 9, 2023, clashes in Juliaca shortly after the events, examining the circumstances of 18 civilian deaths and over 100 injuries, primarily from gunshot wounds as confirmed by autopsies and ballistic evidence.[49] [50] These investigations encompassed the deployment of lethal force by National Police (PNP) and Army personnel, including the use of service firearms and munitions during confrontations near the airport and police stations. By May 2024, the FiscalÃ­a formalized charges of qualified homicide and serious injuries against 20 identified PNP officers and soldiers for specific killings, based on forensic reconstructions, witness testimonies, and chain-of-command reviews attributing responsibility to individual conduct amid escalating violence.[51] [52] These cases formed part of 18 broader formalized inquiries into protest-related deaths nationwide, implicating 154 security personnel for harms to 267 individuals, with procedural steps including detention requests and evidentiary seizures.[49] Prosecutorial efforts extended to protester actions, including analyses of improvised and smuggled weaponry allegedly used in attacks that resulted in one PNP officer's death by firearm, alongside arrests for rebellion, sedition, and property damage tied to organizational roles in the unrest.[53] Preliminary forensic reviews indicated mutual exchanges of projectiles and aggressive tactics by demonstrators, such as attempts to breach secured facilities, though state-issued arms demonstrated superior lethality in the casualty imbalance.[54]

Role of human rights organizations

Human Rights Watch (HRW) conducted a detailed reconstruction of the January 9, 2023, clashes in Juliaca using video footage, autopsy reports, and witness interviews, concluding that Peruvian security forces employed disproportionate and indiscriminate force, including the use of pellets and live ammunition against protesters and bystanders not posing immediate threats.[1] [28] Their analysis identified 18 deaths from injuries sustained that day, with autopsies revealing pellet wounds as a primary cause in several cases, and alleged that such tactics amounted to potential extrajudicial killings by failing to adhere to international standards on use of force.[23] However, HRW's methodology relied heavily on publicly available videos and selected medical evidence, which may limit comprehensive causal attribution amid chaotic confrontations involving protester rock-throwing and a police officer's death in unclear circumstances.[1] Amnesty International similarly documented excessive force in Juliaca through forensic reviews and footage, highlighting pellet and gunshot injuries leading to fatalities and urging investigations into senior officials for possible command responsibility in the repression.[55] [5] Their reports noted that while protesters initiated some attacks with improvised weapons, security responses escalated to lethal levels without de-escalation attempts, contributing to patterns observed nationwide where at least 49 deaths occurred between December 2022 and March 2023, many from pellets or bullets.[56] [57] Amnesty's emphasis on racial targeting in southern regions like Juliaca drew from injury data but faced critiques for underemphasizing protester agency in triggering escalations, as their evidence selection prioritizes state accountability over mutual violence dynamics.[4] Both organizations advocated for independent probes into police and military chains of command, citing 2023-2024 data on over 40 protest-related deaths and hundreds of pellet-induced injuries across Peru, while calling for reforms to non-lethal policing protocols.[23] [5] Their empirical contributions, grounded in verifiable media and medical sources, advanced documentation of force patterns but were constrained by access limitations and potential confirmation bias in interpreting incomplete footage, as alternative analyses have highlighted protester blockades and ambushes preceding lethal responses.[1]

Disputes over evidence and chain of command

Investigations into the Juliaca clashes revealed inconsistencies between official accounts and forensic evidence, particularly regarding the location and nature of fatalities. Government statements claimed that security forces remained confined to the airport perimeter and that deaths occurred primarily outside this area due to protester aggression, but verified videos demonstrated police and military personnel advancing 1.8 kilometers from the airport to engage crowds, with shootings documented inside and near the facility.[1] Autopsies of 15 civilian deaths confirmed gunshot wounds from police-issued assault rifles, handguns, and shotguns, including three from shotgun pellets, contradicting assertions of minimal force usage.[1] [36] Ballistics analyses further fueled disputes, as recovered projectiles matched security force weaponry, while Peruvian National Police officials reported no firearms seized from protesters in Juliaca, undermining self-defense justifications predicated on armed civilian threats.[1] Videos corroborated that shots were fired at unarmed individuals, including those fleeing or at distances exceeding immediate peril, with forensic reports indicating horizontal firing patterns inconsistent with defensive protocols.[36] These findings prompted calls for independent ballistic tracing to distinguish state-issued from potential civilian munitions, though official probes have been criticized for inadequate scene preservation and delayed inspections until October 2023.[5] Chain-of-command scrutiny extended to President Dina Boluarte and senior officers, with evidence of operational plans signed by police commanders that classified protesters as "terrorists" and authorized deployment of heavily armed units with lethal ammunition permissions, despite prior protest fatalities in other regions.[5] Boluarte, as commander-in-chief, publicly endorsed security responses and promoted officials linked to these plans without intervening to curb escalating force, raising questions of superior responsibility under Peruvian law.[5] Regional officers faced parallel inquiries for failing to enforce de-escalation amid known risks, though investigations noted a pattern of archived internal probes absolving superiors. Counter-narratives examined protester coordination, but lacked empirical support for organized incitement via firearms, with available evidence limited to non-lethal items like fireworks in isolated videos.[1][58] ![Necropsies related to the Juliaca clashes][center]

Controversies and Debates

Claims of excessive police force

Human Rights Watch documented that on January 9, 2023, Peruvian security forces employed disproportionate and indiscriminate lethal force during protests in Juliaca, resulting in 18 civilian deaths from gunshot injuries.[1] Autopsies reviewed by the organization indicated 15 fatalities from bullets matching police assault rifles and handguns, such as 7.62mm rounds from AKM rifles and 9mm pistol ammunition, alongside three deaths from shotgun pellets consistent with police-issued weapons.[28] Amnesty International similarly analyzed forensic evidence, attributing 15 deaths to live ammunition and highlighting the use of prohibited metal pellets in shotguns, which violated protocols limiting such munitions to specific threats not evident in the crowd dynamics.[37] Wound patterns from autopsies underscored allegations of overreach, with multiple victims sustaining shots to the head, chest, and backâ€”areas incompatible with international standards for crowd control, which prioritize non-lethal measures and targeted warnings over lethal intent.[37] [1] For instance, Amnesty reported six head wounds, ten chest entries, and cases like Marco Antonio SamillÃ¡n, shot in the back while rendering first aid, and Eberth Mamani, killed by a 9mm headshot.[37] Human Rights Watch noted shootings occurred across dispersed locations, including inside the airport perimeter and 1.8 kilometers away, with forces firing into groups from elevated positions and helicopters, exceeding thresholds for de-escalation in urban protest settings.[28] [1] These claims contrast with official assertions of restrained response, as ballistic matches and eyewitness accounts refuted denials of police presence at key sites, suggesting a pattern of escalated ammunition deployment beyond non-lethal precedents in prior Peruvian unrest.[1] The scaleâ€”over 100 injuries, including 17 from firearm projectilesâ€”has been cited by investigators as evidencing a failure to adhere to principles of necessity and proportionality under the UN Basic Principles on the Use of Force and Firearms by Law Enforcement Officials.[37] [28]

Evidence of protester aggression

Protesters in Juliaca engaged in aggressive actions against police during the January 9, 2023, clashes, including hurling rocks and other objects using improvised slingshots while shielding themselves behind metal plates and road signs.[59][60] Footage and photographs captured demonstrators launching projectiles at security forces positioned in the city center.[61] Such attacks contributed to injuries among police officers, with reports indicating contusions from stones as a primary cause of harm to hundreds of personnel across the Puno region protests, including Juliaca.[62] The Peruvian Ministry of the Interior documented vandalism and arson attempts targeting police vehicles and facilities during these events, exacerbating confrontations beyond initial assemblies.[63] Earlier disturbances in Juliaca on January 5, 2023, saw groups of protesters initiate assaults on officers with stones, sticks, and other blunt objects, injuring 22 police and setting the pattern for escalated violence in subsequent days.[64] These incidents of preemptive and sustained projectile barrages underscore protester-initiated aggression, as verified by official police accounts and visual evidence, countering portrayals of uniformly passive demonstrators.

Broader implications for protest policing

The 2023 Juliaca clashes intensified doctrinal debates in Peru over rules of engagement for security forces confronting violent assemblies, balancing imperatives of public order against constitutional protections for peaceful protest. Proponents of robust policing, including government statements, argued that lethal force becomes defensible when mobs pose imminent threats to life or critical infrastructure, as seen in documented protester assaults involving stones, slingshots, and attempts to overrun police lines, which injured dozens of officers and killed one in Juliaca on January 9.[35][65] In contrast, analyses from human rights monitors highlighted discrepancies between reported threats and force applied, with ballistic evidence suggesting use of live ammunition against retreating or distant groups, raising questions of proportionality under international standards like the UN Basic Principles on the Use of Force.[66][5] These events directly shaped national policy, culminating in Supreme Resolution 013-2023-DE, issued February 20, 2023, which updated the Armed Forces' Rules of Use of Force (RUF) to incorporate graduated escalation protocols during civilian unrest support roles. The revised RUF explicitly banned indiscriminate fire and mandated de-escalation where feasible, responding to criticisms of ad hoc military involvement amid states of emergency declared since December 2022; however, enforcement gaps persisted, as subsequent probes revealed inconsistent adherence in high-tension scenarios.[67][68] Such deployments, totaling thousands of troops across affected regions, achieved deterrence by restoring access to blocked highways and institutions but were critiqued for inflaming local resentments, particularly in indigenous-majority areas where historical mistrust amplified escalatory cycles.[69][16] Comparatively, the 2022-2023 protest cycle exhibited reciprocity patterns, with elevated security responses correlating to protester-initiated disruptions like indefinite roadblocks that stranded civilians and caused 11 non-shooting deaths nationwide. In southern departments like Punoâ€”site of Juliacaâ€”where sustained sieges targeted police stations and airports, 46 of 49 total civilian fatalities occurred, underscoring how mutual escalations in peripheral zones outpaced urban containment efforts elsewhere.[66][65] This dynamic fueled arguments for refined engagement doctrines prioritizing early intelligence on armed elements within crowds, though without evident shifts toward non-lethal alternatives by mid-2023 as protests subsided.[70]

Responses and Perspectives

Government and security forces' position

The administration of President Dina Boluarte described the January 9, 2023, clashes in Juliaca as defensive measures against violent disruptions orchestrated by radical elements intent on overthrowing constitutional order. Boluarte explicitly rejected characterizations of the events as peaceful demonstrations, stating, "This is not a peaceful protest. It's a violent action by a group of radical people who have a political and economic agenda."[36] She emphasized that security forces acted to counter aggression that included attempts to seize the Inca Manco CÃ¡pac International Airport, arson against police vehiclesâ€”such as a tank set ablaze outside the facilityâ€”and widespread attacks on public infrastructure.[24] Security officials from the Peruvian National Police (PNP) and Ministry of the Interior highlighted the imperative of self-preservation amid direct threats to personnel, noting that officers faced barrages of stones, improvised explosives, and coordinated assaults that injured dozens in the Puno region on that day.[71] The government reported broader protest-related harms, including over 980 PNP officers injured nationwide by January 2023, with one officer fatality, underscoring the necessity of lethal force to reestablish the state's monopoly on violence when non-lethal options failed against sustained mob violence.[72] Authorities maintained that no generalized calls for protester surrender were feasible or heeded, as aggressors ignored dispersal orders and escalated confrontations, framing the response as proportionate to imminent dangers rather than premeditated excess.[1] The Boluarte government categorically dismissed "massacre" designations, attributing civilian casualties to the inherent risks of clashes initiated by non-compliant radicals labeled as terrorists or criminals, without evidence of indiscriminate targeting.[73] Officials argued that property destructionâ€”encompassing torched police posts and vehiclesâ€”and officer endangerment validated the deployment of live ammunition as a last resort to prevent anarchy, prioritizing public safety and institutional integrity over concessions to unlawful demands for governmental dissolution.[74]

Views from protesters and regional leaders

Protesters in Juliaca and surrounding Puno region portrayed the clashes as emblematic of systemic oppression by Lima's central authorities, labeling the response to demonstrations a "genocidio" or massacre orchestrated under President Dina Boluarte's administration, with demands centering on her resignation, the release and reinstatement of ousted President Pedro Castillo, and convocation of a constituent assembly for constitutional reform.[23][75] Regional leaders, including those representing Aymara indigenous communities, framed the unrest as righteous resistance against a "genocidal" regime, invoking historical grievances of exclusion from national decision-making and echoing calls for greater southern autonomy amid perceived favoritism toward coastal elites.[76][77] Testimonies from participants highlighted perceived humiliations, such as derogatory dismissals of Puno as "not Peru" by Boluarteâ€”statements that inflamed local sentiments of cultural erasure and economic sidelining in one of Peru's poorest regions, where high poverty rates and limited infrastructure exacerbate rural-urban divides.[78] These narratives positioned militancy as a defensive response to decades of neglect, with leaders like those coordinating mining-area blockades refusing dialogue with the government and vowing an "endless battle" until accountability for alleged prior abuses, including unresolved corruption and resource mismanagement under previous regimes, was addressed.[79][80] While such viewpoints underscore verifiable regional disparitiesâ€”Puno's per capita income lags national averages by over 30% and indigenous groups report persistent discriminationâ€”their emphasis on Castillo's reinstatement overlooks his own dissolution of Congress on December 7, 2022, which precipitated his removal, suggesting ideological loyalty amplified mobilization beyond purely socioeconomic drivers.[65] This framing, though resonant locally, inadequately accounts for how protester tactics, including institutional takeovers, contributed to escalation, as opposed to grievances alone dictating the shift to violence; calls for probes into authority impunity, tied to strike origins in late 2022, similarly blend legitimate demands with partisan revisionism of events leading to Castillo's arrest.[16][79]

International observations and criticisms

The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR), part of the Organization of American States (OAS), issued a report on May 3, 2023, documenting excessive and indiscriminate use of force by Peruvian state officers during the January 9 clashes in Juliaca, while also observing that protests since December 7, 2022, included unwarranted violence by demonstrators exceeding the bounds of the right to protest.[2] The IACHR's on-site visits highlighted instances where security forces fired lethal ammunition into crowds, contributing to 18 protester and bystander deaths, but emphasized the need for proportional responses amid documented protester actions such as property destruction.[2] United Nations human rights mechanisms, including the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR), condemned the clashes on January 10, 2023, calling for investigations into at least 17 deaths in Juliaca, attributing many to security forces' actions, and noting the storming of Juliaca airport by approximately 5,000 protesters alongside destruction of public infrastructure.[81] [82] UN experts in March 2023 urged an end to violence on both sides, expressing concern over arbitrary killings and repression while stressing protesters' obligation to respect human rights and disarm.[83] These assessments, based partly on remote monitoring and limited field access, contrasted with domestic claims by balancing criticism of state force against evidence of protester escalation, though verification challenges persisted due to chaotic conditions and restricted independent access.[84] Non-governmental organizations like Human Rights Watch (HRW) and Amnesty International issued reports in April and May 2023 critiquing the government's response as disproportionate, with HRW's reconstruction of the Juliaca events refuting official narratives by citing video evidence of police firing into crowds, resulting in 18 fatalities, while downplaying protester armament and attacks on officers.[1] [58] Amnesty's analysis framed the repression as potentially racially motivated against indigenous-majority protesters, focusing on lethal force without equivalent emphasis on data showing protesters' use of improvised weapons and assaults on police stations.[85] Such reports, often reliant on victim testimonies and selective footage, have faced scrutiny for institutional tendencies toward amplifying anti-state narratives in unrest scenarios, potentially underweighting empirical indicators of mutual violence.[28] International media outlets, including The Guardian and Al Jazeera, covered the events with prominent focus on the 17-18 protester deaths in Juliaca on January 9-10, 2023, portraying the city as "under siege" and emphasizing humanitarian tolls amid anti-Boluarte unrest, with minimal contemporaneous reporting on verified protester aggression such as the airport incursion or attacks yielding one police fatality.[86] [61] This coverage aligned with broader patterns in left-leaning global press favoring sympathy for demonstrators challenging incumbents, often sidelining on-ground data on violence initiators, as evidenced by lower prominence given to OAS backing of Boluarte's government on January 25, 2023, amid calls for stability over immediate electoral concessions.[87] Diplomatic commentary, including from the EU, deplored casualties but contextualized pressures for reforms against risks of democratic erosion from prolonged instability, prioritizing verified de-escalation over unilateral blame.[86]
