Boilermaker

A boilermaker is a tradesperson who fabricates steels, iron, or copper into boilers and other large containers intended to hold hot gas or liquid, as well as maintains and repairs boilers and boiler systems.[1] Although the name originated from craftsmen who made boilers, boilermakers assemble, maintain, and repair other large vessels and closed vats, in addition to boilers. The boilermaker trade evolved from industrial blacksmithing; in the early nineteenth century, a boilermaker was called a _boilersmith_. The involvement of boilermakers in the shipbuilding and engineering industries came about because of the changeover from wood to iron as a construction material. In the UK, this effective monopoly over an important skill of the industrial revolution led to boilermakers being labeled "the labour aristocracy" by historians.[2]

Application

Boilermaking, welding, and fitting tubes can be a full-time requirement at power plants. Stress fractures, leaks, and corrosion demand continual repair or replacement resulting from operation at very high steam pressures. Other boilermakers might work seasonally or on an individual project such as re-fitting a boiler in a seagoing vessel or remodeling a steam plant. Boiler repair can be a high-value, high-cost service, with replacement costs for even small commercial boilers often exceeding $20,000.[3]

Domestic boilers in the United States of America

In the United States, many boilermakers are employed in repairing, repiping, and re-tubing commercial steam and hot water boilers used for heating and domestic hot water in commercial buildings and multi-family dwellings.[4] Sometimes these boilers are referred to as pressure vessels. Generally, a pressure vessel is a storage tank or vessel that has been designed to operate at pressures above 15 psi (1.03 bar; 103 kPa). The two main responsibilities of boilermakers are the use of oxy-fuel gas torch sets to cut or gouge steel plate and tubes, followed by various arc welding processes to attach and mend the cut sections of tubes and steel plates.[4][5]

Training and certification in the United States

Boilermakers usually go through a formal apprenticeship which includes about four years (typically 6,000 hours) of paid on-the-job training combined with classroom instruction, though some union locals offer three-year programs. Unions and many employers offer these apprenticeships. To become an apprentice, one must be at least 18 years old and have a high school diploma or GED. Attending a trade or technical school combined with employer-provided training can accelerate the learning curve.[6] As of 2024, efforts to increase diversity in apprenticeships, including for women and minorities, are ongoing through programs by the International Brotherhood of Boilermakers and the U.S. Department of Labor.[7][8] According to 2024 U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics data, boilermakers remain one of the most male-dominated occupations in the USA, with approximately 99% males and 1% females (19,000 males and 200 females employed).[9]

R Stamp Welding

Boiler repair in the United States is governed by the National Board of Boiler and Pressure Vessel Inspectors and the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME). The National Board issues an R Certificate of Authorization and R symbol stamp to organizations (companies) that meet quality control requirements under the National Board Inspection Code (NBIC) for repairing and altering boilers, pressure vessels, and related items. To obtain the R stamp, companies must submit an application, demonstrate a compliant quality system, and undergo an onsite review; it is often held alongside ASME construction stamps. Boilermakers and welders employed by R-authorized companies must be qualified to perform welds per ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code Section IX, involving testing of weld procedures and welder performance. Qualified welders typically mark their work with an identifying number using steel stencils.[10][11]

Power Piping

Welding, fitting, and installing the tubes and accessories that attach to the boiler can also be performed by boilermakers and is governed by the National Board of Boiler and Pressure Vessel Inspectors and ASME. Such work must comply with ASME B31.1, the Code for Power Piping, which covers design, fabrication, inspection, and testing. Boilermakers performing this work require qualifications under ASME Section IX for welding to the B31.1 standards.[12]

Trade unions

United States and Canada

The International Brotherhood of Boilermakers, Iron Ship Builders, Blacksmiths, Forgers and Helpers is a labor union for boilermakers in Canada and the United States.

UK

In the UK, boilermakers traditionally joined the Boilermakers Society which merged with the General and Municipal Workers' Union (GMWU), then an unskilled union, to form the modern GMB which still maintains a "craft section" largely for boilermakers.
