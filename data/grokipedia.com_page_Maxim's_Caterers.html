Maxim's Caterers


Maxim's Caterers Limited is a Hong Kong-headquartered multinational food and beverage company founded in 1956 by brothers S.T. Wu and James Tak Wu, initially as a single Western-style restaurant in Central, Hong Kong.[1][2]
The company has expanded into one of Asia's largest restaurant operators, managing over 2,000 outlets that encompass diverse brands in Chinese cuisine such as Jade Garden and Maxim's Palace, fast-food outlets like Maxim's MX, bakeries renowned for mooncakes and pastries, corporate and institutional catering services, and franchise agreements for international chains including Starbucks and Shake Shack, primarily in Hong Kong, mainland China, and select Southeast Asian markets.[1][3][4]
Ownership is split equally between DFI Retail Group, a subsidiary of Jardine Matheson, and Hong Kong Caterers Limited controlled by the Wu family, with the latter retaining operational decision-making authority; it serves around 630,000 meals daily through large-scale food production facilities and has pioneered expansions like its 1980 joint venture in Beijing and a 2023 milestone of 2,000 stores.[2][5][1]
While achieving dominance in Hong Kong's catering sector, Maxim's has encountered scrutiny over environmental practices, including protests against shark fin offerings despite sourcing claims limited to sustainable species, and isolated food safety violations resulting in fines.[6][7]

History

Founding and Early Expansion (1956â€“1970s)

Maxim's Caterers was established in 1956 by brothers James Tak Wu and S.T. Wu, along with a small group of shareholders, initially operating under the name Hong Kong Caterers before adopting the Maxim's brand.[8][9] The inaugural outlet, Maxim's Restaurant, opened on December 3, 1956, in Lane Crawford House, Central, Hong Kong, offering Western fine dining that introduced upscale European-style cuisine to the post-war local market.[1] This high-end focus catered to an emerging affluent clientele amid Hong Kong's economic recovery.[10] In the mid-1960s, the company began diversifying beyond full-service restaurants by launching casual formats. The first Maxim's Boulevard cafÃ© debuted in 1966 at Ocean Terminal, Tsim Sha Tsui, providing affordable Western meals in a more accessible setting, while the inaugural Maxim's Cakes bakery opened in the same venue, capitalizing on demand for packaged baked goods.[1] These additions marked an early shift toward broader market penetration while maintaining a premium positioning.[11] The 1970s saw strategic expansion into Chinese cuisine to complement the Western offerings. In 1971, Jade Garden opened in Star House, Tsim Sha Tsui, introducing Cantonese dishes with Western service standards, such as white tablecloths and formal waitstaff, to appeal to both locals and expatriates.[1][12] This innovation addressed the growing preference for authentic regional flavors in a refined environment. In October 1972, Maxim's Caterers Limited was incorporated as a holding company to oversee the Maxim's and Jade Garden brands, formalizing operations ahead of further growth.[1] By the late 1970s, the company extended its Chinese portfolio with Peking Garden outlets, emphasizing Northern Chinese specialties and multiple branches to build scale.[13]

Partnerships and Mass-Market Growth (1980sâ€“1990s)

In 1980, Maxim's Caterers established Beijing Air Catering Company Ltd., marking the company's first Sino-foreign joint venture and initial foray into mainland China, which facilitated expansion into airline catering services and laid groundwork for broader market penetration in the region.[1] This partnership with Chinese entities enabled Maxim's to navigate regulatory barriers and tap into emerging demand for Western-style catering amid China's opening to foreign investment.[1] By 1982, Maxim's launched Maxim's Cakes, introducing Hong Kong's first bakery chain in Mass Transit Railway (MTR) stations, which targeted everyday commuters and shifted focus toward accessible, affordable products to capture mass-market consumers beyond high-end dining.[1] This initiative capitalized on the growing urban mobility and convenience food trends, with outlets offering baked goods and snacks that appealed to a broader demographic, contributing to the proliferation of chain-style retail in public transport hubs.[1] A pivotal partnership formed in 1986 with Dairy Farm International Holdings, a subsidiary of the Jardine Matheson Group, under which Dairy Farm acquired a 50% stake in Maxim's, providing capital and distribution synergies to fuel domestic and regional scaling.[1][14] This alliance supported the buildup of supply chains and outlet networks, enabling Maxim's to grow its fast food and bakery segments amid Hong Kong's economic boom.[15] Into the 1990s, expansion accelerated with the 1991 opening of Beijing Jade Garden, Maxim's first full-service restaurant in mainland China, further diversifying into the burgeoning consumer market there.[1] By 1998, the establishment of m.a.x. concepts centralized management of diverse restaurant formats, optimizing operations for both mass-oriented chains and themed venues to sustain growth amid intensifying competition.[1] These developments, bolstered by the Dairy Farm tie-up, positioned Maxim's as a dominant player in Hong Kong's catering sector, with increased emphasis on volume-driven segments like quick-service outlets and branded products.[1]

Leadership Changes and Brand Diversification (2000sâ€“2010s)

In early 2000, S.T. Wu, co-founder of Maxim's Caterers, stepped down as managing director, passing leadership to his grandson Michael Wu Wei-kuo, then aged 29, who had previously served as the company's chief financial officer.[16] Michael Wu, who later became chairman, initiated a strategic pivot toward broader market segments, emphasizing international partnerships and multi-cuisine offerings to appeal to younger demographics and reduce reliance on traditional Cantonese dining.[10] This generational shift aligned with economic pressures in Hong Kong, including competition from mainland China tourism and shifting consumer preferences post-Asian financial crisis.[10] A cornerstone of this diversification was the May 2000 joint venture with Starbucks Coffee International, Inc., which introduced the brand to Hong Kong and Macau, establishing Coffee Concepts Ltd. and expanding to over 140 outlets by the mid-2010s.[1] [10] Under Wu's direction, Maxim's ventured into non-Chinese cuisines, launching operations for Japanese (Genki Sushi and sen-ryo in 2006), American (Lawry's The Prime Rib in 2006), and other formats, while acquiring Arome Bakery in 2008 to bolster bakery production.[1] [10] These moves extended to franchised introductions like IPPUDO ramen from Japan in 2011 and COVA Italian bakery in 2016 for Hong Kong and Macau.[1] By the 2010s, diversification accelerated with licensing agreements for Shake Shack in Hong Kong, Macau, and East China in 2017, and The Cheesecake Factory at Shanghai Disney Resort in 2016, alongside joint ventures for Japanese concepts in Southeast Asia.[1] These expansions supported infrastructure builds, including a 150,000 sq. ft. cakes and bakery factory in Tai Po in 2012 and a 100,000 sq. ft. food factory in 2014, enabling scaled production for new brands.[1] Overall, the period saw outlet numbers surpass 900 and employee headcount double to over 24,000, reflecting successful adaptation to globalized dining trends while maintaining core strengths in catering and baked goods.[10]

Ownership and Corporate Governance

Key Shareholders and Ownership Evolution

Maxim's Caterers Limited was founded on April 18, 1969, as a private company, but its origins trace to 1956 when brothers Dr. James Tak Wu (Wu Ting-kwong) and S.T. Wu established the business through Hong Kong Caterers Limited, which initially held full ownership and operational control.[17][11] Initially family-controlled, the Wu family retained majority decision-making authority even as external partners entered.[6] In 1972, Hongkong Land, a subsidiary of the Jardine Matheson Group, acquired a minority stake, marking the first significant external investment and providing capital for expansion while the Wu family maintained primary control via Hong Kong Caterers Limited.[1] This partnership deepened in 1986 when Maxim's formed a formal alliance with Dairy Farm International Holdings Limited, another Jardine Matheson entity, evolving into a 50/50 joint ownership structure between Dairy Farm (now under DFI Retail Group) and Hong Kong Caterers Limited.[1][4] Under the current arrangement, Dairy Farm International Holdings Limited holds 50% of shares, contributing expertise in retail and supply chain, while Hong Kong Caterers Limited, managed by the Wu family, owns the remaining 50% and exercises de facto decision-making power through its board representation and operational leadership.[4][6] Michael Wu, grandson of co-founder James Tak Wu, serves as chairman and managing director, overseeing strategy amid this balanced ownership.[18] No major ownership shifts have occurred since the 1986 restructuring, with the structure supporting diversification into licensed brands without diluting family influence.[6]

Management Structure and Key Figures

Maxim's Caterers Limited maintains a board of directors comprising executive directors from the founding Wu family and non-executive representatives from its co-owner, Dairy Farm International Holdings Limited, reflecting its joint ownership structure established in 1992.[6] The board oversees strategic decisions, with family members holding pivotal executive roles to ensure continuity from the company's origins.[19] Michael Wu Wei-kuo serves as Chairman and Managing Director, a position he has occupied since 2000; as grandson of co-founder S.T. Wu, he directs overall operations across the group's extensive portfolio of over 2,000 outlets.[19][18] Wilson Wai Tsuen Wu, son of co-founder James Wu, acts as Executive Director and Company Secretary, contributing to governance and administrative functions.[19][6] Shun Tak Wu, a co-founder and early Managing Director until 2000, held the role of Honorary Chairman from 2000 to 2003, symbolizing the intergenerational transition within the Wu family leadership.[19] Non-executive directors, including figures like Benjamin William Keswick and Ian James Winward McLeod affiliated with Dairy Farm, provide oversight on financial and operational matters without day-to-day involvement.[19] Day-to-day operations are supported by a layer of senior executives, including regional Chief Operating Officers such as Keith Siu for Hong Kong and Macau, and Norbert Tan for South and East Asia, reporting to the Managing Director amid the group's expansion into multiple cuisines and markets.[20] This structure balances family stewardship with professional management, enabling scalability while retaining Wu family control through entities like Hongkong Caterers Limited, which handles key advisory and fee-based services.[6]

Business Operations

Core Business Segments

Maxim's Caterers Limited's core business segments include restaurant operations, bakery and confectionery production, catering services, and food manufacturing and distribution, collectively supporting over 2,000 outlets across Asia.[4] Restaurant operations form a primary segment, encompassing high-end and casual dining outlets offering Chinese, Western, Japanese, Thai, Vietnamese, and other Asian cuisines, alongside licensed international brands such as Starbucks, The Cheesecake Factory, Shake Shack, and Genki Sushi.[4] [21] These establishments cater to diverse consumer preferences, from quick-service formats introduced in 1972 to full-service venues, generating significant revenue through high-volume locations in Hong Kong, mainland China, and Southeast Asia.[11] The bakery and confectionery segment ranks among Hong Kong's largest, with chains producing breads, cakes, pastries, and seasonal items like mooncakes for retail sale and internal use.[22] This division emphasizes in-house production to maintain quality control and supply chain efficiency, serving both standalone shops and integrated cafe outlets. Catering services constitute another key area, providing banquet, event, and institutional meal solutions, including customized menus for corporate functions and weddings, leveraging the company's scale for large-scale operations.[11] Food manufacturing and distribution underpin these segments through dedicated facilities, such as the 100,000-square-foot Maxim's Food Factory established in Tai Po, Hong Kong, which processes ingredients and distributes products to retail branches and external clients.[1] This vertical integration, supported by extensive supply chain logistics, enables cost efficiencies and product consistency across outlets, with operations extending to frozen foods, sauces, and ready-to-eat items.[23] As of 2023, these segments collectively drive the company's regional dominance in the food and beverage sector, though detailed revenue breakdowns remain proprietary.[4]

Supply Chain and Operational Scale

Maxim's Caterers operates over 2,000 outlets across Hong Kong, mainland China, and Southeast Asia, encompassing restaurants, quick-service outlets, bakeries, and catering facilities under more than 70 brands.[23][4] This scale positions it as Hong Kong's largest food and beverage operator by outlet count, with operations extending to 24/7 production lines in Hong Kong and mainland China to support continuous demand.[24] The company's supply chain emphasizes centralized distribution and multi-temperature logistics, including cold chain solutions managed through partnerships such as with Kerry Logistics since 2017, which handles deliveries to hundreds of outlets from a dedicated central facility.[25] Digital tools like OpenText Business Network and Microsoft Dynamics 365 Supply Chain Management have been implemented to optimize procurement, forecasting, and inventory, yielding reported improvements such as a 5% waste reduction and enhanced production planning efficiency.[26][23] Upstream sourcing includes strategic investments in raw materials like lotus seeds through direct supplier partnerships to ensure quality and sustainability, while broader operations involve complex, high-volume channels for perishable goods across Asia.[27] These elements support daily service to large customer volumes, with historical data indicating capacity for around 480,000 patrons per day in earlier operational phases, scaled up through expansion.[28]

Brands and Portfolio

Proprietary Brands

Maxim's Caterers operates a diverse portfolio of proprietary brands spanning Chinese cuisine, bakeries, fast-casual outlets, and innovative dining concepts, developed internally since the company's founding in 1956. These brands emphasize Cantonese, Peking, and Chiu Chow specialties, alongside Western-influenced cafes and baked goods, tailored primarily to Hong Kong and mainland China markets. Key examples include Maxim's Palace, a flagship chain of upscale Cantonese restaurants established in the 1970s, known for dim sum and banquet services; Jade Garden, launched in 1971 as one of the group's earliest proprietary outlets offering premium seafood and traditional dishes; and Peking Garden, specializing in northern Chinese cuisine with roasted meats and noodles since its inception in the 1970s.[29][10] The group has expanded its proprietary offerings into specialized concepts like M&C Duck, focusing on roasted duck preparations introduced in the 2010s, and Duckee, a casual extension emphasizing affordable Peking duck variants. Chiu Chow Garden caters to Teochew-style seafood and stir-fries, while newer entrants such as wellwellwell (debuted in 2021) blend retro-futuristic aesthetics with modern Cantonese fusion. Bakeries under Maxim's Cakes and Arome Bakery produce mooncakes, pastries, and breads, with Maxim's Cakes holding a dominant position in Hong Kong's seasonal mooncake market, generating significant revenue during Mid-Autumn Festival.[29][4] Fast-casual proprietary brands include Maxim's MX, a chain of affordable Chinese fast-food outlets offering congee, noodles, and baked items, rebranded in the 2000s from earlier iterations to compete in quick-service segments. Additionally, m.a.x. concepts, initiated in 1998, encompasses in-house Western and fusion brands like MAX (steakhouse-style dining), Cellini (Italian-inspired), and Thai Basil (Southeast Asian flavors), designed for mid-to-upscale casual experiences. These proprietary lines, numbering over two dozen in Chinese cuisine alone, support the group's vertical integration through in-house supply chains for ingredients like ducks and bakery goods.[29][10][4] Proprietary cafes such as Simply Life provide grab-and-go options with salads, sandwiches, and coffee, originating as an internal brand in the 2000s to capture urban lifestyle demand. The portfolio's strength lies in localized adaptations, with brands like The Square offering contemporary interpretations of dim sum and Jasmine focusing on lighter, health-oriented Chinese fare. As of 2023, these self-developed brands constitute the core of Maxim's operations in Hong Kong and Macau, supplemented by expansion into mainland China, where outlets like Jade Garden have proliferated since the 1990s.[29][4]

Licensed and International Partnerships

Maxim's Group serves as the exclusive licensee for several prominent international quick-service and casual dining brands in select Asian markets, leveraging these agreements to complement its proprietary offerings and facilitate regional expansion. The most significant partnership is with Starbucks Coffee, initiated in 2000 when Maxim's began operating stores in Hong Kong.[30] This arrangement has since expanded to include Macau, Vietnam, Cambodia, Singapore, Thailand, and Laos, with Maxim's managing over 1,000 outlets across East Asia by September 2024, including the milestone 1,000th store opened in Hanoi, Vietnam.[31] [32] ![Shake Shack at IFC Mall Hong Kong](./assets/Shake_Shack_at_IFC_Mall_Hong_Kong_(20181005113904) Another key collaboration involves Shake Shack, with Maxim's securing licensing rights for operations in South China in prior years, followed by Thailand in 2022 and an expanded agreement for Vietnam announced in August 2025, targeting 15 locations by 2035 and an initial opening in 2026.[1] [33] As of 2025, Maxim's operates 52 Shake Shack outlets in China, including Hong Kong, underscoring the partnership's role in penetrating Southeast Asian markets.[34] Maxim's also holds licenses for Genki Sushi, Ippudo ramen, The Cheesecake Factory, COVA pastries, and Thai Basil, primarily in Hong Kong and extending to parts of mainland China and Southeast Asia.[4] These agreements, managed through joint ventures or direct licensing, enable Maxim's to introduce global concepts while adapting menus to local preferences, contributing to its operational scale of over 2,000 outlets region-wide as of 2024.[23] The partnerships emphasize supply chain integration and localized development, with Maxim's handling full operations from store development to daily management in licensed territories.[30]

Geographic Expansion

Presence in Hong Kong and Mainland China

Maxim's Caterers originated in Hong Kong, where it opened its first restaurant, Maxim's Restaurant, in Lane Crawford House, Central, on December 5, 1956.[1] The company has grown to operate over 70 brands in Hong Kong and Macau S.A.R., encompassing Chinese and Western restaurants, bakeries, quick-service outlets, and licensed chains like Starbucks, with 163 stores in Hong Kong as of September 2024.[35][36] Key quick-service brands include Maxim's MX, which maintains over 70 outlets across Hong Kong Island, Kowloon, and the New Territories.[28] Supporting operations feature multiple factories in Tai Po, such as the 250,000-square-foot food factory established in 2003, a 150,000-square-foot cakes and bakery facility opened in 2012, and a 100,000-square-foot food factory from 2014.[1] In Mainland China, Maxim's entered the market early through the establishment of Beijing Air Catering Company Ltd. in 1980, marking the first Sino-foreign joint venture in the catering sector.[1] Further expansion included the 2005 opening of the Mei-Xin Cakes bakery plant and flagship store in Guangzhou.[1] By 2020, the company had built a 1,000,000-square-foot food factory in Jiangmen to bolster production capacity.[1] Maxim's now deploys over 70 brands across Mainland China, including restaurants and licensed operations like Shake Shack, with plans for 55 locations by 2030.[35][37] These efforts contribute to the group's more than 2,000 outlets spanning Mainland China and Hong Kong.[4]

Southeast Asia and Other Markets

Maxim's Group has established operations in Southeast Asia, primarily through licensed international brands and select proprietary concepts, spanning Thailand, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, and Cambodia. As of 2024, the company manages over 70 brands across these markets, contributing to its regional network of more than 2,000 outlets that include restaurants, cafes, and bakeries.[35] This expansion builds on partnerships that leverage local demand for Western and fusion dining, with a focus on high-traffic urban areas.[38] A cornerstone of Maxim's Southeast Asian presence is its role as the exclusive licensee for Starbucks Coffee in the region. By September 2024, Maxim's had opened its 1,000th Starbucks outlet across seven territories, with Thailand hosting the largest share at 507 stores, followed by 143 in Singapore.[39] Vietnam and Cambodia also feature Starbucks locations managed by Maxim's, supporting steady growth in coffee and casual dining segments.[30] Additional licensed expansions include agreements for Shake Shack, with plans for 15 outlets in Vietnam by 2035 starting with the first in 2026, and a similar target of 15 in Thailand by 2032.[33] In Thailand, Maxim's broadened its portfolio in 2022 by securing rights to develop The Cheesecake Factory, targeting premium casual dining.[40] Beyond Southeast Asia, Maxim's operational footprint remains limited, with no full-scale restaurant or cafe networks established in markets like Australia, Europe, or North America. However, its MX Mooncakes brand achieves global distribution, available in over 80 countries including Australia, Canada, and Germany through export and seasonal sales channels rather than permanent outlets.[35] This product-focused outreach contrasts with the service-oriented expansions in Southeast Asia, where Maxim's prioritizes scalable franchise models amid regional economic growth.[35]

Controversies

Shark Finning and Environmental Campaigns

In 2018, Maxim's Caterers Limited encountered intense scrutiny from environmental advocacy groups for persisting in the sale of shark fin dishes, which are derived from the practice of shark finningâ€”wherein sharks are finned at sea and their bodies discarded, exacerbating population declines in over 100 shark species listed as vulnerable or endangered by the IUCN.[41] Despite Maxim's 2017 assertion of being the first major Chinese restaurant chain to phase out shark fin, investigations revealed ongoing offerings on discreet "premium" Ã  la carte menus at multiple outlets, including superior shark fin soup with chicken, ham, and vegetables, often sourced from blue sharks marketed by the company as "sustainably sourced" but criticized by experts for contributing to overexploitation of a near-threatened species.[42][43] Campaigns intensified in early 2018, with organizations like WildAid, Sea Shepherd, and the Hong Kong Shark Foundation documenting secret menus and launching petitions targeting Maxim's CEO Michael Wu, demanding the removal of shark fin from all menus to curb demand-driven finning.[44][45] On June 15, 2018, dozens of activists protested outside Maxim's Lai Chi Kok headquarters, highlighting the company's role in serving shark fin to an estimated 700,000 daily customers across Hong Kong outlets and linking it to broader marine ecosystem disruption.[46][47] Advocacy extended to Maxim's licensing of Starbucks stores in Hong Kong, with calls for the U.S. chain to terminate the partnership due to its indirect support of the fin trade, though Starbucks maintained the arrangement pending Maxim's compliance.[48] Facing petitions garnering hundreds of signatures and public pressure, Maxim's announced on September 20, 2018, a full ban on shark fin sales across all its restaurants effective January 1, 2020, citing alignment with global conservation trends.[49][50] WWF-Hong Kong praised the pledge as advancing Hong Kong toward reduced shark fin consumption, though activists noted it deferred immediate action amid ongoing finning's annual toll of up to 73 million sharks globally.[51] No verified reports of non-compliance have surfaced since the 2020 deadline, aligning with similar bans by other Hong Kong firms like Cathay Pacific.[41]

2019â€“2020 Political Boycotts and Vandalism

During the 2019â€“2020 Hong Kong protests, Maxim's Caterers outlets became targets for boycotts and vandalism by pro-democracy demonstrators after Annie Wu, daughter of co-founder Woodrow Wu, publicly criticized the protests at the United Nations on September 11, 2019, referring to participants as "rioters" and expressing support for the Hong Kong government's handling of the unrest.[52][53] Her remarks, made during a side event on religious freedom, aligned the company in protesters' eyes with pro-establishment ("blue") positions, prompting calls to shun Maxim's brands as part of the emerging "yellow economic circle" strategy to support pro-democracy ("yellow") businesses.[52][54] Boycotts intensified in late September 2019, with students at institutions like the Chinese University of Hong Kong urging peers to avoid Maxim's catering services and outlets, citing Wu's statements as justification for economic pressure against perceived government backers.[53] This extended to Maxim's operated franchises such as Starbucks, Yoshinoya, and Genki Sushi, which saw widespread graffiti, including anti-police slogans and boycott messages, as well as smashed windows in districts like Wan Chai and Central.[55][52] Vandalism peaked during escalated protest actions in October 2019, with multiple Maxim's MX fast-food locations, cake shops, and sushi outlets defaced or damaged alongside other perceived pro-Beijing targets like Bank of China branches.[56][57] The actions reflected protesters' tactic of selective economic disruption against businesses viewed as opposing the movement for democratic reforms and opposition to the extradition bill, though Maxim's maintained it operated independently of political affiliations.[54][52] Boycotts persisted into 2020 amid ongoing unrest, contributing to revenue pressures on the company, but specific financial impacts from this period were not publicly quantified in detail by Maxim's.[58]

Animal Welfare Allegations (2021â€“2022)

In 2021 and 2022, an animal rights campaign targeted Maxim's Group, alleging that the company's supply chain across Asia involved practices amounting to animal cruelty, particularly in the sourcing of poultry, pork, and other livestock for its restaurant operations. Activists contended that suppliers employed intensive confinement systems, routine mutilations without anesthesia, and slaughter methods that inflicted unnecessary suffering, reflecting systemic deficiencies in regional animal agriculture standards. The initiative, promoted via dedicated online efforts and social media, sought to mobilize consumer boycotts of Maxim's brands, including MX and Maxim's Cakes, to compel reforms toward cage-free or welfare-certified sourcing. These claims echoed wider critiques of factory farming in China and Southeast Asia but lacked corroboration from governmental audits or peer-reviewed studies specific to Maxim's suppliers during the period. Maxim's Group emphasized its adherence to local food safety regulations and sustainability goals, such as waste reduction, without issuing a direct rebuttal to the welfare-focused accusations.[59]

Recent Developments

Post-2020 Recovery and Expansion

Maxim's Caterers experienced significant disruptions from COVID-19 restrictions in Hong Kong and Mainland China, prompting a shift toward digital enhancements including mobile ordering and expanded takeaway services to sustain operations.[60] These adaptations supported recovery as pandemic measures eased, with the company leveraging its diversified portfolio of restaurants, bakeries, and licensed brands to regain revenue streams. Financially, the group achieved a turnaround, as reflected in DFI Retail Group's share of Maxim's underlying profits: US$38 million in 2022, reversing a US$26 million loss from the prior year impacted by lockdowns.[61] Profits grew to US$79 million in 2023 before moderating to US$66 million in 2024, attributable in part to softer mooncake sales amid economic pressures.[62] This recovery aligned with broader Hong Kong catering sector rebound, enabling Maxim's to open its 2,000th outlet in the city by December 2023.[63] Expansion efforts post-2020 focused on international partnerships and new markets. In June 2021, Maxim's committed to developing up to 79 Shake Shack locations in Mainland China by 2031, including 10 additional stores announced that year.[64] Further growth included extending agreements for brands like Genki Sushi and planning entry into Vietnam via a Shake Shack opening targeted for 2026.[65] By September 2025, Maxim's network exceeded 2,000 outlets across Hong Kong, Mainland China, and Southeast Asia, supported by optimized supply chain integrations.[26]

Sustainability Initiatives

Maxim's Group, encompassing Maxim's Caterers, operates under the "Savouring Our Future" sustainability framework, emphasizing waste reduction, carbon mitigation, and circular economy practices across its food and beverage operations.[66] The company prioritizes source-level interventions, such as converting pre-consumer food waste into resources for agriculture and energy-efficient technologies, while integrating these into supply chain management to achieve measurable reductions in waste and emissions.[59] A flagship program is the WeGen Farming initiative, launched in 2022 in partnership with local farms like Hung Yat Farm. The produce is then incorporated into low-carbon menu items, fostering a closed-loop system that minimizes landfill contributions and supports local sourcing in Hong Kong's urban context.[67][68] By 2025, the initiative had expanded to collaborations like the "WeGen-X" low-carbon menu contest with Towngas, promoting sustainable dining options.[69] In waste management, Maxim's introduced a "No Straw Everyday" policy on October 2, 2018, for its MX brand restaurants, eliminating plastic straws from cold drinks unless requested to curb single-use plastics. Complementing this, the group repurposed surplus bread and coffee grounds into upcycled products, such as BOB craft beer launched in collaboration with 7-Eleven in October 2024, which by October 2025 had avoided 43 tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions through diversion from waste streams. Supply chain optimizations, including Microsoft Dynamics 365 implementation, yielded a 5% reduction in overall food waste by 2024.[70][71][23] Carbon reduction efforts include the 2022 adoption of iPaintâ€”a reflective cooling coatingâ€”applied to 120 square meters of headquarters rooftops and pipes, lowering interior temperatures by 5-6Â°C and curbing energy use in air-conditioning.
