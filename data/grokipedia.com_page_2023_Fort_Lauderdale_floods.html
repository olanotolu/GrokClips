2023 Fort Lauderdale floods

The 2023 Fort Lauderdale floods were an extreme flash flooding episode that inundated Fort Lauderdale and surrounding areas in Broward County, Florida, on April 12–13, 2023, driven by a stalled weather system depositing record-breaking rainfall of 25.91 inches (658 mm) in 24 hours at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport.[1][2] This precipitation total, the highest for any 24-hour period in Florida state history, overwhelmed canals, stormwater systems, and pump stations designed for far lesser intensities, resulting in water depths exceeding several feet across urban streets, neighborhoods, and infrastructure.[3][4] The National Weather Service designated the event a 1-in-1,000-year rainfall occurrence based on statistical return periods derived from historical data, though such classifications reflect probabilistic extremes rather than absolute rarity.[1][3] Meteorologically, the deluge stemmed from a slow-moving frontal boundary interacting with abundant tropical moisture, fostering repeated convective bands that persisted over the same location for over 12 hours, with radar estimates indicating rates up to 1.5 inches per hour in peaks.[5][6] Impacts included the shutdown of Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport for over 24 hours, closure of major highways like Interstate 95, evacuation of hundreds from low-lying areas, and disruptions to power and sewage systems, though no deaths were recorded amid the chaos.[7][8] Property damages reached millions, exposing limitations in the region's coastal drainage network, where high groundwater tables and tidal backflow from the Atlantic Ocean compounded runoff challenges during the high-intensity downpour.[9][10] Post-event assessments highlighted causal factors beyond sheer volume, including antecedent wet conditions from prior rains and insufficient pump capacity relative to the rainfall's short-duration peaks, which exceeded engineered thresholds by factors of 5–10 times in localized zones.[1][5] While some analyses invoked broader atmospheric trends, empirical records underscore the primacy of the event's hyper-local stagnation and moisture convergence in producing the flood's scale, independent of long-term attributions requiring rigorous causal modeling.[3] The incident spurred reviews of urban resiliency measures, including enhanced modeling for compound risks in South Florida's low-elevation, impervious-surface-dominated landscape.[11]

Meteorological background

Synoptic conditions

The synoptic pattern leading to the extreme rainfall in Fort Lauderdale on April 12–13, 2023, featured a slow-moving warm frontal boundary initially positioned along the Florida Straits near the Keys, which began lifting northward across South Florida starting late on April 11. This front provided persistent low-level convergence and upward motion, stalling convection over the region and allowing repeated development of thunderstorms. Accompanying the front was a broader upper-level trough over the eastern United States, with weak shortwave disturbances enhancing divergence aloft and supporting lift, though the primary forcing remained surface-based due to the front's quasi-stationary nature.[12][13] A low-pressure system developing in the Gulf of Mexico contributed to the overall setup by drawing moist air northward, while east-southeast winds in the lower troposphere advected high precipitable water values—exceeding 2.2 inches (56 mm)—from the warm Atlantic waters into the frontal zone. Mid-level southerly flow and upper-level westerly winds provided vertical wind shear conducive to organized storm structures, though the synoptic-scale moisture pooling and frontal lift were the dominant factors in concentrating rainfall over Broward County. National Weather Service analyses attributed the event's intensity to this combination, noting the front's slow progression prevented rapid clearing of saturated air masses.[13][12] The pattern resembled a classic "frontal rain" scenario in subtropical latitudes, where stalled boundaries interact with tropical moisture plumes, but lacked strong baroclinic forcing typical of mid-latitude systems, resulting instead in prolonged, training convective bands rather than widespread severe weather. This setup was forecasted with some lead time by operational models, though the exact rainfall totals exceeded predictions due to mesoscale enhancements not fully resolved at synoptic scales.[12]

Rainfall event

The extreme rainfall event unfolded primarily on April 12, 2023, with intense precipitation beginning in the afternoon and continuing through the evening hours, extending into early April 13.[14] Stationary thunderstorms repeatedly developed and trained over the Fort Lauderdale area, facilitated by a slow-moving frontal boundary and an associated low-pressure system in the Gulf of Mexico that supplied abundant moisture.[13] This setup allowed convective cells to persist in place, dumping torrential rain rates exceeding 2-3 inches per hour in localized areas.[15] A non-official rain gauge at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport measured 25.91 inches (658 mm) of precipitation over a 24-hour period from 7:00 a.m. on April 12 to 7:00 a.m. on April 13, marking a potential Florida state record for 24-hour rainfall.[14] Nearby Dania Beach recorded 21.42 inches (544 mm), while broader South Florida regions saw accumulations of 15-20 inches or more.[14] The National Weather Service classified the event as a 1-in-1,000-year occurrence based on statistical return periods for such extreme precipitation volumes.[3] Atmospheric conditions included high precipitable water values exceeding 2 inches, indicative of tropical-like moisture plumes, combined with sufficient instability and weak wind shear that permitted the storms to stall rather than propagate.[13] No tropical cyclone influence was present, distinguishing the event as a mesoscale convective system driven by synoptic-scale features.[15]

Event progression

Initial flooding

The intense rainfall event that initiated widespread flooding in Fort Lauderdale began in the afternoon of April 12, 2023, as a stalled warm frontal boundary and upper-level trough triggered repeated heavy showers and thunderstorms over Broward County.[16] [17] Precipitation rates reached 2 to 3 inches per hour in localized areas, rapidly overwhelming drainage systems and causing the first instances of flash flooding on roadways and in urban lowlands by late afternoon.[18] The National Weather Service in Miami issued initial flash flood warnings around 6:00 p.m. EDT, escalating to a rare flash flood emergency at 7:58 p.m. EDT for Fort Lauderdale, Hollywood, and surrounding areas, citing potential for 2 to 4 feet of water in streets and catastrophic impacts.[19] Early flooding primarily affected major thoroughfares such as Broward Boulevard and intersections near the Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport, where standing water depths of 1 to 2 feet stranded vehicles and prompted initial rescue calls.[20] [18] By 8:00 p.m., reports from local authorities indicated over 6 inches of rain had fallen in the preceding hours at the airport gauge, marking the onset of what would become a record-breaking deluge, with urban streams and canals beginning to back up into adjacent neighborhoods.[10] This phase saw minimal advance preparation due to forecasts underpredicting the event's intensity, leading to sudden inundation without widespread evacuation.[21]

Peak impacts

The peak of the flooding event occurred on April 13, 2023, when Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport recorded 25.91 inches (658 mm) of rainfall in under 24 hours, marking a once-in-1,000-year occurrence according to the National Weather Service.[3] This deluge, concentrated within approximately 12 hours from the evening of April 12, overwhelmed drainage systems, leading to rapid flash flooding across Broward County.[22] Rainfall rates exceeded 2 inches per hour in multiple locations, exacerbating inundation in low-lying urban areas unaccustomed to such intensity outside of tropical cyclone seasons.[5] At its height, floodwaters submerged major roadways, including Interstate 95 and U.S. Highway 1, rendering them impassable and stranding hundreds of vehicles.[18] The airport's runways and terminals filled with several feet of water and debris, halting all flight operations and grounding over 1,000 flights, with the facility remaining closed until April 14.[23] Residential neighborhoods experienced water levels up to several feet deep, prompting the evacuation of approximately 600 residents to emergency shelters amid reports of homes and businesses being swamped.[24] No fatalities were reported, but the event caused widespread power outages affecting thousands and significant disruptions to public transit and emergency services, with rescue teams conducting hundreds of high-water operations during the overnight peak.[22] The convergence of stalled weather systems and urban infrastructure limitations amplified the impacts, highlighting vulnerabilities in stormwater management despite prior investments.[10]

Immediate effects

Airport operations

The Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport suspended all flight operations on the afternoon of April 12, 2023, as floodwaters inundated runways, taxiways, and access roads amid torrential rainfall exceeding 25 inches in the vicinity.[25][13] The closure, prompted by water accumulation and debris hazards, extended through April 13, halting arrivals and departures for approximately 40 hours and stranding aircraft and thousands of passengers.[26][27] Over 1,100 flights were canceled or significantly delayed as a direct result of the shutdown, with airlines rerouting passengers via nearby Miami International Airport where feasible.[28] Airport personnel coordinated with federal agencies, including the Federal Aviation Administration, to clear standing water and assess structural integrity, though operations remained grounded until safety protocols were met.[29] Tornado warnings in the area compounded the suspension, exacerbating risks to ground crews and infrastructure.[25] The facility reopened at 9:00 a.m. on April 14, 2023, with initial flights resuming amid residual delays from backlog and weather-related disruptions.[29][26] Post-reopening assessments revealed no major structural damage to terminals or runways, though the event highlighted vulnerabilities in drainage systems overwhelmed by the unprecedented rainfall rate of over two feet in under 24 hours.[30][31]

Urban inundation

The 2023 Fort Lauderdale floods caused widespread urban inundation, with downtown areas and low-lying neighborhoods experiencing rapid onset of deep standing water. On April 12, rainfall exceeding 25 inches within 12 hours overwhelmed storm drains, turning major streets into impassable lakes and stranding hundreds of vehicles.[10] [21] Flood depths near downtown reached 1-2 feet, prompting rush-hour evacuations on foot as water levels rose to ankle depth even in elevated structures like the city hall parking garage.[10] Aging infrastructure exacerbated the flooding in densely built urban zones. Gravity-dependent drainage systems, reliant on canals for outflow, failed due to high antecedent water levels in canals and groundwater, compounded by tidal backflow prevention measures that restricted discharge during peak rainfall.[9] Known vulnerabilities in neighborhoods like Edgewood and Melrose Manors—identified over a decade prior as prone to inundation from routine storms—resulted in severe residential impacts, including water intrusion into over 1,100 homes, with 766 in Edgewood suffering depths greater than 18 inches.[9] Prolonged drainage delays left streets and underpasses submerged for days, hindering access to commercial districts and generating over 600 emergency calls for high-water rescues after 6:00 p.m. on April 12. Inland urban areas, farther from tidal outlets, fared worst as localized rainfall maxima exceeded system design capacities, highlighting limitations in pump stations and sewer maintenance amid blocked mains.[10] [9]

Human and economic toll

The 2023 Fort Lauderdale floods resulted in no confirmed fatalities or serious injuries, though emergency responders conducted numerous rescues amid widespread inundation. Firefighters addressed calls for help throughout the event and into the following days, assisting residents trapped in vehicles and homes, particularly in low-lying areas like the Edgewood neighborhood where waters reached several feet deep.[32][31] Thousands of people were directly impacted, including airport passengers stranded by the closure of Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport from April 12 to April 13, which halted all flights and affected over 1,000 departures and arrivals.[33][34] Local residents faced temporary displacement, with some resorting to canoes or wading through knee-deep to chest-high water to access essentials, exacerbating vulnerabilities in flood-prone zones known for recurrent issues even in moderate rains.[35][33] Economic losses from the floods totaled an estimated $1.4 billion, as assessed by the National Centers for Environmental Information, encompassing damage to infrastructure, vehicles, homes, and businesses across Broward County.[36] Property damage was extensive, with hundreds of abandoned cars scattered in streets, commercial districts like Las Olas Boulevard submerged, and public facilities such as the central library and police headquarters incapacitated by floodwaters up to 4 feet deep.[35][32] The event prompted a federal disaster declaration on April 28, 2023, unlocking FEMA aid for preliminary damage assessments that identified affected households and public assets, though most standard homeowners' policies excluded flood coverage, shifting recovery burdens to federal programs and individual claims.[37][38] Business interruptions compounded costs, with tourism and aviation sectors hit hard by the airport shutdown and road closures persisting for days.[16]

Emergency response

Rescue operations

Fort Lauderdale Fire Rescue, in coordination with Broward County Fire Rescue and the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission (FWC), conducted extensive rescue operations starting late on April 12, 2023, as floodwaters rapidly inundated neighborhoods and roadways. Crews responded to calls from stranded residents and motorists trapped in vehicles, homes, and underpasses, utilizing high-water vehicles, swamp buggies, all-terrain utility vehicles (UTVs), and small boats to navigate chest-deep waters in areas like Riverland and Lauderdale Lakes.[39][29][9] On the night of April 12 into early April 13, responders rescued approximately 600 individuals from flooded homes and vehicles across Broward County, with operations continuing through April 13 and 14 as waters receded slowly.[39][40] The Broward Sheriff's Office supported these efforts by handling around 300 rescue calls, prioritizing high-risk areas where residents required evacuation by canoe or airboat due to impassable streets.[18] Overall, city teams rescued or relocated about 900 people over the initial response period, with FWC providing additional assets including four swamp buggies, two high-lift UTVs, and 15 four-wheel-drive trucks to assist local fire departments.[40][41][29] No fatalities were reported during the rescues, though operations faced challenges from ongoing rainfall and debris-clogged drainage systems that prolonged inundation.[42] Responders also aided in relocating displaced residents to shelters, such as those opened by the American Red Cross, where hundreds sought temporary refuge amid power outages and contaminated floodwaters.[39][43]

Government actions

On April 13, 2023, Broward County declared a local state of emergency effective from 12:01 a.m. that day, lasting seven days, in response to the severe flooding from overnight rainfall exceeding 20 inches in parts of the county. The City of Fort Lauderdale and the City of Hollywood also issued their own emergency declarations on the same date to facilitate coordinated response efforts amid widespread inundation.[44] Florida Governor Ron DeSantis issued Executive Order 23-65 on April 13, 2023—less than 24 hours after the onset of major flooding—declaring a state of emergency specifically for Broward County to address impacts on critical infrastructure and enable resource mobilization.[45] This order activated the Florida State Emergency Response Team (SERT), which coordinated fuel deliveries to affected areas following disruptions at Port Everglades, and deployed assets from the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission, including three swamp buggies, two high-lift utility task vehicles, and 15 four-wheel-drive trucks for rescue and access support.[46][47] Additionally, the state activated the Florida Small Business Emergency Bridge Loan Program, allocating $5 million in low-interest loans to businesses impacted by the floods.[48] On April 22, 2023, Governor DeSantis formally requested a Major Disaster Declaration from the federal government for Broward County, citing catastrophic damages including flooded roadways, power outages, and disruptions to airport and port operations.[49] President Joe Biden approved the declaration on April 27, 2023, under FEMA Disaster Declaration FEMA-4709-DR, enabling federal Individual Assistance and Public Assistance programs for eligible Broward County residents and governments to cover housing, repairs, and recovery costs.[50] FEMA opened applications for aid via DisasterAssistance.gov, with preliminary damage assessments conducted jointly by state and federal teams starting April 17, 2023.[51][52]

Aftermath

Infrastructure evaluations

Post-flood assessments by city officials revealed that Fort Lauderdale's stormwater infrastructure, primarily reliant on aging drainage pipes and gravity-fed systems spanning nearly 500 miles of sewer mains, was overwhelmed by the unprecedented rainfall volume of over 26 inches in approximately 12 hours on April 12, 2023.[9] The systems, designed to manage roughly 3 inches of rain per day under optimal conditions, could not handle the deluge, leading to widespread ponding and backups exacerbated by elevated canal water levels that impeded outflow in low-lying areas such as Edgewood.[53] After-action reports documented 1,121 residential properties damaged, with 766 sustaining major inundation exceeding 18 inches, underscoring vulnerabilities in catch basins, pumps, and tidal-influenced drainage networks.[9] Engineering evaluations highlighted chronic underinvestment, with progress on resiliency upgrades lagging despite awareness of risks identified over a decade earlier, including insufficient capacity for intensified precipitation patterns.[9] High groundwater and tidal influences compounded the issue, as gravity-dependent systems failed to evacuate water efficiently during the event, necessitating external pumping assistance.[9] City leaders acknowledged that the infrastructure reflected 10 to 20 years of deferred maintenance and expansion, particularly in urban zones with increased impervious surfaces from development.[54] In response, evaluations informed the acceleration of the Fortify Fort Lauderdale initiative, a comprehensive $1.6 billion, 10-year stormwater overhaul targeting 25 flood-prone areas with new pump stations, thousands of feet of underground pipes, tidal control valves, and enhanced catch basins to boost discharge capacity and mitigate future overloads.[54] Initial phases prioritize neighborhoods like Edgewood and Riverwalk, incorporating lessons from the flood to integrate projections for higher rainfall intensities and sea level rise into design standards.[9] Funding pursuits include federal grants and potential utility rate adjustments, with procurement of equipment such as Vactor trucks and portable pumps to address immediate gaps exposed by the event.[9]

Recovery funding and measures

Following President Biden's major disaster declaration for Broward County on April 28, 2023, the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) enabled individual assistance programs, allowing affected residents to apply for grants covering temporary housing, home repairs, and other recovery needs through DisasterAssistance.gov or by calling 1-800-621-3362.[55] [51] FEMA also provided public assistance for debris removal, emergency protective measures, and infrastructure repairs, including $1.5 million approved in September 2025 for the North Broward Hospital District to reimburse emergency actions taken during the event.[56] In December 2024, under the Disaster Relief Supplemental Appropriations Act, 2025, Fort Lauderdale received an $88 million allocation from the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development's Community Development Block Grant Disaster Recovery (CDBG-DR) program specifically for the April 2023 floods.[57] This funding supports home repairs and reconstructions for uninsured or underinsured properties, reimbursements for out-of-pocket resident expenses, infrastructure restoration such as drainage systems, and resilience enhancements to mitigate future flooding.[58] [59] The city's September 2025 action plan prioritizes low- to moderate-income households and flood-vulnerable areas, with public input gathered via surveys to guide allocations.[60] At the county level, Broward County activated the Broward Cares Disaster Recovery Fund in June 2024, distributing emergency grants to over 40 nonprofits for essentials like housing, food, and furniture to flood victims, supplementing federal aid.[61] Statewide, Florida's Rebuild Florida Infrastructure Repair Program allocated $400 million from CDBG-DR funds for 2023-2024 storms, including projects in Broward to repair roads, bridges, and utilities damaged by the inundation.[62] Additionally, FEMA's Flood Mitigation Assistance Program, administered through the Florida Division of Emergency Management, funds long-term measures such as home elevations and buyouts in high-risk zones to reduce future exposure.[63] These efforts form part of broader federal commitments totaling over $925 million in CDBG-DR for Florida's 2023-2024 disasters, emphasizing reconstruction over prevention in initial outlays.[64]

Supply disruptions

The flooding at Port Everglades, a major port handling approximately 40% of Florida's gasoline distribution to 12 counties, halted fuel loading operations and prevented tanker trucks from accessing terminals on April 12-13, 2023, leading to widespread gasoline shortages across South Florida.[65][66] This disruption stemmed from inundated infrastructure, including submerged roads and petroleum facilities, which blocked outbound shipments despite sufficient on-site fuel stocks.[67] By April 17, 2023, 59% of gas stations in the Miami-Fort Lauderdale area reported no fuel available, with an additional 21% having limited supplies, exacerbating the crisis through panic buying and long queues.[68] The shortages extended to Palm Beach County and beyond, with drivers facing hours-long waits and some stations closing entirely due to depleted inventories, though diesel and jet fuel supplies remained largely unaffected as they were less reliant on the flooded terminals.[69][70] Federal and state interventions, including EPA waivers for fuel blending and expedited shipments from alternative ports, helped restore access; by April 21, most outages were resolved, though retail prices spiked to a 2023 high of around $3.70 per gallon statewide.[70][71] Beyond fuel, broader supply chain effects were limited, with no widespread reports of food or potable water shortages at grocery stores, though road closures in downtown Fort Lauderdale delayed general deliveries until at least April 14.[29] Emergency comfort stations provided food, water, and charging to displaced residents, mitigating immediate gaps for vulnerable populations.[72] Power disruptions were minimal, with no large-scale outages documented, allowing utilities to maintain service amid the inundation.[73]

Analyses and controversies

Preparedness and systemic failures

The National Weather Service forecast preceding the April 12, 2023, event anticipated only a few inches of rain, significantly underestimating the actual deluge of approximately 25 inches that fell over 12 hours in Fort Lauderdale.[10] This forecasting shortfall contributed to limited preemptive measures, as the storm lacked the advance notice associated with named tropical systems, catching emergency services unprepared for the scale of inundation.[10] Flash flood warnings were issued during the event, but by the time of the flash flood emergency declaration at 7:58 p.m. EDT, substantial rainfall had already accumulated, overwhelming initial response capacities.[18] Fort Lauderdale's drainage infrastructure, designed to handle about 3 inches of rain per day in peak condition, proved inadequate against the extreme volume, with gravity-fed systems impeded by elevated canal levels from concurrent high tides.[74] [9] After-action reviews revealed that city officials had identified flood-prone low-lying neighborhoods, such as Edgewood, over a decade prior through vulnerability studies, yet progress on drainage upgrades remained slow, with development pressures outpacing infrastructure enhancements.[9] Ongoing construction projects exacerbated vulnerabilities, as critical drainage components were removed without adequate temporary replacements, leading to prolonged flooding where waters lingered for days post-storm due to insufficient outflow capacity.[9] [75] Emergency responders faced systemic gaps, including the absence of a dedicated flood-response plan and insufficient specialized equipment like small boats, resulting in over 900 calls handled amid initial unawareness of the disaster's full scope.[9] These failures highlighted broader issues in maintenance and capacity planning for rare, high-intensity events, where urban impervious surfaces amplified runoff beyond historical design standards, underscoring the need for resilient upgrades despite the event's exceptional nature.[9] [10]

Causal attributions

The 2023 Fort Lauderdale floods resulted primarily from an extraordinary meteorological event characterized by stationary thunderstorms that persisted over South Florida on April 12, 2023. These storms, embedded within a slow-moving band of heavy precipitation, delivered rainfall rates of 3 to 5 inches per hour, accumulating up to 25 inches in approximately 12 hours at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport.[1][17] The lack of significant wind shear and steering currents, combined with a stalled frontal boundary across the Florida Peninsula, allowed individual thunderstorms to remain nearly stationary, repeatedly dumping intense precipitation over the same locations.[15][5] A contributing upper-level low-pressure disturbance and counterclockwise winds from a system in the Gulf of Mexico enhanced moisture convergence, fueling the supercell-like structures within the rain band.[76] Local geography and urban development amplified the flooding's severity, as South Florida's flat terrain and extensive impervious surfaces—such as pavement and buildings—reduced natural infiltration and accelerated surface runoff. The region's stormwater infrastructure, designed for historical rainfall norms rather than events exceeding 20 inches in half a day, was rapidly overwhelmed, leading to widespread flash flooding in low-lying areas.[9][77] Pre-existing high groundwater levels from prior wet-season rains and minor influences from elevated sea levels further limited drainage capacity into canals and waterways.[78] Attributions to anthropogenic climate change remain speculative for this specific event, with no peer-reviewed rapid attribution study conducted. Some modeling by Broward County consultants projected approximately 13% greater rainfall intensity due to warming-induced atmospheric moisture increases, but such estimates rely on probabilistic frameworks rather than deterministic causation.[79] Mainstream analyses often link the event's rarity—a one-in-1,000-year rainfall occurrence—to broader trends in extreme precipitation, yet historical records show similar stalled storm setups in the subtropics without invoking long-term shifts.[80][81] Event-specific causality traces most directly to synoptic-scale weather patterns rather than global forcings, underscoring the role of natural variability in subtropical convection.[15]

Legal proceedings

In April 2025, residents of the River Oaks neighborhood filed a negligence lawsuit in Broward Circuit Court against Ric-Man Construction and engineering firms Hazen and Sawyer and Craven, Thompson & Associates, alleging that ongoing stormwater improvement work exacerbated flooding damages from the April 12-13, 2023, deluge.[75][82] The suit, seeking class-action certification and unspecified millions in damages for property losses including ruined appliances, plumbing, electrical systems, and structural harm, claims contractors removed existing drainage infrastructure starting in February 2023 without installing temporary protective measures, despite contractual obligations to mitigate flood risks during construction.[75][83] A parallel class-action lawsuit was initiated by Edgewood neighborhood residents against Hazen and Sawyer and contractor David Mancini & Sons, asserting similar negligence in a stormwater project that began in January 2022.[75][83] Plaintiffs argue that the defendants' failure to implement interim drainage systems foreseeably worsened inundation from the 25+ inches of rain, violating standards of care for construction in flood-prone areas.[75][82] The City of Fort Lauderdale, which contracted the firms for drainage upgrades, has not been named as a defendant in either case.[83][75] As of late 2025, both actions remain pending without reported settlements or rulings, with community town halls convened in April 2025 to inform affected residents.[83] No broader litigation against municipal entities or insurers has emerged directly tied to systemic flood management failures in these proceedings.[75][82]
