Howl is the third studio album by American rock band Black Rebel Motorcycle Club. It was released on August 22, 2005 in the UK, August 23 in the US, and September 21 in Japan. The record was released in the UK and Europe by The Echo Label and by RCA in the US, Australia, Japan, and the rest of the world. The title of the record is a direct reference to Allen Ginsberg's poem "Howl.[1] In 2025, the band released a deluxe four-LP vinyl edition to mark the album's 20th anniversary, accompanied by a North American and European tour.[2]

Many critics noted that Howl took a different direction from earlier BRMC releases.[3] Critics were generally polarised with the album.[14] While most agreed that their 2001 debut was the better of the first two records, and the follow-up had been rushed and lacklustre, opinion of the third offering was not so unified.[14] Many critics saw the album as an innovative departure from the band's homeground, while others found the record dull. In an August 2018 issue of NME dedicated to great "lost" or "cult" albums, Howl was selected by Guy Garvey of the band Elbow as his favorite "lost" album.[15] In a 2025 retrospective marking the album's 20th anniversary, Spin described Howl as a "polarizing masterpiece" rooted in acoustic gospel, blues, and folk that has more than proven its staying power, despite nearly derailing the band's career at the time.[16]

Singles

Black Rebel Motorcycle Club had claimed that they signed to [The Echo Label](The Echo Label "The Echo Label") in order to release more singles from their albums, having only been allowed to release two from their previous album, Take Them On, On Your Own. Despite this, their first single from Howl, album opener "Shuffle Your Feet", was a download-only single, therefore (at the time) not eligible to chart. Following this, they released "Ain't No Easy Way", the most recognizable and popular song from the record. A third single, "Weight of the World", was released on October 24, 2005, in limited physical formats including CD and numbered picture disc vinyl, but did not chart.[17]
