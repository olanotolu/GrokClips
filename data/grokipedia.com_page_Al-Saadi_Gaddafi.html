Al-Saadi Gaddafi

Al-Saadi Muammar Gaddafi (born 28 May 1973) is a Libyan former professional footballer and military officer, the third son of the late dictator Muammar Gaddafi, whose tenure as Libya's leader from 1969 to 2011 enabled his son's privileged positions in sports administration and security forces.[1][2] Gaddafi captained the Libya national football team and played for domestic clubs such as Al Ahly Tripoli and Al-Ittihad Tripoli, while briefly pursuing a career in Italy's Serie A with Perugia in 2003, though his on-field contributions were minimal and widely attributed to familial influence rather than merit.[1][3][4] As head of the Libyan Football Federation, he was accused of match-fixing, bribing officials, and coercing players to undermine rival teams like Al-Ahly Benghazi after fans insulted him, actions that exemplified the regime's use of sports for control and intimidation.[5][3] In his role as commander of an elite special forces unit, Gaddafi faced charges of ordering the 2005 murder of national team coach Bashir al-Rayani following a dispute, as well as directing crackdowns on unarmed protesters during the 2011 uprising that ousted his father.[6][2] After fleeing to Niger post-revolution, he was arrested in 2014, extradited to Libya for trial, imprisoned until his 2021 release on unclear grounds, and now resides in Turkey without political involvement, though a supreme court ordered his retrial on the murder charge in 2024.[2][7][8]

Early Life and Family

Birth and Upbringing in Libya

Al-Saadi Muammar Gaddafi was born on 25 May 1973 in Tripoli, Libya, as the third son of Muammar Gaddafi, the Libyan leader who seized power in a 1969 coup, and his second wife, Safia Farkash.[9][10] His birth occurred amid the early consolidation of his father's authoritarian regime, which nationalized oil resources and pursued a mix of Arab nationalism and socialist policies, providing the family with access to substantial state wealth.[11] Gaddafi's upbringing in Libya unfolded within the insulated privileges of the ruling family, residing primarily in Tripoli and other regime strongholds, under constant security due to the volatile political environment shaped by his father's rule. He received education that qualified him as an engineer and attained a military rank, indicative of early integration into the state's institutional frameworks, though details of specific institutions remain sparse in public records.[11] From a young age, he developed a passion for football, playing extensively for prominent Tripoli clubs such as Al Ahly and Al-Ittihad, accumulating nearly 100 matches before pursuing opportunities abroad, a pursuit facilitated by familial influence in a country where sports were leveraged for regime propaganda.[12] This early immersion in athletics contrasted with the broader Gaddafi family's involvement in military, diplomatic, and business spheres, positioning Al-Saadi as the member most oriented toward sports amid Libya's oil-funded excesses and repressive governance.[13]

Relations with the Gaddafi Family and Regime

Al-Saadi Gaddafi, born on May 27, 1973, as the third biological son of Muammar Gaddafi and his second wife Safia Farkash, occupied a privileged position within the Libyan leader's family, benefiting from the regime's patronage system that favored relatives with influential roles in state institutions. His integration into the Gaddafi inner circle was reinforced through marriage to the daughter of Khweldi al-Hmeidi, a close military ally of Muammar Gaddafi who participated in the 1969 coup establishing the regime. This familial network provided Al-Saadi access to resources and authority disproportionate to merit-based achievement, exemplifying the nepotistic structure sustaining the Gaddafi rule.[14] Al-Saadi's ties to the regime extended to military command, where he served as a special forces commander by 2011, leveraging family connections to oversee security operations loyal to his father. In this role, he coordinated units tasked with regime defense, including the importation of foreign mercenaries to bolster forces against domestic challengers. Such appointments reflected Muammar Gaddafi's strategy of distributing power among sons to secure loyalty and control key sectors, with Al-Saadi handling security apparatus alongside siblings like Mutassim, the national security advisor.[15][16] During the 2011 uprising, Al-Saadi actively supported the family's efforts to suppress dissent, reportedly directing security elements to conduct arbitrary killings and urging troops to fire on protesters in Benghazi in February of that year. These actions aligned him closely with the regime's survival imperatives, though post-uprising Libyan authorities later charged him with crimes against humanity for his involvement in protest suppression and protester deaths, highlighting the causal link between familial allegiance and operational ruthlessness. His subsequent flight to Niger in September 2011, where he received asylum, underscored the collapse of the family-led structure he had helped enforce.[17][16]

Football Career

Club and International Playing Achievements

Al-Saadi Gaddafi's club career primarily unfolded in the Libyan Premier League, where he began with Al-Ittihad Tripoli in the late 1990s before a stint at Al-Ahly Tripoli from 2000 to 2001. Returning to Al-Ittihad in 2001, he played as an attacking midfielder and helped secure two consecutive league titles in the 2001â€“02 and 2002â€“03 seasons, reportedly scoring 24 goals over 74 appearances during that period.[3][18][19] In 2003, Gaddafi moved to Europe, signing with Italian Serie A club Perugia, where he made one league appearance on 2 May 2004 in a 1â€“0 win against Juventus. He transferred to Udinese for the 2005â€“06 season, registering one Serie A appearance with no goals, and joined Sampdoria in 2006, accumulating two further appearances in the top flight without scoring before retiring in 2007. Overall, his European career yielded just four competitive appearances and zero goals across these clubs.[20][21] Gaddafi earned recognition in Libya as Footballer of the Year three times and top goal scorer once in the domestic league.[22] Internationally, he represented the Libya national team, captaining the side and accumulating 18 appearances with 2 goals between 2001 and 2003. Key fixtures included a 3â€“1 friendly win over Angola on 28 January 2001 (full 90 minutes played) and a 1â€“3 loss to Argentina on 30 April 2003.[23][24]

Administrative Roles in Libyan Football

Al-Saadi Gaddafi assumed the presidency of the Libyan Football Federation (LFF) in 1996, a position he held until the overthrow of his father's regime in 2011.[24][25] In this capacity, he directed the governance of Libyan football, including oversight of the national team, domestic leagues, and international affiliations, while simultaneously serving as captain of the Libya national team during parts of his tenure.[25] The LFF under his leadership managed key events such as qualification campaigns for African Cup of Nations tournaments, though administrative decisions were often intertwined with his family's political influence.[3] Gaddafi's role extended to strategic appointments within the federation, where he influenced referee selections and club transfers to favor Tripoli-based teams like Al-Ahly Tripoli, which he represented as a player.[5] By 2003, U.S. government assessments identified him explicitly as head of the LFF alongside his military commands, highlighting the federation's alignment with regime structures.[25] This administrative control facilitated Libya's hosting of the 2006 Arab Cup and efforts to elevate the national team's profile, culminating in their 2012 African Cup of Nations hosting bid preparations, though post-2011 instability disrupted continuity.[24]

Criticisms and Allegations of Nepotism and Match-Fixing

Al-Saadi Gaddafi faced widespread allegations of nepotism in his football career, leveraging his father's position as Libya's leader to secure administrative roles and playing opportunities disproportionate to his abilities. As head of the Libyan Football Federation, he captained Al-Ittihad Tripoli and reportedly used regime influence to attract talented players from rival clubs like Al-Ahly Benghazi to his team, exacerbating regional rivalries.[5] In Italy, where he briefly appeared for clubs including Perugia and Udinese between 2003 and 2006, his signings were attributed to Libyan state investments and family ties, such as the Gaddafi family's stake in Juventus and owner Luciano Gaucci's efforts to foster Libya-Italy relations encouraged by Silvio Berlusconi; he logged only 26 minutes of Serie A play despite training with top clubs.[26] Gaddafi was also charged with sporting fraud by Libyan prosecutors in July 2005, reflecting broader claims of undue influence in domestic football governance.[27] Match-fixing allegations centered on Gaddafi's efforts to manipulate outcomes favoring his interests, particularly in the 2000 Libyan Premier League clash between Al-Ittihad Tripoli and Al-Ahly Benghazi. Witnesses described dubious referee decisions, including questionable penalties and an offside goal, which sparked fan protests; Gaddafi allegedly coerced officials and players to continue amid unrest on July 20, 2000.[5] Following fan chants mocking him as a "donkey" during the match, regime forces demolished Al-Ahly Benghazi's training ground, offices, and records on September 1, 2000, leading to the club's relegation, indefinite ban from competition until 2005, and imprisonment of 31 supporters and staffâ€”three of whom received death sentences, with one dying by suicide.[5] These actions were portrayed by local accounts as retaliation to suppress dissent, with Gaddafi's federation role enabling such interventions; he denied involvement, but the incidents underscored claims of systemic corruption in Libyan sports under the regime.[5] In 2011, Interpol issued a warrant for his arrest on charges including corruption and intimidation tied to football-related abuses. Gaddafi was accused of orchestrating the 2005 murder of Bashir al-Rayani, a former Al-Ittihad player and coach who had defected to Al-Ahly and allegedly refused to fix matches; relatives and investigators claimed Gaddafi ordered the killing as retribution in the ongoing rivalry.[28] He was extradited from Niger to Libya in March 2014 to face these and other corruption charges, but a Tripoli court acquitted him in April 2018, citing insufficient evidence; Libya's Supreme Court ordered a retrial in July 2024 amid family demands for justice.[29][28][7] These cases highlight persistent skepticism toward post-regime judicial outcomes in Libya, given the entrenched patronage networks during Gaddafi's rule.[6]

Professional and Business Activities

Involvement in Military and Security Apparatus

Al-Saadi Gaddafi served as commander of Libya's special forces, a role documented in international sanctions listings and contemporary reports.[30] [31] This position placed him within the regime's security apparatus, which prioritized loyalty to the Gaddafi family over broader military hierarchy. Prior to 2011, his public profile centered on football administration rather than overt military duties, though familial influence likely facilitated access to command structures reserved for elites.[2] In early 2011, amid the uprising against his father Muammar Gaddafi's rule, Al-Saadi assumed a more active command role, leading a special forces brigade dispatched to Benghazi to quell rebel advances.[24] His unit participated in counterinsurgency operations, including efforts to reinforce regime control in eastern Libya. Reports from the period, drawing on defector accounts and opposition claims, allege that the brigade under his direction engaged in suppressing demonstrations through force, though these assertions emerged primarily from post-regime sources amid the conflict's chaos.[16] [32] The special forces command reflected the Gaddafi regime's reliance on praetorian unitsâ€”personally overseen by family membersâ€”to maintain internal security, distinct from conventional army brigades like the Khamis Brigade led by his brother. Al-Saadi's tenure ended with his flight from Libya in September 2011, as regime defenses collapsed.[33] This involvement drew international scrutiny, contributing to his designation in sanctions for roles in the security apparatus.[30]

Business Ventures and Investments

Al-Saadi Gaddafi served as chairman of the Libyan Arab Foreign Investment Company (LAFICO), a state-owned entity through which he oversaw investments in foreign assets, including a significant stake in Italian football club Juventus. In 2002, LAFICO, under his leadership, acquired 6.4 million shares in Juventus, representing 5.31% of the club's capital following its stock market listing.[34][35] This investment later expanded to approximately 7.5% ownership, and Gaddafi was appointed to Juventus's board of directors the same year.[36][37] Gaddafi also ventured into the entertainment industry by investing $100 million in Natural Selection, a Los Angeles-based film production fund launched in 2009.[38] As the primary financier, the investment supported projects such as the 2010 film The Experiment (budgeted at $12 million) and a biopic on mobster Richard Kuklinski titled The Iceman.[39][40] The fund, which drew additional backing from private equity, aimed to produce multiple independent films amid a post-financial crisis scarcity of indie financing.[41] His personal investments included high-value real estate acquisitions abroad. In May 2008, Gaddafi purchased a 2,218-square-foot penthouse condominium in Toronto's Harbour View Estates for approximately $1.6 million CAD during an extended stay in Canada.[42][43] In 2010, he acquired a Â£10 million mansion at 7 Winnington Close in London's affluent Hampstead Garden Suburb through Capitana Seas Limited, a British Virgin Islands-registered company; Libyan authorities later recovered the property in 2012, alleging it was funded by misappropriated state resources.[44][45][46] Gaddafi listed the Toronto property for sale in 2023 amid ongoing asset recovery efforts.[42]

Personal Life and Public Image

Marriages, Children, and Family Dynamics

Al-Saadi Gaddafi is married to the daughter of al-Khweildi al-Hmeidi, a Libyan military commander instrumental in the 1969 coup that installed Muammar Gaddafi in power.[59] U.S. diplomatic assessments from the mid-2000s described the union as an arranged marriage, motivated in part by Muammar Gaddafi's concerns over his son's reported bisexuality, which reportedly strained their relationship and prompted efforts to align Saadi with regime loyalists through familial ties.[59] Public details on Al-Saadi Gaddafi's children remain limited, with no verified names or exact number disclosed in available records. He has at least two children, as evidenced by reports of him reuniting with his wife and children in Turkey following his 2021 release from Libyan detention.[60] Within the broader Gaddafi family, dynamics reflected patriarchal control, with Muammar exerting influence over sons' personal affairs to maintain regime stability and public image, often prioritizing political alliances over individual autonomy.[59] Al-Saadi's lifestyle, marked by extravagance and reported personal indiscretions, positioned him as less favored compared to siblings like Saif al-Islam, contributing to internal tensions amid the clan's reliance on inherited privilege and security apparatus protection.[59]

Lifestyle, Extravagance, and Media Portrayals

Al-Saadi Gaddafi maintained a lavish lifestyle characterized by frequent international travel, high-end accommodations, and conspicuous consumption funded by access to Libyan state resources. Role in the 2011 Libyan Civil War

Defense of the Regime Against Uprising Forces

Al-Saadi Gaddafi assumed command of Libya's Special Forces brigade in early 2011 amid the initial outbreaks of protests against his father's regime.[25] In this role, his units participated in operations to counter uprising forces, focusing on securing Tripoli and quelling dissent in the capital and surrounding areas.[16] [70] These efforts included deploying elite troops to reinforce regime loyalists against rebel incursions, contributing to the government's initial success in retaining control over western Libya despite defections in the east.[31] On March 7, 2011, Gaddafi publicly stated that his father had refrained from committing the full military might against rebels, signaling a phased escalation in defensive measures rather than all-out confrontation at that stage.[71] His special forces, known for their training and loyalty to the Gaddafi family, were instrumental in bolstering defenses around key regime strongholds, including the Bab al-Aziziya compound in Tripoli, as opposition forces advanced from Misrata and the Nafusa Mountains.[2] However, as NATO airstrikes intensified from March onward, the effectiveness of these units diminished, with Gaddafi eventually fleeing the country in late August 2011.[55] United Nations sanctions imposed on Gaddafi in February 2011 explicitly targeted his position as Special Forces commander for actions perceived as threats to civilian populations during the suppression efforts.[30] Post-regime accounts from Libyan authorities accused his brigade of direct involvement in violent crackdowns, though specific operational details remain limited due to the chaotic nature of the conflict and reliance on regime-aligned reporting at the time.[72]

Accusations of Violence and Suppression Efforts

Al-Saadi Gaddafi served as commander of Libya's Special Forces, known as Al-Saiqa (Lightning), a unit elite paramilitary force loyal to the Gaddafi regime. During the early stages of the 2011 uprising, which erupted in Benghazi on February 15, his forces were deployed to eastern Libya to quell protests against his father's rule. Accusations center on his direct oversight of violent operations, including the use of live ammunition against unarmed demonstrators, as part of the regime's broader strategy to crush dissent through lethal force.[30][70] In Benghazi, where the crackdown intensified from February 17â€“20, Saadi Gaddafi was reportedly sent to assume a leadership role in restoring order, including coordination with local security apparatus. A BBC report, referenced in International Criminal Court documents, alleged that he personally issued orders to shoot at protesters, contributing to the deaths of at least dozens in the city's streets amid clashes that saw security forces using helicopters and armored vehicles. These actions formed part of the regime's response, which Human Rights Watch documented as resulting in over 300 fatalities nationwide by late February, though specific attribution to Saadi's commands remains based on witness accounts and media reports rather than adjudicated findings.[73] Post-uprising Libyan authorities charged Saadi Gaddafi with leading military units responsible for suppressing protesters, including allegations of murder and misuse of authority during the crackdown. He was accused of command responsibility for atrocities in multiple locations, though his flight to Niger on or around February 21 limited his operational involvement after the initial phase. These claims, drawn from defector testimonies and intelligence, underscore his role in operationalizing the regime's repressive tactics, distinct from his brother Saif al-Islam's more propagandistic efforts. No International Criminal Court arrest warrant was issued against him, unlike for other family members, but domestic proceedings highlighted his military directives as exacerbating civilian casualties.[6][54]

Flight, Extradition, and Imprisonment

Escape to Niger and Temporary Asylum

Following the collapse of Muammar Gaddafi's control over major Libyan cities in August 2011, Al-Saadi Gaddafi fled southward amid advancing National Transitional Council (NTC) forces. He crossed the remote Libya-Niger border in a convoy on or around September 11, 2011, entering Niger near the town of Dirkou in the Tenere Desert, accompanied by other regime loyalists and Tuareg allies who facilitated the route due to longstanding Gaddafi support for Tuareg separatist groups.[74][75] Niger's Justice Minister Marou Adamou confirmed Saadi's arrival on September 12, 2011, noting that the government was aware of his presence but had not formally sought him out.[75] Niger provided Saadi with temporary refuge despite NTC demands for his extradition on charges including the 2005 killing of footballer Bashir al-Rayyes and alleged orders to suppress protesters in Benghazi. On November 11, 2011, President Mahamadou Issoufou announced that asylum would be granted on humanitarian grounds, citing Saadi's status as a fugitive without immediate threat to Nigerien security, though this decision strained relations with the NTC and ignored UN Security Council sanctions imposing a travel ban on Gaddafi family members.[54][76][77] Saadi resided primarily in a state guesthouse in Niamey, under loose surveillance, while Niger rejected repeated Libyan extradition requests through 2013, attributing the shelter to humanitarian obligations and ethnic Tuareg ties rather than political allegiance.[13][78] This arrangement lasted until March 2014, when Niger facilitated his handover to Libyan authorities amid mounting international pressure.[79]

Extradition to Libya and Detention Conditions

Al-Saadi Gaddafi, who had fled Libya for Niger in September 2011 amid the civil war, was extradited from Niger to Libya on March 6, 2014.[79] [29] The handover followed prolonged diplomatic pressure on Niger, which had hosted him despite Interpol notices and Libyan requests, and came shortly after the extradition of another Gaddafi associate, intelligence official Abdullah al-Senussi, in February 2014.[80] Upon arrival in Tripoli, Libyan authorities confirmed his transfer to the custody of the judicial police for processing related to charges including the 2005 killing of trainer Bashir al-Rayess.[81] [82] Following initial custody, Gaddafi was detained at al-Hadba prison in Tripoli, where he remained in solitary confinement for the duration of his imprisonment until 2021.[56] His cell was described as windowless but equipped with a fan; he had no direct contact with other detainees and only limited, supervised communication with family members.[56] Gaddafi reported experiencing health deterioration, including chronic back pain from prior surgeries and shortness of breath, for which he received medical examinations and treatment within the facility.[56] Trial proceedings began in May 2015 on charges of murder, illegal alcohol possession, and deprivation of liberty, though he was acquitted of the murder charge in July 2018 while remaining in detention.[56]

Torture Allegations and Human Rights Claims

Following his extradition to Libya on March 6, 2014, Al-Saadi Gaddafi was detained at al-Hadba Corrections Facility in Tripoli, where he faced allegations of ill-treatment and due process violations. In an undated video released on August 2, 2015, by the website clearnews.com, Gaddafi appeared partially blindfolded and connected to an improvised stretching device during an interrogation by uniformed personnel, who slapped him, beat the soles of his feet with a plastic pipe causing audible screams, and forced him to witness beatings of other detainees nearby.[83] [84] Interrogators threatened further beatings, asking Gaddafi whether he preferred strikes to his feet or buttocks, with no legal representation present; the footage, lasting about nine minutes, depicted profanity-laced insults toward prisoners.[83] [85] Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International condemned the video as evidence of torture threats and abuse, urging Libyan authorities to investigate, suspend implicated guards, prosecute perpetrators, and ensure detainee protections amid widespread reports of mistreatment in militia-controlled facilities.[83] [85] In a September 15, 2015, interview with Human Rights Watch at al-Hadba, Gaddafi reported ongoing solitary confinement in a windowless cell since his arrivalâ€”depriving him of contact with other inmatesâ€”and described being "terrorized" through intimidation during interrogations, lack of private lawyer access until May 2015 (with subsequent denials citing security), and pressure on defense witnesses.[56] He referenced the abuse video as illustrative of his treatment, though he noted some amenities like a fan; legal counsel Karim Khan QC described the conditions as "shocking" and called for international scrutiny.[84] [56] Libyan officials in Tripoli responded by initiating an investigation, suspending the al-Hadba prison director, and pursuing three suspectsâ€”one of whom had fled the countryâ€”while claiming no justification for torture exists.[56] [86] Despite these measures, rights groups noted persistent risks in the facility, controlled by the Libya Dawn militia alliance during the country's factional conflicts, with Gaddafi's pretrial detention extending over seven years until his 2021 release, during which similar systemic issues like arbitrary isolation persisted without full resolution.[83] [56]

Release and Post-Detention Status

2021 Release from Prison

Al-Saadi Gaddafi was released from Al-Hadba prison in Tripoli on September 6, 2021, after more than seven years of detention since his extradition from Niger in 2014.[2][87] The release was authorized by the Libyan Chief Prosecutor's Office with the approval of Prime Minister Abdul Hamid Dbeibeh, amid ongoing political fragmentation in post-2011 Libya where rival factions controlled different judicial interpretations.[88][89] The immediate trigger for his freedom was an acquittal on charges related to the violent suppression of protesters during the 2011 civil war, including the alleged murder of a rebel fighter, though he had faced prior convictions that were later contested due to procedural irregularities and claims of coerced confessions under duress.[90][91] Despite the acquittal, his prolonged imprisonment reflected Libya's unstable legal environment, where militia influences and factional loyalties often overrode formal judicial outcomes, as evidenced by his retention in custody even after initial exonerations.[7] Upon release, Gaddafi departed Libya directly for Turkey via airplane, marking his transition from domestic detention to foreign exile, where he had previously sought asylum during his 2011 flight from advancing rebels.[92] This move aligned with Turkey's historical ties to elements of the Gaddafi family, though it drew no immediate international legal challenges at the time, given the evaporation of Interpol warrants tied to his Libyan charges.[89][87]

Exile in Turkey and Current Residence

Following his release from Hadaba prison in Tripoli on September 5, 2021, Al-Saadi Gaddafi departed Libya via a special flight and arrived in Turkey the same day.[93][94] Turkish authorities initially denied reports of his presence in Istanbul on September 12, 2021, amid speculation of his arrival.[95] However, subsequent investigations by a UN Panel of Experts on Libya confirmed his official residence in Turkey, noting that Ankara's hosting violated UN Security Council Resolution 1970, which imposes travel bans on Gaddafi family members linked to the former regime's repression.[96] As of October 2025, Al-Saadi Gaddafi continues to reside in Istanbul, where he maintains a low public profile with no declared political activity.[97][98][99] He has occasionally commented on Libyan affairs via social media, such as a February 2025 X post supporting Turkey's diplomatic presence in Libya amid shifting regional dynamics.[100] Despite his exile, he faces ongoing international scrutiny due to prior INTERPOL notices for charges including murder and suppression of dissent during the 2011 civil war, though Turkey has not extradited him.[94]

Ongoing Sanctions and Legal Status

Al-Saadi Gaddafi remains designated under United Nations Security Council Resolution 1970 (2011), which imposes an asset freeze and travel ban on individuals responsible for or supporting violence against civilians in Libya, including Gaddafi family members involved in the regime's repression.[30] The UN Panel of Experts on Libya has extended limited humanitarian travel exemptions for him periodically, most recently from June 1 to November 30, 2024, allowing unrestricted movement for medical or family purposes under notification to the Committee, though core sanctions persist without delisting.[101] These UN measures are implemented domestically by multiple jurisdictions. In the United States, the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) lists him on the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) roster since March 11, 2011, prohibiting U.S. persons from transactions with him and freezing any assets under U.S. jurisdiction.[102] The European Union maintains him on its consolidated financial sanctions list, subjecting him to asset freezes and prohibiting funds or economic resources from being made available, with the list updated as of August 1, 2025.[103] Similarly, the United Kingdom renewed sanctions against him on December 6, 2023, enforcing asset freezes and travel bans, with confirmation of his listing as of January 2, 2025.[104][105] Legally, Gaddafi faces an Interpol Red Notice for alleged crimes including ordering the violent suppression of protesters in Tripoli in February 2011, stemming from Libyan authorities' requests post-regime fall.[94] No international extradition has occurred, partly due to his residence in Turkey since his September 2021 release from Libyan detention, where he arrived via special flight despite sanctions.[93] Turkey has not officially acknowledged his presence or responded to UN inquiries, leading to accusations of non-compliance with Resolution 1970, though no formal enforcement actions against Turkey have been reported as of October 2025.[96] Libyan courts issued warrants against him prior to his release, but post-2021 efforts for repatriation have stalled amid the country's political divisions.[97]
