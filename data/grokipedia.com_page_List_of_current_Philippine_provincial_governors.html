List of current Philippine governors

The list of current Philippine governors enumerates the chief executives of the country's 82 provinces, each heading the provincial government as the primary authority for local executive functions such as budget allocation, infrastructure oversight, public safety coordination, and implementation of national policies at the provincial level.[1] These officials were elected in the synchronized local elections held on May 12, 2025, assuming office on June 30, 2025, for a standard three-year term renewable up to three consecutive times under the Local Government Code.[2] A defining feature of this roster is the dominance of political dynasties, with 71 of the 82 governors (approximately 87%) originating from families that control multiple elective positions, reflecting entrenched oligarchic patterns in Philippine governance that have resisted reform efforts despite Article II, Section 26 of the 1987 Constitution's explicit anti-dynasty provision.[3][2] This composition highlights causal factors like resource advantages and voter familiarity in low-information rural electorates, perpetuating power concentration amid weak enforcement of term limits and dynasty bans.[4]

Provincial Governance Framework

Role and Responsibilities of Governors

The provincial governor is the chief executive of the provincial government, tasked with exercising powers and performing duties to ensure efficient, effective, and economical governance that promotes the general welfare of the province and its inhabitants, as defined in Section 465 of Republic Act No. 7160, the Local Government Code of 1991.[5] This role encompasses general supervision and control over all provincial programs, projects, services, and activities, including determining policy guidelines and bearing responsibility to the Sangguniang Panlalawigan (provincial board) for the overall program of government.[5] The governor directs the formulation, execution, and implementation of the provincial development plan, in coordination with the provincial development council and subject to approval by the Sangguniang Panlalawigan.[5] Enforcement of laws and ordinances forms a core responsibility, with the governor required to implement approved policies, programs, and projects while issuing executive orders to ensure faithful compliance; copies of such orders must be furnished to the Office of the President within 72 hours.[5] The governor also represents the province and its interests in inter-provincial or inter-regional relations, negotiates contracts or agreements (subject to Sangguniang Panlalawigan approval where required), and visits all component cities and municipalities at least once every six months to assess conditions and verify adherence to laws.[5] In matters of public safety, the governor formulates and enforces the provincial peace and order plan in collaboration with municipal mayors and the Philippine National Police, and may call upon national agencies to suppress disorder or apprehend violators.[5] The governor oversees the delivery of basic services and facilities mandated under Section 17 of the Code, including solid waste disposal, health services, social welfare, public works, and infrastructure like roads and bridges, prioritizing agro-industrial development and countryside growth to foster self-reliance.[5] Resource generation and fiscal management are integral, requiring the governor to maximize revenues, prepare and submit annual executive budgets (and supplemental ones as needed) to the Sangguniang Panlalawigan by specified deadlines, and ensure proper collection and application of funds to obligations.[5] Appointments of provincial officials and employeesâ€”whose salaries are drawn from provincial fundsâ€”fall under the governor's purview, subject to civil service laws and, in some cases, Sangguniang Panlalawigan concurrence.[5] Additional functions include carrying out emergency measures during disasters, heading the provincial health board to advise on health policies and budgets, solemnizing marriages, and conducting annual athletic meets (Palarong Panlalawigan) in coordination with the Department of Education.[5] The governor submits annual reports on socio-economic conditions, management, and post-disaster assessments to the President and Department of the Interior and Local Government, while coordinating with national agencies for technical assistance and enforcing environmental protection laws.[5] These responsibilities underscore the governor's pivotal role in bridging national directives with local implementation, ensuring component local government units operate within legal bounds.[5]

Election and Term Structure

Provincial governors in the Philippines are elected at large by the qualified voters of their respective provinces through a plurality voting system, whereby the candidate receiving the highest number of votes wins the position. These elections occur simultaneously with other local government positions as part of synchronized national and local polls, held every three years on the second Monday of May, with the most recent occurring on May 12, 2025. The Commission on Elections (COMELEC) administers the process, including voter registration, candidacy filing, campaigning, voting, and canvassing by provincial boards. Successful candidates are proclaimed by the provincial board of canvassers, typically within weeks of the election, and assume office on June 30 following the vote. Each gubernatorial term lasts three years, as stipulated in the Local Government Code of 1991 (Republic Act No. 7160). Governors may serve up to three consecutive terms, after which they are ineligible to run for the same position until at least one full term has elapsed, preventing indefinite incumbency while allowing non-consecutive reelection. This structure aims to balance continuity in leadership with opportunities for turnover, though enforcement has faced challenges from political dynasties that alternate family members across terms or related offices.[4] To qualify for election as governor, candidates must be natural-born Filipino citizens, at least 23 years old on election day, registered voters in the province, able to read and write in Filipino or a local language or English, and residents of the province for at least one year immediately preceding the election. Disqualifications include those under the Omnibus Election Code, such as prior convictions for crimes involving moral turpitude or acts of terrorism, enforced by COMELEC during candidacy certification. Vacancies arising mid-term due to death, resignation, or permanent incapacity are filled by the vice governor, with special elections called if the unexpired term exceeds one year.

Equivalent Executive Positions in Non-Provincial Jurisdictions

Independent Component Cities and Municipalities

Independent component cities in the Philippines operate outside provincial jurisdiction, with their residents ineligible to vote for provincial officials and the cities exempt from provincial taxation and legislative representation in provincial boards.[9] This status stems from specific city charters that establish administrative autonomy while maintaining geographic ties to a province. There are five such cities: Cotabato City (geographically in Maguindanao del Sur but administratively independent), Dagupan City (Pangasinan), Naga City (Camarines Sur), Ormoc City (Leyte), and Santiago City (Isabela).[10] Their chief executives, known as mayors, hold executive authority analogous to provincial governors in scope, managing local administration, budgeting, and services without provincial oversight. Mayors serve three-year terms, with the most recent elections held on May 12, 2025, and terms commencing June 30, 2025. No municipalities in the Philippines hold independent component status equivalent to these cities; all 1,493 municipalities remain under provincial governance, with mayors subject to provincial supervision.[11] Thus, executive equivalents in non-provincial jurisdictions here pertain solely to the mayors of independent component cities.

Highly Urbanized Cities Excluded from Provincial Oversight

Highly urbanized cities (HUCs) in the Philippines operate independently of provincial governments, a status designed to enable self-governance in densely populated urban areas with significant economic activity. Under Section 452(c) of the Local Government Code of 1991 (Republic Act No. 7160), HUCs qualify through criteria including a population of at least 200,000 persons, an average annual income of PHP 50 million or more based on the most recent two years' audited financial statements, and a contiguous territory comprising at least 100 square kilometers.[5] Once proclaimed by the President upon recommendation, these cities sever ties with their host provinces, ceasing to share revenues, participate in provincial elections, or fall under gubernatorial supervision. This exclusion fosters localized decision-making but requires HUCs to fund services like infrastructure, health, and education autonomously, often relying on internal revenue allotment (IRA) from national funds and local taxes. The chief executive of an HUC is the city mayor, who holds authority comparable to a provincial governor within city limits, including veto power over city council ordinances, appointment of department heads, and oversight of city police and fire services.[5] Unlike provinces, HUCs report directly to the Department of the Interior and Local Government (DILG) for administrative guidance, bypassing provincial layers that could delay urban-specific policies. This structure, rooted in the 1991 Code's decentralization principles, aims to match governance scale to urban demands, though empirical data from the Philippine Statistics Authority shows HUCs often face higher per capita service delivery costs due to population density without provincial resource pooling. As of October 2025, 33 HUCs exist, including all 16 Metro Manila cities and 17 others such as Angeles, Bacolod, Baguio, Cebu City, and Davao City. Mayors serve three-year terms, with the most recent elections held on May 12, 2025. Outcomes reflected incumbency advantages in many races, particularly in Metro Manila, where reelectionists prevailed amid a 65-70% voter turnout.[21] Specific results included Francisco "Isko" Moreno Domagoso's proclaimed victory in Manila City, defeating incumbent Honey Lacuna by leveraging prior administrative experience.[22] In Cebu City, Nestor Archival secured the mayoralty over reelectionist Raymond Alvin Garcia, marking a shift in local leadership.[23] Rodrigo Duterte won in Davao City despite his ongoing detention at the International Criminal Court, drawing on familial political machinery and voter loyalty in the region.[24] Full rosters of HUC mayors are maintained by the Commission on Elections (Comelec) and DILG, with updates reflecting any post-election proclamations or disqualifications.

Bangsamoro Autonomous Region Leadership

Chief Minister and Transitional Authority

The Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA) functions as the interim regional government for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM), an autonomous political entity established by Republic Act No. 11054, the Bangsamoro Organic Law, ratified in January 2019. Composed of 80 appointed members, the BTA exercises both legislative authority through its parliament and executive functions via appointed ministries until the first regular regional elections can be conducted. The transition period, intended to build institutional capacity and enact enabling laws such as those for districting and political parties, has faced repeated delays; originally slated for conclusion in 2022, parliamentary elections planned for October 13, 2025, were postponed by the Supreme Court to 2026 owing to the lack of a valid districting law required for electoral apportionment.[25][26] The Chief Minister serves as the head of the BTA's executive arm, wielding primary authority over the Bangsamoro Cabinet, which includes 16 ministries covering areas such as trade, health, and education. Key powers include appointing ministry heads, overseeing budget execution, representing BARMM in intergovernmental relations with the national government, and directing policy implementation on regional matters like resource management and local governance. During the transitional phase, the Chief Minister is appointed by the Philippine President rather than elected, reflecting the interim nature of the authority amid ongoing normalization efforts from the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF) peace process.[27][28] Abdulraof A. Macacua has held the position of interim Chief Minister since his appointment by President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. on March 9, 2025, succeeding Ahod "Al Haj Murad" Ebrahim, who served from February 22, 2019, to March 2025. Macacua, previously governor of Maguindanao del Norte and chief of staff to Ebrahim, assumed office amid reported tensions over the unilateral replacement of Ebrahim, a longtime MILF chair, which some observers linked to national government priorities for accelerating BARMM's political normalization. As of October 2025, Macacua continues in this role, with the BTA parliament under his influence recently electing Mohammad Yacob as speaker on October 21, 2025, to address pending legislation for eventual elections.[29][30][31]

Political Dynasties in Governorships

Empirical Prevalence and Data

As of the 2025 midterm elections held on May 12, 71 of the Philippines' 82 provinces are governed by individuals from political dynasties, comprising 87% of all governorships.[2][3] This figure reflects a marginal decline from prior cycles, as 12 incumbent dynastic governors lost their races, enabling non-dynastic candidates to secure positions in 11 provinces including Apayao, Batanes, and Biliran.[2] Political dynasties are defined as families with at least two members who have secured elected positions in at least two separate elections, often spanning multiple generations and jurisdictions.[46] Empirical analyses indicate that dynastic prevalence at the gubernatorial level has remained above 80% since at least the 2010 elections, with family networks leveraging incumbency advantages, resource control, and localized patronage to maintain dominance.[47] Of the 71 dynastic governors elected in 2025, 47â€”over two-thirdsâ€”were incumbents seeking reelection, underscoring the role of term continuity in perpetuating family holdovers despite constitutional three-term limits per individual.[47][48] In contrast, the 11 non-dynastic governorships emerged primarily from competitive races where anti-dynasty sentiment or fragmented clan rivalries allowed outsiders to prevail, though such breakthroughs are statistically rare given the structural barriers.[2] Quantitative data from election monitoring reveals that dynastic candidates won 92% of gubernatorial contests in Mindanao provinces and 85% in Luzon, with Visayas showing slightly lower rates at 78%, attributable to regional variations in clan fragmentation and voter turnout.[2] Nationally, nearly 250 extended political families exert influence across provincial, congressional, and local posts, but gubernatorial control is concentrated among fewer core lineages that rotate relatives into executive roles to evade term restrictions.[47] This pattern aligns with broader metrics of elite persistence, where dynastic incumbents secure victory margins averaging 15-20% higher than non-dynastic challengers due to verifiable disparities in campaign spending and voter mobilization.[2]

Causal Factors and Empirical Outcomes

Political dynasties in Philippine governorships persist primarily due to the 1987 Constitution's term limits, which incentivize incumbents to field relatives to retain family influence, circumventing direct re-election bans. Empirical analysis of gubernatorial elections from 1946 to 2007 reveals that 43% to 49% of term-limited governors were succeeded by relatives, with families adapting through office rotationâ€”such as shifting to congressional seats while placing kin in the vacated governorship.[4] This mechanism, unintended by reformers aiming to curb personalistic rule post-Marcos, instead entrenches dynastic control, as evidenced by over 50% of governors since 1901 having relatives who previously held office. Additional causal drivers include patronage-based electoral systems, where family networks leverage resources for vote-buying and clientelism, amplified by weak political parties and media ownership concentrated among elites. High inequality and poverty further entrench dynasties, as landowning families historically dominate rural provinces, using economic leverage to suppress competition.[49] Empirical outcomes demonstrate dynasties' association with suboptimal governance, particularly in non-Luzon provinces. Provinces led by dynastic governors exhibit higher poverty rates, with data from 2004â€“2016 showing dynasties exacerbating poverty incidence in resource-rich areas outside Luzon, where weaker business competition enables rent-seeking and predation, unlike competitive Luzon markets that constrain such behavior.[50] Dynastic dominance correlates with reduced public goods provision, including lower infrastructure development, health service delivery, and education outcomes, as family clans prioritize personal networks over broad welfare.[51] As of 2022â€“2025, 71 of 82 provinces were governed by dynastic figures, reflecting limited voter resistance despite these patterns, though 11 provinces broke dynastic holds in recent elections. While some studies note bidirectional causalityâ€”poverty reinforcing dynastiesâ€”evidence from difference-in-differences analyses confirms dynasties hinder accountability and foster corruption risks by limiting challenger entry and concentrating power.[2][4]

Recent Developments and Electoral Context

2025 Gubernatorial Elections

Gubernatorial elections occurred on May 12, 2025, as part of the nationwide midterm polls, with voters in each of the Philippines' 82 provinces selecting governors for three-year terms starting June 30, 2025.[52] These contests tested the resilience of incumbent administrations amid economic pressures, infrastructure priorities, and localized issues, though national political alignments influenced several races.[2] Political dynasties preserved dominance, securing governorships in 71 provinces, reflecting entrenched familial networks despite campaigns highlighting anti-dynasty platforms.[2] Of the 12 losses for dynasties, four provinces elected non-dynastic candidates, signaling pockets of voter pushback: Cebu, where Pamela Baricuatro ousted three-term incumbent Gwendolyn Garcia; Laguna, with former Representative Sol Aragones defeating Representative Ruth Mariano-Hernandez; Catanduanes, as Patrick Azanza bested Vice Governor Peter Cua; and Marinduque, where businessman Mel Go overcame Representative Lord Allan Jay Velasco.[2] In 58 cases, ruling clans held their seats, while eight saw one dynasty supplant a competing family; Batangas marked a rare instance of a dynastic figure, Vilma Santos-Recto, replacing a non-dynast.[2] Turnout and proclamation timelines followed standard Commission on Elections protocols, with most winners certified within weeks, though isolated protests delayed a handful of inaugurations until mid-July 2025.[52] The results perpetuated dynastic continuity, with analysts noting that while 12 shifts occurred, broader trends favored incumbency and family ties over reformist challengers, even as midterm dynamics pressured President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.'s allies in select regions.[2]

Notable Changes and Disputes

In the May 12, 2025, gubernatorial elections, voters ousted 12 political dynasties from provincial leadership, leading to non-dynastic governors in 11 provincesâ€”a departure from the pre-election pattern where 71 of 82 governors hailed from such families.[2] This shift, while limited, highlighted localized anti-dynasty sentiment amid broader dominance, with dynasties retaining control in 71 provinces post-election.[2][3] Several high-profile disputes emerged from the contests, with losing candidates filing protests alleging irregularities. In Cebu Province, outgoing Governor Gwendolyn Garcia submitted an electoral protest to the Commission on Elections (Comelec) on June 3, 2025, challenging her narrow defeat and seeking a recount or revision of votes in specific precincts.[53][54] In Marinduque, former House Speaker Lord Allan Velasco filed for a manual recount on May 23, 2025, after conceding his loss but citing discrepancies in automated vote transmission and tallying.[55] In Misamis Oriental, incumbent Governor Peter Unabia announced plans on May 14, 2025, to protest his reelection defeat, pointing to potential fraud in vote counting.[56] Comelec urged petitioners to substantiate claims with affidavits and evidence of anomalies like pre-shaded ballots or overvotes, amid broader scrutiny of the automated system.[57][58] As of late 2025, these cases remained under review, with no final resolutions reported.[59]
