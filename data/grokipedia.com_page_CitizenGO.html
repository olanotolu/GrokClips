CitizenGO


CitizenGO is an international non-governmental organization founded in 2013 in Madrid, Spain, comprising a community of active citizens who utilize online petitions and action alerts to defend and promote life, family, and liberty.[1] Operating from a Christian perspective, it seeks to ensure that those in power respect human dignity and individual rights by influencing governments, institutions, and businesses through grassroots mobilization.[2][1] With headquarters in Madrid and team members across 15 cities on three continents, CitizenGO facilitates campaigns in 50 countries and 12 languages, focusing on issues such as the protection of unborn life, parental authority in education against ideological indoctrination, and resistance to supranational policies perceived as eroding national sovereignty and traditional values.[1][3] Founded by lawyer Ignacio Arsuaga, who serves on its board of trustees alongside international figures like Brian Brown and Luca VolontÃ¨, the organization has coordinated over 500 campaigns, amassing tens of millions of signatures from its reported 19 million members to challenge progressive agendas on abortion, euthanasia, and gender ideology.[4][1][5] While praised by supporters for amplifying conservative voices globally, CitizenGO has faced opposition from left-leaning institutions and media, often portrayed with biased labels despite its emphasis on democratic participation and empirical defense of family structures.[1]

History

Founding and Origins

CitizenGO was established in 2013 in Madrid, Spain, by Ignacio Arsuaga, a Spanish lawyer and entrepreneur who had previously founded the advocacy organization HazteOÃ­r in 2001.[6][7] The initiative emerged as an extension of HazteOÃ­r's domestic petition-based activism, aiming to create an international platform for mobilizing citizens against policies perceived as threats to traditional family values, life issues, and religious freedom.[8] Arsuaga conceived CitizenGO as a multilingual online tool to facilitate global petitions and campaigns, drawing on the model of grassroots digital advocacy that HazteOÃ­r had successfully employed in Spain.[7] The organization's origins reflect a response to increasing legislative and cultural shifts in Europe and beyond, particularly around issues like same-sex marriage legalization and abortion rights expansion, which Arsuaga and associates viewed as eroding foundational societal principles.[9] Launched with a focus on Europe but quickly expanding outreach, CitizenGO positioned itself as a non-partisan citizen-driven entity, though its leadership and early campaigns were rooted in Catholic conservative networks.[10] By its inception, it had garnered initial support from figures in international pro-life and pro-family movements, setting the stage for rapid growth in membership and influence.[4]

Expansion and Milestones

CitizenGO, founded in Madrid, Spain, in 2013 as an outgrowth of the domestic advocacy group HazteOir, initially focused on replicating successful petition-driven mobilization models internationally to address perceived threats to life, family, and sovereignty beyond Spanish-speaking regions.[11] The platform leveraged digital tools for rapid scaling, translating campaigns into 12 languages including English, French, Portuguese, and Polish, enabling participation from users in diverse locales.[1] Expansion accelerated through virtual coordination rather than physical infrastructure, establishing team presence in 15 cities across three continents while conducting actions in 50 countries by the mid-2010s.[1] This decentralized model facilitated partnerships with local conservative and pro-family entities, amplifying offline protests alongside online drives; for instance, by 2017, it had orchestrated coordinated petitions and street actions in multiple European and Latin American nations.[12] Notable milestones underscore this growth: in 2016, CitizenGO mobilized opposition to a Mexican constitutional amendment that proponents argued would enshrine expansive reproductive rights at the expense of fetal protections, claiming significant influence in its blockage via mass signatures and lobbying.[9] A 2019 campaign against a Disney World parade in Orlando promoting LGBT themes collected over 500,000 signatures, demonstrating capacity for large-scale international mobilization on cultural issues. By 2024, the group reported executing over 500 petitions annually, including interventions at the United Nations and efforts to halt the World Health Organization's pandemic treaty, reflecting sustained operational maturity.[13] Membership metrics highlight quantitative expansion, with CitizenGO reporting a base exceeding 19 million active participantsâ€”primarily petition signers and donorsâ€”sustained by small online contributions rather than large institutional funding.[14] This volunteer-driven scaling has positioned it as a transnational counterweight to progressive advocacy networks, though critics from human rights organizations contend its tactics prioritize ideological goals over pluralistic dialogue.[12]

Organizational Structure

CitizenGO was founded on July 12, 2013, by Ignacio Arsuaga, a Madrid-based lawyer who previously established the Spanish advocacy group HazteOir in 2001 to promote conservative social policies. Arsuaga has served as CitizenGO's president since its inception, directing its global campaigns on family and life issues, with some sources also identifying him as chief executive officer.[3][15] The organization's governance is managed by a Board of Trustees responsible for strategic oversight and policy alignment. Current board members include founder Ignacio Arsuaga, Blanca Escobar (secretary and HazteOir board member), Luca VolontÃ¨ (former Italian parliamentarian and conservative lobbyist), Brian Brown (president of the U.S.-based National Organization for Marriage), Gualberto GarcÃ­a (policy expert on bioethics), and Carlos Polo (advocate for pro-life initiatives).[1][16][17] CitizenGO operates as a decentralized international network, with headquarters in Madrid, Spain, and operational teams across 15 cities on three continents, emphasizing volunteer-driven petitions over hierarchical control. It maintains separate legal entities, such as Citizengo Inc. in the United States registered as a 501(c)(4) social welfare organization, allowing advocacy without direct political endorsements. Mission and Principles

Core Values and Objectives

CitizenGO's foundational mission centers on mobilizing citizens to defend fundamental human rights and promote participatory democracy, with a particular emphasis on life, family, and liberty. The organization positions itself as a platform that equips individuals with tools to influence public policy and counter perceived threats to these values, operating from an explicitly Christian anthropological perspective that views human dignity as the basis for all rights.[2] The core principles of the CitizenGO Foundation articulate specific rights derived from human dignity, including the right to life from conception to natural death, bodily integrity and health, respect for individual conscience, freedom of education, religious liberty exercised publicly and privately, marriage defined between a man and a woman, and the right to participate in public life. These principles also encompass freedoms of information, assembly, economic initiative, private property, and protection under the law, underscoring a commitment to subsidiarityâ€”where decisions are made at the most local level possibleâ€”and solidarity among citizens.[2] Objectives include fostering ethical governance and the common good by organizing campaigns that encourage societal participation in politics, protecting the family as the fundamental unit of society, and advancing policies aligned with these rights against ideologies or laws seen as infringing upon them, such as those promoting abortion, gender ideology, or restrictions on religious expression. CitizenGO aims to achieve these through innovation in civic engagement, such as digital petitions and global coordination, to ensure accountability from institutions and promote a culture of responsibility and freedom.[2][26]

Advocacy Methods and Tools

CitizenGO primarily utilizes digital platforms to facilitate grassroots advocacy, with its core tool being an online petition system that enables users to create, sign, and direct campaigns toward policymakers at local, national, and international levels.[1] Supporters are encouraged to launch petitions by providing details on objectives, targets, and rationales, followed by account creation and promotion via integrated sharing features.[27] This platform emphasizes rapid mobilization, allowing petitions to be disseminated through social media networks to exploit viral dissemination dynamics for broader engagement.[28] Complementing online efforts, the organization coordinates offline actions such as public protests, rallies, and in-person signature collections to pressure authorities and raise visibility. For example, in November 2019, CitizenGO mobilized supporters in Orlando, Florida, to present over 500,000 signatures opposing LGBT-themed content in Disney programming and parks. These hybrid approaches integrate digital signatures with physical demonstrations to amplify impact, as seen in campaigns targeting educational curricula and corporate policies perceived as undermining traditional family values.[12] Beyond petitions and events, CitizenGO employs email alerts, donor appeals, and multimedia contentâ€”like videos and infographicsâ€”to sustain member involvement and influence public discourse.[23] The group also engages in targeted lobbying of legislators and international bodies, delivering petition volumes alongside direct communications to advocate for policy changes on issues like abortion restrictions and gender ideology in schools.[8] This multi-channel strategy, operational since the organization's founding in 2013, has enabled campaigns to garner millions of signatures globally, though critics from human rights organizations argue it often relies on emotive framing to counter progressive agendas.[12]

Policy Areas and Campaigns

Protection of Life and Dignity

CitizenGO advocates for the protection of human life from conception to natural death, emphasizing the inherent dignity of every person regardless of age, ability, or health status. This stance is rooted in their foundational principles, which assert a right to life and its preservation throughout all stages of existence.[2] The organization frames its efforts as defending vulnerable populations against practices like abortion and euthanasia, which they argue undermine societal protections and lead to the devaluation of life.[29] In anti-abortion campaigns, CitizenGO has mobilized petitions and statements rejecting unrestricted access, particularly opposing measures that would permit abortion up to birth. For instance, in September 2025, they launched a "Statement for Life" initiative in the UK, urging lawmakers to resist decriminalization efforts and affirm equal dignity for all lives, including the unborn.[30] They have also critiqued high abortion rates in Europe, citing figures of approximately 4.3 million annual procedures as evidence of a demographic and moral crisis.[31] Regarding euthanasia and assisted suicide, CitizenGO conducts international campaigns warning of a "slippery slope" where initial safeguards erode, leading to broader applications, including for minors or the disabled. In August 2021, their African branch initiated opposition to legalization in South Africa, arguing it would expose vulnerable groups to exploitation and open a "Pandora's box" of expanded practices.[32] Similar efforts include a European petition against euthanasia for minors, stressing that life must be valued irrespective of disability, and protests in the UK against the Falconer Bill in July 2024, highlighting expansions in countries like the Netherlands since 2001.[33] In Spain, during the COVID-19 pandemic, CitizenGO participated in civic mobilizations against the 2021 assisted dying law, collaborating with religious and political allies to promote palliative care alternatives over what they describe as state-sanctioned death. These initiatives often involve collecting signatures, lobbying politicians, and public demonstrations to prioritize life-affirming policies, such as improved hospice care, over lethal options. CitizenGO contends that true dignity requires rejecting euthanasia as a solution to suffering, advocating instead for comprehensive support systems that respect natural endings.[34]

Family, Gender, and Education

CitizenGO promotes the defense of traditional family structures grounded in biological sex differences, viewing the complementarity of men and women as essential for child development and societal stability.[35] The organization opposes initiatives that redefine family norms to include non-biological arrangements, arguing such changes erode parental authority and cultural foundations.[36] In the realm of gender issues, CitizenGO campaigns against what it describes as gender ideology, which posits that individuals may be born in the "wrong body" or that gender is fluid and detached from biology.[37] The group has mobilized petitions to halt the promotion of transgender identities in media and education, including a 2019 effort collecting over 500,000 signatures delivered to Disney headquarters in Orlando, Florida, protesting the inclusion of LGBT themes in children's content as indoctrination.[38] [39] Regarding education, CitizenGO focuses on safeguarding children from curricula incorporating gender ideology and comprehensive sexuality education (CSE), which it criticizes for introducing explicit content and normalizing minority sexual identities at young ages.[40] Notable initiatives include a 2025 petition in Victoria, Australia, demanding an end to school teachings on gender fluidity that undermine family roles, and opposition to transgender indoctrination programs in UK primary schools like those in Norfolk, garnering over 22,500 supporters.[37] [41] Internationally, the organization has targeted UN efforts to mandate CSE, framing it as an imposition of immoral ideologies on families.[40] These actions emphasize parental rights as primary gatekeepers over state or activist-driven education on sex, gender, and relationships.[37]

Religious Liberty and Cultural Preservation

CitizenGO advocates for religious liberty as a core principle, emphasizing the right to practice faith publicly and privately without interference from secular policies or international bodies that prioritize other ideologies. In its foundational principles, the organization asserts a "right to religious freedom and to honor God both privately and publically, individually and collectively, according to one's own conscience," positioning this as essential to human dignity alongside protections for life and family.[2] This stance drives campaigns against perceived encroachments, such as efforts to redefine persecution under international law to include sexual orientation and gender identity, which CitizenGO argues undermine free speech and Christian expression by equating traditional beliefs with discrimination.[42] The group has mobilized petitions and advocacy for specific cases of religious persecution. In Spain, it petitioned Pope Francis in July 2024 to intervene against government plans to demolish a 150-meter cross at the Valley of the Fallenâ€”a World Heritage site and Benedictine monasteryâ€”arguing the move secularizes a Christian landmark and rebrands it as a fascist symbol, displacing monks and eroding national religious history.[48][49] In the UK, the organization rallied against Glasgow City Council's 2023 proposal to remove historic statues during redevelopment, decrying it as cancel culture that risks permanent loss of cultural monuments tied to traditional values.[50] Quebec campaigns opposed constitutional changes denying the province's Christian heritage, linking preservation to broader defenses of faith against ideological overhauls.[51] These actions align with CitizenGO's promotion of the 2025 Westminster Declaration, which upholds religious manifestation in public life as protected under human rights law.[52]
CitizenGO frames cultural preservation as resisting media and policy shifts that undermine traditional family norms rooted in Judeo-Christian ethics, exemplified by a 2019 Orlando campaign collecting over 500,000 signatures to protest Disney's inclusion of LGBT themes in children's programming, which the group argued promotes ideologies conflicting with parental rights and cultural standards.[14]

Global Health and Sovereignty Issues

CitizenGO has campaigned against international health agreements perceived as eroding national sovereignty, particularly targeting the World Health Organization's (WHO) proposed Pandemic Agreement and amendments to the International Health Regulations (IHR). The organization argues that these instruments would empower unelected WHO officials to impose binding measures such as lockdowns, vaccine mandates, and travel restrictions, overriding domestic decision-making processes.[53] In petitions launched since 2023, CitizenGO mobilized supporters to urge national delegates to reject ratification, citing risks of a "global health dictatorship" that could replicate COVID-19-era restrictions like church closures and speech censorship.[54] A flagship effort was the "WHO Pandemic Treaty Must Be Stopped" petition, which amassed over 2.5 million signatures by early 2025, directed at WHO representatives and national health ministers to block the accord before its anticipated adoption.[55] Despite the Pandemic Agreement's adoption on May 20, 2025, CitizenGO shifted focus to opposing IHR amendments adopted in parallel, which it claims expand WHO authority to declare emergencies and enforce compliance without sovereign consent.[56][57] The group deployed campaigners to Geneva for events like the 60th Human Rights Council session in October 2025, advocating for withdrawal from these frameworks to preserve policy autonomy.[58] In Africa, CitizenGO's regional affiliate gathered nearly 400,000 signatures by May 2025 on a petition urging votes against the draft Pandemic Agreement, emphasizing threats to local governance and equitable resource distribution amid globalist overreach.[59] Broader advocacy included calls to send figures like former Australian parliamentarian George Christensen to WHO negotiations, framing the treaty as a "UN power grab" that prioritizes centralized control over evidence-based national responses.[60][56] These efforts align with CitizenGO's emphasis on sovereignty as a bulwark against supranational mandates lacking democratic accountability, though critics contend such opposition overlooks cooperative benefits in pandemic preparedness.[61]

Impact and Achievements

Policy Influences and Victories

CitizenGO's campaigns have contributed to the blockage of several proposed policies advancing sexual and reproductive rights agendas at supranational levels. In December 2013, the organization mobilized over 1.5 million signatures in opposition to the Estrela Report in the European Parliament, which sought to mandate comprehensive sexual education in schools, ensure access to safe abortion, and promote reproductive rights across EU member states; the report was ultimately defeated in a close plenary vote (334 against, 327 for, with 41 abstentions), marking a significant setback for proponents.[62][63] This outcome was attributed in part to grassroots pressure from groups like CitizenGO, which framed the report as an overreach imposing ideological mandates on national sovereignty.[64] In East Africa, CitizenGO's advocacy influenced the stalling of the East African Legislative Assembly's Sexual and Reproductive Health Bill, which aimed to expand abortion access and sexual education; the bill was blocked amid opposition campaigns, with networks including CitizenGO claiming credit for rallying regional conservatives against perceived threats to family structures.[23][65] Similarly, in Kenya, efforts by affiliated groups contributed to the rejection of legislative expansions on abortion services, preserving stricter national limits as of 2022.[66] At the United Nations, CitizenGO has supported resolutions qualifying "reproductive rights" to exclude explicit abortion endorsements and emphasizing parental roles in education, influencing language in documents from the Commission on the Status of Women to avoid enshrining contested agendas.[67] These interventions, often through petitions exceeding hundreds of thousands of signatures, have sustained pushback in multilateral forums, though direct causation remains debated amid broader coalitions.[68]

Scale of Engagement and Reach

CitizenGO maintains a self-reported community of approximately 19 million active citizens engaged in its advocacy efforts globally.[14] This figure encompasses individuals who have participated in petitions, alerts, and campaigns, reflecting the platform's emphasis on online mobilization. Academic analyses corroborate a supporter base exceeding 18 million members as of recent assessments.[69] The organization has facilitated over 141 million signatures on petitions it has initiated, significantly outpacing those from external initiators on its platform.[24] These campaigns span hundreds of initiatives targeting issues such as life protection and family policies, with notable examples including a 2019 petition garnering over 500,000 signatures against perceived LGBT promotion in Disney content.[23] Engagement extends beyond digital signatures to offline actions, including coordinated protests and lobbying efforts.[12] CitizenGO operates in more than 50 countries, with headquarters in Madrid, Spain, and distributed teams across 15 cities on three continents, enabling localized campaigns in Europe, the Americas, Africa, and beyond.[70] [1] Its staff comprises 51 to 200 employees, supporting a lean structure focused on digital tools for transnational coordination.[71] While social media presence is modestâ€”such as 5,400 Instagram followersâ€”the primary channels for reach remain email alerts, petition platforms, and partnerships with aligned groups, driving virality through shared content and grassroots amplification.[70][24]

Controversies and Criticisms

Accusations of Misinformation and Bias

CitizenGO has faced accusations from progressive nongovernmental organizations and media outlets of spreading misinformation, particularly in campaigns opposing reproductive health policies and gender-related education. These claims often frame the group's advocacy as deliberate distortion rather than ideological opposition, though critics' sources frequently exhibit left-leaning biases in their selection of "misinformation," prioritizing alignment with establishment views on sexual and reproductive rights.[72][23] A prominent example involves a 2022 Mozilla Foundation report alleging that CitizenGO coordinated disinformation efforts in Kenya between 2020 and 2021 to influence debates on the Reproductive Healthcare Bill and Surrogacy Bill. The report claims CitizenGO paid local influencers approximately $10â€“$15 per campaign via WhatsApp groups to post synchronized tweets using identical hashtags such as #stopsurrogacybillKE and #Notoabortionincovid19, amplifying over 20,000 messages analyzed via Twitter's API. Specific misinformation cited includes assertions that surrogacy leads to behavioral and emotional issues in childrenâ€”described as debunked by the African Population and Health Research Centerâ€”and portrayals of the bills as covertly promoting widespread abortion. Evidence includes screenshots of branded WhatsApp coordination and subsequent Twitter suspensions of over 240 accounts for policy violations; however, CitizenGO did not respond to Mozilla's inquiries.[72] Similar charges appear in investigations by the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, which in 2022 accused CitizenGO and affiliated networks of disseminating false narratives globally, such as equating abortion with murder, depicting sexual health education as encouraging child sexuality, and labeling LGBTQ+ rights initiatives as Western colonial impositions. These efforts reportedly involved petitions garnering 150,000 signatures against organizations like Marie Stopes International and targeted online harassment of activists. The Bureau, drawing on data from Signify and Mozilla, portrays such tactics as part of a broader strategy of intimidation, though the claims' factual basis relies on interpretive framing of moral arguments as disinformation.[23] On bias, media rating site Media Bias/Fact Check in its assessment labels CitizenGO as far-right biased and questionable, citing promotion of Christian-right propaganda, pseudoscience, conspiracy theories, and poor sourcing, with low factual reporting due to alleged failed fact checks. Examples include a 2022 claim that State Farm was "pushing gender ideology on 5-year-olds" via partnership with GenderCoolâ€”a program featuring transgender children's storiesâ€”which was contested by Snopes as exaggerated, though the partnership itself was confirmed and later ended by State Farm amid backlash. The site also references Kenya reproductive disinformation and anti-LGBTQ+ campaigns, while noting third-party designations like the Southern Poverty Law Center's hate group label, an organization criticized for expansive criteria applied disproportionately to conservative entities. CitizenGO maintains its content reflects evidence-based concerns over family structures and child protection, rejecting accusations as ideologically motivated smears.[73][74]

Alliances and Political Oppositions

CitizenGO has established alliances with conservative and religious advocacy organizations across Europe and the United States, including collaborations with the Polish legal group Ordo Iuris on pro-family initiatives.[75] It also maintains ties to the Leadership Institute, a U.S.-based training organization for conservative activists, whose alumni have included high-profile Republican figures.[76] In Spain, CitizenGO shares ideological alignment and operational links with the Vox party, a right-wing political group, including funding support for campaigns that echo Vox's opposition to gender ideology and abortion expansion; Vox leaders have promoted CitizenGO petitions, such as those against Disney's inclusion of LGBT content in 2019, which gathered over 500,000 signatures.[77][65][13] These partnerships extend to broader networks involving U.S. Christian right groups, European Catholic militants, and elements of the Russian Orthodox sphere, as documented in investigative reports that highlight coordinated efforts against reproductive rights and gender policies.[23][23] However, CitizenGO publicly asserts independence from political parties and governments, positioning itself as a grassroots platform driven by citizen volunteers rather than partisan entities.[1][2] Politically, CitizenGO faces opposition from leftist parties, progressive NGOs, and human rights advocates who view its campaigns as threats to sexual and reproductive health rights (SRHR). In Spain, it has clashed with socialist-led governments over proposed trans-rights laws, launching petitions in 2021 that mobilized against legislation allowing self-identified gender changes without medical oversight.[78][78] Internationally, groups like Amnesty International and local LGBT organizations in countries such as Kenya and Poland have criticized CitizenGO for exporting conservative agendas, including opposition to abortion access and gender education, often framing its activities as disinformation-driven.[66][65] Such critiques frequently emanate from outlets and institutions with progressive leanings, including OpenDemocracy and The Guardian, which have portrayed CitizenGO's growth since 2013 as alarming to global human rights norms, though these sources rarely engage with the empirical bases of CitizenGO's claims, such as demographic data on family structures or child protection outcomes.[12][79] In the European Union, CitizenGO's advocacy has encountered resistance during SRHR policy debates, where leftist MEPs and allied NGOs have sought to counter its petitions and lobbying as undue influence from transnational conservative networks.[80] Despite this, CitizenGO's model of mass online mobilization has sustained its influence against these oppositions, evidenced by policy delays or reversals in targeted regions.[12]
