Lesser blind mole-rat

The lesser blind mole-rat (Nannospalax leucodon) is a species of rodent in the family Spalacidae. It is a superspecies comprising multiple chromosomal forms.[2] It is found in Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Bulgaria, Croatia, Greece, Hungary, North Macedonia, Moldova, Romania, Serbia, Montenegro, Turkey and Ukraine.[1]

Taxonomy

Prior to 2012, it was classified in the genus Spalax, but modern authors tend to separate this and some closely related mole rat species into a separate genus named Nannospalax. Studies of karyotypes have shown that Nannospalax leucodon is a superspecies consisting of several cryptic species distinguished by their chromosomal configurations, such as different diploid numbers (2n ranging from 36 to 62 across forms). According to karyological studies, there are five chromosomal forms in the Carpathian Basin (2n = 36, 38, 54, 56, 58), which face threats from habitat loss and fragmentation, though detailed conservation assessments for individual forms are limited. [3] Across its range, up to 25 chromosomal forms have been identified as of 2024, with ongoing taxonomic revisions recognizing some as distinct species. [4]

Behavior and ecology

The lesser blind mole-rat is not completely blind; it possesses simple eyes 1 mm in diameter located under a layer of skin and fur. They act effectively as light meters, sensing only the level of available light.[5]

Life expectancy

The lesser blind mole-rat has a lifespan of about 20 years.[4] One study directed towards life expectancy used this species by virtue of its incredible longevity performance, hypoxia and hypercapnia endurance, as well as cancer resistance. Looking at the fecal and skin samples of this ideal candidate, it was found that the Muribaculaceae bacterial family, known to be linked with longevity, dominated fecal samples.[6]
