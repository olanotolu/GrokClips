Montana -class battleship

The Montana-class battleships were a class of five planned United States Navy battleships, designated hull numbers BB-67 through BB-71 and named Montana, Ohio, Maine, New Hampshire, and Louisiana, intended to represent the pinnacle of battleship design with superior firepower and armor protection.[1] Authorized as part of the 1940 Two-Ocean Navy Act and funded in 1941 for integration into a projected postwar fleet of seventeen battleships, they featured a standard displacement of 60,500 tons and full load of 70,965 tons, a length of 921 feet 3 inches, and a beam of 121 feet 2 inches.[1] Their armament included twelve 16-inch/50 caliber guns in four triple turrets for main battery fire, supplemented by twenty 5-inch/54 caliber dual-purpose guns in ten twin mountings for anti-aircraft and secondary roles.[1] Designed for a top speed of 28 knots powered by 172,000 shaft horsepower steam turbines, the class emphasized heavy armor adequate against shellfire from its own-caliber guns and enhanced protection against underwater attacks, trading the speed of predecessors like the Iowa class for greater endurance in surface engagements.[1] Despite these advancements, no keels were laid down; construction was suspended in May 1942 and canceled in July 1943 amid World War II's demands for aircraft carriers, amphibious ships, and anti-submarine vessels, marking the effective end of battleship primacy in naval strategy.[1]

Historical Context

Pre-World War II Naval Treaties and Limitations

The Washington Naval Treaty, signed on February 6, 1922, by the United States, United Kingdom, Japan, France, and Italy, imposed strict limitations on capital ship construction to prevent an arms race, capping individual battleship standard displacement at 35,000 long tons and main gun caliber at 16 inches, while establishing a 5:5:3 tonnage ratio favoring the US and UK over Japan.[2][3] These constraints compelled the US Navy to design replacement battleships, such as the unrealized South Dakota class of 1920, within the 35,000-ton limit, prioritizing balanced firepower, speed, and protection over maximal size, which empirically curtailed potential advancements in armor thickness and gun numbers compared to unrestricted designs.[2] The London Naval Treaty of April 22, 1930, extended these restrictions through 1936, reaffirming the 35,000-ton displacement ceiling for new battleships while permitting limited replacements for aging vessels, thus shaping the North Carolina classâ€”laid down in 1937 at approximately 35,000 tons standardâ€”as the US Navy's first new construction under the regime, with nine 14-inch guns in triple turrets to comply with caliber allowances.[4] The subsequent South Dakota class (1939), also adhering to the 35,000-ton limit despite innovative "clipper bow" hulls for improved stability, reflected ongoing treaty-driven compromises that sacrificed beam width for longitudinal strength, limiting underwater protection against torpedoes and mines.[4] Japan's denunciation of the treaties in December 1934, with withdrawal effective December 31, 1936, after refusing to sign the Second London Naval Treaty, nullified the international framework and triggered an escalatory cycle, freeing the US to pursue larger vessels like the Iowa class, authorized in 1940 at over 45,000 tons standard, as a baseline for unbound "super-battleship" concepts.[5] Concurrent US naval intelligence assessments in the late 1930s, drawing on shipyard sightings and design analyses, indicated Japan was constructing battleships exceeding 40,000 tonsâ€”potentially up to 60,000 tons by 1940 estimatesâ€”prompting requirements for American designs with superior 16-inch batteries and all-or-nothing armor schemes unhindered by tonnage caps to ensure decisive edge in fleet actions.[6]

Lessons from Early War Experiences and Design Imperatives

The attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, by Japanese carrier-based aircraft inflicted severe damage on the U.S. Pacific Fleet's battleships, with bombs penetrating decks and torpedoes exploiting underwater vulnerabilities, sinking four battleships including the USS Arizona and USS Oklahoma. This empirical demonstration of battleship susceptibility to aerial bombardment and shallow-water torpedo hits underscored the causal imperative for enhanced deck armor to resist plunging shells and bombsâ€”thicker than on preceding classesâ€”and bolstered torpedo bulges capable of absorbing multiple underwater explosions without catastrophic flooding. While some battleships like USS Nevada attempted to sortie and survived, the losses affirmed that gun-armed surface actions remained viable for fleet engagements, necessitating designs prioritizing armored resilience over speed alone to counter potential enemy battleship threats observed in Japanese capabilities. Subsequent British experiences reinforced these imperatives; on December 10, 1941, HMS Prince of Wales and HMS Repulse were sunk off Malaya by Japanese land-based bombers and torpedo planes, lacking adequate air cover and anti-aircraft defenses, which allowed coordinated strikes to overwhelm the ships despite evasive maneuvers. The rapidity of the sinkingâ€”Prince of Wales succumbing after six torpedo hits and multiple bomb near-missesâ€”highlighted systemic deficiencies in anti-aircraft armament density and radar-directed fire control, prompting U.S. naval planners to mandate extensive batteries of rapid-fire guns, including 5-inch dual-purpose mounts integrated with proximity-fused ammunition, to saturate approaching aircraft and mitigate swarm tactics empirically validated as lethal. Torpedo protection systems, informed by these underwater threats, evolved to incorporate layered voids and liquid-filled compartments for better absorption, addressing the causal chain from hull breach to progressive flooding observed in both Pacific and Asian theater losses. Early carrier-centric battles, such as the Coral Sea (May 4â€“8, 1942) and Midway (June 4â€“7, 1942), established air power's primacy in decisive fleet engagements, where opposing carriers were neutralized without battleship involvement, yet revealed air-delivered ordnance's limitations for precision shore bombardment during amphibious operations. Carrier aircraft proved insufficient for sustained, heavy-caliber fire support against fortified positions, as evidenced by the need for surface gunfire in subsequent island campaigns, thus preserving the design rationale for battleships with twelve 16-inch guns to deliver overwhelming volume against land targets while enhanced armor and anti-aircraft suites countered the hybrid threats of air attack and enemy capital ships. These experiences collectively drove imperatives for Montana-class vessels to embody balanced protection against observed vulnerabilities, prioritizing empirical survivability over treaty-constrained predecessors.[7]

Design Evolution

Early Conceptualization and Iowa-Class Foundations

The Montana-class battleships, designated BB-67 through BB-71, were authorized under the Two-Ocean Navy Act signed into law on July 19, 1940, as part of a massive naval expansion program that funded five such vessels in fiscal year 1941 to serve as the pinnacle of U.S. capital ship design after the Iowa class.[1][8] These ships were envisioned without the displacement and armament restrictions of interwar treaties like the Washington and London Naval Treaties, allowing for a first-principles approach to scaling up proven elements for enhanced firepower, protection, and endurance in surface actions.[1] Design conceptualization built directly on the Iowa-class (BB-61) hull form, which had been optimized for high speed and treaty compliance, by extending the waterline length to approximately 890 feet and overall length to 921 feet while widening the beam to 121 feet to improve stability, internal volume for machinery and magazines, and underwater protection against torpedoes and mines.[9] This enlargement addressed limitations in the Iowa's compact layout, such as constrained turret spacing and armor distribution, enabling a larger powerplant and deeper draft without sacrificing the fast battleship lineage's hydrodynamic efficiency.[1] In early 1941 design studies, the primary armament was settled on twelve 16-inch/50-caliber Mark 7 guns in four triple turrets, eschewing larger calibers like 18-inch weapons despite preliminary sketches considering them, primarily to leverage existing production lines, ammunition logistics, and fire control systems already proven in the Iowa class for rapid wartime scalability.[7] This choice prioritized volume of fire and shell compatibilityâ€”using the same 2,700-pound super-heavy projectilesâ€”over marginal increases in individual gun power, reflecting a causal emphasis on practical fleet integration amid accelerating mobilization.[7]

Wartime Refinements and Final Specifications

During 1941 and 1942, the U.S. Bureau of Ships conducted iterative studies to refine the Montana-class design in response to emerging wartime requirements, emphasizing enhanced fire control and anti-aircraft capabilities amid growing threats from air and submarine attacks. These efforts integrated advanced radar-directed fire control systems, building on proven Mark 8 and Mark 13 radars already deployed on earlier battleships, to improve gunnery accuracy in adverse conditions. The secondary battery was expanded to twenty 5-inch/54 caliber Mark 16 dual-purpose guns in ten twin mountings, providing robust anti-aircraft and surface fire support while leveraging automated loading for sustained rates of fire.[1][8] Engineering trade-offs prioritized underwater protection, leading to a widened beam of 121 feet 2 inchesâ€”compared to the Iowa-class's 108 feet 2 inchesâ€”to accommodate deeper internal subdivision and liquid-loaded compartments against torpedo damage, which in turn drove standard displacement to 60,500 tons and full load to 70,965 tons. This increase reflected causal constraints of scaling hull form for survivability without compromising stability, though it rendered the ships incompatible with Panama Canal locks and extended construction timelines.[1] The design was finalized in April 1942 as BB-67 Scheme 4, rejecting proposals for eighteen-inch guns in favor of twelve sixteen-inch/50 caliber Mark 7 guns due to the former's developmental delays, unproven turret designs, and the imperative to utilize existing production lines for faster wartime output. This choice balanced raw firepower against practical build feasibility, as larger guns would have exacerbated weight penalties and delayed commissioning beyond 1945, amid resource strains from carrier and escort priorities.[8][10]

Technical Specifications

Hull, Dimensions, and General Characteristics

The Montana-class battleships featured a hull design measuring 921 feet 3 inches in length overall, with a maximum beam of 121 feet 2 inches and a designed draft of 36 feet 6 inches at deep load.[1] This configuration provided greater internal volume compared to the preceding Iowa class, prioritizing stability, buoyancy, and space for enhanced protection and armament over canal transit compatibility, as the beam exceeded Panama Canal locks by approximately 6 feet. Standard displacement was specified at 60,500 long tons, increasing to 70,965 long tons at full load, reflecting the class's emphasis on mass for structural integrity and combat loading.[1] Propulsion arrangements were planned to deliver 172,000 shaft horsepower, yielding a maximum speed of 28 knotsâ€”deliberately slower than the Iowa class's 33 knots to allocate weight to other capabilities rather than high-speed escort duties. Crew requirements were projected at 2,355 officers and enlisted personnel for standard operations, potentially expanding to 2,789 when configured as a fleet flagship with additional aviation squadron staff.[11] Aviation provisions included two hangars and three catapults aft and amidships, enabling the operation of four to six floatplanes primarily for reconnaissance, gunnery spotting, and over-the-horizon search in surface action contexts.[1]

Propulsion and Performance

The propulsion system for the Montana-class battleships comprised eight Babcock & Wilcox two-drum express-type water-tube boilers feeding steam to four Westinghouse geared steam turbines, producing a total of 172,000 shaft horsepower across four shafts.[8][12] This configuration was selected to balance power output with the class's increased displacement, prioritizing reliability and efficiency over the higher ratings of preceding fast battleships.[1] The designed maximum speed was 28 knots, reflecting a deliberate trade-off in the engineering layout: the Montana-class's greater massâ€”approximately 70,000 tons full loadâ€”necessitated a lower power-to-displacement ratio compared to the Iowa-class's 212,000 shp for similar tonnage scaled to faster performance.[1][13] This slower top speed stemmed from the emphasis on heavier armor, armament, and hull volume, which increased drag and required optimized turbine efficiency to achieve sustainable cruising without excessive fuel demands. Endurance was projected at 15,000 nautical miles when operating at 15 knots, supported by substantial oil bunkers suited for extended transoceanic deployments or blockade enforcement.[14][13] The broader beam contributed to inherent stability under power, minimizing pitch and roll to maintain consistent turbine loading and propeller efficiency in variable sea states, thereby enhancing overall operational sustainability relative to narrower, higher-speed designs.[8]

Primary and Secondary Armament

The primary armament of the Montana-class battleships consisted of twelve 16-inch (406 mm)/50 caliber Mark 7 guns, arranged in four triple turrets positioned in two superfiring pairs forward and two aft.[1][15] These guns, identical to those fitted on the Iowa-class battleships, featured a barrel length of 50 calibers (66 feet, 10 inches or 20.3 meters) and were capable of firing armor-piercing projectiles weighing up to 2,700 pounds (1,225 kg) at a muzzle velocity of 2,500 feet per second (762 m/s).[15] The maximum range with super-heavy shells reached approximately 24 miles (38.7 km) at a 45-degree elevation.[15] Each turret weighed about 1,700 tons and could elevate from -5 to +45 degrees, with a rate of fire of two rounds per minute per gun under optimal conditions.[15] The secondary battery comprised twenty 5-inch (127 mm)/54 caliber Mark 16 dual-purpose guns mounted in ten twin turrets, with five turrets arrayed along each side of the superstructure for balanced fire coverage.[1] These guns, an evolution from the 5-inch/38 caliber weapons on earlier classes, offered improved range and anti-aircraft performance, with a muzzle velocity of around 2,500 feet per second (762 m/s) and effective firing against both surface and aerial targets up to 26,000 yards (23.8 km).[1] Designed for versatility in fleet actions, the secondary armament supported the primary guns by engaging destroyers, cruisers, and aircraft.[1] Light anti-aircraft defenses included provisions for numerous 40 mm Bofors automatic cannons and 20 mm Oerlikon guns, intended to form a layered screen against air attacks, though final configurations were not finalized due to cancellation.[1] Ammunition storage featured deep magazines beneath the armored deck, enabling sustained fire from the main battery during extended engagements, with the larger hull allowing greater capacity than the Iowa class.[1] This arrangement prioritized firepower endurance against peer capital ships.[1]

Armor Scheme and Defensive Features

The Montana-class battleships incorporated an all-or-nothing armor philosophy, concentrating maximum protection on the armored citadel encompassing magazines, propulsion machinery, and steering gear to maintain combat effectiveness against high-velocity shellfire and plunging projectiles, while expending minimal steel on extremities. This scheme evolved from the Iowa-class design by leveraging the Montana's greater beam to extend the citadel's coverage and deepen protective layers, informed by empirical data from World War II engagements revealing vulnerabilities in partial armor schemes.[16] The vertical belt armor measured 16.1 inches (409 mm) thick, inclined at 19 degrees for enhanced oblique impact resistance, and was layered over 1 inch (25 mm) of special treatment steel (STS) to form the primary side protection against surface gunfire up to 30,000 yards.[16] [17] Deck armor over critical spaces varied from 7.05 to 7.35 inches, comprising 5.8 inches of Class B homogeneous plate atop 1.25 inches of STS, calibrated via ballistic tests to defeat 16-inch/50-caliber super-heavy shells at ranges exceeding 25,000 yards.[16] Turret faces received 18 inches of armor, with supporting barbettes and bulkheads up to 18 inches thick, providing resilience against direct hits from equivalent-caliber weapons.[12] [18] Underwater protection emphasized a multi-layered torpedo defense system with 20.5 feet (6.25 m) depth, incorporating void-filled bulges, liquid-loading compartments, and holding bulkheads to dissipate explosive energy from 1,000-pound warheads, surpassing the Iowa-class's 17.9-foot system and addressing weaknesses identified in 1939 caisson trials on South Dakota-class designs.[16] This configuration aimed to limit structural flooding and maintain stability, with the broader hull permitting incremental improvements in compartmentalization without compromising internal volume.[19] Defensive features extended to anti-aircraft integration, featuring radar-equipped directors and proximity-fused ammunition handling to counter dive-bomber tactics observed in early war actions like the attacks on Prince of Wales and Repulse, enabling rapid target acquisition and layered fire from dual-purpose batteries.[1] Splinter protection via lighter STS plating over non-vital areas further mitigated fragmentation risks from near-misses, balancing weight savings with empirical resilience data from fleet reports.[16]

Construction Plans and Cancellation

Authorized Vessels and Shipyard Allocations

The U.S. Congress authorized five Montana-class battleships under expansions to the naval construction program initiated by the Two-Ocean Navy Act of July 1940, with hull numbers BB-67 through BB-71 assigned to reflect their place after the Iowa-class vessels. These ships were named USS Montana, Ohio, Maine, New Hampshire, and Louisiana, honoring states with prior naval naming traditions. Shipyard allocations prioritized East Coast naval facilities equipped for large-scale capital ship construction, drawing on infrastructure investments from pre-war mobilization that enhanced drydock capacities for vessels exceeding 60,000 tons displacement.[1] Construction planning distributed the workload across three primary yards to optimize industrial capacity: Philadelphia Navy Yard for the lead ships, New York Navy Yard for mid-sequence vessels, and Norfolk Navy Yard for the final unit. Projected completion dates spanned from 1945 for the earliest hulls to 1949 for later ones, accounting for sequential laying down starting in 1942â€“1943 and the extended build times required for their massive scale. Each vessel was estimated to cost around $100 million, comparable to contemporary Iowa-class outlays and underscoring the commitment to battleship production despite shifting priorities in carrier and escort vessel output after Pearl Harbor.[1][8]

Strategic Prioritization and Official Cancellation

The Montana-class battleships were suspended in May 1942 amid escalating wartime demands that shifted naval production priorities away from large surface combatants.[20] This decision followed early Pacific Theater engagements, such as the Battle of the Coral Sea and Battle of Midway in 1942, which empirically demonstrated the decisive role of aircraft carriers in fleet actions over battleship-centric formations.[1] Concurrently, the U.S. faced acute shortages of amphibious assault vessels essential for the island-hopping strategy in the Pacific and anti-submarine warfare escorts to counter German U-boat campaigns in the Atlantic, both requiring immediate steel and yard capacity reallocations.[14] Formal cancellation occurred on July 21, 1943, directed by Secretary of the Navy Frank Knox due to persistent resource constraints and the proven efficacy of carrier operations in securing naval superiority.[21] At that point, no keels had been laid for any of the five authorized vessels, allowing full redirection of materials and labor to higher-priority programs.[1] This prioritization enabled the completion of 24 Essex-class carriers (with 17 entering service as fleet carriers by war's end), which provided the air projection critical to victories in subsequent campaigns like Leyte Gulf and Okinawa, versus zero Montana-class ships.[22]

Strategic Role and Debates

Intended Operational Doctrine

The Montana-class battleships were envisioned by the U.S. Navy prior to their 1943 cancellation to form the core of a fast battleship division capable of engaging and defeating enemy capital ships in decisive surface actions, reflecting the service's adherence to a modified Mahanian strategy emphasizing fleet-versus-fleet confrontations within a carrier-centric task force structure.[23] This role prioritized countering heavy adversaries like Japan's Yamato-class, with the Montanas' twelve 16-inch guns and enhanced armor scheme designed to dominate prolonged gunnery exchanges.[1] U.S. Navy analysts projected superiority in such duels through superior fire control radars, which enabled accurate ranging at night or in poor visibility, combined with the ballistic qualities of American super-heavy shells that offered better penetration and damage potential against armored targets compared to Japanese equivalents.[24] In amphibious operations, the class was slated to deliver sustained naval gunfire support for landings, particularly in scenarios like the planned Operation Olympic invasion of Kyushu in late 1945, where established airfields might be absent or insufficient, necessitating battleship barrages to suppress coastal defenses and inland fortifications. Their broad beam and deep draft accommodated extensive ammunition storage for prolonged bombardment, enabling thousands of heavy-caliber rounds to be fired over days or weeks without resupply vulnerabilities inherent to carrier-based strikes.[25] As complements to aircraft carriers, the Montanas were intended to provide an anti-surface and layered anti-aircraft umbrella, screening fast carrier task forces from enemy battleship raids while contributing to air defense through dense secondary and tertiary batteries, thereby allowing carriers to focus on offensive strikes without diverting fighters for fleet protection.[19] This integration stemmed from empirical lessons of Pacific campaigns, where battleships like the Iowa-class had proven effective in both escorting carriers against surface threats and augmenting air power in contested waters lacking forward bases.

Arguments for Obsolescence and Carrier Shift

The Battle of Midway on June 4â€“7, 1942, exemplified the shift toward carrier dominance, where U.S. carrier-based aircraft sank four Japanese carriers, decisively altering the Pacific War's trajectory without significant battleship involvement.[26] Subsequent engagements, including the Battle of the Philippine Sea in June 1944 and the Battle of Leyte Gulf in October 1944, further relegated battleships to supportive roles such as shore bombardment, as carrier air wings determined outcomes at ranges beyond gun capabilities. No major battleship-versus-battleship duels occurred after the Battle of the Denmark Strait in May 1941, underscoring the absence of fleet actions centered on capital ship gunnery.[27] Battleships proved highly vulnerable to aerial attack, as demonstrated by the sinkings of Bismarck and Yamato. The German battleship Bismarck was immobilized on May 26, 1941, by torpedo hits from British carrier-based Swordfish aircraft, enabling surface ships to finish it the next day despite its armor.[28] Similarly, the Japanese super-battleship Yamato, the largest ever built, was sunk on April 7, 1945, off Okinawa by approximately 386 U.S. carrier aircraft delivering bombs and torpedoes, with no enemy surface ships in range; it succumbed after 12 bomb and seven torpedo hits in two hours.[29] These losses highlighted how aircraft could exploit battleships' limited anti-air defenses and inflict catastrophic underwater damage, rendering gun duels irrelevant in practice. The resource demands of battleship construction further argued for obsolescence amid total war priorities. The Montana-class was estimated to require 5â€“7 years from keel-laying to commissioning due to its scale and complexity, diverting steel, labor, and yards needed for faster platforms.[30] In contrast, Essex-class carriers were completed in 15â€“20 months during wartime surges, enabling rapid fleet expansion; for instance, USS Essex went from keel-laying in April 1941 to commissioning in December 1942.[31] This disparity aligned with the empirical need for quantity in carrier operations, where multiple air groups overwhelmed surface threats, justifying the U.S. Navy's 1943 cancellation of the Montana-class to prioritize aircraft production and carrier construction.

Counterarguments and Persistent Relevance Claims

Critics of the carrier-centric doctrinal shift, such as naval analyst James R. FitzSimonds, contend that the narrative of battleship obsolescence overlooks the empirical resilience of heavily armored surface combatants in fleet actions, where carriers required extensive escorts to mitigate vulnerabilities exposed in World War II operations.[32] Aircraft carriers proved susceptible to submarine attacks, as demonstrated by the sinking of escort carriers like USS Block Island in May 1944 by U-549 despite screening efforts, highlighting their reliance on destroyer and submarine hunter-killer groups for survival rather than inherent durability.[33] The Montana-class's projected 16.1-inch belt armor and multi-layered deck protection, designed to resist plunging fire and aerial bombs up to 2,000 pounds, would have enabled independent operations in high-threat environments, sustaining fleet gunfire presence without diverting resources to protect lightly armored platforms.[34] In shore bombardment roles, battleships provided precision and volume of fire unmatched by carrier aircraft under adverse conditions, as evidenced by the Iowa-class's performance at Iwo Jima in February 1945, where USS Missouri and others fired over 4,000 16-inch shells with range errors averaging 0.6% of maximum range, demolishing concrete pillboxes and caves that air strikes struggled to penetrate in low-visibility weather.[35] Carrier-based aviation, limited by cloud cover and the need for spotter coordination, could not replicate the sustained, flat-trajectory delivery of battleship guns, which maintained fire rates of up to two rounds per minute per turret; the Montana-class's twelve-gun main battery would have extended this efficacy for amphibious assaults, addressing gaps in naval gunfire support that persisted into the Korean War.[36] Post-World War II hypotheticals grounded in declassified ordnance data indicate that Montana-class vessels could have integrated nuclear 16-inch projectiles, developed by 1956 for the Iowa-class, enabling strategic deterrence roles against Soviet coastal defenses during the Cold War without the escalation risks of carrier strikes.[37] Naval historians like Norman Friedman argue that the abrupt pivot to carrier dominance risked atrophying surface fleet capabilities, as seen in the U.S. Navy's later struggles with inadequate gunfire support in littoral conflicts, where battleship analogs might have preserved balanced force structures against peer adversaries.[38] This perspective challenges post-war orthodoxy by emphasizing causal factors like armor's proven absorption of kinetic impacts over speculative air power invincibility, urging evaluation based on operational data rather than hindsight narratives.[39]

Legacy in Naval Architecture and Modern Hypotheticals

The Montana-class design marked the zenith of pre-aviation-dominant battleship engineering, integrating twelve 16-inch/50 caliber main battery guns, an armored belt reaching 16.1 inches in thickness, and a standard displacement of 60,500 long tons to maximize offensive and defensive potential without the speed constraints of the Iowa class.[40] These specifications, derived from extensive 1940s studies, underscored causal trade-offs in hull form, stability for gunnery, and compartmentalization against underwater threats, informing post-World War II assessments of capital ship viability in balanced fleets.[1] Naval architects continue to reference the class for its empirical optimization of steel construction limits, as evidenced by preserved blueprints and structural analyses emphasizing all-or-nothing armor schemes refined to withstand 18-inch shell impacts.[25] The conceptual emphasis on heavy gunfire for sustained shore bombardment and fleet actions resonated in the 1980s reactivation of the Iowa-class battleships, where upgraded 16-inch batteries addressed similar operational needs in littoral warfare and anti-surface engagements, albeit on faster platforms.[41] This reactivation, involving over $1.5 billion in modifications per ship for electronics and missile integration while retaining gun-centric firepower, highlighted persistent doctrinal value in non-carrier assets for area control, echoing Montana's projected twelve-gun broadside capable of delivering 57,600 pounds of projectiles per salvo.[42] In contemporary strategic discourse, hypothetical modernizations of Montana-like hulls propose nuclear propulsion and electromagnetic railguns to enable anti-access/area denial roles against peer competitors, such as countering Chinese naval expansions in the Western Pacific, by exploiting the original design's beam for stable weapon platforms and deck space for vertical launch systems.[43] Such analyses, grounded in engineering feasibility studies of legacy displacements supporting high-energy directed weapons, argue for causal advantages in endurance and magazine capacity over slimmer modern destroyers, though official U.S. Navy priorities remain carrier- and submarine-focused.[17] Culturally, the class endures through detailed scale models and digital simulations, including its portrayal in naval wargames like World of Warships as a Tier X vessel with historically accurate armament layouts, reinforcing its legacy as the unbuilt exemplar of steel navy supremacy during the aviation paradigm shift.[44] Physical replicas, such as 1:350-scale kits and 3D-printed variants, facilitate empirical study of its form, perpetuating interest among historians and enthusiasts in the empirical limits of gun-based naval power.[45]
