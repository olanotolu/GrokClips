Italian Riviera

The Italian Riviera or Ligurian Riviera (Italian: Riviera ligure [riˈvjɛːra ˈliːɡure]; Ligurian: Rivêa lìgure [ɾiˈveːa ˈliɡyɾe]) is the narrow coastal strip in Italy which lies between the Ligurian Sea and the mountain chain formed by the Maritime Alps and the Apennines. Longitudinally it extends from the border with France and the French Riviera (or Côte d'Azur) near Ventimiglia (a former customs post) eastwards to Capo Corvo (also known as Punta Bianca) which marks the eastern end of the Gulf of La Spezia and is close to the regional border between Liguria and Tuscany. The Italian Riviera thus includes nearly all of the coastline of Liguria. Historically the "Riviera" extended further to the west, through what is now French territory as far as Marseille.[1][2] The Italian Riviera crosses the Ligurian metropolitan city of Genoa and the provinces of Savona, Imperia and La Spezia, with a total length of about 350 km (218 miles).[3] It is customarily divided into a western section, the Ponente Riviera, and an eastern section, the Levante Riviera, the point of division being the apex of the Ligurian arc at Voltri.[3] It has about 1.51 million inhabitants (2025 est.), and most of the population is concentrated within the coastal area. Its mild climate draws an active tourist trade in the numerous coastal resorts, which include Alassio, Bonassola, Bordighera, Camogli, Cinque Terre, Lerici, Levanto, Noli, Portofino, Porto Venere, Santa Margherita Ligure, Sanremo, San Fruttuoso, and Sestri Levante. It is also known for its historical association with international celebrity and artistic visitors;[4][5] writers and poets like Percy Bysshe Shelley, Lord Byron, Ezra Pound, and Ernest Hemingway were inspired by the beauty and spirit of Liguria.[6] As a tourist centre, the Italian Riviera benefits from abundant sunshine and is known for its beaches, colourfully painted towns, natural environment, food, and luxury villas and hotels, as well as for its popular resort facilities, major yachting and cruising areas with several marinas, festivals, golf courses, sailing, rock climbing and scenic views of centuries old farmhouses and cottages.[7] Industries are concentrated in and around Genoa, Savona, and along the shores of the Gulf of La Spezia. Genoa and La Spezia are Italy's leading shipyards; La Spezia is Italy's major naval base, and Savona is a major centre of the Italian iron industry. Chemical, textile, and food industries are also important.[3] A number of streets and palaces in the center of Genoa and the Cinque Terre National Park (which includes Cinque Terre , Portovenere, and the islands Palmaria, Tino and Tinetto) are two of Italy's 58 World Heritage Sites. The Riviera's centre is Genoa, which divides it into two main sections: the Riviera di Ponente ("the coast of the setting sun"), extending westwards from Genoa to the French border; and the Riviera di Levante ("the coast of the rising sun") between Genoa and Capo Corvo. It is known for its mild climate and its reputation for a relaxed way of life, old fishing ports, and landscapes. It has been a popular destination for travellers and tourists since the time of Byron and Percy Shelley. Many villages and towns in the area are internationally known, such as Portofino, Bordighera, Lerici and the Cinque Terre. Many villages of the Italian Riviera are counted among I Borghi più belli d'Italia ("The most beautiful villages of Italy").[8] The part of the Riviera di Ponente centred on Savona, is called the Riviera delle Palme (the Riviera of palms); the part centred on Sanremo, is the Riviera dei Fiori , after the long-established flower growing industry. History

The history of the Italian Riviera is closely intertwined with that of the broader Liguria region.

Ancient and Roman Period

The area was originally inhabited by the Ligurians, an ancient people, and came under Roman domination in the 1st century BCE, which facilitated early coastal settlements and trade routes along the Ligurian Sea.[3]

Medieval and Renaissance Era

During the Middle Ages, Genoa rose as a powerful maritime republic from the 11th century, gaining control over the Ligurian coast by 1400 and establishing the Riviera as a key hub for commerce and naval power.[3] The region's strategic position contributed to its prosperity during the Renaissance, when Genoa headed a colonial empire and banking center.

Modern Period and Unification

In 1796, Napoleon seized Genoa, ending its independence; following the Congress of Vienna in 1815, Liguria was incorporated into the Kingdom of Sardinia. The region played a role in the Risorgimento and joined the Kingdom of Italy in 1860, with Genoa developing as a major industrial and port city.[3] Historically, the "Riviera" extended westward into what is now France, as far as Marseille, as noted in 19th-century travel accounts.[1][2]

Tourism Development

The mild climate and scenic coastline attracted early tourists in the 19th century, leading to the growth of resorts along the Ponente and Levante sections of the Riviera.[3]

Aquarium

The Aquarium of Genoa (Italian: Acquario di Genova), located in the Porto Antico area of Genoa, is the largest aquarium in Europe, covering 27,000 m² and featuring over 400 animal species and 200 plant species from various ecosystems including tropical seas, temperate seas, cold seas, tropical forests, and fresh waters. It showcases marine life such as dolphins, sharks, penguins, and manatees, attracting millions of visitors annually.

Lighthouses

  • La Lanterna, Genoa – Italy's oldest lighthouse, operational since 1543, standing at 77 m tall.
  • Portofino Lighthouse, Portofino – Built in 1917 on the promontory, 12 m tall with a focal plane of 40 m.
  • Capo Mele Lighthouse, Andora – Constructed in 1856, 25 m tall square tower on Capo delle Mele.
  • Capo Vado Lighthouse, Vado Ligure – Octagonal tower from 1883, 34 m tall, marking the entrance to Vado bay.
  • Sanremo Lighthouse, Sanremo – 15 m white tower on Capo dell'Arma, active since 1948.
  • Savona Lighthouse, Savona – Located on the breakwater, guiding ships into the port since the 16th century (rebuilt).

Trekking and Hiking

The Italian Riviera offers diverse trekking and hiking opportunities, ranging from coastal paths with sea views to inland mountain routes. Key trails include:
  • Alta Via dei Monti Liguri: A long-distance trail spanning 440 km (270 mi) across 44 stages through the Ligurian Alps and Apennines, parallel to the Riviera coastline from Ventimiglia to Ceparana. It is challenging, suitable for experienced hikers, with significant elevation changes up to 2,100 m (6,900 ft).
  • Sentiero Azzurro (Blue Trail): The iconic coastal path in Cinque Terre National Park connecting the five villages (Riomaggiore to Monterosso), totaling about 11 km (7 mi). It features steep sections and stunning Ligurian Sea views; moderate difficulty. As of October 2025, most sections are open, though the Via dell'Amore segment remains closed until 2028 due to landslides—requires a park card for access.
  • Sentiero del Pellegrino: A 5 km (3 mi) easy coastal trail between Noli and Varigotti in the Ponente Riviera, passing cliffs, historical sites like the Cerisola Mausoleum, and offering Mediterranean scenery. Suitable for all levels.
  • Camogli to San Fruttuoso: An easy-to-moderate 5-6 km (3-4 mi) path in the Levante Riviera, taking 1.5-2 hours, through olive groves to the historic Abbey of San Fruttuoso. Features pebbly beaches and clear waters.
  • Sentiero dei Poeti (Alta Via del Golfo): A 44 km (27 mi) multi-day trek from Bocca di Magra to Lerici near Cinque Terre, through woods, vineyards, and terraced landscapes. Moderate difficulty with varied terrain.

Casino

Casino

The Italian Riviera is home to one major land-based casino: the Sanremo Casino (Casinò di Sanremo), located in Sanremo. Established in 1905, it is Italy's oldest operating casino and a key attraction on the Riviera, often dubbed "Italy's Monte Carlo." Housed in an Art Nouveau building designed by Eugenio Costantino, it offers various games including French roulette, blackjack, poker, and slot machines, alongside dining, shows, and events.

Airports of the Italian Riviera

Airports

The Italian Riviera is primarily served by two airports within Liguria, with additional international options nearby.
  • Genoa Cristoforo Colombo Airport (IATA: GOA, ICAO: LIMJ), located in Sestri Ponente near Genoa, is the main international airport handling commercial passenger flights, with approximately 1.3 million passengers in 2024.
  • Riviera Airport (IATA: ALL, ICAO: LIMG), in Villanova d'Albenga, primarily serves general aviation and smaller charter flights.
For broader access, many visitors use:
  • Nice Côte d'Azur Airport (NCE) in France, about 150 km west of the Riviera's western end, offering extensive European and international connections.
  • Pisa International Airport (PSA) in Tuscany, around 100 km east of La Spezia, suitable for the eastern Riviera.
