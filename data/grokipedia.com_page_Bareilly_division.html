Bareilly division

Bareilly division is an administrative division of Uttar Pradesh, India, comprising the districts of Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, and Shahjahanpur, with its headquarters located in Bareilly city.[1] Situated in the Rohilkhand region of northern India, the division encompasses fertile Gangetic plains conducive to agriculture, particularly sugarcane, wheat, and rice cultivation, supporting a predominantly rural economy.[2] Historically linked to the ancient Panchala kingdom referenced in the Mahabharata, the area gained prominence under Rohilla rule in the 18th century, with Bareilly serving as a key center before British annexation in 1801.[3] The division's cultural significance includes Bareilly's renowned zari embroidery and textile industries, contributing to its economic profile alongside emerging manufacturing sectors.[4] Administratively, it falls under the jurisdiction of a divisional commissioner overseeing development, law and order, and revenue matters across its districts.[5]

Geography

Location and topography

Bareilly division occupies the northwestern portion of Uttar Pradesh, India, encompassing the districts of Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, and Shahjahanpur.[6] This administrative unit lies within the historical Rohilkhand region of the Indo-Gangetic plain, centered around coordinates of approximately 28Â°20' N latitude and 79Â°25' E longitude, with the city of Bareilly as its headquarters located near the Ramganga River.[7][8] The division borders Uttarakhand and Nepal to the north via Pilibhit district, while adjoining other Uttar Pradesh divisions such as Moradabad to the west and Lucknow to the south.[4] The topography consists primarily of level alluvial plains with a subtle southward slope, facilitating drainage into the Ganges system. Northern areas, especially in Pilibhit, include transitional tarai and bhabar zones near the Himalayan foothills, featuring coarser, gravelly soils and occasional undulations, whereas central and southern parts exhibit mature, fine-textured bangar and khadar soils rich in clay and organic matter.[9] Key rivers such as the Ramganga, Sarda (Ghaghara), and their tributaries traverse the region, depositing fertile alluvial sediments that vary from sandy loam to clayey types, though low-lying khadar tracts remain prone to seasonal flooding.[9][4] Elevations generally range from 150 to 250 meters above sea level, supporting intensive cultivation without significant topographic barriers.[10]

Climate and natural resources

The Bareilly division experiences a humid subtropical climate characterized by hot summers, a pronounced monsoon season, and mild winters. Summers from April to June feature average high temperatures exceeding 40Â°C, with peaks reaching 45.5Â°C in May, accompanied by low humidity and occasional dust storms. Winters from December to February see average lows around 5Â°C, with daytime highs of 20-25Â°C and occasional fog.[11][9] The monsoon arrives in June, bringing heavy rainfall primarily from July to September, accounting for over 80% of the annual precipitation.[12] Annual rainfall averages 1,000-1,100 mm across the division, with Bareilly district recording about 1,096 mm, concentrated in 60-80 rainy days, leading to flooding risks in low-lying areas. Variations exist due to topography, with northern districts like Pilibhit receiving slightly higher rainfall (up to 1,200 mm) from orographic effects near the Himalayas. Droughts occasionally occur in non-monsoon periods, exacerbated by sandy soils in upland areas.[13][9] Natural resources in the division are dominated by fertile alluvial soils and water bodies supporting agriculture, rather than minerals or extensive forests. Predominant soil types include tarai (loamy in northern floodplains), khadar (silt-rich lowlands along rivers), and bangar (upland sandy loams), ideal for crops like sugarcane, wheat, and rice due to high organic content and irrigation potential.[9][4] The Ramganga River and its tributaries, such as the Aril and Bahgul, provide crucial surface water for irrigation, though some tributaries have experienced drying due to overuse and sedimentation. Groundwater aquifers, recharged by monsoon rains, form a vital resource, with extraction rates supporting rural and urban needs but facing depletion in overexploited blocks.[9] Forest cover remains sparse, covering under 1% of the division's land, with Bareilly district at 352 hectares mostly as reserved patches, while Pilibhit hosts denser terai forests including the Pilibhit Tiger Reserve, harboring sal, teak, and wildlife like tigers and elephants. Mineral resources are negligible, limited to minor deposits of sand and gravel for construction, with no significant metallic or industrial minerals identified.[4][14]

History

Pre-colonial and Mughal periods

The territory of present-day Bareilly division formed part of the ancient Panchala janapada, referenced in the Mahabharata as a realm where Draupadi was born, with Ahichhatra in Bareilly district serving as the capital of northern Panchala.[3] Archaeological excavations at Ahichhatra reveal evidence of settlement continuity from the 2nd millennium BCE, encompassing the Painted Grey Ware culture associated with late Vedic societies (circa 1100â€“800 BCE), through Mauryan, Kushan, and Gupta periods, up to the early medieval era around the 11th century CE; over 5,000 coins predating the Gupta dynasty (pre-4th century CE) have been recovered, alongside pottery, beads, and structural remains indicating urban activity and bead manufacturing.[15][16] In the early medieval period, the regionâ€”known as Kateharâ€”was controlled by Rajput clans such as the Katehriyas, who established local strongholds amid forested terrain, before its subjugation by the Delhi Sultanate's Muslim rulers in the 12th century, marking the transition from Hindu polities to Islamic overlordship extending from Delhi.[17] This incorporation placed the area under Turko-Afghan governance, with administrative units like parganas emerging, though archaeological layers at sites like Ahichhatra show disruption around the 12th century, possibly linked to invasions.[18] Under the Mughal Empire, the region integrated into the imperial framework during Akbar's reign (1556â€“1605), with revenue assessments and divisions formalized by Todar Mal, as documented in administrative records from 1596 that delineated fiscal units including Bareilly.[19] The city of Bareilly itself was founded in 1657 by Raja Makrand Rai, a local Brahmin zamindar acting under Emperor Aurangzeb's authority, who cleared western forests to establish a structured settlement and fort; by 1658, it was designated headquarters of the Budaun province (subah), fostering growth as a commercial and administrative hub within the subah of Delhi.[20][21] The broader Rohilkhand area, encompassing the division's districts, functioned as a strategic frontier zone under Mughal governors, with parganas like Bareilly contributing to imperial revenues through agriculture and trade, though it retained semi-autonomous zamindari elements until the empire's weakening in the early 18th century.[17]

Colonial era and Rohilla rule

The Rohillas, Pashtun Afghans who migrated to northern India in the late 17th and early 18th centuries, established control over the Katehr region (later known as Rohilkhand) under Daud Khan Bangash, who was granted authority by Mughal emperor Aurangzeb around 1707 to suppress local rebellions.[22] Daud Khan's successors, including Ali Muhammad Khan, consolidated power by the 1720s, founding the Kingdom of Rohilkhand with Bareilly as the capital, where they imposed an Afghan-influenced administration over a predominantly Hindu population, minting coins and maintaining a military reliant on Rohilla cavalry.[17] This rule persisted under chieftains like Hafiz Rahmat Khan, who navigated alliances with the declining Mughal Empire and neighboring powers, fostering economic growth through agriculture and trade in the fertile Ganges plains until internal divisions weakened their hold.[23] The First Rohilla War of 1774 marked the end of independent Rohilla dominance, as Nawab Shuja-ud-Daula of Awadh, backed by British East India Company forces under Governor-General Warren Hastings to recover alleged debts, invaded Rohilkhand.[24] Rohilla forces led by Hafiz Rahmat Khan were decisively defeated at the Battle of Miranpur Katra on April 23, 1774, resulting in Rahmat Khan's death and the annexation of most of Rohilkhand by Awadh, though the British facilitated the creation of the smaller Rampur princely state under Faizullah Khan to maintain a buffer.[25] From 1774 to 1801, the Bareilly region fell under Awadh's nominal suzerainty, with British subsidies funding Company troops stationed there, but heavy taxation and administrative interference sowed discontent, culminating in localized uprisings like the 1816 Bareilly revolt against revenue exactions that burdened peasants.[26][27] Direct British control solidified after the 1801 Treaty of Lucknow, when Awadh ceded Rohilkhandâ€”including Bareilly, Badaun, and Shahjahanpurâ€”to the East India Company in exchange for debt relief, integrating the area into the North-Western Provinces as a key division with Bareilly as the administrative headquarters.[27][28] The British reorganized land revenue through zamindari settlements, built infrastructure like canals and railways, and stationed a commissioner in Bareilly to oversee the five districts of Rohilkhand, though Pathan-Rohilla elites retained influence amid ongoing tensions.[29] The 1857 Indian Rebellion erupted prominently in Bareilly, where Khan Bahadur Khan, a Rohilla descendant of Hafiz Rahmat Khan and aged about 70, proclaimed independence on May 31, 1857, establishing a rebel government as viceroy of Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah II, mobilizing around 4,600 cavalry and infantry to challenge British authority.[30][31] British forces recaptured Bareilly by May 1858 after fierce fighting, including massacres of Europeans, leading to Khan Bahadur Khan's capture in Nepal and public execution by hanging on February 24, 1860, in Bareilly; subsequent reprisals dismantled remaining Rohilla power structures, enforcing stricter colonial governance until India's independence in 1947.[30][32]

Post-independence developments

Following India's independence in 1947, the princely state of Rampur, located in the eastern portion of what would become Bareilly division, acceded to the Dominion of India on August 15, 1947, with formal merger into the United Provinces (renamed Uttar Pradesh in 1950) occurring on January 1, 1950.[33] This integration brought the Muslim-majority Rampur district under direct provincial administration, ending the rule of the Nawabs and aligning it with the surrounding districts of Bareilly, Badaun, Shahjahanpur, and Pilibhit, which had been under British control since the early 19th century.[34] A major agrarian reform was the Uttar Pradesh Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act of 1950, which abolished the intermediary zamindari system across the region, including Rohilkhand (encompassing Bareilly division).[35] The legislation, implemented from July 1, 1952, vested intermediary interests in the state, redistributed surplus land to tenants, and introduced bhumi rights for cultivators, aiming to enhance agricultural productivity and reduce rural inequities in areas dominated by sugarcane and wheat farming.[36] While it empowered over 20 million tenants statewide as landowners, implementation in Rohilkhand faced challenges like land fragmentation and incomplete redistribution due to legal loopholes exploited by former zamindars.[37] Administrative consolidation continued with the formalization of divisional commissionerates in Uttar Pradesh during the 1960s, retaining Bareilly as headquarters for coordinating governance across its districts.[38] Subsequent district-level changes included the carving out of Pilibhit as a separate district from Bareilly in 1997 to improve local administration amid population growth.[7] The region also witnessed periodic communal disturbances, such as riots in Bareilly in 1972 and 1982, stemming from disputes over religious processions and underscoring persistent Hindu-Muslim tensions in this diverse area.[39]

Administration

Districts and subdivisions

Bareilly division encompasses four districts: Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, and Shahjahanpur.[40] These districts are subdivided into tehsils, which function as key revenue and magisterial administrative units responsible for local governance, land records, and judicial functions within their jurisdictions. Bareilly District, the divisional headquarters, is divided into six tehsils: Aonla, Baheri, Bareilly, Faridpur, Meerganj, and Nawabganj.[5] Badaun District consists of six tehsils: Badaun, Bisauli, Bilsi, Dataganj, Gunnaur, and Sahaswan.[41] Pilibhit District comprises five tehsils: Bisalpur, Kalinagar, Pilibhit, Puranpur, and Amaria.[42] Shahjahanpur District is organized into four tehsils: Jalalabad, Powayan, Shahjahanpur Sadar, and Tilhar.[43]

Governance structure

The Bareilly division is governed by a Divisional Commissioner, a senior officer of the Indian Administrative Service (IAS) selected and posted by the Government of Uttar Pradesh, who functions as the apex administrative authority for the region. This position oversees the coordination of four districtsâ€”Badaun, Bareilly, Pilibhit, and Shahjahanpurâ€”ensuring alignment between state directives and local implementation. The Commissioner's office, located in Bareilly, maintains supervisory control over district magistrates, revenue boards, and departmental heads to facilitate efficient governance without independent legislative powers, as divisions serve primarily as intermediate executive units between state and district levels.[44][45][46] Key functions of the Divisional Commissioner include monitoring land revenue collection, adjudicating appeals in revenue and tenancy disputes from district courts, coordinating developmental programs across districts, and managing contingency operations such as elections and natural disasters. The role extends to inter-departmental dispute resolution, policy enforcement in sectors like agriculture and infrastructure, and periodic inspections of district-level compliance with state mandates, thereby promoting administrative uniformity. Additional Divisional Commissioners, typically two to three IAS officers, assist in specialized domains including revenue administration, industrial promotion, and rural development, reporting directly to the Commissioner.[47][48] The governance framework emphasizes executive oversight rather than autonomous decision-making, with the Commissioner accountable to the stateâ€™s Revenue Board and Chief Secretary for performance metrics like revenue targets and scheme disbursements. This structure, inherited from colonial-era commissionerates and refined post-independence, addresses regional disparities in the Rohilkhand area by centralizing coordination while deferring primary implementation to district units.[46][45]

Law and order challenges

Bareilly division faces ongoing challenges in maintaining law and order, primarily due to recurrent communal tensions and sporadic outbreaks of violence, particularly in urban centers like Bareilly city. These issues often stem from religious sensitivities, with incidents escalating into protests, stone-pelting, and clashes between demonstrators and police forces. In September 2025, protests over "I Love Muhammad" posters in Bareilly turned violent following Friday prayers on September 26, resulting in vehicle vandalism, injuries to several police personnel, and the arrest of over 40 individuals, including prominent cleric Tauqeer Raza Khan. Authorities described the unrest as a "pre-planned conspiracy" and responded with internet and SMS suspensions extended until October 4 to prevent further escalation, alongside drone surveillance and heightened security during subsequent religious events like Dussehra.[49][50][51] District-level crime data underscores broader enforcement difficulties, with Bareilly reporting a crime rate of 188.88 per 100,000 population in 2022, reflecting challenges in curbing property crimes, drug-related issues, and vandalism amid moderate overall urban crime perceptions. While Uttar Pradesh's statewide crime rate remains below the national average at approximately 171.6 per 100,000 in recent yearsâ€”compared to India's 258.1â€”the division's districts like Shahjahanpur have witnessed communal flare-ups, such as a September 2024 incident leading to cases against 24 individuals. Police effectiveness varies, with historical assessments ranking stations like Shahjahanpur's Sadar Bazar highly for performance, but rural outposts in Pilibhit and Badaun scoring lower, indicating resource strains and coordination gaps in remote areas.[52][53][54] Administrative responses include strict enforcement measures, such as demolitions of properties linked to instigators and vows of impartial action against violators, yet criticisms persist regarding potential overreach, with opposition figures alleging entrapment of innocents in false cases post-violence. These events highlight systemic pressures on local policing, including the need for proactive intelligence to counter organized disruptions, amid a backdrop of decades-long communal polarization in the region that amplifies minor triggers into major law and order threats.[55][56][39]

Socio-economic indicators

The Bareilly division's socio-economic profile reflects agrarian dependence and developmental lags, with key indicators such as literacy, income, and poverty revealing disparities across its five districts (Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, Shahjahanpur, and Rampur). Literacy rates, based on the 2011 Census, average below the national figure of 74%, hampered by gender gaps and rural-urban divides; for instance, Bareilly district recorded 68.11% overall literacy (male 76.70%, female 58.02%), while Rampur had the lowest at 53.01% (male 62.45%, female 42.87%). These rates underscore limited access to education in rural areas, where female literacy often falls under 50%.[66] Per capita income varies significantly, underscoring uneven economic progress; Bareilly district's stood at Rs. 85,559 in 2021-22, supported by agriculture and some industry, compared to Shahjahanpur's Rs. 62,875 and Pilibhit's Rs. 68,454 in the same period.[52][67][68] Division-wide, incomes remain below Uttar Pradesh's state average of approximately Rs. 70,000, with agriculture employing over 60% of the workforce and contributing the bulk of GDP.[69]

Economy

Agricultural sector

The agricultural sector forms the economic backbone of Bareilly division, employing over 59% of the population in districts such as Bareilly and supporting a rice-wheat cropping system supplemented by cash crops like sugarcane.[72] [73] Cereal crops dominate, with wheat and paddy (rice) as principal staples, while sugarcane contributes significantly to regional output due to favorable alluvial soils and irrigation availability. Districts like Shahjahanpur and Bareilly record high sugarcane and wheat yields, though vegetable crop productivity remains relatively low compared to state averages.[74] [75] In Bareilly district, net sown area spans 327,000 hectares with a cropping intensity of 163%, driven largely by rabi wheat (191,000 hectares, 571,000 tonnes production, 2,986 kg/ha productivity) and kharif rice (149,000 hectares, 266,000 tonnes, 1,786 kg/ha).[76] Sugarcane excels here with 97,000 hectares under cultivation, yielding 5.5 million tonnes at 56,564 kg/ha, underscoring the division's role in Uttar Pradesh's sugarcane belt.[76] Across Shahjahanpur, wheat occupies 253,000 hectares with 3,030 kg/ha yields, while paddy covers 210,000 hectares; pulses, groundnut, and horticultural crops like mango and guava add diversity.[74] Rampur and Pilibhit emphasize sugarcane expansion, with Pilibhit showing accelerated area growth post-2000. Irrigation coverage is extensive, reaching 85-90% of cultivable land via borewells (86% of sources in Bareilly) and canals, mitigating reliance on the division's 1,100 mm annual rainfall concentrated in the southwest monsoon.[76] Predominant loam and sandy loam soils (over 60% combined) support intensive farming, though groundwater depletion in semi-critical blocks poses risks. Badaun focuses on wheat, gram, millets, and potatoes, aligning with the division's cereal-oriented profile.[76] [77]

Industrial and commercial activities

The industrial landscape of Bareilly division centers on small and medium enterprises (SMEs), agro-processing, and traditional handicrafts, with limited large-scale manufacturing. Bareilly district, the divisional headquarters, features prominent clusters in zari-zardozi embroidery, furniture making, cane and bamboo products, mentha oil distillation, and rice milling, supported by the Uttar Pradesh State Industrial Development Authority (UPSIDA) through estates like the 356-acre Parsakhera Industrial Area, developed since 1980.[4][78] The Baheri Mega Food Park in the region targets MSMEs in fruit preservation, namkeen, and rice processing, leveraging local sugarcane and paddy resources.[79][80] Shahjahanpur district hosts several public and private large-scale units, including the Ordnance Clothing Factory for military apparel, K.R. Pulp & Paper Mills for paper production, sugar mills, cement factories, and KRIBHCO's fertilizer plant producing urea and ammonia from natural gas.[81] Pilibhit district focuses on wood-based industries, with flutes and wooden crafts identified under the One District One Product (ODOP) scheme, alongside food grain processing to support its role as a major supplier in Uttar Pradesh.[82][83] Badaun district has minimal industrial infrastructure, with economic activity skewed toward agricultural support rather than manufacturing.[4] A key recent initiative is BL Agro's greenfield integrated agro-processing project in Bareilly, with a â‚¹1,660 crore investment over five years announced via MoU with the Ministry of Food Processing Industries in September 2025; it includes a fully automated 500 TPD rice milling unit, edible oil extraction and refining, and is projected to create over 3,500 direct and indirect jobs.[84][85] Commercial activities emphasize trade in agro-commodities, textiles, and handicrafts, facilitated by wholesale markets in Bareilly such as Sadar Bazaar for general goods and Qutubkhana for zari-zardozi products, which export to national and international markets under ODOP promotion.[86][87] These hubs drive local commerce, though the sector remains underdeveloped compared to agriculture, with reliance on road and rail connectivity for distribution.[88]

operational since expansions, offering flights to Delhi, Lucknow, Mumbai, and Bengaluru.[93] Power supply is managed by Uttar Pradesh Power Corporation Limited, with initiatives like 979 solar Culture and society

Cultural heritage and traditions

The cultural heritage of Bareilly division reflects a syncretic blend of Hindu, Muslim, and local folk traditions, shaped by its historical position as a crossroads of Mughal and colonial influences in Uttar Pradesh. Traditional crafts, particularly zari-zardozi embroidery in Bareilly district, involve intricate gold and silver threadwork on fabrics, a practice dating back to Mughal patronage and still practiced by artisan communities for garments and decorative items.[95] In Rampur district, the Rampur-Sahaswan gharana of Hindustani classical music originated around 1820 under the patronage of the Nawabs of Rampur, emphasizing vocal styles like khayal and thumri with distinctive rhythmic patterns.[102] Folk performing arts thrive across the division, including Raslila dances depicting episodes from the Ramayana and Mahabharata, often performed during religious festivals in rural areas of Bareilly and Badaun districts. Nautanki, a popular folk theater form combining music, dance, and dialogue, draws from Awadhi and Braj cultural motifs and remains a staple in Shahjahanpur's communal gatherings.[95] [103] These traditions underscore the division's rural agrarian ethos, where performances reinforce social bonds and seasonal cycles. Festivals form a cornerstone of communal life, with Diwali, Holi, Eid, and Muharram observed vibrantly in urban centers like Bareilly and Rampur, featuring processions, fireworks, and shared feasts that highlight interfaith participation despite occasional tensions. Sufi traditions, evident in dargahs and qawwali sessions in Bareilly, trace to medieval saintly orders and promote devotional music blending Persian and Indian elements.[95] Local cuisine emphasizes wheat-based breads, kebabs, and sweets like petha, adapted from Awadhi recipes and prepared during weddings and harvest celebrations.[104] Preservation efforts, including government handicraft markets, sustain these practices amid urbanization, though artisan numbers have declined due to mechanization.[105]

Education and notable institutions

The education landscape in Bareilly division emphasizes primary and secondary schooling under Uttar Pradesh state initiatives, supplemented by higher education hubs primarily in Bareilly. As per the 2011 Census, literacy rates across the division's districts lag behind the state average of 67.68%, with Bareilly district recording 58.49% overall literacy (67.50% male, 48.30% female), reflecting persistent gender and rural-urban disparities.[57] Similar rates prevail in Badaun (approximately 52-55%) and Shahjahanpur, underscoring challenges in access and quality amid agricultural economies.[52] Mahatma Jyotiba Phule Rohilkhand University (MJPRU), established in 1975 in Bareilly, functions as the key affiliating body for undergraduate, postgraduate, and doctoral programs across disciplines including arts, sciences, engineering, law, and applied economics, serving the division's districts of Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, Shahjahanpur, and Rampur.[106] Accredited with a B grade by NAAC, MJPRU supports over 600 affiliated colleges and emphasizes regional development through research in agriculture and rural studies.[107] The Indian Veterinary Research Institute (IVRI), founded in 1889 and deemed a university since 1983 under the Indian Council of Agricultural Research, operates its main campus in Bareilly as India's premier center for veterinary sciences, offering MVSc and PhD programs in animal health, pathology, epidemiology, and biotechnology. IVRI conducts advanced research on livestock diseases and production, contributing to national food security with facilities including diagnostic labs and regional campuses.[108] Other significant institutions include Shri Ram Murti Smarak College of Engineering and Technology in Bareilly, focused on technical degrees in engineering and management since 1996, and Government Raza Post Graduate College in Rampur, providing arts, commerce, and science education affiliated with MJPRU.[109] In Shahjahanpur, Government Medical College, established post-2010s, addresses medical training needs, while Pilibhit's Autonomous State Medical College supports similar expansions in healthcare education.[110] Private entities like Invertis University in Bareilly offer interdisciplinary programs in business and technology, though public institutions dominate enrollment in the division.[111]

Notable personalities

Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856â€“1921), an influential Islamic scholar, theologian, and founder of the Barelvi movement within Sunni Islam, was born on 14 June 1856 in Bareilly.[112] His works emphasized Sufi traditions and Hanafi jurisprudence, attracting millions of followers across South Asia.[113] Ram Prasad Bismil (1897â€“1927), a revolutionary poet and key figure in India's independence struggle, was born on 11 June 1897 in Shahjahanpur.[114] He co-founded the Hindustan Republican Association and participated in the Kakori conspiracy of 1925 against British rule, for which he was executed by hanging on 19 December 1927 in Gorakhpur.[115] His poetry, including the famous "Sarfaroshi ki tamanna," inspired anti-colonial resistance.[116] Ashfaqulla Khan (1900â€“1927), a close associate of Bismil in the Kakori train robbery, was born on 22 October 1900 in Shahjahanpur to a Pathan family.[117] As one of the youngest executed revolutionaries at age 27, hanged on 19 December 1927 in Faizabad, Khan symbolized Hindu-Muslim unity in the freedom movement, having joined despite familial opposition to armed action.[118] Shakeel Badayuni (1916â€“1970), a prolific Hindi-Urdu film lyricist, was born on 3 August 1916 in Badaun.[119] He penned over 700 songs for Bollywood, earning two Filmfare Awards for lyrics in films like Mughal-e-Azam (1960) and Dhadki (1968), blending devotional and romantic themes rooted in classical poetry.[120] Zohra Sehgal (1912â€“2014), a pioneering dancer, actress, and choreographer, was born on 27 April 1912 into a Rohilla Pathan family in Rampur.[121] Spanning a career over eight decades, she performed in Uday Shankar's troupe, acted in over 100 films including Neecha Nagar (1946), and received the Padma Vibhushan in 2010 for contributions to Indian performing arts.[122]

Contemporary issues

Recent communal tensions

On September 26, 2025, communal tensions escalated in Bareilly city when protesters, organized by the Ittehad-e-Millat Council and led by cleric Tauqeer Raza, clashed with police during a demonstration against FIRs filed over posters displaying the phrase "I Love Muhammad."[123][124] The unrest followed Friday prayers and involved stone-pelting, vehicle arson, and attacks on law enforcement, injuring 22 police personnel and prompting the deployment of additional forces including the Provincial Armed Constabulary.[125] Authorities arrested 81 individuals initially, including key figures like Nadeem, identified as a conspirator, with the total rising to at least 56 by September 30; internet services were suspended in affected areas to curb further mobilization.[126][127] The Uttar Pradesh Anti-Terrorist Squad (ATS) subsequently linked the violence to a broader radical network, conducting raids that uncovered a Mujahideen-inspired plot to incite riots, overthrow the state government, and establish Sharia rule; four arrests were made across districts including Rampur for plotting communal disturbances tied to the "I Love Muhammad" row.[128][129] In response, municipal authorities demolished over 400 illegal encroachments in violence-affected zones starting October 8, 2025, as part of anti-encroachment drives, while security remained heightened with flag marches in Bareilly and neighboring districts.[130][131] Opposition parties, including the Samajwadi Party, condemned the state government for allegedly exacerbating divisions through heavy-handed policing, while SP leader Azam Khan described the events as a conspiracy to undermine communal harmony.[132][133] These incidents reflect persistent underlying polarization in the region, with historical patterns of tension over religious expressions, though mainstream reporting from outlets like The Hindu and Indian Express often frames police responses critically without equally scrutinizing instigative calls for protest.[39] No major inter-community clashes were reported in other Bareilly division districts like Rampur or Pilibhit during 2023-2024, though isolated caste-based altercations occurred, such as a October 25, 2025, skirmish between Koiri and Turha groups in Rampur injuring eight.[134]

Urbanization and environmental concerns

Bareilly division, encompassing districts such as Bareilly, Badaun, Pilibhit, Shahjahanpur, and Rampur, exhibits moderate urbanization levels compared to Uttar Pradesh's statewide average of approximately 22.3% as per the 2011 Census, with Bareilly district leading at about 44.5% urban population (1,568,409 out of 3,521,895 total residents).[135] Urban growth has accelerated post-2011, driven by Bareilly city's role as a regional commercial center; its metropolitan population is projected to reach 1,439,000 by 2025, up from 903,668 in 2011, reflecting an annual increase of roughly 2.3%.[136] State initiatives, including the Uttar Pradesh government's 2025 urban expansion scheme allocating Rs. 3,000 crore for systematic development, support housing projects like the Greater Bareilly Housing Scheme to accommodate this influx, though rapid peri-urban sprawl strains infrastructure.[137][138] This urbanization exacerbates environmental pressures, particularly air pollution in Bareilly city, where pre-2020 PM10 concentrations averaged 226 Î¼g/mÂ³â€”nearly four times the national standard of 60 Î¼g/mÂ³â€”due to vehicular emissions, industrial activity, and dust from construction.[139] The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) monitors sites like the Indian Veterinary Research Institute and has implemented action plans emphasizing road paving, waste management, and treated effluent reuse to mitigate these issues, though compliance remains inconsistent.[140] Water quality in the Ramganga aquifer, vital for the division, shows elevated heavy metals like lead and cadmium exceeding permissible limits, linked to agricultural runoff and urban effluents, posing risks to drinking water supplies.[141] Deforestation rates in Bareilly district are low, with natural forest cover under 0.1% of land area (176 ha in 2020) and annual losses below 1 ha, but broader land degradation from urbanization and agriculture affects soil fertility across the division.[142] Pilibhit district, home to grasslands and forests, faces localized threats from encroachment, contributing to biodiversity loss in areas like the Pilibhit Tiger Reserve. Solid waste management lags, with ongoing efforts to site disposal facilities amid urban expansion, while urban flooding recurs due to inadequate drainage, as seen in 2025 incidents submerging low-lying areas.[143] The National Clean Air Programme targets a 40% PM10 reduction by 2026, but enforcement challenges persist amid Uttar Pradesh's high pollution burden.[144][145]
