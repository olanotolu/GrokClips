Howrah Municipal Corporation

The Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) is the statutory urban local body responsible for civic administration, infrastructure maintenance, and public services in Howrah, an industrial city in West Bengal, India, situated on the western bank of the Hooghly River opposite Kolkata.[1][2] Formed by upgrading the Howrah Municipality—established in 1862 under Act XII of the British Indian government—and further expanded in 2015 through the merger of Bally Municipality, the HMC governs 63.55 square kilometers divided into 66 wards across seven boroughs, serving a population of 1,362,561 with a density of 21,565 persons per square kilometer.[1][3][2] The corporation's core functions encompass water supply (70 million gallons daily, initiated with filtered water in 1896), solid waste management (handling 825 tons daily), road upkeep over 1,394 kilometers, public health initiatives, urban planning, and maintenance of amenities including 83 parks and 12 municipal schools.[3][4] While the HMC has facilitated economic empowerment through 570 self-help groups since inception and supports 4,199 beneficiaries under national social assistance programs, it operates amid challenges typical of densely populated industrial urban centers, such as flood vulnerability due to the region's riverine geography and high waste generation pressures.[5][3] No major defining controversies dominate its record in official documentation, though municipal governance in West Bengal has faced periodic scrutiny over electoral delays and administrative efficiency in state audits.[6]

History

Foundation and Early Operations

The Howrah Municipality was established in 1862 under British colonial administration, pursuant to Section 12 of the Act of 1858, to address the escalating urban pressures in the area opposite Calcutta across the Hooghly River, fueled by burgeoning trade, migration, and early industrial development.[7] This formation responded to the need for organized local governance amid rapid population growth and economic activity tied to the port and riverine commerce, marking one of the initial municipal bodies in the Bengal Presidency.[8] The inaugural board meeting convened on May 6, 1864, where commissioners approved preliminary measures, including adjustments to ward boundaries to better delineate administrative jurisdictions.[9] Early operations centered on rudimentary civic functions, such as revenue collection through property taxes and licenses, which provided the fiscal basis for basic infrastructure and public works, though implementation was constrained by limited resources and colonial oversight.[7] The first municipal election occurred on December 1, 1884, resulting in the selection of commissioners who prioritized sanitation improvements and road maintenance to mitigate health risks from overcrowding and unsanitary conditions.[9] These efforts grappled with challenges posed by industrial expansion, including the proliferation of jute mills starting around 1870 and the growth of railway facilities, which intensified urban density and strained rudimentary drainage and waste management systems without comprehensive public health frameworks at the outset.[10] Initial tax revenues, derived mainly from trade-related levies, funded targeted initiatives like street lighting and conservancy, laying the groundwork for sustained municipal authority amid ongoing colonial priorities.[7]

Expansion and Upgrade to Corporation Status

Following India's independence in 1947, Howrah witnessed accelerated population growth driven by partition-induced migration from East Pakistan (now Bangladesh), improved public health measures, and industrial expansion in jute mills and engineering sectors, which collectively increased the area's demographic pressure on municipal services.[11][12] By the mid-20th century, the existing Howrah Municipality, established in 1862, faced administrative overload from unmanaged urban sprawl, inadequate sanitation, and rising demands for water supply and road maintenance, rendering its limited authority insufficient for coordinated governance.[2] To address these exigencies, the West Bengal state government upgraded Howrah Municipality to Municipal Corporation status in 1984 under the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, 1980, which expanded its fiscal powers, planning capabilities, and oversight of public health and urban development.[7][2] This transition incorporated the original municipal jurisdiction—spanning core urban zones with established wards—into a more robust framework, enabling direct mayoral leadership; the first mayor, Alokedut Das, served from 1984 to 1989.[13] The upgrade facilitated initial elections for a Board of Administrators and enhanced revenue collection for infrastructure, though it did not immediately resolve underlying strains from haphazard growth. Empirical indicators of these pressures included the unchecked emergence of informal settlements, later quantified by HMC surveys identifying 733 slums across municipal, railway, government, and private lands, underscoring causal lapses in pre-upgrade zoning and enforcement that perpetuated service gaps in water, drainage, and housing.[5] Such deficiencies stemmed from the post-independence influx outpacing regulatory capacity, setting precedents for inefficiencies in slum rehabilitation and waste management that persisted despite the corporation's broadened mandate.[14]

Mergers and Boundary Changes

In 2015, the Bally Municipality was merged into the Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) through a notification issued by the West Bengal government on June 26, 2015 (No. 428/MA/O/C-4/IM-36/2014), expanding HMC's jurisdiction to include Bally's territory and increasing the total number of wards from 50 to 66 after delimitation reduced Bally's original 35 wards to 16.[1][2][15] The merger was intended to streamline urban administration and improve service delivery across contiguous areas, but it prompted concerns among Bally residents over potential increases in property taxes aligned with HMC's higher rates and disruptions in local civic services, including waste management and water supply, which had been managed separately under Bally's lower-tax regime.[15][16] This expansion was reversed in 2021 when the West Bengal Legislative Assembly passed the Howrah Municipal Corporation (Amendment) Bill on November 17, 2021, bifurcating Bally's 16 wards from HMC to restore it as a separate municipality ahead of civic polls, thereby reducing HMC's wards back to 50.[17][18] The amendment, which received gubernatorial assent on December 25, 2021, after initial delays, addressed resident grievances from the merger period, such as inconsistent infrastructure maintenance and perceived favoritism in resource allocation favoring core HMC areas over Bally.[19][20] However, the timing of the bifurcation, occurring shortly before elections, highlighted administrative volatility, with Bally's separation enabling independent ward elections and potentially alleviating service backlogs but requiring fresh delimitation for its restored governance structure.[21]

Governance and Administration

Organizational Structure

The Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) operates under the framework established by the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, 1980, which defines its municipal authorities, administrative powers, and departmental divisions for civic management.[22] The structure separates executive functions, handled by appointed bureaucrats, from oversight bodies, emphasizing operational execution over policy formulation.[23] At the apex of administration is the Commissioner, an Indian Administrative Service (IAS) officer appointed by the state government, who oversees policy implementation, departmental coordination, and compliance with statutory mandates.[24] Due to ongoing delays in conducting municipal elections, as permitted under state provisions for supersession, HMC is currently managed by a Board of Administrators, which exercises powers typically held by elected councillors, including approval of budgets and major contracts, while delegating daily execution to the Commissioner and subordinate staff.[25] This interim arrangement, notified by the West Bengal government, underscores the bureaucratic continuity amid electoral disruptions, with the Commissioner retaining authority over routine enforcement and resource allocation.[26] Core departments handle specialized operations: the General Department manages administrative support, record-keeping, agenda preparation for meetings, and vital statistics like birth and death registrations; the Property Tax Department assesses and collects levies forming the bulk of revenue; the Trade License Department issues permits and ensures compliance with business regulations; and the Works Department oversees engineering projects, maintenance, and infrastructure execution through executive engineers.[26][23] Additional units, such as Accounts for budget preparation and utilization certificates, support financial oversight, with allocations prioritizing revenue expenditure on essential services.[27] The organization extends to seven boroughs, each led by an officer-in-charge (typically an assistant engineer), facilitating localized administration across 66 wards.[2] HMC's financial framework, reflected in its 2023 CRISIL rating of 'BBB+/Stable' for bonds, indicates moderate predictability in revenue streams, predominantly from property taxes, amid dependencies on state grants and operational efficiencies in departmental collections.[28] This rating accounts for the corporation's established tax base but highlights vulnerabilities in expenditure control and non-tax revenue diversification.[28]

Leadership and Decision-Making Bodies

The leadership of the Howrah Municipal Corporation is vested in an elected Mayor, selected by the Board of Councillors from among its members, who presides over municipal proceedings and chairs the Mayor-in-Council, a committee responsible for executive policy implementation and oversight of departmental functions.[26] The Deputy Mayor, also elected by the Board, assists in these duties and assumes the Mayor's role in their absence. This structure ensures elected accountability, with the Board of Councillors providing legislative oversight through committees such as the Municipal Accounts Committee, though day-to-day administration falls under an appointed Commissioner, currently IAS officer Bandana Pokhriyal.[29] Historically, mayoral tenures reflect extended service under various political affiliations, beginning post-1984 upgrade with Swadesh Chakraborty holding office from 1989 to 1999, followed by Subinoy Ghosh (1999-2000), Gopal Mukherjee (2000-2008), and Mamta Jaiswal (2008-2013).[13] Rathin Chakraborty served from 2013 to 2018, marking a period of continuity amid shifting electoral dynamics.[13] These terms, often spanning multiple election cycles, underscore mechanisms allowing re-election from the councillor base, though accountability has been tested by instances of internal disputes, as seen in physical altercations involving Mayor Jaiswal during her tenure over enforcement actions.[30] Since the expiration of elected terms without fresh polls, the corporation has operated under a state-appointed Board of Administrators, comprising a Chairperson and Vice-Chairpersons, tasked with interim governance and decision-making to maintain service continuity.[23] Dr. Sujay Chakraborty, a pediatrician appointed Chairperson around 2021, oversaw this board until his resignation on October 26, 2025, attributed to personal reasons, prompting potential state intervention for replacement amid ongoing administrative delays.[31] This administrator model, while ensuring operational stability, limits direct electoral input into leadership, with the Commissioner retaining executive authority over borough-level implementation.[32]

Wards and Electoral Representation

The Howrah Municipal Corporation divides its jurisdiction into 66 wards, organized across 7 borough offices to facilitate zonal administrative oversight and service delivery coordination.[3][2] Each ward functions as an electoral constituency, with one councillor elected per ward to serve on the Board of Councillors, providing grassroots-level representation reflective of local population distributions and densities averaging 21,565 persons per square kilometer.[2] This structure ties electoral outcomes to demographic realities, including high concentrations in slum-heavy wards where informal settlements predominate. Post-2015 merger adjustments with Bally Municipality reduced its original 35 wards to 16 delimited units integrated into the HMC framework, altering representational balances by consolidating oversight without proportional expansion of council seats.[21] Ward boundaries, delineated by the West Bengal State Election Commission, account for population variations to ensure equitable representation, though empirical data indicate persistent challenges in wards dominated by 733 identified slums—mostly on private or non-HMC land—prioritizing issues like sanitation and land tenure over uniform development.[5] Borough-level aggregation enables councillors to address cross-ward concerns, such as density-driven infrastructure strains, while maintaining ward-specific accountability.[32]

Civic Services and Infrastructure

Core Municipal Services

The Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) delivers core municipal services as outlined in the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, 1980, encompassing water supply, sanitation, drainage, solid waste management, street lighting, and registration of births and deaths to support public health amid high urban density.[33] These functions operate through specialized departments, with funding derived from property taxes, trade licenses, and state grants that cover a substantial share of revenues after own sources contribute 22-32%.[34] [28] Water supply is managed by the engineering department, which distributes purified water from the Padmapukur plant via primary and secondary grids, pipelines, and deep tube wells to roads, lanes, and households, including sanctioning connections in areas like Bally (wards 51-66) and maintaining underground infrastructure.[35] Sanitation and waste handling involve daily collection of domestic garbage from lanes in wards 1-50 by the conservancy department, with disposal at Belgachia trenching ground, alongside solid waste management initiatives promoting source segregation into wet, dry, and hazardous categories through distributed bins and awareness campaigns, as piloted in ward 22.[36] [37] Drainage efforts prioritize desilting sewer lines with jet and suction machines to avert waterlogging from clogs, while street lighting services include ward-level installation and maintenance under borough offices.[38] [39] Vital records registration occurs at central and sub-offices, supporting online applications for birth and death certificates to document demographic events.[40] HMC addresses density-driven pressures in slum areas, where 733 sites exist citywide including 22 on municipal land, through targeted rehabilitation via infrastructure for sanitation, water, and housing under AMRUT and PMAY schemes, plus skill training to enhance economic viability without relying on equity-based subsidies.[5] [41] Implementation data reveals gaps, such as ongoing needs for waste and sewage action plans amid population strains, per oversight directives.[42]

Urban Development and Maintenance Responsibilities

The Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) oversees the approval and regulation of building construction through a structured permitting process, sanctioning plans for structures up to ground plus two floors (G+2) via borough offices and higher elevations through the central head office, in alignment with the Howrah Municipal Act of 1980.[43] This includes scrutiny for compliance with land use and development control plans, such as the 2003 Land Use and Development Control Plan (LUDCP) notified for the corporation's jurisdiction, which delineates zoning for residential, commercial, and industrial areas spanning 63.55 square kilometers.[44] Building permits require submission of architectural drawings, structural stability certificates, and fees calculated via multiplying factors based on plot size and usage, with online processing facilitated through the state's Online Building Plan Approval System (OBPAS) since 2022.[45] Road development and maintenance form a core capital-intensive function, encompassing construction using bituminous, mastic asphalt, and concrete methods, alongside restoration of surfaces damaged by utility excavations from entities like the Calcutta Electric Supply Corporation (CESC).[46] In September 2025, HMC partnered with the Kolkata Metropolitan Development Authority (KMDA) to allocate approximately ₹40 crore for repairing deteriorated roads citywide, targeting pothole-prone arterial and local thoroughfares ahead of seasonal monsoons.[47] These efforts prioritize industrial zones, where Howrah's dense manufacturing clusters—historically centered on jute mills and engineering units—demand reinforced infrastructure to support over 21,441 persons per square kilometer as per 2011 census data, with ongoing tenders for cement concrete renovations in high-traffic wards.[3] Public health infrastructure development under HMC involves establishing and upgrading urban primary health centers (UPHCs), with 15 operational facilities providing outpatient services and immunization, strategically located to cover the corporation's 66 wards.[48] Capital projects, often funded through state urban development allocations from the Department of Urban Development & Municipal Affairs, include expansions tied to population pressures in mixed-use areas, though metrics like ward-level coverage remain integrated with broader preventive health mandates rather than standalone metrics.[49] Such initiatives historically emphasize industrial vicinity enhancements to mitigate occupational health risks in Howrah's factory-dense locales, distinct from routine operational upkeep.[50]

Elections and Political Landscape

Historical Election Outcomes

The Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC), originally constituted as Howrah Municipality before its upgrade and expansion, experienced shifts in political control across elections held under the supervision of the West Bengal State Election Commission. Early post-independence polls reflected competition among the Indian National Congress (INC), Communist Party of India (Marxist)-led Left Front, and emerging parties, with mayoral tenures indicating periods of dominance by individual alliances. For instance, Alokedut Das served as mayor from 1984 to 1989, followed by Swadesh Chakraborty from 1989 to 1999, during which Left-leaning governance prevailed amid broader state-level CPI(M) influence, though specific ward-wise data from these eras show fragmented wins rather than outright sweeps.[13] Subsequent leadership under Subinoy Ghosh (1999-2000) transitioned to Gopal Mukherjee's extended term from 2000 to 2008, aligned with INC control that secured key wards in the 2008 municipal elections, where INC captured 9 wards compared to 7 for the All India Trinamool Congress (TMC), underscoring Congress's lingering local foothold despite state-wide Left dominance until 2011.[13][51] This period featured relatively low contestation in some wards, with voter turnout often below 60% as reported in commission records, contributing to stable but contested governance continuity under INC mayors who prioritized basic infrastructure amid urban pressures. The 2013 municipal general elections marked a pivotal TMC sweep in Howrah, enabling the party to form the corporation board after decisively routing the Left Front and INC, a pattern linked to TMC's rising state-level momentum post-2011 assembly victory and reflecting voter preference for development promises over prior administrations' records.[52][53] Ward-wise results from the West Bengal State Election Commission detailed TMC's capture of a majority of the 50+ wards, with turnout around 65%, signaling a causal break from multi-party fragmentation to single-party hegemony that ensured administrative continuity.[54] In parallel, the 2015 elections for Bally Municipality—prior to its merger into HMC—saw TMC secure all wards, wresting control from opposition holdouts and reinforcing the party's dominance in peripheral Howrah areas through high voter mobilization and minimal effective challenges.[55][56] This outcome, with reported turnout exceeding 70% in contested segments, presaged the integrated HMC's post-merger stability under TMC, where low opposition contestation in subsequent cycles perpetuated governance patterns favoring incumbency over alternation.[54]

Delays and Legal Interventions in Recent Elections

The Howrah Municipal Corporation has not conducted full elections since 2013, when the Trinamool Congress secured a sweep, leading to over a decade of administrator-led governance amid repeated procedural hurdles.[57] This extended stasis stems from administrative and legal delays, including boundary adjustments and disputed notifications by the West Bengal State Election Commission (WBSEC).[58] In January 2021, the Calcutta High Court directed the West Bengal government to hold Howrah Municipal Corporation polls "as soon as possible," citing the expiration of the prior board's term and the need to restore elected representation.[59] However, implementation faltered with the November 2021 amendment to the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, which bifurcated Bally Municipality from the corporation's jurisdiction just weeks before proposed polling dates.[17] This pre-poll restructuring, approved by the governor on December 25, 2021, necessitated fresh delimitation and voter list revisions, effectively postponing unified elections.[19] The WBSEC's December 2021 notification scheduled municipal polls for other bodies but excluded Howrah, prompting Calcutta High Court benches to intervene. On December 31, 2021, the court ordered the WBSEC and state government to file affidavits explaining the omission, highlighting procedural irregularities in delimitation and scheduling.[60] A follow-up hearing on January 4, 2022, reiterated demands for justification, underscoring the exclusion's incompatibility with constitutional mandates for timely urban local body elections under Article 243U.[61] These interventions exposed systemic delays but did not yield immediate polls, as bifurcation-related challenges persisted into 2022 and beyond.[62] As of mid-2024, Howrah remained among 15 pending West Bengal civic bodies without notified elections, with administrator rule continuing amid unresolved legal and administrative prerequisites.[62] This pattern reflects broader critiques of state-level election management, where last-minute boundary changes and selective notifications have prolonged unelected governance without verified emergencies justifying the extensions.[58]

Controversies and Criticisms

Financial Management Issues

An ongoing audit of the Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) as of October 2025 has uncovered multiple missing accounts and financial irregularities, leading the auditor to withhold an opinion on the financial statements.[63] This follows earlier audit reports available up to fiscal year 2021-22, with no public resolution of prior discrepancies noted on official records.[64] HMC's revenue from property taxes has been consistently strained by collection shortfalls, particularly in densely populated areas prone to evasion and under-assessment; for fiscal 2019-20, projected property tax revenue was Rs. 30.75 crore, but actual collections reached only Rs. 22.59 crore, representing a 26.5% deficit.[65] Historical assessments, such as in 2002, highlighted systemic under-collection, with billed property taxes at Rs. 4 crore against a potential Rs. 50 crore due to evasion and outdated valuations.[66] CRISIL Ratings assigned a provisional 'BBB+/Stable' to HMC's bond issuance in May 2023, citing moderate risk from operational inefficiencies, weak revenue realization, and dependence on state grants, offset partially by an escrow mechanism from tax receipts.[28] By March 2025, the rating was downgraded to 'B/Stable Issuer Not Cooperating' from 'BB+/Stable', reflecting heightened liquidity risks, inadequate financial disclosures, and non-cooperation in rating reviews, which underscores deteriorating fiscal discipline.[67] The absence of elected boards since approximately 2020, due to delayed elections—prompting a Calcutta High Court directive in January 2021 for immediate polls—has coincided with reduced oversight on expenditures, enabling unscrutinized spending patterns amid administrator-led governance.[59] Bond issuances, secured via escrowed user charges and taxes, have provided short-term liquidity but expose vulnerabilities when collections falter, as evidenced by the rating trajectory.[68]

Service Delivery Failures and Public Complaints

The National Green Tribunal (NGT) directed the Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) on December 7, 2024, to submit an affidavit within 10 days addressing complaints regarding uncollected garbage, with a warning of penal action for non-compliance.[69] Earlier, on October 5, 2024, the NGT criticized HMC for inadequate solid waste management, noting leachate contamination of the Hooghly River and heightened public health risks from unmanaged dumps.[70] Persistent waterlogging due to clogged drains has been a recurring issue, exacerbated by inadequate maintenance; for instance, areas like Pilkhana and Tikiapara experienced prolonged flooding in September 2025 from blocked drainage systems combined with poor waste disposal.[71] Road infrastructure failures, including potholes, have led to safety hazards and mobility disruptions, as seen in Liluah's Kumar Para Road, where residents petitioned HMC in May 2025 for urgent repairs amid water accumulation and structural damage.[72] Similar complaints in Rajaram Das Lane reported eight months of stagnation by February 2025, prompting resident protests over unresponsive remediation efforts.[73] Of HMC's 733 identified slums, 640 are situated on private land, hindering municipal enforcement of sanitation and waste removal standards, which contributes to elevated health risks from open defecation and uncollected refuse without consistent intervention.[5] This distribution—contrasted with only 22 slums on HMC-owned land—limits direct oversight, resulting in normalized inefficiencies in basic hygiene services across densely populated areas.[5]

Political Influence and Governance Challenges

The Trinamool Congress (TMC) has maintained dominant political influence over the Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) since sweeping the 2013 elections, securing all 50 wards without significant opposition challenge.[57] Following the expiration of the elected board's term in December 2018, HMC transitioned to governance by an unelected board of administrators appointed by the West Bengal state government, a status that persisted for over six years as of mid-2024.[57] [59] This prolonged absence of elected representation, amid TMC's unchallenged local hegemony, has enabled extended non-democratic administration, reducing incentives for timely polls and highlighting state-level reluctance to relinquish administrative control.[74] Legal disputes have underscored these governance challenges, with the Calcutta High Court directing the state on January 29, 2021, to conduct HMC elections "as soon as possible" due to the unconstitutional prolongation of the administrator board.[59] Despite this, polls remained deferred, entangled in administrative maneuvers such as the 2015 merger of Bally Municipality into HMC—which expanded its wards from 50 to 66—and the subsequent 2021 bifurcation via legislative amendment, assented by the Governor on December 25, 2021, to restore Bally as a separate entity.[17] [19] Critics, including opposition petitions, have argued that such boundary adjustments served as pre-poll strategies for vote bank management under TMC stewardship, further delaying democratic renewal and inviting High Court scrutiny on exclusion from 2022 municipal election schedules.[60] [61] In the wider West Bengal municipal context, TMC's partisan dominance has correlated with patterns of election-related delays and violence in contested polls, though HMC's extended unelected phase uniquely amplifies accountability deficits by insulating administrators from voter oversight and enabling unchecked resource allocation aligned with party priorities.[75] This structure fosters governance inertia, as evidenced by repeated judicial interventions against state inaction, contrasting with constitutional mandates for periodic elections and perpetuating a cycle where one-party control discourages competitive electoral processes.[74] [76]

Recent Developments

Post-2020 Administrative Changes

In response to ongoing delays in municipal elections exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic and legal challenges, the Howrah Municipal Corporation (HMC) continued to operate under a Board of Administrators appointed by the state government, with IAS and WBCS officers overseeing functions following the lapse of the previous elected term in 2019.[77] This administrative arrangement, initially extended in mid-2020, persisted into 2021 amid unresolved election scheduling, as the West Bengal State Election Commission faced court directives to clarify exclusions of HMC from broader municipal poll notifications.[60] A significant structural shift occurred on November 12, 2021, when Bally Municipality was demerged from HMC through amendments to the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, reversing its 2015 amalgamation and reducing HMC's jurisdiction over 16 wards. The West Bengal Assembly passed the Howrah Municipal Corporation (Amendment) Bill on November 17, 2021, with Governor Jagdeep Dhankhar granting assent on December 25, 2021, following prolonged scrutiny over the bifurcation's timing just before proposed civic polls.[19][17] The Calcutta High Court intensified oversight in late 2021, directing affidavits from the state government and election commission on December 31, 2021, to explain HMC's exclusion from the municipal election schedule, highlighting procedural lapses tied to the Bally separation and governor's pending approvals.[60] This judicial intervention prolonged the administrator-led governance, with verifiable extensions ensuring operational continuity but contributing to delays in ward-specific planning and resource reallocation post-bifurcation.[61] These changes disrupted service delivery timelines, as the bifurcation required redrawing administrative boundaries and reallocating 63.55 square kilometers of prior HMC area, stalling integrated projects like waste management and infrastructure upgrades pending legal finalization and board approvals.[9] The absence of elected representatives until further notice amplified reliance on state directives, with operational impacts evident in deferred civic initiatives amid the transitional uncertainties.[57]

Ongoing Reforms and Audits

In 2024, the West Bengal state government enacted the Howrah Municipal Corporation (Amendment) Act, which modifies the 1980 Howrah Municipal Corporation Act to introduce regulations governing city services delivery, public utility buildings, building plan sanction processes, and coordination mechanisms between municipal bodies.[78][79] The amendments, assented to by the Governor on March 20, 2024, aim to streamline administrative procedures and address operational inefficiencies in urban infrastructure management.[80] These changes followed clearance of the pending bill, enabling smoother fiscal and regulatory implementation amid prior delays in gubernatorial assent.[80] An antecedent reform occurred via an amendment to Schedule V of the 1980 Act, effective August 1, 2023, which revised provisions related to fees, charges, and procedural schedules for municipal operations.[81] Complementing these legislative updates, the Personnel & Administrative Reforms Department of West Bengal appointed Dhaval Jain, an IAS officer of the 2014 batch, as Commissioner of the Howrah Municipal Corporation on October 17, 2023, to oversee executive functions and drive administrative enhancements.[82] Financial audits remain a statutory requirement under section 78(d) of the Howrah Municipal Corporation Act, with reports detailing annual financial statements and compliance observations.[83] The latest publicly available audit covers the fiscal years 2018-19 through 2021-22, highlighting accounting practices, revenue collection, and expenditure verification without noting systemic irregularities beyond routine comments.[64][83] Subsequent annual audits for 2022-23 and beyond proceed as mandated, though detailed reports for these periods are not yet published on official platforms.[64] Historical Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG) reviews have flagged isolated issues, such as a ₹1.16 crore penalty incurred due to delayed service tax payments in prior years, underscoring the need for timely fiscal compliance.
