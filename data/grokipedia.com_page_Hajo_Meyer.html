Hajo Meyer

Hajo Meyer (12 August 1924 – 23 August 2014) was a German-born Dutch physicist, Auschwitz concentration camp survivor, and anti-Zionist activist whose post-retirement advocacy focused on critiquing Israeli policies toward Palestinians by drawing parallels to his experiences under Nazi persecution.[1][2] Born in Bielefeld, Germany, to Jewish parents, Meyer fled alone to the Netherlands in 1939 at age 14 following the Kristallnacht pogroms, but was arrested by the Gestapo in 1944 and deported to Auschwitz-Birkenau, where he endured forced labor until the camp's liberation by Soviet forces in 1945.[3][4] After the war, he studied physics in Amsterdam and joined Philips, rising to become director of the company's Natuurkundig Laboratorium research facility before retiring and pursuing woodworking, including the construction of violins and violas.[1] In his later years, Meyer co-founded the Dutch-Jewish organization Een Ander Joods Geluid (A Different Jewish Voice) and authored An Ethical Tradition Betrayed: The End of Judaism, in which he argued that Zionism and Israeli state actions toward Palestinians represented a betrayal of Judaism's universal ethical principles, equating aspects of Israel's security policies—such as checkpoints and wall construction—with the dehumanization tactics he witnessed in Nazi-occupied Europe.[5][2] His public speeches, including tours titled "Never Again for Anyone," emphasized empathy for Palestinian youth based on his own victimization, but provoked sharp backlash, including accusations of antisemitism from pro-Israel groups who contended that such analogies minimized the Holocaust's uniqueness and delegitimized Israel's defensive measures.[6][7]

Early Life and Holocaust Experience

Childhood and Family Background

Hajo Meyer, born Hans Joachim Gustav Meyer on 12 August 1924 in Bielefeld, Germany, was the son of Therese Melchior and Gustav Meyer, a notary who had served in the First World War.[8] His family belonged to the Jewish community but maintained a secular lifestyle with limited religious observance, reflecting the assimilated middle-class status common among many German Jews at the time.[9] Meyer's early childhood coincided with the rise of the Nazi regime following its seizure of power in January 1933, when he was eight years old, introducing immediate societal exclusion for Jews through discriminatory laws and propaganda.[2] This escalated after the November 1938 Kristallnacht pogrom, forcing him to quit grammar school in Bielefeld as Jewish students were barred from public education.[2][10]

Flight to the Netherlands and Deportation

In January 1939, at age 14, Hajo Meyer was sent alone by his parents from Bielefeld, Germany, to the Netherlands via a Kindertransport convoy, departing on 4 January and arriving shortly thereafter.[11][12] This separation occurred amid escalating Nazi persecution following Kristallnacht in November 1938, which barred him from attending school in Germany.[13] His parents remained in Germany and were later deported, perishing in the Holocaust.[14] Meyer settled into life in the Netherlands, initially under foster care arrangements typical for unaccompanied refugee children, though specific placements are undocumented in available records.[15] The German invasion of the Netherlands in May 1940 initiated systematic anti-Jewish measures, including registration, curfews, and property confiscations, prompting many Jews, including Meyer, to seek refuge in hiding networks by 1943 as deportations intensified.[16] In 1943, Meyer joined the Dutch underground, evading capture for approximately one year through concealed living arrangements. He was arrested by the Gestapo in March 1944 and deported to Auschwitz concentration camp within a week, initiating his ten-month imprisonment there.[4][2]

Imprisonment and Survival in Auschwitz

Meyer was arrested by the Gestapo in March 1944 while in hiding in the Netherlands and deported to Auschwitz-Birkenau approximately one week later.[2] Upon arrival, as a 19-year-old male deemed fit for labor during the selection process on the ramp, he was spared immediate extermination and assigned prisoner number 184981, marked with a tattoo on his arm.[17] He was transferred to Auschwitz III-Monowitz, a subcamp established for forced labor at the IG Farben synthetic rubber and fuel plant, where prisoners endured grueling 12-hour shifts in hazardous conditions, including chemical exposure and construction work.[17] Daily rations consisted of meager portions—typically a watery soup, small bread ration, and ersatz coffee—leading to widespread starvation; Meyer himself lost weight to 35 kilograms (77 pounds) after 10 months of imprisonment.[18] Routine brutality included beatings by SS guards and Kapos, as well as periodic selections where unfit prisoners were sent to the gas chambers at Birkenau; survival often hinged on physical endurance, luck in avoiding selections, and minimal acts of solidarity among inmates, such as sharing scraps of food.[17] In late January 1945, as Soviet forces advanced, Meyer was among evacuees forced on death marches from Monowitz to subcamps like Blechhammer, involving days of walking in freezing conditions with minimal provisions, during which many perished from exhaustion, exposure, or execution.[17] He was liberated by the Soviet Red Army at Blechhammer on January 26, 1945, in a severely weakened state requiring months of recovery.[17] Of his immediate family—parents and sister—all perished in the Holocaust, leaving him as the sole survivor.[2]

Scientific Education and Career

Physics Training and Degrees

Following his survival and liberation from Auschwitz-Birkenau in January 1945, Meyer returned to the Netherlands later that year amid the immediate postwar reconstruction and enrolled at the University of Amsterdam (then known as the Municipal University) to study theoretical physics under Professor J. de Boer.[19] Despite the disruptions of his adolescence due to Nazi persecution, which had barred him from formal schooling after 1938, Meyer demonstrated academic determination by pursuing advanced scientific training in a field requiring rigorous mathematical and experimental foundations.[1] Meyer completed his doctoral research in theoretical physics, earning a PhD that positioned him for subsequent roles in industrial research, including at Philips Natuurkundig Laboratorium (NatLab).[20] His graduate work focused on topics aligned with postwar advancements in solid-state and applied physics, reflecting the era's emphasis on rebuilding scientific infrastructure in Europe.[19] This formal education marked Meyer's transition from Holocaust survivor to professional physicist, underscoring his capacity to rebuild a career in a demanding discipline.[1]

Research Contributions and Professional Roles

Hajo Meyer earned a doctorate in theoretical solid-state physics from Delft University of Technology in the early 1950s, following his post-war studies in the Netherlands.[19] He joined Philips Research Laboratories (NatLab) in Eindhoven shortly thereafter, initially focusing on experimental techniques in solid-state physics and materials science as part of the organization's broad R&D efforts in electronics and semiconductors.[19] By 1964, Meyer had advanced to group leader within NatLab, overseeing teams in physics research pertinent to industrial applications such as cathode ray tubes and early semiconductor devices.[19] His career progressed to section director, where he contributed to the evaluation and prioritization of optical precision technologies, recognizing their potential for high-resolution lithography and data storage systems. In the early 1970s, as a key decision-maker, Meyer supported investments in laser-based optical projects that laid foundational work for the compact disc (CD) technology and the wafer stepper systems later commercialized through ASML, a Philips spin-off.[21] Meyer ultimately served as managing director of the Philips Physics Laboratory (NatLab) until his retirement in 1984, directing a multidisciplinary team of researchers in advancing materials characterization and device physics amid Philips' global expansion in consumer electronics.[19] Under his leadership, NatLab maintained its role as Philips' central hub for fundamental physics research, producing innovations in solid-state devices without publicly documented personal patents or peer-reviewed papers attributable to Meyer in academic journals.[21] His professional legacy centers on administrative and strategic oversight rather than individual experimental breakthroughs, aligning with the applied nature of industrial physics at the time.[19]

Shift to Political Activism

Retirement and Initial Engagement with Jewish Issues

Meyer retired in 1984 from his position as director of the Philips Natuurkundig Laboratorium (NatLab), the company's physics research facility in Eindhoven, Netherlands, where he had worked since 1950 following his studies in theoretical physics.[22][19] This transition provided personal stability, allowing him to pursue woodworking courses in England and craft violins and violas as a hobby.[14] In the post-retirement period, Meyer's attention turned toward reflections on Jewish survival amid the ethical frameworks of Judaism, prompted by the widespread reticence among Holocaust survivors to publicly address their traumas in the decades following World War II. These contemplations marked the onset of his interest in contemporary Jewish identity, distinct from his earlier focus on scientific pursuits. He began aligning with Jewish organizations emphasizing peace and moral consistency, including an early role on the board of A Different Jewish Voice (Een Ander Joods Geluid), a Dutch group formed in 2001 to foster dialogue within Jewish communities on ethical matters.[23][24] This affiliation represented his preliminary step into communal discussions on Jewish issues, prior to more extensive public involvement.

Development of Views on Zionism and Israel

Meyer's formative years in a secular, humanist German-Jewish milieu instilled an initial antagonism toward Zionism, which he and many contemporaries viewed as incompatible with universal ethical principles rooted in Reform Judaism. This pre-Holocaust perspective emphasized Judaism's prophetic tradition of justice and empathy for the oppressed, irrespective of tribe or nation, over nationalist separatism.[18] Postwar, as a survivor, Meyer temporarily aligned with pro-Israel sentiments, accepting state narratives of necessity for Jewish security amid lingering trauma. His views shifted decisively from the late 1960s onward, catalyzed by Israel's post-1967 territorial expansions and policies he interpreted as systematically displacing Palestinians to achieve maximal land control with minimal Arab population—a dynamic he attributed to a "Holocaust religion" prioritizing power retention over moral restraint. This evolution drew on his direct encounters with coercive authority in Nazi Germany, prompting first-principles scrutiny of causation: where vulnerability once demanded ethical universalism, Zionism appeared to invert it into tribal exceptionalism.[6][18] By the 1980s and 1990s, deepened engagement with historical analyses of 1948 events and responses to operations like Sabra and Shatila reinforced Meyer's conviction that Zionism betrayed Judaism's core by subordinating prophetic ethics—centered on tikkun olam (repairing the world) and prohibitions against mimicking tormentors—to realpolitik dominance. He argued that Israel's reliance on blockades, demolitions, and segregation echoed pre-deportation ghettoization tactics, not as identical replication but as a causal failure to transcend power-based oppression through justice-oriented governance.[6][25] This reasoning crystallized in Meyer's formulation of Zionism as inherently xenophobic and colonialist, fostering dehumanization via an "us versus them" binary that contravened Judaism's biologically evolved inter-human empathy. The resultant credo, "Never Again for Anyone," reframed Holocaust imperatives universally, rejecting selective tribal application in favor of prohibiting suffering for any group—a principle Meyer traced to ethical causality wherein unchecked victimhood risks perpetuating cycles of abuse.[6][25]

Advocacy Activities

Publications on Ethics and Judaism

Hajo Meyer authored The End of Judaism: An Ethical Tradition Betrayed, first published in English in 2007 by G. Meyer Books, with the original Dutch edition Het einde van het Jodendom appearing in 2003.[5] In this 264-page work, Meyer contends that Zionism and the policies of the State of Israel represent a profound betrayal of Judaism's prophetic ethical tradition, which he characterizes as rooted in universal moral imperatives such as the Golden Rule and prophets' calls for justice toward the oppressed.[5] He argues that the doctrine of Jews as the "chosen people" fosters tribal exceptionalism, enabling Israeli actions against Palestinians that echo the incremental dehumanization Jews faced under early Nazi policies in Germany and the Netherlands during the 1930s.[5][26] Central to Meyer's thesis is the assertion that authentic Jewish ethics, as derived from Holocaust survival, demand empathy for all victims of oppression, yet Zionism supplants this with nationalist self-interest, eroding Judaism's universalist core.[27] He draws on his Auschwitz experiences to claim that true moral authority stems from rejecting power-based ethics in favor of prophetic non-violence, positioning Israel's security measures as a moral inversion that prioritizes survival through dominance over ethical consistency.[28] Meyer explicitly separates Zionism from Judaism, viewing the former as a political ideology that undermines the latter's ethical foundations by promoting separation and exclusion rather than prophetic solidarity with the weak.[5] The book received limited academic or mainstream Jewish scholarly review, with engagement primarily in anti-Zionist and progressive outlets that praised its call for moral consistency in Jewish thought. For instance, it was highlighted in discussions advocating boycott of Israel as aligning with Jewish ethical imperatives against oppression.[18] Broader reception remained sparse, reflecting its niche appeal among critics of Israeli policy, though user ratings on platforms like Goodreads averaged approximately 4.0 from around 30 reviews, often commending its survivor's perspective on ethical betrayal.[27] No major peer-reviewed analyses in ethics or Jewish studies journals were identified, underscoring its marginalization in institutional discourse.[26]

Lectures and Speaking Tours

Meyer undertook international speaking tours from the late 2000s onward, primarily organized by the International Jewish Anti-Zionist Network (IJAN), targeting audiences in Europe, North America, and beyond to promote his critiques of Israeli policies through the lens of Holocaust lessons.[29][12] In 2010, he joined IJAN's "Never Again for Anyone" tour, which spanned 14 cities across Europe, including events in the UK and Ireland co-hosted with groups like the Scottish Palestine Solidarity Campaign; one session in Scotland drew attention for Meyer's assertions that Israel's actions echoed Nazi tactics, delivered to solidarity activist crowds.[29][30] The tour format paired Meyer with Palestinian speakers such as Haidar Eid, emphasizing universal application of Holocaust-derived ethical imperatives to contemporary conflicts.[10] These efforts extended to the United States in 2011, coinciding with International Holocaust Remembrance Day, with stops at universities including Rutgers on January 29 and the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee, where Meyer addressed student and community audiences on Israel's alleged misuse of Holocaust memory to justify apartheid-like policies.[31][32] Additional tours reached South Africa and other regions, often framed around rejecting selective interpretations of "Never Again" that exclude Palestinians.[33] Recurring messages highlighted moral inconsistencies in Jewish responses to oppression, urging opposition to fear-based defenses of state actions over empathy for the oppressed.[6]

Visits to Conflict Zones

In 2005, Meyer joined a delegation of former European politicians, headed by ex-Dutch Prime Minister Dries van Agt, on a tour of Israel, the Gaza Strip, and the West Bank, occurring shortly after Israel's unilateral settler withdrawal from Gaza.[34] The group examined conditions in the occupied territories, including Israeli settlements, military checkpoints, and movement restrictions imposed on Palestinians.[34] During these visits, Meyer personally observed the daily impacts of checkpoints, where Palestinian youth were frequently detained, hindering their access to education and free movement—a situation he likened to the incremental restrictions and harassment he endured as a Jewish teenager in Nazi-occupied Netherlands prior to his deportation.[6][35] He described such controls as contributing to a form of systemic oppression that stifled personal development, drawing from scenes of confined mobility in Gaza and the West Bank.[6] Meyer incorporated these eyewitness accounts into his advocacy efforts, referencing them in lectures to underscore ethical imperatives against policies he viewed as dehumanizing, thereby strengthening his calls for ending the occupation through direct testimony rather than abstract ideology.[6][35]

Controversies and Criticisms

Accusations of Antisemitism from Jewish Organizations

The Anti-Defamation League (ADL) condemned Hajo Meyer's participation in the 2011 "Never Again" U.S. speaking tour, where he headlined events comparing Israel's Gaza blockade to a "concentration camp" or "ghetto" and equating Israeli policies with Nazi tactics, arguing that such rhetoric inverts the Holocaust by depicting Jews and Israel as perpetrators akin to their historical oppressors.[31] Meyer specifically called Israeli Foreign Minister Avigdor Lieberman a "big friend of Hitler" during a January 29, 2011, appearance at Rutgers University, a statement the ADL highlighted as part of a pattern of demonizing Israel through "offensive Holocaust-related imagery." The organization viewed these analogies as exploiting Holocaust memory to vilify Zionism and portray Israelis as the "new Nazis," aligning with antisemitic tropes that equate Jewish self-defense with historical genocide.[31] HonestReporting, a media watchdog group focused on combating antisemitism, directly labeled Meyer's Nazi comparisons as antisemitic in response to his January 2010 UK tour statements asserting that "Israel acts like Nazis," contending that invoking such parallels by a Holocaust survivor delegitimizes Israel while reviving blood libel-like accusations against Jews.[36] Critics within Jewish organizations argued that Meyer's use of his Auschwitz survivor status amplified these claims, effectively weaponizing Holocaust testimony to portray Israel as morally equivalent to the regime that murdered six million Jews, thereby minimizing the unique historical hatred directed at Jewish victims.[36][31] Meyer's redefinition of antisemitism—stating in 2010 that it had shifted from hatred of Jews "because they were Jews" to merely "somebody who Zionists don't like"—drew accusations from pro-Israel Jewish commentators of trivializing millennia of pogroms, expulsions, and genocide against Jews by conflating legitimate criticism with existential threats.[10] Such remarks were seen as aligning with narratives that dismiss Jewish concerns about antisemitism as politically motivated smears, further eroding distinctions between anti-Zionism and antisemitic delegitimization of the Jewish state.[36]

Meyer's Defenses and Redefinition of Antisemitism

Meyer contended that the contemporary accusation of antisemitism had diverged from its historical meaning, which involved hatred toward Jews based on their ethnicity or supposed inherent traits. He stated, "Formerly an anti-Semite was somebody who hated Jews because they were Jews and had a Jewish soul. But nowadays an anti-Semite is somebody who is hated by Jews."[6] This redefinition, in his view, transformed the term into a tool primarily deployed against those voicing opposition to Israeli government policies, rather than addressing genuine racial or religious prejudice akin to Nazi ideology.[30] In defending himself against such charges, Meyer emphasized that criticism of state actions, including those of Israel, did not equate to animosity toward Jewish people as a whole. He maintained that labeling Israel critics as antisemites served to shield abuses of power from scrutiny, distinct from traditional antisemitism rooted in collective hatred.[6] His framework prioritized adherence to universal ethical principles over ethnic or national allegiance, arguing that true moral consistency demanded applying standards of justice impartially, without exemption for one's own group.[37] Meyer rooted this perspective in what he described as the prophetic tradition of Judaism, which he interpreted as mandating the defense of the oppressed and the pursuit of righteousness for all humanity, irrespective of identity. Raised in a secular German-Jewish milieu influenced by these ideals, he saw deviations toward tribal loyalty as a betrayal of Judaism's core ethical imperatives, such as protecting the weak and advocating peace.[37] This causal reasoning positioned challenges to perceived injustices, even when directed at Jewish-led institutions, as aligned with—rather than opposed to—authentic Jewish values, thereby reframing antisemitism accusations as obstacles to principled discourse.[10]

Broader Critiques of His Analogies to the Holocaust

Critics of Hajo Meyer's analogies, including historians and Jewish intellectuals, contend that they overlook core empirical distinctions between the Nazi regime's genocidal intent and Israel's defensive military actions. The Holocaust involved the systematic extermination of six million Jews through industrialized killing methods, including gas chambers and death camps, driven by an ideological commitment to total annihilation without provocation from victims.[38] In contrast, Israel's conflicts arose from immediate threats, such as the 1947 Arab rejection of UN Partition Plan Resolution 181 and subsequent invasions by Arab states in 1948, followed by defensive wars in 1967 and 1973 where Israel faced existential risks from coalitions aiming to destroy it.[39] Meyer's comparisons, which equated Israeli security measures with pre-Holocaust Nazi persecution, elide this context of Arab-initiated aggression and rejectionism, presenting Israel as aggressor despite its lack of expansionist or exterminationist doctrine.[40] Such parallels are further contested for inverting the victim-perpetrator roles central to Holocaust memory, potentially reflecting psychological projection from survivor trauma but resulting in a false moral equivalence. Historians argue that Nazi policies disarmed and isolated stateless Jews, precluding resistance, whereas Israel operates as a democracy defending a sovereign population against repeated attacks, including rocket barrages and terrorism.[41] This reversal, as noted by commentator Jay Michaelson, ignores counterfactuals like Jewish self-defense capabilities under Nazi rule, which Meyer’s framework dismisses in favor of portraying Zionism as inherently dehumanizing.[40] Broader scholarly critiques emphasize that these analogies erode the Holocaust's historical uniqueness as a singular genocide, facilitating narratives that empirically correlate with rises in antisemitic incidents. By likening a Jewish state born from Holocaust ashes to its perpetrators, such rhetoric aids anti-Zionist campaigns that, per analyses of global trends, coincide with spikes in violence against Jews unrelated to Israeli policy.[42] Institutions tracking antisemitism, including those examining European academia where Meyer lectured, document how such inversions absolve historical perpetrators' descendants of guilt while delegitimizing Jewish self-determination.[43] This distortion, rather than advancing ethical discourse, aligns with patterns where Holocaust minimization precedes broader prejudice, as evidenced in post-2000 data linking inversion tropes to increased hate crimes.[39]

Death and Posthumous Assessment

Final Years and Health

In his later years, Hajo Meyer resided in Heiloo, Netherlands, where he continued limited public engagements despite advancing age and diminishing health.[3] By 2014, at age 90, Meyer described himself as tired during interactions, reflecting physical frailty amid his persistent advocacy.[3] On July 29, 2014, Meyer granted what became his final interview at his Heiloo home, reaffirming his criticisms of Israeli policies and urging Palestinians to persist in their resistance to maintain self-esteem, stating, "If you give up, you might lose your self-esteem with the ongoing oppression."[3] This discussion, conducted amid Israel's military operations in Gaza, underscored his unwavering views even as health constraints limited his travel and speaking.[3] Meyer died in his sleep on August 23, 2014, in Heiloo, six days after his 90th birthday, from natural causes attributed to age-related disease.[44][45] His passing followed a period of reduced activity, marking the end of a life shaped by survival, scientific career, and contentious activism.[44]

Legacy in Scientific and Activist Contexts

Meyer's scientific legacy centers on his applied physics work and administrative leadership rather than groundbreaking theoretical advancements. Early in his career, he researched semiconductor physics and gas discharge phenomena, fields central to post-war electronics development.[46] He spent two decades in management at Philips Research Laboratories in Eindhoven, Netherlands, rising to director of the Natuurkundig Laboratorium (NatLab), where he oversaw multidisciplinary research programs guiding technical innovation.[19] [8] These efforts contributed indirectly to semiconductor manufacturing technologies, including early photolithography systems that influenced subsequent industrial tools.[47] After retiring in 1984, Meyer shifted to acoustics, publishing analyses of arched violin and viola top plates' vibrational frequencies, providing semi-empirical models for predicting thickness and mass from mode frequencies to assist instrument makers.[46] He also examined guitar tone quality, identifying key modes like the (0,1) as determinants of instrument performance.[46] Posthumously, his instrument acoustics papers receive niche citations in lutherie and vibration studies, but his broader physics influence remains tied to Philips-era management rather than seminal discoveries.[46] In activist contexts, Meyer's posthumous impact endures primarily within anti-Zionist and pro-Palestinian Jewish networks, where his reframing of Holocaust lessons as universally applicable—"Never Again for Anyone"—challenges perceived exceptionalism in Jewish responses to oppression.[23] As director of A Different Jewish Voice (Een Ander Joods Geluid), a Dutch group advocating criticism of Israeli policies, he promoted BDS initiatives and authored works like The End of Judaism (2003), arguing that Zionism betrayed ethical Jewish traditions by mirroring persecutory tactics.[8] [12] His speaking tours, including with the International Jewish Anti-Zionist Network, reached audiences in Europe and North America, emphasizing empathy for Palestinians based on his Auschwitz experiences.[12] Following his death on August 23, 2014, at age 90, Meyer's ideas persist in dissident discourse, cited in analyses of Zionism's ethical implications and defenses against antisemitism charges.[3] [48] However, mainstream Jewish organizations and pro-Israel commentators dismiss his analogies between Israeli actions and Nazi policies as inflammatory distortions, limiting his influence to fringe or left-leaning activist circles prone to such critiques.[49] His legacy thus highlights tensions in Jewish identity debates, with supporters viewing him as a principled dissenter and detractors as enabling anti-Jewish narratives under survivor authority.[48][49]
