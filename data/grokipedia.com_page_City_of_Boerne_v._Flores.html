City of Boerne v. Flores

City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U.S. 507 (1997), was a United States Supreme Court decision that invalidated the application of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA) to state and local governments, ruling that Congress had exceeded its remedial authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment.[1][2] The case arose from a dispute over a church's expansion plans in Boerne, Texas, where local zoning officials denied a building permit to St. Peter the Apostle Catholic Church due to historic preservation concerns, prompting Archbishop P.F. Flores to challenge the denial under RFRA.[2][3] In a 6-3 majority opinion authored by Justice Anthony Kennedy, the Court held that RFRA's requirement of strict scrutiny for neutral laws of general applicability substantially burdening religious exercise represented an improper substantive alteration of constitutional rights rather than a permissible remedy for discrimination.[1][2] This ruling reaffirmed the principles from Employment Division v. Smith (1990), which had established that such neutral laws do not violate the Free Exercise Clause absent intentional targeting of religion.[4][2] While RFRA remained intact for federal actions, the decision prompted Congress to enact the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA) in 2000, tailoring protections through Congress's Spending and Commerce Clause powers to address land use restrictions and prisoner rights without directly overriding state sovereignty in the same manner.[2][5] The case underscored the judiciary's role in interpreting constitutional protections, limiting legislative attempts to redefine substantive rights under the guise of enforcement legislation.[1]

Historical and Legal Background

Employment Division v. Smith and Free Exercise Precedent

In Employment Division, Department of Human Resources of Oregon v. Smith, decided on April 17, 1990, the U.S. Supreme Court addressed whether Oregon's denial of unemployment benefits to two Native American employees violated the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment.[6] Alfred Smith and Galen Black, counselors at a private drug and alcohol rehabilitation center, were discharged after testing positive for peyote, a hallucinogenic substance they ingested during a sacramental ceremony of the Native American Church.[7] Oregon law criminalized peyote possession and use without exception for religious practices, classifying the employees' conduct as disqualifying misconduct for unemployment eligibility.[8] The state court initially awarded benefits, finding no violation of state policy, but the Oregon Supreme Court reversed, upholding the denial under the neutral criminal prohibition.[6] The Supreme Court, in a 6â€“3 majority opinion authored by Justice Antonin Scalia, held that neutral and generally applicable laws do not violate the Free Exercise Clause merely because they incidentally burden religious practices, even if those practices are central to sincerely held beliefs.[6] The Court rejected the application of strict scrutinyâ€”the compelling governmental interest and least restrictive means test established in Sherbert v. Verner (1963), where South Carolina's denial of unemployment benefits to a Seventh-day Adventist who refused Saturday work was deemed an unconstitutional burden absent a compelling justification.[9] Under Smith, such heightened scrutiny applies only if a law specifically targets religious conduct, lacks general applicability, or burdens religion in tandem with another constitutional violation, such as free speech restrictions.[7] Scalia emphasized that exempting religious objectors from neutral laws would court "anarchy," as it would undermine democratic processes and open the door to judicial second-guessing of legislative judgments.[8] The decision marked a doctrinal shift, overruling the Sherbert balancing approach for neutral laws and prioritizing legislative authority over individualized religious exemptions.[10] Justice Harry Blackmun's dissent, joined by Justices William Brennan and Thurgood Marshall, argued that this undermined protections for minority faiths, potentially allowing governments to impose incidental burdens without justification, as in the peyote case where no evidence showed harm from religious use.[6] The ruling provoked immediate and bipartisan backlash from religious organizations, civil liberties advocates, and lawmakers, who contended it eroded safeguards for religious minorities against evenhanded but burdensome regulations.[11] Groups including the American Civil Liberties Union, the National Council of Churches, and Orthodox Jewish organizations criticized Smith for leaving vulnerable communitiesâ€”like Amish facing compulsory education laws or Jews denied kosher slaughter exemptionsâ€”exposed without requiring governments to demonstrate overriding needs.[11] This outcry, spanning conservative evangelicals and liberal progressives, highlighted fears that the precedent diminished the Free Exercise Clause's role in accommodating sincere beliefs amid neutral policies.[12]

Enactment of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act

In direct response to the Supreme Court's decision in Employment Division v. Smith (1990), which held that neutral, generally applicable laws do not violate the Free Exercise Clause even if they substantially burden religious exercise, Congress introduced legislation to restore prior protections. The Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) was first introduced in the House as H.R. 1308 on March 11, 1993, by Representatives Charles E. Schumer (D-NY) and Christopher H. Smith (R-NJ), and in the Senate as S. 578 on March 11, 1993, by Senators Joseph R. Biden Jr. (D-DE) and Orrin G. Hatch (R-UT).[13] The bill advanced rapidly through Congress amid broad bipartisan support, reflecting concerns that the Smith ruling diminished safeguards for religious liberty. The Senate passed an amended version on October 27, 1993, by a vote of 97-3.[14] The House then approved the Senate version by unanimous voice vote on November 11, 1993.[15] President Bill Clinton signed RFRA into law on November 16, 1993, as Public Law 103-141.[16] RFRA's core purpose was to mandate that federal and state governments apply strict scrutiny to any law or regulation that substantially burdens a person's religious exercise, requiring proof of a compelling governmental interest and use of the least restrictive means to achieve it, irrespective of the law's neutrality or general applicability.[17] This standard echoed pre-Smith precedents like Sherbert v. Verner (1963), aiming to prevent undue governmental interference with sincerely held religious practices.[18] Congress's statutory findings asserted that post-Smith jurisprudence risked widespread burdens on religious exercise, including instances of persecution against minority faiths, and that neutral laws could impose equivalent harms to targeted ones without adequate judicial review.[19] These findings, drawn from hearings documenting perceived threats to religious communities such as Native American peyote rituals and Orthodox Jewish practices, underscored the legislative intent to broadly protect religious freedom beyond Smith's rational basis deference.[20]

Facts of the Case

Boerne's Historic Preservation Ordinance

The City of Boerne, Texas, a small municipality in Kendall County approximately 30 miles northwest of San Antonio with a population of about 6,000 residents in the early 1990s, adopted a historic preservation ordinance to safeguard its architectural and cultural legacy.[21] The ordinance established the Historic Landmark Commission, empowering it to review and approve proposed changes to structures within designated historic areas.[21] Its stated purpose was to "protect, enhance and perpetuate selected historic landmarks" and to "safeguard the City's historic and cultural heritage" through the preservation of "the historical and architectural characteristics of buildings and places significant in the history of the City, state or nation."[22] The ordinance applied broadly to all buildings over 50 years old located in the historic district, requiring owners to obtain a certificate of appropriateness prior to any exterior alterations, demolitions, or new constructions that could affect the district's character.[22][2] This included prohibitions on expansions or modifications that deviated from the prevailing architectural style, aiming to maintain the district's uniform historical integrity and prevent incompatible developments.[2] The Commission evaluated applications based on criteria such as compatibility with surrounding structures, preservation of original features, and overall contribution to the community's aesthetic and historical value.[21] As a facially neutral land-use regulation, the ordinance imposed no explicit religious exemptions or targeting, extending its requirements equally to secular and religious properties alike within the district boundaries.[21] This general applicability aligned with standard zoning practices for historic preservation, focusing on community-wide protection of built heritage rather than specific uses of land.[22] The district encompassed key areas of Boerne's downtown, including structures dating back to the city's founding in the mid-19th century as a German settler community.[23]

St. Peter the Apostle Church's Permit Application and Denial

St. Peter the Apostle Catholic Church, constructed in 1923, seated approximately 230 worshippers but proved insufficient for its parish, which had grown to more than 400 families by the early 1990s.[1] This expansion led to overcrowding, with 40 to 60 parishioners unable to attend some Sunday masses, inadequate space for the choir, and limited facilities for weddings, communicant classes, and other religious activities.[24] To accommodate the increased demand, the church sought to demolish the existing structure and rebuild a larger facility on the same site within Boerne's historic preservation district.[1] Archbishop Patrick Flores, on behalf of the Archdiocese of San Antonio, submitted a building permit application to the City of Boerne for this expansion project.[21] The city council denied the application in 1993, invoking the city's historic preservation ordinance to protect the district's architectural and historical character, which encompassed the church property.[1] The denial rested on neutral zoning criteria without evidence of discriminatory intent toward the church's religious practices.[1] In 1994, Archbishop Flores filed suit against the city in the United States District Court for the Western District of Texas, contending that the permit rejection substantially burdened the church's religious exercise in violation of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993.[21]

Lower Court Proceedings

District Court Decision

In Flores v. City of Boerne, the United States District Court for the Western District of Texas held on March 15, 1995, that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) was unconstitutional insofar as it applied to state and local governments, ruling that Congress exceeded its authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment.[25][22] Judge Lucius D. Bunton III, in granting the city's motion to dismiss the RFRA claim, determined that RFRA did not merely enforce or remedy violations of the Free Exercise Clause but substantively altered the constitutional protections by restoring a stricter compelling interest test and shifting the burden of proof from that established in Employment Division v. Smith (1990).[25][22] The court emphasized that Section 5 empowers Congress to deter or remedy constitutional violations by the states but not to redefine or expand substantive rights beyond judicial interpretations of the Fourteenth Amendment.[25] RFRA's imposition of new duties on statesâ€”requiring them to justify neutral, generally applicable laws with compelling governmental interests and least restrictive meansâ€”was deemed an overreach absent widespread evidence of constitutional infringements justifying such a federal override of state authority.[22] As a result, the district court upheld the City of Boerne's denial of the permit to expand St. Peter the Apostle Church and dismissed the plaintiff's RFRA-based challenge, allowing the city's historic preservation ordinance to stand without the heightened scrutiny RFRA would have mandated.[25][26]

Fifth Circuit Reversal

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit reversed the district court's dismissal on January 23, 1996, in Flores v. City of Boerne, 73 F.3d 1352 (5th Cir. 1996), holding that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) fell within Congress's enforcement authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment.[3][21] The panel, led by Judge Patrick E. Higginbotham, viewed RFRA as a permissible prophylactic enactment designed to deter future infringements on free exercise rights rather than merely remedy identified constitutional violations, drawing on Katzenbach v. Morgan, 384 U.S. 641 (1966), which affirmed Congress's capacity to define substantive protections prophylactically under Section 5 to effectuate equal protection guarantees.[3][22] Under RFRA's framework, which mandated strict scrutiny for laws substantially burdening religious exercise, the Fifth Circuit concluded that Boerne's ordinance failed the least restrictive means prong despite the city's compelling interest in preserving historic character.[3][27] The court noted that alternatives, such as granting variances or exemptions for religious uses, could achieve the preservation goals without unduly impeding the church's expansion plans for St. Peter the Apostle parish to accommodate growing attendance.[3] This application remanded the case for further proceedings on the merits while upholding RFRA's validity against separation-of-powers challenges, rejecting claims that it usurped judicial interpretive authority over the Free Exercise Clause.[3][28] The reversal, as the first appellate endorsement of RFRA's constitutionality amid varying district-level scrutiny, underscored tensions in interpreting congressional remedial powers post-Employment Division v. Smith, 494 U.S. 872 (1990), and prompted the Supreme Court to grant certiorari on February 20, 1996 (519 U.S. 926), confined to whether RFRA exceeded Section 5's bounds.[3][21][29]

Supreme Court Proceedings and Opinions

Oral Arguments and Case Posture

Oral arguments in City of Boerne v. Flores were heard by the Supreme Court on February 19, 1997.[2][30] The case arrived at the Court following the Fifth Circuit's reversal of the district court's invalidation of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) as applied to states and localities, presenting the core question of whether RFRA validly exercised Congress's enforcement power under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment or instead effected a substantive alteration to the First Amendment's application against the states.[21][1] Petitioner City of Boerne, represented by William M. Barr, contended that RFRA exceeded congressional authority by imposing a stricter standard than the First Amendment required post-Employment Division v. Smith, arguing it represented a substantive change rather than a remedial measure tailored to actual constitutional violations, and intruded on core state police powers over land use without evidence of a pattern of discriminatory enforcement.[2][21] Respondent P.F. Flores, Archbishop of San Antonio, represented by Marci A. Hamilton, maintained that RFRA appropriately remedied the systemic burdens on religious exercise imposed by neutral laws of general applicability following Smith, functioning as a prophylactic enforcement of the Free Exercise Clause under Section 5 by restoring the pre-Smith compelling interest test to address widespread incidental restrictions without requiring proof of intent.[2][21] This framed the proceedings as a tension between federal legislative efforts to safeguard religious liberty and the preservation of state sovereignty in regulating local affairs. Numerous amicus briefs underscored the divide: coalitions of religious organizations, including Catholic, Protestant, and Jewish groups, supported Flores by emphasizing RFRA's role in protecting minority faiths from undue governmental burdens, while a coalition of states, municipalities, and federalism advocates, such as Virginia and various cities, urged reversal, highlighting risks to state autonomy and the absence of a congressional record demonstrating pervasive constitutional harms justifying the law's breadth.[21][31]

Majority Opinion by Justice Kennedy

Justice Anthony Kennedy delivered the majority opinion on June 25, 1997, concluding that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA) exceeded Congress's remedial authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment when applied to state and local laws.[21] The decision effectively reversed the Fifth Circuit's judgment in a 6-3 vote, with Kennedy joined by Chief Justice Rehnquist, and Justices Stevens, O'Connor, Scalia, Thomas, and Ginsburg (Scalia and O'Connor concurring except as to Part III-A-1).[21] RFRA's mandate of strict scrutiny for government actions substantially burdening religious exercise was deemed an invalid substantive alteration of the Free Exercise Clause, as interpreted in Employment Division v. Smith (1990), rather than a permissible enforcement measure.[21] Kennedy underscored that Section 5 authorizes Congress to remedy violations of Fourteenth Amendment rights but prohibits redefining their substantive content.[21] "Congress' power under Â§ 5... extends only to 'enforc[ing]' the provisions of the Fourteenth Amendment... described as 'remedial,'" he explained, rejecting RFRA's imposition of heightened scrutiny as a departure from Smith's neutral, generally applicable laws standard.[21] "Legislation which alters the meaning of the Free Exercise Clause cannot be said to be enforcing the Clause."[21] This distinguished RFRA from valid enforcement acts, which target specific constitutional harms without broadly rewriting judicial interpretations of rights.[21] Central to the rationale was the "congruence and proportionality" standard for Section 5 legislation, requiring alignment between the remedial means and the targeted injury.[21] "There must be a congruence and proportionality between the injury to be prevented or remedied and the means adopted to that end," Kennedy wrote; absent this, measures risk becoming substantive in effect.[21] RFRA failed this test due to its disproportionate breadth, applying strict scrutiny universally without evidence of pervasive Free Exercise violations by states post-Smith.[21] "RFRA is so out of proportion to a supposed remedial or preventive object that it cannot be understood as responsive to, or designed to prevent, unconstitutional behavior," as its "sweeping coverage ensures its intrusion at every level of government, displacing laws... of almost every description."[21] The opinion noted the absence of a congressional record documenting irrational state burdens on religion, contrasting RFRA with narrower remedies upheld in cases like Katzenbach v. Morgan (1966), where legislation addressed proven discriminatory patterns.[21] Ultimately, while preserving RFRA's application to federal actions, the Court invalidated its override of state sovereignty, affirming that "the substantial costs RFRA exacts... far exceed any pattern or practice of unconstitutional conduct under the Free Exercise Clause as interpreted in Smith."[21] This holding reinforced judicial primacy in defining constitutional rights against congressional overreach disguised as enforcement.[21]

Concurring Opinions

Justice Scalia, joined by Justice Thomas, wrote a concurrence emphasizing the correctness of the Court's earlier decision in Employment Division v. Smith (1990), which held that neutral and generally applicable laws do not violate the Free Exercise Clause even if they incidentally burden religious practices, absent evidence of discriminatory intent.[32] Scalia argued that historical evidence from colonial and early state practices supported this interpretation, as "free exercise" protections typically applied only to lawful conduct and did not mandate exemptions from neutral regulations.[32] He critiqued broader readings of the Clause, such as those advocated by Justice O'Connor, for lacking pre-1850 precedents requiring religious exemptions and for undermining democratic processes: "The issue presented by Smith is, quite simply, whether the people, through their elected representatives, or rather this Court, shall control the outcome of those concrete cases."[32] This view reinforced limits on judicial or congressional imposition of religious accommodations, preserving state authority over neutral laws. Justice O'Connor filed an opinion concurring in the judgment that RFRA exceeded Congress's enforcement power under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment but dissenting from the majority's endorsement of Smith.[32] She contended that Smith erroneously narrowed Free Exercise protections, asserting that the Clause affirmatively guarantees the right to engage in religious practices without undue government interference unless justified by a compelling interest and least restrictive means.[32] Drawing on historical sources like early state constitutions and colonial toleration acts, O'Connor argued for a case-by-case balancing approach over rigid categorical rules, stating: "The Free Exercise Clause is best understood as an affirmative guarantee of the right to participate in religious practices and conduct without impermissible governmental interference."[32] While agreeing RFRA substantively altered judicial interpretations of the Constitution beyond remedial bounds under Section 5, she urged reexamination of Smith in future cases to restore pre-1990 jurisprudence.[32] Together, these concurrences underscored the judiciary's role in constraining congressional attempts to redefine constitutional baselines, prioritizing federalism by invalidating RFRA's application to states while debating the precise scope of Free Exercise protections.[32]

Dissenting Opinion by Justice Souter

Justice Souter, joined by Justices Stevens and Breyer, dissented from the majority's holding that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) exceeded Congress's authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment. He maintained that RFRA qualified as valid prophylactic legislation, designed to deter and prevent burdens on the free exercise of religion rather than merely remedying identified constitutional violations.[21][30] Souter invoked precedents like Katzenbach v. Morgan, 384 U.S. 641 (1966), to argue that Section 5 empowers Congress to enact measures addressing incidental restrictions on rights, including those arising from neutral laws of general applicability, as long as they rationally further enforcement of the Fourteenth Amendment.[21] Souter defended RFRA's congruence and proportionality to remedying free exercise infringements, citing Congress's detailed legislative recordâ€”including over 10,000 pages of submissions documenting post-Employment Division v. Smith burdens on minority faithsâ€”as evidence of a tailored response to pervasive risks of unconstitutional state actions.[21] He contended that the Act's restoration of strict scrutiny for substantial burdens mirrored pre-Smith protections and aligned with historical practices accommodating religious exercise, such as exemptions from military drafts or Sunday closing laws.[30] This approach, he argued, prevented the Amendment's guarantees from being diluted by states' incidental regulations, without intruding on core police powers.[21] Critiquing the majority, Souter accused it of undervaluing Congress's fact-finding and substituting judicial skepticism for legislative discretion, thereby erecting barriers to preventive remedies that Fitzpatrick v. Bitzer, 427 U.S. 445 (1976), had endorsed.[21] He warned that the Court's proportionality test imposed an unduly narrow standard, ignoring Section 5's broad remedial scope and risking underprotection for religions facing neutral but substantially burdensome laws, as Smith had permitted. Upholding RFRA, Souter proposed, would recalibrate the balance toward robust free exercise safeguards without altering judicially defined constitutional floors.[30]

Core Legal Doctrines Established

Congressional Authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment

Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment grants Congress the authority "to enforce, by appropriate legislation, the provisions of this article," a power rooted in the post-Civil War Reconstruction era to address state violations of federal rights, particularly racial discrimination in the South.[33] Historically modeled after similar enforcement clauses in the Thirteenth and Fifteenth Amendments, it empowers reactive measures to remedy or deter unconstitutional state actions rather than to independently define or expand the substantive content of those rights. In City of Boerne v. Flores, decided June 25, 1997, by a 6-3 vote, the Supreme Court interpreted this authority as fundamentally remedial, holding that Congress cannot "enforce a constitutional right by changing what the right is."[21] The majority opinion by Justice Kennedy stressed that Section 5 legislation must respond to judicially cognizable violations, preserving the judiciary's primacy in interpreting the Constitution's meaning and preventing Congress from substantively altering its protections through ordinary statutes.[26] This remedial constraint distinguishes Section 5 from the Commerce Clause, under which Congress may regulate broadly without tying actions to specific constitutional harms; Section 5 requires a demonstrable link to enforcing the Amendment against identified state infringements, limiting prophylactic overreach absent evidence of widespread violations.[21] By requiring such ties, Boerne prevents federal dictation of heightened state standards that exceed judicially defined constitutional minima, thereby safeguarding federalism against unsubstantiated expansions of national power.[33]

Congruence and Proportionality Test

The congruence and proportionality test, articulated in the Supreme Court's majority opinion, establishes the standard for evaluating whether congressional legislation enacted under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment constitutes a valid remedy for constitutional violations rather than an impermissible substantive alteration of rights.[21] Under this framework, remedial measures must demonstrate both congruenceâ€”a tailored fit addressing a documented pattern of constitutional injuries, such as widespread or recalcitrant state infringementsâ€”and proportionality, ensuring that the means adopted do not substantially exceed the scope of the targeted harm.[2] Prophylactic legislation, which anticipates and prevents violations beyond direct enforcement, may satisfy the test if supported by evidence of pervasive constitutional breaches, but it cannot impose nationwide standards untethered from such a record.[34] Applied to the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA), the test revealed the law's overreach, as congressional findings lacked sufficient documentation of systemic Free Exercise Clause violations by states following Employment Division v. Smith (1990).[21] The legislative history cited anecdotal instances and policy concerns rather than a "pattern of religious exercise violations so massive" as to justify RFRA's mandate of strict scrutiny for all substantial burdens on religious practice, which diverged from the Constitution's more deferential neutral laws of general applicability standard.[21] This disconnect rendered RFRA disproportionate, imposing undue costs on state governments and courts without commensurate evidence of remedial necessity, thereby exceeding Congress's authority to enforceâ€”not redefineâ€”Fourteenth Amendment protections.[26] The test's enduring framework has shaped subsequent scrutiny of Section 5 enactments, notably influencing Shelby County v. Holder (2013), where the Court invalidated the Voting Rights Act's coverage formula for failing proportionality amid outdated evidence of discriminatory practices, underscoring the requirement for contemporary congruence to state constitutional harms.[35] This application reinforces that while Congress holds deference in identifying violations, judicial review ensures remedies remain responsive and non-excessive to the Amendment's core guarantees.[34]

Implications for Federalism and Legislative Power

Reinforcement of State Sovereignty against Federal Overreach

In City of Boerne v. Flores (1997), the Supreme Court invalidated the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) as applied to states, holding that Congress lacked authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment to impose strict scrutiny on state laws that incidentally burden religious exercise, as this substantively redefined constitutional protections rather than remedying targeted violations.[21] The ruling underscored that federal power does not extend to treating states as mere instrumentalities subject to prophylactic overrides in core domains like land-use regulation, where local governments exercise traditional police powers to maintain community order, density, and aesthetics without deference to federal mandates absent clear constitutional abrogation.[21][36] The decision curbed federal intrusion by affirming deference to state zoning authority, as exemplified in the case's facts: Boerne's denial of a building permit for church expansion under neutral ordinances designed to preserve historic districts and control urban growth could not be second-guessed under RFRA's compelling-interest standard, which disproportionately empowered courts to invalidate valid state regulations.[21] Justice Kennedy's opinion emphasized that such laws, when neutral and generally applicable, reflect states' retained sovereignty unless they evince discriminatory intent or direct constitutional infringement, rejecting RFRA's inversion of this presumption by requiring states to justify every incidental religious impact at the highest level of review.[21] This preserved states' capacity to enforce uniform rules without exemption-driven fragmentation, particularly in zoning where ad hoc religious carve-outs could undermine coherent planning.[37] Empirically, the Court noted the scarcity of pre-RFRA judicial findings of unconstitutional free exercise burdens from neutral state laws, with historical precedents under the Free Exercise Clause rarely striking down such measures, thus justifying judicial restraint and deference to local democratic processes over sweeping federal remedies.[21] This evidentiary shortfall highlighted that RFRA's blanket strict scrutiny was not proportional to any pattern of state malfeasance, reinforcing that federal overreach into state affairs demands concrete proof of remedial necessity rather than speculative assumptions of inferiority.[21] On a broader plane, Boerne exemplified the Rehnquist Court's revival of structural federalism, protecting dual sovereignty by limiting Congress's post-New Deal expansions of remedial powers that had eroded state autonomy in enumerated and reserved spheres, thereby restoring constitutional checks against treating states as subordinates in the federal system.[37][36] The ruling aligned with contemporaneous decisions curbing commandeering of state officials and affirming state immunity, signaling judicial commitment to balanced federalism where national authority yields to state primacy in non-delegated areas unless explicitly overridden by the Constitution.[38]

Limits on Prophylactic and Substantive Legislation

The Supreme Court's decision in City of Boerne v. Flores imposed strict limits on Congress's authority to enact prophylactic legislation under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment, requiring such measures to demonstrate a clear pattern of constitutional violations and to be congruent and proportional to remedying or preventing those specific harms.[21] Prophylactic laws, which aim to deter potential future violations rather than address only proven instances, are permissible only when supported by substantial evidence of widespread, unconstitutional state actions, as seen in historical contexts like systematic racial discrimination under Jim Crow laws where Congress identified pervasive denial of equal protection.[3] In contrast, the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) failed this test because its legislative record contained scant empirical evidence of a broad pattern of free exercise violations post-Employment Division v. Smith; instead, it relied on anecdotal examples and policy disagreements with the Court's neutral, generally applicable rule from Smith, rendering it an unsubstantiated preventive overreach rather than a targeted response to actual harms.[21][3] The ruling further curtailed substantive legislation that effectively redefines the substantive content of constitutional rights, preserving the judiciary's primacy in interpreting the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantees.[3] RFRA crossed into substantive territory by mandating strict scrutiny for all substantial burdens on religious exercise, thereby attempting to alter the Free Exercise Clause's baseline established in Smithâ€”a judicial determination of constitutional meaning that Congress cannot override through legislation absent remedial necessity.[21] This distinction underscores that Section 5 empowers enforcement against identified violations but not wholesale revision of rights' scope, as such changes would allow shifting congressional majorities to supplant judicial exposition of the Constitution, undermining separation of powers.[3] By rejecting RFRA's approach, the Court emphasized causal evidence of real constitutional injuries over symbolic or precautionary enactments driven by normative preferences, ensuring legislative interventions remain tethered to verifiable patterns of state overreach rather than abstract policy aims.[21]

Developments in Religious Freedom Jurisprudence

Partial Survival of RFRA for Federal Actions

In City of Boerne v. Flores (1997), the Supreme Court invalidated the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) insofar as it applied to state and local governments, ruling that Congress exceeded its remedial authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment by imposing a stricter standard than the Free Exercise Clause required.[21] The decision explicitly preserved RFRA's validity with respect to the federal government, as the case did not address federal enforcement powers or the Necessary and Proper Clause, leaving intact Congress's ability to regulate its own actions and those of federal agencies.[26] This partial survival was affirmed in Gonzales v. O Centro EspÃ­rita Beneficente UniÃ£o do Vegetal (2006), where the Court unanimously applied RFRA to a federal prohibition under the Controlled Substances Act on the religious use of a hallucinogenic tea (hoasca) by a Brazilian church.[39] The government bore the burden of demonstrating a compelling interest and least restrictive means, and the Court remanded for further proceedings after finding the blanket ban failed RFRA's strict scrutiny test, rejecting arguments that RFRA's constitutionality remained unsettled post-Boerne.[40] As a result, federal laws, regulations, and agency actions that substantially burden religious exercise remain subject to RFRA's demanding standard, distinct from the rational basis review applicable under Employment Division v. Smith (1990) for neutral, generally applicable laws.[41] This framework has constrained federal policies in areas such as prison accommodations for religious practices and immigration enforcement, requiring evidence of narrow tailoring to override sincere religious objections.[42] States and localities, however, operate under constitutional baselines without RFRA's overlay unless Congress invokes alternative powers like the Commerce Clause for targeted legislation.[17]

Enactment and Scope of the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA)

In response to the Supreme Court's decision in City of Boerne v. Flores, which invalidated the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA) as applied to states under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment, Congress enacted the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA) to address specific areas of religious exercise burdened by government action.[43] President Bill Clinton signed RLUIPA into law on September 22, 2000, as Public Law 106-274.[44] The legislation passed unanimously in both chambers of Congress, reflecting broad bipartisan support for restoring heightened protections against substantial burdens on religious practice in targeted contexts.[45] Unlike RFRA's broader application, RLUIPA invoked Congress's authority under the Spending Clause and Commerce Clause to avoid Section 5 limitations, conditioning federal funding and regulating interstate economic activities tied to land use and institutional settings.[46] Under the Spending Clause, RLUIPA applies to programs or activities receiving federal financial assistance, enabling enforcement against recipient entities without direct commandeering of state legislatures.[47] The Commerce Clause provision extends to land use regulations substantially affecting interstate commerce, such as those impacting religious assemblies' economic viability.[48] This dual basis aimed to tailor protections narrowly while sidestepping federalism concerns raised in Boerne.[49] RLUIPA's scope encompasses two primary protections, each mandating strict scrutiny for government impositions that substantially burden religious exercise. Section 2 safeguards religious assemblies and institutions from land use regulationsâ€”such as zoning or permittingâ€”that impose substantial burdens unless the government demonstrates a compelling governmental interest pursued by the least restrictive means; it also prohibits discrimination or unequal treatment compared to nonreligious assemblies.[50] Section 3 extends similar strict scrutiny to institutionalized persons, including prisoners in facilities receiving federal funds, protecting their religious practices from substantial burdens absent compelling justification.[51] These provisions do not override generally applicable neutral laws but target discriminatory or overly restrictive applications affecting religious conduct.[52] The Supreme Court upheld RLUIPA's institutionalized-persons provision in Cutter v. Wilkinson (2005), rejecting an Establishment Clause facial challenge by Ohio prison officials who argued it excessively favored religion over other interests.[53] The unanimous decision affirmed Congress's authority under the Spending and Commerce Clauses, emphasizing that RLUIPA's accommodations do not endorse religion but mitigate burdens on free exercise, provided they avoid privileging one faith over others or undermining prison security.[54] The Court noted potential as-applied challenges but declined to invalidate the statute wholesale, preserving its core mechanisms.[55] RLUIPA has sustained active litigation into the 2020s, particularly in land use disputes involving synagogues and mosques facing zoning hurdles. For instance, in 2025, the U.S. Department of Justice investigated Orange County, Florida, for potential violations after a synagogue alleged discriminatory property denials, highlighting ongoing enforcement against local barriers to religious facilities.[56] Similarly, Muslims on Long Island settled a protracted zoning battle with the Town of Oyster Bay in August 2025, after alleging RLUIPA breaches in denying expansion of a longstanding mosque amid community growth.[57] For institutionalized persons, RLUIPA claims featured prominently in 2025 Supreme Court briefs, including in cases questioning personal liability of prison officials for damages, as seen in petitions urging accountability for religious denials in state facilities.[58][59] These developments underscore RLUIPA's enduring role in challenging burdens without broader RFRA ambitions.[60]

State RFRAs and Variations in Protections

Following the Supreme Court's decision in City of Boerne v. Flores (1997), which invalidated the federal Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) as applied to states, legislatures in approximately 30 states enacted their own RFRA analogs by 2025 to provide heightened protections against government burdens on religious exercise.[61] These statutes typically apply to state and local laws, filling the gap left by the Court's holding that neutral laws of general applicability need only satisfy rational basis review under Employment Division v. Smith (1990).[62] State RFRAs exhibit significant variations in the level of scrutiny and scope of protections afforded. A majority impose a strict scrutiny standard, requiring the government to demonstrate a compelling interest and use the least restrictive means to justify any substantial burden on religious practice, mirroring the pre-Boerne federal RFRA framework.[63] Others adopt narrower standards, such as intermediate or rational basis review, or limit applicability to specific domains like land use, employment, or prisoner rights; for example, Connecticut's RFRA applies strict scrutiny broadly, while Alabama's version confines protections primarily to zoning and historic preservation disputes.[64] In application, these laws have facilitated exemptions for religious practices in various contexts, including accommodations for kosher meal provision in state prisons and exemptions from uniform policies for religious attire in public employment. For instance, Texas courts have invoked the state's RFRA to mandate halal or kosher diets for inmates where facilities previously denied them under general procurement rules.[65] Such outcomes demonstrate the statutes' role in enabling case-by-case religious accommodations without uniform federal mandates. The approximately 20 states lacking RFRA statutes continue to adhere to the Smith baseline, permitting neutral, generally applicable laws to burden religious exercise absent evidence of targeted discrimination.[66] This patchwork reflects federalism's emphasis on state-level experimentation, allowing jurisdictions to calibrate religious liberty safeguards according to local priorities, demographics, and policy needs rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all national standard.[67]

Criticisms, Defenses, and Empirical Context

Claims of Diminished Religious Liberty Protections

Critics of the City of Boerne v. Flores decision argued that by striking down the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) as applied to states, the Supreme Court diminished federal safeguards against burdens on religious exercise imposed by neutral, generally applicable laws, reverting to the deferential standard established in Employment Division v. Smith (1990).[21][2] Under this framework, governments may enforce regulations that substantially burden sincerely held religious beliefs without demonstrating a compelling interest or least restrictive means, provided the laws do not target religion explicitly or combine with other constitutional violations.[68] Religious liberty advocates, including witnesses in congressional hearings, contended this left practitioners exposed to incidental governmental interference without robust judicial review, characterizing free exercise as an "inferior right" enforceable only through patchwork hybrid claims.[27][69] A primary example cited involves land-use and zoning regulations, where local ordinances have denied permits or expansions to religious institutions, disproportionately affecting smaller or minority congregations lacking resources to navigate bureaucratic processes.[51] In the Boerne case itself, the denial of a building permit for a Catholic church's expansion in Boerne, Texas, exemplified how neutral zoning codes could halt religious growth without heightened scrutiny, a concern echoed for non-Christian or fringe groups facing similar barriers in urban areas.[21][70] Critics, including legal scholars, asserted that such rules often mask subtle biases against unfamiliar practices, burdening minority faiths more severely due to their political marginalization and the rarity of secular exceptions in comparable contexts.[5] During the COVID-19 pandemic, claims intensified that the post-Boerne regime exacerbated vulnerabilities, as state and local restrictions on gatherings and vaccine mandatesâ€”framed as neutral public health measuresâ€”faced rational basis review rather than strict scrutiny under the Free Exercise Clause.[71] Religious objectors, including those seeking exemptions from mandates affecting 10% of the U.S. population citing faith conflicts, argued courts upheld these policies too readily, sidelining individualized assessments of burdens on practices like communal worship or medical refusals.[72][73] Proponents of stronger protections highlighted disproportionate impacts on non-mainstream groups, such as Orthodox Jewish or evangelical communities challenging synagogue closures or employment mandates, where neutral laws allegedly enabled overreach without empirical justification for denying accommodations.[74] These arguments, frequently voiced by religious advocacy organizations and sympathetic academics amid left-leaning institutional narratives emphasizing collective welfare over individual exemptions, often overlook the proliferation of state Religious Freedom Restoration Acts (RFRAs) in 24 jurisdictions post-Boerne, which provide tailored protections absent federal enforcement, or the scarcity of empirical data showing systemic, unremedied violations under the Smith standard.[75][29]

Arguments Upholding Federalism and Evidentiary Requirements

Supporters of the Supreme Court's decision in City of Boerne v. Flores emphasized that invalidating the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) as applied to states safeguarded federalism by preventing Congress from encroaching on core state regulatory authority. RFRA's imposition of a uniform strict scrutiny standard nationwide disregarded the federal structure's allowance for states to experiment with policies tailored to local conditions, such as Boerne's ordinance preserving historic districts amid community growth pressures.[76] This uniformity risked overriding legitimate state interests in areas like land use and public safety, where states traditionally exercise primary sovereignty without federal second-guessing absent clear constitutional defects.[21] The ruling reinforced separation of powers by affirming the judiciary's role in defining constitutional rights, limiting Congress under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment to remedial measures that address documented patterns of violations rather than substantive redefinitions of those rights. Justice Kennedy's majority opinion held that RFRA exceeded this boundary by effectively reinstating pre-Employment Division v. Smith strict scrutiny for all substantial burdens on religion, regardless of a law's neutrality or general applicability, thereby dictating judicial interpretation in a manner inconsistent with the Amendment's enforcement clause.[21] Defenders argued this preserved the Constitution's original allocation of interpretive authority to courts, avoiding congressional overreach that could politicize rights adjudication.[76] Evidentiary requirements underscored in Boerne demanded that prophylactic legislation under Section 5 be congruent and proportional to actual constitutional harms, a threshold RFRA failed to meet due to insufficient proof of widespread Free Exercise violations justifying its sweeping mandate. Pre-Smith jurisprudence, while applying strict scrutiny in select cases like unemployment denials, yielded few successful challenges to neutral, generally applicable laws, suggesting burdens were rarely unjustified under rational basis review.[21] This scarcity of pre-Smith winsâ€”primarily limited to narrow contexts without broad invalidation of state regulationsâ€”questioned RFRA's necessity and highlighted the decision's role in curbing unsubstantiated federal mandates that could destabilize state governance.[76]

Assessment of Congressional Legislative Record

The congressional hearings and debates preceding the enactment of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) on November 16, 1993, relied predominantly on anecdotal testimony rather than systematic empirical analysis to substantiate claims of pervasive Free Exercise Clause violations by state and local governments.[1] Testimonies highlighted isolated incidents, such as mandatory autopsies performed on Orthodox Jews without familial consent and zoning restrictions on religious institutions, but provided no comprehensive data quantifying the scale or pattern of constitutional infringements warranting a uniform national standard of strict scrutiny.[1] This approach contrasted sharply with remedial legislation like the Voting Rights Act of 1965, where Congress amassed extensive documentation of systemic discrimination, including thousands of documented voter suppression cases across multiple states.[1] The legislative record offered scant evidence of modern instances where generally applicable, neutral laws were enacted due to religious animus, with discussions over the prior four decades yielding few such examples amid broader neutral regulatory contexts.[1] Quantitative assessments were minimal; for instance, congressional materials did not compile statistics on the frequency of successful pre-RFRA Free Exercise challenges against states, where courts had applied varying levels of scrutiny but upheld government interests in the majority of litigated disputes.[77] Post-enactment reviews confirmed that Free Exercise claims rarely prevailed even under heightened pre-Smith standards, with judicial deference to state regulations prevailing in most instances, thus questioning the necessity of RFRA's prophylactic override of state sovereignty.[1] RFRA's passage appeared motivated more by dissatisfaction with the Supreme Court's 1990 decision in Employment Division v. Smith, which rejected strict scrutiny for neutral laws of general applicability, than by verified causal patterns of unconstitutional burdens.[1] Hearings emphasized restoring the Sherbert v. Verner (1963) framework symbolically, yet failed to demonstrate that Smith's rational basis test had precipitated a surge in violations beyond what targeted judicial remedies could address.[77] This evidentiary shortfall underscored a disconnect between legislative intent and causal realism, as the record implied Congress anticipated RFRA claims would vastly outnumber actual constitutional infractions, prioritizing breadth over targeted enforcement against documented abuses.[1]
