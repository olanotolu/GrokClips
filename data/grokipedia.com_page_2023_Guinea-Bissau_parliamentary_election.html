2023 Guinea-Bissau parliamentary election

The 2023 Guinea-Bissau parliamentary election was a snap legislative vote held on 4 June 2023 to elect all 102 members of the unicameral National People's Assembly, following the dissolution of the prior assembly by President Umaro Sissoco Embaló in May 2022 amid escalating disputes with the legislature.[1][2] The election marked the return of the opposition African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC), leading the PAI–Terra Ranka coalition, which secured a narrow majority of 54 seats, reversing the 2020 gains of the ruling Movement for Democratic Alternation (MADEM–G15)—the president's de facto party—which took 29 seats, while the Party for Social Renewal (PRS) obtained 12.[1] Despite Guinea-Bissau's history of political volatility, including attempted coups and ethnic factionalism, the polls unfolded peacefully with relatively high participation among the approximately 893,000 registered voters, though international observers documented minor irregularities such as incomplete voter registers and compromised ballot secrecy in some stations.[3][4] The outcome highlighted persistent executive-legislative tensions, as the president's retention of broad powers under the semi-presidential system limited the assembly's influence, foreshadowing further instability including a subsequent dissolution later in 2023.[1]

Background and Political Context

Historical Political Instability

Guinea-Bissau has experienced chronic political instability since independence from Portugal on September 10, 1974, marked by recurrent military coups, civil war, and institutional breakdowns that have undermined democratic governance.[5] The nation has seen four successful coups d'état since 1974, alongside at least 17 attempted, alleged, or plotted ones, often driven by factionalism within the military and disputes over resource control in a cashew-dependent economy.[6] This pattern reflects the enduring influence of the armed forces, forged during the independence war under the African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC), which has repeatedly intervened to resolve or exacerbate power struggles.[7] The initial post-independence regime under PAIGC leader Luís Cabral faced internal dissent, culminating in a military coup on November 14, 1980, that installed Major Joao Bernardo "Nino" Vieira as president and abandoned unification plans with Cape Verde.[8] Vieira's authoritarian rule endured multiple coup attempts through the 1980s but collapsed amid a 1998 army mutiny led by Brigadier Ansumane Mané, sparking a civil war that killed thousands and drew regional intervention by Senegal and Guinea.[9] Vieira was ousted in May 1999, paving the way for multi-party elections in which Kumba Ialá of the Party for Social Renewal (PRS) prevailed in 2000, only for him to be deposed in a bloodless military coup on September 14, 2003, amid accusations of incompetence and ethnic favoritism.[10] Vieira returned to power via election in 2005, but his assassination on March 2, 2009, by junior officers—followed days later by the killing of army chief Batista Tagmé Na Waie—intensified military unrest and claims of drug trafficking ties within the elite.[8] Malam Bacai Sanhá assumed the presidency but died in 2012 amid escalating tensions, prompting a coup on April 12, 2012, that ousted interim Prime Minister Carlos Gomes Júnior and installed Manuel Serifo Nhamadjo.[11] José Mário Vaz's 2014 election victory led to a protracted crisis from 2015, as repeated clashes with Prime Minister Domingos Simões Pereira triggered eight government dissolutions, parliamentary deadlocks, and delayed polls until 2019, highlighting weak constitutional checks and elite capture of state institutions.[12] These cycles of coups and crises have perpetuated poverty, with Guinea-Bissau ranking among the world's most fragile states, as military factions exploit governance vacuums for personal gain while electoral mandates fail to consolidate power amid opposition boycotts and judicial manipulations.[13] The 2019 election of Umaro Sissoco Embaló as president, amid disputed results and violence, extended this instability into executive-legislative standoffs that necessitated the 2023 snap parliamentary vote following assembly dissolution.[14]

Dissolution of the Previous Parliament and Delays

President Umaro Sissoco Embaló dissolved Guinea-Bissau's National People's Assembly on May 16, 2022, citing accusations of corruption against deputies and insurmountable institutional differences between the legislature and executive branches.[14][12] The move stemmed from escalating tensions, including parliament's refusal to lift parliamentary immunity for several deputies, among them African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC) leader Domingos Simões Pereira, amid investigations into alleged financial improprieties.[15] This dissolution interrupted the term of the parliament elected in March 2019, which had been marked by ongoing power struggles between Embaló's administration and the PAIGC-dominated legislature.[6] The decree violated Guinea-Bissau's constitution, which mandates new legislative elections within 90 days of dissolution, a deadline Embaló disregarded without immediate scheduling.[12] Opposition parties and constitutional scholars condemned the action as unconstitutional, arguing it exacerbated the country's chronic political instability and risked authoritarian consolidation.[16] Preparations for elections stalled amid disputes over electoral reforms, institutional legitimacy, and funding, prolonging the parliamentary vacuum for over a year.[6] Elections were eventually rescheduled for June 4, 2023, following pressure from regional bodies like the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) and internal negotiations, though the delay fueled accusations of executive overreach and undermined public trust in democratic processes.[17][15] The interregnum highlighted Guinea-Bissau's pattern of elite-level conflicts prioritizing personal and partisan gains over governance continuity.[14]

Electoral Framework

System and Constituencies

The National People's Assembly, Guinea-Bissau's unicameral legislature, comprises 102 members elected through a proportional representation system using closed party lists.[1][18] Voters cast ballots for parties rather than individual candidates, with seats allocated proportionally based on the votes each party receives within each constituency.[18] The country is divided into 27 multi-member constituencies, known as electoral circles (círculos eleitorais), which generally align with administrative subdivisions such as sectors and regions. These constituencies vary in size, with the number of seats per constituency determined by population and geographic factors to ensure proportional representation. Additionally, two single-member constituencies are designated for the diaspora: one for citizens in Africa and one for those in Europe, allowing expatriates to directly elect representatives.[19] No national electoral threshold is imposed; instead, seat distribution occurs independently in each constituency using the list proportional representation method, typically employing a highest averages formula to allocate seats among qualifying lists. This system aims to reflect the diversity of voter preferences across Guinea-Bissau's regions while accommodating its overseas population.[18][19]

Voter Registration and Eligibility

Eligibility for voting in the 2023 Guinea-Bissau parliamentary election was restricted to citizens aged 18 years and older who possessed full civil and political rights, held a valid voter card issued by the National Elections Commission (CNE), and were inscribed on the electoral roll for their assigned polling station.[20][21] This framework aligns with the Electoral Law of November 11, 2009, which establishes universal suffrage for eligible nationals without deprivation of rights due to criminal conviction or mental incapacity.[20] Voter registration was managed by the CNE through a centralized process involving biometric data collection to update the National Electoral File (NEF), a digital system introduced to enhance accuracy and reduce duplicates.[21] The registration period for the election ran from November 10, 2022, to February 25, 2023, targeting both domestic residents and diaspora citizens; eligible individuals presented identification documents at designated registration centers to obtain or renew voter cards.[21] This effort yielded a total of 893,618 registered voters, including 844,087 within Guinea-Bissau (411,828 men and 432,259 women) and 49,531 abroad (17,922 in Africa and 17,894 in Europe).[21] Despite these measures, the African Union Election Observation Mission reported irregularities, including cases where voters with valid cards were excluded from local electoral rolls, preventing them from casting ballots at certain polling stations.[21] Such omissions highlighted persistent challenges in the NEF's implementation, potentially stemming from incomplete data synchronization or administrative errors during the registration drive.[21] No widespread disenfranchisement was documented, but these incidents underscored the need for improved verification processes in future cycles.

Participating Parties and Campaigns

Major Political Parties

The African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC), founded in 1956 as the leading force in the independence struggle against Portuguese colonial rule, emerged as the dominant political entity in the 2023 election through its leadership of the PAI-Terra Ranka coalition, securing 54 seats in the 102-member National People's Assembly.[1] Led by Domingos Simões Pereira, the PAIGC adheres to centre-left democratic socialism and pan-Africanist principles, positioning itself as a defender of institutional stability amid recent political crises.[22] The party's campaign emphasized anti-corruption measures and economic reforms, capitalizing on public dissatisfaction with the incumbent administration's handling of the parliamentary dissolution delay.[23] The Movement for Democratic Alternation (MADEM-G15), a relatively newer party aligned with President Umaro Sissoco Embaló, obtained 29 seats, reflecting its base among military and urban supporters but marking a decline from prior performances.[1] De facto led by Embaló, MADEM-G15 advocates pragmatic governance focused on security and infrastructure development, though critics attribute its formation to elite networks rather than broad ideological coherence.[24] In the election, it received approximately 21.1% of the vote, underscoring challenges in maintaining parliamentary influence post-dissolution.[25] The Party for Social Renewal (PRS), established in the 1990s as a splinter from PAIGC with a focus on rural interests and social welfare policies, captured 12 seats with about 14.9% of the vote.[1][25] Traditionally drawing support from ethnic Balanta communities in the east, the PRS has oscillated between opposition and coalition roles, promoting agricultural reforms and decentralization.[12] Its 2023 performance highlighted persistent regional strongholds despite national fragmentation.[23] Smaller parties, such as the Bissau-Guinean Workers’ Party (6 seats) and the Assembly of the People United–Democratic Party of Guinea-Bissau (1 seat), played marginal roles, often aligning with larger blocs post-election but lacking the organizational depth of the top three.[1] The PAI-Terra Ranka coalition, encompassing PAIGC alongside minor allies like the United Movement (UM) and Democratic Convergence Party (PCD), aggregated these opposition forces to achieve an absolute majority, altering the balance against Embaló's allies.[1][2]

Campaign Dynamics and Key Issues

The campaign for the 2023 Guinea-Bissau parliamentary election occurred amid acute political deadlock, stemming from President Umaro Sissoco Embaló's dissolution of the National People's Assembly on May 16, 2022, which he justified by alleging widespread corruption within the legislature.[17][26] This action exacerbated tensions between the executive and legislative branches, following a failed coup attempt in February 2022 and a series of prime ministerial changes, fostering an atmosphere of uncertainty and voter fatigue after over a year without a functioning parliament.[17][26] More than 20 parties and coalitions participated, but the contest centered on the rivalry between the president's de facto ruling party, the Movement for Democratic Alternation (MADEM-G15), and the main opposition African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC), with neither expected to secure an outright majority of the 102 seats.[17] Central to the campaign were demands for political stability in a nation plagued by at least 10 coup attempts or successful coups since independence in 1974, with voters prioritizing an end to institutional paralysis that had hindered governance.[17][26] President Embaló's allies in MADEM-G15 campaigned on advancing constitutional reforms to transition from the semi-presidential system to a more centralized presidential model, requiring a three-fifths parliamentary majority to enact, as a means to streamline decision-making and reduce gridlock.[17][26] In contrast, PAIGC and other opposition groups emphasized restoring legislative checks on executive power, framing the dissolution as an overreach that undermined democratic norms.[26] Economic vulnerabilities dominated discourse, including Guinea-Bissau's heavy dependence on cashew nut exports—which account for over 90% of export revenue—and persistent poverty affecting the majority of its 2 million population, compounded by inadequate diversification efforts.[17] Corruption and drug trafficking emerged as intertwined concerns, with the country positioned as a key transit hub for cocaine en route to Europe, fueling elite enrichment and political violence; the 2022 coup attempt was linked by officials to narco-influenced networks resisting anti-trafficking measures.[17] Parties across the spectrum pledged anti-corruption drives, though skepticism persisted due to entrenched patronage systems and weak institutions, with the election viewed as a litmus test for whether a new assembly could enforce accountability or perpetuate elite rivalries.[26]

Election Administration and Conduct

Preparations and Logistics

The National Elections Commission (CNE) of Guinea-Bissau oversaw preparations following a presidential decree issued on December 16, 2022, which officially scheduled the legislative elections for June 4, 2023.[3] This decree came after multiple postponements since the parliament's dissolution in May 2022, with the CNE coordinating key logistical elements including voter registration and the setup of polling infrastructure.[3] Voter registration, managed by the CNE, yielded a total of 893,618 eligible voters, comprising 434,009 men and 459,609 women, including 35,816 from the diaspora.[3] A total of 3,516 polling stations were established nationwide to accommodate voting, with electoral materials such as ballots printed and distributed sufficiently to ensure availability on election day.[3] Polling staff received training, enabling operations to commence on schedule at 7:00 a.m. across observed locations, supported by security personnel who maintained a civilian presence without reported major incidents during the preparatory phase.[3] The official campaign period ran from May 15 to June 1, 2023, allowing participating parties to mobilize voters amid preparations, with no significant logistical disruptions noted in final assessments, though some parties reported minor issues such as delays in campaign material transport.[27][3] International support included deployments from organizations like ECOWAS, which sent 75 observers to monitor logistical readiness.[28]

Voting Day Operations and Turnout

The parliamentary election occurred on June 4, 2023, utilizing 3,516 polling stations nationwide to accommodate voters. Polling stations in observed areas, including Bissau and Biombo regions, opened on time before 7:00 a.m., with ballot boxes unsealed in the presence of political party agents and observers. The voting process adhered to procedures outlined by the National Elections Commission (CNE), proceeding in an orderly manner without reports of widespread intimidation, vote buying, or significant disruptions.[3] Logistical challenges included some polling booths lacking sufficient privacy screening, potentially compromising voter secrecy, and discrepancies in voter registers where eligible individuals with valid cards were absent from lists. Electoral staff were identifiable, though party agents often lacked proper accreditation badges. Counting commenced immediately after polls closed at 6:00 p.m., with minimal invalid votes recorded in monitored stations, such as two across sampled sites. International observers, including the g7+ mission, described the overall atmosphere as peaceful, with no major incidents impeding operations.[3] Voter turnout reached 79.6 percent of registered electors, reflecting strong participation amid the political context. The CNE registered 893,618 eligible voters, comprising 434,009 men and 459,609 women, including 35,816 from the diaspora. This figure marked a relatively high engagement level compared to prior elections, attributed by observers to public desire for legislative stability following over a year of dissolution.[29][3]

Observation Missions

The African Union deployed a 40-member Election Observation Mission (AUEOM) to the 4 June 2023 legislative elections, led by former Mozambican President Joaquim Alberto Chissano, who arrived in Bissau on 1 June.[30] [31] In its preliminary statement on 6 June, the mission commended the overall peaceful conduct of voting, the democratic maturity of Guinea-Bissauan citizens, and the commitment of stakeholders to a calm electoral environment, while recommending enhancements to voter education and result tabulation transparency.[32] [21] The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) fielded a 75-observer mission to monitor polling stations, voter registration verification, and vote counting across the country.[33] Approximately 200 international observers in total participated, contributing to assessments that the elections proceeded without major disruptions.[34] The g7+ group of states affected by fragility also dispatched an observation mission, issuing a post-election report that documented logistical aspects, including the registration of 893,618 voters, and emphasized peer learning for improving future electoral integrity in similar contexts.[3] International observers collectively viewed the elections as generally free and fair, noting high participation and minimal violence, despite allegations of irregularities raised by some domestic opposition groups.[35] The European Union, while not deploying a full mission, issued a statement congratulating Guinea-Bissau on the peaceful and inclusive process.[4]

Results

Vote Counts and Seat Distribution

The African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC) obtained 35.2 percent of the valid votes in the 4 June 2023 parliamentary election, securing 47 seats in the 102-member National People's Assembly. The Movement for Democratic Alternation in the Group of 15 (MADEM-G15), aligned with President Umaro Sissoco Embaló, received 21.1 percent of the votes and won 27 seats. The Party for Social Renewal (PRS) also achieved 21.1 percent, translating to 21 seats. Smaller parties and independents shared the remaining 22.6 percent of votes, collectively gaining 7 seats. The distribution reflects Guinea-Bissau's proportional representation system, with seats allocated based on national vote shares across multi-member constituencies. No single party attained an absolute majority of 52 seats outright, prompting post-election negotiations.[1]

Comparative Analysis with Prior Elections

The 2023 parliamentary election marked a consolidation of power for the African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC) compared to the 2019 vote, with the PAIGC-led Inclusive Alliance Platform/Terra Ranka coalition securing 54 seats in the 102-member People's National Assembly, achieving a slim majority without relying on post-election pacts as in the prior cycle.[1] In 2019, PAIGC had won 47 seats, falling short of a majority and necessitating coalitions with smaller parties like the United People's Assembly–Democratic Party of Guinea-Bissau (APU-PDGB) to reach 54 seats for government formation.[36] This increase for PAIGC reflects sustained voter support amid the political crisis triggered by President Umaro Sissoco Embaló's dissolution of parliament in May 2022, which delayed the vote from its scheduled timeline.[23] Key party performances showed mixed trends: the Movement for Democratic Alternation (MADEM-G15), aligned with presidential interests, slightly expanded from 27 seats in 2019 to 29 in 2023, maintaining its position as the second-largest bloc but failing to capitalize on the snap election context.[1][36] Conversely, the Party for Social Renewal (PRS) experienced a decline from 21 to 12 seats, signaling erosion in its rural base.[1][36] Smaller parties like APU-PDGB dropped sharply from 5 to 1 seat, while the Bissau-Guinean Workers’ Party emerged with 6 seats, indicating fragmentation among minor contenders.[1][36]

Government Formation and Immediate Reactions

Negotiation Process

Following the 4 June 2023 parliamentary elections, in which the African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC)-led Inclusive Alliance Platform – Terra Ranka secured a majority with 54 seats in the 102-seat National People's Assembly, no inter-party coalition negotiations were required for government formation.[1] The PAIGC's outright parliamentary majority positioned it to nominate a prime minister capable of commanding confidence under the constitution, bypassing the need for broader alliances.[24] President Umaro Sissoco Embaló, whose People's Alliance for Guinean Unity and Progress-Social Renewal Party (APU-PDGB) had performed poorly in the elections, engaged in consultations with parliamentary leaders as stipulated by Article 67 of the constitution, which empowers the president to appoint the prime minister while ensuring legislative support. Despite tensions stemming from Embaló's prior dissolution of the previous parliament and his rivalry with PAIGC leadership, the process culminated in the appointment of Geraldo Martins—a PAIGC deputy leader and former finance minister—as prime minister in August 2023.[37] [24] Martins' selection reflected the PAIGC's insistence on a technocrat aligned with its platform to lead the cabinet.[38] The appointment marked a temporary resolution to post-election deadlock, with Embaló inaugurating the new opposition-majority government shortly thereafter, though underlying executive-legislative frictions persisted.[39] This outcome adhered to constitutional norms without reported formal mediation by external bodies, contrasting with prior crises involving military or regional interventions.[12]

Stakeholder Responses

Domingos Simões Pereira, leader of the PAI-Terra Ranka coalition and president of the PAIGC, celebrated the coalition's victory in securing 54 seats, stating that while they would celebrate, they bore responsibilities and pledged to respect all contributors to the results.[40] The coalition viewed the outcome as a mandate to restore parliamentary functions after over a year of dissolution and emphasized unity in governance.[23] President Umaro Sissoco Embaló, whose Madem-G15 party obtained 28 seats, acknowledged the defeat in a public address, remarking that "my party has failed" and that "the people have punished it," while congratulating the winning coalition.[2] He expressed readiness to appoint Pereira as prime minister in line with constitutional requirements for the majority party, signaling acceptance of the results despite thwarting his prior push for constitutional reforms to expand presidential powers.[40][23] The European Union, through its spokesperson, commended the peaceful and participatory nature of the elections, noting their transparency and credibility based on provisional results from the National Electoral Commission, and urged all actors to maintain restraint during the post-electoral phase.[4] This reflected broader international endorsement of the process, with no immediate challenges from major stakeholders disrupting the transition to government formation.

Controversies

Electoral Irregularities Claims

Some opposition parties claimed that government-aligned security forces restricted their campaign activities, including freedom of movement and access to supporters, in the lead-up to the June 4, 2023, parliamentary elections.[41] These allegations focused on pre-electoral interference rather than polling day operations, with no verified reports of widespread fraud or ballot tampering emerging post-vote.[41] The U.S. Department of State noted that, despite such claims, the elections were broadly regarded as fair, free of significant abuses, and lacking major irregularities during the voting process.[41] Critics also highlighted procedural irregularities in the election timeline, as President Umaro Sissoco Embaló's dissolution of the prior parliament on May 16, 2022, contravened constitutional requirements to hold snap elections within 90 days, resulting in a delay exceeding one year.[12] This postponement fueled accusations of executive overreach aimed at undermining legislative opposition, though it did not lead to formal challenges altering the certified results.[15] The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) commended the overall conduct of the vote as a step toward stability, even amid preliminary opposition complaints.[42] No international election monitoring missions documented systemic flaws sufficient to discredit the outcome, under which the opposition PAIGC-led coalition secured a parliamentary majority.[41]

Institutional Conflicts

The institutional conflicts surrounding the 2023 Guinea-Bissau parliamentary election primarily originated from the preceding dissolution of the National People's Assembly by President Umaro Sissoco Embaló on 16 May 2022. This move was prompted by persistent deadlock between the executive and legislature, including the assembly's refusal to debate proposed constitutional revisions aimed at strengthening presidential powers and its rejection of requests to lift immunity for deputies, including PAIGC leader Domingos Simões Pereira, amid corruption allegations.[12][15] The president justified the dissolution as essential to end "guerrilla politics" and institutional paralysis, but it intensified accusations of executive overreach, as the PAIGC-led opposition viewed it as an attempt to circumvent legislative opposition to reforms that would consolidate power in the presidency.[43] Guinea-Bissau's constitution (Article 104) empowers the president to dissolve the assembly following a government defeat in a confidence vote or in cases of irreconcilable political crisis, but the 2022 action bypassed standard consultative processes and occurred without a formal confidence vote, fueling debates over procedural compliance.[44] Critics, including the PAIGC, argued that the dissolution violated the spirit of checks and balances, exacerbating a semi-presidential system's inherent tensions where the president, elected separately, often clashes with a potentially oppositional legislature.[14] The Supreme Court did not intervene decisively, highlighting judicial hesitancy in resolving executive-legislative disputes, which observers attribute to institutional weakness rather than outright bias.[45] Compounding the conflict, the constitution required snap elections within 90 days of dissolution, yet these were postponed until 4 June 2023—over a year later—due to logistical challenges cited by the government and electoral authorities, though skeptics pointed to strategic delays to maintain decree-based rule without parliamentary oversight.[12] This interregnum allowed Embaló to govern unilaterally, issuing decrees on budgets and appointments, which undermined legislative authority and raised concerns about democratic erosion, as documented in assessments of the country's fragile institutions.[46] International analyses emphasized that such delays eroded public trust in electoral bodies like the National Elections Commission (CNE), perceived as insufficiently insulated from executive influence despite formal independence.[15] No acute clashes erupted between the CNE and executive during election preparations or voting, with observation missions reporting procedural adherence despite the polarized context.[3] However, the underlying executive-legislative rift framed the election as a resolution mechanism, yet post-election coalition formations reignited frictions, as the president's party (Madem G15) secured fewer seats than rivals, perpetuating governance challenges without addressing root institutional fragilities.[14] These dynamics reflect Guinea-Bissau's recurring pattern of power struggles between branches, where causal factors like ethnic-political divides and weak rule of law amplify disputes beyond electoral cycles.[45]

Aftermath and Broader Implications

Short-Term Political Outcomes

Following the June 4, 2023, parliamentary election, in which the opposition African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC)-led coalition secured an absolute majority of 54 seats in the 102-member National People's Assembly, President Umaro Sissoco Embaló appointed technocrat Geraldo Martins as prime minister on August 8, 2023.[37][47] Martins, a former World Bank executive and ex-finance minister without strong party affiliation, formed a cabinet of 19 members, including independents and representatives from minor parties, but excluded key PAIGC figures despite their parliamentary dominance.[48] This appointment defied expectations that the president would nominate a candidate from the majority coalition to ensure legislative support, intensifying executive-legislative gridlock in the semi-presidential system.[49] The resulting cohabitation proved untenable, as the PAIGC-led assembly withheld confidence from Martins' government, stalling legislative progress on budgets and reforms.[24] Tensions escalated amid mutual accusations of corruption and obstruction, with Embaló citing parliamentary intransigence and the opposition decrying executive overreach. On December 4, 2023—less than six months after the election—Embaló dissolved the assembly following an alleged coup attempt by military elements linked to PAIGC, reverting to interim governance and postponing stability gains from the vote.[24][50] This rapid unraveling underscored the fragility of electoral outcomes in Guinea-Bissau's polarized politics, where presidential appointment powers clashed with parliamentary majorities, yielding no immediate policy shifts or institutional normalization.[15]

Long-Term Effects on Governance and Stability

The 2023 parliamentary election, which resulted in the African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde (PAIGC) securing 54 of 102 seats and a legislative majority, initially appeared to challenge President Umaro Sissoco Embaló's executive influence by empowering opposition forces.[1] However, this shift failed to foster collaborative governance, as persistent institutional rivalries between the presidency and the legislature deepened, mirroring pre-election tensions that had prompted the body's dissolution in 2022.[12] Instead of stabilizing the semi-presidential system, the outcome intensified power struggles, culminating in Embaló's decree dissolving parliament again on December 1, 2023, following clashes described as a coup attempt by loyalist forces.[6] [50] This repeated dissolution entrenched a pattern of executive overreach, suspending legislative functions and leading to the formation of a presidential initiative government without parliamentary oversight, which undermined checks and balances enshrined in the 1984 constitution.[6] By mid-2024, the absence of a functioning assembly had delayed budgetary approvals and policy implementation, exacerbating governance inefficiencies in a country already plagued by weak institutions and corruption indices ranking it 172nd out of 180 nations in Transparency International's 2023 Corruption Perceptions Index.[12] The crisis persisted into 2025, with Embaló postponing legislative elections—originally due earlier—to November 30, 2025, amid accusations of authoritarian consolidation that eroded public trust in electoral processes.[51] On stability, the election's legacy has been one of heightened volatility rather than consolidation, as evidenced by Guinea-Bissau's track record of four successful coups since independence and recurrent elite factionalism fueled by patronage networks.[52] Post-2023 developments, including armed confrontations at the parliament in late 2023 and ongoing military politicization, signal no abatement in risks, with international observers noting that unresolved executive-legislative antagonism perpetuates a cycle of instability detrimental to foreign investment and development aid, which constitutes over 80% of the national budget.[45] [15] Freedom House assessments highlight how such divisions have impaired democratic legitimacy, contributing to a decline in effective parliament metrics since 2019.[24] Without institutional reforms to clarify power-sharing—such as stricter timelines for election scheduling—the election has arguably reinforced Guinea-Bissau's status as a fragile state, where short-term power grabs override long-term democratic maturation.[53]
