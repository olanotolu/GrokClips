2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final was the championship match of the ninth edition of the FIFA Women's World Cup, contested on 20 August 2023 at Stadium Australia in Sydney, Australia, between the national teams of Spain and England to determine the world champion.[1][2] Spain won the match 1–0 with a first-half goal scored by Olga Carmona in the 29th minute, assisted by Mariona Caldentey, marking the country's first title in the competition and the first Women's World Cup victory for a European nation since 1991.[1][3] The final attracted a sell-out crowd of 75,784 spectators, setting a new record for attendance at a Women's World Cup final.[2] England, the reigning UEFA Women's Euro champions and favorites entering the tournament, reached the final after defeating co-hosts Australia in the semi-finals but struggled to create consistent scoring chances against Spain's possession-dominant style, with goalkeeper Mary Earps making several key saves.[1][4] Spain's victory came amid prior internal conflicts within their national team, including a boycott by 15 players against coach Jorge Vildá earlier in the year over training conditions and federation management, yet the squad demonstrated resilience by advancing through a challenging knockout path that included penalty shootout wins over the Netherlands and Sweden.[1] The post-match celebrations were overshadowed by controversy when Spanish Football Federation president Luis Rubiales kissed player Jenni Hermoso on the lips without apparent consent during the trophy presentation, prompting widespread criticism, Rubiales' initial defense labeling it as mutual and a "euphoric" moment, and eventual resignation amid FIFA suspension and legal investigations.[1] This incident highlighted tensions between athletic achievement and institutional governance, though it did not detract from Spain's on-field accomplishment in a tournament that overall broke attendance and viewership records despite logistical challenges from the unprecedented 32-team format across two host nations.[2]

Background and Context

Tournament Background

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup was the ninth edition of the quadrennial international association football competition for women's national teams organized by FIFA. It marked the first expansion to 32 teams from the previous 24-team format used since 1999, a decision unanimously approved by the FIFA Council on 31 July 2019 to align more closely with the men's tournament structure and promote global growth in women's football.[5] [6] The tournament ran from 20 July to 20 August 2023, featuring a group stage with eight groups of four teams each, followed by knockout rounds culminating in the final.[7] Australia and New Zealand were selected as joint hosts on 25 June 2020 by the FIFA Council, following a bidding process that began in February 2019 and included competitors such as joint bids from Korea Republic and North Korea, as well as single bids from Argentina, South Africa, and the United States.[8] [9] This was the first Women's World Cup to be co-hosted by two confederations (OFC and AFC) and across two countries, utilizing 10 venues: six in Australia (Brisbane Stadium, Lang Park; CommBank Stadium, Sydney; Melbourne Rectangular Stadium; Optus Stadium, Perth; Hindmarsh Stadium, Adelaide; and Stadium Australia, Sydney) and four in New Zealand (Eden Park, Auckland; Waikato Stadium, Hamilton; Wellington Regional Stadium; and Dunedin Stadium).[7] Qualification for the expanded field involved 187 of FIFA's 211 member associations across six confederations, with 143 matches played from 2018 to 2023, allocating slots proportionally: AFC (6), CAF (4), CONCACAF (6 including hosts), CONMEBOL (3), OFC (2 including co-host), and UEFA (11). The draw for the groups occurred on 22 December 2022 in Sydney, setting the stage for the competition's increased competitiveness and global reach.[7]

Venue and Hosting Details

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup was jointly hosted by Australia and New Zealand from 20 July to 20 August 2023, the first edition to feature two co-hosting nations and an expanded field of 32 teams across 10 stadiums in 12 cities.[10] The joint bid, submitted by Football Australia and New Zealand Football, was selected by FIFA in June 2019 ahead of competing proposals from the United States and Colombia, emphasizing geographic spread, infrastructure readiness, and potential for growing women's football in the region.[11] The final match took place at Stadium Australia (also known as Accor Stadium) in the Sydney Olympic Park suburb of Sydney, New South Wales, Australia, on 20 August 2023 at 20:00 AEST.[12] Opened in 1999 for the 2000 Summer Olympics, the venue has a standard capacity of over 80,000 but was temporarily reduced to 75,784 for World Cup matches to meet FIFA's safety, security, and operational standards.[13] Stadium Australia hosted five tournament fixtures, including Australia's group-stage opener against the Republic of Ireland on 20 July, a semi-final on 15 August, and the final, with all drawing capacity crowds.[13] The final attracted a sell-out attendance of 75,784 spectators, contributing to the tournament's overall record-breaking figures exceeding 1.9 million total attendees across all matches.[14] Temporary modifications to the stadium included enhanced broadcasting facilities, LED pitch perimeter boards, and upgraded fan zones to accommodate global viewership projected to surpass previous editions.[15]

Qualification and Tournament Paths

Spain's Route to the Final

Spain were drawn in Group C alongside Costa Rica, Zambia, and Japan.[16] They secured advancement as runners-up with six points from two wins and one loss, scoring eight goals and conceding four.[17] In their opener on 21 July at Wellington Regional Stadium, Spain defeated Costa Rica 3–0, with an own goal by Valeria del Campo in the 21st minute, followed by strikes from Aitana Bonmatí (23') and Esther González (27').[18][16] On 30 July in Auckland, they routed Zambia 5–0, with goals from Bonmatí, González (twice), Lucía García, and Irene Paredes.[19] Their final group match on 31 July resulted in a 4–0 loss to Japan, where Hinata Miyazawa scored twice on counter-attacks, exposing defensive vulnerabilities.[20] Despite the defeat, Spain's superior goal difference over Zambia ensured second place behind Japan.[16] Advancing to the round of 16, Spain faced Switzerland on 5 August in Auckland and won 5–1, recovering from an early goal by Géraldine Reuteler (7') with responses from Bonmatí (11'), Mariona Caldentey (19'), González (25'), Paredes (72'), and García (84').[21] In the quarter-finals on 11 August at Sydney Football Stadium, they overcame the Netherlands 2–1 after extra time, with Jackie Groenen scoring for the Dutch (18'), Bonmatí equalizing (81'), and Salma Paralluelo securing victory (111').[21] The semi-final on 15 August against Sweden in Auckland ended 2–1 in Spain's favor, with Bonmatí opening (30'), Paredes adding (86'), and Kosovare Asllani converting a late penalty (89') that forced a tense finish.[21][22]

England's Route to the Final

England were drawn in Group D with Haiti, Denmark, and China. They topped the group undefeated, recording three wins: a 1–0 victory over Haiti on 22 July at Brisbane Stadium, where Chloe Kelly scored the sole goal in the 89th minute; a 1–0 win against Denmark on 28 July at Sydney Football Stadium, courtesy of Lauren James's curling strike in the 29th minute; and a dominant 6–1 thrashing of China on 1 August at Hindmarsh Stadium, with James netting twice, alongside goals from Ellen White, Lauren Hemp, Kelly, and Alessia Russo.[24][25][26] These results yielded nine points, six goals scored, and one conceded, securing advancement to the knockout stage as group winners.[27] In the round of 16 on 7 August at Brisbane Stadium, England faced Nigeria in a tense 0–0 draw that extended to penalties, prevailing 4–2 after Chloe Kelly converted the decisive kick; Nigeria's Michelle Alozie missed theirs, while England goalkeeper Mary Earps saved one attempt.[28] The quarter-final against Colombia on 12 August at Stadium Australia saw England trail 1–0 at half-time to Leicy Santos's long-range strike, but Lauren Hemp equalized in the 44th minute, and Russo scored the winner in the 57th, resulting in a 2–1 victory despite Colombia's late pressure.[29] The semi-final on 16 August at Stadium Australia pitted England against co-hosts Australia before a record crowd of 75,784. Ella Toone opened the scoring in the 31st minute, Sam Kerr equalized with a stunning volley in the 45th, but Hemp restored the lead in the 52nd and Russo added a third in the 63rd for a 3–1 win, propelling England to their first Women's World Cup final.[30] Throughout the tournament, England's defense conceded just three goals en route to the final, highlighting their resilience under coach Sarina Wiegman.[27]

Pre-Match Arrangements

Match Officials

The match officials for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final were appointed by FIFA on August 18, 2023, with Tori Penso of the United States selected as the central referee, marking the first time an American had officiated a World Cup final in either the men's or women's tournament.[31][32] Penso was supported on the field by her compatriot assistant referees Brooke Mayo (Assistant Referee 1) and Kathryn Nesbitt (Assistant Referee 2), while Armando Villarreal, also from the United States, served in a video capacity.[33] The video assistant referee (VAR) team included Villarreal as VAR, with Nick Walsh of Scotland as assistant VAR and Ella De Vries of Belgium as offside VAR; the fourth official was Casey Reibelt of Australia.[33][34]

England selected a 3-4-1-2 formation for the final, featuring a back three of Lucy Bronze, Millie Bright, and Jess Carter, with Lauren Hemp and Alessia Stokes operating as wing-backs to provide width and defensive cover against Spain's attacking rotations.[35][36] In midfield, Keira Walsh and Georgia Stanway anchored the center, allowing Ella Toone to play as the advanced #10 linking play to the forwards Lauren James—no, wait, Chloe Kelly and Alessia Russo up top, with Kelly drifting wide right and Russo as the central striker to exploit transitions.[37][38] This setup emphasized defensive solidity and quick counters, adapting from their semi-final approach to congest central areas and force Spain wide, though it left vulnerabilities to midfield overloads.[35]

Ceremonies and Atmosphere

The closing ceremony for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup took place at Accor Stadium in Sydney on August 20, 2023, immediately preceding the final between Spain and England. Australian singer Tones and I, who collaborated on the tournament's official theme song "One Dream," performed alongside American rapper BIA and Diarra Syllaz, delivering a celebratory musical display that highlighted the event's global significance and rising popularity of women's football.[41][42] The ceremony featured pageantry with young female football players and emphasized themes of unity and achievement, setting a festive tone for the match.[43] The final unfolded before a capacity crowd of 75,784 spectators at Stadium Australia, marking the highest attendance for a women's football match in Australia and the third-largest in Women's World Cup history.[44][45] This sell-out attendance reflected the tournament's record-breaking overall viewership, with fans from Spain, England, and host nation Australia creating an electric and supportive environment despite the Matildas' earlier elimination.[46] The vibrant crowd noise and national anthem renditions amplified the intensity, contributing to a serious yet festive atmosphere as supporters rallied behind the competing teams.[47]

The Match Itself

Match Summary and Phases

The match commenced with England asserting early dominance in possession and generating opportunities, exemplified by Lauren Hemp's shot striking the crossbar shortly after kick-off.[48] Spain absorbed the pressure before launching a counter-attack, capitalizing on Lucy Bronze's loss of possession; Mariona Caldentey laid off the ball to Olga Carmona, who struck a left-footed shot from outside the penalty area into the far corner for the only goal of the game in the 29th minute.[49][50] Spain consolidated their lead toward the end of the first half, maintaining superior ball control and technical fluency, during which Salma Paralluelo struck the post.[49] At halftime, with the score at 1-0, England manager Sarina Wiegman introduced attacking substitutions including Lauren James and Chloe Kelly to inject pace and width, shifting to a more aggressive posture.[48][49] In the second half, England increased their pressing intensity and territory gained but faltered in converting pressure into clear-cut chances against Spain's organized defense.[48] Spain received a penalty in the 81st minute following a VAR-reviewed handball by Keira Walsh, yet Mary Earps diving save denied Jenni Hermoso's low effort to the goalkeeper's right.[48][51] Spain then managed the closing phases adeptly, repelling late English corners and securing the 1-0 victory after five minutes of added time.[50][49]

Critical Incidents and Decisions

The only goal of the match occurred in the 29th minute when Spain's Olga Carmona volleyed a deflected cross into England's net following a free kick awarded after a foul on Spain's Salma Paralluelo by England's Lucy Bronze.[1][52] The sequence stemmed from a miscommunication between England captain Millie Bright and goalkeeper Mary Earps, allowing Carmona an unmarked shot from 10 yards, which beat Earps low to her right.[35] No VAR review was required, as replays confirmed no offside or foul in the build-up.[53] A pivotal VAR intervention came in the 69th minute when referee Tori Penso initially waved play on after Spain's Mariona Caldentey shot struck the arm of England's Keira Walsh inside the penalty area.[54] Following a five-minute review prompted by VAR officials, Penso consulted the pitchside monitor and awarded Spain a penalty for handball, deeming Walsh's arm position unnatural and enlarging her body silhouette under IFAB Laws of the Game criteria.[55][56] Claudia Pina converted the kick poorly, but Earps saved it with a strong left-hand dive, preserving the 1-0 scoreline and denying Spain a second goal.[53] The decision drew debate, with English observers arguing Walsh's arm was close to her body and the contact accidental, though the call aligned with post-2019 handball rule emphases on arm extension.[57] Penso issued seven yellow cards during the match—four to England (including Walsh for dissent prior to the handball) and three to Spain—without any reds, maintaining control amid physical play.[52] England fans and pundits criticized Penso's overall officiating as inconsistent, particularly for perceived leniency on Spanish fouls and delays in decisions, though no overturned calls beyond the penalty occurred.[58][59] Spanish sources viewed the refereeing as fair, crediting Penso's selection as the first U.S. official for a Women's World Cup final.[60]

Immediate Post-Match Events

Celebrations and On-Field Reactions

Spain's players erupted in jubilation immediately after the final whistle on August 20, 2023, at Stadium Australia in Sydney, embracing one another and their interim coach Montse Tomé in a huddle that symbolized their breakthrough 1-0 victory and first-ever Women's World Cup title.[1] Goal scorer Olga Carmona, who netted the decisive strike in the 29th minute, joined teammates like Aitana Bonmatí and Mariona Caldentey in emotional on-field celebrations, reflecting the intensity of their tournament journey despite internal team disruptions.[63] The squad then participated in the medal ceremony, with captain Irene Paredes hoisting the trophy amid confetti and cheers from the 75,784-strong crowd, underscoring the historic achievement for La Roja.[1] In contrast, England's Lionesses displayed visible dejection, with players such as goalkeeper Mary Earps and captain Millie Bright consoling each other on the pitch, their body language conveying the sting of falling short as runners-up in a tournament they had hosted aspirations for.[64] Coach Sarina Wiegman described the match as an "incredible game" post-whistle, praising her team's effort despite the loss, while players like Beth England and Georgia Stanway voiced being "gutted" in immediate reflections, highlighting the raw disappointment of the 1-0 defeat.[65] The English squad received their silver medals stoically, with Earps later articulating the immediate "heartbreak" felt on the field before any sense of pride could emerge.[66]

Initial Statements from Players and Coaches

Spain coach Jorge Vilda, speaking in the post-match press conference on August 20, 2023, emphasized the team's perseverance amid prior challenges, stating, "I've always said that if all the suffering was necessary to become world champions, it would be worth it."[67] His remarks referenced the internal disputes that had led 15 players to decline national team call-ups in 2022 over conditions and management.[67] England manager Sarina Wiegman, in her immediate post-match comments, conveyed deep disappointment while affirming pride in the Lionesses' run to the final, noting, "Most of all I feel disappointment, because when you play a final you want to win it."[68] She added there were "no regrets" about the campaign, praising the team's resilience despite a first-half goal conceded and a second-half penalty save by Mary Earps.[69][70] Among players, England's goalkeeper Mary Earps, awarded the tournament's Golden Glove for four clean sheets and two penalty saves including one in the final, described the 1-0 defeat as "really raw. Gutting."[71][72] Spain's Olga Carmona, whose 29th-minute goal secured the victory, initially celebrated by lifting her jersey to reveal "Merchi," dedicating the score and title to a close friend grieving her mother's recent death, saying, "I dedicate my goal & this victory to my best friend, who recently lost her mother."[73] Teammate Georgia Stanway echoed the English sentiment of heartbreak, calling it "gutting" and "devastating, but that's football."[74]

Key Controversies

Luis Rubiales Kiss Incident

During the medal ceremony following Spain's 1–0 victory over England in the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final on August 20, 2023, in Sydney, Australia, Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) president Luis Rubiales approached forward Jennifer Hermoso and kissed her on the lips while grasping her head with both hands.[75] The act, captured on video and viewed by millions, occurred amid post-match celebrations on the podium, where Rubiales had earlier been seen making a crotch-grabbing gesture toward the VIP box.[76] Hermoso later testified that the kiss was unexpected and unwelcome, stating she did not consent and felt it violated professional boundaries, describing it as an act by "my boss" that "should not happen in any social or work environment."[77] Rubiales maintained the kiss was consensual and spontaneous, testifying in court that he had sought and received permission from Hermoso beforehand, framing it as a mutual expression of joy in the moment.[78] Initial reactions from Hermoso were mixed; an early statement attributed to her, released via the Spanish players' union, described the kiss as "a mutual gesture, completely spontaneous, due to the immense happiness of winning a World Cup," though she later disavowed this, claiming it was drafted without her input under pressure from federation officials.[79] By August 25, 2023, Hermoso publicly clarified via social media and statements that "in no moment did I consent to the kiss" and that she would not tolerate efforts to question her version of events.[80] Rubiales initially defended his actions in a defiant August 25 speech to the RFEF assembly, refusing to resign and dismissing critics as engaging in "false feminism," while insisting the kiss was not targeted at Hermoso specifically but arose from euphoria.[81] The incident sparked widespread outrage, including from Spanish players like Alexia Putellas and Irene Paredes, who protested by refusing to participate in post-victory events, and drew condemnations from figures such as FIFA president Gianni Infantino and Spanish government officials.[82] FIFA responded swiftly, provisionally suspending Rubiales from all football-related activities for 90 days on August 26, 2023, pending investigation into violations of its code, including the kiss and crotch gesture.[82] Facing mounting pressure, including mass resignations from Spain's women's national team staff, Rubiales resigned as RFEF president on September 10, 2023, though he continued to deny wrongdoing.[83] FIFA extended its ban to three years on October 30, 2023, prohibiting Rubiales from football activities until 2026.[84] Legal proceedings ensued, with Rubiales charged in Spain with sexual assault—one year's imprisonment converted to a fine—and coercion for allegedly pressuring Hermoso to endorse a consensual narrative.[76] In a February 2025 trial at Spain's High Court, Hermoso reiterated the kiss "tarnished" the World Cup triumph, while Rubiales countered that video evidence showed no resistance and that prior camaraderie supported consent.[85] On February 20, 2025, the court convicted Rubiales of sexual assault, imposing a €10,380 fine (payable at €20 daily for 18 months), a five-year prohibition on contacting Hermoso, and €3,000 in compensation to her, but acquitted him and three co-defendants of coercion due to insufficient evidence of direct pressure.[86] [87] Rubiales appealed the ruling, vowing to continue defending his account, with the conviction upheld by a Spanish court in June 2025 before further appeal to the Supreme Court.[88] The case highlighted tensions over consent in celebratory contexts and power dynamics in sports governance, though Rubiales' supporters argued media amplification exaggerated the incident amid broader cultural debates.[75]

Spanish Team Internal Conflicts and Protests

In September 2022, fifteen players from the Spain women's national team, including key figures such as Irene Paredes, Jennifer Hermoso, and Alexia Putellas, informed the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) that they would not make themselves available for selection, citing concerns over "the current situation in the women's national team" and a need for "profound changes" in professional management and coaching practices.[89][90] The players, dubbed "Las 15," did not publicly detail specific grievances at the time but later reports indicated complaints centered on head coach Jorge Vilda's authoritarian style, including overly long training sessions without adequate tactical focus, intrusive oversight of players' personal lives (such as requiring bedroom doors remain open at night and monitoring diets), and inadequate preparation for matches.[91][92] The federation responded by launching an internal investigation, which concluded in October 2022 that Vilda had not violated any protocols, and required the players to submit individual written apologies and detailed reports on team issues to be reintegrated.[93][94] The standoff persisted into 2023, fracturing the squad ahead of the World Cup. Of the fifteen protesters, twelve were excluded from the tournament roster announced in June, with only three—such as Patri Guijarro, who had partially returned earlier—initially reintegrated before further absences.[95][96] Vilda proceeded with a younger, less experienced group supplemented by returning players like Aitana Bonmatí and Mariona Caldentey, who had been among the original dissenters but chose to participate after negotiations.[92] During the World Cup, underlying tensions reportedly lingered in the locker room, with sources describing a divided environment despite on-field success; players adhered to federation demands for unity but expressed privately that victories did not address systemic coaching flaws.[91][97] Spain's 1–0 victory over England in the final on August 20, 2023, amplified scrutiny of the internal rift, as the triumph highlighted the team's talent depth while underscoring the costs of the federation's support for Vilda. Post-match, players including Bonmatí emphasized that the win represented player resilience amid adversity but did not resolve "profound problems" with leadership and culture.[98] On September 5, 2023, less than three weeks after the final, the RFEF dismissed Vilda, citing irreconcilable differences with the coaching staff and players, though the decision coincided with the escalating Luis Rubiales scandal.[99][93] This followed months of player advocacy, with the federation's earlier refusal to act independently—despite player demands—drawing criticism for prioritizing institutional loyalty over empirical evidence of dysfunctional dynamics.[100]

Media, Political, and Legal Ramifications

The incident involving Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) president Luis Rubiales kissing Spain's Jenni Hermoso without consent during the post-match celebrations on August 20, 2023, generated extensive global media coverage, framing it as a symbol of entrenched sexism in sports governance. Outlets portrayed the event as a #MeToo reckoning for Spanish football, with widespread condemnation amplified through social media hashtags like #SeAcabó, which mobilized protests against patriarchal attitudes. Coverage often emphasized Hermoso's statements rejecting the act as spontaneous or consensual, while highlighting Rubiales' defiant speech on August 25, 2023, where he insisted it was mutual and accused critics of ideological bias—a narrative that drew further backlash but received limited mainstream sympathy. Although some conservative voices in Spain questioned the proportionality of the response, viewing it as cultural overreach amid a left-leaning media consensus on gender issues, the dominant reporting prioritized feminist interpretations, contributing to a narrative of systemic abuse in male-dominated institutions.[101][102][103] Politically, the scandal prompted intervention from Spain's socialist-led government, with acting Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez labeling the kiss "unacceptable" on August 22, 2023, and demanding Rubiales' resignation to protect the national team's achievement. The administration initiated a probe via the Superior Sports Council on August 25, 2023, exploring options to suspend the RFEF for damaging Spain's image, reflecting broader tensions over gender equality policies under Sánchez's coalition. Equality Minister Irene Montero connected the event to global patterns of women's abuse, urging structural reforms in sports bodies, while opposition figures like those from the conservative Popular Party criticized the government's politicization but largely echoed calls for accountability. The affair exacerbated divides, with Rubiales alleging a "witch hunt" fueled by leftist ideologies, yet it unified much of the political spectrum in pressuring his ouster, culminating in his resignation on September 10, 2023.[104][105][106] Legally, Spanish prosecutors filed charges of sexual assault and coercion against Rubiales on September 6, 2023, following Hermoso's complaint, with the RFEF initially countering by threatening action against her for allegedly fabricating details—a move later undermined by evidence. FIFA provisionally suspended Rubiales on August 27, 2023, and imposed a three-year ban from football activities on October 30, 2023, for offensive behavior and abuse of position. In a Madrid court trial concluding February 20, 2025, Rubiales was convicted of sexual assault, receiving a 16-month suspended prison sentence, a €60,000 fine, and a restraining order barring contact with Hermoso; the ruling affirmed the kiss's non-consensual nature based on witness testimonies and video evidence, rejecting defenses of cultural norms or consent. Co-defendants, including RFEF marketing head Rubén Rivera and former coach Jorge Vilda, were also convicted of coercion for pressuring Hermoso to endorse Rubiales' account, highlighting institutional complicity.[107][108][109][87][110]

Long-Term Aftermath and Impact

Effects on Spain and England National Teams

Spain's victory in the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final established the team as a dominant force in international women's football, culminating in their first global title and subsequent win in the inaugural UEFA Women's Nations League in February 2024, where they defeated France 5–0 on aggregate in the final.[111][112] This on-field success, driven by key performers such as Aitana Bonmatí and Olga Carmona, attracted record domestic viewership exceeding 5.6 million for the final and spurred increased investment in women's football infrastructure within Spain.[113] However, the triumph intensified pre-existing internal divisions, as the federation's handling of the Luis Rubiales incident and prior coaching disputes under Jorge Vilda prompted further player resignations, including seven senior players in September 2023 who cited ongoing cultural issues under new coach Montse Tomé.[114] Despite these disruptions, the squad's depth allowed sustained competitiveness, qualifying unbeaten for UEFA Women's Euro 2025, though reports of persistent sexism and administrative toxicity have strained team cohesion and public perception.[112][115] For England, the final loss to Spain represented a narrow setback following their UEFA Women's Euro 2022 triumph, prompting tactical introspection under coach Sarina Wiegman, who extended her contract through 2025 amid calls for evolution in playing style.[115] The defeat highlighted defensive vulnerabilities exposed by Spain's Olga Carmona's goal, contributing to England's failure to qualify for the 2024 Paris Olympics after losses in UEFA play-offs and a dip in form during the 2023–24 UEFA Women's Nations League, where they suffered relegation from League A.[115] Nonetheless, the Lionesses secured qualification for UEFA Women's Euro 2025 by topping their qualifying group, maintaining their status as a top-ranked side per FIFA metrics, with the experience fostering resilience among players like captain Leah Williamson, who returned from injury.[116] The final's high visibility reinforced commercial and fan growth, but inconsistent results against elite opponents have raised questions about long-term dominance compared to emerging powers like Spain.[117]

Developments in Spanish Football Governance (2023–2025)

Following the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final, the Royal Spanish Football Federation (RFEF) faced intense scrutiny over its leadership and internal culture, prompting significant governance shifts. Luis Rubiales, the RFEF president, resigned on September 11, 2023, amid widespread condemnation of his non-consensual kiss of player Jenni Hermoso during the victory celebrations, which FIFA had already provisionally suspended him for on August 26, 2023.[118][119] The resignation followed pressure from players, who had cited pre-existing grievances including a player revolt in 2022 where 15 team members stepped away demanding structural reforms, and post-final boycotts conditioned on Rubiales' departure and broader changes.[120] In response, the RFEF established a management commission to oversee operations and pledged reforms, including the dismissal of Jorge Vilda as women's national team coach on September 22, 2023, along with several executives aligned with Rubiales, such as sports director Francisco Rivera.[121] These actions led 21 players, including World Cup winners, to end their boycott on September 20, 2023, after the federation committed to improved protocols on equality, professionalization, and player input in decision-making.[122] Montse Tomé, a former player and assistant coach, was appointed interim coach, marking the first time a woman led the senior team, though her tenure faced criticism for lacking consultation with dissenting players.[112] The transition period extended into 2024 amid legal challenges and electoral delays, including court interventions over candidate eligibility and allegations of irregularities under Rubiales' prior administration. Elections for a new president were postponed multiple times, reflecting ongoing instability, until December 16, 2024, when Rafael Louzan, president of the Galician Football Federation, was elected with 90 votes against rival Salvador Gomar's 43, securing a four-year term.[123][124] Louzan's leadership faced early hurdles, including a overturned seven-year ban from public office by Spain's Supreme Court on February 6, 2025, related to prior administrative misconduct allegations.[125] By April 2025, he was elected to the UEFA Executive Committee, signaling some stabilization and international acceptance.[126] These changes addressed symptoms of entrenched issues, such as insufficient oversight and resistance to player-driven reforms, though reports into 2025 highlighted persistent cultural challenges in women's football administration, including unequal resource allocation and decision-making dominance by male-led structures.[127][114] The RFEF's commitments included enhanced equality protocols and professional contracts, but implementation has been uneven, with players continuing to advocate for deeper systemic accountability to prevent recurrence of pre-2023 governance failures.[128]

Viewership, Commercial Success, and Global Reception

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup final between Spain and England drew record television audiences in several key markets. In the United Kingdom, the match averaged 13.21 million viewers across BBC1 and ITV1, marking the highest viewership for a Women's World Cup final in that country.[129] In the United States, it attracted 1.35 million viewers on Fox, despite airing early in the morning local time.[130] Spain reported peak audiences exceeding 1.7 million on public broadcaster RTVE, a national record for women's football.[131] Globally, the tournament as a whole exceeded 2 billion viewer engagements, more than double the 2019 edition, with the final contributing to this surge through heightened interest in the competing nations.[132] Commercially, the event benefited from expanded sponsorship and revenue streams. FIFA secured 30 partners and sponsors for the tournament, over twice the number from 2019, generating an estimated $307.92 million annually from major deals with entities including Wanda Group, Adidas, and Coca-Cola.[133][134] The federation's total revenue for 2023 reached $1.17 billion, bolstered by the Women's World Cup's performance-based prize fund of $110 million—nearly a 300% increase from prior editions.[135][136] Stadium attendance for the final reached 75,784 at Stadium Australia, contributing to the tournament's overall record of over 1.9 million fans across matches.[131] Global reception was overwhelmingly positive, with the final credited for elevating women's football visibility and inspiring investment in the sport. FIFA described it as the "greatest ever" edition, citing unprecedented digital and broadcast reach that engaged over two billion people worldwide.[132] In host nations Australia and New Zealand, it fostered national pride and long-term infrastructure commitments, while in Europe and beyond, it prompted discussions on parity with men's football, though critics noted persistent gaps in sponsorship spending compared to the men's tournament.[137] The event's success metrics, including hospitality guests up 45% from 2019, underscored its role in mainstreaming the women's game despite concurrent off-field controversies.[129]
