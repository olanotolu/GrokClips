Montrealer (train)

The Montrealer was an overnight named passenger train that provided international service between Washington, D.C., and Montreal, Quebec, from June 15, 1924, until its discontinuation on March 31, 1995, with a hiatus from 1966 to 1972 and further suspensions in the late 1980s.[1][2] Initially operated jointly by the Pennsylvania Railroad, New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad, Boston and Maine Railroad, Central Vermont Railway, and Canadian National Railway, the train featured a heavyweight consist powered by steam locomotives, later transitioning to lightweight sleeping cars, diners, and coaches.[1] The route extended approximately 600 miles northward from the U.S. capital through Philadelphia, New York City, Springfield, Massachusetts, White River Junction and St. Albans, Vermont, before crossing the border into Canada via Canadian National tracks.[1] Under Amtrak, which revived the service on September 30, 1972, operations faced repeated challenges from deteriorating track conditions on the Central Vermont line in southern Vermont, leading to a suspension north of Springfield in April 1987 and a resumption in July 1989 via an alternate path through New London, Connecticut, and Palmer, Massachusetts.[2] The train's final years were marked by declining ridership and financial losses common to long-distance services, culminating in its replacement by the daytime Vermonter, a Vermont state-supported train terminating at St. Albans rather than extending to Montreal.[1][2] As the last such overnight international route in New England, the Montrealer exemplified the era's rail travel for diplomats and business travelers but ultimately succumbed to competition from automobiles, airlines, and infrastructure neglect.[1]

Route and Operations

Route Description and Key Stops

The Montrealer operated along a 650-mile route from Washington Union Station in Washington, D.C., to Central Station in Montreal, Quebec, primarily during overnight hours.[1] From Washington, the train followed the Northeast Corridor northward, making stops at Baltimore, Maryland; Philadelphia, Pennsylvania; and New York Penn Station in New York City.[3] Beyond New York, it continued through Connecticut stations including Stamford, Bridgeport, New Haven, Meriden, and Hartford, before reaching Springfield, Massachusetts.[3] North of Springfield, the route diverged inland along the former New Haven Springfield Line and Boston & Maine tracks through the Connecticut River Valley, serving Northampton, Massachusetts; Brattleboro, Vermont; and Bellows Falls, Vermont.[3] In Vermont, it ascended the Winooski River valley on Central Vermont Railway trackage, stopping at White River Junction (with connections to Lebanon-Hanover, New Hampshire); Montpelier Junction; Waterbury; and Essex Junction (serving Burlington).[3] The final U.S. stops were at St. Albans, Vermont, after which the train crossed into Canada near Canton, Quebec, before arriving in Montreal.[3] Key intermediate stops included New York, New York; Springfield, Massachusetts; White River Junction, Vermont; Essex Junction, Vermont; and St. Albans, Vermont, which facilitated regional connections and access to rural areas.[1] During a 1987â€“1989 suspension due to track rehabilitation on the Central Vermont line, service resumed in July 1989 via an alternate routing through New London, Connecticut, bypassing Springfield and Northampton to avoid disputed trackage rights issues.[2] This modification shortened the Vermont segment but was short-lived, as the train ended operations in 1995.[1]

Schedules and Service Characteristics

The Montrealer operated as a daily overnight passenger train between Washington, D.C., and Montreal, Quebec, providing through service via multiple railroads including Amtrak routes south of the border and Canadian National north of it.[4] Northbound departures from Washington typically occurred in the late afternoon or evening, with arrivals in Montreal the following morning after a journey of about 16 to 17 hours, accounting for stops and border procedures at St. Albans, Vermont.[1] For example, the April 27, 1986, timetable listed northbound departure from Washington Union Station at 6:30 PM EDT, arrival in New York Penn Station at 2:38 AM (with a brief stop), Springfield, Massachusetts, at 5:27 AM, and Montreal Central Station at 11:10 AM EDT.[3] Southbound schedules mirrored this pattern, departing Montreal in the evening for morning arrival in Washington, such as the 1976 timetable showing departure at 9:45 PM EST and arrival at 5:55 AM.[4] Service classes encompassed reserved coach seating with reclining seats for standard passengers and premium sleeping car accommodations, including roomettes and bedrooms, available for occupancy from approximately 9:30 PM until 8:00 AM to align with the overnight timing.[3] [4] Reservations were mandatory for all seats and sleeping spaces, with checked baggage handled at most stations except minor flag stops.[4] Onboard amenities featured a dining car offering complete meals and beverages, a lounge car for observation and relaxation, and sleeper access to private compartments, supporting the train's role as a long-haul connector for business and leisure travelers crossing the U.S.-Canada border.[4] [1] Equipment typically included multiple coaches, one or more sleeping cars, a diner, and a lounge-buffet car, powered by locomotives suited to the mixed freight-passenger tracks north of New York.[1] Delays were common due to track sharing with freight traffic, particularly in Vermont, contributing to variable on-time performance.[1]

Equipment, Amenities, and Passenger Experience

The Montrealer employed heritage fleet equipment in its early Amtrak years, including GG1 electric locomotives for the electrified segment south of New Haven, Connecticut, and E8 diesel locomotives for the remainder of the route northward. Typical consists featured baggage-dormitory cars, multiple 10-section-6-double-bedroom sleeping cars, coaches, and a diner-lounge observation car. Leased Canadian Pacific Chateau-series sleepers, configured as 6-section-6-roomette-4-double-bedroom cars, supplemented the fleet on occasion. By the 1980s, standard Amtrak diesel locomotives such as F40PH units powered the train, paired with Heritage sleepers and lounges, reflecting the carrier's transition from inherited rolling stock to its own inventory.[5][6] Amenities centered on overnight accommodations, with sleeping cars providing berths, roomettes, and double bedrooms for privacy and comfort during the approximately 12-hour journey. Dining options included full-service meals in the diner or diner-lounge, featuring table seating and prepared entrees typical of Amtrak's long-distance offerings. The signature "Le Pub" lounge car, often a converted military baggage car with purple and orange decor, offered casual seating, a piano for entertainment, and extended hours appealing to nightlife-oriented passengers like skiers traveling to Quebec resorts. Coaches provided reclining seats with legroom suitable for budget travelers opting against sleepers.[5][7][1] Passenger experience emphasized reliable overnight connectivity between major cities, with sleeping arrangements mitigating the route's length and border crossing requirements. However, the train's shared trackage with freight operations, particularly on the Grand Trunk Western line through Vermont, introduced variability in on-time performance, though onboard services maintained a focus on rest and modest leisure in an era before widespread modern amenities like Wi-Fi.[5]

Pre-Amtrak History

Inception and Early Operations (1924â€“1950s)

The Montrealer was inaugurated on June 15, 1924, as an overnight passenger train providing service between Washington, D.C., and Montreal, Quebec, with the northbound leg designated the Montrealer and the southbound the Washingtonian.[1] Jointly operated by the Pennsylvania Railroad (from Washington to New York City), New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad (New York City to Springfield, Massachusetts), Boston and Maine Railroad (Springfield to White River Junction, Vermont), Central Vermont Railway (White River Junction to St. Albans, Vermont), and Canadian National Railway (St. Albans to Montreal), the train catered primarily to business travelers, diplomats, and tourists seeking convenient cross-border connectivity.[1] Initially powered by steam locomotives, it featured an all-heavyweight consist including Pullman sleeping cars, coaches, a diner for gourmet meals, and a buffet-lounge observation car, emphasizing luxury accommodations typical of the era's flagship overnight services.[1] Operations in the 1920s and 1930s maintained a consistent schedule, with departures allowing passengers to board in the evening and arrive refreshed the following morning, covering approximately 550 miles in about 14â€“16 hours depending on stops and border formalities.[1] The Pennsylvania Railroad's electrification of its Washingtonâ€“New York corridor, completed on February 10, 1935, improved reliability and speed on the southern segment by replacing steam with electric locomotives, reducing delays from coal and water stops.[1] Key intermediate stops included Philadelphia, New York City (for through passengers and connections), New Haven, Hartford, Springfield, and Burlington, Vermont, with customs inspections handled at the border near St. Albans.[1] During Prohibition (1920â€“1933), the southbound Washingtonian earned an informal nickname as the "Bootlegger" among rail enthusiasts due to anecdotal reports of smuggling activity, though official records emphasize its role in legitimate commerce and travel rather than illicit trade.[8] By the 1940s and into the 1950s, the train adapted to post-World War II demands with gradual equipment modernization, incorporating lightweight Pullman sleeping cars (such as Canadian National's Green series 6-6-4 configurations) for greater efficiency and passenger comfort, while retaining steam power on northern segments until broader dieselization efforts in the late 1950s.[1] Daily service persisted with full dining and sleeping amenities, supporting volumes that reflected steady demand for Montrealâ€“Washington links amid growing air competition, though ridership data from this period indicates resilience in sleeper occupancy compared to daytime trains.[1] No major disruptions or accidents were recorded specific to the Montrealer during these decades, underscoring reliable operations across the partnering railroads' networks.[1]

Post-War Decline and Discontinuation (1960sâ€“1971)

In the post-war period, the Montrealer encountered mounting competitive pressures from automobiles and commercial aviation, which eroded its ridership throughout the 1960s. Widespread car ownership, enabled by economic prosperity and expansive highway networks including segments of the Interstate Highway System completed in the late 1950s and 1960s, allowed travelers greater flexibility and door-to-door convenience compared to fixed rail schedules.[9] Concurrently, the advent of jet airliners shortened New York to Montreal flight times to under an hour, outpacing the train's roughly ten-hour overnight journey and attracting time-sensitive passengers.[9] Railroads like the Delaware & Hudson, operator of the northern segment, bore full maintenance costs for grade crossings and faced regulatory hurdles, while highways and airports received substantial public subsidies, exacerbating the modal shift.[10] These factors culminated in the discontinuation of the overnight Montrealer in September 1966, as the Delaware & Hudson and connecting carriers could no longer justify its operation amid persistently low demand.[8] Daytime passenger service on the route, provided by the Laurentian, continued but operated at a loss, with equipment often consisting of lengthy consists of underutilized coaches and sleepers transferred from New York Central partners.[11] By 1970, the service relied on aging Alco PA locomotives and faced infrastructure challenges, including trackage rights disputes and border customs procedures that added delays.[12] The final pre-Amtrak runs occurred on April 30, 1971, when the Laurentian and residual Montreal Limited segments terminated amid the nationwide contraction of private passenger rail.[13] Amtrak's initial network excluded the New York-Montreal corridor, citing insufficient ridership and profitability, leaving the route without scheduled service until its revival in 1972.[14] This endpoint reflected broader systemic failures in U.S. passenger rail, where private operators absorbed losses without equivalent government support afforded to competing modes.[10]

Amtrak Era

Launch and Expansion (1972â€“1986)

Amtrak launched the Montrealer on September 30, 1972, restoring overnight passenger rail service between Washington, D.C., and Montreal, Quebec, as mandated by a congressional provision in a federal spending bill requiring the carrier to re-establish international routes to Canada. The northbound train bore the Montrealer name, while its southbound counterpart was designated the Washingtonian, though they shared consists and operated as a single daily service linking the U.S. capital to the Canadian metropolis via Pennsylvania Station in New York, Albany, Springfield, Massachusetts, Rutland, and St. Albans, Vermont. This routing prioritized the scenic Vermont countryside over the shorter Delaware & Hudson line through New York, utilizing tracks from former railroads including the Central Vermont and Boston & Maine.[15][16][17] Equipped with inherited heritage fleet from predecessor carriers, the initial consists featured heavy steel coaches with reclining seats and smoking sections, sleeping cars, and a diner offering full meals, supplemented by a lounge car that evolved into the ski-themed "Le Pub" observation space popular among winter travelers. By 1975, much of the rolling stock had received Amtrak's Phase I silver-and-red livery, standardizing the train's appearance amid broader fleet repainting efforts. The southward extension to Washington expanded the pre-Amtrak New York-Montreal scope, integrating the service into Amtrak's Northeast Corridor network and facilitating connections to Philadelphia, Baltimore, and beyond.[17][5] Through the mid-1980s, the Montrealer sustained daily operations without frequency increases or major route alterations, though it endured occasional detours such as a 1986 rerouting via the Central Vermont and Boston & Albany lines due to maintenance issues. A representative 1986 timetable listed the southbound departure from Montreal Central Station at 6:30 p.m. EDT, with arrivals in New York around 2:38 a.m. and Washington by 10:26 a.m., reflecting typical overnight timing and flag stops at intermediate Vermont and Massachusetts stations like Waterbury, Montpelier Junction, and Brattleboro. This period solidified the train's reliability as Amtrak's primary Montreal link, predating the introduction of daytime alternatives via New York.[5][3][18]

Interruptions and Reliability Issues (1987â€“1989)

In early April 1987, severe flooding in southern Vermont damaged tracks along the Montrealer's route, prompting Amtrak to curtail service north of Springfield, Massachusetts, on April 5, establishing Springfield as the temporary northern terminus.[19] An April 20 inspection revealed persistent track deterioration between Brattleboro and Windsor, Vermont, rendering it unsafe for passenger train speeds due to alignment issues, weak substructure, and inadequate maintenance.[20] Amtrak indefinitely suspended the Montrealer north of Springfield on May 6, 1987, citing a deadlock with track owner Boston & Maine (part of Guilford Transportation Industries), which refused to fund or perform necessary repairs amid disputes over costs and the line's low freight viability.[21] [22] The affected 60-mile segment required extensive upgrades, including ballast replacement and tie renewal, but Guilford prioritized freight operations and argued passenger service subsidies should cover fixes, leading to prolonged negotiations.[23] Prior to full suspension, reliability had eroded due to chronic speed restrictionsâ€”capped at 10-25 mph on sections of the Boston & Maine lineâ€”forcing detours, extended travel times (adding up to 4 hours), and frequent delays averaging 2-3 hours per trip in late 1986 and early 1987.[20] Amtrak substituted bus service from Springfield to Burlington, Vermont, accommodating about 125 daily passengers but eliminating rail connectivity to Montreal and reducing overall ridership by over 50% on the truncated route.[21] The impasse highlighted Amtrak's dependence on privately owned tracks, with no resolution until federal mediation and partial Amtrak funding enabled repairs in 1988-1989.[24]

Resumption and Final Operations (1989â€“1995)

Amtrak resumed Montrealer operations on July 18, 1989, after a two-year suspension caused by deteriorating track conditions on the Connecticut River line.[24] The service shifted to a rerouted path via New London, Connecticut, avoiding previous reliability issues in the Springfield area.[25] A special celebratory train ran the day prior, on July 17, 1989, highlighting the restart at Amherst station.[26] The revived Montrealer employed modern Head-End Power (HEP) equipment, featuring Amfleet coaches and sleepers for improved efficiency and passenger comfort on the overnight Washington-to-Montreal journey.[27] This configuration supported consistent daily service through Vermont's Green Mountains and into Canada, with stops including White River Junction, Essex Junction, and St. Albans before crossing into Quebec.[2] Operations stabilized post-resumption, benefiting from state subsidies in Vermont and New York that sustained the route amid Amtrak's broader financial pressures.[28] Service persisted until early 1995, when Amtrak terminated the Montrealer citing ongoing operating losses and the need for cost reductions.[29] The final runs concluded around April 1, 1995, after which the train was replaced by the daytime Vermonter, extending only to St. Albans, Vermont, and eliminating through service to Montreal.[26] This shift reflected Amtrak's strategic pivot toward shorter, subsidized daytime routes over long-distance overnights, despite protests from regional advocates dependent on the connection for tourism and commerce.[28]

Safety and Incidents

Minor Derailments and Operational Mishaps

On June 10, 1978, a freight train rear-ended the stopped southbound Montrealer approximately eight miles north of Washington, D.C., causing eight cars to derail and injuring 68 passengers.[30][31] No fatalities occurred, though damage to track and equipment exceeded $325,000.[32] On February 14, 1982, the Montrealer derailed outside Holyoke, Massachusetts, while carrying about 150 passengers; no serious injuries were reported.[33] On June 29, 1990, the southbound Montrealer, operating three hours late en route to Washington, D.C., derailed at a junction near the Philadelphia Zoo, injuring two passengers and stranding 94 others for nearly two hours.[34][35] The incident involved no fatalities and minimal structural damage relative to the train's length.

1984 Essex Junction Derailment

On July 7, 1984, at approximately 6:50 a.m. Eastern Standard Time, northbound Amtrak train No. 60, the Montrealer, derailed near Essex Junction, Vermont, while operating on the Central Vermont Railway tracks along the Winooski River, about three miles south of Essex Junction and east of Burlington.[36][37] The 13-car train, consisting of two locomotives and 11 passenger cars carrying 294 passengers and crew en route from Washington, D.C., to Montreal, encountered a washed-out culvert and embankment undermined by sudden flooding, causing the lead locomotive and the forward seven cars to plunge into a ravine and streambed.[36][38] The derailment resulted in five fatalities and over 80 injuries, with the dead including passengers trapped in submerged cars; rescue operations involved hundreds of local Vermont emergency responders who extracted survivors from the wreckage amid challenging muddy and flooded terrain.[39][36] Amtrak estimated property damage at $3.3 million, with the derailed locomotives and cars either destroyed or severely damaged, while the remaining rear cars remained on the tracks.[39][36] Investigation by the National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) determined the probable cause as the failure of the Central Vermont Railway to detect and mitigate a track washout resulting from heavy upstream rainfallâ€”estimated at up to 7 inches in the preceding daysâ€”that breached a beaver dam, eroding the culvert beneath the tracks; the NTSB noted inadequate track inspection protocols and lack of real-time flood monitoring contributed, though no evidence of track defects or crew error was found.[36][40][38] The incident highlighted vulnerabilities in rural rail infrastructure to natural hazards, prompting recommendations for improved drainage assessments and beaver activity surveillance on rights-of-way, though the Montrealer resumed service shortly thereafter without immediate route alterations.[36]

Discontinuation and Immediate Aftermath

Factors Leading to 1995 Cancellation

Amtrak discontinued the Montrealer on March 31, 1995, as part of a broader set of service reductions announced in December 1994 amid ongoing financial losses and operational inefficiencies.[29] The carrier reported projected revenues failing to materialize due to declining service quality, increased competition from airlines, and inadequate federal funding, which strained its ability to maintain unprofitable long-distance routes like the Montrealer.[41] These cuts affected multiple corridors, with states stepping in to subsidize select services, but the Montrealer was deemed unsustainable without similar support.[42] A primary operational factor was prolonged border delays at the U.S.-Canada crossing near St. Albans, Vermont, where nighttime arrivals complicated customs and immigration processing.[43] Labor shortages and security protocols at the border station extended dwell times, often adding hours to the schedule and reducing on-time performance, which deterred passengers and amplified costs for crew overtime and equipment idling.[44] These issues were exacerbated by the overnight schedule, as border staffing was limited outside daylight hours, leading to frequent disruptions that Amtrak cited as eroding reliability and ridership.[45] Financial pressures were compounded by the route's low revenue relative to expenses, including track access fees on the Canadian National Railway north of the border and maintenance of aging equipment inherited from earlier resumptions.[28] Amtrak's leadership prioritized daytime, state-subsidized alternatives, such as extending the Vermonter to St. Albans with Vermont funding, which avoided cross-border complexities while preserving some regional connectivity.[46] This shift reflected a strategic retreat from international overnights amid Amtrak's precarious finances, where the Montrealer carried fewer than 100,000 passengers annually by the early 1990s, insufficient to offset deficits.[28]

Short-Term Replacements and Adjustments

Amtrak discontinued the Montrealer effective March 31, 1995, amid broader route rationalization efforts to reduce operating costs.[42] In its place, the carrier launched the Vermonter on April 2, 1995, as a daytime train running between St. Albans, Vermont, and Washington, D.C., via New York City and much of the Montrealer's Vermont routing south of the border.[47] This shift eliminated overnight sleeping car service, substituting Business Class seating to accommodate daytime passengers while trimming expenses associated with crew changes, border inspections, and extended dwell times for customs processing.[48] To address the gap in direct rail access to Montreal, Amtrak arranged connecting Thruway bus service from St. Albans, operated by Vermont Transit Company, which met Vermonter arrivals and departures for seamless transfers.[49] The bus route covered the approximately 45-mile segment to Montreal, with schedules coordinated via Amtrak ticketing for a combined journey time of around 12-13 hours from New York.[50] This interim measure, supported by state subsidies from Vermont and New York to sustain regional connectivity, preserved a multimodal option for Montreal-bound travelers until further infrastructure and policy reviews.[29] An alternative daytime rail path to Montreal persisted via the existing Adirondack train, which followed the Empire Corridor through Albany, New York, rather than Vermont, offering non-overnight service on a parallel but distinct alignment.[51] These adjustments reflected Amtrak's prioritization of efficiency, with the Vermonter carrying roughly 100,000 passengers annually in its early years, though ridership to Montreal via bus connections remained lower than pre-discontinuation levels due to added transfer inconvenience and daytime scheduling constraints.[52]

Legacy and Revival Efforts

Economic and Cultural Impact

The Montrealer contributed modestly to the economies of Vermont communities along its route, primarily through tourism-related spending by passengers traveling between New York City and Montreal. During peak seasons, such as winter for skiing and autumn for foliage viewing, the train facilitated access to resorts near stops like Rutland and Burlington, where southbound daytime passages aligned with daylight tourism activities. Local hotels, restaurants, and outfitters reported ancillary benefits from overnight or connecting travelers, though quantitative data on direct expenditures remains limited due to the route's subsidized nature and competition from automobiles and buses.[28] Amtrak's overall operations in Vermont, including the Montrealer, involved procurement of goods and services exceeding $1 million annually by the mid-1990s, supporting regional suppliers but not offsetting the service's operational deficits.[53] Chronic reliability issues, including track maintenance delays on the Vermont segment, eroded economic viability, as inconsistent schedules deterred business travelers and repeat tourists who preferred faster alternatives like the Adirondack daytime route via Hudson Valley. The train's discontinuation in 1995 reflected broader Amtrak cost-cutting amid stagnant ridershipâ€”estimated in the tens of thousands annually prior to interruptionsâ€”highlighting that while it preserved rail infrastructure for freight and potential future passenger use, its net economic contribution was marginal compared to subsidized losses. Replacement bus connections to Montreal underscored limited demand, with Vermont's passenger rail ridership shifting to daytime services like the Vermonter, which saw subsequent growth of 89% from 2005 to 2014. Culturally, the Montrealer evoked a nostalgic era of overnight cross-border rail travel, fostering informal social experiences among passengers on its 14- to 16-hour journey through rural Vermont landscapes. It carried a reputation for lively onboard ambiance, particularly among skiers and seasonal visitors, evolving from pre-Amtrak traditions of the Boston & Maine route that included Prohibition-era smuggling anecdotes, though such lore predated federal operation. The service symbolized enduring U.S.-Canada connectivity amid declining long-haul passenger rail, influencing local rail advocacy and heritage preservation efforts in Vermont, where it underscored tensions between scenic tourism potential and practical infrastructure challenges.[28] Its legacy persists in pushes for Montreal extensions, reflecting perceived cultural value in restoring binational rail links over highway dominance.

Planned Vermonter Extension to Montreal

Efforts to extend Amtrak's Vermonter service northward from St. Albans, Vermont, to Montreal, Quebec, emerged following the discontinuation of the overnight Montrealer on March 31, 1995, which had provided direct Washington-to-Montreal connectivity until financial constraints prompted its replacement with a daytime service terminating at the U.S. border.[45] These revival initiatives, spanning over a decade, aim to restore cross-border passenger rail by leveraging existing freight infrastructure owned by Canadian National Railway (CN) while addressing customs, track upgrades, and bilateral coordination.[45] The proposed extension would add approximately 60 miles to the route, enabling one daily round-trip train connecting Montreal's Central Station to Washington, D.C., via Vermont's Connecticut River Valley communities including Brattleboro, Montpelier, and Burlington.[54] Key milestones include Amtrak's 2009 design of a U.S. preclearance facility at Montreal Central Station to facilitate seamless border processing, followed by track rehabilitation between St. Albans and the international boundary from 2012 to 2014, raising speeds to 59 mph.[45] A landmark U.S.-Canada preclearance agreement took effect on March 16, 2015, enabling U.S. Customs and Border Protection operations within Canada, a prerequisite for efficient rail service.[45] Quebec allocated CA$1 million in its 2022 budget for related planning, and on May 17, 2024, Amtrak secured an agreement with CN for necessary track improvements north of the border.[45] The Vermont Agency of Transportation's March 2025 rail plan update explicitly endorses the extension as a priority, projecting one daily round-trip to enhance regional connectivity and economic ties.[55] As of mid-2025, the project remains in early planning phases, focused on corridor identification, engineering assessments, and preclearance facility development at Montreal Central Station, with no firm operational timeline due to dependencies on federal funding and intergovernmental approvals.[54] Amtrak has requested $30 million in federal funding for fiscal year 2025 to advance implementation, building on prior feasibility studies like the 2014 analysis of customs infrastructure at Central Station.[45] Involved entities include Amtrak, the Vermont Agency of Transportation, Quebec's Ministry of Transport, and U.S./Canadian border agencies, with Quebec providing a project update at the Eastern Border Transportation Coalition in May 2025.[45] Persistent challenges encompass securing sustained funding amid competing priorities, upgrading CN-owned tracks to passenger standards without disrupting freight operations, and navigating cross-border regulatory hurdles, including site-specific preclearance designs delayed since the 2015 agreement.[45] Proponents highlight potential benefits such as boosted tourism, reduced highway congestion on Interstate 89, and economic stimulus for border communities like St. Albans, though realization hinges on Quebec's commitment to matching U.S. investments and Amtrak's ability to demonstrate ridership viability comparable to pre-1995 Montrealer levels.[54] Despite optimism in state rail plans, no service has commenced by October 2025, reflecting the protracted nature of international rail projects.[55]
