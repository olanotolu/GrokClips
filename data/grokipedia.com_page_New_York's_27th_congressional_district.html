New York's 27th congressional district

New York's 27th congressional district was a congressional district for the United States House of Representatives encompassing western New York, including all of Allegany, Cattaraugus, Chautauqua, and Wyoming counties, as well as portions of Erie, Genesee, Niagara, Orleans, and Steuben counties.[1] The district, characterized by a mix of rural agricultural areas, small manufacturing centers, and Buffalo's eastern suburbs, featured a predominantly white population with a median household income of approximately $71,500 and a median age of 44 as of recent pre-redistricting data.[2] Politically competitive, it leaned Republican in congressional races despite supporting Democratic presidential candidates, reflecting divides between conservative rural voters and more moderate suburban demographics.[3] The district's final iteration ended after the 2020 census apportioned New York one fewer House seat, prompting redistricting that eliminated the 27th and redistributed its territory primarily into the new 23rd district under court-supervised maps adopted for the 118th Congress.[4] From 2013 to 2019, Republican Chris Collins represented the district until his resignation following a guilty plea to federal insider trading charges related to tipping off family members about a biotech firm's failed drug trial. A 2020 special election elevated Republican Chris Jacobs, who resigned in 2021 after backing a bipartisan assault weapons ban in response to a mass shooting in Buffalo, citing personal toll amid backlash. Republican Nick Langworthy then won a 2021 special election and served until the district's reconfiguration, highlighting the area's volatility driven by incumbent scandals rather than broad partisan shifts.[5] Historically, the district traced roots to the 19th century, producing figures like longtime Representative Sereno E. Payne, but its modern era underscored tensions between local economic concernsâ€”such as manufacturing decline and agricultureâ€”and national policy flashpoints.

Geography and Boundaries

Current Boundaries (Post-2022 Redistricting)

New York's 27th congressional district was eliminated following the 2020 United States census, which determined that the state would lose one seat in the U.S. House of Representatives due to relatively slower population growth compared to other states, reducing the total from 27 to 26 districts. This apportionment was announced by the U.S. Census Bureau on April 26, 2021, with New York falling 89 residents short of retaining its full delegation.[6] The 2022 redistricting process, overseen by the New York State Independent Redistricting Commission but ultimately resolved through court intervention after the Democratic-controlled legislature's gerrymandered proposal was struck down as unconstitutional, resulted in a court-approved map drawn by special master Jonathan Cervas. Under this map, effective for the 2022 elections and unchanged through 2024, the territory of the former 27th districtâ€”encompassing all of Allegany, Cattaraugus, Chautauqua, Genesee, Niagara, Orleans, and Wyoming counties, plus portions of Erie County (including eastern Buffalo suburbs and Cheektowaga) and Monroe Countyâ€”was redistributed.[7] The bulk of the district's southern and western rural areas, including the cities of Jamestown, Olean, and Dunkirk, were assigned to the newly configured 23rd district, while northern sections around Batavia and parts near Rochester were incorporated into the 24th district. This reconfiguration maintained the region's representation but altered competitive dynamics by merging it with adjacent upstate areas.[4]

Historical Boundary Changes and Redistricting Disputes

The boundaries of New York's 27th congressional district evolved through successive reapportionments after decennial censuses, adapting to population declines in upstate New York and shifts toward suburban and rural areas in the western region. Established as part of the state's early congressional apportionment in the 1820s, the district initially spanned rural counties in western New York before incorporating industrial areas near Buffalo by the late 19th century. By the 20th century, it stabilized around a mix of agricultural heartlands and manufacturing hubs, with notable expansions after the 1930 census to include more of Erie County amid urbanization.[8] Post-1980 reapportionment, the district absorbed portions of Niagara Falls and surrounding suburbs, reflecting deindustrialization and white flight patterns that bolstered its Republican tilt.[9] After the 2000 census, which prompted New York to lose two seats overall (from 31 to 29 districts), the 27th was redrawn in 2002 to encompass all of Genesee, Wyoming, Allegany, and Cattaraugus counties, plus parts of Erie and Niagara, emphasizing compact rural and exurban precincts with populations totaling around 654,000 by 2010.[8] The 2010 census redistricting in 2012 further refined these lines, adding suburban Buffalo enclaves like Orchard Park while excluding urban Democratic strongholds, creating a swing district with a Cook Partisan Voting Index of R+11 that tested incumbents like Democrat Kathy Hochul in her 2012 reelection bid against redrawn competition.[10] These adjustments prioritized contiguity and population equality under federal Voting Rights Act standards, though critics noted incidental partisan effects favoring incumbents.[4] The most contentious redistricting occurred after the 2020 census, which reduced New York's delegation to 26 seats, triggering the elimination of the 27th district and redistribution of its territory. On February 2, 2022, the Democratic-majority legislature enacted a congressional map bypassing recommendations from the state Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC), proposing to fragment the 27th's Republican-leaning areasâ€”such as ceding parts to the 23rd, 24th, 25th, and 26th districtsâ€”to create more competitive or Democratic-advantageous configurations upstate.[11] [12] Republicans, including affected incumbents like Chris Jacobs, decried the plan as a partisan gerrymander violating the 2014 constitutional amendment mandating IRC primacy and supermajority legislative overrides for rejected maps.[13] [14] Lawsuits, notably Harkenrider v. Hochul filed February 3, 2022, argued procedural illegitimacy and excessive partisanship, with plaintiffs citing irregular district shapes that split communities to dilute GOP voting power in western New York.[14] [15] The New York Court of Appeals invalidated the map on April 28, 2022, ruling it contravened the constitution's redistricting framework by allowing unilateral legislative amendments without required votes, though the court declined to adjudicate partisan gerrymandering claims directly.[4] A special master then drafted a remedial map enacted May 2022, preserving much of the 27th's core counties but merging them into adjacent districts, effectively dissolving the 27th and forcing incumbents like Jacobs to relocate campaigns (e.g., to the new 23rd).[16] This outcome preserved competitive balance but highlighted systemic tensions in New York's process, where Democratic legislative control clashed with independent commission intent amid population losses favoring downstate growth.[17]

Economic and Cultural Characteristics

The economy of New York's 27th congressional district features a median household income of $71,541 as of 2021, which trails the national median of approximately $70,784 for that year but aligns with regional patterns in western New York.[2] This income level supports a mix of rural and exurban communities, where employment centers on manufacturingâ€”particularly in areas like Jamestown known for furniture and metalworkingâ€”agriculture including dairy and viticulture in Chautauqua County, and service sectors such as healthcare and retail in small cities like Olean and Dunkirk. Economic challenges persist from mid-20th-century deindustrialization, contributing to higher reliance on public sector jobs and commuting to nearby urban hubs like Buffalo. Culturally, the district embodies a predominantly rural ethos, with vast swathes of farmland and small towns fostering traditions of self-reliance, community solidarity, and outdoor pursuits such as hunting, fishing, and attendance at county fairs.[22] Residents, with a median age of 44.4, exhibit conservative social values, emphasizing family, Second Amendment rights, and skepticism toward urban-centric policies, as reflected in strong support for Republican presidential candidates like Donald Trump in 2016 and 2020.[2] Limited ethnic diversity, dominated by those of European descent, reinforces a cultural continuity rooted in early 20th-century immigration waves and agrarian heritage, though small Hispanic and Native American communities add localized influences near the Canadian border and reservations.

Political Characteristics

Voter Registration and Partisan Leanings

As of November 1, 2023, voter enrollment in New York's 27th congressional district showed Republicans as the largest affiliated party, followed by no party affiliation, with Democrats in third place, according to data from the New York State Board of Elections.[23] This registration pattern reflects the district's rural and exurban composition, where Republican enrollment typically predominates in counties such as Chautauqua, Cattaraugus, Allegany, Wyoming, Genesee, and Orleans, as well as portions of Erie, Niagara, and Steuben.[24] The district exhibits a Republican partisan lean, rated R+7 on the Cook Partisan Voting Index for the 119th Congress, meaning it supported the Republican presidential candidate by 7 percentage points more than the national average in the 2020 election.[25] This lean aligns with consistent Republican victories in federal elections: in 2020, under the prior map, Republican Chris Jacobs secured 59.7% of the vote against Democrat Nate McMurray's 39.0%.[26] Following redistricting, Republican Nick Langworthy won the 2022 contest with 58.1% to Democrat Max Della Pia's 41.9%. Langworthy defended the seat in 2024 against Democrat Ian Golden, maintaining Republican control amid national trends favoring the party in similar districts.[27]

Influence of Local Issues on Voting Patterns

The rural character of New York's 27th congressional district, spanning counties such as Allegany, Cattaraugus, and Wyoming, amplifies the influence of agricultural concerns on voting patterns, where farming accounts for a significant portion of local economic output. Dairy production and crop farming face pressures from volatile commodity prices, labor shortages, and state-level regulations on land use, prompting voters to favor candidates advocating for federal farm aid and trade protections. In the 2020 election cycle, both major candidates emphasized support for local farmers, reflecting agriculture's role in sustaining rural communities and shaping electoral outcomes toward those prioritizing deregulation over expansive environmental mandates.[28][29] Access to broadband internet emerges as a critical local issue, particularly in underserved rural expanses, where inadequate connectivity hampers remote work, education, and business viability. A 2020 AARP survey found 72% of New York voters, including those in the district, viewing broadband expansion as essential, correlating with support for infrastructure-focused policies that address digital divides without excessive taxation. This concern reinforces patterns of backing Republican incumbents who secure federal funding for rural broadband, as seen in post-2020 investment pushes.[30] Economic recovery and job creation in manufacturing, alongside rising energy costs exacerbated by state energy policies, further drive voter preferences toward fiscal conservatism. Pre-COVID manufacturing strength in the district gave way to inflation-sensitive concerns by 2022, with candidates highlighting small business relief and affordable energy to appeal to working-class households facing median incomes around $71,500 and higher utility burdens in cold-climate rural areas. These factors contribute to the district's partisan tilt, where local economic resilience outweighs national cultural debates in mobilizing turnout.[28][2]

History

Formation and Early Representatives (1789â€“1900)

New York's 27th congressional district was created by the state legislature in 1822 for the elections to the 18th United States Congress (1823â€“1825), following the reapportionment after the 1820 census that increased the state's House delegation from 27 to 34 seats. This expansion reflected New York's rapid population growth, particularly in upstate and western regions opened by the Erie Canal's construction, which began in 1817 and boosted settlement and commerce. The district was drawn to encompass rural and agricultural areas in western New York, initially including Monroe and Livingston counties, areas characterized by farming communities and emerging mill towns along waterways.[31] Early elections in the district were competitive, reflecting national shifts from Federalist dominance to the rise of Democratic-Republicans and later Anti-Jacksonians and Whigs. The district's representatives during this period often advocated for internal improvements, tariff protections for agriculture, and states' rights issues amid growing sectional tensions. Modern Era and Republican Dominance (2000â€“Present)

In the early 2000s, New York's 27th congressional district was represented by Republican Thomas M. Reynolds, who secured re-election in the November 2000 general election against Democrat Thomas W. Pecoraro.[38] Reynolds, who had held the seat since 1993, focused on issues like agriculture and economic development in the district's rural and suburban areas before transitioning to the neighboring 26th district following 2002 redistricting.[39] The district's boundaries, encompassing parts of western New York including Erie and Niagara counties, underwent significant changes after the 2010 census, creating a more compact, Republican-leaning configuration rated as Solid Republican by the Cook Political Report due to its voter composition favoring conservative priorities such as manufacturing support and limited government intervention.[40] The post-2012 iteration of the district solidified Republican control, with Chris Collins winning the open seat in 2012 and serving from January 2013 until his resignation on September 30, 2019, amid a federal insider trading conviction.[41][42] Collins' tenure included comfortable victories in 2014 and 2016, reflecting the district's partisan tilt, though his 2018 re-election was narrower at 51% against Democrat Nate McMurray following his arrest on securities fraud charges.[41] A special election on June 23, 2020, to fill the vacancy was won by Republican Chris Jacobs, a state senator, who defeated McMurray with 58.9% of the vote amid low turnout influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic.[43] Jacobs, serving from July 2020 to May 2023, won a full term in the November 2020 general election and continued Republican representation until his abrupt resignation in 2023 after announcing in June 2022 that he would not seek re-election due to intraparty backlash over his support for enhanced background checks and red-flag laws following mass shootings in Buffalo and Uvalde.[44][45] The district's consistent Republican wins underscored its voters' preference for candidates emphasizing Second Amendment rights, economic conservatism, and opposition to urban-centric policies from Albany and Washington. Following the 2020 census, protracted redistricting litigation eliminated the 27th district effective after the 2022 elections, redistributing its territory primarily into the Republican-held 23rd and 24th districts, preserving the region's partisan alignment.[4]

Profiles of Notable Incumbents

Sereno E. Payne represented New York's 27th congressional district continuously from March 4, 1889, through his death on December 10, 1914, serving in the 51st to 63rd Congresses after initial terms in the neighboring 26th district.[51][52] Born June 26, 1843, in Hamilton, New York, Payne graduated from Union College in 1864, was admitted to the bar in 1866, and established a legal practice in Auburn, where he also served as city clerk.[51] As a Republican, he rose to prominence chairing the House Ways and Means Committee from 1895 to 1911 and becoming the body's first Majority Leader from 1899 to 1911.[51][53] Payne authored the Payne Tariff bill of 1909, co-sponsored with Senator Nelson Aldrich, which lowered some duties but raised others on key imports, preserving protectionist policies amid Progressive Era pressures for reform; the resulting Payne-Aldrich Act strained Republican unity and contributed to the party's 1910 midterm losses.[54] His legislative focus emphasized reciprocal trade agreements and fiscal conservatism, reflecting the district's agricultural and manufacturing interests in counties like Cayuga, Oswego, and Wayne.[51] Payne's 25-year tenure marked one of the longest continuous services in the district's early history, underscoring Republican dominance in western and central New York during the Gilded Age.[53] Chris Collins held the seat from January 3, 2013, to his resignation on October 5, 2019, marking the first Republican representation since 1993 in a district long trending Democratic due to urban centers like Buffalo.[41] Born May 20, 1950, in Schenectady, New York, Collins earned a business degree from North Carolina State University in 1972 and built a career in pharmaceutical sales before entering politics as Erie County Executive from 2007 to 2011. Elected in 2012 amid national Republican gains, he secured reelection in 2014 and 2016 with margins exceeding 20 points each time, prioritizing economic development, Second Amendment rights, and opposition to the Affordable Care Act.[41] Collins resigned following a guilty plea to insider trading charges related to a 2018 biotechnology stock tip, for which he received a 26-month prison sentence in 2020; the case drew scrutiny to congressional ethics amid his prior service on the House Energy and Commerce Committee.[55]

Controversies and Scandals

Chris Collins Insider Trading Case

Christopher Collins, the Republican U.S. Representative for New York's 27th congressional district from 2013 to 2019, was a paid member of the board of directors and held a 16.8% stake in Innate Immunotherapeutics, an Australian biotechnology firm developing a treatment for multiple sclerosis.[70] On June 22, 2017, during a board teleconference, Collins learned of the failure of Innate's Phase 3 clinical trial for its lead drug, misdiagnosed as successful in preliminary data but ultimately negative, which would cause the company's stock to plummet upon public disclosure.[55] That evening, while attending a congressional picnic at the White House, Collins made 11 calls to his son, Cameron Collinsâ€”a major shareholder in Innateâ€”and disclosed the nonpublic adverse results, enabling Cameron to sell approximately 1.39 million shares between June 23 and June 26, 2017, for proceeds of $768,000 and avoiding losses exceeding $570,000 when the stock fell 92% on June 27 after the announcement.[70] [71] Collins also passed the tip to his prospective son-in-law, Stephen Zarsky, who sold 6,000 shares short, avoiding $86,000 in losses, and further disseminated it within his trading group.[55] The scheme violated federal securities laws prohibiting trading on material nonpublic information. On August 8, 2018, the FBI arrested Collins and Cameron at their homes; Collins was indicted by a federal grand jury in the Southern District of New York on 11 counts, including conspiracy to commit securities fraud, wire fraud, and making false statements to the FBI during its investigation, where he initially denied tipping his son.[70] [71] Facing trial, Collins resigned from Congress on September 30, 2019, triggering a special election in the district.[72] The next day, October 1, 2019, he pleaded guilty to one count of conspiracy to commit securities fraud and one count of making false statements, admitting he lied to investigators about the calls and trades.[70] On January 17, 2020, U.S. District Judge Vernon S. Broderick sentenced Collins to 26 months in prison, three years of supervised release, and a $200,000 fine, below the statutory maximum of five years per count but reflecting the deliberate breach of trust by a public official.[55] [71] Cameron Collins received a one-month prison term and six months of home confinement, while Zarsky got probation; all three settled civil charges with the Securities and Exchange Commission, disgorging profits and paying penalties totaling over $1 million.[73] The case, prosecuted by the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York, highlighted enforcement against insider trading by elected officials, with the Department of Justice emphasizing Collins's abuse of confidential board information gained through his position.[55]

Redistricting Battles and Gerrymandering Claims

Following the 2020 census, which resulted in New York losing one congressional seat due to population shifts, the Democratic-majority state legislature enacted a new congressional map on February 1, 2022. This map preserved much of the territory of the 27th districtâ€”encompassing suburban Erie County areas around Buffalo, Niagara County, and rural counties like Wyoming and Alleganyâ€”but incorporated minor boundary adjustments that slightly diluted its Republican lean by adding more Democratic-leaning precincts from old NY-26.[74] Republicans, including voters from the 27th district, challenged the map as an unconstitutional partisan gerrymander under Article III, Â§ 4(c) of the New York State Constitution, which bars districts drawn to discourage competition or favor one party, arguing it aimed to flip up to six Republican-held seats statewide, including competitive upstate districts adjacent to NY-27.[75][76] On April 27, 2022, the New York Court of Appeals invalidated the Democratic map, ruling it violated constitutional standards by prioritizing partisan advantage over compactness, contiguity, and municipal preservation, with evidence of irregular boundaries in upstate areas including those overlapping NY-27's region.[75] A court-appointed special master, Jonathan Cervas, then drew an independent map adopted on May 20, 2022, which eliminated the 27th district as a numbered entity due to the seat loss; its core Republican-leaning suburbs and rural areas were largely merged into the new 23rd district, while northern portions like parts of Niagara County were adjusted into the 26th. This reconfiguration maintained the area's overall Republican tilt (Cook PVI R+11 pre-redistricting), but critics from both parties noted the process's disruption, with Republicans crediting the ruling for preventing a net Democratic gain of seats and Democrats attributing the seat loss to population decline rather than gerrymandering.[3][77] Subsequent litigation in 2023 further impacted the region's boundaries. On December 12, 2023, the Court of Appeals ruled that the state legislature had improperly amended a map proposed by the Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC) in violation of constitutional redistricting procedures established in 2014.[78] The IRC reconvened and released a revised congressional map on February 15, 2024, which the legislature adopted with minimal changes; this map retained the absorption of old NY-27 territory into the 23rd district but made incremental tweaks, such as shifting some Genesee County precincts to enhance compactness. Republicans, including Rep. Nick Langworthy (who succeeded to the reconfigured 23rd seat in 2022), claimed ongoing Democratic efforts subverted fair processes, while Democrats argued the adjustments complied with court mandates and reflected neutral criteria like equal population (each district averaging 777,000 residents). No district-specific gerrymandering suits targeted the former NY-27 post-2022, but the episode highlighted broader tensions over upstate Republican strongholds amid Democratic control of Albany.[79][80]
