2023 European Athletics Team Championships

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships was an international track and field competition organized by European Athletics, held from 20 to 25 June at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, where national teams from member federations vied for points across multiple events to secure division standings, promotions, and relegations.[1][2] The event spanned four divisions—First, Second, Third, and a newly introduced Fourth—emphasizing collective performance over individual accolades, with scoring systems rewarding top placements in disciplines such as sprints, jumps, throws, and relays.[2] In the premier First Division, Italy clinched their first-ever title, amassing superior points through standout contributions, including Olympic high jump champion Gianmarco Tamberi's leadership and victories in seven individual events.[2] This triumph marked a breakthrough for the Italian squad since the competition's inception in 2009, highlighting their depth in both men's and women's fields amid a field of strong contenders like France and Germany.[2] Lower divisions saw competitive battles, with outcomes influencing future participation in higher tiers, underscoring the championships' role in fostering European athletic development through team-based incentives.[3] No significant controversies marred the proceedings, allowing focus on athletic achievements under clear regulations enforced by the governing body.[2]

Event Context

Integration with 2023 European Games

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships were incorporated into the 2023 European Games as the official athletics discipline, with all three divisions (Super League, First Division, and Second Division) contested under the multi-sport event's framework.[4][5] This integration was announced by European Athletics in October 2021, aligning the championships with the European Olympic Committees' initiative to host continental championships within the Games to consolidate European multi-sport competitions.[5] Hosted at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, from June 20 to 25, 2023, the event marked the first occasion where all divisions occurred simultaneously in one venue, distinct from the main European Games sites in Kraków and Małopolska.[6] The format retained the standard team scoring system but introduced individual medals for top performers across divisions, expanding recognition beyond aggregate team results and fostering broader athlete participation from 16 teams in the top division.[7] This combined structure was credited with generating innovative competitive dynamics, such as heightened team strategies and cross-division rivalries, while leveraging the European Games' infrastructure for logistics and visibility.[7] Post-event evaluations highlighted the model's success in elevating athletics' profile within the Games, with European Olympic Committees officials advocating for its recurrence in future editions to streamline European championships.[7]

Format Modifications for 2023

For the 2023 edition, the European Athletics Team Championships underwent significant structural adjustments, primarily reducing the number of divisions from four to three to streamline the competition and improve logistical efficiency. The First Division comprised 16 teams, the Second Division also 16 teams, and the Third Division included all remaining entered teams from European Athletics member federations.[8] This reconfiguration aimed to consolidate competitive levels while maintaining promotion and relegation mechanics between divisions.[8] A key innovation was centralizing all divisions at a single venue—the Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland—over six consecutive days from 20 to 25 June 2023, diverging from prior years' dispersed hosting across multiple sites. The schedule allocated the first three days (20–22 June) to the Third Division in the morning sessions and Second Division in the evenings, followed by the First Division occupying evenings on days four through six (23–25 June).[8] This back-to-back format was designed to boost television coverage and spectator attendance by creating a unified event atmosphere, particularly as it coincided with the athletics program of the European Games 2023.[8] [9] These modifications, implemented in collaboration between European Athletics and the European Olympic Committees, established a template for subsequent championships, emphasizing biennial hosting with all divisions co-located to enhance visibility and financial viability for the governing body.[9] Participation in the Third Division remained open to smaller federations, ensuring broader inclusivity, though teams from Russia and Belarus were listed conditionally pending sanction resolutions.[8]

Geopolitical Exclusions and Participation Rules

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships were open to national teams from the 50 member federations of European Athletics, excluding those under active suspension. Participation required teams to meet qualification criteria based on performance in the prior edition (2021), with promotions and relegations determining divisional assignments; host nations like Poland received automatic entry to the First Division. Athletes representing teams had to comply with World Athletics eligibility rules, including nationality requirements and anti-doping standards, but the event emphasized collective national scoring over individual neutral entries. In a geopolitical context tied to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, European Athletics imposed sanctions on March 2, 2022, suspending the Russian Athletics Federation (RusAF) and Belarusian counterpart indefinitely. This decision barred all athletes, officials, and support personnel from Russia and Belarus from competing in any European Athletics events, including the 2023 Team Championships, as reaffirmed by the Council on March 15, 2023, pending resolution of the conflict. The exclusion applied to team participation without provisions for neutral athletes, distinguishing it from some international frameworks allowing individual "neutral" entries under strict conditions; European Athletics prioritized institutional accountability over selective inclusions.[10][11] No other national federations faced geopolitical exclusions for the 2023 edition, though ongoing World Athletics bans aligned with European policy by maintaining the Russian and Belarusian prohibitions despite lifting RusAF's separate doping-related suspension in March 2023. Ukraine's federation remained fully eligible and active, fielding teams across divisions without restrictions. These measures reflected broader sports governing body responses to the invasion, focusing on solidarity with affected members while upholding competition integrity.[12]

Competition Structure

Division System and Scoring Mechanics

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships utilized a tiered division system comprising the First Division, Second Division, and Third Division, with promotion and relegation mechanisms to determine team placements in subsequent editions. The First Division was expanded to 16 teams from the previous Super League format of 8 teams, reflecting modifications for integration with the European Games in Silesia.[13][14] This structure treated each division as a separate match, with teams competing in a combined men's and women's program since the format's evolution in 2009.[13] Scoring in each division followed a positional points allocation per event, including individual disciplines and relays. The winner of an event scored points equal to the number of teams in that division—for the First and Second Divisions, this equated to 16 points—while the second-place finisher received one fewer point, decreasing sequentially to 1 point for the last-place team.[13][15] Teams achieving no valid performance, disqualifications, or non-finishes earned zero points, with ties resulting in equally divided points among the affected positions.[13] Aggregate scores across all events determined divisional rankings, with ties resolved first by the count of event wins, then seconds, and so forth.[13] Promotion and relegation rules incentivized performance: in the First Division, the bottom three teams were relegated to the Second Division, while the top three from the Second Division earned promotion to the First.[13] Analogous movement applied between the Second and Third Divisions, with the top three from the Third promoted and the bottom three from the Second relegated.[13] These mechanics, rooted in the championships' regulations, aimed to maintain competitive balance across European Athletics member federations, though the 2023 expansion temporarily adjusted participant numbers without altering core scoring principles.[13][16]

Events and Program Details

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships featured a standardized program of track and field events contested across all three divisions, comprising sprints, middle- and long-distance races, hurdles, relays, jumps, and throws for both men and women.[17] The event lineup emphasized individual performances contributing to team scores, with no multi-day combined events such as decathlon or heptathlon included.[18] Each division followed a three-day format, with track events typically incorporating heats and finals, while field events consisted of a single competitive round per discipline.[17] Men's events included the 100 m, 200 m, 400 m, 800 m, 1500 m, 5000 m, 3000 m steeplechase, 110 m hurdles, 400 m hurdles, 4 × 100 m relay, long jump, triple jump, high jump, pole vault, shot put, discus throw, hammer throw, and javelin throw, alongside the mixed 4 × 400 m relay shared with women.[17] Women's events mirrored this structure, substituting the 100 m hurdles for the 110 m hurdles and featuring the same distances, relays, and field disciplines.[17] The program totaled 20 events per gender, excluding the mixed relay, and was designed to balance track (10 events) and field (9 events) contributions to team totals.[18]

Team Selection and Eligibility

Each European Athletics Member Federation in good standing with the organization is eligible to enter a team in the championships, subject to compliance with the event's technical regulations.[13] Federations determine their own internal selection processes for athletes, typically prioritizing recent performance, fitness, and potential contribution to team scoring, as outlined in national policies such as those from member bodies like UK Athletics.[19] Athletes must meet World Athletics eligibility criteria to represent their federation, which generally requires proof of nationality through citizenship, birth, or long-term residency under rules like WR 41 (change of allegiance) or WR 22 (international competition). They must also be at least 16 years old by 31 December of the competition year, with exceptions raising the minimum to 18 for men's shot put and hammer throw due to equipment and safety considerations.[13] Additionally, all selected athletes are required to hold a valid certification from completing the European Athletics Anti-Doping Education Programme ("I Run Clean"), ensuring awareness of anti-doping obligations.[13] Teams consist of up to 50 athletes—25 men and 25 women—with one athlete nominated per individual event (20 events per gender) and four athletes per relay event (4x100 m and 4x400 m).[13] Reserves may be included for substitutions in case of late withdrawals due to injury or illness, with final confirmations required before the event deadline.[13] Participation across all divisions follows these uniform rules, though federation entries are allocated to divisions based on prior results via promotion and relegation systems.[13]

Schedule and Venues

Timeline of Events

The Third Division events commenced on 20 June 2023 at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, with competitions spanning track, field, and combined events across men's, women's, and mixed disciplines, concluding on 22 June after three days of scoring-based team contests.[20] Concurrently, the Second Division followed an identical three-day format from 20 to 22 June 2023 at the same venue, involving mid-tier European national teams in parallel sessions to determine promotions and relegations.[21] The First Division opened on 23 June 2023, immediately following the lower divisions, and extended through 25 June, featuring 37 events (19 men's, 19 women's, and one mixed) with top-ranked teams accumulating points via individual performances.[1] Final results were tallied on 25 June, marking the end of the championships' competitive phase, which integrated with the broader 2023 European Games schedule from 21 June to 2 July.[4]

Hosting Locations

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships were hosted exclusively at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, within the Upper Silesian conurbation.[8] This venue, a multi-purpose stadium with a capacity exceeding 50,000 spectators, accommodated all three divisions of the competition for the first time in the event's history, diverging from prior years where divisions occurred at separate locations across Europe.[6] The decision aligned the championships with the athletics segment of the 2023 European Games, hosted broadly in the Kraków-Małopolska and Silesia regions, enabling centralized operations from June 20 to 25.[8] Stadion Śląski, originally constructed in 1956 and renovated extensively between 2009 and 2017, features an IAAF-certified track and has previously hosted major events including the 2021 European Athletics Team Championships Super League.[8] Its selection facilitated logistical efficiency, with the Third and Second Divisions competing June 20–22, followed immediately by the First Division June 23–25, minimizing travel for teams while maximizing spectator access in a region with strong athletics infrastructure.[22] [23] The stadium's facilities supported the full program of track and field events, contributing to record attendances and seamless execution amid the integrated European Games framework.[6]

Overall Medal Summary

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships awarded team "medals" based on final standings in each division: gold to the first-placed team, silver to second, and bronze to third, determining promotion and relegation. Italy secured gold in the First Division with 426.5 points, ahead of Poland (402.5, silver) and Germany (387.5, bronze). Hungary claimed gold in the Second Division with 456.5 points, followed by Ukraine (435.5, silver) and Lithuania (372.5, bronze). Second Division Outcomes

The Second Division competition took place from June 20 to 22, 2023, at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, integrated into the broader Silesia 2023 European Athletics Team Championships framework. Hungary secured victory with 456.5 points, marking their return to the elite tier through strong performances across multiple disciplines, including wins in events like the men's 400m by Attila Molnár (45.30 seconds).[31][32] Ukraine finished second with 435.5 points, earning promotion via consistent medal hauls, notably in field events and sprints, despite close contention with Hungary throughout the three days. Lithuania claimed third place and promotion with 372.5 points, clinched in a tense conclusion during the mixed 4x400m relay, where Edis Matusevičius's earlier javelin throw victory (84.22 meters) proved pivotal in separating them from Slovenia by a mere 0.5 points.[31] Slovenia placed fourth with 372 points, retaining their Second Division status after a strong relay effort by Anita Horvat (3:14.72 on her leg) fell short of overtaking Lithuania.[31] Under the 2023 format adjustments, the top three teams—Hungary, Ukraine, and Lithuania—were promoted to the First Division for the subsequent edition, reflecting an expansion of the top league. Lower-ranked teams, including Iceland (246.5 points), faced relegation to the Third Division based on overall scores.[33]

Third Division Outcomes

The Third Division of the 2023 European Athletics Team Championships took place from 20 to 22 June 2023 at Stadion Śląski in Chorzów, Poland, featuring 15 national teams competing across 37 events in men's, women's, and mixed disciplines.[34][35] Ireland secured the title with 494 points, earning promotion to the Second Division for the following year, having built a lead through strong performances in sprints, hurdles, and field events on the final day.[34][36] Austria finished second with 473.5 points, also promoted, while Israel took third place with 434 points and likewise advanced.[35] The bottom two teams, San Marino (192 points) and Liechtenstein (32 points), faced relegation to a Fourth Division.[35] Points were awarded based on placements in each event, with scoring mechanics favoring depth across disciplines rather than isolated standout results.[18]

Key Performances and Highlights

Standout Individual Achievements

Samuele Ceccarelli secured victory in the men's 100 m with a personal best-equalling time of 10.13 seconds, contributing maximum points to Italy's tally on the opening day.[37][38] Gianmarco Tamberi, returning from injury, cleared 2.29 m to win the men's high jump, marking his season's best and a pivotal performance as Italy's team captain.[28][37] Alessandro Sibilio set a championship record of 48.14 seconds in the men's 400 m hurdles, the fastest time in the event that season and underscoring Italy's strength in middle-distance hurdles.[37][30] Zane Weir threw 21.59 m to claim the men's shot put, providing Italy with another field event gold and demonstrating consistent power in a competitive division.[39] Nadia Battocletti dominated the women's 5000 m, finishing in 15:25.09 ahead of Great Britain's Hannah Nuttall by over four seconds, her tactical surge on the final lap securing vital points for Italy.[40] Femke Bol of the Netherlands ran 49.82 seconds to win the women's 400 m, a time that ranked among the year's fastest globally and highlighted her versatility in team formats.[37] Håvard Bentdal Ingvaldsen took the men's 400 m in 44.88 seconds for Norway, edging out competitors in a display of speed that boosted his nation's standing despite overall team challenges.[37] These performances, particularly Italy's seven individual wins across sprints, hurdles, jumps, throws, and distance, were instrumental in their overall team victory with 426.5 points.[28]

Team Strategies and Notable Adaptations

Teams employed strategies centered on athlete selection to optimize points in the scoring system, where the winner of each event earned 16 points descending to 1 point for the last-place finisher across men's and women's disciplines, with combined totals determining overall rankings.[8] National federations prioritized athletes in peak form for specific events to ensure coverage without overlaps, as each team fielded one competitor per discipline, emphasizing depth over star reliance in weaker areas.[19] The 2023 edition introduced adaptations to a revised three-division structure, reducing from four leagues and consolidating events at Stadion Śląski, which necessitated logistical adjustments for teams competing on consecutive days and influenced preparation for sustained performance amid shared venues with the European Games.[8] Exclusion of Russia and Belarusian teams, enforced by European Athletics due to the ongoing war in Ukraine, removed historically dominant forces and reshaped competitive dynamics, compelling remaining nations to recalibrate expectations and exploit gaps in field events and sprints where those teams had excelled.[10] Italy's victory exemplified a balanced, cohesive approach, securing 426.5 points through seven event wins and consistent placings, with captain Gianmarco Tamberi adapting post-injury to claim high jump gold at 2.29 m and foster team spirit.[28] Athletes like Zane Weir highlighted the event's team-oriented ethos, shifting focus from individual pursuits to collective gains, as evidenced by Weir's shot put triumph at 21.59 m contributing to Italy's first-ever title.[41] In contrast, host Poland leveraged crowd support for second place (402.5 points) via targeted strengths in women's steeplechase and relays but faltered in maintaining leads, while Germany ensured bronze (387.5 points) through reliable selections like Julian Weber's javelin win at 86.26 m.[28]

Reception and Analysis

Positive Impacts and Achievements

The 2023 European Athletics Team Championships, integrated with the European Games in Silesia, Poland, were lauded by organizers for their innovative format, which combined elite team competition with broader multi-sport participation, enhancing overall event dynamism and athlete exposure. This hybrid approach facilitated novel scheduling and cross-event synergies, contributing to heightened engagement across European athletics communities.[42] Italy's inaugural First Division victory, amassing 426.50 points and 15 medals from June 23–25 at Stadion Śląski, underscored the event's role in elevating national programs through comprehensive team efforts, including dominant performances in sprints, hurdles, and field events. This breakthrough, the first for Italy since the competition's modern inception, demonstrated effective talent depth and strategic depth, inspiring sustained investment in grassroots and elite training across the nation.[43][44] As hosts, Poland's second-place finish with 402.50 points galvanized local interest and infrastructure utilization, while Spain's historic fourth-place ranking signaled rising competitiveness in southern European athletics, promoting regional rivalries and broader participation incentives for mid-tier nations. The championships' structure, spanning divisions, enabled 40 teams to compete, fostering skill development and international benchmarks that bolster long-term athletic progression without over-reliance on individual stars.[42][45]

Criticisms and Ongoing Debates

The exclusion of teams from Russia and Belarus from the 2023 European Athletics Team Championships stemmed from the European Athletics Council's policy suspending their participation following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, a measure upheld to align with broader international sanctions in athletics.[12] This decision mirrored actions by World Athletics and other governing bodies, prioritizing geopolitical accountability over full continental representation, though it reduced the competitive field in a format reliant on national depth. While no widespread criticisms of the event's organization or execution emerged in reports from the time, the absence of these historically strong teams—Russia having dominated prior editions—prompted limited commentary on potential dilution of overall performance standards, as evidenced by Italy's unexpected First Division victory with 426.5 points ahead of Poland's 402.5.[7] Ongoing debates in European athletics governance, indirectly relevant to team events like the 2023 Championships, center on the long-term efficacy of such bans in deterring state-sponsored issues while preserving sport's universality. Proponents argue the suspensions enforce ethical standards amid documented past doping scandals involving Russian athletes, whereas skeptics, including some within the International Olympic Committee, have questioned the blanket approach's impact on individual athletes unaffiliated with military actions, advocating for case-by-case neutral participation.[46] However, European Athletics maintained the full ban through 2023, with no reversals noted for the Silesia event, reflecting a consensus among member federations that reintegration required verifiable compliance reforms absent at the time. No doping violations or judging disputes specific to the Championships surfaced in anti-doping agency reviews or official recaps, underscoring the event's relatively uncontroversial conduct compared to individual championships.[47]
