Barkhan District

Barkhan (Balochi: بارخان, Urdu: بارخان) is a district in the province of Balochistan in Pakistan.[4] It shares its borders with the province of Punjab to the east and the Balochistan districts of Dera Bugti to the south, Kohlu to the west, Loralai to the northwest and Musakhel to the north. The town of Barkhan serves as the district's headquarters. It was granted the status of a separate district on 31 December 1991, prior to which it was a tehsil of Loralai District.

History

The early history of District Barkhan is obscure, but it is said that this area remained under Muslim rulers and conquerors of Kandahar and India.[6] Baro Khan the founder of the Barozai family of Pannis was a great fighter who came to Barkhan with his tribal people. As such, this valley was named Bar Khan, but with the passage of time it was called Barkhan.[7] The Khetran country was one of the sub-districts of Sewistan and of the Hind Province of Thatta. In Akbar's time it was called Janjah. Khetran is a Baloch tribe. Mir Girazo Khan Baloch was first sardar of the Khetrans. He was succeeded by his son Bakhtiar Khan, who was succeeded by Mir Haji Khan, whose rule is memorable among the Khetrans for a defeat which he inflicted on the Zhob, Kakars. Mir Haji Khan was succeeded by a few nominal chiefs. Mir Haji Khan was a strong man, and the Khetrans prospered under him. He also took revenge on the defeat of the Khetrans from Marris by destroying the fort at Mawand of Karam Khan Bijrani Marri. Haji Khan died, leaving three sons: Umar Khan, Nawab Khan and Balu Khan. Yet he was succeeded by his brother Sayed Khan, who was elected by the Khetrans. He too died, leaving three sons: Dost Muhammad, Sorni Khan and Kadir Bakhsh. Syed Khan was succeeded by his brother Balul Khan. Babul Khan was however opposed by Nawab Khan and Kadir Bakhsh. Kadir Bakhsh went to the Marris and gave his stepmother in marriage to Gazan, the Marri Chief and led Marris Lashkar against the Khetrans, who lost many lives and property during the fights. Sir Robert Sandeman (then Deputy Commissioner, D.G. Khan), however, intervened and made peace between Kadir Bakhsh and Babul Khan.

British Raj

Due to prolonged war among Khetrans, Lunis and Marris, in 1880, the Khetrans submitted a petition to the British authorities at D.G Khan, offering to pay revenue and to be taken under British protection. The Khetrans' country remained under the political control of the British authorities of D.G Khan from 1878 to 1883. In 1883, Sardar Baloch Khan met Mr Bruce, the Political Agent of Thaal Chotiali, with a Jirga of his tribesmen. This settled the dispute between the Khetrans, Luni and Marris. In 1884, the Khetrans came under the authority of the Agent to the Governor General in Balochistan. The Leghari Barkhan circle, comprising two valleys, Barkhan and Vitakari, was transferred from Punjab control to Balochistan control in 1884. This district, from where the Leghari tribe migrated to Dera Ghazi Khan, was brought under the direct administration of Balochistan in 1887, when work on the construction of D.G Khan - Pishin Road was started and a Tehsildar was posted at Barkhan. In 1889, Barkhan tehsil was transferred to the Zhob Agency with headquarters at Loralai. This tehsil however was transferred again to Thal Chotaili Agency in 1892. In October 1903, the Thal Chotaili and Zhob Agencies were remodeled and three new agencies were created: Sibi, Loralai and Zhob. The Barkhan Tehsil was transferred to the Loralai Agency. In 1918, the Khetrans allied with the Marri tribe in an uprising against British rule. British forces under Major-General Richard Wapshare inflicted a defeat on the combined forces at Fort Munro on 15 March 1918 and subsequently recaptured Barkhan, ending Khetran involvement in the rebellion.

Geography

Barkhan is the tenth-smallest district of Balochistan and has an area of 3,514 square kilometers.[1] Barkhan District lies between 69°3'-70° 4' East longitude and 29°37'-30°21' North latitude, consisting of one tehsil and eight union councils. The location of Barkhan is 541 km (aerial distance) south-west (220 degrees bearing) of Pakistan's capital city Islamabad and 245 km east (97 degrees bearing) from Quetta City, the provincial capital of Balochistan.

Topography

The landscape of Barkhan consists of plains, valleys and mountains varying in ground elevation ranging from 841 to 2,031 meters above Mean Sea Level (MSL). The district consists of one main valley, called Barkhan, and several smaller valleys, which are separated from the main valley by low ranges of hills running in a southwest direction. The Barkhan valley is enclosed on the north side by scattered low hills, which divide the drainage water of the valley from the waters flowing into the Rakhni stream in the southwest, and on the west and northwest side by the great Jandran range. The valley ends in the Vitakari valley. To the east of the Sukha range is the Kharcha valley, which is bounded by the Phulai range on the east. The hills in the district belong to the Suleman range; the principal hills are known as Kala Pahar in the north, Karwada, Bibar, Jandra and Mar or Mukhmar in the west, Andari and Sukha in the south, Phulai and Khawaj in the east, and Mazara, Tagha, Dig and Deka in the centre and Rakhni areas. The ranges mostly consist of earth sand and stones; some portions are rocky and not easily accessible. The Jandran range is mostly rocky. In the south runs the Han stream with its tributaries. The Han stream runs in a southwest direction and carries the drainage of the Han pass and the southeastern slopes of the Jandran range. At Dhamani (Barkhan Tehsil) the stream becomes perennial up to its junction with the Kah River in Dera Ghazi Khan district. Dhaula stream, its tributary, brings water from the hills situated to the south of the Bagha valley and irrigates a number of villages in the Barkhan valley and joins Han in Vitakari. Rakhni and Han join Kah Stream which then passes into the district of Dera Ghazi Khan. A third important stream is Bala Dhaka. This stream lies in Berg-Sham Mauza of the Barkhan Tehsil. The stream is a tributary of the Narechi River (Duki Tehsil). Water in this stream comes down from the southwestern slopes of hills located north of the Han pass. The stream irrigates a very small area of Mauza Berg-Sham, and then its water passes in the Duki Tehsil.

Archaeological sites

During geological mapping and bio-stratigraphic research activity in Barkhan District, the geologists of the Geological Survey of Pakistan (GSP) discovered the first dinosaur fossils in Pakistan.[10]

Khetranisaurus

Fossils of the titanosaurian sauropod Khetranisaurus barkhani, dating to the Late Cretaceous (approximately 70 million years ago), were discovered in the Vitakri area of Barkhan District.[11] Khetranisaurus barkhani is a species of titanosaurian sauropod dinosaur from the Late Cretaceous Pab Formation. Etymology: Khetrani, honoring the Khetran tribe of Barkhan district; saurus means lizard (from Greek). The species specific epithet barkhani, honoring the Barkhan which is the host District of dinosaurs.[11]

Climate

Barkhan has a hot semi-arid climate (Köppen climate classification BSh) with very hot summers and mild winters. Population

As of the 2023 census, Barkhan district has 23,053 households and a population of 210,249. The district has a sex ratio of 108.95 males to 100 females and a literacy rate of 33.62%: 41.63% for males and 24.93% for females.[15][16] 84,790 (40.33% of the surveyed population) are under 10 years of age. 14,425 (6.86%) live in urban areas.[15]

At the time of the 2023 census, 98.62% of the population spoke Balochi, 0.6% spoke Saraiki, and 0.54% Pashto as their first language.[19] There is also a sizeable community of speakers of the Indo-Aryan language Khetrani.[20] There are four main tribes in Barkhan District: the Khetran, Marri tribe, Hasni tribe and Buzdar tribe. The Khetrans have a chief (called Tumandar) of the tribe, while, each sub-clan/section is represented by a headman (called Wadera).

Government and politics

Barkhan started functioning as a district on 31 December 1991. Previously, it was a tehsil of district Loralai. The main motivation for granting Barkhan the status of a district was to provide basic facilities at a grassroots level and to give people easy access to public offices. District Barkhan comprises only one Tehsil, Barkhan itself. The Balochistan Local Government Ordinance (BLGO) of 1980 was intended to involve local populations in the development of their areas; it was replaced by BLGO 2001, which introduced the concept of district government. Under BLGO 2001, Barkhan District had one tehsil and eight union councils. The district was administered by a single line of administration that covered both the rural as well as the urban areas. The system continued for two tenures and was again modified through the approval of BLGA 2010. The present system, under the Local Government Act 2010 (as amended in 2023), is similar to BLGO 1980 in that it emphasizes a rural-urban division. Local government elections were held across Balochistan, including Barkhan, on 29 May 2022, with subsequent by-elections.[21]

District Council

There are eight union councils' representatives in the District Council of Barkhan, one from each union council. Special seats are allocated to the representatives of workers/peasants, women and minorities. All the elected chairmen of the Municipal Committees are also members of the District Council. The District Council is concerned with rural development activities and functions pertaining to public health, education, agricultural development and economic welfare, articles of food and drink, drainage, livestock and dairy development, culture and public safety. The District Council can generate revenue by levying fees and various taxes as given in part II of second schedule of BLGA 2010.

Federal and government offices and services

A number of Federal and Provincial Government departments provide services at the district level. Major departments, including those for election, police, revenue, and judiciary, are functional at the district level, with some having offices at the tehsil level.[22]

Education and educational institutions

As of the 2023 census, the literacy rate in Barkhan District is 33.62% overall, with 41.63% for males and 24.93% for females.[16] According to the 2023 census, 28.5% of children aged 5-16 are out of school.[16] The Balochistan Education Management Information System (EMIS) reported 661 schools in the district for the 2022-23 academic year, including 589 primary schools, 42 middle schools, 24 high schools, and 6 higher secondary schools. Of these, 449 are boys' schools, 189 girls' schools, and 23 co-educational. Single-teacher primary schools account for 75.2% of primary institutions. Flora and fauna

Barkhan District features diverse vegetation zones adapted to its arid and mountainous terrain, including uphill steep rocky cliffs, foothills, piedmont plains, and dry stream beds. Key native plants include phulai (Acacia modesta), wild olive (Olea cuspidata), jujube (Ziziphus nummularia), karir (Capparis aphylla), tharkha (Artemisia maritima), mazri palm (Nannorrhops ritchiana), dwarf saxaul (Haloxylon griffithii), and salt cedar (Tamarix spp.). Medicinal plants such as prickly chaff flower (Achyranthes aspera) and wild mint (Mentha spicata) are also found. [24]
  • Mammals: wolf, jackal, hyena, fox, wild cat, mongoose, deer, hare, porcupine, Afghan hedgehog [24] [25]
  • Birds: chukar (Alectoris chukar), see-see partridge (Ammoperdix griseogularis), grey partridge, pigeon, sandgrouse, quail, waterfowl, vulture, kite, dove, sparrow, lark, hoopoe [24]
  • Reptiles: Afghan tortoise (Agrionemys horsfieldii), Indian cobra (Naja naja), viper species [25]
  • Major crops:
    • Rabi crops: wheat, barley, vegetables, fodder
    • Kharif crops: sorghum (jowar), millet (bajra), maize, mung bean, mash bean, fruits, onion, potato, vegetables, melon, chili, fodder, garlic, cotton [24]
  • Major fruits: apricot, peach, grapes, almond, pomegranate, apple, plum, watermelon, musk melon [24]

Natural resources

Barkhan District has deposits of coal and gypsum. The coal deposits, primarily in areas of the Chamalang coalfields extending from adjacent districts, are not large. In some places, very minute quantities of coal were found, but exploration was abandoned as there was no prospect of commercial benefits. The Oil & Gas Development Company Limited (OGDCL) discovered gas and condensate at the Jandran X-04 exploratory well in Barkhan in 2021.[26]
