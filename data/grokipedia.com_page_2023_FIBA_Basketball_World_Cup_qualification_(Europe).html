2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup qualification (Europe)

The 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup qualification (Europe) comprised the pre-qualifying and qualifying tournaments through which 12 European national teams earned berths to the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, held across the Philippines, Japan, and Indonesia.[1]
Pre-qualifiers from November 2021 to February 2022 involved 16 teams in four groups of four, with the top two from each group advancing to join the 24 teams that had qualified for EuroBasket 2022, forming a main qualifying field of 32 teams.[2]
The main qualifiers unfolded in two rounds: a first round from August to December 2022 with eight groups of four teams playing home-and-away round-robin matches, followed by a second round where the top two teams from each first-round group combined into four groups of six, with each team facing the four opponents from the other two first-round groups in home-and-away format, spanning November 2022 to February 2023.[2][3]
The top three teams from each of the four second-round groups (I, J, K, L) secured qualification, yielding the 12 European slots based on win-loss records and tiebreakers.[3]
The qualified teams were Finland, France, Georgia, Germany, Greece, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Montenegro, Serbia, Slovenia, and Spain, with Finland becoming the first to clinch a spot in August 2022 and debutants Latvia and Georgia marking historic first appearances.[1][1]
This process underscored the rising competitiveness among mid-tier European nations, as evidenced by the advancement of lower-ranked teams like Latvia (29th) and Georgia (32nd) over some established programs, while powerhouses such as Serbia and France maintained their streaks of consecutive qualifications.[1]

Background

Qualification Slots for Europe

Europe was allocated 12 slots for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, the highest number among FIBA's continental confederations, reflecting the region's depth in international basketball.[4][5] These berths were not granted automatically to any teams based on prior rankings or continental championships; instead, all were contested through a dedicated qualification pathway.[2] The qualification process involved 32 European national teams competing for the 12 spots, comprising the 24 teams that had advanced to the 2022 FIBA EuroBasket and an additional 8 teams that successfully navigated the preceding pre-qualifiers.[2] This structure ensured a merit-based selection, with group stage performances determining advancement to later rounds and ultimate qualification. The top three teams from each first-round group progressed, feeding into second-round groups where the highest finishers secured the World Cup berths.[2] No host nation slots applied to Europe, as the tournament was co-hosted by Asian countries.[4]

Eligibility, Seeding, and Draw Procedures

Eligibility for the European qualification process encompassed all 50 national teams affiliated with FIBA Europe, subject to registration requirements outlined in FIBA's Internal Regulations. Teams that qualified for the 2022 FIBA EuroBasketâ€”comprising 24 nationsâ€”advanced directly to the first round of the World Cup Qualifiers. The remaining registered teams, excluding those competing in the EuroBasket, participated in the Pre-Qualifiers to contest eight spots in the main Qualifiers.[6] Seeding for draws was determined using the FIBA Men's World Rankings, updated periodically by FIBA. For the Pre-Qualifiers second round draw, the eight advancing teams from the first round were assigned seeds 1 or 2 according to their rankings, ensuring higher-ranked teams were positioned to form competitive groups. In the main Qualifiers draw, the 32 participating teams (24 EuroBasket qualifiers plus 8 Pre-Qualifiers advancers) were allocated into eight pots of four teams each, with Pot 1 containing the highest-ranked teams such as Spain, Slovenia, France, and Lithuania.[7][8] Draw procedures for the Pre-Qualifiers second round occurred on April 28, 2021, pairing the seeded teams into two groups of four. The primary Qualifiers draw took place on August 31, 2021, at FIBA's headquarters in Mies, Switzerland, conducted virtually due to pandemic restrictions. To promote group balance, teams from Pots 1, 4, 5, and 8 were drawn into Groups A, C, E, and G, while those from Pots 2, 3, 6, and 7 were assigned to Groups B, D, F, and H; no restrictions prevented same-nation matchups within the first round.[8][6]

Format

Pre-Qualifiers Format

The Pre-Qualifiers determined eight European teams to join the 24 nations that qualified for FIBA EuroBasket 2022 in the main qualification phase for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup.[7] The competition unfolded in two rounds, involving lower-ranked FIBA member federations excluded from direct EuroBasket qualification.[9] In the First Round, held in February 2021, eight teams were divided into two groups of four for single round-robin tournaments. Group A included Albania, Belarus, Cyprus, and Portugal, while Group B comprised Iceland, Kosovo, Luxembourg, and Slovakia. Each team played the other three in its group once, with standings determined by win-loss record, point difference in head-to-head matches, and overall point difference as tie-breakers. The top two teams from each groupâ€”Belarus and Portugal from Group A, Iceland and Slovakia from Group Bâ€”advanced to the Second Round.[7] The Second Round, conducted in August 2021, featured the four First Round qualifiers seeded as Pot 3 alongside eight teams eliminated in the EuroBasket 2022 qualifiers (Austria, Denmark, Latvia, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Romania, Sweden, Switzerland) divided into Pots 1 and 2 based on FIBA rankings.[7] These 12 teams formed four groups of three via a draw in Mies, Switzerland, on April 29, 2021, with geographic and seeding restrictions to avoid same-pot matchups where possible.[9] Within each group, teams played a single round-robin format, with the top two advancing to the main European qualifiers; tie-breakers followed the same criteria as the First Round.[9] The advancing teams were Austria, Belarus, Denmark, Iceland, Latvia, Portugal, Sweden, and Switzerland.[2]

Qualifiers Format

The qualifiers for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup in Europe involved 32 national teams: the 24 that qualified for EuroBasket 2022 and the eight that advanced from the pre-qualifiers.[10] These teams competed across six FIBA international windows from November 2021 to February 2023.[10] In the first round, the 32 teams were drawn into eight groups of four, with each team playing home-and-away round-robin matches against the other three teams in its group, resulting in six games per team.[10] The top three teams from each group advanced to the second round, yielding 24 progressing teams; the bottom team in each group was eliminated.[10] The second round reorganized the 24 advancing teams into four groups of six, with pairings designed to avoid rematches from the first round where possible; teams carried over their results and points from the first-round games against second-round opponents.[10] Within each group, teams played home-and-away round-robin matches against the other five, adding ten games per team.[10] The top three teams from each second-round group secured one of Europe's 12 qualification spots for the World Cup, while the other teams were eliminated.[10]

Qualification Rules and Tie-Breakers

Teams were ranked within each group of the first and second rounds based on the number of victories achieved during the games played in that round, with results from first-round matches between teams assigned to the same second-round group carried over. A win awarded ranking priority over losses, with no points system for draws as basketball games do not end in ties under FIBA rules.[10] In cases of tied win-loss records, tie-breakers followed the procedures outlined in Article 18 of the FIBA Official Basketball Rules (Section D: Classification of teams). For two tied teams that had played each other, the head-to-head result determined the ranking; if not played or still tied, the team with the better goal average (point difference) across all group games prevailed. For three or more tied teams, the process began by classifying them based on results among themselves using the two-team criteria (head-to-head games forming a sub-group), followed by goal average in those sub-group games if needed; unresolved ties then proceeded to overall group goal average, total points scored in the group, and finally the teams' positions in the FIBA World Ranking.[11] Qualification to the FIBA Basketball World Cup 2023 required finishing in the top three positions in one of the four second-round groups, which consisted of six teams each formed by merging two first-round groups (with carried-over results). This structure ensured 12 European teams advanced, matching FIBA's allocation for the continent. No cross-group comparisons were needed for qualification, as advancement depended solely on intra-group standings resolved via the above criteria.[10][12]

Pre-Qualifiers

First Round

The first round of the European Pre-Qualifiers involved eight teams divided into two groups of four, competing in home-and-away round-robin matches across FIBA international windows from 20 to 28 November 2020 and 15 to 23 February 2021. The top two teams from each group advanced to the second round, where they joined eight teams eliminated from EuroBasket 2022 qualification.[13][9] Group A consisted of Albania, Belarus, Cyprus, and Portugal. Belarus topped the group with decisive wins, including a 97â€“41 rout of Cyprus on 27 November 2020, finishing undefeated and advancing alongside Portugal, who secured second place with victories such as over Albania. Albania and Cyprus were eliminated after failing to win enough matches to challenge the leaders.[14][13] Group B featured Iceland, Kosovo, Luxembourg, and Slovakia. Iceland clinched first place with key results, including an 83â€“74 home win over Slovakia on 20 November 2020 and a narrow 78â€“80 loss to Kosovo that did not derail their advancement. Slovakia took second, edging out Kosovo and Luxembourg through consistent performances, such as a 77â€“73 victory over Kosovo on 26 November 2020. Kosovo and Luxembourg did not advance.[15][16][7]

Second Round

The second round of the European Pre-Qualifiers consisted of 12 teams divided into four groups of three following a draw held on 28 April 2021 in Berlin.[9] Each group played a double round-robin format (home and away matches), with teams contesting four games apiece; the top two finishers in each group advanced to the main European qualifiers for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup.[17] Matches occurred between 29 July and 29 August 2021, incorporating teams that advanced from the first round (Belarus, Iceland, Portugal, Slovakia) alongside eight nations that failed to qualify for FIBA EuroBasket 2022 (Austria, Denmark, Latvia, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Romania, Sweden, Switzerland).[7] The advancing teams were Belarus, Iceland, Latvia, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Portugal, Slovakia, and Sweden, completing the 32-team field for the European qualifiers.[18] Latvia and Montenegro each finished undefeated with 4-0 records in their respective groups, while Sweden and Portugal recorded 3-1 marks. Belarus, Iceland, North Macedonia, and Slovakia advanced with 2-2 records, determined by head-to-head results and point differentials where necessary.[18] In the decisive final game of one group on 18 August 2021, Slovakia defeated North Macedonia 82-66 in Skopje, but both teams secured qualification due to insurmountable leads over Romania, who were eliminated with a 0-4 record.[19] Iceland clinched progression with key wins, including 89-73 over Denmark on 17 August 2021 and 95-73 against Austria.[20] Latvia dominated their group unbeaten, highlighted by a 92-64 victory over Romania on 30 July 2021.[21]

External Factors

Exclusion of Russia and Belarus

In response to Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, FIBA suspended the national teams of Russia and Belarus from all its international competitions on March 1, 2022, citing the need to prioritize the safety and security of participants amid the ongoing conflict.[22][23] This initial suspension effectively barred both nations from qualifying activities for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, aligning with broader international sports sanctions against Russia for initiating the war and Belarus for its military support and facilitation of Russian operations.[24][25] On May 17, 2022, FIBA formalized the exclusion by withdrawing Russia from Group H and Belarus from Group B of the European qualifiers, with no replacement teams added to maintain the tournament structure; this decision extended the suspensions indefinitely pending further review.[26][27] Russia's removal left Group H with five teams instead of six, while Belarus's withdrawal reduced Group B similarly, altering the qualification dynamics by eliminating potential spots for these historically competitive European powersâ€”Russia had qualified for the previous three World Cups, and Belarus had participated in qualifiers multiple times.[28] The exclusions were upheld through subsequent FIBA executive committee reviews, with bans extended into 2023 and beyond, reflecting sustained geopolitical pressures rather than basketball-specific performance issues; FIBA emphasized neutrality in sport but prioritized conflict-related risks over reintegration appeals from the affected federations.[29][30] No Russian or Belarusian players were permitted to compete under neutral flags or with other national teams in these qualifiers, enforcing a complete isolation from the European pathway to the World Cup.[31]

Player Participation Challenges

The European qualifiers for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, spanning November 2021, February 2022, and August 2022, presented significant hurdles for national teams in securing player availability, primarily due to overlapping club commitments and the demanding professional schedules of NBA and EuroLeague players. During the in-season windows of November 2021 and February 2022, NBA teams restricted participation to avoid disrupting their regular season and playoffs, resulting in limited involvement from league stars; for instance, many top European NBA talents prioritized club duties, leaving teams like Slovenia and Greece to rely on domestic or lower-tier professionals for early matches. EuroLeague clubs similarly emphasized player preservation amid their competitive calendar, exacerbating absences for non-NBA affiliated Europeans.[32] The August 2022 window, held during the NBA off-season, saw greater NBA engagement with 41 active players participating, including Nikola JokiÄ‡ for Serbia, Giannis Antetokounmpo for Greece, and Luka DonÄiÄ‡ for Slovenia, as teams pushed for final qualification spots. However, this period intensified injury risks owing to its proximity to the EuroBasket 2022 tournament starting September 1, creating a congested schedule that coaches and analysts criticized for endangering athlete health. Notable injuries included Spain's Sergio Llull sustaining a muscular issue against Iceland on August 25, 2022, forcing him to withdraw from EuroBasket; Italy's Danilo Gallinari suffering a knee injury during qualifiers, sidelining him for the continental event; and Georgia's Tornike Shengelia also impacted, highlighting how qualifier demands contributed to broader absences in subsequent competitions.[32][33][33] These challenges disproportionately affected mid-tier and smaller European nations lacking deep rosters, as reliance on a handful of key players amplified the impact of opt-outs or injuries, while ongoing tensions in the FIBA-EuroLeague relationship occasionally complicated releases, though less acutely in 2022 compared to prior disputes. Coaches like Italy's Luca Banchi noted the "extremely tight schedule" as a systemic risk factor, underscoring the need for better calendar coordination to mitigate fatigue and prioritize player welfare without compromising qualification integrity.[34]

Outcomes

Qualified European Teams

The European qualification process for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup awarded 12 slots to teams from FIBA Europe, with the top two finishers from each of the six groups in the second round of the main qualifiers advancing, alongside host nation Germany which received automatic qualification but still participated in the process for ranking purposes.[1] The qualifiers concluded in February 2023 after six windows of games from November 2021 to February 2023, involving 32 teams in total across first and second rounds following pre-qualifiers.[2] The qualified teams were Finland, France, Georgia, Germany, Greece, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Montenegro, Serbia, Slovenia, and Spain.[1] Among these, debutants included Georgia, Latvia, and Montenegro, marking their first appearances in the FIBA World Cup tournament.[1] Finland secured qualification earliest on 11 November 2022 by topping Group G in the second round with a 7â€“1 record, defeating teams including Sweden and Israel.[2] Serbia clinched the final spot on 26 February 2023, finishing second in Group F behind Latvia after a 95â€“86 victory over Belgium.[5]

Performance in the World Cup and Beyond

Slovenia delivered the most notable performance among the qualifier teams, advancing to the quarterfinals before a 100-89 loss to Canada on September 6.[35] In the group stage and second round, they secured key victories, including a 91-80 upset over Australia on September 1, propelled by Luka DonÄiÄ‡'s 26 points and 11 assists.[36] Slovenia then claimed 7th place overall with an 89-85 win against Italy on September 9, where DonÄiÄ‡ recorded 34 points and 8 assists.[37] Greece reached the second round with a first-round victory over New Zealand, 83-74, on August 30, but managed only that win in the tournament, suffering defeats including 109-81 to the United States on August 28 and 92-67 to Lithuania on September 1.[38][39] They finished 15th overall, earning a berth in the Olympic Qualifying Tournament (OQT).[40] Poland advanced from Group A with wins over the Dominican Republic (90-80 on August 29) and Mexico (93-80 on August 31), despite a 107-81 upset loss to South Sudan on September 1.[41] Elimination followed in the second round against stronger opponents, resulting in a 17th-place finish and an OQT spot.[40] Finland exited the first round winless (0-3) in Group B, losing 89-63 to Germany on August 29, 98-88 to Japan on August 27 after leading by 18 points, and 98-72 to Australia on September 3.[42][43] In the 17th-32nd place classification, they defeated Cape Verde 100-77 on August 31 but ended 24th overall, also securing an OQT invitation.[44][40] None of the four teams qualified directly for the 2024 Paris Olympics, with those berths awarded to the top two European finishers, Germany and Serbia.[45] All participated in the OQTs but failed to advance, with Greece notably losing in the final of their event to Slovenia (though Slovenia also did not qualify).[40]
