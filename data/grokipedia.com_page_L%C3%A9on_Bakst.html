Léon Bakst

Léon Bakst (1866–1924) was a Russian artist of Jewish descent renowned as a painter and preeminent stage designer whose opulent costumes and sets revolutionized early 20th-century theatre, particularly through his collaboration with Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes.[1][2] His designs emphasized vibrant, sensual colors, intricate patterns, and exotic motifs inspired by Persian and ancient sources, creating immersive spectacles that fused visual art with performance.[1][3] Bakst's seminal contributions included the sets and costumes for ballets such as Cléopâtre (1909), Scheherazade (1910), and The Firebird (1910), which not only propelled the Ballets Russes to international acclaim but also exerted a lasting influence on fashion, textiles, and decorative arts by promoting bold chromatic experimentation and ornamental richness.[1][2][4] Prior to his theatrical prominence, he participated in the Mir Iskusstva movement, producing paintings and illustrations that reflected a synthesis of symbolism and neoclassicism, before relocating to Paris around 1906 where his work gained wider European traction.[5][6] Though his career spanned easel painting, graphic arts, and theoretical writings on aesthetics, Bakst's legacy endures primarily through his transformative impact on scenic design, which challenged realist conventions and anticipated modernist integrations of color and form in the performing arts.[4][7] His designs' emphasis on psychological depth via visual excess influenced subsequent generations of artists and designers, extending beyond ballet to broader cultural expressions of luxury and fantasy.[3][2]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Family Background

Léon Bakst was born Lev Samoylovich Rosenberg, originally named Leyb-Khaim Izrailevich Rosenberg, on May 10, 1866 (New Style), in Grodno, then a city in the Russian Empire (now Hrodna, Belarus).[8][9] He hailed from a middle-class family of Orthodox Jews, a demographic facing restrictions under the Russian Empire's Pale of Settlement policies confining Jewish residence.[10][9] His father, Samuel Rosenberg, supported the family through modest means, while the household maintained ties to the tailoring trade exemplified by Bakst's grandfather, renowned for his exceptional craftsmanship in garment-making.[10][9] This vocational heritage reflected the practical occupations common among Jewish families in the region amid economic and social constraints. Bakst later adopted the surname "Bakst" as a pseudonym derived from his grandmother's maiden name, a choice that distanced him from his birth surname while pursuing artistic endeavors in a context often hostile to Jewish professionals.[8]

Initial Artistic Training in Russia

Léon Bakst, born Lev Samoylovich Rosenberg, demonstrated an early aptitude for drawing, winning a contest at age twelve that reinforced his ambition to pursue painting professionally.[1] Following his family's relocation to Saint Petersburg in 1883 due to his father's business ventures, he began auditing classes at the Imperial Academy of Arts, initially as a non-matriculated external student.[11] Bakst's studies at the Academy from 1883 to 1887 focused on foundational techniques in painting and drawing, though he did not pursue a formal degree path and supported himself through part-time work as a book illustrator.[12] His training emphasized realist approaches prevalent in Russian academic art at the time, but he departed the institution after approximately 18 months of intensive engagement, reportedly due to the examiners' discomfort with a hyper-realistic rendition of the Pietà that blurred distinctions between art and lifelike depiction.[13] This period marked Bakst's immersion in St. Petersburg's artistic milieu, where he honed skills in portraiture and illustration amid the Academy's rigorous curriculum, laying groundwork for his later stylistic evolutions despite the abrupt end to his enrollment.

European Influences and Studies

In the early 1890s, Bakst embarked on extensive travels across Europe, visiting countries such as Spain, Germany, and Greece, alongside excursions to North Africa including Tunisia and Algeria. These journeys provided direct exposure to Mediterranean and Oriental artistic motifs, contrasting with the more insular Russian academic environment he had known, and sparked an early interest in exotic and historical themes that would recur in his oeuvre.[14][15] Between 1893 and 1896, Bakst established residence in Paris, the epicenter of European modernism, where he pursued formal studies at the Académie Julian. Under the tutelage of Jean-Léon Gérôme, a leading exponent of academic realism noted for his precise anatomical rendering and Orientalist history paintings, and the Finnish landscape painter Albert Edelfelt, Bakst honed skills in draftsmanship, color application, and narrative composition.[14][16][8] Gérôme's influence manifested in Bakst's adoption of a rigorous realist approach, emphasizing detailed observation and sculptural form, which tempered his innate decorative tendencies with technical discipline. This Parisian interlude also acquainted him with emerging currents like Symbolism and the nascent Art Nouveau, though his primary gain was a synthesis of French academic methods with broader European eclectic sources, preparing the ground for his return to Russia and involvement in progressive art circles.[14][15]

Professional Career

Early Artistic Works and Collaborations

Bakst's early artistic endeavors in the late 1890s centered on portraiture and illustrative work within the Mir Iskusstva (World of Art) circle, a group advocating for artistic renewal through engagement with European modernism and Russian heritage.[2] He co-founded the influential Mir Iskusstva magazine in 1899 alongside Sergei Diaghilev, providing graphics and illustrations that highlighted his emerging talent in decorative and symbolic styles.[17] These contributions emphasized a rejection of academic realism in favor of aesthetic refinement and cultural synthesis, aligning with the group's exhibitions and publications from 1898 onward.[8] A pivotal early work was his Portrait of Alexander Benois (1898), rendered in watercolor and pastel on paper (64.5 × 100.3 cm), now held in the Russian Museum.[18] The piece captures Benois, a fellow Mir Iskusstva co-founder and critic, seated and absorbed in reading, employing Art Nouveau influences with fluid lines and introspective mood to convey intellectual depth.[19] This portrait exemplified Bakst's shift from Peredvizhniki realism toward symbolist tendencies, fostering collaborations that elevated his status among St. Petersburg's avant-garde.[20] Collaborations extended to joint exhibitions and theoretical discussions with Benois and Diaghilev, where Bakst's designs for book illustrations and periodical art promoted the group's vision of art as integral to life.[2] By the early 1900s, these efforts included satirical illustrations during periods of social unrest, though his primary focus remained on portraits like Dinner (1902, oil on canvas, 150 × 100 cm, Russian Museum), depicting intimate domestic scenes with emerging decorative exuberance.[3] Such works laid the groundwork for his later theatrical innovations, bridging fine art with applied design in pre-Ballets Russes collaborations.[21]

Breakthrough with Ballets Russes

Léon Bakst's association with Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes began in 1909, marking a pivotal shift in his career from portraiture and illustration to renowned stage design. For the company's inaugural Paris season at the Théâtre du Châtelet on May 19, 1909, Bakst created the sets and costumes for Michel Fokine's Cléopâtre, an Egyptian-themed ballet that showcased his penchant for sumptuous, oriental-inspired aesthetics with rich colors and elaborate textures.[1][22] This production, featuring dancers like Anna Pavlova and Vaslav Nijinsky, highlighted Bakst's ability to integrate visual opulence with narrative drama, contributing to the Ballets Russes' immediate sensational success in Western Europe.[2] The 1909 season's triumph elevated Bakst to the role of principal designer, solidifying his collaboration with Diaghilev, whom he had known since their involvement in the Mir iskusstva movement in the 1890s. Bakst's designs for Cléopâtre emphasized exotic decadence through flowing garments and monumental backdrops, departing from realistic theatre conventions and influencing a broader artistic revival of theatrical spectacle.[1][2] This breakthrough not only garnered critical acclaim for its visual innovation but also positioned Bakst as a key figure in modernizing ballet aesthetics, with his work praised for creating immersive, color-drenched environments that enhanced choreography and music.[22] Building on this momentum, Bakst's subsequent designs, such as for Schéhérazade in 1910 at the Théâtre national de l'Opéra, further amplified his fame with sensual, Persian-infused sets and costumes featuring Nijinsky and Ida Rubinstein, embodying a "perfect tornado of color" that captivated audiences and critics alike.[2][1] By 1914, Bakst had served as the Ballets Russes' artistic director for stage elements, his exotic motifs and vibrant palettes revolutionizing European perceptions of Russian art and propelling his international reputation.[1]

Major Stage Designs and Innovations

Bakst's collaboration with Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes from 1909 onward marked a pivotal phase in his career, where he created sets and costumes that revolutionized theatrical aesthetics through vivid coloration and exotic motifs. His designs for the 1909 production of Cléopâtre, choreographed by Michel Fokine, introduced oriental-style decor and costumes that emphasized dramatic sensuality and fantastical elements, setting a precedent for the company's signature opulence.[1] In 1910, Bakst's work on Scheherazade, also by Fokine with music by Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov, achieved widespread acclaim for its oriental fantasy, featuring intricate patterns, bold hues like turquoise and crimson, and sensual violence in depictions of harem intrigue. The costumes, such as those for eunuchs and sultans rendered in gouache and metallic paints, integrated luxurious textiles and dynamic forms that enhanced the ballet's narrative intensity and influenced contemporary fashion trends toward exoticism.[23][3] That same year, for Igor Stravinsky's The Firebird, Bakst designed the titular bird's costume using gouache, watercolor, and gold accents on feathers and iridescent fabrics, capturing mythical luminosity and movement while harmonizing with Alexander Benois's sets to evoke Russian folklore's supernatural vibrancy. This breakthrough production highlighted Bakst's innovation in subordinating realistic detail to emotional expressiveness, employing non-naturalistic colors to amplify choreographic drama.[24] Bakst's innovations extended to unified stagecraft, where costumes, sets, and lighting formed a cohesive sensory assault, as seen in Daphnis et Chloé (1912) with its pastoral yet erotic Mediterranean motifs in watercolor designs. His emphasis on pattern repetition, sinuous lines, and Eastern-inspired ornamentation broke from 19th-century naturalism, prioritizing psychological impact and audience immersion, thereby reshaping modern theater design's focus on visual rhythm and cultural fusion.[2][3]

Painting, Writing, and Diversified Output

Bakst created a series of easel paintings in the late 1890s and early 1900s, distinct from his theatrical designs, often employing watercolour, pastel, and oil to depict portraits and symbolic subjects. His Portrait of Alexander Benois (1898), executed in watercolour and pastel on paper measuring 64.5 × 100.3 cm, captures the art critic in a contemplative pose and resides in the State Russian Museum, having been acquired in 1903.[18] Similarly, Dinner (also known as Supper), completed in 1902 as an oil on canvas (150 × 100 cm), portrays Anna Kind, wife of Benois, in an intimate domestic scene emphasizing dynamic outlines and Art Nouveau motifs, held by the State Russian Museum.[25] One of Bakst's most notable non-theatrical works is Terror Antiquus (1908), an oil on canvas measuring 250 × 270 cm, depicting the apocalyptic destruction of an ancient city with symbolic elements of chaos and fate, now in the State Russian Museum.[26] Other paintings from this period include Portrait of Lyubov Pavlovna Gritsenko (1903, oil on canvas, Tretyakov Gallery) and a self-portrait (1906, chalk, sanguine, and colored pencil on paper, 76 × 52 cm, Tretyakov Gallery), showcasing his mastery of line drawing and portraiture.[27] Bakst's literary output encompassed essays, theoretical articles, and fiction, often addressing art, theater, and fashion, with contributions to Russian, French, and American publications such as Apollo, Comoedia, and the New York Tribune. Key works include "Nudity on Stage" (1911, in Nikolai Evreinov's collection), "How the Woman of the Future Will Dress" (1913), and "On the Art of Today" (1914), reflecting his views on modernism and performance.[28] In 1923, he published the autobiographical novel Cruel First Love and the travelogue "Serov and I in Greece," alongside an unpublished essay "Le Théâtre de demain" and a libretto for the ballet La nuit ensorcelée.[28] His manuscripts, totaling over 2,600 items, are preserved in the State Tretyakov Gallery archives, underscoring his role as a critic and theorist in Russian Modernism.[28] Beyond painting and writing, Bakst diversified into fashion and textile design, creating apparel, fabric patterns, and accessories that extended his exotic aesthetic into everyday use. He designed screen-printed silk panels for dresses or furnishings, such as a 1922–1924 twill weave piece in the Art Institute of Chicago, and promoted floral-inspired lifestyles through lectures in 1923–1924.[29] Bakst collaborated with producers like Madame Muelle for non-stage clothing and exhibited "textile drawings" in New York in 1927, influencing modern fashion with bold patterns and historical motifs derived from his theoretical writings on attire evolution.[30][31] These efforts positioned him as an innovator in applied arts, bridging theater opulence with wearable design.[32]

Artistic Style and Contributions

Visual Aesthetic and Techniques

Léon Bakst's visual aesthetic emphasized vibrant, emotionally charged colors and intricate ornamentation, blending historical authenticity with stylized exoticism to evoke sensuality and luxury in theatrical contexts.[1] His designs rejected naturalistic representation in favor of decorative intensity, where bold patterns and motifs—such as geometric shapes, rams' heads, and Eastern-inspired elements—created dense, textured surfaces that harmonized costumes with sets.[1] This approach, evident in works like the 1910 Schéhérazade costumes featuring zig-zagging lines and lozenge patterns, prioritized the total visual impact over individual realism, influencing the Ballets Russes' immersive spectacles.[2][23] Bakst demonstrated masterful control over color, using shades to convey specific moods; for instance, certain reds symbolized triumph while others suggested violence or assassination, as in the shifting palettes from calm yellows to violent purples in Daphnis and Chloé (1912).[1] He applied gem-like hues—cadmium yellow, orange with selenium—in watercolor and gouache to achieve jewel-toned vibrancy, enhancing the exotic allure in designs like the 1911 Le Dieu bleu sets.[6] This chromatic intensity, combined with gold and silver paints, produced a "tornado of color" that captivated audiences and extended to his easel paintings, such as the dynamic compositions in Terror Antiquus (1908).[2] In techniques, Bakst relied on watercolor for fluid set designs, as in Les Orientales (1908, 73.2 × 43 cm), often layering with gouache and graphite for detail and opacity in costume sketches like The Firebird (1910).[6] He produced meticulous presentation drawings, then used transfer paper for workshop copies to guide fabrication.[2] Costume realization involved mixed methods: appliqué, hand-painting, dyeing, embroidery, flocking, beading, sequins, metal studs, braids, pearls, and jewels, creating multifaceted textures that amplified movement and light reflection, as seen in the richly adorned Zobeide costume for Schéhérazade.[1] These processes, informed by studies of historical artifacts and nature, ensured stylistic coherence across media while adapting to the demands of live performance.[2]

Orientalism and Exotic Motifs

![For the Negro Boy in Scheherazade by Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov; 1910.](./assets/Negro_boy_by_L.Bakst_19101910%252C_MAGMA1910 Léon Bakst's engagement with orientalism manifested prominently in his stage designs for the Ballets Russes, where he incorporated exotic motifs drawn from Persian, Arabian, Turkish, Central Asian, and Caucasian sources to evoke sensuality and opulence.[15] These elements often blended historical references, such as Persian miniatures and Qajar costumes, with vibrant color palettes and intricate patterns to create a romanticized Eastern aesthetic tailored for European audiences.[33] Bakst's approach emphasized unity in design through repeated motifs like geometric shapes, waves, and antique symbols, enhancing the dramatic and fantastical narratives of the ballets.[1] In the 1909 production of Cléopâtre, Bakst introduced oriental-style decor and costumes for the Egyptian-themed ballet choreographed by Michel Fokine, featuring bold, sumptuous colors such as reds symbolizing triumph and violence, alongside dense textures from appliqué, embroidery, and jewels.[1] This design marked an early shift toward exoticism in Ballets Russes presentations, prioritizing sensuality and cultural depth over traditional ballet conventions.[1] The following year's Schéhérazade, set in a Near Eastern harem with music by Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov, amplified these motifs through a "tornado of color" in revealing costumes and sets that captured the tale's mystery and decadence, drawing heavily from Persian art and architecture for historic cohesion.[2] [33] Bakst extended exotic influences to later works, such as Le Dieu Bleu in 1912, where costumes for the Blue God portrayed Indian-inspired divinity with vibrant hues and detailed watercolor renderings, and Thamar, incorporating stylized Persian Qajar elements.[2] [15] His designs for Aladdin in 1916 further utilized Persian sources in costumes for Arabian characters, employing cheerful colors and line unity to evoke escapism and exotic allure.[33] These motifs not only energized performances with violence and eroticism but also influenced contemporary fashion by popularizing Persian-style novelties in Parisian markets post-Schéhérazade.[2] [1]

Influence on Modern Design and Fashion

Bakst's costume designs for the Ballets Russes, notably the opulent Orientalist ensembles in Schéhérazade (1910), popularized loose silhouettes, rich textiles, and bold color palettes in early 20th-century fashion, prompting couturiers to emulate harem pants, turbans, and metallic embroideries in ready-to-wear lines.[34][35] Paul Poiret, a leading Parisian designer, explicitly drew from Bakst's vivid hues—describing them as "those reds, those greens, those electric blues"—to develop his own "Oriental" collections starting in 1911, which shifted fashion away from corseted Edwardian restraint toward fluid, decorative forms.[36][37] This influence extended to textile innovation, as Bakst produced his own fabric patterns by the 1920s, featuring rhythmic motifs and synthetic dyes that anticipated Art Deco geometry and informed interior design trends.[31][38] Houses like Worth and Paquin adapted Bakst's elaborate patterns for evening wear, while his emphasis on movement and body contouring indirectly shaped later designers such as Elsa Schiaparelli, who incorporated similar exotic elements in the 1930s.[39][40] Bakst's theoretical writings on fashion history further reinforced these shifts, advocating for apparel as an extension of artistic expression unbound by convention.[31] In design broadly, Bakst's integration of pattern, color, and ornament in stage work prefigured Art Deco's eclectic synthesis, evident in the replication of his motifs in bronze sculptures by artists like Demêtre Chiparus and furniture by Émile-Jacques Ruhlmann during the 1925 Paris Exposition.[41][42] His designs' causal role in liberating fashion from pre-war rigidity stemmed from their empirical success in captivating audiences, as contemporary accounts noted a "Bakst spell" driving global color mania among elites by 1912.[31][35]

Legacy and Reception

Posthumous Recognition and Exhibitions

Following Bakst's death on December 27, 1924, a posthumous exhibition of his works was promptly organized in Russia by Lyubov Gritsenko-Bakst, his relative, to showcase his artistic legacy amid efforts to preserve and publicize his contributions to stage design and painting.[10] This initiative reflected early recognition of his influence on theatrical aesthetics, though documentation remains limited due to the era's political upheavals. In the decades thereafter, Bakst's designs gained placement in major institutional collections, underscoring sustained appreciation for his Ballets Russes innovations, with pieces entering holdings at institutions such as the Russian Museum and the Thyssen-Bornemisza Museum by the mid-20th century.[10] Retrospective exhibitions proliferated around the 150th anniversary of his birth in 2016, including a comprehensive show at Moscow's Pushkin State Museum of Fine Arts titled "Leon Bakst: The Beauty of the Ballets Russes," which highlighted over 200 works spanning his career in scenery, costumes, and easel painting.[43] [44] Concurrently, the Nouveau Musée National de Monaco presented "Designing Dreams: A Celebration of Leon Bakst," focusing on his textile and fashion extensions of stage motifs.[45] Marking the centenary of his death in 2024, institutions continued this pattern of revival; Johns Hopkins University's Evergreen Museum & Library mounted an installation emphasizing Bakst's costume and set designs alongside archival materials from the Ballets Russes era, drawing on rare objects to illustrate his interdisciplinary impact.[46] These exhibitions affirm Bakst's enduring status as a pivotal figure in early 20th-century decorative arts, with curatorial emphasis on his synthesis of Orientalist elements and modernist vibrancy, though some critiques note the challenges of contextualizing his output outside Diaghilev's collaborative framework.[3]

Critical Assessments and Achievements

Bakst's stage designs for the Ballets Russes, particularly for productions like Scheherazade (1910) and The Firebird (1910), received widespread acclaim for their vibrant use of color, intricate patterns, and integration of exotic motifs, which critics credited with revitalizing European theater aesthetics and breaking from naturalistic traditions. His 1911 solo exhibition at the Louvre's Pavillon de Marsan in Paris achieved unprecedented success, drawing large crowds and establishing his reputation beyond Russia as a master of decorative art.[47] Contemporary reviewers, such as Arsène Alexandre in his 1913 essay L'Art décoratif de Léon Bakst, praised the synthesis of line, form, and ornamentation in his work, viewing it as a harmonious blend of Russian folk elements with Persian and ancient influences.[48] In recognition of his contributions, Bakst was awarded the Legion of Honour by the French government in 1914, the highest French distinction for his impact on international arts.[49] He became the first Russian artist to gain broad international acclaim in fashion design, with his textile patterns and costume sketches influencing couturiers like Paul Poiret and contributing to the era's shift toward bold, ornamental styles that prefigured Art Deco.[31] However, some analyses noted tensions in his oeuvre, such as juxtapositions of archaic and modern elements that reflected broader uncertainties in early 20th-century artistic modernity, though these were often interpreted as innovative rather than flawed.[50] Bakst's achievements extended to his role in elevating theatrical design to fine art status, with posthumous evaluations affirming his enduring technical mastery in watercolor and gouache for capturing movement and texture.[10] His work's emphasis on sensory immersion over literal representation was seen as a causal driver for subsequent innovations in stagecraft, though reliant on collaborative contexts like Diaghilev's troupe for full realization.

Enduring Impact and Cultural Significance

Bakst's theatrical designs established a precedent for integrated stage aesthetics, where costumes, scenery, and lighting unified to enhance narrative and movement, influencing generations of ballet and opera productions. His work with the Ballets Russes from 1909 onward emphasized psychological depth through visual elements, departing from realistic illusionism toward stylized exoticism that prioritized emotional impact.[1][3] In fashion, Bakst's bold use of saturated colors, fluid drapery, and oriental motifs directly shaped early 20th-century couture, prompting designers like Paul Poiret to incorporate harem-inspired silhouettes and vibrant palettes that supplanted Edwardian restraint. This shift popularized looser garments and patterned textiles, with Bakst himself producing apparel designs and fabrics that gained international acclaim as the first from a Russian artist in the field.[31][51][52] His aesthetic extended to interiors and decorative arts, where rhythmic patterns and repetitive motifs in textiles and wallpapers continue to inform modern maximalist trends. Bakst's influence endures in haute couture, evident in later designers such as Yves Saint Laurent and Christian Lacroix, who echoed his luxurious layering and exotic storytelling.[53][3][42] Culturally, Bakst popularized orientalism as a framework for escapist fantasy in Western performance and design, bridging Russian avant-garde with European modernism and fostering cross-cultural motifs that persist in contemporary theater and visual arts.[21][30]

Personal Life and Death

Family, Relationships, and Personal Challenges

Bakst was born Leib Haim Israilevich Rosenberg on May 10, 1866, in Grodno (now in Belarus), into a Hasidic Jewish family of modest means.[27] His father, Samuel Rosenberg, was a Talmudist, while his mother descended from the Bakster family, Grodno traders who supplied felt to the Russian army.[27] The family later relocated to St. Petersburg following the grandfather's move and subsequent divorce and remarriage.[27] Of ten children born to his parents, only four survived infancy: Bakst as the eldest, followed by sisters Rosalia (who died of starvation in 1918) and Sophia, and brother Isaiah; an older sister had perished from burns in early childhood.[27] In the early 1900s, Bakst married Lyubov Pavlovna Gritsenko (1870–1928), daughter of Pavel Tretyakov's brother and previously wed to artist Nikolay Gritsenko.[27] [54] The couple had one son, Andrei Lvovich Bakst (1907–1972), who became a theater and film designer; Lyubov also brought a daughter from her first marriage, Marina Nikolaevna Gritsenko (1901–1971), who pursued art scholarship.[27] [55] The marriage ended in divorce in 1910, after which Bakst maintained financial and emotional support for his family amid professional travels.[27] Bakst faced significant personal challenges, including chronic health issues exacerbated by stress. In 1914, he suffered a nervous breakdown accompanied by hypertension, requiring treatment in Montreux, Switzerland.[27] His condition relapsed in 1918 following Rosalia's death, with family providing care during recovery.[27] These ailments, compounded by overwork, contributed to his declining health in later years, though he continued to prioritize family ties and artistic output despite separations and financial strains from his nomadic career.[27]

Final Years and Circumstances of Death

![For Ida Rubinstein as Phaedra in Gabriele D'Annunzio's play; 1923, Museum of Avant-Garde Mastery.](./assets/Ida_Rubinstein's_costume_for_Phaedra_by_L.Bakst_19231923%252C_MAGMA1923 Following the Russian Revolution and the death of his sister Rosa from starvation in 1918, Bakst faced personal hardships that compounded the challenges of exile in Paris, where he had established himself as a prominent designer.[47] By spring 1920, relatives described him as being in a severely deteriorated physical and mental state upon their arrival to live with him.[10] Despite these difficulties, he mounted a successful exhibition of watercolors, drawings, and stage designs in New York in April 1920, generating substantial sales.[47] Bakst's collaboration with Sergei Diaghilev and the Ballets Russes ended in 1922 after designing sets and costumes for the revival of The Sleeping Princess (based on Tchaikovsky's The Sleeping Beauty) in London earlier that year.[32] Thereafter, he pursued independent projects, including costume designs for the vaudeville Old Moscow in 1922 and for Ida Rubinstein's portrayal of Phaedra in Gabriele D'Annunzio's play in 1923.[32] In 1924, he created sets for Rubinstein's production of Istar, which premiered at the Paris Opera on July 10.[56] He also delivered lectures on art and design during 1923–1924.[32] Bakst's health continued to decline amid these activities, culminating in a five-month illness.[57] He died on December 27, 1924, in Paris at the age of 58 from lung complications, specifically pulmonary edema.[58][57] His funeral drew notable figures from the artistic community.[32]
