Howrah railway station

Howrah railway station (also known as Howrah Junction) is a railway station located in the city of Howrah, of Kolkata Metropolitan Area, West Bengal, India.[2] It is the largest and busiest railway complex in India,[3][4][5][6] as well as one of the busiest and largest train stations in the world.[7][8] It is also the oldest surviving railway complex in India. Howrah is one of the five large intercity railway stations serving the Kolkata metropolitan area, the others being Sealdah, Santragachi, Shalimar and Kolkata railway station. The first public train from the station was on 15 August 1854, on what is now the Howrah â€“ Hooghly Main Line.[9] At present, approximately 772 passenger trains are handled daily at the station, serving about 900,000 passengers a day as of 2025. Utilising its 23 operational platforms, the station handles a total of 252 Mail/Express trains, 500 suburban EMU trains, and 20 passenger trains daily; ten of the platforms are long enough to cater to trains with more than 24 coaches.[10] Goods and parcel trains also originate and terminate here.[10] The Howrahâ€“Barddhaman main line is the busiest line that connects this station.[11]

History

In 1849, a contract was signed between the East Indian Railway Company and East India Company and an initial amount allocated for the first section between Howrah and Raneegunge (Raniganj) via Pandooah (Pandua, Hooghly) and Burdwan (Bardhaman).[12] Frederick Walter Simms, the consulting engineer to Government of India, initially envisaged a station on the right side of Hooghly in 1846. However, after the funds were sanctioned, Howrah was chosen as the terminus for the new line.[13] A bridge spanning 1,700 feet (520 m) across the Hooghly River was considered unfeasible at the time. In the following years, discussions frequently arose about connecting the rail line to Calcutta. On 17 June 1851, George Turnbull, the Chief Engineer of the East Indian Railway Company and his team of engineers submitted plans for a railway station at Howrah. Unaware of the future significance of railways in India, the government decided against purchasing the land and expensive waterfront required for the project in January 1852. Turnbull then developed alternative development plans that estimated the cost at 250,000 rupees. In October 1852, four tenders for the building of the station were received: they varied from 190,000 to 274,526 rupees.[15] The size of the construction project - of whether Howrah should be a large station or Howrah be a smaller station in favour of a larger station being developed at another time - was debated from time to time during this process. Eventually land was bought. The first experimental locomotive left Howrah on 18 June 1853 for the 37.5 miles to Pandoah (Pandua, Hooghly).[15] There was a gap between laying the line and opening it up since the ship carrying the carriages sank while the locomotive ended up in Australia. Eventually the carriages were built locally and the locomotive was directed to Calcutta. The first public departure from Howrah for the 23.5 miles to Hooghly was on 15 August 1854.[19] During this period, the station was located at what is now the office of the divisional railway manager of Howrah.[20] It consisted of one line and platform, a ticket window and a supporting building.[9] Two weeks later the line to Pundoah (Pandua, Hooghly) was opened.[19] In the first 4 months, over 109,000 passengers used the service.[19] The locomotive was of the same type as the Fairy Queen . From the mid-19th century, following the station's opening in 1854, Howrah served as a key transit point for Indians emigrating to European colonies. The increase of residents in the region around Howrah and Kolkata and the booming economy led to an increasing demand for rail travel. Also, the rail network kept on growing continuously, e.g. the bridge over the Rupnarayan River at Kolaghat was completed on 19 April 1900 and connected Howrah with Kharagpur.[22] The Bengal-Nagpur Railway was extended to Howrah in 1900, thus making Howrah an important railway centre.[23] So in 1901, a new station building was proposed. The British architect Halsey Ricardo designed the new station.[23] It was opened to the public on 1 December 1905,[23][24] and completed by 1911.[20] In the 1980s, the station was expanded to 15 platforms.[25] At the same time, a new Yatri Niwas (transit passenger facility) was built south of the original station frontage. The new terminal complex was finished in 1992, creating a total of 19 platforms.[25] This was extended by a further four platforms in 2009.[25] On 3 March 1969, the inaugural Rajdhani Express departed Howrah for New Delhi.[26][25] In October 2011, India's first double-decker train, Howrahâ€“Dhanbad Double Decker Express, left Howrah for Dhanbad.[27] The first service of the Antyodaya Express, the Howrahâ€“Ernakulam Antyodaya Express, was inaugurated in February 2017.[28] As per Indian Green Building Council, it was awarded CII-IGBC Silver Rating becoming the first green railway station among metropolitan cities in India.[29][30][31] The book Vibrant Edifice: The Saga of Howrah Station by Eastern Railways was released in 2005.[32] This was written to celebrate the centenary of the building. As part of the Amrit Bharat Station Scheme initiated in 2023, Howrah is undergoing major redevelopment to enhance passenger amenities, including lounges, escalators, and a world-class look by 2026. Platform 24, under construction since around 2023, is nearing completion as of 2025, bringing the total to 24 platforms.

Tram terminus, Howrah

Until 1992, there was a tram terminus at Howrah Station. Trams departed for Sealdah Station, Rajabazar, Shyambazar, High Court, Dalhousie Square, Park Circus, Ballygunge, Tollygunge etc. Trams also departed for Bandhaghat and Shibpur. The tram terminus was partially closed in 1971 while the Bandhaghat and Shibpur lines were closed. Many unauthorized vehicles and pedestrians began to traverse the tram tracks and so the routes were not continued. The terminus station was converted to underpasses and a bus terminus. The part of the tram terminus for other routes continued to function until 1992, when the Rabindra Setu (Howrah Bridge) was declared unfit to carry trams because it was a cantilever bridge.

Heritage museum

The nearby Rail Museum, Howrah was opened in 2006, and contains a section dedicated to the heritage and history of Howrah railway station.[33] The railway museum, located south of the station, displays artefacts of historical importance related to the development of Eastern Railway. From 1909 to 1943 the Fairy Queen , the world's oldest operational steam locomotive, was displayed on a plinth outside the station.[34]

Rail services

The Eastern Railway runs local trains to Belur Math, Tarakeswar, Arambagh, Goghat, Katwa, Bandel, Sheoraphuli, Bardhaman, Serampore and numerous intermediate stations (see Howrahâ€“Bardhaman main line, Howrahâ€“Bardhaman chord and Tarakeswar branch line). There are also mail and express trains to Central, North and North-East India. A narrow-gauge line formerly used to connect Bardhaman and Katwa, served by DMU trains; but now this line is also converted to broad gauge and used by EMU trains like all the other lines.[35] The South Eastern Railway, operates local trains to Amta, Mecheda, Panskura, Haldia, Tamluk, Medinipur and Kharagpur and mail and express trains to Central, West and South India. The South Eastern Railway connects with the routes to Mumbai and Chennai. The Eastern Railway and South Eastern Railway sections are connected by two links. One is the Liluahâ€“Tikiapara link (used only by goods train) and the other is the Rajchandrapur/Dankuniâ€“Santragachhi/Andul link, which is used by goods trains and some express trains avoiding Howrah. Four major rail routes end at Howrah. They are the Howrahâ€“Delhi, Howrahâ€“Mumbai, Howrahâ€“Chennai and Howrahâ€“Guwahati routes.

Station facilities

The station is the divisional headquarters for the Eastern Railway. The station has 23 operational platforms as of 2025. Platforms 1 to 16 are in the old complex (Terminal 1), serving local and long-distance trains of the Eastern Railway and local trains of the South Eastern Railway. Platforms 17 to 23 are in the new complex (Terminal 2), serving long-distance trains of the South Eastern Railway. Platform 24 in Terminal 2 is under construction. Under the Amrit Bharat Station Scheme, several platforms including 1, 8, and 10â€“14 are being extended to accommodate 24-coach trains, with work ongoing as of early 2025. There is a large covered waiting area between the main complex and the platforms, along with other areas for passengers awaiting connecting trains. Free Wi-Fi is available at the station. In addition, there is a transit passenger facility with dormitory, single-room, and double-room accommodation. First-class passengers have access to an air-conditioned waiting area with balcony views of the Kolkata skyline and the Howrah Bridge. The station platforms have carriageways for motor vehicles within the complex, including two to platforms 8 and 9 for the Eastern Railway and to platforms 21 and 22 for the South Eastern Railway. Flyovers at the ends of the platforms allow motor vehicles to exit the complex quickly. The Sampath Rail Yatri Niwas and Regional Rail Museum are part of the Terminal 2 Howrah station complex.

Services for Rolling Stock

Services for rolling stock

The station has a diesel-locomotive shed with room for 84 locomotives. The electric-locomotive shed has room for 96 locomotives. There is also an electric-trip shed with the capacity to hold up to 20 locomotives. The sheds accommodate approximately 205 WAP-4 and WAP-7 locomotives (as of September 2025). The EMU car shed has over 15 parking slots. The station has a coach maintenance complex.

Connectivity

Metro station

Howrah station has an underground station, which in turn, is a part of Green Line of the Kolkata Metro serves the area.[38] It is the deepest station of the Kolkata Metro[39][40] and also the deepest in the country.[41][42] To the east, the station connects to Mahakaran station in Kolkata through India's first under-river metro tunnel beneath the Hooghly river;[43][44] whilst to the west the adjacent station is Howrah Maidan. The station was opened to the public on 15th March 2024. [45]
