Monstrous moonshine

In mathematics, monstrous moonshine, or moonshine theory, is the unexpected connection between the monster group MMM and modular functions, in particular the jjj-invariant. The initial numerical observation was made by John McKay in 1978, and the phrase was coined by John Conway and Simon P. Norton in 1979.[3] The monstrous moonshine is now known to be underlain by a vertex operator algebra called the moonshine module (or monster vertex algebra) constructed by Igor Frenkel, James Lepowsky, and Arne Meurman in 1988, which has the monster group as its group of symmetries. This vertex operator algebra is commonly interpreted as a structure underlying a two-dimensional conformal field theory, allowing physics to form a bridge between two mathematical areas. The conjectures made by Conway and Norton were proven by Richard Borcherds for the moonshine module in 1992 using the no-ghost theorem from string theory and the theory of vertex operator algebras and generalized Kacâ€“Moody algebras.

A graded representation whose graded dimension is JJJ, called the moonshine module, was explicitly constructed by Igor Frenkel, James Lepowsky, and Arne Meurman, giving an effective solution to the McKayâ€“Thompson conjecture, and they also determined the graded traces for all elements in the centralizer of an involution of MMM, partially settling the Conwayâ€“Norton conjecture. In 1985, the Atlas of Finite Groups was published by a group of mathematicians, including John Conway. The Atlas, which enumerates all sporadic groups, included "Moonshine" as a section in its list of notable properties of the monster group.[4] Borcherds proved the Conwayâ€“Norton conjecture for the Moonshine Module in 1992. He won the Fields Medal in 1998 in part for his solution of the conjecture.

The Moonshine Module

The Frenkelâ€“Lepowskyâ€“Meurman construction starts with two main tools:
  1. The construction of a lattice vertex operator algebra VLV_LVLâ€‹ for an even lattice LLL of rank nnn. In physical terms, this is the chiral algebra for a bosonic string compactified on a torus Rn/L\mathbb{R}^n / LRn/L. It can be described roughly as the tensor product of the group ring of LLL with the oscillator representation in nnn dimensions (which is itself isomorphic to a polynomial ring in countably infinitely many generators). For the case in question, one sets LLL to be the Leech lattice, which has rank 24.
  2. The orbifold construction. In physical terms, this describes a bosonic string propagating on a quotient orbifold. The construction of Frenkelâ€“Lepowskyâ€“Meurman was the first time orbifolds appeared in conformal field theory. Attached to the â€“1 involution of the Leech lattice, there is an involution hhh of VLV_LVLâ€‹, and an irreducible hhh-twisted VLV_LVLâ€‹-module, which inherits an involution lifting hhh. To get the Moonshine Module, one takes the fixed point subspace of hhh in the direct sum of VLV_LVLâ€‹ and its twisted module.
Frenkel, Lepowsky, and Meurman then showed that the automorphism group of the moonshine module, as a vertex operator algebra, is MMM. Furthermore, they determined that the graded traces of elements in the subgroup 21+24.Co12^{1+24}.Co_121+24.Co1â€‹ match the functions predicted by Conway and Norton.[5]

Borcherds' proof

Richard Borcherds' proof of the conjecture of Conway and Norton can be broken into the following major steps:
  1. One begins with a vertex operator algebra VVV with an invariant bilinear form, an action of MMM by automorphisms, and with known decomposition of the homogeneous spaces of seven lowest degrees into irreducible MMM-representations. This was provided by Frenkelâ€“Lepowskyâ€“Meurman's construction and analysis of the Moonshine Module.
  2. A Lie algebra m\mathfrak{m}m, called the monster Lie algebra, is constructed from VVV using a quantization functor. It is a generalized Kacâ€“Moody Lie algebra with a monster action by automorphisms. Using the Goddardâ€“Thorn "no-ghost" theorem from string theory, the root multiplicities are found to be coefficients of JJJ.
  3. One uses the Koikeâ€“Nortonâ€“Zagier infinite product identity to construct a generalized Kacâ€“Moody Lie algebra by generators and relations. The identity is proved using the fact that Hecke operators applied to JJJ yield polynomials in JJJ.
  4. By comparing root multiplicities, one finds that the two Lie algebras are isomorphic, and in particular, the Weyl denominator formula for m\mathfrak{m}m is precisely the Koikeâ€“Nortonâ€“Zagier identity.
  5. Using Lie algebra homology and Adams operations, a twisted denominator identity is given for each element. These identities are related to the McKayâ€“Thompson series TgT_gTgâ€‹ in much the same way that the Koikeâ€“Nortonâ€“Zagier identity is related to JJJ.
  6. The twisted denominator identities imply recursion relations on the coefficients of TgT_gTgâ€‹, and unpublished work of Koike showed that Conway and Norton's candidate functions satisfied these recursion relations. These relations are strong enough that one only needs to check that the first seven terms agree with the functions given by Conway and Norton. The lowest terms are given by the decomposition of the seven lowest degree homogeneous spaces given in the first step.
Thus, the proof is completed.[6] Borcherds was later quoted as saying "I was over the moon when I proved the moonshine conjecture", and "I sometimes wonder if this is the feeling you get when you take certain drugs. I don't actually know, as I have not tested this theory of mine."[7] More recent work has simplified and clarified the last steps of the proof. Jurisich found that the homology computation could be substantially shortened by replacing the usual triangular decomposition of the Monster Lie algebra with a decomposition into a sum of gl2\mathfrak{gl}_2gl2â€‹ and two free Lie algebras.[8][9] Cummins and Gannon showed that the recursion relations automatically imply the McKay-Thompson series are either Hauptmoduln or terminate after at most 3 terms, thus eliminating the need for computation at the last step.[10]

Generalized moonshine

Conway and Norton suggested in their 1979 paper that perhaps moonshine is not limited to the monster, but that similar phenomena may be found for other groups. While Conway and Norton's claims were not very specific, computations by Larissa Queen in 1980 strongly suggested that one can construct the expansions of many Hauptmoduln from simple combinations of dimensions of irreducible representations of sporadic groups. In particular, she decomposed the coefficients of McKay-Thompson series into representations of subquotients of the Monster in the following cases:
  • T2BT_{2B}T2Bâ€‹ and T4AT_{4A}T4Aâ€‹ into representations of the Conway group Co0
  • T3BT_{3B}T3Bâ€‹ and T6BT_{6B}T6Bâ€‹ into representations of the Suzuki group 3.Suz
  • T3CT_{3C}T3Câ€‹ into representations of the Thompson group Th=F3Th = F_3Th=F3â€‹
  • T5AT_{5A}T5Aâ€‹ into representations of the Haradaâ€“Norton group HN=F5HN = F_5HN=F5â€‹
  • T5BT_{5B}T5Bâ€‹ and T10DT_{10D}T10Dâ€‹ into representations of the Hallâ€“Janko group 2.HJ
  • T7AT_{7A}T7Aâ€‹ into representations of the Held group He=F7He = F_7He=F7â€‹
  • T7BT_{7B}T7Bâ€‹ and T14CT_{14C}T14Câ€‹ into representations of 2.A7A_7A7â€‹
  • T11AT_{11A}T11Aâ€‹ into representations of the Mathieu group 2.M12M_{12}M12â€‹

Modular moonshine

In the early 1990s, the group theorist A. J. E. Ryba discovered remarkable similarities between parts of the character table of the monster, and Brauer characters of certain subgroups. In particular, for an element ggg of prime order ppp in the monster, many irreducible characters of an element of order kpkpkp whose kkkth power is ggg are simple combinations of Brauer characters for an element of order kkk in the centralizer of ggg. This was numerical evidence for a phenomenon similar to monstrous moonshine, but for representations in positive characteristic. In particular, Ryba conjectured in 1994 that for each prime factor ppp in the order of the monster, there exists a graded vertex algebra over the finite field Fp\mathbb{F}_pFpâ€‹ with an action of the centralizer of an order ppp element ggg, such that the graded Brauer character of any ppp-regular automorphism hhh is equal to the McKay-Thompson series for ghghgh. In 1996, Borcherds and Ryba reinterpreted the conjecture as a statement about Tate cohomology of a self-dual integral form of Vâ™®V^\naturalVâ™®. This integral form was not known to exist, but they constructed a self-dual form over Z[1/2]\mathbb{Z}[1/2]Z[1/2], which allowed them to work with odd primes ppp. The Tate cohomology for an element of prime order naturally has the structure of a super vertex algebra over Fp\mathbb{F}_pFpâ€‹, and they broke up the problem into an easy step equating graded Brauer super-trace with the McKay-Thompson series, and a hard step showing that Tate cohomology vanishes in odd degree. They proved the vanishing statement for small odd primes, by transferring a vanishing result from the Leech lattice.[17] In 1998, Borcherds showed that vanishing holds for the remaining odd primes, using a combination of Hodge theory and an integral refinement of the no-ghost theorem.[18][19] The case of order 2 requires the existence of a form of Vâ™®V^\naturalVâ™® over a 2-adic ring, i.e., a construction that does not divide by 2. In 2019, Scott Carnahan constructed a self-dual integral form of the moonshine module over the integers, resolving this case.[20] There remain many additional unanswered questions, such as how Ryba's conjecture should generalize to Tate cohomology of composite order elements, and the nature of any connections to generalized moonshine and other moonshine phenomena.

Conjectured relationship with quantum gravity

In 2007, E. Witten suggested that AdS/CFT correspondence yields a duality between pure quantum gravity in (2 + 1)-dimensional anti de Sitter space and extremal holomorphic CFTs. Pure gravity in 2 + 1 dimensions has no local degrees of freedom, but when the cosmological constant is negative, there is nontrivial content in the theory, due to the existence of BTZ black hole solutions. Extremal CFTs, introduced by G. HÃ¶hn, are distinguished by a lack of Virasoro primary fields in low energy, and the moonshine module is one example. Under Witten's proposal, gravity in AdS space with maximally negative cosmological constant is AdS/CFT dual to a holomorphic CFT with central charge c=24c = 24c=24, and the partition function of the CFT is precisely jâˆ’744j - 744jâˆ’744, i.e., the graded character of the moonshine module.[21] By assuming Frenkel-Lepowsky-Meurman's conjecture that moonshine module is the unique holomorphic VOA with central charge 24 and character jâˆ’744j - 744jâˆ’744, Witten concluded that pure gravity with maximally negative cosmological constant is dual to the monster CFT. Part of Witten's proposal is that Virasoro primary fields are dual to black-hole-creating operators, and as a consistency check, he found that in the large-mass limit, the Bekenstein-Hawking semiclassical entropy estimate for a given black hole mass agrees with the logarithm of the corresponding Virasoro primary multiplicity in the moonshine module. In the low-mass regime, there is a small quantum correction to the entropy, e.g., the lowest energy primary fields yield lnâ¡(196883)âˆ¼12.19\ln(196883) \sim 12.19ln(196883)âˆ¼12.19, while the Bekensteinâ€“Hawking estimate gives 4Ï€âˆ¼12.574\pi \sim 12.574Ï€âˆ¼12.57. Later work has refined Witten's proposal. Witten had speculated that the extremal CFTs with larger cosmological constant may have monster symmetry much like the minimal case, but this was quickly ruled out by independent work of Gaiotto and HÃ¶hn. Work by Witten and Maloney suggested that pure quantum gravity may not satisfy some consistency checks related to its partition function, unless some subtle properties of complex saddles work out favorably.[22] However, Liâ€“Songâ€“Strominger suggested that chiral gravity, which they proposed, may have better stability properties, while being dual to the chiral part of the monster CFT, i.e., the monster vertex algebra.[23] Duncan and Frenkel produced additional evidence for this duality by using Rademacher sums to produce the McKayâ€“Thompson series as (2 + 1)-dimensional gravity partition functions by a regularized sum over global torus-isogeny geometries.[24] Furthermore, they conjectured the existence of a family of twisted chiral gravity theories parametrized by elements of the monster, suggesting a connection with generalized moonshine and gravitational instanton sums. At present, all of these ideas are still rather speculative, in part because 3d quantum gravity does not have a rigorous mathematical foundation. More recent work, such as the 2023 proposal of monstrous M-theory, explores connections in higher-dimensional gravity, offering potential field-theoretic explanations for aspects of moonshine, but the core ideas remain conjectural.[25]

Mathieu moonshine

In 2010, Tohru Eguchi, Hirosi Ooguri, and Yuji Tachikawa observed that the elliptic genus of a K3 surface can be decomposed into characters of the N = (4,4) superconformal algebra, such that the multiplicities of massive states appear to be simple combinations of irreducible representations of the Mathieu group M24M_{24}M24â€‹. This suggests that there is a sigma-model conformal field theory with K3 target that carries M24M_{24}M24â€‹ symmetry. However, by the Mukaiâ€“Kondo classification, there is no faithful action of this group on any K3 surface by symplectic automorphisms, and by work of Gaberdielâ€“Hoheneggerâ€“Volpato,[27] the supersymmetry-preserving automorphisms of any K3 sigma model generate a subgroup of the Conway group Co1\mathrm{Co}_1Co1â€‹ (of which M24M_{24}M24â€‹ is a subgroup), with explicit realizations showing symmetries not contained in M24M_{24}M24â€‹, so the appearance of an action on the underlying Hilbert space remains a mystery. By analogy with McKayâ€“Thompson series, Miranda Cheng suggested that both the multiplicity functions and the graded traces of nontrivial elements of M24M_{24}M24â€‹ form mock modular forms.[28] In 2012, Terry Gannon proved that all but the first of the multiplicities are non-negative integral combinations of representations of M24M_{24}M24â€‹,[29] and Gaberdielâ€“Perssonâ€“Ronellenfitschâ€“Volpato computed all analogues of generalized moonshine functions,[30] establishing the existence of a module realizing the moonshine relations. In 2014, explicit constructions of super vertex operator algebras providing modules for Mathieu moonshine were given.[31] Also in 2012, Cheng, Duncan, and Jeffrey A. Harvey amassed numerical evidence of an umbral moonshine phenomenon where families of mock modular forms appear to be attached to Niemeier lattices.[32] The conjecture was proven in 2015.[33] The special case of the A124A_1^{24}A124â€‹ lattice yields Mathieu moonshine, but in general the phenomenon lacks a geometric interpretation.

Origin of the term

The term "monstrous moonshine" was coined by John Conway and Simon P. Norton in their 1979 paper. According to accounts, Conway, when told by John McKay in 1978 that the coefficient of q1q^{1}q1 (namely 196884) in the qqq-expansion of the jjj-function was precisely one more than the dimension of the smallest faithful complex representation of the monster group (namely 196883), replied that this was "moonshine" (in the sense of being a crazy or foolish idea).[34] Thus, the term not only refers to the monster group MMM; it also refers to the perceived lunacy of the intricate relationship between MMM and the theory of modular functions.

Related observations

The monster group was investigated in the 1970s by mathematicians Jean-Pierre Serre, Andrew Ogg and John G. Thompson; they studied the quotient of the hyperbolic plane by subgroups of SL(2,R)\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{R})SL(2,R), particularly, the normalizer Î“0(p)+\Gamma_0(p)^+Î“0â€‹(p)+ of the Hecke congruence subgroup Î“0(ppp) in SL(2,R)\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{R})SL(2,R). When Ogg heard about the monster group later on, and noticed that these were precisely the prime factors of the size of MMM, he published a paper offering a bottle of Jack Daniel's whiskey to anyone who could explain this fact.[35] These 15 primes are known as the supersingular primes, not to be confused with the use of the same phrase with a different meaning in algebraic number theory.
