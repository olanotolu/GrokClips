2023 Ecuadorian political crisis

The 2023 Ecuadorian political crisis centered on President Guillermo Lasso's dissolution of the opposition-dominated National Assembly on 17 May 2023, invoking Article 148 of the 2008 Constitution during an impeachment trial accusing him of corruption tied to a state-contracted firm.[1][2] This rare "cross-death" (muerte cruzada) mechanism, designed to resolve severe executive-legislative impasses, permitted Lasso to govern by executive decree—limited to economic emergencies—until snap presidential and legislative elections within six months, marking the first such activation since its adoption.[3][4] The standoff arose from chronic gridlock in Ecuador's fragmented political system, where the Assembly, lacking a clear majority and hindered by Lasso's minority government, obstructed key reforms amid escalating national challenges including rampant organized crime, prison riots, and homicide rates surpassing 40 per 100,000 inhabitants by early 2023.[5][6] Lasso justified the dissolution by citing the Assembly's repeated vetoes of security-focused legislation and failure to address a "serious political crisis" as stipulated in the constitutional clause, though critics alleged it circumvented accountability for embezzlement claims in the "Big Godfather" (El Gran Padrino) scandal involving overpriced contracts.[7][3] The maneuver averted Lasso's potential removal but intensified debates over democratic norms, with the Constitutional Court upholding its legality while opposition factions decried it as authoritarian, leading to heightened protests and institutional tensions that persisted into snap elections where Lasso opted not to run, paving the way for Daniel Noboa's narrow victory in a subsequent runoff.[8][9] This episode underscored Ecuador's vulnerability to power struggles amid unchecked gang violence, which Lasso had earlier combated through states of emergency but with limited legislative backing, highlighting causal links between political paralysis and security deterioration.[5][10]

Background and causes

Rise of organized crime and narcotrafficking

Ecuador's geographic position along the Pacific coast, sharing borders with Colombia and Peru—the world's leading cocaine producers—has transformed it into a primary transit corridor for narcotics bound for consumer markets in North America and Europe. Cocaine enters primarily through porous land borders and coastal routes, with over one-third of Colombia's production flowing into Ecuador for onward shipment, facilitated by the country's dollarized economy and relatively permissive maritime access.[11][12] This role intensified as production in neighboring countries surged, with Ecuador lacking significant domestic coca cultivation but serving as a storage and repackaging hub due to inadequate interdiction at entry points.[13] The port of Guayaquil, Ecuador's largest and a critical node in global shipping, has become a focal point for illicit flows, with vulnerabilities including understaffed customs, corruption, and container overload enabling massive cocaine concealment in legitimate cargo. In 2023, Ecuador recorded cocaine seizures exceeding those of Peru and Bolivia combined, highlighting the port's scale in transshipment despite increased detections.[14] Weak border controls and underfunded security apparatus—exacerbated by limited resources for maritime patrols—allowed cartels to exploit these gaps, turning Ecuador from a peripheral route into a "cocaine superhighway."[15] Domestic gangs, notably Los Choneros and Los Lobos, capitalized on this influx, forging alliances with Mexican cartels like Sinaloa for logistics and enforcement while infiltrating prisons to direct external operations. These groups commandeered correctional facilities as command centers, coordinating drug transport, extortion rackets, and retaliatory violence from within, amid chronic overcrowding and guard complicity.[16] Extortion diversified their revenue, targeting businesses and even public institutions in gang-influenced territories, fueled by territorial disputes over trafficking corridors rather than isolated economic pressures.[17][18] This entrenchment correlated with a sharp homicide escalation, from 5.8 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2018 to 47 per 100,000 in 2023, as gang wars over cartel alliances and control of ports and prisons displaced prior low-violence norms. Empirical links trace the surge to intensified narcotrafficking rivalries, with inter-gang clashes accounting for the bulk of killings, independent of broader institutional reforms.[19][20]

Institutional corruption and state capture

Institutional corruption permeated Ecuador's state apparatus, particularly the judiciary, law enforcement, and port authorities, enabling narcotrafficking networks to bribe officials and evade accountability, which facilitated the unchecked expansion of organized crime. Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index consistently ranked Ecuador below the global average, with scores hovering around 34-36 out of 100 from 2018 to 2022, reflecting entrenched public sector graft that paralleled surges in drug-related extortion and money laundering activities.[21] This systemic capture eroded institutional capacity to counter criminal infiltration, as bribes secured favorable judicial outcomes and operational impunity for cartels. Judicial complicity exemplified the depth of narco-influence, with documented instances of judges receiving payments to release high-profile drug operatives or dismiss charges. Investigations uncovered schemes where traffickers compensated judicial actors to manipulate evidence and grant provisional releases, allowing kingpins to resume operations.[22] Similarly, police elements were bribed to falsify records or overlook cartel activities, further weakening enforcement mechanisms.[23] In prisons, corrupt guards enabled gang leaders to orchestrate external violence and smuggling from within facilities, transforming correctional systems into de facto command centers for criminal enterprises.[24] Port infrastructure emerged as a critical vulnerability, where officials colluded with traffickers to embed cocaine in legitimate exports like bananas, exploiting corrupt insiders to bypass inspections and ensure shipments reached international markets undetected.[25] This state capture at export hubs amplified Ecuador's role as a transshipment point, with bribery networks infiltrating customs and logistics to sustain multibillion-dollar flows.[26] The roots of this impunity extended to prior governance, notably Rafael Correa's administration (2007-2017), which faced allegations of fostering environments conducive to graft through policies deprioritizing anti-trafficking vigilance. Correa's 2009 closure of the U.S.-operated Manta air base—used for aerial surveillance of drug routes—removed a key deterrent, correlating with heightened cartel incursions.[27] Compounding this, Correa received an eight-year bribery conviction in 2020 related to the "Briberies 2012-2016" scandal, involving state contracts rigged for illicit gains, which underscored institutionalized favoritism over accountability.[28] Such precedents entrenched gang footholds, as lax oversight and corrupt alliances allowed criminal economies to supplant state authority in vulnerable sectors.

Corruption scandals under Lasso administration

In October 2021, the Pandora Papers leak exposed President Guillermo Lasso's historical connections to at least 10 offshore entities, including companies and trusts in Panama, South Dakota, and Delaware, some of which managed assets like Florida real estate holdings transferred in 2017.[29][30] Lasso maintained that these structures predated his presidency and were fully dissolved prior to his May 2021 inauguration, with no violations of Ecuadorian law or tax obligations during his tenure.[31] The revelations prompted the National Assembly—dominated by opposition factions—to summon Lasso for questioning and launch probes into potential tax fraud, though the comptroller general's office later dismissed related audits for lack of evidence.[32][33] No criminal charges resulted directly against Lasso, but the disclosures intensified legislative scrutiny and public skepticism toward his administration's integrity claims.[34] A more direct challenge emerged in January 2023 with the "El Gran Padrino" investigation by outlet La Posta, which detailed alleged influence-peddling and bribery schemes centered on Lasso's brother-in-law, Danilo Carrera—dubbed "El Gran Padrino" for purportedly acting as an intermediary for family and associate interests in state contracts.[35][36] The report, based on leaked audios, documents, and witness accounts, implicated Carrera in steering a 2020 privatization deal for state shipping firm Flota Petrolera Ecuatoriana (Flopec), where his private company acquired shares at undervalued prices amid irregularities, potentially costing the state millions.[37][38] Recordings suggested Lasso had been briefed on irregularities before and after taking office but did not intervene decisively, contradicting his "zero tolerance" anticorruption stance.[39] Carrera denied direct wrongdoing, attributing actions to legitimate business, while Lasso distanced himself, ordering dismissals of implicated officials like the anticorruption secretary and Flopec executives.[40][41] The scandal escalated when key witness Leandro Arias, linked to the Flopec dealings, was murdered in March 2023, prompting accusations of obstruction and further fueling assembly-led impeachment efforts on embezzlement grounds.[42] Prosecutors pursued the "Caso Encuentro" probe, uncovering ties to broader networks involving public procurement, though Lasso rejected personal involvement and portrayed the allegations as opposition fabrications amid stalled reforms.[43][44] These episodes, occurring against a backdrop of Lasso's push for deregulation and privatization clashing with a polarized legislature, highlighted entrenched patronage in state institutions but also evidenced selective enforcement, as probes often stalled without convictions and were leveraged to block executive initiatives rather than yield judicial resolutions.[45][46]

Political impasse

Impeachment trial of Guillermo Lasso

The impeachment proceedings against President Guillermo Lasso were initiated by Ecuador's opposition-controlled National Assembly in early 2023, primarily over allegations of embezzlement linked to the state-owned fishing company Flota Pesquera Industrial del Ecuador (FLOPEC). Lawmakers accused Lasso of failing to intervene in a scheme where subordinates allegedly misappropriated over $15 million in public funds through irregular contracts for fuel sales, with claims that the president was aware but did not act.[47][44][48] On March 29, 2023, Ecuador's Constitutional Court ruled that the impeachment hearings could proceed, rejecting Lasso's challenge that the process violated constitutional procedures. The Assembly voted 88-46 on May 9, 2023, to advance the trial, meeting the two-thirds threshold required to declare Lasso "politically responsible" for the alleged misconduct.[47][49][50] The formal impeachment hearing commenced on May 16, 2023, in the National Assembly, where opposition legislators, including members of the Revolución Ciudadana bloc aligned with former President Rafael Correa, presented evidence of Lasso's purported oversight failures and ties to FLOPEC corruption. Proponents argued the case exemplified broader governance failures under Lasso's administration, amid ongoing legislative gridlock that had stalled key reforms. Lasso's defenders, however, contended the charges were selectively pursued for political gain, noting that similar corruption allegations against opposition figures, such as those involving Correa-era officials, had not faced equivalent scrutiny, highlighting partisan motivations over impartial accountability.[51][52][53] Lasso maintained throughout that the impeachment represented an abuse of legislative power by a hostile Assembly lacking a Lasso-aligned majority, aimed at derailing his executive agenda rather than addressing substantive wrongdoing. He emphasized his administration's transparency efforts and veto authority, which had previously blocked over 1,000 Assembly proposals, underscoring the institutional deadlock fueling the crisis. The proceedings revealed deep partisan fissures, with Correaist factions seeking to reclaim influence lost since 2017, while Lasso invoked executive prerogatives to defend against what he described as a destabilizing power grab.[52][54][50]

Dissolution of the National Assembly

On May 17, 2023, Ecuadorian President Guillermo Lasso invoked Article 148 of the Constitution to dissolve the National Assembly amid an impending impeachment trial over alleged irregularities in a 2019 banana transportation contract.[1][8] This mechanism, termed muerte cruzada or "mutual death," allows the president to disband the legislature under conditions of a "grave political crisis and internal commotion," provided the assembly has obstructed key executive functions.[3] Lasso's decree halted the impeachment proceedings, which had advanced due to opposition efforts in the assembly dominated by leftist factions aligned with former President Rafael Correa.[8] Lasso justified the dissolution by citing the assembly's repeated obstruction of essential security and economic reforms amid surging organized crime and narcotrafficking, which had paralyzed governance.[3][46] The opposition-controlled body, elected in 2021, had blocked legislation on tax incentives for investment, labor flexibility, and anti-corruption measures, exacerbating Ecuador's institutional deadlock despite empirical evidence of rising violence and economic stagnation.[8] This impasse, Lasso argued, met the constitutional threshold for dissolution, as the assembly's actions hindered the state's capacity to address urgent threats.[3] The move sparked legal challenges from opposition lawmakers, who filed remedies with the Constitutional Court claiming it constituted an unconstitutional power grab, absent verifiable "internal commotion."[46][55] However, the court rejected these claims, and the National Electoral Council proceeded to validate the process, with Ecuador's armed forces and police affirming their commitment to constitutional order.[1][46] Critics, primarily from Correa-aligned groups, labeled it authoritarian, though the provision's existence—untested until then—provided a democratic escape from legislative gridlock, prioritizing institutional functionality over prolonged obstruction.[7] Immediately following the decree, the National Assembly was suspended, and Lasso assumed authority to issue executive decrees on economic matters until new elections.[1] The Electoral Council was required to convene snap elections for both the presidency and assembly within 45 days of validation, redirecting political energies from confrontation to electoral renewal.[56] This shifted the focus from stalled reforms to campaigning, temporarily stabilizing executive governance amid ongoing security challenges.[8]

Surge in targeted violence

Assassination of Fernando Villavicencio

On August 9, 2023, Ecuadorian presidential candidate Fernando Villavicencio was assassinated in Quito shortly after concluding a campaign rally in the La Magdalena neighborhood.[57][58] Gunmen in a vehicle fired multiple shots at Villavicencio's entourage, killing him and injuring nine others, including security personnel; one assailant was killed in the ensuing shootout, with authorities identifying the attackers as Colombian nationals hired by Ecuadorian criminal elements.[59][60] Villavicencio, a 59-year-old former investigative journalist and assemblyman, had built his candidacy on anti-corruption platforms, frequently exposing ties between politicians and narcotrafficking gangs, including allegations against figures from the era of former president Rafael Correa.[61][62] In the days prior, he publicly denounced death threats from Los Choneros, a powerful gang with operations in drug trafficking and alliances to Mexican cartels like Sinaloa, claiming they targeted him for revealing their infiltration of political networks.[62][63] Ecuadorian authorities subsequently attributed the hit to Los Choneros operatives, with the gang's incarcerated leader José Adolfo Macías ("Fito") implicated in ordering such actions amid internal power struggles and retaliatory violence.[64][65] The killing exposed systemic lapses in state protection, as Villavicencio had repeatedly alerted authorities to specific threats since at least 2021, yet received inadequate security despite his high-risk profile; his family later accused the government of "murder by willful omission" for failing to act on intelligence about impending attacks.[66][67] In response, President Guillermo Lasso decreed an expanded state of emergency nationwide, authorizing military deployments for urban patrols and enhanced intelligence operations against gangs, while vowing to combat organized crime's encroachment on democratic institutions.[57][58][68] This event underscored the causal role of narcogangs in eroding political stability, mirroring patterns in Colombia where cartel assassinations of officials stemmed directly from exposés disrupting illicit networks, rather than abstract socioeconomic factors alone; empirical data from Ecuador's rising homicide rates—over 3,000 murders in 2022, largely gang-related—reinforced that unchecked territorial control by groups like Los Choneros enabled such targeted eliminations of threats.[60][69] Subsequent arrests of six Colombian suspects faltered when five were killed in prison riots, highlighting persistent vulnerabilities in custody and investigations, with no confirmed masterminds identified a year later.[70][71]

Attacks on candidates and public officials

In mid-2023, Ecuador experienced a marked escalation in targeted violence against public officials and candidates, with media reports documenting 14 assassination attempts against candidates and elected officials, resulting in multiple fatalities.[72] These incidents, concentrated in coastal regions like Guayas province and Manabí, aligned with narcotrafficking hubs such as the ports of Guayaquil and Manta, where criminal organizations exploited weak state presence to intimidate figures pursuing anti-corruption or security reforms.[73] The pattern suggested deliberate efforts by gangs to deter opposition to their operations, rather than isolated retaliations against specific policies. On June 2, 2023, prosecutor Leonardo Palacios was fatally shot in Durán, near Guayaquil, while driving after attending a murder trial hearing; his secretary was also wounded in the ambush.[74] Palacios had been handling cases linked to organized crime, highlighting vulnerabilities among judicial officials in gang-influenced territories.[75] The violence intensified in July, when Manta mayor Agustín Intriago was assassinated on July 23 during a public works inspection in the port city, a key entry point for drug shipments; he was shot multiple times by gunmen, dying shortly after.[76] Intriago, recently reelected, had faced prior threats amid rising local homicides tied to trafficking routes.[77] Days earlier, on August 6, Durán mayor Luis Chonillo survived an armed attack on his vehicle, part of broader threats against municipal leaders in the same province.[78] Presidential candidates also adopted heightened security measures amid persistent threats. After stepping in as a replacement candidate following prior violence, Andrea González reported wearing a bulletproof vest continuously and limiting public appearances due to death threats from criminal elements.[79] Similarly, frontrunner Luisa González announced on September 1 that she would campaign in protective gear following explicit warnings linked to her anti-crime platform.[80] These episodes underscored how ungoverned spaces enabled gangs to project power, pressuring officials to acquiesce to demands for lax enforcement or favorable policies.[81]

Snap general elections

August 2023 first round

Snap general elections' first round took place on August 20, 2023, to select Ecuador's president and National Assembly amid escalating narcotrafficking violence and institutional deadlock following President Guillermo Lasso's dissolution of the prior assembly. Seventeen presidential candidates competed, with no one securing the 50% threshold required to avoid a runoff; official results from the National Electoral Council (CNE) showed Luisa González, the Citizen Revolution Movement candidate aligned with former President Rafael Correa, receiving 33.21% of valid votes (approximately 3.18 million), while Daniel Noboa of the National Democratic Action alliance obtained 31.72% (around 3.04 million). Other contenders, including Leonidas Iza of the Pachakutik indigenous movement with 16.11%, trailed significantly, reflecting voter fragmentation driven by dissatisfaction with established parties and acute security fears.[82] Noboa, a 35-year-old political novice and son of banana export magnate Álvaro Noboa, surged late in the campaign by emphasizing aggressive anti-gang measures, prison reforms, and military deployment against organized crime, resonating with voters prioritizing safety over Correa-era social welfare policies associated with corruption scandals. González campaigned on reviving Correa's economic redistribution programs and critiquing Lasso's tenure, but faced headwinds from public aversion to Correa's legacy, including judicial interference allegations during his 2007-2017 rule. Voter turnout reached 82.7%, with over 10 million ballots cast from 13.7 million registered, indicating strong civic participation despite pervasive threats from criminal groups.[83] Polling day proceeded with bolstered security, including military presence at voting stations, amid prior assassination of candidate Fernando Villavicencio and ongoing intimidation attempts; while isolated incidents of voter harassment and threats by armed actors occurred, particularly in coastal provinces like Guayas, no large-scale disruptions materialized, allowing the process to conclude orderly and underscoring the snap vote's role in restoring electoral legitimacy post-dissolution. The close top-two finish highlighted anti-establishment currents, with Noboa's outsider appeal and security focus capturing momentum against Correa-linked continuity amid crime's dominance in public concerns.[84]

October 2023 runoff and Noboa's election

The presidential runoff election took place on October 15, 2023, pitting banana magnate heir Daniel Noboa against Luisa González, the candidate backed by former president Rafael Correa's Citizens' Revolution Movement. With over 82% of votes counted, Ecuador's National Electoral Council declared Noboa the winner, securing 52.21% of the valid votes compared to González's 47.79%, a margin of roughly 4 percentage points or 492,000 votes. Voter turnout was low at approximately 50%, reflecting widespread disillusionment amid ongoing violence and economic woes, yet Noboa's appeal as a 35-year-old outsider resonated with younger demographics seeking a break from entrenched political dynasties.[83] Noboa's campaign centered on a "mano dura" (iron fist) approach to combating gang violence, pledging to militarize prisons, facilitate the extradition of high-level criminals to the United States, and deploy military forces more aggressively against organized crime networks that had infiltrated state institutions.[85] This platform positioned him as a proponent of results-driven security reforms, drawing implicit contrasts with Correa-era policies under González's banner, which critics argue fostered gang entrenchment through overcrowded prisons lacking control measures, resistance to extraditions, and a focus on rehabilitation over incarceration that enabled narco-groups to consolidate power during 2007–2017. Empirical data from the election underscored voter prioritization of tangible anti-crime measures over ideological continuity, with Noboa gaining traction among urban youth frustrated by rising homicides—Ecuador's rate had surged to 46 per 100,000 by mid-2023—despite González's promises of social investment alongside tougher enforcement.[86] Noboa was inaugurated on November 23, 2023, at the National Assembly in Quito, assuming office to complete the remainder of Guillermo Lasso's term until May 2025. His narrow victory signaled a generational pivot toward pragmatic governance amid crisis, though it left him facing a fragmented Assembly where his Acción Democrática Nacional coalition held limited seats, complicating legislative pushes for security overhauls.[87]

Noboa's early presidency and security measures

Declarations of states of emergency

Upon assuming office on November 23, 2023, President Daniel Noboa extended the ongoing state of emergency initially declared by his predecessor Guillermo Lasso on August 10, 2023, in response to escalating gang violence following the assassination of presidential candidate Fernando Villavicencio.[57][88] This continuity allowed for sustained military deployment to support police operations, implementation of nighttime curfews in violence-prone zones, and expedited asset freezes targeting gang finances.[68] Noboa issued multiple extensions of the emergency decree in 30- to 60-day increments beginning late November 2023, concentrating efforts on coastal urban centers like Guayaquil in Guayas Province, where gang control over ports facilitated drug trafficking. These measures empowered security forces to conduct raids resulting in hundreds of arrests of mid-level operatives linked to groups such as Los Choneros and Los Lobos, alongside seizures of weapons and vehicles used in extortion rackets.[89] However, operations faced persistent hurdles from institutional infiltration, with reports of corrupt elements within police and prisons undermining enforcement.[90] Empirical indicators revealed short-term efficacy, including localized drops in homicides—Ecuador's national rate peaked at approximately 47 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2023 but began declining to 38 per 100,000 by 2024 amid intensified patrols and disruptions to gang logistics.[91][92] Critics, including human rights observers, contended that while violence spikes were temporarily curbed through visible deterrence, the approach failed to eradicate root causes such as judicial corruption enabling gang impunity, as evidenced by ongoing prison-based command structures.[93] Mainstream outlets like Human Rights Watch, often aligned with progressive advocacy, emphasized potential overreach but underplayed infiltration data from government audits, highlighting selective framing in coverage.[93]

Initial anti-gang operations

Following Daniel Noboa's inauguration on November 23, 2023, Ecuadorian security forces launched intensified joint operations between the National Police and Armed Forces, focusing on intelligence-driven raids against gang logistics and infrastructure amid leadership disruptions in major groups like Los Choneros. These efforts targeted vulnerabilities created by the gang's internal fragmentation after the 2020 assassination of leader Jorge Luis Zambrano and subsequent power struggles, which allowed rivals such as Los Lobos to expand but also exposed operational weaknesses for exploitation by authorities. Operations emphasized dismantling suspected safe houses used for storing weapons and coordinating drug shipments, yielding seizures of arms and ammunition in multiple provinces.[94][95][96] Collaboration with the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration through the U.S.-mentored Port Task Force enhanced interdictions at major ports, including Guayaquil, resulting in substantial cocaine seizures that disrupted gang revenue streams. In 2023, such efforts contributed to a record 221 metric tons of drugs intercepted, with momentum carrying into Noboa's early term through heightened maritime and port patrols. These tactical gains included the confiscation of precursors and equipment tied to drug processing, though gangs adapted by shifting routes.[97][98] By mid-2024, the operations had led to thousands of gang-related arrests, building on over 2,300 detentions in the initial 10 days of heightened enforcement in January 2024 alone, alongside the seizure of hundreds of firearms. However, the rapid influx of suspects exacerbated prison overcrowding, pushing facilities to 174% capacity and straining rehabilitation and control efforts.[99][100]

Declaration of internal armed conflict

January 2024 prison riots and gang response

On January 7, 2024, José Adolfo Macías Villamar, known as "Fito" and leader of the Los Choneros gang, escaped from La Regional prison in Guayaquil, Ecuador's largest penitentiary, highlighting deep-seated corruption and complicity among prison guards.[101][102] Authorities confirmed that at least two guards facilitated the breakout, which occurred just days before Fito was scheduled for transfer to a maximum-security facility amid heightened government scrutiny of gang activities.[103] This event exposed systemic vulnerabilities in Ecuador's prison system, where gangs had long maintained operational control over facilities, including luxury accommodations and external communications for leaders like Fito.[104] The escape precipitated coordinated riots across at least six prisons nationwide, beginning on January 7 and escalating through January 9, as gangs demonstrated their ability to orchestrate widespread retaliation against intensifying state pressure.[105] Inmates seized control of sections in facilities such as Litoral Penitenciario in Guayaquil and others in Cotopaxi, Azogues, and Loja, taking approximately 136 to 150 prison guards and staff hostage in a show of force that paralyzed operations and underscored the gangs' entrenched influence within the correctional network.[106][107] These uprisings involved arson, destruction of infrastructure, and threats of execution, serving as a direct gang counter to recent anti-crime operations that had disrupted narcotics trafficking and leadership structures.[101] Concurrently, on January 9, armed assailants linked to Los Choneros affiliates, including the group La Firma, stormed the TC Televisión studio in Guayaquil during a live broadcast, holding employees hostage and broadcasting threats to amplify public terror.[108][109] The intruders, masked and heavily armed, detonated explosives, fired shots, and forced terrified staff to read scripted messages warning against government interference, an act that extended prison-based defiance into urban centers and exposed the narcoterrorist risks posed by gang coordination beyond incarceration walls.[110] All hostages from both prisons and the studio were eventually released unharmed by January 13, but the synchronized actions compelled a national confrontation with the gangs' operational sophistication and capacity to challenge state authority.[106]

Military mobilization and territorial control efforts

Following the declaration of an internal armed conflict on January 9, 2024, Ecuador's armed forces mobilized over 22,000 troops to support police in securing urban areas and confronting gang activities.[111] These deployments involved widespread patrols in cities like Quito and Guayaquil, where soldiers established checkpoints and conducted house-to-house searches to dismantle gang networks embedded in neighborhoods.[110] Joint task forces, combining military units with national police, targeted gang logistics, including raids on weapons caches and extortion rackets that sustained territorial control by groups like Los Choneros and Los Lobos.[112] In parallel, military operations extended to jungle regions along the Colombian border, where incursions disrupted illicit supply lines tied to drug trafficking and illegal mining—key revenue sources for gangs.[19] By mid-2024, forces destroyed multiple illegal mine sites operated by organized crime, deploying specialized units to remote areas despite ambushes that resulted in troop casualties.[113] These efforts aimed to sever gang access to precursor chemicals and arms smuggling routes, though operations faced logistical challenges from dense terrain and cross-border dynamics.[24] Within prisons, military-led interventions recaptured control of riot-affected wings, freeing over 100 hostages and restoring order in facilities like those in Guayaquil and Esmeraldas by late January 2024.[114] This included the neutralization of eight gang members during clashes and the recapture of 27 escaped inmates, contributing to a temporary decline in large-scale prison uprisings.[114] Overall, the mobilization yielded more than 1,300 arrests in the initial weeks, weakening overt gang strongholds in urban and correctional territories.[114] Civilian police infiltration by gangs, evidenced by the relief of 430 officers on corruption suspicions in 2024, underscored the military's central role in these efforts, as institutional trust in law enforcement eroded amid documented ties to narcotrafficking.[115] Despite gains in territorial reclamation, micro-level gang activities such as small-scale extortion and localized trafficking persisted, indicating that while mass disruptions were curtailed, underlying economic incentives for crime remained intact.[19] Ongoing operations through 2025, including reinforcements of up to 5,000 troops in response to flare-ups, reflect sustained emphasis on military dominance to preserve state authority against fragmented gang resilience.[116]

Key investigations and institutional responses

Caso Metastasis corruption probe

The Caso Metastasis investigation, launched on December 21, 2023, by Ecuador's Attorney General Diana Salazar, targeted systemic corruption within the judiciary and public institutions, revealing deep ties to drug trafficking networks.[24][117] The probe, described as the largest anti-corruption effort in Ecuador's history, implicated 52 officials—including judges, prosecutors, politicians, lawyers, and security personnel—in facilitating narco-impunity through bribes, manipulated plea bargains, and deliberate delays in money laundering and trafficking cases.[24][118] Evidence from intercepted communications and financial seizures showed how corrupt judges accepted payments to favor defendants linked to cartels, such as the Norero family, allowing operations to continue unchecked.[117] Initial raids across the country resulted in more than 30 arrests, with charges centered on influence peddling, extortion, bribery, and organized crime.[118][119] Confessions from detained individuals further exposed case-fixing mechanisms, where officials collaborated with gang intermediaries to undermine prosecutions, including halting investigations into high-profile traffickers.[117] The investigation prompted offshoot probes, such as one in March 2024 that led to the arrest of dozens of judicial staffers, including nine judges in the coastal province of Manabí, for similar narco-corruption.[119] By October 2024, a judge ordered 30 individuals to trial on organized crime and drug trafficking charges stemming from the case.[120] The probe's revelations underscored the judiciary's infiltration by organized crime, with officials allegedly prioritizing cartel interests over legal processes, thereby exacerbating Ecuador's violence crisis.[117][121] While it spurred a broader purge of implicated figures and bolstered institutional credibility through high-profile accountability, critics, including allies of former President Rafael Correa, argued it selectively targeted perceived political opponents under the pretext of anti-corruption, potentially compromising due process.[122] This tension highlights ongoing debates over the balance between aggressive judicial reforms and evidentiary standards in combating entrenched narco-influence.[117]

Judicial purges and prison system reforms

In response to revelations of judicial corruption, Ecuador's government under President Daniel Noboa initiated processes to remove implicated judges and prosecutors, with at least several dozen officials facing dismissal or investigation by late 2023 as part of broader anti-corruption efforts.[123] On December 6, 2023, Noboa publicly threatened to identify and hold accountable judicial actors who released high-risk criminals, signaling a push for stricter vetting and accountability in the judiciary.[123] These actions complemented legislative reforms, including the June 2025 Public Integrity Law, which aimed to streamline anti-corruption processes, enhance judicial independence, and impose harsher penalties for impunity in public office.[124][125] Parallel reforms targeted the prison system to disrupt gang command structures, beginning with the transfer of high-profile inmates, such as Los Choneros leader Adolfo Macías ("Fito"), to the maximum-security facility known as La Roca in January 2024.[126] Such transfers, intended to isolate leaders and limit external communications, were repeated for other gang figures amid heightened military oversight, though they occasionally provoked internal riots.[127] Infrastructure upgrades included the construction of new maximum-security prisons equipped with nine-meter walls, surveillance towers, and biometric identification systems for inmate monitoring, with the first such facility breaking ground in June 2024.[128] Additional high-security sites were planned in remote areas to alleviate overcrowding, drawing partial international technical support for design and operations, despite local opposition from indigenous communities.[129] By mid-2025, these measures contributed to tighter control over key facilities, evidenced by the recapture and secure confinement of escaped leaders like Fito in La Roca following his June 2025 arrest.[130] Prison escape incidents, while persistent in early 2024 with events like the January breakout of 43 inmates from Esmeraldas, showed signs of abatement in high-security units through enhanced surveillance, though overall system capacity remained strained with overcrowding exceeding 30,000 inmates beyond design limits.[131][100] These reforms prioritized breaking cycles of criminal influence but faced ongoing challenges from resource shortages and infrastructural demands.[132]

Controversies and policy debates

Human rights allegations versus security imperatives

Human rights organizations, including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, have documented allegations of arbitrary detentions, torture, and ill-treatment by Ecuadorian security forces following the declaration of an internal armed conflict on January 9, 2024.[133][134] These claims include reports of enforced disappearances attributed to military operations and at least one apparent extrajudicial killing, often occurring in the context of raids targeting gang strongholds.[133][135] The U.S. State Department's 2024 human rights report corroborated credible accounts of unlawful killings and cruel treatment, noting that such incidents arose amid expanded military involvement in urban policing and prison control.[136] However, many of these allegations remain unverified or rely on testimonies from detainees with potential gang affiliations, given the documented infiltration of criminal networks into Ecuador's prisons and judicial system, where gangs have historically orchestrated violence and influenced oversight mechanisms.[137][5] Critics from nongovernmental organizations, which often emphasize procedural safeguards, have framed the militarized response as risking authoritarian overreach, prioritizing abstract rights of suspected perpetrators over empirical restoration of public order.[138] In contrast, proponents of the security measures argue that such trade-offs are necessitated by the prior collapse of state authority, where inaction in 2023 permitted over 7,800 homicides—a rate of approximately 46 per 100,000 inhabitants—driven by unchecked gang territorial control and narco-trafficking.[137] Empirical outcomes underscore the imperatives of decisive action: intentional homicides fell by 14 to 18 percent in 2024 compared to 2023, with first-half figures dropping 16 percent to 3,036 killings, correlating directly with intensified anti-gang operations that reclaimed territorial control from armed groups.[139][140][141] This reduction, while not eliminating violence, reflects causal linkages between military mobilization and diminished gang operational capacity, as evidenced by disrupted extortion rackets and prison riots that had previously enabled thousands of murders annually.[27] Advocates for victims—whose rights were systematically eroded by gang dominance prior to 2024—contend that procedural critiques undervalue the prior humanitarian catastrophe, where civilian casualties from organized crime far exceeded reported security force excesses.[136] The tension highlights a broader debate: short-term rights suspensions in extremis versus the long-term fragility of institutions undermined by criminal impunity.[90]

Political opposition and effectiveness critiques

The political opposition, led by the Revolución Ciudadana party aligned with former President Rafael Correa and 2023/2025 presidential candidate Luisa González, has accused President Daniel Noboa's security measures of prioritizing political spectacle over substantive reform, particularly amid his reelection campaigns. Critics from this camp contend that the January 2024 declaration of internal armed conflict and associated military deployments represent opportunistic escalations timed for electoral advantage, despite the opposition's own support for broadening police and military authority in the April 21, 2024, plebiscite, which passed with 58-78% approval across questions on extradition, prison control, and joint task forces.[142][143] Such accusations downplay the continuity of escalating gang violence that originated under Correa's 2007-2017 tenure, when prison system infiltration by narcotraffickers laid the groundwork for later explosions in organized crime, with homicide rates rising from under 6 per 100,000 in 2010 to over 13 by 2017 due to unchecked corruption and lax oversight.[19] Debates over policy effectiveness center on conflicting metrics, where government data highlight reductions in high-profile organized attacks—such as a 14% national drop in intentional homicides from 2023 to 2024 following the armed conflict declaration and over 100,000 arrests—contrasted by opposition claims of negligible impact on everyday micro-crime like extortion and petty trafficking, which persist amid fragmented gang structures.[139][144] These critiques frequently omit the role of prior institutional failures, including judicial corruption exposed in probes like Caso Metastasis, which enabled gang entrenchment across administrations, while 2025 projections of around 9,100 homicides underscore that short-term disruptions in large-scale operations have not yet translated to systemic eradication.[139] Noboa's hardline strategy reflects bipartisan necessities, building on Guillermo Lasso's six states of emergency issued from May 2022 onward to combat surging violence, efforts undermined by assembly opposition from Correa-linked blocs that blocked budgets and reforms until Lasso's May 2023 dissolution.[13] Ongoing legislative and judicial resistance, including the Constitutional Court's August 2025 temporary suspension of key security provisions on procedural grounds, illustrates patterns of institutional sabotage that have hampered successive governments' responses, reinforcing the case for sustained military involvement despite critiques of its scope.[145][146]

Reactions and international involvement

Domestic political and civil society responses

The National Assembly's fragmentation, stemming from polarized elections in 2023, has impeded rapid approval of security-related budgets and reforms under President Daniel Noboa. With opposition blocs, including those aligned with former President Rafael Correa, holding significant seats, legislative delays affected capital expenditure execution for anti-gang operations in 2024.[147][148] Business associations and economic sectors endorsed Noboa's push for extraditing gang leaders to the United States, viewing it as essential to curb violence disrupting commerce and investment. In July 2025, following the extradition of Los Choneros leader José Adolfo Macías Villamar ("Fito"), chambers of commerce highlighted improved commodity stability as a direct benefit of intensified security cooperation.[149][150] Civil society responses diverged sharply, with families of gang violence victims advocating for stricter laws and military involvement to protect vulnerable, low-income neighborhoods disproportionately targeted by narcoterrorism. Organizations representing over 100,000 displaced persons in 2024 demanded sustained "iron fist" policies amid rising homicides. Conversely, human rights activists criticized militarization for eroding due process, citing arbitrary detentions during prison interventions, though empirical data underscored gangs' control over poor urban enclaves as the primary rights violator.[124][151][152] The crisis amplified political polarization, culminating in the 2025 elections where security overshadowed ideological divides. Noboa's National Democratic Action secured 55% in the April runoff against Correa-backed Luisa González's 44%, with voters prioritizing continuity of armed conflict measures over Correísta promises of social programs, as polls indicated 70% approval for the 2024 security referendum.[153][154][143]

Foreign government and organizational positions

The United States bolstered its partnership with Ecuador in 2024 to enhance public safety, judicial strengthening, and anti-crime efforts, recognizing the nation's critical position in countering transnational criminal organizations and drug trafficking.[155][156] This cooperation facilitated the seizure of hundreds of tons of drugs through joint operations.[157] U.S. assistance emphasized capacity building for law enforcement and institutions, aligning with Ecuador's declaration of an internal armed conflict against gangs on January 9, 2024.[136] The European Union committed €20 million in security aid to Ecuador in December 2024, alongside humanitarian funding exceeding €13 million for the year.[158][159] In September 2025, the EU and Ecuador formalized an agreement enabling Europol to exchange operational and personal data to dismantle organized crime networks.[160] The SERPAZ initiative, launched in July 2025, targeted institutional reinforcement, social cohesion, and resilient security environments amid escalating violence.[161] Latin American neighbors, including Colombia and Peru, voiced apprehensions about cross-border violence spillover from Ecuador's gang activities, spurring proposals for a regional alliance to suppress organized crime through coordinated prevention and response mechanisms.[162] The Organization of American States (OAS) collaborated with Ecuador on pacts to intensify anti-drug trafficking measures and intelligence capabilities, supporting the government's hardline stance against criminal groups designated as terrorists.[163] Human Rights Watch documented potential abuses following the armed conflict declaration, including unchecked violations and risks to due process, while noting the unprecedented homicide surge driven by cartels; similar critiques from Amnesty International highlighted opacity in security policies and calls for preventing extrajudicial actions, though these organizations have faced scrutiny for underemphasizing causal links between lax enforcement and violence escalation in source countries.[133][137][138] The UN Human Rights Committee issued findings in November 2024 urging Ecuador to address systemic issues in rights implementation amid the crisis.[164]

Impacts and ongoing developments

Security and homicide trends

Ecuador recorded approximately 8,008 homicides in 2023, marking a peak in violent deaths amid escalating organized crime activity.[165] This figure reflected a homicide rate of about 47 per 100,000 inhabitants, a sharp rise from prior years.[166] In 2024, total homicides declined to 6,818, representing a 14 percent reduction from 2023 levels.[167] The first half of 2024 saw 3,036 homicides, a 16 percent drop compared to the same period in 2023, though rates remained elevated relative to pre-2023 baselines.[141]

Projections for 2025 indicate around 9,100 homicides, signaling a potential resurgence despite the prior year's decline.[139] Data from January to June 2025 showed a 47 percent increase over the same period in 2024, averaging 25 murders per day.[168] Gang fragmentation contributed to decentralized threats, with at least 37 active gangs documented in 2024—a 54 percent increase from 2023—exacerbating localized violence in urban and rural areas.[27] This "second wave" of splintering correlated with sustained security pressures, including rises in events like 60 percent more violent incidents in 2025 compared to 2024.[169] Extortion cases reached nearly 22,000 in 2023, targeting small businesses amid broader crime surges.[170] While some operations yielded temporary reductions in certain metrics, overall reports indicated persistent or increasing non-homicide crimes, including kidnappings exceeding 2,000 by September 2024.[171] Prison violence remained a chronic issue, with coordinated riots and massacres continuing into 2024 and 2025, including a November 2024 incident at the country's largest facility resulting in multiple deaths and underscoring ongoing control struggles by inmates.[172] Fragmentation within facilities fueled recurrent clashes, hindering stabilization efforts.[139]

Economic and social ramifications

The escalation of gang violence in Ecuador during 2023, driven by ungoverned criminal networks exploiting weak institutional controls, imposed substantial economic burdens, including a 2% contraction in GDP in 2024 partly attributable to insecurity's dampening effects on activity and investment.[173] [174] Fundamentally, empirical analysis links rising homicide rates— which surged to approximately 45 per 100,000 inhabitants in 2023 from 25.9 in 2022—directly to reduced local economic output, with each 1% increase in the murder rate correlating to a 0.5% decline in activity levels through mechanisms like disrupted commerce and deterred private sector participation.[137] [174] Foreign direct investment, already constrained by pervasive risks, fell sharply amid the crisis, reflecting investor aversion to territories under de facto gang influence.[175] Socially, the crisis fueled internal displacement, with over 80,000 people uprooted by violence as of October 2024, primarily due to threats from criminal groups controlling urban and coastal zones.[167] This displacement, concentrated in provinces like Guayas and Manabí, compounded preexisting vulnerabilities, as affected populations faced barriers to employment, healthcare, and education, further entrenching cycles of poverty linked to eroded livelihoods.[173] Youth were particularly impacted, with gang recruitment pressures driving school transfers or dropouts to evade extortion, intimidation, and forced involvement; killings of children and adolescents escalated 640% from 2019 to 2023, reaching at least 770 deaths that year alone, as criminal factions targeted vulnerable demographics amid territorial disputes.[176] [177] Longer-term recovery hinges on institutional reforms to curb criminal governance, potentially restoring investment and stabilizing displacement trends, though persistent ungoverned spaces risk solidifying narco-economic dominance and perpetuating social fragmentation if unaddressed.[174][178]
