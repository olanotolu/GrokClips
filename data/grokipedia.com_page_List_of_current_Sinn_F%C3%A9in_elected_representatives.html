List of current Sinn Féin elected representatives

The list of current Sinn Féin elected representatives enumerates politicians holding office in Irish and British legislatures and local authorities who represent the party, which pursues Irish reunification, democratic socialism, and opposition to British jurisdiction over Northern Ireland through electoral politics and abstention from the UK Parliament.[1] As of October 2025, Sinn Féin holds 39 seats in Dáil Éireann (the lower house of the Oireachtas), 6 seats in Seanad Éireann, 28 seats in the Northern Ireland Assembly, 7 seats in the House of Commons (with MPs adhering to the party's abstentionist policy), 2 seats in the European Parliament, and 240 seats on local councils across the island.[1] Sinn Féin, whose name translates to "we ourselves" in Irish Gaelic, originated in 1905 as an independence movement but reemerged in its contemporary form after the 1969-1970 schism within the republican movement, positioning itself as the political counterpart to armed republicanism during the Troubles.[2] The party's electoral breakthrough accelerated post-Good Friday Agreement in 1998, enabling it to lead the Northern Ireland Executive since 2024 with Michelle O'Neill as First Minister—the first nationalist in that role—and to secure the largest vote share in both the 2022 Assembly election and earlier Republic of Ireland polls, though it faced setbacks in the 2024 local and general elections amid voter concerns over housing, immigration, and internal governance issues.[3][4] Defining characteristics include its rejection of Westminster oaths, promotion of all-island economic policies, and advocacy for progressive causes like public housing expansion and Irish language rights, balanced against historical ties to the Provisional IRA—many senior figures having participated in or supported the paramilitary campaign—which the party frames as a bygone phase supplanted by exclusively peaceful strategies since the peace process.[5] Controversies persist over alleged slow responses to legacy abuse allegations within republican communities and shifts in migration policy that alienated some core supporters, yet empirical voting data underscores its cross-border appeal in mobilizing nationalist and working-class constituencies toward constitutional change via referenda rather than violence.[6]

Electoral and Political Context

Abstentionism and Participation Practices

Sinn Féin's abstentionism policy mandates that its elected representatives to the UK House of Commons do not take their seats or participate in proceedings, a practice rooted in the party's rejection of the Parliament's legitimacy over Irish affairs due to the 1921 partition of Ireland. This stance, formalized in the early 20th century as a form of passive resistance against British rule, has been upheld consistently by the party since the 1918 UK general election, when Sinn Féin candidates won 73 seats but convened the First Dáil instead.[7][8] In the 2024 UK general election, Sinn Féin secured seven seats in Northern Ireland constituencies but, as with prior elections post-Good Friday Agreement, its MPs did not swear the required oath of allegiance to the Crown or engage in Westminster activities, forgoing rights to speak, vote, or access parliamentary resources.[9][10] In contrast, Sinn Féin fully participates in institutions it deems legitimate, including the Northern Ireland Assembly—where it holds 27 of 90 seats following the 2022 election and supplies First Minister Michelle O'Neill—the Republic of Ireland's Dáil Éireann and Seanad Éireann, local councils across both jurisdictions, and the European Parliament.[4][11] This selective engagement allows active policymaking on devolved and Irish matters, such as leading the Executive in Northern Ireland since February 2024 or tabling motions in the Dáil on unification preparations as of October 2025.[5] However, abstention from Westminster precludes voting on UK legislation directly impacting Northern Ireland, including fiscal policies, trade rules under the Northern Ireland Protocol, and Brexit-related amendments; for instance, during 2018 Brexit votes, Sinn Féin's non-participation contributed to narrow defeats for pro-EU measures despite an overall anti-Brexit majority in Northern Ireland.[12][13] The policy has drawn criticisms for diminishing democratic accountability, as constituents in Northern Ireland lack direct parliamentary advocacy on reserved matters like defense, immigration, and international trade, potentially leaving voter expectations unmet regarding comprehensive representation.[14][15] Proponents within Sinn Féin argue it upholds republican principles and avoids legitimizing partition, channeling efforts instead toward local advocacy, media engagement, and cross-border initiatives, though empirical data shows zero recorded instances of Sinn Féin MPs influencing Westminster votes since 1997.[16][17] This approach has sustained high electoral support in Northern Ireland—peaking at 27.5% first-preference votes in the 2022 Assembly election—without altering the party's Westminster non-engagement.[4]

Recent Electoral Developments

In the 2022 Northern Ireland Assembly election held on 5 May, Sinn Féin secured 27 seats, emerging as the largest party for the first time in the body's history and enabling it to nominate Michelle O'Neill as First Minister, though the Democratic Unionist Party's boycott delayed executive formation until February 2024.[18][19] This outcome reflected strong nationalist turnout amid post-Brexit tensions and the Protocol on Ireland/Northern Ireland, with Sinn Féin gaining one net seat from its 2017 total of 27.[18] Sinn Féin further consolidated its position in the 2023 Northern Ireland local elections on 18 May, increasing its councillor representation from 105 to 144 across the 11 councils, particularly strengthening holds in urban areas like Belfast and Derry City and Strabane.[20] This gain, representing about 31% of seats, underscored voter preference for Sinn Féin's local governance focus despite broader unionist fragmentation.[21] The 2024 local elections in the Republic of Ireland on 7 June saw Sinn Féin maintain a presence with approximately 77 seats, a slight decline from 81 in 2019, amid national first-preference vote challenges linked to perceptions of inconsistent stances on housing shortages and immigration pressures.[6][22] In the concurrent European Parliament election, Sinn Féin failed to retain its single seat from 2019, polling 10.8% nationally but falling short due to competition from independents and Greens on all-island priorities like agriculture and regional development.[23] Sinn Féin's performance in the Republic's general election on 29 November 2024 resulted in 39 Teachtaí Dála (TDs), up marginally from 37 in 2020 but below opinion poll peaks that had anticipated a potential path to government.[24] Voter concerns over housing affordability—exacerbated by supply shortages—and rising immigration inflows contributed to this shortfall, prompting post-election policy clarifications from the party on stricter controls.[25][26][27] These results shaped the current legislative representation by reinforcing Sinn Féin's opposition role while highlighting vulnerabilities in southern electorates.[28]

Representatives in the Republic of Ireland

Dáil Éireann

Sinn Féin secured 39 seats in the 34th Dáil Éireann following the general election held on 29 November 2024, comprising the largest opposition grouping in the 174-seat chamber.[29][30] This outcome marked an increase from the party's previous 37 seats in the 33rd Dáil, reflecting voter support for its platform on housing, cost-of-living issues, and Irish unity, though short of a majority or coalition viability.[30] The composition remained unchanged through October 2025, with no by-elections or resignations affecting Sinn Féin representation.[31] Unlike its abstentionist stance in the UK House of Commons, Sinn Féin TDs attend and vote in Dáil Éireann, engaging actively in legislation, scrutiny committees, and opposition business.[32] The party forms the principal opposition bloc, critiquing government policies on public spending and infrastructure while proposing alternatives through private members' bills. In January 2025, party leader Mary Lou McDonald announced a frontbench reshuffle assigning 20 senior spokespeople to key portfolios, including Pearse Doherty (Finance), Eoin Ó Broin (Housing, Local Government and Heritage), David Cullinane (Health), and Mairéad Farrell (Public Expenditure and Reform), to shadow ministerial roles and hold the Fianna Fáil-Fine Gael-Independent coalition accountable.[33] Government formation excluded Sinn Féin due to numerical arithmetic favoring the center-right parties' agreement, finalized on 23 January 2025.[34] The following table lists the 39 Sinn Féin TDs alphabetically by surname, with their constituencies as elected in 2024:

Seanad Éireann

Sinn Féin holds six seats in the 27th Seanad Éireann, comprising 10% of the 60-member chamber, following the indirect elections conducted in January 2025 after the November 2024 Dáil general election.[1] These seats were secured via elections on the vocational panels by an electoral college of approximately 1,200 voters, including city and county councillors, sitting TDs, and outgoing senators.[36] The process allocates seats proportionally based on party strength among electors, though Sinn Féin's gains were constrained by the outgoing coalition government's enduring influence among local authority voters. The senators focus on scrutinizing Dáil legislation, proposing amendments, and advancing all-island policy perspectives, yet the chamber's advisory nature—lacking veto authority and subject to Dáil override—restricts substantive impact given Sinn Féin's opposition status.[37][38] The current Sinn Féin senators, serving five-year terms until 2030 unless dissolved earlier, are distributed across panels as follows:

Representatives in Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland Assembly

Sinn Féin holds 27 seats in the Northern Ireland Assembly, comprising the largest party grouping among the 90 members elected on 5 May 2022 under the single transferable vote system.[42][19] This outcome represented a gain of one seat from the 2017 election, with the party securing 29% of first-preference votes and enabling it to nominate the First Minister for the first time.[42] The Assembly's operations were suspended from October 2022 until 3 February 2024, primarily due to the Democratic Unionist Party's boycott over post-Brexit trade arrangements under the Northern Ireland Protocol (superseded by the Windsor Framework in 2023). Restoration followed a UK government deal providing safeguards and scrutiny mechanisms for Northern Ireland's access to the UK internal market, allowing DUP participation in the power-sharing Executive. Upon resumption, Sinn Féin MLAs assumed key executive roles, including Michelle O'Neill as First Minister (nominated as the largest party), with junior ministers such as Aisling Reilly supporting cross-departmental functions.[43] Other appointees include Caoimhe Archibald as Minister of Finance, responsible for budgeting and fiscal policy in devolved areas, and John O'Dowd as Minister for Infrastructure, overseeing transport and planning.[43] The party engages fully in Assembly proceedings on transferred competences like health, education, and economic development, tabling motions and serving on statutory committees to scrutinize ministers and legislate for Northern Ireland—distinct from its abstentionist stance in the Westminster Parliament. No by-elections or resignations have altered Sinn Féin's representation as of October 2025, maintaining the 2022 composition.[44] The MLAs, elected from 14 of the 18 constituencies (each returning five members), are:

Local Councils

Sinn Féin holds 144 seats across Northern Ireland's 11 local councils following the 2023 elections, making it the largest party overall in local government for the first time.[45] These councillors, elected on May 18, 2023, exercise authority over local planning permissions, rates (property taxes), waste management, recreational facilities, and community services, with the party's gains reflecting strengthened grassroots support amid broader nationalist turnout.[21] No significant shifts have occurred through by-elections as of October 2025, preserving the post-2023 distribution.[46] The party secured its strongest showings in Mid Ulster (23 seats out of 40), Newry, Mourne and Down (19/41), Armagh City, Banbridge and Craigavon (18/41), Derry City and Strabane (18/40), and Fermanagh and Omagh (18/40), enabling influence over local budgets, infrastructure development, and service priorities in these areas.[47] In Belfast City Council, Sinn Féin won 23 of 60 seats, positioning it as the largest group despite lacking an outright majority, allowing leverage in cross-community decisions on urban planning and rates.[48] Derry City and Strabane stands out, where the party's 18 seats mark a breakthrough in traditionally contested wards like Waterside and Faughan, enhancing control over regional development projects and cross-border initiatives.[49] Sinn Féin leads in six councils, often forming alliances or holding key committee chairs to shape policies on housing and economic regeneration, though abstentionism at higher levels does not extend to local participation. Controversies have arisen, such as instances where Sinn Féin councillors boycotted meetings over disputes involving unionist symbols or bonfires, impacting quorum and decision-making in shared councils like Belfast.[47] These local roles underscore the party's focus on community-level governance, with 2023 advances attributed to voter mobilization against Stormont's unionist boycott rather than policy shifts.[50]

Representatives in the United Kingdom Parliament

House of Commons

Sinn Féin holds seven seats in the House of Commons, representing all its elected members from Northern Ireland constituencies following the 4 July 2024 general election, comprising the largest bloc among Northern Ireland's 18 MPs.[51] These MPs adhere to the party's longstanding abstentionist policy, refusing to swear the parliamentary oath of allegiance required to sit and vote, thereby forgoing participation in debates, legislation, or votes on matters such as UK budgets and reserved powers affecting Northern Ireland.[52] This results in zero recorded contributions or votes by Sinn Féin MPs in Hansard records since the election, limiting direct influence despite securing over 27% of Northern Ireland's first-preference votes.[53] The MPs do not receive the standard £91,346 annual salary for not taking the oath but claim allowable expenses for staffing, office costs, and travel under a 1933 Commons resolution permitting such for non-sitting members.[54] [52] This practice has drawn criticism for providing financial support without attendance or accountability in Westminster proceedings, though Sinn Féin maintains it enables constituency work outside the chamber.[55]

Representatives in the European Parliament

Current Members

Sinn Féin holds one seat in the European Parliament following the 2024 elections, represented by Lynn Boylan, elected on 7 June 2024 to the Dublin constituency with the party securing approximately 10.7% of first-preference votes across Ireland.[23][56] Boylan affiliates with The Left group (GUE/NGL) and participates fully in parliamentary proceedings, serving as a member of the Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Food Safety and the Committee on International Trade since July 2024.[57] This active engagement contrasts with the party's abstentionist policy in the UK House of Commons. The single seat out of 13 allocated to the Republic of Ireland reflects proportional representation outcomes in a multi-party contest, where Sinn Féin prioritized issues like climate action and workers' rights.[23] Boylan's work emphasizes cross-border advocacy, including support for maintaining the Windsor Framework to protect Northern Ireland's place in the EU single market and all-island economic ties, as outlined in Sinn Féin's 2024 manifesto.[58][59] Northern Ireland's sole MEP seat, elected separately under UK rules, was not won by Sinn Féin in 2024, limiting direct representation from that region but underscoring the party's focus on EU-level influence for Irish unity objectives.[23]

Local Government Representatives in the Republic of Ireland

Summary of Councillors

Sinn Féin secured 102 seats across local authorities in the Republic of Ireland in the local elections held on 7 June 2024, an increase from 81 seats in 2019.[60][61] The party achieved its strongest representation in urban centers, including 8 seats on Dublin City Council out of 63 total, reflecting gains in areas with higher population density despite a first-preference vote share of 7.7 percent, bolstered by transfers under the proportional representation single transferable vote system.[62][63] Representation was more limited in rural counties, where the party registered fewer seats relative to incumbents like Fianna Fáil and Fine Gael. These councillors, serving five-year terms until 2029, address key local functions including housing development, planning applications, infrastructure maintenance, and setting commercial rates that fund council operations.[64] In councils where Sinn Féin forms part of the opposition—most commonly alongside independents and smaller parties—the group influences outcomes through voting on annual budgets, development plans, and referenda on issues like directly elected mayors or zoning changes, often prioritizing community-driven initiatives on affordability and public services. No significant alterations to Sinn Féin's councillor totals have been reported through co-options or by-election vacancies as of October 2025, maintaining the post-2024 composition amid stable local governance.[64] Unlike higher-level national politics, local roles emphasize granular opposition scrutiny, with Sinn Féin's urban footholds enabling targeted advocacy on housing shortages and planning amid Ireland's ongoing population growth and development pressures. This setup under PR-STV contrasts with Northern Ireland's local councils, which use similar mechanics but feature larger seven-member wards, fostering a parallel yet distinct community-oriented approach in cross-border republican representation.
