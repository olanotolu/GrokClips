Max Barskih

Max Barskih (born Mykola Mykolaiovych Bortnyk; 8 March 1990) is a Ukrainian singer-songwriter recognized for his contributions to pop music and his enlistment in the Ukrainian military during the 2022 Russian invasion.[1][2] Raised in Kherson, Barskih first gained prominence in 2008 through participation in the television talent competition Star Factory, followed by training at the Kyiv Academy of Pop and Circus Arts.[3][4] He has competed in Ukraine's national selections for the Eurovision Song Contest, finishing as runner-up in 2012, and has earned multiple awards, including recognition as Ukraine's best artist at the MTV Europe Music Awards.[5][6] In the wake of Russia's full-scale invasion, Barskih joined the territorial defense forces, withdrew his music from Russian platforms, and released tracks such as "Don't F**k With Ukraine" to express solidarity with his country's resistance.[7][8][9] As of October 2025, he is developing an English-language album and planning a United States tour to expand his international presence.[3]

Early life

Upbringing in Kherson

Mykola Mykolaiovych Bortnyk, known professionally as Max Barskih, was born on March 8, 1990, in Kherson, then part of the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic. He spent his formative years in the city, developing early interests that shaped his artistic inclinations.[3] Barskih has recalled his childhood in Kherson as idyllic, describing the city as "the coziest on earth" from his youthful perspective, with every street explored and every yard a playground for local children.[10] [11] These memories, shared via archival photographs in 2022, highlight a carefree upbringing amid Kherson's urban landscape, though specific family details remain private and unpublicized in available accounts.[12] During this period, Bortnyk attended and graduated from a local art school, fostering creative skills that later influenced his career trajectory before relocating to Kyiv post-graduation.[13] Family influences reportedly steered him toward alternative professions popular in Kherson at the time, diverging from his eventual path in music, though he pursued artistic education amid these expectations.[14]

Education and initial artistic pursuits

Barskih received his early artistic training in Kherson at the Tauride Lyceum of Arts, focusing on fine arts, and at the city's Art School complex, where he developed skills in drawing and visual expression.[4][15] During his school years, he initially pursued ambitions in visual arts, graduating with a diploma in the field and contemplating a career as a painter.[4] Shifting toward music, Barskih relocated to Kyiv following high school graduation around 2007.[16] There, he enrolled at the Kyiv Municipal Academy of Circus and Variety Arts (also known as the Kyiv Academy of Pop and Circus Arts), majoring in pop vocals to hone his singing technique.[3][4] His initial musical pursuits involved vocal training and preparation for competitive performance, culminating in his 2008 audition for the Ukrainian talent show Fabryka Zirok 2 (Star Factory 2), marking his entry into professional entertainment.[17]

Barskih, born Maksym Bortnyk, first gained public attention in 2008 as a contestant on the Ukrainian talent competition Fabryka Zirok 2 (Star Factory 2), a televised show that launched several performers' careers by featuring intensive training and live performances.[2] During the program, he performed original compositions including "ÐÐ½Ð¾Ð¼Ð°Ð»Ð¸Ñ" (Anomaly) and "ÐÐµÐ·Ð½Ð°ÐºÐ¾Ð¼ÐºÐ°" (Stranger), showcasing his songwriting abilities alongside vocal and stage presence, which helped him reach the finals and build a fanbase across Ukraine.[18] Following the show's conclusion, Barskih transitioned to a solo career, debuting professionally in 2009 with the single "S.L." Mainstream success and style development (2013â€“2018)

In 2014â€“2015, Max Barskih transitioned toward a more introspective phase in his songwriting, culminating in the release of his third studio album Po Freydu in September 2015, which functioned as a personal diary chronicling his emotional experiences and hopes during his early twenties around 2013.[23] The 13-track electropop project explored themes of love, loss, and self-reflection through tracks like the title song "Po Freydu" and "Podruga-noch," marking a shift from the high-energy dance focus of his prior work Z.Dance (2012) to deeper, narrative-driven compositions.[24] The album's title track charted for 11 weeks across monitored platforms, contributing to Barskih's growing prominence in Ukrainian and post-Soviet pop markets.[25] Building on this momentum, Barskih released his fourth studio album Tumany on October 7, 2016, a 12-track collection that further refined his electropop sound with atmospheric elements and emotive vocals. Key singles such as "Tumany/Nevernaya" propelled the record's commercial reach, with the music video surpassing 216 million views on YouTube within eight years of release, underscoring its viral appeal and Barskih's ability to connect with audiences through melancholic, synth-driven ballads.[26] Other tracks like "Poproshu" and "P'yana Luna" highlighted his evolving production style, incorporating layered electronics and personal storytelling that resonated in live performances across Ukraine and neighboring regions.[27] This era solidified Barskih's mainstream stature as one of Ukraine's premier pop exports, with Po Freydu and Tumany recognized retrospectively as pivotal works in his discography for blending accessible electronic pop with psychological depth.[3][28] His stylistic maturationâ€”evident in the move from upbeat club anthems to more vulnerable, Freud-inspired introspectionâ€”aligned with broader trends in Eastern European pop, enabling sold-out tours and sustained radio play in CIS countries during the mid-2010s.[29]

In 2019, Max Barskih released his sixth studio album, titled 7, on February 8, comprising seven electropop and dance-pop tracks that built on his evolving synth-driven sound.[30][31] The project marked a continuation of his focus on concise, visually oriented releases, with production emphasizing electronic elements and themes of personal introspection, receiving attention in Ukrainian and Russian-speaking markets for its polished production.[32] Barskih garnered recognition through several awards that year, including the "Video of the Year (male vocal)" at the Top Hit Music Awards for his single "Ð‘ÐµÑ€ÐµÐ³Ð°" (Berega), released in late 2018 but peaking in popularity into 2019, and the "Record-breaking song" award for "Ð›ÐµÐ¹, Ð½Ðµ Ð¶Ð°Ð»ÐµÐ¹" (Ley, ne zhÐ°Ð»ÐµÐ¹).[4] These accolades, presented by the Russian-oriented Top Hit platform, underscored his commercial dominance in post-Soviet territories, where his tracks frequently topped radio and streaming charts. He also received nominations at the Muz-TV Music Awards 2019, a major Russian event, highlighting his cross-border appeal despite his Ukrainian origins.[33] On the performance front, Barskih expanded beyond Ukraine with a concert at Cathouse Concert Hall in Tallinn, Estonia, on July 26, 2019, drawing fans from the Baltic region and signaling early efforts to engage European audiences outside traditional markets.[34] This appearance, part of a broader tour promoting 7, contributed to his growing international footprint, though his primary fanbase remained concentrated in Ukraine and Russia. No involvement in the Eurovision Song Contest occurred that year, as Ukraine withdrew from the event amid internal selection disputes unrelated to Barskih.[35]

Post-2022 releases and wartime adaptations

Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, Max Barskih released the single "Don't F*ck With Ukraine" on April 29, 2022, dedicating it to the Ukrainian Armed Forces and civilian resistance against the aggression.[8][7] The track explicitly targeted Russian invaders, reflecting Barskih's shift toward patriotic expression amid the conflict.[36] In parallel, he immediately removed his catalog from Russian streaming platforms and canceled a scheduled tour in Russia, severing commercial ties with that market despite prior popularity there.[37][38] Barskih's musical output adapted further by emphasizing Ukrainian-language content, aligning with broader cultural efforts to assert national identity during wartime Russification pressures. Wartime involvement

Initial response to the 2022 Russian invasion

Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, Max Barskih, a native of Kherson Oblast which faced early occupation by Russian forces on March 2, publicly aligned himself with Ukrainian resistance by releasing the Ukrainian-language song "Bude Vesna" ("There Will Be Spring") in the initial days of the conflict.[45][46] The track, featuring lyrics evoking hope amid destructionâ€”"The city on fire, my heart hurts"â€”served as an early morale booster, symbolizing defiance and the anticipation of victory, and was recorded amid reports of frequent bombings near his location in Ukraine.[47][48] Barskih also took immediate professional steps to sever ties with Russian markets, withdrawing his music catalog from streaming platforms accessible in Russia and halting all performances and recordings in Russian, a language he had previously used extensively in his career.[37] This decision reflected his stated view of the invasion as a profound personal and national rupture, altering his values and prompting a shift away from prior collaborations with Russian audiences.[49] In the weeks following the invasion, Barskih assisted his family in evacuating to safety abroad before returning to Kyiv, where he enlisted as a reservist in the Armed Forces of Ukraine, undergoing initial training to contribute to the defense effort.[7][50] He described the invasion's onset as mentally transformative, emphasizing in later reflections that it compelled a reevaluation of priorities toward national survival over artistic pursuits.[38] These actions positioned him among Ukrainian public figures who rapidly mobilized in response to the aggression, forgoing international tours scheduled prior to the war.[4]

Military enlistment and contributions

Following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, Barskih evacuated his family to safety abroad before returning to Kyiv, where he enlisted in the Armed Forces of Ukraine as a reservist in late March 2022.[7][8] He publicly announced his enlistment on Instagram on April 4, 2022, stating his commitment to defending the country amid ongoing combat.[50] Prior to frontline duties, he completed a two-week military training program in Kyiv to prepare for service.[51] In his military role, Barskih focused on logistical support, including the procurement and distribution of equipment and weapons to frontline units, as well as fundraising efforts to sustain humanitarian aid operations.[52] His contributions extended to early wartime relief, such as delivering medicine and food to displaced civilians before formal enlistment.[51] Barskih has described this shift from pop stardom to soldier as a personal transformation, noting in interviews that his uniform replaced sequined stage attire, symbolizing broader civilian mobilization in Ukraine's defense.[7][53] As part of his service-related efforts, Barskih released the track "Don't F*ck with Ukraine" on May 2, 2022, explicitly dedicating it to the Ukrainian military's resilience and the civilian resistance against the invasion; the song critiques Russian aggression and highlights the honor of armed defenders.[8][54] No public records indicate continued active frontline deployment beyond initial 2022 involvement, with subsequent activities shifting toward music production amid wartime constraints.[37]

Other professional activities

Songwriting for other artists

Barskih has composed and written lyrics for select Ukrainian artists, primarily in collaborative capacities within the pop genre. His contributions emphasize melodic structures and bilingual elements, reflecting his own stylistic influences from electronic and dance-pop.[55][5] In 2010, Barskih co-wrote the duet "Serdtse Byotsya" (Heartbeat) with Svetlana Loboda, a track blending upbeat rhythms with romantic themes that achieved commercial play in Eastern European markets. The song, released as a single, featured Barskih as a performer alongside Loboda, marking an early instance of his songwriting extending beyond solo work.[55][56]

Acting and media appearances

Barskih's acting debut occurred in 2010 when he portrayed the lover of the protagonist, played by Lara Fabian, in the supporting role for the musical film Mademoiselle Zhivago.[58] Directed by Alan Badoev, the 52-minute production, released in 2013, weaves songs from Fabian's album of the same name into its storyline, marking Barskih's entry into film as a then-emerging performer.[59] In 2015, Barskih appeared in the Ukrainian film Vinnitsa, though details of his specific role remain limited in available credits.[60] Beyond scripted roles, Barskih has featured in media primarily as a musical performer and guest. He debuted on Ukrainian television in 2008 as a contestant on the talent competition Star Factory 2 (Fabryka Zirok 2), which propelled his early visibility.[2] Notable later appearances include a musical guest spot on the Russian late-night show Evening Urgant in 2017 alongside host Ivan Urgant and actor Dmitry Nagiev.[61] He also participated as a nominee in the 2018 Zhara Music Awards broadcast.[62] These outings underscore his crossover from music to broader entertainment platforms, though acting has remained secondary to his primary career in songwriting and performance.

Personal life and public positions

Relationships and privacy

Barskih has maintained a high degree of privacy regarding his romantic relationships, with no confirmed partners or marriages publicly documented as of 2025.[63] Early in his career, during participation in the Ukrainian talent show Fabrika Zirok in 2008, he developed unrequited feelings for fellow contestant and singer Svetlana Loboda, which inspired song dedications but did not result in a mutual relationship.[4] This episode, described in biographical accounts as a youthful infatuation, marked one of the few personal anecdotes he has indirectly referenced through his work, though Loboda has not reciprocated romantically in public statements.[64] In interviews, Barskih rarely discusses intimate aspects of his life, instead channeling themes of love and loss into his songwriting, often drawing from imagined or generalized experiences rather than personal disclosures.[3] He has not acknowledged children or family beyond professional collaborations and national solidarity during wartime, prioritizing his public image around artistic output and Ukrainian advocacy over personal revelations.[7] This reticence aligns with his broader stance against oversharing, as evidenced by the absence of such details in major media profiles from outlets like Billboard and Forbes, which focus exclusively on his career trajectory.[8]

Political and cultural stances

Barskih has voiced unequivocal opposition to the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, framing it as an unprovoked aggression that reshaped his national identity from a broader Slavic affiliation to a distinctly Ukrainian one, where he described compatriots as forming a "big family."[7] In response, he pulled his music catalog from Russian platforms, canceled tours there despite a large fanbase, and halted all recordings in the Russian language to underscore cultural separation from the aggressor state.[37][3] His cultural advocacy emphasizes Ukrainian resilience through music, as seen in the release of "Don't F*ck With Ukraine" on May 2, 2022, an electronic anthem dedicated to the Armed Forces and civilian defenders, which he positioned as a morale booster amid the conflict's third month.[8] Barskih has positioned himself as a cultural ambassador for Ukraine internationally, leveraging pop music to highlight national pride and counter stereotypes of Eastern European artists, while prioritizing defense of sovereignty over artistic pursuits during wartime.[3][37] On foreign policy, Barskih extended his anti-aggression stance beyond Russia by canceling a scheduled solo concert in Baku, Azerbaijan, on September 15, 2022, in solidarity with Armenia following its clashes with Azerbaijan, declaring that nations permitting aggression against others are unsuitable for his performances.[65] This action aligns with a consistent rejection of imperial or expansionist behaviors, though he has not publicly detailed positions on Ukraine's domestic politics or broader ideological affiliations.
