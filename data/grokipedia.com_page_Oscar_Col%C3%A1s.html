Oscar Colás

Oscar Luis Colás (born September 17, 1998) is a Cuban professional baseball outfielder who defected from Cuba in 2020 and has competed at high levels in multiple leagues, including Japan's Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) and Major League Baseball (MLB).[1][2] After playing for the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks in Japan, where he contributed to their 2019 Japan Series championship, Colás signed with the Chicago White Sox organization in 2022 as a top international prospect for a reported $2.7 million bonus.[3][4] Colás made his MLB debut with the White Sox on March 30, 2023, appearing primarily as a left fielder and designated hitter during his tenure.[1] Over two seasons in the majors, he recorded a .223 batting average, five home runs, and 23 RBIs across 88 games, while also spending time in the minors developing his skills.[5] His release by the White Sox in May 2025 marked the end of his time with the team, after which he joined the Los Angeles Angels' minor league system.[4][6] Regarded as a two-way talent early in his career—capable of both hitting and pitching—Colás earned comparisons to Shohei Ohtani, though he has focused primarily on outfield play in professional ball.[7] His journey involved navigating a disputed contract with the SoftBank Hawks following his defection, which delayed his entry into MLB free agency until resolved in his favor.[8][9]

Early life and amateur career

Upbringing in Cuba

Oscar Colás was born on September 17, 1998, in Havana, Cuba.[1][10] He attended Escuela Número 95 Nguyen in Van Troy, Cuba, a neighborhood in the Havana area.[1] Growing up amid Cuba's deep-rooted baseball culture, where the sport permeates youth development from an early age, Colás honed his athletic skills through the nation's structured amateur system.[11] He trained alongside fellow Cuban prospect Luis Robert Jr., later a Chicago White Sox teammate, which helped refine his outfield and hitting abilities prior to professional play.[1] Colás entered Cuba's professional ranks at age 17 during the 2016–17 Cuban National Series season with the Santiago de Cuba team, marking the transition from youth leagues to competitive senior baseball under the state-controlled system.[12] This early entry reflected his standout potential in a pipeline that funnels top talents into the national league, though opportunities remained constrained by government oversight of player movements and international exposure.[3]

Entry into professional baseball

Colás made his professional debut in Cuba's Serie Nacional, the country's premier baseball league, during the 2015–16 season at age 17. In 23 games that year, he batted .278 with a .370 on-base percentage and .494 slugging percentage, compiling an .864 OPS while splitting time between first base and right field. His early performance highlighted his potential as a power-hitting outfielder capable of contributing on both sides of the ball, having already pitched in youth competitions.[3] Prior to entering the Serie Nacional, Colás progressed through Cuba's structured youth baseball system, representing the national under-18 team at the 2015 WBSC U-18 Baseball World Cup, where he hit .368 with six RBI in limited action. This international exposure underscored his athletic versatility, as scouts noted his above-average arm strength and hitting ability from the left side.[13] Transitioning to professional play in the state-controlled Cuban system required navigating provincial teams and government oversight, with players typically advancing from amateur provincial leagues to the national circuit based on performance and selection.[2] Colás' entry aligned with a period when Cuban authorities began allowing select players limited international opportunities under contract, though his initial focus remained domestic development. By the 2016–17 season, he had transferred to the Santiago de Cuba team, continuing to build his profile as a two-way prospect amid the league's emphasis on team-oriented play over individual statistics. His rapid ascent reflected the Cuban system's emphasis on early identification of talent through rigorous provincial and national youth pipelines, though opportunities for professional advancement beyond the island were heavily restricted.[2]

Cuban National Series

Performance and recognition

Colás made his debut in the Cuban National Series during the 2016–17 season with Santiago de Cuba, appearing in 23 games while batting .278 with 22 hits, 4 home runs, and 11 RBIs over 79 at-bats; his on-base percentage stood at .370 and slugging at .494.[6] In the following 2018–19 campaign, he transferred to Holguín for 20 games, achieving a standout .380 batting average with 30 hits, 1 home run, and 16 RBIs in 79 at-bats, complemented by a .432 on-base percentage and .519 slugging percentage.[6] Returning to Santiago de Cuba for the abbreviated 2019–20 season amid the COVID-19 pandemic, Colás played 24 games, hitting .256 with 20 hits, 4 home runs, and 18 RBIs across 78 at-bats, yielding an on-base percentage of .344 and slugging of .449.[6] Across 67 regular-season games in these three seasons, Colás compiled a .305 batting average, 9 home runs, and 45 RBIs, showcasing emerging power potential despite limited playing time as a young prospect often splitting duties between hitting and occasional pitching.[6] He also appeared in 5 postseason games for Ciego de Ávila during the 2018–19 playoffs, batting .240 with 6 RBIs.[14] Early in his career, Colás demonstrated versatility by pitching 10 innings with a 2.70 ERA, though he transitioned primarily to outfield and hitting roles.[14] No individual awards such as All-Star selections or league honors are recorded for Colás in the National Series, reflecting his status as a developing talent in a competitive domestic league where opportunities were constrained by team rotations and national team commitments.[6] Nonetheless, his consistent on-base skills and extra-base power in limited action contributed to his eventual selection for international play, including a loan to Japan's Nippon Professional Baseball prior to defection.[15]

Defection from Cuba

Circumstances and process

Oscar Colás defected from Cuba in early January 2020, shortly after completing his 2019 season with the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks' minor league affiliate in Japan, where he had been loaned by the Cuban Baseball Federation.[16][17] The move was motivated by his desire to secure direct entry into Major League Baseball as a free agent, bypassing the restrictions imposed by Cuban government oversight on player contracts and postings abroad.[2] Prior to this, Colás had received official permission from Cuban authorities to play professionally in Japan starting in 2018, allowing him to compete without initially defecting, though under terms that limited his earnings and autonomy, with a portion of his salary remitted to the federation.[17] The defection process followed the standard pathway for Cuban baseball players seeking MLB eligibility: Colás left Cuba covertly, establishing residency in a third country neither Cuba nor the United States to comply with U.S. Treasury regulations.[17] He then applied for and received a specific license from the U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), which enforces sanctions against Cuba, certifying that he was no longer under Cuban government control.[17][16] Once cleared by OFAC, MLB classified him as an international amateur free agent, subjecting him to the league's signing period rules and bonus pool caps, with potential eligibility opening on July 2, 2020, though his case was further delayed by ongoing contractual obligations to the SoftBank Hawks.[16][2] This multi-step verification ensured compliance with both U.S. law and MLB protocols, typically spanning several months to confirm the player's severed ties to the Cuban regime.[17]

Risks and aftermath

Defecting from Cuba entailed substantial personal and familial risks for baseball players like Colás, including perilous escapes often facilitated by smugglers charging exorbitant fees, with dangers of arrest, injury, or death during clandestine border crossings or sea voyages to third countries such as Mexico or Haiti to establish residency for MLB eligibility.[18][19] Cuban authorities imposed strict controls on athletes' international travel, increasing the likelihood of failed attempts resulting in permanent bans from national teams and potential imprisonment upon repatriation.[20] Family members left behind frequently suffered repercussions, such as government harassment, loss of employment, restricted access to education or healthcare, or even detention, as the regime viewed defection as treasonous betrayal.[21] Specific details of Colás' departure method in December 2019 remain undisclosed in public reports, but as a high-profile prospect, his exit aligned with patterns of evasion during or after permitted international competitions to avoid return.[2] No verified accounts detail acute perils he personally encountered, though the opacity of such operations underscores inherent uncertainties and potential for exploitation by intermediaries.[22] In the immediate aftermath, Colás secured passage to professional baseball abroad, signing a four-year, approximately $9 million contract with Japan's Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks in January 2020 shortly after defecting, marking his establishment of foreign residency.[16] However, he refused to report to spring training, contesting the deal's validity on grounds of inadequate representation and pressure, which prompted the Hawks to place him on their restricted list and bar play elsewhere until resolution.[8] This legal standoff, resolved in his favor by late 2020, delayed his MLB pursuit but ultimately enabled free agency clearance, culminating in a $2.7 million signing with the Chicago White Sox in January 2021.[9] The defection severed official ties to Cuban baseball, prohibiting return without risking arrest, though Colás expressed no public remorse over the separation from national team structures.[23]

Professional career in Japan

Signing with Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks

In 2017, Oscar Colás entered into a developmental contract (ikusei yūyū) with the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks of Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB), facilitated through an agreement between the Cuban government and the organization.[24][8] This arrangement allowed Colás, then a promising two-way talent from Cuba's National Series, to gain professional experience in Japan's farm system while Cuba retained oversight of player dispatches abroad. As an ikusei player, Colás received a modest salary—typically around 2-3 million yen annually—and was ineligible for the NPB major league roster until promoted, focusing instead on development in the Western League.[24] The deal reflected Cuba's selective policy of loaning elite prospects to foreign leagues under government-controlled terms, marking one of the early instances of such partnerships with NPB teams.[8]

Contract dispute and resolution

In 2017, at age 18, Colás agreed to a developmental contract with the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks, structured as a three-year non-roster deal with five club option years thereafter, negotiated partly through Cuban baseball authorities.[8] Following his defection from Cuba after the 2019 season, Colás's representatives challenged the contract's validity in early 2020, asserting that he had not been fully informed of key terms, including the extended option structure, and that the agreement was effectively arranged between the Hawks and Cuban officials without his independent counsel or comprehension due to his youth and circumstances.[25] [7] The Hawks maintained their rights under the contract, rejecting renegotiation proposals from Colás's camp, such as a one-year return in 2020 followed by release, which would have allowed him to pursue Major League Baseball opportunities.[8] By June 10, 2020, Colás publicly claimed the contract was invalid, highlighting procedural irregularities and his limited agency in the original signing process, amid broader concerns over the treatment of young Latin American defectors in Nippon Professional Baseball.[8] [26] Negotiations stalled through mid-2020, with the Hawks exercising leverage from the contract's terms to retain control, though Colás did not play for the team during the dispute.[25] Resolution came on December 2, 2020, when the SoftBank Hawks declared Colás a free agent, effectively releasing him from the remaining obligations and enabling him to petition Major League Baseball for free agency status.[27] [7] This outcome followed months of contention but avoided formal arbitration, allowing Colás to establish residency and pursue MLB signing eligibility under international free agency rules, subject to U.S. Treasury Department clearance for defectors.[7] The dispute underscored vulnerabilities in contracts for underage international prospects, particularly those from state-controlled systems like Cuba's, where player autonomy is often curtailed.[28]

Major League Baseball career

Chicago White Sox tenure

Colás signed a minor league contract with the Chicago White Sox on January 25, 2022, receiving a $2.7 million signing bonus as part of the team's international signing pool allocation.[29] Following the agreement, he ceased pitching to concentrate exclusively on outfield positions.[1] In 2022, his inaugural season in the White Sox minor league system, Colás batted .314 with a .387 on-base percentage, .508 slugging percentage, 23 home runs, and an .895 OPS over 107 games spanning High-A Winston-Salem, Double-A Birmingham, and Triple-A Charlotte.[30] He missed approximately one month in May due to a wrist injury but still ranked among the organization's top prospects entering 2023.[29] The White Sox added Colás to their 40-man roster in November 2022 and selected his contract from Triple-A Charlotte on March 30, 2023, enabling his major league debut that day as a defensive replacement in right field against the San Diego Padres.[1] During the 2023 season, he appeared in 88 games, primarily in left and right field, compiling a .223 batting average, .271 on-base percentage, .309 slugging percentage, five home runs, 23 RBIs, and a .580 OPS, reflecting below-average offensive production with a -1.4 WAR.[5] His struggles prompted an optioning to Triple-A Charlotte on July 4, followed by a September 11 recall amid roster expansion, though he batted just .143 in 11 September games.[1] Colás' 2024 major league opportunities with the White Sox were curtailed, limited to 13 games with a .273 average (9-for-33), one RBI, and four strikeouts before a June 23 recall from Charlotte yielded minimal additional play.[31] He sustained a right wrist sprain on March 6 during spring training, sidelining him for nearly two weeks and contributing to his non-roster status on Opening Day.[32] The White Sox designated him for assignment on March 20, 2025, and outrighted him to Charlotte on March 26, before releasing him on May 24 after a .500 OPS in 29 minor league games that season.[33]

Transition to Los Angeles Angels

Following a disappointing performance in the Chicago White Sox minor league system during the early 2025 season, where Colás appeared in limited action across affiliates, the White Sox released him on May 24, 2025.[33][34] This came after he had been designated for assignment on March 26, 2025, and optioned to Triple-A Charlotte Knights earlier that month.[1] Three days later, on May 27, 2025, Colás agreed to a minor league contract with the Los Angeles Angels, pending a physical examination.[35] The deal was formalized on May 29, 2025, allowing him to join the organization as a free agent outfielder with potential for evaluation at multiple levels.[36] He was initially assigned to the Angels' rookie-level Arizona Complex League (ACL) team on May 30, 2025, before being promoted to the Double-A Rocket City Trash Pandas on June 3, 2025.[1][36] This move provided Colás an opportunity to revive his career in a new system, leveraging his prior experience as a top international prospect.[37]

Playing style and evaluation

Strengths and weaknesses

Colás exhibits plus raw power, derived from impressive bat speed and physical strength in his 6-foot-1, 220-pound frame, enabling him to generate easy pull-side home runs and project for above-average future power output.[15][38][29] His quick left-handed swing allows for solid plate coverage and the ability to drive the ball to all fields, contributing to an average hit tool evaluation.[15][29] Additionally, he possesses a plus throwing arm suitable for right field, with above-average defensive potential when maintaining conditioning.[39] However, Colás's aggressive approach at the plate leads to below-average plate discipline, as evidenced by increasing chase rates on breaking balls low in the zone during his minor league progression and a career MLB strikeout rate exceeding 25% in limited action.[40][38] This swing-and-miss tendency, combined with fringy speed graded at 45, limits his on-base percentage and baserunning impact, while his fielding tool receives a below-average 45 grade, reflecting inconsistent range in the outfield.[15][39] In MLB play through 2024, these deficiencies manifested in a .223 batting average over 278 at-bats, underscoring challenges in translating minor league success against professional pitching.[36]

Scouting reports and projections

MLB Pipeline scouting grades for Colás in 2023 rated his hit tool at 55, power at 60, run at 45, arm at 65, field at 50, and overall future value at 55 on the 20-80 scale.[15] These evaluations highlighted his aggressive approach at the plate, combining a quick left-handed swing with significant strength to generate well-above-average raw power, primarily through pull-side home runs, though he demonstrated the ability to drive the ball to all fields.[15] In center field, evaluators noted average range and solid instincts, supported by a plus arm that deterred runners, positioning him as a viable defender in the position despite his 6-foot-1, 220-pound frame.[15] Earlier 2022 grades from the same source adjusted his hit tool slightly lower at 50 and run speed at 50, while maintaining the power, arm, field, and overall at consistent levels, reflecting observations from his time in Cuba and initial U.S. minor league play.[41] Baseball America assigned similar tool grades, with hit at 50, power at 60, speed at 45, fielding at 45, and arm at 60, emphasizing his potential as a corner outfielder with fringe-average center field capability.[39] Eyewitness accounts from Baseball Prospectus praised his bat speed and pure strength, noting easy extra-base power on contact and the ability to line drives to the opposite field, though his defense was deemed adequate but not elite, with routine plays executed reliably in center.[38] FanGraphs' 2023 prospect evaluation pegged Colás at 45 future value, projecting him as a right fielder with a 40-grade hit tool but carrying 60-grade power potential, based on his minor league performance including a .895 OPS across Double-A and Triple-A in 2022.[42] Pre-debut projections from ZiPS anticipated challenges with contact, forecasting a 26.8% strikeout rate and 5.1% walk rate in his 2023 rookie season, tempering expectations for immediate impact despite his tools.[43] Overall, scouts viewed him as a mid-rotation caliber everyday player with upside in power production, though hit tool limitations and swing-and-miss tendencies were flagged as risks to realizing fringe-star potential.[44]

Personal life and views

Family and relocation challenges

Colás defected from Cuba in January 2020, leaving his family behind amid the typical hardships faced by Cuban athletes seeking professional opportunities abroad, including severed personal ties and limited communication due to government restrictions.[2] His mother remained in Cuba, providing emotional support from afar, as evidenced by heartfelt phone calls where she expressed joy and tears upon learning of his milestones, such as making the Chicago White Sox's 2023 Opening Day roster.[45] [46] This separation underscored the personal sacrifices inherent in defection, with Colás later describing a sense of familial void during his early professional isolation, which he began to fill through bonds with Cuban teammates in the White Sox system.[9] A significant family-related challenge emerged from the disputed pre-defection agreement with Japan's Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks, signed in 2017 when Colás was 19. In July 2025, Colás publicly stated that Cuban authorities had forged his mother's signature on the contract documents without her knowledge or consent, rendering the deal invalid from his perspective and complicating his post-defection path to MLB free agency.[47] This revelation highlighted systemic interference by the Cuban government in athletes' international dealings, where officials often negotiate and endorse contracts on behalf of players and families to extract financial benefits, leading to prolonged legal and relocation uncertainties for defectors like Colás, who spent over a year in limbo before signing with the White Sox in January 2022.[8] Relocation to the United States amplified these strains, as Colás navigated cultural adaptation, language barriers, and the emotional weight of family distance while establishing residency and pursuing citizenship eligibility. Despite the hurdles, his family's encouragement persisted; he reunited with his mother in Miami in December 2022, marking a pivotal moment of reconnection after nearly three years apart.[48] This event, coupled with ongoing virtual support, reflected the resilience required for Cuban defectors to balance professional ambitions with familial bonds severed by political realities.[49]

Perspectives on Cuba and defection

Oscar Colás defected from Cuba in January 2020 at age 21, leaving the island to pursue a Major League Baseball contract after establishing residency in a third country and obtaining clearance from the U.S. Office of Foreign Assets Control.[2][16] This move followed his participation in Cuba's national team from ages 17 to 20 and three seasons in Japan's minor leagues under a developmental contract arranged by the Cuban government in 2017.[3] Colás has described the decision as high-risk, noting the uncertainty of entering free agency after departing both Cuba and Japan without assured outcomes, a path that demanded strong self-belief amid potential professional limbo.[50] A key element of Colás's defection narrative involves disputes over his prior commitments, particularly a 2017 agreement sending him to the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks, which required his mother's consent as he was underage. In a July 2025 interview, Colás alleged that Cuban authorities forged his mother's signature on the contract documents, claiming no detailed explanations were provided during the process and that he never consented to or abandoned a legitimate binding agreement.[47][8] He maintained that "Cuba signed in place of my mother," rendering the deal invalid from inception and complicating his release from the Hawks after defection, as the Japanese club initially asserted rights to his services.[51] This revelation underscores Colás's perspective on opaque handling of player contracts by Cuban officials, who profited from such arrangements while players faced limited autonomy and earnings under state control.[28] Colás's experiences highlight broader challenges for Cuban baseball talents seeking international opportunities, where defection often stems from aspirations for higher competition, financial independence, and freedom from government-mediated deals that retain a portion of earnings—typically 20-30% remitted to the state.[17] Unlike some defectors who publicly denounce the Cuban regime, Colás has focused interviews on personal and contractual hurdles rather than ideological critiques, emphasizing resilience in navigating bureaucratic and legal obstacles to reach MLB, which he achieved via a $2.7 million signing with the Chicago White Sox in January 2022.[52] His case illustrates causal factors in Cuban defections: systemic restrictions on player mobility and earnings incentives driving top prospects like Colás—dubbed the "Cuban Ohtani" for his two-way skills—to risk exile for professional advancement.[53]
