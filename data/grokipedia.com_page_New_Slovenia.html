New Slovenia


Nova Slovenija – Krščanski demokrati (New Slovenia – Christian Democrats; NSi) is a Christian democratic and conservative political party in Slovenia, founded on 4 August 2000 with roots in the country's Catholic and democratic traditions.[1][2] The party adheres to principles of social conservatism, family values, economic liberalism, and pro-European integration, positioning itself as centre-right within the Slovenian political spectrum.[3][1] NSi has participated in several coalition governments, including the 2020–2022 administration under Prime Minister Janez Janša, where former leader Matej Tonin served as Minister of Defence and Vice-President of the Government, overseeing military modernization and responses to regional security challenges.[4] In the 2022 parliamentary elections, the party obtained approximately 7% of the vote, securing eight seats in the 90-seat National Assembly and entering opposition.[5] As a member of the European People's Party (EPP), NSi advocates for Christian democratic policies at the European level, with Matej Tonin elected as a Member of the European Parliament in 2024.[6][7] In September 2025, Jernej Vrtovec, previously Minister of Infrastructure, was elected as NSi's new president, succeeding Matej Tonin amid the party's focus on upcoming 2026 parliamentary elections.[8] The party emphasizes ethical governance, support for small businesses, and protection of life and family, distinguishing itself through its commitment to Slovenia's Judeo-Christian heritage in a politically fragmented landscape.[9][10]

History

Founding and Initial Governments (2000–2004)

New Slovenia (Nova Slovenija, NSi) emerged from internal divisions within Slovenia's center-right political bloc in 2000. Andrej Bajuk, who had assumed the role of Prime Minister on 3 June 2000 leading a coalition comprising the Slovenian People's Party (SLS+SKD), the Democratic Party of Pensioners of Slovenia (DeSUS), and smaller parties, faced policy disagreements with key allies, particularly over economic reforms and party direction.[11] These tensions culminated in Bajuk and his supporters resigning from SLS+SKD to establish NSi on 4 August 2000 as a distinct Christian-democratic alternative emphasizing social conservatism and market-oriented policies.[12][13] The Bajuk government, Slovenia's third post-independence administration, prioritized fiscal stabilization and preparations for European integration amid economic recovery from the late-1990s slowdown, but lasted only until early November 2000 following a successful no-confidence vote that triggered snap elections.[14] In the 15 October 2000 parliamentary elections, NSi, contesting as a new entity under Bajuk's leadership, secured 8 seats in the 90-member National Assembly, reflecting voter support for its platform amid a fragmented center-right vote split by the recent schism.[15] The Liberal Democracy of Slovenia (LDS), led by Janez Drnovšek, formed the subsequent government with 34 seats, relegating NSi to the opposition alongside other conservative groups.[16] From late 2000 through 2004, NSi positioned itself as a principled opposition force, critiquing the LDS-led coalition's handling of privatization, public spending, and EU accession negotiations while advocating for stronger family policies and ethical governance rooted in Christian values.[12] Bajuk retained party leadership, using the parliamentary platform to build organizational infrastructure and alliances, though NSi refrained from major governmental roles during this period, focusing instead on consolidating its base ahead of the 2004 elections. The party's opposition stance highlighted contrasts with the ruling bloc's perceived liberal tendencies, drawing on empirical critiques of rising state intervention and corruption risks in state-owned enterprises.[13]

Coalition Participation and Opposition Dynamics (2004–2020)

Following the 2004 parliamentary election, in which New Slovenia (NSi) secured 9 seats with 9.09% of the vote, the party joined a centre-right coalition government led by Prime Minister Janez Janša of the Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS).[17][18] This coalition, comprising SDS, NSi, the Slovenian People's Party (SLS), and the Democratic Party of Pensioners of Slovenia (DeSUS), held power from December 2004 until September 2008, implementing reforms in areas such as labour market liberalisation and public administration efficiency amid Slovenia's EU accession integration.[18] NSi contributed key figures, including Andrej Bajuk as Minister of Finance, focusing on fiscal prudence and Christian democratic principles in policy execution.[18] In the 2008 election, NSi received 3.40% of the vote, falling below the 4% electoral threshold and losing all parliamentary representation.[19][20] This electoral setback relegated the party to extra-parliamentary opposition during the centre-left government of Borut Pahor (2008–2012), marked by economic recession and austerity measures that NSi criticised from outside the National Assembly for insufficient emphasis on family support and moral governance.[20] The party similarly failed to meet the threshold in the 2011 early election, obtaining approximately 3.4% and remaining sidelined amid the unstable Positive Slovenia-led coalition under Zoran Janković and subsequent Janez Janša minority government (2012–2013), where NSi provided no formal support but aligned rhetorically with conservative fiscal stances.[21] NSi regained parliamentary entry in the 2014 election with 5 seats (7.9% of the vote), positioning itself in opposition to the centre-left coalition under Miro Cerar (2014–2018), which it opposed on grounds of excessive state intervention and lax social policies.[22] The party maintained this oppositional role through the 2018 election, increasing to 7 seats (7.1% of the vote), critiquing Prime Minister Marjan Šarec's minority government (2018–2020) for fiscal expansionism and inadequate defence of traditional values amid coalition dependencies on left-leaning parties.[23] Throughout these periods, NSi's opposition dynamics emphasised principled stances on Christian ethics, economic liberalism, and EU-aligned conservatism, often collaborating informally with SDS on legislative blocks against progressive reforms, though without regaining executive roles until 2020.[24]

Government Role in the 2020–2022 Coalition

New Slovenia (NSi) joined the 14th Government of Slovenia as a junior coalition partner after Prime Minister Marjan Šarec's resignation on 27 January 2020, which triggered negotiations among parties in the National Assembly elected in 2018. NSi, holding seven seats, provided crucial support for Janez Janša's confirmation as prime minister on 3 March 2020 by a 52–38 vote, enabling a coalition with the Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS), Modern Centre Party (SMC), and Democratic Party of Pensioners of Slovenia (DeSUS). The government was sworn in on 13 March 2020, just as the COVID-19 pandemic escalated in Europe.[25][26] The party received two ministerial portfolios reflecting its priorities in security and development: Matej Tonin, NSi president at the time, served as Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Defence from 13 March 2020 to 1 June 2022, overseeing the Slovenian Armed Forces' deployment for civil protection tasks, including field hospitals, disinfection operations, and later vaccine transport logistics. Jernej Vrtovec held the Ministry of Infrastructure over the same period, directing investments in road and rail maintenance to sustain supply chains during lockdowns and advancing post-pandemic recovery plans like the €4.6 billion National Recovery and Resilience Plan submitted to the EU in 2021. These appointments positioned NSi to influence policy on national resilience and connectivity.[27][8][28] Throughout the term, NSi backed the coalition's emergency legislation, including seven intervention acts by mid-2021 that authorized €6.3 billion in direct fiscal support for wage subsidies, liquidity loans, and healthcare expansion to counter economic contraction of 4.3% GDP in 2020. The party emphasized proportionality in restrictions and ethical safeguards, aligning with its platform on subsidiarity and family-oriented aid, such as expanded child benefits and opposition to mandatory vaccinations without exemptions. Tensions arose over DeSUS's withdrawal of parliamentary support in May 2021, reducing the majority to 38 seats after the July 2020 elections redistributed mandates (NSi retained eight), yet NSi upheld stability amid opposition challenges and protests until the government's replacement by a five-party centre-left alliance on 1 June 2022 following the 24 April 2022 elections.[29][30]

Recent Developments and Opposition Status (2022–Present)

In the April 24, 2022, parliamentary elections, New Slovenia (NSi) obtained 8 seats in the 90-seat National Assembly, representing approximately 8.9% of the vote, a result that positioned the party in opposition to the new coalition government led by Prime Minister Robert Golob's Movement for Freedom.[31] This outcome followed NSi's prior role in the Janša coalition, marking a shift to legislative opposition where the party focused on critiquing government policies on economic recovery, defense, and family support.[32] During its opposition tenure from 2022 to 2025, NSi, alongside the Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS), actively proposed legislation, submitting over 30 bills on the first day of the new assembly to address issues such as fiscal stability and administrative reforms.[32] Under leader Matej Tonin, who had headed the party since 2018, NSi emphasized Christian democratic principles in parliamentary debates, opposing measures perceived as expanding state intervention in social spheres while advocating for market-oriented incentives. Tonin stepped down as party president in June 2025 after seven years, citing a desire for renewal ahead of the next national elections.[33] On September 13, 2025, NSi elected Jernej Vrtovec, a former infrastructure minister in the 2020–2022 government, as its new leader, signaling continuity in center-right positioning while preparing for potential coalition dynamics in future elections.[8] Vrtovec's selection, supported by a party congress vote, underscored NSi's strategy to leverage its opposition record on governance efficiency and European integration to contest the incumbent coalition's handling of inflation and public debt. As of October 2025, NSi maintains its 8 seats and opposition status, with no shifts in parliamentary arithmetic.[31]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Core Ideological Foundations

New Slovenia's core ideological foundations rest on Christian democracy, an approach that synthesizes ethical principles from Christian social teaching with democratic institutions and a commitment to the common good. The party, self-identifying as "krščanski demokrati" (Christian Democrats), prioritizes the inherent dignity of the human person as the basis for policy, advocating subsidiarity—decision-making at the most local level feasible—and solidarity to support societal cohesion and aid the vulnerable. This framework emerged from its 2000 founding by figures including Andrej Bajuk, who sought to revive Christian democratic traditions amid Slovenia's post-independence transitions, emphasizing moral renewal over ideological extremism.[2][3] Central to NSi's ideology is the family as the foundational social institution, with policies aimed at reinforcing traditional structures, parental rights, and protections for life from conception. Economically, it endorses a social market model that balances free enterprise, fiscal prudence, and welfare provisions to promote personal initiative while mitigating inequality, reflecting a rejection of both unchecked liberalism and state overreach. Politically, the party upholds the rule of law, anti-corruption measures, and parliamentary democracy as bulwarks against authoritarian legacies from Yugoslavia, while critiquing excessive centralization in EU affairs to preserve national sovereignty.[1][6] These principles align NSi with the European People's Party (EPP), positioning it as center-right within Slovenia's spectrum, distinct from more secular liberal or socialist alternatives. The ideology manifests in opposition to policies perceived as eroding ethical norms, such as expansive state interventions in moral domains, and in support for initiatives fostering civic virtue, education reform, and environmental stewardship grounded in responsible stewardship rather than alarmism. Empirical alignment is evident in its coalition roles, where it has pushed for balanced budgets and family subsidies, as during the 2020–2022 government.[3][8]

Social and Cultural Policies

New Slovenia (NSi) prioritizes the family as the foundational unit of society, advocating for policies that strengthen traditional family structures through financial incentives, parental leave expansions, and support for child-rearing. The party emphasizes Christian values in family policy, promoting measures to encourage marriage and childbirth while critiquing societal trends that undermine parental authority, such as certain educational curricula perceived as ideologically driven. In its platform, NSi supports tax benefits for families with children and opposes policies that dilute the nuclear family model in favor of alternative arrangements.[1] On life issues, NSi maintains a pro-life stance, opposing both abortion and euthanasia as incompatible with a "culture of life." During debates on Slovenia's 2025 assisted dying legislation, NSi lawmakers argued that the bill fosters a "culture of death," voting against its passage with 34 of 87 National Assembly members in opposition. The party's historical positions reflect resistance to expanding abortion access, viewing it as a moral and ethical concern rooted in Christian doctrine, though specific legislative proposals for restrictions have not advanced amid coalition compromises.[34] Regarding LGBT rights, NSi opposes same-sex adoption, maintaining that child welfare is best served by placement in traditional heterosexual families. In July 2022, following a Constitutional Court ruling deeming exclusions of same-sex couples from joint adoption discriminatory, NSi reiterated its determination to block such expansions, prioritizing biological parent-child bonds over legal equality arguments. The party supports civil unions for practical purposes but rejects redefining marriage to include same-sex partnerships, aligning with conservative European Christian democratic norms.[35] In cultural policy, NSi promotes the preservation of Slovenia's Christian heritage and national identity, advocating for public funding of religious education and cultural institutions that reflect traditional values. The party critiques state interventions in cultural spaces that prioritize progressive ideologies over empirical family outcomes or historical continuity, favoring policies that integrate faith-based perspectives into public life without mandating secular neutrality.[1]

Economic and Fiscal Policies

Nova Slovenija (NSi) advocates a social market economy model that balances market freedoms with social solidarity, emphasizing individual responsibility, private property rights, and support for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) as engines of growth. The party promotes competitiveness through innovation, reduced bureaucracy, and an open economy integrated within the European Union to foster job security and higher growth rates. It opposes excessive state intervention and regulation, favoring limited government involvement to encourage entrepreneurship and fair competition while maintaining strategic public ownership in critical sectors such as energy and transport.[36][37] On fiscal policy, NSi prioritizes efficient allocation of public resources, rational spending, and long-term predictability to avoid wasteful expenditure and ensure sustainable public finances. The party supports transparent funding mechanisms for local governments, including objective criteria for resource distribution to enhance regional autonomy and development. During its participation in the 2020–2022 coalition government, NSi backed expansionary measures to mitigate pandemic impacts, including social policy supports reaching 1.2 million citizens, while critiquing subsequent left-leaning administrations for short-sighted fiscal approaches that increase bureaucracy and strain taxpayer funds.[36][38] Tax policy under NSi focuses on simplicity, fairness, and progressivity aligned with ability to pay, with proposals to lower burdens on labor and investment to incentivize work, creativity, and economic participation. The party has championed reductions in administrative hurdles for businesses and efforts to combat evasion, aligning with its 2022–2026 program goals to stimulate growth through tax relief on wages and broader economic liberalization. In practice, NSi contributed to the 2021 labor tax shift reforms implemented during the Janša government, which reduced social security contributions and adjusted income tax brackets to ease the tax wedge, though specific attribution remains tied to coalition dynamics rather than unilateral party action.[36][38]

Foreign and European Policies

Nova Slovenija (NSi) positions itself as a proponent of Slovenia's active engagement within the European Union, viewing the bloc as a historic project of peace and the most successful political initiative of the 20th century, while advocating for a focus on strategic priorities such as strengthening the single market and reducing bureaucratic burdens by 25% to foster innovation and a social-market economy.[39] The party supports qualified majority voting in EU foreign and security policy to enable the Union to speak with one voice, but emphasizes preserving member state competencies in non-core areas to safeguard national sovereignty.[39] As a member of the European People's Party, NSi aligns with center-right pro-European conservatism, promoting digital advancements like a digital euro and e-residency alongside enhanced EU partnerships with the United States to advance freedom and democracy.[39][6] In defense and security matters, NSi regards NATO as the foundational pillar of Euro-Atlantic stability and insists on Slovenia meeting the alliance's 2% of GDP defense spending target, criticizing domestic shortfalls as catastrophic for military readiness and urging investments aligned with NATO guidelines, including 20% of the defense budget for equipment.[39][40] The party favors bolstering the European defense industry for greater self-sufficiency while maintaining transatlantic ties, and supports incremental increases in defense expenditures through measured fiscal approaches rather than abrupt hikes.[39][41] NSi's foreign policy emphasizes geopolitical enlargement of the EU to include the Western Balkans, Ukraine, and Moldova as a means to counter external influences and stabilize Europe's periphery, with Slovenia positioned as a bridge in these efforts.[39] On Russia, the party advocates applying "the only language Russia understands—strength," including sustained military and diplomatic pressure to support Ukraine's defense and reconstruction, consistent with broader Slovenian commitments under past NSi-influenced coalitions.[42] In migration-related foreign policy, NSi calls for fortified EU external borders, targeted attraction of skilled workers, designation of safe third countries to curb asylum misuse, and expedited returns, framing these as essential for orderly integration and security.[39]

Leadership and Internal Organization

Historical and Current Leadership

New Slovenia was founded on 4 August 2000 by Andrej Bajuk, who served as its first president from the party's inception until 2008. Bajuk, an economist who had earlier held the position of Minister of Finance, guided the nascent party to nine seats in the 2000 National Assembly election and briefly led a minority government as Prime Minister from 3 June to 8 November 2000. The party's exclusion from parliament following the 2008 election prompted Bajuk's resignation as leader, after which he largely withdrew from active politics until his death in 2011.[12] Ljudmila Novak assumed the presidency in 2008, maintaining the role for a decade until 2018. During her tenure, NSi regained parliamentary representation with eight seats in the 2011 election and joined the centre-right coalition government from 2012 to 2013. Novak secured re-election at party congresses in 2012 and 2016, emphasizing coalition stability and responsible governance amid economic challenges.[43][44] Matej Tonin succeeded Novak as president in 2018. A long-time party member and vice-president prior to his elevation, Tonin led NSi into the 2020–2022 coalition under Prime Minister Janez Janša, where he concurrently served as Minister of Defence, overseeing military modernization and NATO commitments. His leadership saw the party secure seven seats in the 2018 election and eight in 2022, though it shifted to opposition thereafter.[27] Jernej Vrtovec was elected president on 13 September 2025 at a party congress, following Tonin's announcement in June 2025 that he would not seek another term. Vrtovec, who had served as Minister of Infrastructure in the 2020–2022 government, focuses on revitalizing the party's conservative Christian-democratic base amid ongoing opposition status. His election was unopposed after minimal competition, with subsequent approval of his proposed executive team by the party's top body on 24 September 2025.[8][45][46]

Party Structure and Affiliated Groups

The highest authority in New Slovenia – Christian Democrats (NSi) is the Congress (Kongres), which convenes at least annually and holds electoral sessions every two years to elect the party president, six members of the executive board, five members each of the supervisory board and judicial body, and to approve amendments to the party statute or decisions on dissolution.[47] Between Congress sessions, the Council (Svet) functions as the primary decision-making body, establishing political guidelines, appointing three vice-presidents and the secretary-general, and overseeing broader strategic directions.[47] The president (Predsednik), elected by the Congress for a four-year term, represents the party externally and chairs the Executive Board (Izvršilni odbor), which handles operational management, including policy implementation and internal coordination; the board comprises the president, three vice-presidents, the secretary-general, six members elected by the Congress, and designated representatives from affiliated subgroups such as youth, women, and seniors.[47] The Supervisory Board (Nadzorni odbor) monitors financial and administrative compliance, while the Judicial Body (Razsodišče) adjudicates internal disputes, including membership expulsions requiring a two-thirds majority vote.[47] NSi maintains a hierarchical structure extending from national organs to regional boards (Regijski odbor) and municipal or local committees (Občinski and Krajevni odbor), facilitating grassroots involvement; membership is open to Slovenian citizens aged 15 or older who endorse the party's program and statute, with minors requiring parental consent.[47] Affiliated groups include Mlada Slovenija, the youth wing comprising members aged 15–32 focused on activism and policy advocacy for younger demographics; the Women's Union (Ženska zveza), addressing gender-specific issues; the Seniors' Union (Zveza seniorjev), representing older members' interests; and the Christian Democracy Club (Klub krščanske demokracije), a forum for former party officials and experts.[47][48] These entities provide representation on the Executive Board and contribute to specialized outreach, aligning with NSi's Christian democratic principles.[47]

Electoral Performance

National Assembly Elections

New Slovenia (NSi) first contested National Assembly elections in 2004, securing 9 seats with 9.1% of the vote as part of the centre-right coalition government under Janez Janša.[49] The party failed to enter parliament in 2008, receiving only 3.4% amid a fragmented vote and rising opposition to the incumbent coalition.[49] Subsequent elections saw modest recoveries, with NSi maintaining a presence as a smaller conservative force, often aligning with right-leaning coalitions; it held 7 seats from 2018 to 2022 while participating in the Janša minority government formed in 2020.[50] In 2022, NSi won 4 seats with 6.08% (72,482 votes) in a contest dominated by the anti-incumbent Freedom Movement.[51]

NSi's electoral base has drawn primarily from rural and Catholic voters, reflecting its Christian democratic roots, though it has struggled against larger parties like the Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS) on the right.[49] The 4% electoral threshold has posed risks, as evidenced by the 2008 wipeout following internal splits and voter shift to Positive Slovenia.[49] Post-2011 gains stabilized its representation, with peaks in 2018 tied to anti-corruption appeals amid economic recovery, but 2022 results reflected coalition fatigue under Janša's leadership.[50] Voter turnout in these proportional representation contests, using the d'Hondt method across eight constituencies plus two minority seats, averaged around 50-60%, influencing smaller parties' outcomes.[52]

European Parliament Elections

In the inaugural European Parliament election following Slovenia's EU accession, held on 13 June 2004, New Slovenia (NSi) achieved the highest vote share with 23.57% (102,753 votes), securing two of Slovenia's seven seats under the proportional representation system.[53] The elected representatives included Lojze Peterle, who served as an MEP until 2014.[54] NSi contested the 2009 election independently and received 12.68% of the vote (54,967 votes), but the threshold for seat allocation under d'Hondt method prevented it from gaining representation among Slovenia's eight seats.[55]

In the 2014 election on 25 May, NSi won one seat as part of the EPP group allocation, represented by Ljudmila Novak until 2019.[56] The party maintained its single seat in 2019 with 11.07% of the vote, again affiliated with the EPP.[57][58] NSi's performance declined to 7.68% in the 2024 election on 9 June, yet it secured one of Slovenia's expanded nine seats through proportional distribution.[59] Across these elections, NSi has consistently aligned with the European People's Party (EPP), emphasizing Christian democratic values in EU policy debates.[56][58]

Local and Other Elections

In the 2022 Slovenian local elections, conducted on 20 November (first round) and 4 December (second round) across 212 municipalities, New Slovenia (NSi) elected 16 mayors and secured 291 seats in municipal councils.[60] This outcome marked an improvement in NSi's local footprint, placing it third among parties by mayoral wins, behind the Slovenian People's Party (SLS) and Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS), while contributing to a broader pattern where non-partisan lists dominated overall candidacies.[61] Voter turnout remained low at approximately 47% in the first round, reflecting trends in municipal contests emphasizing local issues over national party affiliations.[62] Earlier local elections, such as those in 2018, saw NSi participate actively but with more modest results; SDS claimed the most council seats (around 400), while SLS led with 23 mayors, underscoring NSi's secondary role amid a surge in independent and group-backed candidates who captured over 940 council positions nationwide.[63] NSi's strength has historically concentrated in smaller municipalities and rural areas aligned with its Christian democratic values, though exact aggregates for prior cycles like 2014 or 2010 highlight variable gains tied to national momentum, without dominating urban centers.[64] Beyond municipal polls, NSi has engaged in other non-parliamentary contests, including presidential elections. In the 2022 presidential race on 23 October (first round), NSi candidate Janez Cigler Kralj received a limited vote share amid 51.74% turnout, failing to advance.[65] Similarly, in 2017, NSi-supported Ljudmila Novak polled under 2%, illustrating the party's niche appeal in direct executive races outside legislative frameworks.

Notable Achievements and Criticisms

Key Policy Accomplishments

During its participation in the 2004–2008 center-right coalition government, New Slovenia (NSi) held key portfolios including finance, labor, justice, and higher education, contributing to Slovenia's economic stabilization and eurozone entry. Finance Minister Andrej Bajuk, an NSi founder, oversaw the successful convergence criteria fulfillment, leading to Slovenia's adoption of the euro as its currency on January 1, 2007; this milestone earned Bajuk recognition from European institutions for advancing fiscal discipline and monetary integration.[66] In the short-lived 2000 Bajuk government, which preceded NSi's formal establishment but involved its precursors, reforms accelerated alignment with EU acquis communautaire, expedited denationalization processes to resolve post-communist property restitution delays, and initiated national strategies for economic and social development.[66] As part of the 2020–2022 coalition under Prime Minister Janez Janša, NSi ministers focused on crisis response and structural enhancements. Defense Minister Matej Tonin prioritized military modernization after years of underfunding, securing government commitments to raise defense expenditures to 2% of GDP by 2030 in line with NATO targets, including procurements to sustain the Slovenian Armed Forces' operational viability.[67] Labor Minister Janez Cigler Kralj advanced workplace safety and family reconciliation measures amid the COVID-19 pandemic, while Infrastructure Minister Jernej Vrtovec supported recovery projects emphasizing transport and digital connectivity.[66] These efforts aligned with NSi's emphasis on security, economic resilience, and Christian democratic values, though broader government outcomes were shared across coalition partners.

Major Controversies and Critiques

In March 2025, a preliminary police investigation was launched into four members of New Slovenia (NSi), including party leader Matej Tonin, for suspected abuse of office in their roles on the parliamentary Commission for the Oversight of Intelligence and Security Services (KNOVS). The probe centered on their alleged efforts to obtain confidential police data related to a corruption investigation at the state-owned motorway company DARS, where six individuals had been suspected of bribery and abuse of office since April 2024. NSi described the proceedings as an abuse by the police, asserting that the MPs had acted within their oversight mandate to monitor public interest matters. A formal criminal complaint was filed against the four—sitting MPs Jernej Vrtovec, Janez Cigler Kralj, Jožef Horvat, Janez Žakelj, with Tonin confirming his inclusion—on July 2, 2025, though the case remained preliminary without charges at that stage.[68][69] During the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, Tonin, then serving as Defence Minister in Janez Janša's coalition government, faced allegations of conflict of interest in personal protective equipment (PPE) procurement. His prior role as a director at Acron d.o.o., one of the firms awarded contracts worth millions for masks and other supplies, prompted scrutiny from the National Bureau of Investigation, which filed complaints suggesting undue influence. Tonin denied any intervention, emphasizing that he had resigned from Acron before the deals and requested review by the Commission for the Prevention of Corruption. State prosecutors rejected the complaints in May 2021, finding insufficient evidence of wrongdoing.[70][71] NSi has drawn internal and external critiques for policy shifts under Tonin's leadership, particularly a perceived softening of its anti-alliance stance toward the Slovenian Democratic Party (SDS) led by Janša. In June 2025, Tonin announced he would not seek re-election as party president following a congress decision to abandon the "no-Janša" rule, which he had championed to maintain NSi's independence; this volte-face was seen by some as pragmatic opportunism amid electoral pressures but eroded Tonin's authority, leading to Jernej Vrtovec's succession in September 2025 with 85% support. Critics from within conservative circles accused NSi of diluting its Christian democratic principles by entertaining coalitions with SDS despite past governance tensions.[72] As a center-right party rooted in Christian democracy, NSi has faced progressive critiques for opposing expansions of LGBTQ+ rights, including active resistance to the 2015 referendum legalizing same-sex marriage, which failed with 63% voting against amid conservative mobilization. The party has advocated for traditional family definitions and expressed reservations over the 2022 Constitutional Court ruling mandating marriage equality, arguing it bypassed democratic processes. Similar positions on restricting abortion access have drawn accusations of regressive social conservatism from left-leaning outlets, though NSi frames these as defenses of life and family values grounded in empirical demographic concerns like low birth rates.[73][74]
