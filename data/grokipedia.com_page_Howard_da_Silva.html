Howard da Silva


Howard da Silva (born Howard Silverblatt; May 4, 1909 – February 16, 1986) was an American actor, director, and singer whose career spanned stage, film, and television, marked by commanding performances in musicals and dramas.[1]
A graduate of Carnegie Institute of Technology, da Silva began as a steelworker before debuting on Broadway in the 1930s, where he originated the menacing role of Jud Fry in Rodgers and Hammerstein's Oklahoma! (1943), earning acclaim for his bass-baritone voice and physical presence.[2][3]
In Hollywood, he appeared in over 50 films, including supporting roles in The Lost Weekend (1945), for which the picture won Oscars, and The Blue Dahlia (1946), often portraying tough, authoritative characters.[4]
Da Silva's career halted in 1951 when he was blacklisted after refusing to answer questions before the House Un-American Activities Committee about his political affiliations, amid investigations into communist influence in the entertainment industry; he denied party membership but invoked the Fifth Amendment, leading to years of unemployment sustained by stage work in New York.[5][6]
He staged a comeback in the 1960s, notably as Dr. Benjamin Franklin in the Broadway and film versions of 1776 (1969), and contributed to anti-apartheid theater efforts.[1][3]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Family Background

Howard da Silva was born Howard Silverblatt on May 4, 1909, in Cleveland, Ohio.[7][8] His parents, Benjamin Silverblatt and Bertha (née Sen), were Jewish immigrants from Russia who spoke Yiddish at home.[9][10] Benjamin worked as a dress cutter, supporting the family in Cleveland's industrial environment.[9] The Silverblatts' immigrant roots reflected the broader wave of Eastern European Jewish migration to the United States in the early 20th century, seeking economic opportunities amid pogroms and restrictions in the Russian Empire.[3][11] Da Silva later adopted his stage name, drawing from Portuguese origins to suit his acting career, while retaining ties to his heritage.[6][12]

Training in Acting

Da Silva attended the Carnegie Institute of Technology (now Carnegie Mellon University) in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, where he studied acting while working in local steel mills to finance his education.[3][2][13] Upon graduating around 1928, he hitchhiked to New York City and joined Eva Le Gallienne's Civic Repertory Theatre as an apprentice, gaining practical experience through immersion in professional repertory productions.[14][2] This apprenticeship emphasized ensemble work and classical techniques under Le Gallienne's direction, marking his transition from academic training to hands-on stagecraft.[14] By late 1928, following his initial season with the company, Da Silva began securing small roles in off-Broadway venues, building on these foundational experiences.[14]

Pre-Blacklist Career

Stage Performances

Da Silva commenced his professional stage career in New York during the late 1920s, securing minor roles in revivals such as Romeo and Juliet (April 21, 1930), where he portrayed the Apothecary, and The Green Cockatoo (October 6, 1930) as Scaevola.[15] He continued with supporting parts in original plays like Alison's House (December 1, 1930) as Hodges and Siegfried (October 20, 1930) alternating between Mr. Patchkoffer and Schumann.[15] In the mid-1930s, Da Silva joined the ideologically left-leaning Group Theatre collective, which emphasized method acting and socially conscious drama.[4] His breakthrough came with Clifford Odets' Golden Boy (November 4, 1937 – May 1938), in which he played the promoter Lewis, contributing to the production's 250-performance run amid the company's focus on working-class themes.[16] That same season, he originated the role of labor organizer Larry Foreman in Marc Blitzstein's proletarian opera The Cradle Will Rock (January 3, 1938), a Federal Theatre Project production that defied government censorship by performing without orchestra or sets, actors rising from audience seats to deliver lines and music.[16] [1] Da Silva's versatility extended to historical drama in Robert E. Sherwood's Abe Lincoln in Illinois (October 15, 1938 – April 29, 1939), where he embodied Jack Armstrong, the brawny friend and wrestling rival of young Lincoln, in a role that highlighted his physical presence and vocal depth during the play's 472-show engagement.[16] He further collaborated with Orson Welles in the Mercury Theatre's adaptation of Richard Wright's Native Son (March 24, 1941 – May 24, 1941), taking a principal supporting role in the socially provocative staging that drew controversy for its unflinching portrayal of racial injustice.[17] During World War II, Da Silva achieved one of his most iconic stage portrayals as the menacing farmhand Jud Fry in Rodgers and Hammerstein's Oklahoma! (March 31, 1943 – May 29, 1948), originating the character in the revolutionary musical that integrated dance and integrated song into narrative, running for 2,212 performances and earning him acclaim for embodying Fry's obsessive menace.[7] Additional pre-blacklist appearances included revivals like Ben Jonson's Volpone and John Steinbeck's Burning Bright (October 18, 1950 – December 30, 1950), where his rugged authority suited the heavy, introspective lead.[13] These roles solidified his reputation as a commanding presence in both dramatic and musical theater, often in works aligned with progressive or labor-oriented narratives reflective of the era's theatrical currents.[14]

Film and Radio Roles

Da Silva began his film career with an uncredited role in the 1935 comedy Once in a Blue Moon.[3] Over the following decade, he appeared in approximately 38 films, frequently portraying tough, villainous, or authoritative figures in genres such as film noir and adventure dramas.[11] Notable early roles included a mutineer in The Sea Wolf (1941) and the character Lem in the biographical war film Sergeant York (1941).) In 1945, he earned an Academy Award nomination for Best Supporting Actor as Nat, the sympathetic bartender in Billy Wilder's The Lost Weekend, a drama depicting alcoholism starring Ray Milland.[1][13] The following year, da Silva received another Oscar nomination in the same category for his portrayal of the brutal ship's captain in Two Years Before the Mast, an adaptation of Richard Henry Dana's memoir about maritime abuse.[1][14] He continued with supporting parts such as the antagonist Garth in Cecil B. DeMille's historical epic Unconquered (1947), set during the French and Indian War, and roles in noir films like The Blue Dahlia (1946) and Border Incident (1949).[18] Parallel to his film work, da Silva leveraged his deep baritone voice for radio performances starting in the 1930s, contributing to anthology series that dramatized literature and original stories.[19] Appearances included episodes of The Magic Key of RCA (e.g., an adaptation of Abe Lincoln in Illinois in 1938) and Repertory of the Air (e.g., R.U.R. in 1937).[20] In the 1940s, he featured in prestigious programs such as Lux Radio Theatre and Suspense, including the 1947 episode "You Take Ballistics," where he played a determined detective pursuing a murderer.[19][21] These radio roles, often requiring vocal intensity for dramatic effect, complemented his stage background and helped establish his versatility before the blacklist curtailed Hollywood opportunities.[17]

Political Affiliations and Activities

Involvement with Communist Organizations

Howard da Silva was identified as a member of the Communist Party USA (CPUSA) during House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) hearings by witnesses including screenwriter Martin Berkeley, who testified in 1951 that da Silva had joined the party in the 1930s.[22] Other friendly witnesses, such as actor Robert Taylor in earlier testimony, corroborated associations with known CPUSA members and activities, though Taylor did not directly name da Silva as a member.[23] These identifications stemmed from defectors and former party affiliates providing detailed accounts of recruitment and unit meetings in Hollywood, amid a broader surge in CPUSA membership during the Great Depression and rise of fascism, when party rolls peaked at around 75,000 nationally.[24] Da Silva's involvement extended to several organizations later designated as CPUSA fronts by HUAC and Attorney General lists, including the National Council of the Arts, Sciences, and Professions (NCASP), for which he helped organize events in 1946 promoting progressive causes aligned with party lines.[25] He also participated in the Actors' Laboratory Theatre, a training group infiltrated by CPUSA cells that hosted political workshops and fundraisers for leftist initiatives, and attended gatherings with figures like Karen Morley and Herschel Bernardi, both named in party contexts.[26] When subpoenaed before HUAC on March 26, 1951, da Silva invoked the Fifth Amendment over 100 times, declining to affirm or refute his CPUSA membership or front affiliations, citing potential self-incrimination—a response typical of "unfriendly" witnesses refusing cooperation.[24] FBI investigations into his background, prompted by these testimonies, documented associations but noted no independently corroborated dues records or cards, relying instead on informant consistency across hearings.[6]

Association with Hollywood Leftist Groups

Howard da Silva was active in several Hollywood-based organizations during the 1930s and 1940s that promoted anti-fascist and progressive causes, many of which were later designated as communist fronts by federal investigations due to their ties to the Communist Party USA (CPUSA) and promotion of Soviet-aligned policies.[25] The Hollywood Anti-Nazi League (HANL), founded in 1936 as the largest such group in the entertainment industry, drew broad initial support against rising fascism but was infiltrated and directed by CPUSA members to advance party lines, including opposition to interventionism before World War II. Da Silva participated in HANL radio broadcasts, such as a 1930s interview advocating its agenda, contributing to efforts that boosted attendance and funding for aligned causes.[27][28] Da Silva's affiliations extended to the Hollywood Citizens Committee for the Arts, Sciences, and Professions (HICCASP), the local chapter of the national Independent Citizens' Committee of the Arts, Sciences, and Professions (ICCASP), which lobbied for New Deal extensions, civil rights, and anti-colonialism but was criticized for front activities like defending Soviet policies and opposing anti-communist measures.[29] HICCASP events and panels featured da Silva, aligning him with figures later identified as CPUSA members, and the group was listed among subversive organizations by the House Committee on Un-American Activities (HUAC) for its role in coordinating Hollywood support for CPUSA objectives.[25] He also appeared at fundraisers and events for the Joint Anti-Fascist Refugee Committee (JAFRC) and Veterans of the Abraham Lincoln Brigade (VALB), both cited as fronts aiding Spanish Civil War veterans with communist ties and disseminating propaganda.[30] These associations placed da Silva in HUAC's scrutiny; witnesses, including during 1951 hearings, identified him as a CPUSA member active in Hollywood cells since the 1930s, though he invoked the Fifth Amendment when subpoenaed on March 8, 1951, refusing to confirm or deny memberships.[25] Post-war publications like Red Channels (1950) listed him among 151 entertainment figures linked to over 50 fronts, based on attendance records and sponsorships, reflecting systemic CPUSA influence in Hollywood's leftist networks during the Popular Front era.[29] While some participants viewed these groups as humanitarian, HUAC evidence showed coordinated efforts to embed party ideology in cultural output, with da Silva's involvement exemplifying actors' roles in recruitment and propaganda amplification.[28]

HUAC Testimony and Blacklisting

1951 Hearings and Refusal to Cooperate

In March 1951, the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) conducted a series of hearings targeting alleged Communist infiltration in the motion picture industry, subpoenaing over two dozen Hollywood professionals as part of its broader mandate to expose subversive activities linked to the Soviet Union during the early Cold War. Actor Howard da Silva, known for roles in films such as The Lost Weekend (1945), was among those summoned for the second wave of such probes following the initial 1947 investigations. The hearings, held in Washington, D.C., under Chairman John S. Wood (D-GA), focused on witnesses' past affiliations with groups identified by the committee as Communist fronts, including the Actors Laboratory Theatre and the Hollywood Writers Mobilization.[31][32] Da Silva testified on March 21, 1951, classifying himself as an "unfriendly" witness by declining to affirm or deny prior Communist Party membership or to identify associates in leftist organizations. When interrogated about specific questions—such as whether he had ever been a member of the Communist Party or attended meetings of the Hollywood Independent Citizens Committee of the Arts, Sciences and Professions (a group previously cited by HUAC as Communist-influenced)—he invoked the First Amendment's protection of free speech and association alongside the Fifth Amendment's safeguard against self-incrimination. His response to a direct query on Party membership was: "I refuse to answer the question on the following basis: The first and fifth amendments and all of the Bill of Rights protect me from any inquisitorial procedure, and I may not be compelled to cooperate with this committee in producing evidence designed to incriminate me and to drive me from my profession as an actor. The historical origin of the fifth amendment is founded in the resistance of the people to attempts to prosecute and persecute individuals because of their political views."[31][3] This stance echoed refusals by other witnesses, including actors Larry Parks and Gale Sondergaard, amid committee evidence drawn from FBI reports and defectors documenting Party recruitment in entertainment unions.[32] Da Silva's testimony lasted approximately one hour, during which he offered limited cooperative statements on his general loyalty to the United States but steadfastly withheld details on political activities, arguing that compelled disclosure violated constitutional principles and risked professional ruin without due process. Committee members, including Representative Harold Velde (R-IL), pressed for names of co-members in groups like the Joint Anti-Fascist Refugee Committee, but da Silva maintained his silence, stating his belief that the proceedings targeted individuals for "political views" rather than criminal acts. Unlike "friendly" witnesses such as Ronald Reagan, who cooperated while denying Party ties, da Silva's non-cooperation aligned him with the tactic of amendment invocation, which HUAC viewed as evasion tantamount to concealment of subversive ties, though legal scholars later debated its constitutional validity in congressional probes absent criminal indictment.[31][33]

Immediate Professional Repercussions

Following his testimony before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) on March 21, 1951, in which da Silva invoked the Fifth Amendment more than 30 times to avoid answering questions about his political affiliations, he faced swift industry backlash.[31] RKO Pictures, the distributor of his recently completed Western film Slaughter Trail (filmed in 1951 under producer Irving Allen), ordered all of da Silva's scenes as lead character Captain Dempster reshot with Brian Donlevy to distance the project from his HUAC appearance, a decision reportedly influenced by RKO owner Howard Hughes.[34] This post-production erasure marked one of the most immediate and visible professional setbacks, as the film—originally featuring da Silva prominently—was released in January 1952 without his involvement, effectively nullifying his completed performance and compensation tied to it.[35] The reshooting of Slaughter Trail exemplified the rapid enforcement of the Hollywood blacklist against "unfriendly" witnesses like da Silva, who refused to confirm or deny Communist Party membership or name associates.[36] Studios, adhering to informal industry pledges post-1947 HUAC hearings and fearing further congressional scrutiny, ceased offering him contracts for film, radio, or television work. Da Silva received no further Hollywood employment offers in 1951 or 1952, severing his momentum from prior roles in major productions like The Lost Weekend (1945) and They Live by Night (1948).[14] This blacklist-induced isolation persisted, with his last credited film appearance before the testimony being Fourteen Hours (March 1951 release), after which he was excluded from the industry's primary revenue streams for over a decade.[13] Financially strained, da Silva resorted to manual labor, including construction work in New York, to support his family during the initial blacklist years, highlighting the blacklist's role as an economic penalty beyond mere professional exclusion.[1] While some blacklisted figures found sporadic outlets in independent theater, da Silva's immediate post-testimony prospects in legitimate stage work were limited by guilt-by-association stigma, delaying any viable alternatives until later in the decade.[37]

Post-Blacklist Professional Revival

Return to Theater

Following his blacklisting, Howard da Silva continued stage work to sustain his career, including the role of Mendele in the 1953 production The World of Sholem Aleichem.[14] He appeared on Broadway in 1957 as District Attorney Horn in Compulsion, a dramatization of the Leopold and Loeb case.[16] Da Silva's post-blacklist revival on stage gained momentum with his portrayal of Ben Marino in the musical Fiorello!, which opened on November 23, 1959, at the Broadhurst Theatre and ran for 795 performances.[15] For this role, he received a nomination for the 1960 Tony Award for Best Featured Actor in a Musical.[38] In 1969, da Silva achieved one of his most iconic stage performances as Dr. Benjamin Franklin in the original Broadway production of 1776, which premiered on March 16, 1969, at the 46th Street Theatre and enjoyed a run of 1,217 performances.[15] Critics praised his depiction for capturing Franklin's wit, humor, and pragmatic patriotism.[14]

Resurgence in Film and Television

Da Silva's film career revived in the early 1960s following the easing of the Hollywood blacklist, beginning with a supporting role as "Roger Corneal" in the ensemble heist comedy Ocean's 11 released on August 31, 1960.[39] He followed this with appearances in independent dramas such as David and Lisa (1962), where he played Dr. Swinford, and the political thriller The Manchurian Candidate (1962), portraying the senator John Iselin.[7] These roles marked his gradual reintegration into Hollywood, often as authoritative or paternal figures, contrasting his pre-blacklist portrayals of heavies.[11] A career highlight came in 1972 when da Silva reprised his Tony-nominated Broadway performance as Benjamin Franklin in the film adaptation of the musical 1776, directed by Peter H. Hunt and released on November 9, 1972; his depiction of the Founding Father emphasized Franklin's wit and pragmatism through songs like "The Egg" and "Cool, Cool, Considerate Men."[6] Later films included the Western Nevada Smith (1966) as preacher Hopestill, the Robert Redford-starring The Great Gatsby (1974) as Meyer Wolfsheim, and Mommie Dearest (1981) as MGM studio head Louis B. Mayer, a performance noted for its gruff intensity amid the biopic's camp elements.[39] His final screen roles encompassed Sharky's Machine (1981) and Garbo Talks (1984), with da Silva continuing character work into his mid-70s despite declining health.[40] In television, da Silva's resurgence paralleled his film work, with guest spots and made-for-TV films providing steady employment. He earned a Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Limited Series for portraying Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev in the docudrama The Missiles of October (1974), a CBS production depicting the Cuban Missile Crisis that aired on October 18, 1974.[41] Additional TV credits included episodes of Archie Bunker's Place and historical reenactments like Hollywood on Trial (1976), reflecting his versatility in both dramatic and comedic formats during the 1970s and 1980s.[39] This phase of his career, spanning over two decades, demonstrated resilience, with da Silva accumulating more than a dozen credits amid an industry shifting toward television dominance.[7]

Personal Life

Marriages and Family

Howard da Silva was married four times. His first marriage was to Evelyn Horowitz on August 13, 1930, in Manhattan, New York City; the union ended in divorce, with no children reported from it.[9] His second wife was actress Jane Louise Taylor, whom he married in January 1941 in Yuma, Arizona; they had one son and divorced in July 1948.[10] Da Silva's third marriage, to actress Marjorie Nelson, took place on August 17, 1950, and ended in divorce on May 9, 1961; they had two children together.[6] His fourth and final marriage was to actress Nancy Nutter on June 30, 1961; it lasted until his death in 1986 and produced three children.[6][42] In total, da Silva had five children: sons Peter and Daniel, and daughters Rachel, Judith, and Margaret.[14][1]

Health Issues and Death

Howard da Silva died of lymphoma on February 16, 1986, at his home in Ossining, New York, at the age of 76.[14][43][13] No prior chronic health conditions or extended illness were publicly detailed in contemporary accounts of his final years.[14][37]

Legacy and Assessment

Artistic Contributions and Critical Reception

Howard da Silva made significant contributions to American theater through originating key roles in landmark productions. He portrayed Larry Foreman in Marc Blitzstein's The Cradle Will Rock in 1937, a pro-labor opera that faced controversy for its political content during its Federal Theatre Project premiere.[44] In 1938, da Silva created the role of Jack Armstrong in Robert E. Sherwood's Abe Lincoln in Illinois, which he reprised in the 1940 film adaptation directed by John Cromwell. His performance as the menacing Jud Fry in Rodgers and Hammerstein's Oklahoma!, which opened on March 31, 1943, showcased his ability to embody complex, brooding characters, contributing to the musical's groundbreaking integration of plot and song. Later, da Silva earned a Tony Award nomination for Best Featured Actor in a Musical for his role as Ben Marino in Fiorello! in 1959.[37] In film, da Silva's pre-blacklist work included supporting roles that highlighted his gravelly voice and imposing presence, such as Nat the bartender in Billy Wilder's The Lost Weekend (1945), a film that won four Academy Awards, including Best Picture, for its unflinching depiction of alcoholism. After his 1951 blacklist, he returned to cinema with a Golden Globe-nominated performance as Dr. Swinford in Frank Perry's David and Lisa (1962), praised for its sensitive handling of mental health themes. He reprised Benjamin Franklin in the 1972 screen adaptation of 1776, directed by Peter H. Hunt, where his witty and authoritative portrayal anchored the historical musical.[3][45] Critics and contemporaries acclaimed da Silva for his versatility and depth, often casting him as rough-hewn yet intelligent figures infused with humanity. Theater historian Trav S.D. noted that da Silva invested ruthless characters with "brains and heart," distinguishing his portrayals in both stage and screen works. His embodiment of Benjamin Franklin in the original Broadway 1776 (opening March 16, 1969) was particularly lauded for capturing the Founding Father's pompous wit and courage, a role he originated and sustained through 1,217 performances. Despite career interruptions, da Silva's post-revival output in theater, film, and television underscored his enduring reputation as a commanding character actor.[4][11]

Debates on Political Stance and Blacklist Impact

Howard da Silva's political activities in the 1930s and 1940s included participation in left-wing theater groups such as the Group Theatre and affiliations with organizations later identified as Communist Party fronts, including sponsorship of events tied to the Communist Party USA.[25] These involvements drew scrutiny during the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) investigations into alleged communist influence in Hollywood.[22] On March 21, 1951, da Silva appeared before HUAC, where he refused to answer questions about his political associations or those of others, citing the First and Fifth Amendments to the U.S. Constitution.[31] This non-cooperation led to his inclusion on the Hollywood blacklist, enforced by studios and producers wary of government reprisal and public backlash against perceived subversives.[46] Informants such as writer Martin Berkeley had previously named him as a Communist Party member during earlier HUAC sessions, prompting FBI investigation, though da Silva never admitted membership.[47] Debates over da Silva's political stance center on the extent of his Communist Party involvement versus mere sympathy for progressive causes. Critics from anti-communist perspectives argue that his refusal to testify indicated evasion of confirmed ties, substantiated by party recruitment patterns in Hollywood's left-leaning circles and declassified evidence of Soviet-directed networks in the U.S. entertainment industry during the era.[22] Supporters, often from academic and media sources aligned with civil liberties advocacy, portray his stance as principled resistance to governmental overreach, emphasizing the lack of public admission or criminal conviction and framing HUAC inquiries as ideologically driven harassment rather than security measures against foreign influence.[48] Such views frequently downplay the causal link between CPUSA membership—known to require oaths of allegiance to Soviet policy—and potential risks to national security, prioritizing free speech protections over empirical assessments of espionage threats documented in post-war revelations. The blacklist's impact on da Silva's career sparked further contention, with estimates placing his effective exclusion from major film and television work from 1951 to approximately 1961.[46] Proponents of the blacklist contend it was a proportionate industry response to self-preservation, as cooperating witnesses like Larry Parks faced reputational damage but retained opportunities, while non-cooperators like da Silva bore consequences aligned with their choices amid a verified communist apparatus in cultural institutions.[49] Opponents highlight the blacklist's chilling effect on dissent, arguing it unjustly prolonged da Silva's hiatus despite his pivot to regional theater and voice work, which sustained him until roles in films like David and Lisa (1963) marked his resurgence.[50] Empirical data on blacklistees shows varied outcomes—da Silva's eventual return to prominence, including his Tony-nominated portrayal of Benjamin Franklin in 1776 (1969), suggests the sanction was not permanently career-ending but did enforce a decade-long demotion from high-profile projects.[11] These debates persist in assessments of whether the blacklist mitigated genuine ideological subversion or exemplified causal overreaction, with source credibility often reflecting institutional biases: mainstream narratives in post-1960s academia tend to emphasize victimhood, while primary HUAC records and defector accounts underscore the targeted risks.[48]

Credits

Stage Roles

Howard da Silva began his Broadway career in the early 1930s with minor roles in revivals such as Romeo and Juliet (1930, as Apothecary) and The Cherry Orchard (1933, as Stationmaster).[15] He gained prominence in the late 1930s, originating roles in labor-themed works like Sailors of Cattaro (1934–1935, as Sepp Kriz) and Black Pit (1935, as Hansy McCulloh).[15] In musical theater, da Silva originated Larry Foreman in Marc Blitzstein's pro-union opera The Cradle Will Rock (1937–1938), performed without sets or orchestra due to Federal Theatre Project controversies.[15] [1] He also played Lewis in Clifford Odets's Golden Boy (1937–1938) and Jack Armstrong in Robert E. Sherwood's Abe Lincoln in Illinois (1938–1939).[15] His most acclaimed early musical role was Jud Fry in Rodgers and Hammerstein's Oklahoma! (1943–1948), the brooding farmhand whose obsessive menace contrasted the show's optimism.[15] [14] Following a hiatus due to the Hollywood blacklist, da Silva returned to Broadway in 1956 with Diary of a Scoundrel (as Neel Fedoseitch Mamaev).[15] He earned a Tony Award nomination for Ben Marino in the musical Fiorello! (1959–1961), portraying a tough political operative in the biopic of New York mayor Fiorello La Guardia.[15] [3] Later credits included the Archbishop in The Unknown Soldier and His Wife (1967) and his signature portrayal of Benjamin Franklin in 1776 (1969–1972), which he reprised in the 1972 film adaptation.[15] [14]

Film Roles

Howard da Silva appeared in over 20 feature films from 1939 to 1984, primarily in supporting roles as rugged or villainous characters, with his Hollywood work largely halted during the early 1950s due to blacklisting following House Un-American Activities Committee testimony.[7] His early film roles established him as a reliable character actor in noir and adventure genres.
  • The Sea Wolf (1941) as George Leach, a leading mutineer aboard a brutal ship commanded by Edward G. Robinson's captain.[51]
  • Sergeant York (1941) as a military figure in the biographical war film starring Gary Cooper.[52]
  • The Big Shot (1942) as Wendel 'Red' Ferrill, in a crime drama with Humphrey Bogart.[7]
  • The Lost Weekend (1945) as Nat, the sympathetic bartender to Ray Milland's alcoholic protagonist.
  • The Blue Dahlia (1946) as Eddie Harwood, a shady nightclub owner in the film noir mystery.
  • Unconquered (1947) as John Gardo, in Cecil B. DeMille's historical adventure with Paulette Goddard and Gary Cooper.[53]
  • They Live by Night (1948) as Mobley, in the influential crime film directed by Nicholas Ray.[54]
  • Border Incident (1949) as Bruno, in the film noir about human smuggling.[55]
  • The Outrage (1964) as the Prospector, a remake of Rashomon with Paul Newman.[56]
  • Nevada Smith (1966) as the Warden, in the Western starring Steve McQueen.[56]
  • 1776 (1972) as Dr. Benjamin Franklin, reprising his stage role in the musical film about the Declaration of Independence.[57]
  • The Great Gatsby (1974) as Meyer Wolfsheim, the gangster associate in the adaptation starring Robert Redford.[58]
  • Mommie Dearest (1981) as Louis B. Mayer, portraying the MGM studio head in the biographical drama.[39]
Post-blacklist, his roles often drew on his theater background, emphasizing authoritative or historical figures, as seen in the resurgence of work in the 1970s.[7]

Television and Radio Appearances

Howard da Silva's television appearances were sparse, largely due to his Hollywood blacklist from 1951 to the mid-1960s, but he resumed work in anthology series and guest spots thereafter. In the early 1960s, he portrayed Arnstein in an episode of Insight (1960) and Arnold Fermuller in The Defenders (1961). He also appeared in Ben Casey (1961).[59] Later, in 1983, da Silva guest-starred as Abe Rabinowitz in Archie Bunker's Place and as General Breznin in an episode of Masquerade. He further featured as a critic in American Playhouse.[60][61] On radio, da Silva was prominent in the medium both pre- and post-blacklist. During the 1940s, he performed in suspense dramas, including the role of a detective in "You Take Ballistics" on Suspense (March 13, 1947). In the 1970s, following his stage resurgence, he voiced characters in 26 episodes of CBS Radio Mystery Theater, a revival series hosted by E.G. Marshall that aired from 1974 to 1982; notable installments include "Come Back With Me" (1975), "The Coffin With the Golden Nails" (1975), "The Only Blood" (1976), and "Hung Jury" (1975).[21][19]
