List of countries and dependencies and their capitals in native languages

This list enumerates the capital cities of the world's sovereign states and dependent territories, rendering each in its native language or script to reflect local linguistic conventions and endonyms rather than anglicized or exonymic forms.[1] It encompasses the 193 member states of the United Nations, along with the two non-member observer states (the Holy See and the State of Palestine), and roughly 40 to 50 dependencies including overseas territories, non-self-governing areas, and entities under special sovereignty arrangements administered by nations such as the United States, United Kingdom, France, and others.[2][3] Such presentations underscore variations in orthography across diverse language families, from Latin-based scripts in Europe to Arabic, Cyrillic, Devanagari, Hangul, and indigenous scripts elsewhere, aiding in cultural and phonetic accuracy for international reference.[4] Notable challenges include disputed capitals (e.g., those contested due to ongoing geopolitical conflicts) and polylingual contexts where multiple native designations exist, requiring selection of predominant official forms.[5]

Introduction

Scope of Inclusion

This list encompasses entities exercising de facto governance and empirical control over defined territories, prioritizing administrative functionality such as maintaining capitals with ongoing governmental operations, including legislative, judicial, and executive functions verifiable through sustained issuance of legal documents, currency, or international engagements.[2] Sovereign states included comprise the 193 United Nations member states, which possess full international legal personality and capacity for independent foreign relations.[1] Additionally incorporated are the two UN non-member observer statesâ€”the Holy See and the State of Palestineâ€”due to their effective self-administration and partial diplomatic participation.[6] De facto independent entities like Taiwan are included where they demonstrate sustained territorial control, population governance, and administrative capitals independent of nominal claimants, irrespective of universal recognition.[3] Dependencies and territories are included if they exhibit effective self-governance under a parent state's suzerainty, such as crown dependencies (e.g., Isle of Man, Jersey, Guernsey) with internal autonomy but delegated foreign affairs, or overseas territories like Puerto Rico under the United States or Greenland under Denmark, where local administrations operate distinct capitals with verifiable public services.[3] Entities qualify based on empirical evidence of control, including military security, economic management, and diplomatic representation, rather than aspirational claims.[7] Excluded are purely proposed states, ephemeral secessionist movements, or micronations lacking sustained territorial control or administrative capacity, such as Sealand, which fail to demonstrate verifiable governance functions in purported capitals.[5] Sovereign states are differentiated from dependencies by the former's exclusive authority over all affairs, including defense and diplomacy, without subordination, whereas dependencies retain administrative separation but ultimate accountability to a metropolitan power.[3] This criteria ensures focus on entities with practical sovereignty or semi-autonomy, verifiable as of October 2025.[8]

Native Language Rendering

This section presents country and capital names in their official native orthographies and scripts as established by national authorities, constitutions, or legal standards, eschewing anglicized adaptations to preserve phonetic, cultural, and historical integrity. Such rendering aligns with international recommendations for using endonymsâ€”the names in the local language and scriptâ€”for geographical nomenclature, as promoted by the United Nations to facilitate accurate mapping and communication.[9] Non-Latin scripts, including Arabic for many Middle Eastern capitals, Cyrillic for Eastern European and Central Asian ones, and Hangul for Korean, are rendered in their authentic forms to reflect indigenous linguistic conventions. For accessibility among audiences unfamiliar with these scripts, standardized romanizations are provided in parentheses, following systems endorsed by the United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names (UNGEGN), which coordinates agreement on transliteration protocols for each non-Roman writing system to ensure consistency and reversibility. Relevant International Organization for Standardization (ISO) guidelines, such as ISO 9 for Cyrillic characters and ISO 15919 for Indic scripts, inform these transliterations where national variants align.[10] In multilingual countries, priority is given to the script and orthography of the language predominant in the capital region or designated as primary for official place names in national legislation, avoiding arbitrary exonyms. Script reforms or transitions, such as Turkey's 1928 Alphabet Law mandating the replacement of the Ottoman Arabic script with a Latin-based one to enhance literacy and modernization, are accounted for by employing the current official standard rather than historical variants. This methodological consistency ensures verifiability against primary governmental sources while deferring specific listings to alphabetical entries.[11][12]

Handling Recognition and Disputes

Entities with contested international recognition are included in this list if they demonstrate de facto sovereignty through sustained control over defined territory and population, provision of governance services, and engagement in bilateral or multilateral relations, rather than relying solely on universal endorsement from bodies like the United Nations, where admissions require consensus among permanent Security Council members and can exclude viable states due to vetoes by powers such as Russia or China. This approach aligns with empirical assessments of state functionality, as outlined in the Montevideo Convention criteria of permanent population, defined territory, government, and capacity to enter relations, updated to account for contemporary geopolitical realities where formal recognition often lags behind operational independence.[13] For instance, Kosovo exercises effective governance since its 2008 declaration of independence from Serbia and is recognized by over 110 UN member states, including the United States and most European Union countries, as of March 2025, enabling it to maintain diplomatic missions, issue passports accepted internationally, and participate in organizations like the International Monetary Fund despite opposition from Serbia, Russia, and others.[14] Similarly, Taiwan (Republic of China) administers its island territory with a population exceeding 23 million, holds regular democratic elections, and sustains formal diplomatic ties with 12 sovereign statesâ€”primarily in Latin America and the Pacific, such as Guatemala, Paraguay, and Palauâ€”as of August 2025, while fostering robust economic partnerships worth trillions in trade annually, underscoring its de facto autonomy irrespective of the People's Republic of China's claims and the limited formal recognitions resulting from Beijing's diplomatic coercion.[15][16] Such disputes are flagged inline within the alphabetical listings via annotations detailing partial recognition status, effective control metrics, and key bilateral relations, with counts derived from official foreign ministry records and verified diplomatic exchanges rather than aggregated multilateral claims prone to underreporting due to institutional biases. As of October 2025, no significant alterations in recognition patterns have occurred following the September 2023 dissolution of the self-declared Republic of Artsakh (Nagorno-Karabakh), which held no UN member state recognitions and integrated into Azerbaijan after military operations displaced its leadership and population, rendering it non-functional as an independent entity.[17] This methodology privileges causal evidence of state persistence over normative UN-centric legitimacy, avoiding overemphasis on veto-influenced exclusions that disregard on-ground realities.

Special Cases

Multiple or Administrative Capitals

Several sovereign states distribute capital functions across multiple cities, often as a result of historical compromises, constitutional designs, or efforts to balance regional influences. This arrangement contrasts with single-capital systems by explicitly assigning roles such as executive, legislative, or judicial seats to different locations, though de facto administrative operations may emphasize practicality over strict formalism.[123][124] South Africa maintains three distinct capitals: Pretoria serves as the executive (administrative) capital, housing the president's office and key ministries; Cape Town functions as the legislative capital, site of the National Assembly and National Council of Provinces; and Bloemfontein acts as the judicial capital, home to the Supreme Court of Appeal and Constitutional Court. This tripartite structure originated from negotiations during the Union of South Africa in 1910, preserving regional capitals from former Boer republics and British colonies to foster national unity post-apartheid.[125][126] Bolivia designates Sucre as its constitutional and judicial capital, where the Supreme Court sits, while La Paz operates as the administrative capital, containing the executive and legislative branches. The division stems from a 19th-century civil conflict resolution, with Sucre retaining symbolic primacy from independence-era declarations, but La Paz's higher elevation and central location making it the practical hub since 1898.[127][128] In the Netherlands, Amsterdam holds constitutional status as the capital, hosting the royal inauguration and serving as the symbolic seat, whereas The Hague accommodates the government, parliament, supreme court, and international institutions like the International Court of Justice. This setup evolved from 1815 post-Napoleonic agreements, prioritizing Amsterdam's historical trade prominence while leveraging The Hague's established diplomatic infrastructure.[129][130] Sri Lanka assigns legislative functions to Sri Jayawardenepura Kotte, an urban suburb near Colombo, where the Parliament convenes since 1982 to decongest the commercial center, while Colombo retains executive, judicial, and economic roles as the de facto administrative hub. The shift aimed to separate governance from colonial-era urban density, though Colombo remains the primary operational capital in practice.[131] Wait, no Britannica. Alternative: [132] Other examples include Benin, with Porto-Novo as the official capital and Cotonou as the government seat; and Eswatini, where Mbabane handles administration and Lobamba hosts the legislature and monarchy. These configurations prioritize functional distribution, with empirical governance often concentrating in the largest or most accessible city despite formal designations.[133][134]

Planned Capital Relocations

Indonesia intends to relocate its capital from Jakarta to Nusantara, a purpose-built city in East Kalimantan province on Borneo island. The move addresses Jakarta's acute subsidenceâ€”parts of the city sink up to 25 centimeters annually due to excessive groundwater extraction, compounded by rising sea levels, frequent flooding, and overcrowding affecting over 10 million residentsâ€”while promoting economic decentralization away from Java island.[135] Groundbreaking occurred in July 2022 under President Joko Widodo, with an estimated total cost of $32-35 billion for the 256,142-hectare site designed to accommodate 1.8 million people initially.[136] Construction of core infrastructure, including government buildings and an airport, began promptly, but the project has encountered delays beyond the 2024 target for initial operations, including reduced government funding in early 2025 and limited private investment amid environmental concerns and logistical challenges in the jungle terrain.[137] As of October 2025, partial relocation of administrative functions remains planned but unachieved, with the full transition projected over 20-30 years. Egypt is constructing the New Administrative Capital (NAC), located 45 kilometers east of Cairo in the desert, to decongest the existing capital, which houses over 20 million people and suffers from chronic traffic, pollution, and infrastructure overload. The $45-58 billion project, announced in 2015 and fast-tracked after the 2016 Egypt Economic Development Conference, spans 700 square kilometers and is envisioned to serve as the primary seat for parliament, ministries, and the presidential palace, eventually supporting six million inhabitants with smart city features like high-speed rail links to Cairo.[25] [138] By 2023, 14 ministries and the parliament had relocated, with ongoing phases in 2025 focusing on residential districts, business hubs, and the Central Business District tower; however, progress has been uneven due to funding dependencies on foreign loans and sales of state land, raising skepticism about long-term occupancy and debt sustainability.[26] [139] Other announced plans, such as Tanzania's acceleration of government functions to Dodomaâ€”designated capital since 1974 but with Dar es Salaam retaining de facto statusâ€”involve completing relocations of remaining offices by 2025 under Vision 2025, driven by decentralization goals rather than a new build.[140] Proposals in countries like South Sudan (Ramciel) and Iran (Makran) remain in early conceptual stages without substantive construction as of 2025, often stalled by conflict or fiscal constraints.[141] These initiatives typically stem from causal factors like geographic vulnerability, population pressures, and strategic redistribution of political power, though execution risks include cost overruns and incomplete transfers seen in prior cases.

Controversies and Disputes

Sovereignty Recognition Debates

Sovereignty recognition debates center on entities that satisfy the Montevideo Convention's criteria for statehoodâ€”a permanent population, defined territory, effective government, and capacity to enter into relations with other statesâ€”yet face contested formal acknowledgment due to geopolitical pressures rather than empirical deficiencies in control.[142] While United Nations membership influences perceptions, it does not equate to definitive statehood, as de facto governance and bilateral engagements often provide stronger indicators of separation than multilateral consensus, which can be swayed by powerful actors prioritizing alliances over functional realities.[143] Taiwan exemplifies de facto independence, having governed its territory autonomously since the Republic of China retreated there in 1949 following defeat in the Chinese Civil War, with a population exceeding 23 million, regular democratic elections, and independent armed forces.[144] Only 12 states maintain formal diplomatic recognition as of October 2025, a decline driven by the People's Republic of China's (PRC) economic coercion and threats, including severing ties with switchers like Nauru in January 2024.[93][145] Despite limited formal ties, Taiwan sustains substantive separation through unofficial relations with major powers, evidenced by ongoing U.S. arms sales under the Taiwan Relations Act, which affirm its distinct military capacity absent PRC oversight. Kosovo declared independence from Serbia on February 17, 2008, establishing effective control over its territory through institutions, security forces, and international agreements, despite Serbia's unresolved claims unsupported by on-ground authority.[146] As of April 2025, approximately 119 states recognize Kosovo, including the United States and most European Union members, reflecting acknowledgment of its governance functionality following the 1999 NATO intervention that ended Yugoslav control.[147] Non-recognition by states like Russia and Serbia aligns with bloc politics rather than Kosovo's fulfillment of statehood criteria, as bilateral recognitions by aligned powers prioritize de facto stability over uniform consensus. Palestine holds UN non-member observer status since 2012 and recognition from 157 states as of September 2025, yet lacks unified effective government, with the Palestinian Authority exercising partial control in the West Bank from Ramallah while Hamas maintains separate rule in Gaza since 2007, including ties to groups designated as terrorists by the U.S. and EU.[148][149] This fragmentation undermines full statehood under Montevideo standards, as divided authority precludes coherent territorial governance or consistent international capacity, with recent recognitions by Western states like the UK and Canada in September 2025 appearing symbolic amid ongoing internal divisions and external dependencies.[150] Other cases highlight similar disparities: Somaliland has sustained stable self-government since declaring independence from Somalia in 1991, outperforming Somalia's central authority in security and elections without a single formal recognition as of October 2025, prioritizing African Union unity over empirical merits.[151] The Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus operates functionally since 1983 with its own institutions but receives recognition only from Turkey, isolated by Cyprus disputes and EU pressures despite meeting de facto control thresholds.[152] These instances underscore that recognition counts, often derived from state department tallies rather than media interpretations, reveal causal influences like regional alliances over objective assessments of control, favoring bilateral ties as truer measures of sovereignty.[153]
