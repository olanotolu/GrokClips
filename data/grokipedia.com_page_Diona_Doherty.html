Diona Doherty

Diona Doherty (born 28 March 1989) is a Northern Irish comedian, actress, writer, and producer from Derry.[1] She rose to prominence through her starring role as Tracy Jones in the BBC Northern Ireland mockumentary series Soft Border Patrol (2018–present), which satirizes post-Brexit border dynamics.[1] Doherty has appeared as Katya, a Ukrainian student, in the Channel 4 series Derry Girls and as Detective Inspector Louise Carrigan in the BBC police drama Blue Lights, including its third season aired in 2025.[2][1] In stand-up comedy, she reached the Northern Ireland final of the BBC New Comedy Awards and has toured with shows such as Get Your Pink Back, achieving sold-out runs at the Edinburgh Fringe Festival in 2025.[3][4] As a writer and producer, Doherty created and staged theatre productions like Bridesmaids of Northern Ireland and co-wrote annual Christmas comedies for the Grand Opera House in Belfast.[5] She serves as a regular panellist on BBC Northern Ireland's The Blame Game and hosts the podcast Girls Groupchat.[5]

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Diona Doherty was born on 28 March 1989 in Derry, Northern Ireland.[1] She was raised in the city alongside her parents and brother Darren.[6] Doherty has described her upbringing as consisting of a nuclear family unit, with her and Darren separated by just two years in age, fostering a close sibling relationship.[6] She characterized the childhood as very normal, emphasizing the absence of major tragedies such as illnesses or deaths within the immediate family, which contributed to strong familial bonds.[6]

Education and initial career aspirations

Doherty attended Thornhill College in Derry, where she excelled academically, earning A*s in her GCSEs and As in her A-Levels.[6][7] Despite her teachers envisioning a career in law due to her strong performance, she was drawn to performing arts, inspired in part by the film Legally Blonde.[7] From a young age, Doherty recognized her performative talents, often singing at family gatherings, such as renditions of songs from Sister, Sister around age eight or nine.[6] Her initial aspirations centered on entertainment, including ambitions to become a pop star—she auditioned for RTÉ's You're a Star at age 13, falsifying her age to participate—and attempts to pursue roles as a television presenter or model.[6][8] By her mid-teens, she shifted focus toward acting, a direction encouraged by her father, who advised against law in favor of drama studies.[6] Following secondary school, Doherty enrolled in a drama degree at Queen's University Belfast, where she began honing her skills through writing and performing sketches with peers.[7][8] During her studies, she co-founded a comedy troupe, producing live sketches that marked her early foray into structured performance and comedy, aligning with her evolving interest in acting and humorous content creation.[8] This university experience solidified her commitment to a career in entertainment, distinguishing it from her prior exploratory pursuits.[7]

Professional career

Early acting and entertainment entry

Doherty's entry into acting occurred during her university years at Queen's University Belfast, where she secured a recurring role as Pam in three episodes of the BBC Northern Ireland sketch comedy series Sketchy with Diarmuid Corr in 2011.[9][10] This debut provided her initial television exposure, blending stand-up elements with character sketches hosted by comedian Diarmuid Corr.[10] Concurrently, she began collaborating with friends on original sketches, marking her early involvement in writing alongside performance.[7] Transitioning to film, Doherty appeared in the short drama 27, Memory Lane in 2014, followed by a lead role in the horror thriller Braxton Butcher (released as The Butchering in some markets) in 2015.[1] These projects represented her first feature-length credits, showcasing her versatility in genre roles amid Northern Ireland's independent film scene.[11] Her early work emphasized character-driven performances, laying foundational experience before larger ensemble and comedy opportunities.[12] By the mid-2010s, Doherty's acting pursuits intersected with emerging entertainment ventures, including theatre commissions that honed her skills in live performance and script development.[13] This phase established her as a multifaceted entrant in Northern Irish entertainment, prioritizing practical on-set and stage experience over formal training in the field.[7]

Television and film roles

Doherty's early film roles included Kaitlin in the 2015 horror feature The Butchering, directed by Brendan Forbes.[14] She followed this with the part of Ellie Nic Shiubhlaigh in the 2018 drama Penance, a thriller centered on guilt and retribution in rural Ireland.[15] On television, Doherty gained prominence playing Tracy Jones, a recruitment officer for border patrol, in the BBC Northern Ireland comedy series Soft Border Patrol (2018), which satirized post-Brexit customs enforcement along the Irish border.[16] [17] That same year, she guest-starred as Katya, a Ukrainian exchange student navigating cultural clashes, in an episode of Channel 4's Derry Girls.[2] In 2023, Doherty portrayed Detective Inspector Louise Carrigan in the BBC One police drama Blue Lights, appearing in two episodes of series three, which depicted frontline policing pressures in Belfast amid community tensions.[18] [19] She has also taken on voice and minor roles, such as Clothes Horse in the children's series Nova Jones (2022). Upcoming credits include Molly Gordon in the 2025 investigative series Art Detectives.[1]

Stand-up comedy and live performances

Diona Doherty entered the stand-up comedy scene with her debut tour, Got MILF?, announced in September 2023.[20] The Northern Ireland-focused production sold out its initial spring leg and prompted additional autumn dates after tickets went on sale, reflecting strong audience demand within five days of launch.[21] [13] Remaining performances occurred in October 2024 across select venues.[22] In 2025, Doherty toured with her second stand-up show, Get Your Pink Back, which explored themes of post-motherhood identity, drawing from observations of flamingo parenting behaviors that cause loss of plumage color.[23] The production ran in May and June before a full-month run at the Edinburgh Festival Fringe in August, marking her stand-up debut at the event and achieving sold-out status.[5] [24] Performances took place at Monkey Barrel Comedy, with shows scheduled daily at 2:55 p.m.[25] Beyond tours, Doherty has delivered live stand-up at international venues, including The Stand in New York City and The Dead Rabbit for events like That's Deadly Comedy NYC in October 2024.[20] [26] She has also created and starred in one-woman dark comedy plays, such as Sunny Side Up, alongside other stage works like Bridesmaids of Northern Ireland and The Hen Do, which drew sold-out crowds in Northern Ireland.[20] [5]

Writing, production, and media ventures

Diona Doherty has written several scripts and plays, including the short BBC film Sht Stepmum*, a thirty-minute radio episode of What Happens In Ulster, and the audio play Dani Dives In for The Mac Theatre in Belfast.[13] In 2019, she penned Bridesmaids of Northern Ireland, a play staged at the Millennium Forum in Derry later that year.[8] Her writing often draws from personal experiences in Northern Ireland, blending humor with themes of family and local culture.[27] In production, Doherty co-founded Cheesy Grin Productions with her husband Sean Hegarty, focusing on comedy, acting, and writing projects by Northern Irish talents.[28] The company supports independent content creation, though specific productions under its banner remain limited in public documentation. Doherty co-hosts the podcast The Girls Groupchat, which features discussions on comedy, motherhood, and everyday life, gaining traction through her social media promotion.[4] The podcast aligns with her stand-up themes, emphasizing relatable female perspectives without formal media outlet affiliations beyond personal platforms.

Personal life

Relationships and family

Doherty has been married to fellow Northern Irish comedian Sean Hegarty since 2016.[29] The couple, both active in the comedy circuit, reside in Craigavon with their blended family.[30] They have two children together: a daughter named Winter, born in 2021, and a son named Rocky, born in June 2023 via IVF treatment.[31][30] Hegarty has three sons from a previous marriage—James, Charlie, and Tom—whom Doherty became a stepmother to at age 22.[6][32] The couple has discussed their family dynamics publicly, including through their joint podcast Meet the Parents, launched in 2023, where they address topics such as parenting in a blended household and balancing comedy careers with family life.[29] Doherty has described their relationship as supportive, noting in interviews that Hegarty's prior experience as a father aided her transition into stepmotherhood and biological parenting.[32] No prior long-term relationships for Doherty are documented in public sources.

Personal challenges and health experiences

Doherty competed in the eighth series of Britain and Ireland's Next Top Model in 2012, during which judges remarked that she "only looked good from the waist up," fostering a persistent body image complex that led her to avoid wearing shorts or skirts for roughly 15 years.[33] She characterized the production as "toxic," emphasizing its entertainment-driven format and absence of participant welfare measures at the time.[33] Doherty credited discussions with podcast colleagues for helping her overcome this phobia in 2025.[34] After approximately seven years of infertility, Doherty and her partner Sean Hegarty pursued in vitro fertilization (IVF), successfully conceiving their daughter Winter, born in 2021; their son Rocky followed in 2023.[35] [36] She has addressed the emotional toll of fertility treatments, including societal stigma around male infertility, in her stand-up routines and the BBC series Sorta Stepmum.[36] Postpartum, Doherty developed postnatal depression after Winter's birth, confiding initially only in her husband and physician amid shame over perceived ingratitude following their conception struggles.[35] She has since advocated openly against its stigma, portraying it as a profound chemical imbalance rather than personal failing, to alleviate isolation for affected mothers.[35] Doherty has explored motherhood's erosion of self-identity in her 2025 Edinburgh Fringe show Get Your Pink Back, likening her post-IVF and postpartum state to flamingos stripped of their vibrant hue, and detailing reclamation efforts via choir participation, Zumba, self-defense classes, and a silent retreat.[37] As stepmother to three teenagers alongside her biological children, she navigates compounded family dynamics that intensify these identity challenges.[36]

Public image and controversies

Achievements and critical reception

Diona Doherty reached the Northern Irish final of the BBC New Comedy Awards.[3] She has appeared as a guest lead in the television series Derry Girls and as a series regular panelist— the only permanent female member— on BBC Northern Ireland's The Blame Game.[38] [39] Doherty secured a role in season 3 of the BBC drama Blue Lights, which premiered on September 29, 2025.[40] Her 2025 Edinburgh Fringe show Get Your Pink Back sold out its run, following a UK tour in May and June.[5] She was selected to host the Bathroom Association's Gala Awards and Connect Conference in 2025.[41] Doherty's stand-up has received positive notices for her audience interaction and observational humor on topics like motherhood and personal recovery. A BroadwayWorld review of Get Your Pink Back praised her as a "natural" at on-the-spot improvisation and an "absolute delight" onstage.[42] Fringesider highlighted her "razor sharp wit" and "surefire mastery in timing," positioning her as a comedian poised for broader stages.[43] Corr Blimey described the show as a "powerful set" blending humor with self-love themes.[44] One assessment in The QR noted her confidence and accomplishment but awarded 3 out of 5 stars, suggesting room for tighter material amid relatable content.[45] Overall, critics have lauded her as an emerging talent from Northern Ireland's comedy scene, with Belfast Telegraph coverage emphasizing her sharp delivery despite the demands of balancing performance with family life.[46]

Criticisms, debates, and public statements

Doherty has publicly critiqued her participation in the 2012 eighth series of Britain and Ireland's Next Top Model, where she was eliminated by judge Elle Macpherson after judges commented critically on her legs during a photoshoot. In May 2025, she described the remark as one that "completely changed my life," resulting in a 15-year phobia that prevented her from wearing shorts or dresses above the knee, and labeled the show's atmosphere as "toxic."[33][47] She credited discussions with podcast co-hosts for helping her overcome the issue, enabling her to wear shorts publicly for the first time in over a decade.[34] In October 2025, Doherty posted on social media advocating that parents remove children from school for extended family holidays, stating, "Take them out of school. There, I said it," and arguing that such absences foster irreplaceable memories without long-term academic harm, despite potential fines or administrative challenges from schools.[48][49] The statement, made while on holiday with her family where one child missed school, elicited mixed responses, with some educators and parents agreeing on the value of experiential learning, while others highlighted risks to routine and curriculum continuity. Doherty has engaged in debates on language in Northern Irish comedy, particularly in a March 2022 episode of the Tea With Me podcast with Shane Todd, where they examined the use of the "f-word"—a sectarian slur in the region—and the associated backlash for comedians employing it for satirical or contextual effect. She has also made pointed public remarks on local cultural norms, such as in May 2025 when responding to singer Nadine Coyle's criticism of actress Nicola Coughlan's Derry accent, quipping, "Nadine, that is not how we behave in Derry."[50] These instances reflect Doherty's tendency to address personal and regional sensitivities through humor and direct commentary, often drawing on her Derry background.
