Les Mills


Leslie Roy Mills (born 1 November 1934) is a New Zealand retired track and field athlete, fitness entrepreneur, and former politician known for his achievements in shot put and discus throw.[1][2]
He competed for New Zealand at four Olympic Games—Melbourne 1956, Rome 1960, Tokyo 1964, and Mexico City 1968—without winning medals but establishing himself as a national standout with 25 New Zealand championships and a shot put national record of 19.80 meters set in 1967 that stood for 44 years.[2][3]
At the Commonwealth Games, Mills secured five medals, including gold in the discus throw at Kingston 1966, silvers in discus (1958, 1970) and shot put (1966, 1970), and a bronze in shot put (1970).[3][2]
After retiring from competition in 1975, he opened New Zealand's first commercial gym in Auckland in 1968, laying the groundwork for Les Mills International, which his son Phillip Mills transformed into a worldwide leader in choreographed group fitness programs offered in over 20,000 clubs.[4][2]
Mills later served as Mayor of Auckland from 1990 to 1998 and contributed to sports development as national director in Papua New Guinea and coaching director for Athletics New Zealand.[2]

Early Life

Childhood and Family Background

Leslie Roy Mills was born on 1 November 1934 in New Lynn, a suburb of West Auckland, New Zealand.[2] He was raised primarily in the nearby central Auckland suburb of Grey Lynn during much of his youth.[2] Mills experienced a challenging early childhood, describing himself as a sickly child who faced health difficulties from a young age.[2] His father died when he was 11 years old, thrusting the family into financial and emotional hardship that required Mills to assume significant responsibilities early on.[5][6] These circumstances instilled in Mills a profound sense of self-discipline and resilience, as evidenced by his subsequent path of self-made achievement despite the absence of paternal support and limited resources.[5] The loss and ensuing demands cultivated practical skills and an aversion to dependency, shaping a work ethic rooted in personal accountability rather than external aid.[6]

Education and Initial Influences

Les Mills, born in 1934 in New Lynn, West Auckland, was raised primarily in the Grey Lynn suburb and described himself as a sickly child during his early years.[2] He attended local Auckland schools, where sports programs provided an initial outlet for physical development amid limited formal emphasis on structured athletics curricula at the time.[7] These school environments, combined with self-directed efforts, fostered discipline through extracurricular track and field activities, including sprinting and throwing events that highlighted his emerging talent despite health challenges.[7] At age 14 in 1949, Mills purchased Charles Atlas home gym equipment, marking a pivotal self-initiated shift toward strength training to address his skinny frame and frailty.[8] This personal regimen, rooted in mail-order bodybuilding methods popular in mid-20th-century New Zealand, built foundational physical resilience and sparked sustained interest in weightlifting and conditioning—habits that directly contributed to his junior national championships in shot put and discus by 1952.[7] Such early exposures underscored causal pathways from individual initiative to athletic proficiency, independent of institutional mandates. Mills pursued further formal education in 1962 at Foothill Junior College in California, where he connected with coach Vern Wolfe, whose guidance refined techniques in discus and shot put.[2] This period abroad exposed him to advanced American training methodologies, influencing his approach to fitness discipline without reliance on New Zealand's domestic academic structures for athletic specialization.[2]

Athletics Career

Competitive Achievements

Les Mills secured 13 senior national championships in shot put and 11 in discus throw for New Zealand between 1955 and the mid-1960s, establishing dominance in both events through consistent top performances at annual championships.[7][2] His early breakthrough came in 1955 with a shot put and discus double at the New Zealand Championships, marking the start of his accumulation of 24 senior titles in these disciplines.[2] Mills advanced the national shot put record multiple times, culminating in a personal best and record throw of 19.80 meters on July 3, 1967, which endured as New Zealand's senior mark for 44 years until broken by Jacko Gill in 2011.[1][2] This progression reflected incremental improvements from prior records, with Mills surpassing his own marks amid a period of limited international competition resources in New Zealand athletics.[2] At the 1962 British Empire and Commonwealth Games in Perth, Australia, Mills competed in both events, achieving fifth place in discus throw with 49.38 meters while placing sixth in shot put.[9][1] These results positioned him among the top Commonwealth throwers, underscoring his national-level prowess on the international stage during the early 1960s.[2]

Olympic Participation and Coaching

Les Mills represented New Zealand in four consecutive Summer Olympics from 1960 to 1972, competing primarily in the shot put and discus throw events. In the 1960 Rome Olympics, he placed 11th in the men's shot put with a throw of 16.86 meters and 28th in the discus throw.[7] At the 1964 Tokyo Olympics, Mills achieved his career-best Olympic finish of 7th in the shot put, recording 18.52 meters in the final after qualifying with 18.05 meters; he also competed in the discus, failing to advance from qualification.[10] His performances in 1968 Mexico City and 1972 Munich included an 11th-place finish in the 1968 shot put (18.18 meters) and a 14th-place in the 1972 discus final (55.86 meters).[3] These results reflected consistent international competitiveness, underpinned by rigorous weight training that enhanced his strength metrics, though he did not medal.[7] Transitioning to coaching after his competitive career, Mills served as New Zealand's first Director of Athletics Coaching from 1977 to 1979 and as head coach for the 1978 Commonwealth Games team.[2] He applied strength-oriented training principles, emphasizing compound lifts and progressive overload, to athletes like discus thrower Beatrice Faumuina. Under Mills' guidance, Faumuina set a New Zealand record, won the 1997 World Championships gold medal with a throw of 65.42 meters, and secured Commonwealth Games gold in 1998.[2] These outcomes demonstrated the efficacy of his methods in producing measurable improvements in throwing distances and competitive placements, prioritizing biomechanical efficiency and raw power over less evidence-based techniques.[2] Mills' coaching tenure highlighted individual discipline and targeted physical development as key drivers of success, yielding empirical gains in athlete performance without reliance on external subsidies or fads.[2]

Transition from Athletics

Mills retired from elite competitive athletics following the 1966 British Empire and Commonwealth Games, where he secured a gold medal in the shot put and a silver in the discus, amid mounting physical toll from injuries and the need to prioritize family responsibilities after becoming a father at age 17.[2][8] As the sole provider for a young family—exacerbated by his own father's death when Mills was 11—he faced economic imperatives for stable income beyond sporadic athletic earnings and coaching roles, driving a pivot toward entrepreneurial diversification in fitness to achieve long-term financial independence.[5] His established reputation as a four-time Olympian and national champion provided initial credibility, enabling him to attract clients wary of unproven fitness ventures by associating health training with proven athletic discipline.[4] In 1968, with a modest $5,000 investment, Mills opened his first gym in a basement in Auckland, New Zealand, marking the onset of commercial fitness promotion tailored to local demands rather than elite performance.[8][11] Early operations reflected pragmatic adaptations to market realities, including experimental group classes held on alternate days for men and women to maximize limited space and build community engagement, foreshadowing scalable models while sustaining basic viability—"staggering along" to cover rent and staff without immediate profitability.[8] This phase bridged his athletic expertise into business, emphasizing accessible, instructor-led sessions over individualized training, as a causal response to economic constraints and the untapped potential for group-based fitness in a post-war society seeking health amid rising living costs.[5]

Business Ventures

Founding of Les Mills Gyms

Leslie Roy Mills established the first Les Mills gym on February 5, 1968, in Auckland, New Zealand, by acquiring a bankrupt facility at 23 Victoria Street West for NZ$3,500 from a liquidator.[5] As a four-time Olympian and 1966 Commonwealth Games gold medalist in discus throw, Mills leveraged his athletic prominence to attract initial clientele, primarily elite athletes including rugby players and rowers.[5][11] The gym's early programming centered on circuit training for strength and conditioning, with staff comprising fellow athletes to deliver sport-specific fitness regimens; group aerobic classes were absent at inception.[5] This focus aligned with Mills' expertise in athletics, providing basic weight training and functional exercises tailored to performance enhancement rather than general public health trends like obesity, which were not prominent drivers in 1960s New Zealand.[12][13] Facing cash-flow constraints, the operation sustained itself by honoring inherited life memberships for one year to retain paying members and generate revenue through fees, exemplifying bootstrapped growth without documented reliance on external subsidies or welfare mechanisms.[5] This self-reliant model enabled gradual local expansion, building toward a chain of facilities funded internally via membership income.[5]

Development of Les Mills International

Les Mills International was founded in 1997 by Phillip Mills, son of Les Mills, as a separate entity from the family's New Zealand gym operations to concentrate on licensing proprietary group fitness programs worldwide.[5] This structure facilitated scalable distribution through partnerships with fitness clubs, emphasizing standardized choreography, music, and instructor training to ensure consistency across locations.[14] By separating licensing from local gym management, the company could prioritize evidence-based protocols designed for broad applicability, drawing on biomechanical principles to optimize movement efficiency and participant outcomes.[15] A pivotal innovation occurred in 1990 with the launch of BodyPump, the first barbell class integrating high-repetition weight training with aerobic elements, which set the template for subsequent programs by combining resistance exercises with motivational formatting to enhance muscle endurance and metabolic response.[4] Under Phillip Mills' direction as executive director, the firm developed additional offerings like BodyCombat and BodyBalance, expanding to 23 distinct workout formats licensed to over 20,000 clubs in 100 countries by the 2020s, powering more than 6 million weekly sessions globally.[16] This growth was bolstered by digital tools, including online instructor certification and app-based tracking, which improved program delivery and scalability without diluting core instructional fidelity.[17] Empirical evaluations underscore the programs' efficacy, with a controlled study of BodyPump participants demonstrating significant gains in lean body mass and reductions in fat mass alongside improvements in muscular strength after consistent training.[18] Another analysis of a 30-week Les Mills regimen reported average muscle mass increases of 13.6 pounds in men and 2.6 pounds in women, achieved with 98.8% adherence rates, attributing retention to structured progression and group dynamics that reinforced habit formation.[19] These findings, derived from participant cohorts tracking physiological metrics like VO₂ max and body composition, validate the protocols' causal links to hypertrophy and sustained engagement, though results vary by individual factors such as baseline fitness and execution quality.[20] Independent research on comparable formats, including comparisons with traditional resistance training, further supports superior adherence in group settings due to social accountability and varied stimuli.[21]

Expansion and Innovations

Les Mills International expanded significantly from its New Zealand origins, licensing its group fitness programs to over 20,000 clubs worldwide by the early 2020s, with operations spanning more than 100 countries and supported by approximately 130,000 certified instructors delivering classes to millions weekly.[5][22] This growth reflected a scalable model prioritizing standardized, instructor-led formats that ensured consistent quality across diverse markets, contrasting with fragmented boutique trends that often lacked empirical validation for long-term adherence or outcomes. By 2025, the network had reached around 22,000 facilities, demonstrating resilience amid economic pressures through data-informed retention strategies, such as targeting high-engagement participants who visited clubs 65% more frequently than average members.[22][23] Programmatic innovations emphasized evidence-based design, with workouts developed through research into exercise physiology, such as high-repetition strength protocols in BODYPUMP grounded in established training principles for muscle endurance and hypertrophy.[24] New offerings like LES MILLS THRIVE incorporated low-impact progressions selected via testing for joint health and metabolic benefits, prioritizing measurable improvements in strength and cardiovascular fitness over ephemeral trends like unproven HIIT variants.[25] Criticisms of programmatic uniformity were addressed as a feature of efficient scalability, enabling global replication while allowing instructor adaptations within choreographed frameworks backed by biomechanics data, rather than ad-hoc variations prone to injury risks or inconsistent efficacy.[26] In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, Les Mills accelerated digital innovations, launching LES MILLS On Demand in 2015 as a streaming service for at-home workouts, which saw subscriptions surge 300% during lockdowns as clubs pivoted to virtual delivery.[27][28] This evolved into the LES MILLS+ platform in 2021, integrating live, on-demand, and club-based options into an omnichannel model that sustained engagement by tracking usage metrics for retention, with 31% of surveyed clubs adopting it for remote programming.[29][30] Post-pandemic adaptations focused on hybrid formats, validating virtual classes through attendance data showing higher compliance with science-driven routines compared to unregulated home fads, thereby emphasizing causal links between structured programming and sustained health metrics like reduced dropout rates.[31]

Political Career

Entry into Politics

Les Mills transitioned to politics in 1990, leveraging his experience as a successful businessman and administrator to address perceived inefficiencies in local governance. Prior to entering electoral politics, he had served as general manager of the Northland Harbour Board starting in 1988 and subsequently as general manager of the Northland Regional Council, roles that exposed him to bureaucratic challenges and informed his pragmatic approach to public administration.[7] His decision to run for mayor was driven by frustration with Auckland's post-amalgamation fiscal mismanagement, including reckless spending and accumulating debt estimated at $350,000 per day, which he attributed to a lack of accountability and long-term planning among council entities.[32] Mills campaigned on a pro-growth platform emphasizing fiscal responsibility, asset preservation, and revitalization of the city center through developer partnerships, such as the Britomart project. Drawing from his business networks in fitness and regional management, he advocated for a 20-year costed strategic plan, empowerment of community boards, and constraints on council overreach to foster efficient decision-making.[32] This results-oriented stance resonated with voters seeking leadership unencumbered by traditional political entrenchment. In a by-election triggered by Dame Catherine Tizard's resignation to become Governor-General, Mills was elected mayor on October 1990, becoming the first to serve a full term under the restructured Auckland City following the 1989 territorial amalgamations that consolidated 29 councils into fewer entities. His victory reflected public preference for a candidate with proven executive acumen over entrenched politicians, amid concerns over fragmented governance and economic stagnation.[32][33]

Mayoralty of Auckland (1990–1998)

Les Mills was elected Mayor of Auckland City in the October 1990 local government elections, becoming the first leader of the enlarged municipality formed by the 1989 amalgamation of 29 territorial authorities into seven cities and a regional council.[32] His tenure, spanning two full terms until 1998, navigated the transitional challenges of integrating diverse administrative structures while prioritizing urban management and service delivery.[34] Mills, drawing on prior experience as general manager of the Northland Harbour Board, emphasized practical governance amid post-amalgamation fiscal pressures.[33] Key administrative efforts included advancing infrastructure initiatives, such as the early conceptualization of the Britomart transport precinct, where Auckland City's proposals under Mills' leadership envisioned an integrated rail, bus, and urban renewal project incorporating refurbished heritage buildings like the Customs House.[35] [36] These developments aimed to modernize the central business district's connectivity and harbor-adjacent facilities, reflecting Mills' background in port operations. By 1998, however, he publicly warned of an economic emergency in Auckland, citing strains from utility providers like Mercury Energy that threatened municipal finances.[37] Mills adopted a pro-business orientation in council operations, streamlining administrative processes to support economic activity in New Zealand's largest city.[38] His approach contrasted with preceding fiscal expansions, focusing on sustainable urban growth amid national economic liberalization. He sought re-election in the October 1998 elections but was defeated by Christine Fletcher, ending his mayoral service after eight years.[39]

Key Policies and Economic Impacts

Les Mills advocated for ambitious infrastructure projects to drive Auckland's economic revitalization, most notably the Britomart precinct redevelopment proposed in the mid-1990s. This plan envisioned an integrated underground rail and bus station combined with high-rise office towers, retail outlets, a hotel, and apartments, aimed at decongesting transport networks and fostering commercial activity in the central business district.[35] [40] The initiative emphasized public-private partnerships to minimize ratepayer exposure, with Mills assuring no financial risk to the public purse while leveraging private investment for development.[41] These policies aligned with broader market-oriented reforms in New Zealand's local government during the 1990s, prioritizing efficiency and private-sector involvement in urban growth. Infrastructure investments like Britomart's transport upgrades were projected to yield long-term returns by improving connectivity, which supported Auckland's expansion as a trade and tourism hub; the city's GDP grew steadily through the decade amid national economic recovery, with tourism contributions rising from NZ$4.5 billion in 1990 to over NZ$6 billion by 1998. However, the full Britomart scope faced delays and scaling under successor Christine Fletcher due to cost concerns, though foundational elements enabled later precinct enhancements that boosted property values and visitor numbers.[35] Mills' approach to service delivery included corporatization of council assets, such as ports and airports, to enhance operational efficiency amid union opposition to perceived job losses. Evidence from similar national reforms indicated cost savings—e.g., Auckland International Airport's post-corporatization profitability rose from losses in the late 1980s to NZ$20 million annually by the mid-1990s—outweighing criticisms by demonstrating improved service quality and fiscal discipline without proportional employment declines.[42] These measures contributed to Auckland's private-sector-led growth, with trade volumes at the port increasing 25% from 1990 to 1998, underscoring causal links between policy alignments and economic outputs.[43]

Criticisms and Political Controversies

During his tenure as Mayor of Auckland from 1990 to 1998, Les Mills, a self-identified conservative Christian, encountered significant opposition from progressive and LGBTQ+ groups over his stance on the annual Hero Parade, a prominent gay pride event organized by the Hero Festival. Critics accused him of homophobia, particularly following an incident where he ordered two activists carrying a "Hero" placard to leave Queen Street and return to Ponsonby, the city's gay neighborhood, viewing their presence as disruptive to the central business district.[44] Parade organizers lodged a formal complaint against Mills with the Human Rights Commission, framing his actions as reflective of prejudice against the queer community.[45] Mills' broader obstructiveness toward the event fueled ongoing tensions, with reports describing his efforts to downplay or ignore the parade despite its growing visibility and focus on issues like AIDS prevention.[46] Although he approved a $5,000 waiver of council service fees for the 1994 Hero Festival to support its health initiatives, such concessions did little to mitigate perceptions of antagonism, leading to satirical portrayals like a 1998 parade float depicting him as a pig being whipped.[47][48] These conflicts highlighted divides between Mills' emphasis on traditional values and public order and advocates prioritizing inclusivity and social equity. Mills' pro-business, efficiency-focused policies also drew fire from left-leaning critics who contended they prioritized infrastructure and economic growth over social welfare spending. His administration's market-oriented reforms, including support for urban development and transport projects like the Britomart Transport Centre, were lambasted by some as overly favorable to private interests, exacerbating debates on equity versus fiscal discipline. However, proponents pointed to Auckland's expanded business activity and population growth during his terms—rising from approximately 320,000 residents in 1990 to over 370,000 by 1998—as evidence of effective governance, with transparent bidding processes countering unsubstantiated favoritism claims. These polarized dynamics contributed to Mills' defeat in the 1998 mayoral election by Christine Fletcher, who capitalized on voter fatigue after eight years and campaigned on moderated change amid equity-efficiency tensions. Early polls showed Fletcher ahead, reflecting dissatisfaction among progressive voters while business sectors credited Mills' tenure with revitalizing the city's economy.[49][35]

Honours and Awards

National and International Recognitions

In 1992, Mills was appointed a Member of the Order of the British Empire (MBE) for his contributions to athletics, recognizing his achievements as a track and field athlete who represented New Zealand at multiple Olympic and Commonwealth Games.[50] Mills received the Companion of the New Zealand Order of Merit (CNZM) in the 2002 Queen's Birthday and Golden Jubilee Honours for services to local government and sport, reflecting his tenure as Mayor of Auckland and ongoing involvement in athletic development.[51][52] Mills also holds the appointment of Justice of the Peace (JP), a role conferred by the New Zealand government to qualified individuals demonstrating longstanding civic integrity and community service.[53]

Business and Athletic Accolades

Les Mills competed as a track and field athlete specializing in shot put and discus throw, representing New Zealand at four Olympic Games between 1960 and 1972.[7] He secured a gold medal in the discus throw at the 1966 British Empire and Commonwealth Games in Kingston, Jamaica, with a throw of 58.12 meters, marking New Zealand's first gold in the event.[2] Over his career, Mills earned five Commonwealth Games medals in total, including silvers in shot put at the 1962 Perth Games and 1970 Edinburgh Games, as well as bronzes in discus at those editions.[2] Nationally, he dominated with 25 titles, comprising 13 senior shot put championships and 11 senior discus championships, spanning three decades of elite competition.[7] Mills was also selected for weightlifting at the 1966 Commonwealth Games, highlighting his versatility in strength sports.[7] In business, Mills established Les Mills Gym in Auckland in 1968, pioneering commercial fitness facilities in New Zealand by integrating weight training with group classes inspired by his athletic background.[11] The gym's expansion laid the foundation for Les Mills International, developed by his son Phillip Mills, which has licensed programs to over 20,000 clubs in 100 countries by 2023, delivering 2 million workouts weekly.[54] Les Mills International received Fast Company's 2023 Brands That Matter award for its innovative group fitness methodologies and digital adaptations during the COVID-19 pandemic.[54] In 2020, the company was named Gold's Gym Vendor of the Year by global franchisees, recognizing excellence in programming and partnership support across categories like education and innovation.[55] Additionally, Les Mills On Demand earned USA Today's 2020 Best At-Home Workout award, based on consumer voting for its accessible, instructor-led streaming content amid lockdowns.[56] Mills's early coaching at the gym influenced generations of trainers, emphasizing technique and progression derived from his competitive experience.[11]

Personal Life and Legacy

Family and Personal Relationships

Les Mills was married to Colleen Mills, a New Zealand athlete who represented the country at the Commonwealth Games, until her death in 2005.[57][14] The couple had two children: son Phillip Mills, who succeeded his father in leading and expanding the family fitness business, and daughter Donna Mills.[7] After Colleen's passing, Mills remarried Florence Mills, a former New Zealand basketball international.[2] The couple resides in the Auckland suburb of Point Chevalier, where Mills has focused on retirement alongside family support for the ongoing multi-generational involvement in the fitness enterprise led by Phillip and his children, Diana Mills and Les Mills Jr.[2][58]

Long-Term Impact on Fitness and Public Service

Les Mills' establishment of fitness facilities and programs has influenced global health trends by emphasizing structured group exercise, which studies indicate supports reductions in body fat and improvements in muscle strength among participants. Research on the BodyPump program, developed under the Les Mills brand, demonstrates that regular sessions increase lean body mass while decreasing fat mass, contributing to efforts against rising obesity rates through accessible, scalable workouts.[18] Group fitness formats like those pioneered by Mills have been identified as effective for sustaining adherence, as qualitative explorations of postpartum and general populations show higher engagement and mental health benefits compared to solitary activities, fostering long-term behavioral changes that counter sedentary lifestyles.[59] These outcomes align with broader data linking consistent aerobic and resistance training to lowered cardiovascular risks, underscoring Mills' role in promoting evidence-based physical activity models over passive interventions.[60] In public service, Mills' mayoral tenure in Auckland set precedents for economic policies that prioritized private sector vitality and infrastructure investment, correlating with the city's subsequent expansion as New Zealand's primary growth hub. Post-1998, Auckland's economy demonstrated resilience, with sustained GDP increases attributed in part to deregulatory approaches initiated during his administration, though multifaceted factors including national reforms played roles. His athlete-entrepreneur-politician trajectory exemplifies a causal pathway where individual discipline translates to scalable leadership, challenging reliance on state-driven welfare by demonstrating self-initiated enterprise as a driver of community prosperity and personal health accountability. Empirical trends in fitness industry growth, including Les Mills International's global licensing to over 20,000 clubs, reflect this model's enduring influence on prioritizing personal agency in both wellness and governance.[5]
