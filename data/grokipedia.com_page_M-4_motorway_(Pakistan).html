M-4 motorway (Pakistan)

The M-4 motorway is a 309 km controlled-access highway in Punjab province, Pakistan, extending from Pindi Bhattian to Multan and passing through Faisalabad, Jhang, Shorkot, and Khanewal.[1] It forms a key segment of Pakistan's national motorway system, designed to enhance inter-city connectivity, reduce travel times, and support economic activities such as agriculture and trade in the region.[2] The motorway, maintained by the National Highway Authority, features four lanes with access-controlled interchanges and was constructed in phases, with the Faisalabadâ€“Gojra section opening in 2014, followed by Gojraâ€“Shorkot in 2019 and other segments achieving full operational status by that year.[3][4]

History

Planning and Approval

The planning for the M-4 motorway emerged in the early 2010s as part of the Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) government's infrastructure priorities, aligned with Vision 2025's emphasis on expanding the motorway network to foster sustainable economic growth through improved north-south connectivity in Punjab province.[5] This initiative sought to address chronic congestion on the existing National Highway N-5 by developing a dedicated access-controlled route for faster freight and passenger movement, diverting traffic from overburdened alternative paths.[6] The National Highway Authority (NHA) managed the approval framework, beginning with an environmental impact assessment (EIA) prepared and approved for the full M-4 alignment in 2007, which was updated in line with project advancements and disclosed publicly.[7] For the Gojra-Shorkot section (approximately 62 km), the Asian Development Bank (ADB) approved a $178 million loan in August 2015, supplemented by a $92 million grant administered from the UK Department for International Development, with a loan agreement signed in October 2015.[8][9][10] Subsequent approvals covered the Shorkot-Khanewal section (64 km), where the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) committed a $100 million loan in June 2016, co-financed with ADB and UK aid to integrate with prior segments and complete the motorway's core Punjab linkage.[4][11] These multilateral funding decisions prioritized engineering feasibility and economic rationale over domestic budgeting constraints, ensuring phased development without direct reliance on China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) financing, though the route supported complementary national transport efficiencies.[12]

Construction Phases

The M-4 motorway from Faisalabad to Multan, totaling 309 kilometers, was constructed in phases as a four-lane, access-controlled highway to enhance connectivity while addressing logistical constraints such as land acquisition. The initial segment, Faisalabad to Gojra (58 km), marked the northern starting point and was completed in 2014 under a multitranche financing facility from the Asian Development Bank (ADB), incorporating standard motorway engineering for high-speed travel.[3] The subsequent Gojra to Shorkot section (62 km) advanced the core build, with the civil works contract awarded by December 2015 to international contractors including Xinjian Beixin Road and Bridge Construction Engineering Co. Ltd. for sub-sections like Gojra to Jhammani; construction overcame terrain challenges in Punjab's central plains, culminating in operational status by February 2019.[13][14][15] Parallel progress occurred on the Shorkot to Khanewal segment (64 km), where contracts were awarded in April 2016 to firms such as China Railway First Group, supported by cofinancing from ADB, the UK Department for International Development, and the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank; work commenced shortly thereafter but encountered delays from protracted land acquisition, extending timelines beyond initial projections while maintaining the access-controlled design.[16][14][17][18] The Khanewal to Multan phase finalized the route, integrating with existing infrastructure at Sher Shah Interchange; involvement of both local oversight by the National Highway Authority and international contractors emphasized cost-effective execution, ultimately linking the full corridor despite acquisition hurdles that had previously stalled earlier bids dating to 2009.[19][20][21]

Inauguration and Initial Operations

The Gojraâ€“Shorkot section of the M-4, spanning 62 km, was opened to traffic on February 2, 2019, following its groundbreaking by Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif in November 2015.[16][22] This partial rollout connected to the earlier-completed Faisalabadâ€“Gojra segment, enabling initial traffic flow through central Punjab while other sections like Shorkotâ€“Khanewal remained under construction. Similarly, the 72 km Khanewalâ€“Multan section had been inaugurated on November 21, 2015, by Sharif, allowing early southern access for freight and passenger vehicles.[23] The full 309 km Pindi Bhattianâ€“Multan motorway achieved operational status by November 2019, with all sections linked to form a continuous northâ€“south corridor integrating with the M-3 at Pindi Bhattian.[6] Initial toll collection commenced at dedicated plazas upon sectional openings, structured under public-private partnerships to fund maintenance and expansion, with rates calibrated for heavy vehicles supporting Punjab's agricultural exports.[6] Early performance metrics demonstrated effective rollout, as average daily vehicle-kilometers reached 2.5 million in the first full year of complete operation, surpassing projected targets by facilitating reduced travel timesâ€”cutting Islamabad to Multan journeys to approximately 6 hours from over 10 hours on national highway N-5.[6] This integration eased congestion on parallel routes, boosted logistics for perishable goods from the Punjab heartland, and recorded initial traffic volumes dominated by trucks (about 60% of usage), reflecting immediate utility for regional trade without reported major operational disruptions.[6]

Route Description

Overall Path and Length

The M-4 motorway extends 309 kilometers in a predominantly north-south alignment across Punjab province, commencing at the Pindi Bhattian interchange with the M-2 motorway (Islamabadâ€“Lahore) and terminating at Multan, where it connects to the M-5 motorway (Multanâ€“Sukkur).[24][25] This positioning integrates the M-4 into Pakistan's broader motorway network, supporting north-south connectivity while linking to east-west corridors via the M-2 and M-5.[26] The route passes through or adjacent to key agricultural and industrial centers, including Faisalabad, Gojra, Jhang, Toba Tek Singh, Shorkot, and Khanewal, traversing the flat alluvial plains of central Punjab to facilitate efficient transit and avoid congested urban cores via controlled-access design.[27][28]

Notable Features and Crossings

The M-4 motorway features several significant river crossings, including bridges over the Ravi River between the Abdul Hakeem and Darkhana interchanges, and spans both the Ravi and Chenab rivers as part of its alignment through Punjab's canal-irrigated plains.[27] These crossings facilitate unimpeded north-south connectivity while traversing flood-prone waterways, with the Ravi bridge positioned upstream of existing structures to minimize hydrological interference.[29] Service areas along the route provide essential stops for long-haul travelers, with four operational rest areas as of August 2023, including facilities at key points to support driver welfare without requiring detours into nearby towns.[30] Additional service areas at Khanewal, Jumani, and Dandewal were under construction at that time, enhancing the motorway's capacity for sustained travel across its 309-kilometer span.[30] The route's design prioritizes bypasses around major urban centers, such as skirting the edges of Faisalabad and Multan to reduce congestion and enable direct freight movement between industrial hubs.[31] This alignment supports efficient linkage of Punjab's textile manufacturing base in Faisalabad with cotton-producing districts in southern Punjab, streamlining agricultural and industrial transport.[32]

Design and Technical Specifications

Engineering Standards

The M-4 motorway adheres to standards for a four-lane, access-controlled divided highway, featuring carriageways with individual lane widths of 3.65 meters and a total pavement width of 13.3 meters.[33][34] The design accommodates a speed of 120 km/h, with a maximum longitudinal gradient of 4%, ensuring efficient flow for intercity travel and freight transport.[33] Construction incorporates asphalt surfacing over prepared subgrades, supported by extensive earthworks to achieve stability in the region's alluvial soils, as specified in National Highway Authority (NHA) guidelines and verified through detailed engineering designs.[33][17] Engineering execution follows NHA general specifications for highways, supplemented by international benchmarks enforced by funding bodies such as the Asian Development Bank (ADB) and Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), which require compliance with plans, technical standards, and construction methods acceptable for durability under heavy axle loads typical of Pakistani freight corridors.[6][17] Median barriers and service roads are integrated to separate opposing traffic and manage local access, enhancing structural integrity against lateral forces and vehicle overruns.[6] Project costs for key sections, such as Gojraâ€“Shorkot (approximately 62 km), totaled around $270 million in combined financing, reflecting investments in robust geotechnical preparations and pavement layering for long-term performance.[35] Across phases, aggregate expenditures align with estimates of $1â€“2 billion when scaled to the full 309 km alignment, prioritizing resilient infrastructure amid variable soil conditions.[36][17]

Safety and Infrastructure Features

The M-4 motorway, as an access-controlled highway, eliminates at-grade intersections, directing all traffic through grade-separated interchanges to prevent collisions between crossing vehicles and pedestrians, a primary risk factor on non-controlled national highways like the N-5.[37][4] This design inherently lowers crash involvement rates by restricting entry to authorized vehicles, thereby reducing exposure to erratic traffic patterns prevalent on open-access roads. Emergency shoulders, typically 2.5 meters wide on each side, provide space for breakdowns or disabled vehicles, minimizing secondary incidents from stopped traffic encroaching on travel lanes.[38] Rumble strips along the shoulders and centerlines alert drivers to potential lane departures, particularly in low-visibility conditions, while high-mast lighting illuminates interchanges and key segments to enhance nighttime visibility and reduce speed-related errors.[38] CCTV cameras, integrated into the motorway's surveillance system, monitor traffic flow and enable rapid response to hazards, contributing to proactive incident management. These elements collectively address human error factors, such as drowsiness or inattention, which account for a significant portion of motorway incidents.[39] Rest areas, spaced approximately every 50-60 kilometers, include facilities for refueling, refreshments, and short breaks at locations such as Makhdumpur, Shorkot, Gojra, and Sahianwala, with four operational as of August 2023 and additional sites under development to mitigate driver fatigue over the 230-kilometer route.[30] Weigh stations, including one at Deputywala Interchange (km 246), enforce axle load limits to curb overloading of heavy vehiclesâ€”a widespread issue on Pakistani roads that exacerbates pavement damage and instability-related crashesâ€”through on-site inspections and fines.[40][41] Such infrastructure directly counters fatigue and mechanical failures, yielding empirically lower crash frequencies on access-controlled motorways compared to the N-5, where uncontrolled access amplifies vulnerability to mixed traffic.[37][42]

Junctions and Interchanges

The M-4 motorway incorporates multiple full cloverleaf and partial interchanges designed for controlled access, totaling over a dozen along its 309 km length from Faisalabad to Multan. These facilities connect the route to national highways, local roads, and adjacent motorways, enhancing regional connectivity while maintaining high-speed traffic flow. The northern terminus features the Faisalabad Interchange (also known as Kamalpur Interchange), linking directly to the M-3 motorway for access to Lahore and northern Pakistan.[43] Key southern interchanges include those in the Gojraâ€“Shorkot section, with three facilities at Toba Tek Singh, Warriyamwala, and Shorkot, supporting local agricultural and urban access.[44] The Shorkotâ€“Khanewal segment includes the Abdul Hakeem Interchange near Pul Bagar Road and bridges over the Ravi River, facilitating crossings to nearby towns.[29] Overall, 16 interchanges serve the corridor from Pindi Bhattian (via M-3) to Khanewal, with additional points in the Khanewalâ€“Multan extension, such as the Shershah Interchange connecting to the M-5 motorway toward Sukkur.[6]

Economic and Strategic Impacts

Connectivity and Trade Benefits

The M-4 motorway facilitates faster freight and passenger movement between Faisalabad and Multan, achieving average speeds exceeding 90 km/h compared to slower national highway routes, thereby reducing overall travel times by approximately 40-50% for the 309 km corridor.[45] This efficiency supports the transport of Punjab's key exports, including textiles from Faisalabad's industrial clusters and perishable agricultural goods such as rice and mangoes from Multan and surrounding districts, minimizing spoilage risks and enabling higher trade volumes.[46] Quantified economic gains include savings in vehicle operating costs and travel time, with projected annual benefits reaching PRs 8,450 million by 2035 through lower fuel consumption and maintenance on the access-controlled alignment.[45] Logistics cost reductions for freight, evidenced in comparable corridor segments dropping from 7% to 5% of total shipment expenses, translate to 20-30% efficiency improvements that enhance competitiveness for Punjab-based manufacturers, which constitute over 60% of Pakistan's national manufacturing output.[46] By integrating with the M-5 motorway, the M-4 strengthens north-south connectivity in the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor framework, streamlining access from Punjab's production centers to Gwadar port for export-oriented cargo and reducing inter-regional shipment delays.[12] Asian Development Bank evaluations of similar infrastructure underscore GDP multipliers of 1.5-2.0 times investment through amplified trade flows and supply chain reliability.[45]

Broader Regional Development

The M-4 motorway has spurred industrial expansion along the Faisalabad-Multan corridor by enhancing logistics efficiency for agro-based sectors, enabling the development of new warehouses and supporting increased exports from Punjab's manufacturing hubs.[47] This infrastructure has positioned the corridor as a center for local employment generation, particularly in construction, transportation, and ancillary industries during and post-construction phases.[48] However, benefits have been uneven, with urban-industrial zones experiencing more pronounced growth compared to peripheral rural economies reliant on subsistence agriculture. Improved rural connectivity has facilitated faster transport of agricultural produce to urban markets, reducing spoilage and post-harvest losses in Punjab's fertile plains, though quantifiable gains remain tied to broader motorway network integration rather than isolated M-4 effects.[8] Socially, the project enhanced market access for remote communities, promoting inclusive development through better linkage to economic opportunities, yet it involved land acquisition affecting thousands of households.[3] In the Shorkot-Khanewal section, 3,429 householdsâ€”impacting approximately 23,186 individualsâ€”faced asset losses, with compensation provided in cash or kind to offset displacements.[17] Displacement challenges included loss of livelihoods, cultural disruptions, and temporary economic hardship for affected families, particularly the 23 households in the Gojra-Shorkot section that lost full residential structures and required physical relocation.[5][49] Mitigation via resettlement plans and grievance mechanisms addressed some issues, but studies highlight persistent vulnerabilities among landless and marginalized groups.[50] Environmentally, construction generated a short-term carbon footprint from material extraction and earthworks, while the operational motorway's controlled access and higher speeds yield lower emissions per vehicle-kilometer relative to congested national highways.[27] Environmental impact assessments reported no significant long-term ecological damage in the flat, agrarian terrain, with minimal disruption to local flora, fauna, or water resources beyond localized construction-phase erosion controls.[29] Overall, indirect outcomes reflect targeted infrastructure gains tempered by localized social costs, without evidence of transformative tourism uplift in the non-scenic corridor.

Operational and Maintenance Aspects

Tolls, Funding, and Management

The M-4 motorway is managed by the National Highway Authority (NHA) of Pakistan, which oversees operations, toll collection, and maintenance to promote financial self-sustainability through user fees rather than ongoing government subsidies.[8][4] Toll revenues fund debt repayment, upkeep, and operational costs, with the NHA implementing periodic rate adjustments to cover inflation and rising expenses.[51] Toll rates on the M-4 are distance-based and vehicle-classified, collected at interchanges and plazas. As of June 2025, the full toll for cars from Pindi Bhattian to Multan (approximately 309 km) stands at Rs. 1,600, equating to roughly Rs. 5.2 per km; rates for trucks and buses are higher, typically 1.5 to 2 times those for cars depending on axle load.[51] Earlier adjustments in April 2025 raised the same section's car toll from Rs. 950 to Rs. 1,050, reflecting annual hikes approved by the NHA to ensure revenue adequacy amid fiscal pressures.[52] While some historical sections involved private concessions for toll collection, current operations remain predominantly under direct NHA control without widespread private partnerships.[53] Funding for M-4 construction combined international loans with government contributions, emphasizing repayable debt over grants to align with fiscal realism. Key sections, such as Gojraâ€“Shorkot (62 km), were financed by an Asian Development Bank (ADB) loan of up to $178 million in ordinary capital resources, an Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) loan of $100 million, and a UK Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office grant of $34 million, with total project costs for expanded segments reaching approximately $590 million.[3][17][54] Repayment is structured through toll revenues, projected to service loans without indefinite reliance on foreign aid or domestic bailouts.[8] Maintenance follows NHA protocols integrated into its annual plans, prioritizing preventive measures like resurfacing and roughness monitoring via the International Roughness Index (IRI), with over 67% of the network maintaining IRI values of 2â€“3 m/km.[3][6] Annual audits and asset management systems ensure accountability, with the 2021â€“2022 plan allocating billions of rupees across motorways for routine interventions, funded primarily by tolls to sustain long-term viability.[55][38]

Traffic Patterns and Usage Data

The M-4 motorway's traffic volumes are monitored by the National Highway Authority (NHA) through automated traffic counting stations and periodic surveys, reflecting sustained demand driven by Punjab province's industrial and agricultural output. Post-opening projections for 2020 estimated average daily traffic (ADT) at 7,915 passenger car units (PCU) for the Gojraâ€“Shorkot section and 10,322 PCU for Shorkotâ€“Khanewal, with freight vehicles comprising a significant portion due to the route's role in transporting goods between Faisalabad's textile hubs and southern markets.[45] Forecast models anticipate growth to 11,225â€“14,426 PCU by 2025 across these sections, aligned with Pakistan's projected GDP expansion of 4â€“5% annually and increased trade via the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, indicating capacity utilization below the motorway's design threshold of approximately 50,000 PCU for six lanes.[45] Traffic patterns show higher volumes during daytime hours and peaks in truck traffic for bulk commodities like cotton and grains, though seasonal data specifics remain limited in public NHA reports. Pre-motorway usage on the parallel National Highway N-5 averaged over 14,000 vehicles per day in comparable segments, with urban stretches near Lahore-Multan exceeding 20,000; the M-4 has induced a modal shift of long-haul traffic toward the controlled-access route for reduced travel times and enhanced reliability, though exact diversion rates post-full completion in 2021 are not publicly quantified beyond model-based estimates.[45][56]

Safety Record

Accident Statistics and Causes

Since its full operationalization in phases from 2019 onward, the M-4 motorway has recorded several major accidents, typically involving commercial vehicles and resulting in fatalities due to high speeds and collisions. A prominent incident occurred on October 4, 2021, near Shamkot Interchange, where a passenger bus carrying approximately 35 people skidded off the road into a ditch, killing 10 passengers and injuring 25 others; preliminary investigations attributed the crash to loss of control, possibly from speeding or poor road conditions at the time.[57] In April 2025, a bus en route from Dera Ghazi Khan to Rawalpindi collided rear-end with a truck near Pansera, igniting a fire that killed the driver and injured passengers among the 43 on board, with the cause linked to failure to maintain distance.[58] More recent collisions highlight recurring patterns, such as a September 25, 2025, head-on crash near Khanewal between a speeding car and a truck, which killed three family members including a woman and her relatives, and injured others; police reports cited excessive speed as the primary factor.[59] Another event in April 2025 near Gojra involved a bus-truck collision resulting in fatalities and requiring rescue operations, underscoring vulnerabilities in intercity transport on the route.[60] Aggregate data specific to the M-4 remains limited in public NHA and police reports, but motorway-wide analyses from 2013â€“2019 indicate an average severity index of approximately 0.032 fatalities per accident across Pakistan's controlled-access roads, lower than open highways due to reduced exposure to uncontrolled traffic.[39] Primary causes of these incidents, drawn from empirical analysis of motorway accidents in Pakistan, include human error factors like careless driving (accounting for 25% of cases) and driver fatigue or dozing at the wheel (23%), often exacerbated by long-haul operations without adequate rest.[61] Mechanical issues contribute significantly, with tyre bursts (18%) and brake failures noted as frequent triggers, alongside overloading of vehicles which compromises stability, particularly for buses and trucks prevalent on the M-4.[61] Weather-related factors, such as fog-induced pile-ups, have also been documented, as in November 2024 closures and collisions near Faisalabad linked to low visibility.[62] Compared to the N-5 national highway, where studies report higher fatality indices (e.g., 0.821 deaths per incident in analyzed sections), the M-4's access-controlled design empirically correlates with fewer head-on crashes, though per-kilometer data specific to the route is not comprehensively published by authorities.[63] Overall, reported motorway fatalities remain below 1 per 100 million vehicle-kilometers traveled in aggregated national data, reflecting stricter enforcement but persistent driver and vehicle deficiencies.[39]

Mitigation Measures and Improvements

The National Highways and Motorway Police (NHMP) introduced updated speed limits on the M-4 motorway, reducing the maximum for light transport vehicles to 100 km/h and for heavier vehicles to 80 km/h, with enforcement through fines and vehicle impoundment for non-compliance.[64] In parallel, NHMP deployed next-generation speed cameras, drone surveillance, and spotter teams across motorways including the M-4 as part of a zero-tolerance enforcement regime launched in June 2022 to curb speeding-related incidents.[65] [66] Awareness campaigns emphasizing rule adherence, such as seatbelt use and fatigue prevention, have been rolled out by NHMP since early 2025 to foster voluntary compliance among drivers.[67] The National Highway Authority (NHA) and NHMP mandate vehicle fitness certification prior to motorway entry, barring non-compliant vehicles to address mechanical failures as a crash factor, with automated checks integrated into broader AI surveillance systems operationalized by March 2025.[68] Emergency response capabilities have been bolstered through a January 2025 pilot deploying 30-35 motorbike patrols for rapid incident attendance and security, alongside national post-crash guidelines prioritizing first-responder arrival to mitigate injury severity.[69] [70] Rest areas along the M-4, spaced for driver breaks, support these efforts by reducing fatigue risks inherent to long-haul travel.[29] AI-driven monitoring and barrier-free tolling upgrades, directed in August 2025, enable real-time violation detection and automated alerts, enhancing overall enforcement efficiency on the M-4 and affirming the controlled-access design's role in lowering collision rates compared to open highways.[71] [72]

Criticisms and Challenges

Construction and Funding Issues

The construction of the M-4 motorway encountered significant delays between 2015 and 2019, primarily due to protracted land acquisition disputes, irregular funding disbursements from the government, and occasional disruptions from heavy flooding in Punjab province.[73][17] Although loan agreements with international lenders were signed in October 2015 and became effective shortly thereafter, early sections faced extensions, with initial deadlines pushed back by up to three years before revised schedules were implemented, enabling phased completion by late 2019.[21][3] These setbacks were mitigated through adjusted payment mechanisms and prioritized resettlement activities, without evidence of major corruption convictions emerging from investigations by Pakistan's National Accountability Bureau or lender oversight.[74] Funding for the project relied on a blend of multilateral loans, including $178 million from the Asian Development Bank (ADB) via ordinary capital resources, $100 million from the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB), and a $34 million grant from the UK Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office, supplemented by Pakistani government contributions.[3][17] Criticisms arose over potential cost overruns, with risks identified from commodity price inflation and raw material hikes, though actual expenditures varied by sectionâ€”some, like the Shorkot-Khanewal segment, came in approximately 30% under budget after efficient contract management.[17][3] Routine performance audits by ADB and AIIB confirmed compliance and successful utilization of funds, rating additional financing as effective despite fiscal conservatives' concerns over debt accumulation in Pakistan's infrastructure portfolio.[75] Pro-development advocates emphasize the motorway's long-term return on investment through toll revenues, which have since supported maintenance and demonstrated economic viability, countering narratives of unchecked overruns by highlighting resolved implementation hurdles and positive lender validations.[54] In contrast, fiscal skeptics point to broader patterns of delays in Pakistani highway projects, where funding shortfalls and bureaucratic inefficiencies have historically inflated costs by 10-15% on average, though M-4's outcomes align more closely with planned benchmarks than many peers.[76][77]

Environmental and Social Concerns

The construction of the M-4 motorway involved land acquisition affecting approximately 3,429 households across the Gojra-Shorkot-Khanewal section, primarily impacting 1,616.7 acres of private agricultural land.[78] Resettlement was managed under Asian Development Bank (ADB) safeguards, with affected persons entitled to compensation for lost assets, livelihood restoration assistance, and relocation support as outlined in the project's Land Acquisition and Resettlement Plan (LARP).[79] In the Gojra-Shorkot segment, 3,674 individuals were directly impacted, facing challenges including loss of farmland, temporary livelihood disruptions, and cultural adjustments during relocation.[49] Local opposition to land acquisition manifested in minor protests, centered on compensation adequacy and acquisition processes, but these were addressed through legal eminent domain procedures and grievance redress mechanisms without escalation to sustained conflict.[17] No verified reports indicate widespread or unresolved social displacement beyond initial phases, with ADB monitoring confirming compliance with involuntary resettlement policies that prioritized rehabilitation over long-term community fragmentation.[50] Environmentally, the project's Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA), approved in 2007 and updated for sections like Gojra-Shorkot-Khanewal, identified construction-phase risks including dust emissions, soil erosion, and noise pollution affecting nearby vegetation and water bodies.[80] Mitigation entailed dust suppression via water spraying, sediment barriers for erosion control, and revegetation of borrow areas, reducing these impacts to temporary and localized levels post-completion.[29] In operation, the motorway's design promotes fuel efficiency through higher speeds and reduced congestion compared to undivided national highways, lowering per-vehicle CO2 emissions despite increased traffic volume; however, roadside dust has led to elevated heavy metals in crops like maize and sugarcane along segments, posing minor contamination risks managed through routine maintenance.[81] No evidence supports claims of irreversible ecological damage, with ongoing environmental management plans focusing on drainage and wildlife crossings to sustain low post-construction impacts.[4]
