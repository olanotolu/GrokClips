Dion Phaneuf

Dion Phaneuf (born 10 April 1985) is a Canadian former professional ice hockey defenseman who played for the Calgary Flames, Toronto Maple Leafs, Ottawa Senators and Los Angeles Kings of the National Hockey League (NHL). He officially retired from professional hockey in November 2021. He was drafted ninth overall in the 2003 NHL entry draft by Calgary and made his NHL debut in 2005 after a four-year junior career with the Red Deer Rebels of the Western Hockey League (WHL), in which he was twice named the Defenceman of the Year. Phaneuf set a Flames record for most goals by a first-year defenceman and was named a finalist for the Calder Memorial Trophy as top rookie in 2005â€“06. Two years later, he was a finalist for the James Norris Memorial Trophy as top defenceman. During his career, Phaneuf was involved in two blockbuster trades. The first came in early 2010, when he was involved in a seven-player trade that saw him move from Calgary to Toronto. Six years later, Phaneuf was the centrepiece of a nine-player trade that saw him sent to Ottawa. He was traded to Los Angeles nearing the trade deadline in February 2018. Phaneuf was a three-time NHL All-Star. He represented Canada internationally five times in his career, winning a silver medal and a gold medal at the World Junior Hockey Championship in 2004 and 2005, respectively, as well as a gold medal at the 2007 Men's World Ice Hockey Championships. Phaneuf is active in the community; the Flames honoured Phaneuf for his role as an ambassador to the Alberta Children's Hospital, where he participated in events that help sick and injured children during his time in Calgary. Phaneuf was a common sight at Toronto events, often appearing at fundraisers, promotional activities or visiting patients at Sick Kids Hospital.

Early life

Dion Phaneuf was born 10 April 1985, in Edmonton, Alberta, to Paul, a construction worker, and Amber, a nurse,[1] both originally from the province of Prince Edward Island.[2] Dion attended Harry Ainlay Composite High School before Hunting Hills High School while with the Red Deer Rebels. Dion has a younger brother, Dane, who played for the P.E.I. Rocket of the Quebec Major Junior Hockey League (QMJHL).[3] Paul and Amber Phaneuf describe their family as close-knit.[4] Paul Phaneuf maintained a backyard rink each winter on which the young Phaneuf practiced. His mother, a former figure skater who competed for Prince Edward Island at the 1975 Canada Winter Games, taught him to skate.[1] Phaneuf also practised his shot relentlessly in the basement of his family's home, frequently breaking the nets his father bought.[4] As a youth, he played in the 1997 Quebec International Pee-Wee Hockey Tournament with the Quebec minor ice hockey team.[5]

Junior

The Red Deer Rebels selected Phaneuf in the third round of the 2000 WHL Bantam Draft. When Toronto hired head coach Mike Babcock the following season, Phaneuf excelled, experiencing fewer penalty minutes and better defensive play under his reduced minutes.

Ottawa Senators (2016â€“2018)

After parts of seven seasons with Toronto, Phaneuf was traded to the Ottawa Senators on 9 February 2016, in a blockbuster nine-player trade.[43] Both fans and media alike viewed the trade with shock, as a significant deal between the arch-rivals was viewed as very unlikely to happen.[44][45][46] Phaneuf, Matt Frattin, prospects Casey Bailey, Ryan Rupert and Cody Donaghey were sent to the Senators in exchange for Jared Cowen, Colin Greening, Milan Michalek, prospect Tobias Lindberg and Ottawa's 2017 second-round draft pick. Due to the Battle of Ontario rivalry, this was the first time the Maple Leafs and Senators had made a trade since 17 March 1998, when Per Gustafsson was sent to Ottawa in exchange for an eighth-round pick.[47] As his usual jersey number 3 was already in use on the team by Marc Methot, Phaneuf took up jersey number 2. Phaneuf played a shortened 2015â€“16 season due to a hairline fracture in his right foot sustained while blocking a shot during a game against the Maple Leafs on March 12 and an abdominal oblique injury suffered on March 22 against the Washington Capitals; he was ruled out for the remainder of the season on March 28, ending with 20 games played with the Senators, amassing one goal and eight points.[48] Phaneuf was named an alternate captain of the Senators at the beginning of the 2016â€“17 season.[49] Phaneuf scored the overtime winner in game two of the Senators' first-round playoff series against the Boston Bruins in the 2017 Stanley Cup Playoffs. The team would be eliminated in the seventh game, double-overtime of the Eastern Conference final by the Pittsburgh Penguins. In the following off-season, Phaneuf was asked by the Senators to waive his no-move clause in his contract for the 2017 NHL expansion draft. He declined and the Senators had to place Phaneuf on its protected list for the draft. In the following season, the Senators stumbled out of the gate. This was later attributed to dissension in the dressing room. After a trade to bring Matt Duchene to Ottawa was followed by poor performances, the team's management started dismantling the team to rebuild. On 13 February 2018, Phaneuf and Nate Thompson were traded to the Los Angeles Kings in exchange for forwards Marian Gaborik and Nick Shore.[50] To complete the deal, Phaneuf had to allow a trade to the Kings under the no-move clause in his contract. As part of the deal, the Senators retained 25% of the remainder of Phaneuf's contract.[51]

Los Angeles Kings and retirement (2018â€“2019)

During the 2018â€“19 season, Phaneuf played in his 1,000th career game on 17 November 2018 in a 5-3 loss to the Nashville Predators.[52] He finished his second season with the Kings recording a career-low one goal and six points and a plus-minus of -21 through 67 games. On 15 June 2019, the Kings bought out the final two years of Phaneuf's contract, making him an unrestricted free agent.[53] On 16 November 2021, Phaneuf announced his retirement from professional ice hockey, over two years after his final game in the NHL.[54] He had spent parts of the previous two seasons job-shadowing with Maple Leafs president Brendan Shanahan.[55][56] The Maple Leafs honoured Phaneuf later that day in a ceremony ahead of their game against the Nashville Predators.[57]

International play

Phaneuf made his international debut for the Canadian junior team at the 2004 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships in Helsinki, earning a leadership role under Head Coach Mario Durocher. A devastating bodycheck to the Czech Republic's Rostislav Olesz led the referee to eject Phaneuf from the semi-final game; he was not suspended, however, and played in the gold medal game. The Canadians lost the final to the United States,[60] while Phaneuf was named a tournament All-Star at defence.[61] The Canadian team at the 2005 World Junior Ice Hockey Championships dominated the tournament, outscoring their opposition 35â€“6 in the games leading up to the gold medal game with Russia.[62] Phaneuf scored a goal and threw a memorable bodycheck that knocked down two Russian players in the game as Canada routed the Russians 6â€“1 to win their first gold medal in eight years.[63][64] He was again named a tournament All-Star,[61] as well as being named the outstanding defenceman of the tournament.[63][65][66] Following the Flames' elimination in the 2007 Stanley Cup playoffs, Phaneuf joined the Canadian senior team at the 2007 Men's World Ice Hockey Championships in Moscow.[67] He recorded eight assists in seven games to help Canada win the gold medal over Finland.[68] Once considered a virtual lock to join Canada at the 2010 Winter Olympics,[69] his struggles in the 2008â€“09 season placed his candidacy in doubt. Team Canada general manager Steve Yzerman continued to express confidence in Phaneuf during a summer camp held in Calgary, but Phaneuf was not among the seven defencemen ultimately selected for the team. After the Maple Leafs missed the 2011 playoffs, Phaneuf served as alternate captain of Canada at the 2011 IIHF World Championship in Bratislava, Slovakia, where Canada finished fifth. Phaneuf was once again alternate captain of the Canadian team at the 2012 World Championship in Helsinki and Stockholm. Canada repeated its fifth-place finish of the prior year. In August 2013, Phaneuf attended the summer orientation camp for Canada in preparation for the 2014 Winter Olympics.[70] However, Phaneuf was not named to the final roster.[71]

Playing style

In junior hockey, Phaneuf was known not only for his physical presence, but also his calm demeanour and offensive ability. He was compared to Hockey Hall of Famer Brent Sutter.[9] Scouts praised his defensive ability, and the poise he showed at both ends of the ice.[9] Praised for his leadership abilities, Phaneuf was named the captain of Team WHL at the 2004 ATD Canada-Russia Challenge,[72] and was counted upon to take a leadership role with the Canadian junior team at the 2004 and 2005 World Junior Championships. Entering his sophomore season, Phaneuf's potential impact on a game was compared to that of Russian star Alexander Ovechkin,[73] and a poll released by ESPN in 2008 revealed that 43% of players asked argued Phaneuf was the hardest hitter in hockey, at the age of 22.[74] He was named an alternate captain by the Flames at the start of the 2008â€“09 season, though Brent Sutter chose to alternate amongst a group of veterans, including Phaneuf, in 2009â€“10.[75] While Phaneuf earned a James Norris Memorial Trophy nomination for his defensive play in 2008, he struggled enough during the 2008â€“09 season that some observers began to question his defensive commitment. Phaneuf's teammates were quick to defend his play and noted that he was among the League leaders in average time on ice per game.[29] His hitting game has also earned criticism at times. The New York Islanders were especially upset at an open-ice charge Phaneuf delivered to Kyle Okposo during a 2009 exhibition game that resulted in the latter suffering a concussion.[76] Phaneuf has also been criticized for occasionally refusing to fight opponents who challenge him after throwing a big hit.[77] For his part, Phaneuf said he does not believe he should be forced to fight after every big hit, though he would do so when necessary. His coaches have praised Phaneuf's mentality.[78] A 2010 Sports Illustrated poll of 272 NHL players saw 21% of respondents name Phaneuf as the most overrated player in the NHL, three times as many votes as any other player. Phaneuf dismissed the poll while several of his current and former teammates expressed their disagreement with the result.[79]

Post-hockey career

On February 23, 2022, it was announced that Phaneuf had taken a role with the CBD and vitamin supplements company, CaniBrands, as the company's Strategic Advisor.[80] He continues to serve as an ambassador for Can-i Wellness, associated with CaniBrands. In late 2022, Phaneuf joined AEG Worldwide in a corporate sales role focused on global partnerships. As of the 2024â€“25 season, he serves as Director of Partnership Development in the Partnership Sales and Activation department.

The Stars later cut ties with Avery permanently.[86][87] Phaneuf and Cuthbert announced their engagement in September 2012,[88] and they married on 6 July 2013 at St. James Catholic Church in Summerfield, Prince Edward Island.[89] During his time with the Toronto Maple Leafs, Phaneuf lived in Toronto, while he and Cuthbert spent their summers at Phaneuf's waterfront estate outside of New London, Prince Edward Island, in his parents' home province.[2][90][91] Cuthbert gave birth to their daughter, Zaphire, in December 2017[92] and their son, Fable, in 2022.
