2023 Jacksonville shooting

The 2023 Jacksonville shooting occurred on August 26, 2023, when Ryan Christopher Palmeter, a 21-year-old white male from Jacksonville, Florida, carried out a targeted attack on Black individuals at a Dollar General store in the city's New Town neighborhood, killing three victims before dying by suicide.[1][2] Palmeter, armed with a handgun and a shotgun marked with swastikas, first shot and killed Angela Michelle Carr, a 52-year-old Black woman, in the store's parking lot as she sat in her vehicle; he then entered the store, where he fatally shot Jerrald De'Shaun Gallion, 29, and Anolt Joseph "A.J." Laguerre Jr., 19, both Black males.[1][2] The attack lasted approximately 11 minutes and was live-streamed by the shooter via a cellphone, during which he used racial slurs and expressed explicit hatred toward Black people.[1][3] Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters immediately classified the incident as a racially motivated hate crime based on evidence including the shooter's writings and the deliberate selection of Black targets in a predominantly Black area.[4][2] Investigators recovered multiple manifestos and notebooks from Palmeter's residence detailing his white supremacist ideology, obsession with prior mass shootings like those in Buffalo and Charleston, and vows of further violence against Black people, with phrases such as "white people deserve their own space" and references to "black vermin."[5][6] These documents, released by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office in January 2024 following the conclusion of the investigation, underscored the causal role of ideological extremism in the attack, independent of mental health claims raised by Palmeter's family.[7][6] The Federal Bureau of Investigation joined local authorities in probing the case as domestic terrorism, confirming Palmeter's legal purchase of the firearms used.[1][2] No prior criminal record or institutional red flags were evident, though the shooter's writings revealed long-held grievances amplified by online radicalization.[5]

Incident Details

Location and Timeline

The shooting occurred at a Dollar General retail store located on Kings Road in the Moncrief neighborhood of Jacksonville, Florida, a predominantly African-American area adjacent to Edward Waters University, a historically Black college.[8][9] On August 26, 2023, the perpetrator left his residence in Clay County at 11:39 a.m. EDT and drove to Jacksonville.[8] He arrived at Edward Waters University around 12:48 p.m., where he equipped himself with a bulletproof vest before departing at 12:57 p.m. after security personnel approached.[8] The gunman reached the Dollar General store at approximately 1:08 p.m., initially firing 11 rounds into a parked Kia sedan, fatally shooting Uber driver Angela Michelle Carr, aged 52, who was waiting for a passenger.[8][10] Entering the store immediately after, the shooter killed store employee Anolt "A.J." Laguerre Jr., aged 29, and pursued fleeing witnesses out the rear door, firing once more at 1:09 p.m.[8] At 1:10 p.m., victim Jerrald De'Shaun Gallion, aged 29, entered the store with his girlfriend; the gunman shot Gallion at 1:13 p.m. after chasing another witness and missing a shot.[8] The perpetrator then retreated to the store's office area by 1:14 p.m. and fatally shot himself, with a gunshot heard by a Jacksonville Sheriff's Office officer at 1:19 p.m.[8] SWAT personnel confirmed the gunman's death at 3:44 p.m.[8] The entire sequence of violence unfolded over roughly 11 minutes.[10]

Victims and Casualties

The shooting at the Dollar General store in Jacksonville, Florida, on August 26, 2023, resulted in three fatalities with no reported injuries among bystanders.[11][12] All victims were Black individuals, and authorities later classified the attack as racially motivated.[13][12] Angela Michelle Carr, aged 52, was the first victim killed; she was shot while sitting in her car in the store's parking lot before the gunman entered the building.[14][15] Carr worked as a diet technician for a company providing meals to nursing homes and was described by family as a beloved mother and church member.[14][15] The remaining two victims were killed inside the store: Anolt Joseph "A.J." Laguerre Jr., a 19-year-old employee at the Dollar General, and Jerrald De'Shaun Gallion, a 29-year-old customer.[12][14] Laguerre, who had recently started working at the store to support his family, was an aspiring online content creator focused on gaming streams.[15][16] Gallion, a Jacksonville resident and father, was shopping at the time of the attack and was known for his community involvement.[15][14]

Perpetrator's Actions

Ryan Christopher Palmeter arrived at the Dollar General store in Jacksonville, Florida, shortly after leaving Edward Waters University around 12:57 p.m. on August 26, 2023, where he had briefly donned a bulletproof vest before being approached by campus security.[8] Armed with a Glock 20 pistol legally purchased on April 6, 2023, and an AR-15-style rifle legally purchased on June 22, 2023â€”both bearing painted swastikas and racial slursâ€”he initiated the attack at approximately 1:08 p.m. by firing 11 rounds into a Kia sedan in the parking lot, fatally wounding the driver.[8] [2] Palmeter then entered the store from the front, where he fatally shot a victim inside before pursuing fleeing witnesses out the rear door and firing once at them around 1:09 p.m.[8] As another victim entered the store at 1:10 p.m., he returned, shooting the individual multiple times at 1:13 p.m., chasing a witness while missing a shot, and firing additional rounds out the door as the witness escaped.[8] [10] At 1:14 p.m., Palmeter entered the store's office area, where he remained until approximately 1:19 p.m., when a single gunshot indicated his suicide by self-inflicted wound to the head, ending the approximately 11-minute rampage.[8] [10] The Jacksonville Sheriff's Office reported that Palmeter appeared "100% lucid" during the attack, with no indications of impairment.[17]

Perpetrator Profile

Personal Background

Ryan Christopher Palmeter, aged 21 at the time of the shooting, resided in Orange Park, a suburb south of Jacksonville, Florida.[18] He was the son of Stephen Palmeter and Maryann Palmeter, with whom he lived at the family home.[19] [20] Palmeter had been involuntarily committed under Florida's Baker Act in 2017 following a mental health crisis, during which authorities expressed concerns about his well-being.[21] [22] Acquaintances and neighbors described him as a socially awkward and introverted individual who kept to himself and had limited interactions with others in the community.[23] Regarding education, Palmeter was a former student at Flagler College, a private liberal arts institution in St. Augustine, Florida, approximately 40 miles south of Jacksonville, though he did not graduate.[24] In terms of employment, he had worked at a Dollar Tree store, part of the same parent company as Family Dollar but distinct from the targeted Dollar General chain.[25] [26] Palmeter had no prior criminal record.[27]

Ideological Motivations

The 2023 Jacksonville shooting was driven by the perpetrator's explicit racial hatred toward Black people, as evidenced by writings left at his residence and racial slurs uttered during the attack.[4] [28] Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters described the incident as a "racially motivated" assault, with the shooter targeting victims based on their race after surveilling a predominantly Black neighborhood.[1] The Federal Bureau of Investigation corroborated this, stating that the perpetrator harbored "hatred toward African Americans" and had prepared materials outlining his animosity.[28] Investigators recovered multiple documents from the shooter's home, including journals and a manifesto released publicly by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office on January 19, 2024, after concluding the federal investigation.[7] These writings articulated virulent anti-Black racism, with the manifesto invoking white supremacist concepts such as the "Day of the Rope"â€”a reference to mass executions of perceived enemies in the neo-Nazi novel The Turner Diaries.[5] The documents expressed a desire for racial separation and violence, including phrases like "Expect more of me" and vows to reclaim territory, aligning with accelerationist white supremacist ideologies that seek to provoke societal collapse through targeted attacks.[6] [5] Physical evidence from the crime scene further supported these motivations: the firearms usedâ€”one a Glock pistol and two shotgun-style weaponsâ€”were spray-painted with swastikas, a symbol associated with neo-Nazi extremism.[1] No affiliations with organized groups were identified, but the combination of symbolic markings, preparatory writings, and selective targeting of Black victims indicated self-radicalized white supremacist beliefs rather than isolated personal grievances.[5] [1] The Anti-Defamation League analyzed the released materials as demonstrating "a wide range of hate," including antisemitic and anti-Black elements typical of online extremist forums.[5]

Manifesto Contents

The manifesto, a 27-page handwritten document authored by Ryan Christopher Palmeter, was publicly released by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office on January 19, 2024, following the conclusion of the investigation into the August 26, 2023, shooting.[7] Copies of the document were left in Palmeter's vehicle for his parents, the media, and federal authorities, indicating an intent to propagate its contents post-attack.[7] The text articulates explicit racial hatred, particularly targeting Black individuals, with the term "n*****" appearing 183 times and advocating for "total n***** death" (TND) based on claims of inherent Black savagery, criminality, and lack of empathy attributed to biological factors like lower oxytocin levels.[7][5] It promotes white supremacist ideology through racial hierarchies, praising white dominance and referencing concepts like "Agartha" in an Aryan context, while denigrating other groups including Jews (accused of media control to conceal Black crimes), Arabs, Latinos, and Han Chinese.[6][5] Ideological motivations draw from extremist figures and texts, citing Adolf Hitler positively (defending against censorship of his words), Timothy McVeigh, Anders Breivik, and Brenton Tarrant as inspirations, with Tarrant noted as the primary model for targeting methods.[7][5] It invokes "Day of the Rope" from The Turner Diaries, calling for the hanging of "race traitors," and extends hatred to the LGBTQ community, labeling them as societal decay requiring extermination.[5] Additional calls to violence include "Democracy has failed. Kill your politicians" and an urging for others to initiate similar acts, framed as "the journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step."[6] Key excerpts emphasize propagation of violence and racial separatism, such as: "No matter what happens, there will be more of me. Expect more of me. By God, weâ€™ll have our home," positioning the attack as part of a broader reclamation effort.[6] The document also reveals tactical considerations, noting travel to Florida for stricter gun laws to avoid intervention by "white people I know."[6] While exempting figures like Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas from genocidal plans, it broadly justifies the Jacksonville rampage as retaliation against perceived racial threats.[6]

Investigation and Evidence

Official Response

The Jacksonville Sheriff's Office (JSO) secured the scene shortly after the shooting on August 26, 2023, confirming three Black victims killed and the white male perpetrator found dead from a self-inflicted gunshot wound. Sheriff T.K. Waters described the attack as "one of the most despicable" he had seen, citing evidence from the gunman's vehicleâ€”including swastika markings on weapons and racially charged writingsâ€”as indicating a targeted assault on African Americans, classifying it preliminarily as a hate crime.[9] The Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) immediately opened a domestic terrorism probe alongside a civil rights violation inquiry, assuming joint leadership with JSO and deploying agents to analyze the manifesto and digital footprints left by Ryan Christopher Palmeter. FBI Director Christopher Wray coordinated a national call with faith leaders and law enforcement on August 28, 2023, emphasizing the bureau's focus on racially motivated violence.[29][29] The U.S. Department of Justice, under Attorney General Merrick B. Garland, pledged full resources to the federal investigation on August 27, 2023, stating that the "heinous act of violence" warranted thorough examination for hate crime statutes.[30] Florida Governor Ron DeSantis condemned the "racially motivated" murders on August 26, 2023, directing the Florida Department of Law Enforcement to support local probes and allocating $1.1 million in state funds for campus security enhancements at nearby Edward Waters University, a historically Black institution.[31][32] President Joe Biden issued a statement on August 27, 2023, expressing prayers for the victims' families and asserting that "white supremacy has no place in America," while urging rejection of hate-fueled violence targeting Black communities.[33]

Weapons and Forensics

The perpetrator, Ryan Christopher Palmeter, was armed with a Glock handgun and an AR-15-style semi-automatic rifle during the attack on August 26, 2023.[34][35] The rifle was decorated with swastikas, consistent with the perpetrator's documented white supremacist ideology.[36] Both weapons were legally purchased by Palmeter's mother in 2021, despite his prior involuntary commitment under Florida's Baker Act in 2017 for mental health reasons, which did not result in a federal prohibition on firearm ownership due to state reporting limitations at the time.[21][22][37] Forensic examination, including ballistics testing and review of store surveillance footage, confirmed that the three victimsâ€”Angela Michelle Carr, Jerrald Gallion, and Anolt Joseph Laguerre Jr.â€”sustained fatal gunshot wounds from the perpetrator's firearms during the sequence of events inside and adjacent to the Dollar General store.[13][10] The perpetrator then fled to a nearby location and died from a self-inflicted gunshot wound using one of the handguns, as determined by autopsy and scene analysis conducted by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office and medical examiner.[13][2] No additional suspects or weapons were identified in the investigation, which relied on recovered casings, projectiles, and video evidence to reconstruct the 11-minute incident.[10][38]

Mental Health Factors

In 2017, at age 15, Ryan Christopher Palmeter was involuntarily detained under Florida's Baker Act for a 72-hour mental health evaluation following concerns about his well-being.[39] [22] The petition originated in Clark County, Nevada, where his family resided at the time, prompting his transport and evaluation in Florida after relocation.[40] Baker Act proceedings allow for such holds when individuals pose a risk of harm to themselves or others or appear gravely impaired, but Palmeter was released afterward without public disclosure of a formal diagnosis or ongoing treatment requirements.[41] Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters confirmed during press conferences that this evaluation did not result in criminal charges or a prohibiting mental health adjudication under state or federal law, enabling Palmeter's later legal firearm purchases in 2023.[42] [2] No additional psychiatric records, therapy involvement, or medication history have been released by authorities, though classmates described him as introverted and socially awkward in high school, potentially indicative of isolation rather than clinical pathology.[43] Prior to the August 26, 2023, shooting, Palmeter's father called 911 expressing alarm over his son's armed departure from home while wearing a mask and acting erratically, but this was framed as immediate behavioral concern rather than a diagnosed condition.[20] Sheriff Waters described Palmeter as "100% lucid" in planning and executing the attack, underscoring that while the 2017 incident flagged early vulnerabilities, it did not preclude deliberate ideological intent as evidenced by his manifesto.[17] Empirical assessments post-incident prioritized racial animus over acute psychosis, with no forensic psychology reports attributing the violence primarily to untreated mental illness.[4]

Reactions and Responses

Political Statements

President Joe Biden issued a statement on August 27, 2023, condemning the shooting as an act "fueled by hate-filled animus" and asserting that "Jill and I are praying for the victims, their families, and the people of Jacksonville."[33] He emphasized that white supremacy has "no place in America," describing it as a "poison" and urging refusal to accept a country where Black individuals live in fear of gun violence.[44] On August 28, during remarks marking the 60th anniversary of the March on Washington, Biden linked the incident to broader "hate-fueled violence," calling for action to combat it and stating, "Silence is complicity and we're not going to remain silent."[45] Vice President Kamala Harris released a statement on the same day, echoing Biden's condemnation and offering prayers for the victims' families while pledging federal support for the investigation.[46] Florida Governor Ron DeSantis described the shooting as "racially motivated" on August 26, 2023, calling the perpetrator a "scumbag" and vowing that such acts would not be tolerated.[31] At a vigil for the victims on August 27, he labeled the violence "totally unacceptable" and affirmed, "We are not going to let people be targeted based on their race."[47] DeSantis faced boos from the crowd at the event, amid criticisms from some Democrats attributing heightened racial tensions to his state policies on education and history.[48] On September 7, he confronted an audience member at a news conference who blamed him for fostering conditions leading to the shooting.[49] Former President Donald Trump did not issue a public statement on the shooting as of August 30, 2023.[50] Republican presidential candidate Vivek Ramaswamy condemned the violence but argued against politicizing it, drawing parallels to media handling of other incidents and stating there is "no place in America for racially inspired violence."[51] [52] Florida State Senator Tracie Davis, a Democrat, stated on August 27, 2023, that the victims "were gunned down because they were Black," highlighting persistent racial violence despite the year being 2023.[53]

Community and Advocacy

In the days following the August 26, 2023, shooting, the Jacksonville community organized multiple prayer vigils and church services to mourn the victims, Angela Michelle Carr, Anolt Joseph "AJ" Laguerre Jr., and Jerrald Gallion. Hundreds gathered at locations including Saint Paul A.M.E. Church, where attendees condemned racism and called for rejecting hate while pleading for tolerance and unity.[54][55][56] These events drew frustration from participants, with Florida Governor Ron DeSantis facing boos during his appearance at one vigil, amid broader expressions of exhaustion over repeated racially motivated violence.[48][57] Advocacy organizations swiftly issued statements denouncing the attack as racially motivated white supremacist violence. The NAACP Legal Defense Fund described the killings as a targeted racist act, attributing racism to learned behavior and criticizing public figures for advancing divisive rhetoric that it claimed contributed to such incidents.[58] Everytown for Gun Safety and its affiliates, Moms Demand Action and Students Demand Action, highlighted the use of an AR-15 rifle and Glock handgun in the "racist act of violence," urging federal action to address hate-motivated gun violence, including support for measures like the Disarm Hate Act to restrict firearm access for those with hate crime convictions.[34][59] Other groups, such as the National Urban League and Advancing Justice | AAJC, echoed calls for nationwide condemnation of white supremacist ideology and renewed efforts against systemic racism.[60][61] Community leaders and residents linked the shooting to historical racial violence in Jacksonville, such as the 1960 Ax Handle Saturday attacks, demanding proactive measures against white supremacy rather than reactive mourning. Black residents expressed demands for policy actions targeting extremist ideologies, with some advocacy tying the event to broader critiques of state education laws perceived as downplaying racism's history.[62][63] One year later, on August 25, 2024, a memorial at the Kings Road site featured remarks from Mayor Donna Deegan emphasizing resolve against the "racist mass shooting," underscoring ongoing community efforts to honor victims and combat hate.[64]

Media Coverage

The 2023 Jacksonville shooting garnered immediate national and international media attention, with outlets like CNN, NBC News, and the BBC reporting on August 27 that Jacksonville Sheriff T.K. Waters had classified the attack as racially motivated based on the shooter's writings expressing white supremacist ideology and his use of racial slurs during the incident.[13][65][66] Coverage This framing, while grounded in official statements, drew implicit critique from conservative perspectives for aligning with narratives of systemic white supremacy amid empirical evidence of the shooter's isolated radicalization rather than organized group ties.[1]

Broader Implications

Racial and Ideological Context

The 2023 Jacksonville shooting was officially classified as a racially motivated hate crime by the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office, based on the perpetrator's targeted selection of Black victims and accompanying writings that explicitly expressed anti-Black hatred. Comparisons to Similar Events

The 2023 Jacksonville shooting shares key characteristics with prior racially motivated mass shootings in the United States perpetrated by white supremacists targeting minority groups, including the use of legally acquired firearms, ideological manifestos or writings espousing replacement theory and racial hatred, and selection of everyday commercial or public venues frequented by the intended victims.[5][1] In each case, the attackers were young white males radicalized online, driven by explicit anti-Black or anti-minority animus rather than personal grievances, and aimed to incite broader racial conflict.[74][75] A prominent parallel is the 2022 Buffalo supermarket shooting, where 18-year-old Payton Gendron killed 10 Black individuals at a Tops store on May 14, 2022, motivated by white supremacist ideology and the "great replacement" theory outlined in his manifesto.[76] Like Ryan Palmeter in Jacksonville, Gendron wore tactical gear, livestreamed the attack, and referenced accelerationist goals to provoke racial civil war, drawing from online extremist forums.[5][77] Both incidents occurred in retail settings serving predominantly Black communities, underscoring a pattern of targeting spaces symbolizing routine minority life.[78] The 2015 Charleston church shooting by 21-year-old Dylann Roof, who murdered nine Black parishioners at Emanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church on June 17, 2015, also mirrors Jacksonville in its explicit anti-Black supremacist rationale, with Roof authoring a manifesto decrying interracial mixing and citing historical grievances.[75] Roof, like Palmeter, had no prior criminal record but was steeped in neo-Nazi and white nationalist materials, using the attack to spark racial violence.[5][69]

policies and national mental health shortcomings as exacerbating vulnerabilities among the unstable, echoing patterns in other incidents where ideological triggers intersected with systemic
