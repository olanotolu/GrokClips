Elizabeth Haysom

Elizabeth Roxanne Haysom (born c. 1964) is a Canadian woman who pleaded guilty in 1987 to two counts of accessory before the fact to first-degree murder for her role in the planning of the 1985 stabbing deaths of her parents, Derek William Haysom (aged 72) and Nancy Astor Haysom (aged 53), in their Bedford County, Virginia home.[1][2] Her then-boyfriend, German national Jens Söring, was convicted by jury in 1990 of committing the murders themselves, receiving two life sentences.[3] Haysom, who met Söring while both were students at the University of Virginia, fled the United States with him after the killings, passing forged checks across Europe before their arrest in London in 1986 on fraud charges that led to the murder investigation.[1] The case drew international attention due to extradition battles, including Söring's successful appeal to the European Court of Human Rights against potential Virginia death penalty exposure, and ongoing disputes over culpability, with Söring maintaining he falsely confessed to shield Haysom while she has portrayed him as the sole perpetrator.[2][4] Sentenced to consecutive 45-year terms totaling 90 years, Haysom served approximately 34 years in Virginia prisons before the state Parole Board granted her release on November 25, 2019, conditioned on deportation.[5][6] U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement took custody upon her prison release and removed her to Canada on January 22, 2020, where she reportedly resides in western provinces under restrictions prohibiting contact with Söring.[7][8] The murders involved over 30 stab wounds each to the victims, ruled as defensive wounds indicating resistance, with forensic evidence linking Söring's blood type to the scene.[1]

Early Life and Background

Childhood and Family Dynamics

Elizabeth Roxanne Haysom was born in 1964 in Salisbury, Rhodesia (now Harare, Zimbabwe), to Derek Haysom, a South African-born engineer and executive in the steel industry, and Nancy (known as Cita) Haysom, an American whose family had ties to Virginia aristocracy.[9][1] Her parents married in Johannesburg, South Africa, in 1960, each bringing five children from prior marriages, with Elizabeth as their sole child together.[9] Derek's career necessitated frequent international relocations across Africa, Canada—where the family settled in Nova Scotia and he managed a steel mill—Great Britain, and Luxembourg, exposing Elizabeth to an upper-class but peripatetic existence.[1][9] Family dynamics were strained, with Derek portrayed as publicly gregarious yet privately authoritarian and reserved; the mother-daughter relationship between Nancy and Elizabeth was particularly tense, compounded by parental alcohol use that Elizabeth later documented in writings, such as references to her mother's frequent gin consumption amid household volatility.[1] During her teenage years at Wycombe Abbey, a boarding school in England, Elizabeth displayed early rebellious tendencies, including botching an interview for Cambridge University, skipping her A-level examinations, eloping with a female companion, and using drugs during travels across Europe.[1] These behaviors reflected her push for independence amid the family's unstable relational structure, though Nancy's connections eventually prompted retirement to Lynchburg, Virginia, from Nova Scotia in the years preceding 1985.[9]

Education and Early Behaviors

Elizabeth Haysom attended Wycombe Abbey, an elite boarding school for girls located outside London, England.[1][10] During her final year there, she openly experimented with drugs and explored her sexuality at a time when homosexuality remained stigmatized in 1983 Britain.[10] Following Wycombe Abbey, Haysom skipped her A-level examinations and botched an interview for Trinity College, Cambridge, reflecting early academic instability.[1] She subsequently traveled across Europe with a female lover, during which she continued using drugs, before returning to live with her parents in Nova Scotia.[1] In the late summer of 1984, at age 20, she enrolled as a freshman Echols Scholar at the University of Virginia, though her university tenure proved brief amid ongoing personal challenges.[1] Haysom exhibited patterns of substance abuse prior to 1985, including a documented history of heavy alcohol consumption starting around age 10 and experimentation with illicit drugs such as marijuana, LSD, and heroin.[11][12] These behaviors contributed to her erratic lifestyle, including running away from school and family oversight, and were later cited in legal contexts as indicative of impulse control issues associated with a borderline personality disorder diagnosis.[1]

Relationship with Jens Soering

Initial Meeting and Romantic Involvement

Elizabeth Haysom, aged 20 and an Echols Scholar at the University of Virginia, met Jens Soering, an 18-year-old German freshman and Jefferson Scholar, at a university orientation event in late summer 1984.[1] Soering, born in Thailand to a German diplomat father and educated partly in the American South, arrived as an exchange student on a full academic scholarship.[1][13] Haysom took the initiative in pursuing Soering, confessing her love to him shortly after their introduction, which led to their first date at a campus movie screening in October 1984.[1] By November, they frequented the student hangout known as the Tree House, and by December, their bond had intensified into declarations of profound mutual affection, fostering a codependent dynamic.[1] Both shared intellectual pretensions, viewing themselves as exceptional scholars disdainful of conventional authority, including Haysom's parents who actively opposed the relationship and threatened Soering's expulsion from UVA.[1][14] Haysom shaped the relationship's trajectory, drawing the more sheltered Soering into her established rebellious patterns, including past experimentation with drugs such as heroin and marijuana, evidenced by a needle mark she displayed to him in January 1985.[1] This influence aligned with their anti-establishment views, positioning their romance as a defiant escape from familial and institutional constraints.[1]

Mutual Influences and Dependencies

Haysom and Soering formed a symbiotic psychological bond rooted in shared substance use and intellectual exchanges that cultivated a sense of mutual exceptionalism. Haysom, portraying herself as sophisticated and rebellious, introduced the more sheltered Soering to drugs such as heroin and LSD, which they consumed together, fostering emotional dependency and a distorted worldview detached from conventional norms.[15][16] This experimentation exacerbated their isolation, with Soering accruing debts to dealers under Haysom's influence, binding them through shared risk and secrecy.[17] Haysom confided deeply in Soering about her familial resentments, articulating profound hatred toward her parents and alleging long-term sexual and emotional abuse by her mother, claims that Soering internalized and echoed in their private communications.[18][19] These disclosures positioned Soering as Haysom's confidant and protector, reinforcing his emotional investment while amplifying her narrative of victimhood and justification for defiance. Their correspondence revealed Haysom exerting persuasive control, leveraging Soering's affections to shape his perceptions and actions, as evidenced by letters depicting her as the dominant force in their dynamic.[20] This interplay created enabling dependencies: Haysom drew validation and agency from Soering's devotion, while he derived purpose from aligning with her worldview, unmoored from external accountability. Such mutual reinforcement, unverified by independent corroboration at the time, sustained their insular ideology, prioritizing personal allegiance over rational restraint.[21]

The 1985 Murders

Circumstances and Execution of the Crimes

On April 3, 1985, the bodies of Derek Haysom, aged 72, and his wife Nancy Haysom, aged 53, were discovered by a family friend, Annie Massie, at their home known as Loose Chippings on Holcomb Rock Road in Bedford County, Virginia, just outside Lynchburg.[1] The autopsies determined the couple had been killed around March 30, 1985, by repeated stabbing with knives, with both victims suffering deep throat slashes nearly severing their heads; Derek sustained 36 stab wounds to the chest, back, and other areas, while Nancy endured at least 38 such wounds in a similarly brutal assault.[1][22] The sheer volume of injuries, including defensive wounds on Derek's hands and arms, pointed to a prolonged, rage-driven confrontation rather than a quick execution.[23] No evidence of forced entry was present at the residence, with doors and windows intact, implying the attacker gained access through familiarity or invitation.[1][22] The crime scene revealed place settings and remnants of a meal on the dining room table, suggesting the attack followed dinner; Nancy was found in the kitchen in a housecoat with a small pool of blood around her head, while Derek lay nearby with massive throat trauma that severed his windpipe and jugular vein.[1][23] Bloody footprints—impressions from socks (approximately 9.5 inches long) and a small tennis shoe (size 6.5–7.5 women's or 5–6 men's)—trailed through the dining room, kitchen, and living areas, alongside a bloody palm print on a dining chair and no missing valuables.[1][23] Serological analysis identified four blood types at the scene: A and AB from the victims, plus B and O from unknown sources on items like a damp rag, screen door, and various swabs.[1] Elizabeth Haysom, the couple's 20-year-old daughter, reported being in Washington, D.C., with her boyfriend Jens Soering over the March 29–31 weekend, citing attendance at events like the Rocky Horror Picture Show as an alibi.[23] However, investigators noted handwriting on alibi timelines—written on Marriott Hotel notepaper in Soering's script detailing parking at National Airport and show times—along with joint diary entries by Haysom and Soering, which raised questions about premeditation and fabrication.[23] No murder weapon was recovered, but the wounds' depth required a sharp, heavy blade capable of cutting through muscle, nerves, and arteries.[23] The local community was stunned, prompting a multi-agency task force to process evidence collected between April 3 and 9, with initial forensic reports issued by August 12, 1985.[1][23]

Motives: Personal Conflicts and Alleged Abuses

Elizabeth Haysom developed profound resentment toward her parents, Derek and Nancy Haysom, stemming from their disapproval of her relationship with Jens Soering, her escalating drug use, and her rejection of the refined lifestyle they expected of her. The couple had hosted Soering for dinner in early 1985, during which they explicitly forbade further contact with their daughter and threatened to disinherit her if she continued the association, viewing it as incompatible with their social standards and her future prospects.[24][10] Haysom's personal writings and correspondence prior to the murders repeatedly articulated wishes that her parents were dead, reflecting a pattern of verbalized hostility amid ongoing family tensions, though she later maintained these were not literal calls to action. Such expressions occurred against a backdrop of her admitted fabrications and inconsistent narratives, including false alibis and shifting accounts of events, which undermined the reliability of her self-reported emotional states.[25][22] Central to Haysom's inferred motives were allegations of physical and sexual abuse by her mother, which she claimed fueled her animosity and prompted discussions of eliminating the parental "threat" to her autonomy; these assertions, however, lacked corroborating evidence from independent witnesses or records and aligned with her history of crafting dramatic personal histories to elicit sympathy or justify rebellion. Family dynamics were further strained by untreated substance abuse issues, including Haysom's own involvement with drugs like cocaine and hallucinogens, which exacerbated conflicts over her behavior and financial dependence, yet provided no causal absolution for plotting or facilitating violence.[26][27][1] Prosecutors emphasized self-interested elements in Haysom's motives, portraying the murders as a means to secure independence from parental oversight and potential loss of inheritance, rather than purely reactive to alleged pathologies; Haysom's guilty plea to accessory charges acknowledged her role in orchestrating the outcome while denying direct participation, a position consistent with her pattern of partial admissions tailored to legal advantage.[22][28]

Post-Murder Actions and Capture

Flight, Fraud, and Lifestyle in Europe

Following the murders on March 30, 1985, Soering and Haysom remained in the United States for approximately six months, dropping out of the University of Virginia and relocating to Washington, D.C., where they sustained themselves through fraudulent use of Derek Haysom's credit cards for travel, lodging, and daily expenses. This period involved initial schemes of financial deception, including unauthorized charges totaling thousands of dollars, as they evaded scrutiny while maintaining a semblance of normalcy.[1] In October 1985, the pair fled internationally, rendezvousing in Paris, France, under the Arc de Triomphe before proceeding to Ettelbruck, Luxembourg, and embarking on an itinerant route through France, Switzerland, Italy, Yugoslavia, and toward Bulgaria and Turkey.[1] A rented Fiat crashed near the Bulgarian border, prompting them to fly to Bangkok, Thailand, from where they traveled by bus to Singapore and then to Moscow, Russia, spending New Year's Eve 1985 at the Sheremetyevo airport hotel.[1] Adopting false identities such as Tim and Julia Holte, they eventually settled transiently in Canterbury, England, embodying a nomadic existence marked by constant movement across Asia and Europe to avoid detection.[1] To finance their lifestyle, Soering and Haysom escalated fraud operations in Europe, opening bank accounts under aliases at institutions like Lloyds in Canterbury to obtain check-guarantee cards, followed by a systematic scheme in London beginning February 24, 1986, involving over 120 checks cashed at Marks & Spencer stores for approximately £6,000 (equivalent to about $9,000 USD at the time) through purchases of jackets redeemed for cash.[1] They extended similar deceptions in Bath, England, using identities Tara Lucy Noe and Christopher Platt Noe, relying on disguises like wigs and varying appearances to perpetrate these crimes without immediate consequence.[1] This criminal opportunism supported a drug-influenced routine, with Haysom reportedly engaging in heroin use amid their peripatetic habits.[1] The couple's relationship deteriorated amid mounting paranoia, as they employed aliases even in private communications and grew increasingly suspicious of external threats and each other's reliability.[1] Mutual dependencies intertwined with accusations, fostering internal conflicts that highlighted the fragility of their bond under the pressures of evasion and shared culpability in ongoing fraud.[1]

Arrest and Initial Interrogations

On April 30, 1986, Elizabeth Haysom and Jens Soering were arrested in London, England, for check fraud after passing forged checks under the aliases Tara Lucy Noe and Christopher P. Noe, respectively.[29][23] British authorities, upon verifying their identities during the fraud investigation, discovered Haysom's connection to the unsolved murders of her parents, Derek and Nancy Haysom, in Bedford County, Virginia, the previous year; she had been a person of interest since providing inconsistent statements shortly after the killings.[1] Initial joint and separate interrogations by British detectives and Virginia investigators began in early June 1986, focusing on the murders after the fraud charges. Haysom, questioned on June 8, admitted her involvement as a planner and accessory, implicating Soering as the actual perpetrator who carried out the stabbings.[30] Soering, in sessions starting June 5, initially denied direct responsibility for the killings but quickly shifted, confessing in detail to committing the murders alone while implicating Haysom as the instigator who urged him to act amid family conflicts.[23][14] These early statements exposed significant inconsistencies between their accounts and prior alibis, including fabricated claims of being in Washington, D.C., on the night of March 30, 1985, contradicted by rental car records, witness sightings near the crime scene, and their immediate flight to Asia afterward.[23] Interrogators noted attempts to coordinate stories, such as mutual references to Haysom's alleged physical abuse by her father as motive, which lacked corroboration and varied in specifics; Soering's confessions included errors on details like victim clothing and wound counts, later attributed by him to protectiveness toward Haysom but revealing narrative discrepancies under scrutiny.[30][14]

Legal Proceedings and Convictions

Extradition from the UK

Elizabeth Haysom and Jens Soering were arrested in London on suspicion of check fraud in June 1986, prompting a formal US extradition request on August 11, 1986, under the 1972 US-UK Extradition Treaty for charges related to the 1985 murders of Haysom's parents in Virginia.[31] The UK's historical reluctance to extradite to jurisdictions permitting capital punishment, where Haysom faced potential charges carrying the death penalty as an accessory before the fact to capital murder, necessitated diplomatic negotiations; Virginia authorities provided assurances that execution would not be sought, addressing concerns over the treaty's compatibility with UK human rights standards.[32] Haysom, a Canadian citizen, chose not to contest the extradition, distinguishing her case from Soering's prolonged resistance. On April 15, 1987, a London magistrate ruled in favor of extradition, finding sufficient evidence under treaty requirements for her committal on the accessory charges.[33] Her cooperation with investigators, including admissions detailing her role in plotting the killings, streamlined the evidentiary review and avoided challenges to confession voluntariness or procedural fairness that complicated Soering's proceedings.[34] The UK Home Secretary approved the order without appeal, leading to Haysom's surrender to US marshals on May 8, 1987, marking the completion of her extradition ahead of Soering's, whose parallel appeals to the European Court of Human Rights—invoking Article 3 protections against inhuman treatment via youth (age 18 at confession), alleged coerced statements, and anticipated death row conditions—were rejected in July 1989.[35][36] This outcome underscored sovereignty tensions in transatlantic extraditions, where UK courts prioritized treaty obligations and US assurances over broader human rights objections raised by Soering.[37]

Trials, Plea, and Sentencing

In August 1987, Elizabeth Haysom, aged 23, appeared before the Bedford County Circuit Court in Virginia for a plea hearing on charges related to the 1985 murders of her parents, Derek and Nancy Haysom.[38] On August 24, she entered an unexpected guilty plea to two counts of accessory before the fact to murder, admitting under oath that she had counseled, procured, and commanded Jens Soering to commit the killings due to escalating family conflicts over their relationship and her lifestyle.[39][40] The plea was entered without a formal plea agreement, and Haysom denied allegations of sexual abuse by her mother during the proceedings, attributing the motive instead to her parents' disapproval of Soering and attempts to control her independence.[41] Haysom's guilty plea established her as the primary instigator, with testimony detailing how she had discussed killing her parents with Soering over months, providing him details on their habits and vulnerabilities to facilitate the attack.[42] In October 1987, Judge William Sweeney sentenced her to 90 years in prison, comprising two consecutive 45-year terms, one for each count, emphasizing the premeditated nature of the crimes and her central role in orchestrating the brutal stabbings that inflicted over 30 wounds on each victim.[28][41] During sentencing, Haysom stated she deserved life imprisonment, acknowledging the gravity of her actions in inciting the violence.[28] Soering's trial followed in 1990 in the same Bedford County Circuit Court, where he was charged with two counts of first-degree murder.[3] Despite pleading not guilty and claiming his earlier confessions were made to protect Haysom, a jury convicted him after five days of deliberations, relying on his detailed post-arrest statements describing the attack's execution, including the use of knives from the Haysom home and the sequence of stabbings.[3][22] Prosecutors presented physical evidence such as type O blood traces at the scene consistent with Soering's blood type, excessive mileage on the rental car used during the murders indicating travel to the crime scene, and forged checks cashed by the pair afterward, alongside motive testimony linking the killings to Haysom's influence and shared resentment toward her parents.[43][22] Haysom testified as a prosecution witness, reaffirming her role in planning and his direct commission of the acts, which the court found corroborated the premeditated brutality of the assault.[42] The jury's verdict upheld judicial findings of Soering's sole responsibility for the physical murders under Haysom's direction, rejecting defenses of duress or alternative perpetrators.[3] He received two consecutive life sentences, reflecting the deliberate and savage nature of the crimes, with no possibility of parole for the first 25 years on each count. The sentences for both defendants underscored the court's determination of a joint scheme marked by extensive planning and execution, prioritizing punishment for the unprovoked violence against the victims.[28][3]

Imprisonment, Appeals, and Parole

Prison Term and Rehabilitation Efforts

Elizabeth Haysom began serving her 90-year sentence in 1987 after pleading guilty to two counts of accessory before the fact to murder.[1] She was transferred to Fluvanna Correctional Center for Women in 1998, where she remained incarcerated until her parole in 2019.[1] During her imprisonment at Fluvanna, Haysom participated in multiple rehabilitation programs, including a computer-aided design (CAD) course through which she obtained certification from the American Drafting Design Association and later instructed other inmates, with some of her students contributing work to Boeing.[1] In 2007, she became certified as a Braille transcriber, producing transcribed books as part of the effort.[1] She also trained rescue dogs through a prison canine program, stating that "this program is the epitome of rehabilitation to me" for providing second chances to both animals and inmates.[44] Additionally, Haysom pursued a bachelor's degree via distance learning from Ohio University and contributed writings on the value of prison education, emphasizing its role in personal transformation.[1] [45] Haysom's behavioral patterns in custody included quitting smoking and were described by her cousin Phyllis Workman as indicative of an "entirely different" individual compared to her pre-incarceration self.[1] However, she maintained romantic correspondence with Jens Soering during their respective prison terms, exchanging handwritten letters that expressed mutual attraction and affection even after decades apart.[13] Unlike Soering, who accrued no disciplinary infractions over more than 25 years at Buckingham Correctional Center and publicly detailed his experiences through writings and appeals, Haysom engaged less visibly with external audiences.[1] Expressions of remorse from Haysom were primarily private; her half-brother Veryan Haysom described them as genuine, with her referring to the murders as "my crime" while denying physical presence at the scene.[1] In contrast to Soering's more public articulations of regret amid his innocence claims, Haysom rarely granted interviews or issued statements, limiting observable contrition to family communications and program-related reflections.[13] [1]

Parole Process and 2019 Release

Elizabeth Haysom's initial parole application was denied in 1995, with the Virginia Parole Board citing the 90-year length of her sentence and the violent nature of the 1985 stabbings of her parents, Derek and Nancy Haysom.[46] Subsequent parole requests were also denied multiple times over the following decades, consistent with Virginia's low parole grant rate, though specific reasons for later denials were not publicly detailed beyond general considerations of offense severity and institutional record.[47] On November 25, 2019, the Virginia Parole Board granted Haysom parole after she had served approximately 32 years of her sentence, simultaneously approving parole for her co-defendant Jens Soering.[48] The board's stated rationale emphasized Haysom's age of 20 at the time of the offense, the extended duration of incarceration, positive institutional behavior, completion of rehabilitative programs, and a structured release plan involving immediate transfer to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) custody.[47] This decision did not constitute a pardon—Governor Ralph Northam explicitly rejected Soering's separate pardon request after review, leaving Haysom's conviction intact—and parole was conditioned on deportation to Canada, her country of citizenship, with any failure to deport triggering immediate return to Virginia Department of Corrections custody.[48][49] The parole outcome drew scrutiny from law enforcement and case investigators, who expressed concerns over the adequacy of rehabilitation given the premeditated nature of the crimes and Haysom's role as accessory before and after the fact, despite her participation in prison programs.[50][51] Critics highlighted that while time served met statutory considerations, the board's criteria did not fully address persistent questions about genuine accountability for facilitating the murders amid family opposition to leniency in related proceedings.[5]

Controversies Surrounding Guilt and Evidence

Claims of Manipulation and Soering's Innocence Assertions

Jens Soering recanted his initial confession to the murders of Derek and Nancy Haysom shortly after providing it to authorities in 1986, asserting that he had falsely admitted guilt to protect Elizabeth Haysom from extradition to Virginia, where she faced potential execution as the primary perpetrator.[1] Soering maintained that Haysom either committed the killings herself during his brief absence from the Haysom residence on March 30, 1985, or had orchestrated them through psychological dominance over him, portraying their relationship as one in which she held manipulative control due to her stronger personality and his youthful infatuation.[52] He emphasized in post-trial statements and writings that his willingness to confess stemmed from a romantic idealization, likening it to sacrificial acts in literature, but insisted no physical evidence tied him directly to the stabbings beyond his recanted admissions.[1] In Haysom's 1987 plea proceedings and Soering's subsequent 1990 trial, she testified under oath that Soering alone carried out the attacks, describing him as obsessively devoted and claiming she had only discussed frustrations with her parents without inciting violence.[53] Haysom admitted to prior lies about the case, including inconsistencies in her accounts of the couple's activities, but denied direct responsibility or manipulation, attributing the planning and execution to Soering's initiative after arguments about their future.[54] During Soering's trial, he countered by testifying that Haysom had stabbed her parents upon his return from fetching cigarettes, alleging she fabricated his involvement to shift blame while he covered for her out of love.[55] Soering's narrative of Haysom's dominance faced skepticism in court, where prosecutors highlighted inconsistencies between his recantation and details only the killer would know, such as wound descriptions matching his confessions but not verifiable independently at the time.[56] Appeals courts, including the Virginia Supreme Court in 1994, upheld his conviction, finding sufficient evidence that his initial statements were voluntary and corroborated by Haysom's testimony and the couple's post-murder behavior, rejecting claims of her sole culpability as self-serving without supporting proof.[52] Haysom, in her guilty plea to accessory charges, acknowledged foreknowledge and encouragement but maintained Soering wielded the knife, a position consistent across her statements despite admitted deceptions.[1]

Forensic Evidence Debates and Recent Developments

Initial serological analysis in 1985 identified type O blood at the crime scene consistent with Soering's blood type, which prosecutors used to link him to the murders, alongside type AB blood matching Nancy Haysom's. However, advanced DNA testing conducted by the Virginia Department of Forensic Science in 2009 on 11 viable blood samples from the Haysom home yielded no genetic profile matching Soering or Elizabeth Haysom, including in samples previously serologically typed as O or AB.[57][58] Further examination revealed male DNA profiles in two type O swabs and two AB swabs inconsistent with Soering's profile, prompting debates over potential unknown contributors or contamination, though proponents of Soering's innocence argue the absence exonerates him while critics note the limitations of partial profiles and scene cleanup possibilities.[22] Handwriting analysis confirmed Haysom's authorship of forged checks used in the couple's fraud scheme across Europe, with exemplars matching her script on documents seized during their 1986 arrest, providing circumstantial evidence of her deceptive capabilities post-murders.[23] Debates persist regarding physical traces like footprints at the scene, which some forensic reviews suggest align with a single assailant's movements rather than two, potentially implicating Haysom as the primary actor given her smaller stature and the wounds' patterns—over 50 stabs and slashes, including defensive injuries on Derek Haysom consistent with one dominant attacker.[56] Wound trajectory analyses have fueled speculation of a lone perpetrator, though no peer-reviewed study conclusively attributes the attack to one individual, and initial autopsy reports emphasized the ferocity without resolving multiplicity.[56] In August 2024, Soering filed a petition for an absolute pardon with Virginia Governor Glenn Youngkin, incorporating re-tested DNA evidence from crime scene blood swabs that reportedly yielded non-matches to his profile and unidentified male contributors, aiming to affirm his actual innocence beyond the 2019 parole granted after a Parole Board review that did not vacate the conviction.[59] As of late 2024, the petition remains under review by state authorities, with no exoneration or conviction overturn, amid ongoing contention that DNA exclusions undermine original serology but fail to address Soering's multiple confessions or Haysom's plea admissions.[59][22] Critics, including case investigators, maintain that forensic ambiguities do not negate the totality of evidence, such as the couple's flight and fraud, while innocence advocates cite the DNA as empirical grounds for re-examination.[22]

Post-Release Life and Current Status

Deportation and Restrictions

Following her parole grant on November 25, 2019, Elizabeth Haysom was immediately transferred from Virginia Department of Corrections custody to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) detention, where she was held at the Irwin County Detention Center in Ocilla, Georgia, under the explicit parole condition that she remain securely detained until removal from the United States.[7] [6] This handover ensured compliance with deportation requirements, as failure to depart would violate parole terms and result in reincarceration.[6] Haysom, a Canadian citizen by birth, was removed from the U.S. on January 22, 2020, departing from Hartsfield-Jackson International Airport in Atlanta and arriving in Canada, where ICE officers handed her over to Canadian authorities pursuant to a final order of removal.[7] Her conviction for accessory before the fact to murder rendered her an aggravated felon under U.S. immigration law, imposing a permanent bar on re-entry to the United States without special waiver, which has not been granted.[7] [60] In parallel, Jens Soering was deported to Germany, his country of citizenship, severing their shared post-release trajectory and effectively concluding any ongoing personal ties forged during decades of imprisonment.[61] Haysom's adherence to these logistics—prompt handover and removal—fulfilled initial parole stipulations, avoiding revocation.[7]

Public Statements and Ongoing Implications

Following her deportation to Canada on January 23, 2020, after serving 32 years in prison, Elizabeth Haysom has resided in Western Canada while maintaining a low public profile, with no verified reports of further criminal involvement or public engagements as of 2025.[7][62] In a reflective statement dated November 2019, prior to her parole approval, Haysom admitted her complicity as the catalyst for the murders, noting she provided an alibi for Soering's visit to her parents and attributing her actions to selfishness, passivity, and the toxic dynamics of their relationship, while expressing remorse and a commitment to atonement through personal rehabilitation.[63] The case exemplifies broader implications for youth crime among privileged young adults, where documented patterns of familial discord, substance abuse, and unchecked rebellion—evident in Haysom's history of drug involvement and resentment toward her parents' authority—can escalate to parricide, underscoring causal links between eroded parental oversight and violent outcomes in otherwise stable socioeconomic contexts.[64] Critics of the parole decision, including U.S. Congressman Ben Cline, have condemned the release after 32 years for accessory roles in a double homicide as excessively lenient, arguing it undermines deterrence for serious offenses and reflects overly permissive norms in Virginia's parole system despite repeated prior denials.[6] Perspectives from the victims' extended family highlight ongoing unresolved pain, with Haysom's half-brothers, Howard and Richard, asserting in 1995 parole hearings that she had not fully confessed her involvement and should remain incarcerated longer to ensure accountability, a stance reflecting persistent familial grief over the unhealed trauma and societal burdens of such intra-family violence.[65]

Portrayals in Media and Culture

Documentaries and Books

The case of Elizabeth Haysom and Jens Soering has been depicted in several books and documentaries, often highlighting the couple's privileged backgrounds, drug use, and intense romantic relationship alongside debates over culpability. Ken Englade's 1988 book Beyond Reason: The True Story of a Shocking Double Murder, a Brilliant and Beautiful Virginia Socialite, and a Deadly Psychotic Obsession details the 1985 murders of Haysom's parents, Derek and Nancy Haysom, portraying Haysom as a manipulative figure influenced by her bohemian lifestyle and Soering's obsession, drawing on trial records and interviews to emphasize forensic links to the pair without endorsing later innocence claims.[66] In contrast, Soering co-authored A Far, Far Better Thing: Did a Fatal Attraction Lead to a Wrongful Conviction? (2017), which argues his innocence by scrutinizing blood evidence and interrogation tactics, reflecting his post-conviction advocacy while minimizing Haysom's role, though reliant on his personal narrative rather than independent verification.[67] Documentaries have frequently sensationalized the class dynamics of the Haysoms' Bedford County estate and the lovers' flight to Europe amid check fraud, sometimes amplifying unproven theories over established convictions. The 2016 German documentary Killing for Love, directed by Marcus Vetter and Karin Steinberger, examines the stabbings on March 30, 1985, and Soering's 1985 confession, questioning its voluntariness and DNA inconsistencies to suggest a potential miscarriage of justice, with Soering's participation shaping its skeptical tone toward the prosecution.[68] [64] The 2023 Netflix four-part series Till Murder Do Us Part: Soering vs. Haysom revisits the evidence, including Haysom's 1985 guilty plea to accessory charges and Soering's disavowal of his confession, framing the narrative around unresolved forensic debates and interpersonal manipulation claims, though it prioritizes Soering's interviews over Haysom's limited public statements.[69] [70] These works vary in factual fidelity, with earlier accounts like Englade's aligning closely to 1980s trial outcomes—such as the matching blood types and receipts tying the couple to the crime scene—while later ones incorporate Soering's appeals, including 2019 DNA retesting that failed to exonerate him, often critiquing systemic pressures without Haysom's counter-narrative due to her reticence.[19] Sensational elements, including the couple's LSD experimentation and Haysom's fabricated personas, recur but risk overshadowing verified details like the 72 stab wounds inflicted.[53]

Public Reception and Interpretations

Public reception of the Elizabeth Haysom and Jens Soering case has long been polarized, with one perspective emphasizing the perpetrators' youth—both were university students in their early twenties at the time of the 1985 murders—and portraying them as products of a tumultuous romance or institutional overreach, fostering sympathy through claims of coercion or factual innocence.[64] This view gained traction via celebrity endorsements from figures like John Grisham and Martin Sheen, who supported Soering's release efforts, and media narratives highlighting unproven abuse allegations against Haysom's mother or DNA reinterpretations excluding Soering from certain evidence.[52] In contrast, a truth-oriented assessment prioritizes the premeditated savagery of the stabbings—over 30 wounds inflicted on Derek and Nancy Haysom, who posed no threat as affluent, non-abusive parents—and the couple's prior forgery of checks to fund their lifestyle, underscoring the victims' innocence and the killers' agency rather than extenuating narratives.[22][52] Critiques of media coverage contend that sympathetic framings humanize the guilty at the expense of evidentiary rigor, as seen in documentaries and articles that amplify Soering's recanted confession—detailed with crime-scene specifics like blood spatter patterns and sock impressions only the perpetrator could know—while downplaying Haysom's role in plotting for inheritance and her subsequent testimony identifying Soering as the assailant.[52] Such portrayals, often driven by European skepticism of American justice, obscure causal realities like the pair's mutual deceptions during their post-murder flight across the U.S. and Canada, where they passed bad checks in 36 cities.[64] The 2019 paroles, following decades served, elicited backlash for prioritizing rehabilitation over retribution, illustrating perceived flaws in systems that may undervalue lifelong accountability for elite-originated crimes enabled by familial wealth and lax oversight.[22] An enduring interpretive divide concerns gender attributions of blame, with Soering's advocates casting Haysom as the manipulative instigator who exploited his devotion, yet countered by her guilty plea to accessory before the fact, expressed remorse, and court findings of collaborative intent rather than unilateral female dominance.[22] Haysom's 2016 statement of "profound shame" for the killings contrasts Soering's persistent denial, fueling debates that sometimes veer into unsubstantiated victimhood for her amid abuse claims she herself denied under oath.[52] These dynamics highlight broader lessons on how narrative preferences can eclipse forensic and testimonial evidence, perpetuating public fascination with the case as a lens on entitlement, relational toxicity, and the limits of redemptive justice.[64]
