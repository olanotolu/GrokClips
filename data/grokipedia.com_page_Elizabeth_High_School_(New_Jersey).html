Elizabeth High School (New Jersey)

Elizabeth High School is a four-year comprehensive community public high school located in Elizabeth, in Union County, in the U.S. state of New_Jersey, serving students in ninth through twelfth grades as part of the Elizabeth Public Schools. Prior to the 2009 reorganization, the school served more than 5,000 students; the current academies collectively serve approximately 5,000 students.[4] In 2009, the school was reorganized into a system of six separate houses, each operating as an autonomous academy with its own principal and area of focus, including the Frank J. Cicarell Academy, which continues traditional programs under the Elizabeth High School name.[4] The school has been accredited by the Middle States Association of Colleges and Schools Commission on Elementary and Secondary Schools since 1978.[3] As of the 2023â€“24 school year, the Frank J. Cicarell Academy had an enrollment of 1,291 students and 68.0 classroom teachers (on an FTE basis), for a studentâ€“teacher ratio of 19.0:1. There were 709 students (54.9% of enrollment) eligible for free lunch and 234 (18.1% of students) eligible for reduced-cost lunch.[1] Starting in the 2016â€“17 school year, the upper and lower academies previously associated with Elizabeth High School were consolidated into the newly built Frank J. Cicarell Academy, located adjacent to the Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy.[5] The academy serves students in grades 9 through 12.

Awards, recognition and rankings

In 2015, Elizabeth High School was one of 15 schools in New Jersey, and one of nine public schools, recognized as a National Blue Ribbon School in the exemplary high performing category by the United States Department of Education.[6][7] In its 2013 report on "America's Best High Schools", The Daily Beast ranked the school 320th in the nation among participating public high schools and 25th among schools in New Jersey.[8] The school was ranked 217th in the nation and 18th in New Jersey on the list of "America's Best High Schools 2012" prepared by The Daily Beast / Newsweek , with rankings based primarily on graduation rate, matriculation rate for college and number of Advanced Placement / International Baccalaureate courses taken per student, with lesser factors based on average scores on the SAT / ACT, average AP/IB scores and the number of AP/IB courses available to students.[9] The school was the 119th-ranked public high school in New Jersey out of 339 schools statewide in New Jersey Monthly magazine's September 2014 cover story on the state's "Top Public High Schools", using a new ranking methodology.[10] The school had been ranked 148th in the state of 328 schools in 2012, after being ranked 294th in 2010 out of 322 schools listed based on school statistics prior to the split.[11] The school had been ranked 302nd in 2008 out of 316 schools.[12] The school was ranked 287th in the magazine's September 2006 issue, which surveyed 316 schools across the state.[13] Following the split, Schooldigger.com ranked the school 21st out of 381 public high schools statewide in its 2011 rankings (a decrease of 8 positions from the 2010 ranking) which were based on the combined percentage of students classified as proficient or above proficient on the mathematics (97.5%) and language arts literacy (100.0%) components of the High School Proficiency Assessment (HSPA).[14] In the 2024â€“25 U.S. News & World Report rankings of the Best High Schools, Elizabeth High School was ranked 32nd in New Jersey and 594th nationally.[15] As of the 2023â€“24 school year, SchoolDigger ranked the school 119th out of 429 public high schools statewide.[16]

Elizabeth High School

History

The all-girls Battin High School and all-boys Thomas Jefferson High School were both closed at the end of the 1976â€“77 school year, after the Elizabeth High School complex was completed and all of the district's students, male and female, were accommodated at the new four-building facility, ending the city's status as "the only community in the state with separate public high schools for boys and girls". The $29.3 million project included renovations to Thomas Jefferson High School, which was integrated into the new complex. The Battin High School building, together with the four existing junior high schools, was repurposed as a middle school for grades six through eight.[17] With over 5,279 students as of 2006, Elizabeth High School had been the largest high school in the nation in terms of student population.[18] Prior to 2010, Elizabeth High School occupied eight campuses, also known as houses: The William F. Halsey house, the John E. Dwyer house, the Thomas Jefferson house, the Thomas Edison house, the Sam E. Aboff house, the Alexander Hamilton academy, the Upper academy, and the Lower academy. In 2009, the Elizabeth Board of Education passed the "Transformation Plan", which split up the houses that made up Elizabeth High School and made each house its own high school, with implementation beginning in the 2009-10 school year to address overcrowding, low test scores, and high dropout rates. The Upper and Lower academies became the new Elizabeth High School.[4] In the period before the 2009 transformation, almost half of each graduating class failed to pass the standard High School Proficiency Assessment (HSPA) and complete required course credits, requiring them to take the Alternative High School Assessment (AHSA) test instead.[19] As of 2009, about one in four students who entered the high school dropped out.[20]

Houses

Prior to 2009, Elizabeth High School operated as a single large school with multiple houses or campuses, including an administration building and an indoor sports center. In 2009, the school was restructured into six independent academies to address overcrowding, each with its own principal and focus area.[4] By 2025, the district operates eight high school academies, evolved from the original houses:
  • John E. Dwyer Technology Academy (formerly part of Main Complex)
  • Thomas A. Edison Career and Technical Academy (formerly Vocational and Technical Academy)
  • Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy
  • Alexander Hamilton Preparatory Academy
  • Elizabeth High School â€“ Frank J. Cicarell Academy
  • Admiral William F. Halsey Jr. Health and Public Safety Academy (formerly Halsey House)
  • J. Christian Bollwage Finance Academy
  • Katherine Johnson, Dorothy Vaughan, Mary Jackson (JVJ) STEM Academy[21][22]

Main Complex

The Main Complex, consisting of the former William F. Halsey House, John Dwyer House, Peter B. Gold Administration Building, and Thomas Dunn Sports Center, now primarily houses the John E. Dwyer Technology Academy. It serves as a central hub for technology-focused education and continues to support some district-wide extracurricular activities and sports, including access to the swimming pool and a unique student courtyard.

Thomas Jefferson House

Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy, located across the street from City Hall and war monuments, is the district's center for visual and performing arts programs. Originally built in 1928 as an all-boys high school, it now operates independently, specializing in visual arts such as drawing, painting, and creative crafts; performing arts including dance, acting, and singing; and additional subjects like creative writing. A freshman annex previously merged into the adjacent Frank J. Cicarell Academy building, but the academy maintains its distinct focus.[21]

Thomas A. Edison Academy For Career & Technical Education

Thomas A. Edison Career and Technical Academy, originally built as Thomas Edison High School in 1935 with its last independent graduating class in 1987, serves as the center for vocational and technical education. Named for the inventor who contributed significantly in New Jersey, it now operates as an independent academy. Williams Field, with the school's football field and outdoor track, is adjacent.

J Christian Bollwage Academy of Finance

The J. Christian Bollwage Finance Academy evolved from the former Sam E. Aboff Alternative House, the smallest in terms of population and area, originally for students with attendance issues. Post-2009, it became the Halsey Academy of Finance, part of the Admiral William F. Halsey Jr. Leadership Academy, focusing on business leadership and affiliated with the National Academy Foundation (NAF) for career preparation in finance and related fields through project-based learning, internships, and partnerships.[23] In the 2016â€“2017 school year, it spun off as an independent academy, relocating to the former Upper Academy building.[21][5]

Frank J. Cicarell Academy/Upper and Lower Academy

The Frank J. Cicarell Academy, serving as Elizabeth High School, was established in 2006 as Upper and Lower Academies with a college-preparatory curriculum emphasizing honors and Advanced Placement courses. Students must wear uniforms and maintain a 3.0 GPA. In September 2016, the academies merged into a new building named after former physical education teacher Frank J. Cicarell (died 2007), located next to Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy. The former Upper Academy site became the J. Christian Bollwage Finance Academy, and the Lower Academy served as an annex for Thomas A. Edison Academy. In 2021, a new freshman facility opened at the former St. Genevieve School site on September 14, following delays from Hurricane Ida. The academy now integrates former Jefferson freshman programs while maintaining its focus on college preparation.[21][24]

Alexander Hamilton Preparatory Academy

Alexander Hamilton Preparatory Academy, the newest original house accommodating all grades, integrates the Advancement Via Individual Determination (AVID) program to prepare students for college through rigorous curriculum, honors and AP courses, and academic support. Students wear uniforms and must maintain a 2.0 GPA. Its campus, farthest from the Main Complex near Westfield Avenue, uses portable buildings for classes. It operates independently as part of the district's academy network.[21]

Transformation Plan

Transformation Plan

From 1977 to 2009, Elizabeth High School was one big high school with eight campuses (or houses). In 2009, the Elizabeth Board of Education passed the "Transformation Plan" that split up the high school and created six smaller high schools.
  • The Upper Academy and Lower Academy became the new Elizabeth High School-Frank J. Cicarell Academy
  • The William F. Halsey House became Admiral William F. Halsey Jr. Health and Public Safety Academy
  • The John Dwyer House became John E. Dwyer Technology Academy
  • The Thomas Jefferson House became Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy
  • The Sam E. Aboff Alternative House became part of Admiral William F. Halsey Jr. Health and Public Safety Academy
  • The Thomas A. Edison Vocational and Technical Academy stood the same
  • The Alexander Hamilton Preparatory Academy stood the same
In 2016, the Board of Education made some additional changes.
  • Elizabeth High School, which had two different locations for 9th grade students and all other students, became one location by moving into the Frank J. Cicarell Academy, located next to the Jefferson Arts Academy. The Academy of Finance, which was part of the Halsey Health and Public Safety Academy, spun off and became its own school, initially as the Academy of Finance and renamed the J. Christian Bollwage Finance Academy in 2017, moving into the former location of Upper Academy. The Thomas A. Edison Vocational and Technical Academy was renamed the Thomas A. Edison Career and Technical Academy.
Subsequent changes have continued the transformation. Starting around 2018â€“19, the freshman annex of the Thomas Jefferson Arts Academy merged into the Frank J. Cicarell Academy building. As of the early 2020s, the Cicarell Academy building houses Jefferson freshmen along with Cicarell sophomores, juniors, and seniors, while a new annex opened for Cicarell freshmen only.[24]

Marching band

The Elizabeth High School Marching Band won the USBands State and National 5A Competition in 2011,[26] 2012, and 2013.[27][28] In the 2014 season, the Marching Band moved up from 5A to 5 Open and went undefeated until their streak was snapped placing 4th in the National 5 Open Championship in MetLife Stadium on November 15, 2014. Throughout the week of Super Bowl XLVIII between the Seattle Seahawks and Denver Broncos, the Elizabeth High School Marching Band was invited to perform at Media Day in the Prudential Center in Newark, New Jersey as well as perform in pre-game festivities at the Super Bowl at MetLife Stadium. In 2019 the band took first at the USBands New Jersey State Championship and The New Jersey Regional Championship. Marking their first championship titles in open class division. As well as becoming a finalist at the Bands of America Mid-Atlantic Regional Championship placing 6th out of 26 schools. In 2021 the band competed in the inaugural New Jersey Marching Band Directorâ€™s Association State Championship and placed first in Class AAA, with a score of 93.7.[29] As of the 2025 season (ongoing), the band earned 1st place at the USBands competition at Northern Valley Regional High School (Old Tappan) on September 20, 2025, with a score of 80.3.[30]

Administration

As of October 2025, the principal is Michael Cummings. His core administration team includes the three vice principals: Harry Sam Kelada, John Markowitz, and Jecenia Solorzano-Castro. [55]
