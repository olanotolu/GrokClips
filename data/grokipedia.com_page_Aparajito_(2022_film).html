Aparajito (2022 film)

Aparajito, subtitled The Undefeated, is a 2022 Indian Bengali-language drama film directed and co-written by Anik Dutta, chronicling the perseverance of a young advertising professional turned filmmaker in realizing his debut feature amid financial and logistical hurdles.[1][2] The narrative draws inspiration from the real-life challenges Satyajit Ray encountered while producing his seminal 1955 debut Pather Panchali, framing the protagonist Aparajito Ray—portrayed by Jeetu Kamal—as a determined artist navigating skepticism, funding shortages, and production delays to capture authentic rural Bengal on celluloid.[2][3] Shot in black and white to evoke Ray's aesthetic, the film stars Saayoni Ghosh as Aparajito's wife and features supporting performances by Paran Banerjee and Debashis Roy, earning acclaim for its authentic portrayal of creative struggle without exaggeration or sentimentality.[1][4] Released on 13 May 2022, it garnered high audience approval, evidenced by an 8.7 rating on IMDb from over 5,000 users, and was praised in reviews for its universal resonance on the barriers to artistic debut in resource-constrained environments.[2][1]

Background

Historical inspiration

The 2022 film Aparajito draws its primary historical inspiration from the protracted and resource-scarce production of Satyajit Ray's debut feature Pather Panchali (1955), an adaptation of Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay's 1929 novel depicting rural Bengali life amid poverty and natural cycles. Ray, then a 32-year-old advertising professional and film society founder with no prior directing experience, conceived the project in the early 1950s after illustrating an abridged children's edition of the novel, which deepened his affinity for its unadorned realism. Initial scripting and location scouting began around 1952, but securing financing proved arduous, as Indian producers dismissed the script for lacking songs, dances, and melodrama typical of commercial cinema.[5][6] Principal photography commenced in late 1953 with modest seed funding of approximately 80,000 rupees (equivalent to about $16,000 USD at the time) from the West Bengal government, enabling shoots in rural villages like Boral and Kelugram using natural locations to capture authentic village decay and monsoon sequences. However, funds depleted rapidly due to Ray's insistence on chronological filming, non-professional child actors such as Subir Banerjee as Apu, and rudimentary equipment including a borrowed 35mm camera; production halted multiple times, spanning nearly three years until completion in 1955. Ray personally mortgaged his insurance policy and jewelry, while overseas aid from figures like American distributor Edward Harrison and German contacts provided critical loans, allowing resumption amid crew attrition and logistical strains like transporting heavy gear by bullock cart.[7][5] Post-production faced further hurdles, including Ray's self-taught editing and scoring—initially using Ravi Shankar's sitar improvisations recorded in post—and domestic skepticism that nearly derailed distribution. The West Bengal government intervened by purchasing 21 prints in 1955 to alleviate Ray's debts, enabling a Kolkata release on August 26, 1955, followed by international acclaim at the 1956 Cannes Film Festival, where it shared the Best Humanistic Film award. This saga of perseverance against institutional indifference and material scarcity underscores Aparajito's portrayal of artistic defiance, reflecting Ray's documented accounts of the ordeal as a blend of "pain and pleasure" in bootstrapping Indian parallel cinema.[6][5]

Relation to Satyajit Ray's works

Aparajito (2022), directed by Anik Dutta, serves as a dramatized account of the production challenges encountered by Satyajit Ray during the creation of his debut feature Pather Panchali (1955), the first installment of Ray's Apu Trilogy.[8][9] The film chronicles Ray's efforts to adapt Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay's novel, emphasizing financial constraints, logistical difficulties in rural Bengal locations, and resistance from skeptical producers who doubted the viability of a non-commercial narrative.[8][10] To secure production permissions from Ray's son Sandip Ray, Dutta agreed to fictionalize names, portraying Satyajit Ray as "Aparajito Ray" (or "Manik" in some contexts) and altering other historical figures, such as casting Jeetu Kamal in the lead role to evoke Ray's persona without direct impersonation.[11] This approach allows the film to explore Ray's creative inspirations, including his viewing of Vittorio De Sica's Bicycle Thieves (1948) in the United Kingdom, which prompted Ray to pursue a realist adaptation of Pather Panchali using non-professional actors and authentic village settings.[10][8] The title Aparajito, meaning "the unvanquished" in Bengali, directly references Ray's own 1956 film of the same name—the second in the Apu Trilogy—positioning Dutta's work as an homage to Ray's oeuvre while extending the theme of perseverance from Apu's personal struggles to Ray's artistic battles.[8][12] Released on May 27, 2022, the film premiered its trailer on April 23, 2022, coinciding with the 30th anniversary of Ray's death, underscoring its intent as a tribute to his foundational contributions to Indian parallel cinema.

Synopsis

Plot summary

Set in 1950s Kolkata, Aparajito frames its narrative around a radio interview with an elderly filmmaker Aparajito Ray, who reflects on his early career struggles while his family listens in, prompting a series of flashbacks to his youth.[12][11] In these recollections, the young Aparajito, employed in advertising, travels to London for work and becomes inspired by Vittorio De Sica's Bicycle Thieves (depicted as Bicycle Riders), motivating him to adapt Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay's novel Pather Panchali (renamed Pather Padabali in the film) into a neorealist feature despite lacking formal filmmaking experience.[8][9][10] Aparajito resigns from his job, garnering initial support from his mother and wife—who pledges her jewelry for funds—but encounters widespread skepticism from family, industry figures, and potential backers who question the viability of a songless, rural-set Bengali film.[8][12] He secures partial financing through creative means, such as a government ruse involving a public works department road project, and assembles a rudimentary crew, including cinematographer Subir Mitter.[9][12] Location scouting leads to rural Bengal villages, where he casts non-professional child actors for roles like the protagonist Manik (a stand-in for Apu) and recreates key sequences, including Indir Thakurun's death, the siblings' glimpse of a train, and a rain scene, amid logistical hurdles like scarce raw film stock and unpredictable elements such as wandering animals.[12][9] The production culminates in the completion and international premiere of Pather Padabali, earning initial acclaim abroad before sparking pride and reevaluation among Bengali audiences and critics who had dismissed Aparajito's vision.[9][12]

Principal cast

Notable supporting roles

Production

Development

Anik Dutta conceived Aparajito as a tribute to Satyajit Ray during his birth centenary celebrations in 2021, focusing on the challenges Ray faced in producing his debut feature Pather Panchali (1955).[8] The screenplay draws from Ray's autobiography My Years with Apu: A Memoir, which details the eight-year gestation period of the film, including funding struggles and creative hurdles.[8] To navigate biopic consent issues and allow artistic flexibility, Dutta fictionalized key elements by renaming the protagonist Satyajit Ray as Aparajito Ray and altering titles like Pather Panchali to Pather Padabali, creating a parallel narrative universe while preserving historical essence.[9] The project secured permission from Sandip Ray, Satyajit Ray's son and a filmmaker in his own right, enabling access to Ray family insights without direct endorsement of the renamed approach.[9] Dutta co-wrote the screenplay with Utsav Mukherjee and Sreeparna Mitra, emphasizing Ray's transition from advertising executive to independent director amid post-independence India's limited film infrastructure.[9] Research involved meticulous recreation of 1950s Kolkata aesthetics, including period costumes, sets, and black-and-white cinematography to evoke Ray's neorealist style, as praised by filmmakers like Shyam Benegal for its fidelity to source material.[8] Produced by Firdausul Hasan and Probal Halder under Friends Communication, development prioritized authenticity over commercial appeal, with Dutta consulting cinematographers like Soumendu Roy—who worked on Ray's originals—for technical accuracy in depicting early Indian cinema production.[15] The script's release as a book in 2024, coinciding with Ray's 103rd birth anniversary, included shooting stills and reviews, underscoring its archival value.[16] This approach reflects Dutta's intent to highlight Ray's perseverance against institutional skepticism, framing the film as a meta-commentary on artistic undefeatedness.[17]

Filming

Principal photography for Aparajito commenced in October 2021, with director Anik Dutta opting for real locations on the outskirts of Kolkata to capture authentic urban and period-appropriate settings reflective of 1950s West Bengal.[18] The production emphasized practical shoots over extensive set builds, aligning with Dutta's intent to ground the narrative in tangible environments amid the story's focus on Satyajit Ray's struggles in post-independence Calcutta.[18] Cinematographer Supratim Bhol handled the visuals, employing techniques to evoke the era without heavy reliance on digital reconstruction, though specific challenges like period props and actor transformations via prosthetics were managed by makeup artist Somnath Kundu.[1] Filming wrapped prior to the film's May 13, 2022, theatrical release, amid broader production hurdles including research into Ray's era and securing permissions for location access.[11]

Technical aspects

Cinematography for Aparajito was handled by Supratim Bhol, who employed a mix of color and black-and-white sequences to evoke the aesthetic of Satyajit Ray's original Pather Panchali, including recreations of key scenes such as the rush through kash fields.[19] Bhol's work received the Golden Sparrow Award for Best Cinematography at the 2022 Diorama International Film Festival.[20] The film's runtime is 138 minutes, incorporating both color and black-and-white footage to blend biographical narrative with historical homage.[21] Sound design was led by Tirthankar Majumdar, emphasizing layered ambient sounds, foley effects, and room tones to enhance immersion, particularly in scenes depicting the challenges of early Indian filmmaking.[4] Reviews noted the meticulous auditory experience, which contributed to the film's technical solidity alongside its music.[11] Anup Kumar Mukhopadhyay also received recognition for sound contributions at the Diorama Festival.[20] Editing focused on pacing the biographical elements without unnecessary slowdowns, maintaining a dynamic flow despite the film's reflective tone on Ray's struggles.[1] Overall production values were described as sound, with technical elements supporting the tribute's authenticity rather than overt innovation.[1]

Music

Composition and soundtrack

The original score for Aparajito was composed by Debojyoti Mishra, who drew inspiration from Ravi Shankar's music for Satyajit Ray's Pather Panchali while incorporating original elements to evoke a sense of tribute without direct replication.[22][23] Mishra experimented with variations on the raga Desh, a key element in Shankar's score, including a specific "budget journey theme" for a nightmare sequence rendered in Shankar's sitar style with precise tuning and beats, alongside influences from Ray's appreciation of Russian composers like Prokofiev.[22] His approach was informed by prior collaboration on Ray's Ghare Baire orchestra and authorship of a book on Ray's compositions, published in 2022, emphasizing emotional depth reflective of Ray's struggles depicted in the film.[23][22] The score prioritizes background music over songs, utilizing traditional Indian and Bengali folk instruments such as sitar, dhol, flute, dotara, khamak, khanjani, and ektara to achieve an authentic, non-electronic texture rooted in ragas like Desh and Patadeep, often via tarshehnai or esraj.[22][23] The soundtrack includes one primary song, "Amader Panchali," with lyrics and music by Mishra, sung by Arko Mukherjee, featuring a 14-beat poyar rhythm inspired by Bengali folk gaji songs and humming by students to celebrate Pather Panchali, Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay, and Shankar; it runs approximately 3:55 in length.[24][22] The full soundtrack, comprising the score and song, became available for streaming on platforms like Amazon Prime Music in 2022.[25]

Release

Distribution and premiere

Aparajito had its special premiere screening at the National Museum of Indian Cinema in Mumbai on May 2, 2022, organized by the Ministry of Information and Broadcasting.[26] The film then held a star-studded theatrical premiere in Kolkata on May 13, 2022, coinciding with its nationwide release in India.[27] Distributed by SSR Cinemas Pvt. Ltd., the film received a wide theatrical rollout across single-screen theaters and multiplexes in West Bengal and other regions on May 13, 2022.[28] By late May, it was screening in approximately 65 halls with around 125 daily shows, contributing to its status as a commercial success.[29] International theatrical releases followed, including in the United Arab Emirates on May 26, 2022.[30] The digital premiere occurred on ZEE5 on August 25, 2022, expanding accessibility beyond theaters.[31] Post-theatrical, the film screened at festivals such as the Bengaluru International Film Festival in March 2023 and the Toronto International Film Festival in September 2022.[30][32]

Reception

Critical responses

Aparajito garnered positive critical reception in India, with reviewers lauding its detailed recreation of the challenges faced by Satyajit Ray during the production of Pather Panchali and its black-and-white aesthetic as a fitting tribute to the master filmmaker.[1][12] Critics highlighted the film's meticulous research, period authenticity through props and costumes, and effective integration of iconic scenes like the train sequence.[1] Performances, especially Jeetu Kamal's portrayal of a young Ray—enhanced by prosthetics to mimic his dental profile and mannerisms—drew widespread acclaim for capturing the director's essence and determination.[1][12][8] Director Anik Dutta's approach was praised for sensitively humanizing Ray as a relatable dreamer rather than an untouchable icon, weaving anecdotes from Ray's memoir My Years with Apu into a narrative of perseverance.[12][33] The Times of India awarded the film 4 out of 5 stars, emphasizing its universal appeal and avoidance of sluggish pacing often associated with Ray's style, while rating direction, story, music, and visuals highly at 4 to 4.5.[1] India Today commended the storytelling of Ray's trials, including funding hurdles and creative genius, alongside strong supporting turns by Saayoni Ghosh as Bijoya Ray and others, portraying the film's focus on cultural and artistic struggles in 1950s Bengal.[33] The Wire described it as a remarkable experience that earned rave reviews and standing ovations, crediting Dutta's brisk pacing and heartfelt homage.[12] Some critiques noted limitations in originality and tension. The Hindu acknowledged its status as a commercial hit and faithful period recreation—praised even by Shyam Benegal—but faulted it for functioning as a "good photocopy" of Ray's life, adhering too closely to known events and resolving conflicts too neatly, thus lacking deeper dramatic insight or re-imagination beyond nostalgia for fans.[8] International critical attention was limited, though the film secured multiple awards abroad, reflecting appreciation for its biographical depth.[34]

Commercial performance

Aparajito grossed ₹3.75 crore worldwide, with an India net collection of ₹3.20 crore and negligible overseas earnings of approximately ₹0.00 crore reported for major markets.[35] The film's opening weekend earned ₹0.37 crore domestically, starting with ₹0.07 crore on its first day (a Friday), followed by ₹0.10 crore on Saturday and ₹0.20 crore on Sunday.[36] By the end of its first week and a half after release on May 20, 2022, it had accumulated ₹2.5 crore, demonstrating steady audience interest despite its biographical and artistic focus on Satyajit Ray's struggles.[37] Overseas performance was limited, with reported grosses including $8,149 in the United Arab Emirates (released May 26, 2022), $2,953 in the United Kingdom (July 1, 2022), and $592 in Portugal (September 1, 2022), reflecting modest international appeal tied to Ray's legacy rather than broad commercial draw.[38] For a black-and-white tribute film in the Bengali industry, Aparajito's earnings marked it as a surprising commercial success amid 2022's box office challenges, outperforming expectations for non-mainstream releases and contributing to renewed optimism in Tollywood's theatrical viability.[39] No official production budget figures were publicly disclosed, limiting direct assessments of profitability, though industry observers noted its "remarkable" run as evidence of niche audience turnout.[37]

Controversies

Permission disputes

Permission to produce Aparajito, a film depicting the challenges faced during the creation of Satyajit Ray's Pather Panchali, was granted by Sandeep Ray, Satyajit Ray's son and executor of his estate. This approval, obtained prior to the film's 2022 release, included access to certain family archives but imposed strict conditions to maintain separation from an official biography.[9][12] Sandeep Ray explicitly stipulated that real names could not be used, necessitating the fictionalization of key figures and elements: the protagonist filmmaker was renamed "Manik" or "Aparajito Ray" instead of Satyajit Ray, his wife Bijoya became Bimala, cinematographer Subrata Mitra became Subir Mitter, and Pather Panchali itself was retitled Pather Padaboli, with child characters Durga and Apu altered to Uma and Manik.[11][9] This condition enabled the filmmakers to exercise creative license while grounding the narrative in documented production facts, though the producer noted that direct use of Satyajit Ray's name was prohibited.[40] The name changes, while avoiding potential legal challenges over portrayal accuracy, drew commentary for risking audience confusion among those familiar with Ray's history, as the alterations permeated the script and could disrupt immersion. No formal disputes or litigation ensued, with director Anik Dutta publicly acknowledging Sandeep Ray's role in the film's disclaimer.[11][41] The approach mirrored strategies in other biopics, balancing consent with artistic interpretation amid sensitivities over depicting deceased icons.[40]

Festival selection issues

Aparajito encountered significant controversy over its exclusion from the Indian Panorama section of the 53rd International Film Festival of India (IFFI), conducted in Goa from November 20 to 28, 2022. Among 354 qualifying Indian feature films submitted, the jury selected only 25 for inclusion, omitting Aparajito despite its thematic focus on the production struggles behind Satyajit Ray's Pather Panchali and prior international recognition, including a screening at the Toronto International Film Festival from September 8 to 18, 2022.[42][32] The decision prompted widespread questioning from filmmakers and critics regarding the selection criteria, with some attributing the snub to potential biases in jury evaluations favoring other narratives over established tributes to Indian cinema heritage.[42] No explicit rationale for the rejection was issued by the IFFI jury, headed by filmmakers such as Aparesh Das, fueling perceptions of opacity in the national festival's curatorial process.[42] Director Anik Dutta, known for prior clashes with state-level film authorities in West Bengal over screening access, highlighted the film's grassroots acclaim post its May 13, 2022 theatrical release, yet the IFFI outcome underscored tensions between independent Bengali cinema and centralized festival programming under the Ministry of Information and Broadcasting.[43] In response, the Union Ministry of Information and Broadcasting later endorsed Aparajito for the Shanghai International Film Festival, where it received a platform, contrasting the domestic rebuff and amplifying debates on inconsistent governmental support for films with historical and cultural significance.[44] This episode contributed to broader critiques of IFFI's Indian Panorama as potentially sidelining regionally rooted works in favor of commercially aligned selections, though proponents of the jury maintained selections prioritized artistic diversity and innovation.[42]

Accolades

Award wins and nominations

At the 70th National Film Awards for films released in 2022, Aparajito won the award for Best Production Design, awarded to Ananda Addhya, and Best Make-up Artist, awarded to Somnath Kundu.[45][46] The film achieved multiple wins at the West Bengal Film Journalists' Association Awards in 2023, including Best Director for Anik Dutta, Best Actor for Jeetu Kamal, Best Screenplay for Anik Dutta, Utsav Mukherjee, and Sreeparna Mitra, Best Background Score, Best Art Direction, Best Make-up, and Best Costume.[47] Internationally, Aparajito received the Golden Sparrow for Best Indian Feature Film and Best Actor for Jeetu Kamal at the 4th Diorama International Film Festival in 2022.[20] It also won the FIPRESCI International Critics Award for the best film in Bengali cinema.[48] At the 7th BRICS Film Festival in 2022, the film earned a special jury recognition.[49] Additionally, it secured the Best Sound Design award for Tirthankar Majumdar at the 6th Joy Filmfare Awards Bangla in 2023, amid several nominations in technical and performance categories.[50]
