Shots Podcast Network


Shots Podcast Network is an American digital media company founded by brothers John Shahidi and Sam Shahidi, focusing on the production and distribution of video podcasts optimized for YouTube alongside audio releases on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, with short-form clips shared on Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok.[1][2]
The network supports podcasts featuring sports personalities and entertainers, such as UFC champion Sean O'Malley, NFL veterans Chris Long and Ryan Clark, and the Nelk Boys group, emphasizing unscripted discussions on athletics, culture, and lifestyle topics.[3]
Established as an extension of Shots Studios, it has secured backing from high-profile investors including Justin Bieber and Floyd Mayweather, raising approximately $15 million to scale operations and content partnerships.[2][4]
While achieving significant audience growth across platformsâ€”with over 600,000 Instagram followers and millions of YouTube viewsâ€”the network's associations with edgier creators like Nelk Boys have drawn indirect attention to content involving pranks, alcohol promotion, and political commentary that challenges mainstream norms.[5]

Overview

Founding and Evolution

The Shots Podcast Network originated from a software development venture founded in 2009 by brothers John Shahidi and Sam Shahidi as Rock Software, initially focused on mobile gaming applications.[6] The company expanded into social mobile experiences with RockLive, but by 2013, it pivoted to launch the Shots app, a selfie-oriented photo-sharing platform designed as a Snapchat alternative, which rapidly gained millions of users and attracted high-profile endorsements, including from Justin Bieber.[7] This shift marked the beginning of Shots' emphasis on user-generated content and digital engagement.[8] In 2016, amid challenges with app retention, Shots rebranded to Shots Studios, redirecting resources toward content production, talent representation, and multi-platform video creation rather than standalone app development.[8] The studio built a roster of social media influencers and creators, producing short-form videos for platforms like Vine, YouTube, and Instagram, while forging partnerships in music and entertainment.[9] This content-centric model laid the groundwork for diversification into longer-form media. By the early 2020s, Shots evolved into the Shots Podcast Network, specializing in video podcast production distributed primarily on YouTube, with accompanying audio episodes on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, and promotional clips across Instagram and Snapchat.[1] The network supports prominent shows featuring creators like the Nelk Boys, leveraging data-driven strategies to optimize viewer engagement and monetization through sponsorships and consumer brand integrations.[3] This transition capitalized on the surging popularity of video podcasts, positioning Shots as a key player in the independent podcasting landscape.[2]

Core Business Model

The Shots Podcast Network operates as a YouTube-focused multi-channel network (MCN) specializing in video podcasts, partnering with creators to enhance production, distribution, and monetization. It signs podcasters to its network, providing services such as video editing, thumbnail optimization, and algorithmic promotion on YouTube to boost visibility and subscriber growth, as demonstrated by deals with shows like I Am Athlete (hosted by former NFL players) and Hotboxin' with Mike Tyson in April 2021.[10] In return, the network shares in revenue streams, aligning with standard MCN practices where operators typically retain 10â€“30% of ad earnings and sponsorships, though exact terms for Shots remain undisclosed.[10] Monetization centers on YouTube ad revenue from long-form video episodes, which the network estimates generate substantial earningsâ€”its official channel reported projected annual income of $760 to $12,000 as of recent analytics, scaling with high-profile content from talents like Theo Von and the Nelk Boys' Full Send.[11] This is augmented by sponsorship integrations, where brands embed promotions directly into episodes; a notable example is the January 2025 mid-seven-figure deal with MoonPay for sponsorships across Theo Von's podcast and Full Send, targeting crypto and youth audiences.[12] Audio versions are distributed to Spotify and Apple Podcasts for additional ad-supported listens, while short clips are repurposed for Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok to funnel traffic back to primary platforms and amplify engagement metrics.[1] The model emphasizes multi-platform synergy over standalone audio podcasting, leveraging video's higher engagement (e.g., YouTube's visual format drives longer sessions and better ad fills) to create a content ecosystem. Inherited from Shots Studios' influencer management roots, this approach includes talent scouting and cross-promotion, enabling podcasters to access network resources like studio production without upfront costs, while the company captures value through revenue splits and exclusive partnerships.[13][1] This structure has supported scaling to over 287,000 YouTube subscribers by October 2025, focusing on comedy, sports, and entertainment genres with viral potential.[14]

Historical Development

Inception as Rock Software (2009â€“2011)

Rock Software, Inc. was founded in 2009 by brothers John Shahidi and Sam Shahidi, who had previously worked in the satellite television industry.[15][16] The company, based in California, initially focused on developing iOS applications, including mobile games and utility apps, capitalizing on the early growth of Apple's App Store.[17][7] The Shahidi brothers' first commercial release was the RunPee app, a utility that provided timed alerts for optimal restroom breaks during movies to avoid missing key plot points.[17] This app exemplified their early emphasis on practical, consumer-oriented software amid the burgeoning mobile ecosystem. By late 2010 and into 2011, Rock Software expanded into celebrity-endorsed apps and social games, partnering with high-profile figures such as boxer Mike Tyson to create interactive content like training simulations and fan engagement tools.[15] These efforts laid the groundwork for Rock Software's pivot toward entertainment-focused mobile experiences, though the company remained primarily a software developer without significant media diversification during this period.[16] The firm's operations were lean, leveraging the brothers' entrepreneurial drive to secure early app distribution and user traction in a competitive market dominated by emerging developers.[15]

Expansion via RockLive (2011â€“2013)

In 2011, Rock Software, founded by brothers John and Sam Shahidi in 2009 as a mobile gaming developer targeting iOS platforms, underwent an expansion and rebranding to RockLive, Inc., pivoting toward interactive applications co-developed with celebrities and athletes to enhance user engagement through branded content and games.[15][16] This shift capitalized on the burgeoning smartphone app ecosystem, where celebrity partnerships drove virality and monetization via in-app purchases and advertising.[15] RockLive produced specialized apps featuring mini-games, virtual interactions, and exclusive media tied to endorsers, including boxing-themed experiences for Mike Tyson, basketball simulations with Shaquille O'Neal, and music-infused content for Snoop Dogg, which collectively generated millions of downloads by integrating celebrity personas directly into gameplay mechanics.[15][16] Additional titles extended to soccer stars like Cristiano Ronaldo, emphasizing skill-based challenges and fan memorabilia to foster loyalty among niche audiences.[18] These efforts marked RockLive's growth from generic gaming to a celebrity-centric model, reportedly securing venture funding to scale development teams in Southern California.[16] By 2012â€“2013, RockLive had solidified its position in the celebrity app niche, amassing a portfolio that demonstrated the viability of talent-driven mobile products amid rising competition from platforms like Apple's App Store, though exact revenue figures from this era remain undisclosed in public records.[16] This phase honed the Shahidi brothers' expertise in digital partnerships, setting the stage for RockLive's eventual transition away from gaming toward social applications in 2013.[17]

Shots App Launch and Growth (2013â€“2017)

In November 2013, brothers John Shahidi and Sam Shahidi, building on their prior mobile gaming ventures, launched Shots (initially styled as "Shots of Me") as an iOS-exclusive social networking app focused on selfie sharing for users aged 13 and older.[19][20] The app differentiated itself by prohibiting text comments to minimize cyberbullying and negativity, instead allowing only positive "shots" (equivalent to likes) and later emoji reactions, fostering a safer environment aimed at teenagers disillusioned with platforms like Facebook and Twitter.[21][22] Canadian singer Justin Bieber, an early investor contributing to a $1.1 million seed round, served as creative director, providing endorsement that propelled initial visibility among young demographics.[23][19] The app experienced rapid adoption, reaching over 1 million users by April 2014, driven primarily by teenage girls sharing filtered selfies and short bios, with daily active users contributing to viral spread through word-of-mouth and Bieber's promotion.[21] By its one-year anniversary in November 2014, Shots had amassed millions of users, establishing a niche in positive, image-centric social interaction amid rising selfie culture.[22] To sustain engagement, the company introduced 10-second video "vines" in July 2015, expanding beyond static photos while maintaining content restrictions, which helped retain its teen base against competitors like Snapchat.[24] Funding supported scaling, with an $8.5 million round in April 2015 from investors including Major League Baseball and WI Harper Group, followed by $4 million in September 2015 led by DCM Ventures, bringing total capital to approximately $15 million by mid-2016.[25][26] This influx enabled Android expansion and acquisitions like music-sharing app Mindie in 2015, aiming to build a teen ecosystem, though growth began plateauing by 2016 due to intensifying competition from Instagram Stories and Snapchat filters, prompting a gradual shift toward original video content production.[27][28] Through 2017, the app maintained a core user base of several million, serving as a testing ground for influencer partnerships that foreshadowed the company's pivot to Shots Studios.[7]

Shots Studios Formation and Media Pivot (2013â€“2020)

In 2013, brothers John Shahidi and Sam Shahidi launched the Shots app as a selfie-sharing social platform targeted at teenagers, which rapidly gained traction with endorsements from celebrities like Justin Bieber and peaked at approximately 8 million users.[8][7] The app's initial success stemmed from its no-comment policy designed to minimize bullying, differentiating it from competitors like Instagram and Snapchat.[29] By mid-2016, amid intensifying competition and challenges in sustaining app growth, Shots rebranded to Shots Studios, formally pivoting from social app development to a digital media production and talent management company.[8] This shift involved phasing out the app's core functionality and redirecting resources toward creating high-production-value video content for platforms like YouTube, with the studio signing prominent creators such as Lele Pons and Rudy Mancuso.[8][7] The new model operated as a multi-channel network, offering creators production assistance, data analytics for content optimization, and marketing support in exchange for a revenue share, while forging brand partnerships with entities like Procter & Gamble and Coca-Cola.[8][9] From 2017 onward, Shots Studios expanded its media output, producing series such as "Awkward Puppets" featuring Mancuso and launching projects like a Mike Tyson-hosted YouTube comedy show, which contributed to creators collectively amassing over 85 million followers across social platforms and 3 billion YouTube views by that year.[30][9] The studio also ventured into music, collaborating with artists like Alesso and integrating data-driven strategiesâ€”led by Sam Shahidiâ€”to prioritize timeless, relatable content for sustained engagement and monetization.[7][9] Through 2020, this media focus solidified Shots Studios' role in digital entertainment, emphasizing original video and cross-platform distribution before a subsequent shift toward audio formats.[7]

Transition to Podcast Focus (2020â€“Present)

In the early 2020s, Shots Studios shifted its media strategy from primarily short-form video content featuring social media influencers to long-form video podcasts, leveraging YouTube's algorithm for conversational formats while distributing audio versions across platforms like Spotify and Apple Podcasts. This transition capitalized on the rising popularity of video podcasts, which combined the intimacy of audio discussions with visual engagement to drive higher viewer retention and monetization through ads and sponsorships.[10] A pivotal step occurred in April 2021 when Shots Studios signed high-profile podcasts including I Am Athlete, hosted by former NFL players Brandon Marshall, Adam Schefter, and others, and Hotboxin' with Mike Tyson, featuring the boxer in interviews with celebrities and athletes. These acquisitions integrated the shows into Shots' YouTube multi-channel network (MCN), enhancing production quality, clip distribution on social media, and cross-promotion to amplify reach.[10] The network further expanded in August 2021 by partnering with the Nelk Boys collective to launch Full Send Podcast, a business and lifestyle discussion series that quickly gained traction among younger audiences through irreverent humor and guest appearances from entrepreneurs and entertainers. This collaboration exemplified Shots' approach to podcasting: producing studio-based video episodes optimized for YouTube, supplemented by short-form clips for Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok to funnel traffic back to full episodes.[31] By 2022, Shots had formalized its podcast operations under the Shots Podcast Network banner, adding shows like The Pivot Podcast with ex-NFL stars Ryan Clark, Channing Crowder, and Fred Taylor, which dissected sports, business, and personal transitions in structured, high-production episodes. The network's model emphasized talent management for hosts with established fanbases, multi-platform syndication, and integration with merchandise ventures, such as tie-ins with Nelk's Full Send brand. This period saw sustained growth, with podcasts collectively amassing millions of views on YouTube and expanding into live events and branded content deals.[32][1]

Operations and Content Strategy

Podcast Production and Talent Management

Shots Podcast Network specializes in video-first podcast production, prioritizing high-quality video content optimized for YouTube distribution, supplemented by audio versions on platforms such as Spotify and Apple Podcasts.[1] The network employs professional production techniques, including studio recording, editing, and post-production to create engaging episodes featuring interviews, discussions, and entertainment segments.[1] Clips from these podcasts are repurposed into short-form videos for social media platforms like Instagram and Snapchat to maximize reach and engagement.[1] In talent management, Shots operates as an integrated division of Shots Studios, which functions as a talent management and production entity focused on digital creators and podcasters.[6] The network signs exclusive or partnership deals with prominent hosts, providing comprehensive support including production resources, multi-platform distribution, and monetization opportunities through sponsorships and partnerships.[33] Key talents under management include Theo Von, host of This Past Weekend, the Nelk Boys collective behind Full Send Podcast, and athletes such as UFC fighter Sean O'Malley and NFL player Maxx Crosby for shows like The Rush.[1] [33] Production oversight is led by co-founders John Shahidi, CEO, and Sam Shahidi, Chief Creative Officer, who leverage their background in digital media to scale content across streaming services.[34] Talent relations emphasize creative autonomy while ensuring alignment with network goals, such as audience growth and brand collaborations, as evidenced by partnerships like the 2025 deal with MoonPay for integrated promotions.[33] This model has enabled the network to host a diverse roster spanning comedy, sports, and lifestyle genres, with episodes often exceeding millions of views on YouTube.[14]

Multi-Platform Distribution

Shots Podcast Network primarily distributes its content through a video-first strategy centered on YouTube, where full-length podcast episodes are hosted and monetized via ads and sponsorships.[1] Audio versions of these episodes are then extracted and made available on major podcast platforms including Spotify and Apple Podcasts, enabling wider reach to audio-only listeners.[1] [35] This approach leverages YouTube's visual format for engagement while syndicating audio to capitalize on established podcast directories.[36] Short-form clips derived from episodes are disseminated across social media platforms such as Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok to drive viral promotion and funnel traffic back to full episodes.[1] The network also produces original content tailored for additional streaming services, including Netflix, Facebook, and other platforms, expanding beyond traditional podcasting into broader digital media ecosystems.[36] This multi-platform model supports cross-promotion, with episodes often referenced as available "anywhere you listen to podcasts" to encourage discovery across aggregators.[37] Partnerships with sponsors like MoonPay integrate ads across these channels, ensuring consistent branding and revenue streams regardless of the consumption platform.[12] By prioritizing YouTube as the core hub while fragmenting content for ancillary platforms, Shots Podcast Network optimizes for both depth of engagement and breadth of audience acquisition.[1]

Video and Short-Form Content Emphasis

The Shots Podcast Network centers its distribution model on video podcasts hosted primarily on YouTube, where full episodes are produced and uploaded to capitalize on the platform's algorithmic promotion of visual content. This format allows for enhanced production values, including guest interactions, reactions, and multimedia elements that enrich the viewing experience beyond audio equivalents. Audio versions of these episodes are then derived and distributed on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, positioning YouTube as the flagship medium for initial consumption.[1] Complementing full-length videos, the network generates short-form clipsâ€”typically 15 to 60 secondsâ€”from key podcast moments, such as debates, roasts, or humorous exchanges, to disseminate across Instagram, Snapchat, and TikTok. Examples include excerpts featuring UFC fighter Sean O'Malley critiquing Henry Cejudo or Nelk Boys' encounters in Japan, designed for rapid sharing and viral potential. These clips function as promotional teasers, directing users to complete episodes while exploiting short-attention-span platforms for broader audience acquisition.[5] This emphasis on video and short-form content reflects a strategic adaptation to digital media dynamics, where YouTube's 2.7 billion monthly users favor video podcasts for their perceived authenticity and entertainment value, outperforming audio in engagement metrics like watch time and shares. By prioritizing visual-first production, Shots differentiates from audio-centric networks, fostering a ecosystem where clips seed discovery and full videos sustain loyalty, as evidenced by the network's channel branding and cross-platform clip integration.[1][14]

Business Ventures

Consumer Goods Initiatives

Shots Podcast Network, through its parent entity Shots Studios, has pursued consumer goods initiatives primarily via branded merchandise and the beverage sector. In 2020, Shots Studios launched Lil Shop, an e-commerce platform dedicated to selling apparel, accessories, and other merchandise featuring content creators affiliated with the network.[38] This initiative expanded through partnerships, such as an August 2020 collaboration with NRG Esports, which integrated NRG-branded items into Lil Shop while enabling joint content production.[39] A cornerstone of these efforts is Happy Dad Hard Seltzer, a canned alcoholic beverage launched in June 2021 by Shots co-founders Sam and John Shahidi in partnership with the Nelk Boys YouTube collective.[40] The product, available in flavors like watermelon and pineapple, emphasizes a light, low-calorie profile targeting young adult consumers and has achieved nationwide distribution in the United States by 2023.[41] Happy Dad is prominently integrated into Shots' ecosystem, serving as a sponsor for multiple network podcasts including Full Send Podcast and One Night with Steiny, with creators modeling and promoting the drink in video content.[42] [43] Earlier explorations into merchandise date to 2016, when Shots Studios announced plans for creator-driven product lines, including apparel and jewelry collections developed by talents like Lele Pons.[27] By 2018, the company had produced physical branded items such as mugs, signaling a shift toward tangible goods to diversify revenue beyond digital media.[13] These ventures leverage the network's audience of millions across YouTube and social platforms to drive sales, though specific revenue figures remain undisclosed in public reports.[6]

Broader Entertainment and Partnerships

Shots Studios expanded into music production through its Shots Studios Music division, signing electronic dance music artist Alesso in early 2017 and leveraging digital creators like Lele Pons and Rudy Mancuso for collaborative music videos and content aimed at millennial and Gen Z audiences.[7] This initiative focused on data-driven content creation, generating over 40 million minutes of daily YouTube views at the time.[7] In February 2017, Shots Studios partnered with former heavyweight boxing champion Mike Tyson to produce a YouTube comedy series and an accompanying iPhone game, marking an early foray into interactive entertainment tied to celebrity talent.[30] The company has also secured content distribution deals with streaming platforms, including Netflix, to amplify creator-produced videos and sketches.[44] The Shots Podcast Network has pursued integrated brand partnerships that extend podcast episodes into promotional entertainment formats, such as sponsored segments and cross-platform clips. In January 2025, it entered a multi-year deal with cryptocurrency payment provider MoonPay as the exclusive crypto sponsor for shows including This Past Weekend with Theo Von and Full Send Podcast, incorporating ads and creative input from CEO John Shahidi, who joined MoonPay as a creative advisor.[12] Additional collaborations include integrations with brands like Gopuff for delivery promotions, Underdog Fantasy for sports betting tie-ins, and Manscaped for product placements within episode narratives.[45] These arrangements emphasize native advertising within unscripted, long-form discussions to maintain audience engagement.[45]

Reception and Impact

Achievements and Market Influence

The Shots Podcast Network has facilitated substantial audience growth for its partnered shows through YouTube-centric video production, with flagship programs like Full Send Podcast accumulating 2.18 million subscribers and generating millions of views per episode. Other network-affiliated channels, such as A Safe Place Podcast, have reached 362,000 subscribers, reflecting the effectiveness of its multi-channel network model in amplifying reach on the platform.[14] This video-first approach has enabled podcasts under Shots to leverage YouTube's algorithmic promotion, resulting in collective video views surpassing 41 million across network-distributed content.[46] Key achievements include strategic signings of high-profile podcasts to its roster, such as Hotboxin' with Mike Tyson and I Am Athlete, which expanded Shots' influence in sports and entertainment niches.[47] The network's management of Nelk Boys' Full Send ecosystem, including extensions like Full Send Golf, has driven cross-promotional success, with episodes featuring celebrities like Suga Sean O'Malley and Lil Yachty drawing significant engagement.[1] These efforts have positioned Shots as a leading operator in the YouTube podcast space, as noted by industry discussions highlighting its role in scaling creator content.[48] In terms of market influence, Shots has contributed to the mainstream adoption of video podcasts by emphasizing YouTube distribution alongside audio releases on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, and short clips on Instagram and Snapchat, thereby bridging traditional audio consumption with visual social media discovery.[1] This model has supported the broader industry shift toward video formats, where platforms like YouTube enable higher monetization through ads and sponsorships for visually engaging content, influencing independent creators to prioritize multi-platform video strategies over audio-only production.[48] The network's focus on bro-culture and unfiltered discussions has carved a niche in attracting young male demographics, fostering advertiser interest in targeted, high-engagement sponsorships.[47]

Criticisms and Challenges

The Shots Podcast Network has faced indirect challenges arising from co-founder David Dobrik's involvement in prior controversies tied to his Vlog Squad YouTube content. In March 2021, a former collaborator accused Vlog Squad member Dom Zeglaitis of rape during a 2018 video shoot orchestrated by Dobrik, prompting YouTube to demonetize Dobrik's channels for violating policies on child safety, nudity, and sexual content.[49][50] Sponsors including Dollar Shave Club and EA Games subsequently severed ties with Dobrik, citing the allegations.[51] These events extended to Dobrik's podcasting efforts, with his show Views losing brand sponsors amid the backlash, highlighting risks for affiliated ventures like Shots Studios' podcast arm.[52] Dobrik issued apologies in March 2021, acknowledging facilitation of unsafe situations but denying personal knowledge of the assault, though critics deemed the responses evasive and insufficient.[53][54] The fallout contributed to a temporary hiatus from content creation for Dobrik, potentially complicating talent recruitment and advertiser confidence for the network launched under Shots Studios.[55] No lawsuits or scandals have directly targeted the Shots Podcast Network itself as of October 2025, distinguishing it from broader industry pressures such as the 2023 advertising revenue downturn that led to widespread podcast layoffs and network consolidations.[56] The network's emphasis on YouTube-centric distribution and short-form clips may mitigate some audio-only market saturation issues, but association with Dobrik's history remains a noted reputational hurdle in media analyses.[55]
