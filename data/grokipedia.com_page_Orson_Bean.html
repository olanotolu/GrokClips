Orson Bean

Orson Bean (born Dallas Frederick Burrows; July 22, 1928 – February 7, 2020) was an American actor, comedian, television personality, and author whose career encompassed stage performances, film roles, extensive television guest appearances, and game show panel work spanning over six decades.[1] Bean first rose to prominence on Broadway in the 1950s, starring in productions such as Will Success Spoil Rock Hunter? (1955–1956), for which he received a Tony Award nomination, and later in Illya Darling (1967).[2] On television, he became a staple as a panelist on game shows including To Tell the Truth and a recurring guest on The Tonight Show with hosts Jack Paar and Johnny Carson, leveraging his quick wit and improvisational skills.[3] His film credits featured supporting parts like the psychiatrist Dr. Matthew Smith in Anatomy of a Murder (1959) and the editor in Innerspace (1987), alongside voice acting in animated projects.[4] Later in his career, Bean appeared in television series such as Dr. Quinn, Medicine Woman and Desperate Housewives, maintaining an active presence until his death.[3] On February 7, 2020, Bean was fatally struck by two vehicles while crossing Venice Boulevard in Los Angeles, an incident confirmed as a traffic-related fatality by the Los Angeles County coroner's office.[5][6]

Early Life

Family Background and Childhood

Orson Bean was born Dallas Frederick Burrows on July 22, 1928, in Burlington, Vermont, to George Frederick Burrows and Marian Ainsworth (née Pollard).[7] His father, an advertising executive and later chief of campus police at Harvard University, was among the founders of the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), instilling a household oriented toward progressive causes and activism.[7] [8] The family, distant relatives of President Calvin Coolidge—whom the infant Burrows reportedly urinated on during a visit—relocated to Cambridge, Massachusetts, where Bean spent his childhood amid the Great Depression in a cramped apartment.[3] Bean's early years were marked by familial volatility; his parents' marriage deteriorated, with his mother exhibiting jealousy and heavy sherry consumption, while his father eventually left the home.[3] Despite the activist environment shaped by his father, Bean later recalled little personal alignment with such pursuits, instead developing an extroverted personality and entrepreneurial instincts as coping mechanisms.[7] At age 16, following his mother's suicide—which he attributed in part to his refusal of a visit request—Bean left home, an event that profoundly influenced his emotional guardedness as detailed in his memoir and one-man show Safe at Home.[3] [7] [9] As a child, Bean pursued interests in magic, captivating neighborhood peers with sleight-of-hand tricks and amateur performances, foreshadowing his later entertainment career.[7] He attended Cambridge Rindge and Latin School, graduating in 1946 before enlisting in the U.S. Army.[10]

Military Service

Bean, born Dallas Frederick Burrows on July 22, 1928, was too young to participate in combat during World War II and instead enlisted in the United States Army following his high school graduation in 1946.[1][11] He served for approximately one year, from 1946 to 1947, during the post-war occupation of Japan.[1][11] Stationed in occupied Japan as part of the Allied forces stabilizing the region under General Douglas MacArthur's administration, Bean's service occurred amid efforts to demilitarize Japan, reform its government, and implement democratic institutions.[1] No public records detail a specific unit or rank for Bean, and his tour appears to have been routine occupation duty without notable combat or special assignments.[1] During or immediately following his enlistment, Bean developed an early interest in entertainment, including magic tricks and stage performances, which he occasionally showcased to fellow soldiers in informal settings.[12] This period laid groundwork for his postwar entry into comedy and acting, though his discharge in 1947 marked the end of formal military involvement.[1]

Adoption of Stage Name

Born Dallas Frederick Burrows on July 22, 1928, in Burlington, Vermont, the future performer adopted the stage name Orson Bean shortly after his discharge from the U.S. Army in 1947, as he transitioned into nightclub acts and early comedic routines.[1] Bean later explained that the pseudonym was bestowed upon him by a pianist named Val while performing at Hurley's Log Cabin, a Boston-area restaurant and nightclub, emphasizing its deliberate oddity to stand out in entertainment circles.[13] In a 1974 appearance on The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson, Bean detailed experimenting with variations such as "Hornsby Bean" or "Cecil Bean" before settling on "Orson Bean," selected for the juxtaposition of a grandiose first name—evoking Orson Welles—with a comically prosaic surname, which he deemed ideal for a comedian seeking memorable absurdity.[13] This choice reflected his post-war pivot from amateur magic tricks developed during military service to professional stand-up, where an eccentric moniker helped differentiate him from standard billing.[14] Bean retained the name throughout his six-decade career, never reverting to his birth name in public professional contexts.[3]

Early Career

Rise as a Comedian

Following his discharge from the U.S. Army in the late 1940s, Bean initially performed as a stage magician in small venues before transitioning to stand-up comedy in the early 1950s. His comedy act premiered at New York City's Blue Angel nightclub in 1952, establishing a foundation for his emerging reputation as a performer with a wry, anecdotal style.[15] He soon became the house comic at the Blue Angel, holding that role from 1950 to 1960, where he refined his material through regular appearances before discerning audiences that included entertainment industry figures.[3][16] At the Blue Angel, Bean adopted his professional pseudonym—combining the grandiose "Orson," inspired by Orson Welles, with the folksy "Bean" from a vaudeville comedian's suggestion that elicited audience laughter—shedding his birth name, Dallas Frederick Burrows.[3] This venue allowed him to develop a distinctive act emphasizing observational humor and personal storytelling, as he later reflected: "I found that I could make people laugh without a deck of cards."[15] The consistent exposure at the Blue Angel, a hub for up-and-coming talent, generated buzz and led to his first television forays, including a 1952 appearance as a panelist on I've Got a Secret. Bean's ascent gained momentum in the mid-1950s through broader media visibility, highlighted by guest spots on The Ed Sullivan Show and hosting duties on the CBS variety program The Blue Angel in 1954, which adapted his nightclub persona for national broadcast.[15] These opportunities showcased his quick wit and charm to mass audiences, transitioning him from niche nightclub success to a recognizable comedic figure and opening doors to game shows like To Tell the Truth, where his improvisational skills further solidified his fame.[17]

Blacklisting and Career Interruption

In the early 1950s, amid the House Un-American Activities Committee's investigations into alleged communist influence in entertainment, Orson Bean was added to the Hollywood blacklist following his attendance at two Communist Party meetings. Bean attributed this solely to romantic pursuit of a female party member, later recounting, "The reason I got blacklisted was not because I was a communist but because I was horny for a communist girl and she dragged me to a couple of meetings."[18] He described himself as pro-union like many young performers of the era but lacking any substantive ideological alignment with communism, emphasizing the meetings were a personal misstep rather than political activism.[19] The blacklist immediately halted Bean's burgeoning television career; after seven guest appearances on The Ed Sullivan Show and other CBS bookings, he was dropped overnight upon his election as vice president of a New York actors' union local, which drew scrutiny for perceived leftist ties.[18] This enforced a roughly one-year absence from TV work, during which Bean noted, "I stopped working on TV for a year" due to the stigma of suspected sympathies.[20] Bean mitigated the interruption by landing a Broadway role, which sustained his stage presence amid the television void. He later reflected on the episode nonchalantly, calling it "my own fault" for prioritizing romance over caution and viewing the personal inconvenience as secondary to broader career resilience, without the deeper persecution faced by others on the list.[19] By the mid-1950s, clearance came through lack of further evidence against him, allowing a return to variety shows and panel programs that propelled his comedic reputation.[18]

Performing Arts Career

Theater Work

Orson Bean debuted on Broadway on April 30, 1953, in the comedy Men of Distinction, playing Edgar Grassthal.[21] That same year, he appeared in the revue John Murray Anderson's Almanac, performing multiple roles including Mike Hammer and contributing the "Chinese Monologue" sketch.[21] In 1955, Bean starred as George MacCauley in Will Success Spoil Rock Hunter?, a comedy that ran for over a year until November 1956.[21] Bean's musical theater roles included Billy Turk in Nature's Way (1957) and Charlie Smith in Subways Are for Sleeping (1961–1962), for which he received a Tony Award nomination for Best Featured Actor in a Musical.[22] [21] He achieved one of his longest runs as Charlie Clinton in the comedy Never Too Late (1962–1965), which completed 1,007 performances alongside Paul Ford and Maureen O'Sullivan.[21] Shorter appearances followed in I Was Dancing (1964), a brief run, and Illya Darling (1967–1968), where he played Homer Thrace opposite Melina Mercouri.[21] Off-Broadway, Bean produced Home Movies and won three Obie Awards for it.[23] After his final Broadway production in 1968, he shifted to regional theater and became a mainstay in Los Angeles' small theater scene, including revivals like I Was Dancing at the Richard Basehart Playhouse in Woodland Hills and roles such as Merlyn in a 2005 concert production of Camelot.[3] [2]

Television Appearances

Bean's early television work included hosting the variety series The Blue Angel for 15 episodes in 1954.[24] He appeared as a panelist on the game show I've Got a Secret starting in 1952. Throughout the 1960s to 1980s, Bean became a prominent figure on televised game shows, serving as a longtime panelist on To Tell the Truth, where he appeared frequently across multiple iterations of the program.[17] He also made regular guest appearances as a panelist on shows including Match Game, Password, What's My Line?, and I've Got a Secret.[17] Bean guested over 200 times on The Tonight Show during Johnny Carson's tenure, often as a guest host or storyteller.[17] In scripted series, Bean portrayed Reverend Brim in the soap opera parody Mary Hartman, Mary Hartman in 1976 and its spin-off Forever Fernwood in 1977.[25] He provided the voice of Bilbo Baggins in the animated television special The Hobbit in 1977.[25] Bean held a recurring role as storekeeper Loren Bray on Dr. Quinn, Medicine Woman from 1993 to 1998, appearing across all six seasons.[17] Later, he recurred as Roy Bender, a love interest, on Desperate Housewives from 2004 to 2012.[17] Bean's guest spots spanned genres and decades, including episodes of The Twilight Zone in the early 1960s, The Love Boat and The Fall Guy in the 1970s and 1980s, The Facts of Life in the 1980s, Ally McBeal and Will & Grace in the late 1990s and early 2000s, and Modern Family and Grace and Frankie in the 2010s.[17]

Film Roles

Orson Bean debuted in feature films with a supporting role as the army psychiatrist Dr. Smith in Otto Preminger's Anatomy of a Murder (1959), a courtroom drama starring James Stewart that explored themes of military justice and psychology.[26] The film received critical acclaim for its screenplay and performances, earning seven Academy Award nominations. Bean provided voice work in animated adaptations of J.R.R. Tolkien's works, voicing Bilbo Baggins in Rankin/Bass's The Hobbit (1977), a musical fantasy that followed the hobbit's quest with Thorin Oakenshield. He reprised voice roles as both Bilbo and Frodo Baggins in the sequel The Return of the King (1980), concluding the animated trilogy with songs and battles against Sauron. In the 1980s, Bean appeared as Lydia's editor in Joe Dante's science-fiction comedy Innerspace (1987), which featured Dennis Quaid miniaturized inside Martin Short's body and grossed over $57 million at the box office. His role contributed to the film's blend of adventure and humor, earning it a Saturn Award for Best Special Effects. Bean portrayed the 105-year-old Dr. Lester in Spike Jonze's surreal comedy Being John Malkovich (1999), though much of his footage was cut from the final edit; the film, starring John Cusack and Cameron Diaz, received three Academy Award nominations for its innovative portal concept allowing entry into actor John Malkovich's mind.[27][28] One of Bean's final film roles was as Sam Rubinstein, a retired intelligence operative, in Antoine Fuqua's action thriller The Equalizer 2 (2018), starring Denzel Washington and marking the character's last on-screen appearance before Bean's death. The film emphasized themes of vengeance and loyalty among ex-spies.

Political Views and Activism

Early Associations and Blacklisting

Bean's early political leanings aligned with the progressive sentiments prevalent among post-World War II youth in entertainment circles, including advocacy for labor unions and social reforms. As a young performer navigating New York and early Hollywood scenes in the late 1940s and early 1950s, he expressed interest in union organizing, reflective of broader generational sympathy for leftist causes amid economic recoveries and anti-fascist sentiments from the war.[29][19] These associations culminated in his placement on the Hollywood blacklist around 1952, following attendance at two Communist Party meetings. Bean later attributed this involvement not to ideological conviction but to romantic pursuit of a female party member, whom he described as having "dragged" him along out of personal interest rather than political enthusiasm. He explicitly denied communist affiliation, emphasizing his actions were peripheral and driven by youthful indiscretion amid a dating context.[30][31][19] The blacklist, enforced by industry figures wary of HUAC investigations and Soviet espionage fears, curtailed Bean's access to high-profile television and film roles, though it did not entirely halt his work. He sustained income through nightclub comedy acts in cities like Baltimore and smaller theater productions, circumventing major studio gatekeepers. Bean reflected on the episode as more a product of personal entanglement than substantive radicalism, noting it resolved relatively quickly without formal testimony or prolonged ostracism.[31][32]

Shift to Conservatism

Bean's early flirtation with leftist circles in the 1950s, primarily driven by romantic pursuits rather than ideological commitment, resulted in his blacklisting by Hollywood, yet he maintained that his personal values remained consistent over time.[18] By the late 1960s, he publicly aligned with conservative positions, supporting Richard Nixon's 1968 presidential campaign over Hubert Humphrey, reasoning that only a conservative leader could prosecute the Vietnam War without accusations of softness toward communism.[30] In a 2015 interview, Bean attributed his conservatism to "common sense," citing disillusionment with the welfare state's destructiveness and the societal shift toward entitlement culture, which he observed eroding principles like Martin Luther King Jr.'s emphasis on judging individuals by character rather than skin color.[33] He insisted that he had not altered his core beliefs since his youth but that the left's embrace of what he called "lunacy of political correctness" had diverged sharply from them.[30] A pivotal influence was his religious conversion in the 1970s, sparked through a 12-step recovery program's search for a "Higher Power," leading him to Christianity and intertwining faith with conservative values.[34] This spiritual awakening reinforced his rejection of progressive ideologies, culminating in later activities such as authoring columns for Breitbart News and participating in Friends of Abe, a discreet network for Hollywood conservatives facing industry ostracism.[18] Family ties further solidified his stance; Bean introduced his son-in-law Andrew Breitbart to conservative thought via Rush Limbaugh's writings, aiding Breitbart's own ideological shift.[33]

Criticisms of Hollywood Leftism

Bean, who underwent a political transformation from early liberal sympathies to outspoken conservatism in the 1970s following personal and religious awakenings, frequently lambasted Hollywood's dominant left-wing ideology for fostering division and anti-American sentiment. In interviews, he asserted that contemporary entertainment industry practices involved more insidious "anti-American propaganda" than during the 1950s blacklist era, when suspected communists like himself faced exclusion for mere associations.[18][32] He argued that television and film routinely embedded political messages aimed at polarizing audiences rather than unifying them, contrasting this with earlier times when overt ideology was more contained.[32] Bean highlighted the suppression of conservative viewpoints in Hollywood, claiming it was "harder now to be an open conservative on a Hollywood set than it was back then to be a Communist," a reversal from his own 1952 blacklisting for dating a Communist Party member despite lacking formal affiliation.[35][3] He described the industry as "very divisive," where even apolitical arenas like sports commentary were tainted by partisan injections from liberal-leaning creators, eroding shared cultural spaces.[36] This critique extended to his observation that pre-McCarthy Hollywood had itself blacklisted right-wing figures under left-wing dominance, suggesting a historical pattern of ideological conformity that persisted into modern times with greater institutional entrenchment.[29] His son-in-law Andrew Breitbart credited Bean with catalyzing his own conservative awakening by recommending Rush Limbaugh's writings in the early 1990s, underscoring Bean's role in challenging Hollywood's echo chamber from within.[37] Bean maintained that this leftism not only stifled dissent but promoted narratives detached from empirical realities, a view he voiced unapologetically amid his continued work in theater and television until age 91.[18]

Personal Life

Marriages and Family

Orson Bean married actress Jacqueline de Sibour in 1956; the couple divorced in 1962 and had one daughter, Michele Bean.[38] Bean's second marriage, to fashion designer Carolyn Maxwell, began on October 3, 1965, and ended in divorce in 1981; they had three children together—sons Max and Ezekiel Bean, and daughter Susannah Bean.[39][40] In 1993, Bean married actress Alley Mills, known for her role on the soap opera General Hospital; the marriage lasted until Bean's death in 2020, spanning 27 years.[41][42] Bean was the father-in-law of conservative journalist Andrew Breitbart, who was married to his daughter Susannah from 1997 until Breitbart's death in 2012; the couple had four children.[43]

Religious and Philosophical Pursuits

Bean described himself as an atheist for much of his life, prioritizing career ambitions, personal pleasures, and material success over spiritual matters.[44] In his earlier years, he pursued psychological and alternative therapeutic approaches to address personal dissatisfaction following a decade of psychoanalysis that left him divorced and unfulfilled.[45] This led him to explore orgone therapy, a controversial theory developed by Wilhelm Reich involving purported life energy accumulation devices, which Bean documented in his 1971 book Me and the Orgone: One Man's Search for the Truth About Sex, Death, and Orgasm, framing it as an experimental quest for insight into human drives and existential questions.[46] A pivotal shift occurred in the 1980s amid struggles with addiction, when participation in a 12-step recovery program introduced the concept of a "Higher Power," initially resisted but eventually embraced out of desperation.[47] Bean recounted a transformative street encounter with a stranger who invited him to pray, during which he experienced a sense of divine presence, marking the onset of his Christian faith.[44] This conversion was deepened by reading C.S. Lewis's Mere Christianity, which provided intellectual grounding for his emerging beliefs in God's existence and human need for redemption.[35] Post-conversion, Bean openly shared his testimony, emphasizing a newfound sobriety, purpose, and rejection of prior hedonism, often analogizing spiritual emptiness to a "hole in the middle of us all" that only Christ could fill.[48] He participated in his wife Alley Mills's baptism in 2016 at First Lutheran Church of Venice Beach, reflecting his commitment to evangelical Christianity rather than nominal observance.[49] Bean viewed this faith as the capstone resolving his lifelong philosophical inquiries into meaning, mortality, and morality, crediting it with stabilizing his personal and family life until his death in 2020.[50]

Death

Circumstances of the Accident

On February 7, 2020, at approximately 7:35 p.m., Orson Bean, aged 91, was fatally struck by two vehicles while crossing Venice Boulevard mid-block near the Pacific Resident Theatre in Venice, Los Angeles, outside of a marked crosswalk.[5][51][52] Bean was walking alone eastward across the westbound lanes of the boulevard, reportedly en route to meet his wife, Alley Mills, for a play at the theater.[5][51] The incident unfolded when a westbound Honda Civic clipped Bean, causing him to fall to the pavement between Shell Avenue and Pisani Place.[5][52] As bystanders rushed to assist, a second westbound vehicle—a Toyota Prius—struck him, dragging his body approximately a quarter of a block before stopping.[5][51] Bean was pronounced dead at the scene by Los Angeles Fire Department paramedics.[52] Both drivers remained at the scene and cooperated with investigators, with no immediate indications of impairment or speeding reported.[5][51] Los Angeles Police Department Captain Brian Wendling described the event as a "jaywalking-type situation," noting that the first driver "did not see him and clipped him," while the second driver was distracted, leading to the fatal impact.[51][52] Witnesses, including the theater's artistic director Marilyn Fox, observed the crash, and local residents confirmed Bean's identity shortly after.[5] The Los Angeles County Coroner's Office ruled the death a traffic-related fatality, with the LAPD's investigation focusing on pedestrian safety in the area, where mid-block crossings are common despite visibility challenges in low light.[5][51]

Legal and Public Aftermath

The Los Angeles Police Department and Los Angeles County Coroner's office investigated Bean's death as a traffic-related fatality, determining that he was initially clipped by one vehicle while crossing Venice Boulevard outside a crosswalk, fell into the path of oncoming traffic, and was then fatally struck by a second vehicle that dragged his body approximately a quarter of a block.[5][52] No criminal charges were filed against either driver, with the incident ruled accidental.[53] Public response included tributes from colleagues and friends highlighting Bean's charisma and kindness; Barbra Streisand, for instance, recalled him introducing her to her first television appearance.[54] Friends gathered at the accident site to mourn, describing his "joyful and exuberant spirit."[55] Bean's death also prompted renewed attention to pedestrian safety in Los Angeles, particularly in Venice, where high traffic volumes and frequent jaywalking contribute to fatalities; advocates cited it as emblematic of broader issues addressed by initiatives like Vision Zero, aimed at eliminating traffic deaths through infrastructure and enforcement changes.[56][57]

Legacy and Works

Awards and Recognition

Orson Bean received the Theatre World Award in 1954 for his performance in the revue John Murray Anderson's Almanac, recognizing his debut as a promising newcomer on Broadway.[58] He earned a Tony Award nomination in 1962 for Best Featured Actor in a Musical for his role as Charlie in Subways Are for Sleeping, a satirical musical that ran for 205 performances.[22] In voice acting, Bean was nominated for a Grammy Award in 1979 for Best Recording for Children for narrating J.R.R. Tolkien's The Hobbit, sharing the recognition with the production team including John Huston.[59] For film, Bean received a Screen Actors Guild Award nomination in 2000 for Outstanding Performance by a Cast in a Motion Picture as part of the ensemble in Being John Malkovich, directed by Spike Jonze.[60] Despite extensive television appearances on game shows like To Tell the Truth and hosting specials, Bean did not receive Emmy Award nominations for acting or hosting roles, though his contributions to daytime and variety programming garnered informal acclaim within the industry.[17]

Books and Writings

Orson Bean authored four books, primarily memoirs exploring personal transformation, therapy, addiction recovery, and spiritual awakening. His writings often drew from his life experiences, including encounters with alternative therapies, substance abuse struggles, and eventual embrace of sobriety and faith. Me and the Orgone: The True Story of One Man's Sexual Awakening, published in 1971 by the American College of Orgonomy Press, recounts Bean's experiences undergoing Wilhelm Reich's orgone therapy, which he credited with liberating repressed emotions and reshaping his understanding of sexuality and psychology. The book details his sessions involving orgone energy accumulators, portraying the treatment as a catalyst for emotional release despite Reich's controversial status in mainstream medicine.[61] In 1988, Bean published Too Much Is Not Enough, an autobiography issued by Lyle Stuart, chronicling his career in entertainment, multiple marriages, and excesses in Hollywood, framed as a candid reflection on indulgence and its consequences. The narrative emphasizes lessons from overindulgence, aligning with Bean's later public persona as a critic of hedonistic culture. Mail for Mikey (2002, Barricade Books) presents a series of emails from Bean to a young recovering alcoholic named Mikey, whom he sponsored in a twelve-step program; the book serves as a practical guide to sobriety, blending humor, tough love, and Bean's own history of drug and alcohol abuse.[62] It highlights Bean's role as a mentor, drawing on his sobriety achieved through Alcoholics Anonymous after decades of substance use.[63] Bean's final book, Safe at Home (2013, Balboa Press), explores his conversion to Christianity and sense of spiritual security, referencing rituals like communion as evoking profound gratitude for redemption amid past chaos.[64] Written late in life, it reflects his shift toward faith-based conservatism, portraying home as a metaphor for inner peace found in religious commitment.[65]

Filmography and Recordings

Orson Bean's film career spanned over six decades, though feature film roles were relatively sparse compared to his extensive television and stage work. His debut came in Otto Preminger's Anatomy of a Murder (1959), where he played the defense attorney’s colleague, Dr. Smith.[4] Subsequent notable appearances included the role of Lydia's editor in Joe Dante's Innerspace (1987), the eccentric Dr. Lester in Being John Malkovich (1999), whose scenes were partially cut but still praised, and Sam Rubinstein, a Holocaust survivor, in The Equalizer 2 (2018).[25] [66] Bean also lent his voice to animated productions, including characters in The Hobbit (1977) and The Return of the King (1980).[24]

On television, Bean became a staple of game shows and variety programs starting in the 1950s. He served as a regular panelist on To Tell the Truth (1956–1968), appearing frequently enough to become a familiar face to audiences.[25] Guest spots extended to classic anthology series like The Twilight Zone and procedural dramas such as Murder, She Wrote, alongside later sitcoms including Desperate Housewives, Two and a Half Men, How I Met Your Mother, and Modern Family.[67] In his final years, he recurred as Bruno Romani in Grace and Frankie (2016–2020) and played Dr. Fogler in Superstore (2017).[66] Bean contributed to several audio recordings, primarily Broadway cast albums.
