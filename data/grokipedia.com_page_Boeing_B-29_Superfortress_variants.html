Boeing B-29 Superfortress variants

The Boeing B-29 Superfortress variants encompass the prototypes, production models, and specialized conversions of the four-engined heavy strategic bomber developed by Boeing for the United States Army Air Forces to meet a 1940 requirement for long-range, high-altitude bombing capabilities exceeding those of existing aircraft.[1] Approximately 3,970 units were manufactured between 1943 and 1946 by Boeing, Bell Aircraft, and Glenn L. Martin Company, with Boeing producing 2,766 at facilities in Wichita, Kansas, and Renton, Washington.[2][1] Key production variants included the XB-29 and YB-29 prototypes for initial testing, the baseline B-29 with remote-controlled gun turrets and pressurized crew compartments, and the B-29A featuring an increased wingspan for improved performance and relocated radar.[1] The B-29B incorporated reduced defensive armament and radar-directed tail guns optimized for low-altitude firebombing missions over Japan, with 311 examples built.[1][3] Specialized variants extended operational utility post-World War II, including the F-13 (later RB-29) for photo-reconnaissance, KB-29 for aerial refueling with flying boom or hose systems, WB-29 for weather reconnaissance, SB-29 for search-and-rescue with lifeboat droppers, and EB-29 as a mother ship for parasite fighters like the XF-85 Goblin.[1] These adaptations supported Strategic Air Command missions during the early Cold War, including atomic deterrence patrols and intelligence gathering.[4] The B-29's variants achieved pivotal roles in ending World War II, such as the Silverplate modifications of B-29s Enola Gay and Bockscar for delivering atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, demonstrating unprecedented destructive power from high-altitude precision strikes.[5] Additionally, four interned B-29s enabled the Soviet Union to reverse-engineer the design into the Tupolev Tu-4, producing over 800 copies that formed the basis of their strategic bomber fleet until the 1960s.[1] Despite initial challenges like engine reliability issues causing fires, iterative variants enhanced reliability and versatility, influencing subsequent bomber designs like the B-50.[1]

Prototypes and Pre-Production Models

XB-29

The XB-29 served as the initial experimental prototype for the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, a four-engine heavy bomber designed to meet U.S. Army Air Forces specifications for long-range strategic operations capable of reaching Japan from bases in Hawaii or Alaska. Boeing received a contract on August 24, 1940, to develop three prototypes under the designation XB-29, with construction occurring at its Seattle facility.[6] The design incorporated advanced features such as a pressurized crew compartment, remote-controlled gun turrets, and high-altitude performance optimized for the Wright R-3350 Duplex-Cyclone radial engines, which proved troublesome from the outset due to overheating and fire risks.[7] The first XB-29, serial number 41-2 (Boeing Model 345-1), rolled out at Boeing Field in Seattle and conducted its maiden flight on September 21, 1942, piloted by Boeing's chief test pilot Edmund T. "Eddie" Allen and co-pilot John "Jack" Schweibold. This 64-minute flight demonstrated the aircraft's basic airworthiness despite lacking full military equipment, with the prototype measuring 98 feet 2 inches in length, a wingspan of 141 feet 3 inches, and powered by four 2,200-horsepower Wright R-3350-23 engines.[8] Subsequent tests focused on structural integrity, aerodynamics, and propulsion, but engine fires repeatedly grounded the aircraft, including one incident shortly after the first flight that required extensive repairs. The second prototype, serial number 41-3, followed with its initial flight on December 30, 1942, also experiencing an engine fire that forced an early landing.[7] Tragedy struck on February 18, 1943, when the second XB-29 crashed minutes after takeoff from Boeing Field during a test flight, with Allen at the controls; an uncontrollable engine fire led to the bomber striking the Frye Meat Packing Plant south of downtown Seattle, exploding on impact and killing all 11 aboard plus 20 people on the ground. This accident, involving nearly 5,000 gallons of flaming fuel, highlighted the R-3350's reliability issues but did not halt the program, as the first prototype continued evaluations leading to production refinements.[9] Unlike the subsequent YB-29 service test models, which incorporated near-production configurations including operational armament and avionics, the XB-29s prioritized fundamental flight testing with provisional or absent defensive systems, such as dummy turrets, to isolate airframe and engine performance.[10] By mid-1943, data from the surviving XB-29 informed modifications that addressed propulsion flaws, paving the way for the B-29's combat deployment.

YB-29

The YB-29 served as the pre-production service test variant of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, designed to evaluate the bomber's performance under simulated operational conditions prior to mass production. Fourteen YB-29 aircraft were constructed exclusively at Boeing's Wichita, Kansas plant as part of an initial order placed on May 17, 1941, which included these service test units alongside early production bombers.[11][12][13] Following the flight testing of the three XB-29 prototypes, the YB-29 incorporated refinements such as the Wright R-3350-21 engines, an upgrade from the prototypes' R-3350-13 variants, along with four-bladed propellers replacing the three-bladed units used earlier.[12][7] The first YB-29 took to the air in late 1943, with subsequent aircraft entering service testing that summer to assess reliability, handling, and systems integration.[12] These tests revealed critical issues, including engine overheating and propeller failures, prompting dozens of modifications that informed the final B-29 production configuration.[12] The YB-29's evaluation program emphasized high-altitude performance, pressurization systems, and remote-controlled turrets, achieving a service ceiling around 33,500 feet and a maximum range exceeding 5,000 miles under loaded conditions.[10] While not intended for combat, the variant's data directly contributed to the B-29's deployment in the Pacific Theater, where production models demonstrated superior speed, altitude, and bomb load capacity over contemporaries.[14] Several YB-29s later supported experimental modifications, such as radar and armament enhancements, extending their utility into postwar testing.[12]

XB-39 Superfortress

The XB-39 Superfortress was a one-of-a-kind experimental prototype derived from the Boeing YB-29 pre-production bomber, specifically modified to assess the performance of liquid-cooled inline engines as a potential substitute for the radial engines originally designed for the B-29 Superfortress.[15][7] This conversion addressed early reliability concerns with the Wright R-3350 Duplex-Cyclone radials, which had plagued initial B-29 testing, by evaluating the Allison V-3420 as an alternative powerplant configuration.[16][17] The aircraft, assigned serial number 41-36954 and nicknamed "Spirit of Lincoln," originated as the first YB-29 built by Boeing before being transferred in April 1944 to the Fisher Body Division of General Motors at Cleveland, Ohio, for the engine refit.[7][18] It featured four Allison V-3420-17 engines, each a 24-cylinder liquid-cooled inline design formed by coupling two V-1710 twelve-cylinder vee engines to a common crankshaft, rated at approximately 2,100 horsepower per engine with turbosupercharging.[15][16][19] The modification preserved the B-29's pressurized fuselage, high-aspect-ratio wings spanning 141 feet, and tricycle landing gear, but required adaptations for the slimmer inline engines, including revised nacelles and propeller arrangements.[15] First flight occurred on December 9, 1944, at Cleveland Municipal Airport, conducted without turbosuperchargers due to production delays in those components.[15][17][18] Despite this limitation, initial tests demonstrated promising results, with the aircraft exceeding 400 miles per hour in level flight, validating the inline engine's potential for high-speed operation in the Superfortress airframe.[18] Subsequent evaluations confirmed a maximum speed of around 405 mph, a service ceiling of 35,000 feet, and an estimated range of 6,290 miles under optimal conditions, though full turbosupercharged performance was not extensively documented before program termination.[15] Further development ceased in 1945 as the Wright R-3350 engines achieved sufficient reliability for mass-produced B-29s already entering combat service, rendering the liquid-cooled alternative unnecessary despite its technical merits.[17][7] The sole XB-39 prototype was eventually scrapped postwar, with no additional conversions pursued.[15]

Primary Production Bomber Variants

B-29

The B-29 represented the standard production model of the Superfortress heavy bomber, entering service with the United States Army Air Forces in 1944 as a long-range strategic platform designed to conduct high-altitude daylight precision bombing. Developed in response to a 1940 Army specification for a "hemisphere defense weapon" capable of carrying 20,000 pounds of bombs over 5,000 miles, the B-29 incorporated advanced features including a pressurized crew compartment for operations above 30,000 feet, electrically heated suits for crew comfort, and a central fire-control system managing remotely operated turrets. Production commenced in 1943 at Boeing's Wichita facility, with initial deliveries plagued by engine reliability issues stemming from the Wright R-3350 Duplex-Cyclone radials' tendency to overheat and ignite fires due to carburetor design flaws.[11][20] Equipped with four Wright R-3350-23 or later -57 series air-cooled radial engines each rated at 2,200 horsepower, the B-29 achieved a maximum speed of 357 miles per hour at 25,000 feet, a service ceiling of 31,850 feet, and a ferry range exceeding 5,000 miles. Defensive armament comprised twelve .50-caliber machine guns mounted in paired turrets atop the fuselage, beneath the fuselage, and in the tail position augmented by a single 20 mm AN/M2 cannon in the tail turret, all controlled via a Sperry A-6 analog computer for synchronized fire. The internal bomb bays could hold up to 20,000 pounds of conventional ordnance, enabling missions far beyond the reach of earlier bombers like the B-17. Crew typically numbered 11, including pilots, flight engineer, bombardier, navigator, radio operator, and five gunners.[21][22] The first combat employment of B-29s occurred on June 15, 1944, when aircraft of the XX Bomber Command, operating from bases in Chengdu, China, struck steel complexes in Yawata, Japan, marking the initial U.S. bombing raids on the Japanese home islands. Early operations faced logistical challenges, including limited range necessitating staging from India for fuel, and persistent mechanical failures that grounded up to 60% of the fleet at times. Following the capture of the Mariana Islands in late 1944, the XXI Bomber Command relocated to Tinian, Saipan, and Guam, from where B-29s conducted over 3,000 sorties by war's end, shifting to low-altitude incendiary attacks under General Curtis LeMay's direction that devastated Japanese urban areas. Production across Boeing, Glenn L. Martin, and Bell plants totaled approximately 3,970 aircraft in the B-29 family by September 1945, with the base B-29 comprising the majority of pre-B-29A output before contracts were curtailed post-surrender.[11][23]

Silverplate Modifications

The Silverplate program, initiated on November 30, 1943, under the Manhattan Engineer District, designated modifications to B-29 Superfortress bombers to enable delivery of the atomic bombs Little Boy and Fat Man.[24] These alterations prioritized weight reduction, bomb bay reconfiguration, and enhanced release mechanisms to accommodate the 10-ton fission weapons, which exceeded the capacity of standard B-29 bomb bays.[25] A prototype, designated "Pullman" and serial number 42-6259 (B-29-5-BW), underwent initial modifications in late 1943, with flight testing commencing on March 6, 1944, at Muroc Army Air Field.[24] Key technical changes included the removal of the center fuselage section and radome to expand the bomb bay into a single 33-foot enclosure, eliminating the standard four bomb bay doors and rear gun stations.[24] An H-frame with sway braces supported the bomb, paired with a C-6 hoist, dual electric and manual release systems, and specialized carrier assemblies for secure suspension and deployment.[25] To optimize performance, most gun turrets and armor plating were deletedâ€”retaining only the tail gunsâ€”while later models incorporated Wright R-3350-41 fuel-injected engines, reversible-pitch propellers for precise low-speed control during release, and pneumatic bomb bay doors.[24] Additional features encompassed motion picture cameras in the bomb bays for documentation, a dedicated weaponeer station for arming and monitoring the weapon, and an electronics test officer position; these enabled a post-release 155-degree diving turn to evade the blast.[25] Each conversion demanded over 6,000 man-hours, reflecting the program's emphasis on reliability for high-altitude, unescorted missions.[24] Production proceeded in phases at the Glenn L. Martin Company's Omaha plant, selected for its secure facilities and proximity to testing sites. Phase I yielded one aircraft in 1943; Phase II produced 17 units arriving by October 1944; subsequent phases added enhanced variants under Project 98228-S, culminating in a total of 65 Silverplate B-29s. Of these, 15 were assigned to the 509th Composite Group for operational use, including the B-29-45-MO serial 44-27297 (Enola Gay), which dropped Little Boy on Hiroshima on August 6, 1945, supported by six accompanying Silverplates.[22] Three days later, B-29-35-MO serial 44-27282 (Bockscar) released Fat Man over Nagasaki.[25] These aircraft served as the U.S. military's exclusive atomic delivery platform until 1949, when they were phased out in favor of B-50 and B-36 bombers.[25]

B-29A

The B-29A Superfortress represented an evolution of the original B-29 bomber, incorporating structural enhancements to address early production challenges with wing fatigue and attachment integrity. Primarily manufactured at Boeing's Renton, Washington facility, it featured a redesigned wing assembly consisting of three sections bolted via a stub center section at fuselage station 47.75, which increased airframe strength but added approximately 706 pounds of weight and reduced internal fuel capacity by 213 gallons compared to the B-29's two-piece wing.[26] [27] This configuration maintained the same overall wingspan of 141 feet 3 inches while improving high-altitude performance and load-bearing capability.[28] Production of the B-29A totaled 1,119 aircraft, spanning 16 blocks from December 1943 to June 1946, with all units assembled exclusively at Renton to streamline wartime output focused on this variant.[27] Key improvements included upgraded defensive armament, such as four .50-caliber machine guns in the forward dorsal turret starting from block 20-BN, doubling the firepower against frontal attacks relative to the B-29's two-gun setup.[26] Later blocks introduced streamlined teardrop-shaped turrets, an enhanced autopilot with formation-keeping controls, and pneumatically operated bomb-bay doors that opened in under one second, reducing vulnerability during low-altitude runs.[26] The aircraft retained the pressurized crew compartments and remote-controlled fire-control system of its predecessor but benefited from refined engine installations using Wright R-3350-23 or -57 radial piston engines, each delivering 2,200 horsepower.[1] In specifications, the B-29A measured 99 feet in length with a height of 27 feet 9 inches, an empty weight of 69,610 pounds, and a maximum takeoff weight of 141,100 pounds.[28] It achieved a maximum speed of 357 to 366 miles per hour, a service ceiling of 31,850 to 32,000 feet, and a combat range of approximately 5,000 miles while carrying a 20,000-pound bomb load, serviced by a crew of 11.[28] [1] Armament comprised ten to twelve .50-caliber machine guns distributed across five remote turrets, plus provisions for a 20 mm cannon in the tail on early models.[27] Operationally, the B-29A entered service in the Pacific Theater in June 1944, initially conducting high-altitude daylight precision strikes from bases in China and later the Marianas Islands.[28] Under General Curtis LeMay's direction in early 1945, tactics shifted to low-altitude night incendiary raids, culminating in the March 9-10 Tokyo firebombing that destroyed 16 square miles and caused around 100,000 casualties using 334 B-29s.[28] Silverplate-modified B-29As, such as Enola Gay and Bockscar, delivered the atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945.[27] Of 414 B-29s lost in WWII, 267 resulted from engine failures, highlighting ongoing reliability issues despite the variant's refinements.[28]

B-29B

The B-29B was a lightweight modification of the standard B-29 designed specifically for low-altitude incendiary bombing missions against Japan. It omitted the forward, dorsal, and ventral defensive gun turrets, along with their sighting and fire-control systems, to reduce weight by approximately 7,000 pounds and enhance speed, range, and maneuverability at altitudes below 10,000 feet.[29][30] Retained armament consisted solely of the tail turret with four .50-caliber machine guns, while the aircraft retained the AN/APG-15B tail-warning radar for basic defensive awareness.[31] The bomb bay configuration supported up to 20,000 pounds of incendiary clusters, optimized for area firebombing tactics.[32] Production of the B-29B totaled 311 aircraft, all manufactured by Bell Aircraft Corporation at its Marietta, Georgia plant from January to September 1945.[3][33] This variant addressed production bottlenecks at Boeing's facilities by leveraging Bell's capacity, which had previously built B-29As, and incorporated field modifications proven effective in combat, such as simplified radar equipment deletion to further lighten the airframe.[12] The B-29B's lighter empty weight of about 69,000 poundsâ€”compared to the B-29's 74,500 poundsâ€”allowed for greater bomb loads or fuel efficiency in short-range Pacific theater operations.[32] In service with the Twentieth Air Force's XXI Bomber Command, B-29Bs arrived in the Marianas starting in March 1945 and quickly integrated into General Curtis LeMay's revised strategy of nighttime low-level attacks, which proved far more effective against Japanese urban targets than high-altitude daylight raids.[3] These aircraft participated heavily in major firebombing operations, including the devastating March 9-10, 1945, raid on Tokyo, where over 300 B-29s, many B-29Bs among them, ignited firestorms that destroyed 16 square miles of the city.[34] Their reduced drag and black-painted finishes minimized visual detection, contributing to lower loss rates despite flak exposure; only a fraction of the 414 B-29s lost in the Pacific theater were B-29Bs, primarily due to accidents or mechanical failures rather than enemy action.[29] Post-war, surviving B-29Bs were repurposed for reconnaissance or scrapped, with none entering long-term strategic bomber roles due to the variant's specialized design.[12]

B-29C

The B-29C was a planned production variant of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, envisioned as a direct modification of the B-29A to incorporate upgraded Wright R-3350 Duplex-Cyclone radial engines for improved power output and reliability over the standard models.[35][36] These enhancements were intended to boost overall performance, including potential gains in speed, altitude, and range, addressing ongoing issues with engine overheating and maintenance that plagued early B-29 operations.[37] However, no prototypes were constructed, and detailed specifications beyond the engine upgrade remained conceptual.[12] In 1945, the United States Army Air Forces authorized an initial production order for 5,000 B-29C aircraft to bolster strategic bombing capabilities amid wartime demands.[35][38] The contract reflected optimism about the variant's potential to extend the Superfortress's service life post-war, but Japan's surrender on September 2, 1945, rendered further heavy bomber procurement unnecessary, leading to the full cancellation of the program.[38][36] This decision aligned with broader demobilization efforts, as thousands of existing B-29s were already in inventory and the shift toward jet propulsion diminished the need for piston-engine upgrades.[12] The B-29C's abandonment prevented any operational testing or deployment, distinguishing it from realized variants like the B-29B, and highlighted the rapid pivot in U.S. military aviation priorities after atomic bombings and victory in the Pacific theater.[37] Elements of its proposed engine improvements later influenced successor designs, such as the B-50 Superfortress, which featured refined R-3350 variants.[12]

B-29D (XB-44)

The B-29D, designated as the XB-44 testbed, represented an effort to upgrade the B-29 Superfortress with more powerful engines to enhance performance amid ongoing World War II demands. In mid-1943, Pratt & Whitney converted a single B-29A airframe (serial number 42-93845) into a flying testbed for the R-4360 Wasp Major radial engines, each producing 3,500 horsepowerâ€”a significant increase over the original Wright R-3350 Duplex-Cyclone engines.[39][40] This modification aimed to address limitations in speed and range, with the XB-44 achieving a cruising speed approximately 50-60 miles per hour faster than production B-29 models during initial evaluations.[39] Key technical changes included installing four-row, 28-cylinder R-4360-35 engines, redesigned nacelles with the oil cooler intake repositioned farther back for improved airflow, a strengthened wing structure, and a taller vertical stabilizer to accommodate the added power.[35][39] Defensive armament was minimized to two .50-caliber machine guns in the tail, prioritizing performance testing over combat readiness.[39] The aircraft completed its maiden flight in May 1945, validating the engine integration but arriving too late for wartime operational deployment.[39][40] Production intentions followed the test flights, with an initial order for 200 B-29D bombers placed in July 1945, later reduced to 50 units after Japan's surrender on August 15, 1945 (V-J Day).[39] However, postwar military budget cuts and congressional resistance to funding extensions of wartime designs led to the program's effective cancellation; no production B-29D aircraft were ever manufactured.[35][39] To preserve development momentum and secure appropriations, the U.S. Army Air Forces redesignated the variant as the B-50A in December 1945, evolving it into a distinct follow-on bomber with further structural enhancements, including a redesigned fuselage and greater fuel capacity.[40][35] The XB-44 thus served primarily as a technological bridge, influencing the B-50 Superfortress's eventual production and entry into service in 1947.[40]

Testbed and Experimental Variants

XB-29E

The XB-29E was a single experimental conversion of a Boeing B-29 Superfortress, designated from serial number 42-24725 (a B-29-45-BW production model), modified as a testbed for an advanced electronic fire-control system.[41] The conversion began in 1946 to evaluate enhancements to the aircraft's defensive armament, focusing on remote turret control and sighting integration beyond the standard Central Fire Control setup.[12] [41] Testing of the XB-29E's fire-control modifications occurred primarily at U.S. Air Force facilities, with the aircraft serving in this role through 1959.[41] It underwent interim redesignation as EB-29 from 1948 to 1953, reflecting its experimental status, before reverting to XB-29E until decommissioning.[41] These trials contributed data on electronic upgrades for weapon systems, aiding post-World War II adaptations of the Superfortress platform, though no production integration of the specific XB-29E-tested features occurred in standard B-29 variants.[12]

XB-29F

The XB-29F designation applied to six B-29 Superfortress bombers converted for cold-weather operations, primarily in Alaska, with modifications including winterization kits to enhance performance in sub-zero temperatures and reductions in overall aircraft weight to improve handling and range under icy conditions.[32][12] These conversions were performed on existing B-29-BW production models built at Boeing's Wichita facility, adapting the aircraft for Arctic environments where standard B-29s faced challenges from engine icing, hydraulic fluid freezing, and reduced lift.[32] One documented example, serial number 45-21847 (a B-29-100-BW), underwent modification at Warner Robins Air Depot in Georgia during April 1946, receiving cold-weather adaptations and redesignation as a B-29F for electronic reconnaissance duties in Arctic conditions; it was later assigned to the 4134th Army Air Force Base Unit at Fairchild Air Force Base, Washington, before crashing into Lake Mead, Nevada, on July 21, 1948, during a research flight.[42][43][44] Such aircraft supported testing of B-29 suitability for high-latitude missions, potentially including speculative reconnaissance over the Soviet Union amid emerging Cold War tensions, though primary roles emphasized environmental adaptation over combat deployment.[12] No production followed the XB-29F conversions, as the variant served experimental purposes to inform broader B-29 fleet modifications for northern operations, with lessons applied to subsequent reconnaissance and weather variants like the RB-29 series.[12]

XB-29G

The XB-29G was a single experimental conversion of a Boeing B-29 Superfortress into a flying testbed for turbojet engines, primarily supporting General Electric's development efforts. Originally built as a B-29B-55-BA by Bell Aircraft Corporation at its Marietta, Georgia facility, the aircraft bore serial number 44-84043 and was delivered to the United States Army Air Forces on June 26, 1945. Post-World War II, it underwent modification at Schenectady County Airport, New Yorkâ€”General Electric's primary testing siteâ€”to accommodate in-flight evaluation of jet propulsion systems, retaining its four Wright R-3350 radial engines for propulsion while dedicating the bomb bay to experimental hardware.[45][12] Key modifications included a retractable trapeze or cradle mounted in the bomb bay, which extended the test engine downward into the B-29's propeller slipstream to simulate operational airflow conditions during flight. This configuration allowed for real-time performance data on thrust, fuel efficiency, and durability without the limitations of static ground runs or wind tunnels, facilitating rapid iteration in early jet engine maturation. The XB-29G evaluated multiple turbojet models, including the Allison J35, General Electric J47, and General Electric J73, contributing to advancements that informed subsequent fighter and bomber designs.[46][32][35] The aircraft operated until July 27, 1953, when it suffered fire damage at Schenectady County Airport during ground operations, rendering it beyond economical repair. It was subsequently scrapped at the same location on May 27, 1954, marking the end of the XB-29G program. No production followed, as its role was strictly developmental, and jet engine testing shifted to dedicated platforms thereafter.[45]

XB-29H

The XB-29H was an experimental testbed variant of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, consisting of one aircraft converted from a standard B-29A-BN model specifically to evaluate armament configurations.[47][12] This conversion focused on special defensive armament setups, allowing for trials of alternative gun turret positions, fire-control integrations, and related systems to enhance the bomber's self-defense capabilities beyond production standards.[41][48] The sole XB-29H, bearing serial number 44-62272, entered service in this modified role in 1947 and remained active until 1953, supporting post-World War II refinements to the Superfortress fleet's armament amid evolving aerial threats.[41] These tests provided empirical data on defensive armament effectiveness, influencing subsequent B-29 upgrades and related bomber designs without leading to a production series.[12][35]

YB-29J, YKB-29J, RB-29J

The YB-29J was a service test variant of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, consisting of six aircraft modified to evaluate the Wright R-3350-CA-2 fuel-injected engines and revised "Andy Gump" nacelles intended for late-production B-29A models.[49][12] These modifications aimed to improve engine performance and cooling efficiency over earlier variants.[49] Two of the YB-29J aircraft, serial numbers 44-86398 and 44-86402, were further converted to YKB-29J configuration to serve as prototypes for aerial refueling, testing the Boeing-developed flying boom system as part of the early KB-29P program.[49] These retained the J-series designation due to their experimental engine setup and were used to assess the feasibility of boom-mediated fuel transfer for extending bomber range.[49][12] Several YB-29J airframes were adapted for photographic reconnaissance duties and redesignated RB-29J, leveraging the test variant's airframe for specialized missions including long-range imaging.[49][12] At least two such conversions, initially termed FB-29J, focused on photo-reconnaissance equipment integration while maintaining the modified nacelles and engines from the YB-29J baseline.[49] These RB-29J examples supported post-war evaluation and operational testing in reconnaissance roles.[12]

Aerial Refueling and Support Variants

KB-29 Tanker Variants

The KB-29 tanker variants of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress were developed by the U.S. Air Force and Boeing starting in 1948 to provide aerial refueling capability for Strategic Air Command (SAC) bombers, extending their operational range amid Cold War demands.[50] These modifications repurposed surplus B-29 airframes by removing defensive armament and bomb bays to accommodate additional fuel tanks, with refueling equipment installed in the aft fuselage.[51] Between 1948 and 1951, a total of 208 B-29s were converted to KB-29 configurations, marking a pivotal advancement in mid-air refueling techniques that transitioned from rudimentary hose systems to more efficient boom operations.[52] The initial KB-29M variant, introduced in 1948, utilized a British-inspired looped hose system, with two flexible hoses trailed from boom operators positioned in the rear compartments.[50] That year, 40 KB-29Ms were produced alongside 40 modified B-29 receivers to test the system, which required the receiving aircraft to fly into a drooping hose loop for connection.[50] While functional for early demonstrations, including support for the B-50 Lucky Lady II's first nonstop circumnavigation of the globe on March 2, 1949 (requiring four refuelings), the hose method proved slowâ€”delivering fuel at rates under 200 gallons per minuteâ€”and prone to instability in turbulence.[52] KB-29Ms entered operational service with SAC units such as the 509th and 43rd Air Refueling Squadrons, primarily refueling B-29s and B-50s until phased out in favor of advanced designs.[51] Addressing the limitations of the hose approach, Boeing engineered the flying boom system, resulting in the KB-29P variant, which achieved initial operational capability in September 1950.[50] The U.S. Air Force ordered 100 new-production B-29s configured as KB-29Ps, with a rigid, telescoping boom extended from the tail gunner's station by a dedicated operator, enabling precise control and fuel transfer rates up to 1,000 gallons per minute.[50] This boom, pioneered for faster and safer operations, became the standard for subsequent U.S. tankers and supported SAC's global strike posture, including refueling missions during the Korean War such as Operation Fox Peter One from July 4 to 17, 1952.[50] KB-29Ps operated from bases like Walker AFB, New Mexico, and Davis-Monthan AFB, Arizona, until the mid-1950s, when they were supplanted by the piston-engined KC-97 Stratofreighter and emerging jet tankers.[51] Additional experimental designations included the YKB-29T, a prototype testbed for early refueling trials conducted by the U.S. Army Air Forces as far back as 1944 concepts, though full-scale implementation awaited postwar resources.[53] KB-29 variants collectively demonstrated the feasibility of routine in-flight refueling, influencing doctrines for intercontinental bomber operations and paving the way for modern aerial tanking fleets.[52]

Reconnaissance and Photographic Variants

RB-29J (F-13, F-13A, FB-29J)

The RB-29J designation encompassed postwar reconnaissance conversions of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, primarily derived from wartime F-13 and F-13A photo-reconnaissance models. These variants were modified by removing bombing equipment to accommodate additional fuel tanks in the rear bomb bay and installing specialized camera suites, while retaining the full defensive armament of up to twelve .50 caliber machine guns. The F-13 prototype, converted from a standard B-29, achieved its first flight on August 4, 1944, from the Continental Air Technical Training Center in Denver. Operational deployment began in late 1944, with the first F-13A mission over Japan occurring on November 1, 1944, flown by aircraft "Tokyo Rose" (serial 42-93852) from Isley Field on Saipan. Approximately 118 aircraft were modified in total, including 117 F-13As produced across Boeing's Wichita (30 units) and Renton (87 units) plants, plus four TF-13A trainers.[36][54][55] Camera equipment typically included three K-17B, two K-22, and one K-18 cameras mounted in the rear pressurized compartment, supplemented by a B-3 driftmeter and provisions for flash bombs to enable night reconnaissance. Crew complement stood at 11 to 13 personnel, incorporating a dedicated photo-navigator and cameramen. Between the end of World War II and 1948, surviving F-13 and F-13A aircraft received interim redesignations as FB-29 and FB-29J, reflecting their continued photographic reconnaissance role. In 1948, with the establishment of the United States Air Force, these were standardized as RB-29, RB-29A, and RB-29J, the latter incorporating upgrades such as fuel-injected Wright R-3350-CA-2 engines tested on six YB-29J prototypes, two of which were adapted specifically for reconnaissance.[47][54][12] Operationally, RB-29J variants supported strategic reconnaissance in the Pacific Theater during World War II through units like the 3rd Photographic Reconnaissance Squadron, providing critical imaging for bombing campaigns against Japan. Postwar, they transitioned to Cold War missions, including electronic intelligence gathering and overflights, and provided support during the Korean War from Yokota Air Base in Japan starting in 1950, assigned to the 91st Strategic Reconnaissance Wing. Powered by four Wright R-3350-23 radial engines each producing 2,200 horsepower, these aircraft achieved a maximum speed of 224 miles per hour, a range of 3,418 miles, and a service ceiling of 31,857 feet. Service continued into the mid-1950s, with the last variants phased out around 1956 as jet-powered reconnaissance platforms emerged.[54][47][36]

Specialized Operational Variants

SB-29

The SB-29, nicknamed "Super Dumbo," was a conversion of the B-29 Superfortress for air-sea rescue missions, primarily to support aircraft operating over extended ocean distances. Sixteen existing B-29 bombers were modified for this role, featuring equipment to locate and assist downed personnel.[12][35] Modifications included the installation of a droppable A-3 airborne lifeboat suspended beneath the fuselage, capable of being released via parachute to survivors in the water. Additional enhancements encompassed specialized radio gear for communication with distress signals and search radar for detecting life rafts or survivors. These aircraft retained the B-29's long-range capabilities, with a combat radius exceeding 1,500 miles, enabling extended patrols far from base.[56][57] The first SB-29s entered service with the U.S. Air Force's Air Rescue Service in February 1947, replacing earlier rescue platforms like the SB-17. During the Korean War, SB-29s conducted operations from late 1951 onward, dropping lifeboats and supplies to airmen forced down at sea, contributing to the rescue of numerous personnel amid intense maritime combat zones.[35][58] The variant's service highlighted the adaptability of surplus heavy bombers to non-combat roles, though it was eventually supplanted by more specialized rescue aircraft like the SA-16 Albatross.[56]

TB-29

The TB-29 designation referred to conversions of standard B-29 Superfortress bombers into crew training aircraft, primarily to instruct personnel in bombing mission procedures during the post-World War II era. These modifications typically retained the aircraft's core structure and Wright R-3350 radial engines but incorporated internal adaptations such as additional instructor stations, simulated bombing equipment, and enhanced instrumentation for multi-crew coordination exercises. Conversions were not part of a dedicated production run but involved repurposing existing airframes, with the first documented assignments occurring as early as March 1946, when examples like serial number 44-61975 were allocated to units such as the 426th Army Air Forces Base Unit's Crew Training Squadron (Heavy) at Mountain Home Army Air Field, Idaho.[59][40] In addition to bombing crew instruction, certain TB-29s served in auxiliary roles, including target towing to support gunnery and radar training by dragging sleeve targets or radar-reflective drones behind the aircraft during exercises. This versatility extended their utility into the early Cold War period, particularly as Strategic Air Command phased out combat B-29s following the Korean War armistice on July 27, 1953, leading to redesignations of surplus bombers for training duties to maintain proficiency in heavy bomber operations. Specific conversions occurred at facilities like Tinker Air Force Base's Oklahoma City Material Area during the 1950s, where airframes were reconfigured to meet evolving instructional needs before the B-29 fleet's overall retirement.[60][61][40] Operational service of TB-29s persisted into the late 1950s, though exact numbers of conversions remain undocumented in aggregate, with individual cases highlighting their ad-hoc natureâ€”such as B-29 44-84053 modified to TB-29B standards. One recorded mishap involved TB-29 44-70039, operated by the 5040th Radar Evaluation Squadron out of Elmendorf Air Force Base, Alaska, which crashed in 1957 during a training flight, underscoring the risks of operating aging heavy bombers in remote evaluation missions. By the mid-1950s, as jet-powered bombers like the B-47 Stratojet entered service, TB-29s were progressively withdrawn, with surviving examples often stored or scrapped at sites like Naval Air Weapons Station China Lake.[62][63]

WB-29

The WB-29 was a weather reconnaissance variant of the Boeing B-29 Superfortress, consisting of production aircraft modified for meteorological data collection and storm penetration missions.[40] These conversions included the addition of specialized equipment such as fuselage-top observation stations, air sampling scoops, and enhanced radar systems for detecting weather phenomena.[64] The designation "WB-29" marked the first use of the "W" prefix by the U.S. Air Force to indicate weather service roles, reflecting the aircraft's adaptation for long-range atmospheric reconnaissance.[40] Primarily operated by units like the 53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron and the 55th Weather Reconnaissance Squadron, WB-29s conducted missions including typhoon tracking in the Pacific and hurricane reconnaissance over the Atlantic.[65] The 53rd Squadron achieved several milestones with the type, including the first intentional flight into a hurricane's eye in 1946, demonstrating the aircraft's reliability despite its origins as a bomber platform.[40] Additional squadrons, such as the 514th Very Long Range Weather Reconnaissance Squadron on Guam, utilized WB-29s for regional storm monitoring, often featuring aircraft like "Typhoon Goon" (serial 45-21838).[65] WB-29 operations extended into the early 1950s, with examples documented at bases like McClellan Air Force Base in California as late as April 1952.[64] The variant's service ended around 1953 when it was supplanted by the more capable WB-50D Superfortress, which offered improved performance for sustained weather missions.[66] Incidents, such as the crash of WB-29A 44-61600 from the 53rd Squadron in October 1955 near Kirkby Lonsdale, England, highlighted operational risks during European deployments.[67]

EB-29

The EB-29 designation applied to several B-29 Superfortresses modified for experimental purposes, with the "E" prefix indicating exemption from standard operational roles. These aircraft primarily served as carrier platforms for launching and recovering parasite fighters and other test vehicles from the bomb bay. Modifications included a trapeze mechanism in the forward bomb bay to deploy and retrieve small aircraft, enabling tests of aerial docking and parasite fighter concepts during the late 1940s and early 1950s.[68][12] One prominent example was the EB-29B, serial number 44-84111, configured as the mothership for the McDonnell XF-85 Goblin, a compact jet fighter intended to defend bombers against interceptors. Flight tests with the XF-85 commenced in 1948, continuing until 1949 when the program was canceled due to docking difficulties and other technical challenges. The EB-29B remained in service for related trials until 1956.[41][38] Additional EB-29 conversions supported diverse experiments, such as the EB-29A (serial 44-62205), a prototype receiver for the KB-29P flying boom tanker system in 1950, and another adapted to carry the Bell X-1 rocket research aircraft prior to its transfer to a B-50 in 1949. Some EB-29s underwent modifications for upper atmosphere research and electronic systems testing, reflecting the versatility of the B-29 airframe in post-war development programs. These variants underscored the transition from wartime bombing to advanced aeronautical experimentation amid emerging Cold War requirements.[41][69][12]

Airborne Early Warning Configurations

In mid-1951, three Boeing B-29 Superfortresses were selected and modified as part of a full-scale development effort to evaluate airborne early warning (AEW) capabilities using the existing bomber airframe.[70] [35] These conversions focused on integrating advanced radar systems to detect incoming aircraft at extended ranges, addressing emerging Cold War requirements for radar picket operations beyond ground-based stations.[47] The primary modification involved reshaping the upper forward fuselage to install the AN/APS-20C search radar, a pulse-based system with a detection range suitable for long-endurance surveillance, originally adapted from maritime patrol applications.[70] Interiors were reconfigured to include additional operator consoles, electronic support equipment, and expanded crew accommodations for radar technicians, enabling real-time threat analysis and data relay to ground or fighter units.[47] No armament or bombing provisions were retained in these configurations, prioritizing sensor endurance over offensive roles.[35] These B-29 AEW prototypes underwent testing to assess radar performance, aircraft handling with the added dorsal radome, and integration with command networks, informing subsequent designs like the Lockheed EC-121 Warning Star.[35] [47] The program demonstrated the feasibility of heavy bomber conversions for radar picket duties but highlighted limitations in radar resolution and platform agility, leading to specialized four-engine propeller-driven AEW aircraft in production fleets.[70] The modified aircraft did not enter operational service and were likely returned to standard configurations or retired post-evaluation.[35]

MX-767 Banshee

The MX-767 Banshee was a short-lived U.S. Air Force project initiated in the late 1940s by Air Materiel Command to convert surplus Boeing B-29 Superfortress bombers into unmanned, long-range guided missiles capable of delivering nuclear warheads.[71] The concept sought to repurpose obsolescent B-29 airframes as standoff weapons, leveraging their established range, speed, and payload capacityâ€”up to approximately 20,000 pounds of ordnance at intercontinental distances exceeding 3,000 milesâ€”to bypass emerging Soviet air defenses during the early Cold War.[71] Guidance was envisioned through radio command from accompanying manned aircraft, with the unmanned vehicle potentially expendable or recoverable after bomb release, though specifics on recoverability remained undeveloped.[72] Development emphasized modifications for remote control, including autopilot enhancements, radio receivers for command signals, and potential removal of crew compartments to accommodate warhead storage, but no full-scale unmanned conversions progressed beyond conceptual studies.[71] The project designated the primary unmanned vehicle as MB-29 ("M" for missile), supported by manned B-29s fitted with guidance transmission equipment for flight testing; a speculative DB-29 drone variant was proposed as an escort or decoy but lacked confirmation in executed trials.[71] Ground and limited airborne tests of guidance systems occurred in manned B-29s between 1946 and 1950, demonstrating basic remote piloting for precision targeting, yet the effort received low priority amid competing priorities like jet propulsion and smaller missile programs.[72] A notable incident in 1948 involved a B-29 crash during Banshee-related guidance tests near Eglin Air Force Base, Florida, killing nine of the 13 crew members and highlighting risks in adapting complex heavy bombers for unmanned operations; the accident prompted legal scrutiny over classified project details but did not advance the program.[72] Ultimately, MX-767 was canceled without operational prototypes or deployments, as technological shifts toward guided ballistic missiles and turboprop drones rendered large, propeller-driven conversions inefficient and costly.[71] Elements of remote guidance research influenced later drone initiatives, such as MX-1457 Brass Ring, but Banshee itself yielded no production aircraft.[72]

Naval and Foreign Variants

P2B Patrol Bomber

The P2B patrol bombers consisted of four Boeing B-29 Superfortress aircraft transferred from the United States Army Air Forces to the United States Navy on March 14, 1947, specifically for long-range maritime search, patrol, and reconnaissance missions.[73][35] These conversions, based on B-29-BW production models built at Boeing's Wichita facility, were assigned Navy Bureau Numbers 84028 through 84031 and redesignated under the P2B series to reflect their patrol bomber roleâ€”the "P" for patrol, "2" as the second Boeing design in that category, and "B" for bomber.[35] The aircraft retained the B-29's core four-engine configuration with Wright R-3350 radial engines, pressurized crew compartments, and remote-controlled turrets, but received naval modifications including updated avionics for over-water operations and provisions for search radar.[73] Of the four P2Bs, three were dedicated to operational patrol duties during the early Cold War era, focusing on anti-submarine warfare and surface surveillance over vast oceanic expanses. One aircraft operated as a P2B-1S for general long-range patrol, while two were configured as P2B-2S variants optimized for anti-submarine missions, potentially incorporating sonobuoy launchers or magnetic anomaly detectors adapted from Army Air Forces equipment.[74][73] These aircraft conducted extended sorties from bases such as Naval Air Station Patuxent River, leveraging the B-29's 5,600-mile ferry range and 20-hour endurance to cover transoceanic routes, detecting potential threats amid rising tensions with the Soviet Union.[73] Their service highlighted the Navy's interim use of heavy bombers for maritime roles pending dedicated patrol platforms like the Lockheed P2V Neptune. The fourth P2B-1S, Bureau Number 84029 (ex-USAAF 45-21787), was loaned to the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) in 1949 and extensively modified as a drop platform for the Douglas D-558-2 Skyrocket supersonic research aircraft, earning the nickname "Fertile Myrtle."[74][75] The forward bomb bay was rebuilt with a trapeze mechanism and strengthened structure to air-launch the 15,787-pound Skyrocket at altitudes up to 34,000 feet, supporting 41 drops between 1949 and 1956 that advanced high-speed aerodynamics data.[75][76] Following the program's conclusion in December 1956, the aircraft was returned to Navy custody, struck from service, and later transferred to civilian ownership, where it remains preserved in disassembled form.[74][75] The P2B conversions represented a pragmatic adaptation of surplus wartime bombers to fill gaps in naval long-range capabilities, but their operational lifespan was limited by the emergence of turbine-powered patrol aircraft and the high maintenance demands of the piston-engine B-29 airframe. All four were retired by the mid-1950s, with no further production or major upgrades pursued.[73]

Tupolev Tu-4

The Tupolev Tu-4 originated from the Soviet Union's internment of three Boeing B-29 Superfortresses that made emergency landings in Vladivostok in late 1944 due to engine failures during combat missions against Japanese targets.[77] These aircraft, named Ramp Tramp, Dinah Might, and a third unnamed B-29, were repaired by Soviet engineers and ferried to Moscow for detailed examination by the Tupolev design bureau.[78] Under Andrei Tupolev's leadership, a team of over 1,000 specialists disassembled the B-29s component by component, replicating airframes, systems, and pressurized cabins while converting imperial measurements to metric equivalents.[79] The first Tu-4 prototype completed its maiden flight on May 19, 1947, from a Moscow airfield, demonstrating near-identical external appearance to the B-29 but incorporating Soviet Shvetsov ASh-73 radial engines rated at 2,400 horsepower each, as reverse-engineering the Wright R-3350 proved challenging due to metallurgical differences.[80] Initial testing revealed issues with engine reliability and fire-prone wiring, echoing B-29 problems, but these were mitigated through iterative modifications.[81] Production commenced in 1947 at multiple facilities, including Factory No. 18 in Kuibyshev, Factory No. 22 in Moscow, Factory No. 23 in Filino, and Factory No. 160 in Molotov, with serial production scaling up by 1949.[82] Approximately 847 Tu-4 bombers entered Soviet service, forming the backbone of Long-Range Aviation until the mid-1950s, when jet-powered designs like the Tu-16 displaced them.[82] The aircraft achieved operational status in 1949, capable of delivering up to 12,000 kg of bombs over ranges exceeding 5,000 km, though actual combat deployment was limited, with primary roles in strategic deterrence during the early Cold War.[79] Some units were exported to China in the late 1950s, where production continued under license as the Xian H-4, extending service life into the 1980s for maritime patrol and training.[80] Key differences from the B-29 included a 1-2% weight increase to about 63,500 kg maximum takeoff mass, attributable to denser Soviet materials and wiring variations, alongside a higher service ceiling of 11,900 m but marginally reduced top speed of 558 km/h.[81] Defensive armament comprised ten 23 mm NR-23 cannons in powered turrets, offering greater firepower than the B-29's .50 caliber machine guns, while avionics integrated Soviet radar and radio systems for compatibility.[78] Despite these adaptations, the Tu-4's piston-engine limitations hastened its obsolescence against emerging turbojet threats, leading to retirement from frontline bomber roles by 1960.[79]
