New Year's Eve Live

New Year's Eve Live with Anderson Cooper and Andy Cohen is an annual live television special produced and broadcast by CNN Worldwide on December 31, originating from Times Square in New York City to cover the Times Square Ball drop and New Year's celebrations.[1][2] The program features co-hosts Anderson Cooper, who has anchored CNN's New Year's Eve coverage since 2002, and Andy Cohen, who joined as co-host in 2017 following the departure of previous co-host Kathy Griffin amid controversy.[3][3] It includes musical performances, celebrity interviews, comedic segments, and global New Year's festivities, often drawing top cable television ratings for the event.[4] The special has evolved from a primarily journalistic broadcast to one noted for its hosts' informal, alcohol-fueled banter, which has both boosted its popularity and sparked debates over journalistic decorum on air.[5][3]

Program Background

Origins and development

CNN's New Year's Eve coverage originated within the network's practice of providing continuous global event programming, exemplified by its 100-hour broadcast tracking millennium celebrations and Y2K concerns on December 31, 1999.[6] The branded New Year's Eve Live format, centered on live reporting from New York City's Times Square, debuted on December 31, 2002, with Anderson Cooper as anchor, capturing the ball drop and crowd energy while integrating news segments on international festivities and domestic security.[3] This shift emphasized on-the-ground journalism from the epicenter of American New Year's observances, distinguishing it from prior studio-based or multi-continent relays. The program evolved from a news-centric broadcast in its formative years (2003â€“2006), where Cooper often hosted solo or with rotating correspondents, prioritizing factual updates on weather, traffic, and post-9/11 safety protocols amid the revelry. To enhance viewer engagement and compete with entertainment rivals like ABC's Dick Clark's New Year's Rockin' Eve, CNN paired Cooper with comedian Kathy Griffin as co-host starting December 31, 2007, introducing comedic banter, celebrity interviews, and lighthearted segments that blended hard news with pop culture appeal.[7] This duo's dynamic, spanning a decade until 2016, expanded the runtime to primetime starts at 8 p.m. ET and incorporated musical performances, fostering higher ratings through Griffin's provocative humor despite periodic FCC complaints over language. Following Griffin's dismissal in May 2017 after a controversial photo shoot depicting a mock severed head of then-President Donald Trump, CNN selected Bravo's Andy Cohen to co-host the December 31, 2017, edition, maintaining the hybrid format while infusing reality TV flair and moderated on-air libations.[8][9] The Cohen-Cooper partnership has since solidified the show's structure, with extensions to streaming on Max, global correspondent feeds, and high-profile guests, culminating in record cable viewershipâ€”such as 15.8 million total viewers for the 2024â€“2025 broadcastâ€”while adapting to digital metrics and alcohol policy fluctuations.[4]

Format and production elements

New Year's Eve Live is structured as a live television special broadcast from Times Square in New York City, airing for approximately 4.5 hours starting at 8:00 p.m. ET on December 31 and continuing past the midnight ball drop to cover early new year festivities.[10] The format emphasizes continuous host commentary and improvisation, blended with scheduled musical performances by artists, celebrity guest appearancesâ€”often via remote feeds from locations like Las Vegas residenciesâ€”comedic interludes, and crowd interactions amid the outdoor celebration.[1] [11] Production planning commences in late August with guest booking and logistics, escalating in December; the crew loads equipment into Times Square the day before airtime, coordinating closely with the Times Square Alliance for event infrastructure and the New York Police Department for security amid large crowds and variable weather.[10] Technical setup incorporates a multi-camera system, potentially up to 21 cameras sharing resources from Countdown Entertainment's ball drop production, supported by video and audio engineers, photojournalists, and field producers to manage live switching and audio capture in an open-air environment.[12] [10] Key personnel include executive producer Eric Hall and supervising producer Deborah Doft, overseeing a patchwork team drawn from CNN's global units for seamless execution; additional elements like virtual production technologies, such as Ncam tracking systems, have enabled remote "beaming" of guests into the Times Square set during select broadcasts.[10] [13] The special is directed live, as exemplified by Brett Kelly in recent editions, ensuring real-time adaptation to performances and surprises while maintaining broadcast quality through redundant systems for reliability in high-stakes, unscripted conditions.[14]

Kathy Griffin era (2007â€“2016)

Kathy Griffin first joined Anderson Cooper as co-host for CNN's New Year's Eve Live broadcast on December 31, 2007, marking the beginning of a decade-long partnership that introduced a comedic counterpoint to Cooper's journalistic demeanor.[3][20] Griffin's role involved delivering irreverent, often profane humor and celebrity gossip, which contrasted sharply with Cooper's focus on live event coverage from Times Square, including the ball drop and crowd reactions.[21] This dynamic quickly became a hallmark of the program, with Griffin frequently engaging in antics such as impromptu songs, audience interactions, and provocative commentary, such as her 2009 remark during coverage of the Balloon Boy hoax where she quipped about the incident's absurdity. The format during this period remained centered on a live multi-hour telecast originating from Times Square in New York City, typically airing from 8:00 p.m. ET to shortly after midnight, incorporating remote reports from global celebrations, musical performances, and interviews with passersby.[22] Griffin's contributions often included physical comedy and boundary-pushing moments, such as stripping to her underwear during the 2010 broadcast or kissing Cooper on-air in 2011, which elicited visible discomfort and laughter from the anchor, enhancing the show's entertainment value through unscripted banter.[23] These interactions frequently led to viral clips, with compilations highlighting instances where Griffin's persistence caused Cooper to break character and "giggle like a schoolgirl."[24] Viewership for the Griffin-Cooper broadcasts grew steadily, culminating in the December 31, 2016, editionâ€”their tenth consecutive yearâ€”which drew over 3 million total viewers and secured the top spot among cable news programs in both total audience and the adults 25-54 demographic.[25] The pairing's appeal lay in its blend of lighthearted escapism and live spectacle, though Griffin's edgy style occasionally sparked criticism for vulgarity amid the family-oriented holiday programming.[3] By 2016, the era had established the special as a ratings staple for CNN, relying on the hosts' chemistry to differentiate it from competitors like Fox News and ABC's Dick Clark's New Year's Rockin' Eve.[26]

Andy Cohen era (2017â€“present)

Andy Cohen, executive producer and host of Bravo's Watch What Happens Live, was selected to co-host CNN's New Year's Eve Live with Anderson Cooper starting in 2017, following Kathy Griffin's departure amid controversy. CNN announced the pairing on October 11, 2017, positioning Cohen's entertainment background as a complement to Cooper's straight-news style, aiming to inject levity and celebrity appeal into the Times Square broadcast.[27] The inaugural joint hosting occurred on December 31, 2017, marking a shift toward a more dynamic, banter-filled format that emphasized on-air camaraderie, including occasional alcohol consumption to heighten the festive atmosphere.[28] The Cooper-Cohen duo quickly established a signature rapport, with Cohen often steering conversations toward pop culture and gossip, contrasting Cooper's discomfort with such topics, which generated viral moments like impromptu shots and playful jabs. This chemistry contributed to strong initial viewership, as the 2017-2018 broadcast became CNN's most-watched New Year's Eve special to date, averaging over 2 million viewers in key demographics during the midnight hour.[29] The format retained core elementsâ€”live performances, correspondent reports from global parties, and the Times Square ball dropâ€”but leaned into the hosts' unscripted interactions, occasionally testing CNN's content guidelines on language and decorum.[5] Since 2018, Cohen and Cooper have hosted annually, with the special airing from 8 p.m. ET on December 31, expanding to include streaming on platforms like CNN Max in later years. Viewership has fluctuated but remained competitive; the 2024-2025 edition drew 3.7 million viewers from 12 to 12:30 a.m., totaling 15.8 million across TV and streaming, a 40% increase from the prior year and ranking as cable's top New Year's program.[30][4] Cohen's role has solidified the broadcast's entertainment pivot, sustaining its position as a staple amid cord-cutting trends, though reliant on the hosts' enduring friendship for authenticity.[31]

Broadcast History

Early editions (2003â€“2006)

The early editions of CNN's New Year's Eve coverage from 2003 to 2006 established Anderson Cooper as the lead anchor for the network's live broadcast from Times Square, emphasizing the midnight ball drop, on-site crowd reporting, and retrospective segments on major events of the preceding year.[32] These broadcasts, often titled "New Year's Eve in Times Square" or simply "New Year's Eve Special," aired in the late evening hours leading into midnight Eastern Time, incorporating remote feeds from international celebrations and domestic festivities to provide global context.[33] Unlike later iterations with co-hosts focused on entertainment, these shows maintained a primarily journalistic tone, blending live event coverage with correspondent dispatches and occasional celebrity remotes, while avoiding the comedic interludes that characterized subsequent years.[34] In the December 31, 2003, edition, Cooper anchored from Times Square, featuring live segments with Hugh Hefner from the Playboy Mansion alongside performers in bunny costumes and a drop-in from singer Cyndi Lauper to assist in the countdown.[32] The program included a review of 2003's key news moments as reported by CNN correspondents, underscoring the broadcast's roots in year-end journalism rather than spectacle.[32] The 2004 special, aired on December 31, similarly centered on Times Square festivities, with Cooper previewing unique traditions such as the drag queen ball drop in Key West, Florida, and live reports on international New Year's parties, maintaining an emphasis on real-time global synchronization.[33] By 2005 and 2006, the format solidified under Cooper's solo hosting, with the 2005 broadcast incorporating a year-in-review montage of significant events and worldwide celebrations, while the January 1, 2006, airing (reflecting the prior evening's coverage) highlighted Times Square's atmosphere post-midnight alongside memorable 2005 highlights.[35][34] These editions drew on CNN's production resources for multi-feed integration but lacked the celebrity-heavy lineup or on-air banter that emerged later, prioritizing factual reporting of the Times Square event amid heightened post-9/11 security measures in the area.[34]

Expansion and Griffin years (2007â€“2011)

In 2007, CNN expanded its New Year's Eve coverage by pairing anchor Anderson Cooper with comedian Kathy Griffin as co-host for the first time, shifting from Cooper's prior solo journalistic style to a hybrid format blending news reporting with comedic banter. The broadcast, titled New Year's Eve Live with Anderson Cooper in Times Square, originated from New York City's Times Square and incorporated live reports from global celebrations in cities including London, Berlin, Dubai, Tokyo, and Beijing. Musical performances featured Earth, Wind & Fire, Miranda Lambert, and Widespread Panic, enhancing the program's entertainment value alongside traditional ball-drop countdown elements.[36][3] Griffin's addition introduced a provocative, irreverent tone that contrasted Cooper's professional demeanor, fostering on-air moments of humor and discomfort that became signature to the special. This dynamic aimed to broaden appeal beyond news audiences, though it occasionally sparked internal network tensions, as Cooper later noted initial reservations about the pairing promoted by CNN. The 2007 edition set the template for subsequent years, with Griffin providing unscripted commentary on celebrities and pop culture while Cooper anchored Times Square festivities and international feeds.[37] From 2008 to 2011, the program solidified its expanded structure, maintaining multi-hour coverage starting in the evening and emphasizing live improvisation between hosts. In 2009, Griffin drew criticism for an on-air rant criticizing the network's language restrictions, leading to a post-broadcast reprimand from CNN executives for her use of profanity and attacks on producer Amy Entelis. By the 2010â€“2011 broadcast, Griffin's antics escalated when she kissed a crew member, then partially stripped and forcibly kissed Cooper on the lips, moments that highlighted the duo's unpredictable chemistry but also tested broadcast boundaries. These years marked growing cultural notoriety for the special, with Griffin's role evolving into a foil that elicited Cooper's involuntary laughter, though specific viewership data from the period remains limited in public records.[38][3]

Peak viewership period (2012â€“2016)

During the years 2012 through 2016, CNN's New Year's Eve Live, co-hosted by Anderson Cooper and Kathy Griffin, achieved its highest viewership figures within Griffin's decade-long run, consistently outpacing other cable news networks in key demographics and total audiences for the late-night segments leading into the Times Square ball drop.[39][40] The duo's dynamicâ€”Cooper's professional restraint juxtaposed with Griffin's irreverent humor, including on-air stunts like mock stripteases and celebrity roastsâ€”drew audiences seeking a blend of news coverage and entertainment, amplified by live performances and remote segments from global locations.[41] Viewership grew steadily across the period. The edition ringing in 2013 averaged 2.3 million total viewers, surpassing the prior year's performance and ranking first among cable news outlets.[41] By the 2015 edition (ringing in 2015), the full special from 9:00 p.m. to 12:30 a.m. ET averaged 1.597 million viewers, with the midnight-to-12:30 a.m. window peaking at 2.622 million, again topping cable news competitors like Fox News (1.170 million total viewers) and MSNBC.[42][39] The apex came in the 2016 edition (ringing in 2017, Griffin's final appearance), which delivered 3.042 million average viewers from 11:00 p.m. to 12:30 a.m. ET, CNN's strongest New Year's Eve performance up to that point and enough to edge Fox News overall despite the latter's lead in some earlier hours.[40][25] This marked a roughly 68% increase from the 1.805 million viewers in the equivalent 2011 segment (ringing in 2012), reflecting cumulative gains from format refinements like extended pre-ball-drop programming and Griffin's growing notoriety.[43]

2017 transition and aftermath

In May 2017, comedian Kathy Griffin, who had co-hosted CNN's New Year's Eve Live with Anderson Cooper since 2007, faced widespread backlash after releasing photographs depicting her holding a bloodied, severed-head prop resembling President Donald Trump. CNN subsequently terminated its professional relationship with Griffin, stating the images were "disgusting and completely inappropriate."[44] This incident effectively ended her involvement with the network's annual broadcast.[45] On October 12, 2017, CNN announced that Bravo executive and Watch What Happens Live host Andy Cohen would replace Griffin as Cooper's co-host for the December 31 special.[45][44] The transition aimed to maintain the show's entertainment value while aligning with CNN's renewed emphasis on journalistic standards amid post-election scrutiny.[45] Cohen, known for his celebrity interviews and lighthearted style, debuted alongside Cooper in Times Square, incorporating elements like on-air toasts and guest appearances to sustain the program's festive atmosphere. The 2017 edition drew 1.7 million total viewers during prime time, with 705,000 in the adults 25-54 demographic, contributing to CNN's highest-rated New Year's Eve broadcast to date at that time. No major on-air disruptions occurred, though the duo's banter, including Cohen's occasional references to alcohol, foreshadowed their signature dynamic.[46] Post-broadcast analysis praised the pairing for revitalizing viewer engagement without the prior controversies, stabilizing the format ahead of subsequent years.

Cooper-Cohen duo stabilization (2018â€“2022)

The Cooper-Cohen duo hosted CNN's New Year's Eve Live annually from Times Square during this period, establishing a reliable format of live musical acts, celebrity interviews, and crowd engagement that contrasted Cooper's measured demeanor with Cohen's lively, entertainment-industry perspective. Hourly toasts with alcohol, often led by Cohen, emerged as a recurring tradition, enhancing the informal tone while Cooper occasionally participated reluctantly.[47] Viewership demonstrated stability relative to cable competitors, though influenced by external factors. The December 31, 2018, broadcast ranked number one in cable news across key demographics, building on the prior year's gains.[48] The December 31, 2019, edition averaged 1.727 million total viewers from 8:00 p.m. to 12:30 a.m., securing the top spot in cable news.[49] The December 31, 2020, special achieved CNN's highest ratings to date with 3.36 million total viewers and 1.21 million in the 25-54 demographic, boosted by pandemic-related stay-at-home viewing patterns.[50] In 2021, Cohen's on-air remarks criticizing non-vaccinated individuals and referencing "straight people" watching the ball drop drew scrutiny, prompting CNN to prohibit alcohol consumption during the subsequent 2022 broadcast, where hosts used mocktails instead.[5] The December 31, 2022, program still topped cable news with approximately 2.07 million viewers overall, reflecting sustained audience interest despite the adjustment.[51][52] This era marked the show's transition to a more predictable entertainment-news hybrid, with guest appearances from artists like Mariah Carey and Jon Batiste contributing to consistent primetime appeal without major format overhauls.[53]

Controversies

Kathy Griffin severed head photo incident

On May 30, 2017, comedian Kathy Griffin, who had co-hosted CNN's New Year's Eve Live broadcast with Anderson Cooper since 2007, posted an image on Twitter and Instagram depicting herself holding a prop resembling the bloodied, severed head of President Donald Trump, photographed by Tyler Shields as part of a provocative shoot intended as political satire.[65][66] Griffin quickly deleted the post and issued an apology on Instagram, stating she had "went too far" and begging forgiveness, though she later described the image as an attempt to "punch up" against the president.[65][67] The image drew immediate and widespread condemnation for appearing to glorify violence against the sitting U.S. president, prompting reactions including a tweet from Trump himself claiming his 11-year-old son Barron had seen it and been disturbed, as well as criticism from celebrities, politicians across parties, and media outlets labeling it "sick" and "disturbing."[68][69][70] Griffin reported receiving death threats and facing professional cancellations, while the U.S. Secret Service briefly investigated the matter but declined to pursue charges, citing no credible threat.[71][72] In direct response to the controversy, CNN terminated Griffin's agreement to co-host its New Year's Eve program on May 31, 2017, stating the image was "disgusting and completely inappropriate" and that she would not appear on the network's coverage moving forward.[73][9][74] This decision marked the abrupt end of her decade-long role on the broadcast, which had featured her comedic segments amid live Times Square coverage, and accelerated the network's shift away from her amid advertiser concerns and public backlash.[68][66] The incident highlighted tensions in political comedy boundaries during Trump's presidency, with Griffin later attributing her career fallout partly to disproportionate targeting by the administration, though empirical evidence of selective enforcement remains debated given the image's graphic nature and timing post-inauguration.[75][76]

On-air incidents and language controversies

On December 31, 2021, during CNN's New Year's Eve Live broadcast, Andy Cohen, after taking multiple tequila shots on air, delivered an unscripted rant criticizing rival ABC's New Year's coverage more sober competitors, and revealed that CNN president Jeff Zucker praised the show's energy despite the controversy.[80] CNN publicly supported Cohen but responded by banning alcohol for hosts during the December 31, 2022, broadcast as a precautionary measure, a policy reversed for the 2023 edition onward.[81] The incident highlighted ongoing debates over language and decorum, with critics arguing Cohen's alcohol-fueled candor undermined CNN's journalistic credibility, while supporters viewed it as authentic entertainment that boosted viewership through unpolished banter.[82] Similar moments of tipsy commentary have recurred in subsequent years, such as Cohen's 2024 call-out of co-host Anderson Cooper for an out-of-touch remark on wealth disparities during live coverage, though these drew more amusement than outrage.[83] No instances of overt profanity have been documented in the Andy Cohen era, but the format's embrace of intoxication has fueled perceptions of lax standards compared to traditional news programming.[3]

Replacement and hosting transition debates

In the wake of on-air incidents and amid CNN's 2023 leadership upheaval, media commentators and industry observers debated the long-term viability of Andy Cohen's role as co-host, questioning whether his Bravo entertainment persona and history of unscripted remarks aligned with the network's push toward journalistic seriousness. Cohen's 2022 New Year's Eve broadcast included a rant labeling then-former President Donald Trump supporters as "stupid" while admitting to being "stupid and drunk" himself, drawing criticism for breaching impartiality on a news network; outlets like BuzzFeed reported calls for accountability, though CNN affirmed his return, emphasizing the special's viewer draw.[84] Under outgoing CEO Chris Licht, the 2023 edition marked a temporary hosting transition by prohibiting alcohol consumptionâ€”a staple of the Cooper-Cohen dynamicâ€”aimed at curbing perceived frivolity and reinforcing CNN's straight-news ethos amid falling prime-time ratings. This sober format fueled speculation about replacing the duo's party-style hosting with traditional anchor-led coverage, as Licht sought to differentiate CNN from competitors like Fox News; the change coincided with broader cost-cutting and format reevaluations, but drew mixed viewer feedback for lacking the prior levity. Licht's dismissal in June 2023, following internal discord over his reforms, effectively ended the experiment, with alcohol reinstated for the 2024 broadcast as Cooper and Cohen "restarted the tradition."[85] With Mark Thompson's appointment as CEO in October 2023, further debates emerged over potential overhauls to specials like New Year's Eve Live, given his mandate to streamline operations and prioritize digital amid cord-cutting and ad revenue pressures; Hollywood Reporter noted the announcement of the duo's return for 2024 "despite network shake-up," implying executive deliberations on whether to retain the high-rated but format-divergent pairing. However, empirical metrics favored continuity, with the 2023 special averaging 2.7 million viewersâ€”outpacing rivalsâ€”and subsequent editions sustaining appeal; Thompson's strategy focused changes on daytime and morning slots rather than tentpole events.[86] By October 2024, Cohen publicly confirmed their eighth consecutive co-hosting for 2025, underscoring resolution against replacement amid stable performance.[87]

Reception and Metrics

Viewership ratings trends

CNN's New Year's Eve Live experienced significant growth in viewership during its formative years, culminating in record-setting audiences by the mid-2010s. The 2017 broadcast marked the program's highest-rated edition up to that point, with total viewers increasing 17% and adults 25-54 rising 26% compared to 2016 across the 8 p.m. to 1 a.m. ET window.[25] This upward trajectory continued into the early 2020s, as the December 31, 2021, edition delivered the largest audience in the show's then-14-year history for both total viewers and the key 25-54 demographic.[88] Recent linear television metrics, measured by Nielsen, indicate a stabilization followed by decline amid broader industry shifts toward streaming and cord-cutting. The broadcast on December 31, 2022 (ringing in 2023), averaged 2.1 million total viewers across its coverage.[52] This rose to 2.33 million viewers for the December 31, 2023, edition (ringing in 2024), a 12% year-over-year increase, with a peak of 3.414 million during the midnight quarter-hour (11 p.m.-12:30 a.m. ET).[51] [89] However, the December 31, 2024, broadcast (ringing in 2025) averaged just 1.88 million viewers from 8:30 p.m. to 12:30 a.m. ET, marking the lowest linear audience in five years and reflecting a roughly 20% drop from the prior year in line with CNN's overall network declines.[90] Midnight peaks provided some uplift, reaching 3.7 million viewers from 12-12:30 a.m. ET, though still down in the 25-54 demo by 19% year-over-year.[30]

Critical assessments

The hosting dynamic between Anderson Cooper and Andy Cohen has been praised for its authentic contrast, with Cooper's journalistic restraint juxtaposed against Cohen's uninhibited commentary, often leading to spontaneous and humorous moments that resonate with viewers seeking light-hearted escapism on New Year's Eve.[92] This interplay, including Cohen's candid asides and Cooper's gradual loosening up, has been highlighted as a strength in post-broadcast analyses, contributing to the show's appeal as unscripted entertainment rather than polished production.[19] Critics and observers have occasionally faulted the production for inadequate preparation, particularly in handling environmental challenges like rain during the 2024 broadcast, where hosts were left sharing a single umbrella without prior shelter arrangements despite weather forecasts.[93] Such lapses have drawn public commentary on social media questioning the event's logistical oversight, potentially undermining the professional presentation expected from a major network special.[93] Overall, assessments emphasize the show's success in prioritizing live unpredictability over scripted formality, though this approach risks perceptions of informality bordering on disarray, as evidenced by reliance on alcohol-fueled segments that enhance viewer engagement but invite scrutiny over journalistic standards.[92][19]
