Carcinoembryonic antigen

Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA), also known as CEACAM5, is a glycoprotein involved in cell adhesion and a member of the carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA)-related cell adhesion molecule (CEACAM) family. CEA is normally produced in gastrointestinal tissue during fetal development, but production ceases shortly after birth. Consequently, CEA is usually present at very low levels in the blood of healthy nonsmoking adults (less than 3 ng/mL).[1] However, serum levels are raised in some types of cancer, which means that it can be used as a tumor marker in clinical tests. Serum levels can also be elevated in heavy smokers (up to 5â€“10 ng/mL).[2][3] Specialized sialofucosylated glycoforms of CEA serve as functional ligands for L-selectin and E-selectin, which may be critical to the metastatic dissemination of colon carcinoma cells.[4][5][6] Immunologically, CEA is characterized as CD66e within the CD66 cluster of differentiation; the broader family includes CD66aâ€“f (covered in the Genetics section).

History

CEA was first identified in 1965 by Phil Gold, a Canadian physician, scientist and professor and Samuel O. Freedman who is also a Canadian professor of immunology in human colon cancer tissue extracts.[7]

Diagnostic significance

The CEA blood test is not reliable for diagnosing cancer or as a screening test for early detection of cancer.[8] Most types of cancer do not result in a high CEA level.[9] Serum from individuals with colorectal carcinoma often has higher levels of CEA than healthy individuals (above 5 ng/mL in non-smokers, or higher in smokers). CEA measurement is mainly used as a tumor marker to monitor colorectal carcinoma treatment, to identify recurrences after surgical resection, for staging or to localize cancer spread through measurement of biological fluids.[10] CEA levels may also be raised in gastric carcinoma, pancreatic carcinoma, lung carcinoma, breast carcinoma, and medullary thyroid carcinoma, as well as some non-neoplastic conditions like ulcerative colitis, pancreatitis, cirrhosis, [11] COPD, Crohn's disease, hypothyroidism[12] as well as in smokers.[3] Elevated CEA levels should return to normal after successful surgical removal of the tumor and can be used in follow up, especially of colorectal cancers.[13] CEA elevation is known to be affected by multiple factors. It varies inversely with tumor grade; well-differentiated tumors secrete more CEA. CEA is elevated more in tumors with lymph node and distant metastasis than in organ-confined tumors and, thus, varies directly with tumor stage. Left-sided tumors generally tend to have higher CEA levels than right-sided tumors.[14] Tumors causing bowel obstruction produce higher CEA levels.[14] Aneuploid tumors produce more CEA than diploid tumors.[15] Liver dysfunction increases CEA levels as the liver is the primary site of CEA metabolism.[2]

Antibodies

An anti-CEA antibody is an antibody against CEA. Such antibodies to CEA are commonly used in immunohistochemistry to identify cells expressing the glycoprotein in tissue samples. In adults, CEA is primarily expressed in cells of tumors (some malignant, some benign) [1] but they are particularly associated with the adenocarcinomas, such as those arising in the colon, lung, breast, stomach, or pancreas. It can therefore be used to distinguish between these and other similar cancers. For example, it can help to distinguish between adenocarcinoma of the lung and mesothelioma, a different type of lung cancer which is not normally CEA positive. Because even monoclonal antibodies to CEA tend to have some degree of cross-reactivity, occasionally giving false positive results, it is commonly employed in combination with other immunohistochemistry tests, such as those for BerEp4, WT1, and calretinin.[16] For cancers that highly express CEA, targeting CEA through radioimmunotherapy is one of the therapy approaches.[17] Engineered antibodies such as single-chain Fv antibodies (sFvs) or bispecific antibodies have been used for targeting and therapy of CEA expressing tumors both in vitro and in vivo with promising results [18][19] Regions of high CEA levels in the body were previously detected using the monoclonal antibody arcitumomab, though it was withdrawn from the market in 2005.[]

Carcinoembryonic Antigen (CEA) Genetics

Genetics

CEA and related genes make up the CEA family belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily. In humans, the carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) family consists of 35 genes, approximately 23 of which are expressed, divided into two main subgroups: the CEA cell adhesion molecule (CEACAM) subgroup involved in cell adhesion and the pregnancy-specific glycoprotein (PSG) subgroup expressed primarily during pregnancy.[20][21] The following is a list of human genes in the CEACAM subgroup which encode carcinoembryonic antigen-related cell adhesion proteins:
  • CEACAM1
  • CEACAM3
  • CEACAM4
  • CEACAM5
  • CEACAM6
  • CEACAM7
  • CEACAM8
  • CEACAM16
  • CEACAM18
  • CEACAM19
  • CEACAM20
  • CEACAM21

Clinical trials

CEA is expressed in many types of cancer, including lung, gastric, pancreatic, and colorectal cancer. Numerous clinical trials targeting CEA have been conducted, particularly in radionuclide therapy, CAR-T cell therapy, and antibody-drug conjugates. CEA serves as a target for [radionuclide therapy](Targeted Radionuclide Therapy "Targeted Radionuclide Therapy"). The cT84.66, a chimeric antibody of murine origin, was tested in phase I clinical trials using indium-111 (111In) for imaging and yttrium-90 (90Y) for therapy.[22][23] 111In is a gamma-emitting radionuclide used for imaging, while 90Y is a Î²-emitter used for therapy. The results were promising, but some patients developed immune responses, leading to withdrawal from the trials.[24] The cT84.66 antibody was subsequently humanized to M5A. In a 2020 phase I clinical trial, 18 patients with advanced CEA-producing cancers received 90Y-DOTA-M5A. The trial showed stable disease in 10 of 18 patients (56%) at 3 months and no immunogenic responses.[25] M5A-DOTA was also coupled with actinium-225 (225Ac), an alpha emitter, and evaluated in an in vivo mouse study combining alpha therapy with cytokine therapy (interleukin-2 immunocytokine). The study demonstrated benefits of this combination for treating CEA-positive tumors.[26] Based on these results, a phase I clinical trial (NCT05204147) was initiated in June 2022 to evaluate the safety and potential benefits of 225Ac-DOTA-M5A in patients with advanced or metastatic CEA-producing cancers, with an estimated completion date of August 2025.[27] Recent trials include CAR-T cell therapies targeting CEA, such as the phase 1/2 EVEREST-1 study (NCT05736731) evaluating A2B530 in CEA-expressing solid tumors, and an antibody-drug conjugate trial (NCT05464030) assessing precemtabart tocentecan in colorectal cancer, both ongoing as of 2025.[28][29]
