Italian aircraft carrier Aquila

The Italian aircraft carrier Aquila was a conversion project undertaken by the Regia Marina during World War II to create Italy's first fleet carrier from the incomplete passenger liner Roma.[1] Construction began in 1941 at the Ansaldo shipyard in Genoa, incorporating design elements influenced by both Italian ingenuity and German engineering consultations, but the vessel remained unfinished at approximately 85% completion by the time of Italy's armistice with the Allies in September 1943.[2] Intended to displace around 23,350 tons fully loaded, with dimensions of approximately 680 feet in length and capacity for up to 51 aircraft including fighters, torpedo bombers, and reconnaissance planes, Aquila featured innovations such as an armored flight deck, hydraulic catapults, and arrestor wires, though these were never fully tested in service.[1][3] Following the armistice, Aquila was seized by German forces at La Spezia, where Italian saboteurs damaged her machinery to prevent operational use by the occupiers.[3] Subsequent Allied air raids further compromised the hull, leading to her scuttling in shallow waters in 1945; she was later salvaged and scrapped between 1946 and 1952.[2] Despite never entering combat or conducting flight operations, Aquila represented a significant, albeit unrealized, effort by Italy to project naval air power in the Mediterranean, highlighting resource constraints, strategic misprioritization, and the challenges of wartime industrial conversion under fascist regime priorities.[1] The project's failure underscored the Regia Marina's doctrinal emphasis on surface fleets over carrier aviation, contrasting with Allied successes in carrier warfare.[3]

Historical Context

Italian Naval Doctrine and Aviation Prior to World War II

Following World War I, the Regia Marina adhered to a battleship-centric doctrine influenced by Alfred Thayer Mahan's emphasis on decisive fleet engagements, adapted to the Mediterranean's confined geography, which favored short-range operations to secure central sea lanes against potential French rivalry.[4] This approach prioritized maintaining a "fleet in being" with modernized capital ships like the Conte di Cavour and Giulio Cesare classes, alongside heavy cruisers for scouting and raiding, to contest dominance in the narrow waters from Sicily to North Africa rather than pursuing blue-water expansion.[4] Italian planners viewed the Mediterranean's land-proximate chokepointsâ€”such as the Strait of Messina and Sicily Channelâ€”as enabling rapid concentration of surface forces for battle, diminishing the perceived need for long-range projection capabilities.[5] Naval aviation development remained limited to reconnaissance-oriented seaplanes and floatplanes, with early experiments dating to 1914 including shipboard launches from cruisers and the construction of auxiliary vessels like the seaplane tender RM Europa in 1920.[6] The 1923 establishment of the independent Regia Aeronautica transferred all military aircraft control from the navy, subordinating maritime air assets to land-based operations and hindering integrated carrier doctrine formation.[6] While seaplane carriers such as RM Miraglia entered service in 1927, supporting up to 24 aircraft for spotting duties, the Regia Marina neglected flush-deck designs, reasoning that the theater's proximity to Italian airfields obviated dedicated fleet carriers amid overconfidence in surface gunnery superiority.[7] Under Benito Mussolini's regime, naval expansion via laws in 1925, 1929, and 1933â€“1934 emphasized capital ship rebuildingâ€”authorizing the four Littorio-class fast battleships in 1934 for speeds exceeding 30 knotsâ€”and cruiser/destroyer construction to achieve parity with France under the Washington (1922) and London (1930) treaties, which allocated equivalent tonnage for battleships and carriers but saw Italy allocate minimally to aviation.[7] Carrier advocacy within the navy was suppressed by 1936, reflecting prioritization of battleship-led "decisive battle" tactics over air-integrated strategies, as leaders dismissed foreign carrier successes like HMS Ark Royal's due to perceived applicability only to oceanic theaters.[8] This subordination persisted, with naval aviators relying on underpowered Regia Aeronautica floatplanes like the Fiat RS.14 for reconnaissance, limiting tactical evolution before 1940.[7]

Strategic Awakening and Conversion Decision (1940â€“1941)

The British carrier-based air raid on Taranto on 11 November 1940, launched from HMS Illustrious, inflicted significant damage on the Italian battle fleet anchored there, torpedoing and disabling three battleships (Littorio, Vittorio Veneto, and Cavour) while sinking none but rendering half of Italy's capital ships inoperable for months.[9][10] This empirical demonstration of carrier aviation's ability to neutralize superior surface forces at anchor exposed fundamental flaws in Regia Marina doctrine, which had prioritized a battleship-heavy fleet reliant on land-based Regia Aeronautica support rather than integral naval air power, leaving fleets vulnerable to preemptive strikes without adequate defensive aviation.[11] The raid shifted the Mediterranean balance temporarily toward Britain, compelling Italian naval leaders to reassess the primacy of big-gun engagements over air-centric operations.[10] Less than two months later, on 7 January 1941, Benito Mussolini authorized the conversion of the laid-up transatlantic liner Romaâ€”selected for its substantial size (gross tonnage of 32,000, length of 240 meters, and beam of 28 meters, allowing adaptation to carry up to 50-60 aircraft)â€”into a full-fledged fleet aircraft carrier, renamed Aquila.[1] This marked an escalation from preliminary 1940 studies for an auxiliary carrier, driven by the Taranto revelations and the Regia Marina's recognition of the need for mobile air cover to contest British carrier dominance in the Mediterranean.[1] Supermarina, the Italian naval high command, issued orders to proceed despite the absence of indigenous carrier blueprints or experience, opting for rapid improvisation on the Genoa shipyards of Ansaldo-Cantiere Navale to address wartime immediacy over deliberate design.[1] The Roma, idle since 1939 due to curtailed civilian shipping, was deemed the most available large hull, bypassing smaller merchant conversions previously considered. Pragmatic adaptation via the Axis alliance incorporated German technical input from the stalled Graf Zeppelin project, including shared specifications for hydraulic catapults and arrester wires, to accelerate development without ideological aversion to foreign expertise.[1][12] However, resource allocation debates ensued, as diverting steel, labor, and engineering from urgent needs like submarine production or convoy escorts strained Italy's limited industrial capacity amid Allied blockades and bombing campaigns.[7] These priorities reflected fascist regime inefficiencies, including bureaucratic delays and failure to mobilize resources effectively for scaled wartime production, ultimately hindering timely completion despite the project's strategic urgency.[7]

Design and Construction

Base Vessel: The Liner Roma and Initial Conversion

The SS Roma was constructed by the Ansaldo shipyard in Sestri Ponente and delivered in 1926 as a transatlantic ocean liner to Navigazione Generale Italiana, serving primarily on routes between Italy and South America.[13] With a gross register tonnage of 32,583, an overall length of approximately 215 meters, a beam of 25 meters, and quadruple-screw steam turbine propulsion enabling speeds up to 21 knots, the vessel featured a steel hull designed for high-speed civilian passenger service, which offered inherent stability and volume suitable for partial adaptation into a warship despite lacking military-grade armor or compartmentalization from inception.[14][1] In November 1941, following Italy's entry into World War II and recognition of naval aviation's strategic value, conversion efforts began at the Cantieri Ansaldo yard in Genoa, where Romaâ€”still bearing its original name until February 1942, when it was redesignated Aquila to avoid conflict with a new battleshipâ€”underwent initial refitting.[1] Workers systematically removed the liner's superstructure, including bridges, funnels, and passenger accommodations, along with internal luxury fittings such as cabins and salons, to expose the main deck and create void space for subsequent military alterations like hangar integration. These early stages faced immediate obstacles from Italy's wartime resource constraints, including acute shortages of steel plating essential for structural reinforcements and delays in procuring welding equipment amid Allied bombing campaigns and disrupted supply lines, which extended the projected timeline beyond the mid-1942 operational target and limited progress to basic demolition by late 1942.[15] Despite these hurdles, the liner's pre-existing machinery and hull integrity allowed for retention of core propulsion elements, minimizing initial overhauls.[3]

Hull and Structural Modifications

The conversion of the transatlantic liner Roma into the aircraft carrier Aquila necessitated extensive hull modifications to enhance structural integrity, stability, and resistance to combat damage, transforming a civilian vessel optimized for passenger service into a warship capable of enduring naval stresses. Work commenced in November 1941 at the Ansaldo shipyard in Genoa, where the hull was lengthened amidships by approximately 15 meters to refine entry lines and accommodate increased machinery power, while deep armored bulges were added along both sides. These bulges, filled with seawater compartments and reinforced with 60-80 mm layers of concrete for splinter protection, served dual purposes: improving transverse stability essential for carrier operations and providing rudimentary torpedo defense by absorbing underwater explosions.[1][1] Internal structural reconfiguration included thorough replanning of the hull's compartments, with the addition of multiple longitudinal bulkheads to bolster rigidity and limit flooding propagation, drawing inspiration from British carrier designs that emphasized subdivided, liquid-filled voids for damage control. The original superstructure was dismantled down to the main deck, gutting passenger accommodations to create two hangar levels below it, separated by a fireproof bulkhead to mitigate fire risks from aviation fuel and ordnance storage. This integration incorporated two octagonal elevatorsâ€”measuring 15 meters across with a 4.5-ton capacityâ€”positioned forward and amidships to facilitate aircraft movement while maintaining structural compartmentalization. These changes elevated the standard displacement to 23,130 tons and full load to 28,350 tons, reflecting the added mass from bulges, armor plating (30-80 mm over fuel and magazine areas), and reinforced framing.[1][1][16] Hull integrity was empirically assessed through a static load test conducted in September 1943, which broadly validated seaworthiness under design stresses but highlighted persistent challenges in vibration damping, particularly linked to the angled flight deck's influence on hull resonance during simulated operations. Enhanced watertight subdivision, comprising numerous bulkheads, further supported underwater damage resistance, though the conversion's wartime constraints limited full-scale dynamic trials.[1][1]

Propulsion and Machinery Systems

The propulsion system of Aquila was upgraded from the original passenger liner Roma's merchant-grade setup to incorporate military-specification components, enabling sustained high-speed operations suitable for carrier duties. It featured four sets of Belluzzo geared steam turbines driving four propeller shafts, with a total output of 151,000 shaft horsepower (shp).[1][17] These turbines were sourced from the incomplete Capitani Romani-class light cruisers Cornelio Silla and Paolo Emilio, providing enhanced reliability over civilian designs.[18] Eight Yarrow-type or Thornycroft boilers supplied steam to the turbines, allowing a maximum speed of 30 knots (56 km/h), critical for keeping pace with Italian battle fleets in the Mediterranean.[1][2] Fuel storage was modified to support both heavy fuel oil for propulsion and aviation gasoline for aircraft, with a total oil capacity of approximately 2,800 tons enabling an operational range of 5,500 nautical miles (nmi) at 18 knots or shorter high-speed dashes up to 1,210 nmi at 29 knots.[1][19] Aviation fuel tanks held around 270,000 liters (71,000 US gallons) of gasoline, stored in cylindrical tanks or cofferdams isolated from the hull by water-filled voids to mitigate explosion risks, emulating British safety practices adapted for Italian engineering constraints.[17][20] Auxiliary systems included enhanced electrical generation and compressed air infrastructure to power catapults and emerging radar installations, with German-supplied Demag components integrated for improved operational reliability under wartime shortages.[17][3] The two parallel Demag catapults, each launching one aircraft every 30 seconds via compressed air, relied on electrically driven compressors tied into the ship's expanded dynamo network, addressing the power demands of carrier-specific equipment without compromising core propulsion.[17][20]

Hangar and Internal Aircraft Facilities

The hangar of Aquila consisted of a single deck measuring 160 meters in length by 18 meters in width, providing approximately 2,880 square meters of space dedicated to aircraft storage, maintenance, and related operations.[1] This area was created by gutting the internal structure of the former passenger liner Roma, reallocating former passenger decks to accommodate aviation needs while integrating workshops and stores for aircraft servicing. Initial configurations allowed for 26 aircraft without folding wings, but plans anticipated expansion to 50-66 units through the use of folding-wing variants like the Reggiane Re.2001, maximizing density despite the vessel's retrofit constraints.[1] [2] Internal facilities included air circulation vents to manage hangar floor heating and prevent overheating during high-speed operations, a precaution derived from the liner's original propulsion layout.[1] Fire safety measures featured seawater-filled compartments isolating aviation fuel tanks from the hangar, reducing explosion risks in the event of damage, though comprehensive suppression systems were limited by wartime material shortages and the conversion's improvisational nature.[1] Workshop setups supported basic repairs and armament loading, but access routes were criticized as cramped and inefficient compared to purpose-built carriers, reflecting doctrinal emphasis on fleet defense over expansive aviation infrastructure.[1] Crew accommodations totaled around 1,200-1,400 personnel, including 1,165 naval crew and 200-243 air group members, with berthing prioritized for operational efficiency rather than pre-war liner comforts, featuring functional multi-bunk arrangements in repurposed spaces adjacent to the hangar.[1] [2] This setup underscored the conversion's compromises, where internal volume was traded for structural reinforcements and machinery rerouting, limiting luxury but aligning with wartime naval pragmatism.[2]

Flight Deck and Launch/Recovery Mechanisms

The flight deck of the Aquila was designed as a single continuous surface measuring 211.6 meters in length and 25.2 meters in width, extending over the converted liner's hull to facilitate fixed-wing aircraft operations. Unlike many contemporary carriers with fully wooden decks for damage absorption and repair ease, Aquila's deck incorporated partial armor plating of 76 mm thickness strategically placed over aviation fuel bunkers and ammunition magazines to enhance protection against bomb or shell impacts. This armored approach reflected Italian engineering priorities for survivability in contested Mediterranean waters, though the overall deck structure retained steel construction with potential wooden planking for traction, consistent with conversion constraints.[1] Launch capabilities centered on two compressed-air-driven catapults positioned side-by-side at the forward end of the flight deck, repurposed from German designs originally intended for the unfinished carrier Graf Zeppelin. Each catapult was engineered to hurl aircraft weighing up to approximately 5 tons at speeds sufficient for takeoff, with a projected cycle time of 30 seconds per launch under ideal conditions, enabling rapid sortie generation for heavier fighters like the Re.2001. These mechanisms represented an adaptation of Axis technical exchanges, prioritizing reliability over steam-powered alternatives due to the vessel's retrofit limitations and available technology.[1] Recovery systems included five sets of arrester gear with four transverse cables stretched across the deck to decelerate landing aircraft via hydraulic or mechanical absorption, drawing partial influence from British hydraulic wire practices observed in pre-war studies. Installation occurred by August 1943, but early trials exposed synchronization and tension issues, rendering the setup unreliable and prompting ongoing modifications that were never fully resolved before operational testing. Postwar U.S. Navy evaluations criticized the German-sourced components as hazardous, highlighting risks of wire snaps or insufficient braking for operational weights, underscoring the unproven nature of Italy's carrier aviation infrastructure amid wartime haste. No dedicated crash barriers were incorporated, relying instead on the arrester wires and pilot skill for containment.[1] The island superstructure was sited amidships on the starboard side to optimize flight deck space while minimizing aerodynamic interference from exhaust and structural wakes. This placement incorporated a prominent vertical funnel trunking boiler uptakes and a multi-level command tower housing radar antennas, optical directors, and conning facilities, balancing visibility for deck operations against the need for streamlined airflow over the straight flight deck. Debates during design favored starboard over port to align with prevailing winds in the Mediterranean and emulate proven Allied configurations, though the overall layout remained experimental without sea trials to validate simultaneous launch and recovery flows.[1]

Armament, Defenses, and Electronics

The Aquila's primary armament consisted of eight 135 mm/45 dual-purpose guns arranged in four twin turrets, repurposed from the incomplete Capitani Romani-class cruisers, providing capability against surface targets and aircraft in a fleet screening role.[1][2] These were supplemented by twelve 65 mm/64 anti-aircraft guns in single mounts for medium-range defense, reflecting Italian emphasis on layered firepower amid limited aircraft carrier doctrine.[1][2] Close-in protection relied on an extensive battery of 132 Breda 20 mm/65 automatic cannons, organized in 22 sextuple mounts plus additional singles, prioritizing volume of fire against low-flying attackers despite the era's technological constraints on fire control.[1][2] Defensive armor emphasized protection for critical internals over comprehensive belt schemes, with 30â€“80 mm steel plates applied over magazines and aviation fuel storage to guard against splinter and bomb damage.[1] Deck armor varied between 40 mm and up to 80 mm in localized areas, sloping downward from the centerline for enhanced resistance to plunging fire while maintaining weight limits from the liner conversion.[1][2] Underwater protection featured deep bulges along the hull sides, incorporating 60â€“80 mm reinforced concrete layers inboard for buoyancy and modest torpedo absorption, augmented by tight internal compartmentation rather than the Regia Marina's standard Pugliese toroidal system, to counter flooding in Mediterranean operations.[1] Electronics were rudimentary, with no radar systems provisioned despite Axis collaboration; German FuMO designs from projects like Graf Zeppelin were considered but not integrated, leaving the ship reliant on optical rangefinders, direction-finding gear, and manual spotting for air and surface detection.[1] This reflected broader Regia Marina shortcomings in adopting electronic warfare technologies by 1943, sufficient for basic coordination in visual-range fleet actions but vulnerable to beyond-line-of-sight threats.[1]

Planned Capabilities and Air Group

Intended Aircraft Complement and Operations

The intended aircraft complement for Aquila comprised 51 Reggiane Re.2001 OR fighter-bombers, selected for their versatility in air-to-air combat, bombing, and reconnaissance roles within the constraints of Italian aviation production.[1] These aircraft featured non-folding wings in the baseline configuration, with 41 stored in the hangar deckâ€”including up to 15 suspended from the overhead structure for space efficiencyâ€”and the remainder positioned on the flight deck for rapid deployment.[1] A folding-wing variant was under development to potentially expand the air group to 66 aircraft, though only prototypes reached testing before work ceased.[3] Modifications for carrier operations included arrestor hooks for deck recovery, naval radios, and reinforced undercarriages; certain Re.2001 G sub-variants were adapted for torpedo delivery, with extended tailwheel struts to accommodate slung ordnance loads up to 650 kg.[3] [1] Sortie generation was projected to emphasize support for the battle fleet through reconnaissance patrols, precision strikes against enemy shipping, and defensive fighter cover, leveraging the carrier's dual catapults for launches at intervals of approximately 30 seconds.[1] This setup prioritized quick-cycle operations over sustained high-volume output, given the Mediterranean theater's demands for rapid response to British naval threats.[1] However, operational effectiveness hinged on addressing Regia Aeronautica pilot limitations, as Italy lacked prior carrier experience; training for the assigned 160Â° Gruppo C.T. involved German advisors from March 1943, using simulated deck practice and familiarization with Arado Ar 96B trainers and Junkers Ju 87C dive bombers adapted for naval use.[1] These efforts mitigated but did not fully resolve gaps in arrested landings and catapult-assisted takeoffs, with arresting gear tests revealing persistent hazards that rendered recoveries "extremely hazardous" rather than routine.[3]

Engineering Achievements and Criticisms

Innovations in Carrier Conversion Under Constraints

The conversion of the passenger liner Roma into Aquila exemplified resourceful engineering amid Axis resource shortages and Allied bombing threats, leveraging the existing hull to expedite carrier development over new construction. Italian designers at Cantieri Ansaldo in Genoa gutted the interior, added sponsons for stability and splinter protection, and extended the hull length by approximately 10 meters, achieving these modifications progressively from November 1941 onward despite steel rationing and supply disruptions. This adaptive approach preserved the liner's structural integrity while incorporating naval-specific reinforcements, such as 60-80 mm concrete-filled bulges along the waterline, demonstrating pragmatic reuse of civilian infrastructure under wartime imperatives.[1] A key innovation was the seamless integration of two German-supplied Demag compressed-air catapults into the forward flight deck, positioned side-by-side for parallel launches capable of deploying one aircraft every 30 seconds. These units, drawn from proven designs and approved by German naval authorities, were adapted to the Italian hull without major redesigns, with installation commencing in late 1941 and functionality validated through static and dynamic tests by August 1943. Complementing this, engineers devised a dedicated rail system to transport aircraft efficiently from hangars to catapults, optimizing internal flow in the constrained space of a converted liner and marking an early modular handling solution tailored to limited deck area.[1] These efforts underscored cost-effective pragmatism, as repurposing Romaâ€”launched in 1926â€”circumvented the protracted timelines and material demands of keel-up builds, projecting operational readiness by November 1943 at a fraction of equivalent fresh-construction expenses. Such successes highlighted Italian technical proficiency in fusing domestic fabrication with Allied-sourced components, yielding a prototype carrier hull tested to sustain 30-knot speeds via upgraded Belluzzo turbines and Thornycroft boilers.[1]

The conversion of the passenger liner Roma into Aquila exacerbated inherent stability issues through the addition of a heavily armored flight deck and superstructure, which elevated the ship's center of gravity; deep bulges were added along the hull to improve metacentric height and provide limited torpedo protection, but wartime haste precluded comprehensive seaworthiness trials, leaving potential high-speed handling deficiencies unaddressed.[1] Vibration problems, stemming from the original liner's propeller shaft design and intensified by structural modifications for carrier use, risked aviation fuel tank ruptures under combat shock, with mitigation limited to seawater ballast in adjacent compartments rather than root-cause engineering fixes.[1] Arresting gear trials in August 1943 revealed critical flaws, including failed stops and the absence of safety barriers, rendering landings hazardous as noted in a postwar U.S. Navy assessment; Fate During and After World War II

Incomplete Status and Axis Seizure (1943)

By mid-1943, Aquila had advanced to roughly 85-90% completion, with her hull fully formed, flight deck installed, arresting gear fitted, and propulsion machineryâ€”including four Belluzzo geared steam turbines and eight Yarrow-type boilersâ€”successfully subjected to static trials in August.[1] [23] However, critical elements remained absent, such as operational aircraft for her planned air group, final armament integration, and comprehensive testing beyond static phases, rendering her non-commissionable amid resource shortages and Allied bombing disruptions.[1] The Italian armistice with the Allies, announced publicly on 8 September 1943, immediately ceased all construction at the Ansaldo Genoa shipyard, where Aquila lay docked, exacerbating chaos within the Regia Marina as German forces launched Operation Achse to neutralize Italian military holdings north of the armistice line.[24] [1] In the ensuing occupation of northern Italy, Wehrmacht units swiftly secured the carrier, transferring nominal custody to the collaborating National Republican Navy of the Italian Social Republic while maintaining direct German oversight.[1] [3] German command anticipated refitting and commissioning Aquila into Kriegsmarine service, drawing potentially on expertise from their stalled Graf Zeppelin project, but efforts faltered due to persistent threats of Italian sabotage, limited engineering resources diverted to defensive operations, and the vessel's vulnerability in an exposed harbor.[1] Loyalist elements of the Royal Italian Navy, operating from southern bases as co-belligerents with the Allies, adopted a strategy of vigilant denial, forgoing risky reclamation attempts in favor of ensuring the carrier could not bolster Axis naval capabilities.[3] This stance reflected broader Regia Marina priorities amid the fractured Italian war effort, where preserving strategic assets for potential postwar recovery outweighed immediate recapture amid German dominance in the region.[1]

Sabotage, Scuttling, and Final Destruction

Following the Italian armistice with the Allies on 8 September 1943, personnel loyal to the Kingdom of Italy sabotaged Aquila at its Genoa berth to deny the vessel to advancing German forces, who had seized control of northern Italy. Crew members opened the ship's sea valves, causing partial flooding and a list that rendered it inoperable and grounded in shallow water, preventing immediate towing or repair by the occupiers.[22][1] This initial act of scuttling confirmed the hull's vulnerability to deliberate internal flooding, as the incomplete structure lacked full compartmentalization. German authorities maintained possession of the partially submerged hulk through 1944, attempting limited salvage efforts amid Allied bombing raids, but the sabotage damage persisted, leaving Aquila unusable for combat or conversion.[1] By early 1945, as Allied advances threatened Genoa, German forces planned to scuttle the carrier as a blockship to obstruct the harbor entrance against invading troops. To counter this, Italian co-belligerent naval commandosâ€”divers formerly of the Decima Flottiglia MAS, now aligned with the Alliesâ€”launched Operation Toast on the night of 19â€“20 April 1945.[2][25] The frogmen approached via manned torpedoes (siluri a lenta corsa), affixing limpet mines and breaching the hull at multiple points below the waterline, exacerbating prior damage and causing further flooding that settled Aquila firmly on the harbor bottom in a non-navigable position.[1][25] Empirical assessments post-attack revealed extensive hull perforations, with breaches measuring several meters in diameter, underscoring the carrier's structural fragility due to unfinished armor and welding inconsistencies from wartime haste. This second scuttling definitively thwarted German blockship intentions, as the wreck could not be repositioned in time. After the war's end in May 1945, Aquila remained submerged until salvage operations raised the hull in 1946 for temporary assessment.[2] Towed to La Spezia in 1949, the irreparably damaged and incomplete vessel was deemed uneconomical to refit amid Italy's post-war naval constraints under the peace treaty. Final destruction followed through systematic scrapping, dismantling the structure for scrap metal by the early 1950s, with no significant components preserved.[1]

Post-War Recovery Efforts and Archaeological Status

Following the end of World War II, the incomplete hull of Aquila, which had been scuttled in La Spezia harbor in September 1943 to prevent German capture, was refloated in 1946 amid post-war salvage operations. The vessel, heavily damaged and rusting, was towed from Genoa to La Spezia in 1949 for further assessment and disposal.[3] In the early 1950s, Aquila underwent partial dismantling, with its steel components recovered to meet Italy's urgent post-war industrial needs for raw materials. By 1951, the hulk was anchored in La Spezia's gulf, documented in photographs showing its deteriorated state before systematic scrapping commenced.[26] The process continued through 1952, resulting in the complete breakup of the ship by 1953, leaving no significant remnants or wreck in the shallows. As of 2025, no archaeological wreck of Aquila exists, with naval archives confirming full scrapping without preservation as a historical artifact. Historical documentation relies on period photos and records rather than recent dives, as the site's industrial salvage precluded ongoing underwater exploration. Debates on retaining portions for their representation of Axis naval engineering ambitions were overshadowed by practical imperatives for steel recovery in reconstruction-era Italy.[3]

Potential Impact on Regia Marina Operations

The conversion of the liner Roma into Aquila was projected by Regia Marina planners to introduce carrier-based air cover capable of defending supply convoys to North Africa against strikes from Malta, where British forces sank over 200 Axis merchant ships between 1941 and 1942, severely straining Italian logistics.[1] Equipped with up to 51 Reggiane Re.2001 fighter-bombers, Aquila was intended to deploy interceptors for close-range protection, addressing the Regia Marina's historical reliance on land-based Regia Aeronautica squadrons that often proved uncoordinated and range-limited.[1] [22] In fleet operations, Aquila's reconnaissance aircraft, such as the SAIMAN 200, were planned to extend scouting horizons beyond Sicily's shore-based limits, enabling earlier detection of Royal Navy movements and challenging British dominance in central Mediterranean waters.[1] This capability aligned with post-1940 doctrinal shifts following vulnerabilities exposed at Taranto, where the carrier's heavy anti-aircraft suiteâ€” including 132 x 20 mm gunsâ€”would support surface fleets against aerial threats.[1] However, these projections were constrained by the Regia Marina's absence of a dedicated fleet air arm and insufficient pilot training for carrier operations, as evidenced by hazardous deck-landing trials with prototype aircraft.[1] A single vessel like Aquila, slated for operational status by November 1943, could not independently overcome these gaps without integrated doctrinal reforms, limiting its projected efficacy to supplementary rather than transformative roles in Axis logistics.[1] [22]
