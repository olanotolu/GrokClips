Hrishikesh Roy

Justice Hrishikesh Roy (born 1 February 1960) is an Indian jurist who served as a judge of the Supreme Court of India from November 2019 until his retirement on 1 February 2025.[1][2] Educated at Delhi Public School, Mathura Road, and obtaining his LL.B. from the University of Delhi's Campus Law Centre in 1982, Roy began his legal career practicing in the Supreme Court and Gauhati High Court.[3][1] He was elevated to the bench of the Gauhati High Court in 2009, serving as its Chief Justice from May to November 2018 before transferring as Chief Justice of the Kerala High Court until his Supreme Court appointment.[3][4] During his tenure on the Supreme Court, Roy authored 46 judgments and participated in 324 benches, contributing significantly to areas such as alternative dispute resolution, legal aid, and environmental law, including upholding the suo motu powers of the National Green Tribunal and emphasizing party autonomy in arbitration.[5][6] He was known for promoting mediation and legal assistance initiatives, reflecting a commitment to efficient justice delivery.[7] In post-retirement interviews, Roy candidly addressed judicial challenges, including the misuse of legal machinery by governments against political opponents and the limitations in handling judicial misbehavior through soft institutional options like transfers or in-house procedures.[8][9][10]

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Hrishikesh Roy was born on 1 February 1960 in Assam, India.[3] His family subsequently relocated to Delhi, where Roy pursued his early education at Delhi Public School, Mathura Road.[3] During his school years, he demonstrated athletic prowess, captaining teams in hockey and football and contributing to his school's victory over Modern School in a notable match.[11] Public records provide scant details on his parental background or specific familial influences, reflecting the typically reserved personal profiles of Indian judicial figures.[3]

Academic qualifications

Hrishikesh Roy earned his Bachelor of Laws (LL.B.) degree from the Campus Law Centre, Faculty of Law, University of Delhi, in 1982.[12][3] This qualification formed the foundational academic credential for his subsequent enrollment as an advocate.[4] No additional postgraduate legal studies or specialized certifications are recorded in official judicial biographies.[13]

Pre-judicial legal career

Enrollment and initial practice

Following his LL.B. graduation from the University of Delhi's Campus Law Centre in 1982, Hrishikesh Roy enrolled as an advocate with the Bar Council of Delhi that same year.[14][15] He began his professional practice in Delhi, handling routine legal matters in the initial phase.[3] Roy soon relocated his practice to Guwahati, where he continued appearing in courts, primarily before the Gauhati High Court, over the ensuing years.[3][16] This transition marked the foundational period of his bar experience, spanning from enrollment through early professional engagements prior to advanced designations.[14]

Areas of specialization and notable cases

Hrishikesh Roy began his legal practice in Delhi after enrolling at the bar upon obtaining his LL.B. degree from the University of Delhi's Campus Law Centre in 1982. He soon relocated his practice to the Gauhati High Court, where he handled cases within its jurisdiction covering Assam and several Northeast Indian states.[3][17] Roy specialized in representing government and public sector entities, serving as Standing Counsel for the Assam State Electricity Board and the Karbi Anglong Autonomous Council, as well as Senior Government Advocate for Arunachal Pradesh. These roles encompassed civil litigation and constitutional matters pertinent to state administration, public utilities, and autonomous regional governance in Northeast India. Designated as a Senior Advocate by the Gauhati High Court on December 21, 2004, his pre-judicial career highlighted expertise in advocacy for public institutions, though specific landmark arguments or case outcomes from this period remain sparsely documented in public records.[17][3]

Judicial career

Tenure at Gauhati High Court

Hrishikesh Roy was sworn in as an Additional Judge of the Gauhati High Court on 12 October 2006.[1] He was confirmed as a Permanent Judge with effect from 15 July 2008.[1] During his tenure, which spanned over a decade until his elevation as Chief Justice of the Kerala High Court in 2018, Roy handled a substantial caseload encompassing civil, criminal, and constitutional matters arising from Assam and the broader Northeast region.[3] As the senior-most puisne judge, Roy assumed key administrative responsibilities, including serving as Judge-in-Charge of the Administrative Department (JAD), where he advised the Chief Justice on court operations and institutional matters.[17] He was also appointed Executive Head of the Assam State Legal Services Authority, overseeing initiatives for legal aid access in the state.[1] Additionally, Roy chaired the Mediation Monitoring Committee, promoting alternative dispute resolution mechanisms within the court's jurisdiction.[5] Roy's judgments during this period addressed regional challenges, such as public service selections and environmental compliance, reflecting the court's role in upholding statutory frameworks amid Northeast India's socio-economic and ethnic complexities.[18] For instance, in Abani Phukan v. State of Assam, he examined the Assam Public Service Commission's selection processes for administrative posts, emphasizing procedural fairness.[18] His approach prioritized evidence-based adjudication, often navigating federal-state tensions inherent to the region's governance.[17]

Chief Justice of Kerala High Court

Justice Hrishikesh Roy was transferred from the Gauhati High Court to the Kerala High Court on May 29, 2018, and appointed as Acting Chief Justice effective May 30, 2018, following the elevation of the previous Chief Justice.[19] [1] He was sworn in as the permanent Chief Justice, the 35th in the court's history, on August 8, 2018.[3] [20] [1] Roy's tenure as Chief Justice lasted approximately one year, concluding with his elevation to the Supreme Court of India, where he took oath on September 23, 2019.[3] [7] During this period, he emphasized judicial efficiency and progressive legal policies as administrative head of the court.[21] His leadership aligned with broader interests in alternative dispute resolution, though specific reforms in arbitration and mediation initiated at the Kerala High Court during 2018-2019 are not prominently documented in official records.[4] Administrative efforts under Roy contributed to maintaining court operations amid the high caseload typical of the Kerala High Court, which handles diverse civil, criminal, and constitutional matters.[21] The brevity of his term limited extensive structural changes, but his oversight ensured continuity in judicial administration until his departure.[3]

Appointment and service at Supreme Court of India

Hrishikesh Roy was elevated to the Supreme Court of India from his position as Chief Justice of the Kerala High Court, with the recommendation approved by the collegium in early 2019. He took oath as a judge on September 23, 2019.[7][1] Roy's tenure spanned over five years, concluding with his retirement on January 31, 2025, upon attaining the age of 65 as mandated by Article 124(2) of the Constitution of India.[22][23] During this period, he participated in 326 benches and authored 46 judgments, reflecting consistent engagement in the court's workload.[22] As the fourth-senior-most judge at the time of his retirement, Roy served as a member of the Supreme Court collegium, which advises on judicial appointments and transfers under the collegium system established by the Second and Third Judges Cases.[24][25] His role contributed to the collegium's deliberations on enhancing high court representation and addressing judicial vacancies, though specific recommendations remain confidential per convention.[25]

Notable contributions and decisions

Advocacy for alternative dispute resolution

During his tenure at the Gauhati High Court, Hrishikesh Roy chaired the Mediation Monitoring Committee, overseeing the production of the training film Shako (Bridge) to support mediation programs nationwide.[1] This resource, developed to illustrate mediation techniques, remains in use for educating mediators and judicial personnel.[3] Roy also spearheaded training initiatives for judicial officers on mediation practices under the high court's framework.[4] As executive head of the Assam State Legal Services Authority, Roy implemented the "Reach Out & Respond" program, aimed at expanding access to legal aidâ€”including alternative dispute resolution mechanismsâ€”for marginalized communities in remote areas.[1] In a parallel effort with the Arunachal Pradesh Legal Services Authority, he led the creation of the short film Apne Ajnabi, which addressed legal aid availability for victims of racial discrimination and promoted awareness of non-litigious resolution options under the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987.[1] These initiatives emphasized proactive outreach to integrate mediation into grassroots legal services, reducing reliance on formal court processes.[3] Roy's advocacy extended to institutional reforms, such as editing the ATMAN newsletter for nearly a decade to disseminate information on legal awareness and dispute resolution alternatives.[1] At the Supreme Court, he continued promoting ADR through public engagements, including inaugurating the North Zone-I regional conference on December 1, 2024, where he highlighted alternative dispute resolution as an efficient tool for resolving conflicts outside traditional litigation.[26] His efforts contributed to broader adoption of mediation in high court ecosystems, with training materials like Shako influencing ongoing programs in northeastern India.[4]

Key Supreme Court judgments

During his Supreme Court tenure from September 23, 2019, until retirement on February 1, 2025, Justice Roy authored 46 judgments, authoring none in the first year following his appointment and averaging 8.63 annually thereafter, the lowest among his appointment cohort.[27] In Municipal Corporation of Greater Mumbai v. Ankita Sinha (October 7, 2021), Roy authored the unanimous ruling affirming the National Green Tribunal's suo motu powers under the NGT Act, 2010, to initiate proceedings via letters, media reports, or representations on environmental violations, enabling faster response to ecological threats and bolstering Article 21 protections without formal petitions.[28][29] Roy advanced arbitration party autonomy through authored dissents, including in NN Global Mercantile Private Limited v. Indo Unique Flame Limited (April 2023), where he held unstamped arbitration agreements curable rather than void ab initio, rejecting broad judicial invalidation to preserve contractual intent, and in Central Organisation for Railway Electrification v. ECI-SPIC-SMO-MCML (JV) (November 2024), defending unilateral arbitrator clauses as valid under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, provided independence safeguards via Sections 12-15, limiting court overrides to evident bias and promoting efficient private resolution.[5][30][31] In Enforcement Directorate v. Kapil Wadhawan (March 27, 2023), Roy authored the decision interpreting Section 167(2) CrPC to include the remand day in the 60/90-day default bail computation, curbing investigative delays and reinforcing liberty safeguards by mandating release absent timely charge sheets, applicable across statutes like PMLA.[32] Benches with Roy stayed defamation proceedings in cases like that against Shashi Tharoor for 2018 remarks on Prime Minister Narendra Modi (September 10, 2024, extended January 22, 2025), halting trial court actions pending quashing pleas and signaling restraint against criminalizing political discourse absent malice.[33][34] Additional authored rulings encompassed Avitel Post Studioz Ltd. v. HSBC PI Holdings (Mauritius) Ltd. (2024), restricting foreign award enforcement refusals to rare public policy breaches under international bias standards, and Bajaj Allianz General Insurance Co. Ltd. v. Rambha Devi (2024), permitting light motor vehicle license holders to operate transport vehicles under 7,500 kg unladen, prioritizing driver livelihoods over unproven safety risks.[35][36] These outcomes enhanced institutional autonomy in green adjudication and arbitration while curbing overreach in bail and speech cases, yielding precedents for procedural efficiency and rights enforcement.[37]

Involvement in legal aid and institutional reforms

During his tenure as a judge at the Gauhati High Court, Hrishikesh Roy served as the executive head of the Assam State Legal Services Authority and implemented the "Reach Out & Respond" outreach programme, designed to enhance access to justice for marginalized communities by providing legal aid services directly to underserved areas.[3][7] This initiative involved mobile legal aid camps and awareness drives targeting remote and economically disadvantaged populations in Assam, addressing barriers such as geographical isolation and lack of awareness about entitlements under the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987.[13][38] Roy also introduced the "Hint of a Smile" programme at the Gauhati High Court, which trained judicial officers to adopt a more empathetic and less intimidating demeanor in courtroom interactions, particularly with vulnerable litigants including children and victims of trauma, aiming to reduce procedural alienation and foster trust in the judicial process.[37] Complementing these efforts, he spearheaded capacity-building training for judicial officers across the Gauhati High Court's jurisdiction, focusing on procedural efficiency and sensitivity to social contexts to strengthen institutional delivery of justice.[3] These measures contributed to measurable improvements in legal aid penetration in Assam, with the programmes facilitating early identification and resolution of disputes at the grassroots level, thereby alleviating caseload pressures on higher courts without compromising substantive rights.[4] While specific quantitative outcomes, such as the number of beneficiaries served, were not publicly detailed in official reports, the initiatives aligned with broader goals of institutional reform by emphasizing proactive outreach over reactive litigation.[39]

Controversies and public statements

Post-retirement remarks on government and judiciary

Following his retirement from the Supreme Court of India on January 31, 2025, Justice Hrishikesh Roy commented in a February 1, 2025, interview with India Today TV that ruling governments have "undoubtedly" employed legal mechanisms to target opposition figures, stating verbatim: "Undoubtedly, we are seeing instances of government in power using the legal machinery to target individuals in opposition."[40] He emphasized that such actions undermine institutional integrity, urging politicians to avoid conduct that erodes public trust in the judiciary.[40] In a separate interview with The Indian Express on the same day, Roy highlighted the importance of judicial independence, asserting that "a mature government wouldn't interfere too much with the judiciary."[8] He described executive overreach as a risk to separation of powers, while acknowledging that no system is immune to pressures, and stressed the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional balance without specifying instances beyond general observations.[8] Roy also addressed internal judicial challenges, noting in the Indian Express discussion that mechanisms for handling errant judges are limited to "soft options" like transfer or elevation decisions by the collegium, as impeachment remains rare and politically fraught.[10] He indicated that current laws inadequately equip the judiciary to address misconduct decisively, advocating for restraint among judges to maintain institutional credibility.[10] These remarks drew varied responses, with some legal commentators defending executive actions as necessary for law enforcement in high-stakes cases, citing examples like probes into corruption allegations against opposition leaders as evidence-based rather than politically motivated, though Roy did not engage these counterviews directly.[8]

Responses to judicial accountability debates

In post-retirement interviews, Justice Hrishikesh Roy articulated that constitutional provisions extend protection to judges regardless of conduct, stating, "The Constitution protects not just good judges, but also bad ones," likening it to a "raksha kavach" (protective armour) that safeguards even those "not on the right track."[41] He emphasized that this framework prioritizes judicial independence but results in limited and protracted accountability mechanisms, with impeachment as the sole formal avenue for removal, noting, "You canâ€™t hurry through to reach a hasty conclusion because executing a decision is also a burden on the same system."[41] Roy highlighted the inadequacy of current responses to judicial misconduct, describing available measures as "soft options" including counseling, transfer to another court, withholding assignment of cases, in-house inquiries, or impeachment, which he deemed largely ineffective due to practical hurdles.[42] For instance, he pointed to challenges in transfers, such as executive reluctance to approve them and states' unwillingness to accept judges flagged for issues, underscoring weak institutional checks particularly for off-bench behavior and attributing deeper problems to selection processes.[42] In referencing the December 8, 2024, remarks by Allahabad High Court Judge Shekhar Kumar Yadav at a Vishwa Hindu Parishad eventâ€”deemed derogatory toward a minority communityâ€”Roy noted an ongoing inquiry by the Chief Justice of India but refrained from direct commentary, illustrating the deliberative pace of such probes.[41][42] Addressing perceptions of impropriety in executive-judicial interactions, Roy commented on Prime Minister Narendra Modi's attendance at a Ganesh Puja hosted by then-Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud at the latter's residence in September 2023, describing the resulting "optics" as "disturbing" and something that "could have been avoided" to prevent public misgivings about separation of powers.[43][10] He clarified, however, that as a private religious event, it involved no discussion of pending cases and exerted no influence on judicial outcomes, while stressing judges' personal duty to uphold ethical standards amid such debates.[10] These views, expressed in early 2025 interviews following his January 31, 2025, retirement, reflect Roy's assessment of systemic constraints without advocating specific reforms beyond reinforcing self-regulation.[41][10]

Retirement and legacy

Demission of office

Justice Hrishikesh Roy demitted office as a judge of the Supreme Court of India on January 31, 2025, concluding a tenure of over five years that began with his elevation on September 23, 2019.[44][45] The formal farewell ceremony took place in the Supreme Court on the same day, attended by fellow judges, including Chief Justice Sanjiv Khanna, and members of the legal fraternity.[46][47] In his farewell address, Roy described the pursuit of justice as a steady process that slowly shifts society and strengthens democracy, underscoring the incremental yet persistent role of judicial adjudication in societal progress.[48] The ceremony featured tributes highlighting his judicial temperament, with Chief Justice Khanna praising Roy's legal acumen, balanced approach, and sense of humor that eased courtroom proceedings.[38][13] Contemporaries noted his wit as a distinctive feature that humanized complex deliberations, contributing to a collegial bench environment.[49]

Overall impact and evaluations

Justice Hrishikesh Roy's tenure at the Supreme Court of India, spanning over five years until his retirement on January 31, 2025, has been evaluated positively by judicial peers for his legal acumen, balanced reasoning, and contributions to institutional efficiency. Chief Justice Sanjiv Khanna commended Roy's "legal wisdom, knowledge, and reasonableness," highlighting his sense of humor as a factor that eased proceedings and enhanced collegiality among benches. Legal observers note his role in promoting alternative dispute resolution (ADR) mechanisms, including mediation and arbitration, where he advocated for minimal judicial interference to uphold party autonomy, influencing a judiciary-wide shift toward efficient dispute settlement outside traditional litigation.[38][13][7] In arbitration law, Roy's judgments have been credited with reinforcing enforceability and equality principles, contributing to a more arbitration-friendly judicial environment in 2024, as per analyses of Supreme Court trends. His decisions upholding the National Green Tribunal's suo motu powers have been seen as bolstering environmental jurisprudence by enabling proactive regulatory oversight without undue procedural hurdles. On legal aid, Roy's initiatives emphasized accessible justice, aligning with broader reforms to reduce case backlogs through non-adversarial methods.[50][5][4] Quantitatively, Roy participated in 324 benches and authored 46 judgments over his 5.33-year term, averaging 8.63 judgments annuallyâ€”a relatively modest output compared to peers, reflecting a focus on quality over volume in complex constitutional and policy matters. While some assessments critique the Supreme Court's overall disposal rates amid rising pendency, Roy's legacy is framed by contemporaries as one of steady pursuit of justice through pragmatic, evidence-based adjudication rather than prolific authorship. No widespread criticisms of his judicial output emerged in post-tenure reviews, with emphasis instead on his mentorship of juniors and humane approach to case management.[22][51][46]
