List of countries by annual cannabis use

The list of countries by annual cannabis use enumerates nations according to the estimated proportion of their adult population—typically those aged 15 to 64—who report having used cannabis at least once in the previous 12 months. These figures stem from self-reported responses in national population-based surveys, aggregated and harmonized by the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) through its World Drug Reports to facilitate international comparability, though methodological variances and reporting biases persist across datasets.[1] Cannabis constitutes the most commonly used controlled substance globally, with approximately 244 million past-year users in 2023, corresponding to 4.6 percent of the world's 15–64 population, marking a rise from 3.9 percent a decade prior.[2] Prevalence disparities are stark, peaking in regions like North America, Oceania, and West and Central Africa—where rates often exceed 10 percent—while remaining negligible under 1 percent in much of Asia and the Middle East, reflecting influences such as prohibitive legal regimes, cultural prohibitions, and enforcement stringency that likely suppress honest disclosure in surveys.[2][3] Shifts toward liberalization in select jurisdictions, including full recreational legalization in Canada, Uruguay, and various U.S. states, correlate with elevated reported usage, attributable to diminished barriers to access and reduced social stigma rather than inherent demand alterations. Empirical challenges in compiling such lists include inconsistent survey frequencies, non-response biases, and the UNODC's reliance on member-state submissions, which may understate true incidence in authoritarian contexts with harsh penalties.[1]

Methodology and Data Sources

Definition of Annual Prevalence

Annual prevalence of cannabis use measures the percentage of a specified population that reports having consumed cannabis at least once during the preceding 12 months.[4] This indicator, often termed "past-year prevalence," is derived primarily from general population surveys targeting individuals aged 15 to 64 years, as this age range captures the majority of non-medical drug consumption patterns while excluding most underage and elderly respondents unlikely to participate in household-based sampling.[5] Such surveys typically employ self-reported data collected through structured questionnaires administered in person, by telephone, or online, with efforts to ensure anonymity to minimize underreporting.[6] The metric serves as a standardized compromise between lifetime prevalence—which indicates ever-use and may inflate estimates by including distant or one-time exposures—and past-30-day (current) prevalence, which focuses on recent activity but excludes occasional users active within the year.[4] For cannabis, annual prevalence is particularly useful for cross-national comparisons, as it reflects recent exposure without capturing chronic dependence or daily habits, which are better assessed via intensity measures like frequency of use or treatment demand indicators.[7] Global estimates, such as those from the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC), apply this definition consistently across regions, adjusting for survey methodology differences where possible, though variations in question wording (e.g., "used cannabis" versus "used marijuana or hashish") can introduce minor discrepancies.[2] In practice, annual prevalence rates for cannabis hover around 3-4% globally among the 15-64 population, but they are sensitive to cultural attitudes toward reporting, legalization status, and sampling biases favoring urban or accessible households.[2] Organizations like the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) align with this definition for European data, emphasizing its role in tracking trends over time rather than absolute incidence.[7] Limitations include potential underestimation due to stigma in prohibitive jurisdictions or overestimation from recall bias, underscoring the need for triangulation with indirect indicators like seizures or wastewater analysis in comprehensive assessments.[6]

Primary Global Sources

The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) serves as the principal global authority for compiling and disseminating data on cannabis use prevalence, drawing from national household surveys, treatment records, and seizure data across member states. Its annual World Drug Report, with the 2025 edition released on June 26, 2025, estimates that 228 million people aged 15–64 used cannabis at least once in the preceding year as of 2023 data, representing approximately 4.7% of the global population in that age group. [8] These figures aggregate country-level estimates where available, though coverage varies due to inconsistent reporting from low- and middle-income nations, with prevalence rates derived via statistical modeling for gaps.[9] The World Health Organization (WHO) supplements UNODC data through periodic global health surveys, such as the World Health Survey and mental health atlases, which include self-reported cannabis use metrics from participating countries. For instance, WHO data from 2016–2022 indicate annual prevalence rates ranging from under 1% in parts of Asia to over 10% in regions like Oceania and North America, though these are less frequently updated than UNODC reports and often rely on smaller sample sizes.[10] WHO's estimates prioritize health impacts but face criticism for under-sampling high-use populations due to reliance on voluntary national contributions.[3] Regional bodies like the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) provide detailed data for Europe, reporting 8.4% annual prevalence among adults aged 15–64 in 2023 based on harmonized surveys, which UNODC incorporates into global aggregates.[11] Similarly, the Inter-American Drug Abuse Control Commission (CICAD) compiles Americas-specific figures, but these feed into UNODC's framework rather than standing as independent global sources. Overall, UNODC's methodology emphasizes empirical triangulation to mitigate biases in self-reported data, such as underreporting in prohibitive legal environments, though absolute country rankings remain approximate due to survey non-comparability.

Survey Techniques and Comparability Issues

Surveys measuring annual cannabis use prevalence primarily rely on self-reported data from nationally representative household surveys targeting the general population aged 15-64, which are considered reliable for estimating cannabis, amphetamine-type stimulants, and cocaine use due to respondents' willingness to disclose such behaviors compared to harder drugs.[12] School-based surveys, such as the European School Survey Project on Alcohol and Other Drugs (ESPAD) or equivalents like the U.S. Monitoring the Future, capture data among 15- to 16-year-olds, with adjustments applied using regional benchmarks from countries like the United States, Colombia, and Pakistan to derive broader estimates.[4] Data aggregation occurs through mechanisms like the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) Annual Report Questionnaire (ARQ), which compiles national survey results from member states, supplemented by regional bodies such as the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) for Europe.[12] Annual prevalence is often derived indirectly from lifetime use data via regional conversion ratios—for instance, a ratio of 0.387 in West and Central Europe—due to inconsistencies in direct reporting across surveys.[4] Extrapolation techniques, including regression models or treatment-to-user multipliers, fill gaps where primary surveys are absent, particularly in low-response regions like Africa (57% ARQ participation) or Oceania (12.5%).[4] Comparability across countries is hindered by variations in survey design, including sampling frames that may exclude transient populations like the homeless or incarcerated, leading to underestimation in urban-heavy samples.[12] Underreporting is prevalent due to social desirability bias, exacerbated in jurisdictions with strict prohibitions, where fear of legal repercussions suppresses disclosure, whereas legalized settings like parts of Canada or Uruguay yield higher reported rates.[13] Differences in question wording—such as defining "use" as any consumption versus repeated instances—and recall accuracy for the past-year period introduce further inconsistencies, with validity of estimates varying by national context and potentially biasing cross-country comparisons.[14] Temporal mismatches arise from outdated data or disruptions like the COVID-19 pandemic, which halted surveys in 2020-2021 and affected response rates, while small nations (population under 100,000 aged 15-64) are often excluded from extrapolations to avoid distortion.[4] Heterogeneity in age standardization, response definitions (e.g., chronic versus occasional use), and reliance on indirect methods like wastewater analysis or seizure multipliers compounds uncertainty, with UNODC applying 10th-90th percentile ranges to quantify variability but noting persistent gaps in over 60 countries lacking recent household surveys.[12][4]

Global and Regional Patterns

Worldwide Prevalence Overview

In 2023, an estimated 244 million people aged 15-64, or 4.6% of the global population in that age group, reported past-year cannabis use, making it the most prevalent illicit drug worldwide.[2] This figure represents a one-third increase from the 3.9% prevalence recorded a decade earlier in 2013, reflecting rising consumption patterns amid expanding production and market diversification.[2] Prevalence varies significantly by gender, with 7.0% of men and 2.3% of women reporting use in the past year.[2] Regional disparities underscore the global overview, with North America exhibiting the highest rates, followed by Oceania (Australia and New Zealand) and parts of West and Central Africa.[2] Among adolescents aged 15-16, past-year use stood at 4.4% globally in 2023, comparable to adult rates but carrying heightened risks of cognitive and mental health impacts.[2] These estimates, derived from surveys across 135 countries covering 70% of the world's population, highlight cannabis's dominance in treatment-seeking for drug use disorders, accounting for 42% of reporting countries' primary substance.[2] However, underreporting remains a challenge in regions with strict prohibitions, potentially understating true prevalence in data-scarce areas like much of Asia and the Middle East.[2]

Regional Variations in Use Rates

Annual prevalence of cannabis use among adults aged 15-64 varies markedly across regions, with North America and Oceania exhibiting the highest rates globally. In North America, prevalence stands at approximately 20.2 percent, driven primarily by high use in the United States and Canada where legalization has coincided with increased accessibility.[1] Oceania follows closely, with rates around 12 percent overall, reflecting elevated use in Australia and New Zealand, where past-year prevalence exceeds 10 percent in national surveys.[1] Europe demonstrates moderate prevalence, averaging about 8 percent, though with subregional differences: Western and Central Europe report higher rates near 10 percent for men, while Eastern Europe tends lower.[1] [15] In contrast, Asia records the lowest regional rates at roughly 1.5 percent, with male prevalence at 2.7 percent and female at 0.3 percent, attributed to strict prohibitions and cultural stigmas in many countries.[1] Africa shows heterogeneous patterns, with West and Central subregions displaying higher prevalence—men at 10.9 percent—potentially linked to local production and traditional uses, while overall continental estimates hover around 6 percent.[1] South America averages 4.1 percent, lower than North America but with variations such as higher rates in Uruguay post-legalization.[1] These disparities underscore the influence of policy, enforcement, and socioeconomic factors on use patterns, though data comparability remains challenged by survey methodologies and underreporting in prohibitive contexts.[1]

Country-Specific Rankings

Countries with Highest Reported Use

The countries with the highest reported annual cannabis use, based on general population surveys, are predominantly those with permissive cultural attitudes or legal frameworks, where self-reporting is less stigmatized. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of studies from 2000 to 2024 identified peak rates exceeding 40% in select cases, though such figures derive from national household or population-based surveys prone to methodological variations, including differences in age groups sampled (typically 15-64 years) and question phrasing on past-year use. These outliers contrast with UNODC global estimates of around 4% annual prevalence, highlighting potential over-reporting in low-stigma environments versus under-reporting elsewhere due to legal risks.[3] Reported highs cluster in Oceania, Western Europe, and North America. For instance, Australia recorded 48.70% past-year use in a 2004 national survey, reflecting broad accessibility and cultural normalization prior to subsequent declines amid policy shifts. The Netherlands followed with 43.90% in 2013, aligned with de facto tolerance in coffee shops, though subsequent European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction data suggest stabilization around 10-15% in recent years due to tighter regulations. In North America, the United States reported 38.12% in 2021 National Survey on Drug Use and Health data for the population aged 12 and older, driven by state-level legalization expanding access, with daily use rising post-reform.[3]

Countries with Lowest Reported Use

Several nations in the Middle East and East Asia report annual cannabis use prevalence rates below 0.5% among adults aged 15-64, reflecting rigorous enforcement of prohibition laws, religious and cultural norms against intoxication, and societal pressures that discourage disclosure in surveys. Qatar records the lowest documented rate at 0.1%, followed by Saudi Arabia at 0.3%, based on aggregated national and regional data compilations. These figures likely underestimate true usage due to fear of legal repercussions—punishments in Saudi Arabia include flogging, imprisonment, or execution for drug offenses—leading to systematic underreporting in self-reported surveys, a common issue in authoritarian or theocratic regimes where data collection relies on voluntary admission rather than objective measures like wastewater analysis.[16] In East Asia, China, Japan, South Korea, Hong Kong, Vietnam, and Taiwan all report past-year prevalence under 0.5%, with China's total registered drug users (across all substances) numbering only 1.12 million in a population exceeding 1.4 billion as of 2022, implying cannabis-specific rates near negligible levels. Japan's illicit drug use remains low overall, with cannabis as the most prevalent but still affecting fewer than 1% annually, per government-monitored trends up to 2017 that show gradual increases yet retention of minimal baseline rates. These low reports align with zero-tolerance policies, such as Japan's three-year minimum sentence for possession and China's execution risks for trafficking, which prioritize deterrence over harm reduction and correlate with suppressed visibility of use. Peer-reviewed analyses confirm Asia's regional average at approximately 2%, far below global norms, though cross-national comparability is limited by varying survey methodologies and non-response biases in conservative societies.[16][17] Other examples include Malaysia, identified in comparative studies as having among the lowest prevalence and dependence rates in Southeast Asia, and Brunei, where Sharia-based prohibitions yield near-absent reported use amid capital penalties for trafficking. In the Islamic Republic of Afghanistan under Taliban rule since 2021, cultivation persists for export but domestic consumption is severely curtailed by public executions and destruction campaigns, resulting in de facto near-zero reported annual use despite historical highs pre-2021. Such patterns underscore causal links between draconian enforcement and visible restraint, though empirical evidence from indirect indicators like seizures suggests hidden consumption persists at low levels; for instance, UNODC data highlights Asia's overall cannabis user estimate at 54 million (2% prevalence) versus higher regional averages elsewhere. Credible sources like UNODC emphasize that these minima are not necessarily indicative of absolute rarity but of effective suppression of open use, with academic biases toward liberalization potentially downplaying enforcement efficacy in interpretations of the data.[18]

Key National Trends and Changes

In Canada, recreational cannabis legalization in October 2018 correlated with an initial rise in annual prevalence, with past-year use among those aged 15 and older increasing from 15% in 2018 to 22% by 2021, driven partly by reduced perceived risks and expanded access.[19] Subsequent national monitoring through 2024 shows stabilization in past-year rates alongside a modest uptick in past-30-day use to 17%, though daily or near-daily consumption edged higher from 7% pre-legalization to 8% post-legalization among workers.[20][21] These shifts reflect broader normalization, with heavier use patterns persisting among young adults aged 20-24, where prevalence exceeds 50% in some cohorts.[22] In the United States, the National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH) documents a substantial long-term increase in past-year cannabis use, rising from 11% in 2002 to 25% in 2022 among individuals aged 12 and older, with per capita rates doubling since 2008 amid progressive state-level decriminalization and commercialization.[23][24] Frequent use (daily or near-daily) among adults also climbed from 1.9% in 2002 to 3.5% by 2014, a trajectory that continued into the 2020s despite federal restrictions, as past-year prevalence reached 19% overall by 2023 estimates.[25][26] Youth trends show volatility, with past-year use among adolescents increasing from 7.6% in 2013 to 15.1% in 2022 before plateauing.[27] Across Europe, the European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA) reports steady growth in cannabis prevalence, with last-year use estimated at 8.4% among adults aged 15-64 in recent national surveys, and past-month use up 27% from 2010 to 2019 levels at 3.9%.[11][14] Among young adults aged 15-34, last-year prevalence hovered at 15.1% as of 2023, with males twice as likely to report use, though country variations persist—higher in nations like Spain and France, lower in Eastern Europe.[28] These increments align with rising potency and market availability, despite uneven policy reforms.[29] Globally, United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) data indicate cannabis remains the most prevalent illicit substance, with 219 million past-year users (4.3% of adults) in 2021, up from prior estimates, though 44% of reporting countries noted stable rates between surveys.[30][31] Increases predominate in legalized or tolerant jurisdictions, while prohibitionist regions like parts of Asia and the Middle East sustain low prevalence below 1%, with minimal documented shifts due to enforcement and cultural stigma.[16]

Factors Influencing Prevalence

Legal Policy Effects on Use Rates

Empirical studies on jurisdictions transitioning from prohibition or decriminalization to regulated recreational cannabis markets indicate that legal policy changes have generally resulted in modest or negligible shifts in overall annual prevalence rates, with variations by age group and timeframe. In the United States, where 24 states had legalized recreational use by 2023, meta-analyses of state-level data from 2012 onward show small increases in past-month adult use (approximately 1-2 percentage points) following implementation, but no consistent rise in youth prevalence; for instance, Monitoring the Future surveys reported stable or declining adolescent use rates post-legalization in states like Colorado and Washington.[32][33] These findings suggest that pre-existing upward trends in use, driven by shifting attitudes, accounted for much of the observed change rather than policy alone.[34] Canada's national legalization in October 2018 provides a controlled national case, where past-year cannabis use among adults aged 15 and older rose slightly from 15% in 2018 to 17% by 2024, aligning with a 2-3% increase in past-30-day use, though rates stabilized after 2020 and remained below peaks in some subgroups.[20] Longitudinal data indicate this increment continued a pre-legalization trajectory of rising use from 2013-2017, with no evidence of exponential growth; youth use (ages 15-24) showed minimal change, hovering around 20-25% past-year prevalence.[35] In Uruguay, the world's first nation to fully legalize recreational cannabis in 2013, annual prevalence remained stable at 7-9% through 2022, with problematic use fixed at 2.1% since 2011 and overall consumption declining from 14.6% in 2018 to 12.3% by 2024 among surveyed populations, attributed partly to regulated supply reducing unregulated access.[36] Decriminalization without full commercialization, as in Portugal since 2001, correlates with sustained low prevalence; lifetime cannabis use among adults fell from 7.8% in 2001 to 5.9% by 2019, alongside reduced treatment admissions, challenging assumptions that laxer policies inherently drive uptake.[37] Conversely, stringent prohibition in countries like Singapore yields reported annual rates below 1%, but surveys likely undercount due to severe penalties (up to 10 years imprisonment for possession), illustrating enforcement's role in suppressing visibility rather than eliminating demand.[38] Systematic reviews emphasize that policy effects are heterogeneous, with modest positive associations for young adult initiation (odds ratio ~1.1-1.3) but limited causal impact on heavy use, as access barriers under prohibition fail to deter determined users while fostering black markets.[39][40]

Cultural, Economic, and Demographic Drivers

Cultural attitudes toward cannabis, shaped by religious doctrines and historical traditions, exert substantial influence on national prevalence rates. In predominantly Islamic countries, where religious texts interpret cannabis as an intoxicant prohibited under Sharia law, annual use remains minimal, often below 1% in nations like Saudi Arabia and Iran, as doctrinal enforcement suppresses both production and consumption. Conversely, in Hindu-majority India, cannabis derivatives such as bhang hold sacramental roles in rituals associated with Shiva worship, contributing to a cultural tolerance that sustains moderate prevalence around 3% among adults, though recreational use is less widespread than in Western contexts.[42][43] These patterns underscore how entrenched cultural norms, independent of legal status, can either entrench stigma or foster acceptance, with secular Western societies exhibiting higher rates due to diminished religious opposition to psychoactive substances. Economic factors, particularly national wealth, correlate positively with cannabis use in cross-national analyses of youth. A study across 31 European and North American countries identified higher GDP per capita as associated with increased frequent use among mid-adolescents, attributing this to greater disposable income facilitating access and social experimentation in affluent settings. However, this trend contrasts with intra-country data from the United States, where lower-income groups report higher usage rates, suggesting that at the global level, prosperity enables recreational rather than coping-driven consumption, while poverty in developing regions may amplify use through informal markets despite lower overall reporting. African nations like Nigeria, with 19.4% prevalence despite modest GDP, illustrate how economic informality and agricultural production can sustain high rates amid underinvestment in alternatives.[44][16] Demographic variables, including gender, age structure, and urbanization, systematically shape prevalence disparities. Globally, males exhibit nearly three times the annual use rate of females, as documented in UNODC estimates, reflecting biological, social, and risk-taking differences amplified across cultures. Youth-dominated populations drive higher national figures, with peak use in the 15-34 age cohort, as older groups show sharp declines; countries with bulging youth demographics, such as those in sub-Saharan Africa, thus report elevated averages despite data inconsistencies. Urbanization further elevates rates, with urban dwellers facing heightened exposure through dense social networks and availability, as evidenced in European contexts where city prevalence exceeds rural by factors of 1.5-2, a pattern linked to migration and lifestyle shifts rather than policy alone.[2][18][45]

Data Limitations and Debates

Sources of Underreporting and Bias

Self-reported surveys, which form the basis of most international cannabis prevalence estimates, systematically underestimate actual use due to respondents' reluctance to disclose illicit behavior. Validation studies using biological markers such as hair assays reveal that self-reports underreport substance use prevalence by 30% to 60%, with an average detection ratio of 1.50 when compared to objective tests.[46] For cannabis specifically, discrepancies arise when users report alternative forms like blunts without acknowledging cannabis content, leading to adjusted past-year prevalence estimates rising from reported figures to 15.5% in some national samples.[47] Legal status exacerbates underreporting in countries where cannabis remains prohibited, as fear of legal consequences prompts non-disclosure or survey non-response. In jurisdictions with harsh penalties, respondents anticipate prosecution risks, even in anonymous surveys, resulting in lower reported rates compared to legalized regions where disclosure barriers diminish.[48] Criminalization frameworks amplify this effect by fostering perceptions of use as inherently deviant, correlating with diminished self-reporting across global datasets compiled by bodies like the UNODC.[49] Cultural stigma compounds these issues, particularly in conservative or collectivist societies where admitting drug use invites social ostracism or familial repercussions, independent of legal risks. Stigma-driven underreporting manifests in reduced treatment-seeking and survey participation, with international comparisons showing higher discrepancies in regions prioritizing moralistic views over harm reduction.[50] Methodological biases further distort data; for instance, interview mode influences candor, with telephone surveys yielding higher underreporting among certain demographics due to perceived lower anonymity.[51] International statistics exhibit regional biases, with more reliable data from Western Europe and North America—where EMCDDA and similar agencies employ standardized, anonymous protocols—contrasting sparse or manipulated reports from authoritarian states or low-resource areas. In policy-restrictive environments, governments may suppress or fabricate low-use figures to align with anti-drug narratives, while post-legalization shifts in perception reduce stigma but introduce overreporting risks from normalized attitudes.[18] These asymmetries privilege empirical data from high-credibility sources like peer-reviewed validations over unverified national claims, highlighting the need for biomarker corroboration in global rankings.[52]

Implications for Policy and Interpretation

Data on annual cannabis prevalence reveal that countries enforcing strict prohibition, such as those in the Middle East and parts of Asia, consistently report rates below 1%, suggesting effective deterrence through severe penalties and cultural stigma when enforcement is rigorous.[53] In contrast, nations with legalization or decriminalization, including Canada and Uruguay, have observed post-reform increases in adult use, with Canada's past-year prevalence rising from approximately 15% pre-2018 to over 20% by 2021, alongside accelerated daily use patterns.[54] [55] These trends imply that policy liberalization correlates with normalized consumption among adults, potentially eroding perceptions of harm, as evidenced by declining risk assessments in legalized U.S. states.[49] Policy formulation must account for causal factors beyond legality, including enforcement capacity and sociocultural norms; empirical evidence indicates prohibition suppresses prevalence more reliably in homogeneous societies with low tolerance for intoxicants, whereas Western contexts show modest use upticks post-legalization without corresponding youth surges.[18] [32] Decriminalization models, like Portugal's, have not demonstrably reduced cannabis-specific prevalence compared to stricter regimes, highlighting that supply regulation may mitigate black-market harms but invites higher potency products and treatment demands.[56] [57] Thus, blanket legalization risks amplifying public health burdens, such as increased healthcare utilization, absent targeted interventions like age restrictions and education.[58] Interpreting rankings requires caution due to methodological variances; self-reported surveys in prohibitive jurisdictions likely understate true use owing to stigma-induced non-disclosure, while liberal environments yield more candid data, potentially inflating comparative prevalence.[53] Cross-national analyses must disentangle policy effects from confounders like economic prosperity and youth demographics, as higher-use nations often feature urbanized, affluent populations predisposed to experimentation.[55] Policymakers interpreting these disparities should prioritize evidence of enforcement efficacy over ideological shifts toward permissiveness, recognizing that sustained low prevalence under prohibition underscores viable alternatives to commodification.[37][59]
