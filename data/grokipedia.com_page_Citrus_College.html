Citrus College

Citrus College is a public community college located in Glendora, California, approximately 25 miles northeast of downtown Los Angeles in the foothills of the San Gabriel Mountains.[1] Founded in 1915 under the leadership of Dr. Floyd S. Hayden with an initial class of 27 students, it holds the distinction of being the oldest community college in Los Angeles County and the fifth oldest in the state of California.[1] The institution serves residents of the Azusa, Claremont, Duarte, Glendora, and Monrovia school districts, offering over 75 associate degree programs, more than 115 certificate and skill awards, and pathways for transfer to four-year universities.[2] Accredited by the Accrediting Commission for Community and Junior Colleges of the Western Association of Schools and Colleges, Citrus College emphasizes quality educational experiences leading to degree completion, workforce preparation, and personal development.[3][4]

History

Founding and Early Development (1915â€“1940s)

Citrus College was established in June 1915 as Citrus Junior College in Glendora, California, initially enrolling 27 students under the leadership of principal Dr. Floyd S. Hayden, who advocated for expanding the local high school's offerings to include junior college-level courses.[1][5] This made it the fifth-oldest community college in California and the first such institution in Los Angeles County, aligning with the emerging community college movement that emphasized accessible postsecondary education tied to local high schools.[1] Hayden's efforts integrated the junior college with the Citrus Union High School, enabling shared resources and facilities while providing two-year programs focused on transfer preparation and vocational training.[5] The institution's first graduating class in 1917 consisted of four students, reflecting modest early output amid limited infrastructure.[1] By 1925, enrollment had expanded to approximately 100 studentsâ€”nearly quadrupling the initial figureâ€”though only 11 diplomas were awarded in the college's first decade, indicating challenges in retention and completion typical of nascent junior colleges.[1] Governance remained under the Citrus Union High School District from 1915 onward, ensuring administrative continuity but subordinating college autonomy to secondary education priorities.[1] Through the 1930s and into the 1940s, Citrus College maintained steady operations amid the Great Depression and World War II, serving as a local hub for higher education in a region dominated by citrus agriculture, though specific enrollment or infrastructural expansions during this span are sparsely documented beyond continued district oversight.[1] The period emphasized core academic offerings, with the college's role solidified as the oldest continuously operating community college in Los Angeles County, preparing students for university transfer or workforce entry without major recorded disruptions.[5]

Post-War Expansion and Modernization (1950sâ€“1990s)

Following World War II, Citrus College experienced its first major expansion to address surging enrollment fueled by the GI Bill and regional population growth. Between 1946 and 1959, the college purchased 88 acres of land, enlarging the campus from 16 acres to 104 acres to support increased student capacity and infrastructure needs.[1][6] In July 1961, the Citrus Junior College District was formed, granting the institution independence from the Citrus Union High School District and initially incorporating the Azusa and Glendora unified school districts; this shift enabled dedicated governance and funding for higher education priorities. The district boundaries expanded in 1967 to encompass the Claremont, Duarte, and Monrovia school districts, broadening its service area and resource base. By 1970, it was renamed the Citrus Community College District, aligning with its evolving role in community-focused postsecondary education.[1][7] Enrollment growth accelerated through the postwar era, reaching over 11,000 students by 1975, during which nearly 700 degrees were conferred, underscoring the college's adaptation to demand for associate degrees and vocational training. Ambitious construction initiatives in the 1960s, 1970s, and 1990s introduced new buildingsâ€”such as updates to the Educational Development Center from its 1970s originsâ€”and extensive renovations, enhancing classroom technology, accessibility, and overall facilities to meet modern pedagogical standards.[7][5][8]

Recent Institutional Changes (2000sâ€“Present)

In 2004, voters in the Citrus Community College District approved Measure G, a $121 million general obligation bond, which funded a comprehensive facilities expansion project aimed at modernizing campus infrastructure to support enhanced student learning environments.[9] This initiative included the construction of key structures such as a new central plant for energy management and a dedicated campus safety building, both completed in 2008 to address identified needs from prior planning documents.[10] The expansion efforts continued with the approval of Measure Y in November 2020, authorizing $298 million in bonds for additional improvements including parking connectivity, pathway refinements, and program-specific facilities; however, as of late 2023, project prioritization and implementation had advanced slowly due to sequencing adjustments by college leadership.[11][12] Complementing these physical developments, the college finalized its 2020-2030 Educational and Facilities Master Plan, which projected long-term site enhancements based on enrollment forecasts and service area needs, followed by a 2024 update incorporating updated demographic data and bond fund availability.[4] Administratively, Citrus College adopted a 2021-2026 Strategic Plan establishing 19 measurable objectives centered on boosting student persistence, completion rates, and equity in outcomes, with annual implementation plans tracking progress through data tools introduced in 2024.[13][14] In response to institutional effectiveness challenges, the college applied for and received support from California's Institutional Effectiveness Partnership Initiative, engaging a Partnership Resource Team in 2022 that conducted an on-site review in March 2023 and awarded a $200,000 grant to aid strategic enrollment and program growth, particularly in noncredit offerings.[15][16] Under Superintendent/President Greg Schulz, appointed to lead since at least the early 2020s, the institution has emphasized targeted enrollment strategies amid observed declines in the 20- to 24-year-old demographic, shifting focus toward high school dual-enrollment pipelines and adults aged 25 to 39 while leveraging state grants for noncredit expansion.[17][18][19] These changes reflect ongoing adaptations to fiscal constraints, demographic shifts, and state-mandated accountability measures in California's community college system.

Campus and Facilities

Location and Physical Layout

Citrus College is located at 1000 West Foothill Boulevard in Glendora, California, within Los Angeles County.[20] The campus sits in the San Gabriel Valley, approximately 26 miles east of downtown Los Angeles, providing access to regional transportation routes including major highways.[21] The institution occupies a 104-acre site featuring 44 buildings and seven outdoor athletics facilities.[22] The physical layout includes administrative structures such as the Administration Building (AD), academic centers like the Campus Center (CC) for student services and dining, and specialized facilities including the Aquatic Center (AQ), Adapted Physical Education area (AP), and Automotive Annex (AA).[23] Athletic amenities encompass a baseball field (BF) and other sports venues, while support buildings house the Owl Bookshop (BK) and various labs and galleries.[24] The arrangement supports both traditional classroom instruction and vocational training, with pathways connecting parking areas, emergency blue light phones, and core academic zones for efficient navigation.[25]

Key Infrastructure and Resources

Citrus College maintains a range of academic infrastructure supporting instruction and student services, bolstered by over $113 million invested through Measure G bond funds since 2004 for modernization, infrastructure upgrades, and facility expansions.[10] The institution's Educational and Facilities Master Plan, updated as of August 2024, guides further development toward 21st-century standards, emphasizing safety, accessibility, and specialized buildings for STEM, career technical education (CTE), library enhancements, and a student union.[4][26] The Hayden Memorial Library functions as the primary learning resource center, occupying 33,000 square feet with 32,500 print volumes, 335,000 e-books, 37,000 streaming videos, and subscriptions to 46 databases.[24] It provides over 325 seating options, 11 group study rooms, more than 200 public computers, and checkout services for Chromebooks and laptops to facilitate in-library use.[24] Laboratory facilities support science disciplines through dedicated structures such as the Life Science Building and Mathematics/Sciences Building, enabling hands-on coursework in biology, biotechnology, earth science, and related fields.[23][27] The STEM Center offers free drop-in and remote tutoring for mathematics and science classes via Microsoft Teams, complemented by specialized equipment for research experiences like summer lab programs.[28][24] The Learning Center, housed in the Education Building, delivers tutorial services, a CRLA-certified Level II Writing Center, a Speech Lab, and adapted testing for students with disabilities.[24] Technology and Computer Services (TeCS) oversees campus-wide computing, networking, communications infrastructure, and software applications to support instructional and administrative functions.[29] All students receive institutional email accounts for official communications, with additional computing access available across facilities.[24]

Academics

Degree Programs and Curriculum

Citrus College offers associate degrees in 78 fields of study, comprising 33 Associate Degrees for Transfer (ADTs) designed for seamless transfer to California State University (CSU) campuses and 45 local Associate of Arts (AA) or Associate of Science (AS) degrees focused on specific career preparation or foundational knowledge.[30] These programs emphasize a balance between general education, major-specific coursework, and electives, with ADTs requiring 60 transferable units including at least 18 in the major and completion of CSU general education breadth requirements.[31] In addition to associate degrees, the college provides certificates of achievement, skill awards, and noncredit certificates across vocational and academic tracks, totaling over 100 options that prepare students for immediate workforce entry or further study.[32] Career and Technical Education (CTE) programs, such as Automotive Technology, Biotechnology, Registered Nursing, and Water Technology, integrate hands-on training with industry certifications, often aligned with regional labor market needs in the San Gabriel Valley.[33] Transfer-oriented majors like Biological Sciences, Business Administration, and Computer Science facilitate pathways to four-year institutions, with articulation agreements ensuring credit transferability.[34] The curriculum is structured around nine academic divisions, including Mathematics, Sciences, and Business; Language Arts and Library; and Visual and Performing Arts, spanning 58 disciplines from Anthropology to Welding Technology.[35] General education requirements under Option I mandate at least 30 units across categories such as natural sciences, social sciences, humanities, and communication, fostering skills in critical thinking, quantitative reasoning, and ethical awareness; alternative options like the Intersegmental General Education Transfer Curriculum (IGETC) are available for CSU or University of California (UC) transfers.[36] Course development and revisions are managed by the Curriculum Committee, which reviews proposals for alignment with state standards, labor market data, and student learning outcomes, ensuring programs remain current as of the 2025-2026 catalog.[37][38] Specialized pathways include the Honors Transfer Program for high-achieving students, offering enriched coursework and priority registration, and STEM-focused tracks in fields like Pre-Engineering and Physical Sciences that incorporate lab-based learning and research opportunities.[39] Online and hybrid delivery options support flexible curriculum access, with all programs adhering to Title 5 regulations for minimum standards in unit calculation and proficiency demonstrations.[34] The catalog explicitly notes that curriculum details are advisory, subject to annual updates based on accreditation reviews and enrollment trends.[38]

Faculty and Instructional Quality

Citrus College employs 161 full-time instructional faculty and 367 adjunct faculty as of fall 2024, with women representing 57% of full-time and 52% of adjunct faculty.[46] This composition reflects a reliance on part-time instructors, consistent with trends in California community colleges where adjuncts often comprise over 60% of the faculty pool. Faculty qualifications adhere to California Education Code Title 5 standards, requiring master's degrees or equivalent for most lower-division courses, with job postings specifying minimum credentials from accredited U.S. or foreign institutions and an equivalency process to broaden applicant diversity.[6] The student-to-faculty ratio is 27:1, indicating relatively large class sizes compared to national averages for community colleges.[41] Teaching effectiveness is assessed via annual evaluations for probationary full-time faculty and periodic reviews for tenured and adjunct staff, incorporating peer observations, student feedback, and self-assessments, though challenges arose in adjunct evaluations during the shift to remote learning in 2020.[6] Professional development includes orientation for new hires, online teaching training (e.g., @One courses and COVID-19 adaptations), and emphasis on active learning methods, with faculty contributing to curriculum updates under initiatives like AB 705 for co-requisite remediation.[6] Instructional quality is maintained through annual program reviews that analyze disaggregated data on student learning outcomes (SLOs), resource needs, and equity gaps, feeding into comprehensive five-year evaluations aligned with the college's strategic plan.[6] The Accrediting Commission for Community and Junior Colleges (ACCJC) oversees these processes, confirming compliance with standards for qualified faculty and effective instruction in the 2021 self-study.[6] Course success rates improved to 71.9% overall and 64.3% for online sections by 2018-19, surpassing the 65% benchmark but revealing a persistent 10% gap between delivery modes and equity disparities (e.g., lower rates for African-American students at 64.3%).[6] These metrics, alongside full-time retention of 70% and a 41% graduation rate, serve as proxies for instructional impact, bolstered by Guided Pathways reforms that increased transfer-level English completion to 71% and math to 45% by 2018-19.[41][6]

Athletics

Athletic Programs and Teams

Citrus College maintains intercollegiate athletic programs under the auspices of the California Community College Athletic Association (CCCAA), with teams competing at the junior college level.[47] The Owls nickname applies to all varsity squads, which participate primarily in the Western State Conference (WSC) for non-football sports, while the football team aligns with the Southern California Football Association (SCFA).[47] These programs emphasize competitive participation, eligibility standards per CCCAA rules, and opportunities for student-athletes to transfer to four-year institutions.[48] The college fields eight men's varsity teams: baseball, basketball, cross country, football, golf, soccer, track and field, and water polo.[49] [50] [51] Women's varsity teams total eight: basketball, cross country, golf, soccer, softball, track and field, volleyball, and water polo.[52] [53] Track and field programs typically integrate with cross country, focusing on events such as sprints, distance running, jumps, and throws during the spring season.[54] All teams adhere to CCCAA eligibility requirements, including academic progress standards and full-time enrollment.

Facilities and Achievements

Citrus College's athletic facilities include the 10,000-seat Citrus Stadium, which hosts football, soccer, and track events on an all-weather Field Turf surface equipped with state-of-the-art lighting and an electronic scoreboard.[55] Adjacent is a dedicated football practice field with Field Turf installed in 2005 to reduce injury risk and enable year-round training.[55] The Owls' Nest Gymnasium, a 2,000-seat venue renovated in 2008, serves as the home for basketball and volleyball teams within a distinctive reverse-dome structure that enhances acoustics and atmosphere.[55] The Aquatics Center features an Olympic-sized pool for swimming and water polo programs, with upgrades including a new scoreboard in 2005 and pool replastering in 2012.[55] Baseball is played at a field with dimensions of 325 feet down the lines, 365 feet to left-center, 385 feet to center, and 400 feet to right-center, supported by batting tunnels and multiple pitching mounds; the softball complex, opened in 2009, includes two fields with Field Turf outfields and night-game lighting.[55] Additional resources encompass a 36-station golf driving range with grass tees, mats, and a 2,500-square-foot putting green, plus Olympic-style weight rooms, a 64-station circuit fitness center, and two athletic training rooms for therapy.[55][24] Notable athletic achievements include the softball team's capture of the Western State Conference-South Division championship in April 2025, marking their first title in over a decade.[56] In football, the Owls achieved a historic 48â€“43 upset over the No. 2-ranked Mt. San Antonio College on September 22, 2025, improving their record to 3-1 early in the season.[57] The football program also secured its second consecutive bowl game victory in the 2023â€“2024 season, defeating College of the Canyons 42â€“13.[58] The Athletics Hall of Fame, established to recognize contributors to the programs, inducted nine individuals and one team in April 2025, honoring past student-athletes, coaches, and staff for their impact.[59]

Student Life

Campus Organizations and Activities

Citrus College maintains an active student government through the Associated Students of Citrus College (ASCC), which organizes campus-wide events, allocates funding for student initiatives, and represents student interests in college governance.[60] The ASCC oversees the Inter-Club Council (ICC), facilitating coordination among clubs via regular meetings where representatives discuss budgets, events, and policies.[61] The college supports over 40 student clubs, categorized by academic focus, service, cultural interests, or social engagement, with requirements for clubs to hold regular meetings, maintain membership rosters, and adhere to financial guidelines outlined in administrative procedures.[62] [63] Examples include the Alpha Gamma Sigma honor society, emphasizing excellence and community service; the Architecture Club, which provides resources and field trips related to design professions; and cultural groups hosting heritage celebrations.[64] New clubs can be established by submitting proposals demonstrating unique purpose, alignment with campus goals, and officer structure, subject to approval by the ASCC and administration.[65] Student Life and Leadership Development programs sponsor co-curricular activities to foster personal growth, including leadership workshops, health and wellness seminars, and cultural observances such as Black History Month events in February, Hispanic Heritage Month programming in September-October, and Women's History Month activities in March.[66] Annual events feature voter registration drives, themed social gatherings like silent disco dances, and seasonal displays such as Day of the Dead altars in late October, often coordinated with clubs to enhance engagement.[62] These initiatives aim to promote student retention and satisfaction through structured involvement, with resources like club advisor training and event planning guides available via the Club Resource Library.[67] [63]

Governance and Administration

Leadership Structure

The Citrus Community College District, which operates Citrus College, is governed by a Board of Trustees that exercises ultimate authority, establishing policies, approving educational plans and programs, and overseeing fiscal matters.[78] The board consists of five members elected at-large by voters in the district for four-year terms, plus a student trustee appointed annually to represent student interests in accordance with California Education Code Section 72000.[79] Board meetings occur monthly on Tuesdays at 4:15 p.m., open to the public, with agendas focusing on strategic priorities such as budget allocation and institutional goals.[80] Current officers include Dr. Cheryl Alexander as president, Laura J. Bollinger as vice president, and Dr. Anthony Contreras as clerk/secretary; Steven Bluitt Flowers and Dr. Randa B. Wahbe were installed as trustees on January 21, 2025.[81][82] The Superintendent/President, Greg Schulz, Ed.D., serves as the chief executive officer, reporting directly to the Board of Trustees and responsible for implementing board policies, managing daily operations, and advancing the college's mission through areas like academics, finance, and external relations.[83] Appointed as the eighth Superintendent/President effective August 2021, Schulz chairs the college's Steering Committee, which coordinates shared governance recommendations from constituent groups before forwarding them to the board.[79] Reporting to the Superintendent/President is an executive team of vice presidents overseeing core divisions: Academic Affairs (Dana Hester, Ed.D.), Student Services (Richard F. Rams, Ed.D.), and Finance and Administrative Services (Claudette Dain, C.P.A.).[84] These vice presidents supervise deans, directors, and specialized units, including executive directors for Human Resources (Simone Brown Thunder) and Strategic Communications (L. McPheron), as well as functional areas like institutional research, facilities, and technology under a Chief Technology Officer (Eric Calderon, Ed.D.).[84] Citrus College adheres to a shared governance framework mandated by California law (Education Code Sections 51023 and 70902), integrating input from faculty via the Academic Senate, classified staff, students through the Associated Students of Citrus College, and management teams.[79] This structure emphasizes participatory decision-making across standing committeesâ€”such as those for institutional effectiveness, professional learning, and physical resourcesâ€”chaired by administrators or faculty and meeting monthly to address accreditation, budgeting, and policy compliance.[79] The model prioritizes empirical data and constituent engagement to mitigate risks and align with the board's strategic vision.[79]

Institutional Policies and Operations

Citrus College operates under a framework of Board Policies (BP) and Administrative Procedures (AP) established by the Citrus Community College District Board of Trustees, organized into chapters covering governance, instruction, students, business operations, and human resources.[85] These policies guide institutional decision-making, with administrative procedures providing operational assessment, and special part-time admits for minors meeting California Education Code standards for concurrent enrollment.[86] Students under 18 without a diploma or GED are ineligible unless enrolled to drops, and grading systems including letter grades, pass/no-pass options, and academic renewal for poor prior performance without course repetition.[89] Catalog rights under AP 4100 allow students including academic dishonesty (e.g., cheating, plagiarism), disruption of classes, theft, harassment, drug/alcohol use on campus, and sexual assault, with violations subject to reprimands, suspensions, or expulsion via procedures in AP 5520.[91] Discipline processes ensure due process, including hearings where students may have an advisor or advocate.[92] A drug-free policy bans illegal substances, mandating reporting and potential referral to authorities.[90] Operational functions fall under Finance and Administrative Services, which manages fiscal recording, federal/state compliance, payroll, facilities maintenance, and business transactions via departments like Fiscal Services and the Bursar Office.[93][94] Human resources policies, including BP 7100 on equal employment opportunity and non-discrimination, govern hiring, academic employee evaluations, and classified staff classifications.[95] Shared governance integrates input from committees on planning, budgeting, and policy review to support operations.[79]

Accreditation

Accrediting Bodies and Standards

Citrus College holds institutional accreditation from the Accrediting Commission for Community and Junior Colleges (ACCJC), a regional accrediting body recognized by the U.S. Department of Education and the Council for Higher Education Accreditation (CHEA), which evaluates associate degree-granting institutions in California, Hawaii, and U.S. territories.[3][96] The ACCJC assesses compliance with its Eligibility Requirements, Accreditation Standards, and Commission Policies, which encompass institutional mission, governance, educational programs, student support services, financial resources, and continuous improvement through self-evaluation and planning.[97][98] These standards require Citrus College to demonstrate student learning outcomes, equitable access to education, ethical practices, and data-driven decision-making, with periodic comprehensive evaluations every seven years and interim reports to verify ongoing adherence.[99][100] The college's accreditation status as of April 14, 2025, confirms full compliance without sanctions, enabling eligibility for federal student aid and transferability of credits.[96] In addition to institutional accreditation, specific programs maintain specialized approvals. The Dental Assisting program is accredited by the American Dental Association Commission on Dental Accreditation and licensed by the Dental Board of California, ensuring alignment with professional competencies in clinical skills and patient care.[101] The Associate degree in Construction Management receives accreditation from the American Council for Construction Education, focusing on industry standards for technical knowledge and safety protocols.[102] The English Language Program holds accreditation from the Commission on English Language Program Accreditation (CEA), verifying standards for curriculum design, faculty qualifications, and learner outcomes in language instruction.[102] These program-specific accreditations supplement the institutional framework by addressing discipline-unique benchmarks, such as licensure eligibility and vocational readiness.[101]

Controversies

Free Speech Restrictions and Lawsuits

In 2003, Citrus College maintained speech codes and designated free speech zones that restricted expressive activities to specific, limited areas of campus, prompting a federal lawsuit filed on May 20 by student Chris Stevens, represented by the Foundation for Individual Rights in Education (FIRE).[107] The suit challenged these policies as unconstitutional under the First Amendment, arguing they unduly limited students' rights to free expression in a public forum.[108] On June 5, 2003, the Citrus College Board of Trustees unanimously rescinded most of the speech codes, marking an early victory in FIRE's campaign against such restrictions at public institutions, and the college settled by paying Stevens $24,500 in damages and fees while agreeing to broader free speech policies.[107][109] Despite this resolution, Citrus College reinstated restrictive measures in February 2013, limiting free speech activities to a small courtyard comprising only 1.3% of the campus and enforcing broad anti-harassment policies that critics argued could chill protected speech.[109] These policies came under renewed scrutiny in 2014 when student Vincenzo Sinapi-Riddle was threatened with removal from campus by a college official for approaching another student to discuss and solicit signatures for a political petition outside the designated zone.[110] Sinapi-Riddle filed suit in July 2014 against the Citrus Community College District, challenging the free speech zone as viewpoint-neutral but overly restrictive of spontaneous expression, as well as the anti-harassment policy for its vagueness and overbreadth in potentially prohibiting unwelcome but protected ideas.[110][111] The 2014 lawsuit, supported by FIRE as part of its Stand Up for Speech project targeting unconstitutional codes, alleged violations of the First and Fourteenth Amendments by confining speech to tiny areas and empowering administrators to censor based on content or discomfort.[112] In December 2014, Citrus settled for $110,000 in damages and attorney fees, agreeing to eliminate the restrictive zone by designating most outdoor open spaces as available for expressive activities without prior approval, clarifying the harassment policy to align with constitutional standards, and revising procedures for student groups.[109][112] These changes addressed the core issues of time, place, and manner restrictions that had persisted despite the 2003 reforms, though FIRE noted the college's reversion to limitations as evidence of recurring administrative overreach.[113] No major subsequent lawsuits on free speech have been reported as of 2025, but Citrus maintains a grievance process for alleged expression violations.[114]

Other Administrative and Policy Disputes

In 2022, Citrus College settled a sexual harassment lawsuit filed by seven student employees, who alleged inappropriate conduct by supervisors during campus events and work shifts spanning several years.[115] The settlement prompted the college to implement mandatory anti-harassment training for staff and enhanced reporting protocols under Administrative Procedure 7101.[115] A former dean of Counseling Programs and Services filed a lawsuit in 2023 against Citrus College, claiming sexual harassment by a superior and subsequent retaliation including demotion and hostile work environment after reporting the incidents.[116] The suit, pending in Los Angeles County Superior Court, seeks damages for emotional distress and lost wages, highlighting alleged failures in the college's whistleblower protection policy under AP 7104.[116][117] In December 2024, the women's junior college basketball coach initiated legal action alleging gender-based pay discrimination, as her starting salary was lower than that of the men's team counterpart despite equivalent qualifications and responsibilities.[118] The complaint further claims retaliation through reduced resources and exclusion from decisions after raising the disparity, violating Title IX equity standards in athletics administration.[118] Classified staff expressed dissatisfaction with contract negotiations in July 2015, protesting at a Board of Trustees meeting over stalled progress on salary equity, workload adjustments, and health benefits amid rising living costs in the region.[119] The dispute centered on perceived inequities compared to faculty agreements, leading to public demands for fairer administrative procedures under collective bargaining policies.[119] Faculty in 2016 advocated for reinstating elected department chairs, arguing that the administrative model adopted in prior years reduced shared governance and exacerbated interpersonal conflicts, such as disputes between chairs and deans over resource allocation.[120] This push reflected broader tensions in policy implementation under Board Policy 2000 series on academic senate roles.[120] A 2017 special Board of Trustees meeting addressed an unnamed employee's complaint, culminating in the individual's resignation; details remained confidential, but it involved allegations of administrative misconduct prompting internal review under employee grievance procedures.[121]

extracurricular initiatives.[123] In 2025, English instructor Becky Rudd received the award for her dedication to fostering student writing skills and academic support in a diverse classroom environment.[124] Priscilla Englert, an automotive technology instructor and college alumna, earned the 2024 honor for her committee service, organization of events like the Auto Open House, and efforts to enhance vocational training programs.[58][125] Earlier, in 2023, speech instructor Tasha Van Horn was selected for pioneering distance education methods over 25 years of service, including innovative online interpersonal communication courses that emphasize critical thinking.[126][123] Beyond internal accolades, automotive technology instructor Mariano Rubio was named "College Teacher of the Year" in 2019 by the California Industrial and Technology Education Association for advancing hands-on technical instruction aligned with industry standards.[127] Spanish professor Ana Afzali, Ph.D., has been honored as a Distinguished Faculty member for integrating cultural immersion through study abroad programs, such as those in Barcelona, enhancing language acquisition and global awareness.[128] History instructor Dr. Senya Lubisich, with nearly two decades at the college since 2004, contributed leadership in academic senate roles and historical education, culminating in recognition from community college associations for field advancements.[129][130] These efforts underscore faculty roles in bridging community college education with practical outcomes, though broader research publications remain limited compared to research universities, reflecting the institution's emphasis on applied teaching.[131]

Alumni Accomplishments

Citrus College alumni have achieved recognition in fields such as engineering, public service, and professional sports. The college's Alumni and Friends Association annually honors distinguished graduates for their contributions, emphasizing career success and community impact.[132] Martin A. Gundersen, who attended Citrus College in the late 1950s, advanced to become a leading electrical engineer specializing in plasma physics and high-power electromagnetics. He served as chair of the Department of Electrical Engineering-Electrophysics at the University of Southern California from 1998 to 2003, held visiting positions at UCLA and MIT, and contributed to research on pulsed power systems and laser-induced plasmas. Gundersen received the 2019 Distinguished Alumni Award from Citrus College for his academic and research achievements.[133][134] Sal Medina, a 1995 graduate, was recognized with the 2019 Distinguished Alumni Award for his career in education and public service. After teaching at Monrovia High School, Medina was elected to the Claremont City Council, where he has focused on community development and local governance.[134][135] In professional baseball, Carlos Fisher attended Citrus College from 2001 to 2003 before transferring to Lewis-Clark State College. Drafted by the Cincinnati Reds in the 11th round of the 2005 MLB Draft, Fisher debuted in the major leagues in 2009, appearing in 53 games over parts of four seasons with the Reds and Detroit Tigers, posting a career ERA of 4.36.[136][137] Football alumni have also reached the professional level. Lionel Manuel played at Citrus College in 1980 prior to transferring to the University of the Pacific, where he earned All-Pacific Coast Conference honors as a wide receiver. Selected by the New York Giants in the seventh round of the 1984 NFL Draft, Manuel played seven seasons, accumulating 235 receptions for 3,601 yards and 18 touchdowns.[138][139] Dane Cruikshank competed for the Citrus Owls football team in 2013 and 2014, initially as a wide receiver before transitioning to defensive back, recording 75 tackles and earning junior college All-American recognition. He continued at the University of Arizona and was drafted by the Tennessee Titans in the fifth round of the 2018 NFL Draft, playing in 51 games across five seasons with the Titans and Chicago Bears.[140][141]
