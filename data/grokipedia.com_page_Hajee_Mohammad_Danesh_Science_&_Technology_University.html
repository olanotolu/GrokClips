Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University


Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University (HSTU) is a public research university in Dinajpur, Bangladesh, focused on science, technology, agriculture, and engineering disciplines.[1] Located 13 kilometers north of Dinajpur town along the highway to Dhaka, it serves as the first science and technology university in the northern region of the country.[2] Originally founded in 1979 as an Agricultural Extension Training Institute to provide diploma-level agricultural education, the institution evolved into Hajee Mohammad Danesh Agricultural College in 1988, offering degree programs, before being upgraded to full university status with its foundation stone laid on 11 September 1999.[1] The university's formal establishment was enacted through legislation passed on 8 July 2001 and gazetted on 8 April 2002, with initial student admissions occurring in the 1999-2000 session.[2] It maintains a policy of no session delays, ensuring timely academic progression, and comprises nine faculties across 45 departments, including agriculture, computer science and engineering, fisheries, veterinary sciences, and postgraduate studies.[1] HSTU enrolls approximately 11,203 students, among them 195 international students from several countries pursuing undergraduate and postgraduate degrees.[2] The university emphasizes research and training to produce skilled professionals, with programs leading to Bachelor of Science degrees, as well as MS and PhD qualifications through its dedicated postgraduate faculty.[1] Notable research efforts include advancements in sustainable agriculture, such as the development of high-yield, poison-free brinjal cultivation techniques achieving three to four times greater output without chemical inputs.[3]

History

Origins and Pre-University Phase (1979â€“1999)

The institution that would become Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University originated in 1979 as the Agricultural Extension Training Institute (AETI) in Dinajpur, Bangladesh, established by the government to provide vocational training in agriculture amid post-independence efforts to bolster rural development and food security.[1] AETI offered a three-year diploma program in agriculture, focusing on extension services, crop production, and basic agronomic practices to train mid-level agricultural workers for deployment in northern Bangladesh's agrarian economy.[1] This phase emphasized practical, hands-on education rather than advanced research, aligning with national priorities for agricultural extension under the Ministry of Agriculture.[1] In 1988, AETI was upgraded to Hajee Mohammad Danesh Agricultural College, named after the prominent Bengali revolutionary and peasant leader Hajee Mohammad Danesh, who advocated for farmers' rights during the language movement and independence struggles.[1] This elevation transformed it into a degree-granting institution affiliated with Bangladesh Agricultural University in Mymensingh, enabling the awarding of bachelor's degrees in agricultural sciences and expanding enrollment to support higher education in the underserved northern region.[1] The college introduced formal undergraduate curricula in subjects such as agronomy, soil science, and animal husbandry, with initial infrastructure developments including classrooms and laboratories funded through government allocations.[1] By the late 1990s, it had graduated several cohorts, contributing to local agricultural expertise but facing capacity constraints that prompted calls for further institutional growth.[1] Throughout the 1990s, the college operated under the Ministry of Agriculture, maintaining its focus on agricultural education while adapting to evolving national needs, such as integrating modern farming techniques amid population pressures on arable land.[1] Enrollment grew modestly, with emphasis on regional accessibility for students from Rangpur Division, though resources remained limited compared to central institutions.[1] This pre-university era laid the groundwork for science and technology emphasis by building a foundation in applied sciences, culminating in governmental recognition of the need for a dedicated university to foster research and multidisciplinary programs in the area.[1]

Establishment as a University (1999â€“2010)

In February 1999, the Prime Minister of Bangladesh announced the upgrade of Hajee Mohammad Danesh Agricultural College to a full-fledged science and technology university, marking the formal initiation of its transformation into Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University (HSTU).[1] This decision aimed to expand higher education opportunities in the northern region, building on the institution's prior focus on agricultural training. On September 6, 1999, administrative control and property were transferred from the Ministry of Agriculture to the Ministry of Education, enabling the shift to university-level operations.[1] The university's official inauguration occurred on September 11, 1999, when the Prime Minister laid the foundation stone, and Professor Asnisur Rahman was appointed as Project Director to oversee initial development.[1] The first batch of undergraduate students was admitted in the 1999-2000 academic session, primarily under the newly established Faculty of Agriculture, which served as the foundational academic unit.[1] Academic activities commenced with a limited number of departments, emphasizing agricultural sciences, as the institution transitioned from diploma and degree programs to bachelor's-level offerings.[1] Legal formalization followed in 2001, with the HSTU Act passed by the Jatio Sangsad (National Parliament) on July 8, granting statutory recognition and autonomy.[1] A gazette notification confirming the university's establishment was issued on April 8, 2002, solidifying its operational framework.[1] During the early 2000s, infrastructure development accelerated, including the construction of basic academic buildings and administrative facilities to accommodate growing enrollment. By 2010, HSTU had expanded significantly from its initial agricultural focus, incorporating additional faculties to reach a total of nine, alongside 45 departments covering disciplines such as veterinary sciences, fisheries, business studies, and engineering.[1] Postgraduate programs leading to MS and PhD degrees were introduced through a dedicated Faculty of Postgraduate Studies, while an Institute of Research and Training was established to support advanced scholarly work.[1] This period laid the groundwork for HSTU's role as the first science and technology university in northern Bangladesh, with steady growth in student intake and faculty recruitment despite resource constraints typical of nascent public institutions.[1]

Since 2010, Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University has expanded its physical infrastructure to accommodate growing academic demands, including the construction of additional academic buildings, such as the third academic building, alongside the Dr. M. A. Wazed Building and administrative facilities.[4][5] These developments have supported the addition of departments, bringing the total to 45 across nine faculties, encompassing fields like engineering, fisheries, and social sciences.[6][7] Student enrollment has grown substantially, reaching nearly 11,000 by 2025, including 195 international students pursuing undergraduate and postgraduate degrees.[8][9] This expansion reflects increased capacity in residential halls and support facilities, with ongoing projects like the installation of elevators in key buildings completed by 2022 to enhance accessibility.[5] Modernization initiatives have focused on digital infrastructure, including the establishment of a buried fiber optic backbone and high-speed local area network, funded by the World Bank and the Government of Bangladesh.[10] The university's Institute of Research and Training has supported applied research and professional development, while systems like hall management software were implemented by 2024 to streamline operations.[11] In 2025, authorities renamed seven buildings, including academic structures, to align with national commemorative themes, signaling continued administrative evolution.[12]

Institutional Structure and Policies

Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University (HSTU) operates under the framework established by the Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University Act, 2001, enacted on July 8, 2001, which defines its authorities, powers, and operational principles as a public institution focused on science and technology education.[19] The Act delineates a hierarchical governance structure typical of Bangladeshi public universities, comprising the Syndicate as the supreme executive body responsible for administrative, financial, and regulatory decisions; the Senate (or Academic Council) for overseeing academic policies, curricula, and faculty matters; and subordinate bodies including finance committees and proctorial units for student discipline and campus security.[20] The Chancellor, the President of Bangladesh (currently Mohammed Shahabuddin), holds ceremonial oversight and appoints key officers.[21] Executive administration is led by the Vice-Chancellor, Prof. Dr. Mohammed Enamullah, who serves as the chief academic and administrative officer, managing day-to-day operations, faculty appointments, and policy implementation, with support from the Pro-Vice-Chancellor, Prof. Dr. Md. Shafiqul Islam Sikdar, who assists in academic coordination and deputizes as needed.[21] Faculties (nine in total) are headed by deans, each overseeing multiple departments (45 overall), where department heads handle curriculum delivery, research coordination, and student advising under faculty-level policies. Supporting roles include the Treasurer (Prof. Dr. Md. Zahangir Kabir) for financial governance and the Registrar (Prof. Dr. Md. Abu Hasan, additional charge) for record-keeping and compliance.[21] The Internal Quality Assurance Cell (IQAC) monitors institutional efficacy, reviewing policies on teaching, research, and governance to align with national standards set by the University Grants Commission of Bangladesh.[22] Key policies emphasize merit-based access and academic rigor. Admission to undergraduate programs requires a minimum combined GPA of 7.00 (or higher for specific units like 8.00 in science faculties) in SSC and HSC equivalents, followed by a centralized entrance examination administered annually, with reservations for quotas including freedom fighters' descendants (10%) and tribal students (2%).[23] [24] Academic regulations mandate continuous assessment, with progression requiring a minimum CGPA of 2.25 for undergraduates and compliance with credit-hour systems outlined in faculty-specific ordinances; postgraduate policies, such as those for M.S. and Ph.D. degrees, stipulate thesis defenses and coursework minima.[25] [26] Disciplinary policies, codified in the Discipline and Appeal Rules, 2004, empower proctors to investigate misconduct like academic dishonesty or campus disruptions, with appeals escalating to the Syndicate; violations can result in rustication or expulsion.[27] Financial policies include subsidized tuition for locals (approximately BDT 20,000â€“50,000 annually per program) and scholarships for high achievers, funded via government allocations and endowments.[28]

Campus and Infrastructure

Location and Layout

Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University is located in Basherhat, Dinajpur Sadar Upazila, Dinajpur District, Rangpur Division, Bangladesh, at postal code 5200.[28] The campus lies approximately 13 kilometers north of Dinajpur town, adjacent to the inter-district highway connecting Dinajpur to other northern regions, in a rural setting that offers a quiet environment away from urban distractions.[29] [9] The university's permanent campus covers 85 acres, supporting expansion of academic and residential facilities since its establishment.[30] Key infrastructure includes administrative buildings, academic blocks for nine faculties, a central library, multiple student dormitories, and recreational areas, arranged in a decentralized layout to accommodate growing student enrollment of around 11,000.[30] Access to the campus is facilitated through gated entrances, with internal roads connecting major structures for pedestrian and vehicular movement.[28]

Academic and Support Facilities

The Central Library at Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University occupies a multi-storied building equipped with sections for acquisition, circulation, processing, and reference services.[31] It houses 23,105 printed books, 800 theses, 300 audio-visual materials, and 89 periodicals, including national and international journals.[31] Users access over 40,000 e-journals and 7,053 e-books through the University Grants Commission Digital Library.[31] Borrowing privileges allow teachers to take five books for 30 days and students or researchers three books for 20 days.[31] A cyber center within the library provides internet access for a nominal fee, supporting research and learning needs.[31] Departmental laboratories form a core component of academic facilities, particularly in science and engineering disciplines.[32] For instance, the Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering maintains labs that facilitate hands-on learning and research activities for students.[32] These facilities enable practical training aligned with the university's focus on technology and applied sciences. The university's medical center operates 24 hours a day, seven days a week, staffed by five doctors and offering ambulance services along with 10-bed inpatient accommodation.[33] It provides both indoor and outdoor treatment to address student and staff health needs.[33]

Residential Halls and Student Housing


Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University maintains nine residential halls, comprising five for male students and four for female students, to house a portion of its approximately 11,000 enrolled students.[30] These facilities support on-campus living for undergraduates and postgraduates, though exact capacities remain unspecified in official records, and not all students receive accommodation due to demand exceeding supply in public universities.[30] [34] Each hall operates under a provost, referred to as the Hall Super, who is supported by house tutors, assistant house tutors, a hall president, and assistant presidents elected from residents.[35] Assignment to halls occurs based on students' faculty admissions, prioritizing merit and availability.[35] In June 2025, the university administration renamed six halls to reflect historical or national figures, including the redesignation of Sheikh Russel Hall as Shaheed Nur Hossain Hall and Sheikh Fazilatunnesa Mujib Hall as Begum Rokeya Hall.[36] Facilities in the halls include basic amenities for communal living, with management enforcing rules on residency and conduct.[35] International students access a dedicated residential hall equipped with dining services, Wi-Fi connectivity, a TV room, and a playground.[37] Incidents of unrest, such as violence during student movements, have prompted disciplinary actions, including restrictions on hall access for involved residents.[38]

Undergraduate Degrees

Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University (HSTU) provides 23 undergraduate degree programs distributed across nine faculties, emphasizing science, technology, agriculture, engineering, and related disciplines. These programs are predominantly four-year honors degrees comprising eight semesters, with total credit hours typically ranging from 150 to 183, though the Doctor of Veterinary Medicine extends to five years (ten semesters, including internship) with 197 credits. Instruction is conducted in English, and students must complete a minimum grade point average for graduation; admission is granted through a centralized entrance examination for the January-June semester, with each semester lasting 21 weeks followed by opportunities for grade improvement.[45] Faculty of Agriculture offers the B.Sc. in Agriculture (Hons.), a four-year program requiring 183 credit hours focused on agronomic practices, crop production, and soil management.[45] Faculty of Computer Science and Engineering includes three engineering degrees: B.Sc. (Engineering) in Computer Science and Engineering (155.5 credits), Electrical and Electronic Engineering (159.5 credits), and Electronics and Communication Engineering (155.75 credits), all spanning four years and covering core topics in programming, circuits, and telecommunications.[45] Faculty of Fisheries provides the B.Sc. in Fisheries (Hons.), a four-year curriculum of 163 credit hours emphasizing aquaculture, marine biology, and resource management.[45] Faculty of Veterinary and Animal Science awards the Doctor of Veterinary Medicine (DVM), a five-year professional degree totaling 197 credit hours, including clinical training in animal health, pathology, and surgery.[45] Faculty of Business Studies delivers four-year BBA programs in Accounting and Information Systems, Management, Marketing, and Finance, designed to build skills in financial analysis, organizational leadership, consumer behavior, and economic policy.[45] Faculty of Engineering encompasses B.Sc. degrees in Food and Process Engineering (157 credits), Agricultural Engineering, Civil Engineering, and Mechanical Engineering (all four years), alongside a Bachelor of Architecture program; these integrate practical training in processing technologies, structural design, machinery, and sustainable infrastructure.[45] Faculty of Science offers four-year B.Sc. (Hons.) degrees in Chemistry, Physics, Mathematics, and Statistics, fostering foundational research in chemical reactions, physical principles, quantitative modeling, and data analysis.[45] Faculty of Social Science & Humanities includes four-year honors programs such as B.S.S. (Hons.) in Sociology, Economics, and Development Studies, and B.A. (Hons.) in English (150 credits), addressing societal structures, economic theories, policy impacts, and linguistic proficiency.[45]

Research and Achievements

Research Centers and Output

The Institute of Research and Training (IRT), established in 2005, coordinates research projects at Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University to develop sustainable technologies, foster economic growth in northern Bangladesh, and enhance education and training in science and technology fields.[49] It oversees internally funded initiatives, primarily from the University Grants Commission, emphasizing applied research in agriculture, engineering, and related disciplines.[50] As of 2025, IRT manages 42 ongoing projects across departments including Horticulture, Soil Science, and Physiology and Pharmacology.[50] Notable examples include "Effect of Boron on Growth, Yield and Quality of Bitter Gourd" led by Dr. Bidhan Chandra Halder in Horticulture, "Improvement of Yield and Nutritional Quality of Cucumber" by Dr. Md. Anis Khan in Horticulture, and "Effect of Peat Soil Application on Rice Cultivation" by Dr. Md. Shahadadat Hossain Khan in Soil Science. Recent developments encompass project acceptances for the 2024-2025 cycle (announced December 12, 2024) and proposal calls for 2025-2026 (issued July 7, 2025, with extensions to July 23, 2025).[49] HSTU disseminates research through the Journal of Science and Technology, a biannual, peer-reviewed publication launched to cover agricultural sciences, engineering, business studies, basic sciences, and humanities.[51] It accepts original articles, review papers, and technical notes, with each submission reviewed by at least two independent referees, and holds ISSN 1994-0386 (print) and 2959-3239 (online). No impact factor is reported, reflecting its regional focus amid limited international indexing.[51] External research output, tracked in databases like Web of Science, totals around 82 indexed publications as of 2024 analyses, predominantly articles in multidisciplinary sciences (majority share) and food science technology (18.29% of output).[52] Approximately 52 of these are open-access, with evidence of international collaborations boosting visibility, though overall volume remains modest compared to larger institutions, aligning with HSTU's emphasis on regional agricultural and technological applications rather than high-volume basic research. Scimago Institutions Rankings places HSTU at 4539th globally in 2025, with a 69th percentile in research metrics.[53]

abuse, queuing humiliations, and senior-led criticisms during freshman orientations.[72] Broader patterns of peer aggression, including physical and psychological bullying, contribute to mental health strains and reduced academic focus, as documented in surveys of Bangladeshi university students where such incidents erode trust and productivity.[73] Political activism exacerbates these issues, with partisan clashesâ€”such as those on July 16, 2024, between student demonstrators protesting quota reforms and Bangladesh Chhatra League affiliatesâ€”leading to violence that disrupted campus operations and prompted the identification of 79 students for disciplinary action.[38] [74] In August 2024, HSTU's administration responded by prohibiting all political activities for students and faculty, aiming to curb recurring instability tied to national party affiliations that have historically fueled chases, counter-chases, and broader disruptions in Bangladeshi public universities since the 1990s.[75] [76] These interventions reflect causal links between unchecked student politics and eroded academic environments, though enforcement remains tested amid ongoing code-of-conduct Controversies and Criticisms

Political Violence and Student Politics

Student politics at Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science and Technology University (HSTU) has historically been influenced by national political parties, particularly through affiliated student organizations such as the Bangladesh Chhatra League (BCL), the student wing of the Awami League, which has been linked to multiple violent incidents on campus.[74][78] These dynamics mirror broader patterns of campus violence in Bangladeshi public universities since the 1990s, where partisan rivalries often escalate into physical confrontations, disrupting academic activities and contributing to socio-economic costs like delayed classes and enrollment declines.[79] A notable early incident occurred on April 2015, when two HSTU students died during a factional clash within the BCL, prompting police to file a case four days later against unidentified individuals involved in the violence.[78] Such intra-party disputes highlight how student wings prioritize political loyalty over campus harmony, with weapons like sticks and sharp instruments commonly used, as reported in similar university conflicts across Bangladesh.[80] Violence resurfaced prominently during the 2024 anti-discrimination student movement, which evolved into nationwide protests against job quotas and government policies. On July 16, 2024, BCL activists at HSTU clashed with demonstrators, resulting in chases, counter-chases, and attacks that injured protesters and damaged campus property.[74] In response, university authorities identified 79 BCL members as perpetrators, recommending punishments including lifetime expulsion, certificate cancellations, and legal proceedings for their roles in the unrest; an additional 38 students faced warnings or fines.[38] This event underscored tensions between ruling party student cadres and reform-seeking protesters, contributing to the broader July uprising that led to the fall of the Awami League government.[81] Following the 2024 upheaval, HSTU administration imposed a campus-wide ban on all political activities for both students and teachers in August 2024, aiming to curb recurring violence and restore academic focus amid demands from students for apolitical environments or regulated union elections.[75] Despite these measures, isolated breaches persisted, such as the December 2024 penalization of nine students for code violations linked to unauthorized activities, and ongoing calls for genuine student unions free from partisan interference.[77][82] The national ban on BCL in October 2024 further signaled efforts to dismantle entrenched violent student politics, though enforcement challenges remain in institutions like HSTU.[80]

Governance Reforms and Institutional Changes

In the aftermath of the 2024 student-led uprising that ousted the Awami League government, Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science and Technology University (HSTU) implemented several administrative measures aimed at reducing political influence on campus. On August 10, 2024, Vice-Chancellor Professor M. Kamruzzaman resigned amid widespread protests against university leadership perceived as aligned with the former regime, part of a national wave where multiple public university vice-chancellors stepped down.[83] Professor Mohammed Enamullah was appointed as the new Vice-Chancellor on October 21, 2024, signaling a shift toward leadership less tied to prior political structures.[18] On August 11, 2024, HSTU authorities issued a ban prohibiting teachers and students from engaging in political activities, including membership in student wings of political parties, to foster a depoliticized academic environment amid ongoing campus tensions.[75] This policy followed violent clashes during the anti-quota protests in July 2024, where Bangladesh Chhatra League (BCL) activists, affiliated with the Awami League, were implicated in attacks on demonstrators.[38] Institutional adjustments included renaming seven buildings on June 19, 2025, to remove associations with figures linked to the ousted government. Bangabandhu Sheikh Mujibur Rahman Hall became Bijoy 24 Hall, Sheikh Russel Hall was renamed Shaheed Nur Hossain Hall, and Sheikh Fazilatunnesa Mujib Hall was changed to Begum Rokeya Hall, reflecting efforts to align nomenclature with non-partisan or revolutionary themes.[12][36] In 2025, HSTU introduced a PhD Fellowship Policy specifying eligibility criteria for supervisors, limiting roles to associate professors or higher with PhDs, as part of broader efforts to standardize postgraduate oversight and research governance.[84] These changes occur within the university's framework established by its 2001 Act, which vests authority in the Syndicate and Senate, though no structural overhauls to these bodies have been documented.[28]

Sultan Mahmud, who obtained his B.Sc. in Agricultural Engineering from Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science & Technology University in 2016 while receiving Dean's List scholarships from the Faculty of Engineering, advanced his studies with an M.Sc. in Agriculture (Engineering) from Dalhousie University in Canada in 2019 and a Ph.D. in Agricultural and Biological Engineering from Pennsylvania State University in 2022.[85] He earned the Outstanding Dissertation Award from Penn State for his doctoral work, along with the Boyd-Scott Graduate Research Award (second place, 2022) and the Paul Hand Award.[85] Currently serving as Assistant Professor of Precision Crop Protection at the University of Georgia since August 2023, Mahmud's research applies artificial intelligence, robotics, and computer vision to plant disease management, reflecting a trajectory from HSTU to international academic leadership.[85] Alumni in fields like electrical and electronic engineering have secured research and employment opportunities abroad, including in the United States, Australia, Germany, and Japan, contributing to global technological advancements.[32] Graduates from computer science and engineering programs have demonstrated excellence in national and international competitions and professional roles, underscoring the university's role in fostering technical expertise.[40] In architecture, alumni have attained notable professional successes, applying their training to practical innovations in design and construction.[86] These outcomes highlight HSTU's emerging impact on alumni career trajectories, particularly in science, technology, and engineering sectors, though the institution's relatively recent establishment limits the breadth of long-term high-profile examples.

Faculty Contributions

Faculty members at Hajee Mohammad Danesh Science and Technology University (HSTU) primarily contribute to applied research in agriculture, engineering, and basic sciences, focusing on regional challenges such as crop improvement, precision farming, and data analytics. The university's faculty have collectively authored 777 publications involving 846 researchers, with emphasis on biology and computer science topics including plant genetics, machine learning, and biostatistics.[87] [88] In agriculture-related fields, professors lead projects on germplasm evaluation and characterization, such as okra breeding for yield enhancement, conducted through the Institute of Research and Training (IRT).[50] Horticulture faculty, including Dr. Md. Hassanur Rahman, advance studies in crop physiology and environmental stress responses, contributing to sustainable farming practices in northern Bangladesh.[89] Similarly, in genetics and animal breeding, Dr. Md. Rashedul Islam and Dr. Abdul Gaffar Miah publish on livestock genomics and breeding efficiency, supporting local food security initiatives.[90] [91] Engineering departments feature contributions in computational and communication technologies. Faculty in Computer Science and Engineering, such as Dr. Md. Abdulla Al Mamun, develop wearable platforms integrating AI algorithms for health monitoring and precision agriculture.[10] [40] In Electronics and Communication Engineering, Dr. Tangina Sultana researches signal processing and embedded systems, with applications in IoT for rural development.[92] Food Engineering faculty explore microbiological and nutritional engineering, expanding knowledge on processing techniques for regional staples.[93] Basic sciences faculty emphasize theoretical and applied modeling. In statistics, researchers address big data and epidemiology, aiding public health analytics.[88] Dr. Md. Ashad Alam in mathematics focuses on robust statistics and topological data analysis for handling noisy datasets.[94] Vice-Chancellor Professor Mohammed Enamullah, from the Chemistry department, oversees interdisciplinary efforts linking chemical analysis to agricultural innovation.[95] These outputs, often published in peer-reviewed journals, prioritize empirical validation over theoretical abstraction, though funding constraints limit international collaboration scale.[96]
