Montana House of Representatives

The Montana House of Representatives is the lower chamber of the bicameral Montana State Legislature, consisting of 100 members elected from single-member districts for two-year terms, with all seats up for election in even-numbered years.[1][2] As the more numerous body compared to the 50-member Senate, the House plays a central role in initiating revenue bills and sharing equal legislative authority to debate, amend, and pass statutes on matters ranging from taxation and appropriations to natural resource management and criminal justice, subject to gubernatorial assent or veto override by a two-thirds majority in each chamber. Legislators face term limits enacted via voter initiative in 1992, restricting service to no more than eight years total in the House, a measure aimed at preventing entrenched incumbency and promoting turnover, though empirical assessments of its effects on policy quality remain mixed due to challenges in isolating causal impacts from broader electoral dynamics.[3] Following the 2024 elections, Republicans hold a narrowed majority of 58 seats to 42 held by Democrats, reflecting voter responses to prior session dynamics including internal quorum disruptions by the GOP caucus that delayed proceedings and highlighted factional divisions over fiscal priorities.[4][5] The chamber's operations emphasize part-time citizen-legislators, convening primarily in odd-numbered years for sessions capped at 90 days absent supermajority extension, underscoring Montana's tradition of limited government intervention in a state defined by vast rural expanses and resource-dependent economies.[6]

Composition and Elections

Districting and Representation

The Montana House of Representatives comprises 100 members, each elected from a single-member district covering a portion of the state's population.[7] These districts are designed to ensure roughly equal representation, with boundaries drawn to approximate one representative per 10,842 residents based on the 2020 United States Census population of 1,084,225.[8] House districts are nested within the 50 state senate districts, such that each senate district encompasses two contiguous House districts, facilitating coordinated legislative representation across the bicameral structure.[9] District boundaries are redrawn decennially following the federal census by the independent Montana Districting and Apportionment Commission, a process enshrined in the state constitution via a 1972 voter-approved amendment to prevent partisan legislative control over maps.[10] The five-member commission consists of two appointees each from the House and Senate majority and minority leaders, plus a non-voting chair selected by the Montana Supreme Court to break ties and ensure procedural neutrality.[11] This structure, upheld against challenges asserting undue judicial influence, prioritizes empirical population equality over political considerations, with statutory criteria mandating compact and contiguous districts that minimize divisions of counties, municipalities, and communities of interest while adhering to federal Voting Rights Act requirements.[12] Unlike states with legislature-led redistricting, Montana's commission model has empirically reduced opportunities for gerrymandering, as evidenced by judicial reviews confirming compliance with one-person, one-vote standards in prior cycles.[13] After the 2020 census, the commission convened in 2021, incorporating public input through hearings and draft proposals before adopting final legislative maps on February 22, 2023, effective for elections from 2024 through 2032.[14] These maps reflect a maximum population deviation of less than 1% across House districts, preserving rural-urban balances and avoiding excessive fragmentation of tribal lands or geographic features.[15] Legal challenges, including suits alleging insufficient compactness in urban areas, were resolved by the state Supreme Court, affirming the maps' adherence to constitutional mandates without evidence of intentional partisan advantage.[16] Representation thus remains tied to verifiable demographic data, enabling constituents to hold members accountable for district-specific issues like resource allocation and local policy impacts.[17]

Member Qualifications and Terms

To serve as a member of the Montana House of Representatives, a candidate must be at least 21 years of age on the day of the general election.[18] Candidates must also qualify as an elector under Montana law, which requires United States citizenship, attainment of age 18, residency in the state for at least 30 days preceding the election, and registration as a voter. Additional residency requirements mandate that candidates reside in Montana for at least one year immediately preceding the general election and, for House districts, reside in the county containing the district (or the district itself if it lies within one county) for at least six months prior to the election.[19] The Montana House itself judges the elections and qualifications of its members, with authority to determine eligibility or delegate contested cases to the courts by statute.[20] Members of the House serve two-year terms, with all 100 seats elected in even-numbered years alongside gubernatorial and presidential elections.[21] Montana imposes lifetime term limits on legislators via Constitutional Initiative 64, approved by voters in 1992; no individual may serve more than eight years total in the House of Representatives during their lifetime, though service in the Senate does not count against this limit and vice versa.[22] These limits apply prospectively and do not retroactively disqualify prior service accumulated before the initiative's effective date in 1993.[22] Vacancies occurring during a term are filled by special election or gubernatorial appointment followed by election, depending on the time remaining, but do not reset term limit accumulations.[18]

Election Procedures and Voter Influence

Members of the Montana House of Representatives are elected to two-year terms, with all 100 seats contested in even-numbered years during federal and state general elections held on the first Tuesday after the first Monday in November. [23] Elections occur within 100 single-member districts apportioned based on population from the decennial census, with redistricting handled by an independent commission established under the Montana Constitution to redraw boundaries every ten years following federal requirements.[15] Candidates for House seats must be U.S. citizens, Montana residents for at least one year preceding the general election, and residents of their district at the time of filing; they file declarations of candidacy with the Secretary of State by deadlines typically in mid-March, accompanied by filing fees or petitions with sufficient signatures.[24] [25] Partisan primaries, conducted in June of even-numbered years, nominate major-party candidates using a plurality vote system where voters select one party's ballot at the polling place without prior affiliation; independent candidates qualify via petition and appear directly on the general ballot.[26] [23] The primary often determines outcomes in Montana's districts, as one party dominates many areas, reducing general election competitiveness.[27] Voter eligibility requires U.S. citizenship, being at least 18 years old by Election Day, and residency in Montana for 30 days prior, with registration mandatory but permitting same-day enrollment at polling places using provisional ballots verified post-election.[28] [29] Registration occurs online via VoteMT.gov, by mail, or in person at county election offices, with no partisan affiliation required; updates for address changes or reactivation from inactive status are straightforward but must comply with recent 2025 legislative tightenings on verification processes to address inactive voter lists.[30] [31] [32] Voting options enhance accessibility and influence: in-person voting on Election Day, in-person absentee voting at county offices up to two days prior without excuse, and mail absentee ballots requested up to Election Day (postmarked by noon); no photo ID is mandated for most voters, though affidavits suffice for verification.[33] [34] Voter turnout in legislative races averages around 50-60% in generals but lower in primaries, amplifying the sway of motivated partisans; same-day registration and flexible absentee rules enable broader participation, particularly in rural districts where geographic isolation might otherwise suppress votes, though primary dominance by established parties limits independent influence.[33] [34]

Leadership and Internal Organization

Speaker of the House and Officers

The Speaker of the Montana House of Representatives serves as the presiding officer, responsible for maintaining order, decorum, and the administration of House proceedings, including the interpretation and enforcement of House rules.[35] The Speaker is elected by a majority vote of House members at the organizational session following general elections, typically held in January of odd-numbered years when the biennial legislative session convenes.[36] This position wields significant influence over the legislative agenda, including appointing members to committees, assigning bills, and recognizing members for debate.[35] As of the 69th Montana Legislative Session beginning January 6, 2025, Representative Brandon Ler (Republican, District 36, Savage) holds the office of Speaker, having been selected by the House Republican caucus on November 12, 2024, amid a 58-42 Republican majority following the 2024 elections.[37][38] Ler, a rancher and former House member since 2021, succeeded Matt Regier, emphasizing fiscal conservatism and rural priorities in his leadership role.[39] The Speaker Pro Tempore, elected similarly by majority vote, assumes presiding duties in the Speaker's absence and assists in procedural matters.[35] For the 2025 session, Representative Katie Zolnikov (Republican, District 45, Billings) serves in this role, having presided temporarily during Ler's absences, such as in February 2025.[37][40] Party leaders include the Majority Leader, who coordinates the majority caucus's strategy and legislative priorities, and the Minority Leader, who represents the minority party's interests and manages its floor activities.[41] In 2025, Steve Fitzpatrick (Republican, District 32, Great Falls) is Majority Leader, while Katie Sullivan (Democrat, District 91, Missoula) is Minority Leader.[37] Non-partisan officers support administrative functions: the Chief Clerk, Carolyn Tschida, oversees bill drafting, records, and session documentation from the State Capitol in Helena.[42] The Sergeant at Arms, Brad Murfitt, enforces security, maintains physical order, and assists with facilities management.[42] These staff positions are appointed per House rules and serve across sessions to ensure continuity.[43]

Committees and Legislative Procedures

The Montana House of Representatives establishes standing committees at the outset of each legislative session to review and recommend action on bills within specific subject areas, with approximately 16 such committees organized into three classes based on meeting frequency to manage workload efficiently. Class one committees, which convene Monday through Friday, include Appropriations, Judiciary, and State Administration; class two committees meet Monday, Wednesday, and Friday and encompass Agriculture, Business and Labor, and Natural Resources; class three committees assemble Tuesday and Thursday for topics like Education, Energy, Technology, and Federal Relations, and Human Services.[44] The Speaker appoints committee members proportionally reflecting the House's partisan composition and selects chairs from the majority party, with rules requiring a majority quorum for action and authorizing proxy voting under limited circumstances for members engaged in official duties.[45] Select or special committees may form for targeted issues, while joint committees with the Senate address appropriations subcommittees or rules coordination, ensuring bicameral alignment on fiscal and procedural matters.[46] All committee meetings remain open to the public, with advance notice required, facilitating citizen testimony during hearings though chairs maintain order and decorum.[47] Legislative procedures in the House follow a structured bicameral process governed primarily by the Montana Constitution, House and Joint Rules adopted via resolution at session start (such as House Resolution 1), statutes, and Mason's Manual of Legislative Procedure for parliamentary guidance.[47] Bills originate from legislator requests to the nonpartisan Legislative Services Division for drafting, limited to five per member in regular sessions excluding resolutions and memorials, then file with the Chief Clerk for introduction.[48] Upon first reading, the bill's title announces its subject, it prints on white paper, and the Speaker refers it to a standing committee aligned with its content, where it undergoes public hearing, debate, potential amendments, and a vote yielding recommendations like "do pass," "do pass as amended," or "do not pass."[49] Favorable committee reports advance the bill to the second-reading calendar (printed on yellow paper) for debate in the Committee of the Whole, allowing amendments by majority vote; unfavorable reports table the bill unless a motion to reconsider garners sufficient support.[49] Following second reading approval, the engrossed bill (printed on blue paper) proceeds to third reading for a final yes/no roll-call vote without amendments, requiring a simple majority for passage before transmittal to the Senate for concurrence or amendment resolution via conference committee.[49] House Rules permit suspension by two-thirds vote for expedited action, prohibit seconding speeches, and vest the Speaker with authority to interpret rules subject to appeal by 15 members to the Rules Committee or full House.[50] Revenue bills must originate in the House per constitutional mandate, and extraordinary majorities apply for veto overrides (two-thirds) or constitutional amendments (two-thirds in each house, followed by voter ratification).[47] This framework ensures deliberate review, with committees serving as gatekeepers to filter legislation based on merit and fiscal impact before floor consideration.[46]

Powers, Functions, and Sessions

Constitutional Authority and Legislative Role

The legislative power of the State of Montana is vested exclusively in the bicameral Legislature, comprising the Senate and the House of Representatives, as established by Article V, Section 1 of the Montana Constitution.[20] This authority empowers the House to enact statutes on all matters of state policy, including taxation, appropriations, criminal justice, education, and natural resources management, subject to bicameral concurrence and executive veto override procedures.[20] The Constitution delineates the House's role within this framework, ensuring it operates as a co-equal chamber with the Senate while holding distinct procedural privileges that reflect its representation of population-based districts.[20] A key prerogative of the House is its origination of revenue-raising legislation, mandated by Article V, Section 6, which states that "all bills for raising revenue must originate in the house, but the senate may propose or concur in amendments as on other bills."[20] This provision underscores the House's foundational role in fiscal policy, requiring all tax increases, fees, and funding mechanisms to begin there before Senate review, thereby channeling budgetary initiatives through the chamber more directly accountable to frequent popular elections.[20] In practice, this extends to appropriations bills, positioning the House as the primary initiator of the state's biennial budget process.[51] The House also holds the sole authority to impeach state officers, including the governor, justices, and other civil officers, under Article V, Section 14, with the Senate conducting trials as specified in Section 15.[20] Impeachment requires a majority vote in the House for charges to proceed, serving as a constitutional check on executive and judicial misconduct.[20] Beyond these, the House contributes to broader legislative functions, such as confirming gubernatorial appointments in joint session when required by statute and participating in the override of gubernatorial vetoes by a two-thirds vote in each chamber, per Article VI, Section 10.[20] This authority is tempered by direct democratic reserves, as Article V, Section 1 explicitly preserves the people's rights of initiative and referendum, allowing statutes passed by the Legislatureâ€”including those from the Houseâ€”to be challenged or supplanted by voter approval, with initiatives needing signatures from 10% of qualified electors (5% for targeted statutes) to qualify.[20] Such mechanisms limit legislative overreach, ensuring the House's role aligns with popular sovereignty rather than unchecked plenary power.[20]

Session Schedules and Rulemaking

The Montana House of Representatives convenes as part of the biennial legislative sessions outlined in Article V, Section 6 of the Montana Constitution, which mandates regular sessions in odd-numbered years starting on the first Monday in January and limiting them to 90 legislative days.[20] These sessions focus on passing a biennial budget, enacting statutes, and addressing policy priorities, with the 2025 session scheduled to begin January 6 and adjourn no later than April 30.[52] In even-numbered years, an organizational session occurs on the first Monday in January solely for swearing in members, electing officers, and appointing committees, unless the governor certifies an emergency necessitating extension for legislative action.[20] Special sessions may be called by the governor upon certification of an emergency or by a petition signed by two-thirds of the members of each house, allowing the legislature to address urgent matters outside regular schedules, such as fiscal crises or natural disasters.[20] Legislative days exclude weekends, holidays, and recesses, enabling efficient progression of bills within the 90-day cap for regular sessions, though the actual calendar duration may extend due to these exclusions.[24] Rulemaking in the House begins with the adoption of standing rules at the outset of each session, typically via House Resolution 1, which establishes procedural frameworks including bill introduction deadlines, committee assignments, voting protocols, and decorum standards.[53] These rules, drawn from prior sessions but subject to majority vote for approval or amendment, classify standing committees by meeting frequencyâ€”Class One for daily sessions and Class Two for tri-weeklyâ€”to manage workload, and permit proxy voting in committees under specific conditions like legislative duties elsewhere.[53] Amendments to rules require a simple majority and can occur mid-session to adapt to emerging needs, such as enhancing debate transparency or committee authority, though changes must align with constitutional limits on session length and public access.[54] The rules emphasize public voting on bills and committees, prohibiting secret ballots to ensure transparency in proceedings.[55]

Historical Background

Establishment in Statehood Era

The Montana House of Representatives was established through the 1889 state constitution, which created a bicameral Legislative Assembly vesting legislative authority in a Senate and a House of Representatives, with the people reserving initiative and referendum powers to themselves.[56] [57] The constitution, drafted by a convention of 75 delegates assembled in Helena from July 4 to August 17, 1889, and ratified by voters on October 1, 1889, outlined the House's structure, including two-year terms for representatives elected from districts apportioned by population.[58] [21] Montana's admission to the Union as the 41st state on November 8, 1889, under the federal Enabling Act of February 22, 1889, marked the formal transition from territorial governance, where a bicameral legislature with a House of Representatives had operated since 1864 but under congressional oversight.[59] [60] The state constitution superseded territorial frameworks by mandating popular election of House members, qualifications limited to U.S. citizenship, residency in the district for one year prior to election, and a minimum age of 25, while prohibiting dual office-holding with federal or other state roles.[61] Elections for the inaugural House occurred in October 1889, resulting in 45 members representing districts carved from the state's counties and population centers, with the body convening alongside the Senate as the First Legislative Assembly on November 23, 1889, in Helenaâ€”15 days after statehood.[62] [63] This session focused on organizing state institutions, adopting laws to replace territorial codes, and addressing immediate governance needs, such as capital location and taxation, under rules derived from the constitution's provisions for sessions, quorum (majority presence), and veto overrides by two-thirds vote.[61] The House's early composition reflected Montana's mining and ranching demographics, with representatives largely drawn from Democratic-leaning urban and agricultural areas amid partisan tensions inherited from territorial politics.[63]

Key Reforms and Structural Changes

The 1972 Montana Constitution, ratified by voters on June 6, 1972, introduced significant structural reforms to the legislature, including the creation of an independent Districting and Apportionment Commission to draw congressional and state legislative district boundaries every decade, aiming to mitigate partisan gerrymandering after prior controversies.[64][12] The commission consists of five members: two appointed by the majority party leaders from each chamber and one by the minority party or an independent, with the Montana Supreme Court appointing a presiding officer if needed.[65] This body first convened post-1980 census and has since redrawn maps, including after the 2020 census on November 12, 2021.[17] The 1972 document also mandated open legislative proceedings, prohibiting closed sessions to enhance transparency, and established flexible parameters for chamber sizes: the House between 80 and 110 members, with the current 100 seats set by statute.[66][67] It preserved the biennial session structure in odd-numbered years but extended allowable duration beyond the prior 60-day limit, allowing up to 90 legislative days plus extensions, while maintaining the citizen-legislator model with part-time service.[68] In 1992, voters approved Constitutional Initiative 64 on November 3, imposing term limits on legislators: no more than eight years in the House (equivalent to four two-year terms) within any 16-year period, applicable similarly to the Senate, to promote turnover and prevent entrenched incumbency.[22] This reform took effect for the 1996 elections, resulting in significant member rotation; for instance, it has led to over half of House seats turning over in some cycles due to eligibility expirations.[36] Minor adjustments to House size occurred earlier, such as reduction from 108 members in the 1920s to the current 100 by mid-century, reflecting population-based reapportionment under statutory law rather than constitutional mandate.[69] These changes, alongside the 1972 framework, have sustained a relatively stable bicameral structure since statehood, with no unicameral proposals advancing despite occasional debate.[64]

Political Dynamics and Composition

Evolution of Partisan Control

The Montana House of Representatives has experienced fluctuating partisan control since statehood in 1889, reflecting broader national political trends, economic conditions, and regional demographics in a predominantly rural, resource-dependent state. Initially, the chamber saw competitive balances between Republicans and Democrats, with third parties like Populists and Silver Republicans influencing outcomes in the late 19th century; for instance, the inaugural 1889 session featured 24 Republicans and 21 Democrats among 45 members.[70] By the early 20th century, Republicans achieved dominance, peaking at 98 of 108 seats in 1921 amid progressive reforms and anti-labor sentiments.[70] This Republican edge eroded during the Great Depression, as Democrats capitalized on New Deal policies to secure overwhelming majorities, such as 81 of 102 seats in 1937.[70] Post-World War II, control alternated more frequently, with Democrats maintaining pluralities in many sessions through the 1970s due to union support in mining and agricultural districts, exemplified by 67 Democratic seats in 1975.[70] Republicans regained ground in the 1980s amid economic deregulation appeals and rural conservatism, but Democrats held slim majorities into the early 1990s, including 61 seats in 1991.[70] A pivotal shift occurred in the mid-1990s, driven by national Republican waves and Montana's growing libertarian-leaning electorate, leading to sustained Republican majorities; from 1995 onward, Republicans controlled the chamber except for tied compositions of 50-50 in 2005 and 2008.[36][70]

Recent Elections and Shifts Post-2024

In the November 5, 2024, general election, Republicans retained control of the Montana House of Representatives but saw their majority reduced from 68 seats to 58, with Democrats expanding from 32 to 42 seats.[4][5] This shift ended the Republican supermajority, which had previously allowed overrides of gubernatorial vetoes without Democratic support, as the new composition fell short of the 67 votes required for such actions under the state constitution.[71] The results reflected gains for Democrats in several districts, particularly in urban and western Montana areas, amid higher voter turnout and national Republican coattails from the presidential race.[72] The partisan realignment stemmed in part from a newly drawn legislative map, approved by the Montana Supreme Court in December 2023 following legal challenges to the initial Republican-led redistricting plan, which incorporated updated census data and aimed for competitive districts.[72] Democrats flipped at least 10 House seats previously held by Republicans, including key races in districts like HD 9 (Missoula) and HD 95 (Helena), where Democratic candidates prevailed by margins of 5-10 percentage points despite overall statewide Republican advantages in voter registration.[71] No independent or third-party candidates secured seats, maintaining the bicameral partisan divide. As of October 2025, no special elections or resignations have altered the 58-42 composition entering the 2025 legislative session, though internal Republican divisions have prompted procedural concessions, such as expanded Democratic committee roles, signaling potential moderation in agenda-setting.[73] This narrower majority has influenced early session dynamics, with bipartisan coalitions forming on budget and education bills, contrasting the more unified Republican control of prior terms.[74]

Controversies and Criticisms

Internal Republican Divisions and Moderation Pressures

The Republican majority in the Montana House of Representatives has been marked by tensions between establishment leadership, which prioritizes legislative productivity and compromise to advance bills, and a hardline conservative faction aligned with the Montana Freedom Caucus, which demands stricter adherence to limited-government principles and often employs procedural tactics to obstruct perceived dilutions of conservative priorities.[75][76] The Freedom Caucus, formed in January 2023 with inspiration from U.S. Rep. Matt Rosendale's federal counterpart, includes House members who criticize party leadership for insufficient opposition to spending increases and regulatory expansions, leading to repeated clashes over amendments and veto overrides.[76][77] These divisions have frequently manifested in battles over House rules, where the hardline group pushes for changes to expand motions to recommit billsâ€”allowing endless amendments that can derail moderate-backed measuresâ€”and limit leadership's control over debate.[78][79] In the 2023 session, simmering factional conflicts surfaced during rules adoption on January 4, with hardliners attempting to alter quorum requirements and amendment processes, though Speaker Matt Regier secured passage of amended rules favoring efficiency over prolonged disruption.[79] Similar maneuvers occurred in prior sessions, such as 2020 interim meetings where hardliners hinted at coups against leadership to enforce ideological purity.[80] Moderation pressures on House Republicans intensify in narrower majorities, as seen after the 2024 elections reduced the GOP edge to 58-42 seats, compelling leadership to corral votes for passage amid veto risks from Gov. Greg Gianforte and the practical need to address constituent demands like property tax caps without fiscal overreach.[4][81] Hardliners face counterpressure from party activists and primary challenges for supporting bipartisan compromises, such as expanded education funding or delayed tax cuts deemed too incremental, while moderates argue that unyielding tactics risk legislative gridlock and electoral backlash in swing districts.[82][74] In the 2025 session, these dynamics contributed to weeks of infighting, with factions delaying agenda items until temporary leadership adjustments, like Rep. Katie Zolnikov's interim speakership on February 14, highlighted the fragility of unity.[82][40] Such internal strife reflects broader causal tensions in Montana's Republican coalition: rural hardliners prioritize base mobilization on cultural issues like abortion restrictions and gun rights, while urban or suburban members, facing competitive reelections, lean toward pragmatic fiscal conservatism to appeal beyond the activist core, often resulting in diluted bills that satisfy neither side fully.[78][74] Despite these pressures, the House has avoided the outright power-sharing seen in the Senate, maintaining GOP procedural control but at the cost of slower progress on priorities like judicial reforms.[79][83]

Debates Over Judicial and Electoral Reforms

During the 69th Montana Legislative Session in 2025, the Republican-majority House of Representatives debated a package of approximately 27 bills aimed at reforming the state's judicial system, primarily in response to perceptions that the nonpartisan Montana Supreme Court had frequently invalidated Republican-backed legislation on issues such as environmental regulations and abortion restrictions.[84] [85] [86] Proponents, including Governor Greg Gianforte, argued that introducing partisan labels in judicial elections and allowing political party funding in campaigns would enhance voter accountability and transparency, given that the current nonpartisan system obscured candidates' ideological alignments.[87] [88] The first such bill, heard in a House committee on January 9, 2025, sought to permit party affiliations in judicial races, but faced opposition from legal groups warning of politicization that could undermine judicial impartiality.[88] On April 10, 2025, the House Judiciary Committee engaged in extended floor debates over more than a dozen judicial bills, including proposals to regulate court procedures, expand legislative oversight of judicial conduct, and potentially impeach justices perceived as activist.[89] Despite the Republican supermajorityâ€”stemming from the 2024 elections where the party secured all statewide offices except one U.S. Senate seatâ€”many reforms stalled due to internal divisions and veto threats; for instance, a bill for partisan Supreme Court elections was defeated on April 7, 2025, and Governor Gianforte vetoed others on May 19, 2025, citing insufficient consensus. [90] [91] Critics, including the State Bar of Montana, contended these efforts represented a partisan overreach, while supporters pointed to prior Senate interim committees from 2023-2024 that documented alleged judicial overreach in striking down 15 laws since 2021.[92] [93] Electoral reform debates in the House centered on bolstering election security amid post-2020 concerns, with bills advancing stricter verification measures such as requiring voters to include their birth year on mail ballot envelopes and implementing photo ID requirements via Senate Bill 276, signed into law in 2025.[94] [95] House Republicans justified these as essential for preventing fraud, citing data from the 2024 elections where the birth-year rule led to rejection rates up to 10 times higher than prior cycles in some counties, though officials reported no widespread irregularities.[95] [96] Additional proposals repealed election-day registration and tightened absentee voting timelines, prompting lawsuits filed on May 16, 2025, by public employee groups alleging voter suppression, while defenders referenced federal audits showing Montana's prior mail ballot error rates at 1-2%.[97] [98] House discussions also addressed ballot initiative processes, with bills sponsored by Representative Braxton Mitchell advancing in March 2025 to raise signature thresholds and limit out-of-state funding, aiming to curb what Republicans described as exploitative direct democracy tactics by progressive groups.[99] These measures passed amid partisan divides, with Democrats arguing they disadvantaged grassroots efforts, but empirical reviews of 2022-2024 initiatives showed over 70% funded externally, supporting claims of reduced local control.[99] Overall, while judicial reforms largely failed, several electoral integrity bills enacted changes that faced immediate legal and operational scrutiny.[87][97]
