Max Miller (politician)


Max Leonard Miller (born November 13, 1988) is an American politician serving as the U.S. representative for Ohio's 7th congressional district since January 2023.[1] A Republican, he was reelected to a second term in the 2024 election, defeating Democratic challenger Matthew Diemer and independent Dennis Kucinich.[2] Prior to Congress, Miller worked on Donald Trump's 2016 presidential campaign and served on the White House staff during Trump's presidency from 2017 to 2021.[1] Born in Shaker Heights, Ohio, Miller graduated from Shaker Heights High School in 2007.[1] He entered Republican politics through involvement in Trump's campaign and administration, focusing on operations and logistics roles.[3] Elected in 2022 following redistricting of Ohio's congressional map, Miller has positioned himself as a staunch supporter of Trump-era policies, including strong national security measures and opposition to certain party leadership decisions, such as voting to remove Speaker Kevin McCarthy in 2023. In Congress, he serves on key committees addressing foreign affairs and homeland security, advocating for aid to Ukraine and Israel amid ongoing conflicts.[4] Miller's tenure has included notable incidents highlighting political tensions, such as a June 2025 road rage encounter where he was threatened with antisemitic slurs by a driver displaying a Palestinian flag, leading to the suspect's arrest.[5] As a Jewish congressman representing a district with significant suburban and rural constituencies, his positions emphasize border security, energy independence, and fiscal conservatism.[6]

Background

Early life and education

Max Miller was born on November 13, 1988, in Shaker Heights, Ohio, where he grew up.[1][3][6] He attended Shaker Heights High School, graduating in 2007.[1] Miller enrolled at the University of Arizona in Tucson but did not earn a degree there. He subsequently received a Bachelor of Arts from Cleveland State University in Cleveland, Ohio, in 2013.[1]

Pre-political career

Max Miller was born on November 13, 1988, in Shaker Heights, Ohio, into a prominent Cleveland family; his grandparents, Sam and Ruth Ratner Miller, were associated with Forest City Enterprises, a major real estate developer.[7] After attending the University of Arizona from 2007 to 2009 without completing a degree, Miller enrolled at Cleveland State University, from which he graduated in May 2013 with a bachelor's degree in history.[7][8] Following graduation, Miller worked at a Lululemon Athletica retail store in the Cleveland area.[7] On December 2, 2013, he enlisted in the Marine Corps Reserve, serving as a corporal and rifleman; he transferred to the Individual Ready Reserve on December 9, 2019, after approximately six years of service.[7][9] In early 2016, prior to broader political involvement, Miller volunteered and worked on Marco Rubio's presidential campaign, earning over $7,000 as an advance team member.[7]

Service in the Trump Administration

Roles and contributions

Miller served in the White House as a lead advance representative, coordinating logistics for presidential public appearances and image-focused events.[7] He later advanced to the role of special assistant to the president and associate director in the Presidential Personnel Office (PPO), where he contributed to vetting and placing appointees across the administration, earning an annual salary of $115,000.[7] [10] In June 2020, Miller accompanied President Trump during the visit to St. John's Church for a Bible-holding photo opportunity amid nationwide protests following George Floyd's death.[7] Transitioning to the 2020 re-election campaign, Miller was appointed deputy campaign manager for presidential operations on July 8, 2020, overseeing advance work for rallies, debates, and the Republican National Convention.[11] His responsibilities included organizing high-profile events that drew tens of thousands of attendees, such as the June 2020 Tulsa rally, which mobilized supporters despite pandemic restrictions.[7] Known internally as "The Music Man," Miller curated playlists for Trump's rallies, selecting tracks like "Y.M.C.A." and "Sweet Caroline" to energize crowds and align with the president's preferences.[7] [12] These efforts supported the campaign's strategy to maintain voter enthusiasm in battleground states through direct, large-scale engagements.[7] Miller's advance expertise extended to the Ellipse rally on January 6, 2021, where he assisted in arrangements preceding the Capitol events, as documented in House select committee investigations.[13] Throughout his service, spanning 2017 to 2021, Miller's operational focus emphasized rapid execution and logistical precision, contributing to the administration's public-facing activities amid a high-turnover environment in personnel placement.[10] [7]

Path to Congress

2022 election campaign

Miller launched his congressional campaign in early 2021, positioning himself as a staunch supporter of former President Donald Trump and announcing his intent to primary incumbent Republican Anthony Gonzalez in Ohio's then-16th congressional district.[14] Trump endorsed Miller on February 26, 2021, praising him as a loyal aide and criticizing Gonzalez for voting to impeach Trump following the January 6, 2021, Capitol riot.[14] Gonzalez, facing this intra-party challenge amid GOP backlash over his impeachment vote, announced on September 16, 2021, that he would not seek reelection, citing the "toxic dynamics" within the Republican Party as a key factor.[15] [16] Following Ohio's 2021 redistricting, which reconfigured districts and created a new 7th district encompassing parts of Cuyahoga, Medina, Wayne, and Holmes counties, Miller shifted his candidacy to this seat on November 19, 2021, viewing it as a viable opportunity despite its more Democratic-leaning profile compared to his original target.[17] His campaign emphasized America First policies, border security, opposition to "woke" cultural shifts, and economic recovery priorities aligned with Trump's agenda, while fundraising efforts raised over $1.5 million by the primary.[18] In the Republican primary held on May 3, 2022, Miller secured the nomination with approximately 65% of the vote, defeating challenger George Sarvis and other minor candidates in the newly drawn district.[19] [18] The victory was attributed to his Trump ties and Gonzalez's unpopularity among base voters, with no significant intra-party opposition emerging after Gonzalez's exit. Miller faced Democrat Matthew Diemer, a Bay Village podcaster, along with independents Vince Licursi and Brian Kenderes, in the general election on November 8, 2022.[20] Campaigning on inflation control, energy independence, and Second Amendment rights, Miller won with 54.6% of the vote (162,586 votes) to Diemer's 43.5% (129,604 votes), securing the seat in the Republican-leaning district.[21] The result reflected strong GOP turnout and Miller's appeal to Trump-aligned voters in a midterm environment favoring Republicans.[20]

2024 reelection

In the Republican primary election held on March 19, 2024, incumbent Max Miller ran unopposed and received 62,075 votes, or 100% of the total. Miller's general election opponents were Democrat Matthew Diemer, a two-time challenger who won his party's primary, and independent Dennis Kucinich, a former U.S. representative who had previously served Ohio's 10th district as a Democrat before running as an independent in this race.[22] The contest was rated "Safe Republican" or equivalent by forecasters including the Cook Political Report, Inside Elections, and Sabato's Crystal Ball, reflecting the district's Republican lean. On November 5, 2024, Miller won reelection with 204,494 votes (51.1%), defeating Diemer, who received 144,613 votes (36.1%), and Kucinich, who garnered 51,264 votes (12.8%), for a total of 400,371 votes cast.[2] During the campaign, Miller highlighted priorities including economic policy and border security as key areas for continued focus in Congress.[23] The Associated Press called the race for Miller shortly after polls closed.[22]

Congressional Record

Tenure and legislative priorities

Max Miller was sworn into the 118th United States Congress on January 3, 2023, representing Ohio's 7th congressional district.[4] He secured reelection in November 2024, entering the 119th Congress with continued service on the House Committee on Science, Space, and Technology and the House Committee on Ways and Means.[24] These assignments reflect his emphasis on innovation, fiscal policy, and technological advancement amid economic and security challenges. Miller's legislative priorities center on bolstering national security and defense capabilities. He endorsed the Fiscal Year 2024 National Defense Authorization Act, which passed the House on December 14, 2023, authorizing investments in military readiness and deterrence.[25] In August 2025, he commemorated victims of the 2021 Abbey Gate bombing in Afghanistan, underscoring ongoing accountability for U.S. foreign policy failures.[26] This aligns with his broader focus on protecting critical infrastructure, exemplified by his introduction of the NEPTUNE Act on July 25, 2025, aimed at enhancing defenses against cyber and physical threats to essential systems.[27] Fiscal conservatism and economic growth feature prominently in his record. Miller sponsored H.R. 137, the TCJA Permanency Act, to extend key provisions of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, including individual and business tax reductions, arguing for permanence to foster long-term investment and job creation.[28] He has also advocated for energy independence through support for the Renewable Fuel Standard, leading a bicameral resolution on August 8, 2025, marking its 20th anniversary and highlighting its role in reducing reliance on foreign oil.[29] Additionally, he cosponsored H.R. 1919, the Anti-CBDC Surveillance State Act, to prohibit the issuance of a central bank digital currency, citing risks to financial privacy and innovation.[30] Election integrity and consumer protections round out his initiatives. Miller backed the SAVE Act, which mandates proof of U.S. citizenship for federal voter registration to prevent non-citizen voting.[31] In June 2025, he introduced the Time is Money Act to impose penalties on airlines for excessive delays, prioritizing passenger rights and accountability in transportation.[32] These efforts demonstrate a consistent orientation toward practical reforms grounded in economic realism and security imperatives during his tenure through October 2025.[26]

Committee assignments and caucus involvement

In the 118th Congress (2023â€“2025), Miller served on the House Committee on Agriculture, where he advocated for rural development and farm bill provisions benefiting Ohio's agricultural sector, and on the House Committee on Science, Space, and Technology, focusing on support for NASA facilities in Northeast Ohio and oversight of environmental research.[33][34] For the 119th Congress (2025â€“2027), Miller retained his position on the Committee on Science, Space, and Technology, assuming the chairmanship of its Subcommittee on Environment to address issues such as NOAA budgeting and environmental policy impacts.[35][36] On December 13, 2024, he was appointed to the influential House Committee on Ways and Means, reflecting his priorities in tax policy and economic reform; within this committee, he serves on the Subcommittee on Work and Welfare, addressing labor and welfare programs, and the Subcommittee on Health, overseeing healthcare financing and Medicare issues.[37][38][24] Miller participates in several Republican caucuses, including the Republican Study Committee, which promotes conservative fiscal and social policies; the Republican Main Street Partnership, emphasizing pragmatic governance; and the Congressional Western Caucus, advocating for resource management and federal land policies despite his Ohio representation.[39]

Key votes and sponsored legislation

Miller has sponsored several resolutions and bills focused on national security, veterans' issues, and government accountability. In May 2024, he introduced a resolution condemning the Biden administration's delays in approving arms transfers to Israel amid its conflict with Hamas, highlighting perceived obstructions to Israel's defense capabilities. Earlier, he sponsored a joint resolution designating August 26 as the anniversary of the 2021 Abbey Gate bombing in Afghanistan, honoring the 13 U.S. service members killed and calling for accountability from the Taliban and Biden administration. Other sponsored measures include H.R. 9723 in the 118th Congress, aimed at enhancing the National Windstorm Impact Reduction Program to improve disaster preparedness through research and mitigation strategies.[40] In the 119th Congress, Miller supported legislation bolstering energy infrastructure and fiscal continuity. He voted yea on H.R. 1047, the Guaranteeing Reliability through Interconnection of Dispatchable Grid Power Act, passed September 18, 2025, which promotes reliable power sources to address grid vulnerabilities.[41] He also voted yea on H.R. 3015, the National Coal Council Reestablishment Act, enacted the same day to revive advisory mechanisms for coal policy and energy security.[41] On appropriations, Miller voted yea for H.R. 5371, the Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act 2026, approved September 18, 2025, extending federal funding to avert shutdowns.[41] However, he opposed H.R. 9747, a continuing resolution funding the government through December 20, 2024, citing excessive spending and lack of reforms, voting nay on September 25, 2024.[42] In the 118th Congress, he voted yea for a September 30, 2023, stopgap measure funding operations for 45 days while criticizing long-term fiscal irresponsibility.[43] On foreign aid, Miller backed the $95 billion national security supplemental package passed April 20, 2024, allocating $26 billion for Israel, $61 billion for Ukraine, and $8 billion for Taiwan, emphasizing strengthened defenses against adversaries like Iran, Russia, and China.[44] [45] He played a role in advancing Israel-specific aid, consistent with his advocacy as one of two Jewish Republicans in the House.[46] Domestically, he cosponsored and supported the SAVE Act (H.R. 22) in the 119th Congress, requiring proof of citizenship for voter registration to prevent non-citizen voting.[31] Miller voted yea on H.R. 5125, the District of Columbia Judicial Nominations Reform Act, passed September 17, 2025, to curb D.C.'s independent judicial confirmation powers.[41] He voted nay on tabling H. Res. 713 censuring Representative Ilhan Omar on September 17, 2025, though the measure failed.[41]

Political Ideology and Positions

Alignment with America First principles

Max Miller has publicly committed to advancing the America First agenda, emphasizing priorities such as defending constitutional rights, combating censorship, and protecting election integrity.[47] His tenure reflects alignment on domestic issues like border security and economic protectionism, though his support for certain foreign aid measures has drawn criticism from strict adherents of non-interventionist principles. On immigration, Miller criticizes the Biden administration's policies for enabling a crisis at the southern border, with millions of illegal entries and influxes of deadly drugs like fentanyl endangering American communities.[48] He has voted for measures to secure the border and ensure only U.S. citizens influence elections, framing these as essential to prioritizing American safety and sovereignty.[49][50] This stance echoes America First emphases on strict enforcement and national self-interest over open borders. In economic policy, Miller advocates for fair trade practices that shield U.S. workers and industries, including legislation to block American taxpayer subsidies from benefiting Chinese firms and to enforce robust agreements enabling domestic competition.[51][52] He supports domestic energy initiatives, such as recognizing the Renewable Fuel Standard's role in bolstering U.S. fuel production and reducing reliance on foreign sources.[29] These positions promote economic nationalism, aligning with America First goals of revitalizing manufacturing and achieving energy independence. Miller's foreign policy record shows partial divergence, particularly in supporting supplemental aid to Ukraine. He voted for the $60.8 billion Ukraine Security Supplemental Appropriations Act in April 2024, arguing it counters administration weaknesses and advances U.S. national security interests amid Russia's invasion.[53][44] This support, influenced by his district's Ukrainian-American population, contrasts with America First skepticism toward prolonged overseas commitments, earning him a "C+" grade from pro-Ukraine groups but criticism from non-interventionists who prioritize domestic spending.[54] His overall Heritage Action score of 57% in the 118th Congress reflects moderate conservative alignment, with strengths in border security but variances on foreign engagements.[31]

Stances on national security, economy, and social issues

Max Miller has advocated for bolstering U.S. national security through increased defense authorizations and supplemental funding to address threats from adversaries. He supported the Fiscal Year 2025 National Defense Authorization Act, which authorizes appropriations for military activities and emphasizes strengthening servicemembers' capabilities.[55] In April 2024, Miller endorsed national security supplemental appropriations bills, arguing they counter the Biden administration's perceived weaknesses in defense posture by providing aid to Ukraine, Israel, Taiwan, and Indo-Pacific partners, despite opposition from a majority of House Republicans on the Ukraine portion.[44] [53] He has been vocal in support of Israel, introducing a resolution in May 2024 condemning the Biden administration's pause on certain arms transfers amid the Israel-Hamas conflict, and played a key role in advancing aid packages post-October 7, 2023.[56] [46] Miller also honors U.S. service members affected by foreign policy decisions, such as introducing a resolution in August 2025 to commemorate victims of the 2021 Abbey Gate bombing in Afghanistan.[57] On domestic fronts tied to security, he views border control as essential, voting in May 2023 to secure the southern border against illegal entries and fentanyl trafficking, and criticizing lax enforcement under Biden as creating a crisis that endangers communities.[48] [49] Regarding the economy, Miller promotes policies favoring tax relief, manufacturing resurgence, and reduced government intervention to foster job growth. Drawing from his family's manufacturing background, he emphasizes commonsense regulations that enable small businesses and workers in Ohio's industrial sector to compete globally.[58] In January 2025, he introduced the Permanent Tax Cuts for American Families Act to extend and make permanent certain tax deductions, aiming to provide ongoing relief for households filing standard deductions.[59] Miller has engaged directly with manufacturers, such as visiting Stellar Automotive in Seville, Ohio, in January 2025 for a roundtable on industry challenges, and supports measures to block taxpayer subsidies for Chinese firms while bolstering domestic agriculture through legislation introduced in May 2025 to enhance food supply chains.[60] [61] He advocates scaling back federal overreach to prioritize economic efficiency.[62] On social issues, Miller holds conservative positions, including opposition to federal abortion funding and support for Second Amendment rights. He has a record of voting to protect the unborn and infants, such as blocking taxpayer dollars for abortions, earning high marks from pro-life organizations.[63] In September 2022, he stated preference for states handling abortion legislation rather than congressional mandates.[62] Miller defends the Second Amendment and Bill of Rights against encroachments.[47] His immigration stance aligns with social order concerns, pushing for enforcement to prevent illegal crossings that he links to crime and drug influxes, including votes in May 2024 to require proof of citizenship for voting.[48] [50] In September 2025, he called for deploying the Ohio National Guard to Cleveland amid rising violent crime, citing homicides as evidence of failed local policies.[64]

Controversies

Primary challenge to Anthony Gonzalez

In early 2021, following Representative Anthony Gonzalez's vote on January 13 to impeach former President Donald Trump for incitement of insurrection related to the January 6 Capitol riot, Max Miller, a former Trump White House aide and Marine Corps reservist, prepared a Republican primary challenge against Gonzalez in Ohio's 16th congressional district.[65] Miller, who had served as director of advance in the White House and deputy campaign manager for Trump's 2020 reelection effort, positioned his candidacy as a defense of Trump loyalty, criticizing Gonzalez's impeachment support as a betrayal of Republican principles and district voters.[65] Gonzalez, a former NFL player elected in 2018, defended his vote by arguing Trump bore responsibility for failing to intervene during the riot and for prior election-related falsehoods, though this drew censures from local GOP organizations including Medina County Republicans.[65] On February 26, 2021, Miller formally announced his bid, securing Trump's "Complete and Total Endorsement," with the former president praising Miller as a "wonderful person" and "true PATRIOT" while faulting Gonzalez for aligning against the party's base.[66][67] Miller, a northeastern Ohio native from a prominent Cleveland family, had purchased a home in Gonzalez's district and raised six-figure commitments from donors, emphasizing his Trump-aligned record to appeal to primary voters amid broader GOP efforts to purge impeachment supporters.[65] Facing mounting pressure, including personal threats requiring security measures, Gonzalez announced on September 16, 2021, that he would not seek reelection, citing family priorities, the GOP's "toxic dynamics" dominated by Trump allegiance, and the viability of his campaign against a Trump-backed challenger like Miller.[15] Gonzalez described Trump as "a cancer for the country and for the Republican Party," reflecting his view that party fealty over policy substance hindered effective opposition to Democrats.[15] After Ohio's redistricting shifted the contest to the more competitive 7th district, Miller won the open Republican primary on May 3, 2022, with over 50% of the vote in a four-way field, advancing to victory in the general election and assuming the seat previously held by Gonzalez.[18]

Personal and family legal disputes

In October 2021, Max Miller filed a defamation lawsuit against his former girlfriend, Stephanie Grisham, a fellow Trump administration official, after she alleged in her memoir I'll Take Your Questions Now that he had physically abused her during their relationship, including an incident where he allegedly threw her against a wall.[68] Grisham's claims emerged amid her public criticisms of Trump and detailed her experiences in the White House, but Miller denied the accusations, asserting they were fabricated to promote her book and damage his congressional candidacy.[69] The lawsuit sought damages and a retraction, highlighting tensions from their brief relationship that ended prior to Miller's entry into elective politics; the case's resolution details remain unreported in public records.[70] Miller married Emily Moreno, daughter of Ohio Senator Bernie Moreno, in 2022, but filed for divorce in Cuyahoga County Domestic Relations Court in August 2024, citing irreconcilable differences and seeking sole custody of their young child.[71] The proceedings escalated rapidly, with Emily Moreno accusing Miller of substance abuse, including claims that he appeared intoxicated and erratic, prompting her to request supervised visitation; Miller countered by alleging her instability and filed for emergency sole parenting time on September 3, 2024.[72][73] On December 31, 2024, Miller called Rocky River police to his home after Moreno refused to leave during a custody exchange, leading to a report of a potential trespass but no arrests, as officers deemed it a civil matter tied to the ongoing divorce.[74] The divorce drew in Miller's in-laws, with subpoenas issued in April 2025 to Senator Bernie Moreno and his wife Bridget for depositions on financial support provided to Emily, including demands for records of transfers exceeding $100,000 that Miller argued blurred family loans and gifts, potentially affecting asset division.[75][76] Tensions peaked ahead of a scheduled trial, but the parties reached a settlement agreement on June 16, 2025, averting testimony from the Morenos and public airing of further details on custody, alimony, and property division in their Rocky River residence and shared assets.[77] The settlement terms were sealed, leaving unresolved public questions about the validity of mutual accusations, though court filings emphasized the child's welfare amid allegations of parental unfitness from both sides.[76]

Encounters with antisemitic threats

On June 19, 2025, U.S. Representative Max Miller, a Jewish Republican from Ohio's 7th congressional district, reported being run off Interstate 90 near Rocky River, Ohio, by a driver in a black SUV displaying a Palestinian flag.[5] The aggressor allegedly forced Miller's vehicle to the shoulder, exited his own car, and shouted antisemitic slurs including "dirty Jew," along with death threats directed at Miller and his family.[78] Miller, who recorded part of the confrontation on his phone, responded by warning the man that police would be notified, emphasizing his identity as a "proud Jewish American."[79] The suspect, identified as 36-year-old Feras Hamdan of Westlake, Ohio, was arrested the following day on charges including aggravated menacing and ethnic intimidation.[80] Hamdan allegedly drove aggressively to block Miller, then approached on foot while brandishing the flag and issuing threats tied to Miller's pro-Israel stance.[81] A Cuyahoga County grand jury indicted Hamdan on July 9, 2025, for felony counts related to the incident, which Miller described as "blatant antisemitic violence" amid rising tensions over the Israel-Hamas conflict.[81] Hamdan appeared in court on August 4, 2025, pleading not guilty; the case highlighted localized antisemitic incidents targeting public figures supportive of Israel.[82] The episode drew bipartisan condemnation, with House Speaker Mike Johnson labeling it "deranged hatred" and the Ohio Jewish Caucus decrying it as an "antisemitic, anti-Israel attack."[83][84] Miller, a U.S. Marine veteran and co-sponsor of the Antisemitism Awareness Act, linked the threats to his vocal defense of Israel but affirmed he would not be intimidated.[85] No prior or subsequent personal encounters with antisemitic threats against Miller were publicly documented in contemporaneous reports.

Personal Life

Family and relationships

Max Miller was born and raised in Shaker Heights, Ohio.[3] In 2022, Miller married Emily Moreno, the daughter of Ohio businessman and U.S. Senator Bernie Moreno.[71] The couple had one child together, a daughter named Ruth, born on November 3, 2023.[86] Miller and Moreno separated, filing for divorce in August 2024 after approximately two years of marriage; the case concluded with a settlement agreement on June 16, 2025.[77][87]

Public persona and affiliations

Max Miller cultivates a public image as a staunch Trump loyalist and combat-tested conservative, drawing on his experience as a U.S. Marine Corps veteran from 2007 to 2011 and his roles in the Trump administration, including special assistant and director of operations in the Office of Presidential Personnel from 2017 to 2020, followed by deputy campaign manager for Trump's 2020 reelection campaign.[1] This background positions him as a defender of America First policies, evident in his endorsement by former President Trump during the 2021 Republican primary against Rep. Anthony Gonzalez, whom Miller criticized for supporting Trump's second impeachment.[14] [1] Miller maintains an active presence on social media platforms like X (formerly Twitter) under @RepMaxMiller and Facebook, where he frequently addresses district concerns such as crime in Clevelandâ€”advocating for National Guard deployment modeled after Trump's approachâ€”and national issues like election integrity, as seen in his January 6, 2025, statement celebrating the certification of Trump's 2024 victory.[88] [89] His media appearances, including on MSNBC and Fox News, reinforce a combative persona focused on unifying the GOP behind Trump while critiquing Democratic policies.[90] In terms of affiliations, Miller aligns closely with pro-Israel advocacy groups, receiving top campaign contributions from the Republican Jewish Coalition ($68,351 in the 2024 cycle) and AIPAC ($44,150), reflecting his participation in congressional delegations to Israel in November 2023 and vocal support for U.S.-Israel security cooperation as a Jewish Republican representative.[91] [6] He also engages with veterans' issues through his military service ties and broader Republican networks, though specific organizational memberships beyond party affiliation remain limited in public records.[1]
