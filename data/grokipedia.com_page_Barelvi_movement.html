Barelvi movement

The Barelvi movement, also known as Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah, is a traditionalist revivalist strain within Sunni Islam that originated in late 19th-century British India, founded by the scholar Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921) in the city of Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh.[1][2] Rooted in Hanafi jurisprudence and Maturidi theology, it emphasizes profound devotion to the Prophet Muhammad—viewing him as possessing a primordial light (nur Muhammad) and partial knowledge of the unseen—along with veneration of Sufi saints through shrine visits, urs commemorations, and milad celebrations of the Prophet's birthday.[1][3] Opposing reformist currents like Deobandism and Ahl-i-Hadith, which it critiques for puritanical rejection of such practices as innovations (bid'ah) akin to Wahhabi influences, the movement positions itself as the guardian of the "true" Sunni majority traditions against perceived scripturalist deviations.[1] This doctrinal rivalry, spanning over 150 years, has shaped sectarian dynamics in South Asia, with Barelvis accusing Deobandis of undermining emotional piety and Sufi intercession while defending rituals grounded in historical Sunni custom.[1] Prevalent among the majority of South Asian Sunnis, particularly in Pakistan and northern India, the Barelvi tradition has influenced popular religious culture through madrasas, Sufi orders, and political mobilization, including support for the Pakistan Movement and later groups like Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan.[1][4] Notable for preserving syncretic folk elements within an orthodox framework, it has faced marginalization in state-favored Deobandi institutions yet demonstrated resilience, occasionally issuing fatwas against global jihadist extremism such as ISIS.[3] Controversies persist over its accommodation of local customs, which critics label superstitious, but empirically it sustains devotional Islam for millions, countering ascetic reformism through causal emphasis on transmitted spiritual authority over textual literalism alone.[1]

Terminology and Etymology

Origins of the Name

The designation "Barelvi" functions as a nisba, an Arabic-derived attributive form indicating origin or affiliation, stemming from Bareilly (also transliterated as Bareli), a city in present-day Uttar Pradesh, India. This city served as the birthplace and lifelong residence of Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), the scholar whose jurisprudential and theological output laid the groundwork for the movement. Khan himself bore the epithet "Barelvi" in his name, reflecting his connection to the locale, which colonial-era administrators and later scholars adopted to categorize the network of disciples and institutions propagating his views.[5][6] The term gained prominence during the late 19th-century polemical exchanges between Khan's circle and rival reformist groups, such as the Deobandis, who used "Barelvi" to pejoratively associate opponents with Khan's personal authority and regional base, mirroring the geographic labeling of Deoband for the latter. Academic analyses, including those by historian Usha Sanyal, trace this usage to the broader context of intra-Sunni contestations under British rule, where geographic and personal identifiers facilitated differentiation amid doctrinal disputes over Sufi practices and prophetic intercession.[7][8] Adherents, however, largely eschew "Barelvi" as an externally imposed label that implies sectarian novelty, preferring self-descriptions like Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah to emphasize continuity with orthodox Sunni tradition rather than innovation tied to a specific figure or place. This preference underscores a meta-awareness among movement proponents of how rival terminologies served to marginalize their defense of established devotional customs against puritanical critiques.[8][6]

Alternative Designations and Self-Identification

The adherents of the Barelvi movement self-identify primarily as Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah (People of the Sunnah and the Community) or its Urdu variant Ahl-e-Sunnat wa al-Jama'at, claiming exclusive representation of orthodox Sunni Islam in South Asia through adherence to Hanafi jurisprudence, Ash'ari/Maturidi creed, and Sufi traditions.[9][6] This self-designation emerged in the late 19th century under Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), who framed his fatwas and writings as defenses of "true" Sunnism against perceived deviations by reformists, emphasizing devotional practices like milad celebrations and shrine veneration as integral to the prophetic path.[6] Organizations such as Jamiat Ahl-e-Hadith and later Sunni bodies like Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan (Barelvi faction) incorporate these terms to assert continuity with classical Sunni scholarship while rejecting labels implying novelty or locality.[9] The appellation "Barelvi" or "Barelwi," derived from Bareilly in Uttar Pradesh, India—the intellectual hub where Khan issued over 12,000 fatwas starting around 1880—is widely viewed as an exonym imposed by opponents, particularly Deobandis, to marginalize the movement as a mere regional sect rather than universal Sunnism.[8] Adherents often repudiate it, arguing it distorts their self-understanding as timeless Ahl-e-Sunnat guardians against Wahhabi-influenced puritanism, a stance reinforced in intra-Sunni polemics where rival groups are deemed outside the fold.[8][9] This rejection aligns with Khan's own emphasis on individual scholarly authority over collective labeling, fostering a identity rooted in doctrinal fidelity over geographic origin.[6]

Historical Development

Founding and Early Ideology under Ahmed Raza Khan

Ahmed Raza Khan was born on June 14, 1856, in Bareilly, in the western United Provinces of British India, into a scholarly Pathan family with roots in Mughal service and Islamic learning.[6] His grandfather, Riza Ali Khan (d. 1865/66), shifted from administrative roles to full-time scholarship and Sufism, while his father, Naqi Ali Khan (1831–1880), served as a mufti issuing legal opinions.[6] Khan received a traditional Islamic education following the Dars-i Nizami curriculum under his father, completing it by age 13 or 14 around 1869–1870, which equipped him with expertise in Hanafi jurisprudence, Maturidi theology, and related sciences.[6] By age 14, Khan began issuing fatwas independently, marking the start of his scholarly output that would define the Ahl-e-Sunnat wa al-Jama'at tradition.[6] He also initiated into the Qadiri Sufi order under Shah Al-e Rasul of Marehra, integrating mystical devotion with orthodox Sunni scholarship.[6] His early writings in the 1870s and 1880s focused on defending established rituals such as milad celebrations and tawassul through saints and the Prophet Muhammad, viewing these as integral to Sunni practice against emerging critiques.[6] A pilgrimage to Mecca in the late 1870s further reinforced his commitment to prophetic centrality and intercession.[6] Khan's ideology crystallized in the late 19th century as a bulwark against reformist movements like the Nadwat al-Ulama (founded 1894) and influences from Deobandi and Ahl-e Hadith scholars, whom he accused of diluting Sufi traditions and undervaluing the Prophet's unique status.[6] In works like the multi-volume Tuhfa-e Hanafiyya (begun around 1897), he articulated Hanafi fidelity alongside emphatic veneration of Muhammad as the exemplar of faith, emphasizing concepts such as the Prophet's pre-eminence (imtina' al-nazir) and the permissibility of devotional expressions.[6] By the 1890s, his campaigns against Nadwa figures highlighted irreconcilable differences over Sufi practices and theological nuances, positioning Ahl-e-Sunnat as the true representative of mass Sunni piety rooted in love for the Prophet and adherence to sharia-mediated mysticism.[6] This framework, disseminated through fatwas and printed treatises, laid the groundwork for a movement prioritizing ritual devotion over scripturalist reform.[6]

19th-Century Conflicts with Reformist Movements

The Barelvi movement's ideological foundations crystallized in the late 19th century amid opposition to reformist initiatives like the Deobandi school, which emphasized scriptural literalism and critiqued Sufi-influenced customs as innovations (bid'ah). Founded in 1866 at Darul Uloom Deoband by Muhammad Qasim Nanautavi (d. 1880) and Rashid Ahmad Gangohi (d. 1905), the Deobandi movement sought to revive Hanafi orthodoxy through rigorous study of Qur'an and Hadith, rejecting practices such as excessive saint veneration, milad celebrations, and intercessory prayers at shrines, which it deemed unprophetic accretions influenced by colonial-era laxity. Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), the movement's progenitor, countered these views by defending such rituals as integral to Sunni tradition, rooted in love for the Prophet Muhammad and Sufi tariqas, issuing early fatwas from the 1870s onward to affirm their legitimacy against reformist puritanism.[6] Central theological flashpoints included disputes over the Prophet's knowledge of the unseen ('ilm al-ghayb) and the impossibility of a prophet equal to Muhammad (imtina' al-nazir). Deobandi scholars, such as Khalil Ahmad al-Saharanpuri, argued the Prophet's knowledge was limited to what Allah revealed, denying inherent omniscience to avoid equating him with divine attributes—a position Khan deemed disrespectful and akin to Wahhabi literalism.[6] In response, Khan's 1882 treatise Hadi al-Hiran and subsequent writings upheld the Prophet's exalted epistemic status, drawing on classical Ash'ari and Maturidi sources to refute Deobandi restrictions. Similarly, the reformist Nadwat al-Ulama, established in 1894 at Lucknow to reconcile madhhab differences and modernize seminary curricula, drew Khan's ire for diluting taqlid (adherence to legal schools) and accommodating Ahl-i Hadith iconoclasm; by the mid-1890s, he had authored around 200 fatwas condemning Nadwa as a vector for doctrinal erosion.[6] These exchanges escalated through mutual fatwa campaigns, with Deobandis labeling Barelvi rituals as polytheistic (shirk) and Khan reciprocating by branding reformist leaders as misguided (gumrah) or worse in a 1896 fatwa targeting Deoband and Nadwa for compromising prophetic superiority.[6] In 1897, Khan launched the periodical Tuhfa-e Hanafiyya to systematically critique Nadwa's ecumenism until around 1910, framing the conflict as a defense of Ahl al-Sunnah against Wahhabi-tinged reformism that undermined communal devotion.[6] Such polemics, while rooted in casuistic debates over God's omnipotence (e.g., whether Allah could lie, as allegedly implied by Gangohi), reflected broader causal tensions: reformists prioritized textual revival to counter colonial disempowerment post-1857, whereas Barelvis preserved syncretic practices to sustain mass piety amid modernization pressures.

20th-Century Expansion and Organizational Formation

In the early decades of the 20th century, the Barelvi movement expanded beyond its Bareilly origins through the dissemination of Ahmed Raza Khan's fatwas, treatises, and poetic works by his son Hamid Raza Khan (d. 1943) and a network of disciples, who established printing presses and madrasas in Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Bengal to propagate Sufi-infused Hanafi teachings. This growth countered reformist critiques from Deobandi and Ahl-i Hadith groups, with Barelvi scholars issuing collective responses emphasizing veneration of the Prophet Muhammad and saints, thereby consolidating support among rural and artisanal Muslim communities. By the 1920s, the movement's influence extended to urban centers like Lucknow and Muradabad, where annual gatherings reinforced doctrinal unity.[10] A pivotal organizational development occurred in 1925 with the founding of the All India Sunni Conference in Muradabad, which served as the central forum for Ahl-e-Sunnat ulama to coordinate activities, including theological debates and public defenses of traditional practices against perceived innovations by rival sects. The inaugural session drew over 250 scholars from across British India, establishing annual conferences that addressed issues like shrine custodianship and opposition to anti-Sufi legislation, while fostering alliances with Sufi orders such as the Chishti and Qadiri. This body formalized the movement's structure, enabling systematic publication of literature and training of imams, which accelerated expansion in Punjab and Sindh regions.[10][11] Following the 1947 partition, organizational efforts bifurcated along new national lines. In Pakistan, the Jamiat Ulama-e-Pakistan was established in 1948 as a Barelvi-aligned political entity to advocate for orthodox Sunni policies, including protection of Sufi shrines and resistance to Deobandi dominance in state religious affairs, marking the movement's entry into formal politics. In India, the All India Sunni Conference persisted, adapting to post-colonial contexts by focusing on madrasa networks and legal defenses of rituals, though it faced marginalization amid secular reforms. These formations solidified the movement's institutional base, supporting sustained growth to encompass an estimated majority of South Asian Sunnis by mid-century.[12][10]

Post-1947 Evolution in India and Pakistan

Following the partition of British India on August 14, 1947, the Barelvi movement experienced divergent trajectories in the newly independent India and Pakistan, shaped by demographic shifts, sectarian rivalries, and state policies. While a portion of Barelvi adherents migrated to Pakistan—particularly urban elites and scholars—the majority remained in India, preserving the movement's heartland in Uttar Pradesh around Bareilly. In Pakistan, Barelvis initially held political sway but faced increasing marginalization amid the rise of Deobandi and Ahl-e-Hadith influences.[13][14] In Pakistan, the All India Sunni Conference restructured as Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan (JUP) in 1948, emerging as the dominant Barelvi political organization and advocating for Sufi-oriented policies. The JUP achieved electoral success, such as winning 10 seats in the 1970 East Pakistan assembly elections, but declined after supporting the creation of Bangladesh in 1971, which alienated its base. During Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's military regime (1977–1988), state-backed Islamization programs disproportionately empowered Deobandi institutions through funding and madrasa networks, sidelining Barelvi voices and fostering perceptions of Barelvis as folkloric rather than orthodox. This marginalization intensified sectarian violence, prompting the formation of Sunni Tehreek in 1990 by Muhammad Saleem Qadri to counter Deobandi militants like Sipah-e-Sahaba Pakistan, who targeted Barelvi mosques and shrines; the group engaged in retaliatory attacks, culminating in Qadri's assassination on April 20, 2001, which sparked riots killing over 100 in Karachi alone.[13][15][16] A resurgence occurred with Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP), founded in 2015 by Khadim Hussain Rizvi, emphasizing strict enforcement of blasphemy laws (Pakistan Penal Code Section 295-C, carrying a death penalty) and veneration of the Prophet Muhammad. TLP mobilized mass protests, including the 2017 Faizabad sit-in that paralyzed Islamabad for weeks and forced the resignation of Law Minister Zahid Hamid over perceived insults to the Prophet. In the 2018 general elections, TLP secured 2.2 million votes (4.2% of the total), winning no seats but establishing itself as a populist force among urban poor and rural devotees, often clashing with state authorities—leading to bans in 2021 and 2024 after violent anti-France and anti-Israel demonstrations that resulted in dozens of deaths. TLP's appeal stems from Barelvi devotional networks around shrines, contrasting Deobandi jihadism, though critics note its vigilante tactics exacerbate instability.[17][18][19] In India, the Barelvi movement maintained continuity through its scholarly and devotional infrastructure, with Bareilly remaining a hub for issuing fatwas and training ulema via institutions like Jami'a Ashrafia. Comprising an estimated 60-70% of Indian Sunnis, Barelvis focused on sustaining practices such as milad-un-Nabi processions and shrine veneration, resisting Wahhabi-influenced reforms while navigating secular governance. Politically decentralized, they lacked a unified national party akin to Deobandi-led Jamiat Ulema-e-Hind, often aligning locally with parties like the Samajwadi Party in Uttar Pradesh for shrine protections and anti-communal violence measures; figures like Tauqeer Raza Khan mobilized protests against perceived threats to Muslim sites, forming entities like the World Hindu Federation opposition groups in the 2010s. Barelvis have defended uniform civil code opposition and personal laws through grassroots advocacy but remain underrepresented in bodies like the All India Muslim Personal Law Board, dominated by Deobandis since its 1973 founding.[20][21][22]

Theological Foundations

Jurisprudential and Creedal Framework

The Barelvi movement's jurisprudential framework centers on the Hanafi school of Islamic law, prioritizing taqlid (adherence to established scholarly precedents) and rejecting the independent interpretive approaches favored by 19th-century reformists. Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), the movement's foundational figure, codified this orientation in his Fatawa-e-Razawiyya, a 33-volume compendium of over 13,000 fatwas spanning topics such as ritual prayer (with 1,203 rulings), funerals (273 rulings), and commercial transactions, while weaving in Sufi ethical considerations and responses to contemporary challenges like colonial influences.[23] This work underscores a practical, tradition-bound application of Hanafi fiqh, integrating rational disciplines (ma'qulat) with legal exegesis to preserve devotional practices against perceived puritanical critiques.[1] Creedally, Barelvis align with Sunni orthodox aqidah, defining the essentials (zaruriyat-e-din) as unassailable beliefs derived from definitive Quranic verses, mutawatir hadiths, and scholarly consensus, including tawhid (divine oneness), Muhammad's prophethood, the afterlife, resurrection, and divine decree.[24] They emphasize the Prophet's unique status as nur-e-Muhammadi (Muhammadan light), the primordial creation endowed with knowledge of the unseen granted by God, while rejecting theological innovations such as imkan al-kadhib (the possibility of divine falsehood) and imkan al-nazir (the possibility of a prophet equaling Muhammad), which they attribute to Deobandi scholars as infractions warranting takfir.[1] This creedal stance, articulated in Khan's writings like Subhan al-Subbuh (1900s), positions Barelvis as defenders of traditional Sunni theology against reformist literalism, incorporating kalam rationalism to affirm divine transcendence and prophetic intercession without compromising monotheism.[1]

Integration of Sufi Orders and Mysticism

The Barelvi movement integrates Sufi orders and mysticism as core components of its theological and devotional framework, positioning tasawwuf as inseparable from Sunni orthodoxy. Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), the movement's founder, received initiation into the Qadiri tariqa in 1877 under Sayyid Shah Al-e Rasul of Marehra, whose lineage traces to Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani (d. 1166). This affiliation underscored a hierarchical spiritual transmission emphasizing the pir-murid bond and intercession by awliya Allah.[6] Khan continued guidance under Al-e Rasul's grandson, Nuri Miyan (1839–1906), and later propagated these teachings through his own Rizwiyya branch of the Qadiriyya, blending mystical authority with scholarly output.[6] While rooted in the Qadiriyya, the movement encompasses practitioners from multiple South Asian Sufi orders, including Chishti, Naqshbandi, and Suhrawardi tariqas, reflecting regional syncretism without diluting doctrinal unity. Khan authorized disciples across these silsilas, as evidenced by certificates issued to figures like Sayyid Didar Ali Alwari (1856–1935) in Chishti-Nizami chains, fostering a broad mystical network.[6] This integration defends popular Sufi practices against reformist movements like Deobandism, which critiqued saint veneration as innovation, by framing them as extensions of prophetic sunnah.[25] Mystical elements permeate Barelvi doctrines, elevating the Prophet Muhammad's status through concepts like his haqiqi nur (pre-eternal light) and the awliya's barzakh presence, enabling ongoing spiritual mediation. Practices such as annual urs at dargahs, milad al-nabi gatherings, and gyarahwin shareef (eleventh-day rituals) embody this mysticism, combining dhikr, sama, and communal supplication to cultivate ihsan (spiritual excellence).[6] These observances, institutionalized via madrasas like Manzar al-Islam (founded 1904), reinforce identity against puritanical challenges, with over 80% of Pakistani Sunnis identifying with Barelvi Sufi traditions by the late 20th century.[26]

Core Doctrines

Veneration of the Prophet Muhammad

The Barelvi movement regards veneration of the Prophet Muhammad as a foundational element of Sunni devotion, emphasizing ishq (passionate love) for him as indispensable to true faith and the primary channel for divine mercy. This focus, articulated by founder Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), counters reformist critiques by affirming the Prophet's unparalleled status as the exemplar of creation and the distributor of spiritual blessings (baraka). Khan's theological works, such as fatwas and treatises, defend practices like frequent recitation of salutations (durud and salawat) upon the Prophet, positioning them as acts of emulation and gratitude rather than innovation.[27][28] Khan exemplified this veneration through prolific poetic compositions, including over 100 na'ats (odes) and qasidas (panegyrics) that extol the Prophet's beauty, mercy, and cosmic role, often recited in devotional gatherings to foster emotional attachment. These works draw on classical Sufi traditions, portraying the Prophet not merely as a historical figure but as a living spiritual presence accessible through love and invocation. Barelvi scholars maintain that such devotion aligns with Quranic injunctions to send blessings upon him (Quran 33:56), interpreting it as a means to elevate the believer's proximity to God.[29][30] This veneration manifests in communal practices, including annual Milad-un-Nabi celebrations on 12 Rabi' al-Awwal, involving public processions, illuminated decorations, and collective recitations of the Prophet's attributes (shama'il), which drew millions in South Asia by the early 20th century. Adherents prioritize defending the Prophet's honor, issuing fatwas against perceived insults and mobilizing against blasphemy, as seen in 19th-century polemics against Deobandi reformers whom Khan accused of diminishing the Prophet's majesty. While critics from Salafi and Deobandi perspectives label these expressions as excessive anthropomorphism or near-deification, Barelvi apologists argue they preserve orthodox Sunni affection (muhabba) against puritanical reductionism, citing endorsements from scholars like those in the 1905 "Husam al-Haramayn" fatwa compilation.[31][32][33]

Concepts of Intercession and the Pre-eternal Light

In Barelvi theology, intercession (shafa'ah and tawassul) refers to the permissible seeking of mediation through the Prophet Muhammad and pious saints (awliya) to attain divine favor, mercy, or forgiveness, grounded in interpretations of Quranic verses such as al-Ma'idah 5:35, which instructs believers to "seek the means of approach to Him." Barelvi scholars, led by Ahmad Raza Khan (1856–1921), maintain that the Prophet's intercessory role extends beyond his lifetime, allowing supplications like "O Allah, by the rank of Your Prophet, grant us..." as a form of tawassul bi al-nabi, distinct from direct invocation of intermediaries as deities, which they deem impermissible.[34] This practice forms a core devotional element, often involving visitation to shrines (dargahs) where devotees request saints' intercession as an extension of the Prophet's authority, contrasting with reformist critiques that label it innovation (bid'ah).[35] The doctrine of the Pre-eternal Light (Nur Muhammadi) posits that the Prophet Muhammad's essence existed as a primordial light (nur) before the creation of the physical universe, derived from divine light and serving as the archetype from which all existence emanated.[34] Ahmad Raza Khan articulated this as the haqiqat muhammadiyyah, affirming the Prophet as both human (bashar) and luminous entity, the first creation predating Adam, which underscores his cosmic primacy and enables attributes like universal knowledge.[36] Drawing from Sufi traditions integrated into Hanafi creed, Barelvis cite hadiths such as the Prophet's statement, "I was a prophet while Adam was between water and clay," to support this pre-existence, viewing it as enhancing veneration without compromising tawhid (divine unity).[37] Critics from Deobandi or Salafi perspectives challenge it as anthropomorphic exaggeration, but Barelvis defend it through classical sources like al-Tirmidhi's narrations on prophetic light. These concepts interlink, as the Prophet's luminous pre-eternity bolsters his intercessory efficacy on Judgment Day, where his shafa'ah kubra (greater intercession) saves his ummah, per sahih hadiths in Bukhari.

Muhammad's Knowledge of the Unseen and Role as Witness

In Barelvi theology, the Prophet Muhammad is held to possess ilm al-ghayb (knowledge of the unseen), bestowed upon him by divine grant (ata') from Allah, distinct from and subordinate to Allah's absolute knowledge. This encompasses awareness of past, present, and future events pertinent to his prophetic mission and ummah, derived from Quranic verses such as Surah Al-Jinn (72:26-27), which states that Allah reveals to His Messenger what He wills of the unseen, and hadiths narrating specific instances of prophetic foreknowledge, like the Battle of Badr.[38][39] Barelvi scholars maintain this knowledge enables the Prophet to fulfill his role as a comprehensive guide, countering reformist views that limit it to revealed portions only, which they argue undermines prophetic perfection. Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856-1921), the movement's foundational figure, vigorously defended this doctrine in works such as Subhan al-Subbuh and fatwas issued from Mecca and Medina in 1900-1906, declaring denial of the Prophet's extensive ilm al-ghayb as heretical deviation from Ahl al-Sunnah consensus. He cited endorsements from over 30 Meccan and Medinan scholars affirming the Prophet's granted unseen knowledge, positioning it against Deobandi assertions of mere partial awareness, which Khan viewed as diminishing Muhammad's superiority over other prophets.[40][41] This stance reflects Barelvi emphasis on the Prophet's preeminence, interpreting ilm al-ghayb as essential for intercession (shafa'ah) and spiritual oversight (hadr wa nazr), where the Prophet remains aware of supplications directed to him post-demise. The Prophet's designation as shahid (witness) in Quranic passages like Surah An-Nisa (4:41) and Surah Al-Ahzab (33:45)—"O Prophet, indeed We have sent you as a witness and a bringer of good tidings and a warner"—is interpreted by Barelvis to necessitate ilm al-ghayb for effective testimony over the ummah on the Day of Judgment. They argue that witnessing deeds requires knowing hidden actions and intentions, supported by hadiths such as Sahih Bukhari (Vol. 4, Book 56, Hadith 735), where the Prophet states he will bear witness against his community based on observed and reported realities extended through divine insight. Opponents, including Salafi and Deobandi critiques, contend this conflates human prophethood with divine omniscience, but Barelvis uphold it as orthodox Hanafi-Maturidi creed, evidenced by classical texts like Imam Ahmad Raza's endorsements of earlier scholars like Shah Waliullah Dehlawi's qualified affirmations.[39][38] This doctrine underscores the Prophet's ongoing role in divine accountability, linking unseen knowledge to his intercessory authority without equating it to Allah's exclusive preserve.

Affirmation of the Finality of Prophethood

The Barelvi movement doctrinally affirms the finality of prophethood, interpreting the Quranic verse "Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but the Messenger of Allah and the Seal of the Prophets" (33:40) as establishing Muhammad as the last prophet, after whom no new prophet—legislative or non-legislative—can arise. This stance aligns with classical Sunni exegesis, emphasizing khatam an-nabiyyin as an absolute temporal and qualitative closure to revelation, rejecting any subordinate or ummatic prophethood post-Muhammad.[42] Barelvi scholars, drawing from Hanafi-Ash'ari traditions, view this finality as integral to tawhid and the perfection of the sharia, with deviations constituting kufr.[9] Founder Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), known as Ala Hazrat, exemplified this affirmation through fatwas against Mirza Ghulam Ahmad (1835–1908), the Ahmadiyya founder who claimed prophethood and messiahship, declaring him a kafir and dajjal (antichrist) as early as 1891 for violating the seal of prophethood.[43] [44] Khan's 1906 treatise Husam al-Haramayn, while primarily refuting Deobandi statements on prophetic attributes, reinforces the uncompromised finality by arguing against any diminishment of Muhammad's unique status, which could implicitly open doors to post-prophetic claims.[45] This work, endorsed by over 30 Hijazi scholars, underscores Barelvi commitment to defending khatm-e-nabuwwat against reformist dilutions.[46] In practice, this doctrine has fueled Barelvi-led initiatives, such as the Majlis-e-Tahaffuz-e-Khatme Nabuwwat (formed post-1947 in Pakistan), which mobilizes to safeguard the belief through legal and clerical advocacy, including support for the 1974 constitutional amendment declaring Ahmadis non-Muslims. Modern expressions include Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP), founded in 2015, which has protested electoral laws perceived to undermine finality, such as provisions allowing Ahmadi votes, framing such issues as existential threats to Islamic orthodoxy.[27] Barelvis distinguish their position from Deobandi counterparts by accusing the latter of ambiguous phrasing on prophetic knowledge that risks blurring finality, while both reject Ahmadiyya unequivocally.[47] This affirmation remains a rallying point, evidenced by Barelvi backing of Pakistan's blasphemy laws tied to prophethood's sanctity since the 1980s.[48]

Religious Practices

Devotional Rituals and Celebrations

![International Mawlid Conference at Minar-e-Pakistan, Lahore][float-right] The Barelvi movement emphasizes devotional rituals centered on expressing profound love (ishq) for the Prophet Muhammad and veneration of Sufi saints through communal celebrations. These practices, rooted in South Asian Sunni traditions, include annual observances such as Milad-un-Nabi and Urs, which feature recitations, music, and gatherings to foster spiritual connection and communal solidarity. Such rituals distinguish Barelvis from reformist groups like Deobandis, who often critique them as innovations (bid'ah).[20] Milad-un-Nabi, commemorating the Prophet's birth on 12 Rabi' al-Awwal (approximately March in the Gregorian calendar), involves mosque and home gatherings where participants recite the Quran, deliver sermons on the Prophet's life (seerah), and perform naat khwani—devotional poetry praising Muhammad. Public processions (juloos) with banners, green flags, and illuminated replicas of the Kaaba are common, particularly in urban areas of Pakistan and India, evolving into large-scale events that blend piety with socio-political expression.[49][50] In Lahore, for example, the Minhaj-ul-Quran-organized International Mawlid Conference draws thousands for multi-day programs including scholarly discourses and naat recitals. Urs celebrations mark the union (wisal) of saints with God on their death anniversaries, held at dargahs with rituals like qawwali (Sufi devotional music), fatiha (prayers for the deceased), and langar (communal feasts). Offerings of rosewater, incense, and embroidered sheets (chadar) adorn shrines, while sama sessions invoke spiritual ecstasy. The annual Urs-e-Razvi of Ahmad Raza Khan at Bareilly Sharif Dargah, observed from 24 to 26 Safar (around late October to early November), features extensive qawwali, naat competitions, and charity distributions, attracting pilgrims nationwide.[51][52] Other rituals include weekly or monthly milad gatherings in homes or madrasas, emphasizing salawat (blessings on the Prophet) and durud recitations, often accompanied by sweets distribution to symbolize joy. These practices, defended by Barelvi scholars like Ahmad Raza Khan through fatwas affirming their permissibility within Hanafi-Sufi frameworks, reinforce doctrinal commitments to intercession and prophetic excellence.[53]

Shrine Visitation and Saint Veneration

The Barelvi movement emphasizes pilgrimage to shrines (dargahs or mazaars) of Sufi saints (awliya Allah), where adherents seek barakah (spiritual blessings) and tawassul (intercession) through the saints' enduring spiritual influence. Practitioners believe the souls of these pious figures maintain a presence capable of facilitating divine aid, supplications made at tombs invoking their karamat (miraculous powers) for personal needs like healing or prosperity. This custom draws from Hanafi jurisprudence and Sufi traditions, with rituals including recitation of Surah Fatiha, lighting incense or lamps, offering chadars (cloths draped over tombs), and distributing food (langar) to the poor.[54][55] Such visitations occur routinely but intensify during annual Urs observances, marking the saint's union with the divine on their death anniversary, often spanning three days with continuous dhikr (remembrance of God) and naat recitations.[56] Urs festivals at Barelvi-favored shrines, such as Bareilly Sharif or Lahore's Data Darbar, feature qawwali (devotional music), sama (spiritual listening sessions), and communal prayers, drawing hundreds of thousands to millions of participants annually. These events reinforce social bonds and doctrinal commitments, with Barelvi scholars issuing fatwas affirming their permissibility based on precedents from early Sunni authorities like Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, who endorsed seeking proximity to the righteous. Critics from Deobandi or Salafi circles label these acts as shirk (polytheism) or bid'ah (innovation), prompting Barelvi defenses emphasizing distinction between veneration (ta'zim) and worship (ibadah) reserved solely for God.[57][1] In Pakistan, where Barelvis constitute a majority of Sunnis, shrine economies thrive on pilgrim donations, funding madrasas and charitable works, though attacks on sites like Data Darbar in 2010 highlight sectarian tensions.[32][58] Saint veneration extends to living pirs (spiritual guides) and historical figures like Abdul Qadir Jilani or Moinuddin Chishti, whose shrines serve as educational hubs for Barelvi theology. Adherents perform esaal-e-thawab (transferring rewards of deeds) at tombs, believing it benefits the deceased saint's ummah (community). This folk-infused piety, blending orthodox Sunni creed with mystical elements, sustains Barelvi identity amid reformist challenges, with surveys indicating higher shrine attendance among Barelvis compared to rival groups in South Asia.[59][60]

Liturgical and Communal Observances

Barelvis maintain liturgical observances rooted in the Hanafi school of jurisprudence, emphasizing taqlid (conformity to established scholarly interpretations) in the performance of salah (obligatory prayers). Key distinctions include tying the hands below the navel during the standing posture (qiyam) and restricting the raising of hands (raf' al-yadayn) solely to the opening takbir, practices derived from classical Hanafi texts such as those of Imam Abu Hanifa (d. 767 CE). These elements underscore a commitment to ritual precision as a means of spiritual discipline, often supplemented by additional sunnah prayers and post-salah supplications invoking blessings upon the Prophet Muhammad.[61] Communal observances prominently feature collective dhikr sessions, frequently conducted aloud (dhikr jalli) in mosques after congregational prayers, especially Jumu'ah. Participants gather in circles to recite salawat, Qur'anic verses, and litanies in unison, fostering spiritual elevation and group solidarity; Barelvi ulama, drawing from Sufi traditions, affirm this as permissible and meritorious, citing precedents in the practices of early pious predecessors.[62] Such majalis (assemblies) extend to weekly or lunar observances like Giyarwee Shareef on the 11th of each Islamic month, dedicated to commemorating Abdul Qadir Jilani (d. 1166 CE) through fatiha recitations and naat khwani (hymns praising the Prophet). These gatherings reinforce doctrinal emphases on intercession and veneration while serving as venues for social cohesion among adherents.[63] Fatiha rituals, involving the communal recitation of Surah al-Fatiha followed by salawat and distribution of food, mark lifecycle events such as the third, tenth, and fortieth days after a death, as well as annual remembrances. Performed in homes, mosques, or near graves, these acts aim to convey spiritual rewards to the deceased via intermediaries, aligning with Barelvi beliefs in the efficacy of such transmissions; critics from reformist schools deem them innovations, but proponents substantiate them through chains of transmission in Sufi and Hanafi sources.[9]

Geographical Spread and Demographics

Presence in India

The Barelvi movement originated in Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh, in the late 19th century under the leadership of Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), and remains deeply rooted there as its spiritual and intellectual center.[22] The city hosts the Dargah Ala Hazrat, the mausoleum of Khan, which serves as a major pilgrimage site attracting millions annually for devotional practices aligned with Barelvi traditions.[22] This location symbolizes the movement's emphasis on saint veneration and Sufi piety, drawing adherents from across northern India. Barelvis form the predominant school among India's Sunni Muslims, estimated to comprise more than two-thirds of the country's approximately 172 million Muslims.[20] Their influence is strongest in rural and semi-urban areas of Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and West Bengal, where high Muslim concentrations—such as 19.26% in Uttar Pradesh and 17% in Bihar—align with traditional Sufi-Barelvi practices over reformist alternatives.[20] These regions feature extensive networks of Barelvi-affiliated madrasas and mosques that propagate Hanafi jurisprudence infused with devotional elements, sustaining the movement's grassroots presence amid India's diverse Islamic landscape. Key educational institutions bolster this footprint, including Jami'ah Ashrafiyyah in Mubarakpur, Uttar Pradesh, a prominent seminary training scholars in Barelvi theology and jurisprudence.[64] Other centers, such as those in Bihar, emphasize milad celebrations and shrine visitations, reinforcing communal ties. The movement's adaptability to local customs has ensured its dominance in shrine-based piety, evident at sites like Ajmer Sharif Dargah in Rajasthan, where Barelvi rituals predominate despite broader Sufi appeal.[20]

Presence in Pakistan

The Barelvi movement maintains a substantial presence in Pakistan, where adherents comprise an estimated 50 to 60 percent of the country's Muslim population, making it the largest Sunni sub-tradition.[48] [65] This demographic weight is particularly pronounced in Punjab and Sindh provinces, where Barelvi practices align with longstanding Sufi traditions and shrine-based devotion, contrasting with Deobandi influence in more urban or northwestern areas.[66] Post-partition migration in 1947 significantly strengthened the movement, as numerous scholars and followers from Uttar Pradesh and other Indian regions resettled in Pakistan, establishing networks of madrasas and organizations.[66] Key institutions include Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan (JUP), formed in 1948 from the pre-partition All India Sunni Conference, which has historically represented Barelvi interests and operates educational seminaries across the country.[13] Other prominent groups encompass Dawat-e-Islami, led by Ilyas Qadri since 1981, focusing on devotional propagation and international outreach from its Karachi base; and Minhaj-ul-Quran International, founded by Muhammad Tahir-ul-Qadri in 1981 in Lahore, which runs schools, universities, and welfare programs emphasizing Barelvi theology.[67] Barelvi influence manifests through control of major Sufi shrines, such as Data Darbar in Lahore dedicated to Ali Hujwiri (d. 1077 CE), which attracts millions annually for urs celebrations and serves as a cultural and religious hub.[61] The movement's madrasas, numbering in the thousands, propagate Hanafi jurisprudence infused with Sufi elements, training clerics who lead communal prayers and milad gatherings in rural and semi-urban settings.[4] Despite institutional fragmentation compared to Deobandi networks, Barelvi popularity endures via grassroots devotional practices and alliances like the Sunni Ittehad Council, underscoring its embedded role in Pakistan's Sunni landscape.[67]

Presence in Other Regions

The Barelvi movement maintains a significant presence in the United Kingdom, primarily through post-World War II migration from Pakistani regions like Mirpur in Azad Kashmir and Punjabi areas, where Barelvi traditions predominate among Sunni Muslims.[68] As of 2017, Barelvi-affiliated mosques accounted for 459 out of surveyed UK mosques, reflecting control over communal spaces amid competition with Deobandi groups.[69] Organizations linked to Barelvi thought, such as those advocating Khatme Nabuwwat (finality of prophethood), have operated in the UK since at least 1990, often mobilizing against perceived blasphemy.[70] In South Africa, Barelvi influences persist among the Urdu-speaking Indian Muslim diaspora, who arrived during the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with adherents drawn to Sufi tariqas and traditional practices distinguishing them from Deobandi counterparts.[71] Prominent scholars, including Shafi Okarvi, have propagated Barelvi teachings through preaching tours and institutions aimed at preserving South Asian Sunni customs.[72] This community supports groups issuing religious rulings aligned with Barelvi jurisprudence, underscoring a foothold in African Islamic networks.[72] Bangladesh hosts a Barelvi segment within its Hanafi Sunni majority, often under the Ahl-e-Sunnat wal Jama'at banner, alongside Sufi-oriented groups in urban centers like Dhaka, Chittagong, and Sylhet.[73] These followers emphasize devotional practices akin to South Asian Barelvi norms, though they face rivalry from Deobandi and reformist influences.[74] Smaller Barelvi communities exist in Western countries like the United States and Canada via professional and family migration from Pakistan and India, sustaining mosques and cultural associations, while limited outreach occurs in Gulf states through labor migrants.[9] Overall, diaspora expansion relies on familial ties and resistance to reformist currents, with numbers tied to South Asian migration patterns rather than independent growth.[75]

Inter-Movement Relations

Rivalry with Deobandi and Wahhabi-Influenced Groups

The Barelvi movement emerged in the late 19th century partly as a response to the reformist Deobandi school, founded in 1866 at Deoband, India, which sought to purify Islamic practice by curtailing certain Sufi customs prevalent among South Asian Sunnis, such as elaborate shrine rituals and intercession through saints. Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), the movement's foundational figure, accused Deobandi scholars of undermining the Prophet Muhammad's unique status through writings that he interpreted as diminutive, leading to mutual declarations of heresy. In 1906, Khan compiled Husam al-Haramayn, a fatwa collection pronouncing takfir (excommunication as unbelievers) on over 20 Deobandi ulama, including prominent figures like Rashid Ahmad Gangohi and Ashraf Ali Thanvi, for alleged blasphemous statements; this was endorsed by several Hijazi scholars, intensifying the schism. Deobandi leaders countered with rebuttals, labeling Barelvi veneration practices as shirk (polytheism), thus framing the rivalry as a contest between "pure" scriptural Islam and folk-influenced devotionalism.[1][20] Theological divergences extended to Wahhabi-influenced groups, such as South Asia's Ahl-e-Hadith movement, which espouses strict tawhid (monotheism) rejecting saint intercession, tomb visitation, and celebratory milad-un-Nabi gatherings—practices central to Barelvi identity. Barelvis positioned themselves against Wahhabi literalism, viewing it as an alien Arabian import eroding indigenous Sufi traditions, while Deobandis, though Hanafi in jurisprudence, adopted reformist elements akin to Wahhabism, such as skepticism toward mystical excesses, fueling Barelvi perceptions of a coordinated puritanical threat. This opposition manifested in Khan's broader fatwas condemning Wahhabi figures alongside Deobandis for denying the Prophet's knowledge of the unseen (ilm al-ghayb), a belief Barelvis uphold to affirm his preeminence. By the early 20th century, these disputes evolved from scholarly polemics into institutional competition, with Barelvi madrasas like those affiliated with Tanzeem-ul-Madaris countering Deobandi networks influenced by Saudi-funded Wahhabi outreach.[14][1] In post-Partition Pakistan, where Barelvis constitute an estimated 50-60% of Sunnis but Deobandis hold disproportionate institutional sway through seminaries (over 12,000 Deobandi-affiliated out of 20,000 total madrasas as of the early 2000s), the rivalry escalated into sporadic violence, particularly from the 1970s onward amid pamphlet wars and clashes over mosque control. Deobandi-linked militants, including Taliban factions, targeted Barelvi shrines—such as the 2010 bombing at Lahore's Data Darbar shrine, killing 42, attributed to Punjabi Taliban with Deobandi ideology—condemning them as idolatrous, while Barelvis retaliated by accusing Deobandis of Wahhabi-inspired iconoclasm. Saudi funding amplified Wahhabi-Deobandi synergies, funding mosques and texts that critiqued Barelvi "superstitions," prompting Barelvi resistance campaigns; yet, internal Barelvi fragmentation among pirs (spiritual heirs) limited unified pushback compared to Deobandi organizational discipline. This antagonism persists, with both sides issuing fatwas of kufr, though empirical data on casualties shows Deobandi extremists perpetrating more shrine attacks, reflecting their militant infrastructure's edge over Barelvi devotional focus.[76][77][66]

Alliances and Conflicts in Sunni Unity Efforts

The Barelvi movement has engaged in selective alliances within the Sunni spectrum, primarily consolidating Barelvi-dominated groups to counter reformist Sunni factions like Deobandis and Ahl-e-Hadith, whom they accuse of deviating from traditional Sunni practices such as shrine veneration and Milad celebrations. These efforts emphasize Barelvi claims to represent authentic Ahl-e-Sunnat wal Jamaat, often excluding groups perceived as influenced by Wahhabi or Deobandi ideologies that reject Sufi customs. In Pakistan, where Barelvi influence is demographically significant, such initiatives have focused on political organization and anti-extremism campaigns, though broader Sunni unity remains elusive due to doctrinal schisms originating in 19th-century polemics between Ahmed Raza Khan and Deobandi founders.[78] A foundational alliance emerged with the establishment of Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan (JUP) on March 28, 1948, in Multan, initiated by Barelvi scholars from the pre-partition All India Sunni Conference to provide political voice to traditional Sunnis amid rising Deobandi dominance in institutions like madrasas. The JUP advocated for Sunni unity under a single Ameer of Ahl-e-Sunnat, positioning itself against perceived threats from Deobandi-aligned parties like Jamiat Ulema-e-Islam, but it suffered from factionalism, limiting its electoral impact to sporadic seats in provincial assemblies.[12][79] In response to escalating sectarian violence and Taliban influence post-2001, Barelvi leaders formed the Sunni Ittehad Council (SIC) in May 2009 as an umbrella of eight Sunni organizations, launching the Save Pakistan Movement to mobilize against "Talibanisation" and label the Taliban a national security threat. The SIC, led by figures like Sarfraz Naeemi (assassinated in 2009 for anti-Taliban fatwas), sought to unify Barelvi resources for peace advocacy and electoral alliances, later gaining 73 general seats in the National Assembly through partnerships like with PTI in 2024 elections. However, the assassination of Sunni Tehreek leaders in 2006 disrupted potential grand Barelvi coalitions, exacerbating vulnerabilities to Deobandi-linked militancy.[80][17] Conflicts persist as Barelvi unity bids clash with Deobandi refusal to endorse Sufi rituals, leading to mutual excommunications and violence, such as the 2006 Sipah-e-Sahaba attacks on Barelvi gatherings and reciprocal Barelvi mobilization via groups like Sunni Tehreek. These tensions undermine pan-Sunni platforms, with Barelvis prioritizing defense of prophetic traditions over compromise, as evidenced by failed reconciliation attempts over issues like Urs observances. In India, analogous efforts through bodies like the All India Sunni Jamiyatul Ulema face similar intra-Sunni fragmentation, prioritizing local shrine networks over national unity.[9][1]

Interactions with Shia and Other Sects

The Barelvi movement, rooted in Sunni Hanafi jurisprudence and Sufi traditions, maintains doctrinal opposition to core Shia tenets such as the infallible Imamate and the superiority of Ali ibn Abi Talib over the first three caliphs, as articulated by founder Ahmed Raza Khan in his fatwas and writings condemning perceived innovations in Shia theology.[81] Despite these theological divergences, Barelvis exhibit greater practical tolerance toward Shia communities compared to Deobandi or Ahl-e-Hadith groups, sharing affinities in devotional practices like shrine veneration, intercession through saints (or imams in Shia context), and mystical elements of piety.[82] This convergence has fostered amicable relations, particularly in South Asia, where Barelvi Sufism's emphasis on folk Islam and emotional devotion overlaps with Shia rituals, reducing overt sectarian friction.[66] In Pakistan, Barelvi and Shia groups have periodically formed tactical alliances against shared threats from Deobandi-led extremism and Taliban insurgency, exemplified by joint participation in anti-terrorism initiatives and inter-sect dialogues aimed at curbing violence.[83] For instance, in 2014, Barelvi organizations collaborated with Shia representatives in platforms opposing Taliban advances, highlighting mutual interests in preserving shrine-based worship and countering Wahhabi-influenced iconoclasm.[84] Such cooperation underscores a pragmatic sectarianism, where Barelvis prioritize defense of their devotional traditions over doctrinal purity in political contexts, though underlying Sunni-Shia divides persist and occasionally surface in theological polemics.[85] Regarding other sects, Barelvis vehemently reject the Ahmadiyya movement's claims of prophethood for Mirza Ghulam Ahmad, with Ahmed Raza Khan issuing fatwas declaring Ahmadis outside the fold of Islam and supporting legislative efforts to classify them as non-Muslims, as seen in Pakistan's 1974 constitutional amendment backed by Barelvi-led parties.[86][13] This stance aligns with broader Sunni orthodoxy, viewing Ahmadiyya as a heretical innovation that undermines the finality of Muhammad's prophethood. In contrast, relations with other Sunni Sufi orders, such as the Qadiriyya (from which Barelvi draws heavily), remain cordial, with shared networks of pirs and madrasas facilitating mutual recognition among transnational Sufi scholars.[87] Tensions occasionally arise with rival Sufi lineages like certain Chishti factions over interpretive differences in saint veneration, but these rarely escalate to systemic exclusion within the broader Ahl al-Sunnah framework.[88]

Political and Social Activism

Historical Campaigns Against Hindu Revivalism

The Barelvi movement, rooted in the teachings of Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), mounted theological and organizational resistance to Hindu revivalist initiatives that sought to undermine Muslim identity in British India, particularly the Arya Samaj's Shuddhi campaign launched in the late 19th century to reconvert Muslims to Hinduism. Khan's writings and fatwas explicitly rejected Hindu influences on Islamic practices, viewing them as dilutions of tawhid (monotheism) and threats to doctrinal purity; he condemned participation in interfaith alliances like the Indian National Congress, which he regarded as Hindu-dominated and incompatible with Islamic separatism. This stance aligned with broader Barelvi efforts to fortify Sunni orthodoxy against external pressures, emphasizing the veneration of the Prophet Muhammad and Sufi traditions as bulwarks against assimilation.[89] A key institutional response emerged through the Jama'at Raza-e-Mustafa, established under the leadership of Khan's son, Mustafa Raza Khan Qadri (1892–1981), which conducted grassroots propagation in northern Indian towns and villages during the 1920s and 1930s to counter Shuddhi proselytization. Operating primarily in Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, the group disseminated fatwas, held public discourses, and mobilized communities against reconversion drives intensified by Arya Samaj activists following events like the 1921 Moplah Rebellion, where thousands of Muslims faced coerced purification rites. These campaigns framed Shuddhi not merely as religious competition but as an existential assault on Islam, urging Muslims to reject Vedic revivalism and reaffirm allegiance to Hanafi jurisprudence and saintly intercession.[90][91] Barelvi activism contributed to the polarization of communal lines, influencing support for the All-India Muslim League's demand for separate electorates in the 1909 Morley-Minto Reforms and later Pakistan, as Khan's fatwas prohibited electoral pacts with "unbelievers" that could subordinate Muslims to Hindu majorities. While Deobandi counterparts focused on puritanical reforms, Barelvis uniquely blended anti-revivalist fervor with devotional mobilization, including milad gatherings repurposed for doctrinal defense, thereby sustaining mass adherence amid revivalist incursions estimated to have targeted over 100,000 Muslims in Punjab and United Provinces by the 1930s. This era's efforts underscored Barelvi prioritization of cultural preservation over political quietism, though they drew criticism for exacerbating Hindu-Muslim tensions.[89]

Defense of Blasphemy Laws and Prophethood Issues

The Barelvi movement places profound emphasis on safeguarding the honor of the Prophet Muhammad, viewing any form of blasphemy—defined as insult or disrespect toward him—as a grave offense warranting severe legal and social repercussions. This stance stems from core theological convictions, including the Prophet's unparalleled status as Khatam an-Nabiyyin (Seal of the Prophets), which Barelvis interpret as affirming Muhammad's finality in prophethood and prohibiting subsequent prophetic claims. Founder Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi articulated this in his 1906 fatwa collection Husam al-Haramayn, a refutation of Mirza Ghulam Ahmad's Ahmadiyya claims to prophethood, which Khan deemed a direct assault on Islamic orthodoxy and mobilized scholars from Mecca and Medina to endorse.[86][48] Barelvis have consistently advocated for robust blasphemy legislation, particularly in Pakistan, where they interpret such laws as essential extensions of Sharia to deter offenses against prophethood. In 2000, when General Pervez Musharraf proposed reviewing blasphemy provisions under Sections 295-B and 295-C of the Pakistan Penal Code—which prescribe life imprisonment or death for insulting the Quran or Prophet—Barelvi groups mounted widespread protests, framing amendments as concessions to Western pressures and dilutions of faith.[9] This defense aligns with their broader opposition to Ahmadiyya beliefs, supporting the 1974 constitutional amendment declaring Ahmadis non-Muslims for denying the finality of prophethood, a position Barelvis reinforced through scholarly consensus and public campaigns.[48] The movement's activism extends to vigilante responses and mass mobilizations against perceived blasphemers, rooted in a causal view that unchecked insults erode communal reverence for the Prophet's sanctity. Barelvi scholars argue that blasphemy undermines the foundational aqeedah (creed) of Sunni Islam, particularly their veneration of the Prophet's intercessory role and noor (light), prompting calls for immediate extrajudicial action in extreme cases, though formal endorsement prioritizes state-enforced penalties.[92] Critics, including some reformist Muslims, contend this absolutism overlooks classical Islamic jurisprudence's nuances, such as Ibn Taymiyyah's distinctions on punishment, but Barelvis maintain their position reflects unadulterated adherence to prophetic traditions over modern reinterpretations.[93][32]

Rise of Militant Barelvi Politics via TLP

The Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP) was established in August 2015 by Khadim Hussain Rizvi, a prominent Barelvi cleric from Punjab, in response to perceived dilutions of Pakistan's blasphemy laws and challenges to the finality of the Prophet Muhammad's prophethood. Rizvi, previously known for his fiery sermons at the Jamia Masjid Rehmatullil Alameen in Lahore, capitalized on Barelvi grievances over incidents like the 2011 assassination of Punjab Governor Salman Taseer, who had criticized the laws, and subsequent legal reforms that Barelvis viewed as concessions to secular or minority interests. Unlike traditional Barelvi organizations focused on devotional practices and shrine-based mobilization, TLP emphasized street-level enforcement of religious orthodoxy, positioning itself as the vanguard against "blasphemers" and state complicity in undermining Islamic honor.[17][94][95] TLP's ascent accelerated through mass protests, beginning with the 2017 Faizabad interchange sit-in, where thousands of supporters blockaded a key highway linking Rawalpindi to Islamabad starting November 8, demanding the resignation of federal officials over an election oath amendment interpreted as equivocal on prophethood's finality. The 20-day blockade, involving armed cadres and sustained by logistical networks of mosques and madrasas, paralyzed transport and exposed government vulnerabilities, culminating in the ouster of the law minister and railway minister on November 27. This success, achieved without significant state crackdown, demonstrated TLP's ability to leverage Barelvi grassroots loyalty—rooted in Sufi veneration of the Prophet—into coercive political power, marking a departure from the movement's historically apolitical or accommodationist stance toward Deobandi-dominated rivals.[96][97] The party's influence peaked following the Supreme Court's October 31, 2018, acquittal of Asia Bibi, a Christian woman convicted of blasphemy, triggering nationwide TLP-led shutdowns that halted economic activity, torched vehicles, and resulted in at least 13 deaths amid clashes with security forces. Protests, coordinated from over 100 locations, demanded Bibi's execution and blocked major cities, forcing the government to sign a November 2 agreement conceding to TLP demands, including no opposition to private appeals for her execution and probes into judges involved. In the preceding July 25 general elections, TLP secured approximately 2.2 million votes (4.2% nationally), winning two National Assembly seats and several provincial ones before disqualifications, underscoring its rapid mobilization of Barelvi voters disillusioned with mainstream parties.[98][99][100] This trajectory introduced militancy into Barelvi politics by normalizing vigilante actions, such as attacks on individuals accused of insulting the Prophet and confrontations with police using sticks, stones, and occasional firearms, framing them as defensive jihad for religious sanctity. TLP's rhetoric, blending populist appeals to the urban poor and rural shrine networks with threats of excommunication against opponents, radicalized segments of the Barelvi base—traditionally tolerant of folk Sufism—toward intolerance of dissent, as evidenced by post-2018 incidents of mob violence against minorities and state symbols. Government responses, including bans attempted in 2020 and 2021 but often reversed via negotiated amnesties, inadvertently legitimized TLP's model, shifting Barelvi activism from cultural preservation to a confrontational force rivaling Deobandi militants in disruptive capacity, though distinct in its non-globalist, Pakistan-centric focus.[18][101][102]

Stances on Extremism and Persecution

Opposition to Jihadist Terrorism

Barelvi scholars have issued prominent religious edicts condemning terrorism as incompatible with Islamic principles, emphasizing the movement's doctrinal opposition to violence that desecrates sacred sites and targets civilians. In March 2010, Muhammad Tahir-ul-Qadri, a leading Barelvi jurist, released a 600-page fatwa explicitly denouncing terrorism, suicide bombings, and the ideologies of groups like Al-Qaeda, declaring such acts as kufr (disbelief) and major sins rather than forms of jihad.[103] This ruling, disseminated through his organization Minhaj-ul-Quran International, has been cited as a key counter-narrative to jihadist recruitment, with Qadri arguing that true Islam prohibits harming innocents and promotes peace.[103] In Pakistan, where Barelvi communities have faced direct attacks from Taliban militants on Sufi shrines and gatherings, organized resistance has included alliances of over 50 Barelvi groups demanding that the government classify Taliban extremists as non-Muslims and dismantle their networks.[84] Following the 2014 Peshawar school massacre and shrine bombings, Barelvi leaders joined broader Sunni efforts, proclaiming February 28 as "Go Taliban Go" day to rally against Tehrik-e-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) and its affiliates, framing their ideology as a distortion of Sunni tradition influenced by Deobandi and Wahhabi puritanism.[104][84] Cleric Sarfraz Ahmed Naeemi, a vocal Barelvi critic of suicide bombings, was assassinated by a Taliban suicide bomber in Lahore on June 12, 2009, highlighting the movement's targeted vulnerability and resolve against jihadist aggression. Large-scale fatwas have reinforced this stance; during the 97th Urs of Barelvi founder Ahmed Raza Khan in Bareilly, India, on December 6, 2015, approximately 70,000 clerics issued a collective decree against terrorism, supported by over 1.5 million Muslims, declaring it a grave sin and urging global Muslim unity against extremists.[105] These pronouncements underscore Barelvi prioritization of spiritual devotion and prophetic love over militant interpretations of jihad, positioning the movement as a bulwark against Salafi-jihadist ideologies that reject Sufi practices central to Barelvi identity.[105]

Experiences of Sectarian Violence

The Barelvi movement has experienced recurrent sectarian violence in Pakistan, primarily from Deobandi-affiliated militants such as the Tehrik-e-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) and Lashkar-e-Jhangvi, as well as Salafi-jihadist groups like Islamic State Khorasan Province (ISKP), who target Barelvi practices of shrine visitation and saint veneration as idolatrous deviations from strict monotheism.[106][107] These attacks intensified after the 2001 U.S.-led invasion of Afghanistan empowered cross-border jihadist networks, with Barelvi-dominated regions in Punjab and Sindh seeing shrine bombings and assassinations of spiritual leaders (pirs).[108] A prominent example occurred on July 1, 2010, when two suicide bombers struck the Data Darbar shrine in Lahore, a key Barelvi pilgrimage site honoring the Sufi saint Ali Hujwiri; the blasts killed 42 devotees and injured 175 others, with the TTP claiming responsibility to punish perceived polytheism.[109][110] Similarly, on February 16, 2017, a suicide bomber detonated at the Lal Shahbaz Qalandar shrine in Sehwan, Sindh, during a devotional gathering, killing 88 people—including women and children—and wounding over 250; ISKP publicly took credit, framing the assault as jihad against "polytheists" who honor the 13th-century Sufi poet-saint.[111][112][113] In addition to shrine attacks, Barelvi leaders faced targeted killings, particularly in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and former Federally Administered Tribal Areas, where TTP operatives assassinated dozens of pirs and clerics opposing jihadist ideology; for instance, between 2007 and 2010, multiple anti-Taliban fatwas issued by Barelvi councils prompted retaliatory murders of prominent figures like Pir Sahib of Zakori Sharif.[114][115] These incidents contributed to a broader pattern, with over 20 shrine assaults recorded since 2005 resulting in hundreds of Barelvi casualties, eroding community confidence in public worship and prompting some defensive mobilizations.[109][108]

Responses to State Policies and Persecution

The Barelvi movement in Pakistan has frequently mobilized against state policies perceived as diluting Islamic orthodoxy, particularly those affecting blasphemy laws and the finality of prophethood (Khatam-e-Nabuwat). Groups like Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP), founded in 2015, have led mass protests accusing governments of concessions to secularism or minority rights that undermine Sharia principles.[17][108] In 2006, Barelvi leaders opposed the Musharraf government's Protection of Women Bill, viewing its reforms to Hudood ordinances as enabling non-Muslim influence over Muslim family law, resulting in widespread street demonstrations that forced partial concessions.[9] From 2017 onward, TLP's Faizabad sit-in paralyzed highways for weeks, protesting an elections act amendment that altered the oath affirming Muhammad's final prophethood, which demonstrators claimed facilitated Ahmadis' political participation.[4] The protests escalated into clashes with security forces, killing at least 10 and injuring hundreds, before a mediated agreement reinstated stricter oath language and dismissed the law minister.[116] Similar mobilizations occurred in 2021 over caricatures of the Prophet Muhammad, with TLP demanding the French ambassador's expulsion; these involved marches from Lahore to Islamabad, road blockades, and attacks on police, culminating in a government deal lifting prior bans on the party and releasing its leader Saad Rizvi.[117] In response to state crackdowns, Barelvi activists have framed bans and arrests as persecution akin to that faced from Deobandi militants, alleging official bias toward rival sects funded by Saudi influence.[92] The Pakistani government banned TLP in 2018 under anti-terror laws following assassination attempts on officials, lifting it in 2021 amid negotiations but reimposing restrictions.[48] On October 23, 2025, the cabinet banned TLP again after protests from October 9 demanding action on Gaza and anti-blasphemy measures turned violent in Lahore and Faisalabad, with over 1,000 arrests and clashes killing several.[118][119] Barelvi responses emphasize reclaiming space lost to state-sponsored Deobandi networks, using shrine-based mobilization and fatwas to rally millions against perceived apostasy in policy.[13] In India, Barelvi responses to state policies have centered on resisting Hindu nationalist measures, such as the 2019 Citizenship Amendment Act, through alliances with other Muslim groups and defenses of syncretic practices against uniform civil code proposals.[120] However, lacking TLP's militant edge, these have manifested in scholarly critiques and localized protests rather than nationwide upheaval, reflecting the movement's historical adaptation to pluralistic governance post-Partition.[121]

Criticisms and Debates

Reformist Charges of Innovation and Polytheism

Reformist scholars from the Deobandi and Ahl-i Hadith traditions have accused the Barelvi movement of introducing bid'ah (religious innovations) and committing shirk (polytheism or associating partners with God), particularly through practices emphasizing intense devotion to the Prophet Muhammad and Sufi saints. These charges stem from a stricter adherence to tawhid (monotheism) and the emulation of early Muslim practices, viewing Barelvi rituals as deviations that risk elevating created beings to near-divine status or inventing unsubstantiated customs.[77][122] A primary allegation of bid'ah targets the celebration of Mawlid al-Nabi (the Prophet's birthday), which Deobandis condemn as an illicit innovation resembling obligatory worship or feasting not evidenced in the Prophet's era or the salaf (righteous predecessors). Similarly, urs commemorations at saints' tombs—involving night-long Qur'an recitations, poetry, and ritual washing of graves—are critiqued as unauthorized additions that foster superstition over scriptural fidelity.[77][122] On shirk, reformists highlight Barelvi concepts like Hadir wa Nazir (the Prophet's spiritual omnipresence and observation) and the Nur Muhammadi (Muhammad as primordial light), arguing these attribute divine omniscience or pre-eternal existence to the Prophet, blurring the creator-creation distinction. Seeking tawassul (intercession) via saints' graves or invoking them with phrases implying independent power—such as in certain darud (blessings) like Durood Taj—is further denounced as polytheistic, akin to attributing qudra (power) separate from Allah. Deobandi refutations of Barelvi founder Ahmad Raza Khan's doctrines, including fatwas labeling aspects of his teachings heretical, exemplify this theological confrontation.[77][122][9][123] Ahl-i Hadith critiques echo these, framing shrine veneration, offerings, and pilgrimages to dargahs as idolatrous breaches of tawhid, with historical opposition to such folk-infused rituals dating to the movement's 19th-century origins in colonial India. These charges have fueled pamphlet wars, fatwa exchanges, and even violence, such as bombings targeting Barelvi gatherings, underscoring the reformists' view that Barelvi practices compromise Islam's foundational purity.[122]

Critiques of Superstition and Folk Practices

Critics from Deobandi, Ahl al-Hadith, and Salafi perspectives have frequently accused the Barelvi movement of integrating superstitious elements and folk customs into religious practice, labeling them as bid'ah (religious innovations) or even shirk (associating partners with God). These include the widespread use of ta'wiz (amulets inscribed with Quranic verses, names of saints, or numerological formulas), which are worn or placed in homes for protection against evil, illness, or misfortune; detractors argue this constitutes impermissible reliance on created objects rather than direct tawhid (monotheism).[124] [33] Such practices are said to blend pre-Islamic South Asian folk traditions with Islamic rituals, as seen in urs commemorations at saints' shrines (dargahs), where devotees engage in ecstatic dances (qawwali performances), offerings of food or cloth, and rituals resembling circumambulation or prostration before graves—actions equated by opponents to idolatrous veneration. Deobandi scholars, such as those from Darul Uloom Deoband, have issued fatwas condemning these as deviations from the Sunnah, emphasizing that authentic Islam prohibits seeking intercession (tawassul) through the deceased in ways that imply inherent power in graves or relics.[124] Further critiques target the syncretic incorporation of local customs, such as astrology-influenced healing rites or spirit exorcism sessions (jhar-phoonk) conducted by pirs (spiritual guides), which are viewed as superstitious holdovers from Hindu or animist influences rather than scriptural mandates. Reformist voices within Sunni orthodoxy, including 20th-century figures like Ashraf Ali Thanvi, documented these in treatises as eroding doctrinal purity, arguing they foster credulity among the masses and obscure core Islamic tenets.[33] While Barelvi apologists maintain these acts express permissible love for the Prophet and awliya (saints), opponents counter that empirical observation of inconsistent outcomes—such as failed amulet protections—reveals their non-causal, placebo-like efficacy, unsupported by prophetic precedent.[124]

Concerns Over Political Radicalization

The emergence of the Tehreek-e-Labbaik Pakistan (TLP) in 2015 has intensified concerns that elements within the Barelvi movement are undergoing political radicalization, shifting from devotional Sufi practices toward aggressive enforcement of blasphemy laws through street mobilization and vigilantism.[17] TLP, founded by the late Khadim Hussain Rizvi, a Barelvi cleric, rapidly gained traction by framing political opposition as apostasy, culminating in its strong performance in the 2018 Pakistani general elections where it secured over 2.2 million votes and eight seats in the National Assembly.[18] This mobilization has been linked to a pattern of violence, including the 2017 Faizabad sit-in that paralyzed highways for weeks and forced government concessions on blasphemy penalties, as well as deadly clashes in 2021 where TLP supporters killed at least seven police officers during marches demanding the expulsion of the French ambassador over cartoons deemed blasphemous.[108] Analysts have highlighted how this radicalization manifests in the glorification of extrajudicial killings, as seen in the 2011 assassination of Punjab Governor Salman Taseer by Mumtaz Qadri, a Barelvi police guard, which drew widespread praise from Barelvi scholars and led to Qadri's veneration as a martyr at his shrine until its demolition in 2016.[9] Such incidents underscore a departure from the movement's historical emphasis on saint veneration and anti-Wahhabi polemics toward a confrontational stance that prioritizes punitive responses to perceived insults against the Prophet Muhammad, often bypassing legal processes and inciting mob justice.[116] Reports from think tanks note that this has blurred distinctions between Barelvi activism and the Deobandi-linked extremism it traditionally opposed, with TLP's rhetoric fostering a climate where false blasphemy accusations—numbering over 1,500 cases since 1987—frequently escalate into lynchings, as in the 2017 murder of student Mashal Khan.[108] These developments have raised alarms about broader societal impacts, including the export of anti-blasphemy extremism to diaspora communities; a 2024 UK government report identified TLP's influence in rising incidents of threats and violence against individuals accused of blasphemy in Britain, such as the 2021 stabbing of author Salman Rushdie's attacker citing similar motivations.[70] In Pakistan, the group's tactics have strained state authority, prompting repeated bans—most recently in October 2025 following clashes near Lahore that left multiple deaths—and highlighting how Barelvi political entryism exploits religious sentiments for electoral gains while undermining pluralistic norms.[118] Critics from security-focused institutions argue this radicalization stems from institutional failures to reform blasphemy provisions, allowing fringe groups to capture mainstream Barelvi sentiment and perpetuate cycles of intolerance.[9]

Notable Figures and Institutions

Influential Scholars and Leaders

Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), commonly known as Ala Hazrat, founded the Barelvi movement through his extensive writings and fatwas defending Sufi traditions, veneration of saints, and orthodox Sunni practices against reformist critiques from Deobandi and Wahhabi scholars.[125] Born on June 14, 1856, in Bareilly, India, he authored over 1,000 works on theology, jurisprudence, and poetry, establishing a Hanafi-Maturidi-Ash'ari framework that emphasized love for the Prophet Muhammad.[5] His efforts positioned the movement as a bulwark against perceived deviations, influencing millions in South Asia.[126] Khan's eldest son, Hamid Raza Khan (1875–1943), served as his appointed successor, continuing the scholarly lineage by compiling and disseminating his father's works while leading the Bareilly community in religious and educational affairs.[127] The younger son, Mustafa Raza Khan Qadri (1892–1981), emerged as a pivotal figure, earning the title Grand Mufti of India for his jurisprudence and leadership in Barelvi institutions; he succeeded Hamid and expanded the movement's madrasa networks across India.[127][128] Mustafa's tenure solidified the family's role in issuing fatwas and fostering devotional practices like milad celebrations.[127] In the 20th century, Mustafa's grandson Akhtar Raza Khan (1943–2018), also known as Taj-ush-Sharia, upheld the lineage as Grand Mufti, recognized globally for his authority in Barelvi fiqh; born February 2, 1943, in Bareilly, he issued thousands of fatwas and was ranked among the world's most influential Muslims in 2016.[129][130] His death on July 20, 2018, drew massive attendance, underscoring his enduring impact on the movement's spiritual guidance.[130] Other prominent leaders include Pir Jamaat Ali Shah (1834–1951), a Naqshbandi Sufi who allied with Ahmed Raza Khan, presiding over the All India Sunni Conference and advocating for Muslim unity during the Pakistan movement while defending shrine rights.[131] In Pakistan, figures like Muhammad Ilyas Attar Qadri have popularized Barelvi teachings through organizations such as Dawat-e-Islami, focusing on mass preaching and madrasas.[132] In India, Tauqeer Raza Khan leads the Ittehad-e-Millat Council, blending scholarship with political activism on issues like minority rights.[133] These leaders have sustained the movement's emphasis on popular piety amid regional challenges.

Major Organizations and Educational Networks

The Barelvi movement's educational infrastructure originated with Madrasa Manzar-e-Islam, established in 1904 in Bareilly, India, by Ahmad Raza Khan Barelvi and associates to institutionalize Ahl-i Sunnat wa al-Jama'at teachings amid colonial-era challenges.[134] This seminary delivers curricula in Quranic studies, hadith, fiqh according to Hanafi jurisprudence, and Sufi devotional practices, producing scholars who uphold veneration of the Prophet Muhammad and opposition to reformist critiques.[134] It marked the movement's first dedicated madrasa, emphasizing traditional Sunni orthodoxy over Wahhabi-influenced puritanism.[134] Jamia Ashrafia in Mubarakpur, Uttar Pradesh, emerged as another cornerstone institution, fostering advanced Barelvi scholarship through rigorous training in Islamic sciences and polemical defenses against Deobandi positions.[134] Founded in the mid-20th century, it has graduated thousands of ulema who lead mosques, shrines, and community outreach, reinforcing the movement's folk-Sufi orientation in northern India.[134] In Pakistan, post-1947 partition, Barelvi networks expanded via organizations blending education with propagation. Dawat-e-Islami, initiated in 1981 by Muhammad Ilyas Attar Qadri in Karachi, operates a global chain of madrasas and seminaries promoting ethical reform, naat recitation, and milad observance, with branches in over 194 countries serving millions through formal schooling and itinerant preaching groups (madani qafilas).[135] Its institutions prioritize accessible Islamic education for youth, countering perceived Deobandi dominance in state-recognized madrasas.[136] Jamiat Ulema-e-Pakistan (JUP), formed in 1948 as the movement's primary political entity, supports affiliated educational bodies advocating Sharia implementation and Barelvi jurisprudence, historically mobilizing ulema networks for constitutional Islamization efforts.[12] Sunni Tehreek, founded in 1990 to safeguard Barelvi mosques from sectarian encroachments, maintains community-based teaching initiatives focused on doctrinal preservation and anti-blasphemy awareness.[16] These entities collectively sustain the movement's emphasis on devotional piety over scriptural literalism, though their influence varies amid competition from rival Sunni groups.
