Howard Greenfield

Howard Greenfield (March 15, 1936 – March 4, 1986) was an American lyricist and songwriter, best known for his prolific output during the Brill Building era through collaborations with composers Neil Sedaka and Jack Keller.[1][2] His partnership with Sedaka, initiated in their teenage years in Brooklyn's Brighton Beach, yielded enduring hits including "Stupid Cupid" for Connie Francis, "Oh! Carol," "Happy Birthday Sweet Sixteen," "Calendar Girl," and "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do," the latter reaching number one on the Billboard Hot 100.[1][3] With Keller, Greenfield co-wrote additional number-one singles such as "Everybody's Somebody's Fool," "My Heart Has a Mind of Its Own," and "Breakin' in a Brand New Broken Heart," alongside television themes for series like Bewitched, Gidget, and The Flying Nun.[2] Greenfield's lyrics contributed to over 450 songs, with later successes including the 1975 Grammy-winning "Love Will Keep Us Together," which became the year's most performed song according to BMI.[3][1] He died in Los Angeles at age 49 and was posthumously inducted into the Songwriters Hall of Fame in 1991.[3][1]

Early Life

Childhood and Education

Howard Greenfield was born on March 15, 1936, in Brooklyn, New York City.[3] He grew up in a Jewish family in the Brighton Beach neighborhood of Brooklyn, residing in a six-story brick apartment building at 3260 Coney Island Avenue.[2][4] Around age 16, Greenfield met 13-year-old Neil Sedaka, who lived in the same building; Greenfield's budding interest in poetry complemented Sedaka's musical pursuits. The two later became classmates at Abraham Lincoln High School in Brooklyn, where they began collaborating on early creative endeavors.[3] Greenfield pursued no formal musical education, focusing instead on verbal aptitude honed through self-directed poetic work.

Initial Interest in Songwriting

During his teenage years at Abraham Lincoln High School in Brooklyn, Howard Greenfield cultivated a budding interest in poetry, which naturally progressed to informal experiments in crafting song lyrics as a means of applying rhythmic and thematic structures to popular music forms. Influenced by lyricists such as Lorenz Hart, Greenfield began composing verses that emphasized emotional narratives suitable for melody, marking an initial shift from pure literary pursuits to hybrid creative endeavors.[2][1] In November 1952, at the urging of his mother, the 16-year-old Greenfield approached his 13-year-old neighbor and aspiring composer Neil Sedaka, proposing that Sedaka set Greenfield's lyrics to music; Sedaka's first such effort, titled "My Life's Devotion," solidified their informal partnership. The duo quickly established a rigorous regimen, collaborating daily to produce approximately one song per day for nearly two years, resulting in a body of unpublished demos and rough recordings that honed their complementary skills—Greenfield's verbal precision paired with Sedaka's melodic instincts—without immediate commercial intent.[2][1] This amateur phase transitioned toward professional aspirations as the teenagers began submitting their early works to music publishers in the early 1950s, pitching demos during visits to nascent industry hubs in New York; these efforts, though initially unsuccessful in securing deals, exposed them to figures like Mort Shuman and Doc Pomus and underscored the practical challenges of breaking into songwriting, prompting refinements in their output to align with market demands.[1]

Professional Career

Partnership with Neil Sedaka

Howard Greenfield and Neil Sedaka formed their songwriting partnership in 1952 after Sedaka, then 13 years old and a piano student at the Juilliard School of Music, was introduced to the 16-year-old Greenfield by Greenfield's mother.[5][1] Sedaka composed the melodies on piano, while Greenfield crafted lyrics to fit them, establishing a clear division of labor that defined their collaboration.[6][7] The duo adopted a rigorous regimen of daily writing sessions, producing hundreds of songs together over more than two decades.[1][8] Following Sedaka's high school graduation in 1956, they escalated their efforts by recording and submitting demos of original material.[9] These demos attracted interest, with several sold to other artists, which helped secure Sedaka's recording contract with RCA Victor in 1958.[10][2] The partnership's professional output intensified from the late 1950s through the mid-1970s, yielding a prolific catalog despite occasional creative tensions toward the end.[11][2] Their collaboration formally concluded in 1973 with the song "Our Last Song Together."[2]

Brill Building Contributions

In 1958, Howard Greenfield and his songwriting partner Neil Sedaka signed with Aldon Music, a publishing firm established by Al Nevins and Don Kirshner at 1650 Broadway, adjacent to the Brill Building at 1619 Broadway, which served as the epicenter of New York's pop songwriting industry during the late 1950s and 1960s.[2] This relocation positioned Greenfield within the commercial "song factory" model, where teams of professional writers operated in cramped offices, systematically crafting catchy, market-oriented compositions tailored for emerging teen idols and vocal groups.[12] Aldon exemplified this assembly-line approach, emphasizing high-volume output over artistic experimentation, with songwriters pitching demos directly to producers and labels to secure recordings.[1] Greenfield's contributions during this era aligned with the Brill Building's signature sound—upbeat, melodically infectious pop emphasizing romantic themes and accessible hooks suited to the era's youth market.[13] Operating alongside contemporaries such as Carole King and Gerry Goffin, as well as Barry Mann and Cynthia Weil, Greenfield focused on lyric provision that complemented Sedaka's melodies, yielding material that fueled the teen pop boom without relying on external collaborations.[14] This independent productivity within Aldon's ecosystem contributed to the firm's reputation for generating chart contenders, as writers competed internally to meet publishers' demands for versatile, demo-ready songs adaptable to various artists.[15] By the early 1960s, the Brill Building framework, including Aldon's operations, had produced hundreds of hits through relentless iteration and demo production, with Greenfield's involvement underscoring the era's shift toward specialized, professionalized songcraft divorced from performer-led composition.[16] His work ethic in this competitive environment highlighted the causal efficiency of the model: proximity to decision-makers accelerated placements, though it prioritized commercial viability over innovation, reflecting the industry's response to rock 'n' roll's demand for disposable, radio-friendly singles.[1]

Collaborations Beyond Sedaka

Beginning in 1960, Howard Greenfield established a regular songwriting partnership with Jack Keller, composing together on Mondays and Wednesdays for six consecutive years.[2] This collaboration produced several hits for Connie Francis, including "Everybody's Somebody's Fool," which reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1960, and "My Heart Has a Mind of Its Own," which peaked at number two later that year.[17] Additional successes included "Breakin' in a Brand New Broken Heart," another top-ten entry for Francis in 1962.[18] Greenfield also partnered with Helen Miller during the 1960s, contributing lyrics to tracks like "Foolish Little Girl" recorded by The Cookies in 1963, which climbed to number five on the Billboard Hot 100.[19] In a notable crossover effort, he co-wrote "Crying in the Rain" with Carole King in 1961; the Everly Brothers' version became a top-ten hit, highlighting Greenfield's ability to adapt his lyrical style to different melodic approaches within the Brill Building ecosystem.[20] By the 1970s, as musical styles evolved toward rock and disco dominance, Greenfield's collaborations outside his primary partnership became less frequent, though he continued selective projects amid a shifting industry landscape that favored artist-songwriters over assembly-line pop composition.[2] These efforts underscored his versatility but yielded fewer chart-topping results compared to the prior decade's output.

Later Career and Challenges

Following the prolific output of the early 1960s, Greenfield's collaborations yielded fewer new chart-topping hits as the music industry transitioned from the structured pop songwriting of the Brill Building era to rock-driven acts influenced by the British Invasion and, later, disco in the 1970s.[2] His partnership with Sedaka, which had produced multiple number-one singles like "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do" in 1962, continued sporadically into the mid-1970s, with Sedaka increasingly working with other lyricists after 1970 but occasionally reuniting with Greenfield.[2] A notable exception came with "Love Will Keep Us Together," co-written with Sedaka and originally recorded by Sedaka in 1973 before achieving massive success via Captain & Tennille's 1975 cover, which held the Billboard Hot 100 number-one position for four weeks and earned the Grammy Award for Record of the Year.[3] This revival aligned with Sedaka's broader comeback in the 1970s, leveraging their earlier catalog amid shifting tastes that favored self-contained artist-songwriters over professional teams. However, verifiable chart data indicates a marked decline in top placements for Greenfield's new works post-1965, with only isolated successes amid the dominance of genres less amenable to his melodic pop style.[17] Into the 1980s, Greenfield contributed lyrics to the Grease 2 soundtrack, including "Back to School Again" (performed by The Four Tops) and "Love Will Turn Back the Hands of Time" (performed by Michelle Pfeiffer and Maxwell Caulfield), reflecting adaptation to film opportunities as standalone pop singles waned.[21] With reduced visibility for fresh compositions, he relied more on royalties from his enduring catalog, which included standards covered across decades and sustained income through licensing and revivals, though specific industry consolidation trends—such as major label dominance—further constrained outlets for independent Brill Building-style writers.[1]

Notable Songs and Achievements

Hit Songs with Sedaka

The songwriting duo of Neil Sedaka and Howard Greenfield produced multiple chart-topping singles for Sedaka's own recordings during the late 1950s and early 1960s, emphasizing upbeat teen-oriented pop themes. Their first significant hit was "The Diary" in 1958, which reached No. 14 on the Billboard Hot 100.[22] This was followed by "Stairway to Heaven" in 1960, a non-rock composition distinct from Led Zeppelin's later track, peaking at No. 9. "Calendar Girl" released in 1961 climbed to No. 4, capitalizing on seasonal novelty appeal.[6] "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do," issued in 1962, became Sedaka's signature song and their biggest success, holding the No. 1 position on the Billboard Hot 100 for two weeks and selling over a million copies.[23] "Happy Birthday Sweet Sixteen" from 1961 achieved No. 6, further solidifying their formula of catchy, youthful melodies. Other notable Sedaka singles co-written with Greenfield, such as "Oh! Carol," "Little Devil," and "Next Door to an Angel," also entered the top 40, contributing to a string of over a dozen charting releases in this period.[6] Collectively, Sedaka's recordings of these compositions amassed sales exceeding 25 million units by 1963, underscoring the commercial potency of their collaboration amid the pre-Beatles pop era.[2] Their output included at least 20 singles that charted on Billboard, with enduring airplay on oldies radio stations reflecting sustained popularity.[24]

Songs for Other Artists

Greenfield extended his songwriting beyond his primary partnership with Neil Sedaka through collaborations with composers Jack Keller and Helen Miller, yielding several chart successes for female vocalists and girl groups in the early 1960s.[2] With Keller, a Brill Building associate, Greenfield co-wrote material during regular sessions from 1960 onward, focusing on teen-oriented pop themes of romance and heartbreak.[25] Their most prominent achievements were two consecutive Billboard Hot 100 No. 1 singles for Connie Francis in 1960: "Everybody's Somebody's Fool," which topped the chart for two weeks starting June 27, and "My Heart Has a Mind of Its Own," which held No. 1 for two weeks beginning September 26.[26][27] These tracks, emphasizing emotional vulnerability in relationships, marked Francis's first and second chart-toppers, respectively, and demonstrated Greenfield's lyrical versatility in crafting relatable narratives for mass appeal.[28] Another Keller-Greenfield composition, "Venus in Blue Jeans," reached No. 7 on the Billboard Hot 100 for Jimmy Clanton in October 1962, contributing to the era's influx of lighthearted, image-driven teen idol fare.[25] In partnership with Helen Miller, Greenfield penned "Foolish Little Girl," a cautionary tale of romantic regret that propelled The Shirelles to No. 4 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May 1963, marking the group's final Top 10 hit.[29] Greenfield's output for other artists encompassed dozens of recordings by performers such as Bobby Vee ("Charms," co-written with Miller) and Clyde McPhatter ("Chinatown," also with Miller), though these yielded modest or regional airplay rather than national blockbusters.[18] Such credits underscored his role in the Brill Building ecosystem, supplying B-sides and album cuts to emerging acts, but with peak commercial impact concentrated in the Keller and Miller collaborations amid the competitive 1960s pop landscape.[19]

Commercial Success Metrics

Greenfield's songwriting achieved significant commercial metrics through chart dominance and record sales in the late 1950s and 1960s. Compositions co-written by Greenfield reached the number one position on the Billboard Hot 100 four times, reflecting peak-era success in pop music.[1][2] These hits, primarily from collaborations with Neil Sedaka and other artists, contributed to Sedaka's recordings selling a combined 25 million copies by the mid-1960s.[2] The Sedaka-Greenfield team's output from 1959 to 1963 generated multiple top-10 Billboard entries, establishing them as a leading Brill Building partnership and driving substantial mechanical royalties from sheet music and phonograph sales during that era.[1] According to Sedaka, their songs outsold all artists except Elvis Presley and the Beatles in those years, underscoring the quantitative impact on the pop market.[30] By 1975, even post-partnership revivals like covers of their earlier work continued to chart highly, maintaining revenue streams into the pre-1986 period.[2] Overall catalog performance, grounded in these verifiable sales and chart data, positioned Greenfield's contributions among the most financially successful of his contemporaries, with royalties accruing from airplay and recordings tracked by organizations like BMI, as evidenced by industry awards in 1962.[31]

Personal Life

Family Background

Howard Greenfield was born on March 15, 1936, in Brooklyn, New York City, into a Jewish family.[4][3] He was raised in the Brighton Beach neighborhood, where he resided in a six-story apartment building at 3260 Coney Island Avenue alongside future collaborator Neil Sedaka.[1][2] Greenfield's mother, Ella, exerted familial influence by urging him, at age 16, to contact the 13-year-old Sedaka on November 11, 1952, fostering the songwriting partnership that defined much of his career.[2] Public records and biographies make no mention of siblings, indicating he grew up as an only child. Details on his father are absent from available accounts, with the family maintaining a modest profile centered in Brooklyn's working-class Jewish milieu.[32]

Relationships and Sexuality

Greenfield identified as homosexual and was open about his sexuality within private circles, despite the social stigma and legal risks associated with homosexuality in mid-20th-century America, where sodomy laws persisted in many states until the 2003 Lawrence v. Texas ruling.[2] His orientation did not intersect publicly with his professional songwriting career, which emphasized romantic themes often tailored for heterosexual audiences.[2] From the early 1960s until his death, Greenfield maintained a committed domestic partnership with cabaret singer Tory Damon (born September 29, 1939; died March 30, 1986), with whom he cohabited in an apartment on East 63rd Street in Manhattan.[33][34] The relationship remained low-profile, consistent with the era's norms that discouraged overt displays of same-sex partnerships to evade professional repercussions or personal harassment. Greenfield never married, and no records indicate other long-term romantic involvements or public scandals related to his personal life.[2]

Death and Health

Illness and Passing

Greenfield's health deteriorated in the mid-1980s due to AIDS, a viral infection then spreading rapidly amid limited medical understanding and treatments.[35] He was hospitalized at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles, where his condition worsened quickly from opportunistic infections typical of advanced AIDS, such as pneumonia or organ failure.[36] Greenfield died on March 4, 1986, eleven days before his 50th birthday, at age 49, with AIDS explicitly listed as a contributing cause in contemporary reporting.[3][35] His partner, Tory Damon, succumbed to similar AIDS-related illnesses just weeks later on March 30, 1986.[2]

Immediate Aftermath

Following Greenfield's death on March 4, 1986, from heart failure at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles, obituaries appeared in major newspapers emphasizing his songwriting legacy. The New York Times published a notice on March 14, detailing his collaboration with Neil Sedaka on over 450 songs, including "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do," "Happy Birthday Sweet Sixteen," and the Grammy-winning "Love Will Keep Us Together," while noting his work with artists like Connie Francis and contributions to films such as Grease II.[3] The Chicago Tribune reported on March 9, highlighting co-writes like "Where the Boys Are" for Connie Francis and "Love Will Keep Us Together" for Captain & Tennille, attributing the death to a brief illness without further specification, as confirmed by spokeswoman Rosemary Link.[36] These accounts focused on professional achievements rather than personal details or cause, reflecting the era's reticence toward AIDS-related cases, where many obituaries avoided explicit mentions to evade stigma.[35] No immediate public statements from Sedaka, Greenfield's longtime partner and childhood friend, were reported in contemporary coverage, though their close professional bond—spanning hits from the late 1950s onward—suggested private mourning. Coverage remained subdued, with no widespread media frenzy or tributes, consistent with the limited attention given to non-celebrity AIDS deaths in 1986 amid societal taboos. Greenfield was interred at Forest Lawn Memorial Park in the Hollywood Hills.[37] Greenfield was survived by his sister, Carole Firestone of Hewlett, New York, who became the primary heir.[3] His estate handled ongoing royalties from ASCAP-registered compositions, including Sedaka collaborations that continued generating income through recordings and performances, providing financial continuity for family without public disputes or litigation reported at the time. The absence of sensationalism underscored the professional respect afforded his catalog amid personal tragedy.

Legacy

Recognition and Awards

Greenfield was posthumously inducted into the Songwriters Hall of Fame in 1991 for his contributions to popular music, particularly his lyric-writing partnership with Neil Sedaka that produced numerous chart-topping hits.[1][38] The song "Love Will Keep Us Together," co-written with Sedaka and recorded by Captain & Tennille, received the Grammy Award for Record of the Year in 1975, highlighting Greenfield's role in crafting enduring pop compositions.[3][36]

Cultural Impact and Underrecognition

Howard Greenfield's lyrics shaped the sound of late 1950s and early 1960s American pop, contributing to the Brill Building era's assembly-line production of chart-topping teen anthems that emphasized catchy, innocent themes amid the rising rock counterculture. Collaborations with Neil Sedaka yielded multiple Billboard Hot 100 successes, including "#1" hits like "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do" (1962) and covers such as Connie Francis's "Stupid Cupid" (1958), helping define a wholesome strain of pop that dominated airwaves before the British Invasion shifted focus to band-driven rock.[39][5] These works, often featuring playful hooks and relatable narratives, sustained high chart placements on both sides of the Atlantic, underscoring Greenfield's role in pop's commercial peak.[39] Despite this prolific output—co-authoring over a dozen Top 40 hits for Sedaka alone—Greenfield remains relatively obscure, frequently overshadowed in credits and retrospectives by his composer partner Sedaka, who gained visibility as a performer. A 2025 analysis describes him as one of the "hidden men of song," noting that audiences recite his words without crediting the lyricist, a pattern emblematic of the industry's historical undervaluation of non-musical contributors.[39][40] This disparity challenges narratives privileging "genius composers" who also perform, as Greenfield's high hit rate demonstrates equivalent craftsmanship without the spotlight of recording or stage presence.[6] Greenfield's underrecognition reflects broader empirical oversights in pop history, where Brill Building pop's structured songwriting has been downplayed in favor of rock's perceived authenticity following the 1960s upheavals. The rock revolution, amplified by the British Invasion, undermined the era's pop machinery, relegating figures like Greenfield to footnotes despite verifiable commercial dominance.[41] Causal factors include the lyricist's behind-the-scenes role and a post-counterculture emphasis on auteur musicians over collaborative craftspeople, resulting in skewed acclaim that favors measurable fame over output efficacy.[39][40]

Enduring Influence on Songwriting

The Sedaka-Greenfield partnership exemplified the Brill Building model's reliance on specialized lyricist-composer duos, which optimized hit production through division of labor and weekly writing routines, influencing later professional songwriting teams in pop and beyond.[42] This approach, where Greenfield focused on lyrics and Sedaka on melodies, contributed to a formulaic yet commercially effective process that persisted in music publishing hubs, as seen in the continued use of collaborative teams for targeted market hits.[43] Themes of innocent, youthful romance in songs like "Oh! Carol" and "Happy Birthday Sweet Sixteen" have endured via covers extending into the 21st century, sustaining their place in nostalgic pop repertoires and adaptations for contemporary artists.[44] For instance, "Love Will Keep Us Together" achieved renewed chart success through Captain & Tennille's 1975 version, which BMI recognized as the most performed song of that year, demonstrating the catalog's adaptability across eras.[1] Catalog vitality is evidenced by ongoing media licensing and performances, with tracks such as "Breaking Up Is Hard to Do" and "Where the Boys Are" appearing in films and television, generating royalties through sustained radio airplay and streaming.[45] These metrics underscore a causal longevity tied to the songs' accessible structures and emotional directness, rather than transient trends, as their output reactivated commercial interest during Sedaka's 1970s resurgence despite Greenfield's absence.[1]
