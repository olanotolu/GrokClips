2023 Jeux de la Francophonie


The 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie, the ninth edition of this quadrennial multi-sport and cultural event organized under the auspices of the International Organisation of La Francophonie, took place in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo, from 28 July to 6 August.[1]
The games featured approximately 3,000 athletes from more than 30 Francophone nations competing in disciplines such as athletics, judo, wrestling, football, and aquatics, alongside artistic performances in music, dance, and literature.[2][3]
Originally scheduled for 2021, the event faced multiple postponements due to logistical and infrastructural challenges in the host country, ultimately proceeding amid heightened security measures in the capital.[4][5]
In the final medal tally, Morocco led with 58 medals including 23 golds, followed by Romania with 38 and Cameroon with 40, while the host Democratic Republic of the Congo ranked lower despite investments exceeding the initial budget by a factor of seven.[1][6]
These games underscored both the cultural unity of Francophone communities and the practical difficulties of hosting large-scale international events in developing regions prone to governance and fiscal overruns.[6][7]

Background and Organisation

Bidding and Host Selection

The Organisation internationale de la Francophonie (OIF) issued a call for bids to host the ninth edition of the Jeux de la Francophonieâ€”originally scheduled for 2021â€”on 1 March 2019, requiring submissions by 31 May 2019.[8] The Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) submitted the only formal bid, proposing Kinshasa as the host city.[9] On 30 October 2019, the OIF's Permanent Council unanimously awarded the hosting rights to the DRC, recognizing Kinshasa's candidacy as the sole contender despite potential logistical challenges in the region.[9] This selection positioned the DRC to host the event for the first time, encompassing both sporting and cultural competitions among Francophone nations.[10] The decision aligned with the OIF's rotating hosting model among member states, though it later faced delays due to the COVID-19 pandemic, shifting the games to 2023.[11]

Postponement and Scheduling

The ninth Jeux de la Francophonie were awarded to Kinshasa in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) in 2018, with the event originally scheduled for July 2021.[12][13] In May 2020, the International Committee for the Games of La Francophonie (CIJF) and DRC organizers postponed the event to August 19â€“28, 2022, primarily due to the COVID-19 pandemic, which disrupted global sporting calendars including the rescheduling of the Tokyo 2020 Olympics to 2021.[12][14][15] A second postponement occurred in February 2022, shifting the dates to 2023, as preparations in the DRC lagged significantly, with inadequate infrastructure and logistical delays preventing readiness by the revised timeline.[16][17][18] The games ultimately proceeded from July 28 to August 6, 2023, in Kinshasa, following CIJF insistence in late 2022 that no further delays were feasible amid ongoing preparation challenges.[19][20][21]

Preparations and Infrastructure Development

The 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie, originally scheduled for 2021 in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo, were postponed to July 28â€“August 6, 2023, to facilitate necessary upgrades to sports infrastructure to international standards.[5] This delay provided additional time for construction and rehabilitation projects, including the development of an athletes' village designed to accommodate up to 3,500 participants and the refurbishment of existing venues such as the annex field at Stade des Martyrs.[22] Preparatory efforts focused on modernizing key facilities, with works encompassing the construction of three new gymnasiums for basketball and the rehabilitation of tracks and stadiums across Kinshasa.[23] By October 2022, rehabilitation projects at the University of Kinshasa campus, selected as a primary site, were underway to prepare multi-sport infrastructures.[24] The Congolese government allocated approximately 66.9 million euros specifically for infrastructure construction, though overall expenditures escalated significantly beyond initial projections.[25] In the lead-up to the event, authorities accelerated completion of remaining works, achieving 95% readiness for venues by mid-June 2023, with final touches on accommodations and competition areas finalized in the weeks prior to opening ceremonies.[26] The government committed to delivering all infrastructures by May 31, 2023, amid ongoing monitoring to ensure operational viability for the multi-sport and cultural competitions.[27]

Controversies and Criticisms

Security and Safety Issues

Prior to the event, several participating delegations expressed significant concerns over health and safety risks in Kinshasa, leading to withdrawals or reduced participation. Quebec canceled its involvement entirely following technical assessment missions in January and May 2023, which highlighted security and health risks deemed unacceptable.[28] Canada and New Brunswick sent scaled-down delegations, while France opted out of athletics and cycling disciplines.[29] These decisions were influenced by the Democratic Republic of Congo's unstable security environment, including ongoing urban crime and instability in the capital.[30] In response, organizers deployed approximately 4,500 additional police officers supported by state security agents across Kinshasa to bolster street-level protection.[5] Athletes were required to travel under police escort, and accommodations were equipped with surveillance cameras for enhanced monitoring.[29] A private security firm handled internal event venues, aiming to mitigate risks amid the city's baseline insecurity, which had already contributed to multiple postponements of the games from their original 2021 schedule.[30] Despite these measures and pre-event apprehensions, no major security breaches or safety incidents involving participants were reported during the July 28 to August 6 competition period.[31] The enhanced protocols were credited with facilitating a largely incident-free event, though broader criticisms of Kinshasa's preparedness persisted.[32]

Budget Overruns and Financial Irregularities

The 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie, held in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo from July 28 to August 6, incurred expenditures of approximately $324 million, representing seven times the initial planned budget of around $46 million.[33][6] The Congolese finance minister, Nicolas Kazadi, attributed the overrun to inadequate planning by the organizing committee (CNJF) and external factors including inflation and logistical delays from the event's prior postponement due to the COVID-19 pandemic.[34][35] The CNJF contested the government's figures, asserting that the full allocated budget had not been disbursed by the Ministry of Finance and that outstanding debts persisted, complicating accurate accounting.[6] In response, the Congolese government initiated a financial audit in late October 2023, dispatching auditors to Kinshasa to scrutinize procurement, expenditures, and compliance with budgetary norms.[35][34] Specific financial irregularities included unpaid obligations to suppliers, such as a $4.2 million contract for bus transportation signed in April 2023, which remained unsettled post-event and strained relations with vendors.[36] Critics highlighted opaque procurement processes and potential mismanagement, though the audit's findings, as of late 2023, had not been publicly released, leaving the extent of deliberate irregularities unresolved.[37] The overruns drew broader scrutiny to the event's financial governance, mirroring patterns in other African-hosted international sporting events where initial estimates often escalate due to infrastructural deficits and currency fluctuations.[37]

Organizational and Logistical Failures

The preparations for the 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie encountered substantial organizational hurdles, including the lack of an experienced event director and unplaced orders for essential sports and scenic equipment, which compromised timely setup across multiple disciplines.[38] Venue readiness lagged critically, with athletics tracks and judo facilities incomplete as of mid-June 2023, forcing last-minute rushes that strained resources just weeks before the July 28 opening.[22] Administrative buildings remained under construction, while the Tata Raphael stadium's renovations were projected to miss deadlines, exacerbating coordination delays for competitions. Logistical deficiencies extended to participant support, with gaps in housing, transportation, visa processing, and health protocols identified by oversight bodies in late 2022, prompting demands for remedial timelines that were only partially met.[38] Road infrastructure posed immediate risks, as evidenced by unrepaired potholes along the 20-kilometer cycling route, requiring urgent fixes to enable safe events.[22] In the event's initial days, athletes and artists faced on-site logistical disruptionsâ€”such as scheduling and access issuesâ€”that necessitated rapid adaptations and peer collaboration to proceed.[39] These shortcomings, rooted in protracted planning inconsistencies, fueled pre-event skepticism and reduced delegation sizes from nations citing unreliable execution.[38]

Political and Diplomatic Tensions

The 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie, hosted in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), from July 28 to August 5, were overshadowed by acute diplomatic tensions between the host nation and Rwanda, exacerbated by the ongoing conflict in eastern DRC. The DRC accused Rwanda of providing military support to the M23 rebel group, which had captured significant territory, prompting heightened bilateral animosity and regional instability.[40]) These strains directly impacted the event's high-level attendance, as Louise Mushikiwabo, Rwanda's former foreign minister and Secretary-General of the International Organisation of La Francophonie (OIF), declined to participate amid unconfirmed reports that her invitation was not extended by DRC authorities.[41][42] Mushikiwabo's absence underscored the politicization of the games, with the OIF's leadershipâ€”intended to symbolize Francophone unityâ€”caught in the crossfire of the DRC-Rwanda rift, as evidenced by independent UN expert reports corroborating Kinshasa's claims of Rwandan involvement in M23 activities.[43] The decision reflected broader diplomatic immaturity, according to analysts, as the host's refusal to engage the OIF head risked undermining the organization's neutrality and the event's prestige.[44] While no formal boycotts by national delegations occurred due to this issue, the tensions contributed to perceptions of the games as a venue for proxy geopolitical maneuvering rather than pure cultural and sporting exchange.[40]

Withdrawals and Notable Absences

Quebec, a regular participant in prior editions as a distinct delegation, opted not to send athletes or artists to the 2023 Games, with the decision announced on June 27, 2023, due to elevated security and health risks in Kinshasa amid the Democratic Republic of the Congo's ongoing instability.[50][51] The Quebec government prioritized participant safety over involvement, despite recognizing the event's cultural and sporting value.[52] This absence drew later criticism from Canadian federal officials, who viewed it as undermining broader national representation within the Francophonie.[28] Rwanda did not field a delegation, as it failed to register athletes or confirm participation by mid-2023, amid diplomatic tensions with the host DRC over accusations of supporting rebel groups in eastern Congo.[53] These frictions extended to the Organisation internationale de la Francophonie, with Secretary-General Louise Mushikiwaboâ€”a Rwandan nationalâ€”declining to attend the opening ceremonies despite an invitation.[54] Luxembourg withdrew its commitment in the lead-up to the event, contributing to a pattern of last-minute dÃ©sistements cited by organizers.[55] Broader European and North American hesitancy reflected perceptions of inadequate infrastructure and persistent risks in Kinshasa, resulting in reduced or absent contingents from several nations beyond those explicitly noted.[56] In specific disciplines like football, seven countries ultimately forfeited, necessitating group reorganizations.[57] The Democratic Republic of the Congo expressed regret over these partial or full withdrawals, attributing them to unfounded concerns despite enhanced security measures.[58]

Events and Competitions

Sports Disciplines

The sports program of the 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie consisted of nine competitive disciplines, selected to balance Olympic-style events with regionally prominent activities in Francophone Africa and beyond, targeting athletes aged 18-35 unless otherwise specified.[64] These included athletics and para-athletics, women's basketball (ages 18-25), road cycling, men's football (under-23), judo, African wrestling, freestyle wrestling, and table tennis.[65] The events ran from July 29 to August 5, 2023, across venues in Kinshasa, with medals awarded in individual and team categories where applicable.[64]
  • Athletics and Para-athletics: Track and field events for men and women, including sprints, middle-distance runs, hurdles, jumps, throws, and road races, held at Stade des Martyrs from July 31 to August 4; para-athletics featured adapted categories for athletes with disabilities.[65][66]
  • Women's Basketball: 5-on-5 team competition, emphasizing fast-paced play and regional African talent.[65]
  • Road Cycling: Individual time trials and road races for men and women, testing endurance on Kinshasa's urban and suburban routes.[65]
  • Men's Football: Under-23 tournament with group stages and knockouts, drawing from national youth squads.[65][62]
  • Judo: Individual weight-class bouts for men and women, contested August 2-5, with over 175 participants from 28 nations.[3][65]
  • African Wrestling (Lutte Africaine): Traditional standing grappling style indigenous to West and Central Africa, for men and women, highlighting cultural heritage alongside sport.[67][62]
  • Freestyle Wrestling: Olympic-format bouts for men and women, held July 29-31, focusing on takedowns and pins.[68][65]
  • Table Tennis: Singles and team events for men and women, emphasizing precision and speed.[65]
This lineup prioritized accessibility and inclusivity, with para-athletics integrated to promote participation among athletes with impairments, though logistical challenges affected some events' execution.[64]

Cultural and Artistic Events

The cultural and artistic events of the 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie featured 11 competitions designed to showcase the diversity of Francophone creative expression among young participants aged 18 to 35.[64] These contests ran concurrently with the sports program from July 28 to August 6 in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo, and were evaluated by international juries awarding gold, silver, and bronze medals based on artistic merit, originality, and technical execution.[64] [69] The events emphasized live performances, submissions, and installations, fostering cultural exchange across participating nations.[70] Key disciplines included arts de la rue (street arts), encompassing hip-hop dance routines, performances with giant puppets, and freestyle ball juggling demonstrations, each highlighting urban and performative innovation.[64] Arts visuels (visual arts) covered painting, photography, and sculpture or installation works, where artists submitted pieces reflecting Francophone themes and techniques.[64] Other categories comprised chanson (solo or group song performances), contes et conteurs (oral storytelling sessions), danse de crÃ©ation (choreographed contemporary dance pieces), littÃ©rature (original short stories or nouvelles), and crÃ©ation numÃ©rique (digital media projects such as animations or interactive works).[64] [71] Official results for each discipline were documented in dedicated livrets, detailing medalists from countries including Canada, France, and host DRC, though comprehensive public breakdowns remain limited to jury selections and national reports.[69] These events served as a platform for emerging talents, with pre-selections conducted nationally before international juries finalized delegations, prioritizing works in French or regional Francophone languages.[72] Participation drew from over 30 nations, underscoring the Games' role in promoting non-sporting cultural vitality amid logistical challenges elsewhere in the event.[73]

Opening and Closing Ceremonies

The opening ceremony of the 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie took place on July 28, 2023, in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo, marking the start of the multi-sport and cultural event that gathered over 3,000 athletes and artists from approximately 30 French-speaking nations.[74] [73] The event featured a lavish spectacle designed to narrate the journey of participants through the Francophonie, emphasizing themes of unity, diversity, and cultural exchange among the competing delegations.[60] Congolese artist Fally Ipupa performed during the ceremony, contributing to the celebratory atmosphere amid final preparations for the games.[75] The closing ceremony occurred on August 6, 2023, primarily at the Stade des Martyrs in Kinshasa, concluding the 10-day competition that included sports, arts, and cultural showcases.[76] It highlighted the achievements of participants, with performances by local artists such as FerrÃ© Gola, who delivered a concert segment, and references to Fally Ipupa's involvement at related venues like the Palace of the People.[77] [78] The ceremonies overall underscored the host nation's efforts to project Francophone solidarity, despite logistical challenges reported in infrastructure readiness prior to the event.[10]

Reception and Legacy

Immediate Post-Event Assessments

The International Committee of the Jeux de la Francophonie deemed the 2023 edition a success, with Director General Zeina Mina highlighting the event's completion despite two postponements and logistical hurdles, crediting the Democratic Republic of the Congo's efforts supported by the Organisation internationale de la Francophonie. Participation included 3,535 athletes, artists, and officials from 37 nations, approaching the levels of the 2017 Abidjan Games, and no major security incidents were reported during the July 28 to August 6 period in Kinshasa.[88][56] Local enthusiasm was evident in large crowds overwhelming venues like the 80,000-capacity Stade des Martyrs, where tens of thousands attended events including the closing football match between Cameroon and Burkina Faso, though many were turned away due to capacity limits. The Organisation internationale de la Francophonie noted high performance levels, with several athletics records broken and cultural competitions judged by international experts, emphasizing the event's role in showcasing francophone youth talent aged 18-35. The host nation achieved a historic fourth-place finish in the medal table with 34 medals, including 5 gold, particularly strong in wrestling.[89][90][56] Challenges included rushed infrastructure preparations, with some facilities completed only days before opening, alongside initial shortages of water, food lines, and transport disruptions that were gradually addressed. Participation from European and Canadian francophone entities was limitedâ€”France entered only wrestling and judo, Quebec withdrew entirely citing safety and health concerns, and Wallonie-Bruxelles secured just two medalsâ€”reflecting reluctance amid perceptions of organizational risks in the DRC. Mina described the outcome as a "Congolese miracle," underscoring the event's improbable realization but also its potential for lasting infrastructure gains for local youth sports.[89][56][88]

Economic and Social Impacts

The organization of the 2023 Jeux de la Francophonie in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), incurred significant economic costs, with the initial budget of 48 million USD escalating to 324 million USD by the event's conclusion. The excess spending strained public finances in a country already facing economic challenges, including reliance on extractive sectors and fiscal pressures from conflict-affected regions, without corresponding detailed reports of proportional revenue generation or long-term fiscal returns. Infrastructure developments included new facilities for combat sports and two standardized halls for table tennis, intended to provide lasting assets for local sports development, though their utilization and maintenance costs post-event remain undocumented in public assessments. Economically, the games aimed to foster sports diplomacy and enhance DRC's international visibility, potentially spurring partnerships in trade and investment through cultural and athletic exchanges among participating nations. However, these benefits were curtailed by widespread withdrawals from major contributors such as France, Canada, Quebec, and New Brunswick, citing health, safety, and logistical concerns, which limited athlete and visitor numbers to around 3,000 from 34 nations and reduced anticipated tourism inflows. No verified data quantifies direct economic multipliers like hotel occupancy or local business revenue, but the event's modest scale and security deploymentsâ€”intensified in Kinshasa streetsâ€”likely diverted resources from other public priorities without evident compensatory gains. Socially, the games promoted youth engagement and Francophonie unity, featuring competitions in sports and arts that highlighted cultural diversity and peace-building initiatives, with legacy programs like awareness campaigns on social issues led by organizations such as Peace and Sport targeting young participants. Hosting the event marked a milestone for DRC, enabling national pride through the first-time accommodation of international delegations and fostering interpersonal ties among French-speaking communities. Yet, the boycotts by prominent Western participants underscored perceptions of instability and insecurity in Kinshasa, exacerbating social tensions and damaging the country's image as a reliable host for global gatherings. Post-event reflections noted limited palpable legacies two years later, with criticisms focusing on unfulfilled promises of community uplift amid the financial scandal's fallout.
