2023 Huwara shooting

The 2023 Huwara shooting was a Palestinian terrorist attack on 26 February 2023, in which gunman Abdel Fattah Kharousha fatally shot two Israeli brothers, 21-year-old Hallel Yaniv and 19-year-old Yagel Yaniv, while they drove through the West Bank town of Huwara south of Nablus.[1][2] The assailant fired multiple rounds at close range into their vehicle before fleeing on foot, prompting an immediate Israel Defense Forces (IDF) manhunt and road closures in the area.[1] In the ensuing hours, dozens of Israeli settlers from nearby communities launched a retaliatory rampage through Huwara, setting fire to Palestinian homes, cars, and businesses, and clashing with local residents.[3] This violence resulted in the death of one Palestinian, 37-year-old Sameh Aqtash, shot in the abdomen, and injuries to over 100 Palestinians, alongside reports of assaults with stones, metal bars, and other weapons.[4][3] The IDF condemned the settler actions as "actions of terror" and "violent riots," deploying additional battalions to restore order, detaining eight suspects, and stating that such vigilantism undermined security efforts.[4][3] The incident, occurring amid rising West Bank tensions, drew international condemnation of the settler violence while highlighting ongoing Palestinian attacks on Israelis, with Defense Minister Yoav Gallant vowing to pursue both the original gunman and riot perpetrators to enforce law and order.[1][4]

Background and context

Location and historical tensions

Huwara is a Palestinian town in the Nablus Governorate of the West Bank, positioned about 9 kilometers south of Nablus city along Route 60, the principal north-south artery traversing the region.[5] With a population of approximately 7,500 residents, the town operates as a vital commercial hub for adjacent Palestinian communities, hosting markets, gold traders, and olive-related enterprises that draw local traffic.[6] A longstanding Israeli checkpoint south of Huwara regulates Palestinian movement toward Ramallah and beyond, while the adjacent roadway facilitates Israeli access to northern areas.[7] The town's placement near several Israeli settlementsâ€”Yitzhar, Itamar, Har Bracha, and Elon Morehâ€”has fostered chronic tensions, as Route 60 serves both Palestinian locales and settler routes branching eastward into the Samarian hills.[8] These dynamics have precipitated repeated clashes, including Palestinian stone-throwing and occasional shooting attacks targeting Israeli vehicles passing through the area, prompting Israeli Defense Forces patrols and operations to mitigate security risks.[9] Pre-2023, the West Bank recorded heightened Palestinian terrorism, with 2022 marking a surge in roadside assaults near Nablus-area hotspots like Huwara, alongside IDF countermeasures such as arrests and infrastructure reinforcements to protect civilian transit.[10] Settler incursions into surrounding Palestinian villages have also occurred, often in response to prior attacks, exacerbating mutual distrust in this contested corridor.

Preceding violence in the West Bank

In 2022, Palestinian terrorist attacks against Israelis in the West Bank (Judea and Samaria) surged significantly, with 86 major incidents recorded, more than a 50% increase from 54 in 2021. These attacks primarily consisted of 46 shootings (54% of total significant attacks), 25 stabbings, and 7 vehicular rammings, concentrated in the West Bank alongside some in Israel proper. The violence resulted in 31 Israeli fatalities, comprising 25 civilians and 6 security personnel, a sharp rise from 3 deaths in 2021.[11][12] The escalation began with a wave of attacks in spring 2022, particularly from March to May, when 21 Israelis were killed in a series of stabbings, shootings, and rammings originating mainly from West Bank perpetrators. This prompted the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) to launch Operation Break the Wave, a counterterrorism campaign involving raids to dismantle attack networks and seize weapons. Shooting incidents alone jumped to 285 documented attacks in 2022, compared to 61 the prior year, reflecting improved access to firearms among militants in areas like Jenin and Nablus.[10][13][12] Into early 2023, the pattern persisted amid rising threats from localized militant groups, such as the Jenin Battalion, which coordinated ambushes and shootings against Israeli targets. On January 26, 2023, IDF forces conducted a raid in Jenin refugee camp targeting suspects linked to recent attacks, resulting in a firefight that killed 9 Palestinians, including alleged militants, and wounded others; the operation was part of efforts to neutralize bomb-making labs and arrest operatives amid an environment of near-daily clashes. Such counterterrorism actions followed a year of heightened Israeli civilian vulnerability on West Bank roads, where security coordination gaps in Palestinian Authority-controlled zones left settlers reliant on self-defense measures.[11][14][15]

Initial Palestinian shooting attack

Incident timeline

On the evening of February 26, 2023, a Palestinian gunman opened fire at close range on an Israeli-owned vehicle during a traffic jam on Route 60 highway near Huwara, a Palestinian town south of Nablus in the West Bank.[1][16] The targeted car carried brothers Hallel Yaniv, 21, and Yagel Yaniv, 19, residents of the Har Bracha settlement located approximately 8 km from the site.[1][16] Investigators recovered 12 nine-millimeter shell casings, indicating the use of a handgun or makeshift submachine gun, with the vehicle riddled by bullets.[1] Medics pronounced both brothers dead at the scene from their gunshot wounds.[1] The Israel Defense Forces immediately classified the shooting as a terror attack, launched a manhunt for the assailant, and closed multiple roads in the northern West Bank to facilitate the search.[1][16]

Israeli victims

The Israeli victims were brothers Hallel Menachem Yaniv, aged 21, and Yagel Ya'acov Yaniv, aged 19, both residents of the Har Bracha settlement in Samaria.[17] [18] As religious Zionist yeshiva students, they were unarmed civilians driving home from studies in Jerusalem in their personal vehicle when attacked.[19] [20] On February 26, 2023, the assailant fired at close range on their car along Route 60 near Huwara, killing both brothers at the scene.[1] [21] Their deaths underscored the vulnerability of Israeli settlers transiting through Palestinian-controlled territories, where routine travel exposes individuals to targeted violence without immediate security presence.[18] Thousands attended the brothers' joint funeral at Mount Herzl military cemetery in Jerusalem on February 27, 2023, reflecting widespread communal mourning and frustration over persistent security lapses in the West Bank.[22] [23] The victims' parents addressed the crowd, expressing devastationâ€”"no words can console us"â€”while urging national unity rather than reprisals against the military.[17] [21] This event amplified calls from the Har Bracha community for enhanced protection amid rising attacks on settlers.[24]

Assailant and motivations

The assailant in the February 26, 2023, shooting was an unidentified Palestinian gunman who fired an M16 rifle at an Israeli vehicle during a traffic jam on Route 60 near Huwara, killing brothers Hallel and Yonatan Eliezer, both residents of Har Bracha settlement.[1][25] The attacker approached the car on foot, fired multiple rounds at close range, and fled toward nearby Palestinian villages without being apprehended on site.[1] Israeli security forces, including the IDF and Shin Bet, conducted an investigation using ballistics evidence from recovered shell casings and witness testimonies describing the gunman's deliberate targeting of the vehicle after confirming its Israeli occupants.[1] In August 2023, authorities identified a prime suspect as a member of the Bani Fadl clan from the village of Aqraba, east of Nablus; the IDF subsequently mapped the suspect's family home for potential demolition under policies targeting terrorist infrastructure, though the individual remains at large as of the latest reports.[26][25] No arrests have been reported by Palestinian Authority security forces, highlighting persistent coordination challenges in Area A jurisdictions where Huwara is located.[26] The incident was classified by Israeli officials as a terrorist attack, consistent with a surge of over 20 similar drive-by shootings in the West Bank since early 2023, often executed by individuals or small cells employing opportunistic tactics against civilian targets.[1] While no specific militant faction claimed responsibility, the methodâ€”exploiting routine travel routes with automatic weaponsâ€”mirrors operations linked to groups like Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad, which have historically glorified such "resistance" actions against Israeli presence in Judea and Samaria.[25] Broader context includes public incitement via Palestinian media and social platforms praising attacks on settlers as legitimate, though direct attribution to organized directives remains unconfirmed in this case.[1]

Israeli settler retaliation

Rampage events

Following the fatal shooting of two Israeli settlers in Huwara on February 26, 2023, groups of Israeli settlers from nearby outposts such as Yitzhar began converging on the town late that evening, escalating into a coordinated rampage that continued into the early morning of February 27.[27] Estimates indicate approximately 400 settlers participated, arriving in vehicles and targeting Palestinian areas along the main road through Huwara.[28] The violence was triggered directly by news of the earlier attack, with settlers shouting calls for revenge as they blocked access roads, preventing Palestinian residents from fleeing or receiving aid.[29] Settlers conducted arson attacks, igniting fires in dozens of Palestinian-owned vehicles and attempting to set ablaze homes and shops, while physically assaulting locals with stones, clubs, and improvised weapons during clashes.[27][30] Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) soldiers were stationed in Huwara at the time, having been present for routine security duties, but reports indicate they exercised restraint and did not disperse the settlers effectively during the peak of the disturbances, with additional troops arriving only after the main violence subsided.[31] Amid the chaos, gunfire erupted from within the settler groups, resulting in the death of at least one Palestinian, Eyad Hmeidan, aged 37, who was shot by a settler.[27][29] The rampage abated by dawn, leaving immediate scenes of burning debris and disrupted infrastructure, though comprehensive damage tallies emerged later.[32]

Palestinian casualties and property damage

One Palestinian man was killed during the clashes accompanying the settler rampage in Huwara and nearby areas on February 26, 2023. Sameh Aqtash, aged 37, was shot dead by Israeli security forces gunfire at the Za'atra checkpoint south of Huwara, amid reports of settlers advancing through the town.[3] Palestinian medics treated approximately 100 to 400 individuals for injuries sustained in the violence, with most cases involving beatings by settlers, stone-throwing, and exposure to tear gas deployed by Israeli forces to disperse crowds.[27][30] The Palestinian Red Crescent Society reported over 100 injuries in Huwara alone, including gunshot wounds to several people, though the exact attribution between settler actions and military responses remains disputed in eyewitness accounts.[33] Property damage was extensive, with settlers setting fire to over 100 vehicles, including cars and trucks, along main roads and in residential areas of Huwara.[34] Several homes, businesses, and olive trees were also torched or vandalized, leading to significant disruption of local commerce and agriculture in the town, which relies heavily on traffic through the area.[27][35] Israeli rights groups documented the destruction as amounting to a coordinated assault on civilian infrastructure.[36]

Immediate responses

Israeli military and government actions

Following the Palestinian shooting attack on February 26, 2023, Israel Defense Forces (IDF) troops rapidly deployed to the Huwara area to secure the scene, evacuate casualties, and initiate a manhunt for the assailant.[3] Security forces established checkpoints and conducted searches in the vicinity to prevent further immediate threats. As the settler retaliation escalated that evening, IDF personnel were already present in Huwara but encountered significant resistance from a large crowd of approximately 400-1,000 settlers engaged in arson and assaults, limiting their ability to fully disperse the groups despite orders to do so.[31] Additional IDF reinforcements were dispatched to the town later that night after the initial violence peaked, with hundreds more troops sent to the broader West Bank region on February 27 to bolster security and enforce order.[31] [30] Authorities implemented temporary road closures, including at Huwara's main entrances, and arrested at least eight settlers on site for suspected involvement in the violence.[30] [37] Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu issued a statement condemning vigilantism, urging restraint with the words, "don't take the law into your hands," and affirming that such actions did not represent Jewish values.[28] Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich, a settler advocate and minister in the defense portfolio, rejected private retaliation while calling for the state to decisively address Huwara's role in facilitating terrorism, stating that the village "needs to be erased" through official action rather than mob violence.[38]

Palestinian Authority and local reactions

The Palestinian Authority strongly condemned the settler rampage in Huwara on February 26, 2023, with President Mahmoud Abbas describing it as a "dangerous escalation that threatens security and stability in the region."[39] Senior PA official Hussein al-Sheikh labeled the violence "unprecedented," attributing it to Israeli government policies and calling for international intervention to safeguard Palestinian communities from further assaults.[30] The PA's statements emphasized the scale of destruction, including burned vehicles and homes, while avoiding endorsement of the preceding Palestinian shooting that killed two Israeli settlers. Huwara residents reported widespread fear and disruption during the attacks, with families sheltering in homes as settlers smashed windows, looted properties, and ignited fires that threatened structures; Israeli forces later evacuated dozens of Palestinians from endangered buildings.[40] Local accounts highlighted a sense of vulnerability, with one resident recounting settlers chanting threats and destroying personal belongings in what was described as the most severe incursion in years, prompting some to flee temporarily or arm themselves with basic tools for self-defense amid reports of sporadic resistance efforts like stone-throwing.[29] Community leaders noted ongoing trauma, including injuries to over 350 Palestinians, underscoring internal concerns over inadequate protection and the risk of depopulation. In contrast to PA condemnations of the settler response, militant groups Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad praised the initial February 26 shooting by their affiliated gunman, Abd al-Fattah Hussein Ibrahim Gharusha, as a "heroic" act of resistance against occupation, urging escalation of similar operations to deter Israeli presence in the West Bank.[41][42] These statements reflected factional dynamics, where PA restraint clashed with militants' advocacy for intensified armed actions following perceived provocations.

Investigations and legal outcomes

Probe into the initial shooting

Following the shooting on February 26, 2023, which killed brothers Hallel Yaniv, 21, and Yagel Yaniv, 19, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) and Shin Bet launched an immediate manhunt for the perpetrator, deploying ground forces and intelligence assets in the Nablus region.[43] Forensic examination of the scene, including bullet casings and vehicle damage, indicated a single gunman armed with an M16 rifle who approached the victims' car at close range before fleeing toward Jenin.[44] Shin Bet intelligence identified Abdel-Fattah Hussein Kharousha, a 49-year-old Hamas operative from the Askar refugee camp near Nablus, as the assailant based on surveillance, informant data, and ballistic matches.[44][45] On March 7, 2023, IDF special forces raided the Jenin refugee campâ€”a known militant hubâ€”where Kharousha was killed during an exchange of fire, alongside five other gunmen.[43] The operation underscored evidentiary and operational obstacles, including the absence of Palestinian Authority handover of suspects from militant-controlled areas and the need for high-risk raids amid armed resistance.[46] No arrest or trial ensued, as the perpetrator was neutralized in the raid, with his family home demolished by the IDF on August 8, 2023, as a deterrent measure.[44] As of 2025, the case remains closed without judicial proceedings, exemplifying challenges in prosecuting West Bank attackers linked to groups like Hamas amid limited inter-authority cooperation.[45]

Indictments and trials of settlers

Israeli authorities arrested several settlers in the days following the February 26, 2023, rampage in Huwara, with reports indicating eight individuals initially detained on suspicion of involvement in assaults and property damage before being released.[47] On March 1, 2023, six additional settlersâ€”four adults and two minorsâ€”were arrested for suspected public disturbances, arson, assault, and property damage during the riots.[48] Prosecutors filed indictments against two settlers in their 20s on March 30, 2023, charging them with grievous bodily harm, vandalism, and acts designated as terrorism for an axe assault on Palestinians in Huwara on March 6, 2023.[49] The attack involved the suspects using an axe and rocks against cars and occupants near shops, wounding two Palestinians while shouting anti-Arab slogans; it occurred amid heightened tensions following the February shooting of Israeli brothers Hillel and Yagel Yaniv.[49] In June 2024, the two settlers were convicted and each sentenced to three years in prison for the axe attack, reflecting judicial determination of intentional violence despite the broader context of retaliatory unrest.[50] Israeli human rights group Yesh Din has criticized overall low accountability for settler violence, noting that 93% of investigations into such incidents from 2005 to 2022 were closed without indictment, though specific Huwara cases represent rare prosecutions amid limited arrests directly tied to the February events.[49]

Political and international reactions

Israeli domestic debate

Opposition figures and military leaders strongly condemned the settler rampage in Huwara on February 26, 2023, following the shooting deaths of brothers Yagel and Hillel Yaniv by a Palestinian gunman earlier that day.[51] [29] National Unity party leader Benny Gantz described the events as a "security disaster," accusing Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of prioritizing coalition politics over effective protection for Israelis amid rising West Bank attacks.[52] Yesh Atid leader Yair Lapid stated that the government had "lost control," while Central Command head Maj. Gen. Yehuda Fuchs labeled the violence a "pogrom" by "Jewish extremists," warning it spread terror and could lead to Israeli fatalities in future clashes.[52] [51] Right-wing coalition members framed the rampage as a spontaneous outburst of frustration amid a wave of Palestinian shootings, with some explicitly endorsing aggressive state responses for deterrence.[53] Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich declared that Huwara, seen as a base for repeated attacks on Israeli vehicles, "should be wiped out" by the state, later clarifying he meant civilian-led destruction rather than an IDF operation but maintaining the need for severe repercussions against villages enabling terrorism.[38] [54] Members of the Otzma Yehudit party, including MK Zvika Fogel, supported the riots as a legitimate reaction, arguing for expanded settlement outposts to enhance security and prevent further vulnerabilities along key routes like Highway 60.[55] Netanyahu himself condemned vigilante actions as playing into enemies' hands but announced IDF enforcement in Huwara to reassert control, aligning with hawks' emphasis on bolstering presence over restraint.[56] [28] Israeli media outlets, such as The Times of Israel, covered the debate by contextualizing the rampage against the backdrop of escalating Palestinian attacksâ€”over 20 Israelis killed in the West Bank in early 2023â€”while critiquing the disproportionate retaliation that injured nearly 400 Palestinians and destroyed property.[51] Reports highlighted internal divisions, with over 50 Knesset members from right-wing parties demanding the release of arrested rioters, viewing administrative detentions as excessive amid perceived military failures to curb terrorism.[57] Defense Minister Yoav Gallant rejected these calls, terming the events a "mark of shame" and underscoring the need for law enforcement to restore order without undermining deterrence strategies.[57]

Palestinian and Arab world perspectives

Palestinian President Mahmoud Abbas condemned the settler rampage in Huwara on February 26, 2023, describing it as "terrorist acts carried out by settlers under the protection of the occupation army" and holding the Israeli government responsible for the violence.[39][28] The Palestinian Authority's Foreign Ministry echoed this, framing the attacks as coordinated with Israeli Defense Forces presence and demanding international intervention to halt settler aggression in the West Bank.[58] Palestinian media and officials portrayed the events as an unprovoked "pogrom" or act of "settler terror" facilitated by Israeli military inaction, with reports emphasizing the burning of over 100 vehicles and homes alongside the death of one Palestinian civilian, while attributing the preceding shooting of two Israeli settlers to isolated resistance rather than a trigger for collective reprisal.[59][60] This narrative often highlighted alleged IDF complicity, such as soldiers standing by during the riots, to underscore claims of systemic protection for settlers amid occupation dynamics.[60] In the broader Arab world, the Arab League issued a statement on February 27, 2023, condemning the settler attacks on Huwara and nearby villages like Einbus and Burin as "crimes" that killed one Palestinian and injured others, urging global accountability for what it described as escalated Israeli aggression.[61] The Arab Parliament similarly denounced the assault on Hawara as part of ongoing settler violence against Palestinians, calling for unified regional measures to counter such incidents.[62] Hamas, operating from Gaza, labeled the riots a "declaration of war" that crossed "all red lines," framing them as evidence of unchecked settler extremism.[63] These responses typically focused on the rampage's destructiveness without referencing the initial Palestinian shooting that killed brothers Hillel and Yagel Yaniv earlier that evening, prioritizing condemnation of Israeli actions over contextual symmetry.[36]

Global media and diplomatic responses

The United Nations Middle East peace envoy Tor Wennesland expressed grave concern on February 27, 2023, over the killings of two Israelis and subsequent injuries in Huwara, urging maximum restraint from both Israelis and Palestinians amid escalating violence. The UN Security Council held an emergency meeting on West Bank violence, including the Huwara events, highlighting a pattern of incidents that contravened resolutions, though statements emphasized de-escalation without specific condemnation of the initial Palestinian shooting.[64][65] The European Union declared itself alarmed by the Huwara violence on February 26, 2023, calling for immediate intervention by authorities on all sides to halt the escalation, with subsequent demands from the EU envoy to Palestinians for accountability, prosecution of perpetrators in the settler rampage, and compensation for affected Palestinians. The United States, via State Department spokesperson Ned Price on February 27, 2023, condemned the Palestinian terror shooting that killed two Israeli brothers while stating expectations for Israel to prosecute settlers involved in the retaliatory attacks, reflecting pressure on outposts and settlements.[66][67][68] Global media coverage, including from BBC and Al Jazeera, prioritized the settler rampage, with BBC describing the events as one of the worst cases of mass settler violence in years and Al Jazeera framing it as "unprecedented" havoc wreaking injury on hundreds of Palestinians and extensive property damage. While both outlets noted the preceding Palestinian gunman's killing of two Israelis, headlines and lead paragraphs emphasized the retaliation's scale, such as arson and assaults, over the initiating attack's context. Critiques from the media monitoring group CAMERA highlighted selective emphases and evidential weaknesses in some reporting, including CNN's June 2023 investigation alleging Israeli military complicity through video analysis and soldier testimony that lacked robust corroboration, prioritizing narrative over comprehensive footage showing soldiers attempting to intervene.[40][30][69]

Broader implications

Escalation of West Bank violence

In 2023, the West Bank experienced a record level of settler violence against Palestinians, with Israeli NGO Yesh Din documenting 1,232 incidents, including the deaths of 10 Palestinians.[70] This surge coincided with heightened Palestinian terrorist activity, resulting in over 30 Israeli civilian deaths from attacks in the region.[71] Specific to Huwara, a Palestinian gunman killed two Israeli civiliansâ€”a father and sonâ€”on August 19 in a shooting at a car wash, underscoring the area's volatility.[72] The October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks on Israel triggered a sharp escalation, with Yesh Din recording 242 settler violence incidents in the subsequent two months alone.[70] Overall, more than 1,000 such incidents occurred from October 2023 onward, contributing to the displacement of over 1,300 Palestinians through intimidation and property destruction.[73] Palestinian attacks also intensified, with multiple fatalities among Israelis, though Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) operations targeting militant networksâ€”such as raids dismantling explosive manufacturing sites and eliminating key figuresâ€”curbed some immediate threats from organized terror groups.[74] By 2025, violence persisted at elevated levels, with an estimated 30 additional settler attacks in the West Bank's early months and ongoing clashes amid broader Israeli counterterrorism efforts.[75] From October 7, 2023, to October 3, 2025, direct violence in the West Bank claimed 1,048 Palestinian lives, largely from IDF actions against militants, while settler-related incidents continued to fuel local tensions.[76] These patterns reflected a bidirectional intensification, with Huwara remaining a flashpoint for retaliatory cycles.

Debates on security, settlements, and causation

Proponents of Israeli settlement policy in the West Bank argue that such communities serve as strategic buffers against Palestinian terrorist incursions, providing early warning and populated depth to deter attacks originating from densely populated Arab areas. This perspective posits that the absence of a robust Jewish presence would leave Israel's pre-1967 borders vulnerable to the kind of cross-border raids seen historically, with settlements enabling rapid response capabilities and intelligence gathering in contested territories. In the context of the Huwara incident, where Palestinian gunmen initiated the violence by killing two Israeli civilians on February 26, 2023, defenders of this view contend that sporadic settler reprisals reflect frustration with perceived failures by Israeli security forces to neutralize ongoing threats, such as the frequent shootings along Route 60, rather than inherent aggression unmoored from defensive needs.[77] Critics, often aligned with left-leaning Israeli and international organizations, assert that settlement expansion inherently provokes Palestinian violence by fragmenting territory, restricting movement, and creating friction points that escalate cycles of retaliation. They cite data from groups like Yesh Din showing hundreds of settler attacks annually, framing these as root causes that undermine security for all parties and fuel recruitment into militant groups. However, empirical analyses challenge this causation by noting that Palestinian terrorist attacks in the West Bankâ€”such as the 29 fatalities from shootings and stabbings in 2022 aloneâ€”predate and persist independently of many outposts, driven more by ideological rejectionism and incitement than territorial disputes. Shin Bet reports indicate over 1,000 thwarted major terror plots in the West Bank in 2024, predominantly initiated by Palestinian networks targeting civilians, underscoring that terror initiations overwhelmingly precede and outnumber settler responses.[78][79] A truth-seeking examination reveals systemic biases in source selection, where mainstream media and UN-affiliated reports frequently emphasize settler actions while contextualizing or minimizing the agency behind Palestinian attacks, such as the Huwara shooting itself, which triggered the subsequent unrest. This selective framing aligns with documented left-wing tilts in academia and outlets like The Guardian, which likened the events to "Kristallnacht" without equivalent scrutiny of the initial fatalities. Causal realism prioritizes verifiable sequences: the February 26 killings by Palestinian assailants as the proximate cause, against a backdrop where Israeli casualties from West Bank terror (23 deaths pre-October 2023) stem from asymmetric warfare, not symmetric provocation. Settlement critics rarely engage data showing terror spikes during periods of minimal expansion, like the Second Intifada, suggesting ideological factorsâ€”Palestinian Authority payments to attackers and Hamas glorificationâ€” as stronger drivers than land use.[80][29][78]
