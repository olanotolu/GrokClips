Hagai Levi


Hagai Levi (born 1963) is an Israeli television writer, director, and producer renowned for his intimate explorations of human psychology and relationships.[1]
Levi created, wrote, and directed the Israeli series Be'Tipul (2005–2008), a critically acclaimed psychotherapy drama later adapted by HBO as In Treatment, which brought him international recognition.[2][3]
He co-created and co-produced the Showtime series The Affair (2014–2019), earning a Golden Globe Award for Best Television Series – Drama as part of the production team.[4]
Levi's other notable works include directing HBO's Scenes from a Marriage (2021), a modern reinterpretation of Ingmar Bergman's classic, and creating Our Boys (2019), a miniseries depicting the aftermath of a 2014 kidnapping in Israel.[5][6]
Born on a religious kibbutz and holding degrees in psychology from Bar-Ilan University and film from Tel Aviv University, Levi often incorporates therapeutic themes informed by his academic background and personal journey from Orthodox Judaism.[7][8][5]
In recent years, he has expanded into historical dramas, with Etty (2025) premiering at the Venice Film Festival as a series adaptation of Holocaust diarist Etty Hillesum's life, and developing The Girl Who Learned How to Kneel, a World War II-era project for Arte France.[9][10]

Early Life and Education

Upbringing in Israel

Hagai Levi was born on July 2, 1963, in Kibbutz Sha'alvim, a religious community in central Israel near Modi'in.[11][12] He grew up in an Orthodox Jewish environment within what has been described as Israel's most religious kibbutz, where communal life emphasized religious observance and collective values during a period of the country's post-independence consolidation.[5] Levi's family background included his mother's position as head of the kibbutz's culture department, which provided early access to media and films screened for the community.[8] This role likely influenced his initial exposure to cinema, as at age 16 he began working as a projectionist, tasked with editing films to remove content deemed inappropriate for the religious audience, such as risqué scenes.[13] By age 17, Levi had developed a strong personal affinity for films, marking an early divergence from the strict religious framework of his upbringing, though he remained immersed in kibbutz life.[8]

Academic Training in Film

Hagai Levi completed his formal academic training in film at Tel Aviv University's Department of Film and Television, graduating in the 1980s.[14] [8] The program, established in 1972 as Israel's oldest university-affiliated film school, focused on developing proficiency in core filmmaking disciplines including directing, screenwriting, editing, and production techniques.[15] This curriculum emphasized hands-on practical experience alongside theoretical study, preparing students for professional entry through workshops, project-based assignments, and exposure to both narrative and documentary forms.[15] Levi's training there honed foundational skills in visual storytelling and character-driven narratives, which later distinguished his approach to intimate, dialogue-intensive works, though specific mentors or coursework influences remain undocumented in available biographical accounts.[5] Upon graduation, Levi transitioned directly into professional filmmaking, leveraging the department's practical orientation to produce his debut feature shortly thereafter, marking the bridge from academic preparation to industry engagement without extended apprenticeship.[14] [3]

Professional Career

Early Work in Film and Criticism

Following his academic training, Hagai Levi entered the professional sphere as a film critic, contributing reviews and analyses to the Israeli daily newspaper Hadashot and local Tel Aviv outlets such as Tel Aviv Times and Zman Tel Aviv. This phase, spanning roughly a decade in the early 1990s, allowed him to engage deeply with cinematic techniques and narratives.[7][1] Concurrently, Levi lectured at leading Israeli film schools, where he instructed aspiring filmmakers on script development and visual storytelling, and served as head of the drama department at a principal Israeli television channel, refining his expertise in evaluating dramatic structure and character arcs. These roles built a foundation in critical assessment, emphasizing the psychological underpinnings of interpersonal dynamics in film.[1][16] Levi's transition to directing materialized in 1993 with his debut feature August Snow, a film he wrote and directed exploring a non-religious Israeli man's quest to reconnect with his lost love amid an Orthodox Jewish enclave. Clocking in at 82 minutes, the narrative delves into themes of romantic longing, cultural alienation, and personal reintegration, marking Levi's initial foray into independent narrative cinema.[17][18][7] Through short-form critiques and this early feature, Levi experimented with intimate examinations of relational tensions, foreshadowing his affinity for character-driven stories without venturing into broader production scales.[17][7]

Israeli Television Breakthroughs

Levi served as head of the drama department at Keshet, Israel's preeminent commercial television channel, from 2003 to 2006, where he supervised the creation of original scripted series and elevated standards for narrative depth amid the expansion of local production capabilities.[1] His pivotal domestic breakthrough arrived with Be'Tipul, a series he created, wrote, and co-directed alongside Ori Sivan and Nir Bergman, which premiered on November 29, 2005, and concluded after three seasons on December 25, 2008. The program centered on the professional and personal life of an Israeli psychotherapist, Reuven Dagan, portrayed by Assi Dayan, through real-time depictions of patient sessions that unfolded almost entirely in a single room, emphasizing unadorned dialogue to uncover layers of trauma, ethical dilemmas, and interpersonal tensions.[2][19][20] Be'Tipul resonated widely in Israel for its rigorous focus on internal psychological processes over external action, achieving strong viewership ratings and critical praise for portraying universal human struggles—such as guilt and relational breakdown—against the backdrop of societal pressures specific to Israeli life. The series earned multiple accolades from the Israeli Television Academy, including recognition for best drama, solidifying Levi's influence in pioneering introspective, character-driven formats that prioritized causal emotional realism in Israeli broadcasting.[21][20]

International Adaptations and Collaborations

Levi's Israeli series Be'Tipul (2005–2008), which he co-created with Ori Sivan and Nir Bergman, was adapted by HBO as In Treatment, premiering on January 28, 2008, with Levi serving as producer and co-director.[22][3] The U.S. version, starring Gabriel Byrne as therapist Paul Weston, retained the original's structure of 25-minute sessions exploring patient-therapist dynamics and the therapist's own therapy, influencing subsequent American prestige dramas focused on psychological introspection.[22] This adaptation highlighted Levi's ability to export intimate, dialogue-driven formats to international audiences, spawning further versions in countries including France and Brazil.[23] In 2014, Levi co-created and co-produced The Affair for Showtime with American writer Sarah Treem, with the series debuting on October 12 and running for five seasons until 2019.[2][24] The drama employed a nonlinear narrative alternating perspectives of two individuals entangled in infidelity, earning critical acclaim for its examination of subjective truth and relational causality, and achieving strong viewership on the network.[10] For its first season, The Affair won the Golden Globe Award for Best Television Series – Drama on January 11, 2015, with Levi credited as co-creator and executive producer.[24][25] These projects marked Levi's expansion into U.S. premium cable, fostering collaborations that bridged Israeli minimalist storytelling with American production scales and thematic emphases on personal accountability over external justifications.[26]

Recent Directorial Projects

In 2021, Levi directed the HBO miniseries Scenes from a Marriage, a five-episode adaptation of Ingmar Bergman's 1973 Swedish television series, starring Oscar Isaac and Jessica Chastain as a couple navigating divorce and self-examination.[27] The production delves into themes of relational intimacy, betrayal, and existential doubt, updating Bergman's framework to emphasize contemporary American psychological depth and interpersonal rawness.[28] Levi's direction employs long takes and confined settings to heighten emotional tension, marking a shift toward introspective character studies in enclosed domestic spaces.[29] Following this, Levi wrote and directed The Girl Who Learned How to Kneel (also known as Etty), a 2025 miniseries for Arte France and other European broadcasters, loosely based on the diaries of Dutch-Jewish writer Etty Hillesum, who documented her life in Nazi-occupied Amsterdam before her deportation to Auschwitz in 1943.[10] The six-episode series, shot in Dutch and German, portrays Hillesum's (played by Julia Windischbauer) journey from personal therapy to spiritual resilience amid rising persecution, drawing directly from her posthumously published writings such as An Interrupted Life.[9] Premiering out of competition at the 2025 Venice Film Festival and later at Israel's Cinema South Festival, the work modernizes Hillesum's introspective narrative by interweaving historical events with themes of inner documentation and endurance.[30] Levi's approach evolves his style toward historical realism blended with philosophical inquiry, using Hillesum's real-time reflections to explore survival without overt didacticism.[31]

Political Views and Public Stance

Criticism of Israeli Government

Hagai Levi has publicly expressed longstanding criticism of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's policies, particularly regarding military actions in Gaza and domestic governance reforms. In a September 2025 interview, Levi described himself as a "longtime critic of Israel's siege on Gaza," linking government policies to broader dehumanization processes that he paralleled in his work on violence justification.[32] He has voiced concerns that Netanyahu's leadership exacerbates internal divisions, stating that many Israeli artists, including himself, feel compelled to consider leaving the country due to the ongoing Gaza war and associated governmental stances.[33] These views align Levi with a cohort of left-leaning Israeli filmmakers opposing perceived encroachments on cultural freedoms under right-wing administrations.[34] In September 2022, Levi joined over 250 Israeli filmmakers, including Ari Folman and Nadav Lapid, in signing an open letter protesting the launch of the Shomron (Samaria/West Bank) Film Fund, which the signatories accused of serving as a mechanism for "whitewashing the occupation" and funding narratives that enable the oppression of Palestinians.[35] [36] The letter explicitly rejected cooperation with or funding from the initiative, framing it as an extension of settlement policies under Netanyahu's coalitions that prioritize ideological content over artistic independence. This action underscored Levi's alignment with dissenters viewing such funds as tools for governmental propaganda rather than neutral cultural support.[35] Levi's critiques extended to domestic protests against Netanyahu's 2023 judicial overhaul proposals, during which he was briefly arrested on March 23, 2023, while filming police actions at a Tel Aviv demonstration. Following the incident, Levi condemned the reforms as "a blasphemy," arguing they undermined democratic checks and mirrored authoritarian tendencies in the government's approach to dissent.[37] [38] He highlighted instances of "censorship by stealth," such as selective withdrawal of public funding for media critical of the administration, positioning these policies as threats to free expression within Israel.[38]

Advocacy Against Cultural Isolation

In September 2025, during an interview at the Venice Film Festival, Hagai Levi highlighted a growing emigration trend among Israeli artists amid the Gaza war, stating that "everyone around me is talking about the possibility of leaving" due to the conflict and fears of international cultural boycotts.[33] He predicted that "many" artists would relocate in the coming years, driven by difficulties in securing work, as well as separations from friends and family.[33] Levi contended that boycotts target the wrong elements within Israel, primarily affecting the artistic community where roughly 90% oppose the Netanyahu government and are already grappling with shrinking budgets and curbs on expression.[33][39] "Boycotting them is actually weakening them," he argued, asserting that such actions fail to pressure those in power while exacerbating vulnerabilities among war critics.[33][39] This stance reflects Levi's view that cultural isolation undermines domestic opposition to Netanyahu, a leader Levi has openly criticized alongside Israel's Gaza policies.[33] By muting internal dissenters, boycotts risk bolstering the government's position relative to its cultural adversaries, as the absence of amplified criticism from within reduces external leverage against official narratives.[33]

Responses to Accusations of Bias

Hagai Levi has defended his work against claims of anti-Israel bias by stressing a commitment to factual accuracy and psychological depth rather than political advocacy. In discussing Our Boys, which dramatizes the 2014 cross-murder of Israeli and Palestinian teenagers, Levi stated that the creators "couldn’t understand how this happened, and we had a real need to figure it out," leading to extensive interviews with investigators, families, and experts to adhere closely to documented events.[40] He emphasized maintaining proximity to the truth in depicting the revenge killing of Mohammed Abu Khdeir by Jewish extremists, while allowing artistic liberty only in personal narratives, positioning the series as an exploration of human motivations amid trauma rather than an indictment of Israel.[40] Levi has embraced backlash from across the political spectrum, noting that Our Boys drew fire from the Israeli right for alleged anti-Israel slant and from the left for perceived sympathy toward Israeli perspectives, which he views as evidence of the series' avoidance of simplistic agendas.[41] This aligns with his broader approach in intimate dramas like In Treatment, where he prioritizes individual psyche over ideological messaging, as seen in his insistence on collaborative Israeli-Palestinian production teams to foster nuanced portrayals of conflict dynamics.[42] Right-leaning critics, however, counter that Levi's focus disproportionately highlights Israeli moral failings, such as the revenge murder, while underemphasizing premeditated Palestinian incitement linked to Hamas, thereby amplifying international narratives of equivalence in asymmetric violence.[43] Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu labeled Our Boys as "anti-Semitic propaganda" that distorts facts to harm Israel's image, urging a boycott of broadcaster Keshet on September 2, 2019.[44] Levi has not directly rebutted such charges but maintains that cultural boycotts risk silencing internal Israeli dissent, as expressed in a September 17, 2025, interview where he warned that external isolation undercuts voices critical of Prime Minister Netanyahu's policies without abandoning national loyalty.[45]

Controversies

Backlash Over "Our Boys"

Hagai Levi co-directed the 2019 HBO miniseries Our Boys alongside Joseph Cedar and Tawfik Abu Wael, dramatizing the June 2014 kidnapping and murder of three Israeli teenagers—Eyal Yifrach, Gilad Shaer, and Naftali Fraenkel—by Hamas operatives in the West Bank, the subsequent vigilante killing of Palestinian teenager Mohammed Abu Khdeir in revenge, and the escalation to the 2014 Gaza War.[43] The series, produced in collaboration with Israeli broadcaster Keshet 12, drew from real events documented in Israeli police investigations and journalistic accounts, but incorporated fictionalized elements to explore motivations on both sides, including sympathetic portrayals of the Palestinian perpetrators' backgrounds.[43] The production faced immediate backlash in Israel for alleged false moral equivalence between the premeditated Hamas terror attack, which involved the teens' execution and disposal in shallow graves, and the impulsive revenge murder by Jewish extremists, with critics arguing it blurred distinctions between state-backed terrorism and individual vigilantism.[43] Right-leaning media watchdogs, such as the Committee for Accuracy in Middle East Reporting and Analysis (CAMERA), highlighted factual distortions, including the series' emphasis on Palestinian family dynamics and grievances while downplaying evidence of Hamas orchestration and the teens' desperate phone calls during captivity revealing coordinated abduction.[43] These analyses contended that such framing favored narratives sympathetic to Palestinian militants over the victimization of the Israeli teens, potentially misleading international audiences about the conflict's asymmetries.[43] On August 30, 2019, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu publicly condemned Our Boys as "anti-Semitic," urging Israelis to boycott Keshet 12 for airing content that he claimed falsified history and damaged the nation's image by equating Jewish victims with their killers. Culture Minister Miri Regev echoed this, labeling the series an act of "cultural terrorism" that tarnished Israel's global standing by promoting a distorted portrayal of events, and she accused it of anti-Semitic undertones in humanizing terrorists responsible for the kidnappings. Despite the high-profile calls for boycott, empirical data indicated minimal impact on viewership or broadcast; Keshet 12's ratings remained stable post-statement, and the series premiered on HBO to critical acclaim abroad without measurable dips in Israeli engagement, suggesting the controversy amplified rather than suppressed its reach.[46]

Protests and Funding Disputes

In September 2022, Hagai Levi signed an open letter alongside approximately 250 other Israeli filmmakers, including Ari Folman, Nadav Lapid, Oren Moverman, and Amos Gitai, protesting the launch of the Shomron Film Fund, a government-backed initiative to support cinematic projects in the Shomron region (also known as Judea and Samaria or the West Bank).[35][36] The fund, established with an initial budget allocation from Israel's Ministry of Culture and Sports, aimed to foster local film production in settlement areas to highlight regional stories and cultural development.[36] The signatories pledged not to apply for or accept funding from the Shomron Fund, nor to collaborate with it, arguing that it served to "whitewash" Israeli settlement activities and the ongoing "oppression of the Palestinian people" by normalizing presence in contested territories.[35][47] Critics of the petition, including voices from pro-settlement outlets, characterized the boycott as a politically motivated refusal by predominantly left-leaning artists to engage with funding mechanisms that diverge from their ideological preferences, potentially skewing public resources toward narratives aligned with opposition to government settlement policies.[48] This stance highlighted broader frictions in Israel's cultural sector, where reliance on state subsidies—totaling over 100 million shekels annually for film production through various funds—intersects with demands for content autonomy, often leading to accusations of selective allocation favoring urban, Tel Aviv-centric viewpoints over peripheral or ideologically contested ones.[36] The controversy underscored causal tensions in funding distribution: while the Shomron Fund represented an effort to decentralize support beyond established industry hubs, the collective pushback from Levi and peers revealed preferences for insulating artistic grants from projects perceived as endorsing territorial claims, thereby influencing the ideological balance of state-backed media output.[35] Despite the protests, the fund proceeded, funding initial projects that depicted life in West Bank communities, though it faced ongoing challenges in attracting broader industry participation amid the filmmakers' non-cooperation pledge.[49]

Impact on Israeli Cultural Discourse

Levi's co-creation of the 2019 HBO miniseries Our Boys, which dramatized the June 2014 kidnapping and murder of three Israeli teenagers by Hamas operatives and the subsequent revenge killing of Palestinian teenager Mohammed Abu Khdeir by Jewish extremists, ignited intense domestic debates on media's depiction of conflict-related traumas. The series' focus on the Abu Khdeir family's grief alongside Israeli investigations drew accusations of imbalance, with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu denouncing it as "anti-Semitic propaganda" on September 3, 2019, and urging a boycott of Keshet Broadcasting, the Israeli co-producer.[44] [50] This backlash amplified discussions on whether artistic portrayals emphasizing Israeli culpability foster empathy or exacerbate divisions, particularly when Hamas's role in initiating the chain of events received comparatively less narrative weight, leading to perceptions of selective moral framing amid ongoing security threats.[43] Public reactions, including protests against the series' airing on Israeli Channel 12, underscored tensions over art's capacity to challenge national narratives versus its potential to incite discord during vulnerability; families of the murdered Israeli teens publicly criticized the production for retraumatizing them without adequate emphasis on Palestinian incitement to violence.[51] Levi defended the work as an exploration of "complex reality," highlighting Israeli law enforcement's pursuit of justice for Abu Khdeir while acknowledging grief's universality, yet detractors contended it risked normalizing critiques that downplay asymmetric threats from groups like Hamas.[13] These exchanges contributed to a polarized discourse, with media analyses noting how Our Boys revived 2014 bereavement politics, prompting soul-searching on extremism within Israeli society but also warnings against media that could undermine collective resilience.[41] Levi's broader advocacy, including his September 2025 statements opposing cultural boycotts of Israel, frames art as a vehicle for internal dissent against government policies, such as those in the Gaza conflict, arguing that isolation disproportionately silences critics like himself who challenge Prime Minister Netanyahu's leadership.[33] He posited that boycotts empower uncritical pro-war voices internationally while stifling diverse Israeli perspectives, thereby enriching cultural debates on accountability but inviting counterarguments that such globalized critiques—via platforms like HBO—prioritize external validation over bolstering unity against existential perils. This duality illustrates art's dual-edged influence: amplifying empathetic examinations of conflict's human toll to diversify discourse, yet potentially fueling perceptions of self-flagellation that ignore causal realities of terrorism, as evidenced by the series' outsized controversy relative to prior Israeli dramas on similar themes.[50]

Awards and Recognition

Key Honors and Nominations

Hagai Levi co-created and co-produced The Affair, which received the Golden Globe Award for Best Television Series – Drama at the 72nd Golden Globe Awards on January 11, 2015, recognizing its excellence in dramatic storytelling and character development.[24][52] For the Israeli series Be'Tipul, which Levi created, co-produced, and co-directed, the production earned the Israeli Academy of Television Award for Best Drama Series, along with eight additional awards in categories such as best screenplay and best actor.[53][54] Levi shared the Israeli Academy's Best Director award with co-director Nir Bergman for their work on the series.[3] The HBO adaptation In Treatment, based on Be'Tipul with Levi as an executive producer and director of several episodes, garnered multiple Primetime Emmy nominations across its seasons, including for outstanding drama series and acting performances in 2008.[55][56] Levi's miniseries Our Boys won the Israeli Academy of Film and Television Award for Best Drama Series at the 2020 ceremony, acknowledging its production achievements amid high viewership.[57] In 2022, Levi received a Hollywood Critics Association TV Award nomination for Best Directing in a Broadcast Network or Cable Limited or Anthology Series for his episode "The Illiterates" in Scenes from a Marriage.[58]

Critical Acclaim for Specific Works

Levi's Be'Tipul (2005), the original Israeli series adapted internationally as In Treatment, garnered acclaim for its unflinching portrayal of psychotherapy sessions, emphasizing psychological realism and the ethical tensions in therapist-patient relationships. Academic analyses have highlighted its role in challenging cultural stigmas around mental health in Israel, where therapy was historically viewed with suspicion, and its narrative structure as a model for exploring transference and countertransference in media representations of psychoanalysis.[59][21] The series' influence extended to shaping the therapy-drama subgenre, with its bottle-episode format cited in studies for prioritizing dialogue-driven introspection over action, thereby influencing subsequent shows focused on emotional causality in personal crises. The global success of Be'Tipul's adaptations underscored its critical resonance, with remakes produced in at least five countries, including the U.S. HBO series (2008–2021, five seasons), France's En thérapie (2013–present), and Brazil's ongoing sixth season on Globoplay as of December 2024.[60] These versions retained the core fidelity to Levi's conception of raw, session-based storytelling, which Levi attributed to addressing universal "local traumas" while boosting Israeli television's export value, as evidenced by the format's licensing to over 20 territories.[61] Levi's 2021 HBO miniseries Scenes from a Marriage, an adaptation of Ingmar Bergman's 1973 work, earned praise for evolving the source material into a modern examination of relational dissolution, with reviewers noting its fidelity to Bergman's themes of intellectual incompatibility and emotional volatility while incorporating contemporary gender dynamics and polyamory.[28] Critics commended the performances of Jessica Chastain and Oscar Isaac for their intensity, with Roger Ebert awarding three stars for moments of "white-hot" confrontation amid occasional narrative lulls.[62] Metacritic aggregated a 70/100 score from 26 reviews, emphasizing superior acting that elevated dialogue-heavy scenes over plot contrivances.[63] Levi's direction, which favored explicit verbal exposition over visual subtlety, was seen as a deliberate extension of his therapy-focused style, prioritizing causal breakdowns in communication.[64]
