Geoffrey Fieger

Geoffrey Nels Fieger (born December 23, 1950) is an American trial attorney based in Southfield, Michigan, who founded the Fieger Law firm and has built a reputation for securing multimillion-dollar verdicts in personal injury, medical malpractice, and civil rights cases over more than four decades of practice.[1][2] Fieger earned a B.A. in 1974 and an M.A. in 1976 from the University of Michigan, followed by a J.D. from the Detroit College of Law at Michigan State University.[1][2] He is particularly noted for representing pathologist Jack Kevorkian in multiple assisted-suicide trials during the 1990s, securing acquittals in several instances.[3][4] Fieger's legal career includes landmark victories such as a $25 million verdict in 1999 against The Jenny Jones Show for contributing to a guest's murder, and a $144 million obstetrical malpractice award in 2011, among the largest in U.S. history.[5][6] His firm reports recovering hundreds of millions in client compensation, often in cases involving birth trauma, police misconduct, and auto negligence.[7] In politics, Fieger won the 1998 Democratic primary for Michigan governor but lost the general election to incumbent John Engler by a 62% to 38% margin.[8][9] He faced federal indictment in 2007 for alleged campaign finance violations involving reimbursed employee donations to political campaigns, but was acquitted on all counts in 2008.[10][11] Fieger's outspoken style and media presence have marked his career, including commentary on high-profile trials and involvement in recent litigation such as a $100 million suit over the 2021 Oxford High School shooting.[3] His approach emphasizes aggressive advocacy, drawing both praise for client outcomes and criticism for personal legal entanglements, including suits against his firm for alleged employment issues.[12][13]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Geoffrey Fieger was born in Detroit, Michigan, to Bernard Julian Fieger and June Beth Fieger (nÃ©e Oberer).[14][15] His father, Bernard, was an attorney who founded the personal injury law firm Fieger & Lee in Detroit in 1950, establishing it as Michigan's second such practice, and participated as a Freedom Rider in the civil rights movement during the early 1960s.[14][16] Fieger's mother, June, worked as a labor organizer for a teachers' union.[17] Fieger grew up in Oak Park, a northern suburb of Detroit, alongside siblings including his younger brother Doug Fieger, lead singer and guitarist of the rock band The Knack, and sister Beth Fieger Falkenstein.[16][15][18] The family's Jewish-Norwegian heritage reflected Bernard's paternal lineage and June's maternal background, influencing a household oriented toward activism, music, and legal advocacy.[15] His upbringing in a legal and socially engaged environment laid early groundwork for Fieger's career, with exposure to his father's firm fostering an interest in trial work from a young age.[16][19]

Academic and Professional Training

Fieger attended the University of Michigan, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1974 and a Master of Arts degree in 1976, both in theater and speech communication.[16] During his time there, he participated in acting and played football for the university.[16][20] Following his graduate studies, Fieger pursued legal education at the Detroit College of Law, obtaining his Juris Doctor degree in 1979.[16] The institution, originally independent, later affiliated with Michigan State University and became the Michigan State University College of Law.[21] His academic background in performance arts informed his later emphasis on dramatic courtroom techniques, though formal professional training beyond law school credentials is not extensively documented in primary records.[20]

Legal Career

Founding and Growth of Fieger Law Firm

The Fieger Law Firm originated from Fieger & Lee, established in 1950 by Geoffrey Fieger's father, Bernard Fieger, in partnership with George Lee in Detroit's Lawyers Building; this was Michigan's second interracial law firm.[22] Bernard Fieger focused the practice on representing injured workers and plaintiffs in personal injury cases, laying the groundwork for the firm's emphasis on advocacy for the underrepresented.[22] Geoffrey Fieger joined the firm upon graduating from Michigan State University College of Law in 1979, marking the beginning of its expansion under his leadership as a trial attorney.[16] In his inaugural trial that year, Geoffrey secured a $1 million verdict, which propelled the firm's reputation and attracted higher-profile personal injury and medical malpractice cases.[16] [22] The father-son collaboration continued until Bernard's retirement, after which Geoffrey assumed principal control, reorienting and renaming the firmâ€”eventually as Fieger Lawâ€”to prioritize aggressive litigation in civil rights, wrongful death, and multimillion-dollar verdicts.[3] Under Geoffrey's direction, the firm grew from a small Detroit operation into a Southfield-based powerhouse with 18 attorneys by the 2010s, handling nationwide cases and earning recognition as Michigan's leading personal injury practice.[3] This expansion was driven by Geoffrey's track record of securing some of the largest settlements in U.S. history for medical malpractice and product liability, including repeated multimillion-dollar awards that funded firm infrastructure and attracted specialized talent.[23] The firm's growth emphasized contingency-fee representation, enabling it to undertake resource-intensive trials without upfront client costs, which further differentiated it in competitive legal markets.[3] By the 2020s, Fieger Law had evolved into a litigation-focused entity with over 70 years of cumulative history, sustaining operations through high-stakes wins amid increasing specialization in catastrophic injury claims.[23]

Medical Malpractice Litigation

Fieger established his legal practice with a focus on medical malpractice suits against hospitals, physicians, and healthcare providers in Michigan, emphasizing cases of birth trauma, surgical negligence, and failure to diagnose. His approach often involves challenging medical experts and hospital protocols in court, resulting in jury awards that have frequently ranked among the highest in the state and nation. By 2019, his firm had secured 30 verdicts or settlements exceeding $1 million each in a single year, as tracked by Michigan Lawyers Weekly.[24] One landmark case occurred in 2011, when a Wayne County jury awarded $144.5 million to the family of an infant who suffered severe brain damage due to oxygen deprivation during delivery at William Beaumont Hospital in Royal Oak. The verdict, the largest obstetrical malpractice award in U.S. history at the time, attributed liability to delays in responding to fetal distress and improper use of delivery instruments; it was unanimously upheld by the Michigan Court of Appeals in 2014 despite claims of trial errors.[25][26][27] In 2018, Fieger obtained a $135 million verdict from a Wayne County jury against Detroit Medical Center Children's Hospital of Michigan for a 10-year-old girl left quadriplegic following a 2011 spinal fusion surgery to correct scoliosis. The award stemmed from allegations of postoperative negligence, including failure to monitor for and treat spinal cord compression; hospital defenses citing an undetected blood clot were rejected by the jury. This verdict was reported as one of the largest medical malpractice awards in Michigan.[28][29][30] Fieger's successes extend to other high-value outcomes, such as a $23.5 million award in 2019 against Presence St. Joseph Hospital in Illinois for brain damage to a child due to delivery complications, and multiple multimillion-dollar recoveries in birth injury and misdiagnosis claims documented annually by legal trackers. These verdicts have drawn scrutiny for their size amid Michigan's statutory caps on noneconomic damages in malpractice cases, which Fieger has contested in litigation and public statements, arguing they undermine accountability for severe harms.[31][32]

Defense of Jack Kevorkian

Geoffrey Fieger began representing Jack Kevorkian, a pathologist who assisted in the suicides of terminally ill patients using devices like the "Thanatron," in legal matters around 1990, following Kevorkian's involvement in the 1990 assisted suicide of Janet Adkins.[33] Fieger handled the defenses pro bono, motivated in part by his father's prolonged suffering from illness, and framed the cases around patient autonomy and the alleviation of intractable pain rather than criminal intent to kill.[33] [5] His representation spanned multiple proceedings, including at least three trials resulting in acquittals between 1993 and 1996, with Fieger's firm claiming successful defenses in seven murder-related trials overall before Kevorkian chose self-representation in his 1999 conviction.[34] [5] In the first major trial in August 1993, concerning the assisted suicide of ALS patient Thomas Hyde, Fieger argued that Kevorkian's actions aimed to end suffering rather than cause death, presenting emotional videotape evidence of Hyde's expressed wishes and securing an acquittal after nine hours of jury deliberation.[34] A subsequent trial involving a doctor with bone cancer and a woman with ALS also ended in acquittal, with Fieger emphasizing the patients' voluntary requests and challenging the prosecution's proof of criminal intent, particularly after an appeals court clarified jury instructions on the state's burden.[34] In December 1994, defending charges related to the 1991 assisted suicides of Marjorie Wantz (multiple sclerosis) and Sherry Miller (chronic pain), Fieger highlighted the absence of a specific assisted-suicide statute at the time and used theatrical tactics, such as displaying a blank poster to symbolize the lack of clear law, leading to another acquittal following extended jury deliberations.[34] [33] Fieger's courtroom strategies consistently relied on emotional appeals, including tearful testimonies from patients' families and video recordings of the individuals affirming their decisions, to underscore the voluntary nature of the acts and the patients' unbearable conditions.[33] He also engaged aggressively with the media to build public sympathy, portraying the cases as challenges to outdated laws prohibiting rational suicide for the competent and suffering. One additional acquittal occurred on May 14, 1996, in a trial tied to earlier assisted-suicide charges.[35] These defenses elevated Fieger's national profile, though he later distanced himself from Kevorkian's 1998 decision to publicly broadcast the lethal injection of Thomas Youk on 60 Minutes, which led to Kevorkian's self-representation and conviction for second-degree murder in 1999 after Fieger's dismissal.[5] [36]

Other High-Profile Cases and Verdicts

In addition to his defense of Jack Kevorkian, Fieger represented the family of Scott Amedure, who was murdered in 1995 by Jonathan Schmitz days after both appeared on The Jenny Jones Show in a "secret crush" segment that ambushed Amedure by revealing Schmitz's unrequited feelings. A Michigan jury awarded the Amedure family $25 million in 1999, including $5 million for Amedure's pain and suffering, $10 million to his mother for loss of society, $5 million to his brother, and $5 million to his father, holding the show's producers negligent for failing to anticipate risks from the format.[37][38] The Michigan Court of Appeals overturned the verdict in 2002, ruling the show owed no duty to protect guests from post-broadcast criminal acts by third parties.[39] Fieger's firm secured a $21 million verdict in 2015 against Beaumont Hospital (formerly Oakwood Hospital) for the 2012 death of 81-year-old Bimla Nayyar, who underwent unnecessary brain surgery after hospital staff mistakenly operated on her instead of performing a routine jaw procedure, removing part of her skull and causing fatal complications. The Wayne County jury found the hospital negligent in patient identification and verification protocols.[40][41] The Michigan Court of Appeals vacated the award in 2017 on procedural grounds, citing Fieger's introduction of improper evidence despite trial court rulings, and the state Supreme Court denied leave to appeal in 2018, leaving the family without recovery.[42][43] In medical malpractice litigation, Fieger co-counseled a $97.4 million verdict in 2022 for the parents of S.K., an Iowa City infant who suffered severe brain damage during a 2016 birth at Mercy Hospital due to delayed delivery and oxygen deprivation, marking Iowa's largest such award at the time; the Johnson County jury apportioned 70% fault to the hospital and 30% to the obstetrician's clinic.[44] The Iowa Supreme Court reversed the verdict in November 2024, ordering a new trial due to erroneous jury instructions on foreseeability and proximate cause.[45] Fieger's firm obtained a $135 million verdict in 2018 against Detroit Medical Center for a 17-year-old Wyandotte girl left quadriplegic after a routine procedure led to paralysis from alleged surgical errors and inadequate post-operative care.[46][28] The award, one of Michigan's largest in medical negligence, compensated for lifelong care needs but faced ongoing appeals amid statutory damage caps.[17]

Political Involvement

1998 Gubernatorial Campaign

Geoffrey Fieger, a Southfield-based personal injury attorney renowned for securing large verdicts and defending assisted-suicide advocate Jack Kevorkian, entered the 1998 Democratic primary for Michigan governor as a political outsider with no prior elected experience.[47] Campaigning on populist themes of challenging incumbent Republican John Engler's policies on education, taxes, and workers' rights, Fieger self-funded much of his effort through his law firm and emphasized his trial lawyer aggression against perceived corporate and political elites.[48] His bombastic style, including sharp attacks on opponents, resonated with some voters frustrated by Engler's tenure but drew rebukes from Democratic Party leaders who viewed him as too erratic for statewide office.[49] In the August 4, 1998, Democratic primaryâ€”a three-way contest against state Senator Larry Owen and businessman Morrison "Morrie" Colemanâ€”Fieger prevailed with 300,458 votes (41.18%), narrowly edging Owen's 272,360 (37.33%) amid low turnout of about 730,000 voters.[50] The upset win, achieved without formal party endorsement, highlighted Fieger's appeal to anti-establishment sentiments but exacerbated tensions with national Democrats; Vice President Al Gore, campaigning in Michigan, conspicuously avoided endorsing him.[51] Fieger selected former state representative James Agee as his running mate, positioning the ticket as a bulwark against Engler's reforms, including school choice expansions and welfare changes.[52] The general election campaign against Engler, a two-term incumbent enjoying high approval ratings for economic growth and education initiatives, devolved into personal sparring, with Fieger likening Engler to Senator Joseph McCarthy and accusing him of neglecting public schools.[53] [54] Fieger's ticket raised $5.8 million, the highest among Michigan gubernatorial candidates that cycle, yet his unfiltered rhetoricâ€”including past remarks dismissing religious figuresâ€”alienated moderates and failed to mobilize sufficient Democratic turnout.[52] On November 3, 1998, Engler secured a third term with 1,883,005 votes (62.20%), while Fieger received 1,146,084 (37.80%), a decisive defeat reflecting voter preference for Engler's record over Fieger's outsider insurgency.[8]

Post-1998 Political Activities and Statements

In the years following his 1998 gubernatorial campaign, Geoffrey Fieger primarily focused on his legal career but periodically engaged in political commentary and speculation about future runs. On January 10, 2017, he launched a television advertisement campaign featuring patriotic imagery and sharp rebukes of then-President-elect Donald Trump, accusing the campaign of fostering "bigotry, misogyny, intolerance and out-and-out racism" and lamenting a perceived regression in American progress.[55][56] The ads, which aired in Michigan markets, were widely viewed as Fieger testing the waters for a political comeback, with media outlets noting their alignment with his history of provocative rhetoric.[57] By May 12, 2017, Fieger publicly declared he was "darn serious" about pursuing the Democratic nomination for Michigan governor in the 2018 election, appearing on public radio to discuss his potential platform centered on economic revitalization and clear vision for the state.[58][59] He explicitly declined to foreclose a 2020 presidential bid, stating, "If I do run for governor, I'm not going to promise not to run for president," and positioned himself as an outsider capable of challenging establishment figures.[60][61] An early May 2017 poll among likely Democratic primary voters placed him ahead of rivals, including a 2-point lead over Gretchen Whitmer, though he ultimately chose not to file candidacy papers, allowing Whitmer to secure the nomination and governorship.[62][63] Fieger's post-1998 statements often drew parallels to his own past experiences, as in an August 2016 interview where he contrasted the Republican Party's handling of Trump's 2016 nominationâ€”deeming it more supportive than the Democratic establishment's treatment of his 1998 bidâ€”with critiques of intra-party dynamics.[64] In a February 2020 interview, he voiced ongoing disapproval of President Trump, offered measured assessments of Governor Gretchen Whitmer's early tenure amid state challenges, and evaluated the Democratic presidential field, reflecting his continued interest in national and Michigan-specific issues without formal involvement.[65] These interventions underscored Fieger's pattern of leveraging media platforms for unfiltered opinions, though they did not translate into sustained campaigns or endorsements of other candidates.

Controversies and Legal Challenges

Courtroom Tactics and Ethical Criticisms

Geoffrey Fieger's courtroom approach is characterized by aggressive, theatrical advocacy, often employing emotional appeals, dramatic reenactments, and pointed accusations against opposing parties and witnesses.[66] This style has yielded substantial verdicts in medical malpractice and personal injury cases but has drawn ethical scrutiny for crossing into misconduct, including discourtesy, misstatements of evidence, and irrelevant prejudicial injections.[66] Critics, including appellate courts, have argued that such tactics risk tainting proceedings with passion or prejudice rather than permitting fact-based deliberations.[67] In a 2007 Ohio Supreme Court ruling on a birth injury malpractice suit, Fieger's conduct prompted the reversal of a $30 million jury award, mandating a new trial.[67] During closing arguments, he impersonated the oxygen-deprived infant plaintiff, imploring, â€œDoctors, nurses, Iâ€™m suffocating. Please help me to be born. I want to play baseball. I want to hug my mother. I want to tell her that I love her. Help me,â€ which the court deemed excessively theatrical and prejudicial.[66] The justices further cited his frequent interruptions of opposing counsel, accusations of witness perjury without evidentiary basis, misrepresentations of record evidence, unfounded claims of evidence cover-ups by defendants, and gratuitous references to race and povertyâ€”none central to the negligence claimsâ€”as violations eroding trial integrity.[67][66] Fieger has faced professional discipline proceedings for extrajudicial statements impugning judicial integrity. Following a Michigan Court of Appeals reversal of a $15 million malpractice verdict on January 28, 1998, he aired a radio tirade labeling the three-judge panel "jackasses," "Hitler," and morally equivalent to Nazis for their ruling.[68] This prompted a grievance under Michigan Rules of Professional Conduct 3.5(c) (barring undignified courtroom conduct) and 6.5(a) (requiring respect for tribunals), resulting in a reprimand by the Attorney Discipline Board.[69] Although the Michigan Supreme Court vacated the discipline in 2006, holding the rules inapplicable to non-pending out-of-court speech absent impairment of justice administration, the episode underscored criticisms of Fieger's inflammatory rhetoric as undermining public confidence in the judiciary.[70][71] Fieger subsequently challenged the civility rule's constitutionality in federal court, but the Sixth Circuit dismissed the suit in 2009 for lack of standing, citing no ongoing harm.[68] Additional critiques have emerged from trial records and internal firm disputes. In federal civil rights litigation, such as Swans v. City of Lansing (1998), courts noted Fieger's prejudicial remarks and pervasive misconduct as factors warranting scrutiny, though not always warranting reversal.[72] A 2012 lawsuit by former partner Ven Johnson alleged Fieger's "professional misconduct" and "intolerable behavior," including profane outbursts and erratic decision-making during client meetings, contributing to their firm's dissolutionâ€”claims settled out of court in 2014 without admission of liability.[73][74] These incidents reflect a pattern where Fieger's high-stakes, confrontational methods, while client-favored for securing recoveries, have invited bar complaints and appellate interventions for ethical boundary-testing.

Federal Campaign Finance Indictment

In August 2007, Geoffrey Fieger and his law partner Vernon Johnson were indicted by a federal grand jury in the Eastern District of Michigan on charges related to alleged violations of federal campaign finance laws.[10] The indictment, returned on August 21 and unsealed on August 24, accused them of conspiring to make illegal corporate contributions exceeding the $2,000 individual limit to the 2004 presidential campaign of John Edwards and the 2004 U.S. Senate reelection campaign of Carl Levin, totaling over $125,000 to Edwards' campaign alone.[10] Prosecutors alleged that Fieger directed his law firm to reimburse more than 60 straw donorsâ€”including employees, family members, friends, and vendorsâ€”who made contributions on behalf of the firm, violating prohibitions on corporate funding of federal campaigns under the Federal Election Campaign Act.[10] The scheme reportedly occurred between March 2003 and January 2004, with Fieger personally charged with additional counts of making false statements to investigators and obstructing justice by concealing evidence and shifting blame.[10] Fieger faced a maximum penalty of five years imprisonment and $250,000 fine per count for conspiracy, illegal contributions, and false statements, plus ten years for obstruction.[10] The U.S. Attorney's Office for the Eastern District of Michigan, in coordination with the Department of Justice's Public Integrity Section, emphasized that Edwards' campaign was unaware of the contributions' true source and had cooperated fully with the investigation.[10] Fieger maintained his innocence, portraying the prosecution as politically motivated, though no evidence of such was presented in court records.[11] The case proceeded to trial in federal court in Detroit, where after a two-week proceeding, a jury acquitted Fieger on all 10 counts on June 2, 2008.[11] Johnson, charged in related counts, was also cleared.[11] Fieger described the verdict as vindication, stating it affirmed that no laws had been broken.[11] Separately, in 2009, Fieger's law firm agreed to a $131,000 civil settlement with the Federal Election Commission over similar donation issues to Edwards' campaign, without admitting wrongdoing.[75]

Personal Life and Public Persona

Family and Relationships

Geoffrey Fieger married Kathleen J. "Keenie" Fieger shortly after completing law school in the early 1980s.[14] The couple has three children: two sons and one daughter.[16] [18] The Fiegers reside in Bloomfield Hills, Michigan, where they have raised their family.[76] Their marriage has experienced strains, with Keenie Fieger filing for divorce twiceâ€”once in 1990 and again in 1995â€”though the couple reconciled and remains married as of 2023.[14] In recent years, Keenie Fieger has publicly supported her husband during his health challenges, including issuing statements on his 2023 stroke and subsequent recovery.[77] [78] Together, the Fiegers have engaged in philanthropy, notably establishing a $1 million endowed scholarship at Kalamazoo College in 2022 to support students.[78] No public records indicate additional significant relationships or family dynamics beyond their immediate household.

Health Issues and Recent Developments

In March 2023, Geoffrey Fieger, then aged 72, underwent a medical procedure to address minor heart arrhythmia, after which he suffered a stroke.[79][80] His family reported that he was awake, resting comfortably, and actively participating in rehabilitation efforts shortly thereafter.[81][82] Prior to the stroke, Fieger had been diagnosed with a heart condition, which he cited in a lawsuit against his insurance provider seeking $16 million in disability benefits, asserting it had terminated his ability to practice as a trial lawyer.[12] Despite this claim, records indicate he continued handling cases in the lead-up to the procedure.[12] By June 2023, Fieger had returned home from the hospital, though his family and firm provided limited updates on his condition.[77] A protÃ©gÃ© assumed leadership of Fieger Law during his recovery, signaling a shift in the firm's day-to-day operations.[77] As of 2025, no public updates on further health complications have emerged, while Fieger Law remains active, filing high-profile lawsuits such as a $100 million claim announced via press conference in September 2025.[83] The firm's ongoing case results, including multimillion-dollar settlements in birth trauma and liability matters, suggest sustained operations independent of Fieger's direct involvement.[7]

Media Appearances and Reputation

Geoffrey Fieger hosted the Fox television series Power of Attorney and the CBS radio program Fieger Time, leveraging his charismatic style to gain visibility as a legal commentator.[84] He has made regular appearances on networks including CNN, Fox News, MSNBC, and NBC, often providing analysis on high-profile trials and legal matters.[84][83] Fieger featured prominently in the 2020 Netflix series Trial by Media, which examined his representation of the Amedure family in the lawsuit against The Jenny Jones Show following the 1995 on-air ambush and subsequent murder of Scott Amedure.[85] Fieger's media presence extends to press conferences and interviews tied to his cases, such as a 2022 event announcing representation for the family of Porter Burks, killed by Detroit police, and discussions of the Jack Kevorkian euthanasia trials.[86][87] He participated in a 2020 NBC Nightly News interview on the death of Cornelius Fredericks in a restraint incident and appeared on PBS's Off the Record in 2017 to discuss Michigan politics.[88][89] Fieger's reputation as a trial lawyer is marked by success in securing multimillion-dollar verdicts, earning national prominence from his defense of Kevorkian in the 1990s, yet it is polarizing due to his aggressive and outspoken demeanor.[90][91] Described as an "enfant terrible" in legal circles for clashing with prosecutors and judges, he has faced criticism for inflammatory rhetoric, including a 2007 radio interview where he labeled Michigan appellate judges "jackasses" and likened them to Nazis after they overturned a $15 million verdict.[92][93] While admired by some for his tenacity in personal injury and civil rights cases, others view him with disdain for courtroom theatrics and public feuds, such as his 2017 libel suit against rival attorney Mike Morse.[91][92]
