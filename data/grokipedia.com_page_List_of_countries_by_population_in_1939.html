List of countries by population in 1939

The list of countries by population in 1939 compiles approximate demographic figures for nations and major dependencies on the eve of World War II's escalation, when the world's total inhabitants numbered roughly 2.3 billion.[1] Dominated by Asian giants, the rankings featured the Republic of China with estimates around 400-500 million amid civil strife and incomplete censuses, British India encompassing nearly 380 million under colonial rule, and the Soviet Union at 170.5 million per its official enumeration.[2][3] Such data, drawn from sporadic national censuses, interpolations, and contemporary assessments, underscore the era's uneven data reliability, particularly for vast agrarian societies lacking modern vital registration, while highlighting imperial aggregates like the British Empire's vast holdings that rivaled sovereign states in scale.[1] The distributions reveal causal factors in geopolitical power, with population size correlating to manpower potential amid rising tensions, though estimates for conflict zones like China carry higher uncertainty due to wartime disruptions and political manipulations.[4]

Historical Context

Geopolitical Boundaries and Demographic Significance in 1939

In 1939, geopolitical boundaries were defined by a patchwork of sovereign nation-states, vast colonial empires, and mandated territories, reflecting the imperial structures inherited from the 19th century and altered by interwar treaties such as the Treaty of Versailles. Entities like the British Raj in India, the French colonies in Indochina, and the League of Nations mandates such as Palestine were treated as distinct administrative units for demographic purposes, rather than fragmented into modern successor states. This delineation captured populations under unified governance, encompassing over 500 million people in the British Empire alone, including dominions like Canada and Australia alongside crown colonies.[5] The Japanese Empire extended into Manchuria and parts of China, while the Soviet Union incorporated territories from the Russian Civil War, shaping how populations were aggregated before the escalations of September 1939. The global population stood at approximately 2.3 billion, with more than 40 percent concentrated in Asia, primarily within agrarian societies like China (around 500 million) and British India (over 370 million), highlighting the demographic weight of densely populated riverine and monsoon-dependent regions.[6] Europe, despite its industrial advancements, accounted for about 25 percent, bolstered by nations such as Germany (69 million) and the United Kingdom (48 million), while Africa's populations remained lower due to colonial extraction economies and disease burdens. This uneven distribution underscored the reliance on rural labor in empires, where urban centers like Tokyo or London represented outliers amid vast peasant majorities. As the immediate prelude to World War II, 1939 marked a period of tentative post-Depression recovery, with industrial output rebounding in Europe and the United States, yet shadowed by militarization and territorial disputes, including Japan's ongoing conflict in China since 1937 and Germany's annexations in 1938-1939.[7] Migration remained relatively unrestricted compared to postwar eras, with transatlantic flows continuing under quota systems established in the 1920s, contributing to demographic stability in settler colonies. These conditions preserved prewar population configurations, minimizing disruptions from famine or exodus until the invasions commencing in autumn, thereby providing a snapshot of global humanity on the cusp of upheaval.[8]

Factors Influencing Population Data Accuracy Pre-World War II

Political instability in authoritarian regimes significantly compromised the reliability of population counts approaching 1939, as state-driven repression and violence led to widespread underreporting and data suppression. In the Soviet Union, the Great Purge of 1936â€“1938 resulted in the execution or imprisonment of millions, fostering an atmosphere of fear that discouraged accurate self-reporting and prompted official manipulation of statistics to align with ideological targets. The 1937 census, which revealed a population of approximately 162 millionâ€”substantially below the expected 170 million due to prior famines and purgesâ€”was suppressed, leading to the purge of the statistical apparatus and reliance on falsified extrapolations for subsequent estimates.[9] This pattern of demographic denial persisted into 1939, rendering Soviet figures systematically inflated to mask excess mortality from repression.[10] In China, the intermittent phases of the Civil War (1927â€“1937) and associated warlord conflicts displaced millions and disrupted administrative continuity, preventing comprehensive national censuses after 1928 and resulting in estimates derived from fragmented provincial data prone to exaggeration or omission. Warfare-induced migrations and localized atrocities, including floods displacing around 400,000 in 1938, further obscured baseline counts, with official tallies often reflecting political control over territory rather than empirical headcounts.[11][12] Such instability privileged survival over record-keeping, yielding 1930s population projections that varied widely (e.g., 400â€“500 million) without verifiable reconciliation.[13] Colonial administrative practices in Africa and Asia exacerbated undercounts by prioritizing taxable urban and settler populations over nomadic or rural indigenous groups, often relying on indirect estimates from local chiefs that missed vast hinterlands. In British Africa, for instance, 1920sâ€“1930s censuses covered unevenly, with systematic under-enumeration of non-coastal regions due to logistical constraints and minimal investment in full territorial surveys.[14] Similar biases prevailed in Asian colonies, where European powers focused on plantation economies, leading to rural omissions that understated total populations by potentially 20â€“30% in under-surveyed areas.[15] In contrast, stable democracies like the United Kingdom maintained higher data fidelity through institutionalized practices, such as extrapolating from the 1931 census using birth, death, and migration records, supplemented by the accurate 1939 National Register compiled for wartime planning.[16] These methods, grounded in transparent civil registries, yielded reliable mid-1930s estimates with minimal political interference, highlighting how administrative autonomy in Western contexts reduced manipulation risks compared to authoritarian or colonial systems.[17] Prior famines, such as the Soviet catastrophe of 1930â€“1933 claiming 5.7â€“8.7 million lives, eroded foundational data for later extrapolations by creating unrecorded gaps in rural demographics, compounding inaccuracies into 1939 amid ongoing collectivization displacements. While less acute in 1939 itself, these events underscored causal linkages between policy-induced mortality and persistent statistical opacity in non-democratic regimes.

Methodology and Sources

Primary Data Collection Methods: Censuses and Extrapolations

In the late 1930s, national population figures were predominantly obtained through periodic censuses, which involved systematic enumeration of residents via household visits, administrative records, or self-reporting, often on a de facto basis counting individuals present at a reference date.[18] Many countries adhered to decennial cycles, with censuses typically scheduled every ten yearsâ€”such as alignments to years ending in 0 (e.g., United States in 1930, with forward projections to 1939 based on registered vital events) or 1 (e.g., British India in 1931, extrapolated ahead using reported birth and death rates from civil registration systems).[19] Where full censuses were delayed or absent, ad-hoc extrapolations incorporated partial vital statisticsâ€”births, deaths, and net migrationâ€”from government registries to update prior benchmarks, though coverage was uneven in regions lacking comprehensive reporting.[20] Interpolation between known census points relied on assumptions of steady demographic momentum, with linear methods adding a fixed annual increment derived from the difference between two prior enumerations divided by the interval years.[21] For instance, if censuses in 1930 and 1940 yielded populations P1930 and P1940, the 1939 estimate approximated P1939 = P1930 + 9 Ã— ((P1940 - P1930)/10), adjusted post-hoc for known mortality trends or migration where data permitted; Key Historical Sources and Their Reliability

The primary aggregators of 1939 population data were the Statistical Year-Book of the League of Nations, particularly the 1938 and 1939-40 editions, which synthesized submissions from member states' statistical bureaus and official estimates for non-members.[24] These yearbooks prioritized empirical aggregates from recent national reports, providing country-level figures cross-referenced against prior years' data to account for growth rates, with a focus on de jure populations including colonies and mandates.[25] Their strength lies in contemporaneous compilation before wartime disruptions, minimizing hindsight biases, though completeness varied by reporting complianceâ€”Europe and the Americas contributed robust, census-backed inputs, while Asian and African territories often relied on extrapolations from decennial surveys. Reliability gradients across these sources reflect institutional transparency and data verification rigor: highest for Western Europe and the United States, where figures aligned with decennial censuses conducted in the 1930s (e.g., U.S. 1930 Census extrapolated to 1939 via vital statistics), yielding margins of error under 1-2%.[26] Medium confidence applies to British Empire components, drawn from systematic colonial decennial censuses (e.g., 1931 Indian Census), but tempered by documented undercounts of nomadic or rural indigenous groups due to administrative priorities favoring taxable settled populations.[1] In contrast, low reliability characterizes Soviet and Chinese data; League estimates for the USSR post-1934 withdrawal from the organization depended on fragmentary pre-purge figures and external extrapolations, susceptible to state suppression of famine and relocation impacts, while China's lacked a unified census, relying on provincial samples prone to wartime evasion and incomplete rural coverage. Supplementary U.S. Bureau of the Census historical tables, as in their international population series up to 1940, offered cross-verification by integrating League data with independent diplomatic and trade-derived estimates, enhancing accuracy for accessible regions but inheriting gaps elsewhere.[26] Pre-1945 publications like these preserve undiluted empirical baselines, preferable to later datasets that may incorporate adjusted methodologies influenced by post-war geopolitical narratives, such as upward revisions for ideologically aligned states; users must thus weight claims by source proximity to primary national archives over modern interpolations.[1]

Regional Aggregations and Empire Totals

In 1939, the global population of approximately 2.3 billion was disproportionately concentrated in Asia, which accounted for about 1.2 billion people or 52%, owing to millennia of agricultural intensification in fertile alluvial plains like those of the Yellow River and Ganges, supporting dense rural settlements. Europe followed with roughly 550 million inhabitants (24%), where higher urbanization rates in industrial heartlands contrasted with peripheral rural sparsity, shaped by temperate climates conducive to mixed farming but limited by mountainous terrains. Africa's estimated 150 million (7%) reflected lower densities outside oases and highlands, exacerbated by disease burdens such as malaria and tsetse fly prevalence, alongside colonial policies prioritizing mineral and cash crop exports over infrastructure for mass settlement. The Americas totaled around 250 million (11%), split between North America's 140 million in expansive settler societies and Latin America's 110 million amid varied agrarian structures, while Oceania's 10 million (0.4%) underscored isolation and aridity constraining habitability.[1]

Uncertainties and Debates

Estimation Challenges in Non-Western and Colonial Regions

In non-Western and colonial regions, nomadic and traditional societies frequently resisted formal enumeration efforts, resulting in significant undercounts of mobile populations. British authorities in Mandatory Palestine, for instance, resorted to tent counts for Bedouin groups between 1917 and 1948, but these methods yielded incomplete figures due to evasion, seasonal migrations, and incomplete territorial control, often missing 20-30% of nomadic households as inferred from post-enumeration discrepancies in administrative records.[35] Similar challenges persisted in African protectorates and mandates, where pastoralist groups avoided censuses perceived as precursors to taxation or conscription, leading to reliance on village chief reports that systematically underestimated totals by integrating guesswork with partial tallies.[36] Colonial priorities further exacerbated inaccuracies by emphasizing fiscal utility over exhaustive demographic coverage. Administrators in territories like French Indochina focused censuses on settled, taxable populationsâ€”such as urban centers and agricultural villagesâ€”while marginalizing remote ethnic minorities and highlands dwellers, as head taxes inherited from pre-colonial systems incentivized selective counting to optimize revenue extraction without comprehensive surveys.[37] This approach skewed 1930s estimates toward elite and compliant subgroups, with rural underreporting compounded by linguistic barriers and administrative understaffing, as evidenced in federation-wide statistical yearbooks that prioritized economic outputs over total headcounts.[38] Stable governance forms the causal prerequisite for verifiable population data, enabling systematic canvassing and cross-verification; in fragmented colonial peripheries lacking such infrastructure, enumerations devolved to extrapolations from urban samples or tributary rolls, fostering both undercounts in ungoverned zones and occasional overestimations where propaganda inflated figures for imperial justification. Empirical bounds thus warrant conservative adjustments, privileging lower estimates corroborated by multiple indirect indicators like trade volumes or missionary reports, to mitigate biases inherent in administratively driven data collection.[39][14]

Broader Implications

Comparisons to Adjacent Historical Periods

The global population expanded from an estimated 2.07 billion in 1930 to approximately 2.3 billion by 1939, reflecting an average annual growth rate of about 1.1 percent amid uneven regional dynamics.[26] Europe's population growth remained subdued in this interval, constrained by the lingering demographic repercussions of World War I, including a sharp collapse in fertility rates during 1914â€“1918 that generated lasting birth deficits equivalent to several million individuals in countries like France and Germany.[48] In contrast, Asia experienced steadier increases averaging around 1 percent annually, driven primarily by incremental reductions in mortality from improved public health measures and famine mitigation rather than fertility surges.[49] Specific national trajectories illustrate these patterns. Germany's population within its 1937 borders grew from 62.5 million in the 1925 census to 69.3 million by 1939, a cumulative rise of roughly 11 percent over 14 years, or 0.8 percent annually, coinciding with state interventions promoting higher birth rates that empirically correlated with a temporary uptick in total fertility from 1.6 children per woman in the mid-1920s to about 2.0 by the late 1930s, though sustained impact remained limited by economic pressures.[50][51] Similarly, Japan's core population advanced from 64.5 million in 1930 to 71.1 million by 1939, fueled by industrialization's effects on mortality compression, including better sanitation and nutrition that lowered infant death rates from 140 per 1,000 births in 1920 to under 110 by 1935, enabling natural increase despite high emigration to colonies.[52] Projections from 1939 anticipated relative demographic stability into the mid-1940s, with global growth continuing at 1â€“1.5 percent annually based on pre-war trends in mortality decline and stable fertility. However, World War II introduced acute disruptions, resulting in 70â€“85 million excess deaths worldwideâ€”predominantly from combat, famine, and diseaseâ€”offsetting much of the expected increment and yielding only marginal net population gain to around 2.4 billion by 1945.[26] In Europe, this manifested as outright declines in belligerent nations like Germany (from 69.3 million to an estimated 65â€“67 million effective post-war amid territorial losses and casualties) and the Soviet Union, contrasting sharply with pre-war baselines of modest recovery.[51]

Insights into Global Demographic Patterns

In 1939, global demographic patterns highlighted a bifurcation between high-fertility agrarian societies in Asia and Africa, where total fertility rates averaged 4-6 children per woman to compensate for elevated infant mortality rates exceeding 150 per 1,000 births and to sustain family-based agricultural labor needs, and lower rates of 2-3 children per woman in industrialized Western Europe and North America, where urbanization and medical advancements had already lowered mortality without immediate fertility adjustments.[53][54] These disparities stemmed from causal drivers rooted in economic structures: agrarian economies incentivized larger families for labor security amid subsistence farming and limited mechanization, while industrialization shifted households toward wage labor, education, and smaller family units as child survival rates improved through public health measures like vaccination and sanitation.[55] Population densities further illustrated resource-driven variances, with hotspots like the Indo-Gangetic Plain in British India supporting rural densities of approximately 200-300 persons per square kilometer in fertile alluvial zones, enabled by monsoon-fed river systems and intensive rice-wheat cultivation that maximized caloric output per hectare.[56] In contrast, sparsely settled regions such as the interior Americas averaged under 5 persons per square kilometer, constrained by geographic barriers, arid climates, and lower agricultural productivity outside coastal enclaves, which limited migration and settlement without large-scale infrastructure.[1] These density gradients reflected first-order constraints of arable land availability and hydrological endowments, channeling population concentrations into river valleys while leaving marginal lands underutilized. Urbanization rates amplified these patterns, remaining below 10% globally but reaching 50-70% in Western Europe, where industrial hubs drew rural migrants via factory employment, fostering denser conurbations like London's 8 million inhabitants amid Britain's total of 48 million.[1] In colonial agrarian territories, urban shares hovered at 5-10%, as populations remained tethered to rural subsistence, exerting Malthusian pressures through periodic famines and disease unchecked by welfare mechanisms, a dynamic unmitigated by redistributive policies and thus revealing raw empirical divergences in human carrying capacities across ecological and institutional contexts.[57]
