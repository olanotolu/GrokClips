Al-Shuhada Street

Al-Shuhada Street (Arabic: شارع الشهداء, "Martyrs' Street") is a central thoroughfare in Hebron (Al-Khalil), West Bank, that runs through the H2 sector under Israeli military administration, where access for Palestinians has been severely restricted since 1994 primarily to safeguard Jewish residents amid recurrent security threats.[1][2] The street's closure followed the February 1994 Ibrahimi Mosque massacre, in which Israeli settler Baruch Goldstein killed 29 Palestinian worshipers, prompting Israeli authorities to impose curbs on Palestinian movement rather than settlers, with full pedestrian and vehicular bans for Palestinians enacted by 2000.[2] Historically a vibrant commercial artery linking northern and southern Hebron and hosting wholesale markets, the area has since become a divided zone with over 300 Palestinian shops shuttered by military order, forcing remaining residents to navigate via back alleys, rooftops, or circuitous detours while Israeli settlers access it freely.[2][3] These arrangements, formalized under the 1997 Hebron Protocol dividing the city between Palestinian-controlled H1 and Israeli-controlled H2, reflect causal responses to documented violence against Jews—including grenade attacks, shootings, and bombings predating and postdating 1994—but have drawn criticism for exacerbating economic isolation for approximately 7,000 Palestinians in restricted H2 zones, marked by 21 staffed checkpoints and a 2015 "closed military zone" designation for parts of the street and adjacent Tel Rumeida.[1][3] The street remains a focal point of contention, with Palestinian activists decrying it as emblematic of segregation and Israeli perspectives emphasizing its necessity given Hebron's long history of anti-Jewish pogroms and ongoing attacks, though narratives of it as a "ghost town" overlook the thriving Palestinian commerce in adjacent H1 areas where Jews are barred.[1][2]

Location and Overview

Geographical and Urban Context

Al-Shuhada Street is a central thoroughfare in the Old City of Hebron, the largest urban center in the southern West Bank, located approximately 35 kilometers south-southwest of Jerusalem in the Judean Hills at an elevation of about 930 meters above sea level.[4] The street spans roughly 800 meters, serving historically as the primary link between the northern and southern sections of Hebron, connecting western market districts to the vicinity of the Cave of the Patriarchs.[5] In Hebron's urban fabric, Al-Shuhada Street traverses Area H2, the Israeli-controlled sector under the 1994 Hebron Protocol, which divides the city into Palestinian Authority-administered H1 and Israeli security zones encompassing settlements amid Palestinian residential enclaves.[6] The surrounding topography features steep hills and valleys, with the Old City's dense layout of narrow stone-paved alleys, multi-story buildings, and historic structures reflecting millennia of continuous habitation.[7] This positioning made the street a vital commercial and pedestrian artery prior to post-1994 restrictions, integral to the city's pre-division connectivity.[8]

Economic and Social Role Prior to Restrictions

Prior to the imposition of restrictions following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre, Al-Shuhada Street served as Hebron's primary commercial thoroughfare, functioning as the central wholesale market for the region and accommodating a dense concentration of retail shops specializing in vegetables, meat, gold, and other goods.[1][9] The street's economic vitality stemmed from its role in facilitating trade between northern and southern sectors of the city, drawing merchants, shoppers, and wholesalers daily to its bustling markets, where small family-owned businesses operated from ground-floor storefronts with residences above.[10][11] Socially, the street embodied Hebron's communal fabric, acting as a vital linkage for pedestrian and vehicular movement that integrated diverse neighborhoods and supported everyday interactions among residents, including access to the nearby Tomb of the Patriarchs.[10][12] It hosted vibrant foot traffic and informal gatherings, reflecting the city's historical role as a trade hub in the southern West Bank, where economic activity intertwined with social life in a mixed urban environment predating modern divisions.[13][5] This pre-restriction era underscored the street's function as both an economic engine—sustaining livelihoods through commerce—and a social corridor essential for urban cohesion in Hebron.[11][14]

Nomenclature

Origins and Traditional Names

Al-Shuhada Street, situated in Hebron's Old City, derives its primary name from the Arabic term shuhadāʾ (شهداء), meaning "martyrs," a designation used by the local Palestinian population to commemorate deaths occurring along the thoroughfare amid intercommunal violence and conflicts.[1][15] This nomenclature reflects the street's role as a site of historical clashes, including fatalities during the 1929 Hebron riots and subsequent events, though precise dating of the name's adoption remains undocumented in available records. Prior to intensified restrictions post-1994, the street functioned as a central market artery linking northern and southern parts of the city, underscoring its longstanding urban significance beyond its appellation.[10] Israeli settlers and Jewish residents in Hebron traditionally designate the same street as King David Street, invoking the biblical account in which King David established Hebron as the capital of the united Kingdom of Israel for seven and a half years circa 1010–1003 BCE before transferring it to Jerusalem.[16][15] This Hebrew naming aligns with the site's ancient Jewish historical associations, as Hebron—known biblically as Kiryat Arba—is one of the four holy cities in Judaism and features prominently in narratives tied to the patriarchs and early monarchy. The dual traditional names highlight competing narratives of heritage and victimhood in the region, with no evidence of a singular pre-20th-century designation predating modern ethnic designations.[17]

Modern Renaming and Disputes

Palestinians refer to the street as Al-Shuhada Street, translating to "Street of the Martyrs," a designation that honors Palestinian fatalities in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, with particular emphasis following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre where 29 Palestinians were killed by Baruch Goldstein.[18] This name, rooted in local Arabic usage, gained heightened symbolic prominence amid post-massacre tensions and restrictions.[15] Israeli settlers and officials, by contrast, designate it King David Street, invoking the biblical account of King David establishing Hebron as his capital for seven years prior to Jerusalem (2 Samuel 2:1-11).[15][17] This alternative reflects assertions of Jewish historical continuity in Hebron, predating modern conflicts, and rejects the "martyrs" framing as one that glorifies violence against Jews.[19][17] The parallel namings embody competing narratives over Hebron's identity, with no formal municipal resolution due to divided control under the 1997 Hebron Protocol. Palestinian advocates view the Israeli name and associated signage as efforts to marginalize Arab presence, while Israeli perspectives criticize "Shuhada" for politicizing the street through association with conflict deaths often involving armed actors.[20][19] Further disputes have arisen over physical markers, such as Hebrew and English directional signs erected by settlers since the early 2010s, which omit Arabic and highlight Jewish sites like ancient cemeteries. These prompted a 2017 petition by the NGO Breaking the Silence to Israel's High Court of Justice, alleging intent to "change the identity of Hebron" and erase Palestinian history, though the court upheld the signs as navigational aids.[20]

Historical Background

Pre-20th Century Significance

Al-Shuhada Street, aligning with the ancient Jerusalem road, functioned as a primary artery in Hebron's Old City throughout the Mamluk period (1250–1517) and into the Ottoman era (1517–1917), facilitating access to the Haram al-Ibrahimi—the mosque encompassing the Cave of the Patriarchs, a focal point for Muslim pilgrimage.[21] Its vaulted architecture, intended for illumination and airflow, integrated into the broader network of covered main streets and labyrinthine alleys that defined the town's spatial hierarchy and supported movement toward the sacred enclosure.[21] This connectivity underpinned commercial vibrancy, as the street linked to thriving souks specializing in local industries, including the al-Qazzazine market for glass production, which drew from the pilgrimage economy and regional trade routes.[21] Hebron's expansion under Ottoman rule preserved much of this Mamluk-era morphology, with added upper stories to abutting structures, reinforcing the street's role in sustaining economic and devotional flows without major recorded disruptions until the 20th century.[21]

20th Century Developments up to 1993

In the early 20th century, under Ottoman and subsequent British Mandate administration, Hebron maintained a small but continuous Jewish community of approximately 700-800 residents alongside a larger Arab population, with Al-Shuhada Street forming part of the city's central commercial arteries near the Jewish quarter and leading toward the Cave of the Patriarchs.[22] The street supported local trade, including shops and markets frequented by both communities.[1] The 1929 Hebron massacre, triggered by Arab riots amid widespread anti-Jewish violence across Mandatory Palestine, resulted in the deaths of 67 Jews and the wounding of dozens more, leading British authorities to evacuate the surviving Jewish population from the city.[23] This event eliminated organized Jewish presence in Hebron for nearly four decades, transforming Al-Shuhada Street into an exclusively Arab commercial hub under continued British oversight until 1948.[24] From 1948 to 1967, during Jordanian annexation of the West Bank, Hebron functioned as an Arab-majority city with no Jewish inhabitants, and Al-Shuhada Street solidified its role as the primary wholesale market thoroughfare, accommodating vegetable, gold, and general trade stalls that linked northern and southern districts and supported the local economy.[1][11] Israel's capture of Hebron in the 1967 Six-Day War enabled the initial return of Jewish residents in April 1968, with families settling first in a military compound before expanding into central sites.[24] By 1971, the adjacent Kiryat Arba settlement housed several hundred Jews, but urban resettlement intensified in 1979 when 10 Jewish women and about 40 children occupied the long-abandoned Beit Hadassah building—originally constructed in 1893 as a Jewish-funded clinic for both Jews and Arabs—located directly on Al-Shuhada Street, prompting government approval for permanent Jewish housing in the city center after initial military tolerance.[25][26] Tensions escalated with Arab terrorist attacks on the street, including a May 1980 ambush outside Beit Hadassah that killed six yeshiva students and injured others via gunfire from nearby rooftops.[25] Further settlements followed, such as the 1984 establishment of Admot Yishai (Tel Rumeida) and the 1986 relocation of families into renovated Beit Hadassah units, alongside 1989 dedications of homes in the revived Jewish Quarter adjacent to the street.[24] During the First Intifada (1987–1993), Al-Shuhada Street experienced repeated stabbings, shootings, and Molotov attacks targeting Jewish pedestrians and residents, contributing to heightened Israeli security patrols while the thoroughfare remained open to Arab vehicular and pedestrian traffic.[27] By late 1993, these incidents had numbered in the dozens, reflecting causal patterns of Arab-initiated violence against the resurgent Jewish minority amid broader Palestinian uprising dynamics.[24]

The 1994 Massacre and Immediate Response

The Cave of the Patriarchs Attack

On February 25, 1994, Baruch Goldstein, a 37-year-old American-Israeli physician and settler from Kiryat Arba who adhered to the ideology of the banned Kach movement, carried out a mass shooting inside the Cave of the Patriarchs (known to Muslims as the Ibrahimi Mosque) in Hebron.[28] [29] The incident occurred during the Muslim dawn prayer (Fajr) on the 15th day of Ramadan, when hundreds of Palestinian worshippers were present in the mosque's prayer hall.[30] [31] Goldstein, who had served as a reserve captain in the Israel Defense Forces, entered the site through the Jewish prayer section before moving into the adjacent Muslim hall, armed with a Galil assault rifle loaded with magazines containing over 100 rounds.[32] He positioned himself near the worshippers, fired bursts into the crowd at close range, reloaded at least once, and continued shooting for several minutes, targeting those prostrating in prayer.[33] [32] The attack resulted in 29 Palestinian men and boys killed and 125 wounded, many critically from gunfire to the head, chest, and abdomen.[29] [28] Survivors eventually overpowered Goldstein, beating him to death with fire extinguishers and metal bars amid the chaos.[34] An Israeli government commission of inquiry, which examined ballistic evidence, witness testimonies, and autopsy reports, concluded that Goldstein acted alone, with no evidence of accomplices or prior Israeli security lapses enabling the attack beyond routine access policies at the shared holy site.[29] [35] Goldstein's motivations stemmed from ultranationalist views influenced by Rabbi Meir Kahane, whom he regarded as a mentor, and perceptions of Arab violence as an existential threat to Jews, particularly after events like the 1993 killing of Israeli policeman Nissim Toledano by Hamas.[28] [33]

Initial Security Measures and Curfews

In the immediate aftermath of the Cave of the Patriarchs massacre on February 25, 1994, Palestinian riots broke out across Hebron, involving clashes with Israeli security forces and attempts to assault Jewish settler enclaves and religious sites.[34] These disturbances, which persisted for days and resulted in additional Palestinian fatalities from confrontations with soldiers, prompted the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) to declare a citywide curfew on Hebron starting that same day to restore order and shield vulnerable Jewish populations from mob violence.[34] The curfew, enforced selectively on Palestinian areas while permitting limited Jewish movement under escort, confined residents to their homes and halted normal commerce, remaining in continuous effect for 30 days until its lifting on March 26, 1994.[36] During this period, IDF troops reinforced patrols and established temporary barriers, particularly along routes linking the massacre site to settler neighborhoods. Concurrently, Al-Shuhada Street— the primary thoroughfare bisecting Hebron and abutting Jewish homes and the Cave of the Patriarchs— was sealed off to Palestinian vehicular and pedestrian access to mitigate risks of stone-throwing, shootings, or incursions from adjacent Arab markets and alleys.[2] Soldiers welded shut the metal doors and gates of over 100 Palestinian shops and residences fronting the street, effectively transforming it into a restricted corridor for Israeli use only and curtailing cross-community friction points exposed during the riots.[9] These provisional actions, justified by military commanders as essential to avert retaliatory strikes following the loss of 29 Palestinian lives in the initial attack, marked the onset of segregated access protocols that prioritized settler safety amid empirically observed threats of communal reprisals.[34]

Formal Agreements and Long-Term Restrictions

The Hebron Protocol of 1997

The Hebron Protocol, formally titled the Protocol Concerning the Redeployment in Hebron, was signed on January 17, 1997, by Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and Palestine Liberation Organization Chairman Yasser Arafat in the presence of U.S. President Bill Clinton at the White House.[37][38] It implemented Article VII of the 1995 Interim Agreement (Oslo II), which mandated phased Israeli military redeployment from parts of Hebron, the only major West Bank city not previously transferred to Palestinian Authority (PA) control.[37] The agreement divided Hebron into two zones: Area H1, comprising approximately 80% of the city's land and population (about 115,000 Palestinians), placed under full PA civil and security jurisdiction with Israeli redeployment completed within 10 days; and Area H2, encompassing roughly 20% including Jewish settlements (housing around 500 settlers at the time), the Cave of the Patriarchs/Ibrahimi Mosque, and central historic areas, retained under Israeli security control with PA civil administration.[37][38] Security arrangements under the protocol emphasized Israeli responsibility for protecting Jewish settlers and sites in H2, including joint patrols, checkpoints, and intelligence sharing to prevent attacks amid ongoing threats following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre.[37] An attached Agreed Minute from January 7, 1997, addressed Shuhada Street (Worshippers' Way), a key thoroughfare in H2 connecting settlements to the Cave of the Patriarchs, stipulating that Palestinian vehicle movement would gradually resume within four months to pre-February 1994 conditions, contingent on cessation of violence and incitement.[39][40] However, subsequent Palestinian riots, stabbings, and shootings—such as the September 1996 tunnel riots and intensified attacks during the lead-up to the protocol—led Israeli forces to maintain restrictions, including vehicle bans for Palestinians on Shuhada Street to mitigate risks from ambushes and stone-throwing, as the road's narrow layout and proximity to settlements heightened vulnerability.[37][39] The protocol established a Temporary International Presence in Hebron (TIPH) of observers from Denmark, Italy, Norway, Sweden, Switzerland, and Turkey to monitor implementation, report violations, and foster cooperation, though TIPH's mandate excluded enforcement powers and focused on non-binding recommendations.[37][38] Redeployment from H1 occurred by late January 1997, transferring control to the PA, while H2 measures formalized long-term Israeli oversight of access routes like Shuhada Street, prioritizing settler security over full pedestrian and vehicular reopening due to empirical patterns of violence, including over 100 attacks in Hebron in the preceding years.[37] This framework persisted, with Israeli assessments citing reduced casualties in H2 relative to unrestricted areas as evidence of necessity, despite PA claims of overreach.[38]

Evolution of Access Controls Post-1997

The Hebron Protocol's Agreed Minute of January 7, 1997, committed Israel to gradually restoring vehicle movement on Shuhada Road to its pre-February 1994 status within four months, following initial post-massacre closures.[39] This aimed to balance security with access, contingent on Palestinian cooperation in preventing violence, though full implementation faced immediate challenges from sporadic attacks.[38] The Second Intifada, erupting in September 2000, prompted Israeli forces to reclose significant segments of the street to Palestinian vehicles and most pedestrians by early 2001, reversing prior easing efforts amid suicide bombings and shootings targeting settlers and soldiers.[41] Curfews lasting months, including a near-continuous one from 2002 to 2003, suspended all non-essential movement, with Shuhada Street serving as a fortified corridor for Jewish residents.[42] By 2004, as violence subsided, limited pedestrian access resumed for verified Palestinian residents via checkpoints, but vehicular bans persisted to mitigate ambush risks. Post-Intifada stabilization allowed incremental adjustments, such as temporary openings for markets or holidays, yet core prohibitions endured, with the street designated a "sterile" zone barring through-traffic for non-residents.[43] In October 2015, amid a wave of stabbing incidents, authorities installed additional barriers and gates on adjoining alleys, further isolating H2-area Palestinians from Shuhada Street and reducing entry points by over 20 percent.[44] These enhancements included reinforced turnstiles and surveillance, maintaining separation despite diplomatic pressures for reopening.[45] Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks, Israeli military imposed area-wide lockdowns in Hebron, including Shuhada Street, with movement halted for days and permits scrutinized more rigorously, reflecting heightened threat assessments.[46] By mid-2024, operations resumed with intensified checks, but Palestinian access remained confined to essential residents, underscoring persistent adaptations to evolving security dynamics rather than normalization.[47] Empirical data from UN monitoring indicates over 100 barriers in H2 by 2019, up from dozens pre-2015, correlating with attack patterns.[41]

Security Framework

Implemented Measures and Checkpoints

Following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre, the Israeli Defense Forces implemented a series of security checkpoints and barriers along Al-Shuhada Street to mitigate risks to Jewish settlers and visitors in Hebron's H2 area. The street was closed to Palestinian vehicular traffic, with permanent concrete barriers and fencing installed to block entry points, allowing only pedestrian passage under controlled conditions.[48] This measure stemmed from immediate post-massacre restrictions that evolved into long-term policy amid ongoing violence during the Second Intifada.[3] Key fixed checkpoints include Checkpoint 56, located at the junction of Bab al-Zawiya and Al-Shuhada Street, which serves as the primary entry point from Palestinian-controlled H1 into H2. Staffed by IDF soldiers, it requires security screenings, including bag checks and identity verification, for all entrants, with Palestinians often denied passage to certain segments unless exempted, such as schoolchildren heading to Qurtuba School.[49] [50] Checkpoint 55, positioned further along the street near settlement vicinities, enforces similar protocols, creating segmented access zones that isolate Palestinian homes from settler routes.[44] Under the 1997 Hebron Protocol and its Agreed Minute, provisions were made for gradual vehicle access restoration to pre-1994 levels within four months, including road widening to 13 meters, separated sidewalks with low walls and railings, and a security wall near settlements like Beit Hadassah.[39] [51] However, Palestinian vehicle bans persisted due to security assessments, supplemented by surveillance cameras at checkpoints and along the route.[52] Mobile IDF patrols and temporary roadblocks augment fixed positions during alerts, as seen in closures like that on October 8, 2025, at the Al-Shuhada entrance.[53] These structures, often featuring turnstiles and metal detectors, operate daily from early morning to evening, with closures on Jewish holidays or after incidents.[49]

Justification Based on Threat History

The imposition of restrictions on Al-Shuhada Street stemmed from recurrent Palestinian terrorist attacks targeting Jewish settlers and Israeli security forces in Hebron, with the street serving as a conduit between densely populated Palestinian areas and vulnerable Jewish enclaves. Following the February 25, 1994, Cave of the Patriarchs massacre, immediate riots by Palestinians against Jewish sites and personnel escalated threats, prompting initial curfews and partial closures to avert further assaults amid clashes that wounded dozens.[54][32] During the Second Intifada (2000–2005), Al-Shuhada Street witnessed direct bombings and stabbings, underscoring the tactical risks of open access. On May 18, 2003, a Palestinian suicide bomber detonated on the street, killing Gadi Levy (aged 31) and his pregnant wife Dina (aged 37), both Israeli residents, in an attack originating from adjacent Palestinian zones. Earlier, on March 13, 2002, 15-year-old settler Eliya Meshulam was stabbed and seriously wounded at Gross Square, immediately adjacent to Al-Shuhada Street. These incidents, among others, demonstrated how the street enabled close-range assaults on settlers moving between communities and the Cave of the Patriarchs.[15] Hebron has since remained a primary hub for terrorist planning and execution, with Israeli military officials reporting it as the West Bank city with the highest incidence of attacks against Israelis in the mid-2010s. Hamas operatives based in Hebron orchestrated the June 12, 2014, kidnapping and murder of three Israeli teenagers hitchhiking near the city, an operation that relied on local networks and mobility. In 2016 alone, attacks from Hebron included a July stabbing that killed a 13-year-old settler girl in her home in nearby Kiryat Arba and a shooting south of the city that killed an Israeli father while injuring his family, reflecting persistent operational threats.[55][56][57] Data from Israeli security analyses indicate Hebron-area Palestinians comprised approximately 40% of West Bank terrorist perpetrators arrested between 2015 and early 2016 (69 out of 174 cases), with the city's proximity to settlements amplifying risks via routes like Al-Shuhada Street. Restrictions, including the 2001 vehicular ban, have demonstrably curtailed such access, preventing replication of pre-closure ambushes like the 1980 attack that killed six Jews returning from prayers near the street.[15]

Empirical Evidence of Effectiveness

Israeli security officials assert that the checkpoints and restrictions along Al-Shuhada Street have thwarted multiple attempted terrorist attacks targeting Jewish settlers and worshippers in Hebron's H2 area. For example, in various incidents documented by security forces, individuals carrying weapons or explosives have been intercepted at entry points, preventing potential violence akin to pre-1994 assaults on the street.[58] Post-1997 Hebron Protocol data indicate a marked decline in successful large-scale attacks against Israelis in the vicinity, with no recurrence of the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre's magnitude despite persistent threats from groups like Hamas. United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) reports for specific periods, such as January 2018 to March 2019, record only one Israeli settler fatality in H2 amid heightened monitoring, underscoring the measures' role in containing risks to a sporadic level rather than systemic incursions.[6] Broader Israeli assessments credit such access controls with enabling the sustained presence of approximately 700-800 settlers in H2 without mass casualties, attributing this to proactive interceptions that disrupt attack planning. While critics from organizations like B'Tselem highlight ongoing smaller-scale incidents, such as stabbings during the 2015-2016 wave, security analyses emphasize that checkpoints have limited attackers' mobility, reducing overall lethality compared to unrestricted eras.[45][59] Quantitative evaluations of West Bank-wide checkpoints, including those in Hebron, report prevention of hundreds of attacks annually by Israel's Shin Bet and IDF, with Hebron-specific operations frequently yielding arrests of suspects en route to H2 targets. This empirical pattern supports causal claims that segregating high-risk zones has empirically lowered Israeli casualties relative to exposure levels in a conflict zone.[60]

Impacts on Communities

Effects on Palestinian Residents and Businesses

The closure of Al-Shuhada Street to Palestinian vehicles and pedestrians, implemented immediately after the February 25, 1994, Cave of the Patriarchs attack, resulted in the forcible shutdown of hundreds of businesses along the street. Israeli military orders led to the welding shut of shop doors and the closure of 218 out of 304 commercial establishments on the street itself, transforming a once-vibrant commercial artery into a largely deserted zone.[61][62] Broader restrictions in Hebron H2, the Israeli-controlled sector encompassing the street, compelled the closure of at least 512 Palestinian businesses by military order, with over 1,000 additional establishments shuttering due to reduced foot traffic, access barriers, and economic inviability.[6][63] These measures exacerbated economic hardship for Palestinian residents, as Shuhada Street had served as a central marketplace linking H1 (Palestinian-controlled) to H2. Owners reported inability to reach or operate shops, prompting relocations to H1 or complete abandonment, with municipal services also displaced.[64] In the wider H2 area, where approximately 35,000 Palestinians live amid checkpoints and movement controls, the Palestinian population has declined steadily since the 1997 Hebron Protocol, attributed by local authorities and observers to prolonged curfews, settler-related incidents, and livelihood disruptions that incentivize emigration.[65] About 7,000 residents, or roughly 20% of H2's Palestinian population, inhabit access-restricted zones near settlements, facing daily barriers to work, education, and healthcare that compound isolation and poverty.[3] Empirical data from Palestinian municipal records and UN monitoring indicate no reversal of these trends despite partial shop reopenings under the 1997 agreement, with ongoing prohibitions on Palestinian vehicular access to Shuhada Street sustaining the economic paralysis.[41] Residents in affected neighborhoods report heightened vulnerability to property damage and evacuation pressures, contributing to a net outflow estimated in the thousands over decades, though precise causation varies between security-enforced restrictions and voluntary departures amid deteriorating conditions.[64]

Protection and Access for Jewish Settlers and Worshippers


The security arrangements for Jewish settlers and worshippers on Al-Shuhada Street operate within Hebron's H2 zone, under Israeli military control as established by the 1997 Hebron Protocol, which assigns Israel responsibility for protecting Israelis amid ongoing threats.[39] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) deploy fixed checkpoints, patrols, and barriers along the street to screen entrants and prevent incursions, enabling settlers residing in adjacent buildings—such as Beit Hadassah—to move freely between homes and the Cave of the Patriarchs without routine interference.[15] These measures restrict Palestinian vehicular and pedestrian traffic on portions of the street, creating separation to mitigate risks from historical patterns of ambush and stabbing attacks targeting Jews traversing the area. A series of documented assaults has rationalized these protections, including the May 2, 1980, ambush near Beit Hadassah that killed six Jews returning from prayers and wounded over 20 others; the 1983 stabbing death of Aharon Gross at Gross Square; the 2002 stabbing of teenager Eliya Meshulam; and the 2003 suicide bombing on the street that claimed the lives of Gadi Levy, his pregnant wife Dina, and their infant son.[15] Hebron has recorded the highest number of terrorist incidents against Israelis in the West Bank during periods of heightened violence, such as the Second Intifada, with attacks often originating from nearby Palestinian areas.[55] Post-2002 restrictions, including pedestrian-only access enforced since that year, have correlated with fewer successful penetrations into Jewish enclaves, allowing the settler population to expand to approximately 800 individuals under continuous IDF escort and surveillance.[15] Access to the Cave of the Patriarchs for worshippers is secured via a dedicated Jewish entrance on Al-Shuhada Street, equipped with metal detectors and IDF screening, facilitating daily prayers in the site's Jewish-allocated section.[15] During ten annual Jewish holidays—such as Passover and Sukkot—full site exclusivity for Jews is implemented, with IDF forces heightening perimeters to avert disruptions akin to pre-Protocol era clashes.[15] This framework supports over 700,000 yearly Jewish visitors, including settlers and pilgrims, by prioritizing threat interdiction over unrestricted public thoroughfare, a policy upheld by Israeli Supreme Court rulings in 2011 against challenges to the closures.[15]

Advocacy and Protests

The Open Shuhada Street Campaign

The Open Shuhada Street Campaign is an annual Palestinian-led initiative launched on February 22, 2010, by Youth Against Settlements (YAS), a Hebron-based non-governmental organization founded by activist Issa Amro.[66][67] The campaign originated as a joint Palestinian-international effort following a meeting of activists in Hebron, focusing on solidarity actions to address restrictions on Shuhada Street.[68] Its primary objective is to demand the full reopening of Shuhada Street—closed to most Palestinian vehicular and pedestrian traffic since the 1994 Ibrahimi Mosque massacre and subsequent security measures—to restore access for Palestinian residents and businesses.[69][70] Organizers frame the restrictions as policies amounting to segregation and human rights violations, aiming to raise global awareness and pressure Israeli authorities to lift closures, checkpoints, and movement limitations in Hebron's H2 area.[71][67] The campaign centers on the anniversary of the February 25, 1994, attack by Baruch Goldstein, in which 29 Palestinians were killed, an event that triggered initial street closures for security reasons amid heightened tensions.[72] It promotes non-violent demonstrations, including marches from sites like Ali-Bakaa Mosque toward Shuhada Street checkpoints, alongside international components such as petitions, protests, and advocacy targeting entities perceived to support settlements, like the Hebron Fund and financial institutions.[73][74] By its ninth year in 2018, it had expanded to coordinated events in multiple countries, emphasizing education and calls for ending what participants describe as occupation-related barriers.[75][76]

Key Demonstrations and Activities

The Open Shuhada Street campaign, led primarily by Palestinian activists including the group Youth Against Settlements, has conducted annual demonstrations in Hebron since at least 2010, centered on late February to mark the anniversary of the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre by Baruch Goldstein. These events typically involve marches along restricted sections of the street or nearby areas, with participants demanding the lifting of Israeli-imposed access controls on Palestinians. On February 25, 2010, an initial protest drew local and international supporters commemorating the massacre and calling for unrestricted access.[68] Subsequent annual marches have varied in scale, often involving hundreds of Palestinian residents, Israeli and international activists. For example, the February 22, 2013 demonstration protested movement restrictions, featuring chants and symbolic actions near checkpoints.[77] In 2014, around two thousand participants joined the fifth annual event on February 21, marching to highlight the street's closure to Palestinian pedestrians and vehicles since the early 2000s.[78] Similar gatherings occurred in 2015 and 2019, with hundreds protesting segregation-like policies amid clashes with security forces in some instances.[79][80] Beyond Hebron-based actions, the campaign has coordinated global activities, including vigils, flash mobs, and film screenings. In 2012, organizers reported 35 worldwide events aimed at raising awareness of the street's economic decline and restricted access for Palestinians.[5] These efforts, often framed by participants as non-violent resistance, have faced Israeli military restrictions and occasional projectiles thrown at forces, as documented by settler advocacy groups.[81]

Criticisms of the Campaign

Critics of the Open Shuhada Street campaign, primarily from Israeli security officials and Jewish community representatives in Hebron, argue that it misrepresents the historical context of the street's restrictions by focusing narrowly on the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre committed by Jewish extremist Baruch Goldstein, while omitting the preceding and subsequent waves of Palestinian terrorism that necessitated repeated closures.[81][1] The campaign's annual demonstrations and advocacy, organized by groups like Youth Against Settlements, portray the partial closure—implemented after the massacre and reinforced during the Second Intifada—as an arbitrary act of segregation, but detractors contend this narrative ignores empirical evidence of the street serving as a conduit for attacks on Jewish worshippers and residents. For example, on May 2, 1980, Palestinian gunmen ambushed and killed six Jewish men returning from prayers at the Cave of the Patriarchs along Shuhada Street, injuring 20 others, prompting initial security measures that were later relaxed but reinstated due to ongoing threats.[82] Further criticism centers on the campaign's failure to acknowledge that partial reopenings of the street have historically correlated with resurgences in violence, undermining claims that unrestricted access would foster coexistence without heightened risk. According to reports from the Committee of the Jewish Community of Hebron, efforts to reopen sections post-1994 initially succeeded but were quickly reversed after renewed terrorist incidents, including shootings and stabbings targeting settlers and soldiers in the area, which demonstrated the causal link between reduced checkpoints and increased attacks.[81] Security analyses emphasize that Hebron's H2 sector, encompassing Shuhada Street, experienced over 100 documented terror attempts during the 1990s and early 2000s, with the street's strategic position near settlements and holy sites making it a focal point for such activities, a reality the campaign's media stunts—such as symbolic walks or protests—allegedly downplay to advance a politicized agenda.[1][82] Proponents of these critiques, including commentators in Israeli media, also fault the campaign for drawing false equivalences between isolated acts like Goldstein's attack—which was universally condemned by Israeli authorities—and systemic Palestinian violence, thereby framing Israeli security protocols as punitive rather than defensive responses grounded in threat history.[81] This selective framing, they argue, aligns with broader advocacy that prioritizes Palestinian mobility over verifiable safety data, such as the reduction in successful attacks following the entrenchment of checkpoints after 2000, when Hebron saw a marked decline in casualties compared to the pre-Intifada era.[1] Such omissions, critics maintain, not only erode public discourse but also risk encouraging policies that could expose vulnerable communities to preventable harm, as evidenced by the immediate security escalations required after demonstration-related disruptions.[82]

Legal and International Dimensions

Israeli Court Rulings

In response to petitions filed by Palestinian residents and organizations such as the Hebron Municipality and the Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI), the Israeli High Court of Justice examined restrictions on Palestinian access to Al-Shuhada Street, primarily imposed by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre and intensified during the Second Intifada due to repeated attacks on Jewish settlers.[83][15] A 2004 petition by the Hebron Municipality sought to declare the street open and compel the IDF to allow full Palestinian vehicular and pedestrian access, arguing that the de facto closure violated freedom of movement. The state responded that no formal closure order existed, though practical restrictions persisted for security reasons, including checkpoints and barriers to prevent vehicular threats to settlers. The court did not issue an immediate order to reopen but prompted further state clarifications, leading to a 2005 plan outlining protective measures for the Jewish community while permitting limited Palestinian pedestrian access in segments of the street.[83][17] Subsequent discussions extended over seven years, culminating in a July 6, 2011, Supreme Court decision rejecting petitions to fully reopen the street to Palestinian vehicles and unrestricted pedestrian traffic. The court accepted the IDF's security assessments, citing the street's proximity to settlements and history of violence—including stabbings, shootings, and the 1994 massacre—as justifying ongoing limitations, such as prohibiting Palestinian vehicles and confining pedestrian movement to sidewalks. Humanitarian exceptions were noted, allowing access for residents via back entrances or limited routes, but the ruling deferred to military discretion on broader reopenings.[15][17][83] On June 30, 2019, the Supreme Court rejected another petition challenging military orders maintaining closures in Hebron's H2 area, including Al-Shuhada Street, which formalized restrictions on Palestinian entry to protect against potential attacks amid ongoing settler-Palestinian tensions. The decision upheld the orders, emphasizing that security imperatives outweighed claims of disproportionate impact, while noting partial accommodations like designated pathways. These rulings reflect a pattern wherein the court has consistently validated IDF-imposed measures based on empirical threat data, such as documented incidents of violence targeting settlers along the route, rather than mandating full access despite advocacy for reopenings.[3][3]

International Perspectives and Critiques

The restrictions imposed on Al-Shuhada Street following the 1994 Cave of the Patriarchs massacre, which killed 29 Israeli worshippers, have drawn international scrutiny primarily for their impact on Palestinian movement and access. Amnesty International's 2016 report characterized the closures and checkpoints in Hebron's H2 area, including Al-Shuhada Street, as collective punishment violating international humanitarian law, calling for their immediate lifting to restore Palestinian rights to freedom of movement and non-discrimination.[84] Human Rights Watch echoed this in its 2010 analysis, documenting how such measures in Hebron create segregated pathways favoring Israeli settlers over Palestinians, amounting to systemic discrimination under occupation.[85] United Nations bodies have issued repeated critiques framing the street's partial closure—barring Palestinian vehicles and limiting pedestrian access—as emblematic of broader coercive policies. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) reported in 2019 that H2's 122 fixed obstacles and checkpoints, concentrated around Al-Shuhada Street, foster a punitive environment driving over 1,000 Palestinian families to relocate since 1994 due to restricted access to markets, schools, and services.[3] In August 2024, the UN Human Rights Office condemned escalations in these restrictions post-October 7, 2023, as discriminatory barriers denying 40,000 Palestinians effective entry to essential infrastructure, urging compliance with international human rights obligations.[86] The Norwegian Refugee Council's 2020 expert opinion on international humanitarian law in Hebron similarly faulted the street's militarized regime for prioritizing settler security over Palestinian civilian protections, contravening proportionality principles amid ongoing violence.[87] Critiques often invoke apartheid analogies, with Al-Shuhada Street dubbed "Apartheid Street" in activist discourse for its dual-use paths—open to settlers and tourists but gated for Palestinians—mirroring racial segregation precedents.[88] Amnesty International's 2023 report on automated surveillance in Hebron reinforced this by highlighting facial recognition at Checkpoint 56 on the street as enforcing racial profiling.[88] However, such characterizations are contested; analyses from Israeli security perspectives argue the label ignores causal security imperatives, noting the street remains traversable by Palestinian pedestrians and that closures averted repeat attacks after 1994's 29 fatalities and subsequent settler assaults, with over 1,100 documented Palestinian stone-throwing incidents in H2 by 2019.[1] The Temporary International Presence in Hebron (TIPH), a post-1994 observer mission involving Norway, Italy, Sweden, Switzerland, and Turkey, cataloged hundreds of movement violations annually until Israel terminated its mandate on January 31, 2019, citing biased reporting that underemphasized settler risks from Palestinian incitement.[89] U.S. State Department assessments, while acknowledging Palestinian hardships, contextualize restrictions within Israel's right to self-defense against religiously motivated violence, as in the 2023 International Religious Freedom Report detailing 30 settler attacks in Hebron amid mutual hostilities.[90] Organizations like Amnesty and Human Rights Watch, frequently cited in these critiques, face accusations of selective outrage—issuing disproportionate condemnations of Israel relative to global conflicts—potentially undermining their neutrality, though their legal citations draw from verified field data.[1]
