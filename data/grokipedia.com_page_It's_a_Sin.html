It's a Sin

It's a Sin is a British television drama miniseries created, written, and executive produced by Russell T Davies, consisting of five episodes broadcast on Channel 4 from 22 January to 6 February 2021.[1] The series is set in London across the decade from 1981 to 1991 and centers on a group of young gay men and their friends whose lives are upended by the onset and progression of the AIDS epidemic, blending elements of joy, denial, and tragedy.[2] The narrative follows protagonist Ritchie Tozer, played by Olly Alexander, as he moves from the Isle of Wight to the city, forming bonds in a shared flat known as the Pink Palace with characters portrayed by Callum Scott Howells, Omari Douglas, Nathaniel Curtis, and Lydia West.[3] Drawing from Davies' personal recollections of the era and real-life inspirations such as activist Jill Nalderâ€”who influenced the character Jill and lost friends to the diseaseâ€”the program depicts the social stigma, medical misinformation, and institutional delays that characterized early responses to HIV/AIDS in the United Kingdom.[4] The title derives from the 1987 Pet Shop Boys song, which underscores themes of guilt and hedonism amid crisis.[2] Upon release, It's a Sin achieved high viewership, becoming Channel 4's most binged new drama series and prompting discussions on historical accountability for the epidemic's toll, which claimed over 100,000 lives worldwide by 1991 per empirical health data.[5] Critically, it garnered praise for its unflinching portrayal of camaraderie and loss, earning an 8.6/10 rating from aggregated user reviews, though some outlets noted its selective focus on personal stories over broader epidemiological causation.[3] The series received multiple BAFTA Television Award nominations, including for limited series and writing, and secured wins at international festivals such as the Banff World Media Festival for emerging program of the year.[6][7] Despite acclaim, it faced scrutiny at the 2022 BAFTA ceremony for not converting all nominations into wins, highlighting subjective variances in judging criteria.[8]

Origins and Development

Background and Writing

"It's a Sin" was composed by Pet Shop Boys members Neil Tennant and Chris Lowe in early 1983, during their initial collaborative songwriting sessions that began shortly after they met in August 1981.[9] The track originated from informal jamming where Tennant contributed lyrics and acoustic guitar ideas while Lowe explored synth bass lines and harmonies on a monophonic synthesizer, yielding several early hits including "West End Girls" and "Opportunities (Let's Make Lots of Money)."[9] The song's lyrics draw directly from Tennant's Catholic education, which emphasized confession and framed everyday behaviors as sinful, fostering lasting guilt that he later critiqued as overly repressive.[10] Tennant intentionally amplified these elements for dramatic impact, aiming to produce a "really big and mega-Catholic" sound that transformed personal reflection into a theatrical synth-pop statement on moral indoctrination.[11] This approach contrasted with the duo's typically understated style, prioritizing "mad ecstatic euphoria" and urgency akin to heavy metal despite their electronic roots.[11] An early demo version was recorded in 1984 in New York with producer Bobby Orlando, incorporating initial melodic ideas that evolved significantly before the final recording for the 1987 album Actually.[11] The composition retained core hymnal chord progressions suggested by Lowe but discarded experimental prefixes like a Euro-style intro melody, refining the structure to heighten its confessional intensity.[12]

Inspiration and Themes

"It's a Sin" was inspired by Neil Tennant's experiences at St. Cuthbert's Grammar School, a Catholic institution in Newcastle upon Tyne, where he was raised in a strict religious environment that emphasized sin, confession, and the prohibition of non-procreative sex.[13] [14] Tennant composed the song in 1982 during a demo session in Camden, drawing from lingering feelings of guilt imposed by his education, which he later described as frustrating and aimed partly at his former teachers.[14] [15] In interviews, he has explained that the track originated as a response to the Catholic doctrine taught to adolescent boys, viewing sexâ€”beyond purposes of procreationâ€”as inherently sinful, a perspective that clashed with personal desires.[15] The primary themes revolve around religious guilt and the psychological burden of confessional practices, depicted through lyrics simulating a dramatic Catholic confession in Latin ("Confiteor Deo omnipotenti...") followed by defiant admissions of wrongdoing.[13] Tennant has characterized the song as a critique of the church's instillation of shame over natural impulses, encompassing sex in a broader sense rather than solely homosexuality, though its context in 1980s Britainâ€”amid cultural tensions around gay identityâ€”lends it resonance with queer experiences of repression.[15] [13] The narrative portrays sin not as redemption-seeking but as an operatic embrace of forbidden actions, reflecting Tennant's unfavorable assessment of ecclesiastical authority and its lasting emotional impact.[15] This thematic focus provoked controversy upon release, including backlash from religious figures who viewed the lyrics as an attack on doctrine.[13]

Recording and Production

"It's a Sin" was produced and engineered by Julian Mendelsohn at Sarm West Studios in London during sessions in 1987, with additional work at Advision Studios.[16][14] Mendelsohn, who handled production on roughly half of the parent album Actually, built the track around layered keyboards and orchestral stabs, incorporating samples from a Fairlight CMI sampler operated by programmer Andy Richards.[16] The studio setup featured a 48-channel E-Series SSL console and Mitsubishi X850 digital 32-track recorder, enabling precise multitracking of elements like Roland rackmounted synthesizers, a LinnDrum, and Synclavier sequences.[16] Sessions typically ran from 10 or 11 a.m. for 10 to 14 hours, with Mendelsohn and Richards often refining details until 1 or 2 a.m.[14] Neil Tennant's vocals were recorded using a Neumann U87 microphone, with multiple takes layered to achieve the desired dramatic effect, as Tennant refined his delivery over repeated efforts.[16] Ambient field recordings from Brompton Oratory, captured on a two-track Nagra recorder, provided Latin mass echoes and church reverb, while additional samples included a thunderclap programmed by Richards, a NASA countdown, and choir elements sourced from Westminster Cathedral.[16][14] Tennant also recited passages from a Catholic prayer book, contributing to the track's thematic intensity rooted in guilt and confession.[14] The production emphasized an overblown, insistent style influenced by acts like ZZ Top, with Chris Lowe focusing on key hooks and arrangements alongside Tennant.[14] Mendelsohn noted challenges in maintaining objectivity, occasionally stepping away to regain perspective on the track's bombastic elements.[16] Stephen Hague, who produced other Actually tracks, provided the final mix, enhancing the song's polished synth-pop sheen.[16] An early demo had been recorded in a Camden studio as far back as 1982, but the 1987 version marked the definitive realization for release as the album's lead single on June 15, 1987.[14]

Release and Promotion

Single Release and Artwork

"It's a Sin" was released as a single by Pet Shop Boys on 15 June 1987 through Parlophone Records in the United Kingdom.[17] The release followed the duo's debut album Please and served as the lead single from their second studio album Actually. Available in multiple formats including 7-inch vinyl, 12-inch vinyl, cassette, and CD, the 7-inch version featured the A-side "It's a Sin" backed with "You Know Where You Went Wrong", while extended editions included the "Disco Mix".[17] [18] The single's artwork, photographed by Eric Watson, depicts Neil Tennant in a dramatic pose within the unused sacristy of Christ Church, Spitalfields, in London, evoking ecclesiastical themes aligned with the song's exploration of guilt and Catholic upbringing.[19] This imagery underscored the track's provocative blend of synth-pop and moral introspection, contributing to its immediate cultural impact upon release.[11]

Music Video

The music video for "It's a Sin" was directed by English filmmaker Derek Jarman and premiered in May 1987 in the United Kingdom.[20] Jarman, renowned for his experimental and avant-garde works, portrayed Pet Shop Boys members Neil Tennant and Chris Lowe as monks subjected to a surreal inquisition, emphasizing themes of guilt and confession central to the song's lyrics.[21] The video features dramatic visuals including flames, ecclesiastical architecture, and symbolic representations of the Seven Deadly Sins embodied by actors in exaggerated costumes, enhancing the track's operatic and theatrical quality.[21] This collaboration marked the first of several between Jarman and Pet Shop Boys, influencing their subsequent visual style with bold, artistic imagery that contrasted the duo's polished synth-pop sound.[22] Shot in a stylized, low-budget aesthetic typical of Jarman's independent approach, the production avoided conventional pop video tropes, opting instead for allegorical narrative elements that evoked religious repression and personal reckoning.[21] Tennant and Lowe appear in monastic robes, delivering the song's dramatic proclamations amid inquisitorial interrogations, which visually underscore the autobiographical undertones of Catholic guilt drawn from Tennant's upbringing.[21] The video's release coincided with the single's chart success, contributing to its cultural impact through MTV and television airplay.[20]

Live Performances and Touring

"It's a Sin" was first performed live by Pet Shop Boys in 1987 at The Roxy Theatre in Los Angeles, shortly after its single release.[23] A notable early rendition occurred at Wembley Arena in 1989, featuring a production directed by Derek Jarman, which highlighted the song's dramatic staging with orchestral elements and visual effects.[24] [25] The duo's inaugural major tour, the Performance Tour of 1991â€“1992, prominently featured "It's a Sin" early in the setlist, typically as the second song following the instrumental "This Must Be the Place I Waited Years to Leave."[26] [27] Performances included dates at Wembley Arena on June 8, 1991, in London, and the Orpheum Theatre on April 3, 1991, in Minneapolis, where it served as a high-energy opener to the vocal portion of the show, backed by a full band and symphony orchestra.[28] In later tours, the song remained a concert staple, adapted with varying arrangements to suit evolving productions. During the Pandemonium Tour in 2009â€“2010, it preceded the encore at select dates, integrating disco influences and elaborate choreography.[29] The Dreamworld: The Greatest Hits Live tour, launched in 2022 and extending into 2025, consistently includes "It's a Sin" in its setlist of fan favorites, with performances such as at the Hollywood Bowl on October 7, 2022, and October 8, 2023, during the co-headlining Unity Tour segment with New Order.[30] [31] Additional 2025 dates in the UK and Europe for Dreamworld emphasize sing-along hits like this track, underscoring its enduring appeal in live settings.[32]

Commercial Performance

Chart Performance

"It's a Sin" debuted on the UK Singles Chart on 21 June 1987 at number two before ascending to number one the following week, where it remained for three consecutive weeks.[33][34] The single spent a total of 16 weeks on the chart.[35] In the United States, the song peaked at number nine on the Billboard Hot 100, marking the duo's third top-ten hit there, and charted for 12 weeks.[36] It also reached number eight on the Canadian RPM Top Singles chart.[37] The track performed strongly across various international markets, achieving top-ten positions in countries including Australia (number ten) and several European territories.[37]

Certifications and Sales

"It's a Sin" was certified Gold in the United Kingdom by the British Phonographic Industry (BPI), recognizing combined sales, downloads, and streaming equivalent to 400,000 units. The certification reflects the track's enduring popularity, bolstered by later streaming surges following the 2021 Channel 4 series of the same name, though original physical sales alone exceeded 448,000 copies in the UK from 1985 to 1994 according to Official Charts Company data.[38][39][40] In Austria, the single attained Gold certification from IFPI Austria on September 21, 1990, for 15,000 units. No certification was issued by the RIAA in the United States, where the single peaked at number nine on the Billboard Hot 100 but did not meet gold threshold sales of 500,000 units at the time.[41]

Reception and Analysis

Critical Reception

Upon its release as the lead single from Actually on 15 June 1987, "It's a Sin" received widespread acclaim from music critics for its dramatic synth-pop production and thematic depth exploring Catholic guilt and repression.[14] The track's bombastic arrangement, featuring orchestral swells and pulsating rhythms produced by Julian Mendelsohn, was highlighted as a standout element that elevated Pet Shop Boys' ironic pop sensibility.[14] Critics praised the song's lyrical critique of religious indoctrination, with Neil Tennant's semi-autobiographical reflections on his Catholic upbringing drawing comparisons to broader cultural examinations of shame and conformity.[42] One contemporary analysis noted its effectiveness in blending personal narrative with synth-driven energy, though some reviewers expressed reservations about its unrelenting intensity, describing it as less nuanced than the duo's later thematic explorations.[43] In retrospective assessments, the song has been lauded as a defining moment in Pet Shop Boys' catalog, often ranked among their top works for its enduring bombast and cultural resonance.[44] Tennant himself critiqued overly literal interpretations, arguing the track's exaggerated styleâ€”complete with mock-operatic vocalsâ€”intended irony rather than solemnity, a view echoed in analyses of its "over-the-top" delivery.[44] Its influence on subsequent pop critiques of institutional authority persists, with outlets affirming its role as an early mainstream challenge to religious dogma.[42]

Musical Composition

"It's a Sin" is a synth-pop song structured in a conventional verse-chorus form, with verses building tension through spoken-word-like delivery over pulsating synth bass and rhythmic percussion, leading into an anthemic chorus that repeats the titular phrase for emphasis.[45] The track features a bridge in Eâ™­ minor that provides contrast to the primary sections, incorporating a middle eight for dynamic variation and reflection, a common element in 1980s pop songwriting.[46][47] This structure supports the song's dramatic arc, escalating from introspective verses to explosive choruses. Composed primarily in C minor, the song maintains a tempo of 127 beats per minute, facilitating its danceable yet urgent feel suitable for both club play and radio.[48][49] The harmonic foundation draws on a chord progression featuring C minor to Eâ™­ minor to G minor, creating emotional depth through minor-key tension, while sections incorporate descending fifths patterns that enhance the sense of cyclical guilt central to the lyrics.[50] Instrumentation relies heavily on synthesizers, with the Fairlight CMI Series III providing key orchestral swells, brass-like stabs, and sampled percussion that mimic a full ensemble, emblematic of mid-1980s electronic production techniques.[51] Chris Lowe's keyboard arrangements emphasize layered synth pads and arpeggiated sequences, contributing to the track's hi-NRG influences and cinematic scale without live instrumentation.[52]

Thematic and Cultural Analysis

The song's lyrics, penned by Neil Tennant, center on the psychological burden of Catholic indoctrination, portraying a confessional narrative where the protagonist retrospectively condemns their entire life as sinful under the rigid moral framework imposed during youth.[21] Tennant drew directly from his attendance at St. Cuthbert's Roman Catholic Grammar School in Newcastle upon Tyne, where education emphasized purity in "thought and word and deed," fostering an internalized guilt that the track critiques as stifling and hypocritical.[42] This theme manifests through dramatic, accusatory refrains like "When I look back upon my life / It's always with a sense of shame," evoking a theatrical rejection of religious authority's failure to equip individuals for human fallibility.[15] Musically, the track amplifies this tension via its fusion of orchestral samples from a Latin Massâ€”sourced from a 1976 recording of Francisco Guerrero's Missa Sex Vocumâ€”with pulsating synth-pop rhythms, symbolizing a clash between ecclesiastical solemnity and secular liberation.[53] Tennant has described the composition as channeling frustration with how such upbringing equated personal agency with moral transgression, positioning the song as an act of personal exorcism rather than overt political statement.[54] Critics have noted its ironic distance, where the bombastic production undercuts literal piety, aligning with Pet Shop Boys' broader oeuvre of detached emotional inquiry.[43] Culturally, "It's a Sin" emerged in 1987 amid the UK AIDS epidemic, where homosexuality faced moralistic condemnation often framed in sin-and-punishment rhetoric by religious and conservative figures, resonating with queer audiences confronting societal stigma.[55] Released shortly before Section 28's enactmentâ€”which prohibited local authorities from "promoting" homosexualityâ€”the track's subtext of repressed desire and guilt offered veiled solidarity to gay subcultures, though Tennant maintained its core as autobiographical critique of Catholicism over explicit activism.[43] Its chart-topping success in the UK and Europe underscored synth-pop's role in mainstreaming ironic explorations of identity during Thatcher-era conservatism, influencing subsequent electronic acts in blending personal catharsis with dancefloor anthems.[15]

Controversies

Response from St. Cuthbert's School

A teacher at St. Cuthbert's Grammar School in Newcastle upon Tyne, where Neil Tennant was educated during the late 1960s and early 1970s, publicly criticized the song's lyrics following its release on 2 November 1987. The teacher took issue with Tennant's portrayal of the Catholic school's emphasis on moral purity and guilt, describing it as an unfair depiction of the education provided.[17] This response reflected broader tensions between Tennant's retrospective critique of institutional religionâ€”rooted in his experiences of strict discipline and doctrinal teachingsâ€”and defenders of the school's approach, which aimed to instill ethical values through rigorous instruction. No formal statement from the school's administration was issued, as the institution had closed in 1976, but the teacher's press comments highlighted perceived ingratitude toward the formative role of such environments.[17]

Plagiarism Accusation by Jonathan King

In June 1987, shortly after the release of "It's a Sin" as a single on 15 June, British music producer and broadcaster Jonathan King publicly accused the Pet Shop Boys of plagiarizing the melody from Cat Stevens' 1970 song "Wild World."[56][17] King made the claim on radio broadcasts and in his column in The Sun newspaper, asserting that the similarities in melody and arrangement were evident and unauthorized.[56] To substantiate his allegation, King recorded and released his own cover version of "Wild World" in 1987, deliberately mimicking the orchestral and rhythmic arrangement of "It's a Sin," including synthesized strings and dramatic builds, in an attempt to highlight the purported copying.[16][57] Despite this effort, King's single failed commercially, achieving minimal chart success.[16] The Pet Shop Boys responded by initiating legal action against King for defamation. King ultimately lost the case, issued a public apology, and agreed to pay damages, which were directed to a charity selected by the duo at their request.[17][57] The incident underscored tensions in the music industry over sampling and melodic similarities but did not impact the chart performance of "It's a Sin," which reached number one in the UK.[56]

Covers and Legacy

Notable Cover Versions

Ghost covered "It's a Sin" on the deluxe edition of their 2018 album Prequelle, transforming the synth-pop original into a theatrical heavy metal rendition featuring Papa Emeritus's operatic vocals and orchestral elements.[58] Paul Anka included a big band swing version on his 2005 album Rock Swings, reinterpreting the track with brass arrangements and his crooner style amid covers of other rock hits.[59] In 2021, Olly Alexander of Years & Years released a piano-led cover as a standalone single, coinciding with his role in the Channel 4 miniseries It's a Sin, which drew renewed attention to the song's themes of guilt and the AIDS crisis; the track amassed millions of streams.[60] That same year, Alexander collaborated with Elton John on a live duet of the song during John's Instagram concert series, blending piano balladry with emotional intensity.[61] Jonathan Davis, frontman of Korn, contributed a somber, bagpipe-infused solo cover to the soundtrack of the 2021 film Paradise City, emphasizing dark, atmospheric tones suited to the thriller's narrative.[62][63]

Long-term Cultural Impact

The song's themes of guilt induced by religious upbringing and defiance against imposed moral purity have contributed to its lasting resonance in queer cultural narratives, serving as an early mainstream pop critique of institutional dogma during an era of heightened social conservatism under Margaret Thatcher's government, which enacted Section 28 in 1988 to prohibit the "promotion" of homosexuality by local authorities.[55][11] Its gothic, orchestral synth-pop arrangement, produced by Julian Mendelsohn with contributions from Trevor Horn's studio techniques, exemplified hi-NRG electronic styles that influenced subsequent dance and synth genres, as noted in analyses of 1980s production innovations.[16][64] A significant revival occurred in 2021 with Russell T.
