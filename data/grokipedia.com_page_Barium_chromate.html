Barium chromate

Barium chromate is a yellow sand-like powder with the formula BaCrO4. It is a known oxidizing agent and produces a green flame when heated, a result of the barium ions. Except where otherwise noted, data are given for materials in their standard state (at 25 °C [77 °F], 100 kPa).

History

Barium chromate was first synthesized in the early 19th century by French chemist Louis Nicolas Vauquelin, shortly after his discovery of chromium in 1797–1798. It was analyzed by reacting barium nitrate with a chromate or dichromate solution. By the mid-19th century, it gained prominence as a pigment known as lemon yellow (PY31), valued for its bright color and durability in oil paintings.[1][2] The first naturally occurring form of barium chromate, as the mineral hashemite, was discovered in 1983 in west-central Jordan. The brown crystals found perched on host rocks were named hashemite in honor of the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan. The hashemite crystals range in color from light yellowish-brown to a darker greenish-brown and are usually less than 1 mm in length. The hashemite crystals are not composed of pure barium chromate but instead contain some sulfur content as well. Barium Chromate

Common Uses

Barium chromate has been found to be useful in many capacities. The compound is often used as a carrier for the chromium ions. One such case is the use of barium chromate as a sulfate scavenger in chromium electroplating baths. Over time the chromium concentration of the bath will decrease until the bath is no longer functional. Adding barium chromate enhances the life of the bath by adding to the chromic acid concentration. Barium chromate is an oxidizing agent, making it useful as a burn rate modifier in pyrotechnic compositions. It is especially useful in delay compositions such as delay fuses. Barium chromate is used as a corrosion inhibitive pigment when zinc-alloy electroplating surfaces. When mixed with solid fumaric acid, barium chromate can be used in the removal of impurities and residual moisture from organic dry-cleaning solvents or from petroleum fuels. Barium chromate is also used in the composition of a catalyst for alkane dehydrogenation. Barium chromate has also been used to color paints. The pigment known as lemon yellow often contained barium chromate mixed with lead sulfate.[12] Due to its moderate tinting strength lemon yellow was not employed very frequently in oil painting. Pierre-Auguste Renoir and Claude Monet are known to have painted with lemon yellow.[14] However, due to its toxicity, particularly the hexavalent chromium content, its use as an artists' pigment has been largely discontinued since the late 20th century.[15]

Barium Chromate Research

Research

In 2004 a method was found for making single-crystalline ABO4 type nanorods. This method consisted of a modified template synthesis technique that was originally used for the synthesis of organic microtubules. Nanoparticles are allowed to grow in the pores of alumina membranes of various sizes. The varying sizes of the pores allow the growth to be controlled and cause the shapes to be reproducible. The alumina is then dissolved, leaving the nanoparticles behind intact. The synthesis can be carried out at room temperature, greatly reducing the cost and constrictions on conditions. Hexavalent chromium compounds are known to accumulate in bronchial bifurcation sites, where they can induce tumors. In 2010, a study was conducted on four such compounds—zinc chromate, barium chromate, lead chromate, and sodium chromate—to assess their genotoxicity and cytotoxicity in human bronchial cells. Using zinc chromate as a standard, the study found that barium chromate is both genotoxic and cytotoxic, with effects similar to but slightly less potent than those of zinc chromate. The cytotoxicity was determined to most likely be a result of the genotoxicity, but the precise cause of the genotoxicity remains unclear. In 2016, researchers developed nano-sized barium chromate for photocatalytic degradation of erythrosine B dye, demonstrating its potential in environmental remediation.[18] In 2024, graphene oxide-based barium chromate composites were synthesized via sonication-assisted hydrothermal process, showing enhanced properties for potential applications in materials science.[19]

Safety

Barium chromate is classified as dangerous under the Globally Harmonized System (GHS), with the signal word "Danger". It poses multiple hazards, including being an oxidizing solid (Category 3), acutely toxic if swallowed or inhaled (Category 4), a respiratory sensitizer (Category 1), carcinogenic (Category 1A), and very toxic to aquatic life with long-lasting effects (Category 1).[20] Key hazard statements include: H272 (May intensify fire; oxidizer), H302 + H332 (Harmful if swallowed or inhaled), H334 (May cause allergy or asthma symptoms or breathing difficulties if inhaled), H350 (May cause cancer), and H410 (Very toxic to aquatic life with long lasting effects).[21] Exposure can cause irritation to skin, eyes, and respiratory tract; potential liver and kidney damage; allergic reactions; and nasal septum ulceration with chronic inhalation. Barium chromate contains hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)), which is a known human carcinogen. It is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) for Cr(VI) compounds, a known human carcinogen by the National Toxicology Program (NTP), and regulated under OSHA standards for hexavalent chromium (permissible exposure limit: 5 µg/m³ as an 8-hour time-weighted average).[22][23][24] Precautions include using personal protective equipment, avoiding dust generation, and ensuring proper ventilation. In case of exposure, seek immediate medical attention.[20]
