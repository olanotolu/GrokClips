2023_Israel–Lebanon_shellings

The 2023 Israel–Lebanon shellings consisted of recurrent cross-border exchanges of artillery fire, rockets, and anti-tank missiles between the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) and Hezbollah militants along the shared frontier, beginning on 8 October 2023 as Hezbollah launched attacks in declared solidarity with Hamas following the latter's assault on southern Israel the previous day.[1][2] Hezbollah, an Iran-backed Shiite militia embedded in southern Lebanon, initiated the clashes by targeting Israeli military positions near disputed Shebaa Farms, prompting immediate IDF artillery responses and airstrikes on Hezbollah launch sites and infrastructure in retaliation.[1][3] These skirmishes, framed by Hezbollah as support for Palestinians amid Israel's Gaza operations, escalated into near-daily tit-for-tat actions through the end of 2023, with Hezbollah firing over 1,000 projectiles into northern Israel—primarily at military targets but occasionally impacting civilian areas—while Israel conducted precision strikes to degrade Hezbollah's rocket arsenal and command nodes, often located amid Lebanese villages in violation of UN Resolution 1701.[3][2] By year's end, the exchanges had killed at least 20 Hezbollah fighters and a handful of Lebanese civilians from Israeli counterfire, alongside 12 Israeli soldiers and 2 civilians from Hezbollah attacks, displacing over 60,000 Israelis from border communities and prompting limited Lebanese evacuations.[4][3] Notable incidents included Hezbollah's use of advanced Kornet anti-tank guided missiles against Israeli vehicles and outposts, met by IDF drone strikes on operatives, reflecting a calibrated escalation aimed at deterrence rather than full invasion, though straining the 2006 ceasefire framework.[5] Controversies arose over Hezbollah's tactic of operating from civilian environs to shield assets, complicating Israeli responses and drawing international scrutiny, while Israel's strikes were criticized by some observers for collateral damage despite targeting verified militant sites—claims often amplified by sources with institutional incentives to emphasize Lebanese casualties over the initiating aggression.[6][7] The shellings underscored Hezbollah's role as a non-state proxy extending Iran's regional influence, with minimal Lebanese government intervention, highlighting the fragility of state authority in southern Lebanon and the persistent threat of broader war.[3][2]

Background

Historical context of Israel-Hezbollah tensions

Hezbollah, a Shia Islamist militant group and political party, emerged in 1982 amid Lebanon's civil war (1975–1990) and Israel's invasion of Lebanon that year, which aimed to expel Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) forces launching attacks into northern Israel.[8] Backed by Iran, Hezbollah coalesced from disparate Shia factions resisting the Israeli occupation of southern Lebanon, framing its ideology as opposition to Israeli presence and Western influence, with explicit calls for Israel's destruction rooted in Iranian revolutionary principles.[9] The group conducted guerrilla operations, suicide bombings, and kidnappings against Israeli forces and allies, including the 1983 Beirut barracks bombing that killed 241 U.S. personnel and 58 French troops, though Hezbollah denied direct responsibility for the latter.[9] Israel maintained a security zone in southern Lebanon from 1982 to 2000, supported by the South Lebanon Army militia, to buffer rocket attacks and cross-border raids, during which Hezbollah's asymmetric tactics inflicted steady casualties on Israeli troops, totaling around 600 killed over 18 years.[10] Hezbollah's resistance, funded and trained by Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, elevated its status among Lebanese Shia communities as a defender against occupation, while Israel faced domestic pressure to withdraw due to mounting losses and political costs.[9] In May 2000, Israel unilaterally withdrew its forces to the international border, a move certified as compliant with UN Security Council Resolution 425 by the United Nations, though Hezbollah disputed the border's delineation in areas like Shebaa Farms to justify continued militarization.[11] The withdrawal boosted Hezbollah's prestige, enabling it to consolidate control over southern Lebanon and expand its arsenal, including Iranian-supplied rockets, without effective Lebanese government interference.[12] Tensions escalated in July 2006 when Hezbollah launched a cross-border raid into Israel, killing three soldiers and capturing two, prompting Israel's 34-day military campaign involving airstrikes and a ground incursion to degrade Hezbollah's capabilities and secure the hostages' release.[13] The conflict saw Hezbollah fire over 4,000 rockets into northern Israel, displacing 300,000 Israelis, while Israeli operations caused extensive damage in southern Lebanon; casualties included 44 Israeli civilians and 119 soldiers killed, alongside approximately 1,200 Lebanese deaths, predominantly civilians per Lebanese reports but including many Hezbollah fighters.[13][14] A UN-brokered ceasefire under Resolution 1701 required Hezbollah's disarmament south of the Litani River and Lebanese Army deployment, but enforcement lapsed, allowing Hezbollah to rebuild and amass an estimated 150,000 rockets by the 2020s, often with Iranian precision-guidance upgrades aimed at Israeli cities.[9] Post-2006, Hezbollah's role as Iran's primary proxy against Israel intensified, with the group supporting Palestinian factions like Hamas through training and funding via Iran, while Israeli airstrikes targeted Hezbollah weapons convoys in Syria, where the group deployed up to 10,000 fighters since 2011 to bolster Assad's regime.[9] Periodic border incidents, such as Hezbollah's 2015 threat to "set Tel Aviv ablaze" over Shebaa Farms disputes, underscored unresolved grievances, with Hezbollah rejecting disarmament and viewing its military wing as essential deterrence against Israeli aggression.[9] This entrenched standoff, characterized by Hezbollah's fortified positions in civilian areas and Israel's preemptive doctrine, set the stage for renewed cross-border exchanges, reflecting causal dynamics of proxy empowerment and territorial disputes unresolved by prior withdrawals and ceasefires.[9]

Immediate prelude following October 7, 2023 Hamas attack

Following the Hamas-led attack on Israel on October 7, 2023, which killed about 1,200 people and saw over 250 hostages taken into Gaza, Hezbollah—an Iran-backed Shiite militant group based in Lebanon and long allied with Hamas—launched its first cross-border assault the next day.[1][15] On October 8, Hezbollah fired rockets and artillery shells targeting Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) positions in the disputed Shebaa Farms area along the Israel-Lebanon border, as well as sites in northern Israel, explicitly framing the barrage as solidarity with Hamas and the Palestinian cause against Israel's Gaza operations.[1][16] This marked the start of daily exchanges, with Hezbollah conducting around 150 such attacks in the initial weeks, primarily using unguided rockets like the 220mm Fadi-1, which lack precision and risk civilian areas due to their inaccuracy.[17] Israel's immediate response involved artillery shelling of Hezbollah launch sites and observation posts in southern Lebanon, aimed at degrading the group's ability to fire from Lebanese territory without escalating to full invasion.[1][18] The IDF reported intercepting most incoming projectiles via its Iron Dome system, with no Israeli fatalities from the October 8 barrage, though air raid sirens prompted evacuations of border communities like Kibbutz Menara and Metula, displacing thousands preemptively amid fears of a northern front mirroring the southern Gaza invasion.[1] Hezbollah claimed hits on 19 IDF sites, but Israeli assessments indicated limited damage, attributing the attacks to Hezbollah's intent to divert IDF resources from Gaza and pressure Israel into concessions on Palestinian issues.[16][19] These initial volleys violated the 2006 UN-brokered ceasefire terms under Resolution 1701, which required Hezbollah's withdrawal from south of the Litani River, a zone where the group maintained an estimated 150,000 rockets and operative presence despite Lebanese government and UNIFIL oversight.[15] By October 11, exchanges intensified with Hezbollah mortar fire prompting further Israeli airstrikes, killing at least one Hezbollah fighter and signaling a pattern of tit-for-tat actions tied to Gaza developments, as Hezbollah conditioned de-escalation on an end to Israel's Hamas campaign.[20][19] While mainstream outlets like Reuters documented the sequence as Hezbollah-initiated, some analyses noted Israel's preemptive alerts and restrained strikes to avoid broader war, given Hezbollah's superior rocket arsenal compared to Hamas.[1][18]

Initiation of hostilities

Hezbollah's coordinated attacks starting October 8, 2023

On October 8, 2023, Hezbollah initiated cross-border attacks against Israel by firing guided rockets and artillery shells at three Israeli military positions in the disputed Shebaa Farms area along the Lebanon-Israel border.[1] The group explicitly stated that the assault was conducted "in solidarity with the Palestinian resistance" in response to the Hamas-led attack on Israel the previous day.[21] Hezbollah described the operation as involving "large numbers of rockets and shells," marking the beginning of a deliberate campaign aimed at supporting its ally Hamas by opening a northern front.[21] These initial strikes were part of Hezbollah's broader strategy to pressure Israel through sustained, coordinated barrages, leveraging its arsenal of short-range rockets and artillery positioned in southern Lebanon.[22] The attacks targeted military outposts rather than civilian areas initially, reflecting a calculated escalation to avoid immediate full-scale war while signaling intent to divert Israeli resources from Gaza.[23] Hezbollah's leadership, including Secretary-General Hassan Nasrallah, later framed the operations as fulfilling obligations under an informal Iran-Hamas-Hezbollah axis to coordinate against Israel, though the group's independent decision-making was evident in the rapid timing post-October 7.[24] From October 8 onward, Hezbollah escalated to near-daily rocket and drone launches, firing thousands of projectiles into northern Israel over subsequent months, with over 8,000 recorded by September 2024.[25] This pattern involved coordinated volleys from multiple launch sites, often synchronized with Hamas activities in Gaza, though Hezbollah maintained operational autonomy backed by Iranian-supplied weaponry.[26] The attacks displaced tens of thousands of Israeli civilians from border communities and inflicted limited but persistent damage on military and infrastructure targets.[27]

Israel's initial defensive responses

On October 8, 2023, following Hezbollah's launch of mortar shells toward Israeli military positions in the Shebaa Farms area, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) conducted artillery barrages targeting the originating sites in southern Lebanon.[1] These strikes hit Hezbollah military infrastructure near the border, including a tent serving as an observation post where operatives had been detected.[28] The IDF described the actions as precise retaliatory measures to neutralize launch points and prevent further incursions, without advancing ground forces into Lebanese territory.[18] Over the next two days, October 9–10, 2023, the IDF maintained defensive posture through additional artillery shelling in response to intermittent Hezbollah rocket and mortar fire, focusing on terrorist positions within 1–2 kilometers of the Blue Line demarcation.[1] Reports indicated that these exchanges resulted in the deaths of at least three Hezbollah fighters from Israeli fire, with no reported Israeli casualties in the initial volleys.[29] Concurrently, Israeli authorities ordered the evacuation of approximately 28 northern communities adjacent to the border, displacing over 60,000 residents to mitigate risks from cross-border attacks.[30] The responses emphasized border security enhancements, including reinforced troop deployments along the frontier and activation of Iron Dome interceptors to counter incoming projectiles, while avoiding deeper strikes into Lebanon to limit escalation.[31] IDF statements underscored the operations as defensive necessities tied to Hezbollah's solidarity attacks in support of Hamas, rather than offensive initiatives.

Escalation in late 2023

October 2023 border clashes and rocket barrages

Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas attack on Israel, Hezbollah initiated cross-border hostilities on October 8 by firing rockets and artillery shells at Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) positions near the Shebaa Farms area, targeting military outposts in a stated show of solidarity with Hamas.[23][18] The barrage included dozens of projectiles, most of which were intercepted by Israeli air defenses or landed in open areas, causing no immediate casualties but prompting evacuations in northern Israeli communities like Kibbutz Menara.[18] In response, the IDF conducted artillery strikes on Hezbollah launch sites and positions in southern Lebanon, followed by airstrikes on a military structure used for surveillance.[18] Exchanges intensified over the following days in a pattern of tit-for-tat actions. On October 9, after Israeli shelling killed at least two Hezbollah members, the group fired additional rockets and anti-tank guided missiles (ATGMs) at IDF troops and vehicles along the border, with no reported injuries on the Israeli side.[29] By October 11, Hezbollah launched another rocket salvo toward northern Israel, prompting IDF artillery barrages on villages in southern Lebanon and an airstrike on a Hezbollah observation post near the border.[20][32] These incidents involved short-range rockets and mortars primarily aimed at military targets, though some projectiles neared civilian areas, leading to heightened alerts and partial evacuations affecting tens of thousands of Israeli residents in the Galilee region.[33] Throughout the rest of October, Hezbollah maintained near-daily barrages, employing rockets, mortars, ATGMs, and occasional drone incursions to probe IDF defenses and support its Palestinian ally.[24] Notable escalations included an October 16 Hezbollah ATGM strike on an IDF tank near the border, which caused no damage or injuries, met with Israeli counterfire.[33] Clashes peaked around October 17, when five Hezbollah fighters were killed during attempted infiltrations and firefights near the Blue Line demarcation.[34] IDF responses focused on precision artillery, tank shelling, and limited airstrikes targeting Hezbollah launchers, command posts, and infrastructure to degrade offensive capabilities without deeper incursions.[30] Casualties remained limited compared to later phases, with Hezbollah reporting losses of at least a dozen fighters from Israeli strikes by mid-October, primarily military personnel engaged in offensive operations.[34] No Israeli soldiers or civilians were killed in these border clashes during October, though the barrages displaced over 60,000 Israelis from border communities and inflicted minor property damage from near-misses.[24] The exchanges underscored Hezbollah's strategy of low-intensity pressure to divert Israeli resources from Gaza, while Israel prioritized containment to avoid a multi-front war.[35]

November-December 2023 exchanges and targeted strikes

In November 2023, Hezbollah intensified cross-border attacks on northern Israel, launching multiple rocket barrages, mortar strikes, and anti-tank guided missiles (ATGMs) in coordination with its support for Hamas in Gaza. On November 2, Hezbollah conducted several strikes on Israeli military positions, including its first use of explosive drones against targets near the border.[36] By November 12, the group fired 15 such attacks, including mortars and rockets, on the Manara region, wounding seven Israeli soldiers and eleven civilians, with one in critical condition from an ATGM strike near Metula.[37][38] In response, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) conducted airstrikes on Hezbollah military targets in southern Lebanon, including infrastructure and operational sites, contributing to at least 70 Hezbollah fighters killed by Israeli fire up to that point.[39][38] These exchanges remained largely confined to border areas, with Hezbollah aiming to divert Israeli resources from Gaza while avoiding broader escalation, though Israeli officials warned of potential wider operations if attacks persisted. Israeli targeted strikes in November focused on Hezbollah's Radwan Force and launch sites, such as an airstrike on a house in Beit Yahoun that killed four elite brigade members.[30] Another strike between Aynata and Aitaroun on November 5 killed three Lebanese girls, attributed by Israel to proximity to Hezbollah activity, though Lebanese sources disputed this.[3] Hezbollah claimed ongoing operations against Israeli troops in areas like Maroun el Ras and Odeisseh, using rockets, missiles, and drones between November 2 and 19, prompting IDF artillery and air responses that destroyed military posts.[40] The pattern reflected Hezbollah's strategy of low-intensity pressure, with over a dozen daily incidents by mid-month, met by Israel's precision counterstrikes to neutralize immediate threats without ground incursions. December 2023 saw continued tit-for-tat actions, with Hezbollah claiming five border attacks on December 1, followed by IDF shelling of Hula village that killed two Lebanese civilians—a woman and her son—and one Hezbollah fighter.[41] On December 2, Hezbollah fired rockets at Israeli army posts, eliciting Israeli airstrikes on over 20 targets in Lebanon, including Hezbollah infrastructure.[42] Exchanges peaked around December 8, when a Hezbollah drone strike killed one Israeli civilian in an orchard near the border, prompting Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu to threaten transformation of Beirut and southern Lebanon into "Gaza and Khan Younis" if attacks continued.[43] Hezbollah responded with drones and missiles on December 10, wounding six Israeli soldiers the next day, after which the IDF struck a Hezbollah cell in southern Lebanon.[44][45] Targeted IDF operations emphasized degradation of Hezbollah's capabilities, including strikes on command posts and weapon storage, as seen in responses to specific launches, while avoiding deep penetration to limit civilian involvement. Hezbollah's attacks caused limited Israeli casualties—primarily injuries and the one civilian death—but displaced thousands near the border, with Israel's responses killing dozens of fighters and some non-combatants amid dense Hezbollah embedding in villages.[45] These months marked a stabilization in intensity compared to October, driven by mutual deterrence, though underlying tensions tied to Gaza persisted.[46]

Hezbollah's offensive operations

Rocket, drone, and ground incursions

Hezbollah initiated its offensive with rocket and artillery barrages on October 8, 2023, firing multiple projectiles toward Israeli military positions in the Shebaa Farms area, framing the attacks as support for Hamas following the prior day's assault on Israel.[47] These launches escalated into near-daily volleys, with Hezbollah firing thousands of rockets, mortars, and anti-tank guided missiles (ATGMs) at northern Israeli communities and IDF outposts through December 2023, totaling approximately 6,000 projectiles in that period.[48] ATGMs, launched from ground positions across the border, struck civilian structures, including homes in towns like Metula and Margaliot, causing damage and necessitating evacuations of over 60,000 Israeli residents from border areas.[49][50] Complementing rocket fire, Hezbollah deployed explosive drones in incursions targeting IDF sites and occasionally civilian areas, with at least three such attacks recorded in October 2023 alone.[51] Drone operations intensified later in the year; on December 10, 2023, a Hezbollah one-way attack drone struck near an IDF position in northern Israel, wounding six soldiers.[45][51] These UAVs, often launched from southern Lebanon, aimed to exploit gaps in air defenses for precision strikes, though many were intercepted or failed to reach targets, prompting Israeli countermeasures against drone infrastructure.[45] Ground incursions by Hezbollah remained limited to small-scale probes rather than large maneuvers, involving operatives attempting to cross or sabotage the border fence with explosives or small arms. On October 17, 2023, IDF troops killed four Hezbollah fighters infiltrating northern Israel to plant a bomb near the Lebanon border, amid concurrent ATGM fire.[50] Similar attempts occurred sporadically, such as a suspected militant incursion near Hanita in early December 2023, but these were repelled without territorial gains, serving mainly to test Israeli border security alongside standoff weapons.[52] Overall, such actions comprised a minor fraction of Hezbollah's operations compared to aerial and rocket threats, reflecting a strategy of calibrated escalation to avoid full invasion while maintaining pressure.[24]

Strategic aims tied to support for Hamas and Iran

Hezbollah's cross-border attacks commencing on October 8, 2023, were framed by its leadership as a direct response to the Hamas-led assault on Israel on October 7, with the explicit objective of solidarity and mutual reinforcement among Iran's "Axis of Resistance" proxies. Hezbollah Secretary-General Hassan Nasrallah articulated in a November 3, 2023, speech that the group's operations sought to impose a "strategic burden" on Israel by opening a northern front, thereby diverting Israeli military resources from Gaza and hindering a decisive campaign against Hamas. This approach aimed to sustain Hamas's survival, preserving a key partner in the broader anti-Israel alliance and preventing Israel's potential eradication of the Gaza-based group, which could embolden further Israeli actions against Hezbollah's southern Lebanon positions.[53][54] The strategic calculus extended to bolstering Hezbollah's alignment with Iran, its primary financier and arms supplier, which provides an estimated annual support of $700 million and advanced weaponry enabling sustained rocket barrages. Iran's doctrine of proxy deterrence relies on Hezbollah to encircle Israel from the north, complementing Hamas in the south and other militias in Syria and Iraq, to impose multi-front attrition without risking direct Tehran involvement that could provoke U.S. retaliation. Captured Hamas documents from 2023 reveal discussions among Iranian, Hezbollah, and Hamas leaders on synchronized attacks to overwhelm Israeli defenses and derail regional normalization efforts, such as Saudi-Israeli ties, aligning Hezbollah's actions with Tehran's goal of regional hegemony through asymmetric warfare.[55][56][57] Hezbollah's support for Hamas also served domestic and regional aims, reinforcing its image as Lebanon's defender against Israel while justifying Iranian funding amid economic collapse, though Nasrallah denied prior knowledge of October 7 specifics, claiming the operation as "100% Palestinian" to mitigate escalation risks. By maintaining calibrated daily exchanges—over 5,000 rockets fired by mid-2024 without full invasion—Hezbollah sought to signal resolve, deter Israeli preemption, and extract concessions like Gaza ceasefires, while preserving its 150,000-rocket arsenal for potential decisive confrontation. This restraint reflected awareness that uncontrolled escalation could invite Israeli invasion, undermining Iran's proxy network and Hezbollah's Lebanese power base.[58][59]

Israel's counteroperations

Artillery shelling, airstrikes, and border security measures

Israel's artillery responses targeted Hezbollah rocket launch sites, observation posts, and military positions in southern Lebanon following the group's initial attacks on October 8, 2023.[60] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) fired approximately 10,000 artillery shells toward Hezbollah targets by late November 2023 as part of broader defensive operations along the northern border.[61] Specific instances included shelling three Hezbollah posts on October 10 after 15 rockets were launched toward the Western Galilee, and artillery fire on November 6 in response to about 30 launches detected from Lebanon.[60][62] On December 1, IDF shelling struck two sites near the border town of Houla, resulting in three deaths, including one Hezbollah fighter.[63] Airstrikes complemented artillery by focusing on Hezbollah infrastructure such as command centers, weapon storage, and launch preparations.[64] These operations, initiated immediately after Hezbollah's cross-border fire began, aimed to degrade the group's ability to sustain attacks without escalating to full invasion. By early November 2023, Israeli strikes had reportedly killed at least 48 Hezbollah members, alongside four civilians according to one assessment, though militant casualty figures from non-Israeli sources warrant scrutiny due to potential underreporting of combatant status.[65] To enhance border security, the IDF ordered evacuations of civilian communities proximate to the Lebanon frontier starting mid-October 2023, prioritizing areas vulnerable to Hezbollah's anti-tank missiles and rockets. On October 16, 28 communities were evacuated, followed by expansion to 14 additional ones on October 22, with Kiryat Shmona—home to over 20,000 residents—cleared by October 20 amid ongoing threats.[66][67][68] These measures displaced tens of thousands to prevent civilian casualties, while IDF reinforcements—including tanks, armored personnel carriers, and additional troops—were deployed along the border to deter incursions and monitor Hezbollah movements.[69][70] No large-scale ground fortifications were constructed in 2023, with emphasis instead on rapid mobilization and targeted interdiction to maintain a defensive posture.

Precision targeting of Hezbollah infrastructure

Israel utilized precision-guided munitions in airstrikes conducted by the Israeli Air Force to target Hezbollah's military infrastructure in southern Lebanon, focusing on sites directly involved in attacks against Israel. These operations emphasized intelligence-driven selection of targets such as observation posts, command buildings, and weapons storage facilities to degrade Hezbollah's capacity for cross-border assaults while limiting escalation.[44][71] Following Hezbollah's rocket and artillery fire starting October 8, 2023, the IDF carried out targeted strikes on military structures used by the group's operatives, including positions in villages like Aita al-Shaab and Kfarkela. For instance, on October 13, 2023, Israeli jets struck Hezbollah military sites in response to coordinated attacks, destroying infrastructure linked to the group's Radwan Unit. Such precision efforts reportedly neutralized launch sites immediately after firings, with the IDF claiming to have hit dozens of targets by late October to counter daily threats.[37] In November and December 2023, as exchanges intensified, Israel expanded precision operations to include underground facilities and logistics nodes, striking over 200 Hezbollah-associated sites according to military assessments. These actions targeted anti-tank guided missile positions and drone launch points, which Hezbollah employed in attempts to infiltrate or strike Israeli border communities. The approach relied on real-time intelligence from drones and satellites to ensure strikes hit verified military assets, contrasting with Hezbollah's less discriminate rocket volleys.[55][46] Casualty figures from these strikes remained low relative to later phases, with the IDF reporting primarily combatant losses and minimal civilian impact due to the targeted nature, though Hezbollah and Lebanese authorities contested some claims of collateral damage. This strategy reflected Israel's doctrine of proactive defense, aiming to impose costs on Hezbollah's operational infrastructure without provoking a full-scale northern front war during the ongoing Gaza operations.[44]

Casualties and damage during 2023

Losses on the Lebanese side

During the 2023 exchanges, Hezbollah acknowledged the deaths of approximately 80 to 100 of its fighters in clashes with Israeli forces along the border, primarily from artillery shelling, drone strikes, and targeted operations.[72][41] By early December, reports indicated at least 80 Hezbollah operatives killed since October 8, with the group publicly confirming losses through announcements of "martyrs" in border engagements.[41] Israeli military statements corroborated higher figures for neutralized Hezbollah personnel, attributing most to precision strikes on militant positions embedded near civilian areas.[34] Civilian casualties on the Lebanese side remained relatively low compared to subsequent escalation, with Lebanese authorities reporting around 20 deaths attributed to Israeli shelling and airstrikes by year's end.[73] Early incidents included four civilian fatalities from shelling by late October, often in border villages like Alma al-Sha'ab where Hezbollah operated.[73] Additional deaths occurred in December, such as three from shelling near Aitaroun and two relatives in a Bint Jbeil strike, highlighting risks from proximity to militant activities.[41][74] The Lebanese Health Ministry's figures, while comprehensive for reported incidents, have been critiqued for potential undercounting of combatant deaths or conflation in Hezbollah-influenced areas.[41] Material damage was concentrated in southern border villages, with Israeli artillery and airstrikes affecting homes, agricultural land, and minor infrastructure. Reports documented damage to about 10 houses in Marwahin from October shelling, alongside injuries and fires from white phosphorus use in areas like Dhayra.[32][73] Hezbollah positions, including observation posts and weapon caches, bore the brunt, with limited broader impact such as scorched fields and disrupted local roads, displacing thousands temporarily but not on the scale of later operations.[73][34] No major urban or national infrastructure, like power grids or hospitals, sustained significant hits in 2023, reflecting the contained nature of the shelling exchanges.[32]

Impacts on Israeli civilians and military

Hezbollah's rocket, missile, and anti-tank guided missile attacks from October to December 2023 resulted in the deaths of four Israeli civilians in northern Israel. Specific incidents included the killing of one civilian in Kibbutz Yiftah by anti-tank missiles on an unspecified date in October and another in Shtula on October 15 from five anti-tank missiles, which also injured three others. These attacks prompted the Israeli government to order evacuations of border communities, displacing tens of thousands of residents by late October, with additional villages evacuated on October 22 amid escalating threats.[49] Property damage occurred in several northern communities, including direct hits on homes and infrastructure in areas like Metula, where shelling began shortly after October 8.[75] The attacks disrupted daily life, closing schools, halting economic activity, and forcing residents into shelters or relocation, with broader effects on agriculture and tourism in the Galilee region.[76] On the military front, nine Israel Defense Forces (IDF) soldiers were killed during the same period, primarily from Hezbollah anti-tank missile strikes on border positions and vehicles during cross-border exchanges. These losses necessitated heightened border patrols, artillery countermeasures, and resource allocation to defend against incursions and barrages, though most rockets were intercepted by air defenses.[77] The IDF reported ongoing threats from ground infiltrations and precision weapons, contributing to operational strains without large-scale ground engagements in 2023.[78]

Violations of ceasefire agreements

Hezbollah's breaches of UNSC Resolution 1701

Hezbollah maintained a substantial armed presence south of the Litani River throughout 2023, including military infrastructure such as observation posts, rocket launchers, and command centers, in direct contravention of UN Security Council Resolution 1701's paragraph 8, which prohibits any armed personnel or weapons in the area beyond those of the Lebanese Armed Forces and UNIFIL.[79] This ongoing violation predated the year's escalation but facilitated Hezbollah's operational capabilities in the region, as evidenced by UNIFIL reports noting persistent restrictions imposed by the group on peacekeeping patrols, impeding verification efforts.[80] On October 8, 2023, one day after the Hamas attacks on Israel, Hezbollah initiated cross-border assaults by firing rockets and artillery shells at Israeli military positions from southern Lebanese territory, breaching the resolution's paragraph 1 mandate for a full cessation of hostilities and paragraph 12's prohibition on offensive military activities by non-state actors.[23][81] These launches originated from sites south of the Litani River, underscoring the group's non-compliance with deployment restrictions and enabling repeated strikes that numbered in the hundreds during the final quarter of 2023.[3] Subsequent attacks incorporated anti-tank guided missiles, drones, and further rocket barrages, with Hezbollah claiming over 1,000 operations by late 2023 in purported solidarity with Hamas, each constituting additional violations by employing prohibited weaponry and conducting military operations from the restricted zone.[82] UNIFIL documented these exchanges as infringing on Resolution 1701, though the force's ability to monitor and report was hampered by Hezbollah's control over access and information in southern Lebanon.[83] The group's actions also involved drone incursions and attempts to infiltrate Israeli territory, further defying the resolution's framework for demilitarization and stability.[84]

Israel's responses in context of self-defense

Israel's military responses to Hezbollah's cross-border shelling and rocket attacks in 2023 were conducted under the framework of self-defense, as articulated by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF), to neutralize immediate threats to Israeli territory, military personnel, and civilians following Hezbollah's initiation of hostilities on October 8, 2023.[18] This date marked Hezbollah's first mortar attack targeting IDF positions in the Mount Dov region, prompting retaliatory artillery fire on Hezbollah launch sites in Ayta ash-Shab and a drone strike that destroyed a Hezbollah observation tent used for surveillance near the border.[28] The IDF described these actions as precise measures to counter the risk of escalation into a broader northern front, urging residents in northern Israel to remain near bomb shelters amid heightened alerts.[85] Throughout October to December 2023, Hezbollah launched hundreds of rockets, missiles, and anti-tank guided munitions toward Israeli communities and positions, displacing over 60,000 Israeli civilians from border areas and necessitating ongoing evacuations.[24] In response, Israel employed artillery barrages and airstrikes targeting Hezbollah military infrastructure, such as launch pads and command posts south of the Litani River, in line with UN Security Council Resolution 1701's prohibition on armed non-state actors in that zone.[86] For instance, on October 17, 2023, after Hezbollah fired anti-tank missiles at the Israeli village of Zar'it, the IDF returned artillery fire to suppress the source, preventing further incursions without ground penetration.[87] These operations were calibrated to degrade Hezbollah's operational capacity while avoiding disproportionate escalation, with IDF officials emphasizing the necessity of restoring deterrence against attacks that violated the 2006 ceasefire and threatened Israeli sovereignty.[18] The legal basis for these responses rested on Article 51 of the UN Charter, permitting force in self-defense against armed attacks until the Security Council takes measures to maintain peace, a position echoed in U.S. advocacy for recognizing Israel's defensive rights amid Iranian-backed threats.[88] Unlike Hezbollah's indiscriminate projectiles aimed at civilian areas, Israeli strikes focused on verified militant targets, as confirmed by post-strike assessments showing minimal collateral damage in initial exchanges.[30] By late 2023, such measures had limited Hezbollah's ability to sustain high-volume fire from Lebanese soil, though sporadic violations persisted, underscoring the defensive imperative to protect Israel's northern frontier amid the concurrent Hamas conflict.[22]

International reactions

Support from the United States and Western allies

The United States provided diplomatic and military support to Israel amid the Hezbollah-initiated shellings that began on October 8, 2023, following the Hamas attacks the previous day. The Biden administration publicly affirmed Israel's right to defend itself against Hezbollah's rocket and artillery barrages from southern Lebanon, which targeted northern Israeli communities and displaced over 60,000 residents.[89] To deter further escalation by Hezbollah and its Iranian backers, the U.S. Navy deployed the USS Gerald R. Ford carrier strike group to the eastern Mediterranean on October 9, 2023, enhancing Israel's defensive posture with advanced air and missile capabilities.[90] Additional U.S. carrier groups followed, positioning naval assets to contain the conflict and signal resolve against proxy threats.[91] U.S. envoy Amos Hochstein engaged in shuttle diplomacy from October 2023 onward, pressing Lebanon to restrain Hezbollah and enforce UN Security Council Resolution 1701, which prohibits armed groups south of the Litani River, while conveying to Lebanese officials that Washington could not restrain Israeli operations if attacks persisted.[92] The administration also accelerated military assistance, approving emergency aid packages that included interceptors for systems like Iron Dome, proven effective against Hezbollah's short-range rockets during the 2023 exchanges.[93] This support aligned with broader post-October 7 commitments, totaling over $14 billion in proposed security aid to bolster Israel's defenses on multiple fronts. Western allies, including the United Kingdom, France, and Germany, issued statements endorsing Israel's right to counter Hezbollah's violations of the 2006 ceasefire framework. On October 23, 2023, leaders from the U.S., UK, France, and Germany jointly emphasized solidarity with Israel's self-defense efforts amid cross-border fire into Lebanon.[94] The UK shared intelligence on Hezbollah threats and advocated for Israel's security in international forums, while France and Germany, despite later calls for restraint, initially condemned Hezbollah's solidarity attacks with Hamas as destabilizing. G7 nations, including these allies, reiterated in October 2023 communiqués that Israel faced legitimate threats from Iranian proxies like Hezbollah, warranting proportionate responses to restore deterrence along the border.[95] This collective stance prioritized de-escalation through diplomacy but upheld Israel's operational freedom against non-state actors breaching international norms.

Responses from Iran, Arab states, and the United Nations

Iran, Hezbollah's primary financial and military patron, responded to the cross-border shellings by affirming support for the group's attacks on northern Israel, framing them as solidarity with Hamas following the October 7, 2023, assault. Iranian officials, including Foreign Minister Hossein Amir-Abdollahian, coordinated directly with Hezbollah leadership during visits to Beirut in October 2023, emphasizing Tehran's commitment to the "axis of resistance" against Israel. As Israeli counterstrikes targeted Hezbollah positions, Iran issued warnings of severe repercussions for any existential threats to its proxy, signaling potential direct involvement to deter further escalation, though Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei avoided explicit promises of immediate retaliation in initial statements.[55][96] Arab states displayed divided reactions, with public condemnations of Israeli airstrikes predominating amid domestic pressures, despite some governments' covert normalization efforts with Israel via the Abraham Accords. Gulf Cooperation Council members like Kuwait and Oman denounced Israel's operations as "brutal aggression" and violations of Lebanese sovereignty, urging restraint to prevent wider war. Egypt, Jordan, and the Arab League collectively demanded an immediate halt to Israeli assaults, expressing fears of refugee influxes and regional destabilization, while avoiding outright endorsement of Hezbollah's rocket barrages; Saudi Arabia and the UAE maintained relative silence publicly but prioritized containing Iranian influence over open support for escalation. These responses reflected a tension between official anti-Israel rhetoric—often amplified by state media—and pragmatic concerns over internal stability and countering Hezbollah's expansion.[97][98] The United Nations called for de-escalation and adherence to UN Security Council Resolution 1701, which mandates Hezbollah's disarmament and withdrawal south of the Litani River—a provision repeatedly violated by the group's presence and attacks since 2006. UNIFIL peacekeepers reported over 100 cross-border incidents by late October 2023, urging both sides to halt fire and protect civilians, while Security Council documents noted Hezbollah's initiation of hostilities on October 8, 2023, with rockets targeting Israeli communities. UN human rights experts expressed alarm at civilian casualties and infrastructure damage, condemning the escalation and specific Israeli strikes near UN positions, though investigations often highlighted disproportionate impacts without equivalent scrutiny of Hezbollah's deliberate targeting of civilians or use of human shields. Secretary-General António Guterres emphasized Lebanon's sovereignty and the need for the Lebanese Armed Forces to assert control, but UN statements frequently equated aggressor and defender, reflecting institutional tendencies to prioritize ceasefires over enforcing prior resolutions against non-state actors like Hezbollah.[86][99][100]

Domestic political dynamics

Reactions within Lebanon and Hezbollah's role

Hezbollah assumed a central role in the 2023 Israel–Lebanon shellings by launching the initial cross-border attacks on October 8, 2023, firing rockets and artillery into northern Israel in explicit solidarity with Hamas's October 7 assault, thereby escalating tensions along the Blue Line demarcation.[47] These operations, framed by Hezbollah as defensive support for Palestinians in Gaza, involved daily or near-daily launches from southern Lebanon throughout late 2023, amassing hundreds of projectiles and positioning the group as the dominant non-state actor initiating violations from Lebanese soil, in breach of UN Security Council Resolution 1701's prohibition on armed presence south of the Litani River.[81] [3] Hezbollah's leadership, under Secretary-General Hassan Nasrallah, justified the shellings as calibrated pressure on Israel to halt its Gaza operations, while avoiding full-scale invasion to prevent devastating Israeli retaliation, though this restraint did not mitigate Lebanese civilian displacement or infrastructure risks.[101] Domestic reactions in Lebanon revealed sharp sectarian divides, with robust backing from Shiite communities—93% of whom held positive views of Hezbollah—but limited enthusiasm elsewhere amid economic collapse and war fatigue.[102] A late October 2023 poll showed 80% of respondents approving Hamas's October 7 attacks, yet only 32% favored Hezbollah opening a northern front, highlighting reluctance to entangle Lebanon in broader conflict despite anti-Israel sentiment.[103] By early 2024, amid sustained exchanges, a slim majority expressed desire for Hezbollah to halt attacks on Israel, reflecting fears of Israeli ground incursions and further devastation in a nation already hosting displaced populations and facing service breakdowns.[104] Lebanese political leaders, lacking leverage over Hezbollah's autonomous command structure, repeatedly implored restraint to avert national ruin, with figures across Sunni, Christian, and Druze factions warning that escalation imperiled sovereignty and recovery.[105] Caretaker Prime Minister Najib Mikati's government condemned provocations risking broader war and reaffirmed commitment to Resolution 1701, but its paralysis—stemming from Hezbollah's veto power in cabinet and the absence of a president—rendered enforcement symbolic.[106] Opposition voices, including from the Lebanese Forces and Progressive Socialist Party, criticized Hezbollah's actions as subordinating Lebanese interests to Iranian directives, fueling protests in Beirut and calls for state monopoly on arms, though these faced suppression in Hezbollah strongholds.[107] Only about one-third of Lebanese broadly endorsed Hezbollah's regional military engagements, underscoring its isolation from national consensus on security priorities.[101]

Israeli government and public responses

The Israeli government authorized immediate defensive measures by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) following Hezbollah's rocket and artillery attacks commencing on October 8, 2023, including artillery barrages, tank fire, and precision drone strikes on Hezbollah observation posts and launch sites in southern Lebanon.[18][1] These actions aimed to neutralize immediate threats while avoiding broader escalation, with the IDF reporting the destruction of multiple Hezbollah targets in the initial exchanges.[20] To safeguard civilians amid daily cross-border fire, the government expanded evacuation orders for northern communities, beginning with voluntary departures and escalating to mandatory ones; on October 20, 2023, over 20,000 residents were evacuated from Kiryat Shmona, the largest city near the border, with additional orders covering 14 more villages by October 22, resulting in roughly 60,000 Israelis displaced from their homes by late 2023.[68][67] Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu publicly affirmed Israel's commitment to restoring security for these evacuees, warning on December 8, 2023—after a Hezbollah attack killed a civilian in the north—that continued aggression would invite consequences comparable to those in Gaza, underscoring the government's stance on deterrence and enforcement of UN Security Council Resolution 1701.[43][3] Public sentiment reflected widespread anxiety over the rocket barrages and displacement but strong backing for the government's defensive posture, with polls among Jewish Israelis showing over 60% support for aggressive measures to counter Hezbollah and enable residents' safe return.[108] No significant protests targeted the handling of the northern front in 2023; instead, national focus emphasized unity and resilience, bolstered by the formation of a broad war cabinet including opposition figures to coordinate responses across fronts.[109]

Strategic assessments and controversies

Debates on escalation risks and deterrence

Analysts assessed the cross-border exchanges beginning October 8, 2023, as a tense deterrence equilibrium, where Hezbollah's limited rocket and artillery fire—totaling hundreds of projectiles in the initial weeks—aimed to signal solidarity with Hamas without provoking a full Israeli invasion, while Israel's airstrikes targeted launch sites and commanders to impose costs and signal resolve.[24] This dynamic reflected mutual deterrence: Hezbollah refrained from unleashing its estimated 150,000-rocket arsenal, which could overwhelm Israeli defenses, due to fears of devastating retaliation amid Israel's Gaza operations; conversely, Israel avoided ground incursions into Lebanon to prevent a two-front war that could strain resources and invite Iranian intervention.[110] [109] Debates centered on the effectiveness of Israel's "campaign between wars" approach, adapted to punitive strikes rather than preemptive operations, with proponents arguing it successfully contained Hezbollah's involvement—evidenced by the group's attacks remaining confined to border skirmishes, displacing over 60,000 Israelis but averting mass southern Lebanon mobilization.[109] Critics within Israel's defense establishment contended that measured responses failed to restore post-October 7 deterrence, emboldening Hezbollah's near-daily shelling and eroding public confidence, as the persistent threat forced prolonged evacuations without eliminating the northern risk.[109] [111] These views highlighted a causal tension: limited escalation preserved short-term stability but risked normalizing low-intensity conflict, potentially lowering thresholds for miscalculation, such as errant strikes hitting civilians or infrastructure. Escalation risks were framed around sequential dynamics, where Hezbollah's deepening involvement—expanding from artillery to drones targeting deeper into Israel by November 2023—could spiral if Israel neared dismantling Hamas, threatening Hezbollah's role as Iran's forward deterrent against nuclear site strikes.[110] U.S. deployments, including an aircraft carrier and additional forces in late October 2023, aimed to bolster Israeli deterrence by signaling allied commitment, while urging restraint to avoid a regional conflagration exceeding the 2006 war's toll (over 1,200 Lebanese and 160 Israeli deaths).[110] [112] Some experts warned of inadvertent escalation from operational fatigue or political pressures, with Israel's right-leaning leadership debating preemptive options against Hezbollah buildup, though Prime Minister Netanyahu prioritized Gaza focus.[110] This calculus underscored deterrence's fragility: Hezbollah's operational autonomy from Iran allowed calibrated pressure, but any perceived existential threat to its capabilities could shatter the equilibrium.[110]

Criticisms of Hezbollah tactics versus Israeli proportionality

Hezbollah's rocket attacks on Israel since October 8, 2023, have been characterized as indiscriminate by human rights organizations, with unguided projectiles launched toward populated areas, resulting in civilian casualties and violating international humanitarian law principles that prohibit attacks not directed at military objectives.[113] [114] By September 2024, Hezbollah had fired over 8,000 rockets, anti-tank missiles, and other projectiles into northern Israel, displacing approximately 60,000 Israeli civilians and turning border communities into uninhabitable zones.[27] [115] These barrages, often conducted in volleys of dozens or hundreds, targeted civilian infrastructure and residential areas, with documented instances in October 2024 killing eight Israeli civilians.[113] [116] Critics, including Lebanese public figures and analysts, have condemned Hezbollah's tactic of launching rockets from densely populated civilian areas in southern Lebanon, such as villages and near schools, mosques, and gas stations, effectively using Lebanese civilians as human shields to deter Israeli counterstrikes.[117] [118] [119] Israeli military assessments indicate that a majority of recent Hezbollah launches originated from such sites, including positions adjacent to UNIFIL outposts and Lebanese Armed Forces compounds, exacerbating risks to non-combatants and complicating international monitoring efforts.[120] [121] This embedding of military assets in civilian zones has been cited as a primary cause of collateral damage during Israeli responses, as strikes on launchers and storage sites inevitably affect surrounding structures.[118] In response, Israel has conducted precision airstrikes targeting Hezbollah's military infrastructure, including rocket launchers, command centers, and personnel, with analyses from military legal experts affirming that these operations adhere to international law requirements for distinction and necessity by prioritizing intelligence-driven, low-collateral targeting protocols.[122] However, organizations like Amnesty International have accused Israel of disproportionate force in southern Lebanon, pointing to extensive destruction of civilian homes and infrastructure—estimated at thousands of buildings damaged or destroyed by August 2025—as exceeding military necessity, though such critiques often overlook Hezbollah's deliberate placement of assets amid populations.[40] Pro-Israel policy analyses counter that the scale of Hezbollah's arsenal and firing rate—averaging daily attacks for over a year—justifies escalated measures to neutralize the existential threat posed by an estimated 150,000 rockets capable of striking major Israeli cities, arguing that proportionality must account for the aggressor's tactics and the defender's right to eliminate ongoing capabilities rather than merely tit-for-tat exchanges.[24] These debates highlight tensions in applying proportionality under international law, where Hezbollah's initiation of cross-border fire post-October 7, 2023, in solidarity with Hamas frames Israel's actions as defensive deterrence rather than escalation, despite resulting Lebanese civilian displacements exceeding 1 million by late 2024.[123] [124] Note that reports from groups like Amnesty, while documenting destruction, have faced scrutiny for inconsistent application of standards, frequently emphasizing Israeli actions over Hezbollah's violations in comparable contexts.[125]
