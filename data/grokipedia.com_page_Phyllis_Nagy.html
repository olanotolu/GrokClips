Phyllis Nagy


Phyllis Nagy (born November 7, 1962) is an American screenwriter, playwright, and film director recognized for her adaptation of Patricia Highsmith's novel The Price of Salt into the 2015 film Carol, which earned her an Academy Award nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay.[1][2][3] Born in New York City, Nagy began her career in theater at the Royal Court in London after relocating to England in 1992, where she wrote plays including The Strip and Never Land.[1][4] Her directorial debut, the HBO film Mrs. Harris (2005), received 12 Primetime Emmy nominations, including for Outstanding Directing and Writing.[5][3] Nagy later directed Call Jane (2022), a drama about pre-Roe v. Wade abortion access, and serves as professor and head of screenwriting at UCLA's School of Theater, Film and Television.[2][3]

Early Life and Education

Upbringing in New York

Phyllis Nagy was born on November 7, 1962, in New York City to Peter Thomas Nagy and Virginia Marie Sottile Nagy.[1][6] She was raised in the city, experiencing its cultural environment during her formative years.[5] In later reflections, Nagy described growing up and coming of age as a lesbian in New York City, noting the contrasts with more insular settings elsewhere.[7] She remained based there until 1992, when she moved to London at age 29.[1]

Academic and Early Artistic Training

Phyllis Nagy pursued her undergraduate education at New York University, earning a Bachelor of Fine Arts degree in 1986.[1] Her training at NYU's Tisch School of the Arts focused on the performing arts, laying the groundwork for her subsequent work in playwriting, directing, and screenwriting.[8] This formal academic preparation equipped her with foundational skills in dramatic writing and theater production, which she applied in her early professional endeavors following graduation.[1] No additional specialized artistic apprenticeships or postgraduate studies are documented in available biographical records prior to her move to London in 1992.[9]

Theater Career

Entry into British Theater and Royal Court Residency

Nagy relocated to London in 1992, initiating her sustained engagement with British theater at the Royal Court Theatre under the artistic directorship of Stephen Daldry.[6] [10] Her play Weldon Rising, which dramatizes the final days of author Fay Weldon, premiered at the Royal Court Upstairs on December 3, 1992, in a production co-presented with the Liverpool Playhouse.[11] [1] This debut marked her emergence as a distinctive voice in contemporary playwriting, blending American influences with themes of identity and obsession.[12] In the mid-1990s, Nagy was appointed writer-in-residence at the Royal Court, a position that facilitated the development and staging of multiple works during Daldry's leadership, which emphasized innovative and politically engaged drama.[2] [5] By late 1996, she held this residency, enabling focused script refinement amid the theater's commitment to emerging international talent.[13] During this period, Disappearedâ€”directed by Nagy herselfâ€”premiered in 1995, touring the UK before a London run at the Royal Court, followed by The Strip the same year, both exploring psychological disintegration and moral ambiguity.[6] [2] The residency extended her influence, culminating in the 1998 premiere of Never Land at the Royal Court, further solidifying her role in nurturing bold, character-driven narratives within London's subsidized theater ecosystem.[2] [14] This phase contrasted with the more commercial orientations of West End venues, positioning the Royal Court as a hub for Nagy's experimental style amid a landscape favoring established British voices.[15]

Key Playwriting and Directing Works

Phyllis Nagy's playwriting career gained prominence in the early 1990s with works premiered at the Royal Court Theatre and affiliated venues. Weldon Rising, her debut major production, premiered at the Liverpool Playhouse in association with the Royal Court before transferring to the Royal Court Theatre in London in 1992.[1] The play dramatizes a confrontation in New York's meatpacking district amid escalating tensions from gay bashing on a sweltering night, drawing on themes of violence and identity.[16] Butterfly Kiss, first produced in 1994, explores the matricide committed by protagonist Lily Ross through fragmented memories blending past, present, and imagined futures, as her lover Martha probes the underlying motives from a prison cell.[17][18] Subsequent plays continued to showcase Nagy's interest in psychological depth and non-linear narratives. Disappeared, written in 1991 and receiving its UK premiere in 1995, centers on Sarah Casey, a 25-year-old New York travel agent who vanishes after encountering the enigmatic Elston Rupp in a bar, weaving mystery with themes of escape and illusion.[19][20] Its Chicago premiere followed at Steppenwolf Theatre in 1999.[21] The Strip, premiered at the Royal Court in 1995, follows intersecting paths of a female impersonator, a repo man, and a journalist traversing America in pursuit of fame, fate, and elusive connections through a mosaic of controlled vignettes. Never Land, staged at the Royal Court in 1998, examines a French family's disintegrating final days as they fantasize about relocation to England.[6] Nagy also adapted literary works for the stage, including Nathaniel Hawthorne's The Scarlet Letter, which premiered in a non-naturalistic version at Denver Center Theatre in 1994, emphasizing obsession and retribution.[16] Her adaptation of Anton Chekhov's The Seagull featured a family gathering lakeside for a performance that upends their dynamics.[16] Additionally, she adapted Patricia Highsmith's The Talented Mr. Ripley, focusing on the protagonist's assumption of a murdered man's identity.[16] In directing, Nagy helmed notable theater productions, including her own adaptation of The Seagull at Chichester Festival Theatre in 2003.[6] She directed The Scarlet Letter in 2005, building on her earlier adaptation to explore its core themes of isolation and vengeance.[6] These efforts, often at prestigious venues like the Royal Court, underscored her dual role in shaping contemporary interpretations of classic and original texts.[22]

Screenwriting and Film Directing

Adaptation of "Carol" and Collaboration with Todd Haynes


Phyllis Nagy adapted Patricia Highsmith's 1952 novel The Price of Salt into the screenplay for the 2015 film Carol, directed by Todd Haynes. Nagy, who befriended Highsmith during her time in Switzerland, received encouragement from the author to adapt one of her books into a screenplay, leading her to select The Price of Salt for its unique hopeful tone amid Highsmith's typically darker narratives.[23] The project originated in the late 1990s, with Nagy completing early drafts by 1997, but faced prolonged development challenges, including attachments to directors such as Guillermo del Toro and Cate Blanchett before Haynes committed in 2013.[24] [13] Nagy's adaptation emphasized subtlety and subtext, preserving the novel's focus on restrained gestures and unspoken desires between the protagonists, Therese Belivet and Carol Aird, set against the repressive social norms of 1950s America. She altered certain narrative elements, such as restructuring the timeline to begin at the story's end and heightening dramatic tension through selective omissions, while maintaining fidelity to Highsmith's original prose style and character psychology.[25] [26] The screenplay's development culminated in principal photography in 2014, with the film premiering at the Cannes Film Festival on May 17, 2015, where it won the Queer Palm and earned Haynes the Best Actress award for Rooney Mara.[27] In collaborating with Todd Haynes, Nagy found a director whose stylistic restraint and period authenticity aligned with her vision, allowing for a swift integration of her script into production after years of revisions. Haynes, a longtime admirer of Highsmith, incorporated Nagy's drafts with minimal changes, praising her work for capturing the novel's elliptical dialogue and emotional undercurrents without overt exposition.[28] Their partnership, marked by mutual respect for source material integrity, resulted in Carol's critical acclaim, including six Academy Award nominations, among them Best Adapted Screenplay for Nagy, though it did not win.[29] The film grossed over $40 million worldwide on a $11.8 million budget, demonstrating the viability of Nagy's long-gestated adaptation.[5]

Direction of "Call Jane" and Other Film Projects

Nagy directed Call Jane (2022), her feature film debut, which dramatizes the activities of the Jane Collective, a clandestine Chicago-based group that facilitated over 11,000 illegal abortions between 1969 and 1973 amid restrictive laws.[30] The film centers on Joy (Elizabeth Banks), a suburban housewife whose health complications from an unwanted pregnancy lead her to join the network, portrayed with supporting roles by Sigourney Weaver as the group's leader and Kate Mara as a medical student recruit.[31] Co-written by Hayley Schore and Roshan Sethi, the screenplay draws from historical accounts while incorporating fictional elements to highlight personal agency and moral complexities in pre-Roe v. Wade America.[32] Nagy emphasized a non-preachy tone, prioritizing narrative accessibility to foster audience reflection on bodily autonomy rather than overt advocacy.[4] Call Jane premiered in the U.S. Dramatic Competition at the 2022 Sundance Film Festival on January 23, generating buzz for its timely release following the U.S. Supreme Court's Dobbs v. Jackson decision.[2] It screened in competition at the 72nd Berlin International Film Festival in February 2022, receiving praise for cinematography by Greta Zovula, who used Kodak film stocks to evoke the era's grainy realism while maintaining visual lightness.[33] The film was released theatrically in the United States on October 28, 2022, by Roadside Attractions and Vertical Entertainment, earning a 6.5/10 average rating on IMDb from over 7,000 users, with reviewers noting its blend of dark humor and tension.[31] Nagy's prior directing credit is the HBO television movie Mrs. Harris (2005), which she also wrote, depicting the 1980 murder trial of headmistress Jean Harris (Annette Bening) for killing her lover, cardiologist Herman Tarnower (Ben Chaplin).[5] The film garnered 12 Primetime Emmy nominations, including Outstanding Directing for a Miniseries, Movie or Dramatic Special and Outstanding Writing for a Miniseries, Movie or Dramatic Special, reflecting acclaim for its psychological depth and courtroom authenticity drawn from trial transcripts.[32] No additional feature or television directing projects have been completed or released as of 2025, though Nagy joined the Directors Guild of America in 2021 ahead of Call Jane's production.[32]

Industry and Guild Involvement

Writers Guild of America Leadership Challenges

In July 2019, Phyllis Nagy announced her candidacy for president of the Writers Guild of America West (WGA West), positioning herself as a challenger to incumbent president David A. Goodman amid escalating tensions over the guild's negotiations with talent agencies. Nagy, an Oscar-nominated screenwriter, criticized the WGA leadership's refusal to engage directly with the Association of Talent Agents (ATA), arguing that the strategy of mass agent firings and boycotts risked alienating allies and endangering writers' residuals and future bargaining power. She advocated for a "reasonable" return to the bargaining table to secure a new agency code, warning that the hardline approach could precipitate an industry-wide strike and undermine writers' primacy in television production.[34][35][36] Nagy led the "Writers Forward Together" opposition slate, which included Craig Mazin for vice president and Nick Jones Jr. for secretary-treasurer, garnering support from nearly 300 prominent writers, including Shonda Rhimes and Ava DuVernay, who signed a letter calling for leadership change to resolve the agency stalemate. In public statements and responses to guild executive director David Young, Nagy emphasized that agencies were not the enemy and that prolonged deadlock could harm writers more than agencies, particularly as packaging fees and other practices under scrutiny had persisted for years without resolution. Her campaign highlighted internal divisions, with Nagy responding to criticisms of her own professional history, such as her tenure heading UCLA's screenwriting program, by defending her record and urging focus on negotiation outcomes.[37][38][39] Despite these efforts, Nagy received 1,282 votes in the September 16, 2019, election, compared to Goodman's 4,395, allowing the incumbent leadership to retain control and continue the agency's standoff, which culminated in the WGA adopting a new code of conduct without ATA agreement, leading to widespread agent terminations. Nagy's bid underscored fractures within the guild over aggressive versus conciliatory tactics in labor disputes, though her warnings about residual risks and strike potential were not immediately realized in the 2019 agency context. The challenge reflected broader debates on guild strategy during a period of industry consolidation and streaming disruptions.[40][41][42]

Personal Life

Relationships and Identity

Phyllis Nagy is an openly lesbian woman who has publicly identified as such since coming of age in New York City during the late 1970s and early 1980s.[7][43] She has emphasized living authentically without apology, stating in a 2015 interview that she has always been out as a lesbian and views personal visibility as a form of commitment to LGBT rights.[43] Nagy's experiences as a lesbian informed her adaptation of The Price of Salt into the film Carol (2015), though she has described her approach as prioritizing narrative subtlety over explicit declarations of identity.[44] Public information on Nagy's romantic relationships remains limited, with no verified records of long-term partners or marriages disclosed in biographical sources or interviews.[45] She has maintained a degree of privacy regarding personal partnerships, focusing public discourse on her professional collaborations and artistic output rather than intimate details.[5] This reticence aligns with her broader emphasis on craft over personal exposition in creative work.[46]

Association with Patricia Highsmith

Phyllis Nagy met Patricia Highsmith in the late 1980s while employed as a fact-checker and researcher at The New York Times Magazine. Nagy, a fan of Highsmith's work, facilitated an assignment that involved touring Highsmith through a New York cemetery, sparking their initial personal interaction.[47][5] This encounter evolved into a sustained friendship, maintained through letters after Nagy relocated to London to pursue playwriting, while Highsmith resided in Switzerland.[46][48] The two women visited each other, with Nagy recounting a weekend at Highsmith's Swiss home marked by discussions over scotch, beer, and cigarettes, where Highsmith shared unfiltered opinions on literature, politics, and personal life.[47] Nagy has described Highsmith as a formative influence, altering her perspective on the possibilities for female writers to live autonomously and unapologetically, emphasizing Highsmith's self-reliant lifestyle amid personal and professional isolation.[4] Their correspondence and meetings provided Nagy with intimate insights into Highsmith's misanthropic tendencies, sharp wit, and disdain for conventional social norms, though Highsmith expressed ambivalence toward female companionship despite her own lesbian identity.[4][49] The friendship endured until Highsmith's death from lung cancer on February 4, 1995, at age 74 in Locarno, Switzerland.[7] Nagy later drew on this relationship in adapting Highsmith's 1952 novel The Price of Salt (republished as Carol) for the 2015 film, beginning the screenplay in 1997 with Highsmith's literary executor, though their prior bond informed her approach to capturing the author's understated emotional realism without romanticizing the characters.[26][25]

Reception and Critical Assessment

Accolades and Professional Recognition

Phyllis Nagy received National Endowment for the Arts fellowships in 1991 and 1993, as well as New York Foundation for the Arts fellowships, recognizing her early contributions to playwriting.[1] Her screen debut, the HBO film Mrs. Harris (2005), which she wrote and directed, earned her a Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Directing for a Miniseries, Movie or a Dramatic Special in 2006; the project received 12 Emmy nominations overall, including recognition for her screenplay.[50][4][2] Nagy's screenplay adaptation of Patricia Highsmith's The Price of Salt for the film Carol (2015) garnered significant professional acclaim, including a nomination for the Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay in 2016 and a BAFTA nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay.[51][52] She also received a nomination from the Writers Guild of America for Best Adapted Screenplay and won the British Screenwriters' Award for Outstanding Screenplay of a British Independent Film.[6][53] Critics' groups honored Nagy's Carol script with the New York Film Critics Circle Award for Best Screenplay in 2015, as well as the Seattle Film Critics Circle award for Best Adapted Screenplay.[54][2]

Analyses of Artistic Contributions

Phyllis Nagy's screenwriting, particularly in the 2015 adaptation of Patricia Highsmith's The Price of Salt for the film Carol, demonstrates a deliberate emphasis on visual subtext and restraint over explicit exposition. By altering the protagonist Therese Belivet's profession from aspiring set designer to photographer, Nagy amplifies the narrative's core theme of perception and mutual observation, aligning the character's gaze with the film's exploration of lesbian desire in 1950s America.[55] This choice facilitates a "two-way mirror" structure, transforming Highsmith's one-sided infatuation into reciprocal attraction through flashbacks and sparse dialogue that prioritizes glances and gestures.[56] Critics have noted how this approach contributes to the screenplay's emotional depth, evoking the "optics of eros" while maintaining fidelity to the source's psychological tension, albeit with modifications like a less tragic resolution to underscore midcentury queer resilience.[55] In Carol, Nagy's style favors classical restraint, using minimal verbal exchange to heighten the drama's visceral anxiety and "queer vernacular" of coded interactions, which distinguishes it from more overt depictions of romance.[56] Her 20-year development of the script involved honoring Highsmith's character dynamicsâ€”such as Carol's melancholyâ€”while adapting for cinematic flow, including substitutions like a Christmas card for the novel's gloves incident to sustain narrative momentum.[55] This method earned the screenplay an Academy Award nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay in 2016, with recognition from bodies like the New York Film Critics Circle for its precise handling of forbidden love's subtle mechanics.[2] Nagy's directorial debut in Call Jane (2022), based on the pre-Roe v. Wade Jane Collective's underground abortion network in Chicago, showcases her ability to integrate personal transformation with historical context, centering protagonist Joy's arc from passive housewife to empowered activist.[57] The film accurately portrays the collective's operations, performing over 12,000 procedures, and highlights women's solidarity against systemic barriers, bolstered by strong performances from Elizabeth Banks and Sigourney Weaver.[57] However, analyses critique Nagy's direction for an uneven tone that injects peppy elements and needle-drops, diluting the era's inherent urgency and resulting in a "low-stakes" feel despite the subject matter's gravity.[58] Subplots, such as those involving racial dynamics or secondary characters, occasionally distract from the core narrative, limiting the film's confrontational edge compared to more taut historical dramas.[57] Despite these flaws, Nagy's work underscores themes of female agency, rendering the story as a timely reflection on reproductive autonomy amid contemporary legal reversals.[58]

Criticisms and Debates Surrounding Works

Phyllis Nagy's theatrical works have elicited divided critical responses, particularly in London productions during the 1990s and early 2000s, where reviewers oscillated between praising their bold exploration of queer themes and female agency and dismissing them as overly opaque or self-indulgent. Plays such as Weldon Rising (1991) and The Strip (1995) were noted for their postmodern ambiguity and complex discourse, which some critics found challenging to parse, leading to characterizations of incomprehensibility that may reflect broader biases against women playwrights venturing into experimental forms.[12] [59] Academic analyses have argued that this skepticism underscores a resistance to Nagy's political engagement with identity and power dynamics, positioning her as a playwright whose relevance extends beyond initial receptions into ongoing debates on theatrical innovation.[12] In her directorial debut Call Jane (2022), which dramatizes the pre-Roe v. Wade Jane Collective's underground abortion network in 1969 Chicago, critics highlighted tonal and historical discrepancies despite its timely release amid U.S. reproductive rights debates. Reviewers contended that the film's brisk, upbeat portrayalâ€”featuring period-appropriate visuals and a sense of humorâ€”clashed with the gravity of illegal abortions, rendering the narrative jarringly optimistic and less revelatory than intended.[60] [57] Others criticized its fictionalized blend of real events, arguing that simplifications of the collective's operations and interpersonal dynamics undermined authenticity, with the screenplay prioritizing dramatic uplift over nuanced historical fidelity.[61] [62] [63] Nagy's screenplay for Carol (2015), adapting Patricia Highsmith's 1952 novel The Price of Salt, faced minimal substantive debate on adaptation choices, with most discourse affirming its fidelity to the source's emotional core amid 1950s repression, though some noted the script's deliberate silences and structural shifts as points of interpretive variance from the book's thriller elements.[64] Earlier works like the teleplay for Mrs. Harris (2005) drew scrutiny for interpretive liberties in depicting real-life figures, but such concerns remained niche compared to broader acclaim.[28]
