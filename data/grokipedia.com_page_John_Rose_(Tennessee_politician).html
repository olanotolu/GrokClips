John Rose (Tennessee politician)


John W. Rose (born February 23, 1965) is an American Republican politician, businessman, and farmer serving as the U.S. Representative for Tennessee's 6th congressional district since 2019.[1][2]
Raised in Cookeville, Tennessee, Rose earned a B.S. in agribusiness economics from Tennessee Technological University, an M.S. in agricultural economics from Purdue University, and a J.D. from Vanderbilt University before building a career owning Boson Software, LLC, a firm specializing in information technology training, and managing a family farm established over 230 years ago.[2][3]
Prior to Congress, he served as Tennessee's Commissioner of Agriculture from 2002 to 2003 and chaired the Tennessee State Fair Association for 12 years, during which he helped revive the century-old event after its abandonment in 2010.[2][4]
In the House, Rose holds seats on the Committee on Agriculture and the Committee on Financial Services, where he focuses on supporting rural development, small businesses, and reducing federal regulations and spending to address the national debt.[2][5]
His legislative priorities include advocating for farmers, lumber producers, and financial technology innovations, with bills advancing rural economic interests passing the House with broad bipartisan support.[5][6]
In March 2025, Rose announced his candidacy for Governor of Tennessee in the 2026 election, emphasizing his private-sector experience in business management.[7][8]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

John Williams Rose was born on February 23, 1965, in Cookeville, Putnam County, Tennessee.[9][3] He is an eighth-generation Tennessean, with his family tracing its roots in the state back to 1790.[10] Rose's family has maintained a farm in the Temperance Hall community of DeKalb County and the Lancaster community of Smith County for over 230 years, reflecting a multi-generational commitment to agriculture in rural Middle Tennessee.[2] His father, Jerry Rose, managed the farm, and the family emphasized preserving this legacy through hands-on involvement.[11][2] Rose was raised in Cookeville, where he began working on the family farm alongside his father during childhood, performing tasks such as fence-building that instilled early lessons in manual labor and farm operations.[2][3] This upbringing in a farming environment near Cookeville shaped his foundational experiences in rural Tennessee, prior to his formal education at Cookeville High School.[12][2]

Academic and early professional training

Rose graduated from Cookeville High School in Cookeville, Tennessee, as salutatorian in 1983.[9][13] He then earned a Bachelor of Science degree in agribusiness economics from Tennessee Technological University in Cookeville in 1988.[9][14] Following this, he obtained a Master of Science in agricultural economics from Purdue University in 1990.[9][13] Rose completed his formal academic training with a Juris Doctor from Vanderbilt University Law School in 1993.[9][2] After law school, Rose entered entrepreneurship by co-founding Transcender Corporation in 1994, a software company specializing in IT certification training tools, which provided practical experience in business management and technology development.[3] This venture marked his early professional shift from legal education toward applied business operations in the information technology sector, leveraging his agricultural economics background for enterprise growth.[3][2]

Pre-political career

Business enterprises

Prior to entering politics, John Rose built a career in the information technology sector, co-founding and leading companies focused on IT certification training and exam preparation software. In 1992, shortly after completing his education, Rose co-founded Transcender Corporation in Nashville, Tennessee, alongside a college friend; the company developed and provided online products for training IT professionals seeking industry certifications.[15][16] Transcender earned recognition as a fast-growing enterprise, receiving the Music City Future 50 Award for five consecutive years during the 1990s, and Rose served as its president.[3][17] The company was sold in October 2000 for $60 million, marking a significant financial success that underscored Rose's entrepreneurial acumen in the burgeoning IT training market.[18] Following the Transcender sale, Rose founded Boson Software, LLC, another Nashville-based firm specializing in IT certification exam simulation and practice software for professionals pursuing credentials from vendors like Cisco, Microsoft, and CompTIA.[19][20] As president and CEO, Rose has overseen Boson since its inception around 1999â€“2000, positioning it as a key player in the niche of realistic exam preparation tools that replicate actual certification testing environments.[21][22] These ventures established Rose as a small business owner with expertise in technology-driven education, employing dozens and serving global clients through digital platforms.[12]

Agricultural and nonprofit activities

John Rose descends from an eighth-generation farming family in the Temperance Hall and Lancaster communities of DeKalb and Smith counties, Tennessee, where he has maintained active involvement in agriculture as a lifelong farmer prior to entering politics.[12] His operations through Rose Farms TN focus on producing yellow corn primarily used as feed for livestock, including cattle, swine, and poultry, contributing to the regional agricultural economy.[23] Rose's early agricultural engagement included leadership in youth programs; he served as president of the Cookeville FFA chapter in 1982 and later as state FFA vice president, fostering skills in agribusiness that informed his later pursuits.[24] In nonprofit capacities, Rose held prominent roles in agricultural organizations, including as president of the Tennessee FFA Alumni Association from 1999 to 2000, secretary from 2004 to 2006, and council member from 1995 to 2006; he also chaired the Tennessee FFA Foundation's board overseeing scholarships and awards.[25] [26] As a voting member of the Tennessee Farm Bureau Federation, he supported rural advocacy efforts, reflecting his commitment to farm policy and community development before his 2018 congressional campaign.[27]

Congressional career

2018 election and entry to Congress

John Rose entered the 2018 race for Tennessee's 6th congressional district following the decision of incumbent Republican Diane Black to vacate the seat and pursue the Republican nomination for governor.[28] The district, encompassing rural and suburban areas in Middle Tennessee including Cookeville and parts of Nashville's outskirts, had been a reliably Republican stronghold. Rose, positioning himself as a conservative businessman and farmer with experience in agriculture and technology, won the Republican primary election on August 2, 2018, defeating challenger Bob Corlew and securing the nomination.[29] [30] In the general election held on November 6, 2018, Rose faced Democratic nominee Dawn Barlow, a former state representative, along with independent candidates David Ross and Lloyd Dunn.[31] Rose prevailed decisively, capturing 172,810 votes or 69.5% of the total, while Barlow received 70,370 votes (28.3%).[32]

Subsequent elections

2020

Incumbent John Rose secured reelection to Tennessee's 6th congressional district on November 3, 2020, defeating Democratic challenger Christopher Finley and independent candidate Christopher Monday.[34] Rose received 257,572 votes, comprising 73.7% of the total, while Finley garnered 83,852 votes (24.0%) and Monday obtained 8,154 votes (2.3%).[34] The election saw a total of 349,578 votes cast, with Rose prevailing by a margin of 49.7 percentage points.[34]

2022

Following redistricting that altered the boundaries of Tennessee's 6th congressional district, John Rose won reelection on November 8, 2022, against Democratic nominee Randal Cooper.[35][36] Rose captured 129,388 votes (66.3%), compared to Cooper's 65,675 votes (33.7%), in a contest totaling 195,063 votes.[37] The Associated Press called the race for Rose shortly after polls closed, reflecting the district's Republican lean despite the map changes.[37]

2024

John Rose was reelected to a fourth term on November 5, 2024, defeating Democratic challenger Lore Bergman by a margin of 36 percentage points.[38] Rose earned 225,543 votes (68.0%), while Bergman received 106,144 votes (32.0%), with 331,687 total votes reported.[38] The Associated Press projected Rose's victory on election night, consistent with the district's strong Republican performance in county-level results.[38]

2020

Incumbent John Rose won re-election to Tennessee's 6th congressional district on November 3, 2020, defeating Democratic nominee Christopher Finley. Rose secured 211,405 votes, or 72.5% of the total, while Finley received 80,739 votes, or 27.5%.[34] The district, centered in Middle Tennessee and encompassing rural and suburban areas including Cookeville, remained strongly Republican-leaning, consistent with Rose's 2018 victory margin.[39] Rose faced no significant opposition in the Republican primary held on August 6, 2020, advancing unopposed after filing in April.[40] Finley's nomination followed his win in the Democratic primary, where he outperformed other candidates amid low turnout typical for the district's partisan imbalance.[41] Voter turnout exceeded 60% district-wide, driven by the concurrent presidential contest, but Rose's campaign emphasized local agricultural issues and economic recovery, aligning with constituent priorities in farming-dependent counties.[34]

2022

Incumbent John Rose sought re-election to a third term in Tennessee's 6th congressional district, which had been redrawn by the Republican-controlled state legislature following the 2020 census to include East Nashville, Donelson, and rural counties such as Cannon, Clay, Cumberland, DeKalb, Fentress, Jackson, Macon, Overton, Putnam, Smith, Sumner, Trousdale, White, and Van Buren.[36] In the Republican primary on August 4, 2022, Rose advanced unopposed.[42] In the Democratic primary, Randal Cooper secured the nomination by defeating Clay Faircloth, receiving 17,071 votes (74.6%) to Faircloth's 5,798 votes (25.4%).[42] Rose defeated Cooper in the general election on November 8, 2022, with 129,311 votes (66.3%) to Cooper's 65,598 votes (33.7%); the Associated Press called the race shortly after polls closed at 8 p.m. CT.[43] [36] During the campaign, Rose highlighted priorities including construction of a border wall, opposition to expanding the Affordable Care Act, and limits on abortion access, consistent with his vote against certifying the 2020 presidential election results.[36] Cooper advocated for abortion rights, affordable healthcare, a higher minimum wage, and increased corporate taxes.[36] The district's strong Republican lean ensured Rose's comfortable margin in the safely red seat.[36]

2024

Incumbent John Rose sought re-election to a fourth term representing Tennessee's 6th congressional district in the 2024 cycle.[44] Rose advanced unopposed from the Republican primary on August 1, 2024, receiving 38,607 votes. His Democratic opponent, Lore Bergman, a retired State Department official, won her party's primary with 42.2% of the vote (8,684 votes) against two challengers. [45] In the general election on November 5, 2024, Rose defeated Bergman decisively, capturing 68.0% of the vote (225,543 votes) to Bergman's 32.0% (106,144 votes), with a total of 331,687 votes cast. [38] The district, rated as safely Republican by multiple forecasters including the Cook Political Report and Sabato's Crystal Ball, saw no partisan shift, aligning with Tennessee's broader Republican dominance in federal races. Rose's campaign emphasized agricultural support, economic growth, and conservative priorities, while Bergman focused on healthcare access and progressive policies.[44][45]

Committee assignments and caucus involvement

In the 119th Congress, Representative John Rose serves on the House Committee on Agriculture, where he holds the position of vice chair on the Subcommittee on Commodity Markets, Digital Assets, and Rural Development and is a member of the Subcommittee on Conservation, Research, and Biotechnology, as well as the Subcommittee on General Farm Commodities, Risk Management, and Credit.[46] He also sits on the House Committee on Financial Services, serving as vice chair of the Subcommittee on Oversight and Investigations and as a member of the Subcommittee on Digital Assets, Financial Technology, and Inclusion and the Subcommittee on Financial Institutions and Monetary Policy.[46] Rose is actively involved in several congressional caucuses, including the Republican Study Committee, the largest conservative organization in the House dedicated to promoting free-market policies, limited government, and traditional values.[47] His other caucus memberships encompass the Pro-Life Caucus, the Congressional Taiwan Caucus, the Rural Broadband Caucus, and the Range and Testing Center Caucus, reflecting his priorities in social conservatism, international relations, rural infrastructure, and defense-related issues.[47] Additionally, Rose chairs the Congressional Tennessee Valley Authority Caucus, focusing on regional energy and economic development concerns pertinent to Tennessee and surrounding states.[48]

Legislative priorities and voting record

Rose serves on the House Committee on Agriculture, Subcommittee on General Farm Commodities, Risk Management, and Credit, and the House Committee on Financial Services.[49] His legislative priorities emphasize support for rural economies through enhanced agricultural programs and protections against financial overreach that could harm investors.[50] He has consistently advocated for fiscal restraint, opposing excessive government spending to combat inflation while pushing for tax reductions on working families.[51] In agricultural policy, Rose supported the advancement of H.R. 8467, the Farm, Food, and National Security Act of 2024, out of the House Agriculture Committee on May 24, 2024, highlighting its provisions for new tools in commodity support, conservation, credit access, and crop insurance to aid producers amid volatile markets and rising input costs.[52] He delivered floor remarks during the bill's markup, underscoring its role in bolstering national food security and rural viability.[53] Rose has also joined bipartisan efforts, such as a letter from over 140 members of Congress in September 2024 urging timely farm bill renewal to address overdue updates for Tennessee farmers.[54] On financial services and economic matters, Rose introduced H.R. 4657 on July 19, 2024, to safeguard retail investors and retirement accounts from activist investment pressures by mandating enhanced disclosures for multi-class share structures, which the bill passed the House on September 19, 2024.[55] He voted in favor of H.R. 3357, the Enhancing Multi-Class Share Disclosures Act, on July 23, 2025, which passed overwhelmingly (381-31) to improve transparency in corporate governance.[56] Rose's voting record reflects a conservative orientation, with a perfect 100% score from Heritage Action in the 117th Congress for opposing measures like gun control packages (H.R. 7910) and supporting deregulation.[57] He opposed the draft of a Republican-backed megabill on May 21, 2025, aligning with fiscal conservatives against perceived excessive spending despite White House involvement.[58] On appropriations, he supported H.R. 5371, the Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act of 2026, which passed on September 18, 2025 (217-212), to maintain government operations amid budget negotiations.[56] Additionally, Rose voted yes on H.R. 3486, the Stop Illegal Entry Act of 2025, on September 11, 2025 (226-197), prioritizing border security as part of broader economic stability efforts.[56] Critics from progressive outlets have faulted his resistance to expansive regulatory bills, arguing it undermines worker protections, though Rose maintains such positions prevent bureaucratic overreach that stifles growth.[59]

Agricultural and rural policy achievements

As a member of the House Committee on Agriculture since January 2023, Rose has served on the Subcommittee on Commodity Markets, Digital Assets, and Rural Development, as well as the Subcommittee on Conservation, Research, and Biotechnology, enabling him to influence policies supporting rural economies and farming operations in Tennessee's Sixth District.[60] Rose received the American Farm Bureau Federation's "Friend of Farm Bureau" award in October 2020 for his support of agricultural priorities, including votes favoring the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, which enhanced market access for U.S. farmers, and advancements in the 2018 Farm Bill reauthorization that bolstered crop insurance and conservation programs.[61] In May 2024, Rose advocated during House Agriculture Committee markup for H.R. 8467, the Farm, Food, and National Security Act of 2024, commending its provisions for expanded commodity support, credit access, and crop insurance to aid producers amid volatile markets; the bill advanced from committee, though full enactment remained pending as of late 2024.[52][53] Rose sponsored and advanced H.R. 2462, the Black Vulture Relief Act of 2025, reintroduced in April 2025, which authorizes livestock producers to mitigate black vulture depredation on herds without federal permits under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act; the bill cleared the House Natural Resources Committee in July 2025, providing targeted relief for farmers facing annual losses estimated in the millions from vulture attacks on calves and weak animals.[62][63][64] In October 2021, Rose led H.Res.789 honoring the Tennessee Farm Bureau's centennial, recognizing its advocacy for rural infrastructure and policy reforms benefiting small farms and agribusiness in the state.[65]

Financial services and economic initiatives

Rose has served on the House Committee on Financial Services since his appointment on January 12, 2023, where he contributes to oversight of the financial sector, enhancement of capital formation, and regulation of digital assets and financial technology.[66] He participates in subcommittees on Digital Assets, Financial Technology, and Inclusion; Financial Institutions and Monetary Policy; and Oversight and Investigation, emphasizing policies that promote innovation while safeguarding consumer privacy and investor protections.[46] A key initiative is the Homebuyers Privacy Protection Act (H.R. 2808), which Rose reintroduced on April 10, 2025, in bipartisan fashion with Representative Ritchie Torres. The bill amends the Fair Credit Reporting Act to restrict consumer reporting agencies from sharing credit reports with mortgage lenders in ways that generate unsolicited contacts, known as "trigger leads," thereby reducing harassment for homebuyers.[67] It advanced unanimously through the Financial Services Committee on June 11, 2025, and passed the House on June 23, 2025, positioning it to potentially benefit millions by streamlining the homebuying process without compromising legitimate lending competition.[68][69] Rose also introduced H.R. 4657 on July 19, 2024, aimed at shielding retail investors and retirement accounts from influence by activist shareholders through measures requiring greater transparency in proxy voting by institutional investors. The legislation passed the House on September 19, 2024, as part of a broader package, reflecting Rose's focus on preserving long-term value in financial markets over short-term activist agendas.[70] In support of economic growth for small businesses, particularly in rural areas, Rose sponsored legislation in 2019 that passed the House on July 11 with 413 votes, facilitating better access to capital and resources for rural enterprises.[71] He reintroduced the Bank Loan Privacy Act (H.R. 2885) on April 10, 2025, to enhance privacy protections in banking transactions, aligning with his broader advocacy for deregulation that eases burdens on small lenders and borrowers while maintaining accountability.[72] These efforts underscore Rose's legislative priorities in fostering a stable financial environment conducive to entrepreneurship and consumer safeguards.

Criticisms of legislative positions

Rose's opposition to the June 2021 House bill awarding the Congressional Gold Medal to law enforcement officers for their response to the January 6 Capitol events elicited criticism from Democratic lawmakers and outlets such as CNN and The Washington Post, which portrayed the votes of the 21 opposing Republicansâ€”including Roseâ€”as a refusal to honor police defenders amid what they described as an insurrection.[73][74] The measure passed 406â€“21, with detractors arguing the no votes undermined bipartisan recognition of officers' sacrifices.[75] His repeated votes against Ukraine aid packages, including the $40 billion supplemental in May 2022 and a 2024 foreign aid measure, have been faulted by Ukraine support advocates and Democratic figures for signaling U.S. isolationism and eroding deterrence against Russia.[76][77] Rose justified these positions by citing insufficient oversight, lack of strategic endgames, and fiscal burdens from borrowed funds, but critics maintained such stances prioritized domestic spending constraints over alliance commitments.[78] In August 2025, activist groups in Tennessee's 6th District convened a town hall to decry Rose's alignment with Republican-led federal spending reductions and Trump-backed bills, asserting these policies eliminated constituent access to federal grants and programs.[79] Rose's unexpected resistance to a May 2025 Republican megabillâ€”encompassing tax extensions, Medicaid adjustments, and spending capsâ€”drew pushback from GOP leaders and bill proponents, who viewed it as impeding unified party advancements despite his advocacy for deeper fiscal restraint.[58] This stance aligned him with hard-line conservatives but frustrated efforts to consolidate legislative wins under President Trump.[80]

Controversies

Personal life scrutiny

Rose married Chelsea Doss in January 2011 after meeting through involvement in the Future Farmers of America (FFA).[24] At the time, Rose was 46 years old and Doss was approximately 20, having graduated high school in 2008; their age difference spans 25 years.[81] Doss, a high school senior in March 2007, attended an FFA event in Gatlinburg, Tennessee, where Rose served as a judge for a competition, marking one of their earliest documented interactions.[24] The circumstances of their relationship have drawn scrutiny from critics, primarily in left-leaning media and online forums, who have questioned the propriety of Rose's contact with a teenage Doss given his adult status and position of influence in FFA circles.[24] Allegations of grooming have circulated, though no legal actions or formal complaints have been reported, and Rose's congressional office has not responded to requests for clarification on the timeline or nature of their initial acquaintance.[82] Rose, a former Tennessee FFA state vice president during his own high school years in the 1980s, has emphasized shared agricultural interests as a foundation for their partnership, but has otherwise maintained a low public profile on the matter.[24] The couple resides in Cookeville, Tennessee, with their two sons, Guy and Sam, born after their marriage.[2] No prior marriages for Rose are documented in public records, and the family has presented a united front in campaign materials, with Chelsea Rose serving as executive director of the Tennessee FFA Foundation.[83] Scrutiny has not extended to allegations of infidelity, abuse, or other familial discord, focusing instead on the origins of their union amid broader cultural debates over age-disparate relationships.[24]

Policy disputes and intraparty tensions

In May 2019, shortly after assuming office, Rose joined two other Republicans in objecting to a $19.1 billion supplemental disaster aid package, effectively blocking its passage by insisting on corresponding spending cuts elsewhere in the federal budget to address fiscal concerns amid rising deficits.[84] This stance positioned him against GOP colleagues prioritizing rapid relief for storm-damaged communities in states like California and Puerto Rico, underscoring early tensions between fiscal conservatives and party members advocating for unoffset emergency spending. During the 117th Congress, Rose was among 21 House Republicans who voted against awarding the Congressional Gold Medal to U.S. Capitol Police officers responding to the January 6, 2021, events, citing reservations over the resolution's broad scope and potential politicization rather than opposition to recognizing law enforcement valor.[85] This dissent from the party majority, which approved the measure, reflected intraparty divides on commemorating the Capitol riot, with critics within the GOP viewing it as insufficiently addressing underlying security failures or as a Democratic-led narrative tool. In May 2025, amid Republican control of the House, Rose emerged as an unexpected holdout against leadership's draft of a comprehensive "megabill" combining tax cuts, border security funding, and spending reforms, aligning with fiscal hard-liners in demanding deeper offsets and stricter enforcement provisions before supporting passage.[58] His opposition, despite pressure from President Trump and GOP leaders who negotiated revisions to appease conservatives, highlighted ongoing fractures between establishment figures and rank-and-file members prioritizing deficit reduction over omnibus packages, though Rose ultimately backed a revised version after concessions. These episodes illustrate Rose's pattern of occasional resistance to party leadership on budgetary matters, driven by commitments to fiscal restraint, while maintaining high alignment on core conservative priorities as evidenced by his near-perfect party-line voting record in non-fiscal areas.[86]

2026 gubernatorial campaign

Announcement and platform

On March 20, 2025, U.S. Representative John Rose formally announced his candidacy for the Republican nomination in the 2026 Tennessee gubernatorial election at an event in Lebanon, Wilson County.[87] Rose, who represents Tennessee's 6th congressional district, highlighted his background as a farmer, small business owner, and self-described conservative outsider committed to advancing Tennessee's interests without career politician entanglements.[88] The announcement positioned his bid amid an emerging primary field, including potential competition from U.S. Senator Marsha Blackburn, with Rose emphasizing direct engagement with voters across the state.[87] Rose's platform centers on leveraging his congressional and business experience for measurable policy outcomes, informed by visits to all 95 Tennessee counties to incorporate regional input from diverse stakeholders.[89] Key priorities include expanding educational options via school vouchers to empower parental choice, particularly in underperforming public schools, while opposing measures that fail to ensure accountability.[89] Economically, he advocates maintaining Tennessee's low grocery tax rates alongside efforts to broaden the tax base for equitable state funding contributions from all residents.[89] Additional focuses encompass fiscal oversight of programs like SNAP benefits to prioritize nutritional support over non-essential expenditures, alongside investments in infrastructure, healthcare (with emphasis on mental health access), energy reliability, and economic development to attract employers and sustain growth.[89] Rose has pledged willingness to debate primary opponents on these issues ahead of the August 6, 2026, Republican primary.[89]

Early reception and challenges

Following his announcement on March 20, 2025, in Wilson County, Rose's entry into the Republican primary for the 2026 Tennessee gubernatorial election drew attention for positioning him as a conservative outsider with business and agricultural roots, emphasizing themes of limited government and rural advocacy.[87] [90] Initial reception highlighted his rapid fundraising, reporting a $6.1 million war chest by July 15, 2025, which underscored strong donor support among Republican base contributors in Tennessee's rural and suburban districts.[91] Grassroots enthusiasm was evident in early straw polls, where Rose outperformed U.S. Senator Marsha Blackburn, his primary rival, including a victory in a statewide Tennessee Republican straw poll and a landslide win in Lewis County, signaling appeal among local party activists.[92] [93] However, broader polling presented challenges, with an August 2025 Beacon Center survey showing Blackburn holding a commanding lead, 35% to Rose's 6% among likely Republican primary voters, reflecting her higher name recognition as an incumbent senator.[94] [95] A separate early poll reinforced Blackburn as the frontrunner, attributing her advantage to statewide visibility gained from her Senate tenure.[96] Rose responded by challenging Blackburn to a debate, touting his loyalty to former President Donald Trump as a differentiator amid intraparty discussions of candidate alignments.[97] These dynamics highlighted Rose's need to consolidate support in a competitive primary against Blackburn's established profile, with former President Trump publicly commenting on the race but not endorsing either contender as of August 2025.[98] Key early hurdles included differentiating his congressional record from Blackburn's senatorial experience, particularly on issues like economic policy and federal overreach, while navigating perceptions of his lower statewide profile outside Tennessee's 6th Congressional District.[99] Despite fundraising strength, the polling gap underscored the challenge of overcoming an entrenched opponent in a primary electorate favoring incumbency and familiarity.[100]
