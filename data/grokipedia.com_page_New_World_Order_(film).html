New World Order (film)

New World Order is a 2009 American documentary film directed by Luke Meyer and Andrew Neel, chronicling the efforts of conspiracy theorists to uncover what they describe as a secretive global cabal intent on establishing a totalitarian "New World Order."[1] The film centers on prominent figures including radio host Alex Jones, journalist Jim Tucker, retired police officer Jack McLamb, and activist Luke Rudkowski, depicting their investigations and protests targeting the annual Bilderberg Group meetings, which they allege coordinate elite agendas including false-flag operations like the September 11, 2001, attacks.[1][2] The documentary offers an intimate, observational portrayal of the anti-globalist movement's grassroots operations, emphasizing the personal motivations and logistical challenges faced by its adherents in confronting perceived institutional power structures.[3] Rather than narrating or endorsing the theories presented, it adopts a fly-on-the-wall approach, capturing raw footage of confrontations, media appearances, and internal deliberations among the protagonists.[2] This style has drawn divided responses, with some reviewers noting its success in humanizing dedicated skeptics of official narratives without overt judgment, while others critique it for insufficient scrutiny of unsubstantiated claims.[2] Reception for New World Order has been modest, earning a 5.7/10 rating on IMDb from over 700 users and a 56% Tomatometer score on Rotten Tomatoes based on nine critic reviews, reflecting its niche appeal within discussions of alternative media and dissent against centralized authority.[1] The film's release coincided with heightened public interest in Bilderberg secrecy and post-9/11 skepticism, positioning it as an early cinematic exploration of figures who would later gain broader notoriety for challenging mainstream consensus on global events.[2]

Production

Development and Directors

Luke Meyer and Andrew Neel, who co-directed the 2006 documentary Darkon exploring live-action role-playing games in Baltimore, initiated development on New World Order during the editing phase of their prior film.[4][5] Their decision to profile conspiracy theorists stemmed from discussions on globalization's societal impacts and the role of mass media in shaping ideologies, coinciding with heightened public skepticism toward official 9/11 narratives in the mid-2000s.[4] The filmmakers aimed to objectively document conspiracy culture through veritÃ©-style footage of key figures, emphasizing unfiltered access to subjects such as Alex Jones without overt judgment or debunking.[4] Neel stated their goal was to humanize the theorists by portraying their dedication firsthand, allowing audiences to experience their worldview as observers rather than critics.[4] This approach drew from observations of the activists' relentless efforts against perceived globalist agendas, including protests at events like the Bilderberg meetings.[4][6] Production proceeded independently in structure but with financial support from IFC Films, which backed the project from inception through completion, produced by Tom Davis under SeeThink Productions in association with Cactus Three Films.[4][6] This funding enabled extended filming from approximately 2006 to 2008, capturing raw interactions without the constraints of traditional studio oversight.[4]

Release

Premiere and Distribution

The documentary New World Order, directed by Luke Meyer and Andrew Neel, had its world premiere at the South by Southwest (SXSW) Film Festival in Austin, Texas, on March 13, 2009.[1][7] The festival screening provided an early platform for the film's exploration of anti-globalist conspiracy theorists, drawing attention from niche audiences interested in alternative political narratives.[7] Following the premiere, the film received a limited theatrical release in the United States, beginning in select independent cinemas around May 2009.[8][12] Without backing from major studios, distribution relied on smaller exhibitors, such as those covered in reviews from outlets like The New York Times, which noted screenings in New York City.[12] This approach aligned with the film's low-budget, independent production ethos, eschewing wide commercial promotion in favor of grassroots visibility within conspiracy and activist communities. Home video distribution emphasized DVD formats through online retailers like Amazon, where the film became available for purchase shortly after its theatrical run.[13] Streaming options emerged later, with platforms offering digital access by March 2017, though specifics on major services like Netflix remain unconfirmed in primary distribution records.[2] Overall, marketing efforts were minimal, depending on word-of-mouth dissemination via alternative media networks and festival buzz rather than traditional advertising campaigns.[7]

Availability and Formats

The film became available on DVD through retailers such as Amazon shortly after its 2009 release, with copies still offered for purchase as of 2025.[13] Physical media distribution has remained limited to independent sellers and online marketplaces, without evidence of widespread remastering efforts or expanded formats like Blu-ray.[13] By the 2010s, digital access expanded to streaming platforms, including full availability on Netflix, where it streams the documentary focusing on anti-globalist activists' efforts against secretive groups.[9] It is also accessible via Apple TV for rental or purchase, providing on-demand viewing options.[14] Clips and potentially full uploads persist on YouTube, though platform policies have led to occasional restrictions on related conspiracy content, without specific takedowns reported for this title.[15] Regional availability varies due to content moderation; for instance, Netflix access may be limited in jurisdictions with strict regulations on conspiracy-themed material, such as parts of the European Union under digital services laws, though the film maintains presence in archives and alternative video hosts catering to niche audiences.[9] No official sequels or updated editions have been produced, preserving its original format in conspiracy theory collections and online repositories.[1]

Reception and Analysis

Critical Reviews

The documentary received mixed critical reception, with a 56% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on nine reviews, reflecting ambivalence toward its observational style.[2] Critics praised the filmmakers' unprecedented access to anti-globalist activists protesting the Bilderberg Group's 2008 meeting in Chantilly, Virginia, which allowed for a humanistic portrayal of the subjects' personal motivations and daily operations.[6] However, many faulted the film for its lack of analytical depth, failing to scrutinize the conspiracy theories' evidentiary basis or the activists' methodologies beyond superficial encounters.[6] Metacritic aggregated a score of 32 out of 100 from four reviews, underscoring general unfavorable assessments of its execution.[8] Variety's Justin Lowe commended the directors Luke Meyer and Andrew Neel for shifting focus from the alleged "international cabal" to the fervent believers themselves, humanizing figures like Luke Rudkowski and Alex Jones without overt mockery, yet critiqued the absence of probing into underlying psychological or ideological drivers.[6] In contrast, The New York Times' Stephen Holden described the film as "unrelentingly tedious," portraying the protagonists as unglamorous and their protests as lacking coherence, which diminished the documentary's dramatic tension.[12] The Los Angeles Times' Robert Abele viewed it through a comedic lens, likening it to a "bittersweet, all-too-human comedy" akin to the directors' prior work on live-action role-playing, emphasizing interpersonal dynamics over substantive debate.[16] User-driven platforms showed divergence, with IMDb logging a 5.7 out of 10 rating from 701 votes, lower than audience enthusiasm on Rotten Tomatoes, highlighting critics' greater skepticism toward the film's non-confrontational approach.[1] Independent reviewer John Adams in Movie Habit noted the deliberate neutrality, observing that Meyer and Neel avoided endorsing or debunking claims, resulting in a work that neither validates the theories nor dismisses the activists outright, though this restraint left some questioning its journalistic rigor.[17] Overall, assessments prioritized the documentary's empathetic access and avoidance of sensationalism, while lamenting its reluctance to engage empirically with the claims' validity, prioritizing portraiture over critique.[18]

Public and Theorist Responses

Among audiences aligned with anti-globalist perspectives, the film was praised for humanizing conspiracy theorists and showcasing their commitment to protesting events like the Bilderberg meetings, with some viewers describing it as a "non-judgmental character study" that highlighted genuine passion against perceived elite control.[19] These responses emphasized the film's depiction of activists' daily efforts, including efforts to expose alleged 9/11 inconsistencies, as a counter to mainstream dismissals framing such inquiries solely as irrational paranoia.[19] However, other sympathetic viewers expressed frustration over perceived misrepresentation, arguing the documentary prioritized eccentric personal traits over evidentiary arguments for New World Order claims, leading some to feel it undermined the theorists' credibility rather than validating their worldview.[20] Featured figures like Alex Jones drew mixed theorist-aligned reactions, with reviews noting his portrayal evoked sympathy for his fervor but also reinforced critiques of him as overly sensational, diverging from endorsements that might affirm the film's alignment with their narratives.[20] In alternative online communities, discussions viewed the film as contributing to awareness of globalist agendas, with audience feedback citing it as prompting doubts about official stories and aiding in "discovering the new world order," though it sparked debates on whether the focus on activists' lifestyles diluted substantive red-pilling potential.[19] Right-leaning respondents particularly appreciated its challenge to post-9/11 consensus narratives, positioning the work as a rare mainstream-adjacent platform for questioning institutional accounts without outright mockery.[19]

Accuracy of Depictions

The film includes footage of protests organized by activists such as Alex Jones and Luke Rudkowski outside the 2008 Bilderberg Group meeting held at the Westfields Marriott in Chantilly, Virginia, from May 30 to June 1, where approximately 140 political leaders, business executives, and media figures convened in secrecy. These demonstrations, involving chants, signage decrying globalist agendas, and attempts to approach attendees, correspond to archival records and contemporary press accounts of the event, including police deployments to manage crowds estimated at several dozen to a few hundred participants. No verified instances of staged or fabricated protest footage appear in the documentary, though sequences are montaged with rapid cuts and overlaid narration to emphasize perceived urgency and suppression. Broadcast clips incorporated into the film, such as mainstream media dismissals of conspiracy theories or official statements on global institutions like the United Nations, draw from verifiable public records; for instance, excerpts align with archived CNN and BBC coverage of events like the 2007-2008 financial crisis discussions at elite forums. However, the editing juxtaposes these with theorists' interpretations to imply coordinated deception, introducing narrative framing not inherent in the original broadcasts, as confirmed by side-by-side comparisons with source material. Regarding empirical claims, the documentary highlights anomalies in the September 11, 2001, attacks, including the symmetric collapse of World Trade Center Building 7 at 5:20 PM EDT, which theorists attribute to controlled demolition rather than fire-induced failure. This depiction references witness accounts of explosions and the building's 2.25 seconds of free-fall acceleration, as documented in NIST's own final report released on November 25, 2008. While NIST concluded that thermal expansion from uncontrolled fires on multiple floors caused the progressive collapse without modeling global structural failure modes, over 3,500 architects and engineers have since petitioned for reinvestigation, citing discrepancies such as unaccounted energy dissipation and the rarity of steel-framed high-rises collapsing solely from fire prior to 9/11. Independent analyses, including those using finite element modeling by non-government researchers, have replicated free-fall dynamics only under removal of support columns, underscoring ongoing technical debates unresolved by official simulations. Depictions of secretive gatherings like the Bohemian Grove, referenced through Jones' prior infiltration footage from July 2000, accurately capture ritualistic elements such as the "Cremation of Care" ceremony involving effigy burning before a large owl statue, as corroborated by eyewitness accounts and leaked videos from the event site in Monte Rio, California. Yet, the film's extension of these to infer a unified "New World Order" cabal lacks direct causal linkage, relying on associative rhetoric rather than documented coordination among disparate elites.

Impact and Controversies

Influence on Awareness of NWO Theories

The 2009 documentary New World Order, which chronicled the efforts of conspiracy theorists including Alex Jones to protest the Bilderberg Group's 2009 meeting in Vouliagmeni, Greece, offered viewers an unfiltered glimpse into the organizational tactics and rhetoric of the anti-globalist movement at a moment of growing public distrust following the 2008 financial crisis.[1] By embedding with figures like Jones and Luke Rudkowski of We Are Change, the film highlighted claims of elite orchestration toward a supranational governance structure, thereby disseminating these narratives to festival and later streaming audiences.[9] Its world premiere at the South by Southwest Film Festival on March 13, 2009, exposed these ideas to an independent film crowd, potentially amplifying visibility among non-core adherents.[7] Post-release data indicate sustained interest in "New World Order" terminology during 2008â€“2010, with Google search peaks aligning temporally with the film's distribution amid broader economic anxieties and alternative media expansion, though isolating the documentary's causal role proves challenging amid concurrent events like the rise of films such as Zeitgeist (2007).[21] The portrayal of Jones, a central figure, coincided with Infowars' trajectory as a hub for institutional skepticism, which saw audience expansion in the ensuing decade through online platforms, correlating with a documented uptick in alternative media consumption that paralleled conspiracy-themed content proliferation.[22] Later documentaries and analyses referenced early activism depicted in the film as foundational to the mainstreaming of globalist critique narratives.[3]

Criticisms of the Film's Approach

The film's observational style, eschewing narration or explicit debunking in favor of raw footage of activists like Alex Jones confronting the Bilderberg Group's 2008 meeting, has drawn criticism for insufficient analytical rigor. Mainstream reviewers argued that this methodology humanizes the subjects without subjecting their claimsâ€”such as assertions of a coordinated elite plot behind events like 9/11â€”to evidentiary scrutiny or counterarguments, potentially amplifying unverified narratives under the guise of neutrality.[12] [6] The New York Times characterized the result as "unrelentingly tedious," faulting directors Luke Meyer and Andrew Neel for failing to contextualize the theorists' pursuits beyond personal obsession, which leaves the documentary vulnerable to perceptions of bias toward sympathy over journalism.[12] Proponents within the anti-globalist community have countered that the film's emphasis on interpersonal dynamics and logistical challenges of activismâ€”such as infiltrating secure eventsâ€”undermines substantive discussion of documented elite gatherings like Bilderberg, where over 120 policymakers convene annually in secrecy without public transcripts.[7] This selective focus, some argued, inflates perceptions of fringe eccentricity by prioritizing unglamorous moments over rational critiques of supranational influence, though raw footage from Jones's own archives shows consistent patterns of elite coordination verifiable through attendee lists. Left-leaning outlets, including Wired, highlighted psychological drivers like desperation for validation among "true believers," a framing echoed in broader media tendencies to pathologize dissent against institutional power without addressing empirical anomalies in globalist policies, such as the European Union's centralization efforts post-2008 financial crisis.[7] Such critiques overlook evidence of coherent argumentation from figures like Jim Tucker, whose decades-long reporting on Bilderberg predates the film's subjects and aligns with public admissions of policy influence from attendees.[6] Overall, the Metacritic score of 32/100 from four critics underscores methodological dissatisfaction, with reviewers citing incoherent structure as exacerbating the absence of balanced evidential presentation.[8]
