2023 Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu presidential campaign

The 2023 Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu presidential campaign was the effort by Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu, chairman of the Republican People's Party (CHP) since 2010, to win the Turkish presidency as the joint candidate of the opposition Nation Alliance in the elections held on 14 May and runoff on 28 May.[1][2] Selected as nominee on 6 March 2023 following internal primaries and negotiations among alliance partners, Kılıçdaroğlu positioned his bid as a restoration of democratic norms eroded under incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's rule.[3] The campaign, under the slogan "Promise You, Spring Will Come Again," emphasized economic stabilization through policies like raising the minimum wage and curbing inflation, alongside judicial independence and reconciliation initiatives to heal societal divides exacerbated by the February 2023 earthquakes.[4] Despite forging a coalition with diverse parties including the nationalist İYİ Party, Islamist Felicity Party, and center-right DEVA and Future parties, Kılıçdaroğlu obtained 27,134,841 votes (44.90%) in the first round and 27,786,526 votes (47.82%) in the runoff, falling short against Erdoğan's majorities of 49.51% and 52.18% respectively.[5][6][7] Notable for its attempt to unite fragmented opposition forces amid economic crisis and disaster recovery critiques, the campaign drew controversy over Kılıçdaroğlu's accusations of Russian-backed disinformation and the alliance's inclusion of conservative elements, which some viewed as diluting CHP's secular Kemalist roots, ultimately underscoring persistent conservative voter loyalty to Erdoğan.[8][9]

Background

Pre-election political context

Turkey's economy entered 2023 amid a prolonged crisis characterized by hyperinflation and currency depreciation, with annual inflation reaching a peak of 85.5 percent in October 2022 before moderating slightly, driven by expansionary fiscal policies and resistance to conventional interest rate hikes.[10] The Turkish lira had depreciated by approximately 70 percent in real effective terms from 2012 to 2022, exacerbating import costs and eroding purchasing power for households.[11] These conditions fostered widespread dissatisfaction, as real wages stagnated and unemployment hovered around 10 percent, creating fertile ground for opposition critiques of incumbent economic management without implying exclusive causal responsibility.[12] Compounding these pressures, twin earthquakes on February 6, 2023, struck 11 provinces in southeastern Turkey and northern Syria, resulting in over 50,000 deaths in Turkey alone and direct physical damages estimated at $34.2 billion, equivalent to 4 percent of GDP.[13] [14] The government's disaster response faced scrutiny for slow mobilization of resources and enforcement of building codes, with reports highlighting bureaucratic delays in search-and-rescue operations and aid distribution, which strained public trust in the ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP) ahead of the May elections.[15] This catastrophe shifted voter priorities toward accountability in governance and recovery efforts, amplifying calls for institutional reforms. Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu had served as leader of the main opposition Republican People's Party (CHP) since September 2010, following the resignation of Deniz Baykal amid a party scandal.[16] Under his tenure, the CHP, rooted in secular Kemalist traditions, consistently failed to secure national victories in the 2011, 2015, and 2018 general elections, though it achieved breakthroughs in major cities during the 2019 local polls.[17] Often portrayed as a methodical former civil servant emphasizing anti-corruption and judicial independence, Kılıçdaroğlu projected an image of bureaucratic reliability but drew criticism for perceived lack of mass appeal and charisma in mobilizing broader electorates.[18] In response to President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's People's Alliance of AKP and the Nationalist Movement Party (MHP), the opposition coalesced into the Nation Alliance—known as the "Table of Six"—formalized in early 2022, comprising the CHP alongside the center-right Good Party (İYİ), and smaller entities like the Future Party, Democracy and Progress Party (DEVA), Felicity Party, and Democrat Party.[19] [20] This coalition bridged ideological divides, including tensions between the CHP's secular base and conservative-leaning partners like the Islamist-leaning Felicity Party, aiming to consolidate anti-incumbent votes through a unified front while navigating internal frictions over candidate selection and policy compatibility.[19]

Nomination and opposition alliances

Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu, as chairman of the Republican People's Party (CHP), positioned himself as the party's presidential candidate following internal deliberations that highlighted preferences for more popular figures such as Istanbul Mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu or Ankara Mayor Mansur Yavaş, though no formal primary election was held within the CHP.[21][22] On March 4, 2023, the CHP officially endorsed Kılıçdaroğlu, emphasizing his leadership experience despite polls suggesting İmamoğlu or Yavaş might perform better against incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan.[23] The nomination extended to the broader Nation Alliance, comprising six opposition parties including the CHP, the İYİ Party led by Meral Akşener, the Felicity Party, the Democrat Party, the Future Party, and the DEVA Party, which convened as the "Table of Six" to present a unified challenge.[24] Initial tensions arose when Akşener walked out of alliance talks on March 3, 2023, rejecting Kılıçdaroğlu's candidacy in favor of İmamoğlu or Yavaş, but she reversed course after further negotiations and endorsed him on March 6, 2023, allowing the alliance to formalize his selection as the joint candidate.[25][26] The alliance pledged a "Turkish-type" presidential system incorporating checks and balances, such as enhanced parliamentary oversight, to differentiate from Erdoğan's model while retaining executive authority.[24] Unifying the alliance's diverse ideologies proved challenging, particularly with the pro-Kurdish Peoples' Democratic Party (HDP), which was not a formal partner but provided tacit support by indicating willingness to back Kılıçdaroğlu if core principles aligned, a stance that drew nationalist criticism for potentially relying on votes from a party accused of PKK ties.[27][28] This indirect endorsement, reaffirmed during the runoff phase, underscored the opposition's fragile coalition dynamics, as Kılıçdaroğlu navigated accusations of compromising on national security to secure electoral margins without explicit HDP alliance commitments.[29][30]

Campaign Platform

Economic policies

Kılıçdaroğlu's economic platform emphasized orthodox monetary and fiscal policies to address Turkey's severe inflationary pressures and currency instability, contrasting sharply with incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's unorthodox low-interest-rate approach, which Kılıçdaroğlu argued had exacerbated economic woes.[31][32] The Nation Alliance manifesto, released on January 30, 2023, promised to restore central bank independence by limiting its mandate strictly to price and financial stability, abolishing the interfering Price Stability Committee, and requiring a qualified parliamentary majority for any changes to the Central Bank Law.[32] This was positioned as a technocratic fix to enable higher interest rates and transparent management of foreign reserves, aiming to stabilize the Turkish lira and reduce inflation to low single digits within two years.[32][31] Fiscal discipline formed a core pillar, with proposals for a binding "Fiscal Rule" to ensure long-term predictability and limit public contracts denominated in foreign currencies, thereby curbing fiscal profligacy blamed for fueling inflation.[32] Kılıçdaroğlu critiqued Erdoğan's policies—including frequent central bank governor dismissals and suppression of interest rates—as empirically failed, citing Turkey's consumer price inflation peaking at 85.51% in October 2022 and averaging 72.31% for the year, which had eroded purchasing power and deterred investment.[31][33] To attract foreign direct investment, the platform advocated aligning public procurement with EU standards and accelerating negotiations to update the EU-Turkey Customs Union, framing these as incentives for capital inflows under a rules-based economic regime.[32][4] On employment, Kılıçdaroğlu pledged to generate at least five million additional quality jobs within five years through public-private partnerships, targeting single-digit unemployment by fostering high-value manufacturing and sustainable development.[32] Anti-corruption measures included establishing an "Office for the Recovery of Assets" to audit and reclaim funds lost to graft, such as those intended for post-February 2023 earthquake reconstruction, with promises of transparent guarantees in PPP projects to prevent irregularities.[32] These initiatives were presented as essential to efficiently redirect resources toward recovery efforts, avoiding the construction-centric and tax amnesty policies Kılıçdaroğlu deemed generators of unearned income and economic distortion.[32][4]

Domestic and social policies

Kılıçdaroğlu advocated returning to a parliamentary system by abolishing the executive presidency established via the 2017 constitutional referendum, arguing it centralized excessive power and undermined democratic checks.[34] He promised to depoliticize the judiciary through reforms ensuring judicial independence from executive interference and to liberalize media by ending state capture, including repealing restrictive provisions such as those criminalizing insults to the president under Article 299 of the Turkish Penal Code.[35] These commitments aimed to restore institutional autonomy and rule of law, framing the executive presidency as a driver of personalization in governance.[36] On immigration, Kılıçdaroğlu proposed repatriating Syrian refugees, pledging to facilitate the return of up to 3.6 million registered Syrians within two years through coordination with the European Union for safe zones and financial aid, emphasizing voluntary departure while criticizing the incumbent government's open-door policy as unsustainable.[37] [38] This stance, articulated in campaign rallies such as in Kırıkkale on May 10, 2023, sought to address public concerns over demographic shifts and resource strain but drew accusations of xenophobia from critics.[39] In education, Kılıçdaroğlu outlined reforms to shift from rote memorization to skill-based learning, proposing an independent Ministry of National Education focused on meritocratic teacher appointments and curriculum modernization to foster innovation and critical thinking.[40] [41] He criticized existing practices for prioritizing quantity over quality, vowing executive oversight only for coordination rather than control.[40] Regarding infrastructure, Kılıçdaroğlu opposed the 2019 closure of Atatürk Airport in Istanbul, labeling it wasteful and detrimental to aviation capacity, and pledged to repurpose the site as a hub for aviation research and space industries on April 28, 2023.[42] This plan highlighted inefficiencies in mega-projects like the new Istanbul Airport, positioning repurposing as a cost-effective alternative to full demolition.[43] Kılıçdaroğlu committed to bolstering women's rights by enshrining protections against discrimination and violence, refusing negotiations that could erode existing gains such as those under Law No. 6284 on family protection, and promoting gender equality in public appointments.[44] These social pledges emphasized anti-discrimination measures across ethnic and gender lines while upholding secular frameworks for institutional reforms.[45]

Foreign policy and national security

Kılıçdaroğlu advocated for a foreign policy reorientation toward greater integration with NATO and the European Union, emphasizing compliance with European Court of Human Rights rulings and resumption of stalled EU accession negotiations to align Turkey more closely with Western institutions.[46][47] He pledged support for Sweden's and Finland's NATO membership bids, reversing Erdoğan's delays, and expressed willingness to return Russian S-400 systems to rejoin the F-35 program, framing these steps as restoring rational alliance commitments over personal diplomacy.[48][49] This vision critiqued Erdoğan's approach as overly assertive and ideologically driven, promising instead a reinstitutionalized policy focused on national interests without domestic political interference.[47][49] On Russia and Ukraine, Kılıçdaroğlu outlined a balanced yet West-leaning stance, offering to mediate Moscow-Kyiv talks while prioritizing NATO obligations and potentially reducing Turkey's role as a sanctions conduit for Russia.[48] He supported conditional aid to Ukraine but stressed pragmatic management of energy dependencies on Russia, avoiding Erdoğan's equidistance that enabled economic ties amid Western sanctions.[48][50] This positioned his policy as less autonomous than Erdoğan's, with potential risks to Black Sea grain deals and regional mediation if Western alignment intensified.[50] Kılıçdaroğlu sought to mend ties with neighbors through dialogue and non-intervention, including normalization with Syria's Assad regime to facilitate refugee repatriation and de-escalation in Iraq.[47][48] He emphasized improved relations with Greece via reduced tensions and expressed openness to Armenia, though without detailed breakthroughs, contrasting Erdoğan's inconsistent "zero problems" framework that evolved into military engagements in Syria and Libya.[49][48] In national security, Kılıçdaroğlu promised evidence-based counterterrorism against the PKK and YPG, opposing U.S. backing of Syrian Kurdish groups and vowing to combat terrorism while securing borders through mass repatriation of 3.7 million Syrian refugees and irregular migrants within two years.[48][38] These appeals to nationalists were undermined by his alliance with pro-Kurdish parties like the DEM Party (formerly HDP), which drew PKK endorsements and demands for softer policies, such as potential prisoner releases, fueling skepticism about his deterrence commitments compared to Erdoğan's operations.[50][51] He critiqued Erdoğan's Syria interventions as adventurist, advocating withdrawal to focus on border stability over regime-change pursuits.[49][50]

Campaign Activities

Debates and public statements

Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu sought to engage incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan in a formal televised debate throughout the campaign, issuing public challenges that highlighted perceived ruling party avoidance of scrutiny on governance failures, but Erdoğan declined all such invitations, opting instead for separate media appearances.[52] [53] Lacking direct confrontation, Kılıçdaroğlu focused on public statements and interviews emphasizing his administrative competence, anti-corruption stance, and critiques of elite extravagance, positioning himself as a steady alternative amid economic turmoil. These efforts aimed to underscore causal links between policy mismanagement and public hardship, though post-campaign analyses attributed limited voter persuasion to his measured, often defensive rhetorical style, which observers described as lacking the charismatic energy needed to counter Erdoğan's populist delivery.[54] In a notable April 21, 2023, statement, Kılıçdaroğlu pledged that, if elected, he and his family would personally fund any non-official expenses at the presidential complex, implicitly contrasting this with the facility's operational costs—estimated by opposition figures at around 900,000 Turkish lira per hour—and its construction amid national austerity measures exceeding 5 billion lira in taxpayer funds.[55] [56] He framed the palace as emblematic of systemic disconnect, arguing it diverted resources from essential services while inflation surpassed 80% in 2022, a claim rooted in official expenditure reports but contested by pro-government sources as exaggerated for political effect. Empirical polling from outlets like Euronews showed no measurable uptick in Kılıçdaroğlu's support following such critiques, with his first-round share stabilizing at 44.9% despite pre-election surveys indicating potential volatility.[57] Kılıçdaroğlu supplemented traditional statements with social media videos to humanize his campaign and simplify policy explanations for broader audiences, launching initial clips on March 28, 2023, that narrated personal journeys and promised systemic reform.[58] These digital efforts, including direct appeals for change in outlets like TIME magazine on April 27, 2023, targeted urban and youth demographics but drew mixed reception—praised for accessibility yet critiqued as overly scripted and insufficiently dynamic to shift entrenched voter loyalties, as evidenced by stagnant runoff polls favoring Erdoğan by margins of 5-7 points.[52] [57] Overall, while Kılıçdaroğlu's communicative strategy privileged substantive critiques over spectacle, it failed to generate the polling momentum needed, with analyses linking this to broader opposition challenges in countering state media dominance.[54]

Rallies and mobilization efforts

Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu's ground campaign following his March 6, 2023, nomination as the Nation Alliance candidate featured a series of rallies emphasizing national unity and the prospect of political change.[59] The effort built chronologically through April and early May, with key events in urban centers like Istanbul and Izmir drawing thousands of supporters.[60] A prominent mega rally on May 7 in Istanbul, co-hosted with alliance leaders, attracted large crowds to demonstrate opposition cohesion ahead of the May 14 first round.[61] Attendance estimates placed these gatherings in the tens to hundreds of thousands, though consistently lower than incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's events, such as his May 7 Istanbul rally reported at 1.7 million by state-aligned sources.[62] [63] In earthquake-devastated regions like Hatay and Kahramanmaraş, Kılıçdaroğlu's mobilization focused on messages of solidarity amid criticism of the government's response, but rally turnout remained subdued compared to urban areas.[64] These efforts faced challenges in rural conservative strongholds, where Erdoğan's base proved resilient, contributing to lower opposition mobilization.[65] To counter this, the campaign targeted urban youth—approximately five million first-time voters disillusioned with long-term rule—through public appeals for renewal, alongside encouragement for diaspora participation that saw record overseas turnout.[66] [67] After securing 44.9% in the first round, Kılıçdaroğlu intensified rallies in the lead-up to the May 28 runoff, including a final push on May 27, while pivoting toward nationalist-leaning districts to consolidate support from third-place finisher Sinan Oğan's voters.[68] [69] This phase highlighted logistical strains in expanding reach beyond core urban and secular demographics, with events underscoring alliance unity despite internal tensions.[53]

Controversies

Identity and religious appeals

Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu, leader of the secular Republican People's Party (CHP), publicly acknowledged his Alevi heritage for the first time on April 19, 2023, stating in a campaign video, "I am an Alevi," as part of an effort to promote pluralism and counter perceptions of otherness amid Turkey's Sunni-majority demographics.[70][71] This disclosure, historically taboo for Turkish politicians, drew immediate backlash from President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's camp, which portrayed Kılıçdaroğlu's identity as incompatible with the pious Sunni values central to the Justice and Development Party (AKP) base.[72][73] Erdoğan himself questioned the timing of the revelation at Kılıçdaroğlu's age of 74 and accused him of inciting sectarian division, while emphasizing a unified Islamic identity over sectarian labels.[72] In response to accusations of irreligiosity, Kılıçdaroğlu sought to demonstrate personal piety and position the CHP as inclusive of religious voters, rejecting claims that his background distanced him from conservative constituencies.[74] However, such efforts faced scrutiny for perceived inauthenticity, exemplified by an April 2, 2023, incident where a photograph surfaced showing Kılıçdaroğlu appearing to step on a Muslim prayer rug during a visit, which pro-AKP outlets amplified to undermine his religious credentials among devout Sunnis.[75] Conservative commentators argued that these gestures clashed with the CHP's longstanding Kemalist secularism, rendering appeals to piety unconvincing to voters accustomed to the party's historical antagonism toward overt religiosity. Empirical election outcomes underscored the limited success of Kılıçdaroğlu's identity bridging, with minimal shifts in support from pious, conservative demographics toward the opposition; in the May 14, 2023, first-round presidential vote, he garnered 44.9% nationally compared to Erdoğan's 49.5%, while AKP retained dominance in religiously conservative Anatolian provinces.[7] In the May 28 runoff, Kılıçdaroğlu received 47.8% to Erdoğan's 52.2%, analyses attributing the incumbent's edge to effective mobilization of Islamist-leaning voters through identity-based rhetoric, in contrast to the opposition's broader inclusivity which failed to overcome entrenched sectarian and historical divides.[7][76]

Policy-specific criticisms

Kılıçdaroğlu's proposal to repurpose the shuttered Atatürk Airport as an aviation and space center, in partnership with U.S.-based Sierra Nevada Corporation executives, faced rebuke from Turkish defense industry figures, including Baykar CEO Haluk Bayraktar, who argued it unduly favored American entities over indigenous firms capable of leading such initiatives.[42] Opponents of the plan contended that Atatürk's closure in 2019 was essential for operational efficiency, enabling the $12 billion Istanbul Airport to serve as a larger hub with capacity for up to 200 million annual passengers, compared to Atatürk's prior limit of around 60 million, while addressing the older site's runway and geographical constraints.[42] Campaign pledges on education, including rendering the Ministry of Education independent from political interference, were assailed as insufficiently detailed, offering broad aspirations without concrete mechanisms for enhancing outcomes amid Turkey's persistent challenges in PISA rankings and teacher quality.[40] [77] Similarly, promises of media liberalization were deemed vague by analysts, lacking specifics on regulatory overhaul, with skeptics citing CHP-administered municipalities' records—such as delays in public service delivery in Istanbul and Ankara—as evidence undermining reform feasibility.[77] The candidate's commitment to deport millions of Syrian refugees within a year garnered nationalist backing via pacts like that with the Victory Party, yet clashed with tacit reliance on votes from the pro-Kurdish DEM Party (formerly HDP), whose platforms emphasize humanitarian protections for migrants, prompting charges of opportunistic flip-flopping to broaden appeal at the expense of coherence.[38] [78] [79] This tension fueled voter skepticism, as DEM's rights-based stance on refugees underscored the alliance's ideological strains, potentially eroding trust in execution.[80]

Strategic missteps and opposition infighting

The Nation Alliance's candidate selection process in early 2023 exposed deep internal divisions, culminating in İYİ Party leader Meral Akşener's rejection of Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu's nomination on March 3, 2023, and her threat to exit the coalition unless more electable figures like Istanbul Mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu or Ankara Mayor Mansur Yavaş were considered instead.[81][82] Akşener reversed course after concessions, including promises of vice-presidential roles and parliamentary seats to smaller allies, but the episode delayed unified promotion of Kılıçdaroğlu until roughly two weeks before the May 14 first-round vote, fostering perceptions of disarray and eroding pre-election optimism about opposition synergy.[54][9] Additional strains arose from rival Memleket Party leader Muharrem İnce's late entry and withdrawal on May 11, 2023, which fragmented anti-Erdoğan votes and underscored the alliance's inability to consolidate behind a single, broadly appealing leader despite polls favoring alternatives like İmamoğlu.[82] Kılıçdaroğlu's campaign strategy overemphasized negative appeals, framing Erdoğan's re-election as a guarantee of ongoing failures—particularly in the response to the February 6, 2023, earthquakes that killed over 50,000—while offering limited positive vision beyond vague populist pledges like visa-free EU travel.[9] This approach neglected broader economic discontent and inspirational messaging, allowing Erdoğan's targeted aid distribution in quake-hit regions to restore his image among affected voters, who shifted support back to the incumbent by election time and blunted the opposition's core narrative of incompetence.[9] The alliance's elite-driven focus on identity politics and concessions to minor partners further alienated potential grassroots mobilization, as the promised multi-vice-president structure evoked memories of unstable 1990s coalitions without delivering electoral cohesion.[82][54] In the May 28 runoff, Kılıçdaroğlu pivoted aggressively to ultra-nationalist rhetoric four days after the first round, pledging mass deportation of Syrian refugees to capture the 5.2% garnered by third-place finisher Sinan Oğan, yet this late shift failed to sway Oğan's base, who largely transferred to Erdoğan following Oğan's explicit endorsement of the incumbent on May 22, 2023.[83][82] Empirical vote analysis indicated that Erdoğan's first-round 49.5% rose to 52.2% in the runoff, absorbing most of Oğan's support, while Kılıçdaroğlu's perceived insincerity distanced pro-Kurdish allies without compensating gains, exacerbating the infighting's momentum loss.[9][82] This tactical gamble, rooted in misjudging nationalist priorities amid alliance fractures, sealed the opposition's defeat despite initial unity hopes.[54]

Reception and Analysis

Public and polling response

Pre-election opinion polls indicated that Kılıçdaroğlu maintained competitiveness against incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, with projections for the first round placing him in the range of 30-45% support nationally, often trailing but within striking distance in multi-candidate scenarios.[84] [85] Runoff polls from firms such as ORC Research and others frequently depicted a statistical tie or narrow margins, with Kılıçdaroğlu garnering 44-49% in several late surveys amid expectations of a head-to-head contest.[86] These figures reflected heightened voter engagement following the February 2023 earthquakes, though methodological variations among pollsters like MetroPOLL and KONDA led to discrepancies in turnout assumptions and regional weighting.[57] Polling data highlighted Kılıçdaroğlu's stronger resonance among urban and secular demographics, where surveys consistently showed him leading in major cities like Istanbul and Ankara, driven by preferences for opposition platforms emphasizing institutional reform and EU alignment.[87] In contrast, he trailed significantly in rural and conservative Anatolian provinces, where identity-based loyalty to Erdoğan's bloc dominated, with polls indicating support gaps exceeding 20 percentage points in eastern and central regions.[88] Economic discontent, including inflation rates surpassing 80% in early 2023, bolstered initial sentiment toward Kılıçdaroğlu as a change agent, with voter surveys identifying the economy as the top concern for over 50% of respondents and correlating with upticks in his numbers among middle-class urbanites.[89] [90] However, preferences for perceived stability under the incumbent offset these gains, particularly in polls factoring in security and cultural identity issues.[76] Efforts to mobilize youth and diaspora voters yielded mixed polling signals, with first-time voters under 25 leaning toward Kılıçdaroğlu at rates 10-15% higher than the national average in targeted surveys, reflecting frustration with unemployment and restricted freedoms.[66] [91] Diaspora sentiment abroad showed similar generational divides, with younger expatriates in Europe expressing preference for opposition change in informal polls, though overall expatriate sampling indicated limited breakthrough beyond coastal and urban Turkish bases.[92] [67] Turnout mobilization campaigns aimed at these groups correlated with slight poll upticks in the final weeks but failed to substantially expand penetration into conservative heartlands.[93]

Media coverage and disinformation claims

Turkish media during the 2023 presidential campaign was characterized by significant pro-government dominance, with outlets like state broadcaster TRT and major private networks providing extensive coverage to incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan while allocating minimal airtime to challenger Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu. Reporters Without Borders reported that public broadcasters devoted over 90% of election-related airtime to Erdoğan and his alliance, effectively marginalizing opposition voices and contributing to an uneven informational playing field. Opposition figures, including Kılıçdaroğlu, alleged systemic bias, pointing to regulatory pressures from the Radio and Television Supreme Council (RTÜK), which fined or threatened independent outlets critical of the government.[94] Despite this, Kılıçdaroğlu leveraged opposition-aligned channels such as Halk TV and Fox TV, as well as digital platforms, to reach audiences. His May 25, 2023, appearance on the YouTube program "Halkın Sesi" amassed over 22 million views within a day, demonstrating substantial social media penetration that bypassed traditional gatekeepers. Campaign rallies and statements were amplified through these channels, with YouTube analytics showing millions of additional views for key videos, enabling the opposition to counterbalance mainstream media constraints through grassroots digital dissemination.[95][96] Disinformation proliferated across platforms, with allegations targeting both camps; pro-government actors circulated manipulated videos falsely depicting Kılıçdaroğlu making inflammatory statements, while opposition narratives exaggerated government mismanagement in the February 2023 earthquakes, including unsubstantiated claims of deliberate aid delays. Fact-checking organizations like Teyit documented over 100 instances of misleading content, including deepfakes and AI-generated audio, though empirical impact on voter behavior remained debated due to limited independent audits. Erdoğan accused opposition "troll armies" of foreign-backed lies, while Kılıçdaroğlu's team highlighted state-orchestrated fakes, underscoring mutual recriminations without conclusive evidence of decisive electoral sway from any single source.[97][98][99] Internationally, Western outlets like The Guardian and BBC framed Kılıçdaroğlu as a beacon for democratic renewal against Erdoğan's consolidation of power, emphasizing his pledges for institutional reforms and EU alignment. This portrayal contrasted with domestic perceptions of Western media as interventionist, fueled by Erdoğan's rhetoric on foreign plots and skepticism toward outlets seen as aligned with opposition interests, though no verified data linked such coverage to tangible shifts in Turkish public opinion.[100][101][102]

Internal and external critiques

Within the opposition alliance, critiques emerged from allied parties regarding Kılıçdaroğlu's leadership and appeal. İYİ Party leader Meral Akşener, representing nationalist elements, withdrew support from the joint candidacy in early March 2023, labeling Kılıçdaroğlu a weak contender unfit to challenge Erdoğan effectively and accusing the selection process of favoring personal ambitions over Turkey's interests, particularly highlighting deficiencies in nationalist mobilization.[103][104] This reflected broader alliance tensions over Kılıçdaroğlu's perceived inability to unify and energize conservative-leaning voters beyond urban CHP strongholds. External analyses during the campaign underscored Kılıçdaroğlu's charisma deficit relative to Erdoğan's dynamic presence, portraying the CHP leader's soft-spoken, reformist demeanor as insufficiently compelling to counter the incumbent's populist vigor and personal dominance in rallies.[74][105] Commentators noted a failure to empirically dismantle Erdoğan's narrative on economic resilience, despite available indicators of inflation exceeding 80% and lira depreciation, as Kılıçdaroğlu's accusations of data concealment lacked the aggressive, evidence-based attacks needed to sway undecideds.[106] Right-leaning observers criticized the opposition's structural urban bias, arguing it overlooked causal factors in conservative voter loyalty, such as cultural resonance and rural patronage networks, where past elections showed CHP support clustered in metropolitan areas while AKP dominated Anatolian provinces with over 50% margins in key rural districts.[107] This disconnect, they contended, stemmed from the alliance's elite, secular framing that alienated pious and nationalist majorities, prioritizing coastal and urban grievances over broader empirical outreach to Erdoğan's base.[108]

Outcome and Aftermath

First-round results

In the first round of the 2023 Turkish presidential election, held on May 14, 2023, Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu received 44.9% of the valid votes, amounting to 21,007,896 ballots, according to official figures from the Supreme Election Council (YSK).[109][110] Incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan secured 49.5% with 27,014,671 votes, while Sinan Oğan of the ATA Alliance finished third with 5.2% (2,329,228 votes), positioning him as a potential influencer in the ensuing contest.[109][110][111] No candidate attained the absolute majority required for an outright victory, triggering a mandatory runoff between Erdoğan and Kılıçdaroğlu on May 28.[109] Voter turnout stood at 87.05%, the highest in a decade for such elections, amid widespread public discontent over inflation exceeding 70% and currency devaluation.[112] Kılıçdaroğlu's support was concentrated in urban centers, where he led in provinces like Istanbul (49.4% vs. Erdoğan's 44.6%) and Ankara (47.9% vs. 46.5%), reflecting opposition mobilization among educated and secular voters, but he trailed significantly in rural and eastern Anatolian districts dominated by conservative and nationalist bases.[110] In parallel parliamentary voting, the opposition Nation Alliance expanded its seat share to 212 from 137 in 2018, signaling parliamentary momentum yet falling short of upending the presidential outcome.[110]

Runoff phase

Following the first-round results on May 14, 2023, which saw neither candidate secure a majority, Kılıçdaroğlu intensified his campaign efforts from May 15 to 28, emphasizing a pivot toward nationalist themes to consolidate support from ultranationalist voters who had backed third-place finisher Sinan Oğan.[113] In particular, he hardened his stance on migration, pledging to deport irregular migrants and refugees—estimating their number at 10 million under Erdoğan's tenure—to appeal to anti-immigrant sentiments and court Oğan's base.[114] This rhetorical shift marked a departure from his earlier inclusive messaging, aiming to frame the contest as a battle against perceived governmental leniency on borders.[115] Kılıçdaroğlu's team pursued Oğan's endorsement through negotiations focused on these anti-refugee promises, but the bid failed when Oğan announced his support for Erdoğan on May 22, citing shared priorities on security and migration despite past criticisms of the incumbent.[116] This outcome exacerbated opposition disunity, as Oğan's roughly 5% first-round vote share tilted toward Erdoğan, underscoring Kılıçdaroğlu's challenges in unifying disparate factions beyond the Nation Alliance.[117] Concurrently, the campaign ramped up rallies and advertisements contrasting "change" under Kılıçdaroğlu with "continuity" under Erdoğan, highlighting economic woes and democratic erosion, though these efforts were constrained by Erdoğan's advantages in state media access and organizational resources.[68] On May 16, Kılıçdaroğlu issued direct appeals to young voters, promising to combat inflation and restore democratic norms after years of perceived repression, positioning himself as a generational alternative amid high youth unemployment and disillusionment.[118] In parallel, last-minute outreach to Kurdish voters involved reaffirmations of support from pro-Kurdish parties like the DEM Party (formerly HDP-aligned), which had backed him in the first round and reiterated endorsement on May 25 despite tensions over his nationalist pivot.[119] These moves sought to shore up the opposition's diverse coalition but faced skepticism, as Erdoğan's alliances neutralized potential swings from Oğan's ultranationalists while maintaining Kurdish outreach of his own.[120]

Post-election resignation and legacy

Kılıçdaroğlu conceded the May 28, 2023, presidential runoff, in which he secured 47.8% of the vote against incumbent Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's 52.2%, marking his third consecutive national election loss as CHP leader.[7] The defeat triggered immediate internal pressure within the Republican People's Party (CHP), with calls for his resignation intensifying amid accusations of strategic errors and failure to capitalize on economic turmoil and the February 2023 earthquakes.[54] By November 2023, facing a leadership challenge at the CHP's 38th congress, Kılıçdaroğlu stepped aside, allowing Özgür Özel—an ally of Istanbul mayor Ekrem İmamoğlu—to win with 712 delegate votes against rival Örsan Öymen's 414, ending Kılıçdaroğlu's 13-year tenure.[121][122] The campaign's legacy underscores the Turkish opposition's structural challenges in countering Erdoğan's personalized appeal to conservative and nationalist voters, despite empirical conditions ripe for incumbency loss—such as inflation exceeding 80% and widespread devastation from the quakes displacing millions. Kılıçdaroğlu's emphasis on secular, reformist messaging failed to bridge identity divides, particularly his Alevi background alienating pious Sunni majorities and the Nation Alliance's overtures to pro-Kurdish groups evoking security concerns without sufficient nationalist buy-in. This rigidity perpetuated a pattern of opposition defeats, as evidenced by CHP's urban-rural vote gaps persisting across elections.[54] Subsequent CHP successes in the March 31, 2024, local elections—securing 37.8% nationally and majorities in key cities like Istanbul and Ankara—highlighted tactical adaptations under new leadership, prioritizing demonstrable governance over ideological abstraction. These gains, the opposition's strongest since 1977, validated voter preference for incumbents with proven local records like İmamoğlu and Ankara's Mansur Yavaş, contrasting Kılıçdaroğlu's national campaign reliance on untested promises amid perceived elitism. Yet, the 2023 loss reinforced causal insights into opposition dynamics: abstract anti-corruption narratives insufficient against entrenched patronage networks, necessitating candidates embodying cultural familiarity to erode Erdoğan's base.[17][123] Kılıçdaroğlu's ouster thus catalyzed renewal, though persistent alliance fractures suggest enduring hurdles for unified anti-Erdoğan fronts.[124]
