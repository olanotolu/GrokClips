M. J. Alexander

M. J. Alexander (born 1961) is an American photographer, journalist, writer, and educator renowned for her documentary work chronicling the people, landscapes, and cultural heritage of Oklahoma and the American West through portraits, books, and exhibitions.[1] Her seminal project Salt of the Red Earth: A Century of Wit and Wisdom from Oklahoma's Elders (2007) features photographs and interviews with over 100 Oklahoma centenarians, commissioned for the state's centennial and resulting in public art installations, including displays at the Oklahoma State Capitol.[1][2] Alexander's images have appeared in The New York Times and Time magazine, and she has held more than 25 solo exhibitions, notably a 2018 retrospective at London's Crypt Gallery and the first solo show by an Oklahoman at the International Photography Hall of Fame's main gallery.[2][1] In recognition of her fusion of journalistic rigor and artistic vision, she was inducted into the Oklahoma Journalism Hall of Fame in 2019; her second book, Portrait of a Generation: Sons and Daughters of the Red Earth, earned an Oklahoma Book Award.[1][2] Prior to settling in Oklahoma City in 1998, Alexander worked as a reporter and editor for The Associated Press in New York and taught journalism at New York University and St. Michael's College in Vermont.[1][2]

Early Life and Education

Upbringing and Influences

Mary Jane Alexander was born on July 18, 1961, in Dafter, Michigan, a rural community of approximately 250 residents near Sault Ste. Marie.[3] Her parents married on October 22, 1960, in Sault Ste. Marie, Ontario, shortly after her father's mother passed away; her mother, the seventh of nine children from a Polish-speaking household, was the first in her family to graduate high school and worked as a bookkeeper, while her father was a brilliant but restless inventorâ€”having designed a rotary lawnmower as a boyâ€”who built the family home without blueprints.[3] The Alexanders represented the fourth generation in Dafter, with her father, grandfather, and great-grandfather employed as lumberjacks, timber cruisers, and log truck drivers in the Upper Peninsula's timber industry.[3] Alexander grew up in this self-built home amid an economically challenged region plagued by 20-30% unemployment, often accompanying her father on his rural mail route or awaiting his return from work.[3] She attended the two-room Dafter School for grades K-5, participating in educational programs like the "Festival of Song" that introduced her to a wide array of folk and international music.[3] From an early age, she showed aptitude for creative expression, publishing a poem in The Evening News at age 10 and a word search puzzle in Junior Scholastic during junior high; she also experimented with skating starting at age 4 and piano at age 8, though she gravitated more toward reading and writing.[3] Her upbringing by "practical people" in a small town "on the edge of the Delirium Wilderness near the Canadian border"â€”a reference to the untamed forests and economic hardships of northern Michiganâ€”fostered an early exposure to self-reliant, working-class narratives that echoed her family's timber heritage.[2] [3] These formative experiences, set against the backdrop of generational labor in resource extraction and rural isolation, prefigured her professional emphasis on documenting resilient individuals in overlooked American landscapes.[2]

Academic Background

Alexander earned an undergraduate degree from Vassar College.[4][5] She subsequently received a master's degree in journalism from Columbia University's Graduate School of Journalism.[4][6] In addition, she completed a certificate program at Cambridge University.[5][7] These studies equipped her with foundational skills in liberal arts and professional journalism, informing her subsequent career in reporting, photography, and documentary work.[1]

Journalism Career

Initial Positions and Reporting

Alexander's initial journalism roles followed her completion of a Master of Science degree from Columbia University's Graduate School of Journalism in 1986. She commenced her professional reporting at community newspapers in the Great Lakes region, including outlets in her native Michigan, where she covered local stories as a reporter and photographer. These early positions involved documenting regional communities and events, building on her prior experience as a park ranger in the area, which honed her observational skills for on-the-ground journalism.[8][2] In February 1987, Alexander advanced to The Associated Press (AP) bureau in New York City, serving as a reporter, photographer, and editor until July 1994â€”a tenure spanning over seven years. At AP, she contributed to national wire service coverage of urban and cultural subjects, producing photo essays and articles distributed to subscribing newspapers and magazines. Her work emphasized visual storytelling alongside textual reporting, reflecting the wire service's demand for concise, verifiable dispatches on timely events. This period marked her transition from regional to metropolitan journalism, with outputs appearing in major publications.[9][1][5] Early reporting highlights included features on overlooked communities, aligning with her later thematic interests in generational narratives and cultural documentation. While specific bylines from her Great Lakes assignments remain sparsely archived, her AP contributions underscored factual accuracy in high-volume news environments, where she edited copy and photographed assignments amid the bureau's fast-paced operations. These roles established her reputation for blending written analysis with imagery, prior to editorial and academic pursuits.[2][1]

Editorial and Teaching Roles

Alexander held editorial and reporting positions for roughly 15 years before relocating to Oklahoma in 1998, including roles at community newspapers near the Great Lakes and as an editor and reporter for The Associated Press in New York City from December 1986 to July 1994.[5][1] Her Associated Press tenure, spanning over seven years at 50 Rockefeller Plaza, involved editing and reporting for wire services distributed nationally.[10] These experiences built on her training at Columbia University's Graduate School of Journalism, where she earned a master's degree.[2] In academia, Alexander taught news writing courses at New York University during her early career in the city.[5] Later, she chaired the journalism department at Saint Michael's College in Vermont, overseeing curriculum and faculty prior to her 1998 move westward.[2][5] These teaching roles leveraged her professional background to instruct aspiring journalists in reporting techniques and editorial standards.[2] Her academic contributions complemented her newsroom work, emphasizing practical skills in an era of transitioning media landscapes.[1]

Photographic and Documentary Works

Key Projects and Publications


One of M. J. Alexander's prominent photographic projects involved documenting Oklahoma's centenarians to mark the state's centennial in 2007. She photographed individuals aged 100 or older, capturing sepia-toned portraits accompanied by their personal insights on life, resilience, and wisdom derived from experiences spanning the 20th century.[11][2] This work culminated in the fine-art book Salt of the Red Earth: A Century of Wit and Wisdom from Oklahoma's Elders, which she authored and illustrated, published by the Oklahoma Heritage Association.[12][2] Building on this theme of generational continuity, Alexander produced Portrait of a Generation: Sons and Daughters of the Red Earth, a follow-up project focusing on portraits of Oklahoma's children. The book, also authored and illustrated by her, explores the lives and environments of young residents in the American West, blending documentary photography with narrative elements. It received the Oklahoma Book Award, recognizing its contribution to regional cultural documentation.[1][2] These publications highlight her approach to chronicling the people and landscapes of the Great Plains through extended photo series that integrate visual artistry with reported storytelling.[2] Alexander's documentary photographs have appeared in major outlets, including The New York Times and Time magazine, extending the reach of her Oklahoma-focused projects to broader audiences interested in American frontier narratives.[2] Her works emphasize empirical observation of rural and historical communities, avoiding stylized interpretations in favor of direct, unfiltered representations grounded in on-site engagements.[2]

Exhibitions and Installations

M.J. Alexander's photographic exhibitions emphasize portraits of Oklahoma's diverse populations, including Native American cultural figures, youth, and centenarians, often touring multiple venues. Her series Tribal Dancers & Elders was displayed at the Oklahoma State Capitol from January 30 to June 15, 2016, and previously at the TAC Gallery in Tulsa from February 4 to 22, 2014, the Invited Artists Gallery in Oklahoma City from April 27 to July 16, 2009, and the Red Earth Museum from July 20 to October 1, 2009.[13] Similarly, Sons and Daughters of the Red Earth, under the broader Portrait of a Generation exhibition, appeared at the Oklahoma State Capitol's North Gallery from February 13 to April 15, 2012, the Tulsa Historical Society from September 30, 2011, to February 1, 2012, and the Oklahoma City Museum of Art's Foundersâ€™ Gallery from January 20 to February 28, 2011, with selections entering the permanent Heritage Trust Collection in Oklahoma City from June 20, 2017, onward.[13][6] The Centenarians of Oklahoma project, comprising 100 portraits of state elders, debuted at the International Photography Hall of Fame Museum from April 14 to July 9, 2007, followed by showings at the Oklahoma History Center's Devon Great Hall on November 16, 2007, the Oklahoma State Capitol's East Gallery from October 28, 2007, to January 14, 2008, and the Brush Creek Foundation for the Arts in Saratoga, Wyoming, from January 15 to May 1, 2012.[13] Alexander's works have also reached international audiences, with an exhibition of Oklahoma portraits marking her European debut at London's Crypt Gallery from January 11 to 13, 2018.[14] In addition to gallery exhibitions, Alexander has developed site-specific public art installations integrating photography, text, and multimedia. During the COVID-19 pandemic, her practice evolved to include immersive GPS-triggered sound installations.[15] Notable examples include Poems In The Parks, where two original poems were sandblasted as text sculptures into sidewalks across eight Oklahoma City parks in winter 2021; I Killed I Can't, featuring twenty centenarian portraits with their advice installed in the EMBARK transportation system's Light Gallery in the Oklahoma City Underground in January 2021; and a public art photography suite of lenticular, vinyl, and banner works placed in three Oklahoma City recreation centers in July 2020.[16] Permanent installations encompass The Centenarians portraits in the Governorâ€™s Suite of the Oklahoma State Capitol since February 15, 2019.[16]

Core Themes and Approach

M.J. Alexander's photographic and documentary works center on chronicling the people, places, and cultural narratives of the Great Plains and American West, with a particular emphasis on Oklahoma as both a geographic home and symbolic frontier.[2] Her projects often explore the enduring bonds between individuals and their environments, capturing the sacred, fleeting, and near-forgotten dimensions of regional life through intimate portraits and site-specific imagery.[2] This thematic focus extends to intergenerational connections, as seen in her documentation of elders whose stories embody a century of adaptation to the land's challenges and opportunities.[17] A prominent example is the Salt of the Red Earth series, completed in 2007, which features portraits and interviews with over 100 Oklahoma centenarians born in or before 1907, preserving their wit, wisdom, and firsthand accounts of state history from pioneer eras to modern times.[10][18] Alexander traveled nearly 5,000 miles across Oklahoma for this work, initially aiming for a dozen subjects but expanding to encompass a broader cross-section of elders, including Native American tribal members, to reflect diverse experiences of longevity and resilience.[19][17] The project underscores themes of human endurance and cultural continuity, presenting unedited voices that counter sanitized historical narratives with raw, empirical testimonies.[20] Alexander's approach fuses her journalistic backgroundâ€”spanning two decades with outlets like the Associated Pressâ€”with lyrical artistry, employing a methodology that interweaves reportage, poetry, and visual composition to navigate the boundary between verifiable truth and interpretive fiction.[2] She prioritizes immersive fieldwork, combining environmental portraits with subjects' own words to evoke the causal ties between personal agency and place, avoiding abstraction in favor of grounded, evidence-based depictions derived from direct observation and dialogue.[2] This hybrid style manifests in exhibitions and installations that prioritize accessibility, such as public art projects installed in Oklahoma City parks, where photographic elements integrate with textual elements to foster communal reflection on shared heritage.[21] Her work thus serves as a documentary archive, emphasizing empirical preservation over stylized embellishment, while acknowledging the subjective layers inherent in human recollection.[17]

Creative Writing and Collaborations

Poetry, Lyrics, and Plays

Alexander's poetry often intertwines with her documentation of the American West, blending lyrical expression with observations of place and people. In 2021, she created Path Poems, a public art project commissioned by the City of Oklahoma City, featuring two original poems sandblasted into sidewalks across eight parks to guide pedestrians through natural and urban landscapes.[22] [15] The installation emphasizes themes of journey and reflection, drawing from Oklahoma's terrain to evoke a sense of hidden narratives in everyday paths.[10] As a lyricist and librettist, Alexander frequently collaborates with her husband, composer Edward Knight, providing texts that fuse historical research, personal genealogy, and poetic introspection. Their 2007 song cycle Tales Not Told sets six of her poems for high voice and piano, chronicling the unsung lives of Knight's female ancestors, including figures like Patience Brewster from the Mayflower era and Sarah Towne Cloyce, a Salem witch trials survivor.[23] [24] The work highlights resilience amid adversity, performed in settings that underscore its narrative depth.[25] In Of Perpetual Solace (premiered 2021), Alexander authored the libretto for Knight's multimedia choral-orchestral composition, commissioned by Canterbury Voices and performed with the Oklahoma City Philharmonic.[26] [27] This poetic reflection on solace amid loss incorporates her visual art, creating a layered dramatic structure for chorus, orchestra, and projected imagery, evoking themes of endurance and quiet redemption rooted in American experience.[28] While specific standalone plays remain less documented in public records, her librettos function as scripted narratives for performed works, bridging poetry and theatrical form through musical collaboration.[29]

Musical and Performance Works

M.J. Alexander has contributed to musical works primarily as a librettist and lyricist, often collaborating with composer Edward Knight on pieces that integrate text, music, and visual elements to explore themes of remembrance, resilience, and regional identity.[27] Her librettos emphasize poetic reflection on historical events and human endurance, drawing from her background in journalism and photography to infuse narratives with empirical detail and emotional depth.[26] A prominent example is Of Perpetual Solace, a 45-minute multimedia composition for full orchestra, 250- to 300-voice chorus, and visual projections, with libretto by Alexander and music by Knight. Commissioned by Canterbury Voices to mark the 26th anniversary of the 1995 Oklahoma City bombing, the work meditates on grief, loss, love, and life's persistence, inspired by the city's Survivor Tree as a symbol of survival.[27][28] The piece unfolds without pauses, evoking an ethereal soundscape of remembrance and hope.[27] Its world premiere occurred on May 16, 2021, at Oklahoma City's Civic Center Music Hall, performed by Canterbury Voices and the Oklahoma City Philharmonic after delays due to the COVID-19 pandemic.[30][31] Alexander also co-led the development of Sound Trekking: Norman, an interactive public art soundscape installation in Andrews Park, Norman, Oklahoma, debuting in September 2023. As concept and lead artist, she partnered with Knight on composition and Kiegan Ryan on sound design, creating a site-specific audio experience accessible via a mobile app that guides users through layered soundscapes tied to local history and poetry.[32][33] Funded in part by a $15,000 grant from Mid-America Arts Alliance, the project expands on Alexander's earlier "In Their Words" installation, blending spoken word, music, and ambient recordings to foster immersive environmental engagement.[34][35] This work under the OPUS 46 collective highlights her approach to performance as participatory and locative, merging lyrical text with sonic elements for public accessibility.[36] Additional lyrical contributions include the libretto for Nothing Gold Can Stay, premiered in 2021 by Ad Astra Voices following an earlier performance by Canterbury Voices with soprano Randi Ellefson, though specific compositional details remain tied to broader thematic explorations of transience.[37] Alexander's oeuvre in this domain underscores a commitment to collaborative, interdisciplinary performance that preserves cultural narratives through integrated artistic media.[15]

Recognition and Enduring Impact

Awards and Inductions

Alexander was inducted into the Oklahoma Journalism Hall of Fame in 2019, recognizing her two decades of work as a photographer, reporter, and editor for newspapers, magazines, and the Associated Press, as well as her transition to teaching photojournalism at the University of Central Oklahoma.[1] In 2009, she was honored at the UNESCO-sponsored World Humanity Photography Awards in Guangzhou, China, as the sole U.S. photographer selected, for her photographic series documenting Apache Crown Dancers.[8] For her lyrical contributions, Alexander received eight consecutive awards from the American Society of Composers, Authors, and Publishers (ASCAP).[5] Her book Portrait of a Generation: Sons and Daughters of the Red Earth earned the 2011 Oklahoma Book Award in the young adult category, as well as a gold medal from the Independent Publisher Book Awards for best regional nonfiction in the Midwest.[38][39] She has also held artist-in-residence positions, including at the Hedgebrook writers' colony on Whidbey Island, Washington, supporting her interdisciplinary work in writing and photography.[5]

Influence on Cultural Preservation

M.J. Alexander's photographic and documentary projects have significantly contributed to the preservation of Oklahoma's cultural heritage by capturing the life stories, wisdom, and traditions of its eldest residents and indigenous communities. In 2007, coinciding with Oklahoma's state centennial, she interviewed and photographed 144 centenarians across the state, resulting in the book Salt of the Red Earth: A Century of Wit and Wisdom from Oklahoma's Centenarians, which features portraits and oral histories of 100 individuals born in or before 1907.[12][40] These works document personal anecdotes, survival advice, and historical insights from pioneers, ensuring that firsthand accounts of early 20th-century life in the Great Plains are archived against generational loss.[16] Her centenarian portraits have been exhibited widely and installed as permanent public art, including twenty large-scale images with accompanying elder advice in Oklahoma City's EMBARK public transportation system, promoting ongoing public engagement with this vanishing demographic's narratives.[16][5] The traveling exhibition "Portrait of a Generation: The Centenarians of Oklahoma" further disseminates these images, fostering appreciation for resilience and continuity in regional identity.[13] Alexander's focus extends to Native American cultures through projects like "Tribal Dancers & Elders" and Sons and Daughters of the Red Earth, a book of portraits that earned an Oklahoma Book Award and highlights contemporary indigenous life rooted in historical traditions.[1][13] By blending journalistic rigor with artistic expression, her documentation preserves endangered oral traditions and visual records of American Indian elders and youth, countering cultural erosion in the American West.[15] These efforts, featured in over two dozen solo exhibitions and publications such as The New York Times, underscore a commitment to archival storytelling that sustains communal memory.[15]
