Lester Cole


Lester Cole (June 19, 1904 – August 15, 1985) was an American screenwriter and Communist Party USA member who co-authored films including Objective, Burma! (1945) and The Romance of Rosy Ridge (1947), and served as one of the Hollywood Ten after refusing to disclose his political affiliations during 1947 House Un-American Activities Committee hearings, resulting in a contempt conviction, one-year prison sentence, and subsequent industry blacklist.[1][2][3]
Born Lester Cohn in New York City to Polish immigrant parents, with a father who was a Marxist garment union organizer, Cole dropped out of high school at age 16, pursued acting and playwriting, and relocated to Hollywood in the early 1930s where he joined the Communist Party in 1934.[2][4][5]
A co-founder of the Screen Writers Guild, Cole continued scripting under pseudonyms like Gerald L. C. Copley during the blacklist—crediting adaptations such as Born Free (1966)—and in his 1981 autobiography Hollywood Red: The Autobiography of Lester Cole, confirmed that all Hollywood Ten members held Communist Party affiliations, reflecting his unyielding ideological commitment amid the era's anti-communist scrutiny.[6][1][3][5]

Early Life

Upbringing and Education

Lester Cole was born Lester Cohn on June 19, 1904, in New York City to Polish Jewish immigrant parents.[2][6] His father worked as a Marxist organizer in the garment workers' union, exposing Cole to leftist ideologies from a young age; by the eighth grade, he had already distanced himself from conventional American political norms.[2][6] Cole left high school at age sixteen in 1920 without graduating, forgoing further formal education to pursue ambitions in theater.[7][1] Aspiring to act, he began writing, directing, and staging plays in New York during the 1920s, honing skills that later informed his screenwriting career.[7][1]

Professional Beginnings

Entry into Hollywood

Cole transitioned from theater work in New York to screenwriting by relocating to Hollywood in 1932.[7] Prior to this, he had established himself as a playwright and stage director after leaving high school at age 16.[6] His initial Hollywood assignment involved collaborating with 17 other writers on the anthology film If I Had a Million, a Paramount Pictures production released that year featuring episodic segments directed by filmmakers including Ernst Lubitsch and Norman Taurog, with stars such as W. C. Fields and Gary Cooper.[8] [9] Cole contributed to one of the film's segments, marking his first credited screenplay and establishing his foothold in the industry amid the early sound era's demand for prolific writers.[10] This entry aligned with Hollywood's expansion in the 1930s, where studios sought adaptable talent from Broadway to fuel B-picture production and original scripts. Cole's involvement in If I Had a Million exemplified the era's experimental multi-author approach to features, though credits were often shared or pseudonymous due to guild formation pressures.[11] By 1933, he co-founded the Screen Writers Guild (later the Writers Guild of America), advocating for credit standardization and residuals, which reflected his rapid integration into professional networks.[11]

Screenwriting Career

Notable Works and Union Founding

Cole authored or co-authored screenplays for over 40 films, including Objective, Burma! (1945), a World War II action film directed by Raoul Walsh and starring Errol Flynn, The House of the Seven Gables (1940), an adaptation of Nathaniel Hawthorne's novel directed by Joe May, and The Invisible Man Returns (1940), a Universal Pictures horror sequel.[7] [6] Other credits encompassed Blood on the Sun (1945), a spy thriller with James Cagney, and early works such as Pursued (1934) and Men in Her Diary (1945).[5] [12] Following the blacklist's end in the early 1960s, he contributed to Born Free (1966), an Academy Award-nominated family film about lions raised in Kenya.[12] [2] In 1933, amid a studio-imposed 50 percent salary reduction during the Great Depression, Cole co-founded the Screen Writers Guild (predecessor to the Writers Guild of America) with John Howard Lawson and Samuel Ornitz, officially organizing it as a labor union on April 6 to negotiate collective bargaining rights for screenwriters.[11] [13] [5] The Guild's formation addressed exploitative practices, including credit denial and arbitrary pay cuts, marking an early successful push for professional standards in Hollywood writing.[7] Cole later served as the Guild's president from 1944 to 1945, advocating for minimum wages, pension benefits, and protections against censorship.[2] [6]

Political Involvement

Communist Party Membership

Lester Cole joined the Communist Party USA (CPUSA) in 1934, shortly after co-founding the Screen Writers Guild with fellow party members John Howard Lawson and Samuel Ornitz.[2][8] His decision followed exposure to Marxist ideas through guild organizing and leftist intellectual circles in Hollywood, where he adopted the moniker "Hollywood Red" for his advocacy of proletarian themes in screenwriting.[6] Cole's membership involved active participation in CPUSA fractions within the film industry, including efforts to influence union policies and script content toward anti-fascist and class-struggle narratives during the Popular Front era.[8] He paid dues and attended meetings, maintaining formal affiliation as a card-carrying member until at least the late 1940s, though he later described his commitment as ideological rather than rigidly doctrinal.[2] In his 1981 autobiography Hollywood Red, Cole openly confirmed his CPUSA membership and defended it as a response to the Great Depression and rising fascism, rejecting claims of Soviet espionage while acknowledging party discipline on political issues.[6][2] This self-disclosure contrasted with his 1947 refusal to answer House Un-American Activities Committee questions on the matter, which led to his contempt citation, but aligned with post-blacklist admissions by other Hollywood Ten members who verified their affiliations through personal records or testimony.[8] Cole remained ideologically aligned with Marxism lifelong, though he distanced himself from the party's post-1956 shifts following Khrushchev's revelations.[8]

Ideological Activities and Stance

Cole adhered to Marxist-Leninist ideology, joining the Communist Party USA in the early 1930s and remaining a member for decades thereafter.[6] In his 1981 autobiography Hollywood Red: The Autobiography of Lester Cole, he explicitly affirmed his party membership, describing it as a principled commitment to socialism amid the Great Depression's economic hardships and the rise of fascism in Europe.[3] Cole viewed communism as a bulwark against capitalism's failures, aligning with Soviet foreign policy shifts; prior to the 1941 Nazi invasion of the USSR, he maintained discretion about his affiliations, but afterward openly supported the wartime alliance as antifascist solidarity.[6] His ideological activities centered on infiltrating and influencing Hollywood institutions to advance proletarian interests. As a co-founder of the Screen Writers Guild in 1933 alongside fellow travelers John Howard Lawson and Samuel Ornitz, Cole pushed for collective bargaining while embedding party-line advocacy, such as opposing "fascist" studio practices and promoting scripts with social-realist themes.[5] He participated in front organizations like the Hollywood Anti-Nazi League and the Motion Picture Democratic Committee, which funneled support to Popular Front causes, including aid for the Republican side in the Spanish Civil War and defense of the USSR's non-aggression pact with Nazi Germany until its rupture.[5] During World War II, Cole helped establish the Hollywood Writers Mobilization, ostensibly for war bond drives and propaganda, but which served as a conduit for communist networking in the industry.[6] Cole's stance rejected reformist liberalism, insisting on revolutionary change through class struggle, as evidenced by his unwavering defense of Joseph Stalin's policies in internal party circles and his later writings critiquing American imperialism.[3] He dismissed anticommunist critics as reactionary tools of monopoly capital, a position that informed his refusal to disavow the party during congressional scrutiny.[6] Despite opportunities to recant post-imprisonment, Cole upheld doctrinal loyalty, later attributing the blacklist's persistence to capitalist suppression rather than Soviet espionage threats documented in declassified files.[3]

HUAC Confrontation

1947 Hearings and Refusal to Testify

Lester Cole appeared before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) on October 30, 1947, as part of its investigation into alleged Communist Party influence within the Hollywood film industry.[14] The hearings, which commenced on October 20 in Los Angeles, targeted screenwriters and filmmakers suspected of promoting subversive ideologies through motion pictures amid rising Cold War tensions.[15] Cole, a prominent screenwriter and co-founder of the Screen Writers Guild, was among the group later known as the Hollywood Ten—uncooperative witnesses who challenged the committee's inquiries into their political affiliations.[8] At the outset of his testimony, Cole sought to read a prepared statement affirming his loyalty to the United States, adherence to the Constitution, and opposition to totalitarianism from any ideological extreme, while decrying the committee's methods as a threat to democratic principles.[5] Committee Chairman J. Parnell Thomas interrupted, refusing to allow the full statement into the record and directing Cole to answer specific questions.[15] When interrogated on whether he was or had ever been a member of the Communist Party—a query central to HUAC's probe into potential espionage and propaganda risks—Cole declined to respond, citing protections under the First and Fifth Amendments against compelled disclosure of beliefs and self-incrimination.[15] [16] Cole's defiance mirrored that of fellow witnesses, including John Howard Lawson and Albert Maltz, who preceded him, and formed part of a coordinated resistance asserting that HUAC's tactics infringed on constitutional freedoms rather than addressing genuine security threats.[17] The committee viewed such refusals not as legitimate invocations of rights but as evasive maneuvers to shield Communist activities, prompting immediate recommendations for contempt proceedings against the group.[15] This stance underscored broader debates over congressional investigative powers versus individual liberties in the postwar era.[18]

Contempt Conviction and Imprisonment

Following the House Un-American Activities Committee's hearings in October 1947, during which Cole refused to answer questions about his Communist Party affiliations, the House of Representatives voted on November 24, 1947, to cite him and the other nine members of the Hollywood Ten for contempt of Congress.[15][19] The citation stemmed from their invocation of the First Amendment to decline responding to inquiries on whether they were or had been members of the Communist Party, a stance the committee deemed obstructive to its investigation into alleged Communist influence in the film industry. Cole's trial occurred in federal court in Washington, D.C., as part of the proceedings against the Hollywood Ten, where he was one of eight defendants tried together in October 1949 before Judges Edward M. Curran and David A. Pine.[20] The jury convicted him of two counts of contempt for his refusal to produce records or testify as subpoenaed, rejecting the defense's argument that the committee's questions violated constitutional protections against self-incrimination and compelled speech.[20] Federal judges suspended the sentence pending appeal, emphasizing the case's implications for congressional investigative powers.[20] In early 1950, Cole was sentenced to one year in prison and fined $1,000, consistent with penalties imposed on his fellow Hollywood Ten members for similar refusals.[9] Appeals reached the U.S. Supreme Court, which declined to review the convictions in June 1950, upholding the lower courts' rulings that the refusals constituted willful contempt rather than protected speech.[15] Cole subsequently served his full one-year term in federal prison, emerging in 1951 to face industry blacklisting.[21]

Blacklisting Era

Industry Exclusion and Pseudonymous Writing

Following his release from federal prison in June 1951 after serving a one-year sentence for contempt of Congress, Lester Cole encountered systematic exclusion from the Hollywood film industry as part of the broader blacklist enforced by major studios.[7] The industry's adherence to the Waldorf Statement, issued by studio executives on November 25, 1947, explicitly barred employment of individuals who refused to testify before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) or were identified as communist sympathizers, rendering Cole unemployable under his own name.[11] This exclusion persisted through the 1950s, devastating his finances and limiting him to sporadic, clandestine work; he later reported earning only 15 percent of his pre-blacklist salary for any scripts he managed to sell.[7] To circumvent the blacklist, Cole resorted to pseudonymous writing, submitting screenplays through intermediaries or under fictitious credits, a common tactic among blacklisted writers during the era.[6] His most notable post-prison contribution was the 1966 adaptation of Joy Adamson's Born Free, for which he received no official credit during production but used the pseudonym Gerald L.C. Copley; the film, directed by James Hill, grossed over $26 million worldwide and earned two Academy Awards.[9] [2] Cole produced a limited number of other scripts under assumed names during this period, though details remain sparse due to the covert nature of the arrangements, effectively curtailing his once-prolific output of over 40 credited films.[21] The blacklist's enforcement, driven by studio self-policing and loyalty oaths imposed by guilds and producers, not only barred Cole from credited work but also isolated him professionally, forcing reliance on non-industry pursuits like teaching screenwriting classes to sustain himself.[11] Full restoration of credits for blacklisted writers, including Cole, did not occur until 1997, when the Writers Guild of America retroactively acknowledged contributions like Born Free.[22] Despite these efforts, the era's repercussions marked the effective end of Cole's mainstream screenwriting career, with pseudonymous ventures serving as rare exceptions rather than a viable path forward.[6]

Economic and Professional Repercussions

Following his release from federal prison on December 22, 1951, after serving a one-year sentence for contempt of Congress, Cole faced immediate and severe professional exclusion from the Hollywood studio system due to the industry's blacklist enforced by major producers.[6] This banishment prevented him from securing credited work on films or legitimate contracts with studios, effectively halting his established screenwriting career that had previously yielded over 40 scripts between 1932 and 1947.[4] The blacklist, initiated by the Waldorf Statement in November 1947 and upheld through the 1950s, resulted in Cole's financial ruin, as he could no longer command the substantial fees typical for a veteran Writers Guild founder and prolific contributor to projects like the Charlie Chan series.[7][6] To sustain himself during the blacklist era, Cole resorted to ghostwriting screenplays under pseudonyms, such as Gerald L. C. Copley, for fractions of his pre-blacklist compensation—specifically, approximately 15 percent of his prior rates.[9][7] These clandestine contributions, often funneled through intermediaries or front writers, yielded minimal income and denied him residuals, credits, or guild protections, exacerbating his economic hardship amid ongoing legal fines totaling $1,000 from his contempt conviction.[6] By the mid-1950s, such opportunities dwindled, forcing Cole to pivot to non-industry pursuits, including teaching screenwriting classes and authoring novels, which provided scant remuneration compared to his former Hollywood earnings. The professional toll extended beyond immediate job loss; Cole's unfinished contributions to scripts like that for Viva Zapata! (1952) were discarded or unattributed, underscoring how blacklisting not only barred new projects but erased potential legacy work.[8] Overall, the era's repercussions left Cole in prolonged financial distress, reliant on sporadic low-paid gigs and personal resources until the blacklist's erosion in the early 1960s, though he never fully regained his pre-1947 prominence.[6][8]

Post-Blacklist Period

Continued Advocacy and Writings

Following his release from prison in 1951, Cole sustained his commitment to Marxist ideology, identifying as a dedicated Marxist and maintaining ties to communist circles without recanting his earlier affiliations.[6] He continued pseudonymous contributions to screenplays amid lingering industry restrictions, including uncredited work on the 1966 film Born Free, for which he received no official credit due to residual blacklist sensitivities.[5] In the mid-1960s, Cole shifted to the San Francisco Bay Area, where he taught screenwriting courses through the University of California Extension program for approximately two decades, imparting his craft and political perspectives to students.[6] Concurrently, he authored film criticism for People's World, the Communist Party USA's West Coast publication, offering analyses that reflected his enduring advocacy for socialist viewpoints on cinema and culture during the 1960s through 1980s.[6][5] Cole's advocacy extended to international engagements, such as attending a Soviet film festival in Moscow as an invited guest in one of his final public appearances before his death in 1985, underscoring his alignment with Soviet-aligned communist initiatives.[6] These efforts represented a pivot from mainstream Hollywood production to alternative platforms for ideological expression, though they yielded limited professional restoration.[6]

Autobiography and Self-Reflection

In 1981, Lester Cole published Hollywood Red: The Autobiography of Lester Cole, a detailed account of his life as a screenwriter, union organizer, and political activist spanning from his early career in the 1930s to the aftermath of the blacklist.[23] The book, issued by Ramparts Press, chronicles his entry into the Communist Party USA in 1935, motivated by economic hardships during the Great Depression and ideological alignment with labor struggles, while emphasizing his role in Hollywood's progressive screenwriting circles.[3] Cole portrayed his party involvement not as blind allegiance but as a commitment to anti-fascist causes and workers' rights, critiquing internal party rigidities yet defending its broader anti-capitalist ethos without recanting his membership.[24] Cole's reflections on the 1947 House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) hearings centered on his refusal to testify as a defense of constitutional principles rather than a denial of communist ties; he explicitly confirmed in the autobiography that all members of the Hollywood Ten, including himself, were indeed Communist Party members, though they withheld public admission to avoid self-incrimination and to protect the Fifth Amendment's invocation against compelled disclosure.[3] He described the contempt conviction and nine-month imprisonment in 1950 as a badge of principled resistance, rejecting any personal remorse and framing the episode as an assault on free expression by government overreach, while expressing disdain for former colleagues who cooperated with authorities to salvage their careers.[25] The blacklist's professional toll—pseudonymous work, financial strain, and exile-like existence—featured prominently as a catalyst for Cole's enduring resentment toward studio executives and informants, whom he accused of prioritizing profit over artistic integrity and civil liberties.[26] In self-assessing his post-blacklist persistence in leftist writing and advocacy, Cole conveyed no ideological pivot, viewing his unyielding stance as vindicated by history's judgment on McCarthyism, though he acknowledged tactical errors in party secrecy that fueled public suspicions.[27] An appendix and illustrations supplemented his narrative, underscoring personal anecdotes over abstract theorizing, with Cole positioning his life as emblematic of ideological conviction amid persecution.[28]

Death and Legacy

Final Years and Passing

In the years following the blacklist's decline, Cole resided primarily in San Francisco, where he taught screenwriting and related courses for several years at San Francisco State College (now San Francisco State University).[29] He also contributed film reviews to People's World, a publication affiliated with the Communist Party USA, reflecting his ongoing political engagement.[5] Earlier, in 1961, he had relocated to London in an attempt to resume professional work amid persistent U.S. industry barriers, taking on various manual labor roles upon his return stateside, including short-order cook and waiter.[9] Cole published his autobiography, Hollywood Red: The Autobiography of Lester Cole, in 1981, detailing his career, HUAC defiance, imprisonment, and blacklist experiences from a self-described Marxist perspective.[7][6] He maintained his ideological commitments, concluding the book with a reaffirmation of Marxist principles despite personal and professional hardships.[6] On August 15, 1985, Cole died of a heart attack at the University of California Medical Center in San Francisco, at age 81.[6][1][9] He was the third member of the Hollywood Ten to pass away that year, following Albert Maltz in April and Alvah Bessie in July, leaving only two survivors from the group.[2] Cole was survived by his son Michael, a sister, and two grandchildren.[6]

Historical Evaluations and Debates

Cole's refusal to testify before the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) on October 30, 1947, positioned him as a symbol of resistance against perceived McCarthyist overreach, with early evaluations portraying the Hollywood Ten as principled defenders of artistic freedom and the First Amendment. Supporters, including civil liberties advocates, argued that questioning political affiliations infringed on constitutional protections, leading to his contempt citation and one-year imprisonment beginning in June 1950. However, subsequent revelations, including declassified FBI records assigning Cole Communist Party USA membership number 47226, confirmed his active involvement since approximately 1934, challenging narratives of innocuous sympathy.[30] In his 1981 autobiography Hollywood Red: The Autobiography of Lester Cole, he explicitly admitted his Communist Party membership and affirmed that all Hollywood Ten members had been enrolled at various points, presenting it as a commitment to social justice amid the Great Depression rather than allegiance to Soviet directives.[3] This self-disclosure, published amid waning Cold War tensions, shifted some scholarly assessments toward acknowledging the group's ideological discipline, including adherence to party lines that defended Stalin's policies, such as the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact.[31] Critics, drawing on archival evidence of CPUSA's subordination to Moscow and documented espionage cases like those revealed by the Venona Project, contend that Cole's evasion during hearings evaded accountability for potential subversive influence in screenwriting, where propaganda could subtly shape public opinion.[32] Debates over the blacklist's justification continue to divide historians. Left-leaning interpretations, prevalent in academia and media, emphasize its chilling effect on dissent, equating it to censorship despite the industry's voluntary enforcement post-1947 Waldorf Statement.[33] In contrast, defenses rooted in causal analysis of Soviet penetration—evidenced by CPUSA advocacy for overthrowing capitalism and infiltration of unions like the Screen Writers Guild, co-founded by Cole in 1933—view the blacklist as cultural self-defense against a totalitarian ideology responsible for millions of deaths under Stalin.[34] Cole's post-blacklist pseudonymous credits and unyielding socialism until his death on August 15, 1985, underscore evaluations of him as an unreconstructed ideologue whose legacy embodies the tension between individual conviction and collective security imperatives.[6] Mainstream retrospectives often understate these affiliations, reflecting institutional biases that prioritize free-expression martyr narratives over empirical scrutiny of communist threats.[31]

Filmography

Key Screen Credits

Lester Cole's screenwriting career spanned over four decades, with contributions to more than 40 films, primarily in the 1930s through 1960s, often focusing on adventure, war, and drama genres.[6] [35] His pre-blacklist credits established him as a prolific writer for studios like Warner Bros. and MGM, while post-blacklist works were frequently produced under pseudonyms due to industry exclusion following his 1950 contempt of Congress conviction and imprisonment.[7] The Writers Guild of America later restored several credits, acknowledging his authorship.[36] Key pre-blacklist screenplays include Objective, Burma! (1945), a World War II action film directed by Raoul Walsh and starring Errol Flynn as a paratrooper leading a mission behind enemy lines, which earned praise for its realistic combat depictions. Cole co-wrote the script with Ranald MacDougall, adapting elements from real OSS operations. Another notable credit was High Wall (1947), a psychological thriller directed by Curtis Bernhardt, featuring Robert Taylor as an amnesiac veteran accused of murder; the film explored shell shock and courtroom drama, reflecting Cole's interest in mental health themes post-World War II. He also contributed to The Romance of Rosy Ridge (1947), a post-Civil War romance starring Van Johnson and Janet Leigh, which addressed lingering sectional tensions in Missouri. During and after the blacklist era, Cole's output diminished but included pseudonymous works. His screenplay for Born Free (1966), a family adventure film about orphaned lion cubs raised by humans in Kenya, was initially credited to "Gerald L.C. Copley" and earned two Academy Awards, including Best Original Score; the Writers Guild corrected the credit to Cole on April 1, 1997, recognizing his adaptation of Joy Adamson's novel amid efforts to rehabilitate blacklisted writers' legacies.[36] Other post-blacklist credits under aliases encompassed Operation Eichmann (1961), a docudrama recounting the Nazi war criminal's capture, scripted for Allied Artists, and Chain Lightning (1950), an aviation drama starring Humphrey Bogart, written during the early blacklist period.[37] These films demonstrated Cole's versatility in adapting true events and novels while navigating professional barriers.[21]
