Geoffrey Standing Bear

Geoffrey Standing Bear is an Osage Nation lawyer and politician who has served as Principal Chief since 2014, currently in his third term.[1] The great-grandson of former Principal Chief Fred Lookout, he previously practiced federal Indian law for 34 years, specializing in cases including early Indian gaming litigation in Oklahoma, and earned professional recognitions such as inclusion in Best Lawyers in America and Oklahoma Super Lawyers.[1] Standing Bear held prior tribal roles as Assistant Principal Chief from 1990 to 1994 and as a member of the Osage Nation Congress from 2010 to 2014 before his election to the chief position.[1] Under his leadership, the Osage Nation government became debt-free, pursued expansion of its land base, and emphasized initiatives to preserve cultural traditions and revive the Osage language.[2][1] His administration has faced internal political opposition, including congressional rejection of proposals for minerals estate self-governance and criticism regarding certain land acquisitions and leadership decisions.[3][4]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Geoffrey Standing Bear was born on May 11, 1953, into the Osage Nation in Oklahoma.[5] His father, George Standing Bear, was of mixed Lakota and Osage ancestry, while his grandfather, Eugene Standing Bear, was Lakota and married into an Osage family.[5] Standing Bear's grandmother, Mary Lookout Standing Bear, was the daughter of Osage Principal Chief Fred Lookout, who served from the 1920s until 1949, making Standing Bear the chief's great-grandson.[5][6] Standing Bear was raised primarily in Tulsa, Oklahoma, within the local Osage community, where his family maintained connections to tribal heritage through the use of Osage words, though not full fluency in the language.[6] He lived with his grandmother, Mary Lookout Standing Bear, whose influence reinforced his ties to Osage traditions and history.[6] From a young age, he heard family accounts of the Osage's oil-derived wealth in the early 20th century, as well as the pervasive fear of assassination during the Reign of Terror, a period of targeted murders against prosperous Osage individuals; his grandmother's reticence on the topic exemplified this generational trauma.[5] The family's affluence was evident in possessions like a Duesenberg automobile, reflecting the headright system that distributed mineral rights among Osage members.[5] Later in his youth, Standing Bear relocated to Pawhuska, the Osage Nation's capital, and at age 16 began driving his grandmother to community luncheons, where interactions with survivors of the Reign of Terror provided firsthand insights into Osage resilience amid historical violence.[5] His grandfather Eugene had attended the Haskell Indian School, underscoring the family's navigation of federal assimilation policies alongside Osage sovereignty efforts.[5] These experiences shaped Standing Bear's early understanding of tribal governance, economic self-determination, and the causal links between resource wealth, external predation, and internal fortitude.[5]

Academic and Early Professional Training

Standing Bear graduated from Bishop Kelley High School in Tulsa, Oklahoma.[6] He attended the University of Oklahoma, earning a bachelor's degree in 1976 with studies in sociology, psychology, and anthropology; during his sophomore year, he served as president of the American Indian Student Association and pursued independent research on concepts of nations within nations.[4][6] Standing Bear then obtained his Juris Doctor from the University of Tulsa College of Law in 1980, where he contributed to establishing the Native American law program under Professor Rennard Strickland and received mentorship in federal Indian law.[6][4] In law school, he interned with the Muscogee (Creek) Nation, gaining initial exposure to tribal legal matters.[6] Starting in 1982, he joined the Muscogee (Creek) Nation as an attorney, litigating gaming rights cases and supporting re-establishment of tribal courts and police forces.[6] His early practice centered on federal Indian law, encompassing pioneering litigation under the Indian Child Welfare Act and Oklahoma's inaugural federal Indian gaming cases, along with representation of tribes including the Seneca-Cayuga Tribe; he also acted as General Counsel to the Oklahoma Indian Gaming Association.[4][5]

Legal Career

Specialization in Federal Indian Law

Standing Bear practiced law for 34 years prior to his election as Principal Chief of the Osage Nation in 2014, with a primary concentration in federal Indian law.[1] His expertise encompassed tribal sovereignty, gaming rights, and related federal regulatory matters affecting Native American tribes.[6] During law school at the University of Tulsa, from which he graduated in 1980, Standing Bear interned with the Muscogee (Creek) Nation. In 1982, he joined the Muscogee (Creek) Nation as an attorney, where he worked to establish tribal gaming rights, as well as authority for tribal courts and police.[6] He represented multiple tribes, including the Muscogee (Creek) Nation and the Seneca-Cayuga Tribe, in pioneering Indian gaming cases in Oklahoma, including involvement in the state's first federal gaming litigation.[1] Standing Bear also served as General Counsel for the Oklahoma Indian Gaming Association, advocating for tribal gaming policies and their economic impacts.[6] Early in his career, Standing Bear was among the initial attorneys to litigate under the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978, addressing placement and custody issues for Native American children.[4] His work in federal Indian law earned national recognition, including selection to Best Lawyers in America, inclusion in Oklahoma Super Lawyers, and a listing in Chambers and Partners.[1]

Notable Legal Contributions and Recognition

Standing Bear practiced law for 34 years, specializing in federal Indian law.[1][5] His notable contributions included representing tribes in Oklahoma's inaugural Indian gaming cases, such as those involving the Muscogee (Creek) Nation and Seneca-Cayuga Tribe, which helped establish legal precedents for tribal gaming operations under federal law.[1][5] He also served as General Counsel to the Oklahoma Indian Gaming Association, advising on regulatory and litigation matters related to tribal sovereignty in gaming.[1] Additionally, he was among the early litigators addressing issues under the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978, contributing to the application of federal protections for Native American children in adoption and custody proceedings.[4] For his expertise, Standing Bear received national recognition from Best Lawyers in America, inclusion in Oklahoma Super Lawyers, and a listing in Chambers and Partners for Native American law.[1][5]

Political Career

Early Roles in Tribal Governance

Standing Bear's initial foray into Osage tribal governance occurred under the pre-2006 constitutional framework, where he served as Assistant Principal Chief of the Osage Tribe from 1990 to 1994.[1][5] This position placed him in the executive branch alongside Principal Chief Fred Tommie, during a period when voting rights were restricted to approximately 5,000 Osage mineral estate shareholders out of a total enrolled population exceeding 21,000, a system Standing Bear sought to reform due to its exclusionary nature.[6] His involvement stemmed from advocacy against these limitations, which had sparked controversy in the late 1980s, prompting his entry into politics to broaden participation in tribal decision-making.[6] The Osage Nation's governance underwent significant transformation with the enactment of a new constitution in 2006, facilitated by a 2004 U.S. congressional act that enabled a shift to a one-person, one-vote system, expanding democratic access beyond mineral owners.[6] Standing Bear capitalized on this reform by running for office under the updated structure, securing election to the Osage Nation Congress for the term spanning 2010 to 2014.[1][7] In this legislative role, he contributed to the Nation's transitional governance, focusing on issues like jurisdictional clarity in federal Indian law cases, though specific legislative achievements from this period are tied to broader legal advocacy rather than isolated bills.[5] This congressional service directly preceded his successful 2014 campaign for Principal Chief, building on his prior executive experience to position him as a continuity figure amid the new constitutional era.[7]

Elections and Rise to Principal Chief

Standing Bear entered Osage tribal governance in the early 1990s, serving as Assistant Principal Chief from 1990 to 1994 under Principal Chief Jim Ross.[1] After a period focused on his legal practice, he returned to politics by winning election to the Osage Nation Congress, representing District Five from 2010 to 2014.[1] In the 2014 election cycle, Standing Bear announced his candidacy for Principal Chief, emphasizing his legal expertise in federal Indian law and commitment to tribal sovereignty. He secured the Democratic primary on March 18, 2014, with 1,080 votes, representing 60.47% of the vote, advancing over challengers including Margo Gray.[8] In the general election on June 2, 2014, he defeated Gray decisively, receiving 1,615 votes to her 652, or 71.24% of the total.[9][10] Standing Bear was inaugurated as Principal Chief on July 5, 2014, succeeding Scott BigHorse.[11] Standing Bear won re-election in the June 2018 general election, securing a second four-year term as Principal Chief.[6][12] He sought a third term in 2022, advancing through the April 4 primary before facing Joe Tillman in the June 6 general election.[13] In a closely contested race, Standing Bear prevailed by 86 votes, with official results showing 749 votes to Tillman's 663 across absentee, early, and election-day tallies.[14][15] This victory extended his leadership amid ongoing efforts to strengthen Osage economic and cultural initiatives.[16]

Tenure as Principal Chief

Geoffrey Standing Bear was elected Principal Chief of the Osage Nation on June 2, 2014, defeating incumbent John Red Eagle in a runoff election.[17] He was sworn into office on July 2, 2014, becoming the fourth Principal Chief under the 2006 Osage Nation Constitution.[11] Standing Bear's initial appointments included eight key positions in September 2014, subject to congressional confirmation, signaling early efforts to build an administrative team focused on tribal governance.[18] Standing Bear secured reelection on June 4, 2018, for a second four-year term, alongside Assistant Principal Chief Raymond Red Corn.[19] His third term followed victory in the June 6, 2022, general election.[16] Throughout his tenure, he has convened special sessions of the Osage Nation Congress, such as the November 2024 fifth special session of the Fourth Congress and a planned November 2025 session for the Ninth Congress, to address legislative priorities.[20][21] A hallmark of Standing Bear's leadership has been fiscal prudence, culminating in the July 8, 2021, announcement that the Osage Nation government achieved debt-free status.[2] In response to economic pressures, he proposed a 10% wage increase for Osage Nation employees on March 10, 2022, to counter inflation's impact on living costs.[22] By 2025, Standing Bear reported the Nation's continued prosperity amid federal government shifts, underscoring adaptive governance.[23] His administration has earned recognition, including Oklahoma Magazine's 2017 Oklahoman of the Year award for effective tribal leadership.[1]

Policies and Initiatives

Economic Development and Self-Reliance

Under Principal Chief Geoffrey Standing Bear's leadership, the Osage Nation has pursued economic development through a 25-year strategic vision emphasizing diversification beyond traditional mineral revenues from oil and gas headrights, toward sustainable enterprises owned and operated by tribal members. More than half of the initiatives outlined in the original 2007 plan have been completed, with the 2020-2025 update prioritizing Osage-owned businesses in sectors such as drilling services, trucking, tourism, and manufacturing facilities on reservation lands.[24] This approach aims to create a viable economy driven by internal revenue streams, including investments in alternative energy and expanded agriculture for both food security and income generation, such as hemp cultivation and bison herds.[25] Key programs include the launch of the Osage Nation Small Business Program on July 18, 2024, which allocates $5 million in loans and equity investments ranging from $10,000 to $20 million for small businesses in Osage County and those owned by Osage tribal members nationwide, supported by the U.S. Treasuryâ€™s State Small Business Credit Initiative.[26] Standing Bear described this as a strategy to "build a more successful economy" by fostering job creation, financial education, and access to capital in diverse sectors like art, fashion, and food.[26] Complementary infrastructure efforts involve partnerships with local governments for broadband expansion, community healthcare, senior housing, water systems, and road improvements to support broader economic growth.[27] Self-reliance initiatives focus on reducing dependence on federal oversight, exemplified by agricultural projects like Harvest Land, which produces over 6,000 pounds of vegetables annually through hydroponics, aquaponics (yielding 80,000 lettuce heads and 6,000 pounds of fish per year), and a 5-acre orchard of traditional crops, supplying schools and elders.[28] The Osage Ranch manages 3,000 cattle across 43,000 acres, with a new meat-processing facility built using CARES Act funds to ensure food sovereignty, as Standing Bear noted: "Weâ€™ve got to eat, and if we are producing our own food, [we can ensure] that it is healthy, safe, and tasty."[28] These efforts extend to governance reforms, including a 2015 congressional resolution for self-governance and ongoing pushes for full control of the mineral estate by fiscal year 2026, transferring leasing, permitting, and production from the Bureau of Indian Affairs amid proposed federal budget cuts of nearly $1 billion to tribal programs.[29][30] During the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, the Nation independently constructed facilities for meat and produce distribution when external supplies faltered, underscoring Standing Bear's advocacy for tribal self-sufficiency as a "true partnership" with the federal government rather than reliance on it.[31]

Cultural Preservation and Sovereignty

During his tenure as Principal Chief, Geoffrey Standing Bear has prioritized the revitalization of the Osage language as a core element of cultural preservation, announcing in early 2015 that language immersion would be a key focus to achieve fluency among tribal members.[32] The Osage Nation Language Department has advanced this through structured programs defining fluency benchmarks, supplemented by instructors from related tribes like Ponca and UmÃ³nhon, and practical applications such as children reciting prayers in Osage during family dinners to build emotional ties to the language.[33][23] In 2017, the Nation launched a mobile app to expand language access and retention, aligning with Standing Bear's goal of integrating Osage into daily use.[34] Youth-led innovations, including Osage-language keyboards for smartphones, further support these efforts, emphasizing language as a carrier of cultural knowledge.[35] Standing Bear's administration has also sustained traditional practices through community events, arts initiatives, and site preservation, such as the 2015 commitment to maintain the nearly century-old Hominy ceremonial roundhouse for dances, ceremonies, songs, and communal meals that reinforce Osage identity.[36] Programs in ribbonwork, puppet shows, ballet, and museum exhibits address historical trauma while promoting self-expression, with Standing Bear collaborating on the 2023 film Killers of the Flower Moon to ensure authentic depictions of Osage language, ceremonies, and customs, involving community members in production.[5][35] Annual events like Osage Sovereignty Day integrate cultural elements, such as cradleboard demonstrations, to blend preservation with sovereignty celebrations.[37] On sovereignty, Standing Bear frames internal dimensions as self-sufficiency in culture and resources, exemplified by food sovereignty initiatives under the Harvest Land program, which includes 44,000 square feet of greenhouses, a 9,000-square-foot aquaponics processing facility, and a 19,000-square-foot butcher house processing 75 cattle or bison monthly from a herd of 2,000 cattle and 250 bison.[5][35] These efforts, accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic to combat food insecurity in food deserts, enable online ordering and delivery of culturally significant, healthy foods from tribally managed lands, including 43,000 acres reacquired using casino revenues and federal funds like CARES Act allocations.[38] Externally, the Nation under Standing Bear joined the Coalition of Large Tribes in 2025 for collective advocacy on federal policies, while navigating Bureau of Indian Affairs lease issues and upholding the 2006 constitutional government now in its 19th year.[23] This pragmatic approach balances independence with federal engagement to protect land integrity and cultural practices.[35]

Land Management and Resource Stewardship

During his tenure as Principal Chief, Geoffrey Standing Bear has prioritized Osage Nation sovereignty over its 1.47 million-acre reservation, particularly the management of the Osage Mineral Estate, which encompasses subsurface rights to oil, gas, and other minerals underlying much of the territory.[30] Standing Bear advocated for transitioning non-trust functions of mineral estate oversight from the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) to the Osage Minerals Council (OMC), proposing in June 2025 a plan that included hiring permitting staff, personnel, and technicians experienced in oil and gas production to enhance efficiency amid anticipated federal budget cuts.[39] [40] This initiative aimed to fulfill the Nation's perpetual obligation under its code to preserve the mineral estate while enabling direct control over leasing and development.[41] However, in September 2025, the Osage Nation Congress rejected the proposal in a 9-2 vote, citing concerns over the OMC's readiness to assume full responsibilities without BIA support.[3] Standing Bear has also pursued legal measures to assert control, including a August 2024 lawsuit against the U.S. Department of the Interior for delays in negotiations related to resource governance, reflecting broader tensions with federal oversight.[42] In February 2023, he opposed proposed federal oil and gas rules, arguing they would impose excessive regulatory burdens and reduce production revenues critical to the Nation's economy.[43] These efforts underscore a strategy balancing resource extraction for economic self-reliance with stewardship, as the mineral estate generates substantial headrights payments to Osage citizens. On environmental fronts, Standing Bear's administration has addressed legacy pollution from extractive activities, launching initiatives to remediate orphaned oil and gas wells that pose health and ecological risks across the reservation. By September 2023, the Nation had plugged numerous such wells, protecting surface lands and waters from contamination while leveraging federal grants for cleanup.[44] In October 2024, the Osage Nation partnered with the U.S. Department of Energy on methane emissions reduction programs, focusing on workforce training to improve monitoring and mitigation on tribal lands.[45] Additionally, Standing Bear supported the 2016 Standing Rock protests against the Dakota Access Pipeline, emphasizing tribal rights to safeguard ancestral waters and lands from industrial threats.[46] Regarding water resources, he has criticized federal encroachments on Osage water rights and proposed developing sovereign treatment facilities to secure clean supplies, as highlighted in July 2024 discussions on quantifying and protecting reservation waterways.[47] Standing Bear's policies extend to cultural and ecological preservation, including recognition for stewardship of the Eleven Point Wild and Scenic River within Osage ancestral territories; in January 2023, collaborative efforts with the U.S. Forest Service earned awards for river protection initiatives.[48] Overall, these measures reflect a commitment to sustainable resource use, prioritizing tribal autonomy over federal dependencies while mitigating environmental degradation from historical and ongoing extraction.

Controversies and Criticisms

Governance and Internal Disputes

During Standing Bear's tenure as Principal Chief, the Osage Nation's governance has been marked by several internal conflicts between the executive and legislative branches, often centered on constitutional interpretations, budgetary appropriations, and authority over the mineral estate. These disputes have frequently escalated to the Osage Nation Supreme Court, highlighting tensions in the separation of powers under the tribe's 2006 Constitution. For instance, in November 2015, Standing Bear filed a constitutional challenge against the Osage Nation Congress, seeking declaratory judgment on laws that he argued encroached on executive authority.[49] This was followed by a second lawsuit in November 2016, where Standing Bear contested appropriation bills that bundled unrelated expenditures, claiming they violated constitutional requirements for single-subject legislation and forced his approval under threat of government shutdown.[50] In the 2017 case Standing Bear v. Pratt, the Supreme Court addressed jurisdictional questions arising from executive challenges to congressional enactments, affirming its original authority to resolve inter-branch disputes through declaratory judgments while interpreting specific laws in light of constitutional provisions on veto power and appointments.[51] The ruling underscored ongoing frictions, as the executive vetoed bills perceived as overreaching, prompting legislative overrides or further litigation. Similarly, in 2016, the Supreme Court ruled that the Osage Minerals Council constitutes a governmental body subject to open records laws, a decision Standing Bear praised as protecting headright owners' interests against opaque operations, though it fueled debates over the council's autonomy from both branches.[52] Legislative opposition persisted into the 2020s, exemplified by public rebukes from congressmen Eli Potts, Joe Tillman, and John Maker in March 2021, who accused the Standing Bear administration of illegally retaliating against executive employees through investigations and reassignments, potentially violating tribal employment protections.[53][54] These claims highlighted administrative governance strains, with critics alleging politicized personnel actions amid broader policy disagreements. In September 2025, the 9th Osage Nation Congress rejected Standing Bear's proposalâ€”by a 9-2 voteâ€”to transfer non-trust mineral estate functions to the Minerals Council for enhanced self-governance, citing constitutional limits on executive-directed restructuring; the Minerals Council itself opposed the plan via resolution, intensifying inter-entity rivalries over resource control.[3] Transparency issues have also contributed to internal discord, as seen in June 2021 when Osage News pursued legal access to records of Standing Bear's appointees to the Small Business CARES Act Assistance Committee, arguing the appointments lacked public disclosure required for governmental bodies.[55] Such episodes reflect recurring critiques of executive opacity, though Standing Bear's administration has defended actions as necessary for efficient sovereignty exercises amid federal constraints. Overall, these disputes demonstrate a robust, if contentious, system of checks and balances, with the Supreme Court frequently arbitrating to maintain constitutional equilibrium.[23]

Administrative and Ethical Challenges

During his tenure as Principal Chief, Geoffrey Standing Bear faced administrative challenges stemming from internal tribal governance disputes, particularly with the Osage Nation Congress and the Osage Minerals Council. In March 2021, three congressmen publicly questioned the administration's management of the tribe's financial assets, including headright distributions and investment strategies, alleging insufficient transparency and oversight in executive decision-making.[54] They also criticized Standing Bear's frequent use of executive orders to bypass legislative processes, arguing that such actions undermined congressional authority and the separation of powers outlined in the Osage Nation Constitution.[54] These tensions escalated into legal confrontations, with Standing Bear filing lawsuits against the Congress on multiple occasions. In 2016, he sued the Fourth Osage Nation Congress in the Supreme Court, claiming constitutional violations in appropriation bills that allegedly forced his signature under duress by embedding unrelated funding; the court ruled in his favor on jurisdictional grounds but highlighted ongoing executive-legislative friction.[50] A prior 2016 case, Standing Bear v. Pratt, further affirmed the Supreme Court's role in resolving declaratory judgments between branches, underscoring administrative bottlenecks in policy implementation.[51] Administrative hurdles extended to resource management, where Standing Bear's push for greater tribal control over the mineral estate clashed with the Osage Minerals Council. In September 2025, the Congress rejected his proposalâ€”passed by a 9-2 voteâ€”to transfer non-trust functions to the Council amid federal Bureau of Indian Affairs downsizing, citing concerns over accountability and potential overreach.[3] Earlier efforts, including a 2016 Supreme Court ruling classifying the Council as a governmental body subject to Osage law, reflected Standing Bear's strategy to consolidate authority, though opponents viewed it as an erosion of the Council's semi-autonomous status established under federal oversight.[52][56] Ethical critiques, while less substantiated by formal investigations, arose in political discourse, including during the 2022 election where challenger Scott Tillman accused Standing Bear's administration of lacking visionary leadership despite high activity levels, implying mismanagement in thriving amid resource wealth.[57] No major corruption probes or ethics violations have been documented against Standing Bear personally, with disputes largely framed as interpretive battles over sovereignty and fiscal prudence rather than personal malfeasance; local reporting, such as from the Pawhuska Journal-Capital, notes these as partisan challenges from congressional minorities aligned against executive policies.[54]

Federal Relations and Policy Clashes

During Standing Bear's tenure as Principal Chief, the Osage Nation pursued expanded self-governance over its federally held mineral estate, which generates significant revenue from oil and gas production but remains subject to Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) oversight under the 1906 Osage Allotment Act.[42] In March 2024, the Nation filed a federal lawsuit against the U.S. Department of the Interior (DOI), alleging excessive oversight and delays in negotiations for self-governance authority under the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (ISDEAA) of 1975.[42] The suit sought to compel the DOI to transfer administrative functions for mineral estate management, including leasing and production, to the Osage Minerals Council, criticizing federal "stalling tactics" and micromanagement as violations of tribal sovereignty.[42] Standing Bear described these delays as a pattern in Indian Country, emphasizing the Nation's readiness to handle $1.3 million in annual federal funding for such functions without compromising trust responsibilities.[39] The lawsuit, filed on March 11, 2024, in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia, highlighted stalled ISDEAA compacting talks initiated years earlier, with the DOI rejecting the Nation's final offer despite statutory timelines for resolution.[58] Standing Bear argued that federal preemption of state interference in mineral operations required prompt self-governance transfer to maintain efficiency and autonomy.[42] Although the court dismissed the case in September 2025 for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, the action underscored ongoing tensions over federal control of the estate, which the BIA has managed since severing it from tribal oversight in the early 20th century.[59] Concurrently, Standing Bear proposed redirecting mineral permitting, leasing, and accounting to the Osage Minerals Council for fiscal year 2026, amid proposed federal budget cuts of nearly $1 billion to tribal programs, including $700 million from the BIA.[30] Federal efficiency initiatives under the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) exacerbated clashes, particularly with plans to close the BIA's Osage Agency in Pawhuska, Oklahoma, by September 30, 2025, which handles critical trust functions like oil and gas permitting.[60] Standing Bear condemned the abrupt closures of four Oklahoma BIA offices as "organized chaos" and a deliberate effort to "reshape the trust relationship," citing inadequate tribal consultation and risks to lease approvals essential for Osage Countyâ€™s $2 billion annual energy production.[61] He negotiated a new lease to keep the Pawhuska office operational, averting immediate disruption but highlighting broader concerns over federal treaty obligations.[62] These moves aligned with President Trump's fiscal year 2026 budget proposals, which Standing Bear viewed as threats to sovereignty, prompting the Nation to prioritize internal self-reliance while monitoring federal downsizing.[23] Standing Bear also critiqued U.S. Supreme Court rulings like Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta (2022), which expanded state prosecutorial jurisdiction over non-Indians on tribal lands, arguing it undermined federal plenary authority and Osage self-governance.[63] He maintained that such decisions ignored historical federal protections, including the Osage's unique mineral trust, and called for legislative or judicial reaffirmation of exclusive tribal-federal jurisdiction to preserve resource stewardship.[63] Despite these frictions, Standing Bear noted the Nation's programs largely operated independently of federal agencies during the October 2025 government shutdown, demonstrating fiscal resilience from mineral revenues.[64]
