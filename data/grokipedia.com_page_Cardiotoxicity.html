Cardiotoxicity

Cardiotoxicity is toxicity that affects the heart.[1] It can manifest as heart failure, arrhythmia, myocarditis, and cardiomyopathy in patients.[2] Some effects are reversible, while in others, permanent damage requiring further treatment may arise. The heart becomes weaker and is not as efficient in pumping blood. Cardiotoxicity may be caused by chemotherapy (a usual example is the class of anthracyclines)[3][4] treatment and/or radiotherapy;[5] complications from anorexia nervosa;[6] adverse effects of heavy metals intake;[7] the long-term abuse of or ingestion at high doses of certain strong stimulants such as cocaine;[8] or an incorrectly administered drug such as bupivacaine.[9]

Mechanism

Many mechanisms have been proposed to explain cardiotoxicity. While differing etiologies may share mechanisms, they generally depend on the agent inducing cardiac damage. For example, a common mechanism, particularly for anthracyclines, is oxidative stress on cardiac myocytes.[4] Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can overwhelm the antioxidant defenses of cardiac cells, causing direct cellular damage. This oxidative damage can disrupt mitochondrial function, thereby disrupting energy production in the heart muscle, leading to energy depletion via depleted ATP and promoting cell death through apoptosis or necrosis.[10] Other mechanisms of cardiotoxicity include inflammation[11], DNA damaging, and disrupted cell signaling. DNA damage and disrupted cellular signaling are proposed mechanisms for many cardiotoxic chemotherapeutics.[12] Regardless of the mechanism, clinical manifestations include heart failure, arrhythmia, myocarditis, and cardiomyopathy that can be permanent.[2] These conditions can greatly alter mortality and morbidity in patients, meaning careful monitoring is necessary in patients exposed to cardiotoxic agents.

Inciting agents

The list of inciting agents is vast and involves various classes of medication as well as environmental agents. The effects of the cardiotoxic substances vary and are not all identical.

Chemotherapy drugs

[13]
  • Anthracyclines, such as doxorubicin
  • Alkylating agents, such as cyclophosphamide
  • HER2 inhibitors
  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors
  • Antimetabolites
  • Proteasome inhibitors
  • Immunotherapies, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., nivolumab, pembrolizumab)11

Other medications

  • Antipsychotics such as thioridazine which can prolong QT interval [14]
  • Antibiotics such as erythromycin and levofloxacin due to QT prolongation[15]
  • Local anesthetics, such as bupivacaine[9]

Environmental toxins

  • Heavy metals such as lead and mercury[16]
  • Pesticides such as organophosphates [17]

Abused substances

[18]
  • Alcohol: chronic heavy consumption leading to alcoholic cardiomyopathy
  • Recreational drugs: cocaine, methamphetamine

Others

  • Biological toxins such as diphtheria toxin[19]
  • Radiation therapy is known to cause radiation-induced heart disease (RIHD) [20]
These agents can lead to varying degrees of cardiotoxicity, and their effects may be dose-dependent and influenced by individual factors such as pre-existing cardiovascular disease and genetic predispositions that can foster greater sensitivity to any cardiac damage.

Treatment

The most effective initial treatment is to discontinue exposure to the inciting agent as soon as possible, whether pharmacologic or environmental. While some patients may fully recover from cardiotoxicity, many experience permanent damage requiring ongoing management. Management depends on the specific damage but generally follows established guidelines for conditions such as heart failure, arrhythmias, and myocarditis, including the use of ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and diuretics for heart failure symptoms.[21] For cancer therapy-related cardiotoxicity, a multidisciplinary cardio-oncology approach is recommended.[22] For specific etiologies, treatments may include chelation therapy for heavy metal exposure.[23] Patients receiving anthracyclines may be administered dexrazoxane concurrently as a cardioprotective agent to prevent extensive cardiac damage.[24]
