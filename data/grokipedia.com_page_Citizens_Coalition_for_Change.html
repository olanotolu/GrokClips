Citizens Coalition for Change

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) is a Zimbabwean opposition political party launched on 25 January 2022 by Nelson Chamisa and former members of the Movement for Democratic Change Alliance following its effective dissolution.[1][2] Positioned as the primary challenger to the ruling ZANU–PF, the CCC advocates for systemic political and economic reforms amid widespread public discontent with governance.[3] In the 2023 general elections, it secured second place but contested the results, citing irregularities and lack of transparency.[3] Chamisa served as its de facto leader until resigning on 25 January 2024, alleging infiltration and hijacking by impostors, which precipitated internal fragmentation, parliamentary recalls, and leadership disputes.[1][2]

Origins and Formation

Emergence from MDC Alliance splits

The Movement for Democratic Change Alliance (MDC Alliance), formed in 2018 under Nelson Chamisa's leadership after Morgan Tsvangirai's death, fractured amid leadership disputes and legal challenges from a rival faction led by Douglas Mwonzora. In November 2019, Mwonzora's group organized an extraordinary congress that ousted Chamisa as party president, citing procedural irregularities in his 2018 ascension and lack of formal elections. This triggered parallel party structures, with Chamisa's supporters rejecting the move and continuing under the MDC Alliance name, while Mwonzora aligned with the remnants of the original MDC-T.[4] The rift deepened in March 2020 when Zimbabwe's Supreme Court ruled in favor of Mwonzora's faction, affirming his role as secretary-general and declaring Chamisa's presidency invalid due to the absence of a properly convened congress under the party's constitution. Chamisa dismissed the judgment as politically motivated and maintained control over the MDC Alliance's grassroots base, but the decision enabled Mwonzora's group to claim party assets, including funding and headquarters, exacerbating operational chaos. By-elections from 2020 onward highlighted the divisions, as candidates from both factions competed, with Chamisa's slate securing victories despite allegations of electoral manipulation and internal sabotage.[4][5] Facing persistent infiltration by ZANU-PF elements—whom Chamisa accused of hijacking structures through proxies like Mwonzora—and legal threats over the party name, Chamisa dissolved the MDC Alliance's formal apparatus in January 2022. On January 24, 2022, he publicly announced the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) as a rebranded, citizen-driven alternative, emphasizing decentralized leadership to circumvent entrenched disputes and alleged state meddling. The launch rally in Harare on February 20, 2022, marked CCC's debut, positioning it as a fresh vehicle for opposition to the ruling ZANU-PF ahead of the 2023 elections, with early by-election successes validating the shift.[6][7]

Official launch and early mobilization

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) was officially launched by Nelson Chamisa on January 24, 2022, at the Bronte Hotel in Harare, marking a rebranding from the MDC Alliance amid leadership disputes resolved by a court ruling in favor of rival faction leader Douglas Mwonzora.[8] Chamisa positioned the new entity as citizen-centered, declaring "the citizen is back at the center" with a focus on "one man, one vote," and pledged to deliver a "new great Zimbabwe" through hope, freedom, and practical solutions to issues like economic migration and lost dignity.[8] He framed the launch as a response to public demands to abandon a "dirty past," viewing the impending by-elections as a "starter" toward victory in the 2023 general elections.[8] CCC's early mobilization centered on the March 26, 2022, by-elections for parliamentary and local authority seats, where the party fielded candidates to test its organizational capacity and appeal beyond urban strongholds.[9] The effort yielded a strong performance, with CCC securing victories in multiple contests and demonstrating momentum against Zanu-PF incumbents, despite reports of electoral irregularities and violence.[10] This outcome bolstered confidence in the party's viability as a national opposition force. Building on by-election gains, CCC initiated the Mugwazo recruitment and mobilization drive on April 12, 2022, targeting rural areas long dominated by Zanu-PF through grassroots meetings and citizen engagement to expand membership and challenge ruling party hegemony.[11][10] The campaign rattled Zanu-PF structures, prompting internal ruling party responses, and underscored CCC's strategy of penetrating traditional strongholds via direct outreach rather than relying solely on established party machinery.[12]

Leadership and Internal Organization

Nelson Chamisa's role

Nelson Chamisa, previously the president of the Movement for Democratic Change Alliance (MDC Alliance), initiated the formation of the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) on January 24, 2022, by announcing a rebranding from the MDC Alliance amid internal disputes and legal challenges over party naming rights.[13] As the founding leader, Chamisa positioned CCC as a "platform of citizens" rather than a traditional political party, emphasizing broad-based mobilization against perceived governance failures under ZANU-PF rule.[14] He assumed the role of president without formal elections, centralizing authority through personal networks and public rallies that drew on his charismatic appeal as a Pentecostal preacher and former information technology minister.[15] Under Chamisa's leadership, CCC rapidly emerged as Zimbabwe's primary opposition force, contesting the March 2022 by-elections and securing victories in key urban seats, which served as a precursor to the 2023 general elections.[16] Chamisa served as the party's presidential candidate in the August 2023 elections, receiving 1,977,619 votes (44% of the total), according to Zimbabwe Electoral Commission figures, though he rejected the results as fraudulent and refused to concede.[1] His strategy focused on youth engagement, digital campaigning, and anti-corruption rhetoric, leveraging his personal popularity—polls prior to the election showed him leading incumbent Emmerson Mnangagwa by margins of up to 20 points in urban areas—to compensate for the party's lack of institutionalized mechanisms.[14] Chamisa's dominance within CCC manifested in unilateral decision-making, such as candidate selections based on loyalty oaths and informal endorsements rather than primaries, which critics attributed to a cult-of-personality dynamic that prioritized his vision of "God-given" leadership.[2] This approach enabled rapid organizational flexibility but exposed vulnerabilities, as evidenced by post-election recalls of CCC legislators by self-proclaimed secretary-general Sengezo Tshabangu starting in October 2023. On January 25, 2024—exactly two years after the party's formation—Chamisa resigned as president, declaring CCC "contaminated" and "hijacked" by ZANU-PF infiltrators, thereby dissolving his direct involvement and signaling a shift toward a new, undefined political movement.[1][17] His exit underscored the extent to which CCC's viability hinged on his personal agency, with no succession plan or deputy leadership in place.[15]

Absence of formal structures and resulting disputes

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) was established without a formal constitution, elected leadership bodies, or defined internal structures, a deliberate design attributed to leader Nelson Chamisa to minimize bureaucratic hurdles and resist infiltration by the ruling ZANU-PF party.[2] This "structureless" approach, however, facilitated internal disputes by creating ambiguity over authority and decision-making processes.[3] From its informal launch in January 2023, the party relied on Chamisa's personal directives for candidate selection and operations, leading to accusations of personalization and vulnerability to opportunistic challenges.[18] The absence of formalized mechanisms became acutely problematic in October 2023, when Sengezo Tshabangu, self-proclaimed as CCC's interim secretary-general, initiated the recall of over 20 opposition MPs and councillors, citing party disloyalty.[3] Without official records or a constitution to verify positions, Tshabangu's actions exploited the power vacuum, triggering legal battles and parliamentary by-elections that weakened CCC's representation. Chamisa denounced Tshabangu as an impostor and ZANU-PF proxy, but the lack of structures prevented swift internal resolution, amplifying factional rifts.[2] Government officials, including Local Government Minister Daniel Garwe, publicly criticized the setup on October 17, 2023, arguing it bred "confusion and conflict," and proposed legislation mandating political parties to adopt constitutions and structures.[19] These disputes escalated into broader leadership contests, with figures like Welshman Ncube challenging Tshabangu's influence in court by December 2024, highlighting ongoing battles over parliamentary CCC structures.[20] Critics, including opposition analysts, contended that the structureless model fostered a cult of personality around Chamisa, enabling infiltration and eroding democratic accountability, as evidenced by chaotic candidate processes ahead of the August 2023 elections.[2] By January 2024, Chamisa's abrupt exit from CCC amid the turmoil left the party fragmented, with no clear succession or governance framework to mitigate further infighting.[21]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Core principles and manifesto

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) released its election manifesto, titled A New Great Zimbabwe: Blueprint for Everyone, on August 7, 2023, ahead of the August 23 general elections, with a formal launch by leader Nelson Chamisa in Bulawayo on August 8.[22][23] The 100-page document outlines a vision for transforming Zimbabwe into a prosperous, God-fearing nation by 2028, targeting a US$100 billion economy and the creation of 5 million jobs over 10 years through citizen empowerment and moral renewal.[24][22] At its core, the manifesto prioritizes placing "God first and Citizens at the centre" to foster happiness, freedom, peace, unity, justice, and prosperity, drawing on biblical principles such as Psalm 82:3 ("Give justice to the weak and the fatherless; maintain the right of the afflicted and the destitute") and Luke 12:48 ("To whom much is given, much will be required").[22] It emphasizes a "moral revolution" and Christian democratic values, committing to govern on the basis of Judeo-Christian ethics, including restoring a God-fearing society, promoting integrity in leadership, and integrating religious tourism as an economic driver.[25][22] This approach extends to anti-corruption measures, rejecting kleptocracy, and ensuring accountability through transparent institutions and a lean cabinet limited to 15 ministers.[22] The manifesto's five main pillars—leadership, governance, economy, social services, and nation building—guide its policy pledges. In leadership and governance, it advocates for a new constitution, devolution of power, independent judiciary, and digital government to enhance rule of law and citizen participation.[22] Economic policies focus on macroeconomic stability with single-digit inflation, agricultural revival (targeting 33 million hectares under production at 10 tons per hectare yield), mining beneficiation, industrialization via a US$10 billion retooling fund, and positioning Zimbabwe as a global food supplier with 6,000 MW energy capacity by 2029.[22] Social services include universal free primary education, healthcare access, 2 million hectares for housing with title deeds, and poverty eradication programs, while nation-building pledges feature relocating the administrative capital to Gweru and developing Victoria Falls as a financial hub.[22] These commitments aim to fulfill historical liberation promises through inclusive, dignity-restoring reforms.[22]

Criticisms of ideological vagueness and personalization

Critics have argued that the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) exhibited ideological vagueness, prioritizing anti-incumbency sentiment over a defined policy framework. According to political analyst Alexander Rusero, while Nelson Chamisa garnered significant support, the party failed to articulate a clear platform beyond the goal of ousting ZANU-PF from power.[2] This ambiguity was compounded by the party's manifesto, titled "The Great New Zimbabwe" and released on August 8, 2023—just weeks before the general elections—which observers described as lacking substantive, original ideas and relying on broad promises of prosperity without detailed mechanisms.[2] [26] The CCC's approach has been further critiqued for its lack of substantive programmes, with civic activist Wellington Mbofana stating in a CIVICUS interview that the opposition's ideological ambivalence—masked by claims of electoral violence—stemmed from an inability to offer coherent policies resonating with voters, leading to reliance on disruption rather than vision.[27] This vagueness, detractors contend, reflected a broader failure to evolve beyond the Movement for Democratic Change's (MDC) protest-oriented legacy into a programmatically robust entity. A related criticism centers on the personalization of the party around Chamisa's charisma, with the deliberate absence of formal structures and a constitution fostering a cult-of-personality dynamic. Political analyst Ibbo Mandaza described this "structureless party" gamble as a failure, arguing it centralized authority in Chamisa while leaving the organization vulnerable to internal sabotage.[2] Former ZANU-PF minister Jonathan Moyo echoed this, asserting that a "structureless and constitutionless formation may work for a cultic church, but it can never work for a political party," a view that gained traction amid post-election recalls by Sengezo Tshabangu, who exploited the lack of verification mechanisms to oust over 100 CCC legislators and councillors.[2] Proponents of this strategy claimed it prevented ruling party infiltration, but critics maintained it undermined institutional resilience, enabling factionalism and eroding the party's credibility as a collective force rather than Chamisa's personal vehicle.[2]

Participation in 2023 General Elections

Campaign strategies and challenges

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) initiated its presidential campaign on July 16, 2023, in Gweru, under the slogan "For Everyone," with leader Nelson Chamisa emphasizing economic prosperity, poverty alleviation, unemployment reduction, and harnessing Zimbabwe's mineral resources for national benefit.[28] The party unveiled a 100-page manifesto in Bulawayo, titled "A New Great Zimbabwe Blueprint for Everyone," pledging to expand the economy to US$100 billion through targeted investments and reforms, alongside restoring Christian values in governance.[29][30] Campaign efforts focused on rural voters, traditionally aligned with ZANU-PF, while promoting vigilance against electoral irregularities.[28] To engage Zimbabwe's youth demographic, CCC leveraged TikTok for digital outreach, producing content that highlighted Chamisa's charisma, rally attendance, and relatability through videos set to popular Zimbabwean dancehall music and targeted hashtags.[31] These posts accused ZANU-PF of perpetuating socio-economic crises and urged voter registration to combat apathy, framing politics as accessible and entertaining.[31] Despite judicial and police bans on several rallies, the party persisted with alternative mobilizations, such as car rallies, to sustain momentum ahead of the August 23 voting date.[28][32] CCC encountered severe repression throughout the campaign, including widespread harassment, threats, and violence by ZANU-PF supporters that prevented multiple rallies and intimidated participants.[33] Police frequently deployed teargas against gatherings and arrested dozens of supporters; on August 17, 2023, 40 CCC members—predominantly young women—were detained after a car rally in Harare and held overnight before court.[32] A notable incident occurred on August 3, 2023, when CCC supporter Tinashe Chitsike was killed in an ambush by suspected ZANU-PF affiliates while traveling to a rally near Chinhoyi, prompting arrests of 10 suspects but highlighting unchecked aggression.[32][34] Human Rights Watch documented intensified suppression compared to 2018, with CCC activists facing assaults, arbitrary detentions, and judicial hurdles that diverted resources from campaigning to legal defenses.[35]

Election results and fraud allegations

In the presidential election held on August 23–24, 2023, the Zimbabwe Electoral Commission (ZEC) declared incumbent Emmerson Mnangagwa of ZANU–PF the winner with 52.6% of the vote (2,316,725 votes), while CCC leader Nelson Chamisa received 44% (1,841,020 votes).[36][37] In the concurrent National Assembly elections, CCC candidates secured a minority of seats, with ZANU–PF obtaining an outright majority, though exact figures for CCC varied amid subsequent by-elections triggered by recalls.[38] Chamisa immediately rejected the results as a "blatant and gigantic fraud," asserting that parallel vote tabulations by CCC agents demonstrated his victory and accusing ZEC of systemic manipulation, including ballot stuffing and suppression in urban opposition strongholds.[39][40] The party filed a Supreme Court petition on August 29, 2023, seeking to nullify the outcome, but it was dismissed on October 24, 2023, primarily on procedural grounds for failing to properly serve papers and provide verifiable evidence.[41] International observers documented irregularities supporting some allegations, including the EU Election Observation Mission's report of delayed ballot deliveries affecting up to 1.7 million voters in Harare and other CCC-leaning areas, inadequate voter education, and opaque collation processes that undermined credibility.[42] The Southern African Development Community (SADC) electoral assessment similarly highlighted failures in electoral administration and voter intimidation, though its summit later endorsed Mnangagwa's inauguration without mandating a rerun. Mnangagwa dismissed the fraud claims as baseless, attributing opposition grievances to electoral defeat.[41] CCC spokespersons reiterated demands for a rerun under reformed conditions, citing these issues as evidence of ZANU–PF's entrenched control over state institutions.[43]

Post-Election Crises

Sengezo Tshabangu's recalls of MPs

In October 2023, Sengezo Tshabangu, who proclaimed himself the interim secretary-general of the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) despite lacking formal endorsement from party leader Nelson Chamisa, initiated a series of recalls targeting CCC elected representatives, including members of parliament (MPs).[44][45] Tshabangu justified the actions as efforts to enforce internal party democracy, claiming that many recalled individuals had been imposed as candidates without primaries, in violation of CCC's supposed principles.[45] On October 3, 2023, he wrote to National Assembly Speaker Jacob Mudenda requesting the recall of 16 MPs and councillors, triggering legal and political upheaval within the opposition.[44] The recalls escalated rapidly, with Tshabangu notifying authorities of 14 MPs' removal by mid-October 2023, alongside nine senators and 17 councillors, prompting President Emmerson Mnangagwa to proclaim by-elections for December 9, 2023.[46] On November 14, 2023, he announced further recalls of 13 additional MPs and five senators, bringing the total affected MPs to at least 27.[47][48] CCC leadership, including Chamisa, rejected Tshabangu's authority, asserting the party operated without rigid structures and that recalls were illegitimate, potentially orchestrated to weaken opposition representation.[3] Tshabangu maintained he was the sole official empowered to issue such notices, citing CCC's interim constitution.[49] These actions led to multiple High Court interventions. On December 7, 2023, Justice Pisirayi Kwenda barred 22 recalled CCC legislators from contesting by-elections under the CCC banner, upholding Tshabangu's objections to their nominations.[50] Similar rulings in January 2024 prevented recalled MPs and councillors from participating in February 3 by-elections, exacerbating CCC's parliamentary losses as ZANU-PF candidates secured several seats amid low turnout and opposition disarray.[51] By early 2025, Tshabangu faced his own expulsion from CCC and potential recall from the Senate, following a High Court decision limiting his party interference, though he appealed to the Supreme Court.[52][53] The recalls highlighted CCC's structural vulnerabilities, contributing to a reported loss of over 20 parliamentary seats to by-election outcomes favoring the ruling party.[3]

By-elections boycott and parliamentary impact

Following the recalls of multiple CCC members of parliament initiated by Sengezo Tshabangu in late 2023, the party, under Nelson Chamisa's direction, opted to boycott subsequent by-elections, deeming them illegitimate due to the absence of formal internal nomination processes and allegations of judicial interference favoring the ruling ZANU-PF.[3] Chamisa described the by-elections as "criminal," arguing they exploited the party's lack of registered structures to bypass legitimate candidate selection, while a High Court ruling on December 7, 2023, barred CCC-nominated candidates from participating in polls scheduled for December 9, 2023, further prompting non-engagement.[54][55] The boycott extended to several key by-elections in 2024 and 2025, resulting in uncontested victories for ZANU-PF candidates amid low voter turnout. In April 2024, CCC abstained from contests in Harare East and Mount Pleasant constituencies, allowing ZANU-PF to secure both seats without opposition.[56] Similar patterns occurred in June 2025 by-elections for Insiza North and Gutu West parliamentary seats, and August 2025 for Chinhoyi Ward 14 council seat, where CCC's absence and reported voter apathy enabled ZANU-PF wins with vote shares exceeding 70% in some cases based on minimal participation.[57][58] These forfeitures compounded the effects of the initial recalls, leading to CCC's net loss of approximately 18 parliamentary seats by mid-2024, eroding the opposition's representation in the National Assembly from 65 seats post-2023 elections to fewer than 50 viable positions.[59] This shift reinforced ZANU-PF's supermajority, exceeding two-thirds of seats, which facilitated unchallenged legislative agendas including potential constitutional amendments without effective opposition scrutiny.[60] The diminished CCC presence also highlighted internal disarray, as the boycott strategy, while protesting perceived electoral flaws, inadvertently amplified ZANU-PF's control over parliamentary proceedings.[3]

Major Controversies

Allegations of ZANU-PF infiltration

In April 2023, CCC leader Nelson Chamisa alleged that ZANU-PF was attempting to infiltrate the opposition through its unconventional "structureless structure" for candidate selection in the upcoming general elections, warning that the absence of formal party hierarchies created vulnerabilities exploited by political rivals.[18] Chamisa specifically claimed that this decentralized process allowed "foes" to insert impostor candidates, potentially compromising the party's integrity during primaries.[61] These concerns were raised amid reports of irregularities in candidate nominations, though no independent verification of successful infiltration at that stage was documented.[2] Post-2023 elections, allegations intensified around Sengezo Tshabangu, who positioned himself as CCC's interim secretary-general despite lacking formal endorsement from Chamisa, initiating recalls of over a dozen CCC MPs and councillors starting in October 2023.[62] Critics within the opposition, including Chamisa supporters, accused Tshabangu of acting as a ZANU-PF proxy, arguing his actions enabled the ruling party to secure a two-thirds parliamentary majority by triggering by-elections that ZANU-PF dominated.[2] [63] In September 2025, reports emerged that President Emmerson Mnangagwa's administration allocated significant funding—ZiG102,343,972.50 in party grants—to entities linked to Tshabangu, interpreted by detractors as a reward for undermining CCC.[64] Tshabangu denied ZANU-PF ties, framing recalls as enforcement of party discipline against "impostors," but his subsequent suspension by a CCC faction led by Welshman Ncube in January 2025 for constitutional breaches fueled suspicions of external manipulation.[65] These events culminated in Chamisa's resignation from CCC on January 25, 2024, in a 13-page statement decrying "criminal" handover of the party to ZANU-PF infiltrators, whom he accused of weaponizing state institutions to install proxies and erode opposition cohesion.[66] [67] Chamisa attributed the party's fragmentation into multiple splinter groups to deliberate sabotage, noting that expected infiltration had escalated into outright control by adversaries.[68] ZANU-PF officials dismissed the claims as excuses for internal disarray, with party spokespersons celebrating CCC's weakening as a natural outcome of opposition disorganization rather than orchestrated interference.[68] Independent analyses, such as those highlighting CCC's reliance on Chamisa's personal authority over institutional structures, suggested that while infiltration risks were real in Zimbabwe's polarized politics, the party's design amplified vulnerabilities to both genuine subversion and opportunistic internal rivals.[2] No judicial or forensic evidence has substantiated widespread ZANU-PF agent embedding, though the recalls contributed to CCC losing key seats, including Tshabangu's self-awarded senatorial position in March 2024.[62]

Internal factionalism and leadership vacuum

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) suffered from profound internal factionalism stemming from its deliberate avoidance of formal party structures, which Nelson Chamisa framed as a "citizens' movement" rather than a traditional political organization. This approach, lacking elected positions or a constitution for dispute resolution, enabled opportunistic power grabs, most notably by Sengezo Tshabangu, who in October 2023 unilaterally declared himself interim secretary-general and initiated recalls of over 20 CCC MPs and councillors, citing alleged misconduct without party-wide consensus.[62] These recalls, which triggered by-elections favoring ZANU-PF, split CCC into competing camps: Chamisa loyalists accusing Tshabangu of subversion, and a splinter group aligning with him to challenge the leadership's authority.[69] The leadership vacuum intensified as Chamisa's personalized command style—relying on informal appointments and direct appeals to supporters—left no clear succession protocol, fostering paralysis in decision-making post-2023 elections. Rival factions proliferated, including those led by figures like Welshman Ncube and Jameson Timba, each claiming legitimacy through ad hoc national executive committees or shadow cabinets, while expelling opponents on grounds of disloyalty.[70] [71] Internal communications leaked in late 2023 revealed heated disputes over resource allocation and candidate selections, with activists jostling for Chamisa's endorsement amid fears of irrelevance.[72] This disarray eroded CCC's cohesion, as evidenced by court challenges over Tshabangu's actions—dismissed in December 2023 for lack of standing due to the party's amorphous structure—and subsequent factional threats, such as Timba's February 2024 warnings to MPs aligning with rivals.[73] The vacuum not only amplified external pressures but also prompted Chamisa's eventual disengagement, as chronic infighting undermined operational efficacy and public trust in the opposition.[74]

Developments After Chamisa's Departure

Resignation and party fragmentation

On January 25, 2024, Nelson Chamisa, the founder and de facto leader of the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC), announced his resignation from the party, exactly two years after its formation.[1] He described the CCC as having been "hijacked" and "contaminated" by undisclosed actors aligned with the ruling ZANU-PF party, asserting that internal structures had been undermined to prevent him from providing effective leadership.[17] [75] Chamisa emphasized that his departure was not an abandonment of Zimbabwean citizens but a strategic shift to honor the "people's victory" from the disputed 2023 elections outside the party's compromised framework, while taking a sabbatical from active politics.[1] [76] The resignation exacerbated ongoing internal divisions, rooted in prior crises such as the recalls of CCC legislators by self-proclaimed secretary-general Sengezo Tshabangu, which Chamisa and supporters viewed as ZANU-PF-orchestrated infiltration.[77] Without Chamisa's unifying influence, the CCC fragmented into at least three rival factions by mid-2024, each claiming legitimacy and leadership.[68] These groups included one led by Jameson Timba, who in January 2025 declared intentions for a party resurgence ahead of future elections, alongside others rallying behind interim figures like Welshman Ncube or independent operatives.[78] [79] The power vacuum led to public infighting, legal disputes over party assets, and a diluted opposition presence in parliament, where CCC seats had already been reduced by recalls totaling over 20 legislators between 2023 and early 2024.[80] By October 2025, the fragmentation had persisted without resolution, weakening the CCC's ability to mount coordinated challenges to ZANU-PF dominance and prompting ruling party officials to publicly celebrate the opposition's disarray as evidence of its inevitable collapse.[68] [81] Analysts attributed the splits to a combination of elite power struggles, unresolved succession mechanisms in Chamisa's unstructured leadership model, and external pressures from state repression, though Chamisa maintained that the core issue was deliberate sabotage rather than inherent organizational flaws.[77] [82] The party's spokesperson indicated efforts to refocus on long-term electoral preparation, but factional discord continued to undermine unified action against government policies.[82]

Emergence of splinter groups

Following Nelson Chamisa's resignation from the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) on January 25, 2024, in which he cited the party's "contamination" and "hijacking" by ZANU-PF infiltrators, the organization rapidly fragmented due to pre-existing structural weaknesses and leadership disputes.[1][17] The absence of formal party structures, which Chamisa had intentionally avoided to prevent legal vulnerabilities, exacerbated the chaos, allowing rival claims to authority and resulting in multiple competing factions.[2] By late 2025, the CCC had splintered into three primary factions, led by Sengezo Tshabangu, Welshman Ncube, and Jameson Timba, each asserting legitimacy over party resources, parliamentary seats, and decision-making.[68] Tshabangu, who had previously positioned himself as interim secretary-general and initiated recalls of over 20 CCC MPs and councillors starting in 2023—actions upheld by courts despite widespread accusations of ZANU-PF orchestration—maintained control over a faction focused on parliamentary maneuvering, including proposals for a government of national unity with ZANU-PF, which other groups rejected as collaborationist.[83] His faction faced suspensions and expulsions from rivals, including a misconduct guilty verdict in February 2025, amid claims that his recalls had deliberately weakened opposition representation ahead of by-elections.[84][85] The Ncube-led faction, drawing on the veteran politician's history in MDC splits, emphasized restoring party discipline and expelled Tshabangu for "bringing the party into disrepute," positioning itself as the authentic continuation of CCC principles against infiltration.[84] Ncube's group clashed with others over control of remaining parliamentary seats and rejected overtures toward ZANU-PF, though it struggled with limited grassroots support compared to Chamisa's personal following. Meanwhile, the Timba faction, aligned with figures like interim chairperson Jameson Timba, engaged in selective alliances, such as endorsing expelled ZANU-PF members in April 2025, signaling pragmatic outreach amid the broader disarray.[86] These divisions rendered the CCC ineffective as a unified opposition, with ZANU-PF publicly celebrating the fragmentation as evidence of its rivals' self-destruction by October 2025.[68]

Recent Activities and Opposition Role (2024–2025)

Responses to government policies

The Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) criticized the 2024 National Budget presented by Finance Minister Mthuli Ncube on December 1, 2023, as anti-poor and failing to meet regional and international benchmarks, while imposing an excessive burden on already suffering citizens through proposed tax measures.[87][88] CCC leader Nelson Chamisa described the budget as disastrous and a reminder of colonial-era policies, arguing it exacerbated economic hardships without addressing core issues like resource mobilization and investment.[89] In response to the introduction of the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) currency in April 2024, CCC officials contended that it fell short of standards for a robust currency, achieving only temporary stability in its initial four months before devaluing amid ongoing economic pressures.[90] The party highlighted the ZiG's rapid loss of value—devaluing by over 40% by September 2024—as evidence of flawed monetary policy, urging resilience while promising alternative transformative approaches to currency stability.[91] Regarding the government's drought response, CCC Binga South legislator Fanuel Cumanzala accused officials in November 2024 of diverting food aid intended for vulnerable populations, claiming this undermined humanitarian efforts amid the El Niño-induced crisis affecting 7.1 million people.[92] CCC spokesperson Promise Mkwananzi dismissed ZANU-PF's October 2025 push to extend President Emmerson Mnangagwa's term to 2030 via constitutional amendments as an internal party matter irrelevant to broader governance reforms.[93][94]

Arrests, protests, and repression claims

On June 16, 2024, Zimbabwean police raided the Harare home of Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) interim leader Jameson Timba during a private gathering commemorating the Day of the African Child, arresting Timba and 77 other CCC members on charges of disorderly conduct in a public place and participating in an unauthorized political meeting.[95][96][97] Police reported that the event violated notification requirements for public assemblies and that some detainees threw stones, necessitating reinforcements and baton charges.[98] Human rights groups, including Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch, described the detentions as arbitrary and part of a broader crackdown on opposition activities, alleging excessive force such as beatings with batons during the raid.[99][100] Subsequent court appearances intensified tensions; on June 27, 2024, after Timba's bail denial, police used batons to disperse supporters outside the Harare Magistrates Court, beating dozens and arresting several CCC affiliates amid reports of tear gas deployment.[97] CCC representatives claimed these actions exemplified systematic repression to silence dissent ahead of events like the Southern African Development Community (SADC) summit in August 2024, while authorities maintained the responses were proportionate to disruptions.[101][102] Additional arrests followed, including four CCC activists—Robson Chere, Namatai Kwekweza, Samuel Gwenzi, and Vusumuzi Moyo—on August 2, 2024, for alleged disorderly conduct during a separate gathering, with claims of torture in custody raised by the International Federation for Human Rights.[103] In Kariba, activists protesting Timba's incarceration were detained in early August 2024.[104] By November 28, 2024, Timba and 34 co-defendants received suspended six-month prison sentences and effective release after over five months in pretrial detention, which opposition figures and observers criticized as politically motivated punishment rather than genuine accountability for public order violations.[100] CCC protests remained limited in scale during this period due to fragmentation following Nelson Chamisa's January 2024 resignation, but sporadic antigovernment demonstrations in 2024–2025, including one in April 2025 involving around 200 participants where approximately 100 were arrested, highlighted ongoing claims of state repression against opposition remnants.[105] Reports from Amnesty International and Civicus Monitor documented over 150 opposition-related detentions in mid-2024 alone, attributing them to laws like the Maintenance of Peace and Order Act used to restrict assemblies.[104][102]

CCC leadership, including Chamisa, immediately rejected the ZEC-announced results, asserting that internal and parallel vote tallies demonstrated Chamisa's victory and alleging systemic fraud, voter intimidation, and manipulation of the electoral process by ZANU-PF-aligned state institutions.[36][37] Chamisa did not pursue a legal challenge in Zimbabwean courts, citing entrenched judicial bias toward the ruling party, though some CCC figures later argued for courtroom contestation despite anticipated futility.[107] International election observers corroborated elements of CCC's grievances, documenting deficiencies such as delayed voter registration updates, restricted access for monitors, ballot shortages in opposition strongholds, and irregularities in vote counting and collation. The European Union Election Observation Mission's final report highlighted that the process failed to meet regional and international standards for free and fair elections, particularly due to ZEC's lack of independence and pre-poll restrictions on opposition campaigning.[42] The U.S. State Department echoed concerns over ZEC's credibility and the overall flawed conduct.[108] Despite these criticisms, ZEC maintained the results' integrity, and no rerun was ordered. CCC's vote share represented a slight decline from Chamisa's 2018 performance under the MDC Alliance banner (23.2% for Mnangagwa's opponent), reflecting persistent urban-rural divides where CCC polled strongly in cities but weakly in ZANU-PF-dominated rural areas.[106]

Parliamentary elections

In the 2023 harmonised general elections held on 23 and 24 August, the Citizens Coalition for Change (CCC) contested seats in both houses of Parliament alongside other parties, primarily against the ruling ZANU-PF. The National Assembly comprises 280 seats, including 210 directly elected from constituencies, 60 reserved for women allocated proportionally based on party performance, and 10 youth seats introduced under recent electoral amendments. The Senate consists of 80 seats, with 60 directly elected proportionally across provinces and the remaining filled by traditional chiefs and representatives of disabled persons. Official results announced by the Zimbabwe Electoral Commission (ZEC) awarded CCC 104 seats in the National Assembly and 27 seats in the Senate.[109][110]

CCC's parliamentary performance reflected strong urban support, particularly in Harare and Bulawayo provinces, where it captured a majority of constituency seats, but limited rural penetration amid ZANU-PF dominance. Voter turnout was reported at approximately 69% nationwide, though urban areas experienced delays in polling station openings and ballot shortages, contributing to extended voting into a second day in some opposition strongholds.[36] The results faced immediate rejection by CCC leader Nelson Chamisa, who described them as "a brazen robbery" and filed petitions with the Constitutional Court alleging vote manipulation, including discrepancies between presidential and parliamentary tallies in CCC favor, unauthorized voter roll alterations, and intimidation by state security forces. International observers, including the European Union Election Observation Mission and the Carter Center, documented a restrictive pre-election environment marked by opposition harassment, media bias favoring ZANU-PF, and failures in results transmission, concluding the process did not meet regional and international standards despite some administrative improvements. ZEC, often criticized for alignment with the ruling party due to appointee selection processes, upheld the outcomes, with courts dismissing most challenges on procedural grounds by late 2023.[36][42][112]
