D'Mile

Dernst "D'Mile" Emile II is an American record producer, songwriter, and multi-instrumentalist renowned for his contributions to contemporary R&B, soul, and pop music.[1] Born in Brooklyn, New York, to a musician father who worked in Haitian music arrangements, D'Mile demonstrated prodigious talent from childhood, performing advanced piano exercises by age six and mastering instruments including guitar and drums.[1] His production style, characterized by polished retro-modern fusion, has earned collaborations with artists such as H.E.R., Bruno Mars of Silk Sonic, Lucky Daye, and Victoria MonÃ©t, yielding chart-topping tracks and critically acclaimed albums.[2][3] D'Mile's breakthrough came through work on H.E.R.'s projects, including co-writing and producing the protest anthem "I Can't Breathe," which secured the Grammy Award for Song of the Year in 2021.[4] He followed this with contributions to Silk Sonic's "Leave the Door Open," co-winning Grammy Awards for Record of the Year, Song of the Year, and Best R&B Song in 2022, marking him as the first songwriter in Grammy history to claim Song of the Year consecutively.[4][5] That same year, his production on Lucky Daye's Table for Two earned Best Progressive R&B Album.[4] In film music, D'Mile co-wrote and co-produced "Fight for You" for the soundtrack of Judas and the Black Messiah, winning the Academy Award for Best Original Song at the 93rd Oscars in 2021.[4][6] Recent accolades include Grammy wins for Best R&B Album on Victoria MonÃ©t's Jaguar II and multiple 2025 nominations, such as Producer of the Year, Non-Classical, and Song of the Year for "Die With a Smile" by Bruno Mars and Lady Gaga.[4][7] His discography also features early credits with Rihanna, Chris Brown, and Mali Music, alongside nominations spanning engineering, songwriting, and performance categories.[1]

Early life and background

Family heritage and upbringing

Dernst Emile II, professionally known as D'Mile, was born on January 24, 1985, in Brooklyn, New York, to parents deeply embedded in the music industry.[8] His mother, Yanick Ã‰tienne, was a Haitian vocalist known for her work in touring and recording, while his father, Dernst Emile Sr., was a accomplished producer, arranger, musician, and music teacher who blended jazz elements into Haitian genres.[9][3] This Haitian-American heritage fostered strong cultural ties to konpa music, with influences from artists such as Lakol, Zin, and Sweet Micky, though D'Mile himself is not fluent in Creole and has not visited Haiti, maintaining connections through family and friends instead.[9] Raised in the Flatbush neighborhood of Brooklyn, D'Mile grew up surrounded by music from infancy, as his parents' professions made early exposure unavoidable and shaped his classical training.[9][10] He learned piano directly from his father and absorbed lessons from the broader family, all of whom were involved in music, instilling a foundational aptitude that propelled his career.[3][11]

Early musical influences and education

Dernst Emile II, known professionally as D'Mile, was born on January 24, 1985, in Brooklyn, New York City, to parents deeply immersed in music. His father, Dernst Emile, a Haitian-born musician, producer, and arranger renowned for blending jazz elements into konpa and other Caribbean genres, provided early classical training that emphasized song structure, harmony, and arrangements.[12][9] His mother, Yanick Ã‰tienne, a touring and recording vocalist, further embedded music in the household, exposing him to live performance dynamics from childhood.[10] D'Mile's initial musical influences stemmed from this familial environment, where he encountered Caribbean rhythms, Haitian folk traditions, and jazz improvisationâ€”particularly through his father's admiration for guitarist Wes Montgomery.[13][9] By age 10, his interests expanded to contemporary R&B and hip-hop production styles, drawn to innovators like The Neptunes and Timbaland, whose beat-driven approaches contrasted yet complemented his classical foundation.[14] Self-described as a multi-instrumentalist from a young age, D'Mile learned piano alongside drums, bass, and guitar through hands-on family guidance rather than institutional programs, fostering a versatile skill set rooted in both traditional and experimental sounds.[11][8] This informal education, prioritizing practical composition over formal academia, enabled him to internalize genre fusion early, setting the stage for his production philosophy.[12]

Career

2005â€“2012: Career beginnings and early collaborations

In 2005, Dernst "D'Mile" Emile II secured his first major production credits on Rihanna's debut album Music of the Sun, where he contributed arrangements to the track "That La, La, La" alongside Full Force.[10][1] That same year, he co-wrote and participated in production for Mary J. Blige's album The Breakthrough, including a placement co-authored with Wynter Gordon, marking his involvement in a Grammy-winning project.[15][16] Following these initial placements, D'Mile apprenticed under producer Rodney "Darkchild" Jerkins in his New Jersey studio for approximately two years, starting in his late teens and extending into the mid-2000s.[14] This period honed his skills in a competitive environment, leading to contributions on Janet Jackson's 2008 album Discipline, where he co-produced tracks such as "LUV" and assisted on the lead single "Feedback," the latter originating as a quick freestyle session.[13][16] He also collaborated within Jerkins' camp on songs like The Pussycat Dolls' "When I Grow Up" (2008) and material for Danity Kane.[16] By 2007, D'Mile relocated to Los Angeles, broadening his network and earning credits on Justin Bieber's debut release My World (2009), including production on "Favorite Girl."[9] These early efforts established his reputation in R&B and pop production, emphasizing multi-instrumental contributions and collaborative songwriting amid established industry figures.[10]

2013â€“2020: Breakthrough productions and rising recognition

During the mid-2010s, D'Mile expanded his production portfolio through collaborations with emerging R&B talents, notably beginning work with H.E.R. around the time of her 2014 signing to RCA Records, contributing to her early singles and EPs that established her neo-soul sound.[14] His involvement grew with tracks like "Every Kind of Way" from H.E.R.'s 2017 self-titled EP, blending live instrumentation and introspective lyrics to showcase her vocal range.[17] By 2018, he co-produced and co-wrote songs for H.E.R., including "Could've Been" featuring Bryson Tiller, which peaked at number 72 on the Billboard Hot 100 and highlighted relational regret through minimalist production. In 2018, D'Mile co-produced several tracks on The Carters' collaborative album Everything Is Love by BeyoncÃ© and Jay-Z, including contributions to the project that won the Grammy Award for Best Urban Contemporary Album at the 61st Annual Grammy Awards in 2019.[11] This high-profile placement elevated his visibility among major artists, demonstrating his ability to integrate lush strings and rhythmic grooves into hip-hop-infused R&B.[18] The following year, he executive produced Lucky Daye's debut album Painted, released on May 24, 2019, via Keep Cool/RCA Records, handling production on 12 of its 13 tracks and infusing soulful, retro-funk elements that earned praise for authenticity.[19][20] D'Mile's rising recognition culminated in 2020 with seven Grammy nominations for his work on H.E.R.'s I Used to Know Her EP series and Lucky Daye's Painted, including nods for Album of the Year, Record of the Year, and Best R&B Album, reflecting industry acknowledgment of his pivotal role in revitalizing contemporary R&B.[3] That same year, he produced Victoria MonÃ©t's debut EP Jaguar, a 9-track release fusing R&B with pop sensibilities, further solidifying his reputation for crafting breakthrough projects for up-and-coming vocalists.[3] These efforts transitioned D'Mile from niche collaborator to Grammy-contending producer, with his multi-instrumentalist approachâ€”emphasizing bass, piano, and organic texturesâ€”drawing comparisons to classic R&B production while adapting to modern streaming demands.[13]

2021â€“present: Major hits, awards, and business ventures

In 2021, D'Mile co-wrote and produced H.E.R.'s "Fight for You" for the film Judas and the Black Messiah, which earned an Academy Award for Best Original Song.[2] That same year, he secured a Grammy Award for Song of the Year for co-writing H.E.R.'s "I Can't Breathe," a protest anthem addressing police brutality.[2] His contributions to H.E.R.'s album Back of My Mind further solidified his role in contemporary R&B production.[7] The 2021 release of Silk Sonic's debut single "Leave the Door Open," co-written and co-produced by D'Mile with Bruno Mars and Anderson .Paak, topped the Billboard Hot 100 and propelled their album An Evening with Silk Sonic.[21] At the 2022 Grammy Awards, the track won Record of the Year and Best R&B Song, while D'Mile received additional recognition for his album production work.[21] He executive produced the full Silk Sonic project, blending retro soul influences with modern polish.[22] From 2023 onward, D'Mile produced Victoria MonÃ©t's album Jaguar II, which won the Grammy for Best R&B Album in 2024 and featured hits like "On My Mama."[4] He continued collaborations with Lucky Daye on Algorithm, earning a 2025 Grammy nomination for Best Engineered Album, Non-Classical.[4] In 2024, D'Mile co-wrote, co-produced, and performed drums and bass on "Die with a Smile" by Lady Gaga and Bruno Mars, a global chart-topper released on August 16 that garnered a 2025 Grammy nomination for Song of the Year.[23] He also received a 2025 nomination for Producer of the Year, Non-Classical.[6] D'Mile launched his personal brand through dmilemusic.com, showcasing achievements and serving as a platform for his production portfolio, though no major independent labels or diversified ventures beyond music production have been publicly detailed as of 2025.[4] His work emphasizes hands-on instrumentation and songwriting, maintaining a focus on R&B and soul collaborations.[2]

Musical style and influences

Production techniques and sonic characteristics

D'Mile's production techniques emphasize a multi-instrumentalist approach, where he performs and layers elements like drums and bass to achieve a polished, live-band feel, often orchestrating full ensembles for authenticity.[2] He prioritizes emotional intuition in the creative process, trusting feelings to guide decisions on melody, harmony, and tone rather than rigid formulas, which allows for organic development tailored to the artist's personality.[24] This includes compromising with collaborators to integrate their ideas while maintaining versatility across genres, drawing inspiration from figures like Quincy Jones to adapt sounds without imposing a dominant personal stamp.[24] His sonic characteristics feature a warm, nostalgic R&B aesthetic that blends retro soulful depth with modern polish, evoking throwback influences such as 1970s funk and jazz while ensuring contemporary relevance.[2] Productions like Silk Sonic's "Leave the Door Open" exemplify this through lush instrumentation and emotional resonance that prioritizes soul-stirring impact, making the sound recognizable yet adaptable to artists like H.E.R. or Bruno Mars.[24][2] D'Mile views R&B's inherent emotional "cheat code" as a foundation but refines it for cool, relatable appeal, ensuring tracks like "Die With a Smile" with Lady Gaga retain a purist, power-ballad warmth without overproduction.[24][2]

Key inspirations from family and genre evolution

D'Mile's musical foundation was deeply shaped by his Haitian-American family, where both parents were active musicians who immersed him in diverse sounds from an early age. His father, Dernst Emile Sr., a renowned producer and musician known for incorporating jazz elements into Haitian music, played guitar and collaborated extensively with D'Mile's mother, providing hands-on exposure to instrumentation and production techniques.[9][10] His mother, Yanick Etienne, a vocalist who sang in church, further reinforced this environment, fostering D'Mile's early proficiency on multiple instruments and his emphasis on emotional, groove-oriented music rooted in familial performances.[9][10] This heritage extended to Haitian konpa music, with D'Mile raised on artists such as Lakol, Zin, and Sweet Micky, which instilled a rhythmic sensibility that influences his production's focus on feel and organic grooves rather than rigid structures.[9] His father's jazz background emerged as the paramount influence, guiding D'Mile toward improvisational and harmonic complexity, while the family's collaborative dynamic encouraged self-taught experimentation starting at age 12.[24][10] In terms of genre evolution, D'Mile draws from the progression of R&B through its soul, jazz, and funk antecedents, citing icons like Marvin Gaye, Herbie Hancock, and John Coltrane as pivotal in bridging mid-20th-century innovations to contemporary neo-soul and hybrid forms.[24] He emulates producers such as Quincy Jones in blending live instrumentation with digital elements, evolving traditional R&B's lush arrangements into versatile, artist-tailored sounds that incorporate hip-hop rhythms and pop accessibility without diluting core emotional depth.[24] This approach reflects R&B's shift from analog-era purity to modern eclecticism, as seen in his infusions of bluesy undertones and jazz phrasing into tracks for artists like H.E.R. and Silk Sonic, prioritizing timeless melody and harmony amid genre hybridization.[3][24]

Notable works and collaborations

Film and soundtrack contributions

D'Mile co-wrote "What Is Love?" with Diana Gordon for the soundtrack of the 2010 romantic comedy The Back-up Plan, where it was performed by Jennifer Lopez as the lead single.[25] The song appeared on the film's original motion picture soundtrack and aligned with Lopez's role in the production.[26] In 2021, D'Mile co-wrote and co-produced "Fight For You" by H.E.R. for the biographical thriller Judas and the Black Messiah, directed by Shaka King.[27] The track, which addresses themes of civil rights struggle, won the Academy Award for Best Original Song at the 93rd ceremony on April 25, 2021.[11][5] It also received a Grammy nomination for Best Song Written for Visual Media.[3] D'Mile received soundtrack credits for the 2020 romantic comedy The Lovebirds, though specific song contributions are not detailed in primary production listings.[27] He is credited as a writer on "New Love," which appears in the film.[28] For the 2022 biographical drama Till, directed by Chinonye Chukwu, D'Mile co-wrote the original song "Stand Up" with Jazmine Sullivan, who performed it.[29][30] The track, released on October 7, 2022, was submitted for Academy Award consideration in the Best Original Song category but did not receive a nomination.[31][32]

Album and single productions

D'Mile's early album productions established his foothold in R&B, beginning with co-production credits on Rihanna's debut Music of the Sun in 2005, marking his first major industry placement alongside collaborators like Full Force.[2] That same year, he contributed tracks to Mary J. Blige's The Breakthrough, which debuted at number one on the Billboard 200 and earned multiple Grammy nominations for its soulful blend of hip-hop and traditional R&B elements.[2] By 2008, D'Mile expanded his portfolio with five tracks on Janet Jackson's Discipline, including co-production on the lead single "Feedback," which peaked at number 19 on the Billboard Hot 100 and showcased his ability to craft polished, dance-oriented R&B with electronic influences.[15] He further solidified ties with Ty Dolla Sign across the Beach House mixtape series (2011â€“2017), producing multiple songs that helped define the artist's moody, atmospheric trap-R&B sound, such as elements in Beach House 3.[33] In the 2020s, D'Mile's productions achieved greater commercial and critical acclaim. He executive produced Silk Sonic's An Evening with Silk Sonic (2021) by Bruno Mars and Anderson .Paak, handling key tracks like "Leave the Door Open," which won Grammy Awards for Record of the Year, Song of the Year, and Best R&B Song in 2022, emphasizing retro-funk grooves rooted in 1970s soul.[3] For Victoria MonÃ©t, he produced eight of nine tracks on her debut Jaguar (2020) and contributed extensively to JAGUAR II (2023), the latter earning the 2024 Grammy for Best R&B Album for its lush, confessional neo-soul arrangements.[33] [4] He also executive produced Joyce Wrice's Overgrown (2021), a 14-track project fusing 1990s R&B nostalgia with modern hip-hop introspection.[3] Among standout singles, D'Mile co-produced H.E.R.'s "Fight for You" (2021) for the Judas and the Black Messiah soundtrack, which secured the 2021 Academy Award for Best Original Song, and "I Can't Breathe" (2020), a protest anthem that won the Grammy for Song of the Year in 2021 for its raw emotional delivery over minimalist production.[3] Other notable singles include "On My Mama" from Victoria MonÃ©t's JAGUAR II, which peaked at number 23 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 2023, and recent co-writes like "Die with a Smile" by Lady Gaga and Bruno Mars (2024), blending orchestral pop with R&B vulnerability.[34] These works highlight D'Mile's versatility in elevating artists through layered instrumentation and thematic depth, often prioritizing organic songwriting over trend-chasing effects.[14]

Academy Awards

D'Mile co-wrote and produced the song "Fight for You" for the film Judas and the Black Messiah (2021), earning a nomination for Best Original Song at the 93rd Academy Awards.[4] The track, performed by H.E.R. and co-written with H.E.R. and Tiara Thomas, features lyrics addressing themes of resistance and justice tied to the film's depiction of Fred Hampton's life and the Black Panther Party.[37][38] On April 25, 2021, "Fight for You" won the Academy Award for Best Original Song, defeating competitors including "Hear My Voice" from The Trial of the Chicago 7, "Io Si (Seen)" from The Life Ahead, "Speak Now" from One Night in Miami, and "Husavik/My Home Town" from Eurovision Song Contest: The Story of Fire Saga.[39][14] This marked D'Mile's sole Academy Award win to date, recognizing his contribution to a soundtrack that integrated R&B elements with narrative-driven songwriting.[4][38]

Other accolades

D'Mile received two awards at the 2023 BMI R&B/Hip-Hop Awards for his songwriting and production on tracks by Silk Sonic, recognizing high performance airplay and streaming data.[40] As a co-writer and producer, he shared in the 2022 Soul Train Music Award for the Ashford & Simpson Songwriter's Award, presented to Mary J. Blige for "Good Morning Gorgeous" from her album Good Morning Gorgeous. He earned a BMI Film/TV Award in 2018 alongside H.E.R. and Tiara Thomas for the song "Fight For You," honoring its impact in visual media prior to its Academy Award nomination.[41] In 2022, BMI presented him with a Brilliance Dinner honor at an event in West Hollywood, California, on June 25, celebrating his overall creative contributions to music.[5] D'Mile was also a first-time recipient at the 70th Annual BMI Pop Awards in 2022 for the performance success of "Leave the Door Open."[42]

Personal life and legacy

Family and personal challenges

Dernst Emile II, known professionally as D'Mile, was born to Haitian musicians Yanick Ã‰tienne, a vocalist who provided backing vocals on Roxy Music's 1982 hit "Avalon," and Dernst Emile, an arranger and instrumentalist.[3][43] Raised in a musical household in Brooklyn, New York, he learned piano from his father and was exposed to jazz and Haitian konpa music through his mother's influences, with his parents frequently collaborating on projects.[3] Unlike some Haitian households where creative pursuits faced resistance, D'Mile's family encouraged his musical development from a young age, including his first production creditâ€”an interludeâ€”at age six on one of his mother's works alongside his father.[10][43] In his professional journey, D'Mile faced significant personal doubts about continuing in music, contemplating quitting around 2014 due to industry frustrations, politics, and waning passion after years of underrecognition despite placements with artists like Mary J. Blige.[2][13] This low point mirrored challenges experienced by collaborators like Lucky Daye, whom D'Mile met during a mutual period of career reevaluation, ultimately reigniting his commitment through renewed creative partnerships during the COVID-19 pandemic.[16][3] A profound family challenge came with the death of his mother, Yanick Ã‰tienne-Emile, on March 30, 2022, at age 63 from cervical cancer and a rare form of liver cancer.[44][45] D'Mile dedicated his 2022 Grammy wins, including Song of the Year for Silk Sonic's "Leave the Door Open," to her memory, attending the ceremony days after her passing to honor a promise to bring an award home.[43][46]

D'Mile's production approach has significantly influenced contemporary R&B by integrating retro soulful aesthetics and live instrumentation with modern digital techniques, fostering a resurgence of polished, nostalgic sounds amid genre shifts toward pop crossovers.[2] His work on tracks like Silk Sonic's "Leave the Door Open" (2022 Grammy Song of the Year winner) and H.E.R.'s "I Canâ€™t Breathe" (2021 Grammy Song of the Year winner) exemplifies this blend, earning critical acclaim for revitalizing emotional depth and groovy elements in neo-R&B.[3] This style has extended to broader pop impacts, as seen in co-production on Bruno Mars and Lady Gaga's "Die With a Smile," which propelled chart success and 2025 Grammy nominations including Song of the Year.[2] His infusions of blues, jazz, and 1990s R&B nostalgia into artists' discographies, including Victoria MonÃ©t's JAGUAR II and Lucky Daye's Algorithm, have nourished genre evolution by prioritizing artist-specific emotional resonance over transient trends.[3] Multiple Grammy wins, an Oscar for "Fight for You" (2021), and 2025 nominations for Producer of the Year underscore his role in elevating underrepresented R&B voices while engineering for high-profile acts like BeyoncÃ© and Jay-Z.[3][2] Looking ahead, D'Mile's trajectory includes ongoing high-caliber collaborations, such as production on Bruno Mars' forthcoming solo albumâ€”his first since 2016â€”and expansion via VANTA Music (formerly Good Company Records), a Disney Music Group subsidiary partnered with Andscape.[2] The label, co-founded with Natalie Prospere and John Kercy, aims to nurture raw talent across genres through music releases, films, TV, and documentaries like the initial Shyne project chronicling rapper Shyne's life; it has signed artists including Samara Cyn and India Shawn.[47] D'Mile has described the venture's potential as "endless," emphasizing support for authentic artistry beyond social media virality.[47] This positions him to broaden his influence from production to artist development, sustaining R&B's vitality amid industry commercialization.[48]
