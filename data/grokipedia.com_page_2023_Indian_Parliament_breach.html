2023 Indian Parliament breach

The 2023 Indian Parliament security breach occurred on 13 December 2023, when two men, Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D, evaded checks to enter the visitors' gallery of the Lok Sabha chamber in New Delhi's Parliament complex, from where they jumped onto the floor, released yellow smoke from concealed canisters, and shouted anti-government slogans during the Zero Hour session.[1][2] Concurrently, two others, Neelam Azad and Amol Shinde, clashed with security personnel outside the premises, deploying similar smoke devices and raising banners protesting unemployment and social issues, in a coordinated action that exposed vulnerabilities in the complex's multi-layered defenses on the 22nd anniversary of the 2001 Parliament attack.[1][3] The intruders were swiftly subdued and arrested by parliamentarians and guards, with no injuries or further disruption reported, though the incident prompted an immediate evacuation of the chamber and highlighted procedural gaps, including inadequate screening of visitors despite enhanced post-2001 protocols.[4][5] Investigations by Delhi Police uncovered a broader plot involving six individuals, including alleged mastermind Lalit Jha and financier Mahesh Kumawat, who were linked through social media and funded the operation to amplify grievances via spectacle.[6][7] Prosecutors invoked the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, citing the canisters' chemical compositionâ€”potassium chlorate mixtures capable of explosive ignitionâ€”as evidence of potential terror intent, while the accused maintained it was a non-violent protest against economic distress; three chargesheets detailed aims to incite anarchy, erode democratic credibility, and seize media spotlight for personal or ideological gain.[3][6][8] The breach triggered intense political recriminations, culminating in the suspension of 146 opposition MPsâ€”95 from Lok Sabha and 46 from Rajya Sabhaâ€”for alleged disruption of proceedings, the largest such action in parliamentary history, alongside inquiries into security lapses that recommended personnel changes and technological upgrades.[9][10] By mid-2025, two accused received bail amid ongoing trials, underscoring persistent debates over protest rights, security efficacy, and institutional accountability.[11][12]

Historical and Political Context

Preceding Security Framework

The security of the Indian Parliament complex prior to the December 13, 2023 breach was overseen by a multi-agency framework, with the Parliament Security Service (PSS)â€”a specialized unit established under the Lok Sabha Secretariatâ€”responsible for internal vigilance, access control, and facilitation within the premises.[13] Delhi Police managed external perimeter security, including gate checks and surrounding areas, while paramilitary forces provided supplementary support for high-threat scenarios.[14] This division of responsibilities had been in place since the PSS's inception in the mid-20th century, emphasizing manual frisking, metal detectors, and basic surveillance without advanced body scanners or enclosed barriers in key areas like the Lok Sabha visitors' gallery.[15] Entry protocols required visitors to obtain passes from sitting Members of Parliament (MPs), who could issue limited daily quotas, typically verified against identity documents at multiple checkpointsâ€”reportedly up to five layersâ€”including bag scans (with prohibitions on carrying items like mobiles or liquids) and physical pat-downs.[16] The new Parliament building, operational since May 2023, retained these core procedures adapted from the old complex, relying on closed-circuit television (CCTV) coverage and uniformed personnel for monitoring rather than integrated biometric or AI-driven systems.[17] However, the framework lacked mandatory shoe inspections or cavity searches, enabling potential concealment of small devices, as evidenced by post-incident revelations of smuggling methods.[18] Coordination between agencies occurred through joint protocols under the Lok Sabha Secretariat, but operational silosâ€”such as PSS's focus on MP privileges over stringent visitor profilingâ€”contributed to vulnerabilities, including unverified pass issuance and limited real-time intelligence sharing with external intelligence bodies.[19] No comprehensive overhaul had been implemented following the shift to the new building, despite prior terror threats like the 2001 attack, leaving reliance on procedural compliance rather than technology-enhanced perimeters or randomized checks.[5] This setup prioritized accessibility for democratic oversight while assuming adherence to rules, though audits had occasionally flagged gaps in training and equipment standardization.[20]

Broader Political Tensions

The 2023 Indian Parliament security breach unfolded against a backdrop of intensifying political polarization between the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP)-led National Democratic Alliance government and the opposition INDIA bloc, exacerbated by unresolved ethnic violence in Manipur state. Ethnic clashes erupted on May 3, 2023, triggered by a Manipur High Court order directing the state government to consider Scheduled Tribe status for the majority Meitei community, which tribal groups including Kukis opposed as a threat to their affirmative action benefits.[21] The conflict pitted Meitei-dominated valley areas against Kuki-Zo-dominated hill regions, resulting in over 170 deaths, widespread arson destroying thousands of homes, and the displacement of approximately 50,000 people by December 2023, with security forces struggling to contain sporadic attacks despite deploying over 10,000 personnel. Opposition leaders, including Congress president Mallikarjun Kharge, accused the central government of bias toward Meiteis and inadequate intervention, demanding Prime Minister Narendra Modi's personal address to Parliamentâ€”a request unmet since the violence began, fueling claims of executive evasion. These tensions spilled into parliamentary proceedings, with the opposition leveraging adjournment motions and protests to highlight Manipur alongside other grievances, such as alleged cronyism in the Adani Group's business dealings following a U.S. short-seller report earlier in 2023. The Monsoon Session (July-August 2023) was largely washed out by disruptions over Manipur, culminating in a no-confidence motion against the Modi government on July 26, 2023, which the opposition used to amplify criticism of the state's handling, though the motion failed along party lines.[22] By the Winter Session commencing December 4, 2023, opposition ranks had coalesced under the newly formed INDIA alliance, boycotting new Parliament building inauguration events and staging sit-ins outside, decrying procedural dilutions and the government's reluctance to debate pressing issues amid preparations for the 2024 general elections.[23] The breach on December 13, 2023â€”the 22nd anniversary of the 2001 Parliament attacksâ€”intensified this divide, as opposition MPs protested the incident by entering the Well of the House, prompting the Speaker to suspend 14 initially, escalating to 146 total by session's end (95 from Lok Sabha, 46 from Rajya Sabha), predominantly opposition members.[24] The government framed the suspensions as necessary to restore order amid orchestrated disruptions, while opposition figures like Rahul Gandhi alleged an assault on democratic norms, likening the mass expulsions to authoritarian tactics and vowing judicial challenges.[25] This episode underscored deeper causal frictions: the opposition's strategy of parliamentary paralysis to extract accountability clashed with the government's priority on legislative productivity, including bills on telecommunications and Jammu-Kashmir reorganization, amid accusations from both sides of exploiting crises for electoral optics.[26] Investigations later revealed the intruders' intent to protest unemployment and gain fame, but police chargesheets alleged coordination aimed at undermining institutional credibility, echoing opposition narratives of systemic failures without direct partisan links established.[6]

The Incident

Sequence of Events on December 13, 2023

On December 13, 2023, the Lok Sabha session commenced at 11:00 a.m. with Speaker Om Birla paying tribute to the victims of the 2001 Parliament attack, marking its 22nd anniversary.[27] Proceedings proceeded through Question Hour until approximately 12:00 p.m., transitioning into Zero Hour for members' unstarred questions and notices. Around 11:30 a.m., two individuals, Sagar Sharma (aged 26 from Lucknow) and Manoranjan D (aged 34 from Mysuru), gained entry to the visitors' gallery using passes issued by BJP MP Pratap Simha's office, passing through three layers of security checks including metal detectors and frisking without detection of concealed items.[27] [1] At approximately 1:00 p.m. during Zero Hour, with BJP MP Rajendra Agarwal presiding, Sharma and Manoranjan jumped from the public gallery into the chamber, a distance of about 18-20 feet. [27] Sharma landed near MP Dilip Saikia, vaulted over benches toward the presiding officer's area, and shouted slogans while MPs including Gurjeet Singh Aujla and Hanuman Beniwal physically restrained him.[27] Simultaneously, Manoranjan, dangling briefly from the railings, released yellow smoke from a canister hidden in his shoe, filling the chamber with dense fumes for about 30 seconds to one minute; a second canister was also deployed, producing white and yellow smoke intended to create panic but containing no toxic or explosive substances.[1] [27] Concurrently outside the Parliament premises, two other individuals, Neelam Azad (aged 37 from Haryana) and Amol Shinde (aged 25 from Maharashtra), hurled smoke canisters over the boundary wall, releasing gas while shouting slogans protesting unemployment ("Berozgari ke khilaf awaaz uthao") and democracy ("Loktantra khatre mein hai"); they distributed pamphlets criticizing government policies on jobs, inflation, and ethnic violence in Manipur.[27] [1] Security personnel and MPs swiftly overpowered all four intruders inside and outside within moments, with no injuries reported among lawmakers or staff; the Lok Sabha was adjourned amid chaos, and both Houses suspended proceedings for the day after brief resumption attempts.[1] [27] Four used canisters and one unused were recovered, along with mobile phones and pamphlets, prompting an immediate FIR under sections of the Indian Penal Code for trespass, public mischief, and criminal conspiracy, later augmented with anti-terror charges.[27]

Profiles and Stated Motives of Intruders

The four primary individuals who breached the Indian Parliament's security on December 13, 2023â€”Manoranjan D, Sagar Sharma, Amol Shinde, and Neelam Azadâ€”hailed from diverse socioeconomic backgrounds across India and connected through social media platforms, including a group inspired by revolutionary figure Bhagat Singh.[28] Manoranjan D, aged 34 from Mysuru, Karnataka, held a bachelor's degree in computer engineering and had briefly worked in an IT firm before assisting his father in farming; he obtained visitor passes via a local BJP MP's office and was identified by Delhi Police as the primary instigator who proposed the plan during group meetings in Mysuru, Gurugram, and Delhi.[28] [7] Sagar Sharma, 27, originally from Delhi but residing in Lucknow as an e-rickshaw driver, jumped into the Lok Sabha chamber alongside Manoranjan, deploying a yellow smoke canister while shouting slogans.[28] Amol Shinde, 25, from Latur, Maharashtra, son of farm laborers from a scheduled caste background, had repeatedly failed police and army recruitment exams; he activated a smoke canister outside the Parliament premises.[28] Neelam Azad, 37, from Hisar, Haryana, possessed an M.Phil degree, had cleared the NET exam, and worked intermittently as a teacher while remaining unemployed; she had prior involvement in farmers' and wrestlers' protests and similarly deployed smoke outside while raising slogans.[28]

Immediate Aftermath

Capture and Initial Containment

Members of Parliament reacted immediately to the intrusion in the Lok Sabha chamber on December 13, 2023, during the Zero Hour session, overpowering Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D after they jumped from the visitors' gallery, shouted slogans against dictatorship, and ignited yellow and green smoke canisters.[31] [32] MPs, including Congress member Gurjeet Singh Aujla, who snatched a smoke canister from one intruder; Rashtriya Loktantrik Party's Hanuman Beniwal; BJP's RK Singh Patel and Manoj Kotak; BSP's Malook Nagar; and others, grabbed the men's limbs, held their heads, and restrained them on the floor amid physical scuffles.[33] [34] One of the intruders pleaded with the surrounding MPs to spare him, claiming he was present only to protest, as MPs initially mistook the loud thud of their jump for a fall and proceeded to thrash them until uniformed security personnel arrived to take custody.[32] [31] The chamber was briefly disrupted, with proceedings adjourned as the intruders were subdued within seconds of the breach.[35] Concurrently, outside the Parliament premises, two associatesâ€”Neelam Azad and Amol Shindeâ€”were apprehended by security after attempting to release smoke canisters near the gates while raising similar slogans.[36] [37] Delhi Police took all four into custody shortly thereafter, marking the initial arrests in the incident, with the intruders' visitor passes traced to issuance via a BJP MP's office.[36] [35] The rapid physical containment by MPs and handover to security highlighted procedural gaps in gallery oversight, as the smoke devicesâ€”smuggled in footwearâ€”evaded pre-entry scans, though no weapons or explosives were involved.[38] [35] Initial questioning by police focused on their entry methods and affiliations, containing the threat without escalation to broader evacuation.[1]

Emergency Protocols Activated

Upon the intruders entering the Lok Sabha chamber and releasing yellow smoke from canisters on December 13, 2023, Members of Parliament and security personnel immediately intervened, with Congress MP Gurjeet Singh Aujla seizing one canister while others subdued Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D in the aisle.[16] [1] The Lok Sabha Speaker Om Birla confirmed the smoke was non-toxic, designed only to create a sensation, and the chamber's proceedings were halted promptly, with the house adjourned until 2:00 PM before resuming.[16] [1] Concurrently, outside the complex near Transport Bhawan, two additional individualsâ€”Amol Shinde and Neelam Azadâ€”were detained after deploying red and yellow smoke canisters, reflecting a coordinated element to the intrusion that security forces contained without escalation to violence.[16] The Rajya Sabha briefly paused its session in response to the disturbance.[16] No full-scale evacuation of the premises occurred, as the swift on-site apprehension by Parliament Security Service personnel and assisting lawmakers neutralized the immediate threat.[1] [16] In the ensuing hours, emergency measures included suspending all visitor passes to the Parliament complex and deploying Delhi Police's Special Cell for initial investigation, alongside an increase in on-ground security presence to seal access points and screen personnel.[16] [1] The Union Home Ministry activated a high-level probe into the lapse, directing agencies to review entry protocols and canister concealment methods, though standard post-2001 enhancements like RF tags and barricades had failed to prevent the breach.[16] These actions prioritized containment over disruption, allowing parliamentary functions to normalize within the day while underscoring vulnerabilities in visitor gallery oversight.[1]

Investigation and Probe

Delhi Police and Agency Involvement

The Delhi Police assumed primary responsibility for the criminal investigation into the December 13, 2023, Parliament security breach, registering a First Information Report (FIR) under sections of the Indian Penal Code including criminal trespass, mischief, and unlawful assembly, with the case later transferred to its Special Cell for deeper probe.[6] The Special Cell conducted interrogations of the initial four arrested individualsâ€”Sagar Sharma, Manoranjan D, Amol Shinde, and Neelam Azadâ€”uncovering links to two additional suspects, leading to the surrender of alleged mastermind Lalit Jha on December 14, 2023, and the arrest of Mahesh Kumawat.[39] Further raids and technical surveillance by the Special Cell revealed encrypted communications and financial transactions among the six accused, prompting the invocation of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) on December 21, 2023, after obtaining approval from the Lieutenant Governor.[40] In June 2024, the Delhi Police Special Cell filed a 1,000-page chargesheet before the Patiala House Court against the six accused, detailing evidence such as mobile data, witness statements, and recovered items like smoke canisters, while alleging the breach aimed to "discredit Indian democracy" through synchronized protests mimicking historical events like the 2001 Parliament attack.[41] A supplementary chargesheet was submitted on July 15, 2024, incorporating additional forensic analysis and expert opinions on the canisters' chemical composition.[42] The probe received multiple extensions from courts, including a 45-day period granted on March 11, 2024, to complete evidence collection, with police sources emphasizing a "watertight case" based on digital footprints and confessions.[43][44] Parallel to the Delhi Police's criminal inquiry, the Ministry of Home Affairs constituted a high-level committee on December 13, 2023, headed by Central Reserve Police Force Director General Anish Dayal Singh, to examine systemic security lapses in Parliament, including visitor verification protocols and coordination among forces.[45] The Central Industrial Security Force (CISF), tasked with Parliament's physical security, initiated an internal investigation into procedural failures, such as inadequate bag checks and gallery access controls, though findings remained non-public as of late 2024.[46] No transfer of the core criminal case to federal agencies like the National Investigation Agency occurred, with Delhi Police retaining jurisdiction despite opposition demands for broader scrutiny.[47]

Key Interrogations and Evidence

Delhi Police interrogations of Lalit Jha, identified as a key conspirator, revealed that the group aimed to incite widespread anarchy to compel the government to resign and address demands concerning unemployment and farmers' grievances. Jha admitted to destroying evidence by burning the mobile phones of four accomplices prior to his surrender on December 15, 2023, in an effort to erase digital traces of coordination.[48][49] The six primary accusedâ€”Sagar Sharma, Manoranjan D, Amol Shinde, Neelam Azad, Lalit Jha, and Mahesh Kumawatâ€”underwent multiple rounds of face-to-face questioning by Delhi Police in December 2023 and January 2024 to cross-verify accounts of the conspiracy and execution. Polygraph tests were administered to five of them in Gujarat, while Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D additionally submitted to narco-analysis and brain mapping, with results indicating Manoranjan's central role in devising the breach as a means to "create chaos and disruption" modeled partly on a 2017 Kosovo assembly protest.[50][51][52] Charge sheets filed by Delhi Police in May and July 2024 incorporated interrogation disclosures and forensic data, highlighting premeditated planning where Manoranjan, an admirer of Che Guevara, recruited and motivated participants via ideological appeals, including references to The Motorcycle Diaries. Evidence included recovered smoke canisters, deleted digital communications, and timelines showing reconnaissance of Parliament premises weeks prior.[53][42][52] Investigators probed funding sources for travel, devices, and logistics, with Jha's statements pointing to self-financed efforts but raising questions about undisclosed contributions amid suspicions of broader networks, though no foreign links were substantiated in public findings. Some accused alleged custodial torture to extract admissions of political affiliations, claims denied by police.[54]

Formal Charges and Findings

The Delhi Police filed a comprehensive chargesheet on June 7, 2024, against six individualsâ€”Manoranjan D, Lalit Jha, Neelam Azad, Amol Shinde, Mahesh Kumawat, and Sagar Sharmaâ€”in connection with the December 13, 2023, Parliament security breach.[55] The 1,000-page document, presented to Additional Sessions Judge Hardeep Kaur, invoked Sections 16 (punishment for terrorist act) and 18 (punishing conspiracy, etc., for terrorist act) of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), following sanction granted by Delhi Lieutenant Governor V.K. Saxena on June 6, 2024.[56] [57] Additional charges under the Indian Penal Code included Sections 186 (obstructing a public servant), 353 (assault or criminal force to deter a public servant), 452 (house-trespass after preparation for hurt, assault, or wrongful restraint), 153A (promoting enmity between groups), 147 (rioting), and 120B (criminal conspiracy).[42] Investigation findings detailed in the chargesheet described the breach as a premeditated operation to undermine Indian democracy and attract international attention.[6] The accused, who met via social media platforms like Facebook and Telegram, procured yellow and green smoke canisters containing chemicals such as potassium chlorate, sulfur, and lactoseâ€”substances capable of acting as low-intensity explosives under certain conditionsâ€”and coordinated simultaneous intrusions during Parliament's Zero Hour.[3] Police recovered digital evidence, including videos and messages, indicating the group's intent to publicize grievances over unemployment, farmers' issues, and ethnic violence in Manipur through dramatic disruption, rather than isolated protest.[7] A supplementary chargesheet was filed on July 15, 2024, incorporating further forensic analysis and interrogations that reinforced the conspiracy angle, with no external terror linkages identified but emphasizing the act's potential to incite public disorder.[42] The probe, led by the Delhi Police Special Cell, concluded that the intruders' actions constituted an assault on democratic institutions, justifying UAPA invocation despite the non-lethal nature of the smoke devices.[44] As of December 2024, the case remained pre-trial, with evidence deemed sufficient by investigators for conviction under the charged provisions.[44]

Legal Proceedings

Arrests and Custody

Following the security breach on December 13, 2023, Delhi Police arrested four individuals on the same day: Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D, who had entered the Lok Sabha chamber and released smoke canisters, along with Amol Shinde and Neelam Azad, who had deployed gas canisters outside the Parliament premises while shouting slogans.[58] On December 15, 2023, police apprehended the remaining two accused, Lalit Jha and Mahesh Kumawat, identified as key planners who had funded the operation and supplied materials.[58] All six were charged under multiple sections of the Indian Penal Code, including 186 (obstructing public servant), 353 (assault to deter public servant), and 307 (attempt to murder), as well as provisions of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) for terrorist acts and conspiracy.[8] The accused were initially remanded to police custody for interrogation, with extensions granted by a Delhi court to facilitate recovery of devices and evidence linking their actions to prior coordination via social media and encrypted apps.[54] Judicial custody followed, with periodic extensions; for instance, in January 2024, five accused alleged custodial torture to extract admissions of political affiliations, though police denied these claims, and the court extended custody to March 1, 2024, pending further probe.[54] Chargesheets, including a primary one in June 2024 naming Manoranjan D as the "kingpin" and a supplementary in July 2024, detailed their intent to mimic the 2001 Parliament attack for publicity, leading to continued detention under UAPA's stringent provisions.[59][42] Bail applications faced rejections at the trial court level, with Sagar Sharma's plea denied on January 24, 2025, citing risks under UAPA, and Lalit Jha's on April 28, 2025, due to his role in orchestration.[35] However, the Delhi High Court granted bail to Neelam Azad and Mahesh Kumawat on July 2, 2025, ruling their actions constituted political dissent rather than terrorism, subject to conditions including a â‚¹50,000 bond, restrictions on social media commentary, and non-disclosure of case details.[11][35] As of October 2025, Sagar Sharma, Manoranjan D, Amol Shinde, and Lalit Jha remain in judicial custody, with the Delhi High Court directing affidavits on their criminal histories in October 2025 for pending bail pleas, and the next hearing scheduled for November 10, 2025. Earlier trial court denials emphasized the gravity of breaching parliamentary security on the anniversary of the 2001 attack, while High Court proceedings highlight disputes over UAPA applicability to non-violent protest elements.[35][60]

Court Rulings and Bail Developments

The trial court denied bail applications to several accused in the 2023 Parliament security breach case, citing the seriousness of charges under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) and Indian Penal Code sections related to criminal conspiracy, trespass, and endangering public safety. On January 24, 2025, the court rejected the bail plea of Sagar Sharma, one of the individuals who jumped into the Lok Sabha chamber, observing that the investigation into a larger conspiracy was ongoing and release could hamper probe integrity.[35] Similarly, on April 28, 2025, bail was denied to Lalit Jha, the alleged mastermind who did not enter the premises but was linked to planning and funding, with the court emphasizing the gravity of UAPA provisions invoked by Delhi Police.[35] The Delhi High Court intervened in appeals against these denials, questioning the applicability of stringent UAPA sections in some instances. On May 7, 2025, during hearings on bail pleas, the court remarked that while the breach constituted a serious protest disrupting parliamentary proceedings, it did not prima facie qualify as a terrorist act under UAPA, as the actions lacked evidence of intent to overawe the government through terror.[61] This scrutiny culminated in the grant of bail on July 2, 2025, to Neelam Azad (also known as Neelam Ranolia) and Mahesh Kumawat, who were accused of logistical support but not direct intrusion. The division bench ruled that the material did not satisfy ingredients of UAPA Sections 15 (terrorist act) or 18 (conspiracy), noting prolonged detention without trial commencement violated bail as a rule under Section 437 CrPC, subject to conditions including a personal bond of â‚¹50,000 with two sureties each, prohibition on social media posts or interviews regarding the incident, and non-disclosure of investigation details.[62][11][35] Bail pleas for the remaining accusedâ€”Sagar Sharma, Manoranjan D, Amol Shinde, and Lalit Jhaâ€”remained pending before the Delhi High Court as of October 2025, with police opposing release on grounds of a concerted plot to discredit democracy and potential flight risk. On July 24, 2025, the court heard arguments for Sharma and D, observing that disrupting Parliament represented an effective method to generate public alarm akin to terror, yet deferred decision pending further evidence.[63] On August 18, 2025, the court sought Delhi Police's response to Jha's plea, highlighting investigative delays.[64] By October 8, 2025, the bench directed affidavits on the criminal histories of D, Sharma, and Jha to assess bail eligibility, underscoring ongoing scrutiny of their roles in the alleged conspiracy without granting interim relief.[65] No further bails were approved for these individuals, maintaining their judicial custody amid supplementary charge sheets filed in 2024 linking the breach to coordinated actions via encrypted communications.[66]

Ongoing Trials as of 2025

As of October 2025, the trial proper in the 2023 Parliament security breach case has not commenced in the designated special court under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), with proceedings largely confined to bail hearings and pre-trial matters following the filing of a 1,700-page chargesheet by Delhi Police in May 2024.[44] The accused face charges including criminal conspiracy, attempt to commit terrorist acts, and disrupting parliamentary sovereignty, but delays in framing charges have stalled substantive hearings.[67] On July 2, 2025, the Delhi High Court granted bail to Neelam Azad (also known as Neelam Ranolia) and Mahesh Kumawat, observing that the breach reflected "political dissent" rather than orchestrated terrorism, given the absence of proven links to banned organizations or violence beyond smoke canisters.[11][68] Conditions included a personal bond of Rs 50,000 each, prohibitions on social media commentary or media interactions about the case, and no contact with co-accused or witnesses.[12] Azad had protested outside Parliament with canisters, while Kumawat facilitated visitor passes; both had remained in custody for over 18 months prior.[35] Bail applications for principal intruders Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan Dâ€”who entered the Lok Sabha chamber on December 13, 2023, and deployed yellow smoke canistersâ€”along with alleged coordinator Lalit Jha, remain pending. On October 8, 2025, a Delhi High Court bench directed their counsel to submit affidavits detailing any prior criminal records, in line with Supreme Court guidelines on bail under stringent laws, with the next hearing scheduled for November 10, 2025.[69] Lower courts had previously denied their bail, citing risks of tampering and the gravity of breaching high-security zones during a session commemorating the 2001 Parliament attack anniversary.[64] Other accused, such as Amol Shinde, have seen bail pleas advanced to High Court scrutiny after trial court rejections, with police responses sought as recently as August 18, 2025.[70] Of the nine primary UAPA-charged individuals, at least five remain incarcerated, underscoring prolonged detention amid investigative claims of a broader conspiracy involving jobless youth radicalized via social media.[71] No convictions have been secured, and the case's progression hinges on resolving these interlocutory applications before evidentiary trials.

Political Reactions and Consequences

Government and BJP Response

The Indian government, under Prime Minister Narendra Modi, characterized the December 13, 2023, Parliament security breach as a profoundly serious incident that warranted a detailed probe over partisan debate. Modi emphasized that its gravity could not be underestimated and called on all stakeholders to refrain from political resistance, prioritizing corrective measures to bolster security.[72][73] Union Home Minister Amit Shah conceded the breach constituted a "serious lapse" in protocols and pledged stringent actions to seal vulnerabilities, while rebuking opposition parties for transforming the matter into a political tool rather than aiding national security enhancements. Shah underscored the government's duty to rectify gaps without allowing procedural lapses to be exploited for electoral gain.[74][75] The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) asserted potential ties between the intruders and opposition entities, noting that one accused, Neelam Azad, had actively supported the Congress-led INDIA alliance through social media campaigns protesting unemployment. BJP spokespersons, including Amit Malviya, framed the breach as a coordinated effort linked to the Congress-Communist nexus, urging scrutiny of opposition motives amid demands for action against BJP MP Pratap Simha, whose office issued visitor passes to Sagar Sharma, one of the intruders. The party defended Simha, portraying opposition attacks on him as diversions from the core security failure and the accused's premeditated actions.[76][77] To counter opposition protests demanding Shah's parliamentary addressâ€”which stalled proceedingsâ€”the government endorsed the suspension of 141 MPs from opposition parties on December 19, 2023, following privileges committee recommendations for unruly conduct, including slogan-shouting and placard displays that disrupted business. BJP leaders justified these suspensions as essential for restoring order, enabling the Winter Session's legislative agenda, and preventing further politicization that could undermine the ongoing Delhi Police investigation into the breach's orchestration by six arrested individuals.[25][9]

Opposition Criticisms and Counterclaims

Opposition parties, including the Indian National Congress, Trinamool Congress, and Aam Aadmi Party, criticized the Bharatiya Janata Party-led government for a major security lapse on December 13, 2023, when two individuals entered the Lok Sabha chamber undetected and released yellow smoke canisters.[9] They demanded an immediate statement from Union Home Minister Amit Shah in both houses of Parliament, along with a detailed debate on the incident, arguing that the breach exposed systemic failures in parliamentary vigilance despite enhanced security protocols post-2001.[9] Congress leaders specifically questioned the government's intelligence apparatus, asserting the event proved "total intelligence failure" and raised concerns about how intruders accessed the premises amid claims of robust safeguards.[78] A focal point of opposition scrutiny was BJP MP Pratap Simha's office issuing visitor passes to Sagar Sharma, one of the intruders, on December 13, 2023, prompting demands for Simha's expulsion and investigation into potential complicity or negligence.[79][80] Leaders from Congress, Shiv Sena (UBT), and others alleged this act "put the security of the entire Parliament at risk," contrasting it with the government's swift suspensions of 141 opposition MPs for protesting the breach, while no parallel action targeted Simha or MP Ramesh Bidhuri over unrelated misconduct.[81][82] The opposition countered government assertions of a thorough probe by insisting on a joint parliamentary committee for oversight, rejecting the administrative inquiry as insufficient for transparency and accountability.[83][73] In counterclaims, opposition figures like Congress MP Rahul Gandhi attributed the breach to broader policy failures, linking the intruders' actions to youth desperation fueled by unemployment and inflation under the Modi administration, rather than endorsing the government's narrative of isolated anti-national conspiracy.[84] Sonia Gandhi described the ensuing mass suspensionsâ€”totaling 146 opposition MPs by December 2023â€”as evidence of the government "strangulating democracy" and displaying "disdain" for parliamentary norms to evade scrutiny.[85][25] These positions framed the government's response, including the Home Ministry's formation of an inquiry committee on December 14, 2023, as an attempt to politicize the incident and suppress dissent, while opposition protests emphasized institutional lapses over partisan motives.[86][87]

Role and Defense of Pratap Simha

Pratap Simha, a Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) Member of Parliament from Mysuru, Karnataka, came under scrutiny for the issuance of visitor passes from his office to two of the intruders involved in the December 13, 2023, Parliament security breach. Specifically, passes were issued to Sagar Sharma and Manoranjan D, who entered the Lok Sabha chamber from the public gallery and released yellow smoke canisters.[88][89] According to police sources, Manoranjan D had approached Simha's office multiple times over three months seeking passes, introducing Sharma as a friend, leading to the approval of three passes on the day of the incident, though one was not used as the holder arrived with a child.[90][91] Simha defended the action by stating that his office routinely issues such passes to constituents and visitors without personal vetting or prior acquaintance with the individuals, a standard practice among MPs to facilitate public access to Parliament.[92][89] He met Lok Sabha Speaker Om Birla shortly after the breach to explain the circumstances and cooperated with investigations, with his statement recorded by Delhi Police on December 21, 2023.[93][94] In response to opposition accusations labeling him a "traitor," Simha asserted on December 24, 2023, that the public would judge his patriotism, emphasizing that only divine knowledge could confirm his intentions and dismissing the charges as politically motivated.[95][96] The BJP leadership, including Union Minister Pralhad Joshi, maintained that the matter was under investigation and that legal processes would determine outcomes, with no expulsion or charges filed against Simha as of the latest available reports.[94] Simha's office had no recorded prior interactions with the accused beyond the pass requests, and investigations found no evidence of deeper involvement or complicity in the breach planning.[92][97]

Security Reforms and Long-Term Impact

Post-Breach Enhancements

Following the December 13, 2023, security breach in the Lok Sabha, immediate procedural changes were implemented to restrict access and enhance screening. The issuance of visitor passes was suspended indefinitely, limiting entry to essential personnel and escorted groups such as school students under strict supervision.[16][98] Separate gates were designated for members of Parliament (MPs), staff, media, and visitors (when permitted), with MPs allocated an exclusive entrance segregated from others to streamline facial recognition at flap barriers.[16][98] Structural and technological upgrades targeted vulnerabilities exposed in the intrusion. The visitors' gallery in the Lok Sabha chamber was encased in glass barriers to prevent individuals from jumping into the well of the House, addressing the method used by the two perpetrators.[16] Airport-style body scanners were planned for installation at entry points, alongside an increase in security personnel deployed inside the chambers.[16] A three-tier external security perimeter was reinforced with barricades positioned 200 meters from the premises, mandatory double frisking, and routine sniffer dog patrols.[98] The Ministry of Home Affairs established an inquiry committee on December 14, 2023, tasked with investigating lapses, identifying causes, and recommending systemic improvements, including potential enhancements to overall parliamentary security protocols.[45][99] Subsequent integration of Central Industrial Security Force (CISF) personnel into the security apparatus was noted as a response to the incident, augmenting existing arrangements for both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha operations.[100] Additional restrictions barred former MPs from entry and curtailed media access, confining reporters to designated areas and prohibiting prolonged presence in corridors.[98] These measures, while primarily reactive, aimed to mitigate risks from unauthorized intrusions, though their efficacy was tested by subsequent perimeter breaches in 2025, prompting ongoing evaluations without publicly detailed further overhauls as of October 2025.[101][102] The Lok Sabha Speaker also initiated a high-level probe in coordination with Delhi Police to inform broader reforms.[103]

Implications for Parliamentary Functioning

The 2023 Parliament security breach exposed significant vulnerabilities in the visitor entry protocols, prompting immediate restrictions on gallery passes and visitor access to prevent unauthorized intrusions during sessions. Following the December 13 incident, Parliament authorities suspended all visitor passes pending a security review, effectively halting public and guest attendance in the visitors' galleries for several days.[9] [98] This clampdown segregated entry points for MPs, media, and staff, introducing additional checkpoints that extended processing times and altered the daily flow of proceedings.[98] Subsequent reforms mandated prior police verification for all gallery pass applicants, alongside requirements for Aadhaar-linked biometric data, photographs, and QR code-enabled smart cards to track entrants. MPs issuing passes faced heightened scrutiny, including declarations affirming the legitimacy of visitors, which increased administrative burdens and potentially deterred routine guest invitations critical for constituency representation.[104] [105] These measures, implemented by January 2024, layered five existing security levels with enhanced digital verification, aiming to mitigate risks from insider facilitation but raising concerns over reduced public access to democratic processes.[104] [5] The breach disrupted the Winter Session 2023, with opposition demands for a government statement escalating into protests that resulted in the suspension of 146 MPsâ€”95 from Lok Sabha and 46 from Rajya Sabhaâ€”on grounds of unruly conduct, significantly depleting chamber attendance and curtailing debate on key bills. Productivity metrics indicated a dip in functioning post-breach, as reduced opposition participation limited scrutiny and adversarial discourse, though government business proceeded with fewer interruptions.[106] [107] Long-term, the incident underscored the tension between security imperatives and parliamentary openness, fostering a more fortified but potentially insular environment that could erode public trust in institutional accessibility.[103]

Controversies and Alternative Views

Allegations of Deeper Conspiracies

Delhi Police investigations portrayed the December 13, 2023, breach as part of a "larger conspiracy" extending beyond the six arrested individuals, with charges under the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) invoked to reflect suspected organized intent to undermine parliamentary security and evoke memories of the 2001 terror attack on the same date.[108] [60] The agency alleged the intruders aimed to "discredit Indian democracy" through coordinated actions, including reconnaissance attempts and shared planning via WhatsApp groups, while probing potential motives like creating anarchy rather than mere unemployment claims by accused Lalit Jha, identified as the purported mastermind.[6] [109] Relatives of intruder Sagar Sharma alleged entrapment by "someone big" in a high position, suggesting manipulation by influential figures rather than independent action by the group, which included links to a "Bhagat Singh Fan Club" on social media for ideological coordination.[110] [111] Sharma's uncle, Pradeep Sharma, claimed the 27-year-old from Lucknow was "dragged into" the plot, echoing family assertions of framing amid the breach's symbolic timing and execution.[112] Political figures from the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) advanced claims of a deliberate scheme to defame the Modi government, with some linking it to "urban Naxal" networks allegedly sheltered by opposition-led state administrations, though no judicial findings have substantiated external orchestration as of ongoing trials in 2025.[113] [114] Despite supplementary chargesheets filed by July 2024 detailing over 1,000 pages of evidence on the accused's preparations, including smoke devices with potential explosive properties, deeper networks remain unproven, with bail granted to two accused in July 2025 pending further scrutiny.[3] [11]

Partisan Narratives and Media Bias

The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP)-led government framed the December 13, 2023, breach as a deliberate attempt to undermine parliamentary sanctity and national security, emphasizing the intruders' use of smoke canisters and anti-establishment slogans as evidence of orchestrated disruption timed symbolically on the anniversary of the 2001 Parliament attack.[115] BJP leaders, including Prime Minister Narendra Modi, urged focus on investigation over immediate debate, portraying opposition demands for Home Minister Amit Shah's statement as politicization that hindered probe efforts.[73] This narrative highlighted potential complicity, such as visitor passes issued by BJP MP Pratap Simha to one intruder, while downplaying systemic lapses in favor of attributing chaos to opposition protests that followed. In contrast, opposition parties, including Congress, depicted the incident as a profound intelligence and security failure under the Modi administration, arguing that the ease with which unarmed civilians accessed the chamber exposed vulnerabilities despite enhanced post-2001 measures.[9] Leaders like Rahul Gandhi linked the breach to broader governance issues, such as unemployment driving public discontent, and criticized the subsequent suspension of 146 opposition MPsâ€”95 from Lok Sabha and 46 from Rajya Sabhaâ€”as an authoritarian tactic to evade accountability and ram through legislation unopposed.[84] This view positioned the suspensions, the largest in India's parliamentary history, as prioritizing partisan control over democratic discourse, with demands for debate framed as essential oversight rather than obstruction.[116] Media coverage amplified these divides, with government-aligned outlets like those sympathetic to BJP emphasizing the breach's severity and intruders' motivesâ€”later revealed by police as aiming to "discredit Indian democracy" for fame and powerâ€”while portraying opposition protests as disruptive anarchy justifying suspensions.[6][117] Conversely, opposition-leaning and international media, such as Al Jazeera and The Guardian, highlighted security shortcomings and framed mass expulsions as democratic erosion, often equating them to an "attack on democracy" with less scrutiny of procedural disruptions.[116] This partisan skew reflects broader patterns in Indian media, where domestic mainstream channels have faced accusations of proximity to ruling powers, underreporting lapses while amplifying threats, whereas global outlets critiqued institutional biases but occasionally overstated opposition victimhood without addressing their role in post-breach unrest.[118] Empirical analysis of reporting patterns shows uneven emphasis: pro-government narratives prioritized intruder intent and opposition culpability, supported by police chargesheets alleging scripted protests, while critical coverage stressed verifiable breaches like unchecked entry protocols, underscoring how source affiliations influenced causal attributions over neutral facts.[119]
