Elizabeth Johnson (theologian)

Elizabeth A. Johnson, C.S.J. (born 1941), is an American Roman Catholic nun belonging to the Sisters of St. Joseph of Brentwood and a feminist theologian whose work centers on reinterpreting divine imagery, Mariology, and ecological theology within a Catholic framework.[1][2] Distinguished Professor Emerita of Theology at Fordham University, where she taught from 1991 until her retirement in 2018, Johnson previously held positions at the Catholic University of America and has served as president of both the Catholic Theological Society of America and the American Theological Society.[2][2] Her book She Who Is: The Mystery of God in Feminist Theological Discourse (1992) argues for expanding Trinitarian language to include feminine metaphors for God, drawing on scriptural and patristic sources to challenge exclusively masculine depictions, and received the Louisville Grawemeyer Award in Religion.[2] Johnson has authored over a dozen books, including Quest for the Living God (2007), which surveys contemporary theologies of God but was formally critiqued by the U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops' Committee on Doctrine for using imprecise language that fails to affirm essential Catholic teachings on God's immutability, the Incarnation, and the Holy Spirit's personhood, while diverging from magisterial tradition without adequate justification.[2][3][4] The bishops reaffirmed their assessment after Johnson's rebuttal, emphasizing the book's potential to mislead readers on foundational doctrines due to its selective engagement with Church sources.[3] She has received fifteen honorary doctorates and multiple teaching awards, reflecting her impact in academic circles, though her emphasis on experiential and symbolic approaches to theology has fueled ongoing tensions between progressive reinterpretations and doctrinal fidelity in Catholic scholarship.[2][2]

Biography

Early Life and Formation

Elizabeth A. Johnson was born on December 6, 1941, in Brooklyn, New York, to Walter Johnson and Margaret Reed Johnson.[1][5] As the eldest of seven children in a sprawling Irish Catholic family, she was immersed from childhood in the rhythms of parish life and Catholic devotional practices typical of mid-20th-century urban immigrant communities.[2][6] Her early education occurred in Catholic primary and secondary schools, where the faith formation emphasized communal worship and service, fostering an initial sense of religious vocation.[7] Johnson's path toward religious life was shaped by encounters with the Sisters of St. Joseph, a congregation focused on education and social outreach, whose Brentwood, Long Island, motherhouse served as a model of dedicated service.[8] Influenced by their witness during her young adulthood amid the cultural shifts of the early 1960s, she discerned a call to join them, entering the congregation around that period and adopting the religious name that aligned with her commitment to contemplative action in the world.[9][2] This formative step marked the transition from family-rooted piety to structured religious formation, setting the stage for her later theological pursuits without yet involving formal academic study.[1]

Education and Initial Influences

Johnson completed her undergraduate education with a Bachelor of Science degree from Brentwood College in 1964.[2] [10] She then pursued graduate studies, earning a Master of Arts in theology from Manhattan College in 1970.[7] In the 1960s and 1970s, Johnson taught science, religion, and reading in Catholic elementary and secondary schools, holding New York State certification for teaching reading in grades K-6.[2] [10] During this period, as a young member of the Sisters of St. Joseph, the reforms of the Second Vatican Council (1962â€“1965) sparked her deepening interest in theological matters, prompting a shift from classroom instruction toward formal advanced study.[2] In 1977, Johnson commenced doctoral work at the Catholic University of America, culminating in a PhD in systematic theology in 1981; she was the first woman to receive this degree from the institution.[8] [6] This academic milestone facilitated her transition from high school teaching to specialized theological research and eventual university-level engagement.[6]

Religious Vocation and Academic Career

Johnson joined the Sisters of Saint Joseph of Brentwood, New York, in 1959 and professed her first vows with the congregation in 1967, committing to a life of religious service.[7] In the ensuing years, she held positions in education and administration within the order, including teaching roles that aligned with the congregation's emphasis on ministry through schooling and community leadership.[5] Following completion of her Ph.D. in theology from The Catholic University of America in 1981, Johnson joined that institution's faculty as an assistant professor, serving until 1991.[9] She then moved to Fordham University in 1991, where she taught theology for 27 years until her retirement from full-time teaching in 2018, attaining the rank of Distinguished Professor and later Emerita status.[8] [11] Beyond her university appointments, Johnson assumed leadership roles in professional theological organizations, notably serving as president of the Catholic Theological Society of America from 1997 to 1998.[2] Post-retirement, she has maintained involvement in public lectures and scholarly engagements, contributing to ongoing dialogues in Catholic theology.[12]

Theological Methodology

Feminist Lens in Theology

Elizabeth A. Johnson integrates feminism as a core hermeneutical principle in her theological approach, employing a three-phase method of deconstruction, reconstruction, and retrieval to reinterpret Catholic doctrine. Deconstruction unmasks patterns of patriarchal domination embedded in historical theological language and structures, while reconstruction draws on women's experiences and alternative sources to rebuild doctrine free from androcentric distortions. This process prioritizes empirical analysis of how male-dominated imagery has marginalized female perspectives in scripture and tradition.[7][13] Central to Johnson's method is the elevation of women's lived experience as a foundational resource for theology, which she identifies as historically overlooked in favor of elite male viewpoints. This experiential starting point enables critique of androcentric biblical interpretations that render women invisible or subordinate, such as renderings of divine wisdom traditions that downplay feminine agency. By grounding theology in concrete human realitiesâ€”particularly those of women navigating oppressionâ€”Johnson seeks to reveal causal links between linguistic exclusivity and doctrinal reinforcement of inequality.[7] Johnson emphasizes inclusive linguistic practices to counteract the effects of exclusively masculine God-language, which she argues constricts theological imagination and perpetuates cultural hierarchies. She calls for recovering overlooked female imagery from biblical and patristic sources, such as Sophia (Wisdom) as a maternal or companionate divine figure, to foster a more balanced symbolic repertoire without supplanting traditional metaphors. This retrieval aims to empirically demonstrate how diverse imagery expands doctrinal accessibility, drawing on scriptural precedents like Proverbs 8 and Isaiah 66 to support feminine divine attributions.[14][15][16]

Approach to Language and Metaphor for God

Elizabeth A. Johnson maintains that divine incomprehensibility precludes univocal predication, wherein human terms are applied to God as if identical to their creaturely meanings, insisting instead on metaphorical language drawn from created experience to gesture toward the transcendent mystery. In She Who Is (1992), she builds on analogical traditions from Aquinas, where affirmations of divine attributes (e.g., goodness) must be qualified by negation and transcendence, but prioritizes symbols that disclose relational dynamics over static definitions of essence.[17] Such metaphors, rooted in scriptural and worldly realities like nurturing or wisdom figures, function symbolically to evoke participation in God's self-disclosure without claiming exhaustive comprehension, linking human linguistic limits causally to the divine otherness while rejecting skeptical silence.[18] This metaphorical framework distinguishes religious language from literalism, which Johnson views as reductive, by emphasizing its invitational role in fostering encounter with the holy mystery rather than propositional closure. She argues for a multiplicity of imagesâ€”personal, cosmic, and experientialâ€”to counter any single metaphor's inadequacy, ensuring that God-talk remains open-ended yet grounded in revelation's relational core.[15] Johnson further critiques classical theism's predominant focus on immutability and aseity, which can render divine depictions abstract and aloof, favoring instead metaphors that convey vitality and involvement, such as those implying compassionate action within history. This shift underscores language's capacity to illuminate God's covenantal nearness, derived from creation's participatory reflection of the creator, without dissolving into anthropomorphism or equivocity.[18] By this token, theological discourse becomes a symbolic praxis that honors mystery's depth through diverse, non-totalizing expressions.[17]

Integration of Experience and Doctrine

Johnson's revisionist theological methodology prioritizes contemporary lived experienceâ€”especially that of women, the poor, and the natural worldâ€”as a starting point for doctrinal reflection, diverging from approaches reliant solely on scriptural exegesis or magisterial pronouncements. This entails a three-phase process of deconstructing patriarchal or anthropocentric biases in traditional formulations, seeking alternative sources in scripture and history, and reconstructing doctrine to address present realities.[19] Her approach grounds abstract claims in empirical human encounters, such as narratives of oppression and resilience, to test and refine faith assertions against observable causal patterns in social and environmental suffering.[20] Central to this integration is Johnson's emphasis on theology from the vantage of the marginalized, akin to perspectives in liberation theology, where doctrine emerges from the "underside" of history amid exclusion and injustice rather than elite abstraction. She incorporates experiences of suffering, including women's subordination and ecological degradation, as revelatory loci that illuminate divine action, arguing that ignoring these distorts catholicity by sidelining voices essential to holistic truth-seeking.[21] In works addressing suffering, Johnson draws on firsthand accounts of vulnerability to critique doctrines that imply divine impassibility detached from creaturely pain, proposing instead a relational ontology where God's involvement is inferred from patterns of endurance and hope in adversity.[22] Ecological dimensions further exemplify this method, as Johnson integrates scientific data on evolutionary processesâ€”including predation, extinction, and widespread sufferingâ€”to inform creational doctrine, positing God's immanence within nature's empirical dynamics rather than a deistic oversight. In Ask the Beasts: Darwin and the God of Love (2014), she acknowledges that life's history entails inherent pain and death driving adaptation, using this evidence to advocate a theology of divine companionship in creation's struggles, countering views that exempt God from causal involvement in natural processes.[23][24] Johnson balances experiential input with ecclesial tradition, particularly in her treatment of saints, where personal narratives of fidelity amid trials serve as concrete exemplars that vitalize doctrinal categories like the communion of saints. In Friends of God and Prophets (1998), she retrieves hagiographical stories not as static ideals but as dynamic testimonies integrating individual suffering with communal memory, thereby grounding abstract eschatology in verifiable patterns of transformative grace across history.[25] This synthesis avoids subordinating doctrine to subjectivity by subjecting experiences to critical scrutiny against tradition's enduring claims, fostering a praxis-oriented faith responsive to causal realities of marginalization.[19]

Doctrinal Positions

Views on Divine Transcendence and Immanence

Elizabeth A. Johnson affirms that divine transcendence entails God's existence as incomprehensible mystery, surpassing all human categories, concepts, and images, while divine immanence denotes God's intimate presence and active involvement within creation's realities.[26][27] In She Who Is: The Mystery of God in Feminist Theological Discourse (1992), she describes God as the "holy mystery of being" who is "outside of all classes and categories" yet relational and engaged, rejecting any dichotomy that pits otherness against nearness.[27][28] This dual emphasis aligns with her view that transcendence and immanence function as correlatives, not opposites, preserving God's freedom from creaturely dependenceâ€”termed aseity in traditional theologyâ€”while underscoring participatory presence in worldly becoming.[28][29] Johnson critiques classical theistic models that prioritize transcendence to the extent of implying divine detachment or indifference toward creation's contingencies, such as evolutionary flux or historical contingencies, arguing they obscure God's vivifying agency.[28][30] In Quest for the Living God (2007), she contends that an overemphasis on God's "otherness" risks portraying the divine as aloof from suffering or natural processes, thereby necessitating metaphors that evoke compassionate involvement without compromising incomprehensibility.[29][31] Drawing on sources like Karl Rahner, she integrates these poles to affirm God's self-communication as both utterly free and creatively sustaining.[31] To reconcile these dimensions, Johnson proposes panentheistic models wherein the divine reality encompasses and indwells the universeâ€”every part existing within Godâ€”while maintaining distinction to avoid pantheistic fusion, a framework she terms compatible with empirical observations of interconnected systems.[32][29] This "panentheism-lite" approach, evident in her endorsement of God as the intimate ground of being, counters deistic remoteness by positing creation's processes as loci of divine lure and persuasion rather than unilateral control.[33][34] Johnson substantiates immanence through interdisciplinary engagement, invoking natural sciences like Darwinian evolution to depict God's activity as non-coercive companionship with emergent life forms, as explored in Ask the Beasts: Darwin and the God of Love (2014).[30][23] Similarly, she roots divine nearness in human experiences of affliction, portraying God as companion to the vulnerable without implying change in the divine essence, thus challenging impassive transcendence as inadequate for causal realism in a world marked by contingency and pain.[29][35] These elements highlight tensions with scholastic aseity, where God's absolute self-sufficiency precludes any participatory dependence, yet Johnson prioritizes experiential and evidential integration over strict metaphysical isolation.[29]

Trinitarian Theology

Elizabeth A. Johnson conceptualizes the Trinity as a model of pure relationality, wherein the divine persons exist in eternal, mutual self-giving without isolation or hierarchy, serving as a paradigm for interconnected existence.[36] This framework draws on the patristic notion of perichoresis, or mutual indwelling, to depict the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as interpenetrating one another in a dynamic communion that mirrors communal human relations yet transcends them through divine aseity.[37] Unlike classical formulations emphasizing substantial unity with ordered relations (as in Augustine's psychological analogy or the Cappadocian emphasis on monarchy of the Father), Johnson's approach foregrounds relational dynamism, potentially attenuating ontological distinctions in favor of egalitarian reciprocity, which critics argue risks modalism by underemphasizing personal hypostases.[38] Central to her Trinitarian vision is the reclamation of the Holy Spirit as an active, empowering presence often marginalized in patriarchal traditions, reframed not as passive feminine counterpart but as "Holy Spirit Power" enabling liberation and vitality.[39] Johnson critiques historical overshadowing of the Spirit's role, advocating its portrayal as the bond of love (vinculum caritatis) that animates creation and human experience, fostering feminist recovery of pneumatic agency without subordinating it to Father-Son economies.[40] This avoids subordinationism by insisting on the Spirit's coequal procession and operation, aligning with conciliar affirmations of consubstantiality while integrating experiential dimensions of divine presence in diverse cultural contexts.[16] Johnson links Trinitarian life causally to creation through ongoing economic emanation, where the immanent relations of mutual indwelling overflow into sustaining the cosmos, portraying God not as distant monarch but as intimately involved in evolutionary processes and ecological interdependence.[7] This relational emanation posits creation as participatory in divine communion, evoking Rahner's axiom that the economic Trinity reveals the immanent, yet diverges from patristic creation ex nihilo by emphasizing continuous perichoretic influence over punctiliar fiat, which may blur creator-creature distinctions in favor of panentheistic tendencies.[41] Such a model underscores Trinitarian causality as generative love, avoiding Arian-like subordination while inviting critique for anthropomorphizing divine aseity through human relational analogies.[42]

Christology and Divine Suffering

Johnson's Christology underscores the full humanity of Jesus as portrayed in the New Testament, particularly in the Synoptic Gospels and Pauline letters, where he experiences hunger, temptation, anguish, and crucifixion, thereby embodying divine solidarity with human affliction.[13] In her 1996 edition of Consider Jesus: Waves of Renewal in Christology, she interprets this humanity through the lens of kenosis, drawing on Philippians 2:5-11 to depict the pre-existent Logos voluntarily divesting divine privileges to enter fully into creaturely limitations and suffering, not as a diminution of divinity but as its free self-gift for redemption.[13] This kenotic framework, rooted in scriptural witness to Jesus' real emotions and physical tormentsâ€”such as his cry of dereliction in Mark 15:34â€”positions Christ as the paradigm of God's empathetic companionship amid evil, contrasting with abstract metaphysical speculations that minimize Jesus' concrete historicity.[43] Rejecting absolute divine impassibility as formulated in classical theismâ€”which posits God as incapable of passion to preserve aseityâ€”Johnson contends that such impassibility risks portraying God as detached from creation's pain, incompatible with the Incarnation's revelation of a God who chooses vulnerability.[44] Instead, she advocates for a voluntary divine pathos, where God's eternal compassion manifests temporally in Christ's suffering without implying change in the divine essence or coercion by creaturely forces, thus avoiding the necessitarian passibility of process theology that subordinates God to evolving cosmic processes.[45] This stance aligns with patristic insights into the communicatio idiomatum, wherein the divine person of the Son assumes human suffering, disclosing an intrinsic divine capacity for relational fellow-feeling that is freely exercised rather than metaphysically necessitated.[43] Johnson integrates this Christology with feminist theological priorities by challenging atonement theories that emphasize divine wrath appeased through sacrificial victimhood, such as Anselm of Canterbury's satisfaction model, which she views as perpetuating cycles of redemptive violence and glorifying submissive endurance of abuse.[46] In Creation and the Cross (2018), she reframes the cross not as a transaction satisfying punitive justice but as the ultimate expression of kenotic solidarity, where God's non-coercive love absorbs suffering to liberate creation, thereby critiquing patriarchal interpretations that sacralize exploitation while privileging Jesus' prophetic resistance to injustice over passive oblation.[47] This approach maintains scriptural fidelity to the Paschal mysteryâ€”death yielding to resurrectionâ€”while redirecting focus from forensic satisfaction to transformative companionship, cautioning against models that might endorse systemic harms under the guise of divine mandate.[13]

Mariology and Communion of Saints

Johnson's 1998 work Friends of God and Prophets: A Feminist Theological Reading of the Communion of Saints reexamines the doctrine by foregrounding the saints' collective historical witness as exemplars of discipleship, grounded in documented practices of popular devotion across centuries rather than reduced to sparse conciliar definitions.[48] She posits the communion as an interconnected network of holy lives, where empirical records of martyrdoms, charitable acts, and prophetic stancesâ€”such as those chronicled in early martyrologies and medieval vitaeâ€”illustrate causal links between faith responses and communal flourishing, eschewing dogmatic abstraction for tangible patterns of graced living.[49] This approach highlights how saints' stories, verified through hagiographic sources and liturgical traditions dating to the patristic era, serve as prophetic critiques of injustice, fostering solidarity among believers.[50] Central to this framework is Mary's role as a foremost model of graced freedom, depicted in Johnson's 2003 book Truly Our Sister: A Theology of Mary in the Communion of Saints as a first-century Galilean Jewish woman whose affirmative response to the annunciation (Luke 1:38) embodies human cooperation with divine initiative amid socioeconomic precarity, without implying an exemption from the universal human condition of moral struggle.[51] Johnson critiques developments in hyperdulia that inflate Marian privileges beyond biblical narratives and early creedal attestations, such as post-scholasitc elaborations emphasizing perpetual virginity or co-redemptive roles unsupported by direct scriptural or patristic evidence, arguing these divert from Mary's prophetic discipleship toward devotional excesses that obscure her as a relatable paradigm.[52] Instead, she prioritizes historical devotionâ€”evident in second-century prayers like the Sub Tuum Praesidium and archaeological iconsâ€”as causal indicators of Mary's integrative place in the saints' chorus, promoting a companionship theology aligned with empirical faith expressions.[53] Johnson broadens the communion to encompass unnamed holy women whose sanctity is inferred from collective memory and material traces, such as inscriptions on ancient ossuaries or communal feasts in apocryphal traditions, challenging canonizations skewed toward elite male figures and restoring causal recognition of grassroots piety's role in ecclesial vitality.[54] This inclusive retrieval, drawn from women's historical practices of storytelling and relic veneration documented in monastic records from the fourth century onward, underscores the doctrine's realism in reflecting diverse human holiness rather than a minimalist roster of canonized names.[25]

Ecological Dimensions of Creation Theology

In her 2014 book Ask the Beasts: Darwin and the God of Love, Johnson integrates Charles Darwin's evolutionary framework with Christian doctrine, positing that the natural world's processes reveal divine love and serve as a sacramental disclosure of God's creative activity. Drawing from Job 12:7â€”"Ask the beasts, and they will teach you"â€”she argues that evolutionary biology, including mechanisms like natural selection, manifests God's ongoing providence without contradicting faith, as divine causality operates through the immanent empowerment of creaturely agency rather than direct intervention.[55][56] This approach critiques traditional views of providence that prioritize human exceptionalism, instead emphasizing a causal realism where God's sustaining presence permeates evolutionary contingencies, such as random mutations and environmental adaptations, fostering biodiversity as an expression of divine generosity.[23] Johnson's ecological theology explicitly challenges anthropocentric interpretations of scripture and doctrine, which she sees as diminishing the intrinsic value of non-human creation by subordinating it to human utility. She advocates for a "community of creation" ethic, akin to a biotic covenant, where humans participate as kin within interdependent ecosystems rather than as stewards exercising dominion, aligning biblical mandates like Genesis 9's rainbow covenantâ€”extended interpretively to all lifeâ€”with empirical observations of ecological interdependence.[57] This framework counters dominion-based anthropocentrism by rooting moral imperatives in the observed reality of species co-evolution and habitat reciprocity, urging theological reflection on human-induced disruptions like habitat loss, documented in biological data on extinction rates exceeding natural baselines by factors of 100 to 1,000.[58] Extending this in Come, Have Breakfast: Meditations on God and the Earth (2024), Johnson employs scriptural imageryâ€”such as the post-resurrection breakfast in John 21â€”to frame Earth as a site of divine encounter amid its vulnerability to climate degradation and biodiversity collapse. She links doctrinal affirmations of creation's goodness to contemporary environmental crises, including rising global temperatures averaging 1.1Â°C above pre-industrial levels since 1850 and associated biotic shifts, interpreting these as calls for repentance and restorative action grounded in God's promissory fidelity to the whole cosmos.[59] Through this lens, creation theology demands praxis-oriented responses, such as mitigating anthropogenic forcings like greenhouse gas emissions, which empirical models project could drive irreversible tipping points if unchecked, thereby honoring the sacramental integrity of evolutionary processes under divine oversight.[60]

Major Works and Publications

Early Publications on Christology and Mary

Elizabeth A. Johnson's initial contributions to Christology appeared in Consider Jesus: Waves of Renewal in Christology, first published in 1985 by Crossroad Publishing and revised in a second edition in 1990.[13] In this 160-page work, Johnson traces three historical "waves" of Christological renewal within Catholic theology: the first centered on the quest for the historical Jesus in the early 20th century, the second on kerygmatic and New Testament emphases post-World War II, and the third on contemporary contextual approaches including liberation and feminist perspectives.[61] She argues that these developments enrich the understanding of Jesus' identity by integrating scriptural, historical, and experiential dimensions, urging readers to "consider Jesus" as liberator and companion amid modern social realities.[62] Turning to Mariology, Johnson's 1987 book Dangerous Memories: A Mosaic of Mary in Scripture presents Mary of Nazareth as a prophetic figure whose biblical narratives embody resistance to oppressive power structures.[63] Drawing from scriptural mosaics such as the Magnificat and Mary's role at the cross, Johnson interprets these "dangerous memories" as evoking solidarity with the marginalized, positioning Mary not as a passive icon but as a disciple whose fiat challenges patriarchal and imperial dominations in both ancient and contemporary contexts.[64] The work, spanning scriptural exegesis and theological reflection, establishes Mary's significance in fostering communal memory that critiques injustice while affirming her place within the broader narrative of salvation history.[63] In She Who Is: The Mystery of God in Feminist Theological Discourse, published in 1992 by Crossroad, Johnson extends her Christological interests by critiquing anthropomorphic and androcentric language for God, advocating symbols that reveal divine mystery through inclusive metaphors drawn from feminist experience.[65] This 316-page volume systematically addresses the Trinity, incarnation, and divine attributes, arguing that traditional male imagery obscures God's ineffability and proposing feminine symbolsâ€”like "She Who Is" from Exodusâ€”as complementary to enrich Trinitarian discourse without pantheistic reduction.[66] The book received acclaim for advancing theological language reforms, including the 1993 University of Louvain's Grimm Prize for outstanding theological work.[67]

Quest for the Living God (2007)

Quest for the Living God: Mapping Frontiers in the Theology of God was published in October 2007 by Continuum International Publishing Group.[68] The 233-page volume synthesizes theological developments on the doctrine of God within Christianity since the mid-twentieth century, framing them as a "renaissance of new insights" amid contextual shifts including liberation movements, feminist perspectives, and ecological concerns.[69] Johnson structures the work around discrete conceptual chapters, beginning with methodological ground rules for theological discourse on divine mystery, followed by explorations of God in relation to suffering, the Holy Spirit, creation, and interreligious encounter.[68][70] Central to the book's argument is the portrayal of God as an inexhaustible "holy mystery" transcending all human naming and conceptual grasp, yet dynamically present and knowable through symbolic language, historical revelation, and lived experience.[71] Johnson surveys images of the divine drawn from global Christian theologies, including Latin American liberation theology's emphasis on God's preferential option for the poor, African-American traditions' focus on liberating power, and Asian-influenced reflections on divine relationality, critiquing aspects of classical Western theology for an overly abstract, static theocentrism detached from worldly praxis.[72][73] These chapters highlight how diverse cultural and experiential lenses reveal facets of the "living God"â€”a biblical term denoting vitality and engagementâ€”while incorporating elements of religious pluralism by affirming that authentic encounters with the transcendent occur beyond Christianity's boundaries, though rooted in Christocentric revelation.[74][75] Upon release, the book received initial acclaim in theological circles for its concise mapping of contemporary frontiers in God-talk, praised as a faithful yet innovative summary grounded in scripture and tradition.[76] Reviews noted its energy in distilling complex ideas from major theologians like Karl Rahner, Jon Sobrino, and JÃ¼rgen Moltmann, positioning it as a resource for rethinking divine attributes amid modern challenges.[77] By 2008, it had garnered positive assessments for advancing relational and experiential approaches over scholastic abstraction, influencing seminary curricula and discussions on doctrinal renewal.[72][77]

Later Works on Saints, Ecology, and God-Talk

In Abounding in Kindness: Writings for the People of God (2015), Johnson compiled essays spanning her career, emphasizing a compassionate divine image rooted in biblical depictions of God as merciful and relational, aimed at accessible theological reflection for lay audiences.[78] The volume addresses themes of divine benevolence amid human suffering, drawing on scriptural motifs like Exodus 34:6 to portray God as "abounding in kindness" (hesed), while integrating praxis-oriented calls for justice that extend to ecological stewardship.[79] This work reflects an evolution from abstract doctrinal inquiry to practical theology, responding implicitly to post-2011 critiques by prioritizing lived faith over speculative frontiers.[80] Johnson's ecological theology deepened in Ask the Beasts: Darwin and the God of Love (2014), where she reconciles evolutionary biology with Christian doctrine, arguing that God's creative love sustains biodiversity through natural selection rather than supernatural intervention.[2] Engaging Darwin's observations alongside Genesis and Romans 8, the book posits creation as a dynamic process of divine generosity, critiquing anthropocentric views that exacerbate environmental degradation. This represents a shift toward environmental praxis, urging theological language (God-talk) to incorporate scientific realism for addressing climate imperatives, without altering core Trinitarian frameworks but expanding immanence to nonhuman creatures.[24] Subsequent publications intensified this focus, as in Creation and the Cross: The Mercy of God for a Planet in Peril (2018), which reinterprets atonement through an ecological lens, linking Christ's passion to the groaning of creation under human-induced crises like species extinction and habitat loss. Johnson draws on patristic sources and contemporary dataâ€”such as the IPCC's projections of irreversible biodiversity declineâ€”to frame redemption as cosmic mercy extending to all life forms, advocating praxis like policy advocacy and ascetic restraint.[81] Her God-talk here evolves to emphasize divine solidarity with vulnerable ecosystems, countering critiques of earlier works by grounding inclusive metaphors in scriptural ecology (e.g., Job 38â€“41).[82] In Come Have Breakfast: Meditations on God and the Earth (2024), Johnson offers scriptural reflections tying Johannine imagery to planetary emergencies, with 52 meditations linking texts like John 21 to calls for earth-care as eucharistic response.[82] Published amid escalating data on global warming (e.g., 1.2Â°C rise since pre-industrial levels per 2023 reports), it promotes contemplative praxis for ecological conversion, portraying saintsâ€”historical and contemporaryâ€”as intercessors for creation's communion, thus weaving hagiography into environmental theology. This late work sustains her God-talk tradition, adapting metaphors of divine companionship to urgent crises without formal revisions but through applicative depth.

Controversies and Critiques

USCCB Committee on Doctrine Assessment (2011)

The United States Conference of Catholic Bishops' (USCCB) Committee on Doctrine issued a formal statement on March 24, 2011, evaluating Quest for the Living God: Mapping Frontiers in the Theology of God (2007) by Sister Elizabeth A. Johnson, C.S.J.[83] The 21-page document, authorized by the committee chaired by Bishop William E. Lori, concluded that the book constitutes a "serious inadequacy" in presenting the Catholic theological understanding of God, particularly in its handling of divine transcendence, personhood, and relations within the Trinity.[84] This assessment arose from concerns over the text's adoption in seminary and college curricula, prompting the committee to scrutinize its fidelity to magisterial teaching despite the book's post-publication circulation without prior episcopal review.[85] The committee emphasized that its review aligns with the bishops' canonical mandate to safeguard doctrinal integrity, as articulated in Canon 823 Â§1 of the Code of Canon Law, which reserves to the Roman Pontiff and bishops the authority to oversee writings on faith and morals to ensure they do not harm the faithful.[86] In the statement, the committee highlighted specific omissions and ambiguities, such as the book's metaphorical treatment of divine attributes that risks undermining classical theistic affirmations of God's simplicity, immutability, and personal nature as upheld in documents like the Catechism of the Catholic Church and conciliar teachings.[83] The evaluation framed the work as exploratory theology that, while mapping contemporary frontiers, fails to integrate essential elements of the Church's revealed doctrine, potentially misleading readers on core mysteries like God's self-revelation.[84] Following Johnson's detailed "observations" submitted on June 1, 2011, the committee reaffirmed its critique in an October 11, 2011, response, determining that her clarifications did not resolve the identified doctrinal shortcomings.[3][87] This 11-page document, released publicly on October 28, 2011, reiterated the original concerns without alteration, underscoring the bishops' prerogative to issue such judgments to protect the deposit of faith entrusted to their pastoral care.[88] The process exemplified the committee's role in collaborative episcopal oversight, distinct from individual approbatur but binding in advisory force for Catholic educational contexts.[86]

Specific Doctrinal Errors Identified

The USCCB Committee on Doctrine's 2011 assessment of Elizabeth Johnson's Quest for the Living God pinpointed errors in the metaphysical portrayal of God, particularly in the chapter on God as Spirit, where panentheism is endorsed such that "everything abides in God, who in turn encompasses everything," rendering the created order ontologically constitutive of divine being and eroding the Creator's radical transcendence from creation.[85] This depiction undermines the personal nature of the Trinity by treating traditional language of divine persons as merely symbolic, without affirming the real, subsistent relations within the Godhead as revealed in Scripture and defined by conciliar doctrine, thereby risking a conception of God as an impersonal force rather than three distinct persons in eternal communion.[85] Such views contravene the Catechism of the Catholic Church, which holds that God exists wholly apart from creatures in aseity and simplicity.[85] In discussions of redemption, the book introduces ambiguities by asserting that "people are saved through the practice of their religion, not despite it," implying salvific efficacy in non-Christian faiths via the Spirit's universal presence independent of explicit faith in Christ, which dilutes the Christocentric necessity of salvation through Jesus alone as the unique mediator.[85] This fosters a de facto universalism detached from the Church's teaching on the objective order of redemption, conflicting with Dominus Iesus (2000), which affirms that while God's grace operates beyond the visible Church, full salvation requires union with Christ and his body.[85][89] On revelation, Johnson maintains that all theological language functions as metaphorâ€”"like a finger pointing to the moon, not the moon itself"â€”rejecting the analogia entis whereby creatures analogically participate in and reveal God's essence, and subordinating Scripture's sufficiency to human experience as the primary locus of divine self-communication.[85] This approach excludes the objective adequacy of biblical revelation for knowing God truly, though imperfectly, in favor of subjective pluralism, opposing Dei Verbum (1965) and the Catechism, which uphold Scripture and Tradition as the normative sources of faith under the Church's interpretive authority.[85] The Committee's critique extends to pluralism, where the text advocates diverse religious experiences as equally valid avenues to the divine without prioritizing Christ's revelation, thereby promoting indifferentism that equates incompatible truth claims and neglects the uniqueness of Christian dogma as set forth in Lumen Gentium (1964).[85] These errors collectively impair the book's fidelity to Catholic metaphysics, which demands God's immutability, simplicity, and personal relationality as foundational.[85]

Johnson's Responses and Defenses

In June 2011, Johnson issued a 38-page document titled "To Speak Rightly of the Living God: Observations by Dr. Elizabeth A. Johnson, CSJ, on the Statement of the Committee on Doctrine," asserting that the USCCB Committee on Doctrine had "thoroughly misunderstood and consistently misrepresented" her book Quest for the Living God.[3] She argued that the committee misclassified her work as catechetical instruction rather than speculative theology, which explores and interprets doctrine rather than merely restating it, and contended that this error stemmed from a failure to engage her text on its own terms.[90] Johnson emphasized a lack of pre-publication dialogue, noting that the committee did not consult her directly before issuing its critique, which she viewed as a departure from collaborative norms between bishops and theologians.[90] She defended her theological approach as faithful to the Second Vatican Council's Dei Verbum, which encourages ongoing development in understanding the faith through contemplation, study, and preaching, rather than rigid adherence to traditional formulas.[90] In her view, the committee's emphasis on literal repetition of doctrinal language reflected an overly narrow framework that undervalued innovative expressions suited to contemporary contexts.[3] In subsequent public statements, including a October 2011 letter responding to the committee's rebuttal of her observations, Johnson reiterated procedural concerns and framed certain episcopal arguments as circular, pointing to biblical revelations in patriarchal societies as evidence that divine communication adapts to cultural forms without endorsing them as normative.[91] She portrayed the overall critique as potentially stifling theological inquiry, aligning it with broader patterns of institutional resistance to women's voices in doctrinal discourse.[90]

Broader Catholic Hierarchical and Scholarly Reactions

In April 2014, Cardinal Gerhard Ludwig MÃ¼ller, prefect of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (CDF), publicly rebuked the Leadership Conference of Women Religious (LCWR) for selecting Elizabeth Johnson to receive their Outstanding Achievement Award, despite the USCCB's prior identification of serious doctrinal issues in her Quest for the Living God. During an April 30 address to LCWR leaders in Rome, MÃ¼ller characterized the choice as "a rather open provocation against the Holy See," arguing it would further distance the organization from the bishops and the "ecclesial center of the faith," thereby undermining reform efforts under CDF oversight.[92][93] This Vatican intervention underscored a broader hierarchical consensus on the need to prioritize doctrinal fidelity over honors for theologians whose works diverge from established teachings on divine transcendence and the Trinity. Conservative Catholic scholars and outlets have echoed these concerns, warning that Johnson's panentheistic leaningsâ€”evident in her portrayals of God as deeply intertwined with creation's suffering and evolutionâ€”risk collapsing the essential Catholic distinction between Creator and creature, potentially veering toward pantheism. For instance, commentators in First Things critiqued her methodology for reducing divine mystery to metaphorical constructs that dilute Trinitarian orthodoxy, while resources hosted by Catholic Culture highlighted how such ambiguities misrepresent core dogmas like divine impassibility and aseity.[29] These analyses emphasize that speculative theology must remain anchored in magisterial norms to avoid fostering confusion among the faithful. In contrast, progressive Catholic academics, including figures publishing in Commonweal and the National Catholic Reporter, defended Johnson by distinguishing her exploratory work from catechesis and lamenting hierarchical interventions as overly rigid, yet orthodox responders countered that such defenses exacerbate ecclesial polarization by elevating personal theological visions over communal adherence to revealed truth.[94][95] This divide illustrates ongoing tensions, with hierarchical and traditional scholarly voices prioritizing safeguards against heterodoxy to preserve unity.

Reception and Legacy

Awards, Honors, and Academic Influence

Johnson has received fifteen honorary doctorates from institutions including Boston College, Loyola University Chicago, and the University of St. Thomas.[8][10] In 1993, she was awarded the Louisville Grawemeyer Award in Religion for her book She Who Is: The Mystery of God in Feminist Theological Discourse, recognizing its contribution to theological discourse.[96][97] The Catholic Theological Society of America (CTSA) conferred its John Courtney Murray Award upon Johnson in 2004 for distinguished achievement in theology.[98][2] In 2014, the Leadership Conference of Women Religious (LCWR) presented Johnson with its Outstanding Leadership Award, acknowledging her role in advancing women's perspectives in Catholic theology amid ongoing Vatican oversight of the organization.[10] Johnson's academic influence is evidenced by her tenure as president of the CTSA, the oldest and largest professional association of Catholic theologians in North America, and her position as Distinguished Professor Emerita of Theology at Fordham University, where she taught from 1991 to 2018.[2][99]

Impact on Feminist and Progressive Catholic Theology

Elizabeth A. Johnson has been a pivotal architect in the development of Catholic feminist theology, particularly in the post-Vatican II era, where her scholarship advanced critiques of patriarchal language in liturgy and doctrine while retrieving biblical and patristic sources for feminine divine imagery.[7] Her work encouraged theologians to reexamine classical categories like imago Dei through women's experiences, influencing progressive efforts to reconceive God-talk as more inclusive without abandoning Trinitarian orthodoxy.[100] This approach resonated in gender studies within Catholic academia, where her texts are frequently referenced for bridging symbolic theology with social justice concerns.[7] Johnson's influence extends to bolstering women religious and lay leadership in theology, as evidenced by her receipt of the Leadership Conference of Women Religious' Outstanding Leadership Award in 2014, recognizing her as a preeminent U.S. Catholic feminist thinker who models intellectual engagement for female scholars and practitioners.[10] By advocating for women's voices in ecclesial discourseâ€”such as reinterpreting Marian and saintly traditionsâ€”she contributed to initiatives expanding lay theological education and religious community governance, fostering environments where women address issues like sexism and authority from within Catholic frameworks.[5] In pluralistic settings, Johnson's integration of ecofeminism with global perspectives has informed progressive theologies engaging the global South, through writings that link environmental ethics, world religions, and feminist critiques of globalization's impacts on marginalized communities.[54] Her emphasis on contextual divine symbols has supported dialogues in diverse cultural contexts, promoting adaptive theisms that incorporate non-Western insights while maintaining Catholic roots, thus aiding movements for intercultural solidarity in theology.[101]

Orthodox Catholic Critiques and Fidelity Debates

Orthodox Catholic critics, including those aligned with traditionalist perspectives, have charged Elizabeth Johnson's theology with fostering relativism by emphasizing the provisional and symbolic nature of doctrinal language, which they argue undermines the magisterium's authoritative claims to objective, unchanging truth. In works like Quest for the Living God, Johnson's exploration of diverse theological frontiers is seen as prioritizing human experience and cultural adaptation over the fixed deposit of faith, potentially leading to a modernist dilution where dogmas become mere symbols open to endless reinterpretation rather than binding realities. This approach, detractors contend, erodes the coherence of Catholic belief by implying that core truths, such as the Trinity's real distinctions, are negotiable constructs rather than divinely revealed essences, echoing condemned errors of theological relativism.[102] Further critiques highlight Johnson's promotion of feminine and maternal imagery for God as veering toward anthropomorphism, risking idolatry by blurring the Creator-creature distinction upheld in Thomistic analogy and conciliar teachings like those of the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), which prohibit depicting the divine essence in corporeal terms. Traditional voices argue that while scriptural metaphors (e.g., God as rock or eagle) serve to evoke transcendence, Johnson's expansive symbolic innovationsâ€”framed as corrective to patriarchal "idols"â€”invite confusion between analogical accommodation and literal attribution, thereby compromising fidelity to Aquinas's insistence on God's pure act and incomprehensibility. Such shifts, they maintain, depart from the Church's patristic and medieval safeguards against representing God through created likenesses, potentially reducing divine mystery to immanent projections. Debates persist on whether these theological innovations impair evangelization by obscuring doctrinal clarity essential for conversion and formation, with critics asserting that ambiguities in Johnson's framework hinder effective proclamation of the Gospel's unchanging kerygma. In an era of widespread catechetical confusion, her methods are faulted for accommodating secular pluralism at the expense of robust apologetics, fostering a faith less resilient against skepticism and less compelling for seekers drawn to Catholicism's metaphysical rigor. Orthodox commentators warn that this causal trajectoryâ€”prioritizing inclusivity over precisionâ€”weakens the Church's witness, as evidenced by the bishops' concerns over her book's influence in educational settings where it could mislead laity toward a fragmented orthodoxy.[103]

Recent Developments and Ongoing Influence (Post-2020)

In 2024, Johnson published Come, Have Breakfast: Meditations on God and the Earth, a volume of scriptural reflections portraying God as a hospitable figure inviting all creation to sustenance, emphasizing ecological themes drawn from biblical narratives such as John 21:12.[59] The book, issued by Orbis Books, builds on Johnson's longstanding interest in theology's intersection with environmental concerns, offering meditations that reframe divine attributes like creator and lover in relation to Earth's crises.[60] Johnson has maintained an active speaking schedule despite her emerita status at Fordham University, delivering public addresses on ecology and Christology. In May 2024, she presented "Jesus and the Earth," a lecture exploring Jesus' ministry in relation to creation, hosted and recorded for online access.[104] Additional engagements include a November 2024 lunchtime talk reiterating these themes and a discussion of her 2024 book at the Ex Libris Author Lecture Series.[105][106] Her post-2020 output has contributed to Catholic ecological discourse, aligning with papal emphases on care for creation without sparking notable doctrinal disputes akin to earlier critiques.[24] Johnson's emphasis on inclusive theological language for God and Earth resonates in progressive circles addressing women's participation and environmental stewardship, though her influence remains debated among orthodox commentators for prioritizing experiential reinterpretations over strict adherence to traditional formulations.[35] No major hierarchical investigations or public rebukes have emerged since 2020, allowing her emerita-era work to circulate primarily through academic and publishing channels.[107]
