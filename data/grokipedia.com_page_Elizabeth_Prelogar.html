Elizabeth Prelogar

Elizabeth Barchas Prelogar (born 1980) is an American lawyer who served as the 48th Solicitor General of the United States from October 2021 to January 2025.[1] Born and raised in Boise, Idaho, Prelogar graduated from Harvard Law School magna cum laude in 2008, where she served as an articles editor for the Harvard Law Review.[1] Following law school, she clerked for Judge Merrick Garland on the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit and for Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg on the Supreme Court.[1] In her role as Solicitor Generalâ€”the government's top advocate before the Supreme Courtâ€”Prelogar argued over 20 cases, including pivotal disputes on abortion access, racial preferences in university admissions, and the scope of federal regulatory power.[2] Her arguments often defended Biden administration positions amid a conservative-leaning Court majority, yielding mixed outcomes such as partial successes in preserving workplace religious accommodation precedents but reversals in affirmative action and agency deference doctrines.[3] Prior to her confirmation, Prelogar held positions including assistant to Solicitor General Neal Katyal and special counsel in the Justice Department's Civil Division, where she contributed to litigation upholding statutes protecting domestic violence victims.[1][4] After leaving office, she joined the Harvard Law School faculty as a lecturer in appellate advocacy.[5]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Elizabeth Prelogar was born Elizabeth Barchas on March 7, 1980, at St. Luke's Boise Medical Center in Boise, Idaho, to Rudolf "Rudy" D. Barchas and Jeanne Barchas (nÃ©e Bullock).[6] Her father, who died in 2019 from Parkinson's disease, was an Idaho lawyer who graduated from Hastings College of the Law in 1969 and served as the first head of the Idaho Attorney General's Office Consumer Protection Division.[6] [7] Her mother worked for over 20 years as a special education teacher in the Boise School District.[8] Prelogar was the youngest of four children, with older siblings Eric Barchas, Leah Nicholas (nÃ©e Barchas), and Joshua Daniel Barchas, the latter of whom died shortly after birth in the 1970s.[7] Raised in Boise, Prelogar attended local public schools, graduating from Boise High School in 1998.[9] One of her earliest memories involved accompanying her mother to observe her father arguing a criminal trial in an Idaho courtroom, an experience that alternated between boredom and fascination for the young child.[8] Demonstrating early academic precocity, she enrolled in college-level courses at Boise State University while still in middle school, around age 12.[10] Her family's legal background provided an initial exposure to the profession, though Prelogar initially considered journalism, contributing to her high school newspaper and a teen panel for the Idaho Statesman.[10]

Academic achievements

Prelogar began taking college courses at Boise State University at the age of 12 while still in middle school, demonstrating early academic precocity through advanced English classes attended before regular school hours.[8] She earned a bachelor's degree from Emory University, double majoring in English and Russian literature.[11] Following graduation in 2002, she received a master's degree in creative writing from the University of St. Andrews in Scotland as a Bobby Jones Scholar, a prestigious program funded by the Coca-Cola Company for Emory alumni to study at the University of St. Andrews or Oxford.[11][12] Prelogar obtained her Juris Doctor degree magna cum laude from Harvard Law School in 2008, ranking in the top five percent of her class.[13][1] During law school, she served as an articles editor for the Harvard Law Review, a position reflecting high scholarly distinction and involvement in editing legal scholarship.[13][14]

Professional career

Clerkships and private practice

Following her graduation from Harvard Law School in 2008, Prelogar commenced her legal career with a clerkship for Judge Merrick B. Garland of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, serving from 2008 to 2009.[1][15] She subsequently completed two consecutive one-year clerkships at the Supreme Court of the United States: first for Associate Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg during the 2010 term, followed by Associate Justice Elena Kagan during the 2011 term.[1][14][15] These positions provided her with direct exposure to complex constitutional and federal law matters, including drafting opinions and preparing for oral arguments.[16] Upon completing her Supreme Court clerkships in 2012, Prelogar transitioned to private practice as an associate in the appellate and Supreme Court litigation group at Hogan Lovells LLP in Washington, D.C.[1][17] There, she represented clients in high-profile appeals before the Supreme Court and federal courts of appeals, handling cases involving antitrust, intellectual property, and constitutional issues.[18] She remained at the firm for approximately two years, until joining the Office of the Solicitor General in 2014.[15][8]

Roles in the Department of Justice

Prelogar joined the Office of the Solicitor General in the Department of Justice in 2014 as an Assistant to the Solicitor General, where she contributed to briefing and arguing cases before the Supreme Court on behalf of the United States.[19][20] During her tenure in this role, which lasted until 2019, she handled appellate litigation responsibilities, including preparation of merits briefs and oral arguments in high-stakes federal cases.[19][17] From 2017 to 2019, while serving as an Assistant to the Solicitor General, Prelogar was detailed to the Special Counsel's Office led by Robert S. Mueller III, acting as an assistant special counsel in the investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. presidential election and related matters.[17][21] In this capacity, she supported appellate and litigation efforts, including reviewing evidence and drafting legal memoranda, though the investigation's findingsâ€”detailed in the Mueller Report released on March 22, 2019â€”concluded no sufficient evidence of conspiracy between the Trump campaign and Russia while deferring on obstruction due to Justice Department policy.[21] Prelogar departed the Department of Justice in late 2019 but returned in early 2021 as Principal Deputy Solicitor General under Noel Francisco's successor, assuming leadership duties in the office during the presidential transition.[1] On January 20, 2021, following the inauguration of President Joe Biden, she was appointed Acting Solicitor General, overseeing all Supreme Court litigation for the federal government until her formal confirmation in the role later that year; during this approximately nine-month period, she managed emergency applications, merits arguments, and office operations amid a docket including challenges to executive actions and statutory interpretations.[1][19]

Tenure as Solicitor General

Elizabeth Prelogar was confirmed as the 48th Solicitor General of the United States on October 28, 2021, by a Senate vote of 53-36 following her nomination by President Joe Biden on August 11, 2021.[22][23][20] She had previously served as acting Solicitor General since January 20, 2021, upon Biden's inauguration, after holding the position of assistant to the Solicitor General from 2014 to 2019.[17] In this role, Prelogar headed the Office of the Solicitor General, overseeing the preparation of briefs, the decision to seek Supreme Court review, and oral arguments representing the federal government in approximately 70-80% of cases heard by the Court each term.[24] Prelogar's tenure, spanning from her confirmation until January 20, 2025, involved supervising a team that filed numerous merits briefs and petitions, while she personally argued more than 20 cases before the Supreme Courtâ€”more than any other advocate since 2021â€”including high-profile matters on gun rights, social media regulation, reproductive rights, and electoral integrity.[13][25] Notable arguments included United States v. Rahimi (2024), defending a federal law prohibiting firearm possession by individuals under domestic violence restraining orders; TikTok, Inc. v. Garland (2024), supporting a law requiring the app's divestiture from Chinese ownership on national security grounds; and United States v. Skrmetti (2024), challenging Tennessee's restrictions on medical interventions for minors with gender dysphoria.[26] She also argued in Consumer Financial Protection Bureau v. Community Financial Services Association (2024), advocating for the constitutionality of the agency's funding structure.[26] Beyond oral advocacy, Prelogar directed the office's strategic litigation, such as supporting Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act in Allen v. Milligan (2023), where the Court upheld challenges to Alabama's congressional map for diluting Black voters' influence, a case she later described as central to protecting democracy.[27] Her office frequently shifted positions from prior administrations, including urging reversal of precedents on affirmative action and regulatory deference, reflecting the Biden administration's policy priorities.[28] In the 2023-2024 term alone, the Solicitor General's office participated in nearly 20% of argued cases, underscoring Prelogar's intensive involvement amid a docket heavy with challenges to federal authority.[28]

Supreme Court arguments and legal positions

Major cases argued

Prelogar argued more than 30 cases before the U.S. Supreme Court during her tenure as Solicitor General, representing the federal government in disputes spanning constitutional law, administrative agency authority, and civil rights.[13] Among the most prominent were challenges to reproductive rights regulations, where she defended executive branch positions on abortion access and emergency medical standards. In Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (oral argument December 1, 2021), Prelogar urged the Court to uphold the viability standard from Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey as precedent, arguing that Mississippi's 15-week abortion ban conflicted with established federal constitutional protections for pre-viability abortions while acknowledging state interests in fetal life post-viability.[29] The government maintained that overturning Roe would undermine reliance interests and judicial stare decisis without compelling justification. Prelogar represented the Food and Drug Administration in FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine (oral argument March 26, 2024), contending that anti-abortion groups lacked Article III standing to challenge FDA approvals of mifepristone for medication abortion, as they alleged no concrete injury from the drug's availability and distribution protocols.[30] She emphasized that judicial review of agency rulemaking required traceability to specific harms, not generalized ideological objections, preserving the FDA's scientific and regulatory determinations on safety and efficacy. In emergency care contexts, Prelogar argued for the government in Moyle v. United States and the consolidated Idaho v. United States (oral argument April 24, 2024), asserting that the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA) preempted Idaho's near-total abortion ban to the extent it prohibited stabilizing treatments necessary for pregnant patients facing life-threatening conditions, such as ectopic pregnancies or severe preeclampsia.[31] The position held that EMTALA's mandate for hospitals to provide care within their capacity superseded conflicting state criminal laws, ensuring uniform federal standards for Medicare-participating facilities without supplanting broader state abortion restrictions. Prelogar defended federal restrictions on firearms possession in United States v. Rahimi (oral argument November 7, 2023), supporting 18 U.S.C. Â§ 922(g)(8), which prohibits individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders from owning guns, as consistent with historical traditions of disarming dangerous individuals under the Second Amendment framework established in New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen.[26] She argued that the law's narrow tailoring to credible threats of physical harm aligned with longstanding surety and disarmament practices from the founding era, rejecting claims of historical novelty. Addressing transgender youth medical interventions, Prelogar argued in United States v. Skrmetti (oral argument December 4, 2024), challenging Tennessee's ban on gender-affirming treatments like puberty blockers and hormone therapy for minors under the Equal Protection Clause, contending that the law discriminated on the basis of sex and transgender status without intermediate scrutiny justification, as it permitted analogous treatments for cisgender youth with conditions like precocious puberty.[32] The government asserted that emerging medical evidence supported such care's benefits for gender dysphoria, and the ban's categorical prohibition lacked evidence of uniform harm outweighing individualized assessments.[33] In national security matters, Prelogar represented the government in TikTok Inc. v. Garland (oral argument January 10, 2025), upholding a federal law requiring TikTok's divestiture from ByteDance due to data security risks from Chinese ownership, arguing that the measure addressed foreign adversary control over a platform with over 170 million U.S. users without violating the First Amendment, as it targeted ownership rather than content.[32][34]

Outcomes and win-loss record

During her tenure as Solicitor General from October 2021 to 2025, Elizabeth Prelogar personally argued more than 30 cases before the U.S. Supreme Court, a volume exceeding that of most predecessors in a comparable period. Her win rate in these argued cases has been approximately 46 percent, lower than the 59 percent historical average for solicitors general since 2001, amid efforts to defend Biden administration positions before a 6-3 conservative majority that frequently reversed lower court rulings favoring the government.[35] This record includes victories in matters such as United States v. Rahimi (2024), upholding federal restrictions on firearms possession by domestic abusers, and Consumer Financial Protection Bureau v. Community Financial Services Association (2024), affirming the agency's funding mechanism, alongside losses in high-stakes disputes like Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022), which overturned federal abortion protections, and Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024), ending Chevron deference to agency interpretations. The Office of the Solicitor General (OSG) under Prelogar participated in a broader array of cases, achieving a 67 percent overall win rate in the 2021 term (58 percent when the government was a direct party), defying a prior downward trend but suffering defeats in five of seven major ideological cases, including abortion and gun rights challenges.[36] Subsequent terms saw continued variability, with Prelogar's early arguments yielding a 4-4 split by mid-2023, including a narrow loss in Groff v. DeJoy (2023) on religious accommodations that some analysts viewed as partially favorable due to its clarification of undue hardship standards.[3] Analyses attribute the subdued outcomes to the Court's ideological alignment rather than deficiencies in advocacy, as Prelogar consistently ranked among top performers in oral argument metrics like word volume and responsiveness during the 2023 term.[37] Her efforts secured incremental government positions even in adverse rulings, such as partial affirmances in environmental and regulatory disputes.

Criticisms and alternative viewpoints

Prelogar's tenure as Solicitor General coincided with a conservative Supreme Court majority, resulting in pointed questioning from justices during oral arguments, particularly on matters involving federal regulatory authority and constitutional precedents. Justice Samuel Alito, for instance, engaged in extended exchanges with Prelogar in multiple cases, challenging the government's positions on issues such as diversity initiatives and administrative deference.[38] These interactions highlighted tensions between the executive branch's legal defenses and the Court's textualist and originalist leanings. The Office of the Solicitor General recorded a 67% win rate during the 2021-2022 term, appearing in 52 of 63 argued cases, but sustained losses in high-stakes disputes including Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (overturning Roe v. Wade) and New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen (expanding Second Amendment rights).[36] [39] Conservative commentators have interpreted these defeats as evidence of overreliance on precedents lacking firm historical or textual grounding, rather than flaws in Prelogar's advocacy skills, which have drawn bipartisan praise for clarity and preparation.[3] Alternative viewpoints emphasize that Prelogar's arguments often defended expansive federal powers aligned with Democratic priorities, such as in challenges to agency interpretations under the now-overturned Chevron doctrine, where justices questioned the implications for judicial oversight.[40] Critics from originalist perspectives argue this approach contributed to losses by prioritizing policy outcomes over strict statutory construction, underscoring broader debates on the administrative state's scope.[41] In other instances, such as immigration and public health mandates, Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Brett Kavanaugh expressed frustration with the government's resistance to nationwide injunction limits, viewing Prelogar's stance as an overassertion of executive authority.[42]

Post-government activities

Return to private practice

Following the conclusion of her tenure as the 48th Solicitor General on January 20, 2025, Elizabeth Prelogar briefly served as the Steven and Maureen Klinsky Visiting Professor of Practice for Leadership and Progress at Harvard Law School during the spring 2025 semester, where she taught courses on Supreme Court litigation.[43][44] On August 25, 2025, she rejoined Cooley LLP as a partner in its global litigation department in Washington, D.C., tasked with leading the firm's U.S. Supreme Court and appellate practice group.[45][46] Prelogar had previously worked at Cooley as a partner from 2020 to 2021, immediately prior to her appointment as Acting Solicitor General, during which she focused on high-stakes appellate matters.[13] In announcing her return, she stated that the decision crystallized while teaching at Harvard, emphasizing her intent to leverage her government experience in private appellate advocacy.[47] Cooley highlighted her role in representing clients before the Supreme Court and federal appellate courts, drawing on her record of arguing 35 cases as Solicitor General.[48]

Academic and public engagements

Following her tenure as Solicitor General, Prelogar joined Harvard Law School as the Steven and Maureen Klinsky Visiting Professor of Practice for Leadership and Progress, a position announced in January 2025.[49][27] In this role, she has taught on topics including the evolution of Supreme Court jurisprudence and the responsibilities of appellate advocacy, drawing from her experience arguing over 20 cases before the Court.[50] Prelogar previously served as a Lecturer on Law at Harvard, focusing on Supreme Court and appellate litigation.[25] Prelogar has engaged in numerous public lectures and speaking events at academic institutions. In April 2025, she delivered the Klinsky Lecture at Harvard Law School, discussing strategies for representing the United States in Supreme Court arguments and the challenges of high-stakes litigation.[51] She spoke at Yale Law School's commencement in May 2025, outlining five principles for effective oral arguments, including preparation, adaptability, and respect for judicial process.[52] In March 2025, Prelogar presented the Lewis F. Powell, Jr. Lecture at Washington and Lee University School of Law, addressing contemporary Supreme Court dynamics and advising students on public service careers.[53] Additional engagements include the Herma Hill Kay Memorial Lecture at UC Berkeley School of Law in October 2025, where she reflected on landmark cases and institutional changes in federal advocacy.[50] She is scheduled to deliver the 2025â€“26 McCormick Lecture at the University of Arizona James E. Rogers College of Law, focusing on Supreme Court litigation techniques.[54] Prelogar also participated in the Pepperdine Caruso School of Law's annual dinner in September 2025, joining former Solicitor General Noel Francisco to demonstrate civil discourse on legal topics.[55] Beyond academia, Prelogar has spoken at professional gatherings, such as the Federal Bar Association's 2025 Annual Meeting, where she discussed the implications of Supreme Court decisions on regulatory authority, including the overruling of Chevron deference.[56] These appearances emphasize her emphasis on rule-of-law principles and bipartisan approaches to appellate practice.[57]

Personal life

Marriage and family

Prelogar is married to Brandon Prelogar, and the couple has two sons.[28][6] The family resides in Washington, D.C.[1] In 2024, they temporarily relocated to Boise, Idaho, to stay with Prelogar's mother during a period of family support needs.[28]

Public persona and affiliations

Elizabeth Prelogar is widely regarded among legal professionals as one of the nation's premier appellate advocates, noted for her precise preparation and effective oral arguments before the Supreme Court.[13][58] During her tenure as Solicitor General from October 2021 to January 2025, she frequently engaged in pointed exchanges with conservative justices, including Justice Samuel Alito, while defending the Biden administration's positions on issues ranging from gun regulations to administrative authority.[38] Her public appearances, such as lectures at universities including the University of Chicago Law School in January 2024 and Duke Law School in September 2023, emphasize her career trajectory from appellate associate to high-stakes litigator, underscoring a commitment to rigorous legal craftsmanship over partisan rhetoric.[15][16] Prelogar's professional affiliations include membership in the American Law Institute, where she contributes to restatements of legal principles, and the Edward Coke Appellate Inn of Court, focused on advancing appellate practice.[59][13] Following her government service, she rejoined Cooley LLP in August 2025 as a partner leading its Supreme Court and appellate practice group, a role she held prior to her nomination.[46] In January 2025, she assumed a teaching position at Harvard Law School, delivering insights on Supreme Court advocacy drawn from her experience representing the United States before a skeptically inclined bench.[5] Politically, Prelogar aligns with Democratic priorities, as evidenced by her nomination by President Joe Biden and confirmation by a 53-36 Senate vote largely along party lines in October 2021.[60] Campaign finance records indicate personal contributions to Democratic candidates and committees during her time at Hogan Lovells US LLP.[61] She received recognition from Lambda Legal, an organization advocating for LGBTQ rights, at its 2025 Liberty Awards for contributions to related litigation, reflecting alignment with progressive social causes despite her role's institutional demands for nonpartisan representation of government interests.[12]
