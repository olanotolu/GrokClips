New Rochelle High School


New Rochelle High School is a public four-year high school in New Rochelle, New York, enrolling 3,056 students in grades 9 through 12 as the primary secondary institution in the City School District of New Rochelle.[1]
The school opened in 1926 on a campus acquired by the city in 1923, featuring facilities that support academic instruction and athletic programs.[2]
It delivers a broad curriculum with 32 Advanced Placement courses available and a 48% student participation rate in AP exams, alongside extracurricular opportunities in sports, drama, and science competitions where students have earned accolades such as gold medals at the International Genius Olympiad and National Merit recognitions.[3][4][5]

Campus and Facilities

Physical Infrastructure and Layout

New Rochelle High School's campus, situated at 265 Clove Road, features a primary multi-building complex designed to accommodate approximately 3,000 students across grades 9 through 12. The infrastructure supports a total enrollment of 3,056 students during the 2023-24 school year, with facilities including classrooms distributed across multiple structures to facilitate organized learning communities.[1] The layout emphasizes functional spaces for daily operations, though a 2015 capacity study indicated the need for 1-3 additional classrooms by 2020 to match projected growth, suggesting operations near structural limits.[6] Central to the campus is the main academic building, housing administrative offices, standard classrooms, and specialized areas such as science laboratories and a gymnasium for physical education and events. The New Rochelle High School Library serves as a key resource, functioning as a technology-focused, flexible instructional space that accommodates entire classes or professional development groups.[7] Adjacent to the library is the College and Career Center, providing additional support for student guidance. An auditorium supports performing arts activities, contributing to the campus's capacity for extracurricular assemblies.[5] The student-teacher ratio stands at 14:1, reflecting staffing aligned with the facility's scale to manage the high enrollment density.[3] Outdoor areas have been enhanced with new tables for student use during lunch and events, expanding usable space beyond indoor structures.[8] Overall, the physical plant prioritizes efficient space utilization for a diverse student body, with core infrastructure dating to mid-20th-century expansions but maintained through district-wide upgrades.[9]

Maintenance and Recent Developments

In 2022, the City School District of New Rochelle completed a $106.5 million multi-phase capital improvement project, initiated following voter approval of a 2016 bond referendum to remedy aging infrastructure across district facilities, including New Rochelle High School.[10] At the high school, upgrades encompassed exterior enhancements such as roofing, masonry, window, and door replacements; site improvements including athletic field reconstructions, parking lot resurfacing, and sidewalk repairs; and mechanical overhauls like HVAC, electrical, and plumbing system modernizations alongside installation of backup generators.[10] These interventions addressed longstanding deterioration identified in pre-2016 assessments, with the project's completion on schedule mitigating risks to structural integrity and operational efficiency.[11] Post-completion, district priorities shifted to targeted maintenance using capital reserves. For the 2024-2025 fiscal year, the Board of Education allocated funds from existing reserves to finalize renovations at the New Rochelle High School swimming pool, responding to deferred needs within constrained budgets.[12] This work builds on the broader bond efforts but reflects fiscal pressures, as the district navigated a proposed 3.95% tax levy increase for 2025-2026 amid rising operational costs that could limit proactive upkeep.[13] Ongoing challenges stem from the high school's original mid-20th-century construction and enrollment demands exceeding initial design capacities, necessitating sustained investment to prevent recurrence of pre-bond issues like inadequate ventilation or flood damageâ€”as evidenced by prior library remediation.[10] While no major facility failures have been reported since 2022, budget shortfalls projected at $20 million for 2025-2026 highlight vulnerabilities in funding models reliant on local taxes and state aid, potentially deferring non-essential repairs if economic conditions worsen.[14]

History

Founding and Early Development

New Rochelle's public high school originated with the construction of its first dedicated building in 1906, designed by architect Edgar Josselyn to provide centralized secondary education amid the city's transition from smaller academies and private institutions.[15] This facility initially accommodated a modest student body reflective of the suburb's early 20th-century population of approximately 14,000 residents, focusing on core academic preparation for local youth as New Rochelle emerged as a commuter hub following railroad expansions.[2] By the years following World War I, the 1906 structure proved insufficient due to surging enrollment driven by residential growth and immigration, necessitating a larger campus to sustain comprehensive high school offerings.[16] In 1923, the city purchased 43 acresâ€”including Huguenot Park and two adjacent lakesâ€”from the Mahlstedt family, who had operated an ice house on the site, to enable this expansion.[2] The new building, completed in 1926 at a cost of $1 million, represented a significant investment in public education infrastructure tailored to the area's demographic pressures.[2] Originally slated for naming as Woodrow Wilson Memorial High School, the Board of Education voted in July 1926 to rename it New Rochelle High School, prioritizing a neutral institutional identity over presidential commemoration amid local political debates.[17] [16] This relocation and upgrade solidified the school's role as the primary secondary institution, shifting from fragmented educational models to a unified system capable of handling expanded curricula and extracurriculars for a diversifying suburban populace.[16]

Desegregation and Mid-20th Century Changes

In 1961, the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York ruled in Taylor v. Board of Education of the City School District of New Rochelle that the district had intentionally segregated Black students into Lincoln Elementary School through gerrymandered boundaries established in the 1930s and restrictive transfer policies adopted in 1949, which confined nearly all Black childrenâ€”comprising 14% of the city's school-age populationâ€”to that facility by confining transfers and ignoring demographic shifts.[18][19] This landmark decision, among the earliest federal findings of de jure segregation in a Northern suburb, extended implications to the broader district, including New Rochelle High School, which had previously maintained a more balanced composition reflective of the city's demographics but faced feeder-pattern disruptions from elementary-level reassignments.[19] Desegregation implementation encountered significant resistance, culminating in the resignation of the school superintendent and five board members opposed to the court's mandate; full compliance required state intervention, with court-ordered busing commencing in 1964 to redistribute students across schools for racial balance, closing the outdated Lincoln building and reassigning its pupils.[19] At the high school, this policy integrated cohorts of previously segregated elementary students progressing through the system, increasing Black enrollment beyond the pre-1961 baseline and altering the student body's socioeconomic and racial composition amid citywide efforts to prevent resegregation.[19] Early effects included logistical strains from busingâ€”serving two-fifths of affected pupils initiallyâ€”and reports of transitional academic disruptions, though district-wide metrics showed stabilization and gains in overall performance as integration took hold.[19] Causal factors in enrollment shifts included parental responses to busing, with empirical patterns of white family migration to private or parochial schools and suburban exodusâ€”evident pre-ruling through transfers that rendered Lincoln 100% Black by 1949â€”intensifying post-1961, as observed in comparable Northern districts where court mandates accelerated demographic changes without offsetting policies to retain middle-class families.[19] While achieving measurable integration at New Rochelle High School, these dynamics highlighted trade-offs: enhanced racial mixing coexisted with challenges like heightened absenteeism during early busing phases and long-term enrollment volatility tied to housing patterns rather than sustained policy incentives for stability.[19]

Late 20th to 21st Century Evolution

In the late 20th century, New Rochelle High School underwent significant building renovations in 1990 to address longstanding maintenance issues, including concerns over asbestos exposure during abatement processes.[20] These updates were part of broader efforts to modernize facilities amid growing district enrollment pressures from population increases in the diverse urban area.[21] Enrollment expanded markedly from the 1990s into the 21st century, reflecting the City School District's response to demographic shifts and immigration-driven growth in New Rochelle. By 2010, the high school had 3,413 students in grades 9-12, establishing it as one of the largest in Westchester County.[22][23] This surge prompted administrative adaptations, including planning for capacity and program scalability to accommodate the influx.[21] Into the 2010s, further infrastructure investments via a 2017 bond measure funded interior enhancements at the high school, such as renovating eight bathrooms, replacing classroom ceilings, and installing new ventilators to improve air quality.[24] These changes, part of over $100 million in district-wide construction, aimed to sustain operations for the school's expanded scope amid ongoing enrollment fluctuations peaking near 2019 before recent declines.[25][26] Leadership transitions, including the 2018 resignation of Principal Reginald Richardson and subsequent appointments, marked efforts to stabilize administration during this period of scale and adaptation.[27][28]

Student Population Statistics

Diversity, Socioeconomic Profile, and Performance Disparities

New Rochelle High School's student body reflects significant ethnic diversity, with Hispanic or Latino students comprising the largest group at 54% (1,650 students), followed by Black or African American students at 21% (642 students), White students at 20% (612 students), and Asian or Native Hawaiian/Other Pacific Islander students at 5% (153 students). American Indian or Alaska Native enrollment is negligible at 0%. This composition results in approximately 80% minority enrollment overall.[31][3] Socioeconomically, 61% of students (1,864) are classified as economically disadvantaged, primarily determined by eligibility for free or reduced-price lunch programs, indicating a substantial portion from low-income households. This rate aligns with broader indicators such as 56% eligible for free lunch and 2% for reduced-price lunch. High socioeconomic disadvantage correlates empirically with challenges like elevated chronic absenteeism, which stands at 42.8% school-wideâ€”substantially above state averages and linked to factors including family income and mobility.[31][3][1] Performance disparities manifest in metrics such as the school's 86% four-year graduation rate, which lags state benchmarks and reflects compositional influences. Economically disadvantaged students, comprising the majority, typically exhibit lower proficiency on Regents exams and graduation outcomes compared to non-disadvantaged peers, as socioeconomic status serves as a verifiable predictor of academic achievement through mechanisms like resource access and family stability. Racial/ethnic subgroups show analogous gaps, with White and Asian students outperforming Hispanic and Black students in regional data, underscoring causal ties to SES rather than diversity alone; persistent inequities endure despite demographic emphasis, as family income and structure exert stronger empirical influence on absenteeism, proficiency, and completion rates than institutional equity initiatives.[1][29][32]

Academics

Curriculum and Departmental Offerings

New Rochelle High School structures its curriculum around core academic requirements and elective options to fulfill New York State graduation mandates, emphasizing preparation for Regents examinations and advanced pathways. The school requires 22 credits for a local or Regents diploma, including 4 credits each in English language arts and social studies, 3 credits each in mathematics and science (with at least one life science and one physical science for advanced designation), 1 to 3 credits in languages other than English, 1 credit in arts or music, 0.5 credit in health education, 2 credits in physical education, and the balance in electives.[33] These align with New York State Next Generation Learning Standards in subjects like English, mathematics, and science, as well as frameworks for social studies, incorporating Regents-level assessments in core areas such as Algebra I, Living Environment, U.S. History and Government, and English Regents.[33] Students typically enroll in 6.5 credits per year, with honors and Advanced Placement (AP) courses weighted at 1.10 for grade point average calculations.[33] The English department offers sequential courses from English 1 through 4, alongside electives like creative writing and journalism, with honors and AP options in Language and Composition and Literature and Composition requiring teacher recommendation and prior strong performance.[33] Mathematics includes foundational sequences in Algebra I, Geometry, and Algebra II, progressing to pre-calculus, statistics, and AP Calculus AB/BC or AP Statistics, with prerequisites emphasizing Regents proficiency.[33] The science department mandates lab-intensive Regents courses like Living Environment and Chemistry (each requiring 1,200 minutes of hands-on work), supplemented by electives in forensics, astronomy, and AP Biology, Chemistry, Physics C, and Environmental Science.[33] Social studies fulfills requirements through Global History I and II, U.S. History, Participation in Government, and Economics, with AP offerings in U.S. History, World History, Psychology, and Human Geography.[33] World languages cover Spanish, French, Italian, Mandarin, and Latin, with sequences up to AP levels (e.g., AP Spanish Language and Literature, AP French), including heritage speaker tracks and a Seal of Biliteracy pathway for multilingual proficiency.[33] The Performing and Visual Arts department provides the PAVE (Performing and Visual Arts Education) program, a four-year audition-based track in dance, music ensembles, acting, and visual arts like ceramics and AP Studio Art: Drawing or 2-D Design, fulfilling the arts requirement while offering portfolio development.[33] Business and technology courses include accounting, marketing, computer science principles (AP), and robotics, while Career and Technical Education (CTE) pathwaysâ€”such as automotive technology, culinary arts, and nursing assistantâ€”are available through partnerships with Southern Westchester BOCES for vocational endorsements on diplomas.[33] Advanced opportunities encompass over 30 AP courses across disciplines, including dual enrollment options like SUNY-level accounting and marketing, with prerequisites typically requiring 85% or higher on prior Regents exams and departmental approval.[34] Approximately 18% of students enroll in at least one AP course, reflecting selective access based on academic readiness.[34] Specialized initiatives like the three-year Science Research Program (grades 10-12) and the NICE senior internship program integrate elective credits with project-based or career exploration components.[33] Instruction is delivered by New York State-certified teachers, with department chairs overseeing alignment to state standards and course rigor.[35]

Academic Performance Metrics and Outcomes

New Rochelle High School's four-year graduation rate stood at 86% for the cohort as of August 2024, aligning with state requirements but falling below the New York average of approximately 90% for similar periods.[1] [3] This rate reflects the percentage of entering ninth-graders completing a local or Regents diploma within four years, though subgroup disparities exist, with lower rates among economically disadvantaged students comprising 58% of enrollment.[3] On state-required Regents Examinations, proficiency levels indicate middling performance: 46% in mathematics, 71% in reading/English language arts, and 58% in science, contributing to an overall Regents percentile score of 42.7%.[3] These figures, derived from exams administered in August, January, and June, position the school below state averages in core subjects, with an underserved student proficiency gap of -9.5% relative to expectations.[3] Nationally, U.S. News & World Report ranked the school #4,758 out of nearly 18,000 public high schools in 2025, and #412 in New York, reflecting outcomes that lag despite per-pupil expenditures exceeding $30,000 annually.[3] [1] Post-graduation outcomes include 73% of graduates pursuing college or vocational programs, a figure consistent with recent cohorts but tempered by a college readiness index of 36.7/100, based on AP exam performance where only 33% of participants scored 3 or higher despite 48% enrollment in advanced courses.[36] [3] Recognition for high achievers includes six seniors named commended students in the 2026 National Merit Scholarship Program and four semifinalists in the prior cycle, representing top PSAT scorers among 1.3 million juniors nationwide.[37] [38] However, these commendations occur amid broader trends of stagnant or modestly improving Regents proficiency, raising questions about the credibility of reported outcomes given documented issues with grading practices that may inflate credentials without corresponding skill gains.[39]

Advanced Programs, Honor Societies, and Special Initiatives

New Rochelle High School offers 32 Advanced Placement (AP) courses across subjects including world history, United States history, United States government, art, and sciences, enabling students to pursue college-level instruction.[34] The school also provides dual enrollment options through partnerships allowing credits at local colleges, contributing to an expansive advanced academic catalog comparable to those in higher-performing districts.[40] AP participation stands at 48% of students, with an exam pass rate of approximately 54%, though these figures reflect overall enrollment rather than subgroup performance, where socioeconomic disparities may limit access for lower-income students despite available offerings.[3][34] The P.A.V.E. (Performing and Visual Arts Education) program, established in 1999, serves as a key enrichment initiative focused on developing student talent in visual arts, media arts, music, dance, and theater through sequential coursework and performances.[41] This program integrates arts disciplines to foster advanced creative skills, with students participating in events like original dance presentations and theater productions at the school's Linda E. Kelly Theatre.[42] Additional specialized tracks include Advanced Robotics I and II, supporting STEM enrichment amid ongoing budget pressures that have reduced some offerings but preserved core advanced arts and AP components for the 2025-2026 year.[33][43] Honor societies recognize academic excellence and leadership, with the National Honor Society chapter inducting students based on scholarship, service, and character; notable cohorts include 101 members in 2018 and reactivations with new inductees in 2022.[44][45] Subject-specific groups encompass the English National Honor Society for language arts achievement, Rho Kappa for social studies with 12 inductees in 2023, the Business and Marketing Honor Society with 24 members added in 2023, and the National Art Honor Society promoting artistic involvement.[46][47][48] These societies facilitate service projects and events, though participation rates remain selective, potentially excluding broader student engagement due to eligibility thresholds tied to grades and extracurriculars.[49]

Student Activities

Extracurricular Clubs and Organizations

New Rochelle High School maintains a range of non-athletic extracurricular clubs and organizations designed to enhance student engagement, leadership skills, and personal development beyond the classroom. These groups include the Human Rights Club, which focuses on fostering awareness of global events and human rights issues to cultivate a broader worldview among participants.[50] The English National Honor Society serves as a dedicated honor society for English students, offering opportunities for academic recognition and involvement in literary activities.[46] Other active clubs encompass Art Club, Band, Dance, Drama, Future Business Leaders of America, Newspaper, Student Council, and Yearbook, providing avenues for creative expression, business acumen, journalism, governance, and archival documentation.[51] Participation in these organizations is encouraged through school policy, with the student-parent handbook noting that students may propose new clubs to expand offerings, indicating an adaptive structure responsive to interests.[52] Surveys from educational review platforms report substantial involvement, with a majority of students and parents affirming that a large portion of the student body engages in such clubs, supporting skill-building in areas like collaboration and initiative.[53] These activities emphasize leadership roles, such as club presidencies and event coordination, which contribute to resume enhancement and community involvement without overlapping with athletic programs.[36]

Achievements and Participation Rates

The New Rochelle High School debate team secured second place in the overall team category at a regional tournament in February 2023, with teams Shammas & Khan advancing undefeated through three rounds to claim the trophy.[54] In robotics, the school's VEX team earned the Innovate Award at their season-opening competition in December 2024, recognizing creative engineering solutions amid competition in Queens earlier that year.[55] [56] A service-oriented club received the Volunteer Spirit Award from Volunteer New York in 2018 for outstanding community engagement efforts.[57] The school supports over 30 extracurricular clubs, including Model Congress, S.A.D.D., and peer leadership groups like Ignite, fostering skills applicable to college admissions where meaningful involvement is valued over superficial participation.[58] [3] Specific participation rates across these activities are not systematically reported in public district data, though enrollment in competitive programs like debate and robotics indicates targeted engagement among motivated students in a body exceeding 3,000.[1] Such involvement correlates with leadership recognition, as evidenced by club members receiving accolades for broader extracurricular contributions.[59]

Athletics

Sports Programs and Facilities

New Rochelle High School maintains interscholastic athletic programs affiliated with Section 1 of the New York State Public High School Athletic Association (NYSPHSAA), offering teams across fall, winter, and spring seasons. The department, directed by Joseph Donaldson since at least 2023, supports varsity, junior varsity, and modified levels to foster student involvement in competitive sports.[60] Fall sports encompass football, boys' and girls' cross country, boys' and girls' soccer, girls' volleyball, and field hockey; winter activities include boys' and girls' basketball, wrestling, boys' and girls' swimming, indoor track, and bowling; spring teams feature baseball, softball, boys' and girls' lacrosse, boys' and girls' tennis, golf, and outdoor track and field.[61] The school's infrastructure supports these programs through on-campus facilities, including the gymnasium for indoor competitions such as basketball and volleyball, and William H. McKenna Field for outdoor events like football, soccer, and track meets.[62] [60] Additional fields and resources may be shared with the City School District of New Rochelle's broader athletic offerings, ensuring access for practices and games.[63] Programs emphasize opportunities for both male and female students, with dedicated teams in sports like soccer, basketball, lacrosse, and track for each gender, aligning with federal mandates for equitable participation under Title IX.[61] Specific enrollment figures vary annually, but the district's athletic handbook underscores the role of sports in promoting health, discipline, and teamwork among participants.[52]

Accomplishments, Records, and Challenges

The New Rochelle High School football program secured its third New York State Public High School Athletic Association (NYSPHSAA) Class AA championship in 2019, defeating McQuaid Jesuit 28-0 in the state final at Syracuse University's Carrier Dome on November 30.[64][65] This victory highlighted the team's resilience, as it occurred without suspended head coach Lou DiRienzo, who was sidelined amid an investigation into program practices.[64] The 2019 season also earned the team national ranking No. 373 and state Division AA No. 1 by MaxPreps.[66] In track and field, New Rochelle athletes have established school records, including a men's 100m time of 10.76 seconds by Courtland Cargill in 2011 and a 10.90 seconds by Jabari Clarke in 2022.[67] The team captured the Northern Counties indoor track championship in January 2025, contributing to Section 1 successes.[68] Football has seen additional league titles and Section 1 semifinal appearances, such as in 2021, reflecting periodic competitiveness.[69] Challenges have included maintaining consistent performance post-major wins, with the football program's reliance on key personnel exposed by coaching disruptions.[64] District-wide budget shortfalls, including a $20.2 million deficit for the 2025-2026 school year, have prompted program cuts and raised concerns over funding adequacy for equipment, travel, and staffing, potentially hindering athletic equity and competitiveness across sports.[70][71]

Controversies and Criticisms

Grade Manipulation and Integrity Issues

In December 2018, an internal investigative report revealed that 212 grades had been improperly altered in the Apex online credit recovery system at New Rochelle High School, affecting 32 students, including some who had already graduated.[72] The changes, primarily made by Shadia Alvarez, the school's Apex coordinator and assistant principal, converted failing or incomplete scores to passing without documentation, consistent rationale, or quality review of student work.[73] At least two students from the class of 2018 received diplomas despite lacking sufficient legitimate credits due to these alterations.[73] Additionally, former principal Reginald Richardson personally modified a student's transcript to adjust a numerical grade to a passing mark, violating district policy.[72] The 46-page report, conducted by the district's law firm, found no evidence of explicit directives from administrators to engage in unauthorized grade changes, attributing the issues instead to systemic lapses in oversight, inadequate training, and a lack of procedures for the Apex platform.[72] Alvarez, who denied wrongdoing, had been hired by Richardson despite prior disciplinary issues in New York City schools that placed her on a recommended do-not-hire list.[73] These revelations prompted the New York State Education Department to launch its own probe into the district's practices.[74] In response, the district moved to terminate Alvarez's employment, with a hearing scheduled for January 2019, while Richardson and superintendent Brian Osborne had already resigned amid broader controversies.[72] Policy reforms included implementing two-party authorization for grade changes, mandatory training and guidelines for online systems, regular audits, and switching to a new vendor, Edgenuity, to prevent recurrence.[72] Board President Jeffrey Hastie acknowledged that the scandal eroded public trust in district personnel.[72] The incident raised concerns about the integrity of diplomas issued by New Rochelle High School, as unauthorized alterations undermined the evidentiary basis for academic achievement and potentially inflated graduation rates through unverified credit recovery.[72] Such practices reflect broader challenges in public high schools, where pressure to meet graduation benchmarks can incentivize lax standards over rigorous assessment, thereby devaluing credentials for all graduates regardless of individual merit.[73]

Safety, Discipline, and Incident Responses

New Rochelle High School has faced recurring challenges with student discipline and safety, evidenced by a reported 283 percent increase in incidents from 65 in the 2012-13 school year to 249 in the subsequent years leading to 2018, including fights, threats, and disruptions primarily attributed to a small subset of students. A 2018 security audit highlighted inconsistent enforcement of rules, student disregard for authority, and inadequate responses to repeated offenses, prompting recommendations for stricter measures despite partial redactions in the public report obscuring full details. Suspension rates remain relatively low at 4 percent of students receiving at least one full-day suspension annually, per state data, which may reflect restorative approaches over punitive ones but correlates with persistent behavioral issues in a district with high economic disadvantage among enrollees.[84][85][1] In response to violent incidents, such as multiple stabbings in 2018â€”including a classroom attack that led to a lawsuit against the district allowed to proceed in 2021â€”the Board of Education implemented enhanced security protocols, including restricted entrances and increased police presence during threats. That year, three potential threats prompted sweeps and limited access, with authorities investigating as credible risks despite no confirmed attacks materializing. Police involvement has extended to off-campus behaviors impacting school safety, such as the district's closed-campus policy aimed at curbing uncontrolled events, though enforcement challenges persist.[86][87][88] Drug-related emergencies underscore vulnerabilities in monitoring, as in January 2023 when a student collapsed after vaping a substance initially suspected to be fentanyl-laced, prompting nurses to administer Narcan; toxicology later confirmed no opioids, revealing it as a false alarm but highlighting risks from unregulated vaping amid broader fentanyl concerns. The district's PROTECT Team coordinates safety efforts district-wide, prioritizing rapid response, yet such events renew scrutiny on prevention amid empirical links between socioeconomic factors and substance experimentation in urban schools.[89][90][91] More recently, in May 2025, school officials issued warnings prohibiting the unsanctioned "Senior Assassin" game after three students used a toy water gun resembling a firearm near campus, triggering a bystander police call and potential misinterpretation as a real threat. The district emphasized zero tolerance for activities involving simulated weapons, even off-site, to mitigate escalation risks, reflecting ongoing tensions between student traditions and security imperatives in a high school environment prone to disruptions from a minority of participants.[92][93]

Notable Alumni and Faculty

New Rochelle High School has produced alumni who have achieved prominence in fields including journalism, medicine, entertainment, and professional sports. Don Hewitt, creator and executive producer of the CBS newsmagazine 60 Minutes from 1968 to 2004, graduated in 1940 after contributing to the local newspaper during his high school years.[94][95] Henry J. Heimlich, the thoracic surgeon who developed the anti-choking technique known as the Heimlich maneuver in 1974, was a member of the class of 1937.[96][97] In entertainment, Richard Roundtree, best known for portraying the title character in the 1971 blaxploitation film Shaft and its sequels, graduated in 1961 following a standout career on the school's nationally ranked football team.[98][99] Ray Rice, a former NFL running back who played for the Baltimore Ravens from 2008 to 2013 and set New York state single-game rushing records during his senior year, attended the school and later returned as a volunteer assistant coach.[100][101] Journalist Andrea Mitchell, chief foreign affairs correspondent for NBC News since 1994 and host of Andrea Mitchell Reports on MSNBC, graduated in 1963.[102][103] No widely recognized figures among the school's faculty have been documented in major public records or achievements beyond local educational contributions.
