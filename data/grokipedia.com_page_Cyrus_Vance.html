Cyrus Vance


Cyrus Roberts Vance (March 27, 1917 – January 12, 2002) was an American lawyer and diplomat who held senior positions in multiple U.S. administrations, most prominently as the 57th Secretary of State from 1977 to 1980 under President Jimmy Carter.[1][2] Born in Clarksburg, West Virginia, Vance graduated from Yale University with a B.A. in 1939 and an LL.B. in 1942, served in the U.S. Navy during World War II, and later practiced corporate law before entering government service.[3][2] Vance's career spanned defense and diplomatic roles, including Secretary of the Army from 1962 to 1964 and Deputy Secretary of Defense from 1964 to 1967 under Presidents Kennedy and Johnson, where he managed crises such as those in the Dominican Republic and the Panama Canal Zone.[4][2] As a negotiator, he contributed to the Paris Peace Talks on Vietnam in 1968–1969, resigning in opposition to escalation, and later to the 1967 Cyprus settlement.[3][1] In the Carter administration, Vance advanced arms control through SALT II negotiations, facilitated the Panama Canal Treaties, and played a key role in the Camp David Accords leading to the 1979 Egypt–Israel peace treaty.[1][5] His tenure ended dramatically on April 28, 1980, when Vance resigned in protest against Carter's approval of Operation Eagle Claw, a failed military rescue attempt during the Iran hostage crisis, arguing it contradicted ongoing diplomatic efforts.[6][7] This principled stand highlighted Vance's preference for negotiation over force, a stance he detailed in his 1983 memoir Hard Choices.[6] Post-resignation, he advised on foreign policy and served as U.S. envoy to Yugoslavia in the 1990s, emphasizing multilateral diplomacy.[1]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood

Cyrus Roberts Vance was born on March 27, 1917, in Clarksburg, West Virginia, to John Carl Vance II, an insurance executive and former postmaster of Clarksburg (1914–1917), and Amy (née Roberts) Vance.[2][8] He was the younger of two sons, with an older brother, John Carl Vance III.[9] The family, which traced its roots to earlier generations in Harrison County, West Virginia, relocated to Bronxville, New York, in 1918 shortly after Vance's birth.[2] His father died in 1922 at age 50, leaving Amy Vance to raise the children amid the family's established but modest circumstances in the New York suburbs.[9][10] Vance's early years were marked by this relocation and the loss of his father, shaping a childhood spent primarily in Bronxville, where the family adapted to urban life away from West Virginia's coal-region heritage.[2] His mother, a civic leader in Clarksburg prior to the move, maintained family ties to the region's political and business networks, including connections to figures like presidential nominee John W. Davis.[11]

Academic Achievements and Military Service

Vance prepared for college at Kent School in Kent, Connecticut, graduating in 1935. He attended Yale University, earning a Bachelor of Arts degree in economics from Yale College in 1939. He continued at Yale Law School, receiving a Bachelor of Laws degree with honors in 1942.[2][4][1] Upon completing his legal education, Vance enlisted in the United States Navy, serving from 1942 to 1946 during World War II as a gunnery officer aboard the destroyer USS Hale (DD-642). His service included combat operations in the Pacific Theater, with actions off Bougainville, Tarawa, the Philippines, Saipan, and Guam. He was discharged with the rank of lieutenant, junior grade.[4][12][1]

Early Legal and Public Service Career

Wall Street Practice and Initial Government Roles

After graduating from Yale Law School and serving in the U.S. Navy during World War II, Cyrus Vance joined the New York City law firm of Simpson Thacher & Bartlett in 1947, where he established himself as a civil litigator.[13][4] He rose to partner at the firm, handling corporate and commercial disputes, and remained in private practice for a decade until entering public service in 1957.[14] In 1957, at age 40, Vance left Wall Street to serve as special counsel to the Senate Armed Services Committee's Preparedness Investigating Subcommittee, chaired by Senator Lyndon B. Johnson, amid concerns over U.S. military readiness following the Soviet launch of Sputnik in 1957.[15][16] The subcommittee examined gaps in defense capabilities, including missile programs and strategic vulnerabilities, producing reports that influenced national security policy.[17] During this Senate tenure, Vance also acted as counsel to Johnson's Special Committee on Space and Astronautics, where he contributed to drafting legislation that established the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) via the National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958, signed into law on July 29, 1958.[18] His work bridged congressional oversight of defense preparedness and emerging space initiatives, marking his initial foray into federal advisory roles on national security matters.[17]

Advisory Positions in Congress and Defense

In 1957, amid concerns over U.S. technological lag following the Soviet Union's launch of Sputnik 1 on October 4, Senator Lyndon B. Johnson recruited Vance from his Wall Street law practice to serve as special counsel to the Senate Armed Services Committee's Preparedness Investigating Subcommittee.[12][2] In this advisory role, Vance supported investigations into American military readiness, intelligence capabilities, and responses to Soviet advancements in rocketry and space technology, helping shape congressional recommendations for bolstering national defense priorities.[10] Building on this experience, Vance was appointed consulting counsel to the Senate Special Committee on Space and Astronautics in 1958.[4] He played a key role in drafting the National Aeronautics and Space Act of 1958, signed into law by President Dwight D. Eisenhower on July 29, which established the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) as an independent civilian agency to coordinate non-military space activities and promote scientific research.[4][19] His contributions emphasized integrating defense-related expertise with civilian space efforts, reflecting first-hand knowledge of the Preparedness Subcommittee's findings on missile gaps and satellite reconnaissance needs. Transitioning to executive branch advisory work, Vance was named General Counsel of the Department of Defense in 1961 by Secretary Robert S. McNamara.[4] In this position, which he held until July 1962, Vance provided legal guidance on procurement, international agreements, and operational policies, including advice on integrating space assets into defense strategy amid escalating Cold War tensions.[19] His tenure involved resolving complex issues such as arms control interpretations and military aid programs, drawing on his congressional experience to bridge legislative and executive perspectives on national security.[20] These roles established Vance's reputation as a pragmatic advisor capable of navigating interbranch dynamics and technical policy challenges.

Roles in the Kennedy and Johnson Administrations

Secretary of the Army (1962–1964)

Cyrus Vance was nominated by President John F. Kennedy to serve as Secretary of the Army on June 8, 1962, and sworn into office on July 5, 1962, succeeding Cyril F. Smith amid administrative transitions in the Kennedy administration's defense leadership.[4] In this civilian oversight role, subordinate to Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara, Vance managed the U.S. Army's personnel, logistics, procurement, and operational policies, overseeing approximately 900,000 active-duty soldiers and supporting the shift toward flexible response doctrines amid Cold War tensions.[4] His tenure emphasized managerial efficiency, aligning Army structures with broader Pentagon reforms aimed at cost reduction and analytical rigor in resource allocation.[21] A pivotal early challenge occurred during the integration of the University of Mississippi in September 1962, where Vance directed the Army's response to violent opposition against the enrollment of Black student James Meredith. On orders from President Kennedy, Vance authorized the federalization of 12,000 Mississippi National Guard troops and the deployment of 3,000 U.S. Army Military Police from bases including Fort Bragg and Fort Benning, arriving by October 1 to restore order after riots that killed two individuals—a French journalist and a local jukebox repairman—and injured over 160 federal personnel.[1] [22] Vance coordinated real-time operations from the Pentagon, communicating directly with field commanders like General Charles Billingslea to contain the unrest without broader escalation, marking the Army's first major domestic enforcement of federal court desegregation mandates since the 1957 Little Rock crisis.[23] This action underscored Vance's role in bridging military readiness with constitutional enforcement, though it drew criticism from segregationist factions for perceived federal overreach.[1] Vance's leadership also supported Army contributions to national security during the October 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis, mobilizing reserve units and enhancing continental defense postures in coordination with Joint Chiefs recommendations.[24] Internally, he advanced administrative reorganizations, including streamlined headquarters functions outlined in early 1962 planning memos that reduced bureaucratic layers and integrated systems analysis for procurement decisions, reflecting McNamara's influence on data-driven management.[21] By late 1963, under President Lyndon B. Johnson following Kennedy's assassination, Vance focused on sustaining Army readiness amid emerging Vietnam advisory escalations, though major combat commitments postdated his Army tenure. He resigned on January 21, 1964, to assume the Deputy Secretary of Defense position, having stabilized Army operations during a period of doctrinal and civil rights transitions.[4]

Deputy Secretary of Defense (1964–1967)

Cyrus Vance was confirmed and sworn in as the 11th Deputy Secretary of Defense on January 28, 1964, succeeding Roswell Gilpatric and serving under Secretary Robert McNamara during the Lyndon B. Johnson administration.[4] In this capacity, Vance acted as the chief operating officer of the Department of Defense, overseeing day-to-day management, including procurement, logistics, installations, and responses to emerging crises, while McNamara focused on broader policy and systems analysis initiatives.[4] His tenure, lasting until June 30, 1967, spanned 1,249 days—the longest for any deputy secretary up to that time—and coincided with escalating U.S. military commitments abroad and domestic defense expansions.[4] Almost immediately upon assuming office, Vance addressed the January 1964 riots in the Panama Canal Zone, where Panamanian resentment over U.S. control erupted into violence along the Zone border, resulting in at least 20 deaths.[4] President Johnson dispatched Vance to negotiate with Panamanian officials and Canal Zone authorities, leading to agreements on border clearance and tension reduction; Vance publicly attributed the riots' escalation to communist agents trained in Cuba, denying Panamanian claims of U.S. provocation.[25][4] In April 1965, amid civil war in the Dominican Republic following the overthrow of President Donald Reid Cabral, Vance led multiple fact-finding and negotiation missions as a special presidential envoy.[26] The unrest, pitting constitutionalist rebels against loyalist forces, raised fears of a communist foothold similar to Cuba; Vance coordinated with Ambassador William Tapley Bennett Jr. and helped broker temporary truces, including a 24-hour ceasefire, while facilitating the U.S. deployment of over 20,000 Marines and troops to stabilize the situation and protect American citizens.[27][28] His efforts emphasized impartiality amid accusations of U.S. bias toward one side, ultimately contributing to the establishment of a provisional government under Antonio Imbert Barrera.[27] Vance's role extended to internal defense reforms, including oversight of military procurement and logistics amid rapid force expansions, though chronic back pain from an old injury worsened during this period, prompting his resignation effective June 30, 1967.[4] He was succeeded by Paul Nitze.

Involvement in Vietnam Policy and Resignations

As Deputy Secretary of Defense from January 28, 1964, to June 1967, Vance played a central role in shaping U.S. military strategy in Vietnam under President Lyndon B. Johnson. He endorsed the administration's gradual escalation, including the introduction of U.S. combat troops in March 1965 and the initiation of sustained bombing campaigns against North Vietnam, such as Operation Rolling Thunder. Vance viewed these measures as necessary to counter communist aggression and support South Vietnam's government, aligning with Johnson's "flexible response" doctrine that combined military pressure with diplomatic overtures.[4][1] In April 1965, Vance led a fact-finding mission to South Vietnam, where he assessed the situation on the ground and reported to Johnson that the military effort was "going quite well," reinforcing optimism about bolstering South Vietnamese forces through U.S. advisory and air support. He also contributed to key decisions on troop deployments, which grew from approximately 23,000 advisors in 1964 to over 184,000 combat troops by the end of 1965, and advised on the balance between ground operations and aerial interdiction to disrupt North Vietnamese supply lines via the Ho Chi Minh Trail. However, as casualties mounted and progress stalled, Vance began questioning the sustainability of pure military solutions, privately advocating limits on bombing to preserve leverage for negotiations.[4] By late 1965, Vance's support for escalation waned amid evidence of North Vietnamese resilience and domestic unrest, leading him to urge restraint in air power usage and caution against overcommitment. These evolving reservations, compounded by a chronic back ailment exacerbated by stress, prompted his resignation on June 30, 1967, after serving 1,249 days—the longest tenure of any deputy secretary to that point. Although officially attributed to health issues, the departure reflected his growing conviction that the conflict's political dimensions required diplomatic prioritization over further troop surges, which had reached 485,000 by mid-1967.[4][1] Johnson retained Vance as an informal advisor and appointed him deputy to W. Averell Harriman for preliminary Paris talks with North Vietnam in March–April 1968, aimed at achieving a bombing cessation and mutual de-escalation. Vance pushed for halting strikes on North Vietnam to build trust, but the sessions deadlocked over preconditions. Convinced by mid-1968 that military victory was unattainable and that renewed bombing would sabotage peace prospects, Vance resigned from the negotiating role, marking his full withdrawal from Vietnam policymaking and underscoring the limits of coercion in achieving strategic objectives.[4][29]

Secretary of State under President Carter (1977–1980)

Appointment and Key Initial Policies

President Jimmy Carter nominated Cyrus Vance as Secretary of State on January 21, 1977, two days after his inauguration, selecting him for his extensive experience in national security roles during the Kennedy and Johnson administrations.[1] The Senate Foreign Relations Committee held confirmation hearings on January 11, 1977, prior to the inauguration, reflecting Vance's established reputation, and the Senate confirmed him shortly thereafter.[30] Vance was sworn into office on January 23, 1977, assuming leadership of the State Department amid Carter's commitment to restoring U.S. moral authority in foreign affairs following the Vietnam War and Watergate scandal.[19] Vance's initial policies emphasized negotiation over military confrontation and placed human rights at the center of U.S. foreign policy, aligning with Carter's campaign pledges.[1] In a February 4, 1977, memorandum to Carter, Vance outlined early priorities, including advancing negotiations for the Panama Canal treaties to transfer control to Panama and promoting human rights through diplomatic engagement rather than unilateral pressure.[31] He advocated a pragmatic approach, cautioning against overly rigid impositions that could undermine broader strategic goals, as articulated in his May 1, 1977, statements urging realism in human rights advocacy to avoid counterproductive outcomes.[32] Vance also focused on arms control early in his tenure, supporting the continuation of Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT) with the Soviet Union to curb nuclear proliferation through verifiable agreements.[4] These policies reflected a broader effort to rebuild alliances and multilateral cooperation, with Vance undertaking initial diplomatic missions to consult European allies on Carter's agenda, including human rights and détente.[33] This approach sought to balance idealism with practical constraints, prioritizing sustained dialogue to achieve concrete diplomatic results.[34]

Major Diplomatic Negotiations and Treaties

As Secretary of State, Vance prioritized multilateral negotiations to address longstanding geopolitical tensions, emphasizing arms control, decolonization disputes, and regional conflicts in line with the Carter administration's focus on diplomacy over confrontation.[1] His efforts contributed to several landmark agreements, though outcomes varied in ratification and long-term impact due to domestic opposition and international events.[35] Vance played a pivotal role in negotiating the Panama Canal Treaties, convincing President Carter of their necessity early in the administration despite initial reservations.[36] The two treaties— the Treaty Concerning the Permanent Neutrality and Operation of the Panama Canal and the Panama Canal Treaty—were signed on September 7, 1977, in Washington, D.C., by Carter and Panamanian leader Omar Torrijos, providing for the gradual transfer of the canal to Panamanian control by December 31, 1999, while ensuring its neutrality and U.S. defense rights in perpetuity.[36] [37] Vance briefed Carter on unresolved issues ahead of key summits and led a national speaking tour in January 1978 to build Senate support for ratification, which succeeded by April 1978 with narrow majorities of 68-32 and 67-33 votes.[38] [37] In arms control, Vance spearheaded the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT II) with the Soviet Union, conducting multiple high-level meetings, including one with Foreign Minister Andrei Gromyko in Washington in September 1977.[39] The resulting treaty, signed by Carter and Soviet leader Leonid Brezhnev on June 18, 1979, in Vienna, imposed equal ceilings on strategic offensive arms: 2,400 delivery vehicles initially, reducing to 2,250 by 1981, with sublimits on MIRV-equipped missiles and bombers, and a protocol banning new missile development for 18 months.[39] [40] Vance advocated decoupling SALT from regional crises like the Soviet intervention in the Horn of Africa to maintain momentum, though the treaty faced Senate hurdles and was never ratified following the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979.[41] Vance supported intensive shuttle diplomacy in the Middle East, collaborating with Israeli officials Moshe Dayan and Ezer Weizman to lay groundwork for the Camp David Accords.[2] From the administration's outset, he and Carter engaged Arab and Israeli leaders to resolve the post-1973 War stalemate, culminating in the accords signed on September 17, 1978, at Camp David by Egyptian President Anwar Sadat and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin, which outlined a framework for Egyptian-Israeli peace and Palestinian autonomy in the West Bank and Gaza.[42] These paved the way for the Egypt-Israel Peace Treaty signed on March 26, 1979, in Washington, D.C., establishing full diplomatic relations, Israel's withdrawal from the Sinai Peninsula by April 1982, and mutual non-aggression pledges, marking the first Arab recognition of Israel.[1] [2] Vance's preparatory efforts included delivering Carter's invitations to Sadat and Begin, though the summit's success hinged on Carter's direct mediation.[43]

Foreign Policy Controversies and Resignation

Vance's tenure as Secretary of State grew increasingly strained amid escalating crises, particularly the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan on December 27, 1979, and the ongoing Iran hostage crisis that began with the seizure of the U.S. Embassy in Tehran on November 4, 1979, holding 52 Americans captive.[1] In response to the Afghan invasion, Vance advocated a "balanced" approach emphasizing diplomatic pressure and economic sanctions—such as the U.S. grain embargo imposed in January 1980—while cautioning against a return to Cold War-era confrontation, arguing that the U.S. should avoid "indiscriminate" escalation to preserve broader negotiation channels with the Soviet Union.[44] Critics within the administration, including National Security Advisor Zbigniew Brzezinski, viewed Vance's stance as overly conciliatory, accusing it of underestimating Soviet aggression and contributing to a perception of American weakness; this internal feud, which dated back to policy divergences on arms control and human rights, intensified as President Carter shifted toward more assertive measures, often sidelining Vance's counsel.[45][1] The Iran crisis amplified these tensions, with Vance prioritizing multilateral diplomacy, including secret talks mediated by Algeria, over unilateral military action, believing that force would sabotage negotiations and inflame anti-American sentiment in the Islamic world.[46] Despite his objections, Carter approved Operation Eagle Claw—a covert rescue mission involving helicopters and Delta Force troops—on April 4, 1980, after months of deliberation where Vance warned of high risks and diplomatic fallout.[47] The operation aborted disastrously on April 24–25, 1980, in the Iranian desert due to equipment failures and a sandstorm, resulting in the collision of a helicopter and C-130 aircraft that killed eight U.S. servicemen and destroyed vital assets without reaching the hostages.[46] Vance had submitted his resignation letter to Carter on April 21, 1980—prior to the mission's execution—explicitly citing his inability to support the "difficult decision" for military intervention, which he saw as inconsistent with sustained diplomatic efforts.[48] Vance's resignation, announced publicly on April 28, 1980, and accepted by Carter with regret for his "dedicated and effective service," marked the culmination of broader foreign policy controversies over the Carter administration's pivot from Vance's negotiation-centric framework to reactive assertiveness.[7] He was immediately succeeded by Senator Edmund Muskie, signaling a potential thaw in U.S.-Iran talks, though the hostages remained captive until January 20, 1981.[49] Detractors, including administration hawks, faulted Vance's dovish preferences for emboldening adversaries, as evidenced by the Afghan occupation's persistence despite U.S. sanctions and the Olympic boycott, while Vance himself later defended his positions in memoirs as grounded in realism to avert wider conflicts.[50] These debates underscored divisions between diplomatic restraint and forceful deterrence, with Vance's exit highlighting the administration's challenges in reconciling moral imperatives, such as human rights advocacy, against pragmatic geopolitical pressures.[1]

Post-Secretary of State Career

Special Envoy Assignments

Following his resignation as U.S. Secretary of State in April 1980, Vance accepted a series of special envoy roles for the United Nations, primarily focused on mediating conflicts in the Balkans during the dissolution of Yugoslavia. In November 1991, as Personal Envoy of UN Secretary-General Javier Pérez de Cuéllar, Vance negotiated the Vance Plan, a ceasefire agreement aimed at ending the Croatian War of Independence by demilitarizing three UN Protected Areas in Croatia controlled by Croatian Serbs and the Yugoslav People's Army, while preserving Croatia's sovereignty. The plan, signed on January 2, 1992, in Lisbon by Croatian President Franjo Tuđman and Serbian leader Slobodan Milošević, initially held but unraveled by March 1992 amid renewed fighting, leading to UNPROFOR deployment to enforce it. In August 1992, Vance was appointed UN Co-Chairman of the newly established International Conference on the Former Yugoslavia (ICFY), alongside European Community representative Lord David Owen, to coordinate multilateral peace efforts amid escalating violence in Bosnia-Herzegovina. Under this framework, Vance and Owen drafted the Vance-Owen Peace Plan in January 1993, proposing to partition Bosnia into ten ethnically based provinces with loose confederation ties, demilitarization provisions, and safeguards for human rights, while rejecting territorial gains by Bosnian Serbs beyond 1991 borders.[51] The plan gained tentative support from the European Community and Croatia but was rejected by Bosnian Serb leaders in a May 1993 referendum, citing insufficient contiguous territory, prompting Vance to criticize the impasse as undermining international credibility. Vance resigned as ICFY Co-Chairman and UN mediator on the Balkans effective May 1993, citing exhaustion after 17 months of fruitless negotiations and frustration with inconsistent U.S. policy under President Bill Clinton, which he viewed as insufficiently committed to enforcement. His efforts, while yielding short-term ceasefires—such as the January 1992 halt to Croatian fighting—failed to prevent the Bosnian genocide or broader Yugoslav fragmentation, though they laid groundwork for later accords like Dayton in 1995. In a secondary assignment, Vance briefly served as UN envoy to Nagorno-Karabakh in March 1992, assessing the Armenia-Azerbaijan conflict during a fact-finding visit to Stepanakert, but this role did not yield formal agreements.[52]

Return to Private Law Practice

After resigning as Secretary of State on April 28, 1980, in protest of President Jimmy Carter's decision to authorize a military rescue attempt for the Iran hostages, Cyrus Vance rejoined the New York law firm Simpson Thacher & Bartlett, where he had been a partner since 1957 and to which he had previously returned after earlier government roles.[20][4] At the firm, Vance resumed his practice primarily in civil litigation and corporate law, drawing on his pre-government expertise in areas such as antitrust and regulatory matters.[4][53] Vance's tenure at Simpson Thacher post-1980 was marked by selective engagement in private sector work, as his reputation for discreet negotiation frequently pulled him into ad hoc public and international advisory roles, though he maintained an active partnership at the firm until his retirement.[1][54] He contributed to the firm's litigation practice, which emphasized complex commercial disputes, and occasionally addressed professional ethics issues, as evidenced by his writings on legal integrity in government transitions.[53] In addition to firm duties, Vance's private practice period included leadership in legal reform efforts, such as chairing the Fund for Modern Courts from 1986 to 1990, where he advocated for judicial improvements in New York State, reflecting his long-standing interest in enhancing court efficiency and impartiality without direct government affiliation.[55] This role complemented his litigation background, focusing on systemic issues like court modernization rather than individual cases.[55]

Personal Life and Death

Family and Relationships

Cyrus Vance was born on March 27, 1917, in Clarksburg, West Virginia, to John Carl Vance II, a civil engineer, and Amy Roberts Vance.[10] The family, including an older brother, relocated to Bronxville, New York, during his youth.[10] On February 15, 1947, Vance married Grace Elsie Sloane, known as "Gay," a Bryn Mawr College graduate and daughter of John Sloane, chairman of the W. & J. Sloane furniture company.[56][2] The couple resided primarily in New York City and raised five children: daughters Elsie Nicoll Vance, Amy Sloane Vance, Grace Roberts Vance, and Camilla Vance, and son Cyrus Roberts Vance Jr., born June 14, 1954.[2][57] Grace Vance outlived her husband, passing away on March 22, 2008.[58]

Health Decline and Death (2002)

In the years following his retirement from public service, Vance experienced a progressive decline in health due to Alzheimer's disease, a neurodegenerative condition that impaired his cognitive functions over several years.[12][59] This led to increasing dependency and vulnerability to secondary infections, culminating in his hospitalization.[17] Vance died on January 12, 2002, at Mount Sinai Medical Center in New York City, at the age of 84, from pneumonia and associated complications arising from his advanced Alzheimer's.[12][59][60] His passing was confirmed by family members and reported in major outlets, noting the protracted nature of his illness.[17]

Legacy and Historical Assessments

Principal Achievements and Contributions

Vance's most notable diplomatic achievement was his leadership in negotiating the Panama Canal Treaties, signed on September 7, 1977, which committed the United States to transferring control of the canal to Panama by December 31, 1999, while ensuring its neutral operation thereafter; the U.S. Senate ratified the treaties in March and April 1978 despite domestic opposition.[3] [1] As the principal U.S. negotiator in the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT II), Vance conducted multiple rounds of discussions with Soviet Foreign Minister Andrei Gromyko, culminating in the treaty signed on June 18, 1979, in Vienna, which imposed equal numerical ceilings of 2,400 on strategic launchers for both superpowers, reducing to 2,250 by 1981, and limited multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles (MIRVs) to curb the arms race—though the Senate never ratified it due to the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan.[39] [1] Vance also advanced U.S.-China normalization through his visit to Beijing from August 21 to 25, 1977, where he outlined principles for ending formal ties with Taiwan and recognizing the People's Republic, paving the way for full diplomatic relations established on January 1, 1979, and the derecognition of the Republic of China.[1] In Middle East diplomacy, he contributed preparatory work for the Camp David Accords in September 1978, facilitating the framework that led to the Egypt-Israel Peace Treaty signed on March 26, 1979—the first between Israel and an Arab state—by coordinating with Israeli officials Moshe Dayan and Ezer Weizman and emphasizing mutual security guarantees.[3] [1] Earlier in his career, as a delegate to the Paris Peace Talks in 1968, Vance helped negotiate the release of 82 crew members from the USS Pueblo seized by North Korea earlier that year, securing their return without major concessions.[1] As Secretary of State, he institutionalized human rights as a core element of U.S. foreign policy by elevating the human rights coordinator to Assistant Secretary level in August 1977 and establishing related bureaus for refugees and narcotics control, influencing sanctions and aid decisions globally.[33] These efforts reflected Vance's preference for multilateral diplomacy and legal frameworks over unilateral action, shaping Carter administration priorities amid Cold War tensions.[3]

Criticisms and Policy Failures

Vance's handling of the Iranian Revolution has been widely criticized as a policy failure stemming from inadequate anticipation of the Shah's downfall and inconsistent U.S. support for a key ally. The Carter administration's emphasis on human rights led to public criticisms of the Shah's regime, which some analysts argue undermined his stability amid rising domestic unrest, culminating in the revolution on February 11, 1979, and the establishment of an anti-American theocratic government under Ayatollah Khomeini. [61] This misjudgment, compounded by internal policy debates between Vance's diplomatic caution and Zbigniew Brzezinski's calls for firmer backing of the Shah, resulted in the loss of Iran as a strategic partner and set the stage for the U.S. embassy takeover on November 4, 1979, where 52 Americans were held hostage for 444 days.[62] [63] The subsequent Iran hostage crisis exposed perceived weaknesses in Vance's negotiation-centric strategy, as repeated diplomatic overtures, including Vance's direct efforts, failed to secure the hostages' release despite concessions like unfreezing Iranian assets. Critics, including Brzezinski, faulted Vance for prioritizing multilateral talks over decisive pressure, viewing the impasse as emblematic of broader diplomatic ineffectiveness that eroded U.S. credibility abroad.[64] [65] Vance's resignation on April 28, 1980, in protest of President Carter's approval of Operation Eagle Claw—the botched military rescue mission that resulted in eight U.S. deaths due to mechanical failures and coordination issues—highlighted deep administration divisions but did little to mitigate accusations that prolonged reliance on talks prolonged the crisis and humiliated the U.S.[65] Vance's pursuit of the SALT II treaty, signed on June 18, 1979, faced backlash for insufficient verification mechanisms and perceived Soviet advantages in throw-weight and MIRV technology, which critics argued did not curb qualitative arms advancements despite equal numerical ceilings.[66] The treaty's derailment by the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan on December 24, 1979, underscored complaints that Vance decoupled arms control from Soviet adventurism, such as in the Horn of Africa, fostering an impression of U.S. naivety.[41] Similarly, the administration's response to the Afghan invasion—encompassing a U.S.-led Olympic boycott, grain embargo, and UN condemnation—drew rebukes for lacking teeth, with Brzezinski later decrying Vance's "balanced" approach as inadequate to deter Soviet expansionism, contributing to policy paralysis.[64] [67] These episodes fueled broader assessments of Vance's tenure as overly legalistic and multilateral, ill-suited to confronting authoritarian aggression.[45]

Diverse Political Perspectives on His Tenure

Liberals and Democrats often commended Vance for prioritizing diplomatic engagement and multilateralism during his tenure from January 20, 1977, to April 28, 1980. They highlighted his lead role in negotiating the SALT II treaty, signed on June 18, 1979, which aimed to cap strategic nuclear delivery vehicles at 2,400 for both the U.S. and Soviet Union, with sub-limits on multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles (MIRVs), as a significant step toward arms control and reducing superpower tensions.[41][1] Supporters, including figures in the Carter administration, viewed his emphasis on human rights—evident in policies pressuring allies like Brazil over authoritarian practices—as a principled advancement of American values through negotiation rather than coercion.[33] His resignation in protest against the April 24-25, 1980, Operation Eagle Claw rescue mission for the Iran hostages, which he deemed reckless and likely to escalate conflict, was praised by doves as a demonstration of integrity against impulsive militarism.[3][54] Conservatives and Republicans, conversely, critiqued Vance's approach as emblematic of Carter-era foreign policy weakness, fostering Soviet adventurism and eroding U.S. deterrence. Ronald Reagan, during his 1980 campaign, lambasted the administration's handling of security threats, implicitly targeting Vance's detente-focused strategy—including SALT II, which critics argued verified Soviet superiority in certain missile categories without sufficient verification mechanisms—as naive appeasement that invited the December 1979 Soviet invasion of Afghanistan.[68][69] They pointed to the November 4, 1979, Iran hostage crisis, where Vance's preference for diplomacy over early military options failed to secure the release of 52 Americans held for 444 days, as evidence of ineffective leadership amid rising global disorder.[70] Figures like Reagan's advisors argued that Vance's multilateralism, such as in the stalled Panama Canal treaties ratified in 1978, prioritized international consensus over American interests, contributing to perceptions of decline that propelled Reagan's victory on November 4, 1980.[71][72] Centrist analysts acknowledged Vance's bureaucratic competence and contributions to normalizing relations with China via the December 15, 1978, joint communiqué, but faulted internal administration divisions—particularly his clashes with National Security Advisor Zbigniew Brzezinski over balancing human rights with realpolitik—as diluting policy coherence.[73][74] These perspectives reflect broader ideological divides: progressives valuing Vance's restraint amid Cold War pressures, while hawks saw his tenure as exacerbating vulnerabilities exploited by adversaries, with empirical outcomes like the unratified SALT II and hostage ordeal underscoring the limits of negotiation without credible force.[75]
