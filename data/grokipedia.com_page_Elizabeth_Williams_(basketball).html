Elizabeth Williams (basketball)

Elizabeth Olatayo Williams (born June 23, 1993) is a professional basketball player of Nigerian descent who competes as a center for the Chicago Sky of the Women's National Basketball Association (WNBA).[1][2] Standing at 6 feet 3 inches, she is recognized for her defensive versatility and rebounding ability, having amassed 1,680 rebounds and 444 blocks over her WNBA career spanning appearances in 287 games.[3] Williams honed her skills at Duke University, where she became the first Atlantic Coast Conference player to surpass 1,900 points, 1,000 rebounds, and 400 blocks in a career, earning the WBCA National Defensive Player of the Year award in 2015 along with four-time All-America honors.[4] Drafted fourth overall by the Connecticut Sun in the 2015 WNBA Draft and traded to the Atlanta Dream on draft night, she later joined the Chicago Sky, contributing to teams with stints including the 2017 All-Star selection, 2016 Most Improved Player award, and All-Defensive First Team in 2020.[3][2] Of dual Nigerian and American heritageâ€”holding a Nigerian passport since childhoodâ€”Williams sought to represent Nigeria at the senior international level but was ruled ineligible by FIBA for the 2020 Tokyo Olympics due to prior participation in USA youth and AmeriCup competitions, despite an unsuccessful appeal to the Court of Arbitration for Sport.[4][5] She has also played professionally overseas, including for FenerbahÃ§e in Turkey.[6]

Early life and background

Family heritage and upbringing

Elizabeth Olatayo Williams was born on June 23, 1993, in Colchester, England, to Nigerian parents Dr. Alex Williams, a gastroenterologist, and Margaret Williams, a registered nurse.[7][8] As the middle child alongside older sister Victoria and younger brother Mark, she grew up immersed in her family's Nigerian heritage while navigating life as immigrants in the United States after relocating there in 1996 for her father's medical residency at the University of Connecticut.[7] The family later moved to Virginia Beach, Virginia, when Williams was eight years old, following her father's employment at a local hospital, which shaped her formative years in a household prioritizing medical service and professional dedication amid frequent relocations including stints in Connecticut and North Carolina.[7] Her parents' demanding careers modeled a strong work ethic and commitment to helping others, influencing Williams' early aspirations toward medicine and instilling discipline through their emphasis on perseverance and family support despite irregular schedules.[7] Williams' dual Nigerian-American identity fostered resilience, with her initial exposure to basketball occurring around age nine through local programs in Virginia Beach, where her 6-foot-3 frame and athletic potential began drawing attention for the sport's physical demands.[9][1] This early involvement, supported by familial encouragement amid their immigrant emphasis on achievement, laid the groundwork for structured athletic development without overshadowing the household's focus on education and service.[7]

High school career

Elizabeth Williams attended Princess Anne High School in Virginia Beach, Virginia, where she played varsity basketball for four seasons, helping the team compile a 120â€“6 record.[8] Under her leadership, the Cavaliers won Virginia Class AAA state championships in 2009 and 2011, reached the state title game in 2010, and advanced to the semifinals in 2008.[4] As a senior in the 2010â€“11 season, Williams averaged 18.9 points, 11.2 rebounds, and 5.1 blocks per game, showcasing her dominance as a defensive force in the post.[10] She maintained an average exceeding five blocks per game throughout her high school career, establishing her reputation as an elite shot-blocker with quick anticipation and timing.[11] Her defensive prowess contributed to her selection as a top national prospect, ranked among the elite in the class of 2011 by recruiting services such as ESPN HoopGurlz, where she was viewed as the premier post player.[12] Williams earned multiple All-American honors, including first-team Parade All-American, WBCA All-American, and MVP of the 2011 McDonald's All-American Game, where she recorded 23 points and 11 rebounds to set a single-game record.[12][13][14] She also received the 2011 Morgan Wootten National Player of the Year award, Virginia Gatorade Player of the Year in 2010, and Associated Press Virginia Player of the Year in 2010.[8][12] Highly sought after by top college programs, Williams committed to Duke University on November 16, 2010.[15]

Her contributions included strong scoring and rebounding, helping the teams maintain undefeated records in competition.[4][16] In the 2009 FIBA Americas U16 Championship in Mexico City, Mexico, the USA team won gold with a perfect 5-0 record. Williams started all five games, averaged 13.4 points per game, and earned MVP honors for her performance.[4][16][17] At the 2010 FIBA Under-17 World Championship in Rodez, Franceâ€”the inaugural edition of the eventâ€”the USA claimed gold, defeating host France 79-70 in the final. She played in all nine games, averaging 8.9 points and 5 rebounds per game.[16][18][4]

2009 FIBA Americas U16 Championship

The USA women's under-16 basketball team, featuring Elizabeth Williams as a starting center, captured the gold medal at the inaugural 2009 FIBA Americas U16 Championship for Women, held August 10â€“14 in Mexico City, Mexico, by posting a perfect 5â€“0 record.[4][8] The squad demonstrated dominance through efficient offensive execution and stout defense, outscoring opponents by an average margin exceeding 40 points per game, culminating in a 103â€“52 rout of Canada in the championship final.[19] Williams contributed 13.4 points and 5.2 rebounds per game across the five contests, leveraging her 6-foot-3 frame for interior presence and shot-blocking.[16][4] In the final, she led all scorers with 19 points, underscoring her emerging role in anchoring the team's frontcourt against regional competition.[19] Her performances earned her the tournament most valuable player award, highlighting early international prowess at age 16.[17]

2010 FIBA Under-17 World Championship

The United States women's national under-17 team won the gold medal at the 2010 FIBA Under-17 World Championship for Women, held from July 16 to 25 in Rodez and Toulouse, France, defeating the host nation France in the final to finish with an undefeated 8â€“0 record.[20][21] Elizabeth Williams, a 6-foot-5 center, started all eight games and led the team in scoring with 13.5 points per game and rebounding with 7.6 per game, while also averaging 1.8 assists; her tournament total of 108 points was the highest among all players.[16][22] These figures underscored her physical dominance and efficiency rating of 19.6 per game, reflecting early command of interior play against international competition.[16][8] Williams contributed significantly to the team's defensive resilience, leveraging her size for rebounding control and shot alteration, with the USA maintaining the only perfect record amid challenges from European and other rivals.[23] In the championship final against France, she partnered with guard Kaleena Lewis to score 19 of the Americans' final 27 points, securing the lead after a tight contest.[21] Her performance highlighted adaptations to more structured European offenses, emphasizing rebounding and transition efficiency over pure athleticism.[23] This tournament marked Williams' progression from her U16 debut, showcasing rebounding prowess that foreshadowed her college-level impact, as her per-game averages demonstrated maturing post presence against physically competitive foes like France and China.[16][8]

2013 FIBA Under-19 World Championship

Williams represented the United States at the FIBA Under-19 World Championship in Puerto Montt, Chile, where the team secured the gold medal with an 8-1 overall record, including a 77-67 victory over Canada in the final.[4] As a key frontcourt player, she started six of nine games, averaging 8.9 points, 5.0 rebounds, and contributing significantly on defense in a tournament featuring heightened competition from teams like Canada and South Korea.[16] [24] Her field goal efficiency stood at 52.3 percent, underscoring efficient scoring inside against varied global defenses.[24] Williams delivered standout contributions in multiple contests, including 15 points alongside teammate Stefanie Dolson in a decisive win, highlighting her role in interior dominance and rebounding battles.[25] Her defensive impact, rooted in shot-blocking ability honed from high school, proved vital against taller international posts, with the USA outrebounding opponents in championship-clinching efforts. No major injuries were reported, though the team's sole loss to Canada in pool play prompted tactical adjustments toward more aggressive interior play, elevating her minutes and usage.[26] This event capped her youth international tenure, with her averages reflecting matured post presence and leadership in high-stakes scenarios, empirically linking to subsequent professional adaptability through proven efficacy in rebounding and efficiency metrics against elite youth talent.[4]

College career at Duke University

Williams enrolled at Duke University in 2011 and played four seasons for the Duke Blue Devils women's basketball team from 2011 to 2015.[27] Over 136 games, she averaged 14.4 points, 7.9 rebounds, and a nation-leading 3.1 blocks per game, finishing her career with 426 blocks, ranking ninth in NCAA history.[27][3] She became the first player in Atlantic Coast Conference (ACC) history to record over 1,900 points, 1,000 rebounds, and 400 blocks, while ranking second all-time at Duke in rebounds (1,078) and fourth in points (1,955).[8] Williams earned four ACC Defensive Player of the Year awards (2012â€“2015), the only player to achieve this feat, and was a four-time Associated Press (AP) All-American, another ACC first.[28][29] Her defensive impact helped Duke compile a 111â€“26 record, win one ACC regular-season title, and reach two Elite Eights and one Sweet 16 in NCAA Tournaments, though the team fell short of the Final Four.[4] As a freshman in 2011â€“12, Williams started all 33 games, averaging 14.0 points, 7.8 rebounds, and 3.5 blocks per game while shooting 48.6% from the field.[27] She blocked at least one shot in each of her first 91 career games, setting Duke and ACC records, and earned AP All-America Third Team honors along with her first ACC Defensive Player of the Year award.[30][29] Duke advanced to the NCAA Sweet 16 that season. In 2012â€“13, Williams appeared in 36 games (32 starts), boosting her scoring to 15.2 points per game at 50.8% field goal efficiency, with 7.3 rebounds and 3.0 blocks.[27] She repeated as ACC Defensive Player of the Year and secured another AP All-America Third Team selection.[28] The Blue Devils reached the Elite Eight, where they lost to Notre Dame.[4] Her junior year (2013â€“14) saw Williams average 13.8 points, 7.6 rebounds, 3.1 blocks, and a career-high 2.2 assists across 35 games (34 starts), maintaining 52.6% field goal shooting.[27] She three-peated as ACC Defensive Player of the Year and earned AP All-America Third Team honors for the third straight time.[28] Duke again advanced to the Elite Eight, falling to Stanford.[4] As a senior in 2014â€“15, Williams elevated her rebounding to 9.0 per game and added 2.5 assists, averaging 14.5 points in 32 games (31 starts).[27] She was named WBCA National Defensive Player of the Year, AP All-America Second Team, USBWA All-American, and WBCA All-American, while repeating as ACC Defensive Player of the Year and first-team All-ACC.[31][27] Academically, she received Capital One Academic All-America honors and the ACC's Kay Yow Scholar-Athlete of the Year award, maintaining a psychology degree with a 3.46 GPA.[32][33] Duke reached the NCAA Sweet 16 that year.[4]

Professional career

WNBA career

Williams was selected by the Connecticut Sun with the fourth overall pick in the 2015 WNBA Draft out of Duke University.[1] In her rookie season, she appeared in 21 games, averaging 3.3 points, 3.2 rebounds, and 0.9 blocks per game while shooting 43.5% from the field.[1] Prior to the 2016 season, the Sun traded Williams to the Atlanta Dream in exchange for the fourth overall pick in that year's draft.[34] Williams experienced a breakout sophomore campaign with Atlanta, starting all 34 games and posting averages of 11.9 points, 8.1 rebounds, 1.2 assists, and a league-leading 2.3 blocks per game, which earned her the WNBA Most Improved Player Award. She maintained strong production over her six seasons with the Dream (2016â€“2021), appearing in 187 games with averages of 9.1 points, 6.3 rebounds, and 1.6 blocks per game; highlights included selection to the 2017 All-Star Game and All-Defensive First Team honors in 2020 after averaging 10.1 points, 5.7 rebounds, and 1.4 blocks in the COVID-shortened season.[1] Williams signed with the Washington Mystics as a free agent in February 2022.[35] Limited by injury, she played in 30 games that season, averaging 5.4 points, 3.8 rebounds, and 0.6 blocks per game while providing depth in the frontcourt.[1] Ahead of the 2023 season, Williams joined the Chicago Sky, where she has remained through 2025. In her first year with Chicago, she started all 40 games, averaging 9.8 points, 5.8 rebounds, 2.5 assists, 1.3 steals, and 1.5 blocks per game en route to All-Defensive Second Team and WNBA Sportsmanship Award honors.[1] She continued as a defensive anchor in 2024 (averaging 10.0 points and 7.0 rebounds in 9 games before injury) and 2025 (8.5 points, 5.2 rebounds, and 1.8 assists in 43 games).[1]

Connecticut Sun (2015)

Williams was selected by the Connecticut Sun as the fourth overall pick in the first round of the 2015 WNBA Draft, held on April 16 at Mohegan Sun Arena in Uncasville, Connecticut.[36] As a rookie center coming off a standout college career at Duke University, where she earned multiple defensive accolades, Williams faced adaptation challenges in transitioning to professional play, logging limited minutes off the bench in a crowded frontcourt rotation.[1] In 21 regular-season games for the Sun, Williams averaged 3.3 points, 3.2 rebounds, and 0.9 blocks per game in 11.7 minutes of play, showcasing early defensive potential through her shot-blocking but struggling with offensive efficiency and rebounding volume relative to her draft position.[1] The Sun finished the 2015 season with a 15â€“19 record, placing fifth in the Eastern Conference and missing the playoffs, during which Williams contributed as a rotational big in a defense-oriented role under head coach Anne Donovan but did not emerge as a primary scoring option.[37] Her empirical output reflected typical rookie hurdles, including adjustment to WNBA physicality and pace, rather than immediate stardom.[1]

Williams was acquired by the Atlanta Dream via trade from the Connecticut Sun on February 3, 2016, in exchange for the Dream's fourth overall pick in the 2016 WNBA Draft.[34][4] In her debut season with Atlanta, she posted career highs of 11.9 points, 8.1 rebounds, and 2.3 blocks per game across 34 appearances, earning the WNBA Most Improved Player award.[38][39] These figures marked substantial gains from her rookie year, reflecting improved efficiency (49.1% field goal shooting) and rim protection that anchored the Dream's frontcourt during a 17-17 regular season that advanced to the playoffs.[38] Over her six seasons with Atlanta (2016â€“2021), Williams appeared in 187 games, averaging 9.4 points, 6.4 rebounds, and 1.8 blocks per game while starting 163 contests.[40] She accumulated 283 blocks, establishing herself as the franchise's all-time leader in the category.[3] Her defensive prowess peaked in 2020, when she earned All-Defensive First Team honors amid a shortened 22-game season impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic; Washington Mystics (2022)

On February 1, 2022, the Washington Mystics signed Williams to a one-year contract as a free agent, adding frontcourt depth with her prior All-WNBA Defensive Second Team selection in 2021.[35] [45] Williams played in 30 regular-season games for the Mystics without starting, averaging 14.9 minutes, 5.4 points on 48.2% field goal shooting, 3.8 rebounds, 0.6 assists, 0.6 steals, and 0.6 blocks per game.[1] Her contributions focused on defense and rebounding efficiency in limited minutes, recording five double-digit scoring outings including a season-high 12 points against the Connecticut Sun on August 14.[3] The Mystics, bolstered by a league-best defensive rating of 98.6 points allowed per 100 possessions, finished 22â€“14 and clinched a playoff spot as the No. 5 seed.[46] In the first-round playoffs, Washington was swept 0â€“2 by the Seattle Storm, with Williams appearing in both games for 2.5 points, 1.0 rebound, and 0.5 blocks in 8.5 minutes per contest.[1] Her stint marked a reduced role compared to prior seasons, serving as a rotational big in a crowded frontcourt amid the team's push for postseason contention.[47]

Williams signed with the Chicago Sky as a free agent on February 3, 2023.[3] In her debut season with the team, she started all 40 games, averaging 9.8 points, 5.8 rebounds, 2.5 assists, 1.3 steals, and 1.5 blocks per game while logging 29.7 minutes per game.[3] She led the Sky in blocks per game (1.5) and total blocks (60), ranking fifth league-wide in the latter category, and became the first player in franchise history to start every game in a season.[48] Her defensive contributions earned her a selection to the WNBA All-Defensive Second Team.[49] In 2024, Williams appeared in nine games before sustaining a torn meniscus in her knee during a June 6 matchup against the Atlanta Dream, leading to season-ending surgery on June 27.[50] Despite the injury, the Sky signed her to a one-year contract extension on September 19, securing her services through the 2025 season.[48] Off the court, she maintained a leadership role, providing guidance to teammates as the team's sole holdover from its previous playoff appearance.[51] Williams returned to action in 2025 following recovery from her knee injury, playing in 43 games with 15 starts and averaging 21.1 minutes per game.[52] She posted averages of 8.5 points, 5.2 rebounds, 1.8 assists, and 0.7 steals per game, contributing to the Sky's rotation amid various absences.[53] As secretary of the WNBPA, she played a prominent role in union leadership and collective bargaining agreement negotiations, emphasizing her influence in league-wide player advocacy.[51] In her September 12 exit interview, she reflected on her on-court impact and off-court mentorship.[54]

Activism and public engagement

Social justice and league initiatives

In 2020, Williams contributed to the WNBA's social justice initiatives amid heightened attention to the Black Lives Matter movement, including opposition to co-owner Kelly Loeffler's public criticism of the league's planned Black Lives Matter messaging and apparel.[64] Atlanta Dream players, with Williams at the forefront, amplified calls for racial justice through team statements and participation in league-wide actions such as wearing warmup shirts emblazoned with slogans like "Say Her Name" and dedicating the season to victims of police violence.[65] These efforts built on a WNBA social justice council formed earlier that year, which Williams supported as a player representative focused on issues like police accountability.[66] Following the August 23 shooting of Jacob Blake in Kenosha, Wisconsin, Williams read a joint player statement on ESPN on August 26, announcing the postponement of that night's three scheduled WNBA games in solidarity with protests against racial injustice and police brutality.[67] The league extended the pause through August 27, marking one of the WNBA's earliest collective work stoppages in response to social issues, which players framed as a commitment to ongoing advocacy rather than abandonment of the season.[68] This action, echoed across NBA and other leagues, highlighted player-led unity on empirical demands for reform, though outcomes included resumed play with continued messaging like court decals and ad campaigns promoting awareness.[69] As secretary of the WNBPA Executive Committee, Williams played a key role in the union's leadership during the 2025 collective bargaining agreement negotiations, advocating for terms that would deliver players a larger revenue share from the league's growing business, estimated at over $200 million annually by 2024.[70] In August 2025, she voiced doubts about finalizing a deal by the October 31 opt-out deadline, citing persistent gaps in proposals despite weekly player leadership meetings, while emphasizing the CBA's potential to address long-term economic equity tied to players' on-court and off-court contributions.[71] [51] These negotiations, initiated formally in late 2024, built on prior union successes but faced scrutiny for slow progress, with the WNBA denying any intentional delays.[72]

Political endorsements and campaigns

In August 2020, Elizabeth Williams, then a forward for the Atlanta Dream, joined teammates in wearing "Vote Warnock" T-shirts during WNBA games to endorse Raphael Warnock's U.S. Senate campaign against the team's co-owner, Kelly Loeffler, amid Loeffler's public criticism of the league's Black Lives Matter initiatives.[73][74] Williams, serving as secretary of the WNBA Players Association, helped coordinate the players' response, including statements and visibility efforts via social media and on-court actions that amplified Warnock's candidacy in Georgia.[64][75] The players' campaign extended to paid advertisements and rallies, with Williams publicly stating the effort aimed to counter Loeffler's influence and mobilize voters, contributing to Warnock's recognition boost from single-digit polling in August to his projected victory in the January 5, 2021, runoff election.[76][77] On election night, Williams described the outcome as a direct result of the team's "fighting for our lives," attributing Georgia's Senate flip partly to the WNBA's endorsement and grassroots push, which Warnock himself cited as a "turning point" in his campaign.[78][77] These actions aligned with broader WNBA shifts toward explicit partisan engagement, including subsequent league-wide ads opposing restrictive state laws, though Williams' direct involvement remained centered on the 2020 Georgia race.[79][76]

Criticisms and counterperspectives

Kelly Loeffler, then co-owner of the Atlanta Dream, criticized the WNBA's support for Black Lives Matter as an endorsement of a "radical Marxist group" founded on principles promoting socialism and violence, arguing it politicized the league and advanced a divisive agenda rather than fostering unity.[80][81] Loeffler contended in a July 2020 letter to WNBA Commissioner Cathy Engelbert that the movement sought to exploit the league for political gain, urging replacement of BLM slogans on jerseys with American flags to prioritize national cohesion over partisan causes.[82] This stance clashed directly with players like Elizabeth Williams, who, as a Dream forward, voiced frustration over Loeffler's opposition and joined teammates in wearing T-shirts endorsing Loeffler's Senate opponent, Raphael Warnock, escalating the owner-player divide.[74] The rift contributed to internal tensions within the Atlanta Dream franchise, with players demanding Loeffler's removal and the WNBA distancing itself from her views, ultimately prompting her to sell her 49% stake in January 2021 amid ongoing discord.[83] Critics, including Loeffler, argued such activism fostered locker room and ownership divisions, correlating with the team's dismal 23-65 record from 2019 to 2021, though direct causation remains unproven beyond the publicized conflict.[83] Counterperspectives highlight risks of sports politicization, positing that overt partisan endorsements, like the Dream's backing of Warnock, alienated conservative audiences and owners, potentially undermining league neutrality despite claims of electoral impact in Georgia's 2020 Senate runoff.[75] While WNBA advocates attribute Warnock's victory partly to player visibility against Loeffler, broader analyses note confounding factors such as record voter turnout and national Democratic gains, questioning the activism's isolated causal role over statewide trends.[76] Conservative commentators have warned that sustained league-wide political engagement post-2020 risks fan backlash by prioritizing ideology over athletics, though empirical viewership data shows overall growth rather than uniform decline attributable to these efforts.[75]

Awards and honors

In youth international competition, Williams was named MVP of the 2009 FIBA Americas U16 Championship, where she led the United States to the gold medal with standout performances in scoring and rebounding as evaluated by tournament officials.[84] During her college tenure at Duke University from 2011 to 2015, Williams secured the Atlantic Coast Conference (ACC) Defensive Player of the Year award each season from 2012 to 2015, a selection by conference head coaches recognizing her league-leading blocks and defensive efficiency.[8] In 2015, she was voted the WBCA NCAA Division I Defensive Player of the Year by a panel of women's basketball head coaches, honoring her national impact in limiting opponents' scoring opportunities through rim protection and positioning.[31] In the WNBA, Williams earned the Most Improved Player award in 2016, determined by a media panel assessing year-over-year advancements in production and role expansion with the Atlanta Dream.[38] She received All-Defensive First Team honors in 2020 and Second Team in 2023, both chosen by WNBA head coaches based on defensive ratings and contributions to team schemes, though such selections incorporate subjective elements beyond raw metrics.[49] Additionally, in 2023, she was awarded the Kim Perrot Sportsmanship Award by player peers for exemplifying integrity, fair play, and respect in interactions.[85] Abroad, Williams was named the EuroLeague Women Defensive Player of the Year for the 2021â€“22 season with FenerbahÃ§e Safiport, an accolade from league evaluators highlighting her blocks, steals, and overall disruption, selected amid competition from elite European centers.[86]

Personal life and off-court pursuits

Education and professional development

Williams earned a Bachelor of Science degree in psychology from Duke University upon graduating in 2015, achieving a 3.43 grade-point average while competing as a four-time All-American and leading the Blue Devils to multiple NCAA Tournament appearances.[32][8] Her undergraduate coursework included premed classes, reflecting early interests in medicine influenced by her parents' medical professions, though she prioritized a versatile degree compatible with her athletic schedule.[87] In 2024, Williams completed a Master of Science in global health through Arizona State University's online program, leveraging a partnership between the WNBA and ASU designed for active players to pursue advanced education amid professional demands.[88][87] This flexible format enabled her to manage graduate studies alongside WNBA seasons, overseas play, and recovery from injuries such as surgery, with coursework emphasizing healthcare disparities and global systems relevant to athlete wellness.[89][90] Williams has supplemented her formal education with leadership roles in the WNBA Players' Association, serving as secretary since at least 2023, which involved training in collective bargaining and advocacy skills during the 2025 CBA negotiations.[91][92] These experiences, combined with her academic background, have supported her transition into mentorship for younger players, fostering skills in team dynamics and long-term career planning essential for WNBA longevity.[93]

Health and community involvement

Williams earned a Master of Science in Global Health from Arizona State University in December 2024, emphasizing cultural anthropology and evolutionary medicine to inform her advocacy for improved healthcare access worldwide.[87] As part of her coursework, she interviewed a teammate and team physician in Turkey on healthcare delivery for Syrian refugees, highlighting challenges in immigrant and displaced populations.[87] In her role as a Women's National Basketball Players Association representative, Williams led education efforts on COVID-19, organizing Zoom sessions with physicians, mental health experts, and epidemiologists that contributed to a 99% vaccination rate among WNBA players.[87][94] Dubbed the league's "Chief Health Officer" by peers, she drew on her family background in medicine to facilitate these initiatives.[94] Williams extended her outreach through Instagram series "E Talks with Docs," linking followers to medical professionals for direct health discussions.[87] She advocates for expanding representation of Black physicians and healthcare workers of color, arguing it enhances outcomes in underserved communities.[87] Influenced by her Nigerian immigrant heritage, she plans medical school to apply a holistic health perspective addressing disparities.[87][92] Following a meniscus tear in June 2024 that ended her season, Williams underwent surgery and intensive rehabilitation, resuming play with the Chicago Sky in 2025 while finalizing her degree.[95]

Career statistics and playing style

College statistics

Williams competed for the Duke Blue Devils women's basketball team from 2011 to 2015, appearing in 136 games and starting 130.[27] Her career per-game averages included 14.4 points, 7.9 rebounds (3.6 offensive), 3.1 blocks, and 1.9 assists, while shooting 50.3% from the field overall.[27] She concluded her collegiate career with 1,955 points, 1,078 rebounds, and 426 blocks, marking her as the first Atlantic Coast Conference (ACC) player to surpass 1,900 points, 1,000 rebounds, and 400 blocks.[8][27] Williams ranked among ACC and national leaders in blocks per game each season, with her 3.1 career blocks per game placing her second in Duke history.[27]

Analysis of defensive impact and versatility

Elizabeth Williams has established herself as a premier rim protector in the WNBA, averaging 1.5 blocks per game over her career through the 2025 season, with a peak of 2.3 blocks per game in 2020 that earned her All-Defensive Second Team honors.[1][3] Her career total of 483 blocks ranks her among the league's elite shot-blockers, exemplified by her 2023 season where she recorded 60 blocks, placing fourth league-wide.[1][96] This interior presence not only rejects shots but alters opponents' decisions at the rim, contributing to team defensive efficiencies; for instance, in 2023 with the Chicago Sky, her timely blocks set the tone in key moments, such as early-game rejections that anchored the team's identity.[97] Complementing her blocking prowess, Williams excels in rebounding and anticipation, averaging 3.5 defensive rebounds per game career-wide, with a high of 5.0 in 2016, securing possessions and limiting second-chance opportunities.[1] Her 0.9 steals per game reflect strong positioning and reading of passing lanes, peaking at 1.7 in 2024, which underscores her disruptive influence beyond the paint.[1] Advanced metrics further quantify her impact: she posted a defensive box plus/minus of 3.1 in 2024, her career best, and 1.9 defensive win shares in 2018, indicating consistent value in preventing opponent scoring.[1] In the 2022 season with the Washington Mystics, she demonstrated this through a 23-minute bench stint yielding six blocks and 15 rebounds, elevating the team's ceiling against versatile offenses.[98] Williams' defensive versatility stems from her high basketball IQ and physical toolsâ€”standing 6'5" with a 7'2" wingspanâ€”enabling effective help rotations and communication in varied schemes, rather than perimeter switching.[51] She frequently provides diagonal help across the floor to contest guards and wings, as seen in sequences where her conviction in recovery positioning disrupts plays, compensating for lesser lateral quickness typical of traditional centers.[51] This adaptability shone in her 2023 All-Defensive Second Team selection, where her interior anchoring allowed teammates to extend defenses outward, and in college at Duke, where she earned four ACC Defensive Player of the Year awards for similar multi-faceted contributions as a center/forward.[1][8] While not a switch-everything defender, her vocal leadership and precise rotations make her a scheme-versatile anchor, as evidenced by her role in the Sky's interior leadership with 1.6 blocks and 1.1 steals per game in recent play.[99]
