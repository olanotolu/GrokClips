Cystocele

A cystocele, also known as a prolapsed bladder, is a medical condition in which a woman's bladder bulges into her vagina.[1][2] Some may have no symptoms.[3] Others may have trouble starting urination, urinary incontinence, or frequent urination.[1] Complications may include recurrent urinary tract infections and urinary retention.[1][4] Cystocele and a prolapsed urethra often occur together and is called a cystourethrocele.[5] Cystocele can negatively affect quality of life.[6][7] Causes include childbirth, constipation, chronic cough, heavy lifting, hysterectomy, genetics, and being overweight.[1][8][3] The underlying mechanism involves weakening of muscles and connective tissue between the bladder and vagina.[1] Diagnosis is often based on symptoms and examination.[1] If the cystocele causes few symptoms, avoiding heavy lifting or straining may be all that is recommended.[1] In those with more significant symptoms a vaginal pessary, pelvic muscle exercises, or surgery may be recommended.[1] The type of surgery typically done is known as a colporrhaphy.[9] The condition becomes more common with age.[1] About a third of women over the age of 50 are affected to some degree.[2]

Signs and symptoms

The symptoms of a cystocele may include:
  • a vaginal bulge
  • the feeling that something is falling out of the vagina
  • the sensation of pelvic heaviness or fullness[1][10]
  • difficulty starting a urine stream
  • a feeling of incomplete urination
  • frequent or urgent urination[10][1]
  • frequent urinary tract infections[4][10]
  • back and pelvic pain
  • painful sexual intercourse[10]
Many women with mild cystocele have no symptoms, while those with moderate to severe prolapse may experience more pronounced issues.[1][3] A bladder that has dropped from its normal position and into the vagina can cause some forms of incontinence and incomplete emptying of the bladder.[1]

Complications

Complications may include urinary retention, recurring urinary tract infections and incontinence.[1][4] The anterior vaginal wall may protrude through the vaginal introitus (opening). This can interfere with sexual activity.[3] Recurrent urinary tract infections are common for those who have urinary retention.[11] In addition, though cystocele can be treated, some treatments may not alleviate troubling symptoms, and further treatment may need to be performed. Cystoceles may affect the quality of life.[6][7]

Cause

A cystocele occurs when the muscles, fascia, tendons and connective tissues between a woman's bladder and vagina weaken, or detach.[8][12] The type of cystocele that can develop can be due to one, two or three vaginal wall attachment failures: the midline defect, the paravaginal defect, and the transverse defect. The midline defect is a cystocele caused by the overstretching of the vaginal wall; the paravaginal defect is the separation of the vaginal connective tissue at the arcus tendineus fascia pelvis; the transverse defect is when the pubocervical fascia becomes detached from the top (apex) of the vagina.[8] There is some pelvic prolapse in 40â€“60% of women who have given birth.[13] Muscle injuries have been identified in women with cystocele. These injuries are more likely to occur in women who have given birth than those who have not. These muscular injuries result in less support to the anterior vaginal wall.[14] Some women with connective tissue disorders are predisposed to developing anterior vaginal wall collapse. Up to one third of women with Marfan syndrome have a history of vaginal wall collapse. Ehlersâ€“Danlos syndrome in women is associated with a rate of 3 out of 4.[3] Specific subtypes of Ehlersâ€“Danlos syndrome, such as hypermobile and classical types, show higher risks due to collagen defects.[15]

Risk factors

Risk factors for developing a cystocele are:
  • an occupation involving or a history of heavy lifting
  • pregnancy and childbirth
  • chronic lung disease/smoking
  • family history of cystocele[16][17]
  • ethnicity (risk is greater for Hispanic and whites)[18]
  • hypoestrogenism
  • pelvic floor trauma
  • connective tissue disorders
  • spina bifida[3]
  • hysterectomy[19]
  • cancer treatment of pelvic organs[20]
  • number of childbirths
  • forceps delivery
  • age
  • chronically high intra-abdominal pressures
    • chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
    • constipation[10]
    • obesity[3]
Connective tissue disorders predispose women to developing cystocele and other pelvic organ prolapses. The tensile strength of the vaginal wall decreases when the structure of the collagen fibers change and become weaker.[3]

Diagnosis

There are two types of cystocele. The first is distension. This is thought to be due to the overstretching of the vaginal wall and is most often associated with aging, menopause and vaginal delivery. It can be observed when the rugae are less visible or absent. The second type is displacement. Displacement is the detachment or abnormal elongation of supportive tissue.[21] The initial assessment of cystocele can include a pelvic exam to evaluate leakage of urine when the woman is asked to bear down or give a strong cough (Valsalva maneuver), and the anterior vaginal wall measured and evaluated for the appearance of a cystocele.[22][23] If a woman has difficulty emptying her bladder, the clinician may measure the amount of urine left in the woman's bladder after she urinates called the postvoid residual. This is measured by ultrasound. A voiding cystourethrogram involves taking X-rays of the bladder during urination. This X-ray shows the shape of the bladder and lets the doctor see any problems that might block the normal flow of urine.[1] A urine culture and sensitivity test will assess the presence of a urinary tract infection that may be related to urinary retention.[10] Other tests may be needed to find or rule out problems in other parts of the urinary system.[1]

Grading

Several scales exist to grade the severity of a cystocele. The pelvic organ prolapse quantification (POP-Q) assessment, developed in 1996, quantifies the descent of the cystocele into the vagina.[3][24] The POP-Q provides reliable description of the support of the anterior, posterior and apical vaginal wall. It uses objective and precise measurements to the reference point, the hymen. Cystocele and prolapse of the vagina from other causes is staged using POP-Q criteria can range from good support (no descent into the vagina) reported as a POP-Q stage 0 or I to a POP-Q score of IV which includes prolapse beyond the hymen. It also used to quantifies the movement of other structures into the vaginal lumen and their descent.[3][24] The Badenâ€“Walker Halfway Scoring System is used as the second most used system and assigns the classifications as mild (grade 1) when the bladder droops only a short way into the vagina; (grade 2) cystocele, the bladder sinks far enough to reach the opening of the vagina; and (grade 3) when the bladder bulges out through the opening of the vagina.[1][25]

Classifications

Cystoceles can be further described as being apical, medial, or lateral.[15] Apical cystocele is located upper third of the vagina. The structures involved are the endopelvic fascia and ligaments. The cardinal ligaments and the uterosacral ligaments suspend the upper vaginal-dome. The cystocele in this region of the vagina is thought to be due to a cardinal ligament defect.[12][21] Medial cystocele forms in the mid-vagina and is related to a defect in the suspension provided by a sagittal suspension system defect in the uterosacral ligaments and pubocervical fascia. The pubocervical fascia may thin or tear and create the cystocele. This defect can be assessed by MRI.[12][21] Lateral cystocele forms when the pelviperineal muscle and its ligamentousâ€“fascial develop a defect. The ligamentousâ€“ fascial creates a 'hammock-like' suspension and support for the lateral sides of the vagina. Defects in this lateral support system result in a lack of bladder support. Cystocele that develops laterally is associated with an anatomic imbalance between anterior vaginal wall and the arcus tendineus fasciae pelvis â€“ the essential ligament structure.[12][21]

Prevention

Cystocele may be mild enough not to result in symptoms that are troubling to a woman. In this case, steps to prevent it from worsening include:
  • smoking cessation
  • losing weight
  • pelvic floor strengthening
  • treatment of a chronic cough
  • maintaining healthy bowel habits
    • eating high fiber foods
    • avoiding constipation and straining[18][3]

Treatment

Treatment options range from no treatment for a mild cystocele to surgery for a more extensive cystocele.[1] If a cystocele is not bothersome, the clinician may only recommend avoiding heavy lifting or straining that could cause the cystocele to worsen. If symptoms are moderately bothersome, the doctor may recommend a pessary, a device placed in the vagina to hold the bladder in place and to block protrusion.[10][19] Treatment can consist of a combination of non-surgical and surgical management. Treatment choice is also related to age, desire to have children, severity of impairment, desire to continue sexual intercourse, and other diseases that a woman may have.[3]

Non-surgical

Cystocele is often treated by non-surgical means:
  • Pessary â€“ This is a removable device inserted into the vagina to support the anterior vaginal wall. Pessaries come in many different shapes and sizes. Vaginal pessaries can immediately relieve prolapse and prolapse-related symptoms.[26] There are sometimes complications with the use of a pessary.[3]
  • Pelvic floor muscle therapy â€“ Pelvic floor exercises to strengthen vaginal support can be of benefit. Specialized physical therapy can be prescribed to help strengthen the pelvic floor muscles.[1][10]
  • Dietary changes â€“ Ingesting high fiber foods will aid in promoting bowel movements.[1]
  • Estrogen â€“ intravaginal administration helps to prevent pelvic muscle atrophy[10]

Surgery

The surgery to repair the anterior vaginal wall may be combined with other procedures that will repair the other points of pelvic organ support such as anterior-posterior repair and anterior colporrhaphy.[10] If uterine prolapse is also present, treatment may include hysterectomy or uterus-sparing procedures.[27] Since the failure rate in cystocele repair remains high, additional surgery may be needed.[24] Women who have surgery to repair a cystocele have approximately a 17-20% chance of needing another operation within the next ten years.[28] The surgical treatment of cystocele will depend on the cause of the defect and whether it occurs at the top (apex), middle, or lower part of the anterior vaginal wall. The type of surgery will also depend on the type of damage that exists between supporting structures and the vaginal wall.[8] One of the most common surgical repairs is colporrhaphy.[27] This surgical procedure consists of making a longitudinal folding of the vaginal tissue, suturing it into place and creating a stronger point of resistance to the intruding bladder wall. As of 2019, transvaginal surgical mesh is no longer available for anterior vaginal wall repair due to high complication rates, per FDA order; native tissue repair is preferred.[29] In some abdominal or laparoscopic approaches, such as sacrocolpopexy, mesh may be used to strengthen the repair.[27] During surgery, the repair of the vaginal wall consists of folding over and then suturing the existing tissue between the vagina and bladder to strengthen it.[1][9] This tightens the layers of tissue to promote the replacement of the pelvic organs into their normal place. The surgery also provides more support for the bladder. This surgery is done by a surgeon specializing in gynecology and is performed in a hospital. Anesthesia varies according to the needs of each woman. Recovery may take four to six weeks.[1] Other surgical treatments may be performed to treat cystoceles. Support for the vaginal wall is accomplished with the paravaginal defect repair. This is a surgery, usually laparoscopic, that is done on the ligaments and fascia through the abdomen. The lateral ligaments and supportive structures are repaired, sometimes shortened to provide additional support to the vaginal wall.[27] Sacrocolpopexy is a procedure that stabilizes the vaginal vault (the uppermost portion of the vagina) and is often chosen as the treatment for cystocele, especially if previous surgeries were not successful. The procedure consists of attaching the vaginal vault to the sacrum. It has a success rate of 90%.[27] Some women choose not to have surgery to close the vagina. This surgery, called colpocleisis, treats cystocele by closing the vaginal opening. This can be an option for women who no longer want to have vaginal intercourse.[18] If an enterocele/sigmoidocele or prolapse of the rectum/colon is also present, the surgical treatment will take this concurrent condition into account while planning and performing the repairs.[8] Estrogen that is administered vaginally before surgical repair can strengthen the vaginal tissue providing a more successful outcome when mesh or sutures are used for the repair. Vaginal thickness increases after estrogen therapy.[30] Another review on the surgical management of cystocele describes a more successful treatment that more strongly attaches the ligaments and fascia to the vagina to lift and stabilize it.[31] Post-surgical complications can develop. The complications following surgical treatment of cystocele are:
  • side effects or reactions to anesthesia
  • bleeding
  • infection
  • painful intercourse
  • Urinary incontinence
  • constipation[32]
  • bladder injuries
  • urethral injuries
  • urinary tract infection.[27][4]
After surgery, a woman is instructed to restrict her activities and monitor herself for signs of infection such as an elevated temperature, discharge with a foul odor, and consistent pain. Clinicians may recommend that sneezing, coughing, and constipation be avoided. Splinting the abdomen while coughing supports an incised area and decreases pain with coughing.[10] This is accomplished by applying gentle pressure to the surgical site for bracing during a cough.[33][34] Recurrent surgery on the pelvic organs may not be due to a failure of the surgery to correct the cystocele. Subsequent surgeries can be directly or indirectly related to the primary surgery.[24] Prolapse can occur at a different site in the vagina. Further surgery after the initial repair can be to treat complications of mesh displacement, pain, or bleeding. Additional surgery may be needed to treat incontinence.[24] One goal of surgical treatment is to restore the vagina and other pelvic organs to their anatomically normal positions. This may not be the most important outcome to the treated woman, who may only want symptom relief and to improve her quality of life. The International Urogynecological Association (IUGA) has recommended that the data collected regarding cystocele and pelvic organ repair success include the presence or absence of symptoms, satisfaction, and Quality of Life. Other measures of a successful outcome should include perioperative data, such as operative time and hospital stay. Standardized Healthcare Quality of Life should be part of the measure of a successful resolution of cystocele. Data regarding short- and long-term complications is included in the IUGA's recommendations to assess the procedure's riskâ€“benefit ratio more effectively.[24] Current investigations into the superiority of using biological grafting versus native tissue or surgical mesh indicate that grafting provides better results.[35] As of 2025, guidelines from ACOG and others emphasize native tissue repairs and shared decision-making for pelvic organ prolapse treatment.[36]

Epidemiology

Other studies indicate a recurrence rate as low as 3%.[24] In the US, approximately 140,000 surgeries are performed each year for pelvic organ prolapse, with about 81% to correct cystocele.[37] Cystocele occurs most frequently compared to the prolapse of other pelvic organs and structures.[24][38] Cystocele is found to be three times as common as vaginal vault prolapse and twice as often as posterior vaginal wall defects. The incidence of cystocele is around 9 per 100 women-years. The highest incidence of symptoms occurs between the ages of 70 and 79 years. Based on population growth statistics, the number of women with prolapse will increase by a minimum of 46% by the year 2050 in the US. Surgery to correct prolapse after hysterectomy is 3.6 per 1,000 women-years.[24] A 2024 systematic review estimated the global prevalence of pelvic organ prolapse at 30.9% (95% CI: 24.4â€“38.2%).[39]

History

Cystocele has been recognized and treated since ancient times.[40] In approximately 2000 B.C., the Egyptian Kahun Papyrus described symptoms of uterine prolapse, referred to as the "falling of the womb." The Ebers Papyrus (ca. 1550 B.C.) recommended treatments involving honey, oils, and other substances.[40] Around 400 B.C., Greek physicians, including contemporaries of Hippocrates like Euryphon, documented treatments for prolapse. One method, known as succussion, involved tying the patient upside down and shaking her to reduce the prolapse using gravity. Hippocrates attributed prolapse to factors such as recent childbirth, exertion, fatigue, and other causes. Treatments included astringent lotions, vaginal sponges, and halved pomegranates inserted as crude pessaries. In the 1st century A.D., Soranus of Ephesus refined these by using vinegar- or acacia-dipped wool tampons and olive oil baths, and recommended bed rest with restricted diet if needed. For severe cases, he suggested ligation and excision of gangrenous tissue.[40][41] In 1521, Jacopo Berengario da Carpi performed one of the first recorded surgical interventions for prolapse, involving ligation with a rope to induce necrosis, followed by amputation and application of wine, aloe, and honey to the stump.[40] In the 18th century, Swiss physician Johann Conrad Peyer described cystocele anatomically alongside uterine prolapse. Efforts to standardize terminology began, and in 1730, some practitioners associated cystocele with childbirth trauma.[41] The 19th century saw significant advancements due to anesthesia, improved suturing, and Joseph Lister's antisepsis principles, which reduced surgical risks. Early vaginal dissections and repairs were proposed, such as incisions and tissue edge approximation. In 1866, J. Marion Sims developed a procedure resembling modern anterior colporrhaphy, involving plication without full-thickness vaginal wall dissection. In 1888, the Manchester procedure combined anterior repair, cervical amputation, and perineorrhaphy. Abdominal approaches were explored, including reattachment via the inguinal canal.[41] In the 20th century, recurrence rates prompted refinements, such as reattaching the vaginal wall to supportive structures like Cooper's ligament in 1961. Mesh reinforcement began in 1955 with tantalum gauze. In 1970, biological materials like collagen (including porcine-derived) were used. The 1976 Richardson technique emphasized paravaginal repairs. By the 1990s, debates arose over pelvic anatomy models, questioning some traditional support structures.[41] Since the early 2000s, minimally invasive techniques have dominated, including laparoscopic and robot-assisted sacrocolpopexy for cystocele repair, offering lower morbidity. Mesh use peaked but declined after 2011 FDA warnings on transvaginal mesh complications, leading to bans in some countries and a return to native tissue repairs. Emerging regenerative approaches, such as platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections and stem cell therapies, show promise for non-surgical management, with studies from 2020-2025 reporting improved symptoms in pelvic floor disorders. Novel suturing techniques, like rug-weaving plication, aim to enhance native tissue durability without mesh. As of 2025, robotic surgery remains a standard for complex cases, with ongoing research into biomechanical and dynamic support models.[41][42][43]
