List of currencies in Europe

The currencies of Europe consist of the official monetary units employed by the continent's 44 sovereign states, encompassing both national currencies and the euro, which functions as the shared legal tender for 20 Eurozone member countries and is unilaterally adopted by microstates including Andorra, Monaco, San Marino, and Vatican City.[1][2][3] This diversity arises from varying degrees of economic integration, with the Eurozone facilitating a single monetary policy across its participants via the European Central Bank, while non-Eurozone nations such as the United Kingdom with its pound sterling and Switzerland with its franc maintain independent central banking and exchange rate regimes to preserve policy autonomy.[2][4] Notable among these are pegged arrangements, like Denmark's krone fixed to the euro, underscoring partial alignments with the euro area without full adoption, amid broader European efforts toward monetary stability post-1990s convergence criteria.[3][4]

Eurozone Participation and Euro Usage

Official Eurozone Member States

The Eurozone consists of 20 sovereign European Union member states that have officially adopted the euro (â‚¬) as their sole legal tender, irrevocably fixing their previous national currencies to the euro at conversion rates established upon entry.[5] These states include the original 11 members who launched the euro on January 1, 1999, followed by subsequent accessions: Greece in 2001, Slovenia in 2007, Cyprus and Malta in 2008, Slovakia in 2009, Estonia in 2011, Latvia in 2014, Lithuania in 2015, and Croatia in 2023.[5] Monetary policy for all members is centralized under the European Central Bank (ECB), which sets interest rates and conducts operations to maintain price stability across the zone. Euro banknotes are issued uniformly by the ECB and national central banks, featuring shared designs, while euro coins include a common obverse side and national-specific reverse designs, with all denominations interchangeable across the zone. Adoption has empirically reduced cross-border transaction costs, facilitating increased intra-Eurozone trade by eliminating currency exchange expenses and hedging needs, as documented in analyses of trade volumes post-1999.[6] However, the relinquishment of national monetary sovereignty prevents independent adjustments to exchange rates or interest rates tailored to domestic conditions, contributing to challenges during asymmetric shocks, such as the post-2008 financial crisis when divergent inflation ratesâ€”higher in peripheral states like Greece and Spainâ€”eroded competitiveness without devaluation options.[7][8] This loss of policy flexibility has been cited by economists as amplifying recessionary pressures in high-debt members unable to pursue expansionary measures independently.[7]

Non-Sovereign and Unilateral Euro Users

The Principality of Andorra, the Principality of Monaco, the Republic of San Marino, and Vatican City employ the euro through dedicated monetary agreements with the European Union, permitting its official use as legal tender and the minting of denomination-specific euro coins with entity-specific obverse designs, though without participation in the European Central Bank's monetary policy or governance. These entities transitioned to euro banknotes and coins on 1 January 2002, aligning with the eurozone's cash changeover, replacing prior reliance on neighboring states' currenciesâ€”French francs and Spanish pesetas in Andorra, French francs in Monaco, and Italian lire in San Marino and Vatican City.[9] For Andorra, de facto euro circulation commenced in 2002, but formal EU ratification occurred via a signed monetary agreement on 30 June 2011, addressing prior informal usage and enabling coin issuance rights.[9] Monaco's arrangement stems from its 1963 customs union with France, extended to the euro via EU-aligned protocols; San Marino and Vatican City's pacts, rooted in historical ties to Italy, were updated in 2001 and 2003 to incorporate euro provisions, including coin minting quotas.[10][11] In parallel, the Republic of Montenegro and the Republic of Kosovoâ€”neither EU members nor parties to formal monetary pactsâ€”have unilaterally euroized their economies since 2002, designating the euro as de facto legal tender without European Central Bank oversight, seigniorage revenue, or coin-minting authority. Montenegro replaced the Yugoslav dinar with the Deutsche Mark (euro predecessor) in November 1999 amid hyperinflation exceeding 30% annually, formalizing euro use post-2002 independence from Serbia; Kosovo similarly shifted from the dinar after its 1999 autonomy declaration and 2008 independence, driven by currency instability in the post-Yugoslav collapse.[12][13] This approach yields dollarization-like advantages, including rapid disinflationâ€”Montenegro's CPI fell from 32% in 1999 to under 2% by 2003â€”and enhanced transaction efficiency, fostering financial integration with eurozone neighbors.[13] Yet, it constrains domestic monetary autonomy, eliminating seigniorage (estimated at 0.5-1% of GDP for comparable economies) and a lender of last resort, amplifying vulnerability to external shocks; during the 2008-2009 global crisis, Montenegro's fully euroized banks faced acute liquidity strains from deposit outflows and credit contraction exceeding 20%, reliant on foreign parent banks for support absent central bank intervention tools.[14][15]

Currencies of Non-EU Sovereign European Countries

Established Western European Currencies

The currencies of non-EU Western European sovereign states emphasize monetary autonomy, enabling tailored responses to economic conditions without alignment to the eurozone's unified policy framework. These nationsâ€”primarily the United Kingdom, Switzerland, Norway, Iceland, and Liechtensteinâ€”have historically prioritized national control over interest rates, inflation targeting, and exchange rate management, often leveraging natural resources, financial services, or fiscal reserves to maintain stability. This approach contrasts with supranational integration, as evidenced by repeated referenda or policy decisions rejecting euro adoption, such as Switzerland's 1992 European Economic Area accession without monetary union and Norway's 1994 referendum outcome.[27] The United Kingdom employs the pound sterling (GBP), retained post-Brexit completion on December 31, 2020, with no government announcements of euro adoption plans as of 2025; this allows independent Bank of England rate adjustments, such as the 5.25% base rate set in August 2023 to combat inflation exceeding 10% earlier that year.[28] GBP's flexibility supports trade diversification beyond EU dependencies, underpinning post-Brexit growth in sectors like services exports, which reached Â£325 billion in 2024. Switzerland and Liechtenstein utilize the Swiss franc (CHF), legal tender in both since Liechtenstein's 1924 adoption via monetary union, forgoing a national currency to align with Switzerland's stability framework. CHF's safe-haven appeal stems from Switzerland's neutral stance, banking sector assets exceeding 400% of GDP as of 2023, and gold reserves of approximately 1,040 tonnes held by the Swiss National Bank. Inflation averaged 0.62% annually from 2000 to 2024, reflecting disciplined monetary policy and low public debt at 40% of GDP in 2024.[29][30] Norway maintains the Norwegian krone (NOK), established in 1875 and upheld despite two failed EU membership bids (1972 and 1994), with floating exchange rates managed by Norges Bank since 1992 to buffer oil price volatility; the currency's value is supported by the $1.6 trillion Government Pension Fund Global as of 2025, equivalent to over 300% of GDP.[31][32] Iceland uses the Icelandic krÃ³na (ISK), which depreciated by over 50% against major currencies during the 2008 banking collapseâ€”when bank assets ballooned to 10 times GDPâ€”enabling a recovery driven by tourism and fisheries exports that restored pre-crisis GDP levels by 2011 through deliberate devaluation rather than bailouts.[33][34]

Eastern European and Balkan Currencies

Non-EU countries in Eastern Europe and the Balkans employ distinct national currencies influenced by post-Yugoslav fragmentation, Soviet-era legacies, and proximity to EU markets, with some adopting euro pegs to curb inflation and foster trade stability while others maintain floating regimes vulnerable to geopolitical disruptions. The Bosnia and Herzegovina convertible mark (BAM) was introduced on January 1, 1998, replacing multiple interim currencies after the 1995 Dayton Accords to unify the divided economy and anchor post-war recovery; it remains fixed to the euro at 1 EUR = 1.95583 BAM, providing low inflation averaging under 2% annually since adoption but constraining independent monetary responses to local shocks.[35][36]

Transcontinental and Other Non-EU Currencies

Russia, a transcontinental country with the majority of its population and economic activity in its European territory west of the Ural Mountains, uses the Russian ruble (ISO 4217: RUB) as its official currency across both European and Asian regions. The ruble, subdivided into 100 kopecks, has been in circulation since 1998 following the post-Soviet redenomination, with the Central Bank of Russia managing monetary policy through interest rate adjustments and foreign exchange interventions. Western sanctions imposed starting February 2022, in response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine, led to an initial sharp depreciation of the rubleâ€”falling over 30% against the U.S. dollar in the first weeksâ€”and a spike in inflation to a peak of 17.7% year-over-year in April 2022, driven by disrupted imports, supply chain issues, and capital outflows. Inflation subsequently moderated due to capital controls, export revenues from energy sales to non-Western partners, and a floating exchange rate regime, averaging 7.4% in 2023 and reaching 9.52% for full-year 2024, with rates around 8% by September 2025. To counter sanction-induced isolation from SWIFT and dollar-based systems, Russia has pursued partial de-dollarization, increasing ruble-denominated trade settlements (rising from 20% to over 40% of exports by 2023) and leveraging BRICS frameworks for alternative payment mechanisms, though full decoupling remains limited by ruble volatility and dependence on commodity exports.[44][45][46][47] Turkey, spanning southeastern Europe and western Asia with its European Thrace region housing about 10-15% of the population, employs the Turkish lira (ISO 4217: TRY), divided into 100 kuruÅŸ, issued by the Central Bank of the Republic of Turkey. The lira has faced persistent devaluation since the 2018 currency crisis, losing over 80% of its value against the U.S. dollar from 2018 to 2024, exacerbated by unorthodox monetary policies under President Recep Tayyip ErdoÄŸan, who has advocated lowering interest rates to combat inflation on the theory that high rates themselves drive price increasesâ€”a view contradicting standard economic models emphasizing rate hikes to curb demand-pull inflation. This approach contributed to annual inflation averaging approximately 30-40% from 2018 to 2023, peaking at 85.5% in late 2022, with rates remaining elevated at 53.86% in 2023 and 58.51% in 2024 before easing to around 33% by September 2025 following partial policy normalization under new economic leadership. Repeated devaluations have eroded domestic purchasing power, fueled import costs for energy and goods, and prompted capital flight, though recent export gains from a weaker lira have supported trade balances.[48][49][50][51] Belarus, a non-EU Eastern European state closely aligned with Russia, utilizes the Belarusian ruble (ISO 4217: BYN), introduced in 2016 as a redenomination of the prior base unit at 1:10,000, with the National Bank of the Republic of Belarus overseeing issuance and subdivided into 100 kopecks. The currency operates under a managed floating exchange rate loosely pegged to a basket of foreign currencies, historically including the U.S. dollar, euro, and Russian ruble, though the euro's weight was reduced and effectively excluded by December 2022 amid declining EU trade volumes and sanctions alignment with Russia. This basket approach aims to stabilize the BYN against volatility from Belarus's export-dependent economy (heavily reliant on potash, machinery, and refined oil re-exports), but external shocks like the 2022 sanctions have pressured reserves, leading to interventions and periodic devaluations, such as a 20% adjustment in 2023. Inflation has been contained relative to neighbors at around 5-12% annually in recent years, supported by subsidized energy imports from Russia and administrative price controls, though the peg's composition reflects deepening economic integration with non-Western partners over EU ties.[52][53]

Currencies in Microstates, Enclaves, and Dependencies

European Microstates

European microstates, defined as sovereign entities with limited territory and population such as Andorra, Liechtenstein, Monaco, San Marino, and Vatican City, rely on currencies from neighboring larger economies to facilitate trade and monetary stability, given their lack of independent central banking infrastructure.[54] These arrangements, often formalized through bilateral or multilateral agreements, allow seamless integration into regional markets while forgoing the costs and complexities of maintaining national currencies. Four of the five microstates use the euro (â‚¬, EUR) as legal tender, stemming from historical ties to France, Italy, and the European Union, whereas Liechtenstein maintains a longstanding monetary union with Switzerland.

Currencies in Disputed and Partially Recognized Entities

Partially Recognized States

Abkhazia and South Ossetia, which Russia recognized as independent on August 26, 2008, following its military intervention in Georgia, primarily circulate the Russian ruble (RUB) as their de facto currency to enable seamless economic ties with Russia, their principal patron and source of subsidies exceeding 70% of their budgets in recent years.[71] Although Abkhazia maintains the Abkhazian apsar as an official unit with limited-issue coins and commemorative banknotesâ€”such as the 50-apsar "Dolphin" note released in June 2025â€”the apsar lacks widespread circulation and convertibility, rendering the RUB dominant for daily transactions and trade.[72][73] Transnistria, a breakaway region of Moldova with no formal international recognition, issues the Transnistrian ruble (PRB) since its introduction on November 1, 2000, replacing the Moldovan leu to assert autonomy amid ongoing tensions.[74] The PRB operates in isolation, non-convertible beyond Transnistria's borders and subject to high inflation volatility, with its value informally tracked against the Russian ruble due to predominant Russian cultural influence, remittances, and energy supplies from Russia.[75] This peg-like alignment, without formal convertibility, underscores Transnistria's economic dependence on Moscow, where RUB acceptance supplements PRB in cross-border exchanges.[76] The Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (TRNC), declared independent in 1983 and recognized exclusively by Turkey, employs the Turkish lira (TRY) as its sole legal tender, a policy formalized to align monetary policy with Ankara and mitigate isolation from the internationally recognized Republic of Cyprus, which uses the euro.[77] This usage facilitates direct trade and remittances from Turkey, which provides over 30% of the TRNC's GDP through subsidies, while euros and British pounds circulate informally in tourism sectors due to the island's divided history.[78]

Other Disputed Territories

In the disputed region of Nagorno-Karabakh, also known as Artsakh, the Armenian dram (AMD) served as the de facto currency from the region's declaration of independence in 1991 until Azerbaijan's military offensive on September 19, 2023, which prompted the exodus of nearly the entire ethnic Armenian population and the dissolution of the Republic of Artsakh on January 1, 2024.[81] Under subsequent Azerbaijani administration, the Azerbaijani manat (AZN) has been imposed as the legal tender, reflecting the territory's reintegration into Azerbaijan's economic framework despite ongoing international non-recognition of the change in control.[81] The Donetsk People's Republic (DPR), a breakaway entity in eastern Ukraine amid the ongoing conflict since 2014, adopted the Russian ruble (RUB) as its primary currency in late 2015, transitioning fully to it as the sole legal tender by January 1, 2023, to align with Russian economic support and reduce reliance on the Ukrainian hryvnia (UAH).[82][83] Similarly, the Luhansk People's Republic (LPR) shifted to the Russian ruble de facto starting in September 2015, with official adoption formalized by March 1, 2017, and exclusive use enforced from 2023 onward, driven by economic integration with Russia amid severed ties to Ukrainian financial systems.[84][85][83] This ruble dominance underscores practical dependencies on Russian subsidies and trade, with minimal reported local pushback due to the absence of viable alternatives in war-affected areas. Following Russia's annexation of Crimea in March 2014, the Russian ruble was designated as an official currency alongside the Ukrainian hryvnia initially, but became the exclusive legal tender by January 1, 2016, earlier than originally planned, to facilitate administrative control and economic alignment with the Russian Federation.[86][87] Usage of the ruble has persisted de facto since the 2014 transition, supported by Russian banking infrastructure, though international sanctions limit broader recognition and complicate cross-border transactions.[88] In these territories, currency impositions have prioritized geopolitical consolidation over local preferences, with economic realitiesâ€”such as reliance on controlling powers for salaries, pensions, and importsâ€”ensuring widespread practical acceptance despite sovereignty disputes.
