2023 European Le Mans Series

The 2023 European Le Mans Series was the fourteenth season of this endurance sports car racing championship organized by the Automobile Club de l'Ouest, featuring prototype and grand tourer vehicles competing in four-hour races as a developmental pathway toward the 24 Hours of Le Mans.[1][2]
The season comprised six events across prominent European circuits: the 4 Hours of Barcelona in Spain, 4 Hours of Le Castellet in France, 4 Hours of Aragón in Spain, 4 Hours of Spa-Francorchamps in Belgium, 4 Hours of Algarve in Portugal, and the season finale 4 Hours of Portimão in Portugal.[1] Competition occurred in the LMP2 and LMP3 prototype classes, alongside LMGTE grand tourers, with LMP2 featuring a dedicated Pro/Am subcategory limited primarily to bronze-rated drivers.[3] Algarve Pro Racing clinched the LMP2 teams' championship with 113 points, driven by Kyffin Simpson, James Allen, and Alex Lynn in the No. 25 Oreca 07, earning an automatic invitation to the 2024 24 Hours of Le Mans.[4] In LMGTE, Proton Competition secured the title for the fourth time with Ryan Hardwick, Zach Robichon, and Alessio Picariello in the No. 16 Porsche 911 RSR-19, while AF Corse won LMP2 Pro/Am honors through François Perrodo, Matthieu Vaxiviere, and Ben Barnicoat.[4][5] The series emphasized reliability and strategic pit management in variable weather conditions, particularly during the rain-affected Portimão finale won by United Autosports USA.[4]

Overview and Background

Season Format and Objectives

The 2023 European Le Mans Series season consisted of six 4-hour endurance races contested across prominent European circuits, commencing on 23 April at Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya in Spain and concluding on 22 October at Algarve International Circuit in Portugal.[6] The rounds included stops at Imola, Le Castellet, MotorLand Aragón—featuring a novel night race format—and Spa-Francorchamps, emphasizing sustained high-speed competition under varying conditions including daylight and darkness.[6] Each event followed a standard weekend structure with multiple free practice sessions, class-specific qualifying, and the headline race, designed to test driver stamina, team strategy, and vehicle reliability over fixed durations.[7] Points were allocated to competitors based on finishing positions within their respective classes, supplemented by bonuses for pole positions in qualifying, accumulating toward seasonal totals.[1] The series supported three core categories: LMP2 for professional prototype entries, LMP3 for semi-professional prototypes, and LMGTE for grand touring machines, with separate scoring to accommodate diverse team compositions and driver ratings.[3] The primary objectives centered on crowning Drivers' and Teams' Champions in each class via the highest point aggregates, fostering technical innovation and talent progression within the endurance racing ecosystem.[1] By integrating established venues with format innovations like the Aragón night event, the season aimed to elevate competitive intensity while serving as a developmental platform toward elite events such as the 24 Hours of Le Mans, prioritizing empirical performance metrics over broader promotional narratives.[6]

Regulatory and Class Changes

For the 2023 season, the LMP2 Pro/Am subcategory was restructured as a distinct class separate from the standard LMP2 category, enabling independent championships, qualifying, and awards including automatic invitations to the 2024 24 Hours of Le Mans for its overall winner.[8][7] This change aimed to better accommodate professional-amateur lineups while maintaining class parity. Qualifying procedures were updated to include three 15-minute sessions—one each for LMP2, LMP2 Pro/Am, and LMP3/LMGTE—extended from the previous 10-minute format.[7][9] The LMP2 Pro/Am session was restricted exclusively to bronze-rated drivers to emphasize amateur involvement.[8] Technical regulations prohibited all tyre warming and temperature retention equipment, citing environmental considerations, with implementation aligned across ACO series.[9][7] LMP2 and LMP2 Pro/Am tyre allocations were limited to one dry and one wet specification per event, reduced from prior allowances of multiple sets.[8] Additionally, LMP2 power outputs were increased by an average of approximately 15 horsepower to reflect its status as the series' premier prototype class.[10] Safety procedures introduced a modified Full Course Yellow protocol, closing the pit lane entry upon issuance of the "Prepare for FCY" message while keeping the exit open, to enhance deployment efficiency and safety.[7][8] Le Mans entry invitations were revised without reliance on entry number formulas, granting them to the top two LMP2 finishers, the LMP2 Pro/Am winner, the LMP3 winner, and the LMGTE winner.[7]

Pre-Season Testing

The pre-season testing for the 2023 European Le Mans Series occurred on 19 and 20 April at the Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya in Montmeló, Spain, serving as the official collective test ahead of the season-opening 4 Hours of Barcelona on 23 April.[11] [12] The two-day event featured multiple sessions across LMP2, LMP3, and LMGTE classes, allowing the full entry list of 34 cars to complete shakedowns, setup optimizations, and endurance runs under varying conditions, including a bronze driver collective test on the first day.[13] In the LMP2 category, United Autosports USA's No. 25 Oreca 07-Gibson, driven by James Allen, Kyffin Simpson, and Alex Lynn, dominated both test sessions, with Allen posting the overall fastest lap of 1:29.922 in the morning session on 20 April, sweeping the top times and underscoring the team's strong preparation.[14] The session highlighted competitive pacing among prototypes, with Glickenhaus Racing's No. 43 hypercar—though not a standard LMP2 entry—posting a close second in one session at 1:30.012 via Rui Andrade, Oliver Caldwell, and Jonathan Aberdein, while Prema Racing's No. 63 rounded out the top three in LMP2-specific timings.[15] LMP3 testing saw consistent laps from entries like DKR Engineering's No. 7 Ligier JS P320-Nissan, which led class times in preliminary sessions, focusing on reliability for the season's pro-am and gentleman driver lineups, though no single lap record was set amid setup-focused runs.[16] In LMGTE, Proton Competition's No. 77 Porsche 911 RSR-19 topped the timesheets in the afternoon session on 19 April with a 1:44.512 lap by its drivers, emphasizing tire management and aero tweaks for the class's Ferrari, Porsche, and Aston Martin contenders.[17] No major incidents were reported, with the tests providing baseline data for the 18 LMP2, 10 LMP3, and 6 LMGTE entries confirmed for the grid.[18]

Race Schedule

The 2023 European Le Mans Series schedule comprised six four-hour endurance races held between April and October, spanning circuits in Spain, France, Belgium, and Portugal.[1] The calendar initially included a round at Imola in May, but it was canceled due to extensive flood damage to the circuit, prompting organizers to add a second race at Algarve International Circuit as a double-header finale to maintain the six-round format.[19] All events supported LMP2, LMP3, and LMGT3 classes, with the third round at Aragón featuring a night race starting in the evening.[12]

Circuit Characteristics and Additions

The 2023 European Le Mans Series incorporated a diverse set of circuits, each presenting unique challenges for LMP2, LMP3, and LMGTE machinery through variations in length, elevation, and corner types. Key among the additions was MotorLand Aragón, which debuted on the calendar for the third round held on August 26 as the series' inaugural night race commencing at 18:00 CEST. This venue replaced the prior Monza event and introduced a demanding layout with technical sectors, elevation shifts, and a combination of sweeping and tight corners suited to endurance prototypes.[6][22] Circuit de Barcelona-Catalunya, site of the season opener on April 23, adopted a revised Grand Prix configuration for 2023 that eliminated the final chicane, streamlining the track to 14 corners from the standard 16 and facilitating higher speeds and potential passing zones in the extended final straight. This modification aimed to better accommodate the overtaking demands of multi-class fields in four-hour races.[23] Spa-Francorchamps hosted its round on September 24 with ongoing safety upgrades, including an expanded run-off platform at Turn 17 accompanied by a new gravel trap and repositioned barriers, as well as the conversion of the Turn 16 tarmac escape area into gravel to mitigate high-speed impacts on the 7 km high-elevation circuit renowned for sectors like Eau Rouge. Circuit Paul Ricard at Le Castellet, raced on July 16, featured its signature extended Mistral straight exceeding five kilometers in effective high-speed testing, emphasizing fuel efficiency and thermal management for prototypes. The season finale at Algarve International Circuit (Portimão) on October 22 leveraged its undulating 4.65 km profile with steep climbs and descents, demanding precise traction control in the closing uphill sprint.[24]

Entries

LMP2 Class Entries

The LMP2 class featured 18 Oreca 07-Gibson prototypes across the 2023 season, marking the largest entry in series history for that category and comprising 7 professional entries alongside 11 in the Pro/Am subcategory, which mandates at least one Bronze-rated driver per regulations.[18][25] All teams utilized Goodyear tires, with the professional subcategory emphasizing full-professional lineups for outright contention while Pro/Am focused on amateur-inclusive efforts eligible for separate points.[26] Professional entries included established squads like United Autosports USA (#22, Marino Sato/Japan, Philip Hanson/UK, Oliver Jarvis/UK), Algarve Pro Racing (#25, defending LMP2 champions with James Allen/UK among the lineup), IDEC Sport (#28, Paul Lafargue/France, Paul Chatin/France, Laurents Hörr/Germany), Duqueine Team (#30, Nico Pino/Spain, René Binder/Austria, Neel Jani/Switzerland), Inter Europol Competition (#43, Roberto Faria/Brazil, Oliver Caldwell/UK, Josh Pierson/USA), Cool Racing (#47), and Panis Racing (#65, Joel Camathias/Switzerland provisional lineup adjustments noted).[26][27][25] The Pro/Am subcategory saw expansions, including United Autosports' second car (#23), Cool Racing doubling to two entries, DragonSpeed USA's return (#21, Henrik Hedman/Sweden, Juan Pablo Montoya Jr./USA, Sébastien Montoya/Colombia), Proton Competition's full-season Oreca addition (#77), Team WRT (#31, Jakub Smiechowski/Poland, Alex Costa/Portugal, Fabio Scherer/Brazil), AF Corse (#83), DKR Engineering (#4), Nielsen Racing (#35), RLR msport (#24), Vector Sport (#37), and others like TDS Racing's return effort.[25][28] Notable shifts involved Prema's withdrawal for FIA World Endurance Championship priorities, Muehlner Motorsport and BHK Racing's exits, contrasted by newcomers and returnees enhancing depth.[25]

LMP3 Class Entries

The LMP3 class fielded 12 full-season entries in the 2023 European Le Mans Series, down from 13 in 2022, with all prototypes adhering to the Acoustic Performance Regulation (APR) for spec-series standardization. Ten cars utilized the Ligier JS P320 chassis with Nissan VK50 engine, while two employed the Duqueine D08 with Nissan power, reflecting the class's emphasis on bronze- and silver-rated drivers for talent development. New teams entering included Team Virage, WTM by Rinaldi Racing, Racing Spirit of Léman, and Ultimate, offsetting exits by United Autosports and 360 Racing; Cool Racing and Inter Europol Competition each reduced to a single entry from two the prior year.[25][29][30] The entries comprised a mix of established European squads and international drivers, primarily from Europe, with representation from the Americas and elsewhere; driver lineups often rotated across rounds to balance endurance demands and eligibility under FIA bronze/silver rules.[29][31]

LMGTE Class Entries

The LMGTE Am class in the 2023 European Le Mans Series comprised 12 entries, marking the maximum allowed for the category and reflecting strong manufacturer participation from Ferrari, Porsche, and Aston Martin. The field included five Ferrari 488 GTE Evoluzione cars, four Porsche 911 RSR (991.2) vehicles, and three Aston Martin Vantage AMR entries, all adhering to FIA/ACO Balance of Performance regulations designed to equalize competition among GT3-derived prototypes.[18] Teams emphasized gentleman driver lineups, with Bronze-rated drivers required in at least one seat per ACO eligibility rules for the Am subclass.[18] Proton Competition entered three Porsche 911 RSRs, leveraging their prior championship experience to field a multi-car effort focused on consistency across the season's six rounds. AF Corse and Iron Lynx represented Ferrari with competitive lineups blending European and international talent, while TF Sport and JMW Motorsport handled Aston Martin duties, prioritizing reliability on varied circuits. Other notable teams included Kessel Racing, Spirit of Race, and GMB Motorsport, contributing to a diverse grid of 34 drivers from 15 nationalities.[26]

Key Performances and Incidents

In the LMP2 class, Algarve Pro Racing's #25 Oreca 07-Gibson, driven by Kyffin Simpson, James Allen, and Alex Lynn, delivered a consistent performance across the six-round season, securing the drivers' and teams' championships with a second-place finish in the rain-affected finale at Portimão on October 22, despite contact from Panis Racing's #65 entry late in the race.[4] [38] United Autosports USA provided strong competition, claiming multiple victories, including the Portimão round with their #22 Oreca ahead of Algarve Pro, but finished 13 points behind in the teams' standings due to inconsistent results elsewhere.[4] COOL Racing's #17 Ligier JS P320 dominated the LMP3 class, clinching the teams' title with one race remaining after a victory at Algarve on October 20, where they outperformed WTM by Rinaldi Racing's #12 Duqueine by a narrow margin.[39] [37] In LMGTE, Proton Competition's #16 Porsche 911 RSR-19, piloted by Ryan Hardwick, Zach Robichon, and Matteo Cairoli, captured their fourth series crown through reliable finishes, including a class win at Portimão, capitalizing on rivals' mechanical issues and strategic missteps.[5] [40] The 4 Hours of Spa-Francorchamps on September 24 stood out for its high incident rate, with five safety car periods triggered by multiple off-track excursions, including Inter Europol Competition's #43 Oreca driven by Jonathan Aberdein spinning out with 18 minutes remaining and Racing Spirit of Léman's #31 Ligier colliding with United Autosports' entry.[34] Algarve Pro Racing navigated the chaos to victory in LMP2, extending their championship lead. At Le Castellet on July 16, Proton Competition's #77 Porsche endured repeated contact involving driver Mathieu Jaminet, yet recovered to score points, highlighting the class's competitive intensity.[41]

Championship Standings

Drivers' Standings

In the 2023 European Le Mans Series, drivers accumulated points based on class finishing positions across six rounds, with 25 points for a class win, decreasing incrementally to 1 point for tenth place, plus 1 point each for pole position and fastest lap per class. Standings were calculated individually per driver within their class (LMP2, LMP3, LMGTE), with ties broken by best results. An overall drivers' trophy was awarded to the top LMP2 scorer.

LMP2

Algarve Pro Racing's #25 Oreca 07-Gibson secured the LMP2 drivers' championship through consistent podium finishes, culminating in a second-place result at the season finale in Portimão on October 22, earning James Allen and Alex Lynn the title with 113 points each.[32][4] Their teammate Kyffin Simpson supported the effort but scored fewer points due to variable stints. United Autosports USA's #22 entry, with wins at Algarve and Portimão, placed Marino Sato and Phil Hanson second on 100 points.[32][42]

LMP3

Cool Racing's #17 Ligier JS P320 clinched the LMP3 drivers' title at Portimão with a fourth-place finish, where Adrien Chila, Marcos Siebert, and Alejandro García amassed sufficient points through multiple victories and podiums, including wins at Barcelona and Le Castellet.[39][37] Siebert led individual scoring in the class, benefiting from strong qualifying and race pace in the Ligier chassis.[43]

LMGTE

Proton Competition's #16 Porsche 911 RSR-19 won the LMGTE drivers' championship in the series' final season for the category, with Zacharie Robichon, Alessio Picariello, and Ryan Hardwick securing the title via a victory at the Portimão finale, following consistent top finishes against Ferrari and Aston Martin opposition.[38][5] This marked Proton's fourth LMGTE drivers' success, highlighting the Porsche's reliability and the drivers' strategic endurance.[5]

Teams' Standings

In the 2023 European Le Mans Series, teams competed for separate championships in the LMP2, LMP2 Pro-Am, LMP3, and LMGTE classes, with points awarded based on finishing positions in each of the six rounds: Barcelona (March 18), Le Castellet (July 16), Aragón (July 30), Spa-Francorchamps (September 3), Algarve (October 8), and Portimão (October 15).[44][45]

Records and Impact

Statistical Achievements

The 2023 European Le Mans Series season comprised six 4-hour endurance races held across European circuits, involving a total of 138 drivers competing in the LMP2, LMP3, and LMGTE classes.[46] The series achieved full grid capacity in its entry list, marking the second consecutive year of maximum participation, with 12 entries in the LMGTE class featuring five Ferrari 488 GTE Evo, four Porsche 911 RSR-19, and three Aston Martin Vantage AMR vehicles.[47] This saturation reflected strong manufacturer and team interest, contributing to competitive fields in professional and amateur sub-categories within LMP2. New lap records were established at the season-opening 4 Hours of Barcelona due to a revised track layout reducing corners from 16 to 14 and shortening the circuit to 4.657 km. In LMP2, Vladimir Lomko of the No. 47 COOL Racing Oreca 07-Gibson set the class lap record at 1:31.451 (183.3 km/h).[48] Reshad de Gerus secured pole position for LMP2 with a 1:29.396 lap time, while Salih Yoluc took LMP2 Pro/Am honors at 1:30 in qualifying. In LMGTE, pole positions highlighted emerging performances, such as Ryan Hardwick's first series pole for Proton Competition at Spa-Francorchamps, lapping over six tenths faster than rivals.[5] RLR MSport achieved a standout pole and dual sixth-place finishes at the Algarve finale with drivers Horst Felbermayr Jr., Mateusz Kaprzyk, and Gael Julien.[49]

Influence on Broader Endurance Racing

The 2023 European Le Mans Series (ELMS) reinforced its role as a primary development pathway for drivers and teams aspiring to compete in the FIA World Endurance Championship (WEC) and the 24 Hours of Le Mans, with the season's entry list featuring established alumni who carried ELMS-honed skills to global events. The 2023 WEC grid included 12 current or former ELMS drivers' champions across its 38-car field, alongside 6 teams that had previously secured ELMS titles, underscoring the series' direct contribution to elite-level talent pools.[50] This progression was facilitated by regulatory alignment, such as ELMS awarding automatic invitations to the subsequent Le Mans based on top LMP2 and LMGTE finishes, enabling seamless advancement for high performers from the season's six-round calendar.[51] In the LMP3 category, the 2023 ELMS maintained robust grids that exemplified the class's efficacy in nurturing entry-level prototype racing expertise, with spec chassis and engines reducing costs and attracting diverse entrants for endurance-specific training. Fields approaching 40 LMP3 cars per event highlighted sustained demand, providing a controlled environment for rookies to master multi-class dynamics, pit strategies, and reliability demands before scaling to LMP2 or WEC LMP2 equivalents.[52] This model's success influenced parallel series, as evidenced by subsequent enhancements in IMSA's LMP3 program, which adopted longer-race formats inspired by ELMS-style development needs to better prepare competitors for professional endurance campaigns.[53] Regulatory adjustments in 2023, including a approximately 15% power increase for LMP2 prototypes in ELMS to better align with WEC specifications, ensured competitive parity and technological relevance, allowing teams to test evolutions directly applicable to Le Mans Hypercar feeder roles without major reconfiguration.[52] The LMGTE Am class, in its final pre-GT3 transition year, supplied GT drivers versed in amateur-pro pairings, bridging to WEC's outgoing GTE era and informing the shift toward standardized LMGT3 platforms across ACO-sanctioned events. Overall, these elements positioned the 2023 ELMS as a cost-effective proving ground that bolstered the endurance ecosystem's depth, prioritizing empirical progression over expansive manufacturer involvement.
