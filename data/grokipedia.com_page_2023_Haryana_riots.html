2023 Haryana riots

The 2023 Haryana riots, also known as the Nuh violence, were a spate of communal clashes in India's Haryana state that ignited on 31 July 2023 in the Muslim-majority Nuh district when stone-pelting mobs, primarily local residents, attacked a permitted Hindu religious procession organized by the Vishwa Hindu Parishad (VHP) for the Brij Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra, leading to immediate counter-violence, six deaths—including two home guards and a cleric—over 70 injuries, and the arson of vehicles, shops, and a mosque.[1][2][3] The unrest rapidly spread to nearby Gurugram and Sohna, where retaliatory mobs targeted Muslim properties, prompting curfews, internet shutdowns, and the deployment of thousands of security personnel under the BJP-led state government.[4][5] Underlying tensions in Nuh, formerly Mewat—a region with approximately 79% Muslim demographics, elevated crime rates including cow vigilantism disputes, and sporadic inter-community frictions—fueled the escalation, with pre-event social media provocations from figures like cow protection activist Monu Manesar cited by some as inflammatory, though he was absent from the site.[6][7] Eyewitness accounts and video evidence indicate the procession, cleared by authorities, faced organized opposition including projectiles from rooftops, prompting defensive responses that spiraled into broader rioting.[8][9] Authorities registered 55 FIRs, arrested 141 suspects—predominantly from the Muslim community—and invoked anti-terror laws like UAPA in key cases involving attacks on police and infrastructure, while bulldozers razed illegal constructions associated with perpetrators, a measure defended as enforcement against encroachments but criticized in some reports as selective retribution amid claims of police complicity or excess.[5][10] The events highlighted persistent challenges in managing religious processions through demographically sensitive zones, with divergent narratives from fact-finding teams underscoring interpretive biases in media coverage that often downplay initial aggressions against Hindu participants.[11][12]

Background

Demographic and Historical Context

Nuh district, formerly known as Mewat, exhibits a stark demographic contrast to the rest of Haryana state. As per the 2011 Census, Haryana's population stood at approximately 25.35 million, with Hindus comprising 87.46% and Muslims 7.03%.[13] In Nuh, however, the total population was 1,089,263, with Muslims forming 79% (862,647 individuals), predominantly the Meo ethnic group, while Hindus accounted for the remaining 20-21%.[14] This concentration means nearly half of Haryana's Muslim population resides in Nuh, a district spanning 1,507 square kilometers with a density of 729 persons per square kilometer, characterized by rural poverty and lower literacy rates compared to state averages.[15][16] The Mewat region's historical context traces to medieval times, when Meo tribes—descended from Rajput clans—converted to Islam between the 11th and 17th centuries, establishing semi-autonomous principalities amid Delhi Sultanate and Mughal influences. Meos retained syncretic practices, bearing Hindu names and customs alongside Islamic faith, fostering relative coexistence with Hindu neighbors through shared agrarian lifestyles and resistance against external rulers, including joint participation in the 1857 revolt against British colonial forces.[17] This harmony persisted into the early 20th century, despite periodic raids by Meo groups on surrounding Hindu areas, until reformist movements like the Tablighi Jamaat—originating in Mewat in the 1920s—promoted orthodox Islam, eroding cultural overlaps and straining inter-community ties.[18] Post-Partition migrations in 1947 saw significant Meo exodus to Pakistan amid communal violence, followed by returns after Mahatma Gandhi's appeals for reintegration, yet underlying frictions emerged from demographic shifts driven by higher Muslim fertility rates and land disputes.[19] Isolated communal incidents, such as the 1932 riots triggered by a Hindu procession, highlighted vulnerabilities, but the region maintained a reputation for restraint until recent decades, when cow protection vigilantism and unauthorized constructions exacerbated perceptions of imbalance in a Hindu-majority state.[7] These factors, compounded by economic disparities—Nuh's poverty rate exceeding state norms—set the stage for episodic tensions without frequent large-scale riots prior to 2023.[20]

Precursors to the Violence

The primary precursor to the violence was the unresolved murder of Nasir Hussain and Junaid Khan, two Muslim men from Ghatwasan village near Nuh, whose charred bodies were discovered in a forest in Bhiwani district, Haryana, on February 16, 2023.[21] The Haryana Police investigation implicated Monu Manesar, a local cow vigilante and Bajrang Dal leader, along with several associates, in the abduction and killing, which stemmed from allegations of cow smuggling; Manesar denied involvement but faced an FIR under charges including murder and conspiracy.[22] This incident generated widespread outrage in Nuh's Muslim community, which comprises approximately 79% of the district's population, exacerbating distrust toward Hindu nationalist groups associated with cow protection vigilantism.[21] Tensions intensified in late July 2023 when Monu Manesar released a video on July 29 announcing his intention to join the Vishwa Hindu Parishad's (VHP) annual Braj Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra, a religious procession scheduled for July 31 to mark Lord Krishna's birth anniversary by offering water to temples in Nuh.[23] Local Muslim leaders and residents demanded that authorities bar Manesar from participating, citing his accused status and the potential for provocation, while VHP organizers insisted on the event's traditional route through Muslim-majority areas, claiming prior years had passed peacefully with police protection.[24] Haryana Police intelligence reports indicated risks of clashes, including threats of disruption, but the district administration permitted the yatra with enhanced security deployment of over 1,000 personnel.[21] Subsequent police chargesheets in Nuh violence cases attributed the mob's assault on the procession to pent-up anger over perceived police inaction against Manesar in the Nasir-Junaid case, with arrested individuals stating the attack was retaliation for the unresolved murders.[22] This backdrop of vigilantism-related grievances, combined with the symbolic assertion of Hindu religious rights in a demographically sensitive area, created a volatile atmosphere where warnings from both communities about potential violence went unheeded, setting the stage for the clashes on July 31.[21]

Outbreak and Course of Events

Initial Clashes in Nuh District

The initial clashes in Nuh district, Haryana, commenced on July 31, 2023, during the annual Brij Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra, a Hindu religious procession led by the Vishva Hindu Parishad (VHP) and Bajrang Dal affiliates, involving thousands of devotees carrying holy water from the Yamuna River to temples such as Nalhar Mahadev. The permitted route traversed Muslim-majority localities in Nuh, a district with a history of communal tensions but where the event had occurred peacefully in prior years.[4][25] Violence ignited around noon near Nuh Chowk when a group of teenagers pelted stones at vehicles in the convoy bearing "Gorakshak" (cow protector) markings, amid circulating rumors that Bajrang Dal figure Monu Manesar—sought in connection with the February 2023 murders of two Muslim men—was among the participants. A larger mob, reportedly comprising individuals from adjacent villages, rapidly assembled, escalating the assault with stone-throwing, petrol bombs, and arson on over a dozen vehicles, including motorcycles and cars used by devotees and police.[4][26][27] Confrontations intensified as procession participants defended themselves, with the mob surrounding and besieging groups of devotees, including women and children, inside the Nalhar Mahadev Temple for roughly five hours; attempts to vandalize the temple were repelled. Police teams providing security faced direct assaults, including at Khedla Mod, where rioters blocked paths and targeted officers with stones and firearms. En route reinforcements from Gurugram were ambushed near Tauru, resulting in the fatal shooting of two Haryana home guards—Gursewak and Neeraj—and injuries to at least ten personnel, with First Information Reports (FIRs) documenting mobs chanting "Allahu Akbar" and "Pakistan Zindabad" while firing upon law enforcement.[4][26][27] Security forces responded with lathi charges, tear gas, and controlled firing to disperse the aggressors, eventually rescuing the trapped pilgrims with aid from reinforcements across four districts; one additional death occurred in Nuh amid the unrest, bringing the district's initial toll to three. Authorities swiftly enacted Section 144 prohibitory orders across Nuh and neighboring areas, suspended mobile internet for 72 hours to prevent rumor-mongering, and closed educational institutions the following day. Multiple FIRs registered by Nuh police under sections for rioting, arson, and attempted murder highlighted the premeditated nature of the attacks on the procession and responders, with over 200 arrests following in the immediate phase.[4][26][25]

Escalation and Spread to Gurugram

The violence that erupted in Nuh district on July 31, 2023, during a Vishva Hindu Parishad (VHP) procession rapidly escalated and spilled over into adjacent Gurugram district by the evening of August 1. Retaliatory mobs, primarily comprising Hindus angered by the attacks on the procession in Nuh—which had resulted in the deaths of two home guards and injuries to dozens—targeted Muslim-owned properties along the Nuh-Gurugram border and in urban sectors of Gurugram, including Sohna and Manesar. Vehicles were set ablaze on the Gurugram-Sohna highway, and shops belonging to Muslim traders were vandalized and looted, with reports of over 200 individuals involved in some assaults.[28][29] A pivotal incident occurred late on August 1 in Gurugram's Sector 57, where a mosque was torched by a mob, leading to the fatal stabbing of the mosque's cleric, Abdul Wahab, who was attacked while attempting to protect the structure. This event intensified tensions, prompting the deployment of additional police forces and the imposition of an internet shutdown in affected areas to curb the spread of inflammatory content. Clashes persisted into August 2, with sporadic arson and stone-pelting in Muslim-dominated slums and commercial zones, exacerbating the displacement of hundreds from informal settlements.[30][31][32] The spread to Gurugram, a major economic hub with a significant migrant workforce, highlighted underlying communal frictions in mixed urban settings, where initial provocations in rural Nuh triggered chain reactions despite heavy security presence. Police data later indicated that the Gurugram incidents involved coordinated retaliation rather than spontaneous unrest, with 55 FIRs filed and over 140 arrests by early August, predominantly from both communities involved in the escalation.[33][34]

Timeline of Key Incidents

  • July 31, 2023: Communal clashes erupted around noon during the Vishva Hindu Parishad's Brij Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra procession in Nuh district, triggered by stone-pelting from a mob amid rumors of Bajrang Dal member Monu Manesar's participation; vehicles were set ablaze, and participants sought shelter in the Nalhar Mahadev temple complex for several hours until rescued by police reinforcements.[4][35] Two home guards, Gursewak and Neeraj, along with one civilian, were killed in the initial violence, with ten police personnel injured.[4][25] Incidents spread to Sohna, where mobs attacked Muslim areas, firing shots and hurling explosives at shops and a mosque.[12]
  • August 1, 2023: Violence extended to Gurugram district, where the Anjuman Jama Masjid in Sector 57 was vandalized around midnight, resulting in the killing of deputy imam Mohammed Saad; additional attacks targeted shanties in Sector 70, shops in Badshahpur, and five godowns along with two meat shops.[35][12] Authorities imposed curfews in Nuh and parts of Gurugram, suspended mobile internet services in Nuh, Gurugram, and Faridabad, and closed educational institutions; over 80 arrests were made as the death toll reached five.[25][35]
  • August 2, 2023: Bajrang Dal member Pradeep Sharma succumbed to injuries sustained the previous day near Sohna, raising the death toll to six; miscreants attempted to set two mosques ablaze in Tauru town, Nuh district, despite the curfew, though no further injuries were reported.[35][25] By this date, 116 individuals had been arrested, and 41 FIRs registered across affected areas.[35]
  • August 3–8, 2023: Sporadic tensions persisted with no major new clashes reported, as security forces maintained heightened deployment; mobile internet restrictions were partially lifted for specific hours to allow examinations, while investigations into the violence continued under a special committee formed by the Haryana government.[25][35]

Casualties and Damage

Human Toll

The riots resulted in a total of six deaths, according to official reports from Haryana authorities.[2][5] These fatalities included two police home guards who were killed while attempting to control the mob during the initial attack on the Hindu procession in Nuh on July 31, 2023.[32] The remaining four victims were civilians, with three occurring in Nuh amid stone-pelting and arson targeting Hindu participants and vehicles, and one in Gurugram during subsequent retaliatory violence on August 1, 2023, where a Muslim cleric was stabbed to death by a mob.[36][30] Injuries numbered 88 in total, encompassing civilians and security personnel treated at local hospitals for wounds from stones, sharp weapons, and burns.[37][5] Over 20 police officers sustained injuries, primarily from stone-throwing during efforts to disperse crowds in Nuh and Gurugram.[38] No widespread reports of long-term displacement emerged, though affected families in riot-hit areas like Nuh and Gurugram reported immediate hardships including medical costs and loss of livelihood.[32]

Property Destruction and Economic Impact

During the clashes in Nuh district on July 31, 2023, rioters set fire to numerous vehicles participating in a Hindu procession, including cars and motorcycles, while also targeting shops and structures in the vicinity.[11] The violence extended to Gurugram, where mobs vandalized and looted shops in Muslim-majority areas such as Sector 57, torching commercial establishments and private vehicles, leaving remnants of burnt cars and debris across affected sites.[32] In one documented case, an oil mill owned by a BJP leader in Nuh was completely destroyed by fire, resulting in the loss of products and equipment valued at over ₹1.5 crore.[39] Property damage across Nuh and adjoining areas was estimated in crores of rupees, with individual losses including one resident's holdings valued at ₹18-20 lakhs and another shopkeeper reporting ₹1 lakh in damaged supplies amid arson.[40][41] Haryana Chief Minister Manohar Lal Khattar stated that perpetrators would be held financially accountable for the destruction, invoking state legislation to recover costs from those convicted.[42] The government subsequently allocated ₹2.5 crore as an initial fund to compensate verified property losses, though recovery from rioters was anticipated to be protracted.[43] The economic fallout included temporary shutdowns of businesses in affected zones, disrupting local commerce in Nuh and Gurugram, a key industrial hub.[44] Following the violence, Hindu nationalist groups issued calls for boycotts of Muslim-owned enterprises in Haryana, exacerbating livelihood pressures on minority communities and prompting hundreds to flee their homes.[45] These measures, combined with the immediate physical damages, posed risks to investor confidence in the region, though no comprehensive aggregate economic loss figure was officially quantified beyond the cited allocations and isolated valuations.[46]

Allegations of Premeditation

Evidence from Investigations

Haryana Police investigations concluded that the violence during the August 1, 2023, clashes in Nuh district was premeditated, with evidence of coordinated planning to target the Vishva Hindu Parishad's Shobha Yatra procession. Officers reported that attackers had stockpiled weapons including country-made pistols, petrol bombs, and sharp-edged implements, which were deployed in a manner suggesting advance preparation rather than spontaneous reaction. [47] Forensic analysis and eyewitness accounts indicated rioters were prepositioned in elevated areas, such as hills near the Nalhar Mahadev Temple, allowing them to pelt stones and fire upon participants from vantage points before the procession fully entered the district. Police first information reports (FIRs) documented over 55 cases, many citing organized groups of 300-400 individuals armed and mobilized to block and assault the yatra route.[3] [5] By January 2024, investigations led to the invocation of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) in four FIRs against key accused, based on intelligence linking the violence to broader networks allegedly inspired by Islamist ideologies, including references to Al-Qaeda propaganda in recovered materials and communications. Haryana officials, including Home Minister Anil Vij, described the events as part of a "conspiracy" orchestrated by anti-social elements, supported by patterns of synchronized attacks across Nuh, Gurugram, and adjacent areas.[48] [49] While some civil society reports contested the depth of forensic linkages and alleged reliance on unverified intelligence in UAPA applications, official probes emphasized empirical indicators like weapon caches and tactical positioning as corroborating premeditation over incidental escalation. Over 141 arrests by early August 2023 yielded confessions and material recoveries reinforcing the narrative of intent to incite widespread disruption.[50] [5]

Role of Key Individuals and Groups

The Vishva Hindu Parishad (VHP) and its affiliate Bajrang Dal organized the Brij Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra, an annual Hindu religious procession scheduled for July 31, 2023, in Nuh district, which served as the immediate flashpoint for the violence when participants were reportedly attacked with stones, petrol bombs, and firearms from surrounding areas.[51][3] VHP leaders, including those coordinating the event, maintained that the procession followed established routes and complied with permissions, though critics alleged its timing and route through a Muslim-majority area exacerbated pre-existing tensions from prior cow vigilantism incidents.[52] No VHP or Bajrang Dal members were charged with initiating the clashes, but the groups' involvement in the yatra drew scrutiny for potentially provoking a response amid ongoing communal friction.[53] Monu Manesar, a prominent cow vigilante associated with Bajrang Dal, faced accusations of inciting the riots through a video posted on July 29, 2023, where he challenged local Muslims over alleged cow smuggling and referenced prior lynchings of Nasir Hussain and Junaid Khan, whom his group was suspected of killing in February 2023.[23][54] Haryana police issued a lookout notice and arrested him on September 12, 2023, for allegedly promoting enmity, though he denied direct involvement in the violence and claimed the video was not a call to action; investigations linked the riots partly to retaliatory motives over those deaths, per a December 2023 chargesheet.[6][21] Bajrang Dal leader Neeraj Vats was also noted for a post-violence Facebook live session urging retaliation against Muslims in Gurugram, contributing to escalation there.[55] Haryana police investigations attributed the initial assault's premeditation primarily to Muslim groups, citing evidence of coordinated positioning in Aravalli Hills, pre-gathered stones, and circulating threat videos like "Do whatever you can, we are coming" days prior to July 31.[56][57] Key arrested individuals included Munsaid and Saikul, nabbed after an August 10, 2023, encounter for roles in stone-pelting and arson; Congress MLA Maman Khan, charged under UAPA for allegedly funding and inciting rioters via provocative speeches; and over 150 others mostly from the Muslim community, with chargesheets detailing organized attacks killing two home guards and a VHP member.[58][59][10] Home Minister Anil Vij described the violence as a "mastermind's plan" to disrupt harmony, supported by forensic evidence of planned ambushes, though some arrests faced criticism for weak evidence in UAPA cases.[60][48]

Immediate Response

Police and Security Measures

Following the eruption of violence on July 31, 2023, during the Brajmandal Yatra in Nuh district, Haryana Police personnel encountered direct assaults from rioters, who pelted stones, torched vehicles, and fired at security forces, leading to the deaths of two home guards and injuries to seven officers. In immediate retaliation to these attacks, police opened fire to disperse aggressive mobs and defend positions, amid reports of rioters' intent to target law enforcement.[61][62] By August 1, 2023, authorities enacted stringent containment measures, imposing an indefinite curfew in Nuh town to restrict movement and suspending mobile internet services across affected districts to curb the dissemination of inflammatory material via social media. These steps aimed to de-escalate tensions as clashes spread to adjacent areas like Gurugram, where additional violence targeted properties and religious sites.[63][64][1] Security reinforcements were rapidly mobilized, with Chief Minister Manohar Lal Khattar requesting extra central assistance; this resulted in the deployment of approximately 30 companies of Haryana Police alongside 20 companies of Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF), totaling thousands of personnel across Nuh, Gurugram, and surrounding regions. Paramilitary units conducted flag marches to assert presence and deter further unrest, while entry points to sensitive zones were fortified with checkpoints. Initial responding officers, however, were noted to lack adequate anti-riot gear such as tear gas or water cannons, contributing to challenges in early containment efforts before reinforcements arrived.[65][66] On August 2, 2023, the Supreme Court of India intervened, directing both state and central governments to enhance security deployments and suppress hate speech to safeguard against recurrence, underscoring judicial oversight amid the six reported fatalities, including two policemen. Curfew relaxations were selectively permitted from August 7 onward for essential activities, signaling a phased return to normalcy under continued vigilance.[67][68]

Bulldozer Actions and Demolitions

Following the violence that erupted on July 31, 2023, during a Vishva Hindu Parishad procession in Nuh district, the Haryana government initiated a large-scale anti-encroachment drive using bulldozers to demolish illegal structures, beginning around August 2, 2023. Officials stated the action targeted unauthorized constructions on government land, with prior notices issued to owners, and was accelerated in response to the riots to clear pathways and remove properties linked to accused individuals. In Tauru, approximately 20 km from Nuh, over 250 shanties housing migrants—described by some reports as including Bangladeshi Rohingyas—were razed on August 4-5 for encroaching on state land.[69][70] The demolitions extended across Nuh district over several days, focusing on shops, hotels, and residential structures from which stones or attacks were allegedly launched during the clashes, including a hotel targeted on August 6. By August 5, an additional 60 structures—comprising 45 concrete buildings and 15 tin sheds—were demolished across 26 acres, as part of orders from Chief Minister Manohar Lal Khattar to enforce land laws. Overall, the five-day campaign razed at least 1,200 structures, including 162 permanent ones, predominantly affecting Muslim-owned properties in Muslim-majority areas, though the government emphasized compliance with encroachment surveys predating the riots.[71][72][73] Critics, including human rights groups and local residents, described the actions as "bulldozer justice" amounting to collective punishment without due judicial process, disproportionately impacting poorer Muslim communities and exacerbating communal tensions. The Punjab and Haryana High Court intervened on August 7, 2023, halting further demolitions and issuing notices to the state, questioning whether the drive constituted an "exercise of ethnic cleansing." Subsequent Supreme Court rulings in 2024 reinforced restrictions on such summary demolitions, requiring prior hearings, though the initial actions were defended by authorities as lawful enforcement against long-standing violations.[11][74][75]

Political Reactions

Statements from Ruling and Opposition Parties

Haryana Chief Minister Manohar Lal Khattar, representing the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), described the violence on August 1, 2023, as an unfortunate incident stemming from a well-planned attack on the Vishva Hindu Parishad (VHP) procession, characterizing it as part of a larger conspiracy.[76] He emphasized that investigations were ongoing and assured that no culprits would be spared, with the guilty held accountable for damages to life and property.[77] Khattar later blamed Congress MLA Mamman Khan for inciting the clashes, citing evidence of his role in provoking the violence.[78] BJP leaders, including state officials, echoed this by questioning Congress's involvement, framing the unrest as orchestrated by opposition elements amid a broader plot.[79] In contrast, opposition leaders from the Indian National Congress attributed the riots to administrative lapses by the BJP-Jannayak Janata Party (JJP) coalition government. Former Chief Minister Bhupinder Singh Hooda labeled the events an "administrative failure," arguing that even during the 1947 Partition, Haryana had avoided such communal strife, and demanded a judicial inquiry into the police's inaction.[80][81] He contended that the violence reflected a systemic collapse in law enforcement coordination, urging impartial probes over government-led investigations.[82] Congress general secretary Randeep Singh Surjewala criticized the ruling dispensation for failing to maintain law and order, noting that Haryana had witnessed no religious riots in its first 75 years post-Independence until the BJP's tenure.[83] Surjewala called the unrest a bid to polarize communities for electoral gains and appealed for peace and brotherhood among residents of Nuh and adjacent Gurugram districts.[82] The party collectively demanded a high-level probe, accusing the government of prioritizing political maneuvering over preventive measures.[84]

Accusations of Incitement

Cow vigilante Monu Manesar faced accusations of inciting the violence through a Facebook video posted on July 29, 2023, two days before the clashes, in which he urged Hindus to attend the Vishwa Hindu Parishad procession in large numbers, heightening tensions in the Muslim-majority Nuh district.[23][85] Police and local residents cited the video as a trigger, amid prior grievances over alleged lynchings linked to Manesar, such as the February 2023 deaths of Nasir Hussain and Junaid Khan.[23] He was arrested on September 12, 2023, on charges of incitement via inflammatory social media posts.[85] Congress MLA Mamman Khan was accused of inciting and coordinating the attacks, with police investigations revealing phone contact with prime accused Taufiq on July 29 and 30, 2023, as well as his presence at Badkali Chowk shortly before the violence erupted on July 31.[86][87] Khan's social media posts threatened to "crush" Manesar and dared him to enter Mewat, which authorities interpreted as provocative rhetoric guiding rioters, supported by confessional statements from arrested individuals.[86] He was arrested on September 14, 2023, and charged with instigating the unrest that resulted in six deaths.[87] Broader probes identified social media as a vector for incitement, with eleven individuals booked for posting content deemed to provoke violence, five of whom were arrested by early September 2023; threatening videos circulated in the days prior, including calls challenging opponents to act before the procession.[87][56] These accusations, drawn from police FIRs and technical surveillance, highlighted premeditated escalation from both Hindu and Muslim actors, though investigations emphasized coordination among rioters over isolated statements.[86]

Investigations and Legal Outcomes

Arrests and Charges

Following the violence on July 31, 2023, Haryana Police arrested 141 individuals in the initial days, with the number rising to 156 by August 7, 2023, in connection with rioting and related offenses in Nuh and surrounding areas.[5][37] By the conclusion of primary investigations, a total of 441 people had been arrested across 61 FIRs, primarily for offenses including rioting under IPC Section 147, unlawful assembly, arson, and attempt to murder.[88][89] Of these, 49 FIRs pertained specifically to rioting, while 11 involved charges for provocative speeches or social media posts inciting violence.[10] In four key cases linked to the clashes, authorities invoked provisions of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), adding stringent anti-terrorism charges such as membership in a terrorist organization or conspiracy to wage war against the state.[10][90] These UAPA sections were incorporated into three riot-related FIRs in January and February 2024, affecting 63 individuals, including Congress MLA Mamman Khan, accused of delivering inflammatory speeches prior to the violence.[89][91] Separately, Hindu activist Monu Manesar was arrested on September 12, 2023, on charges of inciting the riots through prior social media activity, though he denied involvement and claimed the accusations were politically motivated.[6] By July 2024, approximately 405 of the arrested individuals had been granted bail, with no final convictions reported in the riot cases as of August 2024, amid ongoing judicial proceedings.[10][88] Around 100 accused remained out on bail by early 2024, reflecting delays in trial resolutions despite the scale of arrests.[89]

Court Proceedings and UAPA Invocations

In January and February 2024, approximately six months after the July 31, 2023, violence in Nuh district, Haryana Police invoked sections 10 and 11 of the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) in four FIRs linked to the clashes, citing evidence of organized unlawful assemblies and membership in associations aimed at promoting enmity.[10][90] These sections, which prohibit membership in unlawful associations and support for such groups, were applied to approximately 80 accused across three specific riot-related cases, including charges of rioting, murder, and assault on public servants, as argued by police during bail opposition hearings.[92][93] The invocation extended to 63 individuals in total across the four cases, rendering bail applications more stringent, as UAPA provisions typically bar release for at least one year absent exceptional circumstances.[89] A prominent case involved Congress MLA Mamman Khan from Ferozpur Jhirka, arrested on September 14, 2023, for alleged incitement via inflammatory social media posts preceding the violence; UAPA charges were added against him in February 2024 under FIRs accusing him of conspiring to disrupt the Vishwa Hindu Parishad procession that triggered the clashes.[94][91] Khan's bail applications faced opposition in court, where police presented digital evidence of his posts urging resistance, though his counsel contested the charges as politically motivated; as of October 2024, he remained in custody pending further hearings.[59] By July 2024, one year post-violence, courts had granted bail to 405 of the over 1,000 arrested individuals across 61 FIRs, including some UAPA-tagged cases, based on assessments of insufficient prima facie evidence linking accused to terrorist acts under the Act's definitions.[10][95] However, ongoing proceedings in Nuh district courts involved challenges to UAPA applicability, with defense arguments highlighting delayed invocation and reliance on post-facto witness statements potentially extracted under duress, as noted in fact-finding reports by groups like the People's Union for Democratic Rights (PUDR), which urged quashing the sections for lacking proof of designated unlawful organizations.[96][97] Police countered in filings that the violence's scale—six deaths, over 100 injuries, and targeted attacks on Hindu processions—warranted UAPA to address coordinated communal mobilization, supported by CCTV footage and call data records.[92] No convictions under UAPA had been secured by late 2024, with trials protracted by voluminous evidence compilation across the 61 FIRs.[10]

Controversies and Debates

Claims of Media Bias

Critics, including analysts from right-leaning publications, accused mainstream and international media of downplaying the premeditated nature of the attacks on the Vishwa Hindu Parishad-led Braj Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra procession on July 31, 2023, in Nuh, Haryana, where a Muslim mob pelted stones from hillocks, set vehicles ablaze, and caused five deaths, including two home guards.[98][99] Instead, outlets such as BBC, NDTV, and The Wire framed the ensuing violence as spontaneous "clashes" triggered by the Hindu procession entering a Muslim-majority area or linked it to prior provocative social media posts by Hindu figures like cow vigilante Monu Manesar, omitting eyewitness accounts and videos of the initial one-sided assault on approximately 2,500 yatris who sought refuge in a temple.[98][99][52] Further claims highlighted selective narratives promoting communal harmony, such as reports by Times of India and ABP News emphasizing a Muslim family shielding Hindu police personnel, which were portrayed as countering the dominant mob violence without contextualizing the broader Islamist attacks documented in contemporaneous footage.[98] Coverage by CBS News, Reuters, and Deutsche Welle was criticized for implying the yatra's route through Nuh justified the response, while under-reporting a historical pattern of similar obstructions and assaults on Hindu processions in the Mewat region, including prior incidents in 2022 and earlier years.[99][52] These portrayals, according to detractors, reflected a tendency in certain media to prioritize minority victimhood over empirical sequences of events, as evidenced by police FIRs naming over 1,000 accused primarily from the local Muslim community for rioting and arson.[99] Post-riot reporting shifted focus to retaliatory actions, such as the August 2023 bulldozer demolitions of structures linked to rioters, with outlets like Hindustan Times and The Quint decrying them as collective punishment against Muslims while giving less prominence to the initial casualties among Hindus and security personnel.[99] Commentators argued this selective emphasis perpetuated a narrative of equivalence in a conflict initiated by aggression against a religious procession, potentially influenced by institutional biases favoring avoidance of communal culpability attributions.[52][98]

Interpretations of Causality and Responsibility

The violence in Nuh on July 31, 2023, was triggered by a mob attack on a permitted Hindu religious procession organized by the Vishwa Hindu Parishad (VHP), involving stone pelting, arson, and shooting that killed five participants, including two policemen.[11] [66] Haryana police investigations attributed primary responsibility for initiating the clashes to organized Muslim groups in the district, citing intelligence of prior mobilization and the use of country-made weapons, with over 1,000 arrests predominantly from the Muslim community by September 2023.[100] Interpretations diverge on premeditation and deeper causality. Pro-Hindu accounts, drawing from demographic analyses, link the outbreak to Nuh's skewed population—approximately 79% Muslim per 2018 NITI Aayog data and rising from 62% in 1971—fostering isolationist tendencies, illegal encroachments, and historical patterns of aggression against Hindu events, such as 1930s riots in Mewat.[7] These views emphasize radicalization in the Meo Muslim community, evidenced by cow smuggling networks and retaliatory killings like those of Junaid and Nasir in February 2023, which heightened vigilante responses from groups like Bajrang Dal.[7] Conversely, Muslim advocacy reports claim the yatra's route through sensitive areas was provocative, amplified by inflammatory social media from Hindu nationalists like Monu Manesar, whose videos incited participation and framed the event as assertive reclamation.[101] Responsibility attributions often reflect partisan lenses, with Haryana's BJP-led government and police holding local Muslim leaders accountable for failing to curb mob formation despite advance warnings, while opposition figures and fact-finding teams like PUDR accuse administrative lapses in security deployment—only 300 personnel for thousands of yatra participants—and post-event bias in arrests and demolitions.[96] [66] Empirical sequencing, however, supports the procession as the victim of the initial assault, undermining narratives of symmetric provocation; broader causal factors include unchecked economic stagnation in Nuh, where 41% literacy and high unemployment per 2011 census data exacerbate communal silos, rather than isolated ideological clashes.[102] No evidence substantiates coordinated Hindu instigation as the root, though vigilante excesses post-outbreak prolonged tensions in adjacent Gurugram.[44]

Critiques of Government Response

Opposition parties, particularly the Congress, accused the BJP-JJP government in Haryana of administrative and intelligence failures that allowed the violence to erupt on July 31, 2023, despite prior alerts about potential clashes during the Vishva Hindu Parishad procession in Nuh.[103] Senior Congress leader Randeep Singh Surjewala stated that the administration had neglected to deploy adequate forces or implement preventive measures, leading to a breakdown in law and order.[82] Critics, including former Chief Minister Bhupinder Singh Hooda, argued that the government's inaction exacerbated the spread of riots to Gurugram, resulting in six deaths and over 200 injuries.[104] The post-riot demolition drive, which razed over 1,200 structures—primarily in Muslim-majority areas—faced sharp rebukes for resembling collective punishment rather than targeted enforcement against illegal encroachments.[73] The Punjab and Haryana High Court, acting on suo motu cognizance on August 7, 2023, halted the operations and interrogated whether they amounted to an "exercise of ethnic cleansing," noting the lack of prior notices and hearings for affected residents.[105] Opposition figures and civil society groups contended that the actions disproportionately targeted one community without verifying involvement in the riots, violating principles of due process.[11] Calls intensified for an independent judicial inquiry into the government's overall handling, with Congress alleging deliberate avoidance to shield administrative lapses and biased enforcement.[106] Human Rights Watch linked the response to broader patterns of discriminatory policies that heightened communal tensions, though such assessments have been contested by state officials as overlooking the premeditated nature of the attacks on the procession.[107] These critiques, largely from political opponents and advocacy organizations, highlighted perceived selectivity in arrests and property actions, amid over 100 detentions under stringent charges.[108]

Aftermath and Long-term Impact

Communal Repercussions

The 2023 Nuh violence, which began on July 31 during a Hindu religious procession in the Muslim-majority Nuh district, triggered retaliatory attacks on Muslim properties in adjacent Gurugram, exacerbating communal divides. In Gurugram's Hindu-majority sectors, mobs vandalized over 100 Muslim-owned shops, homes, and a mosque, displacing dozens of families and causing an estimated ₹500 crore in property damage primarily to Muslim businesses.[32] This led to immediate economic hardship for Muslim laborers and traders, many of whom relied on inter-community trade, with reports of severed longstanding economic ties between Hindu and Muslim residents.[109] In the weeks following, Hindu organizations such as the Vishva Hindu Parishad and Bajrang Dal organized rallies calling for an economic boycott of Muslim vendors and laborers in Gurugram and other Haryana districts, framing it as a response to the initial attacks on the procession. Videos from these events showed participants chanting slogans and urging Hindus to avoid Muslim services, contributing to widespread fear among Muslim communities.[45][110] The Supreme Court of India condemned these calls as unacceptable hate speech on August 12, 2023, directing authorities to act against incitement, though enforcement remained inconsistent.[111][112] By late August, threatening posters appeared in Gurugram's Muslim slums, warning of further repercussions and heightening anxiety, particularly among women and children who reported feelings of isolation and insecurity. Over 500 Muslims were arrested in the aftermath, with allegations of custodial abuse and branding as terrorists despite contested evidence, fostering resentment and perceptions of targeted communal policing.[113][114][48] Longer-term effects included deepened mistrust, with Nuh's Muslim residents viewing the violence as part of broader patterns of impunity for Hindu nationalist groups, while some Hindu communities cited prior encroachments and processional disruptions as causal factors. By October 2023, surface-level normalcy returned in Nuh markets, but underlying tensions persisted, evidenced by ongoing hate speech incidents and the violence's role in 2023's tally of 32 communal riots nationwide.[115][116] No large-scale migration occurred, but economic boycotts reduced Muslim employment in construction and informal sectors, straining inter-community relations into 2024.[117]

Policy and Security Changes

In response to the 2023 Nuh riots, the Haryana government expedited the allocation of approximately 50 acres of land in Indri village, Nuh district, for a permanent battalion of the Rapid Action Force (RAF), a specialized anti-riot unit under the Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF).[118][119] This approval, granted on August 2, 2023, followed a five-year delay despite central sanction in 2018, aiming to station rapid-response forces permanently in the communally sensitive area to preempt and control future outbreaks of violence.[120][121] The move included plans for infrastructure development, with initial handovers and foundation ceremonies slated shortly after, though full operationalization required extended construction timelines.[122] Concurrently, the state shifted the headquarters of the 2nd India Reserve Battalion (IRB) to Nuh on August 3, 2023, relocating it from its prior site to bolster local law enforcement capacity and enable quicker deployment during tensions.[123] These enhancements supplemented temporary deployments of 20 Central Armed Police Force (CAPF) companies, including RAF units, immediately following the riots to restore order in Nuh and adjacent districts.[122] For subsequent high-risk communal events, such as the annual Braj Mandal Jalabhishek Yatra, the government adopted preventive measures including 24-hour suspensions of mobile internet, bulk SMS, and dongle services to curb misinformation and inflammatory content that could escalate violence, as implemented in July 2024 and July 2025.[124][125] These curbs, justified under public order concerns, were paired with heavy security deployments—comprising thousands of personnel, drone surveillance, and riot control drills—and ancillary restrictions like school closures and bans on non-vegetarian food sales during the events.[126][127] No broader legislative reforms, such as amendments to riot control protocols or intelligence-sharing mandates, were publicly announced as direct outcomes of the 2023 violence.
