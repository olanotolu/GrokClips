List of critics of the New Deal

The New Deal encompassed a broad array of federal programs, public works initiatives, regulatory reforms, and relief efforts launched by President Franklin D. Roosevelt from 1933 to 1939 to address the Great Depression's unemployment, banking crises, and industrial collapse.[1] Critics of these policies, drawn from economists, business executives, constitutional scholars, journalists, and politicians across ideological lines, contended that the New Deal's interventions—such as wage and price controls, cartel-like industry codes, and expansive welfare spending—distorted free-market signals, suppressed private investment, and extended the Depression by roughly seven years through reduced output and persistent high unemployment.[2][3] Others highlighted constitutional overreach, arguing that agencies like the National Recovery Administration exceeded Congress's commerce powers and eroded separation of powers, as evidenced by the Supreme Court's invalidation of multiple statutes in cases such as Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States.[4] Prominent opponents included the American Liberty League, founded by business leaders and Democrats like Al Smith to decry the programs as dictatorial and antithetical to limited government; journalists such as John T. Flynn, who exposed regulatory failures and fiscal profligacy; and conservative intellectuals who viewed the expansion of bureaucracy as a precursor to centralized planning akin to European models.[5] Even some initial New Deal supporters, like Huey Long and Father Charles Coughlin, turned critical, faulting insufficient redistribution or perceived favoritism toward Wall Street, though their critiques often amplified populist rather than free-market concerns.[6] These voices fueled political backlash, contributing to Republican gains in the 1938 midterms and Roosevelt's failed court-packing scheme, underscoring enduring debates over government intervention's efficacy and limits.[7] The roster of such critics reflects a spectrum of principled objections rooted in empirical observations of stalled recovery metrics—like GDP stagnation until World War II mobilization—and first-hand accounts of policy-induced rigidities in labor and production markets.[8]

Critics Deeming the New Deal Insufficiently Interventionist

Political Figures Advocating Radical Reforms

Huey Pierce Long Jr., a Democratic U.S. Senator from Louisiana serving from 1932 until his death, criticized the New Deal for preserving the existing wealth hierarchy rather than enforcing comprehensive redistribution from the affluent to the impoverished.[9] In a February 23, 1934, national radio broadcast, Long outlined his "Share Our Wealth" program, which demanded capping individual fortunes at $50 million and annual incomes at $1 million, with all excess confiscated through steeply graduated taxes to guarantee every American family a minimum annual income of $2,000 to $5,000, a homestead valued up to $5,000, free college education, and old-age pensions starting at age 60.[10] He argued that New Deal measures, such as the National Recovery Administration established in June 1933, amounted to "halfway measures" that subsidized large corporations while leaving 25% national unemployment unaddressed through direct asset seizure from the top 1% who controlled 24% of U.S. wealth in 1933.[11] By mid-1935, Long's Share Our Wealth Society reported 27,000 local clubs and 7.5 million enrolled members across 27 states, capitalizing on empirical shortfalls in New Deal relief like the short-lived Federal Transient Relief Program of 1933, which aided only 750,000 of an estimated 2 million transients without altering property distribution.[12] Long's rising influence, including threats to form a third party by September 1935, prompted Roosevelt's administration to accelerate social security proposals partly to neutralize the appeal, though Long dismissed these as inadequate without wealth caps.[13] His assassination on September 8, 1935, by Carl Weiss in the Louisiana State Capitol ended his direct challenge, but the program's emphasis on confiscatory taxation influenced later policy debates on inequality.[14] Upton Beall Sinclair Jr., a Democratic candidate for Governor of California in 1934, positioned his End Poverty in California (EPIC) campaign as a state-level escalation beyond federal New Deal initiatives, faulting them for relying on monetary relief and private enterprise incentives rather than public production control.[15] Launched in June 1933 amid California's 30% unemployment rate—higher than the national 25%—EPIC proposed acquiring idle land and factories via eminent domain to establish worker cooperatives producing essentials like food and clothing for direct distribution to the jobless, aiming to employ 200,000 of the state's 500,000 unemployed within state-run facilities funded by a 4% transaction tax on non-essential goods.[16] Sinclair contended that New Deal programs like the Agricultural Adjustment Act of May 1933, which paid farmers to reduce output, exacerbated scarcity without guaranteeing production-for-use, as evidenced by California's 1933 farm foreclosures numbering over 10,000 amid falling crop prices.[17] Securing the Democratic nomination on August 28, 1934, with 246,000 primary votes (53% of the total), Sinclair's platform drew 872,000 votes (44%) in the general election against Republican Frank Merriam, reflecting grassroots support from 1,500 EPIC chapters but ultimate defeat amid business-led opposition that spent $1.5 million in negative advertising.[18] Though EPIC lost, its advocacy for state seizure of productive assets prefigured critiques of New Deal market preservation, influencing local experiments like California's 1930s cooperative farms that sustained 5,000 residents by 1936.[19]

Intellectuals and Activists Pushing for Broader State Control

Mary van Kleeck, a social scientist and director of industrial studies at the Russell Sage Foundation from 1908 to 1948, emerged as a prominent socialist critic of the New Deal during the 1930s. She contended that initiatives like the National Recovery Administration (NRA), established in 1933, merely co-opted unions into state-supervised cartels dominated by corporate interests, failing to deliver genuine worker ownership or control over production.[20][21] Instead, van Kleeck advocated for "industrial democracy" through worker-managed councils and comprehensive economic planning modeled on Soviet structures, arguing that partial reforms perpetuated capitalist exploitation amid the Depression's mass unemployment, which exceeded 20% nationally by 1933.[22] Her critiques, voiced in writings and public statements, highlighted the New Deal's reliance on business self-regulation as a barrier to true socialization of industry.[23] Leaders of the Communist Party USA (CPUSA), including William Z. Foster, its longtime chairman and 1932 presidential candidate, faulted the New Deal for entrenching "social fascism"—a term derived from Comintern directives in the late 1920s equating social democratic reforms with fascist stabilization of capitalism.[24][25] Foster and CPUSA outlets, such as The Communist magazine, portrayed programs like the NRA and Agricultural Adjustment Act (1933) as corporatist schemes that allied the state with monopoly capital, delaying proletarian revolution and nationalization of key industries like banking and heavy manufacturing.[26] In July 1933, shortly after the New Deal's launch, the CPUSA explicitly denounced it as advancing toward fascism by preserving private property relations while offering superficial relief to workers facing wage cuts and evictions during the Depression.[24] The party's 1932 and 1936 platforms demanded immediate expropriation of productive assets without compensation, demands unmet by Roosevelt's policies, which CPUSA viewed as concessions to bourgeois interests rather than steps toward Soviet-style planning.[27] This stance aligned with Comintern's "Third Period" thesis (1928–1935), which prioritized exposing reformism as counterrevolutionary, though CPUSA membership grew from under 10,000 in 1930 to over 25,000 by 1934 amid economic distress.[25][28]

Critics Viewing the New Deal as Excessive Federal Overreach

Political Opponents Defending Limited Government

Former President Herbert Hoover emerged as a prominent critic of the New Deal, arguing in his 1934 book The Challenge to Liberty that its policies undermined voluntary cooperation among individuals and businesses, replacing it with coercive federal regimentation that risked totalitarian tendencies.[29] Hoover contended that programs like the National Industrial Recovery Act centralized economic planning in Washington, eroding federalism and individual liberty without resolving the Depression's root causes of overproduction and credit contraction.[30] Republican Senator Robert A. Taft of Ohio led congressional resistance to New Deal expansions, decrying them as abuses of executive power and threats to constitutional limits on federal authority. In floor debates and speeches during the 1940s, Taft linked New Deal precedents to postwar fiscal imbalances, warning that unchecked spending—exemplified by annual deficits exceeding $40 billion during World War II—had normalized permanent federal deficits and debt accumulation beyond $250 billion by 1946, distorting private investment and state sovereignty.[31] Taft co-sponsored the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 to counter what he viewed as the Wagner Act's (1935) favoritism toward unions, which he argued empowered labor monopolies at the expense of employer rights and market competition, further entrenching federal overreach into labor relations.[32] Southern Democratic senators, such as Harry F. Byrd of Virginia, opposed New Deal centralization on states' rights grounds, filibustering and voting against bills like the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938, which imposed national minimum wages and hours standards they saw as infringing on southern agricultural and low-wage economies. Byrd coordinated with conservative Republicans in the informal "conservative coalition" to block further interventions, emphasizing in 1930s Senate speeches that federal mandates eroded local control and fiscal discipline, as evidenced by his advocacy for balanced budgets amid New Deal expenditures totaling over $20 billion by 1939.[33] This stance reflected broader southern resistance to Washington dictating labor and economic policies traditionally reserved to states under the Tenth Amendment.[34]

Economists Highlighting Market Distortions

Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek, key figures in the Austrian school of economics, contended that New Deal programs like the National Recovery Administration (NRA) interfered with price mechanisms essential for economic coordination, artificially elevating wages and prices above market-clearing levels and thereby suppressing employment recovery.[35] Mises argued in his 1940 work Interventionism that partial interventions, such as NRA codes fixing prices and output restrictions, distorted resource allocation without achieving full planning, leading to cumulative inefficiencies and prolonged maladjustments from prior credit expansions.[36] Hayek extended this in pre-Road to Serfdom writings and lectures during the 1930s, highlighting the "knowledge problem" where government codes prevented decentralized price signals from conveying information on scarcity, resulting in misallocated capital that required liquidation to restore equilibrium.[37] Austrian theory posits that the Great Depression stemmed from 1920s malinvestments fueled by loose monetary policy, necessitating a recessionary purge of unviable projects— a process New Deal rigidities obstructed by propping up wages averaging 20-30% above competitive levels under NRA mandates.[38][39] Empirical analyses later substantiated these distortions' role in impeding recovery. Harold Cole and Lee Ohanian's 2004 general equilibrium model estimated that New Deal cartelization policies, including NRA-enforced wage hikes and industry collusion, raised real wages by approximately 25% relative to productivity, accounting for 60% of the output shortfall from 1933 to 1939 and prolonging unemployment from an expected 8% to observed levels near 17%.[40] Their calibration showed these interventions reduced hours worked and investment, contrasting with faster recoveries in countries without similar labor and price controls.[41] Regarding fiscal stimuli, econometric studies of 1930s data indicate government spending multipliers often fell below unity—around 0.5 to 1.0 at state levels—due to partial offsets from reduced private activity amid heightened uncertainty and tax distortions, undermining claims of robust Keynesian amplification.[42] Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz, in their 1963 A Monetary History of the United States, identified Federal Reserve contraction as the Depression's primary cause but noted how subsequent New Deal labor policies, including Wagner Act enhancements to union power, entrenched nominal rigidities that amplified monetary errors by hindering wage flexibility and sectoral reallocation.[43] These economists collectively emphasized that market distortions—via elevated factor costs and suppressed competition—delayed the creative destruction needed for sustained rebound, with quantitative evidence linking policies to extended stagnation rather than exogenous shocks alone.[44]

Writers, Commentators, and Public Intellectuals

H. L. Mencken, a prominent journalist and editor of The American Mercury, used his columns in the 1930s to satirize the New Deal's expansion of federal bureaucracy as an assault on individual liberty and common sense. He lambasted the proliferation of "alphabet soup" agencies, such as the National Recovery Administration (NRA), which issued over 550 industry codes regulating prices, wages, and production, arguing these measures created unnecessary red tape that hampered free enterprise rather than alleviating economic distress.[5] Mencken dismissed much of the New Deal rhetoric as "puerile" and likened its scope to a creeping dictatorship, emphasizing how such interventions distorted markets and rewarded inefficiency through pork-barrel spending.[45] Elizabeth Dilling, an activist and author, critiqued the New Deal in her self-published 1934 book The Red Network: A 'Who's Who' and Handbook of Radicalism for Patriots, where she documented purported overlaps between New Deal administrators and communist sympathizers or organizations. Dilling contended that programs like the NRA and Works Progress Administration enabled subversive influences to embed within federal agencies, eroding constitutional limits on government by channeling public funds toward ideologically driven initiatives that prioritized collectivism over personal responsibility.[46] Her work highlighted specific personnel connections, such as advisors linked to the Communist Party USA, framing the New Deal's growth as a vehicle for radical agendas that threatened American sovereignty and free institutions.[47] Father Charles E. Coughlin, known as the "radio priest," reached an estimated 30-40 million listeners weekly through his broadcasts from the Shrine of the Little Flower in Royal Oak, Michigan, initially endorsing the New Deal but turning sharply critical by late 1934. He denounced initiatives like the WPA as perpetuating a "dole" system that fostered dependency and masked a favoritism toward Wall Street bankers, despite the programs' surface-level populism, arguing they centralized excessive power in Washington at the expense of local autonomy and moral self-reliance.[48] By 1935, Coughlin's rhetoric portrayed the New Deal as a betrayal of true reform, amplifying concerns over bureaucratic overreach and fiscal irresponsibility in speeches that blended economic critique with warnings against elite capture of federal authority.[49] His shift culminated in opposition to Roosevelt's 1936 reelection, prioritizing isolationism and limited government against what he saw as the erosion of republican virtues.[50]

Organizational and Institutional Opposition

Business and Liberty-Focused Groups

The American Liberty League, established in August 1934 by a coalition of businessmen including Irénée du Pont and political figures such as former New York Governor Al Smith and Jouett Shouse, positioned itself as a defender of constitutional liberties against New Deal encroachments.[51][52] The organization denounced Franklin D. Roosevelt's programs, particularly the National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA), as a "Raw Deal" that centralized excessive power in the executive branch, violated the Constitution's contracts clause by abrogating private agreements, and imposed anti-competitive regulations through industry codes. Its 1935 platform explicitly opposed deficit spending, mandatory employer contributions to social programs, and federal interventions that it argued undermined property rights and free enterprise, advocating instead for balanced budgets and limited government to preserve economic freedom.[53] The National Association of Manufacturers (NAM), founded in 1895 to represent industrial interests, mounted sustained opposition to New Deal labor and regulatory policies, lobbying Congress against the NIRA's price-fixing codes and the Wagner Act's provisions for union organization and collective bargaining.[54] NAM argued that these measures distorted labor markets by mandating minimum wages and hours that increased costs without corresponding productivity gains, leading to documented business closures and employment stagnation in regulated sectors during the early 1930s.[55] The group supported legal challenges to the NIRA, contributing to the intellectual and amicus groundwork for cases like A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States (1935), in which the Supreme Court ruled the Act an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power beyond Congress's commerce authority, effectively dismantling the National Recovery Administration's framework.[56]

Conservative and Anti-Collectivist Associations

The Southern Agrarians, a collective of twelve intellectuals centered at Vanderbilt University, extended their pre-New Deal agrarian manifesto I'll Take My Stand (1930) into pointed critiques of federal interventions that accelerated industrialization in the South.[57] They opposed the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA), established by the Tennessee Valley Authority Act of 1933, as a mechanism of centralized planning that undermined local agrarian traditions and imposed materialistic progress at the expense of cultural continuity.[58] Donald Davidson, a leading Agrarian, specifically decried the TVA for propagating New Deal materialism, which he argued homogenized Southern communities and eroded independent farming lifestyles in favor of technocratic dependency.[58] The National Committee to Uphold Constitutional Government (NCUCG), founded in February 1937 by publisher Frank Gannett and others amid Roosevelt's court-packing proposal, emerged as a principal ideological bulwark against collectivist expansions of federal authority.[59] Emphasizing strict adherence to Article I's enumeration of congressional powers, the NCUCG distributed educational materials and mobilized grassroots petitions to highlight New Deal violations of fiscal and structural limits, framing programs like expansive spending initiatives as unconstitutional accretions of power. Its efforts intensified following the 1937-1938 recession, with critics within the group attributing the downturn—marked by a 10% GDP contraction and unemployment rising to 19%—to prior New Deal interventions such as monetary tightening and reduced deficit spending, thereby bolstering arguments for policy contraction and reinforcing the committee's anti-statist advocacy.[60][59]

Key Publications Articulating Anti-New Deal Arguments

Influential Books and Monographs

John T. Flynn's The Roosevelt Myth (1948) provided a detailed indictment of the New Deal's administrative inefficiencies and fiscal profligacy, asserting that programs like the Works Progress Administration and Agricultural Adjustment Administration squandered over $10 billion in taxpayer funds on duplicative and counterproductive initiatives, drawing on contemporaneous federal budget reports and congressional hearings to demonstrate how such spending stifled private recovery and extended the Depression into the 1940s.[61] Flynn, a former New Deal supporter turned critic, marshaled statistical evidence of agency overlaps—such as multiple overlapping relief bodies—and policy reversals, arguing these reflected ideological experimentation rather than empirical efficacy, ultimately portraying Franklin D. Roosevelt as a charismatic but economically illiterate leader whose interventions prioritized power consolidation over prosperity.[62] Garet Garrett's The People's Pottage (1953), compiling essays from the 1930s and 1940s, framed the New Deal as an internal "revolution" that inverted the American constitutional order, trading the Founding Fathers' emphasis on limited government and individual liberty for a welfare state apparatus that centralized authority in Washington, evidenced by the proliferation of alphabet agencies and executive orders bypassing legislative checks.[63] Garrett contended this shift represented not mere reform but a fundamental betrayal of republican principles, citing specific mechanisms like the National Recovery Administration's price-fixing codes as harbingers of bureaucratic tyranny, with historical parallels to imperial overreach that eroded self-reliance and fostered dependency.[64] Friedrich A. Hayek's The Road to Serfdom (1944), originally published in Britain but rapidly disseminated in the U.S. via a condensed Reader's Digest edition, warned that piecemeal economic planning—as exemplified by New Deal interventions like industrial cartels and wage controls—erodes democratic freedoms by necessitating coercive enforcement, leading inexorably to totalitarian coordination of society.[65] Hayek supported his thesis with logical deductions from first principles of knowledge dispersion in markets, alongside historical cases of wartime planning in Europe, and American readers interpreted chapters on "planning and the rule of law" as direct admonitions against precedents set by the New Deal's regulatory expansions, which fragmented property rights and invited arbitrary state discretion.[66] Murray Rothbard's America's Great Depression (1963) applied Austrian business-cycle theory to argue that New Deal policies, following Herbert Hoover's precursors, artificially propped up wages and prices while suppressing market clearing, thereby converting a monetary-induced contraction into a decade-long stagnation; Rothbard substantiated this with econometric reconstructions of industrial production data and banking statistics, critiquing measures like the National Industrial Recovery Act for cartelizing industries and delaying structural readjustments essential for recovery.

Essays and Pamphlets

Albert Jay Nock's essays in the 1930s, including those compiled in A Journal of Forgotten Days (published posthumously but reflecting his contemporaneous writings), portrayed New Deal programs as a mechanism of class warfare that privileged a bureaucratic elite over the "forgotten man"—the independent producer burdened by state favoritism toward the unproductive. Nock contended that such interventions fostered moral hazard by insulating beneficiaries from market discipline, thereby eroding incentives for voluntary cooperation and self-reliance essential to societal order. These critiques, serialized in outlets like The Atlantic Monthly, emphasized causal distortions from federal overreach, linking policies like the National Recovery Administration to suppressed entrepreneurship rather than genuine recovery.[67] The American Liberty League produced and disseminated pamphlets in 1935 and 1936 that dissected specific New Deal measures, such as the Social Security Act, as unconstitutional expansions of federal power that bypassed enumerated powers and imposed unfunded liabilities on future generations.[68] These tracts argued the Act's payroll taxes and mandates represented coercive redistribution without state consent, violating Tenth Amendment reservations of social welfare to local spheres.[69] Distributed widely to counter administration narratives, the pamphlets drew on constitutional scholarship to claim such programs incentivized dependency, with empirical illustrations from persistent unemployment rates exceeding 15 percent amid rising federal spending.[70] In response to the 1937 recession, critics amplified these arguments through targeted essays and leaflets citing Bureau of Labor Statistics figures: industrial production fell 33 percent from its May 1937 peak to June 1938, while manufacturing employment stagnated below 1929 levels despite prior stimulus, attributing the downturn to New Deal-induced rigidities like wage floors and uncertainty from policy shifts.[60] Pamphlets from libertarian-leaning publicists, such as those echoing H.L. Mencken's satirical barbs in The American Mercury, framed the recession as evidence of malinvestment from artificial props, urging reversion to laissez-faire to restore output incentives without bureaucratic intermediation.[71] These writings, often under 50 pages, prioritized accessible reasoning over academic treatises, influencing grassroots skepticism toward further expansions like the Wagner Act.[72]
