Maxillary hypoplasia

Maxillary hypoplasia , or maxillary deficiency , is an underdevelopment of the bones of the upper jaw. It is associated with Crouzon syndrome, Angelman syndrome, as well as fetal alcohol syndrome. It can also be associated with cleft lip and cleft palate. Some people could develop it due to poor dental extractions.[1]

Signs and symptoms

Maxillary hypoplasia is characterized by underdevelopment of the upper jaw bones, resulting in a recessed or sunken midface appearance.[2] This can lead to malocclusion, such as an underbite, and difficulties with eating and speech.[3] In severe cases, it may contribute to airway narrowing, potentially causing obstructive sleep apnea or snoring.[4] Complications of untreated sleep apnea can include cardiovascular issues, endocrine disorders, weight gain, and cognitive impairments.[5]

Cause

Although the exact genetic link for isolated maxillary hypoplasia has not been identified, the structure of the facial bones as a whole relies on genetic inheritance and therefore there is likely an inheritance pattern. Maxillary hypoplasia can be present as part of genetic syndromes such as Crouzon syndrome[6] and Angelman syndrome. It is commonly associated with cleft lip and palate.[7] Fetal alcohol syndrome is associated with maxillary hypoplasia. Injury to facial bones during childhood can lead to atypical growth. Exposure to phenytoin in the first trimester of pregnancy has also been associated with the development of maxillary hypoplasia.[8]

Pathophysiology

Maxillary hypoplasia involves abnormal development of the maxillary bones, often as a secondary effect of underlying developmental abnormalities. In conditions like Crouzon syndrome, premature fusion of cranial sutures due to FGFR2 gene mutations restricts midfacial growth, leading to maxillary underdevelopment.[6] In Angelman syndrome, caused by loss of maternal UBE3A function, maxillary hypoplasia is part of characteristic dysmorphic facial features, though the exact mechanism is linked to neurodevelopmental disruptions.[9] Fetal alcohol syndrome results from prenatal alcohol exposure disrupting embryonic facial development, contributing to maxillary hypoplasia as a key facial dysmorphology.[10] When associated with cleft lip and palate, the hypoplasia can arise from intrinsic deficiencies in maxillary growth inherent to the cleft, as well as extrinsic factors such as soft tissue scarring from surgical repairs.[11]

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of maxillary hypoplasia is made primarily through clinical examination and radiographic imaging. Computed tomography (CT) scans can be used to compare the size of the maxilla and mandible. Additionally, lateral cephalometric radiographs are commonly employed to assess anteroposterior and vertical skeletal relationships, while cone-beam computed tomography (CBCT) provides detailed three-dimensional evaluation of the maxillofacial structures.[12]

Treatment

Corrective surgery is the most common treatment to correct this disorder. It involves the repositioning of the upper jaw to align with the lower jaw, to provide symmetry. The surgery may be performed in consultation with an orthodontist who works on repositioning the teeth in the mouth.[1] Severe cases require surgical correction after completing craniofacial growth around age 17-21.[13] For milder cases, particularly in growing patients, non-surgical orthodontic treatments such as protraction headgear or functional appliances may be used to stimulate maxillary growth.[14]

Prognosis

Maxillary hypoplasia is associated with various complications depending on severity and underlying cause. When it leads to airway restriction, such as nasopharyngeal narrowing, individuals may adopt a forward head posture to facilitate breathing, potentially resulting in neck pain, back pain, and in some cases, numbness or tingling in the arms and hands due to nerve compression.[15] Additionally, it increases the risk of obstructive sleep apnea and snoring, particularly in syndromic cases.[8] With appropriate treatment, such as surgical advancement or orthodontic interventions, individuals can achieve significant improvement in function and aesthetics, allowing for a relatively normal quality of life.[7][16]

Recovery

Recovery after surgery for maxillary hypoplasia, such as Le Fort I osteotomy, varies depending on the extent of the procedure. Patients typically remain in the hospital for 1 to 3 days.[17] Initial healing of the jaw bones usually takes about 6 weeks, while full recovery, including resolution of swelling and return to normal function, can take up to 12 weeks.[8] During the initial post-operative period, patients are advised to follow a soft or liquid diet for 4 to 6 weeks to facilitate healing and avoid stress on the surgical site.[18] Swelling is common and peaks around 2 to 3 days after surgery, generally subsiding within 2 weeks; ice packs and pain medications are used for management.[13] Regular follow-up appointments with the surgeon are essential to monitor healing, assess for stability or relapse of the bone segments, detect signs of infection, and address any other complications.[1]

Epidemiology

In the general population of the United States, the prevalence of severe maxillary hypoplasia is approximately 0.3%.[13] Maxillary hypoplasia is the most common secondary deformity that results from cleft lip and cleft palate. Because of the subjective nature of the diagnosis, the incidence of maxillary hypoplasia in people with cleft lip and palate varies between 15-50%. It is estimated that 25-50% of these patients require surgical intervention.[7]

Research directions

Research on maxillary hypoplasia has emphasized improving treatment and management approaches, particularly surgical accuracy and long-term stability. A retrospective study published in January 2020 evaluated the accuracy of virtual surgical planning (VSP)-assisted management for maxillary hypoplasia in adult patients with cleft lip and palate. The study concluded that VSP is a highly accurate alternative to conventional model surgery.[16] More recent studies have explored advancements in surgical techniques. A 2025 study on segmental Le Fort I osteotomy combined with anterior maxillary clockwise rotation reported favorable stability up to one year postoperatively.[19] Another 2025 investigation found that a modified Le Fort I osteotomy effectively minimizes nasal changes during maxillary advancement.[20] Research has also addressed non-surgical options. A 2024 review examined the effects of non-invasive ventilation on maxillary hypoplasia, highlighting potential impacts on growth and function.[21] Additionally, a 2024 study on bone-anchored maxillary protraction (BAMP) in patients with mild hypoplasia demonstrated changes in growth patterns toward anterior advancement.[22]
