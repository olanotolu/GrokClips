2023 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification (UEFA)

The UEFA section of the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification process selected 12 teams from Europe to compete in the finals tournament co-hosted by Australia and New Zealand, marking the confederation's allocation in the competition's first expansion to 32 participants.[1][2] Involving 51 of UEFA's 55 member associations, the format featured nine groups of five or six teams each played in a home-and-away round-robin from April 2022 to December 2022, with the group winners—Denmark, England, France, Germany, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Spain, and Sweden—advancing directly based on points, goal difference, and goals scored as tiebreakers.[3][4] The nine runners-up then entered single-elimination play-offs structured into three paths of three teams, contested via semifinals and finals in October and December 2022, yielding three additional qualifiers: Belgium, Republic of Ireland, and Switzerland.[1][5] This qualification marked historic debuts for Belgium, Republic of Ireland, and Switzerland at the Women's World Cup, while established powers like England and Germany maintained their streaks of consecutive appearances dating to 1991 and 1995, respectively.[5] The process highlighted competitive depth, with narrow group margins influencing play-off entries and underscoring the growing parity driven by increased investment in women's national teams across the continent.[4]

Background

Tournament Expansion and UEFA Slots

The FIFA Council unanimously approved the expansion of the Women's World Cup from 24 to 32 teams, effective for the 2023 edition hosted by Australia and New Zealand.[6] This decision, made on 31 July 2019, aimed to broaden global participation in women's international football while maintaining the tournament's structure of group stages followed by knockouts.[6] The expansion prompted a reevaluation of qualification slots across FIFA's confederations, with allocations finalized and announced on 24 December 2020.[7] Of the 29 direct qualification spots (excluding the automatic qualification of co-hosts Australia and New Zealand), UEFA received 11—the highest number awarded to any confederation—reflecting Europe's strong performance in prior tournaments and its 55 member associations.[7] This marked an increase from the 8 slots UEFA held for the 24-team formats of the 2015 and 2019 editions.[8] In addition to the direct slots, UEFA's qualification process included a pathway for a potential 12th team: the highest-ranked non-direct qualifier advanced to FIFA's inter-confederation play-off tournament, contested among six teams from various confederations for the three remaining World Cup places.[9] This mechanism, detailed in UEFA's Women's European Qualifiers format, ensured competitive depth while aligning with FIFA's emphasis on ranking-based access to the playoffs.[9]

Historical Participation Trends in UEFA Women's Qualification

The allocation of slots for UEFA teams in the FIFA Women's World Cup finals has increased progressively, from two slots in the 12-team tournaments of 1991 and 1995 to eight slots in the 24-team editions of 2015 and 2019, and 11 direct slots for the 32-team 2023 tournament.[10] This expansion has incentivized wider participation in the qualification process, as more associations seek to secure berths amid growing domestic investment in women's programs. Early qualifications were dominated by a handful of Northern European powerhouses, such as Germany and Norway, which claimed the limited slots with minimal competition from emerging nations. Concomitant with slot growth, the number of UEFA member associations entering qualification has risen sharply, driven by UEFA's development initiatives and the professionalization of women's leagues across the continent. Whereas initial cycles drew entries primarily from established federations, recent processes have approached near-universal involvement, with over 80% of UEFA's 55 members competing by the late 2010s.[11] This surge correlates with expanded grassroots participation and infrastructure, enabling lower-ranked teams to challenge traditional elites through structured leagues and play-offs.

Entrants

Participating National Teams

Fifty-one UEFA member associations entered national teams into the qualification process, marking a record participation compared to the 46 entrants for the 2019 FIFA Women's World Cup qualifiers.[3] These teams represented a broad spectrum of European football nations, from established powerhouses like Germany, England, France, and the Netherlands to emerging programs seeking their first major tournament appearances. The entrants were determined by each association's decision to register a senior women's team, with participation requiring affiliation with UEFA and adherence to FIFA eligibility standards. Cyprus entered a team for the first time in Women's World Cup qualifying history, fielding a squad in Group C.[3] Luxembourg also competed in a complete group stage for the first time, having previously only played preliminary rounds or friendlies in earlier cycles; they were placed in Group D.[3] Russia initially entered and played matches in Group B starting in September 2021, but FIFA suspended the Russian team indefinitely on 28 February 2022 amid the invasion of Ukraine, leading to the annulment of all their results and their exclusion from further competition.[12] The participating teams were allocated to seeding pots for the group draw on 30 April 2021 based on UEFA women's national team coefficients calculated from matches between 2017 and 2020, ensuring competitive balance across groups.[3] Pot 1 included the highest-ranked teams: Denmark, England, France, Germany, Iceland, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, and Sweden. Subsequent pots distributed the remaining 42 teams, including lower-ranked entrants like Malta, North Macedonia, and Moldova, which had limited prior international experience but met entry requirements through domestic league structures.

Exclusions, Debuts, and Returns

Russia was excluded from the UEFA qualification process following its invasion of Ukraine, with UEFA suspending all Russian teams from competitions on 28 February 2022 and extending the ban to women's events, including World Cup qualifying, on 2 May 2022.[13][14] The Russian women's national team had initially been drawn into Group E alongside Denmark, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro, and Malta, but their results were nullified, allowing Denmark to advance directly as group winners without playing further matches.[12] Cyprus made its debut in the Women's World Cup qualification, entering the competition for the first time and being drawn into Group C with the Netherlands, Iceland, Czech Republic, and Belarus.[3] Luxembourg returned to the qualification process, participating in a full group stage for the first time, having previously appeared only in preliminary rounds; it was placed in Group D with England, Austria, Northern Ireland, and North Macedonia.[3]

Qualification Format

Group Stage Structure

The group stage of the UEFA qualification for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup featured 51 national teams divided into nine groups labeled A through I.[3] Groups A through H each comprised six teams, while Group I had five teams, reflecting the total entrants after four UEFA member associations opted not to participate.[3] Teams within each group competed in a double round-robin format, playing home and away matches against every other team in their group, resulting in 10 matches per team in the six-team groups and 8 matches per team in the five-team group.[3] Matches were scheduled across five international windows: 13–21 September 2021, 18–26 October 2021, 22–30 November 2021, 4–12 April 2022, and 29 August–6 September 2022, with a pause during July 2022 for UEFA Women's EURO 2022.[3] The group stage draw occurred on 30 April 2021 at the UEFA headquarters in Nyon, Switzerland.[3] The 51 teams were allocated into six pots based on their UEFA women's national team coefficient rankings as of February 2021, with Pot 1 containing the top six teams, Pots 2 through 5 having eight teams each, and Pot 6 with five teams.[3] Drawing proceeded alphabetically by group, placing one team from each pot into Groups A–H (with the final Pot 6 team assigned to Group I), subject to restrictions such as avoiding matchups between Armenia and Azerbaijan or between Russia and Ukraine.[3] Group winners qualified directly for the World Cup finals, securing nine of UEFA's 11 direct slots, while the nine runners-up advanced to the UEFA play-offs; additionally, the three best third-placed teams (evaluated excluding matches against sixth-placed teams in six-team groups) also entered the play-offs to compete for the remaining spots.[3] Standings were determined by points, with tie-breakers applied sequentially: goal difference, goals scored, head-to-head results, and disciplinary points if necessary.[3]

Play-off Mechanism

The UEFA play-off stage for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification involved the nine runners-up from the group stage, who were initially ranked 1 through 9 based on their results excluding matches against their respective group winners, using standard criteria such as points obtained, goal difference, goals scored, and UEFA coefficients if necessary.[3] The three highest-ranked runners-up advanced directly to the second round, while the six lowest-ranked were paired via a draw held on 9 September 2022 into three single-leg first-round ties, played between 3 and 6 October 2022, with the home team determined by the draw.[3] The winners of the first-round ties joined the three seeded teams in the second round, where a separate draw on 11 October 2022 paired the six teams into three single-leg ties, also played on that date.[15] The three second-round winners were then re-ranked by combining their initial second-place standings (excluding group-winner matches) with their second-round play-off results, applying tiebreakers as needed.[15] The top two in this ranking qualified directly for the World Cup, securing UEFA's 11th and 12th slots, while the third advanced to the intercontinental play-off tournament; Republic of Ireland and Switzerland took the direct spots, with Portugal proceeding to the intercontinental phase.[15] All ties were single-leg knockout matches, with extra time and penalty shoot-outs resolving draws after 90 minutes, and VAR was employed for the first time in UEFA women's qualifiers.[16] This structure ensured competitive balance by seeding stronger performers while providing opportunities for lower-ranked teams through the first round.[3]

Tiebreaker Rules

In the group stage of the UEFA qualification for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, teams level on points were ranked by applying successive tie-breaking criteria as defined in the competition's official regulations. The primary criterion was superior goal difference across all group matches played. If teams remained tied, the greater number of goals scored in all group matches was used next, followed by the greater number of away goals scored in all group matches. Subsequent criteria included the greater number of wins in all group matches, then results from matches between the tied teams (points, goal difference, goals scored, and away goals therein). Further ties were resolved by the fewer disciplinary points accumulated in all group matches (calculated as 1 point for a yellow card, 3 points for an indirect red card via two yellows, 4 points for a direct red card, and 5 points for a yellow followed by a direct red). If still unresolved, the UEFA national team coefficient rankings from the previous four-year period determined the order; a drawing of lots by UEFA officials served as the final arbiter in rare cases of persistent equality. These rules ensured objective resolution based on performance metrics, prioritizing overall group contribution before head-to-head specifics. For the play-off rounds, which consisted of home-and-away ties in the first round and single-leg matches in the second round path B (with path A being single-leg), aggregate scores decided advancement. In two-legged ties, the away goals rule applied if aggregates were level after both legs; no extra time was played, proceeding directly to kicks from the penalty mark if away goals did not break the tie. Single-leg play-offs used 30 minutes of extra time followed by penalties if necessary, aligning with UEFA's standard knockout procedures to favor decisive outcomes without prolonging matches unduly.

Schedule and Draws

Key Dates and Fixtures Calendar

The group stage draw occurred on 30 April 2021 at the UEFA headquarters in Nyon, Switzerland, determining the composition of nine groups ranging from five to six teams each.[3] Group stage fixtures were contested across multiple FIFA-designated women's international match windows between 16 September 2021 and 11 October 2022, accommodating home-and-away round-robin formats while aligning with player welfare and club commitments. Key windows included:
  • 17–21 September 2021 (initial matchdays for most groups)
  • 26 October 2021 and 22–30 November 2021 (subsequent matchdays, with some overlaps due to group sizes)
  • 4–12 April 2022 (postponed or rescheduled from earlier February slots amid logistical challenges)
  • 20–28 June 2022 (preceding the UEFA Women's EURO 2022)
  • 1–6 September 2022 (penultimate matchdays)
  • 3–11 October 2022 (final matchdays for completing outstanding fixtures)
A pause occurred from 6 to 31 July 2022 to accommodate the UEFA Women's EURO 2022 in England. The play-off draw, involving the nine group runners-up divided into three paths of three teams, took place on 9 September 2022.[17] Semi-finals were single-leg ties held on 6 October 2022, followed by single-leg finals on 11 October 2022, with the three path winners securing UEFA's remaining qualification spots for the finals.[17]

Group Stage Draw Procedure

The group stage draw for the UEFA qualification tournament was held on 30 April 2021 at UEFA headquarters in Nyon, Switzerland.[3] The 51 participating teams were seeded into six pots according to the UEFA women's national team coefficient rankings established following the UEFA Women's EURO 2022 qualifying phase.[3] Pots 1 through 5 each contained nine teams, while Pot 6 included the remaining six teams.[3] The draw proceeded sequentially, beginning with Pot 1 and continuing through Pot 6.[3] Teams drawn from each pot were assigned to groups in alphabetical order (A through I), filling positions starting from the top of the group.[3] This resulted in Groups A, B, and C consisting of five teams each, while Groups D through I each had six teams, with the Pot 6 teams specifically allocated to the sixth position in Groups D–I.[3] To ensure logistical and political feasibility, certain teams were restricted from being placed in the same group: Armenia and Azerbaijan; Bosnia and Herzegovina and Kosovo; Russia and Kosovo; Russia and Ukraine; and Serbia and Kosovo.[3] If such a pairing occurred during the draw, the affected team would be redrawn into an available position in another group.[3]

Play-off Draw Outcomes

The UEFA play-off draw for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup qualification took place on 9 September 2022 at UEFA headquarters in Nyon, Switzerland, determining the fixtures for both rounds of the play-offs. The six lowest-ranked group runners-up (Austria, Belgium, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Portugal, Scotland, and Wales) were unseeded and drawn into three single-leg ties for round 1, scheduled for 6 October 2022, with the first-drawn team designated as the home side. The three highest-ranked runners-up (Iceland, Republic of Ireland, and Switzerland) bypassed round 1 and were seeded into round 2, where they were paired via draw against specific round 1 ties to establish the bracket paths.

Group D Outcomes

England dominated Group D, winning all ten of their matches to finish with 30 points, a goal difference of +80 (scoring 80 goals and conceding none), and thereby qualifying directly for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup.[28] This unbeaten run included emphatic victories such as 20–0 against Latvia on 25 November 2021 and 10–0 against North Macedonia on 8 April 2022.[29] England's qualification was confirmed early with a 2–0 away win over second-placed Austria on 2 September 2022, at which point they had already secured 27 points from nine wins and scored 70 goals without reply.[28] [30] Austria finished runners-up with 21 points, advancing to the UEFA play-off rounds alongside the other group runners-up (excluding the hosts' group).[4] Their campaign featured seven wins but included three defeats, two of which were to England.[31] Northern Ireland placed third and were eliminated from contention, as only the group winner qualified directly and the runner-up entered play-offs.[4] Luxembourg, North Macedonia, and Latvia finished lower and were also eliminated. The group's other notable results included Austria's 4–1 home win over Northern Ireland on 12 April 2022, contributing to their strong position for second place.[4] Overall, the outcomes highlighted England's superiority, built on defensive solidity and prolific attacking output under coach Sarina Wiegman, while underscoring the competitive gap with mid-tier teams like Austria and Northern Ireland.[32]

Group E Outcomes

Denmark topped Group E, securing direct qualification to the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup by winning all eight of their matches against the remaining teams, following the exclusion of Russia.[4] The team demonstrated exceptional attacking prowess, netting 40 goals while conceding only 2, with standout performances including an 8–0 victory over Azerbaijan on 21 September 2021 and a 10–0 win against Malta on 5 March 2022.[4] [33] Russia, initially drawn into the group alongside Denmark, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro, Azerbaijan, and Malta, was suspended by UEFA on 28 February 2022 amid the invasion of Ukraine and fully excluded on 2 May 2022, with their prior results annulled and rendered null and void.[34] This reduced the competition to a double round-robin among the five remaining nations, totaling eight matches per team.[34] Bosnia and Herzegovina finished as runners-up, advancing to the UEFA play-off rounds, where they were later eliminated. Montenegro placed third, ahead of Azerbaijan and Malta, which finished last after conceding heavy defeats, including 0–10 to Denmark and 0–8 to Bosnia and Herzegovina on 12 April 2022.[4][35]

Group F included the national teams of Norway, Belgium, Poland, Albania, Kosovo, and Armenia, with each team playing the others home and away between September 2021 and December 2022. As one of the nine group runners-up, Belgium advanced to the UEFA qualification play-offs but ultimately failed to secure a World Cup berth after elimination in the second round by Switzerland.[20][36] The remaining teams—Poland, Albania, Kosovo, and Armenia—finished outside the top two and were eliminated from contention, with Poland securing third place but unable to challenge the leading duo effectively. Runners-up Ranking

Ranking Criteria

The nine group runners-up were ranked to identify the three strongest, who received a bye to the second round of the play-offs, while the six lowest-ranked contested the first round. This ranking accounted for the uneven group compositions, with Groups A–E featuring six teams each and Groups F–I having five; accordingly, results against sixth-placed teams were excluded from calculations for runners-up in the larger groups to enable fair comparison across all nine.[3] Teams were ordered primarily by points accumulated in the adjusted matches (three for a win, one for a draw). Ties were resolved sequentially by:
  • Goal difference in adjusted matches;
  • Goals scored in adjusted matches;
  • Away goals scored in adjusted matches;
  • Number of wins in adjusted matches;
  • Disciplinary points (yellow card: -1; indirect red: -3; direct red: -4; yellow-red: -5);
  • Position in the UEFA women's national team coefficient ranking if necessary.[45]
These criteria aligned with UEFA's standard tie-breaking procedures for qualification competitions, ensuring rankings reflected competitive performance against comparable opposition.[1]

Determination of Additional Qualifiers

The three winners of the UEFA play-off second round—Portugal, Republic of Ireland, and Switzerland—underwent a ranking process to determine the two additional direct qualifiers for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, with the lowest-ranked advancing to the inter-confederation play-offs.[49] The ranking combined each team's performance from the group stage, where they finished as runners-up, with their result from the second round match played on 15 December 2022.[1] All three teams earned 3 points from their second round victories. Tie-breaking began with overall goal difference across the group stage and second round, followed by total goals scored in those phases. Further criteria included disciplinary points (fewer deducted preferred) and, if still tied, the UEFA women's national team coefficient rankings. The Republic of Ireland topped the ranking with superior goal difference from their group (15 goals scored, 2 conceded in 10 matches) augmented by their 1–0 win over Scotland. Switzerland ranked second, bolstered by their group record (13 goals scored, 3 conceded) and 2–1 extra-time victory over Wales. Portugal placed third, despite beating Iceland 4–1 after extra time, due to a weaker group goal difference (11 scored, 7 conceded).[1][49] As a result, the Republic of Ireland and Switzerland qualified directly as the additional UEFA representatives, joining the nine group winners for a total of 11 slots allocated to Europe. Portugal, as the third-ranked play-off winner, entered the inter-confederation play-offs but was eliminated by Chile on 18 February 2023, forgoing an extra berth.[1][49]

Qualified Teams

Direct Qualifiers from Groups

The nine teams that secured direct qualification to the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup as winners of UEFA's group stage were Sweden, Spain, Italy, England, Denmark, Norway, Germany, the Netherlands, and France.[2] These nations topped their respective groups in a round-robin format contested between November 2021 and July 2022, amassing the highest points totals (ranging from 19 to 24 out of a maximum 24) with undefeated records in most cases.[2] The group stage featured 54 teams divided into nine groups of six following Russia's exclusion by FIFA in February 2022 due to the invasion of Ukraine, ensuring all group winners advanced without playoffs.

Controversies and Criticisms

Exclusion of Russia

On 28 February 2022, FIFA and UEFA jointly suspended all Russian national and club teams from participating in their competitions until further notice, in response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine that began on 24 February 2022.[62][63] On 2 May 2022, UEFA specifically excluded the Russia women's national team from the European qualification process for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, rendering all prior results in UEFA Group E null and void.[34] Russia had been drawn into Group E alongside Iceland, Belarus, and North Macedonia but did not contest their scheduled matches in April 2022 due to the ongoing suspension.[34] This exclusion prevented Russia from advancing through the UEFA qualification pathway, which consisted of group stages followed by play-offs for the Women's World Cup.[34] The decision aligned with broader sanctions excluding Russian teams from UEFA's Women's EURO 2022 and other tournaments, with no reinstatement for senior women's teams permitted by FIFA or UEFA as of the qualification's conclusion.[34][62]

Scheduling and Structural Disparities

The UEFA qualification for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup utilized a league-based format across five divisions (A–E), with matches confined to four primary international windows in 2022: April 4–12 for initial group stage fixtures, June 20–28 for the second set, September 1–6 for the third, and October 3–11 for play-offs. This compressed timeline, spanning roughly six months, often overlapped with domestic club seasons in Europe, where many women's leagues run from August to May, leading to midweek international calls that disrupted club commitments and increased travel demands.[64][65] Player welfare concerns arose from these scheduling pressures, as highlighted in a FIFPRO workload report based on surveys of international players. Approximately 33% of respondents reported insufficient recovery time between qualifying matches and subsequent club games, with additional issues including sub-standard pitches in some venues and inconsistent pre-match medical screenings, which compromised performance and injury risk management. These factors disproportionately affected teams from lower-resourced associations, where access to sports science support lagged behind top nations like England or Germany.[66][67] Structurally, the format yielded disparities in competitive exposure compared to the men's UEFA World Cup qualifiers for 2022, where teams contested 8–10 group stage matches over a longer period (March 2021–November 2021). Women's League A teams played only six matches each, limiting opportunities for match sharpness, tactical refinement, and depth testing amid injuries—evident in play-off outcomes where fresher squads advanced over fatigued ones. Lower leagues (B–E) featured even fewer high-stakes games, exacerbating gaps between elite and emerging nations; for instance, promoted teams from League B entered play-offs with minimal top-tier experience. This brevity stemmed from FIFA's calendar constraints and UEFA's adoption of a Nations League-integrated system to minimize mismatches, though it arguably prioritized efficiency over depth.[68][69] Resource imbalances amplified these issues, with 29% of surveyed players across confederations (including UEFA) reporting no appearance fees for qualifiers, though this was rarer in professionalized UEFA nations; payments, when provided, often depended on performance outcomes rather than guaranteed stipends, unlike men's cycles. Travel logistics favored centrally located teams, while peripheral associations like those in Eastern Europe faced longer hauls, contributing to uneven fatigue levels. FIFPRO's analysis, drawn from player data, underscores how such structures reflect broader underinvestment in women's international football, with UEFA's model offering more parity than other confederations but still trailing men's in volume and support.[70][64]
