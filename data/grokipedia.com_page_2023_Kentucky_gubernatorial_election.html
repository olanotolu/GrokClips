2023 Kentucky gubernatorial election

The 2023 Kentucky gubernatorial election was held on November 7, 2023, to elect the governor and lieutenant governor of the U.S. state of Kentucky.[1] Incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear and Lieutenant Governor Jacqueline Coleman defeated Republican state Attorney General Daniel Cameron and state Representative Michael Knapp, with Beshear receiving 694,482 votes (52.5 percent) to Cameron's 627,457 votes (47.5 percent).[2] Primaries for both parties occurred on May 16, 2023, in which Beshear faced token opposition in the Democratic contest while Cameron prevailed in a crowded Republican field that included former U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations Kelly Craft and state Agriculture Commissioner Ryan Quarles. Beshear's re-election represented a continued Democratic hold on the governorship in a state dominated by Republicans at the federal and state legislative levels, where the GOP maintained supermajorities in both chambers of the Kentucky General Assembly and Donald Trump had carried the state by 26 percentage points in the 2020 presidential election. The race drew national attention as a test of Democratic viability in Southern states amid economic recovery from the COVID-19 pandemic and debates over social issues including abortion restrictions following the U.S. Supreme Court's Dobbs decision.[3] Cameron, endorsed by Trump, emphasized opposition to Beshear's pandemic-era policies and support for Kentucky's near-total abortion ban, while Beshear highlighted his response to natural disasters and vetoes of legislative overrides on social conservatism.[4]

Background

Kentucky's political context

Kentucky underwent a pronounced shift toward the Republican Party in federal elections beginning in the early 2000s, transitioning from a competitive swing state to a consistent Republican bastion. In the 2000 presidential election, George W. Bush defeated Al Gore by 14.8 percentage points, with margins widening in subsequent cycles: Bush's 2004 win over John Kerry by 19.8 points, John McCain's 2008 victory over Barack Obama by 21.9 points, Mitt Romney's 2012 triumph over Obama by 22.7 points, Donald Trump's 2016 margin of 29.8 points against Hillary Clinton, and Trump's 2020 edge of 25.9 points over Joe Biden (62.1% to 36.2%).[5] This federal alignment starkly contrasts with Kentucky's gubernatorial politics, where voters have historically exhibited split-ticket tendencies, prioritizing state-specific issues and candidate performance over national party loyalty. The state achieved a Republican trifecta in 2017, with GOP control of the governorship under Matt Bevin and majorities in both legislative chambers following the 2016 elections. Yet, in the 2019 gubernatorial election, Bevin lost narrowly to Democrat Andy Beshear by 0.4 percentage points (49.2% to 48.8%), despite the state's strong Republican lean in concurrent federal races.[6][7] Bevin's defeat highlighted causal factors beyond partisan tides, including policy disputes and governance controversies that eroded his support base. A pivotal trigger was the Republican legislature's December 2018 special session, held in a lame-duck period after midterm gains by Democrats in the House of Representatives; this session rammed through contentious legislation on pensions, taxes, and abortion restrictions, overriding anticipated vetoes and sparking teacher sickouts and public protests that damaged Bevin's approval ratings. Such events underscored Kentucky voters' pragmatic detachment from federal partisanship, enabling Democratic gubernatorial exceptions in an otherwise red-leaning polity.[8]

Beshear's first term: Achievements and criticisms

During his first term from December 2019 to December 2023, Governor Andy Beshear navigated a divided government, with Republicans controlling the state legislature, which limited executive implementation of certain policies while enabling overrides of several vetoes. Beshear's administration emphasized disaster recovery and economic recovery post-COVID-19, achieving measurable gains in employment metrics amid national challenges, though critics attributed persistent social issues like rising overdoses to insufficient policy shifts.[9] Key achievements included robust responses to natural disasters, such as the December 10, 2021, tornado outbreak that killed 76 people across western Kentucky and the July 2022 floods in eastern Kentucky that claimed 45 lives. Beshear established the Team Kentucky Storm Relief Fund, activated price-gouging protections, and secured federal aid for affected counties, including public assistance approvals for all requested areas from the floods and tornadoes.[10] [11] These efforts correlated with temporary boosts in his approval ratings, reaching highs around 60% in polls following the tornado response, as voters in a predominantly Republican state credited visible leadership in recovery.[12] Economically, Kentucky recorded its lowest annual unemployment rate in state history at 3.9% for 2022, with seasonally adjusted rates dipping to 3.7% earlier that year, reflecting job growth from a low of about 64,000 net additions since 2019 amid manufacturing and logistics expansions.[9] [13] Beshear also advanced early childhood education by increasing funding for public pre-K programs, expanding enrollment to over 32,000 four-year-olds by 2023 through budget proposals that prioritized access in underserved areas, though full universality remained a legislative goal deferred by GOP opposition.[14] Criticisms centered on Beshear's stringent COVID-19 measures, including statewide school closures starting March 16, 2020, for over a year in many districts, which coincided with documented learning lossesâ€”Kentucky students fell behind national averages in reading and math proficiency by 2022 assessmentsâ€”and business restrictions that prompted lawsuits from churches, parents, and Attorney General Daniel Cameron over unequal treatment of religious gatherings.[15] [16] These policies, while initially curbing case growth in line with CDC guidelines, faced scrutiny for potential overreach, as studies found limited additional impact from school bans without broader lockdowns, and Kentucky's excess deaths surged during restriction lulls in late 2020.[17] [18] On social issues, Beshear vetoed multiple pro-life bills, including a 15-week abortion ban in April 2022 and subsequent clarifications to Kentucky's trigger law post-Roe v. Wade, arguing they lacked exceptions for rape, incest, or fetal anomalies; the Republican legislature overrode these vetoes, enacting near-total bans.[19] [20] Similar overrides occurred on election-related measures, such as bills strengthening voter ID and purging inactive registrations, which Beshear opposed as potentially disenfranchising, though data showed Kentucky's 2020 turnout at 63% with minimal fraud reports.[21] The opioid crisis persisted as a vulnerability, with age-adjusted overdose death rates rising 74.7% from 29.2 per 100,000 in 2019 to 51.0 in 2021, totaling over 2,200 deaths in 2022 despite state investments in treatment; critics, including Republican challengers, argued Beshear's focus on harm reduction over enforcement failed to reverse pre-existing trends exacerbated by fentanyl influx.[22] [23] Beshear's moderate positioningâ€”vetoing progressive priorities like marijuana legalization while signing bipartisan infrastructure dealsâ€”sustained incumbency advantages in a state where Republicans held supermajorities, preventing deeper policy shifts but enabling targeted wins like disaster aid that resonated empirically with voters facing tangible crises over ideological divides.[24]

Path to the 2023 election

Incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear was eligible for re-election, as the Kentucky Constitution limits governors to two consecutive four-year terms, allowing him to seek a second term after winning in 2019.[25] Beshear formally filed for re-election on October 1, 2021, retaining Lieutenant Governor Jacqueline Coleman as his running mate, and encountered no opposition in the Democratic primary.[26] The Republican field coalesced after the 2021 state elections, where the party secured a legislative supermajority despite Beshear's incumbency, prompting twelve candidates to vie for the nomination, including statewide officials and business figures. Attorney General Daniel Cameron established himself as an early frontrunner through his statewide visibility, particularly from leading the investigation into the 2020 Breonna Taylor shooting as the state's top law enforcement officer. Candidates filed by the January 10, 2023, deadline for the May 16, 2023, primaries, preceding the general election on November 7, 2023.

Primaries

Democratic primary

Incumbent Governor Andy Beshear sought re-election on the Democratic ticket alongside Lieutenant Governor Jacqueline Coleman, who faced no opposition for her position. The primary occurred on May 16, 2023, with Beshear confronting two minor challengers: Geoff M. Young, a state representative known for repeated unsuccessful bids in Democratic primaries, and activist Peppy Martin.[27][28] Beshear dominated the low-turnout contest, capturing 91.3 percent of the vote with 176,589 ballots, compared to Young's 5.1 percent (9,865 votes) and Martin's 3.6 percent (6,913 votes), for a total of 193,367 votes statewide.[29] This outcome underscored Beshear's firm consolidation of Democratic support in a state where Republicans hold supermajorities in the legislature, reflecting the absence of viable intra-party alternatives despite Kentucky's rightward shift in recent elections.[28][30] The challengers mounted negligible campaigns; Young criticized Beshear's progressive stances on issues like abortion and criticized party leadership for insufficient opposition to Republican policies, while Martin focused on grassroots appeals but lacked organizational backing.) Voter turnout remained subdued at under 10 percent of registered Democrats, consistent with expectations for an incumbent-heavy primary lacking contention.[31] Beshear's retention of Coleman as running mate ensured continuity of the 2019 ticket, signaling internal party cohesion ahead of the general election.[32]

Republican primary

The Republican primary election for Kentucky governor took place on May 16, 2023, featuring twelve candidates seeking the nomination to challenge incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear.) Attorney General Daniel Cameron, who had gained prominence for his legal challenges against Biden administration policies and his handling of high-profile cases as the state's first Black attorney general elected statewide, dominated the field.[33] Cameron's campaign emphasized conservative priorities such as school choice, Second Amendment rights, and opposition to COVID-19 mandates, drawing support from both populist and establishment wings of the party through endorsements from former President Donald Trump in June 2022 and Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell.[34][35] Key challengers included former U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations Kelly Craft, backed by self-funding and ties to coal industry donors; Agriculture Commissioner Ryan Quarles, highlighting rural economic issues; Somerset Mayor Alan Keck, positioning as an outsider focused on local governance; and businessman Scott Jennings, along with other lesser-known entrants like Nick Comer.[36] U.S. Senator Rand Paul, often speculated as a potential contender due to his national profile and libertarian leanings, declined to enter the race, opting to prioritize his U.S. Senate duties.[37] The crowded field reflected internal GOP tensions between Trump-aligned insurgents and McConnell-influenced moderates, but Cameron's broad appeal led to a consolidating victory that signaled post-primary unity.[38] Voter turnout for the May 16 primary was 14.44% of registered voters statewide, with approximately 500,902 ballots cast out of over 3.4 million registered, the majority from Republican voters given the lopsided Democratic primary.[39] This relatively low participation highlighted potential challenges in mobilizing the GOP base despite the competitive gubernatorial contest, though Cameron's margin suggested strong enthusiasm among participating conservatives.[40]

General election

Nominees and running mates

Incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear, who had won election in 2019 as the son of former Governor Steve Beshear and after serving as state attorney general from 2015 to 2019, secured the Democratic nomination in the May 16, 2023, primary with 95 percent of the vote alongside his incumbent lieutenant governor, Jacqueline Coleman. Coleman, a lifelong Kentuckian and former educator who founded a nonprofit focused on education and economic development, formally rejoined the ticket on July 18, 2023, emphasizing her background in teaching and coaching to underscore the administration's priorities on education reform and rural development.[42] [43] The Beshear-Coleman ticket was certified by the Kentucky State Board of Elections following the primary, with no significant legal challenges to their nomination.[1] On the Republican side, state Attorney General Daniel Cameron, the first Black person elected to that office in Kentucky and previously a legal counsel to U.S. Senate Minority Leader Mitch McConnell, won the crowded May 16, 2023, primary with 73 percent of the vote. Cameron announced state Senator Robby Mills as his running mate on July 19, 2023, selecting the conservative lawmaker from Henderson in western Kentuckyâ€”who had served in the legislature since 2017 after prior local government rolesâ€”to broaden appeal in rural and conservative strongholds while aligning with Cameron's emphasis on law enforcement and traditional values.[44] [45] The Cameron-Mills ticket faced no major legal disputes and was certified for the general election ballot.[1]

Campaign strategies and platforms

Incumbent Governor Andy Beshear's campaign strategy emphasized continuity and his personal popularity, positioning him as a results-oriented leader who transcended partisan divides in a Republican-leaning state. Advertisements showcased his administration's handling of natural disasters, such as the December 2021 western Kentucky tornadoes that killed 77 people, and economic metrics including over 100,000 jobs added since 2019.[46] Beshear appealed to moderate Republicans by highlighting bipartisan legislative successes, like pension reforms and infrastructure investments funded partly through federal legislation, while distancing himself from national Democratic figures to broaden his coalition.[47] Republican nominee Daniel Cameron pursued an offensive approach, framing the election as a rejection of Beshear's governance and national progressive influences, with promises of tax reductionsâ€”including phasing out the state income taxâ€”and expanding school choice options to empower parents.[48] His platform stressed aggressive action on public safety and economic deregulation to counter perceived liberal policies, conducting high-energy rallies modeled after former President Donald Trump's style to mobilize the GOP base, bolstered by Trump's early endorsement in June 2022.[49][34] Both candidates allocated substantial resources to broadcast and digital media, with total general election ad expenditures exceeding $70 million, shattering prior state records and dominating airwaves in key markets.[50] Beshear's targeting leaned toward suburban and urban voters with themes of proven stability, whereas Cameron focused on rural turnout through base-rallying events and critiques linking Beshear to federal economic challenges.[51]

Key issues

Abortion remained a central policy flashpoint following the U.S. Supreme Court's 2022 Dobbs decision, which triggered Kentucky's existing near-total ban prohibiting abortions after detection of a fetal heartbeat, typically around six weeks, with no initial exceptions for rape or incest.[52] Republican nominee Daniel Cameron, as attorney general, defended the law's enforcement and opposed legislative efforts to add exceptions, though he later stated during the campaign that he would sign bills incorporating rape and incest exemptions.[53] Democratic incumbent Andy Beshear, who had vetoed bills seeking to remove limited exceptions for the life of the mother or severe fetal anomalies, pledged to pursue restoration of broader access through special legislative sessions if necessary, highlighting cases where the ban allegedly endangered women's health.[54] This divide echoed the prior year's rejection of Amendment 2, a failed ballot measure to explicitly bar abortion rights protections in the state constitution, signaling voter divisions on codifying restrictions amid empirical data showing Kentucky's abortion rate dropping to near zero post-Dobbs while neighboring states saw procedural increases without corresponding rises in overall maternal mortality.[55][52] Economy and education debates centered on Kentucky's post-pandemic recovery, with the state achieving an annual unemployment rate of 4.2% in 2023, near historic lows, amid Beshear's emphasis on attracting over $20 billion in private investments and job announcements exceeding 10,000 positions.[56][57] Critics, including Cameron, argued that regulatory hurdles and high taxes stifled broader growth, proposing tax cuts and deregulation to enhance competitiveness, while advocating workforce training programs to address skills gaps in a labor market where participation rates lagged national averages despite low joblessness.[58] On education, Cameron prioritized school choice via vouchers and charter expansions to counter declining national proficiency scores, faulting Beshear's resistance to such reforms amid teacher protests over stagnant funding and curriculum disputes.[59][60] Beshear focused on increasing public school budgets and teacher salaries, vetoing voucher proposals as diverting resources from core districts, though empirical outcomes showed mixed proficiency gains post-COVID disruptions without clear causal links to either approach.[48][61] Public safety and the opioid crisis highlighted contrasting records, with Kentucky recording 2,200 drug overdose deaths in 2022â€”a 2.5% decline from 2021â€”followed by 1,984 in 2023, a further 9.8% drop, amid Beshear's initiatives like expanded naloxone distribution and treatment access funded partly by settlements.[23][62] Cameron, leveraging his attorney general tenure, touted aggressive fentanyl prosecutions and securing over $800 million in opioid settlements through multidistrict litigation, proposing a comprehensive safety plan to bolster law enforcement and border interdiction against rising synthetic opioid imports driving fatalities.[58][63] Both candidates claimed credit for abatement efforts, but data indicated persistent high per-capita ratesâ€”exceeding national averagesâ€”attributable to fentanyl's potency rather than isolated policy failures, with causal analyses linking declines to multifaceted interventions including supply disruptions over singular attributions.[64][63]

Debates, polling, and predictions

Two televised debates between incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear and Republican Attorney General Daniel Cameron occurred in October 2023, with additional forums hosted by local outlets and universities. The first major debate on October 16 in Northern Kentucky focused on abortion, where Beshear accused Cameron of supporting a near-total ban without exceptions for rape or incest, while Cameron criticized Beshear's vetoes of restrictive measures as extreme.[65][66] Cameron adopted an aggressive stance, pressing Beshear on unfulfilled promises like teacher pay raises and COVID-19 restrictions, portraying the governor as out of touch with Kentucky's conservative leanings.[67] Beshear defended his record on economic growth and disaster response, countering Cameron's attacks by highlighting the challenger's ties to national Republicans.[68] Subsequent debates, including one on October 23 via Kentucky Educational Television, emphasized education policy, crime rates, and COVID-19 handling, with Cameron linking rising urban crime to Beshear's policies and Beshear touting low unemployment and budget surpluses.[69][70] A fifth debate on October 24 grew contentious over economics and abortion, as both candidates urged direct answers on policy specifics without resolution.[71] Overall, the exchanges highlighted partisan divides, with Cameron aiming to nationalize the race around inflation and law enforcement, while Beshear stressed local achievements to insulate from national Democratic unpopularity.[72] Polling throughout the campaign showed a consistently tight contest, with Beshear holding a narrow lead in most surveys despite Kentucky's R+16 partisan lean per the Cook Political Report.[73] Early polls like Mason-Dixon in January 2023 gave Beshear a 49% to 40% edge among likely voters, but the race narrowed by fall.[73] An Emerson College poll from October 6 reported Beshear at 49% to Cameron's 38%, reflecting incumbency advantages, while the final Emerson survey on November 3 found them essentially tied at 46% each among likely voters.[74][75] Aggregates indicated Beshear leading by about 2 points on average (48-46), with stability in trends showing minimal shifts after Labor Day.[73] Pre-election predictions viewed the race as competitive but tilted toward Beshear due to his personal popularity and the structural benefits of incumbency in off-year cycles, defying the state's strong Republican presidential performance.[76] Betting markets like PredictIt showed modest favoritism for Beshear, with shares trading around 55-60 cents for a Democratic win, reflecting perceived momentum from his 2019 narrow victory in a similar environment.[77] Analysts noted polarization's erosion of traditional incumbency edges but forecasted Beshear's reelection as plausible if he decoupled from national Democrats, though a Cameron upset remained viable given Trump's 2020 margin in Kentucky.[78][47]

Fundraising and endorsements

Incumbent Democratic Governor Andy Beshear's campaign raised approximately $19 million during the 2023 election cycle, significantly outpacing Republican nominee Daniel Cameron's $4.35 million.[79] Beshear's funds derived primarily from individual contributions ($8.8 million), transfers from the Kentucky Democratic Party ($3.7 million), and rollover from his primary committee ($6.2 million), enabling substantial spending on advertising and ground operations.[79] Cameron's fundraising relied more on individual donors ($3.2 million) and Republican Party support (over $1 million), but lagged in overall volume, limiting direct campaign expenditures.[79]

Voter turnout for the 2023 Kentucky gubernatorial election stood at 38.07% of registered voters, totaling approximately 1.326 million ballots cast, a decline of 6 percentage points from the 44% turnout in the 2019 election.[87] [88] Turnout rates differed by party registration, with Republicans participating at a marginally higher rate than Democrats despite the latter's incumbent advantage.

Analysis and aftermath

Factors in Beshear's victory

Beshear's incumbency provided a significant advantage in a state that Donald Trump carried by 26 percentage points in 2020, enabling him to cultivate a personal brand centered on crisis management rather than partisan policy achievements. His approval rating stood at 60% in January 2023, the highest among Democratic governors in Republican-leaning states, largely due to visible leadership during the December 2021 tornado outbreak that killed 77 Kentuckians and the July 2022 eastern Kentucky floods that claimed 45 lives.[91][92][93] By mid-2023, Beshear ranked as the fifth-most popular governor nationally, with unusually strong crossover appeal among Republican voters compared to other Democrats, reflecting perceptions of competence in disaster response over ideological alignment.[94] This incumbency insulated him from national Democratic unpopularity, including Joe Biden's low approval, allowing Beshear to frame the race around his record of bipartisan aid distribution and daily briefings that humanized governance amid repeated calamities.[47] Opposition to Kentucky's near-total abortion ban, triggered by the 2022 Dobbs decision, energized Democratic turnout in urban and suburban counties like Jefferson (Louisville) and Fayette (Lexington), where Beshear improved margins by emphasizing exceptions for rape, incest, and maternal health.[95] Campaign ads highlighting personal stories of women denied care under the ban, defended by Cameron, resonated in areas with higher education levels and younger demographics, contributing to Beshear's 5-percentage-point statewide win despite the state's conservative lean.[96] However, abortion's mobilizing effect was not decisive on its own, as the 2022 ballot amendment to constitutionally affirm no abortion rights failed by only 3 points (52-48%), signaling persistent pro-life sentiment that limited broader rural gains for Beshear and underscored the issue's role in consolidating anti-ban voters rather than flipping the electorate.[97][98] The Republican primary's internal divisions indirectly aided Beshear by hindering unified opposition momentum. Featuring 12 candidates and marked by aggressive attacksâ€”such as Kelly Craft's $14 million self-funding against Daniel Cameron's Trump-backed prosecutorial imageâ€”the May 16, 2023, contest saw Cameron secure 47.9% amid splintered votes for rivals like Ryan Quarles (21.1%) and Craft (12.6%), fostering lingering acrimony that tempered GOP enthusiasm heading into the general.[99][100][101] Beshear capitalized on this by positioning himself as a steady alternative, avoiding the primary's fatigue that left some conservative voters disengaged or open to his moderate pitch on economic recovery and infrastructure, thereby preserving his incumbency edge without needing to overhaul the state's partisan baseline.[90]

Republican shortcomings

Daniel Cameron's selection as the Republican nominee emphasized his record as attorney general, particularly on social issues like enforcing abortion restrictions after the Dobbs v. Jackson decision, but this approach overlooked broader economic discontent in Kentucky, where inflation and stagnant wages impacted working-class voters.[102] [70] Cameron's platform prioritized culture war elements, such as opposition to "woke" policies and transgender issues, over populist appeals to economic reform, failing to exploit voter frustration with high energy costs and manufacturing job losses in a state reliant on coal and autos.[103] This misalignment contributed to limited crossover appeal in a state with a Republican Partisan Voter Index of R+16, where empirical voting patterns from the 2020 presidential election showed Donald Trump winning by 26 points, yet Cameron underperformed relative to that baseline.[104] The campaign also neglected to aggressively highlight Beshear's COVID-19 policies, including statewide school closures ordered in March 2020 and extended through the end of the 2020-2021 academic year, which research indicated had negligible independent effects on reducing case growth rates without accompanying restrictions.[105] These prolonged shutdowns correlated with documented declines in student proficiency, as Kentucky's public schools operated remotely for extended periods, exacerbating learning gaps in reading and math without commensurate public health gains.[106] Cameron's relative silence on this overreach missed an opportunity to tie Beshear's decisions to tangible harms for families in rural and low-income areas, where educational setbacks persisted into 2023 testing data. Internal Republican divisions further hampered unity, with post-election recriminations revealing tensions between Trump-aligned factions, which had propelled Cameron's primary win via endorsement, and McConnell's establishment wing, blamed by Trump for insufficient support in the general election.[107] This factionalism manifested in subdued coordination, contributing to suboptimal voter mobilization in an off-year race lacking national headwinds. Overall turnout reached only 38 percent, down from 2019, with Republicans failing to drive sufficient base enthusiasm despite their registration advantage, relying instead on assumed partisan loyalty that proved insufficient against Beshear's targeted outreach.[87] [88]

Long-term implications for Kentucky and national politics

Beshear's re-election preserved Kentucky's divided government structure, with Republican supermajorities in both legislative chambersâ€”80 seats in the House and 31 in the Senateâ€”ensuring the ability to override the governor's vetoes via simple majority, a threshold met consistently on contentious legislation.[108][109] This dynamic, unchanged from prior sessions, limits Beshear's influence on policy priorities like restrictions on abortion and diversity initiatives, as evidenced by over 100 veto overrides during his tenure, including recent enactments tightening abortion laws and prohibiting certain equity programs despite his objections.[110][111] Such overrides underscore the legislature's dominance on social conservatism, maintaining Kentucky's policy trajectory aligned with its predominantly Republican electorate despite the Democratic executive.[112] The Republican Party in Kentucky responded to Cameron's defeat by initiating internal assessments of campaign strategies, focusing on messaging and voter mobilization for the 2027 cycle, amid acknowledgment that national endorsements and cultural wedge issues underperformed.[113] Cameron's alignment with former President Trump, including endorsements, drew scrutiny post-loss, with analyses attributing part of the shortfall to overemphasis on anti-transgender rhetoric that alienated moderate voters without sufficiently energizing the base.[114] While the defeat halted Cameron's immediate ascent to the governorship, it amplified intra-party debates on balancing Trump-era populism with broader appeals, evidenced by rival Republicans leveraging the outcome to critique reliance on such tactics.[115] Nationally, Beshear's victory did not signal a rejection of Republican conservatism or the MAGA movement, as Kentucky's electorate remains firmly redâ€”having supported Trump by 26 percentage points in 2020â€” with Beshear's success rooted in personal approval ratings exceeding 60% and centrist positioning akin to West Virginia's Joe Manchin, rather than ideological shifts.[116] Claims in left-leaning outlets of an anti-Trump repudiation overlook the state's entrenched Republican leanings and Beshear's crossover appeal, including higher turnout among registered Republicans who split tickets in his favor.[88] The election's low turnout of 38.07%â€”down 6 points from 2019â€”further diminishes its predictive value for higher-stakes presidential contests, highlighting split-ticket persistence in off-year races without broader partisan realignment.[87][117]
