New York's 11th congressional district

New York's 11th congressional district is one of 26 districts in the U.S. House of Representatives apportioned to New York, comprising the entirety of Staten Island and neighborhoods in southwestern Brooklyn including Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, and adjacent areas.[1] The district has a population of 759,734 as of 2024 estimates, with a median age of around 41 and median household income exceeding $89,000, reflecting a mix of suburban and urban communities.[2] It is currently represented by Republican Nicole Malliotakis, who has held the seat since 2021 after defeating incumbent Democrat Max Rose in a closely contested election.[3] The district stands out for its electoral competitiveness amid New York City's overwhelming Democratic lean, largely attributable to Staten Island's conservative electorate, which has supported Republican incumbents in recent cycles despite statewide trends.[4] Following the 2020 census, the district's boundaries underwent significant revision due to state redistricting processes invalidated by courts for partisan gerrymandering that violated constitutional requirements for compactness and contiguity, resulting in maps more closely aligned with neutral criteria drawn under judicial oversight.[5] This R+3 to R+4 leaning district, per empirical voting data, underscores causal factors like demographic concentrations of working-class and ethnic communities favoring limited-government policies over urban progressive platforms predominant elsewhere in the city.[6]

Geography and Composition

Current Boundaries and Areas Covered

New York's 11th congressional district, effective from the 2023 elections following redistricting after the 2020 United States Census, comprises the entire county of Richmondâ€”commonly known as Staten Islandâ€”and portions of Kings County in southwestern Brooklyn.[7][8] The district's configuration resulted from legislative action in April 2022, after the New York Court of Appeals invalidated earlier maps drawn by the state's Independent Redistricting Commission for violating constitutional standards on compactness and contiguity.[9] In Brooklyn, the district includes neighborhoods such as Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Bath Beach, and sections of Bensonhurst, areas characterized by diverse residential communities along the waterfront and near the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge connecting to Staten Island.[10] These boundaries maintain a land area of approximately 64 square miles, with Staten Island forming the bulk of the district's geography, including its urban, suburban, and parkland expanses like the Greenbelt and conference house areas.[7] The district's shape reflects efforts to group communities with shared interests across the boroughs, though it has faced scrutiny for crossing borough lines despite intra-city alternatives.[11] As of the 2024 elections, no further boundary alterations occurred, preserving this delineation for the 119th Congress commencing in 2025. The U.S. Census Bureau delineates the district for statistical purposes using census blocks aligned to these political lines, ensuring precise data tabulation.[7]

Historical Boundary Changes and Redistricting

Following the 1980 census, which resulted in New York losing five congressional seats, the state legislature redrew district lines in 1982, establishing the modern configuration of the 11th district by combining all of Staten Island (Richmond County) with southern Brooklyn neighborhoods including Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, and Bath Beach to create a geographically contiguous district linking the boroughs via the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge.[12] This pairing reflected efforts to balance population while connecting communities with shared suburban characteristics and ferry/bridge access, though it joined politically divergent areasâ€”Staten Island's more conservative electorate with Brooklyn's diverse urban pockets.[13] Subsequent redistricting after the 1990 census in 1992 made minor boundary tweaks to account for population shifts, such as adjusting lines in Brooklyn to equalize district sizes under the one-person, one-vote standard established by Baker v. Carr (1962) and subsequent rulings, but preserved the Staten Island-Brooklyn core without major geographic reconfiguration.[14] The 2000 census prompted another redraw in 2002 by the legislature, which retained the district's essential compositionâ€”all of Staten Island plus Brooklyn sections like Bay Ridge, Dyker Heights, Fort Hamilton, and portions of Gravesendâ€”while fine-tuning for equal population (approximately 654,000 residents per district) and compactness, amid criticisms of incumbent protection in the lines drawn under Democratic control of the state assembly and Republican control of the senate. The 2010 census redistricting process, delayed by partisan deadlock, culminated in a federal court adopting a new map on March 19, 2012, after the legislature failed to agree; for the 11th district, this involved slight expansions in Brooklyn to include more of Sunset Park and adjustments to exclude some waterfront areas, aiming to enhance contiguity and reduce gerrymandering claims while maintaining Staten Island's entirety and the district's R+3 partisan lean at the time.[15][9] Redistricting after the 2020 census proved highly contentious, highlighting New York's shift to an Independent Redistricting Commission (IRC) under 2014 constitutional amendments, which requires the commission to propose maps prioritizing compactness, competitiveness, and communities of interest before legislative approval. The IRC's proposals were rejected twice by the Democrat-controlled legislature, which enacted its own congressional map on February 3, 2022, altering the 11th district by removing some Republican-leaning Brooklyn areas and adding Democratic strongholds like parts of Bensonhurst and Borough Park to dilute Staten Island's conservative influence, potentially shifting the district's Cook Partisan Voting Index from R+8 toward even.[9] This map was invalidated on April 27, 2022, by the New York Supreme Court as an unconstitutional partisan gerrymander violating the state constitution's prohibition on maps drawn predominantly for partisan advantage.[16] A court-appointed special master then proposed replacement lines, adopted on May 21, 2022, which largely restored pre-2022 boundaries for the 11th districtâ€”encompassing Staten Island and southern Brooklyn communities like Bay Ridge and Dyker Heightsâ€”used for the 2022 midterms.[17] On December 12, 2023, the New York Court of Appeals ruled 4-3 that the 2022 special master process deviated from constitutional requirements by allowing excessive legislative amendments, ordering the IRC to redraw maps for the 2024 cycle.[18] The IRC submitted proposals, but after rejection, the legislature passed a revised congressional map on February 28, 2024 (Assembly vote 115-35, Senate 45-18), signed by Governor Kathy Hochul, which for the 11th district refined the 2022 court map by trimming some Brooklyn extensions for greater compactness (e.g., excluding portions of Kensington while retaining core areas like Fort Hamilton) and equalizing population to 761,000, while preserving Staten Island intact and adhering more closely to municipal boundaries to mitigate prior gerrymandering concerns.[6] These changes reflect ongoing tensions between independent processes and legislative influence, with courts repeatedly intervening to enforce neutrality over partisan optimization.[19]

Demographics

Population and Ethnic Breakdown

As of 2023, the population of New York's 11th congressional district stood at 753,792, reflecting a slight decline of 0.761% from the previous year.[20] The district's median age was 40.8, indicating an older demographic profile compared to the national average.[20] The racial and ethnic composition, drawn from American Community Survey estimates, shows a majority non-Hispanic White population at 52.4%, followed by 19.3% non-Hispanic Asian, 6.55% non-Hispanic Black or African American, and 18.6% Hispanic or Latino of any race.[20] Remaining residents include smaller shares identifying with two or more races or other categories, contributing to the district's overall diversity index.[20] This breakdown aligns with the district's geography, encompassing the predominantly White Staten Island borough alongside diverse southern Brooklyn communities such as Bay Ridge and Dyker Heights, where Asian and Hispanic populations are concentrated.[20]

Socioeconomic and Housing Profile

The median household income in New York's 11th congressional district stood at $89,683 in 2023, exceeding the national median of approximately $75,000 but aligning closely with state-level figures around $81,000.[20] Per capita income was reported at $40,738, about 80% of the state average of $48,847.[21] The poverty rate was 14.5%, comparable to New York's statewide rate of 14.2% but roughly 16% above the U.S. average of 12.5%.[21] Educational attainment for residents aged 25 and older shows 84.3% holding a high school diploma or higher, slightly below the state rate of 88.3%.[21] Bachelor's degree attainment lags behind urban centers like Manhattan but reflects a mix of blue-collar and professional occupations prevalent in Staten Island and southern Brooklyn neighborhoods. Employment patterns indicate a labor force oriented toward public sector jobs, construction, healthcare, and retail, with an employment rate of 57.8% of the working-age population.[22] Housing in the district features a homeownership rate of approximately 65-70%, notably higher than New York City's overall 32% but driven by Staten Island's suburban character.[23] The median value of owner-occupied units was $708,500 in recent estimates, reflecting steady appreciation amid limited supply and proximity to Manhattan.[21] Median gross rent averaged $1,750, with increases of about 17% since 2006 outpacing inflation due to demand from commuters and limited multifamily development.[24] Total housing units number around 291,000, with vacancy rates low, contributing to affordability pressures for renters spending over 30% of income on housing.[22]

Political History

Origins and Early Representation (1789â€“1900)

New York's 11th congressional district traces its origins to the reapportionment following the 1800 United States census, which expanded the state's House delegation from 10 to 17 seats, necessitating the creation of additional districts including the 11th. Established for the 1804 elections to the 9th Congress, the district initially encompassed northern upstate counties such as Clinton and Essex, areas characterized by frontier settlements and agricultural economies. Peter Sailly, a Democratic-Republican immigrant from France residing in Plattsburgh, Clinton County, was elected as the inaugural representative, serving from March 4, 1805, to March 3, 1807; his overwhelming victory reflected strong support for the party of Thomas Jefferson in rural districts.[25][26] Sailly declined renomination, leading to the election of Federalist Thomas Ruggles Gold, a Yale-educated lawyer from Madison County, who represented the district in the 11th (1809â€“1811) and 12th (1811â€“1813) Congresses. Gold's tenure occurred amid partisan tensions over issues like the Embargo Act of 1807 and the lead-up to the War of 1812, with Federalists in upstate districts often opposing Democratic-Republican foreign policies. After boundary adjustments post-1810 census, which shifted the district southward to include counties like Saratoga and Oneida, Democratic-Republican John W. Taylor succeeded Gold, holding the seat from 1813 to 1823. Taylor, a prominent figure who twice served as Speaker of the House (1820â€“1821 and 1825â€“1826), advocated for internal improvements and tariff protections benefiting manufacturing interests in the district's evolving economy.[27][28][29] Subsequent decades saw frequent redistricting after each decennial census, altering the district's composition from predominantly rural upstate areas to include parts of the Hudson Valley and Catskills by mid-century. Representation shifted with the rise of new parties; for instance, Zadock Pratt, a Democrat and tannery owner from Greene County, served nonconsecutive terms from 1837 to 1839 and 1843 to 1845, focusing on economic development initiatives like infrastructure funding. The district's boundaries continued to evolve, incorporating counties such as Ulster and Columbia in later 19th-century configurations, reflecting population growth and migration patterns. By 1900, after the 1890 census reapportionment reduced New York's seats slightly, the 11th district had stabilized in eastern New York, represented by Republicans amid the party's national ascendancy post-Civil War. Early representatives generally prioritized local concerns like land grants, trade routes, and opposition to federal overreach, with party affiliations mirroring broader state trends from Democratic-Republican dominance to Whig and Republican influences.[30][31]

20th Century Developments and Shifts

The 11th congressional district underwent significant boundary adjustments following the 1910, 1930, 1950, 1970, and 1990 censuses, reflecting New York City's population dynamics, including urban density in Manhattan and later shifts toward outer boroughs amid suburban migration and seat reductions for the state. Early in the century, the district comprised parts of lower Manhattan, encompassing immigrant-heavy neighborhoods that supported alternating Democratic and Republican incumbents amid machine politics and economic booms. Republican dominance prevailed in the 1920s, aligned with national trends under Presidents Harding and Coolidge, but the Great Depression catalyzed a pivot to Democrats by the 1930s, as urban working-class voters, including recent European immigrants, backed Franklin D. Roosevelt's relief programs and the nascent welfare state.[32] Mid-century redistricting after the 1930 and 1950 censuses relocated the district to central Brooklyn areas, such as Bedford-Stuyvesant, fostering a solidly Democratic profile driven by demographic changes: rising African American populations post-Great Migration and entrenched party machines like Tammany Hall's successors, which delivered patronage and voter mobilization in high-density, low-mobility communities. This era saw uninterrupted Democratic control, with representatives like Edolphus Towns (D) holding the seat from 1983 to 1993, benefiting from 80-90% margins in general elections due to the district's socioeconomic compositionâ€”high poverty rates and reliance on public assistance correlating with support for redistributive policies.[33][34] The 1990 census prompted a pivotal reconfiguration in 1992, when Democratic-controlled state legislators redrew the 11th to pair conservative Staten Island (previously in the Republican-leaning 17th district) with Democratic precincts in southern Brooklyn's Bay Ridge and Dyker Heights, aiming to neutralize Staten Island's GOP strength through vote dilution. This causal maneuver backfired electorally, as higher turnout and ideological cohesion among Staten Island's middle-class, home-owning residentsâ€”less dependent on city welfare and more aligned with fiscal conservatismâ€”enabled Republican Vito Fossella to capture the seat in 1996 and hold it through 2008 with comfortable margins, marking a late-century shift toward competitiveness in an otherwise Democratic state.[14][9]

Post-2000 Realignments and Competitiveness

Following the 2010 census and subsequent redistricting, New York's 11th congressional district underwent a significant reconfiguration, shifting from a predominantly Brooklyn-based safe Democratic seat to one encompassing all of Staten Island (Richmond County) and portions of southern Brooklyn, including Bay Ridge and Dyker Heights. This change, implemented for the 2012 elections, transformed the district's political dynamics by pairing Staten Island's historically Republican-leaning electorateâ€”evidenced by consistent support for Republican presidential candidates, with 56.9% voting Republican in the 2020 presidential electionâ€”with Democratic strongholds in Brooklyn, resulting in heightened competitiveness.[6][35] Prior to redistricting, from 2000 to 2010, the district (then centered in central Brooklyn) was solidly Democratic, with incumbents Major Owens and Yvette Clarke securing victories by margins exceeding 50 percentage points in most cycles.[6] Post-redistricting elections have alternated between narrow Republican holds and occasional Democratic flips, underscoring the district's swing status. In 2012, Republican Michael Grimm narrowly defeated Democrat Mark Murphy, 52.2% to 46.8%, marking the first Republican win in the reconfigured district. Grimm retained the seat in 2014 (54.8%), followed by special election victor Daniel Donovan in 2015 (58.3%) and 2016 (61.6%). Democrat Max Rose flipped it in 2018 amid a national Democratic wave, winning 53.0% to 46.6%, but Republican Nicole Malliotakis reclaimed it in 2020 by 53.1% to 46.8%. Malliotakis expanded margins in subsequent cycles, securing 61.7% in 2022 and 63.8% in 2024.[6] The Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI) reflects this rightward tilt, moving from R+3 in 2018 to R+6 in 2022 and 2024, indicating the district votes roughly 6 points more Republican than the national average in presidential elections.[6]

Detailed Congressional Election Results

In the 2018 general election, Democrat Max Rose defeated incumbent Republican Dan Donovan, marking a Democratic flip in the district amid a national wave favoring Democrats. Rose received 101,823 votes (53.0%), while Donovan garnered 89,441 votes (46.6%), with Green Party candidate Henry Bardel receiving 774 votes (0.4%).[39] The 2020 election saw Republican Nicole Malliotakis unseat Rose in a close contest, with Malliotakis securing 155,608 votes (53.1%) to Rose's 137,198 (46.9%).[40] This outcome reflected stronger Republican turnout in Staten Island offsetting Democratic strength in Brooklyn portions, amid broader suburban shifts toward the GOP. Malliotakis won reelection decisively in 2022 against Rose in a rematch, capturing 113,485 votes (62.1%) to Rose's 69,241 (37.9%).[41] The expanded margin aligned with Republican gains in the district during the midterm cycle. In 2024, Malliotakis again prevailed over Democrat Andrea Morse, receiving 153,105 votes to Morse's 93,586, yielding approximately 62.1% to 37.9% of valid votes cast.[42]

Performance in Presidential and Statewide Races

New York's 11th congressional district, encompassing Staten Island and portions of southern Brooklyn, has exhibited a consistent Republican tilt in presidential elections since at least 2000, diverging markedly from the statewide Democratic majorities. In the 2024 presidential election, Republican Donald Trump secured approximately 64% of the vote in Staten Island (Richmond County), the district's population core, against Democrat Kamala Harris's 35%, marking Trump's third consecutive victory there with an expanded margin.[43] Similarly, in 2020, Trump won Richmond County with 56.1% to Joe Biden's 42.3%, a result reflective of the district's overall partisan voting index (PVI) leaning Republican by about 3 points relative to national averages based on that cycle.[35] In 2016, Trump's margin was even wider at 62.9% to Hillary Clinton's 31.4% in the county, underscoring the area's resistance to Democratic nominees amid New York's 23-point Biden statewide win in 2020.[35]

Current Dynamics and Issues

Profile of Incumbent Nicole Malliotakis

Nicole Malliotakis, born on November 11, 1980, in Manhattan, New York, is the Republican U.S. Representative for New York's 11th congressional district, serving since January 3, 2021.[46] The daughter of a Greek immigrant father and a Cuban mother who fled Fidel Castro's regime, Malliotakis grew up on Staten Island, graduating from New Dorp High School in 1998 and earning a B.A. in communications from Seton Hall University in 2003.[46] [47] Prior to her political career, she worked in public affairs, including as director of community relations for a Staten Island assemblyman and in government relations roles focused on community advocacy.[48] Malliotakis entered elective office in 2016, winning election to the New York State Assembly for the 64th district, where she served three terms until 2020.[46] In the Assembly, she prioritized reducing property taxes, improving infrastructure resilienceâ€”particularly after Superstorm Sandyâ€”and opposing policies she viewed as enabling illegal immigration, such as New York City's sanctuary status.[47] Her 2020 congressional bid succeeded in flipping the 11th district, defeating one-term Democratic incumbent Max Rose with 53.3% of the vote amid voter concerns over COVID-19 lockdowns and crime.[49] She secured reelection in 2022 and again in 2024 against Democrat Andrea Morse, with the district's Republican lean solidified by its heavily Greek-American and Italian-American Staten Island base.[50] In the 119th Congress, Malliotakis holds assignments on the House Committee on Financial Services, the Select Subcommittee on the Weaponization of the Federal Government, and the House Committee on Homeland Security, emphasizing oversight of federal overreach and border enforcement.[3] Her legislative record reflects fiscal conservatism, including support for spending cuts and opposition to expansive regulatory measures, as seen in her votes against major omnibus appropriations bills exceeding $1 trillion.[51] She has advocated for election integrity reforms, animal welfare protections, and enhanced emergency response capabilities tailored to coastal vulnerabilities in her district.[52] Malliotakis remains the sole Republican representing any part of New York City in Congress, positioning her as a bridge between suburban conservatives and urban moderates on issues like public safety and economic recovery.[53]

Key Policy Positions and District Priorities

Congresswoman Nicole Malliotakis has emphasized public safety as a core priority, reflecting the district's concerns over rising crime rates in New York City. She has introduced legislation such as the Right to Remain Safe Act of 2021, which conditions federal grants on cities maintaining full police funding levels, and the Prosecutors Need to Prosecute Act to enhance accountability for district attorneys declining to pursue cases.[54][55] Malliotakis opposes cashless bail policies and has secured endorsements from all New York City police unions, arguing that state reforms have exacerbated violent crime in areas like Staten Island.[56] District voters have highlighted crime as a top issue in recent elections, with Malliotakis campaigning on restoring law enforcement resources amid perceptions of leniency under local Democratic leadership.[57] On immigration and border security, Malliotakis advocates for stricter enforcement, including full funding for Customs and Border Protection and resumption of policies like Remain in Mexico. She has criticized New York City's sanctuary policies, blasting refusals to cooperate with ICE raids and opposing migrant shelters in residential neighborhoods, which she links to strained local resources in the district.[58][52] Cosponsoring bills like H.R. 4828 for border wall construction and H.R. 1259 to reinstate expedited removals, her stance aligns with district priorities amid the influx of over 200,000 migrants to NYC since 2022, burdening Staten Island's infrastructure and public services.[56] In economic policy, Malliotakis supports tax relief measures tailored to middle-class families and small businesses in the district, including expanding no-tax on overtime pay via bipartisan legislation introduced in September 2025 and increasing the state and local tax (SALT) deduction cap, which disproportionately affects high-tax New Yorkers.[59][60] She voted against 336 tax hikes in the state assembly and backs extensions of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, opposing expansive spending plans like Build Back Better for inflating costs.[52] District priorities include job creation and fiscal restraint, with constituents facing elevated living expenses and commuting challenges via the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge.[61] Malliotakis also prioritizes veterans' affairs and transportation infrastructure, securing over $10 billion in federal funding through the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law for NYC transit, including Staten Island Ferry enhancements, while sponsoring bills like H.R. 2475 for disabled veterans' HOV lane access.[52] She opposes congestion pricing tolls, arguing they burden working-class commuters without alleviating traffic, and has pushed for federal intervention to block the program.[62] These positions address longstanding district needs for reliable ferry service, bridge maintenance, and support for the area's significant veteran population, estimated at over 10,000 in Staten Island alone.[61]

Major Controversies and Criticisms

The redistricting following the 2020 census generated substantial controversy for New York's 11th congressional district, as the Democratic-majority state legislature in February 2022 proposed maps merging conservative Staten Island with liberal Brooklyn neighborhoods like Park Slope to diminish Republican voting power.[63] This configuration was ruled an unconstitutional partisan gerrymander by the New York Court of Appeals on April 28, 2022, in Harkenrider v. Hochul, for disregarding the 2014 constitutional mandate for an Independent Redistricting Commission and instead prioritizing Democratic gains.[64] A federal court then appointed a special master to draw remedial maps emphasizing compactness and population equality, which preserved the district's Staten Island core with southern Brooklyn portions and enabled incumbent Republican Nicole Malliotakis to win reelection in November 2022 by 5.5 percentage points.[64] Republicans and good-government advocates criticized the initial Democratic maps as a deliberate subversion of neutral processes to eliminate GOP-leaning seats, eroding public trust in electoral fairness.[64] Malliotakis has encountered partisan attacks on her voting record, with progressive critics citing her "no" votes on measures like the Women's Health Protection Act of 2022 (H.R. 8296, passed July 15, 2022), which sought to codify broad abortion access, and the Assault Weapons Ban of 2022 (H.R. 1808, voted on July 29, 2022), amid heightened post-Dobbs scrutiny.[65] Such positions, alongside early skepticism toward COVID-19 vaccine mandates, have been lambasted by Democratic operatives as extreme, though supporters argue they reflect constituent priorities in a district that favored Donald Trump by 11 points in 2020.[66] Conversely, her support for the November 2021 bipartisan infrastructure investment law, which allocated over $550 billion for new spending on roads, bridges, and broadband, provoked backlash from conservative activists, who flooded her office with complaints viewing it as excessive federal expenditure.[67] Financial disclosures drew further scrutiny in 2023â€“2024, when Malliotakis reported purchasing $1,001â€“$15,000 in New York Community Bancorp stock on March 17, 2023â€”days after Ways and Means Committee briefings on banking instabilityâ€”yielding a 32% gain by March 20 amid regional bank acquisitions.[68] Democratic challenger Andrea Morse demanded an ethics probe, alleging exploitation of nonpublic information, but the Office of Congressional Ethics cleared her of violations that March, with her office dismissing the claims as politically motivated slander.[68] Additionally, her January 6, 2021, objections to certifying Arizona and Pennsylvania's electoral votesâ€”joining 147 Republicans despite condemning the Capitol riotâ€”prompted Democratic accusations of hypocrisy and election denialism, potentially alienating moderate Brooklyn voters while bolstering Staten Island support.[69]
