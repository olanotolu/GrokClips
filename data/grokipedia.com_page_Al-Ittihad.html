Al-Ittihad

Al-Ittihad Club (Arabic: نادي الاتحاد, Nadi Al-Ittihad, meaning "The Union Club") is a professional multi-sport association based in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, founded on 26 December 1927 by a group of football enthusiasts including Hamza Fitaihi, Abdel Samad Najib Al-Saadi, and Abdulaziz Jameel.[1][2] As the oldest continuously operating sports club in the Kingdom, it pioneered inclusive participation in athletics during an era of limited formal organization, defeating rival Al-Hijazi Sports Club in 1932 and introducing ticketing systems that same year.[2] The club fields teams across various disciplines but is predominantly renowned for its men's football squad, which competes in the Saudi Pro League and has amassed a record of sustained excellence, including 14 domestic league titles and seven King's Cup victories.[3] Al-Ittihad's football team achieved continental prominence by winning the AFC Champions League in consecutive years, 2004 and 2005, marking the first such back-to-back success for a Saudi club and securing fourth place in the 2005 FIFA Club World Cup.[1][2] Domestically, it has dominated through strategic development and, more recently, substantial investments following its partial privatization in June 2023, when Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund acquired a 75% stake, enabling high-profile signings and back-to-back Saudi Pro League titles in 2023 and the 2024–25 season, alongside a domestic double in 2025.[1][2] The club's emblematic yellow-and-black colors and fervent supporter base underscore its status as a cornerstone of Saudi sporting identity, though it has occasionally faced disciplinary actions for fan and player conduct, such as fines for misconduct during matches.[4]

Primary Sports Club: Al-Ittihad Jeddah

Foundation and Early History

Al-Ittihad Club, based in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, was established on December 26, 1927, by a group of football enthusiasts seeking to promote sports and unity in the community.[2][1] The founders included Hamza Abdullah Fitaihi, Abdul Samad Najeeb, Abdulaziz Jameel, Abdullatif Jameel, Mohammed Saleh Mohammed Salama, Abdullah bin Zaqr, and Abdulrazzaq Mohammed Ajlan, among others, who gathered near the city's telegraph office to formalize the club as the first organized football entity in the region.[2] Predating the unification of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia by five years, Al-Ittihad emerged in an era of limited formal sports infrastructure, emphasizing inclusivity and entertainment for Jeddah residents under the name "The Union."[5][6] In its formative years, the club focused primarily on football, organizing matches against local teams in informal regional tournaments amid scarce resources and rudimentary facilities. A notable early milestone occurred in May 1932, when Al-Ittihad secured a 3-0 victory over Al-Hijazi Sports in a highly anticipated fixture, marking one of the club's first documented competitive successes and drawing significant crowds.[2] This match also introduced ticketing to Saudi football, as advertised in the Sawt Al-Hijaz newspaper on May 8, 1932, reflecting growing public interest and the club's role in popularizing the sport.[2] Through the 1930s and 1940s, Al-Ittihad built a dedicated fanbase by participating in Jeddah-based competitions, fostering rivalries such as the eventual "Old Derby" with Al-Wehda, while expanding to include other sports like basketball and volleyball as multi-disciplinary activities gained traction.[7] By the 1950s, the club had solidified its status as Saudi Arabia's oldest surviving sports entity, transitioning from ad-hoc games to more structured regional play that laid the groundwork for national involvement. Early successes in local cups and tournaments during this decade enhanced its reputation, culminating in participation in the inaugural King's Cup editions starting in 1957, where it competed against emerging national rivals.[5][8] These years underscored Al-Ittihad's resilience, operating continuously despite political and infrastructural challenges, and establishing yellow-and-black as its enduring colors symbolizing unity and ambition.[1]

Football Department: Achievements and Records

Al-Ittihad's football department has secured 10 Saudi Pro League titles, establishing it as one of the competition's most decorated clubs, with victories spanning from the inaugural national season to recent dominance.[9] The club also holds records in domestic cup competitions, including 7 King's Cup wins and 8 Crown Prince Cup triumphs, contributing to a total of over 30 major domestic honors. Internationally, Al-Ittihad stands out as the first Saudi club to win the AFC Champions League, achieving back-to-back titles in 2004 and 2005, and maintaining an unbeaten run in key campaigns that underscored its Asian prowess.[3][10]

In continental competitions, Al-Ittihad's two AFC Champions League victories remain a benchmark, with the 2004 triumph featuring a 5–0 aggregate semifinal win over BEC Tero Sasana and a final penalty shootout against Seongnam Ilhwa Chunma, followed by the 2005 defense marked by tactical discipline under coach Anghel Iordănescu.[3] The club also claimed the 1999 Asian Cup Winners' Cup, defeating Verdy Kawasaki 3–2 in the final, and the 2005 Arab Champions League. Records include the largest margin in a Pro League match, an 8–0 victory over Al-Qadisiya on March 3, 2007, during Crown Prince Cup play.[3][12] Recent successes, such as the 2022–23 Pro League title bolstered by high-profile signings like Karim Benzema, highlight sustained competitiveness, though the club has faced challenges in maintaining consistency post-2009 until the mid-2020s resurgence.[5]

Other Sports Departments

Al-Ittihad Club operates departments in multiple sports beyond football, encompassing basketball, volleyball, handball, tennis, swimming, athletics, weightlifting, cycling, chess, and electronic games.[1] The club supports women's sections in athletics, weightlifting, and basketball, aligning with broader efforts to promote female participation in Saudi sports.[1] The basketball department fields a professional team that competes in domestic leagues and international competitions, including the West Asia Super League.[13] Historically, it earned third place at the 1998 Arab Club Basketball Championship and advanced to the quarterfinals of the 1999 ABC Champions Cup, where it lost 55-64 to Sagesse after topping its preliminary group with a 2-1 record.[14] In volleyball, the team achieved silver medals in the 2024-25 Saudi Volleyball Premier League, finishing second to Al-Hilal, and also secured silver in a national volleyball trophy event organized by the Saudi Arabian Volleyball Federation.[15][16] The handball department participates in the Saudi Handball League, contributing to the club's multi-sport framework since the club's founding in 1927.[17] Departments in tennis, swimming, athletics, weightlifting, cycling, chess, and electronic games maintain active programs, though specific competitive records in these areas remain less documented in public sources compared to team sports.[1]

Ownership, Management, and Recent Developments

Al-Ittihad Club is jointly owned by the Public Investment Fund (PIF) of Saudi Arabia and the Ittihad Non-Profit Foundation, with PIF holding a 75% controlling stake following the club's transformation into a corporate entity on June 5, 2023.[1][18] In July 2025, 75% of the club's ownership was transferred to the Jeddah Central Development Company (JCDC), a strategic partner aimed at enhancing local development and club operations in Jeddah.[19][20] The club's board of directors is chaired by Fahd Hamza Cynndy, a Saudi executive with over 25 years in aviation, services, and investments, alongside members including Abdullah Hamad Alhussaini, Fares Salah Alhejailan, Suhaib Marwan Jamjoom, Abdullah Yousef Alghamdi, and Abdulaziz Salem.[21] Executive leadership includes CEO Domingos Soares de Oliveira, appointed in September 2023 to oversee commercial and sporting strategies, with a focus on sustainable growth and infrastructure.[22][23] The football department's management features Sporting Director Ramón Planes, who has driven player acquisitions such as long-term contracts for Jan-Carlo Simic and Roger Fernandes extending to 2029.[24] In recent developments, Al-Ittihad secured a domestic double in the 2024–25 season by winning the Saudi Pro League title—their 10th—for the second time in three years after defeating Al-Raed 3–1, alongside the King's Cup.[25][26] The club parted ways with manager Laurent Blanc and appointed Sérgio Conceição on October 2, 2025, to bolster competitive performance amid the Saudi Pro League's global investments.[27] Additionally, the basketball department competed in the FIBA West Asia Super League (WASL) for the 2024–25 season, positioning the club as a multi-sport contender.[28]

Other Sports Clubs

Football Clubs

Al-Ittihad Alexandria Club, based in Alexandria, Egypt, was founded in 1914 and is among the oldest football clubs in the country.[29] The club has secured the Egyptian Cup on five occasions, with victories in the 1935–36, 1947–48, 1962–63, 1972–73, and 1975–76 seasons.[30] Al-Ittihad Sporting, Cultural and Social Club of Tripoli, Libya, established on July 29, 1944, is one of the nation's most successful football teams, competing in the Libyan Premier League.[31] It plays home matches at Tripoli Stadium and has a history of dominance in domestic competitions, though specific title counts vary by reporting; it is recognized as a prominent club with multiple league and cup successes.[32] Al-Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo Sports Club, located in Aleppo, Syria, was formed in 1949 and holds a significant place in Syrian football as a multi-title winner in the Syrian Premier League.[33] The club has competed actively in recent seasons, including matches in the 2025 Syrian Premier League, demonstrating ongoing competitiveness despite regional challenges.[34] Kalba Football Club, commonly referred to as Al-Ittihad Kalba and based in Kalba, United Arab Emirates, originated in 1972 and participates in the UAE Pro League. It maintains a professional setup with recent fixtures in the league, such as a 0–0 draw against Shabab Al-Ahli Dubai in October 2025.[35]

Basketball and Other Teams

Al-Ittihad Alexandria, based in Alexandria, Egypt, fields a professional basketball team that competes in the Egyptian Basketball Premier League. The basketball section of the club was established in 1982, with team colors of green and white.[36] The team has gained regional prominence through participation in the Basketball Africa League (BAL), where it achieved the first unbeaten regular season record in league history with a 6-0 mark during the 2025 campaign, culminating in a 98-74 victory over FUS Rabat.[37][38] In Syria, Al-Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo operates a highly successful men's basketball program as part of its multi-sport club, founded in 1951 with colors of red, white, and black.[39] The team holds 17 Syrian League titles and 10 Syrian Cup victories, establishing it as one of the country's most decorated basketball clubs.[40] It has competed internationally in the FIBA West Asia Super League, reaching the Final 8 stage and qualifying via strong domestic performances, such as a runner-up finish in the 2022-23 Syrian Basketball League.[28][41] Al-Ittihad Tripoli in Libya maintains a basketball team within its sports club framework, established in 1944, and participates in the Libyan Basketball League.[42] The program features international players and competes domestically, though specific championship records are less extensively documented compared to counterparts in Egypt and Syria.[43] Beyond basketball, various Al-Ittihad-named clubs engage in other sports such as volleyball and handball, often as part of broader multi-sport associations in Arab countries, but these sections receive less international attention than their basketball counterparts.[38]

Newspapers and Media Outlets

Al-Ittihad (United Arab Emirates)

Al-Ittihad is an Arabic-language daily newspaper published in the United Arab Emirates, recognized as the country's first and oldest such publication. Its inaugural issue appeared on 20 October 1969, amid Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan's unification efforts for the emirates that would form the UAE.[44] Initially issued as a 12-page weekly with a circulation of 5,500 copies distributed free of charge to compete with established regional papers, it quickly established itself as a national voice.[45] Fully owned and operated by Abu Dhabi Media Company (ADMC), a state entity under the Abu Dhabi Media Network, Al-Ittihad functions as a primary outlet for official UAE perspectives on domestic and international affairs, including politics, economy, sports, culture, and society.[46] By the 1970s, following the UAE's formation in 1971, it expanded to daily publication with 32 pages, prioritizing comprehensive coverage aligned with national development narratives.[44] As state media, its editorial stance reflects government priorities, often emphasizing unity, progress, and Sheikh Zayed's legacy, though it maintains journalistic standards in reporting verifiable events.[45] Technologically, Al-Ittihad achieved milestones such as becoming the first UAE newspaper to launch an online edition in 1996 and adopting satellite printing—the first in the Arab world—to enable broader distribution.[44][47] It now offers digital access via its website and maintains an English-language section for wider reach, focusing on UAE-centric news while upholding its role in shaping public discourse within the federation's controlled media environment.[48]

Al-Ittihad (Israel)

Al-Ittihad is an Arabic-language newspaper published in Haifa, Israel, serving as the official organ of the Maki (Communist Party of Israel).[49] Founded on May 14, 1944, during the British Mandate for Palestine, it began as a weekly publication associated with the National Liberation League, a group advocating Arab-Jewish unity and opposition to both Zionism and partition plans.[50] [51] The newspaper was established by key figures including Emile Touma, Tawfiq Toubi, Fuad Nassar, and Emile Habibi, who positioned it as a platform for leftist Palestinian and Jewish activists aligned with the Palestine Communist Party.[52] Following the 1948 Arab-Israeli War and the establishment of Israel, Al-Ittihad transitioned in September 1948 to become the primary Arabic outlet for the newly reorganized Israeli Communist Party (Maki), reflecting the party's binational composition of Arab and Jewish members.[50] Under Touma's editorship until his death in 2010, it maintained a focus on workers' rights, anti-imperialism, and criticism of Israeli government policies toward Arab citizens, while fostering cultural output among Palestinian writers in Israel.[53] The paper expanded to daily publication by 1983, achieving a circulation of approximately 12,000 copies at that time, though exact current figures are unavailable.[54] As Israel's oldest continuously operating Arabic-language newspaper, Al-Ittihad has played a distinctive role in the media landscape for Arab Israelis, distinguishing itself from state-influenced outlets by its independence under Maki ownership and its advocacy for class-based solidarity over nationalist divisions.[49] [50] It has historically covered labor struggles, Palestinian refugee issues, and cultural revival, including poetry and literature from figures like Emile Habibi, though its communist ideological framework—rooted in Soviet-influenced Marxism—has drawn criticism for aligning with authoritarian regimes and downplaying certain Arab nationalist concerns.[55] The newspaper maintains an online presence via its website, launched in digital format by Maki in 2019, continuing to publish articles on domestic Israeli politics, regional conflicts, and party positions.[49] [56]

Political and Militant Organizations

Al-Itihaad al-Islamiya (Somalia)

Al-Itihaad al-Islamiya (AIAI), also known as the Islamic Union, emerged in Somalia between 1982 and 1984 as a merger of smaller Islamist factions seeking to overthrow the secular Siad Barre regime and establish an Islamic state governed by strict sharia law.[57] The group drew ideological inspiration from Salafi-jihadist principles, emphasizing armed struggle against perceived apostate governments and foreign influences, while rejecting clan-based politics in favor of transnational Islamic unity.[58] Its early activities focused on clandestine operations, including assassinations and bombings targeting Somali officials, amid the regime's weakening grip amid civil war.[59] Following the collapse of the Barre regime in 1991, AIAI rapidly expanded, becoming Somalia's largest militant Islamist organization by controlling key ports and southern territories such as the Gedo region near the Ethiopian border.[59] The group imposed sharia courts and taxes in these areas, funding operations through extortion, smuggling, and donations from Gulf-based charities linked to al-Qaeda networks.[60] Notable leaders included Hassan Dahir Aweys, who directed military efforts, and Hassan Abdullah Hersi al-Turki, who oversaw Ethiopian-border operations.[61] [62] AIAI conducted cross-border raids into Ethiopia starting in 1996, including bombings in border towns that killed dozens, prompting Ethiopian counteroffensives that expelled the group from major strongholds by 1997.[63] AIAI maintained operational ties to al-Qaeda, providing safe haven for training camps and facilitating fund transfers via hawala systems and entities like the Revival of Islamic Heritage Society, which U.S. authorities designated for supporting AIAI's activities.[60] [64] By the early 2000s, internal fractures and military defeats fragmented the organization; many cadres integrated into the Islamic Courts Union (ICU) in 2006, which briefly controlled Mogadishu before Ethiopian-TFG intervention dismantled it.[65] Elements of AIAI later merged into Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen, absorbing its jihadist expertise and operatives.[66] Designated a terrorist entity by the UN in 2001 under al-Qaeda sanctions resolutions and by the U.S. via Executive Order 13224, AIAI's core structure has been dormant since the mid-2000s, with no independent major operations reported as of 2024.[57] [67] Remaining loyalists operate marginally within al-Shabaab, which has eclipsed AIAI as Somalia's primary al-Qaeda affiliate, though AIAI's sanctions persist due to its historical role in regional jihadist networks.[68][69]

Geographical and Miscellaneous Uses

Al-Ittihad (Ramallah Locality)

Al-Ittihad is a Palestinian municipality located in the Ramallah and al-Bireh Governorate of the West Bank, approximately 12.5 kilometers northwest of Ramallah city center.[70] Formed in 1997 through the administrative merger of the local councils of Beitillu, Deir 'Ammar, and Jammala, the municipality derives its name from the Arabic term for "the union," signifying this consolidation into a single governing entity.[70] The area falls under Area B of the Oslo Accords, granting the Palestinian Authority responsibility for civil administration while Israel maintains authority over security matters. Geographically, Al-Ittihad occupies hilly terrain typical of the central West Bank highlands, with elevations ranging around 528 meters above sea level. Coordinates place it at approximately 31°58'31"N latitude and 35°06'57"E longitude, bordered by localities such as Kobar and Al-Zaytouneh to the east.[71] The region features agricultural lands and natural wadis, including the Beitillu Nature Reserve (also known as Wadi Janatah), a protected area 19 kilometers north of Ramallah emphasizing biodiversity conservation and ecotourism within the municipality's boundaries.[72] Demographically, the population consists primarily of Palestinian Arabs, with the combined localities of Beitillu, Deir 'Ammar, and Jammala supporting communities engaged in agriculture, olive cultivation, and limited local services.[70] Early post-merger estimates indicated around 6,800 residents, though updated figures reflect ongoing growth patterns in the governorate amid constraints from regional security and land use restrictions.[70] The municipality manages basic infrastructure, including roads and water access, in a context of fragmented territorial control.
