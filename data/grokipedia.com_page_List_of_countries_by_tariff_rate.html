List of countries by tariff rate

A list of countries by tariff rate enumerates sovereign states and certain territories according to the average customs duties levied on imported goods, with the weighted mean applied tariff rate for all products serving as the standard metric. This rate computes the effective ad valorem tax on imports by multiplying each product's tariff by its import value, aggregating these values, and dividing by total import value, thereby capturing the trade-distorting impact proportional to actual commerce flows.[1][2] Data for such lists are principally drawn from WTO tariff schedules and notifications, supplemented by World Bank compilations via the WITS database, offering empirically grounded assessments of applied policies distinct from bound commitments that set legal ceilings.[3][4] Lower rates, often under 2% in integrated markets like the European Union, signal commitments to trade liberalization, whereas elevated averages above 10% in select developing economies reflect deliberate barriers to shield nascent sectors from import competition.[5][2] Tariffs function as fiscal instruments for revenue and as tools to address perceived asymmetries in market access, yet empirical evidence links sustained high rates to diminished allocative efficiency, elevated consumer costs, and retaliatory cycles that erode mutual gains from specializationâ€”effects most pronounced absent compensatory domestic reforms.[6][7]

Definitions and Measurement

Types of Tariff Rates

Tariff rates are aggregated using either simple averages or weighted averages to summarize protection levels across product lines. The simple average tariff represents the unweighted arithmetic mean of tariff rates applied to individual products or tariff lines, treating each equally regardless of trade volume.[8] This method provides a straightforward measure of nominal protection but can overstate economic significance if high tariffs apply to low-volume imports. In contrast, the weighted average tariff adjusts for import volumes, calculating the mean rate where each product's tariff is weighted by its share of total imports, yielding the effective rate of duty per unit of import value.[8] [9] This approach more accurately reflects the actual economic burden on trade flows, as it diminishes the influence of tariffs on negligible imports while emphasizing those on high-value goods, often resulting in lower figures than simple averages due to concentrated trade in low-tariff sectors.[9] Countries distinguish between applied tariffs, which are the rates actually charged on imports, and bound tariffs, which constitute the maximum levels committed under World Trade Organization (WTO) agreements. Applied tariffs can vary below these bindings based on domestic policy, allowing flexibility for revenue or protective needs without violating international obligations.[10] [11] Bound tariffs serve as ceilings negotiated during WTO rounds, enforceable through dispute settlement if exceeded, and differ by product and country to accommodate varying sensitivities.[12] [10] The gap, or "tariff water," between bound and applied rates provides policy space but complicates cross-country comparisons, as bindings reflect negotiated maxima rather than current practice.[13] Most-favored-nation (MFN) rates form the standard baseline for tariffs on imports from WTO members absent preferential agreements. Under the MFN principle, a country must extend the lowest available tariff treatment to all like products from any WTO partner, preventing discriminatory bilateral favoritism.[14] [12] MFN applied rates thus capture non-preferential duties, while preferential rates under free trade agreements or regional pacts may apply lower levels to specific partners, underscoring the need to specify MFN in analyses for consistent, apples-to-apples evaluations of baseline protection.[12] [15] This distinction ensures rankings prioritize comparable multilateral commitments over bilateral exceptions.

Data Sources and Methodologies

The primary sources for tariff rate data include the World Trade Organization's (WTO) World Tariff Profiles, which compile applied and bound tariff information for over 170 countries and territories based on official notifications and partner country data from 2023 and 2024.[16] These profiles calculate metrics such as simple and weighted average tariffs using Harmonized System (HS) classifications at the six-digit level, drawing from WTO integrated databases and supplemented by International Trade Centre (ITC) and UNCTAD inputs for non-members.[17] The WTO's Tariff & Trade Data platform, enhanced since 2023, offers interactive access to annual tariff and import statistics for more than 150 economies from 1996 onward, facilitating queries on most-favored-nation (MFN) and applied rates while incorporating trade flow weights for accuracy.[10] The World Bank's World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) aggregates tariff data from WTO, UNCTAD, and national sources to compute applied weighted mean tariffs, defined as the import-share-weighted average of effectively applied rates across products and partners.[2] WITS emphasizes empirical rigor by linking tariffs to bilateral trade volumes, enabling adjustments for preferential treatments under free trade agreements (FTAs), though it relies on self-reported schedules that may vary in timeliness across countries.[18] UNCTAD's Trade Analysis and Information System (TRAINS) provides granular HS-code-level tariff data for over 170 economies, serving as a foundational input for WTO and WITS calculations, with methodologies focused on verifiable legal texts and applied rates rather than bound commitments.[19] TRAINS supports detailed analysis by integrating tariff lines with import statistics, but its coverage depends on periodic updates from national authorities. Key limitations across these sources include reporting lags of 1-2 years, as 2025 datasets often reflect 2023 trade flows due to notification cycles and data validation processes.[17] Tariff figures exclude non-tariff barriers (NTBs) such as quotas or standards, which WTO and UNCTAD track separately but do not quantify in ad valorem equivalents within standard tariff averages.[20] Effective applied rates incorporate FTA preferences, potentially understating MFN exposures, and discrepancies arise from differing HS revisions or incomplete partner data, necessitating cross-verification for precision.[2]

Historical Context

Early 20th Century Protectionism

In the early decades of the 20th century, protectionist tariff regimes dominated major economies, with average ad valorem rates on dutiable imports typically ranging from 20% to 50% across industrialized nations such as the United States, Germany, and France.[21] These levels marked a continuation of late-19th-century policies, where tariffs evolved from primary fiscal instrumentsâ€”generating up to 90% of U.S. federal revenue prior to the 16th Amendment's ratification in 1913â€”to overt barriers shielding nascent industries from international competition.[22][23] By prioritizing domestic producers over consumer access to lower-cost imports, such measures inherently disrupted comparative advantages, channeling resources into less efficient sectors and curtailing gains from specialization.[24] Empirical records reveal these high barriers correlated with subdued economic expansion relative to post-1945 liberalization eras, as fragmented markets limited scale economies and innovation diffusion. For example, U.S. tariffs averaged approximately 40% from 1900 to 1913, coinciding with periods of industrial consolidation but also vulnerability to external shocks, while European averages similarly constrained intra-continental trade flows.[25] Protectionism's causal mechanism operated through elevated domestic prices, reduced import competition, and retaliatory cycles that eroded export opportunities, fostering a beggar-thy-neighbor dynamic evident in pre-World War I escalations.[26] The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of June 17, 1930, epitomized this trend's apex in the United States, elevating average duties on dutiable imports to nearly 60% across over 20,000 items and prompting reciprocal hikes in two dozen countries.[27] Retaliation amplified the policy's contractionary effects, with global trade volume contracting by 66% from 1929 to 1934 amid cascading export declines and deepened deflationary pressures during the Great Depression.[28] This episode underscored tariffs' role in propagating stagnation, as diminished trade volumesâ€”falling over 60% in value termsâ€”directly impeded recovery by contracting aggregate demand and industrial output across interdependent economies.[24]

Post-WWII Liberalization

The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), established in 1947, initiated a series of multilateral negotiations aimed at reducing trade barriers, starting from average tariff levels of approximately 22% among major participating countries.[29] Through eight rounds of talks from 1947 to 1994, GATT facilitated progressive tariff cuts, particularly on industrial goods, correlating with an expansion in global trade volumes that grew at an average annual rate of around 8% during this period.[30] These reductions were achieved via reciprocal concessions, enabling countries to specialize according to comparative advantages, which from first principles enhances productive efficiency and resource allocation across borders.[31] The Kennedy Round (1964-1967) marked a significant milestone, achieving an average tariff reduction of about 35% on industrial products among developed nations, building on prior cuts that had already lowered averages substantially from postwar highs.[32] Subsequent Tokyo Round (1973-1979) negotiations further trimmed tariffs, though less dramatically, while the Uruguay Round (1986-1994) halved average industrial tariffs for developed countries to around 3.8-5%, with bindings ensuring permanence against future hikes.[33] Developing countries participated with shallower cuts, reflecting their higher initial rates, yet these rounds laid the groundwork for broader liberalization, as evidenced by the decline in global simple average tariffs from over 15% in the early 1990s to approximately 7% by 2010.[2] The transition to the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995 formalized these commitments, with developed members binding most tariffs below 5% and developing nations maintaining higher but progressively declining bound rates.[33] This framework supported export-led growth in regions like East Asia, where reduced barriers allowed integration into global supply chains, empirically linking lower tariffs to poverty reduction through increased wages and employment in tradable sectorsâ€”lifting over a billion people out of extreme poverty since the 1980s via enhanced specialization and market access.[34] Causally, such liberalization mitigated domestic inefficiencies by exposing producers to competition, fostering innovation and scale economies unattainable under protectionism.[31]

21st Century Shifts and Resurgence

Following decades of post-World War II liberalization, global weighted mean applied tariff rates for all products dipped to approximately 3-4% by the mid-2010s, as reported in World Bank and WTO datasets reflecting multilateral agreements and regional trade pacts.[2] [35] This trend began reversing after the 2008 financial crisis, with modest protectionist measures in response to economic slowdowns, but accelerated markedly from 2018 amid escalating bilateral disputes. World Bank indicators document a slight global uptick in average applied rates post-2018, driven by retaliatory actions that disrupted the prior 70-year decline toward freer trade.[2] A pivotal shift occurred with the US-China trade war initiated in 2018, when the United States imposed tariffs on over $300 billion in Chinese goods, including 25% duties on $50 billion in industrial products and 10-25% on subsequent lists totaling $200 billion more, elevating the trade-weighted average US tariff on Chinese imports to about 19%.[36] [37] China responded with retaliatory tariffs averaging 16.6 percentage points on 84% of US product categories, affecting roughly $110 billion in American exports.[38] These measures exemplified broader retaliatory spirals, as seen in earlier steel sector disputes: in 2002, the US applied 8-30% tariffs on imported steel, prompting EU countermeasures and WTO challenges; a similar pattern emerged in 2018 with US 25% steel tariffs eliciting EU retaliatory duties on $3 billion of US products like bourbon and motorcycles.[39] [40] By 2025, US tariffs on Chinese exports had escalated further under renewed policy directives, reaching an average of 57.6% across 100% of goods, including 10% baseline increases effective February and additional hikes to 100% on specific shipments announced in October.[36] [41] Developing economies, meanwhile, often retained persistently elevated rates amid these tensions; for instance, while global averages fell, countries like Lesotho faced disputed claims of up to 99% tariffs on certain US goods, contributing to reciprocal US actions peaking at 50-60% before partial reductions to 15%.[42] [41] These dynamics marked a resurgence of protectionism, with bilateral escalations offsetting prior multilateral gains and prompting reevaluation of WTO-bound commitments.[10]

Current Data and Rankings

Weighted Average Applied Tariffs (Latest Figures)

The weighted average applied tariff rate measures the average tariff level weighted by the import shares of different products, capturing the effective burden on trade flows rather than unweighted averages. This metric, derived from effectively applied rates including preferences under free trade agreements (FTAs), provides a realistic view of tariffs faced in practice, though it excludes non-tariff barriers such as quotas or standards. World Bank estimates, based on the World Integrated Trade Solution (WITS) system, indicate rates primarily for 2021-2022, with a global simple average around 2.6% across reporting economies, reflecting widespread liberalization but pockets of protectionism.[2] Updates from WTO profiles confirm persistence of low aggregates in major traders, adjusted for post-2018 trade tensions. Rates differ markedly by development level and trade openness: developing nations often maintain higher protections on key imports like manufactures or agriculture, while advanced economies benefit from extensive FTAs that reduce effective rates below most-favored-nation (MFN) levels. For instance, the United States recorded 2.2% in 2023, incorporating retaliatory adjustments from prior disputes. China's trade-weighted average stood at 3.3% in 2024, down from peaks during U.S.-China frictions due to diversified import sourcing and concessions. India's rate reached 12.0% in 2024, driven by elevated duties on non-agricultural goods. Simple Average and Bound Tariffs Comparison

The simple average applied most-favored-nation (MFN) tariff rate is computed as the unweighted arithmetic mean of all tariff lines, treating each product category equally regardless of import volume, which can overestimate effective protection compared to trade-weighted averages that adjust for actual trade flows.[9] This metric often yields higher values, as low-volume goods subject to elevated duties disproportionately influence the average; for example, India's simple average MFN applied tariff was 16.2% in 2024, exceeding its trade-weighted counterpart of roughly 4.6-6% due to heavy imports of low-tariff machinery and electronics offsetting peaks in agriculture and textiles.[45][49] Bound tariffs, by contrast, denote the WTO-negotiated ceilings beyond which members pledge not to raise duties without compensation or negotiation, frequently substantially above applied levels and providing "tariff water" for policy maneuvers. Developing nations typically bind at 30-50% or higher across products, preserving flexibility for infant industries or revenue needs, while advanced economies like the United States maintain tight bindings averaging 3.4% simple bound against 3.3% applied in 2024, limiting escalation potential.[50][48]

Regional and Sectoral Variations

By Geographic Region

Tariff rates exhibit marked regional disparities, primarily driven by differences in economic development stages, the prevalence of preferential trade agreements, and strategic use of protection to foster infant industries in less integrated areas. Advanced regions such as Europe and North America maintain low averages below 2% due to comprehensive customs unions and free trade pacts like the EU single market and USMCA, which facilitate high intra-regional trade volumesâ€”empirical evidence shows these low barriers correlate with intra-bloc trade growth exceeding 20% annually in the EU over the past decade.[52][53] In contrast, developing regions in Africa and South Asia average 7-11%, reflecting policies aimed at shielding domestic sectors amid limited diversification, though this often constrains regional supply chains as evidenced by Sub-Saharan Africa's intra-trade share remaining under 15% despite efforts like the African Continental Free Trade Area.[54]

By Product Sectors

Tariff rates exhibit marked disparities across Harmonized System (HS) categories, with agricultural products (HS chapters 1â€“24) consistently facing higher applied duties than non-agricultural manufactures (HS chapters 25â€“97). The World Trade Organization's World Tariff Profiles 2024 reports that, across over 170 economies, simple average most-favored-nation (MFN) applied tariffs on agricultural goods average 10â€“15% globally, compared to 3â€“5% for non-agricultural products, highlighting a persistent protectionist tilt toward farming sectors despite post-Uruguay Round commitments.[7] This pattern shields domestic producers in uncompetitive areas, such as staple crops and livestock, from import competition, thereby elevating consumer prices for food and raw materials.[7] In developed economies, agricultural protection remains entrenched, as evidenced by the European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), which sustains high tariffs on sensitive items: dairy products average 32.3%, sugar and confectionery 27.0%, and meats 19.0% as of recent analyses.[58] These rates, often bound at 5â€“20% under WTO schedules but applied selectively via tariff-rate quotas, prioritize food security and rural livelihoods over efficiency, contrasting sharply with near-zero industrial tariffs that promote manufacturing integration.[7] Developing countries display inverted priorities, imposing elevated duties on manufactures and textiles/apparel (HS chapters 50â€“63) to foster industrialization, with peaks of 10â€“20% in these subsectors. India's WTO tariff profile illustrates this: simple average MFN applied tariffs reach 36.7% for agriculture but climb to higher effective protection in apparel and textiles to support local processing, while overall non-agricultural rates average 13.0% as of 2024.[45] Such structures causally insulate emerging industries from advanced imports, though they distort resource allocation and hinder value-chain upgrading in export-oriented sectors like garments.[59] Globally, textiles and apparel endure some of the highest non-agricultural tariffs, averaging near 6% on imports, constraining South-South trade in labor-intensive goods.[59]

Economic Impacts and Evidence

Empirical Effects on Growth and Prices

Cross-country econometric analyses spanning over 50 years and covering approximately 150 countries demonstrate a robust inverse relationship between tariff levels and economic growth. Higher tariffs are associated with slower output growth, with estimates indicating that a sustained 10 percentage point increase in average tariffs can reduce real GDP by 0.5 to 1 percentage point in the medium term, reflecting reduced capital accumulation, resource misallocation, and diminished competition.[60][61] Similarly, National Bureau of Economic Research studies using historical tariff variation find that tariff hikes lead to statistically significant medium-term declines in domestic output and total factor productivity, with effects persisting beyond initial implementation due to persistent distortions in trade and investment flows.[62] On prices, recent U.S. tariff implementations in 2025 have empirically elevated consumer costs, with core goods prices running 1.9% above pre-tariff trends as of June 2025, particularly in categories like appliances, electronics, and window coverings, based on pass-through rates of 61-80% to retail levels.[63] Historically, the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 exacerbated the Great Depression by contracting global trade volumes by up to 66%, deepening economic contraction amid prevailing deflationary pressures, though direct price inflation from protected imports was offset by broader demand collapse.[28] Empirical evidence further indicates that tariffs fail to reduce trade deficits, as higher barriers correlate with larger overall deficits through reduced export competitiveness and elevated input costs for domestic producers; Cato Institute analyses of U.S. data show no causal link between tariff escalation and deficit shrinkage, with post-2018 tariff periods exhibiting widening imbalances.[64] Net welfare effects remain negative, as consumer and firm losses from higher prices substantially exceed producer gains; for instance, 2018 U.S. tariffs resulted in $51 billion in losses to buyers versus $9.4 billion in gains to protected sectors, yielding a deadweight loss equivalent to 0.27% of GDP.[65][66]

Causal Mechanisms and First-Principles Analysis

Tariffs function as a tax on imported goods, artificially inflating their domestic price relative to the world market equilibrium, which discourages consumption and import volumes while benefiting protected producers at the expense of overall welfare. This price wedge creates deadweight loss, as the reduction in trade quantity eliminates mutually beneficial exchanges: consumer surplus diminishes more than the sum of producer gains and tariff revenue, with no offsetting efficiency improvements. Retaliatory tariffs from trading partners compound this by curtailing export opportunities, further eroding domestic producer surplus and amplifying the net inefficiency.[65][67] By shielding domestic industries from foreign competition, tariffs distort resource allocation away from sectors aligned with a country's comparative advantage, directing capital, labor, and materials toward less productive uses where domestic costs exceed global opportunities. Protected firms, facing muted incentives to innovate or cut costs, perpetuate inefficiencies, while unprotected export-oriented sectors suffer from elevated input prices, propagating losses across the supply chain. For instance, the 2018 U.S. tariffs on steel and aluminum raised material costs for downstream manufacturers, such as automakers, resulting in steel price increases that disadvantaged U.S. firms relative to foreign competitors and contributed to broader manufacturing job losses exceeding gains in protected metals production.[68][69] In the long term, tariffs fail to foster enduring competitiveness, as they insulate firms from the pressures that drive productivity enhancements, such as technological adoption and scale efficiencies from open markets. Instead, reduced trade barriers enable specialization according to comparative advantage, yielding dynamic benefits through reallocation to higher-value activities and accelerated growth rates, patterns evident in widespread liberalizations from the 1990s onward. Apparent short-term fiscal gains, like revenue inflows and localized job retention, mask these systemic costs, as higher prices permeate the economy, eroding real incomes and investment without building adaptive capacities.[70]

Policy Debates and Controversies

Protectionist Arguments and Empirical Rebuttals

Proponents of protectionism often argue that tariffs safeguard domestic jobs and industries by shielding them from foreign competition, as exemplified by the 2018 U.S. steel and aluminum tariffs imposed under Section 232, which aimed to revive manufacturing employment.[69] However, empirical analyses reveal net job losses across the economy, with downstream industries using steelâ€”such as manufacturing and constructionâ€”experiencing far greater employment declines than gains in protected sectors. A study by the Trade Partnership estimated that these tariffs resulted in 2.57 million total job losses, with nearly eight jobs lost for every one gained in steel production, at a cost of $583,693 per job preserved.[71] Similarly, research on the 2018-2019 U.S.-China trade war found modest net employment reductions, as retaliatory tariffs and higher input costs outweighed any localized benefits.[72] The infant industry argument posits that temporary tariffs enable nascent sectors to develop scale and efficiency before competing globally, a rationale historically invoked for high barriers in developing economies. Yet, cross-country evidence indicates such protections rarely lead to maturation, often entrenching inefficiency due to lack of competitive pressure and political capture. In India, pre-1991 import substitution policies in the automotive sector, featuring tariffs exceeding 100% alongside licensing restrictions, stifled productivity and innovation, delaying the industry's integration into global value chains until liberalization reforms.[73] Post-reform data shows rapid growth in vehicle production and exports only after tariff reductions, underscoring how prolonged protection hindered rather than fostered competitiveness.[73] National security justifications for tariffs emphasize self-sufficiency in critical inputs, such as rare earth elements dominated by Chinese supply, to mitigate supply chain vulnerabilities. While valid in principle for a narrow set of strategic goods, this rationale is frequently overextended, with data illustrating that forced domestic production incurs substantial economic costs without proportional security gains. Achieving U.S. self-sufficiency in rare earth processing could require over a decade and face high capital outlays, technical hurdles, and environmental compliance expenses, potentially raising prices for downstream industries like electronics and defense by multiples of current import levels.[74] Empirical assessments of similar past efforts, including subsidies for domestic mining, confirm elevated unit costs compared to diversified international sourcing.[75] In 2025, expanded U.S. tariffs on imports from China and other partners have demonstrably elevated consumer prices without achieving promised reductions in trade deficits, as retaliatory measures and supply shifts offset revenue gains. Core goods personal consumption expenditures (PCE) prices rose 1.5% in the first half of 2025, compared to 0.3% in the prior year, with economists attributing much of the increase to tariff passthrough effects on categories like vehicles and electronics.[63] Analyses project cumulative consumer costs exceeding $592 billion by year-end, yet trade imbalances persist due to inelastic demand for imports and currency adjustments, mirroring patterns from earlier tariff episodes where deficits narrowed minimally or expanded elsewhere.[76][37]

Free Trade Benefits and Tariff Retaliation Risks

Low tariff regimes have facilitated substantial poverty alleviation through expanded export opportunities and economic integration. Since 1990, improved trade practices, including tariff reductions under multilateral frameworks like the WTO, have underpinned growth that lifted over 1 billion people out of extreme poverty, primarily in developing Asia via manufacturing exports.[77] Countries adopting unilateral low tariffs, such as Chile's average applied rate of around 1% following 1970s-1980s liberalization, have seen GDP per capita triple to approximately $26,000 by 2025, outperforming regional peers through enhanced competitiveness and foreign investment.[78] Similarly, Singapore's near-zero tariff average (0.05% simple mean in 2022) supports its status as a high-prosperity hub with one of the world's highest GDP per capita in purchasing power parity terms, driven by efficient resource allocation and global supply chain roles.[79] Empirical analyses confirm that trade openness correlates positively with productivity and growth, while tariffs exhibit a negative association. A cross-country study spanning five decades and 150 nations found tariff increases linked to annual output growth reductions of up to 1.5% after four years, particularly when hikes exceed three standard deviations, due to distorted incentives and reduced innovation.[60] Another investigation across 100+ countries revealed tariffs' significant negative impact on economic expansion, contrasting with openness's boost via technology diffusion and scale economies.[80] Moderate tariffs fail to address trade deficits effectively, as macroeconomic factors like savings-investment imbalances dominate; Dallas Fed research shows no meaningful deficit reduction from such measures, with retaliation often offsetting any initial import substitution.[81] Tariff escalations risk retaliatory spirals that amplify global disruptions. The 1930 Smoot-Hawley Act, raising U.S. duties to 60% on dutiables, prompted over two dozen nations to enact beggar-thy-neighbor barriers, contracting world trade by 66% from 1929-1934 and exacerbating the Great Depression.[82] In the 2018 U.S.-China trade war, U.S. tariffs covered $380 billion in Chinese imports, met by Chinese duties on $90 billion+ of U.S. goods, disrupting integrated supply chains in electronics and agriculture, raising costs, and diverting trade flows without resolving underlying imbalances.[36][37] While targeted measures like anti-dumping duties can counter specific unfair practicesâ€”offsetting predatory pricing without broad distortionâ€”generalized tariffs typically yield net harm by inviting retaliation and eroding efficiency gains from specialization.[83] Overall, causal evidence prioritizes low-tariff openness for sustained prosperity, with protectionist deviations risking self-inflicted losses absent exceptional justifications like verified dumping.
