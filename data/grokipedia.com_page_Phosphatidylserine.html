Phosphatidylserine

Phosphatidylserine (abbreviated PtdSer or PS) is a phospholipid and a component of the cell membrane.[1] It is particularly abundant in neural tissues.[2] It plays a key role in cellular signaling, particularly in relation to apoptosis.[3] It is a key pathway for viruses to enter cells via apoptotic mimicry.[4] Exposure of phosphatidylserine on the outer surface of the plasma membrane during apoptosis signals the cell for recognition and removal by phagocytes.[3]

Structure

Phosphatidylserine is a phospholipidâ€”more specifically a glycerophospholipidâ€”which consists of two fatty acids attached in ester linkage to the first and second carbon of glycerol and serine attached through a phosphodiester linkage to the third carbon of the glycerol.[1] Phosphatidylserine sourced from plants differs in fatty acid composition from that sourced from animals; for example, plant-derived PS (e.g., from soy) is richer in linoleic acid, while animal-derived PS (e.g., from bovine cortex) contains more stearic and oleic acids.[5] It is commonly found in the inner (cytoplasmic) leaflet of biological membranes, almost exclusively so in healthy cells.[6]

Biosynthesis

In mammalian cells, phosphatidylserine (PS) is synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum through \ceCa2+\ce{Ca^2+}\ceCa2+-dependent base-exchange reactions catalyzed by two enzymes: phosphatidylserine synthase 1 (PSS1) and phosphatidylserine synthase 2 (PSS2). PSS1 facilitates the exchange of the choline headgroup in phosphatidylcholine (PC) for L-serine, while PSS2 exchanges the ethanolamine headgroup in phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) for L-serine.[7]

Composition

Phosphatidylserine (PS) is a glycerophospholipid consisting of a glycerol backbone esterified with two fatty acid chains at the sn-1 and sn-2 positions, a phosphate group attached to the sn-3 position, and a serine head group linked to the phosphate.[6] The fatty acid composition of PS varies by tissue and organism. In the human brain, PS constitutes about 13% of total phospholipids in gray matter and up to 15% in white matter.[2] It is particularly enriched in polyunsaturated fatty acids, especially docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, 22:6 n-3), which accounts for more than 30% of total fatty acids and about 80% of polyunsaturated fatty acids in gray matter PS.[2][8] This high DHA content is crucial for neuronal function. In other tissues, such as liver, PS has lower DHA levels and more saturated fatty acids. In bacteria like E. coli, PS typically features saturated or monounsaturated fatty acids, such as palmitic (16:0) and oleic (18:1) acids, reflecting the overall membrane lipid profile.[9]

Functions

Cognitive function

Phosphatidylserine (PS) is highly enriched in neuronal cell membranes, where it contributes to membrane fluidity and integrity.[8] It plays a key role in brain signaling pathways, including the activation of protein kinase C (PKC), which is essential for synaptic transmission and learning,[10] and modulation of Akt signaling for neuronal survival and neuroprotection.[11] PS also supports the synthesis of neurotransmitters and the formation of short-term and long-term memories.[12]

Apoptosis

PS plays a crucial role in the process of apoptosis (programmed cell death). During apoptosis, PS translocates from the inner leaflet of the cell membrane to the outer leaflet, serving as a signal for phagocytic cells to engulf the dying cell.[3]

Dietary sources

The average daily phosphatidylserine intake in a Western diet is estimated to be 130 mg. Phosphatidylserine may be found in meat and fish. Only small amounts are found in dairy products and vegetables, with the exception of white beans and soy lecithin. Supplementation

Health claims

A panel of the European Food Safety Authority concluded that a cause and effect relationship cannot be established between the consumption of phosphatidylserine and "memory and cognitive functioning in the elderly", "mental health/cognitive function" and "stress reduction and enhanced memory function".[15] This conclusion follows because bovine brain cortex- and soy-based phosphatidylserine are different substances and might, therefore, have different biological activities. Therefore, the results of studies using phosphatidylserine from different sources cannot be generalized.[15]

Cognition

In May, 2003 the Food and Drug Administration gave "qualified health claim" status to phosphatidylserine thus allowing labels to state "consumption of phosphatidylserine may reduce the risk of dementia and cognitive dysfunction in the elderly" along with the disclaimer "very limited and preliminary scientific research suggests that phosphatidylserine may reduce the risk of cognitive dysfunction in the elderly."[16][17] According to the FDA, there is a lack of scientific agreement amongst qualified experts that a relationship exists between phosphatidylserine and cognitive function.[16] More recent reviews have suggested that the relationship may be more robust,[18][19] though the mechanism remains unclear.[8] Some studies have suggested that whether the phosphatidylserine is plant- or animal-derived may have significance, with the FDA's statement applying specifically to soy-derived products.[16][20][22][23] Post-2020 clinical trials and reviews, as of 2025, continue to show modest improvements in cognitive function, memory, and attention in older adults and those with mild cognitive impairment, though larger, rigorous studies are needed.[24][25]

Safety

Initially, phosphatidylserine supplements were derived from bovine cortex. However, due to the risk of potential transfer of infectious diseases such as bovine spongiform encephalopathy (or "mad cow disease"), soy-derived supplements became an alternative.[20] A 2002 safety report determined supplementation in elderly people at a dosage of 200 mg three times daily to be safe.[26] Some manufacturers of phosphatidylserine use sunflower lecithin instead of soy lecithin as a source of raw material production.
