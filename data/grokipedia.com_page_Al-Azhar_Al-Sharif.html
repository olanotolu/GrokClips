Al-Azhar al-Sharif

Al-Azhar al-Sharif is the preeminent mosque-university complex in Cairo, Egypt, founded in 970 CE by the Fatimid Caliph al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah through his general Jawhar al-Siqilli as a center for propagating Ismaili Shiism, which later transitioned into the world's leading institution for Sunni Islamic scholarship and jurisprudence.[1][2] Named after Fatima al-Zahra, daughter of the Prophet Muhammad, it combines religious worship, advanced education in Islamic sciences, Arabic language, and related disciplines, attracting students globally and granting degrees recognized across the Muslim world.[1][3] Over centuries, Al-Azhar evolved under Ayyubid, Mamluk, and Ottoman rule into a bastion of the four Sunni madhhabs, fostering ijtihad while preserving orthodox interpretations of Sharia, and it remains Egypt's oldest continuously operating degree-granting university, predating Europe's Bologna by centuries.[4][3] The Grand Imam, as head of Al-Azhar al-Sharif, wields significant influence over Sunni Muslims, issuing fatwas on theological, legal, and contemporary issues through bodies like the Fatwa Council, which adhere to traditional fiqh methodologies derived from Quran and Sunnah.[5][6] Notable achievements include educating generations of ulama who shaped Islamic thought, from medieval revivals under Salah al-Din to modern curricula incorporating sciences, and positioning itself as a counter to extremism by promoting "moderate" Islam, though self-described as such amid state oversight.[7] Controversies arise from fatwas upholding classical Sharia provisions, such as women inheriting half the share of men in line with Quranic injunctions, permissions for disciplinary measures against wives per Surah an-Nisa 4:34 without severe injury, and endorsements of defensive jihad, including recent support for Hamas against Israel, which critics argue perpetuates gender disparities and militancy.[8][9][10] These rulings reflect causal adherence to scriptural precedents over egalitarian reforms, drawing scrutiny from secular and progressive quarters despite Al-Azhar's claims of renewal.[11]

Modern Revival and 20th-Century Developments

In the early 19th century, Muhammad Ali Pasha, who consolidated power in Egypt from 1805 to 1848, initiated reforms aimed at centralizing administrative control over religious institutions, including Al-Azhar, to align them with his modernization agenda of military and economic strengthening.[34] These efforts involved appointing state overseers to monitor teaching and finances, expanding enrollment through subsidies, and attempting to incorporate rudimentary scientific subjects alongside traditional Islamic studies, though resistance from the ulama limited deeper curricular changes.[35] By the mid-19th century, these measures contributed to a revival in attendance, with student numbers surpassing 7,000, reflecting Al-Azhar's enduring appeal as a hub for Sunni scholarship amid competition from Pasha's new secular schools.[36] Throughout the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Al-Azhar navigated colonial influences and Egyptian nationalist stirrings by issuing fatwas that selectively endorsed state policies, such as land reforms and infrastructure projects, while safeguarding its doctrinal autonomy through adherence to classical fiqh texts like those of the Shafi'i and Hanafi schools.[37] This period saw incremental administrative formalization, including entrance exams and degree structures introduced around 1872 under Khedive Ismail, yet the core curriculum persisted in emphasizing tafsir, hadith, and usul al-fiqh, resisting wholesale adoption of Western legal codes despite pressures from British administrators and local modernizers.[38] In the mid-20th century, President Gamal Abdel Nasser's Law 103 of 1961 nationalized Al-Azhar, integrating it into the state university system, centralizing funding via waqf expropriation, and mandating modern subjects like science and languages to counter perceived obsolescence and Islamist rivals.[39] This sparked ulama protests over secular encroachments, as traditionalists argued that diluting fiqh primacy undermined causal religious authority derived from scriptural sources, leading to temporary faculty purges and enrollment fluctuations before stabilization.[40] Subsequent leaders Anwar Sadat and Hosni Mubarak leveraged Al-Azhar's fatwa apparatus to legitimize policies against the Muslim Brotherhood, portraying the latter's activism as deviant from established Sunni norms, while preserving the institution's role in issuing endorsements for economic liberalization and peace accords.[41] Despite these state interventions, empirical persistence of classical fiqh in over 80% of pre-university courses by the late 20th century demonstrated Al-Azhar's resilient traditionalist core, as enrollment rebounded to tens of thousands, prioritizing empirical fidelity to madhhab methodologies over imported positivist frameworks.[42][43]

Physical Site and Architecture

The Al-Azhar Mosque

The Al-Azhar Mosque was founded in 970 CE by Jawhar al-Siqilli as the congregational mosque of the Fatimid capital al-Qahira, incorporating a hypostyle prayer hall, central courtyard, and surrounding riwaqs with the qibla-facing riwaq as the tallest and largest.[26] [44] It features an early transept aisle running perpendicular to the qibla wall, marking a distinctive Fatimid innovation in Egyptian Islamic architecture.[45] The surviving Fatimid prayer hall core, completed in 972 CE, centers on a mihrab with qibla wall elements bearing Quranic inscriptions in Kufic script, later augmented by a Mamluk dome in the 15th century.[46] [45] Mamluk rulers drove key expansions to the mosque proper: Sultan Barquq directed renovations and structural enhancements in 1397 CE, while Sultan Qaytbay added a minaret circa 1468 CE and decorative portals in the 1480s, embodying era-specific aesthetics through intricate stonework and proportions.[44] [47] [48] The layout supports a capacity of approximately 20,000 worshippers across its courtyard and halls.[49] Integrated into Historic Cairo, the mosque gained UNESCO World Heritage status in 1979, acknowledging its role in preserving Fatimid-to-Mamluk architectural evolution.[50]

Expansion and University Infrastructure

In the 20th century, Al-Azhar transitioned from a mosque-centered madrasa to a expansive university complex, driven by state-led reforms to meet rising demands for higher education in both religious and secular fields. Law 103 of 1961, enacted under President Gamal Abdel Nasser, formalized Al-Azhar University as an independent state institution, distinct from the mosque's traditional scholarly role, and expanded its scope by incorporating faculties in medicine, agriculture, commerce, and engineering alongside Islamic sciences.[39][4] This restructuring necessitated new construction, including an "educational city" with dedicated buildings for classrooms, laboratories, and administrative units to support diversified programs.[51] Subsequent infrastructure growth included dormitories, libraries, and research facilities funded partly through waqf endowments, enabling the institution to scale operations amid enrollment surges. By the late 20th century, these additions allowed Al-Azhar to house expanding student bodies, with modern campuses in Cairo featuring specialized complexes for sciences and humanities. Branches extended to regional sites in Assiut, Tanta, Mansoura, Alexandria, and others, distributing facilities to reduce central overcrowding and enhance accessibility across Egypt.[52][53] Today, the university infrastructure supports approximately 43,000 to 50,000 students in degree programs, with additional capacity for affiliated pre-university schools exceeding two million enrollees nationwide, though the core university maintains focused higher-education operations.[54][55] The physical resilience of these facilities was tested during the 2011 Egyptian revolution, where student activism occurred but major structural damage to university buildings was limited compared to other sites in Cairo.

Educational Framework

Curriculum Structure and Content

The curriculum of Al-Azhar al-Sharif's religious programs, encompassing both pre-university institutes and the university's Sharia faculty, emphasizes traditional Islamic sciences as foundational pillars. Core subjects include tafsir (Quranic exegesis), hadith (prophetic traditions), fiqh (jurisprudence across the Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali schools), tawhid (theology), and Arabic grammar and rhetoric, structured progressively from memorization of texts to advanced application in contemporary issues.[42][56] Instruction relies heavily on abridged editions of medieval works, such as Taysir al-Tafsir al-Nasafi for exegesis and classical fiqh compendia like those of al-Nasafi or Ibn Aqil, which prioritize literal adherence to Sharia-derived rulings over empirical or contextual reinterpretations.[42][57] Fiqh textbooks explicitly justify hudud punishments from classical jurisprudence, including stoning for adultery and sodomy (Grade 9 Maliki Fiqh, pp. 79) and death for offenses like vilifying Muhammad or denying scriptural tenets, interpreted to include apostasy even post-repentance (Grade 9 Maliki Fiqh, pp. 71-72).[42] These elements reflect a pedagogical framework rooted in causal chains from primary sourcesâ€”Quran and hadithâ€”to fixed penalties, without integration of modern evidentiary standards or historical-critical analysis that might challenge their applicability.[42] The curriculum's doctrinal output manifests in recurrent fatwas, such as condemnations of interest-based banking as riba, deeming it impermissible exploitation forbidden by Quranic injunctions (e.g., 2:275-279), thereby influencing economic views among graduates.[58] Reform efforts, including President el-Sisi's 2015 directive to revise materials for extremism prevention and 2019 textbook updates promoting "Wasati" moderation, have introduced tolerance rhetoric (e.g., Grade 10 Islamic Culture, pp. 130-131, citing the Document of Human Fraternity) but failed to excise core literalist prescriptions.[42] Reviews from the late 2010s onward, including curriculum audits, document ongoing endorsement of apostasy-death rulings and hudud, contradicting institutional claims of comprehensive deradicalization and highlighting resistance to substantive shifts toward evidence-prioritizing pedagogy.[42][59] STEM integration in the religious track remains peripheral, with religious institutes allocating minimal hours to sciences amid dominance of doctrinal subjects, though parallel university faculties provide standalone modern training disconnected from Sharia Faculty, Students, and Degrees

Al-Azhar University enrolls approximately 270,000 students across its faculties in Cairo and provincial branches, including around 24,000 international students mainly from Muslim-majority nations in Africa, the Middle East, and Asia. Affiliated programs and distance learning in Islamic studies extend the claimed total to over 400,000 worldwide, though core campus figures verified by global rankings are lower, highlighting potential inflation in official tallies. The student body remains predominantly male, especially in core religious disciplines, with female participation constituting under 40% overall and far less in advanced fiqh training due to segregated facilities and cultural norms prioritizing male clerical roles.[60][61][62] Faculty numbers exceed 18,000 academic staff, drawn largely from graduates of Al-Azhar's own system or analogous traditional madrasas in Egypt and the Arab world, where emphasis falls on mastery of classical texts via apprenticeship rather than Western-style credentials. This recruitment model sustains a scholarly corps versed in orthodox Sunni fiqh schools, particularly Shafi'i and Hanafi, but limits diversity in interpretive approaches, reinforcing institutional insularity amid broader academic globalization.[60] The university awards modern degrees including bachelor's, master's, and PhDs in Islamic studies, alongside traditional ijazatâ€”personal authorizations from senior ulama to transmit specific hadith or fiqh works, often bypassing formal exams in favor of demonstrated oral proficiency. Ijazat hold precedence in religious hierarchies over diplomas for clerical advancement, with PhD programs focusing on specialized theses in sharia interpretation; however, female conferrals in higher fiqh remain minimal, reflecting enrollment disparities. Scholarships, funded by Egyptian state budgets and donors from Gulf states, preferentially support students from conservative Sunni regions such as Indonesia, Palestine, and sub-Saharan Africa, channeling resources to reinforce traditionalist networks rather than broader outreach.[63][64] Notable alumni trajectories underscore political pipelines, with graduates ascending to leadership in Islamist entities like Hamasâ€”whose Gaza branch draws heavily from Al-Azhar affiliatesâ€”and Egyptian state religious posts, evidencing the curriculum's orientation toward governance roles in conservative Muslim contexts over universal scholarly appeal. This output pattern, amid exclusivity in admissions favoring memorized Qur'an proficiency from madrasa backgrounds, sustains Al-Azhar's influence within orthodox Sunni spheres while curtailing engagement with reformist or secular demographics.[9]

Leadership and Administration

The Office of Grand Imam

The office of the Grand Imam, also known as the Shaykh al-Azhar, represents the paramount authority within Al-Azhar al-Sharif, overseeing its religious, educational, and jurisprudential functions as the preeminent Sunni Islamic institution.[41] Traditionally, the position was filled through election by Al-Azhar's senior ulama from among their ranks, emphasizing scholarly merit and consensus among established jurists.[65] However, Law No. 103 of 1961, enacted under President Gamal Abdel Nasser, centralized the appointment process by vesting final authority in the Egyptian president, who selects the Grand Imam from a slate of qualified candidates proposed by Al-Azhar's bodies, thereby introducing direct state control over what was historically an autonomous clerical selection.[66] This shift, rationalized as a reform to streamline administration amid Nasser's secular-nationalist agenda, has causally subordinated religious leadership to political exigencies, as presidents consistently favor appointees amenable to regime priorities over purely doctrinal consensus, evidenced by the law's persistence across successive governments despite periodic scholarly pushback.[41][65] The Grand Imam's core duties include serving as rector of Al-Azhar University, delivering the principal Friday sermon (khutba) at the Al-Azhar Mosque, and supervising fatwa issuance through oversight of consultative councils like the Fatwa Committee, ensuring alignment with traditional Sunni orthodoxy.[66] These responsibilities position the office as the custodian of Sharia interpretation for Egypt and broader Sunni communities, with the Grand Imam wielding veto power over major doctrinal pronouncements and representing Al-Azhar in state-religious dialogues.[67] Appointees are typically drawn from Al-Azhar's senior faculty, exemplifying deep institutional ties; Ahmed el-Tayeb, appointed on March 10, 2010, by President Hosni Mubarak following the death of his predecessor, held a doctorate in Islamic philosophy from Al-Azhar and had served as the university's president from 2003 to 2010, underscoring the preference for academically pedigreed scholars with administrative experience.[68][67] Historical precedents illustrate the office's vulnerability to external pressures, as seen in the tenure of Mustafa al-Maraghi, appointed Grand Imam in May 1928 amid King Fuad I's efforts to modernize the institution. Al-Maraghi advocated for curriculum reforms, including greater emphasis on rational sciences and ijtihad to adapt Islamic education to contemporary needs, but encountered fierce resistance from conservative ulama factions and indirect monarchical interference, culminating in his resignation on October 23, 1935, after failing to secure sustained backing.[69] This episode highlighted causal tensions between reformist impulses within the office and entrenched doctrinal conservatism reinforced by political patrons wary of diluting clerical influence. Empirical records from the 2010s reveal ongoing overrides of internal electoral processes, with presidential appointments frequently diverging from Senior Scholars Council recommendations to prioritize regime stability; for instance, el-Tayeb's 2010 selection proceeded despite reported preferences among ulama for alternative candidates, a pattern repeated in constitutional amendment debates where proposals for direct presidential nomination were floated but withdrawn amid institutional protests, yet the 1961 framework endured, perpetuating state dominance over scholarly autonomy.[70][71] Such interventions, documented in analyses of post-Mubarak transitions, underscore how governmental leverageâ€”via funding control and legal fiatâ€”systematically erodes the office's independence, transforming it into a conduit for state-aligned religious policy rather than unadulterated clerical authority.[41][70]

Religious Doctrine and Authority

Issuance of Fatwas and Scholarly Output

Al-Azhar al-Sharif's fatwas are issued through its dedicated Fatwa Committee (Lajnat al-Fatwa), comprising senior scholars who engage in collective deliberation (ijma') on submitted queries, relying primarily on the Quran, authentic Hadith, ijma', and qiyas from the four Sunni madhhabs. This process emphasizes scholarly consensus to derive rulings, ensuring alignment with classical fiqh methodologies rather than individual ijtihad, and results in non-binding opinions that nonetheless exert considerable influence due to Al-Azhar's perceived authority in Sunni Islam.[6] The committee handles diverse inquiries on ritual, personal status, and economic matters, often prioritizing textual fidelity over adaptive reinterpretation to contemporary contexts.[81] Historically, Al-Azhar's fatwa output has included edicts mobilizing resistance to invasions, such as scholarly endorsements of jihad against Crusader forces in the 12th century, framing such conflicts as defensive imperatives under Sharia. In the modern era, fatwas have upheld traditional prohibitions on riba (usury), rejecting interest-based banking as impermissible despite economic pressures, as articulated in rulings from Al-Azhar's jurisprudence institute that distinguish fiat transactions from classical gold-silver exchanges but maintain the core ban. This consistency underscores a commitment to undiluted scriptural prescriptions, even amid global financial integration.[82] Notable contemporary examples include Al-Azhar's 2014-2017 denunciations of ISIS as perpetrators of khawarij-like extremism and terrorism, issuing open letters and statements condemning their actions as violations of Sharia while adhering to restrictive classical conditions for declaring apostasy (takfir) to prevent broader sectarian discord. Scholarly output complements fatwas through prolific publications, including fiqh treatises, Hadith commentaries, and periodicals from Al-Azhar's presses, which annually produce volumes reinforcing orthodox Sunni positions on issues like inheritance and ritual purity, disseminated via official channels and media to guide adherents across the Muslim world.[83][84] Al-Azhar's approach reveals a pattern of traditionalism, as seen in reluctance to doctrinally innovate on institutions like regulated slavery, where fatwas and texts invoke Quranic permissions (e.g., Quran 23:5-6) and prophetic precedents for captives in war, advocating manumission as meritorious but not mandating abolition, in line with historical fiqh that viewed slavery as a contractual status rather than an inherent evil requiring eradication. This output, while voluminous and influentialâ€”reaching millions through broadcasts and publicationsâ€”prioritizes causal fidelity to revelatory sources over reformist exigencies, maintaining interpretive continuity from medieval scholars like al-Shafi'i.[85]

Core Ideological Positions on Sharia and Fiqh

Al-Azhar al-Sharif upholds the traditional Sunni framework of fiqh, centered on the four major madhahibâ€”Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbaliâ€”as the authoritative methodologies for deriving Sharia rulings. Its doctrinal positions prioritize the Quran and authentic Hadith as primary sources, with ijtihad confined to qualified scholars operating within these schools' usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence), rather than open-ended personal reasoning that could deviate from textual imperatives.[86][87] This approach ensures fidelity to divine revelation over analogical or equitable extensions, viewing unrestricted ijtihad post the madhahib's formation as presumptuous absent consensus (ijma').[88] Sharia is posited as the exhaustive, self-sufficient legal order encompassing personal, penal, and political domains, rendering secular governance an affront to tawhid (divine unity) and hakimiyyah (God's sovereignty). Al-Azhar's fatwas and scholarly endorsements reject secularism as incompatible with Islamic ontology, critiquing "liberal Islam" or modernist dilutions as bid'ah that erode Sharia's immutability; for example, defenses of classical inheritance shares (males receiving double females' portions) and hijab mandates affirm fixed Quranic-Hadith derivations against egalitarian reforms.[89][90] Egypt's constitutional reliance on Sharia principles, interpreted via Al-Azhar, underscores this supremacy, subordinating positive law to fiqh consensus.[91] For non-Muslims, fiqh doctrine normalizes dhimmi status under Muslim rule, granting protected persons (ahl al-kitab primarily) security, property rights, and ibadat autonomy contingent on jizya payment, political loyalty, and restrictions like non-proselytization or architectural subordinationâ€”rooted in Quran 9:29's command to fight until submission and tribute.[92][93] This hierarchical pact empirically privileges Muslims' evidentiary standing in testimony and governance, conflicting with neutral equality norms by design, as Sharia's causal realism derives rights from doctrinal truth-claims rather than universal reciprocity. The abrogation (naskh) principle structures Quranic prioritization, teaching that later Medinan surahsâ€”emphasizing jihad, jizya, and apostasy penaltiesâ€”supersede earlier Meccan emphases on tolerance (e.g., abrogating 2:256's "no compulsion" in warfare contexts per 9:5, 9:29).[94]) Al-Azhar curricula integrate this across madhahib, favoring warlike verses' permanence for dar al-Islam's defense, while taqiyya receives minimal coverage, limited to verifiable persecution without doctrinal endorsement of dissimulation as normative.[95] This textual mechanism underscores Sharia's adaptive supremacy, where empirical pacification yields to expansionist imperatives under divine warrant.

Political Role and Influence

Interactions with Egyptian Regimes

In 1961, President Gamal Abdel Nasser enacted Law 103, which restructured Al-Azhar al-Sharif by centralizing its administration under state oversight, subordinating its scholarly council to government-appointed bodies, and integrating modern subjects into its curriculum to align with socialist reforms.[39][96] This legislation effectively curtailed the institution's financial and doctrinal independence, transforming it into a tool for regime legitimacy, as evidenced by Al-Azhar's issuance of fatwas framing Arab socialism as compatible with Islamic principles to counter opposition from groups like the Muslim Brotherhood.[97] Under President Anwar Sadat, Al-Azhar maintained this subservient posture amid the shift to infitah economic liberalization in the 1970s, providing religious endorsements that reconciled capitalist openings with Sharia to bolster the regime's pivot from Nasserist socialism.[98] The institution's fatwas during this period avoided challenging state policies, reflecting the enduring impact of the 1961 law's structural controls, which prioritized alignment over autonomous critique.[99] Following the 2011 revolution, Al-Azhar's leadership, including Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb, issued cautious statements distancing the institution from revolutionary endorsements, suppressing broader ulama support for anti-Mubarak protests to preserve ties with interim military authorities.[100][39] This restraint underscored the 1961 framework's causal constraints, where state subordination limited fatwa issuance against ruling powers. After Abdel Fattah el-Sisi's 2013 ouster of Muslim Brotherhood President Mohamed Morsi, Al-Azhar forged a close alliance with the regime, with its Council of Senior Scholars endorsing anti-Brotherhood measures through fatwas declaring the group a threat to national security and legitimizing the crackdown on its supporters.[101][102] This alignment, including public backing for Sisi's policies, contradicted claims of institutional autonomy, as Al-Azhar's doctrinal output mirrored state priorities in suppressing Islamist opposition.[43] In July 2025, Al-Azhar briefly published a statement by Grand Imam el-Tayeb condemning Israel's starvation tactics in Gaza as genocide, only to delete it hours later following direct pressure from the Egyptian presidency and Foreign Ministry, who warned of diplomatic repercussions.[103][104] This episode highlighted ongoing regime influence, where even foreign policy-sensitive pronouncements are retracted to align with Cairo's cautious stance toward Israel, further eroding perceptions of Al-Azhar's independence.[105]

Global Diplomatic Engagements

Al-Azhar al-Sharif has pursued global diplomatic engagements primarily through interfaith dialogues and educational outreach, exemplified by the signing of the Document on Human Fraternity for World Peace and Living Together on February 4, 2019, in Abu Dhabi by Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb and Pope Francis.[106][107] This accord calls for mutual respect among religions, rejection of violence in God's name, and cooperation against extremism, positioning Al-Azhar as a Sunni authority promoting peaceful coexistence.[108] However, the document avoids explicit repudiation of Islamic supremacist interpretations that prioritize Sharia over pluralistic equality, leading critics to view it as a pragmatic gesture insufficient for addressing root doctrinal tensions.[109] Through its Center for Education Development for Envoys and Foreign Students, Al-Azhar extends scholarships to Muslim students from Asia and Africa, including programs for Indonesian Muhammadiyah cadres in 2025, Djiboutian students with increased slots announced in May 2025, and applicants from Maldives and Kenya for undergraduate studies covering tuition, accommodation, and stipends.[110][111][112] These initiatives, often coordinated with the Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), aim to disseminate Al-Azhar's curriculum globally and build alliances among Muslim-majority nations.[113] In September 2025, Controversies and Criticisms

Resistance to Curricular and Doctrinal Reform

Al-Azhar al-Sharif has consistently resisted efforts to modernize its curriculum and doctrinal positions, prioritizing adherence to classical fiqh interpretations over empirical or contextual adaptations. Despite Egyptian government pressures in the mid-2010s to revise religious education in response to extremism, Al-Azhar retained core traditional teachings, including penalties for apostasy as stipulated in Sharia texts. In June 2016, Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb explicitly stated that leaving Islam constitutes treason against the Islamic community, warranting execution for unrepentant apostates, a position aligned with the four Sunni madhhabs and reflected in ongoing doctrinal output.[59][117] This doctrinal conservatism extends to scientific integration, where Al-Azhar has issued rulings rejecting Darwinian evolution for human origins, favoring literal Quranic creation narratives in educational materials. Egyptian fatwa institutions, influenced by Al-Azhar's authority, have countered evolutionary theory as incompatible with Islamic theology, limiting its presentation in biology curricula to non-human applications or as a refuted hypothesis.[118][119] Reformist intellectuals promoting hermeneutical rationalism have encountered institutional backlash, exemplified by the 1990s case of Nasr Hamid Abu Zayd, whose contextualist Quranic exegesis drew condemnation from Al-Azhar-linked scholars. Although a Cairo University professor, Abu Zayd's works critiquing rigid scripturalism prompted Al-Azhar ulama to support his 1995 court-declared apostasy, resulting in professional ostracism and forced exile.[120][121] Post-2014, following ISIS's territorial rise and calls for ideological counter-narratives, external critiques highlighted scant curricular overhauls at Al-Azhar, with textbooks and fatwas continuing to emphasize unchanging medieval rulings rather than renewed ijtihad responsive to contemporary threats.[109] Analysts noted persistent glorification of early conquests and hudud prescriptions, underscoring limited adaptation despite official anti-extremism rhetoric.[83]

Stances on Jihad, Apostasy, and Extremism

Al-Azhar's doctrinal positions on jihad emphasize a distinction between defensive jihadâ€”permissible to repel aggressionâ€”and offensive jihad, which classical fiqh texts endorsed for expanding Islamic dominion and removing barriers to da'wa, as reflected in unaltered curricula depicting the Prophet Muhammad's conquests as divinely sanctioned imperatives rather than mere historical events.[122][42] These teachings maintain that territories acquired through such jihad, like historical Palestine, constitute perpetual waqf under Muslim sovereignty, justifying ongoing claims irrespective of modern political boundaries.[123] Fatwas from affiliated bodies, such as the International Islamic Fiqh Academy, frame offensive jihad as protective of Islam's propagation, linking it causally to supremacist expansions that prioritize Islamic governance over coexistence.[124] On apostasy, Al-Azhar affirms the sharia-mandated death penalty for unrepentant adult male apostates who publicly renounce Islam after due admonition, as codified in fiqh manuals taught at the institution and reiterated in a 1978 fatwa committee ruling specifying execution by non-fiery means.[59] Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb reinforced this in 2016, describing apostasy as a capital "crime" and "treason" against the Islamic polity, aligning with classical consensus that equates deliberate rejection of faith with sedition warranting hudud punishment to preserve communal orthodoxy.[125] This stance contradicts selective 2014 rhetoric in Al-Azhar's co-signed open letter to ISIS leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, which critiqued indiscriminate takfir and vigilante executions while implicitly upholding court-enforced apostasy penalties under proper sharia procedure, exposing a doctrinal rigidity that enables violence against perceived internal threats despite public disavowals of extremist applications.[59] Regarding extremism, Al-Azhar under el-Tayeb has promoted counter-narratives condemning ISIS as deviant for its khawarij-like takfir excesses, yet equivocated in 2023â€“2025 statements by endorsing Hamas's October 7 attacks as legitimate "resistance" and defensive jihad against occupation, distinct from ISIS terrorism due to contextual "oppression" rather than doctrinal incompatibility.[126] This differentiation overlooks shared roots in jihadist supremacismâ€”both invoke Islamic conquest paradigms and apostasy enforcementâ€”while Al-Azhar's curricula perpetuate un-reformed elements glorifying martial expansion, fostering causal pathways to violence by framing non-Muslim or dissenting entities as perpetual adversaries.[123] Such positions, issued via fatwas and sermons, undermine global deradicalization by privileging historical conquest justifications over unequivocal rejection of offensive ideologies, as evidenced in post-2023 endorsements of Palestinian "jihad" that mirror ISIS rhetoric minus territorial caliphate ambitions.[126]

Positions on Gender Roles and Women's Rights

Al-Azhar al-Sharif endorses polygamy as a permissible practice under Islamic law, restricting men to up to four wives provided they can treat them equitably, as outlined in its official interpretations of Quranic verses such as An-Nisa 4:3.[127] While Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb has described unrestricted polygamy as an "injustice to women and children," he has maintained it as a conditional right rather than prohibiting it outright.[128] This stance aligns with classical fiqh schools taught at Al-Azhar, which prioritize Sharia-derived male authority in family structures over egalitarian reforms.[129] The institution mandates veiling for women as an obligatory religious duty, rejecting any debate on the hijab's requirement and affirming it as imposed by Islamic texts including Quran 24:31 and 33:59.[130] Official statements from Al-Azhar emphasize that the hijab covers a woman's body except face and hands in public, with non-compliance viewed as defiance of divine command.[131] Fatwas prohibit women from leading mixed-gender prayers, citing prophetic traditions that reserve imamate in such settings for men to maintain order and modesty.[132] Al-Azhar's curricula and fatwas stress absolute wifely obedience to husbands in matters of household authority and permissible conduct, drawing from hadiths such as those in Sahih Bukhari mandating submission as a spousal duty.[133] This includes restrictions on women leaving home without permission and allowances for disciplinary measures like light beating if disobedience persists, though recent clarifications limit severity to avoid injury.[134] Such teachings conflict with universal human rights standards, including UN conventions on equality, by embedding hierarchical gender roles that prioritize male guardianship (qiwama) over mutual autonomy.[131] Access to ijtihad for women remains severely limited at Al-Azhar, with few female scholars granted authority to issue independent fatwas due to traditional criteria emphasizing male scholarly dominance and segregation in advanced studies.[135] While women study fiqh, their roles are confined to advisory capacities rather than mujtahid status, perpetuating reliance on male ulama for legal rulings.[136] Inheritance laws upheld by Al-Azhar allocate women half the share of male counterparts in most cases, as per Quran 4:11, rejecting equalization proposals as violations of explicit scriptural mandates.[137] Despite discussions in 2018 on reassessing certain women's issues, the institution reaffirmed these disparities, citing immutable Sharia principles over contemporary equity demands.[136] Reforms proposed that year, such as optional veiling interpretations, faced internal pushback and did not alter core doctrines on inheritance or obedience.[138]

Allegations of Political Subservience and Fatwa Inconsistencies

Critics have alleged that Al-Azhar al-Sharif has repeatedly subordinated its religious authority to Egyptian state interests, issuing or retracting pronouncements in alignment with ruling regimes rather than consistent doctrinal principles.[139][140] During the Nasser era (1952â€“1970), the institution's ulama were compelled into political submission through state-controlled religious monopolies, setting a precedent for regime dependency that persisted under Hosni Mubarak, where Al-Azhar's Grand Imam provided uncritical endorsement of government policies.[140][40] This pattern intensified post-2011 revolution, as Al-Azhar initially gained autonomy but swiftly backed Abdel Fattah el-Sisi's 2013 coup against Muslim Brotherhood president Mohamed Morsi, despite earlier opposition to Brotherhood electoral gains under Mubarak.[141][100] A stark recent example occurred in July 2025, when Al-Azhar's Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb issued a statement on July 22 condemning Israel's "genocidal starvation" in Gaza and urging global intervention, only for it to be deleted hours later following pressure from Egypt's Foreign Minister Badr Abdelatty and the presidency, citing political considerations.[103][104][142] The retraction, which Al-Azhar justified as a "courageous" initiative to prioritize national interests, drew widespread online criticism in Egypt for prioritizing Sisi's foreign policy restraint toward Israel over humanitarian Islamic imperatives.[103][143] Fatwa inconsistencies further underscore these subservience claims, as Al-Azhar's edicts have appeared to shift with geopolitical or domestic pressures. In June 2017, its legal department issued a fatwa denouncing Berlin's Ibn Rushd-Goethe Mosque for permitting mixed-gender prayer, rejecting burqas/niqabs, and welcoming LGBT Muslims, labeling these practices incompatible with Islam.[144][145] This rigid stance contrasted with Al-Azhar's concurrent interfaith initiatives, such as dialogues with the Vatican promoting tolerance, highlighting selective application of orthodoxy amid efforts to project moderate global influence under Sisi.[146] Such variances, observers argue, stem from institutional reliance on state funding and oversight, eroding independent ijtihad as noted in critiques from dissenting ulama who decry politicized fatwa councils.[76][146]

Influence and Legacy

Contributions to Islamic Scholarship

Al-Azhar al-Sharif's library serves as a major repository for Islamic texts, housing approximately 50,000 manuscripts that encompass core disciplines such as fiqh, hadith, tafsir, and usul al-fiqh, thereby facilitating the preservation and study of pre-modern Sunni scholarship.[147] These holdings, documented across more than eight million pages in some estimates, have enabled ongoing textual analysis and copying traditions that guard against loss of original works amid historical upheavals like invasions and colonial periods.[148] However, this preservation emphasizes rote transmission of medieval corpora over critical reevaluation, resulting in scholarly outputs that largely replicate established interpretations without substantial methodological innovation. The institution's ijazah systemâ€”formal authorizations granted by senior scholarsâ€”has been instrumental in sustaining the four primary Sunni madhabs (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali) by certifying chains of transmission (isnad) for key texts and rulings, extending Al-Azhar's influence to global networks of students and institutions.[149] This mechanism ensures doctrinal fidelity, as recipients gain legitimacy to teach specific works, fostering a decentralized yet interconnected preservation of fiqh and hadith methodologies that have persisted since the institution's consolidation as a Sunni center in the 12th century under Ayyubid patronage.[150] In linguistic scholarship, Al-Azhar scholars have advanced Arabic grammar (nahw) through pedagogical refinements and commentaries on classical foundations, integrating analytical tools into curricula that train generations in precise Quranic exegesis and legal argumentation, though often within rigid madhhab boundaries that limit adaptation to contemporary linguistics.[151] This focus on un-reformed traditionalism underscores Al-Azhar's role as a bulwark for orthodox Sunni intellectual heritage, prioritizing continuity over reformist departures evident in rival centers like those in the Hijaz or Ottoman madrasas.

Impact on Global Muslim Communities

Al-Azhar al-Sharif's influence extends to global Muslim communities primarily through its alumni, who disseminate the institution's traditional Sunni scholarship and jurisprudential approaches. Thousands of Al-Azhar graduates, known as Azharis, have shaped Islamic education and leadership in Southeast Asia, particularly Indonesia, where they have integrated into pesantren (traditional Islamic boarding schools) since the 19th century, adapting Al-Azhar's curriculum to local contexts while promoting wasatiyyah, or centrist Islam.[63] This has reinforced Shafi'i fiqh dominance in the region, influencing religious training for millions of students and countering more literalist imports from the Arabian Peninsula.[152] The World Association for Al-Azhar Graduates (WAAG), established to connect alumni worldwide, supports their roles as imams, educators, and community leaders, with branches in Europe, including the UK, facilitating the application of Al-Azhar-derived fiqh in diaspora settings.[153][154] In Western Europe and the UK, Al-Azhar-trained scholars lead numerous mosques and contribute to informal Sharia advisory bodies, guiding family law, inheritance, and ethical issues according to classical madhhab principles, often prioritizing religious norms over secular legal adaptations.[155] Al-Azhar's Global Fatwa Center issues rulings accessible to Muslims internationally via digital platforms and hotlines, addressing contemporary dilemmas while upholding orthodox interpretations that reject innovations.[156] This framework has helped position Al-Azhar as a counterweight to Wahhabism and Salafism, which it critiques for undermining Ash'ari theology, Sufi practices, and established Sunni consensus, thereby preserving a hierarchical yet inclusive orthodoxy in diverse communities.[157][158] However, the transnational export of its fiqh embeds doctrinal elements of religious gradation, where non-Muslims and certain sects hold subordinate status under classical rulings, influencing diaspora attitudes toward pluralism.[159]

Critiques of Enduring Supremacist Elements

Critics contend that Al-Azhar's curriculum perpetuates doctrinal elements viewing non-Muslims, or kafirs, as inherently inferior, drawing from classical Sharia interpretations that mandate discriminatory practices such as the dhimmi system, where non-Muslims pay jizya tribute and face restrictions on public worship, testimony in court, and social interactions.[160] These teachings, embedded in fiqh texts endorsed by Al-Azhar, normalize a hierarchy privileging Muslims, with fatwas historically prohibiting Muslims from greeting non-Muslims first or emulating their customs, fostering an ethos of separation and superiority.[160] Egyptian reformer Mahmoud Muhammad Taha al-Beheiry, imprisoned for his critiques, argued that such un-reformed content directly contributes to supremacist attitudes, estimating that 70-80% of recent terrorism stems from Al-Azhar's doctrinal output.[161] This supremacist framework, critics assert, causally impedes assimilation by inculcating beliefs that Islamic governance must supersede host societies' laws, leading to self-segregation and parallel structures in diaspora communities. Post-colonial expansion of Al-Azhar's influenceâ€”through training imams and scholars dispatched to over 90 countries via scholarships and institutes since the mid-20th centuryâ€”has embedded these views in global Muslim networks, correlating with observed patterns of ghettoization where integration rates lag due to doctrinal rejection of secular equality.[162] For instance, Al-Azhar-trained clerics in Europe have issued rulings against Muslim participation in non-Islamic holidays or interfaith equality, reinforcing insularity over civic participation. Verifiable links exist between Al-Azhar alumni and jihadist ideologies rooted in these unreformed teachings, such as Abdullah Azzam, a 1960s graduate who founded the precursor to al-Qaeda and authored Join the Caravan, advocating perpetual jihad against non-Muslims based on classical prescriptions of conquest and subjugation.[163] Critics like Raymond Ibrahim highlight how Al-Azhar's endorsement of apostasy lawsâ€”prescribing death for leaving Islamâ€”and warfare doctrines against kafirs until submission provides ideological continuity for such figures, without doctrinal disavowal, sustaining a supremacist legacy that prioritizes expansion over coexistence.[160][163] Despite Al-Azhar's public moderatism, its resistance to purging these elementsâ€”evident in rejecting calls for Sharia reform post-2011â€”ensures their endurance, as noted by internal dissenters facing suppression.[164]

Recent Developments (2010â€“Present)

Tenure of Ahmed el-Tayeb

Ahmed el-Tayeb assumed the position of Grand Imam of Al-Azhar al-Sharif in March 2010, succeeding Muhammad Sayyid Tantawi.[165] Born on January 6, 1946, in Qurna, Luxor, to a family affiliated with a Sufi order, el-Tayeb holds a doctorate in Islamic philosophy from Al-Azhar University and has authored works on jurisprudence, Sharia sciences, and Sufism.[166] [167] Despite his Sufi heritage, his leadership prioritizes the unyielding authority of Sharia as the foundational framework for Islamic doctrine and education at Al-Azhar.[168] Under el-Tayeb's tenure, Al-Azhar has issued public calls for revising religious pedagogy to combat extremism, notably in February 2015 when he advocated overhauling Islamic teaching methods to address flawed Koranic interpretations fueling militancy.[169] Such rhetoric aligns with broader Egyptian government pressures for doctrinal moderation post-Arab Spring, yet substantive curricular reforms have stalled, with Al-Azhar's K-12 and seminary programs retaining core traditionalist elements on Sharia application, jihad, and apostasy penalties as of 2023 analyses.[164] [42] This persistence reflects institutional resistance to external mandates, preserving Al-Azhar's role as custodian of orthodox Sunni jurisprudence over adaptive reinterpretation.[170] El-Tayeb has broadened Al-Azhar's international diplomacy, positioning it as a voice for "moderate" Sunni authority amid global scrutiny.[166] A recent example is his attendance at the Arab Media Summit in Dubai from May 26 to 28, 2025, where he delivered a keynote address alongside regional leaders, underscoring efforts to amplify Al-Azhar's media and cultural outreach while maintaining doctrinal primacy.[171] [172] This engagement highlights continuity in traditionalism, as reformist overtures coexist with uncompromised adherence to classical Islamic texts.[173]

Responses to Terrorism and Geopolitical Crises

In 2015, Al-Azhar issued a fatwa declaring ISIS, Al-Qaeda, and the Taliban non-Islamic organizations whose actions deviated from core Islamic teachings, positioning the institution as a counter to Salafi-jihadist ideologies.[109] This stance extended to broader condemnations of terrorism, with Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb emphasizing religious reforms to combat extremism's textual misinterpretations during international forums.[9] Such pronouncements, however, have proven selective, targeting groups like ISIS while exempting violence categorized as Palestinian "resistance" against Israel, as evidenced by Al-Azhar's post-October 2023 Gaza statements that decried Israeli operations as "genocide" and "unrelenting killing" without analogous rebuke of Hamas's October 7 attacks or subsequent militant tactics.[9] On October 7, 2024, Al-Azhar marked the conflict's anniversary by condemning Israel's "machine of destruction" in Gaza, framing Palestinian steadfastness positively amid civilian targeting claims, yet omitting any fatwa against jihadist elements within the resistance framework.[174] This equivocation intensified in 2025 amid Gaza's humanitarian crisis. On July 22, Al-Azhar appealed globally for urgent intervention to halt what it termed Israel's "genocidal war" and induced famine via siege, but retracted the statement within hours following reported intervention by Egypt's foreign minister, reflecting pressures to align with Cairo's restrained diplomacy under President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi.[103] The deletion drew domestic backlash for perceived capitulation, highlighting Al-Azhar's doctrinal commitments to jihad concepts tempered by state oversight, which constrains unqualified repudiations of offensive jihad in non-Western contexts.[142] Israeli outlets, in turn, escalated rhetoric against Al-Azhar, with Maariv on July 30, 2025, branding it the "head of the snake" for fueling anti-Israel sentiment and advocating its dismantlement to curb ideological influence on global Muslim opinion.[175] On October 8, Al-Azhar countered by denouncing Israel's proposed Gaza occupation as a "stain of shame" aimed at erasing Palestinian identity, underscoring persistent tensions where geopolitical crises elicit partisan fatwas prioritizing ummah solidarity over universal anti-terrorism.[176] This pattern suggests causal constraints from Egypt's Sisi-era realpolitikâ€”prioritizing regime stability and bilateral tiesâ€”over doctrinal purges of supremacist violence endorsements.[9]
