Quantitative electroencephalography

Quantitative electroencephalography (qEEG or QEEG), also known as brain mapping or brain electrical activity mapping (BEAM), is a field concerned with the numerical analysis of electroencephalography (EEG) data and associated behavioral correlates.[1]

Quantitative Electroencephalography

Techniques used in digital signal analysis are extended to the analysis of electroencephalography (EEG). These include wavelet analysis and Fourier analysis, with new focus on shared activity between rhythms including phase synchrony (coherence, phase lag) and magnitude synchrony (comodulation/correlation, and asymmetry). The analog signal comprises a microvoltage time series of the EEG, sampled digitally and sampling rates adequate to over-sample the signal (using the Nyquist principle of exceeding twice the highest frequency being detected). Modern EEG amplifiers use adequate sampling to resolve the EEG across the traditional medical band from DC to 70 or 100 Hz, using sample rates of 250/256, 500/512, to over 1000 samples per second, depending on the intended application. QEEG can be performed by open-source toolboxes such as EEGLAB or the Neurophysiological Biomarker Toolbox. Several QEEG products have received Class 2 FDA medical device clearance and the method has received some medical acceptance for use in epilepsy patients. However QEEG has not been endorsed by the American Academy of Neurology or the American Clinical Neurophysiology Society.[2]

Uses

Quantitative electroencephalography (qEEG) is considered investigational by the American Academy of Neurology and the American Clinical Neurophysiology Society for most clinical diagnostic purposes, though it is used as an adjunct to traditional EEG in limited cases, such as evaluating cerebrovascular disorders, encephalopathy, dementia, and epilepsy when standard neuroimaging or routine EEG results are inconclusive.[5][6] It remains investigational and is not recommended for routine diagnosis of mild traumatic brain injury or psychiatric disorders. The use of qEEG techniques in clinical and research investigations continues as of 2025. QEEG has also been utilized to provide neurofeedback, which is a form of biofeedback, wherein electrical activity in the brain is monitored by a computer program, which is applied to modulate visual or auditory stimuliâ€”these stimuli, in turn, are designed to be controlled by the user.
