Caratacus

Caratacus (Latin: Caratacus; fl. 40s–50s AD) was a chieftain of the southeastern British Catuvellauni tribe who organized and led the most sustained native resistance to the Roman conquest of Britain following the Claudian invasion of AD 43.[1] Emerging as a leader after the death of his father, King Cunobelinus, whose realm had dominated much of southeastern Britain, Caratacus coordinated Brittonic forces against the initial Roman landings under Aulus Plautius, including battles near the Thames and Medway rivers.[2] Following early setbacks and the death of his brother Togodumnus, he shifted to prolonged guerrilla warfare in the western hill country, allying with tribes such as the Silures and Ordovices while evading Roman governors like Publius Ostorius Scapula for nearly a decade.[1] In AD 51, after seeking refuge among the Brigantes, he was betrayed and handed over in chains by their queen, Cartimandua, to Roman custody.[1] Brought to Rome for execution as part of Emperor Claudius's triumph, Caratacus delivered a bold oration emphasizing Roman mercy's greater glory through sparing him, which reportedly moved Claudius to grant clemency to him and his family, allowing them to reside in the imperial capital.[1] His defiance and eloquence, preserved in accounts by Tacitus and Cassius Dio—primary Roman historians whose narratives, while potentially colored by elite perspectives, draw from contemporary records—established Caratacus as an enduring emblem of Brittonic valor against imperial expansion.[1][2]

Name and Etymology

Variants and Linguistic Origins

The name Caratacus represents the Latinized rendering of the Brythonic personal name Caratācos, attested in Roman historical accounts of 1st-century AD Britain.[3] This form appears variably as Caratacus or Caractacus in classical sources, reflecting inconsistencies in Latin transcription of Celtic phonetics, where the 'c' and 't' sounds were adapted from the original Brittonic pronunciation.[4] In modern Celtic languages descending from Brythonic, the name evolves into Welsh Caradog (or archaic Caradawc), Middle Welsh Caratawc, and Breton Karadeg, preserving the core structure while adapting to phonological shifts such as vowel rounding and consonant softening over centuries.[5] These variants stem from the same Proto-Celtic compound, with the first element deriving from karu, an ancient root meaning "to love" or "beloved," akin to Latin carus ("dear") and paralleled in Welsh caru ("love") and cariad ("darling").[3] The suffix -ācos functions as a common Celtic nominal ending for agentive or descriptive names, yielding a meaning approximately "the beloved one" or "loved," without connotations of amicability or peace as sometimes misinterpreted in later folklore.[4] This etymology aligns with Indo-European patterns traceable to Proto-Indo-European *keh₂- or *ka- ("to desire" or "like"), underscoring the name's deep roots in pre-Roman Celtic onomastics rather than Roman invention.[6]

Background and Early Career

Family Lineage and Tribal Context

Caratacus was the son of Cunobelinus, a prominent ruler of the Catuvellauni tribe who governed southeastern Britain from approximately 9 BC until his death around AD 40.[7] He shared leadership responsibilities with his brother Togodumnus following their father's demise, with coin evidence indicating Caratacus controlled secondary territories while Togodumnus held the tribal heartland.[7] Roman historian Cassius Dio explicitly identifies both as sons of Cunobelinus (rendered as Cynobellinus), who had established the tribe's dominance through military expansion and trade networks prior to the Claudian invasion. The Catuvellauni, a Belgic Celtic tribe of Iron Age Britain, occupied the fertile lands north of the River Thames, encompassing modern Hertfordshire, Bedfordshire, Buckinghamshire, and parts of Cambridgeshire, with key settlements at Verulamium (near St Albans) and, through conquest, Camulodunum (Colchester).[8] Emerging in the late 1st century BC, they likely derived their name from ancient Gaulish terms connoting "war-chiefs," reflecting their martial prowess and hierarchical structure, as inferred from archaeological finds including hillforts, oppida, and coin hoards imitating Roman designs.[9] Under Cunobelinus, the tribe subdued neighbors like the Trinovantes and Atrebates, amassing control over much of southeast England and fostering economic ties with Rome via exports of grain, metals, and slaves, evidenced by high-quality coinage bearing tribal rulers' names.[8] This pre-Roman hegemony positioned the Catuvellauni as the most formidable southern British power, prompting direct Roman intervention in AD 43.[10]

Pre-Roman Influence in Britain

Caratacus, son of Cunobelinus, emerged as a key figure among the Catuvellauni tribe, which dominated southeastern Britain during the early 1st century AD through military expansion and control of trade routes. Under Cunobelinus's rule, spanning approximately 9–43 AD, the Catuvellauni extended their influence from their core territory in modern Hertfordshire to encompass Kent (Trinovantes), Essex, and parts of Surrey, evidenced by coin distributions and oppida like Camulodunum (Colchester).[10] Caratacus, alongside his brother Togodumnus, likely contributed to these efforts, as Roman sources attribute the tribe's aggressive posture to the brothers following Cunobelinus's death around 40–43 AD.[7] A pivotal aspect of Caratacus's pre-invasion influence involved conflicts with neighboring tribes, particularly the displacement of Verica, king of the Atrebates in modern Hampshire and Sussex. Cassius Dio records that Verica's ousting by Caratacus prompted the Atrebates ruler to flee to Rome with his seal, providing Emperor Claudius with a pretext for the 43 AD invasion to restore him.[10] This expansionist activity, possibly extending to the Dobunni in Gloucestershire around 41 AD, underscored the Catuvellauni's role in consolidating power amid inter-tribal rivalries, though direct archaeological attribution to Caratacus remains elusive beyond familial coinage links.[11] Coinage provides tangible evidence of Catuvellauni prominence, with issues bearing "CVNOBELINVS" (Cunobelinus) and patronymic references to sons like Caratacus, indicating a centralized authority capable of minting in gold staters for trade with Gaul and Rome.[12] These artifacts, found widely in hoards, reflect economic influence rather than purely military, yet align with the tribe's capacity to field organized resistance later. Roman historiographical accounts, while biased toward justifying conquest, consistently portray Caratacus as inheriting and amplifying his father's anti-Roman stance, influenced by druidic networks opposing continental ties.[7] Primary evidence for his independent actions before 43 AD is sparse, relying on later summaries like Dio's, which emphasize Catuvellauni aggression as a trigger for Roman intervention rather than detailed biography.

Military Resistance to Rome

Response to Claudian Invasion (43 AD)

Caratacus, as ruler of the Catuvellauni tribe, which dominated southeastern Britain following expansions under his father Cunobelinus, responded to the Roman invasion by mobilizing allied tribal forces to contest the landings in Kent during the summer of 43 AD.[13] Alongside his brother Togodumnus, he led initial opposition against the four-legion expedition (approximately 20,000–40,000 troops) commanded by Aulus Plautius, which targeted Catuvellaunian strongholds including Camulodunum (modern Colchester).[14][15] The Britons under Caratacus and Togodumnus first engaged the Romans at the Battle of the Medway, where Dio Cassius reports the defenders arrayed chariots and infantry on the river's far bank amid marshy terrain to impede crossing.[16] Roman forces, divided under commanders like Hosidius Geta and Vespasian, forded the waterway despite resistance, launching a surprise assault that devolved into a two-day melee; Britons suffered heavy losses as many drowned in retreat or were cut down, per Dio's account.[16][17] Togodumnus fell during these engagements or a subsequent skirmish, weakening Catuvellaunian leadership, though Caratacus evaded capture and withdrew forces westward.[18] The Romans then advanced to the Thames, crossing against further opposition—possibly under Caratacus's direction—before Emperor Claudius arrived in late 43 AD to oversee the siege and fall of Camulodunum, marking the invasion's initial success despite ongoing British defiance.[16][19]

Guerrilla Campaigns and Tribal Alliances

Following the Roman consolidation of southeastern Britain after the invasion of 43 AD, Caratacus evaded capture and relocated to the territory of the Silures tribe in southeastern Wales, initiating a prolonged phase of irregular warfare against Roman expansion. There, he capitalized on the region's dense woodlands, steep hills, and river barriers to launch ambushes and skirmishes, eschewing open battles in favor of hit-and-run tactics that inflicted attrition on pursuing legions.[1] Roman historian Tacitus records that Caratacus's forces achieved "many an indecisive and many a favourable success," eroding Roman morale and demonstrating the effectiveness of terrain-dependent guerrilla methods against disciplined infantry formations.[20] These operations, spanning approximately 44 to 47 AD under governors Publius Ostorius Scapula's predecessors, delayed Roman advances westward by compelling garrisons to disperse resources for defense rather than conquest.[1] To amplify his military capacity, Caratacus cultivated alliances among unconquered western tribes, most notably integrating the warlike Silures with the Ordovices of northwestern Wales into a loose confederation bound by shared opposition to Roman subjugation. This partnership, forged around 48 AD amid Ostorius's governorship (47–52 AD), pooled warriors and intelligence, enabling coordinated raids that targeted Roman supply lines and isolated outposts.[21] Tacitus attributes the Silures' heightened resolve to Caratacus's leadership, portraying him as their "prop and stay" who inspired defiance through appeals to liberty and ancestral valor.[1] Such tribal coalitions, though fractious due to pre-existing rivalries, temporarily unified disparate Celtic groups—estimated at tens of thousands of fighters—against a Roman expeditionary force numbering around 20,000–30,000 legionaries and auxiliaries in Britain at the time, thereby sustaining resistance until internal betrayals eroded cohesion.[20]

Defeat, Flight, and Betrayal (47–51 AD)

Following the appointment of Publius Ostorius Scapula as governor of Britain in 47 AD, Roman forces intensified efforts to subdue resistant tribes in the west, where Caratacus had relocated after earlier setbacks and rallied support among the Silures and Ordovices through guerrilla tactics that exploited the difficult terrain of modern-day Wales.[1] Ostorius, prioritizing veteran settlements for security, diverted troops to construct forts and counter these hit-and-run raids, which delayed full conquest but gradually eroded Caratacus' alliances by demonstrating Roman logistical superiority and willingness to sustain prolonged operations.[22] By 50 AD, Ostorius cornered Caratacus in the territory of the Ordovices, forcing a decisive engagement where Roman discipline prevailed over the Britons' numerical advantage and familiarity with the landscape, though the exact site remains unidentified amid debated candidates like Caer Caradoc hillfort.[18] Tacitus records that Caratacus escaped the battlefield but lost significant forces, compelling him to seek refuge among the Brigantes in northern Britain, a tribe under the rule of Queen Cartimandua, who had been installed as a Roman client ruler since 43 AD to maintain border stability.[1] [23] In 51 AD, Cartimandua, motivated by self-preservation and Roman favoritism—which rewarded her loyalty with territorial expansions—betrayed Caratacus, his wife, daughters, and key followers by binding them in chains and delivering them to Roman custody, an act Tacitus attributes to her ambition rather than coercion, as it solidified her position against internal rivals like her husband Venutius.[1] This handover marked the effective end of organized resistance led by Caratacus, allowing Ostorius to shift focus to the still-hostile Silures, though it sowed seeds of future unrest among the Brigantes.[23]

Capture and Roman Imprisonment

Handover by Cartimandua

After his defeat by Roman forces under Publius Ostorius Scapula in the territories of the Silures and Ordovices circa 51 AD, Caratacus sought refuge with Cartimandua, queen of the Brigantes, a tribe allied with Rome through client relationships.[24] [21] Tacitus records that Cartimandua, motivated by loyalty to Rome and the prospect of rewards, betrayed Caratacus despite his expectation of protection; she ordered him placed in chains and handed him over to Ostorius.[24] This act of treachery, as Tacitus describes it, facilitated the Roman consolidation of control in northern Britain, as the Brigantes' cooperation neutralized a key resistance figure.[24] [21] The handover strained internal Brigantian dynamics, contributing to later unrest under Cartimandua's husband Venutius, who opposed her pro-Roman stance, though it immediately secured her position and Roman favor.[24] Caratacus, along with his captured family members—his wife, daughter, and brothers—were then transported southward for shipment to Rome.[24]

Journey to Rome and Imperial Audience

Following his capture by Queen Cartimandua of the Brigantes in 51 AD, Caratacus was delivered in chains to Roman authorities under the command of Publius Ostorius Scapula, the governor of Britain.[1] His wife, daughter, and brothers were also seized and transported with him as captives, symbolizing the comprehensive defeat of British resistance.[1] The journey from northern Britain to Rome likely involved overland travel to a coastal port, followed by sea crossing to the continent and then southward through Gaul, though primary accounts provide no detailed itinerary beyond the endpoint.[17] Upon reaching Rome later that year, Caratacus and his family were paraded through the city's streets in a ceremonial display of Roman triumph, intended to showcase imperial conquests to the populace despite not forming part of Claudius's earlier formal triumph of 44 AD.[1] This public exhibition underscored the political value of his capture, enhancing Claudius's reputation as a conqueror amid internal Roman power struggles.[1] At the subsequent imperial audience before Emperor Claudius and the Roman Senate, Caratacus delivered a defiant speech, as preserved in Tacitus's Annals (12.37). He argued that his prolonged resistance against a vastly superior empire was no crime but a point of honor, contrasting Rome's global ambitions with the trivial scale of his British realm: "If you Romans view the acquisition of wealth as a mark of nobility, why begrudge a poor and insignificant people their liberty?"[1] Tacitus portrays the oration as rhetorically effective, delivered through an interpreter, and notes its impact in evoking admiration even among Romans familiar with his name from prior campaigns.[1] Cassius Dio corroborates the event in Roman History (60.20), adding that Caratacus expressed astonishment at Rome's grandeur upon arrival, remarking to his wife that such splendor explained their subjugation but questioning why further enslavement was necessary after conquest. Claudius, advised by his inner circle including Narcissus, ultimately spared Caratacus's life, granting clemency to him and his relatives to project magnanimity and bolster his regime's legitimacy through publicized mercy toward a notable foe.[1] This decision contrasted with standard Roman practice for defeated rebel leaders, reflecting pragmatic considerations: executing Caratacus risked diminishing the perceived scale of British victories, while pardon allowed Claudius to claim credit for both conquest and clemency.[1] The family was granted modest estates in Italy, where Caratacus reportedly lived out his days without further incident.[1]

Claudius's Decision and Aftermath

In 51 AD, Caratacus, along with his wife, daughter, and brothers, was transported to Rome in chains and participated in Emperor Claudius's triumph celebrating the British conquest. Presented before the Roman senate and emperor, he delivered a defiant yet pragmatic speech, questioning the scale of Roman ambition: "If you Romans choose to overlook the fact that you possess the greatest empire in the world, and still desire to add Britain to it, are you unaware that my destitution will be a more glorious testimonial to your clemency than my destruction?"[25] Tacitus records this address in the Annals, portraying Caratacus as emphasizing how his prolonged resistance amplified Rome's prestige, thereby urging mercy to showcase imperial magnanimity.[25] Claudius, reportedly moved by the speech and possibly influenced by his wife Agrippina's advocacy for clemency to bolster her own prestige, overruled the senate's inclination toward execution and granted pardon to Caratacus and his family.[25] This decision aligned with Claudius's policy of integrating provincial elites to stabilize conquests, as evidenced by similar pardons in other campaigns.[26] The family was released from bondage, provided a residence in Rome, and permitted to live under Roman supervision without further punishment. Following his liberation, Caratacus reportedly toured Rome's opulent structures, including palaces and marble-columned baths, and exclaimed to Claudius: "And all this, would you have given up for these miserable huts?"—a remark attributed to him by Cassius Dio, highlighting his astonishment at Roman wealth contrasted with British simplicity. No records indicate subsequent rebellion or execution; he appears to have remained in Rome until at least Claudius's death in 54 AD, fading from historical accounts thereafter.[27] This outcome underscored Rome's strategic use of mercy as a tool for psychological dominance over defeated foes, transforming a symbol of resistance into a living testament to imperial power.[28]

Historical Sources and Evidence

Accounts in Roman Historiography

Tacitus' Annals, written circa 116 AD, furnishes the most detailed Roman historiographical treatment of Caratacus in Book 12, chapters 31–37.[1] He depicts Caratacus as the son of Cunobelinus, who assumed leadership of British resistance following the Claudian invasion of 43 AD, coordinating guerrilla warfare against Roman governors Aulus Plautius and Publius Ostorius Scapula from approximately 47 to 51 AD.[1] Tacitus describes Caratacus' strategic relocation to the Silures in modern Wales, his alliances with tribes including the Ordovices, and the climactic defeat in 51 AD near the Severn River, where Roman forces under Ostorius overcame fortified positions despite heavy casualties.[1] The narrative includes the capture of Caratacus' wife and daughter, the surrender of his brothers, his flight to the Brigantes, betrayal by their queen Cartimandua, and handover to Roman custody.[1] In Rome, Tacitus records Caratacus' oration before Claudius and the Senate in 51 AD, in which he contrasted his prior freedom with Roman mercy, arguing that execution would diminish the emperor's glory; Claudius commuted the death sentence, granting Caratacus, his wife, and brothers residence in Italy.[1] Tacitus emphasizes Caratacus' personal valor and the respect he commanded, portraying his resistance as a testament to British ferocity while underscoring Roman triumph.[1] Cassius Dio's Roman History, composed in the early 3rd century AD, offers a succinct account in Book 60, chapter 20, focusing on the aftermath of capture. Dio notes Caratacus' presentation among British captives during Claudius' triumph celebrating the conquest, the emperor's pardon to showcase leniency toward a notable foe, and Caratacus' reported astonishment at Rome's opulence, remarking that its defenders would never have surrendered such a prize. Unlike Tacitus, Dio omits extended details of campaigns or the full speech, prioritizing the imperial decision and Caratacus' post-pardon wanderings in the city. His narrative aligns with Tacitus on the mercy granted but derives from separate senatorial or archival traditions, reflecting a later perspective on Claudian propaganda. No other primary Roman historians, such as Suetonius in his Lives of the Twelve Caesars or Josephus in his Jewish works, provide substantive references to Caratacus, limiting the corpus to Tacitus and Dio.[29] Subsequent Roman epitomators like Eutropius briefly echoed these sources in the 4th century AD, but without independent details, affirming the duo's centrality. Tacitus' senatorial background lends his account proximity to events via official Acta Senatus records, while Dio's relies more on prior compilations, introducing minor variances in emphasis but no contradictions on core facts.[1]

Archaeological Corroboration and Debates

Archaeological evidence for Caratacus primarily derives from numismatic finds, which corroborate his leadership of the Catuvellauni tribe and his extension of influence prior to the Claudian invasion of 43 AD. Gold and silver coins inscribed with his name, such as a rare gold stater discovered in 2019 featuring a horse and Latin legend, attest to his authority and adoption of Roman-influenced minting techniques, likely reflecting alliances or trade contacts.[30] These coins, distributed across southeast Britain including sites near modern Reading, align with historical accounts of Catuvellauni dominance and have been found in contexts suggesting pre-Roman economic centralization, such as the planned oppidum at Sleaford or potential urban precursors identified through geophysical surveys.[31] Hillforts and earthworks in southern and western Britain provide indirect support for the guerrilla resistance described in Roman sources, with fortifications like those at Old Oswestry or Caer Caradoc showing Iron Age modifications consistent with defensive adaptations around 43–51 AD. Roman military infrastructure, including forts established by governors like Ostorius Scapula, overlays these sites, indicating conquest phases that followed Caratacus's campaigns, though no artifacts directly name him beyond coins.[19] Debates center on the location of Caratacus's final defeat in 51 AD, as Tacitus describes a battle in a narrow defile with a swift river and commanding hill, but archaeological surveys have failed to yield conclusive evidence like mass weapon deposits or Roman casualty pits at proposed sites. Candidates include Church Stretton in Shropshire, matching topographic features and proximity to Ordovices territory, and areas near the rivers Dyfi or Dee in Wales, where local traditions and limited hoard finds, such as the Malpas hoard of Iron Age coins potentially linked to fleeing supporters, fuel speculation.[32] [33] However, the absence of definitive markers—attributed to perishable materials, post-battle scavenging, and erosion—means reliance on literary topography over material culture, with scholars cautioning against over-interpreting ambiguous hoards or earthworks as direct corroboration.[19]

Legacy and Cultural Depictions

Medieval Welsh and Arthurian Associations

In medieval Welsh literature, Caratacus is preserved under the name Caradog, most notably as Caradog ap Bran, the son of the mythical king Bran the Blessed (Bendigeidfran). This figure appears in the Second Branch of the Mabinogi (compiled circa 12th–13th centuries from earlier oral traditions), where Caradog serves as chief among the seven leaders entrusted with governing Britain during Bran's expedition to Ireland over the abduction of his sister Branwen. Upon Bran's death and the return of survivors bearing his severed head, Caradog hosts a feast at Harddlech (possibly Harlech) and later at Gwales, before slaying a cauldron-born champion in a confrontation that echoes themes of British defiance against invaders, though recast against Irish rather than Roman foes.[34][35] Scholars have proposed that this legendary Caradog euhemerizes the historical Caratacus, conflating him with pre-Roman British rulers to fit a mythic genealogy linking Celtic kings to biblical or early Christian origins, such as Bran's purported role in introducing elements of Christianity to Britain (a later interpolation absent from core Mabinogi narratives). However, the legends diverge substantially from Roman historiographical accounts, lacking references to Roman captivity, tribal alliances, or specific campaigns, suggesting folkloric adaptation rather than direct transmission; Welsh tradition thus recalls Caratacus primarily as a symbol of sovereignty and martial leadership within a silurian or southeastern British context.[36] Arthurian associations arise through name similarity and shared motifs of resistance, with Caradog Vreichvras ("Caradog of the Strong/Steel Arm"), a warrior-king of Gwent and knight in Arthur's court, appearing in Welsh tales like Culhwch and Olwen (circa 11th century) and the Welsh Triads. This Caradog, portrayed as foster-father to Arthur's queen Gwenhwyfar and a bearer of a healing cauldron, embodies heroic valor against supernatural threats, potentially echoing Caratacus's archetype of unyielding defense; medieval genealogies occasionally merge these figures, tracing Arthurian lineages back to Caradog ap Bran as a progenitor of post-Roman British royalty. Such links, however, remain speculative, as Vreichvras's era (traditionally 5th–6th century) postdates Caratacus by centuries, and primary texts show no explicit Roman conquest narrative.[37]

Modern Scholarship and Nationalistic Interpretations

Modern scholarship on Caratacus emphasizes the limitations of primary Roman sources, such as Tacitus's Annals and Cassius Dio's Roman History, which portray his resistance through a lens favoring Roman magnanimity and cultural superiority, potentially embellishing details like his speech to Claudius to underscore imperial clemency.[38] Historians like Graham Webster, in works analyzing Caratacus's guerrilla tactics and leadership from 43 to 51 CE, reconstruct his campaigns using archaeological evidence from hillforts and coin hoards, arguing that his nine-year evasion reflected adaptive tribal warfare rather than a centralized British monarchy.[39] Debates persist over key battle sites, with some scholars proposing locations in the Welsh borders, such as near modern Caer Caradoc, based on terrain suitability for ambushes described by Tacitus, while others question the scale of resistance, noting rapid submissions by southeastern tribes like the Atrebates and rapid Roman consolidation post-43 CE invasion.[39][13] Archaeological corroboration, including fortified sites in the Severn Valley associated with Silures and Ordovices allies, supports Tacitus's account of prolonged western resistance under Publius Ostorius Scapula's governorship, but modern analyses reject notions of a pan-British or unified Celtic identity, viewing Caratacus instead as a Catuvellauni leader coordinating fragmented tribal coalitions against incremental Roman expansion.[39] Scholars critique Roman narratives for understating British military sophistication, such as hit-and-run tactics that delayed conquest until 51 CE, and highlight source discrepancies, like Dio's emphasis on Caratacus as a barbaric foil to Roman opulence, which may exaggerate his tribal disunity to glorify Claudius's triumph.[38] These interpretations prioritize empirical evidence over romanticized unity, attributing his eventual betrayal by Cartimandua to intra-British power rivalries rather than singular Roman inevitability.[13] In Victorian and Edwardian Britain, Caratacus was appropriated as a nationalist icon of defiance and eloquence, featured in school readers like Stories from English History (1881) to instill patriotism by contrasting his noble resistance with Roman admiration, often quoting adapted versions of his plea against enslavement of his "poor tents."[38] This reception, evident in Edward Elgar's 1898 cantata Caractacus dedicated to Queen Victoria, framed ancient Britons as precursors to imperial Britain—fierce yet ultimately benefiting from "civilization"—to justify contemporary empire-building while eliding anti-imperial undertones in Tacitus.[38] Such portrayals selectively emphasized his pardon by Claudius to parallel British tolerance, but modern critiques note their ideological bias, ignoring Roman source manipulations and using Caratacus to reconcile barbarism with progress in a era of expanding colonial narratives.[38] In Welsh contexts, he merged with Caradog legends to symbolize enduring Celtic autonomy, though this romanticization often outpaces verifiable evidence from Roman campaigns in Wales circa 47–51 CE.[39]
