John Quiñones


John Quiñones is an American broadcast journalist born in San Antonio, Texas, who joined ABC News in 1982 as a general assignment correspondent based in Miami.[1] Throughout his over four-decade tenure at the network, he has reported for programs including 20/20, Nightline, and Good Morning America, covering major events and conducting investigative pieces.[1] Quiñones is best known as the creator and host of What Would You Do?, a hidden-camera series that premiered in 2008 and examines public responses to moral dilemmas through staged scenarios.[1][2] His work has earned him seven national Emmy Awards, a George Foster Peabody Award, and recognition for influencing U.S. military policies on sexual harassment.[1]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood

John Quiñones was born in 1952 in San Antonio, Texas, the son of Bruno Quiñones, a janitor, and Maria Quiñones, a domestic worker.[3] His family, of Mexican descent, had deep roots in Texas as fifth-generation residents descended from generations of Spanish-speaking laborers.[4] They resided in extreme poverty in the Mexican-American barrio on the city's west side, where Quiñones grew up alongside two sisters in a close-knit, Catholic household.[5] As a young child, Quiñones spoke only Spanish and entered first grade without knowledge of English, reflecting the linguistic isolation common in his neighborhood.[6] The family's economic hardships shaped his early experiences, with limited resources and reliance on manual labor; by his teenage years, he joined migrant farm work, picking crops alongside his family during summers.[6] These circumstances instilled values of hard work and resilience, as later recounted by Quiñones in reflections on overcoming barrio limitations.[5]

Academic Achievements

Quiñones began his undergraduate studies at Southwest Texas State University (now Texas State University) before transferring to St. Mary's University in San Antonio, Texas, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in speech communication in 1974.[7][8] During his time at St. Mary's, he worked at a local radio station and took broadcasting classes at San Antonio College to support his interest in media.[6] He later pursued graduate education on a fellowship and obtained a Master of Arts degree in journalism from Columbia University's Graduate School of Journalism in 1979.[9][10] This program equipped him with advanced skills in reporting and broadcast techniques, laying the foundation for his professional career in television journalism.[11]

Journalism Career

Early Professional Roles

Quiñones began his media career with an internship at KTRH radio, a country music station in Houston, Texas, in 1971, where he earned $2 for initial tasks.[12] From 1975 to 1978, he served as a reporter and news editor at KTRH, handling news production and reporting duties.[9] [13] During this period, he also worked as a reporter and anchor at KPRC-TV, Houston's NBC affiliate, marking his entry into television news.[13] [9] In 1978, following his undergraduate studies, Quiñones relocated to Chicago to join WBBM-TV, the CBS owned-and-operated station, as a reporter.[14] [15] There, he covered local and neighborhood stories, including urban issues and community events, while earning a master's degree in journalism from Columbia University in 1979.[16] His reporting at WBBM-TV focused on human-interest pieces, establishing his style of on-the-ground, empathetic journalism amid Chicago's diverse communities.[17] Quiñones credited the competitive Chicago market with honing his skills, as he competed against established reporters for airtime and investigative opportunities.[14]

ABC News Integration and Key Assignments

Quiñones joined ABC News in June 1982 as a general assignment correspondent based in Miami, marking him as the network's first Latino news correspondent. In this role, he contributed reports to World News Tonight with Peter Jennings and other ABC programs, focusing initially on regional and international stories from Latin America.[1][18] His early assignments included coverage of civil wars in Central America during the 1980s, where he reported on ongoing conflicts involving U.S. policy implications. In December 1989, Quiñones was among the few American journalists on the ground in Panama City during the U.S. invasion to oust Manuel Noriega, providing firsthand accounts of the military operation. He later covered the 1991 Persian Gulf War, the 1992 Hurricane Andrew's devastation in Florida, and the 1994 Cuban Balsero crisis, which involved mass rafter migrations from Cuba to the U.S.[1][19] In 1994, Quiñones relocated to New York City as ABC's Midwest correspondent before shifting to the Western bureau in Denver in 1995. That year, he became the first national television correspondent to report from the scene of the Oklahoma City bombing, which killed 168 people in the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building attack. He also pioneered network coverage from the U.S.-Mexico border on illegal immigration surges and debates over medical care for undocumented migrants. These assignments established his reputation for on-the-ground reporting in crisis zones and policy-driven stories.[1][17] Over time, Quiñones expanded into anchoring roles, including primetime specials and contributions to 20/20, Nightline, and Good Morning America, while earning seven Emmy Awards for investigative pieces such as the 1988 "Burning Questions" series and coverage of the Congo rainforest. His integration into ABC's core operations reflected a trajectory from field reporting to multifaceted on-air presence, emphasizing ethical dilemmas and human impact in journalism.[1][18]

Notable Reporting and Investigations

Quiñones conducted investigative reporting on Primetime Live that exposed U.S. military complicity in a 40-year pattern of prostitution in the Philippines, documenting the hardships faced by roughly 9,000 Amerasian children fathered by American servicemen and local women.[20] During the 1996-97 season, he investigated cases of parents abducting their own children in defiance of custody orders, highlighting legal and familial disruptions.[21] That same period, a Primetime Live segment under his reporting scrutinized police racial profiling practices, contributing to national discourse on law enforcement biases against Black individuals following high-profile incidents.[16] On 20/20, Quiñones led coverage of the 2020 murder of U.S. Army soldier Vanessa Guillén at Fort Hood, Texas, which exposed systemic sexual harassment and assault in the military, spurring the 2021 I Am Vanessa Guillén Act signed into law to reform reporting procedures.[3] His reporting followed Guillén's disappearance on April 22, 2020, and the subsequent discovery of her remains, amplifying victim testimonies and prompting congressional hearings.[3] Earlier, in 1997-98, he contributed to a one-hour 20/20 special on the Albanian refugee crisis amid the Kosovo conflict, detailing displacement and humanitarian challenges on the ground.[16] Quiñones also pursued undercover investigations, including a Primetime Live exposé on fraudulent mortgage loan schemes targeting banks through false pretenses.[21] His work earned multiple Emmy Awards for investigative segments on Primetime Live, Burning Questions, and 20/20, recognizing depth in probing social injustices and institutional failures.[18] Additionally, a Gabriel Award honored his poignant report tracking a young boy living with AIDS, underscoring personal impacts of public health crises.[22]

What Would You Do?

Development and Format

"What Would You Do?" originated as short hidden camera segments featured on ABC's Primetime investigative series from 2005 to 2007, initially designed as brief explorations of public reactions to moral quandaries.[23] John Quiñones, an ABC News correspondent, developed the concept to examine ethical dilemmas and societal compassion, aiming to "hold up a mirror to American society" by staging scenarios that prompt bystander intervention or indifference. The segments gained popularity for their unscripted authenticity, leading to expansion into a standalone half-hour program hosted by Quiñones, with its first full episodes airing in early 2008 before formalizing as a recurring series by January 2009.[24] [25] The show's format centers on social experiments conducted in everyday public settings such as restaurants, parks, or stores, where professional actors portray protagonists in contrived ethical conflicts—ranging from discrimination and bullying to personal crises like infidelity or neglect.[26] Hidden cameras, often disguised in nearby objects or operated by crew members posing as patrons, capture spontaneous reactions from unaware bystanders without prior scripting of their responses.[27] Quiñones provides voiceover narration to introduce each scenario and repeatedly poses the titular question to viewers, emphasizing the moral stakes involved.[28] Each episode typically features two to three distinct scenarios, lasting 10-15 minutes apiece, with production emphasizing ethical staging to avoid real harm while provoking genuine outrage or empathy.[29] Following the filming of reactions, actors and producers reveal the setup to participants, at which point Quiñones emerges to conduct on-site interviews, probing the decision-making processes of interveners and observers alike.[30] This debriefing phase highlights acts of courage or apathy, often incorporating expert commentary from psychologists or ethicists to analyze broader societal patterns. Episodes conclude with reflections on human behavior, underscoring Quiñones' intent to inspire prosocial action rather than mere entertainment.[24] The program airs weekly on ABC, with seasons typically comprising 10-13 episodes, and has maintained this structure across 17 seasons as of 2024.

Episode Themes and Production

The episodes of What Would You Do? center on staging real-world ethical dilemmas to observe bystander interventions, focusing on scenarios involving discrimination, harassment, and social injustices. Common themes include racial prejudice, such as actors portraying confrontations over interracial relationships or ethnic minorities facing verbal abuse in public settings; gender-based harassment, like unwanted advances toward women in bars or workplaces; bullying of vulnerable groups, including teenagers targeted for perceived sexual orientation or homeless individuals mocked by peers; and family-related conflicts, such as parental neglect or child abuse in restaurants.[31][32] These dilemmas are designed to test public responses to moral quandaries, revealing patterns of apathy, confrontation, or assistance without prior scripting of bystander actions.[13] Production employs hidden cameras placed in public venues like diners, parks, and stores to capture unscripted reactions, with professional actors initiating the provocations while a crew monitors feeds from a nearby trailer. Quiñones, positioned off-site with producers, observes the unfolding events in real time via live video, intervening only after sufficient reactions are recorded to reveal the setup and conduct on-camera interviews with participants and witnesses.[33][34] Scenarios are filmed over several hours or days to gather diverse responses, emphasizing authenticity by avoiding cues that might alert passersby, though ethical considerations limit depictions of extreme violence or illegality.[35] The process prioritizes locations with high foot traffic for natural variety in demographics, and episodes often juxtapose reactions from urban versus rural areas to highlight regional differences in social norms.[36] Post-production editing compiles footage to underscore recurring behavioral trends, with Quiñones providing narration grounded in journalistic observation rather than opinion.[37]

Reception, Impact, and Criticisms

The series has garnered a generally positive reception for its exploration of moral dilemmas and bystander intervention, achieving an IMDb user rating of 7.1 out of 10 based on over 1,200 reviews, with some seasons receiving higher viewer scores, such as season 14's 8.7 out of 10.[25][38] Its longevity, spanning 17 seasons since 2008, underscores sustained audience interest, with episodes often drawing strong ratings for provocative scenarios involving discrimination or ethical conflicts.[39] The show has earned accolades, including awards from the Chicago International Television Festival and the Avon Foundation's 2006 Voice of Change award for its focus on social issues.[40] In terms of impact, "What Would You Do?" has influenced public discourse on human behavior and ethics by staging real-world scenarios that highlight the bystander effect and encourage viewers to reflect on their own responses to injustice, such as harassment or prejudice.[41] Episodes have amplified awareness of marginalized voices, prompting discussions on topics like discrimination against the homeless or LGBTQ+ individuals, and inspired related media like books distilling lessons on moral decision-making.[18][42] Proponents credit it with fostering empathy and behavioral change, as evidenced by viewer testimonials and its role in educational contexts examining ethical journalism.[43] Criticisms center on ethical lapses in production, including the use of hidden cameras to stage deceptive scenarios without participant consent, akin to unregulated psychological experiments that manipulate public behavior for entertainment.[23] Detractors argue it distorts reality and biases outcomes by selecting scenarios that elicit predetermined reactions, potentially misleading audiences about genuine social dynamics while prioritizing sensationalism over rigorous research.[44] Columbia Journalism Review has questioned the journalistic integrity of such enactments, noting they provoke thought but risk eroding trust in media through contrived provocation rather than authentic reporting.[43] Despite defenses from producers emphasizing debriefings and social good, the format's reliance on actors and editing has drawn accusations of dishonesty and undue influence on bystanders' actions.[45]

Other Professional Endeavors

Authorship

John Quiñones has authored and co-authored books that reflect his journalistic career, emphasizing themes of courage, ethics, and community resilience drawn from his reporting and television production.[46] These works often blend personal narratives with profiles of ordinary individuals facing moral dilemmas or crises.[47] In Heroes Among Us: Ordinary People, Extraordinary Choices, co-written with Stephen P. Williams and published on January 6, 2009, by Harper, Quiñones recounts true stories of everyday heroism encountered during his decades of fieldwork as an ABC News correspondent.[48] [49] The book highlights individuals who intervened in emergencies, resisted injustice, or aided strangers, underscoring patterns of altruism observed across diverse global assignments.[50] What Would You Do?: Words of Wisdom About Doing the Right Thing, published on April 21, 2015, by Kingswell, draws directly from Quiñones' ABC hidden-camera series, interweaving episode-inspired scenarios with autobiographical reflections on his upbringing in San Antonio, Texas, and career challenges.[42] [46] The 160-page volume explores ethical decision-making, crediting influences like his mother for instilling a commitment to integrity amid adversity.[46] Quiñones' most recent book, One Year in Uvalde: A Story of Hope and Resilience, co-authored with María Elena Salinas and released on May 7, 2024, by Hyperion Avenue, examines the aftermath of the May 24, 2022, Robb Elementary School shooting through on-the-ground ABC News coverage.[51] [52] The 240-page narrative focuses on survivor activism, parental advocacy, and communal recovery efforts in Uvalde, Texas, rather than forensic details of the incident itself.[53]

Public Speaking and Advocacy

John Quiñones has established himself as a sought-after motivational speaker, delivering keynotes that highlight his journey from poverty in San Antonio's barrios to prominence in broadcast journalism. His presentations underscore the role of education in overcoming adversity and champion the opportunities available through the American Dream, with a particular emphasis on Latino achievement.[54][30] Drawing extensively from his work on What Would You Do?, Quiñones addresses ethical decision-making, the bystander effect, and the need for intervention in scenarios involving bullying, racism, and discrimination. He urges audiences to embody moral courage by acting against injustice, even absent external validation, framing such behavior as a measure of true character.[55][56][57] Among his notable engagements, Quiñones spoke at the University of Wisconsin-Madison on November 5, 2019, advocating for active bystanderism in ethical dilemmas. On March 8, 2022, he addressed University of Oklahoma students, linking his show's scenarios to real-world calls to confront prejudice and bullying. In 2024, he presented at The Immunization Partnership's annual dinner, focusing on community-driven action for healthier outcomes.[55][56][58] Quiñones' advocacy extends to promoting resilience and hope, as seen in speeches referencing events like the 2022 Uvalde school shooting, where he emphasizes collective responsibility in crisis response. His messages consistently prioritize individual agency in fostering ethical societies, informed by journalistic observations of human behavior under pressure.[59]

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

John Quiñones married Nancy Loftus, his high school sweetheart, in April 1988 in a private ceremony in West Palm Beach, Florida.[60][61] The couple had three children together: sons Julian Quiñones and Nicco Quiñones, and daughter Andrea Quiñones.[61][62] Their marriage ended in divorce in 2009 after over two decades.[61][62] Following the divorce, Quiñones married Deanna White, a registered nurse, in 2010.[60][62] No children from this marriage are publicly documented. Quiñones has shared family moments on social media, including celebrations with Deanna and his children from his first marriage, such as Andrea's 22nd birthday in 2016 and Julian's wedding in 2018, where he officiated.[63][64]

Civic Engagement and Later Activities

Quiñones has maintained a longstanding commitment to youth development through his affiliation with Boys & Girls Clubs of America, where he was a member during his childhood in San Antonio and credits the organization with providing a safe environment that fostered his personal growth and aspirations.[65] In recognition of his contributions, including delivering motivational speeches to youth groups and Clubs nationwide, he was inducted into the Boys & Girls Clubs Alumni Hall of Fame on May 5, 2023.[66] These efforts emphasize empowering underprivileged children through mentorship and opportunity, drawing from his own experiences overcoming poverty. He actively supports educational access for first-generation college students, particularly within Latino communities, by participating in events and receiving honors from organizations dedicated to this cause. On October 22, 2025, Quiñones was awarded the Founder's Award at the 13th Annual Visiones Gala hosted by LUCA, a nonprofit that aids high school students in achieving higher education through mentorship and resources.[67] This accolade highlights his dedication to breaking cycles of disadvantage via education, aligning with LUCA's mission to transform lives for underserved youth. In recent years, Quiñones has extended his civic involvement through public speaking at nonprofit gatherings focused on community improvement. On September 19, 2025, he addressed community organizations in Houston, sharing strategies for partnerships between nonprofits and congregations to drive positive local change.[68] Similarly, he spoke at the El Centro de Corazón fundraiser luncheon on October 8, 2025, underscoring health and community impact initiatives. On October 1, 2025, he received the Eagle Leadership Award at the 75th Latino Leaders Luncheon Series, affirming his role in inspirational leadership.[69] These activities reflect a post-primetime focus on leveraging his platform for direct societal betterment beyond broadcasting.

Awards and Honors

Major Broadcast Awards

Quiñones has earned seven News & Documentary Emmy Awards for his investigative reporting and on-air contributions to ABC programs including Primetime Live, Burning Questions, and 20/20.[17][7][70] These awards recognize specific segments on topics such as social issues, health crises, and human interest stories, with notable honors including coverage of Hurricane Andrew in 1992 and reports on child labor in the United States.[71][72] In 1999, Quiñones contributed reports to ABC News' ABC 2000 (also known as ABC 2000 Today), a 24-hour global Millennium broadcast that received the George Foster Peabody Award for excellence in electronic media.[17][22] The Peabody citation highlighted the production's comprehensive live coverage of global events marking the turn of the millennium.[7] He also received the Robert F. Kennedy Journalism Award's Grand Prize for International Reporting, honoring his broadcast work on human rights and social justice themes across ABC platforms.[70] Additionally, Quiñones was awarded a Gabriel Award for a 20/20 report on a young boy who died from AIDS-related complications, commending its compassionate portrayal of family struggles amid public stigma.[7][22]

Additional Recognitions

Quiñones received the Lifetime Achievement Award from the National Hispanic Media Coalition at its 19th Annual Impact Awards Gala in 2016, recognizing his pioneering role as ABC News' first Latino correspondent and his contributions to Hispanic representation in media.[73] In 2022, he was honored with the inaugural Félix Varela Lifetime Achievement Award by the AL DÍA Foundation for his trailblazing journalism career and impact on Latino communities.[74] In 2022, the Society of Professional Journalists named Quiñones a Fellow of the Society, an honor for his extraordinary contributions to the profession over nearly four decades, including investigative reporting and ethical storytelling on programs like What Would You Do?.[75] The National Association of Hispanic Journalists inducted him into its Hall of Fame in 2024, alongside other pioneers, for advancing Hispanic voices in U.S. journalism.[76] Universities have conferred honorary degrees on Quiñones for his inspirational journey from migrant farm work to broadcast prominence. These include a Doctor of Humane Letters from Utah Valley University and a Doctor of Letters from Davis & Elkins College.[22] [7] In 2021, Ohio University awarded him the Carr Van Anda Award for Excellence in Investigative Journalism, highlighting his nearly 40 years at ABC News.[19] DePaul University's Center for Journalism Integrity and Excellence presented him with its 2024 Distinguished Journalist Award for his persistence, passion, and ethical reporting standards.[77]
