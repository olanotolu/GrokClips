Howard Weitzman


Howard Lloyd Weitzman (September 21, 1939 – April 7, 2021) was an American trial lawyer specializing in civil, criminal, antitrust, and regulatory litigation, particularly for clients in the entertainment sector.[1][2]
Educated at the University of Southern California, where he earned a B.A. in 1962 and a J.D. in 1965, Weitzman taught trial advocacy at USC Law School for 12 years and lectured at institutions including Harvard University and Georgetown University Law Center.[1][3]
Throughout his over five-decade career, he acted as lead counsel in more than 300 civil and criminal jury trials, representing over 1,000 individuals and companies on diverse matters.[1][2][4]
Weitzman held executive roles, such as Vice President of Corporate Operations at Universal Studios from 1995 to 1998, and received accolades including the Jerry Giesler Memorial Award twice for outstanding trial work in Los Angeles County and recognition by The National Law Journal as one of the top 15 lawyers in the United States.[1][5]
Known for handling high-stakes cases involving prominent figures, he contributed to legal defenses and estate matters in the entertainment industry until his death from cancer in Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles.[2][4]

Early Life and Education

Family and Childhood

Howard Lloyd Weitzman was born on September 21, 1939, in Los Angeles, California.[2][6] He grew up in the city, the son of Wilfred and Billie Weitzman, who operated a local grocery store.[2][7] As a child, Weitzman assisted in his family's grocery business, which provided his first exposure to work and commerce.[6][3][8] He developed a strong interest in baseball during this period, actively playing the sport and initially aspiring to pursue it professionally, which later influenced his college athletics before shifting to legal studies.[6][3]

Academic and Early Career Aspirations

Weitzman attended Los Angeles City College before transferring to the University of Southern California, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1962 and a Juris Doctor from USC Gould School of Law in 1965.[9][1] Prior to law school, his early work experience centered on assisting in his family's grocery business in Los Angeles, reflecting no initial orientation toward legal pursuits.[9] Law did not feature in Weitzman's early ambitions; in a 2008 interview, he recounted that pursuing a legal education arose "out of the blue" from an external suggestion, noting he knew no lawyers and the field was entirely absent from his considerations.[10] Contemporary accounts confirm this serendipitous entry, emphasizing that he had never contemplated a legal career and was uncertain if he had encountered any attorneys beforehand.[5] Despite this unplanned path, Weitzman demonstrated aptitude in legal studies and, upon graduation, immediately commenced practice in criminal law, establishing foundations for a career marked by courtroom proficiency.[2][5] His early professional inclinations leaned toward trial advocacy, as evidenced by his subsequent role teaching the subject at USC Gould School of Law for 12 years, a position he held while building his practice.[1] This engagement suggests an emergent aspiration to mentor aspiring litigators, aligning with his proven courtroom instincts rather than premeditated career planning.[5]

Legal Career

Initial Practice and Criminal Defense Foundations

Following his graduation from the University of Southern California Gould School of Law in 1965, Howard Weitzman launched a criminal defense practice in Los Angeles, focusing initially on serious felony cases.[2][8] This marked the onset of a career emphasizing courtroom advocacy, where he handled matters requiring meticulous preparation and strategic witness examination to challenge prosecutorial narratives. Weitzman specialized in murder prosecutions during this foundational period, securing acquittals in notable instances such as the 1971 defense of Mary Brunner, a figure linked to the Charles Manson circle, on charges stemming from a confrontation at a New Mexico residence.[11] His approach prioritized exposing inconsistencies in evidence and leveraging forensic critiques, which contributed to his growing proficiency in jury trials. Over time, these experiences formed the core of his trial methodology, serving as lead counsel in more than 300 civil and criminal jury trials across his tenure.[3] In parallel, Weitzman taught trial advocacy at USC Gould School of Law for 12 years, imparting techniques drawn from his active practice, including cross-examination tactics and case theory development.[3] This instructional role reinforced his command of evidentiary rules and procedural nuances, solidifying the intellectual and practical underpinnings of his criminal defense foundations before transitioning to broader celebrity and corporate representations.

High-Profile Criminal Representations

Weitzman's criminal defense practice, established after his 1965 admission to the bar, emphasized jury trials in high-stakes matters, positioning him as a specialist in murder and related offenses early in his career.[8] He handled over 300 such trials overall, often representing clients facing federal and state charges ranging from homicide to drug trafficking.[2] [1] His initial prominent criminal representation occurred in 1973 as court-appointed counsel for Mary Brunner, a Charles Manson associate charged with the 1969 murder of Gary Hinman during a botched robbery; Weitzman obtained her acquittal on the murder indictment by successfully moving to dismiss based on a prior immunity grant for her testimony against other Family members.[12] [13] Brunner, who had borne Manson's son, remained the sole Manson Family participant charged but not convicted in a Tate-LaBianca-related killing.[10] During the 1970s, Weitzman defended members of the Black Panther Party in multiple proceedings amid the group's clashes with law enforcement, though specific case outcomes varied amid broader political scrutiny.[10] He also represented Louis Tom Dragna, identified as a purported organized crime figure in Los Angeles, securing a reduced two-year sentence in a federal racketeering matter.[10] These cases established his reputation for tackling controversial defendants, including those tied to countercultural or underworld elements, prior to his pivot toward celebrity clientele.[6] In later high-profile instances, Weitzman briefly led O.J. Simpson's defense in June 1994 amid the investigation into the murders of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ronald Goldman, advancing an initial alibi of Simpson's Chicago flight before withdrawing within days to avoid conflicts.[4] [14] For Paris Hilton's 2007 misdemeanor prosecution for driving on a suspended license, he negotiated a plea yielding a 45-day incarceration term, later shortened on appeal.[10] Such engagements underscored his strategic use of media and investigative resources, including private detective Anthony Pellicano, to challenge prosecutions, though Pellicano's later convictions for unrelated wiretapping did not implicate Weitzman.[10]

Transition to Entertainment and Civil Law

Following high-profile criminal defenses, such as the 1984 acquittal of John DeLorean on federal cocaine trafficking charges, Weitzman expanded his practice to encompass civil litigation and advisory roles for entertainment industry clients, including agencies like William Morris, ICM, and CAA.[2] This shift reflected a broader evolution from trial-focused criminal work to deal-making and dispute resolution in media and celebrity matters, leveraging his growing network in Hollywood.[2] A pivotal step occurred in August 1995, when Weitzman left his 30-year private practice to join MCA Inc. (parent of Universal Studios) as executive vice president of corporate operations, the first major executive hire under new ownership by Seagram Co.[15] In this role, he focused on business restructuring, operational efficiencies, and navigating corporate transitions amid industry consolidation, providing direct immersion in entertainment sector management rather than courtroom advocacy.[10] His tenure ended in April 1998 following a management reshuffle, after which he returned to private practice to apply these insights to client representations.[16] By the early 2000s, Weitzman's work emphasized civil trials, intellectual property disputes, estate planning, and transactional negotiations for high-profile figures in music, film, and talent management, handling over 300 combined civil and criminal jury trials across his career while prioritizing non-criminal entertainment law.[3] This phase culminated in 2006 with the co-founding of Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump LLP (later rebranded), a firm dedicated to entertainment litigation, family law, and business deals, solidifying his status as a Hollywood power attorney.[10]

Corporate Roles and Firm Development

In the mid-1980s, Weitzman advanced in business litigation within entertainment law by becoming a managing partner at the Los Angeles firm Wyman Bautzer, where he handled high-stakes deals and disputes for clients including Interscope Communications.[17] In February 1991, amid the dissolution of Wyman Bautzer Kuchel & Silbert—a 39-year-old firm with about 65 lawyers—Weitzman orchestrated its merger into the Chicago-based Katten Muchin & Zavis, renaming it Katten Muchin Zavis & Weitzman and assuming the role of chairman of the national executive committee to expand its West Coast entertainment practice.[18][19] Weitzman's corporate executive tenure began in August 1995 when Music Corporation of America (MCA) appointed him Executive Vice President of Corporate Operations at Universal Studios, leveraging his litigation expertise for internal restructuring, operational efficiencies, and negotiating the $5.7 billion sale of the studio to Seagram Co. in 1995.[20][17] He remained in this position until April 1998, when Universal ousted him amid ongoing corporate transitions following the Seagram acquisition, prompting his return to trial work due to dissatisfaction with bureaucratic meetings.[16][2] Resuming firm leadership, Weitzman joined Greenberg Glusker Fields Claman & Machtinger LLP in May 2005 as a veteran trial attorney focused on civil litigation in entertainment.[21] In 2006, he co-founded the Santa Monica-based boutique Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump & Aldisert LLP (later Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump LLP), a specialized litigation practice emphasizing intellectual property, contract disputes, and celebrity representations, which grew into a key Hollywood player under his partnership until his death in 2021.[6][14] The firm rebranded in 2023 as Kinsella Holley Iser Kump Steinsapir LLP, removing Weitzman's name despite his foundational role.[22]

Notable Cases and Representations

John DeLorean Cocaine Conspiracy Trial


John Z. DeLorean faced federal charges of conspiracy to distribute approximately $24 million worth of cocaine, stemming from a sting operation by the FBI and DEA in which undercover agents posed as drug traffickers to fund DeLorean's struggling automobile company.[23] The case centered on videotaped discussions and a staged transaction where DeLorean was recorded handling a briefcase containing cocaine on October 19, 1982, at a Los Angeles hotel.[24] Howard Weitzman served as DeLorean's lead defense attorney alongside Donald Re, mounting an entrapment defense that portrayed DeLorean as a victim of aggressive government inducement by informants including James Hoffman, a convicted drug smuggler turned informant, and Carol Tisa, an actress posing as a banker.[23][25] The trial commenced on April 18, 1984, in U.S. District Court in Los Angeles before Judge Robert Takasugi, drawing intense media scrutiny due to DeLorean's celebrity status as a former General Motors executive and creator of the iconic DMC-12 sports car.[23] Weitzman's strategy emphasized undermining the credibility of prosecution witnesses through rigorous cross-examinations, highlighting Hoffman's history of fabricating stories to authorities and Tisa's scripted role in luring DeLorean into the scheme after initial reluctance.[26][25] Defense arguments framed the operation as government overreach, with Weitzman asserting that agents initiated and pressured DeLorean into the deal amid his financial desperation following the 1982 bankruptcy of DeLorean Motor Company.[27] Prosecutors, led by James P. Walsh Jr. and Robert Parry, relied on the videotapes and DeLorean's own statements to demonstrate knowing participation, but the jury deliberated for five days before acquitting him on all eight counts on August 16, 1984, effectively accepting the entrapment claim.[23][24] Weitzman's post-verdict remarks underscored his view of the case as emblematic of prosecutorial excess, stating, "This case is a sick case. It is a case of the Government trying to create a crime where none existed."[27] The acquittal bolstered Weitzman's reputation for high-stakes criminal defense, particularly in challenging federal sting operations, though it drew criticism from some quarters for potentially glorifying a desperate financial maneuver as mere victimhood.[25] No retrial occurred on the drug charges, allowing DeLorean to pursue subsequent civil litigation against the government, which he lost.[23]

O.J. Simpson Murder Case Involvement

Howard Weitzman was retained as O.J. Simpson's initial attorney shortly after Simpson became a suspect in the June 12, 1994, murders of his ex-wife Nicole Brown Simpson and her friend Ronald Goldman.[6] He represented Simpson during a voluntary three-hour police interrogation on June 14, 1994, guiding him through questioning about the killings.[28] Early in the investigation, Weitzman publicly stated to the media that Simpson had been en route to Chicago on a flight at the time of the murders, providing an initial alibi.[6] Prior to fully stepping aside, Weitzman assisted in assembling Simpson's defense by introducing him to key members of what would become known as the "Dream Team," including Robert Shapiro and Johnnie Cochran.[29] On June 16, 1994, two days after the interrogation, Weitzman withdrew from the case, publicly citing an excessively heavy workload as the reason.[28] His departure immediately preceded Shapiro's entry as lead counsel, who reiterated Simpson's lack of connection to the slayings.[28] Reports later indicated an additional factor in Weitzman's exit: a potential conflict of interest, as he had previously represented Nicole Brown Simpson in a 1989 domestic violence case filed against O.J. Simpson.[6] Weitzman did not participate in Simpson's criminal trial, which began in January 1995, nor in the subsequent civil proceedings; his involvement remained limited to the preliminary investigative phase.[2][29]

Michael Jackson Legal Matters

Howard Weitzman acted as Michael Jackson's primary criminal defense attorney during the 1993 investigation into allegations that Jackson had sexually abused a 13-year-old boy, Jordan Chandler, who had been a frequent companion at Jackson's Neverland Ranch.[30] The Los Angeles Police Department launched a criminal probe in August 1993 following the boy's disclosure to a psychiatrist, conducting searches of Jackson's properties and interviewing potential witnesses, including other children associated with the singer.[31] Weitzman, alongside civil counsel, managed Jackson's public statements and legal strategy amid intense media scrutiny, asserting that seized evidence from raids did not implicate his client and emphasizing Jackson's deteriorating health under stress, describing him as "barely able to function."[30] [31] In parallel with the criminal inquiry, the Chandler family filed a civil lawsuit in September 1993 seeking $30 million in damages for alleged molestation occurring over several months.[32] Weitzman collaborated with attorneys like Bertram Fields and Johnnie Cochran to contest the sharing of civil discovery materials with prosecutors, arguing it would prejudice the criminal case, though a judge ultimately granted limited access in December 1993.[33] The civil suit settled out of court in January 1994 for approximately $23 million paid to the accuser's family, therapists, and lawyers, with Jackson's team insisting the agreement included no admission of wrongdoing and was intended to resolve the matter privately amid ongoing criminal scrutiny.[34] Criminal charges never materialized; in September 1994, Los Angeles County District Attorney Gil Garcetti announced no prosecution, citing evidentiary challenges including the settlement's implications and the passage of time affecting witness credibility.[35] Weitzman hailed the decision as appropriate, reiterating Jackson's consistent denial of the allegations and framing the outcome as vindication after a protracted ordeal that had spanned grand jury considerations and subpoenas to associates.[35] [36] The absence of charges aligned with investigations finding insufficient corroboration for the claims, though the settlement amount drew public skepticism regarding Jackson's innocence despite the legal closure.[37] Weitzman's representation extended to defending Jackson against related media portrayals and subsequent claims during this period, establishing his role in high-stakes celebrity crisis management where criminal and civil fronts intersected without yielding a trial.[2] Later, following Jackson's 2009 death, Weitzman continued advising the estate on litigation, including dismissals of posthumous abuse suits deemed non-meritorious, but his core involvement in Jackson's legal matters centered on navigating the 1993 crisis to avert criminal liability.[38]

Other Celebrity Defenses

Weitzman represented Paris Hilton in 2007 amid her prosecution for driving on a suspended license, which stemmed from a prior DUI conviction; he publicly contended that the 45-day jail sentence reflected undue external pressures to make an example of her celebrity status rather than standard application of law.[2] [10] In defending rock musician Ozzy Osbourne, Weitzman handled multiple lawsuits from families alleging that subliminal messages and lyrics in Osbourne's songs, such as "Suicide Solution," incited teen suicides; in a 1986 Georgia case involving the death of 16-year-old Michael Waller, Weitzman depicted Osbourne as a devoted family man uninvolved in promoting self-harm, contributing to the eventual dismissal or settlement of claims on First Amendment grounds.[39] [40] Weitzman served as counsel for actor Sean Penn during his 1988 battery conviction for assaulting a film extra on the set of Colors, where Penn received a 60-day jail term (serving 33 days) after pleading no contest; Weitzman advocated in court for Penn's remorse and commitment to improved self-control, emphasizing the incident's provocation amid ongoing professional pressures.[5] [41] For singer Justin Bieber, Weitzman managed civil litigation including a 2013 lawsuit from a former bodyguard alleging assault by Bieber, negotiating a confidential settlement that avoided trial; he also provided ongoing representation for Bieber's various legal entanglements during the artist's early career controversies.[7] [42]

Legacy, Recognition, and Criticisms

Professional Achievements and Teaching

Weitzman instructed trial advocacy courses at the University of Southern California Gould School of Law for 12 years, emphasizing practical courtroom skills for aspiring litigators.[3] He also guest-lectured on legal strategy and advocacy at Harvard Law School, Georgetown University Law Center, UCLA School of Law, and various other institutions, sharing insights from his extensive trial experience.[1][43] In recognition of his litigation prowess, Weitzman received the Jerry Giesler Memorial Award twice from the Los Angeles County legal community for exemplary trial performance.[44] Over five decades, he served as lead counsel in more than 300 civil and criminal jury trials, establishing a reputation for handling complex, high-stakes cases.[2] Additionally, from 1995 to 1998, he held the position of Executive Vice President of Corporate Operations at Universal Studios, bridging legal practice with executive oversight in the entertainment sector.[14]

Influence on Media and Entertainment Law

Weitzman's handling of high-profile trials amid pervasive media coverage established early precedents for integrating public relations into legal defense strategies within the entertainment sector. During the 1984 federal cocaine trafficking trial of John DeLorean, he became one of the first attorneys to contend with nonstop television broadcasting of court proceedings, employing courthouse-steps press conferences to counter negative narratives and discredit prosecution witnesses, ultimately securing an acquittal on entrapment grounds.[2] This approach, which emphasized shaping public perception alongside courtroom advocacy, influenced subsequent practices for celebrity litigators facing tabloid scrutiny and defamation risks, as seen in his defenses of clients like Michael Jackson against pedophilia allegations and creditor claims.[3] His firm's successful settlements in media-heavy disputes, such as the 2011 Charlie Sheen lawsuit against producer Chuck Lorre seeking $100 million, further demonstrated a model of rapid resolution to minimize reputational damage.[3] In corporate capacities, Weitzman contributed to operational legal frameworks at major studios. From 1995 to 1998, as Executive Vice President of Corporate Operations at Universal Studios, he oversaw the restructuring of key divisions including legal affairs, human resources, and consumer products, adapting internal practices to the consolidating media landscape following Edgar Bronfman's acquisition of MCA.[1] This role informed his later advisory work for productions and talent agencies such as CAA, ICM, and William Morris, where he navigated intellectual property, contract negotiations, and privacy issues central to entertainment transactions.[2] His experience underscored the need for integrated legal-corporate strategies in an industry increasingly intertwined with global media conglomerates. Weitzman also shaped the field through education and firm-building. He taught trial advocacy at the University of Southern California Law School for 12 years and delivered lectures on litigation tactics at institutions including Harvard and Georgetown, imparting techniques for managing media-influenced cases to emerging entertainment lawyers.[1] As co-founder of Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump & Aldisert LLP (later rebranded), he led a practice handling over 300 jury trials and representing more than 1,000 clients in entertainment matters ranging from estates to defamation, earning perennial recognition as a "Power Lawyer" by The Hollywood Reporter and Variety.[1] These efforts collectively advanced pragmatic, client-focused methodologies that prioritized settlement efficiency and media savvy over protracted public battles.[2]

Criticisms of Defense Strategies and Client Choices

Weitzman's brief representation of O.J. Simpson following the 1994 murders of Nicole Brown Simpson and Ron Goldman drew scrutiny for the client's decision to undergo a police interview without counsel present, despite Weitzman's explicit advice against it, which some legal commentators later viewed as a tactical misstep that compromised the defense's position early on.[45] [46] He withdrew from the case on June 16, 1994, citing an unmanageable workload, but the episode highlighted risks in handling impulsive high-profile clients under intense media pressure.[28] His longstanding defense of Michael Jackson amid multiple child sexual abuse allegations, including the 1993 Santa Barbara County investigation and subsequent civil suits settled for approximately $23 million without admission of guilt, elicited controversy from accusers' supporters who argued that such financial resolutions prioritized damage control over rigorous factual scrutiny, potentially enabling further harm by avoiding trial disclosures.[2] Weitzman maintained Jackson's innocence, portraying claims as financially motivated extortion, and pursued aggressive countersuits against accusers and media outlets, strategies that secured no criminal convictions but fueled ongoing public debate about whether celebrity lawyers like him shielded powerful figures from accountability rather than contesting evidence head-on.[42] [47] Posthumously, observers noted the "bad optics" of Weitzman's unwavering support despite patterns of settlements with prior accusers, questioning whether his commitment reflected genuine belief or professional incentives tied to lucrative estate litigation.[47] [6] Critics of Weitzman's client selection broader pattern—encompassing figures like John DeLorean accused of cocaine trafficking—have contended that favoring notorious cases amplified perceptions of an entertainment law elite more invested in preserving fame and fortunes than in upholding public interest or victim perspectives, though empirical outcomes like DeLorean's 1984 acquittal on entrapment grounds underscored the legal merits of his entrapment-focused approach.[2] Such choices, while ethically defensible under presumption-of-innocence principles, contributed to accusations of moral hazard in a system where aggressive defenses often yield non-convictions irrespective of underlying facts.[6]

Personal Life and Death

Family and Personal Interests

Weitzman was married twice. His first marriage was to Stacey Weitzman from 1969 to 1976.[6] He later married interior designer Margaret Weitzman, with whom he remained until his death in 2021.[3] [2] He had two sons, Jed Weitzman and Armen Weitzman.[3] [4] [6] Weitzman was an avid sports enthusiast, particularly a fan of the Los Angeles Dodgers and Lakers.[48] He had a lifelong passion for baseball, having played second base for a University of Southern California team that won the national college championship.[9] He and his wife Margaret were frequent patrons of Madeo Ristorante in Los Angeles, where they were known regulars.[48]

Illness and Passing

Howard Weitzman died on April 7, 2021, at his home in Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles, at the age of 81, following a battle with cancer.[2][3] The illness was described by family representatives as relatively short, though public statements emphasized his ongoing fight against the disease.[4][6] A spokeswoman for his wife, Margaret Weitzman, confirmed cancer as the cause of death to major outlets, while his law firm, Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump LLP, issued a statement mourning the loss of their founding partner without detailing the specific type of cancer.[2][14] Weitzman continued professional engagements until shortly before his passing, reflecting his characteristic resilience amid health challenges.[3]
