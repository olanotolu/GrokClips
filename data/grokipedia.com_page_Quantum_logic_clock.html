Quantum logic clock

A quantum logic clock is a type of atomic clock with laser cooled single ions confined together in an electromagnetic ion trap. First developed in the mid-2000s by physicists at the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology, with a major advancement in 2010, the clock was 37 times more precise than the then-existing international standard.[1] The quantum logic clock is based on an Al spectroscopy ion with a logic atom. Both the Al-based quantum logic clock and the Hg-based optical atomic clock track time by measuring the frequency of electronic transitions in the ion at an optical frequency using a UV laser, that is 100,000 times higher than the microwave frequencies used in NIST-F1 and other similar time standards around the world. Quantum logic clocks like this are able to be far more precise than microwave standards. As of July 2025, an upgraded version of the clock achieves a fractional frequency uncertainty of 5.5Ã—10âˆ’195.5 \times 10^{-19}5.5Ã—10âˆ’19, making it the most accurate atomic clock in the world.[2]

Accuracy

The NIST team are not able to measure clock ticks per second because the definition of a second is based on the standard NIST-F1, which cannot measure a machine more precise than itself. However, the aluminum ion clock's measured frequency to the current standard is 1121015393207857.4(7) Hz.[3] NIST have attributed the clock's accuracy to the fact that it is insensitive to background magnetic and electric fields, and unaffected by temperature.[4] In March 2008, physicists at NIST described an experimental quantum logic clock based on individual ions of beryllium and aluminum. This clock was compared to NIST's mercury ion clock. These were the most accurate clocks that had been constructed, with neither clock gaining nor losing time at a rate that would exceed a second in over a billion years.[5] In February 2010, NIST physicists described a second, enhanced version of the quantum logic clock based on individual ions of magnesium and aluminium. Considered the world's most precise clock in 2010 with a fractional frequency inaccuracy of 8.6Ã—10âˆ’188.6 \times 10^{-18}8.6Ã—10âˆ’18, it offers more than twice the precision of the original.[6] [7] In terms of standard deviation, the quantum logic clock deviates one second every 3.68 billion years, while the then current international standard NIST-F1 Caesium fountain atomic clock uncertainty was about 3.1Ã—10âˆ’163.1 \times 10^{-16}3.1Ã—10âˆ’16 expected to neither gain nor lose a second in more than 100 million years.[8] [9] In July 2019, NIST scientists demonstrated such a clock with total uncertainty of 9.4Ã—10âˆ’199.4 \times 10^{-19}9.4Ã—10âˆ’19 (deviates one second every 33.7 billion years), which is the first demonstration of a clock with uncertainty below 10^{-18}.[10][11][12] In July 2025, NIST reported further improvements to the Al+^++ quantum logic clock, achieving a total systematic uncertainty of 5.5Ã—10âˆ’195.5 \times 10^{-19}5.5Ã—10âˆ’19 (deviates one second every 57.7 billion years), the lowest uncertainty for any atomic clock to date.[13] In a 2020 paper, scientists illustrated how quantum clocks could experience a possibly experimentally testable superposition of proper times via time dilation from the theory of relativity, by which time passes slower for one object in relation to another when the former moves at a higher velocity. In quantum time dilation, one of the two clocks moves in a superposition of two localized momentum wave packets, resulting in a quantum correction to the classical time dilation.[14][15][16] A 2025 theoretical study further proposed that optical ion clocks, such as those using 27Al+^{27}\mathrm{Al}^{+}27Al+, could detect quantum signatures of proper time through second-order Doppler shifts in squeezed states, enabling proper time interferometry.[17]

Other accurate experimental clocks

Optical lattice clocks based on strontium-87 and ytterbium-171 have at times surpassed the accuracy of quantum-logic clocks, such as in the mid-2010s and again in 2024, though the quantum logic clock holds the current record as of 2025.[10][11][12] An experimental optical lattice clock was described in a 2014 Nature paper.[18] In 2015 JILA evaluated the absolute frequency uncertainty of their latest strontium-87 429 THz (429228004229873.0 Hz[19]) optical lattice clock at 2.1Ã—10âˆ’182.1 \times 10^{-18}2.1Ã—10âˆ’18, which corresponds to a measurable gravitational time dilation for an elevation change of 2 cm (0.79 in) on planet Earth that according to JILA/NIST Fellow Jun Ye is "getting really close to being useful for relativistic geodesy".[20][21][22] Subsequent improvements have pushed uncertainties lower; for example, a 2024 JILA strontium optical lattice clock achieved a total systematic uncertainty of 8.1Ã—10âˆ’198.1 \times 10^{-19}8.1Ã—10âˆ’19, enabling even finer measurements of relativistic effects.[23] At this frequency uncertainty, this JILA optical lattice optical clock is expected to neither gain nor lose a second in more than 15 billion years.[24]
