Carbetocin

Carbetocin , sold under the brand names Pabal among others, is a medication used to prevent excessive bleeding after childbirth, particularly following Cesarean section.[2] It appears to work as well as oxytocin.[3] Due to it being less economical than other options, use is not recommended by NHS Scotland.[2] It is given by injection into a vein or muscle.[3] A heat-stable formulation is available for prevention of postpartum hemorrhage in low-resource settings without reliable cold chain.[3] Side effects differ little from that of no treatment or placebo.[3] Use is not recommended in people with epilepsy or eclampsia.[2] Carbetocin is a manufactured long acting form of oxytocin.[3] It works by activating the oxytocin receptor which causes the uterus to contract.[4][3] Carbetocin was first described in 1974.[5] It was approved for medical use in Canada and the United Kingdom in 1997.[3] It is on the World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines.[6] It is not available in the United States or Japan.[7][3]

Medical uses

Carbetocin has been approved for use immediately following an elective Cesarean section when a local or spinal anesthesia has been used.[8] Since the uterus cannot contract on its own following incision during a Cesarean section, exogenous administration of oxytocin or an analog is necessary to restore uterine tone and prevent hemorrhage.[8][9] Safety of carbetocin following vaginal births and emergency Cesarean sections has not been established, though studies have suggested efficacy following vaginal births to that following Cesarean sections. Some studies have shown that a 10-70 ug dose following vaginal delivery caused contractions and no adverse side effects.[10] Carbetocin has also been shown to increase uterine involution (the return of the uterus to its contracted state after the birth of the baby) in humans, horses and cows.[11][12]

Comparison with other medication

In 2018, heat-stable carbetocin, a formulation that does not require strict refrigeration, was found to be as good as oxytocin for reduction of postpartum hemorrhage after vaginal delivery.[13] Heat-stable carbetocin was prequalified by WHO in 2019 and, as of 2024, has market authorization in 19 countries for PPH prevention after vaginal birth.[14] It is hoped that this will make oxytocic hemorrhage control more widely available and less expensive,[13] which will be particularly useful in regions of developing countries where the cold chain (in drug transport and storage) is unreliable because of power outages or equipment problems.[15][16] Due to carbetocin's considerably longer half-life, its effects are longer lasting than other oxytocin homologs such as oxytocin.[17] Studies have shown that a single carbetocin dose requires less additional uterotonic therapy than an oxytocin infusion following a Cesarean section.[8] Oxytocin receptor antagonists, such as barusiban or atosiban have the opposite effect of depressing oxytocin receptor activity and can be used to stop premature labor and uterine contractions.[17]

Adverse effects

Frequent adverse effects (10â€“40% or greater in some clinical trials) include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, itching (pruritus), flushing, feeling of warmth, headache, and trembling (tremor). Infrequent adverse effects (1â€“5%) include back pain, chest pain, dizziness, chills, sweating, metallic taste, tachycardia, and respiratory distress (dyspnoea); anaemia may also occur but is often related to the procedure. Clinical trials indicate that the adverse effects of carbetocin are similar in type and frequency to those of oxytocin and often comparable to placebo.[18][19] Contraindications for the use of carbetocin include administration prior to delivery of the infant (e.g., to induce labor) or hypersensitivity to carbetocin or oxytocin. Carbetocin is also contraindicated in patients with cardiovascular disease (e.g., coronary artery disease, valvular heart disease, cardiomyopathy, heart failure); use with caution in those with hypertension or preeclampsia. Overdosage of carbetocin may be associated with uterine hyperactivity and pain. Signs of overdose may arise from water intoxication and hyponatremia, especially with excessive fluid intake. Treatment consists of the administration of a dilute solution of ethanol, diuretics, supportive therapy, and symptomatic treatment of hyponatremia if needed.[18]

Interactions

No specific drug interactions have been reported with carbetocin in clinical studies. However, as an analogue of oxytocin, it may interact similarly to oxytocin. For example, carbetocin may potentiate the effects of vasoconstrictor agents (e.g., leading to hypertension when used with caudal block anesthesia) or prostaglandins such as dinoprostone and misoprostol, which can increase uterine contractions. Concurrent use with other uterotonics like oxytocin or methylergometrine may result in cumulative effects. Careful monitoring is recommended if co-administered postpartum.[20] Carbetocin has greater selectivity for oxytocin receptors compared to vasopressin receptors V1a (vascular/uterine) and V2 (renal), with much lower binding affinity, reducing the likelihood of significant interactions with vasopressin pathways.[21]

Pharmacology

Mechanism of action

Carbetocin works as an oxytocic, antihemorrhagic and uterotonic drug in the peripheral nervous system. The most common causes of postpartum hemorrhage are lack of tone in the uterus from overstretching or the use of an anesthetic.[22] Carbetocin functions as an agonist at peripheral oxytocin receptors, particularly in the myometrium. Oxytocin receptors are G protein-coupled[23] and their mechanism of action involves second messengers and the production of inositol phosphates.[17] Carbetocin mimics this mechanism.[24] Binding for carbetocin and other oxytocin agonists has been shown to be nonselective at the extracellular N-terminus and loops E2 and E3.[17] The oxytocin receptor shows similar affinity for oxytocin and carbetocin; clinically, carbetocin is as effective as oxytocin in preventing postpartum hemorrhage.[24][17] Carbetocin has a much longer lasting effect than oxytocin, necessitating only a single dose. Carbetocin is a biased agonist of the oxytocin receptor.[25] During pregnancy, the synthesis of oxytocin receptors in the uterus greatly increases, reaching a peak during labor and delivery. Consequently, the administration of carbetocin or another oxytocin analog during or immediately following birth will have increased uterotonic and contractile effect. The application of carbetocin does not affect a non-pregnant uterus with lower oxytocin receptor expression.[9] Carbetocin should not be used to induce or augment labor since it could cause cardiac or respiratory distress to mother or infant.[8][9]

Pharmacokinetics

Carbetocin is to be used in the hospital by prescription only. It can be administered intravenously or intramuscularly. In both cases, the recommended dose for an average adult female is 100 micrograms. Contractile effects of the uterus are apparent within two minutes and can be observed for approximately one hour,[26] though maximum binding occurs about 30 minutes after intramuscular injection. Administration is performed immediately following parturition to minimize risk of postpartum hemorrhage by inducing uterine contractions, increasing muscle tone. If further uterine stimulation is needed, treatment with other forms of oxytocic uterotonic drugs should be used.[26] A heat-stable formulation of carbetocin is available, which maintains efficacy without refrigeration requirements.[27] Endogenous and synthetic oxytocin has a half-life of approximately 3.5 minutes.[9][24] Carbetocin, in comparison, has a much longer half-life of approximately 40 minutes following intravenous administration (or 44-55 minutes intramuscular).[9][24] The bioavailable dose is around 80%.[28] The elimination half-life following intravenous administration is around 40 minutes, though the elimination mechanism is not entirely known.[26] Studies have shown that elimination is only minimally renal (0.7%), but may occur at least partially through enzymatic degradation of peptides, primarily on the C-terminal end.[24] Both elimination and volume of distribution are not dose dependent.[26]

Society and culture

Legal status

Carbetocin is approved in more than 80 countries worldwide, excluding the United States and Japan. [29] It is marketed under brand names such as Duratocin, Pabal, and Lonactene. A heat-stable formulation of carbetocin, suitable for use in low- and middle-income countries without cold chain requirements, has been approved in additional countries including India (2021), South Sudan, Sierra Leone, and is under registration in others as of 2024. [30] [14] Duratocin is also approved for veterinary use in several European countries, including Poland, Germany, Italy, Belgium, Luxembourg, France, and the Netherlands. [19]

Brand names

Duratocin, Pabal, Lonactene (human use); Depotocin, Comoton, Decomoton (historical or veterinary).
