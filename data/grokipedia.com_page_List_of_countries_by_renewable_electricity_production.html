List of countries by renewable electricity production

A list of countries by renewable electricity production ranks sovereign states according to the total volume of electricity generated from renewable sources, including hydropower, wind, solar, geothermal, and biomass, expressed in terawatt-hours (TWh) annually.[1] These rankings reflect variations in national energy demands, natural resource availability, policy incentives, and technological deployment, with absolute production favoring large economies over per capita or proportional shares.[2] In recent years, global renewable electricity generation has expanded rapidly, surpassing 9,000 TWh in 2023 and comprising roughly 30% of worldwide electricity output, driven by record additions in solar and wind capacity alongside steady hydroelectric contributions.[1][3] China dominates the list as the leading producer, generating over 3,700 TWh in 2022â€”more than any other nationâ€”primarily from massive hydroelectric projects like the Three Gorges Dam and explosive growth in utility-scale solar and wind farms.[4][5] Trailing are the United States, Brazil, and Canada, where hydroelectric power accounts for a substantial share of renewables, enabling reliable baseload supply amid variable weather-dependent sources.[6] Notable characteristics include the outsized role of hydropower in top rankings, which provides dispatchable power unlike intermittent wind and solar that necessitate backup systems or storage to maintain grid stability, a factor often understated in policy-driven expansions.[2] While absolute figures underscore scale advantages for developing giants like China and India, high-penetration leaders such as Norway (over 90% renewables, mostly hydro) exemplify feasible integration without compromising reliability.[7] Debates persist over environmental trade-offs, including habitat disruption from large dams and the full lifecycle emissions of solar panel manufacturing concentrated in China, highlighting the need for empirical assessment beyond aggregated capacity metrics.[1]

Production Rankings

Absolute Output in Terawatt-hours (TWh)

Absolute renewable electricity output measures the total volume generated from sources including hydropower, wind, solar photovoltaic, other solar technologies, bioenergy, and geothermal power. Unlike relative shares, this metric underscores the scale of production, often correlating with countries possessing expansive river systems for hydropower or vast areas for wind and solar farms. Global renewable electricity reached 8,928 TWh in 2023, up from prior years due to capacity expansions primarily in Asia.[8] China maintained its position as the absolute leader, producing over one-third of the world's renewable electricity through a mix dominated by hydropower (1,141 TWh) alongside surging wind and solar output exceeding 1,300 TWh combined.[9] The United States followed, generating roughly 900 TWh, with wind at approximately 340 TWh, solar at 239 TWh, and hydropower contributing about 250 TWh.[10] Brazil's production, heavily reliant on hydropower amid variable precipitation, totaled around 600 TWh, supplemented by wind (over 80 TWh) and emerging solar (52 TWh).[11]

Share as Percentage of National Electricity Generation

The share of renewable sources in a country's total electricity generation indicates the relative reliance on hydropower, wind, solar, biomass, and geothermal power compared to fossil fuels and nuclear, often driven by natural resource availability rather than policy alone. Globally, renewables constituted 30.1% of electricity generation in 2023, up from previous years due to expansions in solar and wind, though hydropower remains the dominant renewable source at around 15% worldwide.[13] This figure masks stark disparities: hydro-abundant nations achieve shares exceeding 90%, while arid or import-dependent economies lag below 10%, highlighting causal factors like terrain and precipitation over universal scalability of intermittent sources. Countries with the highest shares typically leverage hydroelectricity, which provides dispatchable baseload but depends on stable water flows and infrastructure investments. Norway, for instance, derived 98% of its electricity from renewables in 2023, overwhelmingly from hydropower, enabling low-carbon exports while minimizing fossil fuel use domestically.[14] Paraguay similarly reached approximately 99-100% renewable generation, primarily via the ItaipÃº Dam shared with Brazil, though this includes exports that exceed domestic consumption.[15] Other leaders include Iceland (nearly 100% from hydro and geothermal), Albania, Bhutan, Nepal, Ethiopia, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo, where hydropower exceeds 99% of output in many cases, reflecting equatorial river systems and mountainous geography rather than diversified renewables.[16] In regions without vast hydro potential, higher shares require variable renewable energy (VRE) integration, often paired with grid upgrades. Denmark achieved over 50% renewables in recent years through onshore and offshore wind, supplemented by biomass.[17] Uruguay and Brazil also surpass 80%, blending hydro with emerging wind and solar.[17] The European Union averaged 46.9% in 2024, led by Denmark and Austria, but this aggregates diverse national mixes where VRE growth offsets hydro variability.[18] Data from aggregators like Ember and IEA draw from national utilities and statistical agencies, though discrepancies arise from reporting lags, inclusion of net metering, or exclusion of self-consumed solar; hydro-rich tallies are generally verifiable via dam output records, reducing bias risks compared to self-reported VRE claims in politically incentivized contexts.

Global and Regional Context

Leading Countries and Absolute Leaders

China leads global renewable electricity production in absolute terms, generating an estimated 2,600 TWh in 2023, representing over 29% of the worldwide total of 8,928 TWh. This dominance stems from vast hydropower resources, including the Three Gorges Dam complex contributing significantly to the 1,141 TWh from hydro alone, combined with aggressive deployment of wind and solar photovoltaic capacity that added hundreds of gigawatts in recent years.[8][9][19] The United States follows as the second-largest producer, with approximately 1,000 TWh of renewable electricity in 2023, driven primarily by onshore wind (434 TWh), hydropower (251 TWh), and expanding solar (239 TWh including distributed systems), alongside biomass and geothermal contributions. Brazil ranks third, producing around 600 TWh, overwhelmingly from hydropower (about 60% of its total electricity), augmented by growing wind (52 TWh) and biomass from sugarcane. Canada and India complete the top five, with Canada's output centered on hydropower exceeding 370 TWh and India's mix of hydro, wind, and solar reaching about 340 TWh.[10][20][11]

Regional Disparities and Hydro-Dominated Economies

Renewable electricity production exhibits stark regional disparities, influenced by geography, infrastructure investment, and policy frameworks. In 2024, South America achieved the highest regional share at 75% of electricity from renewables, predominantly hydropower, while Europe reached approximately 51%, driven by a mix of wind, solar, and legacy hydro.[4] [21] In contrast, Asia and North America both hovered around 26-27%, with Africa and Eurasia at 24%, reflecting lower deployment in resource-constrained or fossil-fuel-dependent areas despite untapped hydro and solar potential.[22] These gaps persist due to causal factors like uneven capital accessâ€”Asia dominates absolute additions (over 70% of global capacity growth in 2024)â€”and infrastructural barriers in Africa, where grid limitations hinder scaling.[23] Hydro-dominated economies, often in regions with abundant river systems and precipitation, skew national renewable shares upward by providing dispatchable, high-capacity-factor generation that contrasts with intermittent sources elsewhere. Norway derives over 88% of its electricity from hydropower as of 2024, enabling near-total renewable reliance without widespread intermittency issues.[24] Paraguay generates nearly 100% from hydro, primarily via the Itaipu Dam shared with Brazil, exporting surplus to neighbors.[25] Brazil, a Latin American leader, produced over 70% of its electricity from hydro in recent years, though droughts have prompted diversification.[25] Canada similarly relies on hydro for about 60% of generation, concentrated in provinces like Quebec and British Columbia with vast reservoirs.[26]

Methodological Foundations

Definitions of Renewable Sources

Renewable electricity refers to electric power generated from sources that draw on naturally replenishing flows of energy, such as solar radiation, wind, water cycles, geothermal heat, and biomass growth, which are considered inexhaustible on human timescales unlike finite fossil fuel reserves.[28] These sources are distinguished by their reliance on environmental processes that renew the energy input without significant depletion, though their output remains constrained by natural variability and geographic availability.[29] Standard classifications exclude nuclear fission, which, despite low-carbon attributes, depends on mined fissile materials with limited replenishment rates, and fossil fuels, which involve combustion of stored ancient biomass.[30] The primary categories of renewable electricity sources include:
  • Hydropower: Generated by harnessing the kinetic energy of flowing or falling water, typically via dams, reservoirs, or run-of-river systems; it accounted for the largest share of global renewable electricity in historical data, though subject to seasonal and climatic fluctuations.[28][29]
  • Wind power: Derived from turbine conversion of atmospheric wind kinetic energy; onshore and offshore variants exist, with output varying by wind speed and location.[28]
  • Solar power: Produced through photovoltaic panels converting sunlight to direct current electricity or via concentrating solar thermal systems that use mirrors to generate steam for turbines; intermittent due to diurnal and weather dependencies.[30]
  • Geothermal power: Extracted from subterranean heat reservoirs using steam or hot water to drive turbines; baseload-capable but geographically limited to tectonically active regions.[28]
  • Biomass electricity: Generated by burning or gasifying recent organic matter (e.g., wood, agricultural residues, or municipal waste) to produce steam or syngas for turbines; includes biogenic components but excludes fossil-derived wastes, with emissions varying by feedstock sustainability.[31]
Minor contributors, such as tidal barrages exploiting gravitational lunar-solar forces or wave energy converters, fall under marine renewables but represent less than 1% of global output as of recent assessments.[29] International bodies like the International Energy Agency (IEA) and U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) align on these categories for statistical consistency, though some national definitions may adjust for policy incentives, such as excluding large-scale hydro in "modern renewables" metrics to emphasize variable sources.[29][32] This classification prioritizes the renewability of the fuel or flow over infrastructure durability, recognizing that while sources replenish, conversion technologies entail material and maintenance costs not captured in the label.[33]

Data Sources, Collection, and Potential Biases

Primary data on renewable electricity production by country derive from international agencies such as the International Energy Agency (IEA) and the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), which compile statistics from national submissions.[29][34] The IEA aggregates electricity generation data through its annual energy balances, drawing on official reports from member countries and estimates for non-members based on trade data, fuel consumption proxies, and satellite observations where direct metering is unavailable.[35] IRENA collects renewable-specific capacity and generation figures via annual questionnaires sent to over 170 member states and regional groups, focusing on technologies like solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, and bioenergy, with data traceable to 2000 and validated against national utility records.[36][37] Supplementary sources include aggregators like Our World in Data, which harmonize IEA and BP Statistical Review inputs for cross-verification, and Ember's Global Electricity Review, which uses hourly grid data from operators in select countries to refine shares.[2][38] Collection involves standardized reporting protocols, but variances arise from national methodologies: developed economies like those in the OECD rely on real-time metering from transmission system operators (e.g., ENTSO-E in Europe), while developing nations often extrapolate from capacity factors and historical output due to incomplete grid monitoring.[35] IRENA's process emphasizes direct country inputs, supplemented by expert consultations for gaps, such as in off-grid solar or small hydro, ensuring coverage of non-interconnected systems.[39] However, delays in reportingâ€”typically 1-2 yearsâ€”mean latest figures (e.g., 2023-2024) incorporate provisional estimates, revised as final national data emerge. Cross-agency reconciliation, as in IEA-IRENA joint reports, mitigates discrepancies by prioritizing primary generation metrics over capacity alone, since actual output depends on variable factors like weather for wind and solar.[37] Potential biases stem from incentives in source reporting and institutional frameworks. Governments, particularly in jurisdictions with renewable subsidies or emissions targets (e.g., EU's REPowerEU or China's 14th Five-Year Plan), may classify borderline sources like large-scale biomass or waste-to-energy as fully renewable to inflate figures, despite lifecycle emissions debates.[40] Data quality varies inversely with institutional transparency: authoritarian regimes or resource-constrained countries underreport or overstate via optimistic capacity utilization assumptions, as evidenced by discrepancies between claimed hydro output in Brazil and independent satellite hydrology validations.[41] IEA statistics, while rigorous, have faced criticism for methodological conservatism in non-fossil categories, potentially understating rapid solar/wind ramps due to lagged national inputs favoring established fuels.[42] IRENA, as a renewables advocacy body under UN auspices, risks selection bias by prioritizing positive deployment narratives, though its raw data remain empirically grounded; users should cross-check with IEA for balance.[34] Overall, empirical verification via multiple sources reduces systemic errors, but unverified estimates in 20-30% of global data (especially Africa and parts of Asia) introduce uncertainty, underscoring the need for skepticism toward aggregated shares without granular utility-level audits.[35]

Historical and Current Trends

Growth from 2000 to 2020

Global renewable electricity generation rose from approximately 2,902 terawatt-hours (TWh) in 2000, accounting for 19% of total electricity output, to 7,540 TWh in 2020, equivalent to 29% of global generation.[43] [44] This absolute increase of over 4,600 TWh reflected a compound annual growth rate exceeding 5%, outpacing overall electricity demand growth of about 3% annually, though much of the expansion stemmed from hydropower capacity additions in emerging economies rather than diversified sources.[45] Hydropower dominated early gains, contributing roughly 2,500 TWh in 2000 and stabilizing around 4,000 TWh by 2020, while non-hydro renewablesâ€”wind, solar, biomass, and geothermalâ€”surged from under 400 TWh to over 3,500 TWh, driven by technological cost reductions and policy incentives.[2] China led absolute growth among countries, with renewable output climbing from an estimated 200 TWh (primarily hydro) in 2000 to more than 2,000 TWh in 2020, representing over 40% of the nation's total electricity by the latter year.[46] This expansion was propelled by state-directed infrastructure, including the 22.5 gigawatt (GW) Three Gorges Dam operationalized in phases from 2003, which alone added hundreds of TWh annually, alongside rapid wind (over 280 GW installed by 2020) and solar scaling.[46] The United States followed with renewable generation increasing approximately 130% from around 350 TWh in 2000 to 800 TWh in 2020, fueled mainly by onshore wind farms exceeding 100 GW capacity and federal tax credits, though hydropower output remained flat at about 250-300 TWh amid environmental constraints on new dams.[44] [47] In Europe, collective renewable production grew from roughly 700 TWh to 1,500 TWh, with shares rising from 20% to over 35% of regional generation; Germany and Spain exemplified this through feed-in tariffs and auctions, adding over 60 GW wind and 50 GW solar by 2020, though growth was tempered by grid integration challenges and subsidy dependencies.[45] Brazil and Canada, hydro-reliant, saw more modest proportional increasesâ€”Brazil from 300 TWh (85% share) to 500 TWh, and Canada from 350 TWh (60% share) to 400 TWhâ€”reflecting limited new hydro viability due to environmental and geographic limits, with supplementary wind and solar comprising under 5% each by 2020.[2] India emerged as a late-decade accelerator, boosting from 100 TWh to 300 TWh via hydro and early solar initiatives under national targets.[29]

Developments from 2021 to 2025

Between 2021 and 2024, global renewable electricity generation expanded by over 20%, with annual additions accelerating from approximately 500 TWh in 2022 to a record 858 TWh in 2024, driven predominantly by solar photovoltaic output that more than doubled to exceed 2,000 TWh by the end of 2024.[49] This growth elevated the renewables share in total electricity production from 28.9% in 2021 to 32% in 2024, outpacing overall demand increases and marking the first year wind and solar combined surpassed hydropower's contribution.[49][21] Hydropower remained the largest renewable source at 14.3% of global generation in 2024, though its growth stagnated due to droughts in key regions like Brazil and China, while wind reached 8.1%.[49] China dominated absolute production gains, accounting for 53% of global solar additions in 2024 alone and meeting 81% of its electricity demand growth through low-carbon sources including renewables.[49] In the United States, renewable output rose steadily, supported by the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act, with solar and wind contributing to a 10% increase in clean generation share by 2024.[49] The European Union saw renewables reach 44% of its mix in 2024, up from 38% in 2021, fueled by wind expansions in offshore projects and policy mandates, though grid constraints limited full utilization.[49] India and Brazil maintained strong hydro bases but faced variability, with India's solar capacity tripling over the period to bolster non-hydro renewables.[50] In the first half of 2025, renewables grew 7.7% year-on-year to 5,072 TWh globally, surpassing coal generation for the first time and outpacing demand growth amid heatwave-driven consumption spikes.[51] Solar continued its trajectory, with over 25 countries exceeding 25% solar share in electricity production, up from fewer in prior years, reflecting maturing supply chains and cost declines below 0.03 USD/kWh in optimal sites.[51] However, hydro output dipped in regions affected by weather anomalies, underscoring renewables' vulnerability to hydrological variability despite overall upward trends.[8]

Key Challenges and Realities

Intermittency, Backup Requirements, and Grid Reliability

Renewable electricity sources, particularly solar photovoltaic (PV) and wind, are characterized by intermittency arising from their dependence on variable weather conditions and diurnal cycles, leading to output fluctuations uncorrelated with demand.[52] Global capacity factors for solar PV typically range from 13% to 25%, while onshore wind averages 25% to 35%, compared to 50-60% for combined-cycle natural gas plants, necessitating compensatory measures to match supply with continuous load requirements.[53] These low utilization rates imply that installed renewable capacity must be substantially oversizedâ€”often by factors of 2-3 times peak demandâ€”to approach baseload equivalence, exacerbating land use and material demands without eliminating the need for supplemental power.[54] Backup requirements for high renewable penetration involve a combination of dispatchable generation (e.g., natural gas peaker plants), grid-scale storage (e.g., lithium-ion batteries or pumped hydro), and interconnectors for import/export balancing.[55] In scenarios exceeding 50% variable renewable energy (VRE) share, backup capacity must cover extended low-generation periods, such as multi-day "Dunkelflaute" events in Europe where wind and solar output can fall below 5% of rated capacity, requiring near-full redundancy from non-VRE sources to avert shortfalls.[52] Storage alone proves insufficient at scale; for instance, achieving 80-100% renewable grids modeled by the IEA demands battery durations of 10-15 hours for daily cycling but far longer for seasonal gaps, with current global deployment covering only fractions of such needs as of 2024.[56] Gas-fired plants remain the primary flexible backup due to their rapid ramping (under 30 minutes to full load) and high availability, though this perpetuates emissions during high-VRE curtailment periods.[57] Grid reliability suffers from VRE-induced volatility, manifesting in frequency deviations, voltage instability, and heightened blackout risks without synchronous inertia from rotating generators.[57] In Germany, the Energiewende's push toward 80% renewables by 2030 has amplified these issues, with 2025 seeing a 31% drop in wind generation contributing to supply shortfalls and sustained gas plant operations for stability, alongside frequent negative pricing and curtailments exceeding 5 TWh annually.[58] [59] Similarly, California's 2020 heatwave blackouts and Texas's 2021 freeze exposed vulnerabilities, where VRE output collapsed to near-zero during critical demand peaksâ€”solar absent at night and wind turbines icedâ€”prompting reliance on fossil backups and revealing that even modest VRE shares (20-40%) strain ancillary services like reserves.[60] Empirical analyses confirm that VRE integration reduces overall system inertia by up to 50% in high-penetration grids, increasing the probability of cascading failures unless mitigated by synthetic inertia from inverters or retained thermal capacity.[61] These dynamics underscore that while interconnectivity and forecasting mitigate some risks, fundamental causal limits of weather-dependent generation demand robust, non-intermittent backups to preserve reliability standards equivalent to historical norms.[52][57]

Full Economic Costs Including Subsidies and Infrastructure

The full economic costs of renewable electricity production incorporate not only generation expenses but also subsidies that enable deployment, infrastructure expansions for grid stability, and system-level outlays for intermittency management, including backup capacity and storage. Standard metrics like levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) often exclude these elements, leading to underestimation of total societal burdens, as intermittency requires overprovision of capacity and dispatchable reserves to maintain reliability. Analyses using levelized full system costs of electricity (LFSCOE) demonstrate that high renewable penetration elevates overall expenses compared to dispatchable alternatives, due to the need for redundant infrastructure that operates suboptimally.[62][63] Subsidies for renewables, comprising production tax credits, investment incentives, and guaranteed payments, totaled at least USD 168 billion across G20 countries in 2023, representing transfers from public funds that obscure unsubsidized viability and distort competitive markets by favoring intermittent sources over reliable ones.[64] In the United States, federal incentives under the Inflation Reduction Act, including renewable tax credits, have driven capacity additions but imposed ongoing fiscal costs estimated in the hundreds of billions over the decade. European feed-in tariffs and contracts for difference similarly propped up deployment, with total support exceeding EUR 100 billion annually in recent years, often without full accounting for long-term dependency on fossil backups. These mechanisms, while accelerating rollout, elevate taxpayer burdens and hinder efficient resource allocation, as evidenced by economic models showing subsidy phase-outs would reduce overinvestment in uneconomic projects.[65] Infrastructure demands amplify costs, with grid upgrades to handle variable output requiring tens of billions of euros yearly in the European Union to interconnect dispersed renewables and mitigate congestion, projecting cumulative needs of over EUR 2 trillion by 2050.[66] In the United States, renewable integration contributed to USD 14 billion in grid modernization spending in 2024, including transmission lines and smart grid technologies to address variability. China's rapid renewable expansion, exceeding 500 GW of wind and solar added since 2020, has incurred curtailment losses of USD 2 billion in 2022 alone due to insufficient transmission, prompting escalated grid investments estimated at hundreds of billions of yuan through 2025. Intermittency further necessitates backup gas plants and emerging storage, adding USD 5-15 per MWh in system integration costs globally, as reserves must cycle inefficiently to fill generation gaps.[67][68][69]

Environmental Trade-offs and Lifecycle Assessments

Renewable electricity technologies, while reducing operational greenhouse gas emissions to near zero, incur significant environmental costs across their full lifecycle, encompassing raw material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, installation, operation, and decommissioning. Scaling solar and wind production demands vast land areasâ€”e.g., utility-scale solar farms requiring 5-10 acres per MW, leading to habitat fragmentation, soil compaction, and biodiversity loss in deployment zones, often competing with agriculture or native ecosystems.[75] Wind turbines contribute to avian and bat mortality, estimated at 140,000-500,000 bird deaths annually in the U.S. alone from collisions, alongside noise and visual impacts.[76] Hydropower alters aquatic habitats through damming, causing fish migration barriers, sedimentation buildup, and upstream flooding that submerges carbon-storing forests, elevating net methane emissions in tropical reservoirs by factors of 10-100 over natural rivers.[77] Material-intensive supply chains introduce further pressures: renewable deployment projected to triple mining needs for metals like copper (up 500% by 2050 for net-zero scenarios), neodymium for wind magnets, and silver for PV conductors, intensifying ecological threats in mining hotspots such as the Democratic Republic of Congo or Indonesian rainforests, where extraction correlates with deforestation rates exceeding 1 million hectares annually and toxic runoff contaminating waterways.[78] Biomass electricity, reliant on wood pellets or agricultural residues, risks exacerbating soil erosion, nutrient depletion, and indirect land-use change if sourced unsustainably, with some European imports linked to primary forest loss in Southeast Asia and North America.[77] Geothermal systems, though compact, involve drilling-induced seismicity and potential groundwater contamination from geofluids containing arsenic or mercury.[79] End-of-life management compounds impacts: PV panels, with 80% recyclability potential, often end in landfills due to economic barriers, leaching heavy metals like cadmium in thin-film variants; wind blades, composed of non-recyclable composites, accumulate as waste exceeding 50,000 tons yearly in Europe.[74] These trade-offsâ€”trading diffuse climate benefits for concentrated local harmsâ€”necessitate site-specific LCAs accounting for regional baselines, as global aggregation masks disparities, such as higher embodied emissions from coal-powered manufacturing in China, which supplies 80% of solar modules. Empirical evidence from harmonized studies emphasizes that while renewables yield net environmental gains over fossils, unsubsidized improvements in recycling, mineral sourcing, and hybrid systems (e.g., with storage) are required to curb secondary impacts.[73][72]
