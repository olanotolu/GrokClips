D-10 Strategy Forum

The D-10 Strategy Forum is a Track 1.5 dialogue platform initiated in 2014 by the Atlantic Council, convening senior policy planning officials and strategy experts from ten leading democracies—Australia, Canada, France, Germany, India, Italy, Japan, the Republic of Korea, the United Kingdom, and the United States—to foster strategic coordination on global challenges such as threats to the international order and geopolitical competition.[1][2] The forum builds on informal policy planning discussions originating from a 2008 U.S. Department of State initiative, evolving into regular meetings that enable candid exchanges between government officials and independent analysts without formal diplomatic constraints.[3] Its primary goal is to align democratic partners on assessments of systemic risks, including authoritarian influences and disruptions to liberal norms, thereby enhancing collective responses rather than producing binding agreements.[4] While not a decision-making body, the forum has facilitated discussions on topics like Russian aggression and supply chain vulnerabilities, contributing to informal networks that inform national strategies among participants.[5]

Origins and Development

US State Department Initiative (2008)

The D-10 concept emerged in 2008 from the U.S. Department of State's Office of Policy Planning, where staffers David Gordon and Ash Jain initiated informal bilateral discussions with Canadian counterparts to address global strategic challenges through enhanced coordination among like-minded democracies.[6][7] These early talks, coordinated with Canada's Global Affairs Institute, sought to extend G7-style collaboration beyond economic matters to include emerging democratic powers such as Australia, South Korea, and India, recognizing their growing geopolitical weight amid rising authoritarian assertiveness from actors like China and Russia.[3][8] The discussions emphasized pragmatic alignment on core interests, including upholding a rules-based international order, harmonizing technology governance standards, and building resilient supply chains less vulnerable to coercion, driven by empirical assessments of threats rather than expansive multilateral frameworks.[6][9] Without a formal organizational structure or public documentation at the outset, the initiative functioned as an ad hoc policy-planning dialogue, prioritizing candid exchanges among senior officials to identify shared strategic priorities over bureaucratic rituals.[3][7] This foundational effort reflected a first-principles recognition that democracies, collectively representing over 60% of global GDP, required dedicated mechanisms to counter coordinated authoritarian influence campaigns, particularly in domains like critical infrastructure and normative standards where unilateral vulnerabilities could cascade into systemic risks.[8][3] Gordon and Jain later advocated formalizing such coordination in op-eds, arguing that the absence of structured democratic alliances left the international system exposed to revisionist powers exploiting institutional gaps.[6]

Atlantic Council Track 1.5 Forum (2014 Onward)

The D-10 Strategy Forum was established in 2014 by the Atlantic Council, under the leadership of senior fellow Ash Jain, as a Track 1.5 dialogue platform to foster strategic coordination among leading democracies. This semi-official format blends participation from government officials, such as policy planning directors, with independent experts and think tank representatives, enabling candid discussions on global challenges without producing binding commitments. The inaugural meeting convened in Ottawa, Canada, on July 14–15, 2014, where participants from a core group of capable democracies assessed emerging threats to international stability, operating under rules that prioritized informal exchange over public attribution.[10][11] Defined explicitly as a Track 1.5 framework, the forum distinguishes itself from purely governmental (Track 1) or non-official (Track 2) processes by integrating official insights with external analysis to evaluate risks like the erosion of liberal democratic norms and state-sponsored disruptions. Co-organized with partners such as the Centre for International Governance Innovation, it emphasizes practical assessments of geopolitical pressures, including hybrid warfare tactics and authoritarian influence operations, drawing on empirical observations of real-world dynamics rather than ideological prescriptions. This structure allows for iterative refinement of shared understandings among participants, who represent nations with significant combined economic, military, and technological capacities.[4][12] The forum's evolution marked a transition from an initially U.S.-inspired initiative toward a balanced multilateral venue, with subsequent convenings building momentum through focused agendas. For instance, the second meeting, held in Rome, Italy, on June 15–16, 2015, at the Italian Ministry of Foreign Affairs, delved into specific vulnerabilities such as corruption networks, economic coercion by revisionist powers, and the need for resilient countermeasures grounded in observable causal mechanisms. Reports from these sessions highlight participants' consensus on prioritizing evidence-based strategies to counter systemic threats, underscoring the forum's role in cultivating informal alignment without formal alliances.[4][13]

Expansion Proposals (2020)

In May 2020, UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson proposed expanding the G7 framework into a D-10 summit by incorporating Australia, India, and South Korea, positioning it as a coalition of leading democracies to address pressing global issues.[14] This initiative emerged amid the COVID-19 pandemic's disruptions to global supply chains and escalating tensions with China over technology dominance, particularly in 5G infrastructure and telecommunications standards.[15] The U.S. administration under President Trump endorsed the concept, viewing it as a mechanism for aligned democracies to establish alternative governance norms in tech and economic resilience, distinct from broader forums like the UN.[7] The empirical foundation for the proposal rested on the D-10 nations' outsized economic leverage, collectively representing approximately 50% of global GDP in nominal terms by 2020, which enabled them to counterbalance authoritarian-led initiatives such as China's Belt and Road, with its estimated $1 trillion in infrastructure investments across 140 countries.[16] This approach emphasized causal linkages between democratic coordination and supply chain security, prioritizing like-minded partners capable of enforcing standards over ideologically diverse groupings, as evidenced by vulnerabilities exposed in pandemic-era dependencies on Chinese manufacturing.[17] Although a dedicated D-10 summit did not convene, partial implementation occurred via extended G7 engagements, including virtual outreach during 2020 and formal guest invitations to Australia, India, and South Korea at the 2021 UK-hosted G7 summit in Cornwall.[3] The Atlantic Council amplified this vision through reports advocating structured D-10 dialogues on democratic priorities, yet the absence of a permanent summit-level body underscored practical barriers to institutionalizing such expansions amid varying alliance appetites among members.[3]

Membership and Framework

Core Participating Democracies

The core participating democracies of the D-10 Strategy Forum comprise the G7 members—Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, the United Kingdom, and the United States—along with Australia, India, and South Korea.[18][19] These ten nations form a stable grouping focused on strategic coordination among advanced liberal democracies equipped with significant economic, technological, and military resources. Selection emphasizes empirical criteria such as high levels of democratic consolidation, measured by consistent electoral competition and rule of law adherence; leading positions in global GDP rankings, collectively accounting for over 50% of the world's nominal GDP; and capabilities in defense spending and alliances like NATO, QUAD, and AUKUS that enable deterrence against authoritarian powers including China and Russia.[3][20] The inclusion of Australia and South Korea bolsters Indo-Pacific representation, while India's participation adds demographic scale and emerging technological prowess in software and space, prioritizing geostrategic utility over ideological uniformity. The European Union participates occasionally as an observer, facilitating broader transatlantic input without formal membership, as its supranational structure complements rather than competes with the sovereign focus of the core states.[21] This configuration underscores causal priorities of collective resilience through aligned capabilities rather than expansive inclusivity.

Operational Structure as Track 1.5

The D-10 Strategy Forum functions as a Track 1.5 initiative, blending official government representatives—typically senior policy planners and strategy directors—with non-governmental experts from think tanks and academia to enable informal yet authoritative strategic deliberations.[3][4] This hybrid composition avoids the constraints of purely official (Track 1) diplomacy while leveraging insider perspectives, fostering environments for unfiltered analysis of global challenges.[22] Primarily organized by the Atlantic Council since its inception in 2014, the forum features rotating host venues across participating democracies to distribute leadership and encourage equitable input, such as sessions held in Rome in 2015 and Washington, D.C., in 2022. Discussions operate under Chatham House rules, permitting the use of shared insights without attributing them to individual speakers, which facilitates forthright exchanges on sensitive matters including sanctions alignment and establishing norms to counter authoritarian influences.[4][10] Unlike hierarchical or treaty-bound entities such as NATO, the D-10's structure prioritizes non-binding, consensus-oriented foresight exercises that emphasize long-term strategic alignment over immediate operational directives, allowing participants to explore scenarios without formal commitments.[11][17] This approach supports causal analysis of geopolitical trends while mitigating risks of premature public discord among allies.[4]

Objectives and Focus Areas

Advancing Democratic Coordination

The D-10 Strategy Forum advances democratic coordination by facilitating strategic alignment among ten leading democracies—Australia, Canada, France, Germany, India, Italy, Japan, South Korea, the United Kingdom, and the United States—to defend the rules-based international order. Established as a Track 1.5 dialogue mechanism, it convenes senior officials and experts to conduct collective assessments of pressing global challenges, including erosions of international norms by authoritarian actors, and to formulate joint strategies that prioritize systemic resilience over reactive measures.[4][2] This coordination draws on observable patterns of authoritarian expansion, such as economic coercion tactics that create dependencies in vulnerable states, to underscore the urgency of unified democratic responses.[5] Central to this effort is a recognition of democracies' structural advantages in generating innovation through open markets and research ecosystems, contrasted with the limitations of authoritarian models reliant on state-directed coercion and opacity. The forum promotes shared outlooks that reject deterministic views of multipolar shifts favoring autocracies, instead emphasizing causal factors like alliance reliability and adaptive governance that have historically enabled democratic coalitions to outpace rivals in technological and economic domains.[10] By focusing on these first-mover strengths, participants aim to construct resilient supply chains and normative frameworks that mitigate risks from adversarial influence operations.[23] In pursuit of a sustainable vision, the D-10 seeks to evolve into a institutionalized platform for ongoing strategic deliberation, functioning as a democratic complement to diluted multilateral venues like the G20, where autocratic members exert disproportionate sway. This pragmatic institutionalization avoids idealizing expansive liberal orders, concentrating instead on targeted coordination that aligns national interests with collective defense of core principles such as sovereignty and fair competition.[21][8] Such an approach fosters causal realism by grounding strategies in verifiable democratic performance metrics, including higher rates of patent generation and treaty compliance, to sustain long-term preeminence without overextension.[19]

Key Strategic Priorities

The D-10 Strategy Forum prioritizes coordination on technology standards to counter competitive advantages held by authoritarian regimes, particularly in telecommunications infrastructure. A central focus has been the development of secure 5G networks, with deliberations advocating for exclusion of high-risk vendors like Huawei to mitigate espionage risks and reduce dependency on Chinese supply chains. This emphasis contributed to policy alignments among members, such as the UK's 2020 decision to phase out Huawei equipment by 2027, framed within D-10 discussions on building democratic technology alliances.[24][25][26] Economic statecraft ranks as a core theme, targeting resilience against coercion through diversified supply chains in critical sectors like semiconductors and rare earth minerals. Forum participants have explored "ally-shoring" mechanisms to prioritize trade among democracies, linking coordination to tangible outcomes such as the post-2020 acceleration of friend-shoring policies that reduced vulnerabilities exposed by pandemic disruptions and trade tensions. These efforts underscore a realist view that selective decoupling enhances strategic autonomy without reverting to broad protectionism, as demonstrated by initiatives like the US CHIPS Act of 2022, which allocated $52 billion to domestic semiconductor production.[27][28] Hybrid threats, including state-sponsored disinformation, feature in priorities aimed at bolstering collective defenses through shared intelligence and norm-setting. Discussions emphasize causal linkages between uncoordinated responses and amplified societal divisions, advocating for proactive measures like joint fact-checking frameworks and cyber resilience standards to preserve democratic cohesion amid information operations from actors such as Russia and China.[26] Adaptations to geopolitical shocks, notably Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, have elevated energy security and sanctions efficacy as recurring imperatives. Forum insights have informed synchronized sanctions that isolated Russia from global finance, with over 16,500 restrictive measures imposed by mid-2023 leading to a 30% drop in its oil exports to OECD countries and contributing to a 2022 GDP contraction of 2.1%. This coordination highlights empirical benefits of democratic alignment in enforcing costs on aggressors while accelerating alternatives like LNG imports to Europe, which rose 60% in 2022 to offset Russian gas reductions.[10][5]

Activities and Meetings

Early Convenings (2014–2015)

The inaugural meeting of the D-10 Strategy Forum took place on July 14–15, 2014, in Ottawa, Canada, hosted by the Centre for International Governance Innovation in partnership with the Atlantic Council.[10] This Track 1.5 gathering assembled policy planners, defense strategists, and experts from the ten core democracies—Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, South Korea, the United Kingdom, and the United States, with European Union representation—to assess threats to the post-World War II liberal order.[11] Discussions, conducted under Chatham House rules, examined geopolitical flashpoints including Russia's annexation of Crimea, intervention in eastern Ukraine, China's maritime assertiveness in the South China Sea and East China Sea, and turmoil in the Middle East amid the rise of the Islamic State.[10] Participants identified coordination shortfalls among democratic states, advocating a "contrainment" approach—combining deterrence, engagement, and resilience-building—to address authoritarian revisionism without escalating to Cold War-style isolation.[11] The non-binding report underscored the forum's role in enabling candid, off-the-record exchanges to bridge gaps in official channels.[10] The second convening occurred on June 15–16, 2015, at Italy's Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Rome, co-organized by the Atlantic Council, the Centre for International Governance Innovation, and the Istituto Affari Internazionali.[4][2] Senior officials and analysts from the D-10 group reconvened to deepen strategic alignment, focusing on internal vulnerabilities like corruption in governance, barriers to inclusive development, and the adaptive resilience of authoritarian models in states such as Russia and China.[4] Sessions emphasized practical measures for democratic coordination, including shared intelligence on hybrid threats and economic tools to promote rule-of-law standards abroad.[29] Reiterating the framework's value established in Ottawa, the meeting generated recommendations for proactive, collective responses to erode authoritarian advantages, such as targeted sanctions and capacity-building in partner nations.[4] The resulting report formalized the forum's emphasis on iterative, expert-driven dialogues to inform national policies without producing formal commitments.[29] These early sessions laid the groundwork for the D-10's ongoing Track 1.5 model, prioritizing empirical threat assessments over declarative consensus.[11]

Recent Engagements (2016–Present)

The D-10 Strategy Forum has held regular Track 1.5 meetings since 2016, evolving to address escalating geopolitical challenges including Russian aggression and Chinese technological influence. The third convening occurred in Brussels on January 25–26, 2016, hosted with support from European institutions to deepen policy coordination on global security.[2] Subsequent sessions included Tokyo on September 12–13, 2016, focusing on Asia-Pacific dynamics; London on May 18–19, 2017, emphasizing transatlantic alignment; and Seoul on September 11–12, 2018, organized in partnership with the Asan Institute for Policy Studies to discuss threats to the rules-based order.[2] [30] In May 2019, the seventh meeting in Berlin convened policy planning officials from the ten democracies to reinforce commitments to international norms amid rising authoritarian pressures.[31] Discussions increasingly incorporated strategies for constraining adversarial actions, as evidenced by the 2017 Atlantic Council-CIGI report advocating "constrainment" toward Russia—combining containment and engagement to limit its capacity to undermine democratic security without direct confrontation.[12] This approach reflected empirical assessments of Russia's post-Crimea interventions and hybrid tactics.[32] The forum reconvened in Washington, DC, in late October 2022, with U.S. Secretary of State Antony Blinken hosting receptions for policy directors and ambassadors to align on responses to the Russia-Ukraine war and supply chain vulnerabilities exacerbated by China's dominance in critical technologies like semiconductors and 5G infrastructure.[33] [5] These engagements have sustained biennial or ad hoc cycles, prioritizing practical coordination on technology standards and economic resilience, while adapting to post-2022 shifts such as enhanced NATO integration and democratic countermeasures against authoritarian export controls.[22] Recent outputs, including 2025 Atlantic Council analyses, underscore ongoing emphasis on collective democratic strategies amid persistent tensions.[5]

Impact and Assessments

Achievements in Countering Authoritarian Challenges

The D-10 Strategy Forum has facilitated discussions among leading democracies that informed coordinated restrictions on high-risk telecommunications equipment, particularly Huawei's 5G infrastructure. In 2020, following the United Kingdom's ban on Huawei's involvement in its 5G networks—announced on July 14 and effective from 2027—the UK proposed expanding the D-10 framework into a formal alliance of ten democracies (G7 plus Australia, India, and South Korea) to develop alternative 5G technologies and standards, reducing collective reliance on Chinese vendors.[34][35] This initiative aligned with prior exclusions in Australia (2018 ban) and the United States, and subsequent actions in Canada (2022 prohibition) and Japan, correlating with Huawei's market share in 5G core networks dropping to near zero in these jurisdictions by 2023, as operators shifted to vendors like Ericsson and Nokia.[36][37] Forum convenings have also contributed to strategic alignment on semiconductor supply chains and AI governance, influencing broader G7 efforts to mitigate authoritarian dependencies. D-10 reports and meetings emphasized diversified tech ecosystems, paralleling the G7's 2021-2023 initiatives on semiconductor resilience, including the establishment of a G7 Semiconductor Point of Contact group in 2022 to enhance supply chain security and export controls on advanced chips to China.[18][38] These discussions supported policies like the U.S. CHIPS and Science Act (2022) and EU Chips Act (2023), which allocated over $50 billion and €43 billion respectively for domestic production, correlating with a 15-20% reduction in collective exposure to Chinese-dominated nodes in global semiconductor fabrication by 2024.[39] In addressing Russian aggression, early D-10 assessments of Moscow's energy weaponization—dating to 2014 meetings—provided foresight that aided post-2022 invasion responses, including synchronized sanctions and diversification strategies among members.[10] The forum's emphasis on rules-based coordination underpinned G7+ actions, such as the 2022 price cap on Russian oil and EU efforts to cut gas imports from 40% of total supply in 2021 to under 8% by 2024 through LNG imports from the U.S., Australia, and Qatar, thereby limiting Russia's war funding by an estimated $100-200 billion in foregone revenues.[18] These measures empirically constrained authoritarian leverage, as evidenced by Russia's pivot to discounted sales to China and India, while bolstering democratic energy security.[40] The forum's informal networks have empirically supported evolving proposals for democratic alliances, such as the UK's 2021 "Network of Liberty" concept, which built on D-10 dialogues to counter narratives of Western decline by promoting shared prosperity and norm enforcement.[41] This has manifested in enhanced policy synchronization, including joint statements on countering autocratic influence operations, fostering resilience against hybrid threats from Russia and China.[3]

Criticisms and Limitations

The D-10 Strategy Forum's informal Track 1.5 structure, involving officials and experts rather than heads of state, has been critiqued for constraining its ability to drive enforceable outcomes, as discussions yield recommendations without binding commitments or sanctions for non-compliance.[3] This non-binding format relies on voluntary policy alignment among members, which skeptics argue diminishes efficacy against coordinated authoritarian challenges, such as supply chain dependencies or technology standards, where divergent national interests—evident in varying approaches to Huawei restrictions—hinder unified action.[4] Following UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson's 2020 proposal to expand the G7 into a D-10 by incorporating Australia, India, and South Korea for enhanced democratic coordination, no formal leaders' summits have convened, perpetuating the forum's lower-profile status and limiting high-level political momentum.[3] Analysts have noted that without proactive elevation to Track 1, the initiative risks stagnation amid competing multilateral engagements, potentially contributing to forum fatigue where overlapping dialogues dilute focus and resources.[42] The forum's membership, centered on economically advanced democracies with aligned strategic capabilities, has drawn accusations of elitism and exclusionary bias, omitting major emerging powers like Brazil and forgoing full EU institutional representation despite individual European participation. Critics from multipolar perspectives portray it as a U.S.- and U.K.-led mechanism to consolidate Western influence and isolate China, fostering perceptions of an Indo-Pacific tilt that prioritizes hawkish containment over inclusive global governance, even as European inclusion tempers pure regionalism. Such selectivity may alienate Global South neutrals, complicating broader democratic appeals in a post-Western order where efficacy hinges on wider buy-in beyond established allies.[42]

Leadership and Organization

Key Organizers and Figures

Ash Jain, a senior fellow in the Atlantic Council's Scowcroft Center on International Security, has directed the D-10 Strategy Forum since its establishment in 2014, coordinating Track 1.5 dialogues among senior policymakers from the ten participating democracies.[5] Prior to joining the Atlantic Council, Jain served on the U.S. State Department's Policy Planning Staff, where he co-developed the D-10 concept in 2008 with David Gordon, then-director of policy planning, as a mechanism for enhanced strategic coordination among leading democracies.[8] Jain's publications, including the 2021 report "From the G7 to a D-10: Strengthening Democratic Cooperation for Today’s Challenges," advocate expanding democratic alliances to address geopolitical threats through shared institutional strengths and coordinated responses, drawing on empirical assessments of authoritarian challenges.[18] The Atlantic Council acts as the primary institutional host, leveraging its expertise in global strategy to facilitate the forum's convenings, with Jain emphasizing pragmatic alignments based on democratic governance advantages over ideological uniformity.[43] Collaborating organizations include the Centre for International Governance Innovation (CIGI), which co-hosted early meetings such as the 2015 Rome convening, and the Istituto Affari Internazionali (IAI), contributing European policy perspectives.[4] Participant involvement typically features policy planning directors or equivalents from member states' foreign ministries, maintained anonymously in public reports to preserve candid strategic discourse.[11]
