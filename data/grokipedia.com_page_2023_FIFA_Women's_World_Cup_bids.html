2023 FIFA Women's World Cup bids

The bidding process for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup involved FIFA member associations submitting proposals to host the ninth edition of the tournament, which marked the expansion to 32 teams and emphasized enhanced infrastructure, commercial viability, and fan engagement criteria compared to prior editions.[1][2] FIFA initiated the process in December 2018 by inviting expressions of interest, with a deadline of March 2019, followed by formal bid submissions due in December 2019; four associations or joint groups complied, including the Football Federation Australia and New Zealand Football partnership, the United States Soccer Federation, the Japan Football Association, and the Colombian Football Federation.[1] The United States withdrew its candidacy before full evaluation, leaving three bids for detailed assessment in FIFA's June 2020 report, which scored the Australia-New Zealand joint bid highest at 4.1 out of 5 for its proposed stadiums, logistics, and revenue potential across 13 venues in both nations.[3][2] On June 25, 2020, the FIFA Council selected the Australia-New Zealand bid via secret ballot, with it securing 22 of 35 valid votes against Colombia's 13, making it the first transcontinental hosting and the first joint bid to win for a Women's World Cup. The process highlighted FIFA's strategic push for women's football growth amid limited global bidding interest, as only initial expressions from over a dozen associations narrowed to few viable submissions, reflecting uneven development in hosting capabilities outside established markets.[4][5]

Background

Historical Context of Women's World Cup Hosting

The FIFA Women's World Cup began in 1991 with China as the host nation, marking the first official global championship for women's national teams organized by FIFA. Subsequent editions up to 2019 were hosted by single countries: Sweden in 1995, the United States in 1999 and 2003, China again in 2007, Germany in 2011, Canada in 2015, and France in 2019.[6] These choices reflected FIFA's emphasis on nations possessing adequate football infrastructure, including multiple stadiums with capacities exceeding 20,000 seats, as seen in the United States' utilization of venues like the Rose Bowl for the 1999 final, which drew 90,185 spectators.[7] Early selections prioritized geographic rotation and promotion of the sport in regions with emerging interest, such as Asia for 1991 and 2007, while later hosts like Germany and France demonstrated Europe's strong domestic leagues and facilities.[8] Host designations in the tournament's initial phases were determined through FIFA executive committee decisions rather than competitive bidding processes, allowing direct awards to capable federations. For example, Sweden's 1995 hosting followed its successful organization of the 1994 men's UEFA European Championship, highlighting FIFA's reliance on proven logistical capabilities and government guarantees for security and funding.[9] By the 1999 edition, formal interest declarations emerged, with the United States Soccer Federation announcing its bid in February 1995, underscoring factors like transportation infrastructure and accommodation availability for teams and media.[10] Critical considerations included stadium quality and capacity to handle expanding team numbersâ€”from 12 nations in 1991 to 24 from 1999 onwardâ€”as well as financial backing to cover operational costs without undue reliance on FIFA subsidies. Over time, the tournament evolved toward larger-scale production akin to the men's World Cup, driven by rising global interest and attendance. The 2019 edition in France achieved an average of 21,756 spectators per match across 52 games, a marked increase from earlier tournaments, reflecting improved marketing and host investments in fan zones and broadcasting facilities.[11] This progression favored hosts with established event-management expertise, such as Canada's 2015 selection, which benefited from proximity to major population centers and upgraded venues, though challenges like weather and venue distribution influenced evaluations.[10] Such patterns emphasized causal links between host infrastructure and event success, prioritizing reliability over novelty in selections.

FIFA's Expansion Plans and Bidding Objectives for 2023

In July 2019, following the commercial and attendance success of the 2019 FIFA Women's World Cup in France, which drew over 1.1 million spectators and generated record revenues, the FIFA Council unanimously approved expanding the tournament from 24 to 32 teams starting with the 2023 edition.[12] This decision, proposed by FIFA President Gianni Infantino, aimed to accelerate the global growth of women's football by increasing participation opportunities for more national teams, particularly from underrepresented confederations, while leveraging larger player pools to enhance competitive depth and viewer engagement.[13][14] The expansion's primary objectives centered on sustainable commercial viability rather than purely symbolic measures, with FIFA emphasizing the need for host bids to demonstrate robust economic models, including projected revenue from broadcasting rights, sponsorships, and ticket sales scaled to accommodate 64 matches across 32 teams.[15] This approach sought to build on empirical evidence from prior tournaments, where infrastructure investments yielded long-term returns through upgraded facilities that supported domestic leagues and youth programs, prioritizing causal pathways to financial self-sufficiency over unsubstantiated equity narratives.[16] Bids were required to outline verifiable plans for stadium upgrades and logistical capacity to handle the increased scale, ensuring that hosting would catalyze measurable advancements in women's soccer infrastructure without relying on disproportionate public subsidies.[2] On February 19, 2019, FIFA initiated the bidding process with an open invitation to all member associations, explicitly encouraging expressions of interest from confederations that had not previously hosted the tournament, such as Oceania, to promote geographic diversity and tap into untapped markets for broader revenue potential.[17] This targeted outreach reflected FIFA's strategic intent to rigorize the selection criteria, focusing on bids that could deliver empirical evidence of fan base expansion and economic multipliers, thereby fostering causal realism in the tournament's role as a driver for women's football's institutionalization worldwide.[18]

Bidding Process

Announcement and Key Timeline Events

FIFA initiated the bidding process for the 2023 Women's World Cup on 19 February 2019, inviting member associations to express interest in hosting the expanded 32-team tournament.[19] The deadline for submitting expressions of interest was set for 15 March 2019, followed by a bidding registration deadline of 16 April 2019.[19] Full bid submissions were due by 13 December 2019, by which date four eligible bids had been received: a joint bid from Australia and New Zealand, as well as individual bids from Brazil, Colombia, and Japan.[1] The global COVID-19 pandemic disrupted the original timeline, postponing on-site inspections and shifting subsequent decisions to virtual formats.[20] Brazil withdrew its bid on 9 June 2020, citing financial pressures exacerbated by the pandemic.[21] Japan followed with its withdrawal on 22 June 2020, also attributing the decision to economic challenges amid the crisis.[22] On 25 June 2020, the FIFA Council conducted an online vote and awarded hosting rights to the joint Australia-New Zealand bid.[23]

Submission Requirements and Eligibility Criteria

FIFA permitted bids from any of its 211 member associations, either individually or jointly, with eligibility restricted to associations from the AFC, CAF, Concacaf, CONMEBOL, OFC, and UEFA confederations.[24] Joint bids were allowed provided they demonstrated feasible cross-border logistics and supporting infrastructure, marking a departure from prior editions which lacked joint hosting precedents for the Women's World Cup.[24] Bidders were required to submit a comprehensive bid book by December 13, 2019, detailing the event vision, infrastructure plans, team and referee facilities, accommodation, transportation, safety and security measures, commercial revenue projections, and commitments to human rights and sustainability aligned with ISO 20121 standards.[1][24] Essential accompanying documents included unaltered government declarations and guarantees covering visas, customs clearance, security provision, tax exemptions, and financial liabilities, alongside legal statements affirming compliance with FIFA statutes, anti-doping conventions, and fiscal responsibilities to ensure enforceable delivery.[24] Stadium standards mandated a minimum of eight venues, with at least six confirmed and three existing at the time of bidding; capacity thresholds included 20,000 seats for group stage matches, 35,000 for semi-finals, and 55,000 for the opening match and final, with exclusive use granted from 14 days before the first match to five days after the last.[24] Security protocols required host governments to furnish a comprehensive safety strategy assuming full liability across all competition sites, while commercial elements demanded evidenced projections for national supporter attendance, ticketing, hospitality, merchandising, and food/beverage revenues, supplemented by any subsidies or contributions.[24] Verification emphasized fiscal realism through on-site inspections conducted by FIFA from January to February 2020, culminating in a bid evaluation report assessing compliance with minimum hosting requirements; non-compliance with these thresholds, such as infrastructure or guarantee shortfalls, could disqualify bids prior to FIFA Council review.[1][24] This process prioritized bids backed by verifiable legal and financial assurances over speculative commitments to mitigate risks of delivery failure.[24]

FIFA's Evaluation Framework and Methodology

FIFA developed a robust evaluation model for the 2023 Women's World Cup bids, comprising three key components: a technical evaluation assessing infrastructure and commercial criteria via a predefined scoring system, a risk assessment rating potential issues such as human rights and sustainability, and a descriptive summary of each bid's hosting vision without assigned scores.[25][1] The technical evaluation prioritized quantitative metrics, including stadium capacities, transportation logistics, accommodation availability, and commercial revenue projections, with emphasis on existing infrastructure over planned developments to favor empirically verifiable feasibility and reduce execution risks.[25][26] To inform these assessments, FIFA delegations conducted on-site inspections of proposed venues and facilities in bidding countries during early 2020, verifying compliance with requirements for safety, accessibility, and operational readiness through direct observation and data collection.[27][26] The resulting Bid Evaluation Report, published on June 10, 2020, presented these findings transparently to the FIFA Council, serving as the empirical foundation for the hosting decision on June 25, 2020, and mitigating subjective influences by relying on standardized, data-backed ratings.[25][25]

Official Bids

Australia and New Zealand Joint Bid

The joint bid, branded "As One," was submitted on 12 December 2019 by Football Federation Australia and New Zealand Football to host the expanded 32-team tournament across both nations.[1][28] It proposed utilizing 10 existing stadiums in six Australian cities (Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide, and an initial regional option later adjusted) and four New Zealand cities (Auckland, Wellington, Hamilton, and Dunedin), with the final match at Stadium Australia in Sydney, which has a capacity of 83,500.[29][30] The proposal emphasized leveraging established infrastructure to minimize construction needs, including rectangular stadiums like Melbourne Rectangular Stadium and Eden Park in Auckland, alongside provisions for match scheduling across four time zones to facilitate trans-Tasman travel via short flights between the countries.[31] Government commitments included up to NZ$55 million from New Zealand for operational support and infrastructure enhancements, complemented by Australian federal and state pledges totaling around US$75 million across both nations for event delivery and legacy projects.[32][33] Commercial aspects highlighted opportunities in Oceania's underdeveloped women's football market, projecting revenue growth from Australia's population of over 25 million and its robust sports engagement culture, including high attendance at domestic leagues, while New Zealand's involvement aimed to expand regional participation.[31] The bid addressed logistical challenges through dedicated transport corridors and fan zones, positioning the event as a platform for cross-confederation collaboration between AFC and OFC affiliates.[28]

Colombia Bid

The Colombia bid for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup was submitted by the Colombian Football Federation on 13 December 2019.[1] It proposed hosting the expanded 32-team tournament across ten venues in ten cities, aiming to stage the event in South America for the first time.[34] The bid emphasized leveraging Colombia's geographic centrality in the continent to attract regional spectators, projecting higher attendance from neighboring countries compared to bids in more isolated locations.[35] Key proposed venues included Estadio Nemesio Camacho El CampÃ­n in BogotÃ¡, planned as the site for the opening match and final with a capacity of approximately 39,500 following upgrades; Estadio OlÃ­mpico Pascual Guerrero in Cali (capacity 38,558); and Estadio Metropolitano Roberto MelÃ©ndez in Barranquilla, the largest at 46,692 seats.[36] Other host cities encompassed Armenia, Bucaramanga, Cartagena, CÃºcuta, Manizales, MedellÃ­n, and Pereira, utilizing existing stadiums with proposed enhancements to meet FIFA standards.[3] The selection reflected a strategy to distribute economic benefits nationwide while capitalizing on Colombia's established soccer infrastructure. Bid Evaluation Report

Technical Scoring and Overall Assessments

The FIFA Bid Evaluation Report for the 2023 Women's World Cup, published on 10 June 2020, assessed the three eligible bidsâ€”Australia/New Zealand, Colombia, and Japanâ€”across 11 technical criteria weighted by importance, yielding overall average scores out of 5.[25] These included infrastructure, stadiums, accommodations, transport, telecommunications/IT, security/human rights, legal provisions, commercial aspects, match schedule, government support, and women's football development, with scores derived from document reviews, site visits, and expert analysis.[3] The Australia/New Zealand joint bid achieved the highest aggregate score of 4.1, reflecting strong performance in infrastructure (due to a diverse array of existing modern venues across 13 stadiums in 12 cities, minimizing construction needs) and commercial viability (with revenue forecasts exceeding USD 100 million from ticketing, broadcasting, and sponsorships).[37][38] Japan's bid scored 3.9 overall, demonstrating balance across categories such as stadium readiness (leveraging facilities from the 2019 Rugby World Cup and 2020 Olympics) and women's football development, though it was marginally outpaced by the joint bid's larger scale and transcontinental fan accessibility.[37][3] Colombia received the lowest score of 2.8, primarily due to deficiencies in stadium infrastructure (heavy reliance on renovations for six of eight venues, with potential delays) and government guarantees (incomplete assurances on funding and legal frameworks).[34][3] These disparities highlighted objective gaps in readiness and scalability, with the joint bid's metrics indicating superior alignment with FIFA's emphasis on commercial growth and logistical feasibility for an expanded 32-team tournament.[38]

Comparative Analysis of Bid Strengths and Deficiencies

The Australia and New Zealand joint bid demonstrated superior infrastructure readiness compared to Colombia's, primarily through utilization of pre-existing venues such as Sydney's Stadium Australia (capacity over 80,000) and Melbourne's Cricket Ground (over 100,000), which largely met or exceeded FIFA's capacity mandates without extensive renovations, reducing execution risks. Colombia's infrastructure, while featuring viable stadiums like BogotÃ¡'s El CampÃ­n, required substantial upgrades for media, hospitality, and pitch standards across multiple sites, including the proposed final venue in Barranquilla, heightening vulnerabilities to construction delays amid local fiscal constraints and regulatory hurdles. The trans-Tasman geographic dispersion in the joint bid, spanning approximately 2,200 kilometers between major host cities, introduced logistical complexities like extended team travel but facilitated wider regional accessibility and diversified fan bases in Oceania, contrasting Colombia's more centralized approach in five cities that risked overburdening transport networks in a seismically active zone.[3][37] In commercial and financial domains, the joint bid projected robust revenue streams, including higher ticket sales estimates driven by established markets in Australia (with the A-League Women averaging over 2,000 attendees per match pre-bid) and sponsorship alignments with global brands leveraging stable Australian and New Zealand dollars against inflation. Colombia's projections suffered from conservative media rights and sponsorship forecasts, exacerbated by the Colombian peso's historical volatility (depreciating over 20% against the USD in 2019) and limited appeal to international broadcasters due to perceived security and economic instability, though offset partially by lower operational costs from domestic labor rates. These disparities underscored fiscal realism in the joint bid's government-backed funding model, totaling around USD 75 million in guarantees, versus Colombia's reliance on national commitments amid broader budgetary pressures from pandemic-era fiscal deficits.[3][34] For development impact and legacy, both bids emphasized women's football growth, but the joint proposal integrated measurable strategies tied to Oceania's maturing ecosystems, such as expanding the W-League's infrastructure and participation rates (which had grown 15% annually pre-2020), aiming for sustained post-event programs in coaching and grassroots initiatives across Asia-Pacific confederations. Colombia highlighted transformative potential for CONMEBOL by elevating visibility in a region with lower federation investments in women's programs, yet lacked equivalent empirical baselines, with domestic leagues showing sporadic attendance under 1,000 and governance challenges including labor disputes that could undermine long-term causal pathways to participation surges. The joint bid's edge stemmed from demonstrable pre-existing momentum, enabling more credible projections of legacy multipliers like increased female registrations, whereas Colombia's ambitions faced causal barriers from infrastructural dependencies and regional disparities in gender equity metrics.[3][25]

Withdrawn Bids

Brazil Withdrawal

The Brazilian Football Confederation (CBF) submitted a bid to host the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup on December 13, 2019, one of four official entries alongside joint bids from Australia-New Zealand, Colombia, and Japan; the proposal centered on ten venues across eight host cities, prominently featuring the MaracanÃ£ Stadium in Rio de Janeiro alongside stadiums in SÃ£o Paulo, Belo Horizonte, and BrasÃ­lia.[1][39] On June 8, 2020, the CBF withdrew the bid prior to FIFA's formal evaluation process, citing an inability to secure required financial guarantees from the federal government amid severe economic disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic.[40][21] The decision stemmed from fiscal austerity measures and budget shortfalls that rendered government-backed assurances unfeasible, with CBF president RogÃ©rio Caboclo emphasizing a "combination of factors" including the pandemic's impact on public finances, which had already strained resources post-2014 men's World Cup and 2016 Olympics.[41][42] This pre-voting pullout underscored Brazil's prioritization of fiscal realism over hosting ambitions, as the country faced recessionary pressures with GDP contracting by 4.1% in 2020 and public debt exceeding 80% of GDP; no provision for re-entry was made, effectively narrowing the competition to three bids ahead of the June 25, 2020, FIFA Council decision.[39][43] The withdrawal exemplified wider bidding disruptions from the global health crisis, though Brazil pledged support to regional rival Colombia's candidacy.[42]

Japan Withdrawal

The Japan Football Association (JFA) announced on June 22, 2020, its withdrawal from the bidding process for the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, just three days prior to the scheduled FIFA Council vote on June 25.[44] This decision followed an executive board meeting and positioned the JFA to endorse the joint Australia-New Zealand bid, effectively narrowing the contest to that proposal and Colombia's solo entry.[45][46] In FIFA's Bid Evaluation Report released on June 10, 2020, Japan's proposal received an overall technical score of 3.9 out of 5, placing it second behind the Australia-New Zealand joint bid's 4.1 while surpassing Colombia's 2.8.[37][3] The bid emphasized Japan's established infrastructure, including venues like Saitama Stadium (capacity 63,000), and its track record in women's football, highlighted by the national team's 2011 World Cup victory and consistent performances in subsequent tournaments.[46] Despite these strengths, particularly in technical and stadium criteria, the joint bid's innovative cross-confederation format and projected commercial appeal were deemed superior in the evaluation.[3] The withdrawal stemmed from a strategic reassessment that the Australia-New Zealand bid was poised to prevail, prompting Japan to avoid diluting support within the Asian Football Confederation (AFC) and instead consolidate backing for the joint proposal to strengthen regional influence in FIFA decisions.[46][47] JFA President Kozo Tashima cited the evaluation's insights and the joint bid's momentum as key factors, noting that competing further risked fragmenting votes without altering the likely outcome.[48] This move also reflected broader considerations, including the proximity of Asia's hosting of the 2022 FIFA men's World Cup in Qatar, which may have tempered enthusiasm for another major tournament in the region so soon after the 2019 women's edition in Europe.[49] Following the announcement, the AFC formally endorsed the Australia-New Zealand bid, underscoring the diplomatic rationale behind Japan's exit.[50]

Other Expressions of Interest

Non-Submitted or Preliminary Interests

Several FIFA member associations expressed preliminary interest in hosting the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup following the organization's call for bids in early 2019, but did not proceed to submit formal bid books by the December 13, 2019, deadline.[51][1] These efforts remained exploratory, often citing insufficient government guarantees, infrastructure limitations, or logistical challenges as barriers to advancement. Argentina's football association submitted an expression of interest by the March 15, 2019, deadline but did not register for the full bidding process or submit documentation thereafter.[51] The interest faded without further development, amid the country's economic constraints at the time, though no official withdrawal statement was issued.[52] Belgium's association indicated interest in August 2019, bringing the total expressions to ten, but withdrew before FIFA dispatched updated bidding documents in early September 2019.[53][54] This early exit likely stemmed from challenges in securing necessary stadium capacities and government endorsements for a standalone bid.[55] Bolivia expressed interest in March 2019 but similarly dropped out by September 2019, prior to the registration of detailed proposals.[51][54] Key obstacles included the high-altitude locations of primary venues, which posed health and acclimatization risks for international teams, compounded by economic limitations on infrastructure upgrades.[55] South Africa's association conveyed interest in March 2019 and advanced further in preparations but opted not to submit a bid on December 12, 2019, one day before the deadline.[51][56] The decision reflected inadequate assurances on funding and venue readiness beyond existing facilities from the 2010 men's World Cup.[57] The Korea Football Association expressed interest in a potential joint bid with North Korea in March 2019, aiming to leverage inter-Korean cooperation, but abandoned the effort by December 13, 2019.[51][58] Negotiations with FIFA failed to resolve conflicts between required organizing committee structures and South Korean legal frameworks, preventing government approval; geopolitical tensions further complicated any unified Korean approach.[59][60]

Host Election

Voting Procedure and FIFA Council Dynamics

The FIFA Council, a 37-member governing body comprising the presidentâ€”who chairs proceedings but generally abstains from votingâ€”and delegates apportioned across the six confederations based on association numbers (UEFA: 9 seats, CAF and AFC: 7 each, CONMEBOL and CONCACAF: 5 each, OFC: 2), selected the host through a secret ballot process.[61][62] On 25 June 2020, during an online meeting prompted by the COVID-19 pandemic, the Council voted between the remaining bids of a joint Australiaâ€“New Zealand submission and Colombia's standalone proposal, after earlier withdrawals by Brazil and Japan reduced the field to a binary choice.[23][63] The procedure required a simple majority for selection, with FIFA committing to enhanced transparency by publicly disclosing aggregate ballot outcomes on its website, though individual votes stayed confidential to preserve deliberation integrity.[1][20] This yielded 35 valid votes, announced as 22 for Australiaâ€“New Zealand and 13 for Colombia, but obscured granular insights into preferences or pre-vote negotiations, which FIFA did not document publicly.[23] Council members affiliated with the bidding associationsâ€”Australia (via AFC), New Zealand (via OFC), and Colombia (via CONMEBOL)â€”recused to address conflict-of-interest concerns, narrowing participation accordingly.[64] Confederation-based dynamics influenced alignments, with regional solidarity evident: AFC and OFC delegates, tied to the joint bid's proponents, provided core backing, while CONMEBOL's bloc prioritized Colombia; however, deviations like UEFA's collective endorsement of the lower-scoring South American optionâ€”despite the evaluation report favoring Australiaâ€“New Zealandâ€”signaled strategic maneuvering, possibly weighing future hosting rotations or geopolitical factors over pure technical viability, though such motives remain inferential absent disclosed deliberations.[65][25] The result empirically tracked the bids' assessed strengths, underscoring resilience in merit-based outcomes amid bloc pressures and procedural opacity.[23]

Breakdown of Votes by FIFA Council Member

The FIFA Council vote for the 2023 Women's World Cup host was held via secret ballot on June 25, 2020, resulting in 22 votes for the joint Australia-New Zealand bid and 13 for Colombia, from 35 valid votes cast among the 37-member council.[23] Individual member votes were not publicly released by FIFA, but subsequent disclosures, statements, and analyses from confederation officials revealed clear patterns of bloc voting, particularly along continental lines, with limited cross-confederation shifts.[52] UEFA's nine council representatives unanimously supported Colombia, marking a complete bloc against the Australia-New Zealand bid despite FIFA's technical evaluation favoring the Oceania-Asia joint proposal.[69][70] This European alignment contributed significantly to Colombia's total, though it fell short of overcoming the broader coalition for the winning bid. In contrast, the Asian Football Confederation (AFC) provided unified backing for Australia-New Zealand, leveraging Australia's AFC membership and the bid's projected commercial viability in the region. The Oceania Football Confederation (OFC), with New Zealand as co-host, similarly aligned fully with the joint bid.[71] CONMEBOL exhibited partial bloc cohesion for Colombia, with four of its six representatives reportedly voting for the South American bid, reflecting regional solidarity but not unanimity.[72] The Confederation of North, Central America and Caribbean Association Football (CONCACAF) showed splits, adhering to its pre-vote neutrality declaration; notably, United States Soccer Federation president Carlos Cordeiro voted for Australia-New Zealand.[52] The Confederation of African Football (CAF) votes were divided, serving as a key swing factor without full disclosure of individual preferences.
