Elizabeth Helgelien

![Elizabeth Halseth in 2010](./assets/Elizabeth_Halseth_2010_croppedcroppedcropped Elizabeth Nicole Helgelien (nÃ©e Schworak; formerly Halseth; born February 5, 1983) is an American conservative politician and real estate agent who served as a Republican in the Nevada State Senate for District 9 from November 2010 to February 2012.[1] At age 27 upon election, she was the youngest woman ever elected to the Nevada Legislature.[2] Her brief legislative career focused on business ownership perspectives but concluded with resignation amid a contentious divorce, child custody disputes, and challenges balancing single motherhood with public service. Post-Senate, Helgelien pursued real estate in Las Vegas alongside her husband Tiger Helgelien.[3] In 2021, her ex-husband Daniel Halseth was fatally stabbed by their 16-year-old daughter Sierra Halseth and her boyfriend Aaron Guerrero, an event stemming from ongoing family custody conflicts that had previously awarded primary custody to Helgelien.[4][2] She mounted an unsuccessful bid for the Republican nomination in Nevada's 3rd Congressional District in 2024, emphasizing Christian conservative values.[5][6]

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Elizabeth Helgelien, born Elizabeth Nicole Schworak, entered the world in 1983 in Portland, Oregon.[1] She grew up in Salem, Oregon, in a household headed by her mother alongside four siblings.[7] This family structure reflected a single-parent environment, with no public records indicating paternal involvement during her formative years.[7] Helgelien completed her secondary education at Salem High School, graduating in 2001.[7] Limited details exist on her immediate family's professional or socioeconomic background, though her maternal grandparents had relocated from New York to Las Vegas in 1940, where her grandfather worked as a saxophone player in big bands.[7]

Academic background

Elizabeth Helgelien, born Elizabeth Nicole Schworak, graduated from North Salem High School in Salem, Oregon.[8] She subsequently attended Corban University in Salem, Oregon, enrolling in its adult-degree completion program, which offered flexible online and limited on-campus options.[1][9] Helgelien completed three semesters of coursework there without declaring a major and did not earn a bachelor's degree.[9] In early 2011, amid scrutiny during her tenure as a Nevada state senator, Helgelien addressed reports questioning her educational claims, clarifying that she had previously described herself inaccurately as holding a bachelor's degree in psychology and taking a break from an MBA program.[9] She attributed the discrepancy to a lack of clarity in stating that any break occurred during undergraduate studies rather than graduate work, and Corban's registrar confirmed her enrollment but no degree completion.[9] Helgelien was a lifetime member of Psi Chi, the International Honor Society in Psychology, indicating involvement in psychology-related coursework during her time at Corban.

Political career

2010 Nevada State Senate election

Elizabeth Halseth, then a 27-year-old small business owner and political newcomer, ran as a Republican for the open Nevada State Senate District 9 seat in Clark County during the 2010 elections. The incumbent Democrat, Allison Copening, announced in May 2010 that she would not seek re-election, creating an opportunity in a competitive suburban Las Vegas district with roughly even partisan voter registration.[10] Halseth secured the Republican nomination in the August 10 primary, facing limited opposition as challengers withdrew or underperformed. In the November 2 general election, Halseth defeated Democratic nominee Benny Yerushalmi, a real estate investor and community activist. She received 41,658 votes (49.96 percent), while Yerushalmi garnered 37,152 votes (44.55 percent), with the remainder going to minor candidates including independent John Wagner and Libertarian Joseph Brown.[11] The narrow margin of victoryâ€”approximately 4,500 votesâ€”reflected the district's battleground status amid Nevada's Republican wave in 2010, driven by anti-incumbent sentiment and economic concerns following the Great Recession. Halseth's campaign emphasized conservative priorities such as limited government, tax cuts, and Second Amendment rights, while criticizing Yerushalmi's support for higher taxes and regulatory expansion.[12] A key campaign tactic involved highlighting Yerushalmi's past business associations and a mailer featuring his photograph alongside ostrich eggs, tying into accusations of unconventional investments that opponents portrayed as risky for taxpayers.[13] Despite being outspent in advertising by Democratic-aligned groups, Halseth benefited from grassroots support and national GOP momentum, becoming the youngest woman ever elected to the Nevada Legislature.[9] She was sworn in on November 3, 2010, assuming office for a four-year term.[14]

Legislative tenure

Elizabeth Halseth assumed office in the Nevada State Senate representing District 9 in Clark County following her election on November 2, 2010.[14] Her legislative service covered the 76th Session in 2011, during which she participated as a Republican member in a chamber with a slim GOP majority amid efforts to resolve a significant state budget deficit through expenditure reductions and structural reforms.[1] Halseth served on the Senate committees on Commerce, Labor, and Energy; Transportation; and Judiciary, where she engaged in deliberations on relevant legislation.[15] [16] [17] She also contributed to conference committees, including one reconciling differences on Assembly Bill 277 concerning public administrator provisions.[18] A notable initiative was her sponsorship of Senate Bill 356, introduced to establish the state crime of "stolen valor" by prohibiting false representations of military service or decorations for tangible benefits, with penalties including misdemeanor or felony charges depending on the gain obtained.[19] [20] During Assembly Judiciary Committee testimony, Halseth emphasized the bill's alignment with federal precedents and its importance to veterans, becoming emotional while defending its provisions against concerns over free speech implications.[19] The measure advanced from Senate Judiciary but did not ultimately become law.[17]

Resignation from the Senate

Elizabeth Halseth, then known by her married name, resigned from the Nevada State Senate on February 17, 2012, after serving approximately 15 months in office representing District 12.[21][22] Her tenure had been marked by irregular attendance at legislative sessions, prompting questions from colleagues and media about her residency in the district and commitment to her duties, as she had relocated to Reno amid ongoing personal matters.[23][21] In her resignation letter, Halseth cited the challenges of single motherhood following her divorce from husband Daniel Halseth, as well as difficulties in replacing her former chief of staff, as primary reasons for stepping down.[21][22] The divorce proceedings, which involved contentious child custody disputes over their two young children, had become public and acrimonious, with court filings revealing mutual allegations of infidelity and personal misconduct.[23][22] These issues contributed to her absences, including missing key committee meetings and floor sessions during the 2011 legislative term, which drew criticism from fellow Republicans who questioned her effectiveness as a lawmaker.[23] The resignation created a vacancy in a Republican-held seat, leading to a special election won by Democrat Justin Jones in April 2012.[22] Halseth's departure was viewed by some political observers as politically damaging to the Nevada GOP, given her election as part of the party's 2010 midterm gains, but it allowed her to prioritize family amid the unresolved custody battle, which continued through Nevada courts for years.[23][24]

2018 Nevada State Senate campaign

In August 2017, Elizabeth Halseth (now Helgelien), a former Republican state senator who had resigned from District 9 in 2012 amid a contentious divorce, announced her candidacy for the open Nevada State Senate District 8 seat in the 2018 election.[23][25] The district, encompassing parts of Las Vegas suburbs including Summerlin and parts of the northwest valley, was vacated by Independent Senator Patricia Farley, who opted not to seek re-election amid a Republican-backed recall effort that did not succeed.[23] Halseth cited her return to Las Vegas after a period in Alaska, improved personal stability, and encouragement from her children as factors enabling her to "finish what I started," emphasizing priorities such as education reform as a mother of three, economic diversification beyond tourism and gaming, and addressing women's issues without pursuing partisan agendas.[23] Halseth entered the Republican primary as one of several candidates challenging for the nomination in the June 12, 2018, primary election.[26] Her platform focused on conservative principles including limited government intervention and local representation, drawing on her prior legislative experience despite the brevity of her 2010-2012 tenure.[23] In the Republican primary, Halseth received 1,365 votes, or 10.3% of the total, placing behind Valerie Weber and Dan Rodimer, who advanced to the general election.[27] She did not proceed to the November 6 general election, where Democrat Marilyn Dondero Loop defeated Weber by securing 51.63% of the vote to Weber's 48.37%.[28] The loss marked the end of her 2018 bid, with District 8 flipping to Democratic control.[26]

2024 U.S. House campaign

In March 2023, Elizabeth Helgelien announced her bid for the Republican nomination in Nevada's 3rd congressional district, targeting Democratic incumbent Susie Lee.[5] Helgelien emphasized restoring strong leadership, criticizing federal policies on inflation, border security, and crime, while pledging to prioritize free enterprise by cutting regulations and taxes to foster economic growth.[5][29] Her platform focused on completing the southern border wall to curb illegal immigration and fentanyl trafficking, enforcing immigration laws, increasing police funding, and holding prosecutors accountable for leniency toward criminals.[29] She also committed to congressional term limits, signing a pledge for a constitutional amendment to impose them.[30] Helgelien secured endorsements from conservative groups, including Legacy PAC, which highlighted her prior 100% rating from the American Conservative Union and A-rating from the NRA, and Veterans for America First.[31][32] The campaign drew criticism from Helgelien's former mother-in-law, Christine Halseth, who in a March 2024 op-ed described her as unfit for office, citing personal and familial disputes.[33] Federal Election Commission records show Helgelien's campaign raised $243,660 from January 2023 to March 2024.[34] Helgelien lost the June 11, 2024, Republican primary to Drew Johnson, who advanced to face Lee in the general election.[6][35]

Professional career outside politics

Real estate and business activities

Helgelien has worked as a licensed real estate salesperson in Nevada since obtaining her license (S.0177370) through the Las Vegas REALTORSÂ® association, specializing in the Las Vegas metropolitan area.[36] She previously held a real estate license in Anchorage, Alaska, indicating early professional experience in property sales outside Nevada.[37][38] In her current role with Simply Vegas Real Estate, Helgelien focuses on residential transactions, including listings and sales in neighborhoods such as those around 3042 S Durango Drive in Las Vegas.[39] She co-operates Tiger & Elizabeth Helgelien, a real estate team with her husband, Tiger Helgelien, targeting buyers and sellers in the greater Las Vegas region, with reported activity in both high-value and entry-level home sales.[3][40] The team's efforts emphasize local market expertise, as evidenced by their tracking of recent sales data for properties ranging from the most expensive to the least expensive homes sold in the area over short periods.[41] No other business ventures beyond real estate brokerage are documented in public professional profiles.[42]

Political positions and ideology

Conservative principles and policy stances

Helgelien adheres to core conservative principles of limited government, fiscal conservatism, and individual responsibility, as evidenced by her leadership of a caucus modeled after the federal Freedom Caucus during her Nevada State Senate tenure.[43] She earned a 100% conservative voting score from the American Conservative Union for her legislative record, reflecting consistent alignment with policies favoring reduced government intervention.[43] Helgelien has also received recognition as "Conservative of the Year" from Citizens Outreach for prioritizing taxpayer interests over special-interest lobbying.[43] On economic matters, she advocates free-market policies, including cutting regulatory burdens, lowering taxes, and streamlining business operations to position the United States as the most entrepreneur-friendly nation.[8] In the Nevada Senate, where she served on the Revenue Committee, Helgelien opposed all proposed tax increases, arguing they harm working families and economic growth.[43] Her committee roles in Commerce, Labor, and Energy further emphasized deregulation to bolster job creation and energy independence.[43] Helgelien's immigration stance frames illegal entry as a criminal act and national security threat, calling for completion of the southern border wall and stricter enforcement to deter crossings and related crimes.[8] She criticizes federal policies under President Biden for enabling chaos at the border, linking them to increased fentanyl trafficking and human smuggling.[8] In criminal justice and public safety, Helgelien supports bolstering law enforcement funding, imposing tougher penalties on offenders, and ensuring accountability for judges who leniently handle violent crimes, while rejecting progressive reforms she views as undermining order.[8] Her national security positions oppose perceived weaknesses in Democratic leadership, citing the 2021 Afghanistan withdrawal, ongoing Ukraine conflict, Chinese espionage incidents, and border vulnerabilities as failures necessitating a more assertive U.S. posture.[8] Helgelien's opposition to liberal priorities, which earned her low rankings from left-leaning Nevada reporters and lobbyists, underscores her commitment to constituent-driven governance over establishment accommodations.[43]

Alignment with Republican factions

Helgelien has positioned herself within the populist, Trump-aligned wing of the Republican Party, particularly during her 2024 congressional campaign for Nevada's 3rd district. She has publicly identified as a "proud Trump Republican," emphasizing support for former President Donald Trump's policies on issues such as border security and economic nationalism.[44] This alignment is demonstrated through her endorsement by Veterans for America First (VFAF), a group explicitly branded as "Veterans for Trump," which praised her as a candidate committed to America First principles.[32] Additionally, Helgelien contributed to VFAF's promotional efforts, including filming segments for a documentary titled "VFAF Veterans for Trump - The Movement," underscoring her active involvement in Trump-centric Republican advocacy.[45] Her campaign rhetoric further reflects this factional orientation, with frequent invocations of Trump as the "Peace President" and calls to "take back our Country" in alignment with MAGA messaging.[46] [47] Endorsements from Nevada conservative figures, including former lawmakers and activists like Sam Peters and Danny Tarkanian, who share Trump-supportive stances, reinforce her ties to this grassroots-oriented segment of the GOP rather than establishment moderates.[48] This contrasts with her earlier 2012 endorsement of Mitt Romney in the Republican presidential primary, indicating a shift toward the more insurgent, anti-establishment faction dominant in the post-2016 party landscape.[49] Critics from within and outside the party, including family members of her former spouse, have labeled her a "MAGA candidate," though such characterizations often appear in adversarial contexts questioning her personal conduct rather than policy substance.[50] Helgelien's rejection of bipartisan compromises in favor of stringent conservative positionsâ€”such as opposing omnibus spending bills associated with GOP leadershipâ€”further distances her from the institutional Republican faction, prioritizing instead voter-driven populism.[51] Her campaign's focus on Nevada-specific issues like rejecting federal overreach aligns with the decentralized, state-sovereignty emphases common in Trump faction coalitions.[52]

Personal life and controversies

Marriages and family

Elizabeth Helgelien, nÃ©e Schworak, married Daniel Halseth in 2001.[1] The couple had three children: Dana, Jordan, and Sierra.[1] Halseth filed for divorce in 2011 amid allegations of domestic issues, including his arrest on charges of open and gross lewdness against Elizabeth.[53] The divorce was finalized in 2012, with Elizabeth awarded primary custody of the children.[54] Following the divorce, Elizabeth married Tiger Helgelien, a real estate professional.[55] The couple relocated briefly to Alaska before returning to Nevada in 2018, where they co-own and operate a real estate firm specializing in Las Vegas-area properties.[55][3]

Family-related legal incidents

In October 2011, Daniel Halseth, husband of then-state Senator Elizabeth Halseth (later Helgelien), was arrested following a domestic incident at their home, initially charged with open and gross lewdness after Elizabeth reported he had touched her against her will out of jealousy; police responded to a welfare check prompted by her mother.[53] Daniel Halseth filed for divorce shortly thereafter, citing an 11-year marriage and three children.[53] In August 2012, he was indicted on two felony counts of coercion and battery plus one misdemeanor count of open and gross lewdness related to the incident, but pleaded guilty in October 2012 to reduced misdemeanor charges of coercion and battery, receiving six months' probation, anger management classes, no contact with Elizabeth, and either a $975 fine or 97 hours of community service.[56] In March 2014, the Nevada Supreme Court upheld a district court order requiring Daniel Halseth to pay Elizabeth Halseth $2,274 monthly in child support, determining he was willfully underemployed based on his last known income and lack of evidence rebutting the presumption of ability to work.[24] Post-divorce, a prolonged custody dispute arose over their daughter Sierra Halseth, with Elizabeth initially granted primary custody; in 2020, Daniel sought primary custody, alleging a "toxic environment" at Elizabeth's home, leading to multiple hearings.[4] Key proceedings included an August 2020 order by Judge Charles Hoskin for Sierra's return to Elizabeth (which Daniel violated), a September 2020 hearing returning Sierra to Daniel, and a March 2021 hearing where Elizabeth raised concerns about Daniel's home and the judge contemplated sole custody for Elizabeth; the judge later mandated reunification therapy in October 2021 amid worries over Sierra's communication with her mother, her well-being, and potential parental influences.[4] On April 9, 2021, during the ongoing custody conflict, Sierra Halseth (aged 16) and her boyfriend Aaron Guerrero (aged 18) fatally stabbed and cut Daniel Halseth approximately 70 times in his Las Vegas home garage before attempting to burn the body and residence; Sierra alleged sexual and physical abuse by her father as a motive.[57] Both were indicted on murder charges, pleaded guilty, and on October 20, 2022, received life sentences with parole eligibility after 22 years plus $5,000 in restitution.[57]

Public criticisms and responses

In March 2024, Christine Halseth, the mother of Elizabeth Helgelien's late ex-husband Daniel Halseth, published an op-ed in The Nevada Globe titled "The Worst Congressional Candidate Ever?", accusing Helgelien of serial infidelity during their marriage, including affairs with lobbyists, staffers, and lawmakers while serving in the Nevada State Senate.[58] Halseth further alleged that Helgelien neglected her senatorial duties, missing committee meetings and abandoning constituents before resigning in February 2012 amid the ensuing divorce, which she claimed left Daniel to care for their three children alone in Las Vegas.[58][23] Halseth linked these personal failings to broader family dysfunction, prominently criticizing Helgelien for "raising a teenage daughter who committed a brutal murder," referring to their daughter Sierra Halseth, who in April 2021, along with her boyfriend Aaron Guerrero, stabbed Daniel over 70 times, dismembered his body, set it ablaze, and posted celebratory videos online.[58][59] Sierra was convicted and sentenced in October 2022 to life imprisonment with parole eligibility after 22 years, during which Helgelien expressed no remorse toward Daniel and blamed systemic failures rather than her daughter's actions, according to Halseth.[57][58] Additional claims included Helgelien fabricating educational credentialsâ€”pursuing an MBA without a bachelor's degreeâ€”and posing nearly nude for Maxim magazine shortly after her resignation.[58] Halseth demanded Helgelien withdraw from her 2024 U.S. House campaign in Nevada's 3rd District, deeming her unfit for public office.[58] Helgelien's campaign did not issue a direct public rebuttal to the op-ed but has countered family-related criticisms by highlighting Daniel's 2011 misdemeanor convictions for battery and open lewdness, as well as family court rulings granting her sole custody of the children due to documented abuse allegations against him.[55][60] Strategist Woodrow Johnson, speaking on her behalf, emphasized that Sierra bore personal responsibility for the murder despite prior claims of paternal abuse, which an 18-month investigation substantiated in custody proceedings but did not preclude the daughter's accountability.[60] Primary opponents, including Republican Drew Johnson, publicly endorsed Halseth's piece as validation of Helgelien's character flaws, though no formal ethics complaints or legal actions stemmed from the infidelity or divorce allegations.[61]
