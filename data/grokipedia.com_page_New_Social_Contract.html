New Social Contract

The New Social Contract (Dutch: Nieuw Sociaal Contract, abbreviated NSC) is a centrist political party in the Netherlands founded in August 2023 by Pieter Omtzigt, a veteran parliamentarian renowned for exposing government administrative failures such as the childcare benefits scandal.[1][2] The party seeks to renew the social contract by prioritizing rule of law, institutional trust, and pragmatic reforms to public services, drawing on Christian-democratic principles while critiquing bureaucratic overreach and political elitism.[3] In the November 2023 general election, NSC secured 20 seats in the 150-seat House of Representatives, establishing itself as a key player amid voter disillusionment with established parties.[3] This breakthrough enabled NSC's participation in the four-party coalition that formed the Schoof cabinet in July 2024, alongside the Party for Freedom (PVV), People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (VVD), and Farmer-Citizen Movement (BBB), with the party holding ministerial portfolios focused on justice, interior, and social affairs.[4][5] However, internal strains and leadership challenges soon emerged; Omtzigt resigned as party leader in April 2025 citing health impacts from burnout, followed by NSC ministers' withdrawal from the cabinet in August 2025 over policy disputes, including foreign affairs positions, contributing to governmental instability ahead of snap elections.[6][7] These developments marked a swift decline from NSC's initial promise, highlighting tensions between its reformist ideals and the pragmatics of coalition governance.[8]

History

Founding by Pieter Omtzigt in 2023

Pieter Omtzigt, a former member of parliament for the Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA) who had been operating as an independent since July 2021, founded the Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) party on August 19, 2023.[9] The establishment followed Omtzigt's growing disillusionment with established parties, particularly after his role in exposing the Dutch government's child benefits scandal (toeslagenaffaire), which highlighted systemic failures in administration and accountability.[1] Omtzigt positioned NSC as a response to these issues, drawing directly from his 2021 book Een nieuw sociaal contract, which argued for a fundamental overhaul of the social contract in the Netherlands to restore trust in institutions through better governance and rule of law.[10] The party's formation was announced publicly by Omtzigt on social media the following day, August 20, 2023, with the explicit goal of contesting the snap general election scheduled for November 22, 2023.[11] In initial statements, Omtzigt emphasized that NSC would prioritize realizing policy ideals over mere power acquisition, focusing on centrist principles of personalism, fiscal responsibility, and institutional reform rather than ideological extremes.[11] The party quickly organized its first members' meeting, where Omtzigt outlined the foundational commitment to transparency and anti-corruption measures, building on his investigative work in parliament.[12] NSC's rapid setup included recruiting initial candidates and members from disillusioned centrists, leveraging Omtzigt's personal popularityâ€”polls at the time showed him as one of the most trusted politicians in the Netherlands.[13] Unlike traditional parties, the founding avoided immediate coalition entanglements, with Omtzigt stressing a deliberate approach to candidate selection to ensure alignment with core values of integrity and competence.[14] This groundwork positioned NSC as a fresh alternative amid the political fragmentation following the collapse of the fourth Rutte cabinet in July 2023.[1]

Breakthrough in the 2023 General Election

The Dutch general election on 22 November 2023 was triggered by the resignation of Prime Minister Mark Rutte's fourth cabinet on 7 July 2023, primarily due to irreconcilable differences within the coalition over migration policy and budget allocations for asylum seeker reception.[15] New Social Contract (NSC), founded by Pieter Omtzigt on 5 August 2023 after his departure from the Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA), participated in its inaugural election and secured a breakthrough by winning 20 seats in the 150-seat House of Representatives.[16] The party received 1,254,945 votes, accounting for 12.85% of the valid votes cast, positioning it as the fourth-largest party behind the Party for Freedom (37 seats), GroenLinksâ€“Labour (25 seats), and People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (24 seats).[17][15] NSC's rapid ascent was facilitated by Omtzigt's established reputation as a fiscal expert and whistleblower in the childcare benefits scandal (toeslagenaffaire), where administrative failures led to wrongful accusations against thousands of parents, eroding public trust in government institutions.[18] Campaigning on themes of rule-of-law restoration, anti-corruption measures, and competent governance, the party appealed to centrist voters disillusioned with traditional parties amid a fragmented political landscape marked by 26 competing lists.[3] Voter turnout reached 76.9%, reflecting heightened engagement following the government's collapse.[17] The breakthrough underscored demand for Omtzigt's emphasis on systemic reforms, including better parliamentary oversight and depoliticized civil service operations, contrasting with the election's dominant narrative of immigration-driven polarization led by the Party for Freedom's victory.[19] Despite pre-election polls projecting NSC around 15-25 seats, the final tally validated its organizational efficiency, with Omtzigt topping the candidate list and securing a personal mandate through preferential voting.[16] This performance enabled NSC to play a pivotal role in subsequent coalition negotiations, highlighting its influence as a kingmaker in the 81-seat majority threshold.[18]

Participation in Coalition Government Formation (2023-2024)


Following the Dutch general election on November 22, 2023, in which New Social Contract (NSC) obtained 20 seats in the House of Representatives, the party participated in exploratory talks to form a coalition government with the Party for Freedom (PVV), People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (VVD), and Farmerâ€“Citizen Movement (BBB), which collectively held 88 seatsâ€”a majority.[20] These discussions, led by informateur Kim Putters starting in January 2024, focused on policy alignments, particularly NSC's emphasis on institutional reforms to enhance rule of law and prevent administrative scandals.[21] On February 6, 2024, NSC leader Pieter Omtzigt abruptly withdrew the party from negotiations, stating that his specific demands for structural changesâ€”such as establishing an independent committee to oversee government functioning and binding agreements on good governanceâ€”were not being adequately addressed by the other parties. [22] Omtzigt publicized internal negotiation notes to substantiate his claims, revealing resistance from PVV and VVD negotiators to incorporating these reforms into the coalition agreement, which he argued undermined the process's credibility.[21] This move stalled talks and prompted PVV leader Geert Wilders to explore alternative government configurations, including minority options.[23] Negotiations resumed after interventions by Putters and renewed commitments to address NSC's concerns. On March 7, 2024, the four parties agreed to advance to the detailed coalition agreement phase, with Omtzigt indicating progress on integrating governance safeguards.[20] This culminated in the Framework Coalition Agreement announced on May 16, 2024, which included provisions aligning with NSC priorities on administrative integrity and fiscal responsibility.[24] NSC continued involvement through cabinet formation, leading to the Schoof cabinet's inauguration on July 2, 2024, where the party secured key portfolios including the Ministry of Health, Welfare and Sport and the Ministry of Social Affairs and Employment.[25]

Internal Strains and Omtzigt's Resignation (2024-2025)

In September 2024, Pieter Omtzigt, founder and leader of Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC), temporarily stepped down from his leadership role due to health concerns following an intense year of political activity.[26] This marked the second significant health-related break in his career, the first occurring in 2021 amid earlier political pressures.[6] Omtzigt cited the cumulative strain of 21 years in national politics, including coalition negotiations and governance demands, as contributing factors to his burnout.[27] Omtzigt returned to parliamentary duties on a part-time basis in November 2024, expressing readiness to resume active involvement.[6] However, by April 18, 2025, he announced his permanent resignation from national politics and NSC leadership, stating that full recovery was impossible in the prevailing "toxic climate" and that politics had exacted too great a toll on his health.[6][28] He emphasized the government's failure to visibly address the Netherlands' core problems, such as administrative inefficiencies and public trust deficits, which amplified the personal and professional pressures.[29] The resignation highlighted emerging strains within NSC, as the party grappled with leadership uncertainty despite Omtzigt's endorsement of deputy Nicolien van Vroonhoven as successor.[6] Party members bid him farewell with applause on May 24, 2025, but expressed anxiety over the party's future direction and electoral prospects amid declining polls and coalition fragilities.[30] These internal tensions were compounded by broader challenges, including debates over policy implementation in the four-party coalition and the demands of maintaining NSC's rule-of-law focus in a polarized environment.[31] Omtzigt's departure, after NSC's breakthrough 20 seats in the 2023 election, underscored the vulnerabilities of a nascent party reliant on its founder's personal credibility.[6]

Withdrawal from Government and 2025 Political Crisis

On August 22, 2025, the New Social Contract (NSC) party withdrew its ministers from the Dutch cabinet amid irreconcilable differences over foreign policy toward Israel and the Gaza conflict.[32] [7] The immediate trigger was a cabinet deadlock on imposing sanctions against Israel, with NSC pushing for harsher measures in response to the ongoing situation in Gaza, a position opposed by remaining coalition partners.[33] [34] Foreign Minister Caspar Veldkamp, an NSC appointee, resigned on August 23, 2025, citing the failure to advance these sanctions as a breaking point.[33] [7] This was followed by the resignations of Deputy Prime Minister Mona Keijzer-van Hijum, Ministers Judith Uitermark, Corrine Bruins, and Marinus Jansen, along with four state secretaries, effectively removing NSC's representation from the Schoof cabinet.[32] [35] Prime Minister Dick Schoof, in a statement to King Willem-Alexander, expressed deep regret over the departures but respected the party's decision, noting it occurred after exhaustive cabinet discussions on August 22.[35] [34] The NSC exit compounded an already precarious political situation, following the June 3, 2025, collapse triggered by Party for Freedom (PVV) leader Geert Wilders withdrawing his party over unmet demands for stricter migration controls.[36] [37] Despite attempts to stabilize as a minority government, the NSC walkout reduced the coalition to just VVD and BBB remnants, rendering governance untenable and accelerating the slide into caretaker status under Schoof.[7] [38] This sequence of eventsâ€”exacerbated by NSC founder Pieter Omtzigt's April 2025 resignation from leadership due to health strainsâ€”highlighted deep ideological fractures within the right-leaning Schoof coalition, formed in July 2024 after prolonged negotiations post-2023 elections.[31] [6] The 2025 crisis prompted snap general elections scheduled for October 29, 2025, marking the third national vote in under five years and underscoring chronic instability in Dutch politics.[39] Public sentiment reflected widespread frustration, with a October 2025 survey indicating 59% of respondents believed the country was heading in the wrong direction, amid perceptions of governmental paralysis on key issues like migration, foreign policy, and rule-of-law reforms central to NSC's platform.[40] [41] The withdrawals eroded NSC's post-2023 momentum, contributing to its diminished polling ahead of the vote and raising questions about the viability of cross-ideological coalitions in addressing fiscal and administrative challenges.[8]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Emphasis on Rule of Law and Anti-Corruption

The New Social Contract (NSC) places significant emphasis on restoring trust in government institutions through robust enforcement of the rule of law, drawing from founder Pieter Omtzigt's investigations into administrative failures such as the toeslagenaffaire, a child benefits scandal involving wrongful accusations and financial ruin for thousands of families between 2005 and 2019.[1] This episode, which Omtzigt helped expose, highlighted systemic issues in bureaucratic accountability and abuse of power, prompting the party's manifesto to advocate for a renewed social contract that prioritizes institutional integrity over political expediency.[42] In its 2023 election program, NSC outlined the highest ambitions among Dutch parties for anti-corruption measures, according to an analysis by Transparency International Netherlands, including proposals for enhanced screening and training in public roles to prevent fraud and conflicts of interest.[43] The party calls for stricter enforcement of post-office employment rules for officials and the establishment of a public lobby register for ministers to increase transparency in decision-making processes.[44] Additionally, NSC advocates implementing recommendations from the Group of States against Corruption (GRECO) into Dutch law to address deficiencies in political financing and lobbying oversight.[44] To bolster judicial independence, NSC proposes splitting the advisory and judicial functions of the Council of State (Raad van State) and requiring a two-thirds parliamentary majority for any alterations to judicial budgets or structures.[44] The party supports creating a Constitutional Court to safeguard fundamental rights against legislative overreach and invests in reducing judicial backlogs through targeted funding for the court system.[44] Accountability mechanisms include personal liability for officials involved in criminal acts under their authority, as reinforced by precedents like the Pikmeer-arrests, and a "citizen loop" provision in administrative law allowing individuals to seek corrections for government errors.[44] NSC also prioritizes transparency by legally entrenching Article 68 of the Dutch Constitution to ensure timely and complete disclosure of government information to Parliament and citizens.[44] Proposals extend to open-source standards for government algorithms, with mandatory periodic reviews to mitigate biases or discriminatory outcomes, and VOG (Certificate of Conduct) screenings for high-risk public positions.[44] Party figures like Judith Uitermark have underscored that the rule of law transcends ideological divides, requiring vigilant protection to enable citizens to enforce their rights effectively.[45] These positions reflect NSC's broader commitment to countering elite capture and restoring public confidence, as evidenced by Omtzigt's participation in international forums on democratic erosion and corruption.[46]

Fiscal Conservatism and Economic Reforms

The New Social Contract (NSC) prioritizes fiscal discipline by advocating for orderly management of public finances, emphasizing solid, transparent, and realistic budgeting to prevent waste and ensure long-term sustainability.[47] This approach stems from founder Pieter Omtzigt's longstanding scrutiny of government expenditure, including his parliamentary work on public spending oversight since 2017. Party documents stress restoring public trust through efficient resource allocation rather than expansive deficits, aligning with Dutch traditions of trend-based fiscal policy that ties revenues to economic cycles while maintaining expenditure frameworks.[48] Economic reforms under NSC focus on tax simplification and incentives for workforce participation. The party proposes streamlining the income system to lower taxes on labor, thereby boosting economic independence and reducing administrative burdens on citizens and businesses.[49] Specific measures include tax cuts on natural gas consumption to support household security without broad deregulation.[50] While raising the statutory minimum wage to enhance basic living standards, NSC avoids abolishing mortgage interest deductions, prioritizing targeted relief over wholesale elimination.[51] In labor market reforms, NSC endorses recommendations from the 2019 Borstlap Commission, aiming to curb false self-employment and platform economy abuses by strengthening worker protections and employer responsibilities, while preserving flexibility for genuine independent contractors.[52] These changes seek causal improvements in income stability without stifling entrepreneurship, informed by empirical data on rising precarious work since the 2000s. During 2024 coalition talks with PVV, VVD, and BBB, NSC pushed for balanced budgets amid planned defense and infrastructure investments, though internal disagreements over implementation contributed to its 2025 withdrawal.[53] Overall, these positions reflect a pragmatic conservatism: restraining unchecked growth in outlays while reforming structures for verifiable efficiency gains.

Social Policies and Welfare State Adjustments

The New Social Contract (NSC) proposes reforms to the Dutch welfare state centered on enhancing bestaanszekerheid (existential security) through simplification, reciprocity, and incentives for labor market participation, arguing that the current system fosters dependency and administrative failures. The party's 2023 election program emphasizes a bilateral social contract where citizens actively contributeâ€”such as through work or self-relianceâ€”while the state ensures basic adequacy, drawing from Pieter Omtzigt's exposure of scandals like the child benefits affair that eroded trust in benefit administration.[54][55] Key adjustments include tying unemployment benefits (WW), state pension (AOW), and other social security payments to the minimum wage, with a mandated review in 2024 under EU Directive 2022/2041 to assess adequacy against poverty thresholds. Disability benefits would undergo sufficiency checks aligned with International Labour Organization standards, alongside introducing a universal public disability insurance scheme as recommended by the 2020 parliamentary commission to cover all workers and reduce gaps in protection. The party advocates halving the number of households with problematic debts by 2030 via stricter credit regulations and debt collection oversight, while proposing social tariffs for essentials like the first 1,000 cubic meters of natural gas annually to combat energy poverty.[54][54] To promote activation, NSC supports employer subsidies, no-risk policies, and job coaching for hiring long-term unemployed or partially disabled individuals, expanding sheltered employment options for those distant from the labor market. Childcare funding would shift to institution-based models with income-dependent contributions managed via the DUO education agency, aiming to eliminate recovery disputes that burden families. The 2025 program builds on this by pledging a minimum wage increase to â‚¬18 per hour and comprehensive simplification of the benefits and tax systems to minimize errors and administrative burdens, as seen in UWV reassessments affecting tens of thousands.[54][54][56] These policies reflect fiscal conservatism, prioritizing sustainable funding over expansion, with proposals like a personal development budget for worker retraining funded by reallocating severance pay equivalents, and adherence to the Borstlap Commission's labor market recommendations for better false self-employment protections without undermining flexibility. Critics from left-leaning outlets argue such activation measures risk cutting benefits for vulnerable groups, though NSC counters that un reformed systems perpetuate poverty traps, citing empirical data on rising disability claims amid labor shortages.[57][52][58]

Foreign Policy Priorities

The Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) advocates a realistic foreign policy centered on advancing Dutch national interests, bolstering European security, and upholding international rule of law, while emphasizing conflict prevention over ideological commitments.[59] This approach prioritizes increased defense expenditures in response to global instability, such as the Russian invasion of Ukraine, and seeks to link foreign aid to stability efforts that mitigate migration pressures on Europe.[59] In European security matters, NSC commits to sustained military and humanitarian support for Ukraine against Russian aggression, viewing it as essential for preserving continental stability.[59] [60] The party supports NATO's collective defense framework, including a legally binding commitment to allocate at least 2% of GDP to defense spending, and calls for enhanced European cooperation on defense without pursuing deeper political integration.[61] Regarding the European Union, NSC favors collaboration on geopolitical issues like mediation and conflict preventionâ€”proposing an EU-wide institute for resolution effortsâ€”but opposes "ever closer union" and limits EU involvement in domestic areas such as social security.[59] [14] On the Middle East, NSC endorses a two-state solution to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, advocating a secure Israel alongside an independent Palestinian state excluding Hamas, while opposing illegal West Bank settlements and urging an end to Gaza's humanitarian crisis.[59] This stance manifested in August 2025, when NSC's Foreign Minister Caspar Veldkamp resignedâ€”prompting the party's full withdrawal from the coalition governmentâ€”after failing to secure cabinet approval for additional sanctions against Israel over its Gaza operations.[33] [62] Broader priorities include reinforcing the international legal order through support for the International Criminal Court, pursuing justice for MH17 victims and ISIS atrocities via specialized prosecutors, and directing development cooperation toward stabilizing conflict zones to curb irregular migration.[59] NSC also emphasizes consular protections for the over one million Dutch citizens abroad and bilateral ties, such as expanded energy and visa cooperation with Suriname.[59]

Leadership and Organization

Evolution of Party Leadership

Pieter Omtzigt founded the Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) party on August 20, 2023, assuming the role of party leader following his departure from the Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA) amid frustrations with internal party dynamics and a desire to prioritize rule-of-law reforms.[13] Under his leadership, NSC achieved a breakthrough in the November 22, 2023, general election, securing 20 seats in the House of Representatives, positioning the party as a key player in subsequent coalition negotiations.[18] Omtzigt's tenure involved navigating complex government formation talks, including a temporary withdrawal from negotiations in February 2024 after expressing shock at the state of public finances.[63] Omtzigt resigned as party leader and from national politics on April 18, 2025, citing the severe toll on his health from a "toxic climate" and burnout, as well as frustration that the government was not visibly addressing the Netherlands' core problems such as housing shortages and administrative inefficiencies.[6] [29] His departure marked a significant transition for NSC, which had entered a coalition government in 2024 but faced mounting internal pressures from governance challenges. Nicolien van Vroonhoven, previously the party's second candidate, succeeded him as interim leader starting April 19, 2025, emphasizing a stronger defense of party ideals amid ongoing coalition strains.[64] Van Vroonhoven's leadership proved short-lived; on September 1, 2025, she quit politics after party members voted to demote her from second to a lower position on the candidate list for upcoming elections, reflecting internal dissatisfaction with her performance.[65] [66] The NSC executive committee then nominated Eddy van Hijum, the Minister of Social Affairs, as the new leader in June 2025, with his sole rival withdrawing from the race, leading to his confirmation as party leader on September 2, 2025.[67] [68] Van Hijum's ascension occurred against the backdrop of NSC's withdrawal from the coalition government on August 22, 2025, over disagreements on foreign policy, particularly sanctions related to Israel-Gaza issues.[32] This series of rapid leadership changes underscored the party's struggles with stability following its electoral success.

Key Internal Figures and Structures

Pieter Omtzigt founded the Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) on August 19, 2023, after leaving the Christian Democratic Appeal (CDA), and initially served as its leader and primary ideological driver.[12][9] Omtzigt, a former Member of Parliament known for his focus on rule-of-law issues, shaped the party's emphasis on administrative integrity and citizen trust in government until his resignation from national politics on April 18, 2025, due to prolonged burnout after 21 years in office.[27] Eddy van Hijum emerged as the key successor figure, endorsed by the NSC executive board (partijbestuur) in June 2025 following Omtzigt's departure and a brief leadership contest where rival Diederik Boomsma withdrew.[67][69] Van Hijum, previously a CDA parliamentarian and NSC minister for Social Affairs and Employment, assumed the role of party leader and lead candidate (lijsttrekker) for the October 29, 2025, general elections, prioritizing restoration of government reliability.[70][71] The party's internal structure includes a national board responsible for strategic decisions and nominations, regional coordinators for local organization, and a parliamentary group in the House of Representatives that handles legislative activities.[72] This framework, established post-founding, has faced criticism from regional leaders for operational inefficiencies as of late 2024, though it supported the leadership transition in 2025.[72] Membership-driven with a focus on active citizen involvement, NSC operates without a formal youth or women's wing publicly detailed in organizational charts as of 2025.[73]

NSC's performance exceeded pre-election polling expectations for a newly formed party, drawing on founder Pieter Omtzigt's prior independent parliamentary experience and public profile in child benefits scandal inquiries. Voter turnout was 76.9%, up from 77.9% in 2021, amid debates on housing, immigration, and governance accountability.[15]

Performance in European Parliament Elections

In the 2024 European Parliament elections held on 6 June, the New Social Contract (NSC) participated for the first time since its founding, contesting the 31 seats allocated to the Netherlands under a proportional representation system.[74] The party secured 3.75% of the valid votes cast, translating to one seat.[74] This result positioned NSC behind major parties such as the combined GroenLinksâ€“PvdA alliance (21.09%, 8 seats), the Party for Freedom (16.97%, 6 seats), and the People's Party for Freedom and Democracy (11.35%, 4 seats), but ahead of smaller parties like the Reformed Political Party (3.66%, 1 seat).[74] The elected representative, Dirk Gotink, affiliated with the centre-right European People's Party (EPP) group in the Parliament.[74] Voter turnout in the Netherlands was 45.72%, with NSC's performance reflecting its emerging status as a centrist conservative alternative amid a fragmented political landscape.[74] Prior to 2024, NSC had no record in European elections due to its recent establishment.[74]

Voter Base and Demographic Analysis

The voter base of Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) emerged prominently in the 2023 Dutch general election, where the party secured 20 seats with approximately 1.3 million votes, representing 15.3% of the electorate.[17] This support drew from a diverse array of previous voters, including substantial shares from centrist and center-left parties such as VVD, D66, PvdA, SP, and ChristenUnie, alongside smaller contingents from right-wing groups like BBB and PVV.[75] Such breadth highlighted NSC's appeal as a reformist alternative critiquing entrenched governance failures, particularly administrative mismanagement exemplified by the childcare benefits scandal.[75] Demographic profiles indicated a relatively older supporter base, with polling data showing stronger resonance among voters over 50, who prioritized issues like poverty reduction, housing affordability, and regional development over migration concerns.[76] Ideologically, NSC voters leaned economically conservative yet emphasized social security and ethical governance, associating the party with values of reliability, normativity, and anti-corruption rather than populist or identity-based platforms.[75] Pieter Omtzigt's reputation for tenacity in parliamentary inquiries served as a primary draw, with many citing personal trust in his authenticity as a decisive factor.[75] By the 2024 European Parliament elections, NSC's share declined to 4.4% (two seats), signaling early erosion amid coalition negotiations and policy compromises.[74] Further attrition followed internal instability, culminating in Omtzigt's resignation on April 18, 2025, due to health impacts from political pressures.[6] Subsequent 2025 polls registered NSC at near-zero seats, with former adherents migrating back to CDA, VVD, or other centrists disillusioned by perceived inconsistencies in upholding reform pledges.[77] This contraction underscores the base's heavy reliance on Omtzigt's individual credibility, rendering sustained organizational appeal vulnerable to leadership voids.[6]

Controversies and Criticisms

Internal Party Instability and Leadership Transitions


Pieter Omtzigt, founder and initial leader of the New Social Contract (NSC), encountered health challenges including burnout shortly after the party's establishment in August 2023, leading to his temporary withdrawal from active political duties in mid-2024.[78] This absence exacerbated internal tensions, as the party struggled with organizational cohesion and policy implementation amid its rapid rise following the November 2023 general election, where it secured 20 seats.[79] Omtzigt's return in November 2024 coincided with significant instability, including the resignations of two NSC state secretaries and two Members of Parliament, prompting a high-tension party congress aimed at restoring unity.[80] Reports highlighted frequent emotional outbursts by Omtzigt as a catalyst for an internal investigation into his leadership style and the functioning of the parliamentary faction, conducted by the party board over the summer of 2024.[81][82] Omtzigt acknowledged understanding some grievances of the departing members, attributing unrest to broader challenges in maintaining party discipline.[83] On April 18, 2025, Omtzigt announced his permanent exit from politics, stating that recovery from burnout had not succeeded, prioritizing his health and family.[84] This departure triggered a contentious succession process, with a secret party meeting in June 2025 descending into disarray over potential leaders, including Nicolien van Vroonhoven who ultimately declined the role, risking a party split.[85] Eddy van Hijum emerged as interim or subsequent leader, but internal democracy issues persisted, exemplified by the board's blockage of a fraud investigation reported by the treasurer, who was subsequently suspended in early 2025.[86] These events underscored systemic challenges in leadership continuity and internal governance within the nascent party.

Disputes Over Foreign Policy Positions

In August 2025, ministers from the New Social Contract (NSC) resigned from the Dutch caretaker government amid irreconcilable differences over proposed additional measures against Israel in response to its military actions in Gaza. Foreign Minister Caspar Veldkamp, an NSC member and former Dutch ambassador to Israel, stepped down on August 22 after the cabinet rejected his push for an import ban on goods from Israeli settlements and other sanctions, citing Israel's alleged violations of international humanitarian law.[87][62] Veldkamp stated his frustration stemmed from an inability to implement policies aligned with NSC's view that Israel's conduct necessitated stronger Dutch accountability measures.[33] The resignations escalated when all NSC cabinet members, including Social Affairs Minister Eddy van Hijum, withdrew from the coalition the following day, leaving only the VVD and BBB parties in the diminished government. Van Hijum argued that "the actions of the Israeli government are contrary to international agreements" and that the Netherlands must "draw a line" by pursuing targeted restrictions, reflecting NSC's emphasis on a "realistic foreign policy" prioritizing adherence to international norms and Dutch interests.[7][88] This stance clashed with VVD and BBB positions, which favored maintaining existing EU-aligned policies without unilateral escalation, amid broader coalition fragility following the PVV's July 2025 exit over asylum issues.[62][87] The episode highlighted internal NSC cohesion on foreign policy but drew criticism for contributing to governmental instability, with VVD leader Sophie Hermans labeling the walkout "irresponsible" and Prime Minister Dick Schoof expressing regret over the loss of NSC participation.[7] Proponents within NSC framed the move as principled defense of international law, while opponents argued it undermined Dutch foreign policy consistency and strained relations with Israel, a key trade partner.[89] The dispute preceded parliamentary votes rejecting a weapons purchase ban from Israel and Palestinian state recognition, further isolating NSC's position and accelerating calls for snap elections on October 29, 2025.[7][87]

Accusations of Policy Inconsistency and Governance Failures

Critics, particularly from left-leaning parties such as DENK, have accused Nieuw Sociaal Contract (NSC) of hypocrisy in its participation in the Schoof cabinet coalition with the Partij voor de Vrijheid (PVV), despite the party's founding emphasis on restoring trust in government through competent administration and rule-of-law adherence. DENK leader Stephan van Baarle described NSC's endorsement of the May 2024 hoofdlijnenakkoordâ€”outlining coalition priorities including stringent asylum restrictionsâ€”as a "whole big mistake" and "unbelievable, hypocritical," arguing it undermined NSC's proclaimed commitment to the rechtsstaat by proposing measures like maximum reductions in support for asylum seekers without adequate legal grounding.[90] This critique posits that NSC compromised its principles for power, aligning with PVV policies NSC leader Pieter Omtzigt had previously distanced himself from during opposition, including populist approaches to migration that risked constitutional tensions. The Schoof cabinet's collapse on June 3, 2025, after less than 11 months in office, fueled further allegations of governance failure, with opponents attributing the instability to NSC's inability to enforce its reform agenda amid coalition frictions. The breakdown stemmed from irreconcilable disputes over migration policy, where PVV leader Geert Wilders issued an ultimatum demanding stricter measures, leading Prime Minister Dick Schoof to resign; NSC's centrist stance on balanced governance was blamed by some for exacerbating gridlock rather than resolving it, contradicting the party's 2023 manifesto promises of "good administration" to prevent scandals like the prior childcare benefits affair Omtzigt had exposed.[91] PVV figures reciprocated by labeling NSC the "Nederlandse Sabotage Club," accusing it of undermining the coalition through rigid adherence to proceduralism over pragmatic delivery.[92] Subsequent actions by NSC ministers amplified claims of policy inconsistency, particularly their collective resignation in August 2025 over Dutch policy toward the Gaza conflict, which critics portrayed as selective moral posturing timed for electoral advantage ahead of October 2025 elections. Opinion contributors in de Volkskrant argued the move exemplified "Haagse selectieve hypocrisie," noting NSC's prior silence on historical atrocities like Rwanda (1994) or Srebrenica (1995) while now prioritizing Gaza to appeal to voters, without demonstrating sustained governance reform during their tenure.[93] One commentator asserted, "Van goed bestuur is geen seconde sprake geweest," highlighting perceived abandonment of coalition responsibilities as opportunistic rather than principled. Internal NSC discontent also surfaced, with party members voicing open criticism in September 2025 over leadership's handling of compromises, marking the first public rift in its base.[94] These accusations, often from ideologically opposed sources like DENK (which advocates pro-immigration stances) and mainstream outlets with left-leaning editorial slants, reflect partisan framing but underscore verifiable tensions between NSC's aspirational rhetoric on institutional integrity and the practical challenges of coalition governance in a fragmented parliament. NSC defenders counter that such compromises were necessary for influence on priorities like fiscal sustainability, though the cabinet's brevityâ€”failing to enact major reformsâ€”lent empirical weight to claims of unfulfilled promises.[95]
