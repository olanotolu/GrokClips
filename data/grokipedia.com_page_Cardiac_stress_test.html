Cardiac stress test

A cardiac stress test is a cardiological examination that evaluates the cardiovascular system's response to external stress within a controlled clinical setting. This stress response can be induced through physical exercise (usually a treadmill) or intravenous pharmacological stimulation of heart rate.[1] As the heart works progressively harder (stressed) it is monitored using an electrocardiogram (ECG) monitor. This measures the heart's electrical rhythms and broader electrophysiology. Pulse rate, blood pressure and symptoms such as chest discomfort or fatigue are simultaneously monitored by attending clinical staff. Clinical staff will question the patient throughout the procedure asking questions that relate to pain and perceived discomfort. Abnormalities in blood pressure, heart rate, ECG or worsening physical symptoms could be indicative of coronary artery disease.[2] Stress testing does not accurately diagnose all cases of coronary artery disease, and can often indicate that it exists in people who do not have the condition.[3] The test can also detect heart abnormalities such as arrhythmias,[4] and conditions affecting electrical conduction within the heart such as various types of fascicular blocks.[5] A "normal" stress test does not offer any substantial reassurance that a future unstable coronary plaque will not rupture and block an artery, inducing a heart attack. As with all medical diagnostic procedures, data is only from a moment in time. A primary reason stress testing is not perceived as a robust method of CAD detection â€” is that stress testing generally only detects arteries that are severely narrowed (~70% or more).[6]

Stress Testing and Echocardiography

Stress testing and echocardiography

A stress test may be accompanied by echocardiography.[7] The echocardiography is performed both at rest and during stress (via exercise or pharmacological agents) so that changes in heart wall motion can be compared. A resting echocardiogram is obtained prior to stress. The ultrasound images obtained are similar to the ones obtained during a full surface echocardiogram, commonly referred to as transthoracic echocardiogram. The patient is subjected to stress in the form of exercise or chemically (often dobutamine). After the target heart rate is achieved, 'stress' echocardiogram images are obtained. The two echocardiogram images are then compared to assess for any abnormalities in wall motion of the heart. This is used to detect obstructive coronary artery disease.[8]

Cardiopulmonary exercise stress testing

While also measuring respiratory gas exchange parameters (e.g., oxygen uptake [VOâ‚‚] and carbon dioxide output [VCOâ‚‚]) to determine maximal oxygen consumption, with oxygen saturation often monitored separately via pulse oximetry, the test is often referred to as a cardiopulmonary exercise test. Common indications for a cardiopulmonary exercise test include evaluation of shortness of breath, workup before heart transplantation, and prognosis and risk assessment of heart failure patients. The test is also common in sport science for measuring athletes' maximal oxygen consumption, VÌ‡Oâ‚‚ max. In 2016, the American Heart Association published an official scientific statement advocating that cardiorespiratory fitness, quantifiable as VÌ‡Oâ‚‚ max and measured during a cardiopulmonary exercise test, be categorized as a clinical vital sign and should be routinely assessed as part of clinical practice.[10] The CPX test can be done on a treadmill or cycle ergometer. In untrained subjects, VÌ‡Oâ‚‚ max is 10% to 20% lower when using a cycle ergometer compared with a treadmill.[11]

Stress testing using injected nuclear markers

A nuclear stress test uses a gamma camera to image radioisotopes injected into the bloodstream. The best known example is myocardial perfusion imaging. Typically, a radiotracer (Tc-99m sestamibi, Myoview or thallium-201 chloride) may be injected during the test. After a suitable waiting period to ensure proper distribution of the radiotracer, scans are acquired with a gamma camera to capture images of the blood flow. Scans acquired at rest and during stress (induced by exercise or pharmacological agents) are examined to assess myocardial perfusion and infer the state of the coronary arteries of the patient. By showing the relative amounts of radioisotope within the heart muscle, nuclear stress tests more accurately identify regional areas of reduced blood flow.[12] Stress and potential cardiac damage from exercise during the test is a problem in patients with ECG abnormalities at rest or in patients with severe motor disability. Pharmacological stimulation from vasodilators such as dipyridamole or adenosine, or positive chronotropic agents such as dobutamine can be used. Testing personnel can include a cardiac radiologist, a nuclear medicine physician, a nuclear medicine technologist, a cardiology technologist, a cardiologist, and/or a nurse. To reduce radiation exposure, stress-first protocols are often used, where stress imaging is performed first, and rest imaging only if needed. The typical effective dose of radiation received during this procedure ranges from 7 to 25 mSv for common technetium-99m protocols, and higher (up to 40 mSv or more) for thallium-201 based studies (as of 2024).[13]

Recommended utility of this procedure

According to the 2023 ACC/AHA Multimodality Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC), exercise ECG treadmill testing is appropriate (score 7) as an initial diagnostic test for symptomatic patients with intermediate pretest probability of chronic coronary disease, such as those aged â‰¥50 years with likely anginal symptoms and/or â‰¥2 cardiovascular risk factors including smoking, family history of coronary artery disease, hypertension, diabetes, and high cholesterol.[14] For asymptomatic patients with intermediate atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk (7.5%â€“20%), it may be appropriate (score 5). In 2013, the AHA's "Exercise Standards for Testing and Training" indicated that high frequency QRS analysis during ECG treadmill testing has useful performance for detection of coronary heart disease.
  • Perfusion stress test using myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI), such as with 99mTc-labeled sestamibi, is appropriate for select patients, including those unable to exercise adequately or with an abnormal resting electrocardiogram that limits interpretation of exercise ECG.[16][14]
  • Intracoronary ultrasound or angiogram can provide more definitive information on coronary anatomy but is invasive and carries risks associated with cardiac catheterization procedures.[17]

Stress Testing: Diagnostic Value

Diagnostic value

The common approach for stress testing recommended by the American College of Cardiology[18][19] and the American Heart Association[20] involves several methods to assess cardiac health. These methods provide information for diagnosing and managing heart-related conditions. Two primary stress tests utilized are a treadmill test using electrocardiogram (ECG)/electrophysiology metrics and nuclear testing, each with unique sensitivity and specificity values. The treadmill test, employing the modified Bruce protocol,[21] demonstrates a sensitivity of 60-70% and a specificity of 70-85% for detecting coronary artery disease. Sensitivity refers to the percentage of individuals with the condition correctly identified by the test, while specificity denotes the percentage of individuals without the condition correctly identified as not having it.[22] The nuclear stress test exhibits a sensitivity of 80-90% and a specificity of 75-90%.[23] To arrive at the patient's post-test likelihood of disease, the interpretation of the stress test result necessitates the integration of the patient's pretest likelihood with the test's sensitivity and specificity. This method, initially introduced by Diamond and Forrester in the 1970s, provides an estimate of the patient's post-test likelihood of disease.[24][25] Stress tests have limitations in assessing the significance and nature of cardiac problems; they are more effective for risk stratification than definitive diagnosis, particularly in patients with low pretest probability, and should be seen in contextâ€”as an initial assessment that can lead to a number of other diagnostic approaches in the broader management of cardiac diseases.[26] In many cases, the first manifestation of coronary artery disease is a sudden event such as a heart attack. Consequently, stress tests performed shortly before these events may not be highly relevant for predicting infarction in affected individuals.[27]

Contraindications and termination conditions

Stress cardiac imaging is not recommended for asymptomatic, low-risk patients as part of their routine care.[28] Some estimates as of 2012 show that such screening accounts for approximately 45% of cardiac stress imaging, and evidence does not show that this results in better outcomes for patients.[28] Unless high-risk markers are present, such as diabetes in patients aged over 40, peripheral arterial disease, or a risk of coronary heart disease greater than 2 percent yearly, most health societies do not recommend the test as a routine procedure.[28][29][30][31] Absolute contraindications to cardiac stress test include:
  • Acute myocardial infarction within 48 hours
  • Unstable angina not yet stabilized with medical therapy
  • Uncontrolled cardiac arrhythmia, which may have significant hemodynamic responses (e.g. ventricular tachycardia)
  • Severe symptomatic aortic stenosis, acute aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, and acute pericarditis
  • Decompensated or inadequately controlled congestive heart failure
  • Severe pulmonary hypertension
Relative contraindications include:
  • Known left main coronary stenosis or significant multivessel disease
  • Moderate to severe aortic stenosis of uncertain relation to symptoms
  • Resting hypertension (systolic blood pressure >200 mmHg or diastolic >110 mmHg)
  • Tachyarrhythmias or bradyarrhythmias associated with symptoms
  • Acquired advanced or complete heart block
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy and other forms of outflow tract obstruction
  • Acute systemic infection or other acute illnesses precluding safe testing
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Severe anemia
Indications for termination: A cardiac stress test should be terminated before completion under the following circumstances:[32][33] Absolute indications for termination include:
  • Systolic blood pressure decreases by more than 10 mmHg with increase in work rate, or drops below baseline in the same position, with other evidence of ischemia.
  • Increase in nervous system symptoms: Dizziness, ataxia or near syncope
  • Moderate to severe anginal pain (above 3 on standard 4-point scale[33])
  • Signs of poor perfusion, e.g. cyanosis or pallor[33]
  • Request of the test subject
  • Technical difficulties (e.g. difficulties in measuring blood pressure or ECG[33])
  • ST-segment elevation >1.0 mm in leads without preexisting Q waves (other than aVR, aVL, V1)
  • Sustained ventricular tachycardia
Relative indications for termination include:
  • Systolic blood pressure decreases by more than 10 mmHg with increase in work rate, or drops below baseline in the same position, without other evidence of ischemia.
  • ST or QRS segment changes, e.g. more than 2 mm horizontal or downsloping ST segment depression in non-Q wave leads, or marked axis shift
  • Arrhythmias other than sustained ventricular tachycardia e.g. Premature ventricular contractions, both multifocal or triplet; heart block; supraventricular tachycardia or bradyarrhythmias[33]
  • Intraventricular conduction delay or bundle branch block that cannot be distinguished from ventricular tachycardia
  • Increasing chest pain
  • Fatigue, shortness of breath, wheezing, claudication or leg cramps
  • Hypertensive response (systolic blood pressure > 250 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure > 115 mmHg)

Adverse effects

Side effects from cardiac stress testing are generally mild and transient, with serious complications being rare (occurring in approximately 1 in 5,000 tests). They may include:
  • Palpitations, chest pain, myocardial infarction (rare), shortness of breath, headache, nausea, or fatigue.
  • Adenosine and dipyridamole (pharmacological stress agents) can cause mild hypotension, flushing, or bronchospasm.
  • In nuclear stress tests, the ionizing radiation from radioactive tracers involves a small increase in lifetime cancer risk (approximately 0.05-0.1% per test), which may accumulate with frequent use.[34]

Use of pharmacological agents to stress the heart

Pharmacologic stress testing relies on coronary steal. Vasodilators are used to dilate coronary vessels, which causes increased blood velocity and flow rate in normal vessels and less of a response in stenotic vessels. This difference in response leads to a steal of flow and perfusion defects appear in cardiac nuclear scans or as ST-segment changes.[35] The choice of pharmacologic stress agents used in the test depends on factors such as potential drug interactions with other treatments and concomitant diseases. Pharmacologic agents such as adenosine, regadenoson (Lexiscan), or dipyridamole is generally used when a patient cannot achieve adequate work level with treadmill exercise, or has poorly controlled hypertension or left bundle branch block. However, an exercise stress test may provide more information about exercise tolerance than a pharmacologic stress test.[36] Commonly used agents include:
  • Vasodilators acting as adenosine receptor agonists, such as adenosine itself, and dipyridamole (Persantine),[37] which acts indirectly at the receptor.
  • Regadenoson (Lexiscan), which acts specifically at the adenosine A2A receptor, thus affecting the heart more than the lung.
  • Dobutamine â€“ The effects of beta-agonists such as dobutamine can be reversed by administering beta-blockers such as propranolol.
Regadenoson or dobutamine is often used in patients with severe reactive airway disease (asthma or COPD) as adenosine and dipyridamole can cause acute exacerbation of these conditions. If the patient's asthma is treated with an inhaler then it should be used as a pre-treatment prior to the injection of the pharmacologic stress agent. In addition, if the patient is actively wheezing then the physician should determine the benefits versus the risk to the patient of performing a stress test especially outside of a hospital setting. Caffeine is usually held 24 hours prior to an adenosine stress test, as it is a competitive antagonist of the A2A adenosine receptor and can attenuate the vasodilatory effects adenosine. Aminophylline may be used to attenuate severe and/or persistent adverse reactions to adenosine and regadenoson.[38]

Cardiac Stress Testing

History

Early precursors to modern cardiac stress testing include Arthur Master's two-step exercise test developed in 1929 for evaluating cardiac capacity.[39] The contemporary form, involving ECG-monitored treadmill exercise, was pioneered by Robert A. Bruce, who established guidelines in 1956 and published the multistage Bruce protocol in 1963.[40] Cardiac stress testing, used since the 1960s, has a history rooted in the diagnostic and prognostic assessment of patients with suspected coronary artery disease. It has evolved to evaluate inducible myocardial ischemia as an indicator of adverse outcomes. The factors influencing mortality risk have changed over time due to decreasing angina symptoms, increasing prevalence of conditions like diabetes and obesity, and the rise in pharmacologic testing for patients unable to exercise during stress tests.[41]
