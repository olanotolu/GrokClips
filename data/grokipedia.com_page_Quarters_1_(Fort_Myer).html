Quarters 1 (Fort Myer)

Quarters 1 at Fort Myer is a 21-room historic mansion constructed in 1899 on what is now Joint Base Myer-Henderson Hall in Arlington, Virginia.[1][2] Originally built as the post commander's residence, it has served continuously as the official quarters for the Chief of Staff of the United States Army since its first occupancy by Major General J. Franklin Bell in 1908.[3][1] The structure occupies a prominent position on "Generals' Row," a line of six large officers' residences flanking the west side of the former Fort Myer parade ground, now known as Whipple Field.[3] Completed on May 27, 1899, Quarters 1 exemplifies late-19th-century military architecture and has housed successive Army Chiefs of Staff, including luminaries such as George C. Marshall, Douglas MacArthur, and Dwight D. Eisenhower.[4][1] Its enduring role underscores Fort Myer's significance as a hub for senior military leadership and early aviation milestones, with the adjacent grounds hosting the Wright brothers' first military flights in 1908.[2] Designated a historic landmark, the residence reflects the U.S. Army's institutional continuity and the personal lives of its highest-ranking officers amid national security demands.[2][4]

Background and Location

Fort Myer Context

Fort Myer is situated in Arlington County, Virginia, approximately 38Â°52â€²49â€³N 77Â°04â€²47â€³W, adjacent to Arlington National Cemetery and overlooking the Potomac River and Washington, D.C.[5] Originally established as Fort Whipple in 1863 during the American Civil War, it formed part of the extensive network of Union fortifications encircling the national capital to repel Confederate advances.[6][5] The site, previously owned by descendants of Martha Washington, was selected for its elevated terrain providing strategic visibility and defensive advantages.[7] Following the Civil War, the fort transitioned from active defense to peacetime military functions, evolving into a key U.S. Army installation. Renamed Fort Myer in honor of Brigadier General Albert J. Myer, founder of the Army Signal Corps, it served as a hub for signal communications, including the erection of radio towers known as "The Three Sisters" that enabled the first transatlantic wireless transmission to Paris in 1915.[5][2] The post also became renowned as a showcase for Army cavalry units, hosting elaborate demonstrations and equestrian events that highlighted mounted military traditions into the early 20th century.[8] In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Fort Myer gained prominence in military aviation history, with Orville Wright conducting the first powered military airplane demonstration there on September 12, 1908, under Army observation.[7] This period coincided with infrastructure expansions, including the construction of officer quarters such as Quarters 1 in 1899, initially intended for the post commander amid the fort's growing role in hosting high-level military activities and dignitaries.[9] Today, as part of Joint Base Myer-Henderson Hall, Fort Myer continues to support ceremonial units like the 3rd U.S. Infantry Regiment, preserving its historical significance while adapting to modern garrison needs.[2]

Physical Description and Site Features


Quarters 1 is a three-story brick mansion constructed in 1899, featuring a modified Queen Anne architectural style with a long front porch and red brick exterior laid on a stone foundation.[10][1] The structure measures 40 by 54 feet, topped by a slate roof, and encompasses 21 rooms with a total gross floor space of 10,111 square feet distributed across the three main floors and a basement.[9][10] Exterior elements include window shutters and screened porches, while later additions such as an east wing incorporate a sunroom and garden terrace.[9] The residence is prominently sited at the terminus of Grant Avenue within Fort Myer, Arlington, Virginia, facing southeast toward the post's lower grounds and offering unobstructed views of the Washington, D.C. skyline.[1][10] Its entrance is flanked by cannons relocated from Fort McNair, emphasizing its ceremonial role, and it is enveloped by Victorian-era landscape features including expansive grass lawns, shrubs, large shade trees, and a flagstone patio.[1][11] Positioned on the fort's elevated ridge near Summerall Field and adjacent to Arlington National Cemetery, the site integrates with the broader Fort Myer Historic District, originally developed as part of the Signal Corps cantonment overlooking historic Civil War fortifications.[9][11]

Construction and Early Use

Planning and Construction (1897â€“1899)

In the late 1890s, Fort Myer served as a key post for the U.S. Army Signal Corps, necessitating expanded and improved facilities for senior officers amid growing military demands following the Spanish-American War preparations.[2] Planning for Quarters 1, designated as the commanding officer's residence, fell under the Quartermaster Corps, which developed standardized designs for officers' dwellings to ensure uniformity and efficiency across Army posts.[11] These plans emphasized durable brick construction suited to the Arlington Heights terrain, reflecting post-Civil War efforts to modernize permanent installations while adhering to fiscal constraints imposed by Congress. The design incorporated a modified Queen Anne style, featuring a long front porch and asymmetrical massing to provide both aesthetic appeal and practical functionality for a high-ranking occupant.[1] Construction commenced around 1897 as part of a broader building campaign at Fort Myer's north end, which included multiple officers' quarters to accommodate the Signal Corps' expansion.[2] The structure, a two-and-a-half-story brick mansion with 21 rooms, was erected using pressed brick laid in running bond, with hipped roofs and ornamental detailing typical of Quartermaster General's Office specifications for field officers' housing.[1] [11] Site selection at the end of Grant Avenue prioritized visibility and prominence, overlooking the post's parade grounds and Washington, D.C., to symbolize command authority. No individual architect is credited, as the project followed prototypical plans disseminated by the Quartermaster Department rather than custom commissions.[1] The building was completed on May 27, 1899, marking the culmination of two years of coordinated Quartermaster oversight amid logistical challenges like material sourcing and labor availability in the post-war period.[3] Little alteration to the original footprint occurred during construction, preserving the intended layout of public reception areas on the ground floor and private family spaces above. This timely finish aligned with the Army's reorganization under the Signal Corps, enabling immediate occupancy by senior leadership.[11]

Initial Occupancy and Pre-Chief of Staff Era (1899â€“1907)

Quarters 1 was completed on May 27, 1899, under Plan 95 of the Quartermaster Corps at a cost of $18,471, serving initially as the designated residence for the commanding officer of Fort Myer, a post focused on cavalry training, experimental maneuvers, and ceremonial functions overlooking Washington, D.C. Between 1899 and 1903, the quarters housed successive post commanders, though Fort Myer's frequent leadership changesâ€”over a dozen commanders in this spanâ€”make identifying specific occupants difficult without detailed assignment logs. In 1903, the residence transitioned to Major General Charles F. Humphrey, who occupied it as Quartermaster General of the United States Army, a position he held until 1907 before retiring. Humphrey's tenure marked a period of stable occupancy amid the post's ongoing role in supporting Army logistics and equestrian demonstrations, with the quarters remaining unmodified from its original two-and-a-half-story brick design featuring 21 rooms. During this pre-designation era, the building functioned as standard high-ranking officers' housing, integral to the Fort Myer Historic District's "Generals' Row" along Grant Avenue, without the formal protocols later associated with the Chief of Staff role.[12]

Establishment as Army Chief of Staff Residence

Assignment to the Chief of Staff (1908â€“1910)

In 1908, Quarters One was formally designated as the official residence of the Chief of Staff of the United States Army, marking its transition from general officers' housing to a dedicated quarters for the service's highest-ranking officer.[9][1] Major General J. Franklin Bell, who had assumed the role of Chief of Staff on April 14, 1906, became the first occupant when he moved into the residence on June 1, 1908.[9] This assignment reflected the growing administrative and representational demands of the position, established by the Dick Act of 1903, which centralized Army command under the Chief of Staff reporting directly to the Secretary of War.[9][1] To adapt the existing structure for official functions and family needs, an addition to the east wing was constructed during Bell's occupancy, incorporating a formal dining room and master bedroom at a cost of $16,341.[9] These modifications enabled expanded hosting of military and diplomatic events, underscoring the residence's role in supporting the Chief's duties proximate to Washington, D.C. Bell, noted for his emphasis on physical fitness and tactical reforms, utilized the quarters' location at Fort Myer for convenient oversight of cavalry demonstrations and early aviation experiments on the adjacent parade ground.[9] He was the first Chief of Staff to commute daily by automobile from the residence to his War Department office, a practical innovation amid the era's improving road infrastructure.[9] Bell resided in Quarters One until the conclusion of his tenure on April 21, 1910, after which General Leonard Wood succeeded him and maintained the residence as his home through 1914.[9][1] This initial period established the precedent of permanent assignment to the Chief of Staff, with no subsequent interruptions except for General John J. Pershing's tenure during World War I, when alternative arrangements were made due to wartime exigencies.[9] The designation enhanced the prestige of Fort Myer as a key Army post, leveraging its strategic overlook of the Potomac River and proximity to the capital for both operational efficiency and symbolic authority.[1]

Operational Role and Traditions (1910â€“Present)

Since its designation as the official residence of the Chief of Staff of the United States Army in 1910, Quarters One has served as both a private home and a venue for official functions integral to the Chief's representational duties.[9] General Leonard Wood, the first occupant from 1910 to 1914, utilized the quarters for family activities amid Fort Myer's equestrian facilities, while subsequent Chiefs, including Hugh L. Scott (1914â€“1917), hosted international military figures such as Marshal Joseph Joffre of France.[9] Peyton C. March (1918â€“1921) relied on family members as hostesses during World War I demands, establishing a pattern of the residence facilitating diplomatic and social engagements that underscored the Army's leadership role.[9] Occupancy was interrupted from 1921 to 1923 when General John J. Pershing, a widower, opted for off-post living, but all subsequent Chiefs of Staff have resided there, reinforcing its status as a symbol of the position's continuity and prestige.[9][12] The operational role has emphasized hosting formal dinners, receptions, and meetings with foreign dignitaries and Allied leaders, particularly during major conflicts. George C. Marshall (1939â€“1945) modernized the facilities in 1939 for $12,854.60 to accommodate wartime needs, using the residence to entertain World War II figures.[9] Dwight D. Eisenhower (1945â€“1948) hosted Winston Churchill and Bernard Montgomery there while drafting his memoir Crusade in Europe in the study.[9] Later residents, such as Matthew B. Ridgway (1953â€“1955) and Harold K. Johnson (1964â€“1968), conducted extensive renovationsâ€”including rewiring, structural repairs for $35,242, and additions like marble fireplaces from West Pointâ€”to support ongoing official use.[9] The quarters' proximity to Fort Myer's flagpole and Myer Monument positions it as a backdrop for post-wide ceremonies, enhancing its role in Army traditions of public military pageantry.[9] Traditions at Quarters One include maintaining historical continuity through archives of past residents, initiated by Mrs. Frederick C. Weyand in the 1970s, and honoring predecessors via portraits and furnishings, such as the 1964 addition of General William Gardner Bell's portrait.[9] Family-oriented events, like the 2006 wedding of General Peter J. Schoomaker's daughter, reflect its dual personal and institutional function, while protocols emphasize the Chief's support for Army family well-being, as exemplified by Raymond T. Odierno's occupancy starting August 2011.[9] These practices, evolving with each administration's modificationsâ€”such as Douglas MacArthur's 1931 elevator installation for $5,432 or Creighton W. Abrams' 1972â€“1974 kitchen updatesâ€”preserve the residence as a living emblem of Army leadership without public access.[9][12]

Architectural Characteristics

Exterior Design and Materials

Quarters 1 is a two-and-a-half-story red brick residence constructed in the modified Queen Anne style, completed in May 1899 as the post commander's quarters.[1][12] The structure features a side-gable roof with a projecting front cross-gable, sheathed in slate, and rests on a stone foundation, contributing to its Victorian-era appearance consistent with contemporaneous officers' quarters on Generals' Row.[9][3] The southeast-facing principal facade includes window shutters and a one-story front porch that extends leftward to form a porte-cochere, supported by paired round columns on pedestals, providing sheltered access and a hospitable entrance.[9][3] A sleeping porch was added to the second floor in 1914, and an east wingâ€”encompassing a dining room and master bedroomâ€”was constructed in 1908, with the garage and expanded porte-cochere completed between 1935 and 1939.[9] These modifications, along with a 1953â€“1955 renovation addressing structural repairs, represent the primary exterior changes, though the overall envelope has remained largely unaltered since original construction.[12][9] The brick masonry, selected for durability and harmony with adjacent buildings, has experienced minor weathering such as spalling in some Fort Myer structures but retains its integrity.[13][9]

Interior Layout and Historical Furnishings

Quarters 1 comprises 21 rooms distributed across three stories and a basement, totaling approximately 10,000 square feet of living space.[12] The interior retains much of its original Victorian-era configuration with minimal structural alterations since construction in 1899, though superficial updates have occurred, including a major renovation in 1953 for rewiring and repairs, and further refurbishments in 1983 focusing on the kitchen and overall interiors.[12][9] Woodwork throughout is finished in oak and mahogany, contributing to the residence's formal, enduring aesthetic.[3] The first floor includes a reception hall, living room with marble fireplace (salvaged from West Point cadet barracks), library with fireplace and half-bath, dining room (expanded in 1908 via east wing addition to seat 28â€“40 guests), breakfast room, powder room, kitchen, pantry, storage room, and office, alongside a sunroom adjacent to the living and dining areas providing access to an east-side garden terrace.[12][9] The second floor features two master bedrooms each with full bath (one in the 1908 east wing), a smaller bedroom with full bath, a sitting room functioning as a sunporch, two studies, and multiple closets; notable spaces include the library (dedicated to Katherine Tupper Marshall with books by Army chiefs' wives) and guest rooms such as the Eisenhower Room.[12][9] The third floor houses three bedrooms, two baths, additional closets, and storage, highlighted by the Bob Hope Room with its large picture window and a caricature of the entertainer.[9] The basement contains an orderly's dressing room, half-bath with shower, secondary kitchen, laundry, storage, and utility spaces.[12] An automatic self-service elevator connects the levels, supporting accessibility within the historic structure.[12] Historical furnishings emphasize military heritage and personal artifacts from past residents. Artwork from the U.S. Army Center of Military History adorns the first floor, official bedrooms, and staircases, including portraits of Army chiefs of staff and their families in hallways, a portrait of J. Franklin Bell in the foyer, and paintings such as The Chaplain on the Western Frontier in the library.[9] Notable items include General Pershing's table, Dwight D. Eisenhower's paintings and Mrs. Eisenhower's chaise lounge, and a foyer plaque listing residents since 1903.[9] Original features like the marble fireplaces persist, with modern additions such as wall-to-wall carpeting and drapes in principal rooms maintained in good condition as of historical surveys.[12] The residence supports official entertaining with appliances including multiple stoves, refrigerators, and a dishwasher, aligned with its role as the Chief of Staff's home since 1910.[12][9]

Notable Residents and Events

Prominent Army Chiefs of Staff

Major General J. Franklin Bell was the first Chief of Staff to occupy Quarters 1, beginning in 1908 during his tenure from January 1906 to April 1910.[1] Bell, who emphasized rigorous training reforms including the "Bell hikes" to enhance soldier endurance, used the residence as the official home for the position, establishing its role in hosting military and diplomatic functions.[9] His successor, Leonard Wood, resided there with his wife and three children from April 1910 to April 1914, along with family pets and aides, integrating domestic life with the demands of leadership during early modernization efforts in the Army.[9] [1] Wood's occupancy reinforced traditions of the quarters as a family venue amid his advocacy for a stronger general staff system.[9] Occupancy was interrupted in 1921 by John J. Pershing, who as Chief from September 1919 to September 1924 preferred urban living as a widower and declined to reside there, highlighting early flexibility in the tradition before it became standardized.[9] Douglas MacArthur briefly resided at Quarters 1 in 1930 during his term as Chief from November 1930 to October 1935, amid efforts to navigate Great Depression-era budget cuts and advocate for air power integration.[3] George C. Marshall occupied the residence from September 1939 to November 1945, overseeing massive Army expansion from 200,000 to over 8 million personnel during World War II, with the quarters serving as a hub for strategic deliberations.[2] Dwight D. Eisenhower made Quarters 1 his family home from November 1945 to February 1948 as Chief, managing postwar demobilization and unification debates, including hosting key meetings that influenced early Cold War military policy.[3] Omar N. Bradley resided there during his tenure from February 1948 to August 1949, focusing on implementing the National Security Act of 1947 amid the Army's transition to a smaller, technologically advanced force.[2]

Key Events and Military Significance

Quarters 1's location on Fort Myer positioned it near pivotal early aviation milestones, including the Wright brothers' demonstrations for the U.S. Army Signal Corps in 1908. On September 9, 1908, Orville Wright completed a flight of 57 circles around the Fort Myer parade ground, covering 4,647 meters in 1 hour and 14 minutes, marking a key step toward military adoption of powered flight.[14] A subsequent demonstration on September 17, 1908, ended in a crash that killed passenger Lieutenant Thomas E. Selfridgeâ€”the first powered-flight fatalityâ€”and injured Wright, underscoring the risks and innovations that shaped Army aviation.[9] These events, observed from the post including Quarters 1, facilitated the Army's purchase of its first airplane in 1909 after successful 1909 trials.[15] During World War I and II, Quarters 1 served as a venue for high-level military diplomacy under successive Chiefs of Staff. French Marshal Joseph Joffre visited during his 1917 U.S. tour, while Winston Churchill and Bernard Montgomery hosted receptions there amid Allied coordination in the 1940s.[9] General George C. Marshall, resident from 1939 to 1945, used the quarters for frequent wartime gatherings, including a 1941 Christmas event with British Ambassador Lord Halifax amid U.S. entry into the war, and strategic discussions reflecting its role in pre- and post-Pearl Harbor planning.[16][9] As the official residence of all 30 Chiefs of Staff since 1908, Quarters 1 holds enduring military significance as a center for official protocol and representation. It enables the Chief to host receptions, dinners, and ceremonies fostering interservice relations, congressional engagement, and alliances, as seen in ongoing Gold Star family events honoring fallen Soldiers.[2][17] These functions reinforce Army leadership's ceremonial duties, distinct from operational command, while the site's continuity symbolizes institutional stability across conflicts from World War I to modern operations.[9] Designated a National Historic Landmark in 1973, it embodies the evolution of U.S. military executive residence traditions.[9]

Preservation and Legacy

Historic Designations and Recognition

Quarters 1 was designated a National Historic Landmark on November 28, 1972, in recognition of its historical association with the U.S. Army's senior leadership and its exemplary preservation as a modified Queen Anne-style officers' residence completed in 1899.[1][18] This status underscores its continuous use as the official residence of every Army Chief of Staff since Major General J. Franklin Bell occupied it in 1908, including such figures as Generals Leonard Wood, George C. Marshall, Douglas MacArthur, Dwight D. Eisenhower, and Omar N. Bradley.[1] The National Historic Landmark designation automatically conferred listing on the National Register of Historic Places on the same date, with reference number 72001382.[1] Quarters 1 forms a contributing property within the broader Fort Myer Historic District, which encompasses late-19th- and early-20th-century cavalry-era structures along Grant Avenue and received concurrent National Historic Landmark and National Register of Historic Places designations (reference number 72001380) for its role in military innovation, including early aviation experiments.[6] Complementing these federal recognitions, Quarters 1 was entered on the Virginia Landmarks Register on June 19, 1973, affirming its architectural integrityâ€”with minimal alterations to its original 21-room brick configurationâ€”and its prominence at the terminus of Generals' Row overlooking Washington, D.C.[1] These designations have guided preservation efforts, ensuring the structure's maintenance amid its ongoing operational use by the Army's highest-ranking officer.[1][6]

Renovations, Maintenance, and Current Status

Quarters 1 underwent a complete renovation in 1980, addressing structural and functional needs of the era, followed by ongoing minor repairs and maintenance to sustain habitability and historic integrity.[12] These efforts have included periodic updates to utilities, roofing, and interior elements, guided by facility condition assessments to prevent deterioration in the structure built in 1899.[19] As part of the U.S. Army's Family Housing Renovation Program, initiated to modernize 53 historic quarters at Fort Myer and Fort McNair constructed between 1896 and 1935, Quarters 1 was slated for comprehensive upgrades approved by the Commission of Fine Arts in May 2023.[20][21] The planned work encompasses foundation repairs, waterproofing enhancements, HVAC system improvements, installation of fire suppression systems, and interior modernizations, all executed in compliance with National Historic Landmark standards to preserve exterior and contributing architectural features within the Fort Myer Historic District.[21][19] This multi-year initiative prioritizes occupant safety and efficiency while adhering to Secretary of the Interior standards for rehabilitation of historic properties.[20] The renovations for Quarters 1, alongside Quarters 2, 5, 6, 7, and 8, also involve the demolition and in-kind replacement of associated garages to support overall site functionality.[21] Facility condition assessments inform the scope, focusing on repairs identified as necessary for long-term preservation amid the building's role in a high-profile military context.[19] As of 2025, Quarters 1 remains the official residence of the Chief of Staff of the U.S. Army, actively maintained under Joint Base Myer-Henderson Hall protocols to ensure operational readiness and historical authenticity.[22] The structure continues to house official functions, with maintenance emphasizing minimal alterations to its modest interior changes and largely intact exterior since construction.[3]
