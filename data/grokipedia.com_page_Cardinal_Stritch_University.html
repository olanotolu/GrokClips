Cardinal Stritch University

Cardinal Stritch University was a private Catholic institution of higher education located in Fox Point and Glendale, Wisconsin, sponsored by the Sisters of St. Francis of Assisi.[1] Founded in 1937 as St. Clare College to prepare Franciscan sisters for teaching roles, it evolved into a comprehensive university offering undergraduate and graduate programs in fields such as business, education, nursing, and liberal arts, with a focus on serving diverse and underserved student populations.[2][3] The university, named after Samuel Cardinal Stritch, Archbishop of Milwaukee, expanded its campus northward from its original south-side location and developed a reputation for accessible education rooted in Franciscan values of service and ethical leadership.[2] Despite these efforts, Cardinal Stritch faced persistent challenges from national trends in higher education, including demographic shifts leading to fewer traditional college-age students and intensified competition for enrollment.[4] In April 2023, university president Dan Scholz announced the institution's closure effective at the end of the spring semester, citing insurmountable fiscal pressures, a multi-year decline in enrollment exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic, and insufficient resources to sustain operations without compromising educational quality.[5][6] This decision marked the end of 86 years of operation, reflecting broader vulnerabilities in small private colleges amid rising costs and shifting student preferences toward larger or more specialized institutions.[7] The closure prompted teach-out agreements with nearby universities to accommodate displaced students and preserve academic records through official verification processes.[8]

History

Founding and Early Development

Cardinal Stritch University traces its origins to the Sisters of St. Francis of Assisi, the first vowed Franciscan women's community established in the United States in 1849 within the Milwaukee diocese. In response to the need for trained educators during the Great Depression, Milwaukee Archbishop Samuel A. Stritch urged women's religious orders to create a college for nuns' education; the Sisters founded St. Clare Junior College in 1932 as a teacher training institution, which evolved into the four-year St. Clare College in 1937 on Milwaukee's south side.[3][9] Initially sharing facilities with St. Mary's Academy, the college enrolled 23 novices and postulants, focusing on preparing sisters for teaching roles amid limited opportunities for women's higher education.[10] By 1943, under the leadership of Mother Bartholomew Frederick as its first president, St. Clare College expanded to a degree-granting institution serving sisters from multiple religious communities.[10][9] This development reflected the Catholic Church's emphasis on intellectual and spiritual formation for religious women, aligning with broader efforts to professionalize teaching within parochial schools. In 1946, the college opened its doors to laywomen for the first time and was renamed Cardinal Stritch College in honor of the archbishop, who had left Milwaukee in 1940 to become Cardinal of Chicago.[3][10] This shift broadened its mission beyond exclusively religious training, incorporating lay students while maintaining a Franciscan commitment to service and education. Early growth emphasized programs in education and liberal arts, with the institution dedicating resources to students with developmental disabilities and establishing a reading clinic for children, underscoring its practical orientation toward community needs.[3] By the mid-1950s, enrollment had increased sufficiently to support further expansion, including the introduction of a co-educational graduate division in 1956 offering master's degrees in special education and reading.[10] These steps laid the groundwork for the college's transition into a comprehensive university, driven by post-World War II demands for higher education among women and returning veterans' families.[3]

Growth and Transition to University Status

In the 1960s, Cardinal Stritch College experienced significant enrollment growth, driven by the relocation to a new 52-acre campus in Fox Point, Wisconsin, in 1962 and the post-World War II baby boom that increased the pool of college-aged students.[3] By 1967, enrollment had reached 650 students, reflecting expanded facilities including new academic buildings and dormitories that supported a transition from its earlier, more limited operations on the Assisi campus.[3] This period marked the institution's shift toward broader accessibility, including coeducational admissions for men starting in the early 1950s and the introduction of diverse undergraduate programs in liberal arts, education, and nursing.[11] Subsequent decades saw further expansion through innovative programs tailored to non-traditional students, such as evening and weekend classes for working adults, which contributed to an enrollment surge from approximately 400 students in the late 1970s to over 3,000 by the 1990s.[12] These initiatives, including degree-completion pathways, aligned with demographic shifts and labor market demands for higher education among older learners, prompting infrastructure investments like library expansions and the addition of graduate offerings in business and education.[10] The culmination of this growth occurred in 1997, when the Board of Trustees, following a two-year research and planning process evaluating academic scope, program diversity, and institutional maturity, approved the transition from college to university status.[13] This re-designation recognized the institution's evolution into a comprehensive entity offering associate, bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees, with a focus on Franciscan values integrated across expanded disciplines.[14] The change formalized its role as one of the largest Franciscan higher education institutions in North America at the time.[9]

Enrollment Decline and Financial Pressures

Cardinal Stritch University's enrollment peaked at 4,407 students in 2013 but declined steadily thereafter, reaching 1,365 by fall 2021 and approximately 1,332 in 2022.[15][16] This represented a loss of nearly 1,000 students over roughly a decade, with annual drops accelerating in the late 2010s.[17] Contributing factors included broader demographic trends, such as declining U.S. birthrates from the 2000s reducing the pool of traditional college-age students, compounded by the COVID-19 pandemic's disruption of enrollment patterns and a strong job market drawing potential students away from higher education.[4] The enrollment contraction directly eroded tuition revenue, which fell from $50.8 million in 2015 to $32.4 million by 2020, as the institution relied heavily on student payments amid limited endowments typical of small private colleges.[15] Despite efforts to mitigate losses through staff reductions and expense cuts, the university operated at persistent deficits for years, unable to offset fixed costs like facility maintenance and operational overhead.[18] These pressures were exacerbated by the pandemic's acceleration of enrollment declines and increased demand for institutional resources, such as enhanced online infrastructure, without corresponding revenue recovery.[7] By early 2023, mounting financial unsustainability prompted university president Dan Scholz to announce closure on April 11, citing "fiscal realities, downward enrollment trends, the pandemic, the need for more resources and the mounting operational and facility challenges."[5][7] The institution ceased operations at the end of the spring 2023 semester, reflecting vulnerabilities common to tuition-dependent, non-elite private colleges facing secular declines in higher education demand.[19]

Mission and Catholic Franciscan Identity

Core Values and Educational Philosophy

Cardinal Stritch University's core values were deeply rooted in the Franciscan tradition, emphasizing four principal tenets: creating a caring community, showing compassion, reverencing all of creation, and making peace.[9] These values, derived from the Sisters of St. Francis of Assisi who founded the institution in 1937, informed institutional policies, student conduct expectations, and community standards throughout its operation until closure in 2023.[14] Students and faculty were expected to embody these principles, fostering an environment where diverse faith and cultural backgrounds aligned with Franciscan ethics of responsibility and ethical computing use, among other applications.[13] The educational philosophy at Cardinal Stritch integrated Catholic Franciscan heritage into a comprehensive liberal arts framework, prioritizing exposure to broad human experiences beyond specialized majors to cultivate well-rounded servant leaders.[20] This approach supported an "integrated education and formation program" for undergraduates, blending academic rigor with spiritual and ethical development to advance personal excellence and service-oriented outcomes.[14] Rooted in the university's mission as a Catholic Franciscan institution, the philosophy emphasized transformative, value-centered learning that prepared students for leadership roles in underserved communities, aligning coursework and campus life with principles of compassion and peacemaking.[3][21] In practice, this philosophy manifested through programs that encouraged civic engagement and alignment with Franciscan ideals, such as community service initiatives that reinforced the commitment to acting responsibly within established ethical standards.[22] The university's dedication to these values extended to graduate offerings, where leadership programs in higher education and other fields incorporated Franciscan-guided ethical reasoning to promote servant leadership models.[23] While institutional catalogs and mission statements consistently highlighted these elements, their implementation faced scrutiny amid enrollment declines, though empirical data on outcomes like graduation rates or alumni impact remains limited in public records prior to closure.[24]

Integration of Faith in Academics and Campus Life

Cardinal Stritch University integrated its Catholic Franciscan identity into academics through a core curriculum revised in 2014 to explicitly incorporate the institution's four foundational values: creating a caring community, showing compassion, reverencing all creation, and making peace.[3][9] This approach aimed to embed these principles across disciplines, fostering servant-leadership and ethical reasoning in students' intellectual development.[14] The Franciscan Servant-Scholars Program, available to full-time undergraduates, provided a structured formation initiative that combined academic coursework with intentional reflection on social justice, community service, and Franciscan spirituality, requiring participants to engage in volunteer activities and leadership development aligned with the university's heritage.[14][22] In campus life, faith integration occurred primarily through University Ministry, which organized liturgies, prayer services, and sacraments to support students' spiritual journeys within a Franciscan framework of communal care and peacemaking.[25] The St. Francis of Assisi Chapel in Bonaventure Hall served as the central venue for worship, hosting daily Masses on Mondays and Thursdays at 12:20 p.m., alongside opportunities for adoration, reconciliation, and ecumenical events such as monthly "Mystic Pizza" gatherings for faculty, staff, and students.[14][25] Service learning initiatives, often tied to the Franciscan emphasis on compassion and creation care, encouraged participation in local outreach, with programs like the Servant-Scholars extending these values beyond the classroom into practical community engagement.[26] While rooted in Catholic tradition and sponsored by the Sisters of St. Francis of Assisi, the university welcomed students from diverse faith backgrounds, promoting inclusive spiritual enrichment without mandating adherence to Catholicism.[27][28]

Campus and Facilities

Main Campus Location and Layout

The main campus of Cardinal Stritch University was located at 6801 N. Yates Road in Fox Point, Wisconsin, a affluent northern suburb of Milwaukee spanning the border with Glendale.[29][30] The site occupied approximately 40 acres in a large suburban environment, positioned about 10 miles north of downtown Milwaukee and surrounded by residential neighborhoods.[31] This setting facilitated a compact, walkable campus conducive to community-oriented activities, though it lacked the urban density of central Milwaukee institutions.[1] The campus layout centered on a main quadrangle with key administrative and communal buildings clustered for accessibility. The Campus Center served as the hub, incorporating the student union, auditorium, bookstore, and offices for the Dean of Students and other services like the registrar and security.[32] Surrounding structures included academic halls for classrooms and laboratories, residence halls for on-campus housing, a library for research resources, a gymnasium for athletic and recreational use, and fine arts facilities supporting performing and visual arts programs.[33] Athletic fields and parking lots bordered the periphery, with pathways connecting buildings amid green spaces typical of mid-20th-century suburban expansions. Following the university's closure in May 2023, approximately 80% of the buildings were slated for demolition in a redevelopment project, preserving the library, gymnasium, Campus Center, and fine arts structures for adaptive reuse.[8][33]

Key Infrastructure and Resources

The main campus of Cardinal Stritch University occupied 43.5 acres in the Fox Point and Glendale suburbs of Milwaukee, Wisconsin, encompassing 12 buildings totaling approximately 607,000 square feet.[34][35] Key structures supported academic, administrative, athletic, and spiritual functions, with expansions in the mid-20th century adding facilities such as a library, fieldhouse, and student union to accommodate growing enrollment.[3] Central to academic resources was the university library, which provided access to print and digital collections, interlibrary loans, and study spaces for students and faculty.[36] The library integrated with broader campus IT infrastructure, requiring user authentication via university credentials for online resources and services.[37] Administrative and instructional activities centered in buildings like Bonaventure Hall, housing offices for academic affairs, admissions, business operations, career services, and a chapel for Franciscan-inspired reflection.[32] Athletic infrastructure included a gym and fieldhouse, supporting varsity sports in the Chicagoland Collegiate Athletic Conference, with spaces for basketball, track and field, and wellness programs.[3][33] A campus center served as a hub for student life, dining, and events, while fine arts facilities enabled programs in performing and visual arts.[33] These resources aligned with the institution's emphasis on holistic education, though maintenance challenges contributed to eventual financial strain leading to closure in May 2023.[8]

Academics

Academic Programs and Degrees

Cardinal Stritch University structured its academic programs across four primary colleges: the College of Arts and Sciences, Kellner College of Education, Literacy and Leadership, Ruth S. Coleman College of Nursing and Health Sciences, and College of Business and Management.[14][38] These units delivered associate, baccalaureate, master's, and doctoral degrees in fields emphasizing liberal arts, professional preparation, education, business, health sciences, and leadership, with a focus on Franciscan values integrated into curricula.[14] Undergraduate offerings included associate degrees such as the Associate of Arts in General Studies (60 credits, comprising 31 core and 29 elective credits) and Associate of Science in Business Administration (60 credits, with 30 business and 24 liberal arts core credits, available in evening/online accelerated formats).[14] Baccalaureate programs required 120 credits, typically with 30-32 core credits, major-specific credits (minimum 34-45), and professional cores; these encompassed Bachelor of Arts degrees in areas like communication, English/writing, history, political science, psychology, sociology, Spanish, theater, music, and philosophy; and Bachelor of Science degrees in biology, chemistry, computer science, criminal justice, environmental science, mathematics, physics, accounting, finance, management, marketing, sport and recreation business, nursing (with an eight-semester plan including pharmacology and clinical transitions), respiratory therapy completion (requiring Registered Respiratory Therapist credential and 2.33 GPA), and various education tracks (elementary, secondary, special education in behavioral disabilities, autism/communication disorders, or learning disabilities).[14] Business administration programs offered concentrations in economics/accounting, human resources/marketing, advanced principles, and international business (including study abroad and foreign language requirements). Minors were available in disciplines such as graphic communication, gender studies, and finance, with culminating experiences like capstones or internships mandated in many majors.[14] Graduate programs featured master's degrees, including Master of Arts in clinical psychology, pastoral studies (30 credits), psychology (with clinical or psychometry options), teaching (with licensure tracks from K-3 to PK-12), education (specializations in early childhood, literacy, special education, bilingual), religious studies (ministry or theology), and Spanish (translation/interpretation); Master of Business Administration; and Master of Science in educational leadership (31 credits, leading to principal and director licenses), higher education student affairs, management/leadership, nursing (educator or leader concentrations), special education (cross-categorical, K-9 or 4-12 licensure), sport/recreation business (30 credits), and regular/special education dual licensure.[38] Doctoral offerings comprised Doctor of Philosophy in language/literacy (54 credits) and leadership for advancement of learning/service (with health care or higher education variants), alongside Doctor of Education in leadership for advancement of learning/service; transfer credits could fulfill up to 50% of doctoral requirements from regionally accredited institutions.[38] Certificate programs, such as in language interpretation/translation (12 credits, online), supplemented degree paths, with policies allowing concurrent degrees (up to 9 shared credits) and double majors under single awards.[38]

Faculty and Enrollment Trends

Enrollment at Cardinal Stritch University declined markedly from the early 2010s onward, reflecting broader challenges in private higher education amid demographic shifts and competition. Accreditation and Institutional Outcomes

Cardinal Stritch University held regional accreditation from the Higher Learning Commission (HLC), which oversees institutions in the North Central region, including Wisconsin, from its establishment until closure.[14][42] In June 2023, following the university's announcement of closure, the HLC approved its provisional closure plan and teach-out agreements with partner institutions such as Alverno College and Concordia University Wisconsin to ensure completion of student programs.[43] This accreditation affirmed the institution's compliance with standards for academic quality, governance, and student achievement prior to its financial insolvency.[44] Institutionally, Cardinal Stritch reported a full-time freshman retention rate of 63%, indicating the percentage of first-year students returning for their sophomore year, which trailed national averages for similar doctoral/professional universities.[16] The six-year graduation rate for full-time, first-time undergraduates stood at 51.5%, reflecting completion within 150% of normal program time based on cohort data from the Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS).[16] Four-year graduation rates were lower, at around 45%, underscoring challenges in timely degree attainment amid enrollment declines.[45] Post-graduation outcomes included median early-career earnings of $56,514 for alumni, derived from earnings data tracked one to five years after completion, positioning graduates competitively in fields like education, business, and nursing but below selective institutions.[46] Employment services were provided to students, though specific placement rates were not publicly detailed in federal reports; the university's focus on Franciscan values emphasized holistic preparation, yet systemic financial pressures likely constrained long-term outcome tracking.[47] No evidence of federal student loan default rates exceeding thresholds for sanctions appeared in available IPEDS data prior to closure.[48]

Athletics

Program Overview and Conference Affiliation

Cardinal Stritch University's intercollegiate athletics program, known as the Stritch Wolves, competed exclusively at the National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics (NAIA) level.[49] The program maintained membership in the Chicagoland Collegiate Athletic Conference (CCAC), a NAIA conference founded in 1941 that sponsored competition across multiple sports for institutions primarily in the Midwest, including teams from Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Wisconsin, and Missouri.[49] The department fielded varsity teams in five men's sportsâ€”basketball, soccer, tennis, track and field, and volleyballâ€”and six women's sports, adding softball to the same core offerings.[50] These programs supported 158 student-athletes (81 men and 77 women) in the years leading up to the university's closure in May 2023, with participants receiving an average of $5,551 in sports-related student aid annually.[50] Athletics operations were overseen by 11 head coaches and generated approximately $2.3 million in revenue per year, yielding a small net surplus of $31,477 after expenses.[50]

Basketball Achievements and Challenges

The men's basketball program at Cardinal Stritch University reached its highest point in 2013, capturing the NAIA Division II National Championship with a 73-59 victory over William Penn University in the final game held on March 12 in Point Lookout, Missouri. This triumph represented the program's inaugural national title and highlighted a competitive era within the NAIA framework.[51][52] Building toward that success, the team defended its Chicagoland Collegiate Athletic Conference (CCAC) Tournament championship in the 2011-12 season, securing the title as the sixth-seeded entrant after prevailing in the final matchup. Players like forward Sam Smit earned all-conference recognition annually from 2011-12 through 2014-15, including first-team honors in his senior year, underscoring individual contributions amid team efforts. The program also advanced as the top seed and defending national champion in the 2014 NAIA tournament, defeating Northern New Mexico College 74-53 in the opening round before elimination.[53][54][55] The women's basketball team, coached by John Pfaffl starting in his 13th season by 2022-23, participated in NAIA Division II and the CCAC without securing national or conference tournament titles on record, emphasizing consistent competition and academic-athletic balance for participants.[56] Both programs encountered mounting challenges in later years, reflected in suboptimal performance statistics; the men's team in 2022-23 averaged 62.5 points per game across 26 contests with a 39.3% field goal percentage and 30.0% from three-point range, indicative of recruitment and competitive difficulties amid broader institutional enrollment declines. The women's squad similarly averaged 55.9 points per game in 25 games that season, shooting 33.8% from the field.[57][58] The university's April 10, 2023, announcement of permanent closure effective May 22â€”driven by sustained enrollment drops, pandemic-related fiscal strains, and operational shortfallsâ€”halted all athletic activities, including basketball, after the 2022-23 campaigns concluded as the final ones. This disrupted ongoing player development and forced remaining student-athletes to rapidly transfer to other institutions, compounding recruitment hurdles in a small private NAIA setting already strained by demographic shifts in higher education.[5][12][59]

Track and Field Highlights

The track and field program at Cardinal Stritch University, competing in the NAIA as part of the Chicagoland Collegiate Athletic Conference, produced its most prominent achievements through standout individual performances in distance events. Sophomore Daniel Garcia, from Barcelona, Spain, won the NAIA indoor national championship in the 1000 meters at the 2016 championships in Johnson City, Tennessee, recording a time of 2:28.79; he also anchored the winning distance medley relay team.[60] In 2017, Garcia secured the NAIA outdoor national title in the 1500 meters at the championships in Gulf Shores, Alabama, with a championship-record time of 3:50.47, earning him the NAIA Midwest Regional Men's Track Athlete of the Year award for that season.[61][62][63] Garcia's successes marked the program's highest-profile national accomplishments, with the teams regularly qualifying athletes for NAIA indoor and outdoor championships through strong showings at CCAC meets, though no team conference titles or additional national team placements were recorded in available results.[64]

Notable People

Alumni Contributions

Alumni of Cardinal Stritch University have made contributions across politics, business, community development, and the performing arts, often leveraging skills in management, education, and ethical leadership emphasized in the institution's Franciscan tradition. Over 40,000 graduates have assumed leadership roles in businesses, health care organizations, corporations, and educational institutions worldwide, with the university ranking first among Wisconsin colleges for alumni mid-career salary outcomes as of 2023 data.[27][65] In politics, Rosemary Hinkfuss, who earned a bachelor's degree in education from Cardinal Stritch College in 1954, served as a Wisconsin State Representative for the 88th Assembly District from 1988 to 1994, advocating for progressive policies during her tenure on the Brown County Board and Green Bay City Council; she was recognized as a pioneering female legislator in the state.[66][67] John C. Pederson, holder of an MBA from the university, represented Minnesota's 35th Senate District as a Republican from 2011 to 2016, focusing on fiscal policy and local government issues while owning and operating a concrete block manufacturing company that supported regional infrastructure.[68][69] Business and nonprofit leaders include Rose Spano Iannelli, who received a bachelor's degree in management in 1994 and co-founded Spano Pratt Executive Search, specializing in placing leaders in nonprofit organizations with an emphasis on aligning professional skills with mission-driven roles; she facilitated over $1 million in legacy gifts to the Greater Milwaukee Foundation as a board member for Columbia College of Nursing.[70] Michael Hostad, with a 2010 bachelor's in management, co-founded The Commons under the Greater Milwaukee Committee to connect students with businesses for collaborative projects and led the Light the Hoan initiative, raising $3.5 million to install programmable LED lighting on the Hoan Bridge, which has supported events like national memorials and STEM education programs such as Code the Hoan.[71] Nelson Soler, who obtained his doctorate from Stritch, serves as president and CEO of the Multicultural Entrepreneurial Institute, directing programs that have assisted over 6,300 entrepreneurs with $2.1 million in microloans, grants, and technical aid while developing a bilingual youth leadership pipeline for 90 participants launching ventures.[72] In the arts, James T. Alfred, a graduate with a B.A. in international business, has advanced as an actor, writer, producer, and director, performing in productions like Jitney (Broadway tour), Fat Ham, and Black Odyssey, and using theater to address social issues through affiliations with institutions such as Penumbra Theatre and Steppenwolf.[73][74]

Faculty and Leadership Figures

Sister Camille Kliebhan, a member of the Sisters of St. Francis of Assisi, joined Cardinal Stritch University as a faculty member in 1955, initially teaching statistics after earning a Ph.D. from The Catholic University of America; she later served as president during a period of institutional growth.[75][76] Sister Mary Lea Schneider, OSF, was inaugurated as president in 1991, marking her as the final member of the founding religious order to hold the role amid transitions toward expanded lay involvement in governance.[9] Helen Sobehart became the university's first lay president, overseeing developments including concurrent enrollment programs for high school students.[77] James P. Loftus, Ph.D., assumed the presidency around 2011 following a career at St. Ambrose University and led until his resignation on May 31, 2017.[78][79] Kathleen A. Rinehart was named interim president in September 2017 and formally installed in 2018, guiding the institution during a phase of administrative stability.[79][80] Dan Scholz, Ph.D., served as interim president before being appointed the 10th president on June 28, 2021, by the Sisters of St. Francis leadership team; he remained in the role through the university's announced closure and final commencement in May 2023.[81][82] Among faculty, Dawn Wankowski held the position of assistant professor from 2002, contributing to STEM education and recognized for her work in mathematics and related disciplines.[83]

Closure and Aftermath

Announcement and Immediate Reasons

On April 10, 2023, Cardinal Stritch University President Dan Scholz announced the institution's permanent closure, effective May 22, 2023, following the end of the spring semester.[84][85] The announcement, delivered via a video message and public statement, cited an inability to sustain operations amid escalating financial pressures.[5] Scholz emphasized that the decision came after exhaustive efforts to address challenges, including strategic planning and reserve drawdowns, but concluded that continuation was untenable.[86] The primary immediate reasons articulated were persistent operating deficits and sharply declining enrollment, which had eroded the university's financial reserves over multiple years.[15][5] Enrollment had fallen dramatically in the preceding decade, dropping from over 3,000 students in the early 2010s to approximately 1,500 by 2022, exacerbating revenue shortfalls that outpaced expenses.[6] Scholz noted that reserves, previously used to cover deficits, were depleted, rendering further operations unsustainable without external intervention.[7] Tax filings confirmed annual losses in at least nine of the ten years prior to closure, underscoring the structural fiscal imbalance.[17] While broader demographic shifts in higher educationâ€”such as fewer high school graduates and competition from larger institutionsâ€”contributed to the enrollment crisis, the announcement framed these as culminating in an acute fiscal emergency rather than isolated events.[6] Scholz's statement avoided attributing closure to recent events like the COVID-19 pandemic alone, instead highlighting long-term trends that had intensified budgetary strains.[87] No viable merger or acquisition partners emerged in time to avert shutdown, despite exploratory discussions.[5]

Impacts on Students, Faculty, and Community

The abrupt announcement of Cardinal Stritch University's closure on April 10, 2023, elicited widespread shock and grief among students, who described the news as having "dropped out of the sky" with little prior warning, disrupting academic plans and personal attachments to the institution.[88] Approximately 805 students were displaced, with the university prioritizing transfer agreements and teach-out programs to minimize credit loss; by September 2023, at least 63% (around 510) had enrolled at partner institutions such as Marquette University, Mount Mary University, and UW-Whitewater, often retaining class standing and financial aid eligibility.[89] [90] Undocumented students faced acute challenges, losing a campus known for supportive resources tailored to their needs amid Milwaukee's limited alternatives.[91] Faculty and staff, numbering 476 full- and part-time employees, confronted immediate job losses effective after the May 22, 2023, closure, prompting the university to coordinate employment verification and career transition support through partnerships.[92] [93] While some faculty secured positions at nearby schoolsâ€”such as one professor relocating to Marquette after nine years at Stritchâ€”others expressed concerns over the broader implications for academic justice and job market saturation in Wisconsin's higher education sector.[94] [95] President Dan Scholz emphasized a "no-win" focus on aiding transitions, though the layoffs contributed to local economic strain in Glendale, where the campus had anchored community ties for 86 years.[7] The closure reverberated through Milwaukee's educational and Franciscan communities, amplifying a national trend of small private colleges shuttering amid enrollment declines, with alumni and locals mourning the loss of Stritch's role in fostering ethical leadership and regional talent development.[5] Other institutions stepped in with recruitment drives and credit evaluations to absorb displaced members, yet the event underscored vulnerabilities in nonprofit higher education, prompting reflections on sustainability without mitigating the emotional and institutional voids left behind.[12] [96]

Asset Transfer and Ongoing Legacy

Following its closure on May 22, 2023, Cardinal Stritch University's primary physical assets, including its 43-acre campus at 6801 N. Yates Road spanning Glendale and Fox Point, Wisconsin, were sold to the Ramirez Family Foundation for $24 million in July 2023.[97][34] The foundation, established by industrialist G. Robert "Bob" Corolis and focused on advancing Christian education through initiatives like St. Augustine Preparatory Academy, acquired the property to support its mission of developing educational ministries.[98][34] Secondary assets, such as kitchen equipment and furnishings, were liquidated through online auctions managed by Badger Corporation in August 2023 to generate additional revenue during the wind-down process.[99] The university's closure announcement emphasized that degrees conferred prior to shutdown remain valid, with transcripts available via the National Student Clearinghouse for verification and transfer purposes.[100] In terms of ongoing legacy, the Ramirez Family Foundation announced plans to repurpose the site as an extension of St. Augustine Preparatory Academy, establishing a north-side voucher-funded Catholic high school targeted to open by fall 2025.[101][102] This $100 million project involves demolishing approximately 80% of existing structures while preserving key facilities like the gymnasium, library, campus center, and fine arts building to align with the new educational vision.[33] The closure also led to the disbandment of the university's alumni association, which had served around 40,000 graduates, though individual alumni networks persist informally.[12]
