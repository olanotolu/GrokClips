Phosphogypsum

Phosphogypsum is the calcium sulfate dihydrate byproduct formed during the wet-process production of phosphoric acid, in which sulfuric acid reacts with phosphate rock to extract phosphorus for fertilizers.[1] Approximately five tons of phosphogypsum are generated for each ton of phosphoric acid produced, resulting in vast quantities stored worldwide, often exceeding hundreds of millions of tons in large stacks.[2] Its composition includes not only gypsum but also impurities such as residual acids, phosphates, fluorides, heavy metals, and naturally occurring radioactive materials (NORM) like uranium, thorium, and radium-226, which decay to produce radon gas.[3] These contaminants render phosphogypsum unsuitable for many applications without treatment, leading to its primary disposal in open-air phosphogypsum stacks that can emit radon and risk leaching pollutants into soil and water under weathering or structural failure.[4] In the United States, federal regulations under the Environmental Protection Agency prohibit most uses of phosphogypsum due to radioactivity levels, confining it largely to stack storage or limited agricultural trials, while research explores purification for construction or soil amendment despite ongoing environmental concerns.[5][4]

Definition and Composition

Chemical and Mineralogical Makeup

Phosphogypsum consists primarily of calcium sulfate dihydrate, with the chemical formula CaSOâ‚„Â·2Hâ‚‚O, which constitutes the bulk of its solid mass following filtration from the phosphoric acid production slurry.[6][7] This composition arises directly from the neutralization of sulfate ions with calcium derived from phosphate rock, yielding a material chemically analogous to natural gypsum mineral.[8] Mineralogically, phosphogypsum predominantly manifests as fine gypsum crystals, often in dihydrate form, alongside minor crystalline phases such as quartz or, under varying drying conditions, traces of hemihydrate (CaSOâ‚„Â·0.5Hâ‚‚O) or anhydrite (CaSOâ‚„).[9][10] These crystal structures differ from natural gypsum primarily in particle size and morphology due to rapid precipitation in acidic media, resulting in smaller, more uniform grains typically under 100 micrometers.[11] Residual phosphorus compounds, retained from incomplete extraction during processing, are present as trace phosphates, with Pâ‚‚Oâ‚… content generally ranging from 0.5% to 2% by weight, though values up to 2.7% have been reported in specific samples.[12][13] These levels reflect process efficiency and phosphate rock variability, embedding phosphorus primarily in associated calcium phosphate phases rather than altering the dominant sulfate lattice.[14]

Impurities and Source Variability

Phosphogypsum contains impurities derived from the phosphate rock feedstock, including radionuclides such as radium-226, uranium-238, and thorium-232 series elements, as well as heavy metals like cadmium and arsenic.[15][16] These contaminants concentrate in the gypsum during the wet-process phosphoric acid production, reflecting the natural composition of the parent phosphate deposits.[17] Radium-226 concentrations in phosphogypsum typically range from 5 to 30 pCi/g, with U.S. stack samples averaging 18.6 pCi/g and varying from 6.3 to 27.9 pCi/g.[18][15] Uranium-238 levels are lower, often 16.8 to 38.9 Bq/kg (approximately 0.45 to 1.05 pCi/g), while thorium-232 ranges from 27.3 to 83.8 Bq/kg, depending on particle size and processing conditions.[19] Heavy metals such as cadmium and arsenic occur at trace levels, with cadmium noted as a primary pollutant impurity from phosphate rock.[20] Impurity levels vary significantly by the geological origin of the phosphate rock, with sedimentary deposits exhibiting higher radionuclide concentrations than igneous sources.[17] For instance, phosphogypsum from sedimentary rocks in Florida and Morocco shows elevated radioactivity due to naturally higher uranium and radium content in the ore, whereas igneous phosphate rocks yield phosphogypsum with comparatively lower levels.[21][22] Phosphogypsum also retains fluorine compounds at 1-2% by weight, primarily as water-soluble fluorosilicates and hydrofluoric acid residues from the production process.[23] Fresh phosphogypsum exhibits acidity with an initial pH of 2-3 due to these residual acids.[24][25]

Global Production Volumes and Economic Context

Global phosphogypsum production is closely linked to the phosphoric acid manufacturing process in the phosphate fertilizer industry, where it emerges as a byproduct at a ratio of approximately 4.5 to 5.5 tons of phosphogypsum per ton of Pâ‚‚Oâ‚… produced.[35] Annual global output reached an estimated 245 million metric tons as of 2025, representing contributions from producers accounting for over 85% of worldwide totals.[36] This volume aligns with broader estimates ranging from 100 to 280 million metric tons per year, driven primarily by demand for phosphate-based fertilizers.[37] Leading producers include China, which dominates with roughly half of global output due to its position as the top phosphate rock extractor at over 110 million metric tons annually; the United States, concentrated in Florida with approximately 30 million metric tons of phosphogypsum yearly; and Morocco, a key player leveraging its vast reserves exceeding 50 billion metric tons of phosphate rock.[38][36] These nations account for the majority of phosphoric acid capacity, underscoring phosphogypsum's scale relative to global Pâ‚‚Oâ‚… demand, which supports fertilizer production amid rising agricultural needs.[39] Economically, phosphogypsum imposes storage burdens on producers, with management costs in the United States estimated at about $1.5 per ton, accumulating over time for stacks exceeding 1.7 billion tons domestically.[26] Globally, stockpiles surpassed 7 billion metric tons by 2025, growing at rates tied to fertilizer expansion despite limited utilization, which hovers below 15% of production.[40] This accumulation reflects the byproduct's low commercial value and regulatory constraints on reuse, straining industry economics in major producing regions.[36]

Physical and Chemical Properties

Key Characteristics

Phosphogypsum is characteristically a fine, powdery solid or granular material, with particle sizes typically less than 1 mm and often exhibiting a yellowish-white to grayish-white or dark gray coloration due to impurities.[6][41][42] Its true particle density ranges from 2.27 to 2.4 g/cmÂ³, while bulk density varies between 0.9 and 1.7 g/cmÂ³ depending on moisture and compaction.[43] The material demonstrates low to moderate water solubility, approximately 2 to 3.8 g/L under standard conditions, primarily attributable to its dominant calcium sulfate dihydrate composition.[44] Chemically, freshly produced phosphogypsum is acidic, with pH values ranging from 2.37 to 5.33 owing to residual phosphoric acid and other solubles, though it tends to neutralize toward pH 6-8 over time through natural processes like atmospheric carbonation or deliberate aging.[42] Thermally, phosphogypsum exhibits dehydration behavior akin to gypsum, losing crystallization water in stages: initial endothermic dehydration from dihydrate to hemihydrate occurs between 100Â°C and 150Â°C, with complete dehydration to anhydrite by approximately 180Â°C under non-isothermal conditions.[45][46] This thermal profile influences its handling, as it requires controlled drying to prevent premature hardening or structural changes.[47]

Comparison to Natural Gypsum

Phosphogypsum and natural gypsum both consist primarily of calcium sulfate dihydrate (CaSOâ‚„Â·2Hâ‚‚O), comprising 80-95% of phosphogypsum's mass and aligning closely with high-grade natural gypsum in chemical composition and crystal structure.[48] [49] This shared dihydrate form imparts comparable hydration kinetics and thermal dehydration properties, allowing phosphogypsum to function as a direct substitute for natural gypsum in processes like hemihydrate production.[50] Phosphogypsum particles are generally finer, with maximum sizes ranging from 0.5 to 1.0 mm and typically 50-75% passing a 0.075 mm sieve, in contrast to the coarser aggregates (often >1 mm) prevalent in mined natural gypsum for industrial use.[51] [52] This reduced particle size in phosphogypsum enhances surface area and dissolution rates, potentially increasing reactivity relative to unprocessed natural gypsum.[53] Natural gypsum exhibits higher inherent purity, with radionuclide levels such as radium-226 typically below 1 pCi/g due to the low uranium content in evaporitic deposits, while phosphogypsum averages 5-20 pCi/g Ra-226 inherited from phosphate rock processing.[54] [55] Phosphogypsum also contains process-derived impurities including residual phosphates (0.5-2% Pâ‚‚Oâ‚…), fluorides (0.1-2% F), and trace heavy metals, absent or negligible in natural gypsum.[56] Stack phosphogypsum, stored in unrefined form post-production, retains these contaminants at higher concentrations than processed grades, which undergo washing or flotation to achieve purity suitable for commercial markets (e.g., >90% CaSOâ‚„Â·2Hâ‚‚O).[57] [43]

Beneficial Applications

Agricultural and Soil Amendment Uses

Phosphogypsum serves as an effective amendment for reclaiming sodic and saline soils by supplying calcium ions (CaÂ²âº) that displace exchangeable sodium ions (Naâº), thereby reducing soil sodicity, enhancing hydraulic conductivity, and improving aggregate stability.[58][59] Application rates typically range from 1.5 to 4.5 tons per hectare, with field trials showing yield improvements such as a 61% increase in barley grain production relative to unamended controls.[60][61] As a source of calcium and sulfur, phosphogypsum addresses nutrient deficiencies and alleviates aluminum toxicity in acidic subsoils by promoting CaÂ²âº exchange with toxic AlÂ³âº, which facilitates deeper root penetration and better crop establishment.[62][63] IAEA-reviewed studies confirm that radionuclide transfer from phosphogypsum-amended soils to crops remains negligible, with no significant accumulation in edible plant parts under typical agricultural doses.[64] In Brazil's Cerrado region, phosphogypsum application has been adopted for sugarcane cultivation to neutralize subsoil acidity and enhance phosphorus efficiency, contributing to sustained yields on weathered tropical soils.[62][65] Despite these benefits, global agricultural utilization of phosphogypsum does not exceed 15%, constrained primarily by high transportation costs and stack proximity to farms.[60]

Construction and Industrial Materials

Phosphogypsum has been evaluated for use as a road base stabilizer due to its gypsum content providing binding properties similar to natural aggregates. In February 2025, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency approved a pilot project by The Mosaic Company in Florida to construct test road sections incorporating phosphogypsum, following a December 2024 federal notice outlining four segments with varying phosphogypsum mixtures in the base to assess performance.[66][67] Construction began in October 2025, utilizing approximately 1,200 tons of phosphogypsum excavated from an existing stack and relocated less than a mile for the segments.[68][69] In building applications, phosphogypsum is processed via calcination or purification to produce plasters, boards, and tiles, substituting for natural gypsum in cementitious materials. Peer-reviewed studies confirm that treated phosphogypsum yields binders with compressive strengths and setting times viable for structural elements, such as panels and mortars, when impurities are minimized through flotation or grinding.[70][48] This recycling approach lowers energy demands and emissions compared to virgin gypsum mining, as phosphogypsum requires less intensive processing for hemihydrate conversion.[71] Regulatory constraints limit widespread U.S. adoption, with EPA rules under 40 CFR Part 61 generally banning phosphogypsum in construction absent demonstrated equivalent protection to stacking, due to elevated radionuclides like radium-226.[4] Approvals occur via variances for low-exposure scenarios, as in the Mosaic road test.[66] In contrast, China employs phosphogypsum in gypsum block production through patented dehydration methods, while European research integrates it into fire-resistant plasters, achieving physicomechanical properties meeting standards for non-load-bearing walls.[72][73]

Resource Recovery and Emerging Technologies

Phosphogypsum, a byproduct of wet-process phosphoric acid production, contains recoverable valuables such as rare earth elements (REEs) at concentrations typically ranging from 0.05% to 0.3% by weight, varying by phosphate rock origin, alongside purifiable gypsum and fluoride compounds.[74] Emerging hydrometallurgical processes, including sulfuric or hydrochloric acid leaching combined with pretreatment like gravity separation or roasting, enable REE extraction efficiencies up to 75%, producing a purified gypsum residue while minimizing wastewater generation.[75] For instance, the REEPS system integrates acid leaching with solvent extraction to remediate phosphogypsum stacks and yield marketable REE concentrates, addressing both environmental liabilities and supply chain vulnerabilities for critical minerals used in electronics and renewable energy technologies.[76] Gypsum purification technologies focus on impurity removalâ€”such as organics, phosphates, and and reduced fluoride).[77] Concurrently, fluoride recovery from phosphogypsum leachates employs sequential precipitation or adsorption, achieving up to 98.8% fluoride extraction as calcium fluoride or hydrofluoric acid (HF), which can be valorized in chemical manufacturing while enabling closed-loop sulfate and phosphate recycling.[78] These methods, often integrated in pilot-scale operations, to Inventory," advocates a circular economy framework by reclassifying phosphogypsum as a resource stockpile, projecting full utilization feasibility by 2035 through scaled recovery of REEs, purified gypsum, and other elements, potentially offsetting global stockpiles exceeding 6 billion tons.[36] Innovations such as ammonia-enhanced CO2 sequestration coupled with low-temperature SO2 generation site-specific adaptation to phosphogypsum variability and economic viability assessments, with ongoing research emphasizing energy-efficient leaching to compete with primary mining.[81]

Environmental and Health Impacts

Radiological Components and Exposure Pathways

Phosphogypsum contains elevated concentrations of radionuclides from the uranium-238 decay series, predominantly radium-226, which serves as the parent isotope for radon-222. In U.S. phosphogypsum stacks, radium-226 levels average 18.6 pCi/g, with individual samples ranging from 6.3 to 27.9 pCi/g, exceeding typical natural soil concentrations of 0.5â€“5 pCi/g by factors of 3 to over 50.[15][82] These levels originate from the phosphate rock feedstock, where uranium and its decay products concentrate during phosphoric acid production, with radium-226 partitioning preferentially into the gypsum byproduct.[18] Radium-226 has a half-life of 1,600 years, enabling long-term persistence in phosphogypsum deposits, while its decay product, radon-222 (half-life 3.8 days), is a noble gas prone to diffusion. Radium exhibits low mobility in soils and sediments due to strong adsorption onto clay minerals and hydroxides, limiting its transport except under low-pH or high-ionic-strength conditions that reduce sorption efficiency.[83][84] Primary radiological exposure pathways from phosphogypsum stacks are:
  • Radon-222 inhalation: Emanation from stack surfaces elevates ambient air concentrations near facilities, with measurements indicating averages around 0.4 pCi/L above background over stack areas.[85][86]
  • Dust inhalation: Airborne particulate matter resuspended by wind carries adsorbed radium-226 and other series nuclides, facilitating direct respiratory uptake.[86]
  • Groundwater contamination: Leaching of soluble radium into aquifers occurs slowly due to limited solubility and retardation by soil matrices, though process water infiltration can enhance mobility in unlined stacks.[87][88][84]

Non-Radiological Contaminants

Phosphogypsum harbors non-radiological contaminants including heavy metals, residual phosphates, fluorides, and acidic residues, which originate from impurities in the phosphate rock feedstock and the wet-process phosphoric acid production involving sulfuric acid digestion.[89] These pollutants can leach from storage stacks or application sites, particularly under acidic conditions, potentially contaminating soil, groundwater, and surface waters.[17] Leaching potentials vary with environmental factors like pH, rainfall infiltration, and stack integrity, with routine monitoring of stack leachates often showing concentrations below acute toxicity thresholds but elevated relative to background levels in unmanaged scenarios.[90] Heavy metals such as cadmium (Cd), lead (Pb), arsenic (As), chromium (Cr), zinc (Zn), copper (Cu), and nickel (Ni) occur at trace levels, with concentrations influenced by phosphate rock provenance. Worldwide data indicate ranges of Cd at 0.8â€“40 mg/kg, Pb at 0.5â€“16 mg/kg, As at 1â€“42 mg/kg, Cr at 1.6â€“75 mg/kg, Zn at 4â€“315 mg/kg, Cu at 2â€“195 mg/kg, and Ni at 1.7â€“250 mg/kg.[91] In specific samples, such as Moroccan phosphogypsum, Cr, Pb, Ni, Zn, and Cu ranged from 0.2â€“243 mg/kg.[92] These metals exhibit pH-dependent mobility, with enhanced solubility and transport in acidic leachates (pH < 4), though precipitation and sorption reduce bioavailability at neutral pH.[93] Residual phosphates, present as soluble P compounds, pose eutrophication risks upon runoff or infiltration into aquatic systems, where they stimulate algal overgrowth and oxygen depletion, exacerbating hypoxia in phosphorus-limited waters.[94] Leachable phosphate fractions can reach several percent of total P content, with studies confirming release mechanisms tied to dissolution under percolating rainwater.[95] Fluorides, concentrated at 0.5â€“2% by weight (primarily as hydrofluosilicates and free HF), leach preferentially in water-soluble forms, threatening aquatic biota through bioaccumulation and inhibiting enzyme activity at concentrations above 1â€“2 mg/L.[96][97] Acidity arises from residual phosphoric and sulfuric acids, yielding initial pH values of 2â€“3 in fresh phosphogypsum, which can acidify receiving aquifers and mobilize other contaminants if stacks breach.[98][23] Neutralization practices, such as lime addition, elevate pH to 6â€“8, curtailing metal and fluoride solubility, though incomplete treatment sustains chronic low-level risks in leachates.[99] Empirical assessments of stack breaches demonstrate amplified impacts, with pH drops to below 4 correlating to spikes in total dissolved solids and pollutant plumes extending kilometers downgradient.[17]

Storage and Management Practices

Phosphogypsum Stack Systems

Phosphogypsum stack systems, commonly referred to as gyp stacks, consist of above-ground impoundments designed to store the large volumes of phosphogypsum generated as a byproduct of phosphoric acid production. These structures are engineered as multi-phase facilities where phosphogypsum slurry is deposited, allowing solids to settle while process wastewater is retained in surface ponds. In Florida, which hosts approximately 25 such stacks containing about 1 billion tons of phosphogypsum, these systems are constructed using dikes formed from the phosphogypsum itself to progressively raise the stack height.[107][108] Design specifications emphasize containment and structural integrity, with modern stacks required to incorporate composite liners, including a minimum 1.5 mm high-density polyethylene (HDPE) geomembrane over a low-permeability soil layer, to minimize leachate migration into groundwater. Stack heights vary by site but commonly reach 30 to 60 meters, with dike crests maintained at widths of at least 18 feet and exterior slopes no steeper than 2 horizontal to 1 vertical for heights exceeding 50 feet. Leachate collection and removal systems are integrated beneath the liner to monitor and manage any downward seepage, ensuring compliance with environmental containment standards.[109][110][111] Operationally, process wastewaterâ€”acidic and laden with residual phosphoric acid and impuritiesâ€”is co-stored in ponds atop the stack, where it undergoes recirculation back to the production plant to recover additional phosphoric acid values through enhanced P2O5 extraction efficiency. This wet stacking method contrasts with dry deposition by maintaining hydraulic head for ongoing slurry settling and water management, with rainfall and drainage captured for reuse to minimize freshwater demands. Hydrology is managed through perimeter ditches and pumps to control pond levels and prevent overflow, while interstitial water within the phosphogypsum matrix contributes to the overall liquid inventory.[112][113] Stability engineering addresses risks from fluid saturation, potential liquefaction under dynamic loading, and regional seismicity through geotechnical assessments, including limit equilibrium and finite element analyses that confirm factor-of-safety margins exceeding 1.3-1.5 under static and pseudo-static seismic conditions in low-hazard areas like Florida. Berms and dikes are compacted in lifts during construction, with internal drainage layers incorporated to relieve pore pressures, and ongoing monitoring via piezometers and inclinometers ensures long-term integrity against settlement or slope failure.[114][111]

Historical Spills and Remediation Efforts

In April 2021, a breach in the liner of a 77-acre wastewater retention pond atop a phosphogypsum stack at the former Piney Point phosphate processing facility in Manatee County, Florida, prompted the emergency release of approximately 215 million gallons of acidic process wastewater into Tampa Bay over a 10-day period from March 30 to April 9 to avert structural collapse.[115][116] The discharge, laden with nitrogen, phosphorus, and heavy metals, created a low-pH plume that contributed to localized fish kills and algal blooms, with causal factors including liner degradation and inadequate capacity to handle prior heavy rainfall accumulation.[117] Earlier incidents in Florida highlighted recurring vulnerabilities in phosphogypsum stack integrity. In September 2016, a sinkhole formed beneath a Mosaic Fertilizer phosphogypsum stack at its New Wales facility in Mulberry, Polk County, allowing over 200 million gallons of contaminated wastewater to infiltrate the underlying aquifer, driven by karst geology and subsurface dissolution rather than surface breach.[118][119] Between 2016 and 2020, multiple smaller leaks and structural concerns at Florida phosphogypsum sites, including liner tears and pond overflows, were reported amid ongoing heavy precipitation events, exacerbating risks from elevated water levels in unlined or aging stacks.[120] Remediation at Piney Point post-2021 involved intensive dredging to remove sediments and lower pond levels, alongside installation of an injection well system for dewatering, reducing stored volumes to about 193 million gallons by mid-2025 through targeted excavation and wastewater transfer.[121] Neutralization efforts included chemical treatment of effluents to mitigate acidity before further discharge or containment, coupled with long-term groundwater monitoring to track contaminant migration.[122] Similar approaches, such as sinkhole sealing and perimeter diking, were applied at the 2016 Mosaic site to isolate affected aquifers, though full restoration remains challenged by persistent subsurface flows.[119] These events underscored causal roles of extreme rainfall overwhelming stack drainage designs and Florida's karst terrain promoting sinkhole formation, prompting refinements in hydrological modeling for stack stability assessments.[123] Critiques persist regarding underestimation of rare, high-intensity precipitation in risk models, as historical data showed designs often failed to account for compounded storm events, leading to calls for probabilistic rainfall simulations incorporating climate variability.[120] As of September 2025, Piney Point's primary pond closure milestone was achieved, with indefinite monitoring protocols established to detect recurrence, reflecting incremental adaptations yet highlighting unresolved systemic exposure to hydrological extremes.[122][121]

In China, the world's largest producer of phosphogypsum, reuse rates stand at 30-40%, with applications in cement manufacturing, road base materials, and agricultural soil amendment, supported by product meeting buyer-specified low-radionuclide criteria not uniformly mandated domestically.[140] Globally, IAEA guidelines promote utilization over indefinite storage, advocating life-cycle assessments and residue management plans that integrate monitoring for radionuclides and heavy metals, though adoption varies: developing norms in high-production regions like Asia and Africa favor resource recovery with site-specific risk evaluations, diverging from precautionary bans in regions with mature environmental oversight.[138][141] This shift reflects empirical data on manageable risks at low-exposure reuse scenarios, tempered by national enforcement capacities.[142]

Sustainability and Future Prospects

Utilization Challenges and Rates

Global utilization rates of phosphogypsum remain low, with approximately 15% of annual production reused as of recent assessments, despite annual global output exceeding 200 million tons.[143] This figure contrasts with higher rates in select countries like China and Brazil, where reuse approaches 35-50% through targeted agricultural and construction applications, but global averages are constrained by inconsistent quality and economic barriers.[144] The resulting stockpiles have accumulated to over 6 billion tons worldwide, far outpacing niche market demands for gypsum-based materials.[143][145] Key challenges include high transportation costs, which can account for up to 10% of phosphoric acid production expenses when moving phosphogypsum to end-users, particularly in regions distant from production sites.[23] Logistical variability in phosphogypsum compositionâ€”stemming from differing phosphate rock sourcesâ€”further complicates standardization for reuse, as impurities such as heavy metals and radionuclides necessitate site-specific testing and processing.[22] These factors elevate purification and handling expenses, deterring widespread adoption beyond localized markets.[146] Market saturation exacerbates the issue, as phosphogypsum competes with abundant natural gypsum supplies, limiting demand in construction and cement sectors where purity standards are stringent.[147] Economic analyses indicate that while reuse can yield savings in stack maintenance and water management for producers, upfront investments in impurity mitigation often render it unviable at scale without subsidies or regulatory incentives.[37] Consequently, the mismatch between massive stockpiles and constrained utilization perpetuates environmental storage pressures without viable broad-market outlets.[134]

Circular Economy Strategies and Innovations

The International Fertilizer Association's 2025 report, "From Waste to Inventory," outlines strategies to elevate phosphogypsum from waste to a circular economy asset, proposing policy incentives such as tax credits and regulatory streamlining to achieve utilization rates exceeding 50% globally by integrating it into construction aggregates and soil amendments.[36][147] These pathways emphasize collaborative industry frameworks under the UN Resource Management System, targeting near-100% reuse through investments in processing technologies that separate contaminants while preserving gypsum's value in phosphate fertilizer supply chains.[144] Innovations in rare earth element (REE) extraction from phosphogypsum have advanced through 2025 pilots, including Rainbow Rare Earths' low-cost recovery process from stacks, yielding prospective production outside China with economic viability assessments confirming feasibility for ongoing phosphoric acid operations.[148] Complementary pretreatment strategies, such as acidic leaching enhanced by mechanical activation, enable selective REE recovery rates up to 90% while decontaminating the residue for safer reuse, as demonstrated in recent preprints evaluating pilot-scale asymmetric impeller columns.[149] Bio-remediation approaches, including microbial fermentation to produce bio-lixiviants, facilitate decontamination by precipitating radionuclides and heavy metals, with pilot plants achieving REE leaching efficiencies alongside transformation of phosphogypsum into cultivable anthrosol for agricultural applications.[150][151] These strategies link phosphogypsum valorization to fertilizer sustainability by recycling phosphorus byproducts, reducing reliance on virgin phosphate rock amid projected demand growth, yet empirical radiological neutrality dataâ€”verifying post-processing radium and radon levels below environmental thresholdsâ€”is essential to substantiate safety claims against stakeholder opposition rooted in precautionary concerns.[152] Peer-reviewed lifecycle assessments project that scaled innovations could cut disposal emissions by 20-30% while generating revenue from REEs, provided regulatory harmonization addresses site-specific contaminant variability.[135] Ongoing trials, such as reverse-direct flotation for zero-waste separation, further support prospects for full-stack reintegration, contingent on validated long-term field data.[153]
