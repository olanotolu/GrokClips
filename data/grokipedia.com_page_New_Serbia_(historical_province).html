New Serbia (historical province)

New Serbia was a short-lived military province created by the Russian Empire in 1752 on the steppe lands of southern Ukraine, primarily settled by Orthodox Serb migrants from Habsburg territories to serve as a frontier defense against nomadic incursions and Polish influences.[1][2] Led by Colonel Ivan Horvat, who petitioned Russian authorities for resettlement following the Austrian-Russian War of 1740–1748, the colony was established through imperial decrees in late 1751 and early 1752, organizing settlers into hussar and pandur regiments totaling around 16,000 personnel for border policing duties.[1][2] Located on the right bank of the Dnieper River in the modern Kirovohrad region, New Serbia featured fortifications like the St. Elizabeth fortress and faced ongoing tensions with neighboring Zaporozhian Cossacks over land and resources.[2] The province encountered internal challenges, including a 1760 rebellion and administrative corruption, before its dissolution in 1764 and incorporation into the broader Novorossiya Governorate, after which Serb settlers largely assimilated into the local Ukrainian population while contributing to Russian military expansion.[2][1]

Geography

Location and Boundaries

New Serbia was a military province of the Russian Empire established in 1752, situated in the steppe zone of what is now central Ukraine, primarily within the boundaries of modern Kirovohrad Oblast.[3] Its territory extended between the Inhul River to the west, which marked the border with lands of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, and the Dnieper River to the east.[3] This positioning placed it in the former Wild Fields (Dikoe Pole), a vast open plain historically vulnerable to raids by the Crimean Khanate.[3] The northern boundaries adjoined existing Russian administrative districts, while the southern frontier remained largely undefined and open to the Pontic steppe, facilitating its role as a buffer against southern incursions.[4] To the east, across the Dnieper, lay the related settlement of Slavo-Serbia, established in 1753, which extended further toward the Donets River basin. The province's demarcation was formalized through an imperial charter granting lands previously under the Buhogard palanka, emphasizing defensive militarization over fixed geopolitical lines.[4] These boundaries persisted until the province's dissolution in 1764, when its territories were reorganized into the New Russia Governorate.[3]

Origins and Establishment

Serb Migration from Habsburg Territories

In the early 1750s, dissatisfaction among Orthodox Serbs in the Habsburg Military Frontier prompted emigration to the Russian Empire, driven by centralizing reforms under Maria Theresa that curtailed religious autonomy and military privileges.[5] [6] These Grenzer (border guards) from regions including Slavonia, Syrmia, and the Banat faced increased taxation, enforced Catholic education, and diminished Orthodox ecclesiastical rights, leading figures like Colonel Jovan Horvat to seek alliances with Russia for preservation of their Orthodox faith and way of life.[7] Horvat, a Serb officer in Habsburg service, proposed recruiting fellow Serbs as military colonists to bolster Russia's southern defenses against Ottoman and Tatar threats, promising land grants, tax exemptions, and self-governance.[3] [8] The initial migration began in September 1751, when Horvat led a convoy of approximately 218 individuals—including 77 military personnel, their 82 family members, servants, and 59 civilians—across Habsburg territories to Kiev, arriving by October.[6] Horvat himself reached St. Petersburg later that year, securing Empress Elizabeth's approval for the venture, which aligned with Russia's strategy to populate and fortify the Pontic steppe.[7] Subsequent waves followed in 1752, drawing primarily from Habsburg Serb communities experienced in frontier warfare, with estimates of total initial settlers numbering in the thousands, though exact figures vary due to incomplete records.[8] The Habsburg court viewed this exodus as a demographic and military loss, interpreting it as a direct challenge to its mercantilist population policies and prompting diplomatic protests to Russia.[7] These migrants, often referred to as Vlach-Serbs or Grenzers, brought martial traditions from Habsburg service, including light infantry tactics suited to irregular warfare, which Russia leveraged to form autonomous Serbian regiments.[3] The move was not merely economic but ideologically rooted in pan-Orthodox solidarity, as Russia positioned itself as protector of Orthodox Slavs against both Catholic Habsburg and Muslim Ottoman pressures.[6] By 1752, the settlers had established foundational communities in the designated New Serbia territory, laying the groundwork for its administrative and military structure.[8]

Imperial Charter and Initial Settlement (1751–1752)

On December 29, 1751, Empress Elizabeth issued a manifesto inviting Orthodox Serbs and other South Slavs from Habsburg territories to settle in the Russian Empire's southern frontier, promising vast tracts of fertile steppe land between the Dnieper and Southern Bug rivers, tax exemptions for up to 30 years, religious freedom, and limited self-governance under military administration. This document, formalized in the Polnoe sobranie zakonov (PSZ) as part of colonization decrees, aimed to populate and defend the unsecured borders against nomadic incursions by providing colonists with arms, livestock, and plows from state stores.[9] The charter emphasized the settlers' role as a buffer force, requiring them to form hussar and infantry regiments for imperial service while retaining internal Orthodox customs and exemption from serfdom.[10] In response, Colonel Ivan Horvat, a Serb officer who had petitioned Russian authorities for asylum amid Habsburg religious and administrative pressures, was appointed as the provisional governor of the new territory on January 7, 1752, following a resolution by the Supreme Privy Council.[1] Horvat, arriving in Kiev with 121 officers and officials in October 1751, coordinated the vanguard of migrants fleeing Austrian Military Frontier obligations.[11] The initial contingent comprised approximately 620 families, totaling several thousand individuals primarily from Syrmia, Banat, and Slavonia regions, who traversed Ottoman and Polish lands under Russian protection. Settlement commenced in early 1752, with colonists allocated 60 desyatins (about 162 acres) of land per family along river valleys suitable for agriculture and fortification, establishing temporary stanitsas (military villages) such as Elizavetgrad (named after the empress) as administrative centers.[9] By mid-1752, these pioneers had begun clearing steppes, constructing wooden stockades, and organizing into five hussar regiments under Horvat's command, though logistical delays in provisioning led to early hardships including disease and supply shortages.[1] The territory, spanning roughly 3,000 square versts (about 3,240 square kilometers), was delimited by the Inhul River to the south and Bakhmut to the northeast, subordinating it directly to the Senate and Military Collegium while granting Horvat broad powers over civil and martial affairs.[7]

Governance and Administration

Autonomous Structure and Privileges

New Serbia was established as a semi-autonomous military settlement directly subordinated to the Russian Senate, granting its Serb colonists a degree of self-administration distinct from standard provincial governance.[12] Formed by imperial decree on 13 (24) July 1751 under Empress Elizabeth Petrovna, the territory encompassed a fortified strip approximately 64 km wide along the right bank of the Dnieper River, divided into military regiments and companies for border defense.[13] [12] Initial leadership was vested in Colonel Ivan Shevich (later General-Major Ivan Khorvat), appointed commander on 11 (22) January 1752, with oversight from figures like I.F. Glebov as chief commandant; by 1759, administration shifted to the Novomyrhorod Chancery for local judicial and fiscal matters.[14] [12] This structure emphasized military organization, with settlers formed into two initial regiments—hussar (cavalry) and pandur (infantry)—expanding to four by the late 1750s, each responsible for specific territorial sectors and fortifications like the Saint Elizabeth Fortress.[13] Privileges extended to the Orthodox Serb migrants included substantial land allocations to incentivize settlement and agriculture: officers received 27.3–55 hectares per household, while rank-and-file settlers obtained 11–16 hectares, alongside financial subsidies of 10 rubles per individual upon arrival.[12] Tax exemptions covered most duties and levies, with local revenues from "Novoserbian sums" funding internal needs and granting fiscal autonomy under Senate oversight, though settlers bore military obligations such as border patrols against Polish and Tatar incursions.[14] Religious freedoms permitted unrestricted Orthodox worship, construction of monasteries, and exclusion of non-Orthodox groups like Catholic Croats or Slovenes, reinforcing ethnic and confessional cohesion.[14] Trade privileges allowed duty-free internal commerce and, for merchants, foreign trade exemptions, fostering economic self-sufficiency.[14] These arrangements, codified in Senate resolutions of 29 December 1751 (9 January 1752), prioritized frontier defense over full integration, enabling New Serbia to operate as a self-governing enclave until reforms under Catherine II in 1764 curtailed its autonomy.[12] The system's emphasis on regimental self-organization mirrored Habsburg frontier models but adapted to Russian imperial needs, with colonists retaining internal disciplinary authority while contributing to campaigns like the Seven Years' War (1756–1763).[12]

Leadership and Internal Organization

New Serbia's leadership was military in character, with Colonel Ivan Khorvat (also known as Jovan Horvat), a Serb officer from Habsburg service, appointed by Empress Elizabeth I in 1752 as the chief commander responsible for recruiting settlers, organizing defenses, and administering the territory.[3][15] Horvat, granted the rank of major general, oversaw the initial migration of approximately 3,000 Serb families and other Balkan groups, directing the construction of the administrative center at Saint Elizabeth Fortress (present-day Kropyvnytskyi).[3][15] Internally, the province operated as a self-contained military corps, subdivided into two hussar regiments and two pandour (infantry) regiments to fulfill obligations of frontier defense against Ottoman and Crimean threats.[3][15] The hussar regiment, focused on cavalry, was headquartered at Novomyrhorod, while the pandour regiment handled foot soldiers from bases in Kryliv (later Svitlovodsk); each regiment comprised multiple companies under Serb subalterns who managed daily operations, land distribution, and villagization into about 50 settlements with Balkan-derived names such as Subotsi.[3] In 1754, a Slobidskyi Cossack regiment was incorporated on the southern border to enhance patrols and reconnaissance.[3] Regimental officers exercised local authority over colonists—primarily Serbs, Vlachs, Bulgarians, Greeks, and Montenegrins—who received hereditary land allotments (typically 60-100 desiatins per family) and privileges like tax exemptions and internal judicial autonomy in exchange for perpetual military service, with all able-bodied males aged 18-60 liable for duty.[15] This structure mirrored Habsburg Grenzer systems, emphasizing communal liability for regiment quotas, but Russian oversight via the Military Collegium limited full self-rule, requiring reports on recruitment and fortifications.[3] Horvat's tenure eroded due to allegations of authoritarianism, including fund embezzlement, withholding of pay, and punitive measures against dissenters, prompting investigations; he was arrested in 1764, exiled to Vologda, and stripped of command amid broader imperial reforms.[3][15] Subsequent interim leadership fell to regimental colonels until dissolution, highlighting tensions between granted autonomies and central control.[3]

Demographics and Society

Ethnic Composition and Population Dynamics

The initial settlers of New Serbia, arriving primarily between 1751 and 1752, were Orthodox migrants from the Habsburg Empire's Military Frontier, including Serbs, Vlachs (Romanian-speaking pastoralists often grouped administratively with Serbs due to shared faith and military roles), and smaller numbers of other South Slavs fleeing Ottoman incursions. These colonists numbered several thousand families, with estimates of 2,000–3,000 individuals from Balkan territories over the province's existence. Administrative records from 1757 record a total population of 5,482, broken down as approximately 75% Moldavians (predominantly Vlach settlers or migrants from the Principality of Moldavia), 12% Serbs (Slavic Orthodox), and 13% others, including local Cossacks and Russian military personnel. This composition highlights that self-identified or administratively designated Serbs formed a minority among the colonists, with Vlach/Moldavian elements dominating due to recruitment from frontier regions where ethnic boundaries were fluid under Habsburg Orthodox privileges. Ukrainian sources contend that up to two-thirds of the inhabitants were ethnic Ukrainians, incorporating Zaporozhian Cossacks and steppe peasants drawn into the military settlements for defense duties.[3] Population growth occurred through natural reproduction, limited further Balkan migrations, and integration of local nomadic and Cossack groups amid ongoing steppe conflicts with Tatars. By 1764, at dissolution, the province's inhabitants exceeded 44,000, reflecting rapid expansion in a frontier zone previously under sparse Cossack control. Ethnic dynamics involved tensions between colonists and locals, including clashes over land and autonomy, alongside gradual intermarriage and cultural blending under Russian oversight; Serbian historiography, as in Mita Kostić's analysis, stresses preserved Serb military traditions, while Ukrainian accounts highlight assimilation into Cossack and Little Russian (Ukrainian) structures.[16]

Social and Religious Structure

The social structure of New Serbia revolved around a militarized hierarchy, with settlers organized into hussar and pandur regiments totaling approximately 7,000 military personnel by the mid-1750s, supplemented by families numbering around 20,000–30,000 individuals overall. Leadership was vested in Serbian officers such as Major Ivan Horvat, who commanded the initial two hussar and two pandur regiments formed in 1752, granting them administrative authority over land distribution and local governance in exchange for border defense duties against Ottoman incursions. Enlisted settlers, drawn largely from Habsburg Military Frontier veterans, functioned as both farmers and soldiers, cultivating allotted plots while maintaining readiness for campaigns; this dual role fostered a patriarchal, regiment-based community where extended family units (zadrugas) provided the basic social cohesion under officer oversight.[15][1][17] Religiously, the colonists adhered to Eastern Orthodoxy, which served as a core element of their ethnic identity and communal organization, with priests integrating spiritual guidance into the military framework to sustain morale on the steppe frontier. Churches constructed from the 1750s onward, including wooden structures dedicated to saints like Peter and Paul, functioned as focal points for liturgy, baptisms, and social rites, bridging Serbian traditions with Russian imperial ecclesiastical authority. This Orthodox orientation, inherited from the migrants' Habsburg-era communities, reinforced solidarity amid isolation but gradually aligned with Moscow's synodal oversight, minimizing schisms despite linguistic and ritual variances.[18][19]

Economy and Military Role

Agricultural and Frontier Economy

The economy of New Serbia was predominantly agricultural, integrated with military service to sustain frontier colonization in the Russian steppe. Serbian settlers, fleeing Habsburg territories, were organized into regiments that functioned as a land militia, combining cultivation of granted lands with defense against Crimean Tatar and Nogai raids. This dual role, established by imperial decree on December 24, 1751, positioned the province as a buffer in the "Wild Fields," where economic activity focused on subsistence farming and pastoralism to support self-reliance and imperial expansion.[1][20] Land grants were allocated according to standards outlined in the 1751 decree and practical guidelines from January 11, 1752, enabling regiments to clear steppe terrain for arable use. Two hussar regiments and two pandur (infantry) regiments, each with around 4,000 personnel including grenadiers, received territories bounded by rivers and landmarks between the Dnieper and Southern Bug. Agriculture emphasized grain production on fertile chernozem soils and livestock rearing, though harsh conditions, including malaria and nomadic threats, limited output and caused significant settler attrition—initial cohorts of several thousand families dwindled rapidly.[1] As a frontier economy, New Serbia contributed to taming uncultivated steppes through Serbian military-agricultural settlements, aligning with Russian strategic goals of population influx and territorial control from 1752 to 1764. Privileges such as tax exemptions for service incentivized settlement, fostering rudimentary trade in surplus produce with nearby Cossack hosts, but the system's reliance on martial labor over commercial development constrained broader economic growth.[20][1]

Military Obligations and Contributions

The Serb colonists in New Serbia were established as military settlers, obligated to provide border defense services in exchange for land grants and exemptions from certain taxes and civilian duties. Under the imperial charter of December 24, 1751, and subsequent Senate decrees on December 29, 1751, and January 11, 1752, they were required to organize into a land militia comprising four regiments—two hussar and two pandur (irregular infantry)—each nominally consisting of 4,000 men, including 1,000 grenadiers per infantry unit.[1] This structure subordinated New Serbia directly to the Russian Senate and Military Collegium, functioning as a self-governing frontier entity focused on maintaining security along the southern Ukrainian steppe.[21] Military service was compulsory for able-bodied male settlers, who served as "border police" to patrol and fortify the region against incursions from nomadic groups such as the Crimean Tatars and potential Ottoman threats. The hussar regiments, drawn from experienced Serbian cavalry traditions, emphasized light cavalry tactics for reconnaissance and rapid response, while pandur units provided infantry support with grenadier elements for fortified defense.[1] Commanded initially by Major General Ivan Horvat, these forces integrated into the Russian Imperial Army's broader framework, receiving Russian officers for training and equipping, though retaining Serbian internal organization.[22] Contributions included bolstering Russia's southern frontier defenses during a period of expansion into the Pontic steppe, enabling the pacification of unsecured territories and facilitating further colonization. The regiments participated in local skirmishes and patrols that deterred raids, contributing to the stability that allowed agricultural development and Russian control consolidation before the Seven Years' War (1756–1763).[1] By 1764, elements of the New Serbian Hussars were reorganized into regular Russian units, such as the Black and Yellow Hussar Regiments, preserving their role in imperial military campaigns until further reforms.[22] This service underscored the settlers' value as a reliable, combat-hardened auxiliary force, recruited from Habsburg Military Frontier veterans skilled in irregular warfare.[7]

Dissolution and Aftermath

Catherine II's Reforms (1764)

In 1764, shortly after consolidating power, Catherine II enacted administrative reforms that dissolved the semi-autonomous status of New Serbia, integrating its territory into the newly established Novorossiya Governorate.[23] This restructuring ended New Serbia's direct subordination to the Russian Senate and Military Collegium, which had granted it special privileges since its founding in 1752, including self-governance under a elected kniaz and exemptions from certain imperial taxes in exchange for frontier defense duties.[24] The reform aligned with broader efforts to centralize control over the southern borderlands, merging New Serbia's approximately 2,400 square miles (including settlements like Elizavetgrad) with adjacent areas such as Slavo-Serbia and parts of the Wild Fields into a single guberniya under a governor-general appointed by St. Petersburg.[25] The dissolution curtailed the colonists' military autonomy, reassigning their hussar and infantry units to regular Russian army commands while preserving obligations for border patrols against Ottoman and Crimean threats.[23] Although some land tenure rights and religious freedoms for the Orthodox Serbian population were retained, the loss of fiscal privileges—such as duty-free trade and reduced corvée labor—prompted petitions from local leaders like former kniaz Rafael Horvat, who argued for maintaining the original charter to sustain settler loyalty.[26] Catherine's decree emphasized uniformity in imperial governance, reflecting a shift from ad hoc frontier experiments under Elizabeth I to standardized provincial administration, though implementation faced resistance due to the settlers' entrenched ethnic-military identity.[24] These changes marked the effective end of New Serbia as a distinct entity by late 1764, with its administrative center at Elizavetgrad repurposed as a key district in the guberniya; population estimates from the period indicate around 30,000 inhabitants, predominantly Serbs and Vlachs, were now subject to guberniya-level taxation and conscription quotas.[25] The reform's rationale, as articulated in imperial manifests, prioritized fiscal efficiency and strategic consolidation ahead of potential conflicts with the Ottoman Empire, though it diluted the incentives that had drawn Balkan migrants two decades earlier.[23]

Integration into Broader Russian Administration

Following the ukase of May 27, 1764, issued by Catherine II, New Serbia lost its autonomous status as a military frontier directly subordinated to the Senate and Military Collegium, with its territories reorganized into the newly established Novorossiya Governorate (also known as New Russia Governorate).[23] This integration aimed to standardize provincial administration across the southern frontier, subsuming the special privileges granted to Serb settlers—such as self-governance through elected colonels and exemption from certain taxes—under a uniform guberniya framework modeled on Peter the Great's collegiate system.[25] The governorate, headed by a governor-general appointed by the empress, encompassed former New Serbia lands between the Dnieper and Southern Bug rivers, alongside adjacent areas from Slavo-Serbia and Zaporozhian territories, with initial administrative centers at Yekaterinoslav (modern Dnipro) and other fortified posts.[27] Administrative restructuring divided the former New Serbia into multiple districts (uyezds), transitioning from ethnic-based regimental organization to territorial subdivisions under Russian civil and military officials, thereby dissolving the New Serbian Corps and reallocating its hussar and infantry units into regular Imperial Army formations.[25] Local governance shifted to appointed voivodes and land captains, enforcing centralized tax collection, conscription, and land surveys, which revoked communal land tenure in favor of individual allotments registered in state cadastres.[27] This process encountered resistance from Serb elites, who petitioned for retention of Orthodox privileges and officer ranks, but Catherine's reforms prioritized imperial uniformity, leading to the relocation of some dissenting elements to the Danube region or integration as rank-and-file soldiers.[23] By 1765, the Novorossiya Governorate's structure included separate districts for military settlers and civilian populations, with New Serbia's core areas—such as Novomyrhorod and fortresses along the Inhul River—fully incorporated, facilitating broader colonization by Russian, Ukrainian, and other ethnic groups under the governorate's oversight.[25] These changes extended Catherine's 1775 Provincial Statute reforms southward in subsequent years, subdividing the governorate into smaller provinces like Yekaterinoslav and Voznesensk, further eroding residual Serb autonomies through elected noble assemblies dominated by non-Serb landowners.[27] The integration enhanced fiscal control, yielding increased revenues from agricultural tithes and frontier duties, but diluted the distinct military-agricultural identity of the Serb colonies, aligning them with the empire's expansionist priorities in the Pontic steppe.[23]

Legacy and Historiography

Linguistic and Cultural Remnants

The Serb colonists of New Serbia, numbering around 5,000 families by 1764, underwent rapid linguistic assimilation into the surrounding Ukrainian and Russian-speaking populations following the province's dissolution, with Serbian (a South Slavic language) leaving no documented impact on Ukrainian dialects, which belong to the East Slavic branch and exhibit mutual intelligibility challenges with Serbian.[28] Persistent linguistic traces are primarily onomastic, manifesting in Serbian-derived surnames prevalent in former New Serbia territories like modern Kirovohrad Oblast and adjacent regions, where intermarriage preserved patronymic elements despite language shift.[29] Toponyms also retain subtle Serbian imprints from the settlement era; settlers adapted local names such as rendering Luhansk as "Lugansko" and establishing "Subottsi" after the Serbian town of Subotica, though many original Serbian designations faded under imperial Russification.[15] Culturally, remnants are indirect and attenuated, evident in anecdotal genetic ancestry claims linking some eastern Ukrainians to Serb migrants, but Orthodox religious practices and frontier militarism introduced by the hussar regiments blended seamlessly with Cossack traditions, yielding no distinct artifacts or folklore isolable from broader Ukrainian-Slavic synthesis by the 19th century.[29][15]

Interpretations in Serb, Russian, and Ukrainian Narratives

In Serb historiography, New Serbia is portrayed as a pivotal chapter in the Serbian Great Migration of the 18th century, where approximately 3,000-5,000 Serb families, primarily from Habsburg territories, relocated under leaders like Jovan Šević to establish military settlements in the Russian Empire's southern frontier between the Dnieper and Southern Bug rivers from 1751 onward.[17] This narrative emphasizes the Serbs' agency in seeking Orthodox Russian protection against Habsburg Catholic pressures and Ottoman threats, framing the colony as an extension of Serbian martial tradition and a bulwark for Slavic Orthodoxy, with settlers granted privileges like self-governance and land allotments in exchange for border defense duties. Historians such as Mita Kostić highlight its role in preserving Serbian ethnic and cultural identity amid diaspora, viewing dissolution in 1764 not as failure but as assimilation into broader Russian Slavic domains, underscoring enduring Serb contributions to imperial security.[4] Russian historical interpretations present New Serbia as a pragmatic instrument of imperial frontier policy under Empress Elizabeth, established via ukase on January 28, 1752, to populate and fortify the Wild Fields against Crimean Tatar raids and Ottoman incursions, integrating loyal Serb Orthodox migrants as hussar regiments totaling over 1,200 fighters by 1754.[7] This view, evident in works linking it to the Eastern Question's origins, casts the settlement as a strategic success in Slavic colonization, fostering pan-Slavic unity under Russian aegis and preempting Habsburg influence, with Serb autonomies seen as temporary concessions yielding military dividends like campaigns in the 1768-1774 Russo-Turkish War.[7] Russian accounts, such as those in imperial archival analyses, downplay ethnic particularism, interpreting integration into Novorossiya Governorate post-1764 as natural evolution toward centralized efficiency, crediting it with stabilizing the Pontic steppe for agricultural and defensive expansion.[30] Ukrainian narratives, particularly in studies of southern Ukraine's colonization, interpret New Serbia as an exogenous Russian imposition on Cossack steppe lands, where Serb influxes from 1752 disrupted indigenous Zaporozhian and proto-Ukrainian pastoral economies by claiming vast tracts—up to 50,000 desyatins—for military farms, often at the expense of local tenures.[3] Historians like Natalia Polons'ka-Vasylenko frame it within broader imperial settlement schemes (1750-1775) that subordinated Ukrainian autonomies, viewing Serb privileges—such as exemption from hetman oversight—as tools of Russification that marginalized native populations and foreshadowed the 1775 Zaporozhian Sich liquidation.[7] This perspective, drawn from regional ethnogenesis accounts, critiques the colony's short lifespan as evidence of unsustainable foreign militarization, with lingering Serb toponyms (e.g., in Kirovohrad Oblast) seen as relics of colonial layering rather than organic integration, informed by awareness of Russian historiography's tendency to prioritize imperial unity over peripheral ethnic disruptions.[17]
