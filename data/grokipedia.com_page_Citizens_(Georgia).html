Citizens (Georgia)

Citizens (Georgian: მოქალაქეები), also known as the Citizens Party, is a liberal opposition political party in the Republic of Georgia founded in 2020 and led by Aleko Elisashvili, a former Tbilisi city councilor who ran as an independent candidate in the 2017 Tbilisi mayoral election.[1][2] The party emerged as part of Georgia's fragmented opposition landscape, emphasizing pro-European Union integration and resistance to Russian influence amid the country's geopolitical tensions.[3] It participated in the 2020 parliamentary elections shortly after its establishment, contributing to the multi-party contest where nine opposition lists crossed the electoral threshold, though the ruling Georgian Dream retained power amid subsequent disputes over the vote's integrity.[4] Citizens has since aligned with coalitions like Strong Georgia, uniting with groups such as Lelo for Georgia and For the People to challenge the incumbent government's policies on foreign agents legislation and EU accession. A defining characteristic is Elisashvili's frontline involvement in Ukraine's defense against Russia's 2022 invasion, which prompted Russian authorities to issue an arrest warrant and impose a six-year prison sentence on him in absentia for alleged mercenary activities—actions the party frames as solidarity with Ukraine rather than criminal conduct.[5][3] This episode underscores the party's staunch anti-Russian stance, contrasting with criticisms of the ruling party's perceived softening toward Moscow.[6] While lacking major electoral breakthroughs, Citizens represents a voice for urban, pro-Western voters disillusioned with established opposition dynamics, often prioritizing direct civic engagement over traditional party machinery.[7]

History

Founding and Establishment (2020)

The Citizens party (Georgian: მოქალაქეები) was founded on August 4, 2020, by Aleko Elisashvili, a former independent deputy in the Tbilisi City Council (Sakrebulo). Elisashvili announced the party's establishment during a press conference in Tbilisi, emphasizing its role as a new political force independent of established coalitions. The formation occurred amid Georgia's preparations for the October 2020 parliamentary elections, with Elisashvili declaring his candidacy as the party's majoritarian contender in the Saburtalo district of Tbilisi. Levan Ioseliani, a childhood friend and associate of Elisashvili, emerged as a key early figure in the party, contributing to its initial organizational efforts. The party's founding manifesto outlined priorities such as economic growth, poverty reduction, and unemployment alleviation, framing Citizens as a citizen-focused alternative to the dominant Georgian Dream party and fragmented opposition.[8] This positioning reflected Elisashvili's prior experience as an anti-corruption activist and independent councilor, where he had gained visibility through public protests against municipal governance issues. Registration with Georgia's Central Election Commission followed the announcement, enabling the party's participation in the 2020 elections as a standalone entity rather than aligning with larger blocs like the United National Movement-led alliance. Initial membership drew from civic activists and disillusioned voters seeking reform outside traditional party lines, though the party secured only one parliamentary seat—held by Elisashvili—via proportional representation in the contested vote.[2] The establishment marked a deliberate break from prior affiliations, with Elisashvili citing the need for authentic representation untainted by elite interests.

Early Opposition Activities and 2021 Parliamentary Engagement

Following its founding in August 2020, the Citizens party positioned itself as an opposition force against the ruling Georgian Dream coalition, emphasizing liberal reforms and criticism of alleged electoral irregularities. The party rapidly prepared for the October 31, 2020, parliamentary elections, submitting its candidate list led by Aleko Elisashvili to the Central Election Commission on October 13, 2020.[9] Although it garnered insufficient votes to secure seats independently in the proportional representation system, where Georgian Dream dominated with 48.66% of the vote, Citizens aligned initially with broader opposition efforts challenging the results amid widespread fraud allegations.[4] In the ensuing political crisis, most opposition parties boycotted the new parliament, refusing recognition of the election outcomes. Citizens diverged from this stance; on January 29, 2021, Elisashvili announced an agreement with Georgian Dream, enabling the party to enter parliament with two seats allocated to Elisashvili and Levan Ioseliani as compensation for perceived electoral shortcomings.[10] This memorandum committed Georgian Dream to electoral reforms, including changes to the election code and judiciary appointments, which Citizens endorsed to facilitate oversight from within the legislature rather than external protest.[11] Once seated, Elisashvili and Ioseliani formed the "Aleko Elisashvili – Citizens" parliamentary group, actively participating in the 2021 spring and extraordinary sessions. The group engaged in legislative debates, tabling proposals on transparency and anti-corruption measures, while receiving state funding of 497,406 GEL for operations.[12] This engagement drew criticism from boycotting opposition factions, who labeled the deal a political accommodation, yet Citizens maintained it advanced democratic accountability through institutional presence over street protests.[13]

Developments from 2022 to 2024 Elections

During 2022 and 2023, Citizens maintained its presence in the Georgian Parliament as an opposition faction, with MPs including leader Aleko Elisashvili participating in legislative processes and supporting select cross-party initiatives, such as candidate nominations for parliamentary roles.[14] The party positioned itself against the ruling Georgian Dream's governance, aligning with broader opposition efforts amid ongoing political tensions following the 2021 elections.[15] In mid-2024, ahead of the October parliamentary elections, Citizens engaged in negotiations to consolidate opposition forces and formally joined the Strong Georgia coalition on August 13, 2024, alongside Lelo for Georgia and other pro-European parties, aiming to challenge Georgian Dream's dominance.[7] This alliance, led by Lelo's Mamuka Khazaradze, reconciled prior rivalries and focused on EU integration and anti-corruption platforms.[16] Citizens contested the October 26, 2024, parliamentary elections as part of Strong Georgia, which secured 8.81% of the vote and 14 seats in the 150-seat legislature according to official results from the Central Election Commission.[17] The elections occurred amid international observer reports of an uneven playing field and procedural concerns, though voters had a wide choice of options.[18] The coalition's performance reflected modest gains for unified opposition lists compared to fragmented 2020 results.[19]

Role in Post-2024 Political Crisis

Following the October 26, 2024, parliamentary elections, in which the ruling Georgian Dream party secured an official 53.93% of the vote amid opposition allegations of systematic fraud, the Citizens party rejected the results as illegitimate.[20] As part of the Strong Georgia coalition, which it joined on August 13, 2024, Citizens aligned with pro-European opposition forces in demanding the annulment of the vote and new elections under international supervision.[16] Leader Aleko Elisashvili, a co-leader of the coalition, publicly criticized the Central Election Commission's certification process, citing discrepancies between exit polls showing Georgian Dream below 30% and official tallies.[21] Citizens contributed to the mass protests that erupted on October 28, 2024, in Tbilisi and other cities, where demonstrators waved EU flags and accused the government of electoral manipulation to derail Georgia's European integration.[22] Elisashvili participated in these rallies, framing the crisis as a defense of democratic norms against authoritarian consolidation.[22] The party's involvement bolstered the opposition's unified front, which sustained demonstrations through the winter despite declining turnout and government countermeasures, including water cannons and arrests.[23] In November 2024, Citizens joined the opposition boycott of the new parliament convened on November 25, refusing to recognize its legitimacy and escalating calls for President Salome Zourabichvili to dissolve the body.[24] This stance persisted into 2025 amid heightened repression, with Elisashvili facing a Russian in absentia sentence in August for prior military support to Ukraine, highlighting the party's anti-Russian positioning amid the crisis.[5] Observers noted irregularities like voter intimidation and media bias, though no international body called for outright invalidation, underscoring the contentious nature of the opposition's claims.[25] By October 2025, as Georgian Dream dominated municipal elections, Citizens' role shifted toward sustaining civil resistance against perceived democratic erosion.[26]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Shift from Centrist to Liberal Orientation

The Citizens party, established on August 4, 2020, by urban activist Aleko Elisashvili, initially positioned itself as a pragmatic, citizen-focused alternative emphasizing anti-corruption efforts and local governance reforms, appealing to voters seeking a non-ideological, centrist option amid polarization between the ruling Georgian Dream and established opposition groups.[27] This founding orientation drew from Elisashvili's background in civic activism, including protests against urban development abuses, prioritizing practical solutions over partisan divides.[1] By 2022–2024, amid escalating political tensions and the Georgian Dream's perceived authoritarian drift, Citizens increasingly aligned with pro-European opposition platforms, adopting stances on rule of law, judicial independence, and civil liberties that align with liberal democratic principles.[16] A key marker of this evolution occurred on August 13, 2024, when Elisashvili's party joined the Strong Georgia coalition, partnering with the explicitly liberal Lelo for Georgia to contest parliamentary elections, signaling a strategic embrace of liberal foreign policy integration and domestic reforms over isolated centrism.[7] This shift reconciled prior tensions with Lelo leader Mamuka Khazaradze and reflected broader opposition convergence toward EU-aligned values, though Citizens retained focus on grassroots accountability.[2] Critics from more nationalist opposition factions have attributed this orientation change to electoral pragmatism rather than ideological conviction, noting Citizens' small vote share—1.33% in 2020—necessitated alliances for viability.[28] Nonetheless, the party's post-alliance emphasis on human rights advocacy, including Elisashvili's public support for Ukrainian resistance against Russia, underscores a causal pivot toward liberal internationalism driven by Georgia's geopolitical vulnerabilities.[6] Empirical polling data from the period shows limited but growing support among urban, educated demographics favoring such positions, contrasting with Georgian Dream's conservative base.[29]

Stances on Foreign Policy and EU Integration

The Citizens party prioritizes Georgia's integration into Euro-Atlantic institutions as a foundational element of its foreign policy, explicitly supporting constitutional commitments to pursue European Union membership and NATO accession. Party leader Aleko Elisashvili has advocated for opposition consolidation to enable "smooth" advancement toward European integration, positioning it as essential for national progress amid perceived ruling party obstructions.[2] Citizens has vocally opposed legislative measures perceived to undermine EU candidacy, including the "transparency of foreign influence" law adopted on May 14, 2024, which mirrored Russian models and prompted the European Commission to recommend suspending accession talks initiated after candidate status was granted in December 2023. Elisashvili participated prominently in parliamentary confrontations and street protests against the bill, framing it as a barrier to Western alignment.[30][31] In relations with Russia, Citizens maintains a confrontational posture, rejecting extraditions of Georgian nationals to Moscow and defending volunteers fighting Russian forces in Ukraine. Elisashvili, who personally volunteered in Ukraine and faces a Russian arrest warrant issued August 2025 for alleged mercenary activity, demanded in September 2025 that the Foreign Ministry safeguard such individuals from reprisals, underscoring the party's view of Russia as a direct security threat.[32][3] Through its inclusion in the pro-European Strong Georgia coalition for the October 2024 parliamentary elections, Citizens aligns with platforms emphasizing reduced Russian geopolitical leverage, including post-2022 influx controls, while critiquing Georgian Dream's pivot toward neutralism as eroding public support for EU paths—polls consistently show over 80% Georgian backing for integration despite elite divergences.[2][33]

Domestic Priorities Including Rule of Law and Anti-Corruption

The Citizens party identifies combating elite corruption as a central domestic priority, arguing that systemic graft among political and business elites undermines public trust and economic opportunity. Founded in 2020, the party has pledged to install "true, honest, and professional people" in governance roles, explicitly barring candidates with histories of tender-winning business dealings, frequent party affiliations, or other indicators of entrenched interests.[1] This stance reflects leader Aleko Elisashvili's self-described "one man's battle against the system," positioning the party as an outsider force dedicated to disrupting corrupt networks rather than perpetuating them.[1] On rule of law, Citizens advocates for institutional integrity to safeguard democratic processes, frequently criticizing the ruling Georgian Dream party's influence over the judiciary and electoral bodies as a threat to impartiality. Elisashvili and party members have participated in opposition efforts to demand judicial independence and transparent election administration, including negotiations for electoral reforms in 2021 that aimed to enhance oversight and reduce administrative manipulation.[11] However, the party's small parliamentary footprint—holding one seat post-2020 elections—has limited its legislative impact, leading to reliance on protests and alliances to amplify calls for vetting corrupt judges and prosecutors.[34] Persistent corruption in sectors like procurement and law enforcement, as noted in international assessments, aligns with Citizens' narrative of elite capture eroding legal accountability.[35] Elisashvili has reiterated an uncompromising anti-corruption ethos, declaring "no corruption, no enrichment, no tolerance" in response to perceived political manipulations, such as selective pardons and arrests targeting opposition figures.[36] These priorities intersect with broader domestic goals like fostering decent employment and education, which the party links causally to eradicating corruption's drag on resource allocation and merit-based advancement.[1] While lacking detailed policy blueprints in public records, Citizens' platform emphasizes preventive measures, such as candidate vetting, over reactive enforcement, distinguishing it from establishment parties accused of selective prosecutions.

Leadership and Organizational Structure

Key Founders and Current Leaders

Aleko Elisashvili established the Citizens party in 2020 as its founding leader, drawing from his prior experience as an independent member of the Tbilisi City Council.[1] He has chaired the party continuously, guiding its opposition activities against the ruling Georgian Dream coalition.[2] In August 2024, Elisashvili integrated Citizens into the Strong Georgia alliance, maintaining his leadership role amid ongoing protests and electoral disputes.[7] Levan Ioseliani emerged as a key early figure alongside Elisashvili, serving as a party MP and co-leader during initial parliamentary engagements.[37] Nominated by Citizens for the Public Defender position, Ioseliani was elected on March 7, 2023, with 96 votes in parliament, transitioning to this independent ombudsman role while retaining ties to the party's foundational principles.[38] [39] As of 2025, Elisashvili remains the primary current leader, actively representing Citizens in coalitions and facing external pressures, including a Russian in-absentia sentence on August 14, 2025, for his volunteer combat role in Ukraine.[5] The party's leadership structure emphasizes Elisashvili's central authority, with Ioseliani's ombudsman tenure marking a shift to institutional oversight rather than direct party management.[40]

Party Organization and Membership

The Citizens party operates as a voluntary political association under the Organic Law of Georgia on Political Unions of Citizens, which requires parties to maintain a general congress as the supreme body, a political council for executive functions, a chairman elected by the congress, and an auditing commission of at least three members elected for a term defined in the party charter. The party is chaired by founder Aleko Elisashvili, who has led it since its establishment on August 4, 2020.[27] Key organizational figures include parliamentary members Aleko Elisashvili and Levan Ioseliani, who secured mandates in the 2020 elections representing the party's core activist base.[41] Membership is restricted to Georgian citizens possessing electoral rights, with termination possible upon enlistment in another party or other statutory grounds, though no official membership figures have been publicly reported by the party. As a small opposition entity, the party's structure emphasizes centralized leadership around Elisashvili, with decisions often driven by its limited cadre of urban activists and former journalists rather than extensive regional branches.[42] Internal dynamics have seen occasional departures, such as a prominent member exiting in early 2021 citing personal differences, indicating a tight-knit but potentially fluid membership. The party has not disclosed details of regular congresses or auditing activities in public records, aligning with patterns observed in Georgia's smaller parties where formal compliance meets minimal transparency requirements.

Electoral Performance

Parliamentary Elections

In the 2020 parliamentary elections held on October 31, Citizens contested independently, securing 1.33% of the proportional vote (25,508 votes) and earning two proportional representation seats in the 150-seat parliament.[4] The party, led by Aleko Elisashvili, positioned itself as a liberal alternative emphasizing anti-corruption and civic engagement, but its modest performance reflected challenges in building broader voter support amid a fragmented opposition landscape dominated by Georgian Dream's 48% share.[4] These seats allowed Citizens representation in the 10th convocation of parliament until 2024, where Elisashvili served as an MP focusing on oversight of government accountability.[43] Ahead of the October 26, 2024, parliamentary elections—conducted under a fully proportional system with a 1% threshold—Citizens joined the Strong Georgia coalition in August 2024, alongside Lelo for Georgia and For People, aiming to consolidate centrist-liberal opposition votes against Georgian Dream.[16] The coalition, emphasizing EU integration and rule-of-law reforms, received 8.7-8.8% of the vote according to official Central Election Commission tallies with over 99% of precincts reporting, translating to 14 seats.[44][17] This outcome positioned Strong Georgia as the third-largest opposition bloc, behind Coalition for Change (10.8%) and Unity-National Movement (10%), though far short of displacing the ruling Georgian Dream's 53.93% and 89 seats.[17] The 2024 results sparked widespread disputes, with opposition parties including Strong Georgia rejecting the tallies as fraudulent, citing voter intimidation, ballot stuffing, and discrepancies between exit polls (showing opposition leads) and official figures; international observers like OSCE noted an uneven playing field, misuse of public resources by incumbents, and pressure on voters, though administration was efficient.[25][17] IRI's post-election assessment highlighted similar irregularities, including familial voting and inadequate safeguards against coercion, undermining public confidence in the process.[45] Strong Georgia leaders, including Elisashvili, boycotted the new parliament's convening, framing non-recognition as resistance to authoritarian consolidation rather than acceptance of manipulated outcomes.[17] Voter turnout reached 53.93%, lower than 2020's 56.97%, amid protests alleging Russian influence on Georgian Dream's pivot away from EU aspirations.[17]

Municipal and Local Elections

In the 2021 Georgian local elections held on October 2 and 30, the Citizens party, contesting independently under leader Aleko Elisashvili, participated in proportional representation contests across municipalities but secured no mayoral wins or significant council mandates. In Tbilisi, the party received 1.5% of the proportional vote.[46] Nationwide, Citizens failed to surpass the 4% threshold required for proportional seats in most local councils, reflecting its status as a newly formed entity with limited organizational reach beyond urban centers.[47] The elections occurred amid a polarized political environment following the disputed 2020 parliamentary vote, with international observers noting competitive but flawed processes marked by allegations of irregularities favoring the ruling Georgian Dream party.[48] Citizens' modest performance underscored challenges for smaller liberal parties in mobilizing voter support at the local level, where incumbency advantages and resource disparities hindered opposition gains. By the 2025 local elections on October 4, Citizens had integrated into the Strong Georgia coalition, comprising Lelo for Georgia, For the People, and other groups, enabling broader candidacy nominations in regions like Samegrelo, Imereti, and Guria.[16] Unlike nine major opposition parties that boycotted the vote in protest against the post-2024 parliamentary crisis and perceived electoral manipulations, Strong Georgia chose participation, nominating joint candidates to contest Georgian Dream's dominance.[49] Preliminary Central Election Commission results showed Georgian Dream capturing over 80% of the proportional vote and winning all mayoral races, with participating opposition lists like Strong Georgia relegated to negligible shares amid low turnout of around 33%.[50] [51] Coalition leaders later criticized the partial boycott by rivals as counterproductive, arguing it facilitated Georgian Dream's unchallenged sweep by depressing overall opposition participation.[52] This outcome further entrenched local governance under the ruling party, limiting Citizens' influence despite its strategic alliance.

Alliances and Opposition Dynamics

Collaborations with Other Anti-Georgian Dream Forces

In July 2024, Citizens initiated active negotiations to join an emerging opposition alliance led by Lelo for Georgia, amid efforts to consolidate anti-Georgian Dream forces ahead of the October parliamentary elections.[2] These discussions reconciled prior tensions between Citizens leader Aleko Elisashvili and Lelo founder Mamuka Khazaradze, facilitating a unified pro-European platform emphasizing rule-of-law reforms and opposition to the ruling party's foreign agents law.[7] On August 13, 2024, Citizens formally joined the Strong Georgia coalition, which also incorporated For Georgia and the Freedom Square movement, positioning the alliance as a centrist alternative to larger opposition blocs like the United National Movement-led groups.[16][7] The coalition contested the elections under this banner, securing seats in parliament despite Georgian Dream's victory, and has since pursued independent strategies, declining to fully participate in the broader opposition boycott announced by other anti-GD factions in 2025.[53] Strong Georgia's collaborative framework enabled joint campaign activities and shared advocacy against perceived electoral irregularities, though internal dynamics revealed divergences, with Citizens emphasizing pragmatic engagement over full parliamentary abstention to maintain leverage against the ruling coalition.[54] This alliance marked Citizens' primary structured partnership with other opposition entities, contrasting with ad hoc joint protests earlier in 2024 against the foreign agents legislation, where Elisashvili coordinated with diverse anti-GD lawmakers without formal coalition ties.[55]

Negotiations and Coalition Attempts

In July 2024, the Citizens party, led by Aleko Elisashvili, entered active negotiations to join the Lelo-led Strong Georgia opposition alliance ahead of the October parliamentary elections.[2] These discussions built on efforts to consolidate fragmented opposition forces against the ruling Georgian Dream party, amid concerns over electoral fairness and democratic backsliding.[7] The negotiations culminated on August 13, 2024, when Elisashvili formally joined the Strong Georgia coalition following a reconciliation with Lelo leader Mamuka Khazaradze, resolving years of prior tensions stemming from ideological and personal differences.[7] This merger incorporated Citizens into a broader alliance that included Lelo for Georgia and other smaller groups like For the People, aiming to present a unified electoral front with a focus on pro-European policies and anti-corruption measures.[16] Parallel attempts to expand the coalition further faltered, as the emerging Strong Georgia alliance, now including Citizens, failed to reach an agreement with former Prime Minister Giorgi Gakharia's For Georgia party by September 19, 2024.[56] Disagreements over leadership roles, candidate lists, and strategic priorities prevented a larger unified opposition bloc, contributing to the fragmented field in the October 26 elections where Strong Georgia ultimately secured limited seats before the opposition's boycott.[57] Post-election, amid ongoing protests and calls for new polls, Citizens remained aligned within Strong Georgia, with no reported successful negotiations for broader coalitions or government formation as of late 2024.[58] In July 2025, Strong Georgia's Lelo component signed a memorandum with For Georgia for municipal election cooperation, potentially extending to Citizens' involvement, though specific terms for the party's participation were not detailed.[58] These efforts reflect persistent challenges in opposition unity, often undermined by internal rivalries and differing visions for challenging Georgian Dream's dominance.

Controversies and Criticisms

Internal Party Disputes

In January 2021, amid the opposition boycott of the 2020 parliamentary elections, Citizens party MPs Aleko Elisashvili and Levan Ioseliani jointly announced their decision to accept their mandates and enter parliament, diverging from the broader opposition strategy of non-recognition. This move, framed as a pragmatic step to engage in legislative oversight, was executed without reported internal opposition within the party, which at the time held only these two seats.[59] Levan Ioseliani's election as Public Defender on March 7, 2023, with 96 parliamentary votes including majority support from Georgian Dream members, represented a cross-party compromise to resolve a prolonged vacancy. The process faced immediate backlash from 16 civil society organizations for insufficient public consultation and deviation from international standards on ombudsman selection. While Elisashvili did not publicly contest the nomination, Ioseliani's subsequent role has highlighted contrasts in approach: his restrained responses to controversies, such as delaying criticism of the foreign agents law despite appeals from over 400 NGOs and minimizing police actions during 2024 post-election protests, prompted a rare public dissent from 34 office staff members who accused him of failing to defend citizens' rights.[37][60] These developments underscore limited but notable tensions in strategic alignment within Citizens' leadership, with Elisashvili maintaining a more adversarial posture—evidenced by his April 2024 physical altercation with a ruling party MP over the foreign agents bill and his reported combat role in Ukraine. No formal resignations, expulsions, or factional splits have emerged, reflecting the party's compact structure and centralized control under Elisashvili since its 2020 founding.[61][5]

Accusations of Extremism and External Critiques

The ruling Georgian Dream party has labeled opposition factions, including Citizens, as radicals seeking to destabilize the country through protests and revolutionary tactics. Prime Minister Irakli Kobakhidze stated on October 23, 2025, that "radicals" had failed to block Tbilisi's Rustaveli Avenue for the first time in a year following the enactment of a new law restricting protests.[62] Similar rhetoric appeared in May 2024, when Kobakhidze accused civil society organizations and opposition figures of instigating violent demonstrations against the foreign influence transparency law, framing them as a "radical minority."[63] These claims align with broader Georgian Dream narratives portraying anti-government protests as orchestrated extremism rather than legitimate dissent, amid international concerns over the party's authoritarian measures.[64] A notable incident fueling such accusations occurred on April 15, 2024, when Citizens leader Aleko Elisashvili punched parliamentary majority leader Mamuka Mdinaradze during a committee debate on the foreign influence bill, an act Elisashvili later described as targeting Mdinaradze's "Russian face."[65] The confrontation, which escalated into a brawl, prompted ejection of Elisashvili from the session and criticism from Georgian Dream for undermining parliamentary decorum and inciting violence.[66] Supporters of Citizens viewed the event as a spontaneous response to perceived pro-Russian aggression by the ruling party, but detractors, including government-aligned media, cited it as evidence of opposition extremism.[67] External critiques of Citizens remain sparse and largely tied to intra-opposition dynamics rather than ideological extremism. International observers, such as those from the Journal of Democracy, have urged opposition parties like Citizens to unify against Georgian Dream's backsliding, implicitly critiquing fragmentation without labeling the party radical.[68] Russian authorities, however, charged Elisashvili in absentia in August 2024 with terrorism-related offenses for his participation in Ukraine's defense against the 2022 invasion, a move reflecting Moscow's pattern of targeting pro-Western Georgian figures amid strained bilateral ties.[69] Such accusations lack substantiation beyond geopolitical rivalry and contrast with Citizens' pro-EU, liberal platform, which prioritizes anti-corruption and democratic reforms over militant agendas.[70] Georgian Dream's domestic labeling of opposition as extremists appears motivated by efforts to justify crackdowns, as evidenced by U.S. sanctions on GD officials for human rights abuses against protesters, rather than empirical threats from groups like Citizens.[71]
