List of current United States circuit judges

The list of current United States circuit judges enumerates the active Article III judges serving on the thirteen United States courts of appeals, which function as intermediate appellate bodies reviewing judgments from the ninety-four federal district courts, decisions of federal administrative agencies, and specific original jurisdiction matters.[1] These judges, appointed for life terms by the President with Senate confirmation under Article III of the U.S. Constitution, occupy 179 statutorily authorized judgeships apportioned across the circuits according to caseload demands, though the precise number of active judges fluctuates with retirements, senior status assumptions, and unfilled vacancies.[2] The courts comprise twelve geographically defined regional circuits encompassing the states, District of Columbia, and territories, supplemented by the Federal Circuit with nationwide jurisdiction over specialized areas such as patents, veterans' claims, and international trade.[1] Judgeship allocations vary significantly, from six each in the First, Third, and Eleventh Circuits to twenty-nine in the Ninth Circuit, reflecting disparities in population and litigation volume.[3] Panels of three judges typically hear cases, with en banc rehearings possible for intra-circuit conflicts or exceptional circumstances, ensuring collegial decision-making amid the courts' substantial annual caseload exceeding eighty thousand appeals.[1]

Overview

Structure of the United States Courts of Appeals

The United States Courts of Appeals comprise 13 intermediate appellate courts positioned below the Supreme Court in the federal judiciary hierarchy.[1] These courts primarily review appeals from the 94 U.S. district courts and decisions of federal administrative agencies, focusing on errors in law application rather than retrying facts.[1] Established by the Judiciary Act of 1891, known as the Evarts Act, the system initially created nine regional courts to alleviate the Supreme Court's workload, with expansions over time to the current configuration.[4] Twelve courts operate as regional circuits, each encompassing multiple district courts within defined geographic territories: the First through Eleventh Circuits cover specific states and territories, while the District of Columbia Circuit handles cases from the D.C. district court and nationwide administrative appeals.[1] The thirteenth, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, exercises nationwide jurisdiction over specialized areas including patent and trademark disputes, appeals from the Court of International Trade and Court of Federal Claims, and certain government contract cases.[1] The regional circuits' boundaries align with clusters of district courts, ensuring localized review of trial-level decisions.[1] Congress authorizes 179 permanent judgeships across these courts pursuant to 28 U.S.C. Â§ 44, with allocations varying by circuit caseload and size: the First Circuit has 6 judgeships, the Ninth Circuit the largest at 29, and the Federal Circuit 12.[5] [3] Circuit judges, appointed for life by the President with Senate confirmation under Article III, typically hear cases in randomly selected panels of three, without juries or new evidence.[1] [6] En banc rehearings by the full court, limited to cases of exceptional importance per Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure Rule 35, allow reconsideration of panel decisions.[1] Each circuit is led by a chief judge, selected as the most senior active judge under age 65, who administers court operations, assigns duties, and represents the circuit in the Judicial Conference of the United States.[6] Circuit decisions establish binding precedent within their jurisdiction, contributing to circuit splits that may prompt Supreme Court review, with the courts handling over 50,000 appeals annually as of recent data.[1] Senior judges, who have taken senior status after reaching age 65 with sufficient service, may continue participating in cases, effectively expanding judicial capacity beyond the authorized active judgeships.[6]

Composition by Appointing President

As of October 2025, the 177 active United States circuit judges (out of 179 authorized judgeships, with 2 vacancies) were appointed by presidents spanning multiple administrations, reflecting the lifetime tenure under Article III of the Constitution.[7] Recent presidents account for the largest shares due to limited turnover, with older appointees more likely to have taken senior status or retired. Democratic presidents have appointed approximately 87 active judges, while Republican presidents have appointed approximately 90, resulting in a slight Republican majority overall.[8] President Joe Biden's 45 appointments to the courts of appeals, confirmed between 2021 and 2024, constitute the most recent cohort and all remain in active service.[9] President Donald Trump's first-term confirmations totaled 54 circuit judges between 2017 and 2021, with the vast majority still active as of late 2025; his second term has added a small number of additional confirmations, including at least 3 reported by October.[10] [11] President Barack Obama's 55 circuit appointments from 2009 to 2016 form a substantial active bloc, though some have transitioned to senior status.[10] Earlier Republican presidents hold influence through enduring appointments: George W. Bush confirmed 62 circuit judges from 2001 to 2008, many of whom continue active service despite some senior statuses.[12] Presidents from prior decades, such as Bill Clinton (64 circuit appointments from 1993 to 2000) and Ronald Reagan, contribute smaller active contingents owing to higher rates of retirement and senior status among longer-serving judges.[13] This distribution underscores the long-term impact of presidential nominations on appellate composition, with ideological leanings often aligning with appointing administrations but varying by individual judge.[6]

Vacancies and Recent Appointments

As of October 24, 2025, the United States Courts of Appeals have two reported vacancies out of 179 authorized judgeships, though one was filled by Senate confirmation shortly thereafter.[14] The First Circuit vacancy stems from Judge William J. Kayatta Jr. assuming senior status effective October 31, 2024; Joshua D. Dunlap was nominated to fill the seat on July 15, 2025, and remains pending confirmation.[14] The Seventh Circuit vacancy arose from Judge Diane S. Sykes assuming senior status on October 1, 2025; Rebecca L. Taibleson was nominated on September 15, 2025, and confirmed by the Senate on October 23, 2025, thereby filling the position.[14][15] In 2025, the Senate confirmed four new circuit judges, addressing prior vacancies from retirements and senior status transitions. Emil J. Bove III was confirmed on July 29, 2025, for the Third Circuit, succeeding Judge Joseph A. Greenaway Jr., who retired on June 15, 2023.[16] Jennifer Lee Mascott was confirmed on October 9, 2025, for the Third Circuit, replacing Judge Kent A. Jordan, who retired on January 15, 2025.[16] Whitney Downs Hermandorfer was confirmed on July 14, 2025, for the Sixth Circuit, following Judge Jane Branstetter Stranch's transition to senior status on the same date.[16] These appointments, alongside Taibleson's confirmation, have kept circuit vacancies minimal, at approximately 1% of authorized positions.[17]
