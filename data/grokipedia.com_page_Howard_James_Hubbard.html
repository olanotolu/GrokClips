Howard Hubbard

Howard James Hubbard (October 31, 1938 – August 19, 2023) was an American prelate of the Roman Catholic Church who served as the ninth bishop of the Diocese of Albany from 1977 to 2014.[1][2] Appointed at age 38 by Pope Paul VI, Hubbard was the youngest bishop in the United States at the time and held the position longer than any predecessor in the diocese's history, overseeing a period of social outreach initiatives amid evolving challenges within the Church.[2][3][4] Prior to his episcopal ordination, Hubbard earned recognition as a "street priest" for his work in urban ministry, including co-founding Providence House, a shelter for the homeless in Albany.[4] He promoted interfaith dialogue and social justice efforts, such as reconciliation services between Christians and Jews and participation in community task forces addressing poverty and discrimination.[5] Hubbard's tenure, however, became defined by controversies over the handling of clergy sexual abuse cases; he admitted in a 2021 deposition to reassigning accused priests without notifying civil authorities or victims' families, a practice he described as consistent with norms of the era but which drew widespread criticism.[6][7] Facing multiple lawsuits alleging personal involvement in abusing a minor during the 1990s, Hubbard denied the claims but in 2022 requested laicization from Pope Francis, though no decision was reported before his death.[8][3] In July 2023, weeks before his passing, he married a woman in a civil ceremony, prompting further scrutiny of his legacy.[9][10]

Personal Background

Early Life and Family

Howard James Hubbard Jr. was born on October 31, 1938, at Samaritan Hospital in Troy, New York.[11][12] His father, Howard James Hubbard Sr., worked as an accountant for the state of New York, while details on his mother's occupation or name remain limited in public records.[3] Hubbard grew up in the Lansingburgh section of Troy, a working-class neighborhood in the Capital District region.[12] He received his early education in local parochial and preparatory schools, attending Haskell School and St. Patrick's School in Lansingburgh, followed by La Salle Institute, a Catholic military high school in Troy.[12][11] These institutions emphasized discipline and religious formation, aligning with the family's apparent Catholic background in the industrial city of Troy during the mid-20th century.[2] No public records detail siblings or extended family influences on his formative years.[3]

Education and Formation

Hubbard received his early education at St. Patrick's School and La Salle Institute, both in Troy, New York.[2][13] In 1956, he commenced priestly formation at Mater Christi Seminary in Albany, New York.[12][14] He subsequently transferred to St. Joseph's Seminary in Dunwoodie, New York, where he completed a bachelor's degree in philosophy.[11][3] At St. Joseph's, Hubbard also obtained a licentiate in sacred theology.[3] He further pursued graduate studies at the Catholic University of America in Washington, D.C.[11]

Priestly Ministry

Ordination and Early Assignments

Howard James Hubbard was ordained to the priesthood for the Diocese of Albany on December 18, 1963, at the Church of St. Ignatius in Rome, during the final sessions of the Second Vatican Council.[12][2] His initial pastoral assignment was at the Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception in Albany, New York, where he served from 1963 to 1965 as one of several priests on staff.[15][16] In 1965, Hubbard pursued graduate studies at The Catholic University of America in Washington, D.C., completing his program by 1966.[15] From 1966 until his episcopal appointment in 1977, Hubbard resided at St. John the Evangelist Church in Albany's South End, a neighborhood marked by urban poverty, where he engaged in ministry focused on social outreach to the disadvantaged.[15][17] During this period, he also held leadership roles in diocesan initiatives, including serving as moderator of the Catholic Interracial Council from 1967 to 1977, chair of the Commission for Ecumenical and Interreligious Affairs from 1972 to 1976, and director of Pastoral Planning from 1974 to 1977.[15] These assignments emphasized interracial justice, ecumenism, and urban ministry amid the social upheavals of the 1960s and 1970s.[17]

Social Activism and Public Role

Hubbard's early priestly assignments in Albany placed him in direct contact with the city's marginalized communities, particularly in the impoverished South End neighborhood. Ordained in 1963, he quickly earned a reputation as a "street priest" for his hands-on ministry to the poor, homeless, and individuals battling addiction, living and working among them to address immediate social needs such as education, housing, and rehabilitation.[17][12] In 1966, while serving in these roles, Hubbard founded Providence House, a crisis intervention center aimed at providing emergency support to at-risk individuals, and Hope House, a dedicated drug rehabilitation program to combat substance abuse in urban areas. These initiatives reflected his commitment to practical social services over abstract advocacy, drawing on Catholic social teaching to prioritize aid for the vulnerable.[12][18] His public role as a priest extended to broader commentary on urban poverty and social inequities, positioning him as a vocal figure within Albany's Catholic community who bridged ecclesiastical duties with grassroots engagement. This period laid the foundation for his later prominence, though it remained focused on local, empirical interventions rather than national policy debates.[17][11]

Episcopate

Appointment and Tenure Overview

Howard James Hubbard was appointed the ninth Bishop of Albany by Pope Paul VI on February 1, 1977, at the age of 38, making him the youngest bishop in the United States at that time.[1][2] He was the first native son of the Albany Diocese to hold the position.[2] His episcopal ordination and installation occurred on March 27, 1977, in the Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception in Albany, succeeding Bishop Edward J. Maguire.[4][19] Hubbard's tenure lasted 37 years, from 1977 until his resignation on February 11, 2014, at age 75, marking the longest episcopate in the history of the Diocese of Albany.[1][20] During this period, he oversaw a diocese spanning 14 counties in northeastern New York, with approximately 300,000 Catholics served by over 200 parishes and numerous institutions.[2] His leadership emphasized social justice initiatives, including advocacy for the poor and programs addressing addiction, such as the establishment of Hope House, a treatment center that continues to operate.[21] The bishop's resignation was accepted by Pope Benedict XVI in accordance with canon law, which mandates retirement at age 75, though bishops may continue if requested.[1] Following his retirement, Bishop Hubbard retained the title of Bishop Emeritus and resided in Albany, occasionally participating in diocesan events until later restrictions.[17] His tenure concluded amid growing scrutiny over clergy abuse handling, though these issues were addressed in subsequent investigations.[4]

Diocesan Leadership and Achievements

Hubbard's 37-year tenure as Bishop of Albany, from his ordination on March 27, 1977, until his retirement on February 23, 2014, marked the longest in the diocese's history and made him, at one point, the longest-serving active bishop in the United States.[11][22] As the youngest bishop in the country at age 38 upon installation, he focused on adapting the diocese to demographic shifts and declining clergy numbers, overseeing the closure of 33 parishes and churches between 2007 and 2011 through the "Called to Be Church" pastoral planning process to consolidate resources and enhance viability.[11][22] This initiative addressed a priest shortage that reduced active clergy from around 400 to fewer than 150, prioritizing evangelization and stewardship amid population changes.[22] Under his leadership, the diocese advanced evangelization efforts, including the "Renew" program, primetime television retreats, and the "Our Amazing God" initiative, which encouraged parishioners to share faith experiences through retreats and materials like DVD series.[22] He issued pastoral letters such as "We Are His People" and "We Are God’s Priestly People" to guide spiritual renewal.[11] Infrastructure improvements included a $20 million renovation of the Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception.[11] In education, he launched the Covenant to Educate planning process in 2010 to strengthen Catholic schools, building on earlier efforts like piloting a Head Start program in Albany.[23][11] Hubbard supported social service expansions, serving as founding president of LIVCORP to provide group homes for individuals with developmental disabilities and co-founding Hope House as a residential recovery program for addiction, which remains operational.[11][22] He established The Foundation of the Roman Catholic Diocese of Albany in 1995 as a nonprofit for fundraising and resource management. Interfaith engagement included leading a 1986 Palm Sunday reconciliation service between Christians and Jews at the cathedral.[11] These efforts reflected his emphasis on outreach to the marginalized, continuing his pre-episcopal "street priest" work in providing housing, food, and support for the poor and addicted.[11]

Policies on Clergy Sexual Abuse

During his tenure as Bishop of Albany from 1977 to 2014, Howard Hubbard oversaw the handling of clergy sexual abuse allegations through internal diocesan processes that prioritized confidentiality and rehabilitation over immediate civil reporting. In a 2021 deposition, Hubbard stated that his office received reports of child sexual abuse involving 11 priests between 1977 and 2002, with practices including psychological evaluations, treatment referrals, and reassignments to ministry or other dioceses without notification to law enforcement, victims' families, or parishioners.[24][7] He attributed these approaches to contemporaneous expert advice viewing abuse as treatable via therapy rather than a fixed predisposition, and noted no legal requirement existed in New York for clergy to report suspicions until later reforms.[7] Hubbard admitted in the same deposition to routinely transferring accused priests from treatment facilities back into pastoral roles to avoid scandal and uphold the priesthood's reputation, while maintaining sealed personnel files accessible only to top diocesan officials.[7] These methods aligned with widespread pre-2002 U.S. Catholic practices but drew later criticism for enabling recidivism, as Hubbard acknowledged covering up allegations to preserve institutional trust.[25] In response to the 2002 national clergy abuse crisis, the Diocese of Albany implemented the U.S. bishops' Charter for the Protection of Children and Young People, adopting zero-tolerance measures that mandated reporting credible allegations to civil authorities, permanent removal from ministry for substantiated offenders, and establishment of a Sexual Misconduct Review Board for investigations alongside a Victims Assistance Coordinator for survivor support.[26][27] Hubbard enforced priest removals under these policies, such as that of Rev. Edward Pratt in 2002 for sexual misconduct, though successor Bishop Edward Scharfenberger later described decades of diocesan cover-ups under Hubbard as systemic.[27][25]

Investigations into Personal Allegations

In early 2004, Bishop Howard Hubbard faced public allegations of homosexual misconduct, including claims of sexual relationships with priests such as Fathers Robert Chevalier, Charles Butler, and John Burke, paid encounters with a teenage male in Albany's Washington Park in the late 1970s, frequent visits to gay bars like the Playhouse and Waterworks, and involvement in a purported homosexual network within the clergy.[27] These accusations originated from sources including a 1995 letter by Father John Minkler, testimony from Anthony Bonneau regarding the Washington Park incidents, and assertions by Andrew Zalay linking Hubbard to his brother Tom Zalay's 1978 suicide via a homosexual affair.[27][28] Hubbard categorically denied the claims under oath, emphasizing his commitment to celibacy.[27] The Diocese of Albany commissioned an independent investigation led by the law firm Debevoise & Plimpton, involving former U.S. Attorney Mary Jo White, which conducted approximately 300 interviews across multiple states, reviewed over 20,000 documents including Hubbard's calendars and phone records, and administered polygraph examinations to Hubbard and several implicated individuals on April 22, 2004.[27] Hubbard passed the polygraph, truthfully denying participation in homosexual activities, visits to gay bars, or any related misconduct, as did others such as former priest Michael Pratt.[27] Key accusers like Bonneau and Andrew Zalay declined to cooperate, and allegations such as the Zalay notes were undermined by questions of authenticity and lack of corroboration; many claims traced to misidentifications or unsubstantiated rumors, sometimes amplified by attorney John Aretakis.[27] The June 24, 2004, report concluded there was no credible evidence supporting the allegations, clearing Hubbard of any homosexual conduct or lifestyle.[27][29] Following New York's 2019 Child Victims Act, which extended the statute of limitations for abuse claims, Hubbard faced at least seven lawsuits alleging personal sexual abuse of minors, primarily from the 1970s to 1990s.[30] Specific claims included abuse of a 16- to 18-year-old boy at St. Michael the Archangel Church in Troy from 1976 to 1978 alongside priest Joseph Mato; a teenage girl at Immaculate Conception in Schenectady in the late 1970s; a 9- to 10-year-old boy at St. John in Chestertown in the late 1980s; and a 9- to 11-year-old boy at St. James in Albany from 1974 to 1976 involving anal rape.[30][31] Hubbard denied all such accusations, and in August 2019, he voluntarily withdrew from public ministry pending review.[30][31] The Albany Diocese initiated investigations into some post-2019 claims under the 2019 papal framework Vos estis lux mundi, which mandates probes of bishops accused of abuse, though outcomes remained pending at Hubbard's death on August 19, 2023.[30] No criminal charges were filed against Hubbard personally, and the diocese neither admitted liability nor detailed investigative findings in public records; several cases proceeded civilly, with some broader diocesan settlements but no specific resolution attributing fault to Hubbard.[30] Hubbard maintained his innocence throughout, attributing the surge in claims to the extended legal window rather than new evidence.[30]

Later Years

Retirement from Active Ministry

On November 18, 2022, Bishop Emeritus Howard Hubbard announced his retirement from active priestly ministry in the Diocese of Albany, stating that a U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops policy—implemented under the 2002 Charter for the Protection of Children and Young People—prevented him from publicly exercising priestly functions due to pending civil allegations of sexual abuse against him.[32][8] Hubbard, who had retired as diocesan bishop on February 11, 2014, at the canonical age of 75, expressed hope during that earlier transition to continue serving the diocese as a priest but cited the policy's restrictions as rendering such service untenable.[1][33] The Diocese of Albany responded that Hubbard had not been formally barred from public ministry but had voluntarily withdrawn from it since August 2019, following the public filing of the first civil lawsuit accusing him of abusing a minor in the 1970s—an allegation he has repeatedly denied, asserting no criminal charges or ecclesiastical findings of guilt.[34][30] At least four additional lawsuits emerged by 2020 under New York's extended Child Victims Act statute of limitations, all of which Hubbard contested as unsubstantiated.[35] This policy, which Hubbard helped shape as a U.S. bishop during its 2002 adoption, mandates temporary removal from ministry for priests facing credible accusations pending investigation, though "credible" determinations rely on internal reviews rather than judicial convictions. Hubbard's decision aligned with his simultaneous petition to the Vatican for laicization, or return to the lay state, to resolve his status amid these constraints, though the retirement itself marked the end of any formal priestly engagements. No canonical penalties had been imposed on him prior to this step, and the diocese continued to provide emeritus support without endorsing the allegations' validity.[34]

Laicization Request and Civil Marriage

In November 2022, Howard J. Hubbard, the retired Bishop Emeritus of Albany, formally requested laicization from the Vatican, citing his inability to exercise priestly ministry effectively amid ongoing civil lawsuits and ecclesiastical investigations related to historical allegations of sexual abuse.[8] Hubbard stated that U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops policies restricted accused bishops from public sacramental roles, rendering his clerical status untenable.[36] The request, described as rare for a bishop, sought release from the obligations of clerical celibacy and ministry to return to the lay state.[37] The Holy See denied Hubbard's petition earlier in 2023, reportedly in April, without public disclosure of specific reasons, though Vatican norms for laicization of bishops typically require demonstrated grave cause and papal approval.[38] Hubbard, then aged 84, proceeded in July 2023 to enter a civil marriage with a woman described in his statement as a longtime caregiver who supported him amid legal challenges.[39] He announced the union publicly on August 1, 2023, via a letter to colleagues and friends, expressing hope that the Church might recognize it and requesting privacy as litigation continued.[40] The civil ceremony held no sacramental validity under canon law, as clergy are bound by perpetual vows of celibacy, and such unions without dispensation constitute public scandal potentially warranting automatic excommunication or dismissal from the clerical state.[39] Albany's apostolic administrator, Bishop Edward Scharfenberger, responded on August 1, 2023, affirming Hubbard's ongoing episcopal status while declaring the marriage invalid and the action "unexpected," urging fidelity to Church teaching on celibacy.[41] Scharfenberger noted the irregularity could prompt Vatican reconsideration of laicization under canons addressing persistent violation of clerical discipline.[38] Hubbard's move drew commentary as unprecedented, bypassing denied permission and risking further canonical penalties, though no immediate laicization followed before his subsequent health decline.[42]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Howard James Hubbard suffered a major stroke on August 17, 2023, and was hospitalized in critical condition at Albany Medical Center in Albany, New York.[43][11] He died there two days later on August 19, 2023, at the age of 84.[17][44] The Diocese of Albany announced his death, noting that he had received the sacrament of the anointing of the sick following the stroke, and requested prayers for him and his family.[43] A public Mass of Christian burial was held on August 25, 2023, at St. Pius X Church in Loudonville, New York, presided over by Bishop Edward Scharfenberger, Hubbard's successor as ordinary of Albany.[45][46] Scharfenberger's homily acknowledged Hubbard's lengthy tenure and contributions to social justice while describing him as "not uncontroversial" in handling certain issues, including clergy abuse scandals that had marked his retirement.[46] Despite Hubbard's recent civil marriage—which canon law deems invalid for clergy and incurs automatic excommunication—the diocese proceeded with an ecclesiastical funeral, with Scharfenberger stating that the Church did not recognize the union.[47][48] Hubbard's extended family, including nieces and nephews, publicly criticized Scharfenberger's homily as "detestable" for allegedly downplaying Hubbard's six decades of service and prioritizing political influences over his legacy, claiming it reflected the Church hierarchy's alienation from Hubbard due to his progressive stances.[13][49] Hubbard remained named in seven pending civil lawsuits alleging personal sexual abuse, which he had denied throughout his life, though these claims were not formally addressed in the funeral proceedings.[50] Commentary from Catholic outlets questioned the appropriateness of a full episcopal burial for a bishop whose retirement involved admitted cover-ups of clerical misconduct and unresolved canonical status.[51]
