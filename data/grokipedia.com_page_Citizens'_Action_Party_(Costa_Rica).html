Citizens' Action Party (Costa Rica)


The Citizens' Action Party (Spanish: Partido Acción Ciudadana, abbreviated PAC) is a political party in Costa Rica founded in 2000 to advance democratic renewal, combat corruption, and increase citizen participation in governance, emerging from civil society initiatives and a split within the established National Liberation Party.[1][2][3]
The party disrupted the long-standing two-party system dominated by the National Liberation Party (PLN) and the Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC), achieving near-victory in the 2006 presidential election before electing Luis Guillermo Solís as president in 2014 and Carlos Alvarado Quesada in 2018, thereby governing the country from 2014 to 2022.[2][4]
Its platform prioritizes transparency, environmental protection, social inclusion, and ethical governance, though its administrations faced challenges including fiscal deficits and public protests over proposed tax reforms.[1][5]
In the 2022 general elections, PAC secured 10 seats in the 57-member Legislative Assembly, positioning it as a significant opposition force amid a fragmented political landscape.[4][6]

History

Founding and early years (2000–2001)

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) was established in December 2000 by economist Ottón Solís Fallas, a former planning minister in the National Liberation Party (PLN) government of José María Figueres Olsen (1994–1998), who had grown disillusioned with the PLN's direction following its loss in the 1998 presidential election to Miguel Ángel Rodríguez of the Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC).[7][8] Solís, along with other dissidents from both traditional parties, sought to challenge the entrenched bipartidismo system amid rising public frustration over corruption scandals, including irregularities in state telecommunications contracts that implicated figures from PLN and PUSC administrations.[8] The party's formation represented a civil society-driven response to perceived institutional stagnation, drawing initial support from non-governmental organizations focused on transparency and reform.[2][9] Organized informally through grassroots assemblies and coalitions of activists, the PAC rapidly developed a platform prioritizing anti-corruption measures, fiscal responsibility, and participatory democracy, explicitly rejecting the clientelistic practices associated with the PLN-PUSC duopoly.[2] By early 2001, the party had mobilized hundreds of volunteers, including professionals, academics, and NGO leaders, to draft statutes emphasizing ethical governance and economic modernization without the neoliberal extremes of PUSC or the statist tendencies of PLN.[8] Ottón Solís emerged as the de facto leader, leveraging his academic background and prior government experience to articulate a centrist-progressive vision aimed at restoring public trust in politics.[7] Legal recognition came on June 27, 2001, via Resolution 086-01 from the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, granting national scope under Folio 152 of the Political Parties Registry and enabling participation in the impending 2002 elections.[3] During the latter half of 2001, the PAC concentrated on internal structuring, including the establishment of regional committees and candidate selection processes, while conducting public forums to refine its agenda on issues like public sector efficiency and environmental sustainability.[2] This foundational period solidified the party's identity as a reformist outsider force, with early membership estimates reaching several thousand by year's end, though it faced skepticism from established media and political elites accustomed to the two-party dominance.[8]

Breakthrough and opposition role (2002–2010)

In the 2002 general elections on February 3, Ottón Solís, the Citizens' Action Party's (PAC) candidate for president, secured 552,342 votes, equivalent to 26.12% of the valid ballots, finishing third behind Abel Pacheco of the Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC) at 38.54% and Rolando Araya of the National Liberation Party (PLN) at 30.63%.[10] This performance forced a presidential runoff between Pacheco and Araya—the first in Costa Rican history under the 40% threshold rule—effectively denying the PLN an outright victory by siphoning votes from its base, many disillusioned with traditional bipartism and corruption scandals.[11] In the concurrent legislative elections, the PAC won 14 seats in the 57-member Assembly Legislativa, establishing itself as a significant third force capable of influencing legislative balances.[12] As the primary opposition during President Pacheco's term (2002–2006), the PAC positioned itself as an anti-corruption alternative, capitalizing on PUSC-linked scandals such as the Cemexgate bribery affair involving public works contracts, which eroded trust in the ruling party.[2] PAC legislators, led by Solís, critiqued fiscal mismanagement and advocated institutional reforms, including transparency measures, while avoiding alliances with the weakened PLN to maintain its outsider appeal. This period solidified the party's role in fragmenting the PLN-PUSC duopoly, drawing support from urban professionals, youth, and civil society groups frustrated with clientelism.[13] The PAC further consolidated its opposition stature in the 2006 elections on February 5, where Solís garnered 845,184 votes (39.88%), narrowly edging PLN candidate Óscar Arias in the first round and advancing to a runoff that Arias won 50.76% to 49.24%.[14] Legislatively, the party expanded to 25 seats, tying the PLN and diminishing the PUSC to five, which enhanced PAC leverage in debates over economic policy.[15] Under Arias (2006–2010), the PAC mounted vigorous resistance to the Central American Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA-DR), organizing protests and legislative blocks against ratification; Solís campaigned against the 2007 referendum, which passed 51.6% to 48.4%, framing it as a threat to sovereignty and public services. This stance reinforced the party's progressive, reformist identity amid ongoing corruption probes into prior administrations.[16] By 2010, despite not fielding a strong presidential contender and seeing legislative seats drop, the PAC had entrenched itself as the chief counterweight to PLN dominance, setting the stage for future gains through sustained advocacy for ethical governance.[17]

Ascendancy to government (2014–2018)

In the 2014 Costa Rican general election held on February 2, Luis Guillermo Solís, the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) candidate, topped the first-round presidential vote with 1,329,508 ballots (25.66% of valid votes), narrowly surpassing Johnny Araya of the National Liberation Party (PLN) by 0.66 percentage points.[18] This outcome reflected widespread voter disillusionment with the dominant PLN and Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC) due to corruption scandals, economic stagnation, and perceived elite entrenchment, propelling PAC—a party founded on anti-corruption and institutional renewal—as a viable alternative.[19] In the legislative race, PAC captured 14 of 57 seats in the unicameral Assembly, emerging as the largest single bloc amid a fragmented result: PLN secured 18 seats, PUSC 12, and smaller parties divided the rest, ensuring no outright majority.[20] Araya initially led in polls but withdrew before the April 6 runoff, allowing Solís to win decisively with 1,006,176 votes (77.9% of the counted ballots), the highest share for any candidate in modern Costa Rican history up to that point.[21] Solís assumed office on May 8, 2014, as the first non-traditional party president since 1953, forming a minority government reliant on ad hoc alliances with leftist groups like the Broad Front and occasional cross-aisle support from PUSC to pass legislation.[22] The administration's "Rescate Nacional" plan emphasized ethical governance, fiscal sustainability, and social equity, including bolstering anti-corruption bodies such as the Comptroller General's office and pursuing transparency in public procurement to address systemic graft that had eroded trust in prior regimes. Key initiatives included targeted poverty alleviation, leveraging the national Multidimensional Poverty Index to prioritize vulnerable households; this contributed to a decline from 21.7% in 2014 to 18.8% in 2017.[23] Monetary poverty rates also eased from 18.3% in 2014 to 16.5% by 2018, aided by expanded conditional cash transfers and job programs amid steady GDP growth averaging 4.2% annually.[24] However, fiscal challenges persisted, with public debt exceeding 50% of GDP and repeated failed attempts at comprehensive tax reform due to legislative opposition and public protests, underscoring the limits of PAC's reformist agenda without a working majority.[25] By term's end, these efforts solidified PAC's image as a progressive force, paving the way for its 2018 reelection despite internal frictions and unfulfilled promises on deficit reduction.

Second term and challenges (2018–2022)

Carlos Alvarado Quesada, the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) candidate, won the presidency in a runoff election on April 1, 2018, defeating Fabricio Alvarado of the National Restoration Party with 60.6% of the vote amid intense polarization over a recent Inter-American Court of Human Rights ruling mandating same-sex marriage recognition.[26] [27] The PAC's legislative caucus shrank to 13 deputies in the 57-seat Assembly, marking the smallest government-aligned fraction in Costa Rican history and complicating governance.[28] The administration inherited a fiscal crisis characterized by public debt surpassing 60% of GDP and chronic deficits averaging 5-6% annually, prompting immediate action on structural reforms.[29] The Ley de Fortalecimiento de las Finanzas Públicas, enacted in 2018, imposed a fiscal rule targeting debt stabilization, curtailed automatic public salary adjustments, reformed pension systems to limit early retirements, and streamlined public hiring while modestly boosting revenues through excise taxes and efficiencies rather than broad tax hikes.[30] [31] These measures aimed to avert default risks but ignited 93 days of nationwide protests in 2018, led by public sector unions decrying benefit cuts and austerity, culminating in road blockades, strikes, and direct confrontations with Alvarado, such as the October 2018 theater standoff over tax elements.[32] Implementation faced ongoing resistance, including teacher and health worker strikes, exacerbating legislative delays in a fragmented Assembly. The COVID-19 pandemic, declared a national emergency in March 2020, amplified economic strains despite an effective public health response featuring early border closures, expanded testing, and a vaccination rollout that yielded one of Latin America's lowest case fatality rates at under 1% by mid-term.[33] [34] Lockdowns and stimulus spending drove a 4.5% GDP contraction in 2020, pushed unemployment above 20% from 11% pre-pandemic levels, and elevated poverty to 23% while inflating the fiscal deficit to 8% of GDP amid debt-financed aid packages.[35] [36] Public debt climbed toward 70% of GDP by 2022, constraining recovery efforts despite tourism rebounds and export growth in semiconductors and medical devices.[37] Persistent challenges included rising inequality, with growth disproportionately benefiting urban elites, and social fractures from policy disputes over labor reforms and environmental enforcement, such as fumigation bans affecting agriculture.[35] Alvarado's administration pursued decarbonization targets and multilateral climate commitments, but low approval ratings—often below 30%—reflected fatigue with gridlock and perceived elitism, contributing to the PAC's legislative rout in 2022 when it retained just 9 seats.[38] The term underscored the PAC's vulnerability as a minority government navigating exogenous shocks and entrenched interests without traditional bipartisan pacts.

Decline and opposition (2022–present)

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) suffered a precipitous electoral decline in the February 6, 2022, general elections, securing just 12,135 votes (0.8%) for its presidential bid in the first round, failing to advance to the April runoff where Rodrigo Chaves ultimately prevailed with 50.96% of the vote.[39] [40] This outcome capped eight years of PAC governance under Presidents Luis Guillermo Solís and Carlos Alvarado, during which public approval plummeted—reaching 62% negative ratings for Alvarado's term by May 2022 amid fiscal stagnation, unemployment spikes to 22% in 2020, and criticisms of ineffective pandemic response and institutional overreach.[41] [42] Legislative results compounded the reversal, with the PAC's seat count dropping sharply from prior majorities to a diminished minority, as voters fragmented support toward revived traditional parties like the National Liberation Party and emerging alternatives like Chaves' Social Democratic Progress Party.[43] [6] In opposition since May 8, 2022, the PAC has positioned itself against Chaves' administration, decrying executive confrontations with the judiciary, media, and unions as threats to democratic norms, while Chaves in turn lambasted the PAC's legacy as a "scam" requiring national reconstruction.[44] The party formed alliances, such as with Agenda Democrática Nacional, to amplify critiques of policy implementation and institutional erosion under Chaves.[45] Internal introspection emerged, including the party's 2023 congress repudiating select Alvarado-era decisions on fiscal and administrative matters, amid broader voter fatigue with progressive reforms perceived as fiscally unsustainable.[46] By mid-2025, the PAC intensified preparations for the 2026 elections, convening a closed national assembly in April to select presidential aspirants and announcing Claudia Dobles as its nominee alongside a legislative candidate slate in September, aiming to reclaim relevance through anti-corruption appeals and institutional defense.[47] [48] Persistent challenges include competition from Chaves' popularity and fragmented opposition dynamics, with the party's base retention limited to urban progressives amid national disillusionment.[39]

Ideology and platform

Anti-corruption and institutional reform

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) was established in 2000 as a response to entrenched corruption within Costa Rica's traditional political parties, the National Liberation Party (PLN) and the Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC), which had dominated the bipartidismo system and faced multiple scandals involving public contracts and misuse of funds.[49] The party's statutes explicitly mandate a tenacious fight against all forms of political corruption, emphasizing the safeguarding of public resources and the promotion of ethical governance to prevent elite capture of state institutions.[50] This foundational stance frames anti-corruption as central to institutional reform, aiming to dismantle nepotism, undue influence, and opaque decision-making through structural changes rather than mere punitive measures. Key proposals in PAC's platforms include reducing public expenditure by eliminating corruption-enabled duplications and superfluous costs, alongside advancing transparency legislation such as access to public information, lobby regulation, and whistleblower protections.[51] The party advocates strengthening participation in the Open Government Partnership to foster accountability and citizen oversight, including digitalization of services for universal access and open data portals measured by the Institutional Transparency Index.[51] Institutional reforms proposed encompass lifting the corporate veil for corruption probes (Legislative File 18.213), regulating lobbying in public administration (File 19.251), and mandating loss of congressional investiture for probity violations (File 19.117), alongside transparency mandates for collegial public bodies (File 20.103).[49] PAC promotes civic education and citizen congresses to embed accountability in public management, aligning reforms with United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 16 on inclusive institutions.[51] Recent party congresses, such as the III Congreso Ciudadano in 2023, reaffirm the need for broad state reforms to modernize institutions while preserving public entities like the Costa Rican Electricity Institute (ICE) from privatization-driven corruption risks.[52] However, analyses of PAC's government plans note that while preventive measures like enhanced controls are emphasized, comprehensive systemic overhauls—such as judicial independence reforms or prosecutorial capacity-building—remain underdeveloped compared to fiscal austerity focuses.[51]

Economic and fiscal policies

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) has positioned itself as favoring fiscal sustainability amid Costa Rica's longstanding public debt challenges, advocating for structural reforms to enhance revenue collection while prioritizing progressive taxation and anti-evasion measures. Central to this stance was support for Ley 9635, the Fiscal Responsibility Law enacted in July 2018, which established a fiscal rule capping the structural deficit at 0.75% of GDP by 2021 and mandated improvements in tax administration to combat evasion and inefficiency.[53] This legislation contributed to raising tax revenues to 15.8% of GDP by 2021 and achieving a primary surplus of 0.85% of GDP in March 2022.[53] In its 2018 government plan under presidential candidate Carlos Alvarado, the PAC proposed deepening tax reforms, including a shift to global income taxation for higher earners, conversion of the sales tax into a value-added tax (VAT) with rebates for the lowest income deciles, and introduction of environmental levies such as taxes on plastic packaging alongside incentives for electric vehicles.[54] These measures aimed to generate an additional 751 billion colones (approximately 2.3% of GDP) in revenue, with a focus on modernizing collection systems to reduce informal economy distortions.[54] The party emphasized progressive elements, arguing that broader tax bases and reduced exemptions would equitably distribute the fiscal burden without stifling investment.[54] On spending, PAC platforms called for targeted increases in social and human capital investments, such as elevating education expenditures to 8% of GDP and addressing 60% of the state's debt to the Costa Rican Social Security Fund, while proposing modest cuts of 0.53% of GDP through efficiency reviews.[54] Growth strategies centered on export diversification and foreign direct investment (FDI), particularly in high-value sectors like modern services and agro-industry; during the 2014–2017 period under Luis Guillermo Solís, these efforts secured 118 FDI projects worth $12 billion, generating over 32,000 jobs.[53] The party highlighted export reactivation, achieving 4.5% annual growth since 2016 and positioning Costa Rica as a regional leader per CEPAL data, alongside public-private partnerships for infrastructure and tourism development.[53] Fiscal outcomes included debt-to-GDP reduction to 66.46% by 2022 and controlled inflation below 3% in 2017.[53]

Social and environmental policies

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) has promoted policies aimed at expanding reproductive rights and strengthening public health infrastructure. In September 2015, during Luis Guillermo Solís's presidency, the government issued a decree authorizing in vitro fertilization procedures within the Costa Rican Social Security Fund (CCSS), marking a step toward broader access to assisted reproduction.[9] In May 2016, an allocation of $420 million was made to the CCSS to stabilize its finances and fund hospital expansions, addressing chronic underinvestment in healthcare delivery.[9] The party has also prioritized gender equity in political representation, implementing horizontal parity requirements for candidacy lists in July 2013 to ensure balanced male-female slates in elections.[9] In education, PAC governments invested over ₡200 billion in infrastructure improvements, including school construction and renovations, to enhance access and quality in underserved areas.[55] The party's platform emphasizes transformative education reforms, such as expanding technical and dual training programs to boost youth employability, alongside digital modalities for secondary education to reach remote populations.[56] Social equity initiatives include programs to combat poverty through inclusive development and minimum vital income supports, with targeted protections for vulnerable groups such as migrants, indigenous communities, and persons with disabilities.[56] During Carlos Alvarado's term (2018–2022), the administration facilitated the implementation of same-sex marriage nationwide effective May 26, 2020, in compliance with an Inter-American Court of Human Rights ruling, aligning with the party's stance on civil unions and family rights expansions.[57] On environmental matters, PAC has committed to advancing Costa Rica's renewable energy leadership, achieving a national energy matrix of 99% renewables from 2014 to 2016 through sustained hydroelectric, geothermal, and wind investments.[58] Key legislative actions include the December 2017 Law for the Promotion of Electric Transport, which incentivized vehicle electrification, complemented by the acquisition of 60 electric and hybrid vehicles for public use in March 2017 via a $3 million Japanese donation.[58] Conservation efforts expanded marine protected areas from 2.7% to 30% of territorial waters (54,844 km²) by December 2021, with the creation of the Bicentennial Marine Management Area and new national parks such as Volcán Miravalles-Jorge Manuel Dengo and Isla San Lucas.[58] The February 2019 National Decarbonization Plan sets a target of net-zero emissions by 2050, supported by transitions to zero-emission public fleets across 37 institutions by the end of Alvarado's term and infrastructure projects like the Metropolitan and Limón Electric Trains.[58] The party's platform further proposes reducing pesticide use by 50%, phasing out single-use plastics, enhancing Payments for Environmental Services via increased FONAFIFO funding, and integrating climate risk assessments into public investments, while endorsing the Escazú Agreement for environmental governance and justice.[56] These positions reflect a focus on balancing economic growth with biodiversity preservation and climate adaptation, drawing on Costa Rica's established conservation framework.[56]

Foreign policy and international relations

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) has pursued a foreign policy aligned with Costa Rica's constitutional principles of active, non-permanent neutrality, emphasizing multilateralism, the promotion of democracy and human rights, and economic diplomacy to attract investment and trade. While maintaining traditional stances on sovereignty and regional stability, PAC administrations under Presidents Luis Guillermo Solís (2014–2018) and Carlos Alvarado (2018–2022) prioritized proactive international engagement, sustainable development, and geopolitical expansion without subordinating political objectives to purely commercial interests.[59][60] During the Solís administration, foreign policy focused on establishing Costa Rica as a proactive regional actor through balanced North-South relations, rooted in principles like pragmatism and activism in areas such as security, investment, sustainable development, and climate change. The government rejected involvement in ideologically driven initiatives like Petrocaribe while advocating for reforms in the Central American Integration System (SICA) and dialogue with bodies like UNASUR, ALBA, and the Pacific Alliance. Economic diplomacy was strengthened by transforming embassies into hubs for trade, investment, and tourism promotion, including a new convention with the Foreign Trade Corporation (Procomer) and collaborations with the Ministry of Foreign Trade and the Coalition for Development Initiative. Geopolitical reach expanded to its historical maximum with six new embassies in Australia, Azerbaijan, the United Arab Emirates, Indonesia, Kenya, and Turkey, plus a consulate in Shanghai, fostering ties in Central Asia, the Caucasus, and Southeast Asia. In relations with Nicaragua, the administration prevailed in five International Court of Justice (ICJ) cases, securing sovereignty affirmations and compensation, including $378,890.59 for environmental damage from dredging the San Juan River.[59][60][60] The Alvarado administration advanced a forward-looking "Acción Exterior" emphasizing decarbonization, green and blue economies, and multilateral leadership amid global challenges like the COVID-19 pandemic. It chaired SICA and the UN Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean (CEPAL), promoting democracy and human rights, and led a UN Human Rights Council resolution recognizing a healthy environment as a human right. On climate, Costa Rica committed to protecting 30% of oceans and land by 2030 as part of the High Ambition Coalition, proposed a Pacific conservation corridor with Colombia, Ecuador, and Panama, and highlighted decarbonization efforts at COP26. Multilateral commitments included advocating a "renaissance" of UN-centered cooperation for inclusion and justice, proposing a $500 billion COVID-19 Economic Relief Fund financed by 0.7% of GDP from major economies for concessional loans via development banks, and securing over $24 million in donations plus more than 1 million vaccine doses internationally. Bilateral ties were bolstered, such as a $10 million, four-year agreement with South Korea and cooperation with the UAE on sustainable initiatives. The PAC expressed concern over Nicaragua's internal confrontations due to geographic and economic ties but urged Costa Rica to avoid direct intervention in its sovereign affairs.[61][62][61]

Electoral performance

Presidential elections

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) first fielded a presidential candidate in the 2002 general election, with Ottón Solís receiving approximately 26% of the vote in the first round, placing third behind Abel Pacheco of the Social Christian Unity Party and Rolando Araya of the National Liberation Party; no candidate reached the 40% threshold required to avoid a runoff, which was contested between Pacheco and Araya. In the 2006 election, Solís again represented the PAC and secured 39.8% of the vote, falling short of Óscar Arias of the National Liberation Party by about 4.5 percentage points; Arias achieved 44.3%, exceeding the 40% needed to win outright without a runoff.[14] Solís's strong showing marked the PAC's emergence as a viable alternative to the traditional two-party dominance of the National Liberation Party and Social Christian Unity Party.[63] By the 2010 election, Solís ran for a third time, obtaining 25.1% of the first-round vote, while Laura Chinchilla of the National Liberation Party garnered 46.7%, securing victory without a runoff.[64][65] The PAC's performance reflected a plateau after the near-miss in 2006, amid voter concerns over economic issues and corruption.[66] The PAC achieved its first presidential victory in 2014 with Luis Guillermo Solís, who won 31.0% in the first round on February 2, advancing to a runoff against Johnny Araya of the National Liberation Party.[67] In the April 6 runoff, Solís triumphed decisively with 77.8% of the vote after Araya's withdrawal amid low turnout.[22] This outcome ended over 60 years of alternating power between the two major parties, propelled by anti-corruption appeals and disillusionment with the incumbent administration.[68] In 2018, the PAC's Carlos Alvarado, then a cabinet minister under Solís, placed second in the first round with 21.6% on February 4, behind Fabricio Alvarado of the National Restoration Party; the race was influenced by a Inter-American Court of Human Rights ruling favoring same-sex marriage, mobilizing conservative opposition.[26] Alvarado won the April 1 runoff with 60.7%, defeating Fabricio Alvarado by emphasizing progressive policies, economic stability, and defense of judicial rulings on social issues.[27] This consecutive win solidified the PAC's governing role but highlighted internal party tensions over ideological positioning.

Legislative elections

The Citizens' Action Party first contested legislative elections in 2006, receiving 3.94% of valid votes but securing no seats in the 57-member Legislative Assembly due to the proportional representation system requiring sufficient thresholds in multi-member districts.[71] In the February 7, 2010 elections, the PAC improved to 17.25% of the vote and won 10 seats, establishing itself as a significant opposition force behind the victorious PLN's 24 seats.[72][73] The party's electoral breakthrough came in the February 2, 2014 elections, where it captured 25.55% of votes and 13 seats, positioning it as the second-largest bloc after the PLN's 18; this outcome facilitated coalition-building for minority governance following its presidential success.[74][75][76] Subsequent elections on February 4, 2018, saw modest gains to 14 seats with 25.13% of votes, enabling support for President Carlos Alvarado amid fragmented opposition.[77] The PAC's representation fell to 10 seats in the February 6, 2022 elections (16.57% vote share), reflecting voter discontent with fiscal policies and scandals during its eight-year rule, as newer parties fragmented the vote.[78][79]

Municipal and local elections

In the early years following its foundation in 2004, the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) secured a limited number of mayoral positions in municipal elections, peaking at six alcaldías in both the 2010 and 2016 contests.[80] This performance reflected the party's growing national profile amid anti-corruption appeals, though it consistently trailed the traditional bipartisanship of the National Liberation Party (PLN) and Social Christian Unity Party (PUSC), which dominated local governance.[80] PAC's vote share for mayoral candidates hovered between 11.62% and 14.64% from 2006 to 2016, translating to totals ranging from 88,630 to 127,466 votes nationwide.[80] By the 2020 municipal elections, PAC won four alcaldías, including reelections in key urban cantons, amid a fragmented field with rising cantonal parties.[81][80] However, its overall vote share fell to 6.88% (83,123 votes), signaling early erosion linked to governance fatigue from the concurrent Luis Guillermo Solís administration.[80] The party's local strength remained concentrated in progressive-leaning areas like parts of the Central Valley, but it struggled against localized competitors and abstentionism rates exceeding 50%.[82] The 2024 elections marked a nadir for PAC, with zero alcaldías won and a minuscule 0.09% vote share (951 votes total), averaging under 1,000 votes per mayoral candidacy across 84 cantons.[80][83] This positioned PAC as the lowest-performing national party, outperformed by over 40 cantonal options and even some provincial ones, amid 68% abstentionism and voter disillusionment following the Carlos Alvarado presidency's scandals.[83][84] No independent regidurías (council seats) were secured, though minor coalition gains occurred in two cantons.[83]

Governance and policy implementation

Luis Guillermo Solís presidency (2014–2018)

Luis Guillermo Solís of the Citizens' Action Party assumed the presidency on May 8, 2014, marking the first victory for the party in a national election after securing 77.03% of the vote in a runoff against the National Liberation Party's Johnny Araya. With only 13 seats in the 57-member Legislative Assembly, his administration encountered significant obstacles in advancing legislative agendas, necessitating coalitions and compromises. Core priorities aligned with PAC's platform included combating corruption through enhanced transparency mechanisms, promoting economic growth via infrastructure investments, and addressing social inequalities, though implementation was constrained by fiscal pressures and opposition resistance. The Solís government prioritized anti-corruption and open governance reforms, establishing a national policy on transparency and public ethics in September 2014 that aimed to standardize administrative procedures and empower citizen oversight in procurement and public spending. This included ratifying international anti-bribery conventions and strengthening the Public Ethics Solicitor's Office to investigate irregularities, contributing to Costa Rica's participation in the Open Government Partnership with action plans focused on data publication for corruption prevention. Despite these efforts, legislative gridlock limited broader institutional overhauls, and public debt servicing rose as a share of GDP amid persistent deficits averaging 5-6% annually. Economically, the administration oversaw average annual GDP growth of approximately 3.7% from 2014 to 2017, driven by exports in medical devices, tourism, and agriculture, with rates reaching 4.2% in 2016. Unemployment hovered around 8%, declining slightly to 7.73% by 2017 before rising to 9.01% in 2018 amid global slowdowns. However, fiscal deficits fueled a rise in public debt from about 40% of GDP in 2014 to 52% by 2018, as repeated attempts at tax and expenditure reforms stalled in the assembly until a partial agreement in 2018. Infrastructure investments, such as road expansions and port upgrades, were pursued but fell short of campaign promises due to budgetary constraints.[85][86][87] Social policies emphasized poverty alleviation and education access, achieving a reduction in the national Multidimensional Poverty Index from 21.7% in 2014 to 18.8% in 2017 through targeted welfare programs and conditional cash transfers. Environmental commitments built on Costa Rica's renewable energy tradition, maintaining electricity generation at 95-98% from renewables, including hydropower and geothermal sources, while advancing payments for ecosystem services to protect forests and watersheds. Challenges persisted in education reform, marked by teacher strikes in 2014 over funding disputes, which delayed curriculum updates despite increased budget allocations to 7% of GDP.[88]

Carlos Alvarado presidency (2018–2022)

Carlos Alvarado Quesada, the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) candidate, assumed the presidency on May 8, 2018, following a narrow victory in the April 1 runoff election against Fabricio Alvarado of the National Restoration Party, securing 60.6% of the vote amid debates over same-sex marriage legalization.[89] His administration prioritized fiscal reform to address a persistent deficit, culminating in the approval of a fiscal rule in 2018 that aimed to cap spending and stabilize public finances, though implementation faced legislative resistance and contributed to public discontent.[36] Economically, Costa Rica experienced steady growth of around 3-4% annually pre-pandemic, but the 2020 COVID-19 contraction led to a -4.1% GDP drop, with unemployment peaking at 24% that year before declining to 11.6% by 2022; public debt rose to 81.33% of GDP by 2022, exacerbating fiscal pressures inherited from prior administrations.[34][90][35] The PAC-led government under Alvarado emphasized environmental decarbonization, positioning Costa Rica as a "decarbonized nation" laboratory with policies advancing renewable energy targets—already over 98% of electricity from renewables—and international commitments to net-zero emissions by 2050, earning global recognition despite domestic economic trade-offs.[91] Social policies included expanding universal healthcare via the Caja Costarricense de Seguro Social, which underpinned a relatively effective COVID-19 response with low case fatality rates through early testing, contact tracing, and vaccination drives, avoiding stringent lockdowns while sustaining essential health services.[33][92] However, the pandemic amplified poverty and inequality, with austerity measures and fiscal tightening drawing criticism for insufficient social safety nets amid rising youth unemployment and informal sector vulnerabilities.[35] In foreign policy, Alvarado's PAC administration advocated multilateralism, supporting human rights and democracy regionally—such as backing Venezuelan opposition leader Juan Guaidó—and leading on climate diplomacy, including calls for global intellectual property pooling for COVID-19 technologies.[93][94] PAC's legislative plurality enabled initial policy advances but eroded amid coalition dependencies and opposition from traditional parties, leading to Alvarado's approval rating dropping to historic lows of around 10-15% by late 2021 due to perceived governance failures on security and corruption probes like the UPAD employment scandal.[95][96] The term ended with PAC facing internal divisions and electoral setbacks in 2022, as economic hardships overshadowed environmental gains.[97]

Controversies and criticisms

Internal corruption scandals

In December 2016, a San José Criminal Court convicted two officials of the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) of fraud related to the party's 2010 presidential campaign financing.[98][99] Maynor Sterling Araya, the former PAC treasurer and identified mastermind of the scheme, received a six-year prison sentence, while party official Manuel Antonio Bolaños Murillo was sentenced to three years.[98] The fraud involved creating 263 fictitious contracts claiming payments for professional services provided by volunteers who had actually worked without compensation, enabling the party to improperly collect approximately ₡353 million (about $632,000 at the time) in public reimbursements from the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE).[98] The court held the PAC liable for civil damages, ordering repayment of ₡600 million (about $1 million) to the TSE plus ₡40 million (about $72,000) in trial costs.[98] The case, investigated by the Public Ministry, confirmed the penal responsibility of the convicted individuals in 2019 after appeals.[99] Over 200 witnesses, including PAC founder Ottón Solís, testified during the trial.[98] In response, the PAC announced it would expel the convicted members in accordance with its statutes, though the party maintained the scheme did not reflect its broader anti-corruption ethos.[98] This incident highlighted vulnerabilities in party internal financial controls, as the treasurer's role facilitated the fabrication of documents to exploit public funding mechanisms designed to support electoral campaigns.[98] No additional convictions of senior party leadership resulted from the probe, but the scandal contributed to public scrutiny of the PAC's governance practices beyond its governmental roles.[99]

Estafa Única Digital and related cases

In the 2010 electoral campaign, the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) submitted 260 contracts for special services to the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (TSE) to claim reimbursement under Costa Rica's political debt system, but investigations revealed widespread irregularities, including fictitious services, inflated costs, and falsified signatures on 98% of the contracts.[99][100] The scheme involved charging the state for advertising, consulting, and other purported campaign expenses that were either not delivered or duplicated across providers, resulting in an overclaim of approximately ₡619 million colones.[101] In December 2016, the San José Penal Tribunal sentenced PAC's former treasurer, Maynor Sterling Araya, to six years in prison for fraud against the state exceeding ₡300 million colones at the time, though the civil liability extended to the party itself for repayment.[102] Two PAC members, including Sterling, had their penal responsibility confirmed by appellate courts in 2019 for orchestrating the falsifications to access public funds.[99] The Prosecutor's Office and Attorney General's Office pursued additional claims, identifying 81 unresolved contracts in ongoing proceedings as of 2024, potentially adding ₡500 million more in liabilities.[103] The PAC has repaid portions of the condemnation in installments, including ₡200 million in August 2021 sourced from private donations and party funds, ₡26 million in 2022, and ₡137 million in January 2025, asserting full compliance despite disputes over using state financing reimbursements for legal defense costs.[104][105] Party founder Ottón Solís denied personal involvement or knowledge, attributing the irregularities to isolated actors and criticizing conflation with unrelated issues, while current leaders like Fabián Solano have avoided labeling it an "error" but emphasized repayment as resolution.[106] Related cases include legislative probes in 2021 demanding full restitution and TSE investigations into whether campaign financing was misused to offset penalties, though no broader criminal conspiracy charges against party leadership were upheld beyond the convicted individuals.[101] Critics from opposition parties, such as the Broad Front, argued the fraud undermined PAC's anti-corruption platform, prompting calls for deeper audits of 2010 expenditures.[101]

Policy and governance critiques

Critics of the Citizens' Action Party (PAC) have focused on its handling of fiscal policy during the presidencies of Luis Guillermo Solís (2014–2018) and Carlos Alvarado (2018–2022), arguing that the party exacerbated Costa Rica's public debt crisis through ineffective reforms and overreliance on borrowing rather than structural spending cuts. Public debt rose from approximately 40% of GDP in 2013 to nearly 50% by the end of Solís's term, marking the eighth consecutive annual increase, as the administration's multiple attempts at comprehensive fiscal reform failed amid legislative opposition and union resistance, leaving deficits unaddressed and forcing continued debt issuance.[107] Under Alvarado, debt climbed further to around 60% of GDP by 2022, with critics attributing this to delayed austerity measures and concessions to public sector interests that prioritized short-term stability over long-term solvency.[108] A pivotal governance critique emerged from Alvarado's 2018 "combo fiscal" package, which combined tax hikes, pension adjustments, and public employment reforms to stabilize finances but sparked the largest strike in decades, paralyzing hospitals, schools, and ports for 23 days and costing an estimated 1% of GDP in lost output. Unions and opposition lawmakers condemned the reforms as regressive, burdening middle- and low-income households with higher VAT rates (from 13% to 15%) and fuel taxes while failing to curb wasteful spending, leading to widespread protests that highlighted the PAC's perceived weakness in negotiating with entrenched public sector lobbies without resorting to coercive measures.[109] Despite eventual passage via 34–17 congressional vote, the episode underscored critiques of administrative inflexibility, as the government prioritized IMF-aligned orthodoxy over consensus-building, resulting in eroded public trust and electoral backlash.[110] Broader policy implementation drew fire for contributing to economic stagnation and security lapses. Solís's administration faced accusations of unfulfilled infrastructure promises and botched debt placements, such as a failed $1.5 billion bond issuance in 2018 that threatened fiscal credibility, while Alvarado's tenure saw persistent high unemployment (peaking near 25% during COVID-19 lockdowns) and rising violent crime rates, with homicide figures increasing from 10.7 per 100,000 in 2017 to 11.8 in 2021 amid critiques of inadequate anti-narcotics enforcement under neoliberal frameworks that prioritized international aid over domestic policing reforms.[108][111] Labor groups labeled Alvarado's overall governance a "failure" for not resolving chronic deficits, with public employment costs consuming over 50% of revenues by 2022 despite reform pledges.[112] These shortcomings, opponents argued, stemmed from the PAC's ideological commitment to expansive social spending without corresponding revenue discipline, fostering dependency and gridlock in a fragmented legislature where the party held minority seats.[113]

Organization and key figures

Leadership structure

The Asamblea Nacional serves as the supreme governing body of the Partido Acción Ciudadana (PAC), responsible for establishing political guidelines, approving candidacies, and electing key leadership positions. Composed of delegates elected by provincial assemblies—typically 10 proprietors and 6 alternates per province, plus youth representatives—it operates with a requirement for gender parity and a minimum 10% youth participation. The assembly convenes periodically to amend statutes, resolve internal disputes, and oversee party direction, requiring a two-thirds majority for major reforms.[50] The Comité Ejecutivo Nacional functions as the primary executive organ, implementing decisions from the Asamblea Nacional and the Comisión Política while managing daily operations, finances, and internal audits. It comprises core roles including the Presidencia, which leads the committee, represents the party externally, and chairs assemblies; the Secretaría General, which handles organizational structure, member registries, and legal co-representation; and the Tesorería Nacional, which oversees financial compliance and reporting. Members are elected every four years by the Asamblea Nacional, with provisions for alternates and gender equity. As of the latest records, the committee is headed by Presidente José Fabián Solano Fernández.[50][3] Supporting bodies include the Comisión Política, a 12-member strategic entity that defines policy directions and reports to the Asamblea Nacional, comprising executive members, appointees, and non-voting legislative heads; and specialized tribunals such as the Tribunal de Ética y Disciplina for ethical oversight and sanctions, and the Tribunal Electoral Interno for internal electoral processes. The structure extends hierarchically to provincial, cantonal, and district levels, with corresponding assemblies and executive committees elected locally to execute national directives and foster grassroots involvement, all aligned with Costa Rica's electoral laws.[50]

Notable members and factions

The Citizens' Action Party (PAC) was founded in 2000 by Ottón Solís Fallas, a former National Liberation Party (PLN) member and economist who defected amid allegations of corruption within the PLN; Solís served as a legislative deputy from 2002 to 2010 and ran as the party's presidential candidate in the 2002, 2006, and 2010 elections, securing 26.1% of the vote in the tight 2006 runoff. Luis Guillermo Solís Rivera, a historian and former diplomat, led the PAC to its first presidential victory in 2014 with 77.9% in the runoff, governing until 2018 and focusing on anti-corruption reforms despite internal legislative resistance.[114] Carlos Alvarado Quesada, an academic and former labor minister under Solís, won the 2018 presidency at age 38 with 60.6% in the runoff, emphasizing environmental policies and fiscal stabilization before leaving office in 2022. Epsy Campbell Barr, an economist and Afro-Costa Rican activist, served as vice president from 2018 to 2022 under Alvarado, becoming the first black woman in that role in Latin America and advocating for social inclusion.[115] Current party leadership includes president Marta Eugenia Solano Arias and secretary general Anthony Cascante Ramírez, elected to guide post-presidential recovery efforts as of 2022.[116] Other notable figures include Claudia Dobles, a former communications minister under Alvarado, positioned as a potential 2026 presidential contender and praised by Solís for her renewal potential.[117] The PAC has experienced internal divisions, particularly over ethical standards, economic policy, and responses to scandals like Cochinilla, with tensions surfacing in competitive primaries such as the 2017 contest that selected Alvarado.[118] Founder Ottón Solís has maintained influence through public commentary, distancing himself from party scandals while affirming its viability, as in his 2023 defense against estafa allegations and 2025 endorsement of renewal efforts.[119] [120] These frictions, including debates on fiscal reform and leadership accountability, prompted calls for faction reconciliation in early 2025 to rebuild voter trust ahead of municipal elections. No formal factions are codified in party statutes, but primary competitions and public critiques reflect ideological strains between founding anti-corruption purists and governing pragmatists.[50]
