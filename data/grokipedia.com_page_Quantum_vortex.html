Quantum vortex

In physics, a quantum vortex represents a quantized flux circulation of some physical quantity. In most cases, quantum vortices are a type of topological defect exhibited in superfluids and superconductors. The existence of quantum vortices was first predicted by Lars Onsager in 1949 in connection with superfluid helium.[1] Onsager reasoned that quantisation of vorticity is a direct consequence of the existence of a superfluid order parameter as a spatially continuous wavefunction. Onsager also pointed out that quantum vortices describe the circulation of superfluid and conjectured that their excitations are responsible for superfluid phase transitions. These ideas of Onsager were further developed by Richard Feynman in 1955[2] and in 1957 were applied to describe the magnetic phase diagram of type-II superconductors by Alexei Alexeyevich Abrikosov.[3] In 1950, in his book Superfluids, Fritz London published a very closely related work on magnetic flux quantization in superconductors.[4] London's fluxoid can also be viewed as a quantum vortex. Quantum vortices are observed experimentally in type-II superconductors (the Abrikosov vortex), liquid helium, and atomic gases[5] (see Boseâ€“Einstein condensate), as well as in photon fields (optical vortex) and exciton-polariton superfluids. In a superfluid, a quantum vortex "carries" quantized orbital angular momentum, thus allowing the superfluid to rotate; in a superconductor, the vortex carries quantized magnetic flux. The term "quantum vortex" is also used in the study of few body problems.[6][7] Under the de Broglieâ€“Bohm theory, it is possible to derive a "velocity field" from the wave function. In this context, quantum vortices are zeros on the wave function, around which this velocity field has a solenoidal shape, similar to that of irrotational vortex on potential flows of traditional fluid dynamics.

Vortex-quantisation in a superfluid

In a superfluid, a quantum vortex is a hole with the superfluid circulating around the vortex axis; the inside of the vortex may contain excited particles, air, vacuum, etc. The thickness of the vortex depends on a variety of factors; in liquid helium, the thickness is of the order of a few Angstroms. A superfluid has the special property of having phase, given by the wavefunction, and the velocity of the superfluid is proportional to the gradient of the phase (in the parabolic mass approximation). The circulation around any closed loop in the superfluid is zero if the region enclosed is simply connected. London's flux quantization in a superconductor

Magnetic flux quantization in superconductors was predicted by Fritz London in 1935 as a macroscopic quantum phenomenon. A principal property of superconductors is that they expel magnetic fields; this is called the Meissner effect. If the magnetic field becomes sufficiently strong it will, in some cases, "quench" the superconductive state by inducing a phase transition. In other cases, however, it will be energetically favorable for the superconductor to form a lattice of quantum vortices, which carry quantized magnetic flux through the superconductor. A superconductor that is capable of supporting vortex lattices is called a type-II superconductor; vortex quantization in superconductors is general. Constrained vortices in ferromagnets and antiferromagnets

Vortex states in ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic materials are of interest for applications in information technology.[9] Unlike in superfluids, the description involves the magnetization vector mâƒ—\vec{m}m with a fixed magnitude constraint âˆ£mâƒ—âˆ£=M0|\vec{m}| = M_0âˆ£mâˆ£=M0â€‹, where M0M_0M0â€‹ is the saturation magnetization. In analogy to superfluid vortices, the effective "vorticity" in magnetic systems is localized along the vortex line, but the vector nature and constraint lead to a more complex structure. The magnetization curls in-plane around the core, with the core polarized out-of-plane (up or down). A single magnetic vortex stores two bits of information: one bit for the sense of circulation (clockwise or counterclockwise) and one bit for the core polarity (up or down).[9] In ferromagnets, these states enable compact data storage, with readout via microwave emissions from vortex dynamics. In antiferromagnets, vortices exhibit faster dynamics due to the absence of net magnetization and have been electrically created and controlled for potential memory devices. Changing the circulation or polarity involves topological transitions.[10] Recent advances include three-dimensional vortices in synthetic antiferromagnets, which move differently from 2D cases and offer enhanced stability for storage as of 2025.

Statistical mechanics of vortex lines

As first discussed by Onsager and Feynman,[1][2] if the temperature in a superfluid or a superconductor is raised, the vortex loops undergo proliferation leading to a second-order phase transition. This happens when the configurational entropy overcomes the energy cost, as the Boltzmann factor increasingly allows the thermal generation of vortex lines at higher temperatures. The lines proliferate uncontrollably. This proliferation destroys the long-range phase order of the superfluid or superconductor, transforming it into the normal state as the vortex cores, which are regions of normal liquid or normal conductor, respectively, come to dominate. The ensembles of vortex lines and their phase transitions can be described efficiently by a gauge theory.

Statistical mechanics of point vortices

In 1949 Onsager analysed a toy model consisting of a neutral system of point vortices confined to a finite area.[1] He was able to show that, due to the properties of two-dimensional point vortices the bounded area (and consequently, bounded phase space), allows the system to exhibit negative temperatures. Onsager provided the first prediction that some isolated systems can exhibit negative Boltzmann temperature. Onsager's prediction was confirmed experimentally for a system of quantum vortices in a Bose-Einstein condensate in 2019.[11][12]

Pair-interactions of quantum vortices

In a nonlinear quantum fluid, the dynamics and configurations of the vortex cores can be studied in terms of effective vortexâ€“vortex pair interactions. The effective intervortex potential is predicted to affect quantum phase transitions and giving rise to different few-vortex molecules and many-body vortex patterns.[13][14] Preliminary experiments in the specific system of exciton-polaritons fluids showed an effective attractiveâ€“repulsive intervortex dynamics between two cowinding vortices, whose attractive component can be modulated by the nonlinearity amount in the fluid.[15]

Spontaneous vortices

Quantum vortices can form via the Kibbleâ€“Zurek mechanism. As a condensate forms by quench cooling, separate protocondensates form with independent phases. As these phase domains merge quantum vortices can be trapped in the emerging condensate order parameter. Spontaneous quantum vortices were observed in atomic Boseâ€“Einstein condensates in 2008.[16]
