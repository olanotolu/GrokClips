City of Bell scandal

The City of Bell scandal encompassed the embezzlement and misappropriation of public funds by municipal officials in Bell, California—a small, low-income city of approximately 37,000 residents—primarily from 2005 to 2010, through the awarding of exorbitant salaries, unapproved benefits packages, and pension enhancements that collectively exceeded reasonable compensation levels for a jurisdiction of its size and fiscal constraints.[1][2] Central to the scheme was Robert Rizzo, the city's chief administrative officer, whose base salary escalated to $787,638 annually by 2010, supplemented by overtime pay, deferred compensation, and other perks that pushed his total annual remuneration above $1 million; his assistant, Angela Spaccia, received $336,000 in base pay, while Police Chief Frank Adams earned $457,000, and city council members were compensated around $100,000 per year—figures vastly disproportionate to those in comparable municipalities and funded in part by illicitly raised utility taxes and raided special funds like gas taxes and housing reserves.[1][3][4] Following arrests in September 2010, prosecutors charged officials with felonies including conspiracy, misappropriation exceeding $5.5 million, and conflict of interest; Rizzo pleaded no contest to 69 counts and was sentenced to 12 years in state prison with millions in restitution ordered, while Spaccia received nearly 12 years federally for related fraud, and five former council members were convicted of salary-related theft, drawing jail terms of one to two years alongside probation and repayments.[5][6][7] The affair underscored systemic vulnerabilities in local governance, including minimal resident oversight, opaque decision-making via unpublicized meetings, and inadequate checks on executive authority, ultimately spurring state-level pension reforms and enhanced transparency mandates while exposing how unchecked discretion in under-engaged communities enables predation on public treasuries.[4][8]

Background and Context

City of Bell Demographics and Governance Structure

The City of Bell, located in southeastern Los Angeles County, had a population of 35,477 according to the 2010 United States Census.[9] Approximately one-third of residents were under the age of 18, reflecting a young demographic with significant family-oriented households.[10] The population was overwhelmingly Hispanic or Latino, accounting for over 90% of residents, with more than half being foreign-born primarily from Central and South America.[11][12] Educational attainment was low, as nearly two-thirds of individuals over age 25 lacked a high school diploma, and median household incomes placed Bell among the poorer municipalities in the county, contributing to limited civic engagement and voter turnout.[12] Bell operates as a charter city under a council-manager form of government, as outlined in its municipal charter.[13] The legislative authority resides with a five-member City Council, whose members are elected at-large to staggered four-year terms and serve on a part-time basis without full-time salaries.[14] The council is responsible for enacting ordinances, approving budgets, and setting policy, but it delegates extensive administrative duties to an appointed Chief Administrative Officer (CAO), also known as the City Manager.[13] The City Manager holds broad executive powers, including oversight of all city departments, contract negotiations, personnel management, and implementation of council directives, functioning as the de facto chief executive.[13] There is no separately elected mayor; the council annually selects one of its members to serve as mayor pro tempore, who presides over meetings and represents the city ceremonially but possesses no independent veto authority or additional powers beyond those of other council members.[14] This structure, modeled on the council-manager plan prevalent in many small U.S. municipalities, prioritizes professional administration over direct elected executive control, though it depends on robust council scrutiny and resident participation to prevent unchecked authority.[12]

Pre-Scandal Financial and Administrative Practices

The City of Bell operated under a council-manager form of government, featuring a five-member city council elected at-large to staggered four-year terms, which appointed a city manager responsible for administering city operations, preparing budgets, and directing staff.[12][15] The council, which selected a mayor from among its members, focused on policy-setting, while the city manager exercised broad executive authority over departments, contracts, and financial proposals submitted for council approval.[12] Robert Rizzo assumed the city manager role in November 1993 with an initial annual salary of $72,000, a figure $7,000 below his predecessor's, and over time expanded administrative control through direct oversight of key functions like public safety and redevelopment initiatives.[8][16] In 2005, Rizzo proposed and the city council endorsed a special election to amend Bell's charter, converting it to a charter municipality; the measure passed with participation from approximately 400 voters, representing about 1.1% of eligible residents, which exempted the city from certain state-imposed limits on municipal salaries and administrative practices.[16][12] This structural shift granted greater local discretion in compensation and governance, with the city manager's office handling personnel contracts, benefit packages, and supplemental payments often embedded in employment agreements rather than base salaries.[12] Financially, Bell maintained an annual general fund operating budget of roughly $13 million to $17 million in the years leading to 2010, augmented by revenues from its redevelopment agency (RDA), which captured property tax increments for urban renewal projects and city operations.[17][18] RDA budgets, along with general fund proposals, were prepared by administrative staff under the city manager and adopted directly by the city council, bypassing a separate RDA governing board and integrating redevelopment expenditures—such as loans and land acquisitions—into broader city finances without distinct minute-keeping or expense segregation.[18] Administrative practices emphasized internal management of fees, bonds, and inter-fund transfers, with council meetings approving budgets annually but limited public scrutiny due to at-large elections and historically low voter engagement in a predominantly low-income, immigrant-heavy population.[12]

Exposure of Corruption

Los Angeles Times Investigative Reporting

In July 2010, Los Angeles Times reporters Ruben Vives and Jeff Gottlieb initiated an investigation into governance irregularities in Bell, California, prompted by public records requests and tips about exorbitant public salaries in the small, low-income city.[19] Their probe began after examining financial disclosures from neighboring Maywood, which revealed similar anomalies, leading them to scrutinize Bell's operations, including city council meetings and payroll data obtained via California's Public Records Act. On July 22, 2010, the Times published a front-page article headlined "Is a city manager worth $800,000?", exposing City Manager Robert Rizzo's annual compensation of approximately $787,000, supplemented by additional benefits pushing his total pay over $1 million, in a city with a median household income under $30,000.[20] [21] The reporting detailed how Rizzo and Assistant City Manager Angela Spaccia received salaries far exceeding those of comparable municipal executives, with Spaccia earning over $376,000 annually, justified through opaque supplemental contracts and board approvals lacking public transparency.[19] Further articles revealed that city council members were paid up to $100 per legitimate council meeting but received thousands more through hidden "consulting" fees and participation in phantom committees, totaling over $1.6 million in unauthorized compensation from 2005 to 2010.[22] Investigators cross-referenced state pension records, tax filings, and attendance logs, uncovering that officials often claimed full pay for non-existent meetings, such as sports leagues or redevelopment boards that met infrequently or not at all.[23] Vives and Gottlieb faced resistance, including threats from officials and denials of record access, but persisted by leveraging whistleblower accounts from disgruntled former employees and analyzing municipal budgets showing deficits masked by utility tax hikes and redevelopment fund diversions.[19] Their series, published between July and September 2010, estimated public fund misappropriation exceeding $5 million, sparking resident protests, state audits, and FBI involvement.[24] The exposure culminated in the September 22, 2010, arrests of Rizzo, Spaccia, and six council members on charges including theft and conflict of interest, with Los Angeles County District Attorney Steve Cooley describing the scheme as "corruption on steroids."[23] [25] The Times' work earned the 2011 Pulitzer Prize for Public Service, recognizing its role in dismantling entrenched corruption through meticulous data-driven journalism that prompted legislative reforms, such as California's 2010 law mandating online disclosure of local official salaries.[22] [26] Subsequent reporting tracked legal proceedings, including Rizzo's 2013 no-contest plea to 69 felony counts and a 12-year prison sentence, underscoring the scandal's scale in a city of 37,000 residents where poverty rates exceeded 20%.[27]

Immediate Arrests and Public Outcry

On September 21, 2010, Los Angeles County District Attorney Steve Cooley announced the arrests of eight current and former Bell city officials, charging them with multiple counts of misappropriation of public funds totaling more than $5.5 million, along with related corruption offenses such as conflict of interest and filing false documents.[28][6] The arrested individuals included former City Manager Robert Rizzo, Assistant City Manager Angela Spaccia, Mayor Oscar Hernandez, Vice Mayor Teresa Jacobo, and council members George Mirabal, Luis Artiga, George Cole, and Victor Bello.[29][30] Cooley described the scheme as "corruption on steroids," emphasizing how officials had inflated their compensation through secret agreements and misused taxpayer money in a city where median household income was approximately $30,000 annually.[31][28] The arrests capped a summer of escalating investigations triggered by July 2010 Los Angeles Times reporting on exorbitant salaries, intensifying preexisting resident fury in Bell, a predominantly Latino working-class enclave with poverty rates exceeding 20%.[19] Prior to the arrests, hundreds of locals had marched in July and August, protesting outside City Hall and officials' homes while demanding resignations and launching recall petitions against council members, decrying the betrayal by leaders who paid themselves up to 10 times the regional average for similar roles.[32][33] Following the raids—which involved SWAT teams and a battering ram at one suspect's home—community reaction mixed vindication with deepened distrust, as residents displayed celebratory banners outside City Hall and called for broader reforms to prevent future abuses.[6] The events fueled nationwide media coverage and scrutiny of municipal governance, with Bell's scandal highlighting vulnerabilities in small, unincorporated-adjacent cities lacking robust oversight.[34][35] A state audit released the next day corroborated the charges, documenting millions in misused funds for non-essential expenses like lavish perks, further stoking demands for transparency and accountability from California's political leaders.[36]

Mechanisms of Corruption

Excessive Compensation and Pensions

Robert Rizzo, the city administrator of Bell, California, received an annual base salary of approximately $787,000 in 2010, supplemented by benefits that pushed his total compensation above $1 million annually, including contributions to deferred compensation plans and other perks.[3] [5] Rizzo's salary had escalated through mechanisms such as supplemental contracts disguised as consulting fees and performance bonuses, which prosecutors later deemed illegal as they lacked proper city council approval or public disclosure.[37] [12] Angela Spaccia, Rizzo's assistant city manager, earned up to $564,000 annually by 2010, with her pay similarly inflated via unauthorized raises and add-ons that violated state laws on public compensation transparency.[38] [39] City council members, meanwhile, were paid about $7,666 monthly—equating to roughly $92,000 yearly—far exceeding typical part-time council stipends in comparable small municipalities, often justified under the city's charter as full-time roles despite minimal oversight duties.[1] These salaries funded lavish pensions under California's Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS), with Rizzo's initially projected at over $600,000 annually upon retirement, calculated on his inflated earnings and including deferred compensation pots exceeding $2 million.[3] [40] Spaccia's pension was similarly bloated, leading to California Attorney General lawsuits in 2010 to claw back overpaid salaries and void pension accruals tied to illegal pay, arguing that such compensation violated public trust laws and deprived the impoverished city of funds for services.[1] [41]

Misappropriation of Public Funds

In the City of Bell scandal, officials misappropriated public funds through unauthorized loans, improper asset purchases, and diversion of bond proceeds, separate from excessive compensation arrangements. A 2010 state audit by the California State Controller's Office identified $5.5 million in such misappropriations by eight arrested officials, including City Manager Robert Rizzo and Assistant City Manager Angela Spaccia, who faced charges for conspiracy and theft exceeding $5.5 million via these mechanisms.[36][42] A primary method involved illegal loans from city coffers, totaling approximately $1.5 million distributed between 2002 and 2010 to about 50 public employees and associates, including amounts ranging from $1,000 to $130,000 per recipient. These loans, approved without City Council consent or legal authorization under city ordinances, were later deemed illegal gifts of public funds by auditors, as they provided no discernible public benefit and lacked repayment mechanisms.[16][36] Rizzo personally directed nearly $95,000 in city money to repay his own private loans in 2005 and 2006, bypassing council approval and falsifying records to conceal the transactions.[42] Bond funds approved by voters in 2003 for infrastructure were also mismanaged, with over $50 million issued without feasible plans or demonstrated need, including $23.5 million left idle in a non-interest-bearing account earmarked for an unbuilt sports complex that remained a vacant dirt lot as of 2010. Rizzo exercised unchecked control over these funds, enabling their diversion without oversight.[36] Additional misappropriations included a $4.8 million city purchase of vacant commercial property from a former mayor in the mid-2000s, lacking documentation for justification, appraisal, or subsequent use, and $10.4 million funneled to development firms owned by a contractor simultaneously serving as the city's planning director, raising conflict-of-interest concerns. Five former council members—Robert Vasquez, Teresa Jacobo, George Mirabal, Luis Arteaga, and Victor Bello—pleaded no contest in 2016 to felony misappropriation charges related to these schemes, receiving probation and restitution orders.[36][16][23] Rizzo and Spaccia's convictions on misappropriation counts contributed to their lengthy prison sentences in 2014, with Rizzo receiving 12 years after pleading no contest to 69 related felonies.[5]

Imposition of Illegal Fees and Taxes

In 2007, the Bell City Council passed a resolution imposing a 50% property tax increase over three years, ostensibly to fund pension obligations, but an audit by California State Controller John Chiang determined this exceeded state caps and was illegal, resulting in the collection of approximately $2.9 million from over 4,000 property owners.[43][44] The tax hike was rolled back following the scandal's exposure, and on September 13, 2010, Lieutenant Governor Abel Maldonado signed Assembly Bill 900, mandating the refund of $2.8 million plus interest to affected residents through Los Angeles County.[43] Separately, Bell increased sewer fees in 2007 without obtaining the required voter approval mandated by state law, charging up to $33.12 per parcel—exceeding the allowable maximum of $12.70—and collecting $621,737 in overpayments from property owners between 2007 and 2010.[45] State auditors identified this as a third instance of unlawful taxation, with no immediate constitutional mechanism for refunds of prior overcharges, though officials were advised to offset future bills or issue rebates if confirmed illegal.[46][45] Business license taxes were also illegally inflated by more than 50% over the decade prior to 2010, contributing to broader revenue schemes that state audits linked to funding exorbitant city salaries and pensions.[47] Additional unauthorized hikes included trash collection fees and utility assessments, which a 2010 state controller's review classified as part of $5.6 million in total illegal taxes and fees requiring repayment.[48][49] These impositions lacked proper legal authorization, such as voter consent or compliance with state limits, and were executed under the direction of city administrators to obscure and sustain fiscal irregularities.[36]

Electoral Irregularities

Manipulation of Charter Votes

In 2005, City Administrator Robert Rizzo proposed a special municipal election to convert Bell from a general-law city to a charter city, a change that would exempt municipal salaries from state-imposed population-based limits under California Government Code Section 36505.[50] The measure passed on November 8, 2005, with 336 votes in favor and 54 against, representing turnout from fewer than 400 voters in a city of approximately 40,000 residents—less than 1% of the population.[50][19] This minimal participation, achieved through a low-profile special election rather than a regularly scheduled one, effectively bypassed broader public scrutiny and ensured the measure's approval without significant opposition.[51] The charter conversion enabled Bell officials to subsequently approve exorbitant compensation packages, including Rizzo's annual salary exceeding $787,000 by 2008, far beyond the state cap of roughly $97,000 for a city manager in a municipality of Bell's size.[50] Critics, including subsequent investigations, highlighted the election's obscurity as a deliberate tactic to manipulate outcomes, as special elections typically yield lower turnout dominated by informed or motivated insiders, allowing entrenched officials to alter governance structures in their favor.[2] While no direct evidence of ballot tampering emerged for this specific vote, the process exemplified broader electoral irregularities in Bell, where low-visibility voting facilitated unrepresentative decisions that entrenched corruption.[52] Post-scandal analyses noted that the charter status, secured through this vote, directly contributed to the misappropriation of public funds by removing external salary constraints.[8]

Instances of Voter Fraud

In 2010, as part of the broader corruption probe, the Los Angeles County District Attorney's office investigated allegations of voter fraud in Bell's municipal elections, focusing on improper handling of absentee ballots. Residents reported that city officials, including council members, visited homes to collect absentee ballots, often pressuring voters to sign blank forms or fill them out in favor of incumbents.[53] These practices were said to have inflated support for candidates linked to the Rizzo administration, with absentee ballots comprising an unusually high 61% of votes in some contests, far exceeding typical rates and enabling low-turnout victories for entrenched officials. The investigation originated from a lawsuit filed by former Bell police officer Jason Barragan, who alleged systematic fraud in prior elections to perpetuate corrupt leadership. California Attorney General Jerry Brown expanded the inquiry by establishing a dedicated hotline in August 2010 for reports of voting irregularities, particularly absentee ballot abuses such as unauthorized collection and electioneering at voters' residences.[54] Complaints highlighted officials picking up ballots directly from voters, violating state laws on ballot secrecy and independence.[55] Analysts linked these tactics to the scandal's origins, arguing that fraudulent absentee voting in earlier elections, such as those in the late 1990s and 2000s, secured the election of figures who later enabled exorbitant salaries and fund misappropriation.[56] A notable prior instance involved former Bell Mayor Robert Dellavalle, who resigned in 2002 after conviction on charges of voter fraud and conspiracy related to manipulating local election outcomes to benefit allies. While no high-profile convictions directly tied top scandal figures like Robert Rizzo to voter fraud, the probes underscored how electoral manipulations sustained the corrupt regime, contributing to the overall collapse of democratic accountability in Bell.[57]

Principal Actors

Robert Rizzo and Angela Spaccia

Robert Rizzo served as Bell's chief administrative officer starting in 1993 and later as city manager until his resignation amid scandal in July 2010.[58][59] Alongside assistant city manager Angela Spaccia, Rizzo orchestrated a scheme to inflate their compensation through unauthorized salary hikes, excessive benefits, and pension enhancements, exploiting lax oversight in the small city of roughly 37,000 residents.[5] Rizzo's base salary reached $787,637 annually by 2010, with total compensation surpassing $1 million including deferred compensation and other perks, and peaking at over $1.5 million when factoring in supplemental retirement contributions.[60][3] Spaccia joined Bell in July 2003 as assistant city manager with an initial salary of $102,310, which escalated to $376,000 by 2008 via backroom deals and fabricated justifications like a "secret formula" for pay calculations.[61] Her total annual pay and benefits hit $564,000, augmented by 26 weeks of vacation and sick leave treated as cash equivalents, effectively adding nearly 50% to her base through misclassified accruals.[62][63] The duo concealed these arrangements from the city council by relying on verbal contracts, destroying records, and securing no-bid loans from city funds, diverting millions intended for public services.[64] Rizzo pleaded no contest in October 2013 to 69 felony counts, encompassing 46 charges of misappropriation of public funds, conflict of interest, and related corruption offenses.[59] In April 2014, he was sentenced to 12 years in state prison and mandated to pay $8.8 million in restitution, having already repaid partial amounts from seized assets like his pension fund.[65] Separately, in a federal tax evasion case, Rizzo admitted underreporting over $700,000 in income via a sham corporation and received 33 months imprisonment in 2014.[5][37] Spaccia, tried separately, was convicted in December 2013 on 10 of 13 counts including conspiracy to misappropriate public funds, conflict of interest, and secretion of official records.[66] She was sentenced in April 2014 to 11 years and eight months in prison, with restitution exceeding $8.2 million to Bell, reflecting her complicity in salary manipulations and fraudulent benefit schemes.[61] Both expressed remorse at sentencing—Rizzo admitting he "lost sight" of public trust, Spaccia deeming her pay "perhaps unethical" but denying illegality—yet courts emphasized their deliberate exploitation of unchecked authority.[5][62]

City Council Members

The City of Bell's council members played a central role in the scandal by approving exorbitant compensation packages for themselves and city administrators, including votes to create and staff lucrative board positions that inflated their annual earnings to approximately $100,000 for part-time duties, far exceeding norms for similar small municipalities.[6][67] Key figures included Mayor Oscar Hernandez, Vice Mayor Teresa Jacobo, and council members George Mirabal, Luis Artiga, George Cole, and Victor Bello, who were arrested on September 21, 2010, alongside administrator Robert Rizzo on state charges of misappropriation of public funds, conspiracy, and falsifying records related to these pay hikes.[6][31] These officials had routinely rubber-stamped Rizzo's recommendations, including their own salary increases via interim board roles, without public disclosure or justification tied to the city's limited budget and population of about 37,000 residents.[67][4] In March 2013, Hernandez, Jacobo, and Mirabal were convicted by a Los Angeles County jury on five felony counts each of misappropriation of public funds for voting to pay themselves excessive fees from city-created boards like the Community Sustainability Agency and Public Finance Authority, actions prosecutors described as self-dealing that drained taxpayer resources.[68][69] Cole and Bello, who served shorter terms, were convicted on one count each of similar misappropriation for participating in these schemes.[69] Artiga faced separate charges from the California Fair Political Practices Commission for failing to report income from these positions, though he was not part of the core 2013 trial group.[70] By April 2014, five former council members—Hernandez, Jacobo, Mirabal, Cole, and Bello—accepted a plea deal, pleading no contest to misappropriation charges in exchange for reduced sentences, with the city agreeing to seek up to $7 million in restitution from them collectively.[71] Sentencing outcomes included one year in county jail for Jacobo and Hernandez, plus five years' probation and community service for others like Mirabal, who received 1,000 hours alongside restitution obligations averaging $200,000–$250,000 per individual to repay illicit gains.[7][72] These convictions stemmed from evidence of closed-door meetings and undisclosed votes that prioritized personal enrichment over fiscal accountability, contributing to Bell's near-bankruptcy.[73]

Police and Other Officials

Randy Adams served as Bell's chief of police from July 2009 to July 2010, during which he earned an annual salary of $457,000 plus $260,000 in retirement benefits, exceeding compensation for chiefs in much larger agencies like the Los Angeles Police Department despite overseeing a 46-officer force in a low-income city of about 37,000 residents.[74][75] Adams testified that he initially questioned whether Bell could afford him but proceeded after negotiations with city administrator Robert Rizzo, later expressing surprise at the city's capacity to hire him given its demographics and budget constraints.[76][77] He resigned shortly after the Los Angeles Times exposed the city's exorbitant executive pay in July 2010, amid public outrage over the department's disproportionate costs relative to its minimal workload, including low crime rates and limited patrols.[4][78] Adams faced no criminal charges despite his role in the compensation scheme, which prosecutors later described as involving concealment from public oversight; he testified as a defense witness in the 2013 federal corruption trial of assistant city manager Angela Spaccia, arguing his pay aligned with benchmarks from other agencies after factoring in longevity supplements and benefits.[79][80][77] In 2015, the California Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS) halved his requested pension—reducing it from over $200,000 annually—ruling that his salary had been illegally spiked through accumulated leave and hidden from transparency requirements, a decision that established precedent for challenging similar public pensions derived from fraudulent pay structures.[81][78] Adams had sought reinstatement from prior retirement to claim these benefits, but CalPERS rejected the application due to the underlying irregularities.[82] Internal police department whistleblowing highlighted further misconduct; Sergeant James Corcoran reported instances of voter fraud, unlawful vehicle seizures for revenue, and other abuses to Adams in 2010, but claimed the chief responded with anger and that he suffered retaliation including demotion and forced retirement.[83] Corcoran prevailed in a 2012 whistleblower lawsuit, securing a $400,000 settlement from the city, though experts noted this was below potential trial awards given evidence of systemic retaliation against dissenters.[83] Separate probes uncovered pension abuses extending to prior police leadership; by 2012, Bell disclosed that three former chiefs and additional officers had received over $10 million in disability pensions, often approved without rigorous medical verification and tied to the same opaque administrative practices that inflated Rizzo-era contracts, prompting state scrutiny of the department's overall fiscal accountability.[84]

Investigations and Legal Actions

State-Level Probes and Hearings

The California State Controller's Office, under Controller John Chiang, launched multiple audits of Bell's finances shortly after the scandal's exposure in July 2010.[18] These probes examined administrative and internal accounting controls, bond fund management, and overall fiscal practices for periods including fiscal year 2009-2010.[85] A September 22, 2010, audit report detailed the city's misuse of over $50 million in bond proceeds intended for public improvements, including improper transfers to general funds and undocumented expenditures.[36] It also identified nearly $7 million in illegally collected business license taxes from 2005 to 2010, which exceeded state caps and lacked voter approval, alongside exorbitant salaries that violated public contracting laws.[36][86] Further audits revealed a general fund shortfall exceeding $1 million as of June 2010, stemming from unsustainable spending on administrative costs that consumed 80% of revenues, and questioned $199,528 in park improvement expenditures funded by state grants due to missing contracts and documentation.[87][18] In response, Chiang mandated all California cities to disclose employee salaries online by December 1, 2010, and ordered Bell officials to repay misappropriated funds.[57] The California Attorney General's Office complemented these efforts with a civil lawsuit filed in September 2010 against seven officials, seeking recovery of over $7 million in excessive compensation and invalidation of pension benefits calculated on inflated salaries.[1] Public hearings on the audit findings occurred on November 8, 2010, in Bell, where state officials presented evidence of fiscal mismanagement to residents and outlined recovery steps, including demands for restitution.[88][89] These sessions highlighted systemic failures, such as loans from bond funds to private entities without oversight, deemed improper by auditors.[8] State legislative leaders, including Assembly Speaker John Pérez and Senate President pro Tem Darrell Steinberg, referenced the probes in August 2010 announcements of reform bills targeting local government transparency, though no formal legislative committee hearings on Bell-specific testimony were convened.[90] The audits' revelations informed subsequent state laws enacted in 2011, such as requirements for public salary disclosures and caps on interim official pay.[41]

Federal Charges and Trials

In December 2013, Robert Rizzo, the former City Manager of Bell, agreed to plead guilty to federal charges of conspiracy to defraud the United States and filing a false federal income tax return, stemming from efforts to evade taxes on income derived from his inflated compensation in the Bell scandal.[91] The scheme involved Rizzo establishing R.A. Rizzo Incorporated to fraudulently claim business losses and deduct personal expenses—such as country club dues and luxury vehicle costs—against over $1 million in unreported or mischaracterized income received from Bell between 2005 and 2009, resulting in approximately $500,000 in evaded federal taxes.[37][92] Rizzo formalized his guilty plea in January 2014 in the U.S. District Court for the Central District of California.[37] The case, investigated by the FBI and IRS Criminal Investigation, highlighted how Rizzo manipulated corporate structures to shelter illicit gains from municipal corruption, including fraudulent deductions totaling $571,530 from 2006 to 2009 and misreported personal expenditures of $80,000 in 2009 and $120,000 in 2010.[37] No trial occurred, as Rizzo's plea resolved the matter; he was sentenced on February 3, 2014, to 33 months in federal prison, to run consecutively with his state corruption sentence, plus restitution of $255,984 to the IRS.[37] Federal charges did not extend to other Bell officials, such as Angela Spaccia or city council members, whose prosecutions for core corruption acts like salary inflation and phantom benefits proceeded exclusively in California state courts.[5] The U.S. Department of Justice emphasized the tax case as targeting Rizzo's secondary efforts to conceal corruption proceeds, rather than the underlying municipal fraud, which state authorities handled through indictments under California Penal Code provisions for misappropriation of public funds.[37]

Convictions, Sentencing, and Restitution

Robert Rizzo, the former chief administrative officer of Bell, was convicted on 23 felony counts including conspiracy to misappropriate public funds and was sentenced on April 16, 2014, to 12 years in state prison.[93] He was ordered to pay nearly $9 million in restitution jointly with Angela Spaccia, of which approximately $8.6 million remained after prior payments of $242,719.56.[93] In a separate federal tax evasion case, Rizzo was sentenced on August 28, 2017, to 33 months in prison and $255,984 in restitution to the IRS for underreported income tied to the scandal.[37][94] Angela Spaccia, Rizzo's former assistant, was convicted on 11 felony counts of corruption and sentenced on April 10, 2014, to 11 years and 8 months in state prison.[38][61] She was ordered to pay over $8.2 million in restitution to the City of Bell, sharing liability with Rizzo for exorbitant salaries and benefits extracted without public input.[95][96] Five former City Council members—Oscar Hernandez, Teresa Jacobo, George Mirabal, George Cole, and Victor Bello—were convicted in March 2013 on multiple counts of misappropriation of public funds for approving inflated salaries and benefits in secret meetings.[68] Their sentences, handed down in mid-2014, ranged from 1 to 2 years in prison or county jail, with each ordered to pay $241,000–$242,000 in restitution reflecting their pro-rated share of illicit compensation.[7][97][98]

Immediate Aftermath

2011 Special Election

In response to the City of Bell corruption scandal, which involved criminal charges against multiple officials for misappropriating public funds through exorbitant salaries and other schemes, voters held a combined general municipal and special recall election on March 8, 2011.[99] The recall targeted Mayor Oscar Hernandez, council members Teresa Jacobo and George Mirabal, and the seat previously held by Luis Artiga, who had resigned amid the probe.) Incumbent councilman Lorenzo Velez, the sole member not facing charges, sought re-election but garnered just 613 votes.[100] Voters overwhelmingly approved all recalls, with more than 95% supporting the ouster of Hernandez, Jacobo, Mirabal, and Artiga's successor.[101][100] This resulted in the complete replacement of the five-member city council, a historic outcome reflecting widespread public anger over the scandal's revelations, including city manager Robert Rizzo's reported annual salary exceeding $787,000.) The newly elected council consisted of Violeta Alvarez, Danny Harber, Ana Maria Quintana, Ali Saleh, and Nestor Valencia, who were viewed as reform-oriented candidates backed by community groups opposing the prior regime.[99]) Voter turnout reached approximately 33% of the city's 10,485 registered voters, or about 3,460 ballots cast—unusually high for Bell's typically low-participation elections and indicative of the scandal's galvanizing effect.[99] The results were certified by the Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors, clearing the new officials for swearing-in and paving the way for interim governance amid ongoing investigations.[102] This election represented a direct civic repudiation of the entrenched leadership implicated in the financial abuses that had drained millions from the impoverished municipality.[101][100]

Short-Term Financial Crisis

Following the July 2010 exposure of widespread corruption, a financial review by Los Angeles County officials revealed that the City of Bell had incurred deficits totaling several million dollars across at least the prior three fiscal years, primarily due to exorbitant compensation packages for top officials—including over $1.5 million annually in total pay and benefits for city manager Robert Rizzo—and the diversion of bond proceeds for non-permissible uses.[103] By early January 2011, the county auditor-controller estimated a $2.5 million to $4.5 million shortfall specifically for the 2010-2011 fiscal year, stemming from unrealistic revenue projections, underestimated expenditures, and illegal revenue streams such as unauthorized property taxes and business fees that could no longer be sustained.[104] This deficit threatened the city's $13.5 million general fund, serving a population of approximately 40,000, and was compounded by liabilities exceeding $5 million in mandated refunds for illegally collected taxes, plus over $600,000 in legal fees accrued since the scandal broke.[103][104] The immediate crisis risked default on roughly $35 million in municipal bonds and prompted considerations of severe austerity measures, including the potential dissolution of Bell's police department—which consumed about $6 million yearly—and replacement with Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department services at an estimated $3.8 million cost, alongside broader cuts to essential operations.[103][40] Interim administrators responded by drafting fiscal sustainability plans, rolling back illicit fees, and leveraging state legislation (AB 900) to facilitate taxpayer refunds, though these steps offered only partial mitigation amid ongoing legal and administrative upheaval.[104]

Reforms and Recovery Efforts

Implemented Anti-Corruption Measures

Following the 2010 exposure of widespread corruption, the City of Bell implemented several targeted anti-corruption measures aimed at enhancing transparency, ethical standards, and financial oversight. These reforms, largely enacted between 2011 and 2013 under a new city council elected in a special March 2011 election and subsequent professional management, included the adoption of formal ethics codes and structural governance changes to prevent recurrence of excessive compensation, conflicts of interest, and opaque decision-making.[8][105] A key initiative was the establishment of a Personal Code of Conduct for City Councilmembers, requiring members to prioritize the public interest, disclose potential conflicts of interest, and annually reaffirm their commitment to these standards; all councilmembers signed the code post-scandal. Complementing this, an Employee Code of Ethics was introduced for city staff, mandating honesty, integrity, and avoidance of personal gain from official duties. These codes were designed to address the prior lack of enforceable ethical guidelines that enabled officials like former City Manager Robert Rizzo to amass salaries exceeding $787,000 annually without adequate scrutiny.[8] Transparency efforts were bolstered through a revamped city website launched in spring 2013, featuring a dedicated Transparency Portal that publicly posts detailed budgets, itemized expenditures, employee salaries, vendor contracts, and council meeting minutes and agendas, with materials available in Spanish and optimized for mobile access. This portal received an "A-" rating in the 2013 Sunshine Review awards for accessibility, marking a shift from pre-scandal opacity where financial data was not routinely disclosed. Additionally, an online public records request form streamlined citizen access to information, fostering greater accountability.[106][8] Financial controls were strengthened via revised procurement procedures, which mandate competitive bidding for contracts above specified thresholds and require recusal from decisions involving personal conflicts, directly countering the no-bid deals and self-dealing prevalent under the prior regime. The city also cleared a three-year audit backlog by completing reviews for fiscal years 2009/10 through 2012/13 by 2013, enabling ongoing independent verification of fiscal health. In governance, the hiring of permanent department heads for finance, community services, and development in fall 2012, alongside City Manager Doug Willmore in June 2012—who adhered to the International City/County Management Association's Code of Ethics—professionalized operations and reduced reliance on interim or conflicted appointees.[8][106][105] These measures were supported by heightened civic oversight, with city council meetings drawing crowds of up to 2,000 residents by the mid-2010s—compared to fewer than five attendees pre-scandal—and the incorporation of public input into annual goal-setting forums, promoting sustained community vigilance against mismanagement. Conflict-of-interest policies were further embedded citywide, limiting officials' ability to hold multiple remunerated roles or benefit from public funds, as evaluated in independent assessments. While these reforms contributed to fiscal stabilization, including debt reduction and reserve growth to 200% of expenditures by 2014, their effectiveness relies on consistent enforcement and resident engagement.[107][8]

Challenges in Rebuilding Trust

Despite the convictions of key figures like former City Administrator Robert Rizzo, who was sentenced to 12 years in prison in 2014 for misappropriating over $5.5 million in public funds, residents of Bell exhibited deep-seated skepticism toward local government, manifesting in public hostility such as jeers directed at interim officials and demands for police escorts at council meetings.[5][108] This distrust persisted due to perceptions that mismanagement continued, with residents voicing concerns like "Our money is still being abused" amid delays in refunding over $3 million in overcharged taxes as late as 2013.[109][108] Rebuilding operational capacity proved challenging, as the scandal left only one department head in place, creating widespread vacancies in key positions that hampered service delivery and financial stabilization efforts.[105] New council members, all inexperienced first-time public officials elected in the 2011 recall, faced difficulties attracting qualified candidates wary of the city's tarnished reputation, further straining governance.[105][108] Structural limitations exacerbated trust issues, including Bell's small size and limited budget, which restricted internal oversight and made the city reliant on external agencies like the Los Angeles County District Attorney for corruption detection, often only after major scandals surfaced.[8] Incomplete online transparency, such as missing or non-searchable meeting minutes and financial disclosures, continued to erode confidence, while the city manager's dual role in enforcing anti-fraud policies lacked sufficient independence to assure impartiality.[8] Legal challenges from ongoing prosecutions of former officials diverted resources and prolonged uncertainty, underscoring the need for enhanced mechanisms like an independent ombudsman to foster verifiable accountability.[105][8]

Persistent Issues and Further Probes

Subsequent Financial Mismanagement

Following the exposure of the 2010 corruption scandal, the City of Bell grappled with severe fiscal distress exacerbated by prior illicit bond issuances and uncollected revenues. An audit released on January 6, 2011, projected that continued spending patterns would result in a $2.2 million deficit by the end of the fiscal year, highlighting inadequate budgetary controls amid the leadership vacuum.[110] The city entered default on $35 million in lease-revenue bonds originally issued during Robert Rizzo's tenure to finance property acquisitions, with credit rating agencies downgrading Bell's obligations to junk status due to insufficient cash flows and repayment capacity.[111][112] A May 2013 state controller's audit further revealed a general fund balance shortfall exceeding $1 million, compounded by over $3 million owed to residents in refunds for illegally levied taxes such as trash fees and business licenses that had been deemed unauthorized.[87] These obligations stemmed from the scandal's revelation of improper revenue practices, but post-scandal administrators failed to promptly refund collections or restructure liabilities, pushing the city toward insolvency and necessitating emergency interventions like property short sales.[112] The persistence of negative fund balances and hiring difficulties for financial overseers underscored ongoing weaknesses in fiscal governance.[113] Debt restructuring efforts dragged into 2014, including a $257,000 IRS fine to retain tax-exempt status on the defaulted bonds, while approximately 55 pending lawsuits threatened additional liabilities capable of bankrupting the municipality.[114][115] Although legal settlements totaling $5.5 million and property disposals yielding $15.5 million provided temporary relief by late 2013, the episode illustrated how entrenched debt from corrupt practices, combined with delayed corrective actions, prolonged financial instability into the mid-2010s.[112]

2021 Corruption Charges

In September 2021, Los Angeles County District Attorney's Office filed charges against Paul Philips, then-city manager of Bell, for his role in misappropriating public funds during his prior tenure as city manager in the City of Industry. Philips faced one felony count of misappropriation of public funds, stemming from the approval of approximately $20 million in advances to San Gabriel Valley Water and Power—a company owned by developer William Barkett—for a proposed 450-megawatt solar energy project on the Tres Hermanos Ranch spanning Chino Hills and Diamond Bar. The funds, disbursed between 2016 and 2018, were intended to initiate the project but were never repaid after it collapsed without progress, with allegations that Barkett diverted at least $8.3 million to personal uses including luxury purchases and falsified invoices for overbilling, such as inflating a $166,828 expense to $300,000.[116][117] Barkett, arrested on September 1, 2021, and held on $1 million bail, was charged with multiple felonies including embezzlement, grand theft, money laundering, and misappropriation of public funds. Former State Senator Frank Hill and attorney Anthony Bouza, who held financial interests in the deal, each faced multiple counts of conflict of interest and one count of misappropriation of public funds; Bouza was additionally accused of owing $1.5 million in unrepaid obligations and was released on $45,000 bond following his arrest the same day. Philips surrendered to authorities shortly after the charges were announced, posting $25,000 bail ahead of a plea hearing scheduled for September 10, 2021, while Hill's arraignment was set for September 7. The case, investigated amid a 2019 civil lawsuit by the City of Industry against Barkett's firm, underscored oversight lapses in regional public-private energy ventures, though no direct ties to Bell's 2010 scandal were alleged beyond Philips' subsequent hiring by the city.[116][117]

Broader Implications

Role of Journalism and Oversight

The Los Angeles Times investigation was instrumental in uncovering the City of Bell scandal, beginning with public records requests that revealed exorbitant compensation for officials in a city with a median household income of approximately $30,000. On July 20, 2010, the newspaper reported that City Manager Robert Rizzo received an annual salary of $787,637—nearly double President Barack Obama's pay at the time—along with benefits pushing total compensation over $1 million, while assistant city manager Angela Spaccia earned $376,288.[118][19] Part-time city council members drew nearly $100,000 yearly, including supplemental payments disguised as redevelopment agency stipends, exceeding legal limits and norms for cities of Bell's size.[119] This reporting, led by journalists such as Jeff Gottlieb and Ruben Vives, extended from probes into neighboring Maywood's finances and ignited public outrage, prompting three officials' resignations within days and accelerating scrutiny of opaque municipal practices like unadvertised meetings and hidden pension enhancements.[24][120] The Times' series, which documented over $5 million in alleged misappropriation, earned the 2011 Pulitzer Prize for Public Service, recognizing its role in spurring arrests of eight officials on September 21, 2010, by the Los Angeles County District Attorney.[121][19] Governmental oversight prior to exposure proved inadequate, as annual audits by firms like Moss Adams—hired by the city itself—overlooked red flags such as illegal tax levies, bond fund mismanagement exceeding $50 million, and conflicts of interest, reflecting broader failures in self-policing by small municipalities with limited internal controls.[122][36] State and county regulators, including the California Controller's office, had not intervened despite Bell's chronic budget deficits and low resident engagement, allowing corruption to persist unchecked for years.[4] Post-exposure, oversight intensified through coordinated probes: the district attorney's office filed 20 felony counts against Rizzo and others for salary padding and fund looting; federal authorities, including the FBI, investigated related pension abuses; and a state legislative hearing on November 8, 2010, examined governance lapses, leading to convictions like Rizzo's 12-year sentence in 2013.[19][2] These actions recovered millions but revealed systemic vulnerabilities, such as weak checks in council-manager structures, underscoring journalism's compensatory function where bureaucratic inertia prevailed.[8] The episode demonstrated how media-driven accountability can catalyze prosecutorial and legislative responses in locales lacking robust independent auditing.[123]

Lessons for Local Government Accountability

The City of Bell scandal exemplified systemic failures in local accountability, where unchecked executive authority and minimal public scrutiny enabled officials to siphon public funds through exorbitant salaries—such as city manager Robert Rizzo's $787,637 annual pay—and opaque decision-making processes, persisting undetected for years until journalistic exposure in 2010.[12] Analyses of the case underscore that small municipalities, with limited resources and low civic participation, are particularly vulnerable to such abuses, necessitating robust structural safeguards beyond reliance on individual ethics.[8] A primary lesson is the imperative for comprehensive transparency in compensation, budgets, and contracts, as Bell's officials concealed salary hikes via deceptive ordinances and non-public approvals, allowing council members to earn over $96,000 annually despite state caps intended for part-time roles.[124] Post-scandal reforms in Bell, including a dedicated transparency portal providing searchable access to financial data and meeting minutes, demonstrate how centralized, machine-readable public records can deter concealment, with recommendations for municipalities to standardize such disclosures statewide to enable automated outlier detection, such as flagging executive pay exceeding $300,000.[8][2] Strengthening internal oversight mechanisms emerges as another critical takeaway, given Bell's absence of effective checks like independent ethics reviews or fraud detection policies prior to the scandal.[124] Experts advocate for consolidated ethics codes encompassing all officials and contractors, appointment of an independent ombudsman for corruption investigations, and annual integrity audits, as implemented variably in Bell's recovery efforts, to enforce fiduciary duties and prevent self-enrichment.[8] In council-manager systems, where professional administrators wield significant discretion, term limits and compensation caps tailored to city size—absent in Bell—can mitigate power concentration.[12] Civic engagement and electoral integrity represent foundational accountability pillars undermined in Bell by voter apathy, with turnout below 5% in a 2005 election that rubber-stamped exemptions from compensation laws.[12] Lessons include bolstering absentee ballot monitoring through county-level oversight and fostering citizen review panels or whistleblower protections to counteract disengagement, which professionalized governance often exacerbates by distancing decisions from public view.[2][124] Ultimately, the scandal illustrates that while state-mandated data portals aid detection, local governments must cultivate active participation to sustain self-governance, as passive reliance on external journalism or audits proved insufficient in preempting the crisis.[12]
