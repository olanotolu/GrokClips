Howard (film)

Howard is a 2018 American documentary film written and directed by Don Hahn that chronicles the life and career of Howard Ashman, the lyricist and playwright whose songwriting revitalized Disney's animated musicals in the late 1980s and early 1990s.[1] Ashman co-wrote iconic songs such as "Part of Your World" from The Little Mermaid (1989), "Beauty and the Beast" from the 1991 film of the same name, and earlier hits from the stage musical Little Shop of Horrors (1982), often partnering with composer Alan Menken to blend sharp storytelling with memorable melodies.[2][3] As a gay man, Ashman concealed his AIDS diagnosis from Disney leadership while working on key projects, succumbing to AIDS-related complications on March 14, 1991, at age 40 in New York City, just months before Beauty and the Beast premiered.[2][3][4] The film premiered at the 2018 Tribeca Film Festival, drawing praise for its use of archival footage, interviews with collaborators like Menken, and unflinching depiction of Ashman's creative triumphs alongside his health decline, achieving a 93% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 46 reviews.[5][6] Hahn, a former Disney producer on Beauty and the Beast, highlights Ashman's role in Disney's renaissance through two Academy Awards for Best Original Songâ€”posthumously for "Beauty and the Beast"â€”and emphasizes how his death cut short further contributions that might have shaped additional films.[2][7]

Content and structure

Synopsis

The documentary Howard traces lyricist Howard Ashman's life from his childhood in Baltimore, Maryland, where he developed an early interest in theater through puppet shows and school productions, to his formative years studying medieval literature and music at Indiana University and Indiana University Jacobs School of Music in the early 1970s.[4] It details his move to New York City in 1974, initial collaborations with composer Alan Menken on projects like the 1979 musical God Bless You, Mr. Rosewater, and breakthrough with the 1982 off-Broadway hit Little Shop of Horrors, which ran for over 2,200 performances and established Ashman's style of character-driven lyrics infused with dark humor and pop influences.[8] Archival footage and interviews with Menken, family members, and colleagues illustrate Ashman's innovative approach to songwriting, emphasizing emotional depth and narrative integration akin to Broadway standards. The film shifts to Ashman's pivotal involvement in Disney's animation renaissance, beginning in 1986 with contributions to unproduced projects and culminating in his lyrics for The Little Mermaid (1989), where songs like "Part of Your World" advanced character development through Broadway-caliber musical numbers in animation.[9] It covers his ongoing partnership with Menken on Beauty and the Beast (1991), including the Oscar-winning title track, and partial work on Aladdin (1992) before his death, highlighting how Ashman's insistence on integrated storytelling elevated Disney's formula from generic tunes to psychologically resonant pieces.[2] Home videos and session recordings depict Ashman's hands-on directing of vocal performances to capture character arcs.[10] Interviews reveal Ashman's HIV/AIDS diagnosis in 1988, which he concealed from Disney executives amid stigma and career fears, yet he persisted in writing amid physical decline, relocating to Los Angeles for treatment while completing key contributions to Beauty and the Beast.[11] The narrative culminates in his death on March 14, 1991, at age 40 from AIDS-related complications, underscoring his output of over 20 Disney songs in his final years despite weighing under 80 pounds and losing vision in his later months.[12][2]

Production

Development and research

Don Hahn, a longtime Disney producer who collaborated with Howard Ashman on Beauty and the Beast, initiated the project to address Ashman's underrecognized influence on the Disney Renaissance, where his lyrics and story restructuring elevated films from potential failures to cultural and commercial triumphs. Development formally began in November 2015 after Hahn's lunch meeting with Ashman's sister, Sarah Gillespie, which facilitated essential permissions from the family and estate for accessing private materials, overcoming initial skepticism stemming from prior unsuccessful documentary efforts like Jonathan Polenz's halted project.[16] Research involved systematic archival dives starting at the Library of Congress, where Ashman's donated papers, photographs, unpublished recordings, and personal journals provided foundational insights into his creative process and health struggles, including entries revealing his private grappling with an HIV diagnosis received in January 1988â€”a fact he withheld from Disney colleagues amid the epidemic's stigma to sustain productivity on projects like The Little Mermaid and Beauty and the Beast. Balancing these sensitive disclosures against verifiable public records of his 1991 AIDS-related death, the team prioritized empirical evidence from production documents over anecdotal claims, while navigating access restrictions to intimate items like answering machine tapes to avoid sensationalism. Disney's cooperation, initiated through discussions with executive Bob Iger, yielded rare internal clips and logs without direct studio production involvement, enabling verification of Ashman's structural interventions that causally boosted narrative coherence and market performance.[16][17] Collaborations with Ashman's longtime composer partner Alan Menken were central to authenticating claims of his impact, cross-referencing personal accounts with financial data and session notes to substantiate how Ashman's Broadway-honed techniquesâ€”such as character-driven songsâ€”differentiated Disney's output, evidenced by pre- and post-intervention metrics in animation logs showing improved audience engagement and revenue trajectories for Renaissance-era releases. This rigorous approach ensured the documentary's assertions rested on primary sources rather than hagiography, highlighting Ashman's agency in averting flops through first-principles revisions to source materials.[16]

Filming and interviews

The principal interviews were recorded in 2017 and 2018 with key figures in Ashman's life, including his sister Sarah Ashman Gillespie, partner Bill Lauch, collaborator Alan Menken, and Disney executive Jeffrey Katzenberg, yielding audio testimonies that detailed his disciplined work habitsâ€”such as late-night revisionsâ€”and collaborative songwriting methods grounded in theatrical storytelling traditions.[16][18] Filming prioritized primary-source authenticity by incorporating archival video, photographs, and audio from the WPA Theaterâ€”site of Ashman's off-Broadway productions like Little Shop of Horrorsâ€”alongside materials from Disney's internal archives, including work tapes and personal correspondence held at the Library of Congress; these elements enabled a direct depiction of Ashman's same-sex relationship and AIDS progression through artifacts like letters and partner accounts, eschewing unverified narrative embellishments in favor of corroborated evidence.[16][19] Post-production editing maintained strict chronological causality by sequencing events via timestamped archives and interview timelines, avoiding reenactments or visual speculation; audio from interviewees was layered over static images and footage in a non-talking-head format akin to director Don Hahn's prior work, with the final 81-minute cut refined after feedback from the film's world premiere at the Tribeca Film Festival on April 22, 2018.[16][19][18]

Release

Premiere and distribution

The documentary premiered at the Tribeca Film Festival on April 22, 2018, marking its world debut with a screening followed by a question-and-answer session featuring director Don Hahn.[20] This festival appearance highlighted Howard Ashman's contributions to Disney's Renaissance-era musicals, drawing attention to his lyrics for films like The Little Mermaid and Beauty and the Beast.[21] Following the Tribeca premiere, the film entered a limited theatrical release in the United States on December 18, 2018, distributed independently amid a modest festival circuit that included screenings at events like the Heartland International Film Festival, where it won a documentary award.[22] The rollout emphasized Ashman's pivotal role in reviving Disney's animation legacy through Broadway-style songwriting, though commercial performance remained subdued due to the niche subject and restricted playdates.[21] Prior to Disney's acquisition of worldwide distribution rights in 2019, Howard saw no wide streaming availability, underscoring the film's initial independent status outside major studio control despite its focus on Disney-associated archival material.[23] Physical home media releases, including digital and disc formats, aligned with the late-2018 theatrical window to capitalize on festival buzz and Ashman's enduring influence on musical theater.[24]

Streaming availability and 2023 controversy

The documentary Howard premiered exclusively on Disney+ on August 7, 2020.[25] It has since been available only through that streaming service, with no reported physical media rereleases or alternative digital platforms hosting it as of October 2025.[15] In May 2023, Disney announced plans to remove Howard from Disney+ effective May 26, 2023, alongside dozens of other titles in a content purge aimed at reducing operational costs.[26] The decision prompted immediate backlash, particularly from LGBTQ+ advocates and Disney fans, who highlighted the film's portrayal of lyricist Howard Ashman's life as a gay man who died of AIDS-related complications in 1991, arguing that the removal erased key cultural history at a moment when Disney was promoting its live-action remake of The Little Mermaidâ€”a film scored with Ashman's lyricsâ€”set for theatrical release the same day.[26] [27] Disney reversed course within days, confirming that Howard would remain on the platform after reviewing the removal list amid the public outcry.[26] A spokesperson stated, "The list of titles coming off of Hulu and Disney+ next week is still being finalized and â€˜Howardâ€™ will remain on the service."[26] The company attributed such content decisions to financial considerations, including licensing and residual payments, rather than ideological suppression, as evidenced by the retention of other documentaries on similar themes.[28] The film has stayed available on Disney+ continuously since the reversal.[15]
