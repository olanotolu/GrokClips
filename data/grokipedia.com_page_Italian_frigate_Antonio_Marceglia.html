Italian frigate Antonio Marceglia

The Italian frigate Antonio Marceglia (F597) is the eighth unit of the Italian Navy's Carlo Bergamini-class frigates, developed under the Franco-Italian FREMM (European Multi-Mission Frigate) program, and the fourth configured for general-purpose operations.[1] Commissioned on 16 April 2019, the vessel measures 144 meters in length with a crew of approximately 190 personnel, incorporating stealth features for reduced radar cross-section and enhanced multi-role capabilities in anti-submarine, anti-air, and surface warfare domains.[2][3] Named after Lieutenant Colonel Antonio Marceglia, a World War II naval engineer awarded the Gold Medal for Military Valor for his role in the 1941 Raid on Alexandria that damaged British battleships Valiant and Queen Elizabeth, the frigate was launched by Fincantieri in 2018 and entered service based at Taranto.[4][5] Equipped with advanced systems including Aster 15 and 30 surface-to-air missiles, Exocet anti-ship missiles, and a 127 mm gun, Antonio Marceglia supports NATO and international maritime security through versatile deployment options.[6] Since commissioning, the frigate has participated in multinational exercises, such as Formidable Shield 2021 where it successfully launched an Aster 30 missile, and bilateral drills with the Indian Navy's INS Tabar in 2021.[2] In January 2025, Antonio Marceglia embarked on a six-month operational projection to the Indo-Pacific region, visiting ports in Japan, Indonesia, and other nations to strengthen defense ties and conduct freedom-of-navigation operations.[7][8] This deployment underscores Italy's commitment to extended naval presence beyond the Mediterranean.[9]

Program development

Emerging from French national efforts in the late 1990s to replace aging destroyers, the project transitioned to bilateral collaboration in the early 2000s to distribute development costs and integrate complementary technological strengths, with France focusing on sonar and propulsion expertise and Italy contributing shipbuilding scale via its extensive yards. This partnership between Naval Group (formerly DCNS) and Fincantieri emphasized empirical optimization for littoral and open-ocean operations, prioritizing fuel-efficient designs and weapon flexibility over specialized single-mission platforms to maximize fleet utility amid budget limitations.[10][11] On November 27, 2005, the Organisation Conjointe de CoopÃ©ration en matiÃ¨re d'Armement (OCCAR) formalized the program through a â‚¬3.4 billion contract for 18 frigatesâ€”eight for the French Navy in anti-submarine and multi-mission variants, and ten for the Italian Navy, including six initial units with options for four more. Italy's allocation reflected a strategic balance, with early emphasis on general-purpose frigates equipped for surface and air defense to support NATO task forces in the Mediterranean, complemented by anti-submarine models for convoy protection.[12][13] The collaboration's design rationale drew on post-Cold War naval realities, favoring versatile platforms interoperable with NATO allies' sensors and munitions to enable joint operations without proprietary lock-in, as demonstrated by integrated combat management systems compatible with Link 16 data links. Adaptation for Italian Bergamini-class

The FREMM design was adapted for Italian service as the Carlo Bergamini-class, designated for 10 frigates procured by the Marina Militare between 2005 and 2008, comprising six anti-submarine warfare (ASW) variants and four general-purpose (GP) variants to balance specialized underwater threat neutralization with broader multi-domain engagements.[14][15] The Antonio Marceglia (F597) constitutes the eighth unit overall and the final GP hull, delivered in 2021 to prioritize surface and air warfare primacy alongside secondary ASW roles.[7] Key structural modifications included extending the hull to 144.6 meters in length and achieving a full-load displacement of 6,700 tonnes, exceeding the baseline FREMM parameters to enhance fuel capacity, stability in variable sea states, and internal volume for expanded stores, crew endurance, and modular mission systems.[15] These changes supported Italy's operational doctrine, emphasizing platforms resilient to contested littorals in the Mediterranean while capable of sustained transoceanic transits for alliance commitments.[16] The GP variant's configuration reflects a doctrinal shift post-Cold War, favoring empirical versatility for integrated task group escorts, deterrent patrols, and expeditionary interventions over singular ASW optimization, as evidenced by Italy's allocation of resources to air defense and surface strike enablers amid evolving threats from non-state actors and regional powers.[17] This approach aligns with NATO's post-1991 emphasis on adaptable, forward-deployable assets for crisis management and collective defense in hybrid environments.[18]

Design and capabilities

Hull, propulsion, and performance

The hull of the Antonio Marceglia measures 144.6 meters in length overall, with a beam of 19.7 meters and a draft of 8.6 meters, resulting in a full-load displacement of approximately 6,700 tons.[19] These dimensions support multi-mission capabilities while incorporating design elements for reduced acoustic and magnetic signatures to enhance stealth in anti-submarine warfare roles.[20] The propulsion system utilizes a CODLAG (combined diesel-electric and gas) configuration, featuring one 32 MW GE/Avio LM2500+G4 gas turbine for high-speed operations, two 2.5 MW reversible electric motors for low-speed and silent running, and four diesel generators powering the electric components.[19][14] This setup enables efficient power management, with electric propulsion prioritized for stealthy anti-submarine missions at speeds up to 15.6 knots.[20] Performance specifications include a maximum speed exceeding 27 knots under gas turbine power and a range of 6,000 nautical miles at 15 knots, allowing extended deployments with endurance up to 45 days.[14][21] Sea trials for the Bergamini-class vessels, including Antonio Marceglia, validated maneuverability and fuel efficiency in varied sea states, confirming operational reliability for task group command roles.[14]

Armament and combat systems

The Antonio Marceglia (F597), as a general-purpose variant of the Bergamini-class frigate, features a modular vertical launching system (VLS) comprising two Sylver A50 modules with a total of 16 cells, loaded with MBDA Aster 15 or Aster 30 surface-to-air missiles for point and extended area air defense, respectively.[19][22] These missiles enable engagement of multiple aerial threats, including aircraft and anti-ship missiles, through active radar homing and vertical cold launch for rapid response.[19] Anti-surface capabilities are provided by eight MBDA Otomat/Teseo Mk2/A anti-ship and land-attack missiles, deployed from four quadruple canister launchers, offering a range exceeding 180 kilometers with inertial and active radar guidance for over-the-horizon strikes.[23][24] The primary surface gun is a single Leonardo 76 mm OTO Melara Super Rapid, mounted forward, which supports versatile fire missions including anti-surface, anti-air, and shore bombardment, with compatibility for guided Vulcano or DART ammunition to enhance precision against small, fast targets.[23][25] For close-in defense and anti-submarine warfare, the frigate incorporates two triple-tube launchers for MU-90 Impact lightweight torpedoes, capable of neutralizing submarines at depths up to 1,000 meters via acoustic homing and wire guidance.[20] Secondary armament includes two 25 mm or 30 mm remote weapon stations for point defense against small surface threats or drones.[26] Weapon integration is managed by Leonardo's CMS-410 combat management system, which facilitates networked fire control, data fusion from offboard sources, and simultaneous handling of air, surface, and subsurface threats through distributed processing and modular software architecture tested in Italian Navy exercises.[19] This setup supports interoperability with NATO allies via standardized Link 16 datalinks, prioritizing real-time threat prioritization over platform-centric operations.[19]

Sensors, electronics, and aviation facilities

The primary surveillance and fire-control radar is the Leonardo Kronos Grand Naval multi-functional active electronically scanned array (AESA), operating in the C-band with gallium arsenide technology for 360-degree azimuthal coverage of air and surface targets at detection ranges exceeding 400 km for aircraft.[16][27] This system, derived from the earlier EMPAR radar, supports simultaneous tracking of multiple threats and integrates with the ship's Aster missile defense for ballistic missile discrimination, as demonstrated in naval exercises.[28] Supporting radars include the Leonardo SIR-M5-PA identification friend-or-foe (IFF) interrogator and two NA-25X directors equipped with RTN-25X X-band radars alongside electro-optical/infrared sensors for precise target acquisition and weapon guidance.[20][25] The sonar suite for the general-purpose variant comprises a bow-mounted Thales UMS 4110 (BlueMaster) hull sonar for medium-frequency active and passive detection of submarines, lacking the towed array and variable-depth sonar of anti-submarine warfare-optimized sisters.[20][14] Electronic warfare systems feature the Elettronica Nettuno 4100 integrated suite for radar warning, jamming, and electronic support measures, augmented by two SCLAR-H (Oto Melara/Selex) 105 mm decoy launchers deploying chaff, flares, and infrared decoys to counter anti-ship missiles.[23][18] Aviation facilities include an enclosed double hangar and reinforced flight deck rated for two helicopters up to 10 tons each, typically NHIndustries NH90 for anti-submarine warfare with sonar and torpedoes or AgustaWestland AW101 for transport and utility missions in multinational operations.[14] These enable embarked rotary-wing assets to extend sensor reach for over-the-horizon targeting and vertical replenishment.

Construction and commissioning

Keel laying, launch, and outfitting

The construction of the Antonio Marceglia commenced at Fincantieri's integrated shipyards of Riva Trigoso and Muggiano in 2015, utilizing a modular approach where hull sections were prefabricated and assembled to enhance industrial efficiency in the FREMM program.[14] The vessel's naming commemorates Antonio Marceglia (28 July 1915 â€“ 13 July 1992), a captain in the Regia Marina's naval engineering corps who, during World War II, piloted a manned torpedo in the 1941 raid on Alexandria harbor alongside Petty Officer Spartaco Schergat, successfully attaching an explosive device to the British battleship HMS Queen Elizabeth despite subsequent capture by British forces; he was awarded the Gold Medal for Military Valor for this action, reflecting Italy's naval tradition of innovative special operations.[5] The frigate was launched on 3 February 2018 during a ceremony at the Riva Trigoso facility near Genoa, marking the transition from hull fabrication to subsequent fitting-out phases.[29] Following the launch, the incomplete hull was transferred to the adjacent Muggiano yard in La Spezia for outfitting, where superstructure erection and integration of pre-outfitted modular blocks for key systems occurred, leveraging coordinated supply chains from European partners to meet program timelines without reported delays.[21] This phase emphasized precision assembly to align with the multipurpose configuration, culminating in readiness for final systems checks prior to sea trials.[30]

Sea trials, testing, and delivery

Following its launch on 3 February 2018, the Antonio Marceglia commenced sea trials with its initial voyage in June 2018, marking the start of intensive testing to verify propulsion performance, system integration, and combat capabilities.[31] Over the ensuing five months, the frigate underwent thirteen scheduled trials coordinated by the Italian Navy and prime contractor Fincantieri, encompassing empirical evaluations of hull dynamics, machinery endurance, weapons firing sequences, and sensor-electronic interfaces under operational stresses.[31] These assessments confirmed the vessel's baseline seaworthiness and tactical readiness without reported major deviations from design parameters.[32] Combat system validation formed a core component, with integrated testing of fire control, radar tracking, and missile launch protocols completed by late November 2018, enabling full handover preparations.[32] All platform and equipment benchmarks were met during this phase, reflecting iterative refinements from prior Bergamini-class units to ensure reliability in multi-mission scenarios.[33] The trials concluded successfully on 27 November 2018, ahead of schedule relative to earlier siblings like Carlo Bergamini.[31] Final acceptance followed in spring 2019, culminating in official delivery to the Marina Militare on 16 April 2019 at Fincantieri's Muggiano facility in La Spezia, where the Italian Navy assumed custody after verifying contractual performance metrics.[21] This milestone positioned the Antonio Marceglia as the eighth FREMM unit transferred under the program, with post-delivery inspections focusing on sustainment logistics rather than further at-sea validation.[21]

Operational service

Commissioning and early deployments

The frigate Antonio Marceglia (F 597) was formally commissioned into service with the Italian Navy on 16 April 2019, immediately following its delivery from the Fincantieri consortium at the Muggiano shipyard in La Spezia.[21] This marked the transition from construction and builder-conducted sea trials to full operational control by naval personnel, with the vessel assigned to the Taranto homeport as part of the Bergamini-class fleet.[5] Initial crew integration focused on certification for anti-submarine warfare and general-purpose missions, including systems familiarization and baseline readiness assessments in the Mediterranean Sea. Early post-commissioning activities emphasized shakedown operations and routine patrols to validate performance under fleet command. In October 2019, the frigate conducted operations off Djibouti City in the Red Sea region, participating in multinational maritime security tasks shortly after entering service.[34] These efforts included crew training milestones for combat systems integration and interoperability protocols, ensuring seamless handoff from industrial sea trials to independent naval operations. By 2021â€“2022, Antonio Marceglia engaged in Mediterranean patrols and NATO exercises, including a deployment with Standing NATO Maritime Group 2 (SNMG2) to enhance collective defense readiness through joint maneuvers and surveillance missions.[35] In September 2021, it departed for the Gulf of Guinea to support anti-piracy and presence operations, further certifying its multi-role capabilities in extended patrols while maintaining focus on regional stability verification. These initial assignments confirmed the ship's operational baseline prior to more extended taskings.

Indo-Pacific mission and recent activities

In July 2023, ITS Antonio Marceglia transited Germany's Kiel Canal en route to the Baltic Sea, where it joined Standing NATO Maritime Group 1 for support operations until January 2024.[36][37] The frigate participated in multinational training exercises, including fast roping drills by embarked Marines, and conducted port visits to Riga, Latvia in October 2023; Tallinn, Estonia in November 2023 for allied cooperation drills; and KlaipÄ—da, Lithuania on November 23, 2023, to commemorate Lithuanian Armed Forces Day.[30][38][39] These activities focused on enhancing NATO interoperability in the region amid heightened tensions following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, with no reported combat incidents.[40] On January 20, 2025, ITS Antonio Marceglia departed La Spezia for a six-month operational projection campaign in the Indo-Pacific, traversing approximately 20,000 nautical miles, visiting 18 ports across 14 countries, and returning to base on July 9, 2025.[7][8][41] The mission prioritized naval diplomacy, freedom-of-navigation operations, and multinational engagements to affirm Italy's stake in a free and open Indo-Pacific, including encounters with North Korean vessels during transits.[42] Notable port calls included Yokosuka, Japan in March 2025 for bilateral activities; Jakarta, Indonesia for operational welcomes; and Singapore from May 4 to 9, 2025, coinciding with IMDEX Asia for institutional and promotional events.[43][44] The frigate joined exercises such as Komodo 2025 in Indonesia and collaborative patrols with the Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force in May 2025 to monitor regional stability.[17][45] On June 5, 2025, it conducted a bilateral naval exercise with the Indian Navy in the Indian Ocean as part of EUNAVFOR ATALANTA contributions.[46][47] The deployment yielded empirical gains in bilateral defense ties and economic linkages without kinetic engagements, underscoring the efficacy of forward presence for deterrence and cooperation in contested waters, though reliant on allied coordination rather than unilateral hard-power assertion.[9][48] No major disruptions or escalations occurred, aligning with broader NATO and EU objectives for maritime domain awareness.[49]
