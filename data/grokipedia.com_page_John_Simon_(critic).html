John Simon (critic)

John Ivan Simon (1925–2019) was a Yugoslav-born American critic whose trenchant assessments of theater, film, and literature prioritized aesthetic rigor and linguistic precision over accommodation of popular tastes or ideological concessions.[1] Immigrating to the United States in 1941 from Subotica amid rising threats to his Jewish family, Simon acquired English as his fifth language—following Serbo-Croatian, Hungarian, German, and French—and pursued higher education at Harvard University, where he earned a Ph.D. in comparative literature.[2] Simon's professional tenure included over five decades of criticism, most prominently a 37-year role as drama critic for New York magazine from 1968 until his 2005 dismissal, alongside contributions to outlets such as The New Leader, National Review, and The New Criterion, where he penned 76 essays between 1989 and 2020.[1][2] He produced roughly a dozen books, chiefly anthologies of his reviews, including the expansive three-volume collection of writings published by Applause Books and Paradigms Lost: Reflections on Literacy and Its Decline (1980), which critiqued the debasement of English usage and broader cultural standards.[1][2] Renowned for his erudition and wit, Simon positioned himself as a bulwark against what he saw as the erosion of high art through mass-media populism, identity-driven dilutions, and lax craftsmanship, often delivering reviews that savaged specific flaws in execution, such as improbable plots or subpar performances.[2] This approach garnered praise from proponents of traditional criticism for upholding objective benchmarks but provoked backlash, including charges of bigotry and sexism that contributed to his marginalization from mainstream venues by the mid-2000s, underscoring conflicts in an era increasingly averse to unfiltered aesthetic judgment.[2]

Early Life and Education

Birth and Immigration

John Ivan Simon was born on May 12, 1925, in Subotica, then part of the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes (present-day Serbia), to Joseph Simmon, a Hungarian textile merchant, and Margaret (née Reves) Simmon.[1][3] Of Hungarian descent, he demonstrated early linguistic aptitude, becoming fluent in Serbo-Croatian, German, and Hungarian by age five.[1] Simon grew up primarily in Belgrade, where his family resided amid rising regional tensions leading into World War II. In 1941, following the Axis invasion of Yugoslavia in April of that year, the family immigrated to the United States when Simon was 16; they entered on a tourist visa and settled in New York City, where his parents later naturalized as citizens.[1][4] Upon arrival, he anglicized his given name from Ivan to John and eventually his surname from Simmon to Simon.[1][5]

Academic Formation

Simon attended the Horace Mann School, a preparatory institution in New York City, prior to university.[1] He enrolled at Harvard University in 1942, initially focusing on literary studies, but his education was interrupted by service in the U.S. Army Air Forces from 1944 to 1945.[1][6] Resuming his studies postwar, Simon earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in English in 1946, followed by a Master of Arts in 1948.[1][7] During his undergraduate years, he composed plays, fiction, and poetry, reflecting an early engagement with creative writing alongside criticism.[1] Simon completed a Ph.D. in comparative literature at Harvard in 1959, after an extended period that included further academic preparation and possibly teaching stints.[8][9] His doctoral work emphasized cross-cultural literary analysis, aligning with his multilingual background—having learned English as a fifth language after Serbo-Croatian, German, French, and Hungarian—and foreshadowing his later critical breadth across theater, film, and literature.[2][10] Archival records from Harvard document his coursework notes and dissertation, underscoring a rigorous formation in literary scholarship.[6] This advanced training equipped him with a foundation in aesthetic judgment rooted in textual and dramatic traditions, distinct from contemporaneous trends favoring theoretical or ideological approaches.[7]

Professional Career

Initial Positions and Publications

Simon's entry into professional criticism occurred in the late 1950s, with contributions to Commonweal magazine beginning in 1958, where he reviewed drama until 1963.[6] His first dedicated role as a drama critic followed in 1960 at The Hudson Review, a quarterly publication focused on literature and arts, holding the position through 1980 and establishing his reputation for incisive theater analysis.[6] [1] In 1962, he briefly served as drama critic for Theatre Arts magazine, expanding his scope to include more formal theatrical commentary.[6] He returned to Commonweal for additional drama reviews in 1967 and 1968.[1] In 1963, Simon released Acid Test, his inaugural collection of essays and reviews on theater and film, published by Stein and Day with an introduction by Dwight Macdonald; the volume gathered his earlier pieces, showcasing his acerbic style and emphasis on aesthetic standards.[11] [12] This publication marked a consolidation of his nascent critical voice, drawing from freelance and periodical work in the preceding years while he balanced academia with writing.[6] Subsequent early collections, such as those anthologizing his Hudson Review contributions, reinforced his focus on high culture amid Broadway's commercial output.[1]

Tenure at New York Magazine

John Simon served as the theater critic for New York magazine from 1968 until 2005, a tenure spanning nearly four decades during which he established himself as one of the most influential and polarizing voices in American drama criticism.[13] His weekly reviews, often delivered with erudite precision and unsparing judgment, covered Broadway and Off-Broadway productions, emphasizing classical standards of dramaturgy, acting, and aesthetics over commercial appeal or innovation for its own sake.[2] Simon's position at the magazine, founded in 1968 by Clay Felker, aligned with its mission to provide incisive cultural commentary on New York life, allowing him to shape public discourse on theater amid the era's experimental movements and blockbuster musicals.[14] During this period, Simon's critiques frequently lambasted what he viewed as the decline of American theater into mediocrity, targeting directors like Joseph Papp for prioritizing accessibility over excellence and actors for physical or vocal shortcomings that he deemed incompatible with tragic roles.[1] For instance, in his 1982 review of Cats, he offered rare praise for its lavish production and fidelity to T.S. Eliot's verse, calling it opulent kitty-literature at its grandest, though he critiqued its lack of deeper substance.[15] His methodology drew on European traditions, insisting on verisimilitude in casting—arguing, for example, that performers should approximate the age, physique, and cultural background suited to Shakespearean or Chekhovian characters—principles he defended as essential to artistic integrity rather than discriminatory intent.[2] This approach garnered a loyal readership among those valuing rigorous standards but alienated producers, performers, and audiences who perceived his commentary as elitist or ad hominem.[13] Simon's influence extended beyond reviews; he contributed to broader cultural debates through essays in the magazine, critiquing the rise of director-centric theater and the dilution of language in modern plays.[16] Circulation data from the era indicate New York magazine's theater section, bolstered by Simon's byline, became a must-read for industry insiders, with his pan of a 1970s production like A Chorus Line highlighting tensions between populist success and aesthetic merit—praising its energy but decrying its sentimentality.[5] By the 1990s, as Broadway increasingly prioritized spectacle, Simon's consistent advocacy for literary depth positioned him as a contrarian voice, though his readership remained steady until shifts in editorial priorities.[17] In May 2005, editor Adam Moss informed Simon of his dismissal, citing a desire for "new blood" in the critic's chair, with Jeremy McCarter appointed as successor; Simon later stated he anticipated the move amid accumulating pressures from offended parties within the theater community.[18] His final review, of the revival Sweet Charity, appeared in the magazine that week, marking the end of an era that had defined New York's theater coverage through uncompromising scrutiny.[19] The departure reflected broader industry fatigue with Simon's style, though supporters argued it underscored a retreat from substantive criticism in favor of consensus.[2]

Expansion to Film and Literature

During his tenure at New York magazine from 1968 to 2005, Simon broadened his criticism to encompass film, reviewing Hollywood productions alongside theater with consistent disdain for perceived artistic superficiality and commercial pandering.[1] He had initiated film commentary earlier, contributing reviews to National Review in the 1960s and 1970s that favored European art films like The Lacemaker (1977) over mainstream American fare, viewing cinema through the same lens of classical standards he applied to stage work.[20] Collections such as Movies into Film (1971), compiling pieces from 1967–1970, and Reverse Angle (1982), analyzing American films from 1970–1980, showcased his acerbic dissections of directorial choices and performer inadequacies, often prioritizing narrative coherence and visual restraint over populist appeal.[21] Simon's literary criticism paralleled this expansion, appearing in outlets like The Hudson Review, The New Leader, and The New Criterion, where he evaluated contemporary novels, poetry, and nonfiction for fidelity to linguistic precision and intellectual depth.[1] In Paradigms Lost: Reflections on Literacy and Its Decline (1980), he critiqued the erosion of English usage in American writing, attributing it to slovenly habits among authors and editors that undermined expressive clarity.[1] His selected essays in The Sheep from the Goats (1988), comprising 49 mostly book reviews, applied similar rigor, lambasting works deficient in originality or craftsmanship while occasionally praising those upholding traditional virtues, such as formal experimentation grounded in substantive insight.[22] Across both domains, Simon's method rejected relativism, insisting on objective benchmarks derived from historical masterpieces, even as his unsparing tone drew rebukes for elitism.[23]

Final Years and Departure

In May 2005, after serving as theater critic for New York magazine for nearly 40 years, Simon was dismissed from his position.[19] He was replaced by Jeremy McCarter, then chief theater critic for The New York Sun.[24] The departure marked the end of Simon's long tenure at the publication, where his reviews had shaped discourse on Broadway and off-Broadway productions.[13] Following his exit from New York, Simon continued his career as a theater critic for Bloomberg News, contributing reviews from June 2005 until November 2010.[6] In his later professional years, he wrote theater commentary for The Westchester Guardian and other outlets outside Manhattan, maintaining his focus on dramatic arts amid a shifting media landscape.[25] Simon remained active in criticism until advanced age, with his final public appearance on the television program Theater Talk occurring in May 2018.[26] He died on November 24, 2019, at Westchester Medical Center in Valhalla, New York, at the age of 94; his death was confirmed by his wife, Patricia Simon.[1]

Critical Methodology

Core Principles of Judgment

Simon's evaluations of theater, film, and literature centered on aesthetic excellence, with beauty regarded as an indispensable foundation for any worthwhile work. He maintained that beauty permeated all production elements—encompassing sound, movement, performers' facial and bodily attributes, scenery, and costumes—and deemed its absence disqualifying, insisting, "Beauty is non-negotiable and foundational; without it, there is nothing." This principle extended to a broader imperative for art to pursue truth and human fulfillment, aligning with classical ideals where aesthetic merit intertwined with moral integrity and coherence. Simon assessed works not merely on technical execution but on their fidelity to these standards, measuring them against an internalized ideal informed by his philosophical beliefs and historical precedents.[10] Central to his methodology was the discernment of aesthetic and moral value, prioritizing how effectively a production realized its stylistic aims and conveyed authentic meaning over ephemeral trends or audience appeal. He advocated judging modest endeavors on their own terms if they succeeded authentically, yet applied rigorous scrutiny to pretentious failures, defending authors' intentions against directorial impositions that distorted core truths. Simon positioned critics as guardians of cultural standards, upholding beauty and veracity amid what he saw as pervasive artistic decline, with his reviews probing deeper into works only to affirm or reject their alignment with enduring principles of form, intelligence, and emotional resonance.[10][27] Honesty formed the ethical bedrock of Simon's approach, as he declared his "greatest obligation is to what, correctly or incorrectly, I perceive as the truth," rejecting compromise for the sake of approbation. This demanded unflinching standards and self-critique, where even evolving opinions—such as his reassessment of Stephen Sondheim's oeuvre—reflected a commitment to intellectual rigor over consistency for its own sake. By these lights, criticism transcended summarization to enforce accountability, ensuring art served elevating purposes rather than pandering to mediocrity or ideological agendas.[1][10]

Stylistic Approach

Simon's prose was characterized by erudition and precision, drawing on vast literary knowledge and multilingual fluency to craft reviews rich in allusions and arch rhetoric.[2][1] As a non-native English speaker who adopted the language late, he wielded it with meticulous care, often defending grammatical standards like the use of "whom" and treating words as "hard-earned gold."[2] His style featured vivid, playful phrasing—such as describing performers with "rutilant princess and a dainty redhead with a steamily rubescent epidermis"—blending analytical depth with poetic flair to elevate criticism beyond mere summary.[2] In approach, Simon rejected simplistic verdicts, favoring exploratory essays that probed aesthetic and moral dimensions of works, trusting unfiltered personal judgment over audience consensus or editorial pressures.[2] He viewed criticism as an art form demanding bold, uncompromised assertions to combat cultural insensitivity, often delivering vitriolic assessments that savaged mediocrity, as in deeming Star Wars "completely dehumanizing" or Cats a "delightful albeit trivial Gesamtalmost kunstwerk."[2] This haughty, hermetic method prioritized high standards, measuring theater, film, and literature against classical benchmarks, with disdain for superficiality evident in zingers like likening Cher's face to an "equine visage."[28] His writing's incisive wit and egotistical edge made reviews compelling reads, even when negative, serving as advertisements for his supercritic persona rather than neutral reportage.[29] Lines like "Like springs, adaptations can only go downhill" exemplified this acidulous economy, combining intelligence with memorable meanness to underscore perceived artistic failings.[29] Though controversial, this unbridled style aimed to raise discourse, insisting critics pierce pretense without pandering.[1]

Engagement with Broader Culture

Simon's criticism frequently addressed the erosion of aesthetic and intellectual standards in American culture, which he attributed to egalitarian impulses and a reluctance to enforce merit. He argued that the democratization of the arts, driven by populism and inverted snobbery, had led to a proliferation of substandard works that prioritized accessibility over excellence.[2] In essays and reviews, he positioned himself as a defender of high culture against what he termed cultural debasement, stemming from factors including Marxism, demagoguery, and moral cowardice, which he believed diluted artistic rigor.[2] This stance manifested in his insistence that criticism must resist indulging either creators or audiences, focusing instead on objective judgment to prevent further decline.[23] A key aspect of Simon's broader cultural engagement involved decrying the "dumbing down" of language and literacy, which he saw as symptomatic of societal intellectual decay. In his 1980 collection Paradigms Lost: Reflections on Literacy and Its Decline, he analyzed the degradation of English usage in media, education, and public discourse, linking it to a broader failure to uphold precision and tradition.[30] Simon contended that such linguistic sloppiness reflected and reinforced cultural insensitivity, warning in a 1978 interview that it colored the age and undermined critical faculties essential for artistic evaluation.[2] He extended this critique to theater and film, where he opposed productions that sacrificed form for ideological messaging, advocating instead for art's visual and structural integrity as a counter to politicized relativism.[2] Simon's views on aesthetics emphasized beauty, intelligence, and physical suitability in performers, positioning these as non-negotiable for authentic representation in the performing arts. He frequently lambasted casting practices that favored diversity quotas over talent, as seen in his reviews of Joseph Papp's productions, which he criticized for affirmative-action selections that compromised aesthetic coherence.[31] In a 2005 interview, Simon proudly identified as an elitist, rejecting egalitarian pressures that he believed lowered barriers to incompetence and eroded cultural excellence.[32] This extended to his broader condemnation of political correctness and pop culture's influence, which he argued inflicted lasting damage on artistic criticism by enforcing conformity over candid assessment.[33] Through such positions, Simon engaged cultural debates by championing meritocracy, even as his uncompromising tone drew accusations of insularity from more progressive outlets.[23]

Controversies and Public Backlash

Attacks on Performers and Aesthetics

Simon's reviews frequently targeted performers' physical appearances, arguing that aesthetic suitability was essential for roles in visual media like theater and film, where illusion and verisimilitude demand congruence between actor and character.[20] In a 1983 assessment of Glenda Jackson's screen work, he described her as possessing "everything about her, including her walk, is just a little too ugly for my taste," contending that such traits undermined her suitability for glamorous or refined parts.[34] Similarly, in critiquing Liza Minnelli's performance, Simon likened her to a dog, emphasizing mismatches between performers' looks and the aesthetic demands of characters requiring beauty or poise.[14] He explicitly stated in a Time Out New York interview that he avoided endorsing "ugly women in parts that call for glamour," framing this as fidelity to artistic standards rather than personal animus.[14] This emphasis extended to ethnic and physiognomic authenticity, with Simon insisting that casting should align actors' backgrounds and features with dramatic requirements to preserve realism. In a 1980s review of a production featuring Christopher Reeve, he derided Mandy Patinkin's appearance as resembling a "cartoon Jew" and Alfre Woodard's as evoking a gorilla, arguing such incongruities disrupted the play's intended ethnic and visual coherence.[35] During a 1973 critique of Shakespeare's The Winter's Tale, Simon lambasted the casting of performers he deemed too unattractive for roles demanding beauty, such as Perdita, suggesting their plainness shattered the production's aesthetic illusion and proposing alternatives like enhanced makeup or recasting to meet classical ideals.[35] These judgments reflected his broader aesthetic conservatism, prioritizing objective beauty and suitability over subjective talent alone, as he viewed theater as a holistic art form intolerant of visual discord.[13] Such commentary provoked widespread backlash, with detractors like Roger Ebert expressing "repugnance" for Simon's fixation on immutable traits, arguing actors "can't help how they look" and decrying it as gratuitous cruelty akin to mocking the critic's own unprepossessing features.[36] Performers and peers accused him of lookism and insensitivity, interpreting his standards as veiled prejudice, particularly when intersecting with ethnicity or gender, which fueled calls for his censure by theater institutions.[17] Simon countered that lax aesthetics in American criticism had eroded artistic rigor, insisting his bluntness served truth over flattery and that overlooking physical mismatches equated to dishonesty in evaluating performance art.[20] This stance, while polarizing, underscored his commitment to uncompromised judgment, even amid professional isolation from an industry favoring leniency.[23]

Ideological Clashes

Simon's critiques frequently positioned him against the encroaching influence of political correctness in the arts, which he viewed as a constraint on candid aesthetic judgment. In a 1970s Esquire essay, he interrogated "Genderspeak," rejecting neologisms like "poetess" while staunchly defending "actress" as a precise term, framing such linguistic shifts as impositions from the New Left that prioritized ideology over clarity and tradition.[2] He attributed broader cultural debasement to factors including populism, Marxism, and an overzealous social conscience, arguing these eroded objective standards in theater and film by substituting political messaging for artistic merit.[2] His resistance extended to identity-driven interpretations of performance, where he lamented that political correctness rendered impolitic blunt descriptors, such as labeling a production a "faggoty Jewish musical," thereby stifling critiques of works he deemed inferior on stylistic grounds.[29] This stance fueled clashes with progressive sensibilities, as Simon favored highbrow European traditions—praising Ingmar Bergman's films despite their pretensions—over popular American outputs influenced by Jewish or multicultural elements, which he dismissed as repetitious or lacking rigor, exemplified by his panning of Woody Allen's Annie Hall as "shapeless, sprawling."[29] Such preferences reflected a broader individualism in his methodology, opposing what he saw as collectivist groupthink in modern criticism that deferred to inclusivity norms over individual excellence.[28] Perceptions of sexism amplified these ideological tensions, with Simon's unsparing remarks on female performers—such as decrying Barbra Streisand's features as evoking pogroms or likening Liza Minnelli to a beagle—interpreted by detractors as symptomatic of resistance to feminist gains in representation.[29] Yet Simon maintained these were aesthetic necessities, not personal animus, insisting that physical suitability for roles warranted scrutiny to preserve verisimilitude, a principle rooted in traditions like Italian Neorealism rather than contemporary identity politics.[28] His critiques of directors like Federico Fellini (post-1953) or Quentin Tarantino similarly targeted departures from realism, which he linked to ego-driven ideologies supplanting craft.[28] These positions culminated in professional repercussions, including his 2005 dismissal from New York magazine by editor Adam Moss, who cited Simon's writing as "too controversial," "bigoted," and "sexist," reflecting a broader institutional intolerance for dissent against evolving cultural orthodoxies.[2] Simon's later affiliations, such as contributing to National Review, underscored his alignment with conservative outlets that valued uncompromised standards amid the culture wars, where he warned that yielding to populist or ideological pressures would proliferate mediocrity at the expense of enduring art.[23]

Defenses and Counterarguments

Supporters of Simon's critical approach argued that his stringent aesthetic judgments, including critiques of performers' physical appearances and non-traditional casting, stemmed from a commitment to artistic authenticity and realism rather than personal prejudice. For instance, Simon maintained that stage roles demanded a congruence between actor and character to preserve dramatic integrity, framing his opposition to race-blind or cross-gender casting as a "long-held aesthetic judgment" about representational purity.[37] Publishers like Edward Kosner of New York magazine defended this stance, asserting that Simon's "views are often unpopular, but that in no way disqualifies him from being a critic," emphasizing the critic's prerogative to challenge prevailing trends without deference to ideological pressures.[37] Counterarguments to charges of misogyny, racism, or homophobia highlighted Simon's consistency in prioritizing objective excellence over identity-based exemptions, with admirers portraying his barbs as witty exposures of cultural mediocrity rather than malice. In The New Criterion, his resistance to "populism, Marxism, [and] bad social conscience" was presented as a bulwark against the erosion of high standards, where provocations served to "pierce" audience complacency by demonstrating alternative viewpoints.[2] Andrew Cusack, in an appreciation for The Critic, rebutted blanket accusations by noting Simon's personal decency—such as his courteous interactions with junior staff—and his role in honing readers' discernment through incisive prose, as in pithy dismissals like "It just doesn’t" for flawed productions.[38] Defenses of Simon's overall caustic style underscored its effectiveness in combating theatrical decline, with critics like Bill Marx praising his "deadly eye for the meretricious" and ability to proclaim "that the emperor was naked as a jaybird with as much savvy panache."[39] Advocates contended that his vitriol was not gratuitous but a necessary tool for upholding linguistic precision, structural rigor, and classical ideals in an industry prone to self-congratulation, arguing that softer critiques would fail to elevate discourse or protect art from dilution.[2] This perspective positioned Simon as the "apotheosis of criticism," whose unyielding standards, though polarizing, preserved a tradition of discerning judgment amid broadening permissiveness.[38]

Legacy and Reception

Influence on Criticism

Simon's extended tenure as theater critic for New York magazine from 1968 until his dismissal in 2005 positioned him as a pivotal figure whose reviews commanded significant attention in the New York arts scene, fostering a model of authoritative criticism that emphasized erudition and unyielding judgment over accommodation to commercial or audience preferences.[2] His approach, characterized by precise wit and a focus on aesthetic integrity, contributed to the magazine's prominence in theater discourse during that period, as subsequent coverage there reportedly declined in influence following his exit.[2] Through essays and reviews collected in volumes such as Uneasy Stages (1975) and later contributions to publications like The New Criterion—where he published 76 pieces between 1989 and 2020—Simon advocated for critics to "trust themselves: to review in the way that they really feel or really think," resisting editorial or cultural pressures toward conformity.[2] This stance reinforced a tradition of independent, standards-driven evaluation, particularly appealing to reviewers wary of what he termed the "dumbing down" of art amid rising populism and identity-focused interpretations in the late 20th century.[40] His critiques, often targeting perceived declines in language and form, exemplified a defense of classical European highbrow tastes, providing a template for subsequent arts writers prioritizing formal rigor over accessibility.[29] While Simon's vitriolic persona solidified the archetype of the "critic as hater"—a haughty guardian battling cultural erosion, akin to contemporaries like Pauline Kael—his legacy elicited mixed emulation, with some adopting his combative elitism and others reacting against its perceived excess, contributing to broader debates on criticism's role as cultural arbiter rather than endorser.[20] In an era when critics wielded power to shape productions, his uncompromised assessments underscored the value of substantive analysis, influencing perceptions of theater reviewing as a bulwark against mediocrity even as institutional shifts toward milder tones diminished such figures' dominance by the 2010s.[27]

Evaluations of Contributions

Simon's critical oeuvre, encompassing thousands of reviews across theater, film, literature, and the arts from the 1960s to the 2000s, has been lauded for its erudition and insistence on classical standards amid cultural democratization. His deep knowledge—bolstered by fluency in multiple languages and a Harvard Ph.D. in comparative literature—enabled analyses rooted in European traditions, as in his advocacy for "truth and beauty" as benchmarks for human fulfillment in works like Acid Test (1963) and Private Screenings (1967).[39][2] Admirers, including fellow critics at conservative outlets, credit him with defending high art against mediocrity, arguing his negativity stemmed from a principled refusal to indulge subpar work, as evidenced by recommending just 15 of 245 films in Reverse Angle (1972).[23] This approach, they contend, benefited readers by modeling rigorous thinking over populist approval, with his prose—playful, precise, and often epigrammatic—elevating criticism to literature itself.[2][23] Conversely, evaluations from broader arts commentary highlight flaws in his methodology, particularly an overreliance on ad hominem judgments that prioritized aesthetics like performers' physicality over dramatic merit, fostering perceptions of cruelty rather than insight.[1] His anthologies, such as Uneasy Stages (1975) and John Simon on Film (1991), while comprehensive, are faulted for excessive dismissals—panning cultural staples like The Godfather or Diane Keaton's performances—which some attribute to personal biases, including apparent antipathies toward certain ethnic or sexual demographics, as in remarks on Barbra Streisand or AIDS victims.[29][23] These traits, critics argue, diminished his influence by alienating audiences and peers, contributing to a vitriolic style that outlasted its analytical utility in later decades.[29][20] In aggregate, Simon's legacy divides along lines of taste: proponents view him as a bulwark against relativism, enriching discourse through provocative independence, while opponents see a net negative from his combative tone, which prioritized shock over balanced appraisal.[39][2] His 37-year tenure at New York magazine (1968–2005) and contributions to The New Criterion underscore a contrarian thread in American criticism, inspiring successors like Armond White, though mainstream assessments often prioritize his polarizing persona over substantive impact.[23][1]

Posthumous Assessments

Following Simon's death on November 24, 2019, at age 94 from complications of a stroke, assessments of his critical oeuvre emphasized his erudition and unflinching standards amid a cultural landscape increasingly tolerant of mediocrity, though mainstream tributes often highlighted his vitriol over his principled rigor.[1] Admirers, particularly from outlets skeptical of prevailing artistic leniency, lauded his 50-year career—spanning theater, film, literature, and art—for provoking readers to interrogate their tastes rather than passively affirm trends.[23] Jason L. Riley, in a Wall Street Journal-affiliated piece, recalled Simon as "far and away my favorite" critic for pursuing "their own reactions down to the rock bottom of their subjectivity and expressing them with utmost artistry," arguing that charges of excessive harshness inverted reality, as contemporaries erred toward generosity.[23] Simon's insistence that "indifference to bad art" would "give rise to further bad art and drive out the good" resonated in these defenses, positioning him as a bulwark against declining standards in post-1960s American culture.[23] Conservative-leaning publications reinforced this view, portraying Simon as the "last of a generation" of independent critics unafraid to defy political correctness and institutional pieties. The New Criterion, where he contributed 76 pieces over 31 years, eulogized his prose for handling "American words and phrases like hard-earned gold in his pocket," crediting his wit and precision for doubling the impact of reviews and honoring him through "each venue lost" to controversy.[2] His final, unfinished review of Nabokov underscored a lifelong dedication to elevating discourse, even as his acerbic dismissals—such as the 1973 incident where actress Sylvia Miles dumped steak tartare on him after a scathing notice—cemented his outsider status.[2] Critics from broader media, however, framed his legacy through the lens of antagonism, often amplifying anecdotes of personal invective over analytical depth, a tendency reflective of institutional preferences for affirmative rather than judgmental criticism. Variety's Peter Debruge contended Simon "defined the image of the critic as hater," citing barbs like his description of Barbra Streisand's nose "cleav[ing] the giant screen from east to west" and a reported quip wishing AIDS on theater's gay contingent, which underscored perceptions of elitism and bigotry.[20] The New York Times obituary echoed this, labeling him "vitriolic and vilified" for savaging works with "acid wit" rooted in a view of arts in inexorable decline, though it acknowledged his self-professed duty: "My greatest obligation is to what, correctly or incorrectly, I perceive as the truth."[1] [41] These polarized retrospectives, including in the Los Angeles Times on the deaths of Simon and Clive James, debated his role in a shifting ecosystem where expertise yields to accessibility, suggesting his passing signaled the obsolescence of authoritative, standards-driven criticism.[27] Despite divisions, his 12 books and decades at New York magazine (1968–2005) left an indelible mark, with proponents arguing his method—demanding excellence without compromise—remains vital against subjective relativism in evaluation.[1]

Awards and Honors

Simon received the George Polk Award for Film Criticism in 1968 for his work as a film reviewer.[14][42] In 1970, he was awarded the George Jean Nathan Award for Dramatic Criticism for reviews published in The Hudson Review and New York magazine during the 1969–1970 season.[43]

Major Works

Simon's major published works consist primarily of essay collections and compilations of his criticism on theater, film, and the performing arts, reflecting his decades-long tenure reviewing for outlets like New York magazine.[2] Among his earlier books, Ingmar Bergman Directs (1974) analyzed the Swedish filmmaker's techniques and oeuvre, drawing on Simon's expertise in cinema.[1] Uneasy Stages: A Chronicle of the New York Theater, 1963-1973 (1975, Random House), a 489-page volume, gathered his contemporaneous reviews of Broadway and off-Broadway productions, offering a panoramic critique of the era's dramatic output.[44] Similarly, Singularities: Essays on the Theater, 1964-1973 (1975, Random House) assembled additional theater essays, emphasizing analytical depth over chronological narrative.[45] In later years, Applause Books issued expansive anthologies of his periodical writings, including John Simon on Theater: Criticism 1974-2003 (2005), spanning over 800 pages of reviews from New York, The Hudson Review, and National Review; John Simon on Film: Criticism 1982-2001 (2005); and John Simon on Music (2005), the latter covering opera and classical performance critiques.[46] These volumes, part of a three-book set totaling more than 2,000 pages, encapsulate his signature precision and polemical style across disciplines.[2] Other notable titles include Reverse Angle: A Decade of American Films (1972), focusing on 1960s Hollywood output.[47] Overall, Simon authored around a dozen such books, prioritizing substantive evaluation over popular appeal.[2]
