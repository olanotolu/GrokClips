Bare (film)

Bare is a 2015 American drama film written and directed by Natalia Leite, centering on a young woman's evolving relationship with a drifter in rural Nevada.[1] The story follows Sarah (Dianna Agron), a sheltered high school graduate feeling trapped in her small-town life, who befriends the enigmatic Pepper (Paz de la Huerta), a nomadic stripper and drug user whose influence draws Sarah into experiences involving exotic dancing, substance use, and sexual awakening.[1] Produced independently with a focus on raw emotional realism, the film explores themes of identity, desire, and escape without resorting to overt moralizing.[2] Featuring supporting performances from Chris Zylka as Sarah's boyfriend and Louisa Krause as a fellow dancer, Bare premiered at film festivals in 2015 before a limited theatrical release.[1] Critics offered mixed assessments, with some commending Leite's sensitive direction and the hazy intimacy of the central romance, while others noted narrative meandering and underdeveloped subplots.[3][4] On aggregate, it holds a 56% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on nine reviews and a Metacritic score of 52 out of 100 from four critics, reflecting its niche appeal in independent cinema rather than broad commercial success.[3][5] The film's portrayal of same-sex attraction and fringe lifestyles drew attention for Agron's departure from her Glee persona, though it generated no major awards or box-office milestones.[6]

Synopsis

Plot

Sarah, a young woman in her early twenties, lives with her parents in a rural Nevada town and works as a cashier at a local store, feeling increasingly disillusioned with her stagnant existence.[1] She forms a connection with Pepper, a charismatic drifter and drug dealer who frequents the area, leading to an evolving friendship marked by shared conversations and mutual attraction.[3][7] As their bond deepens into a romantic and sexual relationship, Pepper introduces Sarah to a subversive lifestyle, including employment as a stripper at a nearby club and the use of hallucinogenic drugs that induce metaphysical visions and altered states of consciousness.[1][8] This immersion challenges Sarah's prior conformity, sparking a period of self-examination amid the risks of her new environment, including interpersonal conflicts and the precariousness of Pepper's nomadic ways.[9][4]

Cast and characters

The principal cast of Bare includes Dianna Agron as Sarah Barton, a sheltered young woman living in a rural Nevada town who embarks on a path of self-exploration after meeting a drifter.[1][10] Paz de la Huerta portrays Pepper, a free-spirited outsider involved in drug dealing and stripping who draws Sarah into an intense romantic and lifestyle entanglement.[11][3] Chris Zylka plays Haden, Sarah's initial male romantic interest and a local figure representing conventional expectations.[1][12] Louisa Krause appears as Lucille Jacobs, a supporting character tied to the community's social dynamics.[13] Mary Price Moore depicts Linda Barton, Sarah's mother, who embodies familial pressures and concern.[11][14]

Production

Development

Natalia Leite wrote the screenplay for Bare in her early twenties, drawing inspiration from personal experiences of self-discovery, including a relationship with a woman and the challenges of transitioning into adulthood, though the story is not strictly autobiographical.[15][16] She initially set the script aside to pursue full-time work assisting producers and directors while taking side jobs such as shooting weddings and babysitting.[7] Leite revived the project following a breakup and job loss, relocating temporarily to New Mexico where location scouting led her to a strip club that influenced key elements of the narrative and setting.[7][17] She collaborated with producer Dahlia Heyman on revisions and later involved producer Derrick Tseng, though early attachments delayed progress as both pursued other commitments.[16] Financing proved challenging for the low-budget independent production, with Leite and co-producer Alexandra Roxo ultimately securing private investors after multiple false starts and shifting to self-financing; funds were supplemented by connections from Leite's prior web series Be Here Now-ish and a Kickstarter campaign.[16][15][7] Key producers included Roxo, Leite herself, and Chad Burris, with mentorship from figures like Rose Troche aiding pre-production decisions such as forgoing a casting director to cast principal roles through agent relationships.[16] Production was announced for New Mexico in July 2014.[18]

Filming

Principal photography for Bare took place primarily in Albuquerque and Moriarty, New Mexico.[19][18] Shooting began at the end of July 2014 and wrapped by mid-August 2014.[18] The production, a low-budget independent feature, employed at least 35 local crew members from New Mexico and 15 local actors to support the shoot.[18] These locations were selected to depict the small-town Nevada setting of the story, leveraging New Mexico's diverse rural and urban landscapes for authenticity on a constrained schedule.[18][20]

Post-production

Editing for Bare was completed by Joe Murphy.[11] The film incorporates original music composed by Kyp Malone of TV on the Radio, alongside a licensed soundtrack featuring tracks such as "Kill For Love" by Chromatics, "Velveteen" by Still Corners, and "The Line" by Mood Rings, which underscore its themes of desire and disorientation.[21][22] Post-production followed principal photography, which wrapped on August 23, 2014, enabling a premiere at the Tribeca Film Festival on April 19, 2015. No significant visual effects work was reported, consistent with the film's low-budget indie aesthetic emphasizing naturalistic cinematography by Tobias Datum.[11] Sound design details remain undocumented in available production records, though the audio mix supports the intimate, gritty tone of interpersonal scenes.[23]

Themes and analysis

Portrayal of sexuality and relationships

The film portrays Sarah's initial relationship with her boyfriend Josh as stagnant and emotionally unfulfilling, characterized by routine intimacy and a lack of genuine passion, reflecting her broader dissatisfaction with small-town conformity.[24] This dynamic shifts upon her encounter with Pepper, a free-spirited drifter whose nomadic lifestyle and overt sensuality draw Sarah into a burgeoning same-sex attraction, evolving from tentative friendship to physical consummation in a single, understated sex scene emphasizing mutual vulnerability over explicit eroticism.[4][25] Sexuality in Bare is depicted as fluid and exploratory, with Sarah's first experience of same-sex desire framed as a catalyst for personal awakening amid existential malaise, intertwined with Pepper's introduction to stripping as an act of bodily autonomy and thrill-seeking.[4][7] The relationship between the two women is presented as a complex interplay of dependency and inspiration, where Pepper's hedonistic influenceâ€”encompassing casual nudity, drug use, and non-monogamous encountersâ€”both liberates Sarah from her prior inhibitions and exposes her to relational instability, culminating in emotional turbulence rather than resolution.[23][26] Critics have observed that while the film sensitively captures the quiet intensity of sapphic bonding and quarter-life sexual transitions, it romanticizes risky behaviors as pathways to self-discovery, with some questioning whether the linkage of lesbian intimacy to sex work and substance use undermines claims of empowerment.[27][9] Director Natalia Leite has described the narrative as intentionally ambiguous, avoiding didactic judgments on these elements to mirror the ambiguities of real human connections.[7]

Release

Premiere

Bare had its world premiere at the Tribeca Film Festival on April 19, 2015, in New York City.[33] The event marked the debut screening of the film directed by Natalia Leite, featuring lead actress Dianna Agron in attendance.[34] As part of the festival's programming, the premiere drew attention for its exploration of themes involving a young woman's entry into stripping and complex relationships in rural Nevada.[23] Subsequent festival screenings followed, including at Frameline on June 28, 2015, but Tribeca served as the initial public unveiling.[33]

Distribution and box office

Bare was acquired by IFC Films following its premiere at the Tribeca Film Festival, securing domestic distribution rights for theatrical, video on demand (VOD), and home video release.[16] The film received a limited theatrical rollout in select U.S. markets on October 30, 2015, coinciding with its availability across major VOD and digital platforms including iTunes and Amazon.[35] This simultaneous release strategy targeted niche audiences rather than broad commercial appeal, typical for independent dramas.[33] Subsequent home media distribution included a DVD release on December 12, 2016, through independent production channels.[36] Streaming availability expanded to platforms like those aggregated by Rotten Tomatoes on March 29, 2017.[3] No international theatrical distribution details are prominently documented, with focus remaining on U.S.-centric digital and limited cinema channels.[1] Due to its restricted theatrical engagement in a handful of venues, Bare's box office performance generated negligible reported gross, untracked by major aggregators like Box Office Mojo, underscoring its reliance on ancillary markets for revenue.[35] The film's modest commercial footprint aligned with its independent production budget and arthouse positioning, prioritizing critical and festival exposure over widespread exhibition.[37]

Reception

Critical response

Bare received mixed reviews from critics, with a 56% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on nine reviews, indicating a generally unfavorable response.[3] On Metacritic, the film holds a score of 52 out of 100 from four critics, reflecting divided opinions.[5] Critics frequently praised lead actress Dianna Agron's performance for bringing nuance to her character, Sarah, a sheltered young woman navigating personal turmoil, and director Natalia Leite's handling of sensitive themes, which elevated familiar indie drama tropes into something occasionally fresh.[3] Slant Magazine highlighted Leite's "emotional intelligence and sensitivity," arguing it distinguished the film from its "smutty late-night cable premise."[5] The Los Angeles Times commended the film's exploration of female relationships amid rural stagnation, awarding it a 70 out of 100.[5] However, detractors criticized the narrative as derivative and underdeveloped, with The New York Times describing it as "flimsy and unsatisfying," scoring it 40 out of 100 and questioning the portrayal of stripping as empowering self-discovery rather than exploitative.[9][5] The Hollywood Reporter noted its gritty depiction of a small-town woman's entanglement with a drifter but found the story lacking depth beyond surface-level drama.[23] Village Voice echoed these sentiments, rating it 40 out of 100 for failing to transcend predictable indie character studies.[5] Overall, while technical aspects like cinematography in Nevada's expansive landscapes drew some admiration, the consensus faulted Bare for insufficient originality and emotional payoff.[38]

Audience and commercial performance

The film elicited a lukewarm to negative response from audiences, reflected in aggregate user ratings across major platforms. On Rotten Tomatoes, Bare garnered a 47% audience score from over 100 ratings, indicating general dissatisfaction among viewers.[3] IMDb users similarly rated it 5.1 out of 10 based on approximately 2,700 votes, with feedback often critiquing its pacing, character development, and narrative predictability.[1] Commercially, Bare received a limited theatrical rollout on October 30, 2015, consistent with its independent production and festival origins.[3] North American distribution rights were acquired by TLA Releasing, a specialist in niche LGBTQ+ titles, emphasizing video-on-demand and home media over wide cinema exposure.[39] No public box office figures are available, underscoring its modest theatrical footprint; revenue streams primarily derived from streaming and VOD availability on platforms including Netflix, AMC+, Prime Video, and Tubi.[40][41][42] This distribution model sustained visibility in targeted markets but did not achieve broader mainstream commercial success.

Retrospective views and cultural commentary

In a 2020 analysis, "Bare" has been situated as a modern successor to Barbara Loden's 1970 film "Wanda," both chronicling rural women's drift from stifling domesticity into stripping and transient relationships amid cycles of poverty and emotional void. Unlike "Wanda," which culminates in unresolved passivity and patriarchal subjugation, "Bare" infuses queer sexualityâ€”through protagonist Sarah's romance with the drifter Pepperâ€”as a vector for tentative agency and self-actualization, with stripping framed as a rebellious pivot toward autonomy rather than mere entrapment. This parallelism casts "Bare" as a fourth-wave feminist rendering, evolving proto-feminist inertia into narratives of sexual exploration and departure from stagnation, though both films underscore persistent female marginalization in isolated American locales.[31] Cultural commentary highlights the film's distributional fate as emblematic of broader industry neglect toward female-directed works centered on queer female experiences: initially slated for limited theatrical runs post-Tribeca premiere in April 2015, it pivoted to video-on-demand, akin to "Wanda"'s single U.S. screening in 1971, signaling systemic undervaluation of such stories despite critical interest in their raw depictions of existential drift and bodily commodification. The essay by filmmaker Matt McKinzie critiques this as mis-marketing "Bare" as niche arthouse fare, thereby curtailing visibility for explorations of non-normative desire and economic desperation in working-class settings. While the film's non-judgmental lens on stripping and drug involvement invites scrutiny for potentially glossing over exploitationâ€”echoing contemporary reviews skeptical of its empowerment arcâ€”retrospective takes value its restraint in avoiding didacticism, prioritizing atmospheric immersion in quarter-life malaise over resolution.[31][9]
