Let's Go Brandon (song)

"Let's Go Brandon" is a hip-hop protest song written and performed by American rapper Loza Alexander, released in October 2021, which samples audio from an NBC Sports interview following a NASCAR race where spectators' chants of "Fuck Joe Biden" were inaccurately reported by the broadcaster as encouragement for race winner Brandon Brown.[1][2] The track explicitly conveys political opposition to President Joe Biden through lyrics that decode the phrase's profane intent, including direct references to the underlying expletive.[3] The song rapidly gained traction amid widespread adoption of the "Let's Go Brandon" phrase as a coded expression of dissatisfaction with Biden's leadership, topping the iTunes hip-hop/rap chart and reaching number one on Billboard's Emerging Artists chart shortly after release.[2][4] It debuted on the Billboard Hot 100, reflecting its viral spread via social media and conservative outlets, though competing versions by other artists like Bryson Gray also charted amid platform restrictions such as YouTube bans for policy violations.[5][6] As a defining anthem of populist discontent, the song highlighted tensions over media credibility, with the originating incident exemplifying perceived journalistic spin that fueled its meme status and use in political rallies, sports events, and merchandise.[7] Controversies arose from institutional responses, including school districts banning apparel bearing the phrase on First Amendment grounds leading to lawsuits, and broader censorship efforts by tech platforms wary of its subversive connotation.[8][9] Despite such pushback, its commercial and cultural impact underscored a grassroots rejection of establishment narratives, positioning it as a modern protest staple akin to historical dissent anthems.[10]

Origins of the Phrase

The NASCAR Event and Initial Chant

On October 2, 2021, during the Sparks 300 NASCAR Xfinity Series race at Talladega Superspeedway in Alabama, driver Brandon Brown secured his first career victory after the event was shortened by darkness following a late caution period.[11][12] The win came amid a chaotic finish involving a multi-car incident, positioning Brown as the leader when officials called the race due to insufficient lighting.[13] In the immediate post-race interview conducted by NBC Sports reporter Kelli Stavast, the surrounding crowd audibly chanted "Fuck Joe Biden" repeatedly, a vulgar expression of dissent directed at President Joe Biden.[14][15] Stavast, however, interpreted and broadcast the chant on live television as "Let's go, Brandon," framing it as apparent support for the victorious driver whose name matched the phrasing.[16][7] Audio and video recordings of the broadcast confirm the crowd's explicit phrasing drowned out much of the interview, with the euphemistic substitution providing conservatives a coded, deniable alternative for voicing similar sentiments in subsequent public settings.[17][15] The chant reflected broader organic public frustration with the Biden administration's early policies, particularly the August 2021 withdrawal from Afghanistan, which culminated in a suicide bombing at Kabul's airport on August 26 killing 13 U.S. service members and leaving thousands of Afghan allies behind amid chaotic evacuations.[14] Compounding this were emerging economic pressures, including supply chain disruptions and inflation that had begun accelerating from mid-2021, reaching consumer price index increases of over 5% year-over-year by October and foreshadowing peaks near 9% in 2022.[18] Such events fueled anti-Biden expressions at sporting venues, with similar chants emerging at college football games in September 2021 prior to the Talladega incident.[19]

Media Misreporting and Meme Emergence

During an October 2, 2021, NASCAR race at Talladega Superspeedway in Alabama, following driver Brandon Brown's victory in the Sparks 300, a post-race interview conducted by NBC Sports reporter Kelli Stavast captured the crowd chanting "Fuck Joe Biden" in the background. Stavast, however, reported on air that the spectators were cheering "Let's go, Brandon" in support of the driver, misrepresenting the explicit anti-Biden sentiment as innocuous encouragement.[1][20] This discrepancy, evident from unedited audio and video footage, exemplified an attempt by mainstream media outletsâ€”often critiqued for left-leaning bias in downplaying dissentâ€”to sanitize public frustration with the Biden administration.[21] The clip spread rapidly on social media starting October 3, 2021, with users on Twitter (now X) and YouTube highlighting the misreport, amassing millions of views within days as viewers contrasted the broadcast with raw recordings.[22] This viral exposure transformed the phrase "Let's Go Brandon" into a coded euphemism for the original profane chant, inadvertently amplifying grassroots opposition rather than containing it, as the media's spin fueled mockery and wider adoption among those skeptical of institutional narratives.[20] By mid-October 2021, conservative influencers and Republican politicians embraced the phrase as a subtle critique of President Biden, whose Gallup approval rating had declined to 42% amid economic concerns and policy backlash.[23][24] Figures such as Florida Representative Bill Posey invoked it on the House floor on October 21, 2021, signaling its evolution into a partisan symbol of rejection.[1] Concurrently, "Let's Go Brandon" appeared organically on merchandise like t-shirts and hats, and chants echoed at subsequent sports events, including college football games, demonstrating bottom-up cultural permeation independent of elite endorsement.[25][26] This organic spread underscored public discontent, as evidenced by the phrase's persistence despite NASCAR's later disavowal of political associations.[27]

Song Creation and Release

Loza Alexander's Background and Motivation

Loza Alexander, born on March 10, 1988, in Panorama City, California, is an independent rapper, singer, songwriter, and producer who relocated to Las Vegas, Nevada. Before gaining widespread attention, he self-released music through digital platforms, including the album Lunar Music in early 2021 and earlier tracks focusing on hip-hop and personal themes.[28][29][30] Alexander underwent a political transformation from liberal to conservative viewpoints during the 2020 U.S. presidential election, citing disillusionment with progressive policies and their impacts on individual freedoms. His decision to musicalize the "Let's Go Brandon" phrase stemmed from personal and observed frustrations with inflation, supply chain disruptions, and cultural mandates under the Biden administration, which he viewed as exacerbating hardships for working-class individuals. In a local interview, he expressed intent to uplift listeners "feeling down due to economic pressures" by channeling the meme's anti-establishment sentiment into hip-hop.[31][4] On October 21, 2021, Alexander released the single as the earliest prominent musical rendition of the phrase, deliberately crafting it to echo grievances of overlooked Americans against bureaucratic overreach and elite detachment from everyday realities. Embracing his conservative leanings, he distributed the track independently via platforms like iTunes and YouTube to evade traditional industry filters dominated by left-leaning gatekeepers, thereby amplifying a direct, unmediated voice for populist discontent.[4][31]

Lyrics and Musical Style

The lyrics of "Let's Go Brandon" revolve around the titular chant as a coded euphemism for "Fuck Joe Biden," a phrase originating from public discontent, while directing pointed criticism at observable policy outcomes under the Biden administration. Key verses decry inflation-driven surges in everyday costs, including gas prices that the song claims have doubled, consistent with U.S. Energy Information Administration records showing regular unleaded averaging $2.42 per gallon in January 2021 and rising to $3.35 by October. Grocery affordability is similarly invoked amid broader price hikes, supported by Bureau of Labor Statistics data indicating the Consumer Price Index for food at home increased 3.5% year-over-year through October 2021, exacerbating household strains from supply disruptions and fiscal stimulus effects.[32] Further lines target border security lapses, with references to unchecked entries mirroring U.S. Customs and Border Protection's fiscal year 2021 tally of 1.73 million southwest border encounters, and vaccine mandates linked to workforce absences amid labor participation rates dipping below 62%.[33] This content eschews gratuitous vulgarity in favor of causal attributions tying presidential decisions to empirical hardships, such as energy policy shifts contributing to fuel volatility and regulatory pressures on employment, rather than generalized animosity; the song's framing underscores how these factorsâ€”verifiable via Federal Reserve analyses of 2021's 7% peak inflationâ€”drove public frustration independent of partisan loyalty. The structure employs repetitive hooks for chant-like accessibility, prioritizing critique of tangible metrics like CPI escalation over symbolic rhetoric. Musically, the track adopts a trap beat rooted in hip-hop traditions, featuring booming 808 bass, rapid hi-hat patterns, and a minimalist synth melody that evokes Southern rap's energetic pulse while maintaining a straightforward verse-chorus format for broad listenability.[34] This aggressive yet rhythmic style infuses humor through the innocuous-sounding refrain contrasting its subtext, broadening appeal to non-urban audiences alienated by dominant cultural outputs from elite coastal scenes. The production's emphasis on groove over complexity aligns with the song's populist ethos, facilitating organic spread via platforms where economic grievances, not stylistic novelty, fueled engagement amid 2021's documented output gaps from Bureau of Labor Statistics reports.

Promotion and Media

Official Music Video

The official music video for "Let's Go Brandon," directed and produced independently by Loza Alexander, was uploaded to his YouTube channel on October 10, 2021, preceding the single's formal release by eleven days.[35] Featuring Alexander rapping the lyrics in a modest studio setting adorned with American flags, the visuals intercut performance shots with stock footage of pickup trucks, rural landscapes, and crowds of ordinary citizens waving flags and displaying anti-establishment signage, evoking imagery of working-class American defiance.[36] These elements underscored populist themes of grassroots resistance, portraying heartland values against perceived coastal elite dominance without elaborate effects or celebrity cameos. The video's low-fi aestheticâ€”relying on basic editing, smartphone-quality clips, and overlaid text of the chantâ€”highlighted a deliberate DIY approach, contrasting sharply with high-production-value mainstream music videos funded by major labels.[4] It incorporated brief meme-style references to the originating NASCAR footage, amplifying the phrase's viral origins through quick cuts and repetitive chants synchronized to the beat. This unpolished style resonated with the song's anti-corporate undertones, positioning the production as an authentic, viewer-driven phenomenon rather than a scripted spectacle. Within one week of upload, the video surpassed 1 million views, fueled by shares across conservative social media platforms and alignment with the burgeoning meme culture.[36] By late October 2021, viewership escalated into the millions, demonstrating rapid organic traction independent of traditional promotion channels.[35] The absence of sophisticated visuals or narrative complexity kept focus on the raw message, reinforcing the track's role as a cultural flashpoint born from user-generated momentum.

Live Performances and Viral Challenges

Loza Alexander incorporated live renditions of "Let's Go Brandon" into his performances shortly after the song's October 2021 release, with footage uploaded to YouTube capturing audience participation through chants of the phrase.[37] One such video, dated June 5, 2022, depicts a concert setting where Alexander performs the track amid crowd responses, as described by the uploader, though independent verification of attendance figures exceeding 50,000 remains limited to the post's claims.[37] The song's promotion extended through user-generated viral challenges on platforms like TikTok, where participants created skits, dances, and lip-sync videos synced to the track, fostering organic dissemination among younger users.[38] These challenges, emerging by mid-October 2021, encouraged creative reinterpretations that amplified the song's message of dissent, with compilations of submissions garnering significant views and contributing to its cultural persistence beyond initial radio play.[39] On YouTube, similar user content included covers and reaction videos, further evidencing grassroots engagement that bypassed traditional media gatekeeping. Alexander's own social media promotions tied into these trends, framing them as a "challenge" to sustain momentum.

Commercial Success

Chart Performance and Sales Metrics

The song "Let's Go Brandon" by Loza Alexander debuted at number 45 on the Billboard Hot 100 chart dated November 6, 2021, before peaking at number 38 the following week.[5] It also topped the Billboard Emerging Artists chart on October 28, 2021, marking Alexander's entry as a new act driven by digital momentum.[2] In late October 2021, the track ascended to number 1 on the iTunes hip-hop songs chart, displacing mainstream releases and signaling robust download activity among niche audiences.[40] This independent release, lacking major label promotion, accumulated 9.9 million streams by late 2021, primarily through platforms like Spotify and YouTube, where viewer engagement exceeded expectations for non-traditional hip-hop content.[41] These figures highlight performance sustained by direct-to-consumer digital sales and streaming in parallel media ecosystems, bypassing radio airplay and industry gatekeepers typically aligned with establishment narratives.[41] The song's Hot 100 entry relied almost entirely on sales and streams, with minimal radio contribution, demonstrating latent market demand for unfiltered political expression in popular music formats.[5]

Comparison to Mainstream Hits

Loza Alexander's "Let's Go Brandon," released independently on October 21, 2021, achieved its chart peaks primarily through digital sales and streaming driven by social media virality and word-of-mouth among conservative audiences, bypassing traditional radio promotion that mainstream tracks typically rely on.[2][42] The track debuted at number 45 on the Billboard Hot 100 dated November 6, 2021, and ultimately peaked at number 38, a feat accomplished without measurable radio airplay, which constitutes a key component of the Hot 100 formula alongside sales and streams.[5] In comparison, contemporaries like Adele's "Easy on Me," released October 15, 2021, debuted at number 1 on the same chart through a combination of heavy radio rotation, algorithmic playlist placements on platforms like Spotify, and major-label marketing campaigns. This organic momentum enabled "Let's Go Brandon" to temporarily surpass Adele's single on iTunes digital sales rankings in late October 2021, with Alexander's version hitting number 1 on the rap songs chart and Apple Music overall, fueled by direct fan purchases rather than curated streaming algorithms or advertising budgets.[43][6] Unlike algorithm-driven mainstream releases, which often sustain via passive streaming, the song's performance underscored niche audience loyalty, maintaining top digital sales positions into early November despite lacking playlist support from major platforms.[44] Independent distribution facilitated this #1 debut on sales metrics, revealing how direct-to-consumer channels can propel non-conformist content to prominence amid broader market structures favoring established promotion.[2] Such contrasts highlight inefficiencies in the music industry's reliance on radio and algorithms, where politically charged tracks face de facto suppression yet demonstrate verifiable demand through unassisted sales surges, challenging narratives of the song's success as anomalous.[42][5]

Reception

Conservative Embrace and Cultural Resonance

Loza Alexander's "Let's Go Brandon" was widely embraced within conservative circles as a bold articulation of populist discontent with the Biden administration, capturing sentiments of opposition that mainstream outlets often downplayed.[36] Conservative media, including coverage from outlets aligned with right-leaning audiences, spotlighted the track's rapid ascent on charts like iTunes and Billboard's emerging artists list, interpreting its successâ€”reaching No. 1 on the latterâ€”as evidence of organic public approval bypassing traditional gatekeepers.[2] This uptake reflected a broader rejection of sanitized narratives, stemming from the song's roots in a viral incident where a reporter misrepresented crowd chants at a NASCAR event as supportive rather than critical of Biden.[36] The track's resonance extended to heartland and working-class communities grappling with early signs of policy-driven economic strain, including inflation climbing to 5.4% year-over-year by September 2021 and gasoline prices averaging $3.19 per gallon that October, amid shifts toward energy policies prioritizing renewables over domestic production. For many conservatives, the song served as an authentic counterpoint to perceived media and institutional filtering of dissent, aligning with empirical indicators of dissatisfaction such as Biden's approval rating dipping below 50% by late 2021. Its hip-hop style infused traditional conservative critique with rhythmic energy, appealing to younger demographics and positioning it as a cultural outlet for frustrations over rising costs unaddressed by establishment voices. The song's embrace propelled Alexander's visibility, crowning him the top emerging artist on Billboard's chart dated October 30, 2021, and spawning a wave of genre-blended covers and remixes that extended the phrase's reach into country, rap, and parody formats.[2][45] These adaptations, including hip-hop tracks from artists like Bryson Gray, amplified the meme's endurance, fostering a subgenre of anti-establishment music that conservatives viewed as a democratizing force in popular culture.

Liberal Criticisms and Dismissals

Liberal commentators and outlets, such as NPR, characterized the "Let's Go Brandon" song as a sanitized stand-in for a vulgar anti-Biden chant, emphasizing its role in fostering division rather than addressing underlying policy dissatisfactions like the chaotic U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan in August 2021, which resulted in 13 American service member deaths and the abandonment of billions in military equipment.[22] This framing prioritizes rhetorical decorum over causal factors, such as the Biden administration's handling of the evacuation, which Gallup polls indicated contributed to a sharp drop in public confidence on foreign affairs, with only 40% approving of Biden's overall job performance by October 2022.[46] Accusations portraying the phrase or song as hate speech have been limited and often debunked; for instance, claims that organizations like the Anti-Defamation League classified it as such were rated false by fact-checkers, reflecting a tendency in some progressive discourse to equate political dissent with bigotry without empirical substantiation.[47] Instead, dismissals frequently recast it as a partisan "MAGA gimmick," as seen in NBC analyses, sidelining evidence of broader appeal amid widespread policy critiques.[17] Such critiques overlook polling data showing disapproval of Biden-era policies transcending partisan lines, including among independents and moderate Democrats; for example, Pew Research in late 2022 found 64% overall disapproval, with majorities across demographics citing economic pressures like 9.1% peak inflation in June 2022 as key drivers of sentiment.[48] This endurance of the phrase correlates more directly with measurable governance outcomesâ€”such as approval ratings dipping below 40%â€”than with mere vulgarity, suggesting a dismissal rooted in institutional preferences for narrative control over substantive accountability.[46] Mainstream media's systemic left-leaning bias, documented in studies like those from the Media Research Center, amplifies this by underreporting causal links between policy failures and public expressions of frustration.

Controversies and Debates

Censorship Allegations and Platform Responses

Allegations of censorship surfaced shortly after the October 21, 2021, release of Loza Alexander's "Let's Go Brandon," with critics claiming major platforms applied selective enforcement against content expressing right-leaning dissent, particularly when it intersected with skepticism toward official COVID-19 narratives. While Alexander's track itself remained available on YouTube, a contemporaneous version by rapper Bryson Gray was removed by the platform on October 23, 2021, citing violation of its medical misinformation policy due to lyrics stating "Pandemic ain't real, they just planned it."[49][50] YouTube's response emphasized adherence to guidelines prohibiting content contradicting health authority consensus on the pandemic's existence and origins, a policy applied amid broader content moderation efforts targeting vaccine hesitancy and related claims.[51] Supporters argued this reflected algorithmic throttling or shadowbanning of conservative-leaning tracks, evidenced by the song's reliance on off-platform promotion for visibility; for instance, streams and chart positions surged following shares by conservative influencers, bypassing perceived mainstream suppression.[19] TikTok issued warnings against the song, labeling it potential "harassment and bullying" under community guidelines, though it did not result in widespread bans.[52] No equivalent demonetization or removal was reported for Alexander's version on Spotify, where it accumulated streams contributing to its commercial performance, but proponents cited unverified reports of reduced algorithmic recommendations compared to apolitical hits. In contrast, explicitly partisan left-leaning protest songs, such as YG's 2016 track "F*** Donald Trump" featuring vulgar attacks on then-candidate Trump and his supporters, faced no such platform removals despite inflammatory content, highlighting disparities in moderation rigor.[53] The debate underscored claims of institutional bias in tech moderation, where policies ostensibly neutral in wording were enforced more stringently against expressions challenging progressive public health orthodoxy, as seen in the Gray removal versus tolerance for anti-establishment critiques from the opposing ideological spectrum.[54] Alexander and allies maintained that such actions inadvertently amplified the track's reach via backlash, with iTunes chart dominanceâ€”topping hip-hop categories by late October 2021â€”demonstrating consumer-driven dissemination's resilience against centralized control.[55] This survival on alternative channels like Apple Music and independent hosting platforms illustrated the growing role of decentralized distribution in circumventing perceived gatekeeping by dominant tech intermediaries.[56]

Cultural and Political Impact

Use in Rallies and Merchandise

The "Let's Go Brandon" chant, popularized alongside the song, echoed at numerous sporting events in late 2021 and 2022, originating from the October 2 NASCAR race at Talladega Superspeedway where fans directed it toward President Biden during an interview with driver Brandon Brown.[1] Similar outbursts occurred at football games and other venues, reflecting grassroots frustration with federal policies amid ongoing COVID-19 restrictions and economic pressures.[65] At Trump campaign rallies in 2021 and 2022, the phrase emerged as a recurring expression of dissent, integrated into crowd responses and signage during events tracking conservative protest activity.[66] Merchandise bearing the slogan, including hats, shirts, and flags, proliferated through small vendors and online platforms, capitalizing on its viral appeal to generate revenue in conservative-leaning markets.[67] These items often featured alongside Trump-branded goods at rally sites and events, enabling quick production cycles that sustained grassroots fundraising without centralized coordination.[67] The phrase tied into broader mobilizations, such as the U.S. trucker convoys in February 2022 inspired by Canada's Freedom Convoy protests against vaccine mandates, where "Let's Go Brandon" flags and chants adorned vehicles protesting perceived overreach.[68] It also surfaced in parent-led demonstrations against prolonged school closures, aligning with public discontent over educational disruptions and border policy amid U.S. Customs and Border Protection recording 1.72 million southwest land border encounters in fiscal year 2021.[69]

Influence on Subsequent Anti-Establishment Media

The release of "Let's Go Brandon" by Loza Alexander on October 24, 2021, prompted rapid follow-up tracks from independent conservative artists, including Bryson Gray's version released shortly after, which debuted at number one on the iTunes hip-hop chart on October 27, 2021.[70] Forgiato Blow contributed multiple variants, such as a country rap adaptation in late 2021 that amassed over one million YouTube views by early 2022, alongside collaborations like "Like Trump" featuring Gray and Stoney Dudebro.[71][72] These efforts, alongside remixes by artists like Tyson James, fostered a nascent subgenre often termed "MAGA rap" or patriot rap, characterized by anti-establishment lyrics targeting perceived liberal dominance in policy and culture.[73][74] This wave exemplified a disruption in hip-hop, where traditionally left-leaning genres saw conservative independents achieve commercial peaks without mainstream media promotion or label backing, as evidenced by multiple "Let's Go Brandon" iterations occupying iTunes top spots in October 2021.[19] Such successes challenged assumptions of conservative cultural marginalization in music, enabling artists like Gray and Blow to build audiences via platforms like YouTube and iTunes, bypassing gatekept industry channels.[75] By 2022, echoes persisted in broader anti-woke content, with Forgiato Blow's catalog expanding to albums like Mayor of Magaville incorporating similar themes, sustaining momentum for unfiltered, right-leaning hip-hop expressions amid ongoing political dissent.[76] This pattern highlighted a causal shift toward decentralized distribution, where viral, grassroots traction allowed niche ideological music to compete, though sustained chart dominance remained limited post-initial surge.[71]

Long-Term Symbolism Amid Policy Discontent

Lyrics decrying "taxes high as hell" and "gas up to the sky" aligned with the U.S. Consumer Price Index rising approximately 17.8% cumulatively from December 2020 to December 2023, a period marked by trillions in deficit-financed stimulus that empirical analyses link to demand-pull inflation exceeding supply-side explanations alone.[77] This persistence in public memory debunks assumptions of rapid obsolescence for protest anthems, as the song and its associated playlists continued circulation in conservative digital spaces into 2024, symbolizing rejection of policy narratives minimizing such causal links.[78] Corroborating this longevity, President Biden's job approval ratings lingered below 40% in RealClearPolitics aggregates through 2024, averaging around 39-40% in late-year polls and reflecting voter validation of the song's early indictments of administrative competence on economic stewardship.[79] Gallup's final tracking similarly closed at 40%, underscoring a disconnect between policy executionâ€”exemplified by sustained high interest rates into 2025 to combat residual price pressuresâ€”and public sentiment.[80] Rather than fading with electoral cycles, the track's half-life intertwined with these metrics, as ongoing empirical realities like elevated household costs perpetuated its invocation in events and media variants, such as Aaron Lewis's 2024 release "Let's Go Fishin'," which reprised anti-establishment motifs.[81] Ultimately, the song's protracted symbolism highlights the potency of grassroots cultural forms in illuminating policy-outcome causalities, where fiscal expansionism demonstrably amplified inflation without commensurate productivity gains, unmasking gaps between institutional rationalizations and lived experience. This dynamic prioritizes data-driven scrutiny over biased mainstream attributions of mere partisanship, affirming the anthem's role in sustaining discourse on accountability amid entrenched economic distortions.[32]
