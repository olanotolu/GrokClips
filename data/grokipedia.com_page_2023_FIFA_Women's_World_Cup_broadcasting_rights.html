Background and Rights Strategy

Tournament Context and Commercial Goals

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup, co-hosted by Australia and New Zealand from 20 July to 20 August, represented the ninth edition of the tournament and the first expanded to 32 teams across 64 matches, doubling the previous format's scale to enhance global participation and competitiveness. This structure aligned with FIFA's broader Vision 2020-2023 initiative to reform women's football by introducing larger events for senior and youth levels, aiming to elevate the sport's profile through increased exposure and development funding.[9][10] FIFA's commercial objectives centered on treating the event as a standalone product to capture its independent market value, decoupling broadcasting rights from men's tournaments for the first time to drive revenue parity and reinvestment in women's football infrastructure. The organization increased its total investment to USD 499 millionâ€”up from USD 155 million for the 2019 editionâ€”and boosted prize money by 300% to USD 150 million, signaling a commitment to financial equity while urging broadcasters to match this valuation with "fair" bids reflective of the tournament's growth potential.[11][12] FIFA President Gianni Infantino emphasized that low offers undervalued the event's anticipated global reach, targeting billions in cumulative viewership to justify standalone sales and fund long-term commercialization strategies for women's leagues.[13][14] These goals extended to broader economic multipliers, with FIFA anticipating substantial host-nation benefits through tourism, sponsorships, and media partnerships, while prioritizing innovative marketing to break attendance and awareness records as benchmarks for future editions. The strategy underscored a shift toward single-entity control over rights and sponsorships to maximize returns, positioning the Women's World Cup as a viable economic engine separate from male counterparts.[15][16]

Standalone Rights Sales Model

FIFA implemented a standalone rights sales model for the 2023 Women's World Cup, decoupling the tournament's media rights from those of the men's World Cup and other events for the first time in its history. This approach involved offering broadcasting packages exclusively for the women's event to broadcasters, streaming platforms, and digital outlets worldwide, with the explicit goal of elevating its commercial valuation independently. By July 2023, FIFA had concluded deals covering over 95% of the global audience under this framework, reflecting a strategic shift to treat the tournament as a premier standalone property rather than a bundled add-on.[1] The model's rationale centered on accelerating the growth of women's football through targeted revenue maximization, as FIFA believed prior bundling practices undervalued the event's potential amid rising viewership and participation. Rights were tendered regionally via competitive bids, allowing FIFA to negotiate terms based solely on the women's tournament's metrics, such as projected attendance exceeding 1.5 million and anticipated digital engagement. This differed from earlier editions, where rights were often subordinated within broader FIFA packages, yielding lower feesâ€”estimated at around $10-20 million globally pre-2023 versus FIFA's $100 million-plus target for the standalone sale.[16][8] Implementation emphasized flexibility, with FIFA retaining control over free-to-air quotas in host markets while prioritizing pay-TV and streaming deals elsewhere to capture premium pricing. The strategy yielded mixed initial results: successful high-value agreements in markets like Australia and the United States contrasted with protracted disputes in Europe, where bids were deemed insufficient relative to the men's event's â‚¬130 million French rights fee. FIFA rejected several offers outright, insisting on parity in valuation to reflect the tournament's expanded formatâ€”32 teams across 64 matchesâ€”and cultural significance.[17][18][19]

Bidding and Negotiation Process

Initial Global Tenders

FIFA initiated the process for selling broadcasting rights to the 2023 Women's World Cup as a standalone product, decoupling it from the men's tournament for the first time to capture independent commercial value amid rising interest in women's football.[20] The governing body launched invitations to tender (ITTs) starting in April 2022, targeting media rights across multiple territories to build a global broadcast platform.[21] This approach aimed to reflect the tournament's projected growth, with FIFA estimating global media rights value at approximately $300 million.[22] Initial bids received in major markets, including the UK, France, Germany, Italy, and Spain, were rejected by FIFA as undervaluing the event, often amounting to just 1% of comparable men's World Cup rights fees.[2][23] In October 2022, FIFA Chief Business Officer Romy Gai publicly criticized these offers as a "slap in the face" to the women's game and urged broadcasters to submit higher proposals aligned with its commercial potential.[24] Specific ITTs were issued sequentially, such as in the UK on June 9, 2022, and in France on June 2, 2022, emphasizing free-to-air and pay-TV opportunities but yielding insufficient responses initially.[25][26] The low bid valuations stemmed from broadcasters' assessments of historical viewership data, where prior Women's World Cups generated significantly less audience and revenue than men's editions, prompting FIFA to pivot toward prolonged negotiations rather than a unified global package sale.[27] Ultimately, these early tenders fell short of revenue targets by about $100 million, highlighting a disconnect between FIFA's ambitions for parity and market-driven pricing based on empirical demand.[8]

Regional Bid Evaluations and Rejections

FIFA's evaluation of regional broadcasting bids for the 2023 Women's World Cup emphasized a standalone sales model, assessing offers against the tournament's projected commercial value and parity with investments in the women's game, rather than bundling with men's events. Bids were scrutinized for undervaluing the event, with FIFA prioritizing revenue maximization to fund prize money increases and future development, rejecting proposals that fell short of internal benchmarks derived from global media market analyses.[2][28] In Europe, the primary theater of rejections, FIFA dismissed initial tenders from major public broadcasters in October 2022, citing fees as low as 1% of comparable men's World Cup rights in those markets. Specific rejections included a joint offer from BBC and ITV in the United Kingdom, proposals from ZDF/ARD in Germany, France TÃ©lÃ©visions in France, RAI in Italy, and an anticipated similar outcome for RTVE in Spain, as articulated by FIFA Chief Business Officer Romy Gai, who argued the bids ignored the event's standalone worth.[2][24][23] FIFA President Gianni Infantino publicly condemned these as disrespectful to the sport's growth, noting they equated to â‚¬1-2 million per market versus â‚¬100-200 million for men's equivalents.[29][23] No widespread rejections occurred in other regions during initial evaluations; North American rights were secured by Fox Sports without reported disputes, while host-nation Australia awarded free-to-air rights to the Seven Network and digital to Optus post-negotiation, aligning with FIFA's accessibility goals in key markets.[2] The European standoff persisted into May 2023, prompting FIFA warnings of potential blackouts, but evaluations held firm until a collective European Broadcasting Union deal resolved coverage in the big-five markets on June 14, 2023, at reduced fees compared to FIFA's aspirations.[3][30]

Awarded Broadcasting Agreements

Deals in Host Markets (Australia and New Zealand)

In Australia, Optus Sport acquired the exclusive broadcasting rights for all 64 matches of the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup on June 4, 2021, marking the first standalone sale of such rights by FIFA to a subscription streaming service in the host market.[31] This deal covered live streaming, on-demand access, and additional content across Optus platforms, with an emphasis on promoting the tournament as a major event for the co-host nation.[32] To enhance accessibility, Optus sub-licensed free-to-air rights for 15 key matches to the Seven Network on October 25, 2022, including all of Australia's Matildas group stage games, knockout matches if qualified, and select high-profile fixtures such as the opening match and final.[33][34] These FTA broadcasts aired on Channel 7 and 7plus, ensuring national team games reached non-subscribers without additional cost.[35] In New Zealand, the same Optus Sport agreement from June 2021 extended territorial rights, positioning it as the primary holder for the co-host market, though distribution relied on local partnerships due to Optus's Australian base.[31] Sky Sport New Zealand served as the main broadcaster, providing subscription-based coverage of all matches via pay TV and streaming, with no comprehensive free-to-air deal finalized prior to the tournament.[36] Stuff, a digital news platform, offered supplementary streaming for select games, but the majority remained behind Sky's paywall, limiting broad public access in a market with lower subscription penetration for sports content.[36] FIFA separately awarded limited free-to-air rights in the Pacific region, including aspects relevant to New Zealand audiences through the Pacific Cooperation Broadcasting Limited, but this did not extend to full domestic FTA coverage.[37] The arrangements prioritized comprehensive coverage for dedicated fans while highlighting accessibility gaps in both host nations compared to prior tournaments.

European and UK Agreements

In Europe and the United Kingdom, FIFA faced initial resistance from broadcasters in major markets due to the tournament's scheduling in Australia and New Zealand, which created unfavorable viewing times during European prime hours, alongside perceptions of lower commercial value compared to the men's World Cup. This terrestrial TV arrangement addressed UK government concerns over accessibility, given the event's listed status requiring free broadcast, and followed FIFA's direct negotiations after initial bids were deemed insufficient. North American and Other Regional Deals

In the United States, Fox Sports held the English-language broadcasting rights, airing all 64 matches live across the Fox broadcast network, Fox Sports 1, and Fox Sports 2, with streaming available on the Fox Sports app and Tubi for select games.[43][44] Telemundo, under NBCUniversal, secured exclusive Spanish-language rights, broadcasting 39 matches on its national network, including key knockout stage games, and emphasizing a commentary team with a record number of female analysts for Spanish-language media.[45][46] Canada's coverage was handled by Bell Media, with TSN providing English-language broadcasts of every match via linear TV, streaming on TSN.ca and the TSN app, and additional multilingual options including Punjabi and South Asian languages to reach diverse audiences.[47][48] RDS offered French-language coverage for Quebec viewers, ensuring comprehensive national access.[49] In Mexico, TUDN (TelevisaUnivision) broadcast the tournament, including group stage and knockout matches involving Mexican interests, with streaming on Vix for broader digital reach.[50] Beyond North America, deals in Latin America included FIFA+ providing free live streaming in Brazil, while other countries relied on regional sub-licenses or digital platforms where traditional broadcasters did not acquire rights.[1] In Asia, NHK aired matches free-to-air in Japan, CCTV in China, and ELTA in Chinese Taipei, with FIFA+ serving as the platform in markets like Indonesia and Thailand to fill coverage gaps.[1] African territories saw patchwork distribution through pan-African sports networks and FIFA+, contributing to the overall reach across more than 200 territories.[1] These arrangements reflected FIFA's strategy of standalone sales, prioritizing revenue while using its streaming service to ensure global accessibility in underserved regions.[1]

Controversies Surrounding Rights Allocation

FIFA pursued a strategy of revenue maximization for the 2023 Women's World Cup broadcasting rights by decoupling them from men's tournament packages for the first time, aiming to capture standalone value reflective of growing investments in women's soccer, which totaled approximately USD 499 million for the event.[51] The organization set ambitious reserve prices, projecting around USD 150 million in new media rights sales, but encountered broadcaster valuations significantly below expectations, leading to widespread rejections of initial offers deemed insufficient to justify the tournament's commercial potential.[20] In October 2022, FIFA rejected bids from major outlets including the BBC and ITV in the UK, as well as offers from European broadcasters, citing values as low as and ad revenue yields compared to the men's equivalent.[24] FIFA President Gianni Infantino publicly criticized the bids as "unacceptable" and urged networks to "pay what the women's game deserves," warning in May 2023 of potential broadcast blackouts in Europe absent improved offers, a position echoed by players and administrators advocating for parity in commercial recognition.[29][13][19] Broadcasters countered that high fees risked financial losses given uncertain audience draw, with examples like French offers reportedly under 5% of men's rights payments, highlighting a causal Revenue Realizations and Tournament Investments

The 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup generated total revenues of USD 570 million, enabling the tournament to break even financially.[6] Broadcasting rights formed a key revenue stream, contributing to FIFA's overall television broadcasting revenue of USD 267 million for the 2023 financial year, which exceeded the budgeted figure by 5%.[67] This figure encompassed sales for the Women's World Cup as a standalone event, alongside other FIFA competitions, though specific attribution to the tournament alone was not itemized in public reports; prior estimates had targeted USD 300 million in media rights for the event, but realizations fell short by approximately USD 100 million due to lower-than-expected bids in several markets.[8] [20] Tournament investments by FIFA totaled USD 499 million for the Women's World Cup, representing the largest share of expenditures under the Competitions & Events category for the year.[51] These costs included USD 110 million in prize money distributed to participating teamsâ€”up USD 80 million from the 2019 editionâ€”with allocations based on performance (e.g., USD 10.5 million for the winner, Spain, and USD 1.2 million minimum per team).[6] Additional investments covered event infrastructure, marketing, and operational logistics across host nations Australia and New Zealand, contributing to overall 2023 FIFA investments of USD 1,748 million.[51] The break-even outcome reflected a balance between these outlays and revenues from broadcasting, ticketing (over 2 million attendees), and sponsorships, signaling improved commercial viability for women's international soccer despite broadcasting shortfalls.[6]
