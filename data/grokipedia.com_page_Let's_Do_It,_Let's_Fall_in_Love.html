Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love

"Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" is a song written, composed, and with lyrics by Cole Porter, introduced in his first major Broadway success, the musical Paris, which premiered on October 8, 1928, at the Music Box Theatre and ran for 195 performances.[1] The number, known for its witty and risqué enumeration of animals, insects, and human groups that "do it" as a euphemism for mating or romance, marked Porter's breakthrough as a lyricist and composer of sophisticated, innuendo-filled standards.[2] Originally featuring lines with ethnic stereotypes, such as references to "Chinks" for Chinese people, which were later censored or altered in performances and recordings to remove offensive content, the song exemplifies Porter's playful yet provocative style that blended highbrow wit with popular appeal.[3] It quickly became a jazz and cabaret staple, with early recordings including one by Victor's session musicians in October 1928, and has since been interpreted by artists ranging from Ethel Merman to Ella Fitzgerald, cementing its status as one of the Great American Songbook's enduring hits.[4]

Origins and Composition

Development for Paris

"Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" was composed by Cole Porter specifically for his musical Paris, which premiered on October 8, 1928, at the Music Box Theatre in New York City.[5] [6] This production marked Porter's first significant Broadway success, running for 195 performances until March 23, 1929.[5] The song was introduced by the French performer Irène Bordoni, the show's star, during a cabaret-style sequence evoking the expatriate romance and risqué atmosphere of a Paris café.[7] [8] Bordoni, for whom Porter tailored much of the score, delivered the number in a playful, seductive manner that aligned with the musical's theme of liberated American youth abroad.[9] Originally, Porter had considered "Let's Misbehave," a similarly provocative tune written for Paris but ultimately discarded as too explicit for the production; "Let's Do It" adapted its cheeky, exhortative tone while fitting the show's lighter, more teasing sensuality.[10] This integration helped cement Porter's reputation for crafting sophisticated, adult-themed songs that pushed boundaries within Broadway's commercial constraints.[11]

Cole Porter's Creative Process

Cole Porter composed "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" in 1928 for the Broadway revue Paris, drawing on his expatriate experiences in Europe to craft lyrics that catalogued instinctual romantic behaviors across animals, insects, and humans, emphasizing romance as a pervasive natural phenomenon.[12][13] Having resided in Paris and other European locales since the early 1920s, Porter infused the song with observations from his cosmopolitan milieu, where he witnessed diverse social and biological parallels to human courtship.[12] The song adheres to the 32-bar AABA structure common to jazz-age standards, allowing for rhythmic syncopation in the melody and the deployment of Porter's signature witty, internal rhymes within the repeating A sections.[14] This form enabled Porter to layer enumerative lists in the verses—such as "Birds do it, bees do it"—building a cumulative argument for love's biological universality through empirical analogies rather than abstract moralizing.[13] During the iterative revisions for Paris, Porter substituted this number for an earlier song, "Let's Misbehave," refining the lyrics to employ double entendre and innuendo, thereby evading strict censorship while maintaining provocative undertones aligned with his preference for sophisticated suggestiveness over blunt explicitness.[15] This approach reflected Porter's method of integrating music and lyrics holistically at the piano, prioritizing rhythmic flow and verbal dexterity to convey causal patterns in behavior as self-evident truths.[16]

Lyrics and Musical Elements

Original Lyrics and Structure

The original lyrics of "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love," written by Cole Porter in 1928, follow a verse-refrain form typical of Tin Pan Alley standards, with the core refrain—"Let's do it, let's fall in love"—repeated after each verse to punctuate lists of subjects engaging in mating behaviors. The verses employ cumulative, enumerative catalogs for humorous effect, divided into categories: the first on human cultural groups ("In Spain, the best upper sets do it / Lithuanians and Letts do it"), the second on avian and insect examples ("Birds do it, bees do it / Even educated fleas do it"), and the third on marine life ("The loons do it, the coots do it / Even gulls in the mist do it"). These were finalized prior to the song's premiere in the musical Paris on October 8, 1928.[17][18] The rhyme scheme consists of loose couplets (AABB) with frequent internal rhymes and assonance centered on the phrase "do it," as in "They set a date in a rickshaw too / In old Japan, high hokis do it," reinforcing the refrain's inevitability through sonic repetition. Meter adheres to iambic tetrameter across most lines (e.g., "Let's DO it, let's FALL in LOVE"), providing a bouncy, conversational flow suited to performance. Sheet music, published by Harms Inc. in 1928, confirms this textual framework without later interpolations.[17][19] Musically, the song is notated in 4/4 time as an up-tempo foxtrot, with a syncopated rhythm emphasizing off-beats to evoke dancehall energy; the original key is B-flat major, allowing for brassy orchestration and vocal flexibility that later enabled jazz scat and improvisational variations.[20][21]

Themes of Universality in Love and Sex

The lyrics of "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" present sexual attraction and mating as innate, universal drives observable across species and human populations, framing them as biological imperatives rather than lofty ideals. Porter enumerates instances such as "Birds do it, bees do it / Even educated fleas do it," extending the pattern to ethnic groups and social classes, to argue that these behaviors transcend cultural or intellectual barriers.[22][23] This depiction echoes empirical observations in biology, where reproductive instincts propel animal mating rituals essential for species survival, independent of higher cognition.[24] Porter employs humor to underscore causal determinism in these urges, juxtaposing "educated fleas" with human sophisticates to illustrate that biological drives persist unaltered by learning or morality, thus prioritizing instinct over prudish restraint.[25][14] By reducing romance to a shared animalistic propensity, the song challenges romanticized views of love as a uniquely human virtue, favoring a realist assessment rooted in observable patterns across nature.[23] In musical theater, the composition advanced frank discussions of adult heterosexual impulses, aiding the transition from Victorian-era decorum to the candid expressions of the Jazz Age.[26] Contemporary reviewers occasionally critiqued its levity as mere "fol-de-rol," reflecting discomfort with its unadorned realism.[26] Later interpretations value its role in demystifying sexual attraction as a straightforward, biologically driven phenomenon, consistent across diverse contexts without sentimental overlay.[27]

Historical Context and Reception

1920s Cultural Norms

The 1920s, dubbed the Roaring Twenties, witnessed profound social transformations in the United States following World War I, characterized by economic boom, urbanization, and a rejection of prewar constraints that fostered vibrant nightlife in cities like New York and Chicago. The Eighteenth Amendment's enactment of Prohibition in January 1920, intended to curb alcohol consumption, instead catalyzed underground hedonism, with an estimated 30,000 to 100,000 speakeasies operating nationwide by mid-decade, serving illicit liquor and hosting jazz performances that epitomized escapist revelry.[28] This era's cultural ethos, as articulated by F. Scott Fitzgerald, evoked "a whole race going hedonistic, deciding on pleasure," prioritizing sensory indulgence amid postwar disillusionment. Shifts in gender roles accelerated post-1918, bolstered by the Nineteenth Amendment granting women suffrage in August 1920, enabling greater workforce participation—female employment rose from 8.4 million in 1920 to 10.6 million by 1930—and urban independence.[29] Flappers embodied this evolution, adopting bobbed hair, knee-length dresses, public smoking, and petting parties that defied Victorian propriety, signaling a casual normalization of premarital sexuality and female agency in social spheres. Influences from Sigmund Freud's psychoanalytic theories, popularized through figures like Havelock Ellis, permeated discourse by framing sexual drives as innate rather than taboo, indirectly sanctioning innuendo-laden entertainments that skirted explicitness.[30] Broadway transitioned from sentimental operettas to revue formats, exemplified by Florenz Ziegfeld's Follies series (1907–1931), which blended opulent pageantry, seminudity, and witty sexual double entendres to captivate audiences with minimal formal censorship until the 1930s Motion Picture Production Code's influence extended to theater.[31] These productions reflected the decade's superficial exoticism, drawing on Orientalist motifs and foreign stereotypes for allure without substantive cultural engagement, aligning with expatriate Americans' idealization of Paris as a locus of liberated mores.[32] The 1928 revue Paris, staging 195 performances at the Music Box Theatre through March 1929, mirrored this expatriate infatuation, channeling the Lost Generation's flight to France—where an estimated 30,000 Americans resided by 1925—for respite from Prohibition and puritanism, including freer expressions of sexuality.[5][33]

Initial Public and Critical Response

Upon its debut in the Broadway musical Paris on October 8, 1928, "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love," performed by star Irene Bordoni, elicited praise for its witty lyrics and sultry delivery, marking Cole Porter's breakthrough as a songwriter.[5][34] Reviews highlighted the song's fanciful humor and rhythmic appeal, with Bordoni's engaging portrayal noted for injecting vitality into the production's lighter moments.[35] The musical's 195-performance run through March 23, 1929, at the Music Box Theatre underscored the tune's role in sustaining audience interest amid the era's competitive revue scene.[5] The song's commercial viability was evident in its rapid adoption beyond the stage, establishing it as Porter's inaugural major hit and elevating his profile in New York theater circles.[34] Sheet music and early recordings proliferated, reflecting broad appeal among performers and the public, though exact sales figures from 1928 remain undocumented in primary records.[34] This success empirically propelled Porter's career forward, as subsequent productions sought his sophisticated style, without evidence of widespread cancellation or diminished opportunities. Critics and observers divided along cultural lines: traditionalists occasionally decried the lyrics' innuendo as emblematic of Jazz Age decadence, aligning with broader moralist concerns over Broadway's loosening conventions.[34] Yet, progressive commentators and audiences valued its clever subversion of euphemism, viewing the suggestiveness as artistic sophistication rather than vulgarity, which mitigated any organized backlash and affirmed its place in the show's draw.[35] No major scandals ensued, with the tune's reception prioritizing its entertainment value over puritanical objections.[34]

Controversies and Revisions

Ethnic and Racial References in Lyrics

The original 1928 lyrics of "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" included ethnic references using slang terms common in American English at the time, notably "Chinks do it, Japs do it, up in Lapland little Laps do it." "Chinks" denoted Chinese individuals, "Japs" referred to Japanese people, and "Laps" signified the Sami, an indigenous Arctic people formerly known by that exonym.[19] These phrases appeared in an early verse, extending the song's enumeration of entities engaging in courtship and copulation—from birds and bees to nobility and primitives—to encompass distant nationalities, thereby reinforcing the motif of instinctual universality unbound by geography or culture.[19] In the 1920s context of Tin Pan Alley songcraft, such descriptors drew from vaudeville traditions of ethnic caricature, where shorthand slurs evoked exoticism or humor through stereotyping, as seen in broader popular music's casual deployment of racial and national tropes without evident animus toward targeted populations.[36] Porter's listings, akin to those in contemporaneous works by songwriters like Irving Berlin, prioritized rhythmic scansion and global sweep over sensitivity, reflecting an era when such language normalized otherness in entertainment without sparking organized backlash. No contemporary accounts document complaints or boycotts over these specific lines during the song's debut in the Broadway production Paris, which premiered on October 8, 1928, and ran for 195 performances.[5] This approach aligned with causal patterns in early 20th-century American lyricism, where empirical acceptance is gauged by the absence of archival protests amid commercial viability; the song's rapid integration into revues and recordings post-premiere underscores that the references registered as unremarkable wit rather than provocation.[5]

Debates Over Censorship and Artistic Integrity

The song's suggestive title and lyrics, which enumerated various creatures and humans engaging in mating behaviors with double entendres, prompted early censorship efforts by broadcasters adhering to moral standards. Originally titled simply "Let's Do It," the subtitle "Let's Fall in Love" was appended at the insistence of censors to soften its explicit implications before its New York premiere in 1928.[37] During the 1930s and 1940s, the National Association of Broadcasters' (NAB) code restricted "suggestive" content on radio, leading stations to ban or alter performances of "Let's Do It" alongside other Porter standards like "Love for Sale," as they were deemed unsuitable for family audiences despite their popularity.[38] Post-World War II, revisions to the lyrics facilitated broader airplay amid heightened cultural sensitivities to sexual innuendo, reflecting stricter media regulations aimed at promoting domestic stability. For instance, broadcasters and record producers often substituted milder verses or omitted lines implying promiscuity, such as those referencing animals "doing it" in sly, anthropomorphic ways, to comply with NAB guidelines updated in the late 1940s that prohibited "matter which may be considered obscene or suggestive."[38] Proponents of these changes argued they preserved the song's commercial viability, enabling it to reach mass audiences via radio and early television, where unaltered versions risked outright prohibition; this adaptation contributed to its enduring presence in the Great American Songbook by aligning with evolving broadcast norms that prioritized accessibility over unfiltered wit.[39] Critics of such alterations, however, contend they undermine artistic integrity by diluting Porter's intentional irreverence and sophisticated humor, which relied on adult subtext to convey universal sexual impulses without euphemism. Bowdlerized versions, including those excising potentially offensive ethnic or national references in added verses (e.g., stereotypical allusions to "Argentines" or "Dutch"), are likened to sanitizing literary classics, stripping the work of its contextual edge and reducing its intellectual depth; Porter himself revised verses fluidly but resisted wholesale expurgation that altered core themes.[39] [40] This perspective holds that media-driven changes, driven by regulatory pressures rather than aesthetic merit, erode the song's authenticity, as evidenced by later scholarly analyses decrying the loss of Porter's layered irony in favor of palatable conformity.[39] Contemporary debates amplify these tensions, with advocates for revisions citing cultural evolution and offense avoidance—often aligned with progressive sensitivities to outdated stereotypes—as justification for ongoing modifications in performances and recordings.[39] Opponents, emphasizing free expression and historical context, argue that such sanitization imposes anachronistic moralism, betraying Porter's era-specific candor about human nature and risking the erasure of subversive art that challenged norms; they point to empirical outcomes like diminished lyrical complexity in adapted versions, which forfeit the original's causal link between humor and realism in depicting desire.[41] These views underscore a broader contention: while regulatory shifts post-1945 empirically expanded the song's reach, they causally compromised its unvarnished portrayal of sexuality, prioritizing market conformity over unaltered creative intent.[38]

Recordings and Performances

Early and Mid-20th Century Versions

The song was first performed on stage by Irène Bordoni and Arthur Margetson in Cole Porter's musical Paris, which opened on October 8, 1928, at the Music Box Theatre in New York City.[42] Bordoni's rendition, delivered in a cabaret-style with French-accented English, emphasized the lyrics' playful innuendo within the era's big band accompaniment, setting a template for orchestral interpretations.[43] The earliest commercial recording appeared on October 16, 1928, by Irving Aaronson and His Commanders for Brunswick Records (Br 4001), featuring a dance-band arrangement with vocal harmonies that captured the tune's syncopated rhythm and light-hearted seduction.[42] Soon after, Paul Whiteman and His Orchestra released a version on Columbia Records (1701-D) in late 1928, with vocals by Jack Fulton, Charles Gaylord, and Austin Young; this polished, symphonic jazz take, blending strings and brass, reflected Whiteman's "King of Jazz" style and achieved moderate sales in the pre-chart era through sheet music tie-ins and radio play.[44] By the 1950s, jazz vocalists adapted the song amid shifts from swing to bebop and cool jazz, incorporating improvisational scat and rhythmic flexibility. Eartha Kitt recorded a sultry, purring version on January 13, 1953, backed by Henri René and His Orchestra for RCA Victor, released on the album That Bad Eartha in 1954; her delivery heightened the erotic undertones with elongated phrasing and breathy timbre, aligning with her cabaret persona.[45] Ella Fitzgerald included a scat-infused rendition on her 1956 album Ella Fitzgerald Sings the Cole Porter Song Book (Verve MGV 4001-2), arranged by Buddy Bregman with a small combo; clocking in at 3:32, it showcased bebop-derived vocalese and swung the melody loosely, contributing to the album's commercial success—peaking at No. 2 on Billboard's jazz charts—while establishing the track as a durable jazz standard despite no standalone single hit.[46] These mid-century versions marked an evolution from rigid 1920s orchestration to expressive improvisation, mirroring broader jazz developments without altering core commercial metrics, as sales relied on album formats over 78 rpm singles.[42]

Later Interpretations and Notable Artists

In the 1990s, the song received a punk rock reinterpretation by Joan Jett featuring Paul Westerberg for the Tank Girl soundtrack, transforming Porter's witty standard into a high-energy track with distorted guitars and rebellious attitude, emphasizing its enduring appeal across genre boundaries.[47] This version drew from earlier jazz renditions like Ella Fitzgerald's while adapting the lyrics to fit a grunge-punk aesthetic, released on April 25, 1995.[48] Alanis Morissette offered a contemporary alternative rock take in 2004 for the Cole Porter biopic De-Lovely, where her raw, emotive vocals and layered production blended the original's playful innuendo with post-grunge intensity, clocking in at 3:21 and featured prominently in the film's musical sequences.[49] Her rendition, produced for the soundtrack album released on July 13, 2004, showcased the song's versatility in bridging classic standards with modern female-led rock expressions.[50] Funk influences emerged in the 1970s through the Ohio Players' "Honey," a mid-tempo groove from their September 1975 album Honey, which interpolated melodic and lyrical elements from Porter's composition—such as rhythmic phrasing echoing "birds do it, bees do it"—and explicitly credited him as co-composer in the liner notes, marking an early fusion of jazz standards with R&B-funk grooves.[51] These adaptations underscore the tune's structural flexibility, allowing reinterpretations that preserve its core syncopated rhythm and humorous universality while innovating timbres and tempos for later audiences.

Cultural Impact and Legacy

Appearances in Media and Popular Culture

The song "Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" has been featured in multiple films, often to evoke the wit and sophistication of the Jazz Age. In Woody Allen's Midnight in Paris (2011), it accompanies scenes set in 1920s Paris, aligning with the film's nostalgic portrayal of the era's expatriate artists and musicians.[52] Similarly, the 2004 biopic De-Lovely, which chronicles Cole Porter's life and career, includes a contemporary cover by Alanis Morissette on its soundtrack, performed to highlight the composer's enduring lyrical cleverness.[49][53] In television and animation, the tune's risqué humor has lent itself to ironic or parodic uses, though specific episodes tying directly to scripted references remain limited in documented instances. Its adaptability for lighthearted innuendo appears in broader pop culture crossovers, such as duets evoking classic Hollywood glamour, including a rendition by Frank Sinatra and Shirley MacLaine that captures mid-20th-century interpretive flair.[54] Commercials have repurposed the song for playful marketing, notably in a Cadbury's Cream Egg advertisement featuring a version emphasizing its catchy, seductive rhythm to promote indulgence.[55] These media integrations underscore the lyrics' original heterosexual romantic banter—framed through animal and cultural analogies—while later adaptations sometimes layer queer reinterpretations, informed by Porter's own bisexuality, without altering the core empirical observation of widespread animal mating behaviors inspiring human parallels.[56] Sustained popularity is evident in streaming platforms, where covers and originals continue to accumulate listens, reflecting the song's status as a standard beyond initial Broadway runs.[57]

Enduring Influence on Music and Society

"Let's Do It, Let's Fall in Love" has exerted a lasting influence on musical composition and performance through its innovative list-song format, which enumerates whimsical examples of amorous behavior across animals, humans, and cultures, setting a precedent for clever, rhythmic lyricism in American standards.[58] This structure, characterized by chromatic descents and cascading rhymes, became a hallmark of Cole Porter's style and inspired subsequent songwriters to blend humor with sophistication in addressing romance and desire.[59] The song's melodic accessibility and infectious rhythm have ensured its place in the jazz repertoire, with interpretations emphasizing scat singing and improvisational phrasing that highlight its adaptability.[60] As a jazz standard, the composition has been recorded by over a hundred artists, spanning genres from swing to contemporary vocal jazz, demonstrating its versatility and enduring appeal in live performances and studio sessions.[61] Notable versions include Louis Armstrong's 1957 rendition, which infuses the lyrics with scat elements and bluesy phrasing, and Ella Fitzgerald's 1956 interpretation from her Cole Porter Songbook, praised for its rhythmic precision and emotional depth that influenced later vocalists in the Great American Songbook tradition.[62][63] These recordings have perpetuated the song's role in cabaret and theatrical revues, reinforcing its status as a benchmark for witty innuendo in popular music.[64] On a societal level, the song's playful normalization of sexual impulses as a universal phenomenon challenged early 20th-century taboos, contributing to a gradual liberalization of lyrical content in mainstream entertainment by framing desire through humor rather than prudery.[65] Its risqué yet lighthearted references to ethnic and animal behaviors reflected and advanced the 1920s flapper-era shift toward candid expressions of sexuality, influencing cultural attitudes toward romance in media and influencing later works that blend sophistication with sensuality.[66] Despite occasional censorship concerns over its suggestive tone, the track's longevity underscores its role in embedding a cosmopolitan, irreverent view of love into collective consciousness, evident in its resonance across generations without reliance on explicitness.[67][64]
