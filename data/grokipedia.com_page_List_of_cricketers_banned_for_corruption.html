List of cricketers banned for corruption

The list of cricketers banned for corruption catalogs professional players prohibited from the sport by the International Cricket Council (ICC) or national boards for offenses under the ICC Anti-Corruption Code, including match-fixing (manipulating entire outcomes for gain), spot-fixing (predetermining specific incidents like no-balls), betting on matches, and failing to report approaches from corruptors.[1][2] These sanctions, adjudicated by the ICC Anti-Corruption Tribunal, range from conditional suspensions to lifetime bans, with the code stipulating maximum penalties for the gravest breaches to deter syndicated interference driven by illicit betting markets.[1][2] The enforcement framework emerged in 2000 following Hansie Cronje's confession to accepting bookmaker payments to influence South Africa's matches against India, prompting his lifetime exclusion by Cricket South Africa (recognized internationally) and the ICC's inaugural Anti-Corruption Code.[3][1] Subsequent probes revealed systemic vulnerabilities, particularly in bilateral series and leagues vulnerable to underground wagering, leading to landmark cases like Pakistan's 2010 spot-fixing scandal, where captain Salman Butt and bowlers Mohammad Amir and Mohammad Asif received five-to-ten-year bans for no-ball orchestration.[4] The ICC's Anti-Corruption Unit continues proactive monitoring, education, and investigations, yielding recent sanctions such as six-year bans for West Indies' Marlon Samuels (2023) over bookmaker ties and five-year prohibitions for players like Sri Lanka's Saliya Saman (2025) and Bangladesh's Shohely Akhter (2025) for solicitation and non-disclosure failures, reflecting persistent risks despite enhanced protocols.[5][6][7]

Background on Corruption in Cricket

Historical Development of Scandals

Suspicions of corruption in cricket emerged prominently in the early 1990s, particularly in matches involving Pakistan and India, where underground betting networks proliferated. Allegations surfaced against Pakistani players like Salim Malik, who was accused of offering bribes to Australian cricketers during a 1994 series, leading to a lifetime ban in 2000 following a judicial inquiry that uncovered patterns of inducements tied to illegal bookmakers.[8] Similarly, in India, investigations in 1999 implicated former captain Mohammad Azharuddin in match-fixing during the 1990s, with Justice A.P. Shah's report later confirming his role in influencing outcomes for betting syndicates, highlighting how opaque financial incentives from South Asian betting markets began eroding player integrity in regions with limited oversight.[9] These early cases demonstrated causal links between high-volume illegal wageringâ€”estimated in billions annuallyâ€”and targeted approaches to players, rather than isolated moral failings. The 2000 scandal involving South African captain Hansie Cronje marked a pivotal escalation, exposing systemic vulnerabilities. On April 7, 2000, Delhi police charged Cronje with fixing one-day internationals against India in March, releasing transcripts of his communications with bookmaker Sanjay Chawla, who offered payments for match outcomes and player underperformance.[10] Cronje confessed on April 11, admitting to receiving approximately $100,000 and orchestrating fixes in series against India and England, which implicated 16 other South African players in varying degrees of awareness or involvement, underscoring syndicate-driven operations that exploited touring teams' isolation.[11] This confession, prompted by intercepted calls, revealed empirical patterns of corruption detection through law enforcement intercepts rather than internal probes, with betting networks leveraging personal contacts to manipulate results for profit in unregulated markets. Subsequent scandals reinforced these patterns, with spot-fixingâ€”deliberate manipulation of specific events like no-ballsâ€”emerging as a refined tactic. In August 2010, during the Lord's Test against England, Pakistan players Salman Butt, Mohammad Asif, and Mohammad Amir were caught in a sting operation by the News of the World, deliberately bowling no-balls for Â£150,000 in payments from a bookmaker, leading to their convictions in 2011 and bans by the ICC.[12] The 2013 Indian Premier League incident involved Rajasthan Royals players S. Sreesanth, Ajit Chandila, and Ankeet Chavan, arrested by Delhi police for spot-fixing overs in exchange for bribes from bookies, exposing how T20 formats' rapid scoring created exploitable betting opportunities amid lax franchise monitoring.[13] These events illustrated persistent syndicate influence from South Asia, where weak regulatory enforcement in high-stakes gambling hubs facilitated repeated incursions into international and domestic cricket. Recent cases, such as the November 2023 six-year ban on West Indies' Marlon Samuels for failing to report undisclosed benefits and approaches during the 2019-20 Pakistan Super League and 2021 Caribbean Premier League, reflect ongoing challenges despite heightened awareness.[14] In February 2025, Bangladesh's Shohely Akhter became the first woman banned by the ICC, receiving a five-year suspension for corrupt approaches to teammates during the 2023 Women's T20 World Cup in South Africa, admitting breaches that involved attempting to recruit players for fixing.[15] These developments empirically trace a continuum from broad match-fixing to targeted solicitations, driven by the same causal factors of lucrative betting ecosystems preying on vulnerabilities in less-monitored domestic and emerging formats, with detection increasingly reliant on player disclosures and intelligence sharing.

Forms of Corruption and Their Mechanisms

Match-fixing involves the deliberate manipulation of an entire match's outcome to favor betting syndicates, where players or officials agree to underperform or predetermine results in exchange for payments, thereby ensuring predictable results that enable gamblers to profit from insider knowledge.[2] This mechanism undermines cricket's integrity by severing the causal link between skill, strategy, and victory, as outcomes become decoupled from on-field merit, eroding the sport's competitive essence and spectator confidence in authentic contest.[16] Empirical patterns show syndicates target vulnerable players through direct financial inducements, exploiting asymmetries in information and enforcement to place large bets on manipulated events.[17] Spot-fixing targets discrete, observable incidents within a matchâ€”such as bowling a no-ball at a specified delivery or conceding a set number of runs in an overâ€”allowing fixers to bet on these micro-events without altering the overall result, which preserves plausible deniability for participants.[18] These actions create artificial predictability in granular data streams, which betting markets aggregate for odds calculation, enabling syndicates to arbitrage discrepancies between fixed occurrences and public expectations.[19] By isolating corruption to non-decisive elements, spot-fixing maintains the facade of competitive play while siphoning value from the game's inherent uncertainty, gradually corroding trust as anomalies accumulate and statistical analyses reveal non-random patterns.[20] Additional violations include direct participation in betting on cricket matches or failure to report corrupt approaches from intermediaries, which facilitate syndicate entry by normalizing illicit contacts and withholding intelligence that could disrupt fixing networks.[2] These acts perpetuate corruption cycles through omission, as unreported overtures signal player receptivity, inviting escalation to active involvement.[21] Corruption mechanisms exploit player vulnerabilities, particularly in lower-tier domestic cricket where modest salariesâ€”often below sustainable living costs in high-betting regionsâ€”create incentives for supplemental income, compounded by coercion from organized groups leveraging threats or debts.[22] While short-term personal gains from fixes provide immediate financial relief, they causally precipitate systemic erosion: diminished fan engagement, sponsorship flight, and league viability, as repeated incidents quantify the sport's reliability at near-zero, prioritizing individual agency over unsubstantiated cultural rationales.[23]

Anti-Corruption Institutions and Responses

ICC Anti-Corruption Unit and Code

The International Cricket Council's Anti-Corruption Unit (ACU) was established in 2000 under the leadership of Sir Paul Condon to combat match-fixing and betting-related corruption exposed in prior scandals, providing a centralized mechanism for investigation, education, and enforcement across international and member events.[24][25] The unit operates under the ICC Anti-Corruption Code, a strict liability framework that prohibits actions such as failing to report corrupt approaches, facilitating fixes, or accepting benefits from bookmakers, with violations adjudicated by independent tribunals imposing bans ranging from months to lifetime durations.[2] Key mechanisms include real-time monitoring of betting patterns via partnerships with licensed operators and data analytics firms to detect irregularities, alongside mandatory education workshops for players and officials on recognizing and reporting approaches.[26][27] The code, amended effective January 1, 2021, and replaced by a unified global version on June 1, 2024, emphasizes proactive intelligence gathering and whistleblower protections to enforce reporting obligations.[2] Recent investigations demonstrate its application, such as the August 15, 2025, five-year ban on former Sri Lankan domestic player Saliya Saman for attempting to recruit others in corrupt activities during the Abu Dhabi T10 league.[28] The ACU's zero-tolerance approach has resulted in dozens of bans since inception, correlating with heightened player vigilance and fewer publicly confessed fixes, as evidenced by tribunal decisions and reduced overt scandals in ICC events.[4] However, underground betting markets persist, underscoring limitations in fully eradicating external influences despite deterrence through exemplary penalties.[29] Enforcement relies on self-reporting and intelligence, with critics noting potential gaps in uniform application across varying national board capacities, though empirical data shows sustained investigations yielding consistent sanctions.[5]

National and Domestic Enforcement

The Pakistan Cricket Board (PCB) has enforced strict anti-corruption measures in domestic cricket following high-profile international scandals, aligning its code with ICC standards to impose lengthy bans and education mandates on violators. For instance, after the 2010 spot-fixing incident, the PCB supported the 10-year ban on Salman Butt, prohibiting his participation in national leagues and administrative roles until its partial expiry, signaling a commitment to rigorous oversight in Pakistan Super League and first-class competitions.[30][31] In India, the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI) maintains a dedicated Anti-Corruption Unit for domestic and IPL probes, independent of ICC jurisdiction for league-specific breaches. The BCCI imposed a lifetime ban on S. Sreesanth in 2013 for spot-fixing in IPL matches, citing deliberate underperformance for financial gain, though an internal committee reduced it to seven years in 2019 amid appeals and evidentiary reviews.[32] This case highlights BCCI's focus on franchise integrity but also variances in sanction durability, as reductions reflect procedural leniency not always extended to lesser-known players. The England and Wales Cricket Board (ECB) exercises jurisdiction over county cricket, enforcing its anti-corruption code with decisive action against imported players in domestic fixtures. Lou Vincent, a New Zealand batter, received a lifetime ban in 2014 from the ECB's Cricket Discipline Commission for admitting 18 breaches, including match-fixing approaches in three county games, though rehabilitation efforts led to its revision in 2023, allowing limited domestic return.[33][34] Bangladesh Cricket Board (BCB) enforcement in domestic leagues mirrors ICC protocols for code breaches outside international play, as with Nasir Hossain's two-year ban in 2023â€”six months suspendedâ€”for failing to report corrupt approaches and related inducements, restricting him from Bangladesh Premier League participation until 2025.[35][36] Similarly, Sri Lanka Cricket has addressed domestic-level manipulations in franchise formats, upholding the five-year ban on first-class player Saliya Saman in 2025 for attempting to fix outcomes in the 2021 Abu Dhabi T10 League, backdated to his provisional suspension.[37] These national efforts reveal disparities in rigor, with boards like the PCB and ECB applying severe initial penalties in structured leagues, while reductions in cases like Sreesanth's underscore potential inconsistencies favoring high-profile figures. Weaker monitoring in semi-professional domestic circuits exacerbates vulnerabilities, as unregulated incentives in T10 and county formats enable localized fixing absent ICC scrutiny.[38][39]

Banned Cricketers by Competition Level

International Level Bans

The most prominent international-level corruption bans stem from the 2000 South African match-fixing scandal, where Hansie Cronje, captain of the South African team, admitted to accepting payments from bookmakers to influence outcomes in One Day Internationals against India in 2000; he received a lifetime ban from all cricket on October 11, 2000, imposed by the United Cricket Board of South Africa and endorsed under emerging ICC oversight.[10][40] In the 2010 Pakistan spot-fixing scandal during a Test match against England at Lord's, an ICC tribunal found three players guilty of deliberate no-balling for bets; Salman Butt, the captain, was banned for 10 years effective February 5, 2011, Mohammad Asif for five years, and Mohammad Amir for five years, with the bans reflecting their roles in orchestrating and executing the fixes.[41][42]
  • Salman Butt (Pakistan): 10-year ban from February 5, 2011, for instructing spot-fixing in the Lord's Test.[41]
  • Mohammad Asif (Pakistan): Five-year ban from February 5, 2011, for delivering predetermined no-balls.[41]
  • Mohammad Amir (Pakistan): Five-year ban from February 5, 2011, for bowling no-balls as part of the fix; he served the full term and returned to international cricket in 2016 after rehabilitation.[41]
Marlon Samuels of West Indies faced an ICC Anti-Corruption Tribunal in 2023 for seven breaches of the code, including failing to report approaches and associating with suspected corruptors during his international career; he was banned from all cricket for six years effective November 11, 2023.[43] In a case tied directly to international women's cricket, Shohaly Akhter of Bangladesh admitted to five breaches, including offering bribes to players for corrupt conduct during the ICC Women's T20 World Cup 2023 in South Africa; she received a five-year ban effective February 10, 2025, marking the first such penalty for a female cricketer in ICC events.[15][44] These bans highlight a concentration in matches involving high-stakes betting markets, particularly from Pakistan, where subcontinental dynamics amplify vulnerability, as evidenced by tribunal findings across scandals.[12]

Domestic and League-Level Bans

In domestic cricket, national boards enforce bans for corruption in first-class, List A, and T20 competitions under their oversight, often addressing spot-fixing or match-fixing that exploits weaker monitoring in lower-stakes games compared to international fixtures. These cases highlight vulnerabilities in cash-incentivized leagues, where syndicates target less scrutinized matches to build networks potentially extending to higher levels. Enforcement varies by board, with penalties aligned to ICC codes but adjudicated locally, emphasizing failure to report approaches or direct manipulation.[45] Notable instances include Bangladesh's Mohammad Ashraful, banned eight years by the Bangladesh Cricket Board in June 2014 for accepting money to fix outcomes in a domestic T20 league match, with the ban reduced to five years (two suspended) after appeal.[46][45] In Pakistan, the Pakistan Cricket Board imposed a five-year ban on Sharjeel Khan in August 2017 for spot-fixing during the 2017 Pakistan Super League, involving deliberate underperformance in specific overs.[47] Similarly, PCB banned Asif Afridi for two years in 2021 for breaching anti-corruption rules by failing to report a corrupt approach in domestic play.[48] The England and Wales Cricket Board issued a lifetime ban to Pakistani bowler Naved Arif in June 2014 for spot-fixing six no-balls in a 2011 county match between Sussex and Kent, following his guilty plea to multiple code breaches.[49][50] Such domestic-level violations underscore how corruption often originates in regional leagues before risking escalation, with boards like PCB and ECB imposing severe deterrents to curb syndicate infiltration.[51]

Patterns and Outcomes of Bans

Geographic and Demographic Trends

The majority of bans for corruption in cricket have concentrated in South Asian nations, particularly Pakistan, India, and Bangladesh, where expansive illegal betting networks exert strong influence on players. Pakistan alone has produced numerous cases, including the 2010 spot-fixing scandal resulting in initial lifetime bans for three national team membersâ€”Salman Butt, Mohammad Amir, and Mohammad Asifâ€”amid approaches by bookies during a Test match against England.[52] India's domestic leagues have seen violations like the 2013 IPL spot-fixing involving players such as S. Sreesanth, while Bangladesh recorded its first international women's ban in February 2025 with offspinner Shohely Akhter's five-year suspension for attempting to recruit a teammate via social media for a deliberate underperformance.[15] These patterns correlate with the region's massive underground betting volumes, estimated at over $100 billion annually in India alone, peaking during events like the IPL and fueling syndicate operations that target vulnerable athletes.[53] Demographically, banned individuals are exclusively male except for Akhter's landmark case, which highlights corruption's spread to women's cricket amid the sport's professionalization.[7] Offenders typically fall in mid-career stages (ages 25-35), when international exposure peaks and financial pressures from inconsistent earnings or family obligations intensify in lower- to middle-income contexts. South African cases, such as Hansie Cronje's 2000 lifetime ban for match-fixing a ODI series, and West Indian Marlon Samuels' 2023 six-year prohibition for undisclosed bookmaker contacts, show similar profiles but fewer instances outside Asia.[54] Critics have questioned enforcement disparities, noting rarer bans in affluent Western nations like England or Australia despite their players' involvement in global leagues; yet, data reveals elevated violation rates in high-betting ecosystems, where syndicates' persistence yields more detections via player confessions or investigations. Recidivism underscores systemic risks in under-monitored environments, as evidenced by repeat breaches among Pakistani domestic players post-2010 reforms, countering narratives of disproportionate penalties with the need for uniform deterrence.[4]

Overturned, Reduced, or Served Bans

Several corruption bans in cricket have been overturned through legal challenges, reduced via appeals to bodies like the Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS), or fully served permitting rehabilitated players to return, highlighting tensions between punitive measures and opportunities for redemption. These outcomes have prompted debates on the ICC Anti-Corruption Code's application, with successful modifications in isolated cases underscoring variability in enforcement rather than systemic leniency. While full reintegration remains rare for severe offenders, instances of post-ban productivity challenge notions of permanent irredeemability, though they coexist with risks of recidivism evidenced by repeat violations in the sport.[55][56] Saleem Malik's lifetime ban, imposed by the Pakistan Cricket Board (PCB) in May 2000 for match-fixing involvement, was overturned by a Lahore district court on October 23, 2008, citing insufficient evidence in the original proceedings. Despite the judicial ruling, the PCB and ICC did not immediately recognize it for international eligibility, limiting Malik's practical return to advisory roles much later, such as a PCB batting consultant appointment in 2020. This case illustrates how national courts can nullify bans but may not restore full professional status without governing body concurrence, potentially eroding uniform deterrence.[55][57][58] Mohammad Amir completed his five-year ban from September 2010 for spot-fixing deliberate no-balls during the Lord's Test, resuming international play on January 15, 2016, against New Zealand in a T20I where he claimed figures of 1/28. His subsequent Test return at Lord's in July 2016 yielded 1/58 in England's first innings, contributing to Pakistan's series victory and signaling rehabilitation through sustained performances until his 2020 retirement from internationals. Amir's trajectory demonstrates effective deterrence via served penalties paired with reintegration protocols, including counseling, yielding a player who avoided further breaches and bolstered team success.[59][60] Umar Akmal's initial three-year ban from April 27, 2020, for failing to report corrupt approaches ahead of the Pakistan Super League, was halved to 12 months by CAS on February 26, 2021, with an additional fine of approximately $27,000. He recommenced competitive cricket in September 2021 with Central Punjab in domestic tournaments, marking a reduced sanction that balanced accountability with career salvage. This appeal success reflects procedural scrutiny of evidence thresholds, yet Akmal's limited international recall post-return underscores persistent trust deficits, informing calibrations between initial severity and appellate equity to retain talent without compromising integrity.[56][61][62]
