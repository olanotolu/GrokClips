New People (political party)


New People (Russian: Новые люди) is a Russian political party founded on 1 March 2020 by entrepreneur Alexey Nechayev, co-founder of the cosmetics company Faberlic, which promotes reduced state control over economic and social spheres to foster individual initiative and competition.[1][2] The party positions itself as a vehicle for "new people" to influence policy through mechanisms like direct referendums and decentralization, criticizing excessive bureaucracy while supporting market-oriented reforms.[3] In the September 2021 State Duma elections, New People crossed the 5% threshold with 5.32% of the vote, securing 13 proportional representation seats and forming the smallest parliamentary faction, marking the first such breakthrough for a newly registered party in over a decade.[2][4] Despite its calls for liberalization, the party has endorsed core government actions, including recognition of the Donetsk and Luhansk "republics" and subsequent military operations, prompting accusations from independent analysts that it functions as a controlled opposition to channel dissent away from genuine challengers to the ruling United Russia without posing systemic risks.[5][1]

History

Founding and Registration (2020)

The New People political party was established on March 1, 2020, through a founding congress in Moscow initiated by Alexey Nechayev, the founder and president of the cosmetics firm Faberlic.[6][7] Nechayev, drawing from his entrepreneurial background, positioned the party as a platform for injecting fresh perspectives into Russian politics, targeting urban professionals and younger demographics disillusioned with established entities.[1] Registration followed swiftly, with the party entered into the Unified State Register of Legal Entities (EGRUL) on March 24, 2020, under OGRN 1207700135972, confirming approval by the Ministry of Justice.[8] This process required the party to meet federal criteria, including gathering signatures from at least 10,000 supporters distributed across no fewer than 50 percent of Russia's federal subjects to demonstrate nationwide viability amid a regulatory framework designed to limit frivolous entrants.[9] The party's early documentation emphasized "new people" as non-career politicians capable of addressing systemic inefficiencies through pragmatic reforms, reflecting Nechayev's vision of business-oriented governance.[5] This foundational appeal aligned with broader allowances under Russia's post-2012 party laws, which reduced barriers for new formations compared to pre-2000s thresholds, enabling entities like New People to emerge without parliamentary legacy.[10]

Initial Organizational Growth and Activities (2020–2021)

Following its founding convention in March 2020, the New People party, led by entrepreneur Alexey Nechayev from August 2020, rapidly expanded its organizational base by drawing on Nechayev's Faberlic company employees and graduates from the "Captains of Russia" management training program.[5] This recruitment strategy emphasized young professionals and business-oriented individuals, aligning with the party's "Humanism 3.0" ideology aimed at modern urban voters seeking alternatives to established politics.[1] The party's growth was facilitated by a lack of obstruction from regional administrations, enabling it to submit candidate lists for legislative assemblies without the barriers faced by genuine opposition groups.[11] In the September 2020 regional elections, New People achieved breakthroughs by securing seats in several regional parliaments, including 7.46% in Kostroma, 8.08% in Kaluga, 5.72% in Ryazan, 7.19% in Novosibirsk, and 15% in Tomsk, as well as representation in various city councils.[5][1] These results qualified the party to bypass signature collection requirements for the federal elections, marking a key step in its pre-electoral buildup. The party invested heavily in regional campaigns, media advertising—spending at least 15 million rubles in the first quarter of 2021—and social media outreach to build visibility.[1] New People's activities positioned it as a liberal, non-confrontational option critiquing government inefficiencies and repression without direct opposition to United Russia, incorporating rhetoric on fighting corruption to appeal to disillusioned voters.[12] Favorable coverage on state-aligned channels, such as those owned by Yuri Kovalchuk, and the absence of repression signaled tacit regime approval, distinguishing it from suppressed entities like Alexei Navalny's network.[5][12] Party congresses, including one in Moscow in 2020, further mobilized supporters and refined messaging for urban professionals.[12] This expansion reflected a controlled diversification of the political landscape, siphoning potential protest votes while maintaining alignment with presidential priorities.[1][11]

Breakthrough in 2021 State Duma Elections

In the 2021 Russian legislative elections held from September 17 to 19, the New People party achieved a national vote share of 5.32 percent in the proportional representation component, crossing the five percent threshold required for parliamentary representation and securing 13 seats in the 450-member State Duma.[13][5] This marked the first entry of a newly registered party into the Duma since 2011, expanding the number of factions to five and providing a nominal liberal voice amid a landscape dominated by United Russia.[14] The campaign, led by founder Alexey Nechayev—a cosmetics industry executive—emphasized pro-business policies, including tax relief and reduced bureaucracy for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), alongside decentralization to empower regional governance over federal overreach.[5] The party leveraged social media and targeted urban outreach to appeal to voters frustrated with economic stagnation, positioning itself as pragmatic reformers rather than systemic challengers.[15] Analysts noted its voter base skewed toward educated urban youth and professionals in major cities like Moscow and St. Petersburg, who sought alternatives to United Russia's incumbency but avoided alignment with boycotted opposition figures like Alexei Navalny; this dynamic effectively fragmented potential liberal turnout, with New People capturing votes that might otherwise have bolstered abstention or tactical anti-United Russia efforts.[5][16][12] In the immediate post-election period, the party formalized its Duma faction on October 12, 2021, comprising its 13 proportional-list deputies, enabling procedural rights such as committee assignments and bill initiation.[17] Initial activities focused on submitting proposals for SME credit access and pilot programs for regional fiscal autonomy, though these faced limited traction amid United Russia's majority control; critics, including independent observers, attributed the party's success to tacit administrative tolerance rather than organic momentum, viewing it as a mechanism to simulate pluralism without altering power dynamics.[18][19][14]

Evolution and Activities Post-2021 to 2025

Following its entry into the State Duma in late 2021, the New People party's faction prioritized legislative proposals to ease regulatory burdens on small and medium enterprises, adapting to economic pressures from Western sanctions imposed after Russia's February 2022 recognition of the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics and subsequent special military operation in Ukraine.[20] The faction consistently aligned with Kremlin priorities, including backing the partial mobilization decree issued by President Putin on September 21, 2022, which aimed to bolster military efforts amid ongoing hostilities.[21] In alignment with its self-described liberal orientation, the party advocated for wartime economic measures such as digitalization incentives for businesses and tax relief to mitigate sanction impacts, submitting multiple bills to the Duma between 2022 and 2024 that emphasized innovation over direct confrontation with foreign policy stances.[11] Faction leader Alexey Nechayev, leveraging his background as Faberlic founder, positioned these initiatives as pragmatic responses to isolation, arguing in Duma sessions for reduced bureaucracy to sustain domestic production amid supply chain disruptions.[22] Nechayev's December 2023 decision to register as a presidential candidate for the March 2024 election highlighted the party's role in managed pluralism, framing his platform around entrepreneurial freedoms and anti-corruption reforms without challenging core national security narratives.[22] This move, reportedly coordinated with administration signals, underscored continuity in the party's systemic opposition function, focusing activities on visibility within sanctioned parameters rather than grassroots mobilization.[23] By early 2025, the party's resilience manifested in sustained institutional engagement, including a March 12 meeting with Prime Minister Mikhail Mishustin to review fiscal support for regional economies strained by war expenditures, and its fifth congress on March 19, where President Putin extended greetings affirming the faction's contributions to legislative bodies.[20][24] A November 19, 2024, Kremlin meeting between Putin and Nechayev further evidenced operational stability, discussing policy alignments amid geopolitical tensions.[25] Membership hovered around 100,000 registered affiliates by mid-2024, with no major reported influxes or defections tied to mobilization waves, reflecting plateaued growth in a controlled political ecosystem.[26]

Political Positions

Economic and Business Policies

The New People party positions itself as a proponent of market-oriented reforms within Russia's predominantly state-directed economy, emphasizing support for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), deregulation, and incentives for innovation to foster growth amid bureaucratic constraints. Influenced by founder and leader Alexey Nechayev's background as an entrepreneur who established the Formula 10 retail chain, the party's platform prioritizes reducing administrative burdens on businesses and promoting a shift toward a digital and high-tech economy.[1][27] This approach contrasts with the incumbent United Russia's heavier reliance on state intervention and resource extraction, advocating instead for policies that empower private initiative while acknowledging the need for targeted industrial development. Key proposals include tax system simplification, such as replacing value-added tax (VAT), corporate profit tax, and social insurance contributions with a unified turnover-based tax for enterprises, aimed at easing compliance for SMEs and stimulating economic activity.[28] In response to proposed 2025 fiscal changes that would lower the revenue threshold for mandatory VAT registration—potentially affecting up to 300,000 SMEs—party leader Nechayev warned of widespread closures and urged reconsideration to protect small businesses from increased burdens.[29] The party has also pushed for safeguards against arbitrary tax authority actions, including prohibiting the Federal Tax Service from blocking entrepreneurs' accounts without due process and limiting pre-audit deductions to 10% of inflows, as part of over 70 emergency measures outlined in early 2022 to mitigate sanction-related disruptions.[30] On industrialization and innovation, New People supports state-backed initiatives like a federal project for developing industrial robotics and production automation, which Nechayev proposed and which gained government endorsement in 2025 to enhance manufacturing efficiency.[20] The party advocates preserving VAT exemptions for domestic software developers to bolster the digital economy and has called for channeling more SME tax revenues to local budgets to incentivize regional business support.[31] In February 2022, amid economic pressures, Nechayev proposed introducing a "new economic policy" modeled on partial liberalization principles to aid business adaptation, though specifics emphasized evolutionary rather than radical shifts.[32] These stances reflect a pragmatic blend of pro-business deregulation and selective state involvement, without challenging core statist elements like energy sector dominance.

Social and Domestic Policies

The New People party emphasizes decentralization as a core domestic policy, advocating for the devolution of legislative powers to regions to enable tailored local laws rather than uniform federal mandates, alongside redirecting financial resources from the center to subnational levels to foster regional self-sufficiency. This approach critiques the inefficiencies of Russia's centralized governance model, which the party argues stifles local innovation and responsiveness, though in practice, federal dominance has constrained substantive shifts toward autonomy, with regional budgets remaining heavily dependent on Moscow's allocations as of 2025.[33][34] In anti-corruption efforts, the party positions itself against entrenched "old elites" by proposing structural reforms, including a January 2023 bill to introduce criminal liability for corruption modeled on the European Criminal Law Convention, aiming to impose personal accountability on officials beyond mere administrative penalties. Party rhetoric frames this as empowering new, merit-based leadership over legacy networks, yet Russia's entrenched patronage systems and selective enforcement—evident in persistent high corruption perceptions indices—highlight gaps between such proposals and realized outcomes.[35] On social issues, New People supports conservative-leaning family policies aligned with broader Russian emphases on tradition, including a project to preserve family values and reduce divorce rates through state incentives, as well as a October 2025 proposal to extend subsidized family mortgages to childless couples committing to parenthood within specified timelines. The party has also advanced bills addressing domestic violence, targeting protections amid Russia's centralized legal framework where regional variations in enforcement remain limited.[36][37][11] Youth empowerment features prominently, with the party—self-described as representing "new people" over aging elites—backing expanded youth parliaments and participatory mechanisms, such as updated principles for Moscow's Youth Chamber adopted in September 2025 to enhance young voices in local governance. In education, proposals include granting parental committees voting rights on curricula, menus, and teaching methods, alongside calls to boost federal subsidies for school meals by at least 50% and prohibit harmful additives like antibiotics in food. Healthcare positions are less delineated but include advocacy for heightened accountability against assaults on medical personnel, reflecting a focus on service delivery amid centralized resource constraints. These initiatives appeal to younger demographics but have yielded incremental legislative progress rather than systemic overhaul in Russia's top-down domestic policy environment.[38][39]

Foreign Policy and National Security Stance

The New People party aligns closely with Russia's official foreign policy framework, endorsing a multipolar world order that prioritizes national sovereignty, strategic autonomy, and countering perceived Western hegemony. Party members, including leader Alexei Nechayev, have publicly affirmed support for Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov's approach, which views NATO expansion and EU policies as existential threats to Russian security interests.[40] This stance reflects the party's integration into the systemic opposition, where foreign policy discourse reinforces Kremlin red lines without deviation, despite occasional rhetorical emphasis on pragmatic diplomacy over confrontation.[41] On the Ukraine conflict, New People has backed the "special military operation" as a necessary measure to protect Russian-speaking populations and prevent NATO encirclement, consistent with State Duma resolutions post-February 2022. The party supported the recognition of the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics on February 21, 2022, and subsequent referenda leading to the annexation of four regions (Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia) in September 2022, framing these as corrections to historical injustices and defenses against Ukrainian aggression.[42] Nechayev and faction colleagues have advocated for political unity in response to Western isolation efforts, rejecting calls for troop withdrawal or negotiations conceding territorial integrity.[42] In national security terms, the party promotes Eurasian integration through mechanisms like the Eurasian Economic Union (EAEU) and Collective Security Treaty Organization (CSTO), viewing them as bulwarks against unipolar dominance. This includes proposals for enhanced military-technical cooperation with partners such as Belarus, Kazakhstan, and Armenia to bolster regional stability. While positioning itself as business-oriented and open to selective Western engagement—such as trade diversification—New People avoids critiquing core Kremlin positions, like the indivisibility of Crimea since 2014, evidencing alignment over professed independence. Academic analyses of Russia's managed pluralism highlight how such parties, including New People, contribute to legitimizing aggressive postures without introducing substantive alternatives.[41]

Leadership and Internal Structure

Key Figures and Leadership Transitions

Alexey Nechayev, born on 30 August 1966, serves as the founder and chairman of the New People party since its inaugural congress on 8 August 2020.[43] An entrepreneur with no history of political repression, Nechayev built his career founding and leading Faberlic, a major Russian cosmetics firm, reflecting establishment-aligned business success rather than oppositional activism.[5] His leadership has emphasized economic liberalism, drawing from prior involvement in pro-government initiatives like the All-Russia People's Front.[44] Alexander Davankov heads the party's executive committee, supporting Nechayev's direction as a key operational figure with a background in business and regional politics.[45] Following the December 2023 merger with the Party of Growth, Boris Titov, the latter's former leader and a prominent business advocate as presidential commissioner for entrepreneurs' rights, integrated into the broader structure, enhancing New People's pro-business orientation without altering core leadership.[45] Leadership has remained stable post-2021 State Duma entry, with Nechayev retaining the chairmanship and faction head role through 2025, evidenced by ongoing Kremlin engagements and absence of internal purges typical in non-establishment groups.[43] This continuity underscores causal ties to tolerated elite networks, enabling persistence amid Russia's managed political competition.[11]

Party Organization and Membership

The New People party maintains a centralized organizational structure typical of Russian political parties under federal law, featuring a federal leadership council chaired by Alexey Nechayev, an executive committee for operational decisions, and regional branches established across at least 45 of Russia's federal subjects to qualify for national parliamentary participation.[8] Candidate selection follows a formula emphasizing merit-based primaries and business-oriented profiles, prioritizing urban professionals and entrepreneurs aligned with the party's liberal economic agenda, though final approvals rest with central leadership to ensure compliance with electoral regulations.[1] This setup facilitates coordinated nationwide campaigns while mirroring the hierarchical control mechanisms prevalent in Russia's managed political system. Membership expanded rapidly post-founding, reaching approximately 50,000 by late 2021 to meet legal thresholds for federal elections, with a demographic skew toward urban centers like Moscow and St. Petersburg, drawing middle-class voters disillusioned with established parties but supportive of incremental reforms.[5] The base remains concentrated among younger, educated professionals in services and business sectors, reflecting the party's origins in targeting "new" socioeconomic groups rather than broad rural or industrial constituencies. Funding derives primarily from private donations and business contributions, including substantial input from Nechayev's personal wealth via his Formula KARTING franchise, supplemented by post-election state allocations based on vote share, which totaled over 400 million rubles annually by 2022.[26] Internally, the party demonstrates minimal dissent and high operational cohesion, with members routinely participating in state-mandated events such as unity rallies and policy endorsements, indicative of effective top-down discipline that prioritizes alignment over autonomous debate.[33] This compliance extends to low incidence of factional splits or public criticisms, enabling sustained legislative functionality despite the controlled environment of Russian politics.

Electoral Performance

Federal Parliamentary Elections


In the 2021 Russian legislative elections for the State Duma, held from September 17 to 19, the New People party obtained 5.32% of the proportional representation vote, surpassing the 5% threshold necessary to secure seats from the party lists.[33] This performance translated into 13 total seats in the 450-member chamber, comprising allocations from both proportional and single-mandate districts.[33] The party's approach emphasized contesting single-mandate constituencies where opposition fragmentation created opportunities, enabling wins in districts lacking strong challengers to United Russia candidates.[46]
Analysts have characterized New People's electoral debut as functioning in part as a vote-splitter for liberal-leaning constituencies, diverting support from non-parliamentary opposition parties such as Yabloko and thereby reinforcing the systemic parties' control by preventing additional entrants to the Duma.[14] Following the 2021 elections, the party has experienced no substantial alterations to its parliamentary representation through by-elections or other federal mechanisms.[47] As of October 2025, New People continues to hold its 13 seats amid preparations for the next full State Duma elections scheduled for 2026, with no reported gains or losses in interim federal contests.[48]

Presidential Elections

In the 2024 Russian presidential election, held from March 15 to 17, the New People party nominated State Duma deputy Vladislav Davankov as its candidate.[49] Davankov, positioned as a younger, business-oriented alternative, campaigned on themes including economic liberalization, youth engagement, and a negotiated end to the Ukraine conflict without conceding territory, while adhering to Russia's constitutional red lines on the issue.[50] Official results showed Davankov securing 3,362,484 votes, or 3.85% of the total, placing third behind incumbent Vladimir Putin (87.28%) and Communist Party candidate Nikolai Kharitonov (4.31%).[51] This performance underscored the party's marginal electoral appeal at the national executive level, with its vote share reflecting limited penetration beyond urban and entrepreneurial demographics despite targeted outreach via social media and regional tours.[23] Participation in the contest, without calls for boycott, aligned with the framework of systemic opposition parties approved by the Central Election Commission, thereby contributing to the election's procedural legitimacy amid widespread international criticism of restrictions on anti-war candidates like Boris Nadezhdin.[52][53] Prior to 2024, the New People party had no involvement in presidential elections, having been established in October 2020 following the 2018 vote. Its debut bid highlighted a strategy of incremental visibility within Russia's managed electoral system rather than disruptive opposition, though the low tally empirically constrained its influence on national policy discourse.[54]

Regional and Local Elections

The New People party has recorded vote shares typically ranging from 3% to 7% in regional and local elections since 2021, with isolated wins in by-elections and urban assemblies shortly after its formation. In September 2021, coinciding with federal polls, the party secured mandates in several local dumas, leveraging momentum from its national debut to penetrate select municipal bodies. Performance remained inconsistent, showing early footholds in areas like Astrakhan's city duma, where representation doubled post-election.[55] By the 2023 single voting day, New People maintained low-to-mid single-digit results across regions such as Bashkortostan (3.41%) and others, failing to establish broad dominance but gaining sporadic seats amid limited competition. Urban areas exhibited relative strength, with higher percentages in city councils compared to rural districts, reflecting appeal among younger, city-dwelling voters disillusioned with established parties. Rural penetration lagged, underscoring geographic disparities in grassroots support.[56] In the September 14, 2025, single voting day elections across 11 regions with legislative contests, New People passed the 5% threshold in 8 of 9 participating areas for regional parliaments, securing seats in entities including Chelyabinsk (8.11%), Novosibirsk (7.9%, yielding 4 deputies in the oblast assembly), Kaluga (5.47%), and Voronezh (5% regionally, 16.58% in the city duma). Failures occurred in Ryazan (below 5%) and non-participation or no seats in Tambov and Belgorod, highlighting selective administrative backing rather than uniform integration.[57][58][59] These outcomes evidence administrative constraints on the party's expansion, with successes tied to regional leadership directives and Kremlin coordination—such as in Chelyabinsk and Novosibirsk—rather than independent voter mobilization or systemic embedding akin to United Russia. Analysts note the party's role as a managed "administrative project" for channeling controlled opposition, evident in cautious campaigning that avoids direct confrontation and relies on top-down support, limiting organic growth beyond urban enclaves.[59]

Controversies and Criticisms

Allegations of Kremlin Orchestration and Vote-Splitting

Independent analysts and media outlets have characterized the New People party as a Kremlin-orchestrated "administrative project" designed primarily to act as a spoiler, diverting protest votes from genuine opposition forces such as Alexei Navalny's supporters and anti-United Russia factions during the 2021 State Duma elections.[60][61] Founded on March 1, 2020, by entrepreneur Alexey Nechayev—who lacked prior political experience—the party encountered no significant registration obstacles, in contrast to repeated denials faced by authentic opposition initiatives.[60] It secured 5.32% of the proportional vote in September 2021, gaining 13 single-mandate seats and entering the Duma for the first time, thereby fragmenting liberal and protest ballots that might otherwise have bolstered non-systemic parties.[61][60] Supporting these allegations, the party has benefited from state-aligned promotion, including favorable coverage by pro-Kremlin sociological agencies and coordination with regional administrations under figures like Sergei Kiriyenko, the presidential aide overseeing domestic policy.[60][59] On February 15, 2023, Nechayev met with President Vladimir Putin at Novo-Ogaryevo, where he affirmed alignment with Kremlin priorities on economic sovereignty and technological independence, reporting the party's endorsement of over 70 government initiatives with more than 30 implemented.[62] Unlike independent media and opposition groups routinely labeled foreign agents, New People has evaded such designations and repression, enabling its persistence as a controlled entity.[59] During the September 2025 Single Voting Day across 11 regions, it secured legislative seats—except in Tambov and Belgorod—primarily through administrative directives rather than voter mobilization, underscoring stagnation without broader appeal.[59] Party representatives reject these characterizations, portraying New People as an authentic alternative to entrenched systemic parties rather than opposition or Kremlin proxy.[63] Nechayev has emphasized initiatives like the "Ya v dele" project, active in 62 regions and involving 85,000 young participants, as evidence of grassroots engagement independent of state orchestration.[62] Nonetheless, critics argue its consistent avoidance of core Kremlin critiques and reliance on personalist leadership around Nechayev reinforce perceptions of managed legitimacy over organic political viability.[59]

Positions on the Ukraine Conflict and Sanctions

The New People party has expressed unwavering support for Russia's special military operation (SVO) in Ukraine, aligning with the Kremlin's position that diplomatic efforts to address security concerns had been exhausted prior to the February 24, 2022, launch of the operation.[64] Party leader Alexey Nechayev and faction members have publicly endorsed the SVO as a defensive measure essential for protecting Russian interests, consistently voting in the State Duma to approve related legislation, including budgets and resolutions reinforcing military efforts.[21] The party backed the September 2022 referendums in Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts, recognizing the results as legitimate expressions of local will and supporting their subsequent annexation into the Russian Federation via federal law ratified by the State Duma on October 3, 2022.[21] This stance reflects the party's broader patriotic framework, prioritizing territorial integrity and self-determination in disputed regions over international criticism of the process as coerced.[21] On Western sanctions, New People has rejected them as economically destructive tools aimed at undermining Russia's resilience, arguing that they exacerbate inflation and supply shortages without altering Moscow's strategic objectives.[65] In response, the party advocated for domestic countermeasures, including a proposed "new economic policy" (NEP) on February 28, 2022, to bolster citizen support through subsidies, import substitution, and fiscal incentives amid sanction-induced pressures.[65] These positions have resulted in reciprocal sanctions: the European Union targeted the party on December 16, 2022, for enabling the SVO and annexation, freezing its assets and barring EU dealings; Ukraine imposed similar measures, designating New People as a sanctioned entity for its alignment with Russian policy.[66][67] This pro-SVO consensus has fostered internal unity, with no recorded factional splits on Ukraine-related votes, distinguishing the party from more vocal domestic critics while reinforcing its role as managed opposition within Russia's political system.[68]

Domestic Policy Critiques and Internal Dissent

Critics of New People's domestic policy platform have highlighted its emphasis on incremental reforms, such as restoring direct mayoral elections and unblocking social media platforms like Instagram and YouTube, as insufficient to address entrenched bureaucratic inefficiencies or corruption under United Russia's dominance.[16] The party's legislative record in the State Duma shows consistent support for Kremlin-backed domestic measures, including municipal governance reforms and the expansion of electronic voting, which observers argue dilutes any genuine challenge to the ruling party's control over administrative and electoral processes.[16] [14] Further scrutiny focuses on perceived inconsistencies, such as the party's advocacy for anti-migrant policies—including restrictions on migrant labor entry, expedited deportations of criminal offenders, and proposals to ban niqabs or censor film content—which conflict with its self-proclaimed liberal values and have been labeled opportunistic or xenophobic by analysts.[16] High-profile initiatives, like standardizing shawarma recipes or imposing speed limits on electric scooters, have drawn attention for their triviality amid broader failures to enact meaningful economic liberalization or anti-corruption measures, reinforcing views of the party's approach as superficial rather than transformative.[16] Internal dissent within New People is infrequent, characterized by tight factional discipline enforced by founder Alexey Nechayev, particularly on major bills where the party aligns with Kremlin priorities to preserve its systemic status.[16] Rare instances of deviation occur on peripheral domestic issues; for example, in April 2025, Duma deputy Ksenia Goryacheva condemned regional authorities in the Altai Republic for offering cash bonuses to schoolgirls who become pregnant, framing such incentives as promoting "tragedy" over heroism and questioning their alignment with effective demographic strategies.[68] Early reservations among some members regarding the party's post-2022 policy shifts were quickly subordinated to leadership directives, avoiding escalation into broader factional rifts.[16] No significant internal splits or expulsions have been reported as of late 2025, underscoring the party's limited autonomy and its function as a controlled outlet for mild reformist rhetoric without disrupting United Russia's legislative hegemony on core domestic matters.[14]

Impact and Assessment

Legislative Achievements and Influence

The New People faction in the State Duma, holding around 15 seats since its formation in October 2021, has focused legislative efforts on co-sponsorships and amendments rather than independent bills, reflecting its minority position within a United Russia-dominated chamber. Between October 2021 and May 2023, the faction introduced 160 federal bills and 400 amendments, culminating in 49 laws signed into effect by the president, primarily addressing business deregulation and economic adjustments.[69] Subsequent activities through 2025 continued this pattern, emphasizing targeted reforms such as raising damage thresholds for economic crimes to reduce minor prosecutions, an initiative credited in official proceedings.[70] On national security priorities, the faction has demonstrated alignment through voting support for war-related legislation. In September 2022, 13 of 15 deputies voted for a bill tied to partial mobilization implementation, while proposing clarifying amendments to define mobilization criteria and enhance public oversight.[71][72] This pattern extended to consistent endorsement of measures protecting participants in the special military operation and their families, alongside broader war economy laws from 2022 to 2025, in coordination with other parliamentary groups.[73] Influence remains marginal due to the faction's size, with achievements manifesting as incremental contributions to coalition-backed reforms rather than transformative standalone legislation. Co-sponsorships on economy and security bills have facilitated passage of supportive measures, but the party's outputs prioritize alignment with executive priorities over oppositional gains, limiting distinct legislative imprint.[11]

Public Reception and Polling Data

The New People party has garnered modest public support since entering the State Duma in 2021, with national polling consistently placing it in the low single digits, reflecting limited breakthrough beyond its initial electoral momentum. VCIOM surveys from 2022 to mid-2025 showed ratings fluctuating between 4% and 7%, reaching 7.6% in October 2025 amid broader political stability assessments.[74] Independent polls by the Levada Center indicated steadier figures around 5-6%, such as 6% in April 2025 and approximately 5% in August 2025, underscoring a plateau rather than growth.[75][76] These trends contrast with pre-2021 hype from pro-Kremlin pollsters predicting potential surges above 5%, which did not materialize into sustained popularity.[1] Support dipped notably in early 2025, with some surveys recording as low as 3% in February, coinciding with heightened scrutiny over the party's policy stances and internal dynamics.[77] Urban centers like Moscow and St. Petersburg show marginally higher approval, driven by the party's pro-business rhetoric, but rural and older demographics exhibit broader indifference, contributing to national averages below 6%.[78] Demographic breakdowns reveal appeal concentrated among youth and educated professionals: in analyses of 2024 presidential candidate performance tied to the party, younger and higher-educated voters disproportionately backed New People's figures, viewing them as agents of economic reform amid systemic stagnation.[79] Conversely, surveys capture cynicism, with only niche segments expressing hope for genuine renewal, while a majority perceives limited differentiation from established parties in addressing core grievances like inflation and regional disparities.[80] VCIOM data, as a state-affiliated pollster, may overstate viability compared to Levada's methodology, which accounts for non-response biases in authoritarian contexts.[81]

Analytical Perspectives on Viability and Role

Analysts characterize the New People party as a manufactured entity within Russia's managed opposition framework, designed to project an illusion of multiparty pluralism while aligning with Kremlin priorities.[82] It functions primarily as a vote-splitter for urban, liberal-leaning constituencies, siphoning potential support from genuine critics and undermining coordinated opposition strategies, such as those previously advocated by Alexei Navalny.[14] This role sustains a controlled political diversity in the State Duma, where the party supports key regime legislation, including municipal reforms and electronic voting mechanisms, without challenging core power structures.[16] Observers note its reliance on presidential administration oversight, exemplified by figures like Sergei Kiriyenko, who has publicly endorsed its parliamentary entry as a "political miracle," reinforcing its position as a Kremlin-orchestrated "career elevator" rather than an autonomous actor.[11] Assessments of viability highlight the party's persistent non-systemic status as of 2025, evidenced by its dependence on administrative resources rather than organic voter mobilization during regional elections.[59] In the September 2025 single voting day across 11 regions, New People secured seats in most legislatures but avoided confrontations with governors, coordinating closely with local authorities and exhibiting caution to evade conflicts, which underscores its fragility absent top-down directives.[59] This administrative character limits its capacity to rival established systemic parties like the Communist Party or Liberal Democratic Party, as it lacks high-profile independents or a stable ideological core, with electoral gains—such as the 5.32% threshold in 2021—attributed to engineered outcomes rather than broad appeal.[33][14] Prognostic analyses project constrained growth potential heading into the 2026 Duma elections, where ambitions to position as a "second-choice" option for discontented urban voters hinge on continued Kremlin tolerance, yet face risks of absorption into United Russia or marginalization if perceived as redundant.[16] Without rivalry from a dominant United Russia, the party's utility diminishes, potentially leading to reconfiguration of the party system toward even more consolidated control.[14] Causally, it mitigates political vacuums by channeling entrepreneurial and intellectual energies into regime-compatible channels, averting the emergence of uncontrolled alternatives amid wartime consolidation, though this comes at the expense of authentic pluralism.[82][16]
