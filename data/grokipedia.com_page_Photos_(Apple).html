Photos (Apple)

Photos is a photo management and editing application developed by Apple Inc. It was released as a bundled app in iPhone OS 1, and released as a bundled app to macOS Yosemite users in the 10.10.3 update on April 8, 2015, replacing iPhoto and Aperture. It was also introduced for watchOS 1.0 on April 24, 2015. It was released for tvOS 10 on September 13, 2016. It was released for visionOS 1.0 alongside the Apple Vision Pro on February 2, 2024.

History

The Photos app was originally released as a bundled application with iPhone OS 1 on June 29, 2007, providing basic photo viewing capabilities on the iPhone. In June 2014, Apple announced its plan to discontinue the applications iPhoto and Aperture, to be replaced by a new application, Photos, in 2015.[1][2] The app was included with OS X Yosemite 10.10.3, released as a free update to users on April 8, 2015.[3][4][5] On September 13, 2016, the app was later included in tvOS 10.[6] With the introduction of the Apple Vision Pro on the WWDC 2023 on June 5, 2023, the Photos app came to visionOS, enabling access to the iCloud photo library and spatial photo/video viewing.[7] With iOS 18, iPadOS 18, and macOS Sequoia, the Photos app received a comprehensive redesign at WWDC 2024, marking its biggest feature update since its introduction. The goal of the redesign was to improve usability, simplify library organization, and provide new AI-powered features. Photos

Features

Photos is intended to be less complex than its professional predecessor, Aperture.[3] Through version 4.0 (released with macOS 10.14 Mojave) the Photos app organized photos by "moment", as determined using combination of the time and location metadata attached to the photo.[5] Starting in version 5.0 (released in 2019 with macOS 10.15 Catalina), photos can instead be browsed by year, month, or day.[9] As of iOS 18, iPadOS 18, and macOS Sequoia (released in 2024), the Photos app features a redesigned interface with a unified library view for easier navigation, including new collections such as Recent Days, Recent People & Pets, and Featured Photos.[10]

Editing

Photos includes robust editing functions that are utilized with simple controls, such as a one-click auto-enhance button.[11] As of iOS 18 and macOS Sequoia (2024), Apple Intelligence adds advanced tools including the Clean Up feature, which uses machine learning to remove distracting objects from photos while preserving the original quality.[12]

iCloud Photo Library

iCloud Photo Library is heavily integrated into the app, keeping photos and videos in sync with various Apple devices designated by the user (such as Macs, iPhones, iPads, and Apple Vision Pro), including edits and album structures.[13] iCloud integration is still optional, but it is much more central to Photos as compared to iPhoto.[11] As of visionOS 2 (2024), it supports spatial photos captured on compatible devices.[14]

Professional printing

Like its predecessors, Photos initially included a number of options for professional printing of photos, which could then optionally be turned into books or calendars and mailed to an address. With Photos, Apple added new types of prints, including square sizes and the ability to print panoramas.[5] In July 2018, Apple announced, via a pop-up message in Photos, that they would be discontinuing these services, adding that users should submit any final orders by September 30, 2018.[15]

Sharing

iCloud Photo Sharing allows sharing photos with others. Others can view, like or comment existing shared photos or contribute new photos to the shared album.[16] Other ways of sharing include e-mail, social platforms that integrate through iOS Extensions, or Apple's peer-to-peer AirDrop technology.[17] As of iOS 18 and macOS Sequoia (2024), enhanced search and Apple Intelligence enable natural language queries for sharing specific content, and users can create AI-generated memory movies to share.[12]

Criticism

Critics noted the loss of functionality in Photos as compared to its predecessors. For example, images could no longer be ordered as Events but were either automatically ordered chronologically into Moments or had to be put into albums. The latter did not allow for automatic sorting[18] and it was necessary to configure Smart Albums with customized user-defined rules to do so.[19] Customers who had been using the Aperture application, abandoned by Apple on the release of Photos, were particularly angry about loss of professional-standard functionality. Apple customers who upgraded to OS X 10.11 El Capitan, which was launched in 2015, found that if they had not first updated iPhoto to its most recent version (9.6) before upgrading, they could no longer update it through the App Store and needed to reinstall it from their purchase history, or otherwise transition to Photos.[20] In 2024, the redesign of the Photos app in iOS 18 and equivalent macOS updates drew significant criticism for its cluttered, unified scrolling interface, removal of navigation tabs, redundant auto-generated collections, and difficulty in locating specific photos. Users reported confusion and reduced usability, leading Apple to remove features like the Carousel in beta updates and restore elements such as pinch-to-zoom in iOS 18.2. At WWDC 2025, Apple announced further refinements for iOS 26, reintroducing separate tabs for Library and Collections to address these concerns.[21][22]
