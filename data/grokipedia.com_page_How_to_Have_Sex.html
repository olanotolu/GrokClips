How to Have Sex

How to Have Sex is a 2023 British coming-of-age drama film written and directed by Molly Manning Walker in her feature-length directorial debut.[1] The story centers on three teenage girls—played by Mia McKenna-Bruce, Lara Peake, and Enva Lewis—who travel to Malia, Crete, for a post-exam holiday filled with partying, clubbing, and sexual exploration, but which confronts the protagonists with issues of consent, peer pressure, and sexual assault.[2] Premiering in the Un Certain Regard section of the 2023 Cannes Film Festival, the film won the Un Certain Regard Prize, recognizing its innovative approach to adolescent experiences.[1] It received critical acclaim for its authentic depiction of female friendship and the ambiguities of young adulthood, earning nominations at the British Academy Film Awards, including for Outstanding Debut by a British Writer, Director or Producer.[3] While praised for addressing consent realistically without didacticism, the film has sparked discussions on its portrayal of assault amid holiday excess, reflecting broader debates on youth culture and vulnerability.[4]

Plot

Synopsis

How to Have Sex centers on three 16-year-old British best friends—Tara (Mia McKenna-Bruce), Skye (Lara Peake), and Em (Enva Lewis)—who embark on a celebratory trip to the party resort town of Malia on Crete, Greece, following the completion of their GCSE exams.[5][6] The film depicts their immersion in the hedonistic environment of sun-drenched beaches, all-night clubbing, excessive alcohol consumption, and casual sexual encounters, capturing the euphoric chaos of youthful freedom and peer-driven expectations to "have sex" as a rite of passage.[7][8] As the narrative unfolds over the course of their week-long holiday, the group navigates the dynamics of friendship under the influence of relentless partying and flirtations with local and fellow British tourists, including a group of boys from their hotel.[9] The story highlights Tara's particular journey of sexual awakening, marked by initial excitement and bravado, which increasingly confronts the ambiguities of consent, regret, and emotional fallout amid the blurred boundaries of intoxicated encounters.[4][10] Directed by Molly Manning Walker, the film employs a vivid, neon-lit aesthetic to contrast the vibrant highs of adolescent revelry with the sobering undercurrents of vulnerability and trauma, ultimately examining how such experiences reshape personal identity and interpersonal bonds.[11][12]

Cast

Principal Actors

Mia McKenna-Bruce stars as Tara, the central character and introspective member of the trio of British teenage girls on a holiday in Malia, Crete, navigating pressures of friendship, sexual awakening, and consent during a rites-of-passage trip.[2][9] McKenna-Bruce, born March 2, 2001, previously appeared in roles such as in the 2022 Netflix adaptation of Persuasion and as Tee Taylor in the BBC's The Dumping Ground series from 2013 to 2021, marking this as her breakout lead in a feature film.[13][14] Lara Peake portrays Skye, Tara's outgoing and confident best friend who initiates much of the group's partying and hookups.[2][15] Peake, known for supporting roles in films like The Capture (2019) and Amulet (2020), brings authenticity to the character's bold energy in this debut feature collaboration with director Molly Manning Walker.[13][16] Enva Lewis plays Em, the third friend in the group, characterized by her quirky humor and loyalty amid the escalating tensions of the vacation.[2][15] Lewis, a newcomer to major features, had appeared in shorts prior to this role, which contributed to the film's focus on raw, non-professional-like performances from the young ensemble.[13][17] Supporting the core trio, Shaun Thomas appears as Badger, a local party promoter facilitating the group's club experiences, while Samuel Bottomley plays Paddy, a British tourist whose interactions with Tara drive key plot developments.[2][18] Thomas, with prior credits including The Commuter (2018) and Boiling Point (2021), and Bottomley, recognized from The Outlaws (2021) and Glue (2014), provide established foils to the leads' youthful dynamics.[15][19]

Supporting Roles

Shaun Thomas portrays Badger, a boisterous partygoer and one of the British lads the protagonists befriend in Malia, who becomes Tara's initial romantic interest amid the group's holiday escapades.[13][19] Samuel Bottomley plays Paddy, Badger's outgoing friend within the same group, whose interactions with Tara escalate into a pivotal incident central to the film's exploration of consent and peer dynamics.[19] Laura Ambler appears as Paige, contributing to the ensemble of young holidaymakers surrounding the main trio.[19] Additional supporting performers include Eilidh Loan as Fi, Finlay Vane Last as Josh, and Daisy Jelley as Gemma, depicting peers in the chaotic social environment of the resort.[19] Smaller roles are filled by actors such as Anna Antoniades as the airport announcer and female club representative, Elliot Warren as the male club rep, and Eleni Sachini as the hotel receptionist, providing contextual backdrop to the protagonists' experiences.[19] Olivia Brady voices Tara's mother in off-screen communications.[19]

Production

Development and Pre-production

How to Have Sex originated from writer-director Molly Manning Walker's personal reflections on Mediterranean party holidays, conceived shortly after attending a wedding and following the Cannes Critics' Week selection of her short film Good Thanks, You? in 2020.[20] Walker, who trained as a cinematographer at the National Film and Television School and had lensed projects including the HBO Max series Mood (2021) and the film Scrapper (which premiered at Sundance in 2023), drew from her own experiences of sexual assault to inform the narrative's exploration of consent and adolescent sexuality.[21][22] The script began as a treatment submitted to Film4 before expanding into a 58-page screenplay, refined through focus groups conducted with young participants in UK cities such as Manchester, Nottingham, and London to capture contemporary attitudes toward sex, friendship, and pressure.[20] Development was backed by Film4 and the British Film Institute (BFI), which financed the project alongside co-producers mk2 Films, with Wild Swim (led by Ivana MacKinnon and Emily Leo) and Heretic (Konstantinos Kontovrakis) handling production.[20][23] mk2 Films formally boarded the project on October 21, 2022, ahead of principal photography.[23] Walker participated in the 7th session of the Cannes Next Step workshop during pre-production to further hone the film.[20] Pre-production efforts included early casting calls and location scouting, with the setting shifted from the Spanish resort of Magaluf to Malia, Greece, to better suit the story's dynamics of British youth tourism and club culture.[20] These preparations culminated in filming scheduled for September 2022, emphasizing a naturalistic approach to capture the sensory intensity of the characters' experiences.[20]

Filming

Principal photography for How to Have Sex took place primarily on location in Malia, a coastal resort town on the Greek island of Crete, selected to authentically capture the film's setting of a raucous Mediterranean party destination frequented by British teenagers.[9][24] Filming commenced in September 2021 and concluded approximately two months later in November 2021, coinciding with the end of the tourist season to facilitate access to venues while minimizing disruptions.[9] The production emphasized immersion and realism, with the cast residing on the Malia strip for several weeks prior to and during principal photography to absorb the environment's chaotic energy.[25] Cinematographer Nicolas Canniccioni conducted two pre-production scouting trips to observe local behaviors and secure sites, adapting techniques to leverage available lighting and architecture, such as using on-site nightclub strobes and natural midday sun for party and beach sequences.[24] The shoot began with two weeks of intensive night exteriors for club and street party scenes, employing over 2,000 extras to replicate crowded, disorienting atmospheres; actors were encouraged to improvise and physically emulate intoxication through spinning and fluid movement.[25] Technical choices prioritized handheld mobility and documentary-style intimacy, utilizing an Arri Mini LF digital camera paired with Canon K35 prime lenses for close-ups and zooms for tension-building pans, while minimizing artificial setups to preserve the location's raw authenticity.[24] Challenges included navigating the balance between scripted fiction and the unpredictable real-world strip activity, as well as relocating a key nighttime beach scene after the original site became unavailable, requiring on-the-fly adjustments to maintain narrative flow.[24] Rehearsals incorporated handheld camera interviews to build character depth, fostering a supportive environment for the young cast during vulnerable intimate sequences.[25]

Post-production

Post-production for How to Have Sex was overseen by supervisor Verity Wislocki, ensuring the film's completion ahead of its premiere at the 2023 Cannes Film Festival.[26] The process emphasized refining the naturalistic footage captured on location in Malia, Crete, with a focus on visual and auditory elements to capture the chaotic energy of adolescent holiday experiences.[27] A key aspect of editing involved director Molly Manning Walker reviewing the 90-minute assembly without sound, following advice from her mother, editor Lesley Manning, to assess whether the frames alone conveyed the story's emotional and narrative intent.[28] This approach prioritized the power of imagery in depicting themes of consent and peer pressure amid party scenes. Finishing editing was handled by Stuart London.[29] Color grading, conducted by Joseph Bicknell at Company 3, enhanced the film's sun-drenched, vibrant palette to reflect the deceptive allure of the setting while underscoring tonal shifts toward unease.[29] Manning Walker noted that post-production for visual design, sound, music, and grading was structured in two distinct halves, allowing iterative refinement of the film's immersive quality.[30] Sound design was integral, amplifying ambient club noise and subtle emotional cues to heighten the audience's sense of disorientation and isolation for protagonist Tara.[31] No significant visual effects were employed, aligning with the film's documentary-like realism derived from handheld cinematography.[32]

Release

Premiere and Festival Run

The film had its world premiere at the 76th Cannes Film Festival on May 19, 2023, in the Un Certain Regard section.[1] Directed by Molly Manning Walker in her feature debut, How to Have Sex received immediate acclaim for its raw depiction of teenage holiday experiences, emerging as a breakout entry in the sidebar competition.[33] On May 26, 2023, the film won the Un Certain Regard Prize, the section's top award, selected by a jury presided over by French filmmaker Audrey Diwan.[34] [35] This victory highlighted the film's exploration of consent and youthful hedonism, with jury members praising its "vibrant and authentic" approach to complex social dynamics.[36] Following Cannes, How to Have Sex continued its festival circuit at the 2023 Toronto International Film Festival in the Discovery program, where it screened to further buzz among industry attendees.[37] Additional screenings included the Calgary International Film Festival on September 25, 2023, and the Melbourne International Film Festival later that year, solidifying its presence in international showcases.[38] [39] The film also competed at the Zagreb Film Festival in November 2023, winning the top prize in the international competition, which underscored its ongoing recognition for technical and thematic innovation.[40]

Theatrical and Streaming Distribution

Mubi acquired distribution rights to How to Have Sex following its premiere at the 2023 Cannes Film Festival, handling theatrical releases in multiple territories including the United Kingdom and the United States.[41] In the UK, the film opened in cinemas on November 3, 2023, after a premiere at the BFI London Film Festival on October 10, 2023.[42] The US theatrical rollout began with a limited release on February 2, 2024, primarily in select arthouse theaters.[43] International theatrical distribution varied by region; for instance, in Greece, rights were acquired by Cinobo for both cinema and platform release in October 2023.[44] Following its limited theatrical runs, the film transitioned to streaming platforms. It became available on Mubi's subscription service in the US starting January 3, 2024, aligning with the distributor's strategy to integrate theatrical and on-demand access.[45] By mid-2024, it expanded to Netflix in various markets, including options for standard and ad-supported tiers.[46] [47] Additional availability includes rental and purchase on platforms like Amazon Prime Video.[48] As of 2025, streaming options remain active on Mubi and Netflix in key regions, reflecting the film's appeal to on-demand audiences post-festival circuit.[47]

Commercial Performance

Box Office Results

How to Have Sex earned a worldwide box office total of $1,231,316, with $177,380 from the domestic (North American) market and $1,053,936 from international territories.[49] The film, distributed by MUBI, received a limited theatrical release in the United States starting on February 2, 2024, accumulating $167,725 over its run. In the United Kingdom, where it opened on November 3, 2023, across 150 sites, the film grossed £493,674 (approximately $628,000 at contemporaneous exchange rates), including an opening weekend of £101,630.[50] This performance positioned it as a modest success for an independent art-house title, bolstered by critical acclaim from its Cannes premiere but constrained by niche appeal and limited marketing reach compared to mainstream releases.[49] International earnings were driven primarily by European markets, with additional contributions from releases in countries including Germany and Croatia, though specific territorial breakdowns beyond the UK remain partial in available data.[49] The film's box office reflects the challenges faced by festival darlings in achieving broad commercial viability, prioritizing prestige over high grosses.[49]

Reception

Critical Response

The film How to Have Sex garnered strong critical acclaim upon release, earning a 97% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from 108 reviews, with critics consensus highlighting its authentic capture of female teenage experiences and the power of director Molly Manning Walker's debut.[51] On Metacritic, it holds a score of 79 out of 100 based on 37 reviews, indicating "generally favorable" reception.[52] Reviewers frequently commended the film's unflinching examination of adolescent sexuality, peer pressure, and consent within the context of a raucous holiday party scene, praising its raw emotional authenticity over didactic messaging. Mia McKenna-Bruce's performance as the protagonist Tara received particular acclaim, with critics such as those from Roger Ebert awarding the film four out of four stars for masterfully conveying the "drive and apprehension of sexual coming of age" through her nuanced portrayal of vulnerability amid hedonism.[4] The Guardian described it as a "searing study of consent," noting the emergence of "two stars" in McKenna-Bruce and co-star Lara McDonnell, while emphasizing Manning Walker's kinetic visual style that immerses viewers in the chaotic energy of youth without romanticizing it.[53] Similarly, Mashable lauded its "brutally honest" depiction of early sexual encounters, arguing it avoids tutorial-like moralizing in favor of visceral realism drawn from the director's own experiences.[6] Some critics offered tempered praise, acknowledging the film's strengths in evoking the highs and lows of hookup culture but critiquing occasional ambiguity in character motivations or resolution. For instance, Punch Drunk Critics rated it 3 out of 5, appreciating the "raw and real" coming-of-age drama but suggesting it could more sharply interrogate how societal expectations distort personal agency.[54] Despite such notes, dissenting voices were rare among professional reviewers, with the consensus affirming the film's role as an essential, if harrowing, rites-of-passage narrative that prioritizes lived consequences over escapism.[52]

Audience and Public Reaction

The film elicited mixed responses from audiences, reflected in its 6.4/10 average rating on IMDb from over 22,000 user votes.[2] Similarly, the audience score on Rotten Tomatoes stood at 59%, indicating a divide between those who valued its unflinching realism and others who found its portrayal of adolescent excess and trauma unrelenting or off-putting.[55] Viewers often noted the deceptive title, which suggested a lighthearted guide but delivered a stark examination of hookup culture's perils, leading some to describe it as a "curveball" far removed from typical teen comedies.[56] Public discourse centered on the film's depiction of consent and sexual assault, with many praising its role in fostering candid discussions about peer pressure and bystander complicity among youth.[57] Screenings reportedly drew highly engaged crowds, including audible reactions during dramatic scenes, underscoring its emotional impact as "devastating and essential" for confronting real-world vulnerabilities in party environments.[58] However, segments of the public expressed discomfort, with reports of viewers feeling "grubby" after exposure to the raw, unromanticized consequences of binge drinking and casual encounters, highlighting tensions between the film's intent to educate and its visceral intensity.[59]

Awards and Accolades

How to Have Sex won the Un Certain Regard Prize, the top award in Cannes Film Festival's Un Certain Regard section, on May 26, 2023, recognizing director Molly Manning Walker's debut feature for its innovative depiction of adolescent experiences.[35] At the 2023 British Independent Film Awards, held on December 3, 2023, the film secured victory in the Best Lead Performance category for Mia McKenna-Bruce's portrayal of Tara, amid 13 total nominations including Best British Independent Film and Best Director for Manning Walker.[60][61] The film earned three nominations at the 2024 BAFTA Film Awards, announced January 18, 2024, including Outstanding British Film for the production team comprising Manning Walker, Emily Leo, and Ivana MacKinnon.[62] It also received the European Discovery – Prix FIPRESCI at the 2023 European Film Awards, highlighting its emergence as a notable new work in European cinema.[63]

Themes and Analysis

Depiction of Youth Party Culture

The film portrays youth party culture through the lens of a post-exam holiday in Malia, Crete, a Mediterranean resort known for attracting large groups of British teenagers seeking unsupervised hedonism. Set in self-catering hotels teeming with students, the narrative opens with the protagonists—three 16-year-old girls, Tara, Skye, and Em—arriving amid boozy poolside antics and blagging access to accommodations, establishing an atmosphere of youthful exuberance fueled by cheap alcohol and peer camaraderie.[10][64] Visuals emphasize neon-drenched nights on the main strip, with flashing club lights, glowsticks, and relentless electronic dance music (EDM) soundtracks accompanying scenes of staggering revelry and giggly escapades like night swims.[10] Clubbing forms the core of the depiction, orchestrated by "robo-cheery" resort reps who guide groups into 24-hour bacchanals of drinking games, flirting, and casual pairings. Heavy alcohol consumption is central, with unlimited drink deals enabling rapid intoxication, leading to hungover mornings amid garish, deserted daytime streets that evoke a post-apocalyptic hangover haze.[64][65] Social dynamics highlight group bonding through dares and "I have never" style challenges, as the girls interact with male peers like Badger and Paddy in adjacent hotel rooms, blending friendly hedonism with pressures to pursue sexual encounters amid the frenzy.[10][64] Director Molly Manning Walker draws from personal experiences in similar resorts like Magaluf and Crete, capturing the nonstop cycle of clubbing, public displays of sexuality (such as observed stage performances), and experimental pursuits that mix joy with discomfort, without idealizing the risks of inebriation-driven decisions.[65] The resort's vibe is rendered as a budget approximation of Ibiza delirium, saturated with suntan lotion and flavored gloss, underscoring a culture where youthful freedom manifests in reckless, boundary-testing behaviors among tourists detached from everyday oversight.[10][65]

Consent, Sexual Assault, and Personal Responsibility

In the film, consent is depicted as an ongoing process that can be explicitly withdrawn at any point, even during physical intimacy, with protagonist Tara's encounter with Badger illustrating this when she verbally signals discomfort mid-act but is ignored, constituting assault amid heavy alcohol consumption.[66][67] The narrative underscores peer pressure and intoxication as factors blurring boundaries, as Tara's initial enthusiasm wanes under the influence, highlighting how alcohol impairs judgment and communication in party environments like the Malia resort setting.[6][11] Sexual assault in the story arises within a culture of binge drinking and casual hookups, where characters voluntarily engage in excessive alcohol intake—often shots and all-night partying—leading to vulnerability; empirically, alcohol is involved in at least 50% of sexual assaults among college-aged individuals, with both perpetrators and victims consuming it in roughly half of cases, impairing the capacity for clear consent.[68][69] The film avoids overt moralizing but implicitly critiques the normalization of such risks, as Tara's friends prioritize fun over caution, reflecting broader youth party dynamics where 75% of campus assaults occur under alcohol's influence.[70][6] Personal responsibility emerges through the characters' agency in choosing environments and behaviors that heighten exposure to assault; Tara's decisions to drink to blackout levels and isolate with strangers exemplify causal links between self-imposed impairment and adverse outcomes, as intoxication reduces one's ability to revoke or assert consent effectively.[71][72] While the film emphasizes perpetrator accountability—Badger's persistence despite signals—it also portrays the consequences of diminished personal vigilance, aligning with data showing that avoiding heavy intoxication mitigates assault risks, though media analyses often prioritize systemic factors over individual choices to evade perceptions of blame.[73][68] This depiction challenges viewers to consider proactive measures, such as maintaining sobriety and boundary-setting, as essential complements to consent education in high-risk settings.[67][69]

Broader Social Commentary

The film How to Have Sex portrays the hedonistic party culture of youth resorts like Malia, Crete, as a microcosm of broader societal commodification of leisure and sexuality, where economic incentives for 18-30 holidays foster environments of relentless excess, heavy alcohol consumption, and transactional encounters that prioritize immediate gratification over long-term well-being.[74] This depiction aligns with critiques of hookup culture as a competitive "sexual marketplace" amplified by dating apps like Tinder, in which individuals' social value becomes tied to sexual experience, pressuring inexperienced youth—particularly young women—to participate despite potential emotional costs.[74] Such dynamics, as observed in the film's characters, illustrate how constant stimulation delays reflection, normalizing blurred boundaries around consent in intoxicated settings and contributing to a culture that anesthetizes participants to exploitation.[74] Director Molly Manning Walker has stated that the film interrogates how society imparts knowledge of sex, revealing persistent gaps in consent education that leave youth, especially girls, vulnerable to "grey areas" in drunken scenarios where prior flirtation is misinterpreted as ongoing agreement.[75] This reflects wider societal contradictions, including mixed messaging from sex-positivity campaigns that frame casual encounters as inconsequential alongside consent-focused warnings that portray them as inherently risky, fostering uncertainty and performative detachment akin to pornographic ideals rather than mutual intimacy.[76] Empirical research supports the film's emphasis on post-encounter regret, particularly among women, with studies indicating that 46% of women experience regret after casual sex compared to 23% of men, often linked to emotional dissatisfaction and misalignment with relational preferences.[77] Gender differences in hookup regret, where women report higher rates tied to factors like low emotional connection and perceived partner quality, suggest underlying biological and psychological asymmetries that hookup norms overlook, potentially exacerbating mental health issues such as anxiety and depression in young adults.[78][79] The narrative's treatment of virginity as a burdensome milestone to shed urgently critiques a cultural script that equates sexual debut with maturity, yet underscores the causal role of peer pressure and alcohol in outcomes like assault, where personal agency is compromised without negating individual responsibility in high-risk choices.[76] Walker's work, informed by her own experiences of clubbing holidays and assault, has prompted discussions on the failures of progressive sex education to address these realities, as evidenced by unprogressive attitudes among UK teens—such as victim-blaming based on attire—that persist despite institutional efforts.[75] While mainstream interpretations often frame the film through a lens of systemic victimhood, its unflinching realism highlights causal chains from societal liberalization to individual harms, including delayed emotional processing and fractured friendships, urging reevaluation of norms that prioritize hedonistic freedom over protective boundaries.[74][76]

Criticisms and Controversies

Portrayal of Hookup Culture and Consequences

Critics have faulted the film's initial depiction of hookup culture as overly euphoric and normalized, presenting relentless partying, alcohol-fueled hookups, and peer pressure among 16-year-old British girls in Crete as a rite of passage, which delays confrontation with inherent risks until a pivotal assault scene.[80] This setup, involving unchaperoned minors in a hedonistic resort environment, has been described as contrived and manipulative, potentially understating real-world vulnerabilities like impaired judgment from binge drinking, which empirical studies link to heightened sexual regret and emotional distress in casual encounters.[80] [81] The portrayal emphasizes social valuation through sexual conquests, framing virginity loss as a competitive milestone in a "sexual marketplace" where women's worth ties to participation, yet it critiques this through the protagonist Tara's non-consensual experience and ensuing trauma, including self-blame and isolation.[74] However, reviewers note the film's observational restraint avoids explicit moral judgment, leading to accusations of ambiguity that normalizes recklessness among peers who dismiss violations as "successes," mirroring documented patterns where hookup participants, particularly women, report psychological costs like anxiety and depression without adequate narrative resolution.[74] [82] Broader critiques highlight the film's narrow focus on consent failures and immediate aftermath—such as Tara's PTSD-like symptoms—while sidelining systemic consequences of hookup culture, including elevated STI transmission risks and long-term relational dissatisfaction disproportionately affecting women due to evolutionary mismatches in attachment.[80] [83] Studies indicate casual sex correlates with lower self-esteem and higher stress for female participants, outcomes the film gestures toward but does not substantiate with causal depth, potentially reflecting directorial intent to prioritize empathy over didacticism.[84] [74] This approach has drawn fire for framing harms primarily as external predation rather than intrinsic to permissive environments, where alcohol and group dynamics erode personal agency.[81]

Ideological Debates

The film's ambiguous depiction of consent in intoxicated encounters has fueled debates over whether it reinforces a progressive emphasis on affirmative consent models or exposes the limitations of such frameworks in real-world party settings. Director Molly Manning Walker has argued that traditional sex education fails adolescents by focusing on mechanics rather than emotional navigation and contextual pressures, drawing from her own experiences to illustrate how alcohol-fueled ambiguity can lead to regret and trauma without clear villainy.[85] [86] Critics aligned with feminist perspectives, such as those in Jezebel and Glamour, praise the film for centering female agency and bystander complicity—particularly male friends who enable assault—while urging systemic cultural shifts to prioritize explicit verbal agreement, viewing the narrative as a subtle indictment of patriarchal norms in hookup environments.[87] [88] However, this interpretation has drawn counterarguments from reviewers who contend that the film's restraint avoids preaching affirmative consent as a panacea, instead highlighting personal choices in high-risk behaviors like excessive drinking and casual partnering, which some conservative-leaning outlets see as validating longstanding cautions against hedonistic youth culture without excusing individual accountability.[89] [90] Debates also extend to the portrayal of hookup culture's ideological underpinnings, with progressive commentators lauding the "female gaze" for demystifying adolescent sexual exploration and critiquing its commodification, as Walker intended to reclaim narratives from male-dominated perspectives.[85] [65] In contrast, detractors, including some independent critics, argue that the initial exuberance of the party scenes inadvertently glamorizes the very excesses—such as relentless pursuit of casual sex as a rite of passage—that culminate in harm, potentially downplaying causal links between cultural normalization of binge drinking and vulnerability to exploitation, a view echoed in broader discussions of post-#MeToo media's tension between empowerment and realism.[91] [92] Sources from mainstream outlets like The Guardian, often critiqued for left-leaning biases in cultural analysis, frame the film as a progressive call for better education on relational dynamics, yet this overlooks empirical data on alcohol's role in 50% of sexual assaults among college-aged individuals, per U.S. National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism studies, suggesting the narrative prioritizes experiential nuance over preventive causality.[65] Broader ideological contention arises over the film's avoidance of explicit moralizing, which some interpret as a rejection of "woke" didacticism in favor of unvarnished truth about youth autonomy's perils. Walker has positioned the story as autobiographical caution rather than advocacy for sexual liberation, emphasizing how peer pressure and inexperience amplify risks in environments like Malia, Greece, where British teens annually report high rates of alcohol-related incidents.[65] [93] Conservative reviewers appreciate this subtlety as an implicit critique of permissive ideologies that downplay consequences, aligning with data from the U.K.'s Office for National Statistics showing rising regret among young women in casual encounters, while progressive voices risk overemphasizing victim narratives at the expense of agency, as evidenced by the film's Cannes Un Certain Regard win on May 27, 2023, amid acclaim for its non-preachy humanism.[90] This divide underscores tensions between causal realism—prioritizing behavioral antecedents like intoxication—and institutional biases in academia and media that favor structural explanations over individual decision-making.

Factual and Ethical Critiques

Critics contend that the film's portrayal of casual, alcohol-involved sexual encounters glosses over well-documented empirical risks, presenting them as primarily social rites of passage rather than high-stakes behaviors with measurable health consequences.[80] Research consistently links alcohol use to elevated sexual risk-taking, including reduced condom use and heightened STI transmission rates; for example, a meta-analysis of studies found that intoxicated individuals are more likely to engage in unprotected intercourse due to impaired decision-making.[94] Similarly, patterns of heavy drinking among young adults correlate with multiple casual partners and subsequent infections, with one study of adolescents reporting that alcohol-involved sex triples the odds of regret and unintended pregnancy compared to sober encounters.[95] The depiction of hookup culture as a normative, consequence-minimal adventure also diverges from data on long-term outcomes, where uncommitted sex—especially when paired with intoxication—associates with increased emotional distress, including depression, anxiety, and shame.[96] Longitudinal analyses reveal that frequent casual encounters in early adulthood predict lower relationship satisfaction and higher rates of mental health disorders later, effects attributed to disrupted pair-bonding mechanisms and oxytocin-mediated attachment responses disrupted by repeated novelty-seeking.[97] While the film highlights a single assault, it underemphasizes how alcohol routinely blurs consent boundaries, with empirical evidence showing that 50-70% of campus sexual assaults involve perpetrator intoxication, impairing victims' agency and recall.[98] Ethically, the narrative's sympathetic framing of virginity as an burdensome "albatross" to shed through partying has drawn rebuke for reinforcing cultural pressures that prioritize experiential milestones over precautionary realism, potentially misleading viewers on the intrinsic asymmetries of sexual risks—such as women's disproportionate vulnerability to physical harm, pregnancy, and reputational fallout.[76] This approach, while lauded in progressive film circles for its "authenticity," sidesteps causal accountability, as the film's party-centric resolution implies resilience without addressing how such environments systematically erode personal agency via disinhibition and peer conformity.[64] Broader ethical concerns arise from the film's acclaim amid evidence that hookup normalization correlates with societal rises in youth STI epidemics (e.g., a 2023 CDC report noting 2.5 million cases among 15-24-year-olds, many tied to casual encounters) and mental health crises, raising questions about media's role in amplifying behaviors with net negative utility.[99] Mainstream critiques often overlook these angles, reflecting institutional tendencies to prioritize affirmative depictions of sexual liberation over data-driven warnings.[76]
