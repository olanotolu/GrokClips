2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup qualification (Europe) – Qualifiers

The 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup qualification (Europe) – Qualifiers constituted the primary selection tournament for European national teams seeking 12 slots in the FIBA Basketball World Cup, held across six international windows from November 2021 to February 2023.[1] In the first round, 32 teams competed in eight groups of four, each playing a double round-robin schedule of six matches, with the top three finishers from each group advancing their results to the second round's four groups of six teams, where another double round-robin determined the qualifiers via the top three positions per group.[1] This format emphasized endurance and consistency, as teams played up to 10 games total, factoring in home-court dynamics and cross-window scheduling amid club commitments.[2] Established powers like Spain, France, Serbia, Slovenia, and Lithuania dominated their respective second-round groups (I, J, K, and L) to secure advancement, leveraging superior depth and experience in high-stakes matches.[2] Notable achievements included Germany's early clinching of qualification through unflinching group leadership and Italy's resurgence under veteran leadership to edge out competitors in Group L.[3] Upsets defined the campaign, with debutants Finland and Latvia punching above their weight—Finland topping Group K with a perfect early record and Latvia surging in Group I via clutch victories, such as against Turkey—highlighting the qualifiers' meritocratic intensity over seeding predictions.[3] Greece and Bosnia and Herzegovina also advanced amid tight races, underscoring tactical adaptability in a field blending EuroBasket standouts with pre-qualifier climbers.[4] The process yielded a balanced field for the World Cup, where European squads ultimately contended for medals, with Germany claiming bronze as the host nation's proxy success.[5]

Background and Eligibility

Exclusion of Russia and Belarus

On March 1, 2022, FIBA suspended the Russian Basketball Federation and all Russian teams and officials from participating in FIBA basketball and 3x3 basketball competitions until further notice, citing the ongoing military conflict in Ukraine initiated by Russia's invasion on February 24, 2022.[6] Belarus was similarly suspended on the same date due to its political and military alignment with Russia in the conflict.[7] These suspensions effectively barred both nations' national teams from the European qualifying process for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup. On May 17, 2022, FIBA's Central Board formalized the exclusion by withdrawing Russia from Group H and Belarus from Group B of the European qualifiers, with no replacement teams assigned to those groups.[8] This decision followed initial suspensions and aligned with broader international sports sanctions against the two countries, ensuring their absence from the first and second rounds of qualification without altering the overall number of spots available for other European teams.[9] The exclusions were part of FIBA's response to security concerns and geopolitical pressures, though the organization emphasized that the measures were temporary pending resolution of the conflict.[10] The suspensions were extended indefinitely by FIBA's Executive Committee in November 2023, maintaining the exclusion of Russian and Belarusian teams from all international competitions, including any retroactive considerations for the 2023 World Cup cycle.[7] Prior to the invasion, both nations had been drawn into qualifying groups based on FIBA rankings, with Russia in Group H alongside teams like Bosnia and Herzegovina and Finland, and Belarus in Group B with the Netherlands and Israel; their removal left those groups proceeding with reduced rosters.[8]

Competition Format

First Round Structure

The First Round involved 32 teams: the 24 nations that had qualified for the 2022 FIBA EuroBasket and the eight teams that advanced from the Pre-Qualifiers.[1] These teams were drawn into eight groups of four, designated as Groups A through H.[1] Within each group, teams competed in a double round-robin format, facing every other team twice—once at home and once away—for a total of six matches per team.[1] Matches were contested across three FIBA international windows: 22–30 November 2021, 24 February – 2 March 2022, and 23 June – 3 July 2022.[2] Standings were determined primarily by points earned (two for a win, one for a loss in overtime, zero otherwise), with tiebreakers applied as needed based on head-to-head results, point difference, and points scored.[1] The top three teams from each group advanced to the Second Round, yielding 24 progressing teams while eliminating the eight lowest-ranked sides.[1] This structure ensured broad participation while progressively narrowing the field through competitive group play.[1]

Second Round Structure

The second round of the European qualifiers featured the 24 teams that advanced as the top three from each of the eight first-round groups, divided into four new groups of six teams each by pairing adjacent first-round groups (Groups A and B forming Group I, Groups C and D forming Group J, Groups E and F forming Group K, and Groups G and H forming Group L).[1] Results from first-round matches played among the advancing teams from the same original group were carried over to the second-round group standings, while no prior results existed between teams from paired groups.[1][11] In each second-round group, teams competed in a home-and-away round-robin format against the three teams from the paired first-round group, adding six matches per team (three home, three away) across the remaining qualification windows from November 2021 to February 2023.[1] The top three teams from each of the four second-round groups qualified directly for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, securing 12 spots for European teams.[1] Due to the exclusion of Russia following its invasion of Ukraine, Group H effectively advanced only two teams alongside the top three from Group G to form a group of five, with analogous adjustments applied where necessary to maintain competitive balance without altering the overall top-three qualification rule.[11]

Qualification Criteria and Tiebreakers

The qualification for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup from the European qualifiers was determined in the second round, where the top three teams from each of four groups of six advanced to the tournament.[12] The 24 teams entering the second round—comprising the top three from each of the eight first-round groups of four—were paired such that one first-round group of four merged with the top two from another to form each second-round group, with results from intra-group first-round games carried forward.[13] Teams played a double round-robin schedule against the other five in their group, yielding 10 games per team overall in the second round (including carried-over results). This format secured 12 spots for Europe via the qualifiers, in addition to direct qualifications from other paths such as FIBA rankings and continental championships.[3] Tiebreakers for group standings followed FIBA's standard procedures for preliminary rounds. For two teams tied on win-loss record: (1) head-to-head game result; (2) point differential across all group games; (3) total points scored in all group games; (4) current FIBA men's world ranking. For three or more tied teams: (1) win-loss record in head-to-head games among the tied teams; (2) point differential in those head-to-head games; (3) points scored in those head-to-head games; (4) point differential across all group games; (5) total points scored in all group games; (6) FIBA men's world ranking. These criteria ensured rankings reflected competitive performance and were applied sequentially until ties were resolved.[14]

Participating Teams

Teams Advancing from Pre-Qualifiers

The European pre-qualifiers for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup featured 28 teams not qualified for EuroBasket 2022, divided into six groups of four in the first round during June and July 2021, with the top two teams from each advancing to three groups of four in the second round held in August 2021.[15] The winners of each second-round group secured advancement to the main European qualifiers, joining the 24 EuroBasket 2022 participants to form 32 teams overall.[15] The eight teams that advanced were Belarus, Iceland, Latvia, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Portugal, Slovakia, and Sweden.[15] Belarus topped Group E with a 5–1 record, defeating opponents including Georgia and Romania. Iceland won Group F undefeated at 6–0, overcoming Denmark and Portugal in key matches. Latvia claimed Group G with victories over Estonia and Luxembourg. Montenegro led Group H, Slovakia secured Group I on the final day by edging out Bosnia and Herzegovina, and North Macedonia clinched Group J against Kosovo.[15] Portugal and Sweden also advanced as group winners, with the full field confirmed on August 18, 2021, following the second round's conclusion.[15]

Seeding and Pots

The seeding for the first round of the European qualifiers was based on the FIBA World Rankings for Men presented on August 20, 2021, which positioned teams according to their recent competitive performance and results in international competitions.[16] The 32 teams—comprising the 24 that qualified via EuroBasket 2022 and the 8 that advanced from the pre-qualifiers—were allocated into eight pots of four teams each, with Pot 1 containing the highest-ranked teams and Pot 8 the lowest.[17] This structure aimed to distribute competitive balance across groups by preventing multiple high-seeded teams from being drawn together. Draw

Draw Procedure

The draw for the first round groups of the European qualifiers was held on 31 August 2021 at the Patrick Baumann House of Basketball in Mies, Switzerland, commencing at 12:00 CET and conducted behind closed doors due to the ongoing health pandemic, with live streaming on FIBA's YouTube and Facebook channels.[16][18] The 32 teams—comprising the 24 nations that qualified for EuroBasket 2022 plus the eight that advanced from the pre-qualifiers—were seeded into eight pots of four teams each based solely on their FIBA World Ranking for Men presented by Nike as of the draw date, with no geographical restrictions applied.[17][16] Pot 1 contained the highest-ranked teams (Spain, Slovenia, France, Serbia), Pot 2 the next tier (Italy, Lithuania, Greece, Germany), Pot 3 (Czech Republic, Poland, Russia, Turkey), and so on through Pot 8 with the lowest-ranked entrants.[17] To balance group strengths across the eight groups of four teams each, the procedure divided the pots into two sets for allocation: teams from Pots 1, 4, 5, and 8 were drawn and assigned to the four odd-numbered groups (A, C, E, G), ensuring each such group received one team from Pot 1 as the top seed alongside lower-seeded teams; teams from Pots 2, 3, 6, and 7 were similarly drawn into the even-numbered groups (B, D, F, H), with Pot 2 providing the leading team per group.[16][17] Within each pot and group set, teams were drawn in sequence to determine their specific group placement and seeding position, preventing early matchups among the highest-ranked teams while distributing overall competitiveness.[16]

Seeding Criteria

The seeding for the European qualifiers draw was determined by dividing the 32 participating teams into eight pots of four teams each, based solely on their positions in the FIBA World Ranking for Men presented by Nike.[16][18] The rankings applied were those effective as of the seedings announcement on 30 August 2021, immediately prior to the draw ceremony held the following day.[17] This approach ensured an even distribution of team strengths across the eight groups, with Pot 1 comprising the four highest-ranked European teams (Spain, Slovenia, France, and Serbia) and lower pots containing progressively lower-ranked nations.[16][17] No additional factors, such as recent tournament results or continental protections beyond ranking order, were incorporated into the pot assignments.[16]

First Round

Group A

Group A featured the national teams of Belgium, Latvia, Serbia, and Slovakia, drawn together following the seeding procedure based on FIBA rankings after EuroBasket 2022 qualifiers.[2] The competition unfolded in a double round-robin format across two FIBA windows: 25–29 November 2021 and 24–28 February 2022, with each team contesting three home and three away games.[2] Latvia clinched first place with a 5–1 record, highlighted by strong defensive performances and key victories, including over Belgium and Slovakia.[19] Serbia secured second position at 4–2, propelled by dominant wins such as 102–95 against Latvia and 101–76 over Belgium.[20] [19] Belgium earned third place with a 2–4 tally, advancing via upsets like an 87–76 home win over Latvia, but struggled in losses to Serbia and others.[19] Slovakia ended fourth at 1–5, with their sole victory against Latvia, but were outmatched in most encounters, including an 83–57 defeat to Belgium.[19] The top three teams—Latvia, Serbia, and Belgium—advanced to the second round, where results contributed to overall qualification standings. Slovakia was eliminated.[2]

Group B

Group B originally comprised Greece, Turkey, Great Britain, and Belarus, drawn based on FIBA rankings and EuroBasket 2022 qualification status.[2] Belarus was suspended by FIBA on 1 March 2022 amid the Russia-Ukraine conflict, with their prior results annulled and no further participation allowed. Group C

Group C featured Croatia, Finland, Slovenia, and Sweden, drawn together following the seeding procedure after the pre-qualifiers.[32] The teams competed in a double round-robin format across FIBA international windows from 25 November 2021 to 28 February 2023, with each playing six matches (home and away against the other three opponents). The top two teams advanced to the second round, where results carried over. Finland led the group with a strong performance, winning five of six games, including narrow victories over Croatia (77–71 and 81–79).[19] Their only loss came against Slovenia. Slovenia secured second place with four wins, highlighted by road triumphs over Sweden (84–81) and a tight win against Croatia (76–74 away).[33][34] Sweden managed two victories, primarily against Croatia, but struggled overall. Group D

Group D featured Germany, Poland, Israel, and Estonia, drawn from pots based on FIBA rankings following EuroBasket 2022 performance and pre-qualifier results.[36] The teams competed in a double round-robin format across six FIBA windows from November 2021 to February 2023, with each team playing six matches (three home, three away).[2] Germany dominated the group, securing a perfect 6–0 record and advancing to the second round as hosts and group winners. Poland placed second with a 4–2 record, also progressing to the second round. Israel finished third (2–4), while Estonia ended last (0–6) and was eliminated.[2] Notable results included Estonia's upset 69–66 win over Germany in one match, though Germany won the return fixture decisively, and Israel's 96–77 home victory against Estonia.[19]

Group E

Group E consisted of the national teams representing France, Hungary, Montenegro, and Portugal. These teams participated in a double round-robin tournament format, playing home and away matches during FIBA's designated international windows from November 2021 through July 2022.[1][2] Key results included France's 73–67 home victory over Montenegro on 26 November 2021, followed by Montenegro's narrow 70–69 home win against France on 1 July 2022.[37][38] Hungary secured both encounters with Portugal, winning 81–75 away on 26 November 2021 and 69–68 at home on 1 July 2022.[39][40] France also defeated Hungary 78–54 away on 29 November 2021. Additional matches, such as France's 69–56 home win over Portugal on 24 February 2022 and Hungary's 88–84 home victory against Montenegro on the same date, contributed to the group dynamics.[41] France topped the standings with a strong record, losing only to Montenegro, while Montenegro finished second through consistent performances, including their upset over France.[38] Both teams advanced to the second round in Group I. Hungary and Portugal did not progress, with Portugal struggling across most fixtures.[2]

Group F consisted of Bosnia and Herzegovina, Bulgaria, the Czech Republic, and Lithuania, who competed in a double round-robin format from 25 November 2021 to 3 July 2022, playing home and away matches against each other for a total of six games per team.[2] Lithuania led the group with a 5–1 record, securing advancement to the second round after suffering their sole defeat to Bosnia and Herzegovina (78–83 on 27 February 2022).[19] Bosnia and Herzegovina finished second with a 3–3 record, including key victories over Lithuania and Bulgaria (76–73 on 3 July 2022).[42] The Czech Republic placed third with a 2–4 record, advancing on the basis of head-to-head results and point differential against Bulgaria, who ended with 1–5 and were eliminated.[2]

Group G

Group G featured Georgia, North Macedonia, Spain, and Ukraine, drawn together following the qualification procedure based on FIBA rankings and EuroBasket 2022 performance.[44] The teams contested a double round-robin tournament across two windows: 25–28 November 2021 and 24–27 February 2022, with each side playing six games. Spain, as defending FIBA World Cup champions, asserted dominance throughout, securing all six victories to finish atop the standings with 12 points and advance directly to the second round. Ukraine claimed second place with eight points (four wins, two losses), also progressing, while Georgia earned four points (two wins, four losses) and North Macedonia zero points (six losses), resulting in their elimination from qualification.[2] Key results underscored Spain's superiority, including an 89–61 rout of Georgia on 29 November 2021 in Almería, where Spain's balanced scoring overwhelmed Georgia's defense.[45] Spain also prevailed 88–74 over Ukraine on 16 February 2022 in Córdoba, leveraging strong interior play and free-throw efficiency.[46] Georgia notched a narrow 88–83 home win against Ukraine in the final group match on 27 February 2022, but it proved insufficient for advancement. North Macedonia, hampered by limited depth and poor shooting percentages, suffered heavy defeats, such as 65–91 to Georgia and consistent losses to the higher-ranked opponents.[19]

Group H

Group H originally consisted of Italy, Iceland, the Netherlands, and Russia, as determined by the draw conducted on 31 August 2021. The remaining three teams proceeded with a reduced double round-robin schedule, playing home-and-away matches against each other for a total of four games per team between November 2021 and February 2023. Points were awarded as follows: 2 for a win, 1 for a loss. The Netherlands finished last with no wins. Second Round

Group I

Group I consisted of six teams that advanced as the top three finishers from first-round Groups C (Belgium, Great Britain, and Turkey) and Group G (Greece, Latvia, and Serbia).[2] The group operated in a double round-robin format across FIBA international windows from August 2022 to February 2023, with each team playing 10 total games: results from the three first-round matches against opponents from the paired group carried over, and five new games scheduled against the other subgroup's teams.[2] The top three teams qualified directly for the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup hosted in the Philippines, Japan, and Indonesia.[2] Latvia dominated the group, losing only once and clinching first place on February 26, 2023, with a 93–85 victory over Serbia.[2] Serbia secured second place despite the final loss, while Greece edged out Turkey and Belgium for the third qualifying spot after defeating Turkey 83–76 on February 26, 2023.[2] Great Britain struggled throughout, winning just one of their 10 games.[2]

Group K

Group K comprised France, Lithuania, Montenegro, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Hungary, and the Czech Republic, drawn from the advancing teams of the first-round groups.[51] The group matches were played across three windows in August 2022, November 2022, and February 2023, with each team facing the others home and away.[51] France entered as a favorite, bolstered by emerging talent including Victor Wembanyama, while Lithuania relied on veterans like Jonas Valančiūnas and Domantas Sabonis; Montenegro, despite a smaller talent pool, competed aggressively with players such as Nikola Vučević.[52] [53] France secured qualification early, finishing atop the group despite a narrow 69–70 upset loss to Montenegro on July 1, 2022, in Podgorica, where Nemanja Radović scored 15 points for the hosts.[38] In the decisive February 2023 window, France defeated the Czech Republic behind Wembanyama's standout performance, contributing to their strong overall record. Lithuania clinched their spot with key wins, including a 90–73 victory over Montenegro on August 29, 2022, in Kaunas, led by Sabonis' double-double.[52] [53] They also overcame Hungary in the final window, building a substantial lead in the middle quarters. Montenegro edged into the top three through resilient play, including the early win over France, qualifying for their second consecutive World Cup appearance.[38] The top three teams—France, Lithuania, and Montenegro—advanced to the 2023 FIBA Basketball World Cup, joining other European qualifiers in the tournament held across the Philippines, Japan, and Indonesia. Bosnia and Herzegovina, Hungary, and the Czech Republic were eliminated, with Bosnia securing a do-or-die win in the final window but falling short overall.[3] [52]

Italy finished second with six victories, while Georgia secured third place on tiebreakers over Iceland, both at 4–4, thanks to a better head-to-head record and overall performance.[55][57]

Notable Performances and Records

In the FIBA Basketball World Cup 2023 European Qualifiers, Germany's Dennis Schröder set a new single-game scoring record with 38 points against Poland on 3 July 2022, leading to a 93-83 victory that eliminated Poland from qualification contention.[59] This performance, which included seven three-pointers and 14 points in the fourth quarter, surpassed the prior mark of 37 points established by Michael Dixon in 2017.[59] Iceland's Tryggvi Hlinason achieved the highest efficiency rating in European Qualifiers history at 50 during a 107-105 double-overtime upset win over Italy on 24 February 2022, recording 34 points, 21 rebounds, and 5 blocks.[60] The mark eclipsed the previous record of 37 shared by Edin Atić, Giorgi Shermadini, and Jānis Strēlnieks from 2019 games.[60] France's Victor Wembanyama tied Rudy Gobert's senior national team single-game rebound record with 17 boards alongside 22 points and 6 blocks in a 72-59 win over the Czech Republic during the February 2023 window.[61] At age 19, Wembanyama's line mirrored Gobert's 20 points and 17 rebounds in 30 minutes from EuroBasket 2022, underscoring his rapid emergence in international play.[61] Among overall statistical leaders, Belarus's Maksim Salash topped scoring averages at 21.5 points per game across two appearances, while Iceland's Elvar Friðriksson led in total points with 186 over nine games at 20.7 per contest.[62] These qualifiers featured multiple 30-plus point outings, with Schröder's 38 standing as the pinnacle, highlighting the competitive depth across 26 participating nations.[63]
