Al-Qadir Trust

Al-Qadir University Project Trust is a Pakistani non-profit organization established in December 2019 to develop Al-Qadir University, an institution in Sohawa, Punjab, dedicated to fostering leadership and scholarship by integrating traditional Islamic principles with contemporary academic disciplines such as management and Islamic studies.[1][2] The trust, chaired by former Prime Minister Imran Khan and with his wife Bushra Bibi as a trustee, emphasizes a curriculum grounded in Tawhid (the oneness of God) to address modern challenges through research and holistic education, including bachelor's programs in management studies and Islamic studies, alongside short courses and spiritual development initiatives.[3][4] Its global advisory board features scholars like Seyyed Hossein Nasr and Hamza Yusuf, underscoring ambitions for international Islamic intellectual contributions.[4] The trust's establishment followed Imran Khan laying the foundation stone for the university in May 2019, modeled partly on his earlier Shaukat Khanum cancer hospital project, with assets initially valued at around 660 million rupees dedicated to poverty alleviation and educational infrastructure.[2][5] However, it has been embroiled in the Al-Qadir Trust case, initiated by Pakistan's National Accountability Bureau (NAB), which alleges that during Khan's premiership, a Â£190 million settlement from UK authorities involving real estate tycoon Malik Riaz was diverted from state funds to the trust in exchange for over 450 kanals of land and construction aid donated by Riaz's Bahria Town for the university, causing purported loss to the national exchequer through abuse of authority.[6][7] Khan and Bibi were convicted in January 2025â€”receiving 14 and 7-year sentences, respectivelyâ€”though they have appealed, contending the donations were transparent philanthropy with no fiscal harm, amid claims of NAB's selective enforcement against political opponents.[8][9][2] The case highlights tensions between charitable endeavors and governance scrutiny in Pakistan, where anti-corruption probes have drawn criticism for potential institutional biases favoring ruling coalitions.[10]

Establishment

Founding and Initial Setup

The Al-Qadir University Project Trust was formally registered on December 26, 2019, through a trust deed executed in the Sub-Registrar's Office in Islamabad, with Babar Awan, a lawyer and political associate of Imran Khan, handling the registration process.[11] The entity was established primarily to facilitate the creation of Al-Qadir University in Jhelum, Punjab, focusing on educational initiatives blending modern sciences with Islamic scholarship. Imran Khan, serving as Prime Minister at the time, is identified as the founder and patron-in-chief of the trust, with his wife Bushra Bibi serving as a key trustee from inception.[2] [4] Initial governance was structured around a board of trustees, which included political allies such as Zulfi Bukhari and Babar Awan alongside Bushra Bibi, though subsequent adjustments to the trustee composition occurred amid operational developments. The trust's foundational documents outlined its non-profit status, emphasizing poverty alleviation and educational access, with assets initially comprising land donations intended for the university campus. Foundation stone-laying for the university site had preceded formal registration, occurring in May 2019 under Imran Khan's oversight, signaling early momentum for the project prior to legal incorporation.[12] [2] Operational setup began modestly, with the trust securing necessary approvals from provincial authorities, including a no-objection certificate from the Higher Education Commission of Punjab to proceed with university development. By early 2021, teaching activities commenced on a preliminary basis, reflecting the trust's rapid transition from registration to implementation, though full institutional accreditation remained pending.[13]

Key Personnel and Governance

The Al-Qadir Trust, established as a non-profit entity in 2019, is primarily governed by a minimal board of trustees consisting of Imran Khan as founder, chairman, and trustee, and his wife Bushra Bibi (also referred to as Bushra Khan) as trustee.[4][5] This structure, with only two trustees, has been highlighted in investigations into the trust's operations, underscoring a concentrated decision-making process lacking broader oversight typical of larger charitable organizations.[5] Imran Khan, former Prime Minister of Pakistan, initiated the trust to support the Al-Qadir University Project, focusing on educational and poverty alleviation initiatives aligned with Islamic principles.[2] Bushra Bibi's role as trustee involved administrative responsibilities, though the trust's deed indicates governance by the board without unilateral decision-making by any single member.[14] Additional advisory input comes from global advisors including Ahmad Javaid, Shaykh Amin Kholwadia, and Shaykh Hamza Yusuf, who provide expertise on curriculum and spiritual guidance but hold no formal trustee positions.[4] Operational roles include executive directors such as Muhammad Amjad ur Rehman and academic deans like Amanullah Khan and Dr. Zeeshan Ahmed, appointed to manage day-to-day affairs and development of the university project.[15][16] The trust's governance has faced scrutiny in legal proceedings, where allegations of inadequate transparency and potential conflicts of interest due to the familial trustee composition were raised, though defenders argue it reflects a focused philanthropic effort without formal salaries or personal gains.[17][2]

Objectives and Activities

Charitable Mission

The Al-Qadir Trust's charitable mission focuses on promoting education as a means of spiritual, intellectual, and societal upliftment, with an emphasis on Islamic teachings integrated with modern disciplines. Established to address educational disparities, particularly among the poor, needy, and women in Pakistan, the trust prioritizes non-discriminatory access to quality learning opportunities regardless of caste, creed, race, religion, or financial status.[18] This aligns with its vision of fostering reflection on divine signs through Quranic and Sunnah guidance to realize human potential in service to God and society.[1] Per Clause 3 of the trust deed, core objectives include founding Al-Qadir University to encompass Islamic studies, sciences, and other fields, while extending to additional schools, colleges, and universities across the country for broader educational promotion.[18] [14] The mission entails delivering holistic programsâ€”such as BS degrees in Islamic Studies and Management, alongside short courses on contemporary issuesâ€”that blend traditional Islamic wisdom with scientific inquiry, rooted in the principle of Tawhid (unity of God).[1] These efforts aim to cultivate leaders and scholars equipped to tackle modern challenges while reviving ethical values.[1] Operated independently under a board akin to that of established philanthropic entities, the trust prohibits personal remuneration for trustees and bars asset diversion for private gain, directing all proceeds toward institutional development and public welfare.[18] The university project, located outside Islamabad, embodies this by offering combined religious and secular curricula to underprivileged students, though actual implementation has centered on site development rather than widespread operations to date.[19]

Educational Focus

The Al-Qadir Trust, through its Al-Qadir University Project Trust (AQUPT), emphasizes an educational model that integrates Islamic scholarship with contemporary disciplines to foster holistic development among students. Established to address perceived gaps in modern Pakistani higher education, the trust's vision prioritizes the revival of authentic Islamic teachings, particularly Sufi traditions and the principles of the Medina welfare state, alongside practical skills in fields like management and technology. This approach aims to produce leaders capable of applying religious ethics to real-world challenges, with a curriculum designed to blend traditional wisdomâ€”such as tarbiyah (moral and spiritual upbringing)â€”with evidence-based modern methodologies.[3][20][21] Core degree programs offered include Bachelor of Science degrees in Management Science, Islamic Studies, and Computer Science, with admissions prioritizing merit-based scholarships for talented students from diverse backgrounds. The curriculum incorporates hands-on elements like case studies, simulations, and role-playing to bridge theoretical knowledge with practical application, while electives cover topics such as project management, total quality management, and agile methodologies. Emphasis is placed on character building through Islamic values, including spiritual growth and ethical decision-making, within an environment that promotes research into historical Islamic governance models for contemporary relevance.[22][1] Beyond academics, the trust promotes extracurricular initiatives like environmental awareness drives and sessions aligning faith with professional skills, reflecting a broader objective of cultivating well-rounded individuals committed to societal welfare. Imran Khan, as the trust's patron, articulated the mission in 2021 as creating a global center for Islamic learning that inspires youth to embody prophetic ethics in leadership roles, countering secular influences in education. While operational since 2019, the project's scale remains modest, with facilities planned for up to 800 students in dedicated blocks for Sufi studies and sciences, underscoring a long-term commitment to interdisciplinary Islamic research despite ongoing legal scrutiny of funding sources.[23][24][25]

Al-Qadir University Project

Site Acquisition and Development

The site for Al-Qadir University was selected in Punjab, Pakistan, specifically in Mouza Bakrala, Sohawa tehsil of Jhelum district. Bahria Town, a major real estate developer, acquired approximately 458 kanals (about 57 hectares) of land from various local landowners between April 4 and April 30, 2019, valued at over PKR 7 billion. This land was subsequently transferred to the Al-Qadir University Project Trust as a donation to facilitate the establishment of the university.[26] The acquisition occurred amid the trust's efforts to materialize its educational objectives, with the land intended for constructing academic buildings, residential facilities, and other infrastructure aligned with the project's focus on Islamic scholarship and modern education. Then-Prime Minister Imran Khan laid the foundation stone for the university following the land transfer, marking the formal initiation of development activities.[2] Development progressed to initial construction phases, but the project has been hampered by ongoing legal disputes over the legitimacy of the land donation and associated financial arrangements. In January 2025, an accountability court ordered the transfer of the Al-Qadir University assets, including the site, to the federal government pending resolution of corruption allegations.[27]

Planned Curriculum and Facilities

The planned curriculum for Al-Qadir University Project Trust focuses on bachelor's degree programs in Management Studies, Islamic Studies, and Computer Science, aimed at producing leaders who integrate Islamic ethical principles with modern professional skills.[1][3] These programs emphasize holistic development, drawing from the Quran, Sunnah, and Prophetic traditions to address contemporary challenges in leadership, management, and technology.[1][28] Instructional approaches incorporate interactive, hands-on methods such as real-life case studies, simulations, and role-plays, while blending traditional Islamic scholarshipâ€”rooted in Tawhid and Seerahâ€”with empirical scientific research and global pedagogical practices.[1][29] The BS Islamic Studies curriculum includes core courses like Textual Study of Al-Quran, Textual Study of Al-Hadith, Arabic Language, and History of Tafseer, with minors available in related areas to allow flexibility.[30] Departments in Management Science and Islamic Studies form the foundational structure, with gradual enrollment expansion to ensure quality.[3] Facilities were designed as purpose-built infrastructure on acquired land, completed within two years of inception, to support customized high-quality education.[3] Planned amenities include state-of-the-art modern classrooms, PhD-qualified faculty support, fully equipped student hostels, a mess hall, gymnasium, a contemporary library, high-speed internet, and dedicated prayer areas to foster an Islamic environment conducive to spiritual, intellectual, and physical well-being.[3][31][32] The campus prioritizes a green, eco-friendly ecosystem, including plantation initiatives, to enhance overall student health.[33]

This settlement effectively transferred the forfeited sum to the NCA for potential repatriation, with Riaz maintaining that the payment addressed regulatory issues rather than admitting guilt.[37][34]

Government Repatriation Decision

On December 3, 2019, the federal cabinet of Pakistan, chaired by Prime Minister Imran Khan, approved a confidential summary authorizing the government to enter into a settlement agreement with the United Kingdom's National Crime Agency (NCA) concerning the Â£190 million (approximately Rs 51 billion at prevailing exchange rates) paid by property developer Malik Riaz Hussain to resolve civil recovery proceedings related to money laundering allegations.[38][26] The approval was granted via a sealed envelope, limiting disclosure of the agreement's details to cabinet members, which including a Rs 5 billion fine and orders for restitution.[41] Under the approved arrangement, the Â£190 million served to offset these obligations, but the federal government accepted, in lieu of Supreme Court account without direct state expenditure.[41] The decision has been criticized for resulting in a net loss to the national exchequer, as the land donation's value fell short of the Â£190 facilitating Riaz's travel and negotiations with UK authorities, underscoring active involvement in structuring the repatriation to align with the Trust's educational project.[40] As of March 2025, the funds remained in the Supreme Court account, prompting the subsequent Shehbaz Sharif administration to redirect them toward public sector university and school construction.[42]

Corruption Allegations

Core Accusations of Abuse of Authority

The core accusations in the Al-Qadir Trust case center on Imran Khan's alleged misuse of authority as Prime Minister to divert Â£190 million recovered from the UK's National Crime Agency (NCA) settlement, resulting in a corresponding loss to Pakistan's national exchequer.[6] [43] On December 3, 2019, Khan's cabinet approved a sealed settlement agreement with the NCA concerning assets seized from Malik Riaz, chairman of Bahria Town, who faced money laundering probes in the UK; the Â£190 million (equivalent to approximately PKR 50 billion at the time) was repatriated to Pakistan but, per NAB allegations, was effectively credited against Riaz's liabilities in exchange for his donation of land to the Al-Qadir Trust, a charitable entity founded by Khan and his wife Bushra Bibi for an educational project.[43] [44] This arrangement purportedly allowed the fundsâ€”intended as state-recovered assetsâ€”to bypass the treasury and indirectly benefit the private trust, constituting an abuse of public office under the National Accountability Ordinance.[6] Prosecutors from the National Accountability Bureau (NAB) claimed Khan exploited his position to facilitate the deal without full cabinet disclosure, enabling Riaz to offset his Â£190 million obligation through the land transfer rather than direct payment to the state, thereby depriving the exchequer of the full amount for public use.[43] The donated land, comprising 458 kanals, 4 marlas, and 58 square feet in Jhelum district, Punjab, was valued by NAB at over PKR 7 billion and registered in the trust's name, allegedly as illegal gratification for approving the settlement terms favorable to Riaz.[6] Bushra Bibi, as a trustee and spiritual advisor to Khan, was accused of complicity in soliciting and accepting these benefits, with intermediaries like Farhat Shahzadi purportedly acting as fronts to obscure the transactions.[6] [44] NAB's reference, filed in December 2023, framed the scheme as a quid pro quo where Khan's authority was leveraged to legitimize the fund diversion under the guise of charitable development for Al-Qadir University, violating fiduciary duties and causing quantifiable financial harm to the state.[43] The allegations extended to non-disclosure of conflicts of interest, as the trust's beneficiaries included Khan's family-linked entities, and failure to pursue alternative recovery options that would have deposited the full sum into government accounts.[6] These charges culminated in convictions on January 17, 2025, with Khan sentenced to 14 years for corruption and abuse of authority, and Bibi to 7 years for aiding the offenses, alongside fines and forfeiture of trust properties to the federal government.[44]

Links to Malik Riaz and Bahria Town

Malik Riaz, the founder and chairman of Bahria Town Pakistanâ€”the country's largest real estate developerâ€”established a key connection to Al-Qadir Trust through a substantial land donation. In March 2021, Bahria Town transferred 458 kanals (approximately 57 acres) of land in Sohawa, Jhelum District, valued at roughly PKR 7 billion (about USD 50 million at contemporaneous exchange rates), to the Trust for the development of Al-Qadir University.[8][45] This donation occurred shortly after the Pakistani government's decision to repatriate Â£190 million (equivalent to PKR 52 billion) from the UK's National Crime Agency (NCA) settlement with Riaz's family over money laundering probes involving Bahria Town assets.[46] National Accountability Bureau (NAB) investigators allege that the land served as quid pro quo for favors extended by then-Prime Minister Imran Khan's administration, specifically the facilitation of repatriating the NCA funds to offset a PKR 460 billion Supreme Court fine imposed on Bahria Town in 2018 for illegal acquisition of 16,696 kanals of forest land in Islamabad.[45][8] Without repatriation, the Â£190 millionâ€”settled by Riaz in December 2020â€”would have accrued to the UK treasury; instead, its return to Pakistan effectively reduced Bahria Town's net liability on the domestic fine by crediting the amount toward state dues.[46] NAB contends this arrangement diverted public funds, causing an unlawful loss to the exchequer equivalent to the donated land's market value, as the Trustâ€”chaired by Khan's wife Bushra Bibiâ€”was positioned to receive the benefit without transparent procurement.[45] Riaz's involvement deepened scrutiny, with NAB naming him a co-conspirator alongside Khan and Bibi in the graft probe launched in May 2022.[8] By late 2022, Riaz was declared a proclaimed offender (absconder) after failing to appear for questioning, prompting NAB to seek his extradition from the United Arab Emirates, where he resides.[47] Subsequent enforcement actions included court-approved auctions of Bahria Town-linked properties, such as a 405-kanal plot owned by Riaz's son, to recover outstanding dues tied to the Al-Qadir case and the Supreme Court penalty.[48] These measures, initiated in 2025, underscore ongoing efforts to liquidate assets amid allegations of money laundering through Bahria Town's overseas projects.[49]

Legal Proceedings

Investigation and Charges

The National Accountability Bureau (NAB) initiated an investigation into the Al-Qadir Trust in December 2022, prompted by allegations that emerged in June 2022 accusing former Prime Minister Imran Khan and his wife Bushra Bibi of receiving land donations worth billions of rupees from real estate tycoon Malik Riaz for the trust's university project.[50][6] The probe focused on a 2019 settlement under Khan's PTI government, where Â£190 million repatriated from Riaz's assets seized by the UK's National Crime Agency was allegedly adjusted to offset NAB fines imposed on Riaz's Bahria Town for illegal land acquisitions, rather than being deposited into the national treasury, resulting in an estimated loss of Â£190 million (approximately Rs 50 billion or $239 million equivalent) to the state exchequer.[50][6][45] NAB summoned Khan and several cabinet members, including former advisor Mirza Shehzad Akbar, on December 1, 2022, to examine the decision-making process behind the repatriation and adjustment of funds.[6] The bureau alleged a quid pro quo arrangement, claiming Riaz provided 458 kanals of land in Jhelum, valued at over Rs 7 billion, to the Al-Qadir Trust as a bribe to secure favorable treatment in his ongoing NAB cases.[50][6] Khan ignored multiple summons, leading NAB to secure an arrest warrant on May 1, 2023, and arrest him on May 9, 2023, outside an Islamabad court, citing non-cooperation despite substantial evidence of corruption.[50][45] The charges, formalized in a NAB reference under the National Accountability Ordinance 1999, accused Khan, Bibi, Riaz, his son Ahmed Ali Riaz, Akbar, former aide Zulfi Bukhari, and two others of corruption, abuse of authority, and receiving illegal gratification.[6][45] Specific counts included deliberately causing financial loss to the state through unauthorized fund diversion, accepting bribes to shield Bahria Town from prosecution, and failing to disclose trust-related transactions.[45] An accountability court indicted Khan and Bibi in February 2024, marking the transition from investigation to trial proceedings.[45]

Trial, Conviction, and Sentencing

The trial in the Al-Qadir Trust case commenced in an Islamabad accountability court following the framing of charges against Imran Khan, Bushra Bibi, and several co-accused on February 27, 2024, under the National Accountability Ordinance for alleged corruption and abuse of authority.[51] The National Accountability Bureau (NAB) prosecuted the case, presenting evidence that Khan, as prime minister, approved a settlement allowing real estate developer Malik Riaz to use Â£190 million in repatriated funds to offset a Supreme Court-imposed fine, in exchange for land donations valued at over 458 kanals to the Al-Qadir Trust, purportedly causing undue state loss.[7] Proceedings involved witness testimonies, including from NAB investigators and involved officials, and spanned multiple hearings amid procedural delays, including court vacations and petitions for stays filed by the defense in the Islamabad High Court.[52] The verdict, initially scheduled for earlier dates in January 2025, was postponed thriceâ€”first to January 13 due to non-appearance of the accused, then to January 17â€”before being announced on January 17, 2025, by Judge Nasir Javed Rana.[9] The court convicted Khan and Bibi, ruling that they had facilitated the illegal adjustment of funds and acceptance of undue benefits through the trust, constituting criminal breach of trust and corruption. Khan was sentenced to 14 years of rigorous imprisonmentâ€”the maximum penalty under the relevant sectionsâ€”and fined PKR 1 million, while Bibi received 7 years of rigorous imprisonment and a PKR 500,000 fine.[53][54] Co-accused, including former officials Zahid Hussain and Mushtaq Ahmad Khan, were acquitted for lack of direct involvement in the alleged graft.[55] The convictions were delivered in absentia for Khan and Bibi, who were already incarcerated on separate charges, with the court emphasizing the evidence of quid pro quo arrangements that bypassed legal deposit requirements for the repatriated funds into the national treasury.[10] The ruling highlighted the trust's role in channeling benefits, rejecting defense arguments that the land was donated voluntarily for charitable educational purposes without state loss.[56]

Appeals and Ongoing Developments

Following their conviction on January 17, 2025, in the Al-Qadir Trust case, Imran Khan and Bushra Bibi filed appeals in the Islamabad High Court (IHC), challenging the trial court's ruling on grounds of insufficient evidence, procedural irregularities, and lack of proof of personal financial gain.[57][58] The appeals, registered as Criminal Appeal Nos. 63/2025 and 64/2025, argued that the Â£190 million settlement funds were legitimately deposited into the Al-Qadir Trust's account without causing loss to the national exchequer, and that the National Accountability Bureau (NAB) failed to demonstrate abuse of authority or corruption.[59][60] The IHC issued notices to NAB and other respondents in May 2025, with initial hearings on suspension of sentences scheduled for June 5 and subsequent dates including June 26, where the court granted NAB four weeks to prepare its response.[61][62][63] In July 2025, the appellants sought an early hearing on their plea to suspend the convictions, citing ongoing detention without resolution, but the court maintained that appeals at the motion stage could not be prioritized over 279 pending criminal cases per judicial policy.[58][64] As of October 2025, the appeals remain unheard and unscheduled for substantive arguments in the IHC, with sources indicating no hearings are likely before 2026 due to backlog and procedural constraints.[65][66] This delay contributes to Khan's continued incarceration, as the Al-Qadir conviction upholds his 14-year sentence alongside other cases, despite interim bails or acquittals in unrelated matters.[67] No final resolution has been reached, and the case exemplifies protracted judicial processes in high-profile NAB prosecutions.[68]

Defenses and Counterarguments

PTI and Khan's Perspective

Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) has consistently maintained that the Al-Qadir Trust case constitutes a politically motivated fabrication designed to disqualify Imran Khan from political participation and undermine the party's leadership.[69][70] PTI spokespersons argue that no evidence of corruption or personal gain was presented during the trial, emphasizing that the trust's establishment for the Al-Qadir University project involved transparent donations approved by the federal cabinet on December 2, 2019.[71] Imran Khan has denounced the January 17, 2025, conviction as part of a series of "false cases" orchestrated by political rivals, including references to Nawaz Sharif's alleged corruption, while vowing to continue advocating for democracy and the rule of law.[72][73] He has criticized procedural delays, such as the ten-month postponement of hearings leading to bail denials, as tactics to deny justice and pressure PTI.[74][75] From PTI's viewpoint, the Â£190 million repatriated funds from a UK settlement with Typhon were not state assets but proceeds from laundered money involving Malik Riaz, and their allocation to the trust caused no loss to the national exchequer, as cabinet decisions ensured equivalent value in land donations for public welfare.[71] The party highlights that the project aimed at establishing an educational institution in Punjab, with land contributions documented as charitable rather than quid pro quo, rejecting claims of abuse of authority.[72] PTI has announced intentions to appeal the verdict in the Islamabad High Court, framing the legal battle as a defense against selective prosecution amid broader political negotiations.[70][57] Khan and party leaders assert that the absence of proven wrongdoing underscores the case's meritlessness, positioning it within a pattern of judicial overreach targeting opposition figures.[71][76]

Critiques of Prosecutorial Motives

Supporters of Imran Khan and the Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) party have argued that the prosecution in the Al-Qadir Trust case represents a politically motivated effort to sideline Khan from electoral politics following his ouster via a no-confidence vote on April 10, 2022.[50] Khan has described the charges as a "witch-hunt" orchestrated by political rivals, including the Pakistan Muslim irregularities as evidence of bias, including the Accountability Court's rapid progression to a verdict on January 17, 2025, sentencing Khan to 14 years and Bushra Bibi to 7 years, despite PTI claims of absent witnesses, forged documents, and no proof of personal financial gain by Khan, who maintained he held no ownership in the Al-Qadir University project.[77] PTI leaders, such as those filing charged environments often prioritize political expediency over evidentiary standards.[78] Khan's defense further alleges selective prosecution, pointing out that similar land donations for educational trusts by prior governments, including Nawaz Sharif's, faced no scrutiny, suggesting the case targets PTI's anti-corruption stance rather than substantive wrongdoing.[44] PTI has vowed to challenge the conviction, framing it as part of a broader pattern where NAB, reformed under Khan to curb elite capture, was repurposed post-2022 to harass opposition figures, with appeals citing undue haste and lack of cross-examination opportunities as hallmarks of predetermined outcomes.[79] These critiques portray the prosecution not as accountability but as a tool for consolidating power amid Pakistan's polarized politics, where institutional bias against populist challengers like Khan is evident in the conviction's alignment with pre-election disqualification efforts.[71]

Impact and Current Status

Broader Implications for Pakistani Politics

The Al-Qadir Trust case has amplified scrutiny over the selective enforcement of anti-corruption laws in Pakistan, where convictions like that of Imran Khan on January 17, 2025â€”sentencing him to 14 years for facilitating a Â£190 million settlement adjustment allegedly benefiting a real estate tycoonâ€”contrast with unprosecuted similar arrangements by prior administrations.[10] This disparity fuels PTI assertions of institutional weaponization by the PML-N-led coalition and military establishment, eroding public confidence in bodies like the National Accountability Bureau (NAB) as impartial arbiters.[80][81] By redefining "gratification" under corruption statutes to encompass indirect benefits such as land donations tied to policy favors, the ruling sets a precedent that could constrain future leaders' use of charitable trusts as vehicles for influence peddling, though enforcement remains inconsistent amid entrenched elite networks.[80] The case's linkage to Malik Riaz's Â£190 million recovery from UK authorities in 2022 highlights how recovered illicit funds are rerouted, exposing systemic vulnerabilities in financial oversight and the nexus between political office and private sector leverage.[81] Politically, the scandal undermines PTI's foundational anti-corruption narrative, which propelled Khan's 2018 victory, by providing ammunition for opponents to portray the party as hypocritical, potentially hampering its mobilization ahead of elections despite Khan's enduring grassroots support.[82] Ongoing appeals, including Islamabad High Court hearings scheduled for June 5, 2025, intersect with broader negotiations between PTI and the government, illustrating how legal proceedings serve as leverage in Pakistan's hybrid political bargaining.[61] This dynamic perpetuates instability, as corruption cases like Al-Qadir reinforce cycles of retribution rather than systemic reform, deterring investment and governance efficacy.[81]

Status of the Trust and University Project

In January 2025, an accountability court convicted Imran Khan and Bushra Bibi in the Al-Qadir Trust case, sentencing them to 14 and 7 years in prison, respectively, and ordering the forfeiture of the Al-Qadir University Project Trust's property to the federal government under Section 10(a) of the National Accountability Ordinance.[83][84] The ruling declared the trust a "sham" entity used to facilitate alleged corruption involving land donations worth billions of rupees in exchange for the release of Â£190 million in settled funds from the UK's National Crime Agency.[85] Appeals against the conviction were filed on January 31, 2024, but as of September 2025, the Islamabad High Court had only adjourned related pleas without fixing a hearing date, leaving the forfeiture unenforced.[86][65] Despite the legal directive, the trust has continued operations into late 2025. Its official website and social media channels announced admissions for Fall 2025 in undergraduate programs including BS Management, BS Islamic Studies, and BS Computer Science, with merit-based scholarships and need-based aid offered.[1] Walk-in tests and interviews were conducted in August 2025, followed by an orientation week from September 10â€“12, 2025, for incoming students.[87][88] The trust, established on December 26, 2019, for educational and poverty alleviation initiatives, maintains academic centers focused on blending Islamic principles with modern disciplines, though it has hosted workshops and short courses rather than full-scale degree conferral.[89] The project remains unregistered as a formal university with the Punjab Higher Education Commission, having never applied for accreditation, and had enrolled approximately 200 students across four years as of January 2025.[90][91] Federal Defence Minister Khawaja Asif highlighted this in January 2025, arguing the entity does not meet university criteria and questioning its legitimacy amid the scandal. Police were deployed outside the Sohawa campus following the verdict, but no subsequent reports confirm disruption to ongoing activities or enforcement of the handover.[91][92]
