Dina Merrill

Dina Merrill (born Nedenia Marjorie Hutton; December 29, 1923 – May 22, 2017) was an American actress, heiress, socialite, and philanthropist whose career spanned film, television, and stage, alongside her inherited wealth from the Post cereal fortune.[1] The daughter of Marjorie Merriweather Post, founder of the General Foods Corporation through Post cereals, and Wall Street financier Edward Francis Hutton, Merrill grew up in privilege, wintering at the family's Mar-a-Lago estate in Palm Beach, Florida, which she later helped sell to Donald Trump in 1985.[2][3] Her acting breakthrough came in the late 1950s with roles in films such as Desk Set (1957) opposite Spencer Tracy and Katharine Hepburn, Operation Petticoat (1959) with Cary Grant, and Butterfield 8 (1960) alongside Elizabeth Taylor, establishing her as a poised supporting actress in Hollywood's golden age.[4][5] Merrill appeared in over 100 film and television projects, including guest spots on series like Batman and The Twilight Zone, while maintaining a commitment to philanthropy, particularly supporting causes related to diabetes research following the death of her son from the disease, and serving on boards for arts and environmental organizations.[2][6]

Early Life and Family Background

Birth and Parentage

Dina Merrill was born Nedenia Marjorie Hutton on December 29, 1923, in Manhattan, New York City.[1][7] She was the only child of her parents' marriage.[3] Her mother, Marjorie Merriweather Post, was an American businesswoman and philanthropist who inherited the Postum Cereal Company fortune from her father, C. W. Post, and later expanded it into General Foods.[1][3] Her father, Edward Francis Hutton, was a financier and stockbroker who co-founded the Wall Street brokerage firm E. F. Hutton & Company in 1904.[2][3] The couple married in 1905 and divorced in 1935, when Merrill was 11 years old.[1]

Upbringing and Family Wealth

Dina Merrill, originally named Nedenia Marjorie Hutton, grew up as the only child of Marjorie Merriweather Post, heiress to the Post cereals fortune established by her grandfather C.W. Post through the Postum Company (later General Foods), and Edward Francis Hutton, founder of the Wall Street brokerage E.F. Hutton & Company.[5][8] Her parents' combined assets positioned the family among America's elite, with Marjorie Post inheriting and expanding a fortune that included shares worth millions by her early adulthood and made her one of the wealthiest women in the United States.[9][10] Her upbringing reflected this vast wealth through multiple opulent residences and lifestyles of extreme privilege. She divided time between the family's triplex apartment on Fifth Avenue in New York City, a lavish 177-acre Tudor estate in the Hamptons serving as a summer retreat, and the sprawling Mar-a-Lago estate in Palm Beach, Florida, which featured 126 rooms across 17 acres.[11][12][13] The family yacht Sea Cloud, a 360-foot four-masted barque, accommodated extended voyages, with Merrill spending up to six months annually aboard it, hosting notable guests including the Duke and Duchess of Windsor.[1] Her parents' divorce in 1935, when she was 10, shifted dynamics but preserved the luxurious environment under her mother's influence.[14]

Inheritance Decisions and Mar-a-Lago

Upon the death of her mother, Marjorie Merriweather Post, on September 12, 1973, Dina Merrill and her two half-sisters, Adelaide Close and Eleanor Post Close, inherited the bulk of an estate valued at more than $250 million, including control over family trusts and properties such as the Mar-a-Lago estate in Palm Beach, Florida, where Merrill had spent much of her youth.[8][1] In her will, Post explicitly bequeathed Mar-a-Lago—a 126-room Renaissance-style mansion built between 1924 and 1927—to the United States government, stipulating its use as a winter retreat for presidents and foreign dignitaries, with the National Park Service tasked for maintenance.[15] This decision reflected Post's long-term vision for the property's public utility, though it imposed no immediate divestment from the family-held Marjorie Merriweather Post Foundation, which the daughters oversaw as beneficiaries and trustees.[16] The federal government initially accepted the bequest but reversed course in 1981, citing prohibitive annual upkeep costs exceeding $3 million amid budget constraints and shifting priorities.[17] With the donation rejected, ownership reverted to the Post Foundation, prompting Merrill and her sisters to cease maintenance on the aging estate, which fell into disrepair as they prioritized a sale over personal retention or further philanthropic operation.[6] This pragmatic choice aligned with the family's broader inheritance strategy of liquidating non-core assets to fund philanthropy and personal endeavors, rather than sustaining a financially burdensome landmark; Merrill herself later directed portions of her inheritance toward causes like the arts and health research.[1] In 1985, facing limited buyer interest and even approving potential demolition permits, the sisters listed Mar-a-Lago for $20 million.[18] Developer Donald Trump acquired it in December 1985 for $10 million—half the asking price—transforming it into a private club while restoring its structures amid local preservation debates.[18] Merrill's involvement in the transaction drew public scrutiny, including a subsequent feud with Trump, who criticized the family's neglect of the property's condition; she countered by emphasizing the estate's impracticality for private upkeep and the necessity of the sale to honor her mother's fiscal realism.[19] This disposal exemplified the heirs' decisions to prioritize liquidity and endowment over sentimental or operational retention of dynastic holdings.[6]

Education and Early Aspirations

Formal Education

Merrill completed her secondary education at Miss Porter's School, a preparatory institution in Farmington, Connecticut.[20] At the urging of her father, Edward Francis Hutton, she enrolled at George Washington University in Washington, D.C., but departed after one year without completing a degree, opting instead to focus on dramatic training.[5][1] She subsequently attended the American Academy of Dramatic Arts in New York City, a professional conservatory dedicated to theater and performance instruction.[1][21]

Influences Leading to Acting

Despite her privileged upbringing as the daughter of Marjorie Merriweather Post and E.F. Hutton, Merrill exhibited a strong personal determination to pursue acting from a young age, rebelling against her family's expectations that she prioritize high-society marriage and philanthropy over a public career in the arts.[1][22] Her mother, in particular, envisioned Merrill marrying European royalty, viewing acting as undignified for an heiress, while her father dismissed the ambition as foolish.[1] After one year at George Washington University in 1943, Merrill withdrew to enroll at the American Academy of Dramatic Arts in New York, marking a deliberate shift toward professional training without relying on family financial support.[23][24] To fund her independence, she accepted modeling assignments at $10 per hour, including work for Vogue, deliberately forgoing the substantial inheritance—already exceeding $1 million from her father's investments—that could have eased her path.[25][1][2] This self-imposed financial discipline underscored Merrill's commitment to earning legitimacy in acting on merit rather than pedigree, a resolve that propelled her 1945 stage debut in The Mermaids Singing on Broadway, though she briefly paused her pursuits after marrying Stanley M. Rumbough Jr. in December 1946 to focus on family.[26][27] Her early choices reflected not external mentorships or theatrical family traditions but an intrinsic drive to forge an identity distinct from her vast wealth, which her parents' opposition only intensified.[22][9]

Professional Career

Acting Debut and Film Work


Dina Merrill made her feature film debut in 1957 with the romantic comedy Desk Set, portraying Sylvia Blair, an assistant in the reference department at a television network, alongside Spencer Tracy and Katharine Hepburn.[28] The film, directed by Walter Lang, depicted the introduction of an early computer system threatening jobs, with Merrill's character contributing to the ensemble of office workers.[29] Her subsequent film roles in the late 1950s and early 1960s often cast her as elegant, upper-class women, leveraging her real-life heiress persona. In 1958, she appeared in A Nice Little Bank That Should Be Robbed as the wife of a bank robber, followed by comedic supporting parts in Don't Give Up the Ship (1959) and the submarine farce Operation Petticoat (1959), where she played Lieutenant Barbara Duran, a nurse stranded aboard with Cary Grant and Tony Curtis.[28] Merrill continued with The Sundowners (1960), a drama with Deborah Kerr and Robert Mitchum, and Butterfield 8 (1960), as Emily Liggett, a socialite friend to Elizabeth Taylor's Gloria Wandrous.[30] Other notable early films included The Young Savages (1961) opposite Burt Lancaster and The Courtship of Eddie's Father (1963) with Glenn Ford.[31] Merrill's film work tapered in the 1970s but resumed sporadically in later decades, including roles in True Colors (1991), Robert Altman's The Player (1992) as Celia, and the comedy Caddyshack II (1988).[28] She appeared in independent films like Suture (1993) and continued into the 2000s with parts in Shade (2003) and The Magnificent Ambersons (2002 television film adaptation, though primarily noted for feature films).[30] Over her career, she amassed credits in approximately 20 feature films, frequently portraying sophisticated characters without major award recognition for her cinematic roles.[32]

Television Roles and Stage Appearances

Merrill frequently appeared as a guest star on television series from the 1960s onward, accumulating over 100 credits across various genres. Notable roles included appearances on Dr. Kildare, The Alfred Hitchcock Hour, Burke's Law, Rawhide, Bonanza, Batman, Mission: Impossible, The Virginian, The Odd Couple, Hawaii Five-O, Quincy M.E., Murder, She Wrote, The Nanny, and Roseanne.[4][28] She also participated as a panelist or mystery guest on game shows such as I've Got a Secret, Hollywood Squares, What's My Line?, Match Game, and To Tell the Truth during the 1960s and 1970s.[33][32] In television films and miniseries, Merrill starred in The Bo Jo Jones (1971), Family Flight (1972), The Letters (1973), The Tenth Month (1979), and had a featured role in the miniseries Roots: The Next Generations (1979).[28] Later credits encompassed 100 Centre Street (2001) as Judge Helen Randolph and The Glow (1998) as Phoebe Janusz.[32] She briefly led the short-lived ABC series Hot Pursuit (1984).[30] On stage, Merrill debuted on Broadway in John Van Druten's The Mermaids Singing in 1945, following earlier involvement in Moss Hart's USO troupe during World War II.[34][35] She returned for the 1975 revival of Patrick Hamilton's Angel Street, co-starring as a lead.[36] Merrill later portrayed Peggy Porterfield in the 1983 Broadway revival of Rodgers and Hart's On Your Toes, which ran until May 1984, and reprised the role on the 1986 national tour.[36][37]

Business Ventures and Board Positions

Merrill joined the board of directors of E. F. Hutton & Co., the investment firm founded by her father Edward Francis Hutton, in February 1980.[38] She retained her position through the firm's merger with Shearson Lehman to form Shearson Lehman Hutton and subsequent developments into Lehman Brothers, serving on its board as well.[5] Her tenure on the E. F. Hutton board lasted over 18 years, reflecting her active role in the family's financial enterprise.[39] In the late 1980s, Merrill partnered with her third husband, Ted Hartley, to establish a film and entertainment development and production company.[40] In 1989, they acquired the remnants of RKO Pictures, the historic Hollywood studio, with Merrill appointed as creative chief.[40] By 1991, they merged their Pavilion Communications with RKO Pictures Corporation to form RKO Pictures LLC, where Merrill served as vice chairman, overseeing aspects of film production and distribution.[5] Under this venture, the company co-produced films such as Milk Money (1994).[41] This acquisition revived RKO's library of over 900 films, positioning it as a niche player in entertainment assets.[8] Beyond corporate boards, Merrill held a presidential appointment to the board of trustees of the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in 1982, contributing to its governance in arts and culture, though this intersected with her philanthropic interests.[42] Her business roles underscored a blend of inherited financial acumen and entrepreneurial extension into media production.

Philanthropy and Civic Engagement

Charitable Organizations and Donations

Merrill co-founded the Juvenile Diabetes Foundation (now Breakthrough T1D) in response to her son David's diagnosis with type 1 diabetes at age 13, serving as an early ambassador and advocate for diabetes research funding.[43][34][44] She established an annual award for scientific excellence within the organization to recognize advancements in treatment.[45] In 1992, she hosted a benefit event for the foundation's New York chapter at her East Hampton residence.[46] As chairman of the board and director emeritus of the New York City Mission Society for over 50 years, Merrill supported programs aimed at alleviating intergenerational poverty among urban youth and families, beginning with her involvement introduced by her mother-in-law in the late 1940s during her early marriage.[47][34] She and her husband Ted Hartley frequently hosted fundraising benefits at their East Hampton home for the society, contributing to its mission of providing educational and social services.[48] Merrill also served as a founding board member of ORBIS, an international organization focused on preventing blindness through eye care programs, and chaired a 1976 summer fundraising gala for Southampton Hospital.[46] Her philanthropy extended to sustained donations supporting Guild Hall in East Hampton, where the Dina Merrill Pavilion honors her contributions to the arts community.[48]

Advocacy in Arts and Health Causes

Merrill served as a founding trustee of the Eugene O'Neill Theater Center, contributing to its development for over 25 years, during which the organization's on-campus theater was named in her honor.[49][34] In 1982, President Ronald Reagan appointed her to the Board of Trustees of the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts, where she served for 12 years, supporting its mission to advance performing arts accessibility and programming.[5][3] She also held an early directorship at the Paley Center for Media, aiding efforts to preserve and promote media arts through public education and exhibitions.[50] In health advocacy, Merrill co-founded the Juvenile Diabetes Foundation (now Breakthrough T1D) following her son David's diagnosis with type 1 diabetes at age 13, focusing on research funding and awareness.[46][34] She established the David Rumbough Award in 1974 to recognize scientific advancements in diabetes research, honoring her son who died in a boating accident in 1985.[1][51] As an early ambassador, she participated in telethons, major events, and was a gala honoree in 1976, raising funds for type 1 diabetes initiatives.[43] Additionally, as vice chairwoman of the Republican Pro-Choice Coalition, she advocated for women's health issues, emphasizing reproductive rights within a conservative framework.[1]

Political Views and Activities

Republican Party Involvement

Dina Merrill identified as a moderate Republican and actively participated in party-affiliated organizations promoting pro-choice positions. She served as vice chairwoman of the Republican Pro-Choice Coalition, advocating for abortion rights within the GOP framework.[1][52] In the mid-1990s, Merrill joined the board of Republican Majority for Choice, a group dedicated to supporting Republican candidates who favored abortion access, and she assumed the role of president in 2002.[53] Her involvement emphasized broadening the party's "big tent" to include pro-choice conservatives, reflecting her efforts to influence GOP policy on women's health issues from within.[53][54] Merrill's financial support for Republican causes included a $250 donation on June 29, 2000, to the Republican Main Street Partnership PAC, which backs moderate GOP candidates.[55] While her political engagement centered on reproductive rights rather than broader electoral campaigns, it positioned her as a bridge between traditional Republican values and progressive stances on social issues.[40]

Positions on Social Issues

Dina Merrill identified as a liberal Republican and advocated for abortion rights within the party, serving as vice chairwoman of the Republican Pro-Choice Coalition.[1] She expressed frustration with the increasing dominance of social conservatives in the Republican Party, describing their success in prioritizing anti-abortion positions as "upsetting" and criticizing former President George H.W. Bush's alignment with that shift.[56] Merrill committed to supporting pro-choice Republican candidates and organizations, viewing such efforts as essential to maintaining a "big tent" party rather than yielding to the social-conservative agenda that gained prominence after the 1980 Republican National Convention.[53] Her pro-choice stance aligned with broader advocacy on women's health issues, though she did not publicly articulate positions on other contemporary social debates such as same-sex marriage or gun control.[1] Merrill's involvement in these areas reflected a moderate approach, prioritizing individual choice on reproductive matters over the platform orthodoxy that later defined much of the party's social conservatism.[23]

Personal Life

Marriages and Relationships

Dina Merrill's first marriage was to Stanley M. Rumbough Jr., an heir to the Colgate-Palmolive fortune and entrepreneur, on September 6, 1946.[5][57] The union lasted approximately 20 years, ending in divorce on December 8, 1966.[57][23] Her second marriage, to actor Cliff Robertson, occurred on December 21, 1966, at Hillwood, the Washington, D.C., estate of her mother, Marjorie Merriweather Post.[57][58] The couple separated in 1985 and divorced in 1989 after 23 years together.[58][20] Merrill married her third husband, Ted Hartley—a former Navy pilot, actor, and producer—later in 1989.[3][5] The marriage endured until Merrill's death in 2017, spanning nearly 28 years.[3] No children resulted from this union, and Hartley later managed aspects of her professional and philanthropic endeavors, including the revival of RKO Pictures.[3]

Children and Family Challenges

Dina Merrill had four children from her two marriages. With her first husband, Stanley M. Rumbough Jr., she had sons Stanley H. Rumbough III (born 1946) and David Post Rumbough (born September 27, 1949), and daughter Nedenia Colgate Rumbough (born 1952).[1] Her second marriage to Cliff Robertson produced daughter Heather Robertson (born 1968).[5] One significant family challenge involved David Rumbough, who was diagnosed with type 1 diabetes in childhood, prompting Merrill to co-found the Juvenile Diabetes Foundation (now JDRF) in 1970 to support research and advocacy for the condition.[43] David died at age 23 in a boating accident on September 8, 1973, in Gardiner's Bay off Long Island, when the twin-engine racing boat he was piloting capsized during a storm; his body was recovered five days later by fishermen.[59][5] Merrill also endured the loss of her daughter Heather, an aspiring actress and screenwriter, who died of ovarian cancer in 2007 at age 39.[5] These tragedies compounded the strains from Merrill's divorces—her first in 1966 after 20 years, coinciding closely with David's death, and her second in 1989—though she maintained relationships with her surviving children and grandchildren.[1]

Later Years and Death

Health Decline and Final Projects

Merrill's health began to decline in her later years due to Lewy body dementia, a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by cognitive fluctuations, visual hallucinations, and motor symptoms akin to Parkinson's disease. In 2015, her condition was publicly revealed as an advanced stage, contributing to her eventual death from heart failure on May 22, 2017, at age 93 in East Hampton, New York.[60] Family statements confirmed the dementia as the underlying cause, noting she had experienced symptoms for several years prior.[34] Despite the advancing dementia, Merrill maintained some professional activity in her 80s and early 90s, reflecting her enduring commitment to acting. Her final screen role came in the 2003 crime drama Shade, in which she played a character named Dina.[31] Preceding this were supporting parts in independent films such as Milk and Money (1997), where she portrayed Ellen, and Suture (1993) as Alice Jameson.[31] She also appeared in a 1998 made-for-television film, FFOCOL: A Chance of Snow, as Merilee Parker.[31] In theater, Merrill took on a role in the 2004 summer stock production of Only a Kingdom near age 80, one of her last stage endeavors before health limitations intensified.[28] These projects, often in smaller or ensemble capacities, showcased her persistence amid personal challenges, though no major productions followed the 2015 diagnosis.

Death and Estate Disposition

Dina Merrill died on May 22, 2017, at her home in East Hampton, New York, at the age of 93.[1][22] The cause of death was Lewy body dementia, as confirmed by her son Stanley H. Rumbough Jr. and husband Ted Hartley.[34][5] Details of Merrill's estate disposition remain largely private, consistent with her family's approach to wealth management. She had inherited substantial assets from her mother, Marjorie Merriweather Post, estimated at over $250 million upon Post's death in 1973, much of which supported Merrill's philanthropic efforts during her lifetime.[8][12] One notable post-death transaction involved the sale of her East Hampton property on West Dune Lane to her daughter, Nedenia Rumbough, for $8.2 million in 2018, approximately a year after Merrill's passing.[61] Merrill's survivors included her husband Ted Hartley and children Stanley Rumbough Jr. and Nedenia Rumbough Hutcheson, with no public records indicating contested probate or major charitable bequests specified in her will.[1]

Legacy and Honors

Professional Recognition

Throughout her career spanning film, television, and stage, Dina Merrill garnered professional recognition primarily through lifetime achievement honors rather than competitive awards from major industry bodies such as the Academy Awards or Primetime Emmys, where she received no nominations. Her contributions were acknowledged by institutions tied to her training and performance history. In 2005, the American Academy of Dramatic Arts, Merrill's alma mater, presented her with a lifetime achievement award celebrating her enduring impact on the dramatic arts.[4] The Guild Hall Academy of the Arts in East Hampton further honored her in 2011 with its Lifetime Achievement Award, specifically citing her decades of summer stock performances at the venue, including roles in productions that showcased her versatility as an actress.[5][34] In tribute to these efforts, Guild Hall subsequently renamed its primary theater and adjacent backstage facilities the Dina Merrill Theater.[62]

Enduring Impact and Assessments

Dina Merrill's philanthropic endeavors left a lasting mark on health research and cultural institutions, particularly through her establishment of the Juvenile Diabetes Foundation (now JDRF) following her son David's diagnosis with the disease in the 1960s, which advanced efforts in diabetic research and support services.[34][40] She also served for 12 years as a presidential appointee to the National Council on the Arts and Humanities, influencing federal arts policy, and held directorships at organizations like the New York Mission Society and the Paley Center for Media, fostering media literacy and community outreach.[3][20] Assessments of Merrill's career highlight her as a symbol of poised independence, defying her wealthy upbringing—daughter of Marjorie Merriweather Post and E.F. Hutton—to pursue acting and modeling, often portraying refined socialites in over 100 film and television roles spanning seven decades from the 1950s to the 2000s.[5][2] Critics and obituaries praised her blend of elegance and resilience, noting her as the "quintessential upper-crust American sophisticate" who bridged elite society with Hollywood without relying on family wealth for sustenance, instead earning through Vogue modeling at $10 per hour early on.[46][2] Her enduring influence extends to exemplifying women's agency in mid-20th-century America, as a moderate Republican advocate for pro-choice causes and environmental conservation, while maintaining a low-profile yet effective presence in East Hampton's cultural scene.[23] Overall evaluations portray her legacy as one of quiet determination and multifaceted contributions, outshining her heiress status through personal initiative rather than inherited privilege.[5][3]

Filmography

Feature Films

Merrill debuted in feature films in the late 1950s, often portraying elegant, supporting characters in comedies and dramas, leveraging her patrician background and poised demeanor.[28] Her early roles included appearances alongside established stars like Spencer Tracy and Katharine Hepburn in Desk Set (1957), where she played Sylvia Blair, a reference librarian entangled in a workplace automation plot.[29] She followed with Operation Petticoat (1959), as Lt. Barbara Duran, a nurse aboard a submarine in a World War II comedy directed by Blake Edwards and starring Cary Grant.[63] Subsequent credits encompassed BUtterfield 8 (1960), portraying Emily Liggett in the adaptation of John O'Hara's novel about a socialite's downfall, co-starring Elizabeth Taylor.[64] Merrill continued in films like The Sundowners (1960), as Jean Halstead in Fred Zinnemann's Australian outback drama with Deborah Kerr and Robert Mitchum; The Courtship of Eddie's Father (1963), as Elizabeth in the romantic comedy with Glenn Ford; and I'll Take Sweden (1965), playing Karin Granquist opposite Bob Hope.[28] Later works shifted to character parts, including Cynthia Young in the comedy sequel Caddyshack II (1988) and Celia in Robert Altman's meta-Hollywood satire The Player (1992). Her final feature film appearance was in Shade (2003), a crime thriller where she played a supporting role as Dina.

Television Credits

Dina Merrill maintained a prolific television presence, with over 100 guest appearances spanning drama, anthology, and procedural series from the 1960s through the 1990s, often portraying elegant or authoritative figures that aligned with her patrician image.[30] Her early TV work featured guest roles on medical and Western programs such as Dr. Kildare, Rawhide, and Bonanza, as well as suspense series like The Alfred Hitchcock Hour and detective shows including Burke's Law.[4] She secured a regular role in the ABC adventure-drama Hot Pursuit (1984–1985), appearing in all 12 episodes as Estelle Modrian, a wealthy and resourceful contact aiding fugitive protagonists.[58] Later guest spots included episodes of Mission: Impossible (1969), Hawaii Five-O, The Love Boat, Quincy, M.E., Murder, She Wrote (1990–1992), Roseanne (1996), and The Nanny (1995).[4] Merrill also starred in several made-for-television films, such as The Lonely Profession (1969) as a secretary entangled in espionage, Seven in Darkness (1969) as Joan Haymess, a survivor in a plane crash scenario forcing interpersonal confrontations among the blindfolded passengers, and Roots: The Next Generations (1979) in a supporting capacity during the historical miniseries' depiction of post-Civil War African-American experiences.[28] Additional TV movies encompassed The Tenth Month (1979), exploring pregnancy and relationships, and 100 Centre Street (2001) as Judge Helen Randolph in the Sidney Lumet-produced legal drama.[32]
