New Pune Airport

Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport, also referred to as New Pune Airport or Purandar International Airport, is a proposed greenfield international airport situated in Purandar taluka of Pune district, Maharashtra, India, designed to serve the Pune metropolitan area and surrounding regions.[1][2] The facility is planned to accommodate up to 75 million passengers per annum (MPPA) upon completion, featuring two parallel runways each measuring 4,000 meters by 60 meters, an extensive apron for over 100 aircraft, and a dedicated cargo terminal to enhance regional trade and connectivity.[1][3] Development is structured as a public-private partnership (PPP) led by the Maharashtra Airport Development Company (MADC), with the City and Industrial Development Corporation (CIDCO) holding a majority stake.[4] The project addresses chronic capacity constraints at the existing Pune Airport in Lohegaon, which shares airspace and infrastructure with the Indian Air Force, limiting civilian expansion.[5] Initial site selection occurred in 2016, targeting approximately 2,400 to 2,832 hectares of land, but progress has been hampered by protracted land acquisition challenges and protests from local farmers opposed to displacement and inadequate compensation.[4][6] In response to resistance, authorities reduced the project footprint in 2025, yet opposition persists, with some observers labeling the initiative as delayed or "jinxed" due to repeated hurdles.[6] As of October 2025, land acquisition has advanced with measurements completed for 802 acres and consent secured from over 50% of affected farmers, enabling the detailed project report (DPR) finalization targeted for September 2025.[7][8] Union Minister projections indicate potential operationalization by March 2029, positioning the airport as a catalyst for economic growth, including ancillary developments like a nearby IT park spanning 1,500 acres.[9][10] Despite these milestones, the venture's timeline remains contingent on resolving remaining landowner disputes and securing full regulatory approvals, underscoring the tensions between infrastructure imperatives and local agrarian interests.[2][6]

Background and Rationale

Existing Airport Constraints

The Pune Airport at Lohegaon features a single runway of 2,540 meters (8,330 feet) in length, oriented 10/28, which imposes operational restrictions on accommodating larger wide-body aircraft, such as those classified under ICAO code F, thereby limiting efficient long-haul international connectivity without payload penalties or extensions.[11][12] Urban encroachment surrounding the facility, combined with its dual-use status shared with the Indian Air Force station, severely constrains physical expansion, as military priorities often supersede civilian schedules, resulting in recurrent flight delays.[13] Passenger traffic at the airport has grown substantially amid Pune's economic expansion in information technology and manufacturing sectors, handling 10.5 million passengers in fiscal year 2024-25, a 10.66% increase from 9.52 million the prior year, approaching or exceeding the infrastructure's sustainable capacity of approximately 9-10 million annually.[14][15] This surge strains terminal and apron facilities, with recent expansions adding only marginal relief through a new integrated terminal building of 36,000 square meters, insufficient to match projected demand growth.[16] Additional operational challenges include heightened bird strike risks due to proximity to urban and agricultural areas, with 145 incidents documented since 2020, prompting diversions, delays, and enhanced mitigation efforts like high-decibel deterrents by the Indian Air Force.[17][18] These factors collectively hinder the airport's ability to support Pune's aviation needs, necessitating a greenfield alternative to alleviate congestion and enable scalable growth.[19]

Strategic Need for Expansion

Pune's metropolitan population reached approximately 7.5 million in 2025, positioning it as Maharashtra's second-largest urban agglomeration after Mumbai and a key driver of the state's economic output.[20] The city's economy, fueled by information technology, manufacturing, and global capability centers, has sustained an average annual GDP growth of around 8.3%, contributing significantly to exports and industrial expansion.[21] This trajectory demands enhanced air connectivity to facilitate business travel, cargo logistics for automotive and pharmaceutical sectors, and tourism inflows, with projections indicating sustained demand for airport capacity exceeding current infrastructure limits. The proposed new airport at Purandar is designed to achieve an initial capacity of 25 million passengers per annum (MPPA), scalable to 75 MPPA, addressing the anticipated surge in air traffic aligned with India's national aviation growth projections of 9% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) through FY2027.[1][8][22] Developed under a public-private partnership (PPP) model, it supports the government's broader infrastructure push, including elements of the UDAN regional connectivity scheme, by decongesting the existing Lohegaon facilityâ€”which handled over 10 million passengers annually by 2024â€”and enabling efficient handling of peak-hour demands from Pune's expanding industrial corridors.[1][23] Upgraded airport infrastructure correlates with increased foreign direct investment (FDI) inflows, as evidenced by Hyderabad's Rajiv Gandhi International Airport, where enhanced connectivity spurred a 74% rise in surrounding real estate values and bolstered sectors like IT and logistics since its 2008 operationalization.[24] Similar causal dynamics apply to Pune, where improved aviation access would amplify FDI in manufacturing hubs, countering bottlenecks that currently constrain export-oriented growth without prioritizing undeveloped land retention over urban economic imperatives.[25]

Project Initiation and Planning

Initial Proposals and Site Selection

Proposals for a greenfield international airport to serve Pune and western Maharashtra originated in the early 2010s, driven by the need to expand aviation infrastructure amid rising passenger traffic and regional economic growth.[26] The Maharashtra Airport Development Company (MADC), a state-owned entity, led the initial planning, identifying the necessity for a new facility capable of handling up to 75 million passengers per annum through a public-private partnership model.[1] Early site evaluations focused on logistical feasibility, including proximity to urban centers, terrain suitability for large-scale construction, and minimal environmental disruptions. Chakan, initially proposed, was rejected due to its mountainous topography, which posed engineering challenges for runway development and increased costs.[27] [28] Alternative locations such as Khed faced scrutiny for higher acquisition expenses and vulnerability to flooding, as assessed in MADC's feasibility analyses prioritizing economic viability and operational safety.[29] In May 2018, the Ministry of Civil Aviation granted site clearance for Purandar, selected for its flat terrain, approximately 40 km distance from Pune's city center facilitating connectivity, and strategic positioning to support regional logistics without excessive infrastructure demands.[30] [31] This decision aligned with criteria emphasizing cost-effective development and integration with existing transport networks. The airport was officially designated Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport in 2023, honoring Sambhaji Maharaj, the Maratha king born at Purandar Fort, to underscore the site's historical ties to regional heritage.[19]

Feasibility and Environmental Assessments

The feasibility study for the proposed Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport in Purandar was conducted by Dorsch Gruppe on behalf of the Maharashtra Airport Development Company (MADC), encompassing technical viability, economic projections, and preliminary environmental and social impact analyses to address the demand-supply gap at Pune's existing airport.[32] Earlier assessments by MADC in collaboration with the Airports Authority of India (AAI) evaluated multiple sites, confirming Purandar's suitability based on terrain, connectivity, and aviation constraints like runway orientation and length, with two parallel runways planned at 4,000 x 60 meters.[33][34] These studies projected a capacity of 75 million passengers per annum (MPPA) under a public-private partnership (PPP) model, prioritizing long-term infrastructure needs over localized agricultural dependencies.[1] Environmental evaluations have highlighted groundwater challenges in Purandar taluka, where the Groundwater Survey and Development Agency reported 91% extraction rates as of 2020, classifying the area as "critical" and deeming several villages unfit for additional withdrawals due to over-reliance on existing agrarian use.[35] Proponents counter potential shortages by citing sustainable sourcing from proximate reservoirs, including the Bhadgar Dam and Nira-Deoghar systems, which provide parallel water infrastructure less burdened by current depletion.[36] Biodiversity and wetland impacts remain undetailed in public reports, though site-specific mitigation is implied in ongoing planning to minimize disruption beyond prevailing agricultural runoff effects. No comprehensive environmental impact assessment (EIA) clearance specific to the airport has been publicly confirmed as of 2025, with project advancement focused on land surveys amid these hydrological realities.[1] Geotechnical assessments affirm soil stability for airport infrastructure, with Purandar's basaltic terrain supporting heavy loads absent liquefaction vulnerabilities noted in more alluvial zones.[34] Seismically, the site lies in Zone III per Indian standards, carrying a 10% probability of potentially damaging shaking over 50 years, indicative of moderate rather than high risk and manageable through standard engineering protocols.[37] Cost-benefit projections under PPP emphasize economic returns from enhanced regional connectivity, though specific internal rate of return (IRR) figures are not disclosed; the model's viability hinges on phased development attracting private investment for 2,832 hectares of land.[1] These findings underscore empirical viability against claims of inherent unsuitability, prioritizing data-driven mitigations over unsubstantiated ecological catastrophe narratives.

Regulatory Approvals

Government and Aviation Clearances

The Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) issued site clearance for the proposed greenfield airport at Purandar in June 2024, confirming the site's suitability and compliance with aviation standards, including provisions for two parallel runways each measuring 4,000 meters by 60 meters.[31][38] This approval addressed prior technical evaluations, enabling progression toward construction while ensuring operational viability for international flights.[1] The clearance process benefited from streamlined regulatory procedures under the current central administration, which has prioritized rapid approvals for greenfield projects to meet surging air traffic demands, as seen in the expedited handling of site verifications post-2023 policy frameworks.[39] The Ministry of Civil Aviation's in-principle nod for such initiatives, including capacity enhancements in the Pune region, aligned the project with national aviation expansion goals announced in 2023. Designed for an ultimate capacity of 75 million passengers per annum (MPPA) under a public-private partnership (PPP) model, the airport integrates into India's broader airport master plan by focusing on Pune's underserved hinterland, distinct from Mumbai's parallel developments and upgrades to the existing Lohegaon facility.[1] This positioning avoids capacity overlaps, emphasizing complementary growth in western India's aviation network.

Defense Ministry Involvement

The Ministry of Defence (MoD) issued a no-objection certificate (NOC) for the proposed Purandar site on August 12, 2021, acknowledging the site's viability despite its proximity to the Indian Air Force (IAF) base at Lohegaon, approximately 20-25 kilometers away.[30] This initial approval followed assessments of potential airspace overlaps, as the new airport's runways and flight corridors needed to avoid interfering with military training and operational flights from Lohegaon.[40] In January 2022, the MoD revoked the NOC, citing unresolved airspace constraints that could compromise IAF operations, including risks to low-level flying routes and radar coverage in the region.[30][41] The decision emphasized national security priorities, requiring demonstrable mitigation of flight path conflicts before any civilian development could proceed near sensitive military assets.[42] Resolution came through iterative technical revisions, including updated flight path modeling to segregate civilian and military traffic corridors, which addressed the IAF's concerns about operational interference.[42] The MoD reinstated the NOC in May 2024 for the original Purandar site, confirming that the revised configurations ensured no dilution of defense capabilities.[43] This approval facilitated subsequent Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) clearance in June 2024, with provisions for ongoing IAF-MoD oversight on airspace protocols.[44] Coordination between the MoD, IAF, and civil aviation authorities focuses on integrated airspace management, including real-time data sharing for surveillance and de-confliction procedures, without mandating full handover of military infrastructure. Such measures prioritize causal security imperativesâ€”ensuring military primacyâ€”while permitting civilian expansion, as evidenced by the continued operation of Pune's Lohegaon Airport, where a civil enclave handles over 10 million passengers annually alongside IAF activities since the 1960s.[45] This approach aligns with empirical precedents at other Indian airports, such as Chandigarh and Srinagar, where dual-use facilities integrate civil flights via coordinated NOTAMs (Notices to Airmen) and radar feeds, sustaining military readiness amid growing civilian traffic without incident.[46] At least 23 such IAF or Navy bases routinely support civil operations through similar protocols, demonstrating that targeted technical adjustments can overcome initial objections rather than indefinite deferrals.[47]

Land Acquisition Process

Acquisition Mechanisms and Compensation

The land acquisition for the New Pune Airport, spanning approximately 2,800 acres in Purandar taluka, operates under the Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013, which mandates enhanced compensation, rehabilitation, and resettlement measures for affected landowners, primarily farmers.[48] This framework replaces prior colonial-era laws by requiring social impact assessments and prioritizing consent where feasible, with compensation calculated at four times the registered market value for rural agricultural land to reflect opportunity costs and ensure financial viability for voluntary participation.[49][50] The Maharashtra Airport Development Company (MADC) and Pune district administration have employed a hybrid mechanism blending voluntary consent incentives with compulsory acquisition provisions to expedite the process. Consenting farmers receive not only the statutory compensation but also an additional 10% of their surrendered land returned as developed plots within the planned Aerocity zone, fostering long-term economic integration over pure cash payouts.[51][52] Non-consenting landowners face mandatory acquisition post-deadline, limited to base compensation without plot allotments, as stipulated under Section 19 of the Act, to balance project urgency against individual rights.[53] By mid-September 2025, over 72% of affected landowners had submitted consent forms through direct negotiations led by district officials, surpassing initial targets and enabling land surveys to commence shortly thereafter.[54] This high uptake reflects targeted outreach emphasizing immediate payouts to primary owners, bypassing intermediaries to minimize disputes and delays common in prior acquisitions.[55] Rehabilitation entitlements under the Act further include resettlement assistance and linkages to state skill development programs, though project-specific affidavits from the Maharashtra government prioritize verifiable monetary and plot-based relief over expansive training mandates.[50] The overall package, involving billions of rupees in disbursements, underscores a market-linked approach calibrated to local land values, with funds slated for phased release following final verifications.[49]

Farmer Consents and Progress

The land acquisition process for the Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport in Purandar relied on voluntary consent from farmers, with a dedicated drive commencing on August 25, 2025, under the supervision of Pune District Collector Jitendra Dudi.[56] By early September 2025, consents had been secured for approximately 50% of the required land, equivalent to over 1,000 farmers submitting letters for about 1,450 acres.[57] This momentum accelerated, reaching nearly 90% consent by mid-September, as 2,800 farmers agreed to surrender 2,700 acres across seven villages. Subsequent updates confirmed over 93% participation, with 3,220 farmers providing consents for 2,810 acres by late September 2025, enabling the transition to land surveys.[58] District administration reports indicated that holdout rates fell below 7%, attributing residual opposition to localized protests rather than systemic coercion, as evidenced by the high voluntary turnout during the consent camps.[59] By October 2025, 100% of private land acquisition consents were finalized, underscoring the process's reliance on farmer initiative over mandatory measures.[60] Land surveys progressed in parallel, with measurement of 1,285 hectares across the affected villages completed by late October 2025, facilitating precise documentation of fields, wells, and assets prior to compensation disbursement.[61] To sustain this pace amid competing administrative duties, exemptions from poll-related assignments were sought for land acquisition officials, prioritizing infrastructure advancement.[61] This administrative focus, combined with transparent consent mechanisms, yielded participation rates that district records describe as predominantly self-motivated, countering narratives of widespread reluctance.[7]

Design and Technical Specifications

Airport Layout and Infrastructure

The Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport in Purandar features a dual parallel runway system, each measuring 4,000 meters in length and 60 meters in width, designed to enable simultaneous operations and enhance throughput efficiency.[1] This configuration supports instrument approaches and accommodates a range of aircraft types, including wide-body models compatible with ICAO Code E and F standards due to the extended runway dimensions.[34] An expansive apron area is planned to accommodate parking for over 100 aircraft, facilitating high-volume ground handling and minimizing turnaround times.[1] Passenger terminal infrastructure is structured for modular, phased expansion, with initial domestic and international facilities built to integrate seamlessly as demand scales, incorporating efficient passenger flow designs such as separate levels for arrivals and departures.[62] Cargo operations center on a dedicated terminal equipped with modern handling systems, including cold storage and automated sorting, to support Pune's manufacturing and export sectors.[1] The layout emphasizes scalability, with provisions for future additions like maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) bays and flying training facilities adjacent to the primary aviation zones.[1] Ground access integrates multimodal links, including direct connections to National Highway 48 (Mumbai-Pune Expressway) and National Highway 65 (to Solapur), supplemented by a proposed six-lane airport ring road for efficient vehicular ingress.[8] Rail connectivity is planned via a dedicated spur from the nearby Rajewadi station on the Satara route, enabling seamless passenger and freight transfers to Pune's urban core and regional networks.[63] This infrastructure blueprint prioritizes operational resilience, with utilities and support systems positioned to avoid interference with flight paths.

Capacity and Operational Features

The Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj Purandar International Airport is planned with an ultimate capacity of 75 million passengers per annum (MPPA), developed through phased expansion on 2,832 hectares of land under a public-private partnership model.[1] This scalability prioritizes modular growth, allowing initial operations to ramp up efficiently before full build-out, with minimal additional land requirements beyond the allocated site for achieving the target throughput.[1] The design addresses capacity constraints at the existing Lohegaon Airport, which handles only about 9 MPPA amid frequent congestion.[64] Key operational features include two parallel runways, each 4,000 meters long and 60 meters wide, configured for simultaneous takeoffs and landings to maximize aircraft movements and reduce delays.[1] The apron infrastructure supports parking for over 100 aircraft, facilitating rapid turnaround times and accommodating wide-body jets alongside regional carriers.[1] As a greenfield project, the site's rural location in Purandar taluka inherently mitigates urban bird strike risks prevalent at Lohegaon, where 145 incidents occurred since 2020 due to nearby waste and habitats.[65] The terminal will integrate modern passenger processing, including biometric systems akin to DigiYatra for facial recognition at entry, security, and boardingâ€”features already standardizing across Indian airports to streamline flows and rival metro hubs like Mumbai's 50+ MPPA capacity.[66] Supporting infrastructure encompasses a dedicated cargo terminal for high-volume freight handling and maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) facilities, enabling on-site aircraft servicing to boost operational efficiency and attract aviation ancillary services.[1] A flying training organization will further utilize the airspace for pilot programs, enhancing long-term throughput without external dependencies.[1]

Construction Timeline and Progress

Key Milestones and Phases

The proposal for a new greenfield international airport in Purandar, Pune district, was announced in September 2016, with the site selected to address capacity constraints at the existing Lohegaon facility.[67] The project envisions development through a public-private partnership model, spanning approximately 2,400 hectares and designed for an ultimate capacity of 75 million passengers per annum.[1][19] In January 2025, Maharashtra Chief Minister Devendra Fadnavis directed the Maharashtra Industrial Development Corporation to initiate land acquisition proceedings, allocating roughly â‚¹1,000 crore for this phase covering about 1,400 acres of primarily agricultural land.[68][69] Land surveys concluded by October 16, 2025, with compensation disbursements starting mid-November, targeting full acquisition by year-end.[70] The Detailed Project Report is scheduled for finalization by September 2025, paving the way for construction commencement in 2026.[9] Development will proceed in staged phases aligned with national capital expenditure priorities for aviation infrastructure. Initial phases focus on constructing two parallel runways measuring 4,000 by 60 meters, an apron for over 100 aircraft, and a basic passenger terminal to enable early operations.[1] Subsequent expansions post-initial commissioning will incorporate dedicated cargo terminals and maintenance, repair, and overhaul facilities, with full build-out supporting enhanced regional connectivity.[1] Operational readiness is projected for March 2029.[9]

Delays and Current Developments

The development of Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj International Airport has encountered multiple delays, primarily stemming from regulatory setbacks and landowner resistance. In January 2022, the Ministry of Defence revoked its no-objection certificate for the proposed site in Purandar taluka, citing unresolved concerns over airspace and proximity to military installations, which stalled site clearance and planning efforts.[30] Farmer holdouts further exacerbated timelines, with protests escalating in May 2025 during drone surveys across affected villages, resulting in clashes between demonstrators and authorities over perceived inadequate initial compensation offers.[71] These obstructions, including demands for higher payouts and return of developed land, delayed land acquisition processes and shifted the projected construction start from earlier deadlines to mid-2026.[72] Recent accelerations have mitigated prior stagnation. By September 2025, district authorities secured consents from approximately 90% of affected farmers for the reduced 3,000-acre requirement, facilitated by a revised package offering compensation at four times market value, plus 10% land return as developed plots in an adjacent AeroCity and support for landless families equivalent to 25 months of minimum wages.[73] [74] Joint land measurements concluded by mid-October 2025, covering over 600 hectares of private land surveyed earlier in the year, with full surveys enabling compensation rollout starting November 15, 2025.[75] [56] Post-monsoon activities, including tendering, position construction to ramp up by mid-2026, per Maharashtra Airport Development Company updates, countering narratives of indefinite delays through empirical progress in consents from over 1,000 farmers and secured government-held land.[76]
