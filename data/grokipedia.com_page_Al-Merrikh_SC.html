Al Merrikh SC


Al-Merreikh Sporting Club (Arabic: نادي المريخ الرياضي) is a professional association football club based in Omdurman, Sudan, that competes in the Sudan Premier League, the highest level of Sudanese football. Founded on 14 November 1927, the club is one of the oldest and most prominent in African football, with a history rooted in early 20th-century Sudanese sports organization.[1][2] Al-Merreikh has achieved dominance in domestic competitions, securing 23 Sudanese championships across various formats since the inaugural national title in 1934, often sharing supremacy with arch-rival Al-Hilal, as the duo has claimed nearly every league crown in modern eras.[2] The club's red and white colors symbolize its identity, supported by a fervent fanbase that contributes to the intense Khartoum Derby rivalry, a cornerstone of Sudanese sporting culture. While yet to claim a major continental trophy, Al-Merreikh's consistent national success underscores its status as a powerhouse amid Sudan's challenging football landscape, including recent disruptions from civil conflict prompting exiled competitions.[3][2][4]

History

Founding and early years (1908–1960)

Al-Merreikh Sporting Club traces its origins to 1908, when it was established as Al-Masalma Sporting Club by students from the Al-Masalmah district in Omdurman, Sudan.[5] The club initially focused on fostering local football talent amid the colonial-era development of organized sports in Sudan.[6] In 1927, following a meeting that emphasized a more nationalistic identity, the club was renamed Al-Merrikh Sporting Club on November 14, reflecting "the soldier" in Arabic and symbolizing resilience and discipline.[7] This rebranding marked a shift toward broader Sudanese representation, distancing from district-specific roots.[8] During the 1920s and early 1930s, Al-Merrikh participated in nascent regional leagues, such as the Khartoum State League, which featured around 14 clubs by the mid-1930s.[2] The club's early prominence grew alongside rivals like Al-Hilal, positioning it as an "uncrowned king" of Sudanese football.[2] In 1934, Al-Merrikh claimed victory in the inaugural Sudanese national championship, defeating competitors in a tournament that signified the formalization of inter-club competition under the Sudan Football Association.[2] This success underscored the club's organizational strength and player development in an era when football infrastructure, including venues like Omdurman Stadium (opened 1935), was rudimentary.[2] By the late 1930s, Sudanese football expanded, with up to 36 clubs active in the 1936/37 season, though national titles remained sporadic due to regional structures.[2] Al-Merrikh sustained its competitive edge through the 1940s and 1950s, navigating post-World War II disruptions and colonial administration influences on sports governance.[2] In the Khartoum Premier League—established in 1951 as a playoff among provincial champions—Al-Merrikh secured titles in 1953 and 1955, demonstrating dominance in urban football ahead of Sudan's independence in 1956.[2] These victories highlighted the club's role in elevating standards, though records from this period rely on association archives amid limited documentation.[2]

Rise to national prominence (1960s–1980s)

During the 1960s, Al-Merrikh SC transitioned from regional contender to a national powerhouse in Sudanese football, frequently challenging for honors in the evolving national championship structure, which featured playoffs among provincial winners. The club secured the Sudan Cup in 1963 and finished as runners-up in the national championship in 1964, 1967, and 1969, often facing rival Al-Hilal Omdurman in key matches that underscored the emerging intensity of their derby clashes.[2][9] This period marked Al-Merrikh's growing infrastructure and fanbase in Omdurman, positioning it alongside Al-Hilal as one of Sudan's elite clubs amid the sport's professionalization post-independence.[10] The 1970s represented Al-Merrikh's zenith of domestic dominance, with the club capturing seven national league titles in 1970, 1971, 1972, 1973, 1975, 1977, and 1978, including consecutive wins from 1970 to 1973.[2] Complementing these successes, Al-Merrikh won the Sudan Cup in 1970, 1971, and 1972, achieving doubles in the first three of those years and solidifying its status as the era's preeminent force.[9] These triumphs, frequently over Al-Hilal in finals or decisive fixtures, intensified the "Khartoum Derby" rivalry, which drew massive crowds and defined Sudanese football's competitive landscape, with the two clubs collectively monopolizing titles.[11] Into the 1980s, Al-Merrikh maintained its prominence despite intermittent league disruptions, clinching the national championship in 1982 and 1985 while securing five consecutive Sudan Cup victories from 1983 to 1986, plus another in 1988.[2][9] Administrative stability, exemplified by leadership from figures like Central Bank Governor Mahdi al-Faki, supported sustained competitiveness, though Al-Hilal edged ahead in some league editions.[12] By decade's end, Al-Merrikh's tally of league and cup hauls in this era cemented its role in the duopoly that has since controlled over 90% of Sudanese Premier League titles.[11]

International success and domestic dominance (1990s–2010s)

During the 1990s, Al-Merreikh SC solidified its position as one of Sudan's premier football clubs, securing multiple Sudan Premier League titles including those in the 1990/91, 1992/93, 1993/94, 1995/96, and 1996/97 seasons.[13] This era marked the beginning of a duopoly with rivals Al-Hilal, where the two clubs alternated victories and prevented any other team from claiming the league crown since 1992.[14] Complementing these league successes, the club won the Sudan Cup in 1989/90 and 1992/93, enhancing its domestic trophy cabinet.[13] Entering the 2000s, Al-Merreikh continued its dominance with Sudan Premier League triumphs in 1999/00, 2000/01, 2004/05, 2005/06, 2006/07, and 2007/08, alongside Sudan Cup victories in 2000/01 and 2001/02.[13] The club's consistent qualification for continental competitions stemmed from these national achievements, reflecting superior squad depth and tactical consistency compared to other Sudanese sides.[13] By the 2010s, further league titles in 2009/10, 2011/12, 2012/13, 2013/14, and 2014/15, plus cups in 2010/11, 2012/13, and 2014/15, underscored sustained excellence amid growing infrastructure investments.[13] Internationally, Al-Merreikh made frequent appearances in the CAF Champions League as Sudanese champions, participating in multiple editions throughout the period, though without reaching the final.[15] A highlight came in the CAF Confederation Cup, where the club advanced to the 2007 final after navigating group stages and knockout rounds, demonstrating competitive prowess against African peers before falling short in the decisive match.[15] Earlier continental efforts included quarter-final exits in the CAF Champions League during select campaigns, bolstered by key players and coaching strategies adapted to regional travel and varying pitch conditions.[15] These runs elevated the club's profile, attracting regional talent and fostering rivalries with North and West African outfits.[15]

Recent challenges amid political instability (2020s)

The outbreak of civil war in Sudan on April 15, 2023, between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces severely disrupted domestic football, including operations for Al-Merrikh SC, based in Omdurman where intense fighting occurred.[16] The conflict, which has resulted in over 150,000 deaths and widespread displacement, led to the suspension of the Sudan Premier League and forced top clubs to relocate matches and training abroad to sustain competition.[16] Al-Merrikh, alongside rival Al-Hilal, temporarily joined the Mauritanian Super D1 for the 2024-25 season, finishing sixth after competing against local teams in a foreign league structure unfamiliar to Sudanese players.[17] [4] In July 2025, amid partial lulls in fighting, the league briefly resumed domestically as the Sudan Elite League, with Al-Merrikh hosting matches in Khartoum; however, they suffered a 4-0 defeat to Al-Hilal on July 22, which clinched the title for the latter and highlighted logistical strains from damaged infrastructure and security risks.[18] [19] Persistent violence, including renewed clashes in Omdurman, prevented sustained home operations, prompting Al-Merrikh's participation in continental competitions like the CAF Champions League to be affected by venue changes and postponements, such as the September 13, 2025, deferral of their match against Saint-Louisienne.[20] By October 2025, escalating instability compelled Al-Merrikh, Al-Hilal, and Al-Ahli Wad Madani to seek affiliation with the Rwanda Premier League for the 2025-26 season, approved by the Rwanda Football Federation to preserve competitive play amid Sudan's humanitarian crisis, which has displaced millions and crippled local sports facilities.[21] [22] This relocation underscores broader challenges for the club, including player safety concerns, disrupted youth academies, and financial strains from forfeited home revenues, though their role in sustaining the national team's qualifiers has been credited with maintaining some football infrastructure.[23][24]

Club identity and facilities

Name, crest, and colors

Al-Merrikh Sporting Club derives its name from the Arabic term "Al-Merrikh," meaning the planet Mars, chosen to evoke the celestial body's reddish appearance and symbolizing vigor and prominence as the first club in Sudan and the Arab world to adopt this nomenclature.[25] The club's crest features a shield-shaped emblem with a central five-pointed red star, outlined in black to represent Mars, accompanied by the text "ALMERREIKH SC" in white lettering below the star.[26] Al-Merrikh's primary colors are red and white, reflecting the club's nickname "Red Devils" and the "Red Castle" moniker of their home stadium; red dominates home kits, signifying intensity and historical identity, while white serves as the secondary tone.[27][28] Yellow has traditionally appeared in alternate kits but was officially retired for the 2024/25 season to emphasize the core red-white palette.[29]

Home stadium and training grounds

Al-Merrikh SC's home stadium is Al-Merrikh Stadium, situated in Omdurman, Sudan. Locally known as the "Red Castle," the multi-purpose venue has a reported capacity of 43,000 and was established in 1962.[30] [31] It primarily hosts football matches for the club and has occasionally accommodated fixtures for the Sudan national team. The stadium complex supports the club's training activities, including sessions for football and athletics, though dedicated training grounds beyond the main pitch are not extensively documented in available records. Since the Sudanese civil war erupted in April 2023, displacing operations from Omdurman due to security risks and infrastructure damage, Al-Merrikh has relied on temporary foreign venues for both matches and training. The team competed in Mauritania's Super D1 for the 2024–25 season, utilizing Cheikha Ould Boïdiya Stadium in Nouakchott.[24] As of October 2025, following acceptance into Rwanda's Premier League amid ongoing conflict, training has shifted to facilities in Rwanda, such as those in Huye District, to maintain competitive readiness.[17][32]

Organisation and management

Governance and ownership structure

Al-Merreikh SC operates as a member-based sporting club governed by a board of directors elected through general assemblies, which holds authority over strategic decisions, including coach appointments, contract terminations, and disciplinary measures for officials. On January 8, 2025, the board mutually terminated the contracts of head coach Giovanni Solinas and his staff amid performance reviews.[33] In June 2024, the board unanimously dismissed Al-Siddiq Haftar, son of Libyan general Khalifa Haftar, from his role as honorary president due to his affiliations with Sudan's Rapid Support Forces, highlighting the board's role in maintaining neutrality amid the country's civil conflict.[34] Ownership is attributed to Sudanese businessman Hazim Mustafa, a UAE-based entrepreneur with significant financial influence over the club, who was elected president via general assembly but faced suspension following an internal probe into club affairs around 2021.[35][36] This structure reflects traditional African football associations, where boards balance member elections with influential patrons, though disputes over leadership legitimacy have led to arbitration, such as the 2022 Court of Arbitration for Sport case affirming Mustafa's election amid challenges from rival factions.[36] The club's governance has been strained by Sudan's ongoing political instability since 2023, prompting temporary relocations and adaptations in league participation.[24]

Current coaching staff

As of October 2025, Darko Nović is the head coach of Al-Merrikh SC.[37] The 53-year-old Serbian, appointed on August 29, 2025, succeeded a series of short-term coaches amid the club's displacement due to Sudan's civil war, with his contract extending to June 30, 2026.[38] Nović arrived with his backroom staff to oversee preparations, including continental competitions where the team was eliminated from the 2025/26 CAF Champions League under his guidance on October 4, 2025.[39] Specific details on assistant coaches, goalkeeping coaches, or fitness trainers remain unreported in recent verified sources, reflecting limited public disclosure during the club's transitional period playing abroad in Mauritania's Super D1 league.[38]

Notable former players and achievements

Hamid Brimah, a Sudanese goalkeeper and long-time captain of Al-Merrikh SC, is widely regarded as one of the club's most legendary figures. Selected by the Confederation of African Football (CAF) as one of the top ten players on the continent, Brimah played a pivotal role in Al-Merrikh's 1989 victory in the African Cup Winners' Cup, also known as the Mandela Cup, and contributed to regional successes including the CECAFA Club Championship.[43][44] Essam El-Hadary, the renowned Egyptian international goalkeeper, joined Al-Merrikh SC from 2012 to 2013. During his tenure, he helped secure the 2012–13 Sudanese Premier League title and the Sudanese Cup, adding to his extensive career honors that include four Africa Cup of Nations triumphs with Egypt and multiple domestic titles elsewhere.[45][46] Other notable former players include Abd-Alamjed Othman, a prolific Sudanese striker celebrated for his goal-scoring prowess in the mid-20th century, and Boraî Ahmed El Bashir (1932–2012), an early club stalwart who represented Sudan internationally. Sudanese midfielder Faisal Agab, active in the 1990s and 2000s, also earned recognition for his contributions to national team efforts and club campaigns.[47][48]

Rivalries

Rivalry with Al-Hilal SC

The rivalry between Al-Merrikh SC and Al-Hilal SC, commonly known as the Sudanese Derby or Omdurman Derby, represents the most prominent contest in Sudanese football, featuring the two dominant clubs from Omdurman.[49][11] These matches draw intense national interest due to the clubs' historical supremacy in the Sudan Premier League, where they have collectively secured all but four championships since the league's establishment in 1965.[11] Head-to-head encounters have been closely contested, with statistics from domestic and continental fixtures indicating a balanced record; for instance, across 41 documented meetings tracked by sports analytics, Al-Hilal holds 12 victories, while draws and Al-Merrikh wins fill the remainder, reflecting mutual competitiveness.[50] The derbies often influence league outcomes, as seen in the 2024/25 Sudan Elite League, where Al-Hilal's 4-0 victory over Al-Merrikh on July 22, 2025, clinched the title in a war-disrupted season played domestically to restore normalcy.[19][49] The fixture has extended to African competitions, heightening its stakes. In the 2009 CAF Champions League group stage, their opening clash in Omdurman ended 0-0, while Al-Merrikh claimed a 2-1 win over Al-Hilal in a 2022 CAF Champions League preliminary match held in Cairo.[51][52] Earlier continental derbies, such as in the 2012 CAF Confederation Cup, further underscore the rivalry's endurance beyond national borders.[53] Despite Sudan's ongoing civil conflict, these matches persist as symbols of resilience, fostering unity among fans.[11]

Supporters and culture

Fan base and attendance

Al-Merrikh SC maintains one of the largest and most passionate fan bases in Sudanese football, rivaled primarily by Al-Hilal SC, with supporters demonstrating strong loyalty despite the country's ongoing civil war and displacement of populations. The club's popularity stems from its historical success and central role in the Khartoum Derby, Sudan's most prominent football rivalry, which unites and divides fans nationwide, fostering a culture of fervent support even in exile.[11] Supporters have adapted to disruptions by organizing viewings for away matches played abroad, such as in Mauritania and Egypt, where groups gather to follow games via broadcasts, underscoring the depth of allegiance beyond physical attendance.[54] While organized ultras groups are not prominently documented, fan expressions include chants, flags, and communal celebrations, contributing to the club's identity as a symbol of resilience in a war-torn context.[55] The Al-Merrikh Stadium in Omdurman holds about 35,000 spectators, serving as a key venue for high-turnout matches when security permits play in Sudan; however, comprehensive attendance records remain scarce due to inconsistent reporting in the Sudanese Premier League and conflict-related relocations.[1] In recent seasons, including the 2025 return of limited home games amid downsized competitions, derbies and continental fixtures have drawn notable crowds relative to available capacities abroad, though exact figures are not systematically tracked.[56]

Traditions and fan groups

The primary organized supporter group for Al-Merrikh SC is Ultras Olympus Mons, an ultras collective that occupies the Curva Sud section of the club's stadium and is noted for its coordinated displays of chants, tifos, and pyrotechnics during matches.[57] This group, established around 2013, emphasizes intense vocal support and has produced audio tracks and videos of matchday anthems tailored to the club, fostering a "volcano-like" atmosphere in the stands.[58] [57] Supporter traditions include collective rehearsals for chants and songs, often shared via social media prior to fixtures, as well as banners expressing solidarity with broader causes such as Palestinian rights and opposition to institutional corruption within Sudanese football governance.[57] During the 2021 CAF Confederation Cup match against Al Ahly SC, the club allocated 80% of available tickets to Ultras Olympus Mons members to maximize organized backing, highlighting their central role in fan mobilization.[59] Chants frequently reference club loyalty and rivalry intensity, with examples including calls for player trust amid competitive pressures, though specific lyrics often adapt to contemporary events. Beyond matchdays, Ultras Olympus Mons has integrated football support with public demonstrations, transforming stadium gatherings into sites for political expression during Sudan's 2018–2019 revolution and subsequent unrest, such as anti-coup protests featuring chants like "Blood must be avenged with blood."[57] This fusion reflects a cultural tradition among Sudanese ultras where fan identity intersects with civic activism, though it has occasionally led to tensions with authorities and rival supporters from Al-Hilal SC.[57]

Honours and achievements

Domestic competitions

Al-Merrikh SC has secured 23 Sudanese Premier League titles, establishing it as one of the most successful clubs in Sudanese football history.[2] These victories span from the inaugural national championship era, including early wins in the Khartoum Premier League in 1934, 1953, 1955, and 1961, to the modern national format with triumphs in 1970, 1971, 1972, 1973 (both championships that year), 1974, 1975, 1977, 1978, 1982, 1985, 1990, 1993, 1997, 2000, 2001, 2002, 2008, 2011, 2013, 2015, 2018, 2019, and 2020.[2] The club's dominance is particularly evident post-1992, during which period only Al-Merrikh and rival Al-Hilal have claimed league honors.[2] In the Sudan Cup, Al-Merrikh holds a record 25 titles, reflecting consistent knockout success.[9] Key winning years include 1963, 1970–1972, 1974, 1983–1986, 1988, 1991, 1993–1994, 1996, 2001, 2005–2008, 2010, 2012–2015, and 2018, with a potential additional claim in the disputed 1992 edition per some records.[9] These achievements underscore Al-Merrikh's prowess in domestic cup competitions, often held irregularly due to logistical challenges in Sudanese football.[9] No other major domestic honors, such as a formalized Sudanese Super Cup, are prominently recorded for Al-Merrikh, with the club's accolades centered on league and cup formats.[2][9] Recent seasons have been disrupted by the ongoing Sudanese civil war, leading to abandoned campaigns like 2023 and non-official formats in 2024, but historical tallies remain intact.[2]

Continental and regional competitions

Al-Merrikh SC secured its sole continental title by winning the 1989 African Cup Winners' Cup, defeating Nigeria's Bendel United 1-0 in the second leg of the final after a 0-0 first leg draw, for a 1-0 aggregate victory.[60][61] The triumph marked the club's most significant achievement in CAF-organized competitions, with key contributions from goalkeeper Hamid Brimah.[43] In the CAF Confederation Cup, Al-Merrikh reached the 2007 final but lost 2-5 on aggregate to Tunisia's CS Sfaxien, following a 2-4 defeat in the second leg after prevailing 4-2 in the first.[62][61] The club has made numerous appearances in the CAF Champions League since 1971, advancing to the quarter-finals in 1975 as its deepest run, though without securing the title.[61] Regionally, Al-Merrikh has claimed the CECAFA Club Championship (also known as the Kagame Cup) three times, in 1986, 1994, and 2014.[5] The 2014 edition culminated in a 1-0 final win over Rwanda's APR FC at Amahoro Stadium, with the decisive goal scored by a Sudanese forward.[63] These victories underscore the club's dominance in East and Central African club football.[64]

Competition performance

Sudanese Premier League record

Al-Merrikh SC has won the Sudanese Premier League 19 times, making it one of the competition's most successful clubs.[17] Alongside rival Al-Hilal's 31 titles, these two Omdurman-based teams have secured 50 of the 54 league championships awarded to date.[17] No other club has claimed the title since 1992, underscoring the duopoly in Sudanese domestic football.[65] The club's league successes span decades, with notable streaks including five consecutive titles from 1970 to 1974 (including a second tournament in 1974) and three in a row from 2000 to 2002.[2] Recent victories include the 2015, 2018, 2019, and 2020 seasons, the latter preceding the suspension of regular league play due to the ongoing Sudanese civil war.[2] Al-Merrikh's consistent contention for the top spots has been facilitated by strong infrastructure and talent development, though the league's format has varied over time, with some seasons featuring playoffs or regional qualifiers before standardizing in the 1990s.[2]

CAF Confederation Cup and Champions League results

Al-Merrikh SC has participated extensively in the CAF Champions League as Sudanese Premier League champions, accumulating 68 matches in the competition as of 2025. The club's deepest runs have typically ended in second rounds or group stage exits, with recent group stage form showing one win, three draws, and eight defeats in the last 12 matches. In the 2022/23 group stage (Group A), Al-Merrikh suffered a 1–3 home loss to Al Ahly SC on March 18, 2022, and a 0–1 defeat to Al-Hilal.[3][66][67] In the 2024/25 preliminary round, Al-Merrikh was eliminated by FC Saint-Éloi Lupopo of the Democratic Republic of Congo, losing 0–1 away on September 27, 2024, before drawing 0–0 at home on October 4, 2024.[68] Al-Merrikh's participation in the CAF Confederation Cup has been less frequent, with eight recorded appearances. The club's best result came in the 2006/07 season, reaching the final but losing to CS Sfaxien of Tunisia on a 2–5 aggregate (2–4 home loss in the first leg, 0–1 away loss in the second leg). In 2008, Al-Merrikh finished second in Group B during the group stage. The following seasons saw further involvement, including advancement to the intermediate round (round of 16) in 2010, where they faced AS FAN of Niger.[69][70][71]

CECAFA Club Championship performances

Al-Merrikh SC has competed in the CECAFA Club Championship, a regional tournament for clubs from East and Central African nations, on several occasions, achieving notable success with three championship titles. The club won the inaugural edition they contested in 1986, defeating Young Africans of Tanzania 2–2 (4–2 on penalties) in the final held in Tanzania.[72][73] In 1994, Al-Merrikh claimed their second title by topping their group unbeaten, which included victories over Express FC (2–1) and Simba SC (via group standings), en route to the championship in Sudan.[74][73] Their most recent victory came in 2014, ending a 20-year drought by beating hosts APR FC of Rwanda 1–0 in the final at Amahoro Stadium in Kigali, with the decisive goal scored by a Sudanese player in the second half.[64][63][75] The club reached the final three additional times but finished as runners-up. In 1987, they lost 0–1 to Uganda's Nakivubo Villa in the Uganda-hosted decider.[73] Hosting the 1988 edition in Khartoum, Al-Merrikh advanced to the final but fell 0–2 to Kenya Breweries despite strong group stage showings including a 1–1 draw with Breweries.[76][77] In 2009, as hosts in Khartoum and Omdurman, they topped Group A with wins like 1–0 over Mathare United but were defeated in the final by Rwanda's Atraco FC via a late goal from Kitagenda.[78][79][80] Al-Merrikh secured third place in the 2011 tournament in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania, by winning the playoff 2–0 against Ethiopia's Saint George SA after semi-final elimination.[81] These performances underscore the club's dominance among Sudanese entrants in the competition, which Sudan has won three times overall per historical records.[73]

Controversies and challenges

Political affiliations and government interference

Throughout its history, Al-Merrikh SC has faced repeated instances of government interference, including the appointment of high-ranking officials to administrative positions. In the 1980s, the club was led by Mahdi al-Faki, governor of the Central Bank of Sudan, exemplifying state influence over club governance.[57] Similarly, after riots during a 1976 Health Revolution Cup match against Al-Hilal SC, President Jaafar Nimeiry dissolved all sports clubs nationwide, though fan chants later accused him of favoritism toward Al-Merrikh.[57] In more recent years, the club has been affiliated with paramilitary figures, notably Rapid Support Forces (RSF) commander Mohammed Hamdan Dagalo (Hemedti), who holds the position of honorary president.[82] On March 27, 2022, Al-Merrikh ultras publicly resisted Hemedti's proposed intervention in stadium renovations, framing it as an effort to legitimize RSF actions amid ongoing political tensions.[57] An administrative crisis at the club in 2021 escalated to threaten Sudanese Football Association elections, drawing FIFA scrutiny and necessitating state judicial intervention for resolution.[83] Further illustrating external political pressures, Al-Merrikh appointed Al-Saddiq Haftar—son of Libyan general Khalifa Haftar—as honorary president in April 2023, coinciding with the club hosting matches in Benghazi to secure funding amid infrastructure issues in Omdurman; the move faced fan backlash for compromising the club's apolitical stance.[34] Haftar was dismissed in June 2024 following his vocal support for the RSF in Sudan's civil war against the Sudanese Armed Forces.[34] Earlier, in June 2022, Hemedti leveraged a meeting with Al-Saddiq Haftar to press for donations to the club after it lost Saudi sponsorship.[82]

Disqualifications from continental competitions

In July 2017, Al-Merreikh SC was disqualified from the CAF Champions League following FIFA's suspension of the Sudan Football Association (SFA) for third-party interference in its affairs, which violated FIFA statutes requiring autonomy of member associations.[84][85] The Confederation of African Football (CAF) enforced the disqualification as a direct consequence, barring Al-Merreikh and fellow Sudanese club Al-Hilal from continuing in the tournament; this decision was announced on July 7, 2017, and affected matches already underway, including a forfeit in Al-Merreikh's group stage fixtures.[86] The ban was short-lived, with FIFA lifting the SFA suspension on July 20, 2017, after assurances of restored autonomy, allowing CAF to reinstate both clubs for the remainder of the competition.[87] A similar incident occurred in September 2021, when FIFA again suspended the SFA—citing ongoing government interference and failure to hold elections—resulting in Al-Merreikh's disqualification from the 2021–22 CAF Champions League preliminary rounds alongside Al-Hilal.[88] CAF confirmed the ejection on September 17, 2021, emphasizing that clubs from suspended associations cannot participate in continental events to uphold FIFA's governance standards.[88] Unlike the 2017 case, reinstatement did not occur promptly, as the SFA suspension persisted amid Sudan's political instability, forcing Al-Merreikh to focus on domestic play without continental involvement that season.[88] These disqualifications highlight recurring administrative vulnerabilities tied to national federation issues rather than club-specific misconduct, with FIFA's interventions prioritizing structural independence over individual team merits. No further disqualifications of Al-Merreikh from CAF competitions have been recorded as of October 2025, though ongoing Sudanese instability has prompted alternative arrangements like neutral-venue hosting for eligibility in recent tournaments.[84][88]

Impact of the Sudanese civil war (2023–present)

The Sudanese civil war, erupting on April 15, 2023, between the Sudanese Armed Forces and Rapid Support Forces, prompted the indefinite suspension of the Sudanese Premier League, severely disrupting Al-Merrikh SC's operations and forcing the club to seek alternative venues for competition.[89][16] With domestic matches impossible amid widespread fighting, infrastructure damage, and displacement in Khartoum—Al-Merrikh's home base—the club relocated its activities abroad to maintain player fitness, fulfill CAF commitments, and preserve eligibility for continental tournaments.[24][90] For the 2024–25 season, Al-Merrikh joined Mauritania's Super D1 league alongside rival Al-Hilal, marking the first time Sudanese clubs competed in a foreign domestic league to sustain professional play.[91][17] There, Al-Merrikh secured victories, including an opening win, but the arrangement was temporary, aimed at keeping the team competitive rather than prioritizing titles.[16] This exile highlighted broader challenges, including logistical hurdles for travel, player recruitment amid national instability, and financial strains from disrupted sponsorships and fan attendance.[92] In July 2025, amid ongoing conflict, Sudan briefly revived elite league football with seven rounds of fixtures in safer northern areas like Atbara, allowing Al-Merrikh to return home temporarily.[16][18] The club started with a 1–0 victory over Ahly Madani but suffered a 4–0 derby loss to Al-Hilal, which clinched the title and dual-championship status across borders.[16][18] However, persistent violence prevented a full resumption, leading Al-Merrikh, Al-Hilal, and Al-Ahli Wad Madani to secure approval for the 2025–26 Rwanda Premier League, pending CAF ratification, to continue operations in a stable environment.[91][93] This serial relocation underscores the war's existential threat to Sudanese club football, with Al-Merrikh's survival hinging on international accommodations while domestic revival remains elusive.[22][94]
