Hair (Hair song)

"Hair" is the title song of the 1967 rock musical Hair: The American Tribal Love-Rock Musical, with lyrics by Gerome Ragni and James Rado and music by Galt MacDermot.[1][2] The track exuberantly extols long hair as a "freak flag" symbolizing hippie identity, freedom, and rejection of societal norms during the 1960s counterculture.[3] The song originated in the musical's off-Broadway premiere at the New York Shakespeare Festival Public Theater in October 1967, before transferring to Broadway in April 1968, where Hair became a landmark production for its integration of rock music, improvisation, and provocative themes including anti-Vietnam War sentiment, interracial relationships, and communal nudity.[4] As part of the original Broadway cast recording released in 1968, "Hair" contributed to the album's commercial success, which topped charts and sold millions, marking one of the first rock musical soundtracks to achieve mainstream pop dominance.[5] A pop rendition by The Cowsills propelled "Hair" to broader popularity, reaching number two on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1969 and earning gold certification for over one million copies sold.[6][5] The song's infectious energy and lyrical celebration of nonconformity resonated amid the era's youth rebellion, though it was inextricably linked to the musical's controversies, including public backlash against onstage nudity and depictions of drug use, which led to protests, arrests, and bans in some venues.[4] Despiteâ€”or because ofâ€”such uproar, "Hair" endures as an anthem of 1960s liberation, influencing subsequent musical theater by challenging conventions of content, form, and audience expectations.[2]

Background and Composition

Creation and Songwriters

The song "Hair" was composed by Galt MacDermot with lyrics by James Rado and Gerome Ragni as the title track for the rock musical Hair.[7][8] Rado and Ragni, both actors immersed in New York City's countercultural scene, conceived the lyrics to celebrate long hair as a symbol of rebellion against conventional norms, drawing from their encounters with hippie communities in the mid-1960s.[9] MacDermot, a Canadian-born composer known for blending jazz and popular styles, provided the upbeat, rhythmic music that propelled the song's anthemic quality, completing the score after being recruited by producer Joseph Papp in 1966.[10] Development of "Hair" began in late 1964 when Rado and Ragni, having met while performing in an off-Broadway production, started outlining the musical's script and songs amid the rising visibility of anti-war protests and youth experimentation with personal appearance.[11] The pair drew autobiographical elements into the work, with Rado's character Claude reflecting internal conflicts over draft induction, and the song itself emerging as a defiant ode to nonconformity during script workshops.[9] By 1967, the full musicalâ€”including "Hair"â€”premiered off-Broadway at the Public Theater on October 17, under Tom O'Horgan's direction, before transferring to Broadway on April 29, 1968, where the song served as a thematic opener addressing societal scrutiny of physical traits like hair length.[7] This collaborative process emphasized improvisation and real-time cultural observation, yielding a score that captured the era's raw energy without reliance on traditional Broadway structures.[10]

Lyrics and Themes

The lyrics of "Hair," written by Gerome Ragni and James Rado with music by Galt MacDermot, center on a character's exuberant defense of his long, unkempt hair against societal disapproval. The song opens with lines such as "She asks me why / I'm just a hairy guy / I'm hairy noon and night / Hair that's a fright," portraying hair as an uncontrollable, pervasive aspect of identity that defies conventional grooming norms.[12] It escalates into a celebratory anthem, urging "Flow it, show it / Long as God can grow it / My hair," and repeating the word "hair" in rhythmic incantation to evoke sensory abundanceâ€”"like a wigwam, like a wigwam"â€”while associating it with natural elements like wind, sun, and flight.[13] This structure builds from personal anecdote to communal invocation, emphasizing hair's tactile and visual exuberance as an act of defiance. Thematically, the song employs hair as a potent symbol of the 1960s counterculture's rejection of conformity, particularly the military's demand for cropped hair among draftees during the Vietnam War era. Long hair served as the hippies' "freak flag," representing not mere aesthetics but a broader ethos of personal autonomy, anti-authoritarianism, and resistance to institutional dehumanization, including opposition to conscription and the war itself.[14] Within the musical's narrative, the lyrics satirize establishment pressuresâ€”such as parental or romantic expectations for tidinessâ€”while affirming hair's role in expressing sexual liberation, racial integration, and spiritual openness, aligning with the tribe's bohemian lifestyle.[3] This symbolism drew from real hippie practices, where uncut hair signaled solidarity against "the Man" and celebrated bodily naturalness over regimented appearance.[15] Critics have noted the song's ironic undertone, as its playful enumeration of hair's ubiquity underscores a deeper critique of societal repression, though its optimism masked the era's underlying tensions like draft evasion and cultural clashes.[14]

Musical Style and Structure

The song "Hair" embodies the rock musical style that defined the production, blending high-energy rock rhythms with Broadway orchestration to capture the exuberance of 1960s counterculture.[16] Composed by Galt MacDermot with lyrics by James Rado and Gerome Ragni, it draws on MacDermot's jazz background to infuse soulful grooves and syncopated phrasing into a predominantly rock framework, requiring vocalists capable of raw, belting delivery over electric instrumentation.[17] The arrangement features a rhythm section of drums, bass, and electric guitars driving an upbeat pulse, supplemented by horns and percussion for a tribal, ensemble feel that mirrors the musical's themes of communal rebellion.[2] Structurally, "Hair" adheres to a verse-chorus format common in contemporary pop-rock, with two principal verses enumerating hair's attributesâ€”such as "shining, gleaming, streaming, flaxen, waxen"â€”before transitioning to a hook-laden chorus repeating "Hair" in escalating intensity. The progression modulates through minor keys, starting in C minor with chords like Cm-Ab-Eb, creating a hypnotic, mantra-like repetition in the chorus that builds via layered vocals and rhythmic ostinatos. This form supports the song's 3-4 minute duration in performances, allowing for improvisational extensions in live settings to heighten its anthemic quality.

Role in the Musical Hair

Placement and Performance

In the musical Hair, the title song "Hair" is positioned in Act I, immediately following the ensemble number "Dead End," which explores interpersonal tensions among the hippie tribe, and preceding "I Got Life," a high-energy affirmation led by Dionne.[18] This placement occurs after the initial establishment of characters and countercultural themes through songs like "Aquarius" and "Sodomy," positioning "Hair" as a pivotal anthem that reinforces the tribe's rejection of societal norms early in the narrative arc.[19] The song underscores the protagonists' embrace of personal expression amid the Vietnam War draft pressures, with Claude, the central figure facing conscription, leading the celebration of his long hair as a badge of nonconformity.[20] The performance features Claude as the primary vocalist, joined by Berger and the full tribe in a raucous, participatory ensemble style that embodies the musical's rock-concert atmosphere.[13] Stage directions in the script call for Claude to interrupt an ongoing orgy to initiate the song, prompting affectionate reactions from parental figures and the tribe's exuberant response with thrown glitter, sounding bells, and horns to amplify the chaotic joy.[18] In the original 1968 Broadway production, directed by Tom O'Horgan, the number involved dynamic choreography with cast members flaunting and interacting with their hair, often extending into audience engagement to blur stage boundaries, reflecting the improvisational ethos of the Public Theater off-Broadway origins.[14] This staging emphasized the song's rhythmic, gospel-inflected structure, with layered vocals and percussion driving its infectious energy, typically lasting around 2:45 minutes in cast recordings.[19] Subsequent revivals, such as the 2009 Broadway production, retained this vibrant, tribal performance while adapting for contemporary sensibilities, maintaining the focus on hair as a visceral symbol of rebellion.[21]

Symbolic Significance Within the Show

In the musical Hair, the song "Hair" functions as a declarative anthem where tribe members Claude and Berger explain to arriving tourists the profound emblematic value of their long, unkempt locks, positioning it as the central motif of countercultural defiance.[14] Long hair embodies the hippies' "freak flag," a deliberate emblem of rebellion against the era's rigid societal norms, military discipline, and the Vietnam War draft, which enforced short haircuts as a marker of conformity and subservience.[3] This symbolism underscores the tribe's rejection of dehumanizing institutions, portraying hair not merely as a style but as a visible assertion of personal autonomy and resistance to authority's homogenizing pressures.[22] The lyrics exalt hair's tactile and mystical attributesâ€”flowing, shining, and billowing like a "river in winter"â€”to evoke a sensory liberation that mirrors the show's broader advocacy for uninhibited self-expression amid political turmoil.[14] Performed early in Act I after the opening "Aquarius," the number establishes the tribe's communal identity as politically active bohemians opposing war, racism, and repression, with hair serving as a non-verbal banner that provokes confrontation and highlights generational schisms.[3] By framing hair growth as a commitment requiring time and defiance of convention, the song reinforces causal links between physical appearance and ideological stance, critiquing how enforced uniformity stifles individuality and fuels broader societal ills like violence and intolerance.[22] This motif recurs thematically, amplifying the narrative's exploration of authenticity versus assimilation, as seen in Claude's eventual draft-induced shave, which precipitates tragedy and exposes the costs of compromising one's "flag."[14]

Commercial Versions and Chart Success

The Cowsills Recording

The Cowsills, a family pop band consisting of siblings Bob, Bill, Barry, John, Susan, and Paul along with their mother Barbara, recorded a cover of "Hair" in late 1968.[5] The sessions took place over two days, likely beginning on October 1, at TTG Studios in Hollywood, utilizing a pioneering 16-track 2-inch tape machine custom-built by engineer Tom Hidley.[5] Produced by brothers Bill and Bob Cowsill, the track marked the first Cowsills single where the band performed all instrumentation themselves, including John's Ludwig drum kit, Barry's Fender Jazz Bass, Bill and Bob's acoustic guitars, Bob's 1965 Gretsch Tennessean electric guitar and 12-string acoustic, and Bob's piano and harpsichord contributions amplified through a Fender Twin Reverb.[5][23] Vocals were arranged with the family members positioned in a circle around a Neumann U47 microphone, doubled using a Leslie speaker and TTG echo chamber for effect; a separate 1:02 intro backing track was recorded to facilitate lip-sync for television appearances, with each member singing individual lines.[5] The band adapted the song from the Broadway musical Hair, infusing it with their harmonious pop style while omitting a reference to wearing hair "like Jesus wore it" to suit AM radio play standards, though this line was restored in subsequent live performances.[23] Released as a single by MGM Records in March 1969 with "What Is Happy?" on the B-side, the recording propelled the band to commercial success.[24] It peaked at number 2 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May 1969, held from the top spot by The 5th Dimension's "Aquarius/Let the Sunshine In," and reached number 1 on the Cash Box chart.[25] The track was later included on the band's live album The Cowsills in Concert, released in May 1969, which charted at number 16 on the Billboard 200.[25]

Chart Performance

The Cowsills' single "Hair," released in March 1969 by MGM Records with "What Is Happy?" as the B-side, achieved significant commercial success in the United States. It debuted on the Billboard Hot 100 at number 84 on March 15, 1969. The track reached a peak position of number 2, which it held for two weeks during the chart dated June 14, 1969, and remained on the Hot 100 for a total of 14 weeks.[26] This performance was blocked from the top spot primarily by The 5th Dimension's "Aquarius/Let the Sunshine In," a medley from the same musical Hair. The song's chart trajectory reflected the era's countercultural popularity, bolstered by the Broadway production's ongoing run and media exposure. Internationally, "Hair" also charted well, topping charts in countries including Canada and South Africa, though specific weeks on those listings varied. In the UK, it peaked at number 12 on the Singles Chart. These results underscored the single's broad appeal amid the late-1960s youth movement, despite not universally reaching number 1 outside the US Cash Box ranking, where it held the top position for two weeks.

Other Covers and Releases

Doug Parkinson in Focus released a cover of "Hair" in September 1969 as the B-side to their single "Without You" on Columbia (EMI), which peaked at number 5 on the Kent Music Report in Australia.[27][28] The 1979 film adaptation of Hair, directed by MiloÅ¡ Forman, featured the song performed primarily by Treat Williams as Berger, with contributions from Don Dacus, Dorsey Wright, and the ensemble chorus; this version appeared on the RCA Victor soundtrack album released the same year.[29] Mexican rock group Los Yaki recorded a Spanish-language adaptation in 1970, incorporating the song into their pop-rock style.[30] Additional releases include versions on international cast recordings, such as the 1969 French production single and various regional adaptations, though these did not achieve significant commercial chart success outside their local markets.[31]

Reception and Analysis

Initial Critical Response

The title song "Hair," performed as an opening number in the musical's off-Broadway premiere on October 17, 1967, and Broadway debut on April 29, 1968, was received as a spirited anthem embodying the counterculture's embrace of personal freedom through physical appearance. Critics praised its infectious rock rhythm and lyrical celebration of long hair as a rejection of societal norms, viewing it as emblematic of the show's raw energy. The song's structure, blending upbeat verses with a communal chorus, contributed to the production's immediate appeal among younger audiences attuned to the era's anti-establishment ethos.[32] Clive Barnes of The New York Times highlighted the musical's score, which featured "Hair" prominently, for its "rock and swingy" vitality and "rough, tough and lusty quality," deeming the overall work a "happy show musically" despite describing composer Galt MacDermot's style as "merely pop-rock" infused with soul and country elements.[32][33] Time magazine echoed this by noting the score's "insistent, primitive beat" that propelled songs like "Hair," providing cohesion to the otherwise episodic format.[33] In London, following the West End transfer in September 1968, Philip Hope-Wallace of The Guardian found the "tribal love-rock" elements, including the title track's boisterous tone, "funny" and "charming at times," though he critiqued the show's exhibitionism as occasionally "boringly" overwrought.[34] While some reviewers tempered enthusiasm for the lyrics' irreverenceâ€”Alan Brien of The London Sunday Times appreciated MacDermot's "basic, insistent rock beat" refined with "plangent overtones" but implied the words risked preachinessâ€”the song's musical hooks were widely credited with making Hair accessible and memorable, foreshadowing its commercial breakout via recordings.[33] This reception underscored the track's role in bridging theatrical innovation with popular music trends, though detractors saw it as symptomatic of the hippie movement's superficiality. Overall, the positive musical assessments outweighed reservations, aiding the show's longevity amid controversy over nudity and draft themes.[35]

Cultural Symbolism of Long Hair

In the song "Hair" from the 1967 musical Hair, long hair serves as a central emblem of youthful rebellion against mid-20th-century American conformity, particularly the rigid grooming standards enforced by institutions like the military and corporate America.[14] The lyrics exalt it as a natural, flowing attributeâ€”"as free and as yellow as the sun" or "black as the midnight bear"â€”contrasting it with the "short hair" associated with establishment figures, thereby framing it as a visceral rejection of authority and a celebration of individual authenticity.[36] This portrayal drew from the emerging hippie subculture, where long hair functioned as a "freak flag," a deliberate visual marker of non-participation in mainstream societal expectations.[14] The symbolism intensified amid the Vietnam War era, as long hair directly challenged the U.S. Armed Forces' grooming regulations, which mandated short cuts to symbolize discipline and uniformity among draftees.[36] By the mid-1960s, as anti-war protests escalatedâ€”with over 500,000 U.S. troops deployed by 1968â€”young men growing long hair signaled resistance to conscription and the war itself, associating cropped hair with the "military-industrial complex" critiqued in the musical. Historical analyses trace this trend's acceleration to rock musicians like The Beatles, whose shift to longer styles post-1964 tour influenced youth culture, transforming hair length into a proxy for broader anti-establishment sentiments including opposition to consumerism and sexual repression.[37] Beyond aesthetics, long hair embodied hippie ideals of peace, love, and return to nature, rejecting the "clean-cut" image tied to post-World War II prosperity and Cold War patriotism.[38] In Hair, this extends to racial and sexual liberation, with diverse "tribe" members sporting afros and flowing locks to defy segregationist norms and heteronormative constraints, though critics later noted the musical's romanticization overlooked practical backlash like workplace discrimination against long-haired individuals.[37] By 1968, when the original cast album topped charts, long hair had permeated global youth movements, from U.S. campuses to European protests, solidifying its role as a non-verbal protest against generational authority.[3]

Criticisms of the Song's Message

The song's message, which celebrates long hair as a mystical emblem of freedom, enlightenment, and the transition to the "Age of Aquarius," faced backlash from conservative commentators and cultural critics in the late 1960s for promoting effeminacy and undermining traditional gender norms. Observers frequently characterized male long hair as "disgusting, disgraceful and effeminate," linking it to perceived homosexuality and a blurring of masculine identity that challenged societal expectations of discipline and grooming.[39][37] This critique extended to the song's glorification of hair as a symbol of rebellion, which was seen as encouraging youth to prioritize superficial aesthetics over personal responsibility, hygiene, and conformity to authority.[40] Further criticism highlighted the superficiality of equating hair length with profound ideological or spiritual transformation, arguing that the symbolism quickly devolved into commodified fashion rather than substantive change. By the early 1970s, long hair had "outgrown its parameters" as a marker of authentic hippie rebellion, becoming a mere style associated with commercial exploitation and detached from deeper countercultural ideals.[41][40] Detractors contended that the song's lyrics overstated hair's cosmic significanceâ€”portraying it as "the flower of flowers" tied to astrological shiftsâ€”while ignoring empirical realities, such as the lack of evidence for the Age of Aquarius as a delineated era of peace and harmony.[42] Instead, the emphasis on appearance was faulted for masking associations with drug use and sexual promiscuity, reducing complex social dissent to a visual signifier that failed to yield lasting societal progress.[37][43]

Legacy and Media Appearances

Enduring Influence

The song "Hair," with its exuberant ode to long tresses as a conduit for touch, sight, and identityâ€”"giving you back the man you were before / Rubber cement stuck in your hair"â€”crystallized the hippie ethos of bodily autonomy and defiance against establishment grooming norms, contributing to a broader cultural shift where male long hair transitioned from fringe rebellion to mainstream acceptance by the early 1970s.[38] This portrayal drew from observations of New York City's East Village scene, embedding hair as a tactile, almost mystical extension of self that challenged military short cuts and corporate conformity.[44] Through commercial covers like The Cowsills' 1969 version, which peaked at No. 2 on the Billboard Hot 100, the song disseminated countercultural symbolism into pop radio, influencing fashion trends such as beads, fringe, and loose clothing that emphasized comfort over convention, as audiences emulated the "tribe's" aesthetic.[44][38] Its integration of rock elements into Broadway scoring helped legitimize the rock musical genre, fostering innovations in later productions that blended contemporary music with social critique.[44] Thematically, "Hair" endures in revivalsâ€”such as the 2008 Central Park production and 2009 Broadway returnâ€”as a reminder of youth's ephemerality and communal bonds amid societal upheaval, retaining relevance for its unfiltered embrace of free love and anti-war sentiment despite evolving contexts.[44] By breaking theatrical taboos on nudity and explicit themes, the song's parent musical accelerated the end of stage censorship in 1968 London, enabling frank explorations of sexuality and politics that persist in modern works.[36] Its legacy lies in normalizing discussions of personal expression through appearance, though some analyses note limited direct politico-social transformation beyond amplifying 1960s aesthetics.[38]
