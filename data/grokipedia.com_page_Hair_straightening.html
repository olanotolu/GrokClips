Hair straightening

![Hair straighteners](./assets/Hair_straighteners_333
Hair straightening encompasses cosmetic procedures that reshape curly, wavy, or coily hair into a straighter configuration by disrupting hydrogen bonds through heat or breaking disulfide bonds via chemical agents, thereby altering the structural proteins within the hair shaft.[1] These methods range from temporary thermal applications, such as flat irons operating at temperatures up to 230°C to temporarily hydrolyze keratin, to semi-permanent keratin infusions and permanent relaxers using alkaline compounds like sodium hydroxide.[1][2] Historically, rudimentary straightening techniques trace back to ancient civilizations, including Egyptian use of alkaline pastes and Roman heated rods, but widespread adoption in the modern era emerged in the early 20th century with the hot comb patented by African American inventors and the development of chemical relaxers amid cultural pressures for straightened hair among Black communities.[3][4] Chemical relaxers, which permanently restructure hair by cleaving cysteine residues in keratin, became staples in the United States by the mid-1900s, often marketed to alter tightly coiled textures.[1] While effective for achieving sleek results, hair straightening carries notable risks, including structural weakening leading to breakage, scalp irritation from caustic agents, and release of formaldehyde—a confirmed carcinogen—from heated smoothing products, prompting FDA warnings on respiratory and neurological effects.[5][1] Epidemiological data further link frequent use of relaxers, particularly among Black women who apply them at rates exceeding 80% in some cohorts, to elevated risks of uterine cancer (up to 4-fold increase with heavy use) and breast cancer, attributable to endocrine-disrupting chemicals absorbed through the scalp.[6][7][8] Despite these concerns, the practice persists globally for aesthetic and professional conformity, underscoring tensions between desired appearance and empirical health evidence.[3][6]

History

Pre-20th century techniques

Prior to the 20th century, hair straightening primarily involved rudimentary thermal and mechanical approaches, as chemical treatments had not yet been developed. These methods temporarily altered hair structure by applying heat to disrupt hydrogen bonds or by using substances to weigh down and smooth strands, though results were short-lived and often damaging due to uneven heat application and lack of protective technologies.[9][10] In ancient Egypt, where straight, sleek hair symbolized status and was depicted in art as desirable, people used heated combs—metal tools warmed over fire—to press and straighten locks, particularly for those with naturally curly or woolly textures. This technique, evidenced in archaeological interpretations of grooming tools, relied on direct heat conduction to flatten hair shafts, sometimes combined with animal fats or plant oils for shine and temporary hold.[11][12] Ancient Roman practices similarly employed heated metal rods or irons, heated in flames and then drawn through sections of hair to achieve straighter appearances, especially for smoothing coarse or unruly growth. These tools, akin to early tongs, were adapted from curling devices but used inversely for pressing, as described in historical accounts of Roman beauty regimens; however, the process carried risks of burns and breakage from excessive heat exposure.[9] During the 19th century in Europe and North America, Victorian-era women with wavy or frizzy hair often resorted to flat irons or tongs heated on stoves or in fires, pressing hair between plates to enforce straightness, a method requiring careful temperature control to avoid scorching. African American women, facing cultural pressures for Eurocentric styles, applied heavy emollients such as goose fat, lye-based soaps, or oils alongside vigorous brushing or heated pressing to mitigate curl, though these yielded inconsistent, humidity-sensitive results and could weaken hair over time.[13][10] Late-19th-century innovations, such as French stylist Marcel Grateau's heated metal plates introduced around 1872, marked a refinement in thermal straightening tools, enabling more precise application for both waving and flattening effects, though still manual and fire-dependent. These pre-electric methods underscored the causal limitations of heat-only approaches: temporary reconfiguration of hair's alpha-helix proteins without altering disulfide bonds, leading to reversion upon moisture exposure.[9][14]

Chemical relaxers and the 20th century

Chemical hair relaxers emerged in the early 20th century as a method to permanently alter the structure of tightly curled hair, primarily targeting African American consumers seeking straighter textures. In 1909, inventor Garrett Augustus Morgan, while developing a lubricant to reduce friction in sewing machines, accidentally created a chemical paste that straightened his assistant's hair when applied as a protective coating. This led to the formulation of a hair refining cream, marketed through the G.A. Morgan Hair Refining Company established in Cleveland, Ohio, by 1913. Morgan's product, applied with a hot comb for activation, represented the first documented chemical approach to hair straightening, though its exact composition—likely involving mild alkaline agents rather than high-concentration lyes—remained proprietary and less aggressive than later formulations.[15][16][17] Initial adoption was limited, often requiring professional application and serving mostly men in barbershops, with products like Morgan's emphasizing temporary softening of curls rather than full permanence. By the 1950s, commercialization accelerated as Chicago chemist George E. Johnson refined the technology for broader use, introducing Ultra Sheen in 1956—a milder, lower-pH formula designed for at-home application by women, incorporating emulsified oils and weaker alkalines to minimize scalp burns while achieving longer-lasting results. Johnson's innovation, sold through Johnson Products Company, marked a shift toward consumer-friendly relaxers, boosting market penetration among African American women amid cultural pressures for straightened hairstyles conforming to mainstream aesthetics. Sales data from the era indicate rapid growth, with such products becoming staples in beauty supply stores by the decade's end.[17][18][19] The mid-to-late 20th century saw the standardization of lye-based relaxers using sodium hydroxide (NaOH) as the active agent, which hydrolyzes disulfide bonds in hair's keratin structure for semi-permanent straightening. While earlier claims suggest lye sales from 1917, verifiable commercial dominance occurred post-1950s, with full-strength formulations proliferating by the 1970s; Pro-Line introduced the first widely available no-lye alternative in response to burn risks, but lye variants like Dark and Lovely's 1971 release—comprising NaOH, water, petroleum jelly, and mineral oil—captured significant market share due to their efficacy on coarse textures. By the 1980s and 1990s, relaxer use peaked, with industry estimates showing over 70% of African American women applying them regularly, though professional oversight declined as DIY kits emphasized speed over safety. These developments prioritized chemical potency over long-term hair integrity, setting the stage for later scrutiny of alkaline damage.[16][20][21]

Post-2000 innovations and trends

In the early 2000s, keratin-based hair smoothing treatments emerged as a major innovation, originating in Brazil where formulations were developed to reduce frizz and straighten curly hair using hydrolyzed keratin proteins applied with heat, offering semi-permanent results lasting 3-5 months without the alkaline damage of traditional relaxers.[22] These treatments, often marketed as Brazilian keratin or blowouts, gained rapid popularity in salons worldwide by 2005, driven by their ability to create sleek, manageable hair on textured types previously reliant on daily thermal styling.[23] Thermal reconditioning techniques, building on Japanese straightening methods from the late 1990s, proliferated in the 2000s with improved chemical formulations involving thioglycolic acid for permanent restructuring of hydrogen bonds in hair keratin, achieving straight results enduring up to a year but requiring professional application to minimize breakage.[24] Concurrently, flat iron technology advanced with ceramic and tourmaline plates introduced around 2003-2005, which distributed heat more evenly at temperatures up to 450°F (232°C), reducing hotspots and enabling faster, less damaging temporary straightening compared to earlier metal-plate models.[25] By the 2010s, health concerns over formaldehyde—a preservative and bond-reformer in many keratin products that releases carcinogenic vapors during application—prompted innovations in "formaldehyde-free" alternatives, such as those using glyoxylic acid or cysteine, though efficacy varied and some products were later found to release trace formaldehyde despite labeling.[26] The U.S. FDA issued warnings in 2011 about these risks, citing eye irritation, respiratory issues, and potential cancer links from occupational exposure, leading to regulatory scrutiny and a proposed ban on formaldehyde in hair straighteners announced in 2021 but delayed as of 2025 due to industry pushback and formulation complexities.[26][27] Into the 2020s, trends shifted toward hybrid treatments like nanoplasty, which employ nanotechnology to infuse smaller protein particles for smoother penetration and longer-lasting frizz control without harsh acids, reflecting a broader demand for safer, customizable options amid rising awareness of chemical sensitivities.[24] Usage data from salon surveys indicate keratin treatments accounted for over 20% of professional smoothing services by 2015, though overall straightening demand fluctuated with cultural pushes for natural textures, particularly among Black consumers post-2010, yet persisted in humid climates and for event styling.[28] Despite these advances, empirical studies link repeated keratin applications to cumulative protein loss and weakened cuticles, underscoring the trade-off between smoothness and long-term hair integrity.[26]

Scientific Principles

Hair structure and chemistry

Human hair is a filamentous biomaterial primarily composed of keratin proteins, which constitute approximately 65-95% of its dry weight, along with lipids, water (about 10-15% in normal conditions), and trace elements such as zinc, copper, and iron.[29] Keratin in hair belongs to the alpha-keratin family, characterized by alpha-helical coiled-coil structures that assemble into intermediate filaments embedded in a matrix of high-sulfur proteins.[30] These proteins are rich in amino acids like cystine, derived from cysteine, enabling extensive cross-linking that imparts mechanical strength and determines hair's natural curvature or straightness.[31] The hair shaft, the visible portion emerging from the follicle, exhibits a hierarchical structure consisting of three concentric layers: the cuticle, cortex, and medulla. The cuticle forms the outermost protective sheath, made of overlapping, scale-like cells (each about 0.5-1 μm thick) derived from flattened keratinocytes, which seal the fiber and influence surface properties like shine and friction.[30] Beneath lies the cortex, accounting for 80-90% of the hair's mass, where bundled keratin intermediate filaments (7-10 nm diameter) align parallel to the fiber axis within an amorphous matrix, providing tensile strength up to 150-200 MPa before breaking.[32] The medulla, an optional inner core present in thicker hairs, consists of loosely packed cells with air-filled spaces, contributing minimally to overall mechanics but potentially affecting opacity.[30] Chemically, hair's resilience and shape stem from intra- and inter-molecular bonds stabilizing the keratin structure. Disulfide bonds (-S-S-), formed by oxidation of cysteine thiol groups, are covalent and numerous (up to 1 per 10-20 amino acids in cortical keratins), crosslinking adjacent protein chains to resist deformation and maintain permanent configurations like curls or waves.[31] Hydrogen bonds, weaker non-covalent interactions between polar amide and carbonyl groups, along with ionic (salt) bridges between charged side chains, further modulate elasticity but are readily disrupted by water, heat, or pH changes, allowing reversible swelling (up to 15-20% in diameter).[32] These bonds collectively enable hair's anisotropic properties, with longitudinal Young's modulus around 2-5 GPa, far exceeding transverse values.[33] Variations in bond density, particularly disulfide content, explain differences in hair texture across individuals, influenced by genetic factors like follicle shape.[31]

Mechanisms of temporary versus permanent straightening

Temporary hair straightening primarily disrupts non-covalent interactions within keratin proteins, such as hydrogen and ionic bonds, which contribute to the hair's secondary structure and temporary shape retention. These bonds are weaker and reversible, allowing heat or mechanical force to align the alpha-helical keratin filaments in the cortex while the hair is under tension, typically after wetting to facilitate bond breakage via increased molecular mobility. For instance, thermal tools like flat irons or blow dryers apply temperatures between 155–250°C to induce keratin hydrolysis, temporarily breaking hydrogen bonds and dehydrating the hair shaft to maintain alignment; however, exposure to humidity or water reforms these bonds, reverting the hair to its natural curl pattern.[1][34] In contrast, permanent straightening targets the covalent disulfide bonds (-S-S-) between cysteine residues in keratin, which provide structural rigidity and determine the hair's inherent curliness due to their asymmetric distribution in coiled follicles. Chemical relaxers employ reducing agents, such as ammonium thioglycolate at pH 9–9.5, to cleave these bonds into free thiols (-SH), enabling the straightened configuration under mechanical tension before neutralization with an oxidizer like hydrogen peroxide reforms the disulfide linkages in the new shape. Alkaline relaxers, using sodium hydroxide at pH 12–14, achieve a similar effect through lanthionization, converting about one-third of cystine residues to lanthionine (a mono-sulfide bond), which is more stable but irreversible, permanently altering the cortex without requiring oxidation.[1][34] This bond reformation ensures longevity until new hair growth emerges, distinguishing it from temporary methods that do not modify the underlying covalent framework.[1] The differential bond strengths—hydrogen bonds requiring only 2–5 kcal/mol to break versus 50–60 kcal/mol for disulfides—underpin the reversibility of temporary straightening versus the durability of permanent alterations, as confirmed by spectroscopic analyses of keratin transformations from alpha-helical to extended beta-sheet conformations post-treatment.[34] Over-reliance on temporary heat can cumulatively weaken hair through repeated hydrolysis, while permanent chemicals risk cortex degradation if over-applied, highlighting the trade-offs in causal efficacy for shape change.[1]

Methods

Thermal and mechanical approaches

Thermal hair straightening involves applying heat to disrupt the hydrogen bonds within the hair's keratin structure, allowing the strands to be reshaped and set straight upon cooling.[1] This process temporarily alters the hair's configuration without breaking the stronger disulfide bonds, resulting in effects that last until exposure to moisture reforms the bonds.[1] Common tools include hot combs, pressing irons, and flat irons, which heat hair to temperatures typically between 180–230°C (356–446°F) to achieve straightening.[1] The hot comb, developed in the late 19th century and popularized in the early 20th century, consists of a metal comb heated to high temperatures and combed through sections of hair to press and align the strands.[1] This method, often used on coarse or curly hair, combines direct heat application with mechanical tension from the comb's teeth to flatten the hair cuticle and elongate the shaft.[35] Modern flat irons, or hair straighteners, operate similarly by clamping heated ceramic or metal plates around small sections of hair, applying even pressure and heat for 5–10 seconds per pass to minimize damage while maximizing alignment.[36] Excessive heat above 175°C (347°F) for prolonged periods can cause irreversible damage, such as cuticle lifting, protein denaturation, and loss of elasticity, leading to brittle, limp hair.[37][36] Blow drying with a round brush or tension provides a combined thermal and mechanical approach, where hot air (around 100–120°C) is directed at damp hair under tension to evaporate moisture and set the straight form.[1] This technique relies on the Maillard reaction and dehydration to temporarily hydrolyze keratin, but repeated use increases the risk of thermal degradation, with studies showing structural weakening after multiple exposures.[1] Mechanical approaches to hair straightening exclude heat and rely solely on physical tension to stretch and retrain curly or wavy hair patterns over time.[38] Techniques such as the wrap method involve parting damp hair and wrapping it tightly around the head in overlapping sections, secured with pins or bands, to elongate curls as the hair dries naturally or with cool air.[39] Banding uses elastic bands spaced along ponytails to gently pull sections straight, reducing wave amplitude without thermal stress, though results are most effective on minimally curly hair types and last only until rewetting.[38] Brushing with a boar bristle brush on conditioned, damp hair applies consistent tension to align the cuticle scales and stretch the cortex, promoting a smoother appearance that persists briefly in low-humidity environments.[39] These methods preserve hair integrity by avoiding heat-induced bond breakage but offer limited durability, often requiring daily reapplication, and are less effective on tightly coiled textures due to the hair's inherent elasticity and bond strength.[38] Empirical observations indicate mechanical stretching can reduce curl by 20–50% temporarily, but without fixation, reversion occurs rapidly upon bond reformation.[39]

Chemical and semi-permanent treatments

Chemical hair relaxers achieve straightening through the application of strong alkaline or reducing agents that cleave the disulfide bonds in the cysteine-rich keratin proteins of the hair cortex, allowing the reshaped strands to reform in a straightened configuration upon neutralization.[1] This process permanently alters the hair structure until new growth occurs, typically requiring reapplication every 6-8 weeks at the roots.[40] The treatment involves sectioning dry hair, applying the relaxer cream evenly while avoiding the scalp, monitoring processing time based on hair texture (e.g., 10-20 minutes for fine hair, longer for coarse), combing through to straighten, rinsing thoroughly, and applying a neutralizer containing hydrogen peroxide or similar oxidants to reform bonds in the new shape.[8] Relaxers are classified by active ingredients: lye-based formulations using sodium hydroxide (typically 2-10% concentration) penetrate deeply and effectively straighten coarse, tightly coiled hair but pose higher risks of breakage due to their high pH (12-14).[40] [1] No-lye relaxers employ guanidine carbonate combined with calcium hydroxide, achieving a similar pH but with slower action and less penetration, suiting finer, color-treated, or damaged hair while reducing immediate scalp irritation.[40] Thio relaxers, utilizing ammonium thioglycolate (pH 8-9.5), act as mild reducers for fragile or previously processed hair, often in professional salon settings where timing is precisely controlled to avoid over-processing.[8] All types target the three-dimensional disulfide linkages—comprising up to 15% of hair's bonding strength—converting them to lanthionine or cystine residues that lock in straightness.[1] Semi-permanent treatments, in contrast, do not permanently disrupt disulfide bonds but instead deposit hydrolyzed keratin proteins or amino acid complexes onto the hair shaft, filling cuticular gaps, smoothing the surface, and reducing frizz through heat-activated sealing rather than chemical reconfiguration.[8] These last 3-6 months, fading with shampooing (e.g., 20-30 washes), as they primarily enhance manageability without altering the cortex.[40] Keratin smoothing treatments apply a liquid solution of keratin peptides, silicones, and conditioning agents, followed by blow-drying and flat-ironing at 200-230°C to cross-link and encapsulate the hair, yielding straighter results on wavy-to-curly textures while preserving some natural movement.[1] Brazilian Blowout, a branded variant introduced in the early 2000s, utilizes a proprietary blend of amino acids and polymers applied similarly, with results emphasizing shine and frizz control over full straightening, enduring 10-12 weeks on average; earlier formulations contained formaldehyde (up to 3.5%), though subsequent versions shifted to formaldehyde-free alternatives like glyoxylic acid for thermal restructuring.[8] These methods suit clients seeking reversible effects, often on humidity-prone hair, but require avoidance of sulfate shampoos to prolong efficacy.[40]

Health Risks and Safety

Acute effects and hair damage

Thermal straightening using flat irons, typically at temperatures of 155–250°C, induces immediate protein denaturation in the hair cortex, with α-keratin degradation occurring around 237°C, leading to structural weakening and increased brittleness.[41][1] This heat disrupts hydrogen bonds for temporary alignment but also causes cuticle erosion, observable via scanning electron microscopy as lifted or irregular scales, resulting in heightened porosity, dehydration, and acute breakage susceptibility.[41] Protein loss assays confirm greater damage in certain hair types, such as Asian hair compared to Caucasian, with quantifiable reductions in tensile strength post-exposure.[41] Chemical relaxers, including alkaline hydroxides (pH 11–13) like sodium hydroxide, cause immediate hydrolysis of disulfide bonds in the hair cortex, converting cystine to lanthionine and rendering the shaft fragile and prone to snapping during or shortly after application.[1][42] This over-processing can lead to acute hair loss exceeding 40% in severe cases and up to 95% breakage rates in outbreak reports, alongside scalp effects like burns, pain, desquamation, and contact dermatitis from caustic residues.[42] Acid-based straighteners, such as those with formaldehyde or glyoxylic acid, similarly produce rapid cuticle damage and reduced breakage resistance, with increased shaft irregularity and protein mass loss documented in fiber analyses.[42] In both thermal and chemical approaches, these acute alterations compromise hair integrity by elevating friction and moisture loss, often necessitating conditioning to mitigate immediate fallout.[1]

Chronic health concerns from chemicals

Chemical hair relaxers and straightening products often contain endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) such as parabens, phthalates, and bisphenol A, which may interfere with hormonal signaling and contribute to long-term reproductive and metabolic disorders.[43] These compounds can be absorbed through the scalp, particularly when products are applied frequently or left on for extended periods, leading to systemic exposure that has been linked in laboratory studies to altered estrogen and thyroid function.[44] A 2021 analysis identified triclosan, another common ingredient, as an EDC capable of disrupting endocrine activity across multiple animal models, with potential human implications for fertility and pubertal timing.[40] Frequent use of chemical straighteners has been associated with elevated risks of hormone-sensitive cancers, including uterine, breast, and ovarian types. A 2022 National Institutes of Health (NIH) study of over 33,000 U.S. women found that those reporting use of straightening products in the prior year had a 1.8-fold higher incidence of uterine cancer (hazard ratio [HR] = 1.80, 95% CI: 1.11–2.93), with risks doubling for users applying them four or more times annually (HR ≈ 2.0).[6][45] This association persisted after adjusting for factors like age, BMI, and smoking, though causation remains unproven and may involve EDCs mimicking estrogen to promote endometrial proliferation. A separate prospective study of Black women reported moderate to heavy relaxer use correlated with increased postmenopausal uterine cancer risk, potentially reflecting cumulative exposure disparities.[46] Keratin-based smoothing treatments frequently release formaldehyde or its precursors (e.g., methylene glycol) when heated, a known human carcinogen per the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC Group 1). Chronic inhalation or dermal exposure has been tied to nasopharyngeal cancer, myeloid leukemia, and upper respiratory malignancies, with case reports documenting elevated formaldehyde levels in salon air during applications exceeding occupational safety limits.[5] Breast cancer risks also show links to straightener use, particularly among Black women, with one cohort study observing higher odds ratios for estrogen-receptor-positive tumors (adjusted OR = 1.45 for any use).[47] Epidemiological evidence for ovarian cancer similarly implicates relaxer chemicals, though smaller sample sizes limit statistical power in some analyses.[48] Overall, while acute scalp irritation is common, chronic concerns underscore the need for ingredient transparency, as product formulations vary and regulatory scrutiny has intensified post-2022 findings.[43]

Evidence from epidemiological studies

A prospective cohort study involving 33,947 participants from the Sister Study, conducted by the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS), reported that women who used chemical hair straightening products in the preceding 12 months faced an adjusted hazard ratio (HR) of 1.80 (95% CI: 1.11-2.93) for developing uterine cancer compared to non-users.[48] Frequent use, defined as more than four applications per year, was associated with an even higher risk, with an HR of 2.55 (95% CI: 1.28-5.09).[48] This analysis adjusted for factors such as age, race, menopausal status, and body mass index, though residual confounding from unmeasured variables like socioeconomic status or healthcare access remains possible, as the study relied on self-reported exposure data without direct biomarker validation.[48] In the Black Women's Health Study (BWHS), a longitudinal cohort of over 46,000 African American women followed since 1995, long-term use of chemical hair relaxers (cumulative duration of 10 or more years or frequent applications of five or more per year) was linked to a 31% increased risk of breast cancer (HR 1.31, 95% CI: 1.09-1.58) compared to infrequent or non-use, particularly for lye-based formulations. A separate BWHS analysis focused on uterine cancer found that moderate to heavy relaxer use correlated with elevated risk among postmenopausal participants (HR 1.68, 95% CI: 1.00-2.84), but not premenopausal women, suggesting potential interactions with hormonal status. These associations persisted after adjusting for confounders like physical activity, alcohol consumption, and parity, yet the observational design limits causal inference, and chemicals such as parabens or phthalates in relaxers—known endocrine disruptors—provide a plausible but unproven mechanistic link.[49] Smaller or cross-sectional studies have suggested ties to ovarian cancer and other gynecologic conditions, but evidence is sparser and less robust, often limited by sample size and reliance on retrospective recall.[50] Overall, these findings disproportionately implicate frequent users, who are predominantly Black women due to higher prevalence of relaxer use for textured hair management, though risks appear independent of ethnicity in adjusted models.[48] No large-scale randomized trials exist, and epidemiological data emphasize associations rather than definitive causation, underscoring the need for exposure quantification and replication in diverse populations.[7]

Cultural and Social Dimensions

Straightening across hair types and ethnicities

Hair morphology varies significantly by ethnicity, influencing the prevalence and methods of straightening. African-descent hair typically features tightly coiled or kinky patterns with elliptical cross-sections, lower scalp density (148-160 hairs/cm²), and greater fragility due to structural twists, necessitating more aggressive chemical relaxers for effective straightening.[51][52] Asian hair, often straight with rounder cross-sections and higher tensile strength, requires minimal intervention, as it resists bending and breakage during thermal styling.[53] Caucasian hair, generally wavy to straight with oval cross-sections and higher density (214-230 hairs/cm²), responds well to mechanical or thermal methods without extensive damage.[51][52] Among African American women, chemical straightening via relaxers has been widespread, with over 70% reporting use, historically up to 95%, driven by cultural preferences for straight textures originating from practices like hot combing during the slavery era.[54][55][56] In contrast, straightening is less prevalent among Asian and Caucasian populations, where naturally straighter or wavy hair aligns with common aesthetic norms, and thermal tools suffice for temporary adjustments without routine chemical alteration.[53] Coily African hair's structural properties make thermal straightening alone insufficient for lasting results, often leading to breakage, whereas finer wavy Caucasian or straight Asian hair achieves smoother outcomes with lower heat settings (e.g., 180-200°C versus 230°C+ for coily types).[1][57] Epidemiological data highlight disparities: 62% of African American women over age 20 in a New York cohort reported ever using relaxers, compared to negligible rates in non-Black groups for similar chemical treatments.[58] These practices reflect adaptations to hair type—permanent chemical methods dominate for Type 4 coily hair to manage volume and frizz, while Type 1-2 hair favors reversible techniques.[56] Asian hair's resistance to permanent change limits chemical use, preserving its inherent straightness.[59]

Debates on aesthetics, manageability, and identity

Debates surrounding hair straightening often center on conflicting perceptions of beauty standards, practical utility, and personal or cultural self-expression. In professional contexts, straight hair is frequently rated higher for competence and professionalism compared to curly or textured styles, with a 2020 study finding that Black women with natural hairstyles like afros or twists were perceived as less professional by evaluators.[60] [61] This bias persists across ethnicities, as straight hair aligns with Eurocentric ideals historically promoted in media and workplaces, though preferences vary; for instance, a 2023 analysis noted straight hair's association with youthfulness in Western surveys.[62] However, such aesthetics are not universally imposed, with some cultures like Brazil showing 75% preference for natural textures in recent data, reflecting localized appreciation over global uniformity.[63] Manageability debates highlight curly hair's structural challenges, including higher susceptibility to frizz, tangling, and longer styling times due to its elliptical follicle shape versus straight hair's round profile, which allows smoother alignment and reduced breakage under tension.[64] Empirical reports from Black women cite chemical straightening for "achieving manageable hair," often every 6-8 weeks to maintain sleekness amid daily activities, contrasting with natural styles' demands for specialized products and techniques.[58] [65] Yet, proponents of natural hair argue that with proper care, textured hair offers durability and volume unattainable in straightened forms, challenging the narrative that straightening is inherently more practical without acknowledging learning curves for curl maintenance.[66] Identity-related discussions, particularly among women of African descent, frame straightening as a tension between assimilation and authenticity, with 70-80% historically opting for chemical treatments amid pressures for conformity, yet the natural hair movement—rooted in 1960s Black Power activism—has spurred transitions emphasizing cultural pride and rejection of Eurocentric norms.[67] [68] Psychological studies reveal embedded straightening practices tied to maternal traditions and community belonging rather than solely self-hatred, complicating activist claims of universal internalized racism; for example, qualitative data show women voicing relief in straight hair's simplicity alongside identity conflicts during "Big Chop" transitions to natural styles.[65] [69] This duality underscores causal factors like economic incentives for low-maintenance looks over purely coercive narratives, with recent declines in relaxer use (down 26% post-2006) signaling empowered choice amid health awareness.[70] [71]

Regulatory and industry responses to risks

In response to health risks from formaldehyde-releasing compounds in keratin-based hair smoothing treatments, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued consumer alerts as early as 2010, warning that products like Brazilian Blowout release formaldehyde gas—a known carcinogen—when heated, potentially causing eye irritation, respiratory issues, and skin burns.[5] By 2011, the FDA had tested such products and found formaldehyde levels exceeding safe exposure limits set by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), leading to misbranding citations for undeclared methylene glycol, a formaldehyde precursor.[26] In October 2022, following a National Institutes of Health (NIH) study associating frequent use of chemical hair straighteners with a 30% increased risk of uterine cancer among users (particularly Black women applying them every 2-3 months), the FDA proposed a ban on formaldehyde and formaldehyde-releasers in hair straightening products.[6] However, by July 2025, the agency reversed this ban proposal amid regulatory delays, citing insufficient data for immediate action despite ongoing lawsuits alleging inadequate warnings from manufacturers.[72] Internationally, the European Union enforces stricter limits under Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009, capping free formaldehyde at 0.2% in cosmetic products, with hair smoothing formulations exceeding this threshold effectively banned due to inhalation risks during application.[73] Canada prohibited high-formaldehyde hair straighteners outright, while Brazil and the EU have restricted their sale since the early 2010s, prompting some U.S. brands to reformulate for global compliance.[74] In January 2025, France's ANSES agency issued warnings on glyoxylic acid-based alternatives, which can decompose into formaldehyde under heat, though EU-wide assessment remains pending.[75] Industry responses have included partial reformulations to "formaldehyde-free" claims, with companies like Magic Sleek obtaining FDA, OSHA, and EU approvals for non-formaldehyde variants by early 2025, alongside marketing of semi-permanent options using amino acids or plant-derived compounds to mitigate thermal damage and chemical absorption.[76] However, enforcement challenges persist, as OSHA cited salons and manufacturers for hazard communication failures as late as 2021, and post-2022 NIH findings spurred over 10,000 lawsuits against firms like L'Oréal and Procter & Gamble, alleging concealment of endocrine-disrupting chemicals like parabens and phthalates linked to reproductive cancers.[77] These legal actions have accelerated safety data disclosures and ingredient transparency, though critics note slow adoption of fully inert alternatives amid consumer demand for long-lasting results.[78]
