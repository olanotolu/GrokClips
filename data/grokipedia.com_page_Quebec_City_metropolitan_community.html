Communauté métropolitaine de Québec

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) is an administrative entity established under Quebec provincial law in 2000 to coordinate regional governance across 28 municipalities in the Capitale-Nationale and Chaudière-Appalaches (Lévis) regions, encompassing the metropolitan area centered on Quebec City.[1][2] With a population of approximately 882,375 as of 2025 and a land area of 3,230.7 square kilometers, the CMQ addresses cross-jurisdictional challenges through unified planning and resource allocation, replacing the earlier Communauté urbaine de Québec formed in 1970.[3][2] The CMQ's core mandate involves metropolitan-scale interventions in land-use planning, environmental protection, public transit coordination, mobility infrastructure, waste management, and resilience to natural risks, aiming to promote sustainable economic competitiveness while preserving agricultural and natural lands.[4][5] Governed by a council of 17 members—primarily mayors from key municipalities including Quebec City and Lévis—the organization develops binding plans such as the metropolitan land-use scheme to harmonize local decisions with regional priorities, funded partly through shared municipal contributions and provincial grants.[6] Notable initiatives include strategies for agricultural development and climate adaptation, with the CMQ recognized as a finalist in public administration awards for resilience efforts; however, it has faced critiques over perceived inefficiencies in enforcing land-use restrictions amid urban sprawl pressures and occasional provincial overrides of funding requests.[7][8]

History

Predecessor Entities

The primary predecessor entity to the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec was the Communauté urbaine de Québec (CUQ), a supralocal authority responsible for coordinating urban services, planning, and infrastructure across multiple municipalities in the Quebec City region. Established effective 1 January 1970 by the Loi de la Communauté urbaine de Québec (S.Q. 1969, c. 83), the CUQ encompassed 10 initial member municipalities on the north shore of the St. Lawrence River, including Quebec City proper, with a focus on shared responsibilities such as water supply, waste management, and regional transportation.[9] The CUQ's structure reflected Quebec's mid-20th-century push toward metropolitan governance amid rapid suburbanization, drawing from similar models like Montreal's Communauté urbaine de Montréal created concurrently under parallel legislation (S.Q. 1969, c. 84). Over its three decades, the CUQ expanded to include up to 32 member entities by the late 1990s, managing a population exceeding 600,000 and addressing intermunicipal disparities without full amalgamation.[10][11] The CUQ was dissolved in 2001 amid the province's municipal reform under Bill 170 (S.Q. 2000, c. 56), which mandated widespread amalgamations; its north-shore members were merged into the expanded Ville de Québec effective 1 January 2002, transferring residual functions like regional planning to interim mechanisms before the CMQ's formation. This reform aimed to streamline administration but left a coordination vacuum for cross-river metropolitan issues, which the subsequent CMQ addressed by incorporating south-shore entities previously outside the CUQ's scope.[12]

Establishment in 2002

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) was established as a legal entity on January 1, 2002, through Schedule VI of the Act to reform the municipal territorial organization of the metropolitan regions of Montréal, Québec, and the national capital, which received assent on December 20, 2000.[1] This legislation created the CMQ to address metropolitan-scale governance needs in the Québec region, encompassing planning, coordination, and financing functions across a territory larger than the prior urban community structure.[1] The reform was part of Quebec's broader 2000-2002 municipal restructuring efforts, which aimed to streamline administration, reduce fragmentation, and enhance inter-municipal cooperation amid urban growth pressures, including population increases and infrastructure demands in the capital region.[13] The CMQ directly succeeded the Communauté urbaine de Québec (CUQ), which had been operational since 1970 but was limited to the urban core and immediate suburbs; the new entity expanded jurisdiction to include peripheral municipalities, initially comprising 23 members that grew to 28 by subsequent adjustments.[14] Under the enabling act, the CMQ was empowered to develop a metropolitan plan (schéma métropolitain) for land use, transportation, and economic development, with mandatory approval from the Minister of Municipal Affairs.[1] Fiscal mechanisms were introduced, including shared taxation bases and contributions from member municipalities proportional to their assessed property values, to fund regional projects without centralizing all local powers.[15] Implementation involved transitional provisions, such as transferring CUQ assets, employees, and ongoing contracts to the CMQ effective January 1, 2002, while preserving local municipal autonomy except in designated metropolitan competencies.[1] The inaugural council was formed from delegates of member municipalities, with the mayor of Québec City serving as chair, reflecting the city's dominant role in the region's 700,000-plus population at the time.[14] This structure prioritized evidence-based coordination over political consolidation, as evidenced by the act's emphasis on data-driven planning tools like economic observatories to inform decisions.[13]

Evolution Post-Municipal Reforms

Following the municipal mergers that took effect on January 1, 2002, the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) transitioned from its predecessor, the Communauté urbaine de Québec, to coordinate planning across an enlarged metropolitan area including the newly amalgamated City of Québec and 27 surrounding municipalities. Initial operations focused on establishing governance structures and conducting public consultations, with annual expenditures increasing modestly from $2.5 million in 2002 to $2.9 million in 2007 to support early initiatives in land-use coordination, economic development, and inter-municipal harmonization.[13] The absence of successful demerger referendums in the Québec region—unlike in Montréal, where multiple suburbs separated effective January 1, 2006—preserved the CMQ's territorial integrity and reinforced its role in bridging the central city with peripheral municipalities and regional county municipalities (MRCs). This stability enabled the CMQ to prioritize supra-local functions, such as environmental protection and transportation planning, without immediate boundary disruptions. By 2011, the metropolitan population had grown to approximately 750,000, prompting assessments of territorial expansion opportunities, though the core boundaries encompassing 28 municipalities remained unchanged.[16] A pivotal advancement occurred with the adoption of the first Plan métropolitain d'aménagement et de développement (PMAD) on February 3, 2013, which integrated guidelines for urban growth, sustainable mobility, and preservation of agricultural and natural spaces, binding member municipalities to metropolitan-scale objectives. This instrument marked the CMQ's maturation into a proactive planning authority, addressing post-reform challenges like demographic expansion and infrastructure demands through mandatory coherence with local schemes.[17] Subsequent legislative adjustments, including 2017's Loi 122, which bolstered local municipal autonomies in governance and fiscal matters, did not diminish the CMQ's statutory powers under the Loi sur la Communauté métropolitaine de Québec; instead, it sustained mandatory metropolitan oversight to prevent fragmented development. Recent evolutions include the 2025 PMAD revision, emphasizing densification in built-up areas, enhanced public transit, and climate resilience, alongside specialized strategies like the 2026-2034 agricultural support plan to sustain rural-urban linkages. These developments underscore the CMQ's adaptation from a post-merger coordination entity to a resilient framework for long-term territorial management amid population growth projected to exceed 1 million by 2050.[18][19][7]

Governance

Council Structure and Composition

The council of the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) consists of 17 members, comprising elected municipal officials designated to represent the organization's member municipalities.[20][6] It includes the mayor of Québec City and eight additional members designated by the Québec agglomeration council; the mayor of Lévis and four members designated by the Lévis city council; and the prefects of the municipalités régionales de comté (MRCs) of La Côte-de-Beaupré, La Jacques-Cartier, and L'Île-d'Orléans.[20][6] These designations occur through decisions of the respective municipal councils, with provisions for replacements at any time to maintain continuity.[20] The presidency of the council is held by the mayor of Québec City, currently Bruno Marchand, who leads sessions and orientations.[6] A quorum of nine members is required for council meetings, ensuring broad representation in deliberations.[20][6] Sessions are public, allowing for questions from attendees, and emphasize consensual decision-making to foster a metropolitan perspective across the 17-member body.[6] Supporting the council is an executive committee of five members, including the president and vice-president (mayor of Lévis, Gilles Lehouillier), tasked with operational management, such as contracts under $100,000 and staff appointments, under a quorum of three.[6] An advisory agricultural committee of 10 members, blending councillors, prefects, producers, and residents, provides input on land use and environmental matters but holds no decision-making authority.[6] The council retains ultimate authority over budgets, regulations, and strategic directions as per the CMQ's constituting law.[20]

Decision-Making Processes

The Conseil métropolitain, the primary decision-making body of the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec, convenes regular meetings as per an annually established schedule, with public notice published at least three clear days in advance, and holds special or extraordinary sessions convened by the president, the executive committee, or at the request of at least five members.[21] All council meetings are public, including a designated question period for citizens, with provisions for audio or video recording subject to internal by-laws.[21] A quorum of nine members is required for council proceedings, excluding representatives from Ville de Lévis for decisions on residual materials management.[21] Resolutions and by-laws are adopted by a two-thirds majority of members present unless the Act or internal rules specify otherwise, with each of the 17 council members casting one vote.[21] The council may delegate certain powers to the executive committee via by-law, requiring a two-thirds majority for such delegation or withdrawal.[21] The executive committee, consisting of five members chaired by the council president, supports decision-making by exercising delegated authority, preparing budgets and plans, and reporting on contracts exceeding $25,000; its decisions require a simple majority of members present, with a quorum of three.[21] Specialized committees, established by the council, deliberate on advisory matters such as planning or resilience, recommending actions to the council or executive committee via simple majority votes, and hold public meetings unless exempted by by-law.[21] Certain metropolitan competencies demand heightened consensus: the economic development plan requires a two-thirds majority including majorities from Ville de Lévis representatives and those from regional county municipalities; similar thresholds apply to international promotion, tourism strategies, and residual materials plans, ensuring balanced representation across urban and peripheral members.[21] Budget adoption occurs at dedicated sessions by November 15 annually, with parts voted separately and amendments possible, following executive committee preparation and public notice eight days prior.[21]

Powers and Responsibilities

Metropolitan Planning and Coordination

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) holds statutory authority to develop and enforce a metropolitan land use and development plan (PMAD), serving as the primary instrument for coordinating territorial planning across its 28 member municipalities spanning 3,230 km² of organized territory.[22] Under the Act respecting land use planning and development, the CMQ acts as the responsible body for adopting this plan, which municipalities and regional county municipalities (MRCs) must align with in their local schemas, prioritizing growth in already urbanized areas, continuity of development, and preservation of natural and agricultural lands.[23] The revised PMAD, adopted unanimously by the CMQ council on August 28, 2025, emphasizes intelligent densification, sustainable mobility, and conservation to enhance regional competitiveness and attractiveness while directing urban expansion away from green spaces.[19] In transportation, the CMQ exercises exclusive jurisdiction to plan, coordinate, and finance metropolitan public transit systems, integrating them with broader land use objectives in accordance with provincial policies.[1] This includes overseeing strategic infrastructure like reserved bus lanes, bike paths, park-and-ride facilities, and carpooling initiatives to foster interconnected services.[24] The Vision métropolitaine de la mobilité durable à l'horizon 2041, a guiding framework, targets efficient, low-emission mobility by promoting multimodal networks and reducing reliance on private vehicles through coordinated regional efforts.[25] Coordination occurs through the CMQ's council, comprising mayors and councillors from member entities, which enforces compliance via binding orientations in the PMAD and transport plans, while facilitating inter-municipal agreements on shared infrastructure and services of metropolitan scope.[14] This supramunicipal approach addresses externalities like sprawl and congestion that individual municipalities cannot resolve alone, ensuring unified strategies for equipment, environmental protection, and economic development without overriding local autonomy in non-metropolitan matters.[1]

Economic and Infrastructure Development

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) coordinates economic development through territorial planning that prioritizes competitiveness and attractiveness, directing growth toward urbanized areas while preserving agricultural and natural lands to support key sectors such as agriculture and agro-food production.[22] The revised Plan métropolitain d'aménagement et de développement (PMAD), adopted on August 28, 2025, emphasizes smart densification, sustainable mobility, and conservation, serving as the primary tool to frame metropolitan growth and integrate economic objectives with environmental constraints.[19] Complementing this, the Zone économique métropolitaine (ZEM) for the Capitale-Nationale and Chaudière-Appalaches regions outlines a 2024-2029 action plan with 230 action tracks and 79 specific projects aimed at fostering collaborative economic development, including strategies for circular economy transition and agricultural enhancement.[26][27] In infrastructure, the CMQ finances and plans metropolitan-scale facilities, including sustainable transport networks and waste management systems, to reduce environmental impacts and improve connectivity.[5] The 2025-2028 action plan prioritizes transport projects to enhance collective mobility options, positioning them as alternatives to private vehicles amid growing regional demands.[28] Additionally, the Stratégie de développement des activités agricoles et agroalimentaires 2026-2034, involving over 60 organizations, integrates economic support for producers with infrastructure needs like resource coordination along the Saint-Laurent River.[7] These efforts align with broader provincial investments, such as the 2025-2035 Québec Infrastructure Plan's $164 billion allocation, though CMQ focuses on supramunicipal coordination to avoid fragmented local developments.[29][30]

Fiscal and Financial Mechanisms

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec adopts an annual budget divided into parts corresponding to its functional categories, with the executive committee preparing a draft by October 1 and submitting it to the council by November 15 following public notice eight days in advance.[1] If not adopted by January 1, one-quarter of the previous year's appropriations is deemed adopted for each part.[1] Expenses are apportioned among the 28 member municipalities based on their fiscal potential, which reflects their aggregate property assessment values, though the council may adjust this distribution via by-law to account for specific circumstances.[1] This mechanism ensures contributions align with each municipality's capacity to generate revenue, primarily from local property taxes. Revenues derive mainly from these municipal contributions, supplemented by the authority to levy special property taxes on non-residential immovables for designated metropolitan functions and to impose tariffs for services such as waste management or planning consultations.[1] The community may also enter financing agreements with federal, provincial, or municipal governments, as demonstrated by project-specific grants for infrastructure or environmental initiatives.[1] For capital needs, it can contract loans repayable over up to 20 years, subject to approval by the Minister of Municipal Affairs and Housing, and establish working funds limited to 20% of the annual budget.[1] Financial reserves can be created by by-law, funded from surplus general funds or special allocations, with caps at 50% of the operating budget or 30% of the undepreciated value of fixed assets, requiring ministerial authorization for establishment and use.[1] The 2024 operating budget totaled $5,224,597 in expenses, reflecting coordination across planning, environment, and mobility domains.[31] This increased to $6,120,000 for 2025's functioning portion, driven by expanded responsibilities in sustainable development and risk management, underscoring steady growth in operational demands without independent taxing authority beyond specified levies.[32]

Member Municipalities and Demographics

Composition and Boundaries

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) encompasses 28 municipalities, structured primarily around the Agglomération de Québec, the Ville de Lévis, and six municipalités régionales de comté (MRCs): La Jacques-Cartier, La Côte-de-Beaupré, L'Île-d'Orléans, Les Chenaux, Lotbinière, and Portneuf.[14][33] The Agglomération de Québec includes the central city of Québec along with L'Ancienne-Lorette and Saint-Augustin-de-Desmaures, while Lévis serves as the principal municipality on the south shore of the St. Lawrence River; the MRCs incorporate surrounding suburban and rural communities, totaling the 28 local entities under CMQ coordination.[2] The boundaries of the CMQ, as defined by Quebec's Act respecting the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (C-37.02), delineate a metropolitan territory that spans both banks of the St. Lawrence River, extending from urban cores in Québec and Lévis northward into the Laurentian foothills via La Jacques-Cartier and Portneuf, eastward along the Côte-de-Beaupré and L'Île-d'Orléans, and westward and southward through Les Chenaux and Lotbinière.[33] This configuration covers 3,230 km² of municipalized land, excluding non-organized territories that expand the effective planning area to approximately 9,220 km², and integrates diverse landscapes including historic urban districts, agricultural plains, and forested uplands.[14][2] The limits prioritize functional urban-economic integration over strict administrative lines, reflecting provincial designations for regional governance established in 2002 and refined through subsequent municipal mergers and boundary adjustments.[33]

Population and Economic Profile

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec comprises 28 municipalities spanning approximately 3,230 km², with a population of 839,311 as recorded in the 2021 Canadian Census for the corresponding census metropolitan area (CMA).[34] Recent estimates indicate growth to 900,343 residents by 2023, driven by net migration and natural increase, marking a record 2.5% annual rise of 22,200 people in 2024 alone.[35][36] This represents about 10% of Quebec's total population, with a median age of 43.2 years and a structure skewed toward an aging workforce, where over 20% of residents are 55 or older.[14][37] The region's economy is tertiary-dominated, with public administration, health care, and education as leading sectors, reflecting Quebec City's role as a provincial capital.[38] Key industries also include professional, scientific, and technical services; insurance and financial services; tourism, which attracts over 5 million visitors annually generating substantial revenue; and emerging high-technology fields such as optics-photonics, life sciences, information and communications technology, and food processing.[38][39] Real GDP for the Quebec City CMA stood at CA$39.97 billion in 2023, down 0.4% from the prior year amid broader provincial trends, though the region has shown strong per-capita growth outperforming national averages in recent years.[40][41] Employment in the CMA totals around 450,000, with a low unemployment rate of 4.8% as of September 2025, below the provincial average of 5.7% and supported by a high labor force participation rate.[42][43] Median household income reached CA$70,500 in 2021, bolstered by stable government-related jobs and innovation clusters, though challenges persist in diversifying beyond public sector reliance.[35]

Key Initiatives and Impacts

Major Projects and Achievements

The Plan métropolitain d'aménagement et de développement (PMAD) serves as a cornerstone achievement, with its initial adoption guiding sustainable urban growth, transport optimization, and infrastructure efficiency across the metropolitan territory; a revised version emphasizing intelligent densification, durable mobility, and conservation of natural and agricultural areas was advanced through projects adopted in June 2021, public consultations, and a second iteration released on August 28, 2025.[22][19] This framework has issued 41 conformity opinions on municipal plans and modeled 24 watercourses to delineate flood zones, enhancing risk management and territorial coherence.[44] In transport and mobility, the Plan directeur de mobilité Circuit intégré de transport express (CITÉ), unveiled in June 2024 by CDPQ Infra under CMQ mandate, outlines phased improvements to metropolitan mobility, including express transit networks to boost collective transport modal share toward long-term targets set in the 2025-2028 action plan.[45][46] Complementary efforts include adoption of a sustainable mobility vision following 2021 consultations and a transport evolution title authorized in September 2021, alongside a 2021 study quantifying societal costs of automobile use at $5.77 per kilometer to inform policy shifts.[44][28] Environmental initiatives feature the Vision métropolitaine de l’eau Plan d’action 2021-2030, adopted in June 2021 with 52 actions for water resource protection, and a revised Plan métropolitain de gestion des matières résiduelles (PMGMR) 2024-2031 adopted in January 2022 after consultations, advancing waste reduction and recycling coordination.[44] A plan to combat invasive species was also adopted, with implementation commencing in 2022 to safeguard biodiversity.[44] Climate resilience efforts include two major projects under the PIACC and PMVD frameworks to evaluate risks, opportunities, and greenhouse gas emissions, culminating in a GES inventory finalized in 2022; the Comité métropolitain sur la résilience territoriale earned finalist status in the 2025 Prix d’excellence de l’administration publique du Québec for these adaptations.[44][47] Economic development highlights the Stratégie de développement agricole 2026-2034, launched to bolster producers in Capitale-Nationale and Lévis regions through collaboration with over 60 organizations, building on the prior Entente sectorielle agricole 2020-2022 for agro-food advancement.[7][48]

Criticisms and Controversies

Criticisms of the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) primarily revolve around challenges in territorial planning and coordination, where inter-municipal conflicts and political decisions have led to implementation controversies. An academic analysis of land use planning projects within the CMQ identifies recurring disputes stemming from fragmented decision-making processes, including overrides by the City of Québec on peripheral developments, which undermine cohesive metropolitan strategies. These issues highlight causal tensions between local autonomy and regional imperatives, often resulting in delayed or inconsistent outcomes despite statutory frameworks established under Quebec's territorial planning laws.[49] Transportation planning has also drawn scrutiny, with experts critiquing the CMQ's role in the structuring public transit network for inadequate integration and responsiveness to ridership demands. A 2021 study by Université Laval researchers documents controversies surrounding projects like the tramway and third link proposals, attributing inefficiencies to overlapping jurisdictions and suboptimal cost-benefit assessments that prioritize urban core investments over peripheral needs.[50] Broader densification debates in Quebec, intersecting with CMQ policies, reveal divisions over balancing growth containment against property rights, with some stakeholders arguing that metropolitan guidelines fail to curb sprawl effectively amid rising housing pressures.[51] The CMQ's 2024 Plan métropolitain d'aménagement et de développement (PMAD) has elicited further critique from environmental advocates and professional bodies for lacking sufficient actionable measures to reverse urban sprawl trends and bolster climate resilience. Organizations such as Carrefour Vivre en Ville contend that while the plan articulates intentions, it underdelivers on enforcement mechanisms and incentives for compact development, perpetuating reliance on automobile-dependent infrastructure.[52] The Ordre des urbanistes du Québec has similarly urged greater ambition, noting escalating concerns over habitat loss and infrastructure strain in recent years, despite the CMQ's coordination mandate.[53] No major ethical scandals directly implicating the CMQ have emerged, though broader municipal governance issues in Quebec, as exposed by the Charbonneau Commission, underscore ongoing risks of overlapping authorities fostering inefficiencies.[54]

Recent Developments

Updates Since 2020

In November 2020, the Communauté métropolitaine de Québec adopted the Programme d'aide financière 2020-2025, offering up to 25% funding for eligible metropolitan projects in areas such as economic development, infrastructure, and environmental initiatives to support recovery and growth following the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.[55] The organization advanced revisions to its foundational planning framework with the Plan métropolitain d'aménagement et de développement (PMAD), releasing a second draft in November 2024 after public consultations and adopting the updated version unanimously on August 28, 2025; this revision prioritizes intelligent urban densification, enhanced sustainable mobility options, and the preservation of natural and agricultural lands to accommodate projected population growth while mitigating sprawl.[22][19] In parallel, the CMQ endorsed a Stratégie métropolitaine de transition climatique à l'horizon 2040, which integrates principles of resource sobriety, social equity, and territorial resilience to guide long-term adaptation to climate risks, including through coordinated municipal actions on emissions reduction and vulnerability assessments.[56] Further initiatives included the launch of an ambitious agricultural and agro-food development strategy for the Capitale-Nationale and Lévis regions, developed in collaboration with over 60 stakeholders to bolster local production resilience and market access amid supply chain disruptions observed since 2020.[7] In 2025, the CMQ was selected as a finalist for the Prix d'excellence de l'administration publique du Québec, recognizing its Comité métropolitain sur la résilience territoriale for innovative climate adaptation measures; additionally, it appointed François Proulx as Chief Scientific Advisor to drive evidence-based innovations in metropolitan challenges and partnered with Natural Resources Canada on a regional wildfire predisposition study to inform preventive zoning and resource allocation.[47][57]

Ongoing Challenges and Reforms

The Communauté métropolitaine de Québec (CMQ) faces significant pressures from peripheral demographic growth and commuting patterns, with the number of municipalities outside its boundaries contributing substantial daily commuters rising from two in 2001 to six in 2021, exacerbating road congestion, greenhouse gas emissions from transport (which account for 56% of CMQ emissions), and strain on agricultural lands.[58] A March 28, 2025, report mandated by provincial law evaluated potential territorial expansion but recommended against immediate changes, instead urging clarification of inter-municipal coordination frameworks (OGAT) and establishment of an interregional consultation table to address mobility and infrastructure demands without altering boundaries.[58] Climate change adaptation poses another acute challenge, with accelerated impacts including recurrent floods in the MRC de Jacques-Cartier (e.g., Stoneham, Sainte-Brigitte-de-Laval, Lac-Beauport), December 2023 flooding in Lac-Beauport, coastal erosion on Côte-de-Beaupré and Île d'Orléans, and emerging water supply risks, necessitating a shift from isolated municipal responses to coordinated regional strategies.[59] In response, a April 2024 summit convened urban planners, fire chiefs, and civil security directors from CMQ municipalities to forge a unified territorial resilience vision, emphasizing anticipation, rapid reaction, and adaptation through shared protocols and projects like bank stabilization and urban greening funded by provincial and federal programs.[59] The CMQ's Comité métropolitain sur la résilience territoriale continues this work, earning finalist status in the 2025 Prix d’excellence de l’administration publique du Québec for climate action.[4] Transportation and mobility reforms address fragmentation among four regional transport authorities, which struggle with increasing demand and environmental goals, as highlighted in the October 2024-adopted Plan d'action 2025-2028 prioritizing modal shift toward public transit to meet long-term targets.[28] The Vision métropolitaine de la mobilité durable, also released in October 2024, builds on historical studies of structuring projects lacking finalized implementation, incorporating a June 2024 CDPQ Infra master plan to enhance connectivity across the full CMQ territory and reduce automobile dependency.[60] These initiatives aim to integrate sustainable practices amid broader pressures like housing shortages and economic transitions, though coordination with provincial infrastructure funding remains a persistent hurdle.[60]
