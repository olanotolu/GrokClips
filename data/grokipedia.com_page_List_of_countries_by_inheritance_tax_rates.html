List of countries by inheritance tax rates

Inheritance tax, variously termed estate tax or death duty, constitutes a fiscal levy applied by governments to the value of assets transferred to heirs or beneficiaries upon an individual's death, typically structured as progressive rates on amounts exceeding personal exemptions and deductions.[1] Rates and exemptions differ markedly across jurisdictions, with some countries employing top marginal rates as high as 55% on substantial estates—such as Japan—while others, numbering around 20 worldwide including Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and Sweden, have eliminated the tax entirely, often citing its negligible revenue contribution relative to economic distortions.[2][3] Proponents of inheritance taxation maintain it serves to capture revenue from unearned intergenerational transfers and curb the entrenchment of concentrated wealth, yet empirical assessments reveal it accounts for less than 1% of total tax revenues in most implementing OECD nations, prompting debates over its efficiency.[4] Critics, drawing on economic analyses, contend the tax impedes capital accumulation by penalizing savings and productive investments, imposes high compliance costs on estates and family enterprises, and yields limited redistributive effects due to legal avoidance mechanisms accessible to large fortunes, factors that have driven abolitions or reforms in countries like Sweden (2004) and Norway (2014).[5][6] Among nations retaining the levy, South Korea applies a top rate of 50%, France 45%, and both the United States and United Kingdom 40%, with thresholds calibrated to exempt modest estates but escalating sharply for high-value transfers; in contrast, the absence of such taxes in places like Hong Kong and Singapore underscores policy emphases on fostering wealth preservation and attracting mobile capital.[1][7] These variations highlight broader tensions between revenue imperatives and incentives for long-term economic growth, as jurisdictions weigh the tax's role in fiscal systems against its potential to disrupt natural family wealth succession.[4]

Conceptual Foundations

Definitions and Distinctions

Inheritance tax is a levy imposed on the value of property or assets transferred to beneficiaries upon the death of the decedent, with the tax obligation generally falling on the recipients rather than the estate itself. Rates are applied to the net value received by each heir, often varying based on the beneficiary's relationship to the deceased—such as spouses, children, or distant relatives—and may be progressive, escalating with the amount inherited. This structure incentivizes closer family members to receive preferential treatment, with exemptions or lower rates for direct descendants compared to unrelated parties.[8][9] In contrast, estate tax is assessed on the gross value of the decedent's entire estate before any distribution occurs, with payment drawn from the estate's assets prior to transfer to heirs. The key distinction lies in the point of taxation and responsibility: estate taxes target the aggregated holdings at death, potentially reducing the pool available for inheritance, while inheritance taxes apply post-distribution to individual shares, allowing the full estate to pass initially but taxing recipients separately. Some jurisdictions, such as certain U.S. states like Pennsylvania and New Jersey, impose inheritance taxes, whereas the federal U.S. system relies on estate tax; only six states currently levy inheritance taxes as of 2024.[10][11][12] Succession tax, sometimes used interchangeably with inheritance tax, refers to duties on the succession of property rights at death, often emphasizing the legal transfer aspect and applied similarly to beneficiaries in countries like France (droits de succession). Terminology differs globally: the United Kingdom's "Inheritance Tax" operates more akin to an estate tax by charging the estate's value above thresholds, though executors may recover from beneficiaries. These taxes are distinct from gift taxes, which apply to inter vivos transfers during life, and from capital gains taxes potentially triggered by deemed sales at death, though many systems provide basis step-up to avoid double taxation.[13][14][15]

Thresholds, Exemptions, and Effective Rates

Thresholds in inheritance tax systems denote the monetary value of an estate or bequest below which no tax liability arises, often structured progressively based on the beneficiary's relationship to the deceased. These allowances mitigate the tax's impact on smaller estates and family transfers, with amounts varying widely across jurisdictions; for example, the United Kingdom applies a standard nil-rate band of £325,000 per individual, transferable to a spouse for estates up to £1 million combined.[1] In Japan, a basic exemption of ¥30 million plus ¥6 million per statutory heir applies before progressive rates commence.[16] Thresholds for direct line heirs like children typically exceed those for siblings or unrelated parties, as in Greece where Category A beneficiaries (spouses and children) receive €150,000–€600,000 depending on the asset type.[16] Exemptions further reduce taxable bases by excluding specific transfers or assets entirely, prioritizing spousal and descendant inheritances to preserve family wealth continuity. Spousal exemptions are unlimited in countries such as Denmark, Ireland, and the Netherlands (up to €723,526 plus additional reliefs), while children's exemptions often include deductions like €400,000 in Germany or €90,000 in Finland for spouses/minors.[16] Charitable bequests receive full exemptions in jurisdictions including Belgium, France, and Germany, and business or agricultural assets qualify for reliefs—such as 85–100% in Germany or 90% in Ireland—to avoid disrupting operations.[16] Distant relatives or non-relatives face minimal or no exemptions, leading to higher effective burdens; for instance, in Belgium's Flemish region, unrelated heirs encounter rates up to 55% without family home reliefs available to descendants.[1] Effective rates, representing the actual tax paid as a proportion of the gross estate after applying thresholds, exemptions, and reliefs, diverge substantially from statutory marginal rates due to these mechanisms. OECD data show that exemptions for close relatives compress effective rates, with thresholds equivalent to USD 17 million or more in some countries versus near-zero in others, resulting in average effective burdens often below 10% for family estates.[4] For a hypothetical USD 150,000 inheritance with a USD 50,000 exemption and 10% marginal rate, the effective rate falls to 6.7% on the net taxable amount.[17] In practice, large exemptions for spouses and children—combined with planning tools like lifetime gifting (e.g., UK's 7-year rule)—can nullify tax for many estates, though unrelated or high-value non-exempt transfers sustain higher effective rates, as in France where post-allowance progressivity reaches 45% but caps limit overall revenue impact.[18]

Historical Evolution

Origins in Early Modern States

In England, the introduction of probate duty in 1694 under King William III represented an early formalized levy on inheritance transfers, functioning as a stamp duty applied to personal property detailed in wills submitted for probate in ecclesiastical courts. This tax, initially set at rates varying by estate value—such as 3 shillings per £100 for estates over £100—served primarily to fund military expenditures during the Nine Years' War against France, marking a shift from ad hoc feudal dues to systematic state revenue extraction from decedents' estates.[19][20] Across fragmented German states, including principalities that would later form parts of Prussia, inheritance taxes emerged sporadically from the late 17th century onward, often as princely impositions on successions to regulate land and capital transfers while bolstering absolutist treasuries amid mercantilist policies and warfare. These levies, typically flat rates on non-exempt heirs or specific asset classes like urban property, targeted middle-tier estates more than noble ones, reflecting rulers' efforts to centralize fiscal authority without broadly alienating aristocratic supporters; for instance, they built on older customs like the Erbschaftszoll but adapted them for emerging bureaucratic oversight.[21][22] Such duties in early modern Europe underscored causal fiscal pressures—rising state-building costs outpacing traditional revenues—rather than egalitarian aims, with exemptions frequently preserving elite wealth concentration; in absolutist contexts like the Holy Roman Empire's territories, they coexisted with primogeniture laws to maintain military-eligible estates intact, prioritizing dynastic stability over broad redistribution. Empirical records from state archives indicate these taxes yielded modest but reliable sums, often 1-5% of total fiscal intake, dependent on enforcement varying by local administrative capacity.[23]

20th-Century Expansion and Post-War Reforms

The expansion of inheritance taxation in the 20th century was primarily driven by the revenue demands of world wars, with governments leveraging progressive rates on large estates to fund military mobilization and ensure perceived "equal sacrifice" among citizens. In the United States, the Revenue Act of 1916 introduced a permanent federal estate tax with rates progressing from 1% on estates over $50,000 to 10% on amounts exceeding $5 million, explicitly aimed at war financing amid declining tariff revenues.[24] Rates quickly escalated during World War I, reaching up to 25% by 1917, while the tax base broadened in 1918 to include life insurance proceeds over $40,000 and certain powers of appointment.[24] In the United Kingdom, estate duty rates, originally set at up to 8% following the 1894 Finance Act, doubled to 40% by 1919 as wartime needs prompted shifts away from broader excise taxes.[25] Empirical studies across 19 countries from 1816 to 2000 confirm that mass military mobilization—such as the 10% population enlistment in the UK during World War I—correlated with 14-25 percentage point increases in top inheritance tax rates within five years, independent of suffrage expansions or pure fiscal deficits.[25] World War II intensified these trends, pushing top marginal rates higher amid unprecedented debt burdens. The U.S. estate tax reached 77% on large estates by 1942, with further base expansions to capture community property and most insurance policies paid by the decedent.[24] In the UK, estate duty's top rate climbed to 65% immediately post-war and 80% by 1949, supported by an expanded schedule of rate bands to capture more wealth transfers.[26] France saw progressive droits de succession rates rise to 42% for distant heirs on large estates by 1925 following World War I, stabilizing around 22% by mid-century, while Germany's Erbschaftsteuer increased from 8% to 23% and Japan's from 9% to 64% over the same period.[27] These hikes reflected causal links between wartime destruction of capital and sustained high taxation to repay debts, rather than isolated egalitarian motives often emphasized in academic narratives.[25] Post-war reforms in the late 1940s consolidated high rates within broader progressive frameworks, adapting to reconstruction and welfare expansion while addressing avoidance. The U.S. introduced a marital deduction in 1948, allowing up to 50% of the adjusted gross estate to pass tax-free to spouses, as a targeted relief amid 77% top rates, alongside credits for state death taxes to harmonize federal-state systems.[24] European systems, including the UK's estate duty and France's inheritance tax, retained steep progressivity— with top rates exceeding 70% in the UK and 20% in France— to generate revenue for social programs and infrastructure, though evasion via lifetime gifts prompted complementary measures like the U.S. gift tax revival in 1932.[27][26] By 1950, top marginal rates in major economies had risen from under 20% in the early 1900s to 50-77%, marking a peak in wealth transfer taxation tied to 20th-century conflicts rather than endogenous economic theory.[27]

21st-Century Repeals and Reductions

In the early 2000s, several European countries moved to abolish inheritance taxes, reflecting concerns over their limited fiscal contributions—often less than 1% of total tax revenue—and potential distortions to wealth mobility and family enterprises. Sweden eliminated its inheritance tax effective December 17, 2004, after rates had reached up to 30% for distant heirs, with the reform justified by administrative inefficiencies and minimal revenue (approximately 0.2% of GDP).[28] Portugal followed in 2004, replacing the tax with a 10% stamp duty on certain assets but exempting direct descendants entirely.[7] Slovakia abolished its inheritance tax on January 1, 2004, as part of broader tax simplification, eliminating rates that had varied by relationship and asset value. These repeals aligned with a broader shift in Eastern and Northern Europe toward competitive tax regimes to attract investment. By mid-decade, the trend extended beyond Europe. Russia repealed its inheritance tax in 2005, transitioning to a flat 13% personal income tax on certain transfers but exempting close relatives.[7] Hong Kong, seeking to bolster its status as a financial hub, abolished estate duty in February 2006, ending a system with rates up to 15% on non-exempt estates.[3] Austria terminated its inheritance and gift tax on August 1, 2008, after rates as high as 60% proved unpopular and generated negligible revenue relative to compliance costs; real estate transfers now incur a 3.5% land transfer tax instead.[29] Singapore similarly eliminated estate duty in 2008, removing rates up to 10% to enhance appeal for high-net-worth individuals.[30] Later reforms included further abolitions in Central and Northern Europe. The Czech Republic ended inheritance tax at the close of 2013, citing low yields and high evasion rates under prior progressive brackets up to 40%.[31] Norway abolished inheritance tax effective January 1, 2014, with rates previously up to 30% for non-direct heirs; the move introduced a "continuity principle" for income tax basis carryover to mitigate revenue loss while simplifying administration.[32] Significant reductions, rather than full repeals, occurred in jurisdictions retaining the tax but easing burdens. In the United States, the Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001 phased down federal estate tax rates from 55% to 45% by 2006 and raised exemptions, culminating in a one-year repeal in 2010; subsequent legislation in 2011 reinstated it at 35%-40% with a $5 million exemption (inflation-adjusted), doubled to approximately $11 million per person under the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.[33] Germany's 2009 Inheritance and Gift Tax Act reform adjusted valuation methods for family businesses and increased exemptions for business assets (up to 100% relief for qualifying transfers), though top marginal rates remained at 50%; this addressed constitutional challenges and reduced effective burdens for entrepreneurs without lowering headline rates.[34] These changes often prioritized economic incentives over revenue, with empirical analyses showing inheritance taxes yield below 0.5% of GDP in retaining OECD countries.[18]

Current Global Rates

Countries Without Inheritance Tax

A number of countries have eliminated inheritance or estate taxes, often citing reduced economic distortions and minimal revenue yields as justifications. These taxes, when present, typically apply to the value of assets transferred upon death, but their absence does not preclude other levies such as capital gains taxes on deemed dispositions at death or stamp duties on property transfers. As of 2025, abolitions span developed and emerging economies, with many changes occurring in the early 21st century to align with pro-growth fiscal policies.[3][18] European examples include Austria, which repealed its inheritance tax in 2008; Norway, which abolished it in 2014; Slovakia, which eliminated it in 2004; Sweden, which phased it out starting in 2004 and fully in 2019; and Portugal, which ended the tax in 2004 with full exemptions for spouses and direct descendants.[3][35] In Eastern Europe, Czechia abolished estate taxes in 2014, while Estonia and Latvia maintain no such tax, though capital gains may apply to certain inherited real estate.[35] Non-European OECD members like Canada impose no estate tax but trigger capital gains on death; Australia ended federal estate duties in 1979, with states generally following suit; Mexico has no inheritance tax, favoring donation exemptions for lineal heirs; and New Zealand repealed estate duty in 1992.[3][18] In Asia, Hong Kong abolished estate duty in 2006, Singapore in 2008, and Macau in 2001, with no accompanying wealth or gift taxes in these jurisdictions.[3] Israel eliminated estate tax prior to 2000, while other nations like India, China (except real estate transfer fees), and several Gulf states (e.g., UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia) never adopted inheritance taxes, often under civil or Sharia-influenced systems.[35] Jurisdictions such as Vanuatu and Serbia offer no estate tax or broad family exemptions, respectively.[3]

Economic Implications

Revenue Contributions and Fiscal Role

Across OECD countries that impose inheritance, estate, and gift taxes, these levies averaged 0.5% of total tax revenues in 2018, with the figure dipping to 0.36% in 2019.[4][40] This modest share reflects narrow tax bases, high exemptions, and avoidance strategies, rendering the taxes a minor fiscal instrument despite rates often exceeding 40%.[18] In countries like South Korea, the contribution reaches 1.59% of total tax revenue, the highest among OECD peers, driven by broader enforcement on large estates.[37] Variations persist regionally: in Europe, revenues often fall below 1% of total taxation, even in high-rate nations like France and Belgium, where shares hover around 1-2% amid exemptions for family transfers.[41][42] The United States federal estate tax, for instance, yields under 0.5% of federal receipts annually, supplemented minimally by state-level inheritance taxes in a few jurisdictions.[7] In the United Kingdom, inheritance tax receipts totaled approximately £7 billion in recent fiscal years, equating to less than 1% of overall tax income, with volatility tied to asset market fluctuations.[43] Fiscally, inheritance taxes serve more as equity tools than robust revenue sources, given their low yield relative to income or consumption taxes, which dominate budgets at 20-40% shares.[18] Administrative costs, while lower per euro raised than for income taxes due to fewer filers, still strain resources for negligible gains, prompting repeals in over a dozen OECD nations since 2000.[7] Proponents argue the taxes fund public goods without broad economic distortion, but empirical data underscores their peripheral role, often overshadowed by capital gains or wealth taxes in revenue debates.[4]

Incentives for Wealth Transfer and Investment

Inheritance taxes diminish incentives for long-term saving and investment by imposing a levy on accumulated capital at the point of transfer, effectively reducing the after-tax return to heirs and acting as a back-loaded tax on capital that distorts intertemporal consumption-saving decisions.[44] Theoretical models, such as those incorporating bequest motives, demonstrate that higher estate tax rates lower the marginal incentive to accumulate wealth, with elasticities of net worth at death estimated at 0.1 to 0.2, implying that a 10% increase in the tax rate reduces reported wealth by 1-2%.[44] This substitution effect encourages current consumption over future-oriented investment, as individuals anticipate diminished intergenerational payoffs from capital accumulation.[45] Empirical studies confirm these distortions, showing that estate taxes shrink the size of taxable estates through avoidance behaviors and reduced wealth buildup, thereby suppressing broader capital formation available for productive investment.[45] For instance, higher transfer taxes correlate with lower rates of self-employment continuation among older workers, decreasing by 8.3-8.7 percentage points, as heirs face reduced after-tax inheritance to fund or sustain family enterprises.[46] Wealth transfer incentives shift toward inefficient strategies like accelerated inter vivos gifting—evidenced by a quadrupling of gifts following 1976 U.S. tax changes—or asset liquidation, rather than preserving capital for reinvestment.[44] Repeals of inheritance taxes illustrate reversed incentives, as seen in Sweden's 2004 abolition, which prompted capital repatriation (with 8,000 self-corrections reported by the tax agency from 2010-2014), facilitated business successions (92% of owners reported easier transfers), and boosted internal ownership continuity by 6% post-2007, drawing entrepreneurs back and enhancing long-term investment focus.[47] Such reforms reduce the need to divest productive assets for tax settlement, preserving incentives for wealth creation and transfer across generations without the drag of mortality-contingent levies.[45]

Effects on Family Businesses and Entrepreneurship

Inheritance taxes can impose significant liquidity constraints on family-owned businesses, which often hold substantial value in illiquid assets such as real estate, machinery, and goodwill, rather than cash reserves sufficient to cover tax liabilities upon the owner's death. This mismatch frequently compels heirs to sell assets, liquidate operations, or seek external financing to pay the tax, disrupting generational continuity and potentially leading to business failure or acquisition by non-family entities. Empirical analysis of U.S. estate tax data from 1996–2002 indicates that a 10 percentage point increase in marginal estate tax rates raises the probability of family business sale by approximately 4.6 percentage points, with farms and closely held firms particularly vulnerable due to their asset composition. Similarly, Italian firm-level data from 1995–2007 shows that higher succession taxes reduce the likelihood of intra-family transmission by encouraging sales to third parties, thereby diminishing firm investment and growth in subsequent generations.[48][49] These effects extend to entrepreneurship by eroding incentives for founders to build scalable enterprises, as the prospect of heavy taxation upon transfer discourages risk-taking and long-term investment in human capital or innovation tied to family succession. Quantitative models incorporating borrowing constraints and wealth inequality demonstrate that estate taxes reduce entrepreneurial entry rates, with simulations suggesting that eliminating such taxes could increase aggregate business capital by 5–10% through enhanced bequest motives and reduced distortionary avoidance behaviors like premature diversification. Survey evidence from the Panel Study of Income Dynamics further reveals that inheritances boost recipients' business income probability by 13%, implying that taxes diminishing bequest sizes hinder new venture formation, particularly among liquidity-constrained heirs. In high-tax environments, entrepreneurs may preemptively limit firm expansion to minimize future estate tax exposure, as documented in analyses of U.S. closely held corporations where estate tax considerations correlate with subdued growth trajectories.[50][48] Repeals of inheritance taxes in countries like Sweden (2004) and Austria (2008) provide causal evidence of ameliorated pressures on family firms, with post-abolition periods showing no revenue shortfalls—Sweden's inheritance tax had yielded less than 0.2% of GDP annually—and reduced administrative distortions such as delayed deaths or asset flight. In Sweden, the tax's inefficiencies, including forced sales of viable family enterprises and valuation disputes, prompted its elimination; subsequent data indicate sustained or improved firm survival rates without compensatory revenue drops, underscoring how such taxes often fail to achieve fiscal goals while imposing real economic costs on entrepreneurial continuity. Cross-country comparisons reinforce that lower inheritance tax burdens correlate with higher prevalence of family-controlled firms, which tend to exhibit greater resilience and employment stability compared to diffusely owned corporations.[51][52][53]

Debates and Empirical Evidence

Arguments in Favor: Redistribution and Equality

Proponents of inheritance taxation argue that it facilitates wealth redistribution by imposing levies on large intergenerational transfers, which are often viewed as unearned windfalls that perpetuate economic disparities without contributing to the recipient's productive efforts.[54] This mechanism captures revenue from concentrated estates, directing it toward public expenditures that can benefit broader society, such as education and social programs aimed at leveling opportunities.[55] Empirical analyses, including simulations from U.S. tax data, suggest that raising estate tax rates could diminish wealth concentration among the top percentiles by redistributing a portion of elite fortunes, thereby narrowing the gap between the super-rich and others.[56] In terms of equality, advocates emphasize that inheritance taxes counteract the arbitrary advantages of birth, promoting a society where success relies more on individual merit than familial lineage.[57] By taxing bequests progressively, these levies challenge the reproduction of historical inequalities, as large inheritances amplify disparities in starting positions for economic mobility.[58] Cross-country evidence from OECD nations indicates that inheritance taxes, when structured efficiently, enhance equity by targeting transfers that exacerbate Gini coefficients for wealth, outperforming alternatives in balancing progressivity with administrative feasibility.[4] However, such effects are most pronounced on top-end concentration rather than median wealth, as inheritances disproportionately flow to already affluent heirs.[59] Theoretical frameworks, such as those linking estate taxation to reduced intergenerational persistence of advantage, posit that these taxes foster equality of opportunity by incentivizing decedents to allocate more to charitable or productive uses, indirectly broadening societal wealth dispersion.[60] Studies modeling progressive inheritance taxes further support that they can lower relative wealth inequality in the near term by curbing the inequality-augmenting impact of bequests, though long-term outcomes depend on behavioral responses like savings adjustments.[61] Overall, while not eliminating inequality—given that inheritances constitute a modest share of total wealth in many economies—these taxes are defended as a targeted tool for mitigating dynastic accumulation without broadly distorting earned income.[62]

Arguments Against: Double Taxation and Growth Distortions

Critics of inheritance taxes contend that they constitute double taxation, as the underlying assets—typically derived from income or capital gains already subjected to taxation during the decedent's lifetime—are taxed anew upon transfer without generating additional economic activity.[63] This view, articulated by economists such as Kyle Pomerleau, emphasizes that the estate tax applies to the same principal of after-tax income, akin to taxing consumption expenditures derived from prior earnings, which would inefficiently penalize wealth preservation over repeated cycles of transfer.[63] Proponents of this argument note that unlike sales of productive assets, which realize gains taxed once at the point of new value creation, bequests involve no such increment, rendering the levy a redundant burden on prior fiscal contributions.[64] Inheritance taxes are further argued to distort economic growth by discouraging savings and capital accumulation, as individuals and families anticipate erosion of accumulated wealth at death, favoring immediate consumption or suboptimal avoidance strategies over long-term investment.[65] This incentive shift reduces the pool of funds available for productive capital, such as equipment purchases that enhance worker productivity and wage growth, while compelling asset liquidations to cover tax liabilities, which disrupts business continuity and hampers expansion.[65] Empirical analyses support these distortions; for instance, a study by Holtz-Eakin and Smith (2009) estimates that repealing the U.S. estate tax would generate up to 1.5 million additional jobs by alleviating these pressures on entrepreneurship and resource allocation.[66] In family-owned enterprises, inheritance taxes exacerbate growth impediments by necessitating sales or dissolutions to meet obligations, particularly where illiquid assets like business equity predominate, leading to empirically observed correlations between higher tax rates and subdued employment growth.[46] A survey-based analysis of New York business owners found a negative relationship between estate tax rates and firm employment expansion, attributing this to heightened costs and risks that deter reinvestment and succession planning.[46] Such effects compound over generations, as reduced intergenerational wealth transfer undermines incentives for risk-taking and innovation, with broader econometric modeling indicating that estate tax burdens contribute to slower overall capital formation and productivity gains.[67]

Cross-Country Studies and Causal Analyses

Cross-country comparative analyses reveal that inheritance taxes typically generate limited fiscal revenue, averaging approximately 0.5% of GDP across OECD countries as of 2021, underscoring their marginal role relative to income or consumption taxes. These levies aim to curb intergenerational wealth concentration, with empirical assessments indicating they can modestly reduce inequality by targeting substantial estates, yet their efficacy is curtailed by prevalent exemptions for spouses, direct descendants, and certain assets, which narrow the tax base and diminish both revenue yields and redistributive outcomes.[4] Multi-country empirical investigations, drawing on household survey data from Germany, Spain, France, Italy, Great Britain, and the United States, demonstrate that inheritances generally mitigate relative wealth inequality in the short term by distributing assets to a wider array of recipients, though large transfers—defined as those exceeding the 95th percentile of national distributions—exacerbate inequality by amplifying concentration among top wealth holders. Such patterns imply potential for inheritance taxation to counteract the equalizing effects of smaller bequests while addressing distortionary large ones, but alignments between tax exemption thresholds and these large-transfer cutoffs vary widely without a consistent cross-country correlation to observed inequality shifts.[59] Causal identification of inheritance tax effects proves elusive in cross-country settings due to policy endogeneity and data limitations, though quasi-experimental evidence from reforms highlights behavioral responses; for instance, bequest taxation induces negative wealth effects on heirs, elevating their labor supply and thereby boosting labor income tax receipts to partially offset deadweight losses. Broader cross-country evidence, including OECD aggregates, finds no robust causal linkage between inheritance taxation and impeded economic growth, investment, or entrepreneurship, with efficiency costs appearing contained despite theoretical incentives for avoidance and reduced savings.[4][68]
