Geochemical cycle

In Earth science, a geochemical cycle is the pathway followed by chemical elements as they move and transform through the Earth's reservoirs, including the surface, crust, atmosphere, hydrosphere, and biosphere.[1] The term "geochemical" indicates the integration of geological and chemical processes. These cycles involve the migration and transformation of elements through various mechanisms, such as crystallization, weathering, erosion, sedimentation, subduction, and volcanism. The geochemical cycle encompasses the natural separation and concentration of elements (geochemical differentiation) and heat-assisted recombination processes. Changes may not be apparent over short timescales, unlike some aspects of biogeochemical cycles, but over geological timescales, they result in profound transformations, such as the evolution of continents and oceans.[1]

Differentiating biogeochemical cycles

Some may use the terms biogeochemical cycle and geochemical cycle interchangeably because both cycles deal with Earth's reservoirs. However, a biogeochemical cycle refers to the chemical interactions in surface reservoirs such as the atmosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere, and biosphere. The key difference lies in the inclusion of biological components in biogeochemical cycles, which involve living organisms, whereas a geochemical cycle refers to the chemical interactions that exist in crustal and subcrustal reservoirs as well as on the surface, such as the deep earth and lithosphere, primarily addressing abiotic geological and chemical transformations.[1]

Earth system

The Earth, as a system, is open to radiation from the sun and space, but is practically closed with regard to matter.[2] As all closed systems, it follows the law of conservation of mass which states that matter cannot be created nor destroyed, thus, the matter, although transformed and migrated, remains the same as when the Earth was formed. The Earth system contains several reservoirs that are separated into surface reservoirs, which include atmosphere, hydrosphere, biosphere, pedosphere, and lithosphere, and the deep Earth, which consists of Earth's mantle and core.[2] Geochemical cycles are concerned with the interactions between deep Earth and the lithosphere, which consists of the Earth's crust and uppermost mantle.

Pathways in Geochemical Cycles

Pathways

Flux in geochemical cycles is the movement of material between the deep Earth and the surface reservoirs. This occurs through two different processes: volcanism and subduction of tectonic plates. Subduction is the process that takes place at convergent boundaries by which one tectonic plate moves under another tectonic plate and sinks into the mantle as the plates converge. This leads to the sinking of one plate into the mantle which creates a broad range of geochemical transformations or cycling. Volcanism is the process that takes place at various tectonic settings, including divergent boundaries where tectonic plates separate, creating a rift in which molten rock (magma) erupts onto the surface of the Earth, and convergent boundaries where subduction-related melting produces volcanic arcs. This molten rock then cools and crystallizes, forming igneous rocks. If crystallization occurs at the Earth's surface, extrusive igneous rocks are formed; if crystallization occurs within the Earth's lithosphere, intrusive igneous rocks are formed which can then be brought to Earth's surface by denudation.[3] [4]

Important cycles

Examples of important geochemical cycles include:
  • Deep water cycle: The subduction of water-rich sediments and altered oceanic crust into the mantle, with release through volcanic activity, playing a key role in Earth's water budget and plate tectonics.[5]
  • Deep carbon cycle (part of the broader carbon cycle): Involves the transport of carbon into the deep Earth via subduction and its return to the surface via volcanism and metamorphism, influencing long-term climate regulation.[6]
