Carbine Williams

Carbine Williams is a 1952 American drama film directed by Richard Thorpe and starring James Stewart, Jean Hagen and Wendell Corey. The film follows the life of its namesake, David Marshall Williams, who invented the operating principle for the M1 Carbine while in a North Carolina prison. The M1 Carbine was used extensively by the U.S. military during World War II, the Korean War, and the Vietnam War. Originally filmed in black-and-white, it is also shown in a computer-colorized version.[2]

Plot

The film dramatizes the life of David Marshall Williams, who developed the short-stroke gas system for the semi-automatic M1 Carbine while serving a prison sentence, later working with the Winchester team to produce the rifle used in World War II.[3] While on a business trip to the Winchester Repeating Arms Company, preeminent gun designer Marsh Williams (James Stewart) is urgently summoned home by his wife Maggie (Jean Hagen) due to schoolyard gossip affecting their son. Marsh takes his son to meet Capt. H. T. "Cap" Peoples (Wendell Corey), the warden at Central Prison in North Carolina, who recounts Marsh's past. As a young man, after quitting school and serving in the Navy, Marsh marries Maggie and takes menial jobs while awaiting inheritance of land from his strict father, Claude. Impatient, he begins distilling illegal moonshine with partners Redwick Karson and Sam Markley, keeping it secret from Maggie. An explosion at the still injures him and reveals the operation. During a raid by revenue agents led by Jesse Rimmer, a shootout results in the death of Deputy Al Pate, for which Marsh is held responsible and convicted of murder. He is sentenced to thirty years' hard labor at Caledonia prison. Marsh cycles through harsh conditions in the prison system, refusing to work until a doctor intervenes. Eventually transferred, a firm but compassionate warden, H.T. Peoples, allows him to work in a prison tool shop. There, Marsh experiments and invents a rifle mechanism using a short-stroke piston gas system, creating the prototype for his famous carbine. Williams was eventually released from prison on parole and worked with Winchester Firearms on further development of the M1 Carbine, leading to its widespread use by the U.S. military.

Reception

According to MGM records the film earned $1,787,000 in the US and Canada and $802,000 elsewhere, for a total of $2,589,000, resulting in a profit of $575,000. The film received mixed reviews from critics upon release. Bosley Crowther of The New York Times described it as a "bewildering combination of personal attributes" in Stewart's portrayal, praising the performances but noting the story's dramatic inconsistencies.[4] Retrospectively, on Rotten Tomatoes, the film holds a 55% approval rating based on 20 reviews, with an average rating of 5.7/10.[5]
