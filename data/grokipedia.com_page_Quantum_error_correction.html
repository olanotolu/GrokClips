Quantum error correction

Quantum error correction (QEC) is a set of techniques used in quantum computing to protect quantum information from errors due to decoherence and other quantum noise. Quantum error correction is theorised as essential to achieve fault tolerant quantum computing that can reduce the effects of noise on stored quantum information, faulty quantum gates, faulty quantum state preparation, and faulty measurements. Effective quantum error correction would allow quantum computers with low qubit fidelity to execute algorithms of higher complexity or greater circuit depth.[1] Classical error correction often employs redundancy. The simplest albeit inefficient approach is the repetition code. A repetition code stores the desired (logical) information as multiple copies, andâ€”if these copies are later found to disagree due to errors introduced to the systemâ€”determines the most likely value for the original data by majority vote. For instance, suppose we copy a bit in the one (on) state three times. Suppose further that noise in the system introduces an error that corrupts the three-bit state so that one of the copied bits becomes zero (off) but the other two remain equal to one. Assuming that errors are independent and occur with some sufficiently low probability ppp, it is most likely that the error is a single-bit error and the intended message is three bits in the one state. It is possible that a double-bit error occurs and the transmitted message is equal to three zeros, but this outcome is less likely than the above outcome. In this example, the logical information is a single bit in the one state and the physical information are the three duplicate bits. Creating a physical state that represents the logical state is called encoding and determining which logical state is encoded in the physical state is called decoding. Similar to classical error correction, QEC codes do not always correctly decode logical qubits, but instead reduce the effect of noise on the logical state. Copying quantum information is not possible due to the no-cloning theorem. This theorem seems to present an obstacle to formulating a theory of quantum error correction. But it is possible to spread the (logical) information of one logical qubit onto a highly entangled state of several (physical) qubits. Peter Shor first discovered this method of formulating a quantum error correcting code by storing the information of one qubit onto a highly entangled state of nine qubits.[2] In classical error correction, syndrome decoding is used to diagnose which error was the likely source of corruption on an encoded state. An error can then be reversed by applying a corrective operation based on the syndrome. Quantum error correction also employs syndrome measurements. It performs a multi-qubit measurement that does not disturb the quantum information in the encoded state but retrieves information about the error. Depending on the QEC code used, syndrome measurement can determine the occurrence, location and type of errors. In most QEC codes, the type of error is either a bit flip, or a sign (of the phase) flip, or both (corresponding to the Pauli matrices X, Z, and Y). The measurement of the syndrome has the projective effect of a quantum measurement, so even if the error due to the noise was arbitrary, it can be expressed as a combination of basis operations called the error basis (which is given by the Pauli matrices and the identity). To correct the error, the Pauli operator corresponding to the type of error is used on the corrupted qubit to revert the effect of the error. The syndrome measurement provides information about the error that has happened, but not about the information that is stored in the logical qubitâ€”as otherwise the measurement would destroy any quantum superposition of this logical qubit with other qubits in the quantum computer, which would prevent it from being used to convey quantum information.

Bosonic codes

Several proposals have been made for storing error-correctable quantum information in bosonic modes. Unlike a two-level system, a quantum harmonic oscillator has infinitely many energy levels in a single physical system. Codes for these systems include cat,[6][7][8] Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP),[9] and binomial codes.[10][11] Recent advances as of 2025 include hardware-efficient logical qubit memories using linear arrays of bosonic modes.[12] One insight offered by these codes is to take advantage of the redundancy within a single system, rather than to duplicate many two-level qubits.

GKP code

The Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) code encodes a logical qubit into the continuous-variable phase space of a bosonic mode using idealized states that are superpositions of position eigenstates on a square lattice: the logical âˆ£0LâŸ©|0_L\rangleâˆ£0Lâ€‹âŸ© is a comb of delta functions at even integer positions, and âˆ£1LâŸ©|1_L\rangleâˆ£1Lâ€‹âŸ© at odd integers, with analogous momentum-space representations.[9] This allows correction of small displacement errors in position and momentum, which correspond to Pauli-like errors in the encoded qubit, by measuring syndromes via ancillary systems and applying corrective displacements.

A [5-qubit code](Five-qubit_error_correcting_code) is the smallest possible code that protects a single logical qubit against single-qubit errors. - A generalisation of the technique used by [Steane](Andrew_Steane), to develop the [7-qubit code](Steane_code) from the [classical [7, 4] Hamming code](Hamming$7%2C4$), led to the construction of an important class of codes called the [CSS codes](CSS_code), named for their inventors: [Robert Calderbank](Robert_Calderbank), Peter Shor and Andrew Steane. According to the quantum Hamming bound, encoding a single logical qubit and providing for arbitrary error correction in a single qubit requires a minimum of 5 physical qubits. - A more general class of codes (encompassing the former) are the [stabilizer codes](Stabilizer_code) discovered by [Daniel Gottesman](Daniel_Gottesman), and by [Robert Calderbank](Robert_Calderbank), [Eric Rains](Eric_Rains), Peter Shor, and [N. J. A. Sloane](N._J._A._Sloane); these are also called additive codes. - Two dimensional [Baconâ€“Shor codes](Bacon%E2%80%93Shor_code) are a family of codes parameterized by integers _m_ and _n_. There are _nm_ qubits arranged in a square lattice.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=15&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} - [Alexei Kitaev](Alexei_Kitaev)'s [topological quantum codes](Toric_code), introduced in 1997 as the toric code, and the more general idea of a [topological quantum computer](Topological_quantum_computer) are the basis for various code types.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=16&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} - [Todd Brun](Todd_Brun), Igor Devetak, and Min-Hsiu Hsieh also constructed the [entanglement-assisted stabilizer formalism](Entanglement-assisted_stabilizer_formalism) as an extension of the standard [stabilizer formalism](Stabilizer_formalism) that incorporates [quantum entanglement](Quantum_entanglement) shared between a sender and a receiver.[](https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0608027) - The ideas of stabilizer codes, CSS codes, and topological codes can be expanded into the 2D planar [surface code](Surface_code), of which various types exist.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=17&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} As of June 2024, the 2D planar surface code is generally considered the most well-studied type of quantum error correction, and one of the leading contenders for practical use in quantum computing.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=18&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=19&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} - Recent developments include quantum low-density parity-check (qLDPC) codes, a class of sparse stabilizer codes that promise lower qubit overhead for fault-tolerant quantum computing compared to surface codes.[](https://www.ibm.com/quantum/blog/large-scale-ftqc) That these codes allow indeed for quantum computations of arbitrary length is the content of the [quantum threshold theorem](Quantum_threshold_theorem), found by [Michael Ben-Or](Michael_Ben-Or) and [Dorit Aharonov](Dorit_Aharonov), which asserts that you can correct for all errors if you concatenate quantum codes such as the CSS codesâ€”i.e. re-encode each logical qubit by the same code again, and so on, on logarithmically many levelsâ€” _provided_ that the error rate of individual [quantum gates](Quantum_gate) is below a certain threshold; as otherwise, the attempts to measure the syndrome and correct the errors would introduce more new errors than they correct for. As of late 2004, estimates for this threshold indicate that it could be as high as 1â€“3%,{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=20&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} provided that there are sufficiently many [qubits](Qubit) available. ## Experimental realization There have been several experimental realizations of CSS-based codes. The first demonstration was with [nuclear magnetic resonance qubits](Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_quantum_computer).{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=21&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} Subsequently, demonstrations have been made with linear optics,{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=22&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} trapped ions,{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=23&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=24&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} and superconducting ([transmon](Transmon)) qubits.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=25&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In 2016 for the first time the lifetime of a quantum bit was prolonged by employing a QEC code.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=13&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} The error-correction demonstration was performed on [SchrÃ¶dinger-cat states](Cat_state) encoded in a superconducting resonator, and employed a quantum controller capable of performing real-time feedback operations including read-out of the quantum information, its analysis, and the correction of its detected errors. The work demonstrated how the quantum-error-corrected system reaches the break-even point at which the lifetime of a logical qubit exceeds the lifetime of the underlying constituents of the system (the physical qubits). Other error correcting codes have also been implemented, such as one aimed at correcting for photon loss, the dominant error source in photonic qubit schemes.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=26&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=27&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In 2021, an [entangling gate](Controlled_NOT_gate) between two logical qubits encoded in [topological quantum error-correction codes](Toric_code) has first been realized using 10 ions in a [trapped-ion quantum computer](Trapped_ion_quantum_computer).{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=28&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=29&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} 2021 also saw the first experimental demonstration of fault-tolerant Bacon-Shor code in a single logical qubit of a trapped-ion system, i.e. a demonstration for which the addition of error correction is able to suppress more errors than is introduced by the overhead required to implement the error correction.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=30&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=31&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=32&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In a different direction, using an encoding corresponding to the Jordan-Wigner mapped Majorana zero modes of a Kitaev chain, researchers were able to perform quantum teleportation of a logical qubit, where an improvement in fidelity from 71% to 85% was observed.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=33&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In 2022, researchers at the [University of Innsbruck](University_of_Innsbruck) have demonstrated a fault-tolerant universal set of gates on two logical qubits in a trapped-ion quantum computer. They have performed a logical two-qubit controlled-NOT gate between two instances of the seven-qubit colour code, and fault-tolerantly prepared a logical [magic state](Magic_state_distillation).{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=34&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In February 2023, researchers at Google claimed to have decreased quantum errors by increasing the qubit number in experiments, they used a fault tolerant [surface code](Surface_code) measuring an error rate of 3.028% and 2.914% for a distance-3 qubit array and a distance-5 qubit array respectively.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=35&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=36&&&citation_type=wikipedia}}{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=37&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In April 2024, researchers at [Microsoft](Microsoft_Azure_Quantum) claimed to have successfully tested a quantum error correction code that allowed them to achieve an error rate with logical qubits that is 800 times better than the underlying physical error rate.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=38&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} This qubit virtualization system was used to create 4 logical qubits with 30 of the 32 qubits on Quantinuum's trapped-ion hardware. The system uses an active syndrome extraction technique to diagnose errors and correct them while calculations are underway without destroying the logical qubits.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=39&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In January 2025, researchers at [UNSW Sydney](UNSW_Sydney) managed to develop an error correction method using antimony-based materials, including [antimonides](Antimonides), leveraging high-dimensional quantum states ([qudits](Qudit)) with up to eight states. By encoding quantum information in the nuclear spin of an antimony atom embedded in [silicon](Silicon) and employing advanced pulse control techniques, they demonstrated enhanced error resilience.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=40&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} In July 2025, researchers from Princeton University, NIST, and Quantinuum demonstrated the first experimental realization of the quantum threshold theorem using concatenated quantum error-correcting codes on Quantinuum's H2-1 trapped-ion quantum computer, achieving fault-tolerant error suppression with minimal overhead.[](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.00579) In July 2025, researchers integrated a [QMM](Quantum_Memory_Matrix)-based imprintâ€“retrieval cycle with a three-qubit repetition code on an IBM superconducting processor, raising the logical-state recovery fidelity from 77 % to 94 % without additional ancilla qubits.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=41&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} # Error suppression without encoding and parity checks In 2022, research at the University of Engineering and Technology Lahore demonstrated error cancellation by inserting single-qubit Z-axis rotation gates into strategically chosen locations of superconducting quantum circuits.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=42&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} The scheme has been shown to effectively correct errors that would otherwise rapidly add up under constructive interference of coherent noise.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=42&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} This is a circuit-level calibration scheme that traces deviations (e.g., sharp dips or notches) in the decoherence curve to detect and localize the coherent error, but does not require encoding or parity measurements.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=42&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} However, further investigation is needed to establish the effectiveness of this method for incoherent noise.{{grok:render&&&type=render_inline_citation&&&citation_id=42&&&citation_type=wikipedia}} More recent work has explored similar approaches. In 2025, distribution error correction (DEC) was introduced, which corrects the output distribution of a quantum computer to the ideal distribution without explicit encoding or parity checks, as investigated in theoretical studies.[](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.01953)
