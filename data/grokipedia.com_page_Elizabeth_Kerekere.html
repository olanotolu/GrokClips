Elizabeth Kerekere


Dr. Elizabeth Anne Kerekere is a New Zealand academic, artist, and former politician of Māori descent, affiliated with the iwi Te Whānau a Kai and Te Aitanga-a-Māhaki.[1]
She served as a list Member of Parliament for the Green Party from 2020 until May 2023, when she resigned amid an internal party investigation into multiple complaints of bullying and disruptive conduct toward fellow MPs, staff, and volunteers, including claims of rudely shutting down discussions and fostering division.[2][3]
Kerekere continued as an independent MP until the end of the term before retiring from national politics, though she later sought election to a Māori ward in Gisborne in 2025.[4][5]
Prior to and alongside her political career, she has been active in LGBTQ+ advocacy, founding the Tiwhanawhana Trust in 2001 to represent takatāpui (Māori term for sexual and gender minorities) on international stages, and holds a PhD related to Polynesian cultural perspectives on gender variance.[6][7]

Early Life and Background

Family Heritage and Upbringing

Elizabeth Kerekere was born in Gisborne (Turanganui-a-Kiwa), New Zealand, in 1965 or 1966, to a Māori father and an Irish mother.[8] Her father, Karauria Tarao "Bison" Kerekere, was a master carver and artist whose work emphasized traditional Māori motifs, instilling in her an early appreciation for indigenous design principles.[9] Through her paternal lineage, Kerekere affiliates with multiple iwi, including Ngāti Oneone, Te Aitanga-a-Māhaki, Te Whānau-a-Kai, Rongowhakaata, and Ngāi Tāmanuhiri.[10] Her mother, Erin, traces her roots to County Clare and County Tipperary in Ireland, contributing to Kerekere's mixed European-Māori heritage.[1] Kerekere was raised primarily in Dunedin, where her exposure to her father's artistic practice shaped her childhood. She spent much of her early years drawing, painting, and studying Māori design elements, activities that were integral to her family environment and foreshadowed her later pursuits in visual arts.[9] This upbringing fostered a deep connection to Māori cultural expression, though her family's relocation from Gisborne influenced a blend of regional influences in her formative experiences.[8] From a young age, Kerekere encountered activism through her mother's networks; at age 12, she babysat for children of Māori women involved in consciousness-raising groups, providing early insight into social justice discussions.[11] By 15, she was actively participating in advocacy efforts, reflecting the politically engaged atmosphere of her household.[12]

Personal Identity and Relationships

Kerekere identifies as takatāpui, a Māori term encompassing individuals with diverse sexual orientations and gender identities, often aligning with broader LGBTQ+ experiences within indigenous contexts.[11] She has explicitly described herself as a "lesbian, feminist femme," emphasizing a feminine presentation within her queer identity.[11] This self-identification informs her advocacy, where she draws on pre-colonial Māori perspectives of gender and sexual fluidity, though such interpretations remain debated among historians regarding the extent of historical acceptance versus colonial influences.[13] In her personal relationships, Kerekere is married to Alofa Aiono, whom she refers to as her wife.[14][15] The couple resides in Gisborne, New Zealand, along with their dog Indiana and cats Havana and Chicago.[1] Aiono has publicly supported Kerekere's career, including during parliamentary farewell speeches and campaigns.[16][17] No public records indicate children or prior marriages.[18]

Education and Early Professional Development

Academic Qualifications

Kerekere completed a Bachelor of Arts in Māori Visual Arts at Toihoukura, the School of Māori Visual Arts and Design at the Eastern Institute of Technology, graduating in 2010.[19][20] She subsequently pursued doctoral studies at Victoria University of Wellington's Te Kawa a Māui School of Māori Studies, earning a Doctor of Philosophy in 2017. Her thesis, titled Part of The Whānau: The Emergence of Takatāpui Identity – He Whāriki Takatāpui, examined the historical and contemporary dimensions of takatāpui (Māori individuals identifying with diverse sexualities and genders) identity and well-being, marking the first major scholarly work on the subject.[21][22]

Artistic and Creative Pursuits

Elizabeth Kerekere developed an early interest in visual arts through her family's involvement in Māori cultural practices, particularly influenced by her father, master carver Karauria Tarao (Bison) Kerekere, who introduced her to traditional carving styles and designs during visits to institutions like the Otago Museum.[23] Growing up near Te Poho o Rāwiri marae in Gisborne, which features a fully carved and woven wharenui, she engaged with art forms such as carvings and tukutuku panels from childhood, viewing them as vessels for ancestral stories and spiritual connection.[23] [9] Prior to formal higher education, Kerekere taught art in primary schools, but she pursued dedicated training at Toi Hauiti's Toihoukura School of Māori Visual Arts and Design in Gisborne from 2010 to 2012, committing full-time to multi-media practices including drawing, painting, clay work, weaving, carving, sculpting, fashion, and graphic design.[9] [20] Her work centers on kaupapa Māori themes, often exploring takatāpui (Māori LGBTQ+) identities and cultural narratives, adapting media to suit specific projects.[9] [24] During her studies, Kerekere completed a solo exhibition titled Mana Takatāpui as her final-year project, from which one piece was acquired by the New Zealand Parliament Art Committee for permanent display in the Rainbow Room to commemorate the passage of marriage equality legislation.[9] She specializes in public commissions, such as a carved pou for the Gisborne courthouse and a mural for the Gisborne Police Station, completed around 2013.[24] In the same year, she curated the Te Hā o Hineteiwaiwa exhibition at Thistle Hall in Wellington, showcasing multi-media works that highlight Māori artistic traditions.[24] [25] Kerekere has also contributed to group shows, including Claiming Identity: Creating Ourselves at Thistle Hall, emphasizing personal and cultural identity through visual expression.[26]

Activism and Advocacy

Takatāpui and LGBTQ+ Rights

Kerekere co-founded the Tīwhanawhana Trust in 2001 to support takatāpui—Māori individuals with diverse sexual orientations, gender identities, and sex characteristics—by facilitating storytelling, community building, and advocacy for their rights within Māori cultural frameworks.[1] The trust's initiatives emphasized elevating the mana (prestige and spiritual power) of takatāpui through Māori language revitalization, cultural practices, and addressing discrimination in health, education, and family structures.[27] Her early activism included participation in the Black Women's movement and campaigns for homosexual law reform in New Zealand, which decriminalized male same-sex activity on 9 July 1986, reflecting her engagement with intersecting racial and sexual justice issues during the 1980s.[28] As a scholar, Kerekere has researched the historical emergence of takatāpui identities, arguing that pre-colonial Māori society exhibited openness to sexual experimentation and gender fluidity, evidenced by oral traditions and accounts of same-sex relationships not stigmatized by colonial impositions.[13] [29] This perspective aligns with her broader documentation of Māori involvement in global gay liberation movements, intertwined with the Māori cultural renaissance from the 1970s onward, where urbanization facilitated queer organizing within iwi (tribal) networks.[30] She authored Growing Up Takatāpui: Whānau Journeys, which compiles narratives highlighting challenges like family rejection and healthcare barriers, while promoting whānau (extended family) acceptance as essential for mental health and cultural continuity.[31] Kerekere's advocacy extends to frameworks like Te Whare Takatāpui, a holistic model adapting the Māori health concept of Te Whare Tapa Whā (four pillars of well-being: physical, spiritual, family, and mental) to address takatāpui-specific needs, including suicide prevention and cultural affirmation.[32] Through public speaking and community resources, she has stressed that takatāpui belonging is inherent to Māori whakapapa (genealogy), countering narratives of Western importation by grounding identities in indigenous precedents.[10] Her nearly three decades of grassroots efforts have focused on empirical storytelling over abstract theory, prioritizing verifiable whānau experiences to challenge institutional biases in academia and health services that marginalize non-heteronormative Māori.[33]

Māori Cultural and Social Justice Efforts

Kerekere has engaged in grassroots activism centered on kaupapa Māori principles and Te Tiriti o Waitangi-related issues for over 40 years, addressing disparities in health, mental health, suicide prevention, violence prevention, education, justice, and disability services within Māori communities.[34] Her efforts emphasize building relationships and challenging inequities through community dialogue, particularly in contexts where Māori cultural frameworks intersect with systemic barriers.[35] In 2001, Kerekere founded the Tīwhanawhana Trust to promote Māori cultural representation on international LGBTIQ platforms, running weekly lotto fundraisers for 3.5 years to support Māori, Pacific, and low-income participants while uplifting takatāpui (Māori with diverse gender and sexual identities) through language revitalization and cultural practices.[6][10] The organization aimed to foster community storytelling, legacy-building, and advocacy for indigenous rights aligned with pre-colonial Māori values of inclusivity.[1] Kerekere's scholarly work contributes to Māori cultural revival by reconstructing takatāpui as an authentic pre-colonial identity, drawing on historical sources like diaries, missionary records, and artifacts to argue that traditional Māori society accommodated gender and sexual fluidity without colonial imposition.[13][33] In publications such as Takatāpui: Part of the Whānau, she examines how diverse identities face marginalization in modern social services and justice systems, advocating for culturally responsive reforms to enhance Māori well-being and decolonize queer narratives in Aotearoa New Zealand.[10][36] Through mentoring over 50 youth leaders and representing indigenous human rights alongside rainbow issues at the United Nations in Geneva, Kerekere has extended her advocacy to intergenerational cultural transmission and global equity for Māori.[34] These initiatives prioritize empirical recovery of Māori epistemologies over external narratives, though interpretations of pre-colonial fluidity remain debated among historians.[37]

Academic Career

Research Contributions

Elizabeth Kerekere's research primarily centers on takatāpui identity, defined as a traditional Māori term encompassing individuals with diverse genders and sexualities, and its intersections with whānau (family) dynamics, Māori health, and well-being.[21] Her work emphasizes indigenous perspectives, challenging Western queer frameworks by highlighting pre-colonial Māori acceptance of gender and sexual diversity while addressing contemporary colonial impacts on identity formation.[36] This focus stems from her position as a senior lecturer in the Te Kawa a Māui School of Māori Studies at Victoria University of Wellington, where she has contributed to decolonizing queer studies in Aotearoa New Zealand.[38] A cornerstone of her contributions is her 2017 doctoral thesis, Part of The Whānau: The Emergence of Takatāpui Identity - He Whāriki Takatāpui, the first comprehensive study on takatāpui identity and well-being.[21] Drawing on qualitative interviews with takatāpui individuals and whānau, it conceptualizes takatāpui experiences as woven into Māori social fabrics, identifying strategies for resilience such as cultural reconnection and whānau support to mitigate mental health disparities.[39] The thesis has informed community resources, including "Top Takatāpui Tips" for navigating identity challenges, and laid foundational groundwork for subsequent indigenous LGBTQ+ health initiatives.[39] By 2025, her body of work, comprising seven peer-reviewed publications, had garnered 33 citations, reflecting modest but targeted scholarly influence in niche indigenous studies.[38] Kerekere has extended her research into applied health domains, co-authoring works on takatāpui perinatal care and abortion experiences. In a 2025 publication, she contributed to Warming the Whare, an indigenous framework adapting Te Whare Takatāpui for trans-inclusive perinatal guidelines, prioritizing cultural safety and intersectionality in clinical settings.[40] Another study examined whānau relationships' role in enhancing takatāpui belonging and psychological outcomes, using thematic analysis to underscore familial bonds as protective factors against isolation.[41] These efforts, often collaborative with Māori health organizations like Tīwhanawhana Trust, have practical implications for policy, such as improving service delivery for Māori gender non-conforming individuals, though empirical validation remains limited to qualitative insights rather than large-scale quantitative data.[42]

Key Publications and Scholarly Impact

Kerekere's doctoral thesis, Part of the Whānau: The Emergence of Takatāpui Identity, completed in 2015 at Victoria University of Wellington, analyzes the historical traces of takatāpui—Māori individuals with diverse genders and sexualities—within pre-colonial narratives, asserting their integration into traditional whānau structures despite colonial suppression.[21] The 400-page work draws on oral histories, whakapapa, and textual analysis to reconstruct these identities, challenging Western queer frameworks by emphasizing indigenous epistemologies.[21] In 2016, she authored the entry "LGBT Activism among Māori" for The Wiley Blackwell Encyclopedia of Gender and Sexuality Studies, outlining the evolution of Māori-led advocacy from the 1970s onward, including groups like Ngā Wahine Ora and the integration of takatāpui perspectives in broader indigenous rights movements.[30] This peer-reviewed contribution highlights intersections between Māori sovereignty and sexual minority rights, citing specific activism milestones such as the 1980s formation of takatāpui support networks.[29] Subsequent publications include a 2020 analysis of Māori gender non-conforming identities via the takatāpui discourse, published on ResearchGate, which critiques postcolonial reconstructions and advocates for culturally specific identity frameworks over imported terminologies.[37] Kerekere has also co-authored works on whanaungatanga's role in enhancing well-being for takatāpui, published in 2024 in Health Promotion Journal of Australia, linking familial belonging to reduced mental health disparities based on qualitative data from Māori communities.[43] Her contributions extend to health equity, such as guidelines for trans-inclusive perinatal care informed by intersectional Māori frameworks.[44] Kerekere's scholarship, encompassing seven documented research outputs, has garnered 33 citations as of 2023 per ResearchGate metrics, influencing decolonization efforts in queer studies by inspiring community resources like the Part of the Whānau hub for takatāpui support.[38] [10] Her 2017 findings on takatāpui history prompted development of educational materials and policy discussions in Aotearoa, though interpretations of pre-colonial evidence remain subject to debate among historians regarding source limitations in oral traditions.[36] This body of work has informed midwifery curricula and surveys like Counting Ourselves, amplifying Māori voices in LGBTQ+ health research amid documented disparities, such as higher suicide rates among takatāpui youth.[45] [46]

Political Career

Path to Parliament and 2020 Election

Kerekere first engaged with the Green Party around 2013, leveraging her background in Māori cultural advocacy and LGBTQ+ rights activism to build political involvement. She stood as the Green Party candidate for the Māori electorate of Ikaroa-Rāwhiti in the 2017 New Zealand general election on 23 September 2017, but did not secure the seat, with Labour's Meka Whaitiri retaining it.[47][48] Following her 2017 candidacy, Kerekere's selection for the 2020 election reflected her established profile within the party, particularly among Māori and takatāpui communities. The Green Party ranked her ninth on its candidate list, determined through an internal member voting process, with the list finalized and announced on 25 May 2020. She was also nominated again as the electorate candidate for Ikaroa-Rāwhiti.[49][50] In the 17 October 2020 general election, the Green Party received 224,885 party votes, equating to 7.86% of the national vote, which under the mixed-member proportional (MMP) system entitled the party to ten seats in the 120-member Parliament. With no Green wins in electorate seats, the top ten list candidates, including Kerekere at ninth, were allocated seats, marking her entry to Parliament as a list MP.[50][51]

Parliamentary Roles and Legislative Initiatives

Kerekere served as a list MP for the Green Party in the 53rd New Zealand Parliament from November 2020 until her resignation from the party on 5 May 2023, after which she continued as an independent until leaving Parliament on 16 August 2023. She held spokesperson roles for the Green Party including Health from 23 November 2020 until 15 March 2022, Māori Development from 23 November 2020, Rainbow Communities, and acting spokesperson for COVID-19 Response from 15 November 2021. Within the party, she acted as Deputy Whip (Musterer) from 23 November 2020 to 8 May 2023 and chaired the Mātāwaka Caucus, focusing on Māori and Pasifika issues.[52] In select committees, Kerekere was a member of the Health Select Committee from 2 December 2020 until 8 September 2023, contributing to scrutiny of health-related legislation and submissions.[52] She also served on the Pae Ora (Healthy Futures) Legislation Committee from 28 October 2021 to 8 September 2023, which oversaw the implementation of health system reforms under the Pae Ora (Healthy Futures) Act 2022.[52] Additional committee involvement included the Justice Select Committee, where she participated in reviewing submissions on justice matters, and she chaired the Parliamentary Service Commission Artworks Committee from 24 March 2021.[52] She co-chaired the cross-party Parliamentary Rainbow Network and participated in parliamentary friendship groups such as New Zealand-North America (co-chair), New Zealand-Europe, and New Zealand-Latin America and the Caribbean.[52] Kerekere's legislative initiatives centered on expanding protections for rainbow communities. In her maiden speech on 25 November 2020, she announced plans for a member's bill to amend the Human Rights Act 1993 by adding prohibitions on discrimination based on gender identity or expression and variations of sex characteristics, aiming to uphold Te Tiriti o Waitangi and protect takatāpui and rainbow individuals; the bill was proposed but did not progress to enactment during her term.[53] As Green Party Rainbow spokesperson, she advocated for and contributed to the passage of the Conversion Practices Prohibition Legislation Act 2022, which bans practices intended to change or suppress sexual orientation or gender identity. She welcomed the revival and third reading of the Births, Deaths, Marriages, and Relationships Registration Amendment Bill in March 2021, which facilitated self-identification for gender marker changes on birth certificates without requiring medical certification.[54] In February 2022, she launched a Green Party petition calling for the establishment of a dedicated Ministry for Rainbow Communities to coordinate government policy and advocacy for LGBTQ+ rights across agencies.[55]

Controversies and Criticisms

COVID-19 Isolation Breach

On 11 March 2022, a household member of Elizabeth Kerekere tested positive for COVID-19, requiring her to self-isolate for seven days as a close contact under New Zealand's public health guidelines.[56] Despite three consecutive negative tests, Kerekere flew from Tairāwhiti to Wellington on 13 March 2022 to attend parliamentary duties, breaching the isolation rules.[57] [56] Green Party co-leaders expressed disappointment over the lapse in judgment, and Kerekere resigned from her COVID-19 Response and Health portfolios with immediate effect on 14 March 2022, issuing a public apology to constituents, whānau, colleagues, and frontline workers for the error and its potential risks.[57] [56] The party reported the breach to health authorities, who along with police decided not to pursue further action by 30 March 2022, at which point Kerekere was reinstated to the Health portfolio.[58]

Bullying and Misconduct Allegations

In April 2023, anonymous Green Party sources, including current and former staffers, accused Kerekere of bullying and "general mean girl behaviour," citing instances of badmouthing colleagues, undermining caucus members like Chlöe Swarbrick by dismissing her contributions and labeling her "entitled" or a "drama queen," and calling dissenters "racist" to stifle debate.[59] Additional accusers claimed her conduct included rudely shutting down Māori MPs in discussions, belittling staff and volunteers, and factionalizing teams to foster distrust.[60] The allegations intensified after Kerekere accidentally sent group chat messages during party list ranking discussions, criticizing Swarbrick's bill timing and calling her a "crybaby," prompting an internal party review and her temporary withdrawal from caucus activities.[59] [61] Kerekere apologized for the messages but denied they targeted Swarbrick specifically, and on 5 May 2023, she resigned from the Green Party amid the ongoing investigation, accusing co-leaders Marama Davidson and James Shaw of failing to de-escalate tensions and enabling "bad-faith actors" through flawed processes lacking natural justice.[59] [62] Upon her full resignation from Parliament on 15 August 2023 to sit as an independent before departing politics, Kerekere blamed an "epic failure of leadership" and described the bullying claims as "unfounded and increasingly elaborate" without formal complaints or tikanga-based fairness.[18] She countered by alleging racism within the party, though accusers maintained her behavior had created a toxic environment, particularly affecting Māori and Pasifika members.[18] [60]

COVID-19 Isolation Breach

On 11 March 2022, a household member of Green Party MP Elizabeth Kerekere tested positive for COVID-19, designating her a close contact required to self-isolate at home under New Zealand's public health rules during the Omicron outbreak.[63] Despite this, Kerekere flew commercially from Auckland to Wellington on 12 March 2022 to attend parliamentary duties, testing negative for the virus three times prior to the flight.[63] [56] Members of Parliament were not classified as critical workers exempt from isolation requirements, rendering the travel a clear breach of restrictions aimed at curbing transmission.[63] Kerekere, who served as the Green Party's spokesperson for COVID-19 response and health at the time, publicly acknowledged the violation on 14 March 2022, resigning from both portfolios with immediate effect and issuing an apology for failing to uphold the rules she had advocated.[57] [56] Green Party co-leaders reported the incident to health authorities and police on the same day, emphasizing accountability amid the party's emphasis on pandemic compliance.[63] Kerekere expressed dissatisfaction with the party's initial media statement, which she felt did not accurately reflect her personal apology or circumstances.[64] Health authorities and police investigated but opted not to pursue charges or further action against Kerekere by late March 2022, citing the absence of broader public health risk given her negative tests.[58] [65] She was reinstated to the health portfolio on 30 March 2022, though the episode drew criticism for perceived hypocrisy from the COVID-19 spokesperson.[58]

Bullying and Misconduct Allegations

In April 2023, Green Party sources accused Elizabeth Kerekere of exhibiting bullying behavior toward colleagues and staff, including rudely shutting down MPs during discussions and fostering distrust among staff by factionalizing them.[59][60] One anonymous party member described her conduct as "general mean girl behaviour," noting a pattern of labeling dissenters as racist to silence opposition.[59][3] The allegations intensified following an April 2023 incident where Kerekere sent a message calling fellow Green MP Chloe Swarbrick a "cry baby," prompting a formal party investigation into her conduct.[62] Additional complaints emerged from Māori and Pasifika party members, claiming Kerekere belittled and shut out other Māori MPs, staff, and volunteers, particularly those not fully aligned with her views, and weaponized party processes against them.[60][66] Parliamentary staff reportedly avoided interactions with her due to the distressing nature of her behavior.[3] Kerekere's supporters, including Māori and Rainbow Greens caucus members, dismissed the claims as "dirty politics" targeting her advocacy roles.[67] In response, Kerekere expressed hurt over the accusations and alleged racism within the Green Party, claiming the investigation process was flawed and that leaders had prior knowledge of concerns but failed to address them adequately.[68][69] The probe's prolongation led senior party members to threaten resignation, citing leadership failures.[70] Kerekere resigned from the Green Party on May 5, 2023, amid the ongoing investigation, criticizing co-leaders for an "epic fail" in handling internal disputes.[62] She continued as an independent MP until the end of the term, later accusing the party of abuse and racism in August 2023 statements.[4] No formal disciplinary outcomes were publicly detailed following her departure, as the party shifted focus to internal reforms.[71]

Resignation and Post-Parliament Trajectory

Departure from the Green Party

On May 5, 2023, Elizabeth Kerekere announced her resignation from the Green Party, citing dissatisfaction with the leadership's handling of an internal investigation into complaints against her conduct.[2] [62] The investigation had been prompted earlier that year by Kerekere inadvertently sending a critical text message intended for a select committee group chat to the entire Green Party caucus, which escalated tensions and led to formal bullying allegations from colleagues.[4] [72] Kerekere described the party's process as flawed, accusing co-leaders James Shaw and Marama Davidson of an "epic failure of leadership" that prioritized internal politics over resolution.[2] [73] In response, Green Party co-leaders Shaw and Davidson issued a statement confirming Kerekere's intention to serve as an independent MP until the October 2023 general election, after which she planned to retire from Parliament.[74] [75] They expressed regret over the departure but emphasized the party's commitment to addressing workplace issues through its complaints process, without commenting further on the specifics of the allegations.[76] [77] Kerekere's exit highlighted ongoing internal divisions within the Greens, including debates over party culture and accountability, which some observers linked to broader identity and focus struggles ahead of the election.[78]

Subsequent Activities and Public Statements

Following her resignation from Parliament on August 16, 2023, Kerekere returned to academic and activist roles centered on takatāpui (Māori queer) health and community advocacy. In March 2024, she was appointed Adjunct Professor in the School of Health at Te Herenga Waka—Victoria University of Wellington, where she contributes to research on sexual and gender diversity, including Indigenous queer theory in healthcare. She has presented on frameworks like Te Whare Takatāpui in international seminars, such as a September 2025 session hosted by Thammasat University's Research Unit in Sexual and Gender Diversity. Additionally, Kerekere co-investigated the "Warming the Whare" guideline, an Indigenous knowledge-centered approach to queer-inclusive healthcare published in the International Journal of Transgender Health on March 8, 2025, emphasizing intersectional support for takatāpui whānau. Kerekere maintained leadership in queer archives and trusts, serving as Chair of Kawe Mahara Queer Archives Aotearoa since 2023, overseeing the rebranding from LAGANZ and curating queer histories. Under her guidance, the organization launched Aotearoa New Zealand's first Queer History Month in July 2024, hosting a national hui taumata at the National Library to showcase taonga and affirm queer tipuna legacies; she stated that such efforts "affirm queer lives and histories" by educating communities on pre-colonial takatāpui expressions. As founder and ongoing Chair of Tīwhanawhana Trust since 2001, she continued advocating for takatāpui wellbeing through mātauranga Māori-based programs, including youth support initiatives like those under Mana Tipua, established in 2023 to tautoko rangatahi takatāpui in Christchurch. In Tairāwhiti (Gisborne), her iwi region, Kerekere engaged in local governance and community forums, speaking at Manaaki Tairāwhiti's Evidence and Insights Forum on November 27, 2024, discussing whānau-centered health innovations. By August 2025, she announced her candidacy for the Tairāwhiti Māori Ward in the Gisborne District Council elections, positioning herself as a voice for underrepresented communities amid regional recovery efforts. Public statements during this period focused on activism's role in queer visibility, as in a February 2025 Public Health Association webinar where she highlighted her decades-long commitment to rainbow health equity. Kerekere has not issued broad political critiques post-Parliament, instead emphasizing tikanga-guided community strengthening in governance roles since 2024.

Selected Works

Scholarly and Artistic Outputs

Kerekere's primary scholarly contribution is her 2017 PhD thesis, Part of the Whānau: The Emergence of Takatāpui Identity - He Whāriki Takatāpui, completed at Victoria University of Wellington.[21] This work, described as the first dedicated study on takatāpui identity—referring to Māori individuals with diverse sexual orientations and gender identities—and associated well-being, frames takatāpui experiences through a cultural lens of whakapapa (genealogy), mana (prestige), and inclusion within Māori society.[27] The thesis positions itself as a foundational "woven mat" (whāriki) for subsequent research, drawing on qualitative methods including interviews with takatāpui participants to explore identity formation and community belonging. She has also authored Takatāpui: Part of the Whānau (2021), published by the Tiwhanawhana Trust and the Mental Health Foundation of New Zealand, which extends her thesis findings into a resource emphasizing takatāpui integration within whānau (family) structures and mental health contexts.[79] Additional outputs include contributions to edited volumes, such as a chapter on "LGBT Activism among Māori" in The Wiley Blackwell Encyclopedia of Gender and Sexuality Studies (2016), documenting historical and contemporary activism within Māori communities.[30] Kerekere has collaborated on frameworks like Te Whare Takatāpui, an Indigenous model for LGBTQIA+ health and well-being informed by her research.[40] In her artistic practice, Kerekere works across media including drawing, painting, clay modeling, weaving, fashion, and graphic design, often centering takatāpui themes informed by her scholarly expertise.[9] Trained at Toihoukura School of Māori Visual Arts and Design from 2010 to 2012, she held a solo exhibition titled Mana Takatāpui in 2012, featuring works that blend traditional Māori elements with contemporary queer narratives.[9] A standout piece from this exhibition, the rainbow-colored tuktukutu (woven panel) Mana Takatāpui, was acquired by the New Zealand Parliament Art Committee in 2012 to mark the passage of marriage equality legislation and is permanently displayed in Parliament's Rainbow Room.[9] Her art has appeared in resources like takatāpui community publications and contributed to public discourse on Māori queer identities, as evidenced by her 2019 feature in the documentary Rainbow Voices of Aotearoa New Zealand.[10]
