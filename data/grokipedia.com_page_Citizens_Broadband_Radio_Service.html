Citizens Broadband Radio Service

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) is a shared radiocommunication service in the United States operating across the 3550–3700 MHz frequency band, authorized by the Federal Communications Commission under a three-tiered access framework that protects incumbent federal users such as Department of Defense radar systems, provides licensed priority access for commercial operators, and enables general authorized access for unlicensed broadband deployments.[1][2] This framework relies on automated Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) to dynamically assign spectrum while incorporating Environmental Sensing Capabilities (ESC) to detect and avoid interference with incumbents, facilitating coexistence without displacing primary users.[1] Priority Access Licenses (PALs), auctioned in blocks of 10 MHz channels for up to 10-year terms, grant secondary users protection from general access tiers but remain subordinate to incumbents, with a cap of four channels per licensee per census tract to promote broad participation.[1] CBRS has enabled rapid expansion of private LTE and 5G networks for enterprise applications, with deployments growing steadily—evidenced by a 121% increase in registered devices over 21 months through early 2023—and supporting diverse uses from industrial IoT to temporary event coverage, demonstrating the viability of automated sharing to unlock underutilized mid-band spectrum.[3] Recent FCC rulemakings seek to refine operations amid increasing demand, including proposals to enhance access while addressing potential congestion in high-density areas.[4]

Historical Development

Origins and FCC Rulemaking

The origins of the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) trace back to federal efforts to repurpose underutilized mid-band spectrum for commercial broadband while accommodating incumbent federal users, particularly Department of Defense (DoD) radar systems in the 3550-3700 MHz range.[1] In 2010, the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) identified the 3550-3700 MHz band as a "fast-track" candidate for spectrum sharing evaluation, building on recommendations from the President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology (PCAST) for advanced dynamic access technologies to maximize efficiency without exclusive licensing.[5] This initiative addressed growing demand for wireless capacity amid limited new spectrum availability, proposing coexistence with primary federal incumbents through automated protection mechanisms rather than relocation.[6] The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) formalized the rulemaking process via GN Docket No. 12-354, initiating a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) on October 2, 2012, which sought comment on enabling commercial operations in the 3550-3650 MHz portion through a novel three-tier priority system: incumbents (Tier 1), priority licensees (Tier 2), and general access users (Tier 3).[7] Public input during the comment period emphasized the need for robust interference avoidance, leading to a Further NPRM released on April 23, 2014, that expanded proposals to include the full 3550-3700 MHz band and refined spectrum access system (SAS) requirements for real-time dynamic allocation.[8] Coordination with NTIA and DoD was integral, focusing on environmental sensing capabilities to detect and protect shipborne radars, with testing authorized in designated areas to validate feasibility.[9] On April 17, 2015, the FCC adopted a Report and Order establishing CBRS under 47 CFR Part 96, codifying the three-tier framework to promote opportunistic shared use while prioritizing incumbents via SAS-mediated approvals and exclusion zones near coastal radars.[10] This rulemaking diverged from traditional exclusive allocations by mandating automated, database-driven management to enable 150 MHz of shared spectrum, with Priority Access Licenses (PALs) auctioned in 10 MHz blocks for three-year terms in the 3550-3650 MHz segment.[1] Subsequent refinements, including a 2016 Second Report and Order, addressed operational details like power limits and certification, ensuring implementation without disrupting federal missions.[11] The framework's innovation lay in its reliance on commercial SAS providers for enforcement, marking a shift toward market-driven spectrum governance informed by empirical pilots rather than theoretical models alone.[12]

Key Milestones and Initial Rollout

The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) initiated the development of the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) through a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in Docket No. 12-354 in April 2012, aiming to repurpose the 3.5 GHz band for shared commercial use while protecting incumbents.[1] In April 2015, the FCC adopted a Report and Order establishing the three-tiered access framework under 47 CFR Part 96, enabling dynamic spectrum sharing via Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) and Environmental Sensing Capabilities (ESC).[10] This was followed by an Order on Reconsideration and Second Report and Order in April 2016, refining technical requirements and certification processes for devices and systems.[12] Subsequent rulemaking addressed implementation details, including SAS approvals and interference protections. In 2017 and 2018, the FCC finalized rules for Priority Access Licenses (PALs) and General Authorized Access (GAA), with key updates in a 2018 Report and Order promoting investment and deployment.[13] By mid-2019, the FCC certified initial SAS administrators, such as Federated Wireless and Google, after laboratory testing to ensure compliance with spectrum allocation protocols.[1] Initial commercial rollout commenced with FCC approval for Initial Commercial Deployments (ICD) on September 16, 2019, allowing certified SAS providers to manage CBSD (Citizens Broadband Radio Service Device) operations under transitional protections for incumbent naval radar systems. This phase enabled limited GAA deployments, with early adopters like enterprise networks testing private LTE systems; for instance, vendors such as Cambium Networks began beta releases in early 2020. Full Commercial Deployment (FCD) was authorized on January 27, 2020, via Public Notice DA 20-110, lifting transitional restrictions and permitting unrestricted SAS-mediated access across the band, excluding dynamic protection areas.[14] Deployments accelerated thereafter, focusing on indoor and enterprise applications before the PAL auction (Auction 105) opened in July 2020.[15]

Technical Architecture

Spectrum Band and Sharing Model

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service operates in the 3550–3700 MHz portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, a 150 MHz band designated by the Federal Communications Commission for shared commercial and incumbent use.[1] This mid-band allocation supports high-capacity wireless broadband applications, including private LTE and 5G networks, while enabling dynamic spectrum sharing to accommodate varying demand and priority levels.[1] The sharing model employs a three-tiered hierarchy to balance incumbent protections with expanded access for commercial users, enforced through automated Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) that dynamically assign spectrum based on real-time environmental sensing and interference avoidance protocols.[1] Tier 1 (Incumbent Access) grants absolute priority to existing users, including federal incumbents across the full 3550–3700 MHz band (such as naval radar systems), Fixed Satellite Service earth stations in the 3600–3650 MHz sub-band, and grandfathered wireless broadband licensees in the 3650–3700 MHz sub-band; these users receive full protection from interference by lower tiers.[1] Tier 2 (Priority Access) consists of Priority Access Licenses (PALs), which provide licensed access to up to 70 MHz (in 10 MHz blocks) within the 3550–3650 MHz sub-band on a county-by-county basis, with licenses renewable every 10 years and a cap of four PALs per licensee per county; Tier 2 users are protected from Tier 3 interference but must avoid impacting Tier 1 operations.[1] Tier 3 (General Authorized Access, or GAA) permits unlicensed, opportunistic use across the entire 3550–3700 MHz band for any qualifying device, subject to no interference protection and mandatory deference to higher tiers via SAS-mediated channel assignments.[1] This framework promotes efficient spectrum utilization by prioritizing incumbents while enabling broad commercial deployment without exclusive licensing across the band.[1]

Access Tiers and Priority Mechanisms

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) employs a three-tiered hierarchy to manage spectrum access in the 3550-3700 MHz band, ensuring protection for primary users while enabling opportunistic sharing among secondary users.[1] This model prioritizes incumbents with absolute protection, grants licensed secondary access with conditional rights, and permits unlicensed tertiary use on available channels, all coordinated dynamically to minimize interference.[2] The system relies on automated mechanisms rather than static allocations, allowing real-time adjustments based on user activity and geolocation.[1] Incumbent Access (Tier 1) holds the highest priority and encompasses federal radiolocation services (such as U.S. Navy radar systems operating across 3550-3700 MHz), Fixed Satellite Service earth stations (3600-3650 MHz), and grandfathered wireless broadband licensees (3650-3700 MHz, with time-limited protections).[2] These users receive full interference protection from lower tiers, with Citizens Broadband Service Devices (CBSDs) prohibited from causing harmful interference and required to accept any from incumbents.[1] Initial exclusion zones, such as 80 km radii around federal sites, restrict CBRS operations until approved Environmental Sensing Capabilities (ESC) sensors verify no radar activity; upon detection, SAS must suspend affected CBSDs within 300 seconds.[2] Priority Access (Tier 2) consists of Priority Access Licenses (PALs), which are geographically licensed on a county-by-county basis in the 3550-3650 MHz band (100 MHz total), with up to seven 10 MHz channels per county auctioned by the FCC (e.g., via Auction 105 in 2020).[1] [2] PAL holders enjoy protection from General Authorized Access (GAA) users within defined PAL Protection Areas (typically -96 dBm/10 MHz contours) but must yield to incumbents, with SAS dynamically assigning contiguous channels where possible and enforcing relocations to avoid interference.[2] General Authorized Access (Tier 3) provides the lowest priority as a licensed-by-rule, unlicensed regime open to any qualified user across the full 3550-3700 MHz band, but only on spectrum unoccupied by higher tiers.[1] GAA operations receive no interference protection and must cease or adjust upon SAS directives to accommodate PAL or incumbent activity, enabling opportunistic use of idle PAL channels.[2] Priority mechanisms are enforced through the Spectrum Access System (SAS), a centralized database and decision engine approved by the FCC, which all CBSDs must register with prior to operation, reporting precise location, antenna details, and power levels.[2] The SAS performs geolocation-based frequency assignments, monitors for conflicts using propagation models and reported data, and issues heartbeat signals to maintain authorization; violations trigger automatic suspension.[1] For coastal incumbent protection, ESC sensors detect radar transmissions and relay data to SAS, triggering zone-wide evacuations.[2] This automated, data-driven approach ensures causal interference avoidance without manual intervention, though it imposes operational constraints like maximum power limits (e.g., 47 dBm EIRP for Category A indoor CBSDs) and Category B outdoor devices requiring enhanced coordination.[2]

Spectrum Access System and Sensing Capabilities

The Spectrum Access System (SAS) serves as an automated, cloud-based coordinator that authorizes and manages spectrum usage within the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) band, ensuring dynamic allocation of frequencies to Citizens Broadband Radio Service Devices (CBSDs) while protecting higher-priority incumbents and Priority Access License (PAL) holders from interference.[1][2] CBSDs must register with an SAS and receive explicit grants before transmitting, with the SAS performing real-time interference modeling, geolocation-based exclusion zones, and heartbeat signaling to enforce compliance and revoke access if violations occur.[16][17] Multiple approved SAS providers, such as Google, Federated Wireless, and CommScope, operate independently but coordinate via a SAS-to-SAS interface to resolve conflicts across CBRS domains, querying FCC databases for PAL records and incumbent protections.[18] SAS architecture integrates with the three-tier priority model by assigning up to 10 MHz PAL channels preferentially to Tier 2 users in census tracts, falling back to 80 MHz General Authorized Access (GAA) when higher tiers are active, and dynamically adjusting power levels or channels based on propagation models to maintain a maximum 1% aggregate interference probability toward incumbents.[19][20] This system relies on precise CBSD location reporting—accurate to within 50 meters outdoors and 2 meters indoors for Category A devices—to enable granular spectrum grants, with provisions for mobility via periodic heartbeats and relocation directives.[16] Environmental Sensing Capability (ESC) complements the SAS by detecting non-cooperative incumbent signals, particularly federal radar systems in coastal and border areas, to enable opportunistic CBRS access only when incumbents are absent.[1] Deployed as sensor networks in designated Environmental Sensing Areas (covering population census tracts within 164 km of U.S. coasts or Great Lakes), ESC uses radio frequency sensors to monitor for shipborne or fixed radar pulses, employing detection thresholds and algorithms validated by the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA).[21][22] Upon detecting activity, ESC nodes relay alerts to affiliated SAS providers within seconds, triggering immediate suspension of CBRS operations in affected areas to protect Department of Defense incumbents, with FCC-approved ESC operators like Key Bridge and Google demonstrating successful field tests as early as 2019.[23][24] ESC performance standards mandate low false alarm rates (e.g., via multi-trial hypothesis testing) and coverage redundancy, ensuring reliability without over-reliance on self-reporting by incumbents.[22]

Operational Standards and Enhancements

Device Types and Certification Processes

Citizen Broadband Radio Service Devices (CBSDs) function as fixed base stations or networks operating in the 3550-3700 MHz band under Priority Access or General Authorized Access tiers.[2] CBSDs divide into Category A and Category B, differentiated by maximum effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP), power spectral density (PSD), and installation constraints to balance interference risks with deployment flexibility. Category A CBSDs limit EIRP to 30 dBm per 10 MHz channel and PSD to 20 dBm/MHz, permitting indoor use without height limits or outdoor antennas up to 6 meters above ground.[2] These lower-power devices suit small-cell or indoor applications and allow non-professional installation in compliant scenarios.[2] Category B CBSDs enable macro-like coverage with EIRP up to 47 dBm per 10 MHz and PSD up to 37 dBm/MHz, but mandate outdoor-only deployment, professional installation, and additional reporting of antenna parameters such as gain, beamwidth, azimuth, downtilt, and height.[2] In the 3550-3650 MHz sub-band, Category B operations require Environmental Sensing Capability (ESC) approval to protect incumbent federal users.[2] Category assignment depends on certified power capabilities and actual deployment, with devices exceeding Category A limits registering as Category B.[2] End User Devices (EUDs) serve as client endpoints connecting to CBSDs, capped at 23 dBm EIRP per 10 MHz, and barred from peer-to-peer transmissions to minimize self-interference.[2] Unlike CBSDs, EUDs do not obtain spectrum grants but must align with SAS-mediated operations via their associated CBSD.[2] All CBSDs and EUDs require FCC equipment certification under 47 CFR Part 96 to verify compliance with emission limits, out-of-band protections, and dynamic spectrum access protocols.[2] The process entails lab testing for RF characteristics, submission of operational descriptions—including SAS interface, geolocation accuracy (±50 m horizontal, ±3 m vertical), and security against unauthorized use—to a Telecommunication Certification Body (TCB).[2][25] TCBs review data against Part 96 technical rules before granting an FCC ID, with certified details published in the FCC database for public verification.[25] Post-certification, devices must register with approved Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) providers, reporting location and adhering to real-time grants or heartbeats within 60 seconds to enable interference-free operation.[2]

CBRS 2.0 Upgrades and Reliability Improvements

CBRS 2.0, implemented in June 2024 through coordinated operational changes among Spectrum Access System (SAS) providers, enhances the Citizens Broadband Radio Service by refining interference protection mechanisms for federal incumbents without altering FCC rules.[26][27] These adjustments, approved by the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) and the Department of Defense, include shrinking Priority Access License (PAL) protection areas and incumbent user protection zones, thereby expanding unencumbered CBRS coverage to approximately 240 million Americans—an increase of 72 million people compared to prior configurations.[28][29] A core upgrade involves extending the device "heartbeat" interval—the periodic signal CBRS devices send to SAS providers for spectrum authorization—from 10 minutes to 24 hours.[30] This reduces the frequency of potential service disruptions, as devices no longer require near-constant reauthorization, allowing sustained connectivity even in dynamic environments near naval radar systems.[31] SAS providers also adopted refined propagation models and exclusion zone calculations to minimize unnecessary spectrum grants revocations, prioritizing empirical data on signal interference over conservative assumptions.[32] Reliability improvements under CBRS 2.0 have manifested in measurable outcomes, including an 85% reduction in service interruptions attributable to Department of Defense operations, as reported by the OnGo Alliance following initial deployments.[33] These enhancements enable more consistent performance for enterprise applications, such as private LTE/5G networks in industrial settings, by limiting opportunistic incumbent activations that previously fragmented available spectrum.[34] Independent SAS implementations, like those from Google Cloud, confirm broader spectrum availability through these procedural tweaks, which dynamically allocate channels while maintaining protection for incumbents via environmental sensing and database-driven coordination.[32] Overall, CBRS 2.0 shifts the framework toward greater predictability, fostering investment in mid-band private networks without compromising the three-tier priority hierarchy.[35]

Ecosystem and Stakeholders

OnGo Alliance Role

The OnGo Alliance, formerly known as the CBRS Alliance, serves as the primary industry consortium dedicated to advancing the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) ecosystem in the 3.5 GHz band. Established in 2016 by telecommunications and technology leaders, it focuses on promoting the development, commercialization, and adoption of LTE and 5G private wireless networks utilizing shared spectrum. With approximately 89 members as of August 2025, including equipment vendors, network operators, and system integrators, the alliance drives interoperability and ecosystem growth through collaborative efforts.[29][36] A core function of the OnGo Alliance is the development and maintenance of technical specifications that ensure compliance with Federal Communications Commission (FCC) rules while enabling reliable spectrum sharing. It produces documents such as the CBRS Network Services Use Cases and Requirements (version 4.0.0, published March 2021) and supports baseline standards for post-initial certification under CBRS 2.0, including enhancements for reliability and 5G standalone operations adopted in 2024. These specifications address network services, device interoperability, and coexistence mechanisms, such as guidelines for General Authorized Access (GAA) users to minimize interference without relying on centralized directives. The alliance's work emphasizes technology-neutral standards that prioritize empirical performance in dynamic shared environments over rigid allocations.[37][38][33] The OnGo Alliance administers the OnGo Certified program, a voluntary certification process that verifies CBRS devices meet interoperability, performance, and FCC Part 96 compliance criteria. Launched in 2018, the process requires manufacturers to engage authorized test labs for mandatory and optional testing, including spectrum access system (SAS) interactions and environmental sensing capabilities (ESC) validation, before granting the certification seal. By December 2024, this program had certified products facilitating widespread deployments, expediting FCC approvals and reducing deployment risks for enterprises. The alliance maintains a list of authorized test labs and publishes test plans, such as OnGo Release 1 Certification Test Plan (version 1.3.0, January 2023), to standardize evaluations.[39][40][41] Beyond standards and certification, the OnGo Alliance evangelizes CBRS use cases through resources, events, and partnerships, targeting sectors like manufacturing, transportation, and rural broadband. It fosters a trusted ecosystem by providing deployment guides, such as best practices for GAA coexistence (published October 2024), and hosts plugfests for 5G standalone testing to validate real-world connectivity. In 2024, the alliance reported record CBRS deployments post-CBRS 2.0 rollout, attributing momentum to its efforts in addressing industry needs for reliable private networks amid spectrum constraints. This role positions the OnGo Alliance as a counterbalance to traditional carrier-dominated models, enabling opportunistic access that empirical data shows improves utilization efficiency in underused bands.[42][43][44]

SAS Providers and Major Deployers

The Spectrum Access System (SAS) in the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) is managed by FCC-certified providers tasked with dynamically allocating spectrum, registering Citizens Broadband Radio Service Devices (CBSDs), and enforcing interference protection for incumbents, Priority Access Licensees, and General Authorized Access users across the 3550-3700 MHz band.[18] These providers operate approved software platforms that query environmental sensing capabilities, process heartbeat messages from CBSDs, and issue grants or denials for spectrum use in real time, ensuring compliance with power limits and exclusion zones.[19] As of January 2025, the active FCC-approved SAS providers seeking recertification include Google, Federated Wireless, and Sony, following the retirement of Amdocs' SAS operations in April 2025 and CommScope's withdrawal in 2023, with prior approvals extending to seven providers including Key Bridge.[45][46] Google’s cloud-based SAS, for instance, integrates with network operators for automated CBSD management and supports both GAA and PAL tiers, contributing to specifications via the WInnForum.[16] Federated Wireless and Sony similarly handle nationwide coordination, with Sony focusing on SAS operations in the full CBRS band to enable commercial deployments.[47] Major deployers of CBRS networks encompass enterprises, ports, manufacturers, and rural broadband providers leveraging private LTE and 5G for mission-critical applications, with over 400,000 CBSDs deployed nationwide by late 2024.[48] Chevron Phillips Chemical (CPChem) stands out as a significant deployer, operating eight private LTE networks on CBRS spectrum for petrochemical operations as of 2023 to enhance connectivity in hazardous environments.[49] Automotive and logistics sectors feature prominently, such as BMW's CBRS-based network at its Spartanburg factory for warehouse automation and Husky Terminal's deployment at the Port of Tacoma for IoT-enabled port management.[50] Rural fixed wireless access providers like MetaLINK, using Nokia equipment, have expanded CBRS for broadband in multi-state regions including Ohio and Michigan, while educational and urban campuses, exemplified by Howard University's Samsung-Amdocs private network, demonstrate sector-specific scalability.[51][52] These deployments underscore CBRS's role in enabling localized, high-reliability networks without exclusive spectrum ownership.

Deployment and Adoption Trends

Early Field Trials

Initial field trials for the Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) commenced in 2016 following the FCC's adoption of enabling rules in 2015, focusing on validating the Spectrum Access System (SAS) for dynamic spectrum allocation and protection of incumbent users such as naval radar systems. These prototypes tested core elements like Citizens Broadband Radio Service Device (CBSD) registration, interference avoidance, and tiered priority access in controlled environments. A key demonstration in November 2016 showcased SAS-mediated spectrum sharing, confirming basic operational protocols for mobile broadband in the 3.5 GHz band.[53] By 2017, trials advanced to end-to-end implementations using commercial-grade equipment. One such trial employed a live LTE-Advanced network governed by an SAS prototype, dynamically assigning channels while adhering to FCC out-of-band emission limits and aggregate interference thresholds; results indicated reliable handover and minimal disruption to protected services.[54] Another early effort achieved the first live trial with carrier aggregation across shared CBRS channels, enhancing user throughput by up to 50% compared to single-carrier baselines and verifying compliance with coexistence requirements.[55] These tests, often coordinated by the Wireless Innovation Forum, emphasized interoperability among SAS providers, CBSD vendors, and Environmental Sensing Capability (ESC) sensors.[56] Industry stakeholders, including cable operators, began application-specific pilots around this period. Charter Communications conducted extensive trials starting in 2017 in markets like Tampa, Florida, and Charlotte, North Carolina, to assess CBRS for fixed wireless access and network densification, reporting favorable propagation characteristics in urban settings.[57] At an August 2017 CBRS Alliance gathering, 13 demonstrations from 14 participants illustrated real-time SAS-CBSD interactions, including multi-vendor setups for private LTE networks.[58] Collectively, these efforts established proof-of-concept for the three-tier model but highlighted needs for refined ESC accuracy and SAS scalability, paving the way for FCC-mandated certification testing that concluded in June 2019.[59]

Growth Metrics and Sector-Specific Use Cases

The number of active Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) deployments in the United States grew by 270,621 between April 1, 2021, and July 1, 2024, reflecting sustained expansion driven by enterprise demand for private networks.[60] By December 2024, over 400,000 CBRS devices (CBSDs) had been deployed nationwide, supported by more than 1,000 CBRS operators and over 960 FCC-authorized end-user devices.[33] This momentum continued into mid-2025, with approximately 413,000 deployed radios reported in June, underscoring CBRS's role in enabling scalable, shared-spectrum solutions amid rising needs for localized connectivity.[61] CBRS facilitates sector-specific applications through private LTE and 5G networks, prioritizing low-latency, secure communications in environments where licensed spectrum costs or coverage limitations pose challenges.[62] In manufacturing and industrial settings, enterprises deploy CBRS for factory automation, IoT sensor integration, and real-time machine control, leveraging its mid-band spectrum to support high-density device connectivity without reliance on wide-area public networks.[63] Transportation and logistics sectors, including ports and airports, utilize CBRS for asset tracking, automated guided vehicles, and operational coordination, as evidenced by deployments enhancing efficiency in cargo handling and air traffic support systems.[29] Rural broadband and fixed wireless access (FWA) represent another key application, where CBRS extends coverage to underserved areas via cost-effective small-cell architectures, bridging gaps in traditional infrastructure.[64] Educational institutions and government facilities adopt CBRS for campus-wide networks enabling secure data sharing, video surveillance, and emergency response, with deployments in schools demonstrating improved connectivity for remote learning and administrative operations.[29] In healthcare and utilities, the service supports mission-critical use cases such as remote patient monitoring and smart grid management, where dynamic spectrum sharing ensures reliability without incumbent interference.[65] These implementations highlight CBRS's versatility in addressing industry-specific requirements for coverage, capacity, and prioritization over public alternatives.[66]

Achievements and Impacts

Spectrum Utilization Efficiency

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) enhances spectrum utilization in the 3.5 GHz band (3550–3700 MHz) through a three-tier access model managed by Spectrum Access Systems (SAS), which dynamically allocates 150 MHz of shared spectrum while protecting incumbents like naval radar systems in Dynamic Protection Areas (DPAs). This framework allows Priority Access Licenses (PALs) to secure up to 70 MHz with interference protection, while General Authorized Access (GAA) opportunistically uses available channels across the full band, adapting to real-time demand and reducing idle time inherent in static allocations.[2][10] Aggregate SAS data from April 2021 to July 2024 indicate substantial growth in deployment and usage, with Citizens Broadband Service Devices (CBSDs) rising 208.5% to 400,403 units nationwide, including 67.5% in rural areas. Active grants reached 1,178,625, predominantly GAA (82.3%), reflecting broad opportunistic access. By July 1, 2024, 82.7% of U.S. counties utilized at least one channel, 41% employed all 15 available channels, and mean band utilization per county increased from 5.4 to 10.2 channels, demonstrating effective sharing even in DPA-impacted areas where 49.7% of CBSDs operate.[67] Field trials and coexistence studies support efficiency gains, with optimized GAA grouping schemes yielding up to 20% higher throughput compared to uncoordinated access, and dynamic SAS allocation enabling reliable LTE/5G operation alongside incumbents. However, measurements in urban areas like Atlanta, Kansas City, and Phoenix revealed low utilization, with GAA spectrum remaining unused and only partial channel occupancy (e.g., 9 of 15 channels in some cases), highlighting potential limitations in high-demand environments relative to exclusive licensed spectrum, which has driven 3543% mobile data growth since 2012 through full-power, static use.[68][69][70] Overall, CBRS's SAS-driven model promotes higher utilization in underused scenarios, particularly rural and indoor deployments, though empirical urban data suggest it underperforms exclusive licensing in density and innovation metrics, lacking standardized measures for transmitted data or user throughput.[67][69]

Economic and Innovation Benefits

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) has delivered substantial economic value through its dynamic spectrum sharing model, which allocates 150 MHz in the 3.55–3.70 GHz band among incumbents, priority users, and general access without requiring full exclusive nationwide licenses. The Federal Communications Commission's Auction 105 in 2020 for Priority Access Licenses (PALs) generated $4.58 billion in gross proceeds, assigning licenses across partial economic areas and enabling commercial deployments. Industry analysis estimates the market value of CBRS spectrum at $7.5–$15.6 billion, factoring in adjustments for shared access, geographic coverage, and license durations benchmarked against comparable mid-band auctions like 2.5 GHz. Additionally, CBRS participants have invested approximately $4.7 billion in spectrum access and related infrastructure as of 2025. These investments have spurred broader economic activity, including annual capital expenditures in CBRS radio access network (RAN) infrastructure exceeding $1 billion by 2023, supporting fixed wireless access, capacity augmentation, and enterprise networks. The model's efficiency in spectrum utilization reduces costs for operators and end-users compared to traditional licensed spectrum, with goals explicitly including cost-effective broadband expansion in rural areas. Consumer surplus from enhanced connectivity is projected at $8–$26 billion annually, yielding a net present value of $80–$260 billion under standard discounting, driven by increased wireless broadband availability and competition. On the innovation front, CBRS has accelerated the adoption of private LTE and 5G networks by democratizing spectrum access, with over 400,000 certified base stations (CBSDs) deployed nationwide by late 2024 across more than 1,000 operators. This enables sector-specific applications, such as real-time IoT in factories, logistics hubs, airports, and schools, where enterprises gain direct control over low-latency, secure networks without carrier dependency or spectrum leasing expenses. The framework's Spectrum Access System (SAS) facilitates automated, interference-free sharing, fostering experimentation in industrial automation, drones, and edge computing, while influencing global private wireless markets projected to reach $36 billion by 2030. Studies affirm that such sharing outperforms exclusive licensing in promoting competition, rural innovation, and total welfare, avoiding relocation costs and auction inefficiencies.

Controversies and Criticisms

Interference Management Challenges

The Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) relies on a three-tiered priority system—Incumbent Access (Tier 1), Priority Access (Tier 2), and General Authorized Access (Tier 3)—managed by Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) to dynamically allocate spectrum and mitigate interference across users in the 3550–3700 MHz band.[60] The SAS employs propagation models, exclusion zones, and heartbeat signaling from Citizens Broadband Radio Service Devices (CBSDs) to enforce interference limits, particularly protecting incumbents like U.S. Navy radar systems via Environmental Sensing Capability (ESC) sensors along coastal areas.[71] However, this framework has encountered significant hurdles in maintaining reliable coexistence, especially as deployments have scaled since the band's commercialization in 2020.[72] A primary challenge arises in the GAA tier, where no interference protection is afforded between users, allowing multiple operators to contend for channels without regulatory mitigation, which exacerbates issues in dense urban or indoor environments.[31] Real-world deployments, such as a commercial network in South Bend, Indiana, have revealed persistent co-channel interference (CCI) and adjacent-channel interference (ACI) among secondary users, with measurements indicating signal-to-interference ratios dropping below usable thresholds during peak contention, undermining throughput and reliability.[73] The absence of FCC-mandated coexistence rules for GAA has left SAS administrators unable to enforce equitable access, resulting in scenarios where dominant users—often public carriers—preempt smaller private networks, as reported in private LTE deployments experiencing up to 20–30% performance degradation from overlapping GAA operations.[72][69][74] Inter-SAS coordination poses another hurdle, as multiple approved SAS providers (e.g., Google, Federated Wireless) must synchronize databases to avoid over-allocation, yet discrepancies in propagation modeling and data latency have led to unstable channel grants, with some users reporting intermittent SAS connectivity outages lasting minutes to hours.[75] Incumbent protection remains contentious, particularly in expansive Protection Areas (PAs) covering up to 200 km inland from coasts, where ESC inaccuracies or unmonitored federal transmissions have triggered unnecessary CBRS shutdowns, reducing effective spectrum availability by 15–25% in affected regions as of 2024 quarterly data.[35][60] Aggregate interference models used by SAS often overestimate or underestimate real-world effects due to unaccounted variables like multipath fading and terrain, prompting calls for refined, measurement-based approaches.[71][73] These issues have slowed adoption in high-density sectors like warehouses and campuses, where environmental factors amplify interference propagation.[76]

Regulatory Reallocation Pressures

In response to escalating demand for mid-band spectrum to support nationwide 5G coverage, telecommunications carriers have advocated for reallocating the 3.5 GHz Citizens Broadband Radio Service (CBRS) band from its shared framework to exclusive licensed use. AT&T, in filings to the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) dated October 2024, proposed relocating CBRS operations—including Priority Access Licenses (PALs) and General Authorized Access (GAA)—to the adjacent 3.1-3.3 GHz band, which would enable auctioning the full 3.55-3.7 GHz spectrum for higher-power, fully licensed mobile broadband services with improved propagation characteristics.[77] This push reflects broader carrier arguments that the shared CBRS model limits network performance compared to exclusive allocations, as dynamic spectrum access via Spectrum Access Systems (SAS) imposes operational constraints unsuitable for large-scale deployments.[78] Congressional mandates for expanded spectrum auctions have intensified these reallocation pressures, with reconciliation bills in 2025 proposing pipelines of up to 800 MHz of new auctionable spectrum to fund federal priorities, potentially targeting shared bands like CBRS to meet targets without encroaching on Department of Defense (DoD) holdings.[79] Such requirements, embedded in House-passed legislation like HR 1 in June 2025, could compel the FCC to prioritize exclusive commercial allocations over innovative sharing regimes, despite CBRS's demonstrated success in the 2020 PAL auction that generated $4.58 billion in revenue.[80] The DoD has also expressed concerns over interference risks in the band, where incumbent radar systems in the 3.55-3.65 GHz portion require CBRS devices to vacate dynamically, fueling discussions on restructuring to protect federal incumbents while accommodating commercial needs.[1] Opposition from CBRS stakeholders, including the OnGo Alliance and rural providers, highlights the substantial economic and deployment disruptions of reallocation, estimating relocation costs at $12-14 billion to replace over 413,000 base stations and reprogram millions of devices.[81] Bipartisan senators, in a September 2025 letter to the FCC, urged minimizing interference to established CBRS networks, emphasizing their role in enterprise private LTE, industrial IoT, and underserved areas where affordable shared access has enabled rapid adoption without exclusive licenses.[82] The FCC's August 2024 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on modernizing CBRS rules seeks comment on enhancements like improved SAS certification but stops short of endorsing relocation, signaling ongoing tension between preserving sharing innovations and yielding to pressures for traditional licensing models.[4]

Security and Reliability Vulnerabilities

The centralized Spectrum Access System (SAS) architecture in CBRS is susceptible to hacking and eavesdropping, potentially compromising the privacy of incumbent users and Citizens Broadband Service Devices (CBSDs) by exposing sensitive operational data.[83] Adversaries may exploit SAS-CBSD interactions to infer confidential incumbent details, such as locations via repeated queries from compromised devices analyzing transmission power limits, or operation times and frequencies through forced spectrum vacates.[83] These inference attacks pose national security risks, as leaked incumbent data could reveal military radar activities in the 3.5 GHz band.[83] WinnForum's CBRS Threat Model identifies risks from adversarial control of an SAS, enabling replay or disruption of CBSD-SAS and SAS-SAS communications, which could deny spectrum access or falsify interference protections. Compromised CBSDs might flood SAS servers with excessive queries, executing denial-of-service (DoS) attacks that overwhelm resource allocation and prevent legitimate spectrum grants.[84] Potential attacks target critical components including SAS servers, CBSDs, and Environmental Sensing Capability (ESC) sensors, with vulnerabilities in authentication and database integrity allowing collusion or Byzantine faults. Reliability vulnerabilities stem from SAS overload under high CBSD density, where short heartbeat intervals—required for FCC-mandated rapid channel vacates (e.g., within seconds of detecting incumbent radars)—increase server load and limit scalable deployments, potentially suspending transmissions unnecessarily.[85] The protocol's timing constraints trade off against capacity: intervals under 15 seconds ensure compliance but strain SAS processing for thousands of devices, while longer ones risk delayed incumbent protections.[85] In GAA tiers, simultaneous transmissions from proximate CBSDs exacerbate interference, degrading reliability in dense environments despite SAS coordination.[83] Centralized SAS intolerance to server crashes or dishonest administrators further heightens outage risks, as no decentralized failover exists in the baseline model. Observed interference in private CBRS networks from public carrier operations underscores real-world reliability gaps, prompting FCC proposals for CBRS 2.0 enhancements in 2024.[74]
