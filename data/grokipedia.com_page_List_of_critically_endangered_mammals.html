Critically endangered (CR) species face an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild. As of 2024, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) listed 235 critically endangered mammalian species, including possibly around 31 tagged as possibly extinct (update pending for 2025).[^5] Of all evaluated mammalian species, 3.9% are listed as critically endangered. The IUCN also lists approximately 60 mammalian subspecies as critically endangered (last confirmed 2020; update needed). Of the subpopulations of mammals evaluated by the IUCN, 18 species subpopulations have been assessed as critically endangered (2020 data; recent updates may vary). Additionally 773 mammalian species (13% of those evaluated) are listed as data deficient, meaning there is insufficient information for a full assessment of conservation status. As these species typically have small distributions and/or populations, they are intrinsically likely to be threatened, according to the IUCN.[^3] While the category of data deficient indicates that no assessment of extinction risk has been made for the taxa, the IUCN notes that it may be appropriate to give them "the same degree of attention as threatened taxa, at least until their status can be assessed".[^4] The October 2025 IUCN Red List update (version 2025-2) includes new assessments, such as uplisting Arctic seals to critically endangered due to climate change impacts.[^6] This is a complete list of critically endangered mammalian species and subspecies evaluated by the IUCN. Species considered possibly extinct by the IUCN are marked as such. Species and subspecies which have critically endangered subpopulations (or stocks) are indicated. Where possible common names for taxa are given while links point to the scientific name used by the IUCN.

Cetartiodactyls

Cetartiodactyla includes dolphins, whales and even-toed ungulates. As of IUCN Red List version 2025-2, there are 25 species, an undetermined number of subspecies, and an undetermined number of subpopulations of cetartiodactyls assessed as critically endangered.[4]
