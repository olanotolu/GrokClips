Barnaby Rudge


Barnaby Rudge: A Tale of the Riots of 'Eighty is a historical novel by the English author Charles Dickens, serialized weekly in Master Humphrey's Clock from February to November 1841.[1]
It marks Dickens's initial foray into the historical genre, predating A Tale of Two Cities as his only other work in that mode.[2]
The story unfolds against the backdrop of the Gordon Riots of 1780, a spasm of anti-Catholic violence in London sparked by opposition to the Papists Act 1778, which relaxed restrictions on Catholic worship and practice.[3][1]
Centering on Barnaby Rudge, a mentally impaired young man and his loquacious pet raven Grip, the novel weaves personal mysteriesâ€”including an unsolved murderâ€”with broader themes of mob psychology, religious prejudice, and social disorder.[4][5]
Dickens drew on contemporary accounts and historical records to depict the riots' chaos, which saw prisons stormed, buildings burned, and over 200 deaths, underscoring the perils of fanaticism and unchecked crowd fervor.[3][6]
Though initial reception was mixed due to its serialization format and departure from Dickens's typical contemporary settings, the work endures for its vivid portrayal of historical tumult and character-driven suspense.[4]

Publication History

Composition and Serialization

Charles Dickens commenced planning Barnaby Rudge in early 1839, selecting the Gordon Riots of 1780 as the central historical event for what would become his inaugural historical novel.[7] This choice stemmed from his observation that no prior work of fiction had depicted the riots, prompting extensive research into contemporary accounts and records to capture their chaos and societal impact.[8] The novel's development was tied to Dickens's obligations for Master Humphrey's Clock, a weekly periodical he launched on 4 April 1840, which initially featured framing narratives and promised diverse tales, including historical ones.[9] After completing The Old Curiosity Shop within the same format, Barnaby Rudge followed as the historical fulfillment, serialized in weekly installments from 13 February to 27 November 1841, comprising approximately 42 parts with short chapters suited to the periodical's structure.[9] [3] Serialization constraints shaped the narrative's pacing, necessitating frequent cliffhangers and the integration of the Maypole Inn as a connective frame echoing Clock's club-like storytelling device, while Dickens balanced historical fidelity with dramatic tension amid his rigorous archival studies.[9] The complete novel appeared in book form later in 1841, issued by Chapman & Hall.[3]

Initial Reception and Challenges

Barnaby Rudge elicited mixed responses from critics upon its serialization in Master Humphrey's Clock from February 13 to November 27, 1841. While some reviewers lauded the novel's intense portrayal of the Gordon Riots and its social insights into mob violence, others criticized its intricate plotting, excessive melodrama, and perceived lack of structural unity, viewing the historical setting as a departure from Dickens's more familiar contemporary narratives.[10] Edgar Allan Poe's analysis in Graham's Magazine (February 1842) exemplified these critiques, praising the originality of the titular character as an idiot with shrewd instincts but condemning chronological inconsistencies, such as the implausible timeline between the murder and its revelationâ€”spanning 22 years yet compressed in narrative pacingâ€”and other improbabilities that disrupted the mystery's logic. Poe argued these flaws stemmed from the serialization's demands, which prioritized episodic suspense over cohesive plotting.[11][12] Commercially, the novel failed to match the success of predecessors like The Old Curiosity Shop, with Master Humphrey's Clock circulation plummeting from initial peaks above 100,000 to approximately 30,000 copies per weekly issue by the serialization's close, due to reader disinterest in the shift to historical fiction and exhaustion with the periodical's format.[13] In America, rampant piracy compounded these issues, as unauthorized excerpts appeared in outlets like The Albion, depriving Dickens of revenue amid absent international copyright protections and fueling his broader grievances against transatlantic publishing practices.[14] Dickens himself voiced frustration with the ending's resolutions, constrained by weekly deadlines that rushed character arcs and plot closures, leading to what he later deemed an unsatisfactory denouement for key familial conflicts.[7]

Historical Context

The Gordon Riots of 1780

The Gordon Riots originated from opposition to the Papists Act of 1778, which partially relieved legal disabilities imposed on Roman Catholics in Britain, allowing them limited rights to own property and bear arms while maintaining oaths of allegiance.[15] [16] Lord George Gordon, president of the Protestant Association formed in 1779 to demand repeal of the act, organized a massive petition purporting to bear 120,000 signatures, channeling widespread anti-Catholic sentiment fueled by fears of papal influence amid Britain's military strains during the American War of Independence.[15] [16] On 2 June 1780, between 40,000 and 60,000 protesters, many bearing blue cockades as symbols of the association, marched orderly from St. George's Fields to present the petition to Parliament, but tensions escalated when the procession disrupted parliamentary proceedings and minor clashes occurred with bystanders and constables.[15] Over the following days, the disturbances devolved into widespread violence targeting Catholic chapels, homes, and businesses, with rioters systematically destroying symbols of perceived Catholic resurgence; by 6â€“7 June, the focus shifted to prisons, culminating in the storming and burning of Newgate Prison, which freed approximately 300 inmates who then joined the marauding mobs.[15] [16] Attempts were made to torch the Bank of England on 7 June, known as "Black Wednesday," but defenders repelled the attackers.[15] Escalation was driven by demagogic rhetoric from Gordon and subordinate leaders, who inflamed crowds with apocalyptic warnings of Catholic plots, compounded by opportunistic criminal elements among the lower classes exploiting the chaos for looting and arson; alcohol played a catalytic role, as evidenced by instances where publicans supplied rioters or were coerced into providing free drink, lowering inhibitions and prolonging frenzies.[15] The riots transcended initial religious grievances, revealing underlying social fractures including economic distress and resentment toward authority, as mobs attacked not only Catholic sites but also symbols of state power like the homes of pro-relief MPs, such as Lord Mansfield's residence where his valuable library was incinerated.[15][16] The violence abated by 9 June after King George III proclaimed martial law on 7 June, authorizing troops to fire on rioters without prior warning; soldiers killed an estimated 285 participants, with around 200 more wounded, while approximately 450 were arrested.[15][16] In the aftermath, special commissions tried over 160 suspects, resulting in at least 25 executions by hanging at sites of their crimes to deter future disorder, underscoring the riots' demonstration of how populist agitation could precipitate societal breakdown when unchecked by law and order.[15][16] Gordon himself was acquitted of treason in 1781 but later faced imprisonment for libel.[15] The events caused property damage valued at over Â£180,000, marking the riots as London's most destructive civil unrest to that point.[17]

Dickens's Use of History and Contemporary Parallels

Charles Dickens meticulously researched the Gordon Riots of 1780 for Barnaby Rudge, consulting contemporary historical accounts and eyewitness reports to integrate verifiable events and figures into the narrative, such as the Protestant Association's petition led by Lord George Gordon on June 2, 1780, which escalated into five days of arson, looting, and attacks on Newgate Prison.[18] This approach ensured factual accuracy in mob dynamics, including the rapid shift from organized protest to chaotic destruction driven by rumor and alcohol-fueled opportunism, as documented in period sources like parliamentary records and newspapers.[19] Barnaby Rudge, serialized from February 13 to November 27, 1841, represented Dickens's inaugural effort at a historical novel, influencing his later work like A Tale of Two Cities in blending precise chronology with fictional subplots to trace causal sequences of social breakdown.[3] Dickens contrasted historical fidelity with invented elements, such as the Maypole Inn's proprietors and Barnaby's personal arc, to underscore how inflammatory rhetoric and institutional frailty precipitate anarchy, rather than inherent grievances alone.[20] He avoided romanticizing the unrest, depicting crowds as susceptible to manipulation by figures like Gordon, whose real-life fanaticism mobilized 60,000 protesters before devolving into indiscriminate violence that claimed over 200 lives and damaged 100 buildings.[21] The novel's historical framework served as an analogy for 1830s-1840s Britain, particularly the Chartist movement's agitations, including the 1839 Newport Rising where 3,000 protesters clashed with authorities over suffrage demands.[22] Dickens portrayed riots as inevitable under demagogic influence and lax enforcement, not as justified outlets for reform, reflecting anxieties over Chartist rallies that echoed the Gordon petition's scale but risked similar irrational escalation amid economic distress post-1832 Reform Act.[21] This lens critiqued contemporary weak authority, as seen in the novel's fictional Warren's manipulation paralleling real Chartist leaders' oratory, warning that unchanneled fervor leads to self-destructive chaos rather than progress.[23]

Plot Summary

Narrative Overview

The novel commences in March 1775 at the Maypole Inn near London, where a violent storm coincides with the discovery of a murdered gardener at the nearby Warren estate of Mr. Geoffrey Haredale; the estate's steward, presumed to have fled after switching clothes with the victim, remains missing, initiating a lingering mystery.[8] Five years later, in 1780, the storyline advances to intertwine this unresolved case with escalating public unrest in London over proposed Catholic emancipation legislation, as petitions circulate and assemblies form under the leadership of Lord George Gordon.[3] Barnaby Rudge, a simple-minded youth accompanied by his loquacious pet raven Grip, sets out from his mother's home seeking evidence to exonerate his father, believed by authorities to be the escaped steward guilty of the earlier crime.[8] Parallel subplots unfold among residents of the Maypole Inn and connected families, including the young blacksmith's apprentice Joe Willet, who departs for military service after a quarrel with his father John, the innkeeper, and pursues his affection for the coquettish Dolly Varden, daughter of locksmith Gabriel Varden.[3] Concurrently, tensions mount as manipulative advisors stoke anti-Catholic fervor, drawing disparate characters into processions and petitions that devolve into widespread riots engulfing the city in arson, looting, and assaults on institutions and residences.[8] Opportunists, including a public hangman and a scheming secretary, exploit the mob's fury for personal gain, while Barnaby unwittingly participates in the disturbances, leading to his capture amid the chaos that devastates landmarks from Newgate Prison to private homes.[3] The narrative structure spans rural tranquility at the outset to urban cataclysm, punctuated by Grip's repetitive utterances providing intermittent levity, and builds toward courtroom confrontations, disclosures of concealed identities tied to the 1775 murder, and settlements of romantic pursuits amid the riots' aftermath.[8] Generational threads connect the pre-riot domestic spheresâ€”encompassing feuds between Haredale and the Chester familyâ€”with the rioters' trials and executions, resolving the central enigma through recovered evidence and witness testimonies.[3]

Characters

Major Characters and Roles

Barnaby Rudge serves as the novel's protagonist, characterized as a simple-minded young man living in poverty with his mother, Mary, and reliant on his pet raven, Grip, for companionship; his innocence leads him to be manipulated into participating in the Gordon Riots.[24][25] Grip, Barnaby's talking raven, acts as a recurring narrative device, mimicking human speech and providing ironic commentary that underscores Barnaby's detachment from societal norms.[8] Edward Chester, the idealistic son of the cunning Sir John Chester, functions as one romantic lead, seeking to overcome class and religious barriers in his pursuit of Emma Haredale.[26][24] Joe Willet, initially immature son of the Maypole Inn's proprietor John Willet, matures through his devotion to Dolly Varden and involvement in defending against the riots, representing generational transition in rural life.[26][27] Roger Rudge, Barnaby's absent father and former steward to the Haredales, embodies concealed criminality as the murderer whose unresolved guilt propels the story's personal mysteries.[25][24] Lord George Gordon, drawn from the historical leader of the Protestant Association, incites the anti-Catholic fervor that erupts into the 1780 riots, driving the novel's historical plotline as a figure of misguided zeal.[26][27] Hugh, the brutish hostler at the Maypole Inn, escalates the riots' violence as a key rioter, his primal aggression contrasting with more restrained characters.[27][24] Simon Tappertit, the ambitious apprentice locksmith to Gabriel Varden, pursues personal vendettas and social climbing by aligning with the rioters, illustrating petty scheming amid chaos.[24][26]

Themes and Motifs

Mob Psychology and the Dangers of Anarchy

In Barnaby Rudge, Charles Dickens portrays mob psychology through the process of deindividuation, where individuals surrender personal identity and accountability to the collective frenzy, enabling ordinary men to commit arson and plunder under the sway of demagogues. Characters such as Hugh exemplify this dissolution of rationality, merging into an undifferentiated mass driven by passion rather than reason, described as an infectious "moral plague" that spreads unchecked violence.[28] This causal dynamic underscores how group immersion erodes self-awareness, transforming latent grievances into destructive impulses without individual restraint.[28] Demagogues like Gashford exploit this vulnerability by disseminating rumors and prejudices, fueling escalation from orderly protest to anarchic rampage, as seen in the novel's depiction of the Protestant Association's march devolving into widespread arson targeting Catholic chapels and symbols of authority such as Newgate Prison. Dickens highlights the self-defeating nature of such unrestrained populism, mirroring the historical Gordon Riots where Lord George Gordon's anti-Catholic agitation led not to legislative reform but to the execution of 26 convicted rioters and the suppression of the movement by military force.[28] [29] The riots' net effectâ€”estimated at 200 to 850 deaths, predominantly among participantsâ€”demonstrates how mob actions harm the vulnerable within their own communities, including the impoverished, rather than advancing any coherent cause.[30] Dickens contrasts this chaos with the restorative power of ordered society, as troops quell the anarchy, reimposing structure and revealing the mob's incapacity for sustained agency or progress. The novel thereby critiques the illusion of crowds as instruments of justice, showing instead their tendency toward pathological contagion where leaders ignite but cannot control the ensuing inferno, ultimately inviting decisive countermeasures that punish the perpetrators without yielding the sought-after changes.[28] This portrayal aligns with empirical observations of riot pathologies, where rumor and deindividuation amplify destruction far beyond initial intents, benefiting neither the instigators nor the disenfranchised they claim to represent.[31]

Haunting of the Past and Familial Bonds

In Barnaby Rudge, the unresolved murder of Reuben Haredale in March 1775 forms the novel's foundational enigma, intertwining paternal legacies with enduring secrecy and displacement. The crime, committed at Haredale's estate near Chigwell, leaves his brother Geoffrey as guardian to niece Emma, while the suspected perpetratorâ€”family steward Barnaby Rudge Sr.â€”vanishes alongside a gardener, presumed dead after their bloodied clothes are found. This event enforces a causal chain: Rudge Sr.'s survival in hiding compels his wife, Mary, to sustain a life of evasion, periodically paying hush money to Geoffrey Haredale to conceal her husband's location and preserve meager support for herself and their son, the intellectually impaired Barnaby Jr.[32][33] The motif underscores how a single paternal transgression ripples forward, distorting familial structures and individual fates without resolution until external forces intervene. The narrative posits past sins as inexorable determinants, with inheritance manifesting not merely genetically but through enforced patterns of concealment and psychological burden. Barnaby Jr.'s simplicity, marked by a wrist birthmark evoking blood guilt, echoes his father's deed, as Mary Rudge's suppressed knowledge fosters the son's unwitting complicity in their nomadic existence. Attempts at ruptureâ€”such as Rudge Sr.'s adoption of disguises and false identitiesâ€”ultimately collapse under the weight of discovery during the 1780 riots, affirming that evasion merely postpones causal retribution rather than nullifying it.[34][35] This continuity critiques superficial breaks from history, portraying secrecy as a binding force that perpetuates displacement across generations, evident in the Haredales' isolated vigilance and the Rudges' shadowed survival. Parallel familial dynamics at the Maypole Inn highlight intergenerational stasis, where parental dominion inhibits youthful autonomy until disrupted by broader upheaval. Innkeeper John Willet, embodying ossified tradition, infantilizes his capable son Joeâ€”now in his twentiesâ€”by dictating chores, withholding trust, and enforcing deference, as seen in John's refusal to acknowledge Joe's maturity or romantic aspirations toward Dolly Varden. This tension escalates to Joe's enlistment in the militia, fracturing the household and symbolizing how entrenched paternal authority, akin to the murder's lingering pall, constrains progress absent cataclysmic intervention.[3][36] The riots' chaos, burning the Maypole and wounding Joe, paradoxically liberates him, enabling reconciliation and agency, thus illustrating how historical burdens yield only to forceful rupture rather than internal reform.[37]

Religious Intolerance and Social Division

In Barnaby Rudge, Charles Dickens portrays the Protestant-Catholic tensions culminating in the Gordon Riots of June 1780 as a volatile fusion of religious fanaticism and underlying social discontent, where anti-Catholic prejudice serves less as a principled stand than as a demagogic spark igniting broader chaos. Lord George Gordon, the historical figure leading the Protestant Association's petition against the Papists Act 1778â€”which had eased some restrictions on Catholicsâ€”is depicted as an eccentric zealot whose fervent opposition to perceived papal encroachment rallies a disparate crowd, amplifying pre-existing economic hardships and class animosities into widespread unrest.[36][38] This fanaticism, while rooted in genuine Protestant fears of Catholic influence post-American Revolution, is shown to distort rational grievances into irrational fervor, as Gordon's procession of 60,000 petitioners on June 2, 1780, devolves from orderly protest into mob violence targeting Catholic properties and symbols.[39] Dickens extends this portrayal through fictional elements, illustrating how religious bigotry facilitates elite manipulation and self-perpetuating destruction, as evidenced by the scheming secretary Gashford, who exploits Gordon's piety and the mob's prejudices for personal advancement and revenge. Gashford, a cynical opportunist, inflames anti-Catholic sentiment not out of conviction but to orchestrate riots that engulf London, burning chapels, homes, and even Newgate Prison on June 6-7, 1780, while the violence spills over to harm Protestant establishments and innocents alike, underscoring the indiscriminate nature of such fervor.[5][40] The novel thus reveals intolerance as a causal multiplier when wedded to demagoguery: Gordon's naive idealism blinds him to Gashford's machinations, transforming targeted religious protest into anarchic pillage that claims over 200 lives and inflicts Â£100,000 in damages, disproportionately burdening the working-class rioters' own communities.[39][41] This depiction critiques how Protestant-Catholic divides, historically entrenched since the Reformation and reignited by 18th-century relief acts, fracture society not through equitable justice but via contagious hysteria, where the mob's "No Popery" cries mask deeper resentments over poverty and inequality, ultimately eroding the social order they claim to defend. Dickens, drawing from eyewitness accounts and parliamentary reports, avoids exonerating the prejudice as mere fervor, instead emphasizing its role in enabling leaders to harness volatile crowds for ends far removed from doctrinal purity, as the riots' aftermathâ€”martial law, hangings of 25 participants by September 1780â€”exposes the prejudice's pyrrhic toll on all factions.[42][40]

Critical Reception

Contemporary Critiques

Edgar Allan Poe, in his February 1842 review published in Graham's Magazine, commended the novel's vivid portrayal of the Gordon Riots, stating that the scenes of mob violence and anarchy were "never surpassed" in their intensity and realism.[11] He also praised the eccentricity of certain characters, particularly Grip the raven, whom he called "intensely amusing" though underutilized as a symbolic element tied to Barnaby's psyche.[11] These atmospheric strengths were echoed in other periodicals, which noted the riots' chaotic energy as a highlight amid the domestic intrigue. Critics, however, frequently faulted the plot's excessive density and reliance on contrived elements. Poe highlighted numerous improbabilities and coincidences, such as the contrived recurrence of March 19th across unrelated events and the improbable persistence of a wrist stain as evidence, arguing they strained credibility without enhancing thematic depth.[11] He further criticized the narrative's chronological inconsistencies, including a timeline for the central murder and its aftermath that fluctuated between 22 and 24 years, rendering the five-year gap between the story's halves feel forced and disruptive.[11] The over-dependence on supernatural hints, like ghostly apparitions and prophetic dreams, was seen as misleading readers toward a resolution that prioritized mechanical revelation over organic suspense.[11] Barnaby Rudge marked Dickens's initial venture into historical fiction, blending personal mysteries with real events from 1780, but reviewers deemed it ambitious yet uneven in execution, with the riots overshadowing the core plot. Serialized in weekly installments within Master Humphrey's Clock from February 13 to November 27, 1841, the novel coincided with a sharp decline in the periodical's circulation, dropping from peaks during the prior Old Curiosity Shop to unsustainable lows that ended the publication after 88 issues.[43] This format constraint, tying the story to an overarching frame narrative, amplified perceptions of structural flaws, though a subsequent standalone book edition in 1849 allowed broader access without immediate commercial rebound.[6]

Modern Evaluations and Political Interpretations

In the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, Barnaby Rudge has often been relegated to a minor position within Dickens's oeuvre, overshadowed by his more tightly plotted works and critiqued for its sprawling structure, melodramatic elements, and underdeveloped secondary characters, particularly the female figures like Dolly Varden and Emma Haredale, who serve primarily as romantic foils rather than fully realized agents.[44][45] Scholars have noted its absence from major companions to Dickens's work, attributing this to early dismissals, such as Edgar Allan Poe's complaint about the novel's weak mystery resolution, though recent defenses highlight its psychological acuity in depicting disguise and identity amid chaos, as well as the raw energy of its riot sequences.[44] Post-2020 analyses have elevated the novel's prescience, drawing parallels between the Gordon Riots' descent into anarchyâ€”where mobs supplanted state authority, torching institutions like Newgate Prison and the Bank of England before military suppression claimed hundreds of lives on June 7, 1780â€”and contemporary disorders, including the January 6, 2021, U.S. Capitol incursion fueled by conspiracy and fervor.[46] These readings acclaim Dickens's portrayal of mob psychology as a caution against romanticizing "people power," emphasizing the riots' futility: participants achieved no lasting reform, only widespread ruin and personal devastation, countering narratives that glorify unrest as cathartic or progressive.[44][46] Conservative interpreters view the novel as an endorsement of hierarchical order and civilizing restraints against innate human aggression, with riots embodying an "epidemic" of unchecked instinct that law, custom, and familial bonds must suppress to avert societal collapse.[47] While some progressive readings infer Dickens's sympathy for the urban underclass's grievancesâ€”rooted in his broader reformist leaningsâ€”the text undercuts this by depicting rioters' actions as self-defeating savagery driven by fanaticism, not rational redress, resulting in net harm to the very communities they claimed to represent.[48][46] This portrayal aligns with empirical outcomes of the historical events, where anti-Catholic violence yielded no policy gains and exacerbated divisions, underscoring causal realism over idealistic upheaval.[46]

Adaptations and Influence

Stage, Film, and Radio Versions

The first stage adaptation of Barnaby Rudge appeared soon after the novel's serialization concluded in 1841, with Charles Selby and Charles Melville's Barnaby Rudge: A Domestic Drama, In Three Acts condensing the narrative into a theatrical format that incorporated sensational depictions of the Gordon Riots through stage effects and crowd scenes.[49] These early 19th-century productions, performed at London theatres amid the era's rapid dramatization of Dickens's works, prioritized visual spectacle in portraying mob violence and historical unrest to captivate audiences familiar with the source material's episodic structure.[50] A modern reinterpretation, The Locksmith of London by Eileen Norris, was staged in 2012, reworking the story's locksmith character and riot elements into a contemporary play format while retaining core plot fidelity. The 1960 BBC television series, a 13-episode adaptation broadcast from 30 September to 23 December, remains the most extensive visual dramatization, faithfully interweaving the novel's murder mystery with the 1780 Gordon Riots and featuring period costumes and sets to evoke 18th-century London.[51] [52] Starring Richard O'Sullivan as the simple-minded Barnaby accompanied by his pet raven Grip, the production aired weekly and survived the BBC's early tape purges, allowing later DVD releases. No major cinematic films of Barnaby Rudge have been produced, with post-1960 efforts limited by the logistical challenges of depicting large-scale historical chaos on screen. Radio adaptations have included BBC productions emphasizing sound design for riot sequences, such as the 2014 Radio 4 Classic Serial airing over multiple episodes, which cast actor Daniel Laurie, who has Down's syndrome, as Barnaby to authentically convey the character's intellectual limitations.[53] [54] Earlier BBC radio dramatisations, compiled in collections like the 2016 BBC Radio Drama Collection: Volume Two, utilized voice acting and audio effects to navigate the novel's intricate subplots and mob psychology, though adaptations remain scarcer than for Dickens's more linear narratives due to the source's temporal shifts and ensemble demands.[55]

Allusions and Cultural References

The raven Grip, Barnaby's prophetic companion in the novel, exerted a notable influence on Edgar Allan Poe's 1845 poem "The Raven." Poe, who reviewed Barnaby Rudge favorably in 1841, singled out Grip for particular praise as "intensely amusing" and more effective than the human characters in conveying foreboding, with literary scholars concurring that the bird's ominous utterances and role as a harbinger of doom directly informed Poe's spectral raven.[56][57] G. K. Chesterton referenced the Gordon Riots in Barnaby Rudge within his broader critiques of Dickens, portraying the depicted uprising as a misguided revolt in favor of "ignorant and obscurantist" anti-Catholic sentiments rather than progressive ideals, thereby using the novel to illustrate the perils of fanaticism-driven anarchy in historical revolutions.[58] In 21st-century analyses of populism and unrest, commentators have alluded to the novel's riots as a cautionary archetype, explicitly linking the manipulated mobs under Lord George Gordon to events like the January 6, 2021, U.S. Capitol incursion, where charismatic leaders exploited grievances to incite widespread disorder.[46]
