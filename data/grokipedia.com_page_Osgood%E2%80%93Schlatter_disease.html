Osgood–Schlatter disease

Osgood–Schlatter disease (OSD) is inflammation of the patellar ligament at the tibial tuberosity (apophysitis) usually affecting adolescents during growth spurts. It is characterized by a painful bump just below the knee that is worse with activity and better with rest. Episodes of pain typically last a few weeks to months. One or both knees may be affected and flares may recur. Risk factors include overuse, especially sports which involve frequent running or jumping. The underlying mechanism is repeated tension on the growth plate of the upper tibia. Diagnosis is typically based on the symptoms. A plain X-ray may be either normal or show fragmentation in the attachment area. Pain typically resolves with time. Applying cold to the affected area, rest, stretching, and strengthening exercises may help. NSAIDs such as ibuprofen may be used. Slightly less stressful activities such as swimming or walking may be recommended. Casting the leg for a period of time may help. After growth slows, typically age 16 in boys and 14 in girls, the pain will no longer occur despite a bump potentially remaining. It affects approximately 10% of adolescents aged 12-15. Males between the ages of 10 and 15 are most often affected. The condition is named after Robert Bayley Osgood (1873–1956), an American orthopedic surgeon, and Carl B. Schlatter (1864–1934), a Swiss surgeon, who described the condition independently in 1903.

Signs and symptoms

Osgood–Schlatter disease causes pain in the front lower part of the knee. This is usually at the ligament-bone junction of the patellar ligament and the tibial tuberosity. The tibial tuberosity is a slight elevation of bone on the anterior and proximal portion of the tibia. The patellar tendon attaches the anterior quadriceps muscles to the tibia via the knee cap. Common physical signs include swelling, tenderness to touch, and a prominent bump at the tibial tuberosity. Intense knee pain is usually the presenting symptom that occurs during activities such as running, jumping, lifting things, squatting, and especially ascending or descending stairs and during kneeling. The pain is worse with acute knee impact. The pain can be reproduced by extending the knee against resistance, stressing the quadriceps, or striking the knee. Pain is initially mild and intermittent. In the acute phase, the pain is severe and continuous in nature. Impact of the affected area can be very painful. Bilateral symptoms are observed in 20–30% of people.

Risk Factors

Risk factors include overuse, especially sports which involve running or jumping. The underlying mechanism is repeated tension on the growth plate of the upper tibia.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made based on signs and symptoms. Imaging is not always required but can support the diagnosis. Plain X-ray of the knee is the most common imaging modality and may show fragmentation of the tibial tubercle or be normal.

Ultrasonography

Ultrasonography can detect early diagnostic signs of OSD, such as tissue and cartilage swelling, fragmentation of the tibial apophysis, and increased vascularity around the tibial tuberosity.[1] Its non-invasive nature makes it useful for identifying OSD in early stages. It can also visualize new bone formation around the tibial tuberosity.

Types

OSD may rarely result in an avulsion fracture of the tibial tubercle, where the tuberosity separates from the tibia (usually remaining connected to the patellar tendon). This injury is uncommon due to protective mechanisms limiting muscle pull damage. The fracture can be complete or incomplete and is classified using the Watson-Jones system.[2] Type I: A small fragment is displaced proximally and does not require surgery. Type II: The articular surface of the tibia remains intact and the fracture occurs at the junction where the secondary center of ossification and the proximal tibial epiphysis meet (may or may not require surgery). Type III: Complete fracture through the articular surface, with a high chance of meniscal damage. This type usually requires surgery.

Differential diagnosis

Sinding-Larsen and Johansson syndrome is an analogous condition involving the patellar tendon and the lower margin of the patella bone, instead of the upper margin of the tibia. Sever's disease is an analogous condition affecting the Achilles tendon attachment to the heel.

Prevention

One of the main ways to prevent OSD is to check the participant's flexibility, particularly in the quadriceps. Shortening of the quadriceps muscles, such as the rectus femoris, can be a risk indicator for OSD. Muscles can shorten, which can cause pain but this is not permanent. Stretches can help reduce shortening of the muscles. The main stretches for prevention of OSD focus on the quadriceps. Tight hamstrings and quadriceps are often cited as risk factors in clinical guidelines.[3][4] Direct stretching of the quadriceps can be painful, so techniques to gently restore flexibility, such as self-myofascial release, may be beneficial. Straight leg raises help strengthen the quadriceps without the need to bend the knee. The knee should be kept straight, legs should be lifted and lowered slowly, and reps should be held for three to five seconds.[5]

Treatment

Treatment is generally conservative with rest, ice, and specific exercises being recommended. Simple pain medication may be used such as acetaminophen (paracetamol), or NSAIDs such as ibuprofen. Saline injections have also been proposed for pain reduction. Typically symptoms resolve as the growth plate closes. Physiotherapy is generally recommended once the initial symptoms have improved to prevent recurrence. Surgery may rarely be used in those who have stopped growing yet still have symptoms.

Physiotherapy

Recommended efforts include exercises to improve the strength of the gluteals, quadriceps, hamstring and gastrocnemius muscles. Bracing or use of an orthopedic cast to enforce joint immobilization is rarely required and does not necessarily encourage a quicker resolution. However, bracing may give comfort and help reduce pain as it reduces strain on the tibial tubercle.

Surgery

Surgical excision may rarely be required in people who have stopped growing. Surgical removal of the ossicles generally results in good outcomes, with symptoms improvement after several weeks.

Rehabilitation

Rehabilitation focuses on muscle strengthening, gait training, and pain control to restore knee function. Nonsurgical treatments for less severe symptoms include: exercises for strength, stretches to increase range of motion, ice packs, knee tape, knee braces, anti-inflammatory agents, and electrical stimulation to control inflammation and pain. Quadriceps and hamstring exercises are commonly prescribed by rehabilitation experts to restore flexibility and muscle strength. Isometric exercises, such as isometric leg extensions, have been shown to strengthen the knee and reduce pain and inhibition. They help the tissue repair through mechanotransduction. Other exercises can include leg raises, squats, and wall stretches to increase quadriceps and hamstring strength. This helps to avoid pain, stress, and tight muscles that lead to further injury that oppose healing. Education and knowledge on stretches and exercises are important. Exercises should lack pain and increase gradually with intensity. The patient is given strict guidelines on how to perform exercises at home to avoid more injury. Osgood-Schlatter Disease Prognosis

Prognosis

The symptoms usually resolve with treatment but may recur for 12–24 months before complete resolution at skeletal maturity, when the tibial epiphysis fuses. In some cases the symptoms do not resolve until the patient is fully grown. In approximately 10% of patients the symptoms continue unabated into adulthood, despite all conservative measures. However, more recent studies suggest higher rates of residual symptoms: a 2019 follow-up study found that 60.5% of patients reported OSD-related knee pain after a median of 3.75 years.[6] A 2025 Danish national cohort study indicated that adults with a history of adolescent OSD have significantly worse long-term knee health than the general population.[7]

Long-term implications

OSD occurs from the combined effects of tibial tuberosity immaturity and quadriceps tightness. There is a possibility of migration of the ossicle or fragmentation in Osgood-Schlatter patients. The implications of OSD and the ossification of the tubercle can lead to functional limitations and pain for patients into adulthood. In a 1996 case series of 261 patients followed for 12 to 24 months, 237 responded well to sport restriction and non-steroid anti-inflammatory agents, resulting in recovery to normal athletic activity.

Epidemiology

Osgood–Schlatter disease generally occurs in boys and girls aged 9–16 coinciding with periods of growth spurts. It occurs more frequently in boys than in girls, with reports of a male-to-female ratio ranging from 3:1 to as high as 7:1. The difference may be related to greater participation by boys in sports and risk activities than by girls.[8] Osgood–Schlatter disease resolves or becomes asymptomatic in the majority of cases. Because of the typically short symptomatic period, the number of diagnosed cases is a fraction of the true prevalence.[9] Overall, about 4–5% of adolescents are affected at some point. The condition is more common in athletic populations, with prevalence up to 21%. For adolescents between the ages of 12–15, there is a disease prevalence of 9.8% with 11.0% in males and 8.3% in females. Osgood-Schlatter disease presents bilaterally in about 20–30% of patients.[9] Regular sports practice and shortening of the rectus femoris muscle are associated with increased incidence in pubertal adolescents. Approximately 74% of patients with Osgood-Schlatter disease have shortened rectus femoris. This association highlights the anatomical relationship between the tibial tuberosity and the quadriceps muscle group, connected through the patella and its ligamentous structures. Pain on palpation over the tibial tuberosity is a common finding, reported as a key diagnostic criterion by 97% of healthcare professionals surveyed. This contributes to physical examination being the primary diagnostic method. Research indicates an association between Osgood–Schlatter disease and tibial tubercle fractures, with OSD present in about 30% of such fracture cases. Rapid bone development at the tuberosity may contribute to this risk. Because increased activity is a risk factor for developing Osgood–Schlatter disease, children and adolescents with ADHD may be at higher risk. However, studies remain inconclusive regarding the specific aspects of ADHD contributing to higher incidence.
