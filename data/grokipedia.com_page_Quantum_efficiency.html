Quantum efficiency

The term quantum efficiency (QE) may apply to incident photon to converted electron (IPCE) ratio[1] of a photosensitive device. This article deals with the term as a measurement of a device's electrical sensitivity to light. In a charge-coupled device (CCD) or other photodetector, it is the ratio between the number of charge carriers collected at either terminal and the number of photons hitting the device's photoreactive surface. As a ratio, QE is dimensionless, but it is closely related to the responsivity, which is expressed in amps per watt. Since the energy of a photon is inversely proportional to its wavelength, QE is often measured over a range of different wavelengths to characterize a device's efficiency at each photon energy level. For typical semiconductor photodetectors, QE drops to zero for photons whose energy is below the band gap. A photographic film typically has a QE of much less than 10%,[2] while CCDs can have a QE of well over 90% at some wavelengths.

QE of solar cells

A solar cell's quantum efficiency value indicates the amount of current that the cell will produce when irradiated by photons of a particular wavelength. If the cell's quantum efficiency is integrated over the solar electromagnetic spectrum up to the bandgap wavelength, one can evaluate the short-circuit current density that the cell will produce when exposed to sunlight. The overall power conversion efficiency is then given by Î· = (J_sc Ã— V_oc Ã— FF) / P_in, where V_oc is the open-circuit voltage, FF is the fill factor, and P_in is the incident power density. In the case of multiple exciton generation (MEG), internal quantum efficiencies greater than 100% may be achieved for photons with energy more than twice the bandgap, creating multiple electron-hole pairs per photon, though external quantum efficiency remains at or below 100%.

Types

Two types of quantum efficiency of a solar cell are often considered:
  • External quantum efficiency (EQE) is the ratio of the number of charge carriers collected by the solar cell to the number of photons of a given energy shining on the solar cell from outside (incident photons).
  • Internal quantum efficiency (IQE) is the ratio of the number of charge carriers collected by the solar cell to the number of photons of a given energy that shine on the solar cell from outside and are absorbed by the cell.
The IQE is always larger than or equal to the EQE. A low IQE indicates that the active layer of the solar cell is unable to make good use of the photons, most likely due to poor carrier collection efficiency. To measure the IQE, one first measures the EQE of the solar device, then measures its transmission and reflection, and combines these data to infer the IQE. Absorbed UV light can generate heat if carriers recombine, potentially affecting long-term cell stability.[3]

QE of image sensors

Quantum efficiency (QE) is the fraction of photon flux that contributes to the photocurrent in a photodetector or a pixel. Quantum efficiency is one of the most important parameters used to evaluate the quality of a detector and is often called the spectral response to reflect its wavelength dependence. It is defined as the number of signal electrons created per incident photon. In some cases it can exceed 100% (i.e. when more than one electron is created per incident photon). Typical peak QE for modern back-illuminated CMOS and CCD image sensors exceeds 90% in the visible spectrum, with some reaching over 95% as of 2025.[5]

EQE mapping

Conventional measurement of the EQE will give the efficiency of the overall device. However it is often useful to have a map of the EQE over the sensor array. This mapping provides an efficient way to visualize the homogeneity and/or the defects in the sample. For image sensors, such as CMOS arrays, EQE mapping can be achieved using monochromatic illumination scanning or hyperspectral imaging techniques to assess pixel-level variations.[6]

Spectral responsivity

Responsivity is ordinarily specified for monochromatic light (i.e. light of a single wavelength).
