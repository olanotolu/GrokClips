Hagrama Mohilary

Hagrama Mohilary (born 1 March 1969) is an Indian politician and the president of the Bodoland People's Front (BPF), serving as Chief Executive Member of the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) in Assam's Bodoland Territorial Region.[1] Born in Kokrajhar district to the Bodo ethnic community amid regional ethnic tensions, he rose as a leader of the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT), a militant outfit that engaged in armed struggle for Bodo autonomy before surrendering arms in 2003 under the Bodo Accord, which established the BTC.[2] Following the accord, Mohilary founded the BPF and was elected as the inaugural CEM of the BTC in 2005, holding the position through multiple terms until 2020, during which he oversaw regional development initiatives.[3] In September 2025, the BPF secured a landslide victory in BTC elections, prompting Mohilary's return as CEM on 5 October 2025, despite past rifts with state ruling coalitions.[4] His leadership has been marked by efforts to consolidate Bodo political influence but also by controversies, including allegations of alliance mismanagement and recent scrutiny over a high-value vehicle purchase funded by public resources.[5][6]

Personal background

Early life and family

Hagrama Mohilary was born on 1 March 1969 into a Bodo family in Kokrajhar district, within the Bodoland region of Assam, India.[1] [3] His early years unfolded in an area marked by persistent ethnic conflicts and Bodo demands for greater autonomy and land rights, shaping the socio-political environment of his upbringing.[2] Mohilary married Sewli Mohilary on 22 February 2008; the couple has two sons, who are twins.[3] No public details are available regarding his parents or siblings.[1]

Education and formative influences

Mohilary was born on 1 March 1969 into a Bodo family in Kokrajhar district, Assam, a region central to the Bodoland territorial demands.[3] His early years unfolded amid escalating ethnic tensions between Bodos and neighboring communities, including disputes over land allocation and political representation in Assam.[2] Public records provide scant details on Mohilary's formal education, with no verified accounts of specific schools, colleges, or qualifications attained.[3] This paucity of information reflects the broader context of limited access to structured schooling in rural Bodo areas during the 1970s and 1980s, compounded by recurrent communal violence that disrupted institutional stability. Mohilary's formative influences stemmed primarily from the burgeoning Bodo ethno-nationalist movement, which emphasized preservation of Bodo language, culture, and economic rights against perceived Assamese dominance. The All Bodo Students' Union (ABSU), established in 1968, mobilized youth around demands for a separate state to address systemic marginalization, including inadequate representation and resource inequities.[7] Exposure to these grassroots campaigns and the resultant insurgencies during his adolescence instilled a strong identification with Bodo autonomy aspirations, prioritizing communal solidarity over individual academic pursuits.[2]

Militant involvement

Entry into Bodo Liberation Tigers

Hagrama Mohilary, born in 1969 in Kokrajhar amid ethnic tensions in Assam's Bodo-inhabited regions, entered militancy at age 27 by co-founding the Bodo Liberation Tigers Force (BLTF), also referred to as the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT), on June 18, 1996, alongside Prem Singh Brahma.[2][8] He assumed the role of chairman-cum-commander-in-chief of the outfit, which positioned itself to advance Bodo demands for territorial autonomy through armed struggle.[2][8] The BLTF emerged in the context of longstanding Bodo grievances over land rights, cultural preservation, and political marginalization within Assam, following the perceived inadequacies of earlier accords like the 1993 Bodo Accord that had promised but failed to deliver substantial autonomy.[2] The group's objectives included establishing a separate Bodoland state on the north bank of the Brahmaputra River and autonomous district councils on the south bank, along with incorporating Bodo areas in Karbi Anglong under the Sixth Schedule of the Indian Constitution.[8] Operating primarily in districts such as Kokrajhar, Dhubri, Bongaigaon, Barpeta, Nalbari, and Darrang, the BLTF quickly built a cadre strength that would later number over 2,600 by the time of its surrender.[8] Under Mohilary's leadership from its inception, the BLTF conducted early insurgent operations, including attacks that resulted in civilian casualties, such as the killing of 13 individuals in Darrang district on October 11, 1998, as part of its campaign to assert Bodo territorial claims and counter perceived threats from non-Bodo communities.[8] This marked Mohilary's transition from civilian life to commanding a militant force dedicated to ethnic separatism through violence.[2]

Leadership role and violent campaigns

Hagrama Mohilary, also known as Hagrama Basumatary, co-founded the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT) on June 18, 1996, alongside Prem Singh Brahma, and assumed the role of chairman-cum-commander-in-chief, directing its insurgent operations aimed at establishing a separate Bodoland state.[8][2] Under his leadership, the BLT operated primarily in districts such as Kokrajhar, Dhubri, Bongaigaon, Barpeta, Nalbari, and Darrang, engaging in armed campaigns that included targeted killings, abductions, extortion, and clashes with rival insurgent groups like the National Democratic Front of Bodoland (NDFB) as well as Indian security forces.[8] These activities were framed by the group as necessary to assert Bodo territorial claims against perceived encroachment by non-indigenous populations and to pressure the government for autonomy, though they resulted in civilian casualties and were classified as terrorist actions by Indian authorities.[8] The BLT's violent campaigns intensified in the late 1990s, with Mohilary overseeing operations that prioritized ethnic cleansing of non-Bodo communities to secure demographic dominance in proposed Bodoland areas. On August 15, 1998, BLT militants killed seven people in unspecified attacks, while on October 11, 1998, they massacred 13 Assamese and Bengali-speaking individuals in Darrang district to intimidate settlers.[8] Inter-group rivalries also fueled violence; for instance, on December 12, 2000, a clash with NDFB in Barpeta district left 11 BLT cadres dead, highlighting the factional warfare Mohilary navigated to maintain BLT's position as the dominant pro-India Bodo outfit.[8] Further, on December 21, 2000, BLT forces killed 16 civilians, including 10 Bhutanese traders, across Barpeta, Darrang, and Nalbari districts, underscoring the group's economic disruption tactics amid broader insurgency goals.[8] Mohilary's command extended to coercive practices like abductions and resource control, exemplified by the August 3, 2002, kidnapping of 17 villagers and directives for timber smuggling issued on June 17, 2003, to fund operations.[8] These campaigns, conducted until a unilateral ceasefire declaration on July 14, 1999â€”formalized in March 2000 and extended periodicallyâ€”reflected a strategy of combining militancy with negotiation, distinguishing BLT from anti-talks factions, though violence persisted intermittently until the group's full disbandment.[8] By 2003, under Mohilary's leadership, the BLT had amassed around 2,641 cadres, who surrendered arms on December 6, 2003, in Kokrajhar, paving the way for the Bodoland Territorial Council.[8]

Surrender and the 2003 Bodo Accord

In February 2003, Hagrama Mohilary, as chairman of the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT), engaged in negotiations with the Government of India and the Government of Assam, culminating in the signing of the Memorandum of Settlement on February 10, 2003.[8] This accord established the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC), an autonomous administrative body governing four districtsâ€”Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa, and Udalguriâ€”in western Assam, granting legislative, executive, administrative, and financial powers over specified subjects including agriculture, education, and forestry, while falling short of the BLT's original demand for a separate Bodoland state.[8] [9] The agreement required the BLT to abjure violence, surrender arms, and reintegrate into mainstream society, with provisions for rehabilitation packages including financial assistance, job quotas, and vocational training for surrendered cadres.[8] Mohilary and BLT leaders accepted these terms after internal deliberations, viewing the BTC as a pragmatic step toward Bodo self-governance amid ongoing counter-insurgency pressures and factional rivalries with groups like the National Democratic Front of Bodoland (NDFB).[10] [11] On December 6, 2003, Mohilary led the formal surrender of 2,641 BLT cadres at a public ceremony in Kokrajhar, where they deposited arms and ammunition before government officials, marking the end of the group's armed campaign that had involved ethnic clashes and demands for autonomy since the late 1990s.[8] [12] This event followed the accord's implementation timeline and paved the way for the BTC's inauguration the next day, December 7, 2003, with Mohilary sworn in as its first Chief Executive Member.[12] The surrender reduced immediate violence in the region but drew criticism from hardliners who saw it as a compromise, though it facilitated relative stability and development initiatives under the new council.[13]

Entry into mainstream politics

Formation of Bodoland People's Front

Following the signing of the Memorandum of Settlement on 10 February 2003, which established the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) as an autonomous body under the Sixth Schedule of the Indian Constitution, Hagrama Mohilary, who had led the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT) as its commander-in-chief during its armed campaign for a separate Bodoland, oversaw the surrender of over 2,641 militants and transitioned former cadres into mainstream politics.[14][15] The BLT's decision to lay down arms under the accord created a political vacuum for representing Bodo aspirations, prompting Mohilary to form a dedicated regional party to contest the inaugural BTC elections scheduled for 2005. The Bodoland People's Front (BPF) was formally launched in 2005 as a political outfit rooted in the ex-BLT framework, with Mohilary elected as its inaugural president and retired Indian Army Lt. Gen. Emmanuel Mosahary appointed general secretary.[14] This formation marked a strategic shift from militancy to electoral participation, leveraging the accord's provisions for rehabilitation and political inclusion of surrendered militants, who were allocated 30 reserved seats in the BTC's 40-member house.[15] The party's platform emphasized development, cultural preservation, and autonomy within the BTC, drawing initial support from Bodo communities disillusioned by prior ethnic conflicts and unfulfilled demands for statehood. BPF's establishment filled a gap left by earlier Bodo organizations like the All Bodo Students' Union (ABSU), which had focused on agitation rather than post-accord governance, positioning the party as the primary vehicle for BLT loyalists to secure influence in the BTC's administrative and legislative functions.[15] By mid-2005, the party had mobilized cadres across the four BTC districtsâ€”Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa, and Udalguriâ€”preparing for the polls that would determine control over an annual development budget exceeding â‚¹1,500 crore allocated by the central and state governments.[14]

Early electoral victories and Assam Assembly role

The Bodoland People's Front (BPF), established by Hagrama Mohilary in October 2005 as a political extension of the surrendered militants from the Bodo Liberation Tigers, achieved its debut electoral breakthrough in the April 2006 Assam Legislative Assembly elections. Contesting 29 seats, the party secured 12 victories, all concentrated in constituencies within the Bodoland Territorial Area Districts (BTAD), capturing a vote share of approximately 6.14% statewide and demonstrating strong consolidation of Bodo support in core areas.[16] This outcome positioned BPF as an indispensable regional player, outpacing established parties like the Asom Gana Parishad in Bodo-dominated segments.[17] The election results yielded a hung assembly, with the Indian National Congress winning 53 seats but requiring allies to reach the majority mark of 64 in the 126-member house. BPF's 12 seats proved pivotal, leading to a post-poll alliance with Congress that enabled Tarun Gogoi's second term as chief minister, marking the start of an eight-year coalition partnership.[18][19] Mohilary, as BPF president, emerged as a key architect of this arrangement, leveraging the party's leverage to advocate for enhanced autonomy and development funds for the BTAD within the state government framework. As a BPF legislator during the 13th Assam Assembly (2006â€“2011), Mohilary focused on amplifying Bodo-specific demands, including infrastructure allocation and security measures against insurgent remnants, while navigating coalition tensions over resource distribution. BPF allies, such as Chandan Brahma who held ministerial portfolios like transport, further embedded the party in governance, though Mohilary prioritized his concurrent role as inaugural Chief Executive Member of the Bodoland Territorial Council. This dual position underscored BPF's strategy of dual-track influenceâ€”territorial autonomy via the council and state-level bargaining via the assemblyâ€”to advance post-accord stabilization.[20] The alliance sustained BPF's assembly dominance into the 2011 elections, where it retained all 12 seats amid similar Bodo voter loyalty.[21]

Long-term leadership of Bodoland Territorial Council

Tenure as inaugural Chief Executive Member (2005â€“2020)

Hagrama Mohilary assumed office as the inaugural Chief Executive Member (CEM) of the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) on June 2, 2005, after the Bodoland People's Front (BPF), which he founded, won 22 of the 40 elected seats in the council's first elections held in May 2005.[7] This victory marked the successful transition of former militants, including Mohilary, into democratic governance following the 2003 Bodo Accord that established the BTC under the Sixth Schedule of the Indian Constitution.[7] Mohilary's leadership secured BPF dominance in subsequent BTC elections, with the party capturing 31 seats in 2010â€”over 75% of the elected positionsâ€”and 20 seats in 2015, enabling him to serve three consecutive terms until the 2020 polls.[7][22] During this 15-year period, spanning approximately 17 years of effective rule including interim phases, he consolidated control over the BTC's administration across the four Bodoland Territorial Area Districts (BTAD): Baksa, Chirang, Kokrajhar, and Udalguri.[4][2] The tenure emphasized stabilizing the region post-insurgency, with Mohilary prioritizing Bodo community interests through BPF's regional platform, though independent candidates increasingly challenged the party's hold, winning 6 seats in 2010 and 15 in 2015, signaling eroding mass support amid governance issues.[7] Political alliances with state-level parties, such as Congress until 2011 and later BJP-aligned groups, bolstered BPF's influence in Assam Assembly elections, where it won 12 seats in both 2011 and 2016.[7] Mohilary's administration addressed core regional vulnerabilities, including floods, unemployment, and illiteracy, laying groundwork for targeted development while navigating internal party dynamics and external pressures.[7]

Development initiatives across sectors

Under Hagrama Mohilary's leadership as Chief Executive Member of the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) from 2005 to 2020, initiatives emphasized physical infrastructure to address longstanding deficits in the Bodoland Territorial Area Districts (BTAD). Between 2009 and 2016, the BTC constructed 2,680 gravelled roads and 2,650 blacktopped roads under the Public Works Department, totaling 5,330 kilometers of road development, alongside ongoing work on 56.55 kilometers of national highway as of 2015-2016.[23] Additional efforts included building reinforced cement concrete (RCC) box cell, slab, and Hume pipe culverts starting in 2008-2009, and major public structures such as the BTC Administrative Assembly and Secretariat Complex in Kokrajhar (sanctioned Rs. 17 crore), district centers in Chirang and Baksa (Rs. 5 crore each), and sub-divisional centers in locations like Kazigaon-Salbari and Tamulpur (Rs. 2 crore each).[23] Bodoland Guest Houses were also established in New Delhi, Kolkata, Siliguri, Guwahati, and within BTC districts to support administrative and economic outreach.[23] In education, the BTC government oversaw the construction of schools across the four BTAD districtsâ€”Kokrajhar, Baksa, Chirang, and Udalguriâ€”to expand access amid prior illiteracy challenges, though exact counts remain undocumented in available records.[24] A key milestone was the upgrade and full establishment of Bodoland University as a central university during Mohilary's tenure, aimed at enhancing higher education and human resource development in the region.[24] Health sector initiatives included hospital construction in the BTAD districts to improve service delivery, reflecting progress in basic healthcare infrastructure despite persistent rural gaps.[24][24] Agriculture and related sectors received attention through irrigation expansion, covering 68,799 hectares via major and medium projects and 59,984 hectares via minor schemes from 2009 to 2016, contributing to a gross irrigated area of 86,894 hectares; support extended to providing quality seeds, equipment, and financial aid to small farmers.[23] Fish production rose from 14,777 tons in 2010 to 15,858 tons in 2011, bolstering rural livelihoods.[24] These efforts, drawn from BTC statistical handbooks and field assessments, aligned with broader goals of self-sufficiency but were constrained by floods, unemployment, and limited central funding, as noted in regional analyses.[7]

Political alliances and internal challenges

During Mohilary's tenure as Chief Executive Member of the Bodoland Territorial Council from 2005 to 2020, the Bodoland People's Front (BPF) maintained a strategic alliance with the ruling Congress party in the Assam state government under Chief Minister Tarun Gogoi, spanning approximately 13 years until June 2014. This partnership facilitated access to state resources for BTC development projects, including infrastructure and education initiatives, though it drew criticism for insufficient autonomy enhancements for Bodoland. The alliance dissolved amid BPF accusations of governmental apathy toward regional demands, such as greater fiscal devolution and land rights enforcement, prompting Mohilary to announce the split at a central working committee meeting on June 26, 2014.[25][26] Post-2014, BPF shifted toward cooperation with the BJP-led Assam government after the latter's 2016 assembly election victory, providing external support in key Bodoland constituencies like Kokrajhar and Udalguri to bolster BJP's influence in the region. This realignment secured continued funding flows to the BTC, with Mohilary emphasizing pragmatic "give-and-take" politics to prioritize development over ideological rigidity. However, the partnership frayed by late 2019 over disagreements on the Bodoland Territorial Region (BTR) Accord negotiations, culminating in BPF's withdrawal of support and contributing to its 2020 BTC electoral defeat.[27] Internally, Mohilary's leadership faced challenges from factionalism and accusations of authoritarianism within BPF ranks and broader Bodo political circles, exacerbating splits that undermined party cohesion. A notable rift emerged in 2015 when Pramod Boro, a former BPF executive member, defected to form the United People's Party Liberal (UPPL), citing Mohilary's centralized control and exclusionary decision-making as stifling dissent and innovation. This schism reflected deeper tensions over power-sharing in BTC governance, with Boro positioning UPPL as a more inclusive alternative aligned with ABSU's youth-driven agenda. Such internal dissent, compounded by rivalries with lingering NDFB factions opposed to the 2003 Accord's compromises, periodically disrupted BPF's dominance, though Mohilary retained core loyalty through patronage networks and electoral successes in 2005, 2012, and 2015 BTC polls.[28][29]

Setback and opposition phase

2020 BTC election loss amid BTR Accord

The Bodoland Territorial Region (BTR) Accord, signed on January 27, 2020, between the Government of India, the Government of Assam, the All Bodo Students' Union (ABSU), and factions of the National Democratic Front of Bodoland (NDFB), established the BTR as an autonomous administrative unit under the Sixth Schedule of the Indian Constitution, enhancing powers over the existing Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) while promising development funds, reservations, and rehabilitation for militants.[30] The accord aimed to end decades of insurgency but did not involve Hagrama Mohilary's Bodoland People's Front (BPF), which stemmed from the earlier 2003 Bodo Accord and had dominated BTC governance since inception. This exclusion positioned BPF as an outsider to the new peace framework, amid shifting alliances where BPF aligned with the opposition Congress party, prompting the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) to back the newly formed United People's Party Liberal (UPPL), launched by ABSU leaders to champion accord implementation.[31] BTC elections for its 40 seats, covering districts of Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa, Udalguri, and Tamulpur, occurred in three phases from December 7 to 10, 2020, under the BTR's upgraded structure, with results declared on December 13.[32] BPF, contesting as the incumbent, secured 17 seats, down from its previous majority, while UPPL won 12 and BJP 9; the remaining two went to the Gana Suraksha Parishad (GSP) and an independent. The UPPL-BJP informal alliance claimed 21 seats, sufficient to form the executive council, leading to Pramod Boro of UPPL being elected Chief Executive Member (CEM) on December 15, 2020, ousting Mohilary after his 15-year tenure. Voter turnout averaged 72% across phases, reflecting polarized Bodo sentiments favoring the accord's signatory groups over BPF's established but criticized long-term rule.[32] [31] The loss stemmed from anti-incumbency against BPF's prolonged dominance, coupled with the accord's momentum boosting UPPL as the fresh face of Bodo aspirations, including promises of â‚¹1,500 crore annual central funding and 1,000 police jobs for Bodos. BPF's opposition ties alienated BJP voters, while internal Bodo divisionsâ€”exacerbated by the accord sidelining 2003-era leaders like Mohilaryâ€”split the vote base traditionally loyal to his faction. Mohilary contested from two constituencies but prioritized broader strategy, later decrying the accord's implementation as inadequate for development, though immediate post-election analyses attributed BPF's defeat to the strategic rise of accord-aligned politics rather than outright rejection of his leadership. This marked BPF's transition to opposition, ending its uninterrupted control since 2005.[31][33]

Activities as opposition leader

Following the 2020 Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) election defeat, where the Bodoland People's Front (BPF) secured 17 seats against the United People's Party Liberal (UPPL)-Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) coalition's majority, Hagrama Mohilary positioned the BPF as the principal opposition force in the reconstituted Bodoland Territorial Region (BTR). Mohilary publicly criticized the UPPL-led administration under Pramod Boro for betraying core Bodo nationalist aspirations by endorsing the 2020 Bodo Peace Accord, which he described as a compromise that diluted demands for full statehood and failed to deliver tangible benefits.[6] He labeled Boro a "trader" who prioritized political expediency over ethnic interests, arguing that the accord's provisions for expanded autonomy, special development packages worth â‚¹1,500 crore annually, and rehabilitation for militants had not materialized into improved livelihoods.[6][33] Mohilary's opposition strategy emphasized highlighting governance shortcomings, including stalled job creation, unresolved land rights disputes affecting thousands of Bodo families, and inadequate infrastructure progress despite central allocations. In a September 2025 interview ahead of the polls, he asserted that the BTR Accord "hasn't brought any change," with residents still facing poverty, unemployment, and exclusion from scheme benefits due to alleged corruption in distribution by UPPL-BJP functionaries.[33] The BPF organized public meetings and rallies to amplify these grievances, framing the ruling coalition's tenure as marked by misgovernance and unfulfilled promises, such as delays in establishing promised industrial parks and educational institutions. Mohilary also demanded amnesty and rehabilitation for National Democratic Front of Bodoland (NDFB) leaders like Ranjan Daimary, criticizing the government for sidelining ex-militants integral to the peace process.[33][34] Despite internal challenges, including defections of at least five BPF members to UPPL between 2020 and 2024â€”such as Chandan Brahma in March 2024â€”Mohlilary sustained opposition momentum through targeted political outreach, reclaiming defectors' constituencies via new candidates and leveraging anti-incumbency sentiments. He dismissed UPPL's development claims as exaggerated, pointing to persistent issues like youth migration due to job scarcity and uneven fund utilization, with only partial disbursement of the accord's â‚¹15,000 crore special package by 2025.[6][34] This period saw BPF abstaining from key BTR assembly sessions in protest and issuing resolutions condemning the coalition's "divisive politics," which Mohilary argued prioritized non-Bodo alliances over community welfare.[35]

Political comeback

2025 BTC election triumph

The 2025 Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) election was conducted on September 22, 2025, across 40 constituencies in the districts of Kokrajhar, Chirang, Baksa, Udalguri, and Tamulpur, to elect members to the autonomous council established under the Sixth Schedule of the Indian Constitution.[36][37] Results were declared on September 27, 2025, revealing a resounding triumph for the Bodoland People's Front (BPF), which captured 28 seats, securing a clear majority.[38][39][5] BPF president Hagrama Mohilary's leadership propelled the party to reclaim power, decisively defeating the incumbent United People's Party Liberal (UPPL)-Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) alliance, which had governed since the 2020 polls.[40][37] UPPL secured 7 seats, while BJP won 5, marking a reversal from their 2020 dominance and interrupting the BJP-led National Democratic Alliance's (NDA) regional streak.[39][5] The BPF's victory was attributed to voter dissatisfaction with the outgoing administration's handling of development and ethnic representation issues, alongside Mohilary's longstanding influence in Bodo politics.[40][41] This outcome represented BPF's strongest performance since the council's inception, underscoring Mohilary's resilience following the party's 2020 defeat amid the Bodoland Territorial Region (BTR) Accord negotiations.[38][5] Although BPF aligned with the NDA at the state level, it contested independently in BTC, leveraging local grievances over resource allocation and implementation of accord provisions to mobilize Bodo voters.[42][5] The mandate affirmed BPF's dominance in the region's ethnic polity, with turnout reported at approximately 72% across polling stations.[36]

Reappointment as CEM and immediate priorities

Following the Bodoland People's Front's (BPF) victory in securing 28 of the 40 seats in the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) elections held on September 22, 2025, Hagrama Mohilary was sworn in as Chief Executive Member (CEM) for the fourth time on October 5, 2025, at the Bodoland Secretariat Field in Kokrajhar.[4][43] The oath was administered by Assam Chief Secretary Ravi Kota in the presence of Assam Governor Lakshman Prasad Acharya and Chief Minister Himanta Biswa Sarma, marking BPF's return to power after a five-year hiatus following the 2020 elections.[44][45] In his post-swearing-in remarks, Mohilary reaffirmed his commitment to fulfilling the BPF's election promises, emphasizing the need for cooperation with the state government to address Bodoland's developmental challenges.[46] These promises, outlined in the BPF manifesto released on September 14, 2025, included granting land rights to landless individuals, youth empowerment initiatives, and financial assistance programs, alongside broader goals of peace, unity, and prosperity.[47][48] The BTC's first executive meeting on October 15, 2025, under Mohilary's leadership, set immediate priorities focused on governance reforms and anti-corruption measures. Key initiatives included the legal distribution of land pattas (title deeds) with assistance from retired land revenue officials, the construction of a mini secretariat in Udalguri district, and the prompt release of 2.5 years of pending salaries for fixed-pay employees.[49] To combat corruption, the council announced the cancellation of fraudulent allocations under the Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY), initiating a geo-tagged survey to be completed by October 18, 2025, with legal action against those responsible.[49] Reviews of past recruitments in the Forest Department (Grades I and II) and Village Council Development Committee (VCDC) secretary positions were ordered, with illegal appointments to be revoked. Additionally, the Central Selection Board was dissolved, and unexecuted tenders across 40 departments were canceled, to be replaced with transparent new processes.[49] These early actions underscored a broader emphasis on infrastructure development, overhauling welfare schemes, and enhancing transparency in public works, aiming to restore public trust amid criticisms of prior administrations.[49][39]

Controversies and criticisms

Enduring impact of militant legacy

Mohilary's tenure as commander-in-chief of the Bodo Liberation Tigers (BLT) ended with the group's surrender of arms under the Bodoland Territorial Council (BTC) Accord signed on February 10, 2003, involving 2,641 cadres who were subsequently rehabilitated into mainstream society. This demobilization laid the institutional foundation for the BTC's autonomous governance over the Bodoland Territorial Area Districts (BTAD), transforming armed insurgency into electoral politics and enabling Mohilary's elevation to the inaugural Chief Executive Member position. The process significantly curtailed large-scale Bodo militancy in the region, shifting focus toward administrative control and development initiatives.[10][7] The integration of ex-BLT cadres into the Bodoland People's Front (BPF), which Mohilary founded post-surrender, has sustained a patronage network rooted in militant-era loyalties, underpinning the party's repeated electoral successes in BTC polls from 2005 onward. Former militants and their affiliates have occupied key administrative and legislative roles within the BTC, fostering a political ecosystem where cadre discipline translates into voter mobilization and internal party cohesion. This structure has bolstered BPF's dominance but also entrenched perceptions of a militarized political culture, with ex-combatants leveraging historical allegiances to maintain influence amid factional challenges.[10][50] Mohilary's credibility as a former insurgent leader has extended to brokering further peace efforts, including his 2019 advocacy for initiating talks with the NDFB (Songbijit) faction and contributions to the 2020 Bodo Territorial Region (BTR) Accord, which prompted the surrender of 1,615 NDFB cadres and their arms on January 30, 2020. These initiatives reinforced Bodoland's trajectory toward an "arms-free zone," as Mohilary described it, by drawing splinter groups into the political fold and reducing active insurgent threats. However, the persistence of ex-militant candidates in BTC electionsâ€”such as two BPF-affiliated former NDFB members winning seats in 2020â€”highlights how the legacy sustains a continuum between armed struggle and governance, potentially complicating full civilian oversight.[51][9][52][53] Critics, including political rivals like Assam Minister Himanta Biswa Sarma, contend that the enduring cadre loyalty from Mohilary's militant phase has fostered governance patterns reminiscent of hierarchical command, exacerbating ethnic frictions with non-Bodo populations who comprise a demographic majority in parts of BTAD and report ongoing insecurities tied to unresolved insurgency-era grievances. While the legacy facilitated accords and reduced overt violence, it has not eliminated sporadic tensions or splinter militancy, as evidenced by pre-2020 NDFB attacks, underscoring limits to rehabilitation in addressing broader communal divides.[50][53]

Allegations of corruption and mismanagement

In October 2022, Bodoland Territorial Region (BTR) Chief Pramod Boro accused Hagrama Mohilary of rampant corruption and fund misutilization during his prior tenure as BTC Chief Executive Member, citing massive anomalies uncovered in departmental audits following the change in council leadership.[54] Boro specifically highlighted irregularities in financial handling across BTC departments, attributing them to Mohilary's oversight from 2005 to 2020.[54] On January 24, 2023, BTR MLA Lawrence Islary echoed these claims, alleging widespread corruption and diversion of public funds under Mohilary's administration, including misuse of development allocations for personal or unauthorized gains.[55] Islary demanded accountability for the alleged mismanagement, pointing to stalled infrastructure projects and unaccounted expenditures as evidence of fiscal irresponsibility.[55] A Public Interest Litigation filed in the Gauhati High Court on December 5, 2020, targeted BTC under Mohilary's leadership for systemic corruption and mismanagement, seeking judicial intervention to probe procurement irregularities and beneficiary scheme abuses.[56] The petition referenced over-reliance on opaque tender processes and failure to deliver on allocated funds for education and health sectors, though no final convictions resulted from the case.[56] Critics, including the United People's Party Liberal (UPPL), have demanded a Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI) probe into Mohilary's undisclosed assets, alleging accumulation through illicit BTC revenues during his 17-year rule, which reportedly left an additional â‚¹2,000 crore in liabilities for successors.[57] [58] In August 2025, Boro reiterated charges of property amassing via corrupt means, including Mohilary's admitted sale of land valued at â‚¹22 crore, with references to Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG) reports flagging discrepancies. Mohilary has denied these graft allegations, as in a 2015 response to protests over village council development scheme frauds, asserting no personal involvement and blaming lower-level functionaries.[59] Political opponents, primarily from UPPL and BJP-aligned factions, have driven most accusations amid electoral rivalries, with no independent judicial findings of guilt documented to date.[60] Recent scrutiny intensified in October 2025 over Mohilary's purchase of a Toyota Vellfire vehicle costing â‚¹1.20 crore shortly after his reappointment, raising questions about funding sources amid prior claims of amassed wealth from public coffers.[61]
