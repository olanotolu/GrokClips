New Oriental

New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. is a Chinese holding company that provides private educational services, including English language training, test preparation for domestic and international examinations, and overseas study consulting, primarily targeting students and professionals in China.[1][2] Founded in 1993 by Yu Minhong in Beijing as a small English training school, it expanded rapidly amid rising demand for global education opportunities, establishing a nationwide network of learning centers and becoming the largest provider of such services in the country.[1][3] The company achieved a milestone in 2006 by listing on the New York Stock Exchange as the first education firm from China to do so, which fueled further growth and international recognition.[4][5] In July 2021, New Oriental faced severe disruption from China's "Double Reduction" policy, which banned for-profit tutoring in core K-12 academic subjects to alleviate student workloads and family financial burdens, resulting in the closure of thousands of centers, layoffs of approximately 60,000 employees, and a sharp decline in revenue.[6][7] To adapt, the company pivoted to non-academic offerings such as holiday camps, vocational training, live-streaming e-commerce for agricultural products, and expanded study abroad services, which unexpectedly generated viral publicity and partial revenue recovery.[8][9] By 2023â€“2024, as regulatory pressures eased and demand for permissible education services rebounded, New Oriental resumed expansion of compliant operations, reporting profitability and stock value gains amid a broader sector revival.[10][11] This resilience highlights the company's ability to navigate state-driven economic interventions, though it underscores ongoing vulnerabilities to policy shifts in China's tightly regulated education landscape.[12][13]

Founding and Early Growth

Establishment and Initial Focus (1993â€“2005)

Beijing New Oriental School was established on November 16, 1993, in Beijing by Yu Minhong, an English teacher and Peking University graduate who had begun offering private tutoring sessions prior to formalizing the venture.[14][15] The founding came amid rising demand for English language skills among Chinese students seeking overseas education opportunities, with Yu leveraging his experience to address gaps in test preparation. Initial operations focused primarily on cram courses for the Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL) and similar exams like the Graduate Record Examination (GRE), emphasizing practical skills for international academic admissions rather than general language instruction.[11][16] These programs targeted university students and professionals, starting small with group classes that quickly gained popularity due to their results-oriented approach and Yu's reputation from university teaching. By the end of the first year, enrollments reached 15,000 students.[14] Through the late 1990s and early 2000s, New Oriental expanded its footprint while maintaining a core emphasis on overseas test preparation, opening a bookstore in 1996 and establishing schools in major cities such as Guangzhou and Shanghai in 2000, alongside launching an online education platform.[15] This period saw steady growth in program diversity, including supplementary English training, but the primary revenue driver remained exam-specific tutoring for study abroad. By 2003, the company had enrolled its 1,000,000th student cumulatively.[14] By 2005, New Oriental had developed into a network of schools across China, with new branches in cities including Changsha, Jinan, Harbin, Hangzhou, Zhengzhou, Changchun, and Taiyuan, supported by a faculty and staff exceeding 4,000.[14] Annual student enrollments reached 800,000 that year, and cumulative registrations approached 3 million, reflecting the institution's dominance in private English test preparation amid China's expanding middle class and internationalization push.[7][14]

Expansion and IPO (2006â€“2020)

In September 2006, New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. completed its initial public offering on the New York Stock Exchange under the ticker symbol EDU, becoming the first Chinese educational institution to list on a major U.S. exchange.[17] The company issued 7.5 million American depositary shares (ADSs), each representing four common shares, at $15 per ADS, raising approximately $112.5 million before underwriting discounts.[18] Shares opened at $22 on the first trading day, reflecting a 46.7% premium over the offering price and indicating strong investor interest in China's burgeoning private education sector.[19] Following the IPO, New Oriental utilized proceeds to fuel geographic and operational expansion, growing its network of schools and learning centers across China. As of May 31, 2006, the company operated 25 schools and 111 learning centers, primarily in major cities like Beijing and Shanghai.[20] By 2016, this had expanded to 67 language schools and 771 learning centers, with further growth to 112 schools and 1,472 learning centers by August 2020, enabling broader access to tier-1, tier-2, and emerging tier-3 cities.[21] This network buildup supported diversified offerings, including new preschool programs launched in 2007 and expanded K-12 tutoring, overseas study consulting, and online courses, shifting from core test preparation to a fuller educational services portfolio.[22] Student enrollments surged amid this expansion, rising from approximately 872,000 in fiscal year 2006 (ended May 31) to 10.6 million by fiscal year 2020, driven by increased demand for English language training, standardized test prep (e.g., TOEFL, SAT, GRE), and supplementary academic subjects.[20][7] Net revenues correspondingly grew from RMB 770.3 million (about US$96.1 million) in fiscal 2006 to higher levels, with fiscal 2020 reporting a 15.6% year-over-year increase and enrollments up 26.3%, reflecting robust pre-regulatory demand in China's private tutoring market.[20][23] By 2017, the company's market capitalization exceeded US$10 billion, underscoring its scaled operations and brand dominance.[15] The period also saw strategic enhancements, such as investments in content distribution, faculty development, and technology integration for hybrid learning models, though challenges like regional competition and regulatory scrutiny in select provinces began emerging by the late 2010s. New Oriental's focus remained on organic growth and program innovation, avoiding major acquisitions in favor of internal scaling to maintain quality control in a fragmented industry.[20][24]

Core Operations and Business Model

Educational Offerings

New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. specialized in private tutoring and test preparation services, primarily targeting Chinese students seeking academic advancement domestically and abroad.[25] Its core offerings included after-school programs for K-12 students covering subjects such as mathematics, Chinese language, physics, chemistry, geography, and other core disciplines, aimed at enhancing performance in compulsory education and entrance exams.[26][27] The company provided extensive test preparation courses for domestic examinations, notably the gaokao (China's national college entrance exam), alongside supplementary tutoring for middle and high school curricula.[25] For international aspirations, New Oriental offered preparation for standardized overseas tests, including TOEFL and IELTS for English proficiency, SAT and ACT for undergraduate admissions, and GRE and GMAT for graduate programs.[25][28] These programs emphasized intensive training, mock exams, and skill-building in reading, writing, speaking, and quantitative reasoning.[25] Language training formed a foundational segment, with English courses for beginners to advanced learners across age groups, including adults pursuing professional certifications like BEC and TOEIC.[28] Additional offerings encompassed pre-school education, online learning modules, and non-academic enrichment classes, though test prep and K-12 tutoring constituted the majority of enrollment prior to regulatory changes.[27][29] Delivery modes included in-person classes at over 100 learning centers, virtual platforms, and hybrid formats to accommodate diverse student needs.[29]

Overseas Consulting and Additional Services

Beijing New Oriental Vision Overseas Consulting Co., Ltd., a subsidiary of New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc., provides comprehensive study abroad services, with its predecessor, the New Oriental Overseas Consulting Service Center, established in 1996 and formalized as the current entity in 2004.[30] The subsidiary operates branches in 43 cities across China and maintains overseas offices in Canada, Japan, Australia, and the United Kingdom to support students pursuing international education.[30] Core offerings encompass one-stop guidance, including examination preparation, personalized study abroad planning, university and program applications, scholarship assistance, visa and immigration processing, and post-arrival support such as on-campus and off-campus housing arrangements.[30][31] Experienced consultants collaborate with students from initial assessment through departure, supplemented by specialized training programs to prepare for overseas academic and cultural challenges.[31] Since 2016, the firm has expanded into art study abroad applications following the acquisition of SFK, enhancing its portfolio for creative disciplines.[30] Additional services include hosting international education exhibitionsâ€”59 events across more than 40 cities as of 2024â€”and annual publication of study abroad reports for a decade, providing data-driven insights on trends, destinations, and application strategies.[30] These initiatives complement the consulting by fostering awareness and partnerships with global institutions, while the business line has demonstrated resilience, with overseas study consulting revenues rising 27% year-over-year in certain fiscal quarters amid broader market recovery.[32][33]

Leadership and Key Figures

Yu Minhong's Role

Yu Minhong, also known as Michael Yu, founded New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. in 1993 as Beijing New Oriental School, initially focusing on English language training for students preparing for international standardized tests such as the TOEFL and GRE.[34] Drawing from his own experiences as a Peking University English graduate who faced repeated failures in early academic pursuits due to language barriers, Yu positioned the company as a pioneer in private overseas education consulting and test preparation in China.[35] Under his leadership, New Oriental expanded from a single classroom in Beijing to a network of over 100 learning centers by the early 2000s, emphasizing practical skills and motivational teaching methods that resonated with ambitious Chinese youth seeking opportunities abroad.[4] Yu assumed the role of chief executive officer upon the company's formal incorporation in 2001 and led it through its initial public offering on the New York Stock Exchange in September 2006, which raised approximately $108 million and valued the firm at over $1 billion at debut.[34] He served as CEO until September 2016, during which period New Oriental diversified into K-12 education, online platforms, and international study abroad services, growing annual revenues from $23.8 million in fiscal 2006 to $3.1 billion by fiscal 2020.[34] Throughout this expansion, Yu maintained oversight as chairman of the board since August 2001, steering strategic decisions amid China's evolving regulatory landscape for private education.[34] Following the 2021 Chinese government crackdown on for-profit tutoring, which halted core K-12 operations and erased over $10 billion in market value, Yu resumed hands-on CEO responsibilities in addition to his chairmanship, orchestrating a rapid pivot to e-commerce and live-streaming sales of agricultural products.[36] This transition, initiated in July 2021, involved personally hosting broadcasts that generated over 100 million yuan in sales within weeks, leveraging New Oriental's teaching expertise for product education and marketing.[37] Yu's direct involvement in this reinvention preserved thousands of jobs and stabilized finances, with the e-commerce arm reporting 1.5 billion yuan in gross merchandise value by mid-2022, though it represented a fraction of pre-crackdown revenues.[4] His public reflections on the regulatory shift, including regrets over the U.S. listing's compliance burdens, underscored his adaptive leadership amid geopolitical and domestic pressures.[38]

Notable Executives and Influencers

Chenggang Zhou has served as chief executive officer of New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. since September 2016 and as a director since November 2010; he joined the company in 2000 and previously held roles including president of the company's international education division.[39] Zhou succeeded founder Yu Minhong in the CEO position following a management transition aimed at streamlining operations amid expansion.[40] Stephen Zhihui Yang serves as executive president and chief financial officer, overseeing financial strategy and operations, including commentary on quarterly results to highlight core educational segments post-regulatory shifts.[41] Yang's role has emphasized fiscal prudence during the company's pivot from tutoring to diversified services.[42] Tung-Jung Hsieh acts as senior adviser and director, contributing to strategic oversight based on prior executive experience within the firm.[43] Among influencers, Dong Yuhui, a former English teacher at New Oriental, rose to prominence as a top livestreaming host under the company's Oriental Selection brand following the 2021 regulatory crackdown, generating significant sales through educational and cultural content.[44] In December 2023, Dong was appointed cultural assistant to the chairman and vice president of cultural development and operations at New Oriental's cultural tourism and vocational training arm, amid internal disputes over content and branding.[45] His influence extended to collaborations like a 2023 Taobao debut, boosting the e-commerce pivot.[46] Sun Dongxu, former CEO of New Oriental's livestreaming e-commerce platform, was dismissed in December 2023 after controversies involving influencer promotions and internal conflicts with figures like Dong Yuhui, highlighting tensions in the post-tutoring business model.[44]

Intellectual Property Conflicts

ETS Lawsuit and Outcomes (2001)

In February 2001, the Educational Testing Service (ETS), along with the Graduate Management Admission Council (GMAC), filed a copyright infringement lawsuit against Beijing New Oriental School in a Beijing court, alleging that the institution had reproduced, published, and distributed unauthorized copies of TOEFL, GRE, and GMAT test questions and answers, including "live" questions still in active use.[47][48] ETS claimed these materials were obtained illicitly during paper-based test administrations, compromising exam security and violating copyrights protected under China's commitments to the Berne Convention.[47] The suit sought an injunction to halt the infringement, compensation for lost profits and legal costs, destruction of infringing materials, and a public apology in Chinese media.[47] New Oriental, which at the time prepared approximately 80% of China's roughly 24,000 annual applicants for U.S. graduate studies, initially denied stealing questions and argued that higher student scores resulted from effective teaching rather than leaked materials.[47] The case highlighted broader tensions over intellectual property enforcement in China's burgeoning test-preparation industry, where reproducing exam content was a widespread practice despite ETS's prior warnings and a brief authorization period that New Oriental allegedly violated.[47] Proceedings spanned several years, marking one of the first major enforcements of foreign copyrights in Chinese courts.[49] In September 2003, an initial court ruling imposed a fine of 10 million yuan on New Oriental.[49] By December 29, 2004, the Beijing No.1 Intermediate People's Court found New Oriental guilty of copyright infringement but rejected claims of trademark violation, ruling that references to TOEFL, GRE, and GMAT were descriptive of test types rather than branded misuse.[48] The court ordered payment of 6.4 million yuan (approximately US$774,000) in damages to ETS and GMAC, surrender of all infringing materials, and publication of a public apology in the Legal Daily.[48] New Oriental acknowledged past errors, stated it had ceased unauthorized copying, and committed to promoting intellectual property rights compliance.[48] New Oriental appealed the decision. In February 2005, the Beijing Higher People's Court reduced the compensation to 3.74 million yuan (approximately US$450,624) plus 22,000 yuan in court fees, upholding the core infringement finding while adjusting the damages amount.[50] The resolution reinforced ETS's test security measures in China, where annual TOEFL and GRE takers numbered around 100,000, and deterred similar practices among competitors, though New Oriental reported the financial impact as manageable.[47][50]

Regulatory Pressures in China

2021 Tutoring Crackdown

In July 2021, the Chinese government introduced the "Double Reduction" policy, formally titled "Opinions on Further Alleviating the Burden of Homework and After-School Training for Students in Compulsory Education," aimed at curbing excessive academic pressure on children by restricting for-profit tutoring in core subjects such as mathematics, Chinese, and English for K-12 students.[10][51] The policy, issued by the General Office of the Communist Party of China Central Committee and the General Office of the State Council on July 24, prohibited tutoring companies from operating on weekends, holidays, or school vacations; raising capital via listings for mandatory education subjects; hiring foreign teachers, recent graduates, or current students; and using aggressive marketing or preying on anxiety.[10][52] Existing firms like New Oriental were required to register as non-profits for core-subject tutoring, effectively dismantling their profitable K-12 segments which had driven much of the company's revenue.[7][53] New Oriental, China's largest private tutoring provider with a market capitalization exceeding $12 billion pre-crackdown, faced immediate devastation as over 70% of its revenue stemmed from K-9 academic tutoring.[54] Shares of New Oriental Education Technology Group Inc., listed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE: EDU) and Hong Kong Stock Exchange, plunged more than 90% from their July 2021 peak, erasing over $10 billion in market value within weeks, as investors anticipated regulatory enforcement would halt core operations.[55][56] The policy's rollout triggered a sector-wide evaporation of approximately $100 billion in combined market value for major players including New Oriental and TAL Education, with trading halts and delisting risks amplifying the fallout.[57] In response, New Oriental swiftly shuttered hundreds of physical centers and online platforms focused on prohibited subjects, leading to the layoff of around 60,000 employeesâ€”roughly 60% of its workforceâ€”by early 2022, as confirmed by founder Yu Minhong.[6][58] The company reported a net loss of $951 million for the fiscal year ending May 2022, contrasting with prior profitability, and allocated funds for severance while donating unsold teaching materials to charity amid operational wind-down.[54] Enforcement involved local authorities raiding facilities and fining violators, compelling New Oriental to pivot away from its legacy model toward non-core areas like adult education and overseas test prep, though these segments could not immediately offset the losses.[59][60]

Operational and Financial Impacts

Following the issuance of China's "Opinions on Further Reducing Students' Homework Burden and After-School Tutoring Burden" on July 24, 2021, New Oriental Education & Technology Group Inc. suspended its core K-9 academic tutoring operations, which had constituted the bulk of its business, leading to the closure of numerous campuses across major cities like Beijing and Shanghai.[61][62] The company issued refunds for prepaid tuition, terminated employee contracts, and canceled leases on teaching sites, incurring costs of approximately 20 billion yuan (about US$3.1 billion).[61] These measures resulted in the layoff of around 60,000 staff members by early 2022, representing a significant portion of its workforce amid the abrupt halt in for-profit tutoring for compulsory education subjects.[58][63] Financially, the regulatory changes triggered an 80% drop in revenue and a 90% decline in market capitalization, with New Oriental losing roughly US$28 billion in shareholder value during 2021.[58][64] Shares of the company, listed on the New York Stock Exchange and Hong Kong Exchange, plummeted immediately after the policy announcement, erasing tens of billions in market value across the sector.[65] For the six months ended November 30, 2021, New Oriental projected net losses between US$800 million and US$900 million, reflecting the cessation of high-margin tutoring revenue streams.[66] By the fiscal fourth quarter of 2022, quarterly revenue had fallen 56.8% year-over-year to US$524 million, underscoring the policy's enduring pressure on legacy operations.[67]

Strategic Pivot to E-Commerce

Transition to Live Streaming Sales

In the aftermath of China's "double reduction" policy enacted on July 24, 2021, which banned for-profit tutoring in core academic subjects and compelled New Oriental to shutter many of its learning centers, the company accelerated its diversification away from education toward e-commerce. By January 2022, New Oriental had laid off approximately 60,000 employeesâ€”over 90% of its workforceâ€”and redirected resources to new ventures, including an online platform for selling agricultural products sourced from rural farmers to aid economic revitalization in those areas.[6] This strategic shift culminated in late 2021 with the launch of Oriental Selection, New Oriental's dedicated live streaming e-commerce arm operating primarily on Douyin (China's TikTok), focusing initially on everyday consumer goods like fruits, books, and educational materials infused with the company's teaching heritage. Early efforts yielded limited traction, with daily gross merchandise value (GMV) remaining under RMB 1 million through mid-2022, as the platform experimented with product assortments and hosting formats amid a competitive livestreaming market dominated by platforms like Taobao Live.[68][69] A pivotal acceleration occurred in June 2022 when former English instructor Dong Yuhui emerged as a star host, leveraging his pedagogical background to deliver bilingual, content-rich streams that blended product promotion with cultural and literary insights, drawing millions of viewers and propelling GMV growth. This approach not only differentiated Oriental Selection from pure sales-focused competitors but also aligned with New Oriental's intellectual assets, contributing to the unit's rapid scaling and the founder's recovery of billionaire status by mid-2022.[70][71]

Growth and Market Adaptation

Following the 2021 regulatory crackdown on for-profit tutoring, New Oriental rapidly expanded its live streaming e-commerce arm, Oriental Selection (later rebranded East Buy), leveraging former educators' public speaking skills to sell agricultural products, books, and private-label goods targeted at rural revitalization efforts. This pivot enabled initial explosive growth, with live-streaming sales volumes surging amid viral popularity of anchors like Dong Yuhui, who drove high single-session sales exceeding 100 million yuan in peak periods. By early 2022, the segment's revenue contribution had propelled overall company revenues toward recovery, marking a pragmatic adaptation to prohibited core activities by capitalizing on China's burgeoning live commerce market, which itself expanded to over 5 trillion yuan in gross merchandise value by 2023.[8] The e-commerce operations demonstrated sustained market adaptation through product diversification and channel expansion. East Buy developed private-label lines emphasizing "healthy and high-quality" items, growing from nascent offerings to 488 stock-keeping units (SKUs) across categories like food and consumer goods by August 2024, distributed via livestreams, online platforms, and offline stores to mitigate reliance on single sales formats. This multi-pronged approach aligned with competitive pressures in live commerce, where real-time engagement and instant purchases boosted conversion rates, contributing to New Oriental's overall revenue rebound to 92% of pre-crackdown levels by late 2023.[72][73] Financial metrics underscored the pivot's efficacy despite internal disruptions, such as anchor transitions. For fiscal year 2025 ended May 31, 2025, New Oriental reported total net revenues of US$4,900.3 million, a 13.6% year-over-year increase, with core segments excluding East Buy private-label and livestreaming revenues growing faster at rates up to 32.5% in new educational initiatives. In the first fiscal quarter of 2025 (ended August 31, 2024), total revenues rose 30.5% to US$1,435.4 million, while excluding East Buy contributions, growth accelerated to 33.5%, reflecting resilient adaptation through integrated online-offline models and AI-enhanced product curation amid e-commerce saturation.[41][73]

Internal Disputes and Key Resolutions

2023 Livestreaming Controversy

In December 2023, a dispute erupted at East Buy (also known as Oriental Selection), New Oriental's e-commerce and livestreaming arm, involving star host Dong Yuhui, a former English teacher who had become central to the company's pivot to selling agricultural products and books through culturally infused sales pitches. On December 15, the East Buy editorial team commented on a Douyin promotional video from Dong's account "Sixteen Studies from the Desk of Dong Yuhui," stating that his signature "little essays"â€”eloquent scripts blending literature and product promotionâ€”were largely team-written and critiquing them as overly literary and unsuitable for driving sales.[74][75] This revelation undermined the perception of Dong's content as his solo creation, prompting fans to accuse the company of exploiting his personal brand while downplaying his contributions.[76] The backlash intensified rapidly, with supporters flooding social media to defend Dong, calling for boycotts of East Buy products and deleting app content, resulting in a loss of approximately 800,000 to 3 million Douyin followers within daysâ€”from over 31 million to around 28 millionâ€”and a nearly 20% plunge in East Buy's Hong Kong-listed stock price, from HK$32.75 to HK$26.25.[75][76] New Oriental founder Yu Minhong publicly apologized on December 14, describing the incident as an "internal matter" that exposed "big holes" in management and poor handling of personnel relations, while emphasizing the need for better governance in the livestreaming sector's star-dependent model.[75][76] In response, East Buy removed CEO Sun Dongxuâ€”also New Oriental's vice presidentâ€”on December 16 for management shortcomings, with Yu Minhong assuming the CEO role and joining Dong in a livestream to reaffirm unity and address the "chaos."[76] Dong was promoted to senior partner at East Buy and vice president of New Oriental's tourism and culture division, granted an independent brand "Time with Yuhui" (or "Walk with Hui"), and confirmed his intent to remain with the company, though the episode highlighted tensions over credit attribution, team dynamics, and overreliance on individual influencers in China's competitive livestreaming e-commerce landscape.[74][76] The incident drew regulatory calls for improved corporate governance amid fandom-driven volatility, erasing hundreds of millions in market value and underscoring vulnerabilities in East Buy's post-2021 tutoring ban adaptation strategy.[76]

Dong Yuhui's Independence (2024)

In July 2024, Dong Yuhui, a prominent live-streaming host who had become a key figure in New Oriental's e-commerce pivot, resigned from East Buy Holding, the company's live commerce subsidiary also known as Oriental Selection.[77][78] The departure, announced on July 25, 2024, allowed Dong to pursue independent operations with his personal brand channel, "Time with Yuhui" (Yuhui Tongxing).[79][80] As part of the separation, East Buy agreed to transfer 100% equity in the "Time with Yuhui" entity to Dong for 76.5855 million yuan (approximately $10.6 million USD at prevailing exchange rates).[79][80] East Buy cited Dong's professional development goals and need for personal time management as the primary reasons for the exit, while Dong confirmed the move in an online statement, emphasizing his intent to operate the channel autonomously.[45][79] This transaction followed prior internal tensions, including a 2023 livestreaming dispute, but was framed as an amicable resolution enabling Dong's entrepreneurial shift.[77] The announcement triggered an immediate market reaction, with shares of East Buy (listed as Oriental Selection) dropping over 8% in Hong Kong trading on July 26, 2024, reflecting investor concerns over the loss of Dong's drawing power in live commerce.[45] Despite this, Dong's independent debut livestream under "Time with Yuhui" on July 26, 2024, attracted 54.31 million viewers over four hours, generating significant sales and underscoring his personal brand's standalone viability in China's competitive e-commerce streaming sector.[45] By late 2024, the channel had amassed over 20 million followers, though East Buy continued to grapple with revenue challenges post-departure.[81]

Recent Performance and Developments

Financial Results (2024â€“2025)

For fiscal year 2024, ended May 31, 2024, New Oriental reported net revenues of US$4,313.6 million, marking a 43.9% increase from the prior year, driven primarily by expansion in non-academic tutoring services, overseas study consulting, and e-commerce initiatives following the 2021 regulatory crackdown on for-profit tutoring.[82] Operating income reached US$350.7 million, reflecting improved margins from cost controls and revenue diversification into live-streaming sales and new educational programs.[82] Net income attributable to New Oriental stood at US$309.5 million, underscoring the company's adaptation to a post-regulatory environment through pivots away from core K-12 academic tutoring.[82] In fiscal year 2025, ended May 31, 2025, net revenues grew to US$4,900.3 million, a 13.6% year-over-year increase, with contributions from overseas test preparation (up 14.6% in Q4) and study consulting (up 8.2% in Q4), alongside sustained e-commerce growth excluding the divested East Buy platform.[41] Operating income rose 22.2% to US$428.3 million, supported by operational efficiencies and scaling of new initiatives, though Q4 saw an operating loss of US$8.7 million due to seasonal factors and investments in expansion.[41] Net income attributable to New Oriental increased 20.1% to US$371.7 million for the full year, but declined 73.7% to US$7.1 million in Q4 amid higher costs.[41] The first quarter of fiscal year 2025 (ended August 31, 2024) showed robust momentum with net revenues of US$1,435.4 million, up 30.5% year-over-year, fueled by test preparation and non-GAAP operating income expansion.[83] This performance highlighted resilience in core segments post-pivot, though overall FY2025 growth moderated from FY2024's surge as the company navigated maturing e-commerce channels and regulatory constraints on educational content.[41]

Current Business Directions and Resilience

Following the 2021 regulatory crackdown on for-profit K-9 academic tutoring, New Oriental has redirected its operations toward compliant educational segments, emphasizing overseas test preparation, study abroad consulting, and non-academic tutoring programs. In fiscal year 2025 (ended May 31, 2025), the company's new educational initiatives, including holiday camps, after-school programs, and vocational training, achieved 32.5% year-over-year revenue growth.[41] Non-academic tutoring expanded to 60 cities, enrolling 918,000 students, while intelligent learning systems reached 255,000 paid users across the same network.[41] Adult and university test preparation revenues increased 17.0% year-over-year, and overseas test preparation grew 14.6%, reflecting sustained demand for international education services unaffected by domestic core-subject bans.[41] The firm has integrated technology into its core offerings, launching AI-powered devices and smart study solutions to enhance online-offline hybrid (OMO) teaching models.[41] Concurrently, live streaming e-commerce via platforms like East Buy (formerly Oriental Select) persists as a diversified revenue stream, focusing on private-label products such as agricultural goods and educational merchandise, though it showed slower growth in fiscal 2025 compared to educational segments.[41] Excluding East Buy revenues, core business net revenues rose 18.7% year-over-year in the fourth fiscal quarter of 2025, underscoring the relative strength of education-focused pivots.[41] New Oriental's resilience is evidenced by its overall fiscal 2025 net revenues of US$4.9 billion, a 13.6% increase from the prior year, alongside non-GAAP operating margin expansion to 12.8% through cost optimizations and scalable new ventures.[41] This recovery follows massive layoffs and asset writedowns post-2021, with the company's swift shift to non-prohibited areasâ€”executed within monthsâ€”enabling network expansion to 77 schools and 1,318 learning centers by May 31, 2025.[41] [84] Despite ongoing regulatory scrutiny in China's education sector, these adaptations have stabilized operations and positioned the firm for long-term value creation amid a landscape where many peers struggled or exited.[41]
