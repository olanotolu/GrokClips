Cities of the Underworld

Cities of the Underworld is an American documentary television series that premiered on the History Channel on March 2, 2007, delving into subterranean structures, tunnels, and concealed historical sites beneath cities across the globe.[1][2] The program employs advanced exploration techniques to uncover layers of ancient civilizations, wartime bunkers, and forgotten infrastructure, often venturing hundreds of feet underground in locations such as Istanbul, Berlin, and Edinburgh.[1][3] Initially hosted by Eric Geller in its first season, the series transitioned to Don Wildman as host for subsequent seasons, who guides viewers through these hidden realms while consulting archaeologists and experts.[4][1] Spanning multiple seasons through at least 2021, it has garnered a 7.9 rating on IMDb from nearly 1,000 user reviews, praised for its detailed historical insights into urban underbellies like Scotland's "Sin City" vaults and Hitler's Berlin bunkers.[2][5] The series highlights engineering feats and societal secrets, such as New York's abandoned subway lines and Cairo's ancient cisterns, without notable controversies but emphasizing empirical discovery over speculation.[3][6]

Overview

Premise and Concept

Cities of the Underworld is a documentary television series produced by the History Channel that investigates subterranean structures and hidden histories beneath major urban centers worldwide. The program delves into forgotten tunnels, ancient catacombs, wartime bunkers, and other underground features, illustrating their roles in shaping surface-level events, cultures, and architectures.[1][2] Each episode centers on a specific city, such as New York, Cairo, London, or Tokyo, where explorers descend through layered remnants of past civilizations to uncover archaeological and historical secrets. The series employs on-location footage, expert interviews, and historical context to connect subterranean discoveriesâ€”ranging from mass graves and secret passages to ancient cisterns and dungeonsâ€”to broader narratives of human endurance, conflict, and innovation.[3][7] The conceptual framework emphasizes revealing "cities beneath cities," highlighting how underground spaces served practical purposes like defense, burial, and resource management while harboring mysteries that challenge conventional historical accounts. By prioritizing empirical exploration over speculation, the show aims to provide viewers with tangible insights into urban evolution, often using advanced mapping and imaging technologies to access restricted or hazardous sites.[1][6]

Format and Presentation Style

Cities of the Underworld follows a documentary format in which each episode examines subterranean structures beneath specific cities, such as Edinburgh's vaults or Berlin's bunkers, revealing their historical construction, uses, and associated events through on-site investigations.[1] Episodes typically run 42 to 46 minutes, structured around the host's journey from surface entry points into deeper underground networks, interspersed with archival footage, expert interviews, and contextual historical explanations.[8] The presentation style emphasizes immersive exploration, with host Don Wildmanâ€”taking over narration after initial episodes by Eric Gellerâ€”leading viewers via first-person narration and physical navigation of confined spaces like tunnels and catacombs.[1] Wildman's hosting approach conveys enthusiasm for historical discovery, often employing dramatic phrasing to highlight mysteries while consulting archaeologists or local guides for factual insights into engineering feats and past inhabitants.[2] Visually, the series utilizes high-definition cinematography to capture dimly lit environments, employing artificial lighting, handheld cameras for dynamic movement, and occasional computer-generated reconstructions to illustrate inaccessible or collapsed areas.[1] Cutting-edge tools, including ground-penetrating radar and 3D mapping, are demonstrated on-screen to uncover hidden chambers or pathways, enhancing the adventurous tone without relying on scripted reenactments.[1] This blend of real-time adventure and evidentiary analysis distinguishes the show's style from purely archival documentaries, though some viewer feedback notes repetitive host commentary as a stylistic drawback.[2]

Production History

Development and Initial Premiere

Cities of the Underworld was produced by Authentic Entertainment in association with Thirty Four Productions for the History Channel, expanding on the concept introduced in the earlier special Ancient Marvels: Cities of the Underworld.[2][9] The series concept centered on investigating hidden underground structures, tunnels, and historical remnants beneath major cities worldwide, employing on-location exploration to uncover archaeological and cultural insights.[2] It premiered on March 2, 2007, with historian Eric Geller serving as the initial host and narrator for the first season, which consisted of eight episodes focusing on sites including New York City's subterranean layers and ancient Roman catacombs.[2] The debut episode examined the underworld beneath New York, highlighting Prohibition-era tunnels and forgotten infrastructure.[10] This launch aligned with the History Channel's mid-2000s emphasis on documentary series delving into historical mysteries.[2]

Hosts, Narrators, and Key Personnel

Don Wildman served as the primary host and narrator for Cities of the Underworld from the latter episodes of season 1 through its subsequent seasons, guiding viewers through underground explorations with on-location narration and historical commentary.[11][1] His role involved physically navigating subterranean sites while providing contextual insights into their archaeological and cultural significance.[12] Eric Geller hosted and narrated the initial episodes of season 1 (episodes 1 through 8 and 14), establishing the series' format of descending into hidden urban layers to uncover engineering feats and forgotten histories.[11][12] The transition to Wildman occurred mid-season, reflecting a production decision to maintain continuity in presenting the show's investigative style.[13] Key production personnel included director Emre Sahin, who oversaw multiple episodes, contributing to the visual storytelling of confined underground environments.[7] Producer Sarah Wetherbee handled executive oversight, ensuring logistical coordination for international shoots in challenging subterranean settings.[7] Writers such as Chris Bray and Carolyn Townsend crafted scripts that integrated expert interviews with on-site discoveries, emphasizing factual historical narratives over speculation.[7][13] The series was produced by Authentic Entertainment in collaboration with other entities, focusing on authentic documentation rather than dramatization.[11]

Episodes portray these spaces as repositories of layered histories, spanning ancient engineering feats like rock-hewn refuges to 20th-century military installations, underscoring human persistence in exploiting subsurface environments for survival amid threats such as invasions or disasters.[1] Key motifs include the interplay between surface prosperity and hidden vulnerabilities, with explorations revealing functions like defensive tunnels, catacomb ossuaries, and aqueduct systems that supported urban growth while concealing activities from adversaries. For example, the program highlights how limestone quarries beneath Paris evolved into extensive ossuaries holding remains from over six million individuals, relocated from overcrowded cemeteries between 1786 and 1814 to manage public health crises. Such narratives emphasize causal connections between geological opportunities, technological capabilities, and cultural practices, without unsubstantiated speculation on lost civilizations.[1][7] Archaeologically, the focus lies on urban stratigraphy and the integration of geophysical surveys with historical records to map and interpret subsurface features, often prioritizing sites with verifiable material evidence over anecdotal lore. The series Cities of the Underworld has garnered limited professional critical reviews, with assessments highlighting both its entertainment value and concerns over sensationalism. Allegations of Sensationalism and Exaggeration

Critics have accused Cities of the Underworld of sensationalism through its emphasis on dramatic speculation and urban legends over rigorous historical analysis. In a 2009 review of Season 3, Casey Burchby characterized the series as "overhyped, semi-sensationalistic," arguing that it prioritizes "attention-grabbing speculations" rather than verifiable facts, such as presenting accidental slashes in London limestone mines as deliberate pagan symbols linked to medieval witchcraft.[62] Burchby further contended that the show confuses viewers by treating myths as undisputed history, exemplified by an episode's portrayal of Jack the Ripper maintaining a "SPECTRE-style base of operations" in sewers, which lacks evidential support and veers into fictional exaggeration.[62] The series has also been faulted for blending factual elements with unsubstantiated myth to heighten entertainment value, potentially misleading audiences about the veracity of underground structures' purposes. A review from EDGE Media Network described the program as following the History Channel's pattern of "generously mixing history and fact with sensationalism and myth," noting that host Don Wildman's explorations often amplify intrigue at the expense of scholarly depth.[69] User discussions on urban exploration forums echoed this, criticizing episodes for "over-dramatized" presentations that inflate mundane sites into portentous mysteries, such as portraying routine subterranean features as harbingers of catastrophe.[70] These allegations align with broader critiques of History Channel programming, which some observers claim exploits viewer interest in the arcane by favoring narrative hype over empirical evidence, though the series' defenders highlight its role in popularizing lesser-known archaeological sites. Burchby ultimately dismissed Cities of the Underworld as a "misleading, anti-educational program" that abandons academic history for "low-concept adventure," advising audiences to approach it skeptically.[62] No formal rebuttals from producers addressing these specific claims appear in available reviews, and expert historian or archaeologist critiques remain limited, suggesting the allegations stem primarily from media analysts rather than academic consensus.
