Geography of Cape Verde

Cape Verde (formally, the Republic of Cabo Verde) is an archipelago of 10 volcanic islands and several islets located about 570 km (350 mi) west of the Cape Verde Peninsula in Senegal. It is a group of arid Atlantic islands which are home to distinct communities of plants, birds, and reptiles.

Location and description

The Cape Verde Islands are located in the mid-Atlantic Ocean some 570 km (350 mi) off the west coast of the continent of Africa.[1] The landscape varies from dry plains to high active volcanoes with cliffs rising steeply from the ocean. The climate is arid. The total size is 4,033 km2 (1,557 sq mi). The archipelago consists of ten islands and five smaller islands known as islets, divided into the windward (Barlavento) and leeward (Sotavento) groups.[2] The six islands in the Barlavento group, which sits in the trade winds, are Santo AntÃ£o, SÃ£o Vicente, Santa Luzia, SÃ£o Nicolau, Sal, and Boa Vista.[2] The total land mass is 2,239 km2 (864 sq mi). In the west of the Barlavento group, Santo AntÃ£o, SÃ£o Vicente, SÃ£o Nicolau, Santa Luzia and islets Branco and Raso are volcanic and somewhat rocky yet quite widely capable of supporting semi-arid agriculture. In the east of the Barlavento group, Sal and Boa Vista are lightly hilled, desert islands whose economies were based on salt and rely on fishing and tourism, having more in common with the Sotavento island Maio. There are four main islands of the Sotavento group.[2] The western three islands, Brava, Fogo and Santiago, are rocky and volcanic agricultural islands, with the longest histories of human habitation. The fourth and easternmost island Maio is a flat desert island whose economy was primarily based on salt, giving it more in common with the Barlavento islands Sal and Boa Vista. The IlhÃ©us do Rombo are barren islets north of Brava. The total area of the Sotavento Islands is 1,803 km2 (696 sq mi). Three islands â€“ Sal, Boa Vista, and Maio â€“ generally are level and lack natural water supplies.[2] Mountains higher than 1,280 metres (4,199 ft) are found on Santiago, Fogo, Santo AntÃ£o, and SÃ£o Nicolau.[2] Sand carried by strong winds has caused erosion on all islands, especially the windward ones.[2] Sheer, jagged cliffs rise from the sea on several of the mountainous islands.[2] The lack of natural vegetation in the uplands and coast[2] also contributes to soil erosion.[3] The interior valleys support denser natural vegetation.[2]

Borders

Cabo Verde shares maritime boundaries with Mauritania and Senegal. Cabo Verde has a treaty in force with Senegal (signed 1993) delimiting their maritime boundary. A treaty with Mauritania (signed 2003) has been signed but is not yet in force. There is also an unresolved short maritime boundary with The Gambia. Due to the numerous islands it has an Exclusive Economic Zone of 800,561 kmÂ² (309,098 sq mi).[7][8]

Climate

Rainfall is irregular, historically causing periodic droughts and famines.[2] Desalination plants provide approximately 80% of the country's water supply, serving the majority of the population.[9][10][11] Experiments with fog collectors have been conducted since 1962, and have expanded beyond Serra Malagueta on Santiago Island, including to Brava Island where they produce over 1,500 liters daily as of 2025.[13][14] The average annual precipitation in Praia is approximately 173 mm (6.8 in).[15] Cape Verde's climate is classified as hot semi-arid to hot desert (KÃ¶ppen BSh/BWh).[15] During the winter, storms blowing from the Sahara sometimes cloud the sky; however, sunny days are the norm year round.[2] The clearest skies are found between February and June, with very little rainfall during these months. Saharan dust, Harmattan wind, laden with dust come from the Sahara. This occurs between November and March and is generally similar to the "Calima" affecting the Canary Islands. The ocean near Cabo Verde is an area of tropical cyclone formation; since these storms have the whole Atlantic over which to develop as they move westward, they are among the most intense hurricanes, and are called Cape Verde-type hurricanes. The Cape Verde islands experience environmental degradation due to aridity. Most islands do not receive monsoon rains, resulting in limited tropical precipitation. Rain arrives irregularly, usually between August and October, the single main rainy season. High temperatures and humidity during this period can lead to dew formation in mountainous areas. Orographic effects increase humidity on windward slopes of high mountains, supporting semi-arid or dry forest habitats in some elevated areas, though degraded by human activity. Northeastern slopes often receive more rain than southwestern slopes due to their position as umbria (shaded, moist) areas in the Northern Hemisphere, receiving less solar radiation. These areas are cooler and moister botanically. The current position north of the Canaries has a cooling effect on the islands, making air temperatures more bearable. The islands lack coastal upwellings, so sea temperatures are warmer than nearby Senegal. Steep mountains on islands like Santo AntÃ£o support lush vegetation where humid air condenses. Hurricanes often begin forming in the waters around the islands of Cabo Verde, but rarely affect the islands directly. A Cape Verde-type hurricane forms south of the islands after a tropical wave from Africa during the rainy season, gaining strength over warm Atlantic waters. The laurel forest, a type of cloud forest, develops on mountains where dense moisture from the ocean precipitates due to orographic lift. Warm, moist air rises, cools, and condenses, creating cool, saturated habitats. The subtropical anticyclone and orography bring cool, wet air via trade winds, moderated by ocean proximity. These occur in umbria areas between 600 and 1,500 m, benefiting from trade winds. Recent climate trends indicate increasing aridity; forecasts predict below-average rainfall in 2025, heightening drought and famine risks.[2][16]

Forests

In Cabo Verde forest cover is 12.67% of the total land area, equivalent to 51,060 hectares (ha) of forest as of 2025, up from 33,500 hectares (ha) in 1990.[20][6][21] In 2025, naturally regenerating forest covered 13,680 hectares (ha) and planted forest covered 37,380 hectares (ha). As of 2020, 100% of the forest area was reported to be under public ownership, managed by the Ministry of Rural Development.[20][6][21] The islands constitute the unique Cape Verde Islands dry forests ecoregion, according to the World Wildlife Fund.

Flora

Cape Verde is the driest archipelago of the ecoregion of Macaronesia. That with a greater influence of African species due to its geographical location near the African mainland of the Sahel. At first, the islands of Cabo Verde housed an extensive savanna and dry forest cover, but mostly it was removed to convert to agricultural land, which, together with the arid climate and rugged terrain, has led to a soil erosion and desertification widespread. However, the archipelago can be divided into four broad ecological zones (arid, semiarid, subhumid and humid), according to altitude and average annual rainfall ranging from less than 100 mm in the arid coastal areas to 300-500 mm in the humid mountainous regions.[22] Mostly rainfall precipitation is due to condensation of the ocean mist. Today much of the forest cover comprises relatively immature agroforestry plantations, in which are used non-native species such as Prosopis juliflora, Leucaena leucocephala and Jatropha curcas. Native dry forest species persist only in wet mountainous areas. Cape Verde hosts about 92 endemic plant species (14% of its total flora), including the marmulan (Sideroxylon mermulana), Canary Island dragon tree (Dracaena draco subsp. caboverdeana), and various succulents like Aeonium glanduliferum.[23] On the lower and drier islands the vegetation, before human colonization, consisted of savanna or steppe vegetation, with the flattest inland portion supporting semi-desert plants. At higher altitudes, a form of arid shrubland was also present. These islands were covered with savanna on the plains and arid shrubland on the mountainsides, but after over 500 years of human habitation (after Portuguese colonisation) nearly all the original vegetation has been cleared in favour of widespread agriculture including the grazing of goats, sheep and cattle and the planting of imported crop species. There are some remaining patches of dry forest high on steep mountainside including a number of endemic plant species, but these are inaccessible and hard to study. On the higher islands and somewhat wetter islands, exclusively in mountainous areas, like Santo Antao island, the climate is suitable for the development of dry monsoon forest, but most vegetation has now been converted to agriculture and forest fragments are now restricted to areas where cultivation is not possible, such as mountain peaks and steep slopes. The demand for wood has resulted in deforestation and desertification.

Fauna

There are five endemic bird species including the Raso lark along with more common swifts, larks, warblers, and sparrows.[24] The islands are an important breeding site for seabirds including the Cape Verde shearwater and Fea's petrel (Pterodroma feae), which breeds only here and in Madeira. Santiago Island holds the only breeding site of the endemic and endangered Bourne's heron.[25] The 23 endemic reptile species include a giant gecko (Tarentola gigas), and there are other geckos and skinks in abundance.[26] The giant skink (Chioninia coctei) is now thought to be extinct.

Threats and protection

Much of the natural environment has been degraded by conversion to agriculture, logging for firewood, overgrazing by livestock, and soil erosion, threatening endemic species of birds and reptiles. The remaining original dry forests are limited to high altitudes. Additional threats include illegal beach sand extraction, overfishing, and invasive introduced mammals such as cats and rats, which prey on nesting seabirds and sea turtles. Emerging issues as of 2025 include climate change impacts like rising sea levels, prolonged droughts, and marine debris, particularly plastics, affecting coastal ecosystems.[1][web:9][web:2] Cape Verde has established several protected areas, covering approximately 16% of its terrestrial land as of 2020, including national parks like Fogo Natural Park and Cabo de Santa Maria Marine Reserve, to conserve biodiversity and habitats.[6][web:25] Efforts also focus on climate adaptation, such as water resource management and reforestation programs through international partnerships.[web:7]
