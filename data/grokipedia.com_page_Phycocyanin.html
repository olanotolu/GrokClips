Phycocyanin

Phycocyanobilin Phycocyanin is a pigment-protein complex from the light-harvesting phycobiliprotein family, along with allophycocyanin and phycoerythrin.[1] The most common form is C-phycocyanin, primarily extracted from Spirulina platensis for commercial use. It is an accessory pigment to chlorophyll. All phycobiliproteins are water-soluble, so they cannot exist within the membrane like carotenoids can. Instead, phycobiliproteins aggregate to form clusters that adhere to the membrane called phycobilisomes. Phycocyanin is a characteristic light blue color, absorbing orange and red light, particularly 620 nm (depending on which specific type it is), and emits fluorescence at about 650 nm (also depending on which type it is). Allophycocyanin absorbs and emits at longer wavelengths than phycocyanin C or phycocyanin R. Phycocyanins are found in cyanobacteria (also called blue-green algae), red algae, and cryptophytes.[2] Phycobiliproteins have fluorescent properties that are used in immunoassay kits. Phycocyanin is from the Greek phyco meaning â€œalgaeâ€ and cyanin is from the English word â€œcyanâ€, which conventionally means a shade of blue-green (close to "aqua") and is derived from the Greek â€œkyanosâ€ which means a somewhat different color: "dark blue". The product phycocyanin, produced by Aphanizomenon flos-aquae and Spirulina, is for example used in the food and beverage industry as the natural coloring agent 'LINABLUEÂ®' or 'EXBERRYÂ® Shade Blue' and is found in sweets and ice cream.[3] [4] In addition, fluorescence detection of phycocyanin pigments in water samples is a useful method to monitor cyanobacteria biomass.[5] The phycobiliproteins are made of two subunits (alpha and beta) having a protein backbone to which 1â€“2 linear tetrapyrrole chromophores are covalently bound. C-phycocyanin is often found in cyanobacteria which thrive around hot springs, as it can be stable up to around 70 Â°C, with identical spectroscopic (light absorbing) behaviours at 20 and 70 Â°C. Thermophiles contain slightly different amino acid sequences making it stable under these higher conditions. Molecular weight is around 30,000 Da. Stability of this protein in vitro at these temperatures has been shown to be substantially lower.

Structure

Phycocyanin shares a common structural theme with all phycobiliproteins.[6] The structure begins with the assembly of phycobiliprotein monomers, which are heterodimers composed of Î± and Î² subunits, and their respective chromophores linked via thioether bond. Each subunit is typically composed of eight Î±-helices. Monomers spontaneously aggregate to form ring-shaped trimers (Î±Î²)3, which have rotational symmetry and a central channel. Trimers aggregate in pairs to form hexamers (Î±Î²)6, sometimes assisted with additional linker proteins. Each phycobilisome rod generally has two or more phycocyanin hexamers. Despite the overall similarity in structure and assembly of phycobiliproteins, there is a large diversity in hexamer and rod conformations, even when only considering phycocyanins. On a larger scale phycocyanins also vary in crystal structure, although the biological relevance of this is debatable. As an example, the structure of C-phycocyanin from Synechococcus vulcanus has been refined to 1.6 Angstrom resolution.[7] The (Î±Î²) monomer consists of 332 amino acids and 3 thio-linked phycocyanobilin (PCB) cofactor molecules. Both the Î±- and Î²-subunits have a PCB at amino acid 84, but the Î²-subunit has an additional PCB at position 155 as well. This additional PCB faces the exterior of the trimeric ring and is therefore implicated in inter-rod energy transfer in the phycobilisome complex. In addition to cofactors, there are many predictable non-covalent interactions with the surrounding solvent (water) that are hypothesized to contribute to structural stability. R-phycocyanin II (R-PC II) is found in some Synechococcus species.[8] R-PC II is said to be the first PEB containing phycocyanin that originates in cyanobacteria.[8] Its purified protein is composed of alpha and beta subunits in equal quantities.[8] R-PC II has PCB at beta-84 and the phycoerythrobilin (PEB) at alpha-84 and beta-155.[8] Numerous crystal structures of phycocyanin have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank. Recent cryo-EM studies have also determined structures of phycocyanin within intact phycobilisome complexes, providing insights into their native assembly.[[9]]

Spectral characteristics

C-phycocyanin has a single absorption peak at ~621 nm,[10][11] varying slightly depending on the organism and conditions such as temperature, pH, and protein concentration in vitro.[12][13] Its emission maximum is ~642 nm.[10][11] This means that the pigment absorbs orange light, and emits reddish light. R-phycocyanin has an absorption maxima at 533 and 554 nm.[8] The fluorescence emission maximum of R-phycocyanin is 646 nm.[8]

Ecological relevance

Phycocyanin is produced by many photoautotrophic cyanobacteria.[14] Phycocyanin has ecological significance in indicating cyanobacteria bloom. Normally chlorophyll a is used to indicate cyanobacteria numbers, however since it is present in a large number of phytoplankton groups, it is not an ideal measure.[15] For instance a study in the Baltic Sea used phycocyanin as a marker for filamentous cyanobacteria during toxic summer blooms.[15] Some filamentous organisms in the Baltic Sea include Nodularia spumigena and Aphanizomenon flos-aquae. Lichen-forming fungi and cyanobacteria often have a symbiotic relationship and thus phycocyanin markers can be used to show the ecological distribution of fungi-associated cyanobacteria. As shown in the highly specific association between Lichina species and Rivularia strains, phycocyanin has enough phylogenetic resolution to resolve the evolutionary history of the group across the northwestern Atlantic Ocean coastal margin.[16]

Biosynthesis

The two genes cpcA and cpcB, located in the cpc operon and translated from the same mRNA transcript, encode for the C-PC Î±- and Î²-chains respectively.[17] Additional elements such as linker proteins, and enzymes involved in phycobilin synthesis and the phycobiliproteins are often encoded by genes in adjacent gene clusters, and the cpc operon of Arthrospira platensis also encodes a linker protein assisting in the assembly of C-PC complexes.[18] In red algae, the phycobiliprotein and linker protein genes are located on the plastid genome.[19] Phycocyanobilin is synthesized from heme and inserted into the C-PC apo-protein by three enzymatic steps.[20] Cyclic heme is oxidised to linear biliverdin IXÎ± by heme oxygenase and further converted to 3Z-phycocyanobilin, the dominant phycocyanobilin isomer, by 3Z-phycocyanobilin:ferredoxin oxidoreductase. Insertion of 3Z-phycocyanobilin into the C-PC apo-protein via thioether bond formation is catalysed by phycocyanobilin lyase.[21] The promoter for the cpc operon is located within the 427-bp upstream region of the cpcB gene. In A. platensis, six putative promoter sequences have been identified in the region, with four of them showing expression of green fluorescent protein when transformed into E. coli.[22] The presence of other positive elements such as light-response elements in the same region have also been demonstrated.[23] The multiple promoter and response element sequences in the cpc operon enable cyanobacteria and red algae to adjust its expression in response to multiple environmental conditions. Expression of the cpcA and cpcB genes is regulated by light. Low light intensities stimulate synthesis of CPC and other pigments, while pigment synthesis is repressed at high light intensities.[24] Temperature has also been shown to affect synthesis, with specific pigment concentrations showing a clear maximum at 36 Â°C in Arthronema africanum, a cyanobacterium with particular high C-PC and APC contents.[25] Nitrogen and also iron limitation induce phycobiliprotein degradation. Organic carbon sources stimulate C-PC synthesis in Anabaena spp., but seem to have almost no negative effect in A. platensis.[26][27] In the rhodophytes Cyanidium caldarium and Galdieria sulphuraria , C-PC production is repressed by glucose; the biosynthetic pathway is induced by light and Î´-aminolevulinic acid (ALA).[28]

Biotechnology

Pure phycocyanin can be extracted and purified from algae. The basic procedure involves rupturing the cell wall using mechanical forces (such as freeze-thawing) or chemical agents (such as enzymes). C-PC is then isolated by centrifugation and purified using ammonium sulfate precipitation or chromatographyâ€”either ion-exchange or size-exclusion. The sample is subsequently freeze-dried.[29] Recombinant expression in heterologous hosts like Escherichia coli has enabled production of fluorescent phycocyanin subunits. Heterotrophic and mixotrophic cultivation of cyanobacteria enhances yield for industrial applications.[21]

Applications

Phycocyanin is used in various biotechnological applications, particularly in medicine and food. It can also serve as a fluorescent label in immunoassays due to its natural fluorescence.[30]

Medicine

Anti-oxidation and anti-inflammation
Phycocyanin has both antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.[31][32][33] Peroxyl, hydroxyl, and alkoxyl radicals are all scavenged by C-PC, with a greater effect on peroxyl radicals. C-PC acts as a metal-binding antioxidant, preventing lipid peroxidation.[34] The peroxyl radicals are stabilized by the chromophore (a subunit of C-PC). Scavenging of hydroxyl radicals requires low light and high C-PC concentrations.[36] As an antioxidant, C-PC scavenges these damage-inducing radicals at sites of inflammation, thereby acting as an anti-inflammatory agent.[34]
Neuroprotection
Excess oxygen in the brain generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), which damage neurons and lead to decreased neurological function. C-phycocyanin scavenges hydrogen peroxide, a type of ROS, from within astrocytes, reducing oxidative stress. C-PC also induces astrocytes to increase production of growth factors like BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and NGF (nerve growth factor), enhancing nerve regeneration. Additionally, C-PC prevents astrogliosis and glial inflammation.
Hepatoprotection
C-phycocyanin provides protection against hepatotoxicity.[31][39] Vadiraja et al. (1998) found that SGPT activity was restored to near control levels when C-PC was administered prior to hepatotoxins such as carbon tetrachloride (CCl4) or R-(+)-pulegone. C-PC protects the liver via the cytochrome P450 system.[39] It can reduce the production of menthofuran or the formation of Î±,Î²-unsaturated-Î³-ketoaldehyde, key components that generate reactive metabolites binding to liver tissues and causing toxicity. Another mechanism is scavenging of reactive metabolites (or free radicals in the case of CCl4).
Anti-cancer
C-phycocyanin (C-PC) exhibits anti-cancer effects. C-PC prevents uncontrolled cell growth.[40] It arrests the cell cycle in the G0/G1 phase, preventing entry into the S phase and DNA synthesis, thus inhibiting tumor proliferation.[41] Furthermore, C-PC induces apoptosis. Treatment with C-PC generates ROS, which decrease Bcl-2 (a regulator of apoptosis) production. Bcl-2 inhibits caspases, proteins in the apoptosis pathway. Reduced Bcl-2 leads to increased caspase expression, resulting in apoptosis.[42][41] C-PC alone is insufficient to treat cancer and must be combined with other drugs to overcome tumor cell persistence.

Food

C-phycocyanin (C-PC) can be used as a natural blue food coloring.[43] Due to its thermal instability, this colorant is suitable only for low-temperature prepared goods unless stabilized with preservatives or sugars.[43][44] The type of sugar has little impact; high sugar content stabilizes C-PC. Thus, C-PC is applicable to various foods, including syrups ranging from green to blue shades achieved by adding yellow colorings.
