2023 Huwara attacks

The 2023 Huwara attacks consisted of a violent rampage by hundreds of Israeli settlers through the Palestinian village of Huwara in the West Bank on 26 February 2023, triggered by the earlier murder of two Israeli brothers, Hallel Yaniv (21) and Yagel Yaniv (19), by Palestinian gunmen in the same area. [1] The settlers torched dozens of homes, vehicles, and storefronts while assaulting Palestinian residents, resulting in one Palestinian death and numerous injuries amid clashes with locals and Israeli security forces. [2] [3] The Israeli military, present during the events, later characterized the unrest as a "pogrom" and admitted to being unprepared for its intensity, with initial arrests leading to few sustained prosecutions. [4] The attacks unfolded hours after the Yaniv brothers, residents of the nearby Har Bracha settlement, were shot dead while driving through Huwara, an incident classified as terrorism by Israeli authorities. [1] In reprisal, approximately 400 settlers descended on the village, setting fires that caused extensive property damage and prompting evacuations, with the United States calling for accountability and compensation for victims. [5] [6] While the Israeli government condemned the violence, statements from some far-right ministers, including suggestions to "wipe out" Huwara, fueled controversy over incitement and the adequacy of responses to settler extremism. [4] The events underscored persistent security challenges in the West Bank, where Palestinian attacks on Israelis have escalated alongside instances of settler retaliation, contributing to a cycle of violence that drew scrutiny from both domestic and international observers regarding enforcement of law and protection of civilians. [7]

Background

Inciting Palestinian Terror Attack

On February 26, 2023, a Palestinian gunman opened fire on an Israeli vehicle traveling through Huwara in the West Bank, killing two brothers from the Har Bracha settlement: Hallel Yaniv, aged 21, and Yonal Yaniv, aged 16.[1][8] The attack occurred on Route 60, a major highway frequently targeted by Palestinian shooters, as the victims were driving home from central Israel.[1] The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) classified the incident as a terror attack, with the gunman fleeing the scene after the shooting.[1] The Yaniv brothers were unarmed civilians, and the assault exemplified a pattern of drive-by shootings against Israeli motorists in the area, with Huwara having seen multiple such incidents in preceding months.[1] No group immediately claimed responsibility for the attack, though Palestinian militant organizations have historically conducted similar operations without formal attribution.[9] Israeli security forces launched a manhunt for the perpetrator, but details on his identity or capture remain limited in public reports as of the event's immediate aftermath.[1] This shooting directly preceded the subsequent settler violence in Huwara later that evening, serving as the stated provocation for reprisals by Israeli residents from nearby settlements.[8][10] The U.S. Department of State later referenced the incident in its annual terrorism report as part of broader Palestinian terrorist activities in the West Bank during 2023.[9]

Historical Tensions and Patterns of Violence

Huwara, a Palestinian village in the Nablus Governorate of the West Bank with approximately 7,000 residents, occupies a strategically sensitive position along Route 60, the primary north-south artery linking central Israel to Jewish settlements in Samaria.[11] This location exposes it to routine interactionsâ€”and conflictsâ€”between Palestinian locals and Israeli travelers, including settlers from nearby outposts like Yitzhar, founded in 1983 on lands historically used by Palestinians for agriculture.[12][11] The settlement's Od Yosef Chai yeshiva has drawn scrutiny for ideologies perceived as inciting confrontations, contributing to a backdrop of mutual suspicion exacerbated by the broader Israeli-Palestinian territorial dispute since the 1967 Six-Day War.[11] Violence in the Huwara area follows a recurrent pattern, predominantly triggered by Palestinian assaults on Israeli vehicles and security forces along the bypass road and checkpoint, which serve as chokepoints for settlers commuting to Nablus and beyond. Rock-throwing attacks, a staple tactic, have inflicted casualties repeatedly; on May 25, 2022, Palestinians hurled stones at a car near Huwara, injuring the son and two grandchildren of Israeli Knesset member Bezalel Smotrich.[13] Further incidents include an April 7, 2025, barrage damaging two Israeli vehicles and lightly wounding a passenger, and a March 10, 2025, attack injuring a Jewish infant.[14][15] These ambushes, often involving coordinated groups, have prompted Israeli defenses like a planned security fence along the route in 2024 to mitigate risks.[16] More lethal Palestinian operations, such as the February 26, 2023, shooting that killed two Israeli brothersâ€”Yagel and Hallel Yanivâ€”near Huwara, illustrate the area's role as a hotspot for terrorism, with U.S. State Department data recording 23 Israeli fatalities from West Bank terror attacks in 2023 prior to October 7.[17][18] Settler responses typically manifest as reprisals targeting Palestinian property rather than systematic lethality, though documented cases include arson, vandalism, and assaults following terror incidents. Israeli watchdog Yesh Din reported 2023 as the peak year for such settler actions West Bank-wide, with over 1,200 incidents, many clustered around Nablus-area villages like Huwara amid rising Palestinian violence.[19] Pre-October 7, 2023, fatalities skewed heavily toward Israelis from Palestinian attacks (13 killed) versus Palestinians from settler or security operations (63, largely in IDF counter-terror raids), highlighting the causal chain where roadside terrorism drives retaliatory cycles.[20] This dynamic, rooted in territorial competition and security asymmetries, perpetuates de facto vigilantism on both sides, with limited prosecutionsâ€”settler impunity rates exceeding 90% per some NGOs, while Palestinian perpetrators often evade capture amid community support.[21]

The Settler Attacks

Timeline and Execution

The settler attacks in Huwara commenced on the evening of February 26, 2023, immediately following a Palestinian gunman's fatal shooting of two Israeli brothers, Hallel and Yagel Yaniv, while they drove through the town earlier that day.[22] Hundreds of settlers, primarily from nearby Jewish communities such as Yitzhar, mobilized via WhatsApp groups and converged on Huwara's main street around 6:00 PM local time.[23] The rampage involved coordinated groups chanting slogans and advancing through the town, initiating widespread arson and vandalism.[23] Between 6:00 PM and 6:30 PM, attackers set fire to tires, trash cans, wooden pallets, and multiple vehicles parked near Palestinian homes and businesses, with flames quickly spreading to adjacent structures.[23] Specific incidents included settlers igniting the door of a residence at 6:27 PM, as captured on CCTV footage, and torching a car dealership shortly thereafter.[23] Methods employed encompassed arson using flammable materials, stone-throwing at residents and properties, physical assaults with clubs and improvised weapons, and sporadic live gunfire.[23] [24] The violence escalated into the night, with settlers blocking access routes and preventing Palestinian residents from fleeing or defending their properties; one family reported being trapped inside their burning home for over two hours.[23] By 8:25 PM, attackers had obstructed emergency fire trucks at the Huwara roundabout, pelting them with stones and delaying response efforts.[23] The rampage extended to surrounding villages, involving similar tactics of fire-setting and pursuits of locals, persisting for several hours until partial dispersal in the early morning of February 27.[22] During the assaults, at least one Palestinian, Sameh Aqtash, was killed by gunfire near Za'tara junction adjacent to Huwara.[22][25]

Scope and Methods

The 2023 Huwara attacks by Israeli settlers primarily targeted the Palestinian town of Huwara, located south of Nablus in the West Bank, along with adjacent villages such as Burin and Zaatara, following a Palestinian shooting earlier that day on February 26 that killed two Israeli settlers.[4][23] The rampage involved hundreds of settlers emerging from nearby outposts and settlements, conducting coordinated assaults that extended over several hours from late evening into the early morning of February 27.[10][26] This scale marked one of the largest instances of settler-initiated violence in the area in recent years, with participants blocking roads and overwhelming local Palestinian defenses.[27] Settlers executed the attacks through arson, igniting fires in over 100 vehicles, multiple homes, and agricultural fields using flammable materials, which caused widespread property destruction visible in charred remains across the town.[27][26] Physical assaults involved throwing stones and rocks at Palestinian residents and their properties, alongside direct beatings that injured dozens, while groups systematically vandalized shops and infrastructure to maximize disruption.[25][28] The tactics emphasized mob dynamics, with attackers operating in large, unprotected groups that exploited limited initial intervention by Israeli security forces present in the area.[23][29]

Casualties and Immediate Damage

Human Casualties

One Palestinian, Sameh Aqtash, a 37-year-old blacksmith and resident of the nearby village of Za'tara, was killed by gunfire during the settler rampage on February 26, 2023.[30] [10] Aqtash was shot in the stomach while reportedly attempting to close a village gate amid the violence; his relatives alleged the fatal shot came from an IDF soldier, though the circumstances remain disputed pending investigation outcomes.[30] [31] Palestinian medics treated numerous injuries sustained by residents of Huwara and adjacent areas, with reports varying by source: the Palestinian Health Ministry and Red Crescent documented over 100 injuries from beatings, stone-throwing, tear gas exposure, and smoke inhalation, while some accounts from local officials cited figures exceeding 300 affected individuals.[32] [25] [33] Most injuries were non-fatal and treated on-site or at nearby clinics, including cases of fractures, bruises, and respiratory issues; a smaller number involved gunshot wounds or severe trauma requiring hospitalization.[20] [23] No Israeli casualties were reported directly attributable to Palestinian counteractions during the settler violence in Huwara that evening.[24]

Property and Infrastructure Damage

During the settler rampage on the night of February 26, 2023, in Huwara and adjacent Palestinian villages, attackers engaged in extensive arson and vandalism targeting private property. More than 100 vehicles were torched or severely damaged, with reports documenting dozens of cars and trucks reduced to charred husks along roadsides and in parking areas.[34] [35] Seventy-five houses sustained vandalism or fire damage, including interiors gutted by flames, shattered windows, and structural compromise from thrown stones and incendiary devices. Businesses and outbuildings in Huwara faced similar destruction, with shopfronts smashed and goods looted or burned.[34] [26] The overall economic impact of the property destruction was estimated at approximately $5 million USD, encompassing repair costs for vehicles, homes, and lost business inventory. Vegetation along roadsides, including olive trees, was also set ablaze, contributing to localized environmental damage.[34] Infrastructure damage remained limited, with no major disruptions to utilities or roadways reported beyond temporary blockages cleared post-incident; however, the scale of private property losses underscored the intensity of the coordinated assault.[29][4]

Security and Governmental Response

IDF Presence and Actions

The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) maintained a presence in Huwara on the night of February 26, 2023, following the Palestinian shooting attack earlier that evening, with troops stationed primarily to secure roads and respond to potential unrest. However, an internal IDF review later determined that insufficient forcesâ€”approximately a company-sized unitâ€”were deployed to the area, underestimating the scale of the anticipated settler response and failing to reinforce positions promptly.[36][37] During the rampage, which involved hundreds of settlers, IDF soldiers attempted limited dispersal efforts using non-lethal means but did not halt the violence effectively, with eyewitness accounts and soldier testimony indicating troops stood by as arson and assaults continued, prioritizing the protection of Israeli vehicles over Palestinian residents. One Palestinian, Eyad al-Rimawi, was killed amid clashes in the town, with his relatives attributing the fatal shooting to IDF gunfire, though the military did not publicly confirm the shooter and described the incident as occurring during riot suppression.[37][30] In the immediate aftermath, the IDF condemned the settler actions as "acts of terror" and a "pogrom," with Central Command chief Maj. Gen. Yehuda Fuchs stating that extremists were "sowing terror" and warning of risks to Israeli lives in future clashes. IDF Chief of Staff Herzi Halevi acknowledged operational mismanagement in the riots but declined disciplinary measures against commanders, emphasizing instead a commitment to investigations and prevention of recurrence. The military's response highlighted systemic challenges in balancing security against Palestinian terrorism with containment of settler vigilantism in the West Bank.[38][4][36]

Initial Political Statements

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's office released a statement on the night of February 26, 2023, announcing that the government was working to restore calm following the settler violence in Huwara and urging restraint to prevent vigilante actions.[17] Netanyahu personally condemned the rampage in a video address on February 27, asserting that such violence against innocents would not be tolerated and that law enforcement would act decisively.[20] Defense Minister Yoav Gallant visited Huwara on February 27 and warned of "difficult days ahead," directing security forces to prioritize capturing the initial Palestinian gunman while maintaining order amid heightened tensions.[39] He emphasized operational focus on preventing further escalation without explicitly labeling the settler actions at the time.[1] National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir, on February 27, opposed settlers taking the law into their own hands during the rampage but expressed understanding of their frustration after the killing of two Israelis earlier that day, vowing to "crush enemies" through enhanced security and Jewish presence in the area.[40] [41] Palestinian Authority Civil Affairs Minister Hussein al-Sheikh tweeted on February 27 describing the settler attacks as an "unprecedented pogrom" against Palestinians, calling for international intervention to halt the violence.[2] PA officials, including President Mahmoud Abbas, condemned the rampage as state-backed aggression, reporting over 350 injuries and one death, and demanded accountability from Israeli authorities.[20]

Palestinian Reactions

The Palestinian Authority (PA), led by President Mahmoud Abbas, condemned the settler rampage in Huwara on February 26, 2023, with Abbas describing it as unacceptable and emphasizing that such violence was "not our way."[2] The PA's statement aligned with broader diplomatic efforts, as Israeli and Palestinian security officials had met earlier that day in Jordan to discuss de-escalation measures aimed at curbing West Bank violence, though the riots undermined these talks.[47] Hamas, the Islamist militant group governing Gaza, issued a strong denunciation, with a political bureau official labeling the attacks a "declaration of war" that "crossed all red lines" and warning of potential escalation.[48] This response from Hamas, which had praised the initial Palestinian shooting that killed two Israeli settlers earlier on February 26, framed the Huwara violence as justification for further resistance while condemning the retaliatory scale.[48] Public sentiment among Palestinians shifted toward greater militancy following the Huwara events, as evidenced by an Arab Barometer survey conducted in early March 2023, which found a rise in support for armed struggleâ€”from 54% in December 2022 to 63%â€”explicitly linked to recent settler attacks in Huwara and northern West Bank areas.[49] Residents of Huwara reported heightened fears of daily activities, contributing to a sense of vulnerability that fueled calls for protection and retaliation, though no large-scale organized protests were documented immediately after the rampage.[50]

International Reactions

The United States government strongly condemned the settler violence in Huwara, with State Department spokesman Ned Price stating on February 27, 2023, that the Biden administration expected Israel to prosecute the perpetrators and to pursue them with "equal rigor" as it did Palestinian attackers.[51][52] On March 1, 2023, the State Department described remarks by Israeli Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrichâ€”suggesting Huwara should be "erased"â€”as "repugnant" and intolerable incitement to violence, emphasizing that such statements undermined Israel's security.[53] The European Union expressed alarm over the February 26, 2023, events, stating that authorities on all sides must intervene to halt the "endless cycle of violence" and holding the Israeli government responsible for protecting Palestinian civilians.[10][17] EU Special Representative for the Middle East Peace Process Sven Koopmans demanded accountability for the rampage on March 3, 2023, calling for perpetrators to be brought to justice amid a pattern of settler impunity.[54] An EU delegation visited Huwara shortly after, advocating for compensation to affected Palestinians and underscoring the need to address escalating West Bank tensions.[55] United Nations officials voiced grave concern over the destruction and casualties, with UN Middle East envoy Tor Wennesland highlighting on February 27, 2023, the urgent need to break cycles of retaliation following the preceding Palestinian attack that killed two Israeli brothers.[56] The UN Security Council convened an emergency session on February 28, 2023, to address the weekend's violence, including Huwara, amid broader West Bank escalations since Israel's new government formed.[57] UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Volker TÃ¼rk criticized Smotrich's comments on March 3, 2023, as irresponsible incitement exacerbating the volatile situation.[58]

Investigations and Legal Outcomes

Israeli Investigations

The Israel Police, in coordination with the Shin Bet (Israel Security Agency), opened a criminal investigation into the settler violence in Huwara on February 26, 2023, focusing on acts of arson, assault, and property damage committed by Israeli civilians.[27] The Shin Bet, tasked with countering Jewish extremism and terrorism, played a key role in identifying suspects through intelligence gathering, video footage analysis, and witness interrogations, leading to initial detentions of individuals suspected of participating in the rampage.[27] By early March 2023, police had detained at least eight suspects, including minors, with some held under administrative detention orders signed by Defense Minister Yoav Gallant to prevent further violence.[59] Separately, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) conducted an internal military inquiry into the performance of its troops during the incident, examining why soldiers present in Huwara failed to intervene effectively despite the scale of the unrest.[60] The probe, overseen by Central Command under Maj. Gen. Yehuda Fuchsâ€”who publicly described the events as a "pogrom" against Palestiniansâ€”revealed operational shortcomings, including delayed reinforcements and inadequate dispersal of rioters, with findings submitted to IDF Chief of Staff Lt. Gen. Herzi Halevi by early March 2023.[4][60] This review highlighted tensions between security forces' mandates to protect both Israeli civilians and Palestinian residents, amid claims of settler coordination with or obstruction of military units.[37] Investigations faced evidentiary challenges, such as reliance on security camera footage and Palestinian witness statements, which critics from settler advocacy groups contested as biased, while Palestinian sources alleged insufficient follow-through by authorities.[19] Attorney General Gali Baharav-Miara authorized probes into related incitement by public figures, including a police inquiry into far-right MK Zvika Fogel's statements endorsing the destruction of Huwara, though such cases often stalled due to free speech considerations.[61] Overall, the dual-track inquiries by civilian law enforcement and the military underscored Israel's institutional framework for addressing intra-Jewish violence in the West Bank, though data from monitoring groups indicated historically low indictment rates for similar incidents, attributed to prosecutorial discretion and jurisdictional complexities.[19]

Arrests, Charges, and Trials

Israeli security forces arrested approximately eight Jewish settlers in the immediate aftermath of the February 26, 2023, rampage in Huwara, though reports varied slightly with some indicating up to 17 suspects detained in the following days.[62][63] Most were released shortly thereafter without charges, reflecting a pattern where investigations into alleged settler violence often conclude without indictments.[63] On March 2, 2023, two suspectsâ€”29-year-old David Chai Chasdai from Yitzhar and an unnamed 17-year-old minorâ€”were placed under administrative detention for four months due to suspected involvement in the violence, a measure typically used for security threats without criminal charges.[59] The minor's detention was shortened by one month on March 6, 2023, following a court review.[64] In a related incident on March 6, 2023, during heightened tensions in Huwara, two settlers were indicted on March 30, 2023, for a terrorist assault involving an axe and rocks against Palestinians, shouting "Death to Arabs" and causing grievous bodily harm to two victims, alongside vandalism charges.[28] Hanoch Akiva Rabin, 26, and Raz Chaim Giron, 22, were convicted of racially motivated assault and sentenced on June 19, 2024, to 3.5 years and 3 years in prison, respectively.[65] No further indictments or convictions directly tied to the core February 26 rampage were reported as of late 2023, with analyses noting Israel's broader failure to prosecute participants despite the scale of the violence, consistent with historical data showing over 90% of settler violence investigations closed without indictment from 2005 to 2022.[63][28]

Controversies and Debates

Allegations of Official Complicity

Critics, including human rights organizations and Palestinian residents, alleged that the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) demonstrated complicity through operational failures during the February 26, 2023, settler rampage in Huwara, despite the presence of dozens of soldiers and armored vehicles on site. A CNN investigation revealed that IDF forces, while patrolling the area, did not effectively repel advancing settlers or protect Palestinian homes and businesses from arson, allowing the violence to escalate unchecked for hours; soldiers reportedly fired tear gas and stun grenades primarily at Palestinians throwing rocks in self-defense, rather than at the rioters.[23] An IDF internal inquiry later acknowledged insufficient troop deployment and lack of preparedness for potential revenge attacks, with Chief of Staff Lt. Gen. Herzi Halevi describing the events as a "severe incident" that "should not have happened," though no disciplinary actions were imposed on personnel.[23] [36] Further allegations pointed to prior knowledge of planned violence, as far-right settler groups announced intentions to target Huwara via WhatsApp and Telegram channels, including groups monitored by two Israeli Knesset members who attended related events, yet the IDF made no preemptive arrests or reinforcements.[66] Soldier testimonies, including one cited in investigations, claimed forces "allowed the settlers to advance" while the village burned, and initial arrests of settlers were released without charges, fueling claims of preferential treatment.[66] [23] These operational lapses were attributed by detractors to a broader pattern of IDF tolerance for settler vigilantism in the West Bank, where soldiers have been accused in some reports of aiding or witnessing attacks without intervention, though such claims remain contested and unproven for Huwara specifically.[18] On the political level, allegations extended to senior government figures enabling the violence through inflammatory rhetoric that signaled impunity. Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich stated on March 1, 2023, that the State of Israel should "wipe out" Huwara in response to the settler attacks, a comment U.S. officials labeled as "incitement to violence" and "repugnant," though Smotrich later apologized, clarifying he meant state-led security operations by the IDF, not civilian actions.[67] [68] Critics argued such statements from coalition partners emboldened extremists, contrasting with Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's condemnation of the riots as "unacceptable" and orders for additional forces, while highlighting a perceived reluctance to curb far-right ministers' influence.[67] Israeli officials rejected complicity claims, emphasizing the IDF's efforts to separate combatants and subsequent self-investigation, but Palestinian authorities and NGOs maintained that systemic biases in enforcement perpetuated settler impunity.[23]

Questions of Proportionality and Retaliation

The settlers' rampage in Huwara on February 26, 2023, immediately following the terrorist shooting death of two Israeli brothers, Hallel and Yosef-Elie Ban Joel, aged 21 and 9, by a gunman who fled into the village, ignited debates over the proportionality of retaliatory violence. The initial attack targeted specific Israeli civilians traveling on a public road, killing two and wounding others, while the subsequent settler actions involved widespread arson and assaults affecting Palestinian property and residents indiscriminately. Approximately 100 vehicles were torched, dozens of homes and businesses damaged or destroyed with estimated losses around 5 million NIS (about $1.4 million USD at the time), and over 400 Palestinians injured, primarily from tear gas, rubber bullets, and physical confrontations, with one civilian, Sameh Aqtash, killed by gunfire whose source remains disputed between settlers and Israeli forces.[29][69] Critics, including Israeli Defense Minister Yoav Gallant and international organizations, condemned the response as a "pogrom" and disproportionate collective punishment, arguing that targeting an entire village of roughly 7,000 for the acts of one perpetrator violated principles of proportionality under international humanitarian law, which prohibits reprisals against civilians even in retaliation for terrorism. Palestinian officials and human rights groups like B'Tselem described it as state-tolerated vigilantism, noting the Israeli military's presence without effective intervention to halt the violence, exacerbating perceptions of impunity. The scaleâ€”one death and targeted attack versus property destruction and mass injuriesâ€”was cited as evidence of imbalance, with comparisons drawn to historical pogroms despite the absence of mass killings.[20][23] Proponents of the settlers' actions, primarily from Israel's right-wing spectrum, framed the retaliation as a necessary deterrent against Huwara's documented history as a hub for anti-Israeli violence, including frequent stone-throwing incidents and sheltering terrorists, with data from Israeli security sources indicating the village's role in multiple prior attacks along the Nablus road. National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir initially defended the unrest as an understandable outburst of grief and anger, suggesting condemnation of settlers while Palestinian attackers face no repercussions perpetuates asymmetry. Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich advocated for the state to "erase" Huwara as a terror nestâ€”clarifying later this meant security operations, not vigilante destructionâ€”arguing that weak responses to repeated aggression from the area invite further bloodshed, though he apologized for wording implying military orders. Some settlers and commentators invoked "price tag" tactics, where property damage aims to impose costs on communities supporting terrorism, positing that proportionality should account for cumulative threats rather than isolated incidents.[70][71][72] These perspectives highlight tensions between immediate tactical proportionalityâ€”balancing harm in the momentâ€”and strategic deterrence in asymmetric conflicts, where empirical patterns of West Bank violence suggest villages like Huwara continue facilitating attacks despite such events. Israeli military probes later deemed the failure to contain the rampage a severe lapse, but no conclusive evidence emerged that the violence significantly reduced local terrorism, as attacks persisted in the region. The episode underscored broader questions of whether non-state retaliation can ever be proportionate absent state monopoly on force, with legal outcomes focusing on individual culpability rather than systemic justification.[23][9]

Incitement from Both Sides

The terror attack on February 26, 2023, that killed Israeli brothers Hallel and Yigal Yaniv near Huwara was claimed by Hamas, reflecting ongoing incitement within Palestinian militant groups that praise such operations as resistance against Israeli presence in the West Bank.[24][47] A poll conducted shortly after indicated that 71% of Palestinians supported the shooting, underscoring widespread societal endorsement fueled by decades of PA and Fatah rhetoric glorifying "martyrs" and violence against Israelis, including through school curricula, media broadcasts, and public naming of institutions after past attackers.[73] The Palestinian Authority's "pay-for-slay" policy, which allocates millions annually to families of terrorists based on the severity of attacks and imprisonment duration, further incentivizes such acts by providing financial rewards equivalent to salaries, a practice unchanged despite international criticism and applicable to perpetrators of West Bank shootings like this one.[74] On the Israeli side, statements from far-right officials following the attack contributed to heightened tensions. Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich remarked that Huwara, described by him as a "terrorist nest," should be "erased," prompting U.S. condemnation as incitement to violence, though Smotrich later clarified he meant state-led action against terror infrastructure rather than vigilante destruction.[68][75] Otzma Yehudit MK Zvika Fogel faced a police probe for incitement after posting that Huwara "needs to be burned" in response to the killings, echoing calls for collective punishment that aligned with sentiments among some settlers amid a wave of over 1,200 West Bank terror incidents in 2022-2023.[61][76] These remarks, from coalition members with histories of extremism, were criticized domestically and abroad for risking escalation, though they occurred after the initial Palestinian attack and amid frustration over inadequate security responses to rising fatalitiesâ€”13 Israelis killed in West Bank attacks by early 2023.[20]

Broader Context and Implications

Patterns of Palestinian Terrorism

Palestinian terrorism in the West Bank against Israeli civilians and security forces has followed consistent patterns of asymmetric, opportunistic violence, primarily employing readily available weapons to target vulnerable individuals on roads, at checkpoints, or in settlements. Drive-by shootings, where gunmen fire from passing vehicles or vantage points at Israeli cars, represent a hallmark tactic, enabling attackers to strike and flee with reduced risk of capture. This method exploits the interspersed geography of Israeli roads amid Palestinian population centers, as seen in the February 26, 2023, ambush near Huwara that killed brothers Hallel and Yagel Yaniv, residents of the Har Bracha settlement, while they drove through the area.[8][77] Similar incidents have recurred frequently, contributing to heightened Israeli travel precautions on routes like Highway 60.[78] Stabbing attacks, often impulsive and executed by lone actors in urban or mixed areas, constitute another prevalent pattern, peaking during waves of incited violence such as the 2015-2016 surge dubbed the "Knife Intifada," where perpetrators wielded knives, axes, or screwdrivers against unsuspecting victims. These close-quarters assaults, numbering in the hundreds during peak periods, frequently target civilians at bus stops or shopping centers, with attackers motivated by promises of martyrdom payments from the Palestinian Authority's "martyr fund." Vehicle rammings, involving cars or tractors plowing into pedestrians or vehicles, and improvised explosive devices along roadways further diversify the arsenal, though shootings and stabbings accounted for the majority of pre-October 2023 fatalities in the West Bank.[78] Data from Israel's Shin Bet intelligence agency and analyses by security think tanks reveal an escalation in these tactics during 2022-2023, with over 1,000 plots thwarted in the West Bank and Jerusalem in subsequent years, reflecting sustained intent amid operational disruptions. In 2023 prior to the October 7 attacks, Palestinian terrorists killed at least 15 Israelis in the West Bank through such means, injuring hundreds more, often in uncoordinated but ideologically aligned operations inspired by Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad cells like the Lions' Den in Nablus, or unaffiliated individuals. Incitement via social media, sermons, and PA glorification of attackers sustains this cycle, with groups framing violence as legitimate resistance despite international designations of these acts as terrorism.[79][80][81]

Trends in West Bank Violence Post-2023

Following the February 2023 Huwara attacks, Palestinian terrorist activity in the West Bank continued to escalate through 2023, with 414 significant attacks recorded that year, many involving shootings, stabbings, and vehicular assaults targeting Israeli civilians and security personnel.[79] This wave contributed to heightened Israeli security measures, including raids to dismantle terror cells affiliated with Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad. The October 7, 2023, Hamas-led assault from Gaza further intensified West Bank dynamics, prompting a surge in attempted Palestinian attacks, resulting in 64 Israeli deaths from West Bank-based terrorism since that date.[82] Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) operations expanded significantly in response, targeting terror infrastructure in areas like Jenin and Tulkarm, where militant networks had proliferated. By mid-2025, these efforts correlated with a marked decline in monthly terror attacks, reaching the lowest levels in five years despite persistent incidents, such as shootings wounding soldiers. Over 700 Palestinians were reported killed by Israeli forces in the West Bank since October 2023, with IDF statements attributing most to confirmed terrorists during exchanges of fire or explosive device detonations, though independent verification remains contested.[83] Israeli settler violence against Palestinians also rose, with UN-monitored incidents increasing from 1,189 in 2023 to 1,420 in 2024, often involving property damage, assaults, and access restrictions in over 80 affected villagesâ€”double the prior average. Such acts frequently followed Palestinian attacks on settlements, framing them as retaliatory by perpetrators, though Israeli authorities condemned and investigated many as unauthorized vigilantism. By early 2025, this contributed to the displacement of over 3,200 Palestinians since October 2023, exacerbating tensions amid ongoing settlement expansion.[84][85]
