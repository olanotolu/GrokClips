List of countries and territories where Spanish is an official language

The countries and territories where Spanish functions as an official language consist of 20 sovereign states—primarily in Latin America, along with Spain and Equatorial Guinea—and several dependent territories such as Puerto Rico.[1][2] This designation reflects the language's establishment through Spanish colonial administration from the late 15th century onward, which imposed Castilian Spanish as the administrative and legal medium across vast regions of the Americas and parts of Africa.[3] These jurisdictions encompass a combined population exceeding 500 million, with Spanish serving de jure in national constitutions or statutes in most cases, though de facto usage prevails in others like Mexico.[4] The list highlights the linguistic unity binding former colonies despite political independence, facilitating regional integration efforts such as those under the Organization of Ibero-American States, while also underscoring variations in co-official status with indigenous or regional languages in nations like Bolivia and Peru.[5] Notable among these is Spain, the language's origin, where it remains the primary official tongue amid regional autonomies for Catalan, Basque, and Galician; Equatorial Guinea stands out as the sole African entry, resulting from Spanish colonial rule until 1968, though French and Portuguese hold equal status there.[1] Territories like Puerto Rico, under U.S. sovereignty, maintain Spanish alongside English in official capacities, illustrating ongoing geopolitical ties to imperial legacies.[6] This configuration positions Spanish as a key vector for cultural and economic exchange across hemispheres, with no major disputes over official status in recent decades beyond decolonization-era transitions.

Sovereign States with De Jure Official Status

States Where Spanish is the Sole or Primary Official Language

Seventeen sovereign states recognize Spanish as their sole or primary official language, encompassing Spain and the majority of former Spanish colonies in the Americas. In these nations, Spanish is constitutionally or de facto established as the principal medium for national governance, judiciary proceedings, public administration, and primary education, with indigenous or regional languages typically accorded only local or supplementary recognition rather than equal national status.[1] [7] This status reflects the historical legacy of Spanish colonization and the practical dominance of Spanish in unifying diverse populations, where it is spoken natively by over 90% of residents in most cases.[8] The states, listed alphabetically, are as follows:
  • Argentina: Spanish serves as the de facto official language, used exclusively in federal institutions despite the absence of an explicit constitutional declaration.[1]
  • Chile: The 1980 Constitution designates Spanish as the official language, with no co-official languages at the national level.[1]
  • Colombia: Spanish is the official language per the 1991 Constitution, predominant in all public spheres.[1]
  • Costa Rica: Spanish is the sole official language under the 1949 Constitution.[1]
  • Cuba: Spanish is the official language, as affirmed in the 2019 Constitution, with uniform application across the island.[1]
  • Dominican Republic: Spanish is the sole official language, enshrined in the 2015 Constitution.[9][1]
  • Ecuador: The 2008 Constitution establishes Spanish as the official language, alongside recognition of indigenous languages in intercultural contexts but without national parity.[1]
  • El Salvador: Spanish is the only official language per the 1983 Constitution.[1]
  • Guatemala: Spanish is the official language under the 1985 Constitution, with Mayan languages holding co-official status only in indigenous municipalities.[1]
  • Honduras: The 1982 Constitution names Spanish as the official language.[1]
  • Mexico: Spanish functions as the de facto sole official language for federal purposes, despite the 1917 Constitution's silence on the matter, serving over 98% of the population linguistically.[10][1]
  • Nicaragua: Spanish is the official language according to the 1987 Constitution, predominant amid regional indigenous usage.[1]
  • Panama: The 1972 Constitution declares Spanish the official language.[1]
  • Peru: Spanish is the national official language per the 1993 Constitution, with Quechua, Aymara, and other indigenous languages co-official only in areas of prevalence, ensuring Spanish's primacy in unified state functions.[11][1]
  • Spain: The 1978 Constitution designates Castilian Spanish as the official state language, mandatory for understanding across the nation, while permitting co-official regional languages (e.g., Catalan, Basque) solely within autonomous communities.[7]
  • Uruguay: Spanish is the de facto and sole official language, with no constitutional alternatives.[1]
  • Venezuela: The 1999 Constitution establishes Spanish as the official language.[1]
These designations promote administrative coherence in multilingual societies, though de facto usage can vary in rural or indigenous areas where Spanish proficiency is near-universal but supplemented by local tongues.[8]

States Where Spanish is a Co-Official Language

In Bolivia, the 2009 Constitution designates Spanish as one of the official languages of the state alongside 36 indigenous languages, including Aymara, Quechua, and Guaraní, granting them equal legal status for public administration, education, and legislation.[12] This plurilingual framework reflects the country's indigenous demographic, where approximately 41% of the population identifies as indigenous, though Spanish remains the most widely spoken language, used by over 80% of Bolivians.[13] Paraguay's 1992 Constitution establishes Spanish and Guaraní as the nation's two official languages, mandating their use in government, courts, and public signage, with Guaraní serving as a symbol of national identity spoken fluently by about 90% of the population.[14] This bilingual policy, unique in South America for the vitality of an indigenous language, emerged from historical Jesuit missions and post-independence efforts to integrate rural Guarani-speaking majorities, though Spanish dominates formal and urban contexts.[15] Equatorial Guinea recognizes Spanish, French, and Portuguese as official languages under its 1991 Constitution (amended to include Portuguese in 2010), with Spanish functioning as the primary vehicular language in education and media despite French's growing administrative role in the Francophone-dominated region. This trilingual status stems from colonial legacies—Spanish from its time as Spanish Guinea until 1968 independence—and membership in international bodies like the CPLP and OIF, though actual proficiency in all three remains limited, with Spanish spoken by around 85% of the 1.7 million population.[16]

Non-Sovereign Territories and Dependencies with De Jure Official Status

Territories Where Spanish is the Sole or Primary Official Language

Puerto Rico, formally known as the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, is an unincorporated territory of the United States situated in the northeastern Caribbean Sea. It functions as a non-sovereign entity under U.S. sovereignty, with its residents holding U.S. citizenship but lacking full voting representation in the U.S. Congress.[17] Spanish and English are both designated as official languages of the Government of Puerto Rico pursuant to Law No. 1 of January 28, 1993, also known as the Puerto Rico Official Languages Act, which stipulates that both may be used interchangeably across government departments and proceedings.[18][19] Despite this bilingual legal framework, Spanish serves as the primary language in governmental operations, public education, judicial proceedings, and everyday communication among the island's approximately 3.2 million inhabitants.[20] This predominance of Spanish reflects the territory's historical ties to Spain, dating back to Christopher Columbus's arrival in 1493, and its cultural continuity following the U.S. acquisition in 1898 via the Treaty of Paris. While English is mandated for federal interactions and is taught in schools, surveys indicate that over 95% of Puerto Ricans primarily speak Spanish at home, underscoring its de facto primary status.[21][22] No other non-sovereign territories maintain Spanish as a sole or primary official language under de jure recognition.

Territories Where Spanish is a Co-Official Language

Puerto Rico, an unincorporated territory of the United States located in the northeastern Caribbean, designates both Spanish and English as official languages under its local government framework. This co-official status is codified in the Laws of Puerto Rico Annotated, Title 1, Section 5, which explicitly establishes Spanish and English as the official languages for governmental proceedings and communications.[23] The policy reflects the territory's historical linguistic heritage from Spanish colonial rule, combined with American influence following the 1898 annexation, though Spanish predominates in daily life, education, and media.[24] Despite the bilingual mandate, Spanish is the primary language for approximately 95% of Puerto Rico's 3.2 million residents, with English proficiency varying widely—around 50% report conversational ability, but fluency is lower outside urban and tourist areas.[22] Federal U.S. matters require English, reinforcing its role, yet local laws and the 1952 Constitution emphasize Spanish in public administration to preserve cultural identity. No other non-sovereign territories formally recognize Spanish as a co-official language alongside another tongue in de jure terms.[25]

Entities with De Facto Official or Predominant Use

Sovereign or Partially Recognized States

Mexico serves as a sovereign state where Spanish operates as the de facto national language, spoken natively by approximately 92% of its 130 million inhabitants and employed exclusively in federal government, legislation, education, and media. The 1917 Constitution, as amended, contains no provision designating an official language, rendering Spanish's dominance practical rather than legally enshrined, though indigenous languages hold co-official status in specific regions per Article 4.[4][26] Chile exemplifies another sovereign nation with Spanish as the de facto official and administrative language, utilized by 99.3% of its 19.5 million residents in all state institutions without constitutional mandate. The 1980 Constitution, including subsequent reforms, omits any language policy, allowing Spanish's unchallenged role in governance, courts, and public administration amid a monolingual context for official purposes. The Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (SADR), partially recognized by 46 United Nations member states as of 2024 and controlling roughly 20-25% of disputed Western Sahara, incorporates Spanish de facto in official broadcasts, diplomacy, and education alongside Arabic, which is the sole de jure official language per its constitution. This usage traces to the territory's status as Spanish Sahara until 1975, with Spanish facilitating communication among the Polisario Front's leadership and in refugee camps near Tindouf, Algeria, though Hassaniya Arabic predominates among the estimated 500,000 Sahrawis.[27]

Non-Sovereign Regions

In the United States, the state of New Mexico exemplifies a non-sovereign region with de facto official use of Spanish alongside English, stemming from historical constitutional provisions and legislative mandates rather than a singular de jure designation of an official language. The state's 1911 constitution required bilingual publication of laws in English and Spanish for the first 20 years following ratification, a practice that evolved into broader bilingual policies reflecting the significant Hispanic heritage population.[28] Subsequent statutes, including the 1969 Bilingual Policy Statement and the Bilingual Multicultural Education Act of 1973—the first such law in the U.S.—institutionalized Spanish's role in public education, government services, and official documents, where it is routinely employed for accessibility and cultural preservation.[29][30] Spanish speakers constitute about 23% of New Mexico's population, with the language predominant in rural northern and traditional Hispano communities, where unique dialects like New Mexican Spanish persist. Government entities, including courts and legislatures, provide interpretations and translations, underscoring Spanish's practical equivalence to English in administrative functions despite the absence of formal co-official status. This de facto framework contrasts with stricter English-only policies in other states, prioritizing empirical accommodation of linguistic demographics over uniformity.[31] Other U.S. states like Texas exhibit similar patterns of predominant Spanish use in border regions and urban centers, where the language is de facto integrated into local governance due to demographic realities—Spanish speakers comprise roughly 24% of the population—but without New Mexico's codified bilingual mandates. State agencies offer multilingual services, ballots, and signage, reflecting causal adaptation to a large Spanish-speaking workforce and electorate, though English remains the operative default.[31][32]

Places with Partial Official Recognition or Special Legal Status

Regional or Historical Official Recognition

In the United States, the state of New Mexico accords Spanish a special legal status through constitutional provisions dating to its admission to the Union on January 6, 1912. The state's 1910 constitution required that all laws be drafted and published in both English and Spanish, with this bilingual mandate extending for 20 years post-statehood to accommodate the longstanding Spanish-speaking population rooted in colonial and Mexican territorial eras. Although New Mexico lacks a designated official language, subsequent statutes have perpetuated bilingual publication of laws and regulations, and state agencies provide language access services, including interpretation in courts and public documents, reflecting Spanish's practical role alongside English. This framework stems from demographic realities, with approximately 25% of residents speaking Spanish at home as of recent surveys.[28][33] Historically, Spanish held official status in the Philippines from the late 16th century through the 20th century. Introduced during Spanish colonization beginning in 1565, it served as the sole language of governance, education, and ecclesiastical affairs for over 300 years until the 1898 Spanish-American War transfer to U.S. control. Under American administration, Spanish remained co-official with English from 1901 onward, a status reaffirmed in the 1935 Commonwealth Constitution. The 1973 Constitution initially demoted it, though a presidential decree briefly restored co-officiality; however, the 1987 Constitution definitively established Filipino and English as the official languages, relegating Spanish to auxiliary use in diplomacy and heritage contexts. This shift aligned with post-independence nation-building emphasizing indigenous languages, despite Spanish's enduring lexical influence on Filipino via loanwords comprising up to 20% of its vocabulary.[34][35][36]

Disputed Territories

Western Sahara remains a disputed territory, primarily administered by Morocco with the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (SADR) controlling eastern portions since its declaration of independence on February 27, 1976. The SADR's 1999 constitution explicitly designates Arabic as the sole official national language.[37] However, Spanish maintains a prominent de facto role in SADR-governed areas, utilized in education, media broadcasts, and administrative functions due to the region's history as Spanish Sahara from 1884 until its withdrawal in 1975.[27] [38] In SADR-controlled territories, Spanish serves as a working language alongside Arabic, particularly in radio and television programming operated by the Polisario Front, the SADR's political and military leadership. It is taught in schools and appears in official documents, reflecting colonial linguistic legacies rather than constitutional mandate.[39] This partial status underscores Spanish's practical utility in a context of limited recognition, where the SADR is acknowledged by about 46 United Nations member states as of 2023. No other major disputed territories accord Spanish similar partial official or predominant administrative recognition.

Related Linguistic Contexts

Spanish-Based Creole Languages with Official Recognition

Palenquero is a Spanish-based creole language originating from contact between Spanish and Kikongo (a Bantu language) during the colonial era, primarily spoken in the town of San Basilio de Palenque, located in Colombia's Bolívar Department. This community, founded in 1619 by escaped enslaved Africans led by Benkos Biohó, maintains Palenquero as a marker of cultural resistance, with an estimated 3,000 to 6,000 speakers coexisting alongside Spanish in a bilingual setting.[40][41] Article 10 of Colombia's 1991 Constitution designates Spanish as the national official language while granting official status to indigenous languages within their territorial jurisdictions, a provision extended to minority creoles like Palenquero through legal recognition and cultural preservation efforts. Since the 1990s, Palenquero has been incorporated into local education at the Benkos Biohó school, where it is taught as a subject to preserve its use among younger generations, reflecting governmental support for linguistic diversity in Afro-Colombian communities.[40][42] No other Spanish-based creole holds equivalent de jure official recognition at a territorial level. Chabacano, another Spanish-lexified creole spoken by around 450,000 people mainly in Zamboanga City, Philippines, functions as a regional lingua franca and is promoted in local media and governance but lacks formal co-official status under national law, where Filipino and English predominate.[43] Papiamento, official in Aruba, Bonaire, and Curaçao, derives primarily from Portuguese with substantial Spanish lexical influence but is not classified as Spanish-based by linguistic consensus.[44]

Judeo-Spanish and Dialectal Variants

Judeo-Spanish, also known as Ladino, Judezmo, or Djidió in certain communities, emerged as a distinct variety of medieval Spanish among Sephardic Jews following their expulsion from Spain in 1492. This language retained core features of 15th-century Castilian Spanish while incorporating lexical and grammatical influences from Hebrew, Aramaic, Turkish, Ladino-Arabic dialects, Greek, and Slavic languages due to migrations across the Ottoman Empire, North Africa, and the Balkans. By the 20th century, its speakers numbered around 100,000 to 200,000 globally, primarily in Turkey, Israel, and the Balkans, though numbers have declined sharply due to assimilation, the Holocaust, and language shift to dominant local tongues.[45][46] Despite its historical ties to Spanish, Judeo-Spanish holds no national official status in any country, functioning instead as a community language preserved through oral tradition, religious texts, and folklore. In Bosnia and Herzegovina, it received formal acknowledgment as a minority language under the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages, ratified by the state in 2010. This ratification extends protections to Judeo-Spanish (as Djidió) for the remaining Sephardic Jewish population, estimated at fewer than 1,000 speakers as of 2018, primarily in Sarajevo; these include rights to education, media, and cultural preservation, though implementation has been limited by community size and resources.[47][48] Other dialectal variants of Spanish, such as Andalusian, Canarian, or Rioplatense, lack independent official recognition, as they are subsumed under standard Spanish (Castilian) in jurisdictions where Spanish holds official status. In Equatorial Guinea, the local Equatoguinean Spanish variant—marked by African substrate influences and serving as a lingua franca—underpins the country's co-official Spanish usage alongside French and Portuguese, but it is not codified separately from metropolitan norms. Similarly, historical argots like Caló (a Spanish-Romani hybrid spoken by Gitano communities in Spain) receive no official elevation, despite Romani's minority protections under Spain's 1992 ratification of the same European Charter. These variants persist de facto in regional speech but do not confer distinct legal or administrative privileges.[49]

Supranational and Organizational Contexts

International Organizations

Spanish is an official language in the United Nations, one of six alongside Arabic, Chinese, English, French, and Russian, enabling its use in all principal organs, including the General Assembly, Security Council, and Economic and Social Council, with documents and interpretations provided accordingly.[50] The Organization of American States designates Spanish as one of four official languages, with English, French, and Portuguese, as stipulated in its charter and rules of procedure for meetings and documents.[51] In the European Union, Spanish holds official status among 24 languages, reflecting Spain's membership since 1986, with provisions for translation and interpretation in legislative and judicial proceedings.[52] The African Union includes Spanish among its official languages—Arabic, English, French, Portuguese, Kiswahili, and others—following Equatorial Guinea's accession in 2011, supporting multilingual operations across its 55 member states.[53] The Antarctic Treaty System recognizes Spanish as one of four official languages, with English, French, and Russian, for consultative meetings and secretariat functions since the 1959 treaty's entry into force.
