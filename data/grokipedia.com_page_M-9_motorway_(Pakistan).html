M-9 motorway (Pakistan)

The M-9 motorway is a 136-kilometre-long, six-lane controlled-access highway in Sindh province, Pakistan, linking the major port city of Karachi with Hyderabad.[1][2] Constructed between 2015 and 2018 by the National Highway Authority at a cost of approximately Rs36 billion, it serves primarily as a conduit for commercial traffic from Karachi Port and Port Qasim, replacing the congested parallel N-5 Super Highway.[1][3] The motorway includes four interchanges, service areas, and toll collection points, enabling higher design speeds of up to 120 km/h and enhancing regional connectivity within Pakistan's expanding motorway network.[1][4] As the first motorway in Sindh, its operational status since 2018 has supported economic activities by streamlining logistics and reducing transit times for freight, though recent discussions involve potential upgrades to eight lanes amid ongoing maintenance and jurisdictional debates between federal and provincial authorities.[5][6]

History

The M-9 Motorway project originated as an upgrade to the existing 136-kilometer Karachiâ€“Hyderabad Super Highway, a four-lane road without access control, aimed at enhancing capacity for inter-city connectivity and supporting economic corridors between Pakistan's largest port city and interior regions.[7] Pre-feasibility assessments, including one conducted by Louis Berger International in 1998, laid the groundwork, followed by a World Bank-supported study finalized on March 19, 2003, which targeted rehabilitation of a 48-kilometer section to address substandard alignments, drainage Inauguration and Early Operations (2010s)

The M-9 Motorway project received presidential oversight in November 2012, when President Asif Ali Zardari reviewed progress and instructed authorities to accelerate construction while prioritizing material quality and structural integrity.[10] This review focused on upgrading the existing 136-kilometer Karachi-Hyderabad Super Highway into a controlled-access six-lane motorway under a public-private partnership model, specifically a 25-year build-operate-transfer (BOT) arrangement awarded to the Frontier Works Organisation (FWO).[11] Construction formally commenced following the project's ceremonial launch on March 11, 2015, with an initial target completion date of August 2017, though segments opened progressively ahead of schedule.[12] Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif inaugurated a substantial 75-kilometer completed section of the M-9 on February 3, 2017, marking the transition to operational use for portions of the route and enabling early traffic diversion from congested national highways.[13] [14] Full operational handover occurred by early 2018, with the motorway accommodating initial daily volumes of commercial and passenger vehicles primarily serving Karachi Port and Port Qasim freight corridors.[5] Early performance demonstrated effective handling of escalating truck traffic, as the divided lanes and access controls minimized bottlenecks compared to the prior two-lane superhighway, though exact inaugural flow data remained limited to internal National Highway Authority monitoring. Toll collection commenced concurrently with segmental openings under the BOT framework, featuring plazas at key points like Jamshoro to fund maintenance and operations, though initial user resistance and enforcement issues arose due to the shift from non-tolled national routes.[15] Basic upkeep protocols were established by FWO, including routine pavement inspections and debris clearance, to address minor post-opening wear from heavy axle loads, with adjustments made to signage and barrier systems for smoother integration into the national network.[11] These measures supported the motorway's core function in streamlining north-south logistics in Sindh during its formative years.

Integration into Broader Networks

The M-9 motorway holds the designation M-9 within the National Highway Authority's (NHA) standardized numbering system for Pakistan's controlled-access motorways, where the 'M' prefix signifies its status as a high-capacity, limited-access route integrated into the national grid.[16] This classification aligns it with the broader motorway framework managed by the NHA, facilitating standardized operations, tolling, and maintenance across the system. At its southern terminus near Karachi, the M-9 interconnects with the M-10 motorway, which extends access to Karachi Port, while its northern endpoint at Hyderabad links via the M-6 motorway near Jamshoro, providing onward connectivity to the M-5 and further northern segments toward Multan, Sukkur, and Islamabad.[17][18] The M-9 forms the southernmost segment of the Eastern Alignment under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), a bilateral infrastructure framework formalized in 2013 and advanced through the mid-2010s to bolster regional linkages. Spanning 136 kilometers from Karachi to Hyderabad, it anchors the alignment's coastal initiation, enabling direct motorway-grade progression inland to subsequent CPEC components like the M-6 and M-5, within a projected network exceeding 3,000 kilometers from China's Kashgar to Pakistan's Arabian Sea ports.[19][20] This positioning causally supports national goals of decongesting legacy national highwaysâ€”such as the N-5â€”by channeling port-originated freight onto dedicated corridors, thereby streamlining logistics flows from maritime entry points to interior distribution nodes without interruption from mixed-traffic routes.[21] Such integration underscores the M-9's role in realizing Pakistan's strategic aim of a unified north-south arterial spine, where its alignment reduces transit dependencies on lower-standard roads and amplifies the motorway system's capacity to handle escalating inter-regional volumes, as evidenced by ongoing expansions tying it to auxiliary links like the proposed M-10 bypass through the Kirthar Range.[22]

Route and Geography

Overall Alignment and Length

The M-9 Motorway connects Karachi to Hyderabad in Sindh province, commencing at the northern periphery of Karachi near the Lyari Expressway and Sohrab Goth interchange, and terminating at the Hyderabad bypass near the Jamshoro T-junction.[23][24] The entire alignment spans 136 kilometers, providing a direct six-lane controlled-access route primarily upgraded from the former National Highway N-5 Super Highway corridor.[25][26] This motorway traverses the flat, arid plains of Sindh, characterized by level terrain that facilitates consistent high-speed travel with minimal elevation changes.[20] The path shifts from urban outskirts in Karachi District through rural expanses in Thatta and Jamshoro districts before approaching the semi-urban setting of Hyderabad, emphasizing spatial efficiency across predominantly agricultural and sparsely populated landscapes.[24] In contrast to the older Super Highway, which permitted at-grade access and mixed traffic at lower speeds, the M-9 incorporates full controlled access and grade-separated design elements, supporting a design speed of 120 km/h and enhanced safety for vehicular flow.[20][27]

Key Segments and Terrain

The M-9 motorway spans 136 kilometers from Karachi's northern bypass to Hyderabad, traversing segments differentiated by land use and operational demands. The southern segment, commencing at the Karachi outskirts, contends with urban congestion zones exacerbated by dense port-adjacent traffic from Karachi Port and Port Qasim, where commercial vehicles predominate. Central rural mid-sections interface with agricultural expanses in Sindh's Indus Valley plains, enabling efficient passage for freight amid expansive farmlands. The northern segment approaching Hyderabad features industrial linkages, channeling traffic toward manufacturing clusters and distribution networks.[28] The terrain consists of flat alluvial plains with negligible elevation variations, generally near sea level, which supports uniform high-speed travel across the route. This low-relief geography, characteristic of Sindh's coastal and riverine lowlands, minimizes gradients and facilitates consistent motorway design standards. However, the area's proneness to monsoon-induced floodingâ€”stemming from heavy seasonal rainfall and inadequate natural drainage in the Indus deltaâ€”influences operational usability, as evidenced by severe inundation events that submerge sections and auxiliary facilities.[29] These terrain attributes underpin standardized speed limits of 120 km/h for light transport vehicles (LTV) and cars, with 100-110 km/h for heavy transport vehicles (HTV) and public service vehicles (PSV), permitting cars, trucks, and buses while prohibiting motorcycles and non-motorized traffic to maintain flow on the flat alignment. Flood vulnerability prompts temporary closures and diversions during monsoons, overriding nominal limits to prioritize safety amid water accumulation, though the overall level profile enables rapid resumption post-drainage.[30]

Interchanges and Connectivity

The M-9 motorway incorporates multiple interchanges engineered for efficient vehicular merging and local access, primarily featuring trumpet, cloverleaf, and diamond configurations to minimize weave conflicts and support six-lane capacity. At the southern origin near Karachi, a trumpet interchange links directly to the M-10 Karachi Northern Bypass, enabling high-volume entry from urban arterials without grade-level crossings. The northern terminus at Kotri utilizes a cloverleaf interchange to connect to the N-5 national highway, facilitating divergence to Hyderabad and beyond while incorporating loop ramps for directional flow separation.[31][24] Intermediate interchanges, upgraded as part of the 2010s expansion from four to six lanes, include key nodes at Dadabhai, Industrial Valley, Nooriabad, and Thana Bulla Khan, each with multiple slip roads and underpasses designed to integrate with secondary roads serving districts like Jamshoro and Tharparkar. Additional points such as Lonikot, Panjal Makan, Hubsari, and Commander City provide localized entry-exit ramps, typically featuring two to four lanes per direction for traffic diversion from rural feeders to the controlled-access mainline. The Bahria Town interchange near the southern end adds three underpasses and service roads spanning approximately 8 km, enhancing connectivity to adjacent developments without impeding through-traffic.[32][33][34] These access points adhere to National Highway Authority standards for signage visibility and ramp gradients, promoting safe acceleration to posted speeds of 120 km/h, though initial post-inauguration reports noted occasional merging delays at high-demand nodes due to pre-upgrade geometries. The overall system, expanded to include eight new and two modified interchanges during rehabilitation, prioritizes grade-separated junctions to link the M-9 with the N-5 and peripheral networks, reducing reliance on at-grade intersections.[35][36]

Design and Technical Features

Capacity and Structural Specifications

The M-9 Motorway is configured as a six-lane divided highway, with three lanes in each direction, providing a carriageway width optimized for bidirectional high-volume traffic including heavy commercial vehicles from Karachi's ports. Lane widths measure 3.65 meters each, supported by 3-meter outer shoulders and 1-meter inner shoulders to accommodate emergency maneuvers and maintenance access while maintaining structural stability under load. The pavement consists of asphaltic concrete layers engineered for durability against repeated heavy freight traffic, adhering to National Highway Authority standards that prioritize resistance to rutting and fatigue cracking in flat terrain. This design accommodates empirical axle load capacities typical of Pakistani motorways, with single axles limited to 12 tons, tandem axles to 22 tons, and tri-axles to around 30 tons, ensuring the subgrade and base courses can bear the dynamic stresses from port-originated truck convoys without premature deterioration.[20][37][38] Design speeds are established at 120 km/h for light transport vehicles to maximize throughput efficiency on the level alignment, while heavy transport vehicles are capped at 80-110 km/h to mitigate vibrational impacts on the asphalt surfacing and bridge elements. These parameters reflect first-principles considerations for causal factors like vehicle mass distribution and traffic density, enabling a theoretical daily capacity exceeding 50,000 vehicles per direction under uncongested conditions, though actual throughput is constrained by enforcement of weight limits to preserve long-term pavement life. In response to observed traffic growth, federal authorities approved expansion to eight lanes in 2022, incorporating reinforced asphalt overlays to sustain elevated load-bearing demands from intensified trade logistics.[39][30][31]

Safety and Auxiliary Facilities

The M-9 motorway incorporates service areas at intervals designed to offer refueling, food outlets, and rest facilities, supporting driver fatigue mitigation through brief stops. These amenities, including locations near toll plazas and along the 136 km route such as Nooriabad, are intended to enhance user convenience on the Karachi-Hyderabad alignment. However, independent assessments have highlighted deficiencies, with uneven maintenance leading to substandard conditions in rest areas and limited availability of quality services compared to motorways like the M-2.[41][31] Emergency support relies on the National Highways and Motorway Police helpline 130 for roadside assistance, breakdowns, and incident response, enabling rapid coordination without on-road call boxes specific to the M-9. Studies indicate an absence of dedicated emergency call boxes or fixed assistance points along the route, contrasting with features on other Pakistani motorways and contributing to reliance on mobile reporting. Fuel stations operated by entities like Pakistan State Oil are present at key points, such as along the Hyderabad-Karachi section, facilitating refills but without integrated comprehensive auxiliary hubs every 25-50 km as planned in initial upgrades.[42][43][44]

Economic and Strategic Role

Facilitation of Trade and Commerce

The M-9 motorway functions as a primary artery for transporting goods from Karachi Port and Port Qasim to inland markets, handling a significant share of Pakistan's road-based freight, which constitutes approximately 97% of total goods movement nationwide. As a six-lane controlled-access highway spanning 136 kilometers from Karachi to Hyderabad, it directly links port facilities to national networks like the N-5, enabling efficient onward distribution to industrial hubs in Sindh and Punjab.[45] Daily traffic volumes on the M-9 exceed 25,000 vehicles, including substantial commercial loads such as containers and bulk cargo originating from the ports, supporting the flow of imports for manufacturing and exports like textiles and agricultural products.[25] Upgrades to the M-9, including rehabilitation and widening, have enhanced its capacity to manage increased port throughput amid rising trade volumes at Karachi, where external merchandise has grown rapidly.[46] These improvements have empirically lowered logistics costs by shortening transit times and reducing fuel expenditures compared to the pre-motorway super highway configuration; for instance, resurfacing efforts targeted bottlenecks to streamline freight movement serving over 80% of national economic traffic on key corridors like the M-9.[47] For perishable goods, such as fruits and vegetables routed from port-adjacent processing zones to Punjab markets, minimized delays decrease spoilage rates and associated losses, directly contributing to cost efficiencies in the supply chain.[48] The motorway's alignment fosters causal enhancements in export competitiveness by accelerating connections between Sindh's port economies and Punjab's manufacturing base, where integrated transport infrastructure correlates with accelerated regional development and trade volumes.[49] This linkage supports faster turnaround for export-oriented cargo, reducing overall lead times and enabling Pakistani producers to meet international deadlines more reliably, though aggregate logistics costs remain elevated due to broader sectoral inefficiencies.[50]

Contribution to National Connectivity

The M-9 motorway bolsters Pakistan's national connectivity by linking the southern port hub of Karachi directly to Hyderabad in Sindh province, facilitating integration with the country's expanding northern motorway system through interchanges and auxiliary link roads. This 136-kilometer controlled-access highway enables reliable north-south passenger and freight movement within Sindh and beyond, serving as a foundational segment for domestic network unification.[28][24] Unlike the parallel N-5 National Highway, which experiences frequent disruptions from seasonal flooding and congestion, the M-9 provides all-weather access with design speeds up to 120 km/h for light vehicles, ensuring consistent connectivity across diverse terrains. Recent developments, such as the M-9 to M-5 link road opened in 2025, further shorten travel distances and enhance cargo flow from ports like Port Qasim to central Pakistan, reducing regional isolation in southern provinces.[51][47] By prioritizing passenger mobility and intra-provincial flows over purely commercial routes, the M-9 contributes to broader economic cohesion through verifiable time savings; for instance, motorway upgrades have generally halved journey durations between major Sindh cities compared to legacy highways, per transport analyses. Together with N-5, it handles over 80% of national freight traffic, underscoring its role in sustaining domestic supply chains and unifying disparate regions without reliance on vulnerable alternate paths.[52][53][47]

Linkage to China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC)

The M-9 motorway constitutes the 136-kilometer southern segment of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC)'s Eastern Alignment, linking Karachi to Hyderabad and serving as the foundational link in the Peshawar-Karachi motorway chain.[54] This alignment prioritizes upgraded infrastructure through Pakistan's more populated eastern regions, contrasting with the Western Alignment's focus on underdeveloped western provinces, to expedite connectivity for freight and passenger movement from southern ports northward.[55] The full Eastern route encompasses over 1,400 kilometers of motorways and highways from Islamabad to Karachi, with M-9 enabling seamless integration into subsequent sections like the M-6 Sukkur-Hyderabad motorway, thereby forming a high-capacity backbone for CPEC's overland transport objectives.[56] While direct Chinese financing has predominantly supported northern CPEC motorway segments such as the M-4 and M-5, the M-9's 2015 upgrade from a four-lane superhighway to a six-lane controlled-access facility aligned with broader CPEC timelines and standards, executed by Pakistan's Frontier Works Organization to facilitate corridor-wide traffic flows.[57] This integration enhances logistical efficiency by providing a tolled, high-speed conduit for goods originating from Karachi Port and Port Qasim, reducing transit times to northern industrial hubs and indirectly bolstering redundancy for Gwadar-Karachi trade pathways via complementary coastal links like the N-10 East Bay Expressway.[55] Empirical data from CPEC project evaluations indicate that such southern linkages have increased average motorway freight speeds by up to 50% compared to national highways, mitigating bottlenecks in Pakistan's export-oriented supply chains without relying on congested alternate routes.[54] Project documentation attributes any phased implementation delays in the Eastern Alignment, including M-9 enhancements, primarily to domestic financing constraints and procurement timelines rather than design or geopolitical flaws, underscoring the corridor's viability through adaptive Pakistani-led execution within the CPEC framework.[58] This approach has ensured M-9's operational readiness as a CPEC artery, with daily commercial traffic volumes exceeding 20,000 vehicles, directly supporting the corridor's goal of streamlined regional trade integration.[59]

Operations and Management

Toll Collection and Financial Model

![National highway Mâˆ’9M-9Mâˆ’9 Jamsshoro Toll Plaza Overfly.jpg](./assets/National_highway_Mâˆ’9M-9Mâˆ’9 The M-9 motorway operates under a Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) framework, with the concessionaire responsible for toll collection to finance construction recovery, operations, and maintenance until the transfer phase.[60] This model ensures self-sustainability through user fees, distinct from government-subsidized highways.[25] Toll collection occurs at multiple plazas, including Jamshoro and the expanded Hyderabad facility, operational since December 2020 as Pakistan's largest.[61] Systems employ artificial intelligence for automated processing: entry/exit cameras capture vehicle images, license plates are recognized via AI and OCR, enabling cashless deduction without halting traffic for tagged vehicles.[62] M-Tags facilitate seamless electronic tolling across Pakistan's motorways, integrated with centralized databases for real-time fee adjustment and enforcement.[63] In 2025, toll revisions included hikes for non-M-Tag vehicles, with cars charged Rs. 550 one-way on the Karachi-Hyderabad stretch, alongside increases for larger vehicles such as wagons at Rs. 950 and buses at Rs. 1,850.[64] An additional 25% surcharge applies to untagged vehicles from February 2025, while a 50% penalty for low-balance or non-compliant tags took effect in June.[33][65] These revenues directly support the BOT financial structure, covering debt servicing, upkeep, and eventual asset handover to the National Highway Authority without ongoing fiscal burden on public funds.[66]

Maintenance Responsibilities

The National Highway Authority (NHA) holds primary responsibility for the operation and maintenance of the M-9 motorway, encompassing routine upkeep such as surface resurfacing, drainage system management, and structural inspections, as mandated under the National Highway Authority Act of 1991.[67] This includes periodic audits and preventive measures to ensure pavement integrity across the 136 km stretch connecting Karachi and Hyderabad.[60] Emergency protocols involve rapid response teams for incident-related repairs, such as pothole patching or barrier reinstatement following accidents, coordinated through NHA's regional directorates in Sindh.[68] Private sector involvement occurs via concessions for specific operational segments, where entities like SCORE Pvt Ltd, a subsidiary of the Frontier Works Organization, handle delegated maintenance duties under NHA oversight, though this has led to coordination challenges in aligning routine schedules with emergency interventions.[60] Divided responsibilities between NHA and concessionaires have contributed to causal gaps in quality control, as evidenced by inconsistent implementation of standardized protocols across sections.[69] Reports indicate verifiable maintenance lapses, including uneven surfaces and cracks on portions of the M-9, as documented in commuter complaints and inspections dating back to 2017, with persistent issues noted in 2024 affecting ride quality and safety.[70][71] NHA budget allocations for national highway repairs, including motorways like the M-9, have faced shortfalls, with overall maintenance funding often deprioritized in favor of new construction, resulting in delayed actual repairs despite annual revenues exceeding Rs110 billion by 2024.[72][73] For instance, while NHA's total portfolio received Rs161 billion in FY2024-25, specific repair expenditures on motorways remained under-resourced relative to identified needs, exacerbating wear from high traffic volumes.[74]

Traffic Volume and Usage Patterns

The M-9 motorway accommodates an average daily traffic volume of approximately 25,000 vehicles, with roughly 60 percent consisting of heavy vehicles such as trucks, reflecting its primary role in freight transport from Karachi's ports.[41] This composition underscores the dominance of commercial haulage, as the route links key industrial and port facilities to inland distribution networks. Traffic counts at toll plazas, such as Jamshoro, recorded an average daily total of 16,207 vehicles in 2007, increasing to 20,331 vehicles per day by 2012 in sections like Hyderabad-Nooriabad. Usage patterns reveal steady growth, estimated at five percent annually, driven by expanding port activity and regional commerce.[41] Peak hourly flows account for about 5.9 percent of daily traffic on the motorway, typically occurring during daytime commercial hours. National Highways and Motorways Police (NHMP) advisories highlight surges during holidays like Eid, with increased vehicular flow across motorways prompting enhanced patrolling and management measures.[75] Seasonal dips, particularly during the monsoon period from July to September, are inferred from broader weather-related disruptions on Sindh routes, though precise M-9 metrics remain documented primarily through periodic counts rather than continuous monitoring.

Notable Crashes and Casualties

On September 26, 2020, a passenger van traveling from Hyderabad to Karachi overturned and caught fire on the M-9 near the Loni Kot area, killing 13 people initially, with the death toll rising to 17 as additional victims succumbed to injuries.[80][81] In July 16, 2024, six individuals died and four were injured when a mini-truck carrying passengers collided with a trailer on the M-9, ejecting victims from the vehicle.[82] A collision between a passenger coach and a trailer near Jamshoro on July 26, 2024, resulted in three fatalities on the M-9.[83] On September 25, 2025, two separate crashes on the M-9 in the Nooriabad and Loni Kot jurisdictions claimed two lives and injured over 30 people.[84] The M-9 reopened on October 18, 2025, after a 34-day closure attributed to operational challenges, though specific links to prior incidents remain unconfirmed in reports.[85]

Identified Risk Factors

Bad road conditions on the M-9 motorway, including potholes, cracks, and uneven surfaces, contribute to vehicle instability and skids, particularly when combined with high speeds typical on this controlled-access route. These irregularities arise from rapid deterioration under heavy traffic loads, exacerbating loss of control during overtaking or lane changes.[76][71][86] Inadequate lighting along stretches of the motorway reduces nighttime visibility, increasing the risk of head-on collisions from wrong-way driving or failure to detect obstacles. This infrastructural shortfall, compounded by worn-out signage, hinders timely hazard recognition and proper navigation, especially in low-light conditions prevalent in rural Sindh sections.[87] Poorly designed intersections, such as those linking the M-9 to adjacent highways, foster congestion and erratic merging, promoting wrong-side travel and rear-end impacts due to insufficient barriers and alignment flaws. These geometric deficiencies amplify collision probabilities during peak traffic flows, independent of driver behavior.[87][88] The motorway's vulnerability to weather events, notably monsoon flooding, stems from inadequate drainage capacity, leading to water accumulation that erodes surfaces and obstructs lanes. In September 2025, heavy rains caused severe inundation on the Karachi section, submerging facilities and exposing foundational weaknesses to hydrodynamic forces.[29][89]

Criticisms and Challenges

Construction Quality and Durability Issues

The Supreme Court of Pakistan, in August 2018, expressed concerns over the substandard construction of the M-9 motorway, noting that the road's poor quality forced vehicles to proceed at reduced speeds despite its designation as a high-speed corridor.[90][91] Justice Gulzar Ahmed highlighted deficiencies in execution during hearings on National Highway Authority (NHA) performance, attributing the issues to inadequate build standards shortly after the 2013-2014 upgrade from the Karachi-Hyderabad Super Highway.[92] Early deterioration manifested within two to three years of completion, with reports in 2017 documenting broken patches, tire marks, and uneven surfaces across segments, prompting parliamentary motions on the motorway's eroding condition.[70] By 2024, persistent flaws included bumps, cracks, and irregular pavement that compromised structural integrity under regular traffic volumes exceeding design capacities in peak hours.[71] These defects stem from improper material selection and compaction, as evidenced in broader analyses of Pakistani highway projects where subpar asphalt mixes fail to resist shear stresses from heavy axle loads common on freight routes.[93] Durability challenges are exacerbated by the original asphalt surfacing's inability to endure Pakistan's variable climate and overloading, leading to accelerated rutting and surface unraveling without reinforced binders or adequate sub-base density.[94] NHA audits and field inspections have confirmed that initial construction skipped rigorous quality controls, resulting in pavements that degrade faster than international benchmarks for similar motorways.[71] Despite these shortcomings, the M-9 has achieved average travel times of under two hours between Karachi and Hyderabadâ€”halving previous durations on the predecessor roadâ€”demonstrating functional gains amid execution lapses.[95] Ongoing remediation efforts, including periodic resurfacing, have mitigated some wear but underscore the need for enhanced material standards in future upgrades.[70]

Encroachments and Unauthorized Developments

The environmental impact assessment (EIA) report for the M-9 motorway, prepared by EMC Pakistan in October 2015, determined that 95% of structures along both sides of the alignment were unauthorized.[96] These included 89 petrol and compressed natural gas (CNG) stations, 70 hotels and restaurants, 86 mosques, 160 tyre repair shops, five residential colonies, and six schools, totaling over 500 affected establishments.[96] The report highlighted challenges in land acquisition and compensation as key barriers to relocating these encroachments, with affected parties expressing concerns over resettlement and relocation feasibility.[96] Encroachments have proliferated along the motorway's shoulders and service roads, narrowing the available right-of-way and obstructing planned widening projects.[97] A 2022 audit by the National Highway Authority (NHA) identified that developers, including Bahria Town Karachi and DHA City, had unauthorizedly occupied 785 kanals (approximately 99 hectares) of NHA land adjacent to the M-9 for constructing interchanges and related infrastructure without prior approval.[98] Such developments, often involving private housing societies and commercial outlets, have compounded land acquisition hurdles by entangling legitimate right-of-way claims with disputed unauthorized expansions.[99] These unauthorized occupations contribute to reduced roadway buffer zones, elevating risks of vehicular deviations and pedestrian-vehicle conflicts near access points.[97] Traffic gridlocks frequently occur due to spillover from encroached adjacent areas, particularly at entry-exit ramps and underpasses, where informal vending and structures impede smooth flow.[97] Persistent encroachments along stretches from Karachi to Hyderabad have stalled dualization and lane addition initiatives, as unresolved compensation demands for affected parties delay clearance.[100]

Maintenance and Service Shortcomings

The M-9 motorway has faced persistent criticism for inadequate upkeep of its service areas, which include facilities for rest, refueling, and basic amenities along the Karachi-Hyderabad route. Reports from 2020 highlighted that these areas were not maintained properly, leading to unhygienic conditions and degraded infrastructure that deterred user safety and convenience.[41] Such neglect contributes to operational inefficiencies, as service areas are intended to support long-haul travel but often fail to meet basic standards due to insufficient cleaning and repairs. Road surface deterioration has compounded these issues, with uneven pavement and potholes reported in multiple segments, particularly the 30 km stretch between Nooriabad and Jamshoro.[71] By 2025, lack of adequate maintenance was identified as a primary concern, exacerbating wear from heavy traffic and environmental factors, including damaged barriers and accumulating debris that poses hazards to motorists.[87] The National Highway Authority (NHA), responsible for motorway maintenance, collects toll revenues explicitly earmarked for upkeep, yet persistent flaws suggest gaps in oversight and resource allocation.[101] These shortcomings reflect broader funding and management inefficiencies, as toll collectionsâ€”intended to self-sustain operationsâ€”have not translated into proportional improvements despite generating substantial income from high-volume usage.[102] Public and media accounts underscore that while revenues support NHA's mandate for expressway maintenance, systemic delays in repairs indicate underutilization or misprioritization of funds.[103]

Legal and Regulatory Developments

Court Interventions and Disputes

In August 2018, the Supreme Court of Pakistan voiced strong concerns over the M-9 motorway's construction quality during a hearing, highlighting substandard work that forced vehicles to travel at slow speeds and deeming the toll rates excessively high relative to the road's condition.[104] Justice Gulzar Ahmed specifically criticized the National Highway Authority (NHA) for the poor execution, warning that any road accident fatalities due to these deficiencies would result in criminal cases against the NHA and mandatory compensation to victims' heirs.[104] Disputes over the Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) concession for the M-9 arose shortly after its 2004 award to Bina Puri Holdings Bhd, a Malaysian firm, leading to contract termination by the NHA amid performance issues.[105] Bina Puri challenged the termination in the Sindh High Court in April 2013, which referred the matter to arbitration; the tribunal ruled in the company's favor in 2019, awarding damages for breach of agreement.[106][105] In 2025, an out-of-court settlement directed the NHA to pay Rs1.29 billion to resolve the lingering claims, averting further litigation.[107] The Sindh High Court has adjudicated multiple constitutional petitions related to M-9 alignments, toll implementations, and access issues, including challenges to the lack of service roads for nearby residents post-conversion to a controlled-access motorway.[108] Land acquisition disputes for expansions have also featured prominently, with around 70 petitions filed between 2017 and 2021â€”primarily contesting right-of-way claimsâ€”heard by the court as recently as August 2024, delaying project progress amid unresolved compensation for affected landowners.[109][100] These cases underscore ongoing tensions between infrastructure development and property rights, with courts emphasizing fair valuation and procedural compliance in acquisitions.[109]

Policy Shifts and Reversion Proposals

In August 2024, a division bench of the Sindh High Court questioned the National Highway Authority's (NHA) conversion of the Karachi-Hyderabad Super Highway into the M-9 motorway, describing it as a superficial upgrade lacking new alignment, modern engineering standards, and essential features like adequate service roads for local access.[109] The court highlighted resultant issues, including fencing that restricted public entry points, toll plazas positioned within or near urban areas imposing undue burdens on residents, and the elimination of roadside facilities such as fuel stations and eateries without equivalents, arguing these flaws undermined the purported benefits of motorway designation.[109] This judicial scrutiny prompted reversion proposals to downgrade the M-9 back to super highway status, emphasizing that the upgrade represented a low-cost reconfiguration rather than substantive infrastructure investment, with private sector contributions of approximately Rs40 billion failing to justify ongoing toll revenues or access limitations for non-motorway users.[109] Proponents of reversion cited empirical discrepancies in cost-benefit analyses, noting that while the six-lane configuration improved freight throughput and reduced travel times for long-haul traffic, it exacerbated local connectivity disruptions and maintenance demands without proportional safety or capacity gains over the original highway.[109] As an alternative policy shift, the NHA advanced plans for a parallel M-6 motorway spanning 306 km from Karachi through Hyderabad to Sukkur, approved by the Sindh government in October 2024 via public-private partnership or joint federal-provincial funding, to address capacity shortfalls without further altering the M-9's designation.[110] These proposals reflect NHA evaluations balancing upgrade expenses against redundant parallel corridors, prioritizing new builds for high-speed toll operations while potentially reverting the M-9 to alleviate provincial grievances over land use and toll equity.[110] By September 2025, right-of-way disputes had stalled M-9 expansions, intensifying calls for reversion to prioritize fiscal efficiency and public access over sustained motorway pretensions.[100]

Recent Updates (2020s Onward)

Expansion and Upgrade Projects

In the 2020s, upgrade projects for the M-9 have emphasized improved interchanges and connectivity links to accommodate rising traffic volumes between Karachi and Hyderabad. A notable private-sector initiative, the Bahria Town interchange, was completed and opened to the public in early 2020, spanning 8 km with three underpasses, multiple slip roads, access and service roads, a bridge, and a causeway, all fully funded by the developer Bahria Town as Pakistan's first large-scale privately constructed public infrastructure of this type.[111][112] The M9-M5 Link Road project, executed by the National Highway Authority and Frontier Works Organization at a cost of Rs3.53 billion, represents a key public enhancement, constructing a 22 km route with a new interchange at kilometer 34+740 on the M-9, including southbound, northbound segments, and a bridge over the motorway.[51] Progress advanced significantly by mid-2025, with the chief minister directing full opening by July 25, 2025, though the interchange completion was targeted for October 2025 to integrate seamless access between the M-9 and M-5 motorways.[51][113] Foreign investment has also targeted M-9 upgrades, with Azerbaijan expressing interest in 2024 to fund improvements via a government-to-government model, aiming to enhance goods and passenger transport links to Central Asia and Eastern Europe, following a planned cooperation finalization meeting in December 2024.[114] These efforts, supported by entities like the Special Investment Facilitation Council, involve contractors such as a Malaysian firm for specified works, focusing on capacity boosts without altering the core six-lane configuration.[115]

Environmental and Weather-Related Disruptions

In September 2025, heavy monsoon rains caused severe flooding along the M-9 Motorway near Karachi, with overflow from Thado Dam in Gadap Town submerging sections of the route toward Hyderabad and disrupting traffic flow.[29] Floodwaters from the Malir and Lath Rivers also inundated areas adjacent to the motorway, including near Jamali Bridge, leading to the submersion of the Motorway South Zone DIG office and multiple vehicles under up to five feet of water.[116] [117] This event exacerbated longstanding vulnerabilities in the motorway's drainage systems during monsoon seasons, where inadequate capacity to handle overflow from nearby dams and rivers has repeatedly led to track inundation despite engineered culverts and viaducts.[29] [118] The flooding prompted a full closure of the M-9, lasting 34 days until its reopening on October 18, 2025, as repair crews addressed water damage and cleared debris from affected segments.[85] Flat terrain in the Sindh region, characteristic of the Indus River plain, contributes to rapid water accumulation and slower drainage, amplifying monsoon impacts on elevated infrastructure like the M-9, though initial design elevations provide partial mitigation against routine overflows.[119] Prior monsoon events, such as those in June 2025, have similarly affected access routes to the M-9, highlighting persistent challenges in balancing high-speed corridor functionality with regional hydrological risks.[118]

Current Status as of 2025

The M-9 Motorway is fully operational as of October 2025, having reopened on October 18 following a 34-day closure attributed to disruptions including weather events and protests affecting multiple routes.[85] The National Highways and Motorway Police (NHMP) maintains active travel advisories, issuing cloudy weather alerts for various sections of the national motorway network, including potential impacts on the Hyderabad-Karachi stretch of M-9, urging drivers to exercise caution due to reduced visibility and possible hazards.[120] Toll collection on the M-9 reflects recent hikes, with a 25% increase implemented in February 2025 for standard vehicles, alongside a 50% additional surcharge for non-M-Tag and low-balance vehicles effective June 15, 2025, aimed at enforcing electronic tolling compliance.[33][121] These adjustments have drawn scrutiny from parliamentary committees questioning the frequency and justification of revisions at M-9 toll plazas.[122] High traffic volumes persist on the M-9, underscoring its role as a vital artery for regional and domestic connectivity between Karachi and Hyderabad, even amid policy debates on motorway management.[21] Capacity enhancements from prior upgrades continue to support this demand, though localized issues like encroachments and maintenance needs remain under National Highway Authority oversight.[123]
