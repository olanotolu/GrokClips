Czech cuisine

Czech cuisine has both influenced and been influenced by the cuisines of surrounding countries and nations. Czech cuisine has contributed significantly to many cakes and pastries popular in Central Europe. Czech cuisine is notably meat-based, with meat availability increasing during the 20th century, particularly under communist rule, though contemporary consumption has decreased compared to that era due to health trends, as of the 2020s.[1][2] Traditionally, meat has been reserved for once-weekly consumption, typically on weekends. The body of Czech meals typically consists of two, or sometimes more, courses; the first course is traditionally soup, and the second course is the main dish. If a third course is eaten, which mainly happens at more festive occasions, it is usually a sweet dessert or compote (kompot). In Czech cuisine, thick soups and many kinds of sauces, both based on stewed or cooked vegetables and meats, often with cream, as well as baked meats with natural sauces (gravies), are popular dishes usually accompanied by beerâ€”especially Pilsnerâ€”with the Czech Republic having the highest per capita beer consumption in the world as of 2024.[3] Czech cuisine is also very strong in sweet main courses, a unique feature in European cuisines.

History

Czech cuisine has roots in the diet of early Slavic tribes who settled in the region around the 6th century, relying on locally available ingredients such as grains for porridges and breads, root vegetables, wild game, and dairy products from farming and herding. Beer brewing, using barley and hops, emerged as a staple early on, with evidence of production dating back to the 11th century.[4] During the medieval period, under the influence of the Holy Roman Empire and neighboring German states, Czech cuisine incorporated more structured baking techniques and meat preservation methods. Jewish communities contributed dishes like stuffed cabbage (holubce), while pork remained central to non-kosher diets. KnedlÃ­ky (dumplings), a quintessential side, are documented from the 13th century, evolving from simple steamed dough to versatile accompaniments.[5][6] The Habsburg monarchy (1526â€“1918) introduced Austrian, Hungarian, and French culinary elements, particularly in urban and noble settings. Elaborate multi-course meals became common among the elite, blending local hearty fare with refined techniques like saucing and pastry work. This era solidified beer as a national beverage and saw the rise of baked goods influenced by Viennese styles.[5] In the 19th century, amid the Czech National Revival, local cookbooks preserved and adapted these influences. The Czech-language cookbook PraÅ¾skÃ¡ kuchaÅ™ka by KarolÃ­na VÃ¡vrovÃ¡, first published in the 1860s with later editions such as 1881, shows influences of French cuisine in the order of multi-course meals common throughout the Habsburg monarchy, beginning with soup, followed by fish entrÃ©es, meat, and sweets. VÃ¡vrovÃ¡ deviates from this standard order for the sweets of Mehlspeisen type. These flour-based sweets, including baked puddings, strudels, doughnuts, and soufflÃ©s, could be served either before or after the roast meats, but stewed fruits, creamy desserts, cakes, ice cream, and cookies were to always be served after the roast, and for multiple dessert courses, would follow this stated order. The 20th century brought significant changes: World War II and subsequent communist rule (1948â€“1989) emphasized collective farming and rationing, leading to standardized, meat-heavy dishes using affordable ingredients like potatoes and cabbage. Post-Velvet Revolution in 1989, Czech cuisine diversified with global influences, healthier options, and a revival of traditional recipes, though staples like vepÅ™o-knedlo-zelo remain iconic. As of 2023, there's growing interest in sustainable and regional Moravian specialties.[8][9]

Side dishes

Dumplings (knedlÃ­ky) (steamed and sliced like bread) are one of the mainstays of Czech cuisine and are typically served with meals. They can be either wheat or potato-based and are sometimes made from a combination of wheat flour and dices made of stale bread or rolls. Puffed rice can be found in store-prepared mixtures. Smaller Czech dumplings are usually potato-based. When served as leftovers, sliced dumplings are sometimes pan-fried with eggs. Czech potato dumplings are often filled with smoked meat and served with spinach or sauerkraut. Fried onion and braised cabbage can be included as a side dish. There are many other side dishes, including noodles and boiled rice. Potatoes are served boiled with salt, often with caraway seed and butter. Peeled and boiled potatoes are mixed into mashed potatoes. New potatoes are sometimes boiled in their skins, not peeled, from harvest time to new year. Because of the influence of foreign countries, potatoes are also fried, so French fries and croquettes are common in restaurants. Buckwheat, pearl barley and millet grains are rarely served in restaurants. These are more commonly a home-cooked, healthier alternative. Pasta is common, either baked, boiled, cooked with other ingredients, or served as a salad. Pasta is available in different shapes and flavors. This is an influence of Italian and Asian cuisine. Rice and buckwheat noodles are not common but are becoming more popular. Gluten-free pasta is also available, made from corn flour, corn starch, or potatoes.
