Al-Fashaga conflict

The Al-Fashaga conflict is a protracted territorial dispute between Ethiopia and Sudan over the Al-Fashaga triangle, a fertile lowland area spanning roughly 250 square kilometers along their shared border, prized for its agricultural productivity and situated between Sudan's Gedaref state and Ethiopia's Amhara region.[1][2] The roots trace to the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, which established the Gwynn Line as the boundary, granting the territory to Sudan under colonial demarcation, though Ethiopia exerted de facto administrative control and encouraged Amhara settlement throughout much of the 20th century, fostering Ethiopian farming communities there.[3][4] Tensions simmered with periodic encroachments but boiled over in late 2020, when Sudanese armed forces exploited Ethiopia's preoccupation with the Tigray war to occupy the area, evicting thousands of Ethiopian farmers and destroying settlements, prompting armed responses from Ethiopian federal troops and Amhara militias.[3][2] This escalation displaced populations, disrupted cross-border trade, and heightened risks of broader regional instability, including potential involvement from actors like Egypt amid Nile water tensions.[2][5] As of mid-2025, Sudan maintains physical control while Ethiopia pursues diplomatic restraint and a long-term reclamation strategy, deferring demarcation talks amid mutual internal crises, though underlying claims persist without resolution.[4][5]

Historical and Legal Context

Colonial-Era Foundations

The Al-Fashaga region's disputed status originated in the late 19th-century clashes between the Ethiopian Empire under Emperor Menelik II and the Mahdist state in Sudan, which controlled territories extending toward the Atbarah River. Ethiopian campaigns in the 1880s and 1890s, culminating in the reconquest of areas like Metema by Amhara forces and settlers, incorporated the fertile Al-Fashaga triangleâ€”spanning approximately 1,200 square kilometersâ€”into Ethiopian administration, with local populations paying tribute to imperial authorities.[6][7] Following Britain's defeat of the Mahdists at Omdurman in 1898 and establishment of the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium over Sudan, colonial powers negotiated borders with Ethiopia to stabilize frontiers. The Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty of 1902, signed on 15 May by Menelik II and British representatives, delimited the Ethiopia-Sudan boundary along the "Gwynn Line"â€”named after British officer Percy Edward Gwynnâ€”running from the Setit River confluence with the Atbarah eastward, positioning Al-Fashaga north of this line and thus within Sudanese territory.[3][2] This demarcation aimed to resolve overlapping claims but reflected unequal bargaining, as Ethiopia lacked detailed surveys and faced pressure from British imperial interests in securing Sudan's eastern flank.[8] A supplementary 1907 Anglo-Ethiopian agreement further clarified segments of the border but did not alter the 1902 placement of Al-Fashaga, though on-the-ground demarcation remained partial and contested. Anglo-Egyptian Sudan exercised nominal sovereignty, granting farming concessions primarily to Ethiopian Amhara settlers who maintained de facto control and tax collection under Ethiopian governors, fostering a hybrid administration that blurred legal lines.[9][9] This tension between treaty-based de jure Sudanese ownership and persistent Ethiopian settlement sowed seeds for post-colonial friction, as neither side fully implemented boundary markers amid sparse colonial oversight.[3][4]

Post-Colonial Administration and Effective Control

Following Sudan's independence from Anglo-Egyptian condominium rule on January 1, 1956, the Al-Fashaga region was formally incorporated into the new state's administrative framework as part of Gedaref Province (later reorganized under the Gezira and Sennar states). Sudanese authorities asserted sovereignty based on the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty and subsequent colonial delimitations, which placed the international boundary south of the fertile triangle, excluding Ethiopian claims to the area. However, enforcement of this nominal control remained limited, as local Sudanese administration focused primarily on taxation and minimal governance rather than physical occupation of the farmlands.[9][10] In the decades immediately after independence, Ethiopian Amhara farmersâ€”encouraged by imperial and later Derg regime settlement policiesâ€”expanded cultivation across much of Al-Fashaga's approximately 250 square kilometers of arable land, growing crops such as sesame, sorghum, and groundnuts. These settlers established de facto effective control through continuous presence, agricultural investment, and self-defense militias, often paying land taxes and fees to Ethiopian regional authorities rather than Sudanese officials, which underscored the practical dominance of Ethiopian interests despite Sudan's legal title. Sudanese farmers were largely confined to peripheral areas, and sporadic low-level clashes occurred, but Sudanese state presence was intermittent, relying on occasional patrols rather than sustained administration.[9][3] This status quo persisted into the post-1991 era under Ethiopia's Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF) government, with Ethiopian farmers leasing or occupying up to 70-80% of the productive lands by the 2000s, according to regional estimates. A 2007-2008 bilateral understanding between Sudan and Ethiopia tacitly permitted continued Ethiopian access and farming in exchange for Ethiopia's non-pursuit of formal territorial claims, allowing Sudanese nominal oversight while Ethiopian entities handled most on-ground management, including irrigation and harvest logistics. Effective control thus remained fragmented: Sudanese sovereignty in law and limited bureaucracy, contrasted by Ethiopian dominance in economic exploitation and security through farmer cooperatives and Amhara militias, setting the stage for escalation amid Ethiopia's internal distractions in late 2020.[3][2]

Geographical and Economic Significance

Terrain, Resources, and Agriculture

The Al-Fashaga region forms a triangular area in eastern Sudan, part of Gedaref State, spanning approximately 415,000 hectares and bordered by the Setit River to the north, the Atbara River to the west, and Ethiopia to the east.[11] This lowland terrain, wedged between Sudan's Gedaref State and Ethiopia's Amhara and Tigray regions, features flat to gently undulating plains conducive to large-scale farming.[2] The rivers serve as natural boundaries and potential water sources, though agriculture remains predominantly rain-fed.[11] The region's semi-arid climate includes a single rainy season from June to October, with precipitation decreasing from south to north, enabling seasonal cultivation on fertile heavy-cracking black vertisols that retain moisture effectively.[11] These vertisols, characteristic of the area, support intensive crop production without widespread irrigation.[11] Natural vegetation, such as Acacia mellifera and Acacia seyal-Balanites woodlands, has declined due to agricultural expansion, covering only 13.7% of the land by 2020 compared to 25% in 1990.[11] Agriculture dominates Al-Fashaga, with over 75% of the land under cultivation by 2020, driven by mechanized rain-fed schemes producing cash crops like sesame, alongside sorghum, millet, and cotton.[11] Sesame, particularly high-value white varieties, represents a key export commodity, fueling cross-border trade and local economies.[2] [12] The annual value of crops from the region has been estimated at around $250 million, underscoring its economic significance as a fertile breadbasket amid regional disputes.[3] Recent diversification includes sunflowers, chickpeas, and lentils, reflecting adaptations to market demands and land pressures.[11]

Strategic Value in Regional Trade and Security

The Al-Fashaga region's strategic location along the Ethiopia-Sudan border facilitates critical cross-border trade, particularly in agricultural commodities such as sesame, which constitutes a significant portion of informal economic exchanges between the two countries.[2] Prior to the 2020 Sudanese military advance, Ethiopian farmers, primarily from the Amhara region, cultivated sesame on leased lands in Al-Fashaga, exporting yields through Sudanese ports and markets, thereby supporting Ethiopia's foreign exchange earnings estimated in millions of dollars annually.[12] [13] The Sudanese Armed Forces' (SAF) occupation disrupted these routes, enabling SAF-aligned entities to capture sesame production and trade revenues, which have funded Sudan's ongoing civil war economy.[12] In terms of regional security, control over Al-Fashaga serves as a buffer against cross-border insurgencies and ethnic militias, given its proximity to volatile areas in Ethiopia's Tigray and Amhara regions as well as Sudan's eastern frontiers.[14] [15] The 2020-2021 clashes, coinciding with Ethiopia's Tigray War, allowed Sudan to exploit Ethiopian military distractions, deploying forces that drew in Eritrean support for Ethiopia and Egyptian backing for Sudan, heightening risks of proxy escalations.[2] [15] Ethiopia's restrained response has prioritized internal stabilization while viewing Al-Fashaga retention as essential for securing western borders against potential alliances between Sudanese forces and Ethiopian dissidents, thereby preventing broader Horn of Africa destabilization.[3] [4] The dispute amplifies tensions over shared water resources, including tributaries feeding the Nile system, indirectly linking to downstream concerns like Egypt's opposition to Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, though Al-Fashaga itself lacks direct dam infrastructure.[15] External powers' involvement, including arms supplies and diplomatic maneuvering, underscores the area's role in great-power competition within the Red Sea basin, where border control influences trade corridor viability and counter-terrorism efforts against groups operating across porous frontiers.[3] [16] Sustained conflict has reduced bilateral trade volumes by severing pre-2020 "soft border" arrangements, exacerbating food insecurity and economic pressures in both nations amid regional instability.[14] [2]

Conflicting Territorial Claims

Sudanese Claims and Justifications

Sudan's primary legal justification for sovereignty over Al-Fashaga rests on the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, a colonial-era agreement between Britain and Ethiopia that delineated the border along the Tekezze and Atbara rivers, positioning the 250-square-kilometer fertile triangle within what was then Anglo-Egyptian Sudan.[9] [3] Sudanese authorities interpret the treaty's wordingâ€”specifying the boundary "to the east" of certain landmarksâ€”as explicitly excluding Al-Fashaga from Ethiopian territory, a demarcation reinforced by subsequent colonial surveys and maps from the British administration.[17] [18] Complementing this treaty-based claim, Sudan emphasizes its historical exercise of administrative control over the region since the early 20th century, including taxation, security patrols, and land allocation to Sudanese farmers, while tolerating seasonal Ethiopian Amhara cultivation under informal agreements that did not confer ownership.[3] A 2007 bilateral memorandum permitted continued Ethiopian farming access but explicitly affirmed Sudanese sovereignty, which Khartoum views as binding despite Ethiopia's non-ratification of the original 1902 treaty.[19] Sudanese officials argue that effective control, rather than mere presence of cross-border herders or farmers, establishes de facto jurisdiction, aligning with principles of uti possidetis in post-colonial border stability.[2] In justifying recent military assertions, particularly the deployment of troops into Al-Fashaga starting in late 2020, Sudan frames these actions as defensive reclamation of undisputed territory amid perceived Ethiopian encroachments and internal chaos from the Tigray War, which weakened Addis Ababa's border enforcement.[15] Sudanese spokespersons, including military leaders, have repeatedly denied occupying foreign land, insisting instead on restoring pre-1990s status quo ante where Sudanese authority prevailed, and citing Ethiopian militia activities as provocations necessitating border fortification.[20] This position was reiterated in April 2016 when Sudan reaffirmed Al-Fashaga as "Sudanese land" in official statements, prioritizing national security and resource protection in the agriculturally vital area yielding sesame and sorghum exports.[21] Economic imperatives underscore these claims, as Al-Fashaga's loess soils support Sudan's agribusiness sector, justifying exclusion of foreign claimants to prevent revenue loss estimated in millions annually from illicit cross-border farming.[1] Sudanese justifications also invoke broader regional stability, arguing that unresolved ambiguities from colonial pactsâ€”accepted by both states at independenceâ€”favor treaty fidelity over revisionist interpretations that could destabilize Horn of Africa frontiers.[3] Khartoum seeks formal Ethiopian acknowledgment of its ownership while offering regulated access for Ethiopian farmers, positioning Sudan as the aggrieved party responding to Ethiopia's failure to demarcate or respect the 1902 line despite diplomatic overtures in the 2010s.[18]

Ethiopian Claims and Counterarguments

Ethiopia maintains that sovereignty over Al-Fashaga, also known as Metema in Ethiopian parlance, derives from pre-colonial imperial administration, where the region fell under Ethiopian governance prior to European encroachments.[4][7] Ethiopian authorities assert that Amhara settlers established continuous agricultural presence and effective control since the early 20th century, cultivating sesame and other crops across approximately 250 square kilometers of fertile land, with local administration and tax collection reinforcing de facto jurisdiction.[22][3] Legally, Ethiopia interprets the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty as delimiting the border along the main channel of the Setit River, positioning Al-Fashaga east of this natural feature and thus within Ethiopian territory, rather than adhering to the subsequent, unratified Gwynn line demarcation proposed by British surveyor Thomas Gwynn, which Sudan favors.[7][2] Ethiopian officials argue that the treaty's textual provisions, not extraneous maps rejected by Emperor Menelik II, govern the boundary, and that post-independence uti possidetis principles preserve Ethiopia's inherited frontiers intact.[7] They further contend that a 2008 memorandum of understanding facilitated joint farming arrangements without conceding sovereignty, allowing limited Sudanese access to peripheral areas while preserving Ethiopian primacy in core zones.[3] In countering Sudanese assertions, Ethiopia dismisses Khartoum's reliance on colonial-era documents as selective, noting Sudan's historical inability to enforce administration amid Ethiopian settler dominance and minimal border patrols until recent decades.[22] Ethiopian spokespersons characterize Sudan's December 2020 military incursion, which displaced over 10,000 Ethiopian farmers, as an opportunistic exploitation of Ethiopia's Tigray conflict rather than a legitimate reclamation, violating prior deconfliction protocols and international norms against unilateral border alterations.[9][3] Addis Ababa insists on bilateral demarcation via a dormant joint commission, rejecting Sudan's unilateral surveys and troop deployments as provocative, and highlights Ethiopia's restraint in 2021-2023 despite retaliatory capabilities to prioritize internal stabilization.[18][4]

Debates Over Sovereignty and International Law

Sudan's sovereignty claim over Al-Fashaga primarily derives from the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, which delineated the border between Anglo-Egyptian Sudan and Ethiopia, placing the fertile triangle within Sudanese territory, supplemented by the 1903 MacMichael Protocols that further clarified boundary demarcations. Sudanese officials maintain that these colonial-era agreements, ratified internationally, establish unambiguous legal title under principles of treaty law, rejecting Ethiopian interpretations that question their validity as products of unequal imperial bargaining. This position aligns with the broader African norm of respecting colonial boundaries to avert territorial revisions, as enshrined in the 1963 Organization of African Unity Charter, which prioritizes stability over historical revisionism. Ethiopia counters that the 1902 treaty's border mapping was imprecise and imposed without full regard to pre-colonial Ethiopian administrative control over Al-Fashaga, where Amhara settlers have exercised de facto possession for over a century, including tax collection and agricultural development. Ethiopian arguments invoke effective control as a counterweight to nominal legal title, asserting that long-term habitation and economic integration supersede outdated colonial lines, particularly since Ethiopia avoided full colonization and thus views such treaties as non-binding impositions rather than consensual delimitations. Critics of this stance, including Sudanese diplomats, contend it undermines international stability by favoring possession over juridical sovereignty, potentially encouraging irredentist claims elsewhere. A pivotal 2008 bilateral agreement saw Ethiopia formally acknowledge Sudanese sovereignty while securing permission for its farmers to continue cultivating approximately 250 square kilometers of Al-Fashaga land, effectively creating a pragmatic "soft border" arrangement amid mutual economic interests. However, this compromise unraveled post-2018 with political shifts in Ethiopia, where Amhara regional leaders denounced it as an unauthorized concession, reigniting demands for demarcation based on effective occupation rather than treaty lines. Sudanese forces reasserted control in November 2020, citing treaty precedence, while Ethiopia has pursued diplomatic channels without conceding ground. Under international law, the dispute highlights tensions between uti possidetis jurisâ€”preserving colonial borders as status quoâ€”and arguments for equitable adjustments via effective control or historical equity, though no binding arbitration like International Court of Justice referral has occurred. The African Union and IGAD have advocated joint technical committees for demarcation since 2021, emphasizing dialogue over unilateral assertions, but progress stalls amid domestic instabilities, with neither side yielding on core sovereignty interpretations. External actors, wary of escalation, prioritize de-escalation without endorsing claims, underscoring the challenge of enforcing treaty-based borders against entrenched local realities.

Prelude and Initial Tensions

Pre-2020 Arrangements and Disputes

Following the overthrow of Ethiopia's Derg regime in 1991, Ethiopian federal forces established effective administrative control over Al-Fashaga, enabling large-scale resettlement by Amhara farmers and integration of the area into Ethiopia's Amhara Region for governance and security purposes.[3] Sudanese authorities maintained formal sovereignty claims based on Anglo-Ethiopian treaties from 1902 and 1907, but in practice permitted cross-border seasonal farming by Sudanese tenants alongside Ethiopian cultivators under informal local understandings that avoided major confrontations.[4] This arrangement reflected a pragmatic "soft border" dynamic, where Sudanese herders and farmers accessed the fertile lowlands during the rainy season for crops like sesame, while Ethiopian security presence deterred encroachments.[14] In 2008, high-level bilateral discussions between Ethiopian and Sudanese officials yielded a compromise preserving the status quo: Sudan reaffirmed its territorial claims, while allowing Ethiopian farmers undisturbed access and cultivation rights, effectively endorsing continued Ethiopian de facto management to foster economic cooperation and prevent escalation.[3][14] This accord, though not formally demarcating boundaries, supported cross-border trade in agricultural goods, with Al-Fashaga contributing significantly to sesame exports for both nationsâ€”Ethiopia producing over 200,000 tons annually from the region by the mid-2010s.[2] Periodic minor incidents, such as localized disputes over grazing or water access, were resolved through ad hoc joint committees rather than military means, maintaining relative stability.[1] Tensions began intensifying from 2018 onward under Ethiopian Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's administration, which prioritized border area development through investor incentives and Amhara regional initiatives to expand mechanized farming and infrastructure, including road construction and land titling that encroached on areas traditionally used by Sudanese farmers.[9] Sudanese officials lodged diplomatic protests in 2019, accusing Ethiopia of violating the 2008 understanding by evicting Sudanese tenants, deploying heavy machinery to clear land, and erecting fences that restricted cross-border movementâ€”actions that displaced hundreds of Sudanese cultivators and prompted calls in Khartoum for boundary demarcation surveys.[3][9] Amhara nationalists, viewing Al-Fashaga as ancestral territory, supported these efforts, framing them as reclamation from historical neglect, while Sudan interpreted them as aggressive expansionism, straining bilateral ties despite ongoing talks in forums like the Tripartite Mechanism involving the African Union.[2] These disputes remained non-violent but highlighted underlying frictions over resource allocation, with Sudanese media reports documenting over 10 such eviction episodes between 2018 and mid-2020.[15]

Triggers Linked to Ethiopian Internal Conflicts

The outbreak of the Tigray War on November 4, 2020, between the Ethiopian federal government and Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF) forces diverted significant Ethiopian National Defense Force (ENDF) resources northward, weakening border defenses in the Al-Fashaga region.[23] With Ethiopian troops preoccupied by the escalating internal conflict, Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) initiated an incursion into disputed areas of Al-Fashaga starting in December 2020, expelling Ethiopian settlers and asserting control over approximately 250 square kilometers of fertile farmland previously farmed by Amhara Ethiopians under informal arrangements.[2] [24] This opportunistic advance by Sudan capitalized on Ethiopia's internal divisions, as Amhara regional militiasâ€”key actors in both the Tigray fighting and Al-Fashaga cultivationâ€”were redeployed to western Tigray fronts, leaving local Amhara farmers vulnerable without federal support.[3] The Tigray conflict's ethnic dimensions exacerbated the issue, with Amhara claims to Al-Fashaga intertwined with their territorial grievances against Tigrayan administration, fostering militia mobilization that strained Ethiopia's unified response to Sudanese moves.[15] Although Sudanese planning for border reinforcement predated the war, the Tigray crisis provided the immediate causal window, as evidenced by the timing of SAF deployments coinciding with peak ENDF commitments in Tigray, where over 600,000 troops were engaged by early 2021.[3] This linkage extended to refugee flows, with tens of thousands of Tigrayans crossing into Sudan, heightening Khartoum's securitization incentives amid its own domestic instability.[25] The resulting border clashes, including artillery exchanges in January 2021, underscored how Ethiopia's federal fragility amplified longstanding territorial ambiguities into active confrontation.[9]

Military Engagements

2020 Sudanese Advance and Ethiopian Response


In mid-December 2020, Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) advanced into the Al-Fashaga triangle, a fertile border area long disputed with Ethiopia, expelling Ethiopian settlers and militias who had controlled much of the territory for decades.[3][19] The move capitalized on Ethiopia's preoccupation with the escalating Tigray War, which had begun in November 2020, diverting Ethiopian federal resources northward.[3] Sudanese officials justified the deployment as reclaiming sovereign territory under historical maps from the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian boundary agreement, where Ethiopian farmersâ€”primarily Amharaâ€”had been farming without formal state administration.[3] Clashes intensified on December 15, 2020, when Ethiopian forces ambushed a Sudanese patrol in Al-Fashaga, killing four Sudanese officers and prompting Sudanese Prime Minister Abdalla Hamdok to condemn the attack on social media.[26] In response, Sudan escalated on December 19, launching a broader military operation that regained control over approximately half of the 400-square-kilometer area previously held by Ethiopian "shifta" gunmen and farmers, according to Sudanese military sources.[27][28] By late December, Sudanese troops had established positions across key sites, including the towns of Al-Qarora and Lakatay, effectively securing the triangle after 25 years of de facto Ethiopian occupation.[28] Ethiopia's immediate response was restrained, prioritizing the Tigray conflict over a full-scale border confrontation, with federal officials issuing diplomatic protests and summoning the Sudanese ambassador while affirming Al-Fashaga's inclusion in Ethiopian territory per bilateral maps and administrative records.[3] Amhara regional militias, viewing the area as ethnically Amhara land, engaged in sporadic firefights with Sudanese forces but lacked coordinated federal support, leading to the displacement of thousands of Ethiopian farmers.[2] Ethiopian Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's government emphasized dialogue and adherence to international law, avoiding escalation that could strain relations further amid internal divisions.[4] This approach reflected strategic calculations, as a two-front war risked exacerbating Ethiopia's domestic instability.[3]

2021 Counteroffensives and Stalemates

In January 2021, Ethiopian federal forces and Amhara regional militias intensified counteroffensives against Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) positions in Al-Fashaga, aiming to reverse Sudan's late-2020 territorial gains. These operations followed an initial ambush on December 15, 2020, by Amhara-backed Ethiopian elements against a Sudanese patrol in the Abu Tyour area, which killed several Sudanese officers and prompted Sudan to deploy additional brigades. Ethiopian reinforcements, including artillery and militia units, targeted Sudanese outposts in sub-districts such as Al-Qarora and Jebel Moya, resulting in sporadic clashes that claimed dozens of lives on both sides through mid-January.[29][10] Sudanese forces, bolstered by rapid mobilization of up to 10,000 troops under the 8th Infantry Division, repelled these advances, consolidating control over approximately 250 square kilometers of farmland by February 2021. Ethiopian efforts were hampered by the concurrent Tigray War, which diverted federal troops and Amhara militiasâ€”many of whom held dual roles in both conflictsâ€”leaving border defenses under-resourced. Skirmishes persisted into March, with reports of Ethiopian drone surveillance and artillery exchanges, but neither side achieved decisive breakthroughs, fostering a fragile stalemate characterized by entrenched positions and intermittent firing.[23][20][10] By mid-2021, the frontlines stabilized along de facto lines favoring Sudan, which administered the area through local committees and displaced over 5,000 Ethiopian farmers, primarily Amhara settlers. Ethiopian counteroffensives yielded limited territorial recovery, confined to peripheral highlands, while Sudanese logisticsâ€”supported by proximity to Gedaref Stateâ€”sustained their hold. This impasse reflected causal constraints: Ethiopia's internal preoccupation prevented escalation, while Sudan's military leadership leveraged the dispute for domestic cohesion amid its own transitional challenges. Occasional incidents, such as a July 2021 clash killing at least 20, underscored the volatility but reinforced mutual restraint to avoid full-scale war.[2][3]

2022 Border Incidents and Escalations

In June 2022, tensions in the Al-Fashaga region escalated following an incident on approximately June 22, when Sudanese authorities accused Ethiopian forces of capturing and executing seven Sudanese soldiers and one civilian near the border.[30][31] Sudanese military officials claimed the victims were taken during a routine patrol in the disputed area and subsequently killed, prompting vows of retaliation from Sudanese leadership.[32] In response, Sudan recalled its ambassador from Addis Ababa and Sudanese Armed Forces commander Abdel Fattah al-Burhan visited forward positions in Al-Fashaga to bolster troop morale.[33][34] Ethiopian officials denied direct involvement by federal forces, attributing the deaths to actions by local Amhara militia groups operating independently in the region, which have historical ties to farming communities in Al-Fashaga.[35][36] This denial aligned with patterns of decentralized control in the Ethiopian-controlled portions of the area, where Amhara regional forces and irregulars have maintained presence amid Ethiopia's internal conflicts.[18] On June 28, Sudanese forces launched a targeted assault on Ethiopian positions in Al-Fashaga, capturing several outposts and reporting the neutralization of Ethiopian troops, though specific casualty figures from both sides remained unverified and contested.[37][38] The operation marked a brief intensification of military engagements, with cross-border gunfire and artillery exchanges reported, but it did not escalate to full-scale invasion, partly due to ongoing diplomatic channels and mutual exhaustion from prior clashes.[39] These incidents reflected broader fragility in the border truce, with Sudanese advances in 2020-2021 having secured much of the fertile triangle, yet persistent Ethiopian and Amhara encroachments fueling sporadic violence throughout 2022.[40] By late 2022, hostilities subsided without formal resolution, as both nations prioritized internal crisesâ€”Sudan's political instability and Ethiopia's Tigray war aftermathâ€”over sustained confrontation.[23]

Post-2023 Developments and Restraint

Following the escalations of 2022, the Al-Fashaga dispute has seen limited military activity since 2023, characterized by mutual restraint amid pressing domestic crises in both Sudan and Ethiopia. Sudan's Armed Forces (SAF), engaged in a civil war against the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) since April 2023, have prioritized consolidating control over eastern border areas, including Al-Fashaga, to leverage its fertile agricultural landsâ€”particularly sesame productionâ€”for war funding, while avoiding broader confrontation with Ethiopia.[2] Ethiopia, grappling with ongoing internal conflicts such as the Fano insurgency in the Amhara region, has similarly focused on domestic stabilization rather than border offensives, framing its approach as a strategic long-game emphasizing diplomacy and regional stability over immediate retaliation.[4] Tensions persisted through sporadic accusations rather than open clashes. In July 2025, Sudanese officials, including spokesperson Mubarak Al-Nour, alleged Ethiopian-backed militias conducted incursions into Al-Fashaga, expelling Sudanese farmers and clearing land under military escort, exploiting Sudan's internal divisions.[4] Ethiopia denied these claims on July 7, 2025, via Ambassador Nebiat Getachew, attributing any activities to local dynamics and rejecting support for cross-border militias.[4] Independent monitoring reports corroborated reports of Ethiopian militia movements but noted no large-scale engagements, with low-level violence remaining contained.[41] This restraint stems from pragmatic calculations: Sudan's SAF lacks capacity for a multi-front war amid its civil conflict's displacement of over 10 million people, while Ethiopia seeks to secure Red Sea access and trade routes without alienating neighbors.[4] Diplomatic efforts, including African Union and IGAD calls for dialogue, have yielded no binding resolution, leading both parties to defer sovereignty claims indefinitely as of September 2025.[5] The disputed area's economic valueâ€”spanning over 1 million acres of arable landâ€”continues to incentivize de facto arrangements for local farmers, though formal land-sharing pacts from prior decades remain suspended.[2]

Key Actors and Internal Dynamics

Sudanese Military and Local Militias

The Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) spearheaded operations to reclaim the Al-Fashaga triangle starting in mid-December 2020, capitalizing on Ethiopia's focus on the Tigray conflict to deploy troops that expelled Ethiopian farmers and militias from disputed farmlands. This advance involved establishing military positions across approximately 250 square kilometers of the fertile border region, marking a shift from decades of de facto Ethiopian control.[3][2] By February 2021, SAF units had secured an estimated 95% of Al-Fashaga, including key agricultural zones, with Sudanese leader Lt. Gen. Abdel Fattah al-Burhan declaring the restoration of sovereignty after 25 years of Ethiopian occupation. Military sources reported the deployment of around 96 Sudanese soldiers in initial phases, supported by engineering units to fortify outposts and checkpoints. These efforts displaced thousands of Ethiopian settlers and dismantled informal Ethiopian administrative structures in the area.[1][28] In June 2022, the SAF responded to the alleged execution of seven Sudanese soldiers by Ethiopian forces with a targeted offensive, recapturing additional border points and neutralizing Ethiopian gunmen described as shifta (bandits) in Sudanese accounts. This operation underscored the SAF's strategy of combining regular infantry advances with artillery support to deter cross-border incursions.[37][39] Local militias on the Sudanese side have played a subordinate role compared to the SAF's professional forces, with operations primarily under centralized military command to avoid escalation into irregular warfare. Sudanese Arab pastoralist communities and farmers from adjacent regions, such as Gedaref state, have assisted through intelligence gathering and civilian defense patrols, but no large-scale autonomous militia formations have emerged. In contrast to Ethiopian Amhara irregulars, Sudanese local groups operate integrated with SAF units, focusing on protecting reclaimed lands from sporadic raids rather than offensive actions.[15][28] Following Sudan's 2023 civil war outbreak between SAF and Rapid Support Forces, SAF maintained dominance in Al-Fashaga, repelling reported Ethiopian-backed militia probes in 2025. Sudanese officials claimed these incursions exploited internal divisions, prompting reinforced SAF deployments without significant militia mobilization.[4]

Ethiopian Federal Forces and Amhara Militants

The Ethiopian National Defense Force (ENDF) has maintained a presence in the Al-Fashaga region to assert federal control over the disputed territory, particularly in response to Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) advances that began in December 2020.[3] These deployments intensified after SAF units, taking advantage of ENDF commitments in the Tigray War, occupied key areas and evicted thousands of Ethiopian farmers by January 2021.[10] Ethiopian federal authorities protested the incursions diplomatically while reinforcing border positions, leading to counteroffensives in mid-2021 that recaptured portions of the area and established a fragile stalemate.[9] Amhara regional forces and militants, including the Amhara Special Forces and irregular Fano groups, have played a prominent role alongside or independently of ENDF units, driven by ethnic Amhara claims to the fertile lands where Amhara farmers have cultivated sesame and other crops for decades.[9] These militants, often comprising local ethnic Amhara residents, engaged in direct clashes with SAF troops, such as skirmishes in early 2021 near Abu Toyour where Ethiopian militias repelled Sudanese patrols.[1] By 2023, amid escalating internal tensions between Amhara groups and the federal government, Fano militants continued border activities, including reported raids on Sudanese villages in July 2025 adjacent to Al-Fashaga, exacerbating cross-border volatility.[42] The interplay between ENDF and Amhara militants has been complicated by diverging priorities: federal forces prioritize national sovereignty and diplomatic resolution, while Amhara actors emphasize regional autonomy and historical administrative rights predating the 1970s border agreements.[6] Instances of coordination occurred during joint operations against Tigrayan forces spilling into border areas, but frictions arose as Amhara militants pursued aggressive reclamation amid ENDF's stretched resources from domestic insurgencies.[23] Casualty figures from these engagements remain disputed, with reports of dozens killed on both sides in 2021 incidents, underscoring the militants' role in sustaining Ethiopian presence despite federal restraint post-2023.[1]

Role of Local Farmers and Ethnic Factors

The fertile Al-Fashaga triangle, spanning approximately 250 square kilometers, has long been dominated by agricultural activities centered on sesame production, often termed "white gold" due to its economic value, with Ethiopian farmers of Amhara ethnicity comprising the majority of cultivators since the mid-20th century.[43] These Amhara settlers, numbering in the tens of thousands by the 2010s, established extensive farms under informal arrangements tolerated by Sudanese authorities until the 1990s, when Sudan began asserting stricter sovereignty claims, leading to periodic expulsions and land disputes exacerbated by seasonal crop cycles and water access.[6] Sudanese local farmers and pastoralists, primarily from ethnic groups such as the Funj and Arab communities in Gedaref state, have historically used the area for grazing and limited cropping, viewing Amhara encroachments as violations of territorial integrity and contributing to low-level skirmishes over farmland boundaries even before 2020.[24][12] Ethnic dimensions intensified the conflict, as Amhara farmers' presence intertwined with broader Ethiopian regional identities, particularly amid Amhara grievances over federal land policies during the Tigray war (2020â€“2022), prompting armed Amhara groups like Fano militias to defend or reclaim farming areas as ethnic patrimony.[3] Sudanese forces, supported by local militias from diverse ethnic backgrounds in eastern Sudan, framed their 2020 advanceâ€”expelling over 10,000 Amhara farmers by January 2021â€”as a reclamation from "illegal settlers," with ethnic framing portraying Amhara militants as "shifta" (bandits) invading Sudanese soil.[17][24] This ethnic polarization fueled cross-border raids, such as Amhara-led counteroffensives in 2021 that briefly recaptured sesame fields, while Sudanese locals benefited from redistributed lands but faced retaliatory destruction of crops and infrastructure.[2] Local farmers on both sides have played pivotal roles beyond cultivation, mobilizing as informal combatants or informants; Amhara farmers provided logistical support to Ethiopian federal and regional forces, leveraging kinship networks for intelligence, while Sudanese herders and smallholders aligned with the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) for protection against perceived Amhara expansionism, embedding the dispute in ethnic patronage systems that prioritize communal land rights over bilateral treaties.[3][43] Post-2021 stalemates saw partial returns of Amhara farmers under Ethiopian military escort, but ongoing ethnic mistrustâ€”rooted in Amhara claims of historical settlement versus Sudanese assertions of undivided sovereigntyâ€”has perpetuated hybrid warfare, with farmers' economic stakes amplifying recruitment into militias and hindering demobilization efforts.[4][6]

Reactions and Diplomatic Efforts

Regional and African Union Responses

The African Union responded to the initial Sudanese military advances into Al-Fashaga in late 2020 by urging dialogue and deploying mediation efforts. On February 18, 2021, an AU mediator arrived in Khartoum to discuss de-escalation and border resolution, amid heightened rhetoric between Addis Ababa and Khartoum.[44] The AU Commission emphasized restraint to avert escalation, framing the dispute within broader Horn of Africa stability concerns, though mediation yielded no immediate demarcation agreement.[3] In June 2022, following renewed skirmishes, the AU reiterated calls for both parties to cease military actions and pursue technical boundary talks, warning of risks to regional peace amid Ethiopia's Tigray conflict spillover.[18] These statements aligned with AU's broader mandate under the African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance, prioritizing peaceful dispute settlement, yet faced challenges from Sudan's insistence on sovereignty recognition and Ethiopia's claims to historical usage rights. The Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD), as the primary sub-regional body including Ethiopia and Sudan, advocated diplomatic channels to contain volatility. On June 29, 2022, IGAD issued a statement calling for a peaceful resolution to border clashes in Al-Fashaga, urging joint mechanisms for verification and demilitarization.[45] IGAD's efforts built on prior summits, including proposals for bilateral committees, but were hampered by internal divisions and the organization's perceived waning influence in Horn diplomacy.[46] Neighboring states like Eritrea expressed tacit support for Ethiopia's position, while Egypt linked Al-Fashaga concerns to Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam negotiations, indirectly amplifying regional pressure without formal IGAD alignment.[47] By 2023, both AU and IGAD responses shifted toward monitoring de facto restraint, as domestic crises in Sudan and Ethiopia deferred active escalation.[5]

International Involvement and Mediation Attempts

The African Union (AU) has engaged in diplomatic efforts to mitigate the Al-Fashaga dispute, primarily through calls for restraint and de-escalation. In February 2021, AU Special Envoy Mohamed Hassan Lebatt visited Khartoum to press Sudan and Ethiopia toward a political resolution, emphasizing dialogue over military confrontation, though the AU lacked a dedicated mediation mandate for the border issue.[48] The AU High-Level Implementation Panel, chaired by former South African President Thabo Mbeki, holds a broader mandate on regional conflicts but has received limited backing from Sudanese authorities amid their transitional challenges.[48] Sudan indicated openness to third-party mediation predicated on maintaining the post-2020 status quo of Sudanese control over the disputed territory.[48] Proposals emerged from South Sudan, Turkey, and the United Arab Emirates (UAE), but these initiatives faltered without mutual endorsement from Addis Ababa and Khartoum, hampered by mutual distrust and domestic political pressures.[48] In April 2021, Sudanese and Ethiopian officials convened in Abu Dhabi under UAE auspices, where the host nation floated an investment scheme in Al-Fashaga's agriculture to foster economic incentives for demarcation and joint management, though no binding commitments materialized.[49] By 2022, a UAE-backed proposal reportedly envisioned Sudanese Armed Forces withdrawal to pre-September 2020 lines in exchange for Emirati funding of farming ventures, with revenue sharing, but it encountered resistance over sovereignty concerns.[50] Egypt's involvement has intensified bilateral frictions, with Ethiopia accusing Cairo of stoking the conflict via a January 2021 military cooperation agreement and defense pact with Sudan, which includes joint training and arms transfers supportive of Sudanese operations in Al-Fashaga.[48] [15] Egyptian backing aligns with broader Nile River interests, including opposition to Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, yet has drawn criticism for prioritizing geopolitical leverage over neutral facilitation.[48] Broader international actors, including the United Nations and European Union, have issued generalized appeals for stability in the Horn of Africa but mounted no targeted mediation for Al-Fashaga, reflecting the dispute's framing as a bilateral sovereignty matter amid overlapping regional crises like Sudan's civil war and Ethiopia's internal conflicts.[47] Efforts remain constrained by hardline stancesâ€”Sudan's insistence on full administrative control and Ethiopia's rejection of pre-1950s concessionsâ€”yielding no demarcation progress as of late 2023, despite intermittent leader-level detentes.[3] [18]

Consequences and Future Outlook

Humanitarian and Displacement Effects

In late 2020, Sudanese forces advanced into the disputed Al-Fashaga triangle, evicting thousands of predominantly Amhara Ethiopian farmers who had cultivated the fertile lands for decades under informal arrangements.[3][2] These displacements, peaking in early 2021 amid Ethiopia's Tigray conflict, forced returnees to resettle in Ethiopia's Amhara region, exacerbating local resource strains without triggering large-scale refugee inflows into Sudan.[24] Sudanese authorities justified the evictions as reclaiming sovereign territory, but the abrupt operations disrupted cross-border agrarian communities accustomed to shared farming practices.[3] Clashes between Sudanese troops and Ethiopian militias or federal elements have resulted in limited but recurrent casualties, with Sudanese reports citing at least a dozen military personnel killed in border incidents by mid-2021, including four in a single ambush.[3] Exact figures remain unverified due to restricted access and mutual accusations, though no evidence indicates mass civilian deaths or widespread injuries tied directly to the dispute.[3] Recent militia raids into Sudanese villages near Al-Fashaga in July 2025 caused property damage but no reported fatalities or further displacements.[51] The conflict's humanitarian footprint has remained contained compared to regional wars like Tigray, with Al-Fashaga hosting pre-existing Ethiopian refugee camps from that crisis rather than generating new ones from the border tensions.[52] Persistent low-level skirmishes risk amplifying vulnerabilities for local Sudanese farmers regaining access to the area, potentially compounding food insecurity in Gedaref state amid Sudan's broader civil war, though dedicated aid responses to Al-Fashaga-specific needs have been minimal.[2]

Economic Disruptions and War Economy Ties

The Al-Fashaga conflict has inflicted significant disruptions on cross-border trade and agriculture along the Ethiopia-Sudan frontier. The Metema-Gallabat border crossing, a vital conduit for commerce, was closed from late 2020 until 2022 due to escalating tensions and the Sudanese Armed Forces' (SAF) takeover of the disputed area, resulting in civilian traffic plummeting from 153 crossings in 2020 to only 15 in 2022.[2] Militarization intensified with the erection of 12 checkpoints between 2021 and 2023, elevating transport costs, risks, and delays for goods movement, while prompting a surge in smuggling activities that distorted local commodity prices.[2] Agricultural output in Al-Fashaga, a fertile triangle prized for sesame and other cash crops, faced acute setbacks from the SAF's incursion starting in late 2020, which displaced thousands of Ethiopian farmers previously operating under informal mid-2000s land-sharing pacts.[2] By 2022, the SAF had secured control over roughly 95% of the territory, including through the construction of four bridges and seven military outposts, leading to farmland abandonment and rerouted sesame supply chains that hampered production cycles.[2] Ongoing hostilities have threatened key planting windows, such as the late May to mid-July sesame season, exacerbating food insecurity and economic losses for local producers on both sides.[12] These disruptions intersect with war economy dynamics, as rival factions exploit Al-Fashaga's resources to sustain operations. Sesame revenues, derived from the region's contested farmlands, have become a linchpin in Sudan's conflict financing, with SAF consolidating economic dominance to capture crop profits amid the broader civil war that erupted in April 2023.[12][53] Smuggling of sesame into Ethiopia, often facilitated by Amhara militias, alongside arms and human trafficking networks traversing the porous border, perpetuates a cycle where illicit gains reinforce military entrenchment and ethnic militancy.[2][54] This resource-driven competition prolongs instability, as control over arable land yields tangible fiscal incentives for sustained belligerence rather than resolution.[12]

Prospects for Resolution and Persistent Risks

Sudan's ongoing civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and Rapid Support Forces (RSF), which erupted in April 2023 and continued into 2025, has diverted resources from enforcing border claims in Al-Fashaga, effectively deferring active resolution efforts with Ethiopia amid mutual domestic instability.[5] Ethiopia, grappling with internal conflicts in Amhara and Oromia regions following the 2022 Tigray peace accord, has adopted a strategy of restraint, avoiding large-scale military re-engagement despite Sudanese accusations of supporting local militias.[4] Bilateral talks, historically mediated by the African Union and Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD), remain stalled, with no verified agreements post-2021 Sudanese advances into the triangle, as trust deficits persist from Ethiopia's northern war and Sudan's perceived opportunism.[3] Prospects for demarcation hinge on stabilizing Sudan's internal front, potentially enabling renewed joint boundary commissions under 2008 frameworks, though Ethiopia's emphasis on effective occupation by Amhara farmersâ€”rooted in pre-1950s cultivationâ€”clashes with Sudan's colonial-era treaty claims, complicating legal arbitration.[2] External actors like Egypt and Eritrea, aligned variably with Sudan against Ethiopian interests in the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD), could incentivize de-escalation through backchannel diplomacy if Nile water-sharing talks advance, but as of September 2025, no such linkage has materialized into concrete proposals.[55] Persistent risks include sporadic militia incursions, such as Ethiopian Amhara groups raiding Sudanese border villages in July 2025, which Sudanese officials attributed to Addis Ababa-backed destabilization amid Khartoum's war economy reliance on Al-Fashaga's sesame and sorghum exports for funding.[51] These actions exacerbate refugee flowsâ€”over 100,000 Sudanese displaced into Ethiopia by mid-2025â€”and heighten spillover from Sudan's conflict, where SAF control weakens, allowing non-state actors to exploit the porous 250 kmÂ² fertile zone.[55] Broader escalation looms if Sudan's war resolves in favor of one faction, potentially reigniting full Sudanese mobilization, or if Ethiopia's federal forces prioritize border reclamation post-internal pacification, drawing in Eritrean proxies or Egyptian support and risking a Horn-wide conflagration.[56] Economic incentives, including Al-Fashaga's role in sustaining Sudan's arms procurement via agricultural revenues, further entrench zero-sum dynamics absent enforced demilitarization.[2]
