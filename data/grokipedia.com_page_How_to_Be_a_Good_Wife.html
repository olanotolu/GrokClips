How to Be a Good Wife


How to Be a Good Wife (French: La Bonne Épouse) is a 2020 French comedy-drama film written and directed by Martin Provost.[1] The story is set in 1967 Alsace, France, at a private housekeeping school where adolescent girls are trained in domestic skills and submissive wifely behaviors to prepare for marriage and motherhood.[2] Starring Juliette Binoche as Paulette Van der Beck, the school's founder and headmistress, the film portrays her efforts to maintain the institution's traditional curriculum—emphasizing meal preparation, household management, and unquestioning devotion to husbands—against financial pressures and the encroaching influences of the sexual revolution and women's liberation movements.[1] Supporting cast includes Yolande Moreau, Noémie Lvovsky, and Édouard Baer, with the narrative highlighting tensions between established gender norms rooted in post-World War II family structures and emerging challenges to female subordination.[3] Premiering at the Venice Film Festival, the film received mixed critical reception for its satirical take on 1960s domestic ideology, achieving a 67% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on limited reviews.[2]

Production Background

Development and Script

The screenplay for How to Be a Good Wife was co-written by director Martin Provost and Séverine Werba, drawing from the historical context of France's écoles ménagères (home economics schools) that trained rural women in domestic skills such as budgeting, mending clothes, cooking, and cleaning during the mid-20th century.[4] These institutions, which peaked in popularity post-World War II but began declining by the late 1960s amid emerging gender equality movements, provided Provost with a framework to explore rigid patriarchal norms in Alsace-Moselle on the German border.[4] Provost cited personal reflections on his mother's submissive role in a male-dominated household as a catalyst, aiming to satirize the era's confinement of women to homemaking roles just before the May 1968 protests and legal reforms like the 1965 law allowing married women to work without spousal permission and the 1967 contraceptive pill legalization.[4] Development began prior to 2019, with the project promoted internationally at the American Film Market that November, where Memento Films International showcased promo reels highlighting its 1960s setting and lead casting considerations.[5] Pre-production decisions emphasized tailoring the script's dialogue-heavy structure and choreographed sequences to suit performers like Juliette Binoche, focusing on a transition from mechanical domestic routines to more fluid expressions without delving into interpretive themes.[4] Provost and Werba structured the writing process around authentic period practices from these schools, incorporating elements of humor derived from the absurdity of enforced subservience, while planning principal photography at the preserved site of a former école ménagère in Bitche to maintain historical fidelity.[4] This phase aligned with early 2020 preparations, navigating initial COVID-19 disruptions before filming commenced in July.[6]

Casting and Crew

Juliette Binoche was selected for the central role of Paulette Van der Beck, leveraging her established reputation in French and international cinema for depicting nuanced female protagonists in transformative narratives.[1] Her prior work in films requiring emotional depth and period authenticity, such as Chocolat (2000) set partly in the mid-20th century, aligned with the character's arc from conformity to independence.[2] Director Martin Provost, known for character-driven stories centered on women like in Séraphine (2008), chose Binoche to anchor the film's exploration of mid-20th-century gender roles.[7] The supporting cast featured French actors with strong pedigrees in ensemble and dramatic roles to foster authentic interpersonal dynamics reflective of the era's social structures. François Civil portrayed Bob, drawing on his experience in contemporary French productions emphasizing relational tensions, such as Le Chant du loup (2019).[8] Other key roles included Yolande Moreau as Gilberte Van der Beck, Noémie Lvovsky as Marie-Thérèse, and Édouard Baer as André Grunvald, selected for their versatility in portraying multifaceted supporting characters within French cinematic traditions.[1] Key crew included cinematographer Guillaume Schiffman, who partnered with Provost to craft visuals capturing the film's 1960s-1970s aesthetic through period-appropriate lighting and composition.[9] Schiffman's prior collaborations on visually evocative projects contributed to the authentic recreation of the story's Alsatian setting and domestic interiors.[10] The production team emphasized expertise in historical accuracy to support the narrative's focus on evolving societal norms without modern anachronisms.[7]

Filming and Technical Details

Principal photography for How to Be a Good Wife occurred primarily in the Alsace region of France, with key locations including the village of Bergheim in Haut-Rhin for urban scenes such as city walls and the Fuchs café on Rue de la Poudrière, and Le Hohneck in Stosswihr for mountain top sequences.[11] Shooting took place from July 3 to August 30, 2019, capturing the film's 1960s setting through on-location work supplemented by constructed period sets to evoke the housekeeping school environment.[6] The film's technical specifications include a runtime of 109 minutes, shot in color with a 2.39:1 aspect ratio to emphasize wide domestic and communal spaces.[1] Costume designer Madeline Fontaine crafted outfits reflecting conservative 1960s French provincial styles, including modest dresses and aprons for the school's instructors and students, which contributed to the film's César Award win for Best Costume Design in 2021.[12] Grégoire Hetzel composed the original score, incorporating orchestral elements to underscore the narrative's interpersonal dynamics within household settings.[13]

Narrative Structure

Plot Summary

In 1968, at the Van der Beck School of Housekeeping and Good Manners in Alsace, France, Paulette Van der Beck oversees the instruction of teenage girls in traditional domestic skills, including cooking, sewing, budgeting, child-rearing, and techniques for pleasing a husband sexually.[14] [15] The curriculum emphasizes preparing students to marry wealthy local men by embodying subservient wifely virtues.[16] Following the sudden death of her husband Robert from a heart attack, Paulette uncovers the school's severe financial debts, threatening closure.[1] [17] To avert bankruptcy, Paulette collaborates with her staff to modernize the program, incorporating practical classes such as self-defense and automobile maintenance to appeal to prospective enrollees.[18] [19] Concurrently, the students secretly tune into the feminist pirate radio station Bobonne 88, hosted by Gilberte, which broadcasts ideas challenging conventional gender roles.[20] [14] These transmissions coincide with the escalating May 1968 protests across France, infiltrating the school's insulated environment and prompting the girls to question their prescribed futures.[17] [21] As disruptions mount, including student rebellions against outdated lessons and encounters with protest influences, Paulette navigates tensions between preserving the school's core mission and embracing emerging notions of female autonomy.[22] The narrative culminates in the institution's survival through hybrid adaptations, with Paulette achieving personal financial independence via a bank account and rekindling a past romance with the institution's banker, setting the stage for evolution into the 1970s.[18] [20]

Characters and Performances

Juliette Binoche portrays Paulette Van der Beck, the dedicated headmistress of a housekeeping school in 1960s France, where she teaches adolescent girls skills in cooking, cleaning, and subservience to male authority figures.[1] Her performance centers on Paulette's adherence to the school's rigid protocols, guiding daily lessons and enforcing discipline among pupils.[2] François Berléand enacts Gilles Van der Beck, Paulette's spouse and the school's financial overseer, whose presence reinforces hierarchical gender dynamics through interactions with staff and students.[1] Édouard Baer plays André Grunvald, a local businessman exerting external influence on the institution's operations and curriculum.[1] The ensemble of students, including Anamaria Vartolomei as Duchesse Des-deux-Ponts, Marie Zabukovec as Fuchs, and Lily Taïeb as Ziegler, populate group sequences depicting classroom routines and communal activities such as meal preparation and etiquette drills.[23] Supporting instructors like Yolande Moreau as Gilberte Van der Beck and Noémie Lvovsky as Sister Marie-Thérèse contribute to the collective portrayal of the school's environment, facilitating scenes of collective instruction and interpersonal exchanges.[1]

Release and Commercial Performance

Premiere and Distribution

The film La Bonne Épouse received early festival screenings in France, including at Antony on January 15, 2020, and at events such as Drôle d'endroit pour des rencontres and Rueil Film Festival on January 31, 2020.[24] Its national theatrical premiere in France occurred on March 11, 2020, distributed by Memento Films across approximately 650 cinemas.[14] The rollout was disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic, prompting theaters to close shortly after, which limited initial audience access.[25] A re-release in French theaters followed on June 22, 2020, as cinemas reopened amid ongoing restrictions.[3] Internationally, the film entered the festival circuit under the English title How to Be a Good Wife, with screenings at events such as the Luxembourg City Film Festival on March 12, 2020, and Cinéfest Sudbury in Canada on September 20, 2020.[16] Distribution beyond France remained limited, relying on subtitled presentations for non-French-speaking markets, which posed challenges in conveying the film's nuanced dialogue on 1960s gender norms.[18] Subsequent availability shifted to streaming platforms, with broader international access emerging post-theatrical windows, including a noted streaming release in select markets by March 2023.[2] This phased approach prioritized festival exposure and domestic theatrical runs before expanding digitally, reflecting the era's pandemic-constrained exhibition landscape.[22]

Box Office and Financial Outcomes

La Bonne Épouse opened in France on March 11, 2020, generating 80,296 admissions on its first day across 606 theaters.[26] The film's theatrical run was abruptly curtailed by COVID-19 lockdowns, with French cinemas closing nationwide on March 15, 2020, after only four days of release.[27] Upon partial reopening on June 22, 2020, it regained momentum, leading the domestic box office in late June and early July, including 99,217 admissions for the week ending July 4.[28] [29] The film ultimately tallied 632,661 admissions in France, equivalent to an estimated gross of approximately €4.4 million based on the era's average ticket price of around €7.[3] [30] This outcome positioned it as a mid-tier performer among French releases during a year when the national box office contracted sharply due to pandemic restrictions, with total admissions falling by over 70% compared to pre-2020 levels.[31] International earnings remained negligible, confined to select arthouse markets with no significant wide distribution, reflecting the film's niche appeal and timing amid global theater disruptions.[32] Production budget details are not publicly disclosed, precluding direct profitability assessment, though comparable mid-range French comedies from the period often required 500,000–1 million admissions for theatrical break-even before ancillary revenues.[33] The pandemic's suppression of attendance—French cinemas operated for only about 200 days in 2020—likely diminished potential returns, as evidenced by broader industry data showing arthouse titles struggling more than blockbusters.[34] No verifiable data on streaming or home video revenues is available to supplement theatrical figures.

Critical and Public Reception

Reviews and Analysis

The film received mixed reviews from critics, earning a 67% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 12 reviews.[2] Audience reception was similarly moderate, with an average rating of 5.8 out of 10 on IMDb from over 2,400 user votes.*[1] Critics frequently praised lead actress Juliette Binoche's comedic performance as Paulette Van der Beck, highlighting her ability to blend earnestness with emerging self-awareness in a period satire.*[17] [7] French reviewers appreciated the film's humor derived from domestic satire, noting well-timed situational comedy in depicting 1960s housekeeping school absurdities.[14] *[15] International critics, however, often critiqued the narrative for predictability, particularly in its feminist character arc, describing the humor as hit-or-miss and the overall tone as uneven or campy.*[2] [35] Some observed cultural specificity limiting broader appeal, with the film's ironic take on traditional gender roles feeling overly didactic or resolved in a puzzling manner.[7] *[36]

Awards and Recognition

La bonne épouse garnered recognition primarily within French cinema circles, earning five nominations at the 46th César Awards held on March 12, 2021, for films released in 2020.[37] Juliette Binoche, in the lead role of Paulette Van der Beck, did not receive a Best Actress nomination, though the film's supporting performances were acknowledged: Noémie Lvovsky and Yolande Moreau were both nominated for Best Supporting Actress for their portrayals of Marie-Thérèse and Gilberte Van der Beck, respectively, while Édouard Baer earned a Best Supporting Actor nomination for André Grunvald.[38][39] The film secured one win at the César Awards: Best Costume Design for Madeline Fontaine's period-appropriate designs evoking 1960s domesticity.[40][41] None of the acting nominations resulted in victories, with awards going to competitors in Adolescentes and DNA.[38] Beyond the Césars, La bonne épouse had limited accolades at international festivals, with screenings at events like the Zurich Film Festival—where Binoche received a personal Golden Icon Award on September 30, 2020, coinciding with the film's presentation—and the Sarasota and Calgary International Film Festivals in 2021, but no competitive wins reported.[42][9] The absence of major global honors, such as Academy Award nominations, underscores its niche appeal within European arthouse comedy rather than broader mainstream validation.[39]

Themes and Historical Context

Depiction of Gender Roles


The film portrays traditional gender roles through the Van der Beck School of Housekeeping and Good Manners, a fictional institution in 1960s Alsace where adolescent girls receive training to embody ideal wifely virtues. Curriculum emphasizes domestic proficiency, including meticulous meal preparation such as properly cooking a chicken, prudent household budgeting, and personal grooming to ensure spousal satisfaction.[15] These lessons frame homemaking as the pinnacle of female fulfillment, promoting unwavering obedience to husbands and self-sacrifice for family harmony as essential to marital success.[2] This scripted ideal of submissive domesticity is visually reinforced by recurrent motifs of enclosed spaces, particularly kitchens where students don aprons while performing chores, symbolizing spatial and societal confinement to the private sphere.[15] The narrative contrasts these teachings with emerging disruptions, as external influences from the 1968 protests infiltrate the school, prompting characters like headmistress Paulette to interrogate the prescribed submissiveness.[2] Students and staff gradually exhibit defiance, challenging the notion that wifely duties preclude personal agency or intellectual pursuits.[14] Through Paulette's evolving perspective following her husband's death, the film subverts the initial role model by depicting her pursuit of financial independence and solidarity among women, thereby highlighting tensions between entrenched expectations and aspirations for autonomy.[1] This progression underscores a narrative arc where rigid gender prescriptions yield to questioning, without resolving into outright rejection within the school's confines.[43]

Empirical Realities of Traditional Marriage

In the mid-20th century, when traditional marriage roles—characterized by male breadwinning and female homemaking—predominated, U.S. divorce rates remained low, with crude rates hovering around 2.1 to 2.5 per 1,000 population in the 1950s and early 1960s, compared to peaks of over 5 per 1,000 in the 1980s and a current rate of approximately 2.3 per 1,000 but with lifetime dissolution risks for recent cohorts estimated at 40-50%.[44][45] The divorce rate per 1,000 married women aged 18-64 doubled from 11 in 1950 to 23 by 1990, reflecting a shift away from specialized roles toward egalitarian models amid no-fault divorce laws and rising female labor participation.[46] Longitudinal data indicate that role specialization contributed to stability by minimizing conflicts over labor division, as couples benefited from comparative advantages in household production.[47] Children raised in stable two-parent homes, typical of traditional structures, exhibit superior outcomes across metrics including academic achievement, emotional regulation, and behavioral health, with studies showing lower incidences of problems compared to single-parent or unstable family environments.[48][49] The Harvard Study of Adult Development, tracking participants since 1938, underscores that enduring marital relationships—facilitated by family stability—predict long-term well-being and longevity more than socioeconomic status or IQ, with early marriage quality correlating to reduced midlife distress.[50][51] Marital satisfaction in traditional setups often exceeded modern egalitarian arrangements, as specialization allowed efficiency gains and reduced role overload; for instance, pre-1970s surveys revealed higher reported happiness among women in homemaker roles, preceding a relative decline in female subjective well-being documented since the 1970s amid workforce entry.[52] Economic models, such as Gary Becker's theory of household production, demonstrate that spousal complementarity—husbands specializing in market work and wives in home production—maximizes joint utility and lowers conflict by leveraging comparative advantages, leading to greater overall household output and individual fulfillment than dual-career strains.[47][53] Empirical evidence links such divisions to decreased stress, with husbands experiencing elevated cortisol when wives' earnings exceed 40% of household income, disrupting traditional provider norms.[54]

Feminist Influences and Critiques

The film's narrative arc culminates in protagonist Paulette's embrace of the May 1968 student protests, portraying them as a liberating force that awakens her to personal autonomy and challenges the subservient housewife ideal taught at her school. This pivot mirrors the historical stirrings of second-wave feminism in France, where the events of May '68—marked by widespread strikes, demands for social reform, and anti-authoritarian fervor—catalyzed women's groups advocating for reproductive rights, equal pay, and rejection of traditional domesticity.[55][56] While the film depicts this shift optimistically as unalloyed progress toward equality, it omits scrutiny of feminism's broader societal ramifications, including the erosion of stable family structures. Following the 1975 liberalization of divorce laws, which introduced no-fault options and eased proceedings, France's divorce rates surged, roughly doubling the lifetime divorce probability for cohorts marrying around 1975 compared to those in 1960.[57][58] This contributed to a rise in single-parent households, with nonmarital births climbing from negligible levels in the early 1970s to over 58% of total births by 2016, disproportionately affecting maternal-led families.[59] Such changes correlated with elevated child poverty risks, as OECD data indicate children in lone-parent households face poverty rates around 32%, nearly three times higher than in couple households, due to factors like reduced household income and limited parental time.[60][61] The film's focus on immediate emancipation sidesteps these causal links, where feminist-driven norms prioritizing individual autonomy over marital permanence have empirically heightened family instability without commensurate safeguards. Feminist influences did yield verifiable gains, such as the sharp post-1968 rise in women's labor force participation—from about 43% in 1968 to 75% for prime-age women by recent decades—fostering economic independence and narrowing gender wage gaps through policies like equal pay mandates.[62][63] Yet surveys reveal trade-offs, including work-life imbalances; studies estimate 8-17% of parents, with higher rates among mothers facing unmet expectations, report regretting parenthood amid persistent domestic burdens and career-family conflicts.[64][65] This ambivalence underscores the film's selective lens, privileging aspirational critique over data on unintended costs like maternal dissatisfaction.[66]

Cultural Impact and Debates

Influence on Media and Discourse

La Bonne Épouse (2020) has contributed to cinematic discourse on domesticity by depicting the transition from rigid gender roles in mid-20th-century France, echoing themes in earlier films like Mona Lisa Smile (2003), which similarly critiques instructional environments preparing women for subservient marital roles through a Wellesley College setting in 1953.[67] Both narratives highlight institutional efforts to instill traditional wifely virtues—such as homemaking and deference—against emerging feminist awakenings, though La Bonne Épouse grounds its portrayal in the specific historical closure of French écoles ménagères post-1968.[3] Following its March 2020 release, the film prompted French media to revisit the real-world foundations of such institutions, with outlets examining archival domestic manuals and curricula that prescribed behaviors like meticulous housekeeping and spousal obedience, thereby rekindling public curiosity about pre-1960s prescriptive literature on marital conduct. Articles linked the film's portrayal to historical texts emphasizing the bonne épouse as a supportive companion, drawing parallels to vanished training programs that enrolled thousands of girls annually until societal shifts rendered them obsolete by 1971.[68] Its subsequent availability on streaming services, including Prime Video and Plex as of 2023, has facilitated access for younger viewers exploring period-specific gender dynamics, with global platforms hosting the title across at least 14 services to broaden exposure beyond initial theatrical runs curtailed by the COVID-19 pandemic.[2] [69] This digital distribution has sustained conversations on evolving representations of domesticity in cinema, without claiming transformative influence on broader media trends.[70]

Conservative Perspectives and Counterarguments

Conservative critiques of portrayals emphasizing traditional wifely duties, as in the film's setup, contend that such depictions caricature these roles by ignoring their empirical contributions to family stability and broader societal cohesion. Robert Putnam's Bowling Alone (2000) empirically links the erosion of social capital—manifest in declining civic engagement and trust— to post-1960s family disruptions, including fewer intact nuclear households, which historically buffered communities against isolation and anomie through structured intergenerational ties.[71] This contrasts with pre-1960s patterns where stable marriages correlated with higher community participation rates, suggesting traditional roles were not mere relics but functional adaptations for long-term relational success. A core counterargument highlights causal evidence from no-fault divorce reforms, enacted in California in 1969 and nationwide by the mid-1980s, which eased marital exit without requiring proof of wrongdoing like adultery or abuse. U.S. divorce rates rose sharply from 2.2 per 1,000 population in 1960 to a peak of 5.3 in 1981, yielding lifetime risks exceeding 40% for first marriages versus under 20% in fault-based eras, with downstream effects including doubled child poverty rates and increased male suicide among the divorced.[72][73] These outcomes, conservatives argue, stem from diminished incentives for mutual investment, undermining the voluntary hierarchies that sustain partnerships over egalitarian individualism. Jordan Peterson, drawing on evolutionary psychology, posits that traditional family structures reflect competence-based hierarchies of responsibility—men as providers, women as nurturers—that mitigate chaos through biological realism, critiquing feminist-driven abandonments as fostering resentment and instability.[74] In 12 Rules for Life (2018), he advocates precise role fulfillment over ideological revolt, aligning with data showing higher marital satisfaction in couples adhering to complementary duties despite cultural shifts. Such views frame narrative rebellions against tradition as ahistorical, overlooking how pre-no-fault marital resilience supported economic and psychological well-being absent in modern fragmentation.
