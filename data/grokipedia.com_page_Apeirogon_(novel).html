Apeirogon (novel)

Apeirogon is a 2020 novel by Irish-American author Colum McCann that fictionalizes the experiences of two fathers—an Israeli and a Palestinian—who each lost a daughter to violence in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and forged a cross-border friendship dedicated to peace advocacy.[1][2] The work draws directly from the lives of Rami Elhanan, whose 13-year-old daughter Smadar was killed in a 1997 Jerusalem suicide bombing, and Bassam Aramin, whose 10-year-old daughter Abir died in 2007 from a rubber bullet fired by an Israeli border policeman, with both men co-founding the Parents Circle-Families Forum to promote dialogue among bereaved families.[3][4] Structured innovatively as an "apeirogon"—a polygon with infinitely many sides—the narrative unfolds across 1,001 short, non-linear chapters blending personal narratives, factual digressions on migration, birds, and checkpoints, and reflections on storytelling akin to One Thousand and One Nights.[5] Published by Random House on February 25, 2020, it garnered literary recognition including a Booker Prize longlisting and the National Jewish Book Award for Fiction, praised for its empathetic portrayal of shared grief amid enmity.[1][6][7] However, the novel provoked controversy, particularly from pro-Palestinian commentators who argued it imposed a false equivalence on unequal power dynamics, framing the conflict as symmetrical suffering while underemphasizing structural asymmetries like occupation and military disparity.[8][9]

Publication and background

Author and creation

Colum McCann, an Irish novelist born and raised in Dublin, authored Apeirogon.[10] McCann, recipient of the National Book Award for his 2009 novel Let the Great World Spin, drew on his familiarity with sectarian violence from the Irish Troubles in approaching the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[11] McCann first encountered the real-life inspirations for the novel's protagonists, Bassam Aramin and Rami Elhanan, through Narrative 4, a global storytelling organization he co-founded in 2015 to foster empathy via narrative exchanges.[12] The idea for Apeirogon originated after McCann heard Aramin and Elhanan speak together in November 2015 at a Narrative 4 event.[13] He spent subsequent years researching their lives, conducting interviews, and incorporating historical, artistic, and scientific elements into the work, which blends factual accounts with fictionalized narrative.[14] The novel, described by McCann as a "nonfiction novel," required approximately five years of development before its completion.[12]

Inspiration from real events

The novel Apeirogon is inspired by the real-life experiences of Bassam Aramin, a Palestinian activist from Anata, and Rami Elhanan, an Israeli graphic designer from Jerusalem, who each lost a daughter to violence amid the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and subsequently collaborated on peace advocacy.[2][15] Their stories form the backbone of the book's dual protagonists, with McCann structuring the narrative around their shared grief and dialogue efforts, drawing from extensive interviews and observations.[16] Elhanan's daughter Smadar, aged 13, was killed on September 4, 1997, during a Hamas-orchestrated suicide bombing on Ben Yehuda Street in Jerusalem, where three attackers detonated explosives, claiming 5 lives including Smadar's and injuring over 180 others.[17][18] The attack occurred as Smadar was shopping with friends, underscoring the random targeting of civilians in such operations.[19] Aramin's daughter Abir, aged 10, was struck by a rubber-coated steel bullet fired by an Israeli border policeman on January 16, 2007, outside her school in Anata during an incident involving stone-throwing by Palestinian youths at security forces; she succumbed to her head injuries three days later in Hadassah Hospital.[20][21] An Israeli court in 2010 ruled the state responsible for her death, citing failure to investigate adequately, and in 2011 ordered compensation of 1.25 million shekels to the family, though the shooter was never prosecuted.[22][21] Aramin and Elhanan connected shortly after Abir's death through the Parents Circle-Families Forum (PCFF), a grassroots organization founded in 1995 that unites over 600 bereaved Israeli and Palestinian families to foster dialogue, reject revenge, and promote nonviolent conflict resolution via joint lectures and projects.[23][24] As co-directors of PCFF initiatives, they have spoken together at hundreds of events worldwide, emphasizing empathy over political solutions.[25] McCann first encountered them in 2014 during a Narrative 4 exchange program he co-founded, which pairs individuals to swap personal stories, prompting him to research their lives deeply for the novel published in 2020.[16][15]

Form and structure

Narrative technique

Apeirogon employs a fragmented narrative structure divided into 1,001 numbered sections, ranging from single sentences to extended passages, photographs, and blank pages, evoking the storytelling frame of the Arabian Nights with its 1,001 nights.[26] This episodic form eschews linear chronology, instead pivoting around a central event—a 2016 meeting between the protagonists—while expanding through associative digressions into disparate topics such as historical events, scientific concepts, and cultural references.[26] The technique creates a kaleidoscopic effect, layering personal testimonies with broader contextual explorations to reflect the infinite complexity symbolized by the apeirogon, a polygon with countably infinite sides.[27] The novel blends documentary elements, including real interview transcripts from the protagonists, with authorial fiction, as McCann explicitly shapes their accounts in the author's note to forge a hybrid realism.[26] Nonlinear jumps mimic the workings of memory and grief, interspersing intimate family tragedies with tangential meditations on subjects like birds, medieval art, and political history, which serve as midrashic commentaries expanding the core narrative.[27] This mosaic-like assembly underscores thematic interconnections, presenting the Israeli-Palestinian conflict not as a binary opposition but as a web of multifaceted human experiences.[27] The structure functions meditatively and musically, with short fragments providing rhythmic pauses amid longer reflections, challenging readers to engage actively with the text's labyrinthine progression.[27] By prioritizing accumulation over resolution, the technique avoids didactic closure, instead fostering an ever-expanding exploration that parallels the protagonists' real-life activism through shared storytelling.[26] This approach, while ambitious, has been noted for its risk of fragmentation overwhelming cohesion, yet it aligns with the novel's aim to disrupt conventional empathetic narratives around violence and loss.[26]

Symbolic elements

The title Apeirogon refers to a polygon with a countably infinite number of sides, symbolizing the novel's depiction of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict as an endlessly complex phenomenon that resists reduction to opposing sides. Colum McCann describes the term as mirroring the book's structure, which allows readers to enter the narrative from multiple angles, emphasizing shared human experiences of loss and the disruption of simplistic narratives.[28][28] The division into 1,001 chapters draws from the framework of One Thousand and One Nights, where Scheherazade's sequential tales avert execution, representing storytelling as a vital means of preserving life, processing trauma, and bridging divides between the protagonists' worlds.[29] Birds recur as a motif evoking unrestricted migration and transcendence of barriers, contrasting sharply with the territorial restrictions, checkpoints, and walls that confine human movement in the story's setting.[30][31] Water symbolizes the fundamental interconnectedness of all life while highlighting resource scarcity and disputes, as seen in references to the diminished Jordan River and targeted destruction of Palestinian water infrastructure.[32] Personal artifacts like the candy bracelet Abir Aramin wore during her fatal shooting serve as haunting emblems of lost innocence, perpetually evoking the arbitrariness of violence for her father Bassam.[33] The daughters' names—Smadar, meaning "almond blossom" in Hebrew, and Abir, meaning "fragrance" in Arabic—evoke natural beauty and commonality in mourning, underscoring thematic unity amid ethnic division.[33]

Content overview

Protagonists' personal tragedies

In Apeirogon, the Israeli protagonist Rami Elhanan experiences profound loss when his 13-year-old daughter, Smadar, is killed in a Palestinian suicide bombing on Ben Yehuda Street in central Jerusalem on September 4, 1997.[34][35] Smadar was walking home from a shopping trip with friends when two bombers detonated explosives, killing her and four other civilians in the attack.[36][37] Rami, a graphic designer and former fighter pilot, learns of the incident while en route to the airport and rushes back to identify her body amid the chaos.[34] The Palestinian protagonist Bassam Aramin suffers a parallel tragedy when his 10-year-old daughter, Abir, is fatally shot in the head by an Israeli border policeman on January 16, 2007, outside her school in the East Jerusalem village of Anata.[22][38] Abir had just finished a math exam and was walking with her sister and friends to buy snacks when a rubber-coated metal bullet struck her from a Border Police jeep patrolling nearby during a minor demonstration; she succumbed to her injuries three days later in Hadassah Hospital.[39][40] An Israeli court later ruled the state responsible for her death, though no individual officer faced charges.[22][38] Bassam, a former militant who had served seven years in Israeli prison, discovers Abir's condition upon arriving at the scene and witnesses the inadequate initial response from authorities.[41]

Interwoven historical and factual digressions

The novel Apeirogon employs a fragmented structure of 1,001 numbered chapters—ranging from single sentences to extended passages—that intersperses the protagonists' personal narratives with concise, fact-based digressions on history, science, politics, and culture, often drawing parallels to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict's asymmetries and human costs.[26] These insertions, evoking W.G. Sebald's essayistic style, include blank pages, photographs, and factual vignettes that disrupt linear storytelling to mimic the conflict's disorienting repetition and infinity, as symbolized by the apeirogon's boundless sides.[42] Historical digressions reference specific conflict milestones, such as the 1997 suicide bombing on Jerusalem's Ben Yehuda Street during the Second Intifada, which killed eight civilians including protagonist Rami Elhanan's daughter Smadar, and the Yom Kippur War of 1973, in which Rami served as a soldier.[42] Political realities appear in accounts of Israeli checkpoints, exemplified by the hours-long delay of an ambulance carrying Bassam Aramin's critically injured daughter Abir in 2007, underscoring restrictions on Palestinian movement and medical access.[2] Weaponry features prominently, with chapters on the slingshot's ancient tactical use in assaults on fortified cities—evoking David versus Goliath—and modern adaptations like the M16 rifle or rubber bullets employed in crowd control.[7][42] Scientific and natural digressions provide tangential illumination, such as ornithological facts about frigatebirds remaining aloft for two months without landing or the illicit falcon trade in Bethlehem, positioning birds as metaphors for freedom transcending borders.[42] Broader cultural references include the Dead Sea Scrolls' discovery and 19th-century missionary expeditions from the Sea of Galilee to the Dead Sea, linking ancient artifacts to contemporary territorial disputes.[42] More starkly forensic elements detail the "mushroom effect" in suicide bombings, where the perpetrator's head detaches and travels intact, or the origins of Zyklon B, invoking historical precedents of industrialized violence.[42] These digressions, while factually grounded, serve to layer empirical context onto the fathers' grief, highlighting causal chains like Bassam's seven-year imprisonment for a teenage grenade attack on Israeli forces, without resolving the conflict's structural imbalances.[42] Critics note that such integrations risk aestheticizing violence by juxtaposing clinical details with personal tragedy, yet they underscore verifiable asymmetries, such as the over 1,000 Palestinian minors killed since 2000 per UN data cited in related activism.[43] The approach prioritizes raw data over narrative closure, reflecting the Parents Circle-Families Forum's emphasis on shared loss amid ongoing occupation dynamics.[14]

Themes and interpretations

Grief, forgiveness, and human connection

The novel portrays the profound grief experienced by protagonists Rami Elhanan and Bassam Aramin following the deaths of their daughters, Smadar Elhanan and Abir Aramin, which anchor the narrative's emotional core. Smadar, aged 14, was killed on September 4, 1997, during a suicide bombing in Jerusalem's Ben Yehuda Street, an attack that claimed five lives including hers.[44] Abir, 10 years old, died on April 16, 2007, after being struck by a rubber bullet fired by an Israeli border policeman near a school checkpoint in Anata, East Jerusalem.[13] McCann depicts their bereavement not as abstract sentiment but as a visceral, enduring force that reshapes identity, with non-linear vignettes capturing the raw immediacy of loss alongside its lingering distortions, such as Rami's compulsion to retrace Smadar's final steps and Bassam's confrontation with the asymmetry of accountability in Abir's case.[2] This shared parental anguish transcends national divides, illustrating grief's universality as a catalyst for empathy rather than perpetuated enmity.[45] Forgiveness emerges as a deliberate, arduous process in the novel, rooted in the real-life commitments of Elhanan and Aramin through the Parents Circle-Families Forum, an organization they helped sustain since 1995 to foster dialogue among bereaved families.[46] McCann attributes to them an active rejection of vengeance—Rami publicly absolving Palestinian perpetrators despite societal pressures, and Bassam extending understanding to Israeli actors amid his own community's skepticism—framing it as a causal break from cycles of retaliation driven by unexamined tribalism.[47] Such acts, drawn from their documented lectures and joint appearances, challenge deterministic views of trauma by emphasizing agency: forgiveness here demands confronting the "other's" humanity without excusing violence, as evidenced by their mutual acknowledgment of each side's narratives without equivalence.[48] Critics note this portrayal avoids sentimental resolution, instead highlighting forgiveness's pragmatic utility in mitigating further loss, though its rarity underscores institutional barriers like politicized commemorations that hinder cross-community reckoning.[12] Through these men's evolving bond, Apeirogon underscores human connection as an emergent property of sustained encounter amid antagonism, where grief and forgiveness dismantle dehumanizing abstractions. Elhanan and Aramin's real-world collaboration, including hundreds of talks since the early 2000s, models this by prioritizing personal testimony over abstract ideology, fostering incremental trust that McCann amplifies via the novel's mosaic structure of 1,001 fragments evoking Scheherazade's tales of survival through story-sharing.[49] This connection manifests causally: initial meetings in the Forum's support groups evolve into public advocacy, revealing shared vulnerabilities—like parallel experiences of state investigations yielding impunity—that humanize adversaries and counteract media-amplified polarization.[25] The narrative posits that such links, though fragile against broader conflict dynamics, derive potency from empirical precedents of reduced hostility in dialogue-based initiatives, offering a counter to zero-sum paradigms without denying the asymmetry of power or historical grievances.[44]

Portrayal of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict

Apeirogon frames the Israeli-Palestinian conflict primarily through the parallel personal losses of its protagonists, Bassam Aramin, a Palestinian whose 10-year-old daughter Abir was killed on January 16, 2007, by a rubber bullet fired by an Israeli border policeman near her school in Anata during a confrontation involving stone-throwing at security forces, and Rami Elhanan, an Israeli whose 13-year-old daughter Smadar was killed on September 4, 1997, in a suicide bombing by Palestinian terrorists at Jerusalem's Mahane Yehuda market amid the Second Intifada's wave of attacks.[50][35] The novel details these incidents with vivid, fragmented reconstructions drawn from the men's real testimonies, emphasizing the immediacy of the violence—Abir's fatal head wound from the projectile meant for crowd control, and Smadar's death alongside five others in the deliberate explosive assault targeting civilians.[42][2] The conflict's broader dynamics emerge through the novel's non-linear structure of 1,001 short sections, inspired by One Thousand and One Nights, which interlaces the fathers' grief-driven activism in the Parents Circle-Families Forum with factual digressions on related historical events, such as the Deir Yassin massacre referenced in Albert Einstein's 1948 letter protesting violence against Palestinians, the use of rubber bullets in crowd suppression, and the mechanics of suicide bombings.[50][42] These elements blend verifiable history with fictional embellishments, as McCann notes in his preface, to illustrate cycles of retaliation and the occupation's daily impositions, like checkpoints and restricted movement, while highlighting the men's joint lectures across divides to foster empathy.[50] Symbolic motifs, such as migratory birds indifferent to borders, underscore human interconnectedness amid entrenched enmity, avoiding explicit political advocacy in favor of personal testimony.[42] Critics have praised this approach for its even-handed humanization of both Israeli and Palestinian suffering, presenting the conflict not as abstract ideology but as devastating familial rupture that motivates cross-enemy solidarity, with the men's real interviews at the structural center providing raw authenticity.[51] However, others contend that the novel's emphasis on symmetrical grief—equating a terrorist bombing's intentional carnage with an errant non-lethal round fired in response to aggression—imposes false parity on an asymmetric reality marked by Israel's security measures against Palestinian-initiated violence and the latter's structural disadvantages under occupation.[8][52] Such critiques, often from pro-Palestinian outlets, argue it sidesteps power imbalances, like systemic dispossession and routine military enforcement, in favor of feel-good reconciliation narratives that obscure causal roots in rejectionist ideologies and failed peace processes, potentially sanitizing Israeli actions while reducing Palestinians to victims or extremists.[8] McCann's defenders counter that the work's aim is empathetic witness rather than geopolitical adjudication, drawing directly from the protagonists' lived experiences without imposing resolution.[53]

Real-life foundations

Bassam Aramin's background

Bassam Aramin was born and raised in the ancient city of Hebron in the West Bank, where he became involved in the Palestinian struggle against Israeli occupation from a young age, beginning at 13.[54] [55] As a teenager, he participated in stone-throwing incidents and, at age 17 in the early 1980s, was arrested by Israeli authorities for planning an attack on soldiers, including an attempt to throw a grenade.[54] [56] He served seven years in an Israeli prison, during which he endured harsh conditions but began educating himself by learning Hebrew and reading extensively about Jewish history, the Holocaust, and Israeli perspectives, leading to a profound shift toward non-violent resistance.[56] [57] [58] Upon release, Aramin co-founded Combatants for Peace, an organization uniting former Palestinian fighters and Israeli ex-soldiers to advocate jointly for ending the occupation through dialogue rather than violence.[57] He later studied history and became president of the Al Quds Association for Democracy and Dialogue, focusing on Palestinian-Israeli reconciliation efforts.[41] Aramin resides in Anata, near East Jerusalem, with his wife and six children.[55] Tragedy struck on January 17, 2007, when his 10-year-old daughter, Abir Aramin, was struck in the head by a rubber-coated metal bullet fired from an Israeli Border Police vehicle while walking home from school in Anata amid a demonstration; she died two days later in the hospital.[21] [22] An autopsy confirmed the cause of death as the projectile to her head, though initial Israeli police investigations closed the case without charges, citing inconclusive evidence; a civil court later ruled Israel responsible, awarding the family compensation in 2011 without admitting liability.[22] [21] Following Abir's death, Aramin joined the Parents Circle-Families Forum, a grassroots group of bereaved Palestinian and Israeli families promoting empathy and non-violence, where he serves as international spokesperson and co-director.[57] [59]

Rami Elhanan's background

Rami Elhanan was born in 1949 in Jerusalem as a seventh-generation Jerusalemite of Jewish descent.[60][36] His father survived Auschwitz concentration camp, while his maternal grandfather, Matti Peled, was an Israeli general who commanded forces in the 1948 Arab-Israeli War and later advocated for peace and Palestinian rights as a Knesset member and professor.[36][61] Elhanan served as a reserve soldier during the 1973 Yom Kippur War, fighting in the Sinai Peninsula, where he lost comrades and developed a sense of anger and cynicism toward the conflict.[36] By profession, he is a graphic designer.[36] He married Nurit Peled-Elhanan, daughter of Matti Peled, and they had four children, including a daughter named Smadar, born on the eve of Yom Kippur in 1983.[36] On September 4, 1997, Smadar, then 13 years old, was killed in a suicide bombing carried out by two Palestinian militants on Ben Yehuda Street in central Jerusalem, an attack that also claimed four other lives, mostly teenage girls.[36][34] Elhanan learned of the incident while en route to the airport and later attended her funeral at Kibbutz Nachshon, where she was buried beside her grandfather Matti Peled.[36] In the aftermath, Elhanan initially grappled with rage but, after meeting bereaved Israeli parent Yitzhak Frankenthal—whose son was killed by Hamas in 1994—joined the Parents Circle-Families Forum around 1999 to promote dialogue between bereaved Israelis and Palestinians as a means to counter the cycle of violence.[36][61]

Role of the Parents Circle-Families Forum

The Parents Circle–Families Forum (PCFF) is a joint Israeli-Palestinian grassroots organization comprising over 800 bereaved families who have lost immediate relatives due to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, focusing on dialogue, storytelling, and advocacy for reconciliation and non-violence.[62] Founded to counter cycles of retaliation through personal encounters, the group organizes events, school programs like "Listening from the Heart," and international speaking tours to humanize victims on both sides and promote empathy over enmity.[63][64] In Apeirogon, the PCFF serves as the pivotal institution uniting protagonists Bassam Aramin and Rami Elhanan following their daughters' deaths—Aramin's Abir in 2007 and Elhanan's Smadar in a 1997 suicide bombing—transforming individual grief into collaborative activism.[65][14] The novel depicts their initial meeting through the forum shortly after Aramin's loss, where shared bereavement fosters an unlikely friendship, enabling joint lectures worldwide that interweave personal testimonies with calls for mutual recognition and cessation of violence.[13][66] McCann portrays the PCFF not as a political entity but as a humanizing force, emphasizing its members' rejection of revenge—Aramin, a former militant, and Elhanan, a veteran—through raw, unfiltered narratives that challenge entrenched narratives of perpetual victimhood.[12][46] The forum's activities anchor the novel's polyphonic structure, with digressions mirroring real PCFF talks that blend factual accounts of loss, historical context, and appeals for dialogue, underscoring themes of forgiveness amid asymmetry in the conflict.[67] Aramin and Elhanan, as co-directors, exemplify the organization's model of "encounter," where bereaved families disrupt hatred by prioritizing the "enemy's" pain, a dynamic McCann elevates as a counter to dehumanizing rhetoric on both sides.[23][25] This portrayal highlights the PCFF's empirical approach—rooted in over 600-800 members' lived experiences—over abstract peace processes, though critics note its marginal impact amid ongoing hostilities.[46][62]

Reception and impact

Critical acclaim

_Apeirogon garnered significant critical praise upon its February 2020 publication for its experimental structure comprising 1,001 fragmentary chapters, which reviewers described as mirroring the infinite sides of an apeirogon while weaving factual digressions with the protagonists' narratives.[2] The New York Times characterized the novel as a "powerful and prismatic" work that functions as an "empathy engine," effectively bridging divides through nonlinear storytelling and intimate first-person accounts of grief.[2] Critics highlighted the book's emotional depth and its portrayal of unlikely friendship amid intractable conflict, with The Guardian calling it a "masterpiece" that "buoys the heart" despite profound sorrow, emphasizing the "great and sustaining beauty" in the bond between Bassam Aramin and Rami Elhanan.[26] The hybrid form—blending novelistic elements, historical facts, photographs, and mathematical inspirations—was praised for its urgency and potential to foster understanding of the Israeli-Palestinian divide, as noted in the Booker Prize readers' guide, which deemed it a "gripping novel" and "moving reflection" on peacemaking.[68] The novel's shortlisting for the 2020 Booker Prize on September 15, 2020, amplified its acclaim, positioning it among six finalists for its ambitious handling of real-life tragedy and hope. Reviewers in outlets like the Los Angeles Review of Books commended its contextual richness, arguing that the layered digressions enhance rather than overshadow the core human stories of loss and reconciliation.[42]

Criticisms and debates

Criticisms of Apeirogon have centered on its depiction of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, particularly accusations that the novel imposes a false equivalence between the two sides by emphasizing shared grief over structural asymmetries. Palestinian-American author Susan Abulhawa argued in an Al Jazeera opinion piece that the book "mystifies the colonisation of Palestine as a ‘complicated conflict’ between two equal sides," thereby implying parity between Israeli experiences of security and Palestinian resistance to occupation, which she likened to equating settler violence with indigenous pushback.[8] Abulhawa further contended that this framing normalizes oppression by suggesting "all fear is the same, all violence is the same," stretching graphic details of the daughters' deaths to desensitize readers to colonial realities rather than confronting them directly.[8] Literary reviewers have also debated the novel's unconventional structure, consisting of 1,001 short, numbered sections that ascend to 500 and then descend, drawing comparisons to One Thousand and One Nights. A New York Times review described this as providing "structure in place of content," critiquing the fragmented, digressive form as a collage that prioritizes stylistic innovation over narrative depth in addressing the protagonists' stories.[69] Some assessments, aggregating professional reviews, have questioned the work's foundational premise as creative nonfiction, noting its reliance on the "problematic belief that literature should do good in the world," which risks oversimplifying complex historical traumas through empathetic but selective vignettes.[70] Debates have extended to the ethics of fictionalizing real individuals' experiences, given the novel's basis in the lives of Bassam Aramin and Rami Elhanan, members of the Parents Circle-Families Forum. While the organization has endorsed the book for highlighting reconciliation efforts, critics like Abulhawa viewed its outsider perspective—written by an Irish author—as reinforcing colonial narratives by prioritizing interpersonal bonds over systemic critiques of power imbalances.[8] [71] These objections, often from pro-Palestinian outlets, contrast with broader acclaim for the novel's intent to foster understanding, underscoring tensions between artistic license and historical fidelity in conflict literature.[9]

Awards and honors

Major nominations and wins

Apeirogon received several prestigious literary honors following its publication in 2020. It was longlisted for the Booker Prize in 2020, recognizing its innovative structure and exploration of grief amid the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[6] The novel won the National Jewish Book Award for Fiction in 2020, awarded by the Jewish Book Council for outstanding works addressing Jewish themes, experiences, or history.[72] In France, it secured the Prix du Meilleur Livre Étranger in 2020, selected by a jury for excellence among foreign fiction translated into French.[73] Apeirogon was shortlisted for the International Dublin Literary Award in 2021, nominated by libraries worldwide for its global impact and literary merit.[74] It also earned a place on the longlist for the Andrew Carnegie Medal for Excellence in Fiction in 2021, highlighting exceptional narrative achievement.[75] Further recognition included shortlistings for the Dalkey Literary Awards in 2021 and the Orwell Prize for Political Fiction in 2021, the latter commending its engagement with political narratives.[76][77]
