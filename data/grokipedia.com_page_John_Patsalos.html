John Patler

John Christ Patsalos (born January 6, 1938), formerly known as John Patler, is an American former neo-Nazi activist and convicted assassin best known for murdering George Lincoln Rockwell, the founder of the American Nazi Party (ANP), on August 25, 1967.[1][2] Born in New York City to Greek-American parents, Patsalos enlisted in the United States Marine Corps in 1958 and was discharged in 1960 following an arrest at a Nazi rally.[1] He anglicized his surname to Patler upon joining the ANP that year, rising to a senior position while contributing as a propagandist and cartoonist, including creating comic books such as Here Comes Whiteman.[3][1] Patler's relationship with Rockwell deteriorated amid internal party disputes, leading to his expulsion from the ANP in early 1967 after advocating for organizational changes that clashed with Rockwell's leadership.[3][4] On the day of the assassination, Patler shot Rockwell twice through the windshield of his car with a Mauser pistol while the ANP leader was parked at a laundromat in Arlington, Virginia, killing him instantly from a wound to the heart.[2] Arrested shortly afterward based on eyewitness accounts, footprints, and recovered evidence, Patler was tried in Arlington Circuit Court and convicted of first-degree murder by a jury in December 1967.[2][5] Sentenced to 20 years in prison, Patler served approximately four years before being paroled in 1975, though he violated terms and returned to incarceration until the early 1980s.[5][2] After his release, he reverted to his birth name, pursued work as a commercial artist and photographer, and attempted to distance himself from his neo-Nazi past, including launching a short-lived Spanish-language newspaper.[2] The killing fragmented the ANP further, contributing to its decline, while Patler's act stemmed from personal and ideological grievances rather than external conspiracy, as affirmed by the judicial process.[6][5]

Early Life

Birth and Family Background

John Christ Patsalos, later known as John Patler, was born on January 6, 1938, in New York, New York.[1][7] His birth name reflected likely Greek heritage, which he anglicized to Patler during his involvement in far-right activism.[8] Patsalos grew up in a poor family in an urban environment, with limited public details available on his parents or siblings.[8]

Military Service and Discharge

John Patsalos enlisted in the United States Marine Corps and served from 1958 to 1960.[1] During his service, he was stationed at the Marine base in Quantico, Virginia.[9] Patler's military tenure ended amid revelations of his affiliation with Nazi activities. He was exposed as a member of the American Nazi Party while still in service, prompting an investigation by Marine Corps authorities.[10] Some accounts describe his arrest at a Nazi rally as a precipitating factor.[1] These incidents violated military standards of conduct and loyalty, leading to his administrative separation. He received a general discharge under honorable conditions in 1960, a status that acknowledges satisfactory service in some respects but bars full veteran benefits due to the circumstances.[10] [9] Following the discharge, Patsalos legally changed his surname to Patler and pursued deeper involvement with the American Nazi Party.[1]

Entry into Far-Right Activism

Joining the American Nazi Party

John Christ Patsalos, born on January 6, 1938, in New York City, enlisted in the United States Marine Corps in 1958 and served until 1960 while stationed at Quantico, Virginia, near the American Nazi Party's headquarters in Arlington.[1] During his service, Patsalos attended a Nazi rally, leading to his arrest, after which he received an honorable discharge in 1960.[1] Following his discharge, he legally changed his surname to Patler, an anglicized version, and formally joined the American Nazi Party (ANP) that same year.[1] Patler's entry into the ANP aligned with his growing interest in neo-Nazi ideology, facilitated by proximity to the party's activities from his Marine posting.[9] Upon joining, he adopted the persona of "Nazi Stormtrooper John Patler," reflecting his immediate immersion in the organization's paramilitary structure and propaganda efforts.[9] This transition marked his shift from military service to active participation in far-right extremism, where he began contributing as a cartoonist, producing materials like the white supremacist comic "Here Comes Whiteman" for recruitment purposes.[3]

Roles and Contributions Within the ANP

John Patler joined the American Nazi Party (ANP) in 1960 shortly after his discharge from the United States Marine Corps, adopting the surname "Patler" to align with the organization's ideological preferences.[7] He rapidly advanced within the group's hierarchy, attaining the rank of captain and serving as the officer in charge of printing, a position that placed him among the ANP's national officers responsible for producing propaganda materials.[11][1] In this capacity, Patler contributed significantly to the ANP's visual and print propaganda efforts. He worked as the editor and cartoonist for the party's magazine, Stormtrooper, creating illustrations, flyers, and leaflets that disseminated the organization's antisemitic and white supremacist messages.[12] Additionally, Patler produced the 1965 propaganda comic book Here Comes Whiteman, which promoted ANP ideology through caricatured depictions of racial and political themes.[3] Patler's printing oversight ensured the dissemination of ANP literature, including posters and publications, supporting street activities and recruitment in areas like New York, where he was based. His technical skills in graphic design and editing bolstered the party's ability to generate and distribute materials amid internal and external challenges.[1] These roles highlighted his operational importance until ideological frictions led to his expulsion in 1967.[13]

Ideological Divergences and Rival Efforts

Formation of the American National Party

In 1961, John Patler co-founded the American National Party (ANP), a short-lived splinter group from George Lincoln Rockwell's American Nazi Party, alongside associate Dan Burros.[14] The formation stemmed from internal disagreements within the original ANP, with Patler positioning the new organization as an alternative vehicle for anti-communist and white supremacist activism.[15] Patler assumed the role of chairman, while Burros served as second-in-command.[9] The American National Party, subtitled "The White Man's Working Party," operated primarily out of New York and focused on street-level agitation against perceived Soviet influences and domestic "red" elements.[16] It produced propaganda handbills, such as "We Want War! Red Russia Must Perish!," featuring Patler's illustrations of aggressive anti-communist themes, including depictions of Nikita Khrushchev being disposed of by Uncle Sam.[17] The group also organized public rallies, including a New York division street meeting protesting peace negotiations with Khrushchev, where Patler was advertised as the keynote speaker addressing potential communist dupes in American society.[14] Despite these efforts, the organization remained marginal and dissolved shortly after its inception as Patler and key members reintegrated into the broader neo-Nazi milieu.[16]

Publication of Kill!

In 1962, John Patler, alongside Dan Burros, launched Kill! magazine under the auspices of the American National Party, a short-lived splinter organization from the American Nazi Party. The publication, subtitled "Dedicated to the Annihilation of the Enemies of the White People," promoted neo-Nazi ideology with calls for aggressive action against perceived racial adversaries, including features on disrupting rallies and criticizing political figures like President John F. Kennedy.[18][19] Patler served as editor for the initial issues, with the first volume encompassing numbers 1 and 2 released between July and September 1962. Archival records indicate that only the first three issues were published under Patler's direction, reflecting the limited lifespan and resources of the American National Party.[20][18] The magazine's masthead and content emphasized militant white supremacist themes, aligning with Patler's divergences from George Lincoln Rockwell's leadership in the parent organization.[18] The endeavor ultimately faltered, producing no more than four issues in total before the group's dissolution, as Patler and associates reconciled or shifted allegiances back toward the American Nazi Party. This publication represented an early, independent platform for Patler's ideological expressions, distinct from Rockwell's official outlets like Stormtrooper magazine.[18]

Attempts at Reconciliation and Expulsion

Following the publication of Kill!, in which Patler had critiqued aspects of Rockwell's leadership and promoted his own factional views, Patler indicated a shift toward reconciliation by publicly stating in the magazine that he no longer rejected Rockwell's authority.[21] This declaration facilitated his brief return to the American Nazi Party (recently renamed the National Socialist White People's Party in January 1967), where he had previously held roles as a captain, editor, and cartoonist for the party's Stormtrooper publication.[8] [21] However, internal tensions persisted due to Patler's ideological divergences, including leanings described as Marxist or Bolshevik, which clashed with the party's core National Socialist principles.[8] These differences manifested in Patler's efforts to foment division by attempting to organize a coup among members of Mediterranean ("dark-eyed") heritage against those of Northern European ("blue-eyed") descent, whom he derogatorily labeled "blue-eyed devils."[8] As a result, Rockwell expelled Patler from the party in early 1967, with the action occurring between March and April.[8] [3] The expulsion reflected broader factionalism within the group, where personal ambitions and ethnic sub-grievances undermined unity under Rockwell's command.[8]

Assassination of George Lincoln Rockwell

Prelude to the Act

Following his expulsion from the American Nazi Party (ANP) in March 1967 for fomenting internal dissensionâ€”including derogatory remarks toward fair-skinned, blond party members as "blue-eyed devils"â€”John Patler maintained contact with George Lincoln Rockwell while nursing a deepening grudge.[22] Patler, who had briefly formed a rival faction in 1961 before rejoining the ANP, pursued ideological variances such as advocating reduced German symbolism and faced accusations of Marxist influences, exacerbating tensions.[2] A letter from Patler seeking reconciliation was found in Rockwell's wallet at the time of his death, indicating ongoing but strained attempts at rapprochement amid Patler's resentment over his demotion from a senior Storm Trooper captain role.[2] Post-expulsion, Patler resided at 2522 Lee Highway in Arlington, Virginia, working as a typesetter while conducting surveillance on Rockwell's routines to track his movements.[23] He acquired a Mauser pistol and ammunition, evidencing premeditation in response to perceived personal and ideological betrayals.[23] These activities reflected Patler's shift toward independent far-right organizing, including prior efforts with the short-lived American National Party, though immediate prelude focused on targeted animosity toward Rockwell rather than broader recruitment.[18] On August 25, 1967, around 11:30 a.m., Rockwell arrived at the Dominion Hills shopping center in Arlington to use a coin-operated laundromat, parking his 1958 Chevrolet.[22][2] As he backed out of the parking space, Patler positioned himself to fire two shots striking Rockwell in the head and chest, exploiting familiarity with the location from prior observation.[22][23] Eyewitnesses reported a young man fleeing the vicinity immediately after, aligning with Patler's subsequent arrest about 45 minutes later, three-quarters of a mile away at a bus stop.[22] This culmination of post-expulsion surveillance and armament directly precipitated the fatal encounter.[23]

The Shooting and Immediate Aftermath

On August 25, 1967, George Lincoln Rockwell was shot twice by John Patler while preparing to drive away from the Dominion Hills shopping center in Arlington, Virginia.[24][25] Patler, positioned on the rooftop of a beauty salon across the street from the laundromat where Rockwell had been, fired the shots through the windshield of Rockwell's Chevrolet as he backed out of a parking space around 11:55 a.m.[24][25] The bullets struck Rockwell in the head and chest, causing his vehicle to crash into another car.[24] Rockwell died instantly at the scene, as confirmed by the coroner.[24] Witnesses reported the gunfire, prompting an immediate police response to the 600 block of Wilson Boulevard.[23] American Nazi Party members at the nearby headquarters learned of the incident shortly after and gathered at the site, where they identified Patler as a suspect based on prior tensions.[25] Patler fled the rooftop after the shooting, discarding his weapon in nearby Four Mile Run before attempting to board a bus.[25] He was apprehended approximately 30 minutes later by Arlington police, who noted his wet trousers from the stream crossing, and charged with first-degree murder.[25][24] The arrest followed tips from party associates who had seen Patler near the scene, marking the rapid closure of the initial investigation into Rockwell's assassination.[23]

Claimed Motives and Disputes

The prosecution in Patler's trial asserted that his motive stemmed from deep resentment over his expulsion from the American Nazi Party by Rockwell in April 1967, amid escalating ideological clashes and Patler's ambitions to lead a splinter faction. Evidence included Patler's statements to associates expressing hostility, such as declaring Rockwell "an evil genius" who "must be stopped," alongside his active efforts to organize a rival group challenging Rockwell's authority.[5] This narrative framed the killing as a politically motivated act of intra-factional rivalry rather than random violence, with the Virginia Supreme Court later upholding the sufficiency of this circumstantial motive alongside physical evidence in affirming the conviction.[5] Patler maintained his innocence throughout the proceedings, denying any involvement in the shooting and offering an alibi placing him at home approximately three miles from the scene until shortly after the 11:37 a.m. killing on August 25, 1967.[5] He did not concede a motive, instead portraying the accusations as fabrications tied to party infighting; defense arguments emphasized the lack of direct forensic links, such as negative gunpowder residue tests on his clothing, to undermine the prosecution's causal chain.[5] A letter discovered in Rockwell's wallet post-assassination suggested ongoing attempts at reconciliation between the two, which some interpreted as evidence of a more personal grievance, potentially over unpaid contributions or shared projects like Patler's Kill! magazine, though prosecutors prioritized the expulsion as the core driver.[2] Disputes over the motive extended beyond the courtroom into far-right circles, where some party members speculated that successors like Matthias Koehl orchestrated the hit to consolidate power, citing internal power struggles and Rockwell's recent push to rebrand the group as the National Socialist White People's Party.[2] These theories lacked evidentiary support and were dismissed in official proceedings, which focused on Patler's documented instabilityâ€”including prior psychiatric evaluations labeling him a "potential murderer"â€”and his Marxist-leaning pushes to "Americanize" the party by purging Teutonic elements, actions that fueled his rift with Rockwell.[2] No credible alternative perpetrators emerged, and the jury's December 15, 1967, verdict of first-degree murder implicitly rejected such claims in favor of the prosecution's account of vengeful disloyalty.[2][5]

Trial, Conviction, and Appeals

Prosecution and Defense Arguments

The prosecution, led by Arlington County Commonwealth's Attorney William Hassan, argued that Patler premeditated the murder out of personal grudge following his expulsion from the American Nazi Party in April 1967. They contended that Patler's bitterness stemmed from his dismissal for internal disputes, including accusations of disloyalty and attempts to undermine party leadership, culminating in reported threats to "stop" Rockwell.[5] Evidence presented included eyewitness accounts of a man matching Patler's descriptionâ€”dark-haired, wearing a dark coatâ€”fleeing the rooftop vantage point from which the shots were fired at approximately 11:58 a.m. on August 25, 1967.[5] Ballistics linked the .22-caliber Mauser pistol used in the killing to Patler, who had possessed it between 1964 and 1966, with FBI tests confirming that shell casings and bullets recovered from the Ervin farm in Highland Countyâ€”property associated with Patler's father-in-lawâ€”matched those from the crime scene.[5] Additional circumstantial links included Patler's arrest less than two miles away shortly after the shooting, with his clothing appearing wet in a manner consistent with recent exposure on the rain-dampened rooftop, and footprints at the scene traced to him.[26] The prosecution sought the death penalty, emphasizing the deliberate nature of the ambush as Rockwell pulled into the Dominion Hills shopping center parking lot.[26] Patler's defense team entered a not guilty plea and centered their case on an alibi, asserting he was three miles away at his Lyon Village residence on Lee Highway around noon, handling personal errands with his wife and young child, including a stop at Arlington Paper Supply on Washington Boulevard.[26] They challenged the reliability of the circumstantial evidence, noting the absence of direct eyewitnesses to the shooting itself and highlighting negative scientific results, such as the lack of gunshot residue on Patler and discrepancies in footprint and weapon possession timelines.[5] Efforts to suppress the farm ballistics evidence failed, as the court ruled it admissible under the open fields doctrine, deeming the search warrant invalid but the location outside protected curtilage.[5] The defense portrayed the links to the murder weapon as tenuous, arguing Patler no longer owned the Mauser at the time and disputing chain-of-custody claims, while downplaying motive by framing the party expulsion as a mutual ideological rift rather than personal vendetta.[5] Despite these contentions, the jury convicted Patler of first-degree murder after four hours of deliberation on December 15, 1967, recommending a 20-year sentence over the prosecution's capital push.[26]

Verdict, Sentencing, and Legal Challenges

On December 15, 1967, a jury in the Circuit Court of Arlington County, Virginia, convicted John Patler of first-degree murder in the shooting death of George Lincoln Rockwell.[8] On February 23, 1968, the court sentenced Patler to a 20-year term of imprisonment.[8][5] Patler appealed his conviction to the Supreme Court of Virginia, arguing primarily that the trial court erred in admitting shell casings and bullets recovered from an open field on his father-in-law's farm, which he claimed resulted from an illegal search, and that the circumstantial evidence was insufficient to support the verdict.[5] The court rejected these claims, applying the "open fields" doctrine under which no reasonable expectation of privacy exists in such areas, and affirmed the conviction and sentence on November 30, 1970.[5] The U.S. Supreme Court denied certiorari on June 12, 1972.[6] Patler then sought federal habeas corpus relief under 28 U.S.C. Â§ 2254 in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia, filed on October 9, 1972, raising issues including violations of due process from allegedly suggestive pre-trial identifications, the same Fourth Amendment challenge to the farm evidence seizure, and a Brady violation for delayed disclosure of exculpatory ballistic test results.[6] The district court granted summary judgment to the state without a hearing on March 29, 1973, finding no merit in the claims.[6] The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit affirmed on August 28, 1974, holding that witness testimony relied on independent observations rather than tainted show-ups, the open fields search was lawful, and the evidence disclosure satisfied Brady requirements.[6] These rulings exhausted Patler's direct and collateral challenges, upholding the original verdict and sentence.[6]

Imprisonment and Parole

Prison Experience

Following the Virginia Supreme Court's upholding of his conviction on December 1, 1970, Patler began serving his 20-year sentence in the state's correctional system.[27] He was assigned to a low-security correctional camp near Martinsville, Virginia, where he served approximately four years.[28] This placement reflected the minimum sentence allowable under Virginia law for first-degree murder at the time, which carried no possibility of parole before 20 years but allowed for classification in less restrictive facilities based on inmate assessment.[8] Patler's incarceration during this period involved standard regime activities typical of such camps, though specific personal accounts or incidents from his time there remain undocumented in public records. He was paroled in August 1975 after serving about five years total, having earned credits reducing his effective time.[28]

Release in 1975

John Patler was granted parole after serving roughly eight years of his 20-year sentence for the first-degree murder of George Lincoln Rockwell.[2] On August 22, 1975, he was released from Field Unit I, a correctional facility in Pulaski County, Virginia.[29] The Virginia Parole Board approved his early release, citing good behavior and time served, though specific conditions of paroleâ€”such as restrictions on association with extremist groupsâ€”were not publicly detailed in contemporaneous reports.[30] Patler's parole marked the end of his incarceration stemming from the 1967 assassination, during which he had maintained a low profile in prison without notable incidents.[31]

Later Life and Reflections

Name Change and Post-Parole Career

Following his parole in November 1975, John Patler legally reverted to his birth name, John Christ Patsalos, which he had changed to Patler in 1960 upon joining the American Nazi Party to adopt a more Germanic-sounding moniker.[32] Patsalos then returned to the New York City area, where he resided thereafter.[8] No verifiable records detail a notable post-parole career; available accounts indicate he maintained a low public profile, with no documented resumption of political activism or professional pursuits such as his prior work in cartooning for the party's publications.[13] In 1976, Patsalos faced rearrest in Charlottesville, Virginia, on charges related to firearm possession as a felon but was acquitted, after which he continued living privately.

Family Perspectives and Personal Regrets

Patsalos's son, Nicholas Patler, described in the 2013 afterword to William H. Schmaltz's book Hate: George Lincoln Rockwell and the American Nazi Party that his father renounced his neo-Nazi past, viewing his time in the American Nazi Party as a misguided phase influenced by youthful extremism and personal turmoil, including a violent childhood where his father killed his mother in 1943. However, Patsalos reportedly harbored no regrets over the 1967 assassination of Rockwell, framing it as an ideologically necessary act to halt what he saw as Rockwell's corruption of the movement through compromises on antisemitism and internal power plays.[33] This perspective from Nicholas Patler, a historian specializing in civil rights history, highlights a selective remorse confined to organizational affiliation rather than the violent act itself, with Patsalos allegedly telling family members post-release that the shooting prevented further dilution of "racial purity" doctrines. No public statements or interviews from Patsalos directly corroborate these family-reported sentiments, and other relatives, such as his brother, offered no recorded views on the matter.[33]

Enduring Controversies and Alternative Interpretations

The official narrative, upheld by the 1967 conviction, portrays Patler's killing of Rockwell as a premeditated act driven by personal resentment following his April 1967 expulsion from the American Nazi Party (ANP) over alleged factionalism and ideological deviations, such as advocating Marxist-influenced reforms like removing German symbols from the party.[2] Patler himself maintained in post-conviction statements that the shooting arose from a private dispute, rejecting any political assassination framing and emphasizing threats he believed Rockwell posed to him personally.[2] This interpretation aligns with trial evidence, including witness identifications, matching footprints at the scene, and Patler's possession of the murder weapon, a .22-caliber pistol, which prosecutors argued demonstrated intent rather than impulse.[2] Alternative views within ANP splinter factions disputed the lone-actor personal motive, alleging internal power struggles. Shortly after Rockwell's death, anonymous fliers circulated among party members accusing Matthias Koehl, Rockwell's successor as ANP commander, of complicity or orchestration to consolidate control; Koehl dismissed these as baseless smears by rivals seeking to undermine his leadership.[2] No corroborating evidence emerged in investigations or appeals, and such claims appear rooted in post-assassination infighting that fragmented the group, with Koehl's faction rebranding as the National Socialist White People's Party while expelling suspected dissidents.[34] Fringe interpretations in neo-Nazi and white supremacist circles have persisted, positing Patler as an infiltratorâ€”potentially of Jewish or communist background, as speculated by Rockwell himself due to Patler's Greek heritage (original surname Patsalos) and perceived disloyaltyâ€”acting on behalf of external enemies like Jewish organizations or federal agents to decapitate the movement.[35] These theories, echoed in self-published far-right literature and online forums rather than peer-reviewed or official records, lack forensic or testimonial substantiation and contradict the Arlington police investigation's conclusion of a solo perpetrator motivated by internal grievances.[2] Historians attribute their endurance to the ANP's paranoid worldview, which framed Rockwell's death as martyrdom amplifying his ideology, though empirical data shows the assassination accelerated the party's decline amid leadership vacuums and legal scrutiny.
