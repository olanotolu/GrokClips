Elizabeth Dowdeswell

![Dowdeswell wearing dark blue smiling towards the camera](./assets/Elizabeth_Dowdeswell_2020-01-01_(DSCF0094) Elizabeth Dowdeswell, OC OOnt (born Violet Elizabeth Patton; November 9, 1944), is a Northern Irish-born Canadian public servant and diplomat who served as the 29th Lieutenant Governor of Ontario from September 23, 2014, to November 14, 2023, the longest tenure in the province's history.[1][2][3] Born in Belfast, Northern Ireland, she immigrated to rural Saskatchewan with her family in 1947 and later earned a Bachelor of Science in Home Economics from the University of Saskatchewan.[4][5] Her career spanned education, environmental policy, and nuclear safety, including roles as a high school teacher, executive director of the United Nations Environment Programme from 1998 to 2002, and president and CEO of the Nuclear Waste Management Organization from 2005 to 2014.[6][7] As Lieutenant Governor, Dowdeswell focused on initiatives promoting reconciliation with Indigenous peoples, mental health awareness, and community engagement, conducting over 5,000 official engagements while representing the Crown and advising on constitutional matters such as prorogations.[8][3] She holds honours including Officer of the Order of Canada and the Order of Ontario, along with multiple honorary doctorates.[9]

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Immigration

Violet Elizabeth Patton was born on November 9, 1944, in Belfast, Northern Ireland, to Desmond Granville Patton, a United Church minister, and his wife, a school teacher.[5][10] In 1947, at the age of three, she immigrated with her parents to rural southern Saskatchewan, Canada, amid post-World War II emigration from Northern Ireland driven by economic opportunities in agriculture and clerical roles within expanding prairie communities.[4][11] As the eldest of what would become a family of eight children, Dowdeswell experienced frequent relocations across small Saskatchewan towns tied to her father's ministerial assignments, fostering early exposure to self-reliant rural life amid farming landscapes and tight-knit United Church congregations.[9][5]

Academic Background

Elizabeth Dowdeswell completed her undergraduate studies at the University of Saskatchewan, where she earned a Bachelor of Science degree in home economics along with a teaching certificate.[12][5] These qualifications prepared her for initial roles in education, emphasizing practical disciplines such as nutrition, family resource management, and consumer economics within the home economics curriculum prevalent at the institution during the mid-20th century.[13] She pursued graduate education in the United States, obtaining a Master of Science degree in behavioural sciences from Utah State University in 1972.[14] This program focused on interdisciplinary approaches to human behavior, drawing from psychology, sociology, and education, which aligned with her emerging interests in policy and environmental decision-making, though her thesis details remain undocumented in public institutional records.[15] No scholarships or specific academic awards from these programs are detailed in verified university or governmental biographies.

Pre-Lieutenant Governorship Career

Early Professional Roles

Dowdeswell commenced her professional career as a high school teacher in Saskatchewan, specializing in home economics after earning her Bachelor of Science in Home Economics from the University of Saskatchewan in 1966.[16] [17] Her teaching focused on practical skills in household management and nutrition, reflecting the rural agricultural context of the province where family resource allocation and food production were central to community life. She transitioned from classroom teaching to entry-level public service roles within the Saskatchewan government, beginning with positions in the Department of Education that involved advisory work on curriculum and policy implementation.[5] This included serving as an educational consultant, where she contributed to provincial efforts to align educational programs with local economic needs, such as agriculture-related vocational training.[18] Following a brief stint as a lecturer in economics and marketing at the University of Alberta, Dowdeswell returned to the Saskatchewan Department of Education, advancing into administrative capacities that emphasized evidence-based improvements in teacher training and resource distribution for rural schools.[5] These roles laid the groundwork for her subsequent policy influence by applying her academic expertise in behavioral sciences to practical governance challenges, though specific measurable outcomes from this period, such as enrollment increases or program adoption rates, are not extensively documented in available records.[15]

Environmental and International Positions

In the 1980s, Dowdeswell directed the Ontario office of Environment Canada before advancing to federal leadership roles, including Regional Director-General in 1986 and subsequently Assistant Deputy Minister responsible for the Atmospheric Environment Service.[19][5] In this capacity, she oversaw national weather monitoring, atmospheric research, and international negotiations, notably contributing to the Framework Convention on Climate Change adopted in 1992, which established foundational commitments to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations but has faced criticism for lacking enforceable mechanisms, resulting in limited empirical progress on emissions reductions as global CO2 levels rose from approximately 355 ppm in 1992 to over 370 ppm by 1998.[18][12] Her work emphasized data-driven atmospheric assessments, aligning with first-principles approaches to identifying causal drivers of climate variability, though outcomes depended on subsequent national implementations often hampered by economic priorities. Dowdeswell's Canadian experience positioned her for international leadership, culminating in her appointment as Executive Director of the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) in Nairobi from 1992 to 1998.[18] During her tenure, UNEP prioritized scientific assessments to inform global policy, including the 1995 Global Biodiversity Assessment, which documented severe threats to Earth's biological resources from habitat loss, overexploitation, and pollution, estimating that 25-50% of species could face extinction risks without intervention.[20] This report provided empirical baselines for biodiversity metrics but highlighted implementation gaps, as international frameworks like the Convention on Biological Diversity (1992) struggled with compliance, evidenced by ongoing deforestation rates exceeding 15 million hectares annually in the 1990s.[20] Under Dowdeswell, UNEP also advanced the inaugural Global Environment Outlook (GEO-1) released in 1997, integrating data on atmospheric degradation, freshwater scarcity, and land productivity declines, revealing that human activities had pushed multiple systems toward unsustainable thresholds without reversing trends like ozone depletion or desertification.[21] She promoted technology-enhanced monitoring to improve assessment accuracy, yet UNEP encountered structural challenges, including a budget reduction from $150 million in 1992-1993 to $90 million by the mid-1990s, which critics attributed to diffused priorities and overextension across mandates, diminishing strategic influence amid competing UN agendas.[18][22] These limitations underscored causal realism in international environmentalism: while assessments generated verifiable data for awareness, lacking binding enforcement and facing donor fatigue, programs often failed to alter root drivers like population growth and resource demands, with metrics such as global fish stocks continuing to decline by 10-20% in capture fisheries during the period.[23][24]

Advisory and Executive Leadership

Elizabeth Dowdeswell served as the founding President of the Nuclear Waste Management Organization (NWMO) from October 2002 to early 2010, tasked with studying long-term management options for Canada's used nuclear fuel under the Nuclear Fuel Waste Act.[25] [26] During her tenure, the NWMO conducted extensive public dialogues involving over 600 events and submissions from thousands of participants, evaluating options like deep geological disposal against alternatives such as surface storage.[27] The organization's 2005 report, Choosing a Way Forward, recommended Adaptive Phased Management (APM)â€”a system combining centralized containment in a deep underground repository with staged implementation, site-specific monitoring, and adaptability based on new evidenceâ€”which was submitted to the Minister of Natural Resources on November 3, 2005.[27] This empirical approach prioritized geological stability and engineered barriers for containment, drawing on international data from sites like Finland's Onkalo repository, while emphasizing informed consent from host communities to address social dimensions.[28] The Canadian government adopted APM as the national strategy on June 14, 2007, crediting the NWMO's rigorous analysis for balancing technical feasibility with public input, though implementation revealed causal challenges in securing willing hosts amid stakeholder conflicts.[26] [29] Site selection, initiated post-Dowdeswell in 2010, encountered opposition from Indigenous groups and municipalities over risks to water resources and cultural sites, resulting in narrowed options and ongoing delays despite empirical safety demonstrations from geoscientific assessments showing negligible long-term environmental impact under proper engineering.[30] [27] These frictions underscored the limits of top-down policy without localized buy-in, as national imperatives for secure disposal clashed with community-level risk perceptions, even when supported by data on repository performance.[31] From January 2010 to September 2014, Dowdeswell was President and CEO of the Council of Canadian Academies (CCA), an independent body commissioning expert panels to provide evidence-based advice on science and policy issues.[32] [33] Under her leadership, the CCA produced reports emphasizing peer-reviewed data and multidisciplinary analysis, such as the 2013 assessment of conducted energy weapons (e.g., Tasers), which concluded rare but possible cardiac and respiratory fatalities under specific conditions like close-range deployment on vulnerable individuals, informing police protocols with quantitative risk models.[34] In energy policy, the 2014 Environmental Impacts of Shale Gas Extraction in Canada evaluated hydraulic fracturing's lifecycle effects, finding manageable air and water risks through monitoring and regulation but highlighting seismic and methane emission uncertainties, based on panel reviews of over 200 studies.[35] The CCA's process under Dowdeswell involved assembling ad hoc expert panels free from government direction, fostering causal realism by weighing empirical evidence against policy assumptions, though reports occasionally faced delays from panel deliberations and stakeholder critiques on scope limitations, such as incomplete integration of economic costs in shale gas analysis.[36] This non-partisan framework contributed to decisions like enhanced oversight recommendations for resource extraction, prioritizing verifiable data over advocacy, with outputs cited in federal strategies for their independence from industry or activist biases.[37]

Tenure as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario

Appointment and Swearing-In

Prime Minister Stephen Harper announced on June 26, 2014, the appointment of Elizabeth Dowdeswell as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario, to be formalized by Governor General David Johnston on the Prime Minister's advice.[1][38] This selection followed the end of David Onley's term, with Dowdeswell chosen for her extensive public service experience, including roles in environmental policy and international organizations, to ensure the viceregal office's impartial representation of the Crown.[39][40] The appointment process adheres to constitutional convention, where the federal government nominates candidates deemed non-partisan and meritorious, independent of the provincial government's political affiliation.[41] Dowdeswell's installation occurred amid Ontario's governance by the Liberal Party under Premier Kathleen Wynne, elected in June 2014, underscoring the Lieutenant Governor's role as a neutral figure bridging federal and provincial spheres without partisan influence.[38] The selection by a Conservative Prime Minister to serve under a Liberal Premier exemplified the deliberate apolitical criteria for viceregal appointments, prioritizing institutional continuity over electoral politics.[42] On September 23, 2014, Dowdeswell was sworn in as the 29th Lieutenant Governor during a ceremony in the Queen's Park Legislative Chamber, where she subscribed to the required oaths of allegiance to Queen Elizabeth II and office, before assuming the throne.[11][43] In her initial address, she pledged to consult Ontarians on priorities, emphasizing engagement to identify societal challenges and opportunities, including Ontario's global positioning.[44][45] This event marked her formal entry into the role, succeeding Onley effective immediately upon completion of the oaths.[46]

Constitutional Duties and Public Engagements

As Lieutenant Governor of Ontario from 2014 to 2023, Elizabeth Dowdeswell exercised the Crown's reserve powers in the province, including summoning, proroguing, and dissolving the Legislative Assembly on the advice of the Premier. She granted royal assent to 419 bills enacted by the legislature, signed 17,497 Orders-in-Council, and issued 261 writs of election for two general elections and 13 by-elections. Dowdeswell also delivered Speeches from the Throne outlining government priorities and administered oaths to members of the Executive Council upon their appointments. These functions were performed impartially across the tenures of Premier Kathleen Wynne's Liberal government (2014â€“2018) and Premier Doug Ford's Progressive Conservative government (2018â€“2023), ensuring continuity in the ceremonial and constitutional framework without partisan influence.[47] Dowdeswell's public engagements emphasized representation of the sovereign in community settings, with a total of 6,529 official events conducted across all 124 provincial ridings. These routine activities included attendance at local ceremonies, civic functions, and commemorations, averaging more than 700 engagements annually as detailed in office activity reports. She issued over 900 special event messages, extended more than 30,000 greetings, and produced over 200 video messages to support provincial observances and citizen initiatives. Such engagements facilitated direct interaction with diverse populations, underscoring the viceroy's role in bridging governmental authority with public life.[47] In fulfilling her representational duties, Dowdeswell completed 100 official municipal visits spanning Ontario's geographic extremesâ€”from Kenora in the west to North Glengarry in the east, Kitchenuhmaykoosib Inninuwug in the north to Point Pelee in the southâ€”and 26 visits to First Nations communities to advance reconciliation efforts. These outings promoted awareness of Indigenous treaties and cultural heritage, such as the 2022 dedication recognizing Treaty 13 during the Queen's Platinum Jubilee. Community-focused travel and participation aligned with standard viceregal promotion of social cohesion, environmental stewardship, and regional development, grounded in empirical outreach data from her office.[47]

Key Initiatives and Priorities

During her tenure from 2014 to 2023, Elizabeth Dowdeswell emphasized the theme of "building resilience and sustainability" as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario, framing it around inclusive economic prosperity, environmental stewardship, and social cohesion.[47] This initiative involved convening dialogues and events to promote awareness rather than enacting policy, given the ceremonial constraints of the role, which limited direct causal influence on outcomes beyond fostering public discourse.[47] Dowdeswell prioritized environmental sustainability through targeted engagements, including the launch of the SDGs Emerging Practices Guide in May 2019 and hosting the Great Lakes Circular Economy Forum in June 2019, alongside participation in a biodiversity symposium and beach cleanups.[48] She conducted community roundtables on climate change adaptation, such as one in Hamilton in April 2019, and visited municipalities like Oakville in March 2015 to discuss resource use and economic sustainability.[49][50] These efforts reached diverse audiences via 100 official municipal visits across all 124 provincial ridings, contributing to broader conversations on stewardship without measurable enforcement mechanisms.[47] On reconciliation with Indigenous peoples, Dowdeswell visited 26 First Nations communities, including Wahnapitae in May 2019 and Mohawks of the Bay of Quinte in August 2019, and hosted a roundtable on urban Indigenous placemaking in September 2019.[48] She dedicated sites like Gi-Chi-Twaa Gimaa Nini Mississauga Anishinaabek AName Amik in 2022 and supported initiatives recognizing treaties, such as the Queen's Platinum Jubilee Garden for Treaty 13, aiming to advance kinship and traditional knowledge integration.[47][51] Mental health dialogues formed part of resilience-building, particularly virtual sessions during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 and addresses at events like the Canadian Foundation for Physically Disabled Persons gala in February 2020, focusing on stigma reduction.[47][48] Overall, these priorities generated 6,529 engagements, including 689 public events in 2019â€“2020 alone, but their impact remained symbolic, enhancing participation metrics like attendance at roundtables without verifiable long-term societal shifts attributable to the office's non-binding authority.[47][48]

Notable Decisions and Events

On December 8, 2022, Dowdeswell granted royal assent to Bill 39, the Better Municipal Governance Act, 2022, which conferred expanded powers on the mayors of Toronto and Ottawa, including veto authority over bylaws and the ability to pass certain measures without full council approval to align with provincial housing and infrastructure goals. Immediately following the assent, she delivered an address to the Legislative Assembly, stating that "democracy is not a spectator sport" and underscoring the shared responsibility to protect institutional integrity and public participation amid evolving governance challenges.[52][53] In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, Dowdeswell adapted constitutional and ceremonial duties to virtual formats from March 2020 onward, conducting over 200 engagements annually via online platforms while proroguing and summoning sessions on the premier's advice to maintain legislative continuity.[54] Her public messaging emphasized societal resilience, with addresses in 2020 and 2021 highlighting community solidarity and adaptation, such as in her 2021 New Year's reflection urging Ontarians to support those affected and foster hope amid health restrictions.[55] Dowdeswell delivered the Speech from the Throne on August 9, 2022, formally opening the 43rd Parliament's first session after the June provincial election, articulating the government's priorities including economic recovery, housing acceleration, and skilled trades expansion.[56] On September 9, 2022â€”one day after Queen Elizabeth II's deathâ€”she granted royal assent to pending legislation, pausing briefly for reflection before signing in the sovereign's name, an act among the final such royal assents in Ontario under Elizabeth II's reign.[57]

Criticisms and Controversies

During the prorogation of the Ontario Legislative Assembly on August 19, 2020, requested by Premier Doug Ford's government amid scrutiny over its COVID-19 response including long-term care facility outbreaks, opposition leaders such as NDP Leader Andrea Horwath condemned the move as an evasion of legislative accountability, implicitly questioning the lieutenant governor's approval as facilitating executive dominance over parliamentary oversight. However, constitutional scholars emphasized that lieutenant governors are bound by convention to grant prorogation on ministerial advice unless exceptional reserve powers apply, a threshold not met here, underscoring Dowdeswell's adherence to established practice rather than personal overreach. In December 2022, Dowdeswell granted royal assent to Bill 39, the Better Municipal Governance Act, which empowered select mayors with expanded veto and bylaw-making authority to expedite housing developmentâ€”a measure decried by municipal advocates and some opposition members as undermining local democratic checks in favor of provincial priorities.[52] Immediately following assent on December 8, her address to the legislature warned of "erosion in public trust" and the imperative to safeguard deliberative processes against hasty interventions, prompting interpretations from outlets like Global News as a veiled rebuke to the Ford government's pattern of overriding norms via the notwithstanding clause.[53] Proponents countered that assent was a non-discretionary formality, with her subsequent remarks reflecting permissible moral suasion within the viceregal role, not contradiction of duty. Critiques of potential tension between Dowdeswell's pre-tenure environmental leadershipâ€”including her tenure as UNEP deputy executive director advocating sustainable developmentâ€”and the Ford administration's resource extraction emphases, such as nuclear extensions and mining acceleration, surfaced sporadically among progressive commentators, who speculated on subdued influence amid policy divergences.[58] Yet, empirical review of her tenure reveals no overt clashes or partisan interventions; throne speeches she delivered aligned with government agendas, including critical minerals promotion, while her public engagements emphasized consensus-building over confrontation, consistent with the office's apolitical mandate.[59] Overall, Dowdeswell faced minimal direct opprobrium, with her record evidencing restraint amid polarized governance.

Post-Tenure Activities

Public Speaking and Advocacy

Following the conclusion of her mandate as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario on November 14, 2023, Elizabeth Dowdeswell engaged in public discourse through targeted speaking engagements that drew on her extensive experience in governance, environmental policy, and institutional resilience. On January 16, 2024, she participated in a conversational event at the Balsillie School of International Affairs in Waterloo, Ontario, reflecting on her viceregal tenure and her earlier leadership as executive director of the United Nations Environment Programme from 1992 to 1998, emphasizing themes of global environmental stewardship and adaptive leadership in complex systems.[60][61] In her post-tenure advocacy, Dowdeswell has highlighted the intersections of optimism, civil discourse, and sustainability, linking these to her pre-viceregal expertise in environmental risk assessment and international diplomacy. At the Public Policy Forum's 2025 Testimonial Dinner on April 25, 2025, she delivered an acceptance speech as an honouree, describing herself as a "stubborn, incurable optimist" amid unprecedented global challenges, and underscoring Canadians' unique capacity to foster dialogue in a world marked by "difference and indifference."[62][9] Her remarks advocated for resilient institutions and genuine conversation as foundational to democratic health, echoing causal mechanisms from her career-long focus on evidence-based policy to mitigate systemic risks like environmental degradation.[63] Dowdeswell's reflections on her lieutenant governorship, as detailed in her End of Mandate Report released in November 2023, have informed these addresses, where she has reiterated priorities such as community cohesion and long-term ecological viability without revisiting ceremonial duties.[47] These activities demonstrate a continuity in her advocacy for proactive, data-driven responses to societal pressures, grounded in first-hand observations from over 5,000 engagements during her tenure and prior roles in federal environmental commissions.[47]

Organizational Involvement

Following her tenure as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario, which concluded on November 14, 2023, Elizabeth Dowdeswell joined the board of directors of The King's Trust Canada, an organization focused on youth development programs.[64] She also serves on the board of the Rideau Hall Foundation, which supports initiatives aligned with the Governor General's priorities in community building and sustainability.[65] Additionally, Dowdeswell is a board member of the Samara Centre for Democracy, dedicated to strengthening democratic institutions through research and civic education.[66] In environmental and regional sustainability efforts, Dowdeswell was appointed an honorary patron of the Council of the Great Lakes Region on December 5, 2024, a non-profit advancing cross-border collaboration on Great Lakes issues, including water quality and economic resilience.[67] She holds the position of honorary chair of the Special Advisor Group for The ClearWater Foundation, which promotes freshwater conservation and sustainable water management in Canada.[68] Dowdeswell maintains an affiliation with the Leadership Institute as a listed expert, contributing to leadership development programs in public and private sectors.[69] Her prior mentorship role with the Pierre Elliott Trudeau Foundation, established before 2023, continues to be noted in organizational directories, focusing on policy leadership for emerging scholars.[70] These positions reflect her ongoing emphasis on governance, environmental stewardship, and institutional strengthening, though outputs such as co-authored reports or chaired panels from these roles post-2023 remain limited in public documentation.[71]

Honours, Styles, and Legacy

Titles and Official Styles

During her tenure as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario from September 23, 2014, to November 14, 2023, Elizabeth Dowdeswell held the viceregal style "Her Honour the Honourable Elizabeth Dowdeswell, O.C., O.Ont., Lieutenant Governor of Ontario", with the prefix "Her Honour" applicable exclusively while in office per established Canadian protocol.[72][73] In formal spoken address, she was referred to as "Your Honour" or "Ma'am", reflecting the ceremonial representative role subordinate to the Governor General's "Her Excellency" and "Right Honourable" distinctions.[74] Post-tenure, she retains the lifelong courtesy title "The Honourable Elizabeth Dowdeswell, O.C., O.Ont.", without the "Her Honour" prefix, as granted to all former lieutenant governors under the Table of Titles to be Used in Canada.[74] The post-nominal letters denote her appointments as Officer of the Order of Canada and member of the Order of Ontario, appended in official correspondence and listings. This nomenclature ensures consistency in protocol documents, prioritizing precision over informal variations.[75]

Awards and Orders

Elizabeth Dowdeswell was appointed an Officer of the Order of Canada on May 24, 2012, recognizing her dedication to advancing public policy in areas including environment, education, and culture, including her role in developing the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change.[76] She was invested with the insignia on November 23, 2012, at a ceremony in Rideau Hall.[76] The Order of Canada, established in 1967, honours individuals for extraordinary contributions to the nation through merit-based selection by an independent advisory council, though appointments are formally made by the Governor General on the advice of the Prime Minister. Concurrent with her installation as Lieutenant Governor of Ontario on September 23, 2014, Dowdeswell was admitted as a Member of the Order of Ontario, the province's highest civilian honour, for her lifetime of public service in policy leadership and institutional roles.[77] The Order, created in 1986, selects recipients based on recommendations to the Lieutenant Governor, with final approval by the provincial executive, often aligning with viceregal appointments to affirm prior achievements. On November 26, 2014, she received the rank of Dame of Justice in the Most Venerable Order of the Hospital of St. John of Jerusalem, honouring her commitment to community welfare and health initiatives aligned with the order's charitable mission. Investiture occurred in March 2015. This priory-level distinction within the sovereign order, founded in the 11th century, is conferred for exemplary service, vetted through the order's chapter-general process independent of political oversight. Dowdeswell also holds campaign medals including the Queen Elizabeth II Diamond Jubilee Medal, awarded on June 18, 2012, for significant contributions to Canada during the sovereign's reign,[78] and the Queen Elizabeth II Platinum Jubilee Medal (Nova Scotia variant) in 2022. In October 2025, she was among recipients of the King Charles III Coronation Medal, recognizing community service amid the 2023 coronation commemorations.[79] Pre-tenure environmental recognitions include the Memorial Gold Medal from Charles University in Prague, awarded for her international policy work during her United Nations Environment Programme tenure.[41] Post-tenure, she received the 2024 Pollution Probe Award for sustained environmental advocacy.[80] These merit environmental distinctions underscore her career focus but lack the formal order structure of national or provincial honours.

Honorary Degrees and Heraldry

Dowdeswell has received multiple honorary doctorates recognizing her contributions to environmental management, public service, and governance. These include a Doctor of Laws from the University of Saskatchewan on May 25, 1994; a Doctor of Environmental Management from Utah State University in 1995; a Doctor of Laws from the University of Victoria in June 1997; a Doctor of Laws from York University in spring 1999 for public service in environmental management; an unspecified honorary doctorate from Royal Roads University in fall 1999; a Doctor of Science from Ontario Tech University in 2013; and a Doctor of Laws, jure dignitatis, from Western University in 2015.[5][81][82][83][19][84][85] Dowdeswell's personal coat of arms was granted by the Governor General of Canada on June 15, 2016, and registered with the Canadian Heraldic Authority.[86] The achievement features an oval shield in azure (blue), a shape traditionally used for women's arms in Canadian heraldry, bordered by an orle of argent (silver) and environed by alternating wheat sheaves and trilliums, symbolizing her Saskatchewan agricultural heritage and her service in Ontario, respectively. At the center is a golden balance surmounted by a terrestrial globe, representing her environmental protection work and role in legislative assent as Lieutenant Governor. The crest issues from a circlet of wheat garbs and trillium flowers, supporting a demi-globe with a balance, reinforcing themes of global equity and balance. A badge of a trillium flower proper further evokes Ontario's provincial emblem.[86] The design was created by Bruce Patterson, Deputy Chief Herald of Canada.[86]
