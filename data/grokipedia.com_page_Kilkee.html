Kilkee

Kilkee (Irish: Cill Chaoi)[7] is a coastal town in County Clare, Ireland. It is located in the parish of Kilkee (formerly Kilfearagh). Kilkee is midway between Kilrush and Doonbeg on the N67 road and is a popular seaside resort.[8] The horseshoe bay is protected from the Atlantic Ocean by the Duggerna Reef.

History

In the early 19th century, Kilkee was a small fishing village. Around the 1820s, a paddle steamer service from Limerick to Kilrush made Kilkee more accessible as a tourist destination, particularly for the Anglo-Irish aristocracy.[9] Catty Fitzgerald opened the first hotel, which operated for 40 years. By the 1830s, two more hotels opened in Kilkee. Along with these, three churches were built, a Roman Catholic church in 1831, a Protestant church in 1843, and a Methodist church in 1900.[10] Descriptions of Kilkee during the Irish Famine can be found in John Mannersâ€™s travel narrative Notes of an Irish Tour, in 1846[11] and Sydney Godolphin Osborne's Gleanings in the West of Ireland , originally published in 1850.[12] These describe a provincial town primarily attractive for landscapes and sea-bathing. In the 1890s, Kilkee saw further growth when the West Clare Railway extension opened in 1892, which facilitated the transportation of goods and people. Many prominent people travelled to Kilkee during this time, including Sir Aubrey de Vere, Charlotte BrontÃ«, Sir Henry Rider Haggard, and Alfred, Lord Tennyson. In 1896, the Crown Princess of Austria visited the town.[13] The entertainer Percy French was a regular performer in the town and an incident on the West Clare Railway on the way to Kilkee prompted him to write the song "Are Ye Right There Michael". Kilkee has regularly been awarded the Blue Flag by the European Commission[14]. In 2006, a statue of Richard Harris was unveiled in Kilkee by actor Russell Crowe. [15]

Shipwrecks around Kilkee

On 30 January 1836, the Intrinsic , a ship from Liverpool bound for New Orleans, was blown into a bay near Bishops Island in Kilkee. The ship was damaged against the cliffs and sank along with her crew of 14, of whom none survived. The shipwreck site is now called 'Intrinsic Bay'.[16] A chartered passenger sailing vessel named the Edmond sank at Edmond Point on 19 November 1850. The ship was sailing from Limerick to New York City but was driven into Kilkee Bay by a storm. Due to high tides, the ship was driven to Edmond Point, where it split in two. Of the 216 on board, 98 drowned in the disaster.[17] Exactly 50 years to the day after the Intrinsic sank, on 30 January 1886, the Fulmar sank just north of Kilkee in an area known as Farrihy Bay. The ship was a cargo vessel transporting coal from Troon in Scotland to Limerick, but never reached its destination. Of the 17 crew members aboard only one body was ever recovered.[18] At some point between 28 and 29 December 1894, the Inishtrahull went missing somewhere near the Kilkee coast. At the time of the disappearance, the ship was transporting a consignment of coal from Glasgow to Limerick but never reached its intended destination. The ship was only confirmed to have sunk on 3 January 1895, when a section of a port bow from a ship with a brass plate marked "Glasgow" was picked up by the Kilkee coastguard.[19]

Culture

Kilkee hosts several annual cultural festivals and events that highlight its heritage and artistic community. Between 2007 and 2009, the town was the site of the Cois Fharraige music and surfing festival, a three-day event combining live music performances with west coast surfing culture.) The Kilkee BrontÃ« Festival, held annually in September, celebrates the 1854 honeymoon of Charlotte BrontÃ« and Arthur Bell Nicholls in the town, featuring talks, workshops, exhibitions, and performances. As of 2025, the festival includes an official opening with literary discussions and a writer's workshop.[20] Another prominent event is the Che do Bheatha Festival, an annual celebration of Latin-American culture inspired by Che Guevara's visit to Kilkee in 1961. It includes music, dance, poetry, film screenings, and workshops, fostering links between Irish and Latin-American communities. The festival has been running since at least 2011.[21][22] Kilkee also participates in national events like Culture Night, with traditional Irish music sessions and heritage tours. The Kilkee Civic Trust promotes local arts through exhibitions, concerts, and opera evenings, preserving the town's cultural legacy.[23][24]

Sport and recreation

As well as bathing on the Strand, beachgoers can choose from the Pollock Holes, New Found Out, and Byrnes Cove. The Pollock Holes, also known as Duggerna Reef, are three natural rock-enclosed pools with water that change with every tide. This brings in fresh water and replenishes the marine life in the many rock pools surrounding it. The diving boards at New Found Out allow up to 13 meters (45 feet) into the open sea. The annual Pat Lawler Memorial Diving Competition is held at these boards.[25] Every year, there are many participants in the Bay Swim, a race of approximately 1.35 km from Byrnes Cove to New Found Out across the bay. Byrnes Cove is a sheltered cove close to George's Head, a prominent headland. There is also a mini Bay Swim for children under fifteen from Sandy Cove to the Pier. As of 2025, the event continues annually in August.[26] The last weekend in June sees an influx of triathletes as Kilkee hosts the 'Hell of the West Triathlon', the longest-running triathlon in Ireland since 1985. This is one of the biggest and toughest triathlons on the Irish Triathlon calendar, with around 500 athletes taking part in a 1500-metre swim, 44 km cycle, and 10 km run. In 2025, it marked its 40th anniversary.[27] A local wall racquet game, similar to squash, has been played against the high sandstone walls in the West End for generations. The main trophy, the Tivoli Cup, was first competed for in Kilkee in 1935. Richard Harris, who would go on to become an internationally known actor, won the cup four years in a row, from 1948 to 1951, a record unsurpassed to this day. The Strand Races are horse races contested annually on the Kilkee strand. They first began in the 19th century on the sand hills where the golf club is now. The races are normally held over two days in September when the summer season is drawing to a close. The course is made by placing poles on the beach and when the tide goes out the races begin. Traditionally it was a celebration for farmers when the harvesting season was over. As of 2025, the event continues annually.[28] A short-lived greyhound racing track was opened by the Kilkee Greyhound Racing Company on 2 July 1936.[29] The land and assets of the track (behind the Olympia Hall and Merton Square) were put up for sale in 1942.

Transport

Before the West Clare Railway reached Kilkee in 1892, the only way to get to the town was by paddle steamer from Limerick to Kilrush and then by horse and cart from there. This service ran from 1816 until 1918 but was stopped after World War I due to the popularity of the railway, although for many years the railway and steamer services ran together with a special "Steamer Express" train to and from Kilkee.[31] After the railway closed in 1961, access to the resort was primarily by car or bus but as the mainline rail system now connects Limerick and Galway to Ennis, it is still possible to get the train as far as Ennis. As of 2025, the main bus routes offered by Bus Ã‰ireann from Kilkee are Routes 333 and 336 to Kilrush, Ennis, and Limerick (via Shannon Airport), with additional local services by TFI Local Link (e.g., Route 339 to Loop Head). It is possible to get to Cork or even Dublin through connecting buses or trains.[32][33][34] For international visitors, the closest airport is in Shannon.
