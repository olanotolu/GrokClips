al-Zulfikar

Al-Zulfikar was a left-wing militant organization founded in 1979 by Murtaza Bhutto and Shahnawaz Bhutto, sons of executed Pakistani Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, to avenge their father's judicial hanging and overthrow General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's military regime through armed insurgency.[1][2] Operating primarily from Kabul, Afghanistan, under the protection of the Soviet-backed Afghan government and its KHAD intelligence agency, the group received financial, logistical, and military support from Libya under Muammar Gaddafi, Syria under Hafez al-Assad, and the Palestine Liberation Organization led by Yasser Arafat.[1][2] The organization achieved international notoriety through its orchestration of the 1981 hijacking of Pakistan International Airlines Flight 326, which lasted 13 days and involved diverting the aircraft from Karachi to Kabul and then Damascus, demanding the release of over 90 political prisoners held by Zia's government; the operation culminated in the murder of hostage Major Tariq Rahim but secured the freeing of 54 detainees.[1][2][3] Al-Zulfikar also conducted assassination attempts on Zia-ul-Haq, including a 1982 missile strike on his plane, and carried out bombings targeting Pakistani institutions and officials, reflecting its commitment to socialist revolutionary tactics amid internal Pakistani opposition to Zia's Islamization policies.[1][2] Despite initial successes in garnering attention for the Bhutto cause, Al-Zulfikar faced condemnation from the mainstream Pakistan Peoples Party leadership, including Benazir Bhutto, for its terrorist methods, and it disintegrated by the mid-1980s due to leadership deathsâ€”Shahnawaz by poisoning in 1985â€”and internal factionalism, with remaining members either arrested or killed.[1][2] The group's activities highlighted the volatile interplay of familial vendettas, ideological militancy, and Cold War proxy dynamics in Pakistan's political landscape during Zia's rule.[1][2]

Background and Formation

Historical Context

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto established the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) in 1967 as a populist movement advocating socialist reforms, nationalization of industries, and opposition to military dominance in politics.[4] After the 1971 Indo-Pakistani War and secession of East Pakistan, Bhutto assumed the presidency in December 1971 and transitioned to prime minister under the 1973 constitution, implementing land reforms and expanding state control over the economy. His administration, however, encountered mounting opposition due to economic stagnation, suppression of dissent, and claims of electoral manipulation in the March 1977 general elections, which the PPP won amid allegations of widespread rigging by rivals, precipitating violent protests across urban centers.[2] On July 5, 1977, General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq, the army chief of staff, executed Operation Fair Play, a bloodless military coup that ousted Bhutto's government, dissolved the National Assembly, and declared martial law nationwide.[5] Bhutto was arrested shortly thereafter on charges of conspiring to murder a political opponent in Lahore in 1974; following a trial in a special court, he received a death sentence on March 18, 1978, which Pakistan's Supreme Court upheld in a 4-3 decision on February 6, 1979, despite international appeals for clemency. Bhutto was executed by hanging on April 4, 1979, at Rawalpindi Central Jail, an outcome decried by supporters as politically motivated retribution under Zia's regime, which proceeded to Islamize laws, ban political activities, and postpone promised elections indefinitely.[6][7][8] Zia's consolidation of power through purges of PPP loyalists and imposition of hudud ordinances created a repressive environment that radicalized segments of the opposition, particularly Bhutto's family in exile. Initially organized as the underground People's Liberation Army to coerce the regime into releasing Bhutto unharmed, the group reemerged post-execution as Al-Zulfikar in late 1979, led by Bhutto's sons Murtaza and Shahnawaz from bases in Kabul and Tripoli, with the explicit aim of overthrowing the dictatorship via armed resistance amid suppressed democratic aspirations.[1][9][2]

Founding and Early Organization

Al-Zulfikar, a militant organization also known as AZO, was established in late 1979 by Mir Murtaza Bhutto, then aged 25, and his younger brother Shahnawaz Bhutto, in direct response to the execution of their father, former Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, on April 4, 1979, under General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's military regime.[1] [2] The group's primary aim was to overthrow Zia's government through armed resistance and to avenge Zulfikar's death, after diplomatic efforts by the Bhutto family in London failed to prevent the execution.[1] Initially conceived as the People's Liberation Army (PLA), an underground network intended to pressure Pakistan's military rulers, it was renamed Al-Zulfikarâ€”meaning "the sword" in Arabic, referencing Zulfikar's nameâ€”and operated from exile to evade Zia's crackdown on PPP loyalists.[9] [10] Following Zulfikar's execution, Murtaza and Shahnawaz fled Pakistan for Kabul, Afghanistan, which had come under a Soviet-backed communist government after the 1978 Saur Revolution, providing a sympathetic base for anti-Zia operations.[11] [2] From Kabul, the brothers established the group's headquarters, leveraging the Afghan regime's asylum for Pakistani exiles opposed to Zia.[1] External support was secured early on, including training facilities, logistics, and funding from Libyan leader Muammar Gaddafi, the Syrian government, and the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), which facilitated guerrilla tactics and ideological alignment with leftist causes.[1] [2] In its formative phase, Al-Zulfikar remained a small, clandestine network comprising a core of Pakistan People's Party (PPP) activists, members of the PPP's student wing (Pakistan Students' Federation, or PSF), and select military defectors, with initial recruitment focused on ideologically committed exiles in Kabul.[1] [2] Key early figures included Raja Anwar, a close associate who joined the founding efforts, and Salamullah Tipu, a young operative involved in planning from the outset.[1] Basic military training occurred in Afghan camps, emphasizing small-unit tactics suitable for sabotage and assassination, though the organization suffered from limited resources and internal coordination challenges in its first years.[2] By 1980, efforts expanded to include outreach to imprisoned PPP sympathizers in Pakistan, setting the stage for high-profile actions to bolster recruitment and secure prisoner releases.[9]

Leadership and Structure

Key Figures

Mir Murtaza Bhutto (1954â€“1996) founded al-Zulfikar in late 1979 alongside his younger brother Shahnawaz Bhutto, shortly after the execution of their father, former Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, by the regime of General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq.[1] As the group's commander-in-chief, Murtaza directed its transformation from a loose network of Pakistan People's Party (PPP) sympathizers into a structured militant outfit with training camps in Kabul, Afghanistan, and later Libya, focusing initially on armed reprisals against Zia's military rule before evolving toward Sindhi nationalist objectives.[12] He oversaw major operations, including the 1981 hijacking of Pakistan International Airlines Flight 326 and attempted assassinations of Zia using Soviet-supplied SAM-7 missiles in 1982, while enforcing internal discipline through executions of suspected infiltrators, such as 27 members purged between 1981 and 1984.[12] Murtaza returned to Pakistan in 1993, founding the PPP faction Jeay Sindh, but was killed in a September 20, 1996, police encounter in Karachi amid disputed circumstances involving alleged corruption and separatist activities.[1] Shahnawaz Bhutto (1958â€“1985), Murtaza's co-founder, handled early intelligence gathering and recruitment from PPP ranks, serving as head of the group's political wing and personal bodyguard to his brother during exile operations in Kabul.[12] He contributed to planning the 1981 hijacking and internal purges, including the 1981 execution of operative Dilip Kumar Afridi for suspected treason, while composing propaganda materials like revolutionary songs broadcast during the incident.[12] Shahnawaz relocated to France in the mid-1980s, where he died on July 18, 1985, from cyanide poisoning under unresolved circumstances, officially ruled a suicide but widely suspected as assassination by Zia's agents.[1] Salamullah Tipu (d. 1984), a fiercely loyal lieutenant and former Pakistani army cadet from Karachi, emerged as al-Zulfikar's chief operational commander and executioner, leading the March 2, 1981, hijacking of PIA Flight 326 from Karachi to Kabul, which secured the release of over 50 imprisoned PPP activists in exchange for the hostage of a cabinet minister.[1] Tipu, alongside hijackers Nasir Jamal and Arshad Ali Khan, enforced Murtaza's directives in Kabul, including the killings of operatives Pervez Shinwari (1983) and Tariq Rahim, but fell out with leadership over strategic disputes and was executed by Afghan authorities on Murtaza's orders in July 1984 following his arrest.[12] Raja Anwar, an early adviser and co-founder with prior ties to Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's circle, provided ideological and organizational input during al-Zulfikar's formation in 1979 but clashed with Murtaza's authoritarian style, leading to his four-year imprisonment in Kabul's Pul-i-Charkhi prison from 1980 to 1984.[1] Later authoring The Terrorist Prince (1997), Anwar detailed the group's internal dynamics from an insider perspective, highlighting its reliance on foreign patrons like Libya's Muammar Gaddafi and Afghanistan's Babrak Karmal regime for arms and sanctuary.[12]

Organizational Hierarchy

Al-Zulfikar operated under a centralized leadership dominated by Mir Murtaza Bhutto, who served as secretary-general and primary operational commander, directing activities from exile bases including Kabul, Damascus, and later India.[1][13] Co-founded by his brother Shahnawaz Bhutto in late 1979, the group drew initial recruits from disaffected members of the Pakistan People's Party's student wing, the People's Students Federation (PSF), and left-wing activists affiliated with the Communist Mazdoor Kissan Party, forming a core of around 200 young operatives aged 17-27 by the early 1980s.[1][2] The structure emphasized clandestine cells for infiltration and sabotage within Pakistan, supported logistically by host governments in Afghanistan (via KHAD intelligence), Libya under Muammar Gaddafi, and Syria under Hafiz al-Assad, with additional bases in London and Tripoli.[1][13][2] Operational roles were assigned to trusted figures such as Salamullah Tipu, who led the 1981 hijacking of Pakistan International Airlines Flight 326, and other key operatives including Raja Anwar, Nasir Jamal, and Arshad Khan, who handled specific missions like assassinations and bombings.[1][13] This cell-based model allowed compartmentalized actions but fostered internal paranoia, resulting in purges, executions of suspected traitors (e.g., Tipu in 1984), and eventual fragmentation by the mid-1980s.[1][2]

Ideology and Objectives

Political Orientation

Al-Zulfikar adopted a left-wing orientation, positioning itself as a militant extension of the socialist principles originally championed by the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) under Zulfikar Ali Bhutto. The group emerged in response to General Zia-ul-Haq's 1977 military coup, which executed Bhutto in 1979 and imposed Islamization policies antithetical to the PPP's secular-leaning socialism. Al-Zulfikar's founders, including Murtaza Bhutto, sought to revive the PPP's foundational manifesto of "food, clothing, and shelter" through armed struggle, rejecting electoral politics in favor of revolutionary tactics against perceived authoritarianism.[1] The organization's ideology drew from Marxist-influenced socialism, emphasizing class struggle and anti-imperialism, while opposing Zia's alliances with conservative Islamic elements and the United States. Members described their efforts as socialist revolutionary action to dismantle military rule and restore a populist, egalitarian order inspired by Bhutto's era of nationalization and land reforms. This stance aligned Al-Zulfikar with global leftist militant movements, including training affiliations in Libya and Syria, though it maintained a nationalist Pakistani focus.[2][14] Critics, including Pakistani authorities, labeled Al-Zulfikar a terrorist entity devoid of coherent ideology beyond personal vengeance, but its manifestos and operations consistently invoked socialist rhetoric against feudalism, capitalism, and military dictatorship. The group's secularism starkly contrasted with Zia's religious conservatism, contributing to its portrayal in official narratives as subversive and atheistic. Despite internal factionalism, Al-Zulfikar's political compass remained anchored in leftist radicalism until its decline in the late 1980s.[15]

Strategic Aims

Al-Zulfikar's primary strategic aim was the violent overthrow of General Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq's military regime, which had seized power in a coup on July 5, 1977, and executed Zulfikar Ali Bhutto on April 4, 1979. Founded by Bhutto's sons, Murtaza and Shahnawaz, the group explicitly sought to avenge their father's death through armed struggle, viewing Zia's Islamization policies and suppression of the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) as existential threats to democratic socialism.[16][17] This objective aligned with broader destabilization efforts to undermine Zia's authority and facilitate a return to civilian rule under PPP influence.[1] The organization pursued these goals by establishing operational networks abroad, including training camps in Libya and Afghanistan, to conduct guerrilla-style attacks on Pakistani military and government targets. Key tactics included high-profile operations like assassinations of Zia loyalists and disruptions such as the 1981 hijacking of Pakistan International Airlines Flight 326, intended to secure prisoner releases and draw international attention to the regime's illegitimacy.[10] Al-Zulfikar also aimed to exploit ethnic and sectarian divisions within Pakistan's security apparatus, recruiting disaffected Sindhi and Baloch elements to erode Zia's support base.[16] Long-term, the group envisioned a post-Zia Pakistan restoring Bhutto-era policies of land reforms, nationalization, and nuclear development, while countering perceived Punjabi military dominance through alliances with leftist factions and foreign patrons like the Soviet Union during the Afghan conflict. However, internal CIA assessments noted that Al-Zulfikar's uncompromising focus on vengeance limited broader coalition-building, alienating mainstream PPP leaders who preferred non-violent opposition.[17][18]

Operations and Key Events

Training and Operational Bases

Al-Zulfikar established its initial training facilities in Libya following the execution of Zulfikar Ali Bhutto on April 4, 1979, with Muammar Gaddafi setting up a camp near Tripoli specifically for early recruits in guerrilla warfare, operated by Palestine Liberation Organization instructors.[11][19] This camp provided logistical and financial support from the Libyan government but had closed by the time subsequent batches of members arrived in the early 1980s.[11] The group's primary operational base was in Kabul, Afghanistan, established by late 1979 with backing from the Babrak Karmal regime and its intelligence agency, KHAD, which facilitated recruitment transit through Pakistan's tribal areas and hosted Al-Zulfikar's headquarters in a compound in the Wazir Akbar Khan neighborhood.[11][10] A second wave of recruits reached Kabul in 1981, using the location for planning cross-border activities, though Afghan authorities later pressured the group to reduce visibility after high-profile operations like the March 1981 PIA hijacking.[11][20] Additional training occurred in Pakistan's Kurram Agency under Shahnawaz Bhutto's command, serving as a forward area for infiltration and preparation near the Afghan border.[10] Following the 1981 hijacking, Murtaza Bhutto relocated operations to Damascus, Syria, where Hafiz al-Assad's government provided refuge and funding, allowing some members to settle there while others dispersed or vanished en route from Kabul.[11] Relations with the Afghan hosts deteriorated by 1984, exemplified by the execution of operative Alamgir (Mohammed Salamullah) in Kabul amid internal disputes, prompting further fragmentation of bases.[20] These foreign-supported sites enabled Al-Zulfikar's asymmetric tactics but exposed it to geopolitical shifts, including Soviet influence in Afghanistan and Libyan volatility.[15]

The 1981 PIA Flight Hijacking

On March 2, 1981, three members of al-Zulfikarâ€”Salamullah Tipu, Nasir Jamal, and Arshad Ali Khan Tegiâ€”hijacked Pakistan International Airlines Flight PK-326, a Boeing 720B en route from Karachi to Peshawar with approximately 132 passengers and 9 crew members aboard.[21][22] The hijackers, armed with pistols and explosives, seized control mid-flight and diverted the aircraft to Kabul, Afghanistan, where they claimed responsibility on behalf of al-Zulfikar to avenge the execution of Zulfikar Ali Bhutto and pressure General Zia-ul-Haq's regime.[15][21] Their primary demand was the release of 92 political prisoners held by Pakistan, later adjusted to 55, including al-Zulfikar operatives arrested for prior subversive activities.[22][21] Initially, Afghan authorities refueled the plane and provided logistical support, amid reports of assistance from the Afghan intelligence service KHAD.[22] Zia-ul-Haq refused to negotiate directly or yield to the demands, prompting the hijackers to escalate by releasing select passengersâ€”29 women, children, and the sick on March 4, followed by two more ill individuals on March 7â€”while holding the remainder hostage under harsh conditions of confinement and threats.[22] On March 6, to underscore their resolve, the hijackers executed passenger Tariq Rahim, a Pakistani diplomatic service officer and son of a retired general, dumping his body onto the Kabul tarmac as a warning.[15][21] This act of violence, the hijacking's only fatality, intensified international pressure but did not immediately alter Pakistan's stance.[22] By March 8, the aircraft was flown to Damascus, Syria, extending the standoff as negotiations stalled; the hijackers threatened further killings, including three American passengers, unless concessions were made.[22][21] Pakistan eventually agreed to release 54 prisoners, who were flown to Syria on March 14, prompting the hijackers to surrender to Syrian authorities later that evening.[15][21] All remaining hostages were freed after 13 daysâ€”the longest aircraft hijacking on record at the timeâ€”allowing the plane's return to Pakistan.[22][23] The operation, while securing limited releases, drew condemnation from the Pakistan Peoples Party leadership, including Benazir Bhutto, who disavowed al-Zulfikar's methods despite shared opposition to Zia's rule.[15]

Other Notable Attacks and Activities

Al-Zulfikar undertook a series of assassinations and attempted attacks targeting individuals linked to the Zia ul-Haq regime, often resulting in the capture and execution of perpetrators by Pakistani authorities. In the early 1980s, the group's initial batch of operatives assassinated an unnamed civilian member of the regime upon infiltration into Pakistan.[11] A subsequent operation targeted Justice Anwarul Haq, the judge who sentenced Zulfikar Ali Bhutto to death; the attempt failed, leading to the 1984 hangings of involved members Usman Ghani, Idris Todi, and Idrees Beg.[11] Further assassinations included that of Chaudhri Zahoor Elahi, a cabinet minister aligned with Zia, for which Razaq Jharna was arrested in Lahore and executed by hanging in 1983.[11] In 1985, a third wave of Sindhi recruits murdered a pro-Zia politician in Karachi, though several participants were soon arrested and faced execution.[11] Pakistani police also attributed to Al-Zulfikar several unspecified attacks on law enforcement personnel and bomb explosions in the early 1980s.[13] Beyond domestic operations, Al-Zulfikar pursued international plots, including a 1983 scheme to assault the US and Israeli embassies in Europe; operatives dispatched to Vienna were apprehended with weapons and sentenced to 15-25 years imprisonment.[11] An effort to assassinate Zia during his visit to India similarly miscarried, hastening the 1984 execution of planner Salamullah "Tipu" Jayaraj.[11] The organization claimed responsibility for 54 operations against government installations and personnel, though independent verification of this figure remains limited.[10]

Decline and Aftermath

Internal Challenges and Losses

Al-Zulfikar's internal cohesion eroded due to leader Murtaza Bhutto's increasing paranoia regarding infiltrators from the Zia regime, prompting him to authorize the elimination of suspected traitors within the group, often in collaboration with Afghanistan's KHAD intelligence. This mistrust manifested in factional tensions, such as the rift with deputy leader Raja Anwar, who advocated for political rather than militant strategies and was imprisoned by KHAD from late 1980 to 1983 on Murtaza's orders, amid accusations of disloyalty. Similarly, operative Salamullah Tipu clashed with Murtaza over operational recklessness; after Tipu was tasked with targeting alleged internal threats in 1983, Murtaza implicated him in a murder, leading to Tipu's arrest and execution by KHAD in 1984.[11][1] Significant losses compounded these divisions, with the organization's first cadre of recruitsâ€”primarily young PPP sympathizers released from Pakistani jailsâ€”suffering near-total attrition by early 1981 through failed bank robberies, attacks, arrests, and executions during incursions into Pakistan. Between 1979 and 1993, approximately 200 operatives, mostly aged 17 to 27, were killed or imprisoned, including high-profile cases like the 1983 hanging of Razaq Jharna and the 1984 execution of Usman Ghani; many were buried in unmarked graves in Kabul or Libya. The sudden death of Murtaza's brother and co-founder Shahnawaz Bhutto in July 1985 from suspected poisoning in Cannes, France, further demoralized the group, prompting the closure of its Kabul operations and a pivot toward Sindhi ethnic nationalism by 1986, which alienated non-Sindhi members and accelerated fragmentation.[11][1] These internal purges and casualties, alongside poor training and operational failures, rendered Al-Zulfikar ineffective by mid-decade, with surviving members dispersing to exile in Libya, Syria, or Europe; the group formally disbanded around 1990, its Tripoli training camp shuttered, though sporadic violence persisted until the last known operative's death in 2000.[11][1]

Suppression by Pakistani Authorities

Following the March 1981 hijacking of a Pakistan International Airlines flight by Al-Zulfikar operatives, the government of President Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq exploited the incident to launch a broader crackdown on the group and associated opposition elements, including the Movement for the Restoration of Democracy (MRD). Martial law authorities arrested thousands suspected of subversion, with over 1,400 detentions reported in Punjab and Sind provinces in February 1982 alone, targeting networks sympathetic to the Pakistan People's Party (PPP) and Al-Zulfikar.[24][11] The Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI) and military supported intensified operations against Al-Zulfikar, leading to the capture of members attempting domestic actions such as assassinations and bank robberies. Several operatives faced swift trials in special martial law courts, resulting in lengthy imprisonments and public executions by hanging. Notable cases included the 1983 hanging of Razaq Jharna in Lahore for the assassination of federal minister Chaudhry Zahoor Elahi, the 1984 execution of Nasir Baloch in Karachi for facilitating the hijacking, and the 1984 hangings of Usman Ghani, Idris Todi, and Idrees Beg in connection with an attempt on Supreme Court Chief Justice Anwar-ul-Haq.[11][1] In 1985, Ayaz Samu was hanged in Karachi for murdering a pro-Zia politician.[11] These measures, combined with the loss of foreign patronage from Afghanistan and Libya by 1985, severely eroded Al-Zulfikar's operational capacity, forcing survivors into exile or incarceration. Additional arrests occurred internationally, such as the 1983 detention of members in Vienna for plotting to storm the Israeli Embassy, where they received sentences of 15 to 25 years. By the late 1980s, the group's activities within Pakistan had ceased, culminating in its formal dissolution by founder Murtaza Bhutto in 1990.[11][1]

Impact and Controversies

Political Legacy

Al-Zulfikar's political legacy is marked by its ultimate failure to effect systemic change, serving instead as a cautionary example of the futility of armed militancy against Pakistan's entrenched military establishment. Formed in late 1979 by Murtaza Bhutto and his brother Shahnawaz with the explicit goal of overthrowing General Zia-ul-Haq's regime through socialist revolution and vengeance for their father's execution on April 4, 1979, the group achieved limited tactical successes, such as the March 2, 1981, hijacking of PIA Flight PK-326, which compelled the release of 54 political prisoners by March 15, 1981.[1][15] However, by the mid-1980s, internal paranoia, purges, and state crackdowns had dismantled the organization, with key figures like Salamullah Tipu executed in 1984 and Shahnawaz dying under suspicious circumstances in 1985 in Cannes, leaving no enduring institutional structure or ideological movement.[2][1] The group's activities deepened fractures within the Bhutto family and the Pakistan People's Party (PPP), as mainstream PPP leadership under Benazir Bhutto explicitly denied any ties to al-Zulfikar, prioritizing electoral strategies that secured her premierships in 1988 and 1993 over revolutionary violence.[15] Murtaza's persistence in militant paths isolated him; upon his 1993 return to Pakistan after 17 years in exile, he founded the PPP (Shaheed Bhutto) faction to challenge his sister's dominance, but it failed to garner significant support amid accusations of ethnic favoritism toward Sindh.[1] This schism highlighted a broader shift in PPP ideology away from Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's radical socialism toward pragmatic democracy, underscoring how al-Zulfikar's excesses discredited extra-legal resistance.[2] In its waning phase from 1986 to 1992, al-Zulfikar pivoted toward Sindhi nationalism, orchestrating sabotage in Sindh province, yet these efforts yielded no territorial or political gains and dissipated with Murtaza's killing in a September 20, 1996, police encounter in Karachi, an event that spawned enduring conspiracy theories implicating Benazir's government and perpetuating dynastic martyrdom narratives without resurrecting the group's methods.[1] Of its estimated 200 young operativesâ€”mostly aged 17 to 27â€”many perished in operations or custody, their graves scattered in Kabul and Libya, symbolizing the human cost of a venture that reinforced the security state's resilience rather than inspiring subsequent leftist insurgencies.[1][2] Ultimately, al-Zulfikar's collapse validated electoral politics as the viable opposition path in post-Zia Pakistan, marginalizing armed struggle as a relic of personal vendetta over collective reform.[2]

Criticisms and Ethical Debates

Al-Zulfikar's tactics, including the 1981 hijacking of Pakistan International Airlines Flight PK-326, drew widespread condemnation for endangering civilian lives and employing terrorist methods to achieve political ends. The operation, led by operatives under Murtaza Bhutto's direction, involved diverting the plane to Kabul, holding over 100 passengers hostage for 13 days, and demanding the release of imprisoned Pakistan Peoples Party members; during the standoff, hijackers executed a passenger and threatened further killings, actions that violated international aviation norms and prompted international outrage.[15][10] Benazir Bhutto, then co-chairperson of the PPP, publicly denounced the hijacking, arguing it undermined the party's non-violent opposition to General Zia-ul-Haq's regime and alienated potential democratic allies.[11] Critics, including Pakistani authorities and independent analysts, accused Al-Zulfikar of treasonous alliances with foreign adversaries, such as training camps in Libya under Muammar Gaddafi, Syria, and Soviet-backed Afghanistan's KHAD intelligence during the 1980s, which provided logistical support but aligned the group with regimes hostile to Pakistan's sovereignty.[1] These partnerships fueled debates over whether the group's armed struggle against Zia's dictatorship constituted legitimate resistance or compromised national interests by importing external ideologies and resources, potentially prolonging internal instability.[2] Raja Anwar's biography of Murtaza Bhutto, The Terrorist Prince, portrays such collaborations as opportunistic, arguing they transformed a vengeance-driven outfit into a proxy for anti-Pakistan elements rather than a pure anti-dictatorship force.[1] Ethical concerns extended to Al-Zulfikar's internal practices, including summary executions of suspected traitors and botched assassination attempts that killed unintended targets, such as the 1981 Lahore bombing aimed at Zia but resulting in civilian casualties.[1] Reports indicate the group assassinated members or affiliates blamed for operational failures, reflecting a culture of paranoia and authoritarian control under Murtaza Bhutto that mirrored the very military rule it opposed.[1] Broader debates question the moral calculus of prioritizing familial revengeâ€”avenging Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's 1979 executionâ€”over civilian safety and democratic norms, with some scholars contending that such militancy eroded public trust in the PPP's broader platform and invited state crackdowns that stifled legitimate opposition.[11] These actions, documented in declassified accounts and contemporary journalism, underscore tensions between causal justifications for insurgency against authoritarianism and the indiscriminate harm inflicted, without evidence of proportionate strategic gains.[2]
