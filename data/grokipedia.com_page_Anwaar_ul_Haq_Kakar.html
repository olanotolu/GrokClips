Anwaar ul Haq Kakar


Anwaar-ul-Haq Kakar (born 15 May 1971) is a Pakistani politician who served as the caretaker Prime Minister of Pakistan from 14 August 2023 to 4 March 2024, marking the longest tenure in that interim role.[1][2] Hailing from Muslim Bagh in Qilla Saifullah district of Balochistan, he was born into a middle-class Pashtun family and obtained a master's degree in political science and sociology from the University of Balochistan.[3][1] Kakar's early career included journalism and public relations, notably as spokesperson for the Government of Balochistan from 2015 to 2017.[4] He entered formal politics with affiliations to the Balochistan Awami Party (BAP) and was elected to the Senate in March 2018, serving until March 2024 initially as an independent but aligned with BAP interests.[5][6] His selection as caretaker prime minister followed the dissolution of the National Assembly in August 2023, amid a period of political transition after the removal of the prior administration via a no-confidence vote.[5] During his premiership, Kakar focused on economic stabilization, securing international financial support, and conducting foreign engagements such as leading Pakistan's delegation to COP28 in Dubai, where he addressed climate vulnerabilities.[7] He oversaw the holding of delayed general elections in February 2024, navigating logistical and security challenges in Balochistan and other regions.[8] Notable controversies arose from his administration's handling of Baloch protests in Islamabad, where he publicly criticized external support for demonstrators amid ongoing insurgency concerns in his home province.[9] Kakar's tenure highlighted tensions between federal authority and regional grievances, particularly in Balochistan, where BAP's pro-establishment stance has drawn scrutiny for prioritizing stability over separatist demands.[10]

Early life and education

Family background and upbringing

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar was born on May 15, 1971, in the Muslim Bagh area of Qilla Saifullah district, Balochistan province, Pakistan.[3][11] He belongs to the Kakar tribe, a prominent Pashtun ethnic group primarily residing in northern Balochistan and known for its historical influence among Pashtun communities.[8][12] Kakar was raised in a middle-class family with roots tracing to the Kan Mehtarzai area of Balochistan.[12] His paternal grandfather, Dr. Noor ul Haq Kakar, served as the personal physician to the Khan of Kalat, the pre-partition ruler of the princely state that encompassed much of modern Balochistan.[13] His father, Ehtesham ul Haq Kakar, worked as a civil servant in various government positions, reflecting a family tradition of public service amid the region's tribal and administrative dynamics.[14][11] The family's Pashtun heritage and connections to Balochistan's governance structures shaped Kakar's early environment, in a province marked by ethnic diversity, resource scarcity, and ongoing tribal influences.[8] Limited public details exist on his immediate siblings or maternal lineage, though the emphasis on civil and medical professions in his paternal line underscores a emphasis on education and state involvement over purely tribal affiliations.[14]

Academic qualifications

Kakar obtained master's degrees in political science and sociology from the University of Balochistan during the 1990s.[3][15][16] He later pursued but did not complete legal studies at Birkbeck, University of London.[17]

Pre-political career

Journalistic endeavors

In December 2015, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar was appointed as spokesperson for the Government of Balochistan, serving until January 2018 under the Pakistan Muslim League-Nawaz (PML-N) administration led by Chief Minister Nawab Sanaullah Zehri.[3][16] In this capacity, he handled media relations, issuing official statements and engaging with journalists on provincial issues, which enhanced his reputation for media proficiency.[16] During his tenure as spokesperson, Kakar founded the Voice of Balochistan (VOB) in 2016, an organization he established as its chief executive officer and later served as patron-in-chief.[18][19] VOB operates as a non-governmental initiative focused on perception management, primarily through social media campaigns that promote narratives aligned with Pakistani state interests in Balochistan, including countering allegations of human rights abuses and enforced disappearances.[3][19] The entity maintains a network of online accounts to disseminate information on regional development, security, and counter-insurgency efforts, reflecting Kakar's strategic emphasis on digital media for public discourse.[18] Kakar's involvement in VOB extended to advisory roles in information dissemination, leveraging his position in the provincial information department to shape coverage of Balochistan's challenges, such as insurgency and resource disputes.[18] These efforts positioned him as a key figure in provincial media strategy prior to his full entry into elected politics, though VOB's activities have drawn scrutiny for prioritizing official viewpoints over independent reporting.[19]

Academic and professional roles

Prior to his entry into formal politics, Kakar commenced his professional career as a school teacher in Balochistan.[20][4] In his academic roles, Kakar served as a visiting faculty member at the Command and Staff College in Quetta, a premier institution for training Pakistan Armed Forces officers, where he delivered lectures on strategic and international relations topics.[21][22] He also held a similar position at the National Defence University in Islamabad, contributing to courses on foreign policy and defense studies as an alumnus of its programs.[22][23] These engagements leveraged his master's degrees in political science and sociology from the University of Balochistan, emphasizing practical insights into regional geopolitics and security dynamics.[3][15]

Political career

Initial involvement and party affiliations

Kakar's entry into formal politics occurred in the mid-2000s when he joined the Pakistan Muslim League (Qaid-e-Azam) (PML-Q), a party aligned with former President Pervez Musharraf's regime. He contested the 2008 general election as a PML-Q candidate for the National Assembly seat from NA-260 (Quetta), facing off against established opponents but ultimately losing the race.[24][25] Following this, Kakar shifted affiliations toward the Pakistan Muslim League (Nawaz) (PML-N), serving briefly as Information Advisor to Balochistan Chief Minister Nawab Sanaullah Zehriâ€”a PML-N leaderâ€”in 2013, and later as spokesperson for the PML-N-led Balochistan provincial government starting in December 2015, where he advocated publicly for security operations against insurgents.[26][19][16] In early 2018, amid internal dissent within PML-N's Balochistan chapter, Kakar co-founded the Balochistan Awami Party (BAP) on March 29, drawing from disaffected PML-N and PML-Q members focused on provincial issues. He was appointed BAP's central spokesperson that year and ran as an independent in the subsequent Senate elections from Balochistan, securing a seat while maintaining close ties to the party.[3][8][26]

Senatorial service (2006â€“2013)

Kakar affiliated with the Pakistan Muslim League (Qaid-e-Azam) in 2006, marking the start of his formal political engagement, but he did not serve as a senator during the 2006â€“2013 period.[19] Instead, his activities focused on party involvement and electoral participation, including an unsuccessful candidacy for a National Assembly seat from Quetta (NA-272) in the February 2008 general elections, where he secured fewer votes than the Pakistan Peoples Party contender.[24][27] This defeat reflected the PML-Q's weakening position amid the post-Musharraf political transition, as the party, once dominant, struggled against rising opposition.[25] Throughout this timeframe, Kakar remained active within PML-Q circles in Balochistan, advocating for provincial issues amid ongoing insurgency and governance challenges, though without a parliamentary platform.[24] No records indicate his election to the Senate, whose terms during these years were filled via provincial assembly votes in 2006 (for half the seats) and 2009 (for the remainder), predominantly by established PML-Q, PPP, and independent figures from Balochistan.[3] His subsequent shift to PML-N in 2013 preceded a later independent Senate victory in 2018.[6]

Senatorial service (2018â€“2024)

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar was elected to the Senate of Pakistan as an independent candidate from Balochistan on March 12, 2018, securing one of the general seats in the province-wide elections held that month.[13] His term was scheduled to run until March 2024.[6] During his senatorial tenure, Kakar served as a founding member and central spokesperson of the Balochistan Awami Party (BAP), a group formed by dissident senators from Balochistan in 2018 to advocate for provincial interests.[3] In committee roles, he chaired the Senate Standing Committee on Overseas Pakistanis and Human Resource Development, focusing on policies for expatriate Pakistanis and labor exports.[3] He also held memberships in the Senate Standing Committees on Foreign Affairs, Housing and Works, and Science and Technology, contributing to deliberations on international relations, urban development, and technological advancement.[28] Kakar participated in parliamentary efforts addressing minority protections, including as chairman of the Parliamentary Committee to Protect Minorities from Forced Conversions, where in October 2020 he expressed support for proposed legislation aimed at curbing forced conversions and enhancing minority rights.[29] His Senate activities included speeches critiquing governance issues, such as calls for accountability and responses to security incidents in Balochistan, though specific legislative initiatives sponsored by him were limited.[30] Kakar resigned from the Senate upon his appointment as caretaker Prime Minister on August 14, 2023, effectively concluding his active senatorial service ahead of the term's end.[13]

Tenure as Caretaker Prime Minister (2023â€“2024)

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar was sworn in as Pakistan's eighth caretaker prime minister on 14 August 2023, shortly after the dissolution of the National Assembly on 9 August.[31] His selection followed consultations between outgoing Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and opposition leader Raja Pervez Ashraf, with President Arif Alvi approving the nomination.[32] As an independent senator from Balochistan, Kakar was viewed as a neutral figure to oversee the interim period leading to general elections, amid a backdrop of economic distress, political polarization, and escalating security threats.[26] He resigned his leadership role in the Balochistan Awami Party to assume the position, emphasizing continuity in governance without partisan influence.[33] The administration prioritized economic stabilization, inheriting a $3 billion Stand-By Arrangement with the International Monetary Fund (IMF) approved on 12 July 2023.[34] Kakar met IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva on 21 September 2023, expressing commitment to the program's reforms, including fiscal consolidation, debt reduction, and protecting social spending.[35] By November 2023, a staff-level agreement was reached on the first review, focusing on policy priorities like increasing the petroleum levy to Rs60 per litre and broader revenue mobilization to address a fiscal deficit exceeding 7% of GDP.[36] [37] These measures aimed to curb inflation, which hovered around 30% in mid-2023, and bolster foreign reserves, which stood at approximately $8.4 billion by late 2023.[38] In security matters, the government confronted a surge in terrorist incidents, with over 400 fatalities recorded in Balochistan alone in 2023 from militancy and counter-operations.[39] Kakar publicly linked the rise in attacks, particularly by Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP), to safe havens in Afghanistan post-2021 Taliban takeover, urging international cooperation against cross-border terrorism.[40] Domestic responses included intensified military operations under the ongoing Zarb-e-Azb framework, though specific attribution of policy shifts to Kakar's direct initiatives remains tied to established counterterrorism laws like the 1997 Antiterrorism Act.[41] Foreign engagements underscored efforts to secure economic aid and diplomatic support. Kakar visited China on 17-19 October 2023, meeting President Xi Jinping to advance Belt and Road Initiative projects and bilateral trade, which exceeded $20 billion annually.[42] He also participated in multilateral forums, including discussions with U.S. counterparts on trade and security, reviving the U.S.-Pakistan Trade and Investment Framework Agreement after an eight-year hiatus.[43] Ties with Saudi Arabia were reinforced through meetings with Saudi officials on investment and regional stability.[44] Kakar's tenure concluded on 4 March 2024, with the formation of a new coalition government following the 8 February elections.[45]

Domestic policy initiatives

During his tenure as caretaker Prime Minister from August 14, 2023, to March 4, 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar's government maintained a focus on administrative continuity and preparatory measures for elections, adhering to the constitutional mandate of interim administrations to avoid substantive policy shifts. Key efforts centered on enhancing governance efficiency through targeted administrative adjustments rather than sweeping legislative changes.[46] Kakar advocated for bolstering local government systems to resolve civic challenges in underserved regions, emphasizing their role in bridging urban-rural divides. On November 24, 2023, he underscored the necessity of devolved governance in remote areas such as South and North Waziristan to foster inclusive administration and address local grievances directly.[46] This approach aimed to promote self-reliance in peripheral districts while preserving federal oversight, reflecting a pragmatic response to longstanding decentralization demands without altering the constitutional framework.[46] In administrative capacity-building, the government prioritized human resource development in essential sectors. Initiatives included plans to train 1 million nurses over the next decade via specialized programs to strengthen public health infrastructure. Additionally, exploratory partnerships with international entities, such as Siemens of Germany, were pursued for skill enhancement programs to improve workforce competencies in technical fields.[46] Regulatory measures extended to digital media, with consultations involving stakeholders to establish guidelines that balanced operational freedom and accountability, avoiding undue restrictions on expression. These steps were framed as contributions to a "legacy of good governance," prioritizing procedural reforms over ideological overhauls in line with the caretaker role's constraints.[46]

Economic stabilization efforts

During his tenure as caretaker Prime Minister from August 14, 2023, to March 4, 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar prioritized compliance with the International Monetary Fund's (IMF) $3 billion Stand-By Arrangement, approved in July 2023, to avert sovereign default and restore macroeconomic balances. The government under Kakar implemented prior actions for the program's first review, including fiscal consolidation measures that generated $1.34 billion in additional taxation through hikes on non-filers and under-taxed sectors, alongside revisions to the federal budget and restraint in non-priority spending. These steps yielded a primary budget surplus of 0.4% of GDP in the first quarter of fiscal year 2024, protecting social expenditures via the Benazir Income Support Programme while enforcing tight monetary policy with elevated interest rates to anchor disinflation.[47][48] Energy sector reforms formed a core component, with adjustments to electricity and natural gas tariffs in late 2023 to address circular debt accumulation, complemented by a market-determined exchange rate that stabilized the rupee amid external pressures. In October 2023, Kakar announced fuel price reductions and enhanced price-control mechanisms to mitigate inflation, which hovered above 30% following a peak of 38% in May 2023, though projections anticipated a decline to 18.5% by June 2024. The IMF Executive Board completed the first review on January 11, 2024, disbursing $700 million and bringing total releases under the arrangement to $1.9 billion.[49][48][47] Foreign exchange reserves rebounded to $8.2 billion by December 2023, up from $4.5 billion in June 2023, bolstered by bilateral rollovers from allies including Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and China, alongside crackdowns on illegal currency trading. Kakar emphasized enduring commitment to these IMF-agreed reforms, including revenue mobilization and public financial management improvements, crediting them with resolving long-standing structural vulnerabilities in a compressed timeframe. While these measures imposed short-term hardships through subsidy removals and price adjustments, they facilitated relative stabilization by enabling import coverage and averting deeper crisis, though sustained progress depended on post-election continuity.[48][50][51]

Security and foreign relations handling

During Anwaar ul Haq Kakar's tenure as caretaker prime minister, Pakistan confronted a marked escalation in terrorist activities, with the number of attacks and resulting fatalities exceeding 50 percent compared to 2022 levels, according to U.S. State Department assessments.[52] This surge was largely attributed to intensified operations by the Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP), which maintained sanctuaries across the Afghan border. Kakar's administration prioritized counter-terrorism, publicly attributing the rise in militancy to the Afghan Taliban's failure to dismantle TTP networks; on November 8, 2023, he declared that terrorism in Pakistan had proliferated since the Taliban's 2021 takeover of Afghanistan.[53][40] To counter cross-border threats, the government initiated a large-scale deportation of undocumented Afghan nationals starting in late October 2023, targeting over 1.7 million individuals by early 2024, with the explicit aim of pressuring Kabul to address militancy exports.[54] Security forces conducted ongoing operations against insurgents, contributing to high casualties among personnelâ€”reaching an eight-year peak in the first nine months of 2023â€”while civilian advisor roles under Kakar emphasized military-led stabilization without launching major new offensives beyond inherited campaigns.[55] In a BBC interview on October 4, 2023, Kakar defended the military's robust institutional role as justified for maintaining national security amid persistent threats.[56] On foreign relations, Kakar adopted a non-aligned stance focused on Pakistan's strategic autonomy, asserting in a September 2023 opinion piece that "every nation for itself" amid U.S.-China rivalry and declining to prioritize one over the other.[57] He engaged major powers through bilateral meetings, including with Chinese President Xi Jinping on October 19, 2023, to reinforce economic corridor ties, and Russian President Vladimir Putin on October 17, 2023, discussing energy and regional stability.[42] Multilaterally, Kakar attended the UN General Assembly in September 2023, COP28 in Dubai, and the World Economic Forum in Davos in January 2024, advocating adherence to international law and UN principles while highlighting climate vulnerabilities and counter-terrorism cooperation.[7][58] These efforts underscored a policy of diversified partnerships, including with Azerbaijan during regional summits, to bolster security and economic resilience without ideological concessions.[43]

Ideology and policy positions

Stance on counter-terrorism

During his tenure as caretaker Prime Minister from August 2023 to March 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar articulated a resolute stance against terrorism, emphasizing Pakistan's unwavering commitment to eradicating terrorist threats, particularly those emanating from Afghanistan. In a September 22, 2023, address to the United Nations General Assembly, he declared countering terrorism as Pakistan's "first priority," specifically targeting "all terrorism from and within Afghanistan," while calling for global action against all forms, including state-sponsored variants and far-right extremism.[59][60] He attributed a 60% rise in terror incidents and a 500% increase in suicide attacks in Pakistan to the post-2021 Taliban takeover in Afghanistan, accusing elements of the Afghan regime of enabling groups like Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) through sanctuary and support.[61][62] Kakar repeatedly vowed no surrender to militants, framing counter-terrorism as a non-negotiable national imperative. Following a deadly August 22, 2023, ambush in Balochistan that killed six army personnel, he stated on August 23 that "surrender is not an option" and pledged to sustain the fight against terrorism "no matter what."[63][64] In December 2023, after multiple attacks including one on a military convoy, he reiterated that Pakistan had "fought and won the war against terrorism" and would "not surrender," urging terrorists to "surrender unconditionally" without negotiations, while highlighting the 90,000 Pakistani lives lost in the broader conflict.[65][66] He linked deportation of undocumented Afghan refugeesâ€”over 1.7 million by late 2023â€”to counter-terrorism, arguing it addressed the influx of militants fueling attacks.[67] Kakar's policy extended to operational resolve and international pressure on Afghanistan, including demands for the Taliban to suppress TTP and extradite involved Pakistani nationals. At a January 11, 2024, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa police event, he affirmed that terrorist actions would not erode national determination, claiming a "moral victory" in the ongoing war.[68] In October 2023, he committed to eliminating all "anti-state elements," reinforcing a "no surrender" doctrine amid heightened TTP activity.[69] His rhetoric also encompassed broader extremism, such as Hindutva-inspired terrorism, underscoring a comprehensive approach beyond Pakistan's borders.[70] This stance aligned with intensified military operations, though implementation details remained under military oversight rather than civilian policy innovation.[53]

Positions on Baloch insurgency and separatism

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar has positioned Baloch separatism as a terrorist threat backed by external actors, particularly India's Research and Analysis Wing (RAW), rather than a legitimate political movement, emphasizing state unity and security force operations to counter it. In a January 1, 2024, media interaction in Lahore, he stated that "separatists are receiving funding from Indiaâ€™s RAW" and described their efforts as an "armed rebellion" aimed at breaking Pakistan, underscoring the need for action over mere rhetoric in combating terrorism.[71] Kakar has rejected claims of state-sponsored enforced disappearancesâ€”a central allegation fueling separatist grievances in Balochistanâ€”denying Pakistani authorities' responsibility during a February 28, 2024, appearance at the Islamabad High Court on a case involving missing Baloch students, and instead urging critics to condemn militants for targeting civilians and officials.[72] He linked protests by families of the missing to militancy, warning on January 1, 2024, that "advocates of terrorists in Balochistan should go and join them if they are convinced on the veracity of their issue and fight the state along with them," framing such activism as aligned with groups like the Baloch Liberation Army (BLA).[73][71] While acknowledging Baloch aspirations for an independent state and separate identity in a January 28, 2024, interviewâ€”marking a rare admission of underlying discontentâ€”he maintained that these must be confronted through robust security measures, rejecting negotiations as ineffective against what he views as a plot by a small terrorist minority (about 1% of the population) to fragment Pakistan.[74] In August 2024, amid escalating attacks such as the killing of 23 passengers in Musakhel district and 10 personnel in Kalat, Kakar reiterated his support for unrestricted military and law enforcement operations, calling it a "national war" requiring full legal, financial, equipment, and narrative backing for forces like the Frontier Corps, Pakistan Army, and intelligence agencies, while dismissing dialogue with insurgents as a "delusion" perpetuated by misleading narratives.[75] This reflects his broader advocacy for empowering security apparatus to address the insurgency's root as external-orchestrated violence, not addressable solely through rights-based concessions.

Foreign policy orientations

During his tenure as caretaker Prime Minister from August 2023 to March 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar oriented Pakistan's foreign policy toward pragmatic economic diplomacy, prioritizing mutually beneficial relations to enhance national security and socio-economic well-being. He emphasized that foreign policy should reflect Pakistan's status as an independent actor, focusing on peace, connectivity, and counter-terrorism cooperation rather than ideological alignments.[76][77][78] Kakar underscored the Pakistan-China relationship as the cornerstone of Islamabad's foreign policy, describing the two nations as all-weather strategic partners and ironclad friends. In October 2023, during a visit to Beijing for the Belt and Road Forum, he met President Xi Jinping, reaffirming commitment to the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) as a corridor for growth, innovation, and green development, while pledging to accelerate stalled projects for mutual economic gains.[79][80] He highlighted CPEC's role in regional connectivity and assured enhanced security for Chinese investments amid rising attacks on personnel.[81] Relations with the United States were framed as a collaborative partnership emphasizing peace, security, and economic stability, with Kakar calling for constructive engagement to avoid driving Pakistan deeper into exclusive alignments. In September 2023, he addressed the UN General Assembly in New York and engaged in discussions at the Council on Foreign Relations, stressing shared interests in countering terrorism and regional stability without subservience.[43][82] He expressed concerns over U.S. policies perceived as bolstering India, Pakistan's adversary, and advocated a transactional approach prioritizing bilateral benefits over geopolitical favoritism.[83][57] Ties with Afghanistan deteriorated under Kakar due to persistent cross-border terrorism, with him publicly criticizing the Taliban regime for failing to curb Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) militants operating from Afghan soil, including those armed with U.S.-left equipment post-2021 withdrawal. In November 2023, he announced escalated measures, including deportations of undocumented Afghans and border fortifications, while urging Kabul to choose bilateral cooperation over supporting insurgents; despite this, he expressed hopes for a peaceful, interconnected Afghanistan benefiting both nations.[53][84][85] Kakar pursued balanced engagement with Gulf states and regional forums, visiting the UAE and Kuwait in late 2023 to bolster economic ties and investment inflows, while participating in multilateral dialogues, such as meetings in Tashkent, to advance connectivity with Central Asia. He positioned Gulf partnerships as key to economic stabilization, aligning with broader goals of diversifying dependencies beyond traditional powers.[86]

Economic and governance perspectives

Kakar's economic perspectives centered on maintaining policy continuity from the prior administration to build upon existing reforms and drive further improvements in Pakistan's economy, as articulated shortly after assuming office on August 14, 2023.[87][88] He prioritized economic stabilization as the government's foremost objective, directing the finance ministry on September 4, 2023, to develop a comprehensive strategy amid challenges like inflation and fiscal deficits.[89][90] This approach included advocating deregulation to reduce bureaucratic hurdles and promoting "responsible autonomy" to empower sectors for growth, while underscoring the need for an economic revival plan to underpin national stability.[87][91] In specific initiatives, Kakar supported incentives for industrial competitiveness, such as providing affordable electricity tariffs to attract businesses and bolster exports, particularly within the framework of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC).[92] He credited the caretaker administration with resolving longstanding economic bottlenecks in a short period, including measures to avert default and secure international financing, though these were framed as interim steps rather than structural overhauls.[93] Public welfare remained integral to his vision, with commitments to align economic policies toward alleviating immediate hardships while urging successor governments to sustain momentum.[94][95] On governance, Kakar emphasized inclusive mechanisms that integrate economic opportunities, education, and equitable resource distribution to mitigate regional disparities, particularly in Balochistan, where he previously advocated for enhanced economic and political rights as a Balochistan Awami Party spokesperson.[96] His approach rejected personalized decision-making in favor of preserving Pakistan's foundational Islamic principles, positioning governance as a tool for national unity and countering instability through pragmatic, non-ideological administration.[97] This reflected a centrist outlook aligned with his party's progressivist stance, focusing on devolved autonomy without endorsing radical decentralization.[98]

Controversies and criticisms

Election scheduling disputes

The dissolution of Pakistan's National Assembly on August 9, 2023, triggered expectations for general elections within 90 days under Article 224 of the Constitution, targeting mid-November 2023. However, the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) postponed the polls on August 17, 2023, citing the necessity of four months to redraw constituencies based on the delayed 2023 digital census results, a process mandated by a Supreme Court ruling to ensure fair representation.[99][100] Caretaker Prime Minister Anwaar ul Haq Kakar, who assumed office on August 14, 2023, engaged in consultations with the ECP and President Arif Alvi but deferred to the commission's authority on timing, while publicly committing to constitutional obligations and ruling out additional delays amid border security challenges in September 2023.[43][101] The ECP finalized February 8, 2024, as the election date on November 2, 2023, following Supreme Court hearings on petitions demanding earlier polls; the court affirmed the ECP's role in scheduling while emphasizing timely conduct.[102][103] Disputes intensified with Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), led by imprisoned former Prime Minister Imran Khan, filing Supreme Court petitions accusing the ECP and caretaker government of unconstitutional delays to suppress opposition, especially after PTI's intra-party elections were invalidated and its electoral symbol revoked in January 2024.[100][104] On January 5, 2024, the PTI-dominated Senate passed a non-binding resolution urging further postponement due to security and logistical hurdles, but Kakar's cabinet rejected it, and the ECP proceeded with the February schedule.[105] Kakar defended the timeline in December 2023 by pledging probes into reported obstacles to the electoral process and reiterating in January 2024 at Davos that elections would occur on February 8 without extension, attributing preparations to delimitation completion despite criticisms of institutional biases favoring establishment-aligned parties.[106][107] PTI and analysts contended the delays enabled manipulation, including mobile service disruptions and candidate disqualifications, though official sources maintained procedural integrity for updated voter lists covering over 128 million eligible voters.[108]

Wheat importation decisions

During his tenure as caretaker Prime Minister from August 14, 2023, to March 4, 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar's administration permitted the private sector to import approximately 3.59 million metric tonnes of wheat between August 2023 and February 2024, exceeding the approved quota of 2.40 million metric tonnes despite sufficient domestic production and stockpiles.[109][110] The imports, valued at around Rs225-330 billion, were facilitated through letters of credit by the State Bank of Pakistan, ostensibly to meet perceived shortages amid global price fluctuations from the Ukraine war.[111][112] This policy led to a domestic surplus, as local wheat production reached record levels of over 28 million tonnes in the 2023-24 season, rendering imports unnecessary and causing the government to withhold purchases from farmers at the support price of Rs4,000 per 40 kg.[113] Farmers faced substantial losses, estimated in billions of rupees, prompting nationwide protests on May 21, 2024, organized by peasant unions demanding the abolition of private-sector import allowances and compensation for unsold crops.[114][115] A July 2025 special audit by the Auditor General of Pakistan revealed serious irregularities, including over-importation, negligence in quota enforcement, and potential corruption during the Kakar-led interim setup, contributing to an estimated Rs300 billion loss to the national exchequer.[110][116] An inquiry committee formed by Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif in May 2024 examined the matter but did not implicate high-level officials in its report, amid claims of the scandal being downplayed.[117][118] Kakar defended the imports as a response to provincial government requests and market demands, attributing low global prices to the Ukraine conflict and denying personal oversight or corruption, while characterizing the ensuing controversy as a "storm in a teacup."[119][120] He maintained that wheat importation had been routine policy for years and shifted responsibility to interim provincial administrations for production assessments, rejecting media portrayals blaming the federal level.[121] In July 2024, the government imposed a ban on wheat imports and exports to address the glut.[122]

Associations with military influence

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar's appointment as caretaker prime minister on August 14, 2023, was viewed by analysts as reinforcing the Pakistani military's dominance in political transitions, given his reputed proximity to the establishment.[123] Reports indicated that Kakar began his political career with support from military circles, particularly in Balochistan, where he aligned with operations against ethnic separatists.[25] Critics, including opposition figures, accused him of advancing military priorities during his tenure, such as delaying elections amid security concerns raised by the armed forces.[8] In public statements, Kakar openly acknowledged the military's enduring role in Pakistan's governance. During a September 26, 2023, interview, he described the armed forces as "here to stay" in the country's politics, responding affirmatively to queries about their influence and emphasizing institutional necessities over democratic ideals alone.[124][125] On BBC HARDtalk on October 4, 2023, he rejected apologies for perceptions of being the military's representative, framing such ties as pragmatic given Pakistan's security challenges.[56] He further highlighted an "excellent working relationship" between his caretaker government and the army, underscoring coordination on national stability.[126] Kakar's administration demonstrated military alignment through key decisions, including the November 2023 extension of Lieutenant General Nadeem Anjum's tenure as Director-General of the Inter-Services Intelligence (ISI), justified as ensuring policy continuity amid regional tensions.[127] In Balochistan, his background with the Balochistan Awami Party involved endorsing expanded military operations against insurgents, prioritizing security over separatist grievances despite criticisms of human rights implications.[19] These actions reflected a broader pattern where caretaker leadership, by design, operates under military oversight to manage interim governance, as evidenced by Kakar's deference to institutional consensus on matters like election timing and counter-terrorism.[128]

Personal life and assets

Family and personal details

Anwaar ul Haq Kakar was born in 1971 in Muslim Bagh, Qilla Saifullah District, Balochistan, into a middle-class Pashtun family belonging to the Kakar tribe.[3] His grandfather, Dr. Noor Muhammad Kakar, served as a physician to the Khan of Kalat.[129] Kakar's father, Ihtisham ul Haq Kakar, worked as a civil servant, including as a naib tehsildar.[129] Kakar maintains a private personal life, with limited public information available about his immediate family. He is married, but his wife's name and background have not been disclosed in available sources.[11] He has at least one son.[11]

Wealth and financial disclosures

In November 2023, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar submitted his statement of assets and liabilities to the Election Commission of Pakistan (ECP) for the financial year ending June 30, 2023, declaring total assets worth approximately Rs48 million.[130][131] These included inherited agricultural land spanning 20 acres in Balochistan, valued at Rs8 million, and a residential bungalow in the Defence Housing Authority (DHA) in Karachi.[132][133] Kakar reported holding 10 tolas of gold personally, valued at Rs80,000, while his spouse declared an additional 10 tolas of gold and jewelry.[134][135] He also owned 50,000 shares in Pakistan Chagai Mining Limited and had cash holdings, bank deposits, and household items collectively valued at around Rs69.7 million when including spousal assets, though liabilities such as loans were not prominently detailed in public summaries.[136][131] The ECP filings explicitly confirmed that Kakar held no vehicles, no businesses or properties outside Pakistan, and no foreign assets or accounts, countering any speculation about overseas wealth.[137][135] These disclosures, published via the Gazette of Pakistan, reflect standard requirements for public officials under Pakistan's election laws, emphasizing transparency in movable and immovable properties.[130] Prior asset statements from his time as a senator or earlier roles showed modest growth aligned with his declared income sources, primarily from public service and limited private holdings.[132]

Post-premiership activities

Following the conclusion of his tenure as caretaker prime minister on 4 March 2024, Anwaar ul Haq Kakar has engaged in public commentary on national security challenges, particularly attributing the recent surge in terrorism to the proliferation of weapons abandoned by NATO forces after their withdrawal from Afghanistan.[138][139] He has also warned of the potential for a military operation in Balochistan within one to one-and-a-half years, emphasizing the need for decisive action against insurgent threats.[140] Kakar has advocated for enhanced private sector involvement to drive economic development, stating that sustainable growth requires moving beyond state-dominated models.[141] In foreign policy discussions, he expressed understanding of India's reluctance to engage in bilateral talks with Pakistan while underscoring that such a stance cannot remain indefinite, and affirmed strong bilateral ties with China, describing them as an "ironclad brotherhood."[142][143] Domestically, Kakar participated in events such as the Balochistan Breakfast discussion in March 2025, focusing on provincial issues, and met with Punjab Chief Minister Maryam Nawaz in April 2025 to discuss mutual interests including political stability.[144][145] He addressed students at the University of Karachi in December 2024, urging them to prioritize constructive dialogue over negative propaganda.[146] Additionally, he highlighted the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation's potential to foster regional economic integration during an October 2024 forum.[147] Kakar has maintained visibility through multiple media appearances, including interviews in March, May, June, and October 2025, where he critiqued civil-military dynamics and reflected on his prior tenure.[148][149][150][151] His activities reflect ongoing influence as a Balochistan-based political figure without a formal elected position since his Senate term expired in March 2024.[6]
