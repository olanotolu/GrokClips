Methodology and Data Sources

Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth (SALN) Framework

The Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth (SALN) is mandated under Republic Act No. 6713, the Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees, which requires all public officials and employees, including members of Congress, to submit an annual sworn declaration of their assets, liabilities, net worth, business interests, and financial connections.[9][10] This filing must occur by April 30 of each year and reflects the declarant's financial status as of December 31 of the preceding calendar year.[11][12] SALN declarations encompass personal assets such as real and personal properties, cash, and investments; liabilities including loans and debts; and separate disclosures of business interests and financial connections, which may involve family members or related entities.[13][14] For married declarants, joint filing with spouses is permitted, requiring the inclusion of all real and personal propertiesâ€”such as paraphernal or capital assets belonging to either partyâ€”regardless of whether the spouse is a public official; separate filings are also an option, but spousal business interests must still be disclosed if applicable.[15][16][17] In 2025, the Office of the Ombudsman, under Samuel Martires' successor Jesus Crispin Remulla, reversed prior restrictions on public access to SALNs by issuing guidelines that facilitate requests from the public, media, and academics, subject to identity verification and redaction of sensitive personal data like signatures.[18][19] This policy shift, effective October 14, 2025, mandates agencies to transmit SALNs to the Ombudsman by May 15 annually and allows direct requests, though dissemination requires submission of published outputs to the Ombudsman.[20][21] Amid these changes, some legislators, including Senator Risa Hontiveros, opted for voluntary disclosure by releasing copies directly to media outlets in October 2025, bypassing formal request processes.[22][23]

Net Worth Calculation and Recent Disclosures (as of 2025)

Net worth in SALN disclosures is derived by subtracting total liabilities, such as loans and debts, from total assets, which encompass real properties valued at acquisition cost, personal properties including vehicles and household items at purchase price or zonal value (whichever applicable), financial instruments like stocks and bonds, and liquid assets such as cash on hand and in banks.[24][25] Minor personal items valued under â‚±50,000 are excluded from itemized reporting to focus on significant holdings.[16] This computation yields the declared net worth as of the reporting date, typically December 31 of the prior year. The 2025 SALN cycle covers declarations for assets and liabilities as of December 31, 2024, with submissions due by April 30, 2025, for members of the 19th Congress.[26] Public releases commenced in October 2025, initially limited to a handful of senators and House members who voluntarily disclosed or responded to requests.[27] For example, Sen. Limitations of Self-Reported Data

The Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth (SALN) relies on self-declarations by officials without mandatory independent appraisals, enabling undervaluation of assets such as real estate and stocks, where reported figures often reflect acquisition costs rather than prevailing market values.[30] This vulnerability has been highlighted in expert analyses noting loopholes in verification, with declarations susceptible to manipulation absent third-party validation.[31] Consequently, net worth rankings derived from SALN data may systematically understate actual wealth, fostering skepticism toward their precision as empirical measures. SALN disclosures are not proactively published but released only upon request, creating gaps in available data that hinder comprehensive rankings. In 2025, this process revealed disparities in timeliness, as opposition figures like Senator Risa Hontiveros promptly shared their statements, while pro-administration lawmakers and executive branches exhibited delays in finalizing releases amid internal deliberations.[26][32] Such inconsistencies exacerbate incompleteness, particularly when requests face procedural hurdles or non-response, rendering aggregated wealth lists provisional at best. Underreporting persists due to the absence of routine cross-verification by bodies like the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) or Commission on Audit (COA), allowing omissions or inaccuraciesâ€”such as unadjusted values amid economic shifts or concealed interestsâ€”to go undetected without targeted probes.[31] Historical probes into deceptive declarations underscore this weakness, with calls for stricter BIR-led validation to mitigate falsification risks, yet no systemic reforms have implemented ongoing audits.[33] These structural flaws compel caution in interpreting SALN-based wealth metrics, as they prioritize compliance over verifiable accuracy.

Instances of Delayed or Incomplete Disclosures

Under Republic Act No. 6713, Philippine public officials, including members of Congress, are required to file their Statements of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth (SALNs) annually by April 30, covering assets as of December 31 of the prior year;
