New Nation Party

The New Nation Party (NNP) was a minor centre-right political party in New Zealand, registered with the Electoral Commission on 12 April 2023 and voluntarily deregistered on 29 January 2025 at its own request.[1][2] Founded on principles of common sense, rationality, and freedom of choice, the party sought to restore national prosperity through reduced government intervention, emphasizing individual rights and economic self-reliance.[3] Under the leadership of Guy Slocum, who served as leader, secretary, and treasurer, NNP contested the 2023 general election, fielding candidates such as Slocum in Auckland Central, but received negligible support and won no seats in Parliament.[4][5] The party's platform opposed vaccine mandates, the Three Waters infrastructure reforms, and supranational influences like United Nations treaties that it viewed as eroding New Zealand's sovereignty, while advocating for provincial autonomy, lower taxes, bureaucracy reduction, and a rejection of expansive welfare systems in favor of temporary support for self-sufficiency.[6][7][8] Its deregistration reflected the challenges faced by small parties in sustaining operations amid New Zealand's proportional representation system, where threshold requirements and voter fragmentation limit minor parties' viability without coalition alliances or significant funding.[2]

Formation and Early Development

Founding in 2022

The New Nation Party was established in 2022 amid widespread discontent with the New Zealand government's handling of the COVID-19 crisis, including mandatory vaccination policies and expanded bureaucratic controls viewed by supporters as infringing on individual liberties.[6] This formation reflected a broader backlash against centralized decision-making that prioritized compliance over personal autonomy, with the party positioning itself against measures seen as coercive and economically damaging.[3] At its inception, the party articulated a commitment to foundational values of common sense governance, rational policy-making, freedom of choice, and revitalizing New Zealand's economic prosperity alongside its traditional "Kiwi lifestyle" of self-reliance and community focus.[3] These principles aimed to counter what founders perceived as a drift toward overregulation and diminished opportunity, advocating instead for policies that empower citizens and provincial economies without undue state interference.[3] The party quickly engaged with parallel freedom advocacy groups, becoming a founding component of the Freedoms New Zealand alliance on 22 August 2022, which united entities like the Freedoms & Rights Coalition and Vision New Zealand to amplify anti-mandate and rights-based voices. This association provided an initial platform for coordinated efforts but ended by mid-2023, as New Nation pursued independent operations to maintain focus on its core tenets.[6]

Registration and Initial Activities

The New Nation Party achieved formal registration with the Electoral Commission on April 12, 2023, under Part 4 of the Electoral Act 1993, which permitted the party to participate in national elections, nominate candidates, and utilize its registered logo.[1] This step followed the party's establishment earlier in the year and enabled it to position itself as a centre-right option distinct from established parties.[6] In the lead-up to the 2023 general election, the party nominated candidates and conducted initial outreach to garner support, focusing on issues such as enhancing provincial autonomy to devolve power from central government in Wellington, strengthening law and order through proposed increases in police resources and training reforms, and opposing New Zealand's entanglements in international bodies like the United Nations.[9][10][6] Early opinion polling reflected modest visibility, with support levels typically ranging from 0.2% to 0.5%, appealing primarily to voters critical of establishment policies on mandates, resource reforms, and global commitments.[11]

Ideology and Policies

Core Values and Principles

The New Nation Party articulates a centre-right worldview centered on common sense, reason, and limited government, positing that the state's role should be confined to essential services while minimizing bureaucracy to enable individual self-determination. Fundamental to this is the principle of freedom of choice, viewed as inherent to human individuality, which underpins opposition to coercive state-driven social engineering. The party advocates for genuine democracy characterized by equal opportunity and a merit-based society free from preferential treatment of vested interests, emphasizing personal responsibility as the antidote to welfare dependency.[7][12] Central to its principles is the prioritization of national prosperity through decentralized economic empowerment, favoring autonomous regional governanceâ€”termed "provinces" in party rhetoricâ€”over centralized authority to better harness local resources and reduce import reliance for sustainable growth. This approach reflects a commitment to economic liberty, including lower taxes to retain wealth with citizens and incentivize self-reliance, rather than expansive state redistribution. Sovereignty is upheld as paramount, with resistance to supranational agreements perceived to erode domestic control.[7] The party's foundational stance critiques prevailing left-leaning orthodoxies on governance issues, such as top-down reforms, as detached from observable human incentives and practical outcomes, instead endorsing decision-making aligned with individual rights and verifiable causal mechanisms in social and economic systems. This entails fostering communities where aid is transitional, aimed at restoring self-sufficiency, and where empirical evidence guides policy over ideological mandates.[7][12]

Key Policy Positions

The New Nation Party positioned itself as a centre-right advocate for personal liberties, opposing COVID-19 vaccine mandates as coercive measures that resulted in widespread job losses and social division. It criticized associated lockdowns and border closures for infringing on citizens' rights, such as the ability to return home during emergencies, and pledged to implement checks and balances, including a written constitution drafted within the first three months of governance to safeguard freedoms against future overreach.[13] Regarding infrastructure and local governance, the party vowed to repeal the Three Waters reforms enacted in October 2021, which it argued centralized water assets into three remote entities, stripping councils and ratepayers of ownership and control while imposing unattainable standards to justify the transfer. Alternatives included maintaining local asset management, reallocating existing taxpayer funds for upgrades, and boosting the economy to afford sustainable infrastructure without bureaucratic expansion.[8] On sovereignty, New Nation sought to limit undue external influence by withdrawing from or renegotiating international commitments, including United Nations frameworks, World Health Organization treaties, and climate accords, which it claimed diluted New Zealand's policy autonomy in favor of global agendas pursued by successive Labour and National governments.[7] In law and order, the party proposed enhancing public safety through a 25% expansion of frontline police, stricter bail conditions to prevent repeat offenses, a comprehensive overhaul of the Sentencing Act for tougher penalties, and night courts to reduce trial backlogs and delays.[10] Economically, it favored fiscal discipline via lower taxes, curtailed welfare as a temporary bridge rather than permanent support, and market-driven growth by harnessing domestic resources, cutting imports, and devolving powers to provinces for localized decision-making that counters Wellington-centric inefficiencies and promotes prosperity.[7]

Leadership and Internal Structure

Leadership Transitions

The New Nation Party was initially led by Guy Slocum, who signed the party's financial statements as leader, secretary, and treasurer for the fiscal year ending March 31, 2024.[4] Melissa Oâ€™Hagan served as party secretary, submitting the application for official registration to the Electoral Commission in March 2023.[14] Slocum, a vocal advocate for individual liberties, positioned the party against government-imposed COVID-19 vaccine mandates and centralizing reforms like the Three Waters policy, framing these stances as defenses of personal autonomy amid perceived overreach.[6] Post-2023 general election, where the party garnered negligible support, the leadership role transitioned to vacant status, underscoring internal hurdles in retaining key figures and building sustained viability as a minor entity.[2] This shift occurred amid broader challenges for fringe parties dissenting on pandemic-era restrictions, with no successor named and the organization ultimately opting for voluntary deregistration in January 2025.[2] The vacancy highlighted the difficulties of translating policy advocacy into enduring political structure without electoral breakthroughs.

Organizational Framework

The New Nation Party operated with a board comprising the president, vice president, secretary, leader, treasurer, and up to three additional businesspersons, responsible for managing party affairs, approving policies, and overseeing operations.[12] This structure emphasized democratic processes, with the president, vice president, and regional representatives elected biennially by members through ballots, ensuring member input in key leadership selections.[12] Membership required natural persons to apply with personal details including name, occupation, address, and preferred electorate, alongside payment of a subscription feeâ€”set at $25 for a three-year termâ€”to join and participate in party activities.[12][15] The board retained discretion to reject applications or expel members, while a national council facilitated grassroots engagement by fostering local groups and tapping into regional concerns for candidate development and policy remits.[12] Decision-making centered on member-driven mechanisms, including annual general meetings where resolutions could be proposed and policy shaped via a dedicated policy committee incorporating member remits, with constitutional amendments requiring a two-thirds majority vote.[12] Accountability was enforced through periodic elections and an arbitration process for disputes, prioritizing solutions-focused governance over hierarchical party machinery.[12] The party's small-scale operations relied heavily on volunteer networks and online platforms, such as Facebook groups for discussion and lobbying, to build support without parliamentary representation or extensive formal infrastructure.[16] This approach underscored a commitment to devolving power to members and local initiatives, calling for donations and memberships to sustain activities amid limited resources.[15]

Electoral History

By-elections and Pre-2023 Contests

The New Nation Party's initial foray into electoral politics occurred during the Tauranga by-election held on 18 June 2022, following the resignation of National Party MP Simon Bridges.[17] The party nominated Andrew Hollis, a local Tauranga City councillor known for his vocal opposition to government COVID-19 measures, as its candidate on 1 May 2022.[18] Hollis's candidacy provided an early platform for the nascent party to articulate its critiques of centralised authority and public health mandates, framing its message as a call for decentralised governance and individual freedoms in contrast to mainstream parties.[18] In the official results, Hollis secured 260 votes out of 20,784 valid votes cast, equating to roughly 1.25% of the electorate vote.[17] This placed him seventh among 13 candidates, behind major parties like National (11,613 votes) and Labour (5,259 votes), but ahead of several other minor party contenders such as ONE Party (182 votes).[17] The low turnout of 40.5% among 51,706 enrolled voters underscored the by-election's limited scope, yet Hollis's result reflected fringe appeal among voters disillusioned with pandemic-era policies, though insufficient to challenge the dominance of established parties.[17] The Tauranga contest highlighted the structural barriers for emerging parties in New Zealand's mixed-member proportional (MMP) system, where minor outfits must either surpass the 5% nationwide party vote threshold or secure an electorate seat to gain parliamentary representation. With no prior national contests and minimal resources, the New Nation Party's modest performance illustrated the difficulty of penetrating a fragmented opposition landscape, particularly without alliances or significant media coverage. No other by-elections were contested by the party prior to the 2023 general election, confining its pre-2023 activity to this targeted local test of voter sentiment.[17]

2023 General Election Performance

In the 2023 New Zealand general election on 14 October 2023, the New Nation Party garnered 1,530 valid party votes nationwide, equating to 0.05% of the total valid party vote share from approximately 2.85 million votes cast.[11] The party fielded candidates in select electorates but secured no electorate wins and fell far short of the 5% threshold or one electorate seat required for list representation, resulting in zero parliamentary seats.[19] This outcome underscored the challenges for fringe parties in a mixed-member proportional system dominated by larger blocs, where vote fragmentation diluted support for non-coalition alternatives. The campaign emphasized critique of the incumbent Labour government's policies, particularly opposition to COVID-19 vaccine mandates and the Three Waters reforms aimed at centralizing water infrastructure management.[6] Positioning itself as a centre-right option, the party sought to attract voters alienated by perceived overreach in public health measures and resource allocation reforms, amid widespread disillusionment reflected in Labour's overall defeat.[19] However, lacking significant media traction or pre-election polling visibility, its platform resonated with only a narrow segment of right-leaning skeptics, many of whom consolidated behind National, ACT, and New Zealand First in a rightward electoral pivot.[11] The results highlighted dynamic shifts in minor-party alignments, including the New Nation Party's decision to contest independently after initially aligning with the proposed Freedoms NZ grouping announced by Brian Tamaki in August 2022.[20] This separation contributed to splintered protest votes on the right, contrasting with the electoral success of coalition-capable parties that absorbed anti-Labour sentiment without exceeding the fragmentation threshold. Freedoms NZ itself received comparably minimal support at around 1,600 votes, illustrating the inefficacy of uncoordinated challenges to the duopoly of National-led and Labour-led blocs.[19]

Deregistration and Legacy

Voluntary Deregistration in 2025

The New Nation Party requested the cancellation of its registration with the Electoral Commission, which determined to effect the deregistration on January 29, 2025, under section 70 of the Electoral Act 1993.[2] This provision mandates the Commission to cancel a party's registration upon application from one of its officers, thereby removing its eligibility to contest elections, field candidates, or appear on ballots as a registered entity. The Commission's announcement on January 30, 2025, confirmed the removal of both the party registration and its associated logo from the official registry.[2] The move aligned with the party's limited resources and persistently low electoral viability, as demonstrated by its negligible performance in prior contests, including the 2023 general election where affiliated efforts under the Freedoms New Zealand umbrella garnered just 8,058 party votes, or 0.22% of the national total.[11] Such marginal results fell far short of the 5% threshold required for list seats or the single electorate win needed for representation, underscoring ongoing challenges in mobilizing voter support and sustaining organizational infrastructure. Post-deregistration, the party shifted focus to non-electoral mechanisms for influence, maintaining activity on social media platforms to promote its positions and engage supporters, while potentially pursuing informal lobbying on policy issues without the compliance obligations of registered status, such as financial reporting and audit requirements under the Electoral Act.[16] This strategic adjustment allowed circumvention of registration fees and administrative hurdles, which for minor parties often exceed available funding given donation limits and disclosure rules.

Post-Deregistration Status and Influence

Following its voluntary deregistration on 29 January 2025, the New Nation Party has operated as an unregistered political entity in New Zealand, maintaining an online presence through its website and associated social media channels to promote its core values of rationality, freedom of choice, and opposition to perceived government overreach.[2][3] The party's leadership position remains vacant following the departure of prior leader Guy Slocum, with no announced successors or formal internal elections as of October 2025.[3] The party's website continues to host policy outlines and calls for donations, emphasizing themes such as provincial autonomy, law and order, and critiques of internationalist policies, while linking to a Facebook discussion group for member engagement on current political issues.[3] This group remains active, with posts addressing fiscal critiques of past governments and broader concerns like debt accumulation, though participation appears limited to a niche audience without evidence of widespread mobilization.[3] As an unregistered party, it lacks eligibility for party list voting under New Zealand's mixed-member proportional system, restricting any potential electoral activity to individual electorate nominations, which it has not pursued since 2023.[21] Its influence remains marginal, evidenced by the absence of parliamentary seats, minimal vote shares in prior contests (under 0.1% nationally in 2023), and no documented policy adoptions by major parties, though it articulates dissenting perspectives on issues like vaccine mandates and centralization that resonate in certain online conservative circles.[6] Critics, including mainstream political analysts, have dismissed it as electorally irrelevant due to its fringe status and failure to build coalitions, while supporters highlight its role in rational policy discourse outside establishment frameworks.[22]
