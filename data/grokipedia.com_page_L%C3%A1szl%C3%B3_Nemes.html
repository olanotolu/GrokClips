László Nemes

László Nemes (born 18 February 1977) is a Hungarian film director renowned for his debut feature Son of Saul (2015), a stark Holocaust drama centered on a Sonderkommando prisoner's futile quest for a rabbinical burial amid the horrors of Auschwitz, which earned the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film.[1][2] Born in Budapest to a Jewish mother and the theatre director András Jeles, Nemes relocated to Paris at age 12, where he pursued studies in history, international relations, and screenwriting before assisting acclaimed director Béla Tarr on films such as The Man from London (2007).[3][4] Nemes' filmmaking is distinguished by its immersive, subjective aesthetic—employing shallow focus and extended takes to evoke individual disorientation within historical cataclysms—eschewing omniscient narration in favor of visceral proximity to the protagonist's viewpoint, as pioneered in Son of Saul's unblinking portrayal of industrialized genocide.[5][2] Subsequent works include the period mystery Sunset (2018), exploring pre-World War I Budapest's undercurrents of violence and identity, and Orphan (2025), a Venice-premiered drama drawing from familial trauma tied to his father's experiences.[6][7]

Early life and education

Childhood and family in Hungary

László Nemes was born on February 18, 1977, in Budapest, Hungary.[1] His father, András Jeles, was a prominent Hungarian film and television director, while his mother worked as a teacher and came from a Jewish family background.[8] [9] Nemes's family history included significant losses during the Holocaust, with members on both sides affected; on his mother's side, her maternal grandparents and other immediate relatives perished.[10] Growing up in Hungary during the late communist era, Nemes experienced antisemitism firsthand, recalling instances in school during the 1980s where he was derogatorily called a "dirty Jew."[9] This environment, marked by residual ethnic tensions in a post-Holocaust society under state socialism, shaped his early awareness of identity and historical trauma, though he developed an interest in filmmaking influenced by his father's profession.[8] His childhood in Budapest lasted until 1989, when, at the age of 12, he relocated to Paris with his mother amid the political upheavals leading to the end of communist rule in Hungary.[1]

Relocation to France and formal studies

In 1989, at the age of 12, Nemes relocated from Budapest to Paris with his mother following his parents' divorce, during a period of political transition in Hungary as communist rule waned.[11] He grew up in the French capital, where the city's cultural environment, including its cinematic heritage, began shaping his interests despite his initial focus on academic pursuits.[8] Nemes pursued formal higher education in Paris, enrolling at the Institut d'Études Politiques (Sciences Po), a prestigious institution known for its programs in political science and history.[11] There, he studied history alongside international relations, gaining a foundation in analytical frameworks that later informed his thematic explorations of trauma and societal upheaval in film.[12] He supplemented these with screenwriting courses, bridging his academic background to practical filmmaking skills amid Paris's vibrant intellectual scene.[13] This multidisciplinary education, completed in the early 2000s, equipped him with critical tools for narrative construction, though he has credited informal influences like Hungarian cinema masters more directly for his stylistic development.[1]

Entry into the film industry

Assistant director positions in Europe

After completing his studies in Paris, Nemes worked as an assistant director on short films in France before relocating to Budapest in his mid-20s.[8] In Hungary, he transitioned to assisting on feature-length projects, beginning a key collaboration with director Béla Tarr in 2003.[8] Nemes first contributed to Tarr's prologue segment in the anthology film Visions of Europe (2004), a collection of shorts by European directors addressing continental identity and migration.[8] He then served as Tarr's assistant director for roughly two years on The Man from London (2007), a black-and-white crime drama adapted from Georges Simenon's novel and shot over an extended period with Tarr's signature long, unbroken takes.[1][8] This role involved coordinating production logistics and supporting Tarr's rigorous on-set process, which Nemes later described as an intensive form of practical film education.[14] These European assistant positions, spanning France and Hungary, honed Nemes' understanding of narrative construction and technical execution amid demanding auteur-driven workflows, laying groundwork for his independent directing pursuits.[15]

Development of short films and early recognition

Following his experience as an assistant director, including two years working with Béla Tarr, László Nemes transitioned to directing his own short films in Budapest. His debut, the 14-minute With a Little Patience (Türelem, 2007), marked his first project in 35mm and featured a fixed camera observing an office clerk's stoic routine amid societal collapse, establishing his signature immersive style.[13][16] The film premiered at the Venice International Film Festival and secured the European Film Academy Award for Best Short Film in 2008, along with honors at the Bilbao International Festival of Documentary and Short Films and Angers European First Film Festival.[17][18] Nemes followed with The Counterpart (2008), a Hungarian-Dutch co-production shot in Romania, depicting two estranged friends reuniting in dire circumstances.[4][19] This 14-minute drama earned recognition at festivals including DaKINO International in Bucharest. He then completed The Gentleman Takes His Leave (Az úr elköszön, 2010), which won Best Director and Best Cinematography awards at the Hungarian Film Week.[20][21] These three shorts collectively amassed over 30 awards across more than 100 international festivals, including venues in Odense and Lisbon, garnering critical attention for Nemes' precise visual language and thematic focus on human endurance.[13][22] This early acclaim facilitated development funding for his feature debut and highlighted his emergence as a distinctive voice in European cinema.[4]

Feature film career

Son of Saul (2015): Breakthrough and Holocaust depiction

Son of Saul (original title: Saul fia), released on June 17, 2015, at the Cannes Film Festival, represented László Nemes' first feature-length directorial effort, co-written with Clara Royer and produced primarily in Hungary with a budget of approximately €1.5 million.[23] The narrative unfolds over 135 minutes in October 1944 at Auschwitz-Birkenau, tracking Saul Ausländer (played by Géza Röhrig), a Hungarian-Jewish member of the Sonderkommando—a group of prisoners compelled to assist in the extermination process by handling corpses post-gassing—who fixates on securing a rabbi to perform the Kaddish prayer for a nameless adolescent boy he encounters amid the crematoria operations and claims as his son, diverting him from an impending Sonderkommando uprising.[9] Cinematographer Mátyás Erdély employed a 35mm film stock with natural lighting for exteriors to evoke raw authenticity, recreating camp structures on Hungarian sets without relying on archival footage.[24] The film achieved immediate breakthrough status upon its Cannes premiere, securing the Grand Prix—the festival's second-highest honor—and the FIPRESCI Prize, propelling Nemes from obscurity to international prominence as a voice in Holocaust cinema.[11] Subsequent accolades included the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film on February 28, 2016 (the first for Hungary since 1981's Mephisto), a Golden Globe for Best Foreign Language Film, and the European Film Award for Best Film, among over 60 wins worldwide, which distributors cited in marketing to underscore its technical and emotional rigor over commercial appeal.[25] These victories, rare for a debut amid competition from established directors, validated Nemes' approach against conventional Holocaust narratives, with critics like those at The New York Times noting it "expands the language" of such films by prioritizing visceral immediacy over historical exposition.[11] Nemes' depiction of the Holocaust eschews survival heroism or panoramic atrocity surveys, instead immersing viewers in the protagonist's fragmented moral universe to convey the extermination's dehumanizing scale through implication rather than graphic display.[9] Stylistically, the film adopts a square 4:3 Academy aspect ratio—evoking early cinema's constraints—to tunnel vision onto Saul via continuous tracking shots averaging 2-3 minutes, with the Steadicam positioned inches from Röhrig's face in shallow depth of field (f/0.7 lens equivalents), rendering peripheral gas chambers, shootings, and disposals as blurred auditory infernos dominated by multilingual shouts, machinery whirs, and cries in Hungarian, Yiddish, Polish, German, and French.[23] This technique, which Nemes described as forcing confrontation with "the face of the perpetrator" through Saul's obsessive quest amid collective doom, draws from influences like Béla Tarr's long takes while rejecting aesthetic detachment, as the unblinking focus on one man's futile ritual underscores individual agency dissolving into industrialized murder without redemptive arcs.[26][9] Such formal choices, rooted in Nemes' family history—his grandparents' Auschwitz survival and his rejection of sanitized representations—aimed to evoke the Sonderkommando's ethical paradoxes, where complicity in cremation enabled brief self-preservation, yet Nemes insisted the film probes existential inertia over judgment, with off-screen violence (e.g., gassings heard but veiled) amplifying causal horror's ungraspability.[27] Analyses in film journals praise this as anti-spectacular, countering prior works' tendencies toward voyeurism, though some reviewers questioned if the tight framing risks insulating viewers from full camp mechanics; Nemes countered that broader vistas would falsify the prisoners' perceptual entrapment.[23][9] The result, per Senses of Cinema, transforms Holocaust cinema by privileging subjective dissonance—Saul's denial amid evident finality—over objective tallying of 1.1 million Auschwitz deaths, grounding depiction in verifiable Sonderkommando testimonies like those from Filip Müller without narrativizing collective resistance as triumph.[23]

Sunset (2018): Exploration of pre-WWI Budapest

Sunset, released in 2018, is set in Budapest in 1913, capturing the Austro-Hungarian Empire on the brink of collapse amid rising nationalist fervor and social unrest. The film follows Irisz Leiter, a young milliner seeking employment at the renowned Leiter hat emporium, whose personal quest intertwines with the city's undercurrents of decadence and violence, reflecting the fin-de-siècle tensions that presaged World War I.[28][29] Nemes recreates Budapest's opulent architecture and bustling streets without CGI, employing meticulously constructed sets to evoke the era's grandeur, including the Danube's bridges and the hat shop's lavish interiors, shot on 35mm and 65mm Kodak film stock for textured authenticity.[12][30] The narrative explores pre-WWI Budapest as a microcosm of imperial decay, where aristocratic elegance masks antisemitic pogroms, family scandals, and apocalyptic rumors, such as the mythical "Leiter" figure symbolizing lost innocence. Nemes draws on historical realities like the empire's ethnic fractures and economic disparities, portraying the city as a labyrinth of class divides—from elite salons to mob violence—foreshadowing the 20th century's catastrophes.[31][32] In interviews, Nemes has stated his aim was to trace the "birth of the twentieth century" and understand the perceptual and ethical failures that led from this belle époque to events like the Holocaust, emphasizing individual agency amid collective madness rather than overt patriotism.[33][7] Visually, Nemes employs his signature shallow-focus technique, centering the protagonist while blurring peripheral chaos—riots, equestrian upheavals, and societal unraveling—to immerse viewers in subjective disorientation, mirroring Budapest's prewar fragility. This approach critiques nostalgic views of the Habsburg era, highlighting causal links between suppressed traumas and eruptive violence, as evidenced by the film's depiction of a real 1913-like pogrom against perceived Jewish influences.[34] Critics note the film's historical fidelity in evoking Budapest's role as a cultural hub—home to figures like hatmaker legends—yet underscore Nemes' invention of plot elements to probe deeper existential questions, avoiding mere period recreation.[35][36]

Orphan (2025): Postwar generational trauma

Orphan (2025) portrays postwar generational trauma through the experiences of a young Jewish boy in 1957 Budapest, set against the backdrop of the Soviet suppression of the 1956 Hungarian Revolution. The narrative centers on the boy's mother, a Holocaust survivor who has fabricated an idealized image of his deceased father to shield him from the brutal realities of her wartime experiences and the family's survival struggles.[37] This protective myth crumbles when a rough-mannered man emerges claiming paternity, forcing the protagonist to confront suppressed family histories marked by antisemitism, loss, and adaptation under successive fascist and communist regimes.[38][39] The film draws directly from director László Nemes' father's childhood in postwar Hungary, illustrating how unprocessed trauma from World War II manifests in the subsequent generation through emotional isolation, distorted narratives, and simmering resentment. Nemes depicts the boy's psychological unraveling as a microcosm of broader societal wounds, including the shift in Hungarian antisemitism from Nazi-era pogroms to Stalinist purges, compounded by the failed 1956 uprising's aftermath of crushed hopes and renewed oppression.[40][41] Critics note that this exploration highlights the "silent generation's" inheritance of parental silence on atrocities, leading to identity crises amid economic scarcity and ideological conformity in 1950s communist Hungary.[42][43] Nemes employs his signature immersive style—long takes and restricted framing—to immerse viewers in the child's disorienting perspective, emphasizing causal chains from historical events to personal rupture: wartime survival instincts evolve into postwar deception, which the revelation exposes as a fragile barrier against inherited rage and disillusionment.[44] While some reviews praise this as a poignant fable of intergenerational resentment, others argue the film's deliberate pacing risks inertness in conveying the trauma's emotional depth, prioritizing technical evocation over narrative propulsion.[45][46] The Venice Film Festival synopsis frames it as a "chronicle of a child's coming to terms with his own family history," reflective of twentieth-century European upheavals where individual psyches bear the weight of collective catastrophes.[47]

Upcoming projects and international expansion

Following the release of Orphan in 2025, which premiered at the Venice Film Festival on August 28 and was selected as Hungary's entry for the Best International Feature Film at the 2026 Academy Awards, László Nemes has announced two major upcoming projects signaling a shift toward international co-productions and English-language filmmaking.[6][48] One project is an adaptation of Cormac McCarthy's 1968 novel Outer Dark, titled Odakint a sötétség in Hungarian, featuring an international cast including Jacob Elordi and Lily-Rose Depp.[49] This English-language film, set to begin shooting in 2026, marks Nemes's first venture into adapting American literature and working with Hollywood-adjacent talent, expanding beyond his prior Hungarian-focused historical dramas.[50] The second is a biopic centered on Jean Moulin, the French Resistance leader executed by the Nazis in 1943, representing Nemes's entry into French historical cinema and further international collaboration.[51] These endeavors reflect Nemes's expressed ambition to produce films in English and engage global narratives, as articulated in interviews following Orphan's premiere, while maintaining his signature immersive style amid broader historical themes.[6][52]

Artistic approach and philosophy

Key influences from cinema masters

László Nemes has frequently cited Hungarian director Béla Tarr as a pivotal influence, stemming from his experience as Tarr's assistant director on the 2007 film The Man from London. This collaboration provided Nemes with insights into Tarr's rigorous approach to space, immersion, and long takes, which shaped his own stylistic preferences for creating enveloping cinematic environments. Nemes described Tarr as "a very important master," crediting the mentorship with helping him "find my own voice" amid Tarr's demanding process.[10][10] Roman Polanski's work also exerted a significant impact, particularly on Nemes's handling of historical trauma and moral ambiguity in Son of Saul. Nemes explicitly named Polanski as a "big influence" for the film's intense focus on individual survival within atrocity, echoing elements in Polanski's Holocaust-related films like The Pianist. This connection underscores Nemes's admiration for Polanski's ability to blend personal narrative with broader historical horror without overt didacticism.[53] Among international filmmakers, Stanley Kubrick stands out for Nemes, who has highlighted Barry Lyndon as a favorite for its meticulous visual composition and philosophical depth. Kubrick's influence appears in Nemes's pursuit of precision in framing and lighting to evoke existential isolation, aligning with his broader interest in cinema's capacity to confront human limits. Similarly, French director Robert Bresson impressed Nemes as a "lost tradition," influencing his emphasis on stripped-down performances and spiritual realism over theatrical excess.[54][8][13]

Innovative techniques and stylistic choices

Nemes' most distinctive innovation, evident in Son of Saul (2015), involves long, unbroken takes with a shallow depth of field, where the camera remains affixed close to the protagonist's face and body, rendering the surrounding Auschwitz horrors as blurred, peripheral chaos.[2] This technique, executed in collaboration with cinematographer Mátyás Erdély, prioritizes subjective immersion in the individual's sensory experience over panoramic historical spectacle, using 35mm film stock to achieve an organic texture that digital capture could not replicate.[23] The approach confines explicit violence to off-screen implication, amplified by layered, non-diegetic sound design that evokes the camp's auditory inferno, thereby forcing viewers to inhabit the moral numbness of survival rather than detached observation.[55] In Sunset (2018), Nemes refines this methodology for a pre-World War I Budapest setting, employing extended tracking shots and persistent shallow focus to track protagonist Írisz Leiter through teeming urban crowds and hat shop intrigues, blurring the encroaching societal decay into impressionistic menace.[56] The style evolves to incorporate more dynamic camera movement—roving laterally and vertically across multi-level sets constructed to mimic historical grandeur—while maintaining focalization on the character's limited viewpoint, which disorients audiences amid the film's elliptical narrative of identity and empire's twilight.[30] This persistence in analog filming and choreographed long takes underscores Nemes' commitment to pushing cinematic grammar toward visceral, embodied realism, contrasting with contemporary trends favoring fragmented editing and wide lenses.[34] For Orphan (2025), Nemes adapts his signature apparatus to a postwar Hungarian family drama viewed through a child's perspective, integrating zooms and in-camera tracking shots on 35mm to reveal fractured domestic spaces and suppressed traumas without relying solely on the prior films' unrelenting proximity.[57] While retaining visual austerity through desaturated palettes and location-shot authenticity, the technique here yields to narrative restraint, using selective depth modulation to underscore generational silences rather than overwhelming horror, marking a maturation in applying immersion to introspective rather than cataclysmic scales.[58] Across his oeuvre, these choices—rooted in deliberate rejection of objective mastery—aim to restore film's capacity for ethical confrontation by privileging perceptual fidelity over interpretive ease.[59]

Core themes: Individual agency amid historical horror

In László Nemes's films, individual agency manifests as deliberate personal actions pursued amid overwhelming historical atrocities, often in defiance of dehumanizing systems or chaotic violence. In Son of Saul (2015), set during the October 1944 Jewish Sonderkommando revolt at Auschwitz-Birkenau, protagonist Saul Ausländer, a Hungarian prisoner, fixates on securing a rabbinical burial for a deceased boy he claims as his son, navigating gas chambers, crematoria, and prisoner hierarchies over a 36-hour period. This quest, drawn from survivor accounts like Miklós Nyiszli's memoirs, represents a singular moral imperative—restoring dignity to one amid industrialized murder—rather than broader survival or rebellion narratives. Nemes has stated that the film avoids "the story of survival," centering instead on Saul's unyielding focus, which underscores human volition persisting against mechanistic extermination, where over 1.1 million perished at the camp complex.[9][60] Nemes reinforces this theme stylistically through shallow-focus cinematography, locking the camera to Saul's face and torso while blurring peripheral horrors, compelling viewers to inhabit the protagonist's constrained agency without panoramic detachment. This technique, inspired by early cinema like Abel Gance's J'accuse (1919), isolates individual choice from historical totality, implying that agency endures not through comprehension of evil's scale but through ethical persistence in its midst—Saul's pursuit halts even as he aids the revolt's dynamite procurement. Critics note this as "ordinary decency applied in indecent circumstances," shifting emphasis from victim passivity to active, flawed humanity, countering portrayals that moralize differences among the oppressed. Nemes has emphasized returning responsibility to perpetrators, yet Saul's actions affirm prisoner autonomy, rejecting narratives of uniform helplessness.[27][2] The motif recurs in Sunset (2018), where young milliner Írisz Leiter traverses 1913 Budapest amid aristocratic intrigue, arson, and proto-fascist unrest, driven by an obsessive search for her vanished brother and familial truth. As the Austro-Hungarian Empire frays—foreshadowing World War I's 16-20 million deaths—Írisz's agency emerges in her refusal to accept imposed identities, confronting violence from hat-shop ruins to equestrian upheavals, embodying personal resolve against encroaching historical rupture. Nemes frames this as microcosmic resistance, where individual inquiry pierces societal facades of civility masking brutality. In Orphan (2025), Nemes extends the theme to postwar Hungary after the 1956 uprising's Soviet suppression, which killed 2,500 and displaced 200,000. A boy, raised on myths of an idealized deceased father, encounters a brutish claimant, forcing confrontation with paternal reality and inherited trauma in a devastated Budapest. Drawing from Nemes's family history, the narrative probes agency in legacy-building—nature versus nurture—amid communist authoritarianism's psychological scars, with the child's quest symbolizing generational reckoning over passive inheritance. Nemes describes this as exploring "archetypal" family dynamics against 20th-century cataclysms, prioritizing individual truth-seeking over collective ideology.[41][61] Across these works, Nemes posits agency not as heroic triumph but as Sisyphean assertion—moral or inquisitive acts amid horror's indifference—eschewing redemptive arcs for raw causality: historical forces crush multitudes, yet one person's focus reasserts humanity's irreducible core. This avoids didacticism, grounding in empirical atrocity details while critiquing escapist cinema that dilutes confrontation with reality.[62]

Reception, impact, and debates

Major awards and global accolades

Nemes' directorial debut Son of Saul (2015) achieved breakthrough global acclaim, winning the Grand Prix (second place) at the 68th Cannes Film Festival on May 24, 2015.[4] The film subsequently earned the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film at the 88th Academy Awards ceremony on February 28, 2016, marking a rare honor for a Hungarian submission.[63][64] It also secured the Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Non-English Language on January 10, 2016, and the BAFTA Award for Best Film Not in the English Language in 2016.[65] These victories highlighted the film's innovative depiction of Holocaust events, drawing praise from academy voters for its technical and narrative boldness. For Sunset (2018), Nemes received the FIPRESCI Prize at the 75th Venice International Film Festival on September 8, 2018, recognizing its stylistic exploration of historical upheaval.[66] The film was selected as Hungary's entry for the Academy Awards' Best International Feature Film category but did not advance to nominations.[67] Nemes' third feature Orphan (2025), premiered in 2025, was chosen as Hungary's official submission for the Best International Feature Film at the 98th Academy Awards, announced on September 12, 2025.[68][69] It earned a nomination for Best Film at the Carmel International Film Festival in 2025, though major wins remain pending as of late 2025.[70] Prior to his features, Nemes' short films collectively amassed over 30 international awards across more than 100 festivals, establishing early recognition for his distinctive visual and thematic approach.[71]

Critical praise for technical and emotional immersion

Son of Saul (2015) garnered widespread critical acclaim for its groundbreaking technical innovations that achieve profound emotional immersion, distinguishing it from conventional Holocaust depictions. The film's cinematography employs extended long takes with a shallow depth of field, maintaining a tight focus on protagonist Saul Ausländer's face or following him in over-the-shoulder shots at head height, while rendering the surrounding Auschwitz atrocities as blurred, peripheral chaos.[72] This approach, paired with an enveloping sound design that constructs a "claustrophobic hell" of distant screams and machinery, forces viewers into Saul's subjective viewpoint, evoking visceral empathy without explicit graphic imagery or detached observation.[72] Critics lauded this formal virtuosity for its gravity and ambition, describing it as an "immersive account" that delivers a profoundly distressing yet personal experience, earning "awed acclaim" at festivals like Cannes, where it secured the Grand Prix.[73] Nemes refined this immersive aesthetic in Sunset (2018), using roving long takes shot on 35mm film to track milliner Írisz Leiter through 1913 Budapest's opulent yet unraveling society, with the camera positioned mere inches from her face to simulate a first-person plunge into escalating disorientation.[74] Tight head-and-shoulders framing and blurred backgrounds amplify the sensory overload of crowded streets and hidden intrigues, mirroring Írisz's emotional turmoil and immersing audiences in a phantasmagoric torrent of events that demands active engagement to parse.[75] Reviewers praised this technique for blending breathtaking beauty with harrowing unease, heightening the film's elegiac depth on prewar Europe's fragility without relying on overt exposition.[74] In Orphan (2025), Nemes' technical precision continued to impress, with meticulous period reconstruction and controlled visuals underscoring postwar familial fractures in 1950s Hungary, though emotional immersion drew more divided responses for its restraint.[42] Critics highlighted the "impressive technical control" in evoking generational trauma through subtle, unsparing details, maintaining Nemes' signature focus on individual agency amid historical shadows.[42][76]

Criticisms regarding historical representation and narrative focus

Some critics have argued that the film's distinctive visual style, employing shallow focus and prolonged close-ups centered on protagonist Saul Ausländer, prioritizes stylistic innovation over a comprehensive portrayal of Auschwitz's systemic atrocities, rendering much of the surrounding horror as indistinct blurs and thereby distancing viewers from the full scale of the genocide. [77] Manohla Dargis of The New York Times described this technique as "intellectually repellent," contending that it transforms "screaming, weeping condemned men, women and children into anonymous background blurs," functioning more as a display of cinematic prowess than a means to convey historical reality.[78] Similarly, Richard Brody in The New Yorker critiqued the approach as a calculated "strategy" rather than genuine immersion, noting that the out-of-focus backgrounds muffle events and emphasize Saul's subjective viewpoint at the expense of broader contextual clarity.[27] The narrative's intense focus on Saul's personal quest to secure a rabbinical burial for a boy he claims as his son has drawn objections for narrowing the Holocaust's collective dimensions to an individualistic drama, sidelining the Sonderkommando's complex moral "gray zone" as articulated by Primo Levi and the banality of industrialized killing.[77] Peter Labuza in the Los Angeles Review of Books faulted the film for simplifying Sonderkommando psychology into dissociated inaction or self-interest, portraying them as passive "pawns" without exploring their active complicity in repression, and for rushing Saul through the camp without sufficient historical or ethical context, reducing atrocities to mere plot devices.[77] This confinement, critics like Brody argued, incorporates thriller-like suspense and contrivances that impose conventional dramatic arcs on Auschwitz, diluting the event's radical inhumanity and evoking moral ambiguities only superficially compared to Claude Lanzmann's Shoah.[27] Concerns over historical fidelity include depictions of Saul's improbable degree of mobility within the camp and the plausibility of his "insane" and "absurd" personal mission amid the Sonderkommando uprising on October 7, 1944, which some view as dramaturgical conveniences that strain verisimilitude for narrative propulsion.[79] Ray Brassier in Film Comment highlighted the "quite unrealistic freedom of movement" as essential to the plot but at odds with documented constraints, while questioning the appropriateness of infusing Auschwitz with thriller suspense, asserting that "vitality is not what the Shoah was all about."[79] Such choices have led to accusations of ethical overreach, with the film labeled an "opportunistic... work of meta-exploitation" that exploits technical fidelity—such as accurate recreations of crematoria layouts—for visceral impact without substantively grappling with the genocide's philosophical or systemic implications.[79][77]
