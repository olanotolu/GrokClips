Bogibeel Bridge

The Bogibeel Bridge is a 4.94-kilometre-long double-decker rail-cum-road bridge spanning the Brahmaputra River in Dibrugarh district, Assam, India, connecting the districts of Dibrugarh and Dhemaji and facilitating both broad-gauge railway traffic and vehicular movement.[1][2] Constructed with 39 main spans of 125 metres each and two end spans of 32.4 metres, it represents India's longest combined road and rail bridge, ranking as Asia's second longest in its category.[3][4] Initiated with foundation laying in 1997 and full construction commencing in 2002, the bridge faced delays due to the challenging riverine environment and seismic activity in the region, ultimately opening to traffic on 25 December 2018 after an investment exceeding â‚¹5,900 crore.[1][5] Its design incorporates fully welded steel-concrete composite girdersâ€”the first such application in Indian bridge engineeringâ€”adhering to European welding standards, alongside earthquake resistance up to magnitude 7 on the Richter scale and a projected service life of 120 years.[6][7] The bridge's strategic placement, approximately 17 kilometres downstream from Dibrugarh, enhances connectivity to northeastern India, reducing travel times, boosting trade, and supporting military logistics across the flood-prone Brahmaputra valley, thereby addressing long-standing infrastructural deficits in the area.[2][8] Engineered by Hindustan Construction Company using incremental launching techniques for the steel superstructure, it utilized over 35,400 metric tonnes of steel supplied by Steel Authority of India Limited, underscoring advancements in domestic heavy engineering capabilities.[9][10]

History

Conception and Early Planning

A techno-economic feasibility study for the Bogibeel rail-cum-road bridge over the Brahmaputra River was conducted from 1982 to 1984, with the report submitted to the Indian Railway Board in 1984.[11] This study addressed the strategic need for enhanced connectivity in northeastern India, particularly to link the Dibrugarh and Dhemaji districts of Assam and facilitate access to Arunachal Pradesh amid challenges posed by seasonal flooding and unreliable ferry crossings.[8] Subsequent detailed investigations, including final location surveys, were undertaken to evaluate hydraulic conditions, geotechnical stability, and alignment options across the river's wide, shifting channel.[12] These efforts confirmed the site's viability despite the Brahmaputra's high sediment load and flood-prone nature, informing the project's design as a multi-span steel truss structure.[11] The Government of India sanctioned the project in the 1996-97 railway budget, allocating initial funds for preparatory works.[8] On January 22, 1997, Prime Minister H. D. Deve Gowda laid the foundation stone, marking formal commitment to the initiative amid expectations for completion within the Ninth Five-Year Plan (1997-2002).[13][14] However, actual construction faced delays due to funding constraints and logistical hurdles, commencing only in April 2002.[14]

Initial Construction Phase and Interruptions

Construction of the Bogibeel Bridge commenced on April 21, 2002, under the leadership of Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee, marking the initiation of foundation and substructure work over the Brahmaputra River in Assam.[15] The project, initially estimated at â‚¹1,767 crore, aimed to build a 4.31 km (later extended to 4.94 km) combined road-and-rail structure using steel truss design, with early efforts focused on sinking large-diameter wells into the riverbed to support piers amid challenging hydraulic conditions.[16] By 2004, progress had slowed sufficiently to project a two-year delay beyond the original schedule, attributed to logistical hurdles in the remote, flood-prone region.[17] The initial phase from 2002 to 2007 encountered significant interruptions primarily due to funding constraints, which hampered the pace of substructure completion and material procurement.[7] These financial shortfalls led to sporadic work halts and inefficient resource allocation, exacerbating the bridge's vulnerability to annual Brahmaputra floods that eroded temporary cofferdams and access routes.[18] Political transitions following the 2004 elections contributed to perceived neglect, with Prime Minister Narendra Modi later attributing the overall 16-year delay to "lackadaisical attitudes" of preceding administrations, though this reflects partisan commentary rather than independent audit findings.[19] By mid-decade, only foundational elements were substantially advanced, with superstructure fabrication lagging, prompting the government in 2007 to designate the project a national priority for enhanced funding and oversight.[13] This status addressed acute budgetary gaps but underscored the initial phase's inefficiencies, as cost overruns had already begun accumulating from idle labor and equipment amid interruptions.[20] The delays shifted the expected 2009 completion by years, highlighting systemic challenges in large-scale infrastructure execution in northeastern India.[18]

Resumption, Completion, and Inauguration

Construction of the Bogibeel Bridge, initiated in April 2002 following the foundation stone laying in January 1997, experienced significant delays over the subsequent decade due to factors including regional insurgency, flooding, and contractor challenges, resulting in only sluggish progress by 2014.[13][21] In July 2014, work gained renewed momentum with intensified efforts by the Hindustan Construction Company (HCC), marking a resumption of substantive advancement after approximately 12 years of intermittent activity.[13] The project received further acceleration through the Pro-Active Governance and Timely Implementation (PRAGATI) initiative launched in 2015, which facilitated resolution of bottlenecks and oversight, transforming a 21-year-delayed endeavor into one nearing completion within three years.[22] By August 2018, officials projected full completion across structural, rail, and road components by October of that year, with girder and connectivity works finalized shortly thereafter.[23][24] The bridge was inaugurated on December 25, 2018, by Prime Minister Narendra Modi, coinciding with the birth anniversary of former Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee, who had envisioned the project.[14][25] During the event, Modi attributed the extended timelineâ€”spanning over 16 years from announcementâ€”to lapses under prior administrations, emphasizing the bridge's role in enhancing connectivity.[19][26] The inauguration enabled immediate rail and road operations, fulfilling the structure's dual-purpose design after a total construction period exceeding 200 months.[27]

Design and Technical Specifications

Structural and Material Innovations

The Bogibeel Bridge features a double-decked composite superstructure consisting of welded steel trusses and reinforced concrete, with 41 spans each measuring 125 m, marking it as India's first fully welded Warren truss girder-type steel bridge without bolts or rivets.[28][10] The lower deck accommodates two railway tracks, while the upper deck supports a three-lane road with footpaths, designed for a service life of approximately 120 years.[28][20] This fully welded construction employed gas metal arc welding techniques compliant with European standards, verified through non-destructive testing methods such as magnetic particle and ultrasonic inspections, enhancing structural integrity and reducing maintenance needs in a corrosive riverine environment.[28] A key innovation is the incremental launching method for erecting the superstructure, the first such application in India and resulting in the world's longest incrementally launched steel bridge at 4.94 km.[10][28] This technique involved sequentially pushing pre-fabricated truss segments into position over temporary supports, minimizing the need for extensive scaffolding in the flood-prone Brahmaputra River and allowing construction despite challenging site conditions.[10] Materials include approximately 80,000 tonnes of steel, primarily copper-bearing plates selected for superior corrosion resistance against the humid, sediment-laden environment, sourced from suppliers such as Jindal Steel, Essar Steel, and SAIL, along with 450,000 imported shear studs for composite action between steel and concrete.[28] The substructure incorporates 42 double D-shaped well foundations, with steining thickness of 3 m and diaphragm walls of 2.5 m, sunk to depths ranging from 42 m at abutments to 68.75 m for shore wells, providing enhanced lateral stability against deep riverbed scour.[28] Twin circular hollow piers, with outer diameters of 5.3 m and inner diameters of 3.3 m, further support the spans.[28] Seismic innovations address the bridge's location in Seismic Zone V, incorporating welded steel-concrete composite beams and trusses capable of withstanding earthquakes exceeding magnitude 7, augmented by seismic restrainers to maintain alignment during ground motion.[10][3] These features collectively represent advancements in durability, constructability, and resilience tailored to extreme hydrological and tectonic risks.[28][10]

Engineering Challenges and Solutions

The Brahmaputra River's extreme hydrological conditions posed significant engineering challenges during the Bogibeel Bridge's construction, including high water velocities exceeding 5 meters per second during monsoons, severe scour depths up to 50 meters, and annual flooding that limited work to a narrow 4-5 month dry season window.[29] To mitigate river-induced erosion and protect construction sites, engineers constructed temporary guide bunds using geotextile sandbags and stone revetments, channeling flow away from pier locations and allowing safe placement of cofferdams for foundation work.[29] These bunds, built progressively upstream and downstream, were designed to withstand peak discharges of over 30,000 cubic meters per second, though their installation required precise hydraulic modeling to avoid upstream flooding.[30] The site's location in Seismic Zone V, prone to earthquakes exceeding magnitude 7.0, demanded foundations and superstructures resistant to intense lateral forces, with subsoil comprising loose alluvial sands and silts susceptible to liquefaction.[31] Solutions included double-D shaped well foundations (caissons) sunk to depths of 58.6 meters for most piersâ€”among the deepest for such bridgesâ€”with innovative cutting-edge techniques like high-pressure water jetting and vibro-compaction to penetrate hard gravel layers and stabilize bases against scour.[32] [30] These caissons, with diameters up to 28 meters, were fabricated onshore, floated into position, and sunk using controlled dewatering, incorporating seismic isolators and energy-dissipating braces to handle horizontal forces up to 8 MN.[33] The bridge's superstructure faced durability issues from constant rail and road vibrations in a corrosive, humid environment, addressed by adopting India's first fully welded steel-concrete composite truss design over bolted connections, reducing maintenance needs and enhancing fatigue resistance.[34] [3] Welded joints, executed with specialized electrodes for high-strength steel (yield strength 355 MPa), were inspected via non-destructive testing to ensure integrity under cyclic loading, while the composite deckâ€”steel trusses encased in reinforced concreteâ€”provided redundancy against seismic shear.[34] Real-time structural health monitoring with embedded sensors for strain, tilt, and scour further enabled predictive maintenance, confirming the design's efficacy during post-construction events like the 2011 Sikkim earthquake.[3]

Connectivity and Operational Role

Rail and Road Integration

The Bogibeel Bridge incorporates a double-deck structural design to enable concurrent rail and road usage, with the lower deck dedicated to a double-line broad gauge railway track capable of supporting trains at operational speeds. This configuration spans 4.94 km across the Brahmaputra River, connecting the Dibrugarh and Dhemaji districts in Assam while integrating with the Northeast Frontier Railway network.[20][35] The upper deck provides three lanes for road vehicles, aligned with National Highway 15, allowing heavy truck traffic and passenger transport without disrupting rail operations below. The shared steel truss superstructure, fabricated to Eurocode standards, transfers loads from both decks to 40 reinforced concrete piers via inclined steel legs, ensuring stability under combined dynamic and static stresses from trains and highway loads.[35][36] Prior to inauguration on December 25, 2018, comprehensive load testing simulated dual traffic by applying concrete blocks and steel structures to both decks simultaneously, confirming no excessive deflections or abnormalities in deck interactions.[37] Post-completion structural health monitoring has verified alignment between the highway and railway decks, with sensors detecting minimal variances under operational conditions.[3] In operation, rail integration supports daily passenger services from Tinsukia to Naharlagun and freight corridors linking Arunachal Pradesh, equipped with axle counters for precise train detection and signaling to prevent conflicts. Road integration has streamlined logistics by reducing reliance on ferries, though toll collection and maintenance protocols manage peak-hour congestion on the upper deck independently of rail scheduling.[38][7]

Regional Transportation Enhancements

The Bogibeel Bridge has markedly improved inter-district connectivity in Assam by bridging the Brahmaputra River between Dibrugarh and Dhemaji districts, supplanting ferry-dependent crossings that previously extended journey durations to several hours amid seasonal flooding and high currents.[39] This infrastructure enables seamless road and rail passage, facilitating daily vehicular traffic on the upper deck and freight/passenger trains on the lower deck, thereby streamlining logistics for Upper Assam's tea estates, oil refineries, and agricultural sectors.[8] Travel efficiencies are evident in reduced durations: the route from Dibrugarh to Dhemaji now takes approximately one hour by road, compared to seven hours via pre-bridge detours, while connectivity to Arunachal Pradesh's Itanagar has shortened distances by 705 kilometers through direct linkage.[3] Rail services benefit similarly, with Delhi-Dibrugarh passenger trains cutting transit by three hours (from 37 to 34 hours), enhancing overall Northeast rail integration and reducing trans-shipment delays for cargo from 12 to two hours at key junctions.[3] [2] These enhancements extend to border regions, providing faster access from Assam's plains to Arunachal Pradesh's hill districts like Likabali and Pasighat, which supports increased freight volumes for local produce and minerals while alleviating bottlenecks in passenger mobility during monsoons.[40] The bridge's role in National Highway alignments further amplifies regional thoroughfares, promoting reliable year-round transport corridors that link remote tribal areas to urban markets in Dibrugarh and beyond.[10]

Strategic, Economic, and Social Impacts

National Security and Geopolitical Relevance

The Bogibeel Bridge significantly enhances India's national security by improving logistical capabilities in the northeastern region, particularly for rapid deployment of military assets to Arunachal Pradesh along the Line of Actual Control (LAC) with China. Prior to its completion in December 2018, transportation across the Brahmaputra River relied on seasonal ferries, which were disrupted during monsoons and limited the movement of heavy equipment and troops. The bridge's dual rail-road design allows for the swift transit of defense convoys, artillery, and supplies, reducing travel times from Assam's rail hubs to forward bases by hours or days.[41][42] This infrastructure bolsters India's deterrence posture amid ongoing border tensions, as evidenced by increased military infrastructure investments in the area following the 2017 Doklam standoff and 2020 Galwan clashes.[8] Geopolitically, the bridge counters China's territorial claims over Arunachal Pradeshâ€”referred to by Beijing as "South Tibet"â€”by facilitating sustained Indian presence and operational readiness in a contested frontier spanning over 1,000 kilometers. It integrates with the Trans-Arunachal Highway and other Border Roads Organisation projects, enabling faster reinforcement to eastern LAC sectors where terrain and weather historically favored defenders. Analysts note that such connectivity mitigates vulnerabilities exploited in past incursions, aligning with India's broader strategy to harden defenses against People's Liberation Army advances.[43][44] The structure's robust design, capable of supporting tanks and heavy military loads, underscores its role in logistics sustainment during prolonged standoffs.[3] Beyond external threats, the bridge aids internal security operations against insurgent groups in Assam, Nagaland, and Manipur by streamlining supply lines to remote outposts and enabling quicker response to cross-border infiltrations from Myanmar. This dual utility supports counter-insurgency efforts, which have persisted in the northeast despite peace accords, by reducing dependency on airlifts prone to weather delays. Overall, the project exemplifies India's infrastructure-led approach to securing peripheral regions, prioritizing empirical enhancements in mobility over diplomatic overtures alone.[8][45]

Economic Growth and Development Outcomes

The Bogibeel Bridge has facilitated enhanced freight movement across the Brahmaputra River, reducing dependency on seasonal ferries and enabling year-round connectivity between upper Assam and Arunachal Pradesh districts. This has lowered transportation costs for goods such as tea, oil, and agricultural produce, with cargo transit times cut by approximately 60% compared to pre-bridge routes reliant on ferries or detours.[46][7] In the fiscal year 2021-22, rail freight volumes utilizing the bridge reached 0.42 million tonnes, supporting aggregation and distribution of commodities from resource-rich areas in both states.[8] Economic activities in districts like Dibrugarh and Dhemaji have surged due to improved access to markets, with the bridge acting as a logistics hub for intra- and inter-state trade. It has boosted sectors including plantations, mining, and agriculture by providing faster rail and road links, thereby raising prospects for industrial development and local employment opportunities in these areas.[47][44] The structure connects nearly five million residents, enabling cost-effective movement of exports and reducing economic isolation of underdeveloped northeastern regions.[10] Post-inauguration in December 2018, the bridge has contributed to broader regional integration, with potential for increased trade volumes tied to complementary infrastructure like inland waterways at Bogibeel. While direct GDP attribution remains challenging amid multiple factors, the elimination of ferry bottlenecks has empirically lowered logistics expenses, fostering private investment in trade and ancillary services.[48][8]

Social and Cultural Effects

The construction of the Bogibeel Bridge resulted in significant displacement among the Mising tribal communities in Dhemaji district, affecting 136 households across Kareng and Dambuk villages through the acquisition of over 140 bighas of land.[49] Lacking formal land pattas, most displaced families received no compensation under the Land Acquisition Act of 1894, leading to landlessness and reliance on temporary embankment settlements or migration to areas like Amchang Wildlife Sanctuary.[49] This upheaval disrupted core livelihoods, including agriculture on lost farmlands, cattle grazing, and riverine fishing curtailed by new guide bunds, while the closure of Kareng Ghat ferry operations impacted approximately 200 dependent households.[49] Consequently, around 70 young individuals from these communities migrated to states such as Kerala and Karnataka for low-wage roles like security guards, exacerbating social fragmentation.[49] Culturally, the displacement eroded traditional practices and institutions; festivals like Aye-Aye-LÃ­gang and Po:rag declined due to reduced community cohesion and youth exodus, while activities such as Eri silk rearing, Gadu weaving, and the Murong Ukom community council weakened amid livelihood pressures.[49] The bridge's commissioning in December 2018 further strained local economies by rendering obsolete ferry services that previously transported passengers and goods across the Brahmaputra, affecting over 120 boatmen operating more than 40 vessels, with individual operators earning roughly Rs 30,000 monthly after expenses.[50] [51] Post-opening, ferry ghats at Bogibeel deserted, idling boats and displacing associated vendors, kiosks, and laborersâ€”totaling 50 to 60 small enterprisesâ€”without widespread alternative employment options beyond limited agriculture.[52]

Controversies and Criticisms

Project Delays and Cost Escalations

The Bogibeel Bridge project faced protracted delays, with foundational work halting after initial site preparation in the early 2000s and substantive construction resuming only in 2011, despite approvals dating back to 1997.[16][13] The original completion target of April 2009 was missed repeatedly, pushing inauguration to December 25, 2018, resulting in a nearly decade-long overrun.[13] Primary causes included the Brahmaputra River's seasonal flooding, which confined construction to approximately five months annually during dry periods, and the need for extensive river training to manage its unpredictable flow and prevent scour damage to foundations.[53][54] These delays compounded logistical challenges, such as slow progress on well foundations and superstructure erection, further exacerbated by design modificationsâ€”including an increase in bridge height from 32 meters to 64.6 meters above the riverbedâ€”to accommodate higher flood levels observed post-2004.[55] In 2004, officials projected a two-year extension beyond prior schedules due to these environmental constraints.[17] By 2015, girder launching faced additional setbacks, with contractors like HCC warning of further timeline slippages tied to material procurement and site access issues.[56] Cost overruns mirrored the timeline issues, escalating from an initial estimate of â‚¹1,767 crore in the early planning phase to a sanctioned amount of â‚¹3,230 crore by 2002, and ultimately reaching â‚¹5,960 crore upon completionâ€”an approximate 85% increase over the sanctioned figure.[13][6] The threefold rise from the original estimate stemmed directly from delay-induced inflation, revised engineering specifications for seismic and flood resilience, and escalated material costs, including a potential â‚¹300 crore hike in girder fabrication alone.[7][56] Parliamentary queries in 2018 highlighted these escalations as symptomatic of broader inefficiencies in mega-infrastructure projects, though Northeast Frontier Railway attributed much of the variance to unavoidable geophysical factors rather than mismanagement.[57]

Political and Naming Disputes

The Bogibeel Bridge has been subject to ongoing disputes over its naming, reflecting ethnic and regional sensitivities in Assam. Prior to its inauguration on December 25, 2018, various indigenous communities advocated for names honoring their historical figures. The Tai Ahom community demanded recognition tied to their legacy, leading to protests including a proposed road blockade on December 18, 2018. Similarly, Mising organizations, such as the Takam Mising Porin Kebang (TMPK), pushed for naming it after Swahid Kamala Miri, arguing that a significant portion of affected villages belonged to their community.[58][59][60] The Deuri community sought to name it after their leader Sati Sadhani, with the Assam government proposing "Birangana Sati Sadhani Setu" on October 17, 2018, as an alternative to the generic "Birangana Sadhani Bridge." Over 3,000 protesters gathered in December 2018 to demand "Sati Sadhani Setu," invoking Ahom dynasty heritage and local historical claims. These demands highlighted inter-community rivalries, as groups like the Misings justified their claims based on demographic impacts from construction. Union Railway Minister Piyush Goyal, on January 3, 2019, dismissed the need for an official rename, stating the bridge's functional identity as Bogibeel sufficed, which drew mixed reactions from local stakeholders who viewed it as overlooking cultural significance.[61][16][62] Despite Prime Minister Narendra Modi's inauguration remarks framing the bridge as a tribute to former PM Atal Bihari Vajpayee on his birth anniversary, no formal renaming occurred, preserving the original "Bogibeel Bridge" designation. In September 2025, the All Assam Students' Union (AASU) proposed renaming it after Assamese singer Zubeen Garg to honor his cultural contributions, but Chief Minister Himanta Biswa Sarma cautioned on October 9, 2025, that such a change could reignite ethnic controversies, underscoring persistent political risks in accommodating singular identities amid Assam's diverse tribal fabric. These naming debates have occasionally intersected with broader political narratives, including partisan critiques during the 2018-2019 period where opposition parties accused the central government of politicizing infrastructure for electoral gain in Northeast India.[63][64][65]

Environmental and Construction Risks

The Bogibeel Bridge's construction encountered formidable challenges stemming from the Brahmaputra River's dynamic hydrology and the site's placement in Seismic Zone V, the highest risk category for earthquakes in India, capable of magnitudes exceeding 7.0.[31] The river's annual monsoon flooding inundates a roughly 5 km wide area near the site, restricting viable construction windows to just 4-5 months of drier weather each year, during which guide bundsâ€”essential temporary embankments to divert flow and protect foundationsâ€”had to be rapidly erected amid turbulent currents and high sediment loads.[33][29] Logistical hurdles included transporting concrete across river channels spanning 600-900 meters wide, compounded by deep scour depths that threatened foundation stability.[29] Seismic vulnerabilities necessitated innovative engineering, such as welded steel-concrete composite trusses elevated 32 meters above the riverbed to mitigate quake-induced lateral forces and ensure post-event functionality, marking one of India's early adoptions of such techniques in high-risk zones.[3] Despite these adaptations, the project's exposure to Assam's frequent seismic eventsâ€”exacerbated by the 1950 Assam earthquake's legacy of altered river morphology through massive Himalayan landslides depositing billions of cubic meters of sedimentâ€”posed ongoing threats to structural integrity during the 2002-2018 build phase.[66] Environmentally, the bridge's 4.94 km span across the Brahmaputra introduced risks of altered fluvial dynamics, including river narrowing that could amplify flood velocities and erosion downstream, as the Brahmaputra is known for its erosive "black serpent" behavior that routinely erodes banks and displaces communities.[67] Post-completion observations confirmed sandbar accumulation on the northern bank due to the main channel's southern shift, contributing to intensified erosion in adjacent Dibrugarh districts as of 2025.[68] While design features like elevated piers aimed to preserve natural flow and minimize aquatic habitat disruption, critics highlighted potential long-term exacerbation of regional flood-erosion cycles in the ecologically fragile Majuli area, where similar Brahmaputra-spanning infrastructure has heightened tensions over water resource allocation and land loss.[69] These concerns underscore causal linkages between anthropogenic river interventions and amplified hydrogeomorphic hazards in sediment-laden systems like the Brahmaputra basin.[70]

Post-Completion Developments and Legacy

Following its inauguration on December 25, 2018, the Bogibeel Bridge has enabled continuous rail and road operations, handling increased freight and passenger traffic that has streamlined connectivity between Assam's northern and southern banks of the Brahmaputra River. Travel distances to Arunachal Pradesh have been reduced by over 170 kilometers, with journey times shortened to about four hours, facilitating quicker movement of goods and people.[71][8] The structure has supported faster disaster response, including flood evacuations in Dhemaji district, where prior reliance on ferries often delayed relief efforts during monsoons.[47] Maintenance efforts post-completion include large-scale structural health monitoring using sensors to track vibrations, strains, and environmental factors, ensuring the bridge's integrity in a seismically active and flood-vulnerable zone. Connecting highways, such as the Dibrugarhâ€“Bogibeelâ€“Silapathar route, are undergoing widening and repaving to accommodate growing vehicular loads and reduce bottlenecks. However, sandbar accumulation near the bridge has contributed to riverbank erosion in Dibrugarh, exacerbating local flooding risks as identified in hydrological assessments.[3][72][47][68] The bridge's legacy endures as a pivotal enabler of Northeast India's integration under initiatives like the Act East Policy, enhancing military logistics to frontier areas and cutting supply chain detours for border defenses. Economically, it has lowered transport costs, spurring trade, agriculture exports, and tourismâ€”particularly riverine excursions leveraging the site's scenic and engineering appealâ€”while symbolizing resilient infrastructure amid prolonged construction hurdles. Despite environmental side effects like erosion, its operational success underscores advancements in managing Brahmaputra's dynamic hydrology, with sustained monitoring vital for longevity.[8][7][73][68]
