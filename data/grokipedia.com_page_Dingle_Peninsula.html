Dingle Peninsula

The Dingle Peninsula (Irish: Corca Dhuibhne ; anglicised as Corkaguiny or Corcaguiny , the name of the corresponding barony) is the northernmost of the major peninsulas in County Kerry. It ends beyond the town of Dingle at Dunmore Head, the westernmost point of mainland Ireland.[1] It is separated from the Iveragh Peninsula to the south by the Dingle Bay.

Name

The Dingle Peninsula is named after the town of Dingle. The peninsula is also commonly called Corca Dhuibhne ([Corca Duibne](Corca Duibne "Corca Duibne")) even when those referring to it are speaking in English. Corca Dhuibhne,[2] which means "seed or tribe of Duibhne"[3] (a goddess from Irish mythology and an Irish clan name), refers to the tÃºath (people, nation) of Corco Dhuibhne who occupied the peninsula in the Middle Ages and who also held territories on the south shore of Dingle Bay in County Kerry, including the Iveragh Peninsula.

Geography

The peninsula exists because of the band of sandstone rock that forms the Slieve Mish mountain range at the neck of the peninsula, in the east, and the Brandon Group of mountains, and the Mountains of the Central Dingle Peninsula further to the west. Ireland's highest mountain outside MacGillycuddy's Reeks, Mount Brandon at 952 m (3,123 ft), forms part of a high ridge with views over the peninsula and North Kerry. Conor Pass, which runs from Dingle on the south-western end of the peninsula towards Brandon Bay and Castlegregory in the north-east, is the highest mountain pass in Ireland, a narrow, twisting road; it weaves its way around the sharp cliff faces and past the high corrie lakes. The Blasket Islands lie off the west coast. They are known for the literary and linguistic heritage of the former inhabitants. However, these remote islands have been uninhabited since the 1950s following an evacuation.

Places of Interest in the Dingle Peninsula

Places of interest

The peninsula is known for the MV Ranga, a Spanish container ship that wrecked at Dunmore Head on 11 March 1982 after losing power during a storm.[5] Gallaunmore is a standing stone and National Monument located 3 km east of Dingle, standing 4.2 m tall. Rahinnane Castle is a 15th-century tower house and National Monument built on a medieval ringfort. Slieveglass, an area of high ground near the village of Brandon, was the site of Ireland's first fatal airliner accident. On 28 July 1943, a BOAC Short S.25 Sunderland III, G-AGES, crashed at 2,000 feet while descending into Foynes in fog, killing 10 of the 25 onboard.[6] The Slea Head Drive is a scenic 50 km loop along the peninsula's southwestern coast, offering views of cliffs, beaches, and historical sites, rated as one of Ireland's top drives as of 2025. Conor Pass, the highest mountain pass in Ireland at 456 m, provides panoramic views of the peninsula and access to hiking trails near Mount Brandon. The Blasket Islands, an abandoned archipelago off the western coast, are accessible by ferry from Dunquin and feature preserved traditional Irish cottages and wildlife, designated as a Special Area of Conservation. Inch Beach is a 6 km long sandy strand popular for surfing, walking, and equestrian activities.

Dingle Peninsula in Film

In film

David Lean's 1970 film Ryan's Daughter takes place at a village on the Dingle Peninsula in the immediate aftermath of the 1916 Easter Rising, and was partly shot on location near DÃºn Chaoin, Coumeenole Beach, Slea Head and Inch Strand. Far and Away , a 1992 film directed by Ron Howard, was partly filmed on the peninsula. The film Leap Year is partly set in the Dingle Peninsula, but none of the filming took place in the area. Several areas on the peninsula were used for filming of Star Wars: The Last Jedi, including Dunmore Head, the site of Luke Skywalker's X-wing crash scene, and Sybil Head near Slea Head, where replicas of the clochÃ¡n huts from Skellig Michael were built.

In music

The Dingle Peninsula has a long tradition of both instrumental music and song. In recent decades, several musicians from the area have gained international recognition for Irish traditional music. Dingle is mentioned in Phil Colclough and June Colclough's "Song for Ireland." The town of Dingle has hosted the music television series Other Voices annually since 2001, featuring performances in St James's Church and other local venues.
