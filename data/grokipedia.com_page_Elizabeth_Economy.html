Elizabeth Economy

Elizabeth C. Economy is an American political scientist specializing in China's domestic politics, foreign policy, and the implications for U.S.-China relations.[1][2] She serves as the Hargrove Senior Fellow and co-chair of the Program on the U.S., China, and the World at Stanford University's Hoover Institution.[1][3] Economy previously directed Asia studies at the Council on Foreign Relations as the C.V. Starr Senior Fellow, where she focused on China's political economy and global influence.[4] From 2021 to 2023, she acted as Senior Advisor for China at the U.S. Department of Commerce, contributing to policy responses to economic and security challenges posed by Beijing.[3][5] Economy has authored several books analyzing the evolution of China's governance under Xi Jinping and its assertive international strategy, including The Third Revolution: Xi Jinping and the New Chinese State (2018) and The World According to China (2022), which detail Beijing's efforts to reshape global norms and institutions in alignment with its authoritarian model.[1][6] Her scholarship critiques the limitations of prior U.S. engagement policies with China, advocating for a realist approach that prioritizes economic decoupling, technology restrictions, and alliances to counter China's state-directed expansionism.[7][2] Through testimony before Congress and contributions to policy journals, Economy has influenced debates on confronting China's resource quests, cyber ambitions, and Belt and Road Initiative as instruments of geopolitical leverage rather than mutual development.[4][8]

Early Life and Education

Academic Background and Influences

Economy earned a B.A. with honors from Swarthmore College in 1984.[9] She subsequently obtained an A.M. in political science from Stanford University.[1] In 1994, she completed a Ph.D. in political science at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, with a dissertation titled Negotiating the Terrain of Global Climate Change: International Institutions and the Domestic Politics of the United States and China, which examined how domestic political factors in both countries influenced participation in global environmental regimes. Her doctoral research focused on the interplay between international institutions and domestic politics in U.S.-China relations, particularly in environmental policy, foreshadowing her later emphasis on how internal Chinese dynamics shape foreign policy outcomes. This training in comparative and international political economy provided a foundation for her analyses of authoritarian governance and global engagement, drawing on empirical case studies rather than abstract theoretical models. While specific academic mentors are not publicly detailed in her biographical materials, her early work reflects engagement with the subfield of international relations and China studies, as evidenced by post-doctoral collaborations such as co-editing China Joins the World: Progress and Prospects (1999) with Michel Oksenberg, a leading scholar of Chinese politics.[1] Economy has also held adjunct teaching roles that extended her academic influence, including courses on U.S.-China relations and Asian politics at Columbia University, the Paul H. Nitze School of Advanced International Studies at Johns Hopkins University, and the University of Washington's Jackson School of International Studies.[9] These positions reinforced her grounding in rigorous, evidence-based scholarship on East Asian security and economic policy.

Professional Career

Early Career and Research Roles

Following completion of her PhD from the University of Michigan, Elizabeth Economy assumed the role of Research Associate at the Council on Foreign Relations, concentrating on Asia-focused research that laid the groundwork for her subsequent analyses of Chinese policy.[1] In this capacity, she co-edited The Internationalization of Environmental Protection (Cambridge University Press, 1997) with Miranda A. Schreurs, a collection assessing how environmental norms and regimes spread across borders, with particular attention to emerging economies like China.[10] Two years later, she co-edited China Joins the World: Progress and Prospects (Council on Foreign Relations Press, 1999) with Michel Oksenberg, which evaluated China's post-Tiananmen participation in global institutions, including the United Nations, arms control frameworks, and trade organizations, based on contributions from specialists in international law and security.[11][1] Concurrently, Economy taught as an adjunct faculty member at Columbia University, Johns Hopkins University's Paul H. Nitze School of Advanced International Studies, and the University of Washington's Jackson School of International Studies, offering undergraduate and graduate courses on international relations, comparative politics, and U.S.-Asia dynamics.[1] These roles honed her interdisciplinary approach, blending empirical case studies of policy implementation with theoretical insights into state behavior under globalization pressures.

Tenure at the Council on Foreign Relations

Elizabeth Economy served as the C.V. Starr Senior Fellow and Director for Asia Studies at the Council on Foreign Relations (CFR) for over a decade until joining the U.S. Department of Commerce in 2021.[1] In this role, she led CFR's Asia program, which produced research reports, backgrounders, interactive data visualizations, and events analyzing Asia-Pacific issues, including economic trends, security challenges, and political developments, with a primary focus on China and U.S.-China relations.[12] Her oversight extended to coordinating expert contributions and fostering interdisciplinary analysis on topics such as China's domestic governance and foreign policy assertiveness.[1] During her tenure, Economy advanced CFR's scholarship on China through high-profile publications and policy engagements. She co-edited China Joins the World: Progress and Prospects (Council on Foreign Relations Press, 1999), assessing China's integration into global institutions post-Deng Xiaoping reforms.[1] She contributed articles to Foreign Affairs, CFR's peer-reviewed journal, including pieces critiquing China's state capitalism and its implications for international norms, such as "Xi Jinping's New World Order" (December 2021).[13] Economy also testified before the U.S.-China Economic and Security Review Commission, for example, on the roots of protests in China and the Chinese Communist Party's suppression tactics in a 2019 hearing.[14][15] Economy frequently participated in CFR events, podcasts, and briefings that shaped elite discourse on U.S. policy toward China. Notable examples include discussions on Xi Jinping's consolidation of power (May 2018) and the export of Chinese authoritarian technologies (May 2020), where she analyzed Beijing's efforts to influence global standards in surveillance and digital infrastructure.[16][17] Her analyses often emphasized empirical evidence of China's non-market practices and ideological expansion, urging a recalibration of U.S. engagement strategies away from unconditional optimism toward competitive realism.[18] This work positioned CFR as a key venue for scrutinizing official narratives on China's "peaceful rise," drawing on data from trade imbalances, human rights reports, and military modernization metrics.[19]

U.S. Government Service

From 2021 to 2023, Elizabeth Economy served as Senior Advisor for China at the U.S. Department of Commerce, advising Secretary Gina Raimondo on policies related to U.S.-China economic relations and national security.[1][20] In this role, she focused on countering China's technological and industrial advancements, contributing to the administration's strategy of managed competition with Beijing.[21] Her work supported the enforcement of export controls through the Bureau of Industry and Security, which restricted China's access to advanced semiconductors, artificial intelligence tools, and other dual-use technologies critical for military applications.[21] These measures, expanded during the Biden administration, built on prior restrictions by targeting entities involved in China's military-civil fusion efforts, aiming to slow Beijing's progress in areas like supercomputing and quantum technology.[21] Economy's advisory input emphasized protecting U.S. supply chains and innovation while navigating trade negotiations and investment screening under the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS).[1] Economy took this position on leave from the Hoover Institution, reflecting her prior expertise in China policy from the Council on Foreign Relations.[1] Post-tenure, she has critiqued aspects of the approach as insufficiently aggressive, arguing for deeper decoupling in critical sectors to address China's state-directed economic coercion.[22]

Role at the Hoover Institution

Elizabeth Economy was appointed Senior Fellow at the Hoover Institution effective September 1, 2020.[23] In this capacity, she holds the title of Hargrove Senior Fellow and co-chairs the Program on the US, China, and the World, a initiative launched on October 1, 2024, to examine bilateral economic, security, and policy dynamics.[1] Her responsibilities include directing research on China's global influence, US strategic responses, and related governance challenges, often through publications, events, and policy analysis.[1] Economy hosts the Hoover podcast China Considered, which debuted on November 18, 2024, and features discussions on competitive aspects of US-China relations, including trade, technology, and geopolitical tensions.[24] From 2021 to 2023, Economy took leave from Hoover to serve as Senior Advisor for China at the US Department of Commerce, resuming her fellowship thereafter.[1] Her work at the institution emphasizes empirical assessments of China's policy shifts under Xi Jinping and recommendations for US decoupling or competition strategies, as evidenced in her contributions to Hoover events and external testimonies.[1]

Expertise on China

Views on Chinese Domestic Policy and Xi Jinping's Reforms

Economy contends that Xi Jinping has initiated a "third revolution" in China, characterized by a reversal of Deng Xiaoping's post-Mao reforms toward greater authoritarian control domestically, including deepened Communist Party penetration into state institutions, civil society, and the private sector.[25] This shift, detailed in her 2018 book The Third Revolution: Xi Jinping and the New Chinese State, involves centralizing power through measures such as the 2013â€“ongoing anti-corruption campaign, which Economy views as a tool not only for purging graft but also for eliminating political rivals and consolidating Xi's personal authority, resulting in the disciplining of over 1.4 million officials by 2017.[26] She argues this campaign, while addressing real corruptionâ€”evidenced by cases like the downfall of Zhou Yongkang in 2014â€”has entrenched a patronage system loyal to Xi, fostering a more hierarchical and less collective leadership structure.[27] On economic policy, Economy describes Xi's "supply-side structural reforms" launched in 2015 as a facade for expanding state dominance, with initiatives like mixed-ownership reforms prioritizing party oversight in private enterprises, as seen in the 2017 requirement for non-state firms to establish party cells.[25] She highlights how this approach has curtailed the autonomy of tech giantsâ€”such as the 2020â€“2021 regulatory crackdown on Alibaba and Tencent, which wiped out over $1 trillion in market valueâ€”contrasting it with the decentralized experimentation of prior decades and warning of stifled innovation due to reduced incentives for risk-taking.[1] Economy attributes China's sustained GDP growth, averaging 6.7% annually from 2013 to 2017, to this state-directed model but cautions that it risks long-term stagnation by prioritizing political control over market efficiency.[27] In social and ideological spheres, Economy portrays Xi's reforms as erecting a "comprehensive national power" framework that intensifies surveillance and censorship, evolving the Great Firewall into a tool for preempting dissent via AI-driven monitoring, with over 2 million internet censors employed by 2013.[25] She critiques the erosion of civil society, including the 2016 Foreign NGO Law that imposed registration and funding restrictions, effectively curbing independent activism, and the expansion of re-education camps in Xinjiang affecting an estimated 1 million Uyghurs by 2018, which she frames as part of Xi's drive for "stability maintenance" through ethnic assimilation and Han-centric nationalism.[26] Economy argues these policies reflect a rejection of pluralistic influences, replacing them with Xi Jinping Thought as the guiding ideology enshrined in the 2017 party constitution, thereby reversing liberalization trends and fostering a totalitarian penetration of daily life.[28] Economy maintains that Xi's domestic trajectory has persisted into the 2020s, with post-2018 moves like abolishing presidential term limits enabling indefinite rule, and the zero-COVID policy from 2020 to 2022 exemplifying rigid top-down control despite economic costs exceeding 5% of GDP in lockdowns.[1] In her analysis, this authoritarian hardeningâ€”while yielding short-term stabilityâ€”undermines the adaptability that fueled China's prior growth, as evidenced by youth unemployment peaking at 21.3% in mid-2023 amid regulatory overreach.[29] She posits that without reverting to decentralized reforms, Xi's model risks internal brittleness, though she notes its resilience in maintaining party legitimacy through propaganda and economic deliverables.[30]

Perspectives on Chinese Foreign Policy and Global Ambitions

Elizabeth Economy argues that under Xi Jinping, China pursues a comprehensive strategy to remake the international system, positioning itself at the center of global affairs and challenging the post-World War II liberal order. In her analysis, this ambition stems from Xi's vision of restoring China's historical centrality, involving assertive sovereignty claims over territories like Taiwan, Hong Kong, and the South China Sea, where China has territorial disputes with 13 countries.[31][32] She contends that these efforts extend beyond regional dominance to supplanting U.S. influence in the Asia-Pacific through institutions like the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization.[32] Central to Economy's perspective is China's "five pillars" for global transformation: realizing sovereignty claims, displacing U.S. leadership in the region, building a self-reliant and dominant economy via policies like dual circulation, aligning other nations' values with China's authoritarian model, and reforming international governance institutions such as the United Nations to reflect Beijing's preferences.[32] The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), launched in 2013, exemplifies this approach, evolving from infrastructure projects into a multifaceted tool encompassing digital, health, and green Silk Roads, with participation from 36 countries in digital components and plans for military bases, as seen in Djibouti.[32][33] Economy highlights how the BRI exports Chinese political values, facilitates military expansion, and enables surveillance technologies, contrasting with Western infrastructure aid by prioritizing geopolitical leverage over transparency.[6][33] Economy critiques China's "Wolf Warrior" diplomacy as a shift to confrontational tactics, including economic coercionâ€”such as trade boycotts against Australia and Lithuaniaâ€”and disinformation campaigns, like interference in Taiwan's elections, to intimidate adversaries and assert dominance.[32] In her 2022 book The World According to China, she details Xi's innovations, including ambitions to control international internet traffic and governance, embedding authoritarian surveillance without legal protections, which she views as a direct challenge to open, rule-based systems.[6][33] She predicts that success in initiatives like dedollarization could raise U.S. borrowing costs and diminish American technological edge, though China's coercive methods often provoke backlash, limiting its soft power and alliances.[32][31] Overall, Economy warns that China's global ambitions threaten Western values and U.S.-led alliances, urging a bipartisan U.S. response focused on countering Beijing's institutional influence and bolstering partners like Taiwan, while noting China's overreach has already strained its relations with much of the developed world.[33][31]

Critiques of U.S. Engagement with China

Economy has contended that the U.S. policy of engagement with China, initiated in the 1970s under Deng Xiaoping's reforms and sustained through economic integration via China's 2001 World Trade Organization accession, failed to achieve its primary goal of promoting gradual political liberalization and alignment with international norms. Rather than converging toward democratic governance and market-oriented reforms, China under the Chinese Communist Party leveraged access to U.S. markets, technology, and capital to reinforce state control, subsidize inefficient industries, and conduct intellectual property theft estimated at up to $600 billion annually to the U.S. economy by 2017.[34] This outcome, she argues, stemmed from a U.S. miscalculation that economic interdependence would inexorably lead to political openness, ignoring Beijing's strategic use of engagement to build asymmetric advantages without reciprocity on issues like human rights, rule of law, or fair trade practices.[35] In her 2018 book The Third Revolution: Xi Jinping and the New Chinese State, Economy delineates how Xi's consolidation of power since 2012 marked a decisive reversal of the limited liberalization seen in prior decades, transforming engagement's assumptions into a policy liability. Xi's campaigns against corruption, ideological conformity, and state-led innovation, she explains, centralized authority in ways that prioritized regime security over global integration, rendering prior U.S. hopes for a "responsible stakeholder" illusory.[26][36] Engagement, in this view, inadvertently enabled China's military modernization and Belt and Road Initiative expansion, which by 2018 encompassed over $1 trillion in investments across 140 countries, often through debt-trap diplomacy that undermined U.S. influence without fostering mutual benefits.[18] Economy attributes this failure partly to Washington's reluctance to condition engagement on verifiable reforms, allowing Beijing to question the policy's value post-2008 global financial crisis while extracting gains.[37] She further critiques the engagement paradigm for overlooking China's weaponization of economic ties, including coercion tactics like rare earth export restrictions in 2010 against Japan and discriminatory trade practices against Australia in 2020 over foreign policy disputes. In Hoover Institution analyses, Economy highlights how sustained engagement delayed U.S. recognition of these patterns, contributing to supply chain vulnerabilities exposed during the COVID-19 pandemic, where China's dominance in critical minerals and pharmaceuticals amplified risks.[38] This, she posits, necessitated a pivot to "strategic competition" under both Trump and Biden administrations, emphasizing decoupling in sensitive technologies, export controls, and alliances like the Quad to counter China's revisionist ambitions rather than presuming cooperative convergence.[21][35] Economy warns against over-reliance on hoping for Chinese self-correction, as in economic slowdowns or policy missteps under Xi, urging instead proactive U.S. measures grounded in realism about Beijing's ideological commitment to party supremacy. While acknowledging partial successes in areas like WTO dispute mechanisms, she maintains that engagement's net effect empowered an adversarial China, costing the U.S. manufacturing jobsâ€”over 2 million displaced between 2001 and 2011 per Economic Policy Institute estimatesâ€”and eroding strategic deterrence.[39][40] This critique underscores her broader call for policies that prioritize national security and economic resilience over outdated optimism.

Publications and Intellectual Contributions

Major Books

Elizabeth Economy's major books center on China's environmental challenges, resource strategies, domestic political transformations, and international aspirations, drawing on her extensive research into the country's governance and global role. These works emphasize empirical analysis of policy outcomes, often highlighting tensions between economic growth and sustainability, as well as the centralizing tendencies under Xi Jinping.[1] In The River Runs Black: The Environmental Challenge to China's Future, published by Cornell University Press in 2004 with a second edition in 2010, Economy examines the severe degradation of China's environment amid rapid industrialization, attributing it to decentralized enforcement where local officials prioritize growth over regulation. The book details historical and cultural factors contributing to pollution, alongside evolving government responses, and argues that unresolved crises could undermine long-term development. It received the 2005 International Convention of Asia Scholars award, was named among the top 10 books of 2004 by The Globalist, and listed as one of the top 50 sustainability books by the University of Cambridge Programme for Sustainability Leadership.[1][41] Co-authored with Michael Levi, By All Means Necessary: How China's Resource Quest is Changing the World (Oxford University Press, 2014) analyzes China's aggressive pursuit of natural resources abroad to fuel its economic expansion, covering strategies like state-owned enterprise investments and diplomatic outreach in Africa, Latin America, and elsewhere. The authors assess geopolitical risks, including tensions with resource-hosting nations and impacts on global markets, while noting China's adaptive "go out" policy since the early 2000s.[1][42] The Third Revolution: Xi Jinping and the New Chinese State (Oxford University Press, 2018) details Xi's consolidation of authority since 2012, framing it as a third revolutionary phase after Mao Zedong and Deng Xiaoping, with reforms in ideology, economics, society, and foreign policy that reverse prior liberalization. Economy documents increased state control over the economy, media, and civil society, supported by data on policy shifts like anti-corruption campaigns and technological nationalism, and was shortlisted for the 2019 Lionel Gelber Prize.[1] Her most recent book, The World According to China (Polity, 2022), explores Beijing's vision for reshaping global governance, including challenges to Western-led institutions through initiatives like the Belt and Road and digital standards promotion. It traces Xi-era foreign policy ambitions to reclaim historical influence, backed by examples of economic coercion and norm-building efforts as of 2021.[1]

Key Articles and Policy Papers

Economy has contributed numerous articles and policy papers analyzing China's political evolution, economic strategies, and implications for U.S. policy, often published in outlets such as Foreign Affairs and Harvard Business Review. Her 2024 Foreign Affairs article "Chinaâ€™s Alternative Order: And What America Should Learn From It," published on April 23, critiques Beijing's efforts under Xi Jinping to construct a parallel global governance framework emphasizing state sovereignty, digital authoritarianism, and economic coercion over liberal democratic norms.[43] In it, she contends that China's model prioritizes regime security and developmental priorities, urging the U.S. to bolster alliances and domestic innovation to counter this challenge without mirroring Beijing's coercive tactics.[43] In a January 2025 co-authored piece with Melanie Hart in Foreign Affairs, "Americaâ€™s China Strategy Is Incomplete," Economy argues that U.S. efforts to compete with China have overlooked key domains like technology standards and global south influence, recommending expanded export controls, investment screening, and multilateral coalitions to address these gaps.[44] This builds on her March 2021 congressional testimony before the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, "Advancing Effective U.S. Policy for Strategic Competition with China in the Twenty-First Century," where she advocated for a comprehensive strategy integrating economic decoupling in critical sectors, human rights advocacy, and military deterrence to counter China's multifaceted ambitions.[45] Her policy-oriented work includes editing the March 2022 Hoover Institution volume "Digital Currencies: The US, China, and the World at a Crossroads" with Darrell Duffie, which examines China's digital yuan as a tool for circumventing U.S. financial dominance and evading sanctions, while proposing U.S. regulatory reforms to maintain dollar hegemony and mitigate risks from state-backed cryptocurrencies.[46] In Harvard Business Review, Economy's February 2024 article "Is Chinaâ€™s Economic Dominance at an Inflection Point?" highlights demographic declines and policy missteps eroding China's growth trajectory, projecting a shrinking working-age population from 2023 onward that could reduce GDP growth to below 3% annually by 2030 absent structural reforms.[47] Earlier contributions, such as her April 2019 ChinaFile piece "Trade: Parade of Broken Promises," documented Beijing's repeated violations of WTO commitments and bilateral trade pledges, supporting arguments for tariffs as leverage rather than naive engagement.[48] These works underscore Economy's emphasis on empirical assessments of China's state-driven model, drawing from official data like National Bureau of Statistics reports on population trends and trade imbalances, while cautioning against overreliance on optimistic engagement narratives prevalent in some policy circles.[47] [43]

Public Engagement and Affiliations

Media Appearances and Commentary

Economy frequently provides commentary on U.S.-China relations, Chinese economic policies, and global strategic competition through television interviews and podcasts. On PBS NewsHour on April 8, 2025, she assessed China's anticipated responses to escalated trade measures under a potential second Trump administration, emphasizing Beijing's limited leverage amid domestic economic pressures.[49] In a CNBC appearance on October 25, 2024, she highlighted structural obstacles to China's shift from investment-led growth to consumption-driven models, including overcapacity in high-tech sectors and Xi Jinping's prioritization of state control over market reforms.[50] She has also featured on Fox News, offering analysis of trade dynamics, such as in a May 15, 2025, discussion concluding no clear victor emerges from prolonged U.S.-China tariff escalations due to mutual dependencies.[51] As host of the Hoover Institution's China Considered podcast, launched in 2023, Economy conducts in-depth interviews with experts on topics ranging from EU-China decoupling to U.S. strategic adaptations, with episodes including a July 24, 2025, conversation with David Shambaugh on the failures of U.S. engagement policies.[52] She has guested on other programs, such as the Foreign Affairs Interview on July 26, 2024, where she critiqued China's ideological push for a Beijing-led international order as incompatible with liberal democratic norms.[53] Additional podcast appearances include the Keep Talking episode on December 20, 2024, examining the Chinese Communist Party's historical resilience, and earlier discussions on platforms like China's Global Sharp Power in January 2021, detailing Xi's centralization of party-state power.[54][55] Her written commentary appears in outlets like Foreign Affairs, including the April 23, 2024, article "China's Alternative Order," which argues Xi's foreign policy seeks to supplant Western institutions with authoritarian alternatives, supported by evidence from Beijing's Belt and Road Initiative and United Nations influence campaigns.[43] Economy has contributed op-eds to The New York Times, The Washington Post, and The Wall Street Journal, often challenging optimistic narratives of Chinese liberalization by citing empirical indicators like declining private sector investment and rising censorship indices.[56] These pieces prioritize data from sources such as World Bank reports and Freedom House assessments over state media claims, reflecting her skepticism toward Beijing's official statistics amid documented manipulations.

Board Memberships and Advisory Roles

Economy serves on the board of managers of Swarthmore College, her alma mater, where she graduated in 1984.[9] She joined the board around 2014.[57] She is a member of the board of directors of the National Endowment for Democracy, appointed in January 2024.[58] The organization focuses on promoting democratic institutions globally, and Economy's involvement aligns with her expertise in international relations and authoritarian regimes.[59] Economy holds a seat on the board of the National Committee on U.S.-China Relations, a nonpartisan organization facilitating dialogue between the two nations.[1] Her election to this board occurred alongside other experts in U.S.-China policy.[60] In advisory capacities, she served as Senior Advisor for China to U.S. Secretary of Commerce Gina Raimondo from 2021 to 2023, providing expertise on China-related trade and economic security issues during the Biden administration.[1] This role was on leave from her Hoover Institution position.[59] Economy is a member of the Aspen Strategy Group, a bipartisan assembly of foreign policy leaders convened by the Aspen Institute to address national security challenges.[61] She also maintains membership in the Council on Foreign Relations, where she previously held senior fellowships focused on Asia studies.[1] Earlier advisory roles include vice chair of the World Economic Forum's Global Agenda Council on the Future of China from 2008 to 2014, and membership on its council for the United States from 2014 to 2016.[1] She previously served on the board of trustees of the Asia Foundation until around 2020.[62]

Personal Life

Family and Private Interests

Elizabeth Economy married David Michael Wah, an investment banker, on August 20, 1994, in a ceremony following her role as an associate fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations.[63] The couple resides in New York City with their three children.[9][4] Public information on Economy's private interests or hobbies remains limited, with no verifiable details available from biographical sources.
