Caracara (genus)

Caracara is a genus in the family Falconidae and the subfamily Polyborinae. It contains one extant species, the crested caracara, and one recently extinct species, the Guadalupe caracara (Caracara lutosa), as well as several extinct species known from the fossil record (see Taxonomy and fossil record). The crested caracara had in recent years been split into a northern form C. cheriway and a southern form C. plancus, but the South American Classification Committee of the American Ornithological Society has voted to again merge the two, retaining C. plancus as the crested caracara.[1] The IOC World Bird List has also merged them.[2]

Appearance

The crested caracara is distinguished by its long legs and medium size.[3] The birds can reach a length of 49â€“58 cm (19â€“23 in) from head to tail.[4] There are usually four points of identification for the crested caracara: strikingly white markings on the neck, rump, tips of both wings, and the tail.[5] Along with their medium length, the caracara also has a wingspan of 122â€“129 cm (48â€“51 in).[4] When flying, the caracara is often noted to have a pattern on their underside that looks like a cross.[6] The extinct Guadalupe caracara (C. lutosa) resembled the crested caracara but had less white on the upper breast, a barred belly, and a brown crest.[7]

Behavior

The behaviors of the crested caracara (C. plancus) are considered quite strange in relation to those of other falcons.[8] The bird is often seen walking on the ground in search of prey.[9] In addition to a preference for walking over flying, the birds also create close bonds with their mates, forming long-term monogamous pairs.[10][11] Crested caracaras are territorial creatures who are year-round residents of trees and land that they occupy. Their aggression is an extension of this, which is why they have been seen taking food from much larger creatures like vultures.[12][13] In flight, this bird is known for having very direct flight. It does not soar for leisure.[14] The extinct Guadalupe caracara (C. lutosa) exhibited different behaviors, being highly gregarious and often observed in flocks. It was bold and fearless around humans, engaging in cooperative foraging and aggressive attacks on prey such as young goats in groups.[15]

Taxonomy and fossil record

The crested caracara is the only extant species in Caracara, and was formerly separated into two species, the northern and southern crested caracaras. The modern range includes Cuba, South America, most of Central America and Mexico, just reaching the southernmost parts of the United States, including Florida, southern Arizona, and Southeast Texas.[10][16]
