D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai (born September 19, 2001) is a Canadian actor of Oji-Cree descent.[1][2] He is best known for portraying the Indigenous teenager Bear Smallhill in the FX on Hulu comedy-drama series Reservation Dogs (2021–2023).[1][3] Woon-A-Tai began his acting career with a recurring role in the CBC series Mercy Street and appeared in films such as Beans (2020).[2] His performance in Reservation Dogs earned him an Emmy nomination for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series in 2024, marking the first such nomination for an Indigenous North American actor in that category.[4][5] Upcoming projects include roles in the war film Warfare (2025) and the adaptation Caught Stealing.[1] At the 2024 Emmy Awards, Woon-A-Tai drew attention to violence against Indigenous women by wearing a red handprint over his mouth, symbolizing the Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women movement.[4][6]

Early life

Family background and upbringing

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai was born in Toronto, Ontario, Canada, to parents who were teenagers at the time of his birth.[7] His father is of Oji-Cree descent, an Indigenous First Nations group from northern Ontario, while his mother is of Guyanese origin.[7] [8] Woon-A-Tai's full name, D'Pharaoh Miskwaatez McKay Woon-A-Tai, reflects this mixed heritage, with "Miskwaatez" drawing from Oji-Cree linguistic roots.[8] He shares a fraternal twin brother, Mi'De Xxavier, making them the eldest siblings in the family.[9] Raised in an urban environment in Toronto, Woon-A-Tai grew up disconnected from traditional reservation life despite his Indigenous paternal lineage, which informed his later appreciation for urban Indigenous experiences.[10] [11] His family background includes additional European and Asian influences, with reported German ancestry alongside the Guyanese elements on his maternal side.[10] [12] This diverse upbringing in a multicultural city like Toronto exposed him early to a blend of cultural identities, though specific details on parental occupations or home life remain limited in public records.[7]

Indigenous heritage and early influences

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai's paternal lineage includes significant Oji-Cree ancestry, a First Nations group originating from northern Ontario and Manitoba regions of Canada.[13] His middle name, Miskwaatez, derives from the Ojibwe language and translates to "red belly turtle," reflecting elements of Anishinaabe cultural nomenclature common among Oji-Cree communities.[13] Maternal heritage traces to Guyana, incorporating Chinese-Guyanese and German elements, contributing to his mixed ethnic background.[13] Born on September 25, 2001, in Toronto, Ontario, Woon-A-Tai was raised in an urban environment distant from traditional Oji-Cree territories, yet maintained familial ties to Indigenous roots.[12] Early influences stemmed from cultural immersion facilitated by family and community. As a child, Woon-A-Tai pursued traditional Indigenous drumming extensively, viewing music as his primary passion before acting emerged.[14] This hands-on engagement with ceremonial practices fostered a personal connection to Oji-Cree expressive traditions, emphasizing rhythm and communal storytelling over Western entertainment pursuits initially.[14] During high school, self-directed reading of Indigenous historical texts profoundly shaped his worldview, prompting a reevaluation of colonial narratives and reinforcing identity amid urban disconnection from reserve life.[10] These experiences, combined with exposure to Oji-Cree oral histories via relatives, cultivated an early commitment to authentic representation, influencing his later career pivot from aspirations like military service or teaching toward performance roles highlighting Indigenous realities.[10][15]

Acting career

Initial roles and training

Woon-A-Tai received no formal acting training, having failed his high school theater class before transitioning to professional pursuits.[16] As a youth in Toronto, he experimented with filmmaking by creating low-budget videos alongside his twin brother, using their mother's camcorder and improvised props such as sticks to simulate rifles.[10] His professional debut occurred in 2018, portraying Chase Whaley in three episodes of the Family Channel series Holly Hobbie.[2][1] In 2019, he appeared as Tom Longboat, an Onondaga long-distance runner, across two episodes of Murdoch Mysteries season 12, depicting the historical figure's participation in events around 1912.[10][1] That same year, Woon-A-Tai guest-starred as Lucky in an episode of the CBBC/Netflix anthology Creeped Out and as Mikey in Tribal.[3][1] In 2020, he made his feature film debut as Hank in Beans, a drama directed by Tracey Deer and set during the 1990 Oka Crisis, which premiered at the Toronto International Film Festival.[10][1] These early television and film appearances, primarily in Canadian productions, marked his entry into the industry prior to larger roles.[3]

Breakthrough with Reservation Dogs

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai was cast as Bear Smallhill, the aspiring leader of a group of Indigenous teenagers navigating life on a rural Oklahoma reservation, in the FX on Hulu series Reservation Dogs, which premiered on August 9, 2021.[17] The comedy-drama, created by Sterlin Harjo and Taika Waititi, followed the Rez Dogs crew's schemes to leave their hometown, blending humor with themes of grief, identity, and community resilience among Native American youth.[18] Woon-A-Tai's portrayal of Bear—a character marked by impulsiveness, vulnerability, and growth—drew from his Oji-Cree heritage, allowing him to infuse the role with authentic emotional depth during the show's three seasons, which spanned 27 episodes and concluded on September 27, 2023.[19][17] The series marked Woon-A-Tai's breakthrough as a lead actor, transitioning him from minor roles in Canadian productions to international recognition for a performance that emphasized unfiltered Indigenous experiences without relying on stereotypes.[20] Critics praised the show's 99% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes, highlighting its innovative storytelling and Woon-A-Tai's ability to anchor the ensemble with raw charisma and relatability.[21] His work contributed to Reservation Dogs being lauded for advancing Native representation, as it featured an Indigenous-led cast and crew, fostering a collaborative environment that Woon-A-Tai credited with personal reconnection to his cultural roots.[22][18] Woon-A-Tai's performance culminated in his first Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series in 2024, underscoring the series' delayed but significant industry acknowledgment after three critically successful seasons.[23] The role's impact extended beyond acclaim, positioning him as a voice for Indigenous narratives and inspiring broader discussions on authentic casting in media, with Woon-A-Tai noting in interviews that the show demonstrated the viability of Native-centered stories in mainstream television.[20][24]

Subsequent projects and diversification

Following the end of Reservation Dogs in October 2023, Woon-A-Tai expanded into feature films, securing roles in projects spanning drama, horror, and action genres. In 2023, he appeared in Fitting In, a comedy-drama directed by Andrew McCarthy about a teenage girl discovering she has Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser syndrome, where he played a supporting role alongside Maddie Ziegler.[2] That same year, he starred in the horror film Only the Good Survive, a vampire thriller set in a Philadelphia rowhouse, marking his entry into genre filmmaking beyond Indigenous-themed narratives.[2] Woon-A-Tai's film work accelerated in 2024–2025 with higher-profile ensemble casts. He joined the cast of Warfare in March 2024, a war drama co-directed by Alex Garland and Iraq War veteran Ray Mendoza, based on Mendoza's memoir of the 2006 Battle of Ramadi, portraying a soldier in the intense, real-time depiction of combat.[25] He also featured in Hell of a Summer (2023, released 2025), a horror-comedy about camp counselors facing a masked killer, directed by Finn Wolfhard, showcasing his versatility in blending humor with suspense.[26] Additionally, he was cast in Darren Aronofsky's Caught Stealing (2025), a crime thriller adapted from Charlie Huston's novel about a former baseball player entangled in 1990s New York underworld schemes.[1] This shift to cinema diversified Woon-A-Tai's portfolio from television comedy to big-budget productions with established directors, emphasizing action-oriented and historical roles over the slice-of-life Indigenous stories of his breakthrough. In October 2025, he was announced for Andrew Haigh's A Long Winter, an undisclosed role in the director's follow-up to All of Us Strangers, further signaling his broadening appeal in dramatic arthouse fare.[27] These projects, often involving non-Indigenous ensembles, reflect a strategic move toward mainstream Hollywood vehicles while maintaining selective ties to authentic representation.[11]

Filmography

Television roles

Woon-A-Tai's early television appearances included a recurring role as Chase Whaley in season 1 of the Family Channel series Holly Hobbie in 2018.[3] In 2019, he played Tom Longboat, the first Indigenous winner of the Boston Marathon in 1907, in a two-episode storyline of Murdoch Mysteries season 12.[28] That year, he also guest-starred as Lucky in the Creeped Out episode "The Takedown," a CBBC anthology series episode involving a competitive hockey scenario haunted by a mysterious curse.[29] Woon-A-Tai appeared as Mikey in one episode of the APTN series Tribal in 2020, a drama centered on an Indigenous family's conflicts with a resource extraction corporation.[1] He gained prominence portraying Bear Smallhill, a directionless Oji-Cree teenager navigating grief and aspirations on a rural Oklahoma reservation, in all 26 episodes of the FX/Hulu comedy-drama Reservation Dogs across its three seasons from 2021 to 2023.[17] In 2022, he made a guest appearance in the TallBoyz episode "Papa You Are My Dad," a Canadian web series following Indigenous friends in Toronto's comedy scene.[30]

Film roles

Woon-A-Tai debuted in feature films with the role of Hank in Beans (2020), a drama directed by Tracey Deer that portrays the 1990 Oka Crisis from the perspective of a 12-year-old Mi'kmaq girl amid Mohawk protests in Quebec.[2] The film premiered at the Toronto International Film Festival on September 13, 2020, and earned acclaim for its Indigenous-led depiction of historical tensions.[2] In Fitting In (2023), a coming-of-age story about a teenager navigating a medical condition affecting her reproductive health, Woon-A-Tai played a supporting role opposite Maddie Ziegler and Emily Hampshire; the film premiered at South by Southwest on March 11, 2023.[31] Woon-A-Tai portrayed camp counselor Mike in the horror-comedy Hell of a Summer (2025), directed by Finn Wolfhard and Billy Bryk, where a group of teenage counselors faces a masked killer at a summer camp; production began in 2022, with the film releasing theatrically on April 4, 2025.[32] He took the lead role of Ray Mendoza, a real-life Navy SEAL communications specialist, in Warfare (2025), a gritty war drama co-directed by Mendoza and Alex Garland, recreating the 2006 Battle of Ramadi in Iraq based on firsthand accounts; Woon-A-Tai underwent military training for the part, and the film premiered on April 11, 2025.[25] In Darren Aronofsky's Caught Stealing (2025), an adaptation of Charlie Huston's novel about a former baseball player entangled in 1990s New York underworld crime, Woon-A-Tai played Dale, the high school best friend of protagonist Hank (Austin Butler); Sony Pictures released the film on August 27, 2025.[31] On October 14, 2025, Woon-A-Tai joined the cast of Andrew Haigh's drama A Long Winter in an undisclosed role, alongside Fred Hechinger and Kit Connor; production was underway in Alberta, Canada, as of late October 2025.[27]

Short films and other media

Woon-A-Tai's earliest credited acting role was in the short film Wenonah (2019), directed by Deanna Armenti, in which he played the character Luc, a young boy navigating family loss and cultural pressures alongside his sister.[33] The story centers on the siblings joining their father's fur trade expedition after their mother's death, culminating in efforts to avert the sister's arranged marriage, drawing on historical Indigenous experiences in Canada.[33] This project marked an initial foray into narrative shorts emphasizing Indigenous themes, predating his breakthrough in longer-form television.[1] No additional short films appear in verified credits, though Woon-A-Tai has participated in promotional shorts and interviews tied to larger productions, such as clips from Normal Ain't Normal (2021), a mini-series where he portrayed Wes in an episode exploring Indigenous land struggles.[34]

Other professional activities

Audiobook narration

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai expanded his professional portfolio into audiobook narration in 2025, lending his voice to literary adaptations that complement his on-screen performances. He contributed as a narrator to The El: A Novel by Theodore C. Van Alst Jr., a dystopian work featuring elements of political intrigue and interpersonal drama. The audiobook, produced by Random House Audio under Books on Tape, runs approximately 5 hours and 45 minutes and employs a cast of multiple narrators, including Woon-A-Tai alongside Michael Beck, Shaun Taylor-Corbett, and others, to deliver a dynamic, ensemble-style reading.[35] Released on August 12, 2025, the production highlights his ability to convey narrative tension through vocal performance, as evidenced in promotional excerpts where he reads key passages.[36] This endeavor marks an early foray into audio storytelling, distinct from his primary acting roles, and aligns with broader opportunities for performers to engage in voice-over work.

Modeling and public appearances

In August 2022, D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai signed with IMG Models, a leading agency, to expand his professional portfolio into fashion editorials, campaigns, and lifestyle endorsements.[37] The agency represents him in Los Angeles, leveraging his 5'10" height and established public profile to secure opportunities in the fashion industry.[38] Woon-A-Tai has featured in high-profile editorials, including a spread in VMAN magazine's Fall/Winter 2025 issue (VMAN 55), photographed by Isaac Anthony and styled with pieces from brands such as Coach, DSquared2, Lanvin, and Gucci.[39] This work highlights his transition into modeling, emphasizing bold, contemporary aesthetics aligned with his personal style.[40] He has also appeared on magazine covers and in fashion portfolios, contributing to his visibility in editorial circuits.[40] Beyond photoshoots, Woon-A-Tai has made notable public appearances at major fashion events. In June 2025, he attended the Louis Vuitton Menswear Spring/Summer 2026 show during Paris Fashion Week, front-row in a look from the brand's Pre-Fall 2025 collection, marking a statement of his growing influence in luxury fashion.[41] His stylist, Avo Yermagyan, has curated Indigenous-made and high-end ensembles for press tours and red carpets, blending cultural elements with designer wear to promote authentic representation in fashion.[42] These engagements underscore his role in diversifying modeling rosters with Indigenous perspectives.

Awards and recognition

Emmy nomination and industry acclaim

In 2024, D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai received a Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series for portraying Bear Smallhill in the FX series Reservation Dogs.[43] This marked the first such nomination for an Indigenous North American actor in a leading category at the Emmys, highlighting his nuanced depiction of a young Oji-Cree man's struggles with identity, family, and community on the Oklahoma rez.[4] Industry observers noted the performance's authenticity, drawn from Woon-A-Tai's own heritage, as a breakthrough in Indigenous-led storytelling that challenged stereotypes of Native characters as mere sidekicks or relics.[20] The nomination underscored broader acclaim for Woon-A-Tai's work in Reservation Dogs, a series lauded for its critical reception, including a 99% Rotten Tomatoes score across three seasons, with reviewers praising his raw emotional range in scenes blending humor, grief, and resilience.[18] He was also nominated for a Critics Choice Award in 2023 for Best Actor in a Comedy Series, reflecting peer recognition for elevating Indigenous narratives in mainstream television.[44] Woon-A-Tai credited the role with personal growth, stating it inspired him to "work harder" amid the historic visibility, though he emphasized the ensemble's collective impact over individual honors.[23] At the 76th Emmy Awards ceremony on September 15, 2024, Woon-A-Tai drew further attention by appearing with a red handprint over his mouth, symbolizing the Missing and Murdered Indigenous People (MMIP) crisis, which amplified industry discourse on his advocacy intertwined with professional acclaim.[45] Despite not winning—the award went to Jeremy Allen White for The Bear—the nod positioned Woon-A-Tai as a rising talent, with outlets like The Hollywood Reporter citing his performance as pivotal to the show's role in fostering Native-led productions.[20]

Other honors

Woon-A-Tai shared in the win for Best Ensemble Cast in a New Scripted Series at the 2022 Film Independent Spirit Awards for Reservation Dogs.[44][46] He received consecutive nominations for Best Actor in a Comedy Series at the Critics Choice Television Awards, first in 2023 and again in 2024, both for his portrayal of Bear Smallhill in Reservation Dogs.[44][47] The cast of Reservation Dogs, including Woon-A-Tai, shared in a Peabody Award nomination in 2023 for the series' overall entertainment value.[44][27] In 2025, Woon-A-Tai was named to Forbes' 30 Under 30 list in the Hollywood & Entertainment category, recognizing emerging talents under age 30 for their contributions to the industry.[47]

Public advocacy and views

Indigenous rights activism

On September 15, 2024, during the red carpet arrivals for the 76th Primetime Emmy Awards, D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai appeared with a red handprint painted across his mouth, a symbol associated with the Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women (MMIW) and Missing and Murdered Indigenous People (MMIP) movements.[48][6][4] This gesture highlighted the disproportionate rates of violence faced by Indigenous women and people, where statistics indicate Indigenous women are murdered at rates up to 10 times the national average in the United States and similarly elevated in Canada.[49] Woon-A-Tai, who is Oji-Cree from the Norway House Cree Nation, explained the action as a call for awareness about the "nationwide scourge" of these cases, many of which remain unsolved due to systemic underreporting and jurisdictional issues on reservations.[6][4] The red handprint motif originates from campaigns protesting the erasure of Indigenous victims in media and law enforcement, representing blood on the mouth to signify silenced cries for justice.[49] Woon-A-Tai's appearance marked one of the highest-profile uses of the symbol at a major awards event, amplifying discussions on federal responses like the U.S. Savanna's Act and Canada's National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls, which documented over 1,200 cases in Canada alone as of 2019.[6][4] He has described his intent as leveraging visibility from his Emmy nomination for Reservation Dogs to push for policy attention and community support, rather than performative allyship.[48] Beyond this event, Woon-A-Tai engages in advocacy through public speaking on Indigenous justice and representation, emphasizing storytelling as a tool to challenge historical marginalization and foster empathy for ongoing rights issues like land sovereignty and cultural preservation.[12] In interviews, he has linked authentic Indigenous narratives in media to broader societal shifts, arguing that visibility reduces stereotypes that perpetuate violence and neglect.[20][11] His efforts align with critiques of institutional failures in addressing Indigenous-specific crises, prioritizing empirical data on violence rates over generalized narratives.[4]

Perspectives on Hollywood and representation

D'Pharaoh Woon-A-Tai has described Reservation Dogs as a landmark in Hollywood for Indigenous representation, marking the first series to center Indigenous comedy with a fully Native writers' room, directors, and principal cast, which he views as long overdue.[11] He credits the show's success to Indigenous control over its narrative, stating, "It was very important that we left on our own foot rather than someone telling us how to do it," and argues that prior Native visibility in film suffered from "very bad representation" dominated by non-Native perspectives.[11] Woon-A-Tai maintains that projects depicting Native people should require Native directors, writers, and casting directors to avoid misrepresentation, asserting, "I think we’re pushing to a time when we don’t need anybody to tell our story for us."[11] He criticizes traditional Hollywood depictions of Indigenous people as perpetuating dehumanizing stereotypes, such as the "native Indian on horseback with long hair and war paint getting shot at by John Wayne," which fail to portray Natives as multifaceted and humorous individuals encountered in everyday life.[28] In contrast, Woon-A-Tai praises Reservation Dogs for allowing Indigenous creators to present authentic stories, declaring it "a project that we can really finally be proud about because we told it rather than other people telling it for us."[28] He anticipates that such efforts, alongside series like Rutherford Falls and Rez Ball, will normalize Indigenous-led content in U.S. television, shifting from rarity to standard.[50] Woon-A-Tai also addresses economic barriers, expressing a direct aim to secure better compensation for Native actors—"My goal is, honestly, to get Natives paid"—while positioning himself as a mentor to young Indigenous entrants, warning of industry obstacles and emphasizing proper navigation to foster hope among reservation youth.[11][28] He acknowledges that accolades like Emmy nominations, while validating, do not resolve underlying systemic issues in representation or community challenges.[28]
