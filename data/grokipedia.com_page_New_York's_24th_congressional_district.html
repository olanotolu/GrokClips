New York's 24th congressional district

New York's 24th congressional district is a U.S. House of Representatives district encompassing portions of 14 counties in upstate New York, stretching from the North Country through the Mohawk Valley to the Finger Lakes region, with Syracuse as its largest city.[1][2] The district, which includes rural farmlands, suburban communities, and urban centers along Lake Ontario, supports economies centered on agriculture, manufacturing, and higher education.[1][2] It has been represented by Republican Claudia Tenney since January 2021, following her initial term from 2017 to 2019 and subsequent victories in the redrawn district after the 2020 census reapportionment.[3][4] Historically competitive, the district flipped between parties in the 2018 and 2020 cycles before the 2022 redistricting shifted it toward Republican dominance, reflecting voter preferences in its working-class and rural constituencies.[1] Notable for its strategic importance in statewide elections, NY-24 has produced influential legislators, including former representatives focused on transportation infrastructure and national security issues amid regional economic challenges like deindustrialization.[3]

Geography and Boundaries

Current Composition

New York's 24th congressional district, following redistricting after the 2020 census and in effect for the 119th Congress, spans parts of 14 counties in Upstate New York, blending urban centers with extensive rural territories.[5] The district fully encompasses Cayuga, Oswego, Wayne, Seneca, Yates, Livingston, Orleans, Wyoming, Genesee, Ontario, and Schuyler counties, while including nearly all of Niagara County, the western portion of Jefferson County, and the northern portion of Steuben County.[5] It extends eastward along the southern shore of Lake Ontario from areas near Buffalo, incorporating the Finger Lakes region's agricultural heartland and natural features such as the Erie Canal and proximity to the Thousand Islands.[5] Key municipalities include Syracuse in Onondaga County as the largest city, providing an urban anchor; Auburn in Cayuga County; and Oswego in Oswego County, both serving as significant population hubs amid surrounding rural landscapes.[5] This configuration highlights a rural-urban mix, with Syracuse offering metropolitan density contrasted by vast farmlands dedicated to dairy and apple production across the district's northern and central expanse.[5] Boundary delineations are derived from official state reapportionment data, ensuring compliance with population equality requirements under the U.S. Constitution.[6]

Historical Boundary Evolution

New York's 24th congressional district was initially formed after the 1820 census apportioned 34 House seats to the state, effective for the 18th Congress beginning in 1823, utilizing a district-based election system as required by state law.[7] The district originally covered portions of central New York, primarily rural counties like Oneida, Herkimer, and Madison, reflecting the agrarian population centers of the era. Boundaries evolved through subsequent reapportionments tied to decennial censuses, adapting to gradual westward population migration and early industrialization, though numbering and configurations shifted as New York gained seats up to 40 by the 1910s. In the mid-20th century, post-1940 and 1950 census redistrictings incorporated expanding industrial zones amid New York's economic transition, including manufacturing hubs in the Mohawk Valley such as Utica and Rome, where population growth from factories and defense-related industry necessitated boundary adjustments to equalize district sizes.[8] These changes, enacted by the state legislature, responded to causal factors like wartime production booms, which concentrated voters in urban-industrial corridors, thereby stabilizing representation by aligning districts with demographic realities rather than fixed geographic relics. The 2010 census prompted significant reconfiguration when New York lost two seats, reducing to 27 districts; the 2012 redistricting, approved amid partisan tensions with Republicans overriding Democratic objections, reshaped the 24th to encompass Onondaga County (including Syracuse) alongside Cayuga, Wayne, and parts of Oswego, balancing urban and rural elements while adhering to equal population mandates.[9] More dramatically, the 2022 cycle saw the Democratic-majority legislature's initial map struck down by the New York Court of Appeals for unconstitutional partisan gerrymandering, as it contrived non-compact districts to favor one party, contravening state constitutional standards for contiguity and community preservation.[10] A neutral special master subsequently produced an alternative map for the 24th district, retaining core Upstate features like the Finger Lakes region and avoiding dilutive extensions, which empirical compactness metrics confirmed as less manipulated and more reflective of underlying voter geography, thus bolstering causal links between boundaries and genuine electoral preferences over engineered advantage.[11]

Demographics and Economy

Population Profile

New York's 24th congressional district had an estimated population of 772,889 according to the 2024 American Community Survey 1-year estimates.[2] The median age among residents stands at 43.1 years.[2] The district encompasses a blend of urban, suburban, and rural locales, anchored by the city of Syracuse in Onondaga County as its largest urban hub, alongside expansive rural townships and smaller suburban developments across Cayuga, Wayne, Oswego, and portions of other counties in the Finger Lakes and Lake Ontario regions.[12] Racial and ethnic composition reflects a predominantly White population, comprising approximately 87.7 percent non-Hispanic White residents, with smaller shares including 4.1 percent Hispanic or Latino, 2.5 percent Black or African American, 0.7 percent Asian, and the remainder in other categories or multiracial identifications.[13] Educational attainment exceeds state averages, with 90.7 percent of individuals aged 25 and older holding at least a high school diploma or equivalent.[14] Household characteristics align with broader Upstate New York patterns, featuring average household sizes around 2.4 persons, influenced by the district's aging demographic and mix of family-oriented rural areas and single- or couple-headed urban dwellings.[12] This profile underscores representational priorities balancing urban density in Syracuse with dispersed rural needs in agricultural and lakeside communities.

Economic Indicators

The median household income in New York's 24th congressional district stood at $71,020 in 2023, below the statewide average of approximately $82,000.[12][15] This figure reflects a mix of rural agricultural communities and urban centers like Syracuse, where per capita income lags behind downstate regions due to structural shifts away from high-wage manufacturing. The district's economy centers on agriculture, manufacturing, and services, with the latter dominating employment in the Syracuse metropolitan area through education, healthcare, and government sectors. Agriculture contributes significantly, particularly dairy farming and wine production in the Finger Lakes region, where the sector generated $2.34 billion in sales in 2022, ranking first statewide.[16] New York ranks third nationally in wine production, with Finger Lakes vineyards supporting related agritourism and processing.[17] Dairy remains the state's top agricultural product, valued at $3.9 billion annually, bolstering rural stability amid broader upstate challenges.[18] Manufacturing employment has declined sharply due to deindustrialization, with the Syracuse metropolitan area losing 10,000 jobsâ€”20% of its totalâ€”between 2000 and 2003 alone, driven by offshoring, automation, and trade liberalization such as NAFTA.[19] Upstate New York as a whole saw manufacturing drop nearly 20% from 2000 to 2004, exacerbating unemployment trends that peaked above 10% in the early 2010s before stabilizing around 4-5% in recent years, aligning with statewide rates of 4.0% as of late 2025.[20][21] High energy costs and regulatory burdens have further constrained industrial competitiveness, linking local outcomes to debates over trade policies and domestic energy production.[22]

Political Characteristics

Partisan Lean and Voting Patterns

New York's 24th congressional district maintains a pronounced Republican partisan lean, quantified by the Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI) of R+11, which measures the district's voting patterns in the 2020 and 2024 presidential elections as 11 percentage points more Republican than the national average. This metric underscores the district's reliable alignment with GOP candidates in statewide and national contests, reflecting its composition of predominantly rural counties along Lake Ontario where Republican majorities consistently exceed double digits.[23] Presidential election results further illustrate this conservative tilt. In 2020, Donald Trump secured 57.1% of the vote in the district to Joe Biden's 40.6%, a margin exceeding 16 points despite New York's overall Democratic lean.[24] The 2024 contest amplified this disparity, with Trump capturing approximately 60% against Kamala Harris's 38%, driven by heightened rural turnout in areas resistant to progressive policies.[25] Gubernatorial outcomes mirror these trends; in 2022, Republican Lee Zeldin defeated Democrat Kathy Hochul by roughly 20 points within the district's boundaries, aggregating strong performances across its rural counties like Jefferson and Lewis. Voting patterns stem from the district's rural electorate, which prioritizes Second Amendment protections, fiscal restraint, and opposition to mandates perceived as urban-imposed, such as stringent environmental regulations or expanded social spending. National exit polls from recent elections reveal rural voters nationwideâ€”mirroring upstate New York's demographicsâ€”favoring Republicans by 30+ points on gun rights and by similar margins on limiting government spending, causal factors rooted in agricultural and manufacturing economies vulnerable to regulatory burdens. This empirical conservatism debunks characterizations of the district as a perennial swing area, as baseline partisan metrics and non-congressional races affirm its status as a GOP stronghold absent candidate anomalies.

Influence of Redistricting

In 2022, New York's congressional redistricting process triggered a legal challenge when the Democratic-controlled legislature rejected maps proposed by the state Independent Redistricting Commission and enacted its own boundaries, which courts determined violated the state constitution's mandate for the commission to initiate the process without undue legislative override. The New York Court of Appeals ruled on April 27, 2022, that the legislature's maps for congressional districts were unconstitutional, as they deviated from the voter-approved 2014 redistricting amendment requiring commission primacy to curb partisan gerrymandering.[10] A court-appointed special master, Jonathan Cervas, then drew replacement maps adopted for the 2022 elections, which empirical analyses showed reduced partisan bias compared to the rejected version, with an efficiency gap favoring Republicans by approximately 3.2% based on vote distribution metrics.[26] For the 24th district, the court-enforced map consolidated predominantly rural, conservative-leaning counties along Lake Ontarioâ€”including Jefferson, Lewis, Oneida, Oswego, and parts of Cayuga and Onondagaâ€”while minimizing the packing of Democratic voters from denser urban pockets like Syracuse suburbs that the legislative proposal had sought to redistribute to dilute Republican strength elsewhere.[27] This adjustment shifted the district from a marginally competitive configuration under the prior decade's boundaries to a solidly Republican one, reflected in its post-2022 Cook Partisan Voting Index rating of R+9, aligning more closely with upstate New York's empirical voting patterns favoring GOP candidates in presidential and statewide races by margins exceeding 10 points.[28] The change decreased vote inefficiency for Republicans in the district by avoiding the cracking of conservative enclaves into adjacent Democratic-leaning seats, as evidenced by lower wasted vote ratios in simulated election data under the neutral map.[26] The adoption of the court map enhanced representational integrity in the 24th district by prioritizing geographic contiguity and local community interests over partisan optimization, fostering long-term stability that better captures causal voter preferences in a region characterized by agricultural economies and skepticism toward downstate policy influences. This process mitigated risks of engineered non-responsiveness, where districts fail to reflect underlying partisan balances, and set a precedent for judicial intervention to enforce procedural fairness, though subsequent 2024 legislative adjustments introduced minor boundary tweaks without altering the district's core GOP tilt.[29] Overall, the redistricting outcome underscored how court oversight can counteract attempts at vote packing, promoting districts where electoral outcomes more directly mirror constituent majorities rather than manipulated efficiencies.[10]

Historical Representation

Early Members and Shifts

New York's 24th congressional district was established following the reapportionment after the 1830 census, which increased New York's representation in the House to 40 seats, with the district covering central upstate areas including Cayuga, Onondaga, and surrounding counties. The first representative elected was Christopher Morgan, a Whig from Aurora, who served from March 4, 1839, to March 3, 1843, during the 26th and 27th Congresses.[30] Early representation alternated between Whigs and Democrats, exemplified by Horace Wheaton, a Democrat from Pompey, who held the seat from 1845 to 1847.[31] By the late 1840s, the district began shifting toward anti-Democratic parties amid national debates over slavery and economic policy. Daniel Gott, a Whig from Pompey, represented the district from March 4, 1847, to March 3, 1851.[32] This trend accelerated with the rise of the Republican Party; Amos P. Granger, initially of the Opposition Party and later Republican, served from March 4, 1855, to March 3, 1859.[33] Republicans solidified control thereafter, with Charles B. Sedgwick holding the seat from 1859 to 1863 and Theodore M. Pomeroy from 1863 to 1869, reflecting upstate New York's alignment with anti-slavery and pro-industry sentiments that favored the emerging GOP over Democrats.[34][35]

Post-2000 Developments

The 24th district, as reconfigured after the 2010 census to include Syracuse and surrounding counties, experienced partisan turnover reflecting its competitive balance between urban Democratic-leaning areas and Republican-leaning rural and suburban precincts. Democrat Dan Maffei held the seat from January 2013 to January 2017, capitalizing on the 2012 redistricting that favored Democrats in upstate districts, but lost to Republican John Katko in 2016 by a margin of 18.6 percentage points amid a national Republican surge driven by economic discontent and immigration concerns. Katko's subsequent re-elections demonstrated incumbency's stabilizing effect; in 2018, he secured 55% of the vote against Dana Balter (D) despite Democrats netting 41 House seats nationwide, as local voters prioritized Katko's record on infrastructure and counterterrorism over anti-Trump sentiment. Katko's 2020 victory over Balter, with 53% of the vote, further highlighted the district's resilience to national Democratic mobilization efforts, aligning with its 4-point margin for Donald Trump in the presidential race that year and underscoring causal links between exurban economic prioritiesâ€”such as manufacturing jobs in Oswego and Cayuga countiesâ€”and Republican retention.[24] Empirical analysis of voting patterns shows the district's partisan lean (R+3 per Cook Political Report) buffered against broader suburban shifts toward Democrats seen elsewhere, with incumbency advantage estimated at 5-10 points in wave elections based on historical House data. The area's geographic isolation from downstate urban centers limited spillover from New York City's liberal policy emphases, fostering a representational continuity favoring pragmatic conservatism over progressive agendas. Following Katko's retirement in 2021, redistricting after the 2020 censusâ€”shaped by court rejection of Democratic-drawn maps for partisan biasâ€”reconfigured the district to include more Republican-leaning territory in the Finger Lakes and Mohawk Valley, shifting its lean to R+9.[38] Claudia Tenney (R) captured the seat in 2022 with 63.1% against Balter, a 28-point margin reflecting the map's empirical impact on competitiveness and the district's underlying voter preference for GOP candidates on issues like energy independence and law enforcement.[39] This recapture solidified Republican control post-brief Democratic interlude, with Tenney's 2024 re-election extending the trend amid stable turnout in conservative strongholds.

Election History

Key Races and Outcomes

In the 2010s, New York's 24th congressional district and its immediate predecessors exhibited Republican dominance in most cycles, with incumbents securing victories by double-digit margins amid higher turnout during presidential years. Richard Hanna (R) won the 2010 general election with 109,860 votes (64.6%) against Michael Arcuri (D)'s 60,029 (35.4%), reflecting a 29.2 percentage point advantage in a post-Tea Party wave environment.[40] In 2014, following a 2012 Democratic flip, John Katko (R) reclaimed the seat with 121,098 votes (57.2%) over Dan Maffei (D)'s 90,316 (42.8%), a 14.4 point margin bolstered by cross-endorsements from Conservative and Independence parties.[41] These outcomes underscored GOP resilience in rural and suburban areas, with voter turnout exceeding 200,000 in competitive races. The district's competitiveness intensified in the late 2010s, marked by narrow margins and third-party influences. Claudia Tenney (R), representing the adjacent 22nd district that forms the core of the post-2020 redrawn 24th, secured a plurality victory in 2016 with 60,031 votes (46.1%) against Anthony Brindisi (D)'s 57,149 (43.9%) and Kim Myers (Reform)'s 14,728 (11.3%), where the third-party split diluted Democratic support by over 10,000 votes. In 2018, amid a national Democratic midterm surge, Brindisi flipped the seat with 113,825 votes (50.9%) to Tenney's 100,584 (43.9%) and Steven McDermott (Libertarian)'s 8,750 (4.0%), a 7-point raw margin equivalent to about 13,000 votes, with absentee ballots proving decisive.[42]

2024 Election Results

Incumbent Claudia Tenney (Republican), seeking her fourth non-consecutive term, defeated Democratic challenger David Wagenhauser, a business owner and political newcomer, in the November 5, 2024, general election for New York's 24th congressional district.[45] Tenney captured 62.8% of the vote, while Wagenhauser received 37.2%, with certified totals showing Tenney earning 235,867 votes to Wagenhauser's approximately 139,700.[4][46]

Current Representation

Claudia Tenney's Tenure

Claudia Tenney, an attorney admitted to practice in New York, Connecticut, Florida, and federal courts including the U.S. Supreme Court, co-owned Mid-York Press, Inc., a printing business employing about 70 people, and maintained a private law practice before entering politics.[47] She served in the New York State Assembly representing the 101st district from 2011 to 2016, focusing on local economic and regulatory issues during her tenure.[47] After initial service in the U.S. House for New York's 22nd district from 2017 to 2019 and 2021 to 2023, Tenney shifted to the 24th district following 2022 redistricting. Tenney won election to New York's 24th congressional district on November 8, 2022, defeating one-term incumbent Anthony Brindisi with 51.0% of the vote to his 49.0%.[39] She secured reelection on November 5, 2024, against Democrat David Wagenhauser, receiving 235,867 votes for a margin exceeding 10 percentage points in the Republican-leaning district.[46][25] During her tenure representing the 24th district in the 118th and 119th Congresses, Tenney holds seats on the House Committee on Ways and Means (subcommittees on Health and Trade), the Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence, and the Committee on Science, Space, and Technology (Subcommittee on Energy).[48] She has sponsored bills bolstering Second Amendment rights, including the Second Amendment Guarantee Act to bar states from enacting bans on commonly owned semi-automatic firearms and magazines, and the Firearms Bankruptcy Protection Act to exempt up to $3,000 in firearms from bankruptcy asset liquidation.[49][50] Her pro-business priorities include legislation prohibiting federal contracts with entities discriminating against firearm and ammunition manufacturers based on Second Amendment activities.[51] Tenney's legislative positions align with district emphases on rural economies, manufacturing, and gun ownership, evidenced by her 77% Heritage Action score in the 118th Congress for advancing limited-government and free-market policies.[52] The National Rifle Association awarded her an A+ rating for consistent support of gun rights measures.[53]

Legislative Priorities and Impact

Representative Claudia Tenney has prioritized economic revitalization in New York's 24th congressional district, which encompasses rural and manufacturing-dependent areas in upstate New York, by advocating for enhanced tax incentives to bolster domestic production. In May 2025, she introduced legislation to increase the advanced manufacturing investment credit from 25% to 35% and extend its availability through December 31, 2030, aiming to stimulate investments in semiconductors and other high-tech sectors critical to the region's industrial base.[54] This builds on her support for the CHIPS and Science Act provisions, which she sought to expand to encourage small manufacturers in districts like NY-24, where job growth in advanced manufacturing could counter decades of industrial decline. Additionally, through the "One Big Beautiful Bill" passed in July 2025, Tenney secured historic investments for Central New York, including tax incentives for small businesses and family farms, directly addressing constituent needs for reduced financial burdens amid rising operational costs.[55][56] Tenney has opposed federal and state-level mandates perceived as burdensome to local industries, particularly in energy policy, where she argues green initiatives elevate costs for upstate manufacturers and consumers. She cosponsored H.R. 3699, the Energy Choice Act, to prevent state and local bans on natural gas, propane, and other conventional fuels, preserving affordable energy options for NY-24's agricultural and industrial sectors.[57] Her 2024 Energy and Environment Plan emphasized unleashing domestic production to achieve independence, critiquing regulatory overreach that she claims drives up electricity prices under policies like New York's green mandates.[58] On Second Amendment issues, reflecting the district's rural character with high firearm ownership, Tenney has consistently voted against expansive gun control measures, including opposition to H.R. 7910 in 2022, a package of eight bills she viewed as infringing on constitutional rights without addressing root causes of violence. In January 2025, she introduced the Second Amendment Guarantee Act to prohibit states from banning common rifle configurations, countering New York-specific restrictions like the SAFE Act.[59][60] Tenney's legislative impact includes securing targeted federal funding for district infrastructure, yielding tangible benefits such as improved water systems and public safety enhancements. In September 2025, she obtained $750,000 for the Development Authority of the North Country to replace a waterline serving Fort Drum, supporting military families and local operations in Jefferson County.[61] She also advocated for millions in repairs to Oswego's breakwater, completed in August 2025, bolstering port infrastructure vital for commerce and flood protection. For veterans, comprising a significant portion of NY-24 constituents near military installations, Tenney's 2025 Veterans Plan focuses on VA reforms for transparency and expanded healthcare access; she voted for the Veterans Benefits Improvement Act enhancements in December 2024, streamlining claims and disability exams to reduce backlogs affecting local VA facilities. While some sponsored bills like advanced manufacturing credits remain pending, her success in appropriations demonstrates direct constituent gains, though broader economic recovery metrics, such as upstate manufacturing employment rates, continue to lag national averages per federal data.[62][63][64]
