It Must Be Heaven

It Must Be Heaven is a 2019 internationally co-produced comedy-drama film written, directed, and starring Palestinian filmmaker Elia Suleiman as a semi-autobiographical version of himself.[1] The film follows Suleiman's character as he leaves his native Nazareth in Palestine seeking a new homeland abroad, only to encounter surreal absurdities and ironic parallels to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict in Paris and New York City, blending silent visual comedy with themes of exile, identity, and belonging.[2][3] Premiering at the 2019 Cannes Film Festival where it competed for the Palme d'Or and received a Jury Special Mention alongside the FIPRESCI Prize, the film marked Suleiman's return to feature directing after a decade, drawing comparisons to the deadpan styles of Jacques Tati and Buster Keaton through its minimal dialogue and observational humor.[2][4] Produced by entities including Nazira Films (Palestine), Possibles Media (France), and others, it features supporting performances by Tarik Kopty, Kais Nashif, and FranÃ§ois Girard but centers on Suleiman's stoic, Chaplinesque protagonist navigating militarized landscapes, cultural clashes, and futile quests for peace.[1][5] Critically acclaimed for its poignant satire on displacementâ€”earning a 96% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from 52 reviews praising its blend of absurdity and melancholyâ€”the film also won Best Director at the 2020 Critics Awards for Arab Films, though its box office performance remained modest, reflecting limited commercial appeal for arthouse cinema addressing geopolitical tensions.[3][4] No major controversies arose from its release, though its unflinching portrayal of occupation checkpoints, settler encroachments, and Western obliviousness to Palestinian realities elicited varied interpretations, with some viewing it as a universal comedy of errors rather than partisan advocacy.[6][7]

Production

Development and Financing

Elia Suleiman, a Palestinian filmmaker known for his earlier works such as Chronicle of a Disappearance (1996) and Divine Intervention (2002), which won the Jury Prize at Cannes, wrote, directed, and starred in It Must Be Heaven as an extension of his signature style of silent, observational satire.[8][9] The project emerged after a decade-long hiatus from feature films, following The Time That Remains (2009), during which Suleiman drew from his personal experiences of displacement and exile from Nazareth.[1] Development began in the mid-2010s, with early support from the Arab Fund for Arts and Culture (AFAC), which awarded a grant in 2016 specifically for research and development phases of the project.[10] Suleiman refined the script to emphasize visual storytelling over dialogue, aiming to critique geopolitical absurdities through deadpan humor while navigating the constraints of limited verbal exposition, a technique consistent with his prior films to evade censorship and broaden appeal amid regional sensitivities.[8] This approach addressed production hurdles inherent to Palestinian cinema, where political content often deters Western backers wary of controversy over the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, compelling Suleiman to balance universal themes with pointed satire on occupation and identity without alienating potential funders.[11] Financing involved a multinational co-production led by Rectangle Productions (France), with contributions from Nazira Films (Palestine), Pallas Film (Germany), and Possibles MÃ©dia (Canada), supplemented by Doha Film Institute (Qatar) co-financing that facilitated completion ahead of its 2019 Cannes premiere.[8][9][12] Additional involvement from Zeyno Film (Turkey) and Arte underscored the film's reliance on diverse Arab, European, and North American sources to overcome funding gaps, as international investors frequently hesitate on projects perceived as risking diplomatic backlash in the Middle East context.[13] This structure highlighted systemic challenges for filmmakers from conflict zones, where state-backed entities like Qatar's DFI provide critical support absent from more risk-averse Western institutions.[12]

Filming Locations and Challenges

Principal photography for It Must Be Heaven took place from May 6 to November 4, 2018.[14] Shooting occurred in Nazareth, Israelâ€”the director Elia Suleiman's hometownâ€”to establish the film's opening portrayal of local life; Paris, France, including specific scenes on Rue Montorgueil; and New York, United States, to reflect the protagonist's search for alternative homelands.[14][15][16] Additional location work included Paris suburbs such as Issy-les-Moulineaux in August 2018 and final scenes in Montreal, Canada, in October 2018.[17][18] The international co-production structure, spanning France, Germany, Canada, Turkey, Qatar, and Palestinian territories, required coordinating logistics across borders, including travel, equipment transport, and location permits in politically sensitive areas like Nazareth.[19] Suleiman employed non-professional actors alongside documentary-style footage to capture authentic absurdities, demanding meticulous direction to maintain the film's sparse dialogue and visual emphasis for broader accessibility.[20]

Synopsis

It Must Be Heaven follows Palestinian filmmaker Elia Suleiman, who portrays a largely silent, observant version of himself, as he departs his hometown of Nazareth in search of an alternative homeland amid the absurdities of daily life under occupation.[8][9] The narrative unfolds through a series of vignette-style episodes highlighting mundane yet surreal encounters, beginning with vignettes in Nazareth depicting neighborly disputes, thefts, and military patrols that underscore the pervasive tensions of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.[8][9] Suleiman's journey takes him to Paris, where he experiences a mix of cultural clashes and ironic parallels, including overly militarized police responses and expatriate Palestinian dancers performing amid indifferent audiences, evoking the inescapability of his origins.[8] In New York, similar motifs recur through encounters with aggressive street behavior, funding rejections for his film due to its perceived lack of violence, and celebratory scenes juxtaposed against underlying strife, reinforcing themes of global displacement and the universality of conflict.[9][8] The film employs Suleiman's signature deadpan, mostly dialogue-free style to blend comedy with poignant observation, culminating in a return to Nazareth where the cycle of absurdity persists.[9]

Artistic Elements

Directorial Style and Techniques

Elia Suleiman's directorial approach in It Must Be Heaven draws heavily from silent comedy traditions, employing deadpan humor where the director's alter-ego protagonist remains largely mute, observing escalating absurdities through exaggerated physical actions and situational irony rather than verbal exchange. This style echoes Buster Keaton's stoic, stone-faced reactions amid chaos, with Suleiman positioning himself as a passive witness in meticulously composed frames that prioritize visual punchlines over narrative exposition.[24][25] The absence of dialogue underscores a commitment to universality, allowing gags rooted in body language and environmental interplay to transcend linguistic barriers, much like the wordless vignettes of Jacques Tati's Playtime.[26] Cinematographer Sofian El Fani's contributions emphasize wide, static shots in a widescreen format (aspect ratio approximately 2.39:1), capturing symmetrical compositions that heighten the film's observational detachment and reveal the surreal within mundane Palestinian village life, urban Paris, and New York streets.[9][27] Long takes and restrained editing further this technique, forgoing rapid cuts to let actions unfold in real time, thereby amplifying the comedic buildup through sustained tension and precise spatial choreography.[16] The film was shot digitally, facilitating these extended sequences without the constraints of film stock limitations.[28] Auditory elements complement the visuals with sparse, ironic scoring that punctuates key moments, often juxtaposing serene or traditional motifs against discordant events to underscore the film's wry tone, while diegetic sounds like footsteps or ambient noise drive the rhythm of silent sequences.[29] This minimalistic sound design reinforces the deadpan aesthetic, avoiding overt emotional cues in favor of subtle amplification of visual absurdity.

Thematic Analysis

The film explores the theme of exile through the protagonist's futile quest for a new homeland, departing Nazareth only to encounter parallel absurdities in Paris and New York, underscoring human displacement as a condition unbound by geography.[30] Suleiman employs silent, deadpan observation to depict this rootlessness, avoiding narrative exposition in favor of visual vignettes that highlight the protagonist's isolation amid bustling urban life.[31] This approach draws from Buster Keaton-esque physical comedy, emphasizing existential detachment rather than overt emotional appeals.[32] Central to the thematic framework is a satire on the "Palestinization" of global society, portraying militarism, surveillance, and bureaucratic overreach as pervasive rather than exceptional to any one region. Suleiman illustrates this universality by juxtaposing local disputes in Nazarethâ€”such as land encroachments echoing real historical tensions over olive groves and propertyâ€”with exaggerated scenes of armed patrols in France and aggressive policing in the United States, suggesting these dynamics reflect broader human tendencies toward control and division.[30] In interviews, Suleiman has articulated this as "Palestinisation is everywhere," critiquing how identity-based conflicts and security apparatuses normalize absurdity worldwide.[30] The narrative critiques the limitations of identity politics and emigration as solutions to displacement, showing the protagonist's attempts to assimilate or seek solidarity repeatedly thwarted by cultural incomprehension and performative solidarity. Western settings reveal hypocrisy in refugee reception, where overt displays of militancy abroad contrast with superficial hospitality at home, as seen in failed funding pitches for a Palestinian film amid oblivious revelry.[33] This extends to a broader commentary on failed transnational belonging, where the Palestinian experience mirrors universal failures of cosmopolitanism, grounded in empirical observations of migration patterns without romanticizing victimhood.[1] Balancing critique with resilience, the film eschews direct portrayals of suffering in favor of humor derived from incongruity, portraying characters' stoic enduranceâ€”termed sumud in Palestinian discourseâ€”as a form of quiet defiance against chaos.[31] Suleiman's use of exaggerated, near-surreal vignettes, such as synchronized neighborhood dances amid tension or olive tree plantings symbolizing rootedness, evokes laughter from the very mechanisms of oppression, affirming human adaptability through absurdity rather than despair.[34] This method aligns with Suleiman's stated rejection of preconceptions about Palestinian somberness, prioritizing comedic observation to reveal causal patterns in displacement and power.[34]

Release and Distribution

Festival Premieres

It Must Be Heaven had its world premiere at the 2019 Cannes Film Festival on May 21, where it competed in the main competition for the Palme d'Or.[9] The film, directed and starring Elia Suleiman, drew attention for its satirical portrayal of displacement, with Suleiman portraying a silent observer navigating absurdities from Nazareth to Paris and New York.[35] In press interactions, Suleiman emphasized themes of exile, noting the film's exploration of universal tensions mirroring Palestinian experiences amid global migration and geopolitical friction.[27] The international co-productionâ€” involving France, Palestine, Germany, Qatar, Turkey, Canada, and Belgiumâ€” facilitated its selection for high-profile festivals, circumventing potential barriers tied to its Palestinian origin and critique of occupation dynamics.[1] Following Cannes, it screened at the Toronto International Film Festival in September 2019, where Suleiman participated in post-screening discussions highlighting the film's vignette-style commentary on cultural alienation.[36] Additional presentations occurred at the New York Film Festival later that year, contributing to early circuit buzz among critics focused on its Tati-esque visual humor and understated political undertones.[7]

Commercial Release

The film had its initial commercial theatrical release in France on December 4, 2019, distributed by Le Pacte, followed by limited rollouts in other European markets such as Italy on December 5, 2019, Germany on January 16, 2020, and Spain on February 14, 2020.[37] In the United Kingdom, Artificial Eye managed distribution, with theatrical screenings commencing in June 2021 after pandemic-related disruptions.[38] [39] North American distribution was handled by Kino Lorber, which arranged a limited theatrical release in the United States in early 2020; however, the COVID-19 pandemic caused widespread delays and theater closures, curtailing broader rollout.[40] [41] Releases in the Middle East remained constrained, aligned with the film's co-production involvement from Qatar and Turkey, prioritizing arthouse circuits over wide commercial penetration.[37] Global box office earnings were modest at approximately $2.3 million, including $1.45 million from France alone, typical for a niche satirical arthouse production with minimal marketing push.[42] [43] Following theatrical windows, the film became available for digital rental and purchase on platforms including Amazon Prime Video and Apple TV, expanding accessibility beyond cinemas.[44] [45]

Reception

Critical Evaluations

It Must Be Heaven garnered predominantly favorable reviews from critics, achieving a 96% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 52 aggregated reviews, with praise centered on its visual wit, deadpan humor, and satirical timeliness in depicting displacement and absurdity.[3] The film's reception highlighted Elia Suleiman's return to directing after a 10-year hiatus since The Time That Remains (2009), often comparing its silent, observational style favorably to his earlier works like Divine Intervention (2002), which similarly employed minimal dialogue and Chaplinesque physical comedy to underscore existential ironies.[11] Reviewers in major outlets frequently awarded scores ranging from 3/5 to 4/5 stars, commending the universality of its humorâ€”which avoids overt preachiness in favor of subtle, relatable vignettes of alienationâ€”while noting its craftsmanship in cinematography and editing as strengths that elevate the narrative's episodic structure.[6][8] Criticisms focused on executional shortcomings, including an elusive mannerism that occasionally rendered scenes superficial or overly passive, as observed in analyses of Suleiman's recurrent aesthetic choices.[6] Some reviews pointed to pacing inconsistencies, with the film's vignette-based progression feeling uneven or protracted in segments outside the Palestinian setting, potentially diluting momentum. Despite these reservations, detractors acknowledged the film's empirical merits in visual storytelling, attributing flaws to stylistic risks rather than fundamental missteps.[27]

Viewpoints on Political Representation

Elia Suleiman, born in Nazareth in 1960 to a Greek Orthodox Christian family, draws from his experiences of exile in cities like New York and Paris to craft films emphasizing ironic observation over direct political confrontation.[46] In It Must Be Heaven, this manifests as a semi-autobiographical satire where Suleiman portrays a filmmaker fleeing Palestinian absurdities for global ones, using silent, deadpan vignettes to highlight displacements and hypocrisies without explicit advocacy.[47] This approach contrasts with more confrontational Palestinian cinema, favoring ridicule to expose universal follies rather than partisan narratives.[47] Supporters interpret the film's political representation as broadening the Palestinian-Israeli conflict into a critique of global absurdities, portraying Palestine as a microcosm extended outward. Suleiman has stated that while earlier works framed Palestine as reflecting the world, It Must Be Heaven inverts this to depict "the world as if it were a microcosm of Palestine," thereby universalizing conflict dynamics like militarized checkpoints and cultural displacements across societies from Nazareth to New York.[48] This avoids one-sided victimhood by satirizing not only Israeli security measuresâ€”such as soldiers in olive grovesâ€”but also Palestinian intra-community theft and Western liberal pieties, suggesting shared human follies underpin all exiles.[48] Critics, particularly from pro-Israel perspectives, argue the satire remains vague on root causes, omitting factors like Palestinian governance failures under entities such as the Palestinian Authority or Hamas, which have perpetuated internal divisions and rejected peace offers since the Oslo Accords in 1993.[48] The film's global lens, they contend, dilutes attention to Israel's specific security necessities amid persistent threats, equating routine precautions like border patrols with unrelated Western excesses, thus reinforcing anti-Western and anti-Israel tropes without causal analysis of historical territorial claims or terrorism's role in escalations.[48] Such analyses, from outlets aligned with Israeli viewpoints, highlight how the oblique style risks portraying Palestinians as passive noble victims while minimally engaging Israeli agency or context.[48] Some reviewers note this elusiveness can feel mannered, prioritizing aesthetic detachment over substantive engagement with conflict drivers.[6]

Awards and Nominations

It Must Be Heaven competed in the main competition at the 2019 Cannes Film Festival, where it received the FIPRESCI Prize for the best film in competition and a Special Mention from the Jury.[49][50] The film won Best Film and Best Director (Elia Suleiman) at the 4th Critics Awards for Arab Films in 2020.[49][51] Elia Suleiman was nominated for Achievement in Directing at the 2019 Asia Pacific Screen Awards.[52] It received a nomination for Best European Film at the 2021 Gopo Awards in Romania.[4]

Cultural Impact

Influence on Cinema

It Must Be Heaven exemplifies a revival of silent satire in arthouse cinema for exploring political displacement, employing deadpan visual gags and minimal dialogue reminiscent of Buster Keaton and Jacques Tati to underscore absurdities of exile without overt exposition.[31][27] This approach balances comedic choreography with subtle socio-political revelation, as seen in symmetrical sequences juxtaposing Nazareth's rural tensions against urban alienation in Paris and New York, fostering stylistic echoes in subsequent niche narratives of global migration post-2020 that prioritize observational humor over verbal tragedy.[31] Within Palestinian filmmaking, Suleiman's emphasis on ironic detachment and physical comedy models an alternative to documentary-style realism or melodramatic suffering, enabling exportation of identity themes to international audiences through universal absurdity rather than localized pathos.[54] This satirical framework, rooted in sumudâ€”or steadfast enduranceâ€”via the protagonist's compassionate witnessing, resists reductive colonial portrayals by highlighting shared human follies, thus contributing to a "transnational atonality" in regional cinema that fractures linear victimhood arcs.[31][54] Academic examinations, including a 2025 Senses of Cinema critique, dissect the film's ethics of the gazeâ€”interweaving director, character, and viewer perspectivesâ€”as a tool for ethical resistance, influencing scholarly discourse on how silent protagonists enable revelatory critiques of power dynamics in arthouse political satire.[31] Such analyses underscore its role in broadening discussions of visual storytelling's capacity to evoke latent violence and quiet defiance, though without evidence of direct emulation in major productions. The film's cinematic footprint stays confined to festival circuits and specialized journals, cultivating a dedicated following among filmmakers interested in minimalist exile portrayals but evincing scant penetration into mainstream or commercial genres, where verbal-driven narratives predominate.[31][54]

Broader Sociopolitical Context

It Must Be Heaven portrays the Israeli-Palestinian conflict through surreal vignettes, such as checkpoint encounters symbolizing restricted mobility and occupation absurdities, reflecting ongoing territorial disputes and security measures in the West Bank and Nazareth since the 1948 establishment of Israel.[8] Released in 2019, the film preceded the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks and subsequent Gaza war, capturing pre-escalation dynamics amid a backdrop of stalled peace processes and settlement expansions documented by UN reports from 2010-2019, which noted over 100,000 new housing units.[7] Suleiman's narrative critiques displacement not merely as victimhood but through the protagonist's futile search for belonging in Paris and New York, underscoring causal factors like historical partition and migration policies over purely emotive oppression frames often amplified in Western media.[55] The film's exploration of Palestinian diaspora aligns with global migration trends, as the UN High Commissioner for Refugees reported 281 million international migrants in 2020, up from 173 million in 2000, with Palestinians comprising a stateless subset estimated at 5.9 million refugees by 2019.[56] Suleiman's relocation to Paris since the 1980s facilitates this detached lens, enabling commentary on cultural alienation without direct subjugation, contrasting state-funded narratives from both Israeli and some Arab sources that prioritize propaganda over individual agency.[57] This approach challenges prevailing left-leaning academic and media emphases on structural oppression devoid of personal resilience or policy trade-offs, as evidenced by the film's ironic depictions of Western indifference to Palestinian specifics.[54] In 2024, Suleiman co-signed an open letter with over 65 Palestinian filmmakers accusing Hollywood of "dehumanizing" representations and censorship, particularly in efforts to disqualify Bisan Owda's Gaza documentary from Emmy consideration, highlighting institutional biases favoring Israeli perspectives amid post-October 7 coverage disparities.[58][59] This event underscores the film's legacy in prompting debates on cinema's capacity for unvarnished truth-telling versus selective advocacy, where Suleiman's oeuvre resists propagandistic simplification by foregrounding existential humor over partisan rallying.[60]
