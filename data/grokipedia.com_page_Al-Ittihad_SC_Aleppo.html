Al Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo SC

Al-Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo SC is a professional association football club based in Aleppo, Syria, founded in 1949 as Halab Al-Ahli Club through the merger of three local teams and later renamed.[1] The club competes in the Syrian Premier League and is recognized as one of the most successful in Syrian football history, having secured seven national league championships, including the most recent in the 2024/25 season, along with ten Syrian Cup titles.[2][3] Internationally, it achieved prominence by winning the 2010 AFC Cup, defeating Al-Jaish of Syria in the final.[4] Home matches are primarily hosted at Aleppo International Stadium, Syria's largest football venue with a capacity exceeding 40,000 spectators.[5] Despite the disruptions caused by the Syrian civil war since 2011, which severely impacted operations in Aleppo, the club has maintained competitive presence, reflecting resilience in a conflict-affected sporting landscape.[2]

Formal establishment followed in subsequent years, with the club gaining official licensing around 1951 and public announcement of its founding on 25 January 1953 by a group of seven pioneers: Muhammad Zamm, Muhammad Khayr Faris, Thuraya Ka'dan, Ghalib Ka'dan, Mustafa Aziz, George Naqous, and others who secured legal recognition from Syrian authorities. Operating initially from modest facilities, Halab Al-Ahli competed in regional leagues and early iterations of the Syrian national championship, emphasizing youth development and local rivalries that solidified its role as Aleppo's premier club. Facilities and infrastructure

Home stadium

Al-Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo SC primarily plays its home matches at Aleppo International Stadium, located in the Al-Hamadaniyah district of Aleppo, Syria. Opened on 15 May 2007 after a 27-year construction period, the venue has a seating capacity of 53,200, making it the largest stadium in Syria.[20] The stadium features modern facilities including floodlights, a natural grass pitch, and amenities for international matches, and has hosted Syrian national team games alongside club fixtures. Due to reconstruction work on Aleppo International Stadium, which began in recent years to upgrade infrastructure damaged during the Syrian Civil War, Al-Ittihad has temporarily shifted home games to Al-Hamadaniah Stadium, also in Aleppo, with a capacity of 18,000.[21] This multi-purpose venue, primarily an all-seater football ground, serves as an alternative during the renovations, maintaining continuity for the club's Syrian Premier League schedule.[22] The club additionally owns Al-Ittihad Stadium, a dedicated 20,000-capacity facility constructed in the 1980s on land allocated specifically for the team, though it has seen limited recent use compared to the larger international venue.[23] These arrangements reflect adaptations to infrastructure challenges in Aleppo, including war-related damage and post-conflict repairs, ensuring the team continues to host matches in its home city.

Training grounds and academy

The training facilities of Al Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo SC are located in the al-Shahbaa district of Aleppo, encompassing the Al-Ittihad Stadium, a multi-purpose venue primarily utilized for team training sessions following infrastructure challenges from the Syrian Civil War.[24] Constructed in the 1980s on allocated land, the stadium accommodates training for both senior and reserve squads, with a reported capacity of 20,000 that supports drills, fitness regimens, and youth development activities when not hosting limited matches.[25] The club's youth academy, formally known as the Al Ittihad Aleppo Sports Club Academy, emphasizes grassroots talent cultivation through structured programs. It operates a dedicated football school providing weekly morning and evening training sessions for players aged 6 to 18, with enrollment periods announced periodically to integrate local prospects into skill-building curricula focused on technical proficiency, tactics, and physical conditioning.[26] Academy activities include competitive fixtures against regional counterparts, as evidenced by recent youth team performances documented on its official channels. The under-23 squad functions as an extension of the academy, sourcing players directly from its pipeline to bridge developmental gaps toward first-team integration, thereby sustaining the club's tradition of promoting homegrown talent amid regional instability.[27]

Supporters and culture

Fan base and attendance

Al-Ittihad Ahli of Aleppo SC draws its core fan base from Aleppo and northern Syria, where the club symbolizes regional identity and sporting heritage. Supporters are renowned for their loyalty, with pre-Syrian Civil War matches at Al-Hamadaniyah Stadium routinely attracting capacity crowds of up to 15,000, reflecting widespread local enthusiasm for the team.[17] The war displaced many residents and halted home games, significantly diminishing attendance and organized support from 2012 onward. In the recovery phase post-2017, fans have gradually returned, evidenced by enthusiastic crowds at the club's first home fixtures after years of absence, including vocal cheering from families and youth clad in the team's green colors.[34] Organized ultras groups, such as Ultras Red Fighters, have emerged to coordinate chants, displays, and travel, fostering a renewed atmosphere despite ongoing security constraints. Controversies and challenges

Corruption in Syrian football

Corruption has long plagued Syrian football, characterized by match-fixing, referee bribery, and institutional nepotism under the Assad regime. In June 2009, the Syrian Premier League faced major allegations of widespread match-fixing and systemic corruption, culminating in the relegation of two prominent clubs, Al-Jaish and Al-Karamah, as a punitive measure by the Syrian Football Association.[46] These events highlighted deep-rooted issues where club officials and players allegedly manipulated outcomes for financial gain, eroding competitive integrity across the league.[46] Refereeing scandals have been particularly rampant, with reports indicating that virtually no Syrian League match proceeds without controversial decisions influenced by external pressures or bribes. Coaches and players have accused referees of deliberate errors, such as ignoring fouls or awarding phantom penalties, often to favor Damascus-based teams aligned with regime interests.[47] In 2010, multiple bribery cases surfaced within the Syrian Football Federation, involving officials soliciting payments to influence referee assignments and match rulings, further entrenching favoritism toward politically connected clubs.[48] Institutional corruption extended to federation dealings, exemplified by pressure on the Syrian Football Association in 2024 to award a commercial contract to a relative of Maher al-Assad, the former president's brother, despite lacking qualifications.[49] This nepotism, coupled with political interference, stifled professional development and perpetuated unprofessional practices, as veteran players noted in 2023 exposÃ©s of media-revealed corruption files sparking public outrage.[50] Following the Assad regime's fall in December 2024, the federation pledged reforms, including a new national team kit symbolizing a break from "nepotism, favoritism, and corruption," signaling tentative steps toward accountability.[51]
