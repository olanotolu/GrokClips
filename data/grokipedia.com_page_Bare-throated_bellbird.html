Bare-throated bellbird

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) is a species of passerine bird in the family Cotingidae, endemic to the Atlantic Forest biome spanning southeastern Brazil, eastern Paraguay, and northeastern Argentina.[1][2] Adult males are characterized by their striking white plumage, stout black bill, and conspicuous bare throat skin that is bluish-black with short bristles, contrasting with the olive-brown, streaked females; the species measures approximately 28 cm in length.[1][3] It is distinguished by its exceptionally loud, metallic bell-like vocalizations, produced by males from exposed canopy perches during breeding displays, which can carry over 1 km and rank among the most intense calls of any bird.[4][5] This frugivorous bird inhabits subtropical and tropical moist lowland and montane forests, often favoring areas with abundant fruiting trees in the canopy where it gleans and sallies for food.[1][6] Males engage in lekking behavior, aggregating to perform aerial displays and chorusing vocalizations to attract females, with breeding occurring primarily during the austral spring.[7] Populations have declined due to extensive habitat loss in the highly fragmented Atlantic Forest, but ongoing conservation efforts in protected areas have stabilized numbers sufficiently for the IUCN to downlist the species from Vulnerable to Near Threatened in 2020.[8][1] The bare-throated bellbird serves as an indicator of forest health, with its presence signaling intact ecosystems supporting fruit-dispersing avifauna.[6]

Taxonomy and description

Classification and etymology

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) is a passerine bird in the family Cotingidae, which comprises the cotingas, a diverse Neotropical group characterized by frugivory and often vibrant plumage or vocalizations in males.[9][3] The full taxonomic hierarchy is as follows: Kingdom Animalia, phylum Chordata, class Aves, order Passeriformes, family Cotingidae, genus Procnias, species nudicollis.[10][11] The binomial name was established by French ornithologist Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot in 1817, based on specimens from South America.[12] The genus Procnias originates from Greek mythology, alluding to Procne, an Athenian princess mythologically transformed into a swallow (Hirundo rustica), though the connection to these fruit-eating cotingas likely stems from early taxonomic conventions linking avian genera to mythological figures associated with birds.[13] The species epithet nudicollis combines Latin nudus ("bare" or "naked") and collum ("neck" or "throat"), directly describing the male's conspicuous bare, bluish-black skin on the throat and face, which contrasts sharply with its otherwise white plumage.[7] The common name "bare-throated bellbird" similarly highlights this anatomical feature alongside the bird's resonant, metallic calls resembling struck bells, a trait shared among Procnias species that inspired the "bellbird" descriptor in English ornithological nomenclature.[7][14]

Physical characteristics

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) displays marked sexual dimorphism in its plumage and size. Adult males are characterized by nearly all-white plumage, rendering them conspicuous during displays from exposed perches.[15] This white feathering contrasts sharply with a distinctive bare, bluish-black skin patch encircling the eyes, base of the bill, and throat, which features a bristly texture.[2][16] Adult females are smaller and exhibit cryptic coloration suited to forest understory, with drab olive upperparts, a blackish crown and throat, and yellowish underparts heavily streaked with olive.[15][17] The species attains a length of 27â€“30 cm, with males reaching up to 29 cm and weights of approximately 225 g.[17][2] Juveniles resemble females in plumage but may show additional streaking.[15]

Vocalizations

The male Procnias nudicollis produces a sharp, metallic call resembling the sound of a bell or hammer striking an anvil, delivered from exposed uppermost branches in the forest canopy.[4] These vocalizations, primarily associated with mate attraction and territorial defense, are among the loudest of any bird species and can be audible over distances exceeding 1 km under favorable conditions.[4] [18] Males often accompany calls with display jumps, rapidly fluttering their wings before leaping to a nearby perch and repeating the sequence multiple times.[4] Female and immature calls are quieter and less frequently documented, lacking the intensity and prominence of male songs.[4] Unlike typical suboscine passerines, which produce innate vocalizations without learning, captive rearing experiments have demonstrated that P. nudicollis exhibits vocal plasticity, including the ability to imitate heterospecific calls such as those of the chopi blackbird (Gnorimopsar chopi) when isolated from conspecific tutors during development.[19] This suggests an independent evolutionary origin of limited song learning within the genus Procnias, supported by observations of abnormal song development in sound-deprived individuals and dialectal variations potentially influenced by local acoustic environments.[20] [21] Acoustic analyses of wild recordings indicate call frequencies peaking around 15 kHz, with developmental shifts such as gradual frequency modulation in maturing males.[22]

Habitat requirements

The bare-throated bellbird primarily inhabits primary forests in the Atlantic Forest biome of eastern Brazil, extending to northeastern Argentina and eastern Paraguay, favoring subtropical or tropical moist lowland and montane forests with intact canopy structure.[1] [23] It exhibits a strong preference for undisturbed primary vegetation, occurring 2.23 times more abundantly in native forests than in planted ones and 2.6 times more frequently in natural Araucaria forests than in plantations, reflecting its reliance on mature, diverse tree assemblages for foraging and display.[1] Although less common, the species tolerates secondary forests provided they contain fruit-bearing trees, and it has been detected in abandoned rubber groves where suitable food resources persist.[1] Elevational distribution ranges from sea level to 1,150 m, with males requiring elevated perches above the canopy for lekking and vocalizations, underscoring the need for tall, unfragmented forest strata.[1] [23] Habitat suitability is critically linked to fruit availability, as the bird is frugivorous, feeding on canopy fruits from families including Arecaceae, Myrtaceae, and Moraceae; this dependence drives potential altitudinal movements, with individuals possibly shifting to lower elevations during the non-breeding season when highland fruit resources diminish.[1] In northeastern Brazil, it occupies higher elevations relative to the altitudinally sympatric bearded bellbird (Procnias averano), further indicating niche partitioning tied to elevational gradients in forest productivity.[23]

Behavior and ecology

Diet and foraging

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) is entirely frugivorous, relying exclusively on fruits for its diet, which it obtains primarily from the canopy and subcanopy of forest trees.[24][1] In regions such as ParanÃ¡ in southern Brazil, it consumes fruits from plant families including Araliaceae, Celastraceae, Euphorbiaceae, and Lauraceae.[24] Across its range in the Atlantic Forest, documented fruit sources encompass Arecaceae, Myrtaceae, Myrcinaceae, Moraceae, Myristicaceae, Apocynaceae, Sapindaceae, Lauraceae, Leguminosae, Burseraceae, Malpighiaceae, Celastraceae, Elaeocarpaceae, Araliaceae, and Liliaceae.[1] Foraging occurs mainly in primary and secondary forests with abundant fruit-bearing trees, where individuals perch to glean or pluck fruits directly or engage in short, somewhat clumsy sallying flights to capture them.[24] Birds may traverse modified landscapes, such as crossing at least 400 m of rubber groves, to reach suitable foraging patches.[1] This behavior aligns with seasonal movements potentially driven by fruit availability, including altitudinal shifts to exploit varying elevations.[1] As a canopy frugivore, the species plays a key ecological role in seed dispersal for Atlantic Forest trees, aiding forest regeneration by transporting seeds via defecation away from parent plants.[1][24] Observations indicate limited time spent actively foraging, consistent with the stationary nature of fruit resources compared to mobile insect prey.[24]

Reproduction and social behavior

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) employs a polygynous mating system, in which males produce extremely loud, explosive vocalizations from exposed high canopy perches to attract multiple females during the breeding season, while providing no parental investment beyond gametes.[4][25] Breeding occurs primarily from September to February, with timing varying slightly across populations in its Atlantic Forest range.[26] Males defend display territories and compete acoustically, often in proximity to other males, facilitating female choice based on call intensity and quality, though formal leks have not been documented for this species.[27] Nest construction, incubation, and chick-rearing are performed exclusively by females, consistent with the genus Procnias, where males exhibit sexual dichromatism and focus solely on mate attraction.[26][28] Only one detailed nest description exists: a cup-shaped structure built by the female, typically containing two oval eggs that are reddish-brown with dark spots concentrated at the broader end.[26] Incubation and fledging periods remain undocumented, but females provision nestlings independently until independence.[28] Outside the breeding season, bare-throated bellbirds are largely solitary, with limited interspecific or intraspecific social interactions observed; juveniles may disperse independently, and adults forage alone in the forest canopy.[2] During breeding, male territorial aggression manifests through vocal contests rather than physical displays, minimizing direct confrontations while maximizing acoustic advertisement.[4] No evidence indicates cooperative breeding or extended family groups in this species.[20]

Conservation

Population status

The bare-throated bellbird (Procnias nudicollis) is classified as Near Threatened on the IUCN Red List, reflecting a population that remains relatively large but is undergoing a continuing decline primarily due to ongoing habitat degradation in its Atlantic Forest range.[1] Global population estimates range from 80,000 to 130,000 mature individuals, with the species distributed across eastern Brazil, northeastern Argentina, and eastern Paraguay.[1] In Argentina, where the bird is rarer, surveys indicate fewer than 250 mature individuals persist, highlighting regional vulnerability.[17] The overall trend is decreasing, with rates inferred to exceed 10% over three generations based on habitat loss models, though precise annual census data remain limited due to the species' altitudinal migrations and forest interior habitat.[1] Monitoring efforts, including those by BirdLife International partners, emphasize the need for updated density estimates in core Brazilian populations, where the species is more abundant but still faces fragmentation pressures. Nationally in Brazil, it holds Near Endangered status, underscoring localized risks despite the global assessment.[1]

Primary threats

The bare-throated bellbird faces primary threats from ongoing habitat destruction and degradation within its core range in the Atlantic Forest of eastern Brazil, Paraguay, and northeastern Argentina, where deforestation for agricultural expansion, urbanization, industrialization, and road infrastructure has reduced available montane forest by extensive margins since the mid-20th century.[1] These activities fragment contiguous forest tracts essential for the species' altitudinal migrations and foraging needs, leading to suspected population declines estimated at 80,000â€“130,000 mature individuals as of recent assessments.[1] Such habitat loss correlates directly with reduced breeding success, as the bird requires large, undisturbed areas of primary or mature secondary forest at elevations of 500â€“2,000 meters for lekking and fruit-dependent diets.[1] Illegal capture for the pet bird trade constitutes another key pressure, driven by demand for the male's striking white plumage, bare throat, and resonant vocalizations, which render it a prized cage bird in regional and international markets.[2] Trapping efforts, often using mist nets in forest fragments, target leks during breeding seasons (Septemberâ€“March), exacerbating local extirpations in accessible areas and contributing to the species' Near Threatened status under IUCN criteria, as subpopulations become isolated and vulnerable to stochastic events.[1][29] While direct hunting for food appears minimal, the synergistic effects of habitat fragmentation and trade amplify risks, particularly in southeastern Bahia fragments where recent surveys document persistent presence but underscore the need for enforcement against poaching amid continued forest conversion rates exceeding 1% annually in unprotected areas.[30] Climate-induced shifts in fruit availability may compound these pressures, though empirical data on such interactions remain limited.[1]
