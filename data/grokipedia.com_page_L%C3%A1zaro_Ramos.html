Lázaro Ramos

Luís Lázaro Sacramento Ramos (born November 1, 1978) is a Brazilian actor, director, presenter, producer, and author whose career spans theater, film, television, and literature.[1] Ramos initiated his professional trajectory with the Bando de Teatro Olodum children's theater company in his native Salvador, Bahia, accumulating over 100 roles and more than 70 awards across three decades of work.[1][1] Prominent early performances include the lead in the biographical drama Madame Satã (2002) and a supporting role in Hector Babenco's prison film Carandiru (2003), followed by an International Emmy nomination for Best Actor in the telenovela Cobras & Lagartos (2006).[1][1][1] In television, he co-starred in the comedy series Mr. Brau (2015–2018), which became a fixture in Brazilian broadcasting, while his directorial efforts, such as the dystopian feature Executive Order (2020)—depicting a future with reversed racial quotas—encountered release delays that ignited disputes over bureaucratic hurdles and potential state interference in artistic output.[1][2] Ramos, married to fellow actor Taís Araújo since November 25, 2008, has co-authored nine books, including the autobiographical Na Minha Pele (2017), and holds UNICEF Goodwill Ambassador status since 2009, focusing on initiatives for child rights and cultural visibility of Afro-Brazilians.[3][1][1]

Early Life

Childhood and Family Background

Lázaro Ramos, born Luís Lázaro Sacramento de Araújo Ramos on November 1, 1978, in Salvador, Bahia, Brazil, grew up in a working-class family in the city's vibrant urban environment.[1][4] His mother, Elza, worked as a seamstress and, despite being illiterate, was known for her industriousness and active involvement in family life.[5] His father, Luís Ramos, shared a similar modest background, reflecting the socioeconomic realities of many Bahian households during that era.[6] From an early age, Ramos displayed an affinity for performance, often imitating movie characters and engaging in local cultural activities like samba dancing and capoeira in Salvador's lively neighborhoods. These experiences in a lower-middle-class setting, amid Brazil's regional traditions of Afro-Brazilian heritage, shaped his initial exposure to storytelling and artistic expression, though formal training came later. No public records detail siblings, suggesting a focused family unit centered on parental labor and community influences.

Education and Initial Training

Ramos first encountered theater during his secondary education at Colégio Estadual Anísio Teixeira, a public school in Salvador, Bahia, where he enrolled after attending private institutions in neighborhoods like Federação and Garcia.[7] [8] There, around age 10, he participated in initial drama classes and small stage works under the artistic pseudonym Lula Somar, marking his entry into performance arts amid a curriculum that emphasized textual engagement and basic acting techniques.[7] His formative training intensified at age 15 upon joining Bando de Teatro Olodum, a Salvador-based collective founded in 1988 that integrated Afro-Brazilian rhythms, dance, and narratives into theatrical productions as a form of cultural resistance.[9] [10] This group served as his primary artistic cradle, offering hands-on immersion through ensemble rehearsals, street performances, and tours that honed skills in improvisation, voice projection, and physical expression without formal academic structure.[11] [12] Ramos credits the Bando's emphasis on collective creation and cultural identity for shaping his early professional ethos, distinguishing it from conventional conservatory methods by prioritizing communal and experiential learning.[13]

Professional Career

Theater Beginnings

Lázaro Ramos initiated his professional acting career in theater at the age of 15 by joining the Bando de Teatro Olodum, a prominent Afro-Brazilian theater company based in Salvador, Bahia, in 1994.[14] The group, founded in the early 1990s and affiliated with the Olodum cultural movement, emphasized performances integrating theater, music, and dance to highlight black Brazilian identity and social issues.[9] Ramos's debut role was in the production Bai, Bai Pelô, a play that showcased the troupe's dynamic style rooted in Bahian carnival traditions and local folklore.[14] Through Bando de Teatro Olodum, Ramos gained foundational experience in ensemble acting and improvisation, performing in subsequent works such as Ó Paí Ó, which explored themes of racial dynamics and urban life in Salvador.[15] These early productions, often staged in community venues and festivals, provided Ramos with exposure to professional rigor while reinforcing his commitment to culturally resonant narratives; he has credited the group with shaping his idols and artistic ethos during this formative period.[9] By the mid-1990s, his involvement extended to minor film cameos with the troupe, such as in Jenipapo (1995), but theater remained the core of his initial development, fostering skills in character embodiment amid live audiences attuned to Afro-diasporic expressions.[14]

Television and Film Roles

Ramos debuted in film with the lead role of João Francisco dos Santos, known as Madame Satã, in the 2002 biographical drama Madame Satã, depicting the life of the early 20th-century Afro-Brazilian performer and criminal figure from Rio de Janeiro's Lapa district.[1][16] In 2003, he appeared as Ezequiel in Héctor Babenco's Carandiru, a portrayal of events leading to the 1992 São Paulo prison massacre, and as André in O Homem que Copiava (The Man Who Copied), a crime comedy-drama about a photocopy shop worker's schemes.[1][17] His early 2000s film work continued with the role of Deco in Cidade Baixa (Lower City, 2005), a drama exploring friendship and rivalry among dock workers in Salvador, Bahia.[1][16] Later films include Zico in Nome Próprio (also known as Basic Sanitation, the Movie, 2007), a character navigating personal and societal challenges; Roque in Ó Paí, Ó (2006), a comedic take on Bahia's carnival culture; and supporting roles in O Palhaço (2011) and Besouro (2009), the latter portraying capoeira elements in a biopic of the legendary fighter.[17][16] In the 2010s, Ramos starred as Laerte in A Violista (The Violin Teacher, 2015), a thriller about a musician's moral dilemmas that screened internationally; Joãozinho in O Grande Kilapy (2012), based on the life of an Angolan anticolonial fighter; and Arandir in O Beijo no Asfalto (2018), earning him Best Actor at the International Filmmaker Festival of New York for a modern adaptation of a classic Brazilian play addressing homophobia and scandal.[1][17] More recent credits include the lead in Medida Provisória (Executive Order, 2020), a dystopian sci-fi drama he also directed, critiquing racial policies through a narrative of black men confined by government decree.[17][16] On television, Ramos first gained notice in Cobras & Lagartos (2006), playing Daniel Miranda Café (Foguinho), earning an International Emmy nomination for Best Actor, followed by Evilásio Caó in the Emmy-winning telenovela Duas Caras (2007).[1] He starred as Zé Maria in Lado a Lado (Side by Side, 2012), another Emmy-winning series set in early 20th-century Rio de Janeiro addressing racial and social divides over 154 episodes.[1][16] Ramos achieved a breakthrough with the titular role of Miguel Brau in Mister Brau (2015–2018), a four-season Globo comedy series about a samba musician turned celebrity chef, which ran for 36 episodes and became a cultural staple for its portrayal of black middle-class life in Rio.[1][17] Subsequent TV roles include Theo in Justiça (2018 miniseries), a narrative on revenge and justice; narrator in Amor de Mãe (A Mother's Love, 2019); Baruk in the animated Arca de Noé (Noah's Ark, 2020); and appearances in Bom Dia, Verônica (2020) and Dom (2021), blending drama with social commentary.[17][16] These roles often emphasize themes of racial identity, urban Brazilian experiences, and cultural resilience, drawing from his Bahia roots.[1]

Directing and Production Work

Ramos expanded into directing primarily through theater, where he has helmed four plays. In 2015, he directed, starred in, and produced O Topo da Montanha, an adaptation of Katori Hall's The Mountaintop co-starring his wife Taís Araújo, which drew over 300,000 spectators across its run.[18] He also directed O Método Gronholm in 2020.[18] Transitioning to film, Ramos made his feature directorial debut with Medida Provisória (English: Executive Order), a dystopian drama completed in 2020 and released theatrically in Brazil on April 14, 2022, after pandemic delays; the film screened for 28 weeks and earned awards including Best Script at the Indie Memphis Film Festival.[18][19] He co-directed the 2018 documentary Bando, um Filme de with Thiago Gomes, chronicling the 28-year history of the Bando de Teatro Olodum group.[20] In television, Ramos idealized and directed Espelho, a long-running program on Canal Brasil spanning 16 years focused on cultural reflections.[18] He served as showrunner and director for Um Ano Inesquecível: Outono (English: An Unforgettable Year: Autumn), released on Amazon Prime Video in 2023.[18] As a producer, Ramos held associate producer credits on early projects including Ó Paí, Ó (2007), The Great Kilapy (2012), and Smile, You're on Camera (2015), often tied to films in which he acted.[16] Through his production banner Cinema Lázaro Ramos, he has supported theater and film initiatives, including his directorial works.[1] Additionally, he has directed two unspecified musical clips and a musical tour.[1]

Writing and Literary Contributions

Lázaro Ramos began his literary career in 2000 with the children's book Paparutas, a work inspired by his own childhood experiences and adapted into a theatrical production.[21] This debut marked the start of his focus on youth literature, emphasizing themes of imagination and personal growth. Over the subsequent decades, Ramos authored multiple children's titles aimed at fostering self-esteem, emotional awareness, and cultural representation, often drawing from Brazilian folklore and everyday challenges faced by young readers.[22] Key works in his children's literature include Edith e a Velha Sentada (2010, reissued by Editora Pallas), which explores intergenerational bonds and resilience; O Caderno de Rimas do João (2014); Caderno sem Rimas da Maria (2018); and Sinto o que Sinto e a Incrível História de Asta e Jaser (2019, in collaboration with Mundo Bita), a title designed to guide children through emotional recognition and expression.[23] [22] Ramos's approach in these books prioritizes accessibility and relatability, using simple narratives to address diversity and perseverance, with several achieving commercial success and reprints.[24] Additional youth-oriented publications encompass O Pulo do Coelho (2021), focusing on balance and ambition, contributing to a body of work that has sold widely in Brazil.[23] [25] Transitioning to adult nonfiction, Ramos published Na Minha Pele in 2017 through Objetiva, a reflective essay collection detailing his encounters with racial prejudice and identity in Brazilian society, based on personal anecdotes and broader social observations.[22] This book marked his entry into mature literature and received attention for its candid examination of blackness in professional and cultural contexts.[26] By 2025, Ramos had released approximately ten books overall, including the biography A Rainha da Rua Paissandu (September 2025), chronicling the life of actress Ruth de Souza from her perspective, underscoring his interest in documenting overlooked figures in Brazilian arts.[27] His literary output complements his acting career, often intersecting with themes of racial equity and human emotion across genres.[18]

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Lázaro Ramos married Brazilian actress Taís Araújo in a civil ceremony on June 9, 2011, in Rio de Janeiro.[28] [4] The couple had begun their relationship in 2004, after meeting during Ramos's work on the television series Sexo Frágil.[29] Their union has been described in Brazilian media as a stable partnership between two prominent actors, with both frequently crediting mutual support in balancing demanding careers with family responsibilities.[30] Ramos and Araújo have two children: a son, João Vicente de Araújo Ramos, born on June 18, 2011, shortly after their marriage, and a daughter, Maria Antônia de Araújo Ramos, born on January 23, 2015. The family resides primarily in Rio de Janeiro and maintains a relatively private personal life, though the parents occasionally share glimpses of family moments on social media to highlight themes of parental involvement and cultural heritage.[31] Ramos has publicly emphasized the importance of instilling values such as resilience and education in their children, drawing from his own upbringing in Salvador.

Public Persona and Lifestyle

Lázaro Ramos maintains a public image as a multifaceted intellectual and cultural figure in Brazil, often regarded as a role model within Afro-Brazilian communities for his advocacy of artistic expression and social awareness.[32][33] He projects a persona that emphasizes ongoing personal reflection, rejecting rigid notions of traditional masculinity in favor of what he describes as a daily "deconstruction" process.[34] In terms of lifestyle, Ramos prioritizes family integration with his professional commitments, living with his wife, actress Taís Araújo, and their two children, João Vicente (born circa 2012) and Maria Antônia (born circa 2015), in a dynamic household where he adopts the informal nickname "Lazinho" to foster authentic connections beyond his celebrity status.[34][35] The couple, married for over 18 years as of 2023, frequently collaborates on projects, blending work and personal life while emphasizing presence and mutual support.[34][36] Ramos describes himself as a multitasker who relies on a traditional paper agenda to manage appointments and tasks, though he has acknowledged challenges in prioritizing physical and mental self-care, particularly amid demanding schedules.[34] Post-pandemic, he has expressed a desire for a "lighter" approach to life, avoiding stressors and focusing on well-being, art's transformative potential, and balanced coexistence.[34] This reflects a philosophy centered on intentional living rather than ostentation, aligning with his broader emphasis on genuine relationships over performative public roles.[34]

Activism and Social Views

Advocacy for Racial and Cultural Issues

Lázaro Ramos has engaged in advocacy for racial equality and cultural representation in Brazil primarily through his artistic works and public platforms. In his 2017 book Na Minha Pele, Ramos explores racial dynamics in Brazilian society via personal anecdotes, framing racism as an issue intertwined with affection and identity formation.[37] [38] The text, one of Brazil's widely read works on racial themes, prompts reflection on self-perception among black Brazilians without prescribing definitive solutions.[1] Ramos extended this focus to film with Medida Provisória (released internationally as Executive Order), a 2021 dystopian production depicting a government policy exiling black citizens amid rising structural racism, referencing Brazil's historical status as the last Americas nation to abolish slavery in 1888.[39] [40] The film highlights ongoing disparities in representation and power, drawing from Brazil's legacy of importing over 4 million African slaves.[41] As a UNICEF ambassador for Brazil, Ramos promotes discussions on racial inequalities and their societal impacts, emphasizing dialogue to address discrimination's effects on children and communities.[42] He has publicly advocated for greater black inclusion in media leadership, noting in 2022 the scarcity of black executives in Brazilian cinema and television despite growing visibility of black actors.[43] In May 2025, during the launch of the Código de Defesa e Inclusão do Consumidor Negro, Ramos highlighted everyday racism faced by black consumers in retail settings, such as differential treatment based on appearance.[44] In a March 2025 book launch, Ramos argued that combating racism requires shifting from awareness to sensitization and rethinking power structures, urging a reevaluation of entrenched certainties to foster broader societal change.[45] He has stressed the role of non-black individuals in active listening rather than debate, positioning diversity as both an economic opportunity and a tool for provocation and transformation in cultural industries.[46] [47]

Criticisms of Positions and Public Backlash

Ramos's directorial debut Medida Provisória (English: Executive Order), released on July 7, 2022, elicited significant backlash from conservative commentators and political figures who viewed the film's dystopian premise—a provisional measure deporting Black Brazilians earning above a certain income threshold to Africa—as an allegorical attack on former President Jair Bolsonaro's administration and a promotion of racial victimhood over individual agency. Sergio Camargo, a Black Brazilian appointed as president of the Fundação Cultural Palmares under Bolsonaro, described the narrative as embodying "pure victim mentality and a defamatory attack on our president," arguing it misrepresented Brazil's racial dynamics by prioritizing grievance narratives amid the country's history of miscegenation and class-based mobility.[48][49] The film's production and distribution faced delays attributed by Ramos to bureaucratic hurdles, which he publicly labeled as potential censorship, further fueling partisan debate; right-leaning outlets contended these claims exaggerated administrative processes to garner sympathy and frame opposition as racially motivated suppression, rather than routine regulatory scrutiny in a fiscally conservative government. Critics in media such as Gazeta do Povo argued the story divided Brazilians along fabricated racial lines, ignoring empirical evidence of socioeconomic advancement among Afro-descendants through education and entrepreneurship, and instead reinforcing a binary oppressor-oppressed framework that critics say distorts Brazil's fluid ethnic continuum where class often supersedes race as the primary barrier.[50] Ramos's broader activism, including books like Na Minha Pele (2017) and its 2025 sequel Na Nossa Pele, which compile essays on personal experiences of racism to advocate for heightened racial awareness, has been faulted by detractors for conflating anecdotal bias with systemic policy failures, potentially incentivizing identity-based entitlements like quotas in arts and media over meritocratic selection. Conservative voices, including those aligned with Bolsonaro's base, have accused such positions of undermining national cohesion by importing U.S.-style racial essentialism ill-suited to Brazil's demographic reality, where intermarriage rates exceed 40% in urban areas and poverty correlates more strongly with geography and family structure than skin color alone; however, these critiques often emanate from outlets skeptical of academia's emphasis on structural racism, prioritizing data from sources like IBGE census reports showing narrowing racial income gaps via market-driven opportunities.[50]

Awards and Recognition

Major Honors and Nominations

In 2025, Ramos was conferred the Ordem do Mérito Cultural by Brazil's Ministry of Culture, recognizing his multifaceted contributions to the arts as an actor, director, and producer.[51] This honor, reinstated after a suspension in 2019, highlights recipients across categories like cinema and performing arts, with Ramos noted for his influence in Brazilian cultural expression.[52] Ramos earned the Best Actor award at the 2003 Havana Film Festival for his role in O Homem que Copiava (The Man Who Copied), directed by Jorge Furtado.[53] In 2019, he received the Troféu Oscarito at the Gramado Film Festival, an accolade given to figures who have significantly impacted Brazilian cinema over their careers.[54] His directorial debut Medida Provisória (Executive Order, 2021) secured 15 nominations at the 2023 Grande Prêmio do Cinema Brasileiro, including for Best Film, Best Director, and Best Adapted Screenplay.[55] The film also earned a nomination for Best Film at the same awards.[56] For television, Ramos received International Emmy nominations for his portrayal of Foguinho in the 2006 telenovela Cobras & Lagartos.[57] He garnered multiple Troféu Imprensa nominations for Best Actor, including for Duas Caras (2008) and Cobras & Lagartos (2007).[58] In 2024, Ramos was selected as the National Honoree at the Vitória Film Festival, acknowledging his body of work in film.[59] In September 2025, he joined the International Academy of Television Arts & Sciences, positioning him to influence evaluations of global television content.[57]

Impact on Brazilian Entertainment

Lázaro Ramos has advanced the representation of Afro-Brazilians in Brazilian cinema and television by portraying complex, non-stereotypical characters, challenging traditional depictions limited to roles as domestic workers, criminals, or slum residents. His breakthrough role in the 2002 film Madame Satã, where he played the historical figure João Francisco dos Santos, earned critical acclaim and positioned him as a leading talent in national cinema, contributing to a shift toward more nuanced narratives about black experiences.[61] This performance, alongside appearances in films like City of God (2002), helped elevate discussions on urban poverty and racial dynamics in Brazilian storytelling, influencing subsequent productions to incorporate diverse casts and perspectives.[62] In television, Ramos co-starred in the Globo series Mister Brau (premiered October 7, 2015), alongside Taís Araújo, depicting a successful Afro-Brazilian musical duo navigating fame and relationships—a rare portrayal of black prosperity and agency on a major network. This marked the first instance of an Afro-Brazilian actor as a romantic lead in a Brazilian telenovela, prompting broader industry reflection on racial casting practices amid Brazil's entrenched media underrepresentation of its 54% black or mixed-race population.[63][36][64] The series' success underscored demand for such content, though critics noted persistent stereotypes in supporting roles, highlighting incremental rather than transformative change.[36] As a director, Ramos's 2022 film Medida Provisória (Executive Order) explored speculative racial quotas inverting privileges, starring an all-black lead cast and addressing systemic inequalities directly—a deliberate effort to create stories "about black people made by black people." With over 35 films and two dozen television appearances in his career, Ramos has modeled reinvention across genres, from theater adaptations like Ó Paí Ó to advocacy-driven projects, fostering greater creative freedom in Brazilian audiovisual production.[1][65] His influence extends to industry recognition, including honors at the 2019 Gramado Film Festival, reinforcing his role in diversifying narratives despite ongoing critiques of limited black presence in decision-making roles.[66][43]

Recent Developments and Legacy

Projects Post-2020

In 2022, Ramos directed and co-wrote Medida Provisória (English: Executive Order), a dystopian drama released on April 14 in Brazil, which he had developed prior to 2020 but delayed due to the COVID-19 pandemic; the film explores racial tensions in a future Brazil where a provisional measure mandates black citizens' relocation, starring Alfred Enoch, Taís Araújo, and Seu Jorge.[19][67] Ramos also appeared in supporting roles in several films that year, including As Verdades as Josué and Papai é Pop (English: Dad Rocks), a comedy about family dynamics.[17] Ramos's television work post-2020 included the 2021 anthology series 5x Comédia, where he performed in comedic sketches, and a guest role in É Tudo Novela! as Foguinho.[17] In 2023, he starred in the telenovela Elas por Elas, a remake focusing on gender-swapped roles in a fashion house setting, and directed the romantic film Um Ano Inesquecível: Outono (English: An Unforgettable Year - Autumn), part of a series emphasizing seasonal love stories.[13] That year, he voiced characters in animated features like O Primeiro Natal do Mundo (English: World's First Christmas) and appeared in comedies such as Minha Irmã e Eu, Olha Isso! (English: Look at This), and Ó Paí, Ó 2, a sequel to the 2006 hit ensemble film.[68][17] In 2024, Ramos portrayed Baruk in the biblical drama Arca de Noé (English: Noah's Ark), a family-oriented production retelling the flood narrative.[17] Ramos continued his children's literature output with the 2025 re-edition and expansion of Sinto o que Sinto: Um Passeio pelos Sentimentos, originally published in 2019 in collaboration with Mundo Bita, focusing on emotional intelligence through the character Dan; a new edition launched in February 2025, alongside announcements of additional titles emphasizing self-discovery and feelings.[69][70] These projects reflect Ramos's ongoing blend of acting, directing, and writing, often centered on social themes, family, and cultural identity in Brazilian media.[1]

Broader Cultural Influence

Lázaro Ramos has significantly shaped Brazilian cultural discourse by championing Afro-Brazilian representation in media and literature, challenging historical underrepresentation of black narratives. Through roles in films like Madame Satã (2002) and television series such as Mister Brau (2015–2017), he has portrayed complex black characters that move beyond stereotypes, fostering greater visibility for Afro-Brazilian stories and histories rooted in regions like Bahia.[63][71] This work has positioned him as a key figure in advancing cultural equity, with his efforts recognized internationally; in 2017, the United Nations honored him as one of the world's most influential black individuals for promoting black excellence and heritage.[72] His authorship of children's books, including O Pequeno Príncipe Negro (2017) and newer titles like Sinto que sinto (2025), extends this influence to younger generations by embedding themes of racial identity, emotional intelligence, and anti-racism into accessible narratives. These works prioritize black protagonists and address societal issues such as prejudice, aiming to reshape children's self-perception and cultural understanding from an early age.[73][74][75] Ramos has emphasized the role of such literature in countering dominant cultural narratives, particularly amid rising screen-based media, by encouraging reading habits that affirm diverse identities.[76] A 2025 Ipsos survey identified Ramos as Brazil's most influential celebrity, highlighting his multifaceted impact on the national cultural landscape through advocacy, artistry, and public engagement on topics like racial emancipation and xenophobia.[77] His UNICEF ambassadorship since 2012 further amplifies this reach, involving campaigns for child rights and awareness that intersect with cultural preservation efforts.[42] Collectively, these contributions have inspired broader societal shifts toward inclusivity, serving as a model for black achievement while prompting ongoing debates on power structures and identity in Brazil.[78][79]
