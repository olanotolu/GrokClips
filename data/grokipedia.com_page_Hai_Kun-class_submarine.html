Hai Kun -class submarine

The Hai Kun-class submarine is a diesel-electric attack submarine class developed indigenously by Taiwan for the Republic of China Navy, representing the nation's first domestically constructed submarines to enhance underwater deterrence capabilities.[1][2] The lead boat, ROCS Hai Kun (SS-711), was launched on September 28, 2023, at the CSBC Corporation shipyard in Kaohsiung following keel-laying in late 2021, with construction achieving approximately 40% domestic content in critical technologies.[3][2][4] This prototype incorporates updated design elements derived from the Dutch Zwaardvis-class (Taiwan's existing Hai Lung class) while integrating modern features such as a hybrid hull structure and advanced sensors, though full specifications remain classified.[5][2] Hai Kun commenced sea trials in June 2025, including initial autonomous harbor maneuvers and open-water testing, signaling progress toward operational entry potentially by late 2025 or 2026.[1][3] The class forms part of the Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program, aiming to produce up to eight vessels at a program cost exceeding US$15 billion, to address Taiwan's historical submarine shortfall and counterbalance regional threats through asymmetric naval power.[6][4]

Development

Program Background and Strategic Rationale

The Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program, producing the Hai Kun-class submarines, was formalized by the Republic of China Navy in 2014 to address Taiwan's acute undersea warfare deficiencies amid escalating threats from the People's Liberation Army Navy (PLAN).[7] With no operational submarines following the decommissioning of two aging U.S.-sourced Guppy-class boats in the early 2020s, Taiwan pursued indigenous construction of eight diesel-electric attack submarines at the CSBC Corporation facility in Kaohsiung, driven by repeated failures to acquire foreign platforms due to Chinese diplomatic coercion on suppliers like the United States and Netherlands.[7] The lead vessel, Hai Kun (SS-711), was launched on September 28, 2023, marking the program's transition from design to production.[8] Strategically, the initiative stems from Taiwan's geographic vulnerabilities in the Taiwan Strait, a narrow chokepoint approximately 130 kilometers wide at its narrowest, where any Chinese amphibious assault would expose surface fleets to submerged threats.[9] Submarines enable asymmetric denial capabilities, interdicting invasion convoys and supply lines with torpedoes and anti-ship missiles while evading PLAN anti-submarine assets, which remain concentrated on offensive rather than defensive postures.[10] This undersea edge compensates for Taiwan's numerical disadvantages against China's expanding surface fleet, projected to include over 400 hulls by 2030, by raising the operational costs and risks of coercion or blockade.[9] Taiwanese administrations under Presidents Ma Ying-jeou, Tsai Ing-wen, and Lai Ching-te have framed the IDS as essential for defense self-reliance, reducing vulnerability to external arms embargoes and integrating local technologies like combat systems to sustain wartime operations.[10] The program's estimated $8.8 billion investment underscores its priority in national defense budgets, prioritizing deterrence through credible undersea threats over symmetric force matching, though analysts note its modest scale limits transformative impact absent broader integration with mines, missiles, and allied support.[9][11]

Design Phase and Technological Integration

The design phase of the Hai Kun-class submarine formed a core element of Taiwan's Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program, formalized in 2014 to produce eight diesel-electric attack submarines amid constraints from foreign arms embargoes.[7] Development emphasized domestic engineering led by the National Chung-Shan Institute of Science and Technology (NCSIST) and CSBC Corporation, with conceptual work advancing through feasibility studies in the early 2010s before detailed blueprints were finalized around 2020.[4] The prototype's keel was laid in 2016, reflecting a phased approach that integrated modular construction techniques to accelerate prototyping while building local expertise.[12] Technological integration prioritized reliability and interoperability over experimental features, opting for a conventional diesel-electric propulsion system without air-independent propulsion (AIP) to mitigate risks in Taiwan's nascent submarine industry.[2] The hull employs a hybrid configurationâ€”a single pressure hull amidships with double-hull sections fore and aftâ€”enhancing buoyancy control and internal volume for batteries and crew accommodations, akin to proven designs like the Dutch Zwaardvis class.[13] Maneuverability is augmented by an X-shaped stern rudder arrangement, tested for submerged stability during harbor trials commencing in 2025.[14] Key subsystems drew on foreign partnerships to compensate for indigenous gaps, including U.S.-supplied sonar arrays, combat management systems, electro-optical masts, and Mk 48 heavyweight torpedoes, ensuring compatibility with allied munitions and sensors.[15] NCSIST contributed a bespoke torpedo countermeasure suite, featuring acoustic decoys and evasion algorithms integrated into the submarine's combat architecture for defense against anti-submarine threats.[4] This selective assimilation of proven external technologies with homegrown structural and auxiliary systems underscored a pragmatic strategy, yielding a 70-meter, approximately 2,500-ton submerged displacement platform operational by late 2025 following sea acceptance trials.[16][4]

Construction and Testing Milestones

Construction of the lead Hai Kun-class submarine, designated SS-711 and named Hai Kun (Narwhal), began at the China Shipbuilding Corporation (CSBC) facility in Kaohsiung with a keel-laying ceremony on November 16, 2021.[17] The prototype vessel was publicly unveiled and launched during a ceremony on September 28, 2023, marking a key advancement in Taiwan's Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program.[18] Post-launch outfitting and initial testing proceeded through 2024, with the submarine achieving completion of critical pre-sea trials phases by early 2025.[19] Harbour trials commenced in mid-June 2025, focusing on systems integration and basic functionality checks in controlled waters off Kaohsiung.[20] The first Sea Acceptance Trial (SAT) occurred on June 17, 2025, involving initial surface navigation and successfully concluding the maiden sea outing.[1] Follow-on surface trials progressed, with the third iteration completed on July 3, 2025, but subsequent phases revealed technical challenges requiring dry-dock interventions.[21] Submerged testing remained pending as of September 2025, deferred until safety assurances were verified amid ongoing adjustments.[22] The SAT schedule missed its September 2025 completion target due to these setbacks, postponing formal delivery to the Republic of China Navy beyond the original late-2025 goal.[23] In June 2025, Taiwan authorized construction of two enhanced Hai Kun-class follow-on submarines, signaling program continuity despite prototype delays.[24]

Design Characteristics

Hull and Displacement

The Hai Kun-class submarine employs a composite hull configuration, with a single pressure hull in the central section and double-hull arrangements at the bow and stern sections to optimize buoyancy control and structural integrity.[13] This hybrid design draws visual and conceptual influences from the Dutch Walrus-class submarines, facilitating enhanced hydrodynamic performance and internal volume utilization.[5] The vessel measures approximately 70 meters in length and 8 meters in beam, contributing to its maneuverability in littoral environments.[4][25] Submerged displacement is estimated at around 2,500 tons, positioning it as a conventional diesel-electric attack submarine comparable in size to contemporary regional designs.[25][4][26] The hull is engineered to withstand operational depths up to 1,380 feet, supporting extended submerged operations.[25]

Propulsion System

The Hai Kun-class submarines utilize a conventional diesel-electric propulsion system with a single-shaft configuration, relying on diesel generators to charge high-capacity batteries that power electric motors for submerged propulsion.[14][24] This setup enables battery operation without frequent surfacing for air intake, though the design prioritizes reliability over extended submerged endurance.[2] The system reportedly incorporates two MTU 12V 4000 series diesel engines, which drive a seven-bladed skewback propeller for efficient hydrodynamic performance.[27][4] Electric generators paired with the engines support battery recharging, with the overall architecture drawing from established diesel-electric principles rather than advanced alternatives.[28] The prototype lacks air-independent propulsion (AIP), a deliberate choice to simplify initial development and testing amid supply chain constraints and technological integration challenges.[2] Speculation regarding lithium-ion batteries persists but remains unconfirmed in official disclosures.[24] Initial harbor-based propulsion trials for the lead boat, Hai Kun (SS-711), occurred from June 17 to 19, 2025, confirming activation and basic functionality of the diesel-electric components without external assistance.[1][14] These tests focused on steering integration, electrical stability, and propulsion fine-tuning prior to open-sea evaluations.[3]

Armament Capabilities

The Hai Kun-class submarine features six 533 mm torpedo tubes as its primary armament system, enabling the launch of heavyweight torpedoes and anti-ship missiles.[28][2] These tubes are positioned forward in the pressure hull, consistent with conventional diesel-electric attack submarine designs.[28] The class is equipped with U.S.-supplied Mk 48 Mod 6 Advanced Technology (ADCAP) heavyweight torpedoes, which provide wire-guided, acoustic-homing capabilities for engaging surface ships and submarines with a range exceeding 30 nautical miles and high-explosive warheads.[2][29][30] These torpedoes represent a key export from the United States to enhance Taiwan's underwater strike precision and lethality against regional naval threats.[30] In addition to torpedoes, the torpedo tubes support submarine-launched UGM-84L Harpoon Block II anti-ship missiles, offering over-the-horizon strike capability with a range of approximately 67 nautical miles and active radar homing for targeting enemy vessels.[28][31] The Hai Kun's design allows for a total weapons loadout exceeding 28 units, surpassing the capacity of Taiwan's older Chien Lung-class submarines and enabling sustained multi-target engagements.[32][31] The tubes may also accommodate naval mines for area denial operations, though specific integration details remain classified or unconfirmed in public disclosures.[28] This armament suite emphasizes asymmetric warfare potential, prioritizing stealthy, long-range precision strikes over volume firepower.[2]

Sensors and Electronics Suite

The Hai Kun-class submarine incorporates a modern sensor and electronics suite primarily sourced from the United States to enhance detection, targeting, and situational awareness capabilities. Key components include advanced sonar systems, electro-optical masts, and a combat management system designed for integration with diesel-electric operations.[15][9] The sonar array features a Northrop Grumman-supplied bow-mounted cylindrical sonar for active and passive detection of underwater threats, complemented by a flank-mounted passive array to improve long-range acoustic surveillance and reduce noise interference from the hull. Electronic support measures (ESM) are integrated to detect and classify electromagnetic emissions from surface vessels and aircraft, providing non-acoustic cues for stealthy threat avoidance.[33][14] Mast systems include U.S.-provided photonics masts with high-resolution electro-optical and infrared sensors for periscope-independent surface search and targeting, minimizing exposure during surfaced or shallow-depth operations. These are paired with an advanced integrated periscope system for traditional optical observation, supporting fire control for torpedoes and missiles.[15][13] The combat management system, supplied by Lockheed Martin, fuses data from sonar, ESM, and mast sensors into a centralized interface for real-time decision-making, enabling automated threat tracking and weapon allocation. Electronic warfare capabilities encompass decoy launchers and jamming systems to counter anti-submarine warfare assets, though specific emitter details remain classified.[34][24]

Strategic and Operational Role

Deterrence Against Regional Threats

The Hai Kun-class submarines form a core element of Taiwan's strategy to deter potential invasion by the People's Republic of China (PRC), which maintains territorial claims over the island and has intensified military exercises simulating blockades and amphibious assaults. These diesel-electric platforms, with their stealthy design and endurance for operations in the confined waters of the Taiwan Strait, enable Taiwan to threaten PRC surface fleets, including amphibious transports and logistics vessels critical for a cross-strait operation. By posing a persistent underwater hazard, the submarines increase the operational risks and costs associated with any PRC attempt at forcible unification, thereby contributing to a "porcupine" defense posture that prioritizes asymmetric denial over symmetric confrontation.[8][35][36] Taiwanese military planners view the submarines as vital for interdicting PRC invasion forces during the vulnerable initial phases of an assault, leveraging capabilities such as mine-laying in chokepoints like PRC ports and ambushes on troop carriers to disrupt momentum and force attrition. Admiral Huang Shu-kuang, who oversaw the Indigenous Defense Submarine program, described the Hai Kun class as a "strategic deterrent" capable of safeguarding maritime domains and countering invasions exploiting Taiwan's geographic vulnerabilities. The integration of advanced combat management systems and vertical launch systems for anti-ship missiles further amplifies this deterrent effect, allowing submerged strikes that evade PRC anti-submarine warfare assets concentrated on surface threats.[18][9][37] With the prototype Hai Kun (Narwhal) entering sea trials in April 2025 following its keel-laying in 2020, the programâ€”backed by an $8.8 billion allocationâ€”signals Taiwan's commitment to indigenous capabilities that enhance survivability against PRC numerical superiority in surface combatants. This underwater force multiplier supports broader deterrence by complicating PRC command of the sea, potentially delaying or deterring escalation amid observed PRC naval expansions, including over 60 submarines of their own. Proponents argue that even a modest fleet of eight planned Hai Kun vessels could impose unacceptable losses on an invading armada, aligning with assessments that submarines provide asymmetric leverage in regional contingencies.[38][10][37]

Asymmetric Warfare Applications

The Hai Kun-class submarines enhance Taiwan's asymmetric warfare posture by enabling stealthy, high-impact operations in contested littoral environments, where numerical inferiority to the People's Liberation Army Navy (PLAN) demands disproportionate force application. Their diesel-electric propulsion, incorporating air-independent propulsion (AIP) systems derived from indigenous development and foreign technical assistance, allows extended submerged endurance for ambush tactics against superior surface fleets, complicating PLAN amphibious operations across the Taiwan Strait.[9][39] This aligns with Taiwan's "porcupine strategy," which prioritizes sea denial to impose attrition costs on invaders through unpredictable, low-signature strikes rather than symmetric fleet engagements.[39][40] Equipped with torpedoes and potentially vertical-launch anti-ship missiles, the class targets vulnerable assets like landing ships and replenishment vessels during invasion scenarios, leveraging acoustic stealth to evade PLAN anti-submarine warfare (ASW) assets concentrated on open-ocean threats.[41][28] Taiwanese defense officials have described the submarines as "important equipment for developing asymmetric warfare capabilities," emphasizing their role in disrupting supply lines and creating uncertainty for blockade or crossing attempts.[42] In simulations and analyses, such platforms could sink multiple high-value targets per sortie, amplifying defensive effects without requiring Taiwan to match PLAN's 70-plus submarines.[35][39] Critics, including some U.S. analysts, question the submarines' alignment with pure asymmetry due to their high unit costâ€”estimated at over $1.5 billion eachâ€”and limited production scale, arguing that cheaper unmanned or missile-based systems might yield higher returns against PLAN ASW proliferation.[10][43] Nonetheless, the platform's integration of advanced sonar and combat management systems supports networked operations with surface and air assets, enabling layered deterrence that exploits geographic chokepoints like the Strait's shallow waters for evasion and repositioning.[9] The prototype's launch on September 28, 2023, underscored this doctrinal shift, with President Tsai Ing-wen highlighting its contribution to self-reliant defense amid escalating cross-strait tensions.[8]

Integration with Broader Naval Strategy

The Hai Kun-class submarines are designed to integrate into the Republic of China Navy's (ROCN) asymmetric defense posture, which prioritizes stealthy, high-impact capabilities to counter the People's Liberation Army Navy's (PLAN) numerical and technological advantages in a potential cross-strait conflict. This approach emphasizes anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) tactics, where submarines serve as a "porcupine" element to impose costs on invading forces through underwater interdiction, rather than symmetric fleet engagements.[9][39] The vessels' diesel-electric propulsion and advanced sonar systems enable covert operations in littoral waters, complementing surface combatants like the Tuo Chiang-class corvettes and shore-based anti-ship missiles to disrupt PLAN amphibious assaults and logistics.[8] In operational terms, the Hai Kun class enhances ROCN's undersea warfare domain by providing persistent surveillance, mine-laying, and precision strikes with torpedoes and submarine-launched missiles, integrating via networked combat systems for real-time data sharing with air and surface assets.[14] This aligns with Taiwan's broader naval modernization under the Indigenous Defense Submarine program, aiming for a fleet of up to eight submarines by the early 2030s to replace aging World War II-era vessels and achieve initial operational capability for three boats by 2027.[39] Such integration supports guerrilla-style naval tactics, including blockade evasion and targeting high-value PLAN units, thereby extending Taiwan's strategic depth beyond its immediate coastline.[8] The submarines' role extends to multinational interoperability, incorporating U.S.-sourced components like combat management and electro-optical systems, which facilitate potential coordination with allied forces in the Indo-Pacific for collective deterrence.[15] However, effective integration hinges on overcoming domestic training and maintenance gaps, as the ROCN transitions from reliance on foreign arms to indigenous sustainment amid supply chain vulnerabilities.[10]

Procurement and Fleet Expansion

Prototype Status and Sea Trials

The prototype Hai Kun-class submarine, designated SS-711 Hai Kun and constructed by the CSBC Corporation at its Kaohsiung shipyard, represents Taiwan's first domestically built attack submarine under the Indigenous Defense Submarine program. Keel laying occurred in late 2023, with construction progressing through 2024 amid supply chain integration from international partners. As of October 2025, the vessel remains in the pre-commissioning phase, having transitioned from harbor-based fit-out to active testing, though full operational certification has been deferred due to unresolved technical integration issues.[44] Sea trials commenced on June 17, 2025, beginning with propulsion system validation and autonomous surface movement within Kaohsiung Port to assess diesel-electric powertrain performance and basic maneuvering.[1] [14] Initial outings confirmed engine reliability under load but highlighted needs for adjustments in hull trim and auxiliary systems. Subsequent surface navigation trials, numbering four to five as planned, evaluated stability, speed, and endurance on open water, culminating in a third sailing test by early July 2025 that cleared prerequisites for submergence.[3] [21] Diving trials followed in phases, achieving shallow immersion to 50 meters in early September 2025, progressing to deeper 200-meter tests shortly thereafter to verify pressure hull integrity and ballast control.[30] Preparations for full-depth evaluations and weapons systems integration continued into late September, despite the abrupt resignation of key program advisors, which did not halt operations.[45] [46] Progress has encountered delays from technical hurdles in sensor fusion and propulsion fine-tuning, extending dockside intervals and missing the September 30, 2025, target for sea acceptance completion and handover to the Republic of China Navy.[47] [23] Defense Ministry officials have characterized on-time delivery as "extremely challenging," emphasizing extended testing to ensure reliability, while the contractor confirmed ongoing adjustments without specifying resolution timelines.[48] As of mid-October 2025, trials persist with focus on iterative validations, underscoring the prototype's role in refining design baselines for follow-on units amid Taiwan's asymmetric deterrence priorities.[48]

Planned Follow-On Submarines

The Republic of China Navy's Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program, of which the Hai Kun serves as the prototype, envisions the construction of up to eight diesel-electric attack submarines in total to modernize its aging undersea fleet.[10] Following the Hai Kun's anticipated commissioning, subsequent boats are intended to incorporate refinements based on sea trial data and operational feedback, potentially including enhanced battery systems, improved sonar integration, and streamlined production processes to address prototype-specific challenges.[49] In June 2025, Taiwan's National Chung-Shan Institute of Science and Technology (NCSIST) and CSBC Corporation signed a contract for the construction of two improved Hai Kun-class submarines, marking the transition from prototype to serial production.[24] These follow-on vessels, designated as the second and third in the class, are projected to enhance patrol endurance and combat capabilities through iterative design upgrades, with delivery timelines aligned to achieve operational status by the late 2020s.[48] The Ministry of National Defense has targeted a fleet of four submarines by 2027, comprising the Hai Kun prototype, this second IDS boat, and the two existing Hai Lung-class vessels, to bolster immediate deterrence amid regional tensions.[48] Longer-term expansion aims to complete the full complement of eight submarines, with the remaining five boats planned for phased procurement contingent on budgetary approvals and supply chain stability, particularly for imported components like combat systems from European partners.[50] Production scaling at CSBC's Kaohsiung facilities is expected to accelerate post-2027, leveraging lessons from the prototype to reduce per-unit costs and construction timelines from the initial five-year build period.[44] However, officials have noted potential delays due to technical integration hurdles and geopolitical pressures on foreign suppliers, emphasizing the need for domestic alternatives to mitigate risks.[48]

Controversies and Criticisms

Technical Challenges and Reliability Concerns

The Hai Kun-class submarine program, Taiwan's first effort to indigenously design and construct modern diesel-electric submarines, has encountered significant technical hurdles stemming from the nation's limited prior experience in submarine manufacturing and the complexities of integrating components sourced from multiple international partners. During harbor acceptance tests in February 2025, the prototype Hai Kun (SS-711) experienced power supply malfunctions, which delayed subsequent phases of testing and highlighted vulnerabilities in the electrical systems critical for propulsion and onboard operations.[44] These issues necessitated extended dry-dock repairs, underscoring the challenges of achieving system reliability in a platform described by observers as a "United Nations of systems" due to its reliance on disparate foreign technologies, including engines from Germany, combat systems from the United States, and sensors from various suppliers.[21] Sea trials, initially planned to commence in early 2025, have been repeatedly postponed owing to unresolved technical deficiencies, with the builder, China Shipbuilding Corporation (CSBC), acknowledging in September 2025 that completion before the targeted November delivery date would be "extremely challenging." Watertight integrity tests and underwater equipment inspections were completed, but persistent integration problemsâ€”such as synchronizing propulsion with auxiliary systemsâ€”have impeded progress toward full operational validation, including shallow dives to 50 meters and surface navigation trials. Former Republic of China Navy adviser Kuo Hsi has attributed these delays to ongoing technical faults that could extend timelines well beyond November, raising questions about the prototype's structural and systemic robustness under real-world conditions.[23][21][48] Reliability concerns extend to the unproven nature of key indigenous elements, such as the hull fabrication and air-independent propulsion (AIP) adaptations, which must withstand prolonged submerged operations in contested waters without prior large-scale testing in Taiwan's domestic shipyards. The program's dependence on imported subsystems introduces risks of compatibility failures and supply chain disruptions, as evidenced by earlier delays in optronic mast deliveries, potentially compromising stealth and sensor performance essential for survivability against advanced anti-submarine warfare threats. Defense analysts note that while the diesel-electric configuration with potential lithium-ion battery enhancements offers tactical advantages, the absence of a mature testing regime could result in undetected flaws manifesting during fleet expansion, necessitating rigorous post-delivery evaluations to mitigate operational downtime.[47][48]

Cost Overruns and Resource Allocation Debates

The Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS) program, encompassing the Hai Kun-class prototype, has prompted debates over projected costs and resource priorities, driven by the challenges of domestic production without prior experience in submarine manufacturing. The prototype Hai Kun incurred construction costs of approximately NT$26 billion (US$812 million), reflecting initial investments in untested supply chains and imported components.[55] For the envisioned seven follow-on submarines, slated for production from 2025 to 2038 in a "two-three-two" batch sequence, the Ministry of National Defense projected a total expenditure of NT$284 billion (US$8.88 billion), or about NT$40.6 billion per vessel, inclusive of upgrades, training facilities, and support infrastructure.[56][57] Critics, including opposition legislators, have contested these figures, arguing that potential technical hurdlesâ€”such as integration of foreign-sourced systems and domestic fabrication limitationsâ€”could inflate totals beyond NT$300 billion, drawing parallels to cost escalations in other nations' submarine programs.[58] In April 2024, Defense Minister Chiu Kuo-cheng rebutted media assertions that per-submarine costs would "soar," emphasizing that estimates accounted for economies of scale absent in the prototype phase and ongoing inflation adjustments.[59] Concerns intensified over the Executive Yuan's August 2024 approval of the mass-production budget prior to the prototype's sea trials, with analysts warning that undiscovered flaws could necessitate retrofits, thereby misrepresenting fiscal commitments and diverting resources from immediate asymmetric capabilities like missiles or drones.[60][61] Legislative scrutiny has manifested in resource allocation constraints, as opposition parties (Kuomintang and Taiwan People's Party) in January 2025 approved a freeze on NT$1 billionâ€”half of the proposed 2025 IDS continuation fundingâ€”conditioning release on successful completion of the Hai Kun's sea acceptance tests.[62][63] Proponents of the freeze cited fiscal accountability amid broader defense budget pressures, including inflation-driven rises in procurement and local opposition to military infrastructure.[64] Detractors, including ruling Democratic Progressive Party lawmakers, countered that such delays undermine deterrence against People's Liberation Army naval expansion, potentially prioritizing short-term savings over long-term strategic autonomy in a resource-constrained environment where Taiwan's overall defense spending targets 3 percent of GDP.[63][65] These disputes highlight tensions between indigenous self-reliance and verifiable performance benchmarks, with no confirmed overruns to date but persistent risks amplified by the program's novelty and geopolitical urgency.

Geopolitical Implications and Opposing Viewpoints

The Hai Kun-class submarines represent a strategic effort by Taiwan to achieve greater self-reliance in underwater warfare, potentially complicating Chinese amphibious operations across the Taiwan Strait by enabling sea denial tactics that target vulnerable supply lines and landing forces.[9] This capability could raise the operational costs for a People's Liberation Army (PLA) invasion, as diesel-electric submarines like the Hai Kun are inherently stealthy and suited for littoral environments, where they might evade detection longer than surface vessels.[11] Proponents argue that even a small fleet of eight planned vessels would force China to allocate significant anti-submarine warfare resources, thereby enhancing deterrence without relying solely on external allies.[10] From Beijing's perspective, the program exacerbates cross-strait tensions, framing it as evidence of Taiwan's pursuit of de facto independence under the Democratic Progressive Party, which China has repeatedly condemned as provocative militarization that undermines peaceful reunification.[8] Chinese state media and officials have historically viewed Taiwan's defense indigenization, including submarines, as a U.S.-backed challenge to its sovereignty claims, potentially accelerating PLA naval expansions in response, such as increased deployment of Type 039A Yuan-class submarines for regional dominance.[66] Opposing viewpoints within Taiwan highlight the program's geopolitical risks, asserting that the high visibility and costsâ€”estimated at over NT$80 billion (approximately US$2.5 billion) for the prototype aloneâ€”divert resources from more survivable asymmetric tools like sea mines, anti-ship missiles, and unmanned systems, which could impose higher attrition on invading forces at lower expense.[67] Legislative opposition, including from Kuomintang lawmakers, has frozen portions of the 2025 budget pending sea trial successes, arguing that delays and technical issues undermine confidence in the fleet's timely operationalization against a numerically superior PLA Navy.[62] External analysts, including former U.S. officials, echo this by questioning the submarines' alignment with Taiwan's needs for rapid, distributed lethality over capital-intensive platforms vulnerable to preemptive strikes.[68] Despite these critiques, supporters maintain that submarines offer unique persistence in contested waters, providing a hedge against potential U.S. intervention hesitancy in a crisis.[69]
