Orthopoxvirus

Orthopoxvirus is a genus of viruses in the family Poxviridae and subfamily Chordopoxvirinae. Vertebrates, primarily mammals including humans, serve as natural hosts. There are 13 species in this genus.[1][2] Diseases associated with this genus include smallpox, cowpox, horsepox, camelpox, and mpox.[3][1] The most widely known member of the genus is Variola virus, which causes smallpox. It was eradicated globally by 1977, through the use of Vaccinia virus as a vaccine. The most recently described species is the Borealpox virus (formerly Alaskapox virus), first isolated in 2015 and renamed in 2024.[4]

Microbiology

Structure

Orthopoxviruses are enveloped with brick-shaped geometries and virion dimensions around 200 nm wide and 250 nm long.[3]

Genome

Member viruses have linear double-stranded DNA genomes around 170â€“250 kb in length.[3]

Lifecycle

Viral replication is cytoplasmic. Entry into the host cell is achieved by attachment of the viral proteins to host glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), which mediate cellular endocytosis of the virus. Fusion of the viral envelope with the plasma membrane releases the viral core into the host cytoplasm. Expression of early-phase genes by viral RNA polymerase begins at 30 minutes after infection. The viral core is completely uncoated as early expression ends, releasing the viral genome into the cytoplasm. At this point, intermediate genes are expressed, triggering genomic DNA replication by the viral DNA polymerase about 100 minutes post-infection. Replication follows the DNA strand displacement model. Late genes are expressed from 140 min to 48 hours postinfection, producing all viral structural proteins. Assembly of progeny virions begins in cytoplasmic viral factories, producing a spherical immature particle. This virus particle matures into the brick-shaped intracellular mature virion, which can be released upon cell lysis, or can acquire a second membrane from the Golgi apparatus and bud as extracellular enveloped virions. In this latter case, the virion is transported to the plasma membrane via microtubules.[3]

Transmission

Natural hosts of orthopoxviruses are various mammals, including rodents. Member viruses are transmitted by respiratory droplets, contact, and zoonosis.[3]

Distribution

Orthopoxviruses exhibit varied geographic distributions. Mpox virus is endemic to Central and West Africa but has spread to multiple countries worldwide following outbreaks starting in 2022, with ongoing cases reported globally as of October 2025.[5] Cowpox virus is primarily found in Europe and parts of Asia. Buffalopox virus occurs mainly in Asia and the Middle East. Camelpox virus is endemic to regions in the Middle East, North Africa, and Asia where camels are prevalent. Ectromelia virus, causing mousepox, was originally identified in Europe and is now mostly confined to laboratory settings worldwide. Vaccinia virus is maintained in laboratories globally but has established wild populations in South America, particularly Brazil.[6] Some orthopoxviruses, including mpox, cowpox, and buffalopox viruses, have the ability to infect non-reservoir species. Others, such as ectromelia and camelpox viruses, are highly host-specific. Vaccinia virus has a very wide host range. Vaccine-derived vaccinia has been found replicating in the wild in Brazil, where it has caused infections in rodents, cattle, and humans.[7] Following the eradication of variola virus, camelpox has become one of the most economically important Orthopoxvirus infections, because many subsistence-level nomadic communities depend heavily on camels.[8]

Evolution

Among the path of evolution of the Orthopoxvirus species, many genes are truncated (but still functional), fragmented, or lost. Cowpox strains tend to have the most intact genes. Recent phylogenetic analyses divide the genus into two major clades: an Old World clade (including cowpox, vaccinia, variola, monkeypox, camelpox, taterapox) and a New World clade (including raccoonpox, skunkpox, volepox, ectromelia). Predicting phylogeny by sequence or by gene content can produce somewhat different results, though both approaches generally support these clades.[10][11]

Some differences in trees are attributed to passage procedures in producing vaccinia strains. The Modified vaccinia Ankara strain has extensive gene loss due to in vitro passage, while horsepox, a vaccinia-like strain from a natural outbreak, has fewer losses.[10]

Infection in humans

Zoonoses

Following the eradication of the human-specific variola virus (smallpox), all human Orthopoxvirus infections are zoonoses.[12] Mpox occurs naturally only in Africa, particularly in the Democratic Republic of the Congo.[13] However, human and prairie dog cases have occurred in the US due to contact with animals imported from Ghana,[14] while in May 2022 an outbreak of mpox began spreading globally. Borealpox virus (formerly Alaskapox virus) has caused at least six human cases in Alaska, United States, from 2020 to 2023, including one fatal infection in an immunocompromised individual.[15] Cowpox only occurs in Europe and adjacent Russian states, and despite its name, occurs only rarely in cattle. One common host is the domestic cat, from which human infections are most often acquired.[16][17] Cowpox virus has also infected a variety of animals in European zoos, such as elephants, resulting in human infection.[18]

Laboratory transmission

Aerosols of concentrated virus may result in Orthopoxvirus infection, especially in unimmunized individuals.[19] In addition, needle sticks with concentrated virus or scratches from infected animals may result in local infection of the skin even in immunized individuals. Cowpox infection in Europe is an occupational hazard for veterinary workers, and to a lesser extent, farm workers.[17]

Signs and symptoms

The initial symptoms of Orthopoxvirus infection include fever, malaise, head and body aches, and occasionally vomiting. With the exception of mpox infection, one lesion is the norm, although satellite lesions may be produced by accidental autoinoculation. Individual lesions, surrounded by inflammatory tissue, develop and progress through macules, papules, vesicles, and pustules, and eventually become dry crusts. (Lesions alone are not diagnostic for Orthopoxvirus infection and may be mistaken for zoonotic Parapoxvirus infections, anthrax or Herpesvirus infections.[17]) With severe infections, severe edema and erythema may affect large areas of the body. Encephalitis (alteration of mental status and focal neurologic deficits), myelitis (upper- and lower-motor neuron dysfunction, sensory level, and bowel and bladder dysfunction), or both may result from Orthopoxvirus infection. Rarely, orthopoxviruses may be detected in cerebrospinal fluid. Regarding specific Orthopoxvirus infections, human mpox most resembles mild smallpox.[13] Human cowpox is a relatively severe localized infection. A survey of 54 cases reported three cases of generalized infection, including one death.[17]

Treatment

Vaccinia immune globulin intravenous (VIGIV) may be administered to infected individuals with severe orthopoxvirus infections. VIGIV is an isotonic sterile solution of the immunoglobulin fraction of plasma from persons vaccinated with vaccinia virus. It is effective for treatment of eczema vaccinatum and certain cases of progressive vaccinia, and may be used for severe mpox or other orthopoxvirus complications. However, VIGIV is contraindicated for the treatment of vaccinial keratitis. VIGIV is recommended for severe cases if the patient is extremely ill or has a serious underlying disease. VIGIV provides no benefit in the treatment of postvaccinal encephalitis and has no role in the treatment of smallpox. As of 2025, VIGIV is available through the CDC's Expanded Access Investigational New Drug (EA-IND) protocol for treatment of orthopoxvirus infections; supplies are limited and reserved for severe clinical manifestations. The recommended dosage is 0.6 ml/kg of body weight, administered intramuscularly as early as possible after symptom onset. Doses can be repeated at 2â€“3 day intervals until recovery.[20] Certain antiviral compounds such as tecovirimat (TPOXX, ST-246)[21] have been reported to be 100% active against vaccinia virus or other orthopoxviruses in vitro and among test animals. Tecovirimat was approved by the FDA in 2018 for the treatment of smallpox and is available for orthopoxvirus infections, including mpox, through the CDC.[22] Another example is brincidofovir. In June 2021, the FDA approved this drug for the treatment of smallpox in humans, making it the first drug approved for an effectively extinct mechanism of action. The decision followed a priority review by the agency, motivated by growing concern of potential bioweapon development. Since the target virus is eradicated the efficacy could not be directly verified but was inferred via proxy, animal survival following infection by related species of Orthopoxvirus. In contrast, safety data was available from trials of the drug in treating Cytomegalovirus infections in humans.[23] Imatinib, a compound approved by the FDA for cancer treatment, has shown preclinical activity against orthopoxviruses but is not currently recommended or evaluated in standard treatment protocols.[24]

Lab Synthesis

In 2017, researchers at the University of Alberta, led by David Evans, synthesized horsepox virus (HPXV) from chemically synthesized DNA fragments as a platform for developing a safer and more effective smallpox vaccine.[3] The work demonstrated protection against lethal vaccinia challenge in mice and highlighted potential applications in oncolytic virotherapy for cancer treatment, though the primary focus was vaccine development.[3] The synthesis, costing approximately US$100,000 using mail-order DNA, sparked significant controversy over biosecurity risks and the need for enhanced oversight of dual-use research involving extinct pathogens.[1]
