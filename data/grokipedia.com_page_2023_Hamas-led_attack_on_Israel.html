October 7 attacks

The October 7 attacks consisted of coordinated incursions into southern Israel launched by Hamas and other Palestinian armed groups from the Gaza Strip on October 7, 2023, involving thousands of militants who breached the border fence using vehicles, paragliders, and motorized boats, while simultaneously firing thousands of rockets.[1][2] The assaults targeted civilian communities in kibbutzim and moshavim, a music festival near Re'im, and military outposts, resulting in the deliberate killing of approximately 1,200 individualsâ€”over 800 of them civiliansâ€”and the abduction of around 250 hostages, many of whom were taken into Gaza.[3][4] Hamas's military wing, the Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, led the operation, with participation from groups including Palestinian Islamic Jihad and others, in what has been documented as involving numerous war crimes and crimes against humanity, such as summary executions, sexual violence, and mutilation of victims.[2][1] The attacks unfolded rapidly starting around 6:30 a.m., with militants overrunning border defenses amid significant Israeli intelligence and military preparedness failures, enabling unchecked atrocities at sites like Kibbutz Be'eri and the Nova music festival, where over 360 festivalgoers were killed.[4] Perpetrators employed tactics including house-to-house searches for civilians, use of grenades and firearms in confined spaces, and burning of homes and vehicles, leading to widespread destruction and the highest single-day death toll for Israel since its founding.[2] The operation, codenamed "Al-Aqsa Flood" by Hamas, aimed to disrupt Israeli security and provoke a broader conflict, succeeding in drawing an immediate Israeli military response that escalated into the Gaza war.[1] Israel's counteroffensive, Operation Swords of Iron, involved airstrikes, ground invasions, and efforts to dismantle Hamas infrastructure, rescuing some hostages while over 100 remained in captivity as of late 2025, amid ongoing hostilities and international scrutiny over the conflict's conduct.[3] The attacks have been characterized by U.S. and Israeli officials as the deadliest terrorist incident in Israel's history and one of the most lethal against civilians globally in recent decades, prompting debates on border security lapses and the ideological motivations rooted in Islamist militancy.[4]

Prelude and Planning

Historical and Ideological Context

Hamas, the Islamist organization responsible for the October 7, 2023, attacks, originated as an offshoot of the Palestinian branch of the Muslim Brotherhood, a transnational Sunni Islamist network founded in Egypt in 1928 that emphasizes the implementation of sharia law and resistance to secular governance.[5] The group formally established itself in December 1987 amid the First Intifada, a Palestinian uprising against Israeli occupation that began in December 1987 following a fatal traffic incident in Gaza, positioning itself as an alternative to the secular nationalist Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) by advocating armed jihad over negotiation.[6] Its founding charter, issued on August 18, 1988, explicitly rejects Israel's existence, declaring that "Israel will exist and will continue to exist until Islam will obliterate it" and framing the conflict as a religious duty to reclaim all of historic Palestineâ€”from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Seaâ€”as an Islamic waqf (endowment) consecrated for future Muslim generations.[7][8] Ideologically, Hamas draws on Salafi-jihadist principles inherited from the Muslim Brotherhood, viewing the liberation of Palestine not as a territorial dispute amenable to compromise but as an eternal religious imperative requiring holy war (jihad) against Jews and infidels, with antisemitic tropes including references to the fabricated Protocols of the Elders of Zion to allege a global Jewish conspiracy.[6] This worldview inherently opposes recognition of Israel, as articulated in the charter's assertion that peace initiatives like the Oslo Accordsâ€”signed between Israel and the PLO in 1993 to establish interim Palestinian self-governanceâ€”are "treacherous" capitulations that betray Islamic principles.[7] Hamas has consistently undermined such processes through campaigns of suicide bombings and rocket fire; for instance, during the Second Intifada (2000â€“2005), it claimed responsibility for numerous attacks that killed hundreds of Israeli civilians, derailing negotiations even after PLO leader Yasser Arafat rejected Israeli offers at the 2000 Camp David Summit, which included proposals for Palestinian statehood on over 90% of the West Bank and Gaza with land swaps.[6][9] In Gaza, where Hamas seized control in a violent 2007 coup against Fatah (the PLO's dominant faction) after winning legislative elections in 2006 on promises of resistance, the group has perpetuated cycles of aggression, launching thousands of rockets into Israel in conflicts of 2008â€“2009, 2012, 2014, and 2021, often justified as retaliation but rooted in ideological rejectionism rather than response to specific triggers.[5] External patrons like Iran have bolstered this stance, providing Hamas with an estimated $70â€“100 million annually in funding, weapons, and training since the early 1990s, enabling militarization despite ideological Sunni-Shiite differences and aligning with Tehran's anti-Israel axis.[10][11] This historical pattern of doctrinal intransigenceâ€”prioritizing maximalist goals over governance or coexistenceâ€”culminated in the October 7 operation, codenamed "Al-Aqsa Flood," which Hamas framed as a defensive jihad but executed as a premeditated assault on civilians and military targets to provoke escalation.[6][12]

Hamas Preparation and Operational Planning

Hamas's military wing, the Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, conducted operational planning for the October 7, 2023 attacks under the direction of Gaza leader Yahya Sinwar, who served as the primary architect.[13][14] Planning commenced by mid-2022 at the latest, as evidenced by a six-page handwritten memo attributed to Sinwar dated August 24, 2022, which outlined directives for a multi-phase assault targeting both Israeli military installations and civilian areas.[15][16] The document emphasized breaching the Gaza-Israel border fence at multiple points using bulldozers, followed by coordinated waves of fighters to seize control of bases and communities, while broadcasting acts of violence to maximize psychological disruption.[17] The memo specified brutal tactics, including burning residential neighborhoods with gasoline or diesel, slaughtering soldiers at close range with knives, and filming atrocities for dissemination to instill fear across Israel and garner support from Arab audiences.[15][16] Sinwar instructed mobilization of all forces in strict adherence to the plan, with preliminary activities disguised as routine operations through intensive camouflaged movements in the weeks prior.[17] Hamas integrated other Palestinian factions, such as Palestinian Islamic Jihad, through a joint operations room established in 2018, though the full scope remained confined to a small cadre of Hamas leaders.[18] Preparations involved extensive training exercises under the "Strong Pillar" banner, commencing in 2020 with joint drills across at least four sessions over three years, the final one occurring on September 12, 2023.[18] These simulations, conducted near the Israeli border, replicated attack elements like fence breaching, compound raids, and hostage-taking using mock bases modeled after Israeli sites.[18] Tactics incorporated diverse infiltration methods, including paragliders for aerial insertion, motorcycles and pickup trucks for rapid ground movement, and motorized boats for coastal incursions, with some assets like paragliders withheld from public view to preserve surprise.[18][17] Operational secrecy was maintained through strict compartmentalization, with the detailed plan accessible only to a handful of senior Hamas commanders; rank-and-file fighters and even some allied militants received activation orders mere hours before the assault began at dawn on October 7.[19] This approach ensured that intercepted communications prior to the attack revealed little beyond routine activities, while post-launch directives via radio confirmed execution of the memo's directives, such as setting fires and taking captives.[15][16] The operation, internally designated "Al-Aqsa Flood," aimed to overwhelm Israeli defenses through sheer volume and simultaneity across southern fronts.[18]

Israeli Intelligence Warnings and Failures

Israeli military intelligence possessed a detailed Hamas operational blueprint, code-named "Jericho Wall," obtained in 2022, which outlined a multi-phase assault involving rocket barrages to overwhelm defenses, drone strikes on surveillance systems and automated weaponry, and mass ground incursions by thousands of fighters to seize border areas and enable hostage-taking.[20][21] The 40-page document mirrored the October 7, 2023, attack's execution but was dismissed by IDF analysts as beyond Hamas's capabilities, categorized as an "aspirational" or unrealistic scenario rather than an imminent threat, and not elevated for senior review or contingency planning.[22][20] Egyptian intelligence conveyed multiple warnings to Israeli counterparts in the days preceding the attack, including a specific alert three days prior from an Egyptian intelligence official stating that Hamas was preparing "something big" from Gaza, urging high-level attention.[23][24] These communications, corroborated by U.S. congressional testimony, were downplayed by Israeli officials as vague or routine, with Israel's national security adviser later denying receipt of an explicit Egyptian warning of an imminent assault.[25][26] Domestically, IDF Unit 8200 signals intelligence and Shin Bet detected anomalies on the eve of October 7, including the activation of numerous Israeli SIM cards in Gazaâ€”indicating potential Hamas reconnaissance or preparationâ€”and unusual Hamas training exercises observed by border surveillance units, such as female observers (tatzpitaniyot) who reported suspicious activities but whose alerts were overridden or not escalated due to prevailing assessments of low threat levels.[27][21] An IDF probe identified five specific pre-dawn indicators on October 7, including Hamas movements and communications spikes, which Southern Command noted but failed to trigger heightened readiness, partly due to the Simchat Torah holiday reducing staffing.[27] Shin Bet had advocated for preemptive strikes on Hamas leadership, including assassination operations, but these were reportedly vetoed by political echelons prioritizing Gaza ceasefire negotiations and underestimating escalation risks.[28] The failures stemmed from a confluence of cognitive biases, including overconfidence in technological superiority (e.g., border barriers and surveillance), underestimation of Hamas's intent and reconstitution post-2014 and 2021 conflicts, and a doctrinal fixation on low-intensity threats like rockets rather than multi-domain invasionsâ€”a conceptual lapse echoed in historical precedents such as the 1973 Yom Kippur War.[29][30] Official inquiries, including a February 2025 IDF report, characterized the lapses as a "complete failure" of anticipation and response, attributing them to institutional complacency, siloed intelligence processing, and inadequate integration of human and signals intelligence with strategic assessments.[31][21] Shin Bet's internal review similarly highlighted missed opportunities in human intelligence penetration of Hamas's elite Nukhba units and failure to connect disparate indicators into a coherent threat picture.[28]

Execution of the Attacks

Initial Rocket and Drone Barrages

The initial rocket and drone barrages commenced at approximately 6:30 a.m. local time on October 7, 2023, as Hamas militants launched a massive salvo from the Gaza Strip to overwhelm Israeli air defenses and provide cover for subsequent ground incursions.[32] This opening phase involved the firing of several thousand unguided rockets toward southern Israeli communities and military sites, with the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) reporting approximately 3,000 rockets launched in the first hours of the attack.[33] An IDF assessment later detailed that 3,700 rockets were fired into southern Israel over the first four hours, marking the largest single-day barrage in the conflict's history up to that point.[34] Concurrently, Hamas deployed small commercial drones modified with explosives to target Israeli border infrastructure, including observation towers, radar systems, and surveillance cameras along the Gaza fence. These drone strikes, executed in the moments leading up to or alongside the rocket launches, aimed to blind IDF monitoring capabilities and facilitate undetected breaches of the border barrier.[35] The combination of high-volume rocket fire and precision drone attacks created a saturation effect, partially saturating Israel's Iron Dome interceptor system, which struggled to cope with the intensity despite intercepting a significant portion of incoming projectiles.[34] The barrages caused limited direct casualties in their initial stage, as many rockets landed in open areas or were neutralized, but they triggered widespread alarms, sirens, and evacuations across southern Israel, sowing chaos and diverting attention from the impending ground assault.[33] Hamas claimed responsibility for firing up to 5,000 rockets in this phase under the banner of "Operation Al-Aqsa Flood," though verified IDF data indicates lower but still unprecedented volumes.[32] Palestinian Islamic Jihad and other allied groups contributed additional rocket volleys, amplifying the coordinated aerial offensive.[34] This multi-vector approach underscored Hamas's tactical emphasis on exploiting technological asymmetries to enable the broader invasion.

Ground Incursions into Southern Israel

Following the initial rocket and drone barrages that began at around 6:30 a.m. local time on October 7, 2023, Hamas-led militants launched coordinated ground incursions across the Gaza-Israel border, exploiting the diversion caused by the aerial attacks.[36] The militants primarily breached the fortified border fence using explosives to blast initial gaps, followed by armored bulldozers to widen breaches for vehicle entry, allowing rapid penetration into Israeli territory.[4][37] These tactics overwhelmed automated surveillance systems and understaffed observation posts, with incursions occurring at nearly 30 points along the approximately 40-mile (65 km) border from the Mediterranean coast near Zikim to the eastern edge near Kissufim.[4][36] An estimated 3,000 Hamas fighters, supported by smaller contingents from Palestinian Islamic Jihad and other Gaza-based groups, participated in the ground assault, advancing on foot, motorcycles, pickup trucks loaded with gunmen, and motorized paragliders flown under cover of ongoing rocket fire.[36][4] The paragliders, numbering in the dozens, landed several kilometers inside Israel, targeting sites such as the Re'im military base and nearby civilian areas, while truck-borne squads raced toward border communities in the Gaza envelope region, including kibbutzim like Be'eri, Kfar Aza, and Nir Oz.[36] A limited amphibious element attempted incursions via speedboats along the coastal border near Zikim beach, though these were smaller in scale compared to the land breaches.[4] The militants carried automatic rifles, RPGs, grenades, and anti-tank weapons, prioritizing speed to outpace any immediate Israeli response.[36] The incursions extended up to 24 kilometers into Israel in some areas, such as toward Ofakim and the Nova music festival site near Kibbutz Re'im, where fighters dispersed into ad-hoc squads to assault military installations and civilian settlements simultaneously.[36][4] Border crossings like Erez and Kerem Shalom were also targeted, with militants disabling security cameras and communication towers en route to facilitate unchecked movement.[36] This multi-vector approach, combining low-tech mobility with synchronized breaches, enabled the fighters to overrun several IDF outposts within hours, though subsequent Israeli reinforcements began containing advances by midday.[4]

Attacks on Civilian Gatherings and Communities

The October 7, 2023, attacks by Hamas and allied Palestinian militant groups extended to civilian communities in Israel's Gaza envelope region, including kibbutzim such as Be'eri, Kfar Aza, Nir Oz, and others near the border. Militants, numbering in the hundreds per community, breached perimeter fences around 6:30 a.m., using motorcycles, trucks, and on foot to enter residential areas, where they conducted systematic searches of homes, safe rooms, and shelters.[3] Tactics included shooting through doors and windows, throwing grenades into bunkers, and setting fire to structures, resulting in over 800 civilian deaths across these towns and kibbutzim combined.[3][2] In Kibbutz Be'eri, approximately 100 Hamas militants overran the community of about 1,000 residents, killing 102 civilians in assaults that lasted over 10 hours; gunmen went house-to-house, executing families and burning buildings, while a group of around 15 hostages was held in one home before being killed in a subsequent Israeli rescue attempt.[38][39] Similar patterns unfolded in Kfar Aza, where militants slaughtered residents including children and elderly in their beds, and in Nir Oz, a smaller kibbutz where nearly a quarter of residents were either killed or abducted.[3] These incursions exploited the lack of immediate military response, allowing militants to loot, vandalize, and prolong the violence against unarmed civilians.[4] A prominent civilian gathering targeted was the Nova music festival near Kibbutz Re'im, attended by around 3,500 people celebrating the Jewish holiday of Simchat Torah. Militants attacked shortly after 7:00 a.m., with some arriving via paragliders from Gaza, firing indiscriminately on crowds and vehicles attempting to flee; festivalgoers hid in bomb shelters, orchards, and roadside ditches, but many were hunted down and executed.[40] The assault, which claimed the highest single-site civilian toll of the attacks, involved coordinated gunfire, RPGs, and abductions, with bodies later found mutilated or burned.[3] This event exemplified the deliberate targeting of non-combatants in open, recreational settings, separate from residential communities.[2] Across these sites, militants from Hamas's Al-Qassam Brigades and other groups like Palestinian Islamic Jihad operated without uniforms distinguishing them from civilians, complicating identification, and prioritized killing over military objectives in populated areas.[4] The attacks on gatherings and communities accounted for the majority of the approximately 815 civilian fatalities from the overall incursion, underscoring a focus on inflicting maximum terror on non-military targets.[41]

Assaults on Military Installations

Hamas-led forces launched coordinated assaults on several Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) installations along the Gaza border starting around 6:30 a.m. on October 7, 2023, shortly after initial rocket barrages and paraglider incursions. These attacks involved elite Nukhba units and other militants breaching fences at multiple points, using explosives, vehicles, and small arms to overwhelm understaffed outposts. The Gaza Division, responsible for the border, had approximately 767 troops deployed against an estimated 5,000 infiltrating terrorists, leading to several bases being overrun or isolated for hours.[42][43] At the Erez Crossing and adjacent IDF base in northern Gaza, approximately 120 terrorists, including an initial wave of 20 Nukhba operatives followed by about 100 more from Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad, and other groups, breached the facility at 6:42 a.m. The attackers infiltrated the base by 7:00 a.m., killing soldiers, abducting three (Ron Sherman and Nik Beizer, who died in captivity, and Tamir Nimrodi, who remains held), and looting equipment before Israeli Air Force strikes neutralized dozens and halted further advances. Nine IDF soldiers were killed in the fighting, with an IDF probe citing inadequate preparation and command breakdowns as factors enabling the initial success.[44] The Nahal Oz outpost, located 850 meters from the Gaza border, was overrun in one of the most severe failures of the day. Rocket fire began at 6:20 a.m., followed by breaches at 6:30 a.m. involving around 70 initial fighters attacking from four directions, with additional militants joining; the war room fell by 7:00 a.m. amid close-quarters combat using grenades and gunfire. Over 60 IDF soldiers were killed, including many unarmed personnel, and seven female surveillance soldiers (Tatzpitaniyot) were abducted, with one later killed, one rescued, and five still captive. An IDF investigation reported 35 terrorists killed inside the base and 10 outside, attributing the collapse to systemic vulnerabilities like destroyed surveillance systems and delayed reinforcements despite prior warnings.[45][46] Further south, at Zikim naval and training base, Hamas militants infiltrated via speedboats and land breaches, targeting the facility amid broader coastal assaults. An experienced infantry company, temporarily replacing trainee recruits, engaged the attackers in prolonged fighting, preventing a full takeover and saving over 100 rookies; the base's defense succeeded due to junior commanders' initiative, though specific casualties included several officer deaths. An IDF probe praised the rapid response but noted initial chaos from the surprise incursion.[47] At Re'im Base, headquarters of the Gaza Division's 143rd Brigade, around 60 Hamas terrorists approached, with 30 isolating the entrance junction while others probed deeper. Defending troops, despite vulnerabilities and hours of isolation, repelled the assault, blocking attempts to capture the command center; no full overrun occurred, though the base's exposure highlighted broader border defense gaps. These attacks on military sites killed dozens of IDF personnel and enabled terrorists to operate unimpeded in adjacent areas for several hours, contributing to the overall tactical surprise.[48]

Hostage Abductions

Scale and Methods of Taking Hostages

Hamas-led militants and affiliated groups abducted approximately 251 people during the October 7, 2023, attacks, including Israeli civilians, soldiers, and foreign nationals from at least 21 border communities and the Nova music festival.[49][50] The victims ranged in age from infants to elderly individuals, with documented cases including entire families seized from their homes.[51] This figure represents the initial count verified by Israeli authorities through forensic identification, survivor accounts, and Hamas-issued videos.[49] Abductions were concentrated in kibbutzim such as Nir Oz, where nearly half of the 400 residents were either killed or taken captive, with militants systematically searching homes and dragging out occupants.[51] In Kfar Aza and Be'eri, attackers entered residences, subdued families at gunpoint, bound hands and feet with zip ties or rope, and loaded victims onto vehicles for transport across the breached Gaza border fence.[52] Similar tactics were employed at the Nova festival near Kibbutz Re'im, where gunmen rounded up fleeing attendees amid gunfire, forced groups onto pickup trucks or motorcyclesâ€”sometimes strapping individuals to the framesâ€”and drove them toward Gaza, as captured in verified video footage disseminated by the perpetrators.[53][54] Transportation methods prioritized speed and concealment, utilizing an estimated 200-300 vehicles including technicals (armed pickup trucks), motorcycles for quick extractions, and even golf carts commandeered from communities.[53] Victims were often beaten, stripped partially or fully to disorient and humiliate, and paraded publicly before crossing into Gaza through pre-planned breaches in the security barrier.[53] Some abductions involved opportunistic seizures during broader assaults on military outposts, where off-duty soldiers were captured after bases were overrun.[55] These operations reflected premeditated coordination, with militants using body cameras to document and later exploit the captures for propaganda.[54]

Treatment and Exploitation of Hostages During Abductions

During the abduction phase of the October 7, 2023, attacks, Palestinian armed groups including Hamas's Qassam Brigades subjected captives to physical violence, restraint, and coercive handling as they were forcibly removed from sites such as kibbutzim, the Nova music festival, and roadside areas, and transported toward the Gaza border. Casualties

Verified Israeli and Foreign Deaths

The October 7, 2023, attacks by Hamas-led militants resulted in 1,139 verified deaths in Israel, according to data from Israel's National Insurance Institute, which cross-referenced social security records, forensic identifications, and official reports to confirm the toll after initial estimates of around 1,400 were revised downward upon eliminating duplicates and unverified cases.[61] This figure excludes deaths from subsequent military operations or indirect causes. Among Israeli victims, 766 were civilians, including 36 children under 18 and 71 individuals over 80, while 373 were security personnel, comprising 305 Israel Defense Forces (IDF) members, 59 police officers, and 9 employees of other security agencies.[61] Of the civilian deaths, a significant portion occurred at specific sites: 364 at the Nova music festival and nearby areas, 18 at Zikim Beach, and others in kibbutzim such as Be'eri (over 100 killed) and Kfar Aza (over 50 killed), where militants systematically entered homes and community centers.[3] Israeli authorities documented these through DNA analysis, eyewitness accounts, and recovered remains, with over 800 civilian names listed on official memorials as of October 2025.[3] Seventy-one foreign nationals from over 30 countries were among the verified deaths, primarily agricultural workers from Southeast Asia employed in southern Israeli communities targeted by the incursions.[61] The largest groups included 47 Thai nationals, 10 Nepalese, 4 Chinese, 3 Filipinos, 2 Sri Lankans, 2 Eritreans, and single fatalities from countries such as Tanzania, Sudan, Germany, Canada, Britain, and France-Mexico (dual).[3] These identifications relied on international cooperation, passport records, and family notifications, with many victims killed while fleeing or hiding in attacked kibbutzim.[1] At least 31 American citizens or dual nationals were also confirmed killed, marking the deadliest toll for U.S. nationals in a terrorist attack since September 11, 2001.[1]

Challenges in Identification and Initial Reporting

The identification of victims from the October 7, 2023, attacks presented unprecedented forensic challenges due to the scale of the violence, with over 1,200 Israeli and foreign nationals killed, many of whose remains were severely mutilated, burned beyond visual recognition, or fragmented into thousands of body parts.[62] Emergency responders, including volunteers from ZAKA, operated in hazardous conditions amid ongoing threats, recovering bodies from sites like the Nova music festival and kibbutzim where fires and explosions had accelerated decomposition.[63] This necessitated adaptations to standard disaster victim identification (DVI) protocols, including rapid deployment of mobile DNA labs and CT scans for non-invasive analysis, as traditional methods proved insufficient for the volumeâ€”estimated at hundreds of unidentified remains even 10 days post-attack.[64][65] Forensic processes relied heavily on comparative DNA matching from family-provided samples, with the Abu Kabir National Center of Forensic Medicine processing remains in shifts around the clock; however, the brutality of the attacksâ€”evidenced by charred vehicles and safe roomsâ€”delayed confirmations for weeks in some cases.[66] Initial casualty figures announced by Israeli authorities exceeded 1,400 deaths by October 10, 2023, but were revised to approximately 1,200 by November after forensic verification distinguished civilian and military victims from the bodies of Hamas militants and other attackers intermingled at scenes.[67] This adjustment highlighted coordination issues between field recovery teams and labs, compounded by the need to prioritize hostage status determinations, where some presumed dead were later confirmed alive in captivity.[68] Initial reporting faced further complications from the fog of war, with unverified social media and eyewitness accounts leading to circulating claims of casualty figures and specific atrocities that required later correction, such as initial reports of dozens of beheaded infants reduced to confirmed cases of infant decapitation in isolated incidents.[69] Official tallies from the Israeli Health Ministry and IDF, while empirically grounded in forensic data, were scrutinized amid Hamas propaganda inflating or disputing numbers, underscoring the causal role of deliberate obfuscation by attackers in prolonging uncertainty.[3] These challenges persisted into 2025 for a subset of remains, including those of hostages returned in decomposed states requiring renewed DNA efforts.[70]

Documented Atrocities

Evidence of Systematic Sexual and Gender-Based Violence

Multiple eyewitness accounts from first responders and survivors documented instances of rape and sexual assault during the attacks on the Nova music festival near Kibbutz Re'im, where militants separated women from men, stripped them, and committed sexual violence before killing them.[71] Paramedics reported finding young women's bodies with bloodied genitals and signs of mutilation, including severed breasts, at the festival site and nearby roads.[72] A survivor from the festival described militants dragging women into tents and vehicles while groping and assaulting them, with screams audible from adjacent areas.[73] In kibbutzim such as Be'eri and Kfar Aza, bodies of women were found partially or fully nude, with legs spread and hands bound, indicative of post-mortem positioning after sexual assault.[74] ZAKA volunteers, who handled body recovery, testified to observing multiple cases of women shot in the genitals or with foreign objects inserted, patterns consistent across sites.[75] Interrogations of captured Hamas militants yielded confessions of participating in or witnessing rapes, including one admission of raping a woman in a house in Be'eri before killing her.[73] A United Nations mission led by Special Representative Pramila Patten, following visits to attack sites in January-February 2024, found reasonable grounds to believe that rape and gang-rape occurred in at least three locations: the Nova festival, Road 232, and Kibbutz Re'im.[76] The report noted patterns of victims found naked or semi-naked, bound, and shot in the head or chest after sexual violence, based on forensic analysis, witness interviews, and video evidence from militants' body cameras showing mutilation of female genitals.[77] While full forensic confirmation was limited by rapid cremation of bodies during combat and initial chaos, the mission verified sexual violence through convergent testimonies from over 30 survivors and witnesses. The "Dinah Project," an Israeli initiative compiling evidence for international prosecution, documented 17 distinct incidents of sexual assault via eyewitness accounts from first responders and analyzed patterns suggesting premeditation, including militants' prior training materials referencing rape as a weapon.[75] This included video footage from a terrorist's phone showing a woman stripped and assaulted near the Gaza border.[78] The systematic nature is inferred from the distribution across civilian sitesâ€”festival, homes, sheltersâ€”and the targeting of women and girls, with at least seven confirmed cases of rape followed by murder.[74] Confessions from interrogated militants further corroborated group rapes, with one stating orders to "take women" for sexual purposes during abductions.[79] Challenges in verification arose from the absence of surviving victims in many cases, as perpetrators often killed victims immediately after assaults, compounded by the destruction of evidence in fires and explosions.[71] Nonetheless, cross-verified patterns from independent sources, including international journalists and forensic teams, support the conclusion of deliberate, widespread use of sexual violence as a tactic, distinct from isolated wartime opportunism.[72][74]

Cases of Torture, Mutilation, and Deliberate Executions

First responders from ZAKA, who handled body recovery, documented extensive mutilations across attack sites, including decapitated corpses, gouged-out eyes, and severed genitals on both male and female victims, often alongside signs of pre-death torture such as bound limbs.[72] Forensic examinations at Israel's National Center of Forensic Medicine revealed that numerous victims were bound defensive postures.[80] These bindings and burns, observed in remains from kibbutzim like Be'eri, point to deliberate infliction of prolonged suffering prior to death.[81] In Kibbutz Be'eri, where over 100 residents were killed, Israeli forensic teams identified patterns of execution-style killings, including groups of civilians shot while bound together and mutilated post-mortem, with some bodies showing knife wounds and dismemberment consistent with non-medical amputation.[82] Similar evidence emerged from Kfar Aza, where bodies exhibited mutilations such as hacked limbs and targeted Military Mobilization and Counterattacks

In the initial hours following the Hamas-led incursion that began at 6:30 a.m. on October 7, 2023, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) units along the Gaza border faced overwhelming numbers of approximately 3,000 infiltrating militants, resulting in the rapid overrun of several military outposts and bases, including the Erez and Nahal Oz crossings. Surviving IDF personnel, numbering in the dozens at key sites, engaged in sporadic firefights, but lacked the immediate capacity for large-scale counteroffensives due to the surprise element and disrupted command structures. The IDF's Southern Command, caught in disarray, prioritized securing remaining border positions while air assets were redirected from routine patrols to strike emerging threats.[105] By late morning, the Israeli Air Force initiated airstrikes on Hamas targets within Gaza, focusing on rocket launch sites, command posts, and militant concentrations to degrade the attackers' support infrastructure; over 400 targets were hit in the first 24 hours, contributing to the neutralization of an estimated 500 infiltrators by ground and air combined efforts. Ground counterattacks commenced piecemeal as reinforcements arrived, with elite units like the Golani Brigade's 51st Battalion deploying to contest militant advances near kibbutzim such as Be'eri and Kfar Aza, where house-to-house clearances began in the afternoon despite ongoing hostage situations complicating operations. These early engagements inflicted significant casualties on militants but also exposed IDF vulnerabilities, as infiltrators exploited breached fences to maneuver deep into Israeli territory.[106][107] Amid the escalating crisis, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu convened the security cabinet and declared a nationwide state of emergency by midday, enabling the activation of reserve forces. Defense Minister Yoav Gallant formally ordered the mobilization of up to 300,000 reservistsâ€”the largest such call-up in Israel's historyâ€”shortly thereafter, with tens of thousands reporting for duty within hours via a streamlined digital system that saw participation rates surpass 120% in initial waves. This rapid influx bolstered active-duty strength from around 170,000 to over 400,000 personnel within days, facilitating the redeployment of armored divisions and infantry brigades to the southern front for systematic sweeps against remaining pockets of militants. Reservist units, including tank and artillery formations, played a pivotal role in encircling and eliminating infiltrator groups by October 8, reclaiming control of most breached communities and the border fence.[108][109] Counteroffensive operations extended into coordinated ground-air assaults, with IDF special forces conducting raids to disrupt Hamas rear areas and prevent further reinforcements; by the end of October 7, official tallies reported over 1,000 militants killed, though independent verifications later adjusted this figure downward to around 800 based on forensic evidence from the battlefield. The mobilization's scale underscored Israel's doctrinal emphasis on total reserve integration during existential threats, enabling a shift from defensive containment to offensive reclamation, though it strained logistics and civilian sectors in the short term. Sustained airstrikes into October 8 targeted high-value Hamas infrastructure, paving the way for the broader Gaza ground incursion announced on October 13.[110]

Instances of Friendly Fire and Application of the Hannibal Directive

During the October 7, 2023, Hamas-led attacks, Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) personnel invoked elements of the Hannibal Directiveâ€”a protocol, officially rescinded in 2016, that authorizes maximum force to prevent the capture of soldiers, potentially endangering their livesâ€”in response to infiltration at border facilities.[111] This included explicit orders at Erez Crossing, Re'im base, and Nahal Oz outpost, where commanders directed drone strikes and other fire on suspected abduction vehicles or sites, despite risks to Israeli personnel present.[112] [111] At 7:18 a.m., for instance, a "Hannibal at Erez" command prompted a drone attack following reports of an abduction attempt.[111] Similar directives at Re'im involved a Hermes 450 drone strike on the base amid ongoing operations by Israeli Shaldag commandos, though no confirmed Israeli casualties from that specific action were reported.[111] By 11:30 a.m., Gaza Division officers issued a broader order prohibiting any vehicles from returning to Gaza, instructing forces to halt them by any means, which extended risks to potential civilian hostages aboard.[111] This aligned with Apache helicopter pilots' accounts of firing on "anything that moved" toward Gaza in the initial hours, prioritizing disruption of infiltrators over precise identification amid intelligence gaps and chaos.[113] IDF investigations later confirmed friendly fire contributions, including the death of 68-year-old Efrat Katz at Kibbutz Nir Oz, struck by an Israeli Air Force helicopter during an abduction attempt on a kibbutz vehicle.[114] [111] At Kibbutz Be'eri, Brig. Gen. Barak Hiram ordered tank shelling of a house holding 14 Israeli civilian hostages and Hamas militants, resulting in 13 hostage deaths, with at least one confirmed by shrapnel.[111] An IDF inquiry acknowledged the shelling but concluded the hostages were likely killed by Hamas prior to or separately from the strikes, contradicting survivor Yasmin Porat's testimony of explosions killing those inside after negotiations stalled.[115] [116] Hiram later apologized to bereaved families for operational failures, amid calls for his resignation.[117] Separate friendly fire incidents at Nahal Oz involved IDF gunfire killing civilians, including Tomer and Dikla Arava, during clashes where over 180 militants overran the outpost in waves; an investigation deemed their deaths likely from Israeli fire amid the battle that claimed 53 soldiers and numerous civilians.[118] These cases highlight operational decisions under extreme pressure, with subsequent probes attributing some deaths to misidentification and lack of coordination rather than deliberate targeting of civilians, though the Hannibal-related orders amplified risks in abduction scenarios.[119] Overall, while exact tallies vary, friendly fire accounted for a minority of the day's casualties, with primary fatalities from Hamas actions.[120]

Investigations and Findings

Israeli Official Inquiries into Preparedness Failures

Interrogations of Captured Militants

Israeli security agencies, including the Shin Bet and Israel Defense Forces (IDF), interrogated dozens of captured militants from Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) in the immediate aftermath of the October 7, 2023, attacks, yielding confessions that detailed the planning and execution of atrocities against Israeli civilians.[132] These interrogations, often conducted within days of capture, focused on operational tactics, orders received, and specific acts committed during incursions into southern Israeli communities such as Be'eri, Kfar Aza, and the Nova music festival site.[133] Captured individuals, including low-level fighters and some commanders, admitted to receiving explicit instructions to target non-combatants, with one militant recounting orders to "kill the men, capture the women" as part of a strategy to maximize terror and hostage acquisition.[134] Key confessions corroborated forensic and eyewitness evidence of systematic violence. For instance, in footage released by Shin Bet in October 2023, a Hamas operative described entering a home in a kibbutz, executing an entire familyâ€”including shooting parents and childrenâ€”and using grenades against civilians sheltering in safe rooms, stating the acts were carried out without resistance from Israeli forces due to the surprise element of the assault.[135] Another detainee confessed to participating in the abduction of civilians from the Nova festival, binding and dragging individuals into Gaza while firing indiscriminately at fleeing festivalgoers.[136] These accounts aligned with estimates that approximately 3,000 militants, including elite Nukhba forces, crossed into Israel, prioritizing civilian areas over military targets.[132] Sexual violence emerged as a recurring theme in the interrogations. In May 2024, the IDF publicized videos of a Hamas father-son pair admitting to raping an Israeli woman during the attack on Kibbutz Be'eri; the son detailed holding her down while his father assaulted her, followed by her execution, emphasizing the act was spontaneous yet unpunished by superiors.[136] Similarly, a PIJ militant, Manar Mahmoud Muhammad Kasem, confessed in March 2024 to raping and murdering an Israeli woman, describing the violence as part of the broader directive to terrorize communities.[137] Interrogators noted that while some militants expressed regret or shock at the scale of civilian deaths, others justified the actions as revenge or religious duty, revealing indoctrination through Hamas training camps where simulations of border breaches included directives for unrestricted killing.[135] The interrogations also exposed operational details, such as the use of paragliders, motorcycles, and bulldozers to breach the border fence, with militants admitting to pre-attack reconnaissance and coordination with Gazan rocket crews to suppress Israeli air defenses.[138] By November 2023, Shin Bet had questioned over 50 suspects, extracting intelligence on hostage locations and Hamas command structures, though some confessions highlighted internal frustrations, including anger at Hamas leadership for abandoning fighters post-infiltration.[132] Critics, including human rights groups, have questioned the conditions of these interrogations for potential coercion, but the consistency across multiple independent sessionsâ€”coupled with alignment to physical evidence like bodycam footage recovered from militantsâ€”lends credibility to the admissions of premeditated civilian targeting.[135] Hamas has denied systematic atrocities, attributing deaths to crossfire or Israeli actions, but the interrogations provide direct militant testimony contradicting such claims.[136]

Reactions

Israeli Domestic Response

Following the October 7, 2023, attacks, Israeli society experienced an initial surge of national unity, with opposition leaders joining a war cabinet to coordinate the response and over 300,000 reservists mobilizing voluntarily in the days after.[153] This cohesion reflected widespread outrage over the killing of approximately 1,200 people and abduction of 251 hostages, fostering a temporary pause in pre-existing domestic protests against judicial reforms.[154] Public opinion polls indicated strong initial support for the military campaign in Gaza aimed at dismantling Hamas, with a majority of Jewish Israelis endorsing robust operations;
