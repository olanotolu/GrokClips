2023 Italian GT Championship

The 2023 Campionato Italiano Gran Turismo was Italy's national series for grand touring cars, sanctioned by ACI Sport and comprising parallel sprint and endurance championships for GT3, GT4, and single-make GT Cup vehicles from manufacturers including Ferrari, BMW, Lamborghini, and Audi, contested across prominent circuits such as Misano World Circuit, Autodromo Nazionale di Monza, Autodromo Internazionale del Mugello, ACI Vallelunga Circuit, and Autodromo di Pergusa.[1][2] The season emphasized professional and gentleman driver pairings in GT3 Pro, Pro-Am, and Am subclasses, with sprint events featuring two 50-minute-plus-one-lap races per weekend and endurance rounds spanning 2-3 hours, drawing fields of up to dozens of entries per class.[3] In the endurance category, Scuderia Baldini 207 clinched the overall teams' title with the Ferrari 296 GT3, driven to the drivers' championship by Giancarlo Fisichella and Matteo Mosca after prevailing in the decisive Vallelunga finale amid tight competition from Lamborghini and BMW squads.[4] Ferrari also secured multiple class honors in endurance, underscoring the marque's dominance in longer-format battles, while sprint events showcased manufacturer rivalries without reported major controversies or disqualifications altering outcomes.[3][4]

Championship Overview

Format and Structure

The 2023 Italian GT Championship, organized by ACI Sport, was structured into two distinct series: the Sprint series and the Endurance series, each awarding separate titles while sharing the GT3 and GT Cup classes. The GT3 class utilized FIA-homologated grand tourer vehicles from manufacturers such as Ferrari, Lamborghini, BMW, and Audi, divided into Pro, Pro-Am, and Amateur driver categories based on performance indices and licensing. The GT Cup class featured lower-powered GT4-specification cars, primarily Porsche 911 GT4s and similar models, without subclass divisions. Across both series, a total of 14 national titles were contested, encompassing driver and team championships in GT3 overall, Pro-Am, Amateur, and GT Cup categories.[3] The Sprint series consisted of four double-header event weekends, held at circuits including Vallelunga, Monza, Mugello, and Imola, with each race formatted as 50 minutes plus one additional lap to determine finishing order under green-flag conditions. Qualifying sessions preceded the races, establishing the grid for Race 1, while Race 2 grids were set by a reverse-top-six or similar method from Race 1 results to promote competitive racing. Points were awarded to the top finishers in overall and class standings, with bonuses for pole positions and fastest laps, emphasizing driver pairings typically consisting of one professional and one gentleman driver in GT3 entries.[5] In contrast, the Endurance series featured four standalone events at Pergusa, Monza, Imola, and Mugello, each comprising a single two-hour race requiring mandatory pit stops for driver changes and refueling, aligning with grand tourer endurance protocols. GT3 entries mandated two-driver lineups, while GT Cup allowed flexibility for single or multi-driver teams, with success ballast and performance adjustments applied post-race to balance competition across classes. Broadcasting occurred live on ACI Sport TV, with results determining championships via cumulative points, prioritizing empirical lap times and reliability over sprint-style overtaking dynamics.[6]

Classes and Regulations

The 2023 Italian GT Championship was divided into two primary classes: GT3, contested with FIA-homologated GT3 grand tourer race cars from manufacturers such as Ferrari, Lamborghini, Mercedes-AMG, and BMW, and GT Cup, featuring GT4-specification and single-make cup cars including Porsche 911 GT3 Cup models.[3][7] Within the GT3 class, entries were further subdivided based on driver experience levels to promote balanced competition: the Pro Cup for pairings of two professional drivers, the Pro-Am Cup for one professional and one gentleman (amateur) driver, and the Am Cup for two gentleman drivers.[8] The GT Cup class operated as a single category without formal driver sub-divisions, though it accommodated a mix of professional and gentleman competitors in production-derived GT4 machinery.[3] Racing formats differed between the Sprint and Endurance series, both governed by Automobile Club d'Italia (ACI) sporting regulations emphasizing safety, technical homologation, and Balance of Performance (BoP) adjustments to equalize car speeds across models.[9] The Sprint series consisted of two 50-minute plus one lap races per weekend event, run separately for GT3 and GT Cup classes to minimize contact risks between differing car specifications, with starting grids prioritized by GT3 entries followed by GT Cup.[10] Endurance events were standardized at two hours, requiring mandatory pit stops for driver changes and refueling, alongside success penalties such as time handicaps applied to the top five GT3 crews in qualifying (+20, +16, +12, +8, and +4 seconds respectively) to handicap repeat frontrunners.[11] All classes awarded national titles across driver, team, and manufacturer categories, totaling 14 championships, with technical eligibility limited to 2023 FIA GT3 and GT4 homologues or equivalent cup-spec vehicles like the newly admitted Lamborghini HuracÃ¡n GT3 Evo2 and Porsche 992 GT3 Cup.[3][7]

Pre-Season Changes

The sporting regulations for the 2023 Campionato Italiano Gran Turismo Sprint and Endurance series were published online by ACI Sport on February 1, 2023, incorporating revisions to the time handicap system applied in the Endurance events to promote competitive balance. In the GT3 class, the top five crews from each race faced progressive time penalties in the subsequent round: +20 seconds for first, +16 for second, +12 for third, +8 for fourth, and +4 for fifth. For the GT Cup class, penalties applied to the top three pairings: +10 seconds for first, +7 for second, and +4 for third.[12][11][7] Further pre-season adjustments to the Sprint regulations included updates to Article 7 on eligible vehicles, effective March 23, 2023, specifying permitted models and technical homologations for GT3 and GT Cup entries.[10] These changes aimed to align with evolving manufacturer offerings while maintaining eligibility under FIA GT standards, without altering the core two-hour Endurance format or Sprint race structure established in prior seasons.

GT Cup Entries

The GT Cup class utilized Porsche 911 GT3 Cup (992) cars exclusively, catering to semi-professional and gentleman drivers in a one-make format with Pro-Am and Am subdivisions across Sprint and Endurance series.[8] Entries were managed by Italian customer racing teams, with participation fluctuating between 8 and 12 cars per round, emphasizing accessibility for non-full-time professionals compared to the GT3 class.[8] At the Sprint series opener in Misano on May 6-7, ten Porsche 911 GT3 Cup entries competed, including Ombra Racing's #77 for Piergiuseppe Di Giusto and Riccardo Pera, Racevent's car for Simone Biolghini and an unspecified co-driver, and additional outfits like those from RTR and other Porsche specialists.[8] Vincenzo Sospiri Racing fielded consistent entries, notably with Belgian driver Gilles Stadsbader, who clinched the Sprint GT Cup Pro-Am title through strong performances across the season.[20] DL Racing supported Luca SegÃ¹, securing second in the same subcategory.[20] In the Endurance series, teams like those backing race winners Adrian Kurzejewski and Davide Balzan at Mugello highlighted the class's competitive depth, with additional participants including defending Sprint champions from 2022 such as Coluccio-Mazzola.[3] Vincenzo Scarpetta claimed the overall Am title, underscoring the emphasis on driver skill in balanced machinery.[21] No international manufacturer diversity existed, as regulations mandated the Porsche platform to maintain cost control and parity.[8]

Schedule

Sprint Series Events

The Sprint Series consisted of four double-header race weekends, each featuring two approximately one-hour races for GT3 and GT Cup classes, contested under dry or wet conditions with mandatory driver changes where applicable.[22] These events emphasized sprint-style racing with starting grids set by qualifying results, attracting fields of up to 43 cars in the opening round.[8] The series opened at Misano World Circuit Marco Simoncelli from May 5 to 7, marking the championship's debut with strong entries across classes.[22] The second round took place at Autodromo Nazionale di Monza on June 23 to 25, benefiting from the high-speed layout that favored powerful GT3 machinery.[22] Round three occurred at Autodromo Internazionale del Mugello from September 29 to October 1, where tactical pit strategies influenced outcomes amid the circuit's elevation changes.[22] The season concluded at Autodromo Enzo e Dino Ferrari in Imola on October 27 to 29, with titles decided in the final races under competitive pressure.[22]

Season Progression

Key Races and Highlights

The 2023 Italian GT Championship Sprint series opened at Misano World Circuit on May 6-7, where Race 1 in the GT3 class was won by Andrea Kimi Antonelli driving a Mercedes-AMG GT3 for MM Motorsport, marking a strong debut for the young driver in the top category. Race 2 saw Stuart D'Auria secure victory for AF Corse in a Ferrari 488 GT3 Evo, demonstrating the competitiveness of Italian machinery early in the season. Subsequent Sprint rounds at Monza, Mugello, and Vallelunga featured intense battles, with BMW M4 GT3 entries from BMW Italia Ceccato consistently challenging Ferrari and Lamborghini squads, setting the stage for a tight points race. The season finale at Imola on October 28-29 delivered a decisive confrontation between BMW and Lamborghini teams, where Race 1 was claimed by Moulin and Llarena in a Lamborghini Huracan GT3 Evo2 for VSR, but the overall Sprint GT3 title went to Jens Klingmann and Bruno Spengler in the BMW M4 GT3 after accumulating sufficient points across the campaign.[30][31] In the Endurance series, the opener at Autodromo di Pergusa on May 19-21 initiated three-hour races emphasizing reliability and strategy, with early dominance shown by Ferrari 296 GT3 and Lamborghini Huracan entries.[3] Rounds at Monza and Vallelunga highlighted pro-am class skirmishes, culminating in a hard-fought finale where Giancarlo Fisichella's Ferrari squad clinched the GT3 outright points lead with 55 points, underscoring the series' focus on sustained pace over sprint bursts.[32] GT Cup classes across both series saw Porsche 911 GT3 Cup cars frequently victorious, with drivers like Gilles Stadsbader repeating wins from Misano to assert manufacturer strength in lower divisions.

Results and Standings

Sprint GT3 Standings

In the Sprint GT3 PRO category, Jens Klingmann of Germany and Bruno Spengler of Canada clinched the drivers' title with 118 points, piloting the BMW M4 GT3 for BMW Team Italia after consistent performances across the season's seven rounds, including key victories at Monza and Mugello.[31][40] Their championship was sealed at the Imola finale on October 29, 2023, despite tight competition from Lamborghini pairings like Mattia Michelotto and Edoardo Liberati, who trailed by a narrow margin entering the event but faced setbacks including a 25-second penalty in race one.[41] The GT3 PRO-AM subcategory crown went to Philippe Denes and Dmitri Gvazava, who amassed 104 points in the Mercedes-AMG GT3 fielded by Imperiale Racing, securing the title through strong results at Imola where they capitalized on rivals' misfortunes to pull ahead of challengers like De Luca-Tamburini (95 points) and Castro-Nurmi.[35] In the GT3 AM class, Italian drivers Giuseppe Fascicolo and Massimo Ciglia won the championship driving the Lamborghini HuracÃ¡n GT3 Evo for Imperiale Racing, clinching it with dual victories in the Imola races that concluded the season on October 29, 2023, leveraging their early-season momentum from Misano to outpace the field in the amateur-focused division.[41][34]

Sprint GT Cup Standings

In the Sprint GT Cup class of the 2023 Campionato Italiano Gran Turismo, standings were segregated into Pro-Am and Am categories to reflect driver experience levels, with points awarded based on the best seven results from eight rounds held between April and October across circuits including Misano, Monza, Vallelunga, Mugello, and Imola.[42] The Pro-Am title went to Gilles Stadsbader of Vincenzo Sospiri Racing, who accumulated 102 points driving a Lamborghini HuracÃ¡n Super Trofeo, securing the championship with consistent podium finishes, including victories at Misano.[43][34] Stadsbader's runner-up was Luca SegÃ¹ of DL Racing in a Porsche 911 GT4, while the category emphasized mixed professional-amateur pairings in GT4-specification cars like Porsche, Lamborghini, and Mercedes-AMG.[20] The Am category crown was claimed by 17-year-old Vincenzo Scarpetta, driving a Ferrari 488 Challenge Evo for the Rossocorsa team, who clinched the title through strong performances culminating in a decisive win at Imola on October 29, 2023.[44][21] Scarpetta's championship success highlighted emerging talent in the gentleman-driver focused division, where entries included Ferrari, Porsche, and Lamborghini machinery operated primarily by non-professional racers. A separate handicap classification adjusted points for performance consistency, but primary titles remained with Pro-Am and Am leaders.[42] Overall, the GT Cup class saw competitive racing among approximately 15-20 entries per event, with no major controversies affecting final tallies published by ACI Sport on October 29, 2023.[45]

Endurance GT3 Standings

The GT3 PRO drivers' championship was secured by Giancarlo Fisichella and Tommaso Mosca of Scuderia Baldini, driving the Ferrari 488 GT3 Evo, with two victories (at Pergusa and Mugello), a second place, and a third place across the four-round season culminating at Monza on September 17, 2023.[46][47] Scuderia Baldini also claimed the corresponding team-constructor title in GT3.[48] In the GT3 PRO-AM class, the drivers' championship went to Stefano Comandini, Marco CassarÃ , and Alfred Nilsson of BMW Italia Ceccato in the BMW M4 GT3, accumulating 52 points through strong performances including a win and multiple podiums.[17][49]

Controversies

Endurance Championship Appeals

The 2023 Italian GT Endurance Championship concluded without documented appeals or disputes that altered final standings or title allocations. Titles were confirmed and awarded by ACI Sport following the season finale at Imola on October 29.[3] Giancarlo Fisichella and Tommaso Mosca clinched the GT3 drivers' championship with consistent performances across the six-round series, securing victory ahead of rivals in the Ferrari 296 GT3 fielded by Scuderia Baldini 1912, which also captured the overall teams' title.[3] Additional class honors went to drivers such as Stefano Comandini, Andrea CassarÃ , and Roberto Nilsson in GT3 Pro-Am.[3] Official results from ACI Sport, the series organizer, reflect no post-race protests or judicial reviews escalating to public controversy, distinguishing the season from subsequent years where appeals delayed announcements.[3] This smooth resolution aligned with the championship's emphasis on technical compliance and on-track adjudication, as per series regulations, without reported challenges to scrutineering or penalties.[50]
