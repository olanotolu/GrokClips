Itacoatiara, Amazonas

Itacoatiara is a municipality in the central eastern portion of state of Amazonas, inland northern Brazil. Its population was 103,598 (2022 census)[1] and its area is 8,892 km².[1] The town is known as "City of painted stone" - which is its name translated from Tupi. Itacoatiara has an important port, which is responsible for a considerable amount of cargo transportation in Amazonas. Its Catedral Prelatícia Nossa Senhora do Rosário, dedicated to Our Lady of the Rosary, is the episcopal see of the pre-diocesan Roman Catholic Territorial Prelature of Itacoatiara.

History

  • In 1655, an aldeia (hamlet) named Aroaquis, on Aibi Island on the Madeira River, was founded by Portuguese Jesuit missionary António Vieira. It was thereafter relocated several times due to aggression by the indigenous Mura people.[2]
  • By the early 18th century, the mission had evolved into or was succeeded by the aldeia Abacaxi. In 1757, its inhabitants moved to the left bank of the Amazon River, where Itacoatiara is now located.
  • In 1758, Francisco Xavier de Mendonça passed through the region for the second time in order to found the captaincy of São José do Rio Negro.[3]
  • In 1759, the aldeia was elevated to the category of village and named Serpa.
  • The village was suppressed as a municipality in 1833 and affected by the Cabanagem revolt from 1835 to 1840, after which it was restored.
  • Itacoatiara became a municipality again by law 74 of December 10, 1857, but in 1858 it was demoted to village, named Nossa Senhora do Rosário de Serpa. Finally, on April 25, 1874, by law 283, Itacoatiara was elevated to the category of city, with its current name.[4]

Demography

Ethnic composition

Itacoatiara's demography reflects the cultural and economic influences from Portuguese colonization, alongside significant contributions from indigenous peoples. The Amerindians initiated human occupation in the Amazon, and their descendants, known as caboclos, adapted closely to the forest environment. The population's historical formation results from the miscegenation of Brazil's three primary ethnic groups: Native Brazilian, European (primarily Portuguese), and African, forming the region's caboclos or mestizos. Later arrivals of immigrants, particularly Japanese, Arabs (including Syrians and Lebanese), and Jews mostly from Morocco, contributed to a distinctive local culture, values, and lifestyle. Geography

The town is located 176 km east by air or 201 km downstream by river from Manaus. It is the second most populous municipality in the state, on the bank of the Amazon River. [6] [7]
