New Moti Bagh

New Moti Bagh is a residential complex in South Delhi, India, established as General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) for central government employees and officials.[1] Spanning approximately 105 acres with over 60% of the area dedicated to green spaces and only 18% ground coverage, the colony emphasizes secure, upscale living in the New Delhi Municipal Council jurisdiction.[2] Redeveloped by the National Buildings Construction Corporation (NBCC), it holds Indian Green Building Council (IGBC) certification as the largest green home complex of its kind, incorporating sustainable design and energy-efficient features.[3] In 2025, New Moti Bagh was designated an "Anupam Colony" by the NDMC for pioneering sustainable waste management, including 100% source segregation and a circular system for household waste reuse.[4][5]

Historical Background

Origins in Post-Independence Delhi

Following India's independence on August 15, 1947, Delhi experienced a rapid population surge due to the influx of approximately 500,000 refugees from Pakistan, nearly doubling the city's population from 917,000 in 1941 to 1.74 million by 1951.[6] This demographic shift, coupled with the expansion of central government operations as the national capital, created acute housing shortages for civil servants and administrative staff.[7] The Central Public Works Department (CPWD) responded by acquiring agricultural lands in South Delhi from villages and initiating planned colonies to provide organized accommodation, prioritizing low-density bungalows and quarters for mid- to senior-level officials.[8][9] The Moti Bagh area, previously comprising green fields and rural settlements, was among the sites selected for such development in the early 1950s, aligning with the Delhi Master Plan's zoning for residential use in southern extensions.[10][11] CPWD constructed modest government quarters here to house expanding bureaucracy, reflecting a broader post-independence strategy to consolidate administrative functions away from overcrowded central zones while preserving green spaces amid rapid urbanization.[9] These initial structures emphasized functionality over luxury, accommodating clerical and executive personnel amid Delhi's transformation from a colonial outpost to a bustling capital.[12] By the late 1950s, the Moti Bagh site's low-rise accommodations had become integral to South Delhi's elite residential fabric under the New Delhi Municipal Council (NDMC), though maintenance challenges and densification pressures foreshadowed future upgrades.[13] The area's post-independence origins thus stemmed from pragmatic responses to partition-induced crises and governmental growth, laying the groundwork for later interventions to modernize aging infrastructure without displacing core zoning for official housing.[14]

Redevelopment Initiative (2007â€“2014)

The redevelopment of the General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) at New Moti Bagh was approved by the Union Cabinet in 2007 as a self-financing initiative to replace aging government housing with modern facilities for senior officials.[15] The project involved demolishing 1,122 outdated Type-II to Type-VI dwelling units and constructing 2,784 new units ranging from Type-I to Type-VIII, including low-rise bungalows and mid-rise apartments, across approximately 105 acres while maintaining low ground coverage of 18%.[16] Funding was generated through the sale of a contiguous three-acre plot to the Leela Group for a hotel development, yielding approximately â‚¹610 crore, of which â‚¹523 crore was allocated directly to the project costs.[17] Construction commenced in late 2007 under the execution of the National Buildings Construction Corporation (NBCC), proceeding in phases to minimize disruption to existing occupants.[2] Key sustainability measures included preserving all existing trees, planting 4,156 new ones (10% fruit-bearing), implementing a 100,000-litre-per-day wastewater treatment plant supplying treated water to the New Delhi Municipal Council, solar water heating for higher-type units, and 50% solar-powered street lighting.[2] The design emphasized 60% green coverage, terrace gardening, and integrated solid waste management, aligning with early adoption of eco-friendly standards in public housing redevelopment.[2] Phased handover began in 2012, with full completion and allocation to the Directorate of Estates by 2014, marking it as one of the first large-scale GPRA projects under a public-private partnership-inspired model without direct government outlay.[17] In January 2014, the complex received Indian Green Building Council (IGBC) Green Homes Silver certification, recognizing it as India's largest such green residential project at the time, with features like energy-efficient systems reducing operational costs.[2] This initiative set a precedent for subsequent GPRA redevelopments in Delhi, demonstrating viability of land-monetization for infrastructure upgrades.[18]

Location and Layout

Geographical Context and Boundaries

New Moti Bagh is situated in the southwestern sector of New Delhi, falling under the jurisdiction of the New Delhi Municipal Council (NDMC) and the South West Delhi district of the National Capital Territory of Delhi. The complex occupies approximately 105 acres of land, characterized by the typical urban topography of Delhi's southern plains, with elevations around 216-236 meters above sea level. Its central coordinates are roughly 28.58Â°N latitude and 77.18Â°E longitude.[2][19][20] The site's boundaries are defined by major arterial roads and adjacent residential zones, including the Inner Ring Road to the north, which facilitates connectivity to central Delhi, and Africa Avenue to the west, linking to areas like Chanakyapuri. To the south, it interfaces with Ramakrishna Puram (RK Puram), while eastern limits approach traditional Moti Bagh extensions and Netaji Nagar. This positioning places New Moti Bagh in proximity to key landmarks such as Bhikaji Cama Place and Sarojini Nagar market, enhancing its accessibility via the Delhi Metro's Magenta Line and multiple bus routes.[21][22][1] The locality's pin code is 110021, reflecting its integration into New Delhi's postal network, and it benefits from the surrounding green buffers typical of government housing enclaves, though urban expansion has integrated it into a densely connected infrastructural grid.[19]

Site Planning and Zoning

New Moti Bagh is situated within Planning Zone D of the Master Plan for Delhi 2021 (MPD-2021), classified primarily as a residential use zone (RD) encompassing bungalow areas and employer housing under a no-industrial-activity designation.[23] Permitted uses include plotted housing for residences and resident welfare association offices up to 50 square meters, as well as group housing featuring residential flats, limited retail such as grocery and chemist shops, community rooms, crÃ¨ches, senior citizen recreation areas, and swimming pools on plots of at least 3,000 square meters (or 1,670 square meters in redevelopment contexts).[23] Professional activities by doctors or lawyers are allowed up to 50% of floor area ratio (FAR) per dwelling unit, while public and semi-public facilities like pre-primary schools and nursing homes require abutting roads with minimum right-of-way widths of 18 meters for A- and B-category colonies or 13.5 meters for E-, F-, and G-categories.[23] Mixed-use developments are restricted to notified streets meeting these right-of-way thresholds, with up to 10% FAR for commercial purposes in existing government buildings and 30% of permissible FAR for residential conversion in public facilities such as police stations.[23] The site's redevelopment planning prioritized efficient land use through clustered block configurations, basement parking to minimize surface imperviousness, and retention of primary street patterns for vehicular access.[24] Pedestrian networks were integrated with continuous walkways, shaded zones, and green linkages to enhance connectivity and usability within the premises.[24] This approach aligned with MPD-2021 provisions encouraging detailed schemes for bungalow zone redevelopment to incorporate modern services, on-site parking, and clearances from traffic police and fire departments, while capping density at up to 200 dwelling units per hectare with 15% FAR allocation for economically weaker vocational training contexts, and building heights limited to 26 meters or as per norms with requisite approvals.[23] Additional height constraints apply via Airport Authority of India guidelines due to aviation proximity.[24] The layout consolidated green spaces for multi-age recreation, emphasizing mature tree preservation and new planting to balance increased road surfaces and density, which rose from 1.12 dwelling units per hectare pre-redevelopment to 11.04 post-completion.[24] Overall, the planning supports MPD-2021's focus on rejuvenating pre-1962 residential areas through incentivized recycling, excluding industrial redevelopment to preserve the site's employer housing character.[23]

Residential Structures

Bungalow and Apartment Configurations

New Moti Bagh features 116 independent bungalows categorized into Type VIII and Type VII configurations, designed for senior government officials under the General Pool Residential Accommodation scheme. Type VIII bungalows, numbering 14 units, are the highest tier, each spanning approximately 8,250 square feet with eight bedrooms, front and rear lawns, two garages, and space for four car parkings.[25] [26] These structures prioritize privacy and space, including attached bathrooms and additional amenities suited for cabinet ministers and top civil servants.[26] Type VII bungalows, comprising 102 duplex units, offer two-storey layouts with an office, drawing and dining rooms, a family lounge, two bedrooms on the ground floor, two on the upper floor (each with attached bathrooms), a modular kitchen, three car parking spaces, and a garden area of about 1,350 square meters.[17] [26] Intended for mid-to-senior level bureaucrats, these bungalows emphasize functionality with separate work and living zones while maintaining lower density compared to central Lutyens' Delhi equivalents.[17] The complex includes 376 Type VI multistorey apartments housed in six towers, each up to 16 storeys high, providing three-bedroom units with drawing and dining areas, two bathrooms, a kitchen, two balconies, and two dedicated car parking spaces per flat.[17] [26] These apartments target slightly lower entitlement levels within the government hierarchy, balancing urban density with spacious interiors exceeding 3,000 square feet in some cases, and incorporate modern construction for energy efficiency.[17]

Occupancy and Allocation Processes

New Moti Bagh primarily houses senior civil servants entitled to Type-VII and Type-VIII general pool residential accommodation (GPRA), including Secretaries to the Government of India and equivalent ranks from the All India Services and central government departments. The complex features 60 such units designated for online allotment to these officials, managed under the Central Government General Pool Residential Accommodation Rules, 2017 (CGGPRA Rules). Allottees occupy the bungalows and apartments on a license fee basis, with tenancies governed by standard GPRA lease agreements that require vacating upon retirement, transfer, or loss of entitlement, typically within specified notice periods.[27] Allocation occurs through the Directorate of Estates' Automated System of Allotment (ASA), an online platform integrated with the eSampada portal, where eligible officers apply based on their posting in Delhi and verified entitlement derived from pay scales, seniority, and rotational posting norms. For the 40 Type-VII houses in the Secretaries Pool, applications are processed via e-Awas, prioritizing secretaries and equivalent officers through a draw-of-lots or seniority-based mechanism when demand exceeds supply, with allotments issued digitally upon payment of initial license fees.[28] Eligibility verification includes cross-checks against employee codes, current accommodations, and compliance with rules prohibiting multiple entitlements or unauthorized sub-letting.[27] Allotment letters specify occupancy conditions, such as restrictions on modifications and mandatory adherence to maintenance protocols enforced by the New Delhi Municipal Council (NDMC). Post-allotment, occupancy involves online payment of monthly license fees scaled to accommodation typeâ€”approximately 7.5% to 10% of basic pay plus dearness allowance for Type-VII/VIII unitsâ€”and periodic declarations for utilities like water and electricity, with non-compliance risking cancellation.[27] The process emphasizes transparency via digital tracking, reducing discretionary interventions, though senior officials may receive priority for prime units based on protocol seniority lists maintained by the Cabinet Secretariat. Vacancy rates are minimized through automated reminders and recovery actions for overstayals, ensuring high utilization for entitled personnel.[29]

Amenities and Services

Recreational and Communal Facilities

The New Moti Bagh residential complex features a central club house serving as the primary venue for communal and recreational activities among residents, primarily central government employees. The club house includes a swimming pool, which was constructed as part of the redevelopment and formally handed over to the residents' welfare association by the Ministry of Urban Development in early 2014.[30] Additional indoor facilities within the club encompass a gymnasium and spaces for games such as billiards and table tennis, fostering social interaction and fitness for occupants.[31] Outdoor recreational amenities emphasize the complex's emphasis on green living, with approximately 7.5 acres of forested areas encircled by dedicated jogging tracks for daily exercise and leisure walks.[32] Children's parks and play areas are integrated into the layout to support family-oriented activities, complementing the Indian Green Building Council's platinum-rated sustainable design that prioritizes open green spaces covering over 50% of the 143-acre site. Communal halls within the club house also host events, though disputes over their management arose post-handover, leading to resident concerns about access and maintenance.[30] These facilities are managed by the residents' welfare association under New Delhi Municipal Council oversight, ensuring upkeep through user fees and government allocations.

Essential Utilities and Maintenance

Essential utilities in New Moti Bagh, a General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) complex, are managed through a combination of municipal and central government agencies, ensuring reliable supply to its residential bungalows and apartments. Electricity is supplied by BSES Rajdhani Power Limited (BRPL), the primary discom for south and west Delhi, with distribution infrastructure including sub-stations serving the area; NDMC facilitates local inquiries and complaint resolution via dedicated service centers for Moti Bagh zones.[33] Water supply is provided by the Delhi Jal Board (DJB), which handles connections, metering, and distribution, supplemented by NDMC's oversight for contamination complaints and local infrastructure maintenance.[34][35] Piped natural gas (PNG) connections are available through authorized providers, as per GPRA allotment guidelines requiring residents to coordinate with relevant utilities. Sewage and wastewater treatment incorporate on-site facilities, including a dedicated waste water management plant operational since the 2007â€“2014 redevelopment, which processes effluent to support sustainability goals; this aligns with the complex's Indian Green Building Council (IGBC) Platinum certification for efficient resource use.[2] Additionally, solar water heating systems are installed in 492 flats and bungalows, reducing reliance on conventional energy for hot water needs.[2] Maintenance responsibilities are divided among agencies, with the Central Public Works Department (CPWD) overseeing structural, civil, electrical, and horticultural upkeep for GPRA accommodations, including routine repairs and asset management as per CPWD protocols.[36] NDMC handles municipal services such as road cleaning, drain maintenance, and waste collection, recently designating New Moti Bagh as its fifth "Anupam Colony" on October 9, 2025, for achieving 100% household waste segregation and implementing circular economy practices like composting and recycling.[37][38] This model emphasizes resident participation in upkeep, supported by NDMC's sanitation drives, ensuring the 143-acre site's infrastructure remains functional amid high occupancy by government allottees.[4]

Environmental and Sustainability Efforts

Green Certification and Design Features

The New Moti Bagh General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) complex, redeveloped by NBCC (India) Ltd., achieved Indian Green Building Council (IGBC) Green Homes Silver certification in 2014, marking it as the largest such certified green residential project in India at the time.[2][39] This certification recognizes adherence to standards for energy efficiency, water conservation, waste management, and sustainable site planning across its 4,608 units ranging from Type-II to Type-VII flats.[40][16] Key design features include an integrated solid waste management system that processes biodegradable waste into compost and fuel, contributing to reduced landfill dependency and on-site resource recovery.[2] The complex incorporates rainwater harvesting infrastructure and an on-site sewage treatment plant (STP) for treated water reuse in landscaping and non-potable needs, enhancing water self-sufficiency.[4] Energy-efficient elements, such as optimized building envelopes and lighting systems compliant with IGBC criteria, were prioritized during redevelopment to minimize operational carbon footprints.[41] In 2025, New Moti Bagh was designated as the New Delhi Municipal Councilâ€™s (NDMC) fifth "Anupam Colony," reflecting 100% source segregation of waste, on-site composting, briquetting of refuse-derived fuel, and a Reduce-Reuse-Recycle (RRR) center, further embedding circular economy principles into its operational framework.[4] These features build on the initial IGBC benchmarks, demonstrating sustained commitment to zero-waste goals without reliance on external certifications beyond the original Silver rating.[39]

Water Conservation and Waste Processing

New Moti Bagh incorporates rainwater harvesting systems to capture and store precipitation for non-potable uses, contributing to groundwater recharge efforts in the complex.[5] [42] An on-site Sewage Treatment Plant (STP) processes wastewater generated within the residential area, enabling the recycling of treated effluent for irrigation and flushing purposes, thereby reducing reliance on municipal water supplies.[43] [4] These measures align with broader sustainability protocols established during the complex's redevelopment by NBCC (India) Ltd., which emphasized treated water recycling innovations.[3] Waste processing in New Moti Bagh achieves 100% source segregation at the household level, with wet waste directed to an Organic Waste Converter (OWC) that produces compost for maintaining the colony's parks and gardens.[5] [43] Horticultural waste, including dry leaves, is converted into fuel briquettes through on-site briquetting processes, while dry waste and plastics undergo recycling via a Reduce-Reuse-Recycle (RRR) center equipped with smart vending machines for material exchange.[4] [42] This integrated waste-to-wealth system minimizes landfill contributions and supports a circular economy model, as recognized by the New Delhi Municipal Council (NDMC) in designating the area as its 5th Anupam Colony on October 9, 2025.[4]

Security Framework

Perimeter Defenses and Surveillance

New Moti Bagh employs a dedicated perimeter security framework designed for the protection of its high-profile residents, primarily senior government officials. The complex features three main entry gates equipped with scanners and comprehensive CCTV coverage to monitor vehicular and pedestrian access.[15] This setup integrates access control measures to verify entrants, reflecting the township-like configuration of the 143-acre enclave within the New Delhi Municipal Council area. On-site security personnel, including guards, maintain round-the-clock vigilance along the perimeter boundaries, supplemented by the overall stringent protocols typical of VIP residential zones in Delhi.[15] While specific details on advanced intrusion detection systems like vibration sensors or infrared fencing are not publicly documented for this site, the foundational reliance on visual surveillance and entry-point screening ensures controlled ingress and egress, minimizing unauthorized access risks. The system's implementation aligns with the project's completion phases around 2011â€“2012, prioritizing resident privacy and safety amid its location in a diplomatically sensitive neighborhood.[44]

Internal Protocols and Governance

The internal protocols and governance of New Moti Bagh, a General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) complex housing senior government officials, are structured around resident compliance with central government regulations and coordination through the local Residents' Welfare Association (RWA). The GPRA Residents Association manages community welfare, maintenance coordination, and liaison with developers like NBCC (India) Limited for initiatives such as waste management and environmental projects.[45][46] Core internal protocols derive from the Central Government General Pool Residential Accommodation Rules, 2017, administered by the Directorate of Estates, Ministry of Housing and Urban Affairs. These mandate exclusive residential use of accommodations, prohibiting commercial activities, sub-letting, or unauthorized alterations to structures or landscaping, with violations incurring allotment cancellation, damages (up to three times the license fee for sub-letting), and potential debarment from future allotments.[47] Residents must maintain cleanliness, pay electricity and water charges promptly, report unsafe conditions, and cooperate fully with repair works by the Central Public Works Department (CPWD) or equivalent agencies; non-compliance can lead to eviction proceedings.[47] Visitor-related protocols, integral to internal security, require allottees to intimate the Directorate of Estates about any non-family guests staying beyond 15 days, facilitating oversight of prolonged stays.[47] Domestic staff and temporary occupants, such as caretakers during allottee absences (limited to six months with prior notice), must align with these rules to prevent unauthorized access. Governance is supplemented by the RWA, recognized under Department of Personnel and Training (DoPT) guidelines, which require adoption of a model constitution for financial grants and operations.[48] The RWA's executive committee, comprising elected office-bearers like president and secretary, handles resident disputes, organizes general body meetings, and advocates for infrastructure upkeep, ensuring alignment with GPRA rules while fostering community cohesion in the 143-acre complex.[49] This dual frameworkâ€”regulatory enforcement by the Directorate of Estates and localized administration by the RWAâ€”maintains orderly internal operations without dedicated RWA-specific security mandates, deferring to broader perimeter defenses.

Controversies and Debates

Criticisms of Elitism and Resource Allocation

Critics, including architect Ranjit Sabikhi, have characterized the New Moti Bagh residences as perpetuating elitist urban patterns akin to Lutyens' Delhi, featuring oversized houses on expansive plots with generous lawns that serve primarily as status symbols for senior bureaucrats and ministers, such as the 14 Type VIII accommodations spanning 2,500 square meters each.[50][51] This setup displaced lower-grade government employees into smaller Type IV-VI units, highlighting disparities in housing quality within the civil services hierarchy.[51] The project's funding model has drawn scrutiny for resource allocation priorities, with â‚¹523 crore of the â‚¹610 crore proceeds from selling 3 acres of public land in the Diplomatic Enclave to a hotel developer directed toward the complex's construction between 2009 and 2012.[17] Detractors argue this exemplifies the power elite leveraging public assets to enhance their own accommodations, a pattern embedded in the broader General Pool Residential Accommodation (GPRA) redevelopment scheme costing â‚¹32,835 crore across seven Delhi colonies.[17][52] Gating the 143-acre complex, despite its status as government land, has been faulted for fostering social segregation by restricting public access and creating securitized enclaves, which privatize communal resources and exacerbate urban divides amid Delhi's housing shortages for non-elite populations.[51] Such developments, funded through self-financing via land sales and commercial exploitation, are seen by some as contravening public trusteeship principles, prioritizing bureaucratic perks over equitable urban resource distribution.[51][52]

Specific Incidents and Resolutions

In 2016, Congress MP Adhir Ranjan Chowdhury faced legal action for overstaying nearly two years in a Type-8 ministerial bungalow at 14, New Moti Bagh, to which he held no entitlement, despite being offered alternative Type-6 accommodation in January 2015.[53] The government enforced eviction by disconnecting utilities, prompting Supreme Court intervention.[53] On February 6, 2016, the court ruled the overstaying a malpractice, ordered immediate vacation, and directed Chowdhury to secure temporary housing independently, upholding no legal right to retain the property.[53] In late 2013, the New Moti Bagh Residents' Welfare Association (RWA) disputed the Union Urban Development Ministry's management of the local community centre, alleging it was fenced off by a contractor for private use, including a charged New Year event at Rs 6,000 per couple, while blocking residents' access to the Rs 600 crore-valued land.[54] The RWA, representing senior officials and judges, accused the ministry of prioritizing "petty private interests" via a December 17 circular.[54] No formal resolution occurred; the RWA threatened litigation against the ministry's actions.[54] A July 24, 2024, burglary targeted a government official's residence in the Moti Bagh area, involving broken locks, ransacked rooms, and theft of jewellery and cash during school hours when families were absent.[55] Police used CCTV of a suspicious vehicle to arrest four suspectsâ€”Ram Ji Lal, Gopal Kumar, Deepak Kumar, and receiver Rahul Rastogiâ€”some with prior criminal records, resolving the case through recovery efforts and gang reconnaissance evidence.[55] In August 2024, tensions arose near a Moti Bagh temple over disputed land, where attempts to remove 18-19 temporary shelters housing CPWD maintenance workers from diverse communities led to reported damage and threats.[56] The conflict involved the temple, local RWA, and CPWD; police investigated following an August 10 call.[56] Affected dwellers were relocated 800 meters to Baratghar Begum Zaidi, with ongoing legal proceedings.[56]

Recent Advancements

Sustainability Milestones Post-2014

Following the completion of its redevelopment, New Moti Bagh achieved formal green building certification on August 28, 2015, recognizing its integration of energy-efficient designs, low ground coverage of 18%, and over 60% green space across 105 acres.[57] This milestone built on earlier GRIHA standards adherence, emphasizing sustainable urban redevelopment for government pooled residential accommodations.[16] In 2016, the complex received the 9th CIDC Vishwakarma Award from the Construction Industry Development Council, honoring NBCC's execution of sustainability features such as rainwater harvesting and treated water recycling processes.[58] These innovations included on-site recycling of sewage treatment plant output for landscape irrigation, reducing freshwater dependency.[41] Operational advancements continued with the establishment of a circular waste management system, achieving 100% household source segregation by 2025.[5] On October 9, 2025, the New Delhi Municipal Council designated New Moti Bagh as its fifth "Anupam Colony,"è¡¨å½° its model practices in waste processing, reuse, and integration with rainwater harvesting and sewage treatment for zero-waste discharge goals.[59][5] This recognition highlighted community-driven initiatives, including staff training for composting and recycling, positioning the complex as a benchmark for urban sustainability in Delhi.[42]

Infrastructure Updates and Future Outlook

In March 2025, the New Delhi Municipal Council (NDMC) announced plans to construct a 9-kilometer dedicated cycling track connecting New Moti Bagh to South Block, aimed at enhancing safe non-motorized connectivity and promoting sustainable urban mobility under NGO oversight.[60] This initiative builds on earlier approvals for a similar 10-kilometer corridor linking the complex to Central Secretariat, reflecting ongoing efforts to integrate pedestrian-friendly infrastructure amid Delhi's traffic challenges.[61] The Sports Authority of India (SAI) received sanction in July 2019 for creating sports infrastructure at New Moti Bagh Complex under the Khelo India scheme, with a budget of â‚¹2.80 crore, focusing on facility upgrades to support resident athletic activities.[62] As of early reports, progress stood at 15% completion with a target finish by December 2020, though delays typical in public projects suggest potential extensions or phased implementation.[63] Additional funding for renovation was allocated in June 2024 to the local association, indicating sustained investment in recreational amenities. Looking ahead, New Moti Bagh's infrastructure aligns with the NDMC's Sub-City Development Plan for New Delhi, emphasizing resilient urban features like expanded green corridors and efficient transport links to reduce vehicular dependency. Completion of the cycling network could serve as a model for adjoining areas, while broader Delhi Master Plan 2021 provisions for enhanced public amenities forecast incremental upgrades in utilities and connectivity, contingent on funding and execution timelines.[64] These developments prioritize empirical improvements in livability over expansive new builds, given the complex's established green framework.
