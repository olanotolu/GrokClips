2023 European Rally Championship

Previous: 2022 European Rally Championship
Next: 2024 European Rally Championship The 2023 European Rally Championship was the 71st season of the FIA European Rally Championship, the European continental championship series in rallying. The season was also the eleventh following the merge between the European Rally Championship and the Intercontinental Rally Challenge. Hayden Paddon became the drivers champion. Hayden Paddon won his first ERC drivers' championship.

Competitions

  • FIA ERC: Main championship for Rally2 cars (RC2), the premier class in the series.[1]
  • FIA ERC3: Second tier, specifically for the Rally3 class.[2]
  • FIA ERC4: Third ERC tier, the first for front-wheel-drive cars. Allows Rally4 and Rally5 cars.
  • FIA ERC Junior: For drivers aged 27 and under on 1 January 2023 in Rally4 and Rally5 cars on Hankook tyres. This championship will be contested over six of the eight rounds.[2]
  • FIA European Rally Championship for Teams: each team can nominate a maximum of three cars (from all categories), counting the two highest-placed cars from each team.[2]

Calendar changes

  • Rally Azores and Rally Catalunya were removed from the calendar.
  • Rally Hungary returned to the calendar after a one-year absence.
  • Royal Rally of Scandinavia, which runs on former Rally Sweden stages, was introduced to the calendar.

Regulation Changes

Regulation changes

Significant changes were made to the tyre regulations for the 2023 season to control tyre usage and costs while ensuring a level playing field for all competitors. Only tyres from FIA-registered manufacturers (Hankook, Michelin, MRF, and Pirelli) were permitted. For tarmac events, each supplier provided one dry tyre specification (featuring three compounds but with the same tread pattern) and one wet tyre specification. For gravel events, each supplier offered one gravel tyre specification (three compounds, same pattern). Manufacturers could substitute one tarmac and one gravel tyre compound per season, an irreversible decision notified to the FIA at least one month in advance. The FIA oversaw tyre barcodes and reserved the right to prohibit or mandate specific tyres.[12] Additionally, the joker declaration option for Rally2 cars was removed, limiting homologation flexibility for those vehicles.[2] The overall rally format remained consistent with previous seasons. Events were held over four days, consisting of free practice and a qualifying stage for Rally2 drivers. Starting positions for leg one were chosen by the drivers, with the top 15 from the qualifying stage selecting first, followed by drivers with FIA priority status, then the top 15 point scorers from the 2022 ERC season, and finally the remaining Rally2 drivers.[13] The first 15 cars started at 2-minute intervals, with the rest at 1-minute intervals. For leg two, the top 15 cars started in reverse order of the leg one classification.[13]
