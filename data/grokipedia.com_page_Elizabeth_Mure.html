Elizabeth Mure

Elizabeth Mure (died before May 1355), daughter of Sir Adam Mure of Rowallan, was a Scottish noblewoman who entered into a clandestine relationship with Robert Stewart—later Robert II, King of Scots (r. 1371–1390)—initially as his mistress before their union was formalized and legitimized.[1][2] Their marriage, contracted around 1346 but irregular due to consanguinity within prohibited degrees under canon law, was validated by papal dispensation issued on 2 May 1347, which also retroactively legitimized their at least ten children born prior to the dispensation.[3][2] Among these offspring were John Stewart, who succeeded as Robert III; Robert Stewart, Duke of Albany; and others whose claims facilitated the establishment and succession of the Stewart dynasty on the Scottish throne.[2] Though little is documented of her personal life beyond her familial ties and burial at Paisley Abbey, Mure's role as progenitor of the royal line underscores her historical significance amid the dynastic maneuvers of 14th-century Scotland.[1][2]

Early Life and Origins

Birth and Parentage

Elizabeth Mure was born around 1320 at Rowallan Castle in Ayrshire, Scotland, the seat of her family's estates.[3][4] She was the daughter of Sir Adam Mure, laird of Rowallan, a knight and local landowner whose family held the barony from at least the early 14th century.[4][5] Her mother is commonly identified as Janet (or Joanna) Mure of Pokelly, a nearby property in Ayrshire, though some genealogical records express uncertainty about this attribution due to limited contemporary documentation.[5][6] The Mures of Rowallan traced their lineage to earlier Norman or Anglo-Norman settlers in southwestern Scotland, maintaining a modest noble status through land holdings and feudal ties rather than high royal office.[7]

Family Background and Social Status

Elizabeth Mure was the daughter of Sir Adam Mure of Rowallan, a knight who held the estate of Rowallan Castle in Ayrshire, Scotland, during the early 14th century.[8] Sir Adam, born circa 1290, succeeded his father William Mure and married around 1315, establishing the family's position through landownership in the Kilmarnock area.[9] The Mure lineage traced back to the 13th century, with the family establishing itself on Rowallan lands by the 1200s as part of Clan Muir, a Lowland Scottish kindred focused on regional agrarian and martial roles.[10] Her mother's identity remains uncertain in historical records, though contemporary genealogical accounts commonly identify her as Janet (or Joan) Mure of Pokelly, from a cadet branch of the same clan, suggesting possible kinship ties within the Mures.[6] This union reflects typical alliances among Ayrshire gentry, prioritizing local consolidation of holdings over broader noble connections. Elizabeth was likely born around 1320 at Rowallan Castle, positioning her within a household of moderate means centered on feudal obligations and knightly service to the Scottish crown.[5] Socially, the Mures of Rowallan occupied the status of lairds and lesser nobility, with knightly rank conferring prestige but limited influence beyond Ayrshire; they lacked the vast territorial power or royal ties of magnates like the Stewarts.[11] Sir Adam's knighthood, possibly earned through service in the Wars of Independence post-Bannockburn (1314), underscored their alignment with Brucean loyalism, yet the family's resources were primarily local, tied to castle-based lordship rather than national offices. This gentry standing elevated Elizabeth above commoners but marked her as a suitable match for concubinage rather than immediate noble wedlock with high stewards, highlighting 14th-century Scottish hierarchies where royal-adjacent figures like Robert Stewart sought dispensations for such unions.[7]

Relationship with Robert Stewart

Initial Union as Concubine

Elizabeth Mure, daughter of Sir Adam Mure of Rowallan, entered into an informal union with Robert Stewart, High Steward of Scotland, around 1336, when she was approximately sixteen years old and likely already pregnant. This relationship, lacking ecclesiastical sanction, functioned as concubinage under canon law, a common arrangement among Scottish nobility despite Church prohibitions on extramarital unions.[3][1] The couple cohabited openly, with Elizabeth bearing Robert their firstborn son, John Stewart (who succeeded as Robert III), on an unspecified date in 1337. Subsequent children followed rapidly, including daughters such as Jean and Katherine, and sons like Walter, establishing a family dynamic centered on Rowallan Castle in Ayrshire. These offspring, born prior to any formal marital rite, faced potential illegitimacy under strict canon law, though Scottish custom often tolerated such handfast-like unions for political alliances.[3][12] Robert, as heir presumptive through his mother Marjorie Bruce, prioritized the union for dynastic continuity amid the Second War of Scottish Independence, where informal partnerships facilitated rapid family expansion without delaying for papal approval. Elizabeth's role as de facto consort involved managing household affairs and supporting Robert's guardianship duties from 1338 to 1341, though her lower social status—gentry rather than nobility—drew no recorded contemporary criticism until succession disputes arose.[1][3]

Birth of Children and Family Dynamics

Elizabeth Mure and Robert Stewart, the High Steward of Scotland, began producing offspring around 1336 following their informal union, with births occurring amid the Second War of Scottish Independence.[1] The couple had at least nine children prior to their papal dispensation for marriage in 1347, consisting of four sons and five daughters born illegitimate under canon law but treated as legitimate heirs by Robert in Scottish custom.[13] Exact birth dates are sparsely recorded, reflecting the era's limited documentation for non-royal unions, though the eldest son, John Stewart (who succeeded as Robert III), was born circa 1337 in Carrick.[14] Subsequent sons included Walter Stewart, created Earl of Fife around 1361 but dying young in 1362 without issue; Robert Stewart, who predeceased his father; and Alexander Stewart, later notorious as the Earl of Buchan and "Wolf of Badenoch" for his turbulent career.[15] The daughters—among them Elizabeth, Jean, Margaret, Mary, and possibly Katherine—were strategically married to Scottish nobles, such as Elizabeth to David Lindsay, Earl of Crawford, to forge alliances amid ongoing feudal conflicts.[4] These unions underscore Robert's use of family ties to consolidate power as Guardian of Scotland (1338–1341 and 1346 onward). Family dynamics centered on Robert's Ayrshire estates, including Dundonald Castle, where the household operated as a de facto royal court despite the concubine status. Robert, as a key Bruce ally and regent figure, integrated the children into governance early, educating sons in martial and administrative roles while leveraging daughters for diplomacy, though the irregular marriage drew ecclesiastical scrutiny absent resolution until 1347.[15] No contemporary accounts detail interpersonal tensions, but the rapid succession of births—spanning roughly a decade—indicates a stable cohabitation, with Elizabeth managing domestic affairs in Robert's frequent absences for military campaigns.[4] This arrangement prioritized dynastic continuity over canonical form, reflecting pragmatic medieval noble priorities where paternal acknowledgment trumped initial illegitimacy.[1]

Marriage and Legitimization

Papal Dispensation of 1347

The papal dispensation of 1347 was issued by Pope Clement VI at Avignon on 22 November, granting Robert Stewart, High Steward of Scotland, and Elizabeth Mure permission to contract a valid marriage despite their impediment of consanguinity in the fourth degree.[4][16] This degree of relation arose from shared ancestry tracing back through Scottish noble lines, rendering their prior union uncanonical under ecclesiastical law, which prohibited marriages within four degrees without dispensation.[17] The document explicitly legitimized all children born to the couple prior to the dispensation, including at least four sons and five daughters from their relationship that had begun around 1336, thereby securing their inheritance rights amid ongoing Scottish noble succession disputes.[4][3] The request for dispensation likely stemmed from Robert's rising political prominence as Guardian of Scotland and heir presumptive to the throne, necessitating formal regularization of his family to preempt challenges to his lineage's legitimacy.[3] Pope Clement VI, during the Avignon Papacy, routinely issued such bulls to influential secular figures, often in exchange for political alignment or fees, though no direct evidence ties this one to specific concessions from Robert.[3] Following receipt of the bull, Robert and Elizabeth underwent a canonical remarriage ceremony circa 1348–1349, affirming the dispensation's effect and aligning their union with church requirements for indissolubility and progeny validation.[16] This act resolved prior irregularities without annulling the children's prior status under Scottish custom, where informal unions were often tolerated among nobility.[17]

Implications for Status and Succession

The papal dispensation of 22 November 1347, granted by Pope Clement VI, transformed Elizabeth Mure's position from that of Robert Stewart's long-term concubine to his lawful wife, despite their prior relationship being impeded by consanguinity in the fourth degree and affinity in the third and fourth degrees. This elevation aligned her status with that of a noble consort to the High Steward of Scotland, affirming the legitimacy of their union under canon law and mitigating prior ecclesiastical irregularities. Prior to this, their cohabitation had produced at least nine children without formal sanction, but the dispensation's validation of the marriage retroactively regularized her role as principal spouse, though she predeceased Robert's accession as king in 1371 and thus never held queenship.[18][2] For succession, the dispensation purported to legitimize the couple's pre-marital offspring, including sons John (later Robert III), Walter, Robert, and Alexander, enabling their inheritance of Stewart estates and titles. However, scholarly analysis reveals canonical limitations: under prevailing Scottish interpretations of church law, children born in concubinage prior to dispensation were not fully retroactively legitimized for royal succession, potentially classifying them as "natural" rather than fully legitimate heirs, though ignorance of the affinity impediment offered partial mitigation. This ambiguity fueled intra-family rivalries, as Robert's subsequent marriage to Euphemia de Ross in 1355 produced sons David and Walter, whose legitimacy was unquestioned and whom Robert initially favored—elevating David to Earl of Strathearn in 1370—prompting parliamentary entailments in 1371 and 1373 to prioritize the Mure sons' line for the crown.[18][19] Despite debates, the legitimization proved effective in practice, as John Stewart succeeded as Robert III in 1390 without successful canonical challenge, securing the Stewart dynasty's hold on the Scottish throne through subsequent generations. Lingering doubts over the Mure children's status resurfaced in later disputes, such as seventeenth-century claims to Strathearn invoking potential illegitimacy to contest royal descent, but these were rejected under doctrines permitting post-nuptial legitimization for inheritance. The arrangement thus stabilized short-term succession while embedding tensions that contributed to Albany stewardship intrigues post-1406.[2][19]

Death and Immediate Aftermath

Date and Circumstances of Death

Elizabeth Mure died before 2 May 1355, as evidenced by a papal dispensation dated that month allowing Robert Stewart to remarry Euphemia de Ross.[1] [20] Her precise date of death remains undocumented in surviving records. No contemporary accounts detail the circumstances surrounding her passing, which appears to have been from natural causes given the absence of references to violence, illness, or other events in reliable historical sources. She was reportedly buried at Paisley Abbey in Renfrewshire, Scotland, a site associated with the Stewart family.[1] [5] Alternative traditions place her burial at the Church of the Blackfriars in Perth, though this lacks corroboration from primary evidence.[21]

Robert's Subsequent Marriage

Following the death of Elizabeth Mure before May 1355, Robert Stewart entered into his second marriage with Euphemia de Ross on 2 May 1355.[22][23] Euphemia (c. 1329–1386), daughter of Hugh, 6th Earl of Ross, was the widow of John Randolph, 3rd Earl of Moray, who had died at the Battle of Neville's Cross in 1346; her first marriage had brought her control over the earldom of Moray and associated northern territories.[3][1] The union appears to have been strategically arranged to bolster Robert's regional power, as Euphemia's inheritance included significant lands in Ross and Moray, areas of ongoing contention amid Anglo-Scottish conflicts.[3][23] A papal dispensation facilitated the marriage, addressing potential consanguinity issues similar to those resolved for Robert's prior union.[24] This alliance integrated Euphemia's holdings into the Stewart patrimony, though it later contributed to succession tensions between children from both marriages upon Robert's accession as king in 1371.[1][25]

Issue and Descendants

List of Children

Elizabeth Mure and Robert II (then Robert Stewart, High Steward of Scotland) had nine children born before their marriage, all of whom were legitimized by the papal dispensation of 22 November 1347.[26][20] These consisted of four sons and five daughters, whose births occurred between approximately 1337 and the mid-1340s.[27] The sons were:
  • John Stewart (c. 1337–1406), later Robert III of Scotland, eldest son and heir who succeeded his father in 1390.[20]
  • Robert Stewart (c. 1340–1420), Earl of Fife (later 1st Duke of Albany), who served as Governor of Scotland and played a key role in regency politics.[20]
  • Alexander Stewart (c. 1343–1405), Earl of Buchan, known as the "Wolf of Badenoch" for his turbulent career involving feuds and ecclesiastical conflicts.[20]
  • Walter Stewart (dates uncertain, d. before 1362), held the position of Lord High Admiral.[20]
The daughters were:
  • Elizabeth Stewart (c. 1346–d. after 1381), married Sir David Lindsay of Crawford as his second wife.[20]
  • Janet Stewart (dates uncertain), married Sir Robert Keith.[20]
  • Margaret Stewart (dates uncertain), married Sir John Swinton.[20]
  • Matilda (or Annabella) Stewart (dates uncertain), married Sir Patrick Hepburn.[20]
  • Mary Stewart (dates uncertain), married Sir William Keith, brother of Robert Keith.[20]
Genealogical records occasionally vary in exact numbering or attribution due to incomplete medieval documentation, but the above represents the core issue accepted in historical accounts.[27][28]

Notable Offspring and Their Roles

John Stewart (c. 1337–1406), the eldest son, succeeded his father as King Robert III of Scots in 1390 following parliamentary confirmation of his legitimacy. Crippled by a horse kick in youth, his rule was undermined by physical frailty and reliance on relatives, leading to governance dominated by his brother Albany and ending with the English capture of his son James (later James I) en route to France in 1406.[3] Walter Stewart (c. 1338–1361), second son, acquired the earldom of Fife by marrying Isabella, suo jure Countess of Fife, around 1360, thereby controlling that strategic lordship. He fought for Scotland at the Battle of Neville's Cross on 17 October 1346 but died without issue, reportedly from wounds sustained there or shortly after.[28] Robert Stewart (c. 1340–1420), third son, rose to prominence as Earl of Atholl, then Earl of Menteith (revoked 1373), and finally 1st Duke of Albany in 1398. As lieutenant to his brother Robert III from 1388 and Guardian of the Kingdom from 1406, he consolidated Stewart power amid royal weakness but faced accusations of neglecting the captive heir James I to preserve his regency until death by stroke.[29] Alexander Stewart (1343–c. 1405), fourth son and known as the Wolf of Badenoch, received the lordship of Badenoch and earldom of Buchan through royal grants. A turbulent Highland magnate, he defied ecclesiastical authority by burning Elgin Cathedral and surrounding lands on 30 May 1390 in retaliation for Bishop Alexander Bur's excommunication over unpaid debts, exemplifying the era's noble lawlessness. Daughters included Jean Stewart, whose marriage to John Lyon, Lord of Glamis, produced descendants linking to later Scottish nobility, notably as an ancestress of Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon, Queen Mother (1900–2002). Other daughters, such as Marjorie (married John Dunbar, Earl of Moray, d. 1390), facilitated dynastic alliances strengthening Stewart influence in northern and eastern Scotland.[1]

Historical Assessment

Role in Stewart Dynasty

Elizabeth Mure served as the foundational matriarch of the Stewart royal line through her union with Robert Stewart, who ascended as Robert II of Scotland in 1371. Their relationship, beginning around 1336 as a clandestine marriage within the fourth degree of consanguinity, produced at least nine children—four sons and five daughters—prior to formal validation. The papal dispensation of 22 November 1347 not only ratified the marriage but also legitimized the offspring, averting potential challenges to their inheritance rights under canon law and securing the Stewart claim to the throne via Robert's Bruce lineage.[4][3] Her progeny, including the eldest son John Stewart (later Robert III, r. 1390–1406), dominated the immediate succession, outnumbering and superseding the children from Robert's subsequent marriage to Euphemia de Ross in 1355. This prioritization of the Mure-Stewart offspring marginalized the Ross line, despite rivalries such as those involving Euphemia's son Alexander Stewart, Earl of Buchan, and ensured dynastic continuity through descendants who ruled Scotland until the union of crowns in 1603. Elizabeth's death circa 1355, before Robert's kingship, precluded any formal queenship or political agency, rendering her influence indirect yet indispensable as the ancestress whose legitimized heirs embodied the dynasty's viability.[4][1] Historians note the resolution of legitimacy disputes—such as those impugning Robert III's status—as pivotal, with contemporary vindications affirming Elizabeth's lawful wifedom based on the dispensation, thereby stabilizing the nascent Stewart monarchy against baronial and ecclesiastical scrutiny. Her modest origins as daughter of Sir Adam Mure of Rowallan contrasted with the dynasty's elevation, underscoring how familial alliances and papal intervention, rather than noble prestige, fortified Stewart rule.[30][4]

Scholarly Views on Her Influence and Legacy

Historians assess Elizabeth Mure's personal influence as minimal and poorly documented, with surviving records offering scant insight into her agency or contributions to Robert the Steward's political maneuvers during the 1330s and 1340s. Contemporary chronicles and charters rarely mention her beyond her familial ties, emphasizing instead the legal irregularities of her union with Robert, which began as a handfast marriage under Gaelic custom before formal papal validation. This obscurity underscores the patriarchal focus of medieval Scottish historiography, where noblewomen's roles were often subsumed under male lineage narratives.[31] Her legacy, however, centers on the dynastic ramifications of the 1347 papal dispensation legitimizing her nine children, which secured the Stewart claim to the throne amid succession crises following David II's childless state. Scholars argue this retroactive sanction not only preempted challenges from rival claimants during the Second War of Independence but also set a precedent for reconciling common-law unions with canon law, influencing later Stewart monarchs' handling of illegitimacy disputes. For instance, analyses of royal bastardy highlight how the dispensation's emphasis on legitimatio per subsequens matrimonium reinforced the viability of her eldest son, John (later Robert III), as heir, thereby embedding the Mure-Stewart offspring as foundational to the dynasty's 300-year rule.[32][33] Critiques of her status also reveal broader scholarly debates on medieval Scottish marriage practices, where her case illustrates the pragmatic flexibility of papal interventions in noble alliances, often prioritizing political stability over strict ecclesiastical doctrine. While some studies view the legitimation as a mere formality expedited by Robert's guardianship role from 1338, others contend it reflected calculated diplomacy to consolidate Stewart influence in Ayrshire and beyond, elevating the Mure family's regional prominence through royal intermarriage. Ultimately, Mure's indirect impact endures via descendants like Robert III and the earls of Fife and Strathearn, whose titles and estates perpetuated Stewart dominance, though her pre-coronation death circa 1354 precluded any queenship or public veneration.[31][34]
