How to Get Away with Murder season 1

The first season of How to Get Away with Murder is the premiere installment of the American legal thriller drama television series created by Peter Nowalk for ABC, consisting of 15 episodes that aired from September 25, 2014, to February 26, 2015.[1][2] Centered on Annalise Keating, a formidable criminal defense attorney and professor at a prestigious Philadelphia university, portrayed by Viola Davis, the season depicts her recruitment of five elite students to work on real cases, only for the group to become ensnared in a high-stakes murder cover-up that unravels their lives through nonlinear storytelling and moral ambiguity.[3][4] Produced by Shondaland in association with ABC Studios, the season drew an average of 10.2 million viewers per episode and achieved an 84% approval rating from critics on Rotten Tomatoes, praised for its suspenseful twists, sharp dialogue, and exploration of ethical dilemmas in the legal system.[5][6] Viola Davis's commanding performance as Annalise earned her the 2015 Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series, marking the first win for an African American woman in that category, alongside nominations for the series in directing and guest acting.[7][8]

Overview

Premise and Episode Structure

The first season of How to Get Away with Murder revolves around Annalise Keating, a formidable criminal defense attorney and professor who teaches a criminal law class provocatively named "How to Get Away with Murder" at a prestigious fictional university in Philadelphia.[9] In this course, Keating challenges her ambitious students with real-world case simulations and selects top performers to intern at her firm, fostering cutthroat competition among them.[10] The core premise examines the intersection of legal advocacy, moral ambiguity, and human frailty as Keating's professional life collides with her personal entanglements.[3] Comprising 15 episodes, the season adopts a nonlinear narrative structure that interweaves present-day events with flash-forwards to a central murder mystery, teasing future consequences from the pilot onward to heighten tension and viewer engagement.[10] [11] This format reveals snippets of the impending crisisâ€”depicting the students in perilâ€”while the main timeline advances Keating's courtroom battles and the group's involvement in her cases, gradually bridging the temporal gaps through episodic revelations.[10] The storytelling emphasizes a fusion of genres: intense legal dramas in courtrooms where Keating employs cunning tactics to secure acquittals, academic pressures testing the students' intellects and loyalties, and underlying personal scandals that erode facades of competence and ethics among the principals.[3] This multifaceted approach propels the narrative, balancing procedural elements with character-driven intrigue without resolving the overarching mystery until the finale.[10]

Broadcast Information

The first season of How to Get Away with Murder premiered on the American Broadcasting Company (ABC) on September 25, 2014.[3] Created by Pete Nowalk and produced under Shonda Rhimes's Shondaland banner, it debuted as part of ABC's Thursday night lineup known as TGIT (Thank God It's Thursday).[12][10] Episodes aired weekly on Thursday evenings at 10:00 p.m. Eastern Time, spanning 15 installments from the pilot through the season finale.[13][2] The season concluded on February 26, 2015, with "It's All Her Fault," marking the end of the initial U.S. broadcast run without mid-season interruptions beyond standard holiday scheduling gaps.[2] In the weeks following its U.S. premiere, distribution deals were secured for initial international rollout, with the series sold to broadcasters across 158 territories by October 2014.[14] These agreements facilitated syndication on various global networks shortly after domestic airing, prior to later streaming availability.[15]

Cast and Characters

Main Characters

Annalise Keating, portrayed by Viola Davis, is a brilliant, charismatic criminal defense attorney and law professor at a prestigious Philadelphia university who teaches a course on criminal law and selects top students to intern at her practice.[3] Her character embodies a shrewd legal strategist known for aggressive tactics in the courtroom and a complex personal life marked by professional ruthlessness.[3] The core group of students, dubbed the "Keating Five," comprises ambitious second-year law students handpicked by Keating for their elite status and involvement in her firm, each displaying competitive drive alongside ethical flexibility in pursuit of success.[13] These include Wes Gibbins (Alfred Enoch), a talented but introspective newcomer favored by Keating; Michaela Pratt (Aja Naomi King), a driven overachiever from humble origins; Asher Millstone (Matt McGorry), a privileged son of a judge with a cocky demeanor; Connor Walsh (Jack Falahee), a cunning and manipulative gay student leveraging intellect for advantage; and Laurel Castillo (Karla Souza), a determined Latina scholar navigating family expectations.[16] Supporting main characters include Nate Lahey (Billy Brown), a dedicated Philadelphia police detective whose role intersects with Keating's legal work, and Frank Delfino (Charlie Weber), her loyal private investigator and fixer who handles discreet operations for the firm.[16]

Recurring and Guest Characters

Sam Keating, portrayed by Tom Verica, serves as Annalise Keating's husband and a tenured professor of psychology at Middleton University, appearing in 11 episodes of season 1.[17] His involvement shapes interpersonal tensions within the Keating household and intersects with the students' legal endeavors, particularly amid suspicions surrounding campus activities.[3] Ophelia Harkness, played by Cicely Tyson, is Annalise's mother, introduced in episode 13 ("Mama's Here Now") to illuminate Annalise's familial origins and emotional underpinnings. Harkness's presence in the season finale underscores themes of maternal influence and Annalise's concealed past, appearing once but with lasting narrative impact on character motivations. Other recurring figures include Lila Stangard (Megan West), a deceased medical student whose case drives investigative arcs across 6 episodes, central to the protagonists' cover-up efforts. Griffin O'Reilly (Lenny Platt), a fraternity president implicated in related events, recurs in 4 episodes, heightening courtroom and interpersonal conflicts. Guest appearances feature case-specific adversaries and allies, such as prosecutor Max St. Vincent (Steven Weber), who challenges Annalise in trial proceedings, and nurse Paula Murphy (Ana Ortiz), involved in a pivotal defense strategy. These one-off roles, often tied to episodic legal battles, introduce twists like witness testimonies or ethical dilemmas without extending beyond single or dual episodes, distinguishing them from multi-episode recurrings by their limited scope in advancing overarching plots.[16]

Production

Development and Writing

Pete Nowalk, who had contributed to Shonda Rhimes's Grey's Anatomy and Scandal since 2008, conceived How to Get Away with Murder as a legal thriller exploring the moral ambiguities of criminal defense work. He pitched the series to Rhimes and ShondaLand partner Betsy Beers by describing a scenario of law students entangled in a murder cover-up, drawing inspiration from 1980s and 1990s films like Presumed Innocent and Jagged Edge that featured layered twists and ethical quandaries in high-profile cases.[18][12] The core pitch highlighted a brilliant but flawed professor mentoring ambitious students through intense courtroom battles, emphasizing dilemmas where defending potentially guilty clients forces confrontations with personal and professional ethics, though dramatized beyond real-world constraints.[12] Nowalk completed the pilot script by December 3, 2013, incorporating research into law school dynamics such as the Socratic method and criminal procedure, aided by consultations with legal experts including Beers's husband, a practicing attorney. ABC greenlit the project straight to series on May 7, 2014, recognizing its potential as a serialized mystery blended with standalone cases modeled on actual defense tactics like aggressive cross-examinations and plea negotiations, but amplified for narrative tension.[19][20] Rhimes served as executive producer, reviewing outlines and scripts to refine the structure while preserving Nowalk's vision of nonlinear storytellingâ€”beginning with flash-forwards to the murder night and weaving multiple perspectives to build suspense around culpability.[12] The season 1 writing room prioritized unpredictability, outlining major twists months ahead during the pilot phase and adapting them amid a rigorous production schedule that yielded episodes every two weeks. This approach embedded causal chains of deception and revelation from the outset, fictionalizing ethical breaches like attorney-client privilege manipulations to heighten stakes without mirroring verifiable precedents, ensuring the framework sustained procedural elements alongside the overarching conspiracy.[18][12]

Casting Process

Viola Davis was cast in the lead role of Annalise Keating on February 25, 2014, shortly after the pilot script was developed under executive producer Shonda Rhimes' banner. This selection aligned with Rhimes' emphasis on diverse, complex protagonists in her productions, marking Davis's first starring television role.[12] Following ABC's series order on May 7, 2014, casting for the supporting ensemble proceeded in mid-2014, focusing on a diverse group of actors to portray the law students known as the Keating Five.[21] Auditions prioritized candidates capable of forming authentic interpersonal dynamics, with the selected performersâ€”Jack Falahee, Aja Naomi King, Matt McGorry, Alfred Enoch, and Karla Souzaâ€”participating in pre-production meetings to cultivate on-screen chemistry before principal photography commenced in July 2014.[22] Securing guest stars for season 1 involved leveraging personal networks; for the role of Ophelia Harkness in episode 16 ("Mama's Here Now"), Davis recommended her former co-star Cicely Tyson from the 1999 television film A Lesson Before Dying, enabling the casting of the 90-year-old actress despite logistical considerations related to her age and schedule.[23][24]

Filming and Technical Aspects

Principal photography for the first season took place primarily in Los Angeles, California, with soundstages used to replicate the Philadelphia settings depicted in the series. Interiors, including law firm and university scenes, were filmed at facilities such as Sunset Gower Studios, while key exteriors like Annalise Keating's residence utilized real locations at 1130 W 27th Street.[25][26] Campus exteriors representing Middleton University were shot at the University of Southern California to convey an East Coast academic authenticity despite the West Coast production base.[27] The visual style emphasized tension through cinematographic choices, including cooler color temperatures around 5600K for morbid or murder-related sequences to produce a stark, cold aesthetic. Sidelighting and backlighting techniques created pronounced shadows and depth, heightening drama in high-stakes courtroom arguments and nonlinear flashbacks. Editing incorporated rapid cuts and handheld camera movements to mirror the chaotic, high-pressure narrative, particularly during pivotal thriller moments.[28] Post-production aligned closely with the broadcast schedule, enabling the season's premiere on September 25, 2014, and subsequent weekly episodes through February 26, 2015, via standard television drama workflows involving alternating editors for efficiency.[29]

Narrative and Themes

Plot Summary and Key Arcs

The first season of How to Get Away with Murder unfolds through a non-linear structure, interweaving present-day events with flash-forwards to a chaotic scene involving the fatal stabbing of an unseen victim and flashbacks to the preceding semester, which implicate five of Annalise Keating's top law studentsâ€”dubbed the Keating Fiveâ€”in a desperate cover-up.[3] The narrative frames the students' academic and professional immersion under Annalise, a brilliant yet ruthless criminal defense attorney and professor who selects Wes Gibbins, Michaela Pratt, Connor Walsh, Laurel Castillo, and Asher Millstone to assist on her caseload at her firm.[30] This hands-on training exposes them to intense trials, including the defense of a woman accused of murdering her husband, a sitting judge, amid evidence of domestic abuse and infidelity.[31] Parallel to these legal battles runs the central mystery surrounding the murder of Lila Stangard, a university student whose disappearance and pregnancy draw scrutiny to suspects connected to Annalise's personal circle, including her husband, Sam Keating, a university professor.[3] Annalise's arc traces her professional dominance clashing with private turmoil, as suspicions of Sam's involvement in Lila's death erode her composure, fueling marital discord and ethical compromises that ripple into her mentorship of the students.[31] The students' journeys highlight shifting alliances and moral erosion: initial rivalries for Annalise's favor give way to paranoia and betrayal as the group's entanglement in the homicide forces improvised concealment efforts, testing their individual ambitions against collective survival.[31] Escalating tensions culminate in the season finale's disclosures, which link the cutthroat classroom dynamics of Annalise's "How to Get Away with Murder" course to profound, irreversible outcomes, underscoring the lethal stakes of ambition and deception.[31]

Legal Procedures and Accuracy

The series' portrayal of defense strategies, such as discrediting witnesses through aggressive cross-examination and introducing alternative narratives to create reasonable doubt, draws from established criminal defense practices under U.S. law, where attorneys must zealously advocate within ethical bounds to undermine prosecution cases.[32] However, these elements are exaggerated for dramatic effect; real cross-examinations require adherence to evidentiary rules like relevance and foundation, often spanning hours or days rather than the show's rapid, theatrical exchanges that yield immediate confessions or reversals.[33][34] Season 1 frequently compresses legal timelines unrealistically, depicting trials and appeals that unfold over days or weeks, whereas actual U.S. felony cases involve months of pretrial motions, discovery, and delays due to court backlogs and procedural requirements under Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure.[35][36] Evidentiary procedures are routinely ignored, including tolerance of hearsay testimony without objectionâ€”prohibited under Federal Rule of Evidence 802 unless exceptions applyâ€”and lax handling of chain of custody for physical evidence, which in reality demands meticulous documentation to prevent exclusion.[33] The absence of gavels in courtroom scenes aligns incidentally with American practice, where judges rarely use them unlike in British or fictional tropes, but this fidelity is overshadowed by procedural shortcuts prioritizing plot twists over rules like the exclusionary rule for illegally obtained evidence.[33][34] Ethical depictions normalize breaches central to the narrative, such as suborning perjury, tampering with evidence, and conflicts of interest among Annalise Keating's team, which would trigger disbarment under American Bar Association Model Rules of Professional Conduct (e.g., Rule 3.3 on candor and Rule 8.4 on misconduct).[37][38] In season 1's cover-up arcs, students and faculty manipulate scenes and witnesses without immediate repercussions, contrasting real-world consequences like mandatory reporting to prosecutors or bar investigations, though the show incorporates nods to plea bargaining dynamics where concessions are traded for testimony.[36] Legal consultants advised production to ground tactics in reality, yet the emphasis on spectacleâ€”evident in unfeasible student participation in trialsâ€”subordinates procedural accuracy to narrative propulsion, as critiqued by practicing attorneys reviewing early episodes.[34][32]

Social Representation and Critiques

The depiction of Annalise Keating in season 1 positions her as a formidable Black female defense attorney, countering the scarcity of such authoritative figures in prior legal dramas where Black women were often marginalized or absent from power roles.[39] This representation highlights her intellectual prowess and courtroom dominance, drawing from Viola Davis's performance to embody resilience amid professional and personal pressures.[40] Critiques, however, contend that Annalise's characterization perpetuates longstanding stereotypes of Black women, including hypersexuality through extramarital affairs and moral compromise in manipulating clients and students, which aligns with the "strong Black woman" archetype burdened by self-sacrifice and emotional suppression rather than multifaceted agency.[41] Scholars note this trope's reliance on traumaâ€”such as childhood abuse and relational betrayalsâ€”to explain her intensity, potentially reducing empowerment to endurance of dysfunction without addressing systemic barriers.[42] The Keating Five students introduce racial diversity, featuring Black (Wes Gibbins), Asian American (Laurel Castillo, of mixed heritage), Latino (Oliver Hampton, though more prominent later), and white characters, exceeding the ethnic homogeneity of many earlier network legal series.[43] This casting addresses underrepresentation, with quantitative analyses showing greater on-screen variety than predecessors.[44] Nevertheless, their interactions underscore intra-group racial tensions, such as suspicions and alliances fracturing along ethnic lines, which critics argue reinforces fragmentation over unified progress against shared institutional biases in legal training.[43] Such dynamics, while dramatic, may overlook opportunities for solidarity, mirroring but not fully interrogating real-world disparities where Black Americans encounter sentencing lengths 13.4% longer for males compared to whites in federal cases.[45] This portrayal's emphasis on individual trauma for minority characters risks normalizing pathology, yet it indirectly reflects empirical realities like Black incarceration rates approaching five times those of whites in state prisons.[46]

Reception and Impact

Critical Reviews


The first season of How to Get Away with Murder garnered widespread acclaim from critics, achieving an 84% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 144 reviews, with praise centered on Viola Davis's commanding portrayal of Annalise Keating and the series' labyrinthine, twist-heavy plotting that hooked viewers from the pilot.[5] Davis's performance was universally lauded for its intensity and nuance, with outlets like Variety describing her as a "commanding screen presence" that elevates the show's blend of legal thriller and domestic melodrama, infusing innovation into familiar procedural formats through non-linear storytelling and moral ambiguity.[47] Critics appreciated the breakneck pace and stylish execution, which Slant Magazine noted as embracing "the flawed and the frail" in a manner emblematic of Shonda Rhimes's influence, though this also drew detractors for leaning on formulaic Shondaland tropes such as high-stakes interpersonal betrayals and rapid-fire revelations reminiscent of Scandal and Grey's Anatomy.[48] Some reviews highlighted mid-season pacing lulls amid escalating subplots, arguing that the emphasis on shock valueâ€”through improbable cover-ups and cliffhangersâ€”often overshadowed substantive legal or character exploration.[49] Detractors further critiqued the season's implausible legal feats, with legal commentary site Above the Law decrying the show's disregard for basic courtroom realities, such as evidentiary rules and procedural timelines, which prioritized sensationalism over credible depictions of the justice system and risked alienating viewers familiar with actual practice.[50] Metacritic aggregated a 68/100 score from 31 reviews, reflecting this divide between addictive entertainment and perceived narrative contrivances.[51]

Viewership Metrics

The season 1 premiere of How to Get Away with Murder, which aired on September 25, 2014, drew 14 million viewers in its first three days of availability, marking a strong debut for ABC's Thursday 10 p.m. ET slot.[52] Including subsequent playback, the episode reached 20.3 million total viewers in L+3 measurements, the second-highest for a new drama series premiere that fall.[53] Across its 15 episodes, the season averaged 9.76 million total viewers and a 3.0 rating in the adults 18-49 demographic, contributing to ABC's improved Thursday night performance against competitors like CBS's The Big Bang Theory.[54] While live + same-day viewership declined from the premiere's peak in subsequent episodes, these losses were substantially offset by DVR gains, with the series maintaining robust engagement in the 18-49 demo that advertisers prioritize.[55] In comparative terms, the premiere outperformed the debut of ABC's Scandal (7.65 million live + same-day viewers in 2012) and established How to Get Away with Murder as the top-rated new broadcast series of the 2014 fall season, despite network rivalries from NBC and CBS offerings.[56]

Awards and Recognition

Viola Davis received the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series at the 67th ceremony on September 20, 2015, for her portrayal of Annalise Keating in season 1, marking the first win for an African-American woman in that category.[57][58] Davis also earned a nomination for Best Actress in a Television Series â€“ Drama at the 72nd Golden Globe Awards in 2015.[59] She was nominated for Best Actress in a Drama Series at the inaugural Critics' Choice Television Awards in 2015, though she did not win.[60] The series itself garnered recognition beyond Davis's individual honors, including a win for Outstanding Drama Series at the 26th GLAAD Media Awards on March 21, 2015.[61] It also won the NAACP Image Award for Outstanding Drama Series and Davis secured the corresponding Outstanding Actress in a Drama Series award in 2015. Additionally, season 1 was named Television Program of the Year by the American Film Institute in 2014. While Davis dominated the accolades, the series received limited nominations for directing or writing aspects of season 1, with no major wins in those categories. Ensemble cast members saw minimal formal recognition, though the season's critical success elevated emerging actors such as Jack Falahee and Karla Souza, contributing to their subsequent career advancements in television and film.

Cultural Legacy and Controversies

The first season of How to Get Away with Murder, which premiered on September 25, 2014, advanced the portrayal of multifaceted Black female protagonists in primetime television through Viola Davis's role as Annalise Keating, a commanding criminal defense attorney and law professor whose vulnerabilities and strengths challenged simplistic archetypes. This depiction contributed to heightened visibility for women of color in legal roles, influencing subsequent dramas by emphasizing intersectional dynamics in professional settings.[62][63] Critics have accused the series of reinforcing negative stereotypes of Black women by centering Annalise's manipulative tactics, infidelity, and emotional volatility, which echo historical tropes like the " Sapphire" or oversexualized figures, potentially prioritizing exploitation for narrative tension over genuine empowerment. A scholarly analysis contends that the character's ethical lapses and reliance on deception amplify these stereotypes, fostering debates on whether such portrayals liberate or confine Black female imagery in media.[41][64][65] Legal practitioners criticized the season for glamorizing illicit strategies, such as cover-ups and courtroom manipulations, which deviate sharply from professional ethics and procedural norms, risking the erosion of public confidence in legal integrity. Publications from the legal sector highlighted factual distortions in trial mechanics and attorney conduct, arguing that the dramatized "how-to" framework misleads viewers on the rigors and moral boundaries of law practice.[50][34][38]
