Citrobacter freundii

Citrobacter freundii is a species of facultative anaerobic Gram-negative bacteria of the genus Citrobacter (which consists of 17 recognized species)[1], in the family Enterobacteriaceae. These bacteria have a rod shape with a typical length of 1–5 μm. Most C. freundii cells have several flagella used for locomotion, although some non-motile taxa do not. C. freundii is a soil-dwelling microorganism, but can also be found in water, sewage, food, and the intestinal tracts of animals and humans. The genus Citrobacter was discovered in 1932 by Werkman and Gillen. Cultures of C. freundii were isolated and identified in the same year from soil extracts.

Cell morphology and features

Citrobacter freundii is a Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium, typically measuring 1–5 μm in length.[1] Most strains are motile, possessing peritrichous flagella, though some non-motile variants exist. Exopolysaccharides produced by C. freundii exhibit antioxidant activity, including scavenging of hydroxyl radicals.[2] These properties are associated with various physical and chemical characteristics of the exopolysaccharides.[2] Exopolysaccharides from certain strains of C. freundii demonstrate in vitro cytotoxicity against HeLa cells, an immortalized human cell line derived from cervical cancer.[2] C. freundii exhibits aggregative adherence to human colon epithelial cells, contributing to its pathogenicity.[3] Biofilm formation plays a significant role in C. freundii infections, involving adherence and biofilm development, particularly at room temperature.[3] Strong biofilm adherence can serve as a reservoir for antimicrobial resistance genes.[3] Non-cytotoxic multidrug-resistant strains of C. freundii form large biofilms at 25°C through aggregative adherence to colon epithelial cells.[3] Transmission electron microscopy reveals that aggregative strains lack visible surface fimbriae.[3] Fimbriae expression for biofilm formation is temperature-regulated, akin to other Enterobacterales.[3] While overall biofilm formation rates are low, growth at room temperature enhances strong biofilm production in more strains, facilitating persistence in hospital environments.[3] This indicates a distinct strategy for host persistence influenced by temperature.[3]

Phylogeny and genome evolution

C. freundii is more phylogenetically diverse than the clades of E. coli and Salmonella spp., indicating C. freundii to be a polyphyletic species.[4] Due to the phenotypic diversity that C. freundii contains, it makes it very difficult to identify, especially because it is versatile not only in its antigenic and pathogenic behaviors, but also in its cell morphology.[4] C. freundii contains high degrees of nucleotide diversity due to the two different lineages with deep separation within the taxon.[5] Based on phylogenetic information these lineage divisions correlate perfectly with information about geography and host species demonstrating that these factors have importance regarding selectivity.[5] When it comes to each gene, species can differ among various taxa, and divergent branches on some trees can appear to be closely related on other trees. Despite this division in lineage, all strains of C. freundii utilize citrate.[5] Further evidence of evolution can be drawn from two classes of beta-lactamase from C. freundii: CMY-2 AmpC beta-lactamase and TEM-1 beta-lactamase. Neither of these confer resistance to the beta-lactam antibiotic cefepime, and they do not pass on any naturally occurring alleles with cefepime resistance.[6] In vitro evolution shows that there is potential for cefepime resistance evolution from both CMY-2 and TEM-1.[6] The evolved enzymes confer higher levels of cefepime resistance in Gram-negative bacteria, with evolved TEM-1 alleles producing higher resistance levels than those from evolved CMY-2.[6]

Metabolic details

C. freundii has the ability to grow on glycerol, and use it as its sole source of carbon and energy.[7] The organism contains a bacterial microcompartment capable of processing propanediol.[8] C. freundii produces a positive methyl red (MR) and negative Voges-Proskauer (VP) test along with a positive catalase and negative oxidase test. The type strain of C. freundii typically does not hydrolyze starch, lipids, or gelatin.[9] C. freundii has also been investigated for biodegradation of tannic acid used in tanneries. C. freundii includes a fenvalerate-degrading bacterial strain, CD-9. Fenvalerate degradation through CD-9 also includes degradation of intermediate products. These results highlight motility deficiencies in the absence of tatC function. However, since swimming is rarely observed in the tatC mutant, it is possible that flagellar function is retained to some extent.[12]

Relevance to broader system

C. freundii is also commonly found to be a member of the soil microbiome. This microbe plays an important role in the nitrogen cycle in the environment. C. freundii can perform nitrogen fixation, as demonstrated by its association with nitrogen-fixing enterobacteria in the living tissues of sassafras trees (Atherosperma moschatum).[13] It is also capable of reducing nitrate to nitrite, contributing to denitrification processes.[14] C. freundii can also be found in the intestinal tracts of humans and other animals from diverse environmental sources. C. freundii is a common component of the gut microbiome of healthy humans.[15] While most strains are beneficial, there are significant phenotypic variations among strains, even those that share >99% of their genome.[16] C. freundii interactions with healthy people are normally regarded as non-pathogenic; nevertheless, once in the bloodstream, C. freundii can cause a life-threatening infection that can progress into sepsis. As a result, C. freundii belongs to a limited group of Gram-negative bacterial species that are frequently encountered in healthcare settings and can cause a variety of illnesses in people with a variety of underlying disorders.[12] Toxigenic C. freundii appears to be rare, and its main influence on human health is as an opportunistic pathogen.[12] As such, C. freundii causes a wide range of illnesses, including infections of the urinary system, respiratory tract, wounds, circulation, and other sites in immunocompromised patients.[12][17] According to North American healthcare facilities, the Citrobacter genus is responsible for 3–6% of Enterobacteriaceae infections and is one of the most frequent strains associated with human infection.[12] Recent studies as of 2025 have highlighted the emergence of carbapenemase-producing C. freundii strains, contributing to nosocomial outbreaks and infections in vulnerable groups such as children, the elderly, and through food sources like poultry meat. These multidrug-resistant variants pose increasing challenges in clinical settings.[18][19] Virulence genes that are identical or homologous to those identified in E. coli pathotypes as well as in Salmonella, have also been identified in C. freundii.[4] Phenotypic changes that are reversible from the parental strain of atypical C. freundii have been identified as a result of a fluctuating environment.[4] A possible explanation for this is the hypothesis that the offspring from a bacterial strain will be produced with varying degrees of phenotypic changes in order to increase the rates of survival in challenging conditions.[4] It has been found that in natural bacterial populations, genomic changes can and do occur, but many of these changes become deleterious, causing survival to occur only in small portions of the population under the conditions of natural selection pressure.[4]
