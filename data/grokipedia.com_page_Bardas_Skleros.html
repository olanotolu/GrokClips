Bardas Skleros

Bardas Skleros (Greek: Βάρδας Σκληρός; c. 915 – c. 991) was a prominent Byzantine general and aristocrat from the influential Skleros family, which held extensive estates on the eastern frontiers of Asia Minor.[1] As brother-in-law to Emperor John I Tzimiskes and former domestikos ton scholon tes Anatoles (commander of the Eastern armies), he wielded considerable military influence among the Anatolian themes.[1] Skleros is chiefly noted for leading two major rebellions against the young Emperor Basil II, proclaiming himself emperor in both instances amid power struggles within the Byzantine military elite.[1][2] In the first revolt, sparked by the death of Tzimiskes in 976 and the dominance of the parakoimomenos Basil Lekapenos over the underage Basil II, Skleros declared himself emperor at Harput in spring 976 with Armenian backing.[1] His forces achieved victories at Lycandus, Rageai, Nicaea, and Pangaleia against imperial troops led by Bardas Phokas the Younger, capturing key cities and demonstrating tactical prowess.[1] However, Phokas, reinforced by 12,000 Georgian cavalry, decisively defeated Skleros at Charsianon on March 24, 979, forcing him to flee to the court of the Buyid emir in Baghdad.[1] Returning from exile in 987 amid Basil II's setbacks against the Bulgarians, Skleros launched a second bid for the throne, invading via Melitene and securing an initial alliance with Phokas, who arrested him shortly after.[2] Following Phokas's death in battle against Basil II at Abydos in April 989, Skleros was released and again proclaimed emperor but, hampered by age, waning Arab support, and imperial momentum, submitted in October 989, receiving the honorary title of kouropalates.[2] These uprisings highlighted tensions between the central administration and provincial magnates but ultimately fortified Basil II's autocracy, curtailing aristocratic revolts thereafter.[2]

Origins and Early Career

Family Background and Ancestry

Bardas Skleros belonged to the Skleros family, one of the preeminent clans of the Byzantine military aristocracy in the 10th century, with roots in northeastern Asia Minor, particularly the themes of Sebasteia and possibly Lesser Armenia. This region, bordering Armenian territories, fostered families like the Skleroi who amassed substantial landholdings and leveraged them for military service and political power, controlling key troops in the Anatolian plateau. The family's estates extended across the eastern frontiers, providing resources that enabled multiple generations to hold high commands and challenge imperial authority.[3][4] Historians frequently describe the Skleroi as of Armenian descent, citing their geographic origins, intermarriages with Armenian nobles such as the Mamikonians, and the broader influx of Armenian military elites into Byzantine service during the 9th–10th centuries; Leo Skleros, an early attested member active as a general around 900, exemplifies this integration. However, direct ethnic evidence remains circumstantial, as Byzantine naming practices often obscured precise ancestries, and some analyses emphasize the family's Hellenized identity within the empire's administrative elite.[5][6][7] Bardas was the son of Niketas Skleros, a patrician tied to these eastern domains, and Gregoria, whose lineage connected the family to imperial circles—possibly through descent from Bardas, brother of Emperor Basil I, or Armenian aristocratic houses like the Mamikonians, enhancing the Skleroi's claims to legitimacy. He had a brother, Constantine Skleros, who similarly attained senior military ranks and forged alliances via marriage to figures like Pulcheria Argyra, reflecting the clan's strategy of consolidating influence through kinship networks among Anatolia's landowning magnates. These ties positioned the Skleroi as rivals to families like the Phokades, fueling recurrent power struggles.[8][9]

Rise in the Military under Nikephoros II Phokas

Bardas Skleros, originating from a distinguished family of the Anatolian military aristocracy, achieved significant advancement during the reign of Emperor Nikephoros II Phokas (963–969). He was appointed domestikos tōn scholōn of the East, the highest command over the empire's eastern field armies, a role that positioned him as a key figure in the prosecution of Byzantine offensives against Arab forces.[1] This elevation underscored Phokas' strategy of empowering proven thematic generals to sustain the momentum of reconquests initiated in the 950s and 960s, including operations in Cilicia and northern Syria, where Byzantine forces under imperial oversight secured strategic gains against Abbasid emirs and local dynasts. As domestikos, Skleros managed the integration of elite tagmata units with regional thematic troops, ensuring disciplined formations and supply lines for extended campaigns that exploited Muslim disunity. His tenure coincided with Phokas' issuance of military landholding reforms in 967, aimed at bolstering soldier-farmer obligations to fund and man these eastern thrusts, thereby enhancing the army's effectiveness in repelling raids and launching counteroffensives.[10] Family connections, such as his brother Constantine's marriage to Sophia Phokaina—a niece of Phokas—likely facilitated Skleros' trust and responsibilities within the emperor's inner military circle. These factors collectively marked his transition from regional command to imperial-level authority, setting the stage for further prominence amid the empire's assertive eastern policy.

Service and Achievements under John I Tzimiskes

Following John I Tzimiskes' usurpation and coronation on December 25, 969, Bardas Skleros, who had married into the imperial family as the brother of Tzimiskes' deceased first wife, was dispatched to suppress an early rebellion led by Bardas Phokas the Younger and Leo Phokas, kin of the slain Nikephoros II Phokas.[11] Skleros' rapid intervention quelled the uprising in Asia Minor, demonstrating his loyalty and securing Tzimiskes' nascent regime against aristocratic opposition rooted in the Phokas clan's resentment of the coup.[5] In spring 970, amid Svyatoslav I of Kiev's invasion of Thrace allied with Bulgarian and Pecheneg forces, Skleros commanded a Byzantine field army of approximately 15,000–20,000, including thematic troops and tagmata, against the Rus' host numbering up to 60,000.[12] At the Battle of Arcadiopolis on August 23, 970, Skleros employed tactical maneuvers to divide the enemy coalition, first targeting the Pecheneg vanguard; he personally slew their chieftain Ioannas in single combat, triggering a rout that inflicted 10,000–20,000 casualties on the invaders while Byzantine losses remained under 1,000.[12] [13] This victory halted the Rus' advance toward Constantinople, buying time for Tzimiskes to consolidate power and later besiege Svyatoslav at Dorostolon in 971. Skleros retained his post as domestikos tōn scholōn tōn Anatolikōn, overseeing eastern defenses from 970 to 976, where he maintained stability against Arab incursions from the Hamdanids and maintained supply lines for Tzimiskes' own offensives into Syria and Palestine (972–975).[1] His administrative acumen ensured no major breaches occurred in Anatolia during the emperor's absences, contributing to the recapture of cities like Emesa and Baalbek, though Skleros' direct field role in these was secondary to Tzimiskes' personal command.[5] As a trusted advisor at court, Skleros influenced military policy but avoided overreach, preserving his position until Tzimiskes' death on January 10, 976.[9]

First Rebellion (976–979)

Triggers and Outbreak

The death of Emperor John I Tzimiskes on 11 January 976 precipitated a succession crisis, as the co-emperors Basil II, aged eighteen, and his brother Constantine VIII lacked experience in governance and military command.[14] The regency was effectively controlled by the parakoimomenos Basil Lekapenos, a eunuch grand-uncle who wielded significant influence and sought to curb the power of the Anatolian military aristocracy.[15] Bardas Skleros, as domestikos tōn scholōn tēs Anatolēs and a prominent general with ties to Tzimiskes through marriage and service, viewed Lekapenos's dominance as a threat to his position and the traditional role of thematic armies in imperial politics.[1] Lekapenos's efforts to sideline Skleros—appointing him doux of Mesopotamia to occupy him with Arab border raids while retaining oversight—fueled resentment among eastern commanders who favored a soldier-emperor over court eunuchs.[1] Skleros, leveraging his prestige from victories under Nikephoros II Phokas and Tzimiskes, exploited this vacuum to rally support, portraying his bid as a restoration of military authority against perceived civilian overreach.[8] In spring or early summer 976, Skleros proclaimed himself emperor near Hierapolis in the East, securing allegiance from key themes like Charsianon and Cappadocia, as well as Armenian and Georgian magnates.[8] His forces, numbering several thousand tagmata and thematic troops, rapidly advanced westward, capturing cities such as Cotyaeum and threatening the capital by besieging key passes in Asia Minor.[1] This outbreak marked the first major challenge to Basil II's rule, drawing on widespread discontent with the regency's policies.[16]

Military Campaigns and Alliances

Following his proclamation as emperor in spring 976 at Harput, Bardas Skleros rapidly consolidated control over eastern Anatolia by securing the treasury at Melitene, which yielded 40,000 gold coins to fund his forces.[1] He forged key alliances with Armenian lords, including Gregorios of Taron, Bagarat, Moqk Zaphranik, and Romanos Taronites, leveraging their regional influence and military support.[1] Additionally, Skleros enlisted aid from Muslim emirs such as Abu Taglib of Mosul and Abu Dulaf of Amida, expanding his coalition across ethnic lines to bolster his campaign against the central government under Basil II.[1] Skleros initiated his westward advance in fall 976, suffering an initial setback at Koukou Lithos against imperial troops led by Eustathios Maleinos, but quickly recovered with a victory at Lapara (or Lycandus) under Sachakios Brachamios.[1] By summer or fall 977, his forces triumphed at Rageai, defeating an imperial army and eliminating commanders Petros and Ioannes while capturing Leo, which propelled further gains.[1] In early 978, Skleros besieged and captured Nicaea, demonstrating his logistical prowess, though his rebel fleet encountered defeat at Phokaia later that year.[1] A pivotal engagement occurred on June 19, 978, when Skleros decisively defeated Bardas Phokas at Pangaleia, temporarily neutralizing a major rival and advancing his threat toward Constantinople.[1] However, the tide turned in spring 979 at Charsianon on March 24, where Phokas, reinforced by 12,000 Georgian horsemen, overwhelmed Skleros' army, forcing the rebel leader to flee to Baghdad and marking the collapse of his eastern strongholds.[1] Skleros' supporters maintained resistance in Thrakesion fortresses until late 979, but the core of the rebellion disintegrated following these defeats.[1]

Defeat, Exile, and Capture

In early 979, Bardas Phokas, commanding imperial forces loyal to Basil II, reinforced his army with approximately 12,000 Georgian cavalry under the command of Prince David III of Tao, enabling a counteroffensive against Skleros' depleted forces in the theme of Charsianon.[1] On March 24, 979, Phokas launched a surprise attack, inflicting a decisive defeat on Skleros' army, which disintegrated amid heavy losses and desertions.[1] [8] Skleros himself escaped the battlefield with a small retinue, initially fleeing westward to Melitene before crossing the Euphrates River into Upper Mesopotamia, seeking refuge among Hamdanid emirs.[1] From there, he proceeded to Martyropolis and ultimately to the Abbasid court in Baghdad, where Caliph al-Ta'i detained him as a political asset against Byzantine interests, effectively confining him in exile for several years.[1] [17] This detention prevented Skleros from mounting further immediate resistance, though remnants of his supporters held out in Anatolian fortresses until late 979, when they were subdued by imperial forces.[1] The rebellion's collapse secured Phokas' temporary dominance in Anatolia, though it highlighted the fragility of Basil II's early rule amid aristocratic factionalism.[8]

Imprisonment and Intermediary Period

Conditions of Confinement

Following his defeat at the Battle of Pankaleia on 24 May 979, Bardas Skleros fled eastward with approximately 300 supporters, initially seeking refuge in Martyropolis before proceeding to Baghdad under the protection of Buyid emir Adud al-Dawla (r. 949–983).[1][18] There, Skleros, his brother, nephew, and family were placed under confinement at the Buyid court, treated as political pawns to extract concessions from Byzantine Emperor Basil II through diplomatic negotiations and threats of renewed support for rebellion.[19][20] This confinement, lasting from circa 980 until late 986, was not characterized by severe physical hardship akin to Byzantine penal incarceration but rather restricted liberty within the courtly environment of Baghdad, where Skleros maintained some status as a high-profile exile while denied autonomy to depart or act independently.[2][8] Adud al-Dawla and his successors, including Samsam al-Dawla (r. 983–998), leveraged Skleros' presence to pressure Constantinople, occasionally parading him in audiences with Byzantine envoys or corresponding with Basil II on terms involving his potential repatriation or backing for imperial intrigue.[20][21] Primary accounts from Buyid chroniclers, such as those preserved in al-Rudrawari's histories, indicate courteous but surveillant treatment, with Skleros residing amid the Abbasid caliphal apparatus under Buyid oversight, his movements curtailed to prevent unauthorized alliances or escapes.[22] The arrangement reflected pragmatic Buyid foreign policy, balancing hospitality to a defeated Byzantine aristocrat against opportunities for leverage in frontier disputes, without evidence of routine mistreatment or deprivation; Skleros' health permitted his later military resurgence upon release, facilitated by shifting Buyid priorities amid internal succession struggles.[17][23] This period of externally imposed restraint underscored the vulnerabilities of Byzantine exiles in Islamic courts, where confinement served diplomatic utility over punitive severity.[8]

Release and Role against Emerging Threats

Bardas Skleros, having sought refuge at the Abbasid court in Baghdad after his defeat in 979, endured approximately seven years of confinement under Caliphs Adud al-Dawla and Samsam al-Dawla, treated with nominal honor but effectively detained alongside 300 followers to prevent independent action.[2] In the winter of 986–987, Abbasid authorities released him, likely amid regional power shifts that diminished the strategic value of holding him as leverage against Byzantium.[8] Byzantine diplomatic efforts had previously sought his extradition through multiple embassies, though without success until this development.[2] Upon re-entering imperial territory near Melitene in eastern Anatolia, Skleros did not reintegrate into loyal service but instead exploited the empire's distractions to proclaim himself emperor once more, rallying disaffected troops and nobles in a swift renewal of rebellion.[8] This resurgence occurred against a backdrop of acute external pressures, notably the Bulgarian victory over Basil II's forces at the Battle of Trajan's Gate (also known as the Battle of the Gates) in 986, where Samuel's army annihilated much of the imperial vanguard, straining resources and exposing vulnerabilities in the Balkans.[24] Skleros' forces initially secured control over key Anatolian districts, compelling Emperor Basil II to appoint Bardas Phokas—recently freed from confinement—to lead the counteroffensive, thereby diverting eastern armies from potential aid against Bulgarian incursions.[8] Skleros' renewed challenge thus functioned as a profound internal threat, undermining the cohesion of the thematic armies essential for defending against both Balkan expansions and persistent eastern raids by Arab and Turkish groups.[24] His strategic positioning in Melitene, a frontier bulwark, risked fracturing defenses at a moment when Basil II's regime grappled with regency influences and fiscal strains from prolonged conflicts, amplifying the empire's exposure to multifaceted aggressors.[8] Primary accounts, such as those preserved in later Byzantine chronicles, emphasize Skleros' determination in leveraging these instabilities, though his ultimate containment required Basil's reinforcement with Varangian mercenaries.[2]

Second Rebellion and Submission (987–989)

Context and Initial Moves

Following his defeat in the first rebellion and flight to Baghdad in 979, Bardas Skleros remained confined under the Buyid emirs, including Caliph Adud ad-Daula and his successors, until late 986.[2] Emperor Basil II, after assuming personal control in late 985 by banishing the parakoimomenos Basil Lekapenos, faced a major setback with his army's defeat by Bulgarian forces under Samuel at the Battle of Triaditsa (modern Sofia) on August 17, 986, which undermined imperial prestige and military cohesion amid ongoing Bulgarian incursions.[2] Exploiting this instability, Skleros secured an agreement with Buyid emir Sharaf al-Dawla in December 986 or January 987, pledging Byzantine border fortresses and the release of Muslim prisoners in return for armed escort and freedom to reenter imperial territory.[2] In February 987, Skleros invaded Byzantine Anatolia near Melitene with his brother Constantine Skleros, son Romanos, 300 loyal companions, and supporting Arab tribal contingents, swiftly seizing the city and detaining its governor, patrikios Kulaib.[2] By spring 987, he proclaimed himself emperor, rallying local Armenian magnates and the Kurdish emir of Amid for reinforcements to bolster his forces in the eastern themes.[2] Basil II countered by reinstating Bardas Phokas—previously demoted to doux of Antioch in 985—as domestikos ton scholon tes Anatoles in April or May 987, tasking him with suppressing the uprising.[2] In summer 987, Phokas, drawing on aristocratic networks in Asia Minor's central themes, opened negotiations with Skleros in Charsianon, floating a partition of imperial authority; Skleros responded by sending general Nikephoros Phokas for discussions while dispatching Romanos to simulate defection to imperial lines, forging a provisional alliance that unified rebel efforts against Constantinople.[2][25]

Confrontation with Bardas Phokas

In 987, Bardas Skleros returned from exile among the Abbasid Caliphate, where he had sought refuge after his first rebellion's failure, and rapidly assembled an army from the eastern Anatolian themes, renewing his challenge to Emperor Basil II's authority. Skleros's forces gained traction amid ongoing Bulgarian threats that diverted imperial resources, positioning him as a potential restorer of military aristocratic dominance. Bardas Phokas, a seasoned general from a rival powerful family and commander of imperial troops in the region, advanced to intercept Skleros's advance, setting the stage for a direct power struggle between the two magnates.[26][8] Instead of a pitched battle, Phokas employed stratagem over force, summoning Skleros under pretext of alliance negotiations to discuss partitioning imperial authority. On September 14, 987, during these parleys near the Opsikion theme, Phokas seized the opportunity to arrest Skleros, who had proclaimed himself emperor, thereby decapitating the rebellion without significant combat. This act of betrayal stemmed from Phokas's ambition to monopolize the revolt, as he viewed Skleros as a competing claimant rather than a partner; primary accounts, such as those preserved in later synopses of tenth-century historians like Skylitzes, attribute the maneuver to Phokas's calculation that Skleros's prestige and eastern connections posed an ongoing threat. Skleros's army, lacking its leader, largely transferred loyalty to Phokas, who then redirected their efforts against Constantinople.[26][25] Phokas's subsequent proclamation as emperor in late 987 transformed the confrontation into a precursor for his own extended civil war, with Skleros held captive in Phokian custody to prevent resurgence. The imprisonment neutralized Skleros's immediate capabilities, averting a prolonged intra-rebel conflict that could have fragmented Anatolian support; however, it underscored the fragile alliances among the empire's provincial elites, where familial ties (Phokas and Skleros were connected through marriage) yielded to personal ambition. Skleros endured confinement until Phokas's fatal collapse during the Battle of Abydos in April 989, after which Phokas's widow facilitated his release.[8][26]

Defeat and Pardon

Following the decisive imperial victory at the Battle of Abydos on April 13, 989, where Bardas Phokas suffered a fatal stroke during the engagement against Basil II's forces bolstered by 6,000 Varangian mercenaries, the rebel leadership fragmented.[25] Phokas' army disintegrated amid pursuit by the Varangians, leaving Skleros—previously confined by Phokas in the Tyropoion fortress after their failed alliance—to assume command of the remnants.[2] Skleros' position eroded rapidly due to widespread desertions, his advanced age (approaching 80), and diminished external backing from Arab allies, as political shifts in the Baghdad caliphate curtailed further support.[2] Basil II's relentless campaigns in Asia Minor, combined with the rebels' logistical exhaustion, compelled Skleros to negotiate submission near Chrysopolis in October 989, effectively ending the uprising without further major clashes.[2] In recognition of Skleros' military stature and to forestall renewed unrest among aristocratic factions, Basil II granted him a full pardon, the prestigious title of kouropalates, and estates in Mesopotamia, allowing him to retire without execution or mutilation—a pragmatic concession reflecting the emperor's strategy of co-opting rather than annihilating elite rivals.[2] This outcome stabilized the eastern themes, enabling Basil to redirect resources toward external threats like the Bulgarians.[25]

Later Life, Death, and Legacy

Final Years and Death

Following his submission to Basil II in the aftermath of Bardas Phokas's defeat and death in 989, Bardas Skleros was granted a pardon and the honorary title of curopalates, permitting retirement from public life. He resided in relative seclusion, likely on estates in Asia Minor or Thrace, amid Basil's consolidation of power against aristocratic rivals.[8] In spring 991, as Basil II advanced toward Bulgarian territories for renewed campaigning, he visited Skleros during a halt in Thrace. The meeting, recounted in Michael Psellos's Chronographia, involved Skleros offering pragmatic counsel on imperial stability, including warnings against undue influence from court women and the necessity of emperors personally vetting provincial governors to curb factionalism.[27] Skleros died on 2 April 991, shortly after this encounter. While natural causes aligned with his advanced age are probable, the Arab chronicler al-Rudhrawari records unverified rumors of poisoning, possibly reflecting persistent suspicions of imperial intrigue.[8]

Descendants and Family Continuity

Bardas Skleros's lineage endured through his son Romanos Skleros, who defected from his father's second rebellion to Emperor Basil II in 987, earning imperial favor and subsequent commands, including leading a Byzantine army against Fatimid forces near Antioch circa 992.[8] [2] Romanos's military service exemplified the Skleroi clan's continued integration into the empire's Anatolian forces despite Bardas's defeats, preserving familial estates and influence in eastern themes.[8] The Skleroi maintained aristocratic prominence into the early 11th century via such loyalist roles and intermarriages with other elite houses, though primary records on further direct descendants remain sparse. By mid-century, the family's visibility waned amid Basil II's centralization and the rise of new bureaucratic elites, transitioning from frontier magnates to lesser functionaries before fading from historical prominence.[8]

Historical Assessment and Impact

Bardas Skleros' military prowess and political ambition positioned him as a quintessential figure among the Byzantine aristocracy of the late 10th century, challenging the fragile rule of the young Basil II through two major revolts spanning 976–979 and 987–989. Historians evaluate him as a strategically adept commander who effectively leveraged thematic armies and provincial loyalties to seize control of key Anatolian territories, including Antioch and much of the eastern themes, demonstrating the fragility of imperial authority amid succession crises following the deaths of Nikephoros II Phokas and John I Tzimiskes.[8] His ability to rally support from diverse ethnic groups, including Armenians, underscored the decentralized power structures that enabled such uprisings, though his repeated failures highlighted the limitations of aristocratic networks against a determined emperor willing to employ foreign auxiliaries.[28] The revolts exacted a heavy toll on Byzantine resources, with pitched battles and sieges—such as those at Abydos in 978 and the final confrontation near Abydos in 989—resulting in significant casualties among imperial forces and disrupting defenses against external threats. This internal strife facilitated Bulgarian incursions under Tsar Samuel, who exploited the empire's preoccupation to consolidate control over the Balkans by the late 980s, delaying Basil II's counteroffensives until after Skleros' submission.[8] Basil II's reliance on Rus' Varangian troops, numbering around 6,000 in 989, to crush the rebellion not only ended the immediate threat but also signaled a shift toward mercenary forces over native aristocratic levies, reducing the military independence of families like the Skleroi.[29] Ultimately, Skleros' defeats reinforced Basil II's autocratic reforms, curbing the influence of magnate clans and centralizing fiscal and military administration, which proved instrumental in the emperor's later triumphs, including the subjugation of Bulgaria by 1018. While Skleros received a pardon and briefly advised the emperor upon his return from exile in 989, his career exemplified the perils of unchecked aristocratic ambition, contributing to a historiographical narrative of Basil II's reign as a pivot from feudal fragmentation to imperial resurgence.[8] The Skleroi family's subsequent marginalization, despite matrimonial ties to the imperial house, reflected this broader realignment, prioritizing bureaucratic loyalty over hereditary entitlement.[28]
