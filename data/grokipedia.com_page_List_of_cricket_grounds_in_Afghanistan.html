List of cricket grounds in Afghanistan


The list of cricket grounds in Afghanistan catalogs the various stadiums and fields developed within the country to support cricket, a sport that has risen prominently since the Afghanistan Cricket Board's establishment in 2001 and the national team's full ICC membership in 2017. These venues, numbering over a dozen across provinces, range from the centrally located Kabul National Cricket Stadium—Afghanistan's primary international-standard facility with a capacity exceeding 25,000—to regional grounds like Kandahar National Cricket Ground and Kunduz Cricket Ground, aimed at grassroots development and domestic competitions.[1][2] Despite infrastructure investments, including renovations funded by international partners, security challenges have restricted hosting of senior international matches primarily to Kabul and occasionally abroad, with most Tests and ODIs played in neutral venues like the UAE due to instability.[3] Key establishments include the Ghazi Amanullah Khan International Cricket Stadium in Jalalabad, which hosted early domestic and limited internationals, underscoring cricket's role in national unity amid ongoing conflicts.[4]

Historical Development of Cricket Infrastructure

Origins and Pre-2001 Facilities

Cricket was initially introduced to Afghanistan through British military presence during the Anglo-Afghan Wars of the 19th century, with records indicating games played by troops in Kabul as early as 1839.[5] The sport remained marginal until the Soviet invasion in 1979 and ensuing civil war displaced millions of Afghans to refugee camps in Pakistan during the 1980s, where exposure to local cricket culture took hold among Pashtun youth in camps near Peshawar and Quetta.[6][7] These refugees adapted the game using improvised pitches on concrete strips or matting laid over dirt, fostering skills that later influenced domestic play upon partial returns or within Afghanistan's eastern border regions.[8] Prior to 2001, cricket facilities in Afghanistan were confined to informal, makeshift setups in urban centers such as Kabul and Jalalabad, consisting of open fields or parks with temporary matting pitches rolled out on unprepared soil, devoid of turf, drainage systems, or defined boundaries.[9] These venues accommodated sporadic local matches among enthusiasts, often limited to club-level or inter-provincial games without recorded spectator capacities exceeding a few hundred or any engineered infrastructure for international standards.[10] Ongoing conflict under mujahideen factions and Taliban rule from 1996 restricted organized development, prioritizing survival over sports amenities, with play concentrated in relatively stable Pashtun areas bordering Pakistan.[6] The formation of the Afghanistan Cricket Federation in 1995, initially organized among refugees in Peshawar, marked the first structured effort to govern the sport domestically, enabling basic tournaments on these rudimentary fields.[11] This groundwork culminated in provisional International Cricket Council affiliate membership in June 2001, recognizing nascent administrative capacity but highlighting the absence of purpose-built grounds amid wartime devastation.[12] No dedicated stadiums existed, as civil strife had eroded prior recreational infrastructure, leaving cricket reliant on ad hoc arrangements until post-intervention reconstruction.[13]

Expansion Through International Aid (2001-2020)

Following the U.S.-led intervention in 2001, international donors supported the establishment of formal cricket infrastructure in Afghanistan to foster sports development amid post-conflict reconstruction. India emerged as a primary contributor, funding the construction of key facilities to address the absence of proper grounds. This aid enabled the creation of international-standard pitches and seating arrangements capable of accommodating over 6,000 spectators, marking a shift from rudimentary fields to structured venues.[14] The Kabul International Cricket Stadium, developed with Indian assistance, opened in 2011 and became Afghanistan's premier venue for hosting domestic and limited international matches. Further expansions included India's $1 million grant in 2014 for the Kandahar International Cricket Stadium, where construction began in 2016 and the facility was inaugurated in 2018 with a capacity of around 9,000. Similarly, Germany provided €700,000 in 2014 to build the Khost Cricket Stadium, completed by December 2016, supporting regional cricket activities. These projects facilitated the launch of the Shpageeza Cricket League in 2015, which utilized venues in Kabul, Kandahar, and Khost to promote competitive T20 cricket.[15][16][17][18] By 2020, this foreign-funded expansion had increased the number of usable cricket grounds from a handful of basic setups to multiple provincial facilities equipped for professional play, underpinning Afghanistan's achievement of full One Day International (ODI) status from the International Cricket Council in June 2017. The infrastructure growth aligned with broader stability during the 2001-2020 period, allowing the Afghanistan Cricket Board to host domestic tournaments and prepare teams for global competition, though security concerns occasionally limited international fixtures.[14]

Infrastructure Under Taliban Governance (2021-Present)

Following the Taliban's resumption of control in August 2021, Afghanistan's cricket infrastructure has seen sustained domestic utilization and targeted development initiatives by the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB). Key venues such as Kabul National Cricket Stadium continued to host national events, including the entire 2025 Etisalat Shpageeza Cricket League from July 19 to August 31, demonstrating operational continuity amid international restrictions on women's participation.[19][20] In 2025, the ACB prioritized revamps at regional grounds, including the Rahmat Wali Masroor Cricket Ground in Khost, where spectator seating expansion commenced in June at a cost of 34 million Afghanis to enhance capacity and facilities. Similar reconstruction efforts were planned for grounds in Kandahar, Nangarhar (Najibullah Taraki), Balkh, and Kunduz, as part of a broader strategy to upgrade four major regional venues to international standards using allocated internal budgets. The Kandahar International Cricket Stadium underwent development works aimed at improving turf and infrastructure, building on its existing 14,000 capacity.[21][22][23] These upgrades have been supported primarily through ACB's domestic funding allocations and procurement for maintenance equipment, enabling resilience in training and match preparation despite reliance on neutral venues like the UAE for some international fixtures. This infrastructure stability contributed to Afghanistan's competitive performances, including a 2-1 ODI series victory over Zimbabwe in late 2024 and subsequent Test success in the same tour. Taliban officials have expressed commitments to further invest in facilities to host international matches, underscoring a focus on cricket as a national priority.[24][25]

Major Cricket Grounds

Kabul National Cricket Stadium

The Kabul National Cricket Stadium, located in Kabul, Afghanistan, serves as the primary venue for cricket in the country. Opened on December 19, 2011, the stadium was constructed with funding from USAID and implemented by CARE International in collaboration with the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB).[13] It has a seating capacity of approximately 8,000 spectators.[26] The stadium houses the ACB headquarters and is the central hub for domestic cricket activities. It hosted the entire 10th edition of the Etisalat Shpageeza Cricket League from July 19 to August 1, 2025, featuring top Afghan players across multiple teams.[20] Thousands of fans attended matches during this period, demonstrating sustained public interest in cricket despite governance changes since 2021.[20] Integrated with the ACB's High Performance Center, the stadium supports player development through specialized training programs and facilities.[27] The venue's pitch offers a balanced surface, though specific conditions vary, contributing to Afghanistan's domestic cricket ecosystem.[28]

Kandahar International Cricket Stadium

The Kandahar International Cricket Stadium, located in the Ayno Mena suburb of Kandahar City, was constructed on 44 jeribs of land with financial support from the Government of India.[1][15] In August 2014, India approved a grant of $1 million under its Small Development Project Scheme to fund the project, following an agreement between the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB) and a private firm.[15][16] Construction work was formally inaugurated on February 29, 2016, marking a key phase in expanding cricket infrastructure in southern Afghanistan during the 2010s.[16] Designed with a targeted seating capacity of 9,000 spectators, the stadium has facilitated domestic matches and national team training sessions since entering partial operational use in the early 2020s.[1] It serves as a primary venue for nurturing talent from Kandahar and surrounding southern provinces, contributing to the ACB's efforts to broaden player recruitment beyond urban centers like Kabul.[1] The facility's development aligns with broader ACB initiatives to establish regional hubs capable of hosting competitive cricket amid Afghanistan's challenging terrain. In 2025, the ACB initiated major upgrades at the stadium, known locally in development contexts as the Rahmat Wali Masroor Cricket Ground, as part of a program targeting four key regional venues including those in Nangarhar, Khost, and Kunduz. These enhancements, allocated significant budget under ACB oversight, focus on elevating facilities to international standards through ongoing construction visible as of June 2025.[23] Such improvements underscore the stadium's evolving role in sustaining domestic cricket growth despite incomplete full-scale completion.[1]

Khost Cricket Stadium

Khost Cricket Stadium, officially known as Rahmat Wali Masroor Cricket Ground, is situated in Khost Province, eastern Afghanistan, and serves as a primary venue for domestic cricket competitions in the region. Owned and operated by the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB), the ground was established in 2016 with an initial capacity of around 6,000 spectators, later expanded to approximately 8,000.[29] It supports training and matches for provincial teams, including the Mis Ainak Knights, fostering grassroots development amid limited infrastructure in rural areas.[30] In June 2025, the ACB initiated major renovations at the stadium, including grandstand expansion and facility upgrades funded by the board to align with international standards.[31] These works, part of a targeted program for four regional grounds in Khost, Kandahar, Nangarhar, and Kunduz, involved backfilling, drainage construction, and seating increases projected to reach 15,000, at a cost of 34 million Afghan afghanis.[32] The expansions were inaugurated on June 24, 2025, with attendance from ACB officials and local authorities, aiming to accommodate higher-volume domestic events and youth programs.[33] The venue has hosted significant domestic fixtures, including the X-Bull Regional List A Tournament 2025 from August 20 to September 6, featuring five regional teams in a round-robin format.[34] Sponsored elements by Etisalat, the tournament included key matches such as Band-e-Amir vs. Speenghar on August 22 and the final between Mis-e-Ainak and Band-e-Amir on September 6, drawing local crowds and providing competitive exposure.[35] [36] These events have bolstered the eastern talent pipeline, with provincial players from Khost contributing to regional squads that feed into national selections, as seen in the participation of ACB-contracted athletes like Hashmatullah Shahidi and Rahmat Shah.[37]

Other Significant Venues

Helmand Cricket Stadium in Lashkargah functions as a primary venue for provincial cricket in southern Afghanistan, hosting local matches and training sessions since its development in the 2010s.[38] Laghman Cricket Stadium in Mihtarlam, with construction nearing completion as of 2024 including a pavilion and seating for 2,000 spectators, supports regional domestic play amid ongoing infrastructure efforts.[39] Sherzai Cricket Stadium in Jalalabad serves domestic competitions in the east, contributing to Nangarhar province's cricket activities alongside major facilities.[38] These grounds, typically featuring spin-friendly pitches due to local soil conditions, have enabled provincial teams to participate in Afghanistan Cricket Board tournaments such as the Provincial Challenge Cup through 2025.[40] Their role remains focused on grassroots and inter-provincial development rather than international fixtures.[41]

Regional and Provincial Grounds

Northern and Northeastern Provinces

Kunduz Cricket Ground, situated in Kunduz Province, represents the primary cricket venue in northern Afghanistan, with a seating capacity exceeding 7,000. Owned and operated by the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB), it supports regional domestic matches, including hosting games in the Etisalat List A Tournament 2024 and the Wakhan National T20 Cup in 2025.[1][42] The ground features basic facilities adapted for local play, though international-standard enhancements remain limited due to the province's remoteness.[1] In Balkh Province, the Balkh Cricket Stadium in Mazar-i-Sharif is under development to bolster provincial cricket infrastructure, with construction initially funded by a grant from the Asian Cricket Council. As of 2021 assessments, it was not yet operational for major events, reflecting the slower pace of expansion in northern areas compared to southern hubs.[43] Northeastern provinces such as Takhar and Badakhshan lack dedicated stadiums of note, relying instead on informal fields for grassroots training; ACB efforts focus on academies rather than full venues here, prioritizing maintenance of existing northern sites amid logistical challenges.[44] Overall, these grounds emphasize regional qualifiers and youth development, with pitches typically using matting over concrete bases suited to arid local conditions.[45]

Southern and Southwestern Provinces

The southern and southwestern provinces of Afghanistan, including Helmand, Zabul, and Paktika, feature provincial cricket grounds adapted from arid, dust-prone sites into functional turfs primarily through construction efforts supported by the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB) and provincial funding in the 2010s.[46][47] These venues play a key role in grassroots cricket amid rugged terrain, hosting local matches that contribute to talent pipelines for domestic competitions like the Ahmadzai Shpageeza Cricket League.[1] Population density in these Pashtun-majority areas correlates with a higher empirical distribution of such facilities compared to more sparsely populated western provinces, enabling sustained local play even post-2021.[48]

Eastern and Central Provinces

Cricket grounds in Afghanistan's eastern provinces, which border Pakistan, reflect cross-border influences from refugee-era training in Peshawar and ongoing exchanges with Pakistani coaches who emphasize spin-friendly pitch preparation suited to local playing styles.[51] These venues primarily support provincial and youth competitions, with recent infrastructure upgrades enabling expanded local leagues. Central provinces, such as Ghazni, have more limited facilities, often consisting of basic fields used for domestic training rather than structured stadiums.
  • Kunar Cricket Ground, Asadabad: Established in 2018 with a $100,000 grant from the Asian Cricket Council, this venue accommodates approximately 1,000 spectators and serves provincial matches in the eastern Kunar Province.[1]
  • Laghman Cricket Ground, Mihtarlam: Handed over by the Afghanistan Cricket Board in May 2025, this newly constructed facility in Laghman Province hosts provincial inter-club matches, supporting youth development amid regional tournaments.[1]
  • Najibullah Taraki Cricket Ground, Jalalabad: Recently built in Nangarhar Province, this eastern venue facilitates local competitions and benefits from proximity to Pakistan for coaching inputs on turf maintenance.[1]
In 2025, minor renovations at these grounds, including turf enhancements, have prioritized youth leagues, with Laghman's new setup exemplifying efforts to bolster grassroots play in eastern areas.[52] Central venues remain underdeveloped, with Ghazni relying on rudimentary fields for occasional use rather than dedicated cricket infrastructure.[53]

Western Provinces

The western provinces of Afghanistan, encompassing Herat, Farah, Badghis, and Ghor, exhibit sparse cricket infrastructure, with fewer than five dedicated grounds identified amid national totals of 11 such facilities developed by the Afghanistan Cricket Board. Local fields in areas like Herat primarily support community-level play and multi-sport usage rather than professional or competitive cricket, lacking the specialized pitches and spectator amenities found in eastern and southern venues. This limited presence reflects geographic isolation from major urban centers and historical underinvestment in the sport's expansion westward, where adoption has lagged behind Pashtun-majority regions due to differing cultural and migratory exposures to cricket via Pakistan refugee communities. Provincial teams consequently depend on travel to central or eastern grounds for domestic tournaments, underscoring operational challenges in sustaining local development.[1]

Usage and Operational Realities

Domestic Competitions and Training

Afghanistan's domestic cricket competitions, including the Shpageeza Cricket League and Regional List A Tournament, rely on key grounds for fostering talent and competitive play. The Shpageeza League, the country's flagship T20 event organized by the Afghanistan Cricket Board, hosted its 2025 edition exclusively at Kabul International Cricket Stadium from July 19 to August 1, featuring five teams in a centralized format that prioritizes operational efficiency and large crowd attendance in the capital.[54][20] This scheduling minimizes travel disruptions amid logistical challenges, enabling consistent match delivery and player development through high-volume games.[55] List A competitions, such as the XBull Regional List A Tournament, utilize provincial venues like Rahmat Wali Masroor Cricket Ground in Khost for decisive stages, including the 2025 final where Mis-e-Ainak Region secured victory by five wickets on September 3.[41] These events, part of broader structures like the Ahmad Shah Abdali Four-Day Tournament, distribute matches across regions to build grassroots participation and identify performers for higher levels.[56] Training operations center on Kabul National Cricket Stadium's High Performance Center and associated academy, which host national team camps and skill-building sessions for senior and emerging players.[57] Khost Cricket Stadium supports specialized youth programs, including the Future Stars skill development camp launched in September 2025 with 19 participants, emphasizing technical refinement to enhance domestic-to-international transitions.[58] Such hubs have correlated with tangible outputs, as domestic leagues supplied key contributors to Afghanistan's 2024 international victories, including T20I series wins against teams like Ireland, via refined provincial talents.[59] Facilities at these grounds include basic amenities like electronic scoreboards and selective floodlight installations for extended play, though Kabul's primary stadium lacks comprehensive night-match capabilities compared to smaller venues like Ayobi Ground.[60] Following the Taliban assumption of power in August 2021, domestic events exclude women's competitions entirely, as public sports participation for females remains prohibited, halting prior national team activities and provincial development for women.[61][62]

Limitations on International Matches

Afghanistan's cricket grounds have hosted no full international matches since February 2019, when the national team played a T20I against Ireland at Kabul International Cricket Stadium, due to escalating security threats that have made hosting unfeasible for visiting teams. The Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB) has maintained a policy emphasizing player, official, and spectator safety as paramount, explicitly avoiding home fixtures amid ongoing instability, including the Taliban takeover in August 2021 that intensified international reluctance. This approach has persisted despite occasional domestic upgrades, rendering all bilateral "home" series at neutral venues, primarily in the United Arab Emirates (UAE).[63] In March 2025, the ACB formalized a five-year exclusive agreement with the Abu Dhabi Cricket and Sports Hub, designating Abu Dhabi as the primary venue for international matches and training camps through 2029, superseding a prior 2022 UAE-wide deal. This arrangement ensures continuity for series like the 2025 T20I and ODI encounters against Bangladesh in Sharjah and Abu Dhabi, bypassing Afghan soil entirely. Such neutral hosting has not hindered competitiveness, as evidenced by Afghanistan's away ODI series victory over Zimbabwe in 2024, where they secured a clean sweep including a match-winning five-wicket haul by Zia-ur-Rehman Ghazanfar in the decider.[64][65][66] The absence of global events has left Afghan venues underutilized for elite competition, with pitches and facilities instead validated through domestic leagues and preparatory workouts, preserving readiness for potential resumption while mitigating risks associated with high-profile inbound travel. ACB officials have indicated that domestic security improvements could enable hosting within 3-4 years, though no firm timeline exists amid persistent threats.[67][68]

Maintenance and Capacity Details

Cricket grounds across Afghanistan typically accommodate 4,000 to 8,000 spectators, with capacities varying by province and reflecting ongoing infrastructure limitations. The Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB) reports that venues like the Kunduz Cricket Ground in the northern region seat over 7,000.[1] These figures support domestic matches but fall short of ICC requirements for full international hosting without upgrades, prompting targeted expansions.[21] Maintenance challenges include inadequate drainage systems prevalent in over 20 provincial venues, exacerbated by regional weather patterns and limited resources, though specific Afghan ground data remains sparse compared to expatriate-hosted events.[3] The ACB's Turf and Facility Department oversees pitch preparation, with a shift from traditional coir matting—dominant before the 2010s—to hybrid grass surfaces in key sites; for example, Kabul Cricket Stadium completed grass cultivation and reconstruction in 2023 to improve playability.[69] In 2025, the ACB accelerated upgrades to align with ICC minima, launching renovations at multiple grounds including grandstand expansions at Khost Cricket Stadium (costing 34 million afghanis) and new construction at Najibullah Tarakai Cricket Ground.[21] [70] [31] These efforts, part of a broader initiative across four regional hubs, aim to enhance capacity and durability, with progress reported in June 2025 at sites like Rahmat Wali Masroor Cricket Ground.[71] Despite these advancements, many grounds retain basic turf setups, limiting high-volume usage.[72]

Challenges and Controversies

Security and Accessibility Issues

Prior to the Taliban takeover in August 2021, cricket grounds in Afghanistan faced recurrent terrorist attacks, including a suicide bombing on May 20, 2018, at a match in Jalalabad that killed eight people and wounded over 40 others.[73] Such incidents, often attributed to groups like the Islamic State Khorasan Province, underscored the vulnerability of public gatherings at these venues during the preceding government's tenure. Following the Taliban's assumption of control, domestic cricket operations persisted with enhanced internal security measures, though risks remained evident in a grenade explosion on July 29, 2022, at Kabul International Cricket Stadium during a Shpageeza Cricket League match, which killed two spectators and injured at least 13 others.[74][75] No comparable attacks on major grounds have been reported since that event, allowing the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB) to host annual domestic leagues without disruption, as evidenced by the continuation of events like the Shpageeza series through 2025.[76] This relative stability for local play contrasts with pre-2021 volatility, where multiple bombings halted competitions frequently. Accessibility for international teams and officials, however, has been severely curtailed due to persistent security advisories from Western governments citing Taliban-linked instability and cross-border threats, despite the ACB's assertions of secure environments for approved domestic events.[77] For instance, no full ICC-sanctioned international matches have occurred at Afghan grounds since 2021, with visiting sides opting for neutral venues amid fears of targeted attacks, even as ACB data highlights uninterrupted local tournaments averaging thousands of attendees per match.[78] This divergence reflects broader geopolitical caution, where media outlets emphasize residual militant capabilities over the ACB's operational continuity, which has enabled over 50 domestic fixtures annually without further verified incidents at established stadiums.[79]

Funding Dependencies and Geopolitical Influences

The development of cricket infrastructure in Afghanistan has relied heavily on non-Western bilateral aid, particularly from Pakistan and India, which facilitated early growth and stadium construction amid limited domestic resources. Pakistan provided foundational support during the 1980s and 1990s refugee crisis, when Afghan exiles in camps along the border accessed training facilities, coaching, and competitive play that seeded the national team's emergence; this grassroots assistance, often through local clubs and Peshawar-based leagues, enabled Afghan players to gain skills without formal state funding at the time.[10] India emerged as a primary funder for physical infrastructure post-2001, granting $1 million in 2014 specifically for the Kandahar International Cricket Stadium in Aino Mina, a project under the Small Development Project Scheme that included construction oversight and capacity building. Similar grants supported facilities in Balkh and Logar provinces, totaling around $1 million per initiative channeled through the Afghanistan Cricket Board (ACB), reflecting India's strategic soft power investments in regional stability rather than multilateral channels. These efforts contrasted with sporadic Western contributions, such as Germany's €700,000 for a Khost stadium in 2014, underscoring non-Western donors' outsized role in tangible builds.[80][15][18] Following the 2021 Taliban resurgence, funding dynamics shifted toward ACB-managed revenues and targeted international partnerships, mitigating Western sanctions on the regime that restricted broader aid flows but spared cricket-specific allocations. The International Cricket Council (ICC) maintained full funding to the ACB for men's programs as of April 2025, rejecting suspension calls despite geopolitical pressures, allowing renovations at grounds in Kabul, Kandahar, Khost, Nangarhar, Balkh, and Kunduz using board budgets derived from match fees and bilateral series. This autonomy debunked narratives of total external dependency, as evidenced by ACB's June 2025 launch of major upgrades without new foreign grants; concurrently, partnerships with UAE venues like Sheikh Zayed Stadium in Abu Dhabi served as de facto home grounds, enabling revenue from hosted internationals amid selective Pakistani aid reductions tied to border tensions.[81][22]

Cultural and Gender Restrictions on Usage

Following the Taliban's takeover of Afghanistan in August 2021, official policy prohibited women from participating in sports, including cricket, citing Islamic principles that deem such activities exposing or incompatible with modesty requirements for females in public settings.[82][83] This edict effectively ended women's access to cricket grounds nationwide, rendering facilities such as Kabul International Cricket Stadium and others exclusively available for men's training, domestic competitions, and limited international preparations, with no recorded instances of female usage since.[84][85] The ban led to the dissolution of the Afghanistan women's national cricket team, with players fleeing abroad and the International Cricket Council (ICC) halting direct funding to women's programs while maintaining support for the men's side through the Afghanistan Cricket Board.[81][86] In response, the ICC established a task force in April 2025 to aid exiled female cricketers, but no resources were diverted from men's operations, preserving ground access solely for male athletes amid ongoing domestic and preparatory activities.[87][88] Empirically, this male-only allocation has coincided with notable achievements by the men's team, including qualification for the semifinals of the 2024 ICC Men's T20 World Cup via an eight-run victory over Bangladesh on June 25, 2024, despite broader international isolation.[89][90] Proponents of the policy frame it as alignment with cultural and religious norms prioritizing male public athletics, arguing resource concentration has enhanced competitive outcomes without the administrative burdens of parallel programs.[82] Critics, including human rights organizations, contend it perpetuates gender exclusion, urging sanctions like ICC membership suspension to pressure reversal, though such measures have not materialized and men's progress has persisted.[91][84]
