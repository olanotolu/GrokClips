List of current Indian opposition leaders


The list of current Indian opposition leaders catalogs the individuals holding the position of Leader of the Opposition (LoP) in the Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, and various state legislative assemblies and councils across India, where the opposition—defined as the largest non-governing party or coalition—secures at least one-tenth of the total seats to qualify for statutory recognition and associated privileges, including cabinet-rank status and involvement in key appointments such as those to the Central Vigilance Commission.[1]
In the national Parliament, following the 2024 general elections in which the opposition INDIA bloc secured 234 seats against the ruling NDA's 293, Rahul Gandhi of the Indian National Congress was appointed LoP in the 18th Lok Sabha on June 25, 2024, restoring the position after a decade-long vacancy since 2014 due to the opposition falling short of the threshold in the prior term.[2][1] In the Rajya Sabha, Mallikarjun Kharge, also of Congress, has held the LoP role continuously since 2021, representing the primary opposition voice in the upper house.[1]
At the state level, these leaders vary by ruling coalition and include figures such as Tejashwi Yadav of the Rashtriya Janata Dal in Bihar's assembly, V. D. Satheesan of Congress in Kerala, and Atishi of the Aam Aadmi Party in Delhi, reflecting the fragmented multiparty landscape where opposition roles shift with electoral outcomes and enable scrutiny of state governments through legislative debates and committees.[3][4] The positions underscore India's parliamentary system's emphasis on adversarial balance, though their effectiveness often hinges on opposition unity amid ideological diversity and historical tendencies toward coalition instability.[1]

Recognition Criteria and Context

Definition and Legal Basis

The Leader of the Opposition in Indian parliamentary and legislative contexts refers to the designated head of the largest political party or pre-poll alliance that does not support the ruling government in a given house, tasked with articulating alternative policy positions, scrutinizing executive actions, and representing opposition interests in debates and committees.[5] This role ensures adversarial balance in legislative proceedings, with the leader typically selected by consensus within their party or alliance.[6] In the national Parliament, the position holds statutory recognition under the Salary and Allowances of Leaders of Opposition in Parliament Act, 1977, enacted on August 18, 1977, which defines the Leader of the Opposition in the Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha as the leader of the largest recognized parliamentary party not aligned with the government, provided that party commands at least 10% of the house's total seats—a threshold derived from procedural rules to qualify as a "recognized party."[7] The Act entitles the Leader to a monthly salary equivalent to that of a Cabinet Minister, daily allowances, and amenities such as secretarial staff and travel facilities, formalizing the office's privileges while prohibiting dual salary draws from membership funds.[7] This legislation stemmed from recommendations to strengthen opposition efficacy post-Emergency, embedding the role in constitutional democracy without direct mention in the Indian Constitution itself.[6] For state legislative assemblies and councils, legal provisions vary: while some states like Punjab and Andhra Pradesh have enacted analogous statutes—such as the Salary and Allowances of Leader of Opposition in Legislative Assembly Act, 1978 in Punjab—providing similar salaries and recognition, others rely on assembly rules of procedure or conventions approved by the Speaker or Chairman.[8][9] The 10% seat threshold is commonly applied nationwide for recognition, though not uniformly codified, ensuring only substantial opposition groups qualify and preventing fragmentation; failure to meet this can result in no formal Leader being appointed, as observed in certain hung assemblies.[10] In union territories with legislatures, provisions mirror state practices under delegated assembly rules.[10]

Threshold Requirements and Exceptions

The recognition of a Leader of the Opposition (LoP) in Indian parliamentary and legislative bodies requires the opposition party or alliance to hold a minimum of 10% of the total seats in the relevant house, as stipulated in the rules of procedure for legislatures. For the Lok Sabha, this threshold equates to at least 55 seats out of 543, ensuring the opposition commands sufficient representation to fulfill statutory roles such as appointments to bodies like the Central Bureau of Investigation director selection committee or the National Human Rights Commission. The same criterion applies to the Rajya Sabha, where 10% of 245 seats requires 25 members, and to state legislative assemblies, scaled proportionally to the house's total strength—for instance, 18 seats in the 200-member Delhi Assembly. This numerical threshold, formalized through parliamentary conventions and rules rather than explicit constitutional mandate, prioritizes substantive opposition presence to enable effective checks and balances, including participation in high-level panels like the Public Accounts Committee chairmanship. In practice, the Speaker (in lower houses) or Chairman (in upper houses) formally recognizes the floor leader of the single largest opposition party or pre-poll alliance meeting this quota, notifying the President or Governor accordingly. Exceptions arise when no opposition group attains the 10% mark, resulting in the non-appointment of an LoP, as occurred in the Lok Sabha from 2014 to 2019 when the Indian National Congress held only 44 seats post-2014 elections, depriving it of statutory roles despite being the primary opposition. Similar vacancies have persisted in certain state assemblies, such as Bihar's until recent adjustments, underscoring that the rule admits no discretionary lowering of the bar to avoid diluting the position's institutional weight. In upper houses like state legislative councils, the threshold mirrors the 10% requirement, applied to the council's total elected and nominated members, with recognition following the largest qualifying opposition's leader. Rare procedural exceptions may involve interim recognitions during leadership transitions, but these do not override the seat criterion; post-election reconfigurations, such as mergers or alliances, can retroactively qualify a group if the aggregate meets 10%, though this demands formal Speaker validation to prevent manipulation. No LoP position exists in bodies lacking this formalized opposition leadership framework, reinforcing the threshold's role in linking recognition to verifiable legislative heft rather than mere political claims.

Parliamentary Leaders

Lok Sabha

The Leader of the Opposition in the Lok Sabha serves as the principal representative of the opposition in India's lower house of Parliament, recognized under the Salary and Allowances of Leaders of Opposition in Parliament Act, 1977, which mandates the position for the leader of the largest parliamentary party or coalition not in government that commands at least 10% of the total seats. This role entitles the holder to privileges equivalent to a Union Cabinet Minister, including participation in key parliamentary committees and consultations on national security matters. Rahul Gandhi, a member of the Indian National Congress (INC) and MP from Raebareli, has held the position since June 25, 2024, following the 18th Lok Sabha elections where the INC emerged as the single largest opposition party with 99 seats out of 543.[11][2] The appointment came after the INC-led INDIA alliance failed to form the government, with the Bharatiya Janata Party-led National Democratic Alliance securing a majority under Prime Minister Narendra Modi. As of October 26, 2025, Gandhi continues in this role, actively critiquing government policies, such as terming the death of a doctor in Satara as an "institutionalised murder" due to alleged mishandling by authorities.[12] His leadership has focused on issues like electoral integrity and youth empowerment, though critics from the ruling BJP have dismissed his campaigns as lacking broader resonance.[13] The position remains with the INC as no shifts in parliamentary strength have altered the opposition dynamics since the election.[14]

Rajya Sabha

The Leader of the Opposition in the Rajya Sabha is Mallikarjun Kharge of the Indian National Congress, representing Karnataka. He has held the position since 16 February 2021, when the Congress was designated as the principal opposition party after securing the requisite one-tenth of the house's total membership, approximately 25 seats out of 245.[15] As of October 2025, Kharge continues in this role, actively engaging in parliamentary proceedings, including raising objections to procedural matters such as security personnel presence in the house on 1 August 2025.[16] Kharge, born on 21 July 1942, serves concurrently as the president of the Indian National Congress since October 2022. His tenure as Leader of the Opposition entitles him to chair key parliamentary committees, including the Public Accounts Committee, and provides a platform for critiquing government policies, as evidenced by his interventions on issues like central policies during sessions in March 2025.[17][18] The position underscores the Congress's status as the largest opposition bloc in the upper house, despite the Bharatiya Janata Party-led National Democratic Alliance holding a majority overall in Parliament.[15]

State Legislative Assemblies

Leaders of the Opposition in state legislative assemblies are recognized in those states where the single largest opposition party or pre-poll alliance collectively holds at least one-tenth of the total membership, mirroring the threshold for parliamentary positions. This ensures the opposition's voice in assembly proceedings, committee nominations, and budgetary oversight. Union Territory Legislative Assemblies

In the National Capital Territory of Delhi, Atishi of the Aam Aadmi Party (AAP) serves as Leader of the Opposition in the Legislative Assembly, having been unanimously elected to the position on February 23, 2025, after the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) secured a majority in the February 2025 elections, reducing AAP to the main opposition with 22 seats.[24][25] In Jammu and Kashmir, Sunil Kumar Sharma of the BJP holds the position of Leader of the Opposition in the Legislative Assembly, elected unanimously by the BJP legislature party on November 3, 2024, following the National Conference-led alliance's formation of government after the 2024 elections, where BJP won 29 seats as the largest opposition party.[26][27] In Puducherry, R. Siva of the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) is the Leader of the Opposition in the Legislative Assembly, a role he has occupied since the All India N.R. Congress-BJP coalition retained power in the 2021 elections, with the opposition INDIA bloc (including DMK) holding 11 seats against the ruling 33.[28][29] These positions are recognized under the framework of the respective union territory assemblies, where the Leader of the Opposition must represent the largest opposition party or coalition meeting the threshold of at least one-tenth of total seats, enabling statutory roles such as participation in selection committees for key appointments.[30] No other union territories currently maintain unicameral legislative assemblies with elected opposition leaders.[31]

State Legislative Councils

Upper House Opposition Leaders

The Leaders of the Opposition in Indian state legislative councils are appointed from the largest opposition party or alliance that holds at least 10% of the total seats, as per the criteria under state legislative rules modeled on parliamentary conventions.[1] These positions exist only in the six states with active bicameral legislatures: Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh. As of October 2025, the post remains vacant in Maharashtra due to ongoing disputes among opposition parties over seat shares and recognition following the 2024 assembly elections.[32]
