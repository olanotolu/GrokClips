Hajong people

The Hajong people are a Tibeto-Burman ethnic group native to the northeastern regions of India and northern Bangladesh, speaking an Indo-Aryan language influenced by Tibeto-Burman substrata and numbering approximately 175,000.[1] Primarily rice farmers practicing Hinduism, they maintain cultural traditions centered on agrarian life, riverine settlements, and distinctive crafts such as weaving and ornament-making.[2] Their history features ancient migrations from Tibetan highlands to the Brahmaputra Valley and later displacements, including the flight of about 30,000 individuals from East Pakistan in 1964 amid religious persecution and territorial changes following partition.[3]

History

Origins and Early Settlement

The Hajong people are classified as a Mongoloid ethnic group belonging to the Tibeto-Burman linguistic family, specifically within the Bodo-Naga subsection and as a sub-tribe of the broader Kachari racial group, which includes related communities such as the Boro, Mech, and Garo.[2] Their ancestors are believed to have originated in Tibet and migrated southward into the Brahmaputra Valley of present-day Assam, initially settling in the Kamrup region as descendants of the Kachari people.[4] Empirical evidence for these early migrations remains limited, with historical records thin prior to the colonial era, relying primarily on linguistic affinities, oral traditions, and sporadic census classifications like the 1881 British Indian Census, which grouped Hajongs under the Kachari race alongside twelve subgroups.[2][5] Early settlements centered in the Hajo township of Kamrup district, Assam, from where the ethnonym "Hajong" derives, possibly linked to a migration of approximately 12,000 individuals following the area's destruction by invasions.[2] Displaced groups relocated to the Garo Hills in present-day Meghalaya, establishing communities such as Hazar-i or Baro Hajari, and expanded into adjacent Goalpara district in Assam, where aboriginal dwelling sites have been identified.[5] From these footholds, Hajongs practiced wet-rice cultivation, clearing forested lowlands along riverbanks like the Simsang and Susong, which facilitated further dispersal into Sylhet and northern Mymensingh regions of present-day Bangladesh by the pre-colonial period.[2] These patterns reflect adaptation to alluvial plains suited for agriculture, with concentrations bounded by natural features such as the Suwarkuna river to the north and Jongkona to the southeast, spanning roughly 1,440 square kilometers.[5] Oral accounts attribute the name "Hajong" to Garo linguistic influences post-migration, where "Ha" signifies earth and "Jong" denotes worm or insect, reflecting perceptions of the settlers' agrarian lifestyle akin to burrowing in soil, though alternative etymologies tie it directly to Hajo without pejorative connotations.[2] While mythological traditions invoke descent from a Kshatriya king named Kartayabirjarjun of the Hajo Kingdom, these lack corroboration from archaeological or documentary evidence and appear as post-settlement rationalizations rather than causal origins.[2] By the 19th century, prior to major 20th-century displacements, Hajong populations were documented in riverine and foothill zones, underscoring a historical trajectory of valley adaptation amid episodic conflicts with neighboring Ahom and Muslim forces.[4]

20th-Century Migration and Displacement

The partition of British India in 1947 resulted in significant displacement for the Hajong people, as their traditional habitats in the Mymensingh and Netrokona districts of Bengal were incorporated into East Pakistan, prompting migrations to adjacent territories in India, particularly the Garo Hills region of Assam (present-day Meghalaya).[5] These early movements involved thousands of Hajongs seeking refuge from communal tensions and economic pressures in the newly formed Muslim-majority state, with estimates indicating that by the mid-1950s, substantial numbers had resettled in Indian-administered areas.[5] A more massive wave of displacement occurred in the early 1960s due to the construction of the Kaptai Dam on the Karnaphuli River in East Pakistan, completed between 1957 and 1963, which submerged over 1,000 square kilometers of land and flooded ancestral Hajong settlements in the Chittagong Hill Tracts and surrounding foothills.[6] This hydroelectric project displaced approximately 100,000 indigenous people overall, including an estimated 30,000 Hajongs, who faced not only loss of farmland and homes but also heightened religious persecution as non-Muslim minorities amid rising communal violence.[3] [7] In response, between 1962 and 1964, these Hajong refugees crossed into India, initially entering through the Lushai Hills district of Assam (now Mizoram) and seeking asylum from the Indian government, which provided temporary relief camps and resettlement in the North-East Frontier Agency (NEFA, now Arunachal Pradesh), Assam, and Meghalaya.[8] [9] The Indian authorities facilitated their integration by allocating land for agriculture in southern Arunachal Pradesh and other northeastern states, though challenges such as cultural adaptation and local resource competition persisted.[8] By the late 1960s, the majority of these displaced Hajongs had been rehabilitated, marking a pivotal shift in their demographic distribution toward Indian territories.[3]

Demographics

Population Estimates

The Hajong population in India was recorded as 79,800 in the 2011 Census of India, with the largest concentrations in Meghalaya (approximately 41,000) and Assam (approximately 28,000), alongside smaller numbers in Arunachal Pradesh and other northeastern states.[10][2] In Bangladesh, the 2022 Population and Housing Census reported 7,996 Hajong individuals, primarily in the northeastern districts such as Netrokona.[11] These figures reflect official enumerations, though ethnographic studies estimate the current total across both countries may exceed 100,000 due to natural growth and unreported migrations since the early 2010s.[2][10]

Geographical Distribution

The Hajong people are distributed across northeastern India and northern Bangladesh, with the majority residing in India.[12] In India, they form significant communities in Meghalaya, where they constitute the fourth largest ethnic group and are concentrated in the plain belts of the Garo Hills and Khasi Hills, including districts such as West Garo Hills (with villages like Kashra Hajong and Gandhipara Hajong), East Khasi Hills, and West Khasi Hills bordering Bangladesh.[9][13][14] In Assam, Hajong settlements span multiple districts, including Goalpara (a historical aboriginal area), Nagaon, Darrang, Chirang, Kokrajhar, Udalguri, Lakhimpur, Dhemaji, Dima Hasao (e.g., Natun Hajong and Purana Hajong villages), and Karbi Anglong.[5][10][15] Smaller populations exist in Arunachal Pradesh, particularly in Changlang district, where some were resettled as refugees in areas like Haripur, Modhupur-I, and Modhupur-II following 1960s displacements from East Pakistan.[16][17] In Bangladesh, the Hajong are primarily located in the northeastern regions of the Mymensingh division, with the largest concentrations in Netrakona district (e.g., Durgapur and Kalmakanda upazilas) and Mymensingh district's northern areas such as Haluaghat and Dhobaura, situated on the southern slopes of the Garo Hills.[18][19] Additional settlements occur in Sherpur district (Nalitabari and Jhenaigati upazilas) and parts of Sylhet.[20] Historical aboriginal habitats in Bangladesh include Sylhet, reflecting pre-partition ties with adjacent Indian territories.[5] These distributions stem from ancestral migrations and 20th-century displacements due to river erosion and flooding, leading to cross-border movements and resettlements.[10][15]

Language

Linguistic Features and Influences

The Hajong language belongs to the Indo-Aryan branch of the Indo-European family, specifically within the Eastern zone's Bengali-Assamese subgroup.[21] It is characterized by phonological patterns akin to regional Indo-Aryan languages such as Assamese and Bengali, including a syllable structure featuring an obligatory vowel nucleus with optional consonantal onsets and codas.[22] Grammatically, Hajong employs postpositional case markers for nouns, such as ge or gon for dative-accusative (marking animate or prominent objects), la for genitive (indicating possession, location, or time), ni or bÊ°aÊ² for locative and allative, and diÉ¯ for instrumental.[21] Reduplication serves as a key morphological device, forming expressives through syllable repetition (e.g., single lexemes like iterated forms for intensification) and lexical or morphological patterns for derivation.[23] Despite its core Indo-Aryan alignment, Hajong displays areal influences from neighboring Tibeto-Burman languages, evident in certain case formatives with cognates in Garo and Koch (e.g., genitive la and locative ni), suggesting substrate effects or prolonged contact due to the Hajong people's historical proximity to Tibeto-Burman groups.[24] [21] Vocabulary and phonology show affinity with Assamese and Bengali, including shared lexical items and segmental distributions, yet retain unique traits such as distinct vowel qualities not fully represented in the Assamese script traditionally used for writing Hajong.[25] These Indo-Aryan dominances likely stem from extended bilingualism and cultural assimilation in Assam and Meghalaya, while Tibeto-Burman grammatical borrowings reflect the multilingual ecology of Northeast India.[22] Dialectal variation exists between northern (Korebari) and southern (Doskani) forms, with high lexical similarity (92-96%) maintaining mutual intelligibility.[26]

Religion

Predominant Beliefs and Practices


The Hajong people predominantly practice a syncretic form of Hinduism, incorporating elements of their indigenous animistic traditions rooted in panentheism, which posits divine power inherent in nature and all things. This belief system emphasizes reincarnation based on one's deeds, with souls believed to wander until proper funeral rites are performed. While most Hajongs identify as Hindus, particularly adherents of the Shakti tradition venerating goddesses like Durga and Kali, a subgroup known as Khatals follows Vaishnavism, worshipping Vishnu and maintaining distinct markers such as basil garlands and uncut hair.[10][27][28] Central to their practices is the worship of both Hindu deitiesâ€”such as Kamakhya, Lakshmi, and Manasaâ€”and traditional nature spirits, including the primary deity Bastu (or Bausto/Bauch), associated with earth fertility and agricultural prosperity. Ancestral deities and local spirits like Khangkhangi-deo for protection or Haowa-deo for prosperity are also revered to avert misfortune or cure ailments, often through rituals involving offerings of rice, fruits, or animal sacrifices such as goats, pigeons, or ducks. These sacrifices, conducted by traditional priests called Deoshi at village altars (Than), persist alongside Hindu rites performed by Brahmin or Adhikari priests, though animal offerings are declining under Hindu influence favoring non-violent bhoga (vegetarian offerings).[10][27][29][28] Life-cycle rituals blend Hindu customs with indigenous elements; for instance, birth involves purification after 5-7 days and feasts, while death rites include cremation, a 12-day mourning period, and shraddha ceremonies on the 13th day. Festivals reflect this syncretism: Hindu observances like Durga Puja and Kali Puja are communal, supplemented by traditional agricultural rites such as Bastu Puja in the Bohag month (April-May) for bountiful harvests, involving sacrifices and homemade liquor, and Biswa, a pre-monsoon harvest celebration with sub-rituals like Kani Puja. Pilgrimages to sites like Kamakhya Temple further underscore Hindu integration, yet belief in malevolent spirits causing illness, appeased by shamans, maintains animistic undertones.[10][27][30][29]

Sects and Variations

The Hajong people exhibit religious variations primarily within their Hindu framework, divided into two main sects: the Shakta-oriented Hajong (or Hojong) and the Vaishnava-oriented Khatal. The Hajong sect predominantly worships Shakti, the divine feminine power often manifested as Durga, incorporating animistic elements such as reverence for ancestral spirits and nature deities alongside Hindu rituals.[27] This group, comprising the majority, emphasizes tantric practices and village-level sacrifices to local gods, reflecting a syncretic blend of indigenous beliefs and Shaivism-influenced Hinduism. In contrast, the Khatal sect adheres to Vaishnavism, focusing on devotion to Vishnu and his avatars, particularly through bhakti traditions that prioritize ethical conduct and communal worship over esoteric rites.[27] This distinction arose historically from regional influences and intermarriages, with Khatal communities maintaining separate priestly roles for Vishnu-centric ceremonies while sharing broader Hajong customs. Both sects observe core Hindu festivals like Durga Puja but adapt them to local animistic customs, such as offerings to household guardians. Contemporary variations include limited Christian influences, particularly Protestant denominations among urban or refugee Hajong populations in Assam and Meghalaya, driven by 20th-century missionary activities; however, these remain marginal, with fewer than 10% adherence reported in ethnographic surveys.[12] Traditional sects persist due to cultural insularity and resistance to full assimilation, though syncretism with folk Hinduismâ€”marked by worship of snake goddess Manasa and annual sacrificesâ€”blurs strict boundaries across groups.[19]

Social Structure

Clan Systems and Kinship

The Hajong people are divided into clans referred to as nikni, which form the basis of their social organization and regulate marriage alliances. There are seventeen recognized nikni: Porachunga, Chondi, Batajore, Balihata, Kendagao, Taragao, Jignigao, Katleygao, Boligao, Kamakha, Kharugao, Sonamoi, Chatigao, Komligao, Ghorabali, Poroshmoni, and Akhigao.[27] Clan exogamy is a core rule, prohibiting marriage within the same nikni to maintain social cohesion and prevent incestuous unions.[27] This practice aligns with broader tribal endogamy, favoring unions within the Hajong community but across clans.[31] Hajong kinship is patrilineal, with descent, inheritance, and lineage traced through the male line.[27] Property passes to sons equally upon the father's death, while daughters typically receive no share unless a widow elects not to remarry and retains control.[27] The society is patriarchal, with the father exercising primary authority over family decisions, and patrilocal, as the bride generally joins the husband's paternal household post-marriage.[27] Traditionally, extended joint families predominated, comprising multiple generations under one patriarch, though nuclear family units have grown more prevalent amid modernization.[27]

Geographical and Social Divisions

The Hajong people inhabit scattered settlements across northeastern India and northern Bangladesh, reflecting historical migrations driven by floods, conflicts, and partition in the mid-20th century. In India, major concentrations exist in Assam's Kamrup and Goalpara districts, Meghalaya's West Garo Hills, East Khasi Hills, and Ri-Bhoi districts, and Arunachal Pradesh's Changlang district, where approximately 67,000 Chakma-Hajong refugees from East Pakistan were resettled in the 1960s following displacement by the Kaptai Dam project.[9][5][32] These areas feature lowland plains and foothills suitable for wet-rice cultivation, the Hajongs' traditional livelihood.[33] In Bangladesh, settlements are denser in the haor regions of Netrokona, Sunamganj, Mymensingh, and Sherpur districts, with specific upazilas including Haluaghat, Dhobaura, Shribordi, Jhinaigati, Nalitabari, Dhorompasha, Taherpur, Duarabazar, Komlakanda, and Durgapur; these low-lying floodplains, once part of greater Sylhet and Mymensingh, host around 15,000 Hajongs as per early 21st-century estimates.[27][18] Cross-border ties persist, with some villages straddling the India-Bangladesh frontier in Meghalaya's Khasi Hills, fostering shared cultural practices despite national divisions.[29] Socially, the Hajongs are divided into seventeen exogamous clans (niknis), which regulate marriage alliances and inheritance: Porachunga, Chondi, Batajore, Balihata, Kendagao, Taragao, Jignigao, Katleygao, Boligao, Kamakha, Kharugao, Sonamoi, Chatigao, Komligao, Ghorabali, Poroshmoni, and Akhigao (the last now largely supplanted by Hindu gotras like Kashyap and Roy among assimilated members).[27] Clan membership traces patrilineally, prohibiting intra-clan unions to maintain genetic diversity and social cohesion, a practice rooted in their Tibeto-Burman kinship norms.[27] Further social distinctions arise from religious subgroups: the Hajong proper, centered on Shakti worship with animistic elements, and the Khatal, who adopted Vaishnavism under historical influences, leading to variations in rituals and dietary taboos despite shared ethnic identity.[27] Administratively, communities organize into hamlets of 5â€“20 households forming villages (gaon or para), which cluster into chaklas and parganas, governed by hereditary gaobura (village heads) and temporary sorey morols (chakla chiefs) to adjudicate disputes and allocate resources.[27][34] These divisions reinforce patriarchal authority, with property passing to sons and joint families predominant in rural settlements.[27]

Marriage and Family

Customs and Practices

The Hajong people practice endogamous marriages, restricting unions to within their tribe, with inter-tribal marriages being rare and generally forbidden.[10][30] Monogamy is the predominant form, while polygamy occurs infrequently.[35] Marriages are typically arranged by parents through a matchmaker known as Jahu or Jasu, with prohibitions against unions between close maternal or paternal kin to maintain clan exogamy.[36][37] Wedding rituals span two days and involve community participation, including traditional music and a brief bride price paid by the groom's family.[38] Key ceremonies include the wedding banquet called Bi'Ã¢-khawa, where the bride and groom partake in shared meals symbolizing union, alongside other rites such as the bride weaving her own wedding garmentsâ€”a custom still observed in some communities.[29][36] For the khutri (warrior) class, a sword ritual underscores the marital bond.[39] Dowry is not practiced in contemporary Hajong society, though daughters may receive money and ornaments from their families at marriage to aid in establishing new households, particularly for those of limited means.[18][28] Hajong families are patriarchal, with inheritance passing primarily through the male line and authority vested in the eldest male.[37] Kinship emphasizes clan affiliations, which regulate social interactions and exogamy rules, fostering extended family networks that provide mutual support in agrarian lifestyles.[37] These practices reinforce communal cohesion, though modernization has introduced variations, such as simplified rituals in urban settings.[18]

Culture

Traditional Attire and Ornaments

The Hajong women traditionally wear the pathin, a handwoven wrap-around lower garment resembling a skirt, typically featuring vibrant stripes in colors such as red, blue, green, black, pink, and yellow, woven from combined yarns on back-strap or frame looms.[27][40][10] This garment measures approximately 4.5 feet in length and 3.75 feet in width, serving as a distinctive identifier of Hajong female attire.[27] Paired with a red blouse and sometimes a pasra (small apron-like upper covering), the pathin is often complemented by the argon, a shuttle-woven shawl with large-scale symmetrical patterns of geometrical shapes, stylized leaves, and flowers, draped over the shoulders for ceremonial or daily use.[30][41] Hajong men traditionally don a gamsa or gamcha (towel-like lower cloth) and a headgear called pag adorned with ornate geometrical patterns, while the kompes, a richly textured fabric with raised patterns woven on traditional salbana or sipnibana looms, functions as a winter scarf or belt.[30][42][29] Women excel in weaving these items, including bridal garments prepared specifically for weddings, underscoring the cultural role of female craftsmanship in preserving textile traditions.[29] Ornaments, referred to as guina in the Hajong language, consist primarily of silver pieces historically worn by both genders, with gold, coral, ivory, and conch shells also incorporated; these include the hasuli or harsora (silver necklaces), katabaju (silver armlets), kanful or kankurya (earrings), nakful (nose pins), baghkharu (bracelets or ankle rings weighing 35-50 grams), gunjari (anklets), and conch shell bangles (haka) for married women.[30][29] Such jewelry, often weighing around 65 grams for neck pieces from regions like Goalpara, symbolized status and heritage but has become rarer in daily use, reserved for festivals and rituals amid modernization.[43]

Housing and Architecture

Hajong villages consist of houses clustered on elevated ground near paddy fields, typically arranged around the Adhikari's residence without a fixed orientation.[28] Traditional homes are single-story with verandas, built from bamboo, wood, reeds, thatch, mud, straw, and bamboo strips.[30][28] Walls comprise split bamboo plastered with mud or cow dung, roofs are thatched, and floors are earthen, differing from raised platforms used by other regional tribes.[44][28] Low doorways necessitate bowing upon entry, and construction historically involved community labor during dry seasons.[28] Homesteads feature specialized structures: Bhat Ghor for sleeping and dining; separate Akhil Ghar kitchen; Tongi or Kasri Ghar dormitory for guests; Thakur Ghar prayer room; Jora or Cupa Ghar for married children; Guli Ghar cattle shed; Dheki Ghar for husking; and Chang Ghar granary for grain storage, alongside Khola Chang for rainy periods.[28] The Chang Ghar is elevated without doors, a design element tied to cultural beliefs preserving household prosperity.[45] Contemporary adaptations include corrugated iron roofs, cement, and bricks, driven by government schemes, though earthen and bamboo elements endure in rural settings.[30][28]

Cuisine and Food Habits

The staple food of the Hajong people is rice, particularly sticky rice, consumed three times daily with accompanying vegetables, fish, or other proteins.[10][46] Fish ranks as a preferred item, while pork and fowl are generally avoided, though pigeon, goat meat, and historically tortoise are consumed.[10] Preparation methods emphasize steaming and frying over boiling, reflecting traditional techniques tied to available resources like banana leaves and bamboo.[47] Traditional dishes include ding pura (or ding-pora), a sweet sticky rice steamed inside green bamboo segments and often paired with tea or milk, which can be stored for several days.[47][10] Leba hak consists of a curry made from ground rice flour mixed with water and sometimes small fish.[47] Fermented rice preparations such as bukni bhat and steamed rice cakes known as pithas (e.g., pat pithÃ¢, batul pithÃ¢) are common, especially during festivals like PusnÃ¢, using sticky rice, banana leaves, and ingredients like tal juice or oil.[47][10] Soda-curry incorporates vegetables such as long gourd, yam, or bamboo shoots with dried fish, flavored using a traditional sodium bicarbonate derived from filtered ashes of burnt banana trees.[47] Protein sources extend to wild and aquatic foods, including river or paddy snails (with pointed varieties preferred), crabs fried with shells intact, and occasionally oysters, sourced from local ponds, streams, or water holes.[47] Turtle paired with sticky rice holds cultural significance, referenced in traditional songs, but consumption has declined due to the animal's endangered status.[47] In regions like northeastern Bangladesh, up to 45% of food derives from homestead forests, incorporating wild plants and sustainable extraction practices.[48] Contemporary habits show some shifts, particularly in areas like Arunachal Pradesh's Changlang district, where rice remains dominant but occasional proteins (e.g., eggs, poultry, mutton) are consumed by about 40% of the population, supplemented by daily milk for 60% and modern items like biscuits.[46] Betel nut leaves chewed raw with tobacco form a habitual accompaniment, and home-brewed rice beer is taken sporadically outside religious contexts.[10] Vegetables like ginger (used by 97.5%) and tubers such as yam or sweet potato appear twice weekly for nearly half the community, influenced by availability.[46]

Arts, Music, and Festivals

The Hajong people's artistic expressions primarily manifest through performing arts, including folk dances, songs, and music tied to agricultural cycles and community gatherings. Traditional dances such as Lewatana, performed in circular or paired formations, symbolize themes of love and communal strength, often accompanied by rhythmic drumming and flute melodies during harvest-related events.[49] Instruments like the dhaak, dhol, flute (bahi), and tala provide the sonic backdrop, with songs invoking mythological narratives or daily agrarian life, as seen in performances featuring stanzas like "Ramai Ram."[50] [51] Festivals serve as central venues for these arts, blending indigenous practices with Hindu-influenced rituals. The Charkhela (or Chorkhela) festival, observed for one week around Kartik Sankranti preceding Deepawali, involves youths in traditional attire visiting households to collect rice and funds through door-to-door singing of songs like Lewatana, Tengla, and Jakha Mara, culminating in dance dramas depicting epics such as the Ramayana.[50] [49] This agro-based celebration illuminates paddy fields with lamps for bountiful yields and ends with communal feasts, fostering social bonds in a traditionally conservative society.[50] Cor-khela, the oldest recorded festival held in January, features nocturnal processions of dance troupes singing cultural songs while traversing villages, incorporating contests and displays of self-woven attire to highlight performative excellence.[52] Similarly, PusnÃ¢ Porbo gatherings emphasize competitive singing and dancing in ornate traditional garments, drawing participants from multiple locales for evening performances.[52] Other dances like Jaka Mara, executed by women carrying Jakoi creels in red patin cloth while chanting agricultural hymns, and Kadongkhel, a pre-ploughing group routine, underscore the integration of music and movement in seasonal preparations.[49] These traditions, preserved through oral transmission, reflect the Hajongs' agrarian ethos and communal harmony, though contemporary performances increasingly occur at inter-tribal events like the Hornbill Festival.[51]

Economy and Livelihoods

Traditional Occupations

The Hajong people have traditionally been an agrarian community, with agriculture serving as their primary occupation since ancient times. They engage in wet rice cultivation (Oryza sativa) in the fertile plains and low hills of their ancestral regions, supplemented by cash crops such as jute (Corchorus spp.), mustard (Brassica spp.), and various pulses. This sedentary farming practice distinguishes them from neighboring shifting cultivators and is believed to have originated their ethnonym "Hajong," derived from a Garo term denoting agricultural expertise.[49][29][49] In addition to field crops, Hajongs rely on forest resources for supplementary livelihoods, including the collection of non-timber products like firewood, fruits, and medicinal plants, which contribute significantly to household incomeâ€”up to a notable portion in forest-dependent settlements. Hunting, particularly of wild pigs (Sus scrofa), provides meat and is conducted using simple, efficient methods such as communal drives with dogs and spears. These activities underscore their deep integration with the surrounding ecosystems for sustenance and economic resilience.[53][54] Hajong women play a central role in textile production through handloom weaving, crafting items like shawls and scarves from locally sourced cotton or fibers, which serve both domestic use and occasional trade. While not the economic core, this craft preserves cultural motifs and provides auxiliary income, especially in land-scarce households where some members work as day laborers or firewood sellers.[19]

Modern Adaptations

In response to land scarcity, recurrent flooding, and limited agricultural viability, many Hajong individuals, particularly the landless, have turned to daily wage labor on rice fields and other farmlands owned by larger holders.[30] This shift supplements subsistence farming, with laborers often hired seasonally for planting and harvesting activities.[29] Animal husbandry has emerged as a key adaptation, with piggery and poultry rearing providing steady supplementary income, especially for women who manage these enterprises alongside household duties. Weaving remains a vital side occupation, where Hajong women produce traditional textiles not only for community use but also for sale to neighboring groups like the Garo, generating cash through local markets.[30] Cultivation of cash crops such as jute, mustard, and pulses marks a transition from pure subsistence to market-oriented agriculture, enabling sales in regional trade networks and buffering against crop failures in paddy fields.[29] Youth migration to urban centers in Assam and Meghalaya for education and informal employmentâ€”such as shopkeeping or domestic workâ€”has increased, driven by government schemes and the recognition of Hajongs as a Scheduled Tribe in Assam, which offers reservations in jobs and schooling.[30] However, low literacy rates, reported at under 50% in some surveys, constrain broader participation in skilled sectors.[55]

Contemporary Issues

Resettlement and Citizenship Debates

The Hajong people, a Hindu ethnic group displaced from the Garo Hills and adjacent areas in present-day Bangladesh during the early 1960s due to flooding from the Kaptai Dam on the Karnaphuli River and religiously motivated persecution amid communal tensions in East Pakistan, sought refuge in India. The Indian government, under Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru, granted asylum and resettled approximately 2,000 Hajong familiesâ€”totaling around 12,000 individualsâ€”primarily in Arunachal Pradesh's Changlang and Papum Pare districts (then part of the North-East Frontier Agency), alongside Chakma refugees. This resettlement was intended as temporary relief but evolved into permanent habitation, sparking debates over land allocation, resource strain, and integration in a region constitutionally safeguarded for indigenous tribes under Article 371H.[56][7] Citizenship claims for these resettled Hajongs hinge on Section 5(1)(a) of the Citizenship Act, 1955, which allows registration for those who migrated before January 26, 1950, though the Supreme Court extended eligibility interpretations to post-1950 refugees domiciled in India. In a 1996 judgment on a National Human Rights Commission petition, the Court mandated protection of their fundamental rights and directed Arunachal Pradesh authorities to process citizenship applications without arbitrary denial, rejecting state claims of non-statutory obligations toward "foreigners." A 2015 Supreme Court order reinforced this, explicitly requiring the state to grant citizenship to eligible Chakma and Hajong applicants, yet implementation lagged due to administrative resistance and local agitations. Indigenous groups, including the All Arunachal Pradesh Students' Union, opposed on grounds of preserving tribal demographics and exclusive access to jobs, education, and land, viewing the influx as a threat to their Sixth Schedule autonomies amid Arunachal's sparse population of about 1.4 million.[57][57][56] In 2017, the central government offered "limited citizenship by registration" to around 1,000 Chakma-Hajong families in Arunachal, conferring voting rights and access to services but explicitly barring Scheduled Tribe status, land ownership, or inner-line permit exemptions to address local fears of cultural dilution and economic competition. This compromise faced criticism from refugee advocates as insufficient for full integration and from Arunachali nationalists as perpetuating demographic risks, leading to renewed deportation demands and violence, such as the 2017 expulsion attempts. Recent proposals, including a 2021 state census for relocation and 2024 discussions to shift communities to Assam under the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019â€”leveraging Hajongs' pre-1971 migrationâ€”have ignited interstate tensions, with Assam residents protesting potential burdens on their resources despite Hajongs' existing Scheduled Tribe recognition there since the 1950s. In Meghalaya, where Hajongs form the fourth-largest ethnic group and hold Scheduled Tribe status, integration has proceeded without comparable strife, underscoring policy inconsistencies across northeastern states.[58][57][59][9]

Cultural Assimilation and Preservation

The Hajong people have experienced varying degrees of cultural assimilation, particularly through religious syncretism and linguistic adaptation. Originally practicing animism with worship of ancestral spirits and local deities via shamans, many Hajongs have adopted Hinduism, incorporating festivals like DurgÃ¢ PÃ»jÃ¢ while retaining elements such as bastu (village deity) rituals.[30] This shift is evident in communities across Assam, Meghalaya, and Bangladesh, where traditional priests (dewsi and udhikari) coexist with Hindu practices, though inter-tribal marriages remain restricted to preserve endogamy.[30] Housing has modernized from bamboo-and-mud structures to cement and tin roofs, and certain customs, such as specific wedding rites and utensils, are diminishing amid urbanization.[30] Linguistically, while Hajong remains the mother tongue, proficiency in regional languages like Assamese, Bengali, and Hindi facilitates integration but contributes to generational shifts away from exclusive use of Hajong.[30] Preservation efforts counter these trends, bolstered by the community's Scheduled Tribe status in Indian states like Assam and Meghalaya, which provides affirmative protections for cultural identity.[9] Organizations such as the All India Hajong Welfare Association advocate for welfare and heritage
