Energizer Park


Energizer Park is a soccer-specific stadium located in the Downtown West neighborhood of St. Louis, Missouri, United States. Opened in November 2022, it has a seating capacity of 22,423 and serves as the home venue for St. Louis City SC of Major League Soccer and its affiliate St. Louis City 2.[1][2][3]
The stadium anchors a 32-acre campus that includes team headquarters, training facilities, practice fields, and a retail pavilion, forming the largest urban professional sports complex in the United Statesâ€”all developed through private investment without public funding.[2][4][3]
Originally named CityPark, it was rebranded as Energizer Park in October 2024 pursuant to a naming rights agreement with Energizer Holdings, a battery manufacturer headquartered in nearby Clayton, Missouri.[5]
Notable for its design, the venue features a pitch situated 40 feet below street level and has been recognized as one of the world's most beautiful sports venues by the Prix Versailles awards.[2][6]

History

Early Proposals and Public Funding Opposition

In late 2016, a group of local investors led by Don Garber's MLS expansion discussions proposed a soccer-specific stadium in downtown St. Louis as part of the city's bid for an MLS franchise, estimating total costs at around $200 million with public contributions sought via bonds or taxes covering approximately $60 million for infrastructure and site preparation.[7][8] The plan, formalized as Proposition 2 on the April 4, 2017, ballot, aimed to allocate revenue from a proposed business use tax increaseâ€”originally tied to broader transit fundingâ€”to the stadium project, positioning it as an economic catalyst amid competition from cities like Cincinnati and Sacramento.[9][10] Voters in St. Louis City and County rejected the measure 53 percent to 47 percent, with opposition strongest in suburban areas wary of fiscal risks.[11][12] Taxpayer advocacy groups, including fiscal conservatives, argued the subsidy constituted corporate welfare, diverting funds from pressing needs like public safety and economic blight without assured returns, as evidenced by pre-vote polls showing 61 percent of likely Democratic primary voters opposing tax dollars for the project.[9][13] Critics highlighted empirical data from economic analyses, such as a Brookings Institution review finding that professional sports facilities rarely generate net positive fiscal impacts due to limited year-round use and leakage of spending to non-local sources, reinforcing preferences for private investment over public guarantees.[14] Skepticism was amplified by St. Louis's experience with the Edward Jones Dome, where public investments exceeding $500 million in bonds and renovations from 1995 to 2010 failed to retain the NFL's Rams, leaving taxpayers obligated for $6 million annually in debt service through at least 2022 even after the team's 2016 relocation.[15][16] This precedent, coupled with broader studies indicating 80 percent of economists view stadium subsidies as outweighed by costs, underscored arguments that similar soccer proposals risked repeating patterns of unfulfilled economic promises seen in projects like Atlanta's failed stadium-anchored developments.[17][18] The rejection signaled a broader pivot toward demanding private funding models, diminishing reliance on government-backed sports infrastructure.[19]

Private Revival, Construction, and Opening

In 2020, the ownership group of St. Louis City SC, led by the Taylor and Kavanaugh families, revived plans for a soccer-specific stadium through a fully private investment exceeding $458 million, utilizing a 32-acre former industrial site in downtown St. Louis that included remnants of a demolished freeway ramp and surface parking lots.[20][1] This approach circumvented earlier public funding opposition that had derailed prior stadium proposals, enabling the project to proceed without taxpayer contributions and demonstrating the viability of self-financed urban redevelopment.[17] The development encompassed not only the stadium but also ancillary facilities on the infill site, transforming underutilized land into a sports district.[3] Construction commenced with site preparation in March 2020, managed by a consortium including Mortenson Construction, Alberici Constructors, and others, achieving a rapid timeline despite pandemic disruptions.[4][3] Key milestones included laying the first structural steel beam in December 2020 and topping out the superstructure in August 2021, with substantial completion by summer 2022.[21][22] This accelerated build-out on the challenging urban parcel highlighted efficient project management and private-sector incentives, positioning the venue for timely integration into Major League Soccer's expansion framework after St. Louis's prior bids had faltered due to fiscal disputes.[4] Energizer Park opened on March 4, 2023, hosting St. Louis City SC's inaugural MLS home match against CF MontrÃ©al before a sellout crowd of approximately 22,500 in its 22,500-seat capacity configuration.[23][20] The debut underscored immediate fan support, with rapid season ticket sellouts reflecting enthusiasm for the privately backed venture that secured the franchise.[23] This opening marked a pivotal achievement in St. Louis's soccer landscape, validating the private model's capacity to deliver infrastructure without public subsidies.[17]

Naming Rights Evolution and Rebranding

In February 2022, Centene Corporation, a Clayton-based healthcare company, agreed to a 15-year naming rights partnership for the stadium, dubbing it Centene Stadium as part of St. Louis CITY SC's founding sponsorship arrangements.[24][25] The deal aligned with Centene's regional growth investments but was rescinded in October 2022 amid shifts in the company's sponsorship priorities, leaving the stadium without a corporate name ahead of its 2023 opening.[26][27] Without a replacement sponsor, the venue opened as CITYPARK in March 2023, adopting a temporary name that emphasized civic identity and the stadium's role as a publicly accessible urban landmark in St. Louis's Downtown West neighborhood.[28] This interim branding avoided taxpayer-funded alternatives, consistent with the project's private financing model, which generated revenue through market-driven partnerships rather than subsidies common in many U.S. sports venues.[29] On October 31, 2024, St. Louis CITY SC announced a multiyear naming rights agreement with Energizer Holdings, a battery manufacturer headquartered in St. Louis since relocating its global base there in 2019, rebranding the stadium as Energizer Park effective January 1, 2025.[29][28][30] The partnership, whose financial terms were not disclosed, includes community initiatives and a new premium seating area, highlighting Energizer's longstanding local presenceâ€”dating to its Energizer Bunny marketing campaigns originating in the 1980sâ€”and the viability of corporate naming rights as a self-sustaining revenue mechanism for privately developed facilities.[31][32] This evolution underscores how targeted local sponsorships can bolster economic stability without relying on public funds, contrasting with arenas often burdened by ongoing subsidy dependencies.[33]

Architecture and Design

Stadium Structure and Capacity

Energizer Park consists of a soccer-specific, open-air bowl with 22,423 fixed seats arranged in a single-tier configuration optimized for proximity to the pitch, where every seat is within 120 feet of the field to ensure superior sightlines and immersion without upper deck overhangs or multi-sport adaptations like running tracks.[4] The design incorporates steep stands to steepen the viewing angles, enhancing the fan experience for soccer while maintaining structural integrity through a steel-frame construction typical of modern venues.[34] Architects HOK and Snow Kreilich prioritized empirical sightline geometry, positioning the seating bowl around a sunken field located 40 feet below street level to accommodate urban grading and foster a compact, enveloping atmosphere.[6][35] The playing surface is a regulation 120-by-75-yard synthetic turf field, selected for durability and performance consistency in MLS matches, surrounded by a continuous 360-degree concourse that promotes efficient circulation and equitable access from all directions without designated "back" entrances.[2] This concourse design leverages the site's 20-foot grade differential for dual-level entry, balancing functionality with the venue's urban integration near Union Station and the MetroLink light rail for pedestrian and transit accessibility.[36][37] While primarily configured for soccer with no provisions for track events or broad multi-use compromises, the stadium supports capacity expansion to approximately 25,000 via temporary safe standing or additional seating for concerts and select non-soccer events, preserving the core bowl's integrity for primary tenants.[4][3] This flexibility stems from modular steel elements and open-air adaptability, though soccer remains the dictating design criterion to avoid diluting pitch-to-spectator intimacy.[38]

Key Design Innovations and Sustainability Features

Energizer Park incorporates a 120-foot-wide cantilevered canopy roof that shelters spectators from weather while permitting natural daylight to reach the pitch and seating areas, thereby reducing reliance on artificial lighting during daytime events.[34] The canopy's design, inspired by the metallic sheen of the Gateway Arch, utilizes lightweight steel framing, trusses, and an outrigger structural system optimized via proprietary engineering software to minimize material use without obstructing sightlines.[34] This open-air configuration, supported by slender columns, amplifies crowd noise for an immersive atmosphere and enables a permeable urban integration with surrounding streets, distinguishing it from enclosed stadiums.[37] The stadium's construction employed 100% recycled structural steel, enhancing material sustainability and reducing embodied carbon compared to virgin steel alternatives.[34] Integrated LED lighting systems, combined with advanced building controls, optimize energy consumption across operations.[34] [39] The overall campus design consolidates team facilities, training pitches, and event spaces within a 25.5-acre urban district, representing the largest integrated soccer campus in Major League Soccer and allowing for adaptable future expansions through modular framing elements.[34] Sustainability efforts culminated in LEED Gold certification for operations and maintenance, awarded by the U.S. Green Building Council on April 16, 2025, marking the first such achievement for a St. Louis sports venue.[40] Key features include an underground cistern for on-site rainwater harvesting to irrigate landscaping and flush fixtures, low-flow water systems to minimize usage, and native plantings that restore pre-urban ecosystems while supporting biodiversity.[34] Zero-waste protocols, enforced during all Major League Soccer matches and extended venue-wide in 2025, prohibit single-use plastics and mandate compostable or recyclable packaging for concessions.[40] [41] The club offsets matchday energy through Green-e certified renewable energy credits, contributing to broader efficiency gains over traditional venues, though specific quantified savings data post-opening remains tied to ongoing operations monitoring.[42] No major design-related cost overruns were reported during construction, which proceeded from site preparation in March 2020 to opening in 2023 despite pandemic disruptions.[3]

Facilities and Campus

Fan and Event Amenities

Energizer Park's concourse spans the entire stadium perimeter in an open-air configuration, providing 360-degree sightlines to the field for circulating fans during matches. This design facilitates seamless movement and enhances the spectator experience by integrating viewing opportunities with concessions. The CITY Flavor initiative partners with over a dozen local vendors, offering diverse options such as Korean tacos from Seoul Taco, wood-fired pizzas from Olio, and barbecue from Saucy Porka, alongside allergen-free and kids' menus to accommodate varied dietary needs.[2][43][44] Premium seating areas, including club levels and loges, feature high ceilings, climate-controlled lounges, and upscale dining like mini beef wellingtons and Szechuan beef sliders, catering to supporters seeking enhanced comfort.[45][46] These spaces contribute to revenue diversification while maintaining focus on matchday immersion. Accessibility is prioritized through inclusive features such as ADA-designated seating, sensory rooms with calming tools, audio descriptions, ASL interpretation, and the Touch2See program, which uses tactile boards and vibrations to convey real-time game action for blind and visually impaired fans. Family zones include adult changing tables and mobility-inclusive autograph areas, while the stadium's location adjacent to MetroLink light rail stations supports transit access for approximately 22,500 attendees, including safe standing rails in dedicated supporter sections for standing-room enthusiasts.[47][48][49] The venue's multi-use layout supports non-soccer events like concerts and youth tournaments by leveraging modular seating and versatile concourse spaces, though primary enhancements target soccer attendance through vibrant supporter zones and interactive fan zones that foster community engagement without disrupting gameplay flow.[50][34]

Team Training and Operational Facilities

The Energizer Park campus encompasses a 32-acre site that integrates St. Louis City SC's operational headquarters, a dedicated training facility, and three full-size practice fields, enabling centralized professional soccer operations within an urban setting.[34][51] This configuration positions City SC as the only Major League Soccer club with all essential team facilitiesâ€”stadium, headquarters, training center, and pitchesâ€”consolidated in a single downtown location, minimizing logistical disruptions and travel time compared to teams reliant on dispersed suburban sites.[34][51] The 30,000-square-foot elite training center includes specialized zones for physical conditioning, such as a weight and cardio room, alongside hydrotherapy pools for player recovery and injury rehabilitation.[3][4] Operational efficiency is enhanced by technological integrations, including a high-end film room equipped for video analysis of matches and training sessions, which supports data-driven performance optimization through detailed tactical breakdowns and biomechanical reviews.[51] An underground tunnel connects the training facility directly to the stadium, streamlining player transitions during game days and reducing exposure to external variables like weather or traffic.[38] These facilities extend scalability to the club's academy and youth development programs, with the three adjacent practice fields accommodating multi-level training sessions and fostering a structured talent pipeline from junior levels to the first team.[1] The centralized design facilitates seamless integration of scouting, coaching, and medical staff, promoting consistent monitoring of player progress and long-term athlete development without the fragmentation common in other MLS setups.[34][51]

Association with St. Louis City SC

Role in Team's MLS Entry and Performance

The commitment to privately finance and construct Energizer Park (then known as CITYPARK) was instrumental in securing Major League Soccer's approval for St. Louis City SC's expansion franchise on August 26, 2020, following the city's failed 2017 bid thwarted by opposition to public subsidies.[52] Ownership, led by the Taylor family, pledged full private funding for the $458 million stadium without taxpayer support, distinguishing the project from subsidy-dependent rivals like Sacramento and San Diego whose bids collapsed.[52] This soccer-specific venue, opening on March 4, 2023, fulfilled MLS requirements for a dedicated home, enabling the team's timely debut and positioning St. Louis as a model for subsidy-free growth in the league. In St. Louis City SC's inaugural 2023 season, the stadium's intimate design and fervent supporter culture contributed to a dominant Western Conference regular-season title, with the team amassing 56 points from a 17-5-12 record, including an 11-2-4 home mark that yielded 35 points.[53] The club achieved the longest winning streak to start an expansion season in MLS history (four games) and tied the record for most home wins by a newcomer (11), bolstered by consistent sellouts averaging 22,423 fans per matchâ€”ninth-highest in the league despite a 22,500 capacity.[54][55] The electric atmosphere, driven by organized supporter groups, has been cited by club executives as enhancing player performance and aiding recruitment of talents like JoÃ£o Klauss and Roman BÃ¼rki, who thrived in the high-pressure environment.[56] Following a sharp decline to 32 points and near-bottom finish in 2024 (8-8-18 overall, 4-6-7 at home), the team showed recovery signals in 2025, climbing power rankings amid coaching changes and roster adjustments, with home form stabilizing around mid-table contention.[57][58] Energizer Park's role underscores a causal link between venue-driven fan engagement and sustained competitiveness, exemplifying how private investment can foster organic growth without distorting markets via public incentives, in contrast to expansion peers reliant on fiscal distortions.[52]

Integration with Broader Soccer Ecosystem

Energizer Park hosts matches for St. Louis CITY2, the MLS Next Pro affiliate of St. Louis City SC, including the 2025 Western Conference semifinal against Minnesota United FC 2 on October 25, where CITY2 secured a 3-0 victory.[59] [60] The venue also accommodates U.S. Open Cup fixtures, such as the Round of 32 match in March 2025, drawing participants from various levels of American soccer. These events extend the stadium's utility beyond Major League Soccer, fostering connections with lower-tier professional and amateur competitions. The facility supports youth development through St. Louis City SC's academy program, which fields U-14 to U-18 teams focused on technical and competitive preparation, with players occasionally integrating into CITY2 matches at the park.[61] Local interactions include exhibition games against regional clubs like St. Louis Scott Gallagher, a prominent youth organization, as seen in a 2021 doubleheader where academy squads split results.[62] While no formal feeder agreement exists, these engagements contribute to a collaborative regional talent network, with St. Louis Scott Gallagher providing pathways from recreational to elite levels independently.[63] Community initiatives anchor the stadium's role in grassroots soccer, including the CITY Futures program launched in 2022, which offers free Kickstart clinics for children ages 5-12 to build foundational skills.[64] Additional outreach encompasses Soccer 101 classes, coaching clinics, and the Play On programâ€”a free 10-week curriculum emphasizing technical, social, and emotional growth for underserved youth.[65] Partnerships, such as with the YMCA, deliver affordable programming for Pre-K to 6th graders across multiple locations, enhancing accessibility since the stadium's 2023 opening.[66] These efforts have broadened participation in St. Louis, a region with historically high youth soccer rates, by integrating stadium-hosted demonstrations and school outreach.[67] By accommodating events like the 2025 Big Ten Women's Soccer Tournament semifinals and final, Energizer Park positions St. Louis as a versatile hub for collegiate and professional soccer, complementing its MLS anchor.[68] This multi-tier usage strengthens the local ecosystem, promoting sustained engagement without relying solely on the top-flight team.

Economic and Community Impact

Site Revitalization and Urban Infill

The site of Energizer Park, prior to development, comprised a vacant post-industrial lot encompassing remnants of a demolished freeway off-ramp and surface parking areas, stemming from 1950s urban renewal projects that displaced the Mill Creek Valley neighborhood and left the Downtown West district underutilized for decades.[34] Construction of the 22,423-seat stadium, completed in 2022 and opened in March 2023, represented a deliberate urban infill strategy on this 32-acre parcel, converting blighted infrastructure into a cohesive sports campus that anchors pedestrian-oriented public spaces and integrates with adjacent greenways.[34][1] Post-opening, the project has catalyzed adjacent redevelopment, including the conversion of the Historic Butler Brothers warehouse into the 200-unit Victor apartments in 2023 and the $232 million Sister Cities residential towers directly across from the stadium, announced in 2025, filling long-vacant lots with mixed-use housing proximate to the site.[76][77][23] This infill approach has bolstered urban density by consolidating underused land into high-activation zones, with over 300 new occupancy permits issued in the district since 2019, while enhancing walkability through proximity to a Metrolink light rail station (two blocks away) and seamless connections via open concourses to Union Station and the Gateway Mall, reducing reliance on vehicular access in a core downtown setting.[34][34] Although broader St. Louis revitalizations, such as in the nearby West End, have sparked localized gentrification debates over rising costs and displacement, the Energizer Park site's prior desolation on non-residential freeway remnants has limited such effects here, with empirical trends showing a 4% citywide drop in vacant properties since 2018 amid infill-focused zoning that prioritizes community plazas and transit-linked job proximity over exclusionary upscale builds.[78][79][34]

Job Creation, Revenue, and Broader Effects

The development and operations of Energizer Park have generated measurable economic activity primarily through private investment, avoiding the public subsidies common in many U.S. stadium projects. Construction of the 22,500-seat venue and its 32-acre campus, completed without direct taxpayer funding, produced an estimated $1.4 billion in regional economic impact from 2020 to 2023.[56] This figure, derived from a team-commissioned analysis, encompasses spending on materials, labor, and related activities, though such studies often rely on input-output multipliers that economists critique for overstating net benefits by not fully accounting for displaced local spending.[17] In the stadium's 2023 inaugural season, St. Louis City SC operations yielded $168 million in local economic output, including contributions from ticket sales, concessions, and merchandise, with 18% of attendees originating from beyond the St. Louis metropolitan area.[38] Revenue streams have included a $200 million MLS expansion fee paid entirely by private ownership in 2019, recouped through team valuation growth and operations.[80] The 2024 naming rights agreement with Energizer Holdings, effective January 1, 2025, adds undisclosed annual payments estimated in the $3â€“10 million range typical for MLS venues, alongside concessions managed exclusively by local vendors.[30] [81] Tax revenues from these activities totaled nearly $24 million in the first year, with $5.1 million directed to the City of St. Louis via sales taxes on tickets, food, and parking; cumulative incremental taxes reached $122 million by mid-2024, including $33 million to local and state governments.[56] Unlike subsidized facilities, where a 2017 survey of economists found costs exceeding benefits in 80% of cases due to opportunity costs and limited spillovers, Energizer Park's private financing model has delivered positive fiscal returns without straining public budgets.[17] Pre-opening projections anticipated 355 annual construction jobs over four years, supporting indirect employment in supply chains, though post-opening permanent roles center on team staff, event operations, and vendor partnerships rather than large-scale direct hiring.[82]

Controversies and Criticisms

Funding and Development Debates

In 2017, proponents of public funding for a St. Louis MLS stadium argued that taxpayer contributions would ensure equitable risk-sharing between the city and private investors, potentially leveraging public resources to catalyze broader economic benefits while mitigating the full financial burden on team owners.[83] However, these efforts faltered when voters rejected Proposition 2 by a margin of 53% to 47%, declining to allocate approximately $60 million in public funds via an increased use tax, reflecting widespread taxpayer resistance to subsidizing professional sports facilities amid prior experiences like the Rams' departure after heavy public investments in the Edward Jones Dome.[9] [84] Following the vote's defeat, St. Louis City SC shifted to a fully private funding model for Energizer Park (originally CityPark), constructing the $457.8 million facility without imposing debt on local taxpayers, which enabled a faster timelineâ€”breaking ground in 2020 and opening in 2023â€”compared to protracted public subsidy negotiations.[52] This approach contrasted sharply with the Rams era, where St. Louis incurred over $500 million in public costs for stadium upgrades and related infrastructure from 1995 to 2016, only for the team to relocate to Los Angeles without repaying bonds or delivering sustained economic returns.[52] In contrast, City SC's privately financed project has demonstrated viability, generating $160 million in regional economic activity during its 2023 inaugural season through ticket sales, concessions, and ancillary spending, underscoring profitability without relying on ongoing public subsidies.[56] Critics of the private model have highlighted potential developer incentives, such as $5.9 million in Missouri state tax credits approved in 2020 for construction, arguing these represent indirect public costs and opportunity forgone for alternative urban investments like infrastructure or social services.[85] Nonetheless, conservative commentators have praised the outcome as a market-driven success, avoiding the fiscal pitfalls of government-backed projects and demonstrating that private capital can deliver infrastructure without taxpayer bailouts, as evidenced by the stadium's completion ahead of schedule and under budget relative to public alternatives.[52] [86]

Incidents Involving Political Expression

In July 2025, during a St. Louis City SC match against Orlando City SC on July 26, fan Michael Weitzel was ejected from Energizer Park after refusing to remove his "Make America Great Again" (MAGA) hat, which security personnel deemed a violation of the venue's policy prohibiting political apparel or banners that could disrupt the event.[87][88] Weitzel, a self-described diehard soccer supporter, recorded the interaction, in which security informed him that the hat fell under restrictions on political items and threatened arrest if he did not comply or leave, leading to his escorted removal with approximately 25 minutes remaining in the game.[89][90] Energizer Park officials cited the stadium's code of conduct, which explicitly bans political banners and items perceived to incite disruption in a venue accommodating over 22,000 spectators, though the policy's application to individual apparel like hats remains ambiguously defined in public statements.[91][92] Weitzel alleged viewpoint discrimination targeting support for former President Donald Trump, arguing the ejection suppressed his political expression without evidence of actual disruption, a claim echoed by free speech advocates who contrasted it with allowances for other merchandise.[93][94] The incident drew comparisons to neutral attire enforcement at other sports venues, where private property rights permit operators to prioritize event safety and fan harmony over unrestricted expression, particularly amid large crowds where political displays could escalate tensions.[95] No lawsuits were filed, and St. Louis City SC maintained that the action aligned with maintaining a focused soccer environment, while critics highlighted potential inconsistencies in policy application absent prior warnings or uniform enforcement across ideologies.[96] No additional verified incidents of political expression controversies, such as protest disruptions, have been reported at the venue, underscoring security protocols designed to balance attendee rights with operational needs for events drawing 20,000-plus fans.[87]
