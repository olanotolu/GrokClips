Hristijan Mickoski


Hristijan Mickoski (Macedonian: Христијан Мицкоски; born 29 September 1977) is a North Macedonian politician, engineer, and professor serving as Prime Minister of the Republic of North Macedonia since 23 June 2024.[1][2] He leads the conservative VMRO-DPMNE party, which he has headed since late 2017, guiding it to a parliamentary election victory in May 2024 that ended seven years of opposition following governance marred by corruption scandals under the prior Social Democratic Union administration.[3][4] Mickoski, who earned a PhD in mechatronics and robotics from Ss. Cyril and Methodius University in Skopje, previously worked as a university professor and held executive roles in the energy sector, including as director of state-owned Power Plants of North Macedonia from 2016 to 2017 and energy advisor to prior VMRO-DPMNE-led governments.[1][5] His premiership has prioritized combating entrenched corruption, advancing judicial reforms, and reforming energy policy amid economic stagnation, while pursuing European Union accession with insistence on protecting Macedonian national identity against perceived encroachments from neighbors Bulgaria and Greece.[6][7] Notable controversies include his assertions of surveillance by security services during opposition tenure and diplomatic frictions, such as referring to his country as "Macedonia" at the 2024 NATO Summit, challenging the 2018 Prespa Agreement's naming compromise.[8][9]

Early life and education

Upbringing and academic background

Hristijan Mickoski was born on 29 September 1977 in Skopje, then part of the Socialist Republic of Macedonia within the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia, into an ethnic Macedonian family.[1] He completed his primary and secondary education in Skopje, laying the foundation for his subsequent academic pursuits in the city.[10] Mickoski enrolled at Ss. Cyril and Methodius University in Skopje, where he earned a diploma in mechanical engineering from the Faculty of Mechanical Engineering between 1996 and 2001.[11] He advanced his studies with a master's degree in technical sciences, specializing in mechanical engineering, completed from 2001 to 2003 at the same institution.[11] [1] Subsequently, Mickoski obtained a doctorate in technical sciences, focusing on mechanical engineering and mechatronics, between 2005 and 2009.[12] [1] This rigorous academic progression equipped him with expertise in engineering principles, analytical problem-solving, and technical innovation, as evidenced by his later role as an associate professor at the Faculty of Mechanical Engineering.[12][13]

Professional career

Engineering, academia, and public sector roles

Mickoski earned a doctorate in technical sciences with a focus on mechanical engineering, including a dissertation on the management of manipulation systems.[14] He began his academic career at the Faculty of Mechanical Engineering in Skopje, initially as an assistant and part-time professor before advancing to associate professor from 2014 to 2020.[1] [15] Since 2020, he has held the position of full professor there, specializing in mechanics and mechatronics, with contributions to at least 12 peer-reviewed publications in related fields.[13] [16] In public sector roles, Mickoski served as energy advisor to Prime Ministers Nikola Gruevski and Emil Dimitriev from 2015 to 2017, providing technical input on national energy policy and infrastructure.[17] In February 2016, he was appointed director general of Elektrani na Makedonija (ELEM), the state-owned enterprise overseeing thermal, hydro, and lignite power plants that supply over 90% of North Macedonia's electricity generation capacity.[17] [18] His leadership emphasized operational management of assets including the REK Bitola lignite complex and major hydropower facilities, though specific quantitative improvements in efficiency remain undocumented in independent audits available during that period.[19]

Political career

Entry into politics and rise in VMRO-DPMNE

Hristijan Mickoski transitioned from public sector management to active involvement in VMRO-DPMNE during the 2016–2017 political crisis, which stemmed from allegations of widespread corruption, illegal surveillance, and authoritarian practices under the party's long-time leader Nikola Gruevski.[20] Appointed as the party's secretary-general earlier in 2017, Mickoski—a civil engineer and former director of the state-owned power utility Elektrani na Makedonija since 2016—emerged as a figure untainted by the scandals that had eroded VMRO-DPMNE's support, leading to its narrow defeat in the December 11, 2016, parliamentary elections (407,681 votes for VMRO-DPMNE versus 436,858 for SDSM) and subsequent inability to retain power.[21][1] In this initial role, Mickoski focused on internal party stabilization amid Gruevski's resignation as leader on December 23, 2017, positioning himself through his technocratic profile—rooted in academic and managerial experience—to attract reform-oriented conservatives disillusioned by the crisis but committed to the party's Macedonian nationalist principles.[22] His ascent reflected VMRO-DPMNE's need to project competence and renewal after years of governance marked by empirical evidence of state capture, including the 2015 wiretapping scandal exposing over 20,000 intercepted communications by government-linked entities.[20] Mickoski's early engagements emphasized scrutiny of the incoming SDSM government's vulnerabilities, such as delays in judicial prosecutions and emerging procurement irregularities, fostering an anti-corruption narrative grounded in demands for transparent accountability rather than partisan amnesty.[22] This approach, while critiqued by some outlets aligned with the prior regime's opponents as insufficient distancing from Gruevski's legacy, appealed to party bases seeking evidence-based opposition to perceived SDSM overreach in consolidating power with ethnic Albanian DUI allies by May 2017.[21]

Party leadership (2017–2024)

Hristijan Mickoski was elected leader of VMRO-DPMNE on December 23, 2017, at the party's 16th congress, receiving 376 votes from delegates and succeeding Nikola Gruevski, who had fled the country amid corruption allegations.[22][23] As a relative political novice previously serving as the party's secretary-general and in Gruevski's government, Mickoski pledged to unify the fractured party and restore its credibility following electoral defeats and scandals that had weakened VMRO-DPMNE's position.[21] Under Mickoski's leadership, VMRO-DPMNE focused on rebuilding by distancing itself from Gruevski-era controversies, emphasizing internal reforms and positioning the party as a defender of national interests against the ruling SDSM government's policies.[24] The party criticized SDSM for economic stagnation, with GDP growth lagging behind regional averages and unemployment remaining high at around 15-16% during much of the period, attributing these to mismanagement and unfulfilled promises.[22] Mickoski's strategy involved leveraging public discontent over corruption scandals within SDSM-led institutions, including allegations of judicial interference and cronyism, to regain voter trust ahead of future contests.[24] A core element of Mickoski's opposition platform was vehement rejection of the 2018 Prespa Agreement with Greece, which VMRO-DPMNE labeled as an "insulting" capitulation that compromised Macedonian identity by mandating the name change to North Macedonia and redefining the national language's historical ties.[25] Party rhetoric under Mickoski highlighted the deal's concessions—such as erga omnes application of the new name and restrictions on ancient heritage claims—as detrimental to sovereignty, fueling protests and sustaining nationalist mobilization without endorsing Gruevski's authoritarian tactics.[25] In preparation for the 2024 parliamentary elections, Mickoski orchestrated broader coalitions, including alliances with Albanian-majority parties like the VLEN bloc, to expand VMRO-DPMNE's base while prioritizing preservation of Macedonian ethnic identity against perceived dilutions in EU accession talks.[26] This approach, blending cross-ethnic outreach with firm stances on cultural preservation, contributed to the party's resurgence, culminating in a strong electoral performance that positioned it to challenge SDSM's seven-year rule.[26]

2024 parliamentary election

Campaign, results, and opposition to prior government

Mickoski's campaign for the 2024 parliamentary elections emphasized combating entrenched corruption, revitalizing the economy through structural reforms, and addressing the stagnation in EU accession talks without concessions that undermined national sovereignty. He positioned VMRO-DPMNE as the antidote to the ruling Social Democratic Union of Macedonia (SDSM)'s governance, which he argued had perpetuated systemic graft and economic malaise since taking power in 2017. Central to the platform was a pledge to dismantle clientelist networks in public institutions and prioritize fiscal discipline to counter rising living costs, drawing on public frustration over unfulfilled promises of prosperity following the 2018 Prespa Agreement with Greece.[27][28] The elections, held on May 8, 2024, resulted in a decisive victory for VMRO-DPMNE, which captured 43.9% of the vote and 58 seats in the 120-seat Assembly, marking the opposition's strongest performance since 2008 and reflecting widespread discontent with SDSM's 18.2% share and 42 seats. Voter turnout was 55.5%, with the outcome underscoring empirical failures of the prior coalition: the at-risk-of-poverty rate stood at 22.2% in 2023, up from pre-2017 levels amid stagnant wages and inflation. This landslide enabled VMRO-DPMNE, in coalition with allies like VLEN, to secure a parliamentary majority, paving the way for the government's transition in June 2024.[29] Mickoski's rhetoric targeted SDSM's judicial capture and stalled reforms, citing EU reports that highlighted persistent politicization of the courts and failure to implement vetting mechanisms despite repeated commitments since 2017. Corruption perceptions worsened under SDSM, with high-level scandals eroding institutional trust, as evidenced by a decline in governance indicators from international assessments. Economic critiques focused on stalled growth—averaging under 2% annually post-COVID—and unaddressed informal sector dominance at around 40% of GDP, which Mickoski attributed to lax enforcement and patronage. These verifiable shortcomings, rather than abstract ideology, drove the electorate's rejection of the incumbent, with exit polls linking the shift to demands for accountability over continuity.[30][31][32]

Premiership (2024–present)

Government formation and initial priorities

Following the May 2024 parliamentary elections, Hristijan Mickoski, as leader of VMRO-DPMNE, negotiated a coalition with parties including VLEN and ZNAM, securing a parliamentary majority excluding the former ruling Albanian partner DUI.[4][33] On June 23, 2024, the Assembly approved the 28-member cabinet with 77 votes in favor and 22 against, comprising 24 ministers and five deputy prime ministers drawn primarily from Mickoski's close political associates and professional backgrounds in engineering, academia, and public administration.[34][4] Mickoski and the ministers were sworn in that evening, affirming the constitutional name of the Republic of North Macedonia during the oath.[35] The new government's stated initial priorities focused on restoring institutional trust through judicial reforms, enforcing the rule of law, bolstering energy security amid regional dependencies, and advancing anti-corruption prosecutions utilizing pre-existing evidentiary bases from prior investigations.[36][6] Mickoski emphasized practical measures over expedited overhauls, pledging to align reforms with EU recommendations while addressing systemic judicial contamination by a limited number of actors.[37] To signal urgency, the administration introduced a 100-day framework for elevating governance standards, including preliminary steps toward fiscal adjustments and institutional accountability, without endorsing unsubstantiated claims of immediate comprehensive renewal.[38][39] This approach aimed to build on campaign commitments to combat entrenched corruption and inefficiency inherited from the previous coalition.[36]

Domestic policies and anti-corruption efforts

Upon assuming the premiership in June 2024, Hristijan Mickoski's government identified the eradication of corruption inherited from the prior Social Democratic Union of Macedonia (SDSM)-led administration as a core priority, emphasizing investigations into high-level misconduct and institutional depoliticization.[6] The administration committed to combating organized crime and graft through transparent mechanisms, with Mickoski stating on May 7, 2025, that such efforts require "commitment and clean hands" to restore public trust.[40] Initial actions included scrutiny of scandals implicating figures from opposition coalitions like VLEN, which encompasses SDSM allies, amid a reported wave of revelations in mid-2025.[41] Anti-corruption measures targeted legacy issues, such as the reactivation of probes into SDSM-era procurement irregularities and public sector favoritism, though quantifiable prosecutions remained limited by late 2025. The Basic Public Prosecutor's Office for Organized Crime and Corruption initiated an investigative procedure in April 2025 against implicated officials, building on pre-existing cases but accelerated under the new administration's oversight.[42] Mickoski's cabinet pursued vetting of judicial and prosecutorial personnel to dismantle perceived protections for former regime actors, with the Prime Minister accusing the judiciary on October 8, 2025, of serving as the "last bastions" shielding SDSM interests.[43] A notable step involved Parliament's passage of a no-confidence motion on July 29, 2025, against Chief Public Prosecutor Ljubomir Joveski, whom Mickoski criticized for enabling selective justice and reputational damage to institutions.[44] Judicial reforms constituted a parallel track, aimed at countering politicization through structural changes, including proposed dissolutions of the Judicial Council and Council of Public Prosecutors—pre-election pledges reiterated in early 2025.[45] By September 24, 2025, the government advanced six laws reforming the Judicial Council, alongside budget increases to elevate judges' and prosecutors' salaries, framing these as incentives for independence and efficiency.[46] Further proposals, announced October 17, 2025, aligned with EU peer review recommendations, targeting "contaminating" influences within the system while committing to transparent implementation.[47] Progress assessments by mid-2025 revealed mixed outcomes, with the European Commission's 2025 Rule of Law Report acknowledging continued judicial reform implementation but highlighting "serious concerns" over interference from executive and legislative branches.[48] Independent evaluations, such as Balkan Insight's June 24, 2025, first-year review, graded the government's anti-corruption drive as requiring substantial improvement, citing delays in high-profile indictments despite rhetorical emphasis.[49] Opposition figures from SDSM, including leader Venko Filipče, contended by September 30, 2025, that no substantive reform laws had been enacted, attributing stagnation to administrative incapacity.[50] These critiques underscore empirical shortfalls in conviction rates and institutional autonomy metrics, even as the administration maintained that entrenched resistance from prior networks impeded faster gains.[51]

Economic reforms and fiscal measures

Upon assuming office in June 2024, Prime Minister Hristijan Mickoski prioritized fiscal measures to stimulate economic growth, including multiple pension increases aimed at supporting retirees amid inflation pressures. The government implemented a linear pension hike effective September 2024, followed by another in March 2025 amounting to approximately 2,500 Macedonian denars (around €40) per pensioner, with further adjustments announced for October 2025 and spring 2026 to address demographic challenges and rising living costs.[52][53][54] Mickoski's administration also pursued tax reductions to attract investment and retain talent, building on North Macedonia's existing 10% flat corporate and personal income tax rate. Key initiatives included proposals for a reduced profit tax rate, potentially as low as 5% on reinvested profits, announced during the 2024 campaign and reiterated post-election to encourage business repatriation of young professionals.[55][56] Additional incentives targeted global firms with lower effective rates, alongside plans to cut tariffs on specific imports like U.S. goods to foster trade exemptions and economic ties.[57][58] Efforts to deregulate business operations focused on improving the investment climate through streamlined permitting and reduced bureaucratic hurdles, though specific legislative changes remained limited in the first year. The government emphasized public-private partnerships and capital project reallocations in budget revisions, prioritizing salaries and pensions over expansive deregulation amid fiscal constraints.[59] In energy policy, leveraging Mickoski's prior experience as director of the state-owned power utility ELEM, the administration established a dedicated Ministry of Energy, Mining, and Mineral Resources in June 2024 to enhance self-sufficiency. Priorities included advancing gas interconnectors, completing the Greek-Macedonian gas pipeline, and enacting a new Energy Law in May 2025 aligned with EU standards to facilitate renewables and lower prices via universal suppliers.[60][61] These measures aimed to reduce import dependence, though implementation faced delays from global energy volatility. Assessments of the first year's impacts highlighted modest growth, with the economy described as "consolidated" by Mickoski in May 2025, targeting a budget deficit below 3% of GDP and public debt under 60% per fiscal rules. However, the general government deficit rose to 4.9% of GDP in 2024, signaling slower consolidation, while critics from economic think tanks questioned budget revision assumptions, including a projected 12.6% revenue increase, amid reallocations for elections and investments that elevated debt levels.[62][63][64] The World Bank urged enhanced fiscal discipline to manage rising debt, noting that while pension and salary boosts provided short-term welfare relief, broader structural reforms were needed to counter global challenges like inflation and underperforming capital projects.[65][49]

Foreign policy, EU accession, and regional relations

Upon assuming office in June 2024, Prime Minister Hristijan Mickoski pledged continued pursuit of North Macedonia's EU membership while rejecting concessions that he deems detrimental to national sovereignty and identity.[66] His administration has critiqued the EU enlargement process for uneven application of standards, particularly in light of Bulgaria's ongoing veto, which has prevented opening accession negotiation chapters since 2020.[67] Mickoski has described EU demands as "humiliating," arguing they prioritize bilateral disputes over merit-based progress and enable veto-wielding members to impose historical reinterpretations.[67][68] The core impasse with Bulgaria stems from Sofia's insistence on constitutional amendments to recognize a Bulgarian minority and affirm Bulgarian roots in Macedonian history and language, demands codified in Bulgaria's French Proposal of 2022 and reinforced by a unanimous parliamentary resolution in May 2025.[69][70] Mickoski has conditioned any such reforms on Bulgaria first lifting its veto and withdrawing contentious June 2025 amendments, proposing instead a renegotiation of the EU accession framework to address these "ultimatums."[71][72] This position reflects a causal view that Bulgaria's veto perpetuates a cycle of concessions without reciprocity, stalling reforms due to eroded public trust rather than domestic shortcomings alone.[73] Relations with Greece center on uneven implementation of the 2018 Prespa Agreement, which resolved the naming dispute by establishing "Republic of North Macedonia" as the erga omnes name. Mickoski maintains that the treaty's text—specifically Articles 7(2) and 7(3)—does not bar domestic or informal use of "Macedonia" or "Macedonian" for the people, language, or institutions, countering Greek accusations of non-compliance.[74] Greece has expressed dissatisfaction with perceived backsliding, demanding strict adherence to mitigate irredentist risks, yet Mickoski's government prioritizes sovereignty preservation over full linguistic uniformity, viewing rigid enforcement as an ongoing erosion of identity post-agreement.[75][76] Broader regional ties involve sustained friction with Bulgaria amid stalled EU talks, contrasted by stable bilateral engagement with Serbia, where foreign policy coordination has emphasized economic cooperation without acute disputes from 2024 to 2025.[77] VMRO-DPMNE's dominant performance in the October 19, 2025, local elections—winning 53 of 81 municipalities in the first round—has fortified Mickoski's negotiating stance, signaling robust domestic mandate to resist external pressures on identity issues.[78][79] This electoral leverage counters opposition narratives of isolation, enabling a recalibration of EU and neighbor relations toward reciprocity.[80]

Ideology and political positions

Nationalism, identity, and cultural preservation

Hristijan Mickoski has consistently advocated for the preservation of a distinct ethnic Macedonian identity, rooted in historical continuity and self-determination, while rejecting external narratives that portray Macedonians as an "artificial nation." In response to Bulgarian assertions denying the uniqueness of Macedonian language and history, Mickoski stated on July 9, 2025, that Bulgaria's objective is "the destruction of the Macedonian nation and identity," emphasizing empirical distinctions in linguistic and cultural development over shared regional heritage.[81] He has opposed concessions that could imply "Bulgarianization," such as constitutional amendments recognizing a Bulgarian minority without reciprocal acknowledgment of Macedonian history by Sofia, arguing these undermine national sovereignty rather than foster integration.[82] Mickoski's defense of cultural heritage extends to international forums, where he prioritizes national dignity against perceived erasures. During his February 21, 2025, address at the Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC) in Washington, D.C., he highlighted double standards in European institutions, vowing to raise Macedonia's voice "with dignity" against efforts to dilute identity for accession incentives.[83] Similarly, on July 3, 2025, he condemned the European Parliament's omission of Macedonian language and identity from a report as "Taliban-like" erasure, attributing such actions to biased pressures from member states like Bulgaria that prioritize historical revisionism over factual ethnic differentiation.[84] Supporters view these stances as essential for maintaining ethnic unity and demographic vitality, with Mickoski on September 30, 2025, describing the formation of large families as "the most patriotic act" for sustaining the homeland's cultural fabric amid low birth rates.[85] Critics, often aligned with EU integration advocates or pro-compromise factions, argue Mickoski's intransigence obstructs progress by framing identity disputes as zero-sum, potentially isolating North Macedonia from Western alliances. Mickoski counters that yielding on core identity—such as language or historical narratives—invites perpetual concessions without guarantees, as evidenced by unfulfilled EU promises post-2018 name agreement with Greece.[86] On October 23, 2025, he reiterated, "We should never give in when it comes to our identity," positioning preservation as a prerequisite for authentic national cohesion rather than a barrier to regional cooperation.[87]

Views on governance, rule of law, and conservatism

Hristijan Mickoski has articulated a governance philosophy rooted in the center-right traditions of VMRO-DPMNE, advocating for limited state intervention focused on enforcing accountability rather than expansive progressive policies. He prioritizes a "system reset" to restore institutional integrity, emphasizing that the state's primary function is to establish clear rules, combat corruption, and ensure prosperity through principled administration. Upon assuming power in 2024, Mickoski identified inherited systemic issues, including clientelism and selective justice, as barriers to effective governance, pledging zero tolerance for abuses that undermine public trust.[88][89][90] Central to Mickoski's views on the rule of law is the conviction that impartial institutions are foundational to national progress, critiquing prior administrations for entrenching political inviolability and favoritism that stifled economic growth. In October 2025, following VMRO-DPMNE's strong performance in local elections—securing victories in key municipalities—he announced plans for an "impartial and unwavering" judiciary, where judges rule by law rather than influence, as a means to eradicate corruption viewed as a "tax on the poor." This approach draws on first-principles accountability, linking rule-of-law reforms to tangible outcomes like reduced poverty and revived economic activity, validated by endorsements from bodies such as GRECO, which noted North Macedonia's departure from eras of systemic corruption under his leadership.[90][91][88] Mickoski's conservatism integrates VMRO-DPMNE's historical emphasis on family values and Orthodox Christian heritage as stabilizers of social order, opposing unchecked state expansions that erode traditional structures. He has invoked spiritual foundations—such as love for family, sacrifice, and humanity—in public addresses, positioning the Macedonian Orthodox Church–Ohrid Archbishopric as a moral guide for humane society-building. This stance aligns with his participation in conservative forums like the 2025 Conservative Political Action Conference, where he underscored loyalty to principles over concessions, while critics from left-leaning outlets have labeled such rhetoric populist despite electoral successes affirming public support for restrained, value-driven governance.[92][93][94]

Controversies and criticisms

Disputes over national name and historical narratives

Upon assuming office in June 2024, Prime Minister Hristijan Mickoski described the "North" prefix in North Macedonia's constitutional name as "shameful," arguing it imposed an unnecessary qualifier on the country's identity during his inaugural address to parliament.[95] This stance echoed long-standing nationalist sentiments viewing the 2018 Prespa Agreement—under which the former Republic of Macedonia adopted "North Macedonia" to resolve Greece's objections over exclusive claims to the "Macedonia" name—as a capitulation that diluted national sovereignty without reciprocal gains in regional stability or EU progress.[96] Mickoski's repeated use of "Macedonia" in official speeches, including references to the "unresolved Macedonian Question," prompted immediate backlash from Athens, which accused Skopje of violating Articles 7 and 8 of the Prespa Agreement requiring erga omnes use of the full name in international contexts.[97] Parallel disputes with Bulgaria center on historical narratives, where Sofia insists that Macedonian national identity, language, and key figures from the 19th- and 20th-century independence movements—such as those in the Internal Macedonian Revolutionary Organization—were inherently Bulgarian, demanding revisions to textbooks and official historiography as preconditions for unblocking North Macedonia's EU accession talks.[98] Mickoski has rejected these as an assimilationist agenda aimed at fabricating a derivative identity, emphasizing empirical evidence of distinct Macedonian ethnogenesis post-Ottoman era and during Yugoslav state-building, while critiquing prior governments for concessions that eroded cultural autonomy without advancing integration.[99] Bulgaria's 2020-2022 vetoes, rooted in the French non-papers and bilateral protocols asserting shared heritage, have been framed by Mickoski as non-reciprocal, with Sofia refusing to recognize a Macedonian minority in its constitution despite census data showing over 1,200 self-identified Macedonians in Bulgaria as of 2021.[100] Mickoski's government has conditioned any constitutional amendments—such as incorporating a Bulgarian minority clause under Article 7—to Bulgaria's verifiable reciprocity, including mutual minority protections and cessation of veto threats, arguing that unilateral changes would entrench inequality in EU enlargement dynamics.[101] This position, reiterated in October 2025, prioritizes causal preservation of national cohesion over expedited accession, citing stalled negotiations since 2020 that have yielded no tangible EU benefits despite compliance efforts by the prior Zaev administration, which saw GDP growth lag at 1.5% annually pre-2024 amid unfulfilled promises.[102] Domestically, polls indicate majority support (around 60% per 2024 VMRO-DPMNE surveys) for resisting identity dilutions, viewing them as legitimate defenses against historical revisionism that could undermine state legitimacy, though EU officials and Bulgarian representatives decry it as self-sabotage exacerbating economic isolation, with foreign direct investment inflows dropping 15% in 2024 amid the impasse.[67]

Relations with predecessors and allegations of continuity

Hristijan Mickoski succeeded Nikola Gruevski as leader of VMRO-DPMNE on December 23, 2017, following Gruevski's resignation amid corruption scandals and his subsequent flight to Hungary.[22] As Gruevski's former economic adviser, Mickoski had received approximately 2 million denars in compensation from public funds during that period, a detail highlighted by critics to underscore his ties to the prior administration.[103] Initially, Mickoski defended Gruevski against what he termed political persecution, as in his 2018 statement following Gruevski's failed appeal in a graft case.[104] However, by late 2023, Mickoski publicly accused Gruevski of colluding with the ruling SDSM-DUI coalition to sow discord within VMRO-DPMNE and facilitate constitutional changes, marking a deliberate break from the ex-leader's influence.[105][106] Allegations of continuity with the Gruevski era primarily emanate from the opposition Social Democratic Union of Macedonia (SDSM), which portrays Mickoski's governance as reviving authoritarian practices, including institutional capture and surveillance reminiscent of pre-2017 VMRO-DPMNE rule.[8] For instance, SDSM has cited Mickoski's push for "radical reforms" in the Judicial Council—aimed at replacing members deemed loyal to the prior government—as an attempt to politicize the judiciary, echoing Gruevski's alleged interference that led to widespread corruption convictions.[107][108] A September 2025 wiretapping scandal, where Mickoski alleged security service abuses against his party during opposition years, drew parallels to Gruevski-era scandals that eroded public trust.[109] Mickoski has rejected these claims, emphasizing VMRO-DPMNE's electoral mandate from the May 2024 parliamentary vote, where the party secured 58 seats amid a 41% turnout, as validation against authoritarianism accusations.[29] He positions his administration's judicial initiatives, such as budget increases for prosecutors and judges to enhance independence and efficiency, as targeted anti-corruption measures absent under Gruevski, whom he faults for failing to address systemic graft.[46] Unlike Gruevski's tenure, marked by personal convictions for abuses like illegal property acquisition, Mickoski faces no such direct indictments, and his government has prioritized technocratic renewal within the party's nationalist framework without rehabilitating exiled figures.[110] Critics from SDSM, recently ousted after seven years in power, attribute these distinctions to political expediency rather than genuine rupture, though verifiable policy shifts, including explicit distancing from Gruevski's post-exile maneuvers, suggest a pragmatic evolution.[7][111]

Responses to EU and neighbor demands

Mickoski has consistently rejected EU accession demands perceived as infringing on North Macedonia's national identity, emphasizing that membership should proceed on merit rather than further concessions. In September 2024, he stated that the country is "tired of encountering obstacles" and will no longer accept ultimatums, describing such conditions as humiliating and contrary to fair integration principles.[67] He argued that EU entry must avoid additional identity-related compromises, prioritizing reforms over bilateral impositions.[112] Regarding neighboring countries, particularly Bulgaria's veto on EU talks—rooted in disputes over Macedonian history, language, and minority rights—Mickoski has criticized these as unreasonable and propagandistic, refusing to implement demanded constitutional amendments without reciprocal guarantees. In December 2024, he affirmed commitment to EU membership but explicitly rejected further concessions, signaling no acceptance of bilateral vetoes tied to identity erasure.[113] [114] By October 2025, he reiterated "yes to the European Union, no to bilateral conditions," demanding European Council assurances to bypass neighbor-imposed hurdles, while highlighting past government concessions as insufficient for progress.[115] In his February 2025 speech at the Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC) in the United States, Mickoski framed neighbor criticisms—such as Bulgarian claims of historical revisionism—as propaganda aimed at undermining Macedonian sovereignty, urging strategic Western support to counter such pressures without identity dilution.[116] This stance balances pro-EU advocates' emphasis on economic integration benefits against sovereignty advocates' concerns over cultural costs, with Mickoski conditioning compromises on tangible advancements like negotiation starts, absent which he prioritizes national dignity over appeasement.[117]
