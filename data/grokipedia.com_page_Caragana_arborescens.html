Caragana arborescens

Caragana arborescens, the Siberian peashrub,[2] Siberian pea-tree,[3] or caragana, is a species of legume native to Siberia and parts of China (Heilongjiang, Xinjiang) and neighboring Mongolia and Kazakhstan.[4] It was introduced to North America in the mid-18th century, primarily as an ornamental plant and for erosion control. In some areas of the United States it is considered an invasive species. Introduced on the Canadian prairies in the 1880s, the hardy caragana provided shelter-belts, wildlife habitat, nitrogen fixation, and windbreaks to prevent soil erosion and snow drifting.[5]

Description

It is a perennial shrub or small tree growing 2â€“6 m (6 ft 7 in â€“ 19 ft 8 in) tall.[4] Typically, it has a moderate growth rate.[6] The leaves vary from light green to dark green, and are alternate and compound with many small leaflets. Fragrant yellow flowers bloom in May or June. The fruits are legumes which contain many seeds, and ripen in July. As the seed pods dry they have a tendency to twist and pop open, releasing the seeds.[6] It is hardy in USDA zones 2 to 7. The plant tolerates a wide range of climatic environments in the United States such as Alaska, California, and New Mexico.[7] It can live up to 140 years.[8]

Uses

Caragana arborescens can be grown as an ornamental plant and has been extensively used in windbreaks.[9] It has an extensive root system, and can be used in erosion control.[10] The production of seeds is very large, but they are small in size and bland in flavor.[11][12] The seeds are edible by humans and chickens, but should be cooked before being consumed by people.[13][11] As a legume, C. arborescens fixes nitrogen, improving soil fertility to prepare for further planting. It is suitable for planting in single-row field windbreaks where a dense, short barrier is desired; and it is recommended for planting in the outer rows of multi-row plantings. However, C. arborescens is considered an invasive species in some areas of the United States, such as Minnesota, where its sale and propagation are restricted as of 2025.[10] C. arborescens is used for nesting by several songbirds but its seeds are rarely eaten by them. The plant is not a preferred food for browsing animals, but its fragrant flowers attract many pollen-consuming animals.
