Elizabeth Quay

Elizabeth Quay is a waterfront mixed-use development precinct in the central business district of Perth, Western Australia, situated on the north shore of the Swan River and encompassing a newly created artificial inlet.[1][2] Opened to the public on 29 January 2016 after construction from 2012, it integrates public promenades, residential towers, commercial spaces, and recreational facilities including a pedestrian bridge and interactive water features, aimed at reconnecting the city with the river and boosting urban vitality.[3][4] The project, developed by the Western Australian government at a cost exceeding $440 million for the core infrastructure, has been credited with transforming Perth's skyline and enhancing tourism through waterfront dining, events, and public art, drawing millions of visitors annually and contributing to increased city living and economic activity.[5][6] However, it faced significant controversy over budget overruns, high initial operating expenses nearing $1.7 million in the first two months, operational issues such as bacterial contamination in the waterpark, and criticisms regarding wind microclimates, overshadowing, and opportunity costs for public funds amid competing priorities like infrastructure needs.[7][8][9] Despite these challenges, ongoing private developments including luxury hotels like the Ritz-Carlton have solidified its role as a key urban landmark.[10]

Overview

Physical Description and Design

Elizabeth Quay is a 10-hectare waterfront precinct in Perth's central business district, centered on a man-made inlet dredged from the Swan River foreshore and a reclaimed island formed by land excavation, encircled by a 730-meter terraced promenade that extends the urban grid to the water's edge.[11][12] The masterplan, developed by ARM Architecture following a 2006 design competition, integrates landscape elements by Taylor Cullity Lethlean, emphasizing curvilinear forms and a ripple-wave pattern in paving and structures inspired by Noongar mythology's Wagyl creation story.[4][11] A defining architectural element is the Elizabeth Quay Pedestrian Bridge, a 110-meter-long, 22-meter-high cable-stayed structure designed by Arup Associates, featuring two leaning steel arches supporting an S-shaped deck clad in eucalyptus wood, enabling pedestrian and cyclist passage over the inlet with 5-meter clearance and providing 360-degree views of the river and city.[13][14][15] The bridge incorporates stainless steel in handrails, balustrades, support posts, and mesh infills for durability and aesthetic continuity with surrounding elements.[16] The island's topography mimics natural undulations with swooping stone revetment walls, indigenous plantings, a sand ramp for water access, and a playground, while the split-level promenade employs contrasting stone paving in dynamic patterns, steel edging, integrated lighting, timber terraces at the landing, and an ETFE-paneled blue canopy over the ferry berth featuring patterned fins.[4][11] Additional features include the BHP Billiton Water Park with mist jets and paved play surfaces evoking Western Australia's dry lakes.[4]

Location and Accessibility


Elizabeth Quay occupies a waterfront site on the northern foreshore of Perth Water, an eastern reach of the Swan River, within Perth's central business district.[17] The precinct spans approximately 730 meters of terraced promenade around a man-made inlet, situated between Barrack Street and William Street, and is bordered by The Esplanade to the north and the river to the south.[4] Its geographical coordinates are approximately 31Â°57â€² S, 115Â°51â€² E, placing it adjacent to key landmarks including the Swan Bells tower and the Perth Convention and Exhibition Centre.[18] The site is highly accessible by public transport, with Elizabeth Quay Station serving the Transperth Mandurah rail line, offering connections to Perth Underground Station and suburbs like Canning Bridge.[19] The adjacent Elizabeth Quay Bus Station features 35 stands for over 55 Transperth bus routes operated by providers including Path Transit and Transdev WA.[20] Ferries from Barrack Street Jetty provide river access, complementing high-frequency CAT bus services looping through the city center.[1] Pedestrian links, including a dedicated footbridge spanning the inlet, enable a short walk from the CBD core.[12] Motorists can utilize City of Perth-operated car parks in the vicinity, such as those near the Barrack Street Jetty precinct, with fees applicable except for designated accessible bays.[21] No on-site parking exists at the railway station itself.[19] For disability access, the precinct incorporates ramps with minimal gradients, handrails on stairs, accessible toilets near the BHP Water Park, ACROD parking bays along Geoffrey Bolton Avenue, and tactile wayfinding signage.[22][23]

Historical Development

Planning and Announcement

The redevelopment of Perth's waterfront, encompassing what became Elizabeth Quay, originated from urban planning discussions in the early 1990s, including a 1991 proposal under Labor Premier Carmen Lawrence to enhance connectivity between the central business district and the Swan River.[24][10] Following the 2008 election of the Liberal-National government led by Premier Colin Barnett, the project gained renewed momentum as a priority for urban revitalization, with cabinet decisions in 2010 advancing site preparation and design on the 10-hectare Esplanade Reserve. In February 2011, Premier Barnett publicly announced the Perth Waterfront project, detailing a $440 million state-funded initiative to create a mixed-use precinct with public spaces, an inlet, and commercial developments, supported by $270 million in the 2011-12 budget for infrastructure like dredging and reclamation.[25][26] The plan aimed to transform the underutilized foreshore into a vibrant hub, drawing on master planning by firms including ARM Architecture, with construction slated to commence later that year.[4] Groundbreaking occurred on April 26, 2012, when Barnett and Planning Minister John Day turned the first sod, marking the shift from planning to execution amid expectations of economic stimulus through tourism and private investment. On May 28, 2012, the precinct was officially named Elizabeth Quay by Premier Barnett, honoring Queen Elizabeth II during her Diamond Jubilee year as a tribute to her 2011 visit to Western Australia, with the designation emphasizing its role in linking the city's heritage to modern development. This announcement solidified the project's identity, though it faced early scrutiny over costs and environmental impacts, including the relocation of public car parks and potential effects on river ecology.[27]

Construction Process

Construction of Elizabeth Quay commenced on 26 April 2012 with initial site preparation and earthworks, following the project's announcement earlier that year.[28] Decmil was contracted for the inlet excavation and revetments, involving the removal of approximately 300,000 cubic meters of material to create the 2.7-hectare inlet connected to the Swan River.[29] The major phase known as the "Big Dig" focused on excavating the inlet, constructing promenades, the ferry terminal, BHP Billiton Water Park, an artificial island, and the pedestrian bridge, with works progressing steadily by mid-2015.[30] Broad Construction, in partnership with CPB Contractors, handled the delivery of the inlet and public realm infrastructure, incorporating complex elements such as suspension bridges and tactile paving for accessibility.[31] Stantec served as principal consultant, coordinating over 45 engineers across disciplines to manage civil, structural, and marine works.[32] The inlet and associated public infrastructure reached substantial completion in January 2016, enabling the official opening on 29 January 2016 ahead of the Perth International Arts Festival.[33] While the core public spaces were delivered on schedule, private developments including residential towers and hotels continued phased construction thereafter, with some towers facing integration challenges for structural components.[34] Geotechnical monitoring ensured stable foundations in the underlying King's Park Formation rock.[35]

Key Contractors and Timeline

The Elizabeth Quay project saw site preparations begin on 26 April 2012, with the official name announced on 28 May 2012.[28] Major construction contracts were awarded in December 2012, including a $200 million deal to a joint venture between Leighton Contractors and Broad Construction Services for the inlet works.[36] [37] Site construction commenced in early 2013, focusing on earthworks and inlet excavation, with contractors mobilizing for the central inlet by August 2013.[38] [39] Earthworks were primarily executed by Decmil and GMF Contractors, handling bulk excavation and site leveling.[29] [40] The Leighton-Broad joint venture led the core infrastructure build, including the 1.2 km infinity edge pool, pedestrian bridge, and public realm elements, under coordination from Stantec for design and documentation.[31] [32] Ventia contributed to ancillary services like roads, parks, and the artificial island.[41] Inlet and associated infrastructure reached completion in January 2016, enabling the precinct's public opening on 29 January 2016 ahead of the Perth International Arts Festival.[42] Subsequent phases, including private developments like the Ritz-Carlton, extended into later years, but the foundational public works adhered to this accelerated timeline despite initial projections of 5-7 years.[42]

Features and Infrastructure

Public Spaces and Amenities

Elizabeth Quay features extensive pedestrian promenades and walkways along the Swan River foreshore, designed for strolling and cycling, connecting the precinct's northern and southern areas to the broader Perth central business district.[43] These paths incorporate shaded seating areas and integrate with public art installations that enhance the urban waterfront experience.[1] The precinct includes family-oriented amenities such as the BHP Water Park, an interactive zero-depth water playground with fountains, jets, and light displays, operating for 50 minutes each hour and featuring the mosaic artwork Pinjah by artists Sandra Hill and Jenny Dawson.[43] [44] Adjacent to this is the Island Playground on the man-made island, equipped with climbing ropes, nautical-themed structures, sand play areas, and additional water elements for children.[44] Tiered decking around the water park provides shaded seating for observers.[43] A key connective feature is the Elizabeth Quay Pedestrian Bridge, a 20-meter-high suspension structure spanning the inlet, accessible to pedestrians and cyclists, and offering panoramic views of the Swan River and Perth skyline.[45] Public facilities include accessible toilets equipped with hoist systems, assisted access, and changing tables, located at Barrack Street Jetty, The Island, the eastern promenade, and opposite the BHP Water Park.[22] [46] The spaces support seasonal events such as markets, concerts, and festivals, with grassed areas available for picnics.[43] [47]

Transportation Links

Elizabeth Quay serves as a major public transportation hub in Perth's central business district, integrated with the Transperth network for seamless access.[48] The Elizabeth Quay railway station, part of the Mandurah Line, provides train services in Zone 1, with connections to Perth Underground Station toward the city center and Canning Bridge outward, facilitating commuter and visitor travel.[19] Adjacent to the station, the Elizabeth Quay Bus Station features 35 stands served by over 50 Transperth bus routes operated by Path Transit, Swan Transit, and Transdev WA, linking to suburbs and intercity services including South West Coach Lines.[49] Transperth ferries also operate from the quay, crossing the Swan River to South Perth in approximately 12 minutes, offering scenic water-based transit.[50] Pedestrian and cyclist access is enhanced by the Elizabeth Quay Pedestrian Bridge, a 22-meter-high suspension structure spanning the inlet to connect the quay's island precinct with the mainland at Williams Landing.[51] This 5-meter-wide bridge, designed for simultaneous use by walkers and cyclists, includes ramps for wheelchair accessibility and restores historical linkages from the Narrows Bridge to the Causeway.[52] The quay lies within Perth's free transit zone for central trains, buses, and CAT services, enabling cost-free movement from nearby stations like Perth and City West.[53] Road access supports vehicular drop-off and private travel, with entry via Riverside Drive and designated pick-up zones at the Perth Convention and Exhibition Centre, opposite the Adina Apartment Hotel.[17] Parking is limited on-site, promoting public transport and active modes, though multi-level facilities nearby accommodate visitors.[54]

Public Art Installations

Elizabeth Quay incorporates a public art program funded by a $4 million allocation within the precinct's development budget, featuring works by local and Indigenous artists integrated into the waterfront landscape.[55] These installations aim to connect visitors with the site's cultural, environmental, and historical narratives, drawing on themes of water, Indigenous perspectives, and conservation efforts.[56] Prominent among them is Spanda, a 29-meter-tall sculpture by Australian artist Christian de Vietri, constructed from steel and carbon fiber and installed in January 2016 at The Landing.[57] This structure, the world's largest free-standing carbon-fiber artwork, evokes rippling water to symbolize the linkage between the Swan River, land, and sky.[58] ![First Contact sculpture](./assets/E37_Elizabeth_Quay_1Feb20161Feb20161Feb2016 First Contact, a 5-meter-tall cast aluminum sculpture by Noongar artist Laurel Nannup, depicts the Indigenous perception of arriving European settlers in 1829 as bird-like figures and was erected around 2016 at the William Street Landing.[59] Inspired by Nannup's original woodcut print, the work narrates the Noongar people's initial encounter with outsiders, blending historical reflection with contemporary artistic expression.[60] The bronze statue of Bessie Mabel Rischbieth, sculpted by Jon Tarry and installed in 2016 on the Island platform, commemorates the early 20th-century activist's campaigns against river pollution and for preserving Kings Park and the Swan River foreshore.[61] Rischbieth, a pioneer in women's rights and environmental advocacy, led protests that influenced urban planning policies in Perth.[62] Additional elements include the Signature Ring, a copper artwork resembling headphones at the Barrack Street Jetty, enhancing nighttime visibility through integrated lighting.[63] Temporary displays in light-box structures along the quay walls further rotate contemporary pieces, maintaining an evolving artistic presence.[64]

Economic and Social Impacts

Tourism Attraction and Visitor Statistics

Elizabeth Quay serves as a prominent tourism draw in Perth's central business district, featuring waterfront promenades, public art installations, dining precincts, and event spaces that appeal to both domestic and international visitors seeking leisure activities along the Swan River. Its design emphasizes pedestrian-friendly access to recreational amenities, including interactive water features and scenic views, positioning it as a key destination for short-stay tourism and family outings. The precinct integrates with nearby cultural sites, enhancing its role in Perth's urban tourism ecosystem.[1] Visitor footfall data, primarily derived from Wi-Fi tracking and mobile phone analytics, indicate high initial usage following the quay's opening on January 29, 2016. In its first year of operation through early 2017, the site recorded approximately 6.6 to 6.7 million visits, surpassing estimates for global landmarks like Rome's Colosseum in equivalent periods, though these figures encompass repeat entries and transient passersby rather than unique individuals. By November 2016, cumulative visits exceeded 4 million, with events such as Arcadia contributing to spikes in attendance. Government reports from the period highlighted robust engagement, including 15,000 attendees at WA Day celebrations in June 2016 and sustained weekend crowds estimated at 15,000 in March 2016.[65] These statistics faced scrutiny from opposition figures, who questioned their validity in 2017, arguing that claims of over 7 million visits implied implausibly high per capita attendance compared to established attractions like the Sydney Opera House's 8.2 million annual visitors, and suggested potential overcounting via passive tracking methods. Independent verification remains limited, with no peer-reviewed analyses available, though official data collection relied on established urban analytics tools. More recent metrics show continued vitality, including a 20 percent year-on-year increase in Saturday night crowds reported in December 2024, amid broader city visitation trends exceeding 1 million in December periods. Overall, the quay has hosted over 200 events since inception, sustaining millions of visits and supporting ancillary tourism spending in dining and retail.[66][67][2]

Broader Urban and Economic Contributions

Elizabeth Quay has played a pivotal role in revitalizing Perth's central business district (CBD) by reestablishing a direct physical and cultural linkage between the city center and the Swan River, which prior infrastructure such as the Narrows Bridge had obscured.[68][32] This reconnection has fostered a more integrated urban environment, blending waterfront public spaces with adjacent cultural and commercial precincts, thereby enhancing pedestrian connectivity and encouraging mixed-use activity across the CBD.[1] The precinct has catalyzed broader urban renewal efforts, including a surge in city living and the development of high-rise office and residential towers, such as those by Brookfield Properties, contributing to a 55% increase in CBD workers since its opening.[69][6] It exemplifies a public-private partnership model under the Metropolitan Redevelopment Authority, which has influenced subsequent infill and precinct developments in Perth, promoting denser, more vibrant urban growth amid post-mining boom challenges.[70] Economically, Elizabeth Quay is forecasted by Deloitte Access Economics to generate over $2.9 billion in direct impacts from private construction and related expenditures, while creating more than 4,000 construction jobs and sustaining an average of over 500 annual jobs in operations and tourism support.[71][72][2] These contributions extend to multiplier effects on local businesses and infrastructure, underpinning Perth's transition toward a more compact, economically resilient city structure.[73]

Private Investment and Revitalization Effects

The initial $440 million government investment in Elizabeth Quay's infrastructure was projected to leverage $2.2 billion in private sector funding for developments including hotels, apartments, and commercial spaces.[74] By 2025, private investments exceeded $2 billion, encompassing high-rise towers, luxury residences, and hospitality venues that transformed the precinct from underutilized foreshore into a vibrant mixed-use hub.[75] Key private projects include the $400 million Ritz-Carlton hotel and 379 residential apartments, developed by private entities and operational since 2016, alongside the $500 million Towers at Elizabeth Quay, Perth's largest single residential release.[76][77] Further investments feature the $1.1 billion Elizabeth Quay West towers, with $367 million in construction costs for commercial office space, and Nine The Esplanade, an office tower reaching practical completion in May 2025.[78][79] International firms such as Singapore's Redhill Partners and Invesco Real Estate have participated, with transactions like Invesco's 50% acquisition of a Brookfield project in 2020 signaling sustained global interest.[80][81] These investments have catalyzed revitalization by reclaiming 37 hectares of waterfront through mixed-use development, reconnecting central Perth to the Swan River and fostering urban renewal after decades of land reclamation and stagnation.[70] Early criticisms noted sluggish private uptake, but recent sales, such as a 20-storey tower listing in 2025 amid rising investor appetite, indicate maturing economic momentum.[27][75] The precinct's evolution has demonstrated public demand for premium infrastructure, contributing to broader city activation through luxury apartments, offices, and a proposed $880 million 135-meter tower expected to draw thousands of residents and workers.[82][83]

Controversies and Challenges

Pre-Construction Opposition

Prior to construction commencing in late 2012, the proposed Elizabeth Quay development faced substantial public and professional opposition in Perth, primarily centered on the transformation of the publicly accessible Esplanade Reserve foreshore into a commercial precinct involving river infill. Critics argued that the $440 million public investment would privatize valuable public parkland established in the 1890s, reducing open green space and altering the natural river edge along Perth Water.[84][85][86] A petition with over 5,000 signatures opposing the waterfront redevelopment plan was presented to Parliament on March 6, 2012, highlighting concerns over loss of public access and environmental degradation from dredging approximately 13 hectares of the Swan River to create the inner harbor.[87][88] Public protests intensified in early 2012, with thousands gathering on the Esplanade Reserve on February 26 to demonstrate against the government's plans, followed by hundreds rallying outside Parliament House on June 13, decrying the project as a threat to the city's "greatest asset" â€” its open foreshore.[85][89][84] Professional bodies, including architects, town planners, and historians, launched a campaign in January 2012 to halt the project, criticizing the design for prioritizing high-rise commercial towers over civic or cultural elements and potentially impacting heritage views and urban character.[88] A July 2012 poll reflected divided opinion, with 41% supporting the $2.6 billion initiative (including associated private developments) and 36% opposing it, amid broader concerns over fiscal viability and ecological effects on the river's biodiversity.[90][86] The Western Australian Labor Opposition, then in minority government status, voiced reservations about the scale and funding, though internal divisions emerged; Premier Colin Barnett defended the vision as essential for urban revitalization despite the backlash.[84] Environmental groups emphasized risks from infill sedimentation and altered tidal flows, potentially harming seagrass beds and fish habitats in Perth Water.[89]

Water Quality and Environmental Concerns

The site for Elizabeth Quay was identified as one of 23 unlined former waste dumps along the Swan River, used in the 1960s and 1970s for disposing hazardous waste, garden refuse, building rubble, asbestos, and poisons.[91] Groundwater from such sites was suspected of discharging leachate containing heavy metals including arsenic, cadmium, manganese, nickel, lead, aluminium, chromium, copper, iron, and zinc into the Swan-Canning estuary, with some samples exceeding Australian guidelines by over 100 times.[91] No systematic monitoring of leachate occurred at these sites prior to development, raising risks of ongoing contamination to the river system despite remediation efforts by environmental consultants addressing contaminated land and acid sulfate soils.[5] Following the quay's opening on January 29, 2016, the BHP Billiton Water Park faced immediate water quality challenges, with non-pathogenic Naegleria detected in backwash water on February 4, prompting a temporary closure and reopening on February 8 after cleaning.[92] Pseudomonas aeruginosa bacteria were identified in surface water and filters on February 14, spreading to balance tanks by February 22 amid high water temperatures reaching 33Â°C, leading to another closure as the pathogen posed public health risks to users.[92][93] The Department of Health had been aware of the bacteria from initial tests but delayed full closure until systemic contamination was confirmed, after which filtration upgrades and super-chlorination were implemented.[93] The quay's semi-enclosed inlet design, excised from the main Swan River flow, contributed to stagnation and reduced flushing, heightening vulnerability to contaminants like fecal bacteria from birdlife and runoff, which prompted cancellation of a triathlon swim in April 2016.[94][95] Elevated cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) levels were recorded in the inlet from November 2015 to early January 2016, further underscoring circulation limitations.[94] Ecologists criticized the monthly monitoring regime as inadequate for detecting rapid changes in such a sensitive, low-flow environment, advocating more frequent testing for parameters like fecal coliforms, especially post-rainfall, over the weekly protocols applied to the broader river.[95] Automated systems tracked pH and chlorine hourly at the water park, but broader inlet oversight relied on private contractors reporting to health authorities.[92] Remedial actions included enhanced filtration, chlorination, and collaboration between the Department of Health, Metropolitan Redevelopment Authority, and engineers, enabling the water park's reopening for the 2016-2017 summer after addressing design flaws.[92] These early incidents highlighted causal links between the inlet's hydrologyâ€”limited tidal exchange and warm, shallow conditionsâ€”and amplified microbial risks, though no major public health outbreaks were reported beyond isolated cases.[93] Subsequent data indicate stabilized operations, with environmental management focusing on preventing recurrence through ongoing approvals and monitoring protocols.[5]

Specific Development Disputes

The construction of Elizabeth Quay involved disputes over subcontractor payments, with multiple small businesses reporting outstanding amounts for work completed in 2016, prompting the Western Australian government to initiate a review of payment compliance.[96] These claims highlighted delays and refusals by head contractors, leading to accusations that the state had neglected small enterprises reliant on timely settlements.[97] The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission intervened by warning major contractors of potential legal action for withholding funds, amid broader concerns over cash flow disruptions in Western Australia's construction sector.[98] In response, the state introduced a builders' code of conduct and enhanced mediation powers for the Small Business Commissioner to address such payment conflicts on public projects.[99] Post-completion, private residential developments within the precinct, notably The Towers apartments, faced significant owner-builder disputes over structural defects reported from 2017 onward, including persistent leaks, crooked walls, broken sewage pipes, and substandard finishes such as uneven paint and surface damage.[100] Owners of units valued up to $2 million withheld occupancy and pursued rectifications, citing inadequate waterproofing and construction quality that compromised habitability.[100] These issues extended to public facilities like the ferry kiosk, where a acknowledged design flaw caused leaks and accessibility shortcomings, requiring remedial works by DevelopmentWA.[101] Strata disputes underscored limitations in defect liability periods, with calls for extended warranties beyond the standard three years to cover major flaws in high-value waterfront builds.[102] Approval processes for subsequent phases also sparked contention, as seen in the 2018 abandonment of affordable housing allocations in The Towers project, which eliminated planned low-income units and fueled debates over the precinct's potential as an exclusive enclave rather than inclusive urban renewal.[103] While not escalating to litigation, this shift drew criticism from housing advocates for prioritizing luxury over mixed-tenure commitments outlined in initial masterplans.[103] Later proposals, such as Brookfield's dual towers, navigated local government endorsements amid design critiques but avoided major halts, though construction deferrals in 2021 due to tenant shortfalls highlighted ongoing commercial viability tensions.[104]

Ongoing and Future Developments

Recent Maintenance and Improvements

The BHP Water Park at Elizabeth Quay undergoes routine weekly maintenance, closing every Tuesday to facilitate cleaning, inspections, and necessary repairs to ensure operational safety and water quality.[105] This schedule supports ongoing functionality amid high visitor usage, with occasional additional closures for essential works, such as those reported in July 2025 addressing specific maintenance needs.[106] As part of the METRONET Swan Ferry Service Expansion, upgrades to existing infrastructure at the Elizabeth Quay terminal were implemented to accommodate expanded electric ferry operations connecting to new routes at Matilda Bay and Applecross, enhancing capacity and integration with the precinct's transport network.[107] These improvements, advanced through 2025, include enhancements to support increased service frequency and accessibility without specified costs publicly detailed in project overviews.[108]

Expansion Plans and Masterplan

The Elizabeth Quay precinct was developed under a master plan coordinated by DevelopmentWA, which outlined the reclamation of a 9-hectare inlet from the Swan River, construction of a 730-meter terraced waterfront promenade, and integration of mixed-use elements including residential towers, office buildings, hotels, retail spaces, and public amenities such as a pedestrian bridge and public art installations.[1][4] The plan emphasized reconnecting central Perth to the riverfront, with built-form guidelines specifying building heights up to 53 storeys in key areas and a focus on high-density urban activation.[109] Post-2016 completion of core infrastructure, expansion efforts have prioritized commercial intensification and transport connectivity. The Nine The Esplanade office tower, a 13-level development, achieved practical completion in May 2025, delivering approximately 20,000 square meters of premium office space leased to tenants including EY and Hub Australia.[79] Plans for Lot 4 at 21 The Esplanade include a $400 million, 53-storey mixed-use tower comprising 70,000 square meters of office accommodation and 176 short-stay units, with development applications advancing under City of Perth oversight.[110] In July 2024, the City of Perth released a new waterfront masterplan extending Elizabeth Quay's framework northward along the Swan River, envisioning enhanced public realms, riverfront activation, and integration with Langley Park through greening initiatives like planting 3,000 trees and new sports facilities to foster recreational and event uses.[111] Complementary transport expansions under METRONET include a Swan River ferry service extension from the existing Elizabeth Quay terminal to new stops at Matilda Bay (University of Western Australia) and Applecross, featuring five electric ferries, fixed jetties, sheltered waiting areas, and floating pontoons, with terminal construction and development approvals progressing as of September 2025.[108][112] These elements aim to boost accessibility and economic viability without altering the original inlet's hydrology.[107]
