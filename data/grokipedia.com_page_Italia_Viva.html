Italia Viva


Italia Viva (IV) is a centrist liberal political party in Italy, established on 18 September 2019 by Matteo Renzi following his resignation as secretary of the Democratic Party (PD), which enabled him to lead a faction of approximately 40 senators in defecting to form the new grouping.[1][2] The party positions itself as reformist and pro-European, emphasizing institutional changes, economic liberalism, and support for EU integration, though its self-description as a "young, innovative" force has been critiqued amid modest organizational growth.[2] Initially influential through its Senate contingent, Italia Viva joined the Conte II coalition government in 2019, providing two ministers, but Renzi withdrew support in January 2021 over disputes concerning the allocation of EU Recovery and Resilience Facility funds, triggering a parliamentary crisis during the COVID-19 pandemic that ultimately led to Giuseppe Conte's resignation and the formation of Mario Draghi's national unity cabinet, which the party backed.[3][4] This maneuver drew accusations of opportunism from opponents, highlighting Renzi's pattern of leveraging small parliamentary leverage for policy influence despite limited voter base.[3] Electorally, the party has underperformed relative to Renzi's ambitions: in the 2022 general election, an alliance with Azione yielded about 8% of the proportional vote but no seats in the Chamber of Deputies due to failing coalition thresholds, while securing minimal Senate representation from prior defectors.[1] In the 2024 European Parliament elections, Italia Viva participated in the "Stati Uniti d'Europa" list, which obtained 3.78% of votes and zero seats.[5] As of 2025, it remains in opposition to the centre-right government led by Giorgia Meloni, retaining a diminished but pivotal Senate group of around a dozen members, often engaging in cross-party negotiations on key legislation.[6]

History

Origins in the Democratic Party Split

Matteo Renzi ascended within the Democratic Party (PD) by challenging Prime Minister Enrico Letta, a fellow party member, in early 2014. As PD secretary since December 2013, Renzi withdrew party support from Letta's government on February 13, 2014, criticizing its slow pace on reforms amid Italy's economic stagnation. This maneuver forced Letta's resignation, paving the way for Renzi to become Italy's youngest prime minister on February 22, 2014, at age 39, backed by the same center-left coalition.[7][8] Renzi's tenure faced mounting internal PD tensions, culminating in the December 4, 2016, constitutional referendum, where voters rejected his proposed reforms by 59.1% to 40.9%, leading to his resignation as prime minister on December 7. Critics within the PD, including left-wing factions, accused Renzi of overly personalizing the campaign and centralizing power, exacerbating factional divides between his reformist supporters and traditionalists opposed to his aggressive style. Despite a 2017 comeback to reclaim PD leadership with over 70% of primary votes, the party's poor performance in the March 4, 2018, general electionâ€”securing only 18.8% of votesâ€”prompted Renzi's resignation as secretary on March 12, 2018, highlighting persistent rifts over strategy and ideology.[9][10][11] By 2019, policy disagreements intensified as the PD navigated the collapse of Giuseppe Conte's first government in August, triggered by Matteo Salvini's Lega withdrawal from its coalition with the Five Star Movement (M5S). Renzi opposed the PD's prospective alliance with the populist M5S to form Conte's second government, viewing it as a betrayal of centrist principles and an accommodation of anti-establishment forces that had long criticized PD governance. On September 17, 2019, Renzi announced his departure from the PD, citing irreconcilable differences and the need for a pro-European, reform-oriented alternative; he was followed by approximately 40 parliamentarians, including 24 deputies and 12 senators, who defected to form a new parliamentary group, underscoring deep factionalism within the PD.[12][13][14]

Foundation and Early Organization

On September 17, 2019, Matteo Renzi, former Prime Minister of Italy and a prominent reformist within the Democratic Party (PD), announced his resignation from the party, criticizing its shift toward a more leftist orientation under Secretary Nicola Zingaretti.[15] This decision stemmed from longstanding tensions over policy direction and leadership, prompting Renzi to form a new centrist group.[12] The following day, September 18, 2019, Renzi formally launched Italia Viva as a new political formation, emphasizing renewal and vitality in Italian politics.[16] The name "Italia Viva" (Italy Alive) was chosen to convey optimism and a proactive stance toward national challenges.[15] Renzi's departure was swiftly followed by defections from over 40 PD parliamentarians, securing Italia Viva an immediate presence in both the Chamber of Deputies and the Senate.[16] Key figures who joined included Teresa Bellanova, a former PD deputy focused on agricultural policy, and Ivan Scalfarotto, a longtime Renzi ally advocating civil rights reforms.[16] These recruits formed the core of the party's initial parliamentary groups, enabling autonomous representation without the need for broader alliances at inception. In its foundational phase, Italia Viva outlined a platform centered on pro-European federalism, liberal economic policies, and institutional reforms to counter populist tendencies in Italian politics.[15] The party prioritized pragmatic governance over ideological purity, drawing from Renzi's prior emphasis on modernization and EU integration.[17] Organizational efforts in late 2019 involved establishing provisional structures, including regional coordinators, to expand beyond parliamentary ranks and prepare for future electoral contests.

Participation in the Conte II Government

Following its formation on September 17, 2019, Italia Viva, led by Matteo Renzi, pledged external support to the Conte II government, a coalition primarily between the Democratic Party (PD) and the Five Star Movement (M5S) that had assumed office on September 5, 2019, after the collapse of the prior PD-League-M5S alliance. This backing proved essential for parliamentary stability, as Italia Viva's delegationâ€”comprising roughly 40 senators and 20 deputies who had defected from the PDâ€”helped secure the government's slim majorities in both chambers amid initial vulnerabilities to no-confidence motions. Renzi explicitly affirmed continued support for Prime Minister Giuseppe Conte, emphasizing the need to avoid early elections and focus on legislative continuity.[13][12] Italia Viva transitioned from external to internal support by securing two ministerial posts in the Conte II cabinet: Teresa Bellanova as Minister of Agricultural, Food and Forestry Policies and Elena Bonetti as Minister for Family and Equal Opportunities, both serving from September 2019. Bellanova, a former PD member with roots in agricultural labor organizing, prioritized sector-specific initiatives, including advocacy for regularizing approximately 600,000 undocumented migrant workers in agriculture to address labor shortages during the COVID-19 crisis. These appointments allowed Italia Viva to influence policy implementation, particularly in economic stabilization efforts amid the pandemic's onset in early 2020.[18][19] During 2020, Italia Viva contributed to the government's response to the economic downturn, supporting the negotiation and accession to the European Union's Recovery and Resilience Facility, which allocated Italy â‚¬191.5 billion in grants and loans for post-COVID recovery. Party lawmakers consistently voted in favor of enabling legislation for these funds, aligning with the coalition's approval of five major decree-laws between March 2020 and early 2021 that mobilized resources for employment preservation and sectoral aid, including agriculture. However, tensions emerged early over justice system reforms, with Italia Viva pushing for streamlining measures that encountered resistance from M5S vetoes on prosecutorial oversight and magistrate accountability, highlighting ideological divergences within the coalition despite shared support for broader fiscal packages.[20][21]

Withdrawal and Government Crisis

On January 13, 2021, Matteo Renzi, leader of Italia Viva, announced the party's withdrawal from the Conte II coalition government during a press conference, primarily criticizing the national Recovery and Resilience Plan for lacking sufficient ambition in addressing structural reforms amid the COVID-19 crisis.[22][23] Renzi argued that the plan allocated inadequate resources to key sectors such as healthcare, culture, and infrastructure, risking inefficient use of over â‚¬200 billion in EU recovery funds without binding commitments to legislative overhauls.[24][3] He also faulted Prime Minister Giuseppe Conte for centralizing power and failing to incorporate Italia Viva's reform proposals, which had been repeatedly sidelined in the veto-prone dynamics of the broad coalition involving the Five Star Movement, Democratic Party, and smaller allies.[22][25] The withdrawal involved the immediate resignation of Italia Viva's two ministersâ€”Teresa Bellanova from Agriculture and Elena Bonetti from Family Policiesâ€”depriving the government of its slim parliamentary majority.[23][4] Italia Viva's 18 senators committed to abstaining from upcoming confidence votes, a tactical move that avoided outright opposition but ensured Conte's defeat.[26] This action exposed the fragility of Italy's fragmented coalition system, where multiparty veto points had stalled over 30 key bills on justice, public administration, and tax reforms in the preceding months, as documented in parliamentary records, undermining the government's ability to deliver the decisive changes Renzi deemed essential for effective fund utilization.[27] Conte secured a confidence vote in the Chamber of Deputies on January 19, 2021, but failed in the Senate the same day, prompting his formal resignation on January 26 after consultations with President Sergio Mattarella.[28][29] The crisis accelerated the formation of a technocratic government under Mario Draghi, whom Renzi had publicly endorsed as a capable leader to prioritize execution and reforms; Italia Viva pledged support to Draghi's cabinet on February 4, 2021, contributing to its broad cross-party backing and averting early elections.[30][31] This shift temporarily isolated Italia Viva politically, with public approval dipping amid pandemic fatigue, yet it facilitated a more unified approach to EU fund implementation under Draghi, contrasting the prior coalition's paralysis.[4][32]

Alliance Building and the Third Pole

Following the political crisis triggered by Italia Viva's withdrawal of support from the Conte II government on 13 January 2021, party leader Matteo Renzi endorsed Mario Draghi as prime minister, facilitating the formation of a national unity cabinet on 13 February 2021 that garnered backing from a broad spectrum of parties, including the Democratic Party, League, and Five Star Movement.[33] This positioning reinforced Italia Viva's role as a reform-oriented centrist force, emphasizing pro-European integration, institutional modernization, and economic recovery measures amid the COVID-19 pandemic, while avoiding alignment with either the progressive left or the conservative right.[34] The Draghi administration, sustained until its collapse on 14 July 2022 following the Five Star Movement's exit, provided Italia Viva a platform to advocate pragmatic governance over ideological polarization. With snap elections scheduled for 25 September 2022 after the government's fall, Renzi pursued strategic partnerships to establish a "Third Pole" as a centrist counterweight to the centre-left coalition of the Democratic Party and Five Star Movement, and the centre-right bloc led by Brothers of Italy. Negotiations with Carlo Calenda's Azione party, which shared liberal and pro-business leanings but had seen prior tensionsâ€”Calenda having publicly distanced himself from Renzi's leadership styleâ€”culminated in an agreement on 10 August 2022 for joint electoral lists under the Actionâ€“Italia Viva banner.[35] Despite overlapping ideologies on fiscal responsibility and European federalism, the talks addressed differences in campaign tactics and candidate selection to present a unified front.[36] The alliance's platform highlighted market-friendly reforms, investment in technological innovation, and a balanced green transition prioritizing competitiveness over regulatory burdens, aiming to appeal to moderate voters disillusioned with populist extremes.[37] Pre-election opinion polls, aggregated in sources like Politico's Europe Poll of Polls, showed the Third Pole consistently registering between 7% and 9% nationally in August and early September 2022, suggesting potential to disrupt the bipolar contest and secure a pivotal role in post-election dynamics.[38]

2022 General Election Results

In the general election on 25 September 2022, the Azione-Italia Viva joint list, known as the Third Pole, received 7.8% of the valid votes in the proportional representation component for the Chamber of Deputies.[39] This performance translated to approximately 1.3 million votes nationwide, drawing primarily from centrist and moderate voters disillusioned with both the centre-left Democratic Party and the Five Star Movement.[40] However, the list won no seats in either chamber of Parliament, as it operated as a single entity without a broader coalition, failing to surpass the 10% national threshold mandated by the 2017 electoral law for proportional seat allocation to non-coalition lists.[39] Additionally, the Third Pole candidates secured no victories in the 232 single-member districts for the Chamber or the corresponding uninominal contests for the Senate, where major coalitions dominated.[41] Regional variations highlighted uneven support, with stronger results in central Italy, particularly Tuscany, where the list approached or exceeded 10% in some provinces due to Matteo Renzi's historical influence and local reformist appeal.[40] In contrast, performance was weaker in the South and peripheral areas, correlating with lower urban turnout and competition from populist forces. Overall voter turnout stood at 63.9%, down from 2018, which may have disadvantaged niche centrist lists reliant on motivated, educated demographics.[41] Pre-election polls had projected the Third Pole at 8-10%, but the actual result fell short amid a fragmented centre-left field, where undecided voters consolidated toward larger coalitions rather than risking support for threshold-vulnerable options.[42] This discrepancy underscored the strategic miscalculation of running independently, as the electoral system's bonuses favored allied groups reaching the 10% coalition threshold, effectively nullifying the Third Pole's parliamentary presence despite its non-negligible vote share.[39]

Post-Election Developments and 2024 European Election

Following the 2022 general election, Italia Viva adopted an opposition stance toward the government of Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, particularly contesting policies on immigration and citizenship, including opposition to a 2025 referendum aimed at easing access to citizenship for long-term residents, which the party supported amid low turnout that invalidated the vote. The party backed five opposition-sponsored referendums in June 2025, including one on citizenship reform, though all failed due to insufficient participation exceeding 50% of the electorate. While critical of the government's social conservatism, Italia Viva has pragmatically endorsed aspects of Meloni's fiscal prudence, aligning with the administration's emphasis on debt reduction and EU compliance amid Italy's post-pandemic recovery. In the period leading to the 2024 European Parliament elections, Italia Viva sought to consolidate a centrist bloc through its prior partnership with Azione under the Third Pole banner, but tensions escalated into a formal split by September 2023, driven by disagreements over electoral strategy and leadership, with Azione leader Carlo Calenda prioritizing autonomy from Matteo Renzi's influence. This rupture prevented a unified centrist list, fragmenting the moderate vote ahead of the June 8â€“9, 2024, polls. For the European elections, Italia Viva joined the "Stati Uniti d'Europa" list alongside +Europa, the Italian Socialist Party, and other liberal groups, with Renzi heading the slate to advocate for deeper EU integration and federalism. The list garnered 3.78% of the national vote, falling short of the threshold for seats in Italy's 76-member delegation, resulting in no MEPs for the alliance. Renzi's personal campaign emphasized pro-European reforms and critiqued both the ruling right-wing coalition and the fragmented center-left, though the outcome underscored challenges in mobilizing centrist support amid high abstention rates nearing 50%. Into 2025, Italia Viva focused on regional contests, endorsing centrist coalitions without fielding direct candidates in key races like Basilicata's 2024 election, where supported lists achieved approximately 7% amid Renew Europe affiliates' gains against the center-right. The party maintained pushes for institutional reforms and local alliances in ongoing regional votes across seven regions through November 2025, sustaining a modest organizational base amid stable but limited membership.

Ideology and Policies

Ideological Foundations

Italia Viva self-identifies as a liberal-centrist formation, rooted in pro-European liberalism and reformist principles associated with Matteo Renzi's Renzismo, which prioritizes individual liberty, meritocracy, and institutional renewal against entrenched elites.[43][44] The party's Charter of Values, adopted in October 2019, underscores commitments to an open society, republican anti-fascist traditions, and the rejection of ideological dogmas, framing Italian identity as a product of cultural exchanges and shared prosperity rather than isolationism.[44][1] Positioned as an alternative to both the social democratic statism of the Democratic Party and the nationalist protectionism of right-wing formations like the League, Italia Viva advocates for governance grounded in empirical evidence and market-oriented efficiency over populist rhetoric or protectionist barriers.[45][46] Renzi has explicitly contrasted this approach with the anti-establishment appeals of the Five Star Movement, promoting instead pragmatic reforms that favor causal mechanisms of economic growth and personal responsibility.[47][46] The ideological shift to explicit centrism post-separation from the PD's centre-left framework reflects a deliberate pivot toward first-principles reasoningâ€”emphasizing liberty and efficiency as drivers of societal progressâ€”while critiquing both left-wing collectivism and right-wing ethno-nationalism for their divergence from data-driven realism.[48][49] This positioning aims to appeal to those seeking moderation amid Italy's polarized landscape, prioritizing verifiable outcomes over ideological purity.[50][45]

Economic and Fiscal Policies

Italia Viva advocates pro-market economic policies centered on fostering growth through labor market flexibility, tax reductions on productive activities, and bureaucratic simplification, drawing from Matteo Renzi's prior reforms as prime minister. The party supports extending principles of the 2015 Jobs Act, which liberalized hiring and firing rules to encourage permanent contracts and contributed to an estimated net increase of around 840,000 jobs between 2014 and 2017 by reducing employer risks and incentives for temporary work, according to analyses adjusting for concurrent tax incentives.[51] While critics argue the reform primarily boosted precarious employment without addressing underlying productivity stagnation, Italia Viva emphasizes its role in reversing Italy's pre-2014 employment decline amid high youth unemployment.[52] On taxation, the party prioritizes cuts to labor income taxes to boost competitiveness, echoing Renzi's 2014 proposal for â‚¬10 billion annual reductions benefiting 10 million low- and middle-income workers, and subsequent calls for further relief on payroll and income taxes to stimulate consumption and investment.[53] [54] Italia Viva critiques both the Democratic Party's statist approach, which it views as overly reliant on public spending without structural incentives, and the current right-wing government's fiscal populism, characterized by insufficient reforms amid rising debt pressures and high tax burdens exceeding 43% of GDP.[55] Regarding fiscal policy, Italia Viva favors a "growth-first" stance over rigid austerity, advocating stable deficits around 2.9% of GDP to accommodate investment while restructuring debt through privatization and efficiency gains, as Renzi proposed during his tenure to balance EU rules with domestic recovery needs.[56] For EU Recovery and Resilience Facility funds under the PNRR, the party insists on conditioning disbursements to productivity-enhancing reforms, such as simplified permitting for infrastructure and youth activation programs offering six-month stipends of â‚¬600 to inactive individuals, criticizing unchecked spending as seen in prior PD-M5S coalitions.[57] This approach aims to leverage the â‚¬191.5 billion allocation (grants and loans) for deregulation in sectors like public procurement, rejecting pure deregulation risks while prioritizing legal compliance to accelerate projects stalled by administrative delays.[58] Deregulation efforts focus on slashing bureaucratic hurdles, with Italia Viva promoting Renzi-era models like Industria 4.0 incentives for digital and manufacturing upgrades, which supported over â‚¬20 billion in investments by 2019, to enhance firm competitiveness without expanding state intervention.[59] The party positions these measures against left-wing interventionism, which it attributes to institutional biases favoring redistribution over innovation, and right-wing promises of broad tax relief untethered from spending cuts, advocating instead for targeted incentives verifiable through employment and GDP metrics.

Social and Institutional Reforms

Italia Viva has advocated for judicial reforms emphasizing efficiency and impartiality, including support for the 2022 referendums on justice that sought to limit parliamentary numbers to 600, abolish mandatory prosecutorial promotions, and enable judge reelection after mandate to promote accountability.[60] Party members like Roberto Giachetti have endorsed these measures to counter political interference and factionalism within the magistracy, arguing they represent a cultural shift toward a "just and efficient" system without succumbing to overzealous anti-corruption pursuits that risk eroding due process.[61] Italia Viva backs inappellability for acquittal sentences and CSM reforms to eliminate internal currents, aiming to reduce Italy's average trial durationâ€”which exceeds three years for civil cases and often doubles for criminal onesâ€”while avoiding excesses that could politicize the judiciary further.[62] On citizenship and social equity, the party opposes mandatory gender quotas, deeming them artificially divisive and counterproductive to merit-based advancement, as articulated by Matteo Renzi during the 2014 electoral law overhaul where his government rejected parity mandates despite voluntarily fielding balanced candidate slates.[63] This stance aligns with empirical observations that quotas can foster tokenism rather than substantive equality, prioritizing competence over imposed proportionality in institutional roles. Regarding immigration, Italia Viva promotes a pragmatic, integration-oriented approach focused on regulated, merit-based entry channels tailored to labor market needs, rejecting both alarmist rhetoric and unchecked inflows.[64] The party critiques narratives implying inevitable open borders, advocating EU-wide mechanisms for redistribution and frontline processing, as seen in Renzi's calls for competence-driven policies that balance security with selective welcomingâ€”evidenced by support for Albania repatriation centers and opposition to irregular surges, which rose 38% from Libya in early 2025 despite containment efforts.[65] Integration mandates, including language and civic courses, underscore causal links between assimilation and reduced social friction, drawing on data showing higher employment (over 16,200 refugee placements in 2024) correlates with structured programs.[66] Institutionally, Italia Viva favors streamlined federalismâ€”devolving powers efficiently without the extremes of rigid centralism or unchecked regional autonomyâ€”while pushing for direct prime ministerial election and abolition of perfect bicameralism to enhance executive stability.[67] This draws lessons from the 2016 constitutional referendum's rejection (59.1% no vote), where empirical analysis revealed voter concerns over power concentration but underscored stagnation from legislative gridlock, with Italy's GDP growth lagging EU averages by 0.5-1% annually pre-reform attempts.[68] The party contrasts this with Democratic Party centralism, arguing devolved competencies in health and education, post-2001 Title V reforms, improved service delivery in high-performing regions like Lombardy (per capita GDP 20% above national average) yet faltered without fiscal accountability.[69] In family policies, Italia Viva champions pro-natalist incentives via the Family Act, enacted in 2020 under party influence, providing universal monthly allowances from the seventh month of pregnancy through age 18â€”totaling up to â‚¬250 per childâ€”to counteract Italy's fertility rate of 1.24 births per woman in 2022.[70] These measures, including tax credits and work-life supports, target economic barriers to parenthood without prescriptive cultural mandates, with Minister Elena Bonetti emphasizing structural investments like â‚¬20 billion annually to boost female employment and reverse demographic decline, yielding initial upticks in family formation among lower-income households.[71] Empirical evidence from similar incentives links them to modest fertility gains (0.1-0.2 increase per policy intensity), prioritizing causal drivers like childcare access over ideological impositions.[72]

Foreign and European Policies

Italia Viva maintains a pro-European stance, advocating for deeper integration within the European Union, including enhanced common foreign policy and defense mechanisms such as a unified European army.[73] The party emphasizes the need for the EU to speak with a single voice on international matters, as articulated by leader Matteo Renzi in response to global trade challenges like U.S. tariffs, calling for robust countermeasures and reduced bureaucracy to strengthen the bloc's position.[74] This Europhile orientation contrasts with Euroskeptic sentiments prevalent among some Italian parties, positioning Italia Viva in favor of advancing fiscal mechanisms like shared borrowing, as evidenced by its historical push for flexible EU fiscal rules to support growth without exiting the framework.[56] In the context of the EU's Recovery and Resilience Facility, Italia Viva played a role in pressing for effective negotiations to secure Italy's substantial allocation of â‚¬191.5 billion in grants and loans, criticizing prior government handling as insufficiently assertive toward Brussels to maximize benefits for economic recovery.[75] [76] The party opposes exits akin to Brexit, viewing them as detrimental to Italy's interests, and promotes pro-trade globalization through initiatives like advancing the EU-Mercosur agreement to counter protectionist pressures.[77] On Atlanticist commitments, Italia Viva endorses Italy's active participation in NATO, opposing Russian aggression and supporting military aid to Ukraine, with Renzi affirming Russia's indisputable responsibility for the invasion while quitting boards of Russian firms post-2022.[78] [79] [80] The party favors arming Ukraine, diverging from pacifist elements in the Italian center-left that prioritize negotiations over weaponry.[81] Regarding Mediterranean priorities, Italia Viva backs balanced EU migration pacts that combine border management with legal pathways, aligning with broader pro-EU reforms to address irregular flows while diversifying energy sources away from Russian dependence to enhance security.[82] This approach underscores empirical realism in favoring cooperative frameworks over unilateral isolationism.[73]

Electoral Performance

National Parliamentary Elections

In the 2022 Italian general election held on September 25, Italia Viva, allied with Azione as part of the Third Pole, secured 7.78% of the proportional vote for the Chamber of Deputies, translating to approximately 800,000 votes, but obtained zero seats in either chamber due to the Rosatellum electoral system's requirements for coalition thresholds and majoritarian uninominal districts favoring larger blocs.[83] The alliance's independent positioning outside the centre-right or centre-left coalitions prevented proportional seat allocation despite surpassing the 3% national threshold for single lists, highlighting vote inefficiency in a system penalizing fragmented centrism.[40] Prior to Italia Viva's founding in 2019, Matteo Renzi led the Democratic Party (PD) to 18.76% of the proportional vote in the 2018 general election for the Chamber of Deputies, yielding 86 proportional seats amid a centre-left coalition total of 22.85%.[84] These results, representing Renzi's peak influence within PD, are not directly attributable to Italia Viva, though post-split analyses indicate the party's 2022 electorate drew significantly from former moderate PD supporters disillusioned with the party's leftward shift under subsequent leadership.[15]

European Parliament Elections

In the 2019 European Parliament elections, held on 26 May, Italia Viva did not field candidates, as the party was founded by Matteo Renzi in September 2019, four months after the vote. Founding members of Italia Viva, including Renzi, had been aligned with the Democratic Party (PD) prior to the split, which received 22.7% of the vote and secured 22 seats allocated to the Progressive Alliance of Socialists and Democrats (S&D) group.[86] This performance reflected broader support for centre-left forces at the time, though Renzi's emerging liberal orientation diverged from the PD's social-democratic positioning, foreshadowing the party's later affiliation with the more centrist Renew Europe group. For the 2024 European Parliament elections, conducted on 8 and 9 June, Italia Viva joined the "Stati Uniti d'Europa" electoral list with +Europa and radical-liberal elements, including figures like Emma Bonino, to contest under a pro-European, federalist banner aimed at Renew Europe. The list garnered 3.78% of the national vote but failed to surpass the 4% threshold for seat allocation, resulting in zero seats out of Italy's 76.[5] This outcome stemmed partly from vote fragmentation among centrists, as Italia Viva's former allies in Azione ran a separate "Azione â€“ Siamo Europei" list that received 3.36%, also yielding no seats; pre-election polls had suggested potential combined centrist support exceeding 7%, but internal divisions and a national turnout of approximately 49.7% contributed to underperformance.[50] The absence of seats limited Italia Viva's direct influence in the 2024â€“2029 parliamentary term, contrasting with EU-wide trends where Renew Europe maintained 80 seats overall amid rightward shifts in other groups, but Italian centrists struggled against dominant centre-right and populist forces like Fratelli d'Italia (28.8%, 24 seats).[5] Renzi, who did not secure a mandate, continued advocating for liberal reforms from his Senate position, emphasizing the need for a stronger "third pole" to counter polarization.[87]

Regional and Local Elections

In the 2020 Tuscan regional election, the Italia Viva list, allied with +Europa, received 4.4% of the valid votes, securing one seat in the 41-member regional council as part of the victorious centre-left coalition led by Eugenio Giani.[88] This performance reflected stronger support in urban areas like Florence, Renzi's former stronghold, where the party garnered higher localized vote shares compared to rural districts.[89] In other 2020 regional contests, such as Liguria and Puglia, Italia Viva participated in opposition coalitions but obtained marginal results, often below 3%, without securing independent council seats.[90] Local administrative elections have shown Italia Viva achieving greater efficacy in direct mayoral races and municipal councils, particularly in central Italy. In Tuscany and surrounding areas, the party supported or fielded candidates who won several mayoral positions in mid-sized towns post-2019, leveraging Renzi's personal networks in Florence and Prato. For instance, in 2021-2022 municipal contests, Italia Viva-backed lists elected dozens of councilors in coalitions, with notable successes in urban centers where centrist appeals resonated amid polarized national politics.[91] From 2023 to 2025, results were mixed, with erosion in proportional regional lists but retention in select local strongholds. In the 2024 Emilia-Romagna and Umbria regional elections, Italia Viva failed to elect any councilors despite alliances, reflecting a vote share under the threshold in both proportional and majoritarian components.[92] Conversely, in 2024 administrative elections across over 1,000 municipalities, the party elected hundreds of councilors and several mayors, tripling its local administrators from prior cycles, with stronger showings in direct contests (often 5-10% in centrist alliances) than proportional systems.[93] In southern regions like Campania and Calabria, performances lagged, with losses attributed to weaker organizational presence and competition from regionalist forces, though pragmatic alliances occasionally yielded council seats.[94] ![A_coloured_voting_box.svg.png][float-right] Empirical trends indicate Italia Viva's subnational persistence in Renzi-aligned urban enclaves, such as Tuscan metropolises, where vote shares held above 5% in 2025 locals despite national declines post-2022. However, in broader regional proportional races, the party often fell short of thresholds (e.g., 3-4% in 2025 Marche and Tuscany), highlighting challenges in scaling beyond direct elections without dominant coalitions.[95] Alliances shifted contextually, including support for centre-right incumbents in regions like Marche 2025, prioritizing viability over ideological consistency.[94]

Organization and Leadership

Party Leadership

Italia Viva was founded on September 18, 2019, by Matteo Renzi, who has served as its leader since inception and assumed the role of national president in 2022. Renzi, a former Prime Minister of Italy from February 22, 2014, to December 7, 2016, and previously secretary of the Democratic Party from 2013 to 2018, established the party after resigning from the PD due to policy disagreements. Under his leadership, the party has maintained a centralized structure without formal term limits for the presidency, reflecting Renzi's dominant influence.[96][97] From the party's formation until 2022, leadership was shared between co-presidents Ettore Rosato and Teresa Bellanova, who handled interim organizational roles amid early instability following the split from the PD. Bellanova, a key figure in agricultural policy, also served as Minister of Agriculture in the Conte II government from 2019 to 2021, representing Italia Viva's parliamentary presence. In a shift consolidating power, Renzi succeeded Rosato and Bellanova as sole president via a revised statute that expanded his nomination authority over party organs, underscoring low internal turnover and a personalized leadership model with minimal competitive elections.[97] On March 3, 2025, Renzi appointed senators Enrico Borghi and deputy Davide Faraone as vice presidents, assigning Borghi oversight on territorial coordination and Faraone focus on social and international outreach. These appointments, made without primary elections, highlight the absence of institutionalized succession mechanisms and ongoing reliance on Renzi's directives, amid reports of internal tensions but no formal challenges to his primacy. Figures like Ivan Scalfarotto have contributed to international relations but hold no current executive roles in the leadership core. Empirical data from party congresses shows infrequent leadership contests, with Renzi's tenure exceeding five years as of 2025, indicative of a founder-centric dynamic rather than broad intraparty democracy.[98]

Internal Structure and Membership

Italia Viva maintains a lightweight, centralized organizational structure emphasizing leadership-driven decision-making over extensive hierarchical layers, as outlined in its statute which prioritizes a "light party" model oriented toward the top echelons.[99] The national framework includes key bodies such as the National Committee, comprising members of right including President Matteo Renzi and parliamentary representatives, and the National Assembly for broader deliberations.[100] Territorial coordination occurs through regional and local coordinators who facilitate grassroots activities, though the party's operations remain predominantly summit-oriented with limited emphasis on formalized federal subunits.[101] Membership figures are not publicly detailed in official disclosures, reflecting the party's modest scale; however, it sustains engagement through tesseramento (membership enrollment) processes and has elected hundreds of local councilors and several mayors as of mid-2024, indicating a niche but active base of supporters and administrators.[102] Funding derives primarily from private donations, the 2x1000 tax designation for voluntary contributions, and reimbursements allocated to parliamentary groups by the Italian legislature, consistent with post-2013 reforms shifting away from direct public party financing toward indirect mechanisms and individual support.[2][103] Decision-making processes revolve around periodic assemblies and congresses, such as the National Assembly convened in September 2024, where strategic directions are debated and ratified under Renzi's presiding influence, fostering a dynamic yet leader-centric approach.[104] The party lacks a prominently structured youth wing, relying instead on broader recruitment via digital platforms and events to attract younger participants, though this has not offset occasional internal attrition, including notable defections like that of deputy Luigi Marattin in September 2024 citing strategic disagreements.[105] These elements underscore Italia Viva's viability as a compact, agile entity sustained by parliamentary leverage rather than mass membership.

Controversies and Criticisms

Accusations of Opportunism and Instability

Italia Viva and its leader Matteo Renzi have faced accusations from politicians in the Democratic Party (PD) and Five Star Movement (M5S) of fostering political instability for personal or partisan advantage, particularly through strategic withdrawals from coalitions that precipitated government crises. In January 2021, Renzi directed Italia Viva ministers Elena Bonetti and Teresa Bellanova to resign from Giuseppe Conte's second cabinet, depriving the coalition of its Senate majority and triggering a national crisis amid the COVID-19 pandemic; Conte publicly attributed the collapse to Italia Viva's "serious responsibility" in opening the impasse, echoing earlier critiques of Renzi's 2016 resignation as prime minister following the defeat of his constitutional referendum on December 4, which he had conditioned on his own tenure, leading to Paolo Gentiloni's interim PD-led government.[106][107] Similar patterns trace to Renzi's 2014 ouster of Enrico Letta's PD government to assume the premiership himself, contributing to perceptions of serial maneuvering that has correlated with five executive transitions between 2014 and 2021.[107] Right-wing commentators and figures, including allies of Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, have portrayed Renzi's centrist shiftsâ€”such as Italia Viva's initial support for Conte's 2019-2021 coalition after Renzi's 2019 PD split, followed by the 2021 exitâ€”as emblematic of unreliable flip-flopping that erodes governance stability, contrasting it with Meloni's steadfast coalition maintenance since 2022. These actions are said to prioritize short-term leverage over consistent policy execution, exacerbating Italy's historical executive fragility, where average government duration from 2011-2021 hovered around 17 months excluding Renzi's outlier term.[108][109] Renzi and Italia Viva have countered these charges by framing withdrawals as principled interventions to avert deeper failures in coalition efficacy, asserting in 2021 that the Conte government lacked institutional rigor and adequate preparation for EU recovery resources, ultimately paving the way for Mario Draghi's broader national unity cabinet in February 2021; Renzi emphasized that the move was not aimed at personal gain but at compelling a more competent administration, with Italia Viva subsequently backing Draghi until its 2022 withdrawal over unrelated fiscal disputes.[4][110]

Policy and Ethical Criticisms

Critics from the political left have argued that Italia Viva's endorsement of Matteo Renzi's earlier Jobs Act labor reforms, implemented in 2015, exacerbated precarious employment by easing dismissal protections and promoting temporary contracts without sufficiently addressing underlying structural issues. Empirical analyses indicate that the share of temporary jobs in Italy remained elevated post-reform, with youth employment rates lagging at around 31.1% for ages 15-29 in recent years, second-lowest in Europe, and studies showing the Jobs Act failed to significantly reduce atypical work or boost overall employment levels. Inequality metrics, such as persistent Gini coefficients hovering near 0.35, have been cited to claim the reforms favored flexibility over security, leading to polarized labor markets without commensurate wage gains.[111][112][113] From the right, Italia Viva's pro-European Union orientation has drawn accusations of prioritizing supranational integration over national sovereignty, with detractors contending that its support for EU fiscal rules and migration frameworks undermines Italy's autonomous decision-making on borders and budgets. Right-wing commentators, including those aligned with Giorgia Meloni's government, have criticized centrist parties like Italia Viva for insufficient enforcement of migration controls, viewing their reluctance to challenge EU pacts as enabling unchecked inflows that strain resources without reciprocal burden-sharing from other member states. This stance is seen as contrasting with stricter national policies, such as Italy's post-2022 deals with Albania for migrant processing, which Italia Viva has not championed.[114][115] Ethical concerns have centered on Renzi's personal and familial financial dealings, including allegations of irregularities in his parents' business invoicing, though they were acquitted by Italy's Cassation Court in July 2023 after initial charges of false billing. During Renzi's premiership, the government's handling of the Monte dei Paschi di Siena bailout in December 2016â€”following the bank's failure to raise â‚¬5 billion in private capital amid â‚¬46 billion in bad loansâ€”faced scrutiny for potential favoritism toward local Tuscan interests and delays in addressing systemic banking risks, contributing to perceptions of cronyism despite the intervention averting immediate collapse. Italia Viva has countered such policy critiques by highlighting economic upticks under Renzi's reforms, including Italy's GDP growth of 0.8% in 2015 and acceleration to 0.9% year-on-year in Q3 2016, alongside pension adjustments that relaxed early retirement options to balance fiscal sustainability with worker flexibility, though these measures proved unpopular and yielded mixed long-term distributional effects.[116][117][118][119]

Reception from Left and Right Perspectives

From the perspective of the Italian left, particularly the Democratic Party (PD), Italia Viva's formation in September 2019 by former PD leader Matteo Renzi has been widely regarded as an act of betrayal that fragmented the center-left coalition and undermined efforts to present a united front against right-wing parties. PD secretary Nicola Zingaretti described Italia Viva as "untrustworthy" in January 2021, reflecting broader sentiments that Renzi's departure siphoned off moderate voters and resources from the PD, reducing its parliamentary presence by approximately one-fourth shortly after the split.[120][15] This fragmentation was seen as exacerbating governmental instability, such as Renzi's withdrawal of support from Giuseppe Conte's second cabinet in January 2021, which critics argued indirectly paved the way for the right's electoral gains by preventing cohesive left-wing governance.[121] Polls and electoral analyses have substantiated claims of vote splitting, with Italia Viva drawing support primarily from former PD voters, thereby diluting the center-left's overall share in subsequent elections and benefiting the center-right coalition. For instance, pre-2022 general election surveys indicated that divisions between the PD and smaller centrist groups like Italia Viva contributed to the right's projected outright majority, as fragmented opposition votes failed to consolidate under a single banner. Left-wing commentators have attributed this dynamic to Renzi's emphasis on pro-business liberalism over traditional social democratic priorities, viewing it as a self-serving maneuver that prioritized personal ambition over collective anti-right strategy.[122] On the right, reception has been mixed, portraying Italia Viva as a tactical enabler of political volatility useful for disrupting left-leaning governments but ultimately a competitive threat to moderate conservative elements like Forza Italia. Right-wing leaders, including Matteo Salvini of the Lega, have criticized Renzi's interventionsâ€”such as the 2021 government crisisâ€”as opportunistic ploys that aligned temporarily with right interests by toppling Conte but failed to forge lasting alliances, positioning Italia Viva as an unreliable "third pole" rivaling Silvio Berlusconi's more establishment-oriented Forza Italia. This view frames Italia Viva's centrist positioning as diluting potential moderate right coalitions while its pro-EU, reformist stance clashes with the nationalist populism of Fratelli d'Italia and Lega, limiting cooperation beyond ad hoc technocratic cabinets like Mario Draghi's (2021â€“2022). Analyses suggest that while Italia Viva's instability contributed to the right's 2022 landslide by weakening the left, it has since complicated right-wing dominance in a multipolar system by appealing to urban, pro-market voters wary of populist extremes.[123] Public opinion polls reflect low personal trust in Renzi, with approval ratings historically hovering in the low 20s to 30s amid perceptions of serial party-switching and government-toppling, though Italia Viva retains niche credit for earlier Renzi-era reforms like labor market liberalization. This duality underscores how Italia Viva's persistence exposes underlying rigidities in the PD's post-Renzi evolution, fostering a fragmented center-left that, through causal fragmentation of voter bases, has empirically aided right-wing consolidation without resolving systemic bipolar tensions in Italian politics.[15]
