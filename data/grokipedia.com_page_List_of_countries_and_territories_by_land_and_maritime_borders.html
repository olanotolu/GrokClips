List of countries and territories by land and maritime borders

This list enumerates the land and maritime boundaries shared by sovereign states, dependent territories, and other entities, specifying the number and identities of contiguous neighbors for each, based on recognized international delimitations and ongoing disputes. Land borders consist of terrestrial frontiers where dry land meets, typically measured in kilometers of shared boundary length, while maritime borders delineate oceanic zones such as territorial seas (extending up to 12 nautical miles from baselines) and exclusive economic zones (up to 200 nautical miles), as codified under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).[1] These boundaries underpin sovereignty claims, resource exploitation, and security arrangements, with land borders facilitating direct terrestrial interactions and maritime ones governing fisheries, seabed minerals, and navigation rights.[2] Among notable patterns, China and Russia each maintain the highest number of land neighbors at 14, reflecting their expansive continental footprints, whereas island nations like Australia possess no land borders but extensive maritime ones with multiple states via EEZ overlaps.[3][4] Defining characteristics include the prevalence of disputed segmentsâ€”such as those in the South China Sea involving China and several Southeast Asian claimants, or contested land frontiers like Kashmir between India and Pakistanâ€”which arise from historical ambiguities, resource stakes, or irredentist assertions, often complicating mutual recognition of entities like Kosovo or the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus.[5][6] Such lists underscore causal factors in border formation, including colonial legacies and post-colonial realignments, while highlighting how empirical border counts influence trade volumes, migration flows, and conflict risks, independent of normative interpretations of state legitimacy.[7]

Definitions and Methodology

Defining Land Borders

Land borders constitute the lines delimiting the terrestrial territories placed under the sovereignty of distinct states, requiring physical contiguity where landmasses of adjacent sovereign entities directly adjoin. These boundaries are grounded in international law's emphasis on effective territorial jurisdiction, typically formalized through delimitation agreements that define the line abstractly, often followed by demarcation to mark it on the ground for practical enforcement.[8][9] Only verifiable, currently operative borders qualify, based on either de jure recognition via treaties and international acceptance or de facto control through sustained state presence, excluding historical claims lacking contemporary enforcement or proposed lines without delimitation. Linear infrastructure such as pipelines or railways does not establish land borders independently, as these elements traverse pre-existing territorial delimitations and remain subject to the overlying sovereignty of the states involved rather than defining sovereign extents.[10][11] Borders involving recognized dependencies or integral territories of a state are attributed to the sovereign entity; for instance, France's overseas department of French Guiana, constitutionally part of the French Republic, contributes land borders with Brazil (730 km) and Suriname, extending France's terrestrial adjacencies beyond Europe.[12]

Defining Maritime Borders

Maritime borders delineate the jurisdictional limits of coastal states' ocean areas, governed principally by the 1982 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), which entered into force on November 16, 1994, and has been ratified by 169 parties as of 2023. These borders define adjacency between states whose maritime zonesâ€”territorial seas and exclusive economic zones (EEZs)â€”overlap due to coastal proximity, prioritizing delimited lines over unilateral assertions of extended influence.[13] UNCLOS establishes the territorial sea as extending up to 12 nautical miles from a state's baseline, conferring full sovereignty akin to land territory, subject only to innocent passage for foreign vessels.[14] The EEZ extends seaward up to 200 nautical miles, granting sovereign rights over natural resources, economic activities, and environmental protection, while preserving high seas freedoms like navigation for non-coastal states.[1] Delimitation of maritime borders occurs where zones of adjacent states intersect, determined first by mutual agreement to achieve precise, enforceable boundaries; absent agreement, UNCLOS prescribes default methods favoring empirical geometry over subjective equity alone. For territorial seas, Article 15 mandates a median line or equidistance from baselines unless historic title or special circumstances justify deviation, ensuring adjacency is verifiable through coordinate-based lines.[13] EEZ and continental shelf boundaries, under Articles 74 and 83, require agreements yielding an "equitable solution," often starting with a provisional equidistance line adjusted for relevant geographic factors like coastline length or island presence, as interpreted by the International Court of Justice in cases such as the 2002 Cameroon v. Nigeria ruling.[13] These provisions emphasize bilateral or multilateral treaties, as in the 1977 US-Mexico Gulf of Mexico agreement, which fixed a geodesic boundary via straight baselines and turning points, over unilateral claims lacking reciprocal recognition.[15] Empirical measurement of maritime borders relies on geodesic linesâ€”arcs of great circles approximating Earth's curvatureâ€”for accuracy beyond 1 nautical mile, contrasting with vague "spheres of influence" by providing surveyable coordinates integrable into global navigation systems like GPS.[13] Contested areas without treaties, such as the South China Sea overlaps beyond UNCLOS baselines, do not automatically confer adjacency for border counts unless provisional arrangements or arbitral rulings (e.g., the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration award rejecting excessive historic claims) establish de jure limits.[15] Thus, maritime neighbors are states sharing such delimited or overlapping zones per UNCLOS criteria, excluding unilateral extensions like Russia's Arctic shelf claim beyond 200 nautical miles, which requires Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf approval and adjacent state consent for validity. This framework privileges verifiable legal delimitations, mitigating disputes through technical precision rather than power-based assertions.[13]

Counting Conventions and Exclusions

Land borders are enumerated by the number of distinct sovereign states or territories sharing any portion of a continuous or segmented terrestrial boundary, treating federations and unitary states alike as single entities regardless of internal subdivisions. For example, Russia maintains land borders with 14 such neighbors: Norway, Finland, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland (via Kaliningrad Oblast), Belarus, Ukraine, Georgia, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, China, Mongolia, and North Korea.[4] [16] Multiple land border segments between the same pair of entities, arising from enclaves, exclaves, or geographical interruptions, are consolidated into one count. The United States-Canada land boundary comprises two primary segmentsâ€”approximately 8,891 km total, including 2,475 km adjacent to Alaskaâ€”yet is recorded as a single neighbor pair. Exclusions encompass internal administrative divisions, such as boundaries between provinces, states, or autonomous regions within a sovereign state, which lack international legal status. Antarctic territorial claims by states including Argentina, Australia, Chile, France, New Zealand, Norway, and the United Kingdom are omitted, as these overlap without mutual recognition and remain suspended under the 1959 Antarctic Treaty, prohibiting new assertions or enlargement of existing ones.[17] [18] Provisional borders, such as temporary demarcations during unresolved conflicts lacking treaty formalization or sustained de facto enforcement, are likewise excluded to prioritize verifiable, enduring international frontiers.[19] Maritime borders adhere to parallel principles, registering the number of distinct coastal states with which a given entity shares boundaries in territorial seas (up to 12 nautical miles), contiguous zones (up to 24 nautical miles), exclusive economic zones (EEZs, up to 200 nautical miles), or extended continental shelves, as defined under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).[20] Segmented or multi-zone delimitations with the same stateâ€”common in archipelagic or island-dotted configurationsâ€”are aggregated as one. Exclusions mirror land practices, barring intra-national maritime divisions (e.g., between overseas territories and metropole under unified sovereignty) and suspended claims in treaty-frozen zones like Antarctica's surrounding seas.[21] This approach ensures reproducibility by relying on geospatial data from baselines, envelope-of-arcs constructions, and bilateral agreements rather than provisional overlaps.

Disputed and Contested Borders

Major Ongoing Territorial Disputes

The Kashmir region, divided since the 1947 partition of British India, exemplifies a multifaceted territorial dispute involving land borders and strategic highland areas spanning approximately 222,000 square kilometers. India administers about 55% (Jammu, Kashmir Valley, Ladakh), Pakistan controls 30% (Azad Kashmir, Gilgit-Baltistan), and China holds 15% (Aksai Chin, Shaksgam Valley), with de facto boundaries fixed by the 1972 Simla Agreement for India-Pakistan and the 1962 Sino-Indian War aftermath.[34][35] India asserts sovereignty over the entirety based on the 1947 Instrument of Accession by Maharaja Hari Singh and subsequent elections, rejecting plebiscites as outdated amid demographic changes and insurgency suppression.[36] Pakistan claims Muslim-majority areas via UN Security Council Resolution 47 (1948) calling for a plebiscite, emphasizing self-determination to counter Indian "occupation," while supporting insurgent groups historically.[37] China justifies Aksai Chin control through Qing Dynasty maps and the 1963 Sino-Pakistani boundary agreement ceding Shaksgam, prioritizing connectivity between Xinjiang and Tibet over irredentist arguments.[38] De facto control prevails amid periodic clashes, such as the 1999 Kargil conflict and 2020 Galwan Valley incident, with legal recognition fragmentedâ€”most states recognize Indian and Pakistani administrations but not China's full claimsâ€”highlighting tensions between historical assertions and stability via militarized lines of control.[39] Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea, a 27,000-square-kilometer Black Sea peninsula, represents a unilateral border alteration via military intervention, altering Ukraine's land and maritime boundaries. Russian forces seized control in February 2014, followed by a March referendum under occupation reporting 97% support for accession, which Russia cites as self-determination akin to Kosovo's 2008 independence.[40] Ukraine maintains sovereignty under its 1991 borders per the Budapest Memorandum (1994), where Russia pledged territorial integrity in exchange for nuclear disarmament, viewing the annexation as invalid aggression.[41] International bodies, including UN General Assembly Resolution 68/262 (2014) affirmed by subsequent votes, recognize Crimea as Ukrainian, with 100+ states rejecting integration into Russia's Southern Federal District as of 2025; de facto Russian control includes militarized borders, passportization, and resource extraction, enforced despite sanctions and Ukrainian counteroffensives.[42][43] Proposals for recognition, tied to peace talks, risk eroding post-WWII norms against conquest, favoring empirical stability under current administration over legal reversion, though Ukraine prioritizes full recovery to deter revanchism.[44][45] In the South China Sea, China's "nine-dash line" asserts historic rights over roughly 2 million square kilometers, including land features like the Spratly and Paracel Islands, contested by maritime neighbors and incompatible with exclusive economic zones under UNCLOS. The 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration award, initiated by the Philippines, invalidated the line's excess claims, ruling features as rocks or low-tide elevations generating no territorial seas and affirming no historic rights beyond UNCLOS entitlements.[46][47] China rejects the ruling as biased, maintaining de facto control via island-building on seven Spratly outposts (adding 3,200 acres since 2013) and naval patrols, enforcing fishing bans and resource claims against Vietnam (Paracels since 1974), Philippines (Scarborough Shoal 2012 standoff), and others.[48][49] Claimant states invoke UNCLOS submissions for extended continental shelves, prioritizing legal baselines over China's cartographic assertions from 1947 maps, with de facto militarization escalating incidents like 2024 laser confrontations, balancing resource access (oil, fisheries) against freedom-of-navigation operations by the US and allies.[50] The Kuril Islands dispute, unresolved since World War II, pits Japan's claim to the southern four islands (Etorofu, Kunashiri, Shikotan, Habomai, totaling 5,000 square kilometers) against Russia's administration following 1945 Soviet occupation. Japan grounds sovereignty in continuous pre-1905 possession under the 1855 Shimoda Treaty and 1875 exchange, excluding them from Yalta concessions as ethnic Japanese territory.[51] Russia defends retention via Potsdam Declaration outcomes and WWII spoils, rejecting return as undermining victory gains, with de facto borders sealed by garrisons and no demilitarization despite 1956 Soviet offer of two islands (unaccepted).[52] Talks persist without peace treaty, as 2024-2025 sanctions over Ukraine hardened positions, weighing historical restitution against strategic buffering from Pacific threats.[53][54] The Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands, an East China Sea archipelago of five uninhabited islets administered by Japan since 1972 reversion from US trusteeship, face Chinese sovereignty challenges based on Ming Dynasty records, contrasting Japan's incorporation in 1895 as terra nullius. China deploys coast guard vessels intruding territorial waters 300+ days annually since 2008, asserting administrative rights without effective control, while Japan enforces via patrols and denies negotiations pending recognition of its title.[55][56] The dispute links to broader EEZ overlaps, with de facto Japanese patrols maintaining status quo amid 2012 nationalization sparking Chinese protests, prioritizing empirical administration over contested history to avert escalation near Taiwan straits.[57][58]

Effects of Disputes on Official Border Counts

Territorial disputes introduce variability in official border counts by creating tensions between de jure sovereigntyâ€”recognized under international lawâ€”and de facto control exercised through military or administrative means. De jure borders establish legally binding delineations that prioritize formal agreements and recognition, fostering stability in quantitative assessments, whereas reliance on de facto realities can lead to inflated counts for aggressors or reduced tallies for claimants based on transient occupations. Scholarly examination of interstate disputes reveals that de jure boundary settlements confer definitive territorial title, mitigating escalation risks more effectively than provisional de facto lines, which often perpetuate ambiguity in neighbor enumerations.[59] A prominent example is Israel's administration of the Golan Heights, seized from Syria in 1967 and annexed domestically in 1981, where de jure Syrian claims persist despite limited international acknowledgment of Israeli sovereignty. This results in divergent land border tallies for Israel, with some sources counting a border with Syria based on legal claims while others omit it in favor of de facto isolation, yielding neighbor figures ranging from four (Egypt, Jordan, Lebanon, Palestine) to five when including the disputed frontier.[60] In Serbia's case, non-recognition of Kosovo's 2008 independence declaration leads to de jure assertions of provincial status, theoretically extending Serbia's borders to encompass Kosovo's interfaces with Albania and North Macedonia, yet de facto partition typically confines Serbian counts to seven or eight neighbors, excluding these as separate international boundaries.[61] The 2023 Azerbaijani military operation in Nagorno-Karabakh further illustrates dispute resolution's impact, as full reassertion of de jure control over the enclave aligned physical borders with legal claims against Armenia, previously diminished in counts due to the unrecognized entity's de facto autonomy since 1994. Such shifts can retroactively adjust official tallies, reducing Azerbaijan's enumerated Armenian frontier segments in pre-2023 data. To preserve analytical rigor, border compilations should employ dual metricsâ€”separating de jure recognized counts from de facto or claimed variantsâ€”enabling transparent evaluation of dispute-induced distortions without subordinating legal constructs to empirical contingencies.[62]

Unrecognized Entities and De Facto Borders

Unrecognized entities, often termed de facto states, maintain effective territorial control and administer internal borders but receive diplomatic recognition from only a handful of countries, typically patrons like Russia or Turkey, rendering their external frontiers de facto rather than de jure in international assessments. These borders arise from secessionist conflicts and frozen disputes, where local authorities enforce checkpoints, customs, and military lines, yet global bodies such as the United Nations attribute sovereignty to the parent state, subsuming these lines into the latter's official border counts. For instance, quantitative analyses of land borders exclude standalone tallies for such entities, treating Abkhazia's boundaries as extensions of Georgia's administrative divisions despite physical separation since the 2008 Russo-Georgian War.[63] Prominent examples include Transnistria, which declared independence from Moldova in 1990 following ethnic tensions and a brief 1992 war, controlling a narrow strip along the Dniester River with de facto borders patrolled by Russian peacekeepers and local forces; internationally, these are mapped as Moldova's eastern frontier with Ukraine, with Moldova's land border count incorporating the segment without crediting Transnistria separately.[64][65] Similarly, Somaliland has governed its claimed territory since unilaterally dissolving its 1960 union with Somalia in 1991 amid civil war chaos, enforcing a 1,600 km de facto border including checkpoints at the Berbera corridor; however, official lists assign Somalia's 2,366 km land borders to the Federal Republic, encompassing Somaliland's lines without independent enumeration, as no UN member beyond informal acknowledgments recognizes its sovereignty.[66][67] In the case of Abkhazia and South Ossetia, both broke from Georgia after conflicts in the 1990s, with Russia recognizing their independence in 2008 alongside four other states (Venezuela, Nicaragua, Nauru, Syria); Abkhazia administers a 221 km de facto border with Georgia proper, fortified since the war, while South Ossetia's 80 km line similarly divides ethnic Ossetian areas, yet Georgia's international border rosterâ€”totaling 1,814 kmâ€”includes these as internal lines, excluding them from counts of neighboring states like Russia or Azerbaijan. The Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (TRNC), proclaimed in 1983 after Turkey's 1974 intervention, controls 36% of Cyprus with a Green Line buffer zone enforced by UN peacekeepers serving as its de facto southern border; Cyprus's official land border metrics, however, treat the entire island's perimeter as national, nullifying TRNC's 180 km internal division in global tallies.[68][69] These arrangements reflect a prioritization of territorial integrity over unilateral secession in international law, as codified in Article 2(4) of the UN Charter, which obliges members to abstain from force threatening a state's political independence or borders, thereby delegitimizing breakaway claims absent mutual consent or broad consensus.[70] Separatist advocates emphasize self-determination for ethnic majoritiesâ€”such as Abkhazians (46% of Abkhazia's population per 2011 census) or Transnistrian Russians/Moldovansâ€”but empirical sovereignty remains illusory without UN admission or trade pacts, confining these entities to patron-dependent economies and isolated diplomacy; parent states like Georgia and Moldova thus retain de jure border authority in treaties and statistics, underscoring how de facto control alone fails to confer recognized frontiers.[71]

Historical Context

Evolution of Modern International Borders

The modern concept of international borders crystallized with the Peace of Westphalia, signed on October 24, 1648, which concluded the Thirty Years' War and introduced the foundational principle of state sovereignty, wherein rulers held exclusive authority within defined territories free from external religious or political interference.[72] This system emphasized territorial integrity as a means to prevent the incessant European conflicts driven by dynastic and confessional claims, establishing borders as juridical lines of mutual recognition among equals rather than fluid conquest zones.[73] The treaties empowered states to conduct independent foreign policies and alliances, shifting from medieval universalism to a decentralized order where non-interference preserved stability by aligning authority with geographic delimitations.[74] Colonial expansion from the 16th to 20th centuries overlaid this framework with impositions that often disregarded local realities, culminating in arbitrary demarcations like the Sykes-Picot Agreement of May 1916, a secret Anglo-French pact that partitioned Ottoman Arab provinces into spheres of influence using straight lines that bisected ethnic, tribal, and sectarian groups.[75] Such delineations, motivated by imperial resource control rather than organic polities, sowed seeds of discord by enclosing heterogeneous populations within artificial confines, fostering irredentism and proxy conflicts as causal precursors to regional volatility.[76] While not the sole architect of postcolonial tensionsâ€”local nationalisms and power politics also shaped outcomesâ€”these borders exemplified a departure from Westphalian mutual consent, prioritizing extraterritorial administration over sustainable sovereignty.[77] Post-1945 decolonization reinforced border stability through the United Nations Charter, adopted June 26, 1945, which in Article 2(4) codified the prohibition against threats or uses of force impairing territorial integrity or political independence, extending Westphalian norms to a global scale.[78] The uti possidetis juris doctrine, applied rigorously in Africa and Latin America from the 1960s onward, mandated that emergent states retain colonial administrative frontiers upon independence to avert partitioning chaos, as affirmed in the 1964 Cairo Resolution of the Organization of African Unity. This pragmatic inheritance, rooted in causal deterrence of balkanization, privileged empirical continuity and interstate recognition over ethnic or ideological revisions, thereby mitigating the proliferation of micro-states and resource wars inherent in redrawn maps.

Key Border Changes Since 1945

The dissolution of the Soviet Union, formalized on December 26, 1991, through the Belavezha Accords and subsequent Alma-Ata Protocol, fragmented the federation into 15 independent republics, converting internal administrative boundaries into international land borders.[79] Russia, as the successor state, retained the Union's pre-existing external frontiers with non-Soviet neighbors but acquired new land borders with 14 former republics, including Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania (via the Kaliningrad exclave), Belarus, Ukraine, Georgia, Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan, China (expanded), Mongolia, and North Korea, alongside Norway, Finland, and Poland (also via Kaliningrad).[80] This reconfiguration multiplied Russia's land border count from the Soviet era's consolidated interfaces to 14 distinct sovereign neighbors, while the new states collectively gained borders with Russia and each other where previously contiguous republics met.[81] The breakup of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia during the 1990s, initiated by declarations of independence from Slovenia and Croatia on June 25, 1991, followed by Bosnia and Herzegovina and North Macedonia in 1992, dismantled the federation into multiple sovereign entities and redrew regional land borders across the Balkans.[82] The Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (comprising Serbia and Montenegro) emerged as the core remnant, inheriting some external borders but facing newly internationalized frontiers with the seceding states, which absorbed segments of Yugoslavia's prior land boundaries with Austria, Italy, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Greece, and Albania.[83] This process effectively reduced Serbia's direct external neighbor count in the short term by interposing new states between it and original neighbors, while proliferating bilateral borders among the successorsâ€”Slovenia gained three (Austria, Italy, Croatia), Croatia four (Slovenia, Hungary, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Serbia), and so onâ€”escalating the total number of land border segments in the region.[84] The independence of South Sudan on July 9, 2011, following a January 2011 referendum where 98.83% voted for secession from Sudan, established a new international land border approximately 2,135 kilometers long between the two states, replacing what had been an internal division.[85] This added South Sudan to Sudan's roster of land neighbors (now including Egypt, Libya, Chad, Central African Republic, Ethiopia, Eritrea, Kenya, and Uganda via South Sudan), increasing Sudan's land border count from seven to eight, while South Sudan itself debuted with five land neighbors: Sudan, Ethiopia, Kenya, Uganda, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo.[86] The change stemmed from the 2005 Comprehensive Peace Agreement's provisions for self-determination, though subsequent disputes over border areas like Abyei and oil fields underscored the shift's instabilities without altering the core bilateral boundary recognition.[87] The Velvet Divorce of Czechoslovakia on January 1, 1993, peacefully separated the federation into the Czech Republic and Slovakia along existing internal lines, creating a new 265-kilometer land border between the two without territorial disputes or violence. Each successor inherited portions of the original state's external bordersâ€”Czechia with Poland, Germany, Austria, and Slovakia; Slovakia with Poland, Ukraine, Hungary, Austria, and Czechiaâ€”resulting in both states gaining one additional land neighbor apiece compared to the unified entity's consolidated frontiers. This amicable partition contrasted with more conflictual secessions but similarly transformed administrative lines into sovereign boundaries, contributing to the post-1945 trend of federation dissolutions fragmenting Europe's map.

Recent Developments and Challenges

Border Adjustments 2020â€“2025

In the period from 2020 to 2025, formal land border adjustments via ratified treaties remained rare, with most shifts arising from de facto military outcomes or delimitations lacking universal recognition. Verified changes were concentrated in the South Caucasus and select maritime domains, while unilateral annexations elsewhere, such as Russia's 2022 claims over Ukrainian territories, failed to alter internationally accepted border counts due to widespread non-recognition. These developments underscore a persistence of frozen conflicts over peaceful redemptions, with no large-scale land cessions documented through bilateral agreements.[88] A significant de facto adjustment occurred in September 2023 when Azerbaijan launched a military offensive against the self-declared Republic of Artsakh in Nagorno-Karabakh, prompting the surrender of Armenian-backed forces on September 20 and the subsequent exodus of over 100,000 ethnic Armenians. This operation restored Azerbaijani sovereignty over the entire region, which had been under Armenian control since the early 1990s, effectively resolving the enclave's disputed status without immediate formal demarcation. The Artsakh leadership dissolved state institutions on January 1, 2024, formalizing the reintegration. In April 2024, Armenia transferred control of four border villagesâ€”Baghanis Ayrum, Ashagh Askipara, Khoznavar, and Kirantsâ€”to Azerbaijan as part of preliminary delimitation under a November 2021 agreement, marking one of the few consensual land adjustments. Peace negotiations advanced with a draft treaty finalized in March 2025, addressing mutual recognition and enclave exchanges, though Azerbaijan conditioned signing on Armenia's constitutional revisions disclaiming territorial claims.[89][90] Russia's September 30, 2022, annexation of Ukraine's Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia oblastsâ€”following staged referendums in occupied areasâ€”expanded de facto control over approximately 18% of Ukraine's territory but garnered no substantive international endorsement. The United Nations General Assembly adopted Resolution ES-11/4 on October 12, 2022, by a vote of 143-5, declaring the annexations invalid under international law and urging non-recognition, thereby preserving pre-2022 border delineations in official tallies. As of October 2025, no states beyond Russia and its allies have acknowledged these changes, maintaining the conflict's frozen character.[88] On the maritime front, the United States announced the outer limits of its extended continental shelf on December 19, 2023, extending jurisdiction beyond 200 nautical miles across seven regions including the Arctic, Atlantic East Coast, and Bering Sea, encompassing over 1 million square kilometers. This delineation, based on geological and geophysical data submitted to the UN Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf, overlaps potential claims by Canada, The Bahamas, and Japan but involves no land alterations and requires bilateral negotiations for boundary fixes. It adds prospective maritime neighbors without shifting land border counts.[91][92]

Maritime Claims and Climate Impacts

The People's Republic of China's "nine-dash line" claim, encompassing approximately 90% of the South China Sea, has been contested by the Philippines, Vietnam, and other littoral states on grounds of exceeding UNCLOS entitlements to territorial seas, contiguous zones, EEZs, and continental shelves. A tribunal under Annex VII of UNCLOS, constituted at the Permanent Court of Arbitration, ruled on July 12, 2016, that the nine-dash line lacks legal basis under international law, invalidating historic rights claims beyond those permitted by maritime zones measured from baselines, and affirming low-tide elevations like Mischief Reef generate no EEZ. China dismissed the award as "null and void," continuing activities such as island-building that the tribunal deemed environmentally harmful and violative of Philippines' sovereign rights.[47] Vietnam has echoed these legal arguments, citing overlapping EEZ claims with China and invoking the ruling to bolster its positions on features like the Paracel Islands.[93] Expansive claims of this nature prioritize unilateral assertions over delimited zones, fostering tensions without resolving underlying geographic entitlements under UNCLOS Article 121, which distinguishes islands generating full zones from rocks limited to territorial seas. Such disputes underscore the preference for negotiated delimitations or third-party adjudication to maintain stability, as provisional arrangements under UNCLOS Article 74 do not prejudice final outcomes. Climate-induced sea-level rise poses theoretical challenges to baselines from which maritime zones are measured, yet legal frameworks emphasize continuity to preserve sovereignty. The International Court of Justice's Advisory Opinion on state obligations regarding climate change, delivered July 23, 2025, held that UNCLOS does not require states to revise established coastal baselines or zone limits due to sea-level changes, allowing retention of fixed EEZs and continental shelf entitlements despite erosion or inundation.[94] This position aligns with state practice, as articulated in the Pacific Islands Forum's 2021 Declaration on Preserving Maritime Zones, which advocates fixed baselines to avert loss of jurisdiction for vulnerable atolls.[95] Empirically, global mean sea-level rise averaged 3.7 mm per year from 2006 to 2015, accelerating modestly to about 4.6 mm per year post-2015, with coastal erosion rates varying by siteâ€”e.g., 0.5â€“1 meter per year in some Pacific low-lying areasâ€”but offset in others by accretion from sediment or reefs.[96] These changes induce localized flooding and habitat shifts rather than wholesale maritime border relocations, as zones derive from legal baselines rather than instantaneous physical contours; historical precedents, like post-glacial adjustments, show boundaries endure via treaties absent renegotiation.[97] Prioritizing ambulatory baselines would destabilize entitlements without causal evidence of uniform submergence, favoring stability under UNCLOS to mitigate disputes over resources like fisheries comprising 12â€“15% of global capture.[98]

Territories and Special Cases

Overseas Territories and Dependencies

Overseas territories and dependencies are subnational entities under the sovereignty of a parent state, often geographically remote, whose land and maritime boundaries are attributed to the administering country in standard geopolitical tallies. This inclusion amplifies the border counts of states like France, the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, and the United States by incorporating exclusive economic zones (EEZs) and territorial seas adjacent to these territories. For example, the United Kingdom's Falkland Islands, located in the South Atlantic approximately 480 kilometers from Argentina's coast, generate maritime boundaries that overlap with Argentine claims, effectively adding a disputed adjacency to the UK's portfolio despite the absence of a land border.[99][100] France exemplifies extensive border extension through its overseas departments and collectivities; French Guiana shares land borders of 730 kilometers with Brazil and 600 kilometers with Suriname, directly contributing to France's total terrestrial neighbors beyond Europe. Maritime boundaries from territories like New Caledonia in the Pacific adjoin those of Australia, Vanuatu, and Solomon Islands, while French Southern and Antarctic Lands assert claims in the Southern Ocean bordering Australian and Norwegian Antarctic territories. These attributions yield France approximately 10-11 unique neighboring states when overseas holdings are factored in, far exceeding metropolitan France's eight continental borders.[101] The Netherlands' Caribbean special municipalitiesâ€”Bonaire, Sint Eustatius, and Sabaâ€”establish maritime boundaries with Venezuela, as delimited in bilateral agreements delineating EEZs around the ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire, CuraÃ§ao), enhancing the Netherlands' transoceanic adjacencies. Similarly, recent pacts, such as the 2021 maritime boundary treaty with the Dominican Republic, further define these zones. In the Pacific, the United States' territory of Guam shares a maritime boundary with the Federated States of Micronesia, as established through equidistance principles and treaty negotiations, extending U.S. maritime frontiers amid overlapping EEZ claims in the region.[102][103][104] This convention of parental attribution underscores how non-sovereign lands project sovereign influence, though disputesâ€”such as Argentina's rejection of Falklands' maritime delimitationsâ€”can complicate recognition. Official U.S. and Dutch boundary records from the State Department affirm these lines via technical delimitations, prioritizing median lines and historical claims over unilateral assertions.[105]

Enclaves, Exclaves, and Micro-Borders

An enclave constitutes a portion of one sovereign state's territory entirely surrounded by the territory of a single other state, while an exclave refers to a detached portion of a state's territory separated from its mainland by foreign territory.[106] These geographic anomalies necessitate precise delineation in border inventories, as they generate additional land contacts that simplistic contiguous mapping overlooks, thereby inflating the total count of neighboring states for the parent country.[107] Russia's Kaliningrad Oblast exemplifies an exclave, comprising 15,125 square kilometers and maintaining distinct land borders of 210 kilometers with Poland to the south and 280 kilometers with Lithuania to the north and east, in addition to maritime boundaries with both.[108][109] Absent this exclave, Russia's European territory would lack direct overland adjacency to either nation, resulting in Kaliningrad effectively multiplying Russia's Baltic neighbors from zero to two via land.[110] Azerbaijan's Nakhchivan Autonomous Republic, covering 5,503 square kilometers, functions as a landlocked exclave bordering Armenia for 71 kilometers, Iran to the south, and Turkey for 17 kilometers to the west.[111][112] This configuration extends Azerbaijan's total land neighbors to fiveâ€”Armenia (via both mainland and exclave), Georgia, Russia, Turkey, and Iranâ€”compared to three without Nakhchivan's contributions. Micro-states illustrate pure enclaves, such as San Marino, an independent republic of 61 square kilometers fully encircled by Italy with a total land border of 39 kilometers, and Vatican City, a 0.44-square-kilometer sovereign entity also embedded within Italy.[113] These cases yield singular bordering states but underscore geometric isolation, complicating logistics like transit and defense without expanding neighbor counts. Complex micro-borders arise in fragmented zones, such as the Baarle-Nassau/Baarle-Hertog municipality straddling Belgium and the Netherlands, where over 20 Dutch exclaves within Belgian territory create numerous short border segments totaling mere meters in places, demanding granular surveying for accurate enumeration.[114] Such intricacies emphasize the primacy of empirical boundary tracing over aggregated national summaries in assessing true border multiplicity.
