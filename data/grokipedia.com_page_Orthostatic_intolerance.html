Orthostatic intolerance

Orthostatic intolerance (OI) is the development of symptoms when standing upright that are relieved when reclining. There are many types of orthostatic intolerance. OI can be a subcategory of dysautonomia, a disorder of the autonomic nervous system occurring when an individual stands up. Some animal species with orthostatic hypotension have evolved to cope with orthostatic disturbances. A substantial overlap is seen between syndromes of orthostatic intolerance on the one hand, and either chronic fatigue syndrome or fibromyalgia on the other. Certain syndromes of orthostatic intolerance, such as postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS), affect more women than men (female-to-male ratio at least 4:1), usually under the age of 35. OI can also be a symptom of mitochondrial cytopathy. Orthostatic intolerance has also been associated with post-viral conditions, including long COVID.[1] Orthostatic intolerance occurs in humans because standing upright is a fundamental stressor, so requires rapid and effective circulatory and neurologic compensations to maintain blood pressure, cerebral blood flow, and consciousness. When a human stands, about 750 ml of thoracic blood are abruptly translocated downward. People who have OI lack the basic mechanisms to compensate for this deficit. Changes in heart rate, blood pressure, and cerebral blood flow that produce OI may be caused by abnormalities in the interactions between blood volume control, the cardiovascular system, the nervous system, and circulation control system.

Signs and symptoms

Orthostatic intolerance can be classified as acute OI and chronic OI.

Acute orthostatic intolerance

Acute orthostatic intolerance includes presyncope (orthostatic symptoms with postural instability but without loss of consciousness) and orthostatic syncope (with transient loss of consciousness). Patients usually manifest the disorder by temporary loss of posture with rapid recovery upon reclining. In cases of syncope, there is transient loss of consciousness; in presyncope, patients remain conscious. This is different from syncope caused by cardiac problems because the triggers for the episode (standing, heat, emotion) and identifiable prodromal symptoms (nausea, blurred vision, headache) are known. As Dr. Julian M. Stewart, an expert in OI from New York Medical College states, "Many syncopal patients have no intercurrent illness; between faints, they are well."[2] Symptoms:[3]
  • Altered vision (blurred vision, "white outs"/gray outs, tunnel vision, double vision)
  • Anxiety
  • Headache
  • Heart palpitations
  • Hyperpnea or sensation of difficulty breathing or swallowing (see also hyperventilation syndrome)
  • Lightheadedness
  • Sweating
  • Tremulousness
  • Weakness
A classic manifestation of acute OI is a soldier who faints after standing rigidly at attention for an extended period of time.

Chronic orthostatic intolerance

Patients with chronic orthostatic intolerance have symptoms on most or all days. Their symptoms overlap with the presyncopal symptoms of acute OI, plus:
  • Exercise intolerance
  • Fatigue
  • Nausea
  • Neurocognitive deficits, such as attention problems
  • Pallor
  • Sensitivity to heat
  • Sleep problems
  • Other vasomotor symptoms[2][4]

Causes

Orthostatic intolerance (OI) can result from various underlying etiological factors that impair the body's ability to compensate for postural changes. These include:
  • Hypovolemia: Reduced blood volume, often due to dehydration, hemorrhage, or inadequate fluid and salt intake, leading to insufficient circulatory compensation upon standing.[4][6]
  • Autonomic nervous system dysfunction: Disorders such as pure autonomic failure, multiple system atrophy, or peripheral neuropathies (e.g., from diabetes or amyloidosis) that disrupt vasoconstriction and heart rate responses.[2][3]
  • Deconditioning: Prolonged bedrest, extended spaceflight, or physical inactivity causing cardiac atrophy, reduced blood volume, and impaired orthostatic tolerance.[7]
  • Post-viral or infectious onset: Often seen in subtypes like postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS), triggered by viral infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr, COVID-19) or post-vaccination in some cases.[4]
  • Neurological conditions: Such as Parkinson's disease, mitochondrial cytopathy, or traumatic brain injury (including concussion), which can lead to autonomic dysregulation.[8][9]
  • Medications and other factors: Drugs causing vasodilation (e.g., antihypertensives, antidepressants) or endocrine disorders (e.g., Addison's disease).[10]
Certain situational factors can exacerbate symptoms in individuals with underlying OI, such as prolonged upright posture, warm environments, emotional stress, or postprandial states, but these are precipitants rather than primary causes.[10]

Dysautonomia

Diagnosis

Many patients go undiagnosed or misdiagnosed and either untreated or treated for other disorders. Initial screening often includes measuring blood pressure and heart rate while lying down and then standing for up to 10 minutes to detect hemodynamic changes, such as a drop of at least 20 mm Hg in systolic blood pressure or 10 mm Hg in diastolic blood pressure within 2 to 5 minutes for orthostatic hypotension, or a sustained increase in heart rate of at least 30 beats per minute for postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS).[11][12] Advanced tests for orthostatic intolerance (OI), including the tilt table test, the NASA Lean Test,[13] the adapted Autonomic Profile (aAP),[14] autonomic assessments (e.g., heart rate and blood pressure variability), and circulatory measurements (e.g., blood flow and vascular compliance), can confirm the diagnosis and guide treatment.[15] Patients with dysautonomia symptoms can be referred to a cardiologist, neurologist, or even a gastroenterologist for treatment and management.[16][17]

Management

Most patients experience an improvement of their symptoms, but for some, OI can be gravely disabling and can be progressive in nature, particularly if it is caused by an underlying condition that is deteriorating. The ways in which symptoms present themselves vary greatly from patient to patient; as a result, individualized treatment plans are necessary.[18] OI is treated pharmacologically and non-pharmacologically. No medications are specifically approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for OI, but off-label use controls symptoms; treatment does not cure OI.[19] Physicians who specialize in treating OI agree that the single most important treatment is drinking more than 2 liters (8 cups) of fluids each day. A steady, large supply of water or other fluids reduces most, and for some patients all, of the major symptoms of this condition. Typically, patients fare best when they drink a glass of water no less frequently than every two hours during the day, instead of drinking a large quantity of water at a single point in the day.[20] For most severe cases and some milder cases, a combination of medications is used. Individual responses to different medications vary widely, and a drug that dramatically improves one patient's symptoms may make another patient's symptoms much worse. Medications focus on three main issues:[20] Medications that increase blood volume:
  • Fludrocortisone (Florinef)
  • Hormonal contraception (in select cases)
Medications that inhibit acetylcholinesterase:
  • Pyridostigmine
Medications that improve vasoconstriction or heart rate control:
  • Midodrine (ProAmatine)
  • Stimulants (e.g., Ritalin or Dexedrine, in select cases)
  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs, e.g., Prozac, Zoloft, and Paxil)
  • Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol)
  • Ivabradine (for heart rate control in POTS)[21]
Behavioral changes that patients with OI can make are:
  • Avoiding triggers such as prolonged sitting, quiet standing, warm environments, or vasodilating medications
  • Using postural maneuvers and pressure garments (e.g., compression stockings)
  • Treating co-existing medical conditions
  • Increasing fluid and salt intake
  • Physical therapy and structured exercise programs (e.g., graded aerobic exercise to build tolerance)[21][22]

Notable case

A notable person with OI is Greg Page, founding member and original lead singer of the Australian children's music group The Wiggles. Due to being diagnosed with this illness, Page left the group in late 2006, and was replaced by his understudy, Sam Moran.[23][24] Two years later in late 2008, he went on to create his own fund for OI to help fund research into this then-little known disorder.[25] Page recovered enough to temporarily return to The Wiggles in early 2012 to help with the transition to the next generation of Wiggles, after which he again left the group at the end of 2012, with the Yellow Wiggle role taken by Emma Watkins in the 2013 lineup change.[26] In January 2020, Page suffered a cardiac arrest onstage during a bushfire relief concert, an incident attributed to his orthostatic intolerance.[27] In August 2024, he discussed the condition's ongoing effects, including fatigue and balance problems, in an interview.[28] As of June 2025, Page reported improved health through better management of the disorder and announced plans for a musical comeback.[29]
