Geography of Canada

Canada is the world's second-largest country by total area, encompassing 9,984,670 square kilometers of land and freshwater, primarily situated in the northern portion of North America.[1] It shares the longest undefended border with the United States to the south and extends to the Arctic Ocean in the north, with coastlines along the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, comprising ten provinces and three territories that divide its administrative landscape.[2] The nation's geography features a diverse array of physiographic regions, including the expansive Canadian Shield of ancient Precambrian rock covering nearly half the country, the rugged Cordillera with its towering Rocky Mountains, the fertile Interior Plains of the prairies, and the sparsely populated Arctic Archipelago of tundra and ice.[3] Climatic zones vary dramatically from humid continental and oceanic in the populated south to subarctic and polar conditions northward, influencing sparse overall population density at about 4 persons per square kilometer, concentrated along the U.S. border.[4] Major hydrological features include the Great Lakes system, shared with the United States, and extensive river networks like the Mackenzie and St. Lawrence, which facilitate drainage across vast basins and support ecosystems from boreal forests to permafrost-dominated taiga.[5] These elements underpin Canada's resource wealth in minerals, timber, and hydropower, while posing challenges such as seasonal ice cover and seismic activity in the west.[6]

Location and Extent

Borders and Territorial Claims

Canada's land borders are primarily with the United States, forming the world's longest undefended international boundary at 8,891 kilometers (5,525 miles), encompassing both terrestrial and aquatic segments across 13 American states and several Canadian provinces.[7][8] This border, established through treaties such as the Jay Treaty of 1794, the Treaty of 1818, and the Webster-Ashburton Treaty of 1842, runs from the Atlantic Ocean along the 45th parallel, follows the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence River, traverses the Prairies, and extends to the Pacific, with a separate segment between Alaska and Yukon-Northwest Territories measuring 2,475 kilometers.[8] In 2022, Canada established a minor land border with Denmark on Hans Island in the Arctic, following a mutual agreement to divide the previously disputed 1.3 square kilometer islet equally, resolving a long-standing overlap in territorial assertions.[9] Maritime boundaries adjoin those of the United States to the south and west, Denmark (via Greenland) to the northeast, and France (via Saint Pierre and Miquelon) to the east, with Canada's territorial sea extending 12 nautical miles from baselines and its exclusive economic zone (EEZ) reaching 200 nautical miles, encompassing approximately 5.75 million square kilometers of ocean area.[10][11] These maritime zones support sovereign rights over resources, though boundaries in areas like the Gulf of Maine (delimited by International Court of Justice in 1984) and Lincoln Sea with Denmark (agreed in 1973 and adjusted in 2022) have been partially resolved through arbitration.[12] Key territorial claims center on the Arctic, where Canada asserts full sovereignty over the waters of the Northwest Passage within its archipelago as historic internal waters, a position codified in the Arctic Waters Pollution Prevention Act of 1970 and reinforced by straight baselines declared in 1985, enabling regulatory control over navigation and environmental protection.[13] The United States contests this, viewing the Passage as an international strait open to transit passage under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, leading to ongoing diplomatic tensions without formal adjudication.[13] Canada has also submitted extended continental shelf claims beyond the EEZ to the Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf, including regions in the Arctic Ocean adjacent to the Lomonosov Ridge, with partial delineations approved as of 2019 but broader assertions pending review amid competing claims from Russia and Denmark.[14] Persistent disputes include the uninhabited Machias Seal Island and adjacent North Rock in the Bay of Fundy with the United States, rooted in ambiguities from the 1783 Treaty of Paris and untested by international courts despite periodic patrols; the Beaufort Sea boundary, where overlapping hydrocarbon entitlements stem from divergent interpretations of the 1825 British-Russian convention versus sector theory; and minor unresolved segments in the Pacific's Dixon Entrance.[12] With France, most boundaries around Saint Pierre and Miquelon were settled by arbitration in 1992, granting France a limited EEZ corridor while affirming Canada's broader resource jurisdiction.[12] These claims underscore Canada's emphasis on security, resource stewardship, and historical usage in polar regions, with no active military confrontations but sustained diplomatic and scientific assertions.[10]

Size, Area, and Topography Overview

Canada possesses a total area of 9,984,670 square kilometers, ranking as the second-largest country by this measure after Russia, encompassing both land and inland water bodies.[15] Its land area stands at 8,788,702.8 square kilometers as of the 2021 census, placing it fourth globally by land extent behind Russia, China, and the United States.[4] This vast expanse stretches approximately 5,500 kilometers from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean and over 4,600 kilometers from the United States border to the Arctic Ocean, with a coastline exceeding 243,000 kilometers including its numerous islands.[16] The country's topography features a diverse array of landforms shaped by ancient geological processes, primarily divided into seven physiographic regions: the Canadian Shield, Interior Plains, Great Lakes-St. Lawrence Lowlands, Appalachian Region, Hudson Bay Lowlands, Western Cordillera, and Arctic Archipelago.[3] The Canadian Shield, encompassing nearly half of Canada's landmass, consists of exposed Precambrian bedrock with thin soils, numerous lakes, and boreal forests, exerting a dominant influence on the northern and central landscape.[3] In contrast, the Western Cordillera rises to rugged mountain ranges including the Rocky Mountains, while the Interior Plains form relatively flat expanses suited to agriculture and resource extraction.[16] Elevations vary markedly, from sea level along coastal lowlands to peaks exceeding 5,000 meters in the Cordillera, with an average elevation of about 380 meters; much of the terrain north of the treeline remains glaciated or tundra-covered, comprising over 27% of the total area.[15][16] These topographic features result from tectonic stability in the Shield, sedimentary deposition in the Plains, and ongoing orogenic activity in the west, influencing drainage patterns, climate variability, and resource distribution across the nation.[3] Inland waters, including the Great Lakes shared with the United States, cover roughly 9% of the total area, underscoring Canada's hydrological prominence.[15]

Hudson Bay Lowlands

The Hudson Bay Lowlands, also referred to as the Hudson Plain, constitute a major physiographic region in northern Canada, encompassing approximately 370,000 square kilometers along the southern and eastern shores of Hudson Bay and James Bay.[38] This area spans portions of Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, and Nunavut, forming a low-relief plain characterized by extensive wetlands, with local elevation variations typically less than 2 meters.[39] The region features subdued glacial landforms, including eskers, drumlins, and a prominent belt of raised beaches along the coast, resulting from post-glacial isostatic rebound and marine transgression following the retreat of the Laurentide Ice Sheet.[3] Geologically, the Hudson Bay Lowlands represent the onshore extension of Paleozoic sedimentary basins surrounding Hudson Bay, with nearly flat-lying successions of limestone, dolomite, and shale deposits ranging from Ordovician to Cretaceous in age, overlain by thin Quaternary glacial and marine sediments.[40] These rocks form a stable, undeformed platform depressed by the weight of past glaciation, now experiencing ongoing uplift at rates of up to 1-2 meters per century in some areas. The landscape is dominated by poorly drained, acidic soils supporting vast peatland complexes, including bogs, fens, and swamps, which cover over 80% of the region and function as significant carbon sinks, storing an estimated 30-35 gigatons of carbon.[38] Vegetation transitions from discontinuous boreal forest in the south, dominated by black spruce (Picea mariana), tamarack (Larix laricina), and shrubs such as willow (Salix spp.) and alder (Alnus spp.), to open tundra-like communities in the north, with sedges, mosses (e.g., Sphagnum spp.), and lichens prevailing on waterlogged or permafrost-affected ground.[41] The subarctic climate features long, cold winters with mean January temperatures around -20Â°C to -30Â°C, short summers averaging 10-15Â°C, and annual precipitation of 300-600 mm, much of which contributes to the region's hydrological regime of slow-flowing rivers like the Nelson and Attawapiskat, draining into Hudson Bay via broad deltas and tidal flats.[42] Permafrost is discontinuous, covering about 50% of the area, particularly in northern peat plateaus, influencing drainage and exacerbating the swampy character.[43]

Western Cordillera

The Western Cordillera encompasses a complex system of mountain ranges, plateaus, and valleys extending along Canada's western margin from the Yukon Territory through British Columbia and into western Alberta.[44] This region, approximately 800 km wide, forms the Pacific-facing edge of the continent and is characterized by steep, rugged terrain shaped by tectonic forces.[44] It includes three primary physiographic belts: the eastern Rocky Mountains, the interior plateaus and ranges such as the Columbia and Cassiar Mountains, and the western Coast and Insular Mountains.[45] The easternmost component, the Canadian Rocky Mountains, features folded and faulted sedimentary rocks thrust eastward, with peaks rising to elevations exceeding 3,500 m; Mount Robson, at 3,954 m, stands as the highest in this subrange.[46] Interior ranges, including the Columbia Mountains, exhibit metamorphic and granitic cores flanked by steep valleys carved by glacial and fluvial erosion.[47] The western belts, dominated by the Coast Mountains and Vancouver Island Ranges, reach heights over 4,000 m and include deep fjords and coastal islands formed by subduction-related volcanism and uplift.[48] Prominent peaks in the northern extension, particularly the St. Elias Mountains straddling Yukon and Alaska, include Mount Logan, Canada's highest at 5,959 m, resulting from rapid uplift due to ongoing convergence.[49] The region's topography influences major river systems, such as the Fraser, Skeena, and upper Columbia, which originate in high plateaus and dissect the ranges, creating narrow canyons and broad intermontane basins.[3] Glacial features, including cirques, U-shaped valleys, and extensive icefields, persist in higher elevations, contributing to the dynamic landscape.[50]

The Canadian Shield constitutes the ancient Precambrian core of Canada's geology, encompassing rocks primarily formed between 4.0 and 1.0 billion years ago, with the bulk stabilizing as a craton by Glacial Influences and Post-Ice Age Changes

The Laurentide Ice Sheet dominated Canada's landscape during the Last Glacial Maximum, approximately 23,000 to 19,000 years ago, covering nearly all regions east of the Rocky Mountains with ice thicknesses reaching up to 3 kilometers.[66][67] This vast ice mass, centered over the Canadian Shield with domes in Keewatin and Labrador, eroded bedrock extensively, depositing vast quantities of accelerating post-15,000 years ago with rates varying from 300 meters per year in central regions to 700-900 meters per year in western Quebec, reducing the ice sheet to remnants in Quebec and Labrador by 8,000 years ago.[70][70] This deglaciation released enormous meltwater volumes, forming proglacial lakes like Lake Agassiz, which influenced drainage patterns and contributed to the scouring of the Great Lakes basins.[70] In northern and coastal areas, fjords and U-shaped valleys emerged from glacial overdeepening, while erratic boulders from the Shield were transported and deposited far southward, evidencing ice flow directions.[71] Post-ice age adjustments continue through glacial isostatic rebound, where the crust rises in response to the removal of ice load, with maximum rates of about 11 millimeters per year near southeastern Hudson Bay.[72] This uplift, ongoing since deglaciation, has caused relative sea-level fall in formerly glaciated interiors, up to 35 millimeters per year historically in Hudson Bay regions, exposing raised beaches and altering coastal morphologies.[73][74] In peripheral areas, forebulge collapse led to subsidence and marine inundation, such as the Tyrrell Sea in Hudson Bay lowlands, before rebound dominated.[75] These processes have redefined hydrology, with rebound facilitating the drainage of ancient lakes into modern river systems like the St. Lawrence, while ongoing uplift mitigates global sea-level rise effects in central Canada.[76][77]

Similarly, Point Roberts, Washington, forms a U.S. exclave south of Vancouver, British Columbia, due to the 49th parallel's intersection with the Strait of Georgia, isolating it from the U.S. mainland and requiring passage through Canada.[207] Disputed insular territories include Machias Seal Island and North Rock, located in the Gulf of Maine approximately 19 kilometres off the Maine-New Brunswick coast. Canada asserts sovereignty based on an exemption in the 1783 Treaty of Paris for islands within the Province of Nova Scotia's limits, reinforced by continuous occupation including a lighthouse established in 1832.[208] The United States contests this, citing ambiguities in subsequent treaties like the 1818 Convention and 1842 Webster-Ashburton Treaty, which did not explicitly resolve the islands; both nations maintain lighthouses and wildlife patrols but avoid formal adjudication to preserve amicable relations.[209] As of 2025, the dispute persists without resolution, affecting potential fisheries and offshore resource claims in surrounding waters.[210] Maritime boundary issues extend to the Beaufort Sea, where Canada and the United States contest a wedge-shaped area of about 21,197 square kilometres north of the Yukon-Northwest Territories-Alaska border. Canada maintains the boundary follows the 141st meridian extension from the land border, per the 1825 Anglo-Russian Treaty inherited post-1867 Alaska Purchase and 1880 British North America transfer.[211] The U.S. advocates an equidistance line from coastal points, emphasizing modern international law principles under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, which Canada has signed but not ratified.[212] In September 2024, both governments established a joint task force to negotiate the boundary, driven by emerging hydrocarbon potential and Arctic navigation interests, though no agreement has been reached as of October 2025.[213] Canada's Arctic sovereignty faces ongoing challenges, particularly over the Northwest Passage through the Canadian Arctic Archipelago. Canada claims these waters as internal, asserting historic title via continuous Inuit presence and state control since the 19th century, requiring foreign vessels to seek permission and adhere to Canadian regulations.[214] The United States and European nations reject this, classifying the Passage as an international strait open to transit passage without prior consent, based on customary international law and precedents like the Corfu Channel case.[13] Heightened melting of sea ice has increased shipping and resource exploration, prompting Canada in 2025 to bolster patrols and update its Arctic Foreign Policy to emphasize sovereignty enforcement amid rival claims from Russia and China.[215] These disputes underscore tensions between Canada's resource jurisdiction under Article 234 of UNCLOS for ice-covered areas and broader navigational freedoms.[216]
