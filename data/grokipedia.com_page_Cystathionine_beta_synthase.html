Cystathionine-β-synthase, also known as CBS, is an enzyme (EC 4.2.1.22) that in humans is encoded by the CBS gene. It catalyzes the first step of the transsulfuration pathway, from homocysteine to cystathionine:[5] \ceL−serine+L−homocysteine⇌L−cystathionine+H2O\ce{L-serine + L-homocysteine ⇌ L-cystathionine + H2O}\ceL−serine+L−homocysteine⇌L−cystathionine+H2O CBS uses the cofactor pyridoxal-phosphate (PLP) and can be allosterically regulated by effectors such as the ubiquitous cofactor S-adenosyl-L-methionine (adoMet). This enzyme belongs to the family of lyases, to be specific, the hydro-lyases, which cleave carbon-oxygen bonds. CBS is a multidomain enzyme composed of an N-terminal enzymatic domain and two CBS domains. The CBS gene is the most common locus for mutations associated with homocystinuria.[6]

Nomenclature

The systematic name of this enzyme class is L-serine hydro-lyase (adding homocysteine; L-cystathionine-forming). Other names in common use include:
  • β-thionase
  • L-serine hydro-lyase (adding homocysteine)
  • methylcysteine synthase
  • serine sulfhydrase
  • and serine sulfhydrylase
Methylcysteine synthase was assigned the EC number EC 4.2.1.23 in 1961. A side-reaction of CBS caused this. The EC number EC 4.2.1.23 was deleted in 1972.[7]

Cystathionine beta-synthase

Structure

The human enzyme cystathionine β-synthase is a tetramer and comprises 551 amino acids with a subunit molecular weight of 63 kDa. It displays a modular organization of three modules: an N-terminal heme domain followed by a central catalytic core that contains the pyridoxal-phosphate (PLP) cofactor.[8] The PLP cofactor is bound in the catalytic domain and forms a Schiff base with lysine 119 (K119) in the active site.[9] A Schiff base is a functional group containing a C=N bond with the nitrogen atom connected to an aryl or alkyl group. The heme domain is composed of 70 amino acids, and the heme cofactor exists in vertebrate CBS and is absent in yeast and protozoan CBS. At the C-terminus, the regulatory domain of CBS contains a tandem repeat of two CBS domains with a β-α-β-β-α secondary structure motif found in other proteins.[8] CBS has a C-terminal inhibitory domain. The C-terminal domain of cystathionine β-synthase regulates its activity via both intrasteric and allosteric effects and is important for maintaining the tetrameric state of the protein.[8] This inhibition is alleviated by binding of the allosteric effector, S-adenosyl-L-methionine (adoMet), or by deletion of the regulatory domain; however, the magnitude of the effects differ.[8] Mutations in this domain are correlated with hereditary diseases.[10] The heme domain contains an N-terminal loop that binds heme and provides the axial ligands cysteine 52 (C52) and histidine 65 (H65). The distance of heme from the PLP binding site suggests its non-role in catalysis; however, deletion of the heme domain causes loss of redox sensitivity, therefore it is hypothesized that heme is a redox sensor.[9] The presence of heme (iron-protoporphyrin IX) in CBS is unique among PLP-dependent enzymes and is found in vertebrate CBS. Drosophila melanogaster and Dictyostelium discoideum have truncated N-terminal extensions lacking the conserved histidine and cysteine heme ligand residues. However, the Anopheles gambiae sequence has a longer N-terminal extension than the human enzyme and contains the conserved histidine and cysteine heme ligand residues similar to human CBS. Heme binding in CBS appears conserved in vertebrates and some invertebrates, suggesting the heme domain is an early evolutionary innovation that arose before the separation of animals and slime molds, though variable across species.[8] The PLP is an internal aldimine. Between the catalytic and regulatory domains exists a hypersensitive site that causes proteolytic cleavage and produces a truncated dimeric enzyme that is more active than the original enzyme. Both the truncated enzyme and the enzyme found in yeast are not regulated by adoMet. The yeast enzyme is also activated by the deletion of the C-terminal to produce the dimeric enzyme.[8] Recent structural studies, including cryo-EM of full-length human CBS (PDB 8S5L, 2024), reveal that the enzyme assembles into higher-order filaments in the basal state, which disassemble into active tetramers upon binding adoMet, providing insights into allosteric regulation and implications for disease-associated mutations.[11] As of 2025, multiple structures have been solved for human CBS and variants, including PDB accession codes 1JBQ, 1M54, 4COO, 4L3V, 7QGT, 8S5L, and 9HIF.

Transsulfuration, catalyzed by CBS, converts homocysteine to cystathionine, which cystathionine gamma lyase converts to cysteine.[12] CBS occupies a pivotal position in mammalian sulfur metabolism at the homocysteine junction where the decision to conserve methionine or to convert it to cysteine via the transsulfuration pathway, is made. Moreover, the transsulfuration pathway is the only pathway capable of removing sulfur-containing amino acids under conditions of excess.[8] In analogy with other β-replacement enzymes, the reaction catalyzed by CBS is predicted to involve a series of PLP-bound intermediates. Addition of serine results in a transchiffization reaction, which forms an external aldimine. The aldimine undergoes proton abstraction at the α-carbon followed by elimination to generate an amino-acrylate intermediate. Nucleophilic attack by the thiolate of homocysteine on the aminoacrylate and reprotonation at Cα generate the external aldimine of cystathionine. A final transaldimination reaction releases the final product, cystathionine.[8] The final product, L-cystathionine can also form an aminoacrylate intermediate, indicating that the entire reaction of CBS is reversible.[13] The measured V0 of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction, in general, reflects the steady state (where [ES] is constant), even though V0 is limited to the early part of a reaction, and analysis of these initial rates is referred to as steady-state kinetics. Steady-state kinetic analysis of yeast CBS yields parallel lines. These results agree with the proposed ping-pong mechanism in which serine binding and release of water are followed by homocysteine binding and release of cystathionine. In contrast, the steady-state enzyme kinetics of rat CBS yields intersecting lines, indicating that the β-substituent of serine is not released from the enzyme prior to binding of homocysteine.[8] One of the alternate reactions involving CBS is the condensation of cysteine with homocysteine to form cystathionine and hydrogen sulfide (H2S).[13] H2S in the brain is produced from L-cysteine by CBS. This alternative metabolic pathway is also dependent on adoMet.[14] CBS enzyme activity is not found in all tissues and cells. It is expressed at low levels in heart, lung, testes, adrenal, and spleen in rats. In humans, CBS shows low expression in heart muscle and is expressed in primary cultures of human aortic endothelial cells.[15] The low expression of CBS in these tissues implies that these tissues have limited capacity to synthesize cysteine and that cysteine must be supplied from extracellular sources. It also suggests that these tissues might have increased sensitivity to homocysteine toxicity because they cannot catabolize excess homocysteine via transsulfuration as efficiently.[13]

Regulation

Allosteric activation of CBS by adoMet determines the metabolic fate of homocysteine. Mammalian CBS is activated 2.5-5-fold by AdoMet with a dissociation constant of 15 μM.[6] AdoMet is an allosteric activator that increases the Vmax of the CBS reaction but does not affect the Km for the substrates. In other words, AdoMet stimulates CBS activity by increasing the turnover rate rather than the binding of substrates to the enzyme.[8] This protein may use the morpheein model of allosteric regulation.[16] Human CBS performs a crucial step in the biosynthetic pathway of cysteine by providing a regulatory control point for AdoMet. Homocysteine, after being methylated to methionine, can be converted to AdoMet, which donates methyl groups to a variety of substrates, e.g., neurotransmitters, proteins, and nucleic acids. AdoMet functions as an allosteric activator of CBS and exerts control on its biosynthesis: low concentrations of AdoMet result in low CBS activity, thereby funneling homocysteine into the transmethylation cycle toward AdoMet formation. In contrast, high adoMet concentrations funnel homocysteine into the transsulfuration pathway toward cysteine biosynthesis.[17] In mammals, CBS is a highly regulated enzyme, which contains a heme cofactor that functions as a redox sensor[8] that can modulate its activity in response to changes in the redox potential. If the resting form of CBS in the cell has ferrous (Fe2+) heme, the potential exists for activating the enzyme under oxidizing conditions by conversion to the ferric (Fe3+) state.[8] The Fe2+ form of the enzyme is inhibited upon binding CO or nitric oxide, whereas enzyme activity is doubled when the Fe2+ is oxidized to Fe3+. The redox state of the heme is pH dependent, with oxidation of Fe2+–CBS to Fe3+–CBS being favored at low pH conditions.[18] Since mammalian CBS contains a heme cofactor, whereas yeast and protozoan enzyme from Trypanosoma cruzi do not have heme cofactors, researchers have speculated that heme is not required for CBS activity.[8] CBS is regulated at the transcriptional level by NF-Y, SP-1, and SP-3.[19] In addition it is upregulated transcriptionally by glucocorticoids, and downregulated by insulin.[20] Methionine upregulates CBS at the post-transcriptional level.[21]

Human disease

Down syndrome is a medical condition characterized by an overexpression of cystathionine beta synthase (CBS) due to trisomy 21, leading to low levels of homocysteine in the blood and overproduction of hydrogen sulfide (H2S), which contributes to cognitive deficits and mitochondrial dysfunction.[22] It has been speculated that CBS overexpression, along with other genetic factors such as DYRK1A, may contribute significantly to some Down syndrome phenotypes. The phenotype of Down syndrome contrasts with that of hyperhomocysteinemia (described below). Pharmacological inhibitors of CBS have been patented by the Jerome Lejeune Foundation (2010), and preclinical research, including animal studies, is ongoing to explore their potential in treating Down syndrome-related intellectual disability.[23] Hyperhomocysteinemia is a medical condition characterized by an abnormally high level of homocysteine in the blood. Mutations in CBS are the single most common cause of hereditary hyperhomocysteinemia. Genetic defects that affect the MTHFR, MTR, and MTRR enzyme pathways can also contribute to high homocysteine levels. Inborn errors in CBS result in hyperhomocysteinemia with complications in the cardiovascular system leading to early and aggressive arterial disease. Hyperhomocysteinemia also affects three other major organ systems including the ocular, central nervous, and skeletal.[8] Homocystinuria due to CBS deficiency is a special type of hyperhomocysteinemia. It is a rare, hereditary recessive autosomal disease, in general diagnosed during childhood. More than 200 different homocystinuria-causing mutations have been identified.[24] A common functional feature of the mutations in the CBS domains is that the mutations abolish or strongly reduce activation by adoMet.[17] No specific cure has been discovered for homocystinuria; however, many people are treated using high doses of vitamin B6, which is a cofactor of CBS.

Bioengineering

Cystathionine beta-synthase (CBS) has been explored in bioengineering applications, particularly for its role in hydrogen sulfide (H2S) production. A 2023 study developed a biomimetic nanozyme mimicking CBS to generate H2S under microwave hyperthermia, reactivating antitumor immune responses in solid tumors.[25] Research has also investigated engineering CBS domains for ion sensing in ATP-binding cassette transporters.[26]

Mutations

Mutations in the CBS gene are the most common cause of classic homocystinuria, an inherited disorder of methionine metabolism.[6] Over 150 pathogenic variants have been identified, most leading to reduced enzyme activity and elevated homocysteine levels.[27] The most frequent pathogenic mutation is c.833T>C (p.Ile278Thr or I278T, rs5742905), accounting for about 25-50% of alleles in various populations, often associated with pyridoxine-responsive forms of the disease.[28] Another common variant is c.919G>A (p.Gly307Ser), prevalent in non-responsive cases.[27] Benign polymorphisms in the CBS gene, such as c.699C>T (rs234706, associated with slightly increased activity) and synonymous variants like c.1078G>A (p.Ala360Ala, rs1801181) and c.636A>G (p.Asn212Asn, rs2298758), do not cause disease but may influence homocysteine levels or interact with other genetic factors.[29][30] These can be detected via standard genetic testing methods.
