2023 Kazakh legislative election

Snap legislative elections were held in Kazakhstan on 19 March 2023 to elect all 98 members of the Mäjilis, the lower house of the country's bicameral parliament.[1] The elections followed President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev's dissolution of the previous Majilis on 19 January 2023, as part of constitutional reforms initiated after the deadly January 2022 unrest, which aimed to dilute the influence of former president Nursultan Nazarbayev's allies and introduce a mixed electoral system comprising 69 proportional party-list seats and 29 single-mandate constituency seats requiring a 50% absolute majority or runoff.[2] Seven parties competed, all aligned with the presidential administration, reflecting limited pluralism despite the multiparty outcome.[3] The pro-presidential Amanat party, formerly Nur Otan and rebranded in 2022, secured a majority with 53.9% of the party-list vote, translating to 29 list seats plus 11 single-mandate wins for a total of 40 seats.[4] The remaining parties crossing the 5% threshold included Auyl (10.9%, 8 seats), Respublika (8.59%, 6 seats), Aq Jol (8.41%, 6 seats), People's Party (6.8%, 5 seats), and National Social Democratic Party (5.2%, 4 seats), while Baytaq fell short at 2.3%; this marked the first time since 2007 that more than two parties held Majilis seats.[4] Voter turnout was 54%, with over 6.3 million ballots cast from 12 million eligible voters.[4][5] The elections, conducted under the "New Kazakhstan" reform banner, were praised domestically for efficiency and increased representation but drew international criticism for lacking genuine opposition, as opposition groups faced registration barriers and no independent candidates succeeded in single-mandate races, underscoring the controlled nature of political competition.[3][6] Observers from the OSCE noted competent administration but highlighted restrictions on freedoms and the absence of meaningful debate, reinforcing Tokayev's consolidation of power without challenging the authoritarian framework.[7]

Historical and political background

January 2022 unrest and immediate aftermath

The unrest in Kazakhstan began on January 2, 2022, in the western city of Zhanaozen, Mangystau Region, triggered by a sharp increase in liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) prices after the government lifted a price cap effective January 1, causing the cost to roughly double from 60 tenge per liter to 120 tenge per liter at local stations.[8][9][10] Protests, initially peaceful and focused on reversing the fuel price hike, rapidly spread to other cities including Almaty, where they escalated into widespread riots, looting, and clashes with security forces by January 4–5; underlying grievances included long-standing corruption, economic inequality, and resentment toward the enduring influence of former President Nursultan Nazarbayev, who had retained significant informal power despite stepping down in 2019.[11][12][13] President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev responded by declaring a nationwide state of emergency on January 5, temporarily restoring the LPG price cap to 50 tenge per liter, and requesting assistance from the Collective Security Treaty Organization (CSTO), prompting the deployment of approximately 2,500 troops from Russia, Belarus, Armenia, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan starting January 6 to stabilize key areas.[14][12] Tokayev authorized security forces to use lethal force against armed groups, stating on January 7 that order to shoot without warning had been given where necessary, leading to the restoration of constitutional order by January 10 amid reports of over 225 deaths—primarily civilians—and initial detentions exceeding 10,000, with total arrests reaching around 12,000 by mid-January; independent human rights monitors, including Human Rights Watch, have documented over 200 protester deaths and alleged widespread torture in custody, though official investigations attributed many fatalities to rioters and looters rather than security operations.[13][15][16] In the immediate aftermath, Tokayev moved to consolidate executive authority by removing Nazarbayev from the chairmanship of the National Security Council on January 5 and initiating a broad purge of Nazarbayev-era officials, including arrests of family members like Kairat Satybaldy and Timur Kulibayev on corruption charges, alongside dismissals in security and economic sectors; this reshuffling, framed as addressing elite capture and public demands for renewal, weakened Nazarbayev's "kenesra" (assembly) influence and positioned Tokayev to pursue systemic changes, though critics from civil society groups questioned the transparency of investigations into the unrest's orchestration.[17][18] The events exposed causal vulnerabilities in Kazakhstan's political economy, where subsidized fuel masked deeper structural issues like oligarchic control over resources, ultimately catalyzing Tokayev's narrative of breaking from the past to legitimize forthcoming reforms.[19]

Constitutional referendum and systemic reforms

In the aftermath of the January 2022 unrest, which exposed deep public grievances against entrenched elite influence and prompted President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev to sideline his predecessor Nursultan Nazarbayev, Kazakhstan held a constitutional referendum on June 5, 2022, to approve a package of 106 amendments across 41 articles.[20][21] These changes aimed to shift from a super-presidential system toward balanced governance by curbing executive overreach, eliminating Nazarbayev's lifetime privileges such as his "elbasy" (leader of the nation) status and Security Council chairmanship, and prohibiting dynastic succession by barring close relatives from key state roles.[22][23] The referendum passed with 77.18% approval from valid votes, amid a turnout of 63.66%, according to the Central Referendum Commission, reflecting broad but not unanimous support for Tokayev's reform agenda as a stabilizing measure post-unrest.[24] Key electoral reforms included reducing the Mäjilis (lower house) from 107 seats—previously 98 proportional plus 9 appointed by the Assembly of People of Kazakhstan—to 98 fully elected seats, with 69 allocated to single-mandate districts (approximately 70%) and 29 to proportional party lists, reintroducing direct constituency representation absent since 2007 to encourage localized accountability and competition.[25] Additional mandates required at least 30% female candidates on party lists with alternating positions, alongside provisions for ethnic minority representation through quotas and independent candidacy in single-mandate races, intended to promote inclusivity without altering core power structures.[26] These systemic adjustments, including enhanced local maslikhat (council) authority over budgets and appointments, were causally rooted in Tokayev's imperative to legitimize his consolidation of power after deploying CSTO forces to quell the unrest, which killed over 200 and targeted Nazarbayev-era clans, while signaling responsiveness to demands for decentralization amid persistent authoritarian dynamics.[20][21] Though framed as fostering multiparty pluralism and reducing centralized patronage, the reforms preserved presidential vetoes, decree powers, and party registration barriers, prioritizing regime stability over substantive opposition empowerment, as evidenced by subsequent electoral outcomes dominated by pro-Tokayev forces.[27]

2022 presidential election outcomes

The snap presidential election held on 20 November 2022 saw incumbent Kassym-Jomart Tokayev secure a landslide victory with 81.31 percent of the vote, according to official results from Kazakhstan's Central Election Commission.[28][29] Tokayev's main challengers included Zhaniya Aubakirova of the Auyl People's Democratic Patriotic Party with 3.55 percent, Zhambyl Akhmetbekov of the Communist People's Party with 1.01 percent, and four other candidates each receiving under 1 percent, reflecting a fragmented and nominal opposition field where only six candidates were ultimately registered out of over 100 initial applicants, many of whom faced disqualifications or withdrew.[30] Voter turnout stood at 63.46 percent, with Tokayev's campaign emphasizing continuity of reforms initiated after the January 2022 unrest.[31] International observers from the OSCE's Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights noted procedural improvements, such as better-trained polling staff and reduced instances of multiple voting compared to prior elections, but criticized the contest for lacking genuine pluralism due to ongoing restrictions on freedom of association, assembly, and expression that limited opposition viability.[32][31] The electoral framework, while amended post-referendum to allow direct presidential elections, still enabled state dominance through media control and administrative barriers to independent candidacies, as evidenced by the low vote shares for non-incumbent options and reports of uneven campaign access.[33] These outcomes underscored skepticism regarding the opposition's capacity to mount a credible challenge, with empirical data showing no candidate surpassing 4 percent support amid a political landscape where pro-government forces held sway.[34] Tokayev's re-election bolstered his mandate for the "New Kazakhstan" agenda, which promised anti-corruption measures, economic diversification beyond oil and gas dependency, and balanced foreign policy, including defiance of Russia on Ukraine-related issues, thereby signaling policy continuity into the subsequent legislative framework.[29] This victory reinforced pro-government dominance, paving the way for parliamentary dissolution and snap elections by affirming public endorsement—per official tallies—of reforms aimed at curbing elite influence while maintaining centralized control, though critics attributed the lopsided results to structural impediments rather than broad-based competition.[35][36]

Parliamentary dissolution and snap election trigger

On 19 January 2023, President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev signed Decree No. 24 dissolving the Mäjilis, the lower house of Kazakhstan's Parliament, of its seventh convocation, citing the chamber's failure to enact key legislative initiatives amid ongoing political reforms.[37][38] The decree invoked subparagraph 2 of Article 63 of the Constitution, which authorizes the president to dissolve Parliament if it twice rejects the government's program within a three-month period or fails to form a government within constitutional deadlines, thereby justifying the action on grounds of legislative gridlock and inefficiency.[39][40] This step followed Tokayev's public statements on the need to "reload" political institutions after the June 2022 constitutional referendum, which introduced measures to redistribute powers from the presidency to Parliament but highlighted persistent obstacles in passing reform-oriented bills.[41] The dissolution precipitated snap elections for the Mäjilis, scheduled for 19 March 2023, shortening the seventh convocation's term—which had begun in January 2021—by nearly two years and bypassing the standard electoral cycle.[42][43] Analysts noted the timing aligned with Tokayev's broader campaign to assert authority post-2022 unrest, including efforts to marginalize remnants of Nursultan Nazarbayev's influence within the ruling Amanat party (formerly Nur Otan), whose loyalists dominated the dissolved chamber and were perceived as resistant to accelerated reforms.[44] While Tokayev framed the move as advancing proactive governance and aligning with referendum-mandated changes to foster multiparty representation, opponents and observers criticized it as a maneuver to consolidate executive control ahead of fully implementing power-sharing provisions, potentially sidelining entrenched elites without addressing underlying authoritarian structures.[45][3]

Electoral framework

Post-referendum modifications to the system

The constitutional referendum held on 5 June 2022 approved amendments that shifted the Mäjilis electoral framework from a fully proportional representation system to a hybrid model, incorporating 29 single-member districts elected by plurality vote alongside 69 seats allocated proportionally from closed party lists in a nationwide constituency.[27] This change, implemented through subsequent electoral legislation, introduced direct constituency-based representation for the first time in the lower house since the early 2000s, with candidates in single-member districts winning via first-past-the-post in a single round without runoffs.[27] The proportional component retained a 5 percent national threshold for parties to qualify for seats, distributed using the largest remainder method after initial allocation by the Hare quota.[27] Independent candidates gained eligibility to self-nominate in the single-member districts, a provision absent since 2004 and designed to broaden participation beyond registered parties, which alone could contest the proportional seats.[27] Electoral laws mandated diversity quotas in party lists, requiring at least 30 percent women and 30 percent candidates under age 29, with non-compliance potentially barring list registration.[7] These modifications sought to dilute entrenched party dominance by enabling local challenges, yet structural factors—including the ruling Amanat party's resource advantages and limited opposition viability—preserved its leverage, as evidenced by international observers' assessments of subdued pluralism despite expanded formal choices.[46][6]

Structure of constituencies and voting mechanisms

The 2023 Mäjilis elections utilized a mixed-member proportional system to allocate 98 seats, with 29 seats filled through first-past-the-post voting in single-member districts (SMDs) and 69 seats distributed proportionally from closed party lists in a single nationwide constituency.[7] This structure, introduced via post-2022 constitutional reforms, aimed to combine local representation with national party competition while reducing the chamber's size from prior configurations.[47] The Central Election Commission delineated the 29 SMDs in December 2022, drawing on 2021 census population data to approximate equal voter distribution across regions, factoring in density variations and administrative boundaries to maintain urban-rural proportionality.[7] District sizes ranged widely, from approximately 142,000 voters in Ulytau to 562,590 in Aktobe, resulting in deviations exceeding 15 percent in 11 districts—surpassing the Venice Commission's 10 percent equality guideline—yet without verified evidence of intentional gerrymandering to favor specific parties or demographics.[7] Voters received dual ballots on election day: one to select a candidate in their assigned SMD and another to choose a party list for the proportional allocation, enabling independent expression across both mechanisms.[7] Paper ballots predominated for transparency, with limited use of automatic voting cartridges for 8,340 voters; all tallies underwent manual counting at polling stations to minimize risks from electronic vulnerabilities, though observers noted occasional procedural inconsistencies in aggregation.[7] Special accommodations extended to overseas Kazakh citizens via 77 polling stations at diplomatic and consular missions, alongside mobile voting for 86,814 registered voters in remote or incapacitated categories and absentee certificates for 8,340 others, ensuring logistical access without compromising count integrity.[7] Voter registration streamlined through unified national databases promoted turnout, particularly in urban centers with higher densities, while rural SMDs relied on territorial commissions for equitable polling site distribution.[7]

Party thresholds, quotas, and candidate eligibility

The electoral system for the Mäjilis required political parties to exceed a 5 percent national vote threshold in the proportional representation component to qualify for allocation of the 69 party-list seats, with votes below this threshold redistributed proportionally among qualifying parties.[27] This threshold, unchanged from prior elections but applied within a reformed mixed system post-2022 referendum, aimed to limit fragmentation while allowing multiple parties representation, though observers noted it favored established entities in a field of seven registered competitors.[7] Parties nominating lists were subject to mandatory quotas ensuring at least 30 percent of candidates were women, youth under age 29, or persons with disabilities, with the requirement extending to the distribution of won mandates to prevent evasion through list positioning.[27] These provisions, introduced via 2021 amendments and expanded for 2023, sought to enhance inclusivity empirically—evidenced by prior cycles yielding over 25 percent female representation despite non-competitive dynamics—but critics argue they enable tokenistic compliance in a state-influenced party landscape, where genuine contestation remains constrained by registration barriers and media controls.[7] Non-compliance could result in list invalidation, enforcing formal diversity without addressing underlying causal factors like opposition suppression. Candidate eligibility applied uniformly: individuals must be Kazakh citizens aged 25 or older, with at least 10 years of permanent residency, barring those with unexpunged criminal convictions or corruption offenses.[27] Parties, to participate, required Ministry of Justice registration with a minimum 5,000 members nationwide—a threshold lowered from 20,000 pre-reforms to ostensibly broaden access—alongside congress approval of lists.[7] Independents were restricted to the 29 single-member districts, self-nominating via voter signatures (typically 0.5-1 percent of constituency electorate, around 400-1,000 depending on district size), underscoring the system's bias toward party-mediated participation over individual bids.[48] These rules, while promoting structured inclusivity, reflect a vetted framework prioritizing stability over unfettered pluralism, as evidenced by the exclusion of unregistered groups despite eased criteria.[7]

Pre-election processes

Official timetable and regulatory steps

The Mäjilis of the Parliament of Kazakhstan was dissolved by President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev on January 19, 2023, triggering the snap legislative elections scheduled for March 19, 2023, in accordance with Article 57 of the Constitution, which mandates elections no later than two months after dissolution.[38][27] The Central Election Commission (CEC) promptly approved the official election calendar on January 20, 2023, outlining administrative milestones to ensure procedural compliance within the compressed timeframe.[49] Candidate registration opened on January 20, 2023, and closed on February 18, 2023, at 6 p.m. local time, allowing territorial election commissions to verify submissions against eligibility criteria under the Constitutional Law on Elections.[49][27] The official campaign period followed immediately on February 18, 2023, extending until March 17, 2023, after which a mandatory pre-election silence period commenced at midnight on March 18, prohibiting all campaign activities to allow voter reflection.[49][27][50] Polling stations operated on March 19, 2023, from early morning to evening, with the CEC tasked to compile and certify results within 10 days, culminating in the official announcement on March 29, 2023.[49][4] The CEC provided regulatory oversight through live-streamed sessions in Kazakh and Russian (with sign language interpretation), prompt publication of decisions, and mechanisms for appeals against commission rulings, facilitating adherence to deadlines without reported delays in administrative processes.[27] This structure demonstrated operational efficiency, as the entire cycle from dissolution to certification spanned roughly 70 days, aligning with legal requirements for snap elections.[27]

Voter registration and accessibility measures

Voter registration for the 2023 Kazakh legislative election was automatic, drawing from the national civil status register to compile a list of 12.19 million eligible citizens aged 18 and older with permanent residence in the country.[51][52] This process ensured inclusion based on verified demographic data, with voters able to confirm or update their status via the eGov mobile application, which also supported digital identification for authentication at polls.[53] Kazakh citizens abroad were accommodated through 77 polling stations at diplomatic missions across 62 countries, enabling diaspora participation without requiring return to Kazakhstan.[54] Accessibility measures prioritized enfranchisement for vulnerable groups, with the Central Election Commission mandating barrier-free polling stations equipped for unhindered entry, separate voting spaces, and tactile aids including Braille ballots for the visually impaired.[55][56] Home voting options, facilitated by mobile teams, extended to persons with disabilities and rural residents facing mobility challenges, allowing ballots to be cast at residences or remote sites.[57] Although no COVID-19 mandates remained in effect by March 2023, polling protocols incorporated ongoing hygiene practices such as surface disinfection and crowd control to maintain sanitary conditions.[46] Central Election Commission figures, cross-verified against civil registry records, demonstrated registration coverage approaching universality, with minimal discrepancies attributable to outdated addresses or expatriate status, thereby addressing and empirically countering claims of systemic voter exclusion.[58][7]

Nominations and participating entities

The Central Election Commission (CEC) of Kazakhstan registered candidates from seven political parties for the proportional representation portion of the Mäjilis election, comprising a total of 283 individuals on party lists submitted by February 15, 2023.[59] [60] These included Amanat with 90 candidates, Aq Jol with 54, People's Party with 52, Auyl with 25, Respublica with 23 (initially listed as 25 in some reports but adjusted post-verification), Nationwide Social Democratic Party with 19, and Baitaq Green Party with 18.[59] All seven parties, the entirety of those legally registered in Kazakhstan, met documentation requirements without rejection at this stage, reflecting the CEC's initial broad admission of established entities.[61] For the 29 single-mandate constituencies—introduced as part of post-2022 reforms to allocate 30% of seats (later adjusted to 29 seats)—the CEC approved 172 candidates following nomination verification completed by February 21, 2023.[62] Of these, 49 were independents (self-nominated individuals unaffiliated with parties), representing the debut of non-party contenders under the mixed system, while the remaining 123 were nominated by parties.[63] Initial nominations exceeded 800 for these districts, but rejections occurred primarily due to invalid or insufficient signatures (requiring support from at least 1% of registered voters in the district, or roughly 500-1,500 per constituency) and legal disqualifications such as prior convictions or administrative violations.[64] Nomination barriers, including stringent signature validation and eligibility checks, functioned as mechanisms to ensure contender viability and exclude potential disruptors, as evidenced by the low success rate of appeals to territorial election commissions and courts—fewer than 10% were overturned, per observer assessments.[7] Proponents, including CEC officials, framed these as empirical safeguards against extremism or fraud, citing verified cases of forged signatures in rejected submissions.[62] Critics, however, contended that subjective interpretations of "validity" and opaque verification processes enabled selective exclusion of reform-oriented or anti-establishment figures, though no systemic bias was conclusively proven beyond anecdotal claims from unregistered groups.[7] This filtering resulted in a managed field of 6 parties effectively competing nationwide (as one underperformed thresholds post-nomination) and limited district challengers, prioritizing stability amid post-unrest reforms.[4]

Political parties and candidacies

Profiles of major registered parties

Amanat, the ruling party since 1999 under its previous name Nur Otan until a 2022 rebranding, positions itself as a centrist force emphasizing national unity, stability, and progressive reforms aligned with President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev's agenda.[65] It functions as a big tent organization encompassing diverse views but prioritizes pro-government policies, having secured 71.09% of the proportional vote and 76 seats in the 2021 Mäjilis elections.[60] With historical dominance reflecting Kazakhstan's managed party system, Amanat has absorbed elements from former allies, underscoring limited ideological competition where opposition remains co-opted or sidelined, as seen post-2018 ban on the Democratic Choice movement.[66] Auyl People's Democratic Patriotic Party, established in 2015, advocates for agrarian and rural development, representing farmers' interests through policies on agricultural modernization and village infrastructure.[67] It garnered 9.11% of the vote in the 2021 elections, translating to seats via the mixed system, and maintains a pro-presidential stance focused on regional equity rather than systemic challenge.[60] Ak Zhol Democratic Party, founded in 2002, promotes center-right liberalism with emphasis on business deregulation, economic liberalization, and private sector growth.[68] It achieved 8.87% in 2021, securing 12 seats, and operates as a constructive partner to the ruling bloc, prioritizing market-oriented reforms over confrontation.[60] People's Party of Kazakhstan, evolved from the Communist People's Party in 2020, leans social-democratic with democratic socialist elements, targeting workers and pensioners through welfare expansion and social justice platforms. It obtained 9.12% in 2021 but aligns broadly with state policies, exemplifying the ideological convergence in Kazakhstan's parties lacking sharp divides.[60][66] Respublica Party, registered in January 2023 by entrepreneurs like Aidarbek Khodjanazarov, focuses on youth engagement, technological innovation, and inclusive governance to foster a high-tech ecosystem.[69] As a newcomer without prior electoral history, it embodies Tokayev-era reforms but within the framework of non-adversarial pluralism.[70] Baytaq Green Party, approved in November 2022 as the first new registration in nearly two decades, prioritizes ecological sustainability, environmental protection, and green policies complementing national reforms.[71] Led by figures advancing Aral Sea restoration initiatives, it supports presidential directions without oppositional edge, highlighting the state's controlled expansion of party options.[72]

Independent candidate participation

Independent candidates were permitted to contest seats in the 29 single-mandate districts for the Mäjilis, a provision reintroduced by 2022 constitutional amendments and implemented for the first time since the 2004 elections, as part of broader electoral reforms aimed at enhancing pluralism.[7] Self-nominated aspirants were required to collect signatures from at least 1 percent of registered voters in their district—typically 1,500 to 5,000 depending on district size—and submit a deposit of 1,050,000 Kazakhstani tenge (approximately $2,200 USD at the time), which was refundable only upon securing at least 5 percent of votes; these hurdles, while formalizing access, posed financial and logistical barriers, particularly for non-affiliated locals.[7] [73] Initial nominations exceeded 350 self-nominated candidates across the districts, with administrative reviews leading to around 370 ultimately competing on election day after deregistrations (54 cases, mainly for tax or early campaigning issues) and some court reinstatements.[7] Campaigning by independents was more visible in urban areas and online, where they promoted localized agendas distinct from party platforms, though many were established local notables such as entrepreneurs or community leaders with implicit ties to regional authorities. Successes occurred in districts with fragmented party competition, where independents capitalized on voter dissatisfaction with established lists, securing victories through personal networks rather than broad opposition to the regime.[7] Seven independents were elected, capturing all their seats from single-mandate districts and comprising about 7 percent of the 98 such seats, a marginal but symbolically significant outcome that observers viewed as providing limited additional voter choice amid dominant party control.[73] [7] While the reform empirically expanded candidacy options beyond party monopolies—addressing prior OSCE critiques of exclusionary systems—it carried risks of co-optation, as elected independents frequently aligned with the ruling Amanat faction in parliament, diluting their autonomy and reinforcing executive influence over legislative dynamics despite the nominal independence.[7] This pattern underscored the reform's milestone status in form but limited causal impact on genuine pluralism, given Kazakhstan's centralized political structure.[2]

Notable exclusions or withdrawals

Several political groups seeking registration to participate in the nationwide proportional representation contest were rejected by the Ministry of Justice, with 17 out of 23 applications denied between 2022 and early 2023 due to administrative discrepancies, including issues with member eligibility and personal data verification, amid criticisms of discretionary application of rules and limited judicial oversight.[7] The Central Election Commission (CEC) maintained that these measures enforced legal standards to ensure participant legitimacy, while opposition figures argued they functioned as systemic barriers to genuine pluralism, preventing broader ideological diversity in a context of post-January 2022 reforms aimed at controlled liberalization.[7] Notable among excluded entities was the opposition initiative Alga Kazakhstan, which had repeatedly failed to secure registration—marking ongoing denials that barred it from nominating candidates, as the group did not meet the criteria despite multiple attempts aligned with the election timeline.[74] Only seven parties ultimately gained approval for the proportional lists: Amanat, Ak Zhol, People's Party of Kazakhstan (PPK), Auyl, National Social Democratic Party (NSDP), Baytaq, and Respublika.[7] In the single-mandate districts, 54 majoritarian candidates faced deregistration primarily for tax declaration inconsistencies or premature campaigning, with 33 additional withdrawals occurring, often attributed to logistical or financial constraints though not detailed in official tallies.[7] Legal recourse was pursued in at least 27 cases, where courts reinstated 22 candidates, including instances where the Supreme Court overturned 14 district election commission (DEC) decisions, highlighting procedural flaws such as inadequate verification of assets like Soviet-era vouchers; however, some denials were upheld, underscoring uneven application of remedies.[7] These episodes illustrated tensions between regulatory enforcement and claims of arbitrary hurdles, with no comprehensive data on funding-related withdrawals but anecdotal reports of resource shortfalls deterring smaller candidacies.[7]

Campaign dynamics

Prevailing themes and policy debates

The campaign for the 2023 Kazakh legislative election centered on socioeconomic recovery and stability in the wake of the January 2022 unrest, with parties framing their platforms around President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev's "New Kazakhstan" agenda, which prioritized political reforms to address grievances from the Qandy Qantar protests, including enhanced social protections and governance modernization. Economic revitalization emerged as a core theme, with proposals focusing on job creation, salary increases, and business support to mitigate inflation and post-pandemic slowdowns, reflecting public concerns over living standards exacerbated by the 2022 events rather than broader ideological shifts.[6] Anti-corruption measures featured prominently, as parties pledged to dismantle oligarchic influences and recover state assets, directly linking these efforts to restoring trust eroded by the unrest and prior elite capture. Social welfare debates highlighted rural development, education access, and healthcare equity, with calls for pension hikes and youth employment underscoring causal ties to domestic instability over external democratic models. National unity was invoked to promote cohesion amid ethnic and regional tensions, positioning the election as a consolidation of reforms initiated post-2022 to prevent recurrence of violent protests.[75] Foreign policy received scant attention, with minimal divergence from Kazakhstan's established multi-vector diplomacy and neutrality in the Russia-Ukraine conflict, as domestic priorities overshadowed geopolitical debates. Policy contrasts were subdued, featuring the ruling party's emphasis on reform continuity against minor parties' advocacy for decentralization—such as local governance laws and judicial elections—and selective liberalization, yet without substantial ideological rifts, as all contenders aligned broadly with state-led modernization. This convergence stemmed from the controlled political environment, where opposition remained marginalized, limiting robust contestation.[46][6]

Strategies of the ruling Amanat party

The Amanat party, rebranded from Nur Otan in 2022 amid President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev's efforts to consolidate authority following the January unrest, undertook internal adjustments to align leadership with Tokayev's vision, including Tokayev's resignation as chairman and the nomination of Yerlan Koshanov as successor.[76][77] These changes facilitated a portrayal of the party as an evolution from the Nazarbayev era, emphasizing renewal while leveraging incumbency for continuity in governance.[47] Amanat's campaign centered on supporting Tokayev's "New Kazakhstan" agenda, highlighting post-unrest stabilization through political and economic reforms, rule of law enhancements, and national sovereignty.[7] The party promoted social and economic policies aimed at addressing public concerns, positioning itself as the guarantor of stability after the 2022 events that exposed underlying tensions.[7][78] Mobilization efforts involved nationwide activities such as workplace visits, indoor meetings, billboards, television appearances, and extensive online presence via social networks like Instagram and Facebook, with high posting frequency to engage supporters.[7] Volunteers and youth organizations played key roles, though campaign visibility remained lower in rural areas compared to urban centers, relying on established rural loyalty to the ruling structures.[7] Outdoor rallies were largely avoided due to regulatory barriers on public gatherings.[7] Financially, Amanat benefited from substantial resources, securing nearly 700 million Kazakhstani tenge in donations alongside 350 million tenge from party funds, enabling the largest share of media advertisements among contestants.[7] This incumbency advantage allowed dominant coverage in state-influenced media, reinforcing the party's stability-focused messaging without direct opposition contestation during the campaign period.[7][45]

Approaches by opposition and minor parties

The Auyl People's Democratic Patriotic Party centered its campaign on advancing rural interests, particularly the development of agriculture and the agro-industrial complex, targeting the roughly half of Kazakhstan's population residing in rural areas. With 25 candidates nominated to its party list—36 percent of whom were women, youth, or persons with disabilities—the party positioned itself as a defender of village infrastructure and sectoral reforms, contrasting its focused agenda with broader national priorities.[79] The Ak Zhol Democratic Party advocated for strengthening parliamentary oversight, implementing checks and balances, and promoting demonopolization to foster economic competition, while supporting a gradual shift toward a parliamentary republic. Nominating 54 party-list candidates and 23 in single-mandate districts, it appealed to voters favoring institutional reforms over the prevailing presidential system.[79] The People's Party of Kazakhstan emphasized representation for diverse ethnic groups and social strata, including workers' rights and inclusivity for youth and minorities, with 52 candidates averaging 50 years old and 40 percent in single-mandate races. Other minor parties, such as Baitak (focusing on environmental protection and green economy) and Respublika (prioritizing agricultural reforms and food security), similarly adopted niche platforms but operated with fragmented resources and limited media access, resulting in campaigns that largely echoed pro-reform themes without robust adversarial contestation. Overall, these parties faced funding shortages and subdued visibility, securing a combined 34 percent of the proportional representation vote across six non-ruling entities, though procedural observations noted no evidence of widespread irregularities impacting their efforts.[79][64]

Media environment and public engagement

State-owned broadcasters, which dominate Kazakhstan's media landscape, provided extensive and predominantly positive coverage to the ruling Amanat party during the campaign, allocating approximately 60 percent of airtime on public television to it according to OSCE/ODIHR monitoring.[7] Private media outlets exercised caution in their reporting, influenced by ongoing government pressure and self-censorship patterns that intensified following the 2022 unrest, resulting in limited critical analysis of electoral processes and avoidance of confrontational opposition coverage.[7] [80] Televised debates were scarce, with public broadcasters rarely facilitating multi-party discussions; the Central Election Commission organized a single nationwide debate on March 16, 2023, broadcast on state channel Khabar TV, featuring representatives from all seven registered parties.[27] [81] Independent platforms like YouTube hosted some candidate-level debates, but these lacked the reach of state media.[27] Public engagement shifted notably toward online spaces, where campaign activity appeared lively and diverse, particularly in single-mandate districts, though the Ministry of Information and Social Development actively monitored social networks for violations.[27] [27] This digital discourse contrasted with traditional media constraints, enabling broader voter interaction despite reports of coordinated fake accounts promoting pro-government narratives.[82] Independent journalists faced heightened risks, with at least seven physical attacks and threats recorded in early 2023 amid election coverage, including assaults on reporters in Almaty such as Daniyar Moldabekov on February 22.[83] [84] Overall, while OSCE/ODIHR observers noted some procedural access for media compared to prior elections, systemic biases and incidents underscored persistent challenges to pluralistic reporting.[7]

Election administration and conduct

Preparatory logistics and public funding

The Central Election Commission (CEC) of Kazakhstan oversaw the establishment of 10,223 precinct election commissions for the 19 March 2023 early parliamentary elections, including 77 stations abroad to facilitate voting for citizens overseas.[7] These commissions handled voter verification, with approximately 40 percent equipped with access to the state civil register for real-time checks.[7] Preparatory activities included finalizing voter lists by 26 February 2023 and registering seven political party lists comprising 281 candidates, alongside 435 majoritarian candidates by 18 February.[7] To ensure administrative readiness, the CEC conducted 7,817 training sessions reaching 86,260 election commission members and an additional 24,723 participants, focusing on procedural aspects such as interactive modules, though coverage of critical elements like vote secrecy and counting procedures was inconsistent.[7] Under Kazakhstan's Constitutional Act on Elections, public funding for campaigns is drawn from the republican budget and allocated to political parties proportionally to their vote shares in the prior parliamentary elections, affording the ruling Amanat party—holder of a parliamentary majority from 2021—the largest share and reinforcing state oversight of resource distribution.[7] Majoritarian candidates received targeted subsidies, with 317 utilizing allocations such as 450,000 Kazakhstani tenge (KZT) for media access and 300,000 KZT for campaign materials, though administrative barriers occasionally impeded equitable access.[7] The CEC introduced transparency protocols, including live-streaming of its sessions and timely online publication of decisions, alongside audits of funding use, to promote accountability in logistics and fiscal management.[7] These measures operated within a framework of centralized state control, as all expenditures were budgeted through republican channels without independent oversight of allocations.[85]

Voting day operations and turnout data

Polling stations opened at 7:00 a.m. local time on 19 March 2023 and remained open until 7:00 p.m., providing a 12-hour window for voting across the country's 10,223 precincts. The process unfolded without major incidents, as confirmed by international observers including the OSCE, who noted efficient operations despite some procedural shortcomings like inconsistent ballot secrecy. Video surveillance systems, installed at all stations per legal requirements, streamed live feeds online to promote accountability, though access was limited to authorized viewers. Early voting, conducted from 10 to 17 March, saw participation from approximately 1.2 million individuals, equating to roughly 10 percent of registered voters, primarily those unable to attend on election day due to travel or health reasons.[86][46][7] The Central Election Commission (CEC) reported a nationwide turnout of 54.19 percent, with 6,517, care of 12,032,550 eligible voters casting ballots. This figure, verified through protocol aggregation and aligned with preliminary tallies from domestic monitors, marked a decrease from the 71.12 percent in the 2022 presidential election but exceeded the 53 percent interim reports from midday voting. Regional variations showed higher engagement in southern oblasts like Turkistan (over 60 percent) and Zhambyl, attributed to stronger rural mobilization efforts, while urban centers like Almaty recorded lower rates around 45 percent. Independent assessments by OSCE/ODIHR observers corroborated the CEC's aggregate without identifying systemic inflation, though they highlighted isolated cases of undue influence not impacting overall validity.[5][87][7]

Provisions for vulnerable voter groups

The Central Election Commission of Kazakhstan facilitated mobile voting for voters unable to reach polling stations due to health impairments, advanced age, or caregiving responsibilities, enabling home delivery of ballots by election officials upon prior application. This provision supported an estimated 86,814 participants in the nationwide proportional contest, reflecting effective logistical outreach to isolated or mobility-limited individuals.[7] Accessibility measures for persons with disabilities included equipping polling stations with Braille voting templates, magnifying aids, and, where feasible, dedicated voting areas or ramps for unhindered entry, as mandated in pre-election preparations. Voter education materials and Central Election Commission broadcasts incorporated sign language interpretation, while ballots adhered to quotas requiring availability in Kazakh and Russian, the state's official languages, to address linguistic barriers among diverse groups.[7][56] Nomadic and rural voters in remote regions benefited from the same mobile teams dispatched to hard-to-reach locales, simplifying procedures for those with seasonal mobility constraints. Implementation data indicated robust participation via these channels, underscoring operational successes in extending franchise access despite observed inconsistencies, such as unsuitable layouts in 9 percent of stations and missing aids in 6 percent.[7]

Observation and evaluation

OSCE/ODIHR assessment and recommendations

The OSCE Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (ODIHR) deployed an election observation mission comprising 292 observers from 38 countries to monitor the 19 March 2023 early parliamentary elections in Kazakhstan.[64] The mission's final report, released on 14 July 2023, concluded that while the elections were efficiently administered in operational terms, they fell short of OSCE commitments and international standards for democratic elections, particularly in ensuring genuine pluralism and protecting fundamental freedoms.[64] Technical preparations, including voter registration and ballot production, were conducted transparently, and polling stations generally operated without major disruptions, with voting procedures mostly adhering to legal requirements.[64] Positive developments included an expansion of voter choice, with 15 parties contesting the proportional seats—more than in the 2021 election—and reforms allowing independent candidates in single-mandate districts, marking a step toward greater inclusivity amid post-2022 unrest pledges.[64] However, the lack of meaningful competition persisted due to the ruling Amanat party's structural dominance, restrictive candidate registration rules that disqualified potential opposition figures, and an uneven playing field favoring incumbents through state resources.[64] Observers noted widespread media bias, with state and private outlets disproportionately covering Amanat while limiting access for others, alongside constraints on freedoms of assembly and expression that hindered opposition campaigning.[64] On voting day, irregularities such as coerced family voting—where one ballot was cast for multiple household members—and instances of ballot stuffing or overvoting were documented in about 10 per cent of observed polling stations, undermining secrecy and voluntariness.[64] ODIHR issued 55 recommendations in the final report, categorized into priority areas like electoral system reform, enhancing the Central Election Commission's independence, ensuring equitable media access, and bolstering safeguards against vote-buying and intimidation.[64] [88] These build on prior assessments, urging comprehensive legal changes to foster political competition and transparency, though implementation of earlier ODIHR suggestions from 2016 and 2021 elections had been partial, with progress mainly in administrative logistics rather than substantive pluralism.[64] The report emphasized that while reforms post-January 2022 events showed intent, sustained adherence to OSCE standards requires addressing systemic barriers to multiparty contestation.[33]

Domestic monitoring efforts

Domestic monitoring efforts in the 2023 Kazakh legislative election relied on accredited citizen observers from political parties and civil society organizations, as stipulated by the Election Law, which formalized accreditation for entities with statutory election observation roles. The Central Election Commission accredited 14 of 17 nationwide organizations, while territorial election commissions approved 238 of 267 local groups, enabling broad participation despite noted concerns over preparation timelines and selective promotion of government-aligned entities.[7] On voting day, March 19, domestic observers covered 82 percent of observed polling stations, monitoring procedures amid reports of minor procedural issues, including restricted visibility in 12 percent of cases and freedom-of-movement limitations in 5 percent. During counting, presence dropped significantly, with observers unable to clearly view processes in nearly 40 percent of observed instances. Filed complaints encompassed allegations of protocol falsification, ballot stuffing, and voter list irregularities, totaling 66 submissions to the General Prosecutor, of which 25 prompted investigations—though few resulted in sanctions, suggesting limited follow-through on substantive violations.[7] These efforts highlighted a tension between procedural oversight and systemic incentives favoring stability, as domestic reports often underscored overall regularity while opposition-aligned observers documented obstructions, reflecting priorities for controlled pluralism over exhaustive contestation. Government-endorsed groups dominated accreditation and narratives of fairness, potentially marginalizing independent voices critical of deeper electoral dynamics.[7]

Comparative analysis of procedural integrity

The 2023 Kazakh early parliamentary elections demonstrated notable administrative efficiency in preparatory and voting phases, with the Central Election Commission (CEC) and lower commissions conducting operations within tight deadlines, including live-streaming of sessions and inclusive voter registration encompassing over 12 million individuals. International observers from the OSCE/ODIHR Election Observation Mission (EOM) assessed voting procedures positively in 97% of 1,201 observed polling stations, highlighting orderly openings and efficient processes that contributed to a calm election day devoid of reported violence or widespread intimidation. These elements aligned with basic OSCE benchmarks for logistical execution, marking incremental advancements in procedural management amid Kazakhstan's post-2022 reform context, where snap elections tested the system's capacity for rapid deployment.[7][89] However, weaknesses persisted in ensuring full procedural integrity, particularly during counting and tabulation, where the EOM evaluated processes negatively in approximately 50% of observed stations due to errors such as unannounced voter totals, pre-signed protocols, and discrepancies in ballot counts. Ballot secrecy faced minor compromises in 3% of stations, linked to polling layouts, group voting, and instances of photography, while training for poll workers inadequately emphasized these safeguards. Hints of undue influence emerged through campaign activities at workplaces, suggesting potential pressure on participants, though no overt vote-buying was documented; the playing field remained uneven, with self-nominated candidates encountering administrative hurdles like delayed bank accounts and high deposit requirements of KZT 1,050,000 (approximately USD 2,300). Cross-observer assessments converged on administrative strengths but diverged on deeper competitiveness, with empirical metrics underscoring transparency gaps that fell short of comprehensive OSCE standards for verifiable, equitable processes.[7][89] Overall, the elections reflected progress in operational reliability within an authoritarian framework—evidenced by a 54.21% turnout and minimal disruptions—but highlighted persistent causal links between institutional biases favoring incumbents and procedural vulnerabilities, prioritizing efficiency over pluralism in observer evaluations. Domestic monitors echoed efficiency praises but noted opaque complaint resolutions, with over 164 CEC and 400 court filings often unresolved due to procedural delays. This positions the contest as a step toward refined administration rather than a fulfillment of democratic benchmarks, where empirical compliance in voting outpaced deficiencies in aggregation and equity.[7][90]

Electoral results

National vote aggregates and seat allocations

The 2023 Kazakh legislative election utilized a parallel mixed electoral system for the 98 seats in the Mäjilis: 29 seats allocated proportionally from nationwide party lists requiring a 5% threshold, and 69 seats from single-member districts won by plurality.[4] In the party-list vote, the Amanat party obtained 53.9% of the votes (3,431,510 votes), Auyl People's Patriotic Democratic Party 10.9% (693,938 votes), Respublica 8.59% (547,154 votes), Aq Jol Democratic Party 8.41% (535,139 votes), People's Party of Kazakhstan 6.8% (432,920 votes), and National Social Democratic Party 5.2% (331,058 votes). Baytaq received 2.3% and was excluded, while votes against all parties totaled 3.9% (248,291 votes).[4]

Breakdown by constituency and party-list

In the 29 single-mandate constituencies, candidates from the Amanat party secured 22 seats, reflecting strong performance in rural and southern regions where the party maintains traditional support bases.[73] Independent candidates, ineligible for party-list allocation, won the remaining 7 seats, with concentrations in urban districts such as those in Almaty and Astana, where 184 of the 435 total majoritarian candidates were self-nominated and competition was more pronounced.[73][89] The nationwide party-list contest for 69 seats showed patterns of urban skew toward parties like Ak Zhol, which appealed to business and city interests, though detailed regional vote disaggregations were not published by the Central Election Commission.[89] Campaign activity was notably subdued in rural areas, with limited visibility for non-Amanat options outside major cities, while northern regions and ethnic-minority areas exhibited marginally higher engagement for alternatives via majoritarian races.[89] In Almaty, for instance, opposition-leaning self-nominees and party campaigns received disproportionate media focus compared to rural southern oblasts like Turkistan, where Amanat dominance aligned with lower contestation.[89] Seat allocation under the party-list system enforced a 30 percent combined quota for women, youth (under 29 years), and persons with disabilities across participating parties' mandates, resulting in fulfillment through prioritized distribution: 18 women, 12 youth, and 5 persons with disabilities elected from the proportional seats.[73] This mechanism ensured representation despite women comprising only 29 percent of party-list candidates and 20 percent of majoritarian contenders overall.[89][73]

Alignment with pre-election polling

Pre-election polling for the 2023 Kazakh legislative election was constrained by the snap timeline, with the dissolution of the Mäjilis announced on 5 January 2023 and voting held on 19 March, limiting comprehensive public surveys. A March 2023 opinion poll by the Demos Research Center indicated low declared party support, with Amanat at 20.8% among respondents identifying a preferred party, reflecting widespread disillusionment where 62.5% reported no party represented their interests; however, this appeared to capture reluctance in stated intentions rather than final behavior, as voter turnout reached 54% and results diverged toward established alignments.[92][6] A pre-election survey by the Strategy Center for Social and Political Studies, released on 10 March 2023, projected a victory for Amanat despite anticipated losses from its prior dominance (over 70% in 2021), forecasting it would retain a leading position amid fragmented opposition; this aligned with the final outcome where Amanat secured 53.99% of the proportional representation vote, translating to 69 of 98 Mäjilis seats via the mixed system.[93] Independent exit polls conducted on election day by the Institute of Public Policy, Asyl Arna, and Halyk Orda further corroborated this stability, estimating Amanat's share at 52.9–54%, closely matching official Central Election Commission (CEC) figures with discrepancies under 1%.[94] The consistency between limited pre-election forecasts, exit polls, and results underscored stable voter preferences following President Tokayev's post-2022 unrest reforms, which emphasized party-list proportionality and lowered thresholds but did not erode Amanat's core base tied to state institutions and rural constituencies. CEC-commissioned data and independent assessments showed minimal variances, countering unsubstantiated rigging claims by highlighting predictive accuracy in a low-volatility context where opposition parties like Auyl (10.9% actual) and Ak Zhol (8.9%) performed as anticipated without exceeding 7% thresholds for irrelevance.[4] This alignment suggested reforms influenced procedural access more than underlying electoral dynamics, with no empirical evidence of systematic manipulation evident in polling-outcome gaps.

Controversies and disputes

Allegations of procedural irregularities

The Central Election Commission (CEC) received 451 complaints prior to election day on 19 March 2023, primarily concerning candidate registration and campaign violations, with responses issued to 223 but lacking public transparency as decisions were not published or discussed in open sessions.[89] Post-election, an additional 164 complaints were lodged with the CEC, none of which resulted in recounts, while prosecutors received 66 election-day complaints and initiated 25 cases, including instances of voting on behalf of others.[7] International observers from the OSCE's Office for Democratic Institutions and Human Rights (ODIHR) documented procedural issues on election day, including group or family voting in 3 percent of visited polling stations and potential multiple voting in another 3 percent, alongside unauthorized persons present in 5 percent of stations and deliberate falsification of voter lists or results in 8 cases.[89] Counting procedures were assessed negatively in 58 of 128 observed polling stations due to omissions such as failing to announce voter totals or count ballots before sorting, though voting itself was orderly in 97 percent of 1,201 stations observed.[7] No widespread vote buying was reported by observers. Authorities responded with administrative fines against some precinct election commission members for violations, while emphasizing incidents as isolated rather than systemic, with the CEC noting overall smooth conduct despite "gross procedural violations" in isolated cases.[90] Empirical indicators support limited impact, as invalid ballots totaled 158,046 or 2.42 percent of votes cast, below typical thresholds signaling systemic irregularities, and no regional disaggregation of results was withheld to conceal discrepancies.[7]

Claims of political interference and media bias

International observers and opposition representatives raised concerns about media bias favoring the ruling Amanat party during the 2023 Kazakh legislative election campaign. The OSCE/ODIHR final report noted a media environment lacking pluralism, dominated by state-owned outlets that provided predominant, often promotional coverage to Amanat while allocating limited airtime to other contestants, which restricted opportunities for substantive policy debate.[7] Independent media monitoring documented harassment of journalists, including over 800 attacks or threats against media workers and outlets from 2022 to 2023, with some instances tied to critical election reporting.[80] Allegations of political interference centered on the misuse of administrative resources to benefit Amanat, including reports of local officials, such as akims, endorsing its candidates and exerting pressure on rivals. Opposition and civil society figures, including analyst Zhar Zardykhan, claimed public institutions systematically promoted Amanat while impeding alternative candidates through nomination barriers. For example, self-nominated aspirants faced legal challenges, as seen in the Supreme Court's rejection of S. Zakirova's appeal against her Majilis candidacy denial on March 7, 2023.[95] [96] Authorities countered that reforms enacted since 2022, including eased party registration thresholds that enabled nine parties to field candidates compared to six previously, enhanced competition and media access. Government officials asserted these changes resulted in more balanced coverage than in the 2021 election, with increased participation by non-ruling parties reflecting reduced interference. However, observers like OSCE/ODIHR maintained that such favoritism persisted despite the reforms.[97] [7]

Fraud accusations versus official rebuttals

Opposition candidates and independent observers alleged instances of ballot stuffing and the use of pre-filled vote protocols, with video evidence circulating of apparent irregularities at select polling stations during counting.[98] These claims centered on procedural lapses that purportedly inflated results for the ruling Amanat party, though specific hacker interference accusations lacked substantiation in public reports.[99] The Central Election Commission rejected assertions of systemic fraud, maintaining that received complaints—numbering around 164 directly to the CEC and hundreds more via courts—were investigated without uncovering evidence to invalidate nationwide tallies.[7] Official protocols emphasized orderly voting in 97 percent of observed stations and final turnout of 54.21 percent, attributing discrepancies to administrative errors rather than coordinated manipulation.[7] The OSCE/ODIHR mission documented isolated falsification indicators in eight of 128 observed countings and procedural flaws like pre-signed documents in over half of stations, yet found no pattern of widespread fraud capable of altering outcomes; transparency shortfalls in complaint resolution and tabulation, however, eroded credibility.[7] Empirical review reveals unsubstantiated mass-scale claims, with opposition underperformance more plausibly tied to weak grassroots mobilization and voter preference for established parties amid limited alternatives, rather than decisive electoral distortion.[6]

Post-election litigation and resolutions

Following the 19 March 2023 elections, Kazakhstan's Central Election Commission (CEC) received 164 complaints alleging violations, primarily concerning voter residency verification and procedural irregularities at polling stations.[7] Courts handled approximately 400 such post-election complaints, accumulating to 473 by 31 March 2023, with administrative courts processing 27 from candidates specifically challenging outcomes in single-mandate districts.[7] The Prosecutor General's Office registered 66 election-day complaints, leading to 25 investigative cases, though none resulted in widespread invalidations or recounts.[7] Most challenges were dismissed due to procedural shortcomings, such as missed 10-day filing deadlines, lack of jurisdiction, withdrawals, or failure to classify precinct protocols as appealable "administrative acts."[7] For instance, of 62 complaints related to residency disputes in regions including Abay, Akmola, Almaty, and others, 17 were rejected outright for these reasons, while the Supreme Court upheld a district election commission's refusal to register a candidate over undeclared Soviet-era assets.[7] Fines were imposed on select precinct election commission members for violations like improper voter marking, but broader remedies remained limited, with no mandates for vote recounts across constituencies.[7] The Supreme Court affirmed the majority of lower court and commission decisions by late March 2023, resolving disputes within expedited timelines—typically 5 days for commission reviews and 3 days for appeals—facilitating the swift convening of the new Mäjilis.[7] Observers noted a low success rate for challengers, with effective judicial remedies granted in isolated registration cases but opacity in decision publication and hearing access persisting as concerns.[7] This pattern of rapid, predominantly negative resolutions underscores the system's emphasis on procedural finality over extensive re-litigation, amid criticisms of insufficient transparency in handling evidence.[7]

Post-election developments

Convening of the new Mäjilis

The first session of the Mäjilis for its 8th convocation convened on March 29, 2023, at 10:00 a.m. in Astana, pursuant to a decree signed by President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev on March 27.[100] The session marked the formal transition to the newly elected lower house, with President Tokayev delivering the opening address to outline national priorities including economic modernization, social welfare enhancements, and anti-corruption measures.[101] All 98 directly elected deputies attended and collectively took their oath of office during the inaugural proceedings, affirming loyalty to the Constitution and commitment to the people's interests.[102] Subsequently, the deputies unanimously re-elected Yerlan Koshanov of the Amanat party as Chairman of the Mäjilis by secret ballot, a position he had held since February 2022; Koshanov's nomination reflected the chamber's alignment with the ruling party's dominant position, supported by its 69 seats from the election.[103][73] The session initiated organizational steps, including the formation of standing committees to handle legislative oversight in areas such as finance, defense, and social policy, ensuring operational continuity while aligning procedural agendas with Tokayev's reform directives emphasized in his address.[104] Full attendance and procedural efficiency underscored the Mäjilis's readiness to legislate on immediate priorities like government restructuring, as evidenced by the session's prompt handling of the executive's dissolution request.[105]

Government composition and leadership changes

Following the 19 March 2023 legislative elections, the Government of Kazakhstan, led by Prime Minister Älihan Smaiylov, resigned on 29 March 2023 in accordance with constitutional requirements, as the new Mäjilis (lower house of parliament) prepared to convene.[106][107] President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev subsequently renominated Smaiylov for the premiership, citing the need for experienced leadership to navigate economic challenges including volatile global oil prices and supply chain disruptions linked to the ongoing Russia-Ukraine war.[108] The Mäjilis approved the nomination on 30 March 2023, with the ruling Amanat party—securing 69 of 98 contested seats—providing the decisive support for continuity.[106] The reappointment preserved the core composition of the Second Smaiylov Government, with no wholesale cabinet overhaul; key portfolios in energy, finance, and national security remained under incumbents such as Energy Minister Almasadam Satkaliyev and Defense Minister Murat Bektanov (later replaced in unrelated September 2023 adjustments), prioritizing technocratic stability over disruption.[107] This approach aligned with the election's outcome, where pro-presidential forces dominated, reflecting voter priorities for policy consistency amid external pressures that threatened Kazakhstan's hydrocarbon export revenues—accounting for over 50% of budget income—and regional security concerns from cross-border refugee flows and sanctions evasion routes.[106] Minor adjustments, if any, focused on enhancing efficiency in digital and anti-corruption agencies, but the overall structure underscored a mandate for incremental reforms rather than radical shifts.[108] The swift approval process, completed within days of the resignation, minimized transitional uncertainty, enabling the government to address immediate fiscal imperatives like diversifying beyond oil dependency while maintaining macroeconomic targets of 4-5% GDP growth for 2023.[107] This continuity in leadership was positioned by official statements as a direct response to the electorate's endorsement of stability-oriented governance, avoiding the policy vacuums that could exacerbate vulnerabilities to global energy market fluctuations.[106]

Broader political and stability implications

The 2023 legislative election solidified President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev's control over Kazakhstan's political system following the January 2022 unrest, as the dominant performance of the pro-presidential Amanat party ensured a compliant parliament aligned with executive priorities. This outcome facilitated policy continuity in areas such as economic diversification and anti-corruption measures, which Tokayev had emphasized in post-unrest reforms, thereby reinforcing institutional stability without the disruptions seen in prior transitions.[3][6] While international observers like the OSCE acknowledged improvements such as the introduction of single-mandate districts enabling independent candidates and mandatory quotas for women, youth, and disabled representatives—enhancing representation beyond pure party dominance—they criticized persistent limitations on political pluralism and fundamental freedoms that restricted genuine competition. These quotas, requiring at least 30% women and 20% youth on party lists, marked a pragmatic step toward broader inclusivity, though OSCE reports, informed by commitments to multiparty systems, highlighted how state influence over media and candidate registration undermined opposition viability. Empirical evidence from the election's peaceful conduct and voter participation, however, counters narratives of outright illegitimacy, as no widespread violence occurred and the mixed electoral system expanded options compared to previous fully proportional setups.[46][109] The resulting legislative-executive alignment has prioritized stability-oriented agendas, including fiscal reforms and regional development, reducing risks of elite fragmentation that fueled the 2022 events. By mid-2023, this setup had enabled the new Mäjilis to endorse Tokayev's initiatives without significant gridlock, contributing to a calmer domestic environment amid external pressures like Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Such consolidation, while critiqued by Western-leaning observers for curbing pluralism, reflects causal priorities of regime survival and order in a resource-dependent state, where empirical post-election metrics show sustained public order absent the protest escalations of prior years.[110][3]
