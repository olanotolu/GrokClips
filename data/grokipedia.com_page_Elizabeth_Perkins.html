Elizabeth Perkins

Elizabeth Ann Perkins (born November 18, 1960) is an American actress recognized for her versatile performances in film and television, including prominent roles in romantic comedies and dramatic series.[1][2] Perkins rose to prominence in the 1980s with supporting roles in films such as About Last Night (1986) alongside Rob Lowe and Demi Moore, and Big (1988) opposite Tom Hanks, where she portrayed sophisticated urban women navigating relationships and professional challenges.[3][4] Her film career continued with lead roles like Wilma Flintstone in the live-action The Flintstones (1994), showcasing her ability to blend comedic timing with familial dynamics.[4] Transitioning to television, she earned critical acclaim for Celia Hodes in Showtime's Weeds (2005â€“2012), a role that garnered an Emmy nomination for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Comedy Series and highlighted her portrayal of a cunning, ambitious real estate agent entangled in suburban intrigue.[5][2] In her personal life, Perkins was diagnosed with latent autoimmune diabetes in adulthood, a form of type 1 diabetes, which she has managed publicly while continuing her career.[6] She maintains a low-profile existence, focusing on acting projects such as recurring appearances in This Is Us and recent films, underscoring a career marked by consistent output rather than blockbuster dominance.[3]

Early life and education

Childhood and family

Elizabeth Perkins was born Elizabeth Ann Perkins on November 18, 1960, in Queens, New York City.[3] Her father, James Perkins, worked as a businessman, farmer, and writer.[1] Her mother, Jo Williams, was a concert pianist and drug treatment counselor.[7] Perkins's paternal grandparents were Greek immigrants from Thessaloniki who anglicized their surname from Pisperikos to Perkins upon arriving in the United States; she is of Greek descent on her father's side and English descent on her mother's.[8] [1] [9] She was raised in Colrain, Massachusetts, near the Vermont border, after her parents divorced around 1963 when she was three years old.[10] Perkins described a rural, tomboyish childhood, including time on her grandfather's 600-acre farm in southern Vermont and summer work picking tobacco in local fields.[11] [12] [13]

Formal training and initial pursuits

Perkins enrolled at the Goodman School of Drama in Chicago, which was then affiliated with DePaul University, in 1978 and studied acting there for three years, graduating in 1981 with a Certificate in Acting.[1][14] The program emphasized practical performance skills through intensive theatrical training, drawing on the school's reputation for producing professional actors via ensemble-based workshops and classical repertory.[15] During her time there, she participated in student productions that honed her stage presence, though specific roles from this period remain undocumented in available records. Following graduation, Perkins remained in Chicago to pursue initial professional opportunities in local theater, including appearances with the Steppenwolf Theatre Company, known for its raw, ensemble-driven style that influenced her early approach to character work.[4] These engagements provided her first paid acting credits and exposure to collaborative play development, aligning with the Goodman curriculum's focus on improvisation and realism. By 1982, she relocated to New York City to seek broader stage roles, debuting off-Broadway in productions such as Life and Limb, which marked her transition from academic training to competitive professional auditions.[16] This period represented her foundational efforts to establish a theater career before venturing into film and television.

Professional career

Theater origins

Perkins initiated her professional acting career in theater following her graduation with a Bachelor of Fine Arts in acting in 1981, initially engaging in regional productions with Chicago's Steppenwolf Theatre Company, where she performed in works such as Lydie Breeze.[4][3] She relocated to New York City to pursue stage opportunities, building experience in off-off-Broadway venues including La MaMa Experimental Theatre Club.[4] Her Broadway debut occurred in 1984 as a replacement for the role of Nora Morton in Neil Simon's Brighton Beach Memoirs, commencing November 27, 1984, and continuing through the production's run until its close.[17] This appearance marked her entry into major New York theater, following the original production's premiere on March 27, 1983.[18] In early 1985, Perkins took on the role of Effie in the Off-Broadway premiere of Life and Limb by Billy Roche at Playwrights Horizons, directed by Thomas Babe, with the production opening on January 24, 1985.[19][20] The dark comedy explored themes of unemployment and marital strain in an Irish immigrant family, providing Perkins an early showcase for her dramatic range in a workshop-style setting at the nonprofit theater.[19] Subsequent Off-Broadway roles followed, solidifying her foundation in ensemble-driven New York stage work prior to her transition to film in 1986.[21]

Breakthrough in film

Perkins' film debut came in 1986 with a supporting role as Joan in About Last Night..., directed by Edward Zwick and co-starring Rob Lowe, Demi Moore, and James Belushi, marking her transition from theater to screen acting.[3] This adaptation of David Mamet's play Sexual Perversity in Chicago received mixed reviews but showcased Perkins in a comedic-dramatic ensemble, earning her initial notice in Hollywood circles. Her career breakthrough arrived in 1988 with the role of Susan Lawrence in Penny Marshall's fantasy comedy Big, opposite Tom Hanks as the child-turned-adult Josh Baskin.[22] Released on June 3, 1988, the film grossed over $151 million worldwide against a $18 million budget, becoming a critical and commercial hit that propelled both leads to stardom.[23] Perkins portrayed an ambitious toy company executive who develops a romantic relationship with the protagonist, unaware of his true age, delivering a performance noted for its blend of sophistication and vulnerability that humanized the film's central wish-fulfillment premise.[24] Critics praised her chemistry with Hanks, with Roger Ebert highlighting her as a "warm and sympathetic" foil to the story's whimsy, contributing to Big's 98% Rotten Tomatoes approval rating.[23] The success of Big established Perkins as a versatile supporting actress capable of anchoring romantic subplots in mainstream comedies, leading to subsequent high-profile roles in films like Avalon (1990) and The Flintstones (1994).[25] Unlike her debut, which was ensemble-driven, Big afforded her a more prominent character arc, solidifying her appeal in family-oriented blockbusters and differentiating her from period-specific theater work.[16] This role, achieved without major prior film accolades, underscored her rapid ascent through merit-based casting in 1980s Hollywood productions emphasizing relatable adult dynamics.[3]

Television prominence

Perkins achieved significant television prominence through her role as Celia Hodes in the Showtime dark comedy series Weeds, which aired from August 8, 2005, to September 16, 2012.[26] She portrayed the character, a domineering suburban mother and antagonist to the protagonist Nancy Botwin, for the first five seasons, from 2005 to 2009.[27] The series followed a widowed mother selling marijuana to support her family, with Perkins' performance earning critical acclaim for depicting Celia's sharp-tongued, image-obsessed personality.[25] Her portrayal garnered three Primetime Emmy Award nominations for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Drama Series, in 2005, 2006, and 2007.[28] These nominations highlighted her ability to blend humor with complexity in a role that contrasted her earlier film work, contributing to Weeds' cult following and its exploration of suburban hypocrisy.[25] Perkins departed the series after season five, citing the character's narrative arc, though she expressed interest in potential reunions.[27] Following Weeds, Perkins starred as Polly Greenfield in the ABC sitcom How to Live with Your Parents (For the Rest of Your Life), which ran for one season in 2013, focusing on multigenerational living dynamics.[3] She later appeared as Virginia Dixon, the mother of Rebecca Pearson, in recurring episodes of NBC's This Is Us from 2019 to 2020.[3] Additional notable television roles include Angela in Why Women Kill (2019) on CBS All Access and Madeline in Apple's Truth Be Told (2021).[29] These projects sustained her television presence, though none matched the acclaim of Weeds.[4]

Later career developments

Following the end of Weeds in 2012, Perkins maintained a steady presence in television through recurring and guest roles in both broadcast and streaming series. She recurred as Birdie, a veteran wrestler promoter, in Netflix's GLOW across its first two seasons from 2017 to 2019. In the NBC family drama This Is Us, she portrayed Janet Malone, the mother of Rebecca Pearson (played by Mandy Moore), in three episodes between 2017 and 2019.[30] Her performance as the calculating Jackie O'Neill in HBO's 2018 limited series Sharp Objects, opposite Amy Adams and Patricia Clarkson, drew attention for its intensity amid the show's psychological thriller elements.[31] Perkins continued with supporting parts in the 2020s, including a role in the second season of Fox's The Moodys in 2021 and appearances in Apple TV+'s Truth Be Told. In 2023, she guest-starred as Elena Daniels, president of the rival network NBN, in the episode "Update Your Priors" of Apple's The Morning Show.[32] That year, she also played Constance in seven episodes of HBO Max's Minx, a comedy about the 1970s adult magazine industry, and portrayed Isabel Villalobos in the second season of Apple's The Afterparty.[33] In film, Perkins provided voice work for the animated My Little Pony: A New Generation in 2021.[34] She is slated for supporting roles in two 2025 releases: as Evelyn in Paul Feig's thriller The Housemaid, starring Sydney Sweeney and Amanda Seyfried, set for theatrical release on December 19, and as Margaret McLanden in Another Simple Favor, the sequel to the 2018 mystery comedy.[35][29] These projects reflect a focus on character-driven ensemble work in prestige television and mid-budget films, with limited lead opportunities post-Weeds.[25]

Personal life

Marriages and relationships

Perkins married actor and director Terry Kinney on November 24, 1984, after meeting through theater connections in Chicago; the couple divorced in 1988 following four years of marriage.[36][37] Following her divorce, Perkins entered a relationship with television director Maurice Phillips, which lasted from 1989 to 1992; during this period, their daughter, Hannah Jo Phillips, was born on September 1, 1991.[37][38] In 2000, Perkins married Argentine-born cinematographer Julio Macat, with whom she has resided since; the union has produced no additional children for Perkins but includes three stepsonsâ€”Maximillian, Alexander, and Andreasâ€”from Macat's prior relationship.[39][40]

Family and parenting

Perkins gave birth to her only biological child, daughter Hannah Jo Phillips, on September 1, 1991, with television director Maurice Phillips.[1][7] Following her 2000 marriage to cinematographer Julio Macat, Perkins became stepmother to his three children from a prior relationship: Max, Iris, and Andy.[41] The blended family has appeared together publicly, including at events in Los Angeles.[42] In the mid-2000s, Perkins scaled back her acting commitments to prioritize hands-on parenting of her then-teenage daughter, stating that children "grow up really fast and then they're gone" and that missing those years means missing them permanently.[43] Her husband Macat has provided consistent support in managing family responsibilities alongside her career demands.[6] Perkins has expressed pride in her role as a parent, sharing sentiments of honor in being part of her children's lives via social media posts celebrating family milestones.[44]

Health diagnoses and management

Perkins was diagnosed with latent autoimmune diabetes in adults (LADA), a form of type 1 diabetes characterized by gradual autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing beta cells, in 2005 at age 44 following a three-year period of escalating symptoms including chronic fatigue, frequent headaches, blurred vision, excessive thirst, and unexplained weight loss.[45][46][47] Post-diagnosis, she reported initial overwhelming challenges, including a 40-pound weight gain attributed to insulin therapy side effects and fluid retention that left her feeling "puffed up like a basketball."[48][49] Management of her condition centers on daily insulin injections, strict blood glucose monitoring, and dietary adjustments to maintain stable levels, with Perkins emphasizing the need for patient self-advocacy after early medical oversights failed to test her glucose despite prior blood work.[50][51] She has integrated continuous glucose monitoring and insulin pumps into her routine, crediting community support from type 1 diabetes groups for helping her adapt and regain control after the initial shock.[6] Perkins has actively promoted awareness through partnerships, including collaborations with Sanofi US on the "Diabetes Co-Stars" campaign, which produced the 2013 documentary Strength in Numbers to highlight peer support's role in coping with the disease's demands.[52][53] By 2020, after 15 years of living with LADA, she described improved management via technology and mindset shifts, though she noted persistent risks like hypoglycemia during high-stress periods such as acting roles.[6][54]

Public advocacy and controversies

Autism awareness efforts

Perkins has participated in fundraising events aimed at supporting autism research and awareness. On September 18, 2006, she attended the "Acts of Love: Dreams" benefit organized by Cure Autism Now at the Geffen Playhouse in Westwood, California, an event designed to raise funds for autism-related initiatives.[55][56] Cure Autism Now, established in 1995, focused on accelerating biomedical research to identify causes and treatments for autism spectrum disorders, emphasizing genetic and environmental factors.[55] The organization merged with Autism Speaks in 2007 to expand its research and advocacy scope. Perkins' attendance at the event aligned with celebrity-supported efforts to promote public engagement and funding for such causes, though no further public advocacy roles or statements from her on autism have been documented.[57]

#MeToo participation and backlash

On November 13, 2017, Elizabeth Perkins joined the Take Back the Workplace March and #MeToo Survivors rally in Hollywood, an event protesting sexual harassment and assault in the entertainment industry, where she carried a handmade sign reading "James Woods #MeToo."[58] [59] The sign publicly linked actor James Woods to the #MeToo movement's focus on alleged perpetrators, though Perkins provided no contemporaneous details or evidence of any personal experience with him.[60] [61] This followed a September 2017 Twitter accusation by actress Amber Tamblyn, who claimed Woods had propositioned her when she was 16 years oldâ€”a charge Woods rejected as fabricated.[61] [58] Perkins' action drew immediate media attention but no subsequent clarification or formal complaint from her against Woods, leaving the gesture open to interpretation as either an implicit accusation or a broader callout amid the movement's momentum following Harvey Weinstein's exposure.[62] Woods, known for his conservative commentary and criticism of #MeToo's handling of unproven claims, did not directly address Perkins' sign but responded aggressively to related online commentary, such as a tweet by poker player Daniel Negreanu equating Woods to Roy Moore amid the rally coverage, calling Negreanu a "cowardly piece of s-" for amplifying unsubstantiated associations.[63] No lawsuits, investigations, or corroborating accounts involving Perkins and Woods materialized.[64] The incident exemplified early #MeToo tensions over anonymous or evidence-light public shaming, with Woods maintaining his innocence across similar allegations and Perkins later reflecting positively on the movement's role in exposing industry sexism without revisiting her sign in detail.[65] Perkins encountered no documented professional fallout, such as lost roles or public ostracism, distinguishing her participation from cases where accusers or accused faced career consequences.[66] Critics of #MeToo, including Woods' supporters, cited the episode as illustrative of potential overreach, where naming without substantiation risked reputational harm absent due process.[67]

Recognition and critical assessment

Awards and nominations

Perkins received three nominations for the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Comedy Series for her role as Celia Hodes in the Showtime series Weeds, in 2006, 2007, and 2009.[68] She earned two nominations for the Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress in a Series, Miniseries, or Made-for-Television Film for the same role, in 2006 and 2007.[69] Additional nominations include the 1992 Chicago Film Critics Association Award for Best Supporting Actress for The Doctor, the 2005 Satellite Award for Outstanding Actress in a Series, Comedy or Musical for Weeds, and the 2019 Critics' Choice Television Award for Best Supporting Actress in a Movie/Miniseries for Sharp Objects.[5]

Achievements and praises

Perkins achieved breakthrough recognition for her role as the sophisticated executive Susan Lawrence in Big (1988), opposite Tom Hanks, which critics praised for showcasing her comedic timing and dramatic range.[16] Her earlier performance as a surly friend in About Last Night... (1986) also drew high praise from reviewers for its sharp emotional depth.[70] These films established her as a versatile supporting actress capable of elevating ensemble casts. She earned critical acclaim for portraying Ann Kaye in Barry Levinson's Avalon (1990), noted for its poignant depiction of family dynamics across generations.[3] In The Doctor (1991), Perkins stood out opposite William Hurt as a terminal cancer patient, with reviewers highlighting her raw vulnerability and authenticity in the role.[3] Her portrayals of Susan Walker in Miracle on 34th Street (1994) and Wilma Flintstone in The Flintstones (1994) further demonstrated her adaptability in family-oriented blockbusters, contributing to their commercial appeal.[16][7] On television, Perkins received two Golden Globe nominations for Best Supporting Actress in a Series, Miniseries, or Made-for-Television Motion Picture for her role as Celia Hodes in Weeds (2005â€“2012), in 2006 and 2007.[5] She garnered three Primetime Emmy nominations for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Comedy Series for the same performance, underscoring her impact in the dramedy genre.[38] Additional honors include a 2019 Gracie Award for her contributions to media as a female performer, particularly highlighted in roles like Jackie O'Neill in Sharp Objects (2018), and a Critics' Choice nomination for Best Supporting Actress in a Limited Series or Movie Made for Television that year.[71][72]

Criticisms and setbacks

Perkins's portrayal of Susan Lawrence in the 1988 comedy Big has drawn retrospective criticism for featuring a romantic and sexual relationship between her adult character and Tom Hanks's character, who is physically adult but mentally a 13-year-old boy trapped in a grown body. Commentators have labeled the film's depiction as endorsing pedophilia or statutory rape, with the iconic piano scene cited as particularly unsettling by contemporary standards. In a 2021 Guardian interview, Perkins confirmed receiving online harassment, including being called a "paedophile" for the role, while defending the film's intent as innocent fantasy but conceding it "would not be acceptable today."[73][65] Early in her film career, Perkins endured a humiliating audition for an unspecified Mel Gibson project, where casting directors delivered stark feedback deeming her performance "too uptight" and lacking sensuality, prompting her to believe her acting prospects were ruined. She later reflected on the incident as her "worst moment," yet emphasized resilience, stating she achieved a "brilliant career" afterward while the film itself "tanked and wasn't any good."[74] Perkins has voiced disappointment over losing coveted roles, notably auditioning for Sally Albright in When Harry Met Sally... (1989), which went to Meg Ryan and became an iconic romantic comedy. In a 2025 interview, she described the missed opportunity as a significant regret, highlighting her strong screen test but ultimate rejection.[75] Certain projects marked professional setbacks through poor critical reception, including her lead as Wilma Flintstone in the 1994 live-action The Flintstones, which earned widespread panning for its script and execution despite grossing over $340 million worldwide. Reviewers faulted the adaptation's fidelity to the source material and overall execution, contributing to perceptions of it as a creatively hollow blockbuster.[76]

Filmography

Feature films

Perkins debuted in feature films with the romantic comedy About Last Night (1986), portraying Debbie Sullivan opposite Rob Lowe and Demi Moore. Her early roles often featured strong, independent female characters, as seen in Judd Nelson's legal drama From the Hip (1987), where she played Jody Thompson. Breakthrough commercial success followed with Big (1988), directed by Penny Marshall, in which Perkins starred as Susan, the executive love interest to Tom Hanks' child-in-adult-body protagonist.[22] Subsequent films included Barry Levinson's family epic Avalon (1990) as Ann Kaye, a supporting role in the romantic comedy Love at Large (1990) as detective Stella Wynkowski, and the comedy Enid Is Sleeping (1990), later retitled Over Her Dead Body, as Jane. In 1991, she appeared in two films: the romantic comedy He Said, She Said alongside Kevin Bacon and Meg Ryan as Lorie Bryer, and the medical drama The Doctor as June Ellis, William Hurt's wife. Perkins took on lead roles in the ensemble dramedy Indian Summer (1993) as Jennifer Morton and the live-action The Flintstones (1994) as Wilma Flintstone, opposite John Goodman. That year, she also played the skeptical executive Dorey Walker in the remake Miracle on 34th Street. Later credits encompass Moonlight and Valentino (1995) as Rebecca, the addiction drama 28 Days (2000) as Lily Cummings, the animated Finding Nemo (2003) voicing the clownfish Coral, Must Love Dogs (2005) as Carol, and The Ring Two (2005) as psychiatrist Dr. Emma Temple.
