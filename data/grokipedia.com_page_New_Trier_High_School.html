New Trier High School

New Trier Township High School District 203 is a public comprehensive four-year high school district founded in 1901, operating campuses in Winnetka and Northfield, Illinois, that serves approximately 3,700 students from Chicago's affluent North Shore suburbs including Glencoe, Kenilworth, Northfield, Wilmette, and Winnetka.[1][2][3] The district emphasizes a rigorous college-preparatory curriculum, with 98% of faculty holding advanced degrees, high Advanced Placement participation rates averaging 4.15 scores on exams, and a 97% college attendance rate among graduates.[4][3][5] New Trier consistently ranks among the top public high schools in Illinois, placing 10th statewide and 348th nationally in recent U.S. News evaluations, driven by strong academic outcomes and a 98% graduation rate.[6][7] Its athletic programs, comprising over 35 interscholastic teams, have secured more Illinois High School Association state championships than any other school in the state's history, underscoring a tradition of competitive excellence.[1][2] The student body is predominantly white (about 75%) with low minority enrollment (24%), reflecting the demographics of its high-income district where median household incomes exceed national averages.[8][6] Notable controversies include parental opposition to district policies perceived as promoting ideological indoctrination, such as equity initiatives and curriculum choices, leading to lawsuits over transparency in activities like Holocaust Remembrance Day events and broader debates on inclusivity measures that some argue foster discomfort rather than unity.[9][10][11]

Campuses and Facilities

Winnetka Campus

The Winnetka Campus of New Trier Township High School District 203, located at 385 Winnetka Avenue in Winnetka, Illinois, serves students in grades 10 through 12.[12] As the district's original and primary facility since its founding in 1901, it accommodates sophomores, juniors, and seniors, while the Northfield Campus handles freshmen.[2] The campus spans multiple buildings, including historic structures like Gates Gymnasium, which along with the campus boiler plant, meets criteria for listing on the National Register of Historic Places under historical and architectural significance.[13] Facilities at the Winnetka Campus support a comprehensive curriculum with dedicated spaces for academics, arts, and athletics. Key additions from a two-phase renovation project included the demolition of three aging buildings and construction of modern structures housing 25 classrooms, three science laboratories, and expanded athletic areas.[14] In 1975, new gyms and dance studios were constructed east of the central tower building to meet growing needs.[2] A $75 million East Side Academic and Athletic Project, completed in phases through 2023, introduced a multi-purpose building featuring a six-lane indoor track, a new competition gymnasium, additional classrooms, and science facilities to enhance both educational and extracurricular opportunities.[15][16] These developments reflect ongoing efforts to modernize infrastructure while preserving the campus's historical core, supporting enrollment of upperclassmen in a rigorous college-preparatory environment.[17]

Northfield Campus

The Northfield Campus is situated at 7 Happ Road in Northfield, Illinois, and exclusively serves the freshman class (9th grade) of New Trier Township High School District 203.[4] This configuration allows for a smaller, transitional learning environment tailored to incoming students before they move to the Winnetka Campus for grades 10 through 12.[2] The campus accommodates approximately 950 freshmen.[18] Originally established as New Trier West High School, the facility opened in 1965 to relieve overcrowding at the Winnetka Campus amid enrollment exceeding 4,000 students.[2] It expanded to a full four-year institution but faced closure as a comprehensive school in 1981 due to postwar enrollment declines that bottomed out after peaking at 6,558 district-wide in 1972; freshmen classes persisted there until 1985, after which the site stood largely vacant. Rising enrollment in the late 1990s prompted its reopening in 2001 as a dedicated freshman campus, a decision endorsed by the school board to manage growth without overburdening the main site.[19] [20] Academic facilities encompass multiple buildings housing departments such as English, social studies, modern and classical languages, and the New Trier Learning Center for alternative education.[21] A cafeteria and administrative offices support daily operations. Athletic amenities include a stadium and fields, which underwent phase-one renovations by August 2025, featuring turf replacements and conversions funded by a $10 million anonymous gift announced earlier that year.[22] [23] The campus also integrates specialized spaces like social work services and extension programs.[21] Leadership includes an associate principal overseeing operations.[24]

History

Founding and Early Development (1901â€“1964)

New Trier Township High School District 203 was formed to provide secondary education to residents of New Trier Township in Chicago's northern suburbs, drawing its name from the township established in honor of 19th-century German immigrants from Trier (Treves), Germany, who settled the area.[25] On August 5, 1899, township voters approved a $60,000 bond referendum to purchase approximately six acres of land in Winnetka and erect the initial school building, designed by architect Norman S. Patton.[20] [26] Construction costs exceeded the bond amount, totaling nearly $100,000 by completion.[27] The school convened for its inaugural day on February 4, 1901, accommodating 76 students and seven faculty members in the new brick edifice amid a snowstorm.[2] [28] Initial enrollment reflected the modest population of the township's villagesâ€”Wilmette, Kenilworth, Winnetka, Glencoe, and Northfieldâ€”with classes emphasizing core academic subjects alongside emerging vocational training.[29] By the 1904â€“05 school year, student numbers had risen to 129 with 10 teachers, surging to 179 students and 13 faculty in 1905â€“06, driven by population growth along the North Shore rail lines.[29] Rapid expansion necessitated immediate infrastructure additions: the East Wing, housing manual arts facilities and a study hall, broke ground in 1906 and finished in 1907, followed by the West Wing's completion in 1908, boosting capacity as enrollment hit 318 students and 16 teachers that year.[29] [27] Further enhancements included one of the nation's earliest indoor swimming pools, installed around 1911, which supported physical education programs and interscholastic athletics.[30] Through the interwar and early postwar periods, the Winnetka campus underwent iterative enlargements, including auditorium and classroom annexes in the 1920s and 1930s, to sustain a comprehensive curriculum amid steady demographic increases from suburbanization.[13] By the mid-20th century, persistent overcrowdingâ€”exacerbated by the post-World War II baby boomâ€”strained the single-campus model, with enrollment approaching limits that foreshadowed the 1965 opening of a freshman campus in Northfield.[25] These early decades established New Trier's emphasis on rigorous academics and extracurriculars, fostering a reputation for educational excellence rooted in community investment rather than external mandates.[31]

Expansion and Postwar Growth (1965â€“2000)

In response to surging enrollment driven by the postwar baby boom, New Trier Township High School District 203 opened a second campus, New Trier West, in Northfield, Illinois, in the fall of 1965, initially serving freshmen and sophomores even before full construction completion.[25][32] This expansion alleviated overcrowding at the original Winnetka campus, which was redesignated as New Trier East for upperclassmen.[25] District-wide enrollment continued to climb, reaching an all-time peak of 6,554 students during the 1972-73 school year.[32] To support the growing student body and extracurricular demands, the district added new athletic facilities on the Winnetka campus in 1975, including gyms and dance studios located front and east of the central tower building.[2] These enhancements reflected ongoing efforts to modernize infrastructure amid demographic pressures, though birth rate declines soon reversed the trend, with enrollment dropping sharply after 1973.[32] By the early 1980s, sustained enrollment reductions prompted operational changes: New Trier West ceased functioning as a full four-year high school in 1981, and the campuses fully merged at the Winnetka site in 1985 to optimize resources.[2][25] Renewed growth projections in the late 1990s, anticipating a 35 percent increase over the decade, led to plans for reactivating the Northfield campus as a freshman-only facility by the late 1990s, marking a return to dual-campus operations to handle capacity constraints.[33][34]

21st-Century Developments

In response to increasing enrollment pressures, New Trier High School reopened its Northfield Campus in August 2001 as a dedicated freshman facility, accommodating approximately 960 students while upperclassmen remained at the Winnetka Campus.[35][20] This marked a return to a two-campus model after the Northfield site had closed in 1981 amid earlier enrollment declines.[2] The reopening was led by Principal Jan Borja, who focused on integrating social-emotional learning into the curriculum.[36] Enrollment trends shifted in subsequent decades, with district officials projecting a continued decline from recent levels, potentially extending through 2029, prompting considerations for facility utilization adjustments.[37] Between 2003 and 2009, updates to the library and other infrastructure occurred at both campuses.[2] Major facility renovations dominated later 21st-century developments. In February 2021, the Board of Education approved the East Side Academic and Athletic Project at the Winnetka Campus, a $79.5 million initiative to renovate and replace aging structures, culminating in the completion of a new three-story complex featuring a 2,000-seat competition gym in August 2023.[38][39][15] This was followed by plans for central campus updates, including the North and Tower buildings, and Phase One renovations beginning in summer 2025, which involved demolishing and upgrading science labs and administrative areas.[40][41] A 15-Year Facility Framework adopted in 2020 outlined ongoing investments through 2035, encompassing roof replacements, mechanical system upgrades, electrical and plumbing improvements, and tuckpointing at both campuses to address aging infrastructure. These efforts reflect sustained commitment to modernizing facilities amid fluctuating enrollment and evolving educational needs.[42]

Governance and Administration

Board of Education

The Board of Education of New Trier Township High School District 203 serves as the district's primary governing authority, consisting of seven members elected at-large to staggered four-year terms.[43] It holds responsibility for formulating district policies, adopting the annual budget, hiring and evaluating the superintendent, overseeing facility planning, and ensuring adherence to educational standards under Illinois law.[1] The board conducts regular public meetings, approves major contracts, and addresses strategic initiatives such as curriculum development and equity programs, often delegating implementation to administrative staff while retaining final decision-making power.[44] Board members are elected during consolidated elections held on the first Tuesday in April of odd-numbered years, with approximately three to four seats up for election biennially to maintain continuity.[45] Candidates typically run nonpartisan campaigns, often vetted and endorsed by the New Trier School Board Caucus, a volunteer group of delegates from feeder districts that interviews applicants and recommends slates based on community priorities like academic excellence and fiscal responsibility.[46] [47] In the April 1, 2025 election, four seats were filled unopposed by caucus-endorsed candidates: incumbents Avik Das, Kimberly Alcantara, and Sally Tomlinson, alongside newcomer Joo Serk Lee, reflecting low contention in the affluent district's electorate.[48] As of October 2025, the board is led by President Jean Hahn (elected 2019, re-elected 2023) and Vice President Sally Tomlinson (elected 2021), with other members including Kimberly Alcantara (elected 2021, re-elected 2025), Avik Das, Joo Serk Lee (elected 2025), Courtney McDonough, and Sally Pofcher (elected 2023).[49] [50] Members serve on standing committees covering areas like finance, policy, facilities, and community engagement to deliberate on specialized issues before full-board votes.[51]

Superintendents and Leadership

Dr. Peter Tragos serves as superintendent of New Trier Township High School District 203, having assumed the role on July 1, 2025.[52] Prior to this appointment, Tragos held positions within the district since 1998, including as a history teacher and later as assistant superintendent for curriculum and instruction.[53] His selection followed a nationwide search by the Board of Education, continuing a pattern of internal promotions for the position, as seen with predecessors who advanced from assistant superintendent roles.[54] Tragos succeeded Dr. Paul Sally, who retired on June 30, 2025, after serving as superintendent from 2017 and contributing 31 years overall to the district in roles such as math teacher and department chair.[55] Sally's tenure emphasized academic excellence and community engagement, with the district maintaining high performance metrics during his leadership.[56] Before Sally, Dr. Linda Yonke held the superintendency from 2006 to 2017, focusing on curriculum enhancements and facility expansions.[54] Earlier, Dr. Henry Bangser led from 1990 to 2006, mentoring numerous educators and overseeing significant administrative developments, including the naming of the district's administration building in his honor upon retirement.[57] The superintendent oversees district operations, supported by a leadership team that includes assistant superintendents for key areas such as curriculum, human resources, and student services, as well as campus principals.[58] Denise Dubravec serves as principal and assistant superintendent for the Winnetka campus, managing daily operations for grades 9-10, while a counterpart handles the Northfield campus for grades 11-12.[59] Additional roles, such as chief communications officer and directors for athletics and fine arts, report to the central administration, ensuring coordinated implementation of board policies across the district's 3,800 students and two campuses.[58] This structure promotes specialized oversight while aligning with the superintendent's strategic direction.[52]

Enrollment and Student Demographics

Enrollment Process and Boundaries

New Trier Township High School District 203 encompasses New Trier Township, bounded approximately south of Lake Cook Road, east of Harms Road/Northfield Road/Forestway Drive, north of Central Street/Isabella Street, and west of Lake Michigan.[60] The district serves the communities of Glencoe, Kenilworth, Northfield, Wilmette, Winnetka, and portions of Northbrook and Glenview.[1] Enrollment is restricted to residents within these boundaries, with automatic eligibility for students living in the district.[61] New students must register through the Registrar's Office, providing proof of residency such as a lease, deed, or utility bill, along with required documents including birth certificate, immunization records, and previous school transcripts. The process coordinates placement testing for subjects like mathematics and foreign languages to determine appropriate course levels, followed by course selection advising.[62] For incoming freshmen, typically from feeder elementary and middle schools in the district, placement testing occurs in November prior to enrollment, such as on November 15 and 22 for the Class of 2030.[24] The Registrar's Office handles all admission aspects, ensuring alignment with the school's rigorous curriculum structure. Non-resident enrollment is limited and may require board approval under specific circumstances, such as interdistrict agreements or tuition-based attendance.[62] As of October 1, 2025, district enrollment stood at 3,685 students, reflecting a slight decline from prior years.[63]

Socioeconomic and Demographic Profile

The New Trier Township High School District 203 serves affluent North Shore suburbs including Glencoe, Kenilworth, Northfield, Wilmette, Winnetka, and portions of Glenview and Northbrook, communities known for high property values and professional demographics.[3] The district's median household income surpasses $200,000, more than double the Illinois state average, correlating with low poverty rates and robust financial support for education.[64] Only 3% of students qualify for free or reduced-price lunch, positioning the district in the lowest decile statewide for low-income enrollment and underscoring its socioeconomic homogeneity.[65] Student demographics reflect the surrounding areas' composition, with enrollment totaling 3,683 as of October 2025.[3] The body is predominantly White at 75%, followed by Asian at 10% and Hispanic at 8%, comprising a total minority enrollment of approximately 25%.[66]

Academic Programs

Curriculum Structure

New Trier High School employs a credit-based curriculum structure aligned with Illinois state standards but exceeding minimums through rigorous core requirements and extensive electives. Students must earn a minimum of 23 credits during their four years, with courses meeting five times per week awarding one credit per year (0.5 per semester) for classes starting with the graduating class of 2022.[67][68] This system emphasizes a balanced progression from foundational to advanced coursework, with mandatory sequencing in core disciplines to build cumulative knowledge.

Beyond core mandates, the curriculum supports personalization through over 200 courses, including honors-level options in grades 9-12 and 36 Advanced Placement (AP) courses restricted to juniors and seniors to ensure prerequisite mastery.[67] World languages, while not required for graduation, feature multi-year sequences in languages like French, Spanish, Mandarin, and Latin, with heritage and immersion pathways available. Elective breadth spans departments such as applied arts, music, theatre, and interdisciplinary programs, reflecting the school's progressive education heritage that prioritizes innovation and student-driven inquiry.[70] A distinctive feature is the Integrated Global Studies School (IGSS), an optional junior-senior program blending social studies, English, and fine arts via project-based learning focused on global issues, experiential fieldwork, and interdisciplinary synthesis.[67] Students develop four-year academic plans upon freshman entry, advising course loads of seven to eight periods daily to exceed minimums and align with postsecondary goals, with college admissions emphasizing transcript rigor over mere credit accumulation.[68] This structure fosters depth in foundational skills while accommodating acceleration, though freshmen and sophomores are barred from AP to prioritize sequential building blocks.[67]

Academic Performance and Outcomes

New Trier High School students demonstrate strong performance on standardized assessments relative to state and national averages. For the Class of 2024, the mean ACT composite score was 27.5, exceeding the Illinois average of approximately 20 and the national average of 19.5.[1] Similarly, the mean SAT score for the Class of 2023 was 1217, significantly above the state average of around 970.[71] [72] However, SAT scores, particularly in English Language Arts, have declined since 2019, with averages dropping from over 615 to around 595 by 2022.[73] [74] Advanced Placement (AP) participation is robust, with 1032 students taking 2,667 exams in spring 2025, yielding an average score of 4.15 out of 5.[3] This average indicates a high proportion of exams scoring 3 or higher, qualifying for college credit, consistent with reported pass rates exceeding 90 percent on administered tests.[75] [6] The school offers over 30 AP courses, restricted to juniors and seniors, emphasizing rigorous preparation without early enrollment for underclassmen.[3] Graduation rates remain elevated, at 97 percent for recent cohorts, surpassing the state average of 87 percent.[6] [76] Postsecondary outcomes reflect this academic strength, with 96 percent of the Class of 2023 enrolling in colleges or universities, predominantly four-year institutions.[71] The Illinois State Board of Education designates the school as exemplary, placing it in the top 10 percent statewide based on metrics including test proficiency and graduation.[77]

Extracurricular Activities

Athletics

New Trier High School's athletic teams, known as the Treviansâ€”a reference to Roman soldiers from Trier, Germanyâ€”compete in the Central Suburban League and are sanctioned by the Illinois High School Association (IHSA). The program offers interscholastic sports across three seasons, including boys' and girls' cross country, field hockey (girls), football (boys), golf, soccer, swimming and diving, tennis, volleyball (girls), basketball, gymnastics (girls), hockey (club), lacrosse, softball (girls), baseball, track and field, and water polo (girls), among others.[78][79] The department prioritizes student-athlete experiences that foster growth beyond competition, with participation involving hundreds of students annually.[80] The school holds the record for the most IHSA state championships among Illinois high schools, with successes spanning multiple decades.[2] Boys' tennis leads with 14 titles, including victories in 1982-83, 1984-85, 1986-87, 1987-88, 1995-96, 1996-97, 1997-98, 1999-00, 2000-01, 2004-05, 2009-10, 2010-11, 2015-16, and 2020-21.[81] Boys' swimming and diving has secured 7 championships, from 2003-04 to 2012-13.[81] Other notable team titles include 3 in boys' soccer (2006-07, 2008-09, 2023-24), 3 in boys' golf, 2 in baseball, and 1 each in boys' basketball (1965-66) and boys' lacrosse (2018-19).[81] Girls' programs have also achieved state success, including titles in swimming, golf, and other sports during periods of strong performance, such as multiple wins in the early 2020s.[82] The boys' track and field team captured its first IHSA state championship since 1944 in 2024, marking a historic resurgence with individual records set, such as Will Landwer's 10.24-second 100-meter dash in 2025, the fastest in IHSA history.[83][84] Facilities supporting these efforts include a competition pool at the Winnetka campus, contributing to swimming dominance.[85] The Athletic Department maintains a Hall of Honor, inducting standout coaches, athletes, and contributors, such as football coach Walter Aschenbach (1923-1965) and recent classes recognizing sectional and state achievements.[86][87] While the school-sanctioned hockey program operates as a club, it has amassed 25 state titles independently.[88]

Clubs, Arts, and Other Programs

New Trier High School maintains over 150 extracurricular clubs and organizations at its Winnetka campus, spanning academic pursuits, political engagement, service initiatives, and special interests.[89] These include competitive teams such as the Chess Club, Debate Team, and Environmental Club, alongside broader social service learning opportunities.[90] Students access tailored club recommendations via the New Trier Club Finder application, which filters options by interests, availability, and campus.[91] Performing arts programs emphasize dance, music, and theatre activities conducted beyond the standard school day.[92] The Music and Theatre department stages eight fully produced or workshop productions annually, including plays, musicals, a student-led variety show, a student-directed play showcase, and an improvisation troupe.[93] Music offerings feature four wind ensembles totaling 150 participants, which have performed at Lincoln Center and Carnegie Hall.[94] Specialized clubs support these efforts, such as Club Classique, Comedy Club, Drumline, Jam Club, and Northfield Dance Ensemble.[95] Visual arts initiatives include open-studio art clubs open to all students without prerequisite experience, complemented by a range of course offerings detailed in the annual Program of Studies.[96] The New Trier Fine Arts Association, a parent-led nonprofit, funds these programs through grants, scholarships, and financial aid to enhance student access and post-high school arts preparation.[97]

Awards and Recognitions

National and Regional Accolades

New Trier High School has received the National Blue Ribbon School designation from the U.S. Department of Education twice, first in 1990 and again in 2009, recognizing its overall academic excellence and performance among public high schools.[98][99] The 2009 award specifically highlighted the school's high state and national test scores, rigorous curriculum, and consistent student outcomes.[100] In national rankings, New Trier Township High School Winnetka placed 371st among U.S. public high schools in the 2025 U.S. News & World Report evaluation, which assesses factors including college readiness, graduation rates, and proficiency in math and reading.[6] Prior assessments ranked it 322nd nationally in 2021 and 307th in 2019.[101][102] At the regional level, the school has ranked among Illinois' top public high schools, placing 13th in the state per the 2025 U.S. News rankings.[103] It received an "Exemplary School" summative designation from the Illinois State Board of Education for its Northfield campus in recent evaluations, reflecting top statewide performance with no underperforming student groups, though the Winnetka campus shifted to "Commendable" in 2023 after holding "Exemplary" in 2022.[104][105] New Trier was the first Illinois high school district to achieve a top financial rating in 2001, contributing to its sustained recognition for operational and academic excellence.[106]

Student and Program Achievements

New Trier High School students have demonstrated strong performance in the National Merit Scholarship Program, with 43 seniors named semifinalists in the 2026 competition cycle announced in September 2025, representing the highest number in recent years and positioning them to advance toward finalist status and potential scholarships totaling nearly $26 million nationwide.[107][108] In the prior year's cycle, 34 students achieved semifinalist recognition based on their PSAT scores, underscoring a pattern of high standardized test performance among top performers.[109] These accolades reflect the school's emphasis on rigorous preparation for college admissions metrics, though semifinalist status does not guarantee scholarships and depends on additional criteria like SAT scores and school recommendations. The Science Olympiad program, a student-led competition team preparing for events in biology, chemistry, physics, and engineering, has secured notable national and regional placements. In May 2025, the varsity team finished 6th overall at the national tournament in Lincoln, Nebraskaâ€”the program's best national result to dateâ€”after advancing from state-level competitions where they earned 2nd place among 59 Illinois schools.[110][111] The team also won 1st place in the varsity division at a regional event in March 2025 and medaled in invitational tournaments at MIT and Solon High School earlier that year, highlighting proficiency in hands-on scientific challenges.[112] Individual and team successes in these areas contribute to the school's reputation for fostering competitive academic skills, with programs like Science Olympiad relying on after-school practices and funding from the New Trier Educational Foundation to support event-specific materials and travel.[113][114] While these achievements are verifiable through competition results, they occur amid broader critiques of overemphasis on metrics like National Merit in affluent districts, potentially at the expense of diverse skill development.

Controversies and Criticisms

Seminar Day and Civil Rights Programming

Seminar Day at New Trier High School involves periodic all-school events, originating in the early 1990s, where regular classes are replaced by student- and teacher-developed seminars on designated topics, with mandatory attendance for students.[115] These are not annual occurrences and have addressed various issues over time. A prominent example occurred on February 28, 2017, under the theme "Understanding Today's Struggle for Racial Civil Rights," featuring a keynote address, small-group adviser sessions, and workshops on topics including systemic racism, white privilege, microaggressions, implicit bias, and historical analyses tied to left-leaning interpretations like those in Howard Zinn's framework.[116][117] The event, costing an additional $30,000 beyond standard daily operations exceeding $533,000, was organized by school staff, students, and administrators to examine racism from purportedly multiple angles.[117][118] The 2017 Seminar Day provoked substantial parental opposition, culminating in the formation of "Parents of New Trier," which protested at a February 20 school board meeting and gathered hundreds of petition signatures demanding cancellation or revisions for perceived ideological imbalance.[119][120] Critics argued the content reinforced racial divisions by prioritizing collective guilt narratives over individual agency or alternative perspectives, such as colorblind policies and opposition to race-based preferences, without including conservative viewpoints from figures like Star Parker, Corey Brooks, or Allen West.[117][118] Superintendent Linda Yonke dismissed these calls, asserting changes were infeasible due to timing and that factual civil rights history inherently lacks need for "opposing" coverage.[117] School officials countered that the programming reflected internal consensus and community values, dismissing objectors as a vocal minority under 1% of the district, while a supporting petition amassed over 4,100 signatures.[118][117] Following the event, student feedback revealed polarization: approximately 60% reported positive impacts on awareness of inequities, while others described sessions as guilt-inducing or ideologically slanted, exacerbating tensions rather than fostering unity.[121] Civil rights-themed Seminar Days have faced recurring scrutiny, including in 2024, when parents again accused the format of promoting unbalanced, divisive content aimed at ideological conformity rather than objective education.[122] Retrospective critiques, such as a 2025 analysis, portray the 2017 iteration as an early exemplar of approaches emphasizing inherent group-based privileges and systemic determinismâ€”doctrines later challenged empirically for overstating structural causes of disparities while underemphasizing behavioral and policy factors.[9] No formal alterations to the Seminar Day model resulted from these disputes, though a separate mandatory "white privilege" seminar was canceled in April 2017 amid analogous parental pushback.[123]

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Initiatives

New Trier High School maintains a Commitment to Equity framework, aiming to foster a culture that appreciates differences by exploring individual and group identities, with equity defined as countering stereotypes to ensure fairness in opportunities and experiences.[124] This includes operationalizing initiatives like "Be Brave, Be Kind, Be Proud" to promote respect and self-expression, alongside the Student Belonging Council to enhance inclusion.[125][126] The district supports at least 14 student affinity groups as safe spaces for marginalized identities, such as the Black Student Alliance, Jewish Affinity Group, and Skittles for LGBTQ+ students, with recent expansions to include staff affinity groups in 2024.[127][128] Additional resources include TrevTips, an anonymous reporting system for bias incidents, and cultural clubs to address equity concerns.[129] Equity efforts are overseen by liaisons, including English teacher Kristan Kenney and social studies teacher Alex Zilka, who coordinate professional development and policy implementation as of 2024.[130][131] Critics, including parents and advocacy groups, have argued that these race- and identity-based affinity groups foster division by segregating students rather than promoting universal inclusion, as highlighted in a 2024 FOIA lawsuit settlement that released over 4,400 pages of emails critiquing separate Arab American and Jewish clubs amid rising antisemitism concerns.[132] A pro-human rights group in 2022 accused the policies of creating an uncomfortable environment for some students by prioritizing identity-based protections over individual merit.[11] Professional development under DEI has drawn scrutiny, including payments of nearly $90,000 from 2014 to 2017 for "Beyond Diversity" seminars facilitated by the Privilege and Equity Group (PEG), which emphasized concepts like "whiteness" and were later criticized as promoting critical race theory frameworks.[133] The district conducted another "Beyond Diversity" training in February 2025, shortly after a U.S. Department of Education advisory on February 14 urging schools to discontinue DEI practices to comply with civil rights laws, with detractors labeling it toxic and abusive for assigning oppressor-oppressed roles based on race.[134][9] Equity liaison Alex Zilka's enrollment in 2024 trainings on "justice for Palestine" and "settler colonialism" further fueled complaints of ideological bias in staff development.[135] In response to a March 2025 executive order targeting indoctrination, school officials affirmed continued support for equity work as essential for student safety, while a Change.org petition urged halting DEI alongside addressing antisemitism, reflecting ongoing parental discontent.[136][137] Despite these efforts, New Trier's student body remains predominantly white at 78% as of 2021, prompting internal calls for more robust diversity measures amid external critiques of the initiatives' effectiveness and divisiveness.[138]

Transparency and FOIA Disputes

In February 2024, Parents Defending Education (PDE), a nonprofit group advocating against ideological content in schools, submitted a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request to New Trier Township High School District 203 seeking emails from January 1 to February 15, 2024, containing the terms "Holocaust," "Palestine," or "Gaza" sent or received by 13 specified administrators and faculty members.[10][132] The request stemmed from controversy over a student-planned Holocaust Remembrance Day activity on January 27, 2024, organized by the German Club, German classes, and Jewish Affinity Group, which involved distributing "#WeRemember" stickers but was canceled by Superintendent Paul Sally due to insufficient notice and preparation concerns amid heightened community sensitivities related to the Israel-Hamas conflict.[10] On March 1, 2024, Associate Superintendent Chris Johnson denied the request, classifying it as unduly burdensome because it would require extensive time and resources to search, review, and redact sensitive student or personnel information, potentially outweighing any public interest.[10][132] District Communications Director Nicole Dizon stated that the administration had asked PDE to narrow the scope but received no response, emphasizing that New Trier applies "the same legal standards" to all FOIA requests with a goal of transparency.[10] PDE filed a lawsuit against the district on April 10, 2024, in Cook County Circuit Court, alleging a willful violation of the Illinois Freedom of Information Act through unlawful withholding of public records and seeking a court order for release, a declaration of noncompliance, and attorney fees.[139][132] The suit contended that the district's "unduly burdensome" exemption was improperly invoked without sufficient justification, such as specifying the volume of emails involved.[139] The case, initially set for an August 8, 2024, hearing, concluded with an undisclosed settlement signed by Superintendent Sally on July 26, 2024, under which the district produced over 4,400 pages of emails without admitting any wrongdoing; PDE withdrew the suit in September 2024 and covered its own legal costs.[132] This resolution followed broader parental concerns about antisemitism and administrative handling of the remembrance event, though the district maintained the cancellation was procedural rather than content-based.[10]
