Al-Mawasi, Rafah

Al-Mawasi, Rafah is a narrow coastal strip in the southern Gaza Strip, part of the broader Al-Mawasi area spanning approximately 1 kilometer in width and 14 kilometers in length, characterized by sandy dunes, fertile soil suitable for agriculture, and reliance on fishing and farming by its traditional Bedouin Palestinian inhabitants.[1][2] The region, covering about 2.2 square kilometers in its Rafah section, supported a pre-war population of around 6,000 people focused on water-dependent livelihoods.[3] Following the Hamas attack on Israel on October 7, 2023, which initiated the ongoing Gaza war, Israeli military operations to dismantle Hamas networks in Rafah led to the evacuation of over one million Palestinians to Al-Mawasi, designated by the Israel Defense Forces as an expanded humanitarian zone to minimize civilian exposure to combat.[4] This relocation transformed the sparsely populated area into a sprawling tent encampment housing hundreds of thousands, resulting in extreme population densities exceeding 30,000 people per square kilometer and exacerbating challenges such as limited access to clean water, sanitation, and medical care.[5] Despite its intended safe status, Al-Mawasi has witnessed Israeli strikes targeting alleged Hamas militants operating within the crowded zone, highlighting the tactical embedding of combatants among civilians that complicates military efforts to neutralize threats without collateral risks.[4][6]

Geography and Environment

Location and Physical Features

Al-Mawasi is a narrow coastal strip situated in the southern Gaza Strip, extending westward from the city of Rafah along the Mediterranean Sea coastline. It forms part of the Rafah Governorate and lies approximately 30 kilometers southwest of Gaza City, bordering Egyptian-controlled territory to the south near the Rafah Border Crossing. The area is characterized by its position on the southwestern edge of Gaza's coastal plain, transitioning into the Sinai Peninsula's desert landscapes eastward.[7] Physically, Al-Mawasi measures about 14 kilometers in length and 1 kilometer in width, comprising roughly 3% of the Gaza Strip's total 365 square kilometers. The terrain consists primarily of sandy hills, dunes, and scrubland, with fertile pockets suitable for limited agriculture adjacent to the beachfront. This coastal zone features a narrow band of dunes and beach near the sea, giving way to drier, less vegetated inland areas.[4][8][9]

Agricultural and Coastal Characteristics

Al-Mawasi constitutes a narrow coastal plain in southern Gaza, stretching about 14 kilometers along the Mediterranean Sea and averaging 1 kilometer in width, dominated by sand dunes and a direct shoreline that forms part of the Gaza Strip's coastal morphology.[2] [10] The terrain features mobile and semi-fixed dunes with sparse scrub vegetation, creating a distinct ecosystem influenced by sea breezes and saline conditions, while the beachfront supports limited natural habitats amid human restrictions on access.[9] [11] Agriculturally, the area's sandy, low-fertility soils are nonetheless cultivated through irrigation drawn from the adjacent coastal aquifer, enabling production of crops suited to semi-arid coastal conditions.[2] Key staples include guava, potatoes, sweet potatoes, onions, and dates, alongside vegetables grown in protected plastic houses to mitigate wind and salinity exposure.[3] Citrus orchards have historically dotted the landscape, leveraging the dunes' stabilization for root crops and fruit trees, though overall yields depend on brackish water management and dune erosion control.[12] The fine sandy texture, while challenging for heavy mechanization, reduces tunneling feasibility and supports specialized farming practices adapted to the coastal interface.[13]

Demographics and Pre-War Economy

Population Composition

Prior to the 2023 Gaza war, Al-Mawasi hosted a small resident population estimated at approximately 9,000 people, primarily Palestinian Arab families of Bedouin origin who relied on agriculture, fishing, and limited livestock rearing in the coastal enclave.[4][14] These communities, numbering over 6,000 individuals in the immediate pre-war period, inhabited a sparse network of fewer than 100 structures amid sand dunes and farmland, with no significant non-Palestinian presence following Israel's 2005 disengagement from Gaza.[14] The onset of the war drastically altered the area's demographics, transforming it into a primary destination for internally displaced persons (IDPs) as Israeli military operations prompted mass evacuations from northern and central Gaza. By March 2024, UNRWA registered 377,854 individuals in Al-Mawasi, reflecting an influx of civilians fleeing urban centers like Gaza City and Khan Younis, resulting in a population density surge from a pre-war baseline of about 1,200 people per square kilometer to 30,000â€“40,000 per square kilometer by mid-2024.[15][16] This displaced population remained overwhelmingly Palestinian Arab, comprising families from diverse Gaza regions, with a high proportion of women and children consistent with Gaza's overall youthful demographic profile, though specific breakdowns for Al-Mawasi are limited.[16] As of mid-2025, Al-Mawasi continues to shelter a densely packed civilian population amid its designation as a humanitarian zone, with densities reported near 48,000 people per square kilometer in tent encampments, driven by ongoing displacement orders and restricted access to other areas.[17] The composition remains homogeneousâ€”nearly all Sunni Muslim Palestinians, including remnants of the original Bedouin residents alongside IDPsâ€”facing acute overcrowding in makeshift shelters, though exact figures fluctuate due to military activities and aid constraints.[17][5] No verified data indicates non-Palestinian or foreign nationals in significant numbers.

Economic Role in Gaza

Al-Mawasi, a coastal strip in Rafah, has historically served as a key agricultural area within Gaza's constrained economy, leveraging its fertile soils, access to groundwater, and proximity to the Mediterranean for crop cultivation and fisheries. Prior to the 2023 war, residents derived their primary livelihoods from farming vegetables, fruits, and other produce on lands enriched by the area's natural aquifers, contributing to local food security amid Gaza's broader import dependencies and export restrictions.[2][18] These activities supported an estimated significant portion of Rafah's rural output, though Israeli-imposed closures since the early 2000s repeatedly curtailed market access, reducing agricultural production by up to 70% in affected periods by limiting sales to internal Gaza markets or Israel.[19] Fishing operations along Al-Mawasi's shoreline formed another economic pillar, with small-scale fleets targeting the coastal waters despite chronic restrictions on Gaza's overall fishing zone, capped at 6-12 nautical miles under the Oslo Accords but often enforced more narrowly. In 2020, Gaza's fishing sector sustained around 18,000 direct livelihoods province-wide, with Al-Mawasi's location enabling seasonal catches of sardines, sardelles, and other species that supplemented household incomes and protein supplies in a region where formal employment averaged below 40% pre-2023.[20] However, naval blockades and permit denialsâ€”averaging over 80% rejection rates in some yearsâ€”stifled growth, confining economic contributions to subsistence levels rather than scalable exports.[21] The area's economic role extended modestly to informal trade near the Rafah border, including limited cross-border exchanges of goods like fuel and construction materials through tunnels, though such activities were predominantly concentrated in Rafah proper rather than Al-Mawasi's dune-dominated terrain. Overall, these sectors underscored Al-Mawasi's function as a peripheral yet vital supplier in Gaza's pre-war economy, where agriculture and fisheries accounted for roughly 10-15% of GDP contributions despite comprising under 5% of the land area, hampered by water scarcity, soil degradation from over-irrigation, and recurrent conflict disruptions.[22][18]

Historical Development

Early History and Restrictions

Al-Mawasi, a narrow coastal strip approximately one kilometer wide and fourteen kilometers long along the Mediterranean shore south of Rafah, has long been inhabited by Palestinian Bedouin communities primarily engaged in agriculture and fishing. The area's traditional economy relied on mawasi farming, an ancient method of cultivating sand dunes by removing surface sand to access shallow groundwater tables, enabling the growth of fruit trees, vines, citrus, grapes, and vegetables. This practice, documented in the southern coastal plain extending into Gaza, saw significant expansion during the late Ottoman period (from the 1870s) and the British Mandate (1917â€“1948), with cultivated areas increasing eightfold to around 176,735 dunams by the 1940s, supported by improved irrigation technologies and transportation infrastructure such as the 1920s railway line.[23] Following the 1967 Six-Day War and Israel's occupation of the Gaza Strip, Al-Mawasi became effectively isolated as an enclave surrounded by the Gush Katif bloc of Israeli settlements, established starting in the 1970s for security and agricultural purposes. These settlements, including Netzarim and Kfar Darom, hemmed in the area to the east, restricting eastward expansion and access to broader Gaza infrastructure. Israeli military policy treated Al-Mawasi as a contained Palestinian pocket within controlled territory, imposing checkpoints and barriers to regulate movement and mitigate threats from the settlements' proximity.[2][12] Restrictions intensified during the Second Intifada after September 2000, with Israel frequently closing access points, applying arbitrary and shifting criteria for entry and exit that left residents stranded or unable to reach Khan Yunis for markets, schools, or medical care. Goods transport to urban centers dropped by up to 90% from pre-intifada levels, causing agricultural produce to spoil and exacerbating economic hardship in a region dependent on water-rich farming. Checkpoints also delayed emergency healthcare, contributing to complications in births and surgeries, while imports of essentials like school supplies were curtailed and items such as electronics or computers often banned. These measures, justified by Israel as necessary for settler security amid ongoing violence, severely curtailed residents' freedom of movement and economic viability until the 2005 disengagement.[2]

Israeli Disengagement and Aftermath

Prior to the 2005 disengagement, Al-Mawasi functioned as an isolated Palestinian enclave surrounded by Israeli settlements in the Gush Katif bloc, with severe restrictions on resident movement imposed by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) since the late 1970s and intensified during the Second Intifada.[2] Access to adjacent areas like Rafah and Khan Younis required permits and checkpoints, effectively confining approximately 1,500-2,000 residentsâ€”primarily Bedouin farmersâ€”to a narrow coastal strip for essential services, education, and medical care, exacerbating poverty and dependency on humanitarian aid.[2] [24] Israel's unilateral disengagement plan, approved by the cabinet in June 2004 and executed between August 15 and September 12, 2005, involved the evacuation of all 21 Gaza settlements, including those near Rafah such as Morag and Rafiah Yam, and the withdrawal of IDF forces from inside the Strip.[25] For Al-Mawasi, this culminated in the dismantling of surrounding barriers and checkpoints, formally ending its enclave status on September 12, 2005, when the last IDF positions were vacated.[26] The move integrated the area into the broader Palestinian-controlled Gaza territory, removing internal isolation that had persisted for five years.[27] In the immediate aftermath, the disengagement yielded tangible gains in freedom of movement for Al-Mawasi residents, who gained unrestricted access to Rafah and Khan Younis for the first time in years, facilitating school attendance for children and access to markets and healthcare previously barred by checkpoints like Tuffah.[24] United Nations assessments noted enhanced internal connectivity across southern Gaza, including Al-Mawasi, with no reported incidents of violence during the final withdrawal phase in the area.[26] However, external controls remained intact, as Israel retained authority over Gaza's airspace, territorial waters, and most land crossings, including the Rafah-Egypt border, which was coordinated via European monitors but prone to closures.[28] Over the longer term, the disengagement failed to stabilize the Rafah region, where Al-Mawasi's coastal proximity to the Egyptian border enabled expanded smuggling networks, including tunnels for weapons and goods that proliferated under Palestinian Authority (PA) governance.[29] Hamas's victory in the January 2006 Palestinian legislative elections, followed by its violent seizure of Gaza from the PA in June 2007, transformed the area into a base for militant operations, prompting Israel and Egypt to impose a blockade that restricted economic development and agricultural expansion in Al-Mawasi despite the end of direct occupation.[30] Rocket fire from southern Gaza, including Rafah, escalated post-2005, with over 4,000 launches recorded by 2008, justifying Israeli security responses that indirectly affected peripheral zones like Al-Mawasi through aerial surveillance and buffer zone enforcement along the Philadelphi Corridor.[30] Local agriculture persisted but stagnated amid chronic unemployment exceeding 40% in Rafah governorate by 2007, as investment inflows anticipated after disengagement did not materialize due to governance failures and conflict.[31]

Strategic and Military Context

Significance to Hamas Operations

Al-Mawasi's coastal location and proximity to Rafah have enabled Hamas to exploit the area for rocket launches and operational concealment amid displaced populations. The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) documented at least 116 rockets fired by Hamas from within the designated humanitarian zone of Al-Mawasi toward Israeli communities by December 13, 2023, with additional barrages, including 14 rockets from the area and vicinity on May 24, 2025, originating near civilian tents.[32][33] Hamas has embedded command-and-control centers in Al-Mawasi, leveraging its status as a purported safe zone to shield military infrastructure. On September 10, 2024, IDF airstrikes targeted such a center in the area, eliminating Hamas operatives who were directing attacks; the military stated the site was integrated into civilian tent encampments housing displaced Palestinians. Similar operations have addressed Hamas militant concentrations, as evidenced by strikes on September 9, 2024, that killed at least 19 in a targeted response to embedded fighters.[34][35] Hamas has also used Al-Mawasi to coerce civilian presence for tactical advantage, preventing evacuations to maintain human shields around operational sites. In October 2024, Gazan residents reported Hamas operatives physically assaulting civilians with sticks to block departures from humanitarian areas in the vicinity, ensuring populated cover for terrorist activities during IDF advances. This pattern aligns with broader Hamas doctrine of operating from civilian-dense zones, complicating Israeli responses while sustaining low-level threats from the strip.[36]

Israeli Security Perspectives

Israeli security officials view Al-Mawasi and the adjacent Rafah area as a critical threat due to persistent Hamas military activity, including rocket launches directed at Israeli territory from within or near the designated humanitarian zone. On multiple occasions, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) have documented rocket fire originating from Al-Mawasi, such as 14 rockets launched from positions near civilian tents on May 24, 2025, underscoring Hamas's exploitation of displaced populations for cover.[33] This pattern aligns with broader assessments that Hamas embeds operational cells in civilian-heavy zones to deter Israeli responses and facilitate attacks.[37] The IDF has cited the presence of Hamas command structures, weapon caches, and tunnel networks in Rafah and Al-Mawasi as necessitating targeted operations to dismantle remaining terrorist infrastructure. For instance, precision strikes in Al-Mawasi have been justified as responses to intelligence on senior Hamas figures, such as the July 13, 2024, airstrike aimed at military commander Mohammed Deif and his deputy Rafa Salama, who were operating amid civilian tents.[9] In Rafah specifically, IDF forces have uncovered and destroyed extensive tunnels used for smuggling weapons, ambushes, and sustaining militant mobility, including a 1-km-long network razed in May 2025 after interrogations of captured Hamas operatives revealed its location.[38] These subterranean assets enable Hamas to evade detection, regroup, and potentially launch cross-border incursions, posing an ongoing risk to southern Israeli communities.[39] From an Israeli strategic standpoint, failing to neutralize Hamas remnants in this southern enclave would allow the group to retain a launchpad for future offensives, undermining the war's objective of preventing repeats of the October 7, 2023, attacks. The IDF has adjusted humanitarian zone boundaries in Al-Mawasi multiple times, such as in July 2024, explicitly due to "significant terrorist activity and rocket fire" emanating from the area, prioritizing force protection and civilian evacuation where feasible despite Hamas efforts to block displacements.[37] Operations continue to focus on high-value targets and infrastructure elimination, with officials emphasizing that Hamas's deliberate co-location with civilians complicates but does not preclude necessary defensive actions.[40]

Involvement in the Gaza War (2023â€“Present)

Designation as Humanitarian Zone

The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) designated Al-Mawasi, a coastal strip west of Khan Younis in southern Gaza, as a humanitarian zone in late 2023 to direct civilians away from active combat areas during operations against Hamas following the October 7 attacks.[41] This designation was part of broader evacuation orders issued via leaflets, phone calls, social media announcements by IDF Arabic spokesperson Avichay Adraee, and maps published by the military, instructing residents of northern and central Gaza to relocate southward for safety.[9] The zone was presented as an area where the IDF would avoid strikes except in cases of identified threats, aiming to facilitate civilian protection amid urban warfare.[41] Initial boundaries encompassed approximately 5.4 square miles of the Al-Mawasi area, expanded from earlier safe corridors in Rafah and Khan Younis to accommodate growing numbers of displaced persons as military operations intensified in southern Gaza.[42] By December 2023, hundreds of thousands had been directed there, with the IDF coordinating limited aid deliveries through the zone via the Kerem Shalom crossing, though international observers noted insufficient infrastructure for long-term habitation.[41] The designation was unilateral, without formal endorsement from the United Nations or Hamas authorities, and relied on dynamic mapping updated via IDF public statements to reflect operational needs.[43] Subsequent adjustments included expansions on May 6, 2024, to include additional coastal areas, and further enlargements in October and November 2024 to support ongoing displacements from Khan Younis and Gaza City.[43][42][44] In September 2025, the IDF continued directing evacuees from Gaza City to Al-Mawasi, describing it as a humanitarian zone despite prior boundary contractions in July 2024 that reduced its safe status in parts.[45] These changes were justified by the IDF as necessary to counter Hamas presence, with maps disseminated to clarify safe paths and areas.[9] The zone's role evolved with the war's progression, serving as a temporary refuge while aid agencies like UNRWA operated limited facilities there under Israeli oversight.[46]

Patterns of Displacement

Following the Hamas attacks on October 7, 2023, the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) issued initial evacuation orders directing over 1 million Palestinians from northern Gaza to southern areas, including the Al-Mawasi coastal strip near Rafah, which was designated as part of a humanitarian zone by late 2023.[47][41] This initial southward movement concentrated displaced populations in Khan Younis and Rafah, with Al-Mawasi serving as an intended safe area amid ongoing military operations.[48] In March 2024, as operations shifted south, the IDF ordered residents of Rafah to evacuate to Al-Mawasi on March 31, marking an early pattern of directing civilians from urban Rafah to the less developed coastal zone.[49] This was followed by larger-scale orders in May 2024; on May 6, the IDF instructed those in eastern Rafah to relocate to an expanded humanitarian area incorporating Al-Mawasi, prompting an estimated 300,000 people to move within days.[43][50] By late May, nearly 1 million individuals had been displaced from Rafah since early May, many concentrating in Al-Mawasi and adjacent zones, exacerbating overcrowding in tent encampments.[51] Subsequent patterns involved iterative evacuations from Al-Mawasi itself. On July 22, 2024, the IDF issued an order for portions of Al-Mawasi previously deemed safe, displacing residents amid targeted operations against militants.[49] Into 2025, between March 18 and June 16, Israel issued 21 displacement orders affecting Rafah, Khan Younis, and Al-Mawasi, leading to further shuffling of populations within shrinking designated areas.[52] By September 2025, renewed orders directed hundreds of thousands from Gaza City southward to Al-Mawasi, continuing the cycle of mass relocations despite its prior expansions and contractions as a humanitarian refuge.[53] Overall, Human Rights Watch documented 184 IDF evacuation orders from October 2023 to August 2024, many funneling civilians toward Al-Mawasi before subsequent adjustments.[48]

Israeli Military Operations in and Near the Area

In May 2024, the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) issued evacuation orders for residents of eastern Rafah to relocate to the expanded humanitarian area in Al-Mawasi, facilitating increased aid supplies, field hospitals, and tent accommodations there.[54] On May 7, 2024, the IDF launched a precise ground operation in Rafah targeting four Hamas battalions estimated to include thousands of fighters, along with command centers, weapons caches, and smuggling tunnels near the Egyptian border.[55] [56] Ground forces, including armored brigades and engineering units, advanced into Rafah, securing the Philadelphi Corridor along the border to disrupt cross-border smuggling routes exploited by Hamas.[57] During the Rafah offensive, IDF troops uncovered extensive Hamas tunnel infrastructure, including a 2-kilometer route adjacent to the Rafah crossing on June 6, 2024, featuring multiple shafts, sub-routes for weapons transport, and attack initiation points.[58] Another 1.5-kilometer tunnel was located near the crossing, used by militants for movement and operations.[59] These discoveries supported IDF claims that Rafah served as a major Hamas logistical hub, with tunnels enabling evasion of detection and sustainment of combat capabilities. Operations continued into late 2024, with IDF forces dismantling additional tunnels and eliminating over 1,000 militants in the Rafah sector by September 2024, according to military assessments.[60] Airstrikes targeted militant positions in Al-Mawasi despite its humanitarian designation, based on intelligence of Hamas operatives embedding among displaced civilians. On July 13, 2024, an IDF strike in the Al-Mawasi area west of Khan Younis eliminated Mohammed Deif, commander of Hamas's military wing, along with Rafi Salama, head of the Rafah Brigade, though Gaza health authorities reported 90 Palestinian deaths in the vicinity.[61] Similar precision strikes occurred on September 10, 2024, targeting a senior Hamas figure in a tented area of Al-Mawasi, resulting in at least 19 deaths per local reports, with the IDF stating the action addressed an immediate threat.[35] In 2025, operations persisted nearby, with Southern Command units encircling central Gaza areas including Al-Mawasi by July to counter regrouping Hamas elements, while airstrikes addressed tunnel networks and fighter concentrations in adjacent Khan Younis.[62] [63] The IDF maintained that Hamas exploited humanitarian zones for operations, including preventing civilian evacuations and storing weapons, necessitating targeted responses.[36]

Humanitarian Impacts

Civilian Living Conditions

Civilians in Al-Mawasi, a narrow coastal strip near Rafah designated by Israel as a humanitarian zone since May 2024, primarily reside in makeshift tents amid extreme overcrowding.[64] By September 2025, Israeli evacuation orders had directed hundreds of thousands of displaced Palestinians to the area, resulting in population densities exceeding those in confined detention facilities.[53] [65] The zone, spanning roughly 14 kilometers in length but limited in width, hosts over one million people in less than 15% of Gaza's territory as of July 2025, exacerbating space shortages for basic activities like sleeping and sanitation.[16] Access to clean water and sanitation remains critically inadequate, with only 40% of drinking water facilities functional across Gaza by June 2025, including in Al-Mawasi.[66] Displaced families report raw sewage flooding tent areas, overflowing trash piles, and insufficient latrines, leading to widespread contamination during summer heat and rainy seasons.[67] [68] In Al-Mawasi specifically, residents like those interviewed in August 2024 described no access to potable water, contributing to the spread of skin infections and gastrointestinal illnesses.[69] Health conditions have deteriorated due to these environmental factors, with diseases linked to poor sanitation and malnutrition comprising 70% of outpatient visits at facilities serving displaced populations as of October 2025.[70] Hypothermia claimed at least four infant lives in Al-Mawasi tent encampments during the December 2024 winter, highlighting inadequate shelter against extreme weather.[71] Malnutrition rates reached 21.5% in nearby Gaza City by August 2025, with similar risks in Al-Mawasi due to limited aid inflows and fuel shortages hampering water treatment.[72] Despite some post-ceasefire aid scaling in October 2025, ongoing shortages in hygiene kits, bedding, and medical evacuations persist, with over 740 deaths awaiting evacuation reported by that month.[73] [74]

Aid Delivery Challenges and Diversion Claims

Aid delivery to Al-Mawasi and Rafah has been hampered by stringent Israeli security inspections at border crossings like Kerem Shalom and Nitzana, which delayed or restricted truck entries, with inflows suspended entirely from March to May 2025 following the end of a ceasefire.[75][76] Internal distribution within these areas faced further obstacles from ongoing military operations, which disrupted aid convoys and access to displaced populations in Al-Mawasi's tent encampments, exacerbating risks of spoilage for perishable goods amid limited cold-chain infrastructure.[64][77] Once inside Gaza, aid trucks en route to southern zones including Rafah and Al-Mawasi encountered widespread interception, with UN data indicating that up to 88% of trucks and 85% of aid tonnage were looted or stolen before reaching intended recipients in the first half of 2025, often by armed groups or criminal elements operating amid chaotic security conditions.[78][79] Distribution sites in Rafah and Al-Mawasi became focal points of violence, with reports of over 1,000 Palestinians killed and 7,200 injured while attempting to access rations, attributed by aid groups to crowd crushes, looting, and Israeli strikes near convoys, though Israeli authorities described some incidents as resulting from Hamas-orchestrated chaos.[80][81] Allegations of systematic diversion by Hamas have persisted, with Israeli officials claiming the group seizes aid for resale or military use, citing intercepted shipments and Hamas's governance role in Gaza as enabling such control; however, a July 2025 USAID internal analysis reviewed 156 theft incidents and found no evidence of massive or widespread Hamas involvement, attributing most losses to unknown actors (40%), armed groups (22%), or unarmed civilians (16%), while acknowledging gaps in monitoring due to operational risks.[82][83] Similarly, Israeli military sources admitted in July 2025 to lacking proof of routine Hamas theft from UN aid stocks, despite earlier assertions, prompting U.S. and UN agencies to emphasize that criminal gangs, not necessarily Hamas, conducted much of the looting under fragmented authority.[84][85] These findings contrast with broader patterns of aid loss, where Hamas's de facto control raises questions about indirect facilitation, though direct evidentiary links remain unverified in official probes.[77]

Controversies and Debates

Civilian Casualties from Strikes

On May 28, 2024, Gaza health authorities reported that Israeli tank shelling struck tents sheltering displaced civilians in Al-Mawasi, killing at least 21 people, including 12 women, and wounding dozens.[86][87] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) denied conducting the strike in the designated humanitarian area, stating it was "contrary to reports" and that no such action occurred there.[86][88] A larger airstrike occurred on July 13, 2024, when the IDF targeted a Hamas command compound in Al-Mawasi, aiming to eliminate Mohammed Deif, head of Hamas's military wing, and Rafa'a Salama, commander of the Khan Younis Brigade.[89][90] Gaza's health ministry reported 90 deaths and over 300 injuries, primarily civilians in nearby tents.[91] The IDF confirmed both Deif and Salama were killed in the strike, with Deif's elimination verified through intelligence on August 1, but did not provide casualty figures for non-combatants, emphasizing the precision against terrorist infrastructure.[92] On September 10, 2024, an IDF strike hit a tent encampment in Al-Mawasi, where Gaza medics reported 19 deaths and 60 injuries among displaced persons.[93] The military stated the operation targeted two Hamas militants embedded in the area.[94] Subsequent incidents included a November 18, 2024, airstrike on tents in Al-Mawasi that killed four people, two of them children, according to medics.[95] On December 4, 2024, another strike on a tent area reportedly killed 17 and wounded others, with the IDF identifying senior Hamas operatives as the intended targets.[96][97] These events have fueled debate over proportionality and verification, as casualty figures from Gaza's Hamas-administered health system often do not distinguish between civilians and combatants, while IDF assessments prioritize operational necessity against militants using civilian areas for cover. Independent analyses, such as those noting repeated strikes on the zone, highlight the challenges of confirming civilian status amid Hamas's reported tactics of embedding in populated sites.[98]

Allegations of Militant Embedding and Human Shielding

Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) officials have repeatedly alleged that Hamas militants embed command centers, rocket launchers, and personnel within Al-Mawasi, exploiting the area's status as a designated humanitarian zone populated by over 1 million displaced civilians to deter strikes and facilitate operations.[99] In a September 10, 2024, airstrike on Al-Mawasi west of Khan Younis, the IDF targeted what it described as a Hamas command and control center, reporting the elimination of three militants while asserting that Hamas deliberately positions such infrastructure amid civilian tents to use them as human shields.[100] Hamas denied the presence of military targets, labeling the strike a "heinous massacre," though IDF intelligence indicated the site was used for coordinating attacks.[99] Similar allegations surfaced following a July 13, 2024, precision strike in Al-Mawasi targeting Hamas military commander Mohammed Deif and his deputy Rafa Salama, with the IDF claiming Deif's hideout was embedded in the humanitarian zone to shield high-value targets amid dense civilian encampments.[101] The operation, which reportedly killed at least 90 people including Deif according to subsequent IDF confirmation, was justified by intelligence on Deif's use of civilian proximity for protection, a tactic corroborated by prior Hamas operative interrogations admitting the group's reliance on populated areas to complicate Israeli targeting.[102] Independent analyses, such as a May 2025 Henry Jackson Society report, document Hamas's systematic strategies including embedding fighters in civilian zones like Al-Mawasi to maximize collateral damage for propaganda purposes.[103] Further evidence includes IDF reports of 14 rockets launched from Al-Mawasi and adjacent areas on May 23, 2025, originating near civilian tents, prompting retaliatory strikes on the launch sites to neutralize immediate threats while highlighting Hamas's pattern of firing from humanitarian-designated zones.[33] In Rafah, contiguous to Al-Mawasi, IDF operations since May 2024 uncovered extensive tunnel networksâ€”over 150 shafts neutralized by August 2024â€”used by Hamas's Rafah Brigade for smuggling and command, with militants allegedly relocating operations into Al-Mawasi's civilian clusters to evade clearance efforts.[104] These embeddings, per IDF assessments, force operational trade-offs, as militants leverage the zone's 100+ strikes since October 2023, many targeting embedded terror infrastructure like weapons caches and observation posts, to shield assets and inflate civilian casualty narratives.[98] Hamas consistently refutes these claims, attributing strikes to indiscriminate bombing, though captured operatives have confirmed the tactical value of civilian density in prior conflicts.[102]

International Responses and Legal Claims

Following the Israeli airstrike on July 13, 2024, in the Al-Mawasi area west of Khan Younis, which Gaza health authorities reported killed at least 90 civilians sheltering in tents designated as a humanitarian zone, United Nations Secretary-General AntÃ³nio Guterres expressed being "shocked and saddened," emphasizing that the incident underscored "nowhere is safe in Gaza."[105][106] The UN Human Rights Office later described repeated strikes on Al-Mawasi tents as targeting displaced families in an area Israel itself had designated safe, with OHCHR reporting continued such incidents into June 2025, killing entire families and highlighting the nominal nature of humanitarian zones.[46][107] Amnesty International investigated Israeli strikes in Rafah, including areas overlapping with Al-Mawasi's humanitarian designation, concluding in August 2024 that forces failed to take feasible precautions to spare civilians despite targeting Hamas fighters, and urged probes into potential war crimes; the organization attributed scores of civilian deaths to these operations.[108] Responses from governments varied: while some Middle Eastern states condemned the strikes as a "brutal massacre," Western allies like the United States defended Israel's right to target militants embedded among civilians, noting the strike aimed at a senior Hamas operative amid evidence of human shielding.[109] Israel's military, in turn, acknowledged the July 13 strike but maintained it was intelligence-driven against Hamas infrastructure, with no prior awareness of civilian tents in the impact zone.[110] On the legal front, the International Court of Justice (ICJ) issued provisional measures on May 24, 2024, in the South Africa v. Israel genocide case, ordering Israel to "immediately halt its military offensive" in Rafah and ensure unhindered humanitarian aid access, directly implicating operations affecting Al-Mawasi as part of the broader Rafah governorate where over 1 million displaced persons were concentrated.[111] Israel contested the order's scope, arguing it did not preclude targeted operations against Hamas threats in designated zones and continued partial evacuations of Al-Mawasi in July 2024 to facilitate such actions against regrouped fighters.[112] The International Criminal Court (ICC) Prosecutor, while pursuing arrest warrants for Israeli leaders on broader Gaza charges including starvation tactics, has not issued specific indictments tied to Al-Mawasi strikes, though advocacy groups like Al-Haq cited them in April 2025 calls for ICC action amid ongoing territorial operations.[113][114] Compliance debates persist, with critics alleging violations of international humanitarian law principles on distinction and proportionality, while Israel maintains adherence through warnings and precision targeting, amid acknowledged challenges from Hamas's use of civilian areas.[115]
