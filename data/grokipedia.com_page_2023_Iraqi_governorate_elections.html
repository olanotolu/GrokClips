2023 Iraqi governorate elections

The 2023 Iraqi governorate elections were local polls conducted on 18 December 2023 in fifteen of Iraq's eighteen governorates, excluding the semi-autonomous Kurdistan Region, to select members of provincial councils responsible for legislative oversight, budget allocation, and public service delivery at the subnational level.[1][2] These elections, the first since 2013, followed years of delays triggered by widespread protests against corruption and elite capture in late 2019, which prompted the dissolution of councils and demands for electoral reform.[3] Voter turnout reached a historic low of approximately 41 percent, reflecting public disillusionment with Iraq's political class amid persistent economic stagnation, sectarian patronage networks, and militia influence.[4][5] The Iran-aligned Shiite Coordination Framework emerged as the leading bloc, securing 101 of 285 total seats across the councils, bolstered by the absence of competition from Muqtada al-Sadr's movement, which had boycotted national polls in 2021 and did not field candidates.[6][7] Other notable performers included Nouri al-Maliki's State of Law coalition and Ammar al-Hakim's Wisdom Movement, while reformist and independent lists inspired by the 2019 protests gained limited traction, underscoring the resilience of established power structures.[8][3] The outcomes reinforced the dominance of Shiite political factions tied to Iran-backed militias, raising concerns over entrenched corruption, family-based dynasties in governance, and potential escalation of ethnic tensions in disputed areas like Kirkuk, where Arab-Kurdish rivalries complicated seat allocation and leadership selection.[9][10] These provincial results are poised to influence the formation of governor positions through council votes and foreshadow dynamics in the upcoming 2025 parliamentary elections, potentially consolidating centralized control while sidelining demands for decentralized authority and anti-corruption measures.[2][11]

Background

Historical Context of Provincial Elections

Provincial elections in Iraq commenced on January 30, 2005, alongside the vote for the transitional National Assembly, establishing the framework for subnational governance after the 2003 U.S.-led invasion and the adoption of a federal constitution emphasizing decentralization. These polls created councils in Iraq's 18 governorates (excluding Kurdistan Region institutions), tasked with selecting governors, allocating local budgets, and managing services like infrastructure and security, though central government oversight often limited their autonomy. Turnout reached about 58%, with over 8 million voters participating despite insurgent violence; Shiite Islamist coalitions, including the United Iraqi Alliance, dominated in Baghdad, southern, and central provinces, while Sunni areas saw lower participation due to boycotts by some groups wary of the process.[12][13] The January 31, 2009, elections renewed councils in 14 governorates, excluding the three Kurdish provinces and Kirkuk due to disputes, with turnout near 50% reflecting improved security post-2007 surge but persistent sectarian divides. Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki's State of Law Coalition surged ahead of the incumbent Shiite alliance, capturing majorities or pluralities in key areas like Baghdad and Basra, signaling a fragmentation of Shiite voting blocs toward personality-driven politics and highlighting Sunni Arab reintegration after 2005 abstentions. These outcomes influenced national dynamics, as provincial gains bolstered al-Maliki's position ahead of 2010 parliamentary polls, though corruption allegations and service failures began eroding public trust in local bodies.[14][15] Elections on April 20, 2013, in 12 governorates saw al-Maliki's bloc retain leads in seven provinces, securing around 30% fewer seats overall from 2009 but maintaining influence amid rising Sunni discontent and al-Qaeda resurgence; polls in Anbar and Ninewa deferred to July over violence. With turnout at 40-45%, results underscored entrenched sectarian patronage, as Shiite parties controlled resource-rich south while Sunnis fragmented, exacerbating grievances that fueled 2014 ISIS advances. Post-2013, provincial terms extended repeatedlyâ€”intended renewal in 2017 postponed amid instability, ISIS war, and 2019 protests decrying corruption and militia interferenceâ€”leading to council dissolution in October 2019 and further delays tied to federal electoral reforms and pandemic restrictions, culminating in the 2023 vote after a decade-long gap that centralized de facto power in Baghdad.[16][17][18]

Delays and Precipitating Events

The provincial councils in Iraq's governorates were dissolved on October 16, 2019, in preparation for elections originally scheduled for April 20, 2020, marking the first such vote since 2013.[19] However, the Tishreen protest movement, which erupted on October 1, 2019, amid widespread public discontent over corruption, unemployment, and Iranian influence, created significant instability that halted electoral preparations. The protests, met with violent suppression by security forces and Iran-backed militias resulting in over 600 protester deaths, led to the resignation of Prime Minister Adil Abdul-Mahdi on November 19, 2019, and prolonged governance vacuums that made conducting fair provincial elections untenable.[20] Subsequent postponements were exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 and, more critically, the political deadlock following the October 2021 parliamentary elections, where Muqtada al-Sadr's bloc secured a plurality but failed to form a government amid rivalries with the Coordination Framework. This crisis, characterized by repeated failures to elect a president and prime minister, violent clashes in Baghdad on August 29, 2022, between Sadrist supporters and security forces, and parliamentary gridlock, diverted political focus from local elections and deepened national divisions.[21] The stalemate persisted until October 13, 2022, when a consensus candidate for president was selected, enabling Mohammed Shia' al-Sudani's appointment as prime minister on October 27, 2022, under a power-sharing arrangement.[22] The resolution of the national crisis precipitated the scheduling of provincial elections, as the new government prioritized stabilizing institutions and addressing demands for local renewal after a decade without such votes. Al-Sudani's administration passed enabling legislation, including amendments to the electoral law in March 2023 and the federal budget in June 2023, which facilitated the Independent High Electoral Commission's preparations. Elections were ultimately set for December 18, 2023, in 15 governorates (excluding Kurdistan Region and Kirkuk due to separate disputes), driven by the need to devolve power and mitigate ongoing public disillusionment from prior delays.[23][24]

Electoral System

Voting Mechanism and Seat Allocation

The 2023 Iraqi governorate elections employed an open-list proportional representation system, with each of the 15 participating governorates serving as a single multi-member electoral district. Voters cast ballots by selecting individual candidates from registered party or coalition lists, allowing preferences to influence the order of elected representatives within each list rather than voting solely for the party.[25][3] Seats within each governorate were allocated proportionally based on the total votes received by lists, using the modified Sainte-LaguÃ« method with an initial divisor of 1.7. This approach calculates quotients by dividing each list's vote total by the sequence of divisors (1.7, 3, 5, 7, and so on), assigning seats to the highest quotients until all seats are filled; the 1.7 divisor adjustment disadvantages smaller lists relative to larger ones, favoring established political blocs.[25][26][3] The number of seats per governorate varied according to population and prior allocations, totaling 285 seats across the districts, excluding the three Kurdish-majority governorates where elections were not held under the federal system.[27] Within lists securing seats, candidates were ranked by the number of individual votes received, ensuring elected members reflected voter preferences over party-imposed orders.[25] This mechanism, reinstated from earlier laws, aimed to balance representation but was criticized for entrenching dominant alliances by limiting small-party gains.[26][3]

Quotas for Minorities and Women

The electoral system for Iraq's 2023 governorate elections, governed by amendments to Law No. 21 of 2008 on Governorate Councils and related electoral regulations, required provincial councils to include no fewer than 25% women members to promote gender representation. This was enforced through candidate list composition rules mandating parties to place women in at least every third position, with post-election adjustments to allocate seats accordingly if lists fell short. In practice, women won 76 of 285 total seats across the 15 federal provinces (excluding Kurdistan Region), exceeding the minimum by a small margin and reflecting a mix of quota enforcement and organic electoral gains.[28] Notably, 17 of these seats were secured by female candidates independently of quota provisions, based on vote tallies from general competition.[28] Ethnic and religious minorities benefited from 10 reserved "component" seats, contested exclusively by candidates from designated groups and allocated to provinces with concentrated populations, such as Nineveh, Baghdad, and Kirkuk.[29] These seats targeted communities including Christians (typically 5 seats distributed across relevant governorates), Shabaks (2 seats, primarily in Nineveh), Yazidis (1 seat in Nineveh), and Sabeans-Mandaeans (1 seat), with the remainder for other recognized minorities like Turkmen in specific areas.[30] A total of 48 minority candidates competed for these positions, separate from the open-list proportional representation used for general seats.[29] The system aimed to counterbalance majority dominance, though critics argued it sometimes favored entrenched party affiliates over broader community interests; minority voters delivered over 42,000 ballots for quota candidates, yielding all 10 reserved seats plus extra wins in non-quota races.[31][30]

Political Landscape

Major Alliances and Parties

The Coordination Framework, an alliance of Iran-aligned Shiite political parties controlling the federal government, emerged as the dominant force in the 2023 Iraqi governorate elections.[6] This coalition, formed after the 2021 parliamentary elections to counter the Sadrist boycott, coordinated campaigns across multiple lists despite internal divisions.[32] Its constituent parties, including the State of Law Coalition led by former Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki and the Wisdom Movement headed by Ammar al-Hakim, secured the largest collective share of seats in Shiite-majority provinces such as Baghdad, Basra, and Najaf.[7] The Framework's success, totaling approximately 101 seats out of 285, reflected strong organizational capacity and voter loyalty in southern Iraq, bolstered by control over patronage networks and militia influence.[6] On the Sunni side, the Taqaddum (Progress) alliance, led by Parliament Speaker Mohammed al-Halbousi, performed strongly in predominantly Sunni governorates like Anbar and Nineveh.[2] Taqaddum capitalized on local grievances over reconstruction and security, outperforming rival Sunni groups such as the Azm Alliance backed by Khamis al-Khanjar. This intra-Sunni competition highlighted fractures within the community, with Taqaddum gaining leverage for provincial leadership positions.[33] Kurdish parties, primarily the Kurdistan Democratic Party (KDP) and Patriotic Union of Kurdistan (PUK), focused efforts on ethnically mixed areas like Kirkuk and Nineveh rather than Kurdistan Region provinces, which held separate regional elections. In Kirkuk, both parties vied for influence amid Arab and Turkmen competition, underscoring ongoing territorial disputes.[2] Notably absent were candidates from the Sadrist Movement, led by Muqtada al-Sadr, which had boycotted national politics since withdrawing its lawmakers in 2021 to protest perceived corruption and Iranian interference. This absence diminished populist Shiite opposition, allowing the Coordination Framework to consolidate power without significant challenge from reformist forces. Independent and civil society lists, emerging from the 2019 protests, failed to translate protest momentum into electoral gains, receiving minimal seats.[3]

Influence of Militias and External Actors

The Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF), a constellation of predominantly Shia militias formalized in 2016 and backed by Iran, exerted significant influence over the 2023 provincial elections through affiliated political entities within the Coordination Framework alliance.[34] PMF-linked groups, including those designated as terrorist organizations by the United States such as Kata'ib Sayyid al-Shuhada, rebranded or operated under political party guises to field candidates, circumventing Iraqi Law No. 36 of 2015 which prohibits parties with armed wings from participating.[35] [36] These factions leveraged their control over local security, economic resources, and patronage networks in Shia-majority provinces to mobilize voters and deter opposition, contributing to the Framework's capture of 101 seats out of 285 across 15 governorates on December 18, 2023.[6] [2] Iran played a pivotal role as the primary external patron of these militias, providing financial, military, and advisory support to embed its proxies deeper into Iraq's governance structure.[34] Tehran's influence manifested in coordinated efforts to bolster the Coordination Framework following Muqtada al-Sadr's 2021 parliamentary withdrawal and his subsequent boycott of the provincial polls, which fragmented anti-Iran Shia opposition and enabled pro-Tehran groups to consolidate power in key areas like Baghdad, Basra, and Najaf.[11] [2] This outcome amplified Iran's leverage over provincial councils, which oversee budgets exceeding $10 billion annually and service delivery, effectively advancing Tehran's goal of transforming Iraq into a compliant satellite state amid regional tensions.[34] In counterpoint, the United States applied diplomatic and sanctions pressure to mitigate militia dominance, designating additional PMF subunits and urging Iraqi authorities to enforce demobilization and electoral integrity.[36] Washington viewed unchecked PMF political gains as a threat to Iraq's sovereignty and U.S. interests, particularly given militia attacks on American forces post-October 2023, yet these efforts yielded limited success in altering the pro-Iran tilt of results, highlighting the asymmetry between Iran's on-ground embeddedness and U.S. remote influence.[37] Other external actors, including Sunni Arab states via the Arab League, exerted negligible direct sway, as their focus remained on broader stabilization rather than electoral micromanagement.[2]

Pre-Election Developments

Campaign Issues and Strategies

The primary campaign issues in the 2023 Iraqi provincial elections centered on deficiencies in public service delivery, including inadequate water, electricity, and infrastructure, which candidates across blocs attributed to mismanagement by outgoing councils.[2][3] Economic challenges, particularly high unemployment and reliance on oil revenues without broader development, featured prominently, with incumbents highlighting stability under their governance while reformers decried corruption as the root cause of stalled progress.[2][3] In diverse provinces like Nineveh and Diyala, sectarian and tribal tensions surfaced, with intra-communal divisions influencing debates over resource allocation and security.[33] Established parties within the Coordination Framework, including Shia blocs like the State of Law coalition, pursued strategies rooted in patronage and power-sharing appeals, mobilizing voters through promises of jobs, goods, and localized infrastructure projects funded via provincial budgets.[2][9] These groups leveraged family tiesâ€”evident in nominations of relatives of 50 prominent politiciansâ€”and tribal networks to consolidate support, often resorting to vote-buying, where electoral cards fetched 25,000â€“150,000 Iraqi dinars in areas like Qayyarah, and coercion via Popular Mobilization Forces affiliates or local brokers.[9][25] Such tactics exploited the 2023 electoral law's favoritism toward large coalitions, enabling heavy spending on populist incentives and fear-based loyalty from clientelist bases.[3] Emerging civic and "change" parties, inspired by the 2019 protests, formed alliances such as the Civil Values Alliance to advocate non-sectarian reforms, emphasizing anti-corruption measures and equitable service provision to appeal to youth and disillusioned urban voters.[3] Their mobilization relied on volunteer-driven efforts, social media outreach, and professional or tribal local networks, but was hampered by limited funding, internal fragmentation, and the absence of major players like the Sadrist movement, which had boycotted following its 2021 parliamentary withdrawal.[3][11] This contrast underscored how resource asymmetries and voter apathyâ€”reflected in the 41% official turnoutâ€”favored incumbents' entrenched machines over reformist narratives.[3][25]

Security Threats and Violence

The 2023 Iraqi provincial elections faced persistent security threats from armed militias, insurgent remnants, and inter-communal tensions, though major insurgent attacks were absent on voting day, December 18. Concerns over violence contributed to voter apathy, with some citizens citing fears of reprisals from powerful factions as a deterrent to participation.[38][39] The Independent High Electoral Commission (IHEC) and security forces implemented extensive measures, including deploying over 1 million personnel to polling stations, yet intimidation persisted in militia-influenced areas.[40] During the campaign period, armed groups affiliated with the Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF) engaged in voter coercion, particularly in Nineveh province's remote districts such as Qayyarah, where PMF members and families were pressured to support aligned candidates using illegal weapons.[25] Political rivalries exacerbated Sunni-Shia tensions, raising risks of armed clashes between provinces, though these did not materialize into widespread violence pre-election.[25] Electoral law changes favoring established blocs further heightened safety concerns in regions with unchecked armed actors, enabling exploitation of state resources for intimidation.[25] On election day, the process unfolded largely peacefully with only scattered incidents reported, averting the large-scale disruptions feared from groups like ISIS remnants or Iran-backed militias.[6][40] Intimidation by security elements persisted, including harassment of female voters and girls in Nineveh sites like Zummar, Sinjar, and Ba'aj, alongside arrests of Kurdistan Democratic Party representatives by the Sinjar Resistance Units (YBS) in Sinjar, which escalated local ethnic frictions.[25] The Supreme Security Committee had concluded preparations by early December, focusing on curbing militia overreach, but enforcement gaps allowed such localized threats.[41]

Election Administration

Voter Turnout Statistics

The Independent High Electoral Commission (IHEC) of Iraq reported an overall voter turnout of 41% for the provincial elections conducted on December 18, 2023, across 15 governorates.[4] [40] This marked the first such elections in a decade, with roughly 6.6 million ballots cast from an eligible voter base exceeding 16 million, leaving over 9 million registered individuals abstaining.[42] The figure represented a slight increase from the 2021 federal parliamentary elections but remained the lowest recorded in Iraqi elections since 2005, signaling persistent voter apathy amid perceptions of entrenched corruption and inefficacy in governance.[43] [5] Pre-election surveys had forecasted subdued participation due to widespread distrust in political institutions, a trend validated by the outcome.[44] Detailed turnout variations by governorate were not publicly disaggregated in IHEC's preliminary or final announcements, though anecdotal reports highlighted disparities, such as relatively higher engagement in Kurdish-majority areas like Sulaymaniyah compared to Sunni-dominated provinces.[43] Special voting for detainees, military personnel, and expatriates on December 16 preceded general polling, contributing marginally to the aggregate without altering the overall low rate.[45]

Reported Irregularities on Election Day

Technical difficulties with biometric fingerprint scanners were among the most frequently reported issues at polling stations, causing delays in voter verification and contributing to extended voting hours in some locations, with polls closing after 11 hours of operation.[46] The Independent High Electoral Commission (IHEC) managed these glitches without widespread disruptions, though they affected turnout logistics in provinces like Kirkuk.[46] International and domestic observers, including those referenced in official assessments, described the voting process as generally well-conducted across the 15 participating governorates, with no large-scale violence or systemic failures recorded.[47] However, isolated irregularities were noted, including allegations of vote buying through cash incentives or material goods offered to voters near polling sites, particularly in areas with strong militia influence.[47] Voter intimidation tactics, such as presence of armed groups discouraging participation or pressuring choices, surfaced in reports from southern and central provinces, though these were not deemed sufficient to invalidate overall results.[47] The absence of UN monitoring, as the Iraqi government did not request it, limited external oversight, potentially underreporting minor infractions.[48] Post-polling complaints filed with IHEC focused more on these procedural lapses than on ballot tampering, with preliminary reviews indicating they impacted fewer than 5% of stations.[49] Overall voter turnout of approximately 41% reflected apathy and boycotts rather than acute day-of disruptions.[40]

Results

National Aggregate Outcomes

The 2023 Iraqi governorate elections, conducted on December 18, resulted in the allocation of 285 seats across provincial councils in 15 governorates, excluding the Kurdistan Region's three provinces which held separate polls later. The Coordination Framework, a coalition of Shia Islamist parties with ties to Iran-backed militias, dominated the outcomes by securing 101 seats in aggregate through its competing lists, reflecting its consolidation of influence in Shia-majority areas amid the boycott by Muqtada al-Sadr's populist movement.[6][2] Breakdown within the Framework included 43 seats for the Nabni Alliance led by Hadi al-Ameri (associated with the Badr Organization), 35 seats for Nouri al-Maliki's State of Law Coalition, and 23 seats for the State Forces Alliance of Ammar al-Hakim and Haider al-Abadi.[6][50] Among Sunni alliances, Mohammed al-Halbousi's Taqadum secured 22 seats, primarily in Anbar, Baghdad, Diyala, and Salah al-Din, while Khamis al-Khanjar's Siyada list won 14 and Muthanna al-Ethari's Azm obtained 9, highlighting fragmentation despite collective Sunni gains of around 45 seats.[6][2] Kurdish parties, contesting mainly in disputed territories like Kirkuk and Nineveh, captured limited seats: the Kurdistan Democratic Party (KDP) with 6 and the Patriotic Union of Kurdistan (PUK) with 5.[51] The remaining seats, approximately 130, were distributed among independent candidates, smaller parties, and quotas for minorities such as Christians, Yazidis, and Shabaks, underscoring persistent ethnic and sectarian divisions in voter preferences.[2] These results, certified by the Independent High Electoral Commission on December 28, positioned the Framework to influence governor selections and local governance, though Sunni and Kurdish disputes in mixed provinces like Kirkuk complicated power-sharing.[4][7]

Specific Governorate Results and Disputes

In Kirkuk Governorate, the Turkmen Front secured the largest bloc with six seats in the 13-member provincial council, followed by the Patriotic Union of Kurdistan (PUK) with four seats and Arab alliances with three, reflecting ethnic divisions in the disputed territory.[52] This outcome thwarted Kurdish hopes for control, leading to prolonged disputes over the council presidency and governorship, with Kurdish parties boycotting sessions and federal courts intervening to mediate power-sharing amid claims of demographic manipulation favoring Turkmen voters.[53] [10] In Diyala Governorate, initial results gave the Coordination Framework alliance a plurality, but the Iraqi Federal Court ruled on January 8, 2024, to reallocate two seats following a challenge by the Azm Alliance, shifting one seat from the Framework to Azm and another adjustment, highlighting irregularities in vote tallying and candidate eligibility.[54] This adjustment intensified sectarian tensions in the mixed Sunni-Shiite province, where militias influence local politics, though no governor was immediately named due to coalition negotiations.[2] Nineveh Governorate saw fragmented Sunni results, with the Taif Alliance (linked to former Governor Najim al-Jubouri) winning nine of 29 seats, followed by the Unified Nineveh List with seven, underscoring intra-Sunni rivalries post-ISIS.[33] Disputes arose over minority quotas for Yazidis and Christians, with allegations of vote-buying in Mosul, but the council elected a governor aligned with pro-Iran factions by early 2024, consolidating militia influence despite reformist challenges.[55] In southern Shiite-majority governorates like Basra and Muthanna, the Coordination Framework dominated, securing majorities that enabled quick governor selectionsâ€”such as Asaad al-Eidani's re-election in Basraâ€”without significant disputes, reinforcing Iran-aligned control over oil-rich areas.[7] [56] Baghdad's council results favored State of Law (part of the Framework) with 20 seats, but cross-alliance bargaining delayed governance formation amid fraud claims from opposition lists.[6] Overall, while final tallies by the Independent High Electoral Commission on December 28, 2023, confirmed Framework gains in 12 of 15 councils, ethnic flashpoints like Kirkuk prolonged instability, with turnout at 41% reflecting voter apathy toward entrenched powers.[4]

Post-Election Aftermath

Legal Challenges and Court Rulings

Following the December 18, 2023, provincial elections, numerous candidates and political entities filed legal challenges with the Independent High Electoral Commission (IHEC) and the Federal Court of Cassation, alleging procedural irregularities, improper application of the Sainte-LaguÃ« seat allocation method, and violations of voter registration rules in several governorates. These complaints primarily targeted vote tabulation discrepancies and claims of undue influence by incumbent parties, though comprehensive data on the total number of appeals remains limited, with most resolved at the administrative level without altering overall outcomes.[57] A prominent case arose in Diyala province, where on January 8, 2024, the Iraqi Electoral Court ruled in favor of a challenger's petition, mandating the reallocation of two seats in the 18-member provincial council. The decision stemmed from arguments that the IHEC's initial distribution contravened electoral proportionality rules, resulting in seats shifting from lists aligned with certain Shia factions to competitors, though the ruling did not invalidate the broader results or trigger recounts. This adjustment highlighted tensions in mixed-sectarian areas but was isolated, as similar claims in adjacent provinces like Wasit yielded no comparable judicial interventions.[54] In disputed territories such as Kirkuk and Nineveh, legal filings focused on ethnic minority quotas, internally displaced voter participation, and historical land claims, exacerbating delays in council formation. However, these disputes largely bypassed decisive court rulings, with resolutions achieved via inter-party pacts by February 2024 rather than judicial mandates, underscoring the Federal Supreme Court's restraint in overturning IHEC certifications absent clear constitutional breaches. The absence of widespread nullifications preserved the elections' legitimacy, despite persistent accusations of bias in judicial processes favoring established Coordination Framework alliances.[53][58]

Formation of Provincial Councils

Following the December 18, 2023, elections, Iraq's provincial councilsâ€”totaling 285 seats across 15 governoratesâ€”were mandated under the Provincial Powers Law No. 21 of 2008 to convene inaugural sessions within approximately one month to elect a council president, deputies, and then a governor by absolute majority vote, with the governor's nomination subject to central government approval if contested.[2] In practice, sessions began in late January and early February 2024, driven by post-election alliances, particularly the Shiite Coordination Framework (CF), which secured 101 seats nationally and formed majorities in several southern governorates through coalitions like State of Law and Wisdom Movement lists.[6] By February 5, 2024, seven councils had completed leadership elections: in Anbar, Mohammed Nouri al-Karbouli (Taqaddum Alliance) was elected governor and Omar Mishaan Dabous al-Dulaimi council president; in Basra, Asaad al-Eidani was re-elected governor with Khalaf al-Badran as president (We Build Alliance); in Najaf, Youssef Knawi (Wisdom Movement) became governor and Hussein al-Issawi (State of Law) president; in Karbala, Naseef Khitabi was governor and Qasim Yasari president; in Nineveh, Abdul Qadir al-Dakhil was re-elected governor with Ahmed al-Hasoud president; in Salah al-Din, Ahmad al-Jubouri governor and Adel al-Smeidi president; and in Wasit, Mohammed Jamil al-Miyali governor with Ali Hussein Soleimoun president.[59] [60] These outcomes reflected CF influence in Shiite areas and Sunni alliances in western provinces, often renewing incumbents amid negotiated power-sharing to avoid gridlock.[2] Subsequent sessions addressed remaining councils: Baghdad, Babil, and Muthanna convened by early May 2024, electing leaders aligned with CF coalitions, while Maysan followed suit shortly after February.[60] However, formations stalled in ethnically mixed "disputed territories" due to disputes over seat quotas, presidencies, and quorum requirements. In Diyala, intra-Shiite rivalries and Sunni-Kurdish tensions prevented sessions into mid-2024. Kirkuk's 13-member council, split among Arabs (7 seats, CF-led), Kurds (6, KDP/PUK), and Turkmen (minimal), delayed its first session until July 11, 2024, following Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani's intervention, but subsequent quorum failures extended paralysis into late 2024, exacerbating governance vacuums.[61] [62] These delays highlighted sectarian and ethnic bargaining, with CF pushing for Arab majorities despite judicial challenges to vote distributions.[2] Overall, by mid-2024, most councils were operational, enabling budget oversight and service delivery, though CF dominanceâ€”evident in 10 of 15 governorshipsâ€”reinforced centralized Shiite influence amid low election turnout (41%), raising questions about representativeness in non-competitive provinces.[40] Persistent disputes in Kirkuk and Diyala underscored vulnerabilities to federal intervention, as councils lacked unified majorities for executive stability.[61]

Controversies

Allegations of Fraud and Manipulation

Allegations of systematic electoral fraud surfaced from critics of Iraq's Independent High Electoral Commission (IHEC), with researcher Yahya al-Kubaisi of the Iraqi Center for Strategic Studies claiming the commission's compositionâ€”largely comprising party-nominated membersâ€”enabled institutional manipulation through a flawed election law that facilitated fraud rather than isolated incidents.[5] Technical glitches in electronic vote-counting machines affected less than 3% of polling stations nationwide, but no widespread fraud was directly observed during the December 18, 2023, voting, according to analyst Firas Elias.[5] Vote-buying emerged as a prominent accusation, particularly targeting female candidates who allegedly distributed cash to exploit voter poverty and limited political awareness, as reported by Hiyam Ali of the Bayan Center for Planning and Studies.[5] In minority Christian seats across Baghdad, Nineveh, Erbil, and Dohuk provinces, the Babylon Movement, led by Rayan al-Kildani, secured all four reserved positions through an aggressive vote-buying operation, prompting calls for federal investigations that went unheeded by authorities.[63] Pre-election concerns included voter card trading and intimidation, with Iraqi security forces arresting individuals for illegal card sales in Dhi Qar province and implementing measures against blackmail in November 2023.[64] [65] An NGO submission to the UN Human Rights Council described the elections as marred by violations, including forced voting in certain areas, though specific locations and evidence were not detailed.[66] Despite these claims, political blocs lodged minimal formal objections, contrasting with prior cycles, and the overall process saw fewer disputes, attributed partly to biometric safeguards and surveillance cameras at polling stations introduced to deter rigging.[5] [67] Low turnout of 26.7%â€”the lowest since 2005â€”exacerbated skepticism, with over one-third of eligible voters failing to receive biometric cards, potentially enabling suppression tactics.[5]

Ethnic and Sectarian Tensions

In multi-ethnic governorates like Kirkuk and Nineveh, the 2023 provincial elections amplified longstanding disputes over resource allocation, territorial control, and political representation, rooted in Iraq's ethno-sectarian power-sharing framework established post-2003. Kirkuk, classified as a disputed territory between the Iraqi central government and the Kurdistan Regional Government, saw intense rivalry among Kurdish parties (primarily the Kurdistan Democratic Party and Patriotic Union of Kurdistan), Arab Shiite and Sunni factions, and Turkmen groups, with each bloc vying for dominance in the provincial council.[52] The elections, held on December 18, 2023, resulted in a fragmented council where no single ethnic group secured a majority, leading to prolonged negotiations and accusations of demographic manipulation through voter registration irregularities favoring Arab returnees resettled under previous Shiite-led governments.[10] Post-election governorship selection in Kirkuk further exposed fissures, as an Arab-majority coalition elected Ahmed al-Azzawi, a Sunni Arab aligned with Shiite coordination framework parties, on August 12, 2024, prompting Kurdish boycotts and Turkmen protests; this outcome defied informal ethnic quotas and reignited claims of Arabization policies displacing Kurds since the 2017 territorial losses by the Kurdistan Regional Government.[68] In Nineveh, a Sunni Arab-majority province with Kurdish, Yazidi, and Christian minorities scarred by ISIS occupation, council formations reflected proxy alignments: pro-Iranian Sunni Islamists (e.g., from the Azm Alliance) clashed with Turkish-backed factions (e.g., Nineveh's Guards), marginalizing minority seats and fueling local grievances over security appointments in ISIS-vulnerable areas.[55] These dynamics underscored how elections reinforced sectarian silosâ€”Shiite dominance in southern provinces, Sunni consolidation in western ones, and Kurdish control in northern enclavesâ€”while heightening risks of localized violence in borderlands, though federal court interventions mitigated immediate escalations.[25] Broader sectarian undercurrents persisted, with Shiite coordination framework victories in majority-Shiite areas raising Sunni fears of marginalization, as evidenced by fragmented Sunni turnout and alliances; empirical voting patterns showed 70-80% ethno-sectarian bloc loyalty despite public opposition to identity politics, perpetuating a consociational system prone to paralysis in mixed settings.[69] No widespread sectarian violence erupted during the polls, contrasting with pre-2014 eras, but unresolved disputes in Kirkuk and Nineveh threatened stability, including oil revenue shares critical to Iraq's economy (Kirkuk fields produce ~400,000 barrels daily).[11]

Iranian Interference Claims

Allegations of Iranian interference in the 2023 Iraqi governorate elections focused on indirect influence through Tehran-backed Shiite political entities, particularly the Coordination Framework (CF) alliance, which encompasses parties like Nouri al-Maliki's State of Law coalition and militias within the Popular Mobilization Forces (PMF). Critics, including Sunni leaders from the Taqadum bloc and Kurdish representatives, claimed that Iranian support enabled CF dominance by funding campaigns and deploying militia intimidation to suppress opposition votes in Shiite-majority provinces such as Baghdad, Basra, and Najaf.[2][11] These assertions pointed to PMF-affiliated groups leveraging their armed presence to deter voter participation from non-aligned Shiite factions, contributing to the elections' record-low turnout of approximately 44 percent, the lowest since 2005.[5] Specific claims highlighted Iranian financial backing, estimated in the millions of dollars channeled through proxy networks to CF candidates, allowing them to outspend rivals and secure 101 of 285 provincial council seats overall, including majorities in seven southern provinces.[2][6] In contested areas like Nineveh, opponents alleged manipulation of voter registries and threats against Sunni candidates by Iran-linked militias, exacerbating sectarian divides and enabling CF gains despite the absence of Muqtada al-Sadr's movement, which had boycotted the polls.[55] Such tactics were said to reflect Tehran's strategic interest in consolidating proxy control over local governance to counterbalance U.S. influence and secure oil revenue streams.[11] Iraqi electoral officials and CF representatives rejected these accusations, attributing successes to genuine popular support amid Sadrists' withdrawal and portraying complaints as sour grapes from losing factions.[56] Independent observers noted that while militia presence posed risks of coercionâ€”evidenced by pre-election arrests of PMF-linked figures for threatsâ€”no forensic audits confirmed widespread ballot tampering directly tied to Iran.[2] The claims underscored broader concerns over Iran's structural sway via economic leverage and armed proxies, though empirical verification remained limited to anecdotal reports and post-election disputes rather than irrefutable causal links.[34]

Implications

Short-Term Governance Effects

The reconstitution of provincial councils after the December 18, 2023 elections initially promised renewed local oversight of services and budgets, but short-term outcomes were marked by extensive delays in leadership elections and power-sharing agreements, exacerbating administrative inertia across multiple governorates.[2] As of March 2024, negotiations among dominant blocs like the Coordination Framework prolonged governor selections, with partisan disputes stalling council functionality and limiting early budgetary allocations for infrastructure and public works.[2] In provinces such as Dhi Qar, the failure to appoint a successor to Governor Murtadha al-Ibrahimi in early 2024 led to outright paralysis in service delivery, including delays in water and electricity projects.[57] Ethnic and sectarian tensions further undermined governance stability, as seen in Kirkuk where a power-sharing arrangement collapsed in May 2024 following the withdrawal of Arab members, halting council decisions on resource distribution.[57] Similar issues plagued Diyala and Saladin, where councils disregarded Federal Court rulings on leadership reinstatements throughout 2024, resulting in repeated session disruptions, physical altercations, and failure to form operational committees.[58][57] Baghdad experienced legal battles over the dismissal of Governor Abdul-Muttalib al-Alawi and council chairman Ammar al-Qaisi in early 2025, which courts partially reversed but did little to restore cohesive administration.[57] These dynamics yielded minimal policy shifts, with incumbent coalitions retaining control and prioritizing internal quotas over reform, leading to persistent corruption allegationsâ€”such as fund misuse in Najaf by April 2025â€”and no measurable gains in public services like sanitation or employment programs.[58] Low council attendance and decision-making gridlock deepened public disillusionment, as councils consumed resources without addressing immediate needs, effectively perpetuating pre-election inefficiencies in local governance.[57][58]

Broader Political Ramifications

The 2023 Iraqi provincial elections strengthened the position of the Shiite Coordination Framework (SCF), an alliance of Iran-aligned parties, which won a majority of seats in the 15 participating governorates and secured governorships in key Shiite-dominated provinces such as Baghdad, Basra, and Najaf.[2] This outcome enhanced the SCF's leverage in national power-sharing negotiations, as provincial councils play a critical role in appointing governors who influence federal resource allocation and policy implementation.[2] The elections thus reinforced the dominance of established political blocs, limiting opportunities for independent or reformist factions emerging from the 2019 protests to challenge entrenched interests.[3] The boycott by Muqtada al-Sadr's movement, which had previously held significant sway, led to its marginalization at the local level, potentially stabilizing short-term governance by reducing populist disruptions but at the cost of alienating a portion of the Shiite electorate.[11] Voter turnout reached a historic low of approximately 44%, reflecting widespread disillusionment with the political process and signaling risks to the legitimacy of resulting councils.[5] In Sunni-majority areas like Nineveh and Anbar, fragmented victories among tribal and party lists underscored persistent intra-sectarian rivalries, complicating efforts toward cohesive national opposition to Shiite dominance.[33] Nationally, the SCF's gains heightened Iranian influence over Iraqi institutions, prompting concerns among U.S. policymakers about militia entrenchment and alignment with Tehran's regional agenda, which could intensify proxy conflicts and strain Iraq's relations with Gulf states.[11] The results also perpetuated nepotistic practices, with family ties increasingly defining political succession and resource control, thereby undermining anti-corruption reforms and democratic accountability.[9] Looking ahead, these dynamics are poised to shape the November 2025 parliamentary elections, where traditional alliances may consolidate further under modified electoral rules favoring larger blocs.[61]
