Boeing South Carolina

Boeing South Carolina is the North Charleston, South Carolina-based manufacturing campus of The Boeing Company, specializing in the final assembly, fuselage fabrication, and delivery of the 787 Dreamliner wide-body jet airliner variants, including the 787-8, 787-9, and 787-10 models.[1][2] Established through groundbreaking on November 20, 2009, and operational with final assembly commencing in 2011, the facility represents the only Boeing location worldwide executing the full production cycle of the 787â€”from component fabrication to flight testing and customer handoverâ€”leveraging extensive use of composite materials for lighter, more fuel-efficient aircraft.[3][4] Spanning two campuses on approximately 240 acres near Charleston International Airport, it employs over 8,000 workers as of late 2024 and supports a network of nearly 200 local suppliers, while recent investments exceeding $1 billion aim to expand capacity to 10 Dreamliners per month by 2026.[1][5][6] The site's development was driven by strategic decisions favoring South Carolina's business climate, including its right-to-work status, but it has faced significant scrutiny over production quality, with Federal Aviation Administration investigations into defects such as improperly installed fuselage fasteners on undelivered 787s and whistleblower allegationsâ€”exemplified by former quality manager John Barnett's claims of overlooked defects and retaliatory pressuresâ€”highlighting persistent challenges in maintaining rigorous standards amid rapid scaling.[7][8][9]

History

Initial Sub-Assembly Operations (2000s)

In December 2004, Boeing suppliers Vought Aircraft Industries and Global Aeronautica announced the selection of North Charleston, South Carolina, as the location for facilities to assemble fuselage sections for the 787 Dreamliner, marking the inception of sub-assembly operations in the state.[10] Vought would fabricate and assemble the aft fuselage, incorporating structures and systems installation, while Global Aeronautica, a joint venture between Alenia Aeronautica and Vought, would integrate mid-fuselage barrels shipped from Italy and Japan.[11][12] This outsourcing strategy aligned with Boeing's global supply chain model for the 787 program, launched earlier that year, aiming to leverage specialized tier-one suppliers for major components.[13] Groundbreaking for the shared site occurred in February 2005, followed by construction starting on March 17, 2005, with Vought's facility targeting completion for aftbody work and Global Aeronautica erecting its first structural steel by October 2005.[14][12] The Global Aeronautica integration plant officially opened in December 2006, enabling the joining of composite fuselage barrels into sub-assemblies for shipment to Boeing's final assembly line in Everett, Washington.[14] Vought's adjacent operations similarly ramped up, with the combined facilities expected to handle approximately 60 percent of the 787's fuselage sub-assembly by early 2007.[15] These plants benefited from South Carolina's economic incentives, including tax credits and workforce training programs, which facilitated rapid site development on former industrial land near Charleston International Airport.[16] Initial production faced integration challenges inherent to the 787's novel composite-intensive design and distributed supply chain, contributing to program delays as sub-assemblies required precise alignment and quality verification before air freight via Boeing's Dreamlifter.[17] By 2008, Boeing acquired Global Aeronautica to gain fuller control over mid-fuselage operations, followed by the $580 million purchase of Vought's North Charleston assets in July 2009, rebranding the site as Boeing Charleston and integrating it directly under the 787 program.[18][19] These acquisitions, prompted by persistent supply chain bottlenecks, transitioned sub-assembly from supplier-led to Boeing-managed processes while preserving the facilities' core role in fuselage join work.[13]

Shift to Final Assembly and Plant Expansion (2010â€“2012)

In late 2009, Boeing announced plans to establish a second final assembly line for the 787 Dreamliner at its North Charleston, South Carolina facility, transitioning the site from primarily sub-assembly operations to full aircraft assembly.[20] This decision followed the South Carolina Legislature's approval of incentives estimated at $900 million over 30 years, aimed at attracting the investment.[21] Groundbreaking for the new final assembly and delivery center occurred on November 20, 2009, marking the beginning of significant infrastructure development to support the expanded role.[3] Construction progressed rapidly during 2010 and 2011, with the 1.2 million square foot final assembly building completed and opened on November 12, 2011. The facility was designed to integrate fuselage sections previously assembled on-siteâ€”such as aft and mid-fuselagesâ€”with forward sections shipped from Everett, Washington, enabling complete aircraft final assembly, including systems installation, testing, and painting. This expansion increased the site's production capacity and workforce needs, building on its existing sub-assembly expertise to position North Charleston as a key hub for 787 variants.[22] By early 2012, the shift culminated in the rollout of the first 787 Dreamliner fully assembled at the South Carolina plant on April 27, 2012, a milestone that validated the facility's capabilities despite ongoing challenges in the broader 787 program, such as supply chain delays.[20] Initial production focused on the 787-8 model, with plans to incorporate future variants, reflecting Boeing's strategy to diversify assembly locations for efficiency and risk mitigation.[23] The expansion not only enhanced vertical integration at the site but also spurred local economic growth through job creation, with employment rising to support the new operations.[24]

Major Production Milestones and Challenges (2013â€“Present)

In March 2015, Boeing's South Carolina facility delivered its first 787-9 Dreamliner, marking the site's entry into production of the stretched variant following initial 787-8 assemblies.[25] This followed the start of 787-9 final assembly at the North Charleston plant in December 2014, expanding capabilities beyond the baseline model.[26] The facility achieved a significant milestone in 2017 with the rollout of the first 787-10 on February 17, the longest variant in the Dreamliner family, followed by its maiden flight on March 31 from North Charleston.[27] Production of the 787-10, exclusive to South Carolina due to facility size requirements, culminated in the first delivery to Singapore Airlines on March 25, 2018.[28] Boeing announced in October 2020 its decision to consolidate all 787 final assembly to the South Carolina site starting in mid-2021, a move accelerated to March 2021 to streamline operations and leverage the plant's automation and capacity for larger variants.[29][30] By December 2024, the company committed to a $1 billion infrastructure expansion at the North Charleston campuses, aiming to increase 787 production to 10 aircraft per month by 2026 and potentially 16 per month upon completion in 2028.[31] This followed a production ramp-up, with the site achieving two additional Dreamliners per month by August 2025 compared to prior rates.[32] Parallel to these advancements, the South Carolina plant has encountered persistent quality control challenges. The Federal Aviation Administration intensified oversight in 2019 after identifying manufacturing errors, including improper fuselage joins and foreign object debris, which at times compromised aircraft integrity.[33] In August 2020, Boeing grounded eight 787s due to substandard tolerances in rear fuselage sections produced at the site, requiring inspections and repairs.[34] Further issues emerged in 2024 when Boeing self-reported that employees at the facility had falsified inspection records on certain 787 components, prompting an FAA investigation into non-performed quality checks.[35] The agency continued scrutiny into 2025, mandating inspections of forward pressure bulkheads on approximately 980 Dreamliners to address production quality deficiencies discovered at the plant.[36] These episodes, including skin-to-fuselage attachment flaws, have delayed deliveries and underscored ongoing efforts to enhance manufacturing rigor amid the site's role as the sole 787 assembly location.[37]

Facility Overview

Site Location and Infrastructure

The Boeing South Carolina campuses are situated in North Charleston, South Carolina, at 5400 International Boulevard and 9775 Patriot Boulevard, adjacent to Charleston International Airport.[38] This proximity to the airport's runways and air traffic infrastructure enables efficient final aircraft delivery flights directly from the production site.[39] The overall site encompasses approximately 240 acres dedicated to manufacturing operations.[40] Key infrastructure includes a primary Final Assembly Building covering 1.2 million square feet, constructed with over 18,000 tons of structural steel and certified LEED Gold for sustainability.[41] Supporting facilities comprise 610,000 square feet of core production space, 375,000 square feet of mezzanine areas for additional operations, and 17,400 square feet of office accommodations.[40] The campuses incorporate advanced energy systems, achieving 100% renewable power usage, with roughly 20% generated from on-site solar panels.[42] In December 2024, Boeing announced a $1 billion investment to enhance infrastructure across both campuses, targeting increased 787 Dreamliner output and adding 500 jobs within five years.[43] Expansion activities commenced in August 2025, incorporating a second final assembly building, expanded employee parking, and upgraded production support systems to accommodate a production rate of up to 10 aircraft per month.[44][45] These developments, executed in partnership with contractors like BE&K and HITT, focus on modular construction to minimize disruptions to ongoing assembly lines.[46]

Production Processes and Capabilities

The Boeing South Carolina facility in North Charleston conducts fabrication, assembly, and systems installation for the 787 Dreamliner's aft fuselage sections, while also joining and integrating midbody fuselage sections supplied from international partners.[47][48] Final assembly processes involve mating forward, center, and aft fuselage barrels, followed by integration of wings, engines, landing gear, and major systems including electrical wiring, hydraulics, and avionics, culminating in functional testing and painting.[47] The facility employs lean manufacturing principles to streamline workflows, with production rates targeted at up to 10 aircraft per month following recent expansions.[44] Capabilities emphasize advanced composites, which constitute over 50% of the 787's structure, utilizing techniques such as automated fiber placement (AFP) for laying carbon-fiber-reinforced polymer tapes and resin infusion for components like wing trailing edges.[49][50] Autoclaves cure composite parts under high pressure and temperature, while a dedicated paint booth incorporates automation for efficient application.[51] Robotic systems, including a quadbot configuration of four robots, perform precise operations such as drilling approximately 3,000 holes in the aft fuselage with sub-millimeter accuracy, reducing human error and cycle times.[52] Digital tools enhance traceability and efficiency, with RFID tags tracking tools across the site and a digital thread connecting design data to production steps for real-time quality monitoring.[53] Since mid-2021, the facility has consolidated all 787 final assembly from Boeing's Everett site, enabling unified process improvements and economies of scale.[29] A $1 billion investment announced in December 2024 supports infrastructure upgrades and adds 500 jobs, while construction of a second final assembly lineâ€”mirroring the existing oneâ€”began in August 2025 to double output capacity.[54][44] An on-site Advanced Manufacturing Research Center, opened in 2015, prototypes innovations in composite fuselage and propulsion systems to refine these processes.[51]

Operations and Aircraft Production

787 Dreamliner Assembly

Boeing's North Charleston facility in South Carolina serves as the primary final assembly site for the 787 Dreamliner, handling fuselage integration, systems installation, and completion for variants including the 787-8, 787-9, and exclusively the 787-10.[20][55] The site also fabricates and assembles midbody and aftbody fuselage sections prior to final joining.[20] Groundbreaking for the final assembly building occurred on November 20, 2009, with the 1,200,000-square-foot facility opening in June 2011.[3][4] The first 787 Dreamliner fully assembled at the South Carolina plant rolled out on April 27, 2012, followed by its maiden flight on May 23, 2012.[20][10] Initial production targeted three aircraft per month at full rate.[4] In October 2020, Boeing announced consolidation of all 787 final assembly to North Charleston starting mid-2021, transferring operations from Everett, Washington, to streamline production.[56] Assembly processes at the facility include the "Join Cell" for fuselage section mating, followed by wing and tail integration, avionics and interiors installation, and painting.[57] The site supports a production capacity sufficient for up to 12 aircraft per month as of 2020, with expansions underway since August 2025 to reach 10 per month by 2027 and potentially 16 per month by 2028.[58][44][59]

Supply Chain Integration and Innovations

The Boeing South Carolina facility in North Charleston serves as the primary final assembly site for the 787 Dreamliner variants, integrating a global supply chain where Tier 1 suppliers deliver large subassemblies such as composite fuselage barrels from Italy and Japan, wings from Mitsubishi Heavy Industries in Japan, and tail sections from Spirit AeroSystems in the United States.[60][61] These components are transported via air, sea, or land to the plant, where they undergo joining, systems installation, and testing in a streamlined process that has supported production of over 1,100 787s since 2011.[62] The consolidation of all 787 final assembly to South Carolina starting in mid-2021 eliminated parallel operations at Everett, Washington, enabling standardized integration and process improvements to address prior inconsistencies.[29] A key innovation in the 787 supply chain was the risk-sharing partnership model, under which suppliers co-invested in development and toolingâ€”totaling billions in supplier commitmentsâ€”in exchange for long-term production contracts and intellectual property rights, shifting from Boeing's traditional "build-to-print" to "build-to-plan" approach where partners managed design and manufacturing of major sections.[63][64] This aimed to distribute financial risks, accelerate innovation in composites and efficiency, and tap global expertise, but resulted in coordination challenges, including misaligned incentives akin to a Prisoner's Dilemma that contributed to delays exceeding two years for the first flight on December 15, 2009.[60][65] At South Carolina, this model has been supplemented by partnerships with nearly 200 local suppliers providing fabrication support, enhancing regional integration while Boeing maintains oversight through supply chain management roles focused on quality and logistics.[1] Persistent issues from the outsourced model, such as fuselage panel gaps, improper fasteners identified in June 2024, and defective parts from suppliers like Leonardo in Italy, have prompted intensified FAA audits and production restrictions at the South Carolina plant, underscoring causal links between distributed responsibilities and quality variability.[60][66] To bolster integration, Boeing announced a $1 billion investment in December 2024 for facility upgrades and 500 new jobs, targeting enhanced manufacturing efficiency and supply chain resilience amid surging 787 demand.[6] These efforts reflect ongoing adaptations to mitigate outsourcing's inherent risks while preserving the model's innovative cost-sharing benefits.[67]

Workforce Dynamics

Employment Scale and Demographics

The Boeing South Carolina operations, centered in North Charleston, employed 8,253 workers across its campuses as of December 31, 2024, encompassing assembly, engineering, and support roles primarily focused on the 787 Dreamliner program.[5] This marked a 21.6% increase from 7,864 employees in January 2024, reflecting hiring to address production ramp-ups and supply chain demands.[68] However, subsequent cost-reduction measures led to approximately 220 permanent layoffs announced in November 2024, followed by 67 in December 2024 and 69 more in February 2025, reducing the headcount to an estimated 7,900 by early 2025.[69][70][71] Employment at the site has expanded substantially since its 2011 opening as a final assembly facility, surpassing initial projections of 3,800 direct jobs; by 2022, the workforce reached about 6,500, driven by production milestones and regional aerospace cluster growth that added over 6,000 related positions statewide.[7][72] The non-unionized workforce consists largely of skilled manufacturing technicians, engineers, and logistics personnel, with a focus on attracting talent through local partnerships and incentives.[73] Specific demographic data for the South Carolina site remains limited, as Boeing's public disclosures aggregate at the corporate level; however, a 2021 company report highlighted elevated representation of Black employees at the North Charleston facility relative to other sites like Washington state, where the local Black population is 4.4%, attributing this to South Carolina's demographics (27.6% African American statewide).[74] Company-wide U.S. figures from recent analyses show 24.2% women and 35.8% racial/ethnic minorities in the workforce, with veterans comprising over 14% nationallyâ€”a proportion likely mirrored locally given recruitment emphases on military transitions.[75][73] These patterns reflect hiring aligned with regional labor pools rather than centralized quotas, though Boeing's overall diversity metrics have drawn scrutiny for underrepresentation of minorities in executive roles (21.8%).[76]

Labor Practices and Non-Union Status

The Boeing North Charleston facility has maintained a non-union status since its opening for 787 Dreamliner final assembly in 2011, strategically selected by the company to establish operations in South Carolina's right-to-work environment, which prohibits mandatory union dues and reduces the risk of labor disruptions observed at its unionized Washington plants. Boeing's 2009 announcement of the site's development followed strikes by the International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers (IAM) in Puget Sound, prompting IAM complaints that the relocation retaliated against protected concerted activities. The National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) filed an unfair labor practice complaint against Boeing in April 2011, alleging the move violated federal law by punishing workers for past strikes, but withdrew it in September 2011 after Boeing reached a new collective bargaining agreement with the IAM in Washington, restoring some job security there.[77] Unionization drives have consistently failed at the plant, reflecting worker preferences in a state with historically low union density. In a February 2017 election supervised by the NLRB, 2,097 of approximately 3,000 eligible production and maintenance workers voted against IAM representation, compared to 731 in favor, resulting in a decisive 74% rejection. Organizers cited grievances over management responsiveness and job security, but opponents highlighted potential drawbacks like dues payments and reduced operational flexibility in a facility producing time-sensitive composite aircraft. Subsequent efforts, including a 2018 IAM petition at a Boeing supplier site on the campus, faced setbacks amid allegations of retaliatory firings; the IAM filed unfair labor practice charges claiming four workers were dismissed for union activity, but an NLRB administrative law judge ruled in Boeing's favor in 2019, finding insufficient evidence of unlawful motive.[78][79] Labor practices emphasize direct employer-employee relations, with compensation structured to compete for skilled labor without collective bargaining premiums. Non-union workers receive base wages averaging around $25â€“$35 per hour for assemblers, supplemented by performance bonuses and profit-sharing, though these have varied with company financials. In response to the IAM's 53-day strike at unionized facilities in 2024, Boeing implemented furloughs for some salaried staff but extended equivalent concessions to South Carolina production employees in December 2024, including a 9% immediate raise, $12,000 retention bonuses, and enhanced 401(k) contributions, effectively mirroring ratified union gains to prevent turnover and maintain output amid delivery backlogs. No strikes have occurred at the site due to its non-union structure, though isolated complaints of workplace threats during organizingâ€”such as reported intimidation of pro-union employeesâ€”have been documented without resulting in broad certification or penalties. This model has enabled Boeing to scale production flexibly, employing over 6,000 workers by 2025, but has drawn criticism from labor advocates for lacking independent grievance mechanisms.[80][81]

Economic Contributions

Direct Job Creation and Wage Effects

The Boeing South Carolina facility in North Charleston directly created approximately 3,800 jobs upon its 787 Dreamliner assembly line opening in 2011, as stipulated in state incentive agreements.[7] By January 2022, direct employment at the site reached 5,521 positions, reflecting steady post-opening growth amid production ramp-ups.[82] Employment expanded further to around 6,500 by the end of 2022, driven by post-pandemic recovery and increased aircraft orders.[72] As of December 2024, Boeing's South Carolina operations employed 8,253 workers, primarily at the North Charleston campuses focused on 787 final assembly and component manufacturing, though recent company-wide cost reductions included 220 layoffs at the site in November 2024.[5][69] A December 2024 announcement of a $1 billion expansion commits to adding 500 direct jobs over five years, targeting enhancements in production capacity and supply chain integration.[31] Boeing's direct hires in South Carolina span skilled trades, engineering, and assembly roles, with compensation structures emphasizing performance-based pay in a non-union environment. Hourly wages for assemblers and technicians typically range from $25 to $33, equating to annual earnings of $52,000 to $69,000 for full-time positions, while engineering and managerial roles average $90,000 to $140,000 annually.[83] These rates exceed South Carolina's statewide manufacturing median of approximately $48,000, fostering localized wage uplift through labor market competition for aerospace talent.[84] In December 2024, Boeing extended annual raises of up to 4.5% to its South Carolina workforce, mirroring negotiated increases at unionized facilities to retain skilled personnel amid industry pressures.[80]

Broader Industry and Regional Growth

The establishment of Boeing's North Charleston facility in 2011 catalyzed significant expansion in South Carolina's aerospace sector, fostering a regional cluster that attracted suppliers and related firms. Analysis indicates the plant generated approximately 6,000 additional aerospace jobs statewide beyond Boeing's initial commitment of 3,800 positions, contributing to a 311% overall increase in aerospace employment in the state over the subsequent decade.[7][82] This growth stemmed from Boeing's role as an anchor tenant, drawing tier-one and tier-two suppliers to the Charleston metropolitan area and beyond, with the local employment multiplier estimated at 2.6 indirect jobs per direct Boeing position.[85][86] Wage effects further underscored the facility's influence, with aerospace industry wages rising by 10% on average in the ten years following the plant's opening, outpacing broader economic trends.[86][82] The number of aerospace establishments in South Carolina increased by 44%, reflecting diversification into areas such as composites manufacturing and avionics, supported by the state's incentives and Boeing's supply chain demands.[82] By 2024, the state's aerospace cluster encompassed over 40 companies in the Greater Charleston region alone, contributing to an annual economic output of $38.3 billion and sustaining more than 100,000 jobs statewide through direct, indirect, and induced effects.[87][88] This cluster development positioned South Carolina as a competitive aerospace hub, with Boeing's operations enhancing inter-industry linkages, including ties to defense and advanced manufacturing sectors. Post-2020 expansions, including Boeing's $1 billion investment announced in 2024 to ramp up 787 production, are projected to further amplify regional growth by bolstering supplier ecosystems and infrastructure investments.[89][90] The facility's non-union status and right-to-work environment have also appealed to ancillary firms, promoting sustained industrial agglomeration without the labor disruptions seen in other states.[91]

Quality Assurance and Safety Concerns

Historical Production Defects

The Boeing North Charleston facility in South Carolina, responsible for final assembly of the 787 Dreamliner, has encountered recurring production quality issues since the plant's operational ramp-up in the early 2010s, including improper fuselage shimming, structural gaps, and foreign object debris. Worker complaints documented in 2019 highlighted defective manufacturing practices, such as leaving debris on aircraft and managerial pressure to overlook violations, which raised concerns about rushed assembly processes prioritizing output over precision. These reports, corroborated by FAA oversight, contributed to a pattern of defects traced to the site's composite barrel joining and skin application stages.[92][93] A significant defect emerged in August 2019 when Boeing identified inconsistencies in shimsâ€”thin fillers used to close gaps between fuselage sections during assemblyâ€”which failed to meet dimensional specifications despite passing load tests. This issue, originating from the North Charleston plant's shim production process, prompted inspections of undelivered aircraft and contributed to delivery pauses, as the gaps could theoretically propagate under repeated pressurization cycles over the jet's lifespan. By September 2020, related problems escalated with two manufacturing flaws in the rear fuselage: improper shim installation allowing skin delamination and inadequate gap filling, compromising structural integrity under maximum stress; this led Boeing to ground eight completed 787s for remediation, all assembled at the South Carolina site.[94][34][95] The FAA responded with intensified scrutiny, halting 787 deliveries twice in 2020-2021 due to these and other non-conformities, such as unvalidated production processes at North Charleston, and mandating enhanced inspections. Further anomalies surfaced in 2023, including paper-thin attachment gaps requiring shim plugs that deviated from tolerances, delaying additional handovers. In 2024, whistleblower allegations from engineer Sam Salehpour detailed fuselage section gaps exceeding 0.05 inchesâ€”far beyond the 0.005-inch targetâ€”potentially risking long-term fatigue from assembly drilling into composites, though Boeing maintained no immediate safety threat and implemented process tweaks like improved section matching. Concurrently, Boeing self-reported falsified inspection records by South Carolina employees on wing-to-body fairing work, triggering an FAA probe into procedural lapses.[96][97][98][99] These defects, often linked to the challenges of scaling composite manufacturing at a newer, non-union facility, have prompted airlines like Qatar Airways to reject certain North Charleston-built 787s in favor of those from Everett, Washington, citing perceived quality variances. FAA audits, including a 2024 review, noted persistent risks at the site, such as metal shavings near wiring and unaddressed defective parts, underscoring systemic oversight gaps despite Boeing's engineering fixes. While no in-service failures have been directly attributed, the cumulative halts have deferred over 100 deliveries since 2020, eroding confidence in the plant's output reliability.[100][101]

Regulatory Scrutiny and Investigations

In 2019, whistleblower complaints from workers at Boeing's North Charleston facility highlighted persistent quality control deficiencies in 787 Dreamliner assembly, including defective manufacturing processes, uncleaned debris in fuselages, and managerial pressure to omit violation reports from records.[92] The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) responded by initiating an investigation into these allegations, which contributed to a broader federal criminal probe by the Department of Justice into Boeing's compliance with a prior deferred prosecution agreement stemming from the 737 MAX crashes.[102] This scrutiny revealed systemic lapses in oversight at the non-union South Carolina plant, where production pressures had allegedly prioritized speed over rigorous documentation and part verification.[103] A significant escalation occurred in April 2024, when Boeing self-disclosed to the FAA that multiple employees at the North Charleston plant had falsified inspection records for wing-to-body fairing bonding on approximately 50 to 70 undelivered 787 aircraft.[9] [99] The FAA promptly launched a formal investigation to determine if required inspectionsâ€”critical for ensuring proper electrical bonding to mitigate lightning strike risksâ€”were actually performed or if records were deliberately misrepresented.[104] Boeing stated that the issue posed no immediate flight safety risk to operational aircraft but committed to re-inspecting affected planes before delivery, halting 787 production line movement temporarily.[105] Subsequent developments in 2024 included Boeing's identification of improper fastener installations on additional undelivered 787s from the same facility, prompting further FAA-mandated corrective actions.[106] A U.S. Department of Transportation Inspector General audit released in October 2024 criticized FAA oversight of 787 production as ineffective, citing inadequate data-driven risk assessments, insufficient verification of delegated engineering authority, and failure to track recurring non-compliances at Boeing facilities, including those in South Carolina.[107] During a June 2024 U.S. Senate Commerce Committee hearing, the North Charleston plant's practices drew specific attention amid broader concerns over Boeing's safety culture, with lawmakers questioning executive accountability for quality escapes.[108] These investigations underscored ongoing challenges in balancing production ramp-ups with verifiable compliance at the site, though no criminal charges directly tied to South Carolina operations had been filed as of late 2025.

Recent Developments

Expansion Projects and New Orders (2024â€“2025)

In December 2024, Boeing announced a $1 billion investment to upgrade infrastructure at its North Charleston, South Carolina facilities, expanding operations across both campuses at 5400 International Boulevard and 9775 Patriot Boulevard to support increased 787 Dreamliner production.[43][109] The upgrades aim to enable a production rate of 10 aircraft per month by 2026, with initial operations expected online in early 2027 and full completion targeted for 2028 to reach a capacity of 16 per month.[43][44] This expansion is projected to create 500 new jobs over five years, focusing on engineering, manufacturing, and support roles.[43][110] Boeing awarded contracts to the BE&K and HITT joint venture in March 2025 to execute the infrastructure work, including facility enhancements for higher-volume assembly of the 787, which is exclusively finalized at the South Carolina site.[111][112] Construction progressed to active expansion phases by August 2025, aligning with Boeing's strategy to address backlog demands amid prior production slowdowns due to quality issues.[44][113] New orders bolstered the rationale for these expansions, including a May 2025 $96 billion agreement with Qatar Airways for approximately 210 South Carolina-assembled 787s, marking Boeing's largest-ever order for planes from the site.[114][115] In September 2025, Turkish Airlines confirmed a firm order for up to 75 additional 787 Dreamliners built in North Charleston, its largest single purchase from Boeing.[116] These commitments contributed to 807 gross 787 orders secured by Boeing from 2020 through July 2025, driving forecasts for sustained production ramps at the facility.[59]

Layoffs and Operational Adjustments

In November 2024, Boeing notified approximately 220 employees at its North Charleston, South Carolina facility of permanent layoffs, effective January 17, 2025, as part of a company-wide reduction targeting 10% of its global workforce, or about 17,000 positions.[69] [117] These cuts primarily affected business support, training, and administrative roles rather than direct production staff, amid broader financial pressures including a machinists' strike, production delays on the 787 Dreamliner, and ongoing cash flow challenges.[118] Subsequent announcements in December 2024 added 67 more layoffs in South Carolina, with 66 positions eliminated at the North Charleston site and one at Joint Base Charleston, effective February 21, 2025.[70] [119] By early 2025, these reductions contributed to a cumulative total exceeding 287 job losses in the state during January and February, reflecting iterative cost-control measures in response to declining commercial airplane orders and regulatory scrutiny over manufacturing quality.[71] Operational adjustments at the facility have included efforts to streamline production processes for the 787 program, such as enhanced quality inspections and inventory management to address persistent defects like fuselage shimming issues identified in prior audits.[120] Concurrently, Boeing committed $1 billion in December 2024 to infrastructure upgrades at the North Charleston campuses, aiming to support a production ramp-up to 10 787s per month by 2027 while creating 500 new jobs over five years, signaling a strategic pivot toward long-term capacity expansion despite short-term workforce contractions.[43] [121] These investments focus on facility expansions to accommodate increased Dreamliner output, though execution depends on resolving supply chain bottlenecks and FAA approvals.[46]
