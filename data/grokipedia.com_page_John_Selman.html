John Selman


John Henry Selman (November 16, 1839 – April 6, 1896) was an American Old West figure who operated as both outlaw and lawman, most notably as the El Paso, Texas, constable who fatally shot the infamous gunman John Wesley Hardin in the Acme Saloon on August 19, 1895.[1][2] Born in Madison County, Arkansas, Selman relocated with his family to Grayson County, Texas, in 1858, where he later enlisted in the Confederate Army on December 16, 1861, serving in a cavalry unit.[1][2] After the Civil War, Selman engaged in cattle rustling and other illicit activities, leading a notorious gang known as Selman's Scouts in New Mexico Territory during the early 1870s, where they were accused of multiple murders, rapes, and robberies against civilians.[3][2] Fleeing territorial indictments, he returned to Texas, intermittently holding deputy sheriff positions in Fort Griffin and other locales amid ongoing conflicts with rivals, including killings tied to personal vendettas.[1][2] By the 1880s, Selman had settled in El Paso, serving as deputy United States marshal and later city marshal, though his tenure was marked by corruption allegations and violent enforcement.[1][2] The shooting of Hardin—whom Selman struck in the back of the head while Hardin stood unarmed at the bar—sparked immediate controversy, with Selman acquitted on self-defense grounds despite evidence suggesting premeditation linked to Hardin's complaints against Selman's handling of Hardin's fiancée's arrest.[4][2] Less than a year later, on April 5, 1896, Selman himself was killed in a street shootout with fellow lawman George Scarborough in El Paso, succumbing the next day to his wounds; the incident stemmed from mutual accusations of misconduct.[5][6] Selman's son, John Selman Jr., later gained infamy for murdering Pat Garrett, the slayer of Billy the Kid, in 1908.[1]

Early Life and Background

Birth and Family Origins

John Henry Selman was born on November 16, 1839, in Madison County, Arkansas.[1] He was the son of Jeremiah Selman, an Englishman born around 1804, and Permelia Norwood, born around 1810 in Georgia.[1][7] Jeremiah and Permelia married on December 3, 1831, and raised a family of at least nine children in Arkansas before relocating westward.[8]

Relocation to Texas and Upbringing

In 1858, the Selman family relocated from Madison County, Arkansas, to Grayson County, Texas, when John Selman was 19 years old.[2][9][10] This move placed Selman in the expanding frontier settlements of North Texas, characterized by agricultural pursuits and the hardships of pioneer existence, including interactions with Native American tribes and the buildup to the Civil War.[1] Historical accounts indicate that Selman's early years in Texas involved adaptation to this rugged environment, though specific personal details of his upbringing remain sparse in primary records.[2] By late 1861, at age 22, Selman had established sufficient roots in Grayson County to enlist in Confederate service, reflecting the regional loyalties and martial culture of his adopted home.[1][11]

Military Service

Enlistment in the Confederate Army

John Selman enlisted in the Confederate Army on December 15, 1861, shortly before the death of his father, Jeremiah Selman, on December 16, 1861.[1] The family had relocated to Grayson County, Texas, in 1858, where Jeremiah passed away, prompting Selman's immediate entry into military service amid the escalating Civil War.[1] He joined as a private in Company B of the 22nd Texas Cavalry Regiment (also known as the 2nd Texas Cavalry), a unit primarily recruited from North Texas counties for frontier defense and operations against Union forces and Native American tribes in the region.[1] [12] The regiment, organized in May 1862 under Colonel George R. Reeves, focused on irregular warfare, including scouting and skirmishes along the Texas frontier rather than major eastern theater engagements.[1] Selman's enlistment aligned with widespread Southern mobilization in Texas, where local militias and cavalry units emphasized defense against perceived threats from Union incursions and Comanche raids.[1]

Experiences During the Civil War

Following the death of his father, Jeremiah Selman, on December 16, 1861, John Henry Selman enlisted as a private in Company B of the 22nd Texas Cavalry Regiment, a Confederate unit formed in early 1862 primarily for frontier defense and operations in the Trans-Mississippi Theater.[12][1] The regiment, under commanders such as R. H. Taylor and later assigned to brigades in Texas and Louisiana, conducted scouting missions against Native American raids, guarded coastal areas near Galveston, and participated in expeditions into Indian Territory and the Red River region to counter Union incursions, though it saw limited major engagements compared to eastern armies.[13] Selman's tenure involved routine cavalry duties typical of Texas Confederate units, including patrols and skirmishes amid the Confederacy's resource shortages and high desertion rates in the western theater, where geographic isolation and family hardships often prompted soldiers to abandon posts.[13] Military records indicate he deserted the regiment at an unspecified point, returning to civilian life in Texas, after which he was apprehended and subjected to court-martial proceedings before rejoining or evading further service as the war concluded in 1865.[12][14] This desertion aligned with broader patterns in the 22nd Texas Cavalry, which suffered from morale erosion and manpower losses, contributing to its eventual disbandment without formal surrender.[13]

Post-War Settlement and Family Life

Marriage and Domestic Affairs

Following the Civil War, John Selman married Edna DeGraffenreid on August 17, 1865, in Grayson County, Texas.[15][2] The couple relocated to Shackelford County, where they raised four children—three sons and one daughter—amid Selman's early ranching and freighting endeavors.[9][1] Their family life involved frequent moves, including a brief stint in Colfax County, New Mexico Territory, before returning to Fort Griffin, Texas, where domestic stability was strained by Selman's growing involvement in local disputes and economic hardships.[2] Edna Selman died on an unspecified date in 1879 during childbirth, delivering a stillborn child, which left the surviving four children requiring alternative care; they were subsequently placed under the guardianship of Edna's niece.[2][1] This loss coincided with escalating legal troubles for Selman, including cattle rustling accusations, prompting his flight from Texas and further disrupting family cohesion as he abandoned immediate paternal responsibilities.[9] In the aftermath, Selman formed a relationship with an unidentified Hispanic woman, described by his son John Marion Selman Jr. as fostering a congenial household environment, though details on formal marriage or offspring remain unverified in primary records.[14] By 1893, at age 54, Selman wed 16-year-old Romula Granadine in El Paso, Texas, marking a late-life domestic arrangement amid his reinstatement as a lawman, with no recorded children from this union.[16] These successive partnerships reflect a pattern of transient family structures influenced by Selman's peripatetic and legally precarious lifestyle.[1]

Initial Economic and Community Involvement

Following his discharge from the Confederate army in 1865, John Selman married Edna Degraffenried on August 17 of that year, establishing a family that would include four children.[1] The couple initially resided in northern Texas before relocating westward amid the post-war expansion of cattle ranching frontiers. By the early 1870s, Selman had taken up ranching near Albany in Shackelford County, engaging in the livestock operations that dominated the region's economy during Reconstruction-era recovery.[10][9] In the mid-1870s, the Selmans moved to the Fort Griffin vicinity, where Selman continued ranching while integrating into local governance structures. He accepted a position as deputy sheriff under John M. Larn, the elected Shackelford County sheriff, which involved patrolling against cattle theft and participating in informal vigilante committees aimed at curbing frontier lawlessness.[1][9] This role marked his early community involvement, as deputies in sparsely settled counties like Shackelford often blended enforcement duties with economic self-interest, including the inspection of hides to verify ownership amid rampant rustling.[1] Such positions were common for former soldiers transitioning to civilian life, providing both authority and supplemental income in areas where formal courts were distant and underdeveloped.[9] Selman's ranching pursuits aligned with broader Texas trends, where open-range cattle raising boomed due to high postwar demand in northern markets, though vulnerabilities to theft and Indian raids necessitated communal defenses.[10] His deputyship under Larn, however, foreshadowed tensions, as the pair's methods drew accusations of overreach, including the selective enforcement that blurred lines between protection and predation in Shackelford County's volatile hide trade.[1] These activities represented Selman's foundational efforts to secure economic stability and local influence before escalating conflicts propelled him toward more notorious enterprises.[9]

Descent into Outlawry

Formation of Selman's Scouts

Following increasing scrutiny for cattle rustling and vigilante excesses in Texas, John Selman fled to Lincoln County, New Mexico Territory, in 1878, where he assembled a gang known as Selman's Scouts.[2] The group, operating primarily in the turbulent aftermath of the Lincoln County War, consisted of Selman as leader, his younger brother Thomas "Cat" Selman, and other associates including John Gunther.[14] Initially framed by some accounts as scouts or regulators, the outfit quickly devolved into outright banditry, with no formal authorization from territorial authorities.[1] The Scouts' formation capitalized on the lawless vacuum in New Mexico, enabling rustling operations targeting horses and cattle across the border regions.[2] In September and October 1878 alone, they conducted raids that pillaged ranches, businesses, and homes, while committing murders of settlers and suspected rivals—acts that drew condemnation from Governor Lew Wallace, who placed Selman atop a wanted list for capture or elimination.[3] Estimates of their depredations include at least a dozen killings, though precise victim counts remain disputed due to the era's sparse records and retaliatory violence.[2] By late 1878, intensified pursuits by posses and U.S. Army units fragmented the gang, forcing Selman into temporary exile across the border into Mexico.[1] The Scouts' brief reign exemplified the blurred lines between self-appointed enforcers and predators in frontier territories, where economic desperation and weak governance fostered such enterprises.[3]

Cattle Rustling and Vigilante Killings

In the mid-1870s, John Selman served as a deputy under Sheriff John M. Larn in Fort Griffin, Texas, where the pair ostensibly led vigilante efforts against cattle rustlers but instead engaged in rustling themselves, stealing livestock from neighboring ranchers and terrorizing Shackelford County residents.[1][2] Under the pretense of pursuing thieves on the Texas range in 1873, Selman and Larn murdered at least eight cowboys, framing the killings as enforcement against rustling to mask their own criminal activities.[17] This vigilante facade enabled them to control local committees, including the Tin Hat Brigade, while expanding their theft operations until Larn's arrest on June 22, 1878, and subsequent lynching by the group the following day, prompting Selman to flee charges of theft and complicity in murder.[2] Following his escape from Fort Griffin, Selman relocated to Lincoln County, New Mexico Territory, in 1878, where he assembled Selman's Scouts, a gang of roughly 15 rough characters including his son Tom Selman, Roscoe "Rustling Bob" Bryant, and Ed Hart, initially facing theft warrants from Texas.[18] From September to October 1878, the Scouts conducted widespread cattle and horse rustling across the region, targeting ranches near Seven Rivers and Fort Sumner while pillaging stores and homes; they also committed murders of innocent men and boys, with internal violence including Selman's killing of Hart in Lincoln County that September and the gang's execution of Bryant near Seven Rivers in October for suspected betrayal.[2][18] Additional deaths, such as those of members John Nelson and V.S. Whitaker, occurred during a posse confrontation near Fort Sumner on October 10, 1878, amid accusations of rape and robbery leveled against the group.[18][17] The Scouts' operations blurred lines between outlawry and self-proclaimed vigilance, as they occasionally posed as enforcers against regional crime but primarily terrorized settlements until disbanded by threats of martial law from New Mexico Governor Lew Wallace in late 1878, forcing Selman to dissolve the gang and retreat toward Texas borders.[2][1] These activities solidified Selman's reputation as a predatory figure who exploited frontier lawlessness for personal gain, with historical accounts emphasizing the Scouts' brutality over any legitimate anti-crime role.[18]

Flight, Relocation, and Return to Law Enforcement

Departure from Texas After Personal Losses

In 1879, John Selman's wife, Edna Beatrice Selman, died in Texas, leaving him to contend with the loss of his spouse amid ongoing legal entanglements from prior activities in the region.[1] [2] This personal tragedy compounded his precarious situation, as the couple had several young children who required care following her passing.[2] The following year, in 1880, Texas Rangers captured Selman and transported him to Shackelford County to face trial for the murder of John Wilson, a charge stemming from earlier vigilante actions.[1] [2] While awaiting trial, Selman escaped custody, prompting his permanent departure from Texas to evade further pursuit by authorities.[1] [2] He relocated to Lincoln County, New Mexico Territory, where the combination of familial bereavement and legal jeopardy had effectively severed his ties to his former life in Texas.[1] This flight marked a pivotal shift, allowing Selman to reorganize amid the turbulent frontier conditions of New Mexico, though it did not immediately resolve his outlaw associations.[2]

Reestablishment in El Paso as a Lawman

Following his departure from central Texas amid legal troubles and personal tragedies, including the killing of his son-in-law in 1881 and subsequent flight to Mexico, John Selman returned to Texas and settled in El Paso County by 1888 after outstanding charges against him were dropped.[1] In El Paso, a booming border town known for its lawless elements amid railroad expansion and cross-border trade, Selman initially operated a saloon and engaged primarily in gambling, activities that aligned with his prior experiences but distanced him from his earlier vigilante and rustling past.[2] This period allowed him to rebuild social and economic ties in a community tolerant of figures with ambiguous reputations, where gunmen often transitioned between outlawry and enforcement roles.[1] By 1892, Selman leveraged local connections to enter formal law enforcement, securing election as constable for El Paso's Precinct One on November 8, 1892.[11] His position involved maintaining order in a precinct encompassing much of the city's turbulent districts, including saloons and brothels frequented by transients and criminals.[1] Despite his history of extralegal killings during the 1870s cattle wars, Selman's election reflected El Paso's pragmatic acceptance of armed enforcers capable of handling violence, as the town lacked sufficient traditional peace officers to curb escalating disputes over gambling, prostitution, and smuggling.[2] He supplemented his constable duties with occasional deputy roles and private security work, often blurring lines between official authority and personal vendettas.[1] Selman's reestablishment solidified through high-profile actions that burnished his image among some residents as a decisive, if ruthless, upholder of order, even as critics viewed him as opportunistic.[2] On August 23, 1893, he married Romula Granadine, a local woman, further anchoring his presence in the community and signaling stability amid his dual life as gambler and officer.[1] This phase marked a calculated pivot from fugitive to accepted authority figure, enabled by El Paso's fluid social dynamics and the scarcity of qualified candidates willing to confront the town's armed factions.[1]

Roles as Deputy and City Marshal

Upon relocating to El Paso in 1888, Selman assumed the role of city constable, a position involving the enforcement of municipal laws, arrest of suspects, and preservation of public order within the city's precincts.[1] This appointment aligned with his efforts to rehabilitate his reputation amid the town's growing lawlessness, though he simultaneously engaged in gambling operations.[2] Selman was formally elected constable of El Paso Precinct No. 1 on November 8, 1892, serving in this capacity until his death.[11] The role, akin to a deputy in scope, empowered him to execute warrants, quell disturbances in saloons and brothels, and mediate disputes in a frontier setting where lines between law enforcement and personal vendettas often blurred. Local accounts described him as an effective, if controversial, officer who leveraged his gunfighting prowess to deter vice, earning the moniker "Uncle John" among some residents for his occasional affable demeanor toward children.[14]

Key Law Enforcement Actions in El Paso

Confrontation and Killing of Bass Outlaw

On April 5, 1894, El Paso city constable John Selman fatally shot Bass Outlaw, a former Texas Ranger recently dismissed for chronic drunkenness and threats against superiors, during a violent disturbance at Tillie Howard's brothel in El Paso, Texas.[1][2] Outlaw, who had previously killed at least three men in his career, arrived at the establishment heavily intoxicated and accidentally discharged his revolver, sparking panic among patrons and staff.[19][2] Selman and Texas Ranger Joe McKidrict, both present or quickly responding to the commotion, attempted to disarm and subdue the erratic Outlaw.[2][19] In the ensuing chaos, Outlaw turned his weapon on McKidrict, firing multiple shots that struck him in the head and back, killing him instantly.[2][19] Outlaw then pivoted toward Selman, firing shots that wounded the constable twice—once above the knee and once in the thigh—while Selman sustained powder burns from a near miss.[2][19] Selman returned fire, striking Outlaw above the heart in the chest, a wound that proved mortal despite Outlaw managing two additional shots before staggering into the street.[2][19] Outlaw surrendered to Texas Ranger Frank McMahon outside the brothel but succumbed to his injuries approximately four hours later.[2] The El Paso Times front page on April 6, 1894, documented the shootout, noting Outlaw's death and Selman's wounding as a stark example of frontier violence.[20] Selman faced trial for Outlaw's killing, but a judge instructed the jury to acquit on grounds of self-defense, and no conviction resulted.[2][1] The incident highlighted the volatile intersections of law enforcement duties and personal risks in El Paso's red-light district, where figures like Outlaw blurred lines between ranger service and recklessness.[1][19]

Tensions Leading to the Hardin Incident

In the months preceding the fatal encounter, John Wesley Hardin developed a romantic relationship with Helen Beulah Mroz, the wife of Martin Mroz, a known cattle rustler who had been driven from El Paso earlier.[21] In April 1895, upon Martin Mroz's return to the city, Hardin reportedly arranged for local law enforcement officers, including Constable John Selman, to eliminate him, culminating in Mroz's death during a shootout with authorities on the Juarez Bridge on June 29, 1895.[21] [22] Some historical accounts speculate that unpaid compensation to Selman for his role in the Mroz killing contributed to underlying resentment, though direct evidence remains circumstantial.[21] [23] Tensions escalated in early August 1895 when Selman's son, John Selman Jr., a city policeman, arrested Beulah Mroz for drunk and disorderly conduct after she discharged a firearm in a saloon while Hardin was out of town.[24] Hardin, incensed by the arrest and the subsequent fine imposed on Mroz, confronted the younger Selman, leading to a heated argument; certain eyewitness reports claim Hardin pistol-whipped him during the altercation. [25] Hardin extended his public criticism to the elder Selman, accusing him of complicity in his son's overreach and fueling a broader feud amid Hardin's ambitions to reestablish influence in El Paso through legal practice and potential political involvement.[26] On August 18, 1895, the day before the shooting, Hardin encountered Selman outside the Acme Saloon and verbally abused him over the arrest of Mroz, reportedly threatening violence and declaring intent to pursue Selman if he lingered nearby.[26] [27] This exchange intensified the personal animosity, with Selman later citing Hardin's threats and ongoing provocations as factors in the subsequent confrontation, though interpretations differ on whether the dispute stemmed primarily from familial defense, professional rivalry, or unresolved business over the Mroz matter.[21] [26]

The Killing of John Wesley Hardin

Immediate Events of August 19, 1895

On the evening of August 19, 1895, John Wesley Hardin engaged in a verbal confrontation with Constable John Selman outside a theater in El Paso, Texas, stemming from the earlier arrest of Beulah Mroz—Hardin's client and romantic interest—by Selman's son, John Selman Jr., for carrying a concealed weapon.[6] The exchange escalated to mutual threats, after which Hardin proceeded to the Acme Saloon on San Antonio Street.[6] Inside the saloon, Hardin stood at the bar on the west end, approximately 10 feet from the entrance, shaking dice with Henry Brown.[6] [26] Selman entered shortly thereafter, armed with a .45-caliber revolver, and approached Hardin from behind while Hardin faced the bar.[6] Without warning, Selman fired the first shot into the base of Hardin's skull, causing the bullet to exit through the upper left eye; Hardin collapsed immediately without turning or drawing his weapon.[6] [27] Eyewitness Frank Patterson reported that Hardin's back was to Selman and observed no motion from Hardin prior to falling, while H.S. Brown corroborated that Hardin faced the bar and dropped after the initial shot.[6] Selman then fired three additional shots: one reportedly missed, striking the floor, with the others hitting Hardin's right breast and right arm as he lay prone.[6] Selman later claimed self-defense, asserting Hardin had reached for his pistol, though eyewitness accounts indicated no such action by Hardin.[6] Hardin was pronounced dead at the scene from the head wound.[21]

Legal Proceedings and Acquittal

Following the fatal shooting of John Wesley Hardin on August 19, 1895, at the Acme Saloon in El Paso, Texas, John Selman was promptly arrested and charged with murder by local authorities.[1] Selman, acting in his capacity as a deputy city marshal, maintained that the killing constituted justifiable homicide in self-defense, asserting that Hardin had reached for a pistol in his waistband upon noticing Selman's approach.[26] Eyewitness accounts varied, with some saloon patrons testifying that Hardin appeared unarmed or posed no immediate threat, while others supported Selman's claim of an imminent draw by the notorious outlaw.[6] Selman's trial commenced in late 1895 before an El Paso jury, where defense arguments emphasized his official duties to disarm Hardin—stemming from an earlier arrest warrant related to Hardin's involvement in a separate shooting—and portrayed the encounter as a necessary preventive action against a man with a documented history of over 20 killings.[28] Prosecutors contended that Selman, aged 56 and positioned outside Hardin's field of vision, fired without warning from behind, firing five shots including three into Hardin's prone body, which suggested premeditation rather than defense. The proceedings highlighted conflicting forensic details, such as the position of Hardin's holstered Colt .41 revolver, found intact but loaded, and debates over whether Selman's actions aligned with peace officer protocols amid El Paso's volatile border-town environment.[6] The jury deliberated extensively but ultimately failed to reach a unanimous verdict, resulting in a hung jury and the trial's declaration as mistried.[1] Selman was released on bond pending a scheduled retrial, effectively restoring his freedom without a conviction.[26] No second trial occurred, as Selman was fatally wounded in a gunfight with U.S. Deputy Marshal George Scarborough on April 5, 1896, less than eight months later.[28] This outcome, while not a formal acquittal, precluded any legal penalty for the Hardin killing, reflecting the era's leniency toward lawmen confronting high-profile fugitives in self-proclaimed defense.[1]

Debates on Justification and Motive

Selman's claim of self-defense hinged on his testimony that, upon entering the Acme Saloon around 11 p.m. on August 19, 1895, he observed Hardin spotting his reflection in the bar mirror, after which Hardin pivoted and reached toward his holstered pistol, prompting Selman to fire first in preemptive defense.[27] Selman recounted firing an initial shot to Hardin's head from behind at close range—approximately five feet—followed by three more rounds as Hardin spun around, striking his body and hand; autopsy details confirmed entry wounds consistent with Hardin facing partially away initially, though the head wound's trajectory fueled skepticism about an imminent draw.[6] At the subsequent murder trial in October 1895, presided over by Judge Jeremiah A. Tolbert, Selman's account garnered sympathy from witnesses and jurors familiar with Hardin's reputation for violence, including over 30 attributed killings, leading to a hung jury unable to convict; Selman was released on bond, and no retrial occurred before his own death in April 1896.[26] Contemporary accounts from El Paso residents, as reported in local press, largely endorsed the killing as prudent against a notorious outlaw, with estimates suggesting three-quarters of polled individuals viewed it as "the proper thing" given Hardin's history of ambushes and threats.[29] Critics of the self-defense narrative, including some later historical analyses, argue the shooting resembled an assassination, pointing to the unarmed status of Hardin's visible pistol at the moment of the fatal head shot and Selman's approach from behind without verbal warning, which aligned with patterns in Selman's prior gunfights where he acted as both aggressor and beneficiary.[4] The preceding altercation—stemming from Selman's son, Deputy Constable John Selman Jr., arresting Hardin's associate and alleged prostitute Martin Mroz on vagrancy charges on August 18, followed by Hardin pistol-whipping the younger Selman and exchanging heated words with the elder—provided a clear personal animus, suggesting premeditation over imminent peril.[11] Hardin biographer Leon C. Metz, drawing on archival records, posits that while self-defense testimony may have held legal weight in the frontier context of lax evidentiary standards, the multi-shot execution-style killing indicated vengeful intent rather than pure reaction, especially as Hardin posed no active law enforcement warrant at the time beyond general repute.[28] Alternative motives debated among historians include financial grievance: records indicate Hardin had reportedly enlisted Selman earlier in 1895 to assassinate Clinton M. Browne, a former associate of Mroz whose death Hardin sought amid romantic and business rivalries, but failed to remunerate Selman for the hit, which occurred on July 9; this unpaid debt, detailed in Texas State Historical Association entries, could explain Selman's pursuit as retribution akin to a bounty collection, blurring lines between lawman duty and hired-gun economics.[21] Such interpretations underscore systemic credibility issues in Old West testimonies, where figures like Selman—himself implicated in at least 15-20 killings as outlaw and enforcer—often invoked self-defense to sanitize personal scores, as evidenced by his unprosecuted 1894 slaying of Bass Outlaw.[6] Proponents of justification counter that Hardin's documented threats post-arrest, including vows to "get" the Selmans, rendered any encounter lethally risky in El Paso's volatile saloon culture, where preemptive action was normalized against reformed-yet-reckless gunmen; this view, echoed in trial-era journalism, prioritizes causal realism of mutual armed hostility over idealized due process.[30] Ultimate assessments vary, with no consensus emerging beyond the acquittal-equivalent mistrial outcome, reflecting the era's fusion of personal vendetta and rough justice.

Final Confrontation and Death

Shootout with George Scarborough

On the early morning of April 5, 1896, Constable John Selman, who had been drinking heavily, encountered U.S. Deputy Marshal George A. Scarborough outside the Wigwam Saloon in El Paso, Texas.[5] [31] The two men, both armed and familiar from prior law enforcement circles, exchanged words inside the saloon before Selman suggested they step outside to continue their discussion, leading them into a nearby alley.[11] Tensions had simmered between them due to Selman's earlier fatal shooting of Bass Outlaw, a former Texas Ranger and close associate of Scarborough, in 1894.[32] Accounts of the ensuing confrontation vary, with period newspapers reporting conflicting details on who initiated the violence. According to Scarborough's testimony, Selman became aggressive, remarking "I believe I will kill you" while reaching for his holstered pistol, at which point Scarborough drew his revolver and fired first in self-defense.[11] Scarborough discharged four shots from his .45-caliber Colt Single Action Army, hitting Selman twice in the back, once in the abdomen, and once in the left arm; Selman returned fire with a single shot that grazed Scarborough's hand but caused no serious injury.[2] [11] The exchange occurred rapidly in the dim alley, with Selman collapsing from his wounds shortly after.[31] Selman was transported to a local physician for treatment but died from his injuries the following afternoon, April 6, 1896, at approximately 2:00 p.m.[5] [2] The shooting highlighted the volatile personal and professional rivalries among El Paso's lawmen, where old grudges often escalated into lethal gunplay amid the border town's rough environment.[5]

Circumstances and Outcome

On the evening of April 5, 1896, in El Paso, Texas, John Selman exited the Wigwam Saloon accompanied by his son, John Selman Jr., when he encountered U.S. Deputy Marshal George Scarborough outside. The two men, who had maintained a bitter feud stemming from prior disputes including Scarborough's dissatisfaction with Selman's killing of John Wesley Hardin, exchanged words that rapidly escalated into a confrontation. Selman drew his revolver first, firing a shot that missed Scarborough, who responded by discharging four rounds from his pistol, striking Selman in the abdomen, leg, and other areas.[1][2] Selman collapsed from his wounds and was rushed to a local hospital for emergency surgery, but he died the next day, April 6, 1896, on the operating table due to blood loss and complications. Scarborough immediately asserted self-defense, claiming Selman had initiated the deadly exchange by reaching for his weapon unprovoked.[1][5] In the ensuing trial, Scarborough's account was accepted by the jury, leading to his acquittal on murder charges in May 1896; no evidence contradicted his version sufficiently to warrant conviction, though witnesses noted the rapid sequence of events. The shooting's aftermath included Scarborough's forced resignation as deputy U.S. marshal amid political pressure from local authorities wary of escalating vigilante-style violence in El Paso. Selman's death marked the end of a turbulent career that blurred lines between law enforcement and outlawry, with contemporaries attributing the feud partly to Scarborough's resentment over Selman's opportunistic reputation.[1][2]

Burial and Immediate Aftermath

Funeral Arrangements

Selman succumbed to his gunshot wounds on the operating table on April 6, 1896, following the confrontation with George Scarborough.[1] He was interred in Concordia Cemetery in El Paso, Texas, in the Catholic section, though no records indicate he was a practicing Roman Catholic.[1] The grave was initially unmarked, with a cenotaph erected later in the Protestant section of the same cemetery.[33] No contemporary accounts detail a formal funeral service, pallbearers, or public attendance, reflecting the contentious nature of Selman's reputation in El Paso at the time of his death.[1]

Family Response and Settlements

Selman was survived by his second wife, Romula Granadine, married in 1893, and four children from his prior marriage to Edna DeGraffenreid, who had died in 1879 while giving birth to a stillborn child.[1] Historical records contain no accounts of public statements or actions by Selman's family in response to his death, reflecting the contentious reputation he held in El Paso as both lawman and gunfighter.[1] No civil lawsuits or settlements were filed by the family against George Scarborough, who was criminally acquitted of murder in May 1896 after a trial that highlighted the mutual antagonism between the two men.[1] [34] Selman's estate details remain undocumented in primary sources, with his burial occurring in the Catholic section of Concordia Cemetery in El Paso, where the grave site is unmarked and its precise location unverified despite later searches.[1]

Historical Legacy and Assessment

Estimated Number of Victims

Estimates of the number of individuals killed by John Selman range from a low of 12 to as many as 30, though contemporary newspaper reports more conservatively attribute around 20 to 23 deaths to him, many classified as justifiable in his capacity as a lawman.[11][6] The Richmond Item on April 6, 1896, specified 20 killings in Texas alone, while The Saint Paul Globe on October 28, 1900, reported 23 justifiable killings over his career.[6] These figures encompass gunfights during his outlaw activities with the Selman Scouts in Lincoln County, New Mexico, in 1878—where his group was accused of murdering unnamed settlers—and later law enforcement actions in El Paso, Texas.[2] Documented killings are fewer and include Texas Ranger Bass Outlaw on April 5, 1894, during a brothel confrontation ruled self-defense after Outlaw fatally shot another ranger and fired at Selman; and outlaw John Wesley Hardin on August 19, 1895, shot three times in the Acme Saloon amid a dispute over Hardin's associate.[2][1] Earlier alleged victims from Selman's ranching and rustling days in Texas and New Mexico lack precise records, contributing to the discrepancy between claimed and verified totals, with some accounts inflating numbers to enhance his gunfighter reputation.[14] Historians note that Selman's dual role as outlaw and deputy often blurred lines between personal vendettas and official duties, complicating attribution.[2] No comprehensive list of all victims exists, and higher estimates exceeding 30 appear unsubstantiated by primary sources.

Scholarly Interpretations of Character

Scholars portray John Selman as a multifaceted individual whose character embodied the ambiguities of frontier justice, oscillating between familial loyalty, military discipline, and opportunistic violence. Leon C. Metz, in his biographical study, emphasizes Selman's role as the eldest son who prioritized his mother's and siblings' welfare after the Civil War's economic ruin, enlisting in the Confederate Army where he exhibited leadership in cavalry units before turning to cattle rustling to sustain his family.[35] Metz further characterizes him as enigmatic, capable of calculated restraint yet prone to lethal impulsivity in disputes, attributing over twenty documented killings to a personality shaped by survival imperatives rather than ideological malice.[36] The Texas State Historical Association highlights Selman's fluidity across roles—vigilante leader in the 1870s Mason County conflicts, deputy sheriff, and constable—interpreting his actions as pragmatic adaptations to the Southwest's lawless vacuum, where personal vendettas often masqueraded as public duty.[1] Historians like those in True West Magazine describe him as inherently flawed, with motives rooted in post-war destitution and a cultural tolerance for gunplay, as evidenced by his 1895 killing of John Wesley Hardin amid escalating threats over Hardin's legal representation of a murder suspect tied to Selman's son.[9] This interpretation underscores a causal realism: Selman's violence was not random but triggered by perceived threats to status or kin, reflecting broader patterns among Old West gunmen who rationalized killings as defensive necessities in an era of weak institutions.[1]

Influence on Old West Narratives and Media Depictions

John Selman's killing of John Wesley Hardin on August 19, 1895, at the Acme Saloon in El Paso, Texas, has cemented his role in Old West lore primarily as the instrument of the demise of one of the era's most notorious gunfighters, thereby contributing to narratives of frontier justice and the transition from lawlessness to order.[27] [4] This event, where Selman fired multiple shots into Hardin—striking him in the back of the head while Hardin was unarmed and rolling dice—exemplifies the moral ambiguity often embedded in gunfighter tales, challenging romanticized depictions of fair duels and heroic lawmen.[27] [11] Historical accounts portray Selman not as an unalloyed hero but as a flawed enforcer whose actions blurred the line between official duty and personal vendetta, influencing interpretations that emphasize causal factors like local feuds and self-preservation over idealized retribution.[2] Biographical works, such as Leon C. Metz's John Selman, Gunfighter (1997), have shaped scholarly and popular understandings by detailing Selman's career as both outlaw and deputy, framing his Hardin slaying as emblematic of the Old West's closing chapter, where surviving gunmen like Selman outlasted peers only to meet violent ends themselves.[35] Metz's analysis, drawing on primary records, underscores Selman's estimated involvement in up to 20 killings, positioning him as a symbol of the era's unchecked violence rather than a mythic avenger.[35] This perspective has informed subsequent histories, including articles in True West Magazine, which highlight Selman's El Paso exploits to illustrate the gritty realism behind frontier myths, countering sanitized media portrayals of unambiguous good triumphing over evil.[17] In visual media, Selman appears sporadically in documentaries rather than mainstream fiction, often as a secondary figure in Hardin-focused narratives that explore gunfighter demises. Productions like those chronicled in compilations of Old West gunfighter stories depict him as a controversial constable whose ambush-style killing provoked debates on legality versus expediency, reinforcing themes of vigilante ethics in the waning days of the frontier.[37] [38] His own death six months later, on April 6, 1896, in a shootout with George Scarborough, further symbolizes the exhaustion of the gunman archetype, as noted in accounts marking it as the effective end of the Old West's active shootist period.[35] Unlike more fictionalized figures, Selman's media footprint remains tied to empirical events, serving to ground exaggerated Western tropes in documented brutality and contingency.[37]
