Maximal ideal

In mathematics, more specifically in ring theory, a maximal ideal is an ideal that is maximal (with respect to set inclusion) amongst all proper ideals.[1][2] In other words, I is a maximal ideal of a ring R if there are no other ideals contained between I and R. Maximal ideals are important because the quotients of rings by maximal ideals are simple rings, and in the special case of unital commutative rings they are also fields. The set of maximal ideals of a unital commutative ring R , typically equipped with the Zariski topology, is known as the maximal spectrum of R and is variously denoted m-Spec R , Specm R , MaxSpec R , or Spm R. In noncommutative ring theory, a maximal right ideal is defined analogously as being a maximal element in the poset of proper right ideals, and similarly, a maximal left ideal is defined to be a maximal element of the poset of proper left ideals. Since a one-sided maximal ideal A is not necessarily two-sided, the quotient R /A is not necessarily a ring, but it is a simple module over R. If R has a unique maximal right ideal, then R is known as a local ring, and the maximal right ideal is also the unique maximal left and unique maximal two-sided ideal of the ring, and is in fact the Jacobson radical J(R). It is possible for a ring to have a unique maximal two-sided ideal and yet lack unique maximal one-sided ideals: for example, in the ring of 2 by 2 square matrices over a field, the zero ideal is a maximal two-sided ideal, but there are many maximal right ideals.

Properties

  • An important ideal of the ring called the Jacobson radical can be defined using maximal right (or maximal left) ideals.
  • If RRR is a unital commutative ring with an ideal mmm, then k=R/mk = R / mk=R/m is a field if and only if mmm is a maximal ideal. In that case, R/mR / mR/m is known as the residue field. This fact can fail in non-unital rings. For example, 4Z4\mathbb{Z}4Z is a maximal ideal in 2Z2\mathbb{Z}2Z, but 2Z/4Z2\mathbb{Z} / 4\mathbb{Z}2Z/4Z is not a field.
  • If LLL is a maximal left ideal, then R/LR / LR/L is a simple left RRR-module. Conversely in rings with unity, any simple left RRR-module arises this way. Incidentally this shows that a collection of representatives of simple left RRR-modules is actually a set since it can be put into correspondence with part of the set of maximal left ideals of RRR.
  • Krull's theorem (1929): Every nonzero unital ring has a maximal ideal. The result is also true if "ideal" is replaced with "right ideal" or "left ideal". More generally, it is true that every nonzero finitely generated module has a maximal submodule. Suppose III is an ideal which is not RRR (respectively, AAA is a right ideal which is not RRR). Then R/IR / IR/I is a ring with unity (respectively, R/AR / AR/A is a finitely generated module), and so the above theorems can be applied to the quotient to conclude that there is a maximal ideal (respectively, maximal right ideal) of RRR containing III (respectively, AAA).
  • Krull's theorem can fail for rings without unity. A radical ring, i.e. a ring in which the Jacobson radical is the entire ring, has no simple modules and hence has no maximal right or left ideals. See regular ideals for possible ways to circumvent this problem.
  • In a commutative ring with unity, every maximal ideal is a prime ideal. The converse is not always true: for example, in any nonfield integral domain the zero ideal is a prime ideal which is not maximal. Commutative rings in which prime ideals are maximal are known as zero-dimensional rings, where the dimension used is the Krull dimension.
  • If kkk is a field, the preimage of a maximal ideal of a finitely generated kkk-algebra under a kkk-algebra homomorphism is a maximal ideal. However, the preimage of a maximal ideal of a unital commutative ring under a ring homomorphism is not necessarily maximal. Generalization

For an RRR-module AAA, a maximal submodule MMM of AAA is a submodule Mâ‰ AM \neq AMî€ =A satisfying the property that for any other submodule NNN, MâŠ†NâŠ†AM \subseteq N \subseteq AMâŠ†NâŠ†A implies N=MN = MN=M or N=AN = AN=A. Equivalently, MMM is a maximal submodule if and only if the quotient module A/MA / MA/M is a simple module. The maximal right ideals of a ring RRR are exactly the maximal submodules of the module RR_R RRâ€‹R. Unlike rings with unity, a nonzero module does not necessarily have maximal submodules. However, as noted above, finitely generated nonzero modules have maximal submodules, and also projective modules have maximal submodules. As with rings, one can define the radical of a module using maximal submodules. Furthermore, maximal ideals can be generalized by defining a maximal sub-bimodule MMM of a bimodule BBB to be a proper sub-bimodule of BBB which is contained in no other proper sub-bimodule of BBB. The maximal ideals of RRR are then exactly the maximal sub-bimodules of the bimodule RRR_R R _RRâ€‹RRâ€‹.
