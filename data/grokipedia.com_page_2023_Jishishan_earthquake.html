2023 Jishishan earthquake

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake was a magnitude MS 6.2 seismic event that occurred at 23:59 local time on December 18, 2023, in Jishishan County, Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture, Gansu Province, northwestern China, with an epicenter at 35.70°N, 102.79°E and a focal depth of 10 km.[1] The shallow thrust earthquake ruptured along a blind fault at the northeastern margin of the Tibetan Plateau, marking the strongest seismic activity in mainland China that year.[1] The event caused 148 fatalities and injured over 700 people, primarily due to widespread collapses of substandard rural masonry homes built with earthen materials in the loess-dominated region, exacerbated by the late-night occurrence and sub-zero winter temperatures that hindered rescue efforts.[1] It damaged or destroyed more than 155,000 residences across Gansu and neighboring Qinghai provinces, displacing tens of thousands and generating economic losses estimated in billions of yuan from structural failures, landslides, and infrastructure disruptions.[2] Aftershocks, including several above magnitude 5.0, prolonged the hazard, triggering secondary effects like rockfalls and river terrace failures that compounded the disaster in this tectonically active zone of compressive strain.[3] The earthquake highlighted vulnerabilities in regional seismic resilience, with empirical analyses attributing disproportionate impacts to localized site amplification on soft sediments and inadequate enforcement of building standards in impoverished Hui communities.[4]

Geological and tectonic setting

Regional tectonics and fault systems

The northeastern margin of the Tibetan Plateau, where the 2023 Jishishan earthquake occurred, represents a zone of intense continental deformation resulting from the Cenozoic collision between the Indian and Eurasian plates, which has driven the plateau's northeastward expansion at rates of approximately 5–10 mm/year. This expansion is partitioned across a network of active fault systems, including north-verging thrust belts such as the Qilian Shan fold-thrust system and left-lateral strike-slip faults like the Haiyuan fault, which accommodate both shortening and lateral escape of the plateau's interior. The regional stress field features predominantly WSW-ENE-directed compression, superimposed on right-lateral shear, leading to oblique convergence and the development of restraining bends and thrust ramps.[5][6] The Lajishan fault zone (LJSF), a key structure in this setting, trends NE-SW and bounds the Laji Shan range, separating the plateau's interior from the stable Sichuan Basin to the east. Comprising the northern Lajishan fault (a south-dipping reverse fault) and the southern Lajishan fault (north-dipping), the system exhibits Quaternary activity with slip rates estimated at 1–2 mm/year, primarily through thrust motion that elevates the Laji Shan topography to over 3,500 meters. Subsidiary blind thrusts and back-thrusts within the zone, often at depths of 10–15 km, facilitate intra-plate shortening under the prevailing compressional regime.[7][8] Adjacent systems, such as the Xiangshan-Tianjingshan fault to the north (left-lateral strike-slip with slip rates ~1–3 mm/year) and the Liupanshan thrust to the east, interact with the LJSF to distribute strain, forming a diffuse boundary between the expanding plateau and the rigid Ordos block. Historical seismicity, including the 1920 Mw 8.0 Haiyuan earthquake on the nearby strike-slip fault, underscores the region's potential for segmented ruptures, though the LJSF's lower slip rates suggest longer recurrence intervals for major events. The Jishishan earthquake activated a blind NNE-dipping thrust segment within this framework, consistent with focal mechanisms indicating reverse faulting at 13 km depth.[9][10][11]

Historical seismicity and precursors

The Jishishan region, situated at the northeastern margin of the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau, exhibits pronounced historical seismicity driven by ongoing crustal shortening and northeast-directed extrusion. More than 20 destructive earthquakes of magnitude 5.0 or higher have occurred in the area, primarily associated with thrust faulting along the Lajishan fault system and adjacent structures such as the Haiyuan and West Qinling faults.[12] Within a 300 km radius of the Lajishan fault zone, historical records document 11 earthquakes with magnitudes Ms ≥ 6.0 over the past century, underscoring the system's persistent activity.[13] Notable events include the 1920 Haiyuan earthquake (Ms 8.5), which ruptured the Haiyuan fault and resulted in approximately 230,000 deaths, and the 1927 Gulang earthquake (Ms 8.0) to the north.[7] [14] Paleoseismic investigations reveal recurrent large-magnitude events, such as those around 3700 years before present that deformed fluvial terraces and induced flooding along the Yellow River tributaries.[7] The Lajishan fault zone comprises multiple parallel thrust segments, including the northern and southern Laji Shan faults, with evidence of repeated slip in the Holocene.[15] These faults accommodate oblique compression at the plateau's margin, contributing to a cluster of moderate-to-large earthquakes that reflect variable rupture behaviors and incomplete strain release on individual segments.[7] No significant foreshocks immediately preceded the 2023 mainshock, though aftershock sequences rapidly followed, exceeding 500 events by late December.[16] Geophysical observations reported anomalous variations in apparent resistivity and regional gravity fields in the preceding months, interpreted by some researchers as potential precursors linked to stress accumulation and fluid migration, but without established causal verification.[17] [18] Pre-seismic ionospheric total electron content perturbations were also noted days prior, possibly coupled to acoustic-gravity waves from subsurface processes, though such signals remain debated as reliable indicators due to atmospheric influences.[19]

The earthquake event

Timing, location, and magnitude

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake struck on December 18, 2023, at 23:59:30 China Standard Time (CST), with its epicenter in Liugou Township, Jishishan County, Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture, Gansu Province, northwestern China, near the provincial border with Qinghai.[20][21] The event originated at coordinates approximately 35.70°N, 102.79°E, about 8 km northwest of Jishishan County's administrative center and 145 km southeast of Lanzhou, the provincial capital.[1][22] The hypocenter lay at a shallow depth of 10 km beneath the northeastern margin of the Tibetan Plateau, within a tectonically active region of compressional deformation.[1][22] The China Earthquake Networks Center (CENC) determined a surface-wave magnitude (Ms) of 6.2 based on regional seismic data, consistent with official Chinese assessments emphasizing local wave propagation effects.[20][21] In contrast, the United States Geological Survey (USGS) reported a moment magnitude (Mw) of 5.9, derived from global teleseismic records and inversion of long-period body and surface waves, reflecting differences in measurement methodologies where Ms may amplify for shallow events in certain crustal settings.[23] Independent analyses, including finite-fault inversions, have yielded Mw estimates ranging from 6.0 to 6.1, aligning more closely with CENC's scale when accounting for rupture directivity and slip distribution.[16][7] These variations underscore the challenges in standardizing magnitudes across datasets, with Ms often higher for reverse-faulting mechanisms like this one due to enhanced surface-wave excitation.[24]

Source mechanism and rupture dynamics

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake ruptured a blind thrust fault characterized by a northeast-dipping plane, consistent with regional compressional tectonics along the northeastern Tibetan Plateau margin.[24] Focal mechanism solutions from multiple inversions confirm a thrust-type event, with representative parameters including a strike of 150°, dip of 28°, and rake of 120°, aligning with the China Earthquake Networks Center's assessment of thrust faulting.[24] [1] The seismogenic structure is an immature basin thrust, potentially activating conjugate southwest-dipping faults, as indicated by aftershock distributions and dynamic modeling.[16] Rupture propagation was predominantly unilateral, directed northward or NNW from the hypocenter, with a relatively slow velocity and low radiation efficiency, suggestive of dynamic processes involving immature fault segments rather than mature shear zones.[16] The total rupture duration was approximately 12 seconds, with peak slip of 0.12–0.47 m occurring at depths of 8–9.6 km, and slip concentrated between 5.7 and 15 km depth without reaching the surface in primary models.[1] [24] Geodetic inversions using Sentinel-1A and ALOS-2 interferometry reveal a seismic moment of 1.69 × 10¹⁸ N·m (equivalent to Mw 6.1), with the main asperity rupture exhibiting asymmetric characteristics.[24] Field observations and high-resolution analyses identified minor coseismic surface deformation, including three oblique-thrust rupture segments totaling ~8.2 km in length (individual lengths of ~4.5 km, 2.0 km, and 1.7 km), with maximum vertical displacements of ~6 cm and horizontal components of ~5 cm, interpreted as antithetic thrusting at the orogenic front.[8] These features, comprising centimeter-scale uplift and bulging over a ~1.2 km zone, contrast with the primarily buried rupture but highlight multi-segment dynamics in this moderate-magnitude event (Mw < 6.5).[8] [16] Dynamic simulations attribute the slow propagation and low efficiency to the fault's geometric immaturity and stress heterogeneity, rather than high friction or fluid effects.[16]

Ground shaking intensity and amplification effects

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake generated intense ground shaking near the epicenter, with the China Earthquake Administration (CEA) reporting a maximum seismic intensity of IX on the China seismic intensity scale in Jishishan County, corresponding to violent shaking that caused near-total destruction of unreinforced masonry structures and significant damage to engineered buildings.[1] Intensities reached VIII across broader areas in Gansu and adjacent Qinghai provinces, with isoseismals elongated in an NNW direction, reflecting the fault strike and rupture propagation.[25] This distribution aligned with field surveys confirming heavy damage patterns consistent with high-intensity zones.[1] Strong-motion recordings captured peak ground accelerations (PGA) exceeding 1.1 g in the near-source region, among the highest for events of comparable magnitude (Ms 6.2), attributable to shallow focal depth (approximately 10 km) and directivity effects from the rupture.[26] Peak ground velocities (PGV) and spectral accelerations further indicated outstanding ground motion levels relative to empirical ground-motion prediction equations (GMPEs) for active tectonic regions.[27] Local site amplification significantly exacerbated shaking due to the region's loess-dominated geology on the Loess Plateau, where thick, unsaturated silty soils (up to tens of meters) resonated with low-frequency seismic waves, producing amplification factors 1.5–2 times higher on soil sites than on bedrock.[28] Nonlinear soil response was evident in records from the event sequence, with velocity-dependent damping reducing high-frequency content but enhancing durations on soft deposits, contributing to observed structural collapses in valleys and sediment-filled basins.[29] Sedimentary layering further amplified motions in the epicentral basin, as inferred from discrepancies between observed PGAs and rock-site predictions, underscoring causal roles of velocity contrasts and impedance mismatches in wave propagation.[26]

Immediate impacts

Casualties and injuries

The earthquake resulted in 148 fatalities, primarily due to the collapse of poorly constructed homes in rural areas during the late-night main shock.[30][1] Most deaths occurred in Jishishan County, Gansu Province, with additional fatalities in neighboring areas of Qinghai Province; official tallies confirmed 126 deaths in Gansu and 22 in Qinghai by late December 2023.[31] Three individuals were reported missing.[28] Injuries exceeded 900, with 968 reported in affected regions as of December 20, 2023, though subsequent medical assessments indicated higher totals approaching 1,000 when accounting for treated cases.[32] Among 257 hospitalized patients analyzed in a clinical study, 802 distinct injuries were documented, dominated by fractures (46.6%) from structural collapses, followed by visceral injuries (20.3%) and soft tissue damage; nearly all (96.5%) arose directly from the initial rupture rather than aftershocks.[33] Sub-zero temperatures compounded risks by hindering rescue efforts and increasing hypothermia among the injured and trapped.[32] No detailed public breakdowns of fatalities by age or demographics were released, though the event's timing and housing vulnerabilities suggest disproportionate impact on sleeping residents in substandard masonry structures prevalent in the impoverished Hui Muslim-majority region.[34]

Structural and infrastructural damage

The earthquake inflicted severe structural damage primarily on residential buildings in rural areas of Gansu Province, where older, non-seismically designed constructions predominated. Official provincial reports documented nearly 15,000 homes collapsing completely, with more than 207,000 additional residences wrecked or severely damaged, displacing over 145,000 people.[32] In the hardest-hit Jishishan County, approximately 5,000 homes sustained damage, many of which were traditional brick-concrete or earthen structures vulnerable to shear failure and foundation instability under intense shaking.[35][36] Public facilities experienced comparable vulnerabilities, with school buildings showing patterns of partial collapse, wall cracking, and roof failure during post-event field surveys, attributed to inadequate reinforcement in beam-column joints and infill walls.[37] Hospital structures similarly exhibited damage mechanisms such as facade detachment and equipment displacement, highlighting seismic shortcomings in critical infrastructure despite post-2008 building code improvements.[38] Infrastructural networks suffered widespread disruptions, including outages in electricity, water supply, telecommunications, and transportation systems, particularly in mountainous villages near the epicenter.[39][40] Dozens of highways and rural roads were blocked or damaged by ground deformation and debris, complicating access for rescue operations, while pipe networks and irrigation canals incurred breaks leading to secondary flooding risks in affected lowlands.[2][41] No major bridge failures were reported, though minor cracks appeared in overpasses due to differential settlement.[30]

Secondary geological hazards

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake induced widespread secondary geological hazards, predominantly landslides and mudflows in the loess-covered terrain of the Loess Plateau. Coseismic landslides numbered approximately 9,340, with distributions concentrated along ridge crests and gully slopes where loess deposits amplified ground motion. [42] These failures were exacerbated by the region's thick, unconsolidated loess layers, which underwent liquefaction under seismic shaking, leading to flow-like deformations. [43] A notable example was the Zhongchuan Town mudflow, a 3-km-long landslide-mudflow on the tertiary terrace of the Yellow River, triggered by loess liquefaction and incorporating river sediments. [44] This event formed expansive complexes near Hongtuwa, where coalescing landslides posed additional risks through debris blocking waterways and amplifying downstream flooding potential. [45] Rockfalls also occurred, particularly in steeper bedrock exposures, contributing to localized infrastructure disruptions. [45] Postseismic effects included acceleration of 372 pre-existing landslides, driven by aftershocks and rainfall infiltration into weakened slopes. [42] Loess-amplified subsidence and novel "ripple hazards"—liquefaction-induced surface waves—further highlighted the terrain's vulnerability, with failures persisting into 2024 due to ongoing tectonic stress and seasonal precipitation. [41] Overall inventories identified up to 2,643 distinct landslides via remote sensing, underscoring the earthquake's outsized geohazard footprint relative to its moderate magnitude. [46]

Societal and economic consequences

Demographic and regional vulnerabilities

The Jishishan County epicenter lay within the Linxia Hui Autonomous Prefecture, a rural, high-altitude region on the northeastern Tibetan Plateau margin characterized by loess tablelands prone to seismic amplification, landslides, and subsidence due to the friable soil structure and steep topography.[45] These geological features, combined with sparse modern infrastructure and remote access routes, heightened vulnerability to ground shaking and secondary hazards, as evidenced by widespread collapses in earthen structures during the shallow Ms 6.2 event.[3] The late-December timing amplified risks, with subzero temperatures and snowfall post-event exposing displaced residents to hypothermia amid limited immediate shelter options in the mountainous terrain.[2] Demographically, the affected area comprised predominantly Hui ethnic minority communities, a Muslim population exceeding 90% in Jishishan County, residing in clustered villages with traditional agrarian lifestyles and lower urbanization rates compared to eastern China.[47] Poverty pervaded the region, with Gansu Province ranking among China's poorest; Jishishan alone harbored 18,195 impoverished households and over 83,000 individuals in poverty as of recent pre-event assessments, constraining household-level seismic retrofitting and reliance on non-engineered adobe or brick masonry homes susceptible to brittle failure under lateral forces.[48] [36] This socioeconomic profile, marked by limited access to education on disaster preparedness and dependence on subsistence farming, correlated with elevated casualty rates in densely settled rural pockets, where nighttime occurrence caught residents indoors during peak vulnerability hours.[49] Regional disparities in building codes enforcement further underscored vulnerabilities, as rural Gansu lagged in adopting reinforced concrete standards prevalent in urban centers, leading to disproportionate structural failures; post-event surveys documented over 46% of injuries as fractures from collapsing walls in such dwellings.[33] The Hui-majority demographics, while fostering community cohesion for informal aid networks, also reflected systemic underdevelopment in ethnic autonomous areas, where historical marginalization limited fiscal resources for hazard mitigation despite national poverty alleviation campaigns.[48] Overall, these intertwined factors—geomorphic instability, entrenched rural poverty, and demographic concentration in fragile housing—drove the earthquake's outsized human toll relative to its moderate magnitude.[36]

Direct economic losses and recovery estimates

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake caused direct economic losses estimated at CN¥14.612 billion (approximately US$2.05 billion), encompassing widespread destruction of residential structures, infrastructure, and agricultural assets in Gansu and Qinghai provinces.[50][36] This figure, derived from official assessments by Chinese authorities and corroborated in post-event analyses, reflects the collapse of around 70,000 homes and severe damage to 99,000 others, alongside disruptions to roads, power lines, and water systems in loess-prone terrain that amplified structural failures.[27] Preliminary sectoral breakdowns highlighted CN¥532 million in losses to agriculture, forestry, animal husbandry, and fisheries, driven by damaged greenhouses, livestock facilities, and irrigation networks in the epicentral counties of Jishishan and Dangchang.[51] Recovery estimates remain preliminary and government-directed, with total reconstruction costs projected to exceed initial direct losses due to the need for seismic-resistant rebuilding in a seismically active region. Individual household rebuilding expenses averaged CN¥200,000 (about US$27,800), offset by subsidies of CN¥75,000 per affected family and low-interest loans up to CN¥50,000, as part of a broader national allocation for permanent resettlement and infrastructure upgrades.[52] Central government funding prioritized "unified planning and construction" of over 100,000 new homes by mid-2024, incorporating anti-seismic standards, though independent evaluations note potential underestimation of long-term costs from secondary hazards like landslides that affected farmland productivity.[53] No comprehensive third-party recovery tally has been published, but state reports emphasize phased funding from emergency reserves, with agricultural restoration targeting full output recovery by 2025 through subsidized replanting and facility repairs.[54]

Chinese government response

Emergency mobilization and rescue operations

Chinese authorities activated emergency protocols immediately following the 6.2-magnitude earthquake that struck Jishishan County, Gansu Province, at 23:59 CST on December 18, 2023. The China Earthquake Administration initiated a level-IV emergency response and dispatched a guidance team to the disaster zone to evaluate impacts and direct relief activities.[55] Concurrently, the national emergency management system mobilized multidisciplinary response units, prioritizing search and rescue in the mountainous, loess-prone terrain where cave dwellings and rural homes collapsed en masse.[56] Rescue forces scaled rapidly, with over 4,500 personnel from provincial public security, fire departments, and military units deploying alongside more than 820 vehicles to the epicentral areas.[57] Firefighting teams contributed approximately 1,500 active rescuers, supplemented by 1,500 on standby, while police dispatched around 1,500 officers and the People's Liberation Army mobilized about 1,000 soldiers for extraction operations.[31][40] The People's Armed Police Force committed over 300 officers and 40 vehicles to Jishishan, focusing on debris clearance and survivor location, with a People's Liberation Army Y-20 aircraft airlifting 14 tons of equipment, supplies, and additional responders to overcome logistical barriers posed by damaged roads.[58][59] Operations unfolded amid sub-zero temperatures and adverse conditions, including eight-foot-deep frozen sludge blocking access to villages and mudslide-obstructed paths that necessitated manual plowing and improvised bridging.[30] Rescuers employed dogs, heavy machinery, and manual digging to probe collapsed structures, rescuing 78 individuals alive in Gansu Province despite the rapid onset of hypothermia risks.[32] Search efforts in the hardest-hit zones concluded by 15:00 on December 20, roughly 39 hours after the initial rupture, transitioning priorities to medical triage and sheltering as no further viable survivors were anticipated under the circumstances.[60][61] The Red Cross Society of China delivered initial relief items within 12 hours, augmenting state-led extractions with humanitarian support.[62]

Relief aid and temporary shelter provisions

Following the earthquake on December 18, 2023, Chinese authorities rapidly deployed relief supplies, including 135,500 items such as cotton tents, rollaway beds, quilts, and heaters, to Gansu and neighboring provinces to address immediate needs amid subzero temperatures.[63] The Ministry of Emergency Management coordinated distribution, with over 87,000 residents in Gansu relocated to temporary shelters by December 20, where food, water, and heating were provided.[64] In Jishishan County, hundreds of displaced villagers were moved to three designated temporary shelters by December 19, prioritizing vulnerable groups including the elderly and children.[60] Temporary housing construction began promptly, with hundreds of prefabricated units erected in affected areas to replace over 14,000 destroyed homes, focusing on wind- and cold-resistant designs suitable for the winter loess plateau conditions.[65] Government funding supported these efforts, including an initial 200 million yuan (approximately $28 million) allocation on December 19 for rescue and relief, supplemented by 400 million yuan on December 25 specifically for disaster mitigation and shelter enhancements.[66][67] International contributions included UNICEF's delivery of 44,500 winter garments for 30,000 children starting December 22, though domestic provisions dominated the response.[68] Survivors in open-air and tent-based shelters faced frigid overnight lows, prompting additional distributions of thermal blankets and portable heaters to prevent hypothermia, as reported in on-site assessments.[69] By late December, relief operations had stabilized basic provisions, with trade unions contributing 18 million yuan ($2.5 million) for targeted aid to affected workers and families.[70] These measures emphasized self-reliance in remote, ethnically diverse regions, minimizing external dependencies despite offers from abroad.[71]

Coordination challenges in remote terrain

The remote and mountainous terrain of Jishishan County in Gansu Province, one of China's poorest regions, severely complicated the coordination of emergency response efforts after the December 18, 2023, earthquake.[30][72] Isolated villages scattered across steep loess hills and valleys lacked robust road networks, delaying the deployment of over 10,000 rescuers mobilized from national and provincial levels.[32][73] Poor pre-existing infrastructure, including narrow paths prone to blockage, forced teams to rely on foot or limited heavy machinery, slowing the extraction of survivors from collapsed structures in hard-to-reach areas.[31] Sub-zero temperatures, dropping as low as -20°C in the immediate aftermath, compounded logistical hurdles by freezing equipment and limiting operational windows for search-and-rescue teams working through the night.[32][74] Snow accumulation and quake-induced road damage further impeded vehicle access to remote hamlets, necessitating manual clearing efforts and aerial assessments where feasible, though helicopter deployment was constrained by weather and visibility.[31][74] Coordination between central, provincial, and local units was strained by initial disruptions to 314 communication base stations, which were restored within 36 hours but initially fragmented command chains across the dispersed disaster zone.[57] These environmental and infrastructural barriers contributed to a compressed rescue timeline, with operations in Gansu concluding within approximately 15 hours despite the scale of casualties exceeding 140 deaths and nearly 1,000 injuries.[75] Relief aid distribution faced similar obstacles, as convoys transporting tents, food, and medical supplies struggled against blocked routes and the need for on-site triage in under-equipped rural facilities.[30][69] Overall, the interplay of remoteness, harsh winter conditions, and seismic damage underscored systemic vulnerabilities in rapid response for China's inland frontier regions, prompting post-event reviews on enhancing prepositioned resources and all-terrain capabilities.[32][73]

Reconstruction and long-term recovery

Government-led rebuilding initiatives

The Chinese central government prioritized permanent housing reconstruction in the earthquake-affected areas of Gansu Province, providing subsidies of up to 75,000 yuan (approximately 10,400 U.S. dollars) per household for rebuilding costs, supplemented by low-interest loans covering additional expenses such as 50,000 yuan per family.[52][53] These measures aimed to enable rapid relocation from temporary shelters, with total rebuilding costs for individual homes averaging around 200,000 yuan, offset through a combination of direct aid and financing to ensure seismic-resistant structures compliant with updated building standards.[52] By March 2025, reconstruction efforts in Jishishan County had resulted in over 110,000 households moving into newly built permanent residences, marking a significant milestone in restoring residential stability for the predominantly rural Hui Muslim population.[76] Complementary infrastructure initiatives included the repair and upgrading of roads, water supply systems, and power grids, with initial funding allocations exceeding 200 million yuan directed toward these sectors to mitigate vulnerabilities in the loess terrain.[67][77] Provincial and local authorities coordinated with central directives to integrate anti-seismic reinforcements in rebuilt schools and medical facilities, drawing on lessons from prior earthquakes to enhance resilience against future seismic events in the region.[78] Additional allocations, such as 30 million yuan for road restoration, supported connectivity in remote villages, though implementation faced delays due to winter conditions and the need for geological assessments.[79]

Ongoing challenges and scientific monitoring

As of late 2024, reconstruction efforts in the Jishishan region continued to address vulnerabilities in loess-dominated terrain, where seismic amplification and slope instability exacerbate long-term risks. Postseismic reactivation of landslides, triggered by residual stress and seasonal rainfall, has sustained hazards in areas like Jintian Village, with over 40 subsidence sites documented initially and potential for further movement due to the earthquake's disruption of loess structure.[80][43] These geological challenges complicate permanent housing and infrastructure rebuilding, as loess failures amplify damage beyond the initial Mw 6.2 event's direct impacts.[81] Scientific monitoring has focused on aftershock sequences and fault dynamics to assess recurrence potential along the involved thrust structures, likely associated with the northeastern Tibetan Plateau margin. By December 20, 2023, 423 aftershocks were recorded, with depths ranging 9.6–14.6 km and a concentration in a 12-km SSE-directed strip; subsequent analyses identified 16 events above magnitude 3.0 primarily within the first two months.[20][54] Relocated hypocenters and focal mechanisms reveal a northeast-dipping main fault at 8–14 km depth, with aftershocks clustering northwest of the epicenter, informing models of multi-segment ruptures and low-radiation efficiency.[82][8] Three-dimensional velocity structure imaging and full-waveform inversions have been employed to map crustal heterogeneities, revealing flow-induced triggering mechanisms that heightened the event's severity in the loess-covered Haiyuan-Gansu gravity zone.[83] Interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR) data from Sentinel-1 satellites continue to track coseismic slip distributions, with peak displacements up to several decimeters, aiding in probabilistic hazard assessments for adjacent faults like Tianjingshan.[24] These efforts, extending into 2025, underscore persistent monitoring needs given the region's tectonic stress accumulation and historical seismic gaps.[7][84] Challenges in integrating monitoring data with recovery include coordinating remote sensing for real-time hazard mapping amid sparse instrumentation in rural Gansu, where tectonic complexities like shallow ruptures demand updated models to mitigate future amplification in sedimentary basins.[12] Ongoing numerical simulations of energy fields confirm the earthquake's alignment with optimal shear planes under regional stress, highlighting the necessity for enhanced seismic networks to detect precursory signals in this high-relapse area.[85] Despite rapid initial restorations of utilities, full recovery remains protracted due to these interplaying geophysical risks, with studies emphasizing causal links between fault kinematics and loess-specific failures.[86][22]

Scientific debates and analyses

Seismogenic fault identification controversies

The identification of the seismogenic fault for the December 18, 2023, Ms 6.2 Jishishan earthquake has generated debate among seismologists, primarily concerning the fault's geometry, dip direction, and precise location within the regional fault system. Geological mapping associates the event with the Lajishan fault zone, a left-lateral strike-slip system with thrust components along its northern margin, where surface ruptures and historical activity suggest a west-dipping structure at depths of 10-15 km.[87] However, interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR) observations of coseismic surface deformation reveal an asymmetric uplift pattern favoring a northeast-dipping thrust fault, with maximum slip of approximately 0.5-1.0 m at shallow depths (3-10 km), challenging the west-dipping model derived from pre-event geological data.[88] [22] This discrepancy arises from the earthquake's occurrence in a tectonically complex region of the northeastern Tibetan Plateau, where the epicenter lies between the main Lajishan fault trace and adjacent structures, prompting proposals of a concealed blind fault or an unmapped splay branching from the northern Lajishan segment.[24] Aftershock relocations and focal mechanisms indicate clustered activity along a NNE-striking plane dipping 40-60° northeast, supporting the InSAR-inferred geometry over purely geological interpretations, though some analyses reconcile the data by invoking a listric fault shape that steepens with depth.[84] [7] Critics of the northeast-dipping model argue that it underestimates the role of inherited basement structures and historical seismicity along west-dipping planes, as evidenced by paleoseismic trenching on the Lajishan fault revealing recurrent thrust events.[89] Further contention involves the fault's relation to broader tectonic loading, with some studies linking the rupture to interseismic strain accumulation on the northern Lajishan fault (coupling coefficient >0.8 at 0-10 km depth) rather than a independent structure, while others highlight the absence of clear surface breakage—limited to minor cracks up to 10 cm—suggesting a buried rupture not aligned with mapped active traces.[90] [16] Resolution awaits integrated datasets, including deeper seismic tomography and long-term GPS monitoring, but the debate underscores limitations in pre-event fault mapping for blind thrusts in loess-covered terrains.[10][89]

Novel amplification mechanisms in loess terrain

The loess plateau surrounding the Jishishan epicenter, composed of thick, unconsolidated aeolian silt deposits with high porosity and low cohesion, facilitated pronounced seismic wave amplification during the Ms 6.2 event on December 18, 2023. These site effects intensified ground motions, leading to peak ground accelerations higher than those observed in comparable moderate-magnitude earthquakes, primarily through impedance contrasts at soil-bedrock interfaces and the low shear-wave velocities inherent to loess (typically 150–300 m/s in shallow layers).[26] This amplification was particularly evident in vertical shaking components, where loess's columnar structure and vertical lamination promoted preferential transmission and resonance at frequencies matching the earthquake's dominant spectrum (around 1–5 Hz).[43] A distinctive mechanism involved the structural collapse of intact loess blocks under cyclic loading, resulting in seismic subsidence exceeding 0.5 m across multiple sites, even within seismic intensity VI zones up to 32 km from the epicenter. Numerical modeling of these collapses indicates that loess's metastable fabric—formed by wind-deposited particles with strong vertical bonding but weak horizontal shear resistance—underwent progressive softening once shear strains surpassed 0.1–0.5%, thresholds lower than for typical cohesionless sands due to the soil's anisotropic fabric.[80] This process was exacerbated by partial saturation from regional irrigation, which reduced effective stresses and enabled quasi-liquefaction behaviors atypical for arid loess, where void ratios often exceed 0.9 but water content is normally below 10%.[43] Further novelty arose from anthropogenic modifications amplifying these effects: land reclamation and agricultural wetting increased pore pressures, triggering a 3-km-long liquefaction-induced mudflow in saturated loess deposits, where flow liquefaction transitioned saturated silts into a viscous state with runout velocities up to 5 m/s. This contrasts with classical loess failures, which rarely exhibit fluidity without high groundwater; here, pre-event saturation (from canal irrigation) lowered the cyclic resistance ratio, allowing undrained shear strengths to drop below 10 kPa under 10–20 loading cycles.[43] The interplay of thrust fault rupture—orienting energy upward into the loess basin—and these site-specific responses produced ground motion anomalies, with simulated peak accelerations in loess basins 1.5–2 times those on firm rock, dictating the distribution of over 9,340 coseismic landslides.[26][43] These mechanisms underscore loess's vulnerability to hybrid amplification, combining geometric (topographic focusing on tableland edges) and material (fabric collapse) factors, which peer-reviewed simulations attribute to the event's shallow focal depth (10 km) channeling energy into soft sediments without significant attenuation.[80] Observations from post-event InSAR and field surveys confirm that such amplification not only initiated failures but also reactivated pre-existing slopes via accelerated creep, with displacement rates increasing 2–5 fold in the far field.[43]

Global context and reactions

International aid offers and acceptance

Following the December 18, 2023, earthquake, the United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF) offered and delivered humanitarian supplies specifically targeting children affected in Jishishan County, Gansu Province. This included 44,500 winter garments such as coats, pants, and sweatshirts for approximately 30,000 young children and adolescents, with the first batch arriving on December 22, 2023, through coordination with national and local Chinese government partners for distribution.[91] The Ban Ki-moon Foundation for a Better Future provided relief supplies valued at about 1 million yuan (approximately US$139,955), with an initial shipment worth 500,000 yuan—including coats, quilts, instant noodles, bread, mineral water, fruit juice, masks, and other emergency materials—delivered directly to Jishishan County residents.[71] Material donations were also accepted from foreign donors, including 10 million Pakistani rupees (about 210,000 yuan) received by the Chinese Embassy in Pakistan from local contributors, as well as unspecified donations from Mongolia.[71] China's Taiwan Affairs Office publicly expressed "sincere gratitude" for relief contributions from Taiwanese businesses and individuals to the quake-stricken areas.[92] While leaders from countries including Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates sent official condolences offering support, accepted international aid focused on material donations from organizations and select bilateral sources rather than operational involvement like foreign rescue teams, aligning with China's emphasis on domestic emergency response capabilities.[71] No major rejections of offers were reported, though the scale of foreign aid remained limited compared to extensive internal mobilization by Chinese authorities.[71]

Comparative analysis with prior Chinese earthquakes

The 2023 Jishishan earthquake, registering a surface-wave magnitude (Ms) of 6.2 at a shallow depth of approximately 10 km, resulted in 151 fatalities and 983 injuries, primarily from the collapse of poorly constructed rural homes in loess terrain.[7][1] This event stands as China's deadliest since the 2014 Ludian earthquake (Ms 6.1), which killed 617 people in a similarly rural, seismically active region of Yunnan Province, where mountainous topography exacerbated landslide impacts on scattered villages.[93] In contrast, higher-magnitude events like the 2008 Wenchuan earthquake (Mw 7.9) in Sichuan Province caused around 69,000 deaths through widespread structural failures in schools and homes, compounded by co-seismic landslides in rugged terrain that buried entire communities.[2] The 1976 Tangshan earthquake (Ms 7.8) remains the benchmark for devastation, with official estimates of at least 242,000 fatalities—potentially up to 655,000 including unreported cases—in an urban industrial hub where unreinforced masonry crumbled en masse under intense shaking.[94] Key differences in impact arise from geological and socioeconomic factors. Loess Plateau settings, as in Jishishan, feature soil amplification of ground motion due to thick, unconsolidated deposits (up to tens of meters), leading to vertical collapses of cave dwellings and adobe structures rather than the lateral spreading or deep-seated landslides prevalent in the thrust-fault dominated mountainous zones of Sichuan or Yunnan.[95] This amplification effect, while severe locally, contained damage to low-density rural areas with fewer high-rises compared to Tangshan's urban epicenter or Wenchuan's school-heavy zones, where substandard concrete enforcement contributed disproportionately to casualties.[96] Post-2008 seismic code revisions in China emphasized urban reinforcements, indirectly benefiting rural preparedness through national campaigns, though traditional loess homes—prevalent in Gansu—remained vulnerable, destroying over 4,700 residences in Jishishan.[7] Casualty ratios reflect these variances: Jishishan's ~24 deaths per 0.1 Ms unit contrasts with Wenchuan's ~8,700 per unit, underscoring how population exposure and construction quality modulate outcomes beyond raw magnitude.[93][2]
