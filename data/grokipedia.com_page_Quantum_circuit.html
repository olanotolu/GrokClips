Quantum circuit

In quantum information theory, a quantum circuit is a model for quantum computation, similar to classical circuits, in which a computation is a sequence of quantum gates, measurements, initializations of qubits to known values, and possibly other actions. Circuits are written such that the horizontal axis is time, starting at the left hand side and ending at the right. Horizontal lines are qubits, doubled lines represent classical bits. The items that are connected by these lines are operations performed on the qubits, such as measurements or gates. These lines define the sequence of events, and are usually not physical cables.[1][2][3] The graphical depiction of quantum circuit elements is described using a variant of the Penrose graphical notation. Richard Feynman used an early version of the quantum circuit notation in 1986.[4]

Reversible classical logic gates

Most elementary logic gates of a classical computer are not reversible. Thus, for instance, for an AND gate one cannot always recover the two input bits from the output bit; for example, if the output bit is 0, we cannot tell from this whether the input bits are 01 or 10 or 00.[5] However, reversible gates in classical computers are easily constructed for bit strings of any length; moreover, these are actually of practical interest, since irreversible gates must always increase physical entropy.[5] A reversible gate is a reversible function on nnn-bit data that returns nnn-bit data, where an nnn-bit data is a string of bits x1,x2,â€¦,xnx_1, x_2, \dots, x_nx1â€‹,x2â€‹,â€¦,xnâ€‹ of length nnn. The set of nnn-bit data is the space {0,1}n\{ 0,1 \}^n{0,1}n, which consists of 2n2^n2n strings of 0's and 1's. More precisely: an nnn-bit reversible gate is a bijective mapping fff from the set {0,1}n\{ 0,1 \}^n{0,1}n of nnn-bit data onto itself.[5] An example of such a reversible gate fff is a mapping that applies a fixed permutation to its inputs. For reasons of practical engineering, one typically studies gates only for small values of nnn, e.g. n=1n = 1n=1, n=2n = 2n=2 or n=3n = 3n=3. These gates can be easily described by tables. Common examples include:
  • For n=1n = 1n=1: The NOT gate, which flips the bit (0 to 1, 1 to 0).
  • For n=2n = 2n=2: The controlled-NOT (CNOT) gate, which flips the second bit if the first is 1.
  • For n=3n = 3n=3: The Toffoli gate, which flips the third bit if the first two are both 1.

Reversible logic circuits

Again, we consider first reversible classical computation. Conceptually, there is no difference between a reversible nnn-bit circuit and a reversible nnn-bit logic gate: either one is just an invertible function on the space of nnn bit data. However, as mentioned in the previous section, for engineering reasons we would like to have a small number of simple reversible gates, that can be put together to assemble any reversible circuit. To explain this assembly process, suppose we have a reversible nnn-bit gate fff and a reversible mmm-bit gate ggg. Quantum computations

Since many important numerical problems reduce to computing a unitary transformation UUU on a finite-dimensional space (the celebrated discrete Fourier transform being a prime example), one might expect that some quantum circuit could be designed to carry out the transformation UUU. Accelerating Quantum Computing Simulations with FPGAs

The development of quantum computing has led to increased interest in simulation tools, such as IBM's Qiskit, to model quantum behavior on classical hardware. However, quantum hardware remains expensive and limited in scale, while classical simulators face exponential slowdowns as the number of qubits grows, due to the need to track 2n2^{n}2n complex amplitudes in the state vector.[7] In quantum circuits, the state is represented by a vector of 2n2^{n}2n complex amplitudes, and gates by 2nÃ—2n2^{n} \times 2^{n}2nÃ—2n unitary matrices. Simulating a gate involves matrix-vector multiplication, which scales as O( 4n4^{n}4n ) in software implementations like NumPy. Field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) accelerate this by exploiting hardware parallelism for linear algebra operations, including pipelining and low-latency on-chip memory.[8] FPGA-based simulators offload computations, such as matrix-vector multiplications, to custom hardware accelerators. General approaches include fixed-point arithmetic for complex numbers, parallel processing units for gates, and optimizations like BeneÅ¡ networks for qubit permutations. These enable practical speedups for small to medium circuits. For example, a 2024 design achieved 368Ã— speedup over CPU-based simulators for 7-qubit circuits. As of 2024, advanced systems have simulated circuits with over 43 qubits using large FPGAs, though resource constraints limit scalability beyond ~20 qubits on single devices.[8][9][10] With sufficient parallelism and logarithmic-depth circuits for summations, gate applications can achieve O( nnn ) latency per gate, contrasting with O( 4n4^{n}4n ) serial time on general-purpose processorsâ€”though overall simulation requires exponential space and is hardware-limited. Hybrid CPU-FPGA systems further enhance flexibility for research and development.[8][10]
