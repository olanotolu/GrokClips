M. Balamuralikrishna

Mangalampalli Balamuralikrishna (né Muralikrishna; 6 July 1930 – 22 November 2016) was an Indian Carnatic vocalist, musician, multi-instrumentalist, playback singer, composer, and character actor.[1][2] He was awarded the Madras Music Academy's Sangeetha Kalanidhi in 1978.[3] He garnered two National Film Awards (1976,[4] 1987[5]), the Sangeet Natak Akademi Award in 1975,[6] the Padma Vibhushan, India's second-highest civilian honor in 1991,[7] for his contribution towards arts, the Mahatma Gandhi Silver Medal from UNESCO in 1995, the Chevalier of the Ordre des Arts et des Lettres by the French Government in 2005,[8] and the Sangeetha Kalasikhamani in 1991 by the Fine Arts Society, Chennai, to name a few.[9] Balamuralikrishna started his career at the age of six.[10] In his life-time, he gave over 25,000 concerts worldwide.[11] He is also renowned for creating several new ragas and talas in Carnatic music.[12] Apart from Pandit Bhimsen Joshi, he presented jugalbandi concerts (duets) with Pandit Hariprasad Chaurasia, Pandit Ajoy Chakrabarty and Kishori Amonkar, among others.[13] He is also known for popularizing the compositions of Sri Bhadrachala Ramadasu, Sri Annamacharya, and others. Balamuralikrishna's concerts combined sophisticated vocal skills and rhythmic patterns of classical music with the popular demand for entertainment value. Balamuralikrishna presented concerts in many countries, including the US, Canada, UK, Italy, France, Russia, Sri Lanka, Malaysia, Singapore, countries in the Middle East, and elsewhere. Apart from his native tongue, Telugu, he has also composed in other languages including Kannada, Sanskrit, Tamil, Malayalam, Hindi, Bengali, and Punjabi.[14] He appeared as a featured soloist with an award-winning British choir, performing the "Gitanjali Suite" with words from Rabindranath Tagore's Nobel Prize-winning poetry and music by "Dr. Joel", the noted UK-based Goan composer. His clear diction in several languages prompted an invitation to record several of Tagore's Rabindra Sangeet compositions in Bengali, preserving them for posterity. He had sung in French, and even ventured into jazz fusion, collaborating with the top Carnatic percussion teacher, Sri T.H. Subash Chandran, in a concert for Malaysian royalty.

Early life

Balamuralikrishna was born in a Brahmin family at Sankaraguptam, East Godavari District, Madras Presidency (now a part of Andhra Pradesh state). His father, Mangalampalli Pattabhiramayya, was a well known musician and his mother, Suryakanthamma, was a veena player. Balamuralikrishna's mother died when he was an infant and he was raised by his father. Observing his interest in music, his father put him under the tutelage of Parupalli Ramakrishnayya Pantulu, a direct descendant of the shishya parampara (lineage of disciples) of Tyagaraja. Under his guidance, the young Balamuralikrishna learned Carnatic music. At the age of ten, on 18 July 1940, he gave his first full-fledged concert at a music festival in Vijayawada. Musunuri Suryanarayana Murty Bhagavatar, a Harikatha performer, saw the musical talent in him and gave the prefix "Bala" (lit. child) to the young Balamuralikrishna.[15] (Prior to this, his name was Muralikrishna; following Bhagavatar's addition of the prefix, he began to be known as Balamuralikrishna.) Having begun his musical career at a very young age, by age fifteen he had mastered all the 72 melakartha ragas and had composed krithis in each of them. His Janaka Raga Manjari was published in 1952 and recorded as Raagaanga Ravali in a nine-volume series by the Sangeeta Recording Company.[13] Not merely content with his fame as a Carnatic vocalist, he also played the kanjira, mridangam, viola, and violin.[16][17][18] He accompanied various musicians on the violin. He also presented solo viola concerts. He was the person who introduced viola to classical Indian music.[19][20]

Experimentation

Characteristic of Balamuralikrishna's musical journey have been his non-conformism, spirit of experimentation, and boundless creativity. Balamuralikrishna experimented with the Carnatic music system while keeping its rich tradition untouched. He created several new ragas, some with as few as three notes (such as Sarvasri and Ganapati) and others with four notes (such as Lavangi and Mahati), representing his quest for new frontiers.[13] He also innovated in the tala (rhythm) system, developing the Mukhi Tala classification, which incorporates gati bhedam in sashabda kriya and includes basic categories like Trimukhi, Panchamukhi, Saptamukhi, and Navamukhi.[21] He authorized S. Ram Bharati to found the "Academy of Performing Arts and Research" in Switzerland.[22] He worked on music therapy.[23] After his death, his family started the 'Dr. M. Balamuralikrishna Memorial Trust' to honor and keep his legacy alive.[24]

Compositions

Balamuralikrishna had over 400 compositions to his credit and was one of the very few people to have composed in all the 72 Melakarta ragas.[13] He created several ragas, including those with four notes (such as Mahati) and three notes (such as Sarvashri), and invented a new tala system known as Mukhi Talas (with variants including Trimukhi, Panchamukhi, Saptamukhi, and Navamukhi). [25] [26] His compositions encompassed every genre in Carnatic music, including varnams, kritis, tillanas, and bhava geethes.[13]

Cinema

Balamuralikrishna has sung in several films in Telugu, Sanskrit, Malayalam, Kannada and Tamil.[27] He made his acting debut with the Telugu film Bhakta Prahlada (1967) as Narada, and has acted in a few other films in Telugu, Tamil and Malayalam.[28][29]

Death

Balamuralikrishna died at his residence in Chennai on 22 November 2016 at around 3:30 p.m.; he was 86.[30][31] He had been unwell for some time.[1] He was cremated at Besant Nagar Crematorium in Chennai the next day. A large number of fans, well-wishers, and celebrities attended his funeral.[31] He is survived by three daughters and three sons who are all doctors.[31] His wife, Smt. Annapurna, outlived him for three months and died on 16 February 2017.[32]

Legacy

After his death, his family established the Dr. M. Balamuralikrishna Memorial Trust on 6 July 2017 to preserve and promote his musical legacy. The trust aims to streamline his musical creations by digitizing them and organizing events, including annual concerts such as the Balamurali Nada Mahotsav on his birth anniversary and Visakham concerts, to honor his contributions to Carnatic music.[33]

Talas created

The 'Mukhi' Tala system, developed by M. Balamuralikrishna, builds on the traditional Adi Tala (Chaturasra Jaati Triputa Tala). In this system, the gati bheda is applied only to the sashabda (sounding) kriyas: the first kriya (mukha, meaning face) of each anga takes the gati corresponding to the tala name (tisra for Trimukhi, kanda for Panchamukhi, etc.), while nishabda (silent) kriyas are in dwi-gati (2 aksharas), and additional sounding kriyas follow suit as per demonstrations.[25] This generates four primary talas based on Triputa structure (laghu-drutam-'laghu' adapted): The Mukhi Talas
TalaStructureAksharas
Trimukhi3 + 2 + 2 + 2 | 3 + 2 | 3 + 2 ||19
Panchamukhi5 + 2 + 2 + 2 | 5 + 2 | 5 + 2 ||25
Sapthamukhi7 + 2 + 2 + 2 | 7 + 2 | 7 + 2 ||31
Navamukhi9 + 2 + 2 + 2 | 9 + 2 | 9 + 2 ||37
The scheme can be extended to all 35 suladi talas, though these four are most commonly practiced.[47] Compositions: Pallavis have been set in these talas, including Balamuralikrishna's own RTPs in ragas such as Bilahari (1982 concert), Todi, and Kalyani (1978 live).[48][49][50] Other examples include 'Isai inbathirku ēdillaye ivulakil – sentamil' in raga Lathangi by Amrutha Venkatesh, and 'Aadi Vaa, Pirai Soodi Vaa, Aalavaai Naathane' in raga Abheri by Rameshvinayak, tuned by S.J. Jananiy.[51][52] Alarippus for Bharatanatyam have also been composed in Panchamukhi tala.[53]
