Cystinuria

Cystinuria is a rare inherited autosomal recessive disorder[1] characterized by high concentrations of the amino acid cystine in the urine, leading to the formation of cystine stones in the kidneys, ureters, and bladder. It affects approximately 1 in 7,000 people worldwide.[2] It is a type of aminoaciduria. Cystine, not cysteine, is implicated in this disease; the former is a dimer of the latter.

Presentation

Cystinuria is a cause of recurrent kidney stones. It is a disease involving the defective transepithelial transport of cystine and dibasic amino acids in the kidney and intestine and is one of many causes of kidney stones. If not treated properly, the disease could cause serious damage to the kidneys and surrounding organs. The stones may be identified by a positive nitroprusside cyanide test. The crystals are usually hexagonal, translucent, and white. Upon removal, the stones may be pink or yellow, but later they turn greenish due to exposure to air. Cystinuria is usually asymptomatic when no stone is formed. However, once a stone is formed, signs and symptoms can occur:[1]
  • Nausea
  • Flank pain
  • Hematuria
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Rarely, acute kidney injury
People with cystinuria often experience recurrent stone formation requiring ongoing care, with frequency varying among individuals. Cystinurics have an increased risk for chronic kidney disease[3][4] and since kidney damage or poor function is often present in people with cystinuria, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or over the counter (OTC) medications should be used with caution. Cystine stones are often difficult to detect using plain X-rays. Computed tomography or ultrasound may be used instead for imaging.[5] Urine odor in cystinuria smells of rotten eggs due to the increase in cystine.[6]

Genetics

Cystinuria has an autosomal recessive pattern of inheritance. Cystinuria is an autosomal recessive disease,[1] which means that the defective gene responsible for the disease is located on an autosome, and two copies of the defective gene (one inherited from each parent) are required to be born with the disease. The parents of an individual with an autosomal recessive disease both carry one copy of the defective gene, but usually do not experience any signs or symptoms of the disease.

Cause

Cystinuria is caused by mutations in the SLC3A1 and SLC7A9 genes. Cystinuria is classified into Type A (mutations in SLC3A1, autosomal recessive) and Type B (mutations in SLC7A9, autosomal recessive with possible incomplete penetrance in heterozygotes).[7] These defects prevent proper reabsorption of cystine and the dibasic (positively charged) amino acids: lysine, ornithine, arginine.[8] Under normal circumstances, the proteins encoded by these genes form a heterodimeric transporter that allows certain amino acids, including cystine, to be reabsorbed into the blood from the filtered fluid that will become urine. Mutations in either of these genes disrupt the ability of this transporter to reabsorb these amino acids, allowing them to become concentrated in the urine. As the levels of cystine in the urine increase, it forms cystine crystals, resulting in kidney stones. Cystine crystals form hexagonal-shaped crystals that can be viewed upon microscopic analysis of the urine. The other amino acids that are not reabsorbed do not create crystals in urine. The overall prevalence of cystinuria is approximately 1 in 7,000 neonates (from 1 in 2,500 neonates in Libyan Jews to 1 in 100,000 among Swedes).[7]

Pathophysiology

Cystinuria is characterized by the inadequate reabsorption of cystine and the dibasic amino acids ornithine, arginine, and lysine in the proximal convoluted tubules after filtration of these amino acids by the kidney's glomeruli, resulting in excessive concentrations in the urine.[9] The transport defect also impairs intestinal absorption of these amino acids, though this rarely causes symptoms. Cystine, being the least soluble, may precipitate out of the urineâ€”particularly if neutral or acidic (its solubility is approximately 250 mg/L at pH 7 and decreases in acidic conditions)â€”and form crystals or stones in the kidneys, ureters, or bladder.[10] It is one of several inborn errors of metabolism included in Garrod's tetrad.

Diagnosis

  1. Blood: Routine hemogram along with blood sugar, urea, and creatinine.
  2. Urine: Microscopic examination for hexagonal cystine crystals. A screening test is the cyanide-nitroprusside test, which detects elevated sulfhydryl-containing amino acids. Definitive confirmation involves quantitative measurement of cystine (and dibasic amino acids lysine, ornithine, and arginine) in a 24-hour urine sample, with cystine excretion exceeding 250 mg/day in adults indicating cystinuria.[10]
  3. Ultrasound or CT scan to detect stones.
  4. Genetic analysis to identify mutations associated with the disease, such as in the SLC3A1 or SLC7A9 genes. Genetic testing is available through specialized laboratories in the United States (e.g., Mayo Clinic Labs, Invitae) and other countries.[11]
Regular X-rays often fail to show cystine stones clearly, however, they can be visualized using intravenous pyelogram (IVP). Stones may appear with a fuzzy gray appearance on X-rays. They are radiopaque due to sulfur content, though more difficult to visualize than calcium oxalate stones.

Treatment

Initial treatment is with adequate hydration, alkalization of the urine with citrate supplementation or acetazolamide, and dietary modification to reduce salt and protein intake (especially methionine). If this fails then patients are usually started on chelation therapy with an agent such as penicillamine.[12][13] Tiopronin is another agent. Once renal stones have formed, however, the first-line treatment is endoscopic laser lithotripsy. ESWL ([Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy](Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy "Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy")) is often not effective because of the hardness of the stones that do not fragment easily. Conventional open-abdominal surgery is rarely used but has proven to be an effective treatment modality for patients with more advanced forms of the disease. Adequate hydration is the foremost aim of treatment to prevent cystine stones. The goal is to increase the urine volume because the concentration of cystine in the urine is reduced which prevents cystine from precipitating from the urine and forming stones. Patients should aim for a fluid intake of 3-4 L per day to produce at least 3 L of urine daily.[14] The rationale behind alkalizing the urine is that cystine tends to stay in solution and causes no harm. To alkalinize the urine, sodium bicarbonate has been used. One must be careful in alkalizing their urine because it could lead to other forms of stones in the process of preventing cystine stones. Penicillamine is a drug that acts to form a complex with cystine that is 50 times more soluble than cystine itself. Percutaneous nephrolithotripsy (PNL) is performed via a port created by puncturing the kidney through the skin and enlarging the access port to 1 cm in diameter. Most of the time, cystine stones are too dense to be broken up by shock (ESWL) so PNL is needed. In February 2017, an article was published in Nature Medicine entitled "Alpha lipoic acid treatment prevents cystine urolithiasis in a mouse model of cystinuria", suggesting that a high dose of the readily available antioxidant, alpha-lipoic acid at 2,700 mg/67 kg body weight daily reduced the incidence of stones in the mouse model. The effects were dose dependent.[15] The results are unprecedented for cystinuria.[16] A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial (NCT02910531, completed as of 2024) using 1200 mg daily alpha-lipoic acid showed reduced stone growth in 40% of patients compared to 14% on placebo, though it is not yet a standard treatment.[17][18]

Occurrence in animals

This disease is known to occur in at least five mammalian species: domestic canines, domestic cats, domestic ferrets, and a wild canid, the maned wolf of South America. Cystine uroliths have been demonstrated, usually in male dogs, from approximately 70 breeds including the Australian cattle dog, Australian shepherd, Basenji, Basset, Bullmastiff, Chihuahua, Scottish deerhound, Scottish terrier, Staffordshire terrier, Welsh corgi, and Newfoundland dogs.[19] It has also been reported in domestic cats, particularly in breeds like the Siamese and related lines.[20] Cystinuria leading to cystine urolithiasis has been documented in domestic ferrets.[21] In maned wolves, cystinuria is prevalent, especially in captivity, and associated with renal calculi.[22]
