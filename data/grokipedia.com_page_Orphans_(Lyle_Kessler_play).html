Orphans (Lyle Kessler play)

Orphans is a play by [Lyle Kessler](Lyle Kessler "Lyle Kessler"). It premiered in 1983 at [The Matrix Theatre Company](The Matrix Theatre Company "The Matrix Theatre Company") in Los Angeles, where it received critical and commercial success and won the [Drama-Logue Award](Drama-Logue Award "Drama-Logue Award"). It was adapted into a 1987 film directed by Alan J. Pakula.[1] The play has been performed by the [Steppenwolf Theatre](Steppenwolf Theatre "Steppenwolf Theatre") and on [Broadway](Broadway theatre "Broadway theatre") in 2013.

Production history

Orphans premiered at the Matrix Theatre in Los Angeles in August 1983, and featured [Joe Pantoliano](Joe Pantoliano "Joe Pantoliano"), [Lane Smith](Lane Smith "Lane Smith") and [Paul Lieber](Paul Lieber "Paul Lieber").[2][3] In January through March 1985 the play was produced at Chicago's [Steppenwolf Theatre](Steppenwolf Theatre "Steppenwolf Theatre"), with direction by [Gary Sinise](Gary Sinise "Gary Sinise") and starring [John Mahoney](John Mahoney "John Mahoney"), [Terry Kinney](Terry Kinney "Terry Kinney") and [Kevin Anderson](Kevin Anderson (actor) "Kevin Anderson (actor)").[4] Sinise said the play "kicked" the three actors "off into the movie business."[5] John Mahoney, who received the Derwent Award[6] and [Theatre World Award](Theatre World Award "Theatre World Award") for his performance said that "Orphans affected people more than any other play I've ever done. I still get mail from it, I still get people stopping me on the street, and it's twenty years later."[7] After its Chicago run, the Steppenwolf Theatre Company production premiered Off-Broadway at the Westside Theatre, running from May 7, 1985 to January 6, 1986, with Mahoney, Kinney, and Anderson reprising their roles. Later, a replacement cast consisting of Steppenwolf member [Gary Cole](Gary Cole "Gary Cole"), [Corey Parker](Corey Parker (actor) "Corey Parker (actor)") and William Wise took over the lead roles.[8] Orphans was the first Steppenwolf production to be performed internationally in London, premiering in the [West End](West End theatre "West End theatre") at the [Apollo Theatre](Apollo Theatre "Apollo Theatre") in 1986.[2] [Albert Finney](Albert Finney "Albert Finney") as Harold won an [Olivier Award](Olivier Award "Olivier Award") as Actor of the Year.[9] The Steppenwolf productions in London and the United States helped establish Kessler's status as a major American playwright as well as the company's signature "rock and roll" brand of theatre. [10] To help highlight the emotional intensity of Kessler's parable, they featured an assortment of compositions by [Pat Metheny](Pat Metheny "Pat Metheny") and [Lyle Mays](Lyle Mays "Lyle Mays") to be played in the background; the pieces have remained optional for every production since. In 2005, [Al Pacino](Al Pacino "Al Pacino") did a workshop of the play at the Greenway Court Theatre, Los Angeles;[10] [Jesse Eisenberg](Jesse Eisenberg "Jesse Eisenberg") and Southland's [Shawn Hatosy](Shawn Hatosy "Shawn Hatosy") co-starred. Orphans made its Broadway debut at the [Gerald Schoenfeld Theatre](Gerald Schoenfeld Theatre "Gerald Schoenfeld Theatre") on April 7, 2013. The production, directed by [Daniel Sullivan](Daniel Sullivan (director) "Daniel Sullivan (director)"), starred [Ben Foster](Ben Foster (actor) "Ben Foster (actor)") as Treat, [Tom Sturridge](Tom Sturridge "Tom Sturridge") as Phillip and [Alec Baldwin](Alec Baldwin "Alec Baldwin") as Harold.[11] The production closed on May 19, 2013 after 37 performances. The play received two [Tony Award](Tony Award "Tony Award") nominations, for Best Revival of a Play and Best Leading Actor in a Play (Sturridge).[12] The production was originally slated to star [Shia LaBeouf](Shia LaBeouf "Shia LaBeouf") as Treat, but he departed the production during rehearsals after coming into conflict with Baldwin.[13] Orphans has reached many fans beyond the typical theater crowd, counting [Lou Reed](Lou Reed "Lou Reed") and [Tom Waits](Tom Waits "Tom Waits") among its most ardent admirers. The play was adapted into a [film of the same name](Orphans (1987 film) "Orphans (1987 film)"). The film stars [Matthew Modine](Matthew Modine "Matthew Modine"), Albert Finney and [Kevin Anderson](Kevin Anderson (actor) "Kevin Anderson (actor)"). According to Kessler, "The play has been done everywhere, from Japan to Iceland to Mexico to South America.... It just boggles the mind. Itâ€™s amazing: the evolution of the play and its reception in the world."[2]

Synopsis

Two grown orphan brothers live in an old dilapidated [row house](Row house "Row house") in North Philadelphiaâ€”deserted in childhood by an unfaithful father and by the death of their mother. Older brother Treat, brutal and violent, provides for his younger brother Phillip by being a petty thiefâ€”interpreting the role of father. With the love and protectiveness of an older brother and an orphan's fear of abandonment, Treat takes away Phillip's chances to grow up, depriving him of knowledge and forcing him to live in a world of illiteracy and innocence: relegating him to their lost childhood. As Treat is out stealing to put food on the table, Phillip never leaves the house, thinking he will die from something outside because of a near deadly allergic reaction he had as a child. Haunted by the death of their mother, he spends his time lying in her closet filled with unworn clothes. Curious about the world, he secretly attempts to understand things by watching reruns of [The Price Is Right](The Price Is Right (U.S. game show) "The Price Is Right (U.S. game show)") and underlining words in newspapers and old books he finds lying around. Treat kidnaps and ties up a Chicago gangster named Harold. Harold, an orphan himself, with the prowess of an escape artist, loosens the ties that bind him, turns the tables around, and with gun in hand, puts himself into the role of teacher, healer and surrogate parent.

Critical reception

A 1985 review of Sinise's production, by [The Record](The Record (Bergen County) "The Record (Bergen County)"), wrote, "while one might be tempted to chuckle at Kessler's old-fashioned dramaturgy, it's a fine example of its kind and gorgeously performed by a cast of three under the direction of Gary Sinise... Sinise has staged the piece in a realistic idiom with highly theatrical accents lifelike scenes that begin and end in tableaux, actors throwing themselves around like rag dolls, extravagantly long pauses..."[14] The play was described by [The New York Times](The New York Times "The New York Times") as "theater for the senses and emotions, not the mind."[15] T.H. McCulloh of the [Los Angeles Times](Los Angeles Times "Los Angeles Times") wrote of a 1999 revival that it is as wise and knowledgeable about the [human condition](Human condition "Human condition") as [Tennessee Williams](Tennessee Williams "Tennessee Williams") and "also as theatrical as Williams. Kessler has something very important to say, and he says it in terms we can't ignore. The biggest message is that we need each other, and that's something the viewer can't ignore...."[16] Tony Adler of the [Chicago Reader](Chicago Reader "Chicago Reader") declared of the Steppenwolf production, "Lyle Kessler's unassuming tale of two nearly [feral](Feral children "Feral children") brothers and the mysterious businessman who befriends them was and remains among the most devastating things I've seen onstage."[17] [John Simon](John Simon (critic) "John Simon (critic)") wrote in the [National Review](National Review "National Review"), "The play was a synthetic contraption out of Pinter and Sam Shepard, but it worked as a showcase for energetic actors and a clever director."[18] [The Miami Herald](The Miami Herald "The Miami Herald") wrote about a 1986 production starring [Judd Nelson](Judd Nelson "Judd Nelson") that the play is "tense, moving and funny as anything you're likely to see." The reviewer said, "Orphans is woven from mysteries, contradictions and unanswered questions," and concluded, "Orphans is violent, shocking and profane. And it's wonderful."[19] The Steppenwolf Theatre's 1985 production, directed by Gary Sinise and starring John Mahoney, Jeff Perry, and Gary Sinise, received acclaim and transferred to New York, earning Gary Sinise the 1985 Clarence Derwent Award.[6][7] The subsequent 1986 London West End production starring Albert Finney won the Laurence Olivier Award for Best Actor.[9] A 2007 New York production was reviewed positively in The New York Times for its emotional depth and performances.[15] The 2013 Broadway revival, starring Alec Baldwin, Ben Foster, and Tom Sturridge, received mixed reviews. Charles Isherwood of The New York Times called it a "limp revival," criticizing its lack of energy despite strong casting.[20] The production earned Tony Award nominations for Best Revival of a Play and Best Leading Actor (Sturridge) but closed after 66 performances amid backstage controversies.[12]

Genre

[Lyle Kessler](Lyle Kessler "Lyle Kessler")'s Orphans , among many of his other pieces of literature, has been praised as a hybrid of 20th century [realism](Realism (arts) "Realism (arts)"), Pinter-esque absurdism[2], but in many ways it aligns itself better with the literary tradition of [Magical Realism](Magical Realism "Magical Realism")[], a more prevalent [genre in Latin American](Latin American literature "Latin American literature") countries than in the North [American theatre](American theatre "American theatre"). The way Orphans can move from a hyper realistic state into a parable while still maintaining its emotional pull and deeply felt sense of reality goes well with what magical realism is understood to beâ€”magical elements blended into a realistic atmosphere in order to access a deeper understanding of reality. The American theatrical tradition tends not to embrace these perceived contradictions as readily. An expressionistic play is expected to be cerebral and conceptual, not visceral. A realistic play is expected to maintain the same logic that one sees in the outside world.

Direction

Orphans has been applauded for its lack of dependence on one particular theatrical approach. As said by Los Angeles Times critic Scott Collins when reviewing a [Deaf West Theatre Company](Deaf West Theatre "Deaf West Theatre") production in 1996, "Whatever the medium, the viewer finds it hard not to be drawn into the emotional journey..." This production of Orphans, by the first sign language theater in the western United States, received Drama-Logue Awards, including for Best Director and Best Ensemble. Further praise for Kessler's ability to create something with such flexibility, while still taking people on its "emotional journey," came from a 2007 production of Orphans at the Penguin Repertory Company in Stony Point, Rockland County, New York. The [New York Times](New York Times "New York Times") critic Sylviane Gold called the production a "splendid revival", and wrote: "...it is strange to say about a play that burst into New York from Chicago in 1985 on the strength of the testosterone-fueled acting of the Steppenwolf Theater Company" that it can be directed "with as much attention to the play's heart as to its fist."[15] In Japan, Orphans premiered in 1991 by the Kaze Theater Troupe, a Tokyo-based group, going on to have a nationwide tour starting in 1992 and performing continually in theaters around Japan ever since. Its success illustrates the play's ability to harmonize with different theatrical variations as well as cultural traditions.[21] In Korea, it premiered in 2017, with a revival in 2019 featuring gender-blind casting that included female actors in key roles such as Treat. A further production was announced for late 2025. The drama's ability to maintain its inherent emotional pull regardless of its theatrical approach is one of the reasons for its continued success.
