Quartet (disambiguation)

A quartet is a group or set of four persons or things, particularly a musical composition for four voices or instruments or the ensemble of four performers executing it.[1][2] The term originates from Italian quartetto, a diminutive of quarto meaning "fourth," reflecting its numerical basis, and entered English usage via French quartette.[1] In musical contexts, quartets encompass diverse forms such as string quartets featuring two violins, viola, and cello, or vocal barbershop quartets, emphasizing balanced interplay among parts without conductor.[3] Beyond music, the word applies generally to any collection of four, including in literature, diplomacy, or games, though the musical sense predominates in common parlance.[1][3]

Compositions

A musical composition for quartet typically involves four performers or voices, with the string quartetâ€”scoring for two violins, viola, and celloâ€”serving as the foundational genre in Western classical music. This form emphasizes intimate dialogue among equal voices, contrasting with larger orchestral works, and crystallized in the Classical era through deliberate structural innovations that prioritized thematic development and contrapuntal balance.[12][13] Joseph Haydn formalized the string quartet in the 1750s with his Opus 1 set of six quartets, composed around 1755, which shifted from earlier divertimentos by treating instruments as autonomous yet interdependent.[13] His later Opus 76 quartets, completed in 1796â€“1797 and dedicated to Count Joseph Georg von ErdÅ‘dy, advanced the genre with cyclic elements, such as the Emperor's Hymn in the finale of No. 3, and rigorous motivic working that influenced subsequent composers.[12] These works, premiered privately in Vienna, exemplify Haydn's intent to elevate chamber music to symphonic complexity without conductor mediation.[14] Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart expanded the form's expressive range in his Dedicatory Quartets for Haydn (K. 387 to K. 465), composed from 1782 to 1785, where fugal textures in the finale of K. 465 ("Dissonance") and lyrical depth in K. 421 demonstrate balanced interplay and emotional nuance.[14] Ludwig van Beethoven's Opus 18 quartets, written 1798â€“1800, marked a transition to Romantic intensity, with No. 4's maligned finale revised to enhance dramatic contrast, while adhering to Classical precedents from Haydn and Mozart.[15] Classical string quartets conventionally adopt a four-movement structure: the first in sonata form (fast tempo, tonic key), a slow lyrical second movement often in a related key, a dance-like third (minuet or scherzo), and a vivacious finale, fostering narrative progression through key relations and thematic transformation.[16][17] Piano quartets, blending piano with string trio, emerged concurrently; Mozart's K. 478 (1785) and K. 493 (1786) integrate keyboard sonority for richer harmonic texture, first performed in Vienna amid mixed reception due to technical demands.[14] Later eras yielded enduring examples, such as Franz Schubert's String Quartet No. 14 "Death and the Maiden" (D. 810, 1824), whose second movement variations on his own lied theme convey mortal introspection through relentless ostinatos.[18] These compositions prioritize composer-documented intentions, like Schubert's focus on programmatic depth, over interpretive liberties.[19]

Recordings

Ultravox's Quartet, released on 15 October 1982 by Chrysalis Records, marked the sixth studio album by the British new wave band Ultravox and featured production by the band alongside Alan Gate.[20] The record incorporated synth-pop and new romantic elements, with singles including "Reap the Wild Wind" (UK #13) and "Hymn" (UK #11), contributing to its chart performance peaking at number 6 on the UK Albums Chart.[21] It achieved gold status from the British Phonographic Industry for over 100,000 units sold.[22] Other commercial recordings titled Quartet include self-titled releases by jazz ensembles, such as the 1957 Atlantic Records album by the Modern Jazz Quartet, which showcased cool jazz interpretations of standards and originals led by pianist John Lewis.[23] These works emphasize studio-captured performances distinct from live ensemble outputs or compositional sheet music. Critical reception for such jazz quartets often highlights their chamber-like precision and improvisational balance, though commercial metrics remain lower compared to pop counterparts.[11]

Board and card games

Quartets, also known as Quartet or Quartett, is a traditional card-matching game for 2 to 6 players, typically using a deck of 32 to 48 cards divided into sets of four matching cards each, grouped by themes such as animals, vehicles, professions, or historical figures.[42][43] The objective is to collect complete quartets by requesting specific cards from opponents, with the player amassing the most sets declared the winner.[42] Gameplay begins with players receiving an equal number of cards (often 5â€“7), with the remainder forming a draw pile. On a turn, a player requests a card from another player that would complete or advance one of their partial sets; if the opponent holds it, they must surrender the card, allowing the requestor to continue asking. If not, the player draws from the deck. Completing a quartet prompts the player to announce it aloud and lay the set face-up, often scoring points equal to the number of cards in the set.[43][44] The game ends when the draw pile is depleted or all cards are claimed, emphasizing memory, deduction, and social interaction over chance.[42] Originating in Europe during the 19th century as a variant of family-collecting games like the British Happy Families (first published around 1851), the Quartet format gained prominence in Germany under the name Quartett, featuring standardized rules for thematic matching sets.[45] Themed editions proliferated in the early 20th century, including educational versions for children to learn categories like instruments or biblical figures, with decks often customized for morale-boosting purposes, such as German military-issued sets during World War II containing 32 cards in 8 quartets focused on vehicles and personnel.[46] Its enduring popularity stems from simplicity and adaptability, with commercial releases continuing into the 21st century, though no comprehensive global sales figures are available; regional variants persist in markets like the Netherlands and Japan, where similar mechanics appear in localized quartet-style games.[45]

These approaches, pioneered in the 1990s, infer larger species trees by maximizing the consistency of induced quartets across subsets of taxa, exploiting properties like quartet compatibility to handle incomplete lineage sorting and reticulation; for instance, the quartet puzzling algorithm combines bootstrap resampling of quartets to build consensus trees, though it assumes additive distances and can suffer from long-branch attraction biases in empirical datasets. Sports and competitions

In track and field, the term "quartet" frequently denotes a four-person relay team competing in events like the 4x100 metres or 4x400 metres relays, where athletes exchange a baton to cover the distance collectively. These quartets emphasize synchronized handoffs and individual sprint speeds, with performance measured by total time; for instance, the United States men's 4x100m quartet set the fifth-fastest time in history at 37.29 seconds to win gold at the 2025 World Athletics Championships in Tokyo.[54] Similarly, Canada's men's 4x100m quartet earned silver in the same event, finishing behind the U.S. in a time reflecting their anchor Andre De Grasse's closing leg amid competitive pressure.[55] Historical precedents include Jamaica's women's 4x100m quartet, which secured multiple Olympic medals through precise baton passes, as seen in their Helsinki 1952 performance where the same four athletesâ€”Arthur Wint, Leslie Laing, Herb McKenley, and George Rhodenâ€”also contributed to the nation's relay legacy.[56] World records underscore the event's demands: the mixed 4x400m relay saw a U.S. quartet establish a mark with Wilbert London, Allyson Felix, Courtney Okolo, and Michael Cherry anchoring efficiently.[57] In championships, such as Tokyo 2025, Ghana's 4x100m quartet clocked 37.93 seconds for fourth place, missing medals by margins determined by sub-second differences in heats and finals.[58] Other quartet formations appear sparingly in team sports with four competitors, though less standardized; for example, curling rinks consist of four players (lead, second, third, and skip) competing in ends to score points via stone placement, as in Canada's youth delegation to the 2024 Gangwon Winter Youth Olympics featuring a quartet like Chloe Fediuk's team.[59] Measurable outcomes in these contexts prioritize aggregate scores or times over individual feats, with Olympic qualification often hinging on tournament wins, such as Scotland's selected quartets for world events.[60]

Organizations and businesses

The Quartet on the Middle East is a diplomatic consortium established in 2002 comprising the United States, the European Union, Russia, and the United Nations, tasked with mediating the Israeli-Palestinian peace process through proposals like the 2003 Road Map for Peace.[61] Its Office of the Quartet, based in East Jerusalem, supports Palestinian institution-building and economic development in the West Bank and Gaza, including sectoral initiatives for industries such as stone and marble.[62] Quartet Health is an American technology-enabled behavioral health company founded in 2014, specializing in value-based care integration for mental health needs within primary care systems.[63] It partners with health plans and providers across 39 U.S. states to identify and deliver services for conditions including serious mental illness, raising $153 million in funding by 2021 before its acquisition by NeuroFlow in January 2025.[64][65] Quartet is an American brand of office productivity products, established in 1954 as Quartet Manufacturing and now under ACCO Brands, known for manufacturing glass whiteboards, dry-erase boards, bulletin boards, and related accessories used in collaborative environments.[66] Quartet Community Foundation is a British independent charity founded in 1987, operating across four unitary authorities in southwest England to manage endowments, grants, and community funds serving a population of 1.1 million.[67]
