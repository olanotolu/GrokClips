John Rae (explorer)


John Rae (30 September 1813 – 22 July 1893) was a Scottish surgeon and Arctic explorer who, serving with the Hudson's Bay Company, pioneered efficient travel methods by adopting Inuit technologies such as snowshoes and light sleds, enabling him to survey nearly 1,800 miles of previously unmapped Arctic coastline between 1846 and 1854.[1][2] His expeditions filled critical gaps in geographical knowledge, including the 1854 discovery of Rae Strait, which completed the charting of the Northwest Passage by linking the Gulf of Boothia to the open sea south of King William Island.[1][3] During the same journey, Rae obtained relics and eyewitness accounts from Inuit hunters detailing the 1845 Franklin expedition's abandonment of its ships, subsequent overland march, and starvation-induced cannibalism among the survivors, information that British authorities initially rejected in favor of narratives emphasizing heroic endurance without such desperation.[1][2] Later forensic analyses of skeletal remains from King William Island have corroborated Rae's report of end-stage cannibalism, validating the empirical reliability of Inuit oral testimony over contemporaneous elite skepticism.[4] Born in Orphir, Orkney Islands, Rae qualified as a surgeon in Edinburgh at age 19 before joining the Hudson's Bay Company in 1833, where his roles in remote posts honed his survival skills through direct reliance on local resources and Indigenous knowledge, contrasting with the heavier, supply-dependent approaches of Royal Navy expeditions that often ended in failure.[5][2] His four Arctic surveys from Repulse Bay and Boothia Peninsula demonstrated exceptional stamina, covering vast distances on foot and by umiak, while preventing scurvy via foraged vitamin sources like cranberries.[2] For pinpointing the Passage's final link and Franklin's relics, Rae received £8,000 of the Admiralty's £10,000 reward, though full recognition was delayed by institutional resistance to his unflattering disclosures.[5] Rae's pragmatic realism—prioritizing functionality over pomp—positioned him as a key figure in causal understandings of Arctic viability, influencing subsequent explorations despite his underappreciation in Victorian-era accounts biased toward naval orthodoxy.[3]

Early Life and Education

Family and Upbringing in Orkney

John Rae was born on 30 September 1813 at the Hall of Clestrain in the parish of Orphir, Orkney Islands, Scotland.[5] He was the sixth child and fourth son of John Rae, who served as the local agent for the Hudson's Bay Company in Orkney and as estate factor for the landowner, and Margaret Glen.[5] [6] Two of Rae's older brothers had entered Hudson's Bay Company service by 1833, reflecting the family's established ties to the fur trade organization.[5] Rae spent his early years on the family farm at Clestrain, immersed in the rugged Orkney landscape of windswept moors, rocky cliffs, and coastal waters.[7] His initial education occurred at home under a private tutor, fostering a foundation in basic academics amid the islands' harsh rural environment.[5] During boyhood, Rae honed practical survival skills through extensive outdoor pursuits, such as sailing small boats, shooting game, hunting, and traversing the hills, moors, and sea cliffs—activities that built his physical endurance and familiarity with challenging terrains later essential to his exploratory work.[5] [7]

Medical Training and Initial Influences

At the age of 16, John Rae enrolled at the University of Edinburgh in 1829 to pursue medical studies, reflecting the era's common path for aspiring surgeons from rural Scottish backgrounds.[5] His training emphasized practical anatomy, surgery, and clinical observation, aligning with Edinburgh's reputation for rigorous, hands-on medical education influenced by figures like Robert Liston and James Syme.[3] Rae completed his qualification as a licentiate of the Royal College of Surgeons of Edinburgh in April 1833, enabling him to practice surgery professionally at just 20 years old.[2] This medical foundation proved instrumental for Rae's subsequent career, providing skills in treating injuries, managing health in remote environments, and conducting dissections that later aided his ethnographic observations among Indigenous peoples.[8] Initial influences shaping his trajectory included familial ties to the fur trade; his father, John Rae Sr., served as the Hudson's Bay Company's local agent in Orkney, facilitating Rae's entry into the company's service amid limited domestic opportunities for young surgeons during economic downturns.[9] These connections, combined with Rae's Orkney upbringing fostering resilience and familiarity with harsh weather, directed him toward exploratory roles rather than urban practice.[10] Upon qualification, Rae secured an appointment as surgeon aboard the Hudson's Bay Company supply ship Prince Rupert in summer 1833, marking his transition from academic medicine to applied fieldwork in North America.[7] This early posting exposed him to maritime challenges and colonial logistics, honing adaptability that defined his later Arctic endeavors, while underscoring the company's preference for medically trained personnel capable of sustaining expeditions.[11]

Hudson's Bay Company Service

Appointment as Surgeon and Fur Trader

In 1833, shortly after qualifying as a surgeon at the University of Edinburgh and the Royal College of Surgeons of Edinburgh, John Rae was appointed assistant surgeon to the Hudson's Bay Company's supply ship Prince of Wales, bound for Moose Factory on James Bay.[12][9] His family's ties to the company—his father served as the HBC agent in Orkney, and two older brothers had already entered its service—influenced the appointment, which combined medical duties with clerical responsibilities during the voyage.[13] The ship departed in July 1833, reaching Moose Factory later that summer, where Rae provided medical care to the crew and personnel amid the harsh subarctic conditions.[5] Upon the Prince of Wales's departure for England the following month, Rae elected to remain at Moose Factory as resident surgeon and clerk, a dual role that encompassed treating illnesses and injuries among fur traders and Indigenous peoples while overseeing trading post operations, including the exchange of furs for European goods.[5] This position marked his entry into the fur trade, where clerks like Rae managed inventories, negotiated with trappers, and ensured post profitability, skills essential to the HBC's monopoly in Rupert's Land.[2] He served in this capacity for over a decade, from approximately 1833 to 1844, adapting to winter isolation by hunting and snowshoeing, which honed his endurance for later explorations.[14] The appointment reflected the HBC's practice of employing medically trained personnel for remote outposts, where surgeons often doubled as traders to maximize operational efficiency.[15]

Adaptation to Arctic Survival Methods

Upon joining the Hudson's Bay Company in 1833 and arriving at Moose Factory on James Bay in 1834, Rae began adapting to sub-Arctic conditions by learning survival techniques from local Cree people, including expert snowshoeing and hunting methods that emphasized self-sufficiency over reliance on imported provisions.[5] This training enabled him to traverse vast distances efficiently; for instance, in one instance, he walked approximately 1,200 miles on snowshoes to Sault Ste. Marie, demonstrating proficiency that contemporaries described as unmatched.[16] He also mastered the use of sleds for transport, a skill imparted by First Nations and Métis associates, which facilitated lighter, more mobile travel compared to European cart-based systems ill-suited to snow-covered terrain.[16] Rae's adoption of these Indigenous practices contrasted sharply with prevailing British exploratory approaches, which depended on heavy supply depots and preserved foods prone to causing scurvy during prolonged winters; he advocated for native methods, critiquing European explorers as "self-sufficient donkeys" who overlooked the efficacy of Indigenous adaptations to local environments.[5] By living off the land through hunting and fishing, Rae minimized baggage, carrying only essential tools and relying on game such as caribou or fish caught with spears and lines, a strategy honed at Moose Factory that preserved his health and extended operational range.[17] As Rae's HBC duties extended northward into Arctic regions around 1846, he further incorporated Inuit techniques, such as constructing snow houses from blocks cut with a specialized saw, which trapped air for superior insulation over stone or wooden shelters used by naval parties.[17] He improved sledge performance by coating runners with a moss-and-damp-snow mixture, yielding smoother and faster travel than iron-shod runners that bogged down in soft snow.[17] For attire, Rae shifted to layered Indigenous-inspired garb, including smoked moose-skin moccasins, fur-lined mittens, and lightweight fawn-skin coats weighing just 4–5 pounds, prioritizing mobility and warmth without the bulk of European woolens that retained moisture and chilled in extreme cold.[17] These adaptations, combined with food caching—a Inuit practice of storing provisions in snow for return trips—allowed Rae to conduct extended surveys with small parties, covering up to 65 miles daily on snowshoes while maintaining stamina through targeted foraging.[16]

Arctic Expeditions Prior to Franklin

Boothia Peninsula Surveys

John Rae conducted surveys of the Boothia Peninsula region as part of his early Arctic expeditions for the Hudson's Bay Company, focusing on confirming its geographical configuration and mapping adjacent coastlines prior to the intensification of searches for the lost Franklin expedition. These efforts, undertaken between 1846 and 1851, demonstrated Boothia to be a peninsula rather than an island, as previously supposed, and charted significant portions of the Gulf of Boothia and surrounding areas.[12][15]

1846–1847 Winter Journey

Rae departed York Factory on June 13, 1846, with a party of ten men and two boats, traveling northward along the western shore of Hudson Bay to reach Repulse Bay by July 27, 1846, where he established winter quarters at Fort Hope.[18] In April 1847, after enduring temperatures as low as -36°C, Rae initiated an overland journey with sleds and dogs, crossing Rae Isthmus to Committee Bay and proceeding northward to Lord Mayor's Bay, arriving on April 19, 1847.[18] From elevated ground at Lord Mayor's Bay (approximately 69°31'N), Rae observed that the Boothia Peninsula connected to the mainland, disproving earlier assumptions of it being an island, and surveyed approximately 600 miles of coastline including Pelly Bay, Colvile Bay, and Cape Ellice (69°42'N, 85°8'W).[18][15] The party faced severe challenges including snowstorms, rough ice, deep snow, and limited provisions, returning to Fort Hope by May 5, 1847, after mapping new features and recording magnetic variations, such as at Cape Lady Pelly (67°28'N, 87°40'W).[18] These surveys contributed geological and botanical observations, identifying rock formations like gneiss and mica-slate, and filled critical gaps in the cartography of the Gulf of Boothia.[18]

1851 Mapping Efforts

In April 1851, Rae launched a sled-based expedition from Repulse Bay with four men, targeting unmapped coastal sections northwest of the bay to advance understanding of the Boothia Peninsula's approaches.[19] The party charted approximately 160 kilometers of shoreline from Point Ogle to Cape Krusenstern, employing snowshoes and sleds to traverse ice and land, thereby extending prior surveys into the Gulf of Boothia vicinity and supporting broader Arctic navigational knowledge.[19] This effort, completed by June 1851 upon return to Repulse Bay, emphasized precise latitude and longitude fixes amid variable ice conditions, though specific challenges like fatigue and weather mirrored earlier journeys.[19] The mapping reinforced Rae's role in delineating the peninsula's connectivity to continental landmasses, aiding subsequent explorations.[15]

1846–1847 Winter Journey

Rae's expedition reached Repulse Bay on August 1, 1846, where the party constructed winter quarters at Fort Hope, becoming the first Europeans to overwinter in the region with assistance from Inuit interpreters.[18][2] During the winter of 1846–1847, Rae organized sledge journeys using dogs and manual hauling to survey local features, departing Fort Hope in November 1846 toward Cape Lady Pelly on the Melville Peninsula coast.[18] These early winter travels covered approximately 150 miles, navigating rough ice and deep snow while enduring northwest winds and temperatures dropping to -36°F on February 17, 1847; the party built snow houses nightly for shelter and relied on cached provisions like pemmican.[18] Geological observations included gneiss and mica-slate formations, with botanical finds such as Saxifraga oppositifolia and Salix arctica.[18] By late winter, Rae extended explorations to Simpson’s Peninsula, mapping coastal contours and noting wildlife tracks of deer, hares, and polar bears, though no evidence of Franklin's expedition surfaced from Inuit encounters.[18][12] Transitioning into early spring on April 5, 1847, Rae led a major northward sledge journey from Fort Hope with five companions, including interpreters, covering nearly 600 miles along the Arctic coast to Lord Mayor Bay by May, the longest such foot and sledge traverse recorded at the time.[18][5] The party faced snowstorms, soft snow causing waist-deep sinking, dog fatigue requiring some to be shot or abandoned, and short rations, yet confirmed Sir John Ross's prior sightings while naming Cape Berens, Halkett Inlet, and Pelly Bay.[18] This effort surveyed Committee Bay and established Boothia as a peninsula rather than an island, advancing knowledge of the Gulf of Boothia.[12][2] Return to Fort Hope occurred in mid-May, with all members intact despite physical strain.[18]

1851 Mapping Efforts

In spring 1851, John Rae led a small party consisting of two men, two sledges, and five dogs from Repulse Bay to conduct surveys of the Boothia Peninsula's western coastline. Following Inuit advice, Rae crossed approximately 40 miles across the base of Melville Peninsula to reach Committee Bay, from where he proceeded to map northward along the uncharted shores. This effort mapped 430 kilometers of previously unexplored coastline on the west side of the Boothia Peninsula, leaving about 240 kilometers south of Bellot Strait unexamined.[19][15] The expedition connected Rae's earlier surveys from 1846–1847, confirming the continuity of the coastline and contributing to the determination that Boothia was a peninsula rather than an island. Rae's meticulous sledge and foot travel, combined with astronomical observations for latitude and longitude, produced accurate charts of the southern Boothia Peninsula and Committee Bay shores. These mappings advanced the geographical knowledge essential for Northwest Passage searches, totaling over 625 miles of new Arctic coastline delineated across his Boothia efforts.[19][15]

Northern Coast Explorations

In 1848, Rae joined Sir John Richardson's overland expedition from Sault Ste. Marie, reaching the Mackenzie River mouth after 96 days of travel by canoe and foot, before surveying the Arctic coast eastward to Cape Kendall near Dolphin and Union Strait.[5] Ice prevented crossing to Wollaston Land, leading the party to winter at Fort Confidence on Great Bear Lake, with no major new coastline mapped but the discovery of the Rae River noted.[15] The expedition returned in spring 1849, confirming existing geographical features while advancing limited surveys amid the primary goal of locating Franklin's lost ships.[2]

1848–1849 Overland Traverse

The traverse emphasized efficient Arctic travel techniques Rae had honed, including snowshoeing and light provisioning, covering rugged terrain from the fur trade routes westward to the Arctic Ocean shores.[15] Despite harsh conditions and one fatality—trapper Albert One-Eye—the effort mapped incremental coastal details east of the Mackenzie Delta, integrating with prior explorations by Dease and Simpson.[5] Rae's observations reinforced the continuity of the mainland coastline, though full shoreline delineation awaited subsequent trips.[15]

1850–1851 Completion of Arctic Shoreline Mapping

Departing Fort Confidence on April 25, 1851, Rae led a small party across Dolphin and Union Strait on spring ice, reaching Victoria Island to survey its southern and eastern coasts up to Collinson Peninsula.[5] Over 42 days, the group traveled 824 miles by sledge and boat, charting approximately 630 miles of previously unmapped shoreline and confirming no navigable strait separated Victoria Island from Wollaston Peninsula.[15] This work filled critical gaps in Arctic cartography, verifying land connections that clarified the Northwest Passage's feasibility and contributing to Rae's cumulative survey of about 1,800 miles of northern coasts across his expeditions.[5] The surveys relied on Inuit guidance for safe routes and provisions, underscoring Rae's adoption of indigenous methods for sustained travel.[15]

1848–1849 Overland Traverse

In 1848, the Hudson's Bay Company sponsored an overland expedition led by Sir John Richardson, with John Rae serving as surgeon and second-in-command, to search for traces of Sir John Franklin's missing expedition while surveying the uncharted Arctic coastline between the Mackenzie River and Coppermine River. The party, consisting of Rae, Richardson, and four other men, departed from the mouth of the Mackenzie River in May 1848, traveling eastward by boat and on foot along the Beaufort Sea coast and into Coronation Gulf.[5] They reached Cape Kendall, approximately 200 miles east of the Mackenzie Delta, where heavy pack ice blocked further progress toward Dolphin and Union Strait, forcing a halt to the coastal survey.[5] The expedition faced severe challenges from Arctic conditions, including unnavigable ice, shortages of game, and the need for rapid overland return marches using snowshoes and sledges pulled by men and dogs. No direct evidence of Franklin's ships or crews was encountered, though the party collected Inuit reports of distant white men, which yielded no conclusive leads.[5] By late 1848, the group retreated overland to Fort Confidence on Great Slave Lake, covering rugged terrain and confirming the absence of a navigable strait separating Wollaston Land from Victoria Island based on coastal observations.[5] In spring 1849, delayed by late thaw, Rae led a reduced team of three—including two Métis voyageurs—on a focused overland extension from Fort Confidence toward Wollaston Peninsula and Victoria Island, employing Inuit-style techniques such as dog-sled travel at night to minimize snow glare and igloo shelters for efficiency. Over 42 days, they traversed 824 miles, mapping 630 miles of previously unexplored coastline along the peninsula and island shores, and recovered fragments of European ship timber suggestive of prior naval presence but not linked to Franklin.[15] The traverse concluded in September 1849 with the party's return to Fort Confidence, having added critical geographical data to British charts without resolving the Franklin mystery.[5]

1850–1851 Completion of Arctic Shoreline Mapping

In the winter of 1850–1851, Rae wintered at Fort Confidence on the shore of Great Bear Lake, a post he had helped establish the previous year as a base for further Arctic surveys under Hudson's Bay Company directives from Governor George Simpson.[15][5] This location facilitated access to the Coppermine River, enabling Rae to prepare sledges, provisions, and a small crew of Indigenous guides and laborers experienced in overland travel.[20] The expedition's primary objective was to extend prior mappings by explorers such as Thomas Simpson and Peter Warren Dease, targeting the uncharted southwestern coast of Victoria Island to link continental surveys with known insular shorelines.[5] On April 25, 1851, Rae departed Fort Confidence on foot with two companions, two sledges, and five dogs, descending the Coppermine River to reach the Arctic coast by early May.[5] Crossing Dolphin and Union Strait on ice, the party advanced to Wollaston Peninsula, then proceeded eastward along the southern Victoria Island coastline, employing snowshoes, sleds, and later small boats for reconnaissance.[15] Over 42 days, they traversed 824 miles primarily by foot, surveying and charting 630 miles of previously unexplored terrain, including confirmation that no navigable strait separated Wollaston Land from Victoria Island.[15] En route to Cape Baring and beyond, Rae documented geological features, currents, and indigenous place names, producing sketch maps that integrated with Admiralty charts.[20] The expedition yielded two fragments of shipwrecked wood, though unlinked to specific vessels, and no traces of the Franklin expedition, which Rae noted in his dispatches without speculation.[15] Returning to Fort Confidence by mid-June via the Coppermine River, Rae's surveys filled critical gaps in the North American Arctic shoreline, completing over 90% of the continental coast from the Bering Strait eastward, as Simpson had tasked, and leaving only minor segments for naval confirmation.[15][5] This overland effort, reliant on Rae's adoption of Inuit sled techniques and lightweight provisioning, demonstrated the efficacy of fur-trade logistics over Royal Navy sail-dependent approaches for such remote mapping.[11] Later disputes arose when Captain Richard Collinson's naval tracing overlapped Rae's routes, but Rae's earlier documentation substantiated his priority in delineating the insular connections.[15]

Discovery of Franklin's Fate

1853–1854 Boothia Expedition

John Rae's 1853–1854 expedition was commissioned by the Hudson's Bay Company to complete the survey of the continental Arctic coastline, specifically the west coast of the Boothia Peninsula from the Castor and Pollux River northward toward Bellot Strait.[5] Rae also aimed to verify whether Boothia was a peninsula rather than an island.[5] Departing from York Factory on Hudson Bay in June 1853, Rae led a party of twelve men equipped with two boats.[5] The party ascended Chesterfield Inlet and spent ten days exploring the Quoich River upstream for approximately 335 kilometers to evaluate an overland route to the Back River, but ultimately proceeded by water to Repulse Bay.[21] They wintered at Repulse Bay from autumn 1853 to spring 1854, initially in tents before constructing snow houses, subsisting primarily on hunted game and fish.[5] On 31 March 1854, Rae departed Repulse Bay with four men and sledges, crossing the isthmus to Committee Bay, then proceeding via Pelly Bay to the Castor and Pollux River on Boothia's west coast.[15][5] Surveying efforts focused on mapping the previously unexplored shoreline northward, covering about 430 kilometers of new coastline over several weeks.[21] Harsh conditions, including soft snow and adverse weather, slowed sledge progress and forced abandonment of a full traverse, leaving roughly 150 to 240 kilometers south of Bellot Strait unsurveyed.[15][21] The expedition confirmed Boothia as a peninsula and identified a separating channel from King William Land.[15] Rae returned to Repulse Bay in May 1854 and reached York Factory by 31 August 1854.[5]

Inuit Testimonies and Physical Evidence

In April 1854, during his surveying expedition on the Boothia Peninsula, John Rae encountered Inuit hunters near the west coast of King William Island who provided detailed oral accounts of the Franklin expedition's demise. These testimonies, gathered primarily on April 21 and corroborated through further interviews in July and August at Repulse Bay, described approximately 40 white men observed in the spring of 1850 dragging a large boat laden with supplies southward over the ice along the western shore of King William Island, beyond the mouth of what is now known as the Back River.[22] The men were reported as emaciated and exhausted, with one described as a tall, stout, middle-aged officer who appeared to lead the group; the Inuit noted the party had abandoned two ships the previous autumn near the northwest tip of the island.[22] The accounts consistently indicated that the entire group perished from starvation and exposure, with bodies discovered scattered along the route in advanced states of decomposition and mutilation. Inuit witnesses reported finding kettles containing human flesh and boiled bones near the remains, alongside evidence of recent survival such as gunshot marks on birds and fresh kills into late May, suggesting a desperate final phase before total collapse.[22] Rae emphasized the reliability of these narratives, noting their uniformity across multiple informants whom he cross-examined separately, attributing the details to direct observations by Inuit who had encountered the sites between 1850 and 1852.[22] Supporting these testimonies, the Inuit produced numerous relics salvaged from the expedition's wreckage and camps, which Rae purchased for verification. Among them were silver tableware including forks and spoons engraved with initials such as "H.D.S.G." (identified as belonging to Henry D. S. Goodsir), "A. McD." (Alexander McDonald), and "J.S.P." (possibly James S. Peddie), as well as a pewter plate inscribed "Sir John Franklin, K.C.H."[22] Additional artifacts included fragments of telescopes, compasses, watches, double-barreled guns, and woodwork from the ships, many of which had been broken down by the Inuit for tools or trade; Rae noted the presence of British naval buttons and cloth, confirming European origin.[22] These items, handed over to the Hudson's Bay Company and subsequently the Admiralty, provided tangible physical evidence aligning with the Inuit descriptions of the party's route and abandonment of vessels.[23]

Controversies and Contemporary Backlash

Report on Cannibalism and Starvation

In April 1854, while conducting surveys west of Repulse Bay as part of his Boothia Peninsula expedition for the Hudson's Bay Company, John Rae encountered a group of Inuit who traded furs and provided oral testimonies about encounters with white men matching the description of Franklin's crew.[22] The Inuit recounted observing around 40 emaciated Europeans in the spring of 1850 near the Great Fish River (now Back River), dragging a heavy boat on a sledge southward while in a state of extreme starvation; many collapsed and died immediately upon reaching Inuit camps, prompting the survivors to beg for seal meat before perishing.[22] Subsequent Inuit accounts detailed discovering a large tent containing 30 to 40 skeletons clad in European clothing, along with a smaller camp of about 5 bodies nearby; evidence of cannibalism included thigh bones with flesh systematically cut away, vertebrae chopped through with axes, and kettles containing boiled human remains, indicating the men had resorted to consuming their deceased comrades to survive.[22] Rae assessed the testimonies as credible, noting the Inuit's consistent details across multiple independent sources and their lack of motive to fabricate stories, as they had traded peacefully with the starving men without hostility.[22] He emphasized that the reports aligned with the expedition's presumed route after abandoning their ice-trapped ships around 1850. Rae acquired physical relics from the Inuit, including six silver spoons and a fork engraved with initials such as "F.V." (likely Francis Crozier) and "H.D.S.G." (Henry D. S. Goodsir), as well as a silver plate fragment marked with an arrow crest matching British naval officers from the Erebus and Terror; these items, obtained in exchange for tobacco and ammunition, directly linked the remains to Franklin's 129-man party.[22] On July 29, 1854, Rae dispatched a formal letter from Repulse Bay to the Secretary of the Admiralty via the Hudson's Bay Company, summarizing the Inuit evidence and concluding that "the long-lost and unfortunate party under Sir John Franklin had met with a fate as melancholy and dreadful as it is possible to imagine," primarily through starvation followed by cannibalism among the remnants. [22] The report reached London in late September 1854 and was published in The Times on October 23, 1854, marking the first definitive public disclosure of the expedition's end after nine years of searches; Rae urged caution in accepting every detail but affirmed the core facts of starvation-induced desperation based on the relics and eyewitness consistency.[24] He received a £10,000 reward from Parliament for determining Franklin's fate, though the cannibalism revelation—drawn solely from indigenous sources—sparked immediate controversy by challenging prevailing assumptions of orderly British naval discipline in extremis.[22]

Attacks from British Intellectuals and Navy

Rae's report, dispatched from York Factory on 1 September 1854 and published in The Times on 23 October 1854, detailed Inuit accounts of the Franklin expedition's starvation, death, and subsequent cannibalism, supported by relics such as silverware bearing expedition members' initials.[25][22] This revelation clashed with prevailing Victorian ideals of British naval heroism and imperial superiority, prompting vehement denial from intellectuals and the naval establishment, who privileged assumptions of disciplined endurance over Rae's empirical evidence from direct interrogations and artifacts.[26][27] Charles Dickens spearheaded the intellectual assault in Household Words, publishing the first installment of "The Lost Arctic Voyagers" on 2 December 1854, in which he dismissed cannibalism as antithetical to the character of British officers, asserting that "no one who has not the vague, dreamy, and fanciful horror of what might be, can sympathise with the broad wake of the calm and confident English pride" in their explorers' fortitude.[28][29] A second article on 23 December 1854 intensified this, questioning Inuit credibility by portraying them as inherently untrustworthy "savages" prone to fabrication for gain, despite Rae's decade of successful Arctic travel using their methods.[29] Dickens' rhetoric, influenced by lobbying from Jane Franklin to safeguard her husband's reputation, reflected ethnocentric biases that devalued indigenous knowledge Rae had validated through consistent results, such as his efficient overland surveys contrasting naval failures.[30][31] The Royal Navy's institutional response amplified the controversy, as the suggestion of cannibalism impugned the service's honor and Franklin's legacy as a disciplined commander; establishment figures rejected Rae's reliance on oral testimonies and native-adapted techniques, favoring narratives of honorable death amid adversity.[32][33] Naval search parties dispatched post-1854, including those under Admiralty auspices, prioritized refuting the cannibalism claims over acceptance of Rae's findings, underscoring a commitment to preserving the expedition's mythic status despite the physical evidence of cut-marked bones and spoons he presented to the Admiralty.[26] This resistance, rooted in professional rivalry between Hudson's Bay Company pragmatists like Rae and rigid naval orthodoxy, delayed official acknowledgment of the report's accuracy until subsequent expeditions unearthed corroborating relics.[16]

Empirical Defense Against Ideological Denial

Archaeological excavations on King William Island in the 1980s provided the first physical corroboration of Rae's 1854 report on cannibalism among Franklin's crew, with anthropologist Owen Beattie identifying cut marks on a right femur shaft consistent with defleshing, alongside evidence of bone breakage for marrow extraction.[4] Further analysis of over 300 bone fragments recovered from sites associated with the expedition revealed that approximately one-third exhibited perimortem knife cuts indicative of systematic flesh removal, while many showed "pot polish"—surface wear from boiling in kettles to access nutrients—patterns aligning with end-stage survival cannibalism rather than sporadic acts.[34][35] Forensic examinations have since quantified the prevalence of these practices, noting that of 13 distinct remains from a single King William Island campsite, four bore multiple cut marks from tools used post-mortem to strip meat, supporting Inuit descriptions relayed by Rae of crews resorting to consuming their dead amid starvation.[36] In 2024, DNA analysis of a mandible fragment from the same region identified it as belonging to Captain James Fitzjames, second-in-command of HMS Erebus, with etched cut marks on the bone confirming it was defleshed and likely boiled after death, directly validating the sequence of desperation Rae documented from native testimonies.[37][38] These findings empirically refute the 19th-century ideological rejection of Rae's evidence, which prioritized narratives of unyielding British stoicism over indigenous accounts dismissed as unreliable; isotopic and trace element studies on the bones further indicate lead poisoning and scurvy as precursors to collapse, but the tool marks and fragmentation patterns irrefutably demonstrate cannibalism as a terminal adaptation, independent of cultural bias.[39][40] The consistency across sites—limb bones preferentially retained for processing—mirrors Rae's sourced reports of kettles containing human flesh, establishing causal links from nutritional failure to anthropophagy without reliance on contested Victorian-era denials.[41]

Later Career and Achievements

Return to HBC Duties

Following his return to York Factory on 31 August 1854 after the Boothia expedition, Rae sailed for England on 20 September to report the Franklin expedition's fate to the Admiralty and Hudson's Bay Company (HBC) officials.[5] Despite the ensuing public backlash in Britain over his findings—particularly the Inuit accounts of cannibalism—Rae continued serving the HBC in an official capacity as Chief Factor, a position he had held since 1850, managing fur trade operations and related administrative responsibilities across northern territories.[42][5] In July 1856, Rae received £8,000 from the British government as partial reward for his Franklin discoveries (£2,000 of which was distributed among his expedition companions), affirming the evidentiary value of his work despite elite opposition.[5] He retired from the HBC later that year, concluding over two decades of service that had combined medical, trading, and exploratory roles, including oversight of districts like the Mackenzie River region earlier in his career.[5][7] Early the following year, Rae provided expert testimony on the HBC's behalf to a British parliamentary select committee inquiring into Arctic affairs and company operations, leveraging his firsthand knowledge of northern logistics and Indigenous interactions.[5] This period marked a transition from active field and district management to winding down his commitments amid ongoing vindication of his empirical approach to exploration.

Additional Surveys and Infrastructure Contributions

Following his retirement from the Hudson's Bay Company in 1856, John Rae undertook several surveys aimed at facilitating transcontinental and transatlantic telegraph infrastructure. In 1860, at the behest of the Hudson's Bay Company, he conducted an overland survey for a proposed telegraph line linking Britain to America, traversing routes through Scotland, the Faroe Islands, Iceland, and Greenland to assess feasibility for Arctic connections.[5] [2] This effort contributed to early planning for overland alternatives to undersea cables, leveraging Rae's Arctic expertise despite the route's harsh conditions and logistical challenges.[12] In 1864, Rae led a detailed survey from St. Paul, Minnesota, to Quesnel, British Columbia, commissioned by the Hudson's Bay Company and Sir Edward Watkin to evaluate a telegraph route westward. Departing St. Paul on June 8, he proceeded via Fort Garry (reached June 15), the Carlton Trail, Fort Edmonton (arrived July 28), Tête Jaune Cache (August 22), and down the Fraser River to Quesnel (September 3), covering approximately 1,320 kilometers to Edmonton alone.[43] [5] The expedition encountered prairie fires, drought-induced water shortages, scarce timber in prairies, and rugged terrain along the Miette River and Yellowhead Pass, yet identified viable paths with sufficient timber resources in forested sections for pole erection.[43] Although materials were procured, the project was abandoned after Western Union's competing Russian-American line advanced in 1865.[43] Rae extended these efforts in 1865 with another Hudson's Bay Company survey for a telegraph line from St. Paul to Vancouver Island, routing through Upper Fort Garry, the Carlton Trail, Fort Edmonton, Yellowhead Pass, the Fraser River to Fort Alexandria, and onward to Victoria.[5] These North American surveys mapped key interior routes across prairies and mountains, providing critical geographic data that informed later Canadian telegraph and transportation developments, even as the immediate schemes faltered due to geopolitical shifts.[2]

Death and Posthumous Legacy

Final Years and Personal Life

Following his retirement from the Hudson's Bay Company in 1856, Rae resided in Hamilton, Upper Canada, from 1857 to 1859, where he practiced medicine and lived near his brothers Richard Bempede Johnstone Honeyman and Thomas.[5][8] On 25 January 1860, he married Catherine Jane Alicia Thompson, daughter of Major George Ash Thompson, in Toronto; the couple had no children.[5][8] Rae and his wife initially retired to Orkney, renting Berstane House east of Kirkwall for approximately two years around 1864–1866, before settling in London, where he maintained frequent visits to his native islands.[44][8] In London, they resided at 4 Addison Gardens, Kensington, reflecting a quieter domestic life after decades of Arctic fieldwork and surveys.[44] Rae died from an aneurysm on 22 July 1893 at his London home, at the age of 79.[5][44][8] His body was returned to Orkney aboard the paddle steamer St Magnus and buried in the graveyard of St Magnus Cathedral, Kirkwall, with a memorial erected inside the cathedral the following year by public subscription; his widow survived him until 1919.[44][8]

Initial Overshadowing and Later Honors

Rae's announcement on July 23, 1854, of the Franklin expedition's fate, based on Inuit testimonies including evidence of cannibalism, provoked intense backlash from British elites unwilling to accept such a grim and culturally unpalatable conclusion.[29] Figures like Charles Dickens and Lady Jane Franklin publicly denounced the reports, with Dickens authoring articles in Household Words that dismissed Inuit accounts as unreliable fabrications and portrayed Rae's reliance on indigenous knowledge as a flaw.[29] [2] Although Rae secured the £10,000 Admiralty reward for providing definitive intelligence on the expedition in October 1854, the controversy smeared his reputation and overshadowed his concurrent mapping of the final 160-mile segment of the Northwest Passage's northern coast.[3] [2] This public ostracism extended to formal recognition, as Rae became the only prominent Arctic explorer of the Victorian era not knighted, despite his pioneering techniques and endurance that enabled efficient overland travel with minimal provisions. [16] His contributions to Arctic surveying for the Hudson's Bay Company continued until his retirement in 1856, but broader acclaim eluded him amid the prevailing sentiment favoring naval officers' narratives over those incorporating native insights.[45] Rae did receive some honors during his lifetime, including the Royal Geographical Society's Founder's Gold Medal in 1852 for his 1846–1847 and 1851 surveys of Boothia Peninsula and Victoria Island coastlines.[9] Posthumously, following his death on June 22, 1893, recognition grew sporadically; a statue was unveiled in Stromness, Orkney, on September 29, 2013, commemorating his achievements.[46] In 2014, a plaque honoring him as the discoverer of the Northwest Passage's final link was installed at Westminster Abbey.[47] Further posthumous tributes included the Freedom of Orkney awarded on October 1, 2017, and honorary status as a chartered surveyor from the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors in 2018.[48] [49]

Modern Vindication Through Archaeology

In the late 20th century, forensic archaeological investigations of skeletal remains from King William Island provided physical evidence corroborating the Inuit testimonies relayed by John Rae in 1854 regarding cannibalism among the Franklin expedition's survivors. During expeditions led by anthropologist Owen Beattie in 1981 and 1982, analysis of bones recovered from sites associated with the expedition revealed longitudinal cut marks on long bones, consistent with defleshing using metal tools, as well as evidence of bone breakage for marrow extraction. These findings marked the first direct osteological confirmation of cannibalism, aligning with Rae's report of mutilated bodies and utensils containing human flesh observed by Inuit hunters.[50][4] Subsequent examinations in the 1990s, including those by Ann Keenleyside and colleagues, documented cut marks on approximately one-quarter of the 13 distinct skeletal elements from a single site on the island's northwest coast, with additional signs of "pot polish"—abrasion and smoothing from boiling in kettles—indicating systematic processing of remains for consumption. Such modifications were absent on pre-contact Inuit remains from the region, ruling out cultural practices unrelated to the expedition's desperation. These results refuted earlier dismissals of Rae's account by British figures like Charles Dickens, who had attributed Inuit reports to fabrication, and instead validated the empirical reliability of indigenous oral histories when cross-verified with physical evidence.[4][34] Recent advances in DNA analysis have further strengthened this vindication. In 2024, researchers from the University of Waterloo and Lakehead University identified a jawbone fragment from the same King William Island site as belonging to Captain James Fitzjames, second-in-command on HMS Erebus, through mitochondrial DNA matching with a known descendant and genealogical records. The bone exhibited multiple cut marks from a sharp implement, indicating it was defleshed post-mortem, making Fitzjames the first specifically identified victim of cannibalism in the expedition. This discovery, part of ongoing work by archaeologist Douglas Stenton, underscores that end-stage starvation drove even senior officers to such extremes, directly supporting Rae's description of survivors resorting to "the lowest degradation" as told by Inuit witnesses.[39][37][38]
