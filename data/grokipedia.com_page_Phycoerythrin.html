Phycoerythrin

Phycoerythrin (PE) is a red protein-pigment complex from the light-harvesting phycobiliprotein family, present in cyanobacteria, [1] red algae[2] and cryptophytes, [3] accessory to the main chlorophyll pigments responsible for photosynthesis. The red pigment is due to the prosthetic group, phycoerythrobilin, which gives phycoerythrin its red color.[4] Phycoerythrins occur in various forms, such as C-phycoerythrin in cyanobacteria, B- and R-phycoerythrin in red algae (often with a γ subunit), and phycoerythrin 545 in cryptophytes, differing in chromophore composition and spectral properties.[5] Like all phycobiliproteins, it is composed of a protein part covalently binding chromophores called phycobilins. The primary chromophore in the phycoerythrin family is phycoerythrobilin, a linear tetrapyrrole molecule.[6] In cyanobacteria and red algae, phycoerythrobilin serves as a light-harvesting pigment in phycobilisomes, along with other phycobilins such as phycocyanobilin.[7] Phycoerythrins are composed of (αβ) monomers, usually organised in a disk-shaped trimer (αβ)3 or hexamer (αβ)6 (the latter being the functional unit of the antenna rods). Some phycoerythrins, such as R-phycoerythrin from red algae, also contain a third type of subunit, the γ chain.[2]

Phycobilisomes

Phycobiliproteins (like phycoerythrin) usually form rods of stacked disks in phycobilisomes.[8] Phycobiliproteins are part of huge light harvesting antennae protein complexes called phycobilisomes. In red algae they are anchored to the stromal side of thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts, whereas in cryptophytes phycobilisomes are reduced and (phycobiliprotein 545 PE545 molecules here) are densely packed inside the lumen of thylakoids. [3][7] In phycobilisomes of cyanobacteria and red algae, phycoerythrin is located in the distal (outermost) positions of the peripheral rods, where it absorbs green light and transfers energy inward to phycocyanin, allophycocyanin, and ultimately to chlorophyll.[7] Phycoerythrin is an accessory pigment to the main chlorophyll pigments responsible for photosynthesis. The light energy is captured by phycoerythrin and is then passed on to the reaction centre chlorophyll pair, most of the time via the phycobiliproteins phycocyanin and allophycocyanin.[7]

Structural characteristics

Phycoerythrins except phycoerythrin 545 (PE545) are composed of (αβ\alpha \betaαβ) monomers assembled into disc-shaped (αβ\alpha \betaαβ)_6 hexamers or (αβ\alpha \betaαβ)_3 trimers with 3-fold or 32 symmetry and enclosing central channel. In phycobilisomes (PBS) each trimer or hexamer contains at least one linker protein located in central channel. B-phycoerythrin (B-PE) and R-phycoerythrin (R-PE) from red algae in addition to α\alphaα and β\betaβ chains have a third, γ\gammaγ subunit contributing both linker and light-harvesting functions, because it bears chromophores.[2] R-phycoerythrin is predominantly produced by red algae. The protein is made up of at least three different subunits and varies according to the species of algae that produces it. The subunit structure of the most common R-PE is (αβ\alpha \betaαβ)_6γ\gammaγ. The α\alphaα subunit has two phycoerythrobilins (PEB), the β\betaβ subunit has 2 PEB and one phycourobilin (PUB), while the γ\gammaγ subunits vary: e.g., 2 PEB + 3 PUB (γ33\gamma ^{33}γ33) or similar.[2][9] The molecular weight of R-PE is 250,000 daltons. Crystal structures available in the Protein Data Bank [10] contain in one (αβ\alpha \betaαβ)_2 or (αβγ\alpha \beta \gammaαβγ)_2 asymmetric unit of different phycoerythrins: Phycoerythrobilin is the typical chromophore in phycoerythrin. It is similar to porphyrin of chlorophyll for example, but tetrapyrrole is linear, not closed into ring with metal ion in the middle. The red algae Gracilaria contains R-phycoerythrin.

Spectral characteristics

Absorption peaks in the visible light spectrum are measured at 495 and 545/566 nm, depending on the chromophores bound and the considered organism. A strong emission peak exists at 575 ± 10 nm. (Phycoerythrin absorbs slightly blue-green/yellowish light and emits slightly orange-yellow light.)

R-Phycoerythrin variations

R-phycoerythrin (R-PE) exhibits variations in light absorption and emission efficiency among different red algal species, influenced by factors such as chromophore composition and environmental adaptations.[2][5] For instance, R-PE from Porphyridium cruentum shows absorption maxima at approximately 565 nm, while forms from deeper-water species like Antarctic red algae may have shifted spectra to better capture blue light penetrating greater depths.[12] These spectral differences are thought to adapt algae to their typical positions in the water column, where light quality and intensity vary with depth; red algae in deeper waters often have higher phycoerythrin content to utilize green-to-blue wavelengths (450–570 nm). Studies have compared fluorescence characteristics of R-PE from various sources for potential use in fluorescent labeling, noting small efficiency differences that could impact applications in microscopy and flow cytometry.[18] Detailed practical uses are discussed in the applications section.

Practical applications

R-Phycoerythrin (also known as PE or R-PE) is useful in the laboratory for labeling antibodies, most often for flow cytometry. It is also used in microarray assays, ELISAs, and other applications that require high sensitivity and brightness despite limited photostability.[19] Its use is limited in immunofluorescence microscopy due to its rapid photobleaching characteristics. There are also other types of phycoerythrins, such as B-phycoerythrin, which have slightly different spectral properties. B-Phycoerythrin absorbs strongly at about 545 nm (slightly yellowish green) and emits strongly at 572 nm (yellow) and could be better suited for some instruments. B-Phycoerythrin may also be less "sticky" than R-phycoerythrin and contributes less to background signal due to nonspecific binding in certain applications. However, R-PE is much more commonly available as an antibody conjugate.
