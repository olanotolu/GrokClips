Phyllis Frelich


Phyllis Frelich (February 29, 1944 – April 10, 2014) was a deaf American actress renowned for originating the role of Sarah Norman in the Broadway production of Children of a Lesser God, earning her the 1980 Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play and making her the first deaf performer to win the honor.[1][2][3]
Born in Devils Lake, North Dakota, as the eldest of nine children—all deaf, like their parents—Frelich attended the North Dakota School for the Deaf, graduating in 1962 before pursuing acting professionally.[4][5][6]
A founding member of the National Theatre of the Deaf, she helped pioneer opportunities for deaf actors on stage and screen, performing in productions that integrated sign language and advocating for authentic representation of deafness in theater.[7][8]
Frelich's portrayal in Children of a Lesser God, written by Mark Medoff in collaboration with her, highlighted themes of communication barriers between deaf and hearing individuals, contributing to broader cultural awareness of deaf experiences.[4][1]

Early Life and Education

Family Background

Phyllis Frelich was born on February 29, 1944, in Devils Lake, North Dakota, to deaf parents Philip Frelich and Esther Frelich (née Dockter).[9][10] Her father worked as a typesetter for the local newspaper, while her mother was a seamstress.[11][12] Both parents were alumni of the North Dakota School for the Deaf and active leaders in the local Deaf community, reflecting a family deeply embedded in Deaf culture.[13][14] Frelich was the eldest of nine siblings, all of whom were also deaf, making theirs a profoundly Deaf household without hearing members.[9][8][7] This familial deafness, spanning parents and all children, underscored the genetic and environmental factors influencing their shared experiences, with the entire family attending the North Dakota School for the Deaf for education.[8][14] The Frelich family's immersion in Deaf institutions from an early age fostered a strong foundation in sign language and community ties that later informed Frelich's advocacy and career.[13]

Schooling and Early Influences

Phyllis Frelich, born deaf in Devils Lake, North Dakota, attended the North Dakota School for the Deaf from an early age, following in the footsteps of her parents who were alumni of the institution.[9][5] She graduated from the school in 1962, during a period when sign language faced prejudice even within deaf educational settings, as oralist methods emphasizing speech over signing were prevalent.[15] Despite these challenges, Frelich displayed an early dramatic flair in the 1950s at the school, though opportunities for deaf students in performance arts were scarce.[13] Frelich's interest in acting deepened at Gallaudet College (now Gallaudet University) in Washington, D.C., where she enrolled after high school and earned a degree in library science.[9][5] There, she actively participated in theater productions, which provided her first substantive exposure to stage performance and honed her skills in deaf theater, contrasting with the limited outlets available in her North Dakota schooling.[8] These experiences at Gallaudet served as a pivotal influence, fostering her ambition to pursue acting professionally despite systemic barriers for deaf performers.[13]

Theater Career

Founding Role in National Theatre of the Deaf

Phyllis Frelich was recruited as a founding member of the National Theatre of the Deaf (NTD) in 1967, during her senior year at Gallaudet College. David Hays, a hearing set designer who initiated the company's formation, visited Gallaudet to identify talented deaf performers after observing student productions; he specifically selected Frelich following her onstage appearance, recognizing her potential to help establish the first professional theater ensemble dedicated to deaf actors.[8][16] The NTD, headquartered in Waterford, Connecticut, sought to create accessible performances by simultaneously employing American Sign Language and spoken English, thereby challenging the exclusion of deaf individuals from mainstream theater.[11] As a charter member, Frelich contributed to the company's early development by participating in its inaugural training and rehearsals, which emphasized ensemble work blending deaf and hearing artists to foster authentic representation. She made her professional debut with NTD in 1968, performing in productions that toured nationally and internationally, thus helping to demonstrate the viability of sign language-integrated theater.[14] Her sustained presence as a core performer through the company's first 12 years solidified NTD's reputation, providing empirical evidence of deaf actors' capabilities in professional settings and influencing subsequent advocacy for inclusive casting practices.[5][17] Frelich's foundational efforts addressed the prior scarcity of outlets for deaf talent, enabling the company to secure federal grants and perform at venues like the Kennedy Center by the early 1970s.[14]

Breakthrough with Children of a Lesser God

Phyllis Frelich originated the role of Sarah Norman, a rebellious deaf woman who rejects spoken language, in Mark Medoff's play Children of a Lesser God, which premiered on Broadway at the Longacre Theatre on March 30, 1980. The production, directed by Gordon Davidson, featured Frelich opposite John Rubinstein as James Leeds, a hearing speech pathologist attempting to bridge their worlds through communication and romance. Medoff developed the script in close collaboration with Frelich, drawing from her experiences as a deaf performer to authentically portray Sarah's isolation and defiance.[18] Frelich's performance, delivered entirely in American Sign Language with voice-over interpretation, received critical acclaim for its raw emotional intensity and challenge to hearing audiences' perceptions of deafness.[9] The play ran for 887 performances over two years, closing on May 16, 1982, and marked a significant milestone in theatrical representation of deaf characters by a deaf actor.[14] Her portrayal earned her the 1980 Tony Award for Best Actress in a Play, making her the first deaf performer to win in a leading acting category.[18] The production also secured the Tony for Best Play, with Rubinstein winning Best Actor.[19] This breakthrough elevated Frelich's career from regional and deaf theater to mainstream recognition, highlighting the capabilities of deaf artists and influencing subsequent casting practices in theater and film.[6] The play's success underscored the demand for authentic deaf representation, paving the way for works like the 1986 film adaptation starring Marlee Matlin, who succeeded Frelich in the role and won an Academy Award.[20]

Subsequent Theater Roles and Productions

Following her Tony Award-winning performance in Children of a Lesser God, Frelich continued collaborating with playwright Mark Medoff, starring as a deaf writer in the world premiere of The Hands of Its Enemy at the Doolittle Theatre in Los Angeles in 1986, opposite Richard Dreyfuss.[21] The production later transferred Off-Broadway to the Hudson Guild Theatre, where it ran in November 1986, emphasizing themes of artistic integrity and personal conflict without centering deafness as the primary narrative device.[22] In the early 2000s, Frelich returned to Broadway in integrated productions featuring American Sign Language (ASL). She performed multiple roles—Sally Phelps, Miss Watson, and ensemble member—in the 2003 Roundabout Theatre Company revival of Big River, a musical adaptation of The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn that incorporated deaf performers and bilingual ASL-English elements to enhance accessibility and representation. The revival ran from July 24 to September 21, 2003, at the Roundabout's venue, highlighting Frelich's versatility in ensemble work.[23] Frelich's final Broadway appearance came in Medoff's Prymate in 2004, where she portrayed Esther, a primatologist involved in experiments with ape sign language communication, alongside roles as ASL master to support the production's linguistic elements.[24] The play previewed on May 5, 2004, but closed after a limited run of three performances on May 8, amid mixed reviews critiquing its thematic execution on human-animal boundaries and ethics.[25] These later roles underscored Frelich's ongoing commitment to theater that bridged deaf and hearing worlds through innovative staging and collaboration.[26]

Film and Television Work

Notable Appearances

Frelich portrayed Janice Ryder, a deaf mother dealing with generational conflicts in a hearing world, in the 1985 Hallmark Hall of Fame television movie Love Is Never Silent.[6] The production, based on a novel by Joanne Greenberg, highlighted deaf family experiences and earned critical attention for its authentic representation.[27] In 1989, she played Amanda Wingfield in the television movie Bridge to Silence, adapting themes from Tennessee Williams' The Glass Menagerie to explore mother-daughter tensions involving deafness.[28] Frelich's performance emphasized communication barriers between deaf and hearing characters.[29] Her role as Sally in the 2008 CBS television movie Sweet Nothing in My Ear, an adaptation of a play about a deaf-hearing couple facing a medical crisis, drew on her advocacy for deaf visibility in media.[30] The film featured sign language prominently and starred Marlee Matlin alongside Frelich. On series television, Frelich had a recurring role as Sister Sarah on Santa Barbara from 1987 to 1988, appearing in 31 episodes as a deaf nun involved in community storylines.[31] She guest-starred as Dr. Lisa Parks on ER in 1994, depicting a deaf physician in a high-stakes hospital setting.[4] Additional guest appearances included Mrs. Betty Grissom on CSI: Crime Scene Investigation, Barbara Collins on Hunter in 1984, and Joan Cugell on Spenser: For Hire in 1986.[31][28] In film, Frelich appeared in Children on Their Birthdays (2002) and Santa Fe (1997), smaller roles that extended her screen presence beyond theater.[29] These credits underscored her efforts to secure casting for deaf actors in diverse narratives.[16]

Challenges in Casting

Despite her pioneering success in theater, Phyllis Frelich faced persistent barriers in film and television casting, where deaf performers were infrequently selected for roles, particularly those involving deafness, leading to a scarcity of opportunities that contrasted sharply with her stage achievements. This frustration with limited parts for deaf actresses directly inspired playwright Mark Medoff, who crafted Children of a Lesser God around her experiences after hearing her complaints about role availability.[1][8] A prominent example occurred in 1989 when Frelich protested the casting of hearing actress Cynthia Nixon as a deaf character in the Equalizer episode "Silent Fury," which depicted a romantically involved deaf woman at a deaf center. Frelich described the decision as "insulting and demeaning discrimination," questioning why producers did not adequately search for deaf talent, and likened it to "hiring a white actress to paint her face black and pretend to be black." She demanded that CBS withhold the episode from airing and rallied viewers to contact network executives.[32] CBS executive James McAdams responded that the production had sought but failed to find a suitable deaf actress for the principal role, while including deaf performers as extras and in supporting parts, citing efforts to balance ethnic representation and hire handicapped individuals where possible. This incident reflected wider industry practices, including logistical hurdles like on-set communication accommodations and a tendency to prioritize hearing actors trained in rudimentary sign language over authentically deaf performers capable of nuanced expression.[32] Frelich's own screen work remained sparse, with notable credits including an Emmy-nominated portrayal of a deaf mother in the 1985 television movie Love Is Never Silent and guest appearances on series such as Barney Miller, Santa Barbara, ER, and CSI: Crime Scene Investigation. These limited engagements underscored the ongoing challenge of breaking into Hollywood, where deaf actors often supplemented income through teaching rather than consistent acting jobs.[11][6][26]

Advocacy and Contributions to Deaf Community

Push for Authentic Representation

Frelich championed the casting of deaf performers in deaf roles to ensure cultural and linguistic authenticity in media and theater. As a founding member of the National Theatre of the Deaf, established in 1967, she contributed to an ensemble that prominently featured deaf actors using American Sign Language, thereby introducing hearing audiences to deaf expressive traditions and countering exclusionary industry norms.[7][5] In March 1989, Frelich led a public protest against the casting of hearing actress Cynthia Nixon as a deaf woman in the The Equalizer episode "Silent Fury," labeling the choice discriminatory and comparable to applying blackface for roles requiring racial authenticity. She urged deaf viewers via the Silent Network to contact CBS executives and halt the broadcast until a deaf actor was selected, highlighting audition rejections faced by qualified deaf performers despite producers' claims of exhaustive searches.[32] Frelich's advocacy extended to institutional influence; in 1991, she became the first deaf individual elected to the Screen Actors Guild board, advocating for policies that expanded opportunities for deaf talent amid persistent underrepresentation.[11] She critiqued mainstream depictions of deaf characters as perpetually angry, bitter, or victimized, insisting on stories authored by deaf creators to capture the community's identity as a linguistic and cultural minority rather than solely a disabled group.[13]

Educational Workshops and SAG Involvement

Frelich was elected in 1991 as the first deaf member of the Screen Actors Guild (SAG) board of directors, joining the 90-member body responsible for the organization's highest policy-making functions.[33] In this role, she advocated for improved representation and opportunities for deaf performers in film and television, emphasizing authentic casting over hearing actors portraying deaf characters.[11] Her involvement extended to organizing and leading workshops and classes through SAG, focused on training deaf actors in professional techniques and addressing barriers to entry in the industry.[15] Beyond SAG, Frelich conducted educational workshops on acting and performance at colleges and universities, leveraging her experience from the National Theatre of the Deaf to mentor emerging talent, including deaf students. These sessions often highlighted the integration of American Sign Language in theater and the challenges of cross-cultural communication in ensemble work. In 2008, for instance, she collaborated with the University of Houston's School of Theatre & Dance to deliver specialized instruction, contributing to their programs in theater education and dance certification.[34] Her efforts as a lecturer and keynote speaker further amplified these workshops, promoting first-hand insights into deaf artistry drawn from her own career milestones.[15]

Personal Life and Death

Marriage and Family

Phyllis Frelich married scenic designer Robert Steinberg in 1968, a union that lasted 46 years until her death in 2014.[12] Steinberg, who has full hearing, collaborated professionally with Frelich on theater productions, including work with Deaf West Theatre.[35] The couple first met in Rhode Island, where Steinberg was employed as a set designer, and their relationship influenced elements of the play Children of a Lesser God, which playwright Mark Medoff drew from Frelich's experiences in a mixed-hearing marriage.[15] Frelich and Steinberg had two sons, Reuben and Joshua. Reuben pursued a career as a cinematographer, while Joshua became a musician.[18] The family resided primarily in California, with the sons growing up amid Frelich's acting career and advocacy work.[26] Frelich herself came from a large deaf family in Devils Lake, North Dakota, as the eldest of nine children born to deaf parents—her father a typesetter and her mother a seamstress—all of whom were deaf.[18] This familial context shaped her early life but contrasted with her own household, where communication bridged hearing and deaf dynamics through sign language and spoken word.[11]

Illness and Final Years

Frelich developed progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP), a rare degenerative brain disorder that impairs balance, eye movements, and mobility, with no known cure or effective treatments.[1][12] Her husband, Robert Steinberg, confirmed the diagnosis and noted its progressive nature, which ultimately led to her decline.[11][18] She spent her final years at her home in Temple City, California, where the disease confined her.[36] Frelich died there on April 10, 2014, at the age of 70, succumbing to complications from PSP.[1][12] The condition, also fatal to actor Dudley Moore in 2002, underscores its severity among high-profile cases.[18]

Legacy and Recognition

Major Awards and Honors

Frelich won the Tony Award for Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Play on June 8, 1980, for originating the role of Sarah Norman in Children of a Lesser God, marking her as the first deaf actor to receive the honor.[18][1] The Broadway production, which opened on March 30, 1980, at the Longacre Theatre, also earned the Tony for Best Play that year.[37] She received nominations for the Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actress in a Play and the Outer Critics Circle Award for Outstanding Debut Performance, both in 1980, for the same role.[2] In recognition of her contributions to North Dakota's cultural heritage, Frelich was awarded the Theodore Roosevelt Rough Rider Award by Governor Arthur A. Link on May 1, 1981, one of the state's highest honors for distinguished natives.[5][8]

Influence on Deaf Performing Arts

Phyllis Frelich co-founded the National Theatre of the Deaf (NTD) in 1967, serving as a core member and performer in its early productions, which established the first professional ensemble dedicated to showcasing deaf artists through American Sign Language (ASL) integrated with spoken word and visual storytelling.[7][5] This troupe toured nationally and internationally, performing works that highlighted deaf culture and capabilities, thereby elevating ASL as a legitimate theatrical medium and challenging hearing-centric norms in performing arts.[9] Frelich remained a mainstay in NTD for its first 12 years, contributing to over a dozen productions that trained subsequent generations of deaf performers.[17] Her starring role as Sarah Norman in Children of a Lesser God (Broadway debut March 1980), written specifically for her by Mark Medoff, marked a breakthrough by centering a deaf protagonist's perspective on communication, identity, and relationships, without relying on hearing actors for deaf roles.[6][20] The play's success, including six Tony Awards, amplified deaf narratives to mainstream audiences, fostering greater awareness of deaf experiences and pressuring the industry toward authentic casting.[38] Frelich's Tony win for Best Actress—the first for a deaf performer—symbolized validation of deaf talent on par with hearing counterparts, inspiring expanded opportunities in professional theater.[7][9] Frelich's career emphasized ASL's expressive potential in performance, advocating against lip-reading or voice-only portrayals that diluted deaf authenticity, which influenced subsequent productions like Deaf West Theatre's ASL-infused revivals.[39] Her work catalyzed a shift toward inclusive practices, where deaf actors lead roles depicting their own community, reducing stereotypical representations and promoting cultural preservation in arts.[20] This legacy persists in modern deaf-led ensembles that prioritize visual language and deaf-directed narratives.[39]
