Al-Fashaga District

The Al-Fashaga District is a fertile lowland administrative district in Ethiopia's Amhara Region, situated along the border with Sudan's Gedaref State and encompassing a disputed triangle of approximately 260 square kilometers prized for its arable black cotton soils supporting rainfed cultivation of sesame, sorghum, and other crops.[1][2] The area's agricultural productivity, particularly high-value white sesame used in exports to the Arabian Gulf, has made it economically vital to Ethiopian farmers, predominantly Amhara settlers who established large-scale farming since the 1990s under historical Sudanese land leases.[2][3] A longstanding territorial dispute traces to differing interpretations of the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, with Ethiopia asserting sovereignty up to the Atbarah River based on effective control since the mid-20th century, while Sudan claims the land eastward of the river as its territory.[1][4] Tensions escalated in late 2020 when Sudanese Armed Forces advanced into the region, establishing military outposts and bridges across the Atbarah River, displacing thousands of Ethiopian residents and seizing up to 95% of the area by 2022 amid Ethiopia's internal Tigray conflict.[2][1] Clashes, including one in 2021 that killed at least 20 troops, have disrupted cross-border trade and heightened regional instability, though Ethiopia has since adopted a strategy of restraint while maintaining claims.[5][6]

Geography

Location and Physical Features

The Al-Fashaga District, commonly referred to as the Al-Fashaga Triangle, is a disputed border territory located at the confluence of Sudan, Ethiopia, and Eritrea. It lies along the international boundary between northeastern Ethiopia's Amhara Regionâ€”specifically the northwest of the regionâ€”and eastern Sudan's Al-Qadarif (Gedaref) State, proximate to the tripoint with Eritrea. The area encompasses approximately 250 square kilometers of lowland terrain, forming a triangular shape defined by historical colonial demarcations.[7][3][4] Physically, Al-Fashaga features flat alluvial plains characterized by fertile black cotton soils ideal for mechanized agriculture. The landscape is naturally irrigated by three major rivers: the Setit (or Teseney) to the north, the Atbara to the east, and the TekezÃ© (a tributary of the Atbara) to the west, which surround the district on most sides except the direct Ethiopia-Sudan border line. These hydrological features contribute to the region's high agricultural productivity, supporting crops such as sesame and sorghum, though the terrain's flatness also renders it vulnerable to seasonal flooding.[3][8][9]

Climate and Agricultural Potential

The Al-Fashaga District lies within a tropical savanna climate zone, marked by a pronounced single rainy season from June to October, during which precipitation follows a south-to-north gradient with heavier and more prolonged rains in the southern portions. The dry season dominates the remainder of the year, with reliance on riverine sources for water. This seasonal pattern supports rainfed agriculture but exposes farming to variability in onset, duration, and intensity of rains. Soils in the district predominantly comprise heavy-cracking black vertisols, which retain moisture effectively and exhibit high fertility due to their rich organic content and clay composition, rendering them ideal for mechanized and smallholder cultivation. These vertisols, combined with alluvial deposits from bordering rivers, enable robust crop productivity without extensive amendments in most areas. The region's agricultural potential stems from its expansive 415,116 hectares of land, of which approximately 75.5% (313,549 hectares) was dedicated to farming by 2020, including 302,475 hectares under formal mechanized schemes. Naturally watered by the Setit, Atbara, and Basalam rivers, Al-Fashaga sustains diverse rainfed and irrigated systems, yielding high outputs of cash crops like sesameâ€”a key export commodityâ€”and food staples such as sorghum and millet, supplemented by cotton, sunflowers, chickpeas, lentils, and riverbank horticulture. Intensification through tractors, harvesters, and limited irrigation has amplified yields, positioning the district as a vital breadbasket in Sudan's Gedaref State amid broader semi-arid constraints.[9][3]

Demographics

Population and Ethnic Composition

The Al-Fashaga District, spanning approximately 250 square kilometers of fertile borderland, supports a population primarily engaged in agriculture, though precise demographic data remains elusive due to the area's contested sovereignty, recurrent clashes, and lack of comprehensive censuses. Settlement patterns reflect seasonal and semi-permanent farming communities rather than dense urban centers, with density likely low compared to broader Gedaref State, which hosts 2.6 million people overall.[10][11] Sudanese inhabitants, who form the longstanding resident base, consist mainly of local farmers and pastoralists from Arab tribes prevalent in eastern Sudan, such as those involved in mechanized sesame and sorghum cultivation. These groups have historically managed large-scale operations in the region, particularly in the "Lesser Al-Fashaga" areas, amid a backdrop of ethnic diversity in neighboring Gedaref and Kassala states that includes Arab, Beja, and immigrant elements. Sudanese authorities assert these communities' indigenous ties, predating significant external influxes, and have prioritized their land access following military assertions of control in December 2020.[9][12] Ethiopian settlers, predominantly from the Amhara ethnic groupâ€”Ethiopia's second-largest and historically influential populationâ€”began substantial migration into Al-Fashaga in the mid-1990s, attracted by the black soil's suitability for cash crops like sesame. These Amhara farmers established permanent and semi-permanent holdings, often paying taxes to Ethiopian regional authorities and comprising a notable portion of cultivators prior to 2020 expulsions by Sudanese forces. Amhara presence persisted through militias and cross-border activities, contributing to ongoing ethnic tensions, though exact numbers of settlers remain undocumented amid fluid displacement. Sudanese-Ethiopian agreements, such as a proposed 2008 zoning that allocated 25% of land to each side's farmers, underscored this dual composition before escalating disputes disrupted implementation.[7][13][1]

Settlement and Displacement Patterns

Ethiopian agricultural settlements in Al-Fashaga, predominantly involving Amhara farmers, have dominated the district's habitation patterns for decades, with thousands cultivating high-value crops like white sesame on the fertile plains.[2] These communities, including established villages such as Shmalagara, Khor Humar, and Abrahajira, reflected a pattern of cross-border migration and informal land use favoring Ethiopian highland settlers over sparse Sudanese pastoralist presence.[2] [10] Historical ethnic diversity in the region stems from waves of immigration, but pre-2020 demographics were marked by a significant Ethiopian-origin population engaged in mechanized farming.[12] Displacement escalated in mid-December 2020, when Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) troops advanced into eastern Al-Fashaga amid Ethiopia's focus on the Tigray War, expelling Ethiopian farmers and militias from key areas.[1] [14] This operation displaced thousands of Amhara settlers, severing access to farmlands and trade routes, with SAF securing up to 95% of the district by 2022 through infrastructure like bridges over the Atbarah River and seven military outposts.[2] [15] Satellite imagery from 2014â€“2023 documents the shift, showing reduced civilian activity and repurposed agricultural land for Sudanese military control.[2] Subsequent clashes, including Ethiopian incursions in June 2022, prompted limited Sudanese displacements but failed to reverse the overall pattern of Ethiopian exodus and SAF entrenchment.[16] The Tigray conflict's spillover exacerbated vulnerabilities, with displaced Ethiopian farmers contributing to broader refugee flows into Sudan, though al-Fashaga-specific returns remain minimal amid ongoing securitization.[17] [18]

Historical Background

Colonial Border Establishment

The Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty of 1902, signed on May 15 in Addis Ababa between representatives of Emperor Menelik II of Ethiopia and Great Britain acting on behalf of the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium in Sudan, established the basic demarcation of the border between Ethiopia and Sudan in the western sector.[1][19] The treaty's Article I specified that the frontier would follow a line indicated on an annexed map, commencing at the tripoint with Italian Eritrea, proceeding along the main channel of the Setit (TekezÃ©) River to its junction with the Atbara River, and then along the Atbara to a designated point near the Mareb River confluence.[20] This agreement arose amid Britain's efforts to consolidate control over Sudanese territories following the Mahdist War and to prevent Ethiopian expansion westward, while Ethiopia sought formal recognition of its sovereignty after the Battle of Adwa in 1896.[1] Demarcation of the border was entrusted to a British-led boundary commission under Major Charles W. Gwynn, a Royal Engineers officer, who conducted surveys between 1902 and 1903 to translate the treaty's map into physical markers.[2] Gwynn's line, known as the Gwynn Line, generally adhered to the treaty but involved on-the-ground adjustments for practical features like rivers and escarpments, placing the border along the Setit River's course and then ascending the eastern foothills of the Ethiopian highlands.[2][21] In the Al-Fashaga regionâ€”a fertile lowland triangle south of the Setit and east of the Atbaraâ€”the line positioned the international boundary at the base of the escarpment, ostensibly assigning the plains to Sudan while leaving higher Ethiopian plateaus on the Ethiopian side.[1][7] A supplementary protocol in 1907, involving Britain, Ethiopia, and Italy, refined aspects of the tripartite border near Eritrea but reaffirmed the 1902 delineations for the Sudan-Ethiopia stretch without resolving ambiguities in the Al-Fashaga sector.[7] These colonial-era decisions prioritized administrative convenience and hydrological features over pre-existing Ethiopian administrative control, which had extended into the lowlands through tributary arrangements with local groups like the Funj and Beja since the 19th century.[1] The resulting border, spanning approximately 1,600 kilometers, left the Al-Fashaga area's ownership open to interpretation, as the treaty text emphasized the "main channel" of rivers without explicit exclusion of adjacent floodplains, sowing seeds for later disputes.[22]

Early Post-Independence Control

Following Sudan's independence from joint Anglo-Egyptian rule on January 1, 1956, the Al-Fashaga District fell under the administrative jurisdiction of the new Sudanese state, integrated into Gedaref Province as part of the inherited colonial boundaries.[23] The 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, which delineated the border south of the Atbara River and assigned Al-Fashaga to Sudan, served as the basis for this control, with Sudanese authorities treating the area as sovereign territory without immediate challenge from Ethiopia.[1] De facto administration involved local Sudanese governance, including tax collection and security by provincial officials, while the region's fertile black cotton soils supported Sudanese agricultural communities, predominantly Arab and Beja farmers engaged in sesame, sorghum, and cotton production.[7] Ethiopia maintained historical claims to Al-Fashaga based on pre-colonial imperial oversight under emperors like Menelik II, arguing that the 1902 treatyâ€”signed without full Ethiopian ratification of the specific boundary lineâ€”was invalid or superseded by effective control assertions.[6] However, in the immediate post-independence decades (1950sâ€“1980s), Ethiopia did not deploy forces or settlers to enforce these claims, allowing Sudanese de facto dominance amid mutual focus on internal consolidations: Sudan on post-colonial state-building and Ethiopia on the 1974 revolution and subsequent Derg regime challenges.[24] Sporadic cross-border farmer disputes occurred over land use, but no large-scale military engagements or formal diplomatic escalations disrupted Sudanese oversight, with the undemarcated border facilitating informal trade and pastoral movements.[25] This period of relative stability reflected pragmatic acceptance of the colonial-era status quo, despite underlying legal ambiguities; Sudanese maps and administration consistently included Al-Fashaga, while Ethiopian maps variably omitted or contested it without practical assertion until later.[14] Population estimates for Al-Fashaga in the 1960sâ€“1970s remain sparse, but Sudanese censuses recorded several thousand residents, mainly seasonal mechanized farming operations under state-encouraged schemes to boost agricultural output in the Gezira extension areas.[9] By the late 1980s, as Sudan grappled with civil war and economic strain, vulnerabilities emerged that enabled gradual Ethiopian encroachments, setting the stage for intensified resettlement in the 1990s.[24]

Ethiopian Resettlement from the 1990s

In the 1990s, Ethiopian agricultural activity in Al-Fashaga intensified under Prime Minister Meles Zenawi, who pursued closer economic ties with Sudan while providing incentives such as marketing boards to encourage cross-border farming.[26] Primarily Amhara farmers from Ethiopia's northwest migrated into the region, drawn by its fertile black soils ideal for sesame and other cash crops, amid a security vacuum created by Sudanese farmers abandoning border areas due to threats from Ethiopian rebel groups and bandits known as shifta.[26][9] This migration marked a shift from sporadic presence to more established cultivation, with Ethiopian authorities tolerating and indirectly supporting settlements to assert practical control over the disputed 260 square kilometer area, despite Sudan's sovereignty claims based on colonial treaties.[26] By the early 2000s, as Sudanese smallholders and large-scale mechanized farmers further withdrew from Lesser Al-Fashaga due to ongoing insecurity, Ethiopian farmers expanded their holdings, investing in land preparation and infrastructure for seasonal and permanent farming under practices like mofer zemet, which allowed smallholders to claim up to 5 hectares.[9][27] The Ethiopian government played a facilitative role by fostering informal cross-border arrangements, culminating in a 2007 agreement between Meles Zenawi and Sudanese President Omar al-Bashir that permitted Ethiopian and Sudanese citizens to cultivate the land without formal demarcation, effectively legitimizing the Amhara-dominated settlements.[26][27] These policies reflected Ethiopia's strategic interest in the region's agricultural output, which supported export economies, though they heightened ethnic tensions as Amhara nationalists viewed Al-Fashaga as historically Ethiopian territory.[26] The resettlement contributed to demographic changes, with thousands of Amhara farmers and their families establishing communities by the 2010s, often backed by local militias, leading to competition over resources and periodic clashes with Sudanese herders and farmers.[26][27] Sudanese sources, including government reports, have portrayed this influx as unauthorized encroachment enabled by Ethiopian state neglect of border enforcement, while Ethiopian perspectives emphasize the economic viability and historical precedents predating modern borders.[9] This phase of settlement solidified Ethiopia's de facto presence until Sudan's military advances in late 2020 disrupted it, evicting thousands and underscoring the fragility of the informal status quo.[26]

Border Dispute Claims

Sudanese Legal Position

Sudan's legal position on the Al-Fashaga District asserts sovereignty based on the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty, in which Emperor Menelik II of Ethiopia agreed to a boundary demarcation proposed by the British administration of Sudan, placing the fertile triangle east of the Atbara River and within Sudanese territory.[1][28] This treaty, supplemented by a 1903 protocol, defined the border line approximately 40-50 kilometers east of Ethiopian historical claims, effectively ceding administrative control of Al-Fashaga to Sudan while allowing limited Ethiopian access for grazing and trade.[1] Sudanese authorities maintain that these colonial-era agreements remain binding under international law, as Ethiopia ratified them without subsequent formal repudiation, and post-independence Ethiopian governments implicitly recognized Sudanese sovereignty through diplomatic correspondence and joint border commissions until the late 20th century.[29][7] Sudanese legal arguments emphasize continuous administrative exercise of sovereignty since 1902, including land grants to Sudanese farmers and tax collection in the district, which predates large-scale Ethiopian resettlement programs initiated in the 1990s under the Derg regime and continued by subsequent Ethiopian administrations.[2] These resettlements, involving thousands of Ethiopian Amhara farmers, are viewed by Sudan as unlawful encroachments on sovereign territory rather than legitimate historical rights, with Sudanese officials citing the lack of any treaty amendment conferring ownership to Ethiopia.[1] A 2007 bilateral agreement permitted Ethiopian farmers to continue cultivating portions of Al-Fashaga on a temporary, lease-like basis without altering border delimitations, reinforcing Sudan's position that such arrangements were concessions from the sovereign owner, not acknowledgments of Ethiopian title.[30] In official statements, Sudanese representatives, including those from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, have reiterated that Al-Fashaga lies unequivocally within Gedaref State per the 1902 treaty's straight-line demarcation from the Setit River to the Atbara, rejecting Ethiopian assertions of administrative maps extending westward as inconsistent with ratified international boundaries.[29] Sudan advocates for resolution through technical boundary commissions referencing these treaties, while viewing unilateral Ethiopian military occupationsâ€”such as those during the 2020-2021 Tigray conflictâ€”as violations of the status quo ante, prompting Sudanese deployments to restore pre-2016 administrative lines where Ethiopian forces had withdrawn.[28] This stance aligns with Sudan's broader invocation of uti possidetis juris, the principle preserving colonial borders at independence, to counter claims grounded in pre-colonial Ethiopian imperial presence, which Sudan deems superseded by formal agreements.[1][31]

Ethiopian Historical and Practical Claims

Ethiopia asserts historical sovereignty over the Al-Fashaga District, citing administrative control exercised by the Ethiopian Empire prior to colonial-era border delineations.[6] The region, encompassing fertile lowlands along the Ethiopia-Sudan border, was integrated into Ethiopian governance structures, with local populations and economic activities oriented toward Ethiopian centers such as Gondar and Humera.[1] Ethiopian perspectives emphasize that indigenous ethnic groups, including Amhara communities, have maintained continuous ties to the area, predating modern state boundaries.[7] Ethiopia contests Sudan's reliance on the 1902 Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty and subsequent 1907 agreements, which purportedly placed Al-Fashaga within Sudanese territory by drawing the border along the Atbarah River's course and extending 40-50 kilometers eastward from certain landmarks.[1] Ethiopian arguments highlight ambiguities in the treaty's demarcation, noting that it was negotiated under duress during Emperor Menelik II's era and lacked precise on-ground surveys, allowing for Ethiopian interpretations that extend claims westward beyond the Sudanese-proposed line.[1] Post-colonial Ethiopian administrations, from Haile Selassie onward, reaffirmed control through maps and diplomatic notes, rejecting full cession of the triangle despite occasional border commissions.[22] On practical grounds, Ethiopia underscores decades of de facto administration, including security patrols by Ethiopian National Defense Forces and regional Amhara militias that secured farming operations until Sudanese advances in late 2020.[2] Ethiopian farmers, numbering in the thousands and predominantly Amhara, have cultivated over 300,000 hectares for sesame, sorghum, and other crops, contributing significantly to Ethiopia's export economyâ€”sesame alone accounting for substantial regional GDP.[7] [10] Resettlement initiatives from the 1990s onward populated the district with Ethiopian highlanders, establishing villages, schools, and infrastructure that integrated Al-Fashaga into Ethiopia's administrative framework, such as the North Gondar Zone.[29] This effective control, Ethiopia argues, demonstrates uti possidetis principles under international law, where long-term possession and demographic reality outweigh undemarcated colonial lines.[30] Despite Sudanese territorial assertions, Ethiopian stakeholders maintain that eviction of these communities would violate customary cross-border farming arrangements tacitly permitted by prior Sudanese regimes.[3]

Key Treaties and Agreements

The Anglo-Ethiopian Treaty of 1902, formally titled the Treaty of Amity and Commerce, signed on May 15, 1902, between Emperor Menelik II of Ethiopia and representatives of the United Kingdom (acting on behalf of Anglo-Egyptian Sudan), established the foundational border delimitation relevant to Al-Fashaga.[1] The treaty's Article 1 defined the frontier starting from the intersection of the Setit (TekezÃ©) River and the Mareb River, extending eastward and then southward, with provisions intended to allocate the fertile Al-Fashaga lowlands to Sudan while granting Ethiopia access to certain grazing areas.[2] British Major Percy Gwynn's subsequent 1902-1903 survey, known as the Gwynn Line, mapped this boundary approximately 40-50 kilometers east of Ethiopian historical claims, explicitly placing the 960 square kilometer Al-Fashaga triangle under Sudanese administration.[1] [28] A follow-up 1907 boundary agreement between Britain and Ethiopia refined aspects of the western frontier, incorporating surveys to address ambiguities in the 1902 treaty's descriptions, though it reaffirmed the general alignment excluding Al-Fashaga from Ethiopian territory.[23] These colonial-era pacts, inherited post-independence under the principle of uti possidetis juris, form the legal basis for Sudan's claim to sovereignty over Al-Fashaga, with the Gwynn Line serving as the operative demarcation despite Ethiopia's assertions of deviations from the treaty text during Gwynn's fieldwork.[32] No bilateral demarcation treaty has superseded these documents, as joint surveys in the early 20th century remained incomplete for the tripoint area involving Eritrea.[1] Post-independence efforts yielded no formal treaty resolving Al-Fashaga specifically, though Sudan and Ethiopia formed a Joint Boundary Commission in 1972 to revisit colonial lines, resulting in a 1967 understanding to maintain the status quo of Ethiopian agricultural presence without endorsing the Gwynn demarcation.[32] In 2008, following negotiations, the governments reached a practical modus vivendi: Ethiopia recognized Sudanese sovereignty per the 1902 treaty, while Sudan granted Ethiopian farmersâ€”numbering around 20,000â€”continued cultivation rights and residency in designated zones covering roughly 70% of the triangle, averting immediate eviction.[7] This arrangement, however, lacked binding legal force and collapsed amid Sudan's 2020 military reclamation, underscoring the absence of a ratified post-colonial agreement to enforce or alter the colonial delimitations.[1]

Economy

Primary Agricultural Activities

The Al-Fashaga District's fertile black clay soils, enriched by seasonal flooding from Atbara River tributaries, support rainfed large-scale agriculture as the dominant economic activity. Primary crops include sesame (Sesamum indicum), sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), and millet (Pennisetum glaucum), cultivated in mechanized schemes that leverage the region's 800-1,000 mm annual rainfall concentrated in a June-September wet season.[9] [33] Sesame stands out as the leading cash crop, often termed "white gold" for its high export value, with Ethiopian farmers dominating production through organized settlements initiated in the late 1990s. These settlers, primarily from Amhara and Tigray regions, expanded cultivation across approximately 600 square kilometers, establishing semi-permanent infrastructure like storage and processing facilities in hubs such as Bereket from 2012 to 2020.[12] [18] [34] Sudanese farmers historically coexisted via informal arrangements, sharing land under seasonal leases, but Ethiopian mechanized farmingâ€”using tractors and combine harvestersâ€”boosted yields and oriented output toward export markets via Ethiopia's ports.[1] Sorghum and millet serve as staple grains for local consumption and fodder, rotated with sesame to maintain soil fertility in crop cycles that typically span 120-150 days per season. Agricultural practices emphasize minimal irrigation reliance, though small-scale wells supplement dry spells, and post-harvest losses from smuggling and border closures have historically affected up to 20-30% of sesame yields due to informal cross-border trade networks.[9] [27] Despite disputes, the district's output contributes significantly to Sudan's sesame exports, which averaged 1.2 million tons annually pre-2020, with Al-Fashaga accounting for a substantial share before Sudanese Armed Forces interventions disrupted Ethiopian-led farming in late 2020.[35] [36]

Trade and Resource Competition

The Al-Fashaga District's fertile soils, spanning approximately 250 square kilometers, support high-yield agriculture, particularly sesame cultivation, which has become a focal point of resource competition between Sudan and Ethiopia.[37] Known as "white gold" for its economic value, sesame production in the region contributes significantly to cross-border trade, with supply chains historically involving Ethiopian farmers leasing land under Sudanese administration prior to 2020.[37] [38] Control over this land enables the dominant party to capture revenues from crop sales, taxation, and export logistics, exacerbating tensions as both nations vie for foreign exchange earnings from the commodity.[38] Sesame exports from the broader border areas, including Al-Fashaga, have historically accounted for substantial portions of national incomes; for Ethiopia, the crop represented about 25% of total merchandise exports, generating around $502 million in 2021, though conflicts led to a $115 million decline in formal earnings between 2016 and 2021 due to disrupted cultivation and illicit rerouting.[37] In Sudan, Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) control of Al-Fashaga since their December 2020 incursion has integrated the area's output into military-linked trade networks, such as those tied to the Military Industrial Corporation, providing funds that sustain Sudan's ongoing civil war economy through informal taxation and sales.[37] [2] This securitization has shifted trade dynamics, favoring Sudanese actors who now dominate local markets and export routes, while evicting Ethiopian farmers and reducing cross-border commercial flows.[37] The competition extends beyond immediate harvests to long-term land access, as Al-Fashaga's productivityâ€”bolstered by proximity to the Atbara Riverâ€”positions it as a strategic asset amid regional droughts and global demand for sesame, which fuels elite rivalries and transnational smuggling networks.[38] Ethiopia's claims emphasize historical settlement and practical economic contributions from its Amhara farmers, who previously exported via Sudanese ports, whereas Sudan's position prioritizes sovereign resource extraction to offset internal fiscal pressures.[1] [37] Resulting border closures and militarization have fragmented trade, increasing costs and risks for producers while heightening incentives for armed groups to contest control, as evidenced by SAF's use of sesame profits to procure weapons during Sudan's 2023 civil war escalation.[2]

Conflicts and Military Engagements

Pre-2020 Tensions

Prior to 2020, tensions in the Al-Fashaga District manifested primarily as low-intensity cross-border incidents involving Ethiopian settlers, armed protectors known as shifta, and Sudanese locals, rather than direct state military engagements. Sudanese authorities maintained sovereignty claims based on 1902-1903 colonial-era treaties, while Ethiopia exercised de facto control over farmlands through resettlement programs initiated in the 1990s, allowing thousands of Amhara farmers to cultivate sesame and other crops in the fertile triangle.[2] [6] A 2008 bilateral understanding permitted this status quo, with Sudan tolerating Ethiopian agricultural presence in exchange for non-interference in its claims, thereby averting escalation despite underlying disputes.[6] Sporadic violence arose from land access conflicts, with Ethiopian gunmen frequently raiding Sudanese villages to enforce farmer interests. In El Gedaref state, such groups killed at least two Sudanese farmers, injured another, and abducted two others for ransom demands exceeding SDG 4 million in one incident near East El Galabat.[39] Similar attacks included the kidnapping of six women and a child near El Leya village for 10,000 Ethiopian Birr ransom, alongside widespread livestock theftâ€”totaling over 9,000 headâ€”and crop seizures, displacing approximately 5,000 Sudanese residents.[40] These acts, linked to unclear post-independence border demarcations since 1956, fueled Sudanese farmer protests and gubernatorial demands for land reclamation and security interventions.[39] Ethnic frictions compounded the disputes, as seen in 2018 clashes between Hausa and El Lahwi communities in Al-Fashaga, resulting in 11 deaths and underscoring resource competition amid Ethiopian occupation.[39] Despite these pressures, both governments restrained from military mobilization, prioritizing trade tiesâ€”including sesame exportsâ€”and diplomatic consultations to manage the volatile frontier, with Sudanese forces occasionally patrolling but avoiding direct confrontation with Ethiopian militias until domestic crises shifted dynamics in late 2020.[2][3]

2020-2021 Escalations

In late November 2020, Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) units began advancing into the Al-Fashaga district, exploiting Ethiopia's preoccupation with the escalating Tigray War, which had begun earlier that month and strained Ethiopian military deployments along its northern borders. Sudanese officials justified the moves as restoring sovereignty over territory they claimed under colonial-era delimitations, leading to the displacement of approximately 10,000 Ethiopian Amhara farmers who had long cultivated sesame and other crops there under de facto Ethiopian administration.[1][30] By mid-December 2020, SAF troops had established control over key farming areas, prompting Ethiopian protests and initial skirmishes; on December 15, the conflict formalized with Sudanese forces occupying border posts previously held by Ethiopian-aligned militias. A day later, on December 16, Sudan reported an ambush by Ethiopian forces on a Sudanese patrol near Al-Fashaga, resulting in the deaths of five Sudanese officers and injuries to others, which Khartoum cited as evidence of Ethiopian aggression despite Addis Ababa's denials. Ethiopian authorities countered that Sudanese incursions violated longstanding practical arrangements allowing Amhara farmers access, while Amhara regional militias mobilized to resist, capturing Sudanese positions in localized fighting.[41][17] Tensions intensified into January 2021, with Sudan capturing 45 Amhara militiamen who crossed into Sudanese territory on January 3, asserting these forces aimed to reclaim lost farmlands; Khartoum paraded the detainees publicly to bolster domestic support amid its own political instability. By February, armed clashes had become routine, involving small-scale firefights between SAF units and Ethiopian federal troops or Amhara irregulars, though verified casualties remained low and unconfirmed reports from Sudanese sources claimed dozens of Ethiopian deaths. Sudan closed its airspace over the region in late December 2020 after alleging an Ethiopian fighter jet violation, further escalating diplomatic rhetoric.[42] In March 2021, Sudanese forces pressed forward, capturing the Bereket areaâ€”described as the last major Ethiopian stronghold in Al-Fashagaâ€”on March 2, displacing additional farmers and securing most of the district's 450 square kilometers under SAF administration. Escalations peaked in April-May 2021, when Sudan reported multiple Ethiopian incursions, including advances by up to 3,000 troops and militias; SAF claimed to repel these on May 5-6 near Lijura and other sites, inflicting over 40 Ethiopian casualties according to Khartoum's tallies, though Ethiopia dismissed the figures as inflated and attributed clashes to Sudanese provocations. These incidents, amid Ethiopia's Tigray offensive, involved artillery exchanges but no large-scale battles, with total verified deaths across 2020-2021 skirmishes estimated in the low hundreds, primarily from Sudanese and Amhara sources. No formal agreements resolved the standoff by year's end, leaving Sudanese troops in effective control while Ethiopia vowed diplomatic reclamation.[3][43]

Post-2021 Standoffs

Following the 2020-2021 escalations, Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) consolidated control over approximately 95% of the Al-Fashaga triangle, constructing military outposts and bridges to secure the territory while displacing thousands of Ethiopian farmers, primarily from the Amhara ethnic group.[2] Ethiopia, preoccupied with the aftermath of its Tigray conflict and internal insurgencies, adopted a strategy of restraint, prioritizing diplomatic channels over military confrontation to avoid broader regional instability.[6] This approach reflected Ethiopia's focus on long-term goals, including securing Red Sea access and countering potential insurgent threats from Sudanese territory amid Sudan's civil war.[6] A notable standoff occurred in June 2022, when the Sudanese military accused Ethiopian forces of abducting and executing seven Sudanese soldiers and one civilian in the disputed area, prompting Sudan to launch artillery shelling into Ethiopia's Amhara region and a ground assault on Ethiopian positions.[16] [44] [45] Ethiopian officials denied direct involvement, attributing the deaths to actions by local Amhara militias, and reported Sudanese advances capturing positions like the "Frankincense Fortress" military camp.[46] [47] The incident heightened tensions but did not escalate into sustained conflict, with violence levels decreasing compared to prior years.[16] Diplomatic efforts provided temporary de-escalation, including Ethiopian Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's visit to Sudan in January 2023, which signaled intent to reduce hostilities amid ongoing border militarization.[16] By 2024, securitization intensified on both sides, with Ethiopia establishing multiple checkpoints along key routes like the Gondar-Metema road, operated by federal police and Amhara militias, while Sudanese forces maintained dominance in Al-Fashaga, disrupting cross-border trade in commodities such as sesame.[2] Tensions persisted into 2025 without major clashes, as both nations deferred resolution due to domestic crisesâ€”Sudan's SAF-Rapid Support Forces war and Ethiopia's internal disordersâ€”despite the fertile region's economic stakes exceeding 1 million acres of arable land.[29] In early July 2025, Sudan alleged Ethiopian-backed militias were expelling Sudanese farmers and clearing land, claims Ethiopia rejected while reaffirming peaceful resolution commitments.[6] Analysts noted risks of reignition linked to broader Nile politics and food security, but mutual restraint prevailed, with no reported large-scale military engagements as of September 2025.[29]

Recent Developments

Diplomatic and Military Restraint

Following the 2020-2021 escalations, both Ethiopia and Sudan have demonstrated military restraint in Al-Fashaga, refraining from large-scale troop mobilizations or offensives despite periodic accusations of militia support and border incursions. This de facto de-escalation stems from mutual domestic priorities, with Ethiopia focusing on post-Tigray stabilization and countering internal insurgencies like the Fano militia, while Sudan's ongoing civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and Rapid Support Forcesâ€”intensified since April 2023â€”diverts resources from border enforcement.[29][6] Ethiopia, in particular, has adopted a strategic policy of non-escalation, viewing immediate military reclamation as counterproductive amid Sudan's internal chaos and potential alliances with Eritrea, which could broaden regional instability.[6] Diplomatically, Ethiopia has consistently affirmed commitment to negotiated settlements, as evidenced by its July 7, 2025, response to Sudan's July 4 accusations of Ethiopian-backed militias in Al-Fashaga, where Ambassador Nebiat Getachew denied involvement and reiterated pursuit of peaceful border demarcation. Sudan, constrained by its war economy's reliance on Al-Fashaga's agricultural outputâ€”spanning over 1 million acres of fertile landâ€”has similarly avoided provocative rhetoric, deferring demarcation talks rooted in the unfulfilled 1902 treaty. Regional bodies like the African Union and Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD) have urged dialogue, though without enforceable outcomes, allowing both parties to prioritize survival over confrontation.[6][29] This restraint carries risks, including unchecked militia activities along the 744 km border, which could ignite sporadic clashes and exacerbate food insecurity in both nations. Ethiopia's measured tone persists even amid Sudan's June 2025 policy granting citizenship to Tigrayan fightersâ€”perceived as a security threatâ€”opting instead for diplomatic warnings, such as those delivered by Intelligence Director Redwan Hussein in Port Sudan, without resorting to reprisals. Analysts attribute Ethiopia's approach to a "long game" strategy, leveraging Sudan's fragmentation to strengthen its negotiating leverage over time rather than risking broader Horn of Africa volatility.[48][6][29]

Economic and Humanitarian Impacts

The Sudanese Armed Forces' (SAF) incursion into Al-Fashaga beginning in December 2020 expelled thousands of primarily Amhara Ethiopian farmers who had cultivated sesame and other crops on the fertile lands for decades, severely disrupting local agricultural production.[18][1] Prior to the escalation, Ethiopian and Sudanese farmers jointly harvested sesame in the area, contributing to regional export chains where Sudan ranks as a top global producer, with Al-Fashaga forming a key transnational production zone.[49] The takeover shifted control to Sudanese actors but reduced overall output due to abandoned fields, restricted access during planting seasons (late May to mid-July), and heightened securitization that deterred cross-border labor and trade.[12] Securitization of the Ethiopia-Sudan border has further eroded legitimate sesame trade while enabling smuggling networks tied to armed groups, including Amhara militias, thereby embedding the crop in Sudan's broader war economy amid its civil conflict.[2][12] This has led to volatile pricing, supply chain interruptions, and lost revenues for Ethiopian farmers, exacerbating economic vulnerabilities in Amhara and Tigray regions already strained by internal war.[50] Humanitarian effects stem primarily from the displacement of expelled Ethiopian farmers, who faced immediate loss of livelihoods, homes, and harvests, with reports indicating thousands affected in the initial 2020-2021 phase.[1][18] The standoff compounded refugee inflows from Ethiopia's Tigray conflict, with UNHCR registering Ethiopian refugees in Al-Fashaga villages and nearby Gedaref camps, straining Sudanese border resources amid Sudan's own instability.[51] No large-scale famine or acute crisis has been documented directly from the dispute, but ongoing exclusion of Ethiopian cultivators perpetuates livelihood insecurity and potential for localized tensions over land access.[9]

International Perspectives

Regional Mediation Efforts

The Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD), comprising Sudan, Ethiopia, and other Horn of Africa states, has maintained a mandate for conflict mediation in the region but has not spearheaded dedicated initiatives for the Al-Fashaga dispute. IGAD's broader efforts have focused on de-escalation amid interconnected crises, such as Sudan's internal instability and Ethiopia's northern conflicts, yet specific talks on Al-Fashaga have remained bilateral without IGAD facilitation.[1] This limited role reflects IGAD's diminished influence in interstate border issues, as evidenced by stalled diplomacy on overlapping tensions like those involving Eritrea.[52] The African Union (AU), through its Peace and Security Council and border governance programs, has advocated for dialogue to resolve interstate disputes, including calls for restraint in Ethiopia-Sudan tensions post-2020 escalations. However, no AU-led mediation panel or shuttle diplomacy has been deployed exclusively for Al-Fashaga; instead, the AU has integrated the issue into wider Horn stability appeals, emphasizing adherence to colonial-era delimitations and joint commissions without enforcing outcomes. Bilateral negotiations, such as those in February 2021, deadlocked over troop withdrawals and land rights, yielding no demarcation progress.[42] By September 2025, both nations deferred resolution amid domestic priorities, with AU-aligned rhetoric prioritizing de-escalation over structured mediation.[29] Neighboring states like Egypt have indirectly shaped dynamics through alliances with Sudan, leveraging shared concerns over Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) to bolster Khartoum's claims, though this constitutes influence rather than neutral mediation. Regional proposals for "soft border" solutions, involving local stakeholders in resource-sharing dialogues, remain conceptual without implementation.[3] Overall, the absence of robust third-party regional intervention has perpetuated a fragile status quo, with Sudanese forces retaining control over much of the triangle since 2021 advances.[1]

Broader Geopolitical Implications

The Al-Fashaga dispute exacerbates vulnerabilities in the Horn of Africa, where overlapping border claims, ethnic militias, and resource competition risk cascading into multi-state conflict, as evidenced by the mobilization of Eritrean armed groups alongside Sudanese and Ethiopian forces since 2020.[4][1] This volatility intersects with Sudan's ongoing civil war, where Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) control of the district since late 2020 has bolstered their wartime economy through sesame exports from the fertile lowlands, potentially prolonging internal fragmentation and diverting resources from national reconstruction.[2][27] Egypt's military backing for Sudan, including reported arms supplies and joint exercises, amplifies trans-regional tensions, primarily driven by Cairo's strategic opposition to Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD), which Sudanese control of Al-Fashaga could leverage to counter Ethiopian upstream influence over Nile flows.[4][17] This alignment risks pitting Egypt and Sudan against Ethiopia, echoing historical Nile Basin rivalries and potentially drawing in Gulf states seeking leverage over Red Sea shipping lanes, where instability could disrupt 12% of global trade volume.[53][54] Eritrea's shifting postures further complicate the tripoint dynamics, with Asmara's post-2018 dÃ©tente with Ethiopia eroding amid accusations of Eritrean incursions into Al-Fashaga and emerging alignments with Sudan that could revive proxy skirmishes, undermining fragile regional peace architectures like the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD).[55][56] Overall, unresolved claims over the 260 square kilometer area threaten to politicize subnational actors, inflate ethnic grievances in Ethiopia's Amhara region, and erode cross-border tradeâ€”already halved since 2020â€”fostering a cycle of securitization that hampers counter-terrorism efforts against groups like al-Shabaab and facilitates irregular migration flows toward Europe.[1][2][57]
