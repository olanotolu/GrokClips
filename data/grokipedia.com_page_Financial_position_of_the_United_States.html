Financial position of the United States

The financial position of the United States refers to the federal government's fiscal balance sheet and ongoing budgetary dynamics, marked by total public debt outstanding of approximately $38 trillion as of October 2025, a debt-to-GDP ratio exceeding 125 percent, annual budget deficits nearing $1.8 trillion in fiscal year 2025, and mounting liabilities from entitlement programs that outpace reported assets.[1][2][3] This position reflects decades of deficit spending exceeding economic growth, with revenues covering only about 75 percent of outlays in recent years, driven primarily by mandatory spending on Social Security, Medicare, and interest on the debt.[3][4] Despite the world's largest nominal GDP of around $30 trillion, the U.S. government's reported assetsâ€”totaling roughly $5.7 trillion in fiscal year 2024, including loans receivable and property, plant, and equipmentâ€”fall far short of liabilities when accounting for unfunded obligations estimated at over $150 trillion for pensions and retiree health benefits.[5][6] Public debt held by the public constitutes the bulk of obligations, financed largely through Treasury securities purchased by domestic and foreign investors, with intragovernmental holdings adding to the total.[7] Interest payments on this debt reached record levels in 2025, consuming a growing share of the budget and exacerbating deficits amid higher borrowing costs.[8] Key defining characteristics include the dollar's status as the global reserve currency, which has enabled sustained deficits without immediate crisis, yet projections from the Congressional Budget Office indicate federal debt rising to 118 percent of GDP by 2035 and far higher thereafter under current policies, raising concerns over long-term solvency and potential crowding out of private investment.[9] Controversies center on the trajectory's sustainability, with empirical analyses highlighting that entitlement spending growth, demographic shifts like aging populations, and resistance to fiscal reforms contribute causally to escalating imbalances, independent of short-term economic cycles.[10] While achievements such as post-war economic expansions have historically supported debt management, the current path underscores systemic vulnerabilities absent structural changes to spending or taxation.[9]

Overall Net Position

Domestic Net Worth

The domestic net worth of the United States consists of the total market value of nonfinancial assets owned by domestic sectors, including households, nonprofit organizations, nonfinancial businesses, and governments, as financial assets and liabilities offset each other within the economy.[11] This measure captures the economy's productive capacity through tangible assets such as real estate, equipment, inventories, and consumer durables, as well as intangible assets like intellectual property, with corporate equities used to approximate unrecorded intangibles in business valuations.[11] As of the second quarter of 2025, domestic net worth totaled $187.0 trillion.[12] Major components include households' and nonprofits' direct holdings of nonfinancial assets at $62.7 trillion, predominantly residential real estate; nonfinancial noncorporate business assets at $19.0 trillion; domestic corporations' nonfinancial assets at $83.3 trillion, encompassing fixed assets and inventories; federal government nonfinancial assets at $4.9 trillion; and state and local government nonfinancial assets at $17.1 trillion.[12] These values reflect current-cost estimates for replacement and market-based valuations for real estate and equities, adjusted for revaluations and volume changes.[12] Domestic net worth has expanded significantly over time, driven by capital accumulation, asset price appreciation, and innovation enhancing intangible values, though subject to fluctuations from economic cycles and valuation adjustments.[11] For instance, from 2015, when it approximated $80 trillion, growth has been fueled by real estate gains and corporate asset expansion, outpacing nominal GDP increases.[11] This stock supports the overall financial position by underpinning domestic production and wealth generation, independent of net international claims.[12]

Net International Investment Position

The net international investment position (NIIP) of the United States measures the difference between U.S.-owned assets abroad and foreign-owned assets in the United States, valued at current market prices or fair values.[13] A negative NIIP indicates that the value of foreign claims on U.S. assets exceeds the value of U.S. claims on foreign assets, positioning the country as a net debtor internationally.[14] As of June 30, 2025 (end of Q2), the U.S. NIIP was -$26.14 trillion, down $1.49 trillion from -$24.65 trillion at the end of Q1 2025 (revised).[15] This deterioration resulted from net financial transactions reducing the position by $428.1 billionâ€”reflecting net U.S. borrowing from foreigners exceeding U.S. lending abroadâ€”and net valuation adjustments subtracting $1.06 trillion, primarily due to price changes (-$2.23 trillion, as U.S. stock prices rose more than foreign equities) offset partially by exchange-rate gains (+$1.17 trillion from appreciation of foreign currencies against the dollar).[15] U.S.-owned assets abroad totaled $39.56 trillion in Q2 2025, up $2.68 trillion from Q1, driven by increases in portfolio investments (e.g., debt and equity securities) and direct investment positions abroad.[15] Foreign-owned assets in the United States reached $65.71 trillion, rising $4.16 trillion, led by portfolio investments (+$1.74 trillion) and direct investment (+$1.51 trillion), with financial transactions showing net foreign purchases of $657.2 billion in U.S. equity and debt securities.[15] Key components include foreign direct investment (FDI), which constitutes a significant share of both assets (U.S. multinationals' equity and earnings abroad) and liabilities (foreign multinationals' holdings in the U.S.), alongside portfolio holdings where U.S. assets abroad are equity-heavy while liabilities are more debt-oriented.[15] Historically, the U.S. NIIP turned negative in 1989 after decades as a net creditor post-World War II, reaching -$1.23 trillion by 2007 before accelerating to current levels amid widening current account deficits financed by capital inflows attracted to U.S. assets.[16] By end-2024, it stood at approximately -$26.54 trillion, with quarterly fluctuations influenced more by valuation effects (e.g., asset price booms and dollar strength) than underlying transactions tied to trade imbalances.[14] Despite the deepening deficit, the U.S. has sustained a positive net primary income balance in recent years, as returns on outward assets (often higher-yield equities) exceed costs on inward liabilities (lower-yield debt), supported by the dollar's reserve currency status encouraging foreign holdings of U.S. securities.[15] BEA data incorporates market valuations and covers positions of U.S. residents (households, businesses, government) excluding official reserve assets in some aggregates, with revisions reflecting updated source data from surveys and administrative records.[15]

Nonfinancial Business Assets

Nonfinancial business assets encompass the tangible and intangible nonfinancial holdings of nonfinancial corporate and noncorporate entities, including equipment, structures, intellectual property products, software, inventories, and land, valued primarily at current cost with real estate often adjusted to market value. As of the second quarter of 2025, nonfinancial assets of nonfinancial corporate businesses stood at $30.1 trillion, reflecting a slight quarterly decline from $30.3 trillion in the first quarter amid fluctuations in equipment and intellectual property valuations.[20] Nonfinancial noncorporate businesses, which include proprietorships, partnerships, and farms with significant real estate holdings, reported nonfinancial assets of approximately $19 trillion in the first quarter of 2025, with similar levels expected for the second quarter based on stable trends in structures and land values.[21] Aggregating these sectors, total nonfinancial business assets reached roughly $49 trillion, constituting a major component of U.S. gross domestic assets and supporting productive capacity outside the financial sector.[19] Key components for nonfinancial corporate businesses include equipment at about 40% of nonfinancial assets, structures and real estate at around 30%, and intellectual property products (such as software and research and development) at 20%, with inventories and other nonproduced assets filling the remainder; these proportions have remained stable over the past decade, driven by ongoing capital investment offset by depreciation.[22] Noncorporate assets skew more toward real estate and land, comprising over 60% due to holdings in rental properties, farms, and noncorporate real estate ventures, supplemented by equipment and inventories in small businesses and sole proprietorships.[23] The Bureau of Economic Analysis estimates the current-cost net stock of private nonresidential fixed assetsâ€”largely attributable to nonfinancial businessesâ€”at approximately $25 trillion as of 2023, with updates incorporating 2024 investment flows indicating modest growth into 2025, though these figures exclude inventories and use replacement cost rather than market valuation for real estate.[24] These assets have expanded in line with nominal GDP growth, increasing from $35 trillion in 2015 to the current levels, fueled by business fixed investment averaging 12-13% of GDP annually, though recent quarters show moderation due to higher interest rates constraining equipment purchases.[19] Inventories, a volatile subcomponent, peaked at $2.5 trillion for corporates in 2022 amid supply chain disruptions but stabilized below $2.2 trillion by mid-2025, reflecting normalized trade dynamics.[25] Data from the Federal Reserve's Financial Accounts, derived from integrated macroeconomic accounts harmonized with BEA national income statistics, provide the primary empirical basis for these valuations, ensuring consistency across sectors while accounting for revaluations and disposals.[19]

Financial Sector Assets

The domestic financial sector of the United States includes monetary authorities, private depository institutions, insurance companies, pension funds, mutual funds and exchange-traded funds, money market funds, security brokers and dealers, financing companies, and other financial intermediaries that facilitate the flow of funds between surplus and deficit economic units. As of the second quarter of 2025, total financial assets held by these domestic financial sectors reached $145.7 trillion, reflecting an increase from prior quarters driven by market valuation gains and new issuances.[26] Key components of these assets include debt securities, such as U.S. Treasury and agency securities, municipal bonds, and corporate debt, totaling $53.2 trillion, which underscores the sector's central role in absorbing government and corporate borrowing. Corporate equities, representing holdings in domestic and foreign stocks, amounted to $43.0 trillion, concentrated among investment funds and institutional investors exposed to equity market fluctuations. Loans, extended primarily to households, nonfinancial businesses, and other sectors, stood at $18.5 trillion, with depository institutions accounting for the bulk through commercial, consumer, and real estate lending.[27][28][29]

Government Assets

The assets of the U.S. government sector include financial holdings such as cash, loans, and securities, as well as non-financial assets like property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), inventories, and infrastructure. These are tracked separately for federal and state/local governments in the Federal Reserve's Financial Accounts of the United States (Z.1 release), which provide quarterly economic estimates incorporating taxes receivable and other claims. Federal assets totaled $9.44 trillion as of Q2 2025, reflecting taxes receivable (a major component estimated at over $5 trillion, representing outstanding tax obligations), financial assets like deposits and credit market instruments, and non-financial assets including equipment and structures.[33] [34] The U.S. Department of the Treasury's Financial Report, using accrual-based budgetary accounting with intra-governmental eliminations, reports lower federal total assets of $5.66 trillion as of September 30, 2024 (fiscal year-end). Key components include loans receivable, net ($1.75 trillion, dominated by $1.6 trillion in federal student loans), PP&E, net ($1.31 trillion, encompassing military installations, federal buildings, and land improvements), cash and other monetary assets ($1.18 trillion), and inventories ($0.45 trillion).[5] Other assets, such as accounts receivable and advances, account for the remainder. Discrepancies between Z.1 and Treasury figures stem from methodological differences: Z.1 emphasizes gross economic positions including unnetted taxes receivable for sectoral consistency, while Treasury consolidates fiduciary and intra-entity items, excluding certain stewardship assets like environmental liabilities' offsets.[19] State and local government assets, primarily infrastructure such as highways, schools, and utilities, totaled approximately $6.5 trillion in recent estimates, with non-financial fixed assets comprising about $3.5 trillion and financial assets (pensions, bonds, and cash) around $3 trillion as of Q2 2025.[19] These holdings fund public services but are often illiquid and valued at historical cost minus depreciation, understating replacement values amid inflation. Overall, government assets represent a modest fraction of total U.S. domestic assets, constrained by accounting standards that do not capitalize future revenue streams beyond receivables.[35]

Nonfinancial Business Liabilities

Nonfinancial business liabilities in the United States primarily comprise credit market instruments such as debt securities (including corporate bonds and commercial paper) and loans (including bank loans and other advances), alongside smaller categories like taxes payable and miscellaneous liabilities such as pension obligations and trade payables. These liabilities reflect obligations incurred by nonfinancial corporations and noncorporate entities (e.g., partnerships and sole proprietorships) to finance operations, investments, and working capital, excluding financial intermediaries. The Federal Reserve tracks these through the Financial Accounts of the United States (Z.1 release), distinguishing nonfinancial corporate business (the dominant subsector) from noncorporate business.[19][39] As of Q2 2025, nonfinancial corporate business debt securities and loans totaled $14.014 trillion, marking a modest increase from prior quarters amid stable economic conditions but elevated interest rates.[40] Within this, debt securities accounted for the largest share at approximately $8.65 trillion as of early 2025, primarily long-term corporate bonds issued to institutional investors, while loans and short-term instruments like commercial paper comprised the remainder.[41] Noncorporate nonfinancial business liabilities add roughly $3.5 trillion in comparable debt instruments, often tied to real estate mortgages and smaller-scale borrowings, bringing total nonfinancial business credit market debt to about $17.5 trillion, or roughly 60% of nominal GDP.[42] This debt-to-GDP ratio for nonfinancial business has hovered around 55-60% since 2019, reflecting post-pandemic deleveraging followed by selective borrowing for share buybacks and capital expenditures rather than broad expansion.[43]

Financial Sector Liabilities

The liabilities of the U.S. domestic financial sector, comprising depository institutions, insurance companies, pension funds, mutual funds, and other intermediaries, totaled $131.5 trillion as of the second quarter of 2025.[47] This figure, derived from the Federal Reserve's Z.1 Financial Accounts, reflects the sector's role in channeling funds from savers to borrowers through a web of financial instruments, with liabilities largely matching corresponding assets held by nonfinancial sectors.[19] Deposits constitute a core component, amounting to the primary liabilities of banks and similar institutions, funding lending and investment activities.[19] Other major liabilities include short-term funding via federal funds, repurchase agreements, and commercial paper; longer-term debt securities issued by finance companies and broker-dealers; direct loans from nonfinancial entities; and miscellaneous obligations such as taxes payable.[48] For nondepository subsectors, significant liabilities encompass mutual fund and money market fund shares outstandingâ€”treated as liabilities to investorsâ€”and actuarial reserves for life insurance policies and pension obligations.[19] The expansion of these liabilities has paralleled the growth of financial intermediation since the 1980s, driven by deregulation, securitization, and the rise of asset management vehicles, resulting in financial sector claims exceeding nonfinancial economic output by multiples.[19] While balanced against assets in aggregate, the composition exposes vulnerabilities to interest rate shifts and credit contractions, as liabilities often carry shorter maturities or withdrawal features than underlying assets, amplifying rollover and liquidity risks during stress periods.[49]

State and Local Government Liabilities

State and local governments in the United States report total liabilities of $7.71 trillion as of the second quarter of 2025, encompassing debt instruments, pension obligations, other post-employment benefits (OPEB), and miscellaneous payables.[50] This figure, derived from the Federal Reserve's Financial Accounts of the United States, reflects a broad balance sheet aggregation where pension and insurance fund liabilities constitute a substantial portion, often exceeding explicit debt.[19] Debt securities and loans alone account for $3.60 trillion outstanding in the same period, primarily municipal bonds issued for infrastructure, schools, and public services.[51] A critical component involves public pension systems, where unfunded liabilitiesâ€”representing promised benefits exceeding assetsâ€”total approximately $1.3 trillion to $1.6 trillion across state and local plans as of fiscal year 2023 and early 2025 estimates.[52][53] Funded ratios for these plans average around 77-81%, with improvements attributed to strong investment returns but offset by demographic pressures from aging workforces and benefit accruals.[54][55] These shortfalls arise from historical underfunding, optimistic actuarial assumptions, and contribution holidays during revenue booms, amplifying long-term fiscal strain as states and localities face rising payments amid constrained tax bases. Similarly, OPEB liabilities for retiree healthcare, which are largely pay-as-you-go and underfunded, reached $789 billion in unfunded obligations by 2022, surpassing pension gaps in some analyses and continuing to grow with healthcare cost inflation.[56] Debt composition includes general obligation bonds backed by taxing authority and revenue bonds tied to specific project streams like tolls or utilities, with total outstanding municipal debt climbing steadily post-2020 due to infrastructure demands and pandemic borrowing.[57] The U.S. Census Bureau's 2022 data, the most recent comprehensive survey, underscores debt levels tied to expenditures on education (over 40% of local outlays) and highways, though underreporting of contingent liabilities in some jurisdictions may understate risks.[58] Fiscal pressures are evident in states like California, where combined state and local debt exceeds $1 trillion including unfunded obligations, highlighting variability: wealthier states maintain stronger credit while others grapple with pension-driven deficits equivalent to 60-70% of own-source revenues.[59][60]

Federal Government Liabilities

The federal government's liabilities encompass outstanding debt obligations, accrued benefits for employees and veterans, and other commitments reported on the official balance sheet. The dominant component is federal debt and interest payable, which as of September 30, 2024, included debt held by the public excluding accrued interest at $28.3 trillion, supplemented by intragovernmental debt holdings totaling approximately $7 trillion.[5] By October 2025, the gross federal debt surpassed $38 trillion, reflecting ongoing deficits and borrowing to finance government operations.[62] Other significant liabilities include federal employee and veteran benefits payable, primarily pensions and post-retirement health benefits, which represent multi-trillion-dollar obligations accrued over decades of service. The Financial Report of the United States Government identifies these as the second-largest category after debt, with environmental cleanup and disposal liabilities adding smaller but notable amounts, estimated in the hundreds of billions.[63] The Government Accountability Office (GAO) has issued a disclaimer of opinion on the consolidated financial statements due to pervasive uncertainties and material weaknesses in accounting for these items, particularly in long-term projections.[64] Beyond the reported balance sheet, federal liabilities are substantially understated by excluding the full present-value costs of mandatory entitlement programs like Social Security and Medicare, which operate on a pay-as-you-go basis without sufficient dedicated funding for future promises. The 2025 Social Security Trustees Report projects trust fund depletion by 2034, implying an unfunded shortfall requiring benefit cuts or revenue increases thereafter.[65] Similarly, Medicare's Hospital Insurance Trust Fund faces insolvency around 2036, with Supplementary Medical Insurance costs driven by demographics and healthcare inflation.[66] Independent analyses estimate the combined present-value unfunded obligations for these programs at over $75 trillion, representing commitments to future generations not reflected in current liabilities.[67] Comprehensive measures incorporating such intergenerational transfers yield a total fiscal imbalance exceeding $160 trillion as of early 2025.[68] These unfunded liabilities arise from statutory promises exceeding projected revenues under current law, exacerbated by aging population demographics and rising per-capita healthcare costs, which strain fiscal sustainability absent reforms. Official reports from the Treasury and GAO emphasize that while the balance sheet captures closed liabilities, the true economic burden includes these contingent and projected shortfalls, underscoring the need for transparency in assessing the government's overall financial position.[69]

Income, Expenses, and Sectoral Balances

Gross Domestic Income Components

Gross Domestic Income (GDI) comprises the aggregate incomes earned by U.S. resident factors of productionâ€”labor, capital, and landâ€”in the domestic production of goods and services, supplemented by production taxes net of subsidies and capital consumption allowances. The U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) constructs GDI as an alternative measure to gross domestic product (GDP), with the two theoretically equivalent but often diverging due to statistical discrepancies arising from differing data sources and estimation methods; BEA regards GDP as more reliable owing to its reliance on more timely expenditure-side information.[70][71] In Q2 2025, real GDI expanded at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 3.8 percent, reflecting robust income growth amid revisions to prior estimates.[71] The core components of GDI are compensation of employees, proprietors' income with inventory valuation and capital consumption adjustments (IVA and CCAdj), rental income of persons with CCAdj, corporate profits with IVA and CCAdj, net interest and miscellaneous payments on assets, business current transfer payments (net), taxes on production and imports less subsidies, and consumption of fixed capital. Compensation of employees forms the dominant share, historically comprising over half of GDI as it captures wages, salaries, and employer-provided supplements like pension and insurance contributions.[72][73] Labor compensation's primacy underscores the labor-intensive nature of U.S. output, though its share has fluctuated with productivity trends and wage pressures; for instance, supplements to wages and salaries reached $2,756 billion (SAAR) in Q2 2025, up from $2,722 billion in Q1.[74] Proprietors' income, reflecting current-production earnings of sole proprietorships, partnerships, and tax-exempt cooperatives net of IVA and CCAdj, captures self-employment and small-business returns excluding dividends and financial interest. This component stood at $1,575 billion (SAAR) for nonfarm proprietors in Q2 2025, marginally above Q1's $1,566 billion, signaling steady unincorporated sector activity amid broader economic expansion.[75] Rental income of persons with CCAdj measures net returns from tenant-occupied housing and other property rentals after depreciation, contributing a smaller but stable portion tied to real estate dynamics. Corporate profits with IVA and CCAdj, the second-largest element after labor compensation, surged $65.5 billion in Q2 2025 to $3,259 billion (SAAR), rebounding from a Q1 decline and driven by output gains outweighing input cost pressures.[76][71] Net interest and miscellaneous payments represent domestic industries' net interest flows on production-related debt and equity, less imputations; elevated Federal Reserve rates since 2022 have compressed this net figure for businesses, subtracting from overall GDI growth as borrowing costs rise.[77] Taxes on production and imports less subsidies encompass sales, property, and excise taxes borne by producers, net of government subsidies, functioning as a non-factor input adjustment rather than direct income. Consumption of fixed capital deducts depreciation on durable assets used in production, ensuring GDI reflects gross rather than net output; this allowance has grown with capital stock accumulation, averaging around 13-15 percent of GDI in recent years. Business current transfer payments (net) include payments to government and persons unrelated to production, such as settlements, forming a minor residual. These elements collectively highlight GDI's focus on production-side earnings, with divergences from GDP often amplifying during periods of rapid interest rate shifts or inventory adjustments.[70]

Gross Domestic Expense Components

The expenditure approach to gross domestic product (GDP) measures the total value of final goods and services produced in the United States by summing spending across major categories, providing insight into the composition of domestic economic activity.[78] Gross domestic expenses, excluding net foreign trade, consist primarily of personal consumption expenditures, private investment, and government spending, which together represent domestic absorption and typically account for over 100% of GDP when net exports are negative.[79] In recent years, these components have reflected a consumption-driven economy, with personal spending dominating due to household income growth, credit availability, and service-oriented demand. Personal consumption expenditures (PCE), encompassing durable goods, nondurable goods, and services purchased by households, form the largest share of domestic expenses, comprising roughly 70% of GDP.[80] In the second quarter of 2025, real PCE rose 2.5%, driven by increases in services (2.6%) and goods (2.2%), contributing 1.68 percentage points to the 3.8% annualized real GDP growth.[81] This component's dominance stems from structural factors like population size, wage gains in service sectors, and fiscal transfers, though it has shown vulnerability to inflation and interest rate hikes affecting discretionary durables. Gross private domestic investment includes nonresidential fixed investment (e.g., equipment and structures by businesses), residential construction, and inventory changes, typically representing 17-18% of GDP.[82] For Q2 2025, overall real private investment contracted sharply by 13.8%, subtracting 2.66 percentage points from GDP growth, largely due to inventory drawdowns; however, fixed investment expanded 4.4%, with nonresidential structures and equipment up 7.3%.[81] Business investment has been supported by corporate profits and tax incentives but constrained by high borrowing costs and supply chain residual effects from prior disruptions. Government consumption expenditures and gross investment, covering federal, state, and local outlays on goods, services, and public capital (excluding transfers), account for about 17-18% of GDP.[83] In Q2 2025, real government consumption edged down 0.1%, with minimal drag on growth (-0.01 percentage points), reflecting steady defense and infrastructure spending offset by cuts in other areas amid fiscal pressures.[81] State and local components have grown via revenue from sales taxes and aid, while federal portions are tied to mandatory programs and discretionary appropriations. Net exports, while not strictly domestic expenses, adjust the total: exports add to production, but imports (domestic spending on foreign output) are subtracted, yielding a net drag of 3-4% of GDP in recent periods due to strong import demand for consumer and capital goods.[82] In Q2 2025, net exports boosted growth by 4.83 percentage points, as imports plunged 29.3% (adding 5.03 points via reduced subtraction) amid economic softening, outweighing a 1.8% export decline.[81] This volatility underscores the U.S. economy's reliance on domestic components for stability, with trade imbalances financed by capital inflows.

Sectoral Financial Balances

The sectoral financial balances framework decomposes national income accounts into the net lending (or borrowing) positions of the domestic private sector (households, non-profits, and nonfinancial businesses), the government sector (federal, state, and local), and the foreign sector (reflected in the current account balance). This approach, derived from flow-of-funds and national income identities, ensures that the algebraic sum of these balances equals zero, as every financial surplus in one sector finances a deficit in another.[19] In the U.S. economy, chronic government deficits and current account deficits have historically been matched by private sector surpluses, indicating net saving in the private domestic economy exceeding investment. In the second quarter of 2025 (seasonally adjusted annual rate), the domestic private sectors recorded net lending of approximately $1.037 trillion, reflecting a surplus where private saving outpaced domestic investment.[84] This position offset combined government borrowing and the foreign sector's net borrowing, the latter corresponding to the U.S. current account deficit of $251.3 billion in that quarter (not annualized).[85] The government sector's net borrowing, encompassing federal deficits partially mitigated by state and local surpluses, balanced the identity; federal outlays exceeded receipts amid elevated spending, while subnational governments maintained fiscal restraint.[86]

Off-Balance-Sheet and Contingent Risks

Derivatives Exposure

The derivatives market represents a significant off-balance-sheet exposure for the U.S. financial system, primarily concentrated in commercial banks. As of the second quarter of 2025, U.S. commercial banks and savings associations held derivative contracts with a total notional amount of $223.5 trillion, an increase of $13.1 trillion or 6.2% from the first quarter. This figure, equivalent to roughly eight times the U.S. gross domestic product of approximately $28 trillion in 2024, underscores the scale of potential leverage, though notional amounts do not directly reflect economic exposure due to netting agreements, collateral requirements, and offsetting positions.[82] Interest rate contracts dominate, comprising about 70% of the total notional, followed by foreign exchange, credit default swaps, and commodities.[88] Exposure is highly concentrated among the largest banks, with the top five institutionsâ€”typically JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citibank, Goldman Sachs, and Wells Fargoâ€”accounting for over 95% of the notional amount. This concentration amplifies systemic risks, as interconnected counterparty exposures could propagate shocks across the financial network, similar to the 2008 crisis where uncleared credit derivatives exacerbated the collapse of firms like Lehman Brothers.[89] Post-Dodd-Frank reforms have mandated central clearing for many standardized derivatives, reducing bilateral counterparty risk through multilateral netting and daily margining; by 2025, over 75% of interest rate derivatives are cleared centrally.[90] Nonetheless, uncleared over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives persist, with gross positive fair values indicating potential losses if counterparties default. Net current credit exposure (NCCE), a key measure of potential credit risk after netting but before collateral, stood lower in early 2025 compared to late 2024, reflecting improved risk management amid stable market conditions.[91] Collateral coverage remains high, with banks posting or receiving margin on most exposures, yet rapid market volatilityâ€”such as in interest rates or credit spreadsâ€”could strain liquidity and lead to fire-sale dynamics.[92] Federal implicit backstops, including the Federal Reserve's lender-of-last-resort function, mitigate outright systemic collapse but introduce moral hazard, as evidenced by interventions during the 2020 COVID-19 market turmoil.[93] Empirical analyses indicate that foreign exchange and interest rate derivatives contribute most to banks' systemic risk contributions, driven by leverage and interconnectivity rather than notional size alone. Overall, while regulatory enhancements have lowered tail risks, the sheer volume and opacity of remaining OTC positions pose contingent threats to U.S. financial stability, particularly in scenarios of correlated defaults or geopolitical shocks.

Other Contingent Liabilities

The U.S. federal government maintains numerous contingent liabilities stemming from explicit guarantees, insurance programs, legal claims, environmental remediation, and other off-balance-sheet exposures that could materialize into actual obligations under specified conditions, such as borrower defaults or catastrophic events. These are disclosed in accordance with federal accounting standards, which require accrual only for probable losses estimable in amount, with reasonably possible losses reported as ranges but not accrued. As of fiscal year 2024 (ending September 30, 2024), probable legal and environmental contingencies were accrued at $51.9 billion, with an estimated range of $51.7 billion to $67.6 billion, while reasonably possible exposures ranged from $23.9 billion to $125.1 billion.[94] Major insurance and guarantee programs constitute the bulk of disclosed contingent exposures, covering deposits, loans, pensions, and mortgages. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) insures approximately $10.6 trillion in deposits as of September 30, 2024, representing potential payouts in the event of widespread bank failures, though historical resolutions have incurred costs far below insured amounts due to asset recoveries and least-cost mandates.[94] Similarly, the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC) faces reasonably possible losses of $2.8 billion from underfunded private pensions, with assets of $146.1 billion against single-employer obligations and $4.5 billion for multiemployer plans.[94] Other programs include the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA) with $1.8 trillion in insured shares, Ginnie Mae's $2.64 trillion in mortgage-backed securities guarantees, and the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) covering $1.28 trillion in policies, which has borrowed $20.5 billion from Treasury to fund claims.[94]

Historical Trends and Fiscal Sustainability

Evolution of Key Metrics

Federal debt held by the public reached its historical peak relative to gross domestic product (GDP) at 106 percent immediately after World War II in 1946, driven by wartime borrowing.[9] This ratio then declined sharply during the postwar economic boom and fiscal restraint, falling to a postwar low of 31 percent by 1981.[98] From the 1980s onward, persistent budget deficitsâ€”stemming from tax cuts, increased defense spending, recessions, and entitlement growthâ€”reversed this trend, with the debt-to-GDP ratio climbing to 49 percent by 1993.[2] Brief surpluses from 1998 to 2001 under fiscal policies emphasizing spending control and revenue growth from the dot-com boom reduced it to 55 percent by 2001, but subsequent wars, the 2008 financial crisis, and the COVID-19 pandemic propelled it above 100 percent by 2020.[98] As of September 30, 2024, debt held by the public stood at $28.3 trillion, equivalent to approximately 98 percent of GDP.[5][99] The federal budget deficit as a percentage of GDP has fluctuated with economic cycles and policy choices, averaging 3.8 percent over the past 50 years through 2024.[9] It spiked to 4.0 percent in the early 1980s amid recession and tax reductions, narrowed to surpluses (averaging -0.2 percent) in the late 1990s, then widened again post-2001 due to military engagements and tax cuts, reaching 10 percent by 2009 during the Great Recession.[100] The COVID-19 response drove another peak of 14.9 percent in 2020, after which deficits moderated but remained elevated at 6.3 percent in 2023 and 6.4 percent in 2024, exceeding historical norms due to sustained spending on entitlements and interest amid slower revenue growth relative to outlays.[101][9] Net interest payments on the federal debt have accelerated with rising debt levels and interest rates, increasing from $458.5 billion (2.3 percent of GDP) in fiscal year 2017 to $523 billion (2.5 percent of GDP) in 2018, and continuing upward as rates normalized post-zero-bound policies.[102] By fiscal year 2024, interest costs consumed a larger share of the budget, reflecting the compounding effect of prior deficits and the Federal Reserve's rate hikes from 2022 onward, though exact annual figures post-2018 require aggregation from monthly statements showing cumulative growth.[103] These metrics underscore a trajectory where debt accumulation outpaces GDP growth, with projections indicating the debt-to-GDP ratio surpassing its World War II peak by 2029 under current policies.[10]

Debt Sustainability Debates and Policy Critiques

The sustainability of U.S. federal debt has become a focal point of economic debate, with projections indicating that debt held by the public will rise from 100 percent of GDP at the end of fiscal year 2025 to 107 percent shortly thereafter, reaching unprecedented peacetime levels by the 2050s.[104] Critics argue that persistent primary deficits, driven primarily by entitlement spending on Social Security and Medicare, will exacerbate this trajectory, with gross federal debt projected to climb from 123 percent of GDP in 2025 to 169 percent by 2055 under current policies.[105] The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) estimates that net interest payments on the debt will total $892 billion in fiscal year 2025, equivalent to 3.1 percent of GDP, surpassing projected spending on national defense and non-defense discretionary programs combined.[9] Proponents of fiscal restraint, including the Government Accountability Office (GAO), contend that the underlying fiscal path is unsustainable because revenues fail to keep pace with expenditures, leading to ever-larger borrowing needs that crowd out private investment and heighten vulnerability to interest rate shocks.[106] The U.S. Department of the Treasury's Financial Report echoes this assessment, defining a sustainable policy as one stabilizing or reducing the debt-to-GDP ratio over time, a threshold current trends will breach without reforms to mandatory spending programs, which account for over 60 percent of outlays.[107] Policy critiques often target congressional inaction on structural imbalances, such as the absence of mechanisms to adjust entitlement benefits for demographic shifts like aging populations, which the CBO projects will push federal spending from 23.3 percent of GDP in 2025 to higher shares by mid-century.[108] In contrast, some economists maintain that outright insolvency remains improbable due to the U.S. dollar's status as the global reserve currency, enabling the government to borrow at relatively low rates and monetize deficits if needed, though this view is contested for underestimating risks of inflation or loss of investor confidence.[109] Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell has articulated a middle ground, stating that the current debt level is manageable but the upward trajectory is not, warning that unchecked growth could constrain monetary policy flexibility and elevate long-term interest costs.[110] Empirical analyses, such as those from the Peterson Foundation, highlight that stabilizing debt at 100 percent of GDP by 2035 would require approximately $7.8 trillion in deficit reduction over the decade, underscoring critiques of recent legislation like tax cuts and spending bills that exacerbate deficits without offsetting measures.[111] Policy prescriptions diverge sharply: fiscal conservatives advocate entitlement reforms and spending caps to prioritize growth-enhancing investments, while others propose revenue enhancements through tax base broadening, though both face political hurdles given the entrenched interests in major programs.[112] The Council on Foreign Relations notes that resolving the dilemma necessitates politically challenging choicesâ€”curtailing spending, increasing taxes, or fostering higher economic growthâ€”absent which rising debt could slow GDP expansion by 0.5 percentage points annually after 2050 per CBO models.[109][104] These debates are informed by historical precedents, where high debt burdens have prompted austerity or inflation, but U.S. exceptionalism via seigniorage privileges is increasingly questioned amid global shifts toward diversification of reserves.[113]
