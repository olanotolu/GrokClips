New York's 19th congressional district

New York's 19th congressional district comprises a geographically diverse expanse of upstate New York, including rural counties in the Catskills Mountains, the Southern Tier near the Pennsylvania border, and suburban areas along the Hudson Valley.[1] The district's boundaries, redrawn after the 2020 census, incorporate all of Broome, Chenango, Delaware, Greene, Otsego, Schoharie, and Sullivan counties, as well as Ulster County and portions of Columbia, Dutchess, Montgomery, Oneida, and Rensselaer counties.[2] With a population of approximately 774,000 as of recent estimates, it features a median household income of $68,506 and a median age of 41.8 years, reflecting a blend of agricultural, manufacturing, and tourism-driven economies.[3] As of 2025, the district is represented in the United States House of Representatives by Democrat Josh Riley, who assumed office following his narrow victory over Republican incumbent Marcus Molinaro in the 2024 election.[4] [5] This seat has been characterized by electoral volatility, flipping from Democratic control under Antonio Delgado (2019â€“2022), who later became New York Lieutenant Governor, to Republican hands in 2022 before reverting in 2024.[6] [7] The district's competitiveness stems from its demographic mix, with conservative-leaning rural precincts offsetting more progressive suburban enclaves, often resulting in margins under 5% in recent contests. Historically, the district traces its origins to the early 19th century and has produced figures of national prominence, such as William A. Wheeler, who served as the 19th Vice President of the United States from 1877 to 1881 during Rutherford B. Hayes's administration. Boundary adjustments, influenced by population shifts and periodic redistricting litigationâ€”including a 2022 state court invalidation of a Democratic-proposed map for excessive partisanshipâ€”have continually reshaped its contours to comply with equal population and compactness standards under the New York Constitution. This evolution underscores the district's role in broader debates over fair representation in a state where urban Democratic strongholds contrast with upstate Republican-leaning areas.

Geography and Composition

Counties, towns, and municipalities

New York's 19th congressional district comprises the entirety of eight counties: Broome, Tioga, Tompkins, Chenango, Delaware, Greene, Sullivan, and Columbia. It also includes portions of Ulster, Dutchess, and Albany counties.[8] These areas span diverse geographic regions, including the Southern Tier, Catskill Mountains, and Hudson Valley.[8] Prominent municipalities within the district include the city of Binghamton in Broome County, Ithaca in Tompkins County, and the city of Hudson in Columbia County.[9][10] Binghamton serves as a small urban center in the Southern Tier, while Ithaca and Hudson represent compact urban and semi-urban locales amid predominantly rural surroundings.[8] The district's terrain features rolling hills and agricultural plains in the Southern Tier counties of Broome, Tioga, and Chenango; the rugged Catskill Mountains in Greene, Delaware, and Sullivan counties, supporting forestry and outdoor recreation; and the more moderated landscapes of the Hudson Valley in Columbia and parts of Ulster and Dutchess counties, characterized by river valleys and historic settlements.[8] Overall, the composition emphasizes rural expanses interspersed with suburban developments near population centers and limited small-scale urban areas.[8]

Boundary changes from redistricting

Following the 2000 census, which maintained New York's 29 congressional seats, redistricting consolidated the 19th district around rural upstate and Catskills regions to achieve population equality near 654,300 residents per district, incorporating full counties such as Delaware, Greene, and Schoharie alongside portions of Ulster and Otsego.[11] The 2010 census, resulting in a reduction to 27 seats and a target population of about 717,707, preserved the district's core rural composition but adjusted boundaries to account for uneven growth, retaining counties like Chenango and Delaware while fine-tuning suburban edges in Saratoga and Oneida counties for balance.[12] After the 2020 census, which apportioned New York 26 seats with an ideal population of 775,621, the special master's court-drawn map effective for 2022 expanded the 19th district to encompass entire counties including Broome (the most populous addition, centered on Binghamton), Tompkins (home to Ithaca), and Sullivan, while removing Madison County and refining splits in Montgomery and Herkimer to equalize population and adhere to contiguity requirements.[13][14] These shifts subtracted more densely populated central areas previously linked to urban influences, emphasizing a rural-suburban mix across approximately 10,000 square miles. Subsequent legislative adjustments in February 2024, signed by Governor Kathy Hochul, introduced minimal tweaks such as boundary refinements in Ulster and Rensselaer counties but retained the full-county inclusions of Broome and Tompkins for ongoing population parity.[15][16]

Demographics and Socioeconomics

Population and ethnic composition

As of the 2023 American Community Survey 1-year estimates, New York's 19th congressional district had a total population of 769,461 residents, reflecting the boundaries established after the 2022 redistricting for the 119th Congress.[17] The district's population density stands at approximately 98 persons per square mile across its 7,848 square miles, indicative of its largely rural character punctuated by small urban areas such as Ithaca and Binghamton.[17] The ethnic composition is predominantly white, with non-Hispanic whites accounting for about 80.3% of the population (623,209 individuals), followed by Hispanic or Latino residents of any race at 6.6% (51,366), those identifying with two or more races at 4.8% (37,698), Black or African Americans at 4.1% (32,020), Asians at 3.5% (27,117), American Indians and Alaska Natives at 0.2% (1,594), and Native Hawaiians and other Pacific Islanders at 0.02% (181).[2] This makeup exceeds the statewide non-Hispanic white share of roughly 52% and contrasts with New York state's more urbanized and diverse demographics, where Hispanics comprise about 19% and Blacks 15%. Ithaca's university-driven diversity contributes to elevated Asian (around 10% locally) and multiracial populations in Tompkins County, while counties like Sullivan exhibit relatively higher Native American representation (over 1% countywide) compared to the district average. The district's median age is 42 years, higher than the New York state median of 39.2 and the national figure of 38.5, signaling an aging populace consistent with rural upstate trends.[17] Post-2020, the area experienced modest net migration gains amid broader rural appeal during the COVID-19 pandemic, though population growth remained subdued outside college-influenced locales.

Economic and educational profile

The median household income in New York's 19th congressional district stood at $68,506 in 2023, below the statewide figure of $81,386 reported for the same year by the American Community Survey.[3] The district's poverty rate was approximately 13%, reflecting economic pressures in rural and post-industrial areas despite anchors in higher education and services. Key industries include manufacturing, which employs workers in advanced materials and machinery production in Broome and Tioga counties; agriculture, dominated by dairy and livestock operations with 4,516 farms across the district generating substantial sales value; healthcare and social assistance; educational services; and tourism tied to the Catskills, Hudson Valley, and Finger Lakes attractions.[3][18] Unemployment rates averaged around 4% district-wide in recent years, though pockets in the Southern Tier such as Broome County have hovered near 5%, influenced by legacy manufacturing declines and slower recovery in smaller towns.[3] Debates over hydraulic fracturing in Delaware County underscore potential for natural gas extraction jobs amid Marcellus Shale reserves, but New York's 2014 statewide moratorium has constrained development, prioritizing environmental safeguards over energy sector expansion.[18] Educational attainment averages 32% of adults aged 25 and older holding a bachelor's degree or higher, trailing the state average of 39% but elevated by urban hubs.[3] In Tompkins County, home to Cornell University and Ithaca College, over 50% of residents possess advanced degrees, driving research and innovation clusters, while rural counties like Chenango lag at under 20%, highlighting disparities between college towns and agricultural interiors.[19] High school completion rates exceed 92% district-wide, supported by community colleges and SUNY campuses in Binghamton and Cortland.[3]

Historical Development

Pre-2003 boundaries and representation

Prior to the 2003 redistricting, New York's 19th congressional district, as redrawn after the 1990 census and effective for the 103rd Congress in 1993, covered suburban and exurban territories in the eastern Hudson Valley and foothills of the Catskills. The boundaries included all of Putnam County, significant portions of Dutchess and Ulster counties along the Hudson River, and extensions into parts of Orange, Rockland, and Sullivan counties, encompassing communities like Beacon, Cold Spring, and Monticello.[20] This configuration shifted the district away from its prior urban focus in the late 1980sâ€”primarily the Bronx and southern Westchester Countyâ€”toward a voter base characterized by commuters to New York City, small-town residents, and rural agricultural interests, reflecting post-World War II population migrations and economic transitions from manufacturing to service-oriented suburbs.[21] Representation during this period transitioned to Republican control amid national GOP gains in the 1994 midterm elections. Sue W. Kelly, a Republican, served from January 3, 1995, to January 3, 2007, after winning the seat in the 1994 election with 51% of the vote against Democratic incumbent's successor challenges.[22] [23] Previously, Democrat Eliot L. Engel held the district from January 3, 1989, to January 3, 1993, under the pre-1992 boundaries that emphasized urban demographics.[24] Kelly's tenure emphasized issues pertinent to the district's suburban-rural makeup, including infrastructure improvements for Hudson Valley commuters and support for Catskills tourism and farming, aligning with the area's conservative tilt driven by fiscal concerns and limited government preferences among upstate and exurban voters. The pre-2003 boundaries remained largely unchanged through the 2000 census, which recorded population increases in Hudson Valley suburbs prompting minor adjustments in later cycles but preserving the district's stable character until the 2002 redistricting process. This era underscored the district's evolution into a GOP-leaning seat, rooted in the conservative inclinations of its post-industrial, commuter-heavy electorate, though it retained competitiveness in statewide races.

2003â€“2013 period

Following the 2000 redistricting, New York's 19th congressional district maintained stable boundaries through the 2010 census, encompassing all of Putnam County and portions of Dutchess, Orange, Ulster, and Westchester counties in the Hudson Valley region. This configuration included suburban areas north of New York City, such as Beacon and Cold Spring, alongside rural communities, blending commuter influences from Westchester with agricultural and small-town economies upriver. Republican Sue Kelly represented the district from 1995 until her defeat in the 2006 midterm elections by Democrat John Hall, a musician and environmental advocate, who secured 51.6% of the vote amid a national Democratic surge driven by dissatisfaction with the Iraq War and Republican scandals. Kelly, a moderate with committee roles on education and homeland security, had won prior elections by wide margins, reflecting the district's Republican lean, but Hall's campaign emphasized independent credentials and local issues like property taxes.[25] Hall retained the seat in 2008 with 58.8% against Republican George Pelekanos, benefiting from Barack Obama's statewide victory and anti-Republican sentiment post-financial crisis. Hall lost to Republican Nan Hayworth in the 2010 wave election, where Hayworth garnered 54.5% amid Tea Party mobilization over fiscal concerns and the Affordable Care Act's passage. Hayworth, an ophthalmologist and fiscal conservative, focused on deficit reduction and job growth during her 2011â€“2013 term, aligning with the district's economic pressures from manufacturing job lossesâ€”Upstate New York shed over 105,000 manufacturing positions from 2000 to 2008, impacting Hudson Valley suppliers tied to sectors like electronics and machinery.[26] The 2008 recession exacerbated unemployment in the district's rural and exurban pockets, fostering voter shifts toward candidates promising economic recovery without expansive federal intervention, though the area's proximity to New York City buffered some decline through commuting and service sector resilience.[27] Hayworth's tenure ended with her narrow 2012 defeat to Democrat Sean Patrick Maloney, but the 2003â€“2013 era underscored the district's swing potential amid alternating partisan control.

2013â€“2023 shifts

Following the 2010 redistricting, which adjusted New York's congressional boundaries to account for post-census population shifts while preserving the 19th district's core rural expanse across the Catskills, Hudson Valley, and parts of the Southern Tier, Republican Chris Gibson maintained representation from January 2011 to January 2017.[12] Gibson, a U.S. Army veteran and moderate Republican, emphasized bipartisan cooperation on issues like veterans' affairs and infrastructure before retiring to pursue other opportunities. John Faso (R), a former state assemblyman, succeeded him by winning the open 2016 election against Democrat Zephyr Teachout with 53.5% of the vote, securing the seat amid the district's competitive but Republican-leaning dynamics.[28] The district flipped Democratic in the 2018 midterm elections, as national anti-Trump sentiment fueled a "blue wave" that ousted Faso. Antonio Delgado (D), a Rhodes Scholar and civil rights attorney, defeated Faso with 54.2% of the vote in a race marked by heavy Democratic spending and focus on health care access. Serving from January 2019 to May 2022, Delgado prioritized economic revitalization, including infrastructure investments and job creation in economically distressed rural communities, while navigating the district's conservative electorate by emphasizing local concerns over national partisan flashpoints.[6] [29] Reelected in 2020, his hold reflected the district's swing status, though persistent rural conservatismâ€”evident in strong Republican presidential marginsâ€”strained Democratic sustainability.[30] Delgado resigned on May 3, 2022, after Governor Kathy Hochul appointed him lieutenant governor, triggering a special election on August 23, 2022, won by Pat Ryan (D), Ulster County executive, who defeated Dutchess County executive Marc Molinaro (R) 53.8% to 46.2% in a contest highlighting abortion rights post-Dobbs.[7] Ryan, however, opted to run in the neighboring 18th district, leaving the full-term November 2022 election open. Molinaro reclaimed the seat for Republicans, edging Democrat Josh Riley 50.2% to 49.8% in a razor-thin victory influenced by inflation concerns and voter fatigue with Democratic national leadership, underscoring the district's volatility amid broader polarization.[31]

2023â€“present redistricting and adjustments

The congressional map for New York's 19th district, implemented following the 2020 census and used starting with the 118th Congress in January 2023, was drawn by a court-appointed special master in 2022 to address legal challenges to the initial state proposal. This map expanded the district's footprint in the Southern Tier region, incorporating the entirety of Broome Countyâ€”including the city of Binghamtonâ€”along with Chenango, Delaware, Greene, Otsego, Schoharie, and Tompkins counties, and portions of Columbia, Herkimer, Madison, Montgomery, Oneida, and Ulster counties.[32][33] The inclusion of full Tompkins County, home to the liberal-leaning city of Ithaca and Cornell University, offset the conservative tendencies of rural strongholds like Delaware and Chenango counties, resulting in a district balanced between urban academic influences and agricultural hinterlands.[32][34] In February 2024, the New York State Legislature approved and Governor Kathy Hochul signed a revised congressional map, effective for the 119th Congress in January 2025, which introduced targeted adjustments to the 2022 boundaries to refine population equality across districts while largely preserving the 19th district's composition. These tweaks involved minor shifts in county portions, such as refinements in Columbia and Ulster counties, but retained the core inclusions of Tompkins and the Southern Tier counties, sustaining the district's geographic and demographic equilibrium.[15][16] As of October 2025, district boundaries remain stable under the 2024 map, with state officials monitoring interim population estimates from the U.S. Census Bureau to anticipate adjustments ahead of the 2030 census, given ongoing rural depopulation trends contrasting with slower urban growth in areas like Ithaca.[35]

Redistricting Controversies

2010 redistricting outcomes

Following the 2010 United States Census, which showed New York losing two congressional seats from 29 to 27 due to slower population growth relative to other states, the New York State Legislature was tasked with redrawing congressional district boundaries to achieve equal population distribution of approximately 717,707 residents per district.[12] The process stalled amid partisan disagreements, with the Democratic-majority Assembly proposing maps favoring Democratic incumbents and the Republican-majority Senate advocating alternatives that protected GOP seats, leading to no agreement by early 2012.[12] Governor Andrew Cuomo threatened to veto any plan deemed excessively gerrymandered, exacerbating the impasse.[11] A federal lawsuit (Favors v. Cuomo) challenged the delay, prompting a three-judge panel to appoint U.S. Magistrate Judge Roanne Mann as special master on March 11, 2012, to develop a remedial map.[11] The court-approved map, finalized on March 19, 2012, largely preserved pre-existing district lines with minor boundary tweaks primarily to equalize population, avoiding significant reconfiguration to minimize disruption.[12] Unlike subsequent cycles, this intervention faced limited further litigation, as the map adhered to one-person, one-vote standards without broader racial or partisan gerrymandering claims succeeding in court.[11] For the 19th district, the new boundaries retained its core composition in the Hudson Valley, encompassing Ulster, Greene, Columbia, and parts of Dutchess and Rensselaer counties, while incorporating small adjustments in suburban areas to account for modest population growth in exurban zones near New York City and static rural demographics upstate.[36] This configuration maintained a rural-suburban mix that historically leaned slightly Republican, reflecting the district's competitive nature without engineered shifts toward one party.[12] The outcome effectively sustained the status quo for upstate districts like NY-19, prioritizing compactness and contiguity over aggressive partisan optimization.[11]

2020 cycle disputes and court interventions

In February 2022, the Democratic-controlled New York State Legislature enacted a congressional redistricting plan following the 2020 census, which included boundaries for the 19th district that incorporated disparate areas such as parts of the Hudson Valley, Catskills, and Southern Tier while splitting communities to favor Democratic incumbents like Antonio Delgado.[33] Republicans challenged the map in Harkenrider v. Hochul, arguing it violated the New York State Constitution's 2014 amendments requiring districts to be compact, contiguous, and respectful of municipal boundaries, and prohibiting partisan gerrymandering.[37] On March 31, 2022, the Steuben County Supreme Court invalidated the plan as unconstitutional due to evident partisan bias and lack of compactness, appointing special master Jonathan Cervas to develop remedial maps.[38] The New York Court of Appeals affirmed the lower court's ruling on April 27, 2022, declaring the legislature's congressional map void for failing constitutional criteria, including excessive gerrymandering that unpacked Republican voters in competitive areas like the 19th district and packed them into others.[39] Cervas submitted proposed maps on May 13, 2022, emphasizing compactness scores and minimal splits of counties and towns; after public input, he finalized maps on May 20, 2022, which the court adopted for the 2022 elections.[40] These court-drawn lines for the 19th district restored a more contiguous configuration spanning rural and suburban areas, reducing the partisan skew evident in the legislature's version.[41] Subsequent challenges emerged in 2023â€“2024 amid Democratic efforts to revise maps mid-cycle. The Independent Redistricting Commission, established by the 2014 amendments, released congressional proposals in late 2023, but the legislature rejected them on February 26, 2024, and enacted an alternative that modestly favored Democrats by adjusting boundaries in swing districts including the 19th.[42] Republican lawsuits, including extensions of Harkenrider litigation, contended the revisions ignored compactness and perpetuated packing of GOP voters; a state appellate court ordered redraws on July 13, 2023, directing the commission to prioritize constitutional standards over partisan outcomes.[43] The final map, passed by the Democratic supermajority in April 2024 following court-mandated modifications, retained moderated lines for the 19th district that avoided extreme gerrymandering, as judicial rulings enforced adherence to empirical metrics like Polsby-Popper compactness rather than electoral projections.[44]

Impact on district competitiveness

The redistricting litigation following the 2020 census preserved the competitiveness of New York's 19th congressional district by invalidating a Democratic legislature-drawn map that sought to maximize partisan advantage. In April 2022, the New York Court of Appeals unanimously struck down the enacted congressional boundaries, determining they violated Article III, Section 4(c) of the state constitution through excessive deviations from traditional districting criteria and an intent to favor one party, as internal documents revealed mapmakers prioritized securing additional Democratic seats beyond what population shifts warranted.[45] This map would have adjusted boundaries to incorporate more reliably Democratic voters into marginal districts, including elements that could have diluted rural conservative strongholds in NY-19 by pairing them with urban-leaning areas from adjacent regions like Albany County, thereby engineering outcomes to align with statewide Democratic majorities rather than local voter distributions.[45] A court-appointed special master subsequently proposed a remedial map, approved in May 2022, which adhered more closely to compactness and contiguity standards while reflecting empirical partisan balances. Under this configuration, NY-19 retained a Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI) of R+3, calculated from presidential voting in 2016 and 2020 relative to national averages, indicating a modest Republican tilt consistent with the district's mix of Hudson Valley suburbs, Catskills rural areas, and Southern Tier communities.[46] This lean contrasted with the blocked map's projected shifts toward even or Democratic tilts in similar districts, preventing the consolidation of urban liberal influences that would have subordinated rural turnout data to broader metropolitan demographics.[46] By upholding constitutional neutrality over partisan optimization, these interventions ensured NY-19 functioned as a genuine battleground, where outcomes hinged on voter mobilization rather than predrawn advantages. The post-ruling boundaries better mirrored causal voter preferences, as demonstrated by sustained narrow margins that defied narratives of inevitable Democratic hegemony in a blue-leaning state, prioritizing representational fidelity to observable turnout over engineered dilution of conservative precincts.[47]

Political Characteristics

Voting patterns in presidential and statewide races

In the 2020 presidential election, voters in New York's 19th congressional district favored Donald Trump over Joe Biden by approximately 5 percentage points, with Trump garnering stronger support in rural counties such as Chenango, Delaware, and Greene, while Biden prevailed decisively in Tompkins County, home to the liberal-leaning Cornell University and Ithaca urban area. This split illustrates the district's underlying partisan dynamics, where agricultural and exurban conservatism offsets pockets of progressive strength.[48] The 2024 presidential contest mirrored this pattern, with Trump again carrying the district amid his national victory, though margins remained tight given the area's even partisan balance as indicated by a Cook PVI score of EVEN, derived from averaging the district's performance against national two-party presidential vote shares in 2020 and 2024.[49] Statewide races further underscore the district's swing character. In the 2022 gubernatorial election, incumbent Democrat Kathy Hochul secured a narrow statewide win over Republican Lee Zeldin by 6.2 percentage points, but district results were closer, reflecting competitive rural turnout for Zeldin and Democratic resilience in suburban Dutchess County portions. Historical gubernatorial contests, such as Andrew Cuomo's 2018 landslide (59% statewide), showed softened margins upstate, with the 19th district delivering more restrained Democratic support compared to downstate urban centers. Senate races exhibit similar variance: Democrat Chuck Schumer won reelection in 2022 by 17 points statewide, yet the district's rural precincts boosted Republican Joe Pinion's share relative to the state average, highlighting vulnerabilities for Democratic incumbents outside high-turnout academic enclaves like Tompkins.[50][51]

Partisan lean and swing district status

The Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI) for New York's 19th congressional district rates it as EVEN, meaning its presidential voting patterns from 2020 and 2024 aligned within 0.5 percentage points of the national average, rendering it a competitive battleground rather than a safe seat for either party.[52] Independent redistricting analyses, such as those from Dave's Redistricting, similarly describe the district as even, with its partisan balance sustained by rural conservative strongholds in counties like Delaware and Greene offsetting the liberal tilt of urban college enclaves such as Ithaca in Tompkins County.[53] Demographic factors contribute to this swing status: the district features a high proportion of white working-class residentsâ€”approximately 70-75% of adults without college degrees, concentrated in rural and exurban areasâ€”which correlates with stronger Republican support on economic and trade issues, while educated voters in higher-education hubs pull Democratic on social and environmental concerns.[3][17] This internal tension prevents partisan dominance, as evidenced by the district's rejection of one-party lock despite national polarization trends. Post-2016 political realignment has amplified volatility, with the district flipping from Republican control in 2018 and experiencing margins under 3% in the 2022 special election and 2024 general election, underscoring its sensitivity to national moods on inflation, immigration, and cultural divides rather than entrenched loyalty.[48] Such patterns challenge narratives of stable leans, prioritizing empirical vote swings over media characterizations of inevitability.

Profiles of key figures

Chris Gibson, a retired U.S. Army colonel with 29 years of service including deployments in combat zones, represented New York's 19th congressional district from 2011 to 2017 after defeating incumbent Democrat Scott Murphy in 2010.[56][54] Gibson prioritized centrist approaches, earning top rankings for bipartisanship among House members by collaborating across party lines on issues like veterans' affairs and agriculture.[57] His retirement announcement on January 6, 2015â€”effective after the 114th Congressâ€”created an open seat, heightening Democratic targeting efforts and contributing to the district's vulnerability in the 2018 blue wave cycle.[58][59] Antonio Delgado, a former hip-hop artist, Rhodes Scholar, and attorney, served as the district's representative from January 2019 to May 2021, becoming its first Black congressman.[60][61] As a progressive Democrat, Delgado advocated for racial equity and social justice initiatives, drawing from his background in addressing systemic inequalities.[62] His congressional tenure, spanning less than two and a half years, concluded with his resignation on May 10, 2021, to accept appointment as New York's Lieutenant Governor under Governor Kathy Hochulâ€”a rapid elevation that underscored his political ascent but limited his legislative output in the House. Critics noted the brevity of his service as potentially curtailing deeper policy impact in the district.[63] Marc Molinaro, who held the Dutchess County Executive position from 2012 to 2022 after earlier terms in the New York State Assembly, represented the 19th district from January 2023 until his electoral defeat in November 2024.[64][65] A moderate Republican attuned to the district's agricultural economy, Molinaro served on the House Agriculture Committee and supported bipartisan farm bill provisions while promoting farmland protection plans during his county tenure.[66][67] He frequently critiqued Democratic fiscal policies amid post-2021 inflation spikes, highlighting rising costs in campaign messaging.[68] Molinaro's congressional record garnered acclaim for bipartisanship, with the Lugar Center ranking him second overall in the 118th Congress for cross-party bill sponsorship and cosponsorships.[69][70]

Elections

2018â€“2022 cycles

In the 2018 midterm elections, Democratic challenger Antonio Delgado defeated one-term Republican incumbent John Faso, receiving 173,504 votes (52.0%) to Faso's 163,353 (47.9%), a margin of 4.1 percentage points. The race reflected broader national anti-Trump sentiment fueling Democratic gains in suburban and rural swing districts, with Delgado emphasizing health care access and economic populism against Faso's support for the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.[71] Faso conceded on November 6, 2018, as Democrats flipped the seat amid a nationwide "blue wave" that secured control of the House.[72] Delgado secured reelection in 2020 against Republican Kyle Van De Water, capturing 218,650 votes (54.6%) to Van De Water's 173,546 (43.4%), prevailing by 11.2 percentage points. The contest aligned with Democratic successes in a presidential year favoring Joe Biden, though the district's rural and exurban composition limited larger margins compared to urban areas.[73] Van De Water, a Dutchess County legislator, focused on fiscal conservatism and law enforcement amid COVID-19 recovery debates, but Delgado's incumbency and alignment with Biden's platform sustained the hold. Delgado resigned the seat on May 24, 2022, after winning the Democratic nomination for lieutenant governor, triggering a special election on August 23, 2022, which Democrat Pat Ryan won over Marc Molinaro (R) by 8,329 votes (3.5 percentage points), with Ryan at 56,763 (52.0%) to Molinaro's 52,934 (48.5%). Ryan's narrow victory in the specialâ€”emphasizing abortion rights post-Dobbs and veteran statusâ€”provided a brief Democratic interim hold amid redistricting uncertainties. For the full term in the November 8 general election, Molinaro defeated Democratic nominee Josh Riley 152,067 (50.6%) to 148,017 (49.4%), flipping the seat by 1.2 percentage points in a Republican midterm wave driven by voter concerns over inflation and immigration.[31] Molinaro's prior tenure (2017â€“2019) and moderate positioning on issues like bipartisanship outweighed Riley's prosecutorial background, countering narratives of unchecked Democratic policy expansion under Biden.

2024 election and outcomes

The 2024 election for New York's 19th congressional district was a rematch between incumbent Republican Marc Molinaro and Democratic challenger Josh Riley, both of whom had previously faced off in the narrowly decided 2022 contest. Four major forecastersâ€”Cook Political Report, Inside Elections, Sabato's Crystal Ball, and Race to the WHâ€”rated the race a toss-up prior to Election Day on November 5, 2024. Josh Riley defeated Marc Molinaro, securing 170,049 votes to Molinaro's 164,001, a margin of 6,048 votes out of 334,456 valid ballots cast (excluding scattering votes).[74] This equated to approximately 50.9% for Riley and 49.1% for Molinaro, flipping the seat from Republican to Democratic control.[74] Total ballots cast reached 389,326, reflecting strong voter participation in the district's mix of rural, suburban, and small urban areas.[74] The outcome underscored the district's persistent competitiveness, with Democratic gains in suburban precincts offsetting Republican strength in rural strongholds amid debates over economic pressures on agriculture and energy policy in a farm-heavy region.[75] Riley's victory contributed to Democratic efforts to reclaim House seats in upstate New York, though the slim margin highlighted ongoing volatility in the district's partisan balance.[76] Molinaro conceded the race on November 12, 2024, after initial delays in final tallies.[77]
