Max Tuerk

Max Tuerk (January 27, 1994 – June 20, 2020) was an American football offensive lineman who excelled at the University of Southern California (USC), earning accolades as a versatile starter, before a brief professional career in the National Football League (NFL) with the San Diego Chargers and Arizona Cardinals.[1][2] At USC, Tuerk was a four-year starter and two-time team captain, accumulating 33 starts across positions including left guard (14), center (13), left tackle (5), and right tackle (1); he received Freshman All-American honors from CollegeFootballNews.com and Phil Steele in 2012, All-Pac-12 honorable mention as a freshman, and first-team All-Pac-12 recognition in 2014, while also winning USC's Offensive Lineman of the Year award.[2][3] Drafted by the Chargers in the third round (66th overall) of the 2016 NFL Draft, Tuerk played in just one regular-season game amid recurring injuries, later signing with the Cardinals but never appearing in another contest.[4][1] Tuerk died at age 26 during a hiking trip with his parents in California's Cleveland National Forest; a subsequent brain examination by the Concussion Legacy Foundation diagnosed Stage 1 chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), a degenerative brain disease associated with repeated head trauma in contact sports.[5][6][7]

Early life

Upbringing and family

Max Tuerk was born on January 27, 1994, as the firstborn son of Gregory and Valerie Tuerk.[8] He weighed 9 pounds 8 ounces at birth.[8] Tuerk grew up in Trabuco Canyon, California, alongside his younger siblings Drake, Abby, and Natalie.[8][9] As the eldest, he assumed a protective and mentoring role within the family, often referred to affectionately as "Officer Max" for his leadership qualities.[6] His close bond with his siblings and parents persisted into adulthood; at the time of his death in 2020, he had been living with his parents for several months.[10] From childhood, Tuerk showed enthusiasm for athletics, engaging in taekwondo, soccer, and other team-oriented pursuits that emphasized camaraderie and group dynamics.[6] This early affinity for sports foreshadowed his later achievements in football at Santa Margarita Catholic High School in Rancho Santa Margarita, California.[11] His family remained supportive throughout his life, later donating his brain to the UNITE Brain Bank for research following his passing.[6]

High school career

Tuerk attended Santa Margarita Catholic High School in Rancho Santa Margarita, California, participating in football from his early years after starting in Pop Warner leagues.[8] Initially aspiring to play tight end, he was reassigned to the offensive line as a junior in 2010 by coaches Harry Welch and Marty Spalding, a move he initially resisted but ultimately embraced, recognizing its role in his development as a lineman.[12] During his high school tenure, Tuerk competed at both offensive tackle and defensive end, contributing to a physically dominant line that averaged 6 feet 4 inches and 299 pounds in his senior year.[13] As a senior in 2011, Tuerk played a key role in Santa Margarita's undefeated run to the CIF Pac-5 Division title and CIF Division I state championship, the school's second state title, highlighted by a 21-17 victory over Bellarmine College Prep in the state bowl game at Cal State Dominguez Hills.[14][15] His performance in high-stakes playoff games, such as a tight third-round matchup against Mission Viejo, underscored his competitive reliability and physical presence.[12] Tuerk earned widespread recognition for his play, including Cal-Hi Sports All-State first-team offense, Super Prep All-American, Prep Star All-American, ESPN Top 150 recruit, Orange County Register Fab 15 first team, All-Orange County first team, All-Trinity League first team as an offensive lineman, and Long Beach Press-Telegram Best in the West first-team offensive lineman.[2][16] Rated a four-star prospect and the No. 7 offensive tackle nationally by Rivals.com, he committed to the University of Southern California, forgoing transfer considerations to remain with Santa Margarita.[12][17]

College career

University of Southern California

Max Tuerk enrolled at the University of Southern California in 2012 as a highly rated offensive line recruit and quickly earned a starting role as a true freshman, appearing in 13 games and starting five at left tackle for the USC Trojans.[2][18] In his sophomore season of 2013, Tuerk transitioned to guard, starting 13 games at left guard and one at right tackle, contributing to an offensive line that supported a Trojans team averaging 28.6 points per game.[2][19] As a junior in 2014, he moved to center, starting all 13 games there and earning first-team All-Pac-12 honors for his blocking in a season where USC ranked 35th nationally in rushing yards per game at 179.8.[2][7] Tuerk's senior year in 2015 solidified his versatility, with starts at multiple positions including center and guard, culminating in USC's Offensive Lineman of the Year award; over his career, he accumulated 33 starts across left guard (14), center (13), left tackle (5), and right tackle (1).[2][20] He was voted team captain twice during his USC tenure, reflecting peer respect for his leadership and adaptability on the line.[6][20]

Professional career

2016 NFL Draft and Chargers years

Tuerk was selected by the San Diego Chargers in the third round, 66th overall, of the 2016 NFL Draft out of the University of Southern California, where he had demonstrated versatility by starting at left guard, center, right tackle, and left tackle across 38 career games.[21][22] The Chargers viewed him as a potential competitor for the starting center position against incumbent Chris Watt, given his experience snapping the ball in college.[23] On July 7, 2016, Tuerk signed a four-year rookie contract with the Chargers, securing his place on the roster as the team's third-round selection.[24][25] Despite making the initial 53-man roster for the 2016 season, Tuerk did not appear in any regular-season or postseason games during his rookie year, remaining primarily as a reserve offensive lineman amid the team's depth at interior line positions.[26] Entering the 2017 offseason, Tuerk aimed to increase his playing weight to around 300 pounds from 290 pounds the prior year to bolster his effectiveness, but his development was interrupted when the NFL suspended him without pay for the first four games of the regular season on August 22, 2017, for violating the league's policy on performance-enhancing substances.[27][26] The Chargers waived Tuerk on October 3, 2017, shortly after the suspension period concluded, ending his tenure with the team without any recorded game appearances.[28]

Arizona Cardinals stint

Tuerk was signed by the Arizona Cardinals off the Los Angeles Chargers' practice squad on November 6, 2017, to fill the roster spot created by placing center Daniel Munyer on injured reserve.[29] The contract was a two-year deal valued at $1,170,000, with an average annual salary of $585,000.[30] As a center, Tuerk provided depth to the Cardinals' offensive line during the latter half of the 2017 season, amid ongoing needs for interior line support.[4] In his brief tenure with Arizona, Tuerk appeared in one game, recording a single snap in the regular season.[1] He did not start or see significant action, serving primarily in a reserve capacity without notable statistical contributions in pass blocking or run support.[31] The Cardinals released Tuerk on April 12, 2018, as part of roster adjustments ahead of the offseason.[32]

Injuries, PED suspension, and retirement

Tuerk suffered a torn anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) and other knee ligaments in his right knee during USC's game against Washington on October 4, 2015, which sidelined him for the remainder of his senior college season and required season-ending surgery.[33][34] The injury limited his participation in the 2016 NFL Combine and contributed to his inactive status for all 16 games during his rookie season with the Chargers in 2016, as he focused on rehabilitation.[35][36] Prior to the 2017 season, Tuerk tested positive for a performance-enhancing substance, resulting in a four-game suspension announced by the NFL on August 22, 2017, for violating the league's policy on such substances; he publicly attributed the violation to a tainted over-the-counter supplement.[37][27] The Chargers waived him on October 3, 2017, immediately after his suspension eligibility ended, and he joined their practice squad before being signed by the Arizona Cardinals on November 6, 2017.[38][39] With the Cardinals, Tuerk appeared in one game and spent additional time on the practice squad during the 2017 season.[40] The Cardinals released Tuerk on April 12, 2018, after which he did not secure another NFL contract or play professional football.[41][42] This marked the effective end of his professional career, which spanned limited appearances across two seasons with a total of one game played.[1]

Health and death

Pre-death symptoms and medical history

During his college career at the University of Southern California, Tuerk suffered a season-ending knee injury on October 8, 2015, tearing ligaments in his right knee during a game against Washington, which required surgery and limited his participation thereafter.[33] He did not fully recover from this injury, contributing to challenges in his professional transition.[43] In August 2017, while with the Los Angeles Chargers, Tuerk received a four-game suspension for violating the NFL's policy on performance-enhancing drugs, which he attributed to contaminated supplements; this occurred amid reports of uncharacteristic aggressive behavior.[37] [26] Following his 2016 NFL Draft selection, Tuerk began exhibiting behavioral and mental health changes, including social isolation, paranoia, suspicion of others, and erratic actions, marking the onset of a mental health crisis.[6] These escalated to include hallucinations, psychosis, extreme depression described as profound emotional darkness, cognitive mental fog, and severe headaches necessitating complete darkness and silence for relief.[6] He experienced two hospitalizations for mental health treatment, the second prompted by uncharacteristic threats of violence; antipsychotic medications provided temporary stabilization, but insight into his condition remained limited.[6] Tuerk reported no formally diagnosed concussions, though he had sustained numerous probable concussive and subconcussive head impacts from tackle football starting at age 9.[6] Undiagnosed cardiac symptoms were later noted in retrospect, though his severe cardiomegaly went undetected prior to his sudden collapse.[6]

Circumstances of death

Max Tuerk collapsed suddenly on June 20, 2020, while hiking with his parents on the Bell View trail near Los Pinos Peak in the Cleveland National Forest, Orange County, California.[5][44] The 26-year-old former NFL offensive lineman was participating in a routine family outing on a trail described by his family as one of his favorites, with no reported prior signs of distress during the activity.[45][46] Emergency responders were alerted after Tuerk fell unresponsive, but efforts to revive him were unsuccessful, and he was pronounced dead at the scene.[47][48] The incident occurred over Father's Day weekend, prompting immediate statements from his family emphasizing his love for family, teammates, and football, while confirming the hiking context without suspicion of external factors such as trauma or substance involvement.[45][49] An autopsy was conducted by the Orange County Sheriff's Department Coroner's Office to determine the precise medical cause, with initial reports indicating a cardiac event as the immediate precipitant.[48][50]

Autopsy, CTE diagnosis, and causal debates

An autopsy conducted by the Orange County coroner's office determined that Tuerk's death on June 20, 2020, resulted from severe cardiomegaly with left and right ventricular hypertrophy, an undiagnosed enlarged heart condition that led to cardiac arrest while hiking.[51][48] No evidence of external factors such as drugs, alcohol, or trauma contributed to the cardiac event, and toxicology tests were negative.[51] Tuerk's brain was donated to the VA-BU-CLF Brain Bank at Boston University, where researchers diagnosed stage 1 chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) in February 2022, characterized by mild tau protein accumulation primarily around small blood vessels in the brain.[5] Stage 1 CTE, the earliest pathological stage, is typically associated with limited neuropathological changes and variable clinical symptoms, if any, in young individuals with exposure to repetitive head impacts.[5] The diagnosis aligns with findings in other former football players examined post-mortem, though the Brain Bank's affiliation with the Concussion Legacy Foundation, an advocacy group focused on traumatic brain injury risks in sports, has drawn scrutiny for potential emphasis on football-related causation over multifactorial analyses.[6] Causal debates center on whether the CTE contributed to Tuerk's pre-death behavioral changes—such as reported paranoia and delusions—or remained incidental to his fatal cardiac condition. Tuerk's parents attributed some of his "dramatic" personality shifts in his final years to the disease, with his mother stating it "provided some understanding for how things changed," while acknowledging the heart enlargement as the direct cause of death.[5] However, medical experts have not established a direct link between stage 1 CTE and sudden cardiac death, and the heart condition's etiology remains unclear, potentially independent of head trauma or related to factors like genetics or prior performance-enhancing drug use (Tuerk served a 2017 NFL suspension for a positive amphetamine test).[51][5] Broader skepticism in neuropathology questions over-attribution of mild CTE stages to severe outcomes in young athletes, emphasizing that tau pathology alone does not prove symptomatic causality without longitudinal clinical correlation, and cardiac hypertrophy can arise from non-neurological stressors like hypertrophy from athletic training or undetected comorbidities.[5]

Legacy

Tributes and impact on football community

Following Tuerk's death on June 20, 2020, during a family hike, USC Athletics issued a statement expressing profound sadness, noting his contributions as a standout offensive lineman from 2012 to 2015, where he earned first-team All-Pac-12 honors in 2014 and 2015.[52] The Tuerk family released a statement emphasizing his love for teammates, coaches, USC, and the NFL, describing him as a devoted son and brother whose absence left "a giant hole in our hearts."[47] A paddle-out memorial ceremony was held on June 27, 2020, at Salt Creek Beach in Dana Point, California, attended by family, friends, former USC teammates, and NFL players to honor Tuerk's life through surfing tradition.[53] USC coaches, including those from his playing era, remembered him as exceptionally generous on and off the field, with one stating he was "a true Trojan through and through" for his spirit and selflessness.[54] Former high school coach Harry Welch, who guided Tuerk at Santa Margarita Catholic High School, described himself as "heartbroken," praising his resilience and character.[9] Tuerk's case amplified discussions on chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) within football, as his brain donation to the VA-BU-CLF Brain Bank revealed stage 1 CTE alongside significant white matter damage, marking him as one of 12 USC alumni diagnosed posthumously.[6][5] His family, attributing mental health declines post-NFL to CTE despite the primary cause of death being cardiomegaly, launched Max's March, an annual event raising awareness about CTE's effects on players, families, and communities through education and advocacy for delayed tackle football participation until age 15 to reduce risks.[55][56] This initiative, supported by cousin Ryan Nash, underscores Tuerk's legacy in prompting broader scrutiny of repetitive head trauma in linemen and youth sports.[57]

Family perspectives and broader implications

Max Tuerk's parents, Greg and Val Tuerk, described profound changes in his personality following his NFL career, attributing symptoms such as paranoia, delusions—including beliefs about Russians unloading nuclear weapons—and isolation to stage 1 chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), diagnosed posthumously by the VA-BU-CLF Brain Bank.[5] Val Tuerk noted that Max accepted treatment for these mental health issues, including psychosis and depression, and fought them with determination until his death on June 20, 2020, from an enlarged heart as determined by autopsy, though the family believed CTE exacerbated his struggles.[5] [6] The Tuerks' outlook on football shifted markedly after the CTE confirmation; Greg Tuerk stated, "I’m over the game, man. I’m more thinking about the people behind it now," and expressed difficulty watching games due to reflections on players' long-term health.[5] Val Tuerk emphasized the sport's risks, recounting Max's "unbearable and excruciating" pain from brain damage linked to repeated head impacts starting in youth, which she viewed as preventable through alternatives like flag football.[6] In response, the family donated Max's brain to the UNITE Brain Bank to advance CTE research and supports the Concussion Legacy Foundation's Flag Football Under 14 initiative, advocating that children play non-contact versions until high school to avoid early cumulative trauma.[6] They also participate in Max's March, an annual event tied to the foundation's Race to Erase CTE, honoring his legacy while promoting awareness.[57] Tuerk's case underscores broader concerns in American football regarding early-onset CTE in players with brief professional tenures, highlighting white matter damage and cavum septum pellucidum from subconcussive hits accumulated from youth leagues onward.[6] It amplifies calls for enhanced youth safety protocols, as the Tuerks argue that delaying tackle football could spare developing brains from thousands of impacts between fourth and ninth grades, potentially averting similar trajectories of mental decline despite official cardiac causes of death.[6] [5] This perspective contributes to ongoing debates on balancing the sport's benefits against empirical evidence of neurodegenerative risks, without resolving causal links between CTE and non-neurological fatalities like cardiomegaly.[5]
