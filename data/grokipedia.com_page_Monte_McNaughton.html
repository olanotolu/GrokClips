Monte McNaughton

Monte McNaughton is a former Canadian politician who represented the Lambton—Kent—Middlesex electoral district in the Legislative Assembly of Ontario as a Progressive Conservative from 2011 until vacating his seat in 2023.[1] Before entering provincial politics, he served on Newbury Town Council and owned a small business in southwestern Ontario.[2] As a cabinet minister under Premier Doug Ford, McNaughton held portfolios including Minister of Infrastructure and Minister of Labour, later expanded to include Immigration, Training and Skills Development, focusing on workforce policies and economic growth.[1] McNaughton's tenure emphasized pro-worker reforms, notably introducing the Working for Workers Acts (2021 and 2022), which banned non-compete agreements, mandated disconnecting-from-work policies for larger employers, and established rights for digital platform workers such as ride-share and delivery drivers.[3][4] He also oversaw the creation of Skilled Trades Ontario, a Crown agency aimed at promoting apprenticeships and addressing labour shortages in construction and manufacturing, contributing to record apprenticeship registrations during his ministry.[5][6] Stakeholders in the construction industry praised his collaborative approach and efforts to elevate skilled trades careers.[7] His resignation from cabinet on September 22, 2023, followed by leaving the legislature on October 6, 2023, was attributed to pursuing private sector opportunities, amid speculation tied to the provincial Greenbelt land-use controversy, though McNaughton stated it was unrelated.[8][9] Following his departure from politics, he joined Woodbine Entertainment as Executive Vice President of Industry Relations and People.[10] Earlier in his career, McNaughton faced criticism for comments linking Ontario unions to corruption scandals in Quebec and for alleged remarks during partisan exchanges, reflecting his combative stance on labour issues.[11][12]

Personal Background

Early Life and Education

Monte McNaughton grew up in Newbury, a small village in southwestern Ontario, where he learned the value of hard work through involvement in his family's business.[13] [14] As a child, he demonstrated entrepreneurial initiative by starting his first business, #1 Total Lawn Care Services, while in grade six.[15] McNaughton developed an early interest in politics, recalling that he aspired to become a member of provincial parliament even during his grade school years; in the 1990s, he gained initial exposure to legislative processes by working as a page at Queen's Park under Bob Rae's NDP government.[16] McNaughton pursued business education, graduating from Westervelt College, and later completed executive programs at the Ivey Business School of Western University.[10] [17]

Family and Pre-Political Career

McNaughton was born in Newbury, Ontario, where his family owned and operated a local hardware and building supplies business known as McNaughton's, established in 1948 and serving the community with appliances, automotive parts, renovation products, and related services for nearly seven decades.[18][19] His father, Gary McNaughton, and brother, Mike, co-owned the enterprise alongside him prior to his entry into provincial politics.[13][20] The family business emphasized practical retail operations in southwestern Ontario, instilling in McNaughton early experience in small business management.[21] McNaughton completed business training at Westervelt College and executive programs at the Ivey School of Business, Western University, which aligned with his involvement in family commerce and administrative roles.[10] He resides in Mount Brydges, Ontario, with his wife, Kate, and their daughter, Annie.[21][9]

Entry into Politics

Initial Candidacies and First Election

McNaughton first entered provincial politics as the Progressive Conservative Party candidate for the Lambton—Kent—Middlesex electoral district in the October 10, 2007, Ontario general election.[22] He garnered 15,295 votes, representing 36.3% of the total cast, but was defeated by the incumbent Liberal member Maria Van Bommel, who received 18,191 votes (43.2%), by a margin of 2,896 votes.[22][23] The riding, encompassing rural southwestern Ontario including parts of Lambton County, Middlesex County, and Chatham-Kent, had been held by Liberals since 2003.[24] McNaughton secured the Progressive Conservative nomination for Lambton—Kent—Middlesex ahead of the October 6, 2011, Ontario general election, following a redistribution that maintained the riding's boundaries.[23] Campaigning on local economic issues such as agriculture and manufacturing in the energy and auto sectors, he defeated Van Bommel by approximately 7,000 votes, capturing the seat for the Progressive Conservatives amid a broader shift toward the party in rural southwestern Ontario.[24] This victory marked his entry into the Legislative Assembly of Ontario as the member for Lambton—Kent—Middlesex, where he would serve continuously until 2023.[25]

Early Legislative Roles

McNaughton was first elected to the Ontario Legislative Assembly on October 6, 2011, as the Progressive Conservative Member of Provincial Parliament (MPP) for Lambton—Kent—Middlesex, defeating the incumbent Liberal MPP Maria Van Bommel in a riding encompassing southwestern Ontario's agricultural and manufacturing sectors.[26] In the ensuing 40th Parliament (November 21, 2011 – May 2, 2014), he was appointed Opposition Critic for Labour and Critic for Economic Development and Innovation, roles that positioned him to challenge the Dalton McGuinty and Kathleen Wynne Liberal governments' policies on employment standards, union regulations, and provincial economic strategies.[1] In his capacity as Labour Critic, McNaughton focused on advocating for measures to enhance labour market flexibility, including support for right-to-work legislation that would allow workers to opt out of union dues, a policy central to PC leader Tim Hudak's 2014 election platform aimed at curbing compulsory unionism and boosting competitiveness in manufacturing-heavy regions like his riding.[27] [28] He critiqued the Liberal government's handling of collective bargaining and workplace regulations, arguing they imposed undue burdens on small businesses and non-unionized workers, drawing on his background in family farming and agribusiness to highlight rural economic pressures.[21] McNaughton also served as a member of the Standing Committee on Finance and Economic Affairs during this period, participating in examinations of provincial budgets, tax policies, and economic development bills, where he often pressed for fiscal conservatism and incentives for job creation in southwestern Ontario's petrochemical and automotive industries.[1] These early assignments established him as an active opposition voice on economic issues, though the PCs remained in minority status without government influence until the 2018 election.

Opposition Period (2011–2018)

Policy Critic Roles

McNaughton served as Critic for Economic Development and Innovation from October 26, 2011, to September 30, 2013, focusing on reducing regulatory burdens and opposing government inefficiencies in economic policy.[1] In this capacity, he advocated for measures to combat red tape and waste, drawing on his business background to critique Liberal government initiatives that he argued hindered job growth in southwestern Ontario.[21] In September 2013, under PC Leader Tim Hudak, McNaughton was appointed Labour Critic, replacing Randy Hillier after internal caucus disputes.[29] [30] He held this role through the 2014 provincial election, engaging in debates on workplace policies, including scrutiny of union influence and proposed reforms like right-to-work legislation, which Hudak later abandoned amid electoral considerations.[31] McNaughton's tenure emphasized representing small business interests against what he described as excessive labor regulations imposed by the governing Liberals.[27] Following the 2014 election and Patrick Brown's ascension to PC leadership in May 2015, McNaughton was named Critic for Economic Development, Employment, and Infrastructure on September 10, 2015, while also chairing the party's Blue Ribbon Panel on Prompt Payment Legislation to address contractor payment delays in construction.[32] He retained elements of this portfolio into 2016, additionally serving as Critic for Infrastructure from June 29, 2016, to February 7, 2017, where he criticized government spending on projects like highways and transit amid fiscal deficits.[1] These roles positioned him as a vocal advocate for infrastructure investment tied to economic growth, often highlighting delays and cost overruns in Liberal-led initiatives such as the Detroit River International Crossing.[1] Throughout the opposition period, McNaughton occasionally held overlapping critic responsibilities, including for Citizenship, Immigration, and International Trade, using these to push for policies favoring skilled immigration and trade expansion beneficial to manufacturing sectors in his rural riding.[1] His critiques consistently prioritized empirical economic indicators, such as employment rates in Lambton-Kent-Middlesex, over broader ideological appeals, reflecting a pragmatic approach informed by local industry needs.[21]

PC Leadership Bid

Monte McNaughton announced his candidacy for the leadership of the Ontario Progressive Conservative Party on September 17, 2014, becoming the second MPP to formally declare following the resignation of Tim Hudak after the party's defeat in the June 2014 provincial election.[27][33] At age 37, McNaughton positioned himself as a candidate rooted in southwestern Ontario's manufacturing sector, drawing on his experience as a factory worker and business owner to advocate for policies addressing industrial decline and appealing to blue-collar voters alienated in recent elections.[27] During the campaign, McNaughton's team directly recruited over 12,000 new party members across Ontario ridings, contributing to membership growth ahead of the May 9, 2015, convention.[34] He ranked third in fundraising among contenders as of February 2015, trailing frontrunners Christine Elliott and Patrick Brown, while emphasizing a break from the party's establishment approaches that had led to four consecutive electoral losses.[35] McNaughton's platform highlighted economic revitalization for working-class communities, though specific policy details remained focused on broad themes of change rather than detailed legislative proposals.[36] On April 9, 2015, with the race narrowing, McNaughton withdrew his candidacy one month before the vote, endorsing Patrick Brown as the preferable agent of party renewal over Elliott, whom he associated with the entrenched leadership responsible for prior defeats.[37][38] He argued that the contest boiled down to "the same old party establishment versus change," citing Brown's youth and outsider status—despite Brown being only two years younger—as aligning with the need to expand the party's base beyond traditional strongholds.[39] McNaughton's endorsement contributed to Brown's victory over Elliott on the fourth ballot, securing 61.8% of the vote and positioning the party for the 2018 election win under Doug Ford.[40]

Key Legislative Initiatives

During his time in opposition, Monte McNaughton introduced several private member's bills aimed at fiscal restraint, local governance, labour relations, and tax administration. One prominent initiative was Bill 76, the Casino Referendum Act, 2012, introduced on April 26, 2012, which sought to mandate municipal referendums prior to the approval of any new casino or gaming expansion by the Ontario Lottery and Gaming Corporation (OLG).[41] [42] The bill passed second reading on May 3, 2012, with support from 57 members, reflecting concerns over OLG's revenue-sharing decisions that had reduced slots at racetracks, impacting the horse racing industry.[43] McNaughton reintroduced a similar measure as Bill 92 in 2013 to ensure community input on gaming sites, underscoring opposition to top-down provincial decisions on local economic developments.[44] [45] In 2013, McNaughton tabled Bill 74, the Fairness and Competitiveness in Ontario's Construction Industry Act, 2013, which proposed amendments to the Ontario Labour Relations Act to terminate certain pre-existing collective bargaining rights dating to 1958, specifically targeting constraints on non-union firms like EllisDon Corporation.[46] [47] The legislation aimed to enhance competitiveness in the construction sector by allowing greater flexibility in hiring and bargaining, but it faced criticism from unions and the NDP for undermining worker protections, leading to its defeat after limited debate.[48] [49] McNaughton also advanced fiscal accountability through a private member's bill introduced in February 2016 to impose a provincial debt cap, limiting net debt to 40% of GDP and requiring balanced budgets absent emergencies, as a response to Ontario's mounting $308 billion debt burden.[50] [51] The proposal drew mixed reactions, with proponents viewing it as a necessary check on borrowing and critics dismissing it as symbolic without enforcement mechanisms.[51] Another effort, Bill 120, the Estate Administration Tax Fairness Act, 2015, introduced to address inequities in estate tax collection, proposed requiring payment of the estate administration tax prior to issuing certificates of clearance, reversing Liberal changes that delayed collections and allegedly favored wealthy estates.[52] [53] Second reading occurred on September 24, 2015, with McNaughton arguing it would streamline administration and ensure timely revenue for public services.[54] The bill highlighted ongoing debates over probate fee reforms but did not advance further amid government priorities.[55]

Government Ministerial Roles (2018–2023)

Minister of Infrastructure

Monte McNaughton was sworn in as Ontario's Minister of Infrastructure on June 29, 2018, shortly after the Progressive Conservative Party's victory in the June 7 provincial election under Premier Doug Ford.[1] In this role, which he held until June 20, 2019, McNaughton focused on enhancing public-private partnerships (P3s), expanding rural connectivity, and streamlining infrastructure procurement to attract private investment.[1] [21] A key initiative involved reforming Ontario's P3 model to foster greater innovation and competition. On March 19, 2019, McNaughton announced changes to reduce prescriptive output specifications in Infrastructure Ontario's bidding processes, rebalance risk allocation, and open projects to more flexible private-sector proposals, aiming to lower costs and accelerate delivery.[56] These reforms followed global consultations with industry leaders and were intended to make Ontario's infrastructure market more appealing to international investors.[57] To promote these updates abroad, McNaughton led a trade mission to Germany in April 2019, engaging construction and manufacturing firms to encourage participation in Ontario projects.[58] McNaughton also prioritized rural and northern infrastructure, including expansions of high-speed internet and natural gas services. His ministry supported announcements for broadband funding in southwestern Ontario, such as projects in Lambton and Middlesex counties, as part of broader efforts to connect underserved areas by 2025.[21] [59] In January 2019, he unveiled assistance for 58 small communities to develop asset management plans, aiding long-term infrastructure maintenance.[60] Additionally, on March 13, 2019, the government under McNaughton's oversight launched a $30 billion infrastructure program, with Ontario committing up to $10.2 billion across streams like rural/northern development, public transit, and green infrastructure, leveraging private and federal contributions.[61] McNaughton's tenure included directives to optimize government property use, with a 2018 mandate letter instructing the ministry to identify and sell or donate inefficiently utilized assets to fund priority projects.[62] These efforts aligned with the Ford government's emphasis on fiscal efficiency and private-sector involvement, though the minister's short term limited implementation visibility before his reassignment to Labour.[63]

Minister of Labour, Training, and Skills Development

Monte McNaughton served as Ontario's Minister of Labour from June 20, 2019, to October 21, 2019, following his appointment in a cabinet shuffle announced by Premier Doug Ford.[64] [1] On October 21, 2019, the portfolio expanded to Minister of Labour, Training and Skills Development, reflecting the integration of responsibilities for workforce development and apprenticeships into the ministry's mandate.[1] This restructuring aimed to streamline oversight of employment standards, occupational health and safety, and skills training programs amid Ontario's evolving labour market needs.[1] McNaughton's tenure emphasized collaboration with labour stakeholders, including unions and employers, to modernize workplace regulations and address skilled trades shortages.[21] He advocated for reducing Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (WSIB) premiums for businesses while maintaining worker protections, positioning safety as a core priority from the outset.[21] The ministry under his leadership managed key areas such as minimum wage enforcement, hours of work rules, and vocational training initiatives, responding to economic pressures including post-pandemic recovery and industrial sector demands.[65] In June 2022, the role was further broadened to include immigration responsibilities, becoming Minister of Labour, Immigration, Training and Skills Development until McNaughton's resignation on September 22, 2023.[1] He stepped down to accept a position as Executive Vice-President at Woodbine Entertainment, marking the end of a four-year ministerial stint focused on bolstering Ontario's workforce competitiveness.[8] [66] His departure prompted a cabinet adjustment, with David McMurray assuming the labour portfolio temporarily.[8]

Policy Achievements in Labour and Skills

As Minister of Labour, Training and Skills Development from June 2019 to September 2023, Monte McNaughton oversaw significant reforms to Ontario's skilled trades system, including the establishment of Skilled Trades Ontario (STO) as a new Crown agency in January 2022, which replaced the Ontario College of Trades to streamline services, reduce administrative barriers, and enhance apprenticeship training amid labour shortages.[67] These changes represented the most substantial updates to the province's trades and apprenticeship framework in decades, aiming to shorten registration times from 60 days to as few as 12 and eliminate mandatory journeyperson-to-apprentice ratios in certain trades to boost workforce entry.[68] McNaughton launched the Skills Development Fund (SDF) in 2021, allocating over $500 million across multiple rounds to support employer-led training projects in high-demand sectors, resulting in 596 initiatives that trained approximately 500,000 workers by mid-2023.[69] The fund prioritized innovative programs to address skills gaps, with subsequent investments including $90 million in September 2022 and $160 million in August 2023, focusing on sectors like manufacturing, construction, and technology to facilitate rapid upskilling and retooling.[70] Complementary efforts included the Skilled Trades Strategy, which contributed to hundreds of thousands of new apprentices entering the system by emphasizing practical pathways and reducing stigma around trades careers.[7] Under McNaughton's tenure, apprenticeship registrations surged by a record 24 percent in the year leading to June 2023, rising from 21,971 to 27,319, driven by targeted initiatives such as expanded dual-credit programs and the introduction of a "Grade 10 to Apprenticeship Pathway" to accelerate student entry into trades.[5] Female participation saw a 30 percent increase in new registrations during the same period, supported by outreach to diversify the workforce and double the number of career fairs to connect youth with trades opportunities.[71] McNaughton also advanced labour protections through the Working for Workers legislative series, including the 2021 Act (Bill 27), which prohibited non-compete clauses in employment contracts to enhance worker mobility, and required licensing for recruiters to curb exploitation.[72] The 2022 Act (Bill 88) established foundational rights for digital platform workers, such as access to information on payment calculations and a minimum wage equivalent to 120 percent of the general rate for certain engagements, marking Ontario as the first province to regulate gig economy labour in this manner.[4] Additionally, policies like the "right to disconnect" mandated written guidelines for employers with 25 or more staff to limit after-hours communications, promoting work-life balance without mandating paid sick days.[73]

Criticisms and Controversies in Ministerial Tenure

During his tenure as Minister of Infrastructure from June to November 2018, McNaughton oversaw the provincial government's response to concerns over the Waterfront Toronto agency's handling of the Quayside redevelopment project with Sidewalk Labs, an Alphabet subsidiary. Following Ontario Auditor General Bonnie Lysyk's December 5, 2018, report, which criticized inadequate oversight, potential lack of value for taxpayers, and risks to data privacy in the proposed smart-city initiative, McNaughton dismissed three provincial appointees from the Waterfront Toronto board on December 7, 2018, including acting CEO Kirstine Stewart.[74][75] The move addressed the report's findings of insufficient due diligence and contractual ambiguities but drew scrutiny for escalating tensions in the project's governance, with critics arguing it reflected broader provincial intervention in municipal affairs without resolving underlying privacy and commercialization issues.[76] As Minister of Labour, Training and Skills Development from 2019 to 2023, McNaughton faced criticism over the administration of the Skills Development Fund (SDF), a $500 million program launched in 2021 to subsidize workplace training amid labor shortages. A October 1, 2025, Auditor General report faulted the fund's allocation process for lacking fairness, transparency, and accountability, noting that ministry officials, including those under McNaughton, overruled civil service recommendations to approve grants to applicants with political connections, such as lobbying ties and donations to the Progressive Conservative Party.[77] Specific examples included multimillion-dollar awards to entities like a dental brokerage firm and training groups linked to PC donors, raising allegations of favoritism despite civil servants flagging higher-impact alternatives.[78][79] The Ontario Public Service Employees Union (OPSEU) contended the fund diverted public resources to private-sector intermediaries rather than direct worker training, exacerbating inequities.[80] McNaughton defended the program as essential for skills upgrading, but the Auditor General's findings highlighted systemic flaws in decision-making during his oversight.[81] Policy initiatives under McNaughton also elicited debate. The 2022 Working for Workers Act, which banned non-compete agreements and introduced a "right to disconnect" from after-hours communications, was praised for worker protections but criticized by labor advocates as insufficiently enforced and symbolic, with vague guidelines failing to curb exploitative practices effectively.[82][83] Conservative-leaning analysts, including the Fraser Institute, argued that embedded minimum wage increases in related "Working for Workers" bills risked job losses for low-skilled employees, citing empirical studies showing negative employment effects from such mandates.[84] Unions, conversely, faulted McNaughton for prioritizing business interests, such as exemptions in essential services legislation like Bill 124 (2019), which capped public-sector wage growth at one per cent annually and withstood legal challenges until partially struck down by Ontario courts in 2022 for violating collective bargaining rights.[84] These measures, while aimed at fiscal restraint and productivity, fueled ongoing tensions with public-sector unions during economic recovery from the COVID-19 pandemic.

Resignation and Transition

Reasons for Departure

On September 22, 2023, Monte McNaughton resigned as Ontario's Minister of Labour, Immigration, Training and Skills Development, effective immediately, and indicated he would soon vacate his seat as MPP for Lambton—Kent—Middlesex to pursue a private sector opportunity.[8] In his statement, he explained that the decision followed "much soul searching over the summer months" and represented "the right one for me and my family," describing his ministerial tenure as "the honour of a lifetime."[85] [86] McNaughton explicitly denied any connection to the contemporaneous Greenbelt scandal, which involved investigations into protected land removals for development and had prompted resignations from two prior cabinet ministers, Steve Clark on September 4 and Kaleed Rasheed on September 21.[8] [87] The timing fueled opposition speculation, including from NDP Leader Marit Stiles, who described it as "awfully coincidental" amid the Progressive Conservative government's reversal of Greenbelt development plans on September 21 following public integrity commissioner probes and public backlash.[88] However, McNaughton maintained the choice predated these events by months and stemmed from a desire to transition after over four years in cabinet and 12 years as an MPP.[89] [90] No evidence emerged linking his exit directly to the scandal or internal party pressures, though some partisan critics later questioned ties to controversies in his labour portfolio, such as grants from the Skills Development Fund, without substantiation in mainstream reporting.[91] McNaughton's private sector role was announced on October 17, 2023, as executive vice-president of industry relations and people experience at Woodbine Entertainment, operator of Woodbine Racetrack and other Ontario horse racing facilities.[92] [93] This position aligned with his prior advocacy for the horse racing industry, including successful 2019 negotiations to restore funding after earlier provincial cuts.[92]

Immediate Aftermath

Premier Doug Ford announced a cabinet shuffle on September 22, 2023, hours after McNaughton's resignation from his ministerial post, appointing David Piccini to replace him as Minister of Labour, Immigration, Training and Skills Development.[94][95] This marked the third cabinet vacancy addressed by Ford within three weeks, following earlier departures amid the Greenbelt development controversy, though McNaughton explicitly stated his exit was unrelated to those events and driven by private sector opportunities.[85][8] McNaughton resigned his seat as MPP for Lambton-Kent-Middlesex on October 10, 2023, after serving since 2011, prompting Premier Ford to call a by-election in the riding within six months.[26][96] In his farewell statement, he described the role as "the honour of my life" and expressed gratitude to constituents and colleagues.[26] The resignation elicited praise from construction and labour stakeholders for McNaughton's policy contributions, including skilled trades reforms, with groups like the Ontario General Contractors Association highlighting his pro-industry stance.[7] Opposition figures, including NDP Leader Marit Stiles, questioned the timing as "awfully coincidental" given the ongoing government scandals, though no direct evidence linked McNaughton's departure to them.[97] Ford publicly commended McNaughton's service, noting his effectiveness as a "top performer" in cabinet.[89]

Post-Political Career

Private Sector Roles

Following his resignation from the Ontario legislature on October 6, 2023, McNaughton joined Woodbine Entertainment, a company operating horse racing tracks and entertainment facilities including Woodbine Racetrack and Woodbine Mohawk Park, as Executive Vice President of Industry Relations and People Experience.[92][93] In this executive position, announced on October 17, 2023, he focuses on stakeholder engagement within the racing industry and employee relations.[98] As of September 2025, McNaughton continues to hold this role, contributing to leadership in Canada's horse racing sector.[59] Prior to entering politics in 2011, he owned a small business in southwestern Ontario, though specific operational details of that venture remain limited in public records.[21]

Ongoing Public Engagements

Following his resignation from provincial politics in September 2023, Monte McNaughton has maintained an active role in public discourse, particularly advocating for the expansion and inclusivity of skilled trades through keynote addresses and conference participation.[99][57] In October 2024, McNaughton delivered a keynote speech at the Alberta Premier's third annual Summit on Fairness for Newcomers in Calgary, where he emphasized the critical role of skilled trades in Canada's economic development and called for greater provincial influence over immigration to address labour shortages.[99][100] He highlighted initiatives from his ministerial tenure, such as Ontario's 24% increase in apprenticeship registrations (including 30% growth among women), and advocated for measures to make trades more welcoming to women, including appropriate personal protective equipment and dedicated facilities.[100] McNaughton stated, "For far too long, the skilled trades have been unwelcoming to many women," underscoring the need for targeted recruitment of newcomers and youth via programs like Level Up career fairs.[100][101] McNaughton has also engaged with trade organizations across provinces, including fireside chats at the Building Trades of Alberta conference in Jasper in August 2024, where he discussed partnerships between building trades and industry stakeholders.[102][103] In September 2025, he was announced as the keynote speaker for the National Electrical Trade Council (NETCO) Annual Training Conference, held October 1–2 in Prince Edward Island, focusing on skilled trades growth, worker protections, and increasing female participation based on his experience as Ontario's Minister of Labour.[59] These engagements reflect his continued emphasis on purpose-driven careers in trades to strengthen families and economies, as he noted in post-event reflections.[104]
