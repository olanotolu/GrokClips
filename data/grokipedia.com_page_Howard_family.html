Howard family

The Howard family is an ancient English noble house originating in East Anglia, which rose to preeminence through strategic marriages and royal favor, culminating in the creation of John Howard as 1st Duke of Norfolk and Earl Marshal by King Richard III in 1483 following his inheritance of the extensive Mowbray estates.[1][2] The dukedom, the oldest still extant in the Peerage of England, has been held by the family continuously despite interruptions from attainders, positioning them as the premier non-royal duke with responsibilities for organizing major state ceremonies, including coronations, through the hereditary office of Earl Marshal, formalized as such in 1672.[3] Throughout the Tudor era, the Howards wielded significant military and court influence, with figures like Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk, earning augmentations to the family arms for victories such as the Battle of Flodden in 1513, yet faced severe setbacks including the executions of Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, in 1547 for alleged treason involving unauthorized quartering of royal arms, Catherine Howard as Henry VIII's fifth queen in 1542 on charges of adultery and treason, and Thomas Howard, 4th Duke, in 1572 for suspected Catholic plotting.[4] These events reflect the family's entanglement in dynastic struggles and religious tensions, as steadfast Catholics amid Protestant reforms, leading to recusancy fines and temporary forfeitures of titles restored under subsequent monarchs.[5] The Howards' resilience is evident in their enduring landholdings, including Arundel Castle acquired through the 1555 marriage of Thomas Howard, 4th Duke, to the FitzAlan heiress, and their continued role in British ceremonial life, exemplified by the 18th Duke, Edward Fitzalan-Howard, overseeing King Charles III's 2023 coronation despite the family's historic recusancy.[3] Branches such as the Earls of Suffolk, Carlisle, and Effingham further extended Howard influence across the peerage, blending martial prowess, political maneuvering, and cultural patronage in shaping English nobility.[6]

Origins and Early Rise

Medieval Ancestry and Initial Prominence

The Howard family originated among the gentry of Norfolk, England, with their earliest documented prominence stemming from Sir William Howard (died 1308), who served as a justice of the Court of Common Pleas from 1296 until his death and was summoned to Parliament in 1295 as a representative from Norfolk.[7] Likely of local Saxon descent, the family held manors near King's Lynn and Wiggenhall, engaging in legal and administrative roles that elevated their status beyond mere landownership.[7] Descendants continued in public service, with figures like Sir John Howard (c. 1310â€“before 1364) acting as sheriff and escheator of Norfolk and Suffolk, and admiral of the northern fleet, consolidating regional influence through royal appointments during the reigns of Edward II and Edward III.[8] The family's ascent accelerated in the early 15th century when Sir Robert Howard (c. 1385â€“1436), a knight and minor royal official, married Margaret Mowbray (c. 1391â€“1459), eldest daughter of Thomas Mowbray, 1st Duke of Norfolk.[9] This union linked the Howards to the ducal Mowbray line, which traced descent from Thomas of Brotherton, Earl of Norfolk and son of Edward I, granting inheritance rights to vast estates, the earldom of Norfolk, and the hereditary office of Earl Marshal.[10] Through such marital alliances and loyal military serviceâ€”evidenced by Robert Howard's participation in campaigns alongside John Mowbray, 2nd Duke of Norfolkâ€”the Howards transitioned from provincial gentry to contenders for premier peerage, positioning their heir, John Howard, for elevation amid the dynastic upheavals of the Wars of the Roses.[10] This medieval foundation, rooted in administrative competence and strategic kinship rather than innate nobility, underscores the causal role of opportunistic inheritance in noble ascendance.[9]

Acquisition of the Dukedom

The Howard family's claim to the Dukedom of Norfolk originated from the marriage of Robert Howard to Margaret Mowbray, the daughter of Thomas Mowbray, 1st Duke of Norfolk.[11] This union positioned the Howards as heirs to the Mowbray estates and titles through the female line, with John Howard, son of Robert and Margaret, emerging as the senior co-heir following the extinction of the direct Mowbray male line.[12] The Mowbray dukedom itself had been created in 1397, passing through three generations before John Mowbray, 4th Duke of Norfolk, died on 6 January 1476 without surviving male issue, leaving his two daughters as co-heiresses and causing the peerages to fall into abeyance.[13] During the reign of Edward IV, John Howard actively pursued his inheritance claims to the Mowbray lands and associated honors, including the hereditary office of Earl Marshal, but the dukedom remained unrevived.[10] Howard's loyalty shifted decisively to Richard III following the latter's usurpation in 1483, providing military and political support that aligned with Richard's need to consolidate alliances among Yorkist nobles.[13] On 28 June 1483, Richard III formally created John Howard as Duke of Norfolk through a new patent, constituting the third creation of the title and recognizing his hereditary rights to the Mowbray inheritance while also confirming him as Earl Marshal of England.[14] [10] This grant elevated the Howards to the premier non-royal dukedom, with John Howard quartering his paternal arms with those of the Mowbrays and their ancestral Plantagenet claims from Thomas of Brotherton, solidifying heraldic and titular precedence.[10] The creation underscored Richard III's strategy to reward key supporters amid dynastic instability, though Howard's tenure ended with his death at the Battle of Bosworth on 22 August 1485, after which Henry VII attainted but soon restored the title to Howard's son, Thomas.[13]

Historical Trajectory

Tudor Era Involvement and Vicissitudes

Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk, commanded the English forces at the Battle of Flodden on 9 September 1513, where his tactical maneuvers led to the decisive defeat of a larger Scottish army under King James IV, resulting in the death of the Scottish king and over 10,000 Scottish casualties.[15][16] For this victory, Howard received an augmentation of honor to his coat of arms in 1513, symbolizing a bend between crosses symbolizing the captured Scottish standards, and was elevated to the dukedom of Norfolk in February 1514, restoring the family's premier noble status lost after Bosworth.[17] His son, Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk, rose to prominence as a key military and political figure under Henry VIII, serving as Lord High Treasurer from 1523 and leading campaigns such as the suppression of the Pilgrimage of Grace in 1536-1537, where his forces quelled the northern rebellion with reported executions of over 200 rebels.[18] The Howards leveraged familial ties to advance influence: the 3rd Duke's sister Elizabeth was mother to Anne Boleyn, Henry VIII's second wife, while his niece Catherine Howard became the king's fifth wife in 1540, both marriages orchestrated partly through ducal patronage.[19] However, Catherine's execution for adultery and treason on 13 February 1542 implicated the family in scandal, eroding favor despite the duke's survival.[20] The family's fortunes further declined with the attainder of Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, the 3rd Duke's son and heir, who was executed on 19 January 1547 for treason, primarily charged with quartering the royal arms without warrant and aspiring to the throne via claimed Plantagenet descent.[21][22] The 3rd Duke was arrested alongside Surrey, convicted, and spared execution only by Henry VIII's death on 28 January 1547; he remained imprisoned until Mary I's accession in 1553 restored him, though he died in 1554 without reclaiming full power.[18] Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolkâ€”Surrey's son, whose title was restored under Mary Iâ€”initially prospered under Elizabeth I as a Protestant convert and privy councillor, but his secret Catholic sympathies and ambition led to entanglement in plots favoring Mary Queen of Scots.[23] In 1569, Norfolk pursued an unauthorized marriage alliance with the captive Scottish queen, and his involvement in the 1571 Ridolfi Plot to overthrow Elizabeth culminated in his execution for treason on 2 June 1572 at Tower Hill, marking the nadir of Tudor-era Howard vicissitudes amid recurring attainders for overreaching royal kinships.[24][25]

Post-Reformation Challenges and Recusancy

The English Reformation under Elizabeth I imposed stringent penalties on Catholic recusants, including monthly fines of Â£20 for nobles who refused to attend Church of England services, alongside risks of imprisonment and property confiscation. The Howard family, as holders of the Dukedom of Norfolk and leading Catholic nobles, bore the brunt of these measures, with multiple members attainted for treason linked to their faith and political intrigues aimed at restoring Catholicism.[26] Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, exemplified these perils through his involvement in the 1571 Ridolfi Plot, a conspiracy to overthrow Elizabeth I, marry Mary Queen of Scots, and reestablish Catholic rule. Arrested in September 1571 and attainted by Parliament in 1572, he was executed by beheading on Tower Hill on 2 June 1572.[27][23] His attainder stripped the family of the dukedom, which remained forfeited until 1660, compounding financial strains from recusancy fines and estate seizures.[26] Philip Howard, the 4th Duke's son and 13th Earl of Arundel, initially conformed outwardly but openly returned to Catholicism around 1584, prompting his attempt to flee England in April 1585. Captured at sea, he was imprisoned in the Tower of London, fined Â£10,000, and sentenced to life imprisonment by the Star Chamber for recusancy and high treason. He died in captivity on 19 October 1595, reportedly from privations and possible poisoning, without seeing his wife or son again.[28][29] His steadfast refusal to recant, despite offers of freedom, underscored the family's commitment to Catholicism amid ongoing persecution.[30] Subsequent Howard heirs, including Philip's son Thomas Howard, 14th Earl of Arundel, navigated similar pressures, facing fines and surveillance while preserving Catholic networks and artifacts, such as religious relics smuggled into England. The dukedom's restoration under Charles II in 1660 to a collateral descendant marked partial relief, but recusancy laws persisted, enforcing two-thirds property registration for Catholic landowners until the 18th century.[26] This era of attainders and confinement solidified the Howards' reputation as England's premier recusant dynasty, sustaining their faith through covert worship and continental education despite systemic disenfranchisement.[10]

20thâ€“21st Century Continuity

The Howard dukedom of Norfolk passed uninterrupted through the 20th century, with Bernard Marmaduke Fitzalan-Howard succeeding as 16th Duke on 31 October 1917 following the death of his father, Henry, 15th Duke. Born on 30 May 1908, Bernard served in the Territorial Army and held honorary military ranks, including colonel in the Royal Norfolk Regiment, while amassing honors such as Knight of the Garter (KG) in 1947, Knight Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order (GCVO) in 1953, and Knight Grand Cross of the Order of the British Empire (GBE) in 1967; he also sat on the Privy Council from 1953.[35] As hereditary Earl Marshal, he oversaw state ceremonies including the funeral of King George VI in 1952 and the coronation of Queen Elizabeth II in 1953, maintaining the family's longstanding role in royal heraldry despite their Roman Catholic adherence, which had historically barred recusants from public office until Catholic emancipation in 1829.[36] Bernard died on 31 January 1975, succeeded by his eldest son, Miles Francis Stapleton Fitzalan-Howard, as 17th Duke. Born 21 July 1915, Miles pursued a distinguished military career, commissioning into the Grenadier Guards in 1936, serving in World War II campaigns including North Africa and Italy where he earned the Military Cross in 1943, and rising to major-general by 1967 with command of the 1st Armoured Division.[37] Post-retirement, he managed family estates and continued as Earl Marshal from 1975, receiving the Knight of the Garter (KG) in 1983 and Knight Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order (GCVO) in 1986; he died on 24 February 2002 at age 86.[37] The succession underscored the family's resilience, with the Howards adapting to 20th-century upheavals like two world wars and the 1999 House of Lords reform, which reduced hereditary legislative sitting but preserved ceremonial offices.[36] Edward William Fitzalan-Howard acceded as 18th Duke on 24 February 2002, born 2 December 1956 as the eldest son of Miles.[38] Educated at Ampleforth College and Christ Church, Oxford, he worked in finance at James Investment before inheriting; as Earl Marshal, he coordinated the state funeral of Queen Elizabeth II on 19 September 2022 and the coronation of King Charles III on 6 May 2023, drawing on family precedents for such events.[3] Married to Georgina Susan Gore since 27 June 1987, with whom he has five children including heir Henry Miles, Earl of Arundel and Surrey (b. 3 December 1987), Edward has overseen the stewardship of Arundel Castle and other estates, converting the property to generate revenue through tourism and events while preserving its role as a Catholic stronghold.[39] Into the 21st century as of 2025, the Howards remain England's premier non-royal dukedom, with the title's continuity affirmed by unbroken male-line descent from Edward I and sustained influence via the Earldom of Arundel (from 1139) and Marshalcy, unencumbered by modern religious disqualifications.[3]

Notable Members and Branches

Premier Dukes of Norfolk

John Howard (c. 1425 â€“ 22 August 1485) became the first Howard Duke of Norfolk when created on 28 June 1483 by King Richard III, shortly before the coronation. Son of Sir Robert Howard and Margaret de Mowbray, he inherited claims to the Mowbray estates through his mother, daughter of Thomas Mowbray, 1st Duke of Norfolk of the earlier creation. A loyal Yorkist, Howard served Edward IV as sheriff of Norfolk and Suffolk in 1461, constable of Norwich Castle from 1462, and was summoned to Parliament as Baron Howard in 1470. Under Henry VIII, he acted as lord high treasurer from 1501 to 1522 and was appointed earl marshal for life in 1510. His key military achievement came at the Battle of Flodden in 1513, where English forces under his command defeated a larger Scottish army, killing King James IV and securing a decisive victory that earned the Howard arms an augmentation of honour depicting the royal banner. Influential Non-Ducal Howards

Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey (c. 1517â€“1547), eldest son of Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk, distinguished himself as a soldier and poet during the Tudor era. He participated in military campaigns, including the capture of Boulogne in 1544 and expeditions against Scotland, where he demonstrated tactical acumen in suppressing rebellions. Surrey pioneered the use of blank verse in English poetry through his translation of Books 2 and 4 of Virgil's Aeneid, published posthumously in 1557, and developed the Elizabethan sonnet form with its characteristic three quatrains and couplet, influencing later poets such as Shakespeare. Appointed to the position in 1585, he oversaw naval operations against Spain, including raids on Cadiz in 1596, amassing significant wealth through prizes while maintaining loyalty to Elizabeth I and James I. His leadership solidified English naval supremacy in the late 16th century, earning him elevation to the earldom in 1597 and the Order of the Garter.[46] Thomas Howard (1561â€“1626), 1st Earl of Suffolk and son of the attainted 4th Duke of Norfolk, served as vice-admiral under his uncle Charles Howard in the Armada campaign and led assaults in the Azores expedition of 1597, capturing Spanish prizes that bolstered his fortunes. Under James I, he rose to Lord Chamberlain in 1603 and Lord Treasurer from 1618, wielding influence over court patronage despite later imprisonment in the Tower of London from 1619 to 1622 on corruption charges involving bribes exceeding Â£52,000. His administrative roles facilitated the Howard family's recovery post-Reformation attainders, though his fall highlighted Jacobean favoritism and fiscal mismanagement.[47] Other non-ducal Howards exerted influence through politics and recusancy; for instance, Henry Howard (1540â€“1614), created Earl of Northampton in 1603, advised Elizabeth I on religious policy and James I on union with Scotland, leveraging scholarly pursuits in theology and history to navigate Catholic sympathies amid Protestant dominance. William Howard (1614â€“1680), 1st Viscount Stafford, faced impeachment in 1680 for alleged complicity in the Popish Plot, becoming the last peer executed by Parliament on 29 December after a trial marked by partisan accusations, underscoring sectarian tensions in Restoration England.[48]

Cadet Branches like Castle Howard

The Howard family produced several cadet branches descending from younger sons of ducal or earlier lines, diverging from the premier Dukedom of Norfolk while retaining ties to nobility, military service, and estates. Notable among these are the Howards of Carlisle, Effingham, Suffolk, and Penrith, each establishing distinct peerages and properties independent of the main ducal succession.[49][50] The Carlisle branch originated with Lord William Howard (1563â€“1640), youngest son of Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, who acquired Naworth Castle in Cumberland through marriage to Elizabeth Dacre in 1577, thereby founding a northern Howard lineage focused on border governance and recusant Catholicism amid Tudor restrictions.[51] His great-grandson, Charles Howard (1629â€“1685), created 1st Earl of Carlisle in 1661 by Charles II for diplomatic and naval contributions, including service in the Second Anglo-Dutch War, solidified the branch's elevation; the title passed to his son Edward Howard, 2nd Earl (1646â€“1692), and then to Charles Howard, 3rd Earl (1674â€“1738), who commissioned Castle Howard in 1699 as a Baroque masterpiece designed by John Vanbrugh and Nicholas Hawksmoor, completed over a century and serving as the family seat in Yorkshire.[51][52] This estate, spanning 10,000 acres and featuring extensive gardens, has remained the primary residence for nine generations of Carlisle Howards, underscoring the branch's continuity in Whig politics, colonial administration (e.g., 5th Earl Frederick Howard's governorship of Jamaica, 1767â€“1771), and cultural patronage, though financial pressures led to partial openings to the public by the 20th century.[53][51] Other cadet lines include the Effingham Howards, stemming from William Howard, 1st Baron Howard of Effingham (c. 1510â€“1573), fourth son of Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk, who earned fame repelling the Spanish Armada in 1588 as Lord High Admiral; their descendants hold the Earldom of Effingham (from 1731) and later Nottingham, with estates like Effingham Hall in Surrey.[54] The Suffolk branch arose from Thomas Howard, 1st Earl of Suffolk (1561â€“1626), eldest son of the 4th Duke, created earl in 1603 for Jacobean loyalty, though the line extinct in the male Howard descent by 1779 after corruption scandals and naval offices.[50] The Penrith Howards, a more recent offshoot from Henry Howard (1862â€“1921), brother of the 15th Duke of Norfolk, produced Baron Howard of Penrith (created 1930) for diplomatic service, including ambassadorships to Spain and the United States.[49] These branches collectively extended Howard influence beyond Norfolk precedence, often navigating religious tensions and political shifts while preserving heraldic and landed legacies.[10]

Titles, Heraldry, and Precedence

Hereditary Peerages and Offices

The Howard family holds the dukedom of Norfolk, the premier dukedom in the Peerage of England, created on 28 June 1483 for John Howard by King Richard III.[55] This title descends through the male line and is currently held by Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk, who succeeded on 31 August 2002.[3] Subsidiary titles attached to the dukedom include the earldom of Arundel (originally created circa 1139, confirmed 1289), earldom of Surrey (1483), earldom of Norfolk (1644), barony of Maltravers (1330), barony of FitzAlan (1627), barony of Clun (1298), and barony of Oswaldestre (1651).[56] These titles underscore the family's ancient precedence as the premier earl (Arundel) and their extensive feudal holdings in eastern England and Sussex.[55] The family also possesses the hereditary office of Earl Marshal of England, responsible for organizing state ceremonies such as coronations, funerals, and the state opening of Parliament, as well as overseeing the College of Arms.[3] Originally held by the Marshal family and later the Bigods, the office passed to the Howards through their Norfolk inheritance and was made formally hereditary in the dukedom in 1672 by King Charles II.[57] The 18th Duke organized the funeral of Queen Elizabeth II in 2022 and the coronation of King Charles III in 2023.[3] Prior to Catholic emancipation, deputy earls marshal were appointed due to the family's recusancy.[3] Cadet branches hold additional peerages. The earldom of Suffolk, created in 1603 for Thomas Howard (son of the 4th Duke of Norfolk), remains extant with the 21st Earl as of 2023.[58] The earldom of Carlisle, granted in 1661 to Charles Howard (grandson of the 1st Duke), was held by Howards until its extinction in 1911 following the death of the 7th Earl without male issue.) The earldom of Effingham, elevated from a viscountcy in 1731 for Charles Howard (descendant of the 2nd Duke), persists in the Effingham branch. Other titles, such as Viscount Stafford (created 1640, attainted 1680) and Earl of Berkshire (1626, extinct 1701), originated in younger sons but have lapsed.

Armorial Bearings and Symbols

![Coat of arms of the Howard Dukes of Norfolk, quarterly with augmentation, Brotherton, Warenne, and Mowbray][float-right] The armorial bearings of the Howard family originated with the ancient Howard arms: Gules, a bend between six cross-crosslets fitchy or.[59] This design, attributed to early Howards such as Sir John Howard in the 14th century, symbolized their rising status in East Anglian nobility.[60] The bend charged with cross-crosslets fitchy denoted steadfastness and crusading heritage, common in medieval English heraldry for knightly families.[11] Following the creation of the dukedom in 1483 for John Howard, 1st Duke of Norfolk, the arms incorporated quarterings reflecting marital alliances and claims to the Earldom of Norfolk. These included the second quarter for Thomas of Brotherton, 1st Earl of Norfolk (Gules, three lions passant guardant in pale or, armed and langued azure, a label of three points argent), inherited via the female line from Edward I's son. The third quarter adopted the Warenne, Earls of Surrey (Chequy or and azure), and the fourth the Mowbray arms (Gules, a lion rampant argent), acquired through the 1481 marriage of Sir Robert Howard to Margaret Mowbray, daughter and heiress of Thomas Mowbray, 1st Duke of Norfolk.[11][61] A significant augmentation of honor was granted in 1513 to Thomas Howard, 2nd Duke of Norfolk, for his decisive role in the English victory at the Battle of Flodden against Scotland. This added an escutcheon to the Howard bend: Or, a demi-lion rampant pierced through the mouth with an arrow gules, derived from the Scottish royal arms to commemorate the slaying of King James IV. The full ducal achievement thus became quarterly: 1st and 4th Howard augmented, 2nd Brotherton, 3rd Warenne, with Mowbray in the fourth until modifications.[11][62] In 1555, Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, married Mary FitzAlan, suo jure Baroness Maltravers and heiress to the Earldom of Arundel, introducing the FitzAlan quarter (Gules, a lion rampant or) which replaced Mowbray in standard usage by 1842 when the 14th Duke prefixed FitzAlan-Howard.[63] The crest features a pair of wings gules each charged with a bend between six cross-crosslets fitchy or, issuant from a ducal coronet, while supporters include a lion argent ducally crowned or for Brotherton and a white lion of Mowbray. Family badges, such as the chained bull from Warrenne lineage, further symbolize their Surrey heritage. Cadet branches differenced the arms, for instance, the Earls of Suffolk adding a crescent argent.[64]

Estates, Patronage, and Cultural Impact

Principal Seats and Properties


The principal seat of the Howard family, specifically the Dukes of Norfolk, is Arundel Castle in West Sussex, England, which has been continuously held by the family since the marriage of Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, to Mary FitzAlan, daughter and sole heiress of Henry FitzAlan, 12th Earl of Arundel, on 25 February 1555.[65] This union transferred ownership of the castle, originally constructed in 1067 following the Norman Conquest, from the FitzAlan family to the Howards, establishing it as their chief residence despite periods of royal custody due to attainders.[66] The castle underwent significant Gothic Revival restorations between 1875 and 1905 under Henry Fitzalan-Howard, 15th Duke of Norfolk, transforming it into its current form while preserving medieval elements like the motte, gatehouse, and barbican.[67] Prior to acquiring Arundel, the Howards' primary residences were concentrated in East Anglia, reflecting their Norfolk roots and inheritance from the Mowbray line. Framlingham Castle in Suffolk served as a key stronghold, modernized by John Howard, 1st Duke of Norfolk, after he received the dukedom in 1483, and remained a power base through the 15th and early 16th centuries.[68] Kenninghall Palace in Norfolk functioned as the principal country residence for Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk, during the Tudor era, as documented in a 1546 inventory detailing its opulent furnishings before its demolition in the 17th century.[69] Among other notable properties, Carlton Towers in North Yorkshire, a Grade I listed Victorian Gothic house rebuilt in the 1870s, is owned by the current Duke and serves as the residence of his brother, Lord Gerald Fitzalan-Howard.[39] The family also retains Greystoke Castle in Cumbria, acquired through earlier unions and held since the 14th century, though it is not a primary seat.[70] These estates, alongside broader holdings in Sussex, Surrey, and Derbyshire, underscore the Howards' enduring landownership, with Arundel remaining the symbolic and functional heart of their patrimony.[1]

Artistic and Architectural Legacies

The Howard family's architectural legacies center on major estates that exemplify evolving English building traditions. Arundel Castle, the ancestral seat of the Dukes of Norfolk since the 11th century, underwent significant Gothic Revival restoration under Henry Fitzalan-Howard, 15th Duke of Norfolk (1847â€“1917), transforming it into one of the earliest fully restored medieval castles in England, with work completed by 1900.[71] This project preserved the motte-and-bailey structure originally established post-Norman Conquest while incorporating Victorian-era embellishments, reflecting the family's commitment to historical continuity amid modernization.[71] In the cadet branch, Castle Howard stands as a Baroque masterpiece commissioned in 1699 by Charles Howard, 3rd Earl of Carlisle, designed by playwright-architect Sir John Vanbrugh with Nicholas Hawksmoor.[53] Construction spanned over a century, featuring dramatic domes, columns, and landscaped grounds that influenced subsequent English country house architecture, blending theatricality with classical elements.[53] Artistically, the Howards were prominent patrons from the Tudor era onward. Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk (1473â€“1554), commissioned portraits from Hans Holbein the Younger, including a 1539â€“1540 tempera depiction now in the Royal Collection, underscoring their role in advancing Renaissance portraiture in England.[72] Later, Thomas Howard, 14th Earl of Arundel (1585â€“1646), amassed extensive collections of paintings, sculptures, and antiquities, patronizing artists like Anthony van Dyck, whose works captured the family's status and contributed to the influx of Continental art into Britain.[73] These efforts, including the acquisition of classical marbles later housed at institutions like the Ashmolean Museum, positioned the Howards as key conduits for artistic heritage preservation.[74]

Contemporary Status and Role

The Current Duke and Family

Edward William Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk, born on 2 December 1956, succeeded to the peerages and office of Earl Marshal upon the death of his father, Miles Francis Stapleton Fitzalan-Howard, 17th Duke of Norfolk, on 24 February 2002.[38] The duke, a Roman Catholic like many of his forebears, holds the position of England's premier duke and most senior non-royal peer.[39] The duke married Georgina Susan Gore, daughter of John Temple Gore, on 27 June 1987 at Arundel Cathedral; the marriage produced five children before ending in divorce in 2022 after a prolonged separation.[38][75] Their children are Henry Miles Fitzalan-Howard, Earl of Arundel (born 3 December 1987), the heir apparent who married Cecilia Mary Dennie on 27 April 2024; Lady Rachel Rose Fitzalan-Howard (born 10 June 1989); Lord Thomas Philip Fitzalan-Howard (born 14 July 1992); Lady Isabel Teresa Fitzalan-Howard; and Lord Philip Edward Fitzalan-Howard.[38][76] The earl of Arundel and his wife have two daughters, born in 2015 and 2017.[39] On 10 November 2022, the duke married Francesca Vaughan Herbert (nÃ©e Bevan), a mother of three from her previous marriage to Harry Herbert, son of the 7th Earl of Carnarvon, at Chelsea Old Town Hall.[77][78] The family maintains close ties to Arundel Castle, the ancestral seat, where the duke and his children continue the Howard tradition of Catholic devotion and public service.[79]

Earl Marshal Responsibilities in Modern Events

The office of Earl Marshal, vested in the Duke of Norfolk, retains responsibility for coordinating ceremonial aspects of major state events in the United Kingdom, including royal funerals, coronations, and the annual State Opening of Parliament. This entails oversight of processions, heraldic displays, participant arrangements, and logistical coordination via the College of Arms, ensuring adherence to historical precedents while accommodating contemporary requirements such as security and media coverage.[80][81] Following the death of Queen Elizabeth II on 8 September 2022, Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk and current Earl Marshal, directed preparations for her state funeral on 19 September 2022 at Westminster Abbey. His duties included organizing the procession from Westminster Hall through London to the abbey, subsequent transfer to Wellington Arch, and onward journey to Windsor Castle for the committal service, involving coordination with military units, foreign dignitaries, and over 2,000 attendees.[82][83] For the coronation of King Charles III on 6 May 2023, the Earl Marshal managed the event's execution at Westminster Abbey, handling guest invitations for approximately 2,200 participants, procession planning along the Mall, and integration of heraldic elements like the College of Arms' oversight of peerage robes and regalia. Adaptations included streamlined timings and enhanced accessibility, reflecting operational efficiencies developed from prior exercises like Operation London Bridge.[84][85] In the State Opening of Parliament, occurring annually or at session starts, the Earl Marshal arranges heraldic processions and ceremonial protocols, such as the sovereign's entry to the House of Lords accompanied by officers like the Lord Great Chamberlain, ensuring symbolic continuity in Westminster Palace proceedings. This role persists despite legislative reforms reducing hereditary peers' legislative influence post-1999 House of Lords Act.[80][86]

Controversies and Debates

Historical Accusations of Treason and Ambition

Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey and eldest son of Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk, was arrested in December 1546 on charges of high treason. The primary accusation centered on his use of undifferenced royal arms in his heraldry, quartered with the Howard arms, which was interpreted as an illicit claim to the English throne or succession rights, reserved exclusively for the monarch.[87] This act was viewed as emblematic of the Howard family's longstanding ambition, leveraging their descent from Edward I through Thomas of Brotherton to assert quasi-royal status amid Tudor power struggles. Surrey was tried at Guildhall on January 13, 1547, convicted by a jury, and beheaded on Tower Hill on January 19, 1547.[88][45] The conviction implicated the 3rd Duke of Norfolk, who signed a confession acknowledging his son's guilt, leading to the Duke's attainder by Parliament on January 27, 1547, and imprisonment in the Tower of London.[43] Although scheduled for execution alongside his son, the Duke's death sentence was not carried out due to Henry VIII's own death on January 28, 1547; he remained imprisoned until released and restored by Queen Mary I in 1553.[43] These events underscored accusations of familial overreach, with the Howards' political maneuveringâ€”rooted in conservative Catholic leanings and rivalry against rising Protestant factions like the Seymoursâ€”portrayed as treasonous ambition threatening royal supremacy. Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk and grandson of the 3rd Duke, faced similar charges of treason in 1571, culminating in his execution on June 2, 1572. Arrested in October 1571 for his role in the Ridolfi Plotâ€”a Catholic conspiracy to overthrow Queen Elizabeth I by arranging his marriage to Mary, Queen of Scots, and installing her on the English throneâ€”Norfolk was accused of corresponding with foreign powers and plotting regicide.[23] At his 12-hour trial on January 16, 1572, before 26 peers, he pleaded innocence but was convicted based on evidence including letters and witness testimony from plotters like Roberto di Ridolfi.[23] Beheaded on Tower Hill, his downfall reflected persistent Howard ambition for dominance, as the Duke sought to leverage his premier noble status and the family's royalist heritage to counter Elizabeth's Protestant regime, despite prior warnings and house arrest.[89] These episodes highlight a pattern in Howard history where political ambition, often tied to Catholic sympathies and claims of ancient precedence, intersected with treason accusations during eras of monarchical consolidation under the Tudors. While convictions were secured through parliamentary acts and trials, contemporary accounts and later analyses suggest elements of factional intrigue, with the family's extensive influenceâ€”holding the hereditary Earldom of Arundel and Marshalcyâ€”inviting preemptive strikes from rivals.[90] Restoration of titles in subsequent generations, such as under James I for Norfolk's son, indicates the accusations did not fully eradicate the lineage's prestige.[89]

Religious Persecution and Familial Resilience

Thomas Howard, 4th Duke of Norfolk, faced execution on 2 June 1572 for high treason linked to the Ridolfi Plot, a scheme involving Catholic interests to depose Elizabeth I in favor of Mary, Queen of Scots, reflecting the perilous intersection of the family's religious allegiance and political intrigue.[91][92] Although Norfolk publicly denied Catholic adherence at his trial, his known sympathies and associations underscored the regime's suspicion of aristocratic Catholics as threats to the Protestant settlement.[91] Philip Howard, the 4th Duke's son and 13th Earl of Arundel, openly embraced Catholicism circa 1584, leading to his arrest in 1585 for recusancyâ€”refusal to attend Anglican servicesâ€”and alleged treasonous correspondence. Imprisoned in the Tower of London for a decade, he endured isolation, denied family access, and suffered health decline, dying on 19 October 1595 at age 38; contemporaries and later the Church viewed his death as martyrdom from privation rather than natural causes.[93][94] Canonized by Pope Paul VI on 25 October 1970 among the Forty Martyrs of England and Wales, Philip's steadfast refusal to recant exemplified individual fortitude amid systemic penalties including fines, property sequestration, and execution risks for recusants.[93] The attainders following these convictions stripped the Howards of ducal and several earldoms' titles until partial restorations under James I in 1604 for Philip's son, Thomas Howard, 14th Earl of Arundel, yet the family persisted in Catholicism, incurring ongoing recusancy finesâ€”exceeding Â£6,000 annually by the 17th century for some membersâ€”while educating heirs abroad in Catholic institutions and conducting secret Masses.[10] This covert continuity, blending pragmatic conformity with private devotion, enabled generational transmission of faith despite penal laws equating recusancy with disloyalty, culminating in the dukedom's full reinstatement in 1660 to a Catholic line that has held it uninterrupted.[10] Familial resilience manifested in strategic marriages to continental Catholic nobility, preservation of heirloom relics like Philip's preserved heart, and advocacy for toleration, as later dukes petitioned monarchs amid intermittent amnesties; by navigating persecution without wholesale apostasy, the Howards retained their premier peerage status as England's foremost recusant house, defying expectations of extinction under sustained legal and social pressures.[26]

Modern Personal and Institutional Criticisms

In 2022, Edward Fitzalan-Howard, 18th Duke of Norfolk, pleaded guilty to using a mobile phone while driving a BMW and running a red light on 12 April in central London, resulting in a six-month driving ban, a Â£1,000 fine, and Â£3,250 in costs.[95][96] He argued for leniency on grounds of "exceptional hardship," citing his irreplaceable role in coordinating the state funeral of Queen Elizabeth II on 19 September 2022 and the coronation of King Charles III on 6 May 2023, which required frequent travel without viable public alternatives.[97][96] The magistrate rejected the plea, emphasizing that no individual, regardless of status, is exempt from road safety laws.[96] The case's sentencing occurred in private at Marylebone Magistrates' Court on 26 September 2022, justified by national security risks from disclosing coronation logistics, prompting criticism from transparency campaigners who argued it exemplified undue privilege for aristocrats in public roles.[98][98] Institutionally, the Howard family's maintenance of hereditary offices, including the Dukedom of Norfolk and Earldom of Arundel, has faced scrutiny in debates over the persistence of feudal privileges in a democratic era. The Labour government's Hereditary Peers Bill, introduced in September 2024, seeks to end the 92 remaining hereditary peers' statutory right to sit and vote in the House of Lords, labeling the system "outdated and indefensible" and incompatible with modern elected representation.[99][100] Critics, including reform advocates, point to the Howards' premier dukedomâ€”held continuously since 1483â€”as a symbol of unearned influence, with the family's vast landholdings (over 20,000 acres) and ceremonial duties underscoring resistance to merit-based governance.[101][99] While the hereditary Earl Marshal position, overseeing state funerals and coronations, is slated for exemption due to its operational necessity, opponents contend it perpetuates aristocratic exceptionalism without electoral accountability.[101][99] Within the family, Kinvara Balfour, granddaughter of the 17th Duke and niece of the current Duke, criticized the law of primogeniture in 2015 as "archaic, mad and bonkers," arguing it unjustly bars female heirs from inheriting peerages and estates, thereby entrenching gender-based institutional inequality.[84] This internal dissent highlights tensions between tradition and contemporary egalitarian norms, though the family has not publicly altered its adherence to male-preference succession.[84]
