M-5 motorway (Pakistan)

The M-5 motorway, officially designated as the Multan–Sukkur Motorway, is a 392-kilometre controlled-access highway in Pakistan linking Multan in Punjab province to Sukkur in Sindh province along the left bank of the Indus River.[1] Constructed as a critical segment of the Peshawar–Karachi Motorway network, it facilitates direct high-speed connectivity between northern trade routes and southern ports, reducing travel time between the endpoint cities from approximately eight hours to four hours on its six-lane configuration designed for speeds up to 120 km/h.[1][2] Initiated under the China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) framework, the project broke ground in August 2016, involved financing through a government concessional loan, and reached completion with inauguration on 5 November 2019 at a total cost of US$2.889 billion, executed primarily by the China State Construction Engineering Corporation under oversight from Pakistan's National Highway Authority.[1] The development generated 28,000 jobs overall, including 25,620 for local workers, bolstering employment in rural areas and contributing to enhanced freight movement critical for Pakistan's agricultural and industrial exports.[1] Despite these advancements, the motorway has faced operational challenges, notably severe flooding in September 2025 that submerged sections near Jalalpur Pirwala and Lodhran, leading to a multi-week closure for repairs and exposing potential shortcomings in drainage and resilience against monsoon-induced inundation despite its substantial investment.[3] Ongoing maintenance tenders by the National Highway Authority indicate persistent needs for upkeep to sustain its role in national logistics.[4]

Overview and Description

Physical Characteristics

The M-5 motorway spans 392 kilometers, connecting Multan in Punjab province to Sukkur in Sindh province, making it Pakistan's longest continuous motorway segment.[5][6] It consists of a dual carriageway with six lanes total—three in each direction—constructed as a controlled-access highway to facilitate high-speed travel.[5][7] Designed for a maximum speed of 120 km/h, the motorway incorporates safety features such as a central median barrier and fencing along both sides to prevent unauthorized access and animal crossings.[5][8] The pavement structure supports heavy traffic loads typical of national motorways, with asphalt surfacing over a flexible base to ensure durability and smooth ride quality.[6] The right-of-way acquisition for the project provided sufficient space for future expansions, including potential widening to additional lanes, though current implementation focuses on the standard six-lane configuration.[6]

Strategic Role in National Infrastructure

The M-5 motorway, spanning 392 kilometers from Multan to Sukkur, constitutes a vital segment of Pakistan's Peshawar-Karachi Motorway network, enhancing north-south connectivity across Punjab and Sindh provinces.[9][2] This six-lane, controlled-access highway supports speeds up to 120 km/h, reducing transit times and improving logistics efficiency for freight and passenger movement in a country where road transport dominates cargo hauling.[10][11] By linking agricultural heartlands with urban centers, it facilitates the faster distribution of perishable goods, thereby bolstering food security and regional trade within Pakistan's national framework.[12] As the largest transportation infrastructure project under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), the M-5 integrates Pakistan's inland roadways with broader Eurasian trade routes, positioning the nation as a potential transit corridor for overland commerce.[13] Constructed with an investment of approximately 2.89 billion US dollars, it connects underdeveloped southern districts to northern economic hubs, stimulating industrial expansion and agricultural productivity through enhanced market access.[12] This role extends to national resilience, as the motorway's design incorporates seismic-resistant features suited to the region's tectonics, ensuring sustained operational integrity amid environmental risks.[14] In the context of Pakistan's overall infrastructure, the M-5 strengthens the motorway system's capacity to handle growing vehicular traffic, which has surged with economic liberalization, while alleviating congestion on parallel national highways like the N-5.[15] Its completion in November 2019 has demonstrably spurred local economic activity, including construction-related employment and downstream industries, underscoring its multiplier effects on gross domestic product contributions from transport-dependent sectors.[16][13]

Historical Development

Planning and Approval

The planning of the M-5 Motorway, spanning 392 kilometers between Multan and Sukkur, formed part of Pakistan's strategic expansion of its motorway infrastructure to link major economic corridors, particularly as the southern segment of the Peshawar-Karachi Motorway. The National Highway Authority (NHA) oversaw initial feasibility assessments, focusing on route alignment, traffic projections, and integration with existing national highways like N-5, to address chronic bottlenecks in inter-provincial freight and passenger transport.[8] These studies emphasized reducing travel time from approximately 8 hours to under 5 hours while accommodating projected annual average daily traffic volumes exceeding 20,000 vehicles.[17] Approval for the project was granted by the Executive Committee of the National Economic Council (ECNEC) on July 4, 2014, at a revised cost of Rs. 259.35 billion for the Sukkur-Multan section, rationalized from earlier estimates through value engineering and design optimizations.[18] [19] This decision integrated the motorway into the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), with approximately 90% of funding secured via concessional loans from Chinese institutions, reflecting a public-private partnership model prioritized for rapid execution.[20] The approval process involved coordination between the Ministry of Communications, Planning Commission, and provincial governments of Punjab and Sindh to mitigate land acquisition challenges and environmental concerns along the alignment.[21] Post-approval, detailed engineering designs and environmental impact assessments were finalized, leading to the award of construction contracts to China State Construction Engineering Corporation Limited in May 2016, underscoring the project's alignment with CPEC's early harvest priorities established in 2013.[22] No significant delays in the approval phase were reported, though subsequent phases faced scrutiny over cost overruns attributed to inflation and material imports.[23]

Construction Phases

The construction of the M-5 motorway, spanning 392 kilometers between Multan and Sukkur, was undertaken as a single major project under the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), with contracts awarded in May 2016 to China State Construction Engineering Corporation (CSCEC).[1] The project was divided into seven sections to enable simultaneous construction, facilitating rapid progress across Punjab and Sindh provinces.[24] Groundbreaking occurred in May 2016 under the administration of Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif, marking the start of physical works.[20] Actual construction began in August 2016, involving the deployment of over 3,500 pieces of heavy machinery and the establishment of 21 worker camps.[1] [24] The six-lane access-controlled alignment required extensive earthworks, bridge construction—including 12 river bridges and 413 minor bridges—and installation of safety features like CCTV and emergency services infrastructure. Despite challenges such as terrain variations and monsoon disruptions, the project adhered closely to its 36-month timeline, generating approximately 29,000 local jobs during the build phase.[25] Completion was achieved in July 2019, two months ahead of the contractual deadline of September 2019, with final handover and inauguration on November 5, 2019, by Prime Minister Imran Khan.[1] [26] The motorway became fully operational by March 2020 after testing and minor adjustments.[2] No distinct sequential phases were reported beyond the parallel package execution, as the design emphasized concurrent advancement to minimize delays in integrating the Peshawar-Karachi motorway network.[27]

Financing and Project Economics

Cost Estimates and Actual Expenditures

The M-5 motorway project was initially estimated at a total cost of $2.89 billion, covering the construction of its 392-kilometer length with six lanes designed for speeds up to 120 km/h.[20][26] This budget accounted for land acquisition, engineering works including 89 bridges and 11 interchanges, and associated infrastructure such as service areas.[5] Actual expenditures upon the motorway's completion in November 2019 reached approximately $2.94 billion, representing a slight increase over the initial projection but without evidence of substantial overruns.[8] The final outlay included costs for accelerated construction phases managed by multiple contractors, with the National Highway Authority overseeing disbursements under China-Pakistan Economic Corridor frameworks.[28] Project timelines from 2016 to 2019 contributed to cost control, as phased openings of sections mitigated inflationary pressures on materials and labor.[26] No official audits have publicly detailed breakdowns of variances, though reports indicate that foreign currency loans stabilized expenditures against local rupee fluctuations during the build period.[20] Overall, the alignment between estimates and actual spending underscores efficient resource allocation relative to similar Pakistani infrastructure projects, which often face delays and escalations.[21]

Funding Mechanisms and Sources

The M-5 motorway, also known as the Multan-Sukkur section of the Peshawar-Karachi Motorway, was financed through a government concessional loan (GCL) mechanism as part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC). This structure involved direct lending to the Government of Pakistan for infrastructure development, with repayment terms including low interest rates and extended maturities to support project viability.[1] The National Highway Authority (NHA) of Pakistan served as the implementing agency, overseeing procurement and execution by contractors such as China State Construction Engineering Corporation.[1] The primary funding source was the Export-Import Bank of China (China Eximbank), which provided the bulk of financing via preferential buyer's credit loans. On April 28, 2016, China Eximbank signed agreements totaling approximately USD 2.8 billion for the project, including a preferential buyer's credit of USD 1.79 billion at 2% interest and an additional buyer's credit of USD 361 million at 5.2% interest with a 20-year maturity and 5-year grace period.[29] [30] A separate government concessional loan component of RMB 4.8 billion at 2% interest supplemented the funding.[30] The total project cost was estimated at USD 2.889 billion, excluding tax exemptions of USD 180 million.[31] Subsequent debt suspension agreements were reached between Pakistan and China Eximbank on July 1, 2020; November 19, 2020; and July 1, 2021, to provide temporary relief amid Pakistan's economic pressures, reflecting the debt-servicing challenges associated with CPEC loans.[30] No significant equity contributions or alternative multilateral financing from institutions like the World Bank or Asian Development Bank were reported for the M-5, distinguishing it from other Pakistani highway projects that incorporated such sources.[1]

Route and Infrastructure Details

Alignment and Sections

The M-5 motorway follows an alignment along the left bank of the Indus River, extending 392 kilometers from Multan in Punjab province to Sukkur in Sindh province. This six-lane, access-controlled highway, designed for speeds up to 120 km/h, connects directly to the M-4 motorway at its northern terminus near Multan and to the proposed M-6 motorway at Sukkur in the south, integrating into the broader Peshawar-Karachi motorway network as part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor.[1] The route traverses southern Punjab and northern Sindh, passing through districts such as Lodhran, Bahawalpur, Rahim Yar Khan, and Ghotki, providing enhanced connectivity to agricultural and industrial hubs while paralleling the older N-5 national highway to the east. Key features include 11 interchanges linking to regional roads, 12 service areas, 10 rest areas, 10 flyovers, and 426 underpasses to accommodate local and wildlife crossings with minimal disruption.[21] [32] Construction divided the motorway into six packages for phased development, spanning from Shershah near Multan southward through intermediate points like Jalalpur Pirwala, Uch Sharif, and Zahir Pir, before reaching interchanges near Rahim Yar Khan and Sadiqabad en route to Sukkur. Notable markers include the Shah Shams Interchange and Rohri Interchange at approximately kilometer 386 near the southern end. This sectional approach enabled parallel progress, with the full alignment operational by November 2019.[33][34]

Interchanges, Bridges, and Technical Features

The M-5 motorway features 11 interchanges facilitating access to major roads and cities along its 392 km alignment from Multan to Sukkur.[32] These include the Shershah Interchange at the northern terminus connecting to Bahawalpur Road, Shujaabad Interchange linking to Lodhran Road, Jalalpur Pirwala Interchange also to Lodhran Road, Jhangra Interchange to the N-5 via Bahawalpur-DHA, Uch Sharif Interchange to Alipur Road, Tarinda Interchange to Tarinda-Muhammad Panah Road, Zahir Pir Interchange to Chachran Sharif Road, Guddu Interchange to Sadiqabad-Kashmore Road, Ghotki Interchange to JDW Unit 3 Sugar Mill Road, Pano Aqil Interchange to local roads, and Sukkur-Rohri Interchange connecting to the N-5 at Rohri/Sukkur.[21]

The infrastructure incorporates 54 bridges, among which a prominent feature is the major bridge spanning the Sutlej River near Jalalpur Pirwala, designed to accommodate the motorway's controlled-access configuration.[35] Additionally, 10 flyovers support overpass requirements at key junctions.[32] Technical specifications include a six-lane, bi-directional design with a posted speed limit of 120 km/h, enabling high-capacity traffic flow as part of the Peshawar-Karachi motorway network.[20] The route includes 426 underpasses for local traffic and wildlife crossings, alongside 12 service areas and 10 rest areas equipped for traveler amenities.[32][21]

Operations and Management

Toll System and Usage Statistics

The toll system on the M-5 motorway is operated by Pakistan's National Highway Authority (NHA), with collections primarily at toll plazas near Multan and Sukkur, as well as select interchanges. Tolls are structured by vehicle class, charging a fixed fee for traversing the full 392 km length, differentiated for light vehicles (e.g., cars, jeeps), wagons, buses, and heavy trucks based on axle count and size. The system integrates the M-Tag electronic toll collection (ETC) mechanism, utilizing RFID tags for automated deductions from linked accounts to minimize delays; vehicles without an M-Tag or with insufficient balance incur a 50% surcharge, effective from June 15, 2025, to encourage adoption and improve efficiency.[36][37] Toll rates undergo periodic adjustments to align with NHA revenue goals amid inflation and maintenance costs. As of March 22, 2025, the base rate for cars and similar light vehicles (Class 1) on the entire M-5 is Rs 1,200, up from Rs 1,100 following the second hike that year; this reflects a cumulative increase from prior levels, such as Rs 1,050 in early 2025. Larger vehicles face proportionally higher fees, with articulated trucks charged Rs 5,750. These revisions support NHA's fiscal targets, including Rs 102 billion in total toll revenue for 2024-25, compared to Rs 64 billion collected in 2023-24 across all motorways and highways.[38][39][40] Publicly available usage statistics specific to the M-5 remain limited, with no comprehensive average daily traffic (ADT) figures routinely published by NHA or the National Highways and Motorway Police (NHMP). The motorway, operational since 2019, handles significant north-south freight and passenger volumes as part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), diverting traffic from the congested parallel N-5 national highway. Pre-construction surveys indicated high demand on the N-5 Multan-Sukkur corridor, with sections exceeding tens of thousands of vehicles daily, underscoring the M-5's role in capacity expansion; however, post-opening metrics are not disaggregated in accessible NHA reports. Toll revenue contributions from M-5 plazas form part of NHA's broader collections, which doubled from Rs 32 billion in 2018-19 to Rs 64 billion in 2023-24, driven by traffic growth and rate adjustments.[5][41]

Maintenance and Safety Measures

The National Highway Authority (NHA) oversees maintenance of the M-5 motorway, conducting periodic and special repairs to address pavement deterioration, cracks, and damage from environmental factors such as flooding.[42][43] In September 2025, following Sutlej River floods that caused ponding and structural damage over 22 kilometers near Jalalpur Pirwala, the NHA initiated full-scale rehabilitation, including drainage improvements and road surface restoration, completing key sections within weeks to minimize disruptions.[44][45] Techniques such as the SAFETRACK® Crack Infill system have been employed for rapid infill and protection of aging asphalt, enabling minimal lane closure times during interventions.[42] Safety operations on the M-5 are managed by the National Highways and Motorway Police (NHMP), which enforces traffic rules, conducts patrols via Raahi rescue vehicles, and provides roadside assistance to mitigate risks like speeding and drowsiness—common contributors to crashes on Pakistani motorways.[46][47] In May 2023, the M-5 became the first Pakistani motorway to implement a fully automated transport system (ATS) with 269 high-resolution cameras for real-time monitoring and AI-driven enforcement, reducing violations and response times to incidents.[48] The infrastructure includes perimeter fencing to prevent unauthorized access and animal intrusions, though breaches in sections like Ghotki have led to accidents and security vulnerabilities, prompting ongoing NHMP interventions. NHMP also promotes measures such as mandatory seat belt use in public transport and driver fatigue prevention, including advisories for night-time hydration to combat dozing.[49][50] Despite these efforts, challenges persist, including crime from adjacent katcha areas and non-functional cameras in some segments, contributing to elevated accident rates.[51][52]

Impacts and Assessments

Economic Benefits and Achievements

The M-5 motorway has facilitated substantial reductions in travel time and transportation costs along the Multan-Sukkur corridor, shortening the journey from approximately 7-8 hours to 3.5-4 hours, which enhances logistics efficiency for freight and passenger movement.[11][17] This improvement supports faster delivery of perishable agricultural goods from Sindh and southern Punjab to northern markets and ports, thereby minimizing spoilage losses and enabling producers to access broader consumer bases with lower overheads.[5][53] Economic growth in the districts of Multan and Sukkur has accelerated relative to national averages, driven by enhanced connectivity that attracts investment in industry, real estate, and construction, with observed surges in property values and building activities post-completion in November 2019.[54] The project generated employment through engagement of over 1,000 local enterprises during construction, contributing to short-term job creation in labor-intensive sectors like civil works and materials supply.[55] As part of the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, the M-5 integrates with broader transport networks, reducing internal trade barriers and fostering competition in global markets by lowering overall logistics expenses.[56][57] The motorway has boosted sectors such as religious tourism between Multan and Sukkur, where improved access to shrines has increased visitor inflows, stimulating ancillary services like hospitality and retail in adjacent areas.[54] Overall, these developments have opened underdeveloped regions to business expansion, with projections indicating sustained contributions to provincial GDP through diversified economic activity, though long-term multipliers depend on complementary investments in skills and industry.[5][58]

Social and Environmental Effects

The construction of the M-5 motorway necessitated the acquisition of approximately 698 acres of land in Pano Aqil Tehsil alone, leading to the displacement of local communities primarily reliant on agriculture, with affected individuals resettled within 1 km of their original locations and compensated at rates of Rs. 1.8 million per acre.[56] While a majority of surveyed displacees reported satisfaction with compensation amounts, which were often reinvested in new land, housing, or businesses, the process lacked prior community consultation, resulting in dissatisfaction among some residents who faced challenges from post-construction land price inflation and fears of long-term economic vulnerability.[56] On the positive side, the motorway has enhanced social connectivity in rural areas like Pano Aqil and Sukkur by reducing travel times—such as from 9 hours to 4 hours to Karachi—facilitating greater access to urban centers for healthcare, education, and markets, alongside improvements in local infrastructure including new schools and electricity supply.[56] Employment opportunities during construction boosted unskilled and semi-skilled wages by up to 50% for locals, while operational phases spurred a real estate boom with land prices rising 50% and increased demand for regional produce, though some residents reported negative externalities like noise pollution, light intrusion, higher accident risks, and disrupted irrigation channels.[56] Environmentally, construction activities posed risks of soil erosion, dust generation, and habitat fragmentation in the semi-arid Punjab and Sindh regions, but mitigation efforts included the planting of over 400,000 trees and shrubs in three rows along both sides of the 392 km route to enhance aesthetics, stabilize soil, and support local biodiversity.[9] These measures contributed to the M-5 receiving the South Asian and Central Asian (SJ SACA) Sustainability and Environmental Award in 2020, recognizing its eco-friendly design amid broader concerns over linear infrastructure's potential to degrade ecosystems without adequate planning.[59] Operationally, the motorway's controlled-access format may reduce overall vehicle emissions compared to the pre-existing N-5 national highway by enabling higher speeds and less congestion, though vulnerability to flooding was evident in September 2025 when Sutlej River breaches washed out sections, creating a 20-25 km inland lake and highlighting risks to floodplain ecosystems.[60] No specific data on wildlife corridors or species impacts for the M-5 were documented in available assessments, reflecting a gap in post-construction monitoring typical of rapid CPEC-linked projects.[61]

Criticisms and Challenges

The construction of the M-5 motorway has faced allegations of corruption, particularly in the procurement process and contract awards. The National Accountability Bureau (NAB) identified irregularities in the awarding of contracts for the project, prompting investigations into potential misappropriation.[62] In 2019, a federal minister claimed billions of rupees were embezzled during the PML-N government's tenure, accusing officials of irregularities in the M-5's execution.[63] China State Construction Engineering Corporation (CSCEC), the primary Chinese firm involved, rejected these charges as groundless, emphasizing its commitment to transparency in the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) framework.[23] Operational challenges include persistent security vulnerabilities, exacerbated by inadequate maintenance of perimeter fencing. Broken or stolen fences along sections, such as between Zahirpir and Ghotki interchanges, have allowed animals like jackals, dogs, and livestock to enter the roadway, contributing to accidents and enabling criminal activities.[64] [65] Banditry has intensified risks, with reports of attacks on motorists along the Guddu to Rohri stretch, prompting police to implement escorted night convoys in Rahim Yar Khan by late 2025.[66] [67] The motorway's infrastructure has proven susceptible to flooding, as demonstrated by severe damage during the September 2025 Sutlej River floods. Breaches occurred at multiple points, including near Jalalpur Pirwala, washing out embankments, culverts, and up to six lanes, leading to prolonged closures—lasting over nine days in some sections—and disruptions to supply chains affecting thousands of vehicles.[68] [60] [69] Officials noted inundation of adjacent villages in Multan, Lodhran, and Bahawalpur districts, highlighting potential shortcomings in flood-resilient design despite the project's elevated features.[60] These incidents have necessitated traffic diversions to National Highway N-5 and raised questions about long-term durability in flood-prone southern Punjab and Sindh regions.[70]
