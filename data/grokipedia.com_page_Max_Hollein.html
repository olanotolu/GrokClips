Max Hollein

Max Hollein is an Austrian art historian serving as the Marina Kellen French Director and Chief Executive Officer of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City since 2018.[1] Born in Vienna, he earned a master's degree in art history from the University of Vienna and a master's in business administration from the Vienna University of Economics.[2] Prior to his appointment at the Met, Hollein directed the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco from 2016 to 2018, where he oversaw operations across two sites housing over 24,000 works of art.[3] Before that, he led institutions in Frankfurt, including the StÃ¤del Museum, Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt, and Liebieghaus Skulpturensammlung, implementing expansions and innovative exhibitions that integrated contemporary art with historical collections.[4] At the Met, he assumed the CEO role in 2023 to consolidate leadership amid institutional challenges, including financial strains from the COVID-19 pandemic that prompted controversial deaccessioning of artworks to support conservation and acquisitions.[1][5] Hollein's tenure has emphasized adapting encyclopedic museums to modern demands, such as enhanced digital access and provenance reviews in response to repatriation pressures, while defending core missions of preservation and public engagement.[6] He has supported policies like mandatory pay-what-you-wish admissions for out-of-state visitors to sustain operations, sparking debate over accessibility.[4]

Early Life and Education

Upbringing in Vienna

Max Hollein was born in Vienna in 1969, the son of the influential postmodernist architect Hans Hollein and Helene Hollein, who had worked as a fashion designer prior to marriage.[7] [8] His upbringing occurred amid Vienna's postwar cultural renaissance, where his family's intellectual and artistic circles provided early immersion in avant-garde design and museum culture. Hans Hollein's pioneering projects, including collaborations with international figures, positioned the household as a hub for creative exchange, with family friends encompassing artists, actors, architects, authors, and designers.[9] [10] This environment emphasized architecture and cultural institutions as core elements of daily life, shaping Hollein's childhood through frequent exposure to exhibitions and professional events. For instance, as a young boy, he accompanied his family to the 1982 opening of the Museum Abteiberg in Germany, a project tied to his father's network of visionary peers.[11] Vacations and social activities mirrored these influences, prioritizing sites of artistic and historical significance over conventional leisure.[12] Such experiences instilled a foundational appreciation for interdisciplinary art, distinct from more conventional Viennese upbringings, though Hollein later pursued economics as a form of youthful divergence from familial norms.[4] [13] In his early years, Hollein engaged with specific artistic traditions, studying early Flemish painting, which aligned with Vienna's rich holdings of European masterpieces and his home's emphasis on visual heritage.[11] This period laid the groundwork for his later curatorial interests, without formal training yet evident in the seamless blend of familial passion and personal curiosity.[8]

Academic and Professional Training

Hollein earned a Master of Arts in art history from the University of Vienna, graduating summa cum laude.[2] He also received a Master of Business Administration from the Vienna University of Economics and Business Administration.[2] These dual degrees equipped him with scholarly expertise in art alongside managerial and economic acumen relevant to institutional leadership.[14] His early professional experience commenced at the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum in New York, where he served as chief of staff and executive assistant to director Thomas Krens.[14] [15] In this capacity, spanning the late 1990s, Hollein gained hands-on training in museum operations, strategic planning, and high-level administration during a period of expansion under Krens's tenure.[16] This role preceded his assumption of directorial responsibilities in Europe by about six years.[14]

Museum Directorships

Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt

Max Hollein was appointed director of the Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt in 2001 at the age of 31, tasked with revitalizing an institution struggling with declining relevance and attendance.[4][8] The Schirn, opened in 1986 as a venue for temporary exhibitions of modern and contemporary art, had faced criticism for inconsistent programming prior to his arrival.[17] Hollein, drawing on his prior experience in curatorial projects and business administration, shifted the focus toward thematic shows that bridged historical and contemporary art, emphasizing 19th- and 20th-century works to build a distinctive profile.[17][3] Under Hollein's leadership, which extended until 2016, the Schirn established an internationally recognized identity through ambitious, interdisciplinary exhibitions that attracted over a million visitors in peak years and garnered critical acclaim for innovative curation.[17][18] He prioritized shows exploring cultural phenomena, such as consumerism in Shopping: A Century of Art and Consumer Culture (2002â€“2003), his first major project there, which examined art's intersection with commercialism from the 19th century onward.[10] Other notable exhibitions included Rent Collection Courtyard (2008), presenting 100 Mao-era Chinese sculptures in their first Western showing, highlighting mass propaganda art; Paparazzi! Photographers, Stars and Artists (2014), co-organized with Centre Pompidou-Metz, tracing celebrity culture through photography; and GÃ©ricault: Images of Life and Death (2013), Germany's inaugural solo exhibition of the French Romantic painter ThÃ©odore GÃ©ricault.[19][20][21] Hollein's tenure integrated the Schirn into Frankfurt's broader museum ecosystem after 2006, when he assumed oversight of the StÃ¤del Museum and Liebieghaus while retaining directorship of the Schirn, fostering cross-institutional collaborations that enhanced resource sharing and programming synergy.[14] This period marked a turnaround, with the Schirn evolving from a locally oriented hall to a platform for global dialogues on art's societal role, evidenced by shows like Ideal Worlds (2009), addressing contemporary Romanticism, and Giacometti â€“ Nauman (2016), juxtaposing Alberto Giacometti's sculptures with Bruce Nauman's installations.[22][23] Critics noted his success in balancing commercial viabilityâ€”through targeted marketing and sponsorshipsâ€”with intellectual depth, avoiding over-reliance on attendance metrics as the sole success indicator.[24][8] By 2016, Hollein's departure to the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco left the Schirn with a legacy of over 100 exhibitions that repositioned it as a key European venue for provocative, context-driven art presentations, contributing to Frankfurt's status as a cultural hub.[18][17]

StÃ¤del Museum and Liebieghaus

In January 2006, Max Hollein assumed directorship of the StÃ¤del Museum and the Liebieghaus Skulpturensammlung in Frankfurt, expanding his responsibilities alongside his existing role at the Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt.[4] [25] He led both institutions until June 2016, when he departed for the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco.[26] Hollein's tenure at the StÃ¤del Museum emphasized infrastructural growth and programmatic integration of historical and contemporary art. Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco

Max Hollein was appointed Director and CEO of the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco (FAMSF) on March 22, 2016, assuming the role on June 1, 2016.[3] In this position, he oversaw the de Young Museum the Metropolitan Museum of Art, leaving a legacy of collection expansion and programmatic innovation during his approximately two-year term.[2] His tenure was noted for injecting new energy into the institutions, though it occurred amid FAMSF's history of leadership turnover.[35]

Metropolitan Museum of Art

Max Hollein was appointed as the tenth Director of The Metropolitan Museum of Art on April 10, 2018, succeeding Thomas P. Campbell, with the board citing his international experience in museum leadership and curatorial innovation.[2] He assumed the role later that year, overseeing the museum's artistic vision, curatorial departments, conservation efforts, exhibitions, and acquisitions for its collection of approximately 1.5 million objects spanning 5,000 years of global art.[14] Under Hollein's direction, the institution has emphasized interdisciplinary approaches to display and interpretation, aiming to connect disparate cultural narratives while addressing modern scholarly debates on provenance and cultural heritage.[39] In August 2022, Hollein was announced to expand his responsibilities to include the Chief Executive Officer title, effective July 2023, integrating operational leadership with programmatic oversight amid the museum's $500 million annual budget and extensive facilities.[1] This dual role has facilitated strategic initiatives to enhance audience engagement, including digital expansions and broadened scholarly access, building on his prior successes in integrating technology at institutions like the StÃ¤del Museum.[40] Key among these is a comprehensive reorganization of the European paintings and sculpture galleries, completed in phases to foster cross-cultural juxtapositions and reduce traditional ethnocentric framing, with over a quarter of the museum's galleries refreshed by late 2023.[41] Hollein has spearheaded major capital projects, notably the opening of the Iris and B. Gerald Cantor Roof Garden and the new Tang Wing for modern and contemporary art, which expanded exhibition space for post-19th-century works and supported acquisitions like significant postwar European and American pieces.[14] His tenure has seen over 100 exhibitions, including blockbusters such as The Medici: Portraits and Politics, 1512â€“1570 (2018â€“2019), which drew record attendance by linking Renaissance art to political history, and ongoing series like the 2024â€“2025 slate featuring thematic explorations of global influences in Asian and African collections.[42] [43] These efforts have prioritized empirical curatorial rigor, with Hollein advocating for evidence-based reinterpretations of objects informed by recent provenance research and international collaborations, such as repatriation discussions with source countries.[39]

Curatorial and Scholarly Work

Key Exhibitions and Projects

Hollein curated "Shopping: A Century of Art and Consumer Culture" at the Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt from October 28 to December 1, 2002, presenting works that traced the evolution of consumerism's influence on art across the 20th century.[10] Under his directorship at the Schirn, exhibitions included "At Your Own Risk" in 2003, which examined viewer-interactive art practices emerging in the 1990s,[44] and "Art for the Millions: 100 Sculptures from the Mao Era" from September 24, 2009, to January 3, 2010, featuring socialist realist sculptures produced during China's Cultural Revolution.[19] At the StÃ¤del Museum, Hollein oversaw the 2012 expansion project, which doubled the institution's gallery space through the addition of a new underground wing.[3] He curated "Georg Baselitz: The Heroes" at the StÃ¤del from June 30 to October 23, 2016, displaying approximately seventy paintings from the artist's early 1960s series addressing post-war German identity.[30] During his tenure at the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco, Hollein developed programming such as an exhibition on American Precisionism and the Machine Age of the 1920s, drawing parallels to contemporary technological advancements.[38] As director of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, Hollein supervised "The Medici: Portraits and Politics, 1512â€“1570," held from June 26 to October 11, 2021, which assembled over 90 works, including paintings, sculptures, and decorative arts, to illustrate the role of portraiture in Medici political maneuvering in Renaissance Florence.[45][46] Key projects at the Met under his leadership include the launch of the Tang Wing for modern and contemporary art, enhancing display capabilities for the collection.[14]

Publications and Writings

Hollein's scholarly writings are predominantly tied to his curatorial practice, encompassing exhibition catalogs, museum guides, and theoretical essays on art presentation and reception. His PhD dissertation, titled Die Ausstellung als Kunstwerk ("The Exhibition as Artwork"), completed in 1999 at the University of Vienna, explores exhibitions as autonomous artistic entities, emphasizing curatorial strategies in shaping viewer experience.[47] Hollein has described this work as highly theoretical, reflecting early interests in how institutional framing influences art's market and perceptual dynamics, though it predates his extensive practical directorships.[47] As director of institutions like the Schirn Kunsthalle Frankfurt and StÃ¤del Museum, Hollein edited or co-authored several catalogs documenting major exhibitions. Notable examples include Shopping: A Century of Art and Consumer Culture (2002), co-edited with Christoph Grunenberg, which examines intersections of consumerism and visual arts from the 19th to 21st centuries through essays and reproductions.[48] Other key contributions feature James Ensor (2006), co-edited with Ingrid Pfeiffer, cataloging the Belgian artist's satirical works with contextual essays; Esprit Montmartre: Bohemian Life in Paris around 1900 (2014), focusing on fin-de-siÃ¨cle Parisian cultural scenes; and Odilon Redon: As in a Dream (2007), analyzing the Symbolist painter's fantastical oeuvre.[49][50] These publications often prioritize visual documentation over novel theoretical advances, aligning with Hollein's emphasis on accessible institutional narratives.[51] At the Metropolitan Museum of Art, Hollein's writings include Modern and Contemporary Art in The Metropolitan Museum of Art (2019), a 184-page survey authored by him that highlights post-19th-century holdings with 200 illustrations, serving as both catalog and interpretive guide.[52] He also penned the foreword to Art = Discovering Infinite Connections in Art History (2020), underscoring thematic linkages across the Met's global collection, and contributed an introduction to Making The Met, 1870â€“2020 (2020), reflecting on acquisition strategies and inclusivity in collecting.[53][54] Additionally, as editor, he oversaw Suspended Moment: The Architecture of Frida Escobedo (2025, Yale University Press), compiling essays on the Mexican architect's spatial designs in museum contexts.[55] Hollein's output remains exhibition-driven rather than standalone academic monographs, with contributions favoring practical curation over abstract theory post-dissertation.[56]

Organizational Roles

Corporate and Advisory Boards

Hollein serves on the Board of Directors of the Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts, having been elected on January 25, 2024.[57] The foundation, established in 1987, supports visual arts through grants exceeding $220 million to date, focusing on innovative contemporary projects. His appointment aligns with the board's composition of museum directors, curators, and scholars guiding grant allocations.[58] He is a member of the TEFAF Global Advisory Board, which advises on strategic matters for The European Fine Art Fair, an annual event facilitating art market transactions valued at over â‚¬7.5 billion cumulatively.[59] The board includes leaders from institutions like the Prado Museum and Christie's, emphasizing ethical standards and market trends in old masters and modern art.[60] Previously, Hollein served on the International Advisory Board of the State Hermitage Museum until 2022, participating in meetings such as the 27th session in September 2021 to discuss curatorial and preservation strategies.[61] This role involved collaboration with directors from the Vatican Museums and Louvre on global museum challenges.[2] His tenure ended amid geopolitical tensions following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, reflecting broader institutional reevaluations of ties with Russian entities.[62]

Non-Profit and Cultural Organizations

Hollein was elected to the board of directors of The Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts in January 2024, joining an organization that provides grants to visual arts projects and institutions supporting contemporary art.[57] The foundation, established in 1987, focuses on funding exhibitions, publications, and artist residencies, with Hollein's appointment leveraging his museum leadership experience.[16] He serves on supervisory and advisory boards of several international cultural institutions, including the State Hermitage Museum in St. Petersburg, Russia, where he contributes to oversight of one of the world's largest art collections spanning over three million works.[2] Similarly, Hollein is a member of the advisory board for the Institute of Fine Arts at New York University, an academic program emphasizing advanced research in art history and conservation.[16] Additional affiliations include the supervisory board of the National Gallery in Prague, Czech Republic, which houses national collections of Czech and international art from the Middle Ages to the present; the Ludwig Foundation in Vienna, Austria, dedicated to promoting contemporary art through acquisitions and exhibitions; and the Neue Galerie New York, a museum specializing in early 20th-century Austrian and German art.[2] These roles reflect Hollein's engagement in global cultural governance, often involving strategic planning, acquisitions, and programmatic decisions across non-profit entities.[14]

Controversies and Criticisms

Deaccessioning Practices

In February 2021, under director Max Hollein, the Metropolitan Museum of Art proposed expanding deaccessioning practices to fund "direct care" of its collection, including conservation, curatorial salaries, and facilities maintenance, amid a projected $150 million operating deficit exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic.[63] This marked a departure from longstanding guidelines by the Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD), which had traditionally restricted such proceeds to new acquisitions; the proposal aligned with a temporary AAMD waiver introduced in April 2020 permitting direct care uses during the crisis.[5] Hollein emphasized that the museum had routinely deaccessioned lower-quality or redundant worksâ€”averaging $20-30 million annually in recent yearsâ€”to refine the collection, arguing that prioritizing stewardship over expansion would preserve more art long-term without compromising ethical standards.[64][65] The initiative drew immediate criticism from former Met staff, art historians, and donors, who warned it could erode public trust, signal financial mismanagement, and deter future bequests by implying collections were disposable assets.[66] A Change.org petition opposing the plan, delivered to the museum on March 8, 2021, amassed over 25,000 signatures, contending that operational shortfalls should not justify liquidating donor-gifted items, potentially leading to "fire sales" of undervalued works.[67] Critics, including figures like former Met curator Ashton Hawkins, highlighted risks of selective deaccessioning favoring marketable modern pieces over encyclopedic holdings, though Hollein countered that decisions would involve departmental heads and board approval, targeting only items not on view or core to the mission.[63][68] By September 2021, the Met proceeded with deaccessioning approximately $1 million in prints, photographs, and drawingsâ€”works deemed duplicates or outside curatorial prioritiesâ€”via Sotheby's auctions, with proceeds allocated to direct care as approved by the board in March.[69] Hollein maintained this approach enhanced collection focus, noting in a September 2021 interview that it avoided broader layoffs beyond initial staff reductions.[70] However, by October 2022, following AAMD's policy reversal limiting direct care uses to a five-year phase-out, Hollein stated the Met had redirected deaccessioning funds primarily to acquisitions, reflecting a return to pre-pandemic norms amid recovering attendance and endowments.[71] No similar controversies arose during Hollein's tenures at the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco (2016-2018) or StÃ¤del Museum, where deaccessioning remained acquisition-focused without public dispute.[72]

Collection Reinterpretations and Ethnocentrism

Under Max Hollein's direction since 2018, the Metropolitan Museum of Art has pursued reinterpretations of its permanent collections to foster a less ethnocentric presentation, reorganizing galleries to integrate global influences and thematic narratives across cultural boundaries rather than maintaining siloed departmental structures. This includes the overhaul of European paintings from 1300 to 1800 across 45 rooms, where works from diverse origins and eras are juxtaposedâ€”such as El Greco's paintings alongside those of Picasso and CÃ©zanne, or Spanish colonial art from Mexico and Peruâ€”to highlight interconnections and challenge traditional definitions of "Europe."[73] The "Crossroads" initiative, launched in 2019, further exemplifies this by drawing artifacts from medieval, Asian, Greek, Roman, and other departments to explore shared themes like migration and exchange, aiming to present art history as a global continuum beyond a Western lens.[74] Hollein has also emphasized provenance research and contextual updates tied to cultural property debates, committing in 2023 to hire additional researchers to review objects acquired between 1970 and 1990 and to develop nuanced narratives informed by interdisciplinary expertise.[75] This has involved repatriating items, such as 16 works to countries including Egypt, Greece, Italy, Nepal, Nigeria, Turkey, and India, while prioritizing deliberate evaluations over hasty returns.[75] Complementary efforts include the 2025 reimagining of the Michael C. Rockefeller Wing for arts of Africa, Oceania, and the Americas, intended to invite broader interpretations of non-Western holdings.[75] Critics, however, have argued that these reinterpretations impose contemporary political ideologies onto historical artifacts, risking anachronistic projections such as modern identity categories onto ancient works like the "Lady of Carthage" mosaic.[73] Conservative commentators contend that exhibitions like "The Last Knight" (2019), which reframe Renaissance armor through lenses of propaganda and power dynamics, prioritize social critique over aesthetic appreciation, selectively targeting European and American art while sparing non-Western holdings from similar scrutinyâ€”potentially inverting ethnocentrism into a form of cultural exceptionalism for favored narratives.[76] Hollein's commissioning of contemporary interventions, such as Kent Monkman's 2019 murals reimagining canonical Western scenes with indigenous perspectives (later added to the permanent collection in 2020), has drawn accusations of transforming the encyclopedic museum into a platform for identity-driven agitprop that undermines its universalist mission.[77] Added wall labels in displays like "Making The Met" (2020 onward) highlighting donors' ties to historical injustices, such as sugar trade exploitation, further fuel claims that such approaches align with progressive activism, potentially eroding public trust in the institution's curatorial neutrality.[77]

DEI Policies and Institutional Responses

In June 2020, following the death of George Floyd and subsequent protests, over 100 Metropolitan Museum of Art staff members signed an open letter accusing the institution of perpetuating "covert and overt white supremacy" through its hiring practices, exhibition choices, and internal culture, prompting demands for structural reforms.[78] On July 7, 2020, Director Max Hollein and President Daniel H. Weiss responded by announcing a 13-point institutional Equity and Diversity Plan, which included mandatory anti-racism training for all staff within 180 days (60 days for senior leadership), the creation of a $3â€“5 million fund to support initiatives on diverse art histories, exhibitions, and acquisitions, and a $10 million endowment commitment for works by Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC) artists within 12 months.[79] The plan also pledged to hire a chief diversity officer within four months and expand paid internships to enhance accessibility by 2022, framing these measures as steps toward accountability amid internal allegations of pay disparities and erasure of minority voices.[79] Hollein publicly aligned the museum with Black Lives Matter, stating in a June 2020 message that the Met's historical development was "connected with a logic of what is defined as white supremacy" and committing to address institutional shortcomings, though he emphasized a professional approach to reforms.[77] Critics from within, including the employee-led group For the Culture 2020, dismissed prior solidarity statements as insufficient and demanded public reporting on progress, citing unfulfilled past commitments on equity.[79] External observers, such as those in conservative outlets, argued that Hollein's embrace of such self-critique undermined the museum's encyclopedic mission by prioritizing contemporary ideological interventions over traditional curatorial discernment, with initiatives like acquiring Kent Monkman's racially themed murals in October 2020 exemplifying a shift toward agitprop over aesthetic focus.[77] Hollein's appointment as the tenth consecutive white male director in 2018 had already drawn scrutiny for overlooking diversity opportunities, despite his financial expertise, amid broader debates on representation in leadership.[80] By April 2025, in response to President Trump's executive actions targeting federal DEI programs, Hollein asserted that such policies "don't apply to us" and would have no impact on the Met's operations, signaling continuity despite political shifts.[81] Reports on implementation remain mixed, with some noting persistent staff turnover linked to DEI imperatives under Hollein's tenure, though specific metrics on training efficacy or acquisition outcomes are limited in public disclosures.[82]

Recognition and Legacy

Awards and Honors

Hollein was appointed Chevalier of the Ordre des Arts et des Lettres by the French Minister of Culture in 2009, recognizing his curatorial achievements and contributions to international cultural exchange.[2][62] In 2010, he received the Austrian Cross of Honour for Science and Art, an award bestowed by the Austrian government for distinguished service in the fields of science, research, or art.[2][62] Hollein was honored with the Goethe Plakette des Landes Hessen, the highest cultural policy accolade of the State of Hesse, in 2015, acknowledging his leadership in Frankfurt's museum institutions and promotion of artistic innovation.[2] In June 2019, the City of Frankfurt awarded him the Goethe Plaque, presented to figures who have significantly advanced the city's cultural life, citing his transformative role as director of the Schirn Kunsthalle, StÃ¤del Museum, and Liebieghaus during his tenure from 2001 to 2016.[83] In 2020, Hollein was named Manager of the Year by Vienna University of Economics and Business (WU Wien), an alumnus award highlighting his global impact in arts management and institutional leadership at institutions including the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco and the Metropolitan Museum of Art.[84]

Impact on Art Institutions

Max Hollein's directorships have emphasized physical expansions, digital integration, and programmatic innovation to enhance accessibility and relevance of art institutions. At the Schirn Kunsthalle in Frankfurt, where he served as director from 2001, Hollein curated exhibitions exploring contemporary societal themes, including Shopping: A Century of Art and Consumer Culture in 2002, which examined art's intersection with commerce.[10] His leadership there contributed to the venue's reputation for dynamic, thematic shows that drew broader audiences beyond traditional art circles.[33] Assuming directorship of the StÃ¤del Museum in Frankfurt in 2006, Hollein oversaw a $69 million expansion that doubled the gallery space, enabling larger exhibitions and improved visitor flow.[35] He implemented a comprehensive digital strategy, including online collection access and virtual tours, which positioned the museum as a pioneer in hybrid physical-digital experiences.[2] [40] These initiatives correlated with sustained high attendance, exceeding 7 million visitors in peak years during his tenure.[2] From 2016 to 2018, as director and CEO of the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco, encompassing the de Young Museum and Legion of Honor, Hollein managed institutions that ranked among the top U.S. art venues by visitation, with over 1.4 million annual visitors.[2] He focused on curatorial enhancements and operational efficiencies to sustain public engagement amid diverse programming. Since 2018 as director of the Metropolitan Museum of Artâ€”and adding CEO responsibilities in 2022â€”Hollein has directed over 100 exhibitions and spearheaded capital projects, including the new Tang Wing for modern and contemporary art completed in 2024.[14] [1] He introduced the "great hall" commission series in 2019, commissioning site-specific contemporary works to bridge historical and modern collections.[42] Additionally, he reorganized the European paintings and sculpture galleries in 2023 to foster cross-cultural juxtapositions, aiming to reduce ethnocentric narratives while maintaining chronological coherence.[73] These efforts have modernized the Met's approach, integrating digital tools and contemporary focus to attract younger demographics and sustain its 6 million annual visitors.[85]

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Max Hollein is married to Nina Hollein, an Austrian fashion designer and former architect.[86][80][6] The couple has three children, who were teenagers as of 2018.[86] Hollein's mother, Helene Hollein, worked as a fashion designer prior to her marriage, while his younger sister, Lilli Hollein, directs Vienna Design Week.[8]

Residence and Lifestyle

Max Hollein resides with his family in a rented townhouse on Manhattan's Upper East Side.[8] Upon relocating to New York City in 2018 for his role at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, he and his wife initially shared a one-room apartment in Hell's Kitchen before moving to the more spacious Upper East Side property.[6] Previously, while directing the Fine Arts Museums of San Francisco, Hollein maintained an art-filled home in that city.[11] Hollein's lifestyle aligns with his professional immersion in the arts, characterized by frequent attendance at cultural events, collaborations, and international travel related to museum initiatives, such as exhibitions in Paris.[87] Described as dapper and charismatic, he engages actively in New York's art scene while balancing family life in the city.[88]
