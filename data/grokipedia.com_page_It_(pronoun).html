It (pronoun)

It is the third-person singular neuter pronoun in Modern English, used as the subject or object to refer to inanimate objects, animals, abstract concepts, and impersonal situations such as weather or time.[1][2] It originated from Old English hit, the neuter nominative form, which evolved into Middle English it amid the loss of grammatical gender distinctions in nouns during the language's development from Old to Middle English.[3] In syntactic roles, it serves as a dummy subject in constructions like "It rains" or cleft sentences emphasizing focus, such as "It was the dog that barked," without referring to a specific antecedent.[1] Traditionally, it is avoided for human referents, as usage for persons implies dehumanization or lack of agency, rendering it derogatory in interpersonal contexts; linguistic consensus holds that singular they is the standard gender-neutral alternative for unknown or unspecified human genders.[4][5] Despite this, isolated modern self-identifications with it pronouns occur in gender discourse, though such usage deviates from empirical patterns in natural language corpora and historical precedent, often drawing criticism for echoing past slurs against marginalized groups.[6][7]

Historical Development

Old English Origins

Possessive and Reflexive Variants

The possessive form of the pronoun it is its, which serves as a possessive determiner (e.g., "The dog wagged its tail") or independent possessive pronoun (e.g., "The choice is its own").[28] Unlike nominal possessives that typically use apostrophes (e.g., "the dog's"), its omits the apostrophe to differentiate it from the contraction it's (for "it is" or "it has"), a convention solidified in English orthography by the 18th century to prevent ambiguity in printed texts.[29] [30] This lack of apostrophe aligns its with other third-person singular possessive pronouns like his and hers, reflecting a historical pattern in pronominal genitives that predates widespread apostrophe use for possession in nouns.[31] The reflexive variant of it is itself, used when the pronoun refers back to it as the subject in constructions emphasizing self-action or reciprocity (e.g., "The machine repairs itself").[32] Reflexive pronouns like itself typically follow the verb or preposition and agree in person, number, and gender with their antecedent, maintaining the neuter gender of it for inanimate or impersonal referents.[32] Etymologically, itself derives from late Old English hit sylf ("it self"), evolving through Middle English to its modern form by the late 14th century, where "self" functions as an intensifier or marker of reflexivity.[33] In emphatic usage, itself can stand alone for stress (e.g., "The decision was bad in itself"), underscoring inherent qualities without external relations.[34]

Syntactic Roles

Nominal Functions

The pronoun it functions nominally as a third-person singular neuter anaphoric reference, substituting for antecedents denoting inanimate objects, abstract concepts, animals, or situations, thereby occupying core argument positions in clause structure.[35][36] In subject position, it initiates the predicate, as in "The ball rolled down the hill. She cleaned it carefully," linking back to the antecedent via anaphora.[35] Objective case forms of it do not inflect further in modern English, remaining invariant across positions, though it contrasts with subjective uses in passivized constructions like "The report was submitted. It was reviewed by the committee."[36] It also appears as the object of prepositions, such as "The decision affected everyone. We must live with it," functioning within adverbial or adjectival phrases to denote the referenced entity.[35] Less commonly, it serves in coordinate or complement-like nominal roles, such as in partitive constructions ("Take some of it") or as a predicate nominal after copular verbs ("That thing is it," though idiomatic).[35] These functions underscore it's role in referential tracking, distinct from its non-referential syntactic placeholders, with anaphoric binding constrained by proximity to antecedents to ensure interpretability.[37] Empirical analyses of English corpora confirm it's predominance in these positions for non-human referents, with over 80% of occurrences in subject or object slots tied to prior nominal mentions in narrative texts.[38]

Dependent Structures

The pronoun it features prominently in it-cleft constructions, a syntactic structure that employs a dependent relative clause to focus or highlight a specific constituent within the sentence. In this formation, it functions as a non-referential dummy subject of the matrix clause, followed by a copular verb (typically be), the focused element, and a relative clause introduced by that, who, or zero relativizer, which provides the presupposed background information. For example, in "It was the butler who committed the crime," the relative clause "who committed the crime" is subordinate and dependent, asserting the exhaustive identification of the focused noun phrase "the butler."[43][44] This dependent structure achieves contrastive or identificational focus, distinguishing it from simple declarative sentences by partitioning information into presupposed (given in the relative clause) and asserted (focused) elements. Syntactic analyses posit that the relative clause in it-clefts behaves as a free relative or fused relative, modifying the dummy it discontinuously, which accounts for restrictions such as the inability to extract from the focused position without island effects. Empirical studies of corpora confirm that it-clefts occur with frequencies around 1-2 per 1,000 clauses in spoken and written English, often in contexts requiring emphasis or correction, as in narrative or argumentative discourse.[43] Unlike extraposition, where a sentential subject is postponed and it anticipates a complement clause (e.g., "It seems that it is raining"), it-clefts integrate the dependent clause as a restrictive modifier, enabling exhaustive listing implicaturesâ€”implying no other alternatives satisfy the relative clause description. This is evidenced by felicity judgments: "It was John who left" implies John is the only one who left, a semantic effect tied to the dependent clause's role in defining the focus uniquely. Theoretical frameworks, including those treating it as a definite description incorporating the relative clause, explain connectivity effects where the focused element links interpretively to gaps in the subordinate clause.[44][45] It-clefts also permit variations in tense and modality within the matrix clause, but the dependent relative clause typically remains non-finite or tensed independently, preserving its subordinate status without licensing main clause phenomena like subject-auxiliary inversion. Diachronic data from English corpora show this structure stabilizing by the 16th century, with increased usage in formal registers by the 19th century, reflecting its utility in complex argumentation. Limitations include incompatibility with certain quantifiers in the focus position, as quantifiers like all resist clefting due to the dependent clause's presuppositional demands.

Semantic Referents

Inanimate and Abstract Entities

In English grammar, the third-person singular neuter pronoun "it" functions as the primary referential device for inanimate objects, which are concrete entities lacking biological life, such as tools, vehicles, or structures. For instance, a sentence like "The engine failed during the test. It overheated due to insufficient cooling." employs "it" to anaphorically link back to the engine, maintaining syntactic continuity without implying agency or personhood. This usage aligns with the pronoun's role in avoiding the gendered forms "he" or "she," which are reserved for animate beings, following the historical shift away from Old English's grammatical gender system toward natural gender distinctions.[46][47] For abstract entitiesâ€”non-physical concepts, states, or processesâ€”"it" similarly provides neutral reference, often in anaphoric, cataphoric, or expletive constructions. Examples include references to ideas ("Justice demands accountability. It cannot be compromised for expediency.") or events ("The merger occurred in 2020. It reshaped the industry."). Linguistic analyses identify these as event-referring or propositional uses, where "it" substitutes for clauses or notions without concrete form, as in "It is essential to verify data before analysis," where "it" extraposes the abstract predicate for emphasis.[46][37][47] Expletive or dummy "it" extends this to impersonal expressions denoting abstract conditions like weather, time, or distance: "It snowed heavily on December 15, 2023," or "It is three miles to the summit." These constructions, lacking specific antecedents, treat environmental or temporal states as neuter referents, a feature unique to English among Germanic languages in its frequency and syntactic flexibility. Usage data from corpora confirm "it" predominates over alternatives like "this" or "that" for such abstracts in formal prose, ensuring clarity in referring to non-agentive phenomena.[46][48] Regional and stylistic variations occasionally personify inanimates with "he" or "she" in dialects or nautical contexts (e.g., ships as "she"), but standard prescriptive grammar, as outlined in major references, defaults to "it" to preserve semantic neutrality and avoid anthropomorphism unless contextually motivated. Empirical studies of pronominal resolution show listeners reliably interpret "it" as non-human and non-sentient in these domains, supporting its efficiency in causal descriptions of object behaviors or abstract relations.[46][47][37]

Animals and Non-Human Organisms

In standard English grammar, the neuter pronoun "it" serves as the default referent for non-human animals, encompassing vertebrates, invertebrates, and other faunal species, unless the animal's sex is known and contextually relevant.[49][50] This usage treats animals as grammatical objects or neuter entities, distinct from personal pronouns reserved for humans, reflecting the absence of grammatical gender in modern English for such referents.[51] For instance, a sentence like "The wolf howled as it approached the den" employs "it" to denote the animal impersonally, prioritizing descriptive neutrality over anthropomorphism.[52] Exceptions arise in informal or affectionate contexts, particularly for companion animals or pets with established names and known sexes, where speakers may substitute "he" or "she" to convey familiarity or perceived personality.[49][53] A 2010 analysis in writing guidance notes that "an animal is referred to as 'it' unless the relationship is personal (like a pet that has a name)," after which "he" or "she" becomes acceptable.[49] However, this personalization is not grammatically obligatory and remains optional; scientific literature, biological texts, and formal descriptions consistently favor "it" to maintain objectivity, as seen in references to species behaviors where individual sex is unspecified or irrelevant.[52][53] For non-animal organisms, such as plants, fungi, protists, and microorganisms, "it" is the exclusive pronoun, underscoring their non-sentient status and lack of any sex-based or personal attribution in English syntax.[51][54] Examples include "The oak tree shed its leaves" or "The bacterium replicated as it encountered nutrients," where "it" aligns with the inanimate or impersonal categorization inherent to biological nomenclature. This consistent application avoids conflating organismal biology with human-like agency, preserving linguistic precision in empirical contexts.[54]

Human and Sentient Applications

In standard English, the pronoun "it" is rarely used to refer to adult humans or other sentient beings, as it typically denotes inanimate objects, animals, or abstract concepts, and its application to persons risks implying dehumanization by equating them with non-sentient entities.[4] Exceptions occur primarily with young children or unidentified individuals, where gender neutrality is prioritized over personalization. Linguistic analyses note that "it" conveys neutrality but can evoke discomfort in modern usage due to its association with objectification.[55] For newborns and infants, "it" serves as a gender-neutral option when sex is unknown or unspecified, as documented in English grammar references dating to at least 2005.[56] For instance, phrases like "The baby is asleep; don't wake it" appear in descriptive grammars, though singular "they" has gained prevalence since the late 20th century to avoid any perceived depersonalization.[57][58] This usage was more common historically for very young children, filling a gap in the language's limited neutral pronouns for humans, but empirical studies of media corpora, such as New Zealand news from recent decades, show it persisting mainly for fetuses or neonates before gender disclosure.[59] "It" also refers to humans in contexts of anonymity or identification, such as "Who is it?" when someone approaches unseen, temporarily treating the referent as an unknown entity until specified.[60] This deictic application underscores "it"'s role in impersonal or preparatory reference, distinct from ongoing sentient description. For other sentients, like hypothetical alien intelligences or advanced AI, "it" defaults unless anthropomorphized with "he" or "she," reflecting English's bias toward personhood markers for perceived agency.[61] Derogatory or dehumanizing employment of "it" for humans has appeared in historical records, such as 19th-century American slave auctions where captives were cataloged as "it" to commodify them as property, stripping linguistic recognition of individuality.[62] Such usages align with broader patterns in conflict rhetoric, where denying personal pronouns facilitates moral disengagement, though specific instances remain context-bound and ethically contested.[63] A small minority of individuals, often in non-traditional gender identity contexts, voluntarily adopt "it/its" as self-referential pronouns, viewing it as a deliberate rejection of binary norms, but this remains non-normative and infrequently encountered in corpora.[58] Overall, these applications highlight "it"'s tension between grammatical utility and cultural aversion to implying subhuman status for sentients.

Gender and Neutrality

Neuter Classification

In English grammar, the pronoun "it" functions as the third-person singular form denoting neuter gender, referring to entities without inherent masculine or feminine attributes, such as inanimate objects, abstract ideas, or situations lacking personal agency.[64] This classification aligns with the natural gender system in modern English, where pronouns reflect biological sex or its absence rather than arbitrary grammatical categories found in languages like German or Latin.[65] Unlike "he" for masculine referents or "she" for feminine ones, "it" and its possessive "its" avoid sex-specific markers, serving as the default for non-human or unspecified antecedents.[66] Linguists note that English nouns themselves carry no grammatical gender inflection, rendering "it" a pragmatic neuter marker primarily for non-sentient or impersonal nouns by default, unless context implies otherwise (e.g., personified ships historically taking "she").[67] This usage underscores a semantic distinction from epicene pronouns like singular "they," which increasingly handles gender-neutral human references while "it" remains tied to inanimacy to prevent dehumanization.[68] Empirical analysis of corpora confirms "it" comprises over 90% of third-person singular pronouns for concrete inanimates in standard texts, reinforcing its core neuter role without overlap into personal domains.[69]

Distinctions from Personal Pronouns

The pronoun "it" functions as the third-person singular neuter form within the English personal pronoun paradigm, sharing grammatical features such as subjective and objective case invariance (it/it) and possessive forms (its/itself) with "he" and "she," but differing fundamentally in semantic application.[70][71] While "he" and "she" typically reference animate entities, particularly humans, implying personhood, agency, and biological sex (masculine or feminine), "it" denotes inanimate objects, abstract concepts, or non-personified entities, eschewing implications of sentience or gender.[72][73] This referential distinction arises from historical Indo-European neuter categories, where "it" (from Old English hit) inherits a grammatical gender devoid of natural gender associations, contrasting with the natural gender alignment of "he" (masculine) and "she" (feminine).[72] In practice, "it" accommodates dummies in impersonal constructions (e.g., "It rains") or placeholders for non-human antecedents (e.g., "The book is on the table; it fell"), roles incompatible with "he" or "she" without anthropomorphization.[74] For animals, "it" prevails unless contextual personification invokes "he" or "she" to convey familiarity or traits akin to human personality.[72] Application to humans underscores the pronoun's impersonal valence: "it" is rarely employed for adults, as it signals dehumanization by negating personhood, a usage confined historically to derogatory or pathological contexts or temporarily for gender-unknown infants (e.g., "The baby is crying; it needs feeding").[4][55] Empirical analysis of English corpora confirms this restraint, with "it" for children appearing in neutral reporting (e.g., news media) at rates below 5% post-gender disclosure, prioritizing "he" or "she" to affirm individuality once sex is known.[55] Unlike the gendered pronouns, which encode social recognition of human distinctiveness, "it" enforces neutrality at the cost of relational distance, rendering it inadvisable for sentient referents in standard discourse.[72][4]

Usage Controversies

Dehumanization and Ethical Concerns

The use of the pronoun "it" to refer to humans has been associated with processes of dehumanization in psychological and linguistic research, where it signals a denial of personal agency, mind, or uniqueness, facilitating detachment or justification for harm. For instance, studies on dehumanized perception indicate that linguistic cues like neuter pronouns contribute to viewing targets as lacking human-like qualities, akin to objects or mechanisms, which correlates with reduced empathy and increased support for aggressive actions. In literary contexts, such as Franz Kafka's The Metamorphosis (1915), the shift to "it" for the protagonist Gregor Samsa underscores familial alienation and ethical erosion, reflecting broader patterns where neuter reference erodes moral standing. Empirical evidence from social psychology links such linguistic dehumanization to outcomes like diminished prosocial behavior, though causation remains debated due to confounding factors like preexisting prejudice.[83] Ethical concerns intensify when "it" is applied to sentient beings like animals, with advocacy groups arguing it objectifies individuals, erasing their sex, sentience, and moral status, thereby perpetuating speciesist attitudes that hinder welfare reforms. Organizations such as PETA have campaigned against default neuter usage, urging style guides like the Associated Press to adopt personal pronouns ("he," "she," or "they") for unnamed animals to foster recognition of their subjectivity and promote ethical treatment, as evidenced by their 2020 petition highlighting how "it" equates feeling beings with inanimate objects. Scholarly analyses in animal linguistics support this view, positing that neuter pronouns reinforce hierarchical animacy scales, potentially desensitizing publics to exploitation in industries like factory farming, where animals are routinely referenced impersonally. However, critics contend that mandating personal pronouns risks anthropomorphic overreach, which may distort biological realities without proven causal impact on policy or behavior changes, as rigorous longitudinal studies on pronoun effects remain scarce.[84][85] These debates underscore a tension between grammatical conventionâ€”rooted in "it" denoting non-personal entitiesâ€”and ethical imperatives for linguistic equity, yet source biases in advocacy literature, often from progressive animal rights circles, warrant scrutiny against neutral empirical baselines. While no large-scale experiments conclusively demonstrate that avoiding "it" enhances animal protections, correlational data from rhetoric studies suggest personal pronouns may amplify empathy in advocacy narratives, influencing public attitudes toward issues like vivisection or habitat loss. For humans, ethical prohibitions on "it" align with universal declarations like the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), which implicitly affirm personal pronouns to safeguard dignity against reductive labeling.[86]

Alternatives in Animal Advocacy

In animal advocacy, groups such as People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA) advocate replacing "it" with personal pronouns like "he," "she," or singular "they" when referring to animals, arguing that "it" objectifies sentient beings and diminishes recognition of their individuality.[84] In March 2020, PETA petitioned the Associated Press Stylebook to update its 2019 rule requiring "it" for unnamed or unsexed animals, proposing instead that animals receive "he" or "she" based on known sex or "they" otherwise to promote empathy.[84] [87] Similarly, In Defense of Animals recommends "they" for singular animals of indeterminate sex or "he or she" as alternatives to "it," regardless of identified sex, to affirm animals' moral status and encourage viewing them as subjects rather than objects.[88] Marin Humane echoes this by urging personal pronouns for animals in communications, such as shelter descriptions, to elevate their perceived status and reduce dehumanizing language.[89] These preferences stem from the view that animate pronouns foster emotional connections and ethical consideration, as evidenced by primatologist Jane Goodall's endorsement of "she," "he," or "they" over "it" to reflect animals' importance in news and discourse.[90] Research in animal studies supports using gendered pronouns even for animals of unknown sex, challenging linguistic norms that treat nonhumans as inanimate to undermine speciesist dualism.[86] Advocates like those at Faunalytics note that emotional bonds, such as with pets, often drive shifts to "he," "she," or "they," correlating with stronger welfare outcomes.[7] Critics within and outside advocacy circles contend that such pronoun shifts risk over-anthropomorphizing animals, potentially conflating biological sex distinctions with human-like personhood, though proponents prioritize causal impacts on public attitudes toward reducing harm.[91]

Ideological Debates on Neutrality

The pronoun "it" has sparked ideological contention over its capacity to serve as a truly neutral referent, particularly in contexts requiring avoidance of sex-based distinctions. Proponents of its neutrality argue from linguistic structure that "it" functions as the impersonal neuter form in English, categorically distinct from personal pronouns like "he" or "she" and thus free of gendered connotations.[92] This view posits that "it" achieves causal neutrality by referencing entities without imputing human agency or sex, aligning with historical defenses such as Samuel Taylor Coleridge's 19th-century endorsement of "it" for indefinite persons to prevent arbitrary gender assignment.[92] Such arguments emphasize empirical grammar over subjective perceptions, contending that alternatives like singular "they" introduce plurality ambiguity without resolving underlying assumptions about personhood.[93] Opposing perspectives, prevalent in progressive linguistic and psychological discourse, maintain that "it" ideologically encodes dehumanization by equating humans with inanimates, thereby eroding moral consideration.[94] Empirical analyses of hate speech document "it" as a discursive tool to deny personhood, as in references to outgroups or victims that justify exclusion or violence, with effects observed across racist, political, and homophobic rhetoric.[94] [95] These claims draw on psychological studies linking neuter pronouns to reduced attribution of agency, though such findings often conflate contextual intent with inherent properties and prioritize harm narratives over neutral referential utility.[96] The divide reflects broader ideological tensions: advocates for unrestricted language use, often aligned with traditionalist or libertarian views, defend "it" as a bulwark against engineered pronouns that impose identity frameworks, arguing that enforced alternatives like "they" compromise clarity without evidence of causal benefit to equity.[97] Conversely, institutional sources in academia and mediaâ€”frequently exhibiting systemic biases toward inclusivity mandatesâ€”promote singular "they" as ethically superior for maintaining personhood while achieving neutrality, evidenced by style guides and surveys showing its rising acceptance since the 2010s.[98] [99] This preference correlates with essentialist resistance metrics, where lower endorsement of binary forms predicts support for "they," but overlooks "it"'s grammatical precedence in non-personal domains.[97] Rare self-adoptions of "it" by individuals, as in certain non-binary or identity-subcultural contexts, challenge dehumanization critiques yet remain marginal against dominant advocacy for personal-neutral hybrids.[100]

Cultural and Idiomatic Impact

Literary and Media References

In Edith Nesbit's 1902 children's novel Five Children and It, the titular Psammeadâ€”a wish-granting sand-fairyâ€”is referred to exclusively as "It" using the neuter pronoun, emphasizing its otherworldly, non-humanoid nature and avoiding gendered terms throughout the narrative.[101] This usage underscores "it" as a deliberate choice for entities defying binary categorization, with the creature's self-identification reinforcing its impersonal, ancient origins.[102] Elinor Glyn's 1927 novella It and its film adaptation popularized "It" as a noun derived from the pronoun, denoting an indefinable personal magnetism or sex appeal, as in the description of a character possessing "that nameless charm... which can only be called 'It'."[46] The story, starring Clara Bow as the "It Girl," elevated the term in 1920s media, transforming the neuter pronoun into cultural shorthand for charismatic allure independent of gender.[103] Stephen King's 1986 horror novel It features a shape-shifting, ancient entity terrorizing Derry, Maine, named and pronominalized as "It" to evoke dread through dehumanization and formlessness.[104] Adaptations, including the 1990 miniseries and 2017â€“2019 films, retain this pronoun usage, capitalizing "It" to signify the creature's eldritch horror, distinct from human pronouns.[105] In children's games like tag, documented since at least the 17th century, the pursuing player is designated "It," embedding the neuter pronoun in global play culture as a marker of temporary, impersonal role assignment.[106] This convention appears in media depictions of childhood, such as folklore collections and films, reinforcing "it" as a neutral identifier for active agents in ritualized pursuit.[106]

Fixed Expressions and Proverbs

The pronoun "it" frequently functions as a dummy subject in English fixed expressions and proverbs, enabling impersonal statements about situations, behaviors, or inevitabilities without specifying a concrete referent. These constructions often convey proverbial wisdom, emphasizing causality or resignation, and have roots in everyday observations or cultural metaphors traceable to the 18th or 19th centuries in many cases.[107] One prominent example is "it takes two to tango," a proverb asserting that certain actions, such as quarrels or romantic entanglements, require mutual participation rather than unilateral blame. The expression draws from the partnered nature of the tango dance and entered common usage in the 1920s, though it gained widespread popularity through Pearl Bailey's 1952 song of the same name written by Al Hoffman and Dick Manning.[108][109] "It is what it is" represents a tautological fixed expression of stoic acceptance toward unalterable realities, avoiding futile complaint or alteration attempts. Documented in print as early as 1949 in a Nebraska newspaper article by J.E. Lawrence, it reflects a pragmatic worldview but has been critiqued for passivity in philosophical discourse.[110] The proverb "it never rains but it pours" illustrates the tendency of misfortunes to accumulate rather than occur in isolation, akin to weather patterns where light rain escalates. Originating in 18th-century English literature, such as Thomas D'Urfey's 1720 song collection Pills to Purge Melancholy, it predates its 1914 adaptation as a Morton Salt advertising slogan highlighting free-flowing product despite humidity.[111] "Take it or leave it" serves as a fixed ultimatum in negotiations or offers, demanding acceptance of terms without modification. Recorded by the 18th century, as in Sir Charles Hanbury Williams' 1760 writings, it underscores non-negotiable finality in transactions.[112] Other expressions include "put a sock in it," an imperative to cease noisy complaints, emerging in early 20th-century British slang possibly from muffling phonographs with socks; and "get over it," urging emotional recovery from setbacks, attested in mid-20th-century American usage.[113] These highlight "it"'s versatility in idiomatic impersonality, preserving linguistic economy across contexts.
