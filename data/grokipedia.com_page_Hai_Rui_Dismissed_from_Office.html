Hai Rui Dismissed from Office

Hai Rui Dismissed from Office (海瑞罢官, Hǎi Ruì bà guān) is a historical drama written by Chinese historian and Beijing vice-mayor Wu Han in late 1959, portraying the Ming dynasty censor Hai Rui's principled stand against corruption by returning illegally seized land to peasants, leading to his dismissal by Emperor Jiajing.[1] The play, styled as a traditional Peking opera, premiered in 1961 and initially received praise for upholding socialist values through Hai Rui's integrity.[2] However, Mao Zedong interpreted it as an allegory defending Peng Dehuai, the defense minister dismissed in 1959 for critiquing the Great Leap Forward's failures, viewing Hai Rui's fate as paralleling Peng's purge.[3] This perception prompted Yao Wenyuan's 1965 critique, published with Mao's endorsement, which accused the play of historical distortion to undermine proletarian dictatorship and incite rightist reversal.[4] The ensuing campaign against Wu Han and the play's ban marked the ideological prelude to the Cultural Revolution, mobilizing attacks on perceived bourgeois elements in culture and party leadership.[1][3] Wu Han's intent appeared rooted in admiration for Hai Rui's rectitude amid post-Great Leap dissatisfaction, but Mao leveraged the drama to consolidate power by framing it as counter-revolutionary poison.[2]

Historical and Literary Context

The Historical Figure of Hai Rui

Hai Rui (1514–1587), courtesy name Ruxian and style name Gangfeng, was a Ming dynasty official celebrated for his uncompromising integrity and campaigns against corruption. Born into a Hui family in Qiongshan, present-day Hainan, he was raised by his mother after his father's early death, receiving a Confucian education that shaped his ethical outlook.[5][6] Hai Rui entered officialdom after passing the provincial civil service examinations in 1553, earning the juren degree. His initial posting as magistrate of Chun'an county in Zhejiang province highlighted his strict adherence to justice; he ordered the flogging of a local military commander's son for raping a servant girl, an act that resulted in his demotion but garnered public acclaim for defying privilege.[5] In subsequent roles, including magistrate of Houguan county in Fujian, he redressed peasant grievances by restoring lands illegally seized by elites, establishing his reputation as a defender of the common people against entrenched power.[5] By 1560, he served in the Office of Scrutiny for Forest and Parks, advancing to supervising secretary in the Ministry of Justice in 1562. As a censor in 1565, Hai Rui submitted a direct memorial to Emperor Jiajing (r. 1521–1567), rebuking the ruler's prolonged neglect of state duties in pursuit of Daoist immortality practices and ritual obsessions, which had exacerbated fiscal distress and administrative decay during the emperor's later years.[5][7] Enraged, Jiajing ordered Hai's arrest and prepared for his execution, viewing the critique as insubordinate. Hai remained imprisoned until the emperor's death in 1567, after which the Longqing Emperor (r. 1567–1572) released him and appointed him vice minister of justice.[5][7] Under the subsequent Wanli Emperor (r. 1572–1620), Hai Rui faced brief re-imprisonment under the regency but was reinstated as minister of war, where he continued advocating reforms until his death in office on November 13, 1587, in Nanjing.[5] His legacy as a symbol of incorruptible governance persisted in Chinese history, influencing later cultural depictions of principled officials willing to risk imperial wrath for moral rectitude.[6]

Wu Han's Background and Motivations for the Play

Wu Han (1909–1969) was a prominent Chinese historian specializing in Ming dynasty studies, having graduated from Tsinghua University in the 1930s with a focus on that era, including authoring a biography of the dynasty's founder, Zhu Yuanzhang.[8] By the early 1940s, he had established himself as a respected scholar teaching at institutions like Southwest Associated University, initially affiliated with the Democratic League before aligning with the Chinese Communist Party after 1949.[9] Within the party, Wu applied Marxist frameworks to historical analysis, interpreting Ming events to draw parallels with contemporary politics, and by the late 1950s, he held the position of vice-mayor of Beijing, blending academic and administrative roles.[10] The motivations for Wu Han's play Hai Rui Dismissed from Office stemmed from the political fallout of the Lushan Conference in July–August 1959, where Defense Minister Peng Dehuai openly criticized Mao Zedong's Great Leap Forward policies, leading to Peng's dismissal and purge as a "right opportunist."[2] Beginning in June 1959, Wu Han published articles praising Hai Rui, a Ming official known for confronting corruption and redressing peasant grievances, only to be dismissed by Emperor Jiajing—events Wu explicitly linked in his writings to contemporary dismissals, including Peng's.[4] This historical allegory served Wu's aim to advocate for Peng's rehabilitation, portraying Hai Rui as an upright figure unjustly removed for speaking truth to power, a narrative Wu developed into the full play by late 1959 amid calls from Mao himself to study Hai Rui's example of "restraining the bureaucracy."[2] Though Wu later denied direct intent to critique Mao, scholarly analyses interpret the play's composition as a veiled plea for political redress, using traditional historical drama—a common tactic among intellectuals—to comment on recent events without overt confrontation, reflecting Wu's dual identity as historian and party cadre navigating intra-party tensions post-Great Leap failures.[11] The work premiered in 1960 and was published in 1961, gaining initial approval before reinterpretation as subversive during escalating Cultural Revolution prelude.[3]

Content and Themes of the Play

Hai Rui Dismissed from Office (海瑞罢官), written by Wu Han in 1959 and first performed as a Peking opera in 1961, dramatizes the historical episode of Hai Rui's tenure as prefect of Yingchuan in 1569–1570 during the Ming dynasty's Jiajing reign.[1] The central conflict revolves around Hai Rui's implementation of a "return farmland" (退田) policy, ordering the restitution of illegally seized lands from peasants by corrupt officials and landlords within ten days.[4] In the narrative, Hai Rui, portrayed as an incorruptible and resolute administrator, executes lesser corrupt officials and sentences the son of the powerful minister Xu Jie to death for involvement in land grabs, thereby provoking backlash from the imperial court.[4] Despite his loyalty to the emperor, Hai Rui's bold remonstrations and actions lead to his public denunciation and dismissal from office, emphasizing the perils of principled governance amid entrenched corruption.[12] Key characters include Hai Rui as the heroic protagonist who prioritizes justice for the peasantry, passive peasants depicted as reliant on official intervention for redress, and antagonists such as Xu Jie, symbolizing exploitative elites who encroach on common lands.[4] The play's structure unfolds through scenes of Hai Rui's investigations, confrontations with local powerholders, and submission of a memorial critiquing systemic abuses while affirming imperial authority.[4] Thematically, the work extols themes of official integrity, the moral duty to rectify injustices against the common people, and the tension between loyalty to the sovereign and adherence to righteous policy.[13] It presents Hai Rui as a didactic exemplar of "speaking truth to power," highlighting how virtuous reforms, such as land restitution, can challenge vested interests and invite retribution from higher authorities.[12] Wu Han frames these elements within a historical lens, drawing on Hai Rui's real-life reputation for anti-corruption efforts, though the play idealizes his role as a solitary savior amid a depiction of inert rural masses.[4] Overall, the narrative underscores causal links between unchecked elite abuses and social unrest, advocating for bold administrative intervention as a remedy, without delving into broader revolutionary upheaval.[1]

Pre-Criticism Political Environment

The Lushan Conference and Peng Dehuai's Dismissal

The Lushan Conference, formally an enlarged meeting of the Politburo of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) Central Committee, convened from July 2 to August 16, 1959, at the scenic mountain resort of Lushan in Jiangxi Province, to assess the ongoing Great Leap Forward campaign and address emerging economic difficulties, including reports of widespread crop failures and resource misallocation.[14] Initially focused on correcting excesses in collectivization and industrial targets, the gathering shifted dramatically when Peng Dehuai, the CCP's Minister of National Defense and a Politburo member with a record of military successes including the Korean War, submitted a private letter to Mao Zedong on July 14. In the letter, Peng candidly critiqued the Great Leap Forward's implementation, highlighting issues such as inflated production statistics, coercive labor practices in people's communes, damage to agricultural productivity from overzealous mobilization, and the resulting hardships for peasants, while praising Mao's overall intentions but urging pragmatic adjustments to avert disaster.[14][15] Mao Zedong, interpreting Peng's input as a veiled assault on his leadership and an alignment with "rightist" elements opposed to the campaign's radicalism, circulated the letter among conference attendees on July 21, framing it as evidence of an anti-party clique seeking to undermine the socialist transformation.[16] This provoked intense debate during the conference's second phase, starting July 23, where Mao rallied support by accusing Peng and allies like Zhang Wentian and Huang Kecheng of factionalism and echoing historical betrayals, such as those by Wang Ming. Peng defended his views in speeches, emphasizing empirical observations from his inspection tours rather than personal ambition, but Mao's dominance prevailed, with the majority aligning against him amid fears of being labeled disloyal.[14] The conference concluded with the "Resolution on the Question of Comrade Peng Dehuai's Rightist Anti-Party Activities" adopted on August 16, 1959, which formally condemned Peng's letter as a "poisonous arrow" aimed at the Central Committee and party unity.[15] Consequently, Peng was stripped of his positions as Defense Minister, Vice Premier, and Politburo member, replaced as Defense Minister by Lin Biao on September 25, 1959, and effectively sidelined from power, though he retained nominal party membership until further persecution during the Cultural Revolution.[17] This purge not only silenced internal dissent on the Great Leap Forward's failures—which empirical data later revealed had contributed to a famine killing an estimated 15 to 45 million—but also reinforced Mao's insistence on continuing high-target policies, delaying corrective measures until 1960-1961.[14]

Initial Reception and Mao Zedong's Early Concerns

Hai Rui Dismissed from Office, completed by Wu Han in late 1960, premiered on February 17, 1961, at Beijing's Jinru Theater under the direction of Zhou Xinfang. The production received immediate and enthusiastic acclaim from audiences, critics, and Party officials, who lauded its depiction of Hai Rui as an incorruptible official who redressed peasant grievances by returning seized lands, themes resonant with the post-Great Leap Forward emphasis on correcting economic excesses and restoring rural stability.[18] By mid-1961, the play had been staged over 200 times in Beijing and toured provinces, generating significant interest in historical dramas as vehicles for socialist education, with Wu Han himself noting in a May 1961 interview its role in popularizing "historical plays with revolutionary spirit."[2] Mao Zedong's early concerns emerged shortly after the premiere, as he privately linked the play's plot to the 1959 Lushan Conference, where he had dismissed Defense Minister Peng Dehuai for criticizing the Great Leap Forward's failures. Mao interpreted Hai Rui's bold remonstrance against Emperor Jiajing, subsequent dismissal, and the posthumous demand to "right wrongs" and reinstate him as an allegory portraying Peng as the virtuous Hai Rui, himself as the errant emperor, and rural land restitution as a coded call to rehabilitate Peng amid famine recovery efforts. In September 1961 discussions, Mao explicitly stated that the play centered on "dismissing Peng Dehuai from office," emphasizing the parallel between the drama's "return farmland to tillers" motif and politically charged rehabilitation demands, viewing it as a bourgeois critique disguised in historical form.[13][1] Despite these reservations, Mao did not publicly oppose the play at the time, allowing its continued popularity as part of broader cultural campaigns promoting Hai Rui as a model official—a figure Mao himself had praised in 1959 to underscore loyalty amid post-Lushan tensions. This initial tolerance reflected the play's alignment with immediate policy needs for grievance redress during the early 1960s recovery, though Mao's analogical reading foreshadowed its later weaponization, with concerns simmering until escalated in 1965.[3][19]

The Criticism and Escalation

Yao Wenyuan's Article in Wenhui Bao

Yao Wenyuan, a Shanghai-based literary critic and propagandist affiliated with the municipal party apparatus, published the article titled "On the New Historical Play 'Hai Rui Dismissed from Office'" in Wenhui Bao on November 10, 1965.[4] The piece, drafted over several months with revisions involving senior figures including Jiang Qing, represented the first major public assault on Wu Han's 1961 play, framing it as a politically motivated distortion rather than mere historical drama.[2] Yao's critique explicitly linked the work to broader ideological struggles, accusing it of inverting class relations and challenging proletarian dictatorship under Mao Zedong Thought.[4] Central to Yao's argument was the claim that Wu Han fabricated historical events to glorify Hai Rui as a selfless defender of the peasantry, thereby promoting feudal loyalty over class struggle. He contended that Hai's 1557 "return cultivated land to tillers" policy primarily expropriated holdings from middle and small landlords as well as rich peasants—such as the 240,000 to 400,000 mou owned by the Hsu clan—rather than aiding the poorest tenants, contradicting Ming dynasty records and Marxist historiography.[4] Yao highlighted fictional elements, like Hai Rui ordering the execution of Hsu Ying (a character based loosely on Hsu Chieh's kin), whereas historical accounts indicate only banishment of offenders' sons, arguing this embellishment served to sanitize Hai as a class-transcending hero detached from feudal exploitation.[4] Yao further asserted that the play functioned as an allegory for reversing socialist policies, drawing parallels to 1961 rural recovery efforts amid post-Great Leap Forward hardships, where demands for "redressing grievances" and land restitution echoed Hai's actions and implicitly critiqued collectivization.[4] He tied this to Wu Han's rightist leanings since 1957, portraying the drama as part of a bourgeois offensive to rehabilitate anti-party elements and foster class conciliation, in direct opposition to Mao Zedong's emphasis on continued revolution and antagonism between classes.[4] By idealizing imperial officials who "dared to speak truth to power," Yao charged, Wu undermined the proletariat's vanguard role and echoed sentiments from earlier intra-party conflicts, such as those surrounding economic rectification campaigns. The article's publication, authorized by Mao Zedong who subsequently directed its nationwide reprinting in major newspapers, marked a deliberate escalation from prior muted concerns about the play, positioning cultural criticism as a tool for purging revisionist tendencies within the party. Yao demanded that Wu Han publicly repudiate his errors, warning that unchecked "poisonous weeds" in literature threatened socialist construction, though he stopped short of immediate calls for organizational sanctions.[4] This critique, rooted in orthodox Leninist cultural policy, prioritized empirical historical fidelity and dialectical materialism over artistic license, reflecting Yao's alignment with Mao-era directives against "feudal residues" in intellectual work.[4]

Wu Han's Self-Criticism and Responses

Following Yao Wenyuan's critical article published on November 10, 1965, in Wenhui Bao, Wu Han, as deputy mayor of Beijing and the play's author, initially coordinated responses through the Beijing Party committee under Peng Zhen, including defensive articles in local newspapers that emphasized the play's historical accuracy and artistic merits without direct political allegory.[20] These responses, appearing in outlets like Beijing Ribao in late November and early December, rejected Yao's interpretation as overreach and portrayed the criticism as ideologically motivated rather than scholarly.[21] Under directives from Peng Zhen, Wu Han drafted a formal self-criticism, published on December 30, 1965, in Renmin Ribao, where he conceded specific historical inaccuracies—such as the play's depiction of Hai Rui's land redistribution policies diverging from Ming dynasty records—and admitted to insufficient class analysis in portraying feudal elements.[2] However, Wu maintained that the work's core intent was to promote anti-corruption themes aligned with socialist values, framing the errors as technical rather than politically subversive, and implicitly defended the play's relevance to contemporary official conduct.[2] Subsequent evaluations by radical critics, including associates of Yao Wenyuan, dismissed this self-criticism as perfunctory and evasive, arguing it served as a veiled counterattack by reasserting bourgeois historiographical methods and failing to uproot the play's alleged rightist essence.[22] Wu Han's document acknowledged external influences like Peng Dehuai's 1959 dismissal at the Lushan Conference but decoupled them from intentional allegory, a stance that intensified scrutiny as Mao Zedong's concerns escalated in early 1966.[22] Despite these concessions, no further public recantations from Wu Han appeared before the campaign's broadening in February 1966, reflecting the Beijing leadership's temporary shielding of him amid intra-party tensions.[21]

The February Outline and Related Party Directives

The February Outline, officially titled the "Outline of the Report of the Five-Person Cultural Revolution Group Concerning the Present Academic Discussion," was drafted on February 7, 1966, and circulated for implementation by the CCP Central Committee on February 12, 1966.[23] This document, prepared under the direction of Beijing Party Secretary Peng Zhen and the Five-Person Group (comprising Peng Zhen, Lu Dingyi, Kang Sheng, Zhou Yang, and Wu Lengxi), aimed to regulate the escalating debate over Wu Han's Hai Rui Dismissed from Office following Yao Wenyuan's November 1965 critique in Wenhui Bao.[13] It positioned the play's evaluation as one element within a larger "academic discussion" on historiography, the inheritance of moral standards from China's feudal past, and the depiction of "incorruptible officials" in literature, explicitly avoiding escalation into broader class struggle or contemporary political analogies.[23] The Outline directed party committees at all levels to organize structured criticism sessions, emphasizing reliance on "left-wing" academic forces to form revolutionary teams and mutual-assistance groups for debating bourgeois influences in culture.[23] It mandated the establishment of dedicated offices, such as an "academic criticism office" under figures like Xu Liqun, to oversee publications and ensure discussions proceeded through "facts and reasoning" rather than administrative suppression or unsubstantiated attacks.[23] Key methodological instructions included encouraging open expression of diverse views to identify and rectify "bourgeois rightist" errors, while prohibiting public naming of individuals without higher approval, hasty organizational conclusions, or linkage of historical critiques to living party leaders' actions.[23] This approach sought to foster long-term cadre development toward being both "red" (ideologically pure) and "expert" (scholastically proficient), framing the process as an ongoing socialist-versus-capitalist ideological contest without immediate purges.[23] Related party directives reinforced containment, instructing lower organs to report progress upward and maintain party unity by tolerating temporary divergences in opinion during debates, provided they aligned with Marxist-Leninist principles.[23] Local committees were to prioritize internal rectification over public mobilization, with an emphasis on self-criticism among intellectuals like Wu Han to resolve contradictions "democratically" within the framework of proletarian dictatorship.[24] However, Mao Zedong rejected this framework as early as February 8, 1966, arguing in a Politburo discussion that the play's core issue—"dismissal from office"—directly echoed the 1959 Lushan Conference purge of Peng Dehuai and constituted a veiled counter-revolutionary defense of rightist elements, which the Outline obscured by reducing it to abstract academic theory.[25] Mao viewed these guidelines as revisionist, protecting entrenched party figures from accountability and diluting the necessary class-based confrontation, leading to the document's internal repudiation by April 1966 and Peng Zhen's subsequent dismissal.[24]

Launch and Consequences in the Cultural Revolution

The May 16 Circular and Official Campaign

The May 16 Circular, formally titled "Circular of the Central Committee of the Communist Party of China on the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution," was issued on May 16, 1966, by the CCP Central Committee under Mao Zedong's direction.[24] This document explicitly warned of "counter-revolutionary revisionists" who had infiltrated party, government, military, and cultural institutions, urging a "great proletarian cultural revolution" to expose and eradicate their influence through mass mobilization and ideological struggle.[26] It emphasized ongoing class struggle under socialism, rejecting notions that antagonistic contradictions had ceased, and called for purging "poisonous weeds" in literature and art that promoted feudal or capitalist ideologies.[24] Central to the circular's accusations was the mishandling of criticism against Wu Han's play Hai Rui Dismissed from Office. It condemned senior leaders, including the "Five-Man Group" on cultural revolution led by Peng Zhen, for restricting debate to academic or historical grounds and prohibiting discussion of the play's "heart of the matter"—its implicit defense of Peng Dehuai's 1959 dismissal at the Lushan Conference, where Hai Rui symbolized Peng's upright remonstrance against Mao's policies.[24] The document dissolved this group and related offices, accusing them of shielding bourgeois elements by declaring such political linkages "impermissible," thereby obstructing Mao's instructions for deeper ideological critique.[24] This directly escalated the suppression of the play from localized journalistic attacks, like Yao Wenyuan's November 1965 article, to a nationwide party-directed purge.[26] The circular formalized the campaign by establishing a new Cultural Revolution Group under Mao's allies, including Jiang Qing, to oversee the movement, while instructing party organs to "touch the souls" of critics through self-examination and mass criticism sessions.[24] It mobilized workers, peasants, students, and intellectuals to identify and denounce revisionist "monsters and demons," with Hai Rui serving as the inaugural target symbolizing resistance to Mao's authority.[26] Within days, this led to intensified propaganda, including editorials in People's Daily linking the play to broader conspiracies against socialist construction, and set the stage for red guard formations that would amplify the campaign into violent factional strife.[24] The directive's emphasis on unrestrained exposure over procedural limits marked a shift from intra-party rectification to open societal upheaval, targeting over 60,000 cadres in initial waves by associating cultural dissent with political treason.[26]

Immediate Purges and Wu Han's Persecution

Following the issuance of the May 16 Notification on May 16, 1966, which explicitly identified Wu Han's play Hai Rui Dismissed from Office as a focal point of ideological struggle, the Chinese Communist Party leadership initiated swift actions against Wu and his protectors in the Beijing municipal apparatus.[24] Wu Han, serving as deputy mayor of Beijing and a prominent historian, was immediately stripped of his party positions and subjected to public denunciation sessions organized by Red Guard groups and party cadres.[27] These sessions, beginning in late May and intensifying through June 1966, involved mass rallies where Wu was accused of poisoning youth with "feudal" and "anti-party" ideas, forcing him to endure physical and verbal abuse while delivering multiple self-criticisms admitting to "rightist" errors.[3] Parallel to Wu's targeting, purges extended to senior officials linked to the suppression of earlier criticisms of the play. Peng Zhen, Beijing's mayor and a Politburo member who had defended Wu and issued the February Outline to contain the debate, was dismissed from all posts on May 25, 1966, and labeled a leader of an "anti-party clique."[28] This purge dismantled the "Group of Five" in Beijing's cultural oversight, including figures like Lu Dingyi, head of the Propaganda Department, who faced isolation and later formal expulsion.[26] By early June 1966, over 100 officials in Beijing's party and cultural organs associated with Wu's network—such as editors at People's Daily who had delayed reprinting Yao Wenyuan's article—were removed, with many confined for "interrogation" and public shaming.[8] Wu's persecution escalated through 1966, marked by house arrest, beatings during struggle meetings, and denial of medical care for his worsening health conditions, including heart issues documented in internal party reports.[29] He attempted further self-criticisms in July and October 1966, conceding the play's alleged parallels to Peng Dehuai's 1959 Lushan critique of Mao, but these failed to halt the campaign, leading to his formal imprisonment by 1967.[2] Wu died on April 11, 1969, in a Beijing hospital under guard, officially from a heart attack but attributed by contemporaries to cumulative torture and neglect, with autopsy withheld.[3] These events exemplified the Notification's call to "bombard the headquarters," purging an estimated 460 "capitalist roaders" in cultural fields within the first three months.[27]

Broader Political Ramifications

The criticism of Hai Rui Dismissed from Office facilitated Mao Zedong's strategic purge of entrenched party elites perceived as obstructing radical ideological renewal, beginning with the Beijing municipal leadership. Peng Zhen, secretary of the Beijing Chinese Communist Party (CCP) committee and Politburo member, had initially approved Wu Han's play and resisted its politicization by organizing a February 1966 symposium that produced the "February Outline," which sought to limit debate to scholarly terms and defend party cultural policies. Mao interpreted this as defiance, resulting in Peng's removal from power in late April 1966, alongside associates like Beijing Mayor Wu Han (already targeted) and municipal party secretaries, effectively decapitating the capital's administrative structure and exposing it to radical takeover.[30][13] This localized purge rapidly scaled to national dimensions, targeting figures aligned with post-Great Leap Forward pragmatism, including PLA chief of staff Luo Ruiqing (dismissed in March 1966 for alleged bourgeois militarism) and Central Propaganda Department head Lu Dingyi, whose resistance to Shanghai-led attacks mirrored Peng's. By enabling the formation of the Central Cultural Revolution Group in May 1966—chaired by Mao's allies such as Chen Boda and Jiang Qing—the campaign shifted authority from conventional party organs to ad hoc radical bodies, bypassing the Politburo and Secretariat dominated by Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping.[3][13] The affair's ramifications extended to ideological reconfiguration, legitimizing mass mobilization against "revisionists" and foreshadowing the Red Guard movement's assaults on authority. Mao's explicit linkage of the play's theme—Hai Rui's dismissal—to his own 1959 ouster of Peng Dehuai at the Lushan Conference framed cultural critique as a proxy for class struggle within the party, eroding bureaucratic norms and precipitating factional cleavages that intensified through 1966. This preemptive strike against potential successors ensured Mao's dominance amid policy disputes over economic recovery, but it also unleashed uncontrolled purges affecting millions, as institutional checks dissolved in favor of personal loyalty tests.[8][3]

Long-Term Impact and Historiographical Debates

Role in Triggering the Cultural Revolution

The criticism of Wu Han's Hai Rui Dismissed from Office marked the opening salvo in Mao Zedong's campaign to purge ideological adversaries within the Chinese Communist Party (CCP), serving as a deliberate pretext to reassert his dominance after perceived marginalization following the Great Leap Forward's failures. Mao had initially viewed the play, first performed in Beijing on December 17, 1961, as an allegorical defense of Peng Dehuai, the defense minister dismissed in 1959 for critiquing Mao's policies at the Lushan Conference; by late 1965, with economic recovery under Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping emphasizing pragmatism over radicalism, Mao selected this cultural artifact to test loyalties and expose "revisionists" without immediate high-level confrontation.[31][13] On November 10, 1965, Yao Wenyuan, a Shanghai propagandist aligned with Mao, published "Critique of the New Historical Play Hai Rui Dismissed from Office" in Wenhui Bao, accusing Wu Han—then Beijing's deputy mayor—of promoting a bourgeois fantasy that justified "rightist" opposition to proletarian leadership by glorifying an imperial official's remonstrance against corrupt rule. The article, drafted with input from Mao's allies like Ke Qingshi and Zhang Chunqiao, deliberately evaded Beijing's censorship under Peng Zhen, signaling Mao's intent to bypass the capital's moderate establishment; it framed the play not as mere historiography but as a coded attack on socialist dictatorship, linking Hai Rui's dismissal and later reinstatement to Peng Dehuai's purge and the need to "reverse verdicts" on such cases.[4][8] This sparked escalating intra-party debates, with Wu Han issuing self-criticisms in December 1965 and January 1966 that failed to contain the fallout, as Mao rejected conciliatory efforts and mobilized radical factions. Peng Zhen, head of the Beijing Party Committee and a key Liu Shaoqi supporter, attempted to delimit the controversy through the "February Outline" on February 8, 1966, which treated the play's issues as academic rather than political class struggle; Mao, viewing this as protectionism for "capitalist roaders," orchestrated its repudiation, leading to the purge of Peng and his allies in April 1966. The chain culminated in the May 16, 1966, Notification from the CCP Central Committee, which dissolved Peng's apparatus, branded the play's defense as symptomatic of bourgeois restorationism, and formally launched the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution as a mass movement to safeguard Mao's revolutionary line.[13][18] Historians emphasize that while the play itself contained no explicit anti-Mao content—Wu Han had positioned it as upholding socialist values—the orchestrated assault exploited its ambiguities to legitimize Mao's causal strategy: initiating conflict in the cultural sphere to unearth and eliminate opposition in the political core, thereby preempting challenges to his authority amid policy setbacks. This approach reflected Mao's realism in using low-risk pretexts to gauge and fracture elite alignments, with the Hai Rui episode enabling the mobilization of over 1.5 million Red Guards by summer 1966 and the sidelining of Liu Shaoqi, whose pragmatic reforms Mao deemed revisionist. Critical analyses, drawing on declassified CCP documents, counter official PRC narratives minimizing the play's significance by highlighting Mao's premeditated role in amplifying it to reverse post-1962 moderation.[31][2]

Official PRC Interpretations Versus Critical Analyses

The official interpretation propagated by the Chinese Communist Party during the Cultural Revolution framed Wu Han's 1960 play Hai Rui Dismissed from Office as a deliberate counterrevolutionary allegory critiquing Mao Zedong's leadership, with the upright Ming official Hai Rui symbolizing Peng Dehuai, whom Mao dismissed at the 1959 Lushan Conference for opposing Great Leap Forward policies.[4] Yao Wenyuan's November 10, 1965, article in Wenhui Bao, endorsed by Mao, accused the play of fabricating history to justify "reversing correct verdicts" and poisoning public opinion against socialist construction, portraying it as evidence of revisionist infiltration in cultural spheres.[4] This view positioned the ensuing critique campaign as a necessary ideological purge to defend the revolution from bourgeois elements within the party apparatus.[13] In the post-Mao era, the People's Republic of China's 1981 Resolution on Certain Questions in the History of Our Party since the Founding of the PRC maintained that the Cultural Revolution stemmed from Mao's overestimation of class struggle intensity, but implicitly upheld the play's role as an early manifestation of the "erroneous line" that justified mass campaigns, while condemning the overall movement as a "severe setback" causing widespread losses.[32] Official historiography continues to depict the Hai Rui controversy as emblematic of pre-Cultural Revolution revisionism, though it subordinates the play to broader attributions of Mao's personal errors in initiating upheaval against perceived capitalist-roaders like Liu Shaoqi.[32] Critical analyses by Western scholars, such as Roderick MacFarquhar in his Origins of the Cultural Revolution series, contend that the official narrative inflates the play's causal role, treating it as a manufactured pretext rather than a genuine spark, given Mao's four-year delay in acting despite early awareness of the work's alleged parallels to Peng's fate.[33] MacFarquhar argues Mao's motives centered on preempting challenges to his authority from pragmatic reformers amid post-Great Leap economic recovery and the Sino-Soviet split, using the literary critique to mobilize allies like Jiang Qing and test reactions before escalating to systemic purges in 1966.[34] This perspective highlights archival evidence of Mao's prior dissatisfaction with party bureaucracy since 1962, suggesting the Hai Rui campaign served strategic signaling to radicals rather than organic ideological defense.[35] Other scholarly critiques emphasize that Wu Han's intent was historical dramatization of Hai Rui's anti-corruption legacy, not coded partisanship, as evidenced by the play's initial approval and popularity in 1961; the official allegory relied on forced equivalences unsubstantiated by Wu's writings or contemporary records.[2] Revisionist readings further question the narrative's coherence by noting Mao's selective endorsement—Yao's article bypassed Beijing censorship under Peng Zhen's influence, indicating orchestrated subversion over spontaneous outrage—and argue it obscured deeper power dynamics, including Mao's fear of marginalization akin to Khrushchev's ouster.[12] These analyses, drawing on declassified documents and memoirs, portray the play's elevation as a "red herring" diverting from structural failures like the Great Leap's 20-45 million excess deaths, which fueled elite dissent Mao sought to preempt.[35][2]

Alternative Viewpoints and Revisionist Readings

Some historians contend that Wu Han's portrayal of Hai Rui was not intended as a veiled critique of Mao Zedong's 1959 dismissal of Peng Dehuai at the Lushan Conference, but rather as a historical affirmation of bureaucratic integrity and loyalty to the state, drawing on Ming dynasty precedents without direct contemporary allegory. Wu Han explicitly stated in pre-escalation defenses that the play celebrated Hai Rui's restitution of land to peasants as a model of just governance, consistent with his earlier scholarly works on Hai Rui as an upright official, and denied any subversive intent. This reading challenges the Maoist interpretation by emphasizing textual fidelity to historical records over imposed political symbolism, noting Mao's own initial endorsement of the play in 1961 as evidence against inherent anti-party content. Revisionist analyses argue that Yao Wenyuan's November 1965 critique in Wenhui Bao functioned less as a genuine ideological rebuttal and more as a manufactured pretext for Mao to consolidate power against perceived rivals within the party apparatus, such as Beijing Mayor Peng Zhen, amid broader frustrations over post-Great Leap Forward recovery policies. Scholars like Yan Jiaqi have documented how Peng Zhen attempted to confine the dispute to academic grounds, but Mao overrode this to frame it politically, revealing the episode's utility in signaling purges rather than resolving historiographical debates. This perspective posits that the Cultural Revolution's origins lay in Mao's preexisting strategic calculations—evident in his private comments by mid-1965 on needing a movement against "revisionism"—rather than the play itself as a spontaneous trigger, with the critique's delayed publication (despite drafting in spring 1965) underscoring opportunistic timing.[8] Western scholarship, including Clive Ansley's 1982 monograph The Heresy of Wu Han, offers a critical reevaluation portraying the attacks on the play as disproportionate to its content, which aligned with Wu Han's long-standing Marxist historiography blending Confucian ethics with socialist principles, and not evidence of "bourgeois" heresy as alleged. Ansley analyzes the play's script to argue it critiqued landlord abuses akin to class struggle, not emperor infallibility, and attributes the escalation to intra-party factionalism rather than textual flaws, cautioning against accepting Gang of Four-era narratives uncritically due to their alignment with Mao's cult of personality. Post-Mao PRC historiography, while condemning the Cultural Revolution overall since the 1981 Resolution, has partially rehabilitated figures like Wu Han by emphasizing policy errors over personal villainy, though state-controlled sources maintain the play's "feudal" elements to avoid broader scrutiny of Mao's role. These readings highlight systemic biases in official accounts, where academic works were retroactively politicized to justify mass campaigns, privileging causal evidence from archival timelines over propagandistic claims.[36]
