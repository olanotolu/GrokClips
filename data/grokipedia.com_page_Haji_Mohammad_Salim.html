Haji Mohammad Salim

Haji Mohammad Salim (born c. 1958) is a Bangladeshi businessman and former Awami League politician who served as a Member of Jatiya Sangsad representing the Dhaka-8 constituency from 1996 to 2001 and Dhaka-7 from 2014 onward, prior to his status as an ex-MP following the 2024 political upheaval.[1][2] As Managing Director of the Madina Group, he oversees operations in international trade across multiple countries including India, the United States, China, and Saudi Arabia.[1] Salim has engaged in philanthropy by founding institutions such as Haji Salim Oxford English School, Haji Salim University College, and an orphanage, while also establishing or repairing over 100 mosques, madrassas, and similar facilities; he has planned a hospital in honor of his father and a women's training center named after his mother.[1] Earlier in his career, he was elected as a commissioner of Dhaka City Corporation in 1994 and acted as mayor in 1999.[1] His public profile has been marked by legal controversies, including a conviction carrying a 10-year sentence in a corruption case prosecuted by the Anti-Corruption Commission, after which he fled to Thailand before returning and receiving bail in 2023.[2] In September 2024, he was arrested by Dhaka Metropolitan Police detectives, and by November 2024, shown arrested in an attempted murder case stemming from an incident during the July uprising.[3][4] Additional allegations involve land encroachment on property belonging to a school for the deaf, despite adverse court rulings.[5]

Personal background

Early life and family

Haji Mohammad Salim was born in 1958 in Dhaka, East Pakistan (present-day Bangladesh).[6] He was raised in a respectable Muslim family known for its longstanding business reputation in the city.[1] His father, the late Haji Chand Miah Shardar, was a notable figure, with a modern hospital planned in his honor, while his mother was Saleha Begum, after whom a women's training and rehabilitation center has been named.[1] Salim had at least one elder brother, Haji Kayes, who died in July 2022 at the age of 72 after suffering from age-related complications.[7] Salim's formal education extended only up to the ninth grade.[6] He was married to Gulshan Ara Begum, who died in 2010 at age 50.[8] The couple had two sons: Mohammad Erfan Salim, who has faced legal issues including a conviction for illegal possession of firearms, and Solaiman Salim, implicated in separate criminal cases.[9][10][11]

Education and initial influences

Salim was born into a respectable Muslim family in Dhaka City, known for its longstanding business reputation.[1] He is the son of the late Haji Chand Miah Shardar and Saleha Begum, and grew up in a commerce-oriented household that instilled entrepreneurial values from an early age.[1] Specific details about his formal education, such as schooling or higher studies, are not documented in available biographical accounts from his business affiliations. His initial influences appear rooted in familial traditions of trade and community involvement in Old Dhaka, fostering a practical orientation toward business rather than academic pursuits.[1]

Business career

Leadership of Madina Group

Haji Mohammad Salim serves as the managing director of Madina Group, a diversified Bangladeshi conglomerate initially established in 1978 as Madina Trading Corporation for marketing imported cement from countries including Indonesia, China, and South Korea.[12] Under his leadership, the group incorporated as a private limited company in 1987 and expanded through diversification into manufacturing sectors such as polymers, cement production under the Tiger brand, maritime activities, trading, and logistics.[12][13] The company's operations, directed by Salim, emphasize research, modernization, and efficient management, resulting in a reported annual turnover of approximately 600 crore taka by 1999 and contributions exceeding 100 crore taka yearly to the national exchequer through taxes and duties.[12] Salim has overseen the development of international business partnerships with entities in India, the United States, Australia, Indonesia, China, Malaysia, Hong Kong, Singapore, Bhutan, Vietnam, Dubai, and Saudi Arabia, facilitating import-export activities and technology adoption.[1] Family members hold key positions in the leadership structure, including Mohammad Solaiman Salim as deputy managing director and Md. Erfan Selim and Zarin Chowdhury Selim as directors, supporting operational oversight across subsidiaries like Madina Polymer, established in 1998 as a major plastic manufacturer.[14] Salim's tenure has prioritized environment-friendly technologies and quality production, with the group claiming to generate thousands of employment opportunities in Bangladesh's private sector.[1] These efforts align with the conglomerate's stated values of honesty, integrity, and national contribution, though the company's self-reported metrics reflect promotional perspectives from its official channels.[12]

Expansion and economic role

Under Haji Mohammad Salim's leadership as managing director, Madina Group diversified from its origins in trading commodities into manufacturing sectors, including polymers established in 1998 through MTC Polycon Industries Ltd., which later became Madina Polymer Industries Limited.[13] The group, initially founded in 1946 by Haji Muhammad Amin and reorganized after his death under the guidance of his four sons, expanded operations starting from Madina Trading Corporation in 1978 to encompass jute processing, cement production via ventures like Three Rings Cement, and import-export activities in fruits and rice.[15] [12] This growth involved establishing production facilities focused on infrastructure-related products, such as plastics for industrial use, leveraging international business connections in countries including India, China, the United States, and Saudi Arabia for sourcing and markets.[1] The expansion contributed to Bangladesh's private sector by generating thousands of direct employment opportunities, primarily in manufacturing and trading hubs around Dhaka, while emphasizing environment-friendly technologies in polymer and jute processing to support national infrastructural projects.[1] Madina Group's output, including cement and polymer products, played a role in domestic construction and export-oriented industries, aligning with broader economic development goals through quality-assured goods that the company claims bolster local supply chains.[1] However, reports from investigative sources have questioned the sustainability of this growth, attributing rapid diversification partly to funds accrued during Salim's parliamentary tenure, amid allegations of illicit practices rather than purely market-driven expansion.[5] These claims, while unproven in civil courts until related corruption convictions, highlight potential over-reliance on political influence for land acquisition and business scaling in a context of weak regulatory enforcement in Bangladesh's private sector.[16] Economically, the group's activities supported import substitution in polymers and jute goods, reducing reliance on foreign inputs for textiles and packaging, though its overall impact remains modest compared to larger conglomerates, with no publicly verified revenue figures exceeding routine trading volumes.[17] Self-reported contributions include aiding rural rice depots and urban development projects, yet independent assessments note that such private entities often amplify inequality by concentrating wealth in family-controlled firms without proportional tax transparency or broad-based job quality improvements.[1]

Political career

Entry into Awami League

Haji Mohammad Salim's initial involvement with the Awami League occurred in the lead-up to the February 1996 general elections in Bangladesh, when he secured the party's nomination for the Dhaka-8 constituency.[18] Running on the Awami League ticket, Salim defeated competitors to win the seat, entering the Jatiya Sangsad as a ruling party lawmaker during Sheikh Hasina's first term as prime minister.[19] This victory represented his formal entry into organized politics, leveraging his background in local business networks in Old Dhaka to build grassroots support within the party's structure.[20] Following his 1996 success, Salim reaffirmed his Awami League affiliation by contesting the 2001 elections as the party's candidate from the same constituency, though he lost to a Bangladesh Nationalist Party rival amid a national swing against the incumbent government.[18] His alignment with the Awami League persisted through subsequent cycles, including implicit party backing during his independent run in 2014 and explicit nomination in 2018, underscoring a consistent, albeit occasionally opportunistic, commitment to the party's platform on issues like urban development and constituency patronage.[21]

Electoral history and parliamentary roles

Haji Mohammad Salim was first elected to the Jatiya Sangsad in the June 1996 general election from the Dhaka-8 constituency as an Awami League candidate, having switched from the Bangladesh Nationalist Party after it denied him nomination for that cycle.[22][18] He lost the same seat in the October 2001 election to BNP candidate Nasiruddin Ahmed Pintu.[23][24] Salim returned to parliament in the January 2014 general election, winning the Dhaka-7 constituency as an independent candidate with backing from Awami League leader Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina.[21][25] He secured re-election from Dhaka-7 in the December 2018 election on the Awami League ticket, serving through the 12th Jatiya Sangsad term until its dissolution following the August 2024 uprising.[26] During these terms, he functioned as a backbench member of the treasury bench without documented assignment to standing committees or leadership positions in the house. In the January 2024 election, Salim did not contest; his son, Mohammad Solaiman Salim, won the Dhaka-7 seat on the Awami League nomination while Salim, as the incumbent, publicly supported the campaign.[27] Salim's parliamentary tenure thus spanned three nonconsecutive terms across two constituencies, primarily aligned with Awami League interests despite the 2014 independent run.

Key legislative activities and positions

Haji Mohammad Salim served as a Jatiya Sangsad member for the Dhaka-7 constituency under the Bangladesh Awami League banner in the 12th National Parliament, which convened from 3 February 2019 until its dissolution amid the 2024 uprising on 6 August 2024.[26] As a ruling party legislator, his activities centered on backing Awami League-backed measures, though no private member's bills or lead sponsorships are documented in official records. In a 2015 parliamentary address, he criticized opposition-led blockades and hartals, accusing them of effectively "placing 160 million Bengalis in handcuffs" by disrupting economic activity and public life.[28] This reflected his alignment with government stances against political disruptions that impeded development projects and stability. Salim's positions emphasized constituency-level advocacy for urban infrastructure in Old Dhaka, consistent with Awami League priorities on economic expansion, but lacked prominent national policy interventions.

Legal issues

2007 corruption case and initial conviction

In October 2007, during Bangladesh's military-backed caretaker government, the Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) filed a graft case against Haji Mohammad Salim and his wife, Gulshan Ara, at Lalbagh Police Station in Dhaka, alleging they had amassed wealth and properties beyond their known sources of income from 1991 to 2007.[29][30] The charges centered on unlawful and dishonest acquisition of assets disproportionate to declared earnings, with the ACC claiming violations under relevant anti-corruption laws.[31][32] The case proceeded to trial in the Dhaka Special Judge's Court, where evidence was presented on the couple's alleged illegal wealth accumulation, including properties obtained without legitimate financial backing.[33] On April 27, 2008, the court convicted Salim on two counts: 10 years' rigorous imprisonment for possessing assets from undisclosed sources and an additional 3 years for related graft offenses, alongside a fine of Tk 2 million (Tk 20 lakh).[34][35][36] Salim challenged the verdict by appealing to the High Court Division of the Supreme Court, which granted him bail pending appeal, allowing him to avoid immediate incarceration.[31][34]

Appeals, imprisonment, and release

Following the High Court Division's verdict on April 28, 2022, upholding a lower court's imposition of 10 years' rigorous imprisonment and a fine of 1 million taka against Salim for amassing wealth beyond known sources of income, he filed an appeal with the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court on May 24, 2022, seeking acquittal.[31][37] After returning from medical treatment abroad, Salim surrendered to a Dhaka court on May 22, 2022, and was remanded to jail custody to serve the sentence.[33][38] Initial bail applications were denied, including by a Supreme Court chamber judge on June 6, 2022, amid ongoing hearings on his leave-to-appeal petition, with further dates set for August 1 and October 23, 2022.[39][40][41] On December 6, 2022, a five-member bench of the Appellate Division, headed by Chief Justice Hasan Foez Siddique, granted interim bail, permitting Salim to remain free pending the appeal's resolution while directing the Anti-Corruption Commission to respond within four weeks.[31][42] He was released from Dhaka Central Jail on January 17, 2023, after approximately 10 months of incarceration, following completion of bail formalities.[32][43]

Immediate post-release legal maneuvers

Following his release on bail from Bangabandhu Sheikh Mujib Medical University hospital on January 17, 2023, Haji Mohammad Salim persisted in challenging his corruption conviction via a pending appeal in the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court, which sought to overturn the High Court's March 9, 2021, ruling upholding a 10-year imprisonment term for amassing illegal wealth.[44][45] The Supreme Court had granted interim bail on December 6, 2022â€”after rejecting an earlier plea in August 2022â€”explicitly pending resolution of this leave-to-appeal petition, allowing Salim to remain free while the Anti-Corruption Commission's opposition to quashing the verdict was addressed.[31][42] No additional filings or motions were reported in the immediate aftermath, with Salim's legal strategy centered on arguing procedural irregularities from the original 2008 trial and the 2011 High Court acquittal that was later reversed on appeal by the ACC in 2015.[32] This approach delayed enforcement of the sentence, as the Appellate Division had not disposed of the case by early 2023, effectively suspending further incarceration amid ongoing hearings. The conviction stemmed from charges under the Penal Code for concealing assets worth approximately Tk 20 lakh beyond declared income, with the bail conditions prohibiting Salim from leaving the country without court permission.[44]

Post-2024 arrest and investigations

Context of 2024 uprising and regime fall

The 2024 Bangladesh uprising originated from student-led protests against the reinstatement of a controversial quota system for government jobs, which reserved 30% of positions for descendants of 1971 Liberation War veterans. In June 2024, Bangladesh's High Court reversed a 2018 decision scrapping the quota, reigniting grievances over perceived favoritism toward Awami League loyalists and limiting merit-based opportunities amid high youth unemployment. Protests began on July 1, 2024, organized by student groups like the Anti-Discrimination Students' Movement, initially demanding quota reform but quickly expanding to criticize Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina's government for corruption, authoritarianism, and electoral manipulation.[46][47] Escalation intensified in mid-July when security forces responded with lethal force, including live ammunition and "shoot-on-sight" orders, leading to widespread violence. By July 16, protests had spread nationwide, with demonstrators clashing with police and Awami League-affiliated groups like the Chhatra League; the government imposed an internet blackout starting July 18, exacerbating chaos and hindering information flow. Official figures reported over 200 deaths by late July, though human rights monitors estimated higher tolls from extrajudicial killings and mob violence, framing the crackdown as emblematic of Hasina's 15-year rule marked by suppressed opposition, rigged electionsâ€”such as the January 2024 poll boycotted by the BNP with under 40% turnoutâ€”and erosion of democratic institutions.[48][49] The movement culminated in Hasina's ouster on August 5, 2024, after protesters defied curfews, stormed key sites including her official residence Ganabhaban, and issued non-cooperation ultimatums. Facing mass defections from security forces and allies, Hasina resigned around 2:25 pm and fled by helicopter to India with her sister, ending Awami League's dominance. An interim government headed by Nobel laureate Muhammad Yunus was swiftly appointed, tasked with reforms amid vows to prosecute regime abuses, setting the stage for investigations into figures like Awami League parliamentarians accused of complicity in the violence and prior graft. The uprising, dubbed the "Monsoon Revolution," highlighted deep-seated public resentment against entrenched power, with over 1,000 deaths and thousands injured overall, though post-fall reprisals against Awami League members underscored risks of transitional instability.[46][47][49]

Arrests and remand in murder and grabbing cases

Haji Mohammad Salim was arrested on September 2, 2024, by the Detective Branch of the Dhaka Metropolitan Police from the Bangshal area of Old Dhaka in connection with the murder of Khalid Hasan Saifullah, a student at Ideal College killed during the July-August 2024 uprising against the Awami League government.[50][51] On the same day, a Dhaka court granted police a five-day remand to interrogate Salim, who was named in the first information report filed at Lalbagh Police Station.[51] Following the remand period, he was sent to jail on September 4, 2024.[50] While in custody, Salim was shown arrested in additional murder-related cases. On November 4, 2024, detectives presented him in court for a case of attempted murder filed against him for allegedly trying to kill Fazlur Karim in the Bangshal area.[4] In another instance, on an unspecified date in late 2024 or early 2025, he was implicated alongside two others in the murder of Rakib during the uprising, with court records reflecting his arrest status in the case.[52] Most recently, on October 20, 2025, a Dhaka court placed Salim and his son Solaiman Salim on a four-day remand in the murder case of jhut trader Monir Uddin, filed on March 14, 2025, at Shahbagh Police Station over the killing during the July uprising; Salim was listed as accused number 8 and Solaiman as number 11 in the charge sheet naming 351 identified and 500 unidentified suspects.[53][54] Regarding grabbing cases, Salim was shown arrested on October 13, 2025, in a case filed on August 28, 2025, by Monirul Islam at a Dhaka police station, accusing him and 13 others of illegally occupying and vandalizing the Deen & Sons business establishment in 2020.[55][56] The court took cognizance of the charges prior to formalizing his arrest status in the matter, amid his ongoing detention from prior cases.[56] These proceedings occurred as part of broader investigations into alleged abuses during his tenure as Awami League lawmaker for Dhaka-7.

Ongoing proceedings as of 2025

As of October 2025, Haji Mohammad Salim remains in custody, with proceedings ongoing in the murder case of fabric waste trader Mohammad Monir, who was killed on July 19, 2024, during the student-led uprising against the Awami League government. On October 20, 2025, Dhaka Chief Metropolitan Magistrate GM Farhad Ishtiak approved a four-day police remand for Salim and his son Solaiman Salim to facilitate interrogation by Shahbagh Police, despite a prosecution request for ten days; the duo appeared subdued in court during the hearing.[11][54] The case, filed on March 14, 2025, by Monir's widow Rojina Akter at Shahbagh Police Station, names 351 individualsâ€”including Salim and Solaimanâ€”along with around 500 unnamed accused, alleging their role in the trader's death amid clashes.[53][57] No chargesheet has been submitted as of late October 2025, indicating continued investigative phases.[58] In parallel, Salim was formally shown arrested on October 13, 2025, in a separate case accusing him of illegally seizing a business establishment in 2020, filed on August 28, 2025, by victim Monirul Islam against 14 suspects including Salim.[56][59] This land-grabbing allegation, investigated by Motijheel Police, remains at the remand and evidence-gathering stage, with no reported trial commencement. Salim, who has been detained since his September 1, 2024, arrest following the regime's collapse, faces these charges without successful bail applications in the referenced matters.[25][19] Further remands or hearings could extend into late 2025, pending police submissions to the court.

Controversies and public perception

Allegations of power abuse and cronyism

Haji Mohammad Salim, a former Awami League lawmaker from Dhaka-7, has faced allegations of leveraging his political position to establish dominance over Old Dhaka through family-controlled organizations and networks. Reports indicate that Salim and his son, Erfan Salim, operated at least 10 such entities, which purportedly promoted community welfare but were accused of serving as tools for territorial control, intimidation, and extortion in areas like Chawkbazar.[60] These groups allegedly utilized surveillance equipment, including walkie-talkies seized during Erfan's 2020 arrest, to monitor and enforce influence over local businesses and residents.[61] Critics have pointed to instances of nepotism and favoritism, where Salim's parliamentary influence shielded family members from accountability for violent acts. For example, Erfan, a Dhaka South City Corporation councillor, was implicated in multiple assaults, including the 2020 beating of a Navy officer, Lieutenant Wasif Ahmed Khan, for which charges were framed against him and associates; Salim reportedly condoned such behavior initially, reflecting a pattern of dynastic protection.[62] [63] Similar claims emerged regarding interference in public duties, such as the 2022 suspension of a railway ticket examiner who enforced rules against Erfan during travel, highlighting alleged misuse of authority to prioritize family privileges.[64] Land acquisition disputes further fueled cronyism accusations, with Salim and associates charged in 2020 with illegally seizing property in Moulvibazar, Old Dhaka, previously under Agrani Bank control; this followed prior evictions and involved claims of political muscle overriding legal ownership.[65] Post-2024 regime change, investigations linked these patterns to broader abuse, including a 2025 remand order citing power misuse in connection with a trader's murder during unrest, alongside money laundering to foreign venturesâ€”allegedly facilitated by Awami League patronage networks that rewarded loyalty with impunity.[66] Such claims, often amplified in opposition-leaning outlets after Sheikh Hasina's ouster, underscore systemic concerns over elite capture in Bangladeshi politics, though Salim's defenders have dismissed them as politically motivated without substantiated judicial findings beyond initial charges.[67]

Viewpoints from supporters and critics

Supporters of Haji Mohammad Salim, largely aligned with the Awami League, portray his 2024 arrests in murder and land encroachment cases as manifestations of political vendetta orchestrated by the interim government following Sheikh Hasina's ouster on August 5, 2024.[68][69] They assert that these charges, including alleged involvement in suppressing July 2024 quota protests, represent a "witch hunt" against party loyalists, with fabricated evidence and selective prosecutions aimed at dismantling the Awami League rather than addressing genuine crimes.[70] Awami League spokesperson Mohammad Arafat has characterized similar International Crimes Tribunal processes against former officials as a "complete sham," implying judicial bias under the Yunus administration.[71] Critics, encompassing opposition figures from the Bangladesh Nationalist Party (BNP), student activists from the 2024 uprising, and investigative reports from outlets like The Daily Star and Prothom Alo, depict Salim as emblematic of Awami League-era cronyism and impunity. They cite his 2007 Anti-Corruption Commission conviction for amassing Tk 2.5 crore beyond known income sourcesâ€”upheld by the Supreme Court in 2022 with a 10-year sentenceâ€”as irrefutable evidence of graft, noting his repeated bail grants and overseas trips (e.g., to Thailand in May 2022 despite conviction) as undue privileges unavailable to opposition convicts like Khaleda Zia.[31][72] Additional allegations include land grabbing, such as the 2019 seizure of property allocated to a deaf community school in Old Dhaka, which local authorities reclaimed multiple times before his parliamentary influence allegedly stalled enforcement.[5] Post-uprising cases, including the September 2024 remand for a student's murder during protest crackdowns and November 2024 charges in an attempted killing, are framed by detractors as accountability for wielding party muscle in violent reprisals, with victim testimonies underscoring patterns of extortion and intimidation under his watch as Dhaka-7 MP from 2009 to 2024.[73][74] These polarized narratives reflect broader divisions in Bangladesh's post-Hasina landscape, where Awami League-affiliated sources emphasize interim government overreachâ€”potentially overlooking prior judicial findingsâ€”while critical voices in domestic media, often highlighting Awami League's 15-year rule's authoritarian tendencies, prioritize empirical case records amid claims of selective enforcement.[24]

Impact on Awami League's image

Haji Mohammad Salim's corruption conviction and subsequent legal privileges under Awami League governance exemplified perceptions of impunity for party affiliates, undermining the party's claims of effective anti-corruption measures. Convicted in 2008 for illegally amassing wealth through graft, with the High Court upholding a 10-year sentence in March 2021, Salim retained his parliamentary seat and obtained bail, allowing him to contest elections and evade immediate imprisonment.[16] This outcome drew sharp rebukes from opposition figures, who highlighted the disparity in judicial treatment compared to imprisoned rivals like BNP chairperson Khaleda Zia, fostering accusations of selective enforcement and cronyism within the Awami League. Salim's overseas travels despite his conviction intensified public and media backlash, portraying the Awami League as tolerant of elite corruption. In April 2022, he departed for Bangkok for purported medical treatment shortly after the upheld verdict, prompting "huge criticism" for evading accountability while ordinary citizens faced stricter oversight.[75] Similar trips to India further fueled narratives of preferential access to resources and legal leniency, with national media decrying the government's handling as evidence of systemic favoritism toward loyalists.[76] These incidents eroded trust in the party's governance, as they contradicted Awami League rhetoric on rooting out corruption, instead reinforcing views of a patronage network that shielded influential members from full legal repercussions.[77] The fallout from Salim's cases contributed to the Awami League's broader reputational decline, amplifying grievances that culminated in the 2024 mass uprising. Post-regime change arrests in September 2024, including charges of land grabbing and extortion tied to his Awami League tenure, exposed alleged abuses accumulated over 15 years of rule, validating long-standing criticisms of institutional capture.[78] Analysts and opposition voices cited such high-profile impunity as a causal factor in public disillusionment, portraying the party as emblematic of elite enrichment at public expense, which hastened its ouster and ongoing scrutiny of its legacy.[25]
