Bareilly Sharif Dargah

Bareilly Sharif Dargah, also known as Dargah-e-Ala Hazrat, is a major Sufi shrine complex in Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh, India, centered on the mausoleum of Ahmad Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), a prolific Islamic scholar, jurist, and theologian revered for reviving traditional Sunni Sufi practices.[1] The site houses the tombs of Khan and several family members, including his successors, and functions as the headquarters for the Barelvi movement, a South Asian Sunni sect that emphasizes veneration of the Prophet Muhammad, shrine-based devotion, and opposition to puritanical reformist ideologies like those of the Deobandi school.[1][2] Khan authored over 1,000 works, including the extensive Fatawa-e-Razviyya compilation of legal opinions defending orthodox beliefs against perceived innovations, and established institutions like Madrasa Manzar-e-Islam to propagate his teachings.[3] The dargah attracts millions during the annual Urs commemoration of Khan's death in Safar, featuring prayers, qawwali music, and scholarly gatherings that underscore its role as a bastion of folk Islam amid theological rivalries in the region.[1][4]

Historical Background

Origins and Founding

The Bareilly Sharif Dargah originated as the mausoleum of Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi, an Islamic scholar, jurist, and Sufi leader who resided in Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh, for much of his life. Born on 14 June 1856 in the city's Jasoli neighborhood to a family of religious scholars tracing descent to Pathan origins, Khan emerged as a defender of traditional Sunni Sufi doctrines against reformist challenges. His death on 28 October 1921 at age 65 marked the immediate catalyst for the site's establishment as a shrine, with his burial occurring locally and a tomb constructed over the grave to honor his legacy.[5][6] The mausoleum's founding in 1921 reflected the rapid veneration by Khan's disciples, who viewed him as a mujaddid (renewer) of Islam and a pivotal figure in countering movements like Deobandism and Wahhabism through prolific writings in fiqh, theology, and poetry. Initial construction focused on the central tomb, incorporating elements symbolic of Qadiri Sufi affiliation, with the dome reportedly conceptualized through innovative means by associates like Shah Mehmood Jaan Qadri. This core structure laid the foundation for the dargah as a hub for Barelvi adherents, emphasizing saintly intercession and devotional practices rooted in Khan's teachings.[7][1] Though basic at inception, the site's origins underscore its role as an organic extension of Khan's scholarly and spiritual influence in colonial India, where Bareilly served as a center for his madrasa, Manzar-e-Islam, established in 1904. The dargah's development was driven by familial and communal efforts rather than state patronage, preserving its independence amid inter-sectarian tensions.[5]

Life and Contributions of Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi

Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi was born on 14 June 1856 in Bareilly, North-Western Provinces, British India (now Uttar Pradesh, India), to Naqi Ali Khan, a Hanafi scholar and Sufi of the Qadiri order.[5] [4] His family traced its lineage to the Prophet Muhammad through Hasan ibn Ali, and his father provided initial religious instruction in Hanafi jurisprudence, Hadith, and Sufism.[8] Khan demonstrated prodigious talent from childhood, memorizing the Quran by age five and mastering advanced texts in Arabic grammar, logic, and Islamic sciences by his early teens under limited formal tutelage, primarily from his father and select local scholars.[5] By age 13, he began issuing independent legal opinions (fatwas) and teaching, establishing himself as a polymath proficient in over 50 fields, including mathematics, astronomy, and poetry.[9] He received ijazah (authorization) in multiple Sufi orders, notably Qadiri, and emphasized mystical devotion while adhering to orthodox Sunni doctrine.[8] His primary contributions centered on defending traditional South Asian Sunni practices against reformist challenges from Deobandi scholars, Wahhabis, and Ahmadiyya proponents, whom he deemed innovators (mubtadi) diverging from the Prophet's path.[10] In 1906, during a pilgrimage to Mecca and Medina, he compiled Husam al-Haramayn (Sword of the Two Sanctuaries), a fatwa collection denouncing over 20 figures—including Deobandi leaders—for alleged blasphemies against the Prophet, which garnered endorsements from Hijazi scholars and catalyzed a distinct Ahl al-Sunnat wa'l-Jama'at identity.[10] This work, alongside his establishment of the Manzar-e-Islam seminary in Bareilly in 1904, laid the groundwork for the Barelvi movement, prioritizing veneration of the Prophet, saintly intercession, and celebratory rituals like the Prophet's birthday (mawlid).[8] Khan authored over 1,000 works in Arabic, Persian, and Urdu, spanning jurisprudence, theology, poetry, and sciences; his magnum opus, Fatawa Razawiyya, comprises 30 volumes of Hanafi legal rulings integrating Sufi perspectives on issues like tawassul (intercession) and visiting graves.[11] [12] He also contributed to astronomy, critiquing modern heliocentrism while reconciling Islamic cosmology with empirical observations in treatises like those on lunar visibility.[9] These efforts preserved folk-Sufi expressions of Islam amid colonial-era reformism, influencing millions of South Asian Sunnis.[8] Khan died on 28 October 1921 in Bareilly at age 65, after which his scholarly lineage and dargah perpetuated his teachings.[5] His fatwas and writings continue to shape Barelvi jurisprudence, though critics from reformist schools contest their doctrinal rigidity.[10]

Expansion Under Successors

Following the death of Ahmed Raza Khan in 1921, his eldest son Hamid Raza Khan (1875–1943), appointed as the first Sajjada Nasheen (custodian) of the dargah, oversaw initial post-founding developments to support the growing influx of pilgrims and institutional needs. In 1927 (1345 AH), he laid the foundation stone for Khanqah Qadiria Nooria Razvia in Bareilly Sharif, with construction commencing in 1938 (1357 AH), expanding the complex's spiritual and educational infrastructure.[13] [14] These efforts included integrating affiliated madrasas originally established by Ahmed Raza Khan, enhancing the site's role as a center for Barelvi scholarship.[14] The mausoleum's iconic dome, modeled after that of Masjid-e-Nabawi with a distinctive black-and-white aesthetic, was designed by Shah Mehmood Jaan Qadri, a khalifa (spiritual deputy) of Ahmed Raza Khan, who donated ₹10,000 and supervised its completion; the design received endorsement from Ahmed Raza Khan and contemporary ulama prior to his death, though final construction occurred under Hamid Raza's custodianship.[15] Hamid Raza's tenure also facilitated the burial of family members within the complex, laying groundwork for its evolution into a multi-mausoleum site.[14] Hamid Raza's younger brother, Mustafa Raza Khan (1892–1981), succeeded as the second custodian and Grand Mufti of India, further expanding the dargah through additional structures and facilities to accommodate surging pilgrimage numbers, including enhanced madrasa networks and administrative expansions for issuing fatwas on contemporary issues.[14] Mustafa's 50-year scholarly influence solidified the site's prominence, with subsequent leaders like grandson Akhtar Raza Khan (1943–2018) maintaining these developments amid millions of annual visitors.[14] By the late 20th century, the complex encompassed multiple mausolea for Raza Khan family members, reflecting sustained growth tied to the Barelvi movement's expansion.[14]

Architectural Features

Mausoleum Design and Construction

The mausoleum housing the tomb of Ahmed Raza Khan was erected in Bareilly following his death on 28 October 1921, serving as the central structure of the Bareilly Sharif Dargah complex.[7] The design of its prominent dome, known as the Gumbad, was crafted by Shah Mehmood Jaan Qadri, a devoted disciple and khalifa of Ahmed Raza Khan, who sketched the blueprint using burnt matchsticks on plain paper due to the absence of drawing tools.[15] This innovative method produced a refined aesthetic inspired by the dome of Masjid-e-Nabawi in Medina, incorporating a distinctive black-and-white color scheme that evoked spiritual symbolism.[15] The design received approval from Ahmed Raza Khan himself prior to his passing, along with endorsements from several eminent Islamic scholars, ensuring its alignment with traditional Sunni architectural principles.[15] Shah Mehmood Jaan Qadri not only authored the plans but also personally funded the initial construction with a donation of ₹10,000 and oversaw the building process until its completion, remaining in Bareilly to supervise the work.[15] While exact completion dates remain undocumented in available records, the structure's erection postdated 1921, reflecting a blend of modern construction techniques with Islamic stylistic elements such as marble arches, Arabic inscriptions on the walls, and intricate carvings.[16] The mausoleum's architecture emphasizes functionality and reverence, featuring an interior finished in warm orange tones with surrounding arches inscribed in Urdu script, designed to accommodate pilgrims while honoring the theological legacy of its occupant.[17] These elements underscore a deliberate fusion of devotional intent and practical engineering, prioritizing durability and aesthetic appeal in a style resonant with Sufi shrine traditions across South Asia.[18]

Dome and Symbolic Elements

The central dome, or gumbad, of the Bareilly Sharif Dargah mausoleum represents a key architectural element constructed over the tomb of Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi. Designed by Shah Mehmood Jaan Qadri, a prominent disciple and khalifa of Khan, the dome's model was ingeniously crafted using burnt matchsticks arranged on plain paper to outline its form and details.[15][7] Qadri personally supervised the construction and funded it with a donation of ₹10,000, ensuring completion before departing Bareilly.[15] The design draws direct inspiration from the dome of Masjid-e-Nabawi in Medina, incorporating a refined black-and-white aesthetic that mirrors its prototype.[15] This stylistic choice was endorsed by Ahmed Raza Khan and other ulama, emphasizing fidelity to prophetic architectural traditions within Sunni devotional practices.[15] The dome's form contributes to the shrine's recognition as a distinctive Islamic landmark in India, blending modern construction with symbolic reverence.[15] Symbolically, the dome evokes the sacred beauty and spiritual elevation associated with the Prophet's mosque, serving as a visual and devotional link to core Islamic heritage.[15] It embodies Ahmed Raza Khan's legacy of intellectual and mystical pursuits, functioning as a perpetual emblem of Barelvi Sufi devotion and doctrinal continuity.[7] While incorporating geometric patterns common in Indo-Islamic mausolea for aesthetic and theological depth—representing unity and divine order—the specific motifs on this dome align with broader traditions of evoking heavenly vaults without figurative iconography.[19]

Surrounding Complex and Additions

The surrounding complex of Bareilly Sharif Dargah extends beyond the central mausoleum to encompass facilities supporting spiritual, educational, and communal functions central to the Barelvi tradition. Key structures include the Khanqah-e-Alia Razvia, a traditional Sufi hospice serving as a residence and center for devotional activities, managed by descendants of Ahmed Raza Khan such as Maulana Subhan Raza Khan.[20] Educational institutions form a prominent part of the additions, with Madrasa Manzar-e-Islam operational for 111 years, providing comprehensive Islamic education to hundreds of students annually, complete with lodging, meals, and a structured syllabus emphasizing Sunni doctrines.[21][22] Adjacent is Jamia Razvia Manzar-e-Islam, which specializes in advanced studies of Ahle Sunnat wal Jamaat principles under the oversight of the same lineage.[20] Further expansions include mosques integrated for congregational prayers, such as the Hamidi Masjid, whose unique design was initiated by the All India Raza Committee and was under construction as of 2020 to accommodate growing pilgrim numbers.[23] The broader Markaz-e-Ahle Sunnat framework within the complex facilitates ongoing religious services, pilgrim welfare, and propagation of Barelvi teachings, reflecting incremental developments tied to the movement's expansion since the early 20th century.[24]

Religious and Theological Role

Centrality to the Barelvi Movement

The Bareilly Sharif Dargah, serving as the mausoleum of Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), functions as the primary spiritual and organizational center for the Barelvi movement, also known as Ahl-e-Sunnat wa Jama'at.[25] Khan, originating from Bareilly, Uttar Pradesh, established the movement's foundational doctrines through extensive writings that defended traditional Sufi practices, veneration of the Prophet Muhammad, and saintly intercession against emerging reformist ideologies in late 19th-century British India.[26] The dargah, constructed following Khan's death on October 28, 1921, embodies these principles as a pilgrimage site where adherents seek barakah (blessings) and reaffirm loyalty to the movement's theological stance.[7] Markaz-e-Ahle Sunnat, headquartered at the dargah complex, coordinates the movement's activities, including religious education, publication of fatwas, and propagation of Hanafi-Sufi jurisprudence across South Asia.[14] Successive leaders from Khan's lineage, such as his descendants who hold positions as khalifas, administer the site and issue guidance on doctrinal matters, maintaining continuity in opposition to Deobandi and Wahhabi influences.[27] The dargah's role extends to fostering unity among Barelvi followers, estimated to number tens of millions in India and Pakistan, by serving as a nexus for scholarly discourse and communal rituals that emphasize tawassul (intercession) and milad (Prophet's birthday celebrations).[15] Annual observances, particularly the Urs-e-Razvi commemorating Khan's death anniversary in Safar, draw hundreds of thousands of devotees to Bareilly Sharif, underscoring its centrality in sustaining the movement's devotional ethos.[4] These gatherings feature qawwali performances, scholarly lectures, and collective prayers, reinforcing the Barelvi emphasis on love for the Ahl al-Bayt and awliya (saints) as integral to Sunni orthodoxy.[14] Through such practices, the dargah not only preserves Khan's legacy but also adapts to contemporary challenges, including digital outreach via associated institutions, ensuring the movement's resilience amid sectarian tensions.[15]

Promotion of Sufi Practices and Doctrines

The Bareilly Sharif Dargah functions as a central hub for advancing Sufi doctrines within the Barelvi tradition, emphasizing mystical devotion, intercession through prophets and saints, and adherence to established Sufi orders. Founded around the legacy of Ahmed Raza Khan (1856–1921), who integrated Sufi elements into Hanafi Sunni orthodoxy, the site promotes practices such as tawassul (seeking mediation via spiritual intermediaries) and the veneration of awliya (saints) as compatible with core Islamic tenets. Khan's extensive fatwas, compiled in works like Fatawa-e Razawiyya (published in 12 volumes from the 1950s to 1980s), systematically defended these against reformist challenges, positioning the Dargah as a doctrinal fortress for Ahl-e Sunnat wal Jamaat.[8][4] Key doctrines propagated include the permissibility of istighatha (direct supplication for aid from the Prophet or saints in times of need) and milad-un-Nabi (annual commemoration of the Prophet Muhammad's birth), which Khan justified through scriptural exegesis and historical precedent in Al-Dawlat al-Makkiyya (1906). These teachings underscore the Prophet's ongoing spiritual presence and the role of saints in facilitating divine proximity, drawing from Ash'ari and Maturidi theological frameworks. The Dargah reinforces such beliefs via public sermons and publications, attracting adherents who view them as preservations of pre-modern Islamic spirituality against puritanical critiques.[8][14] Sufi practices are further institutionalized through affiliation with tariqas (orders), particularly the Qadiri lineage, into which Khan was initiated in 1877 under Shah Al-e Rasul of Marehra, later extending to Chishti, Naqshbandi, and Suhrawardi paths. He established the Khanqah-e ‘Aliyya Rizwiyya in Bareilly in 1915 as a dedicated space for spiritual training under the pir-murid (master-disciple) system, fostering dhikr (remembrance of God) and ethical refinement. Successors, including his son Hamid Raza Khan, have sustained this by authorizing disciples and hosting rituals that blend jurisprudence with mysticism.[8][4] Annual Urs observances at the Dargah, commencing after Khan's death in 1921, exemplify ongoing promotion, featuring Quran recitations, na't (devotional poetry), and displays of saintly relics to evoke baraka (blessing). Events like the 1912 Urs of his pir Nuri Miyan drew 4,000–5,000 participants, evolving into mass gatherings that educate on doctrines such as the saint's intercessory power. Through these, the Dargah not only disseminates Khan's 20-point creed—encompassing beliefs in the Prophet's primordial light (Nur Muhammadi)—but also counters doctrinal erosion by reformist groups, maintaining Sufism's experiential dimension in South Asian Islam.[8][14]

Opposition to Reformist Movements

Ahmed Raza Khan, the founder of the Barelvi movement and central figure associated with Bareilly Sharif Dargah, mounted a sustained theological campaign against reformist currents within South Asian Islam, particularly the Deobandi school, which sought to curtail perceived innovations (bid'ah) in devotional practices such as veneration of saints and seeking intercession (tawassul). In 1906 (1320 AH), Khan authored Husam al-Haramayn (Sword of the Two Sanctuaries), a detailed fatwa accusing prominent Deobandi scholars—including Rashid Ahmad Gangohi, Khalil Ahmad Saharanpuri, and Qasim Nanotvi—of blasphemy for statements in their works that Khan interpreted as diminishing the Prophet Muhammad's unique knowledge and attributes, likening them to Wahhabi denials of prophetic distinction.[28] [29] During his Hajj pilgrimage that year, Khan presented the treatise to over 30 scholars in Mecca and Medina, securing endorsements from figures like Mufti Ahmad ibn Muhammad Zain Dahlan and Sheikh Saleh al-Shami, who affirmed the Deobandi authors' statements warranted takfir (declaration of disbelief) for undermining core Sunni doctrines on prophetic excellence (itmam al-nubuwwah).[28] These endorsements, compiled into the published work, positioned Bareilly as a bastion against reformist scripturalism that Khan viewed as eroding traditional Sufi epistemologies rooted in experiential gnosis (ma'rifah) over literalist hadith-centric puritanism. The fatwa exacerbated the Deobandi-Barelvi schism, with Deobandis responding via refutations like Al-Muhannad 'ala al-Mufannad (1907), denying intent to blaspheme while accusing Barelvis of anthropomorphic excesses in saint reverence.[30] Khan extended his critique to Wahhabi and Ahl-i Hadith adherents, whom he charged with importing Arabian puritanism that rejected shrine visits and milad celebrations as idolatrous, contrasting this with the Dargah's emphasis on embodied piety through urs observances and qawwali. Over 100 fatwas from Khan and his Bareilly successors, issued from the Dargah complex, upheld doctrines like the Prophet's perpetual intercessory presence (hazar wa nazar), directly countering reformist assertions of prophetic limitation post-demise.[31] This opposition framed Barelvi theology as preservative of Hanafi-Maturidi orthodoxy against Wahhabi-influenced iconoclasm, though critics from reformist camps dismissed it as defensive traditionalism unmoored from primary sources. The Dargah's role persisted post-Khan's death in 1921, with successors like Hamid Raza Khan reinforcing these stances amid rising Deobandi institutional growth in madrasas.[32]

Rituals and Annual Observances

Urs-e-Razvi Festival

The Urs-e-Razvi Festival, also referred to as Urs-e-Ala Hazrat or Urs-e-Razavi, marks the annual death anniversary of Ahmad Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), the founder of the Barelvi movement, and is held over three days at the Bareilly Sharif Dargah in Uttar Pradesh, India.[33][34] This observance aligns with the 23rd to 25th of Safar in the Islamic lunar calendar, commemorating Khan's passing on 25 Safar 1340 AH (28 October 1921 CE), with the Gregorian dates shifting annually due to the lunar cycle—for instance, falling on 10–12 September in 2023, 30 August–1 September in 2024, and 18–20 August in 2025.[35][33][34] The festival draws lakhs (hundreds of thousands) of pilgrims, including international visitors from countries such as the United Kingdom, Australia, South Africa, and Pakistan, underscoring its role as a central gathering for adherents of the Barelvi tradition who emphasize Sufi devotional practices.[36][37] Key rituals commence with flag-hoisting ceremonies and chadar processions, where embroidered sheets symbolizing devotion are offered to the shrine, followed by mehfil-e-milad gatherings featuring naat recitations—poetic praises of the Prophet Muhammad—and collective zikr (remembrance of God through rhythmic chanting).[33] Scholarly speeches by Barelvi ulema (clerics) address theological themes central to Khan's legacy, such as defense of Sufi customs against reformist critiques, while dua (supplicatory prayers) and symbolic ghusl (ablution) of the mausoleum occur throughout.[33] The culminating event on the third day, known as Kul Sharif, features the primary congregational prayer at precisely 2:38 PM, the recorded time of Khan's death, emphasizing spiritual continuity and intercession at the site of his burial.[38] While traditionally a physical pilgrimage drawing massive crowds to the dargah complex for immersion in devotional fervor, the 2020 edition adapted to online broadcasts amid the COVID-19 pandemic, highlighting logistical challenges in managing large assemblies.[38] The festival reinforces the Barelvi emphasis on celebrating saintly figures through public rituals, contrasting with austere interpretations of Islam, and serves as a platform for reinforcing doctrinal positions articulated by Khan.[33]

Gyarvi Sharif and Other Commemorations

Gyarvi Sharif is observed monthly on the 11th day of each Islamic lunar month at Bareilly Sharif Dargah, involving communal recitations of the Quran, milad un-Nabi assemblies, and fatiha prayers dedicated primarily to Sheikh Abdul Qadir Jilani, founder of the Qadiri Sufi order to which Ahmad Raza Khan belonged.[39][40] These gatherings emphasize seeking divine mercy through supplications and discussions of the saint's life, aligning with Barelvi devotional emphases on intercession and saint veneration.[39] At the dargah, observances often extend to public processions featuring devotional chants and banners, drawing thousands of pilgrims, though such events have faced disruptions; for instance, the procession planned for October 4, 2025, was canceled by dargah authorities amid heightened local tensions to maintain peace.[41][42] Beyond Gyarvi Sharif, the dargah hosts annual commemorations for Ahmad Raza Khan's birth on the 10th of Shawwal 1272 AH (June 14, 1856 CE), observed as Youm-e-Raza with special prayers, lectures on his scholarly contributions, and charitable distributions to reinforce his legacy in Barelvi theology.[43] Separate Urs observances honor family successors, such as Mustafa Raza Khan (d. 1981), held on the 25th of Rabi' al-Awwal, featuring qawwali performances, fatiha khwani, and pilgrim influxes that sustain the site's role as a Barelvi pilgrimage hub.[1] These events underscore the dargah's function in perpetuating familial spiritual lineage, with attendance peaking during aligned lunar dates but regulated to prevent overcrowding.[18]

Controversies and Criticisms

Theological Debates with Deobandis and Salafis

The theological debates between the Barelvi scholars associated with Bareilly Sharif Dargah and the Deobandi and Salafi movements center on core doctrinal differences regarding prophetic knowledge, saint veneration, and ritual innovations. Ahmad Raza Khan (1856–1921), the foundational figure of the Barelvi school and a key patron of the dargah, accused Deobandi leaders such as Rashid Ahmad Gangohi (d. 1905) and Qasim Nanotvi (d. 1880) of diminishing the Prophet Muhammad's omniscience by asserting his knowledge was temporally and spatially limited akin to ordinary humans, a position Khan deemed blasphemous and grounds for takfir (declaration of disbelief).[44] In response, Deobandi scholars maintained that such views aligned with classical Ash'ari theology, emphasizing the Prophet's knowledge as derived from divine revelation rather than inherent eternity, and countered by labeling Barelvi practices like seeking intercession (tawassul) through saints as bordering on polytheism (shirk).[30] A pivotal escalation occurred in 1906 when Khan authored Husam al-Haramayn ("The Sword of the Two Sanctuaries"), compiling excerpts from Deobandi and Ahl-i Hadith (Indian Salafi) writings to argue they insulted the Prophet and promoted Wahhabi-influenced puritanism, securing endorsements from approximately 33 scholars in Mecca and Medina declaring the targeted figures heretics.[29] Salafis, whom Barelvis equate with Ahl-i Hadith for rejecting Sufi customs such as shrine visits and the Prophet's birthday (milad) celebrations as bid'ah (innovation), faced similar condemnations; Khan viewed their iconoclasm—evident in Saudi destruction of graves—as a threat to established Sunni traditions rooted in Hanafi and Sufi exegesis. Deobandis and Salafis rebutted by critiquing Husam al-Haramayn as selectively quoted and manipulative, arguing Barelvi exaltation of the Prophet risked deification, while their own stances preserved tawhid (monotheism) against perceived excesses like attributing pre-eternal sight to the Prophet.[45][46] These disputes, originating in late 19th-century British India amid revivalist responses to colonial pressures, have persisted through fatwa exchanges from Bareilly's institutions, such as rulings against Deobandi anthropomorphism in divine attributes and Salafi rejection of auditory perception of the Prophet's intercession from the grave. Barelvis defend shrine-based tawassul as permissible mediation supported by hadith narrations, whereas opponents cite Quranic verses like 39:3 prohibiting intercessors besides God, framing Barelvi rituals as cultural accretions diluting scriptural purity. Sectarian sources on both sides exhibit interpretive biases favoring their chains of transmission (isnad), with Deobandi critiques often highlighting logical inconsistencies in Barelvi proofs and vice versa, underscoring a broader Hanafi Sunni schism over balancing literalism and mysticism.[47][31]

Political Engagements and Fatwas

The custodians of Bareilly Sharif Dargah, also known as Dargah-e-Ala Hazrat, have issued numerous fatwas addressing political and security-related matters, often positioning the Barelvi tradition against perceived threats from extremism while navigating India's secular framework. In 2016, the dargah's religious authorities declared a fatwa against Lashkar-e-Taiba leader Hafiz Saeed, labeling him an adherent of anti-Islamic terrorist ideology and urging Muslims to reject such figures.[48] Similarly, following the 2022 beheading of tailor Kanhaiya Lal in Udaipur over alleged blasphemy, Mufti Saleem Noori of the affiliated Madrasa Manzar-e-Islam issued a fatwa condemning the perpetrators, Ghaus Mohammad and Riyaz Attari, and affirming that extrajudicial killings violate Islamic sharia, even in cases of perceived insult to the Prophet, with punishment reserved for state authorities in non-Muslim lands.[49] These rulings reflect a consistent stance against vigilante violence and jihadist groups, distinguishing Barelvi jurisprudence from Salafi-influenced ideologies. Historically, Ahmed Raza Khan Barelvi, the dargah's founding figure, issued fatwas with direct political implications during British colonial rule, declaring India a Dar al-Islam (abode of Islam) under non-Muslim governance due to the protection of religious practices, thereby deeming armed rebellion or jihad against the British impermissible and sinful.[50] He opposed movements like the Khilafat campaign, Non-Cooperation, Hijra (migration to avoid non-Muslim rule), and certain jihad efforts, arguing they lacked valid Islamic grounds and risked communal alliances with Hindus that could undermine Muslim interests.[51] These positions, while criticized by reformist sects like Deobandis as collaborationist, were rooted in Khan's emphasis on doctrinal preservation over revolutionary upheaval, influencing Barelvi political restraint amid partition-era dynamics.[52] In contemporary India, the dargah has engaged politically through public campaigns and edicts on national issues. In 2015, clerics initiated a signature drive to denounce terrorism, aiming to demonstrate Muslim opposition to violence and counter narratives of communal complicity.[53] A 2018 fatwa from senior mufti Mufti Asjad Raza Khan urged Indian Muslims to reject Muhammad Ali Jinnah's legacy, portraying support for the Two-Nation Theory as incompatible with post-independence patriotism.[54] More recently, in October 2025, the Ala Hazrat family criticized Uttar Pradesh police actions following the arrest of Maulana Tauqeer Raza Khan, grandson of a successor to Ahmed Raza Khan, demanding an end to perceived targeted detentions of Muslims amid protests near the dargah that involved stone-pelting and heightened security.[55] Such interventions highlight tensions between devotional autonomy and state enforcement, with the dargah's influence extending to defenses against encroachments on Sufi shrines, as voiced in 2022 against groups questioning sites like Ajmer Sharif.[56]

Incidents of Violence and State Responses

In March 2010, communal riots erupted in Bareilly during a Bara Wafaat procession commemorating the death anniversary of the Prophet Muhammad, an event tied to Barelvi devotional practices centered at Bareilly Sharif Dargah. The violence was triggered by inflammatory speeches delivered by Maulana Tauqeer Raza Khan, the Dargah's prominent cleric and leader of the Ittehad-e-Millat Council, which incited crowds to clash with police and target Hindu properties, resulting in dozens of injuries, attacks on temples, and weeks of unrest across the city.[57] A Uttar Pradesh court in 2024 convicted Khan as the mastermind, citing his role in provoking the riots through speeches that escalated tensions.[58] State authorities imposed curfews in affected areas, conducted arrests including Khan's detention, and deployed police to quell the violence, though preliminary investigations highlighted coordination among rioters stockpiling weapons in mosques.[57] On September 26, 2025, clashes broke out in Bareilly's Kotwali area when over 2,000 protesters, mobilized by Khan in response to a canceled demonstration over the 'I Love Muhammad' campaign against alleged insults to Islam, pelted stones at police following Friday prayers near a mosque.[59][60] The confrontation injured 22 police officers and led to vandalism, prompting a lathi charge to disperse the crowd.[61] In response, Uttar Pradesh police arrested 81 individuals, suspended internet services for several days, deployed 10,000 personnel with drones for surveillance, and conducted flag marches to maintain order; authorities also initiated bulldozer actions against eight illegal properties linked to Khan and shot at an aide of his in an encounter.[62][63][64] These measures, part of a broader state policy targeting rioters' assets, aimed to deter recurrence, with investigations revealing pre-planned stockpiles of bricks and petrol bombs in 390 mosques echoing 2010 patterns.[57]

Societal Impact and Recent Developments

Influence on Muslim Devotional Culture

The Bareilly Sharif Dargah, shrine of Ahmad Raza Khan Barelvi (1856–1921), functions as the primary hub for the Ahl-e Sunnat wal Jamaat movement, which has profoundly shaped Sufi-oriented devotional practices among South Asian Muslims by prioritizing affective piety toward the Prophet Muhammad and saintly figures.[65] Ahmad Raza Khan's theological output, including over 1,000 works in Arabic, Persian, and Urdu, systematically defended rituals such as tawassul (intercession via prophets and saints), fatiha (offerings recited at graves), and public expressions of love for the divine, embedding them within Hanafi jurisprudence while drawing on classical Sufi authorities.[8] This framework countered reformist tendencies toward scriptural literalism, fostering a devotional culture that integrates poetry, music, and communal gatherings as legitimate paths to spiritual fulfillment.[66] Annual observances at the dargah, particularly the Urs-e-Razvi commemorating Khan's death on 25 Safar (October 28 in the Gregorian calendar), draw hundreds of thousands of pilgrims who engage in qawwali recitations, naat poetry praising the Prophet, and collective supplications, thereby perpetuating these practices across generations and regions.[14] The site's associated institutions, including madrasas and publishing houses, disseminate Khan's fatwas—such as those affirming the permissibility of milad-un-Nabi celebrations on 12 Rabi' al-Awwal—which have standardized devotional norms for Barelvi adherents, estimated to constitute the majority of practicing Sunnis in rural India and Pakistan.[67] This influence manifests in widespread adoption of shrine-based rituals, where devotees seek barakah (blessings) through physical proximity to sacred spaces, reinforcing a syncretic yet orthodox expression of faith resilient to puritanical challenges.[68] Beyond ritual observance, the dargah's legacy promotes a cosmology wherein the Prophet's ongoing spiritual presence (hazir-o-nazir) justifies intercessory devotion, influencing everyday Muslim life through amulets, vows (mannat), and dream interpretations tied to saintly figures.[8] Khan's emphasis on inner purification via Sufi orders like Qadiri and Chishti, combined with public theology, has sustained a mass following that views devotional excess as emblematic of true Sunni fidelity, distinct from elite reformism.[69] This enduring impact underscores the dargah's role in maintaining pluralism within Islamic practice, though it has sparked debates over innovation (bid'ah) with rival schools.[70]

Economic and Pilgrimage Effects

The Bareilly Sharif Dargah attracts hundreds of thousands of pilgrims annually, primarily adherents of the Barelvi school of Sunni Islam, who visit to pay respects at the shrine of Ahmad Raza Khan (Ala Hazrat). Peak attendance occurs during the Urs-e-Razvi festival, commemorating Khan's death anniversary, with estimates of up to one million visitors reported for the event in May 2023.[71] Similar large gatherings, described as "lakhs" (hundreds of thousands), were noted for the 107th Urs in 2025 and earlier observances like the 100th Urs in 2018.[33][72] This pilgrimage activity generates substantial economic benefits for Bareilly, a city in Uttar Pradesh, by stimulating demand for lodging, transportation, and local commerce during festival periods. The influx of devotees supports hospitality services, street vendors selling religious paraphernalia, and related businesses, contributing to seasonal revenue in an otherwise industrially oriented region known for textiles and furniture. The Uttar Pradesh government has recognized the dargah's role in religious tourism, incorporating it into broader development plans, including infrastructure upgrades to position Bareilly as a major pilgrimage hub by December 2026. Nationally, religious tourism, of which sites like Bareilly Sharif form a part, accounted for approximately ₹1.34 lakh crore in earnings across India in 2022, underscoring the sector's macroeconomic significance through multiplier effects on employment and ancillary services.[73] However, specific revenue figures attributable solely to the dargah remain undocumented in public reports, with economic impacts varying based on visitor spending patterns and external factors like regional security or pandemics, as seen in the partial online shift of the 2020 Urs.[38]

Post-2020 Events and Tensions

In September 2025, tensions escalated in Bareilly following a nationwide controversy over the "I Love Muhammad" campaign, which originated from FIRs filed in Kanpur on September 9 against individuals for displaying banners perceived as provocative by authorities.[74] In Bareilly, Ittehad-e-Millat Council (IMC) president Maulana Tauqeer Raza Khan, a Barelvi cleric associated with the Ala Hazrat lineage, called for protests against alleged insults to the Prophet Muhammad, leading to large gatherings after Friday prayers on September 26.[59] [75] Protesters marched toward Islamia Ground, defying police restrictions, resulting in stone pelting at security forces near mosques, including areas adjacent to Ala Hazrat Dargah; police responded with lathi charges, tear gas, and arrests, booking over 2,000 individuals and detaining 31, with eight arrests including Tauqeer Raza Khan on September 28.[59] [76] [77] Stones were also pelted at the Ala Hazrat Dargah and Tauqeer Raza Khan's residence during the unrest.[78] The IMC claimed the protests involved coordinated mobilization from 390 mosques, with each tasked to bring at least 100 participants.[76] In the aftermath, police conducted raids on mosques, alleging harassment of imams and worshippers, prompting the Ala Hazrat family to decry "police excesses" and demand a halt to arrests of Muslims on October 1.[55] [79] On the same day, an IMC district president was injured in a police encounter, followed by a demolition drive targeting illegal structures.[63] By early October, 81 arrests had been made, internet services were suspended, and the Gyarvi Sharif procession at the Dargah—typically held on the 11th of each lunar month—was canceled on October 4 amid heavy security deployment around the site and major mosques.[42] [41] [80] Friday prayers on October 3 proceeded peacefully under heightened alert, with Maulana Ahsan Raza Khan, another family member, urging calm.[81] Earlier communal frictions in the area included July 2023 protests by Hindu organizations against a proposed road-widening project near Ala Hazrat Dargah, which they claimed threatened an ancient temple.[82] Separate incidents, such as a Muslim individual allegedly controlling a 250-year-old temple in Bareilly's Katghar area reported in December 2024 and vandalism on a Hindu temple wall with Islamic symbols in January 2025, heightened local Hindu-Muslim strains but were not directly linked to Dargah activities.[83] [84] In contrast, the August 2025 Urs-e-Ala Hazrat observance at the Dargah featured non-contentious initiatives like tree plantation and free medical camps.[85]
