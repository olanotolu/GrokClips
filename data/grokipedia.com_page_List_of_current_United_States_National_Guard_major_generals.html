List of current United States National Guard major generals


The list of current United States National Guard major generals catalogs the two-star general officers (O-8) actively serving in the Army National Guard and Air National Guard, the principal reserve components of the U.S. Army and Air Force, respectively.[1][2] These leaders command divisions, wings, joint task forces, and state-level headquarters, executing dual missions that include homeland defense, domestic emergency response, and federal mobilization for overseas contingencies under Title 10 or Title 32 authority. As of October 2025, the roster reflects federally recognized appointments subject to Senate confirmation for certain positions, with roles often filled by state adjutants general who integrate Guard units into broader military operations while maintaining readiness for civil support tasks such as disaster relief and border security.[3] The composition underscores the National Guard's unique state-federal structure, enabling scalable force projection without relying solely on active-duty components.[4]

Overview and Definitions

Role of Major Generals in the National Guard

In the United States National Guard, major generals (O-8 pay grade) function as two-star general officers who command division-sized Army units (typically 10,000 to 15,000 personnel) or equivalent air formations, while also holding senior staff and advisory roles that bridge state and federal authorities.[5] Their responsibilities encompass ensuring unit readiness, training, equipping, and deploying Guard forces for domestic emergencies, such as natural disasters or civil unrest under governors' direction, as well as federal missions like overseas deployments when mobilized under Title 10 authority.[6] This dual-status framework requires major generals to maintain operational alignment between state-level commands and national defense priorities, often coordinating with the National Guard Bureau and combatant commands.[7] In the Army National Guard, major generals frequently serve as adjutants general or assistant adjutants general for states, acting as the principal military advisors to governors and overseeing the administration, mobilization, and employment of both Army and Air Guard components within their jurisdiction.[8] For instance, they direct the organization, stationing, and coordination of state forces, including response to civil support operations and consequence management.[9] Federally, they may assume roles such as deputy commanding generals, where they supervise readiness across Guard divisions and integrate them into active Army structures for global contingencies.[7] Within the Air National Guard, major generals typically command state air components or major wings, managing airlift, refueling, fighter, and support missions while ensuring compliance with federal air sovereignty and homeland defense tasks.[10] They bear accountability for the operational effectiveness of assigned airmenâ€”often numbering in the thousandsâ€”across training, maintenance, and deployment cycles, including integration with active Air Force units during mobilizations.[11] In joint capacities, such as staff positions at U.S. Northern Command, they facilitate Guard contributions to threat deterrence and aggression prevention within the homeland.[6] Major generals in the National Guard must navigate statutory limits on promotions and assignments, with federal recognition required for state-level roles and selections influenced by both gubernatorial appointments and Department of Defense oversight to sustain force balance across 54 states and territories.[12] Their leadership emphasizes scalable responses, from state active duty for localized crises to Title 32 operations for federally funded domestic missions, underscoring the Guard's unique reserve component role in national security.[13]

Criteria for Federal Recognition and Inclusion

Federal recognition for promotions to major general in the United States National Guard ensures that officers meet federal standards of fitness, capacity, and moral character for potential mobilization under Title 10, United States Code.[14] This process, distinct from state appointments, applies to both Army and Air components and is mandatory for grades of colonel and above, with heightened scrutiny for general officers to align with Reserve Officer Personnel Management Act (ROPMA) requirements under 10 U.S.C. Â§Â§ 14301â€“14319.[15] For inclusion in official listings of current major generals, officers must hold permanent federal recognition in the grade, maintain active status in an authorized position, and remain below mandatory removal dates (e.g., age 62 for Army National Guard major generals, extendable to 64 with waivers).[16] In the Army National Guard, the Chief of the National Guard Bureau, upon recommendation from the state Adjutant General, submits nominees to the annual or semiannual General Officer Federal Recognition Board (GOFRB), convened by the Secretary of the Army per National Guard Regulation (NGR) 600-100.[16] The GOFRB, composed of three general officers (two Regular Army, one ARNG), examines records, including Officer Evaluation Reports, Retirement Points Accounting System data, and security clearances, requiring unanimous approval of physical, professional, and moral qualifications.[16] Prerequisites include at least five years of continuous active status (waivable by the Chief, NGB), completion of the Army War College or equivalent (waivable if pursued within 60 days post-board), a baccalaureate degree from an accredited institution, top secret/sensitive compartmented information clearance, and medical fitness under Army Regulation 40-501, with no disqualifying adverse actions from criminal investigations or inspector general inquiries.[16] Senate confirmation is required under 10 U.S.C. Â§ 14101(a), and temporary recognition may bridge to permanent status for up to 12 months pending resolution of deficiencies. Officers must also demonstrate at least one year time-in-grade as brigadier general and assignment to a Table of Organization and Equipment or Table of Distribution and Allowances position authorized for major general.[16] Air National Guard procedures mirror those for the Army component, governed by Air National Guard Instruction (ANGI) 36-2504, which mandates federal recognition for officer promotions through state nomination by the Adjutant General followed by evaluation of eligibility criteria including professional military education (e.g., Air War College), time-in-service, fitness assessments per ANGI 10-248, and security eligibility.[17] General officer boards assess nominees for command suitability, with requirements for exemplary conduct, no unresolved medical or administrative issues, and alignment with position vacancies, culminating in Chief, National Guard Bureau approval.[17] Delays in recognition, historically exceeding 100 days for some, were addressed by the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2023, which backdates promotion effective dates to selection for cases over 100 days, though core fitness criteria remain unchanged.[18] Inclusion criteria emphasize verifiable federal recognition orders from the National Guard Bureau, excluding those with withdrawn recognition due to incapacity, misconduct, or failure to meet standards under 32 U.S.C. Â§ 323, and prioritizing active-duty status over retired or inactive reserve equivalents. Documentation such as NGB Form 62E (application for recognition) and board findings (NGB Form 89) confirm status, with updates tracked via Bureau personnel systems to reflect current as of October 27, 2025.[16] This dual federal-state framework ensures only qualified officers are listed, mitigating risks of state-level promotions lacking national standards.[19]

Dual Federal-State Status and Verification Methods

National Guard major generals hold commissions issued by their respective state or territory, enabling them to command forces under gubernatorial authority during state active duty or Title 32 status, where operations are federally funded but state-controlled. This state-centric role allows governors to deploy Guard units for domestic emergencies, such as natural disasters or civil unrest, without federal involvement.[20] Concurrently, federal recognition of their rankâ€”required for Title 10 federal active dutyâ€”confirms their authority within the Army or Air Force reserve components when mobilized by presidential order, integrating them into the joint force structure.[21] Federal recognition for promotions to major general typically follows a board review process, ensuring qualifications align with Department of Defense standards, though states may grant interim state-level promotions pending federal approval.[22] This dual structure, unique to the National Guard among U.S. military components, facilitates seamless transitions between state and federal missions, as exemplified by dual-status commanders who exercise authority over both Title 10 federal and Title 32 Guard forces in operations like disaster response. However, discrepancies can arise if a state promotes an officer beyond federally recognized rank, limiting their federal command eligibility until recognition is granted.[18] Federal oversight, managed by the Chief of the National Guard Bureau and service secretaries, ensures compatibility, with military technicians (dual status) required to maintain valid federal recognition for certain positions. Verification of current major general status demands cross-checking multiple official sources to confirm both state commission and federal recognition, avoiding reliance on unverified or outdated listings. Primary methods include consulting the National Guard Bureau's general officer biographies for active assignments and recognized ranks.[23] Department of Defense announcements of promotions and assignments, often released via the General Officer Management Office, provide dated confirmations of federal actions.[24] Senate records of nominations and confirmations validate presidential appointments to federal-recognized grades.[25] State-specific verification via adjutant general directories or official military department sites corroborates state-level status, though federal recognition remains the criterion for inclusion in national listings.[26] As of October 2025, dynamic assignments necessitate periodic review, given frequent rotations and retirements documented in these channels.[27]

Key Units and Formations under Command

The Army National Guard employs major generals to command division-sized formations that integrate brigade combat teams and support elements from multiple states, enabling scalable deployment for federal missions under U.S. Army Forces Command or state emergencies. These units emphasize readiness through collective training, such as annual division-level exercises, and maintain approximately 10,000-15,000 personnel each when mobilized. The structure supports the Guard's dual role, with headquarters typically located at state training centers. Key combat formations include the eight infantry divisions, each led by a major general:
  • 28th Infantry Division (Pennsylvania headquarters): Commands ARNG units primarily from Pennsylvania, with a focus on light and Stryker-equipped brigades for rapid response.
  • 29th Infantry Division (Virginia headquarters): Oversees elements from Virginia and Maryland, incorporating infantry and field artillery brigades for expeditionary operations.
  • 34th Infantry Division (Minnesota headquarters): Directs Red Bull units from Minnesota, Iowa, and other states, specializing in armored and infantry maneuver.
  • 35th Infantry Division (Kansas headquarters): Manages Santa Fe Division forces from Kansas, Missouri, and Nebraska, with emphasis on combined arms training.
  • 36th Infantry Division (Texas headquarters): Leads Lone Star units from Texas, featuring heavy brigade combat teams for high-intensity conflict.
  • 38th Infantry Division (Indiana headquarters): Commands Cyclone Division assets from Indiana and Michigan, focusing on cyber-enabled infantry operations.
  • 40th Infantry Division (California headquarters): Oversees Sunset Division elements from California and other western states, with aviation and sustainment integration.
  • 42nd Infantry Division (New York headquarters): Directs Rainbow Division units from New York and surrounding states, known for historical and modern mobilization expertise.
In addition to divisions, select major generals lead functional commands like the 36th Theater Aviation Command (Texas-based, coordinating ARNG aviation assets across regions) and sustainment or engineer formations under Army Materiel Command alignments, ensuring logistical support for over 330,000 ARNG soldiers. These commands undergo federal evaluation for readiness, with major generals responsible for Title 10 mobilization compliance.[24]

Recent Developments and Nominations

Promotions and Assignments in 2025

In the Army National Guard, Major General Charles D. Hausman was promoted to the rank of major general on May 16, 2025, following his appointment as Adjutant General of Missouri on January 8, 2025, with assumption of duties on February 1, 2025.[47][48] Major General Justin W. Osberg of the Illinois Army National Guard was promoted to major general on June 7, 2025, during a ceremony at Cantigny Park in Wheaton, Illinois.[49] Brigadier General Martin M. Clay, Jr., of the Missouri Army National Guard was promoted to major general on August 16, 2025, at Jefferson Barracks in St. Louis, Missouri, and subsequently assigned as Commander of the 35th Infantry Division at Fort Leavenworth, Kansas, effective September 2025.[50][51] Major General David L. Hall assumed the role of Deputy Commanding General for the Army National Guard within U.S. Army Futures Command in Austin, Texas, effective January 2025.[52] Major General Robert B. Davis was appointed as the 45th Adjutant General of Colorado and executive director of the Colorado Department of Military and Veterans Affairs on September 25, 2025, assuming command following a change-of-command ceremony on October 18, 2025, after serving as Deputy Director of the Army National Guard.[34][53] In the Air National Guard, Brigadier General Christopher A. Jarratt, previously Chief of Staff at 1st Air Force (Air Forces Northern) and associated with the Hawaii Air National Guard, was nominated for promotion to major general on August 4, 2025, while serving as acting director of programs and requirements at the National Guard Bureau.[54][55]

Pending Nominations as of October 2025

As of October 27, 2025, several nominations for promotion from brigadier general to major general within the Air National Guard remain pending before the Senate Armed Services Committee. These include:
  • Brig. Gen. Lisa K. Snyder, Indiana Air National Guard, serving as State Air Surgeon, nominated on October 15, 2025 (PN592).[56][57]
  • Brig. Gen. David W. May, Wisconsin Air National Guard, serving as interim Adjutant General, nominated on September 2, 2025 (PN468).[58][59]
  • Brig. Gen. Christopher A. Jarratt, Air National Guard, serving as Vice Director for Programs and Requirements at the National Guard Bureau, nominated on July 29, 2025 (PN441-2).[60][55]
  • Brig. Gen. Shawn M. Coco, Louisiana Air National Guard, serving as Assistant Adjutant General for Air, nominated on July 29, 2025 (PN440-2).[61][62]
No pending nominations for Army National Guard major generals were identified in Senate records as of this date.[63] These nominations follow presidential submission and await committee review and full Senate confirmation under 10 U.S.C. Â§ 601, with potential for hearings or holds influencing timelines.[64]
