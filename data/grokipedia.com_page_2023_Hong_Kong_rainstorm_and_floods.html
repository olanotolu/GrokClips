2023 Hong Kong rainstorm and floods

The 2023 Hong Kong rainstorm and floods was an extreme meteorological event that occurred on 7 and 8 September, triggered by the remnants of Tropical Cyclone Haikui interacting with a broad trough of low pressure, which funneled persistent heavy rainfall across the territory.[1] This deluge produced over 400 millimetres of rain in many areas, with eastern parts exceeding 800 millimetres, marking one of the most intense rainstorms since systematic records began in 1884.[1] The storm shattered three key rainfall benchmarks at the Hong Kong Observatory headquarters: a one-hour total of 158.1 millimetres, a two-hour accumulation of 201.0 millimetres, and a twelve-hour total of 605.8 millimetres.[2][3] A black rainstorm warning, the highest level, remained in effect for 16.5 hoursâ€”the longest duration on recordâ€”prompting school closures, transport suspensions, and evacuations amid citywide flash flooding and landslides.[4] Impacts included over 3,000 fallen tree reports, 21 flooding incidents, and seven landslides, with two fatalities and more than 140 injuries reported, primarily from slips and vehicle accidents in hazardous conditions.[2][1] While infrastructure investments mitigated widespread structural damage, the event exposed vulnerabilities in urban drainage systems, leading to temporary paralysis of key districts like Wong Tai Sin and Shau Kei Wan, though economic recovery was swift with losses estimated in the tens of millions of Hong Kong dollars.[4]

Meteorological Background

Weather Systems and Conditions

The intense rainstorm affecting Hong Kong on 7â€“8 September 2023 was primarily driven by a trough of low pressure associated with the remnant of Severe Typhoon Haikui (2311), which had dissipated after making landfall on the southeastern coast of mainland China on 5 September.[1] The remnant circulation stalled and moved slowly westward over the Guangdong region, maintaining a high moisture content in the atmosphere and fostering persistent convergence of moist air masses.[1] This synoptic setup triggered bands of heavy rain and squally thunderstorms, with the trough enhancing upward motion and rainfall efficiency over Hong Kong's coastal and hilly areas.[2] Local meteorological conditions amplified the event, as the interaction between the remnant's easterly flow and Hong Kong's topography promoted orographic lift, concentrating precipitation in eastern districts.[1] Southeasterly winds prevailed, channeling humid air from the South China Sea into the region, while weak steering currents allowed the system to linger, prolonging the downpour from the evening of 7 September into the morning of 8 September.[2] The overall September weather pattern, influenced by an active southwest monsoon, contributed to elevated baseline humidity, though the Haikui remnant was the dominant forcing mechanism for the peak intensity.[2]

Rainfall Measurements and Records

The Hong Kong Observatory (HKO) Headquarters recorded an hourly rainfall of 158.1 millimetres from 11 p.m. on 7 September to midnight on 8 September 2023, establishing a new record for the highest one-hour precipitation since observations began in 1884.[2] [3] This measurement shattered the previous hourly maximum of 145.0 millimetres set on 7 June 2008.[3] Over the subsequent two hours, rainfall totaled 201.0 millimetres at the HKO site, marking another record for the two-hour accumulation.[2] [3] The 12-hour rainfall reached 605.8 millimetres, also a new benchmark for that duration.[2] [3] In the 24-hour period from 4 p.m. on 7 September to 4 p.m. on 8 September, the HKO registered 638.5 millimetres, which ranked as the second-highest daily total on record, surpassed only by 686.2 millimetres on 30 May 1889.[2]

Chronology

Preceding Typhoons and Build-Up

Super Typhoon Saola approached within 110 kilometers of Hong Kong's center on September 1, 2023, triggering the issuance of the Hurricane Signal No. 10, the highest warning level, for the first time since Typhoon Mangkhut in 2018. The typhoon brought sustained gale-force winds gusting to hurricane force across much of the territory, with maximum gusts reaching 129 km/h at Tate's Cairn, and caused storm surges that flooded low-lying coastal areas in the eastern New Territories.[5] Saola resulted in no direct fatalities in Hong Kong but injured 86 people and disrupted transportation, including suspension of ferry services and closure of schools.[6] Following Saola's passage, Severe Typhoon Haikui (designated 2311) developed in the western Pacific and intensified before making landfall over eastern Taiwan on September 3, 2023, as a typhoon with maximum sustained winds of 195 km/h.[1] Haikui's remnants weakened into a broad low-pressure area after crossing Taiwan but retained substantial moisture as they drifted southwestward toward the Pearl River Delta region.[7] By September 6, the remnant circulation positioned itself to the east-southeast of Hong Kong, interacting with an enhanced southwest monsoon flow and a mid-level trough aloft, fostering a synoptic environment conducive to persistent heavy rainfall.[1] The build-up to the rainstorm involved the convergence of moist air masses from the South China Sea, drawn northward by the low-pressure trough, under conditions of high atmospheric instability and low wind shear.[7] This setup stalled the system over southern China, allowing for prolonged orographic enhancement over Hong Kong's terrain, with rainfall intensifying from the evening of September 7 as the black rainstorm warning was issued at 18:45 HKT after an initial amber signal. Earlier minor flooding from Haikui's initial remnants on September 4-5 had already strained drainage systems, setting the stage for the record-breaking deluge.[8]

Storm Onset and Peak Intensity

The heavy rainstorm began affecting Hong Kong in the evening of 7 September 2023, as a trough of low pressure triggered persistent showers that rapidly intensified across the territory.[2] Initial accumulations exceeded 70 mm by 7:00 p.m. in areas like Sha Tau Kok in the North District, prompting the Hong Kong Observatory (HKO) to escalate warnings amid rising moisture convergence from the remnants of Tropical Cyclone Haikui interacting with local orographic lift.[2] This marked the onset of what would become one of the most extreme rainfall events on record, with rain bands organizing over urban and hilly terrains, leading to flash flooding in low-lying districts by late evening.[3] Peak intensity occurred between 11:00 p.m. on 7 September and midnight, when the HKO headquarters in Tsim Sha Tsui recorded 158.1 mm of rain in a single hourâ€”the highest one-hour total since observations began in 1884.[2] [3] This extreme rate shattered previous benchmarks, with the ensuing two-hour total reaching 201.0 mm and the 12-hour accumulation hitting 605.8 mm at the same station.[3] The HKO issued its Black Rainstorm Warning Signal at 11:05 p.m., signaling rainfall rates exceeding 70 mm per hour territory-wide, which persisted for 16 hours and 35 minutes until 3:40 p.m. on 8 Septemberâ€”the longest such duration since 1992.[2] Over the 24-hour period from 4:00 p.m. on 7 September to 4:00 p.m. on 8 September, totals reached 638.5 mm at HKO headquarters, while eastern and southern districts saw accumulations surpassing 800 mm, driven by sustained convective activity.[2] [3]

Subsidence and Immediate Resolution

The intense rainfall associated with the low-pressure trough began to subside during the morning of 8 September 2023, transitioning from widespread heavy downpours to scattered showers as the rain bands weakened and shifted away from the territory.[2] The Hong Kong Observatory recorded the 24-hour rainfall total ending at 4:00 p.m. HKT on 8 September as 638.5 mm at its headquarters, encapsulating the period from 4:00 p.m. the previous day and signifying the close of the peak accumulation phase.[2] At 3:40 p.m. HKT on 8 September, the Observatory cancelled the Black Rainstorm Warning, which had been active since 11:05 p.m. on 7 Septemberâ€”a duration of 16 hours and 35 minutes, the longest on record since the system's inception in 1992.[2] This cancellation marked the meteorological resolution of the acute threat, with remaining showers easing further over the following days as the trough dissipated and atmospheric instability diminished.[2] Immediate post-storm stabilization involved the natural drainage of accumulated floodwaters aided by receding rainfall, though urban runoff persisted in low-lying areas until pumping operations could be fully mobilized.[4] By 10 September, government reports indicated over 60 flooding incidents had been addressed preliminarily, with infrastructure assessments underway to mitigate ongoing disruptions from the event's hydrological aftermath.

Causes and Contributing Factors

Natural Meteorological Drivers


The 2023 Hong Kong rainstorm was primarily driven by a trough of low pressure associated with the remnant of Severe Typhoon Haikui (2311), which had made landfall near the Fujian-Guangdong border on September 5 as a tropical storm.[1] After landfall, Haikui weakened rapidly into a low-pressure area over inland eastern Guangdong and drifted westward slowly, lingering near the Pearl River Estuary.[1] This slow movement facilitated the development of a meso-Î±-scale trough over the Guangdong coast, providing sustained synoptic forcing for ascent and convective activity.[7] The trough's position over the Pearl River Delta region channeled abundant tropical moisture from Haikui's remnant circulation into Hong Kong, enhancing rainfall potential through repeated formation of heavy rain bands and squally thunderstorms.[2] Persistent low-level convergence along the trough axis, combined with upper-level divergence, supported prolonged vertical motion, leading to the development of intense, organized convection rather than isolated cells.[7] The system's quasi-stationary nature prevented rapid dissipation, allowing rainfall to accumulate over 24 hours, with the Hong Kong Observatory Headquarters recording 158.1 mm in one hour from 11 p.m. to midnight on September 7â€”the highest hourly total on record since 1884.[1] No broader extratropical influences or monsoon surges were dominant; the event's intensity stemmed directly from the remnant's moisture-laden trough interacting with regional dynamics.[2] Total rainfall from the system reached 641.1 mm at the Observatory, surpassing previous records for tropical cyclone-related events and underscoring the trough's efficiency in concentrating precipitation.[1]

Hydrological and Topographical Influences

Hong Kong's rugged topography, featuring steep hills and mountains that constitute over 60% of its land area, played a key role in amplifying flood severity during the 7â€“8 September 2023 rainstorm by promoting rapid surface runoff and concentrating water flows into low-lying urban zones.[9] Steep gradients accelerated the descent of rainwater from elevated catchments, increasing peak discharge rates and overwhelming stormwater drains designed for lower intensities, with cascading waters observed tumbling down slopes into districts like Wong Tai Sin and Tai Po.[9][10] This terrain-driven funneling effect was evident in areas such as Hong Kong Island, where localized totals exceeded 800 mm in 24 hours, transforming valleys into torrent channels.[4] Hydrologically, the event's extreme precipitationâ€”reaching a record 158.1 mm per hour at the Hong Kong Observatoryâ€”saturated soils and exceeded the infiltration capacity of urbanized watersheds, leading to high runoff coefficients that boosted streamflows beyond historical norms.[11] Nullahs and rivers, including those in Tai Po, received massive inflows from upstream slopes, surpassing drainage infrastructure limits and causing overflows with inundation in low-elevation zones.[10] The combination of topographic steepness and hydrological saturation from prior moisture contributed to flash flood dynamics, where water levels rose meters within hours, as reported in over 60 flooding incidents across the territory.[4]

Infrastructure and Urban Development Role


Rapid urbanization in Hong Kong has significantly altered local hydrology, increasing flood vulnerability by replacing permeable rural landscapes with impervious surfaces such as roads, buildings, and pavements. This reduces natural infiltration and groundwater recharge, leading to higher surface runoff volumes and faster peak discharges into drainage systems during intense rainfall. In the 1980s, development converted floodplains into built-up areas, further constricting natural watercourses and diminishing their capacity to handle excess water.[11] Hong Kong's stormwater drainage infrastructure, managed by the Drainage Services Department, incorporates trunk drains designed to withstand 200-year return period events in urban zones, alongside enhancements like 21 km of drainage tunnels, stormwater storage facilities, and over 100 km of upgraded river channels. However, older systems in established districts adhere to outdated standards, and blockages from debris, rubbish, or landslide materials can impair flow. During the September 7-8, 2023, rainstorm, hourly rainfall reached 158.1 mm in some areasâ€”exceeding design capacitiesâ€”and combined with amplified runoff from urban imperviousness to overwhelm the network, causing widespread flash flooding in low-lying districts like Chai Wan and Quarry Bay.[11][4][12] The concentration of high-density development in flood-prone coastal and valley areas exacerbates these effects, as topography funnels accelerated urban runoff into constrained channels, intensifying inundation beyond what natural terrain alone would produce. While infrastructure improvements have mitigated risks for more frequent storms, the 2023 event's unprecedented intensity highlighted limitations in accommodating both meteorological extremes and cumulative urbanization pressures.[13][4]

Impacts

Casualties and Human Effects

Two fatalities were reported during the September 7â€“8, 2023, rainstorm in Hong Kong, with the bodies of the victims discovered in flooded areas across different parts of the city.[14][1] A total of 144 people sustained injuries, ranging in age from eight to 94 years old, primarily due to slips, falls, vehicle accidents, and incidents related to the flooding and high winds.[15][1] Among the injured, four were in critical condition as of September 8.[16] The casualties stemmed from the sudden onset of flash flooding in urban low-lying districts, where residents encountered waist-deep water, submerged vehicles, and hazardous debris flows, exacerbating risks for pedestrians and motorists caught outdoors during peak rainfall hours.[17][8] No widespread reports of disease outbreaks or long-term health effects directly attributable to the event have been documented in official assessments.[2]

Infrastructure Disruptions and Economic Costs

The extreme rainfall on September 7â€“8, 2023, caused widespread infrastructure disruptions across Hong Kong, particularly affecting transportation networks. Flooding submerged sections of the Mass Transit Railway (MTR) system, including Wong Tai Sin Station and parts of the Kwun Tong Line, leading to closures and service suspensions. Over 80 roads were damaged or blocked by flash floods and debris, with more than 20 buses stranded and traffic paralyzed in multiple districts. The Cross-Harbour Tunnel was inundated, further halting vehicular movement, while landslides exceeding 200 reports obstructed access to areas like Shek O village, necessitating the evacuation of 120 residents.[2][4][8] Power supply experienced temporary interruptions in affected areas, alongside over 60 flooding incidents reported, damaging public facilities, shopping centers such as Temple Mall North in Wong Tai Sinâ€”whose basement was flooded, forcing closureâ€”and car parks. Infrastructure damage stemmed from the record 158.1 mm of rain in one hour, overwhelming drainage systems despite mitigation efforts like 21 km of drainage tunnels and stormwater tanks.[2][18][4] Economic costs were substantial, with insured losses from the black rainstorm totaling HK$1.54 billion (approximately US$198 million), primarily from property damage accounting for 86% of claims. This figure marked the second-highest on record for weather-related insurance payouts in Hong Kong, surpassed only by Typhoon Mangkhut in 2018. Additional impacts included business interruptions, with the stock exchange canceling morning trading on September 8, and agricultural losses such as over 1,000 pigs drowned on a farm, exceeding HK$3 million in damages. Motor insurance claims contributed HK$171 million, reflecting widespread vehicle flooding.[18][4][19]

Landslides and Environmental Consequences

The record-breaking rainfall of 7â€“8 September 2023 triggered over 200 landslide reports across Hong Kong, as documented by the Hong Kong Observatory.[2] These events were concentrated in hilly and coastal areas, where saturated soils failed under the weight of prolonged downpours exceeding 600 mm in 24 hours in multiple locations.[2] A prominent landslide struck Shek O Road on 8 September, involving a sliding failure of approximately 650 cubic meters of material from Slope No. 11SE-D/F47, creating a scar roughly 20 meters long, 16 meters wide, and up to 5 meters deep.[20] This debris collapsed the northbound lane, blocking access and isolating hundreds of residents until clearance efforts were undertaken.[20] In Shau Kei Wan, another landslide on the same day buried sections of Yiu Hing Road under mud and rock, halting traffic and requiring emergency evacuation.[21] Further instability manifested in Repulse Bay, where cliffside erosion threatened multi-million-dollar mansions, prompting structural assessments and resident alerts due to the risk of collapses from loosened slopes.[22] A secondary washout failure at the Shek O site on 14 September displaced an additional 126 cubic meters, underscoring ongoing vulnerabilities during subsequent red rainstorm warnings.[20] Environmentally, these landslides resulted in substantial soil displacement and erosion, with blocked drainage systems causing localized overflows that heightened slope instability and runoff volumes.[20] The events contributed to geomorphological alterations, including scar formation and debris accumulation, potentially increasing sedimentation risks in adjacent coastal zones, though no casualties were directly attributed to landslides.[2] Widespread reports of fallen trees, exceeding 3,000 in the broader September period, indicated additional vegetation disruption exacerbating erosion on denuded slopes.[2]

Emergency Response

Official Warnings and Mobilization

The Hong Kong Observatory (HKO) issued escalating rainstorm warnings on the evening of 7 September 2023 as intense rainfall associated with the remnants of Tropical Cyclone Haikui intensified. At 19:50 HKT, the HKO released a Special Announcement on Flooding for the northern New Territories, alerting residents to potential flash floods.[4] This was followed by an Amber Rainstorm Warning Signal at 21:25 HKT, upgraded to Red at 21:50 HKT, and then to the Black Rainstorm Warning Signalâ€”the highest levelâ€”at 23:05 HKT, indicating rainfall rates exceeding 70 mm per hour.[2][4] The Black signal, which advises the public to stay indoors and suspend non-essential activities, remained in effect until 15:40 HKT on 8 September, totaling 16 hours and 35 minutesâ€”the longest duration since the system's inception in 1992.[2] Concurrently, the HKO hoisted a Landslip Warning at 23:45 HKT on 7 September to address risks of landslides amid saturated ground conditions.[4] These warnings triggered automatic protocols, including the suspension of schools, kindergartens, and non-essential government operations starting from 8 September, as well as advisories for employers to assess work continuation.[2] In parallel with the warnings, the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR) government activated its emergency coordination mechanisms under the Security Bureau's oversight. Departments including the Fire Services Department, Civil Aid Service, and Auxiliary Medical Services were placed on heightened alert, responding to over 100 flooding and landslip incidents reported during the Black signal period.[23] Inter-departmental efforts focused on monitoring drainage systems, clearing blockages, and conducting preliminary rescues, with the Drainage Services Department deploying pumps and barriers at known flood-prone areas.[4] The Chief Executive's Office convened coordination meetings to direct resource allocation, emphasizing real-time assessment of impacts in vulnerable districts like the northern New Territories and eastern Kowloon.[23]

Operational Measures and Resource Deployment

The Hong Kong government activated its mobilisation protocol on three occasions following the issuance of the Black Rainstorm Warning Signal during the September 7â€“8, 2023, rainstorm to coordinate multi-agency responses to widespread flooding.[24] This included deploying auxiliary forces for immediate rescue and relief, with the Civil Aid Service and Auxiliary Medical Service mobilising nearly 300 members to conduct search-and-rescue operations and provide on-site medical treatment in affected districts.[24] The Drainage Services Department established an Emergency Control Centre that operated continuously for over 200 hours to oversee flood mitigation, doubling the number of emergency response teams from the standard 70 to 144 teams during peak flooding, comprising approximately 550 personnel focused on clearing debris-blocked drains, nullahs, and river channels to restore drainage capacity.[25] These teams prioritised high-risk areas, eliminating 59 out of 60 reported flooding blackspots by September 8 evening through proactive inspections and mechanical removal of obstructions like leaves and sediment.[23][24] The Fire Services Department elevated its alert level to "Service Stand-to" and handled nearly 800 special services callsâ€”2.5 times the typical volumeâ€”including evacuations of over 100 residents from isolated areas like Shek O using rescue boats and makeshift pontoons, alongside efforts to pump out stagnant water, clear MTR tunnel blockages with divers and appliances, and deliver essential supplies to stranded communities.[26] Complementing these efforts, approximately 510 civil servants from 13 bureaux and departments were deployed across the three mobilisation phases for post-flood cleanup, such as clearing roads, homes, and schools, while district-level teams in areas like Tsuen Wan activated around 120 members and volunteers to manage temporary shelters and community support.[24]

Controversies

Shenzhen Reservoir Discharge Debate

On September 7, 2023, amid record-breaking rainfall from the remnants of Typhoon Haikui, Shenzhen authorities notified Hong Kong officials of an impending discharge from reservoirs in Shenzhen to prevent overflow due to excessive inflow.[27] The public announcement in Hong Kong came at 23:44 that evening, stating the discharge would begin around midnight on September 8 at an initial rate of 70 to 80 cubic meters per second, later increased to 120 cubic meters per second by 3:15 a.m.[28] Hong Kong authorities warned of potential flooding risks in low-lying areas of the New Territories, particularly northern districts along the Shenzhen River, and mobilized district offices, drainage services, police, and other agencies to notify residents and monitor river levels.[27][28] The discharge sparked debate over its role in exacerbating Hong Kong's flooding, with residents in border villages like Fanling reporting insufficient preparation timeâ€”some receiving notice less than 45 minutes before the startâ€”and subsequent localized inundation of homes and roads.[28] Online discussions and resident complaints questioned whether the release, coinciding with peak rainfall, added to river swells and overwhelmed drainage in the North District, a low-lying area prone to backups.[29] Wong King, former president of the Hong Kong Institute of Utility Specialists, attributed some inevitability to the flooding in these zones, citing the combined effects of the discharge and geographical constraints where water had limited outlets.[30] Hong Kong officials, including Security Secretary Chris Tang Ping-keung, countered that the discharge had no significant impact on city-wide flooding, as monitored river levels showed no unusual rises and no severe inundation occurred in anticipated village areas.[29] Tang emphasized the action as a pre-agreed safety protocol under the 2009 cross-border natural disaster plan, benefiting both regions by averting dam failures in Shenzhen, with notifications provided as early as feasible given the emergency.[28] Engineers like Johnny Cheuk Chi-yin supported this view, noting that flood patternsâ€”concentrated in eastern New Territories and Kowloonâ€”did not align with zones directly downstream of the discharge, such as Tin Shui Wai, underscoring the dominance of local rainfall runoff exceeding 150 mm per hour in some areas.[28] The volume released, while substantial, was deemed minor relative to the unprecedented precipitation, which shattered 140-year records and drove the primary hydrological overload.[29]

Government Preparedness and Response Scrutiny

Critics, including residents and media outlets, questioned the Hong Kong government's preparedness for the record-breaking rainfall on September 7â€“8, 2023, arguing that vulnerabilities in urban drainage and early warning systems were not adequately addressed despite prior extreme weather events.[31] Online commentary from affected residents highlighted perceived inadequacies in infrastructure resilience, such as overwhelmed stormwater systems in low-lying areas like Sheung Shui and Fanling, where flooding submerged streets and ground-floor premises before full emergency measures were enacted.[31] The timing of the black rainstorm warning, issued by the Hong Kong Observatory at 11:05 p.m. on September 7 after more than 100 mm of rain had already fallen in parts of the New Territories, drew particular scrutiny for potentially delaying public precautions and business closures.[3] This signal remained in effect for a record 16.5 hours, the longest since its introduction in 1992, underscoring the event's intensity but also prompting debates over whether amber or red signals issued earlier in the evening could have prompted timelier evacuations or work suspensions.[4] Former Observatory director Lee Wai-chung emphasized the need for enhanced public disaster awareness, noting that such extreme conditions, exacerbated by climate trends, would become more frequent and require proactive behavioral shifts beyond government alerts.[32] In response to mounting public concerns, Chief Executive John Lee conceded the necessity of reviewing the rainstorm warning framework and bolstering communication channels to ensure faster dissemination of risks, while officials classified the delugeâ€”peaking at 158.1 mm per hour in some areasâ€”as a rare "once-in-500-years" event challenging predictive models.[33] The administration mobilized over 2,000 personnel from the Fire Services Department and Auxiliary Medical Services for rescue operations, addressing 60 flood reports and 75 landslide alerts by September 10, yet critics from independent outlets argued that preemptive infrastructure hardening, such as upgraded nullahs and reservoirs, lagged behind known rainfall escalation risks documented in prior studies.[4][32] These evaluations, drawn from post-event analyses, reflect a consensus that while immediate response averted higher casualtiesâ€”limited to two deathsâ€”systemic gaps in foresight contributed to widespread disruptions in a densely urbanized territory.[8]

Aftermath and Lessons Learned

Recovery Operations

Following the declaration of extreme conditions on September 8, 2023, the Hong Kong government activated a mobilization protocol, deploying a quick response unit of approximately 250 civil servants from departments including the Department of Justice, Independent Commission Against Corruption, and Department of Health to support recovery operations.[34][35] These teams concentrated on clearing accumulated floodwaters and debris in heavily impacted districts, with primary efforts in the North District at sites such as Lo Wu Station Road, Ma Mei Ha Tsuen, Fanling, Ta Kwu Ling, and Lee Uk Village.[36][34] District Services and Community Care Teams supplemented these activities by aiding residents and managing temporary shelters.[34] The Drainage Services Department undertook channel clearance and water pumping in affected waterways, including the Lok Ma Chau River, where operations on September 9 revealed additional casualties amid debris removal.[36] Road access in Ta Kwu Ling was largely restored by September 9 through these combined efforts.[36] Public transportation restoration progressed rapidly, with the MTR Corporation resuming full service across all lines by September 9, despite localized flooding at stations like Wong Tai Sin requiring extended repairs.[4] Most bus and other transit services also returned to operation that day, minimizing prolonged disruptions.[36] Evacuation support targeted isolated coastal communities, where authorities used boats and erected a temporary pier to relocate around 200 residents from Shek O and Big Wave Bay to shelters such as Luen Wo Hui Community Hall.[36][34] Chief Executive John Lee inspected relief sites in Fanling and confirmed the provision of expedited financial aid through departmental channels for affected households and businesses.[34] Pre-existing drainage infrastructure investments facilitated this prompt restoration, enabling economic activities to normalize within days.[4]

Policy Reviews and Infrastructure Upgrades

In the aftermath of the September 2023 rainstorm, which recorded an unprecedented hourly rainfall of 158.1 mm and caused widespread flooding, the Hong Kong government's Drainage Services Department (DSD) issued Practice Note No. 2/2023, establishing guidelines on flood resilience to enhance urban drainage design standards and mitigation strategies against extreme events.[37] This policy update directly referenced the storm's impacts, emphasizing improved flood risk assessment and resilient infrastructure planning to handle rainfall exceeding historical design capacities of 10- to 50-year return periods.[4] By October 2023, the government initiated a review of stormwater drainage improvement schemes, prioritizing projects to address vulnerabilities exposed by the flooding, such as backflow in low-lying areas and inadequate capacity during peak rainfall.[38] This included accelerating the implementation of 15 major drainage enhancement initiatives aimed at expanding channel capacities and integrating flood barriers.[39] Concurrently, the Chief Executive's 2023 Policy Address outlined a strategic planning study on flood management against sea level rise and extreme rainfall, with completion targeted for 2024 to inform long-term policy adaptations.[40] Infrastructure upgrades accelerated post-review, with annual spending on stormwater drainage more than doubled over the subsequent two years to bolster system resilience.[41] Key measures included installing flood sensors at over 30 high-risk sites, such as MTR station exits and entrances, enabling real-time monitoring and rapid flood barrier deployment protocols.[42] Additional emergency response teams were mobilized for proactive blockage clearance during heavy rain, reducing response times compared to the 2023 event.[41] In May 2025, authorities allocated HK$300 million specifically for flood-control enhancements, focusing on coastal defenses and shoreline management to mitigate seawater intrusion and rainwater backflow in vulnerable districts.[39] These efforts build on cumulative DSD investments exceeding HK$32 billion in drainage works since 1986, prioritizing empirical data from the 2023 storm to refine designs for compound risks like typhoon-induced rainfall.[42]
