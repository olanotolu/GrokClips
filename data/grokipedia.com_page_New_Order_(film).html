New Order (film)

New Order (Spanish: Nuevo orden) is a 2020 Mexican-French dystopian thriller film written, directed, produced, and edited by Michel Franco.[1][2] Set in a near-future Mexico City, the story centers on a wealthy family's wedding interrupted by a violent popular uprising of disenfranchised masses, which spirals into widespread riots, class warfare, a military coup, and a cycle of retribution where former elites become victims of the new regime's brutality.[3][4] The film premiered at the 77th Venice International Film Festival, where it won the Silver Lion Grand Jury Prize, marking Franco's second major award there after After Lucia (2012).[5] Starring Naian GonzÃ¡lez Norvind as the bride Marianne, alongside Diego Boneta and a cast blending established actors with non-professionals, it runs 85 minutes and employs stark cinematography by Yves Cape to underscore themes of inequality, power shifts, and the inescapability of violence in stratified societies.[1][6] Reception was polarized, with international critics praising its unflinching realism and prophetic depiction of social collapse amid inequality, achieving a 67% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes, while domestic audiences in Mexico decried it as oversimplifying complex divisions and perpetuating stereotypes by portraying darker-skinned indigenous rioters as savage invaders contrasted against lighter-skinned elites.[7][8] Franco defended the work as a cautionary allegory drawn from historical precedents like the French Revolution, intended to expose hypocrisies on both sides without endorsing any faction, though detractors, including some Mexican commentators, accused it of racial insensitivity and elitist paranoia.[9][10] The film's provocative stance on causal chains of resentmentâ€”from elite indifference fueling revolt to revolutionaries' descent into authoritarian excessâ€”has fueled debates on its prescience, especially amid contemporaneous global unrest, positioning it as a stark indictment of unchecked social fissures rather than a partisan narrative.[4][3]

Plot

Synopsis

New Order depicts a near-future Mexico City where a sumptuous wedding among the upper class is suddenly disrupted by escalating street protests that erupt into widespread riots targeting the elite.[1] The festivities at the bride's family mansion turn chaotic as intruders breach the grounds, symbolizing the intrusion of class resentment into insulated privilege.[11] Protagonist Marianne, the bride from a wealthy family, becomes entangled in the turmoil when she encounters a desperate former employee seeking aid for his injured sister amid the violence.[12] As the unrest spirals into a full-scale military coup, Marianne faces abduction and imprisonment, navigating a landscape of class warfare, authoritarian crackdowns, and cycles of retribution that expose underlying societal fractures.[7] The 86-minute dystopian thriller unfolds with raw, unadorned realism, prioritizing visceral depictions of anarchy and power shifts over explanatory dialogue or backstory.[1]

Production

Development

Michel Franco conceived New Order (Nuevo orden) as a cautionary depiction of escalating class tensions in Mexico, drawing from the country's profound social disparities and the upper class's disregard for long-standing protests by the marginalized.[15] He described the film as a "Mexican disaster movie" warning of an impending explosion of unrest, reflecting ignored grievances that protesters had voiced "for decades, hundreds of years."[15] This vision positioned the narrative as an admonition against Mexico's extreme wealth inequality and the risks of militarized responses to social upheaval, with deliberate ambiguity to evoke global resonances amid contemporaneous movements like Black Lives Matter and gilets jaunes.[16][17] The project's roots trace to approximately five to six years prior to its 2020 premiere, around 2014â€“2015, when Franco began exploring themes of class warfare, rural poverty, and institutional corruption amid Mexico's 60 million citizens living in poverty.[17] Franco wrote the screenplay himself, emphasizing narrative vagueness to mirror real-world uncertainties in power dynamics.[15] Financing came through a Mexico-France co-production led by Franco's own company, Teorema, in partnership with Les Films d'Ici, alongside backing from Videocine in Mexico.[15][17] The budget remained modestâ€”far below $10 millionâ€”enabling Franco to retain substantial creative control as writer, director, lead producer, and co-editor, minimizing external demands.[15]

Filming

Principal photography for New Order occurred over six weeks in spring 2019, primarily in Mexico City, incorporating real locations such as urban neighborhoods to simulate riots and upscale districts alongside constructed sets in converted industrial hangars for controlled environments like detention centers and hospitals.[18] Cinematographer Yves Cape utilized a RED Weapon Monstro Carbon 5K camera paired with Leitz Summilux prime lenses (ranging from 18mm to 75mm) to deliver a 2.4:1 aspect ratio image, emphasizing an organic, documentary-style realism through a mix of natural and artificial lighting.[18] The approach began with handheld camerawork for early sequences to evoke initial disorientation and chaos, shifting to dolly-supported stability in subsequent scenes despite escalating disorder, while relying on sequence shots with limited movement to maintain temporal continuity.[18] [19] Crowd scenes employed real extras augmented by minimal digital compositing, such as five layered passes in the cemetery sequence handled by Mikros for duplication effects, avoiding extensive CGI to preserve authenticity.[18] Production challenges encompassed a two-week commitment to the wedding sequence for logistical precision and careful actor blocking to balance visibility in wide frames, with on-set editing facilitating rapid assembly and test screenings after initial weeks.[18] The shoot concluded in mid-2019, preceding global COVID-19 lockdowns, though post-production faced subsequent delays from pandemic restrictions.[18] [20]

Release

Festival premiere

The world premiere of New Order occurred at the 77th Venice International Film Festival on September 10, 2020, in the main competition section.[15][17] The screening took place amid adaptations to the COVID-19 pandemic, with the festival proceeding in a hybrid format under strict health protocols.[15] Subsequent festival screenings followed in fall 2020, including at the Toronto International Film Festival on September 15.[21] The film also appeared at the San SebastiÃ¡n International Film Festival starting September 18.[22] These early international outings positioned New Order as a dystopian thriller navigating limited physical audiences and virtual elements due to ongoing global restrictions.[23] Initial buzz from the Venice presentation prompted Neon to acquire North American distribution rights on September 29, 2020, signaling strong early market interest in the film's provocative narrative.[23][24]

Distribution and box office

The film premiered theatrically in Mexico on October 22, 2020, where it quickly became the top-grossing release that weekend, marking the first time a Mexican film achieved that position in its debut frame.[25][26] In North America, Neon acquired distribution rights following its festival circuit and released it on a limited basis starting May 21, 2021, with an opening weekend gross of $144,533 from 111 theaters.[23][24][1] In Mexico, Nuevo Orden drew over 330,000 admissions, generating more than $950,000 at the box office despite pandemic-related theater restrictions.[25] The film's U.S. and Canadian earnings totaled $303,556, contributing to a worldwide gross of approximately $1.8 million.[1] International rollout remained constrained, limited primarily to festival screenings and select arthouse markets in Europe and Latin America, consistent with Michel Franco's prior works like After Lucia (2012) and Chronic (2015), which also prioritized critical acclaim over broad commercial appeal amid COVID-19 disruptions to global exhibition.[27][1]

Reception

Critical reception

New Order premiered at the 77th Venice International Film Festival on September 10, 2020, where it received the Silver Lion Grand Jury Prize, with jurors praising its bold depiction of societal upheaval and Michel Franco's unflinching directorial style.[15] The film holds a 67% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes, based on 105 reviews with an average score of 6.7/10, reflecting divided opinions on its provocative content.[7] On Metacritic, it scores 62 out of 100 from 23 critics, indicating mixed or average reviews amid acclaim for its technical execution and visceral intensity.[33] Critics lauded the film's technical prowess, including its crowd scenes involving thousands of extras and effective use of visual effects to convey chaos, as well as Franco's economical storytelling that builds an unsettling atmosphere.[12] Variety described it as a "confrontational and deeply cynical look at how a revolution might play out," highlighting its scale as a step up for Franco.[12] European outlets like The Guardian commended its "brutally unforgiving" portrayal of inequality's consequences, viewing it as a stark warning without sympathy for any side.[4] The Hollywood Reporter noted its chronicle of a militarized coup replacing elite rule, appreciating the characteristic austerity in Franco's vision.[2] American reviews were more critical, often faulting the film for excessive brutality and a perceived lack of nuance in handling class violence. The Los Angeles Times called it a "contemptible political chiller" and "the emptiest of provocations," arguing it prioritized shock over substance.[34] Rolling Stone deemed it "one of the most offensive movies of 2021," criticizing its depiction of class warfare as tone-deaf and elitist.[8] RogerEbert.com awarded 1.5 out of 4 stars, acknowledging the relentless dystopia but questioning its inversion of revolutionary assumptions without deeper insight.[3] These critiques frequently highlighted the film's muddled politics and overwhelming violence, which some felt overshadowed any coherent message.[35]

Audience reception

On IMDb, New Order holds a 6.5/10 rating from 10,872 user votes as of late 2023.[1] Audience reviews there often praise the film's tense pacing and unflinching portrayal of social unrest, with some viewers describing it as a gripping thriller that effectively captures the chaos of revolution, though many note its disturbing content including graphic violence and torture.[1] Letterboxd users rate it lower at an average of 2.9 out of 5 stars based on over 24,000 logs, highlighting a sharper polarization.[21] Fans of provocative cinema commend its raw, visceral style and cautionary take on inequality's explosive outcomes, likening it to a "punk rock" assault on complacency. Detractors, however, frequently label it hateful or one-sided, accusing it of dehumanizing protesters as irrational mobs and lacking nuance in depicting class divides.[21] Online discourse remains divided, with festival buzz from Venice and Toronto contributing to steady post-theatrical engagement via streaming platforms like Netflix, where it sustains viewership among thriller enthusiasts despite limited mainstream breakout.[36] In Mexico, the film drew over 330,000 admissions shortly after its October 2020 release, indicating regional resonance amid real-world inequality debates, though global audience metrics show no dominant demographic skew beyond appeal to those valuing stark realism over empathetic narratives.[25]

Controversies

Backlash in Mexico

The trailer for New Order, released on October 5, 2020, provoked widespread outrage on Mexican social media platforms, where users accused the film of racism and classism for depicting an uprising by darker-skinned, lower-class protesters against lighter-skinned elites.[37][38] Critics argued that scenes of violence by the underclass portrayed indigenous and poor Mexicans as inherently savage, reinforcing stereotypes amid ongoing national debates on racial and socioeconomic inequality.[37] This reaction intensified following the film's Silver Lion win at the Venice Film Festival on September 12, 2020, with detractors framing the narrative as prejudicial toward Mexico's progressive efforts to address historical class divides under President AndrÃ©s Manuel LÃ³pez Obrador's administration, which had emphasized reducing inequality since taking office in December 2018.[37] Online discourse highlighted perceived anti-nationalist undertones, viewing the dystopian portrayal of societal collapse as undermining narratives of social advancement post-2018 elections.[38] The backlash, largely driven by trailer footage without access to the full film, amplified discussions on "whitexican" privilege and reverse racism claims, though empirical metrics like hashtag trends or petition drives were not formally quantified in major reports.[37] Media coverage in outlets aligned with leftist perspectives echoed these sentiments, labeling the film's premise as fostering division in a country grappling with entrenched racial hierarchies, where lighter skin correlates with higher socioeconomic status per demographic studies.[37] Despite calls for boycotts circulating online, the controversy did not halt the film's theatrical release in Mexico on October 22, 2020, by distributor Videocine, though it underscored sensitivities around artistic depictions of class conflict in the AMLO era.[38]

Director's responses and defenses

In response to the backlash in Mexico, director Michel Franco expressed satisfaction with the controversy, describing it as "very flattering" for sparking public discourse on social issues. He stated, "Iâ€™m very happy to see that my work triggers such conversations," viewing the film's provocative elements as validation of its intent to provoke reflection rather than endorse any ideology.[9] Franco denied any overt political agenda, noting that the script was written years before recent events and that criticisms were largely confined to Mexico, unlike reactions elsewhere. He emphasized the film's focus on social disparity as a universal human failing, transcending specific classes or races, with the narrative critiquing corruption among elites while depicting how uprisings devolve into arbitrary tyranny, thus exposing flaws in all societal strata.[9] Defenders of the film, including some commentators who interpreted it as a cautionary tale against unchecked inequality fostering violent cycles, argued that it realistically illustrates causal outcomes of elite detachment and mob retribution without romanticizing either side. Franco reinforced this by rejecting mainstream production constraints that might sanitize depictions of Mexico, prioritizing undiluted portrayals of human behavior to maintain artistic integrity.[9][39]

Themes and analysis

Class conflict and power dynamics

The film portrays class conflict through the initial juxtaposition of elite opulence and underclass unrest at a lavish wedding in Mexico City, where affluent guests, including corrupt politicians and business leaders, indulge in excess while ignoring protests by predominantly indigenous demonstrators outside the venue.[12] This indifference manifests in scenes where servants from marginalized backgrounds are treated as invisible or disposable, setting the stage for retaliatory invasion when protesters breach the grounds in a single, unbroken three-minute tracking shot, subjecting attendees to random beatings, humiliations, and executions that mirror the dehumanization they previously inflicted.[40][12] The reversal underscores a cycle of abuse driven by resentment rather than ideology, as captives like the bride are held for ransom and torturedâ€”evidenced by graphic implications of sexual violenceâ€”revealing how perceived symbols of oppression become targets for vengeance without restoring equity.[12] Power dynamics shift opportunistically when the military intervenes amid the chaos, staging a coup that installs a dictatorship marked by public executions and flag-draped spectacles of control, portrayed not as redemptive heroism but as pragmatic self-preservation by forces exploiting the vacuum for their gain.[40][17] Director Michel Franco depicts this through sequences of looted streets and enforced blockades, where soldiers prioritize regime consolidation over justice, leading to the former elite's imprisonment and abuse in makeshift camps, thus extending the mutual dehumanization across all strata without victors.[12] Franco grounds these interactions in Mexico's empirical realities of inequality, citing the nation's 60 million poor as a powder keg of unresolved tensions akin to global unrest, though he emphasizes restrained violence to evoke plausible escalation rather than sensationalism.[17] The film's achievements in visualizing these dynamics include practical filming with minimal digital effects, a mobile camera capturing sprawling crowd scenes of indigenous protesters clashing with security, and surreal motifs like green-dyed water symbolizing inverted hierarchies amid bloodied corpses on iconic avenues.[40] This approach conveys the raw entropy of power vacuums effectively on a modest budget, though detractors argue it risks flattening structural grievances by accelerating the underclass's turn to brutality, potentially overlooking sustained causal factors in favor of symmetrical moral equivalence where all parties emerge as perpetrators.[40] Franco counters such views by framing the narrative as a universal warning of self-interested collapse, with no faction portrayed as inherently virtuous.[40]

Political realism and societal critique

The film New Order employs a form of political realism by depicting societal collapse through a chain of causal eventsâ€”escalating inequality provoking violent uprising, which in turn enables authoritarian consolidationâ€”without endorsing ideological solutions or romanticizing any faction. Director Michel Franco has described this as an exaggeration of Mexico's extant class divides and racial tensions, using real locations in Mexico City to ground the dystopia in observable social fractures rather than abstract theory.[40] This approach prioritizes the mechanics of power vacuums and human opportunism over partisan advocacy, illustrating how initial grievances devolve into indiscriminate brutality that erodes distinctions between oppressor and oppressed.[41] Critics and analysts have diverged on its implications: some view it as a caution against populist mobilizations, where mob justiceâ€”framed as a left-leaning revolt against elitesâ€”mirrors the authoritarianism it seeks to dismantle, challenging media portrayals of such uprisings as inherently progressive.[12] Others interpret it as indicting elite complacency and systemic exclusion, positing the violence as a predictable backlash to entrenched disparities, though Franco counters reactionary readings by emphasizing equal savagery across classes, rooted in innate human tendencies toward exploitation amid chaos rather than ideological purity.[40] The narrative's refusal to assign moral superiority underscores a critique of human nature's invariance: elites exhibit callous detachment, while insurgents perpetrate reprisals that perpetuate cycles of domination, debunking notions of class-based moral exceptionalism.[41] Franco's commentary reinforces this non-partisan lens, noting the film's resonance with global phenomena like the 2018â€“2019 Yellow Vests protests in France and 2020 Black Lives Matter demonstrations in the United States, where initial demands for equity unraveled into broader disorder without resolving underlying tensions.[40] In eschewing redemption arcs or policy prescriptions, the work innovates by committing to grim causalityâ€”violence begets consolidation of power under new guisesâ€”mirroring historical precedents of revolutions yielding dictatorships, as Franco observed in pre-release discussions.[41] However, this unrelenting pessimism risks alienating viewers seeking constructive outlets, as the absence of viable alternatives leaves the critique diagnostically potent but prescriptively barren, potentially reinforcing cynicism over agency in addressing inequality.[3]
