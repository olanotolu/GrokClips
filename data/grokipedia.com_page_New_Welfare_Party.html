New Welfare Party

The New Welfare Party (Turkish: Yeniden Refah Partisi, YRP) is a Turkish political party founded on 23 November 2018, adhering to the Milli Görüş tradition of political Islam and led by Fatih Erbakan, son of former Prime Minister Necmettin Erbakan.[1][2] The party's program emphasizes "önce ahlak ve maneviyat" (morality and spirituality first), an interest-free economy focused on domestic production and exports, social justice to eradicate poverty, family protection, and a foreign policy advancing Muslim unity through D-8 cooperation while defending national interests like Cyprus.[3] YRP has distinguished itself through rapid electoral growth, capturing 6.19% of the national vote in the 31 March 2024 local elections—doubling its prior support—and positioning as a primary alternative to the Justice and Development Party (AKP) for conservative voters critical of economic mismanagement and perceived deviations in foreign policy, such as relations with Israel.[4][5] Under Fatih Erbakan's leadership, the party holds seats in the Grand National Assembly and announced his presidential candidacy in February 2025, signaling ambitions to revive core Milli Görüş principles amid Turkey's shifting political landscape.[6]

Historical Background

Legacy of the Original Welfare Party

The original Welfare Party (Refah Partisi), established in 1983 by Necmettin Erbakan following the authorization of new political parties after the 1980 military coup, embodied the Milli Görüş movement's Islamist framework, advocating economic independence via the "Just Order" (Adil Düzen) model, which prioritized interest-free finance and local production over reliance on Western capital.[7] The party surged in popularity during the 1990s, capturing 21.4% of the vote in the 1995 general elections and enabling Erbakan to serve as prime minister from June 1996 to June 1997 in a coalition government that pursued closer ties with Muslim-majority nations through initiatives like the D-8 framework.[8] However, the Constitutional Court dissolved the Refah Partisi on January 16, 1998, citing its leadership's statements and actions—such as Erbakan's calls for sharia implementation and anti-secular rhetoric—as violations of Turkey's constitutional secularism principle, imposing a five-year political ban on key figures including Erbakan.[9][10] The New Welfare Party (Yeniden Refah Partisi) inherits this legacy through direct familial and ideological continuity, with Fatih Erbakan—Necmettin Erbakan's youngest son—founding the YRP on November 23, 2018, after departing from the Felicity Party (Saadet Partisi), a prior Milli Görüş successor, to "resurrect" the original party's uncompromised vision.[11] The YRP's program explicitly adopts the Milli Görüş zihniyeti (mentality), long assimilated into Turkish society, as its foundation, committing to "Önce Ahlak ve Maneviyat" (First Morality and Spirituality) alongside policies echoing Refah's emphasis on public-entrepreneur reconciliation, heavy industry development, and rejection of riba (usury)-based systems in favor of state-supported welfare and self-sufficiency.[3] This includes reviving Erbakan's 54th government's short-lived projects, such as enhanced social security for workers and promotion of Islamic economic cooperation, while critiquing post-Refah successors for diluting core principles through alliances with secular or nationalist elements.[3] The Refah legacy equips the YRP with a distinct voter base rooted in conservative Islamist circles, particularly those alienated by the AKP's economic liberalization and foreign policy pragmatism, allowing it to frame itself as the authentic steward of Erbakan's anti-imperialist, pro-Palestine stance amid ongoing Gaza conflicts.[12] In the March 31, 2024, local elections, this heritage propelled the YRP to 6.19% of the national vote—third place overall—and control of 65 municipalities, primarily in AKP strongholds, signaling a fragmentation of the Islamist electorate and the persistent causal link between Refah's grassroots mobilization and contemporary appeals for moral-economic reform during Turkey's inflation crisis exceeding 70% in 2024.[13][14]

Founding and Initial Organization (2018–2020)

The New Welfare Party was founded on November 23, 2018, by Fatih Erbakan, son of the late Necmettin Erbakan, who had led the original Welfare Party until its closure by Turkey's Constitutional Court in 1998.[15][1] The establishment involved 99 founding members and aimed to revive the Milli Görüş tradition, an Islamist ideological framework emphasizing moral governance, self-reliance, and the "just order" (adil düzen) economic model that prioritizes interest-free finance and heavy industry development over Western-style capitalism.[1] Erbakan, an engineer by training who had previously headed youth branches of predecessor parties including the Felicity Party (Saadet Partisi), broke away due to disagreements over ideological compromises, particularly the Felicity Party's participation in electoral alliances with the ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP), which he viewed as diluting core Islamist commitments.[12] As the party's inaugural and ongoing chairman, Erbakan centralized leadership to consolidate support among conservative voters disillusioned with the AKP's governance, drawing on his experience in organizations like the National Youth Foundation (Milli Gençlik Vakfı) and the Anatolian Youth Association (Anadolu Gençlik Derneği).[16] From late 2018 through 2020, the party's initial organization emphasized grassroots structuring, with efforts to establish provincial and district branches across Turkey to embed the Milli Görüş principles at local levels.[1] This period saw limited public visibility, as the nascent party garnered negligible national attention and fielded candidates in the March 2019 local elections, securing approximately 0.2% of the vote without winning any municipalities, reflecting its early stage of mobilization among a niche Islamist base.[17]

Ideology and Policy Positions

Core Islamist Principles

The New Welfare Party adheres to the Milli Görüş tradition, a religiopolitical framework originating with Necmettin Erbakan that prioritizes governance informed by Islamic ethical and spiritual principles over secular Western models.[3] This ideology posits that societal progress requires safeguarding core Islamic values, such as justice, compassion, and moral rectitude derived from the Quran and Sunnah, to counter imperialism and internal moral decay. The party's program explicitly bases its approach on "Önce Ahlak ve Maneviyat" (First Morality and Spirituality), establishing a social order where ethical conduct and spiritual development precede material pursuits, with policies designed to instill halal (permissible) and haram (forbidden) awareness in economic and daily life.[3] Central to these principles is the reinforcement of traditional Islamic family structures as the foundation of society, protected from perceived threats like media influences promoting lax morals or individualism.[3] The party advocates strengthening the family institution through state-supported religious and moral education for children, emphasizing roles aligned with Islamic teachings—such as viewing women's contributions to faithful generations as paramount, invoking phrases like "Paradise lies at the feet of mothers."[3] Youth upbringing similarly focuses on cultivating righteousness, loyalty to religious ideals, and resistance to secular erosion, aiming to produce a generation with deep ethical consciousness rooted in Islamic virtue rather than purely materialistic or nationalist fervor.[3] In education and social policy, the party seeks integration of morality, spirituality, and religious awareness alongside scientific instruction, rejecting a strict separation that sidelines faith-based ethics.[3] This extends to broader welfare measures inspired by Islamic notions of communal responsibility, including aid for the needy, orphans, and widows, framed as obligations of justice and brotherhood rather than mere state charity.[3] While not overtly calling for full Sharia implementation, these stances implicitly challenge Turkey's secular framework by elevating Islamic moral imperatives—such as neighborly compassion and anti-usury economics—as guiding norms for public life, distinguishing the party from more accommodationist conservative groups.[14][17]

Economic and Social Policies

The New Welfare Party promotes an economic model rooted in the Milli Görüş tradition, emphasizing a debt-free, interest-free system known as "Faizsiz Ekonomi" to eliminate exploitation through usury and foster equitable growth.[3] It prioritizes domestic production of intermediate goods, agricultural tools, defense equipment, and high-value exports in sectors like technology and drones, aiming to reduce import dependency and boost employment through state-supported small and medium enterprises (SMEs).[3] Tax simplification and reduction are proposed, alongside equitable income distribution to address regional disparities, with resources directed toward national industries rather than waste or foreign debt servicing.[3] The party envisions restructuring industrial zones into high-technology development areas and enhancing cooperation within the D-8 framework for joint production initiatives.[3] On welfare and labor, the program commits to a social state providing universal access to housing, education, and healthcare, with targeted subsidies for farmers, retirees, the disabled, and other vulnerable groups to eradicate poverty under a "no one goes hungry" principle.[3] Worker rights would be strengthened through union protections, fair wages, and state mediation in disputes, while establishing a "Gelecek Planlama Kurumu" (Future Planning Institution) for customized regional development plans.[3] Social policies center on "Önce Ahlak ve Maneviyat" (Morality and Spirituality First), positioning the traditional family as the cornerstone of society and advocating protections for family structure, parental rights in moral education, and measures against economic factors like unemployment that undermine marital stability.[3] Education reforms emphasize moral, spiritual, and practical training to produce employable graduates, with increased teacher salaries aligned to European Union levels and expanded access, particularly for girls and rural areas, including science and innovation programs.[3] Healthcare entails free basic services, preventive care, local pharmaceutical production, and equitable regional distribution, critiquing models like city hospitals for inefficiencies.[3] The party opposes progressive frameworks perceived as eroding traditional values, including the Istanbul Convention on preventing violence against women, which it rejects for promoting gender ideologies incompatible with Islamic principles.[18] It has demonstrated anti-LGBT stances in campaigns, framing such rights as contrary to societal morality and family preservation.[19] [20] Broader efforts target societal ills like drug use and corruption through legal enforcement and ethical education, while promoting environmental protections such as pollution reduction and green urban spaces.[3]

Foreign Policy Stances

The New Welfare Party advocates for a foreign policy rooted in the Milli Görüş tradition, emphasizing Islamic solidarity, opposition to perceived Western imperialism, and the prioritization of Turkish national interests aligned with Muslim-majority nations. Central to this is the expansion of the D-8 Organization for Economic Cooperation into broader frameworks like D-60 and D-160 to foster economic, cultural, and political unity among Muslim countries, aiming to establish a "new world order" based on justice rather than force.[3] The party critiques NATO's post-Cold War focus on operations against Muslim societies, questioning its continued relevance and suggesting potential withdrawal if it persists in such activities.[3][21] Regarding relations with Israel, the party takes a firmly oppositional stance, calling for the severance of diplomatic and economic ties, the closure of U.S. military bases such as Incirlik and Kürecik that allegedly facilitate support for Israel, and international recognition of a Palestinian state. Party leader Fatih Erbakan has accused the Turkish government under President Erdoğan of indirect complicity in Gaza operations through ongoing trade with Israel, demanding an immediate halt to such relations.[22][21] This position aligns with broader campaign rhetoric framing Palestine as a core issue, with Erbakan attributing domestic electoral setbacks partly to insufficient action against Israel.[23] On European affairs, the party seeks "fair and respectful" ties with the European Union while rejecting subordination, criticizing EU biases against Turkey and advocating prioritization of Islamic world partnerships over Western integration. In the Eastern Mediterranean and Aegean, it opposes Greek territorial claims, defends Turkish rights to resources, and supports the independence of the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (KKTC) along with the continued presence of Turkish forces there.[3][21] The party also favors enhanced relations with Russia, Iran, and African Muslim states to counterbalance Western influence, rejecting a foreign policy that renders Turkey a "dependent satellite" of any power.[24][21]

Leadership and Internal Structure

Key Figures and Leadership

Fatih Erbakan serves as the founder and chairman of the New Welfare Party (Yeniden Refah Partisi, YRP), a position he has held since establishing the party in late 2018.[12] Born on January 1, 1979, in Ankara, Erbakan is a trained engineer who entered politics drawing on his father's legacy as Necmettin Erbakan, the former prime minister and leader of the original Welfare Party in the 1990s.[18] Under his leadership, the YRP has pursued a hardline Islamist platform, differentiating itself from the ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP) by criticizing economic policies and foreign alignments, such as those perceived as too conciliatory toward Israel.[25] Erbakan's prominence has grown amid the party's electoral gains, including securing parliamentary seats following the 2023 general elections and contributing to opposition votes in the 2024 local elections.[17] In January 2025, he announced his candidacy for the Turkish presidency in the next election cycle, signaling ambitions to challenge President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan as a leading voice for conservative and religious voters.[26] The party's central leadership remains heavily centered on Erbakan, with a founding board including figures such as Abdulhamid Adak and Abdullah Süratlı, though these individuals hold less public visibility compared to the chairman.[27] This structure reflects a top-down approach typical of Turkish Islamist parties, where the leader's personal authority drives strategic decisions and public messaging.[2]

Party Organization and Membership

The New Welfare Party maintains a hierarchical organizational structure in accordance with Turkey's Political Parties Law, comprising central, provincial, district, town, and neighborhood/village levels. At the apex is the Büyük Kongre, the party's highest decision-making body, which elects the Genel Başkan—currently Fatih Erbakan—for terms of up to three years—and the 50-member Merkez Karar Yürütme Kurulu (MKYK), responsible for policy oversight and establishing auxiliary units. The Merkez Yürütme Kurulu (MYK), led by the Genel Başkan and including specialized roles such as political affairs, organization, and research-development, implements executive decisions. Local structures include provincial (İl) organizations headed by an İl Başkanı with a 15- to 25-member management board (expanded in major cities), district (İlçe) equivalents with 11- to 25-member boards based on population, and subordinate town (Belde) and neighborhood units for grassroots coordination. Auxiliary branches, including Kadın Kolları (women's wings) and Gençlik Kolları (youth wings), operate parallel at every level to promote targeted engagement, with central oversight ensuring alignment with the party's Milli Görüş ideology.[28][29] Membership is open to Turkish citizens aged 18 or older who accept the party's program and statutes, are not affiliated with other parties, and satisfy legal eligibility under the Political Parties Law, excluding those with criminal convictions or bans. Applications require a signed entry declaration endorsed by two existing members, submission to the district management board, and payment of variable dues—ranging from 1 to 5,000 Turkish lira for entry and 1 to 1,000 lira annually—followed by issuance of a membership card and entry into a centralized electronic database. Members enjoy rights such as candidacy for internal positions, participation in congresses, and access to party information, while duties encompass financial contributions, adherence to moral and leadership directives, and active support for activities. Disciplinary mechanisms, including expulsion by the Yüksek Disiplin Kurulu, enforce compliance.[28] As of January 2025, the party reported 622,847 registered members, reflecting a 70.8% increase of 259,080 from 365,767 in 2024, positioning it as Turkey's fastest-growing political party by official Yargıtay Cumhuriyet Başsavcılığı figures. This surge correlates with electoral gains and dissatisfaction among conservative voters, though exact recruitment strategies emphasize monthly targets of at least four new members per polling district via local branches.[30][31][32]

Electoral Performance

Local Elections

In the 2024 Turkish local elections held on 31 March, the New Welfare Party (YRP) achieved its first significant electoral breakthrough, securing 6.19% of the national vote and finishing third behind the Republican People's Party (CHP) and the Justice and Development Party (AKP).[13] [33] This result more than doubled the party's vote share from the May 2023 parliamentary elections, where it had garnered approximately 2.8%, reflecting growing appeal among conservative voters disillusioned with the AKP's economic policies and foreign stances.[5] The YRP won a total of 65 municipalities, primarily small district-level councils in conservative Anatolian provinces, but also secured two provincial capitals: Şanlıurfa, a metropolitan municipality previously held by the AKP since 2004, with candidate Mehmet Kasım Gülpınar elected mayor; and Yozgat, with Kazım Arslan winning the mayoralty.[13] [5] [34] These victories were concentrated in regions with strong Islamist sentiments, where the party capitalized on criticisms of the AKP's handling of inflation exceeding 70% annually and its perceived moderation on issues like interest rates and relations with Israel.[13] Party leader Fatih Erbakan hailed the outcome as the "real winner" of the elections, attributing success to the party's emphasis on "just order" economics and anti-Zionist positions that resonated amid Gaza-related tensions.[5] Prior to 2024, the YRP, founded in November 2018, had negligible involvement in the 2019 local elections, fielding few candidates and recording no mayoral wins or substantial vote shares due to its nascent organization and lack of national recognition.[35] The 2024 performance marked the party's emergence as a splinter force from the AKP's conservative base, potentially fragmenting the Islamist vote in future contests.[13]

Parliamentary and National Elections

The New Welfare Party (YRP) first contested parliamentary elections in the 2023 Turkish general election held on May 14, 2023, running independently without entering an electoral alliance. The party secured 1,506,862 votes nationwide, equivalent to 2.79% of the valid votes cast for parties, falling short of the 7% national threshold required for proportional representation seats in the Grand National Assembly.[36] As a result, the YRP won zero seats out of 600, despite garnering support in conservative-leaning provinces where it polled above 5% in some districts, such as Şanlıurfa with 28,949 votes (approximately 6%).[37] In the concurrent presidential election, the YRP did not field its own candidate and instead endorsed incumbent President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan of the Justice and Development Party (AKP), aligning with his People's Alliance on foreign policy and Islamist priorities while critiquing the AKP's economic management.[26] This tactical support contributed to Erdoğan's narrow victory in the runoff on May 28, 2023, with 52.18% of the vote against Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu's 47.82%, though the YRP later distanced itself from the AKP ahead of local polls.[38] Prior to 2023, the party, founded in November 2018, had not participated in national parliamentary contests, as the previous election occurred in June 2018 under the prior electoral system. No subsequent national elections have been held as of October 2025, with the next scheduled for 2028 barring early dissolution.

Controversies and Criticisms

Relations with the AKP and Intra-Islamist Rivalries

The New Welfare Party (YRP), led by Fatih Erbakan, emerged in 2018 as a critic of the Justice and Development Party (AKP) for deviating from the strict Islamist principles of the Milli Görüş tradition established by Necmettin Erbakan, positioning itself as a more uncompromising alternative amid perceptions of AKP moderation on issues like interest-based economics and secular alliances.[17] Despite this ideological divergence, YRP provided tactical support to President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan during the May 2023 presidential election runoff, aligning informally with the People's Alliance to counter the secular-leaning opposition candidate Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu, driven by conservative voter pressures; this cooperation yielded YRP approximately 2.5-2.8% of the national vote in parliamentary contests but did not involve full electoral merger.[38][39] Tensions escalated after the 2023 elections, with YRP opting to contest the March 31, 2024, local elections independently, securing 6.19-7% of the vote and emerging as the third-largest party by eroding AKP's conservative base in key provinces through appeals to voters disillusioned by economic mismanagement, including inflation exceeding 50%, rising taxes, and unfulfilled promises on poverty alleviation.[17][39] Fatih Erbakan publicly severed ties in September 2024, declaring no future alliances with Erdoğan due to AKP's alleged dishonesty, policy reversals that exacerbated living costs, and actions perceived as alienating youth from religion—such as tender rigging and moral lapses fostering deism or atheism—while criticizing nepotism and the replacement of military oversight with "Beştepe tutelage" under the presidential palace.[38][39] By November 2024, Erbakan reiterated rejection of the People's Alliance, citing broken 2023 agreements and AKP's failure to address corruption or opposition suppression, though YRP's influence has prompted AKP concessions like the 2021 Istanbul Convention withdrawal and a 2024 stray dog euthanasia draft reflecting shared conservative social priorities.[39][17] Intra-Islamist rivalries center on competition within the Milli Görüş lineage, as YRP originated from Erbakan's 2018 departure from the Felicity Party (Saadet Partisi), which he accused of diluting core Islamist stances by forging alliances with secular opposition groups like the Nation Alliance, thereby compromising anti-Western and sharia-oriented policies.[40] This schism intensified electoral fragmentation, with YRP surpassing Saadet in 2024 locals—capturing over six times Saadet's vote share (<1%)—by branding itself the authentic successor to Necmettin Erbakan's Welfare Party legacy, emphasizing rigid conservatism on Gaza, trade boycotts with Israel, and rejection of PKK dialogues in favor of regional Islamist outreach.[17][39] While sporadic contacts occurred, such as Saadet visits to YRP in April 2024 and discussions on constitutional reforms by October 2025, underlying disputes over ideological purity persist, with Saadet struggling to reclaim YRP's voter niche amid perceptions of its own moderation.[41][42][43] This dynamic positions YRP as a hardline challenger not only to Saadet but to AKP's dominance in Islamist politics, potentially fragmenting conservative votes in future national contests.[17]

Secular and International Backlash

The New Welfare Party (YRP) has encountered opposition from Turkey's secular establishment, particularly over its advocacy for revising the constitutional definition of laiklik (secularism), which critics argue threatens the republic's foundational commitment to separating religion from state affairs. In June 2023, party leader Fatih Erbakan explicitly called for changes to the phrasing of secularism in the constitution, stating it requires a clearer definition to align with the party's vision of governance informed by Islamic principles.[44] Secular-leaning media outlets and opposition figures, including those aligned with the Republican People's Party (CHP), have framed such proposals as akin to the anti-secular activities that led to the dissolution of the party's predecessor, the Welfare Party, by Turkey's Constitutional Court in 1998.[9] Further domestic backlash stems from YRP's ultraconservative social stances, such as vehement opposition to LGBT rights and public health measures like COVID-19 vaccinations, which secular and liberal groups decry as regressive and incompatible with modern pluralistic values.[20] These positions, coupled with the party's electoral gains—securing over 6% of the national vote in the March 2024 local elections—have heightened fears among Atatürkist and Kemalist circles that YRP's rising influence could accelerate the erosion of secular norms in education, public spaces, and family law.[45][46] Internationally, YRP has faced condemnation primarily for Erbakan's antisemitic rhetoric, including claims in 2020 that "Zionism is a five-thousand-year-old bacteria" responsible for the COVID-19 pandemic, which the Anti-Defamation League identified as scapegoating minorities and fueling conspiracy theories.[47] The U.S. State Department highlighted similar statements in its 2020 human rights report, noting their potential to incite prejudice amid global health crises.[48] Western analysts have also expressed broader concerns about the party's hardline Islamist orientation, viewing its competition with the AKP as a symptom of deepening polarization that could hinder Turkey's integration with European institutions and exacerbate anti-Western sentiments.[17]

Impact and Future Outlook

Influence on Turkish Politics

The New Welfare Party (YRP) has influenced Turkish politics by eroding the Justice and Development Party's (AKP) monopoly on conservative and Islamist voters, particularly amid economic discontent and perceived policy compromises by the ruling party. In the March 31, 2024, local elections, YRP obtained 6.19% of the national vote, marking a significant rise from prior performances and enabling victories in key municipalities such as Şanlıurfa, a longtime AKP bastion with over 600,000 voters.[49][50] This vote split contributed to the AKP's worst local election defeat since 2001, with YRP siphoning support in conservative strongholds and tilting outcomes in competitive races.[51][52] YRP's leader, Fatih Erbakan, has amplified this impact through public critiques of the AKP, accusing it of nepotism, insufficient adherence to Islamist principles, and endorsement of initiatives like the Canal Istanbul project, which YRP opposes on environmental and ideological grounds. Initially allied with President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's People's Alliance during the 2023 general elections, YRP severed ties afterward, with Erbakan declaring no future partnerships and announcing his presidential candidacy for the next cycle on January 31, 2025.[39][38][26] This intra-Islamist competition has pressured the AKP to address voter grievances on issues like economic welfare and moral conservatism, potentially constraining its coalition flexibility and fostering a more fragmented right-wing bloc. Analysts note that YRP's emergence as a hardline alternative challenges Erdoğan's dominance in the Islamist spectrum, signaling a revival of multi-party dynamics and opportunities for opposition gains in future national contests.[17][53]

Prospects Amid Economic and Geopolitical Shifts

The New Welfare Party (YRP) positions itself as an advocate for Necmettin Erbakan's "Just Order" economic model, emphasizing interest-free banking, state-led heavy industry, and resource-sharing to address inequality, which contrasts with the Justice and Development Party's (AKP) recent orthodox tightening of monetary policy amid Turkey's persistent inflation.[21] As Turkey's annual inflation rate reached 33.29% in September 2025, with forecasts for 28.5% by year-end despite central bank hikes, public discontent with eroded purchasing power has fueled YRP's critique of AKP handling, enabling the party to capture votes from conservative bases frustrated by unfulfilled growth promises.[54] [55] YRP's 2025 medium-term economic program outlines alternative fiscal strategies, projecting appeal in a context where GDP growth is expected at 3.3% for the year, potentially positioning the party to expand beyond its 6.19% national vote share in the 2024 local elections by promising redistributive welfare without reliance on foreign capital inflows.[56] [57] [58] Geopolitically, YRP's ultraconservative platform, advocating Islamic unity and opposition to Western influence, aligns with Turkey's navigation of tensions in the Middle East, including the ongoing Gaza conflict, where the party's vocal support for Palestinian causes has resonated with Islamist voters disillusioned by perceived AKP moderation.[17] This stance, echoed in campaigns framing economic woes as tied to foreign policy compromises, contributed to YRP's third-place finish in 2024 locals, with leader Fatih Erbakan announcing a presidential candidacy for the next cycle, signaling ambitions to challenge AKP dominance amid Turkey's balancing of NATO commitments and relations with actors like Russia and Hamas affiliates.[58] [26] [59] Overall, these shifts offer YRP opportunities for growth, as evidenced by reported increases in membership and polling strength announced in October 2025, though its prospects hinge on sustaining intra-Islamist rivalries without alienating broader conservative coalitions, potentially enabling it to act as a kingmaker in future parliamentary dynamics if economic stabilization under AKP falters further.[60] [45]
