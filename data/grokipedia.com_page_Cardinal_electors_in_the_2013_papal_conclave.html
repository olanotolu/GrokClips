Cardinal electors in the 2013 conclave

The cardinal electors in the 2013 conclave were the 117 members of the College of Cardinals younger than 80 years on the date Pope Benedict XVI's resignation took effect, 28 February 2013, thereby qualifying them under canon law to vote for his successor in the papal election.[1][2] These electors, drawn exclusively from the ranks of bishops and priests elevated to the cardinalate by prior popes, convened in general congregations prior to the conclave and then sequestered themselves in the Vatican from 12 to 13 March 2013 to conduct the secret ballot in the Sistine Chapel.[3] Of the 117 eligible, 115 participated, with two absent due to health reasons, marking one of the largest voting bodies in modern conclave history.[1] Geographically diverse, the electors hailed from 48 countries across six inhabited continents, though Europe dominated with 52 percent of the total, followed by Latin America at 16 percent, Asia at 11 percent, Africa at 9 percent, North America at 9 percent, and Oceania at 3 percent.[4][5] Demographically, their average age was 71 years and 11 months, comprising five in their 50s, 38 in their 60s, and 74 in their 70s; approximately 67 had been appointed by Benedict XVI and 48 by John Paul II.[5][6] About 27 percent served in the Roman Curia or Vatican diplomatic roles, reflecting significant institutional influence within the body.[7] On the fifth ballot, they elected Cardinal Jorge Mario Bergoglio of Argentina as Pope Francis, the first pontiff from the Americas and a Jesuit.[8] This conclave's composition underscored a gradual shift toward global representation amid persistent European preponderance, shaped by recent consistories that expanded the college beyond the nominal limit of 120 electors.[4]

Eligibility and Selection Criteria

Age Limit and Canonical Rules

The eligibility of cardinals to serve as electors in a papal conclave is exclusively reserved to members of the College of Cardinals in communion with the Church, as stipulated in Canon 349 of the Code of Canon Law, which vests the right to elect the Roman Pontiff solely in the cardinals, subject to procedural norms. This right is qualified by the age restriction introduced by Pope Paul VI in the motu proprio Ingravescentem aetatem on November 21, 1970, which bars cardinals who have completed their 80th year from participating in the election of a pope, aiming to ensure the physical and deliberative capacity of electors amid the conclave's demands.[9] Pope John Paul II's apostolic constitution Universi Dominici Gregis, promulgated on February 22, 1996, and governing conclave procedures until modified in 2013, codified this limit in paragraph 33: only cardinals who have not reached their 80th birthday on the day the Apostolic See becomes vacantâ€”due to a pope's death or effective resignationâ€”may vote. Cardinals turning 80 during the interregnum or conclave itself retain eligibility if under 80 at the vacancy's onset, while those over 80 may attend preparatory general congregations to advise but are excluded from the conclave's secrecy and voting. Additional canonical requirements include being ordained bishops (per Canon 351 Â§1) and free from ecclesiastical penalties like excommunication that impede office-holding, though such cases are rare and would require papal intervention to disqualify. In the 2013 conclave, triggered by Pope Benedict XVI's resignation effective 8:00 p.m. on February 28, 2013 (with the See vacant from that moment), 117 cardinals qualified as electors, having not completed 80 years by February 28; the remaining approximately 90 cardinals over 80 were ineligible to vote despite their advisory role in preliminary sessions. This yielded the smallest relative proportion of electors since the age rule's inception, reflecting the College's aging demographics at the time. No electors were disqualified for other canonical reasons, ensuring all 117 entered the Sistine Chapel on March 12, 2013, minus two absentees due to health.

Appointment by Previous Popes

The cardinal electors eligible for the 2013 conclave were appointed exclusively by Popes John Paul II and Benedict XVI, as cardinals created by earlier popes had surpassed the age limit of 80 years established by Pope Paul VI in 1970 and reaffirmed by John Paul II in 1996. Appointments to the cardinalate occur at the sole discretion of the reigning pope, who selects priests or bishopsâ€”typically diocesan ordinaries, curial officials, or theologiansâ€”deemed suitable to advise on Church governance and doctrine, in accordance with Canon 351 of the Code of Canon Law. The pope announces the names during an ordinary public consistory, followed by a liturgical ceremony where the new cardinals receive the red biretta, ring, and titular church assignment, symbolizing their role as princes of the Church and potential electors in a conclave. Pope John Paul II, who reigned from 16 October 1978 until his death on 2 April 2005, exercised this prerogative through nine consistories, beginning with the one on 30 June 1979 and concluding with that of 21 October 2003.[10][11] His selections emphasized expanding the College's international scope, elevating leaders from missionary territories and those committed to the New Evangelization, while maintaining a core of European curial figures experienced in Vatican diplomacy amid the Cold War's end.[12] This approach replenished the college amid natural attrition, ensuring continuity in priorities such as moral theology and interreligious dialogue, without fixed quotas but guided by the informal norm of limiting electors to around 120 as set by Paul VI. Pope Benedict XVI, serving from 19 April 2005 until his resignation on 28 February 2013, convened five consistories to create additional cardinals: on 24 March 2006, 24 November 2007, 20 November 2010, 18 February 2012, and 24 November 2012.[13][14] His choices prioritized doctrinal clarity, liturgical reform, and strengthening ties with orthodox traditions, including elevations of figures from Eastern rites and non-European sees to counter secular influences in Europe.[15] These later consistories, particularly those in 2010 and 2012, addressed vacancies from aging or deceased electors, allowing Benedict to imprint his vision of hermeneutic continuity with Vatican II on a substantial portion of the voting college shortly before the conclave.[16] The discretionary nature of these appointments underscores the conclave's character as a body shaped by successive popes' judgments on ecclesial needs, rather than democratic election.

Numerical and Structural Composition

Total Eligible Electors

The eligibility of cardinals to vote in the 2013 papal conclave was governed by the apostolic constitution Universi Dominici Gregis, which specifies that only those who had not completed their 80th year of age by the day the Apostolic See became vacantâ€”February 28, 2013, following Pope Benedict XVI's resignationâ€”could participate as electors. This age threshold, established to ensure vitality in the electoral body while respecting seniority, resulted in 117 eligible electors from the College of Cardinals, which totaled 207 members at the time.[17][18] No cardinal appointments were made in the interregnum period between the resignation and the conclave's opening on March 12, 2013, preserving the fixed composition of the electorate. The 117 figure represented a slight increase from the 115 electors in the 2005 conclave, reflecting ongoing consistorial expansions under Popes John Paul II and Benedict XVI, though still below the nominal limit of 120 set by Paul VI in 1975 but often exceeded in practice due to the absence of a strict enforcement mechanism.[18] This total underscored the College's evolving size, driven by the absence of mandatory retirements beyond the voting age limit and periodic creations to balance representation.

Breakdown by Appointing Pope

Of the 117 cardinal electors eligible to participate in the 2013 conclave, 50 had been appointed by Pope John Paul II and 67 by his immediate successor, Pope Benedict XVI.[3] No electors remained from earlier pontificates, as cardinals created by Pope Paul VI or his predecessors had exceeded the age limit of 80 by the time of Benedict XVI's resignation on February 28, 2013.[3] [6] Two electors appointed by John Paul IIâ€”Julius Riyadi Darmaatmadja of Indonesia and Keith Michael Patrick O'Brien of Scotlandâ€”did not attend due to health issues and resignation amid scandal, respectively, leaving 115 participants: 48 appointed by John Paul II and all 67 appointed by Benedict XVI.[3] [6] Benedict's appointees thus formed a slim majority (58%) of the voting body, reflecting his nine consistories between 2006 and 2012, compared to John Paul II's 25-year reign that yielded a larger but aging pool of eligible cardinals by 2013.[7] [3]

Distribution by Continent

Of the 117 eligible cardinal electors for the 2013 conclave, Europe held the largest share with 60, comprising 51.3 percent of the total.[4] This figure included 28 from Italy alone.[4] Latin America followed with 20 electors, accounting for 17.1 percent.[4] North America had 14 electors, primarily from the United States (11) and Canada (3).[19] Africa and Asia each contributed 11 electors, representing 9.4 percent apiece.[19] Oceania had the smallest representation with 1 elector.[19] The following table summarizes the distribution:

Of the 115 cardinal electors who participated in the 2013 conclave, Italy supplied the largest contingent with 28, representing about 24% of the total.[19] The United States followed with 11 electors.[19] Brazil and Germany each contributed 6, while France, Spain, and India each provided 5.[19] Canada and Poland each had 3 electors.[19] Australia, Argentina, and the Philippines were represented by 2 each.[19] All other participating countries sent one elector apiece, resulting in representation from 48 nations across six continents.[19]

Participation in the Conclave

Attendance Figures

Of the 117 cardinal electors eligible under the age limit of 80 years as of February 28, 2013â€”the date of Pope Benedict XVI's resignationâ€”115 participated in the conclave that convened on March 12, 2013.[1][3] This figure represents near-complete attendance, consistent with the canonical expectation that electors convene in Rome barring insurmountable obstacles. The two absentees were Cardinal Julius Riyadi Darmaatmadja, archbishop emeritus of Jakarta, Indonesia, who cited progressive vision loss rendering travel and participation impossible; and Cardinal Keith Patrick O'Brien, archbishop emeritus of St. Andrews and Edinburgh, Scotland, who withdrew amid public allegations of inappropriate sexual conduct with priests dating to the 1980s, which surfaced in early March 2013.[20][21] Darmaatmadja's absence was announced in February 2013, reducing the expected voters to 116 before O'Brien's decision.[22] Both cases involved personal circumstances preventing fulfillment of the duty outlined in Universi Dominici Gregis, the apostolic constitution governing conclaves. No further absences occurred during the proceedings, ensuring the required two-thirds majority threshold of 77 votes for election was calculated on the basis of 115 ballots cast across four ballots on March 12 and one on March 13.[3] This turnout exceeded that of the 2005 conclave (115 of 115 eligible) and underscored the logistical coordination by the College of Cardinals, with general congregations held from March 4 to 11 confirming the participants' readiness.[1]

Absences and Justifications

Of the 117 cardinal electors eligible to participate in the 2013 conclave, two declined to attend, resulting in 115 participants.[20] The absences were justified on grounds of health impairment and personal circumstances amid scandal, respectively, with no evidence of broader patterns or institutional pressure influencing the decisions. Cardinal Julius Riyadi Darmaatmadja, the 78-year-old archbishop emeritus of Jakarta, Indonesia, cited progressive deterioration of his eyesight as rendering him unable to participate effectively.[22] He informed Catholic media outlets that his vision problems prevented involvement in the conclave's proceedings, requesting prayers from the faithful while expressing support for the electoral process from afar.[23] Darmaatmadja's decision was announced prior to the conclave's commencement on March 12, 2013, and was accepted without contest by Vatican authorities. Cardinal Keith O'Brien, the 75-year-old archbishop emeritus of Saint Andrews and Edinburgh, Scotland, resigned his position on February 18, 2013, amid allegations of inappropriate sexual conduct with three priests dating back to the 1980s, and subsequently withdrew from the conclave to avoid diverting attention from the proceedings.[24] O'Brien stated that his absence was self-imposed to prevent media focus on the accusations during the papal election, though he later acknowledged instances of sexual misconduct in a public apology on March 3, 2013.[25] The Vatican accepted his resignation and non-participation without further commentary on the matter's impact on the conclave's legitimacy.

Notable Electors and Influences

Prominent Figures and Pre-Conclave Positions

Among the cardinal electors eligible for the 2013 conclave, several emerged as prominent figures due to their leadership roles in the Roman Curia, oversight of major archdioceses, or influence in global Church affairs. Cardinal Angelo Scola, Archbishop of Milan since 2011, was widely regarded as a leading Italian candidate, having previously served as Patriarch of Venice and president of the Pontifical John Paul II Institute for Studies on Marriage and Family; his pastoral experience in northern Italy and theological writings positioned him as a continuity figure with Benedict XVI's intellectual legacy.[6][26] Similarly, Cardinal Marc Ouellet, Prefect of the Congregation for Bishops since 2010 and former President of the Pontifical Commission for Latin America, held significant sway over episcopal appointments worldwide, making him a key Curial insider viewed as papabile for his doctrinal orthodoxy and administrative expertise.[6][26] Cardinal Odilo Scherer, Archbishop of SÃ£o Paulo since 2007, represented the growing influence of Latin America among electors, with his role in Brazil's largest archdiocese highlighting demographic shifts in the Church; pre-conclave analyses noted his pastoral focus on social issues and familiarity with urban challenges as assets for addressing global Catholicism's southern pivot.[26][27] Cardinal Peter Turkson, Ghanaian President of the Pontifical Council for Justice and Peace since 2009, advocated prominently for economic justice and environmental concerns, positioning him as a potential first African pope amid discussions of diversifying leadership beyond Europe.[6][28] Other influential electors included Cardinal Christoph SchÃ¶nborn, Archbishop of Vienna since 1995 and editor of the Catechism of the Catholic Church, whose theological prominence and moderate stance on reforms drew attention; and Cardinal Timothy Dolan, Archbishop of New York since 2009, known for his media engagement and defense of religious liberty in the United States.[26][6] Cardinal Tarcisio Bertone, Vatican Secretary of State since 2006, exerted Curial influence despite criticisms of his diplomatic handling of scandals, underscoring tensions between administrative continuity and calls for Curial reform entering the conclave.[29] These figures' pre-conclave positions reflected a balance between European traditionalists, Curial officials, and voices from the Global South, shaping early discussions without a consensus favorite.[6]

Post-Election Assessments of Electoral Dynamics

The election of Cardinal Jorge Bergoglio as Pope Francis on March 13, 2013, after five ballots prompted analyses emphasizing a swift consolidation of support amid widespread dissatisfaction with the Roman Curia's governance scandals, including the Vatileaks affair that precipitated Benedict XVI's resignation. Observers noted that initial ballots likely favored Italian or European candidates such as Angelo Scola of Milan, reflecting lingering Curial influence, but a decisive shift occurred by the third and fourth ballots, with Bergoglio emerging as a consensus figure for reform-oriented cardinals seeking to break from Eurocentric traditions and address administrative dysfunction. This pivot was attributed to pre-conclave general congregations, where 115 electors (two absent) openly debated Curial reform, financial mismanagement, and the need for a pope untainted by Vatican intrigue, fostering a pragmatic alliance among non-Italian factions.[30] A central post-election assessment centered on informal lobbying networks, particularly the so-called "Team Bergoglio," described by biographer Austen Ivereigh as a loose coalition of reform-minded prelates, including overage cardinals like Cormac Murphy-O'Connor of Westminster, who organized discreet dinners and conversations to highlight Bergoglio's credentials as a pastoral outsider capable of decentralizing power. Ivereigh claimed this group secured non-binding endorsements from approximately 25 to 30 electors before the conclave's sequestration on March 12, framing Bergoglio as an antidote to the "self-referential" Curia criticized in Benedict's 2012 letter. Such activities, while not constituting formal campaigning prohibited by conclave oaths, raised questions about adherence to secrecy vows, as they involved strategic promotion below the radar of official proceedings.[31] Involved cardinals, including Murphy-O'Connor and Oscar Maradiaga of Honduras, rebutted allegations of organized canvassing, insisting interactions were exploratory discussions on electability rather than vote promises, with Maradiaga emphasizing post-election that Bergoglio's appeal stemmed organically from his 2005 runner-up status to Benedict XVI and reputation for humility. Critics, including canon lawyer Gerald Murray, argued that even informal coordination undermined the conclave's spirit of prayerful discernment, potentially invalidating the process under Universi Dominici Gregis rules against pacts, though no formal challenge ensued due to the election's two-thirds-plus-one majority (at least 77 votes). Conservative outlets like Rorate Caeli linked this to broader dynamics of progressive European influence overriding doctrinal conservatives, noting Bergoglio's final-ballot surge reflected tactical voting to avoid a prolonged deadlock.[32][33][34] Analyses also underscored demographic shifts among electorsâ€”52% European but with growing Latin American (14%) and Asian (11%) representationâ€”enabling a "global south" tilt that marginalized traditional Italian blocs, as evidenced by the rejection of Scola despite his pre-conclave frontrunner status in betting markets and media speculation. Network studies post-election modeled conclave dynamics as influence-driven graphs, where Bergoglio's relational ties, amplified by endorsements from figures like Walter Kasper, facilitated rapid vote transfers from initial favorites like Marc Ouellet or Odilo Scherer. Overall, the conclave's brevityâ€”concluding in under 36 hoursâ€”signaled less factional rigidity than in 2005, with causal factors including Benedict's unprecedented resignation creating urgency for renewal over continuity.[35][36]
