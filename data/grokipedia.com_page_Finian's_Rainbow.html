Finian's Rainbow


Finian's Rainbow is a musical fantasy comedy with a book by E. Y. Harburg and Fred Saidy, music by Burton Lane, and lyrics by Harburg.[1][2] The production premiered on Broadway at the 46th Street Theatre on January 10, 1947, and ran for 725 performances until October 2, 1948.[3][2] The story centers on Finian McLonergan, an Irish immigrant who steals a pot of gold from a leprechaun named Og and flees to the American South with his daughter Sharon, intending to bury the gold near Fort Knox in the belief it will multiply like the mythical rainbow's end.[1][2] Settling in the fictional Rainbow Valley, Missitucky, they encounter local tobacco sharecroppers facing foreclosure by a bigoted Senator, leading to romantic entanglements, magical transformationsâ€”including Og turning human and the Senator temporarily Black due to a wishâ€”and themes of racial prejudice and economic injustice blended with whimsical fantasy.[1][2] Notable songs include "How Are Things in Glocca Morra?", "Old Devil Moon", and "If This Isn't Love".[4] The original production, directed by Bretaigne Windust and choreographed by Michael Kidd, earned Tony Awards for Kidd's choreography and David Wayne's portrayal of Og as the first featured actor in a musical to win the honor, along with recognition for conductor Max Meth.[3][5] It was adapted into a 1968 film directed by Francis Ford Coppola, starring Fred Astaire as Finian, Petula Clark as Sharon, and Tommy Steele as Og, which grossed $11.6 million against a $3.5 million budget despite mixed reviews.[6] Revivals, including a 2009 Broadway production, have sustained its legacy, highlighting its innovative mix of Irish folklore, social commentary, and enduring score.[3][2]

Plot Summary

Act I

The action of Act I is set in the fictional Rainbow Valley, located in the state of Missitucky in the American South.[7][8] The valley's sharecroppers face imminent foreclosure on their land due to unpaid taxes, with the auction orchestrated by a corrupt sheriff acting on behalf of the bigoted Senator Billboard Rawkins, who seeks to exploit the property.[7] Among the sharecroppers is Susan Mahoney, a mute woman who co-owns part of the land and awaits the return of her brother Woody from the city.[7] Finian McLonergan, an elderly Irish immigrant, arrives in Rainbow Valley accompanied by his daughter Sharon, carrying a crock of gold stolen from a leprechaun in Ireland.[8][7] Finian intends to bury the gold near Fort Knox, believing its proximity to the U.S. Treasury will cause it to multiply in value, though Sharon expresses homesickness and skepticism about the plan.[7] Woody Mahoney returns to the valley short $70 needed to pay the taxes and delay the auction; Finian lends him the money from the gold, inadvertently making the McLonergans co-owners of the land.[7] Romantic interest develops between Sharon and Woody, leading to their hasty engagement amid misunderstandings.[7] Og, the leprechaun from whom Finian stole the gold, pursues them to Rainbow Valley, warning that without recovering the crock, he will lose his magical powers and become fully human.[8][7] Finian buries the gold in the valley, but Senator Rawkins, upon learning of its presence, attempts to seize the land to claim it.[7] In a pivotal moment, Sharon, unknowingly standing over the buried goldâ€”which has been placed upside down, reversing its usual cursing propertiesâ€”wishes that the senator would experience life as a black man to understand prejudice.[7] This utterance triggers the gold's magic, transforming Senator Rawkins into a black sharecropper.[7] Og begins to show signs of humanization, developing an attraction to Susan, while the initial conflicts over the land and the gold's pursuit intensify the stakes for all involved.[7]

The second act commences several weeks after the events of the first, in Rainbow Valley, where the sharecroppers, under the impression that the buried crock of gold has multiplied their wealth, extravagantly purchase goods on credit from local vendors, unpacking luxuries such as radios and fine clothing.[8] This false prosperity stems from Finian's earlier assurances of the gold's magical leading defenses against Rawkins' henchmen, blending collective action against economic eviction with the ongoing magical interference from Og, whose powers continue to wane due to prolonged separation from the crock.[8] Finian, realizing the gold's failure to grow near the initially planted site, relocates it in a desperate bid to activate its properties, though this scheme yields no survey, averting foreclosure and securing the land's economic viability independent of the crock's unreliable magic.[8] Finian returns the gold to Og, restoring the leprechaun's immortality and voice, which in turn allows Og to reverse the spell on Rawkins, who publicly endorses integration and withdraws opposition to the sharecroppers.[8] Concluding with weddingsâ€”Sharon to Woody and Og's Creation and Development

Background and Influences

E.Y. Harburg, who wrote the lyrics and co-authored the book with Fred Saidy, infused Finian's Rainbow with his socialist worldview, drawing from a career marked by politically charged works that critiqued economic inequality and prejudice. A lifelong advocate of democratic socialism, Harburg had previously penned Depression-era anthems like "Brother, Can You Spare a Dime?" and collaborated on socially conscious musicals such as Cabin in the Sky. His commitment to leftist ideals, evident in the show's satirical elements targeting capitalism and racism, stemmed from early influences including labor movements and the blacklist era's political pressures.[9][10][11] Composer Burton Lane contributed a score characterized by lush, melodic romanticism, building on his pre-war Hollywood experience and post-1940s Broadway successes. Lane's style, often described as elegantly tuneful and supportive of lyrical whimsy, aligned with the era's shift toward integrated book-musical forms amid wartime reflections on hope and fantasy. His partnership with Harburg produced standards like "Old Devil Moon," reflecting a melodic sophistication honed through earlier film scores.[12][13] The musical's conception blended Irish folkloreâ€”featuring leprechauns, enchanted gold, and mythical realmsâ€”with the harsh realities of sharecropping in the American South around 1947, where African American tenant farmers endured exploitative land tenancy and segregation under Jim Crow laws. This fusion allowed Harburg and Saidy to allegorize economic desperation and interracial dynamics through fantastical lenses.[14][15] Developed in the immediate post-World War II years, Finian's Rainbow emerged during a U.S. economic boom, with gross domestic product surging 20% from 1945 to 1947 and consumer spending rising amid suburban expansion, yet against a backdrop of entrenched racial tensions, including ongoing poll taxes, lynchings, and resistance to integration predating major Civil Rights milestones. Harburg's vision leveraged this optimism-tinged unease to explore utopian possibilities, countering capitalist excesses with folklore-driven equity narratives.[16][17]

Writing and Composition Process

E. Y. "Yip" Harburg collaborated with Fred Saidy on the libretto, infusing it with pointed satire targeting racial segregation under Jim Crow laws and economic inequities reminiscent of New Deal-era wealth redistribution debates, while weaving in fantastical elements like a leprechaun's pot of gold to allegorize misplaced faith in get-rich-quick schemes.[18][19] Harburg initially sought composer Harold Arlen for the score, but their partnership dissolved due to disagreements over the project's overtly political content.[20] Burton Lane then joined after a three-month deliberation, contributing music that incorporated Irish folk motifs to evoke the protagonist Finian's heritage and enhance the blend of whimsy and realism.[21] The creative team faced challenges in harmonizing the supernatural fantasyâ€”such as the reversing curse on the pot of goldâ€”with incisive commentary on Southern racism and speculative finance, ensuring the satire landed without alienating audiences amid postwar optimism.[22] Harburg and Saidy aimed to critique bigotry through the transformation of Senator Rawkins, a stand-in for entrenched prejudice, while avoiding didacticism by grounding it in romantic and comedic Irish immigrant tropes.[23] Lane's melodies, including lilting waltzes and ballads, supported this equilibrium, drawing from Celtic rhythms to underscore themes of displacement and hope without overpowering the libretto's edge.[11] Prior to its Broadway premiere on January 10, 1947, the production underwent out-of-town tryouts at Philadelphia's Erlanger Theatre, where audience responses prompted refinements to pacing and satirical bite, tightening the script to better integrate fantasy sequences with social critiques for smoother narrative flow.[24] These adjustments addressed feedback on balancing the show's whimsical tone against its topical provocations, ensuring the final version retained Harburg's subversive intent while broadening appeal.[25]

Characters and Casting

Principal Characters

Finian McLonergan is the central figure, portrayed as a crafty and optimistic elderly Irish immigrant who absconds with a leprechaun's pot of gold in the belief that burying it near Fort Knox will cause it to multiply and secure his wealth.[26] Driven by dreams of prosperity and a penchant for scheming, he relocates to rural Rainbow Valley in the fictional state of Missitucky with his daughter, where he navigates interactions with local sharecroppers while safeguarding his ill-gotten treasure.[27] His larger-than-life personality and elaborate plans extend to matchmaking for his daughter, reflecting a blend of folklore-inspired faith in magic and pragmatic opportunism.[27] Sharon McLonergan, Finian's devoted daughter, embodies youthful idealism as she accompanies her father to America, balancing familial loyalty with emerging romantic interests and community involvement among the valley's tobacco sharecroppers.[28] Her motivations center on pursuing personal happiness amid her father's pursuits, leading her to form a relationship with local farmer Woody Mahoney and participate in efforts to revitalize the struggling farm cooperative.[8] As a lead character, she serves as a bridge between her Irish heritage and the American setting, often expressing aspirations for love and stability through song.[28] Og, the leprechaun from whom Finian steals the gold, pursues the protagonists to reclaim his crock, motivated by the dire consequence of permanently transforming into a human if he fails.) Representing traditional Irish folklore in a whimsical yet determined form, Og's role involves magical interventions in human affairs, including spells that alter events in Rainbow Valley, while grappling with his fading powers and unexpected affections.[8] His pursuit highlights a contrast between supernatural innocence and the greed he encounters.[2] Senator Billboard Rawkins functions as the primary antagonist, depicted as a prejudiced Southern politician intent on foreclosing on the sharecroppers' land to exploit its supposed mineral wealth, driven by self-interest and opposition to progressive changes in the valley.[29] His traits include a reliance on sycophantic aides and a nostalgic fixation on a bygone era, positioning him in direct conflict with Finian and the locals until a transformative event forces reevaluation.[29] In the script, Rawkins embodies institutional resistance, with his arc tied to the unfolding magical and social dynamics.[27]

Supporting Roles

Woody Mahoney functions as the pragmatic anchor among the valley's residents, contrasting Finian's reliance on magical beliefs with his own resourceful, community-oriented approach to survival. As a landowner and informal union leader for the sharecroppers, Woody provides immediate aid to Finian and Sharon by offering them lodging on his property, while organizing resistance against external threats to their land, thereby propelling the narrative toward confrontation with authority figures. His romantic pursuit of Sharon integrates personal stakes into the broader communal struggle, highlighting tensions between individual aspirations and collective needs.[27][30] The sharecropper ensemble, comprising multiracial tobacco farmers like Harriet, Lucy, Howard, and others, drives the depiction of economic precarity through their interdependent dynamics, where shared labor and mutual support reveal both the grind of debt-ridden tenancy and flashes of defiant unity. These characters amplify conflicts by mobilizing as a group to challenge land auctions and exploitation, their interactions fostering a sense of grassroots solidarity that underscores the play's critique of systemic rural poverty without resolving it through fantasy alone. Individual ensemble members, such as the opinionated Harriet or hopeful Lucy, contribute nuanced voices to group deliberations, enriching the portrayal of hardship as a collective rather than isolated experience.[27][4] Buzz Collins, operating as Senator Rawkins' aide and local enforcer, embodies raw opportunism by pressuring residents over unpaid taxes and preparing land seizures, which intensifies the plot's central antagonism and exposes the mechanics of power abuse in rural settings. His role in terrorizing sharecroppers and handling menial tasks for the Senator reveals a sycophantic drive for personal gain, creating friction that tests the valley's resolve and draws in Finian's group to the escalating dispute. Through such actions, Collins heightens ensemble tensions, forcing characters like Woody to navigate alliances amid betrayal risks.[31]

Musical Composition and Songs

Score Overview

The score of Finian's Rainbow, composed by Burton Lane with lyrics by E. Y. Harburg, integrates Irish folk ballad traditions with jazz-inflected rhythms and Broadway melodic conventions, creating a versatile palette that supports the show's fusion of fantasy and social critique.[2] Lane's contributions number over 15 principal songs, many enduring as standards due to their lyrical simplicity and harmonic sophistication, such as the lilting, Celtic-evoking ballads juxtaposed against syncopated, improvisational jazz structures.[32] [33] This stylistic blend allows the music to fluidly transition between ethereal, folk-derived wistfulness and grounded, rhythmic vitality, reflecting the narrative's Irish immigrant perspective amid American socio-economic realities. Orchestrations by Robert Russell Bennett and Don Walker enhance these contrasts, employing a full Broadway pit ensemble to delineate whimsical, airy textures for fantastical elementsâ€”often with harp, flute, and string swells evoking Irish pastoralismâ€”from denser, brass-heavy underscoring for satirical sequences on racial prejudice and economic disparity.[34] [35] Bennett's arrangements, drawing on his extensive experience with tonal differentiation in shows like those of Rodgers and Hammerstein, utilize dynamic rhythmic layering to mirror thematic tensions, such as polyrhythmic pulses underscoring sharecropper struggles against smoother, waltz-like Irish lilt for romantic interludes.[36] This structural approach innovates within the era's musical theater by embedding causal narrative propulsion through musical motifs, where recurring rhythmic motifs signal escalating conflicts without relying on overt leitmotifs.[37]

Key Songs and Their Functions

How Are Things in Glocca Morra? is sung by Sharon shortly after arriving in Rainbow Valley, evoking her deep nostalgia for the mythical Irish village of Glocca Morra and underscoring the immigrant's sense of displacement and yearning for lost innocence amid the American landscape.[4] This opening number establishes the emotional core of the protagonists' journey, blending wistful fantasy with the reality of their new environment.[8] Old Devil Moon, a duet between Sharon and Woody, advances their budding romance by portraying the enchantment of newfound love under a fantastical night sky, where the moon symbolizes irresistible attraction and dreamlike possibility.[4] Performed as they connect despite social differences, the song heightens the narrative's blend of realism and whimsy, propelling the plot toward their engagement.[14] If This Isn't Love features the leprechaun Og interacting with Sharon, injecting humor through his unexpected emotional vulnerability and human-like infatuation, which humanizes the supernatural character and explores cross-species (or fantastical) rapport in a lighthearted manner.[4] This duet follows moments of misunderstanding, using playful dialogue in song to resolve tension and affirm budding affections, albeit comically mismatched ones.[14] When the Idle Poor Become the Idle Rich is delivered by Sharon and the ensemble after the crocus field unexpectedly yields wealth, satirizing how sudden fortune inverts social hierarchies and exposes hypocrisies in class relations among the sharecroppers and locals.[4] The number functions to escalate the plot's chaos from the pot of gold's wishes, highlighting economic absurdity while the characters revel in their temporary reversal of fortunes.[8]

Themes and Symbolism

Economic and Political Satire

The musical critiques economic greed through the central motif of the leprechaun's pot of gold, which Finian steals and buries in the belief it will multiply in value near Fort Knox, parodying contemporary faith in the gold standard as a mechanism for wealth creation amid post-World War II monetary debates.[38] The crock's magical propertiesâ€”doubling under conditions of harmony but turning to lead when touched by avariceâ€”serve as an allegory for how distorted incentives, such as coercive redistribution, erode productive value rather than generate it, as evidenced when attempts to exploit the gold for quick gain disrupt the valley's emerging prosperity.[39] This narrative device underscores a first-principles view that sustainable wealth arises from voluntary cooperation and sound property incentives, not fiat manipulations or envious seizures, though the show's utopian resolution overlooks real-world causal chains where such interventions historically fueled inflation and misallocation, as seen in the era's Bretton Woods tensions over gold convertibility.[40] Sharecropping in Rainbow Valley exemplifies the show's portrayal of agrarian stagnation as a consequence of misaligned incentives under fragmented land tenure, where tenant farmers, lacking secure ownership, invest minimally in infertile soil, yielding perpetual poverty despite collective labor.[41] The valley's tenants represent the post-Depression Southern economy, where Agricultural Adjustment Act payments from 1933 onward often benefited landlords over sharecroppers, exacerbating dependency and reducing output incentives, with data showing tenant farm numbers declining from 2.7 million in 1930 to under 1 million by 1950 due to mechanization and displacement.[38] Contrasted with Woody's individual enterprise in experimenting with tobacco cultivation, the satire implies that collectivist arrangements without personal stakes foster inefficiency, a critique rooted in observable failures of tenure systems that prioritize redistribution over entrepreneurial risk, though librettist E.Y. Harburg's progressive leaningsâ€”later leading to his 1950s blacklistingâ€”frame this as capitalist exploitation rather than incentive misalignment.[10] Senator Billboard Rawkins embodies political overreach, plotting to condemn Woody's land via archaic racial covenants to access rumored gold deposits, mirroring 1940s Southern statutes that enabled eminent domain abuses against minority-held properties and stifled market-driven land use.[42] His scheme parallels real policies like restrictive covenants upheld until the 1948 Shelley v. Kraemer Supreme Court ruling, which invalidated state enforcement of such deeds, and federal programs that inadvertently displaced sharecroppers through subsidized consolidation.[16] The character's downfall via the gold's curse highlights how government intervention motivated by ideological biasâ€”rather than impartial rule of lawâ€”distorts economic signals and property rights, ultimately harming the intervener, aligning with causal critiques that overreach erodes trust and investment, as evidenced by slowed Southern agricultural growth rates averaging under 1% annually in the 1940s amid regulatory entanglements.[38]

Racial and Social Dynamics

In Finian's Rainbow, racial prejudice is embodied by Senator Billboard Rawkins, a Southern politician who fabricates a Jim Crow statute to dispossess black sharecroppers of their collective land claim in Rainbow Valley, reflecting the era's legalized discrimination against African Americans.[43] Rawkins' overt racism manifests in his opposition to interracial cooperation and his support for segregationist policies, positioning him as the primary antagonist driving racial conflict.[39] When Sharon McLonergan expresses outrage at his bigotry by wishing he were black to understand its burdens, the leprechaun Og enacts the transformation via magic, temporarily rendering Rawkins African American and compelling him to endure the prejudice he previously endorsed.[40] This reversal, depicted through blackface in the 1947 original production, culminates in Rawkins' reversal of stance, as he advocates for racial integration upon reverting to his original appearance.[23] The sharecroppers in Rainbow Valley, portrayed as a black community facing foreclosure, demonstrate idealized unity with white counterparts to resist dispossession, framing interracial solidarity as a antidote to racially tinged economic threats.[18] This depiction satirizes Southern racial hierarchies while promoting integration through shared adversity, with the sharecroppers' collective action undermining Rawkins' discriminatory schemes.[44] Yet such harmony diverged from 1947 realities, when Jim Crow laws enforced segregation in public facilities, schools, and employment across the South, with federal challenges only emerging via Truman's 1948 military desegregation and persisting resistance until the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education decision and 1964 Civil Rights Act dismantled core legal structures.[45][46] Immigration dynamics appear through Finian McLonergan, an Irish Ã©migrÃ© who flees to America with a crock of leprechaun gold, intent on burying it near Fort Knox to multiply his wealth amid perceived boundless opportunity.[30] Finian's arc embodies an optimistic assimilation narrative, as he and his daughter Sharon integrate into Missitucky's community, leveraging folklore and determination to navigate cultural displacement.[40] This contrasts with historical Irish immigrant experiences, where 19th-century waves encountered nativist hostility, poverty, and anti-Catholic bias, though by the 1940sâ€”following waves of economic mobility and cultural acceptanceâ€”newer arrivals faced diminished barriers to assimilation relative to earlier eras.[47]

Fantasy Elements and Realism

The fantasy elements of Finian's Rainbow draw directly from Irish leprechaun lore, portraying these entities as elusive fairies who guard pots of goldâ€”often concealed at rainbow's endsâ€”and concede three wishes to humans who capture and steadfastly gaze upon them without distraction.[48] In the musical, this tradition manifests through Og, the leprechaun whose crock Finian steals, grounding the narrative's claim to Irish cultural heritage amid themes of immigrant aspiration; Finian's scheme to bury the gold near Fort Knox presumes it will multiply like folklore-treasured wealth, symbolizing displaced Irish dreams of abundance in the New World.[14] Magical mechanics drive key plot advancements, with the crock enabling wishes that avert economic ruinâ€”Sharon's plea halts the sharecroppers' farm foreclosureâ€”and punitive enchantments, as Senator Rawkins' avowed racism provokes his instantaneous transformation into a Black man upon touching the gold, compelling perspective-taking before a reversal restores him.[39] This spell, tied to the crock's aversion to prejudice, resolves interpersonal and societal tensions through supernatural fiat, blending whimsy with the satire on bigotry and exploitation.[43] Such devices, however, diverge from causal realism by eliding verifiable pathways to resolutionâ€”like courtroom defenses for land tenure or incremental prejudice erosion via policy and discourseâ€”in favor of arbitrary interventions that evade empirical testing or human agency. Premiering in 1947, amid U.S. post-war rebound evidenced by a 3.9% national unemployment rate and agricultural mechanization gains in the South, the story's escapist arc jars against persistent realities like sharecropping dependencies and legally enshrined segregation, where durable fixes hinged on legislative battles (e.g., eventual 1954 desegregation precedents) rather than invoked charms.[49] This inherent tension posits fantasy as narrative convenience, potentially tempering the work's critique by implying systemic inequities yield to mythic expedience over methodical reform.[50]

Criticisms and Controversies

Dated Racial Portrayals

In the original 1947 Broadway production of Finian's Rainbow, the character of Senator Rawkins, a racist white politician, undergoes a magical transformation into a black man, portrayed by the white actor Donald Richards using blackface makeup.[44] This depiction was intended to illustrate the senator's moral reckoning through experiencing racial prejudice firsthand, but it relied on burnt cork traditions associated with minstrel shows.[43] The sharecropper ensemble, consisting of black characters in Rainbow Valley, featured dance sequences that critics later described as evoking minstrel stereotypes, including synchronized routines emphasizing rural poverty and rhythmic movements amid interracial harmony themes.[41] The 1968 film adaptation retained the blackface transformation for Senator Rawkins, played by white actor Albert Sharpe, who appeared in dark makeup after the spell, drawing scrutiny in the post-Civil Rights era for perpetuating visual tropes of racial caricature despite the narrative's anti-bigotry intent.[43] Contemporary reviews noted the film's unchanged racial dynamics, including sharecropper portrayals under Jim Crow-like oppression, as jarringly dated against evolving social norms, with the magical reversal of the senator's skin color underscoring a reliance on physical alteration over substantive empathy.[19] Revival attempts from the late 20th century onward faced challenges due to these elements, with producers citing blackface as tantamount to endorsing racism and the sharecropper scenes as offensively stereotypical, leading to multiple projects being abandoned.[44] By the 2009 Broadway revival, directors avoided blackface by employing dual castingâ€”one white actor for Rawkins pre-transformation and a black actor post-transformationâ€”to sidestep the visual offense while preserving the plot, though some audiences misinterpreted the original as pro-racism.[40] A 2016 production similarly updated slurs and depictions but highlighted persistent hesitancy over the core tropes' alignment with modern sensibilities.[41][25]

Ideological Biases and Utopianism

Finian's Rainbow embodies lyricist E. Y. Harburg's socialist convictions, portraying capitalist greed as the root of inequality and advocating redistribution through leprechaun magic as a corrective, a narrative that prioritizes egalitarian fantasy over the practical incentives driving economic activity.[51][44] Harburg, blacklisted for his leftist views, critiqued sharecropping and corporate exploitation via songs like "When the Idle Poor Become the Idle Rich," implying wealth transfer alone suffices for prosperity without addressing how such schemes distort individual motivations for innovation and labor.[39] This utopianism assumes human behavior bends to ideological fiat, disregarding evidence that voluntary exchange under market rules fosters sustained output. The musical's anti-capitalist satire, normalized in progressive circles despite Harburg's ideological slant, contrasts sharply with post-premiere realities: from 1948 onward, U.S. GDP grew at an average annual rate of 3.15%, propelled by private investment and consumer demand in a largely unregulated capitalist framework, elevating median incomes and debunking the premise that systemic avarice precludes broad-based gains absent supernatural intervention.[52][53] Academic endorsements of the work's "socialist rhetoric" for an integrated utopia often emanate from institutions exhibiting left-wing predispositions, potentially underemphasizing how the fantasy resolution evades empirical tests of policy-induced incentive misalignments observed in redistributive experiments elsewhere.[54] In depicting racial prejudice dissolving via attitudinal reversal and communal windfall, the show over-idealizes harmony as achievable through wishful symbolism, sidelining causal drivers like familial and institutional incentives that data reveal as pivotal to disparate group outcomes, rather than mere prejudice or economic spells.[15] Such portrayals, while aspirational, reflect Harburg's bias toward collectivist solutions unverified by the era's advancing civil rights amid capitalist expansion, where real integration hinged on legal enforcement and opportunity structures over magical equity.[10]

Original Production and Reception

Broadway Premiere

Finian's Rainbow premiered on Broadway on January 10, 1947, at the 46th Street Theatre (now the Richard Rodgers Theatre), where it ran for 725 performances until October 2, 1948.[3][2] The production was directed by Bretaigne Windust and choreographed by Michael Kidd, whose innovative dance sequences integrated the show's blend of Irish folklore and American social commentary.[3][1] The original cast featured Ella Logan as Sharon McLonergan, Albert Sharpe as Finian McLonergan, Donald Richards as Woody Mahoney, and David Wayne as the leprechaun Og the Silent.[3][2] Logistical aspects included production oversight by Lee Sabinson and William Katzell, with musical direction by Milton Rosenstock.[3] Technical elements emphasized the musical's fantastical Southern milieu, with scenic design by Jo Mielziner creating lush, evocative landscapes of Rainbow Valley, Kentucky, that transitioned seamlessly between realism and magic; costumes by Irene Sharaff; and lighting by Hassard Short to highlight whimsical transformations.[3] These designs supported the narrative's causal interplay of economic hardship, racial tensions, and supernatural intervention without relying on overt spectacle.[3]

Initial Critical and Commercial Response

Upon its Broadway opening on January 10, 1947, at the 46th Street Theatre, Finian's Rainbow garnered enthusiastic critical praise for its innovative blend of fantasy, melody, and satire. Brooks Atkinson in The New York Times lauded the book by E.Y. Harburg and Fred Saidy as an "original and humorous fantasy" that discarded much of the "buncombe of the traditional musical show," emphasizing its fresh integration of Irish folklore with American social commentary.[55] Reviewers highlighted the score by Burton Lane and Harburg's lyrics, with songs like "How Are Things in Glocca Morra?" and "Old Devil Moon" cited for their lyrical charm and emotional depth, contributing to the production's whimsical appeal.[4] While the fantasy elements and musical numbers were strengths, some critics noted unevenness in the satire, particularly the abrupt shifts between lighthearted leprechaun antics and heavier themes of racism and economic injustice, which occasionally strained narrative coherence.[56] Nonetheless, the overall reception affirmed the show's pioneering use of an integrated cast, including Black actors in prominent roles, as a bold step amid postwar racial tensions.[57] Commercially, the production proved a hit, sustaining 725 performances through October 2, 1948, amid Broadway's gradual recovery from wartime disruptions.[3] This extended run reflected strong audience demand for escapist fare blending romance and optimism, drawing theatergoers seeking relief from recent global hardships. The show's success culminated in six Donaldson Awards, including best musical and direction, voted by industry professionals and signaling broad esteem.[58]

Subsequent Productions

Early Revivals and London Staging

A London production of Finian's Rainbow opened at the Palace Theatre on October 21, 1947, directed by William Sanford and starring Irish tenor Josef Locke as Finian McLonergan, with a run of 55 performances closing on December 6, 1947.[59][60] This brief engagement contrasted sharply with the Broadway original's 725 performances, amid post-war London's theater scene favoring lighter revues and native works over imported American musicals blending fantasy satire.[59] In the United States, following the Broadway production's closure on October 2, 1948, a national tour launched that year under the auspices of the original producers, featuring Joe Yule Sr. (father of Mickey Rooney) as Finian and Mimi Kelly as Sharon, performing in venues including the Philharmonic Auditorium in Los Angeles starting September 26, 1949.[61][62] The tour, which extended into regional theaters, drew mixed attendance, with the musical's progressive interracial choreography and sharecropper ensembleâ€”innovative for 1947 Broadway but rooted in stylized fantasyâ€”occasionally prompting local adjustments or resistance in conservative markets.[61] The Los Angeles Civic Light Opera Association presented a separate staging in 1949 at the Philharmonic Auditorium, again starring Joe Yule Sr., as part of its series of post-Broadway imports, though specific performance counts remain undocumented beyond the tour's overlap.[63] These pre-1955 efforts totaled fewer than 100 documented performances outside New York, reflecting logistical hurdles for road shows of elaborate productions requiring 30-plus cast members and the era's limited touring infrastructure.[61] Abroad, beyond London, amateur and stock attempts in Europe faced similar constraints, with the show's leprechaun lore and racial utopianism less resonant in non-American contexts.[64]

Major 20th-Century Revivals

The New York City Center Light Opera Company mounted the first major revival of Finian's Rainbow in 1955 at City Center, presenting a limited engagement that emphasized the musical's score while navigating post-original production challenges.[65] This staging, directed by Robert Lewis, featured a cast including Howard Da Silva as Finian and ran for 16 performances from May 25 to June 5.[65] A subsequent City Center production in 1960 opened on April 27 for an initial 15-performance run before transferring to Broadway's 46th Street Theatre on May 3, where it continued until August 20 for a total of 107 performances.[66] Directed by George Balanchine with choreography by Michael Kidd, the revival starred Bert Lahr as Finian, Jeannie Carson as Sharon, and Howard Morris as Og, preserving much of the original's fantasy elements and songs like "Old Devil Moon" amid a runtime of approximately 2 hours and 30 minutes.[66][67] The third City Center revival occurred in 1967, running from April 5 to 23 under the direction of Robert Tucker, with Frank McHugh as Finian and Nancy Dussault as Sharon.[68][69] This 19-performance engagement further demonstrated the score's resilience, though the production remained confined to the venue without a Broadway transfer.[68] Beyond New York, regional theaters sporadically revived the musical in the 1970s through 1990s, often highlighting the Burton Lane-E.Y. Harburg songs' appeal despite thematic obstacles. Notable examples include the 1974 staging at Milwaukee's Melody Top Theatre, the 1977 outdoor production at Jones Beach Theatre, and a 1997 mounting at Connecticut's Goodspeed Opera House from April 25 to July 4.[64][70] The Goodspeed version, directed by Gregory Boyd, contended with the show's racial stereotypesâ€”described in production notes as a "hot potato" inhibiting frequent revivalsâ€”yet underscored the "glorious score" with memorable melodies like "How Are Things in Glocca Morra?"[38][71] Production records reflect the relative rarity of these efforts, with dated portrayals contributing to infrequent full stagings, though the music's quality sustained interest in select venues.[38][64]

21st-Century Revivals and Adaptations

A revival of Finian's Rainbow was presented in concert format by Encores! at New York City Center from March 10 to 22, 2009, directed by Warren Carlyle and starring Jim Norton as Finian McLonergan, Kate Baldwin as Sharon, and Cheyenne Jackson as Woody Mahoney.[72] The production transferred to Broadway's St. James Theatre, opening on October 29, 2009, and running for 117 performances.[73] It received Tony Award nominations for Best Revival of a Musical, Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Musical (Baldwin), and Best Performance by a Featured Actor in a Musical (Norton).[73] In London, a fully staged revival directed by Phil Willmott opened at the Union Theatre on February 12, 2014, and ran through March 15, featuring a cast including Robert Tripps as Finian and Louise Pentland as Sharon.[74] The production later transferred to the Charing Cross Theatre, closing on May 10, 2014, where it was noted for its energetic choreography and vocal performances amid the show's fantastical plot elements.[75] An Off-Broadway revival at the Irish Repertory Theatre, directed by Charlotte Moore and opening on October 20, 2016, featured a tightened script by the director and Greg MacKellan to streamline the narrative and mitigate dated racial stereotypes, such as the original's portrayal of Senator Rawkins and sharecropper dynamics.[76] Starring Melissa Errico as Sharon, the production emphasized the musical's satire on racism and economic inequality through a biracial community subplot, running for 56 performances and receiving praise for clarifying the story's progressive intent despite its 1947 origins.[41][77] No major professional revivals have occurred since 2016, attributable in part to ongoing scrutiny of the show's racial portrayals and utopian themes, which some critics argue resist full modernization without substantial rewriting.[40]

Film Adaptation

1968 Film Version

The 1968 film adaptation of Finian's Rainbow was directed by Francis Ford Coppola in his first major studio musical project, produced by Warner Bros.-Seven Arts with a budget of $3.5 million.[78] The screenplay was written by the original librettist E.Y. Harburg and Fred Saidy, who adapted their 1947 stage work for the screen.[6] Principal photography occurred primarily on soundstages and backlots, with location shooting in California's San Fernando Valley to evoke the rural Fort Knox setting.[79] The film premiered as a roadshow attraction on October 2, 1968, in 70mm format after being originally shot in 35mm, which affected the framing of some dance sequences by cropping tops and bottoms.[79] Fred Astaire starred as Finian McLonergan in what was his penultimate feature film appearance and final musical role, portraying the Irish immigrant with a blend of charm and whimsy; Petula Clark played his daughter Sharon, Tommy Steele embodied the leprechaun Og with acrobatic energy, Don Francks appeared as Woody Mahoney, and Keenan Wynn as Senator Bill Rawkins.[79] Choreography was handled in part by Hermes Pan, Astaire's longtime collaborator, who contributed to expanded production numbers like "Old Devil Moon" and the finale "How Are Things in Glocca Morra?", though Pan departed mid-production due to creative differences with Coppola.[6] These sequences emphasized Astaire's tap dancing and ensemble routines, scaled up for cinematic spectacle with larger sets and crowds compared to stage constraints.[80] To realize the story's magical elements, the production employed practical effects and early optical techniques, including matte paintings for fantastical landscapes, stop-motion for Og's powers (such as accelerating plant growth), and makeup transformations, notably Wynn's character turning "black" via a curse, rendered with blackface prosthetics that replicated the stage original's depiction but later drew criticism for perpetuating racial minstrelsy tropes.[81] Despite the $11.6 million domestic grossâ€”yielding a profitâ€”the film underperformed expectations for a high-profile musical roadshow, hampered by mixed reviews on its whimsical tone and dated racial portrayals amid 1960s civil rights sensitivities.[80][78]

Differences from Stage Original

The 1968 film adaptation of Finian's Rainbow omitted several elements from the original 1947 stage musical to accommodate the cinematic format and runtime constraints, including the Act Two appearance of catalog-store magnates "Shears and Robust" and the young hero Woody's union activism, which reduced the depth of economic and labor satire present in the stage version.[19] A new subplot involving cross-breeding tobacco and mint plants for mentholated cigarettes was added, shifting some focus toward agricultural innovation rather than the stage's broader critique of sharecropping and poverty.[19] Character portrayals were altered for screen dynamics; the leprechaun Og, played by Tommy Steele, was depicted as brash and extroverted, contrasting the more timid stage incarnation, while Fred Astaire's Finian adopted a broader comedic style beyond the original's straightforward humor.[19] Several songs from the stage score were cut or modified, including "If This Isn't Love" and "The Begat," with "Necessity" recorded for the soundtrack but excised from the final 145-minute film to shorten its length after previews.[19][82] "When the Idle Poor Become the Idle Rich" was retained but reassigned solo lines to Finian, originally Sharon's number, altering its ensemble dynamic and diluting the stage's pointed commentary on wealth disparity.[19] Choreography was expanded to showcase Astaire's dancing, with sequences filmed under Hermes Pan before his dismissal, though the enlargement to 70mm format cropped some footwork, adapting stage numbers like "When the Idle Poor Become the Idle Rich" for visual spectacle suited to film.[83][19] The film's tone softened the stage musical's vigorous satire on reactionary politics, consumerism, and economic inequality to appeal to a wider audience, minimizing elements like sharecropping critiques while clashing director Francis Ford Coppola's realistic style against the original's whimsy.[19] The racial transformation of Senator Rawkins into a Black manâ€”intended in the stage as a device to expose prejudiceâ€”remained but drew amplified contemporary criticism in 1968 for its magical-race mechanics amid heightened civil rights sensitivities, shifting reception from bold confrontation to perceived insensitivity.[84][85] These adjustments prioritized fantastical visuals and star performances over the stage's integrated sociopolitical bite.[19]

Legacy and Cultural Impact

Awards and Honors

The original 1947 Broadway production received Tony Awards for Best Choreography (Michael Kidd) and Best Featured Actor in a Musical (David Wayne) in 1947, as well as a Tony for Best Conductor and Musical Director (Max Meth) in 1948.[86] David Wayne also earned a Theatre World Award for his performance.[3] The 2009 Broadway revival garnered three Tony Award nominations in 2010: Best Revival of a Musical, Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Musical (Kate Baldwin), and Best Performance by a Featured Actor in a Musical (Christopher Fitzgerald).[87][73] The 1968 film adaptation received Academy Award nominations for Best Original Score (Ray Heindorf) and Best Sound (Warner Bros.-Seven Arts Studio).[88][6]

Recordings and Performances

The original Broadway cast recording of Finian's Rainbow was made on March 30, 1947, and released by Columbia Records as a six-disc 78 rpm set, marking the label's first full recording of a Broadway musical.[89] Featuring principal performers Ella Logan as Finian McLonergan, Donald Richards as Og, and David Wayne as Senator Playboy Bilbo, the album captured the score shortly after the show's January 10 premiere and included 16 tracks such as the overture and ensemble numbers.[4] A 2000 reissue on compact disc restored omitted sections and improved audio fidelity from the original masters.[90] A cast album from the 1960 revival, which began at New York City Center on April 27 before transferring briefly to Broadway, was recorded in stereo by RCA Victor and released that year.[7] Directed by Herbert Ross, it starred Jeannie Carson as Sharon, Carol Brice in a supporting role, Bobby Howes as Finian, and Howard Morris as Og, preserving 14 tracks including the overture conducted by Max Meth.[91] The 2009 Broadway revival, a transfer from City Center Encores! that opened October 29, yielded a cast recording released on February 2, 2010, by PS Classics.[92] With Jim Norton as Finian, Kate Baldwin as Sharon, Cheyenne Jackson as Woody, and Christopher Fitzgerald as Og, the album featured 20 tracks highlighting the full score in a modern production context.[93] Due to the musical's infrequent revivals, documented recordings remain limited primarily to these principal cast albums, with few studio or live captures beyond early ancillary efforts like a 1946 demo version or Ella Logan's 1954 solo selections from the score.[94]

Influence on Later Works

Finian's Rainbow's innovative blend of leprechaun fantasy with critiques of racism and economic greed influenced early parodies and contributed to the trajectory of social-issue musicals in the Golden Age. In 1948, Stephen Sondheim, as a Williams College student, composed Phinney's Rainbow, a satirical musical on campus life whose title directly referenced the Broadway hit, combining "Finian's" with college president James Phinney Baxter III's name.[95] This early echo highlights the show's immediate cultural footprint among emerging talents. The musical also exemplified early Golden Age works addressing racial tolerance, paving conceptual ground for later productions like South Pacific (1949), though explicit causal links remain undocumented.[96] Composer Burton Lane's lush, Irish-inflected score for Finian's Rainbow, featuring standards like "Old Devil Moon," shaped subsequent Broadway compositions, including Lane's own On a Clear Day You Can See Forever (1965), with reviewers crediting its 1947 innovations for broader genre advancements.[97] However, the show's portrayal of Black sharecroppers and temporary racial transformation motifs, progressive for 1947, has rendered it a cautionary precedent amid post-1960s scrutiny of stereotypes, constraining emulation in fantasy musicals favoring updated sensitivities over whimsical satire.[98] This ideological evolution has confined its echoes largely to thematic precedents rather than structural or narrative derivations in enduring hits.
