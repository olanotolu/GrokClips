Howard W. French


Howard W. French (born October 14, 1957) is an American journalist, author, and professor specializing in international affairs, with a focus on Africa, China, and global history.[1][2] French began his career as a foreign correspondent, serving as a reporter and bureau chief for The New York Times in regions including Central America and the Caribbean, West and Central Africa, and Tokyo, where he covered pivotal events in post-colonial development and geopolitical shifts.[3][4] His reporting emphasized on-the-ground analysis over official narratives, drawing from extensive fieldwork across dozens of countries. Transitioning to academia, he joined the Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism faculty in 2008, teaching investigative and international journalism.[2][5] French has authored five books, including A Continent for the Taking: The Tragedy and Hope of Africa (2004), which critiques post-independence governance failures; China's Second Continent: How a Million Migrants Are Building a New Empire in Africa (2014), examining Chinese economic expansion in Africa through migrant entrepreneurship rather than state-driven industrialization; and the award-winning Born in Blackness: Africa, Africans, and the Making of the Modern World, 1471 to the Second World War (2021), which argues that African resources and agency were foundational to Europe's rise and the global economy, challenging Eurocentric historical accounts.[6][7][8] His forthcoming work, The Second Emancipation: Nkrumah, Pan-Africanism, and Global Blackness at High Tide (2025), reevaluates Kwame Nkrumah's legacy in pan-African unity amid Cold War dynamics.[6] French's scholarship prioritizes primary evidence from archives and interviews, often countering prevailing orthodoxies in media and academia that downplay African centrality in world history.[9]

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Howard W. French was born on October 14, 1957, in Washington, D.C., to Carolyn Alverda Howard French and David Marshall French, a physician specializing in pediatric surgery and public health.[1][10] His father, who had trained at Howard University and participated in providing medical care to civil rights activists during the 1960s marches, later accepted an assignment to direct a regional hospital in Ivory Coast, prompting the family's relocation there.[11][10] This move immersed French in West African life during his formative years, fostering an early affinity for the continent that would shape his career.[10] As an African American, French traces his ancestry to enslaved individuals on both sides of his family, a heritage that informed his later scholarly focus on Africa's global historical role.[9] While younger siblings resided full-time with their parents in Abidjan, attending local French-language schools, French pursued education in the United States, driven by determination amid the contrasts of his family's transnational circumstances.[12] His father's professional commitments in Africa, combined with a U.S.-based upbringing, exposed him to bilingual environments and cross-cultural dynamics from an early age.[10]

Academic Training

Howard W. French earned a Bachelor of Arts degree from the University of Massachusetts Amherst in 1979.[1][13] Following his undergraduate studies, French pursued professional experiences in West Africa rather than advanced formal academic degrees, including work as a French-English translator in Abidjan, CÃ´te d'Ivoire, in the early 1980s.[2] No graduate-level coursework or degrees in journalism, history, or related fields are documented in biographical records from reputable institutions.[1] In later years, French received an honorary doctorate from the University of Maryland, acknowledging his journalistic and scholarly contributions rather than additional academic training.[13]

Journalism Career

Freelance Beginnings in Africa

French's entry into journalism occurred in West Africa during the early 1980s, following his relocation to the region after completing his education. Based in Abidjan, Ivory Coast, he initially worked as a French-English translator and taught English literature at the University of Abidjan for several years, which provided immersion in local languages and cultures essential for reporting.[14] These roles facilitated his transition to freelance reporting, allowing him to leverage bilingual skills amid the linguistic diversity of Francophone West Africa.[1] From 1982 to 1986, French freelanced for The Washington Post and other outlets, including Africa Report, covering events across West Africa.[1] He traveled extensively on the continent, submitting pieces to American publications that highlighted underreported stories from the region, such as political transitions and social dynamics in post-colonial states.[15] This period, spanning roughly four years before his hiring by The New York Times in 1986, honed his expertise in on-the-ground reporting amid challenging environments marked by instability and limited infrastructure.[2][14] His freelance work emphasized firsthand observation over institutional narratives, reflecting a commitment to direct engagement with African realities rather than remote analysis prevalent in Western media at the time.[13] Specific contributions included dispatches on economic and cultural shifts, though exact article titles from this phase remain sparsely documented in public records. This foundational experience in West Africa, distinct from later bureau-chief assignments, underscored the logistical demands of independent journalism in remote areas, including self-funding travel and navigating censorship.[16]

New York Times Correspondent Roles

French joined The New York Times in 1986 as a metropolitan reporter based in New York City, where he covered local stories for three years.[3] In 1990, he shifted to foreign correspondence, serving as bureau chief in Central America and the Caribbean during the early 1990s, with reporting focused on political upheavals in Haiti, Cuba, and regional conflicts.[1] [13] Subsequently, French became bureau chief for West and Central Africa, overseeing coverage of post-colonial developments, civil wars, and economic challenges across the region, drawing on his prior freelance experience there.[3] [2] From 1998 to 2003, he held the position of Tokyo bureau chief, directing reporting on Japan, the Koreas, and East Asian affairs, including economic policies and North Korean nuclear tensions; to prepare, he studied Japanese intensively for a year beforehand.[17] Later, from 2003 to 2008, French served as Shanghai bureau chief for China, managing in-depth stories on the country's rapid urbanization, foreign policy, and internal transformations amid censorship pressures.[18] [19] Throughout his nearly two-decade overseas tenure ending in 2008, French also contributed as a senior writer, producing feature articles and analysis on global affairs.[20] From 2005 to 2008, concurrently with his Shanghai role, he wrote a weekly column on international issues for the International Herald Tribune, the global edition of The Times.[3] His bureau leadership involved coordinating teams of reporters, navigating local challenges, and earning two Overseas Press Club Awards for distinguished foreign correspondence.[3]

Notable Reporting Assignments and Awards

French served as a foreign correspondent for The New York Times from 1990 to 2008, holding bureau chief positions in Central America and the Caribbean, West and Central Africa, and Tokyo, where he covered Japan and the Koreas from 1998 to 2003.[14] [1] He later reported from China based in Shanghai, focusing on economic and environmental issues.[14] Notable assignments included on-the-ground coverage of Haiti's volatile 1988 elections, documenting militia violence and political unrest during a journey from Port-au-Prince to St. Marc.[21] In Africa, he reported extensively on the continent's democratic transitions in the 1990s, highlighting shifts from 1980s military coups toward multiparty politics amid economic challenges.[21] His Asia reporting encompassed China's expanding maritime interests and regional dynamics involving Japan and the Koreas.[21] French's journalism garnered significant recognition, including two nominations for the Pulitzer Prize for his reporting from Africa and China.[22] He received two Overseas Press Club Awards, one as part of a 2007 team for coverage of China's economic transformations.[23] [1] Additionally, in 2008, he contributed to a New York Times series on China's environmental degradation that won the Grantham Prize for Excellence in Reporting on the Environment, awarded to a team including French, David Barboza, Keith Bradsher, and Joseph Kahn. These honors reflect the impact of his in-depth, on-location reporting across multiple continents.[2]

Major Works

Books on Africa and Global History

Howard W. French's books on Africa and global history emphasize the continent's agency, historical contributions, and post-colonial challenges, drawing from his extensive on-the-ground reporting and archival research. His works challenge narratives that marginalize African perspectives, highlighting economic exploitation, internal governance failures, and the long-term impacts of external interventions. Published between 2004 and 2021, these texts integrate personal observations with broader historical analysis to argue for Africa's centrality in shaping modern global dynamics.[6][27] In A Continent for the Taking: The Tragedy and Hope of Africa (2004), French provides a firsthand chronicle of West and Central Africa's turmoil during the 1990s, based on his tenure as a New York Times correspondent. The book details events such as the collapse of Zaire under Mobutu Sese Seko in 1997, the Liberian civil war involving Charles Taylor's arrival in Monrovia in 1990, and the Congolese conflicts that claimed millions of lives, attributing much of the chaos to entrenched corruption, ethnic divisions exacerbated by colonial legacies, and Western disinterest punctuated by resource-driven interventions. French critiques the "disastrous continuum" in Africa-West relations, marked by Cold War-era support for dictators and subsequent neglect, while identifying glimmers of hope in emerging civil societies and leaders like Ellen Johnson Sirleaf in Liberia. Reviewers noted its humane memoir style and focus on "hot spots" like Ebola outbreaks in Congo, praising its vivid portrayal of volatility without romanticizing outcomes.[28][29][30] French's Born in Blackness: Africa, Africans, and the Making of the Modern World, 1471 to the Second World War (2021) reframes global history by centering Africa's role from Portuguese expeditions along the West African coast in the late 15th century through the Atlantic slave trade, gold and ivory economies, and their financing of European ascendancy. Spanning over five centuries, the narrative traces how African labor and resourcesâ€”estimated to have involved 12.5 million enslaved people transported across the Atlanticâ€”underpinned the rise of capitalism, the anchoring of democracy in the Americas, and industrialization in Europe, countering Eurocentric accounts that diminish these connections. French argues that this "centuries-long process of diminishment and erasure" of African agency distorts understandings of modernity, using primary sources like Portuguese chronicles and trade records to illustrate causal links, such as Upper Guinea's role in funding Portugal's maritime empire. The book culminates in the interwar period, linking African contributions to World War II's prelude. It received acclaim for its "incisive" big-picture synthesis and eloquent prose, with endorsements highlighting its challenge to standard Western histories, though some critiques noted its emphasis on exploitation over pre-colonial African complexities.[27][31][32][33]

Views on Race, Modernity, and Politics

French contends that the origins of modernity are inextricably tied to the transatlantic slave trade and Africa's resource extraction, beginning with Portuguese voyages in 1471, which fueled Europe's capital accumulation and global expansion. In Born in Blackness: Africa, Africans, and the Making of the Modern World, 1471 to the Second World War (2021), he argues that this period marked the birth of capitalism and the modern international order, with African labor and commodities like gold and sugar providing the foundational wealth that enabled European powers to finance industrialization, colonial empires, and even the Enlightenment.[27] [69] This framework rejects Eurocentric narratives that portray modernity as an internal European achievement, instead positing Africa as its geographic and human epicenter, where exploitation of black bodies generated the surpluses that propelled Western dominance.[31] Regarding race, French emphasizes the deliberate historical obliteration of Africans' centrality to world-building, which he sees as sustaining contemporary racial disparities by denying black contributions to global progress. He critiques the marginalization of African agency in standard accounts, noting that while European demand drove the slave tradeâ€”enslaving an estimated 12.5 million Africans between 1501 and 1866â€”African rulers and merchants actively participated by capturing and selling rivals, complicating simplistic victim-perpetrator binaries.[58] [70] In essays and discussions, such as his analysis of the disconnect between African Americans and the continent, French attributes ongoing "social distance" to centuries of enforced separation and propaganda that portrayed Africa as primitive, urging a reconnection through acknowledgment of shared historical traumas without romanticizing pre-colonial societies.[71] He has advocated for curricula incorporating this Africa-centered lens as essential to anti-racist education, arguing that ignoring it perpetuates ignorance of how racial capitalism originated.[8] Politically, French aligns with Pan-Africanist ideals, viewing mid-20th-century leaders like Kwame Nkrumah as architects of a "second emancipation" that sought to dismantle colonial legacies through unity and self-determination, though he acknowledges their visions were undermined by internal divisions, Cold War interventions, and economic dependencies. In The Second Emancipation: Nkrumah, Pan-Africanism, and Global Blackness at High Tide (2025), he portrays Nkrumah's Ghana as a beacon for global black liberation post-1957 independence, yet entangled with authoritarian tendencies and foreign meddling that led to Nkrumah's 1966 overthrow.[49] [54] French critiques Western skepticism toward non-aligned nations during decolonization, as seen in his reportage on post-colonial neutrality, and expresses wariness of great-power rivalriesâ€”such as U.S. and Chinese influencesâ€”exploiting African sovereignty today, favoring African-led narratives over external impositions.[21] His analyses, including on Patrice Lumumba's 1961 assassination, frame such events as products of geopolitical realpolitik rather than isolated ideological failures, underscoring a realist appreciation for power dynamics in shaping political outcomes.[53]

Reception, Impact, and Criticisms

Professional Recognition and Influence

Howard W. French has held influential positions in journalism and academia, including serving as an associate professor at Columbia University's Graduate School of Journalism, where he teaches international reporting and emphasizes analysis of power dynamics and geopolitics.[2] His tenure at The New York Times spanned over two decades from 1986 to 2008, during which he worked as a foreign correspondent and senior writer, holding bureau chief roles in Central America and the Caribbean, West Africa, Japan and the Koreas, and China.[13] He also contributed as a weekly columnist on global affairs for the International Herald Tribune from 2005 to 2008.[13] French's reporting and writing have earned significant accolades, including two Overseas Press Club Awards, the Grantham Environmental Award, and two Pulitzer Prize nominations.[13] In April 2025, he was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, recognizing his decades-long contributions to international reporting from Africa, the Americas, and Asia, as well as his authorship of five books and efforts in educating future journalists.[24] He received an honorary doctorate from the University of Maryland and was a fellow of the Open Society Foundations from 2011 to 2012.[2] His books have amplified his influence, with A Continent for the Taking (2004) named non-fiction book of the year by multiple newspapers, and China's Second Continent (2014) selected as one of The New York Times' 100 Notable Books and praised as a top title by The Economist, The Guardian, and Foreign Affairs.[2] Born in Blackness: Africa, Africans, and the Making of the Modern World, 1471 to the Second World War (2021) won the 2022 MAAH Stone Award and the Hurston/Wright Legacy Award, reshaping discussions on Africa's role in global history.[72] French's critiques extend to media practices; in 2015, he co-signed an open letter with over 200 writers and academics denouncing CBS News' Africa coverage as "the worst of journalism" for perpetuating stereotypes.[73] Through these works and his pedagogy, French has influenced scholarly and public understanding of underreported global regions.[24]

Debates Over Historical Interpretations

French's reinterpretation of global history in Born in Blackness (2021), positing Africa's gold and labor as the foundational drivers of European ascendancy and modernity from 1471 onward, directly confronts entrenched Eurocentric narratives that marginalize the continent's agency. He contends that Portugal's early explorations targeted West African wealth rather than Asian spices, enabling the financing of further voyages, Renaissance advancements, and Atlantic expansion, with the transatlantic slave tradeâ€”enslaving roughly 12.5 million peopleâ€”fueling industrialization through commodities like cotton and sugar.[31] [69] This thesis challenges orthodox accounts, such as those emphasizing internal European innovations or the primacy of India-China trade routes, and refutes Hegel's dismissal of Africa as ahistorical by evidencing sophisticated pre-colonial commercial networks.[57] While praised for illuminating a "symphony of erasure" in Western historiography, French's emphasis on African centrality has intersected with broader debates, including comparisons to U.S.-focused projects like the 1619 initiative, which prioritize slavery's domestic legacies over global origins.[57] His inclusion of African elites' complicity in slave captures, linked to fragmented polities lacking ideological unity, balances victimhood with agency, potentially tempering critiques from Afrocentric perspectives that minimize internal responsibility but aligning with empirical records of kingdom-level participation.[69] Such framing invites scrutiny over the relative weights of exogenous exploitation versus endogenous factors in Africa's historical trajectories. In Everything Under the Heavens (2017), French's application of the ancient tianxia ("all under heaven") worldview to explain China's contemporary territorial ambitions and influence operations abroad has fueled discussions on the perils of historical determinism in foreign policy analysis. Critics of analogous reasoning argue it risks conflating imperial nostalgia with pragmatic statecraft, though French's archival grounding underscores continuities in Sinocentric spatial conceptions from Ming voyages to Belt and Road initiatives.[36] [62] This interpretation counters Beijing's self-narrative of peaceful rise, prompting realist counterpoints that prioritize economic incentives over cultural legacies.[74]

Critiques of Methodological and Ideological Approaches

Critics of Howard W. French's historical methodology have pointed to selectivity in source material and interpretive focus, particularly in works like Born in Blackness: Africa, Africans, and the Making of the Modern World, 1471 to the Second World War (2021). Historian Toyin Falola, reviewing the book, argues that French's emphasis on African potentates and economic drivers such as gold and the slave trade overlooks significant intellectual agency among West African thinkers in responding to European encroachment and articulating visions of modernity. Specifically, Falola notes the absence of references to key texts like the 1871 Constitution of the Fante Confederacy, Liberia's foundational documents, and writings by James Africanus Horton, Edward Wilmot Blyden, and Alexander Crummell, which demonstrated early African political philosophy and resistance strategies.[56] This omission, Falola contends, narrows the portrayal of African contributions to elite-level transactions rather than encompassing broader philosophical and institutional innovations from regions like modern Ghana and Sierra Leone. French's ideological approach, rooted in a corrective to Eurocentric narratives, has been observed to prioritize structural and external causal factorsâ€”such as European demand fueling African enslavementâ€”in explaining the continent's integration into global systems, potentially underweighting endogenous cultural or governance dynamics. In earlier journalism like A Continent for the Taking: The Tragedy and Hope of Africa (2004), French attributes post-colonial stagnation largely to leadership corruption and foreign interference, yet reviewers have critiqued this framing for insufficiently integrating pre-colonial internal divisions or ideological rigidities as persistent barriers to development.[75] Such perspectives align with broader debates in historiography, where French's materialist lens is seen by some as echoing dependency theory's emphasis on exploitation over intra-African agency in perpetuating cycles of underdevelopment. As a journalist rather than a trained academic historian, French's reliance on narrative synthesis from secondary sources and fieldwork has drawn implicit methodological questions regarding archival depth and counterfactual rigor. For instance, in analyses of China's expansion in Everything Under the Heavens: How the Past Helps Shape China's Push for Global Power (2017), his historical analogies to imperial tribute systems inform a cautionary view of Beijing's ambitions, but lack quantitative modeling of economic incentives or alternative scenarios, leading some observers to view the work as more interpretive than predictive.[34] These elements reflect a journalistic strength in accessibility but invite critique for favoring causal storytelling over exhaustive empirical falsification typical in peer-reviewed scholarship.
